Abstract
This thesis has investigated the possibility of using photovoltaics together with heat
pumps to generate heat in Danish district heating plants. An economic study has
been carried out in order to assess the feasibility of replacing some current solar
heating plants by the plants using PV panels and heat pumps.
For that, a literature review has been done ﬁrst, where optimistic arguments in fa-
vor of the new technology have been found but large-scale projects using the proposed
technology have not been discovered. A state of the art chapter has been dedicated
to give an overview of the European and Danish heating sector, solar heating tech-
nology and technologies to be used in the new PV-HP system. Heat storage has been
demonstrated to be a crucial technology when using solar energy to produce heat.
Three solar heating plants have been selected due to their representative solar
fraction: Sæby (7%), Gr˚asten (28%) and Dronninlund (50%). In order to replace
them, three new PV-HP plants have been designed. Ammonia heat pumps operate
with an assumed constant COP of 4 powered by the PV panels. The PV subsystem
has been designed using the software PVsyst.
Afterwards, heat demand and heat production proﬁles have been calculated for
each plant and used as input in an Excel model that has been built to control the
storage situation throughout the year.
With this model, it has been possible to
identify overproduction in the solar heating system or electricity surplus in the PV-
HP system.
This electricity surplus from the PV-HP system will be sold to the
electricity market. Dronninglund and Gr˚asten have presented overproduction in the
solar thermal system and also electricity surplus in the PV-HP system. Contrarily,
Sæby has not presented neither overproduction in the solar collectors nor electricity
surplus in the PV-HP system.
The last chapter has economically compared the current solar heating systems
and the newly designed PV-HP systems. An introduction to the theory behind the
economic analysis and the studied economic parameters is followed by the creation
of the economic Excel model. The results have been presented, showing that Dron-
ninglund and Sæby have obtained proﬁtability for both the solar thermal system and
the PV-HP system. Moreover, Sæby has been the most proﬁtable solar heating plant
==PAGE:6==
due to the lack of overproduction. Regarding the PV-HP system, Dronninglund has
had slightly better results than the other plants, just having a higher LCOE than
Sæby, which happens again due to the overproduction. However, Gr˚asten has just
obtained proﬁtability in the solar heating system, since the PV-HP project has re-
sulted unfeasible. The main reason is the low heat price in Gr˚asten, which is around
30% lower than the heat price in Dronninglund or Sæby. Note that for the three
studied cases, economic results have been better for the solar heating plant. Finally,
a sensitivity analysis on the economic results (NPV) of Gr˚asten’s PV-HP system has
been carried out, varying several key inputs of the economic model. The parameters
aﬀecting the most the economic results have been: the COP of the heat pumps during
operation, the discount rate, the investment costs and the heat price. Nevertheless,
when considering the changes of these parameters also in the solar heating plant, the
COP and investment costs become the main way for the PV-HP plant to become
competitive. This is because an increase in the heat price or the discount rate would
aﬀect the feasibility of both the solar heating plant and the PV-HP plant at a similar
level.
In conclusion the PV-HP system is not competitive against solar heating in Danish
district heating plants, since the latest is the cheapest option to generate heat. Some
changes would be needed for PV-HP to reach the same proﬁtability as solar collectors’
plants. Changes in the aforementioned parameters, as well as other possibilities such
as subsidies for heat pumps would enhance the competitiveness of the PV-HP system
in the future.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The worldwide problem of climate change and the dramatic energy demand increase in
the last decades are moving societies toward more sustainable behaviours. Moreover,
growing awareness about the scarcity of natural non-renewable resources has lead to
the use of new renewable energy sources.
This development is not evenly distributed across the planet. Europe is lead-
ing this change with the most restrictive political measures in place, especially in
Denmark. According to the European Union (EU) 20-20-20 targets, until 2020 a re-
duction in EU greenhouse gas emissions of at least 20% below 1990 levels is foreseen.
Furthermore, according to the European Commission (EC) this reduction should be
of 80-95% by 2050 [1].
Currently, new sources of electricity and heat generation compete with conven-
tional generation sources such as fossil fuel based-plants. Heat generation, responsible
for around 50% of total energy demand in Europe, is seeking for sources with higher

1.1.1
Technological problems
Here are presented the main technological problems, which can be solved with current
technology solutions:
• Problem: Time diﬀerence.
Diﬀerent production and demand proﬁles.
→
Solution: Heat storage. Since very often heating demand does not coincide
with renewable supply, several technologies can be used to store heat. The heat
storage type depends on the utilization, having the possibility to install storage
with diﬀerent sizes, heat release rates, heat capacities, etc. Thermal storage
allows for example to increase the use of solar heating in a district heating
plant without changing the solar collector area and can also reduce the number
of start/stop cycles of biomass boilers and heat pumps.
• Problem: Scale diﬀerence. While production often requires large capacities,
demand is mostly required by small units (e.g. households). →Solution: Dis-

and consumers in one system. This solution makes it possible to exploit sources
which are not usable for small applications, such as geothermal heat, biomass
plants, large solar heating plants, etc.
• Problem: Temperature diﬀerence. Diﬀerent temperature levels in produc-
tion and required demand. →Solution: Heat pumps. Heat pumps can use
diﬀerent energy sources to provide diﬀerent levels of temperature. Furthermore,
some heat pumps can be used for both heating and cooling [2].
All these technological solutions will be used as a base for the technical part of
this thesis, so that all the appointed technological problems can be solved.
1.1.2
Non-technological issues
Besides the technological problems, also non-technological factors have to be taken
into account. The following framework conditions are needed to exploit the whole
potential of renewable heating, being most of them already implemented in Denmark:
• Policy and legislation: Binding energy eﬃciency targets and renewable en-
ergy obligations in both new and existing buildings. Politicians must be con-
vinced that renewable heating can play a key role in the future. Besides, autho-
rization processes to deploy renewable heating technologies should be stream-
lined and their costs brought down.

• Financial support. Renewable heating technologies require speciﬁc incentives
until they are cost competitive with conventional technologies, as well as for

1.2
Research question
Due to the upcoming trend in the use of solar collectors in Danish district heating
plants and taking into consideration the aforementioned technological issues, other
solutions using solar energy are being studied. Reductions in photovoltaic (PV) costs
and maturation of heat pump (HP) technology lead to a new possibility: PV-assisted
heat pump water heating connected to heat storage. Solar PV modules would generate
electricity which would be used either to heat up water with the heat pumps or to
be sold to the utility grid. The decision of how to use the produced electricity would
depend on several variables: state of the heat storage, ambient conditions, demand,
etc. From now on this technology will be called ”PV-HP”.
Taking advantage of the coeﬃcient of performance (COP, see equation 3.1) of the
heat pumps, the necessary electricity production will be several times lower than the
ﬁnal heat production. The system will operate generating heat until the moment that
the following conditions are met:
1. The heat production from the PV-HP system is larger than the required demand
3
==PAGE:22==
2. The heat storage is full, and the maximum temperature level is reached at the
top of the storage
When these two conditions are met, which usually happens in the summer months,
the system stops producing heat and starts selling electricity to the grid. In such a
way heat overproduction is avoided by the PV-HP system, whilst in solar thermal
systems this overproduction is very typical. In solar collectors, when the maximum
temperature is reached in the tank during the day, the operators are forced to cool
down water at night, using the solar panels as radiators. This leads to a waste of
heat and thus a decrease in the heat production.
1.2.1
Advantages and disadvantages of the PV-HP system
The new PV-HP system presents several advantages and disadvantages. Some of the
main beneﬁts derived from using this technology are presented below:
• The overproduction can be avoided by using the sun radiation to generate elec-
tricity in moments when heat would be wasted and selling it to the grid. On
the contrary, current solar collectors present overproduction in most of the in-
stallations.
• In case of failure of one of the subsystems the other one could still work. PV
panels would be able to produce electricity and the heat pumps would be able
to operate powered by electricity from the grid. Contrarily, in case of failure in
the solar heating system the operation has to be stopped.
• Photovoltaic panels and heat pumps are expected to have a larger decrease
in their prices than solar heating panels in the coming decades. This means
that projects using PV-HP technology can reach higher proﬁtability, becoming
competitive against solar thermal technology [3].
• This system can add ﬂexibility to a power system with a large share of renewable
energies, by using electricity surplus to power the heat pumps. In this way, heat
is generated with lower costs while the system gains ﬂexibility.
Nonetheless, the PV-HP system also presents some drawbacks:
• Even though prices of PV and HP are decreasing, heat generation costs for
PV-HP technology are still high in comparison with the costs of solar thermal
panels.
4
==PAGE:23==
• The eﬃciency of heat pumps depends on the heat source, which is an issue
if the heat source is exposed to temperature changes throughout the year. In
temperate climates this is not a big problem, but in Denmark this can decrease
the performance of the system during a long part of the year.
• Although both PV and HP technology are widely known, there are not large-
scale projects using both together to produce heat as proposed in this thesis.
Hence, while solar heating systems perform well in many countries, there is still
no proven evidence that PV-HP systems will work that well for such application.
1.2.2
Goal of the thesis
This thesis aims to evaluate whether PV-HP systems can represent a paradigm shift
in the Danish district heating sector.
For that, PV-HP systems will be designed
and compared with three representative solar heating plants in Denmark. Both sys-
tems will be mainly compared in terms of economy, using parameters as Net Present
Value, Internal Rate of Return, Levelized Cost of Energy and Payback Time of the
investment. In this way, it will be known if the PV-HP is economically competitive
currently or if it can become competitive in the future.

Denmark had 23,415 MW of heat installed capacity (including autoproducers) in

3.2
Solar heating
Solar heating has a very large potential since it is based on the solar radiation, an
inexhaustible source of energy. Solar heating is the conversion of sunlight to hot water
by means of solar thermal panels (further details about the technology in section
3.2.2).
Current applications of solar thermal technologies are: domestic hot water (DHW)
for single and multi-family houses, space heating demand (SHD) for single and multi-
family houses and hot water for the tertiary sector [2]. Moreover, in some European
countries such as Denmark, Sweden, Germany or Austria solar heating systems con-
nected to the district heating network are already well established.
Within the heating sector solar thermal technology has some speciﬁc beneﬁts:
• Economic: A solar heating installation can lower the price of a central district
heating plant signiﬁcantly, in some cases by as much as 20%, mainly by low
operational costs in comparison with conventional sources.
• Independence: Once the solar system has been installed, the production is
independent of variable fuel prices, ﬁnite resources and many other conditions.
Moreover, solar thermal plants are easy to combine with a wide range of back-up
heat sources.
• Environment: With a low life-cycle impact, low primary energy consump-
tion and zero operational CO2 emissions, solar panels reduce global warming
contributing to a cleaner and more sustainable future [49].

3.2.2
Danish solar heating technology facts
The 58 Danish solar heating plants have some common characteristics and follow some
trends, leading the market with 9 of the 10 largest solar heating plants connected to
district heating in 2014, and leading also the research and development within the
ﬁeld. With ARCON Solar A/S and SUNMARK (merged on February 3, 2014) as
leading companies in the sector, Danish installations are supplied with the latest
solutions.
All Danish solar thermal plants are installed at ground level, consisting of parallel
rows of large ﬂat plate collector panels (with between 12 and 14 m2 of area) connected
in series. In some plants, there are also small areas with other types of collectors, such
as concentrating solar collectors or evacuated tubular solar collectors. Solar collectors
are facing south in all Danish plants, with a tilt in the range from 25o to 45o, although
the majority are between 35o and 45o [16].

A propylene glycol/water mixture is normally used as solar collector ﬂuid. This

performance and vice versa.

Types of net connection
Following on from a review of current solar heating technology it is necessary to
elaborate on the methods of connection to the district heating network.
In this
respect, there are two main options: distributed and centralized.
Distributed:
In distributed solar district heating plants (see ﬁgure 3.12) the solar
collectors are placed at suitable locations and directly connected to the district heating
network. Often these installations use the district heating grid as a storage. The main
drawback of this option is that there exists the possibility that the grid storage is not
large enough for all the heat surplus in sunny periods, which results in overproduction.
Thus, depending on the size of the plant, the solar installation would be used below
its potential, decreasing the ratio of solar heat in the heat production mix.
Figure 3.12: Sketch of a distributed solar district heating system [52].
Centralized:
In centralized solar district heating plants (ﬁgure 3.13) the solar col-
lectors supply heat to a main district heating central. This central heating plant may
be supplied by other heat sources as CHP, biomass boilers, gas boilers, etc. By using
large seasonal heat storage, solar thermal installations can contribute to more than
50% of the total heating demand.

3.3
Heat storage
An important part of new district heating grids is the heat storage. It is a key enabler
in increasing the overall output of renewable heating, allowing a greater fraction of
the renewable production to be utilized. In the case of solar heating systems, thermal
storage can double the eﬀective yield of the solar heating panels without changing the
solar collector area. Additionally, in district heating plants it can reduce the number
of start/stop cycles of boilers and heat pumps, which for boilers represents the period
with the largest amount of polluting emissions [2].
The integration of thermal storage in district heating systems can therefore en-
hance the systems’ performance, enabling the utilization of more sources and reducing
costs considerably. In this way, it can be stated that thermal storage provides ﬂexi-
bility to the system, and simpliﬁes the combination of diﬀerent technologies and the
advantages of each.
Currently, very diﬀerent heat storage installations can be found, from steel storage
tanks with capacities of some hundreds m3 (buﬀering capability for 1 to 7 days) to
big Pit Thermal Energy Storage (PTES) of 60,000 m3 (seasonal buﬀering capability).
This capacity will depend on the characteristics and the needs of the heating plant.
Lately, water pits are the most commonly used technology in large heating plants in
Denmark, supported by studies demonstrating its cost-eﬀectiveness for large volume
24
==PAGE:43==
applications. A water pit is basically a hole in the ground ﬁlled with water, lined
with a water-proof layer and covered by an insulating ﬂoating lid. Heat losses and
eﬃciencies depend on many parameters, but the most important one is the surface to
volume ratio, as this is where the primary heat exchange occurs. Hence, the eﬃciency
in large pits is normally higher than in smaller steel tanks and can reach values of
80-95% [3].

3.4
Photovoltaics
Photovoltaic systems receive solar radiation as input to produce power and are one
of the most utilized renewable energies in the world. They are used in rooftops of
buildings, as well as in large-scale installations in sunny countries. The potential of
the system depends mainly on the location’s solar energy resource, but is aﬀected by
shading and orientation as well. In Denmark, solar irradiation has a modest potential,
and is mainly received in summer whilst averaging 1046 kWh/m2 per year [3].
In order to increase the gain in energy production some PV systems are mounted
on a sun-tracking device, which can increase the received sun energy by 20%. However,
tracker systems are not considered important in Denmark since the absolute gain in
energy production is negligible in comparison with the added costs [3].

Typical eﬃciencies of contemporary PV modules are 15-17%, with state of the
art systems reaching above 20% (measured at standard conditions). The technical
lifetime of the total system is generally around 30 years. Capacities range from a few
hundred watts to gigawatts [3].
Despite cost reduction during the last decade, PV investment costs are still ex-

for approximately 50% of total investment costs. The remaining costs come from
inverters, cables, combiner boxes, optimizers, monitoring equipment and transform-

plant is 1 e million/MW (or around 171 e/m2 of cell area), whilst O&M costs are

market price) consists of 0.60 DKK per kWh applicable for the ﬁrst 10 years of
operation, and 0.40 DKK for the further 10 years of operation [41].
3.5
Heat pumps
Heat pumps are used for diﬀerent purposes such as industry applications, individual
space heating, heat recovery or district heat production.
Heat pumps move heat
from a low-temperature source to a warmer sink, converting the heat to a higher
temperature by means of a closed cycle. This closed cycle is powered by a compressor
in electrically-driven heat pumps or by heat in absorption heat pumps. So there are
two types of heat pumps:
26
==PAGE:45==
• Electrically-driven heat pumps consist of an electric motor driving the compres-
sor. In this type of heat pump a ﬂuid transfers the heat from a low-temperature
source to a higher temperature sink. Electric heat pumps can be turned on/oﬀ
depending on the electricity price or the solar radiation (in the case of the PV-
HP system), so they can assist the production plant to maintain optimum load.
Furthermore, the cycle direction can be switched, so that the same heat pump
can be used to both heat and cool. Currently, the small heat pumps used for
individual space heating are predominantly electrically-driven.
• Thermally-driven heat pumps (absorption) can make use of heat coming from
diﬀerent sources to cool or refrigerate. In this type of heat pump electricity is
needed just for auxiliary components such as pumps to circulate the working
ﬂuid. This type is mainly used for cooling, and using them low temperatures
can be reached for example in the inlet water of solar thermal panels, enhancing
the COP of the heating system [3].
Heat pumps are the subject of numerous studies and discussion because of their
high eﬃciency. This eﬃciency is measured using the coeﬃcient of performance, which
in electrically-driven heat pumps is calculated as:
COP = Qh

where Qh is the heat transferred to the water (heating case) and Wel is the elec-

The COP of a heat pump depends on many parameters including temperature,
compressor type, etc. For electrically-driven heat pumps (see ﬁgure 3.16) heat out-
put can reach 2-6 times the power input, depending on the sink temperature, e.g.
the forward temperature of the district heating system.
However, for absorption
heat pumps the eﬃciency is not aﬀected by temperature diﬀerences. Eﬃciencies in
absorption heat pumps are around 1,7 [3].
The working ﬂuid used in the cycle can also aﬀect the eﬃciency of both types of
heat pumps. Previous refrigerants such as chloroﬂuorocarbon (CFC) or hydrochlo-
roﬂuorocarbon (HCFC) have been phased out because they were shown to be ozone
depleting. New refrigerants such as carbon dioxide, ammonia, hydrocarbons, water
or even hybrid systems are therefore currently used.
Capacities of heat pumps range from a few watts up to 10 MW. The lifetime is
expected to be about 20 years, but it is increasing constantly as research advances.

3.5.1
Heat sources
An important decision when installing a heat pump is the heat source that is going to
be utilized. In Denmark, the heat source is normally renewable energy and this can
be ambient air, ground or water, but other sources are also available. Importantly,
the performance of the heat pump will depend to a large degree on this decision and
will vary with temperature variations in the source (if applicable). The dominant
sources for heat pumps are the following:
Air is a widely used source for heat pumps in households and buildings, where
heat is extracted from ambient air drawn across a heat exchanger. However, cold
ambient temperatures can signiﬁcantly decrease the COP of the heat pump, leading
to a low performance specially in the heating season. This could be further aﬀected
by the low average annual ambient temperature of 8oC in Denmark. Besides, the
heat capacity of air is four times lower than the heat capacity of water, which means
that for large-scale heat pumps enormous fans would be needed, increasing the noise
of the installation.
Ground, as a medium, is also widely employed for residential heating. Normally,
ground heat collectors are buried in the ground using horizontal piping or vertical
boreholes in order to proﬁt from the stable temperatures during the year. In Denmark,
ground temperatures vary from 0 to 18oC, but the stability increases with deeper
collectors, reaching almost constant temperatures at a depth of around 9-12 meters
[20]. Nevertheless, current ground installations are more expensive for large systems
due to the piping investment cost.
Water presents more advantages for large-scale heat pumps:
• Sewage water is already used in large heat pump systems, for example in Sweden
or Denmark (Frederikshavn, CO2 heat pump). It presents several advantages
including the low ﬂow ﬂuctuation throughout the year and the relatively high
temperatures which vary from around 11oC in winter to around 20oC in summer.
Note that due to some issues with the temperature at which the biological pro-
cess is carried out in water puriﬁcation plants, the best moment to extract the
temperature of the water is just before releasing it to the sea or river. The ﬁnal
releasing temperature has to be agreed with the Technical and Environmental
Committee in each individual municipality [24].
29
==PAGE:48==
• Seawater is another option to use as source, since it has an inexhaustible poten-
tial. Nevertheless, in sea zones near the Danish coast, water temperatures are
of 17-20oC in summer and 0-4oC in winter. Hence, the COP of the heat pumps
would be quite reduced in winter. One way to avoid this problem is to use
seawater from deeper sea zones, where bottom layers have more stable temper-
atures, ranging from 3 to 11oC throughout the year [54]. The main problem of
this option would be the investment costs, making non-viable the installation.
• Groundwater from water wells is also a possibility. This is commonly called
Aquifer Thermal Energy Storage (ATES), and consists of at least two wells in
the same aquifer with enough distance between them. One is called hot well
(where the hot water is injected and extracted) and the other cold well (where
the same occurs for cold water). In Denmark, groundwater temperature ranges
from 5 to 10oC. Unlike the other cases, ATES directly extracts the water from
the ground and uses an open loop, passing the water directly through the heat
exchanger and injecting it later back into the ground.
The stability of the
groundwater temperature has to be considered and annual balance is needed.
• Drinking water ﬂow also presents a constant rate throughout the year. In Den-
mark, temperature of drinking water ranges from 7oC in winter to around 15oC
in summer. However, the executive order [55] states that the supplier should
maintain the forward temperature below 12oC the whole year.
There is no
current regulation for the minimum temperature.
Others: As previously mentioned, there are other options to be used as heat
source in a large heat pump installation. Industrial waste heat or heat from CHP
plants can be transferred to the working ﬂuid in the heat pump cycle. Another oppor-
tunity can be to use the waste condensing heat extracted from cooling applications
(large factories or district cooling) to heat up the working ﬂuid.
Thus, depending on the characteristics of the heat source, it is possible to avoid
the temperature variation throughout the year. In this way it is easier to reach higher
temperature levels than with natural sources, so that better performances during the
whole year can be achieved.
Furthermore, these options can be used together with groundwater in ATES. In
the summer time with low heat demand and high cooling demand, heat from CHP
plants, industry, cooling systems or even solar systems can be stored in underground
aquifers. In winter time, water is pumped up to the surface to use it as source for the
30
==PAGE:49==
heat pumps in order to produce district heating. There exist some recent examples
of this system in Denmark, such as the one in Bjerringbro Fjernvarme, where a low
temperature ATES was constructed in cooperation with the company Grundfos in
2013, or another one at Copenhagen Airport, which also entered in operation in
2013. Both store heat in summer in order to use it in the heat pump system in winter
periods.

4.1
Decision parameter
Many parameters aﬀect the performance and the output of solar heating plants, and
thereby the ﬁnal economic results: type of solar collector, diﬀerent designs of the solar
collector ﬁeld, manufacturer, presence of foil in the solar collector, year of installation,
collector area, collector tilt, collector eﬃciency, solar collector ﬂuid, heat losses in the
pipes of the solar collector loops, weather conditions, moisture conditions inside the
solar collectors, etc. Additionally, changing operational factors as ﬂuid temperature
or volume ﬂow rate through the panels can also aﬀect the performance of the system
a lot.
Due to the large number of parameters inﬂuencing the operation of solar thermal
plants, it was diﬃcult to ﬁnd one parameter to classify the current 58 danish solar
heating plants. Thus, a decisive parameter aﬀecting largely the results was chosen.
4.1.1
Solar fraction
The decision was to choose a general parameter that could be known for all the plants.
This parameter should aﬀect greatly the production of the solar thermal installation,
varying the economic results.
33
==PAGE:52==
Consequently, the chosen parameter was the solar fraction, which shows the
amount of heat provided by the solar thermal panels out of the total heat demand
supplied by the district heating plant:
Solar fraction (%) = Amount of heat provided by solar panels
Total heat demand supplied by the plant
(4.1)
Solar fraction has been increasing during the last decades in solar thermal instal-
lations. The ﬁrst Danish installations, Saltum and Ry in 1988 [56], were directly
connected to the district heating network (distributed solar district heating plants),
which meant that in sunny days when the production was large (maybe even greater
than the total heat demand) heat could be wasted since there was no storage. How-
ever, in cloudy days when there was not production at all, probably the heat demand
was not reached. This caused that other heat sources such as CHP, gas turbines,
biomass plants, etc. had to supply the heat demand most of the time (low solar
fraction), requiring a lot of back-up capacity.
These problems matching production and demand led to the use of heat storage
together with solar heating installations. In this way, heat surplus of sunny days can
be stored and used in cloudy days when the production is lower than the demand,
increasing in this way the solar fraction. Thus, the solar fraction is aﬀected by the
size of the heat storage, but also by the solar collector area, the heat demand or other
factors as the annual solar radiation. Hence, solar fraction values are actually either
measured values or estimated values.

Main design assumptions
In order to design the PV-HP system, some important assumptions are made:
• The main assumption is that the PV-HP system is designed to provide the

currently. Like this, the PV-HP system will replace the solar heating system

plant are as follows:
– Dronninglund →18,000 MWh
– Gr˚asten →9,700 MWh
– Sæby →6,300 MWh
• Regarding the heat pumps, it is assumed that the design of the HP subsystem
is the same in the three studied cases. Thus, regardless of the available heat
sources in the three plants, it is assumed that they all can access a heat source
with the same potential and have the same thermal performance.
• The COP of the HP subsystem is assumed to be constant throughout the year.
This is a rough assumption depending on the studied case, since the COP can
signiﬁcantly change the ﬁnal heat production of the system from season to

season. However, the characteristics of the designed system in this thesis make
this a reasonable assumption, as it will be explained in following sections.
• The design of the PV subsystem is done to provide annually the needed amount

These are the main assumptions for the design, but minor assumptions will be
indicated if necessary along the chapter.
5.2
Heat pumps’ subsystem
The design of the HP subsystem is based on several reports and articles, which study
the most suitable type of heat pump and heat source depending on the available

needed heat capacities can exceed the maximum capacity of the available heat pumps

in order to reach the needed capacity.

Heat source
Many options are available for the heat source, as it was mentioned in section 3.5.1.
Water (sewage water, seawater, groundwater and drinking water), air and ground are
the main renewable possibilities. All these sources can be selected based on their heat
potential using the formula:
˙Psource = ˙msource · cp,source · ∆Tsource
(5.1)
Where ˙msource is the source’s mass ﬂow, cp,source is the speciﬁc heat capacity of the
source and ∆Tsource is the variation of temperature in the source ﬂow [24].
This formula was used in [24] to evaluate the potential of the sources in Denmark,
getting to the conclusion that water would be the source with the highest potential,
followed by air and ground.
However, other options as industrial waste heat, heat from power plants (CHP)
or waste condensing heat from cooling applications can be feasible solutions as well.
The contacted company recommended not to use seawater and air, due to ﬁnancial
unfeasibility (it could be technically feasible). From their point of view, the best
options for such large systems would be to use heat from industrial waste, CHP
plants or cooling applications [62]. Following these indications, it was decided to

industrial heat or large cooling applications are not found near the heating plant, all
three plants can use waste heat from their CHP units. This would avoid the higher

Once the heat source was selected, it was possible to select the type of heat pump

feasible for smaller systems [62]. Thus, electrically-driven NH3 heat pumps are used
for the HP subsystem.

COP of the heat pumps
Taking into consideration that the heat source is assumed to be hardly aﬀected by
seasonal temperature changes and that the COP will be assumed to be constant
throughout the year, the heat pump performance had to be deﬁned. [62] provided
information of heat pump installations in Denmark. According to this source, it is
very rare to ﬁnd similar projects to the studied one being feasible with a COP lower
than 4. Thus, a COP of 4 was assumed for the three systems in order to carry out the
design and the ﬁrst economic comparison. However, as this design parameter is one
of the most relevant regarding the system performance it will be modiﬁed in the ﬁnal
sensitivity analysis to evaluate how a change in the COP would aﬀect the economic
beneﬁts.

Chapter 6
Heat demand and heat production
This chapter aims to calculate and represent the heat demand and heat production
proﬁles for the current solar heating plants and the new PV-HP plants, which will be
used to know the overproduction of the system. An Excel model using both proﬁles
is created to study the storage situation throughout the year, evaluating the presence
of overproduction in the system. These results will be used as input in the economic
study of the next chapter.
6.1
Heat demand
Denmark’s heat demand proﬁles use to have similar trends from year to year, having
higher values in cold periods and low values in warm periods. However, winter periods
can have very diﬀerent temperatures from year to year, which can happen in summer
as well, when sun radiation can vary a lot from year to year. Moreover, each of the
three selected district heating plants may have diﬀerent demand proﬁles depending
on the connected customers. This would make quite diﬃcult to compare the three
of them regarding overproduction and economy, so for this study it is assumed that
the three solar heating plants supply heat to the same heat demand proﬁle (scaled to
each plant’s annual estimated heat demand). In order to make this, Heating Degree
Days (HDD) are used to calculate the space heating demand, to which domestic hot
water consumption and its corresponding losses are added.
6.1.1
Space heating demand (Heating Degree Days)
HDD are a measure used to reﬂect the energy demand needed for space heating. This
measure comes from the outside air temperature. The heating demand at a speciﬁc
place is considered to be directly proportional to the number of HDD at that place,
49
==PAGE:68==
being this parameter commonly used for calculations related to heat consumption in
buildings.
HDD are always deﬁned relative to a base ambient temperature. This tempera-
ture will indicate the limit above which a building does not need heating. The base
temperature is adequate for indoor human comfort and will depend on many condi-
tions such as the nature of the building, occupants, equipment, etc. Therefore, it is
normally used as an approximation and the value will depend mainly on the country.
For Denmark, the utilized base temperature is 17oC [64]. Hence, we can deﬁne easily
HDD as a measure of how much (in degrees) and for how long (in days) the outside
air temperature was below 17oC [65].
It is important to take into account that HDD just provide a rough estimation of
seasonal heating requirements. Diﬀerent levels of insulation in buildings, temperature
drops during the day or diﬀerent indoor temperature levels depending on the customer
would vary the real heat demand.
Calculation of HDD is normally carried out using daily mean temperature data.
However, depending on the source heat demand can be also calculated with hourly
mean temperature (called Heating Degree Hours - HDH), weekly mean temperature,
etc. Likewise, heat demand can be calculated using data from one year or using av-
erage data from many years. For this thesis, daily mean temperature in Copenhagen
is going to be used for the graphs. However, hourly data from the same source will
be used for the calculations and the buﬀer situation model, so that hourly overpro-
duction can be calculated. Both daily data and hourly data are extracted from the
synthetic average yearly data of PVsyst. This assumption can aﬀect the results since
temperatures can change from year to year and from place to place. However, since
the data takes into account thirty years the inaccuracy of the yearly data is mini-
mized. Moreover, since the data is the same for the three plants, the comparison
between the three cases is not going to be aﬀected.
Regarding the mathematical calculation, HDD are calculated with an easy formula
using temperature diﬀerences of 1oC. HDD for a given day is estimated using the
temperature diﬀerence between the base temperature and the outside daily mean
temperature:
HDD = Base temperature −Outside mean temperature
(6.1)
This means that if for example in a winter day the outside mean temperature
was -3oC, the amount of HDD would be 20, and if the outside mean temperature
was 25oC, the amount of HDD would be -8. Obviously, days with negative HDD
50
==PAGE:69==
will be considered as 0, since no heating will be needed and cooling is not taken into
consideration.
For our the calculations HDD are going to be divided in HDH. Knowing the hourly
outdoor mean ambient temperature the heat demand can be calculated for each hour,
in the same way it is done for HDD. Besides, the daily and hourly heat demand values
calculated with HDD and HDH must be treated as a percentage of the total annual
heat demand supplied by the district heating plant. In this way it is ensured that
the heat demand proﬁle calculated matches the real annual heat demand in a yearly
basis.
6.1.2
Domestic hot water demand
Although the main part of the total heat demand is used for space heating, domestic
hot water in households represents a percentage to take into account, mainly in sum-
mer when no space heating is needed. Moreover, losses due to the circulation pipes
and tank losses in households must be considered.
According to [66], DHW consumption per household is in average 60-150 l/day,
which corresponds to 1000-2500 kWh/year. Using an average assumed value of 1700
kWh/year per household and adding losses of 25% (due to tank losses and pipe losses
[66]), total DHW demand per household results to be 2100 kWh/year. This value
will be multiplied by the number of customers connected to each heating plant and
distributed equally along the year. Finally, it will be added to the space heating
demand.

6.3
Overproduction
6.3.1
Solar heating system
Overproduction in current Danish solar heating systems occurs always in summer. In
these months there exists a low heating demand, which in some days corresponds just
to DHW consumption. This low heating demand added to the higher solar radiation
means that in sunny days heat production from the solar thermal panels is higher
than the demand (see ﬁgure 6.6). This energy surplus is stored in the heat storage
until it is needed to cover the demand. However, in summer usually happens that
the heat storage reaches the maximum allowed temperature level, which is given by
the technical speciﬁcations of the storage. When this happens, it is necessary to cool
down some water at night by circulating it through the solar panels, using them as
radiators. In this way, plant operators avoid having too high temperatures in the
storage.
As mentioned in previous sections, this overproduction is inevitable in many sys-
tems and means one of the main drawbacks of solar heating plants. Energy is wasted
55
==PAGE:74==

(Gr˚asten’s case, temperature and solar radiation data from PVsyst and ”Solvarme-
data.dk” [57] for one year).
and beneﬁts decrease. Nevertheless, storage can reduce or even avoid this overpro-
duction by increasing the buﬀer capacity and in such a way increase the solar fraction
of the system.
In order to calculate the overproduction of each plant an Excel model was created,
which is used to control the buﬀer situation throughout the year (see description in
section 6.3.3). Using the heat demand, solar thermal production and the characteris-
tics of the heat storage (total capacity, minimum required energy level and maximum
allowed temperature level) the hourly and yearly overproduction can be calculated.
6.3.2
PV-HP system
The PV-HP system should be able to stop powering the heat pumps when the tem-
perature in the storage reaches the maximum allowed level, avoiding in this way the
heat overproduction. Instead, there exists the possibility to sell the electricity to
the grid, so that economic beneﬁts increase. Therefore, it is important to know in
which moments the maximum temperature level is reached in the storage, in order to
calculate the amount of electricity that would be sold to the grid per hour.
Once again, an Excel model was created (see description in section 6.3.3). This
model works similarly to the model for the solar heating system but in this case the
heat production comes from the PV-HP system..

6.3.3
Excel model
An Excel model was created to know the hourly and yearly overproduction in the
solar heating system and the amount of hourly and yearly electricity that can be sold
to the grid in the PV-HP system. This will be used later in the economic calculations.
The model is used for the three plants, although in each case the diﬀerent storage
characteristics are applied. Moreover, the operation of the storage in Gr˚asten and
Sæby is slightly diﬀerent than the operation in Dronninglund. This is given by the
fact that the former plants use steel tanks as storage and all their heat generation
sources are connected to the buﬀer. On the other hand, Dronninglund is using a
seasonal pit storage and it is just ﬁlled with heat from the solar heating system.
Firstly, the model uses the previous calculations of the hourly heat demand and
the hourly heat production. They are used to know which is the heat surplus or heat
deﬁciency from the solar system in the plant, and in this way know if the production
can cover the total heat demand hourly. This is done for each hour of the year with
the following formula, which can be used for both the solar heating and the PV-HP
system:
Surplus or deficiency = Solar heat production −Total heat demand
(6.3)
If there is a surplus the energy is stored in the buﬀer:
Current energy level + Surplus = New energy level
(6.4)
but if there is a deﬁciency the buﬀer is used to supply this demand:
Current energy level −Deficiency = New energy level
(6.5)
Thus, the energy level in the storage is updated hourly in the model, being the last
value of one period the starting value of the following period.
The functioning of the model varies marginally when the energy level starts to
decrease. In Gr˚asten and Sæby, the storage can just reach a minimum allowed energy
level. This is given by each plant operator, and normally is used to avoid problems
in case that some heat source stops working due to technical problems. Then, when
the minimum level is reached and the buﬀer situation goes below the limit, the other
heat sources (gas turbines, biomass boilers, CHP, etc.) start supplying the needed
demand, and keep the buﬀer above the minimum energy level, being set by the model
as:
Minimum energy level = New energy level
(6.6)

==PAGE:76==
On the other hand, in Dronninglund the pit storage is not ﬁlled by other sources, so
temperatures of about 12oC are reached in winter, without having any limitation of
minimum required level. In winter months the pit storage is ﬁlled with heat from
the solar thermal panels just if they provide water at a higher temperature than the
bottom temperature of the storage. Meanwhile, the other sources are in charge of
supplying the whole heat demand. However, in summer the heat demand is supplied
mainly by the solar panels, introducing the heat surplus in the storage. Thus, the pit
storage is ﬁlled up in sunny months and emptied in cold months.
When the storage reaches the maximum temperature the model of the three plants
work in the same way, existing just a diﬀerence between the solar heatin plant and
the PV-HP plant:
• Solar heating plant →If at the end of the day (when it is possible to cool
down the water) the temperature level at the top of the storage is higher than
the maximum allowed temperature level, the energy excess is considered as
wasted and the energy level is set as the maximum energy level:
Maximum energy level = New energy level
(6.7)
• PV-HP plant →When there exists possibility of overproduction in the system,

value has to be taken into account when assessing the overproduction, but also the
parameters aﬀecting the solar fraction, for example the heat storage capacity. This
can be better understood realizing that Gr˚asten could reach a solar fraction of 28%
in two ways: by having a larger capacity which produces more heat in winter but
overproduces a lot in summer or by having a smaller capacity together with a larger

for winter months.
Finally, although Sæby has almost the same heat storage capacity as Gr˚asten, its
largest demand and smaller size lead to a low solar fraction of 7%. This makes very
diﬃcult the appearance of overproduction, at it is shown in the results. If there was

Besides using the Excel model to calculate the overproductions, the respective plant

the assumed heat production is larger than the measured production of 2014 means
a higher probability of having overproduction during summer, mainly in Gr˚asten,
which has a smaller heat storage capacity.

Chapter 7
Economic analysis
In this chapter, an economic comparison between the existing solar heating plants
and the PV-HP plants is carried out. This economic study allows us to know whether
the system combining photovoltaics and heat pumps can be economically competitive
and feasible against the solar collectors or not. Moreover, a sensitivity analysis on
the economic results is carried out to show which parameters are aﬀecting the most
the competitiveness of the PV-HP technology.
7.1
Theoretical introduction
In order to assess the attractiveness of a project, a feasibility study is needed. This
feasibility study will help us to analyze and evaluate a project in order to decide
whether the project should be undertaken or not. There are diﬀerent types of fea-
sibility studies: technical feasibility, regulatory feasibility, environmental feasibility
and economic feasibility. This section will focus on the last one, assuming that the
other feasibility studies have yielded positive results.
The economic feasibility is achieved if the project is expected to generate more
incomes than expenses during the lifetime. However, each project requires diﬀerent
conditions in order to be undertaken such as diﬀerent ranges of acceptable risks,
diﬀerent aﬀordable capital investments or diﬀerent time horizons until the project is
paid back [32].
For this study, a standard framework for evaluating investment projects will be
used: the Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) analysis. This method is based on assessing
the beneﬁts and costs over the lifetime, making them comparable by discounting.
For that, two important concepts must be taken into consideration: the diﬀerence
between real and nominal values and the time value of money (further explained in
section 7.1.1).
63
==PAGE:82==
In order to carry out this evaluation, some economic key indicators have been
chosen because of their relevance (further explained in section 7.1.2):
• Net Present Value (NPV)
• Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
• Payback Time (PBT)
• Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE)
Moreover, the diﬀerences between the two types of economic study that are going
to be carried out are presented later in this section: socio-economic and private
economic evaluation.
7.1.1
Real values, nominal values and time value of money
The diﬀerence between real and nominal values depends mainly on the inﬂation and
price changes throughout the lifetime. Real prices measure the real purchasing power
and do not take into account price changes due to inﬂation. Thus, if the cost of
a product is going to remain constant in the next few years (assuming the same
economic situation, so that you will be able to aﬀord the same amount as you can
aﬀord now), its real value will not change. However, the nominal value will change
with the inﬂation. For example, if the inﬂation is 2% per year, the nominal price of
the product will increase by 2% as well. In contrast to nominal values, real values
always use a base year from which inﬂation is removed. Normally, in ﬁnancial analysis
and project evaluation, the convention is that the cash ﬂows are shown in nominal
values. If a constant inﬂation rate is assumed, the next formula allows to calculate
the nominal value out of the real value:
Xnominal = Xreal · (1 + i)t
(7.1)
Where i is the inﬂation rate, t the number of years, Xnominal the nominal value

Time value of money refers to the fact that the value of money now is greater
than the expected value of the same amount of money in the future. The reason is
that you could invest that amount of money today and earn in the future a return on
that investment. This is important in our case because when investing in a project

comparable if time value of money is not taken into account.
There are two options to take into account the time value of money, either you use
future values or present values. In ﬁnancial analysis present values are normally used,
where today is used as basis for the comparison. This way is better especially when
comparing projects with diﬀerent lifetimes. Hence, all the future cash ﬂows have
to be discounted, receiving their ”present values”, i.e. discounted by the amount of
beneﬁts that you are not earning because you do not have the money today [33].

7.1.3
Socio-economic and Private economic analysis
The proﬁtability of a project always depends on the perspective from which it is
calculated, as well as the chosen assumptions. Because of that, in order to evaluate
the feasibility of the project for each alternative, two diﬀerent approaches are going
to be used: socio-economic and private economic.
The socio-economic analysis evaluates the project from a societal perspective,
studying whether it is beneﬁcial for society as a whole and trying to maximize social
welfare. This type of study can lead for example to politic decisions of which projects
should be undertaken or which technologies should be implemented and if they should
be supported by the government.
On the other hand, the private economic analysis evaluates from a private investor
or company perspective if the investment in a project is economically proﬁtable for the
company, maximizing the beneﬁts. This type of study will lead to company decisions
to invest or not in diﬀerent projects.
7.1.3.1
Socio-economic evaluation
The socio-economic analysis that is going to be used in this thesis is a simpliﬁed
version that will not deal with all aspects aﬀecting social welfare. A very accurate
socio-economic analysis (sometimes called cost-beneﬁt analysis) would include many
parameters that often are diﬃcult to measure in monetary terms. The socio-economic
analysis tries to embed the project in a social context, so the cash ﬂows are evaluated
as they ﬂow into and out of the society as a whole [36].
Figure 7.2 represents a
simpliﬁed sketch of the boundaries in a socio-economic evaluation. Note that salaries

In socio-economic evaluations it is not relevant to know which are the costs seen
by the market place but what it actually costs. Therefore, real and not nominal

7.1.4
Tax calculation
Taxes must be taken into consideration in the private economic analysis. In order to
calculate the free cash ﬂows after taxes, the operational costs, revenues and payable
taxes must be known. The payable taxes are paid on earnings, not on cash ﬂows.
For this thesis, a simpliﬁed set-up is used for the tax calculation, which can be better
understood with the diagram in ﬁgure 7.4:
Figure 7.4: Tax calculation diagram [35].
To calculate the Free Cash Flows three values must be determined: EBITDA,
Depreciation and Tax rate. EBITDA (Earnings Before Interests, Taxes, Deprecia-
tion and Amortization) are calculated as the revenues minus the operational costs.
Depreciation expresses how much of the total asset’s value has been lost in a given
moment of the project lifetime. Regarding taxes payment, companies can deduct
the depreciation of the installation as business expenses, and in that way reduce the
taxable income (tax shield). Nonetheless, for each year it is possible to deduct only
the depreciation corresponding to that year. For example, a plant with a 20 years
lifetime could deduct 5% of the initial investment each year. Speciﬁcally, in Denmark
a straight-line depreciation over the estimated lifetime is used, which means that a
constant depreciation during the lifetime is assumed [35].
After having calculated and applied the depreciation to the EBITDA, the result
is the EBIT (Earnings before interests and taxes). The multiplication of the EBIT
times the tax rate are the ﬁnal payable taxes.
With respect to the tax rate, it is given by each country. In Denmark, the tax
rate is being reduced from 24,5% in 2014, to 23,5% in 2015 and to 22% from 2016
onwards [40].

Firstly, ﬁgure 7.5 shows the NPV variation with changes in the COP of the heat
pumps. The blue curve represents how the assumption of the average COP can vary
the ﬁnal results. This assumption would change the capacity of the PV installation,
decreasing it when the COP is higher since less power would be needed to supply
the same amount of heat. In this way, investment costs and O&M costs would be
reduced, and the NPV would increase. Obviously, the economic beneﬁts are reduced
if the COP of the heat pumps is lower and the system needs more PV panels to
produce more power. As it can be observed, a change in the COP for example from
2 to 2.4 aﬀects more the NPV than a change from 6 from 6.4, since the curve is
less steep as the COP increases. This is due to the fact that with high COPs, the
additional PV capacity needed when increasing the COP by 10% is smaller.

==PAGE:99==
Figure 7.5: Sensitivity analysis on the NPV with changes in the COP of the heat
pumps.
In the same ﬁgure it is also represented how aﬀects the NPV a variation in the
COP of the heat pumps during operation, once the system has been designed. This
could happen if the temperatures of the heat pump cycle vary along the year, al-
though in the design it was assumed that the temperature would be constant, and
therefore also the COP. The curve shows that COPs from 2.8 until 6.8 there exists
an almost linear correlation between this increase and the increase in beneﬁts. This
is because when increasing the COP, the heat production increases in a linear way.
However, the overproduction increases more with higher COPs, which means that
more electricity can be sold to the grid, but that amount times the COP will not
be sold as heat. Because of that, there is not a complete straight line representing
the values. Moreover, from 2 to 2.8, the curve is slightly steeper than the rest of
the curve. The reason is that with COPs lower than 2.8% the system does not have
overproduction, so the decrease in heat production is the same, but no revenue is
received from the surplus sold electricity, meaning a larger variation in the NPV. The
break-even point is reached at a COP of 6.8 during operation.
Figure 7.6 represents how sensitive is the NPV to variations in the lifetime and
the discount rate.
Regarding the lifetime, the graph depicts that an increase in
the lifetime (technical and economic) means an increment in the ﬁnal beneﬁts, as it
could be expected. This variation does not follow a straight line because the yearly
beneﬁts are discounted in the economic model, which means that in longer lifetimes
the beneﬁts of additional years are reduced.
81
==PAGE:100==
Figure 7.6: Sensitivity analysis on the NPV with changes in lifetime and discount
rate.
Besides, the change in the discount rate input is also studied in ﬁgure 7.6. Ev-
idently, with higher discount rates the NPV decreases, since cash ﬂows are more
discounted yearly. Furthermore, the higher the discount rate, the lower the eﬀect
in the NPV. The reason is that the discount factor is more aﬀected by low discount
rates than by high discount rates. This can be better understood with the formula
of the discount factor (equation 7.16). For example, for the year 5, the diﬀerence in
the discount factor when changing from a discount rate of 4% to 5% is 0.054, whilst
for the same year the diﬀerence of changing from 8% to 9% is 0.025. This makes the
curve less steeper with higher discount rates. In order to reach feasibility in the pri-
vate economic evaluation, the PV-HP system should be assessed using a break-even
discount rate of 4.2%. With lower discount rates the project would result feasible,
and with higher discount rates the project would result unfeasible. Note that discount
rates of 4.2% would be closer to values of socio-economic evaluations. Moreover, the
private discount rate would be similar for a company no matter which of the two
technologies is being evaluated, so this parameter would not make the PV-HP more
competitive against the solar heating system.
Figure 7.7 focuses on the plant costs. Both investment and O&M costs increments
produce a linear decrement in the economic beneﬁts. As the graph depicts, investment
costs aﬀect the most the ﬁnal NPV, with a very little variation from O&M costs.
Moreover, changes in HP costs mean a bigger diﬀerence in the NPV than changes
in PV costs, since the design output capacity of the HP subsystem is larger than

the design capacity of the PV panels, meaning a higher price for the project. Project
proﬁtability could only be achieved with reductions in the CAPEX of the installation.
A reduction of 52% in HP investment costs would be necessary to reach proﬁtability,
while a decrease of 31% would be necessary in total investment costs.
Figure 7.7: Sensitivity analysis on the NPV with changes in investment costs and
O&M costs.
Figure 7.8 shows the eﬀect of energy prices on the plant feasibility.
Perhaps
contrary to expectations, variations in the power spot price and the support scheme
for sold electricity barely aﬀect the NPV. The small amount of electricity sold to
the grid in comparison with the heat sales, makes the variation of the power price
insigniﬁcant in comparison with the heat price.
The heat price clearly aﬀects the revenues of the plant. Taking into considera-
tion that heat prices vary hugely between district heating plants in Denmark, this
parameter is varied from -50% to +100% of the current value. Gr˚asten plant’s heat
price is currently 44 e per MWh, which results in a negative NPV of -1,805,947 e.
The break-even heat price is 62 e/MWh, from where the plant would reach prof-
itability. Furthermore, if Gr˚asten had a heat price equal to the average Danish value
(64 e /MWh [47]), the NPV would be 230,694 e , resulting then the PV-HP plant fea-
sible. Undoubtedly, and using as an argument ﬁgure 7.8 it makes sense to state that
the heat price is one of the parameters that can aﬀect the most the NPV. Besides, due
to the diﬀerences with Dronninglund’s and Sæby’s heat prices, it has been the key
parameter when assessing the economic diﬀerences between the three studied cases.
However, it is worth to notice that an increase of the heat price is also increasing

Chapter 8
Conclusions and recommendations
for further work
This thesis has investigated the possibility of using photovoltaics together with heat
pumps in Danish district heating plants. An economic study has been carried out in
order to assess the feasibility of replacing some current solar heating plants by the
PV-HP plants. The analyses showed that for all the cases studied, solar heating plants
are more economically viable, so the PV-HP plants are not currently competitive.
In order to investigate this, a literature review has been carried out, followed by a
chapter on the state of the art, where heat storage has been demostrated to be a crucial
technology when using solar energy to produce heat. PV panels in combination with
heat pumps have been found to be a good choice for heating purposes, since besides
powering the heat pumps electricity surplus can be sold to the grid. With regard
to heat pumps, future higher COPs and reductions in investment costs create an
optimistic future for this technology.
Three representative Danish solar heating plants have been selected for this study
based on their solar fraction: Sæby (7%), Gr˚asten (28%) and Dronninlund (50%).
Then, three PV-HP systems have been designed to replace them, assuming that they
will produce the same amount of heat as the annual estimated heat production of the
selected solar heating plants. In the new system, the PV panels will power NH3 heat
pumps operating with an assumed constant COP of 4.
Afterwards, an Excel model studying each heat storage situation throughout the
year has been created, based on their heat demand and heat production. It has been
found that Sæby does not have overproduction for any of the systems but Dron-
ninglund and Gr˚asten overproduce in summer months for the solar thermal plant,
being able the PV-HP plants to sell electricity surplus. With these results, it has
85
==PAGE:104==
been concluded that the solar fraction, inﬂuenced by the demand, plant size and heat
storage capacity is aﬀecting the overproduction of the plant.
In the last chapter, the economic study has shown that both Dronninglund and
Sæby have obtained proﬁtability for both the solar thermal system and the PV-
HP system. However, Gr˚asten has just obtained proﬁtability for the solar heating
system since the PV-HP system has been proven to be unfeasible. These negative
results have been mainly given by the low heat price of Gr˚asten, which is around
30% lower than the heat price in the other plants. This highlights the importance
of the location, which can largely vary the economic beneﬁts.
Note that for the
three studied cases, economic results have been better for the solar heating plant.
Finally, a sensitivity analysis on the economic results (NPV) of Gr˚asten’s PV-HP
system has been carried out, varying several key inputs in the private economic model.
The parameters aﬀecting the most the economic results have been: operation COP,
discount rate, investment costs (mostly inﬂuenced by heat pumps) and heat price.
Nevertheless, when considering the changes of these parameters also in the solar
heating plant, the COP and the investment costs become the main way for the PV-
HP plant to become competitive. The reason is that an increase in the heat price or
the discount rate would aﬀect both the feasibility of the solar thermal plant and the
PV-HP plant at a similar level.
In conclusion the PV-HP system is not competitive currently against solar collec-
tors in Denmark, since the latest is the cheapest option to generate heat in district
heating plants. Some changes would be needed to reach the same proﬁtability as
solar collectors. These changes could come mainly from the operation COP of the
heat pumps or the investment costs of the new system. Moreover, new governmen-
tal measures as new subsidies or tax reductions for heat pumps would enhance the
competitiveness of the PV-HP system as well. In order to achieve these ﬁnancial sup-
ports, it could be considered the possibility of using the electricity surplus generated
by renewable sources to power the heat pumps, which would beneﬁt the power sys-
tem adding an extra ﬂexibility. Hence, depending on the heat storage size, the system
would be able to generate heat and store it in summer when the solar radiation in
the PV panels is high but also in days with a lot of wind, when electricity surplus in
the power system could be used to power the heat pumps as well.

8.1
Recommendations for further work
Here are presented some recommendations for future research on the topic of the
present thesis:
- Instead of PV-HP systems, PV/T hybrid panels could be used. With this tech-
nology, solar cells generate electricity and the remaining waste heat of the PV panels
is used by the solar thermal collector to heat up water. In this way, the waste heat is
recovered and the eﬃciency of the PV panels increase (the eﬃciency of a PV panel
increase when reducing its temperature), so that the overall eﬃciency of the system
increases. Note that the thermal component underperforms in comparison with a
normal solar thermal collector.
- Regarding the selection of the three studied cases, a deeper study of all the
variables could be done to ﬁnd more accurately the three most representative solar
heating plants. This would require a further study of the operating variables and
design characteristics of all the Danish solar heating plants, in order to be able to
ﬁnd the plants that make more meaningful the economic study. Another option could
be to study more than three heating plants.
- In this study, solar radiation data comes from diﬀerent periods of time and
diﬀerent sources. In order to reach more accurate results for the calculations of the
overproductions, the same solar radiation data extracted from the same source could
be used.
- Since the COP of the heat pumps is a crucial parameter for the economic results
of the project, a deeper study of the heat sources and the possible performance of the
heat pump system could be carried out. Moreover, hourly heat source’s temperature
variations can be taken into consideration in the model, in order to calculate more
precise values of heat production and overproduction.
- Real heat demand proﬁles for each plant could be used in a further study. In this
thesis, a generalized heat demand proﬁle based on Danish HDD is used, regardless
of the diﬀerences between the three plants. This diﬀerence can deﬁnitely aﬀect the
results depending on the type of consumers connected to the heating plant.
- In this study, the solar system is isolated from the rest of the heating plant when
carrying out the economic evaluation. In a further research, the other heat generation
sources could be included in the calculations, taking into consideration how they are
aﬀected by the operation of the solar collectors or the PV-HP system.
- Due to the increasing share of renewable energies in the Danish power system, a
diﬀerent approach of the heat pump installation could be studied. Using electricity
87
==PAGE:106==
surplus generated by renewable sources (wind, solar, etc.) to run the heat pumps can
be a way to produce heat with lower costs and at the same time beneﬁt the power
system by adding ﬂexibility. Moreover, possible incentives or tax reductions could be
taken into consideration, given the beneﬁcial eﬀect that the installation could have
on the power system.
