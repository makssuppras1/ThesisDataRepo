Abstract
In this project, seventeen strains of newly isolated fructophilic lactic acid bacteria (FLAB) were investigated for their
application in generating sugar-free clean-label plant-based dairy alternatives (PBDA). The strains were provided by
the National Food Institute’s Culture Collection (NFICC).
An initial screening in the newly developed MLS media selective for lactic acid bacteria (LAB) found the 10 best
performing strains based on mannitol production and substrate depletion, as well as growth on different carbohydrate
sources. A mapping of the fermentables produced by Fructobacillus tropaeoli (S1), was mapped over 10 hours of
fermentation in MLS, which highlighted the ability of FLAB to use fructose solely as an external electron acceptor,
reaching a yield of 98% mannitol from fructose.
Three different blends made of potato protein, oat ﬂour and pea protein (POP) were used as the base for generating
a PBDA similar to traditional yoghurt. Acidiﬁcation potential of the ten FLAB strains was tested on eleven combi-
nations of carbohydrates in the POP blends, yielding the best media compositions, along with the four best strains.
The four strains were all Fructobacillus spp. and the best medias combined either an enzymatic pre-treatment with
amylases with a subsequent addition of fructose syrup or glucose and fructose syrup added directly to the blend.
Inhibition trials were conducted to test the ability of FLAB to inhibit growth of the naturally occurring food pathogen
Bacillus cereus. The trials showed that the tested FLAB did not produce any apparent bacteriocins, however a rapid
acidiﬁcation reaching pH<4.5 within 8 hours was shown to serve as an efﬁcient method of inhibition.
The acidiﬁcation potential of the four Fructobacillus spp. was monitored directly by continuous pH measurements
over 24 hours in the three POP blends treated with amylase and fructose syrup. This media composition gave good
conditions for growth and acidiﬁcation, however the enzymatic treatment was found to destory the structure of the POP
blend. This approach was therefore scrapped, and the project continued with POP blends with glucose and fructose
syrup instead.
Two of the four Fructobacillus spp. (S1 and S3) were found to perform the best, and their mannitol production was
determined in the POP blend, reaching a yield of 70% mannitol produced from the initial concentration of fructose.
The rheological effect of fermenting POP with FLAB was quantiﬁed with these two strains. The experiment showed
no apparent exopolysaccharide (EPS) production in the FLAB, and the fermented POP blend showed shear-thinning
behavior similar to traditional dairy yoghurt, however at a lower total viscosity.
Combinatorial microbiology was applied to improve acidiﬁcation rate, off-ﬂavour removal and texture development.
Eleven LAB strains of Lactobacillus plantarum spp., Leuconostoc mesenteroides spp. and Leuconostoc pseudome-
senteroides spp. were tested in an inhibition trial with FLAB. Two strains (LM199 (L. mesenteroides), and LP323 (L.
pseudomesenteroides)) were chosen for co-culturing with the two best performing FLAB strains. Overall the results
showed antagonistic microbial interactions between the strains, where LP323 dominated the co-cultures with FLAB.
The co-culture trials highlighted, that both LM199 and LP323 showed phenotypes similar to FLAB, with high pro-
duction of mannitol and no production of ethanol in the presence of glucose and fructose.
Untargeted GC-MS was used to investigate off-ﬂavour removal and aroma synthesis of FLAB, with and without co-
culturing. The bacteria were able to reduce typical off-ﬂavour compounds found in potato, oat and pea, below their
aroma thresholds, including pentanal and heptanal. Aroma synthesis of diacetyl was limited in FLAB, while LP323
produced substantial amounts of diacetyl as a mono-culture.
Overall the project has highlighted and mapped key phenotypical characteristics in FLAB. The best performing strains
were successfully applied in a PBDA matrix, showing promising results for their future application in the food industry.

Introduction
In the face of the escalating climate crisis, the urgency to address the detrimental impacts of human activities has never
been more pronounced. Among the many sources of pollution, the food industry stands out as a major contributor to
climate change(Lynch et al. 2021). In addition, the demands on the food industry as a whole are increasing. The global
population is expected to increase to 10 billion by 2050, meanwhile the middle class is growing heavily in most devel-
oping countries(The Eat-Lancet Commission 2019). These are all factors, that force food engineers to change the food
industry from within. Shifting towards a plant-based diet has the potential to produce more food for an increas-
ing population, while reducing the climate impact(https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2023/jul/
20/vegan-diet-cuts-environmental-damage-climate-heating-emissions-study). Changing the aver-
age consumer’s perception on plant-based products has shown to be an additional challenge to overcome. Plant based
products still struggle to compete on the market, often due to issues revolved around a diminished quality of the ﬁnal
product (odour, texture and taste). Lower nutritional value compared to similar dairy or meat products and a higher
price serves as additional obstacles for consumers(Tangyu et al. 2019).
The need to develop clean-label plant-based products with a high quality, at an affordable price, is therefore essential
in achieving a successful transition of the food system. The interest in plant-based dairy alternatives (PBDA) is in-
creasing these years, and customers look to these products for a number of reasons, including sustainability, health,
lifestyle and dietary reasons(Carlsson Kanyama, Hedin, and Katzeff 2021).
Problems revolving around product quality of PBDA can be resolved through fermentation with lactic acid bacteria
(LAB), achieving a safe and high quality ﬁnal product. The use of fermentation with LAB has been seen historically
in countless food products. However, fermenting in a plant based media, as opposed to dairy, presents an issue for
the traditional dairy cultures. The need to broaden and diversify the LAB species used for plant-based fermentation is
therefore essential.
Fructophilic lactic acid bacteria (FLAB) are a newly discovered group of LAB, which has been isolated from fructose-
rich niches(Endo et al. 2018). The aim for this project is to investigate the potential of FLAB in the generation of
sugar-free clean-label PBDA(Sed´o Molina, Shetty, Xiao, et al. 2022). FLAB have the ability to produce high concen-
trations of mannitol, a sugar alcohol with a low caloric value, from fructose. This serves as a key feature, which can
be utilized to develop a sweet sugar-free PBDA(Endo 2012).
In this project we screened and characterized 17 strains of FLAB from the National Food Institute Culture Collection
(NFICC) for their application in a blend of potato protein, oat ﬂour and pea protein from yellow split peas. Factors
such as acidiﬁcation potential, fermentation metabolites, mannitol yield, effect on texture, formation of aroma com-
pounds and off-ﬂavour removal have been considered when characterizing the different strains.
As FLAB are not QPS (Qualiﬁed Presumption of Safety) approved yet, the results of this project should be seen as a
preliminary study to highlight the potential of these bacteria in the food industry, which hopefully can lead to a QPS
approval eventually.

Background theory
3.1
Lactic acid bacteria
Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) is a group of gram-positive microaerophillic, non-sporing bacteria, whose main fermenta-
tion product is lactic acid(Konings et al. 2000). LAB comprise of both cocci (Streptococcus, Pediococcus, Leuconos-
toc) and rods (Lactobacillus and Biﬁdobacterium)(Kandler 1983). LAB are found in a huge variety of environments
and are some of the most extensively studied microorganisms(Sed´o Molina, Shetty, Xiao, et al. 2022). These include
fermented foods, such as dairy products, but also plant surfaces, soils and the gastrointestinal tract and oral cavities
of both animals and humans. These many different environments all have unique compositions, which also means
LAB have been forced to adapt to their speciﬁc characteristics, in order to thrive in their speciﬁc niches(Endo et al.
2018)(Andersen, Pedersen, and Bang-Berthelsen 2019).
The evolutionary changes of LAB has generated enormous variations between species which are highly specialized in
thriving in speciﬁc niches. LAB which evolved into nutrient-rich niches lost genes for biosynthetic pathways, while
degradative pathways where acquired or up-regulated(Matthews, Kniel, and Montville 2017).
LAB genomes have a typical range of 1.8 to 3.4 Mbp (a Mbp is 106 base pairs) which contains about 2000-3000
protein encoding genes. The large genome size interval is indicative of LAB evolution, where gene loss, duplication
and gene acquisition is common(Konings et al. 2000). LAB genes can be located either in introns on chromosomes
or located on plasmids. Plasmids are extrachromosomal circular DNA molecules that replicate independently, and
can therefore be lost, if the replication is not linked up with the bacterial division. This can be problematic if the lost
plasmid contains DNA encoding for vital cell functions(Matthews, Kniel, and Montville 2017).
LAB are made up of both hetero- and homofermentative species. Homofermentative LAB produce lactic acid as
the main product from fermentation of glucose, while hetrofermentative LAB produce a range of products including
carbon dioxide, lactic acid, ethanol and acetic acid(Altieri et al. 2017). Homofermentative LAB use glycolysis for
glucose metabolism, which does not include the ethanol production system(Endo et al. 2018).
3.1.1
Carbohydrate metabolism in lactic acid bacteria
LAB use three different major pathways for hexose fermentation, which all have in common that they only use hexose
phosphates with gluco-conﬁguration. The difference comes in the way they split the carbon backbone yielding differ-
ent fermentation products(Kandler 1983).
Glycolysis is characterized by splitting fructose-1,6-bisphosphate using an aldolase into two triose phosphates, which
is then ﬁnally converted to pyruvate generating two molecules of ATP. The ﬁnal step involves pyruvate being con-
verted into lactate, yielding two molecules of lactic acid from one glucose molecule. Hence LAB using glycolysis
does homo-lactic fermentation(H. S. A and I. S. A 2013). Glycolysis is common for Streptoccus spp., Pediococcus
spp. and homofermentative lactobacillus spp.(Kandler 1983).
The Biﬁdus pathway and 6P-Gluconate pathway make up the phophoketolase pathways and are both heterofermen-
tative(Mozzi 2015). 6P-Gluconate pathway is initiated by oxidizing glucose 6-phosphate to gluconate 6-phosphate
followed be a decarboxylation releasing CO2. The resulting pentose 5-phosphate is then split into a C-2 and C-3 moi-
ety. The C-3 moiety is converted to lactate similarly to glycolysis yielding 1 ATP, while the C-2 moiety is converted
to acetate or ethanol(Kandler 1983). Overall it means equimolar amounts of CO2, lactate and acetate or ethanol are
produced in LAB using the 6P-Gluconate pathway for hexose fermentation(H. S. A and I. S. A 2013).
2
==PAGE:15==
The Biﬁdus pathway is initiated by splitting fructose 6-phosphate by the phosphoketolase into a C-4 moiety and a
C-2 moiety. The C-2 moiety, acetyl-phosphate is converted to acetate yielding 1 ATP, while heptose-7-phosphate is
formed by the action of a transketolase, adding another C-3 moietyderived from another fructose 6-phosphate to the
C4-moiety. First the splitting of heptose 7-phosphate occurs, which is followed by the splitting of the resulting pentose
5-phosphate. This end with the formation of lactate and acetate at a molar ratio of 3:2(Kandler 1983).
Pentoses can be fermented by most heterofermentative LAB. Speciﬁc permeases take up pentoses, which are then con-
verted by speciﬁc enzymes to D-xylulose 5-phosphate. These are to be incorporated in the last part of the 6P-Gluconate
pathway yielding lactacte and acetate or ethanol along with 2 ATP. The required enzymes for pentose fermentation are
inducible by their corresponding pentose, as opposed to the constitutive enzymes used in hexose fermentation(Kandler
1983).
3.1.2
Exopolysaccharide production
Exopolysaccharides (EPS) are produced by certain LAB and are interesting in the food industry, as these compounds
are considered bio-thickeners. They are added to products to improve characteristics including viscosity, stability,
emulsiﬁcation and to act as gelling agents(Altieri et al. 2017). Production of EPS has also been connected to improved
gut health and microbiome in humans(Jur´aˇskov´a, Ribeiro, and Silva 2022). The texture of yoghurts has been shown to
improve with bacterial EPS by thickening and stabilizing the product. Bio-thickening by EPS is of huge importance
for producers of PBDA, as manufacturers of these products struggles with a good texture(Pua et al. 2022). EPS have
previously been shown to improve the texture and mouthfeel of PBDA(Jur´aˇskov´a, Ribeiro, and Silva 2022).
EPS are made up of monosaccharides linked together by glucosidic bonds forming big molecules with a high molecular
weight(Poulsen, Moghadam, et al. 2016). These polysaccharides play an important role for the bacteria by protecting
the cells from harsh environments as well as being a main component of bioﬁlm formation, which can help the bacteria
to stick to surfaces(Nguyen et al. 2020).
Bacterial EPS can be divided into two groups, homopolysaccharides and heteropolysaccharides. Homopolysaccha-
rides are made up of chains of the same monosaccharide, while heteropolysaccharides are made up of two or more
different monosaccharides(Jur´aˇskov´a, Ribeiro, and Silva 2022). These polysaccharides vary greatly in structure and
size and can be both branched or unbranched, and charged or uncharged(Poulsen, Moghadam, et al. 2016).
Homopolysaccharides can be produced by certain LAB extracellularly with extracellular enzymes. These EPS are
made of D-glucose, D-fructose or D-galactose, and are classiﬁed as glucans, fructans and galactans(Nguyen et al.
2020). Homopolysaccharides are synthesized by one enzyme which is encoded by one gene, while heteropolysaccha-
rides are genetically more adverse. The genetic sequence for these compounds typically include several glycosyltrans-
ferases, polymerization proteins and regulatory proteins(Jur´aˇskov´a, Ribeiro, and Silva 2022).
Stress factors have shown to increase production of EPS, including nutrient stress, where both starvation and excess of
nutrients, such as sugars and nitrogen can improve EPS production. The hypothesis behind increased EPS production
in excess of sugars, revolves around osmotic stress and high energy availability from the excessive sugar reserves.
Another stress factor is physical stress caused by temperature, pH and osmosis. Raised temperatures have shown to
increase EPS production in LAB, as a means to deal with protein denaturation and cell membrane damage. High
osmotic pressure from salt, as well as sugars, have similarly shown an increase in EPS production, while lower pH
also shows a tendency of increased production of EPS in multiple LAB strains(Nguyen et al. 2020).

3.1.3
Microbial growth kinetics
Microbial growth in batch conditions typically follow the same 5 phases of speciﬁc growth phases. Just after inocula-
tion the lag phase begins. This phase is characterized by the bacteria not showing particular growth or pH reduction.
However, a rapid adaptation to the new environment occurs. This phase include events such as up-regulation of nu-
cleotide metabolism, repair of damaged cells and RNA transcription in preparation for protein and enzyme production
involved in energy metabolism. The cell prepares all cellular components and adapts to the new environment, in order
to allow rapid growth to initiate(Rolfe et al. 2012).
The accelerating phase is initiated following the lag phase. Here the growthrate of the bacteria starts to pick up and
is continuously increasing until it reaches the maximum growthrate (µmax). Once this point is reached the exponen-
tial growth phase is initiated. The growth characteristics of this phase can be described mathematically, as shown in
formula 3.1 below, where C(t) is the concentration of cells at time t based on the initial concentration C0 prior to the
exponential phase, and the species and media speciﬁc maximum growthrate(Stanbury, Whitaker, and Hall 2017b).
C(t) = C0 ·eµ·t
(3.1)
In order to determine the window of exponential growth, it is easier to transform the concentration data with the
natural logarithm, which yields a linear relation between the growth of cells and µmax. The transformation can be
seen in formula 3.2(Molina, Shetty, and Bang-Berthelsen 2023). This way of transforming the data, makes it easy to
systematically determine the maximum growthrate on real data(Stanbury, Whitaker, and Hall 2017b).
ln(C(t)) = ln(C0 ·eµ·t) , ln(C(t)) = ln(C0)+ µ ·t
(3.2)
The exponential phase will stop eventually, which is usually caused by one of multiple potential factors. Substrate
depletion is a common reason for growth arrest, as well as build up of toxic compounds. Another reason can be
chemical or physical stress, which includes factors such as pH, temperature and osmolarity(Jaishankar and Srivastava
2017). This leads to the stationary phase, where production of new biomass is stopped. It has however been found, that
bacteria in the stationary phase can upkeep a constant production of proteins over several days, in order to maintain
vital cell functions and prolong the vitatily of cells under starvation(Gefen et al. 2014). The last phase is the death
phase, where autolysis of cells begins due to prolonged lack of nutrients or improper living conditions (temperature,
pH, toxic compounds)(Prado Barrag´an et al. 2016).
3.2
Fructophilic lactic acid bacteria
Fructophilic lactic acid bacteria (FLAB) are a newly discovered group of heterofermentative LAB(Molina, Shetty,
and Bang-Berthelsen 2023). They have typically been isolated from fructose-rich niches, such as ﬂowers, fruits and
guts of bees(Sed´o Molina, Shetty, Xiao, et al. 2022). FLAB are most easily differentiated from other LAB by a few
biochemical differences. Generally, FLAB grow very poorly on glucose as the sole carbon source, while they prefer
fructose as their optimal substrate for growth(Filannino, Cagno, et al. 2016). This feature of FLAB is due to a partial
mutation of the alcohol/acetaldehyde dehydrogenase gene (adhE), which alters the activity of the corresponding en-
zyme(Filannino, Di Cagno, Tlais, et al. 2019). The most common species of FLAB is Apilactobacillus kunkeei, while
another known FLAB species include Fructobacillus tropaeoli(Sed´o Molina, Shetty, Xiao, et al. 2022).
4
==PAGE:17==
FLAB can be subdivided into two main groups; obligate FLAB and facultative FLAB(Endo et al. 2018)). Obligate
FLAB grow very poorly on glucose as the sole carbon source. However, this can be stimulated positively by adding an
external electron acceptor, typically in the form of pyruvate or fructose. Another way of stimulating growth is having
aerobic fermentation conditions, which means oxygen would be present as an electron acceptor. However, oxygen is
only used as an external electron acceptor in the presence of a NADH oxidase(Endo et al. 2018)(Molina, Shetty, and
Bang-Berthelsen 2023). Obligate FLAB are differentiated from other obligate LAB by their production of acetate,
instead of ethanol. The lack of ethanol production makes FLAB interesting in PBDA product development.
Facultative FLAB grow well on fructose, as well as glucose in the presence of fructose, similar to obligate FLAB.
One difference is that facultative FLAB are able to grow on glucose without any external electron acceptors, however
at a delayed rate, compared to growth on either fructose or glucose supplied with an external electron acceptor(Endo
et al. 2018). FLAB have been found to modify alkaloids and phenolic acids to enhance both nutritional and func-
tional attributes of plant-based fermentations(Filannino, Di Cagno, Tlais, et al. 2019). These features, along with
their ability to produce high amounts of mannitol make them interesting in developing new low calorie plant based
products(Molina, Shetty, and Bang-Berthelsen 2023).
3.2.1
Common identiﬁcation methods for lactic acid bacteria
Throughout the years numerous ways of isolating and identifying LAB has been used. The most common experimental
practice is culturing environmental samples in either a liquid media or on agar plates. Selective synthetic medias, such
as De Man, Rogosa and Sharpe (MRS) broth, M17 and Yeast-Glucose Phosphate (YGP) are among some of the most
commonly used for isolation of LAB species(Filannino, Di Cagno, Tlais, et al. 2019). These common selective medias
have however been found to exclude certain species of LAB(Xiao et al. 2024). For this study the newly developed
MLS media developed by Xiao et al., has been deployed for isolation. The media will be introduced in section 4.1.
One of the major problems with these traditional methods, is that only the most abundant and fastest growing strains
are isolated. New isolation methods are developed to reduce the competitiveness factor, and allow a wider range of
species to be isolated(Lewis et al. 2021). Droplet-based microﬂuidics is one of these new promising technologies for
species isolation. The method is able to generate micro droplets, which can act as microbiological growth chambers.
Single cells can be injected into these droplets, and the droplets are kept stable by speciﬁc surfactants. This makes
it possible to sort and screen for strains, that would normally be out-competed or excluded with more traditional
methods(Chen et al. 2018).
Identiﬁcation of the isolated species is typically based on either DNA sequencing or molecular ampliﬁcation based
on PCR(Sharma, S. Lee, and Park 2020). However, another approach is through mass spectrometry such as LC-MS
(liquid chromatography mass spectrometry) and MALDI-TOF (matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization time-of-
ﬂight). MALDI-TOF serves as a rapid method of identifying already known species based on their respective peptide
matrix ﬁngerprint. The identiﬁcation compares the concentrations of different sized peptides of each species to a
known library of species. This allows for a fast and cost effective identiﬁcation(Singhal et al. 2015).

3.2.2
Biodiversity and strain collections
Only about 1% of bacteria on Earth have been cultivated in vitro, which highlights the enormous microbial biodiver-
sity, that is still unknown and could have huge potential beneﬁts for numerous applications(Vartoukian, Palmer, and
Wade 2010). Development of new isolation technologies has allowed researchers to discover and identify previously
unknown species(Chen et al. 2018). The potential to adopt microbiology as a multi-purposed tool in the development
of new products, within the food industry has been proven numerous times, and the need to develop new clean-label
products for the future only enhances the need for a broad availability of bacteria with speciﬁc phenotypical activ-
ities(Gilbert et al. 2018). Cell banks such as the NFICC are instrumental in allowing researchers to solve issues in
product development using microorganisms.
3.2.3
Mannitol synthesis in fructophilic lactic acid bacteria
Mannitol is known for its wide use in both the food, pharmaceutical and chemical industries. It is a 6-carbon polyol
and a natural sweetener(Xiao 2021). In the food industry, mannitol is used due to its numerous positive effects
on food. It is a sweetening agent with a low glucemic index, while also reducing the crystallization of sugars to
prolong shelf-life(Saha and Racine 2011). Mannitol is naturally produced in a number of both heterofermentative and
homofermentative LAB. Especially some heterofermentative LAB (FLAB included) have been shown to be excellent
producers of mannitol by fermentation(Saha and Racine 2011). FLAB synthesize mannitol by using fructose as an
external electron acceptor in glucose metabolism in the absence of oxygen. This suggest that FLAB are able to both
use fructose as a substrate and an electron acceptor(Endo et al. 2018)(Molina, Shetty, and Bang-Berthelsen 2023).

3.3
Fermentations in plant based foods
Fermentation of plant based food dates back to ancient times, and has always been a way for people to retain or
increase the nutritional value of food(Sethi, Tyagi, and Anurag 2016). The need to develop new food products for
an increasing population means research in development of new plant based food products is gaining an increasing
interest(Huang et al. 2022).
Development of heat sterilization and cold-chain refrigeration systems, has made food safety through bio-preservation
less important in most industrialized areas of the world(Coulomb 2008). However, bio-preservation through fer-
mentation is still vital for retaining food safety in most developing countries(Lianou, Panagou, and Nychas 2016).
Furthermore, fermented food products carry a huge cultural weight and can also be more nutritional than fresh foods.
Sought after properties, such as carbonation, nutrient availability and improved texture can also be achieved(Matthews,
Kniel, and Montville 2017).
LAB and yeasts are some of the most common microorganisms used for preserving foods. Using conventional dairy
LAB on plant based matrices, has however proven to produce sub-optimal results. This could be due to metabolic
adaptation to the carbohydrate sources found in dairy, at the cost of the ability to utilize carbohydrates commonly
found in plant based foods(Madsen et al. 2021)(Andersen, Pedersen, and Bang-Berthelsen 2019). By using strains
isolated from plants or strains adapted to grow in plant based matrices, it is more likely that the bacteria are adapted to
deal with the vastly different protein and carbohydrate sources(Ruiz Rodr´ıguez et al. 2019). Optimizing the fermen-
tation of PBDA has been proven to improve both the nutritional content and sensory proﬁle of food products(Tangyu
et al. 2019).
3.3.1
Development of new products to ﬁt consumer trends
The food industry of the future is inﬂuenced by a huge variety of mega consumer trends. Consumers worldwide are
becoming more conscious about what they eat, where it originates from, how it is produced, the sustainability of the
product and its carbon footprint(Aschemann-Witzel et al. 2020). Some of the most important mega trends, which are
relevant for this project include natural and clean label, plant based food and texture and ﬂavour(Tiefenbacher 2019).
The development of new PBDA with a higher quality and a clean label ticks most of these demands, and has a huge
potential to push more consumers on to a plant-based diet(Sethi, Tyagi, and Anurag 2016). Plant based milks already
has a huge market pull in many countries, which shows that PBDA have a big potential(Huang et al. 2022).
Developing new fermented food products requires numerous approvals. In the EU, the European Food Safety Author-
ities (EFSA) approves microorganisms to be used for food production by a system called Qualiﬁed Presumption of
Safety (QPS) (https://www.efsa.europa.eu/da/topics/topic/qualified-presumption-safety-qps)(Ru
7
==PAGE:20==
et al. 2020)(Koutsoumanis et al. 2020). This system is similar to the American version GRAS (Generally Recognized
As Safe), which is used by United States Food and Drug Administration (USFDA) (https://www.fda.gov/food/
food-ingredients-packaging). The process of getting a microorganism QPS approved involves an extensive lit-
erature search related to safety concerns for both humans, animals, microorganisms and the environment. The aim of
the research is to identify any health or safety concerns revolving the microorganism up for approval. Moreover, a
microorganism that is up for QPS approval needs to have its species speciﬁed on the EFSA QPS recommended list(Ru

of microorganisms, that has to be considered when approving new species as QPS includes (but are not limited to);
origin, non-pathogenecity, metabolic activities, production of toxins and potential side-effects in humans(Altieri et al.
2017).
3.3.2
Novel foods
Novel foods include newly developed food products, which are not commonly consumed within the EU. Since January
1st 2018 a new EU regulation (Reg. (EU) 2015/2283) has been introduced, in order to provide better opportunities for
food producing companies to introduce new food products, while maintaining a high level of food safety(Tiefenbacher
2019). According to EFSA’s homepage the new regulation has eight key features and improvements. These all aim to
bring transparency to the customer and food producer, while expanding the novel food category to allow more innova-
tion and new products into the market (https://food.ec.europa.eu/safety/novel-food/legislation_en).
A new novel food applicant will ﬁrst have to go through a scientiﬁc risk assessment made by the European Food Safety
Authority (EFSA). This includes having a food product with a balanced proportion of carbohydrates, fats, proteins,
vitamins, minerals, trace elements and ﬁbers(Tiefenbacher 2019). Regulations have been in place for novel foods since
the 1990’s and the main aim for these are to secure that food products on the market are safe for the consumers(The
European Parliament and the Council of the European Union 2015). An approved novel food will proven to not cause
harm to the consumer(Koutsoumanis et al. 2020). Harm can be caused by toxins retained in the food product, but
also includes the risk of malnourishing the consumer. This is relevant for products, that intend to substitute already
existing products in the market. If these new products lack important nutrients they will also be considered harmful to
the consumer(The European Parliament and the Council of the European Union 2015).
Most novel foods introduced in the EU originate from plant origins and can generally be put into four categories(Hendrich
2015);
1. Plant species or components used as alternative food ingredient sources.
2. Fruits and vegetables not native or unfamiliar to the country.
3. Plant species or components with medicinal uses but unfamiliar to the country.
4. New pure food ingredients derived from plant species.
Examples of plants used as alternative food ingredient sources could be Agave sisalana which is used as a source
of fructose syrup. Kiwi fruit is another example of one of the exotic fruits considered novel in the EU. Astragalus
membranaceus is one of the medicinal plants in the EU, that is on the novel food list. This plant is thought to beneﬁt
both heart disease and suppress inﬂammatory responses(Hendrich 2015).

3.3.3
FODMAP
The term FODMAP covers a group of simple carbohydrates, which have been put in connection with irritable bowel
syndrome (IBS) in humans. The carbohydrates in FODMAP includes fermentable oligo-, di- and monosaccharides
was well as polyols(Marcason 2012). The connection between IBS and FODMAP compounds is hypothesized to be
caused by high fermentability of FODMAP compounds by microorganisms, as well as poor absorption in the small
intestine. This leads to gas formation in the small and large intestine, as one of the most common symptoms of IBS.
Another issue is the high osmotic pressure created from the poor uptake of FODMAP compounds, causing secretion
of excessive ﬂuid in the intestines(Nanayakkara et al. 2016). Treatment of IBS using a low FODMAP diet has shown
improvements for up to 86% of patients with IBS, by reducing gas production, causing less abdominal pain and
bloating among other symptoms(Nanayakkara et al. 2016).
Multiple plant based foods are considered high in FODMAP compounds, and especially plants and fruits with higher
concentration of free fructose compared to glucose, have been found to cause discomfort in patients with IBS. The
metabolism of fructose by FLAB therefore serves as a potential opportunity to reduce the impact on individuals at
risk(Filannino, Di Cagno, and Gobbetti 2018). It is important however to mention that this serves as a hypothesis,
that still has to be proved through clinical trials. Another risk is that FLAB produce high amounts of mannitol from
fructose, and mannitol is also considered a FODMAP compound(Marcason 2012).
3.3.4
Starter cultures
Starter cultures in food fermentations are used to get a consistent and predictable fermentation process and thereby a
controlled acidiﬁcation. The acidity has a profound effect on many product characteristics(Andersen, Pedersen, and
Bang-Berthelsen 2019). Not only does a low pH contribute to inhibition of pathogens, it also has a signiﬁcant effect on
protein retention, hydration and interactions between proteins, which all affect the ﬁnal product quality. Moreover, a
stable fermentation with a well-deﬁned starter culture can alter the moisture control, mineral composition and increase
good ﬂavours, while removing off-ﬂavours from the food matrix(Huang et al. 2022). All of the aforementioned, shows
the importance of designing strong and viable starter cultures, while protecting them from bacteriophages, that can
attack the starter culture(Matthews, Kniel, and Montville 2017).
Vital technological characteristics of a potential starter culture include growth at different salt concentrations, pH and
temperatures, as well as acidiﬁcation capability and metabolism characteristics(Altieri et al. 2017)(Matthews, Kniel,
and Montville 2017).
Starter cultures are generally classiﬁed based on the complexity of the culture. Traditional starter cultures are either
natural starters, which have been derived from an undeﬁned composition of microorganisms, or mixed-strain starters
(MSS), which are the ’best’ natural starters that have been grown under controlled conditions. The modern approach to
starter cultures is called deﬁned strain starters (DSS). These starter cultures are made of a smaller number of selected
strains. This allows the producer greater control of the properties of the starter culture. DSS cultures are especially
useful for larger industrial productions, as reproducibility is much higher for these cultures(Altieri et al. 2017).

3.4
Issues with plant based dairy alternatives
Creating new plant based food products can be a way to support the transition of the food system to a more sustain-
able and effective future(Aschemann-Witzel et al. 2020). However, developing alternatives to already existing dairy
products based on plants comes with multiple issues. A study conducted by Adamczyk et al. showed that the main
obstacles for consumers to choose PBDA is the taste, smell and price(Adamczyk et al. 2022). Another common issue
for consumers is a diminished nutritional value of PBDA compared to their traditional dairy counterparts. This is
heavily dependent on the type of plant matrix, that makes up the base of the PBDA and comprise to a lower nutrient
density, presence of anti-nutrients, beany off ﬂavor and unbalanced energy proﬁles(Vanga and Raghavan 2018).
3.4.1
Anti-nutrients in potato, oats and pea
Anti-nutrients are commonly present in plants and will reduce the bio-availability of nutrients present in the food
product if not handled properly. These bio-molecules are produced through several metabolic mechanisms(Nath,
Samtiya, and Dhewa 2022). Vegetable tannins are commonly present in high amounts in plants and are considered
as anti-nutritional factors, due to their inhibition of digestive enzymes and negative impact on utilization of vitamins
and minerals in humans(S´aez, Flomenbaum, and Z´arate 2018). Other anti-nutritional factors found in plants include
phytates, oxalates and saponins(Popova and Mihaylova 2019). These compounds bind to minerals such as calcium,
magnesium, iron and zinc to form insoluble complexes with a low bio-availability(Tangyu et al. 2019). Anti-nutrients
found in oats include tannins, phytates, protease inhibitors, rafﬁnose and stachyose, and polyphenols(Sed´o Molina,
Shetty, Xiao, et al. 2022). Oligosaccharides, such as rafﬁnose and stachyose are only digested in humans by intestinal
bacteria, which can cause discomfort and ﬂatulence(Tangyu et al. 2019). Potatoes contain anti-nutrients, too, including
phytic acid, tannins, saponins and cyanide. Legumes such as peas contain the same anti-nutrients as potatoes, as
well as trypsin inhibitors and oxalates(Popova and Mihaylova 2019). Substantial reduction of the aforementioned
anti-nutrients, can be obtained through fermentation in combination with other physical treatments such as cooking,
soaking and sprouting. The ability of LAB to produce phytases, which catalyses the hydrolysis of phytates into myo-
inositol and phosphate to improve digestibility and mineral bio-availability is an example of this(Tangyu et al. 2019).
3.4.2
Volatile off-ﬂavours in potato, oats and pea
A major obstacle for PBDA is the presence of off-ﬂavours, which stops customers from buying the products(Vanga and
Raghavan 2018). Some of the most important volatile off-ﬂavours in legume based food products include n-hexanal
and n-hexanol, which are known to cause the beany smell common to many PBDA(C. Lee and Beuchat 1991). In
addition to these, compounds such as alcohols, ketones, pyrazines and furans have also been found to contribute to
the beany off-ﬂavour(Fischer, Cayot, and Cachon 2022). Hence, this off-ﬂavour is not caused by a single compound,
but can originate from the interaction between multiple compounds(Youssef et al. 2020). These compounds have been
reported to originate from both the enzymatic and non-enzymatic oxidation of plant lipids, as well as amino acid and
protein degradation(Tangyu et al. 2019)(Fischer, Cayot, and Cachon 2022). Lipoxygenases and lipases are some of the
major enzymes involved in the decomposition of unsaturated fatty acids such as oleic acid and linoleic acid in legumes
during storage into volatile off-ﬂavours(Azarnia et al. 2011)(Sed´o Molina, Shetty, Jacobsen, et al. 2024).
Another type of off-ﬂavour is the bitter astringent taste caused by phenols, such as terpenes, glucosinolates and
ﬂavonoids(Tangyu et al. 2019). These compounds are produced by plants as a means of protection by causing a
bitter astringent ﬂavour, which yields the plant unpleasent to eat(Drewnowski and Gomez-Carneros 2000).

3.4.3
Synthesis of aroma compounds and off-ﬂavour removal in LAB
LAB are able to both reduce off-ﬂavours through fermentation, as well as produce sought after aroma compounds.
Various compounds that are essential in achieving a dairy-like aroma are produced by LAB during fermentation.
This includes compounds such as lactate, acetate, acetoin, diacetyl and acetaldehyde(Huang et al. 2022). Pyruvate is
most often converted to lactate in both homofermentative and heterofermentative LAB(Kandler 1983)(Mozzi 2015).
However, one of the most important alternative pathways for pyruvate is the conversion into acetoin and diacetyl for
their sought after butter aroma(S´aez, Flomenbaum, and Z´arate 2018). Diacetyl and acetoin are produced by LAB as
inhibitors of other microorganisms, as both compounds are toxic to bacterial cells(H. S. A and I. S. A 2013). Both
compounds have been shown to cause damage to DNA and proteins, as well as major effects on the lipid composition
of the cell membranes(Cesselin et al. 2021).
The conversion of pyruvate happens through two different pathways. One is forming acetoin from acetolactate and
then oxidizing into diacetyl(Sed´o Molina, Shetty, Xiao, et al. 2022). The other is by direct synthesis from acetyl-CoA
and acetaldehyde. The formation of both compounds can be stimulated depending on the oxidation-reduction balance
of the system, and is lowest when the only carbon source is from hexoses. The production of diacetyl can however be
improved signiﬁcantly when organic acids like citrate is available in the media(Kandler 1983). Acetoin production in
LAB is also used by some LAB, to avoid the over-accumulation of pyruvic acid and reduce internal acid stress on the
cells, by converting pyruvate to acetoin, to allow energy release through glycolysis to continue(Cesselin et al. 2021).
The removal of off-ﬂavours by LAB have been observed numerous times, however the metabolic pathways involved
in the reduction of these compounds are still being outlined. Speciﬁc enzymes involved in breaking down off-ﬂavours
have been identiﬁed and includes alcalase, which breaks down protein-aroma bounds releasing the volatile, as well
as both aldehyde oxidases and aldehyde dehydrogenases. These enzymes both have the ability to reduce the amounts
of aldehydes, such as hexanal(Fischer, Cayot, and Cachon 2022). Alcohol dehydrogenases and hydroperoxide lyase
has also shown potential in off-ﬂavour removal. One example of off-ﬂavour removal has been n-hexanal in soymilk,
which has shown to be greatly reduced by fermentation with LAB(C. Lee and Beuchat 1991).
3.4.4
Bacillus cereus as a pathogen in cereal based food products
Bacillus cereus is a gram positive, obligate aerobe and ever-present spore forming bacteria, common in soil and
on plants and seeds(EFSA (BIOHAZ) 2011). The bacteria is a common food-borne pathogen known to cause two
different types of food poisoning, which can be either emetic or diarrheal syndromes(Browne and Dowds 2001). The
emetic syndrome is caused by cereulide, which is a potent peptide toxin(Jovanovic et al. 2021). Cereulide is produced
enzymatically by genes located on the ces gene locus. The ces transcription is primarily governed by the growth phase,
where cereulide is produced in the exponential growth phase, and ceases production in the stationary phase.(Jovanovic
et al. 2021). Moreover, cereulide is extremely heat stable, as reported by Rajkovic et al.(Rajkovic et al. 2008), who
found cereulide to be stable after heat treatment at 121°C for 80 minutes at pH 9.0. At lower pH, longer heat treatment
times were needed before inactivation of cereulide was achieved.(Rajkovic et al. 2008).
The diarrheal syndrome is caused enterotoxins such as non-hemolytic enterotoxin (Nhe), hemolysin BL (Hbl), and
cytotoxin K (CytK)(Jovanovic et al. 2021). Nhe is the most common toxin produced in almost all enteropathogenic B.
cereus and is made up of a three-component complex, NheA, NheB and NheC(X. Liu et al. 2017). The Nhe complex
is secreted in its three individual parts by B. cereus, before NheB and NheC binds to form the NheBC complex. This
complex can then bind to cell membranes forming an anchor for NheA to bind to. NheA binds to the NheBC complex
11
==PAGE:24==
on the membrane and facilitates the formation of pore formation on the cell membrane(X. Liu et al. 2017).
The proportion of outbreaks with gastrointestinal infections caused by B. cereus is often not possible to determine,
however are seen to occur regularly, which proves the importance of controlling pathogens like B. cereus to protect
the public health(EFSA (BIOHAZ) 2011).
Acid stress is an effective way to limit growth of B. cereus, where a pH below 4.6 has been found lethal for B. cereus
(NCIMB 11796)(Browne and Dowds 2002). Adaptation to acid stress in this bacterium has been investigated, and it
was found that not only induction of sub-lethal levels of acid, but also heat, ethanol, salt and hydrogen peroxide can
adapt the cells to survive previously lethal levels of pH(Browne and Dowds 2002).
3.4.5
Methods of inhibition
Numerous different modes of actions exists to achieve biological preservation in food products(A. Liu et al. 2022).
Speciﬁc LAB are extensively used for their inhibitory effects on pathogens and spoilage organisms, as they allow
food producers to reduce the use of chemical and physical preservatives(Gerez et al. 2013). Common inhibition
methods include production of organic acids and hydrogen peroxide(Chateau, Castellanos, and Deschamps 1993).
Another method, that has grown in interest in the food industry, is the use of bioﬁlm forming LAB, which works as
a biological protection by out-competing the pathogens with anti-adhesive and anti-bioﬁlm attributes(Tarifa, Agust´ın,
and Brugnoni n.d.)(Jalilsood et al. 2015). Other inhibiting compounds produced by some microorganisms include
fatty acids, ethanol, antibiotics and bacteriocins(Yang et al. 2014).
Bacteriocins are produced by most genera of LAB, in order to inhibit growth of other bacteria. The compounds
are antibiotic peptides, which can be divided into three groups, lantibiotics, small heat-stable non-lantibiotics and
large heat-stable bacteriocins. Strains with bacteriocin production can help stabilize fermented products by reducing
growth of unwanted microorganisms, as well as reduce growth of moulds(Altieri et al. 2017). The application of these
compounds is widespread as natural food preservatives. The potential for these compounds is further extended, as
they interact with various bio-macromolecules. An example of this is lantibiotic nisin, which has been shown to be
able to interact with at least nine differently structured compounds. This small peptide thereby has numerous ways of
attacking and killing targeted bacteria(Konings et al. 2000).

4.2.1
Inoculum criteria
It is essential to standardize the inoculum used in fermentation experiments, as variation between experiments is
hugely dependent on the state of the bacterial culture prior to inoculation. Setting up essential criteria for the inoculum
preparation is therefore useful in keeping consistency high. These include, but are not limited to having a culture in a
healthy and active state to limit the duration of the lag phase. It should also be free of contamination, and available for
experiments in sufﬁcient volume to ensure having an inoculum of adequate size. A volume of inoculum between 3%
and 10% of the total medium volume is typically used. Higher addition of inoculum increases biomass formation and
reduce lag phase(Stanbury, Whitaker, and Hall 2017a). Addition of inoculum is standardized at 10% (v/v) of cells at
OD 1.0, yielding an initial cell concentration equivalent to OD of 0.1.

4.4
Species identiﬁcation using MALDI-TOF
Identiﬁcation of bacterial strains has been done using MALDI-TOF Biotyper® Sirius (Bruker Daltonics, Bremen,
Germany). This analysis method identiﬁes species through a library, that links speciﬁc protein’s mass to charge
spectra (m/z). Sample preparation includes extracting proteins from fresh bacterial cultures. Bacterial stock or wild
samples were initially streaked out on MLS agar plates, in order to have single pure colonies. One single colony
was then inoculated and grown in 1.5 ml MLS with 2% glucose and 1% fructose overnight at 30°C. Samples were
centrifuged at 12.000 RPM for 3 minutes before the supernatant was discarded. Then 300 µl demineralized water was
added to each sample, followed by 900 µl 96.6% EtOH. Samples were vortexed for 30 seconds to homogenize before
being centrifuged at 12.000 RPM for 3 minutes. The supernatant was discarded and the pellet left to dry completely.
50 µl of 70% formic acid was added and mixed before 50 µl acetonitrile was added to the samples and vortexed to
mix. All samples were then centrifuged for 3 minutes at 12.000 RPM. 1 µl of supernatant was added to the target plate
and left to dry before 1 µl of saturated matrix was added to each spot. The matrix consists of 475µl miliQ water, 500µl
of pure acetonitrile, 25µl pure triﬂuoroacetic acid and a-cyano-4-hydroxycinnamic acid. The samples were left to dry
before running them in the MALDI-TOF Biotyper® Sirius.
The accuracy of the identiﬁcation is speciﬁed by the software for all successful protein extractions by giving a score
between 1-3. Values between 1-1.69 are considered unreliable, scores between 1.70-1.99 means the result can be
trusted only on a genus level. Scores above 2 means results are reliable on both a species and genus level. Samples
where the protein extraction was inadequate is given a red circle, and new unknown species to the library will have
no score. All MALDI-TOF identiﬁcations were prepared in technical triplicates and only the highest score from each
triplicate is presented.

4.5
Monitoring of cell growth in transparent media in TECAN
For rapid growth screening a setup using two TECAN machines were used. These machines measures growth using
OD and it is therefore essential that the growth media is transparent for these experiments. Therefore, this setup was
only used for screening using the synthetic MLS media with different carbohydrate sources. Growth experiments were
prepared in 96-well microtiter plates with 200 µl of total volume in each well and an inoculum addition of 20µl equal
to an initial OD of 0.1. The experiments were setup to run for 24 hours at 30°C, with measurements being taken every
30 minutes. Just prior to each measurement a brief shaking of the plate was incorporated. All samples were tested
in triplicates. OD readings were taken at 600 nm. Negative controls for growth screenings were MLS media with
speciﬁc carbohydrates added, without any inoculum. Growth characteristics were evaluated by calculating maximum
growthrate, µmax, and ﬁnal OD for each combination of strain and media composition. Calculation of µmax was done
in RStudio, by log-transforming the data with the natural logarithm and conducting a linear regression on the linear
part of the growth curve to get µmax from the slope of the regression.
4.6
Inhibition trial using cell free supernatant
Growth inhibition were conducted in synthetic MLS media with 1% glucose (w/v) and 1% (w/v) fructose spiked with
cell free supernatants (CFS) from overnight cultures. CFS were prepared by incubating 2ml samples of FLAB and
LAB overnight at 30°C in MLS with 1% glucose and 1% fructose. The samples were then centrifuging at 12.000 RPM
for 3 minutes. The supernatant was ﬁltered in sterile 0.2 µm ﬁlters and frozen at -20°C before use. The inhibition trial
was used twice, ﬁrst to test inhibition of Bacillus cereus with FLAB and second as a test of potential LAB strains to
use for co-culturing and their potential inhibitory effect on FLAB.
Monitoring growth of B. cereus and FLAB were conducted in a TECAN measuring optical density at 600 nm every
30 minutes for 24 hours. Samples were prepared in 96-well micro titer plates with 150 µl MLS media with 1% (w/v)
glucose and 1% (w/v) fructose, 30 µl of CFS and 20 µl of washed cells diluted in 0.9% NaCl saline water to OD 1.0,
resulting in an initial OD of cells at 0.1.
B. cereus inoculum was made from fresh overnight cultures grown in MLS media with 1% (w/v) glucose and 1% (w/v)
fructose at 30°C. All combinations of CFS with B. cereus were tested in triplicates. Sterility of CFS were tested by
having single negative controls with each CFS in the same media. Negative controls were B. cereus grown in MLS
media with no CFS added. MLS media with no inoculum was also tested for sterility in a single well. Final OD and
µmax compared to the negative control was used as test parameters to determine the degree of inhibition for each CFS.
The inhibition trial with LAB strains for co-culturing with FLAB was done in a similar fashion with CFS from the 11
candidates for co-culturing. The setup was the same and CFS were prepared similarly, as for the B. cereus trial.

4.9
High performance liquid chromatography
High performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) was used to quantify simple sugars, organic acids and alcohols from
liquid samples using an aminex HPX-87h HPLC (BioRad) column. All samples were compared to known compounds
using standards with deﬁned concentrations. All standards were prepared to a concentration of 10.0 (g/L) using a high
precision scale. Standard concentrations are mass by volume for all powders (maltose, glucose, fructose, sucrose,
mannitol) and volume by volume for liquids (ethanol, glycerol, acetic acid, lactic acid). Standards and samples were

60°C. 5mM H2SO4 was used as the mobile phase. Standard curves were generated by setting up the machine to take
reduced amounts of standard solutions in ﬁve steps. This yielded standard curves ranging from a concentration of 10.0
(g/L) to 0.5 (g/L). Steps used were, 10.0 (g/L), 5.0 (g/L), 2.5 (g/L), 1.25 (g/L), 0.5 (g/L).

Characterising fermentations with a combinatorial microbiology setup
The effect of co-culturing FLAB with other LAB has been tested in a setup combining HPLC, iCinac and GC-MS (gas

taken every 15 minutes for 24 hours. After 24 hours the samples were frozen at -20°C and kept for rheology analysis.

the fermentations of the GC-MS and HPLC samples. The fermentations were stopped once pH had stabilized across

were prepared in triplicates.

4.12
Gas chromatography - mass spectrometry
Analysis of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) from the POP blends was conducted using a dynamic head space
method. The purge and trap method was used to collect volatiles from both fermented and unfermented samples(Bruno
and Harries 2019). Samples were frozen at -20°C and defrosted at room temperature before GC-MS analysis. All sam-
ples were weighed in purge glass bottles and each purge bottle had ⇡0.035 g of internal standard added. The internal
standard used for this experiment was 4-methyl-1-pentanol, and was used to quantify the efﬁciency of the extraction
method. Collection of VOCs was done by placing the samples in a 45°C water bath for 30 minutes, while having
a continuous ﬂow of inert N2 gas at a ﬂowrate of ⇡340 (mL/min) running through the sample and into a TENAX
tube (SKC, USA, https://www.skcltd.com/products2/sorbent-tubes/tenax-sorbent-tubes.html). Af-
ter collection had commenced, TENAX tubes were dried using N2 at a ﬂowrate of 50 (mL/min) for 20 minutes.
VOCs were desorbed and analysed in a gas chromatograph (HP 6890 Series, Hewlett Packard, Palo Alto, CA, USA;
Column: DB-1701, 30 m ×0.25 mm ×1.0 µm; J & W Scientiﬁc, CA, USA). The desorption was setup in four stages,
initially starting with desorption at 45°C for 5 minutes, followed by a temperature rise from 45°C to 55°C at a rate of
1.5°C/min. The next stage was raising the temperature to 90°C at a rate of 2.0°C/min, and ﬁnally heating to the ﬁnal
temperature of 280°C at a rate of 8°C/min. All VOCs were analysed in a mass spectrometry (HP 5973 inert mass-
selective detector, Agilent Technologies, USA; Electron ionization mode, 70 eV, mass to charge ratio scan between 30
and 250).
Quantiﬁcation of relevant volatiles was done using standard curves, made with external standards. Prior to quantiﬁca-
tion, all VOCs from each sample were identiﬁed using mass spectrometry library Wiley 138K (John Wiley and Sons,
Hewlett-Packard). All samples were done in triplicates.

5.2
Mapping of mannitol production in Fructobacillus tropaeoli
The production of mannitol by S1 (Fructobacillus tropaeoli) over 10 hours of fermentation at 30°C was investigated
to highlight the nature of mannitol production in FLAB. Samples were prepared in 2 ml Eppendorf tubes using MLS
media with 1% (w/v) glucose and 1% (w/v) fructose. Fresh overnight culture was centrifuged and washed in saline
water at 0.9% (w/v) NaCl and adjusted to OD600nm = 1.0. 200 µl of inoculum was added to 1800 µl media. After
inoculation all samples were placed in a 30°C incubator and triplicates were taken out every hour for 10 hours. A ﬁnal
triplicate was taken after 24 hours. Negative control was pure MLS media with no inoculum added. All samples were
immediately frozen at -20°C. All samples were prepared for HPLC according to the method described in section 4.9.
The production of mannitol and depletion of glucose and fructose can be seen in ﬁgure 5.2 below. It clearly shows the
high efﬁciency of fructose utilization, as it is depleted roughly twice as fast as glucose, and is completely consumed
after only 8 hours. The production of mannitol follows the complete opposite trend, and the yield of mannitol from
fructose is 98% after 8 hours. This means fructose is completely converted to mannitol, suggesting that F. tropaeoli
utilizes fructose solely as an external electron acceptor, while glucose is present in the media. This follows suit with
previous ﬁndings by Endo et al.(Endo et al. 2018). Once fructose is depleted, consumption of glucose halts and all
concentrations of both substrates and metabolites stabilizes.
The fact that the consumption of glucose almost comes to a complete stop once fructose is depleted, emphasises the
importance of fructose as an external electron acceptor to utilize glucose as a substrate.
Low amounts of lactic acid and acetic acid is observed in samples from 0h and 1h, which is surprising. This could
potentially be from the addition of inoculum. It is possible that the acids were not washed away properly, when
washing the cells with saline water. However, it could also be another compound sharing the same retention time from
the media or a contamination from the column of the HPLC.
Both lactic acid and acetic acid are produced in the fermentation. Lactic acid is produced in higher concentrations in
(g/L). However, when looking at the molar ratios of lactic acid and acetic acid produced from glucose, it shows that
roughly equimolar amounts of both acids are produced from glucose. Molar ratios of lactic acid from glucose was on

Rapid acidiﬁcation screening in POP blends using colorimetric reagent
The next cycle involved testing the 10 best strains for acidiﬁcation capabilities in the POP blend 6 using Multiscan.

map their preference for carbohydrates. All 11 blends were made with POP blend 6 and only the carbohydrate source

supplemented with 1% (v/v) fructose syrup.

5.10
Effect of fermentation and freezing on texture of ﬁnal blend
The texture of POP blends 4 and 6 were assessed using a rheometer as described in section 4.10. Unfermented samples
of each blend and a regular dairy yoghurt was used as controls to compare the change in texture after fermentation.
Each blend was tested after fermentation with S1 and S3 for 24 hours at 30°C. The test was conducted on blends
right after the 24 hours of fermentation, as well as on blends that were frozen at -20°C. Frozen samples were thawed in
water at room temperature. This was done to test the impact of freezing on the fermented blends. Results are presented
in ﬁgure 5.15 just below. All combinations of blends and strains show a worse texture both in terms of viscosity and
stress compared to the traditional dairy. POP blend 4, which is higher in pea protein has a higher initial viscosity and
stress across all shear rates compared to POP6, which has equal proportions from all 3 protein sources. This suggest
that the pea protein contributes with more functional textured proteins compared to the potato proteins. Both S1 and
S3 show similar effects on the viscosity after fermentation. The stress is virtually unchanged for fermented samples
for both blends. The viscosity of fermented blends is increased for low shear rates and lowered for higher shear rates
compared to the unfermented blends, which resembles shear-thinning behaviour typical for yoghurts. The trend of
shear-thinning in the fermented blends are similar to that of the dairy, however at a lower initial viscosity.
The effect of freezing after fermentation shows a decline mainly for the viscosity across all shear rates. However,
the stress seems to decline slightly for the frozen samples compared to the fresh. Overall the internal structure of the
blends is compromised to some degree when freezing. This is to be expected, as freezing has been shown previously
to negatively inﬂuence the internal structure(Tavman and Yilmaz 2017). This will be discussed later.
Overall, the fermentation did not increase the viscosity, but it did change the internal structure to have a more profound
shear-thinning behavior. The analysis method of shaking the blend to homogenize, will undoubtedly also have had
an inﬂuence on any internal structure build up during the fermentation. Without mixing, the PBDA would be phase-
separated in two parts, which is an issue that has to be overcome. These issues will be discussed later as well.

6
Discussion
6.1
Characterisation of FLAB
Through the screening of the 17 FLAB strains multiple interesting patterns were discovered. First of all, it is interest-
ing, that the 10 best performing strains from the initial trial are all either of Fructobacillus or Apilactobacillus genus.
This indicates that the selection criteria used in the initial screening favors speciﬁc genus of FLAB. The criteria were
set up to select the strains with phenotypes having the strongest fructophilic features, including mannitol synthesis and
growth on a mix of glucose and fructose. Out of the best 10 strains, 8 were isolated from different species of bees,
only S13 and S15 were isolated from a ﬂy and a dragonﬂy, respectively. The high prevalence of FLAB isolated from
bees, supports the hypothesis, that LAB adapt to their respective environmental niches and are highly speciﬁc.
The screening excluded strains of numerous different species including Fructilactobacillus sanfranciscensis, Conviv-
ina intestini, Apilactobacillus kunkeei, Fructobacillus pseudoﬁculneus and Fructobacillus cardui, which tells us that
differences in fermentation characteristics are apparent within subspecies of the same genus as well.
The combination of the growth arrest after fructose depletion in , and the non-existent production of ethanol in S1
highlights the need for external electron acceptors for Fructobacillus spp., which indicates that the redox balance of
the system cannot be sustained without external electron acceptors.
Finally, it was interesting to see that the best acidiﬁers in the POP blend all were Fructobacillus spp., which indicates
that high acid production is genus speciﬁc, and that the Fructobacillus spp. both show some of the fastest growth and
highest organic acid synthesis.
6.1.1
Growth characteristics of FLAB
Most FLAB strains tested produced approximately equimolar ratios of lactic acid and acetic acid, which is typical and
to be expected for obligate heterofermentative LAB. The complete lack of ethanol production in all strains, except
trace levels in S9, S10, S13 and S16, serves as an indication of the effect of the partial mutation of the adhe gene,
which completely halts the production of ethanol. This means the strains will need external electron acceptors to keep
to reduction/oxidation balance through glycolysis.
S9, S10 and S13 produced trace amounts of ethanol, which are typical for facultative heterofermentative LAB. Facul-
tative FLAB also grow at a diminished rate on glucose when external electron acceptor are present, which was the case
for both S9 and S10, that were among the slowest in MLS with 1% (w/v) glucose and 1% (w/v) fructose(Filannino,
Cagno, et al. 2016). S9 and S10 were also the only strains capable of growing on only glucose, which further supports
the evidence, that these strains are facultative FLAB.
It is interesting that S9 is the fastest growing strain in MLS supplemented with 1% (w/v) sucrose and 1% (w/v)
fructose. A recent study by Hettiarachchi et al. saw similar tendencies from three strains of Convivina intestini iso-
lated from invasive hornets (Vespa velutina)(Hettiarachchi et al. 2023). Their screening showed that C. intestini lacks
several genes to perform a complete glycolysis, but were however able to grow both on glucose as a sole carbon
source, and were able to uptake and metabolize sucrose by sucrose phosphotransferase, sucrose phosphorylase and
invertase/sucrase. They also found indications that C. intestini was able to produce polysaccharides while degrading
sucrose, which means a screening for EPS production in S9 could be interesting to conduct in the future(Hettiarachchi
et al. 2023).
47
==PAGE:60==
The acid synthesis from S1 and S3 suggest that other metabolites are produced mainly in the initial part of the fermen-
tations. The same trend was seen for S1 in MLS, where acid synthesis was somewhat delayed, which could indicate
that the bacteria produce some metabolites or functional compounds, that are needed initially to stabilize growth and
create the best growth conditions. It also showcases, that glucose is initially used in biomass formation, which is the
highest in the exponential phase. This could also explain where some of the consumed glucose is used. After the ﬁrst
few hours, the molar ﬂow of glucose into lactic acid and acetic acid is roughly equimolar and makes up most of the
glucose consumed for both S1 and S3.
The screening furthermore showed, that fructose is able to be used both as a substrate and an external electron accep-
tor. The results indicate however that glucose is preferred as a carbohydrate source, when both fructose and glucose
are present in the media, while fructose will only be utilized as an external electron acceptor. The disability of most
FLAB to grow on glucose as the sole carbon source further showcase this need for an external electron acceptor.
6.1.2
FLAB as mannitol producers
The mapping of metabolite generation and substrate depletion in F. tropaeoli (S1) showed numerous key phenotypical
attributes, that are typical for FLAB, but most importantly, it highlighted the efﬁciency at which certain FLAB can
produce mannitol from fructose(Endo et al. 2018). Reaching a mannitol yield on fructose of 98%, when grown in MLS
with 1% (w/v) glucose and 1% (w/v) fructose, is in accordance to previous ﬁndings by Ruiz Rodriguez et al., who
observed a 100% yield of mannitol on fructose with two FLAB strains F. tropaeoli (FYP-H3-450) and F. fructosus
(FYP-H2-401) (CRL2430, CERELA Culture Collection, Tucum´an, Argentina)(Ruiz Rodr´ıguez et al. 2019). Previous
studies, who investigated the mannitol synthesis in FLAB typically used medias with twice the amount of fructose
compared to glucose, to achieve a depletion of both substrates.
The consumption of fructose happens twice as fast as for glucose in the mannitol mapping of S1. This is in accordance
to the literature, as the phosphoketolase pathway generates two molecules of NADH, which are in need of an electron
acceptor in order to be oxidized(Endo et al. 2018). Since the reduction of fructose to mannitol only requires one
electron, two molecules of fructose are needed for every glucose molecule being metabolized. This explains why
fructose is depleted rapidly compared to glucose.
The initial screening in MLS showed that the mannitol yield from fructose was strain dependent, as the samples after
6 hours fermentation had a conversion range from 0.66-0.87 mannitol from fructose (mol/mol). This ﬁnding indicate
that FLAB strains have a varying tendency to use fructose solely as an electron acceptor in the presence of glucose.
Some strains will also use it as a substrate for energy metabolism.
The strains with the lowest production of mannitol on a (g/L) basis from the initial screening were isolated from
bumble bees, ﬂowers, dragonﬂies, ant and scorpionﬂies (S4, S7, S9 and S10, S14), while the highest producers in
(g/L) were all isolated from bees and one from a dragonﬂy (S1, S2, S3, S5, S11, S13, S16). This indicates, that the
most optimized phenotype for mannitol production is correlated to the isolation source, more than the genus, as strains
from the same genus are both among the best and worst producers of mannitol.
The mannitol yield of different FLAB strains in the POP blends was determined as well. In this study both S1 and
S3 had a mannitol yield of 96% on fructose consumed in the POP blend, which further supports the evidence that
fructose is solely used as an electron acceptor in the presence of glucose. The yield on total fructose was however
only ⇡70%, since fructose was not utilized to depletion. A previous study by Molina et al., reached mannitol yields
of 0.92 g mannitol/g initial fructose in A. kunkeei (NFICC 2128) in a mix of two side streams; apple pulp and sugar
48
==PAGE:61==
beet molasses(Molina, Shetty, and Bang-Berthelsen 2023). In order to achieve such a high and efﬁcient conversion,
they had to supplement the media with 3% yeast extract and invertases to hydrolyse sucrose and control fermentation
parameters to keep the fermentation anaerobic with a controlled pH of 5.15. These optimizations were not conducted
for the strains in this study. Nitrogen depletion in the POP blends might be a leading factor, as to why both glucose and
fructose were not depleted in the test with S1 and S3 in POP4. The high conversion of fructose to mannitol from both
S1 and S3 in a food side-stream such as the POP blends supplemented with simple carbohydrates, shows promising
results of using F. tropaeoli spp. as mannitol producers.
6.1.3
Potential of FLAB as a bio-preservative
The potential of using a FLAB strain as a bio-preserver, has been assessed through the acidiﬁcation cycles with the best
performing FLAB in the POP blends, with and without co-cultures. The inhibition trial with Bacillus cereus showed
that reaching a pH of 4.5 is sufﬁcient to inhibit the growth of the pathogen. This trial showed that the FLAB tested are
not producing bacteriocins, which are able to inhibit B. cereus. However, optimizing the fermentation with the best
combination of carbohydrates and distribution of protein sources in the POP blends, have shown that reaching a pH
below 4.5 in less than 8 hours is achievable. In these trials, S1 was the only strain to consistently reach this threshold,
however both S3 and the co-cultures with S1 and S3 reached ﬁnal pH of ⇡4.2. The implication of this is massive due to
B. cereus spores high tolerance to heat treatment(Rajkovic et al. 2008). The possibility of optimizing the acidiﬁcation
through co-culturing with another synergistic strain serves as an option to support the bio-preservation effect. Molina
et al., showed a synergistic effect by co-culturing a Lactococcus lactis strain with a Leuconostoc pseudomesenteroides
strain, which gave multiple positive effects, one of which was a faster pH reduction(Sed´o Molina, Shetty, Jacobsen,
et al. 2024). The opportunities and obstacles that co-culturing serves will be discussed later in section 6.3.2.
The need for a rapid fermentation is not only important in achieving high food safety through bio-preservation, but
is also associated to an economical beneﬁt, as a shorter processing time from raw material to ﬁnal product yields a
higher productivity, and hence lower production costs.
6.2
Optimizing FLAB fermentation in the POP blends
The acidiﬁcation experiments in all three POP blends clearly showed, that the growth and subsequent acidiﬁcation by
FLAB in these blends, can be greatly optimized by altering the carbohydrate sources, type of blend and selecting the
best performing strains. Overall POP blend 4 was signiﬁcantly better performing than the two other blends tested.
Possible reasons for this will be discussed later. The rapid screening method using color/pH indicators allowed for
testing a vast number of combinations of strains and media compositions, which generally showed interesting patterns.
Overall medias that performed well had a combination of glucose or other simple carbohydrates with an addition of
fructose. The results from the experiments tend to show that the best medias use fructose almost exclusively as an
electron acceptor, while another other carbohydrate source (typically glucose) has to be readily available for energy
metabolism.
An interesting ﬁnding is that the media with 4% (v/v) fructose syrup did not perform well, even though this media
had the most carbohydrates added, which shows that growth is not optimized when fructose has to be used both as a
substrate and an external electron acceptor. The media with 1% (v/v) fructose syrup also performed almost as well as
the one with 4% (v/v), which also shows that growth is not optimized when adding higher concentrations of fructose.
The medias that performed the worst either only had fructose or were treated with enzyme but not given fructose syrup.

The apple pomace also did not perform well, which most likely was due to the dilution factor of the apple pomace, as
Molina et al., were able to conduct successful experiments in medias composed partly of apple pomace, as mentioned
previously.(Molina, Shetty, and Bang-Berthelsen 2023).
6.2.1
Using amylases to improve growth
The use of amylases as a pre-treatment step for the POP blends showed a great potential by hydrolysing the starch in
the blends and generate readily available simple carbohydrates. As mentioned earlier, the hydrolysis was so effective,
that these medias performed the best (only with fructose syrup added as well). However, these blends also came with
the problem of splitting the internal structure of the blend. Optimizing the enzymatic pre-treatment can however prove
to be difﬁcult. Using the current Novozymes amylases comes with some extra considerations. These enzymes are
optimized to work the best between 70°C and 80°C. Hence inactivation by heat treatment was not deemed optimal, as
the texture change from starch gelatinization and potential protein denaturation was going to affect the ﬁnal texture of
the blend.
For the future, it would be interesting to test how the texture is changed by a heat treatment, sufﬁcent to inactivate the
enzymes. All experiments that used amylases had no processing step to inactivate the enzymes. This means it was
expected, that active enzymes would still be present in the media during fermentation. Therefore, remainders of starch
and bigger polysaccharides could potentially be degraded during fermentation at 30°C, which would have the potential
to alter the composition of the media yielding more fermentable sugars overall. This effect is expected to be low, as
the amylases are optimized to work at much higher temperatures. Assessing the potential of this phenomenon has
been considered, however it was deemed too difﬁcult to quantify, as the POP blends are contaminated naturally with
B. cereus, which would also start growing, thereby changing the composition as well. This ultimately meant that the
amylases most likely hydrolysed the starch completely. Controlling the enzymatic treatment thus poses as a potential
of optimizing the growth while protecting the internal structure of the blend. This could be achieved by reducing the
time of enzymatic treatment, or change to heat sensitive amylases, so inactivation by heat is more plausible. Another
path to pursue could be the use of strains with amylolytic activity to compensate for the enzymatic treatment.
6.2.2
Different digestibility of protein sources in POP blends
Based on the acidiﬁcation experiments in the iCinac, it was clear that all four strains (S1, S2, S3 and S5) acidiﬁed
POP blend 4 signiﬁcantly faster than the two other blends. Since the only difference between the blends were different
proportions of protein sources, this suggests that the pea protein is more readily available for the microorganisms com-
pared to the potato protein. Traditional extraction methods of potato protein often uses high-intensity heat treatment,
which heavily reduces both the solubility and digestibility of the extracted protein(Mao et al. 2020). This type of
extraction method seems to also reduce the availability for the FLAB strains, yielding in a slower growth. Improve-
ments to both solubility and digestibility of potato proteins have been shown by Mao et al., who used a combination
of ultrasonic treatment paired with a rapid shift in pH and a mild heat treatment to extract more functional potato pro-
teins(Mao et al. 2020). This study highlights why better extraction methods of plant proteins are essential to achieve
high quality plant based proteins and products.

6.3
Final product quality
6.3.1
Improvements to the texture
The rheological experiments highlighted an apparent lack of viscosity and texture improvements across all fermenta-
tions and blends. The lack of numerous texture impacting effects could serve as the explanation to these observations.
Previous studies by M¨akinen et al. found that oat milk is inherently unstable and has a rapid sedimentation compared
to other plant based milks and a low viscosity and protein solubility, which all could serve as factors to why any acid
induced gelation is minimized in the POP blend(M¨akinen et al. 2015). Acid induced gelation is seen in dairy fermen-
tations, as the pH reaches the iso-electric point of casein, which then results in the aggregation of casein providing
a thickening effect on the internal structure(Sadeghi et al. 2014). The lacking effect of acid induced gelation on the
proteins could be a combination of insufﬁcient acidiﬁcation as well as denatured proteins in the blends. The pasteuri-
sation process at 75°C for 5 minutes could have contributed to lowering the functionality of the proteins in the blends.
The viscosity of the POP blend 6 (high in potato protein) was systematically lower than that of POP blend 4 (high in
pea protein), which might indicate that the functionality of the potato protein in blend 6 is heavily reduced through the
extraction methods, which might leave the potato protein denatured prior to incorporation in the blend, while the pea
proteins seem to have a higher functionality.
Previous studies on yellow peas by Taherian et al., showed that the main driver for internal structure development was
due to ﬂocculation of starch, which increased the apparent viscosity. It was also found that the yellow peas show a
shear-thinning behavior similar to what was observed in the present trial(Taherian, Lamarche, and Mondor 2015).
Poor emulsion stability of oats is another issue for the texture which is found to be caused by their high starch con-
tent(Sethi, Tyagi, and Anurag 2016). These features of oat in PBDA is thus expected to play a role in the lack of
texture development. After 24 hours a water phase was observed on top of the blend, which corresponds to previous
studies showing the sedimentation of oats.
The effect of freezing was clearly shown to reduce the viscosity of the blends in a similar manner for both blend 4
and 6, which highlights the issues involved with freezing the PBDA both before and after fermentation. In dairy it
has been shown that freezing can cause fat emulsion disruption and protein ﬂocculation, which overall reduce the vis-
cosity of the dairy after thawing(Tavman and Yilmaz 2017). It is suspected that a similar mechanism could be behind
the reduced texture of the frozen samples. Avoiding freezing is currently not viable due to the presence of B. cereus
spores, however with an industrial production line, with a rapid preparation and fermentation, it might be possible to
avoid freezing.
The lack of texture development after fermentation suggests that S1, S3, LM199 and LP323 are non-EPS producing
strains. It has however not been conﬁrmed, and is thus only speculations. With more time it would have been interest-
ing to screen for EPS production in the strains.
Finally the analytical method of shaking the falcon tubes prior to testing also contributes to the destruction of any
internal structure, however it was deemed more appropriate to get a homogeneous sample for viscosity analysis. A
previous study by Madsen et al, showed this particular tendency, where the texture is reduced of the PBDA when
stirring before measuring the viscosity(Madsen et al. 2021).

6.3.2
The effect, obstacles and opportunities of co-culturing FLAB
Overall, it is interesting to see that all fermentations with CFS added from either of the 11 co-culture strains reduce
µmax of both S1 and S3. There is no apparent positive effect, which suggests that any potential proteolytic activity has
not made the nitrogen source more available. It is also interesting to see that µmax is heavily reduced, when growing
both S1 and S3 in media with CFS from Lactobacillus plantarum spp., while both Leuconostoc mesenteroides spp.
and Leuconostoc pseudomesenteroides spp. perform similarly. L. plantarum is known to have numerous bacteriocin
producing strains, which could suggest that these strains are producing bacteriocins as well, which hinders the growth
of S1 and S3(Todorov 2009).
The potential of co-culturing two strains, has been proven numerous times. As mentioned earlier, a recent study
by Molina et al. showed the effect of co-culturing L. lactis (NFICC 2005) with L. pseudomesenteroides (NFICC
2004), where increased growth, increased acidiﬁcation rate, lower ﬁnal pH and higher removal of off-ﬂavours were
achieved(Sed´o Molina, Shetty, Jacobsen, et al. 2024). Those ﬁndings are somewhat in contrast to this study, where
acidiﬁcation rate was slower for the co-cultures compared to the mono-cultures. The synergistic effects achieved
by Molina et al. combined a highly efﬁcient amino acid metabolism from L. lactis with a proteolytic strain of L.
pseudomesenteroides. Achieving a similar synergistic effect with FLAB should be possible with a broader screening.
Co-culturing FLAB with a strain with amylolytic activity serves as an interesting path to pursue in order to release
some simple sugars from the starch, to mimic the effect of using amylases.
The mannitol production of LP323, and LP323 with S3 was signiﬁcantly higher, than for the mono-cultures of the two
FLAB strains, which initially suggests that LP323 would be a better strain for producing mannitol than the FLAB.
However, when looking at the molar ratios of fructose to mannitol, it is clear that both S1 and S3 has a signiﬁcantly
higher yield on mannitol. Surprisingly LM199 has the highest yield on mannitol from fructose alongside S1. Previous
studies have however found strains of both L. mesenteoides and L. pseudomesenteroides with mannitol production of
up to 86% from fructose on a mol/mol basis(Helanto et al. 2005)(Saha and Racine 2011). Historically FLAB were
originally classiﬁed as fructophilic Leuconostoc spp., which could explain why LM199 and LP323 are phenotypically
similar to FLAB(Endo 2012).
The phenotypical similarity between LP323 and S1 and S3 reveals a new interesting approach to PBDA development.
A typical problem with obligate hetero-fermentative LAB in PBDA is their production of ethanol. LP323 does not
produce ethanol in these experiments, but still produces high levels of mannitol and diacetyl. The positive attributes
of FLAB in the development of new PBDA can thus potentially be achieved using strains of L. pseudomesenteroides,
which are already QPS approved. This poses as a much faster way from product development to get on the market.
6.3.3
Production and removal of volatile compounds
The FLAB strains and their co-cultures showed a mixed performance in terms of reducing volatile off-ﬂavours. Pen-
tanal and heptanal were reduced below their aroma thresholds, while hexanal, nonanal and 2-pentyl furan were also
reduced, however not to an extend to reach concentrations below their respective aroma thresholds. 1-heptanol was not
reduced, but however produced by all fermentations to a concentration 3-4 times higher than the concentration in the
control. The reduction of aldehydes such as hexanal, are primarily reduced by aldehyde oxidases and aldehyde/alcohol
dehydrogenases(Fischer, Cayot, and Cachon 2022). The reduction of hexanal by the FLAB strains S1 and S3 might
be diminished slightly, due to the partial mutation on the adhe gene, which hinders the acetaldehyde/alcohol dehy-
52
==PAGE:65==
drogenase activity. The partial reduction of aldehydes in the POP blend indicates that the functionality of the alde-
hyde/alcohol dehydrogenase in the FLAB strains is not completely lost.
A previous study by Youssef et al. studied the effect of co-culturing LAB with yeast in a pea protein-based product
in off-ﬂavour removal(Youssef et al. 2020). Their study used a starter culture of LAB with Lactobacillus acidophilus,
Streptococcus thermophilus, Lactobacillus delbrueckii subsp. bulgaricus, and Biﬁdobacterium lactis, which was co-
cultured with 3 different yeast strains. Their study was able to reduce most of the off-ﬂavour compounds related to the
beany off-ﬂavour typical of pea protein. They found that combining the LAB starter culture with yeasts reduced levels
of (E)-2-heptenal, 6-methyl-5-hepten-2-one, and trans-2-methyl-2-butenal. These compounds were not reduced with
only the LAB starter culture in their study. This lead to a more ”green” tasting and smelling ﬁnal product(Youssef et al.
2020). This study highlights the complexity of aroma metabolism and off-ﬂavour removal, which corresponds to the
ﬁndings in this study, where a substantial amount of off-ﬂavours were still present after fermentation. The application
of mixing yeast with LAB was showed to both reduce off-ﬂavours more efﬁciently, but also introduces the possibility
of ester synthesis by yeast, which can contribute with improvements to the aroma of the ﬁnal product.
The low production of diacetyl and no production of acetoin might be caused by the fact, that the medias did not contain
other carbohydrate sources than hexoses and was not spiked with any organic acids, such as citrate(S´aez, Flomenbaum,
and Z´arate 2018)(H. S. A and I. S. A 2013). Since these compounds have been shown to increase production of both
compounds in LAB. The presence of off-ﬂavours is also expected to contribute more to the perceived aroma, hence
the reduction of off-ﬂavours should be optimized further before improving the diacetyl and acetoin production.
6.3.4
Alternative applications of FLAB in other food products
The characteristics of FLAB, that has been highlighted in this project, suggest that the application of FLAB could
easily be extended to products outside of PBDA. The ability of FLAB to convert fructose to mannitol at high yields
serves as an important feature, which could be utilized in products such as juices. Reducing the amount of simple
carbohydrates available in fruit juice by conversion into mannitol and organic acids, could yield a sweet and slightly
acidic juice-like product with a lower caloric value and lower glycemic index. A product like this could be interesting
for a number of different consumers. By reducing the glycemic index, you could provide a juice-like product for
patients suffering from diabetes. The reduction in simple sugars, would also make it potentially more appealing
to health concerned consumers. This is just one example for alternative uses of FLAB. Another could by for the
biochemical production of mannitol as a ﬁne chemical, that is used in numerous industries and applications.

7
Conclusion
This project overall showed the huge potential of FLAB as mannitol producing strains. They have successfully been
grown in both synthetic and complex media in the form of the newly developed MLS media and three POP blends. An
initial screening highlighted phenotypical differences across the FLAB strains, which could both be related to genus
and subspecies within each genus, as well as the isolation source of the bacteria.
The screening process showed phenotypical differences between subspecies of FLAB, where Fructobacillus tropaeoli
performed best across the laboratory cycles. S1 and S3 were among the best mannitol producers, while being the
fastest acidiﬁers in the POP blends. Screenings in the POP blends showed clear restrictions of FLAB in terms of
carbohydrate sources. Most strains needed a combination of glucose and fructose in the media to be able to grow
effectively and acidify the media fast. S9 (Convivina intestini stood out from the rest by its ability to grow solely on
glucose, as well as its ability to utilize sucrose better than the other FLAB.
The POP blends were shown to be naturally contaminated with B. cereus spores. Inhibition trials with B. cereus
showed the importance of bio-preservation by acidiﬁcation, as heat treatment of the spores is not viable.
The best performing strains achieved high yields of mannitol from fructose in both types of media. The yields of
mannitol from fructose in both synthetic and complex media reached levels similar to previous studies on FLAB
strains. FLAB was also shown to possess key characteristics found useful in the development in clean-label sugar-free
PBDA. These features include their preference for fructose and glucose, their lack of ethanol production, as well as
their high conversion of fructose to mannitol.
Although these positive impacts from FLAB on PBDA have been shown in this project, implementation of FLAB in
PBDA is still to be determined. This is due to their reduced effect on removing important off-ﬂavours such as hexanal,
2-pentyl furan, nonanal and 1-heptanol, and their low production of dairy-like aroma compounds, such as diacetyl and
acetoin. Moreover, their negligible effect on the ﬁnal texture of the POP blend is an issue, that is still to be resolved.
Combinatorial microbiology was used to try and overcome some of these obstacles, however the strains chosen for
co-culturing seemed to contribute with similar phenotypical properties as the FLAB. Both LM199 and LP323 had
high production of mannitol, no ethanol production and did not contribute to a better texture or off-ﬂavour removal.
No apparent synergistic effect on the growth and acidiﬁcation rate was also observed, which suggest that a broader
screening for co-culturing candidates has to be conducted.
L. pseudomesenteroides (LP323) was coincidentally found to have phenotypical characteristics similar to those of
FLAB, which has opened for the possibility to use these strains to achieve similar results in PBDA as FLAB, while
using a QPS approved species.
7.1
Future aspects
Numerous paths are interesting to pursue in the characterization of the FLAB and mapping of their potential uses in
the food industry. This project has focused on characterising FLAB phenotypically for their application in PBDA. The
next step would initially be to investigate the genomes of the characterized bacteria to see any genetic patterns, that
could explain the different phenotypes observed. Other interesting prospects are brieﬂy mentioned below.

7.1.1
Expanding the analysis of combinatorial microbiology
Due to the limited time-frame of the project, the extend of combinatorial microbiology was kept to a minimum,
however going deeper into co-culturing FLAB with other LAB, other bacteria or even yeast is an interesting and
complex task. Expanding on the number of tested strains would hugely expand the amount of experiments to conduct,
and was therefore not viable for this project.
With more time and resources, it would be interesting to validate the co-cultures after fermentation and see if the
proportions are kept the same between the strains, as well as test how altering the proportions of each strain alters the
fermentation proﬁle and end-product.
7.1.2
Untargeted GC-MS and sensory analysis
The potential of ﬁnding interesting aroma compounds produced by FLAB through untargeted GC-MS has been lim-
ited due to time constraints on the project. However, this is an interesting avenue to further investigate in the process
of assessing the viability of FLAB as PBDA starter cultures. Observing and quantifying aroma compounds outside
the standards used can potentially highlight interesting compounds, which could positively inﬂuence the quality of the
ﬁnal product.
Analysing only VOCs based on GC-MS analysis only gives indications on the ﬁnal aroma and taste. Different syner-
gistic and antagonistic effects between the different aroma compounds overall means that the actual aroma and taste
is not determinable. Therefore conducting a sensory analysis with a tasting panel would be a great way to further
investigate the effect of fermenting the POP blend with FLAB.
7.1.3
QPS approval of FLAB
As mentioned in the introduction, FLAB are still not QPS approved in the EU, which currently hinders their appli-
cation in food production. Fructilactobacillus sanfranciscensis has currently met the ﬁrst qualiﬁcation, as the only
FLAB strain, by showing no apparent antimicrobial resistance to relevant antimicrobials. It has however still not been
approved for production purposes with or without cells. The next step of approval is for production purposes, where
no live cells are left in the ﬁnal product. This study has shown positive effects of using newly isolated FLAB strains in
food fermentations and highlighted phenotypical characteristics, that have huge potential in the development of new
plant based products, and should therefore serve as another stepping stone to reach QPS approval in the near future.
