Abstract 
Diabetic retinopathy is considered the most common cause of vision loss among patients 
affected with diabetes mellitus. In spite of the fact that extensive research has been done 
on the field, the molecular mechanisms implicated in the development of the disease 
remain unclear, and thus the design of an effective treatment has been so far prevented. 
In the pathogenesis of diabetic retinopathy, mitochondria are damaged and inflammatory 
mediators are elevated before the histopathology associated with the disease can be 
observed. Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) are modulators of a range of different 
physiological and pathological cellular functions contributing in the retainment of tissue 
homeostasis. When the balance between the production of MMPs and their respective 
inhibitors is disrupted, MMPs and particularly gelatinases (MMP-2 and MMP-9) induce 
inflammation, neovascularization and activation of the apoptotic machinery, 
characteristic conditions of various stages of diabetic retinopathy.  
Drug delivery strategies that exploit the advantages of nanocarriers have gained increased 
attention lately. Particularly, several liposomal strategies have been developed so far, but 
most of them rely on passive drug diffusion or unspecific association between liposomes 
and cells, resulting in low efficiency of the loaded drugs. Therefore, development of 
rationally designed systems for delivery of drugs to specific tissues or cells that utilize the 
biological knowledge of the disease, seems promising.  
In this thesis a novel matrix metalloproteinase sensitive poly-ethylene glycol (PEG) 
coated liposomal drug delivery system for treatment of diabetic retinopathy is presented. 
The system exploits the increased MMP activity present in diseased cells as a site-specific 
trigger of liposomal activation and controlled drug release after accumulation, due to the 
intraocular transport. The functions of the nanocarrier include hydrophilic and flexible 
long PEG chains that prevent nanocarrier non-specific interactions and prolong its 
circulation time until it reaches disease sites where the increased MMP activity results in 
shedding of the PEG coating. Enzymatic cleavage reveals inner cationic surface which 
enhances cell uptake due to the interaction with the negatively charged cell membranes.  
In diseased cells, the MMP landscape was successfully determined by protein separation 
methods such as gelatin zymography and immunoblotting. Increased gelatinases 
production in cells that simulated conditions characteristic of diabetic complications, 
resulted in activation of the liposomal formulations in vitro. Successful proteolytic 
cleavage of the MMP-sensitive chain was identified with the application of flow 
cytometry. These data support the use of gelatinases as a trigger for liposomal activation 
and imply that this strategy provides a promising system for specific delivery of 
encapsulated drugs with enhanced bioavailability.

Resume 
Diabetisk retinopati betragtes som den mest almindelige årsag til synstab blandt patienter, 
der er ramt af diabetes mellitus. På trods af det faktum, at der er udført omfattende 
forskning på området er de molekylære mekanismer, der er involveret i sygdommens 
udvikling, uklare, og således er designet af en effektiv behandling blevet forhindret. I 
patogenesen af diabetisk retinopati er mitokondrier beskadiget, og inflammatoriske 
mediatorer hæves, før histopatologien, der er forbundet med sygdommen, kan 
observeres. Matrixmetalloproteinaser (MMP'er) er modulatorer af en række forskellige 
fysiologiske og patologiske cellulære funktioner, som bidrager til fastholdelsen af 
vævshomostase. Når balancen mellem produktionen af MMP'er og deres respektive 
inhibitorer forstyrres, inducerer MMP'er især gelatinaser (MMP-2 og MMP-9) 
inflammation, neovaskularisering og aktivering af det apoptotiske maskineri, all 
karakteristiske betingelser i forskellige stadier af diabetisk retinopati. 
Drug delivery strategier, der udnytter fordelene ved nanocarriers har fået øget 
opmærksomhed på det seneste. Især er flere liposomale strategier blevet ind til nu, men 
de fleste af dem er afhængige af passiv lægemiddeldiffusion eller uspecifik tilknytning 
mellem liposomer og celler, hvilket resulterer i lav effektivitet af de indkapslede 
lægemidler. Derfor synes udvikling af rationelt designede systemer til levering af stoffer 
til specifikke væv eller celler, der udnytter den biologiske viden om sygdommen, lovende. 
I denne afhandling bliver et novelt matrixmetalloproteinase-følsomt polyethylenglycol 
(PEG) -overtræket liposomalt lægemiddelafgivelsessystem til behandling af diabetisk 
retinopati præcenteret. Systemet udnytter den forøgede MMP aktivitet, som er til stede i 
syge celler som en site-specifik trigger for liposomal aktivering og kontrolleret 
lægemiddelfrigivelse efter akkumulering på grund af intraokulære transport. 
Nanocarrierens funktioner omfatter hydrofile og fleksible lange PEG-kæder, der 
forhindrer nanocarrier-ikke-specifikke interaktioner og forøger dens intraokulæere 
diffusion, indtil den når sygdomssteder, hvor den forøgede MMP-aktivitet resulterer i 
kløvning af PEG-belægningen. Den enzymatisk spaltning afslører indre kationiske 
overflade, som forbedrer celleoptagelse på grund af interaktionen med de negativt ladede 
cellemembraner. 
I syge celler blev MMP-landskabet bestemt ved proteinseparationsmetoder såsom 
gelatinezymografi og immunoblotting. Øget gelatinasesproduktion i celler, som blev 
simuleret med betingelser karakteristisk for diabetiske komplikationer, resulterede i 
aktivering af liposomale formuleringer in vitro. Succesfuld proteolytisk spaltning af den 
MMP-følsomme kæde blev identificeret ved anvendelse af flowcytometri. Disse data 
understøtter anvendelsen af gelatinaser som en trigger for liposomal aktivering og 
indebærer, at denne strategi giver et lovende system til specifik levering af indkapslede 
lægemidler med forbedret biotilgængelighed.

Motivation and objectives 
Diabetic retinopathy is a multi-functional slow progressing disease. It has been shown 
that, transformation of healthy to pathophysiological retina has been related with 
extracellular matrix degradation which is mostly caused by a family of enzymes called 
matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs). Therefore, it is implicated that studying the 
association of MMPs with diabetic retinopathy could potentially lead to the development 
of novel treatment approaches. In this context, the main focus of our work was to 
successfully design stimuli responsive liposomes that would correspond to the increased 
MMP expression of the diseased cells eventually inducing release of encapsulated drug 
agents and improving cell uptake mechanisms.  
➢ Reproducibly induce MMP expression (MMP-2 and/or MMP-9) in one or more of 
the following cell lines (2D cultures) – Ht-1080s, ARPE-19s. 
 
➢ Apply different protein separation methods to effectively identify expression of 
MMPs in both cell lines. 
 
➢ Successfully construct PEGylated and MMP-sensitive liposomes. 
 
➢ Determine uptake differences between healthy and inflamed/MMP expressing cells 
for a small range of liposomes (e.g. stealth, cationic, PCL21).

Introduction 
Diabetes mellitus, commonly referred as diabetes, is a global epidemic with profound 
morbidity.  Diabetes is divided into two main categories. Type one results from the 
pancreas inability to successfully produce sufficient amounts of insulin, while type two 
relies on insulin resistance, a condition developed by cells which fail to respond properly 
to the insulin being already produced. The abnormalities observed in both types of 
diabetes mellitus can lead to a range of several complications divided into macrovascular 
(large blood vessels) and microvascular (small blood vessels). (Petrovič 2013; Safi et al. 
2014; Tarr et al. 2013) 
 
Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is a specific microvascular complication of diabetes and is the 
one of the leading causes of vision loss among people of the working population. 
Specifically, it is characterized by lesions and vascular anomalies in the retina, which 
include micro-aneurysms, hemorrhages, cotton wool spots, exudates (bright spots), 
venous beading, intra-retinal microvascular abnormalities and neovascularization. 
Without treatment and early detection, DR can progress from mild, non-proliferative DR 
to moderate and severe non-proliferative DR before the occurrence of proliferative DR, 
during which formation and proliferation of abnormal new retinal blood vessels, is 
observed. During any stage of retinopathy, patients can develop diabetic macular edema 
(DME), if leakage of proteins or fluid occurs from the blood vessels near the macula, the 
part of the eye that is responsible for the central vision. (Fig. 1) Diabetic retinopathy has 
been linked to various factors, such as the diet, environmental conditions and the 
breakdown of pathways related to the physiological function of the retina. Considerable 
progress has been made in the effort to understand the complicated pathogenetic factors 
that cause DR, but a few of its aspects still remain unclear. (Petrovič 2013; Safi et al. 
2014; Tarr et al. 2013) The reader is advised to look up the article from (Wong et al. 
2016) for further information about the clinical signs of DR and its classification in 
subclasses.

Ocular anatomy  
The eye is one of the most important and complex organs of the human body. From a 
structural point of view, the human eye is composed of three distinct layers (Fig. 2).  
The outer most layer includes the cornea and the sclera. The cornea is responsible for 
refraction and transmission of light to the lens and the retina. It also helps in protecting 
they eye from infectious agents. The sclera has mostly a supportive role for the eye by 
forming a connective tissue coat that protects from internal and external forces and 
maintains structural integrity. Moreover, the visible part of sclera is surrounded by a 
mucous surface that is called conjunctiva. The second and middle ocular layer includes 
the iris, the ciliary body and the choroid. The iris is responsible of controlling the overall 
size of the pupil and as a result of the amount of the light being transmitted to the retina. 
The ciliary body is another supportive ocular element which controls the shape of the 
lens. The choroid is characterized by vasculature which supports with oxygen and 
nutrients the retinal layers. (Willoughby et al. 2010) 
Retina (Fig. 3) composes the inner ocular layer and is characterized by a complex layered 
structure of cells which is necessary for capturing and processing light. Retinal layers 
include cell types that are vastly involved in the development and progression of ocular 
diseases such as diabetic retinopathy and thus, a significant amount of references will be 
directed to them. (Willoughby et al. 2010)  
Fig. 1: Clinical stages and main pathogenetic events of diabeic retinopathy. The first pathological stage that 
can be identified in a fundoscopic examination is non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy (DR), which can be 
divided into mild, moderate or severe non-proliferative DR. Diabetic macular oedema can be considered a 
particular subtype of non-proliferative DR in which vascular leakage involves the macula. Pre-proliferative or 
severe non-proliferative DR is characterized by capillary closures and non-perfused areas. The typical fundoscopic 
finding is the presence of ‘cotton wool’ spots and intraretinal microvascular abnormalities. Proliferative DR, in 
which hypoxia has an essential role, is the final stage of DR and its hallmark is the development of 
neovascularization. Haemovitreous (that is, bleeding in the vitreous cavity) and retinal detachment are advanced 
complications of proliferative DR.  (Wong et al. 2016)

Treatment approaches against retinal diseases however, require access to the posterior 
segment of the eye. The existence of the inner and outer-retinal barriers render injections 
directly to the retina impossible and as a result, bypassing these barriers is needful. 
Overcoming this challenge has been achieved by performing injections at the vitreous 
which is composed mainly by water in a porous collagen-glycosaminoglycan network 
(CGN) (Eriksen et al. 2017). The electronegativity of glycosaminoglycans and the varying 
porosity of CGN, present two major challenges that affect the efficacy of drug delivery 
systems and thus, their omission when designing treatment approaches should be 
avoided.

Matrix Metalloproteinases 
The interaction of cells with their surrounding environment and specifically the 
extracellular matrix (ECM) is critical for the normal development and function of 
organisms. These interactions however, are greatly dependent on the action of 
proteolytic systems which are responsible for the degradation of the components of the 
extracellular space. Such systems regulate the integrity and composition of the ECM and 
thus, play a crucial role in controlling signals elicited by matrix molecules which in turn 
modulate cellular fate. However, uncontrolled proteolysis and remodeling of the ECM, 
leads to the development and progression of pathological conditions. (Chakraborti et al. 
2003), (Stamenkovic 2003) 
 
A family of proteases that is vastly involved in tissue homeostasis and remodelling are 
matrix-metalloproteases (MMPs). The MMPs are zinc-dependent endopeptidases known 
for their ability to cleave one or several ECM constituents and are classified as the 
matrixins subfamily of zinc metalloprotease family M10 in the MEROPS database. 
(Stamenkovic 2003), (Rawlings et al. 2018) 
 
MMP structure 
MMPs are translated as zymogens (inactive enzymes) that contain a signal sequence 
which is responsible for targeting and transition of these enzymes to secretory vesicles. 
The presence of the signal peptide pinpoints that MMPs are either secreted or anchored 
to the cell surface and thereby restricting their catalytic activity to transmembrane 
proteins or proteins located in the extracellular space. To be classified as an MMP, a 
protein must have at least the conserved pro and catalytic domains (Fig. 4). The 
prodomain of a typical MMP is about 80 amino acids long and contains the consensus 
sequence PRCXXPD. The catalytic domain is characterized by the presence of three 
conserved histidines in the sequence HEXXHXXGXXH, which are necessary for 
ligating zinc ions of the active site. Within the catalytic motif, a glutamate residue 
functions as an activator of a zinc-bound H2O molecule providing enzymes with the 
nucleophile that is required for the cleavage of peptide bonds. (Ra and Parks 2007) 
 
With some exceptions (MMP-7, -23, and -26), MMPs have a flexible hinge region rich in 
proline residues with undefined function and a C-terminal hemopexin-like domain, 
which is involved in substrate recognition (Fig. 4). Other structural differences among 
MMPs are confined to specific subgroups. For example, four membrane-type MMPs 
(MMP-14, -15, -16, -24) have transmembrane and cytosolic domains, whereas MMP-17

MMP activation 
An interaction between a zinc ion of the catalytic domain and a cysteine-thiol complex of 
the prodomain keeps MMPs in a latent state. The activation of a proMMP is dependent 
on a widely accepted mechanism called the ‘cysteine switch’. For a proMMP to become 
catalytically active, the thiol-Zn2+ interaction must be disrupted. This can be achieved by 
three mechanisms: 1) direct cleavage of the prodomain by another proteinase 2) 
reduction of the free thiol by oxidants or by non-physiologic reagents such as alkylating 
agents, heavy metal ions, and disulfides and 3) allosteric perturbation of the zymogen 
(Fig. 5). Thiol reduction and allosteric controls would lead to inter or intramolecular 
autolytic cleavage of the prodomain. (Ra and Parks 2007), (Van Wart and Birkedal-
Hansen 1990), (Springman et al. 1990)

MMPs and DR 
As mentioned before, diabetic retinopathy is a multifunctional progressing disease. In the 
initial stages of the disease, abnormal histological features such as thickening of the 
basement membrane result in leakages which can compromise the integrity of the 
vascular wall. In the advanced stages, inflammation, ischemia-induced angiogenesis and 
expansion of the ECM lead to neovascularization and retinal detachment which 
eventually causes vision loss. All these changes implicate that factors related with 
remodeling of the retina and its surrounding tissues, such as MMPs and their respective 
TIMPs, could play a pivotal role in the development and progression of diabetic 
complications. In this context, studies have shown that increased activity of gelatinases 
(MMP-2 and MMP-9) is greatly associated with retinas and vitreous found in diabetic 
eyes. 
Mitochondrial damage and apoptosis 
Among the various factors that become dysfunctional during the progression of diabetic 
retinopathy an important one, is the altered retinal metabolism that becomes obvious 
even during the initial stages of the disease. In detail, even short durations of 
hyperglycemia induce activation of NADPH oxidase which in turn elevates the cytosolic 
levels of reactive oxygen species. Over the course of time, there is a more evident 
increase in ROS levels leading to an opening of the mitochondrial-permeability transition 
pores which in turn alters the mitochondrial membrane potential. As a result, 
mitochondria become dysfunctional and cytochrome c leaks into the cytosol where it 
binds apoptotic protease activating factor-1 (Apaf-1) and activates the apoptotic 
machinery. As apoptosis is induced, capillary cells begin to undergo apoptosis which is a 
typical histological feature of diabetic tissues. (Kowluru 2005; Kowluru and Abbas 2003; 
Mizutani, Kern, and Lorenzi 1996), (Kowluru et al. 2014, 2015) 
The etiology behind the connection between MMP expression and mitochondrial 
dysfunction is the activation of RO species. It has been shown that MMP activation and 
specifically gelatinases, is negatively regulated by ROS (elevated ROS-increased MMPs) 
which is an event that takes place before mitochondria are damaged. In a similar fashion 
as ROS species, gelatinases with the help of heat shock proteins translocate into the 
mitochondria and they damage their membranes. Mitochondria become dysfunctional 
and start an apoptotic cycle. A suggested model of MMP induced mitochondrial 
apoptosis, relies on MMP-2 and MMP-9 upregulation. Particularly, hyperglycemia-
induced overexpression of gelatinases, increases superoxide accumulation and leads to 
cytochrome c leakage in the cytosol and apoptosis of the cells. In support, MT1-MMP 
which is involved in MMP-2 expression is reported to sensitize endothelial cells to 
apoptosis by caspase-3 activation, which can be abrogated by Bcl-2, suggesting once 
more the involvement of MMPs in the mitochondrial pathway. (Mohammad and 
Kowluru 2010a) (Mohammad and Kowluru 2010b, 2011) 
Inflammation 
Another state that contributes to the development of diabetic retinopathy is 
inflammation. In fact, DR has in common various features with other inflammatory

diseases. Most significant of them are the increased levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines 
and chemokines in both the retina and its surrounding vasculature, and leukocytosis 
which is an abnormal increase in leukocytes and is commonly related to inflammatory 
responses. MMPs role in in inflammation is implicated in its regulation and can serve as a 
switch between acute and chronic inflammation. Remarkably, MMPs have the ability to 
cleave several members of the monocyte chemoattractant protein (MCP) family of 
chemokines which are ubiquitously expressed in diabetic complications. Therefore, 
MMPs are characterized as proactive molecules of inflammation. The mechanism 
responsible for MMPs-mediated increase in inflammatory cytokines could include 
invasiveness of immune system cells to the injured tissue due to degradation of a major 
component of the basement membrane. It has also been shown that MMPs induce the 
recruitment of abnormal levels of leukocytes to retinal capillaries which become 
ischemic. Ischemic retinal capillaries are characteristic of inflammatory responses and can 
be observed early in DR tissues. What is more, diabetic environment creates hypoxia, and 
the expression of VEGF is considered to be a response to hypoxic conditions. HIF-1α-
mediated secretion of VEGF from hypoxic Muller cells (glial cells supporting retinal 
cells) is shown to upregulate and activate both gelatinases and in particular MMP-2 in 
adjacent endothelial cells. Thus, there appears to be a strong relationship between MMPs 
and inflammation in the development of diabetic retinopathy. (Hu et al. 2007; Joussen 
2004; Kern 2007a) 
 
Neovascularization 
In the late stages of DR, following the thickening of the basement membrane and the 
loss of pericytes and endothelial cells, neovascularization is observed and new vessels 
appear in the surrounding environment. These collateral blood vessels are fragile and 
bleed easily which results in vitreous hemorrhage and if not treated to retinal 
detachment. MMPs and specifically gelatinases play a pivotal role in neovascularization. 
To begin with, MMPs degrade the basement membrane of capillaries which causes the 
penetration of endothelial cells in the subendothelial matrix and the eventual formation 
of a new lumen. Active forms of MMP-2 and MMP-9 are significantly elevated in the 
neovascular membranes of patients with proliferative diabetic retinopathy, further 
suggesting their involvement in neovascularization. Moreover, MMP-9 can also mediate 
the release of VEGF, one of the most important angiogenic and growth factors, which 
also contributes in the development of new blood vessels.(HOFFMANN et al. 2006; 
Kern 2007b; Rodrigues et al. 2013) 
 
However, MMPs seem to have dual role in the development of diabetic retinopathy. As 
described in a previous section, during the initial stages of DR (pre-neovascularization), 
they induce accelerated apoptosis of retinal capillary cells, making retinal 
microvasculature low in endothelial cells and pericytes, and in later stages they assist in 
new vessel formation relying on mechanisms described in this section. (Kowluru, Zhong, 
and Santos 2012)

Regulation of MMPs in Diabetic Retinopathy 
As described earlier, there are a number of possible pathways by which MMPs and 
specifically gelatinases are involved in the development of diabetic retinopathy. MMP-2 
and MMP-9 are involved in the majority of the steps of this disease and thus, it is crucial 
to get a better understanding of their regulatory effects on the diabetic environment and 
the tissue homeostasis. 
Cell signalling 
The MAPK pathway constitutes one of the biggest and most involved pathways in the 
regulation of cell proliferation, differentiation, and apoptosis. The physiological function 
of the pathway relies on the transmission of extracellular signals into their intracellular 
targets by a network of cross-reacting proteins and multiple extracellular signals. Two of 
its signalling cascades namely Ras/Rac/JNK and Ras/Raf/ERK can be activated by 
exposure to a range of different stimuli and stressful conditions, depending on a 
phosphorylation cascade. The Ras-Raf cascade in particular, is involved in the regulation 
of a range of different transcriptional factors such as c-Jun, AP1 and NF-κB which in 
diabetic complications, mediate the apoptotic machinery of cells depending on ROS-
induced biochemical alterations, implying in this way its contribution in the pathology of 
the diabetic retinopathy. (Mohammad and Siddiquei 2012) 
 
Notably, diabetic complications activate a small molecular weight G-protein, H-Ras, in 
retinal capillary cells and induces apoptosis. It was shown that H-Ras regulate gelatinases 
expression and thus, the activation of this small G-protein is associated with increased 
vascular permeability. In a similar fashion, activation of PI3K/Akt/mTOR pathway is 
involved in the early development of diabetic retinopathy by regulating invasiveness in 
the early stages of the disease. The other hyperglycemia-induced regulators of MMP-2 
and -9 are protein kinase C (PKC), transforming growth factor-β, the renin-angiotensin-
aldosterone system and AGEs. Collectively, these factors regulate MMP-2 and -9 
inductions in diabetic retinopathy.  (Irschick et al. 2009; Kowluru 2010; Kowluru et al. 
2004)

DNA sequence.  Such modifications take place at a specific position of the chromatin, 
mediated by intracellular signaling pathways which are usually responsive to an 
environmental stimulus. Most important of them are DNA methylations, histone 
modifications and noncoding micro-RNAs (miRNAs). (Reddy, Zhang, and Natarajan 
2014)

Liposomes through history 
Liposomes were initially described by A.D Bangham in 1961 following his observations 
that phospholipids when dispersed in water can spontaneously form spherical, self-closed 
structures consisting of one or several lipid bilayers which in turn encapsulate an aqueous 
core. These lipid vesicles were initially called ‘smectic mesophases’ and later renamed as 
liposomes. The first liposomal formulations were composed solely of natural lipids while 
nowadays they can include natural and/or synthetic lipids and surfactants. The 
resemblance of the lamellar structure of liposomes with the natural membranes, their 
capability to discriminate ions (cations diffuse poorly from membranes that are 
permeable to univalent anions and water) and their susceptibility to stabilization or 
destabilization by bioactive molecules similar to biological membranes, have rendered 
liposomes a versatile tool in the field of biology, biochemistry, and medicine The ability 
of the vesicles to swell osmotically, the possibility to vary membrane composition and 
surface potential, have rendered liposomes early since their discovery a preferred lipid 
matrix model of living cells. (Sessa and Weissmann 1968; Sharma and Sharma 1997) 
After their introduction as an artificial model of living cells, research done in this field 
has paved the way for exploiting additional features of liposomal formulations.  With the 
recognition of their biocompatibility, biodegradability, low toxicity and immunogenicity, 
and the capability to entrap molecules liposomes have eventually moved a long way from 
being just another object of biophysical research to becoming a pharmaceutical carrier of 
choice for numerous practical applications. The initial excitement of liposomes as drug 
delivery vehicles was, however, short lived due to insufficient understanding of liposome 
disposition and clearance in vivo, pronounced instability and reduced circulation time of 
liposome-based drugs in vivo. Substantial advances in the late 1980s and early 1990s, 
including detailed understanding of lipid polymorphisms, physiological mechanisms of in 
vivo liposome disposition, and lipid–drug and lipid–protein interactions, overcame many 
of the early disappointments. However, the major breakthrough came with the 
realization that steric stabilization can significantly increase liposome stability and 
prolong their persistence in the blood circulation system after administration. This 
discovery, resulted in the development of tailor-made liposomal formulations with 
increased stability both in vitro and in vivo, improved biodistribution, and optimized 
resident time in systemic (or blood) circulation. (Immordino, Dosio, and Cattel 2006; 
Lian and Ho 2001; Torchilin 2005)

Classification of liposomes 
Liposomes can generally be divided into categories based on their size (small, 
intermediate, or large) and their lamellarity (uni-, oligo-, and multilamellar vesicles). 
Briefly, liposomal vesicles are separated into small unilamellar vesicles SUVs (size < 200 
nm and single bilayer, large unilamellar vesicles (LUVs, size 200–1000 nm and single 
bilayer), giant unilamellar vesicles (GUVs, size > 1000 nm and single bilayer), 
multilamellar vesicles (MLVs, consisting of several concentric bilayers) and multivesicular 
vesicles (MVVs, composed of several small vesicles entrapped into larger ones) (Fig. 13).  
 
 
Size and lamellarity of liposomal formulations have been extensively manipulated when 
designing multifunctional systems used in the field of drug delivery. However, certain 
subclasses and their unique characteristics have rendered them favorable when compared 
with others. In particular, multifunctional systems should be characterized by high 
homogeneity in size and structure. For this reason, SUVs have attracted great attention 
since they have the right composition. They are big enough to avoid the rapid clearance 
through the kidneys and small enough to present a minimal recognition by the 
components of the immune system and in particular uptake by phagocytes. Therefore, 
they facilitate longer circulation lifetime in the body and hence a higher possibility of 
reaching the target cells. What is more, SUVs when compared to MLVs or MVVs, allow 
a more accessible and homogeneous membrane functionalization and this finally a swift 
response of the system to an external stimulus. (Grimaldi et al. 2016), (Allen and Cullis 
2004)

MMP-sensitive liposomes 
Liposomes have contributed greatly in the development of the field of drug delivery. 
Being capable of incorporating various targeting compounds on their membranes such as 
proteins, peptides, sugar molecules and antibodies, they can be used in designing targeted 
therapies. Such stimuli-sensitive liposomes, function by responding to local stimuli 
inducing the release of treatment moieties at the targeted site. Acidic pH, abnormal levels 
of enzymes, temperature, altered redox potential, and external magnetic field are 
examples of internal and external stimuli exploited in the design of stimuli-sensitive 
liposomes. The penetration of the liposomes into the cells can be further enhanced with 
the help of a variety of cell penetrating peptides or alternatively with the alteration of its 
physicochemical properties such as the surface charge. (Aryasomayajula, Salzano, and 
Torchilin 2017) 
 
In vivo, the design of liposomal formulations intended for the treatment of cancer and 
autoimmune disorders are characterized by short half-life and uptake by cells of the 
immune system. Therefore, long systemic circulation allowing accumulation of the 
nanoparticles in diseased tissues is essential for an efficient drug delivery system. Coating 
of liposomes with hydrophilic polymer poly (ethylene glycol)-PEG can achieve this. 
Particularly, PEG coating minimizes binding of serum proteins, uptake by non-targeted 
cells and eliminates recognition by components of the immune system.  However, a 
major drawback of liposomal PEGylation is limited uptake in targeted cells and hindering 
of endosomal escape following uptake, which is caused mainly by the negative charge of 
the PEG chain. In an attempt to solve this PEGylation dilemma, various strategies have 
been designed to trigger dePEGylation at the site of interest. Conjugation of the PEG 
chain to the liposomes through removable linkers such as disulfide bonds, pH sensitive 
and esterase sensitive groups, are some of them. One class of enzymes that is particularly 
attractive for in situ activation of drug delivery systems, MMPs, which regulate various 
cell behaviors, including cell growth, differentiation, apoptosis, migration, invasion in 
both physiological and pathological conditions. While MMP activity needs tight 
regulation in healthy tissue, the degradation of the surrounding extracellular matrix is 
necessary for the uncontrolled cell growth observed in tumours and diabetic 
complications. Therefore, a MMP-cleavable peptide that will take advantage of the 
plethora of MMP molecules present at the diseased region, will act as a linker between 
PEG and the nanoparticle and will trigger dePEGylation of the nanocarrier upon arrival 
to the targeted site. (Bruun et al. 2015)

Self-assembly of liposomes 
As previously described, phospholipids are capable of spontaneously aggregating into 
liposomes, a characteristic attributed to the dual preference of amphiphilic compounds 
for solvents. Since the hydrophobic and hydrophilic features of amphiphiles show a 
preference of solubility in nonpolar and polar solvents respectively, the presence of water 
cause self-assembly above a certain a concentration in a manner that minimizes 
unfavorable hydrophobic interactions. (Finney and Soper 1994)  
 
 
 
Self-assembly (Fig. 16) should be characterized by favorable entropy which relies on 
water–hydrocarbon interactions that force water molecules into an ordered structure 
around the hydrophobic parts of amphiphilic compounds when they are dispersed freely 
as monomers. Ordered water molecules are released, an interaction driven by the egress 
of the hydrophobic parts out of the aqueous solution and their isolation within the 
interior of the aggregate. The resulting increase in entropy leads to an overall gain in free 
energy and eventually causes spontaneous aggregation. The spontaneous aggregation of 
the phospholipids into bilayer structures is not only determined by the hydrophobic 
contribution mentioned earlier but also related to the molecular parameters of the 
amphiphile. The aggregate structure for amphiphiles is predicted by the “surfactant 
parameter” (S), which in turn takes into account parameters such as the hydrophobic 
volume, chain length, and headgroup area that contain information about the geometrical 
shape of the molecule. The surfactant parameter is defined as follows: (Blokzijl and 
Engberts 1993), (Kamaly and Miller 2010)

length of the hydrocarbon chains, and the effective area per headgroup, respectively. The 
value of the surfactant parameter relates the properties of the molecule to the mean 
curvature of the formed aggregates. Small values of S imply highly curved aggregates

Zymography 
Evaluation of the expression of matrix metalloproteinases in this study, was carried out 
by employing the technique of gelatin zymography. Briefly, zymography and its reverse 
phase (reverse zymography) are two similar semi-quantitative techniques used to 
analyze the activities of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and their respective tissue 
inhibitors (TIMPs). Zymography is based on the electrophoretic separation of proteins 
under denaturating but non-reducing conditions (SDS), ran through a polyacrylamide 
gel which contains specific proteolytic substrates for the MMPs of interest. Proteins are 
renatured by the exchange of the SDS with a non-ionic detergent (Triton X-100) and 
the gel is then incubated in a Ca2+ and Zn2+-containing buffer optimized for measuring 
MMP activity toward the specific substrate. After a series of staining and destaining 
steps, further explained in, proteolytic activities are detected against regions of 
degraded substrate. Visually, this means that white clear bands are going to be formed 
in the areas of the zymogram that the substrate was successfully degraded. Comparison 
of the location of the gelatinolytic activity with molecular weight standards run 
simultaneously on the same gel should help identifying the specific MMP involved. 
The success of zymography being used as a tool to study the expression of MMPs 
relies on several features. The fact that proteolytic substrates are retained in the gel 
during electrophoresis enables them to functionalize as an in-situ protease substrate. 
Moreover, proteolytic activity can be reversibly inhibited by SDS during electrophoresis 
and recovered by incubating the gel in an aqueous Triton X-100 buffer and so, 
proteolysis is postponed until the sample proteins have been resolved into bands of 
concentrated activity. The separation of MMP: TIMP complexes by SDS 
polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis enables their activities to be determined 
independently of one another, which is not possible in solution assays. A particular 
advantage of this system is that both the proenzyme and active forms of MMPs, which 
can be distinguished on the basis of molecular weight, can be detected. This is possible

Initially described in 1953, flow cytometry was used as a cell size detection tool. Since

fluorescent features of cells and particles such as microorganisms, nuclei and 
chromosome preparations. Out of the wide range of parameters that can be detected, 
size, granularity and fluorescence derived from either antibodies or dyes, are the most 
significant. (Wilkerson 2012)

Fluorochromes are used in a wide range of applications such as identification of different

Handling procedures of cell cultures 
Seeding out cells 
Handling procedure when seeding out cells in flasks, dishes or well plates may vary 
between different cell lines. HT–1080 and ARPE–19 cell lines have the same 
specifications and thus, the process is identical. DMEM-high glucose and DMEM-F12 
culture medium supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin/streptomycin, were used 
respectively. Initially, prepare the bench and the necessary utensils (medium, flasks, 
centrifuge tubes) before bringing the cells in the lab.  Thaw vials in a 37°C water bath. 
Thawing should be rapid (approximately 2 minutes).  Transfer vial contents to a 15 mL 
centrifuge tube containing 9 mL complete culture medium and spin at approximately 
1000 rpm for 5 minutes and aspirate supernatant. Resuspend cell pellet with 10 mL of 
warm complete medium. It is suggested that, prior to the addition of the vial contents, 
the culture vessel containing the complete growth medium be placed into the incubator 
for at least 15 minutes to allow the medium to reach its normal pH (7.0 to 7.6). Incubate 
the culture at 37°C in a suitable incubator. A 5% CO2 in air atmosphere of the incubator 
is recommended.  
 
Passaging cells 
The following protocol was designed for the HT-1080 and ARPE-19 cell lines. Volumes 
were adjusted for T75 culture flasks and 6-well plates.  
 
Start with microscopy to detect level of cell adhesion. In conditions of low adhesion, 
media renewal could be a good option for the cells, to supply them with more nutrition. 
If a high density of adhesion is present, splitting-passaging cells should be the most 
appropriate choice.  Aspirate media and wash with PBS. Mix well, by tipping/cradling 
the flask gently from side to side, just so the liquid distributes everywhere. Volume used, 
should be half of the volume of the medium used for the culture. Remove PBS and add 
trypsin, usually depending on the size of the flask (1ml per 25 cm2) and mix thoroughly. 
Incubate the cells for 5 minutes. Check if trypsinization was effective by using the 
microscope. The cells should be detached from the bottom surface of the culture flask.

You can help this process on its way by giving the culture flask a gentle hit of the side,

Preparation of cell lysates using detergents 
Place the cell culture container on ice and aspirate culture medium. Wash the cells with 
ice-cold PBS. Aspirate PBS, then add ice-cold lysis buffer (NP-40 or Triton X-100) that 
contains 1X protease inhibitors (approximately 200uL per well for 6-well plate 
formation). Scrape adherent cells off the dish using a plastic cell scraper, then gently 
transfer the cell suspension into a pre-cooled microcentrifuge tube. Alternatively, cells 
can be trypsinized and washed with PBS prior to resuspension in lysis buffer in a 
microcentrifuge tube. Incubate on ice for 15 min. Centrifuge in a microcentrifuge at 4°C 
for 30 min at 14,000 rpm. You may have to vary the centrifugation force and time 
depending on the cell type. Gently remove the tubes from the centrifuge and place on 
ice, aspirate the supernatant and place in a fresh tube kept on ice, and discard the pellet.  
 
Preparation of cell lysates by nitrogen cavitation 
Wash the cells with ice-cold PBS two to three times. Detach adherent cells with a cell 
scraper in ice-cold salt buffer containing 1X protease inhibitors. The volume of the 
buffer depends on the area of surface of the cell container. Transfer in a falcon tube. 
Incubate on ice 15 minutes. Centrifuge for 5 minutes at 300 x g, 4oC. Discard 
supernatant. Resuspend pellet into 800-1000 ul of ice-cold salt buffer containing 1X 
protease inhibitors. Proceed with putting samples in the ‘nitrogen bomb’ instrument and 
increase pressure slowly. Release pressure valve slowly and let the pressure drop. 
Transfer samples in a falcon tube. Centrifuge for 25 minutes at max speed, at 4oC. 
Collect supernatant and mix with loading buffer. Resuspend pellet with ice-cold complete 
lysing buffer containing protease inhibitors. Centrifuge for 15 minutes at max speed, 4oC. 
Collect supernatant and mix with loading buffer. Discard pellet. 
 
Preparation of conditioned media  
Place the cell culture container on ice. Aspirate cell culture medium and transfer to a pre-
cooled microcentrifuge tube. Incubate on ice for 15 min. Centrifuge in a microcentrifuge 
at 4°C for 5 min at 14,000 rpm. You may have to vary the centrifugation force and time 
depending on the cell type. Gently remove the tubes from the centrifuge and place on 
ice, aspirate the supernatant and place in a fresh tube kept on ice, and discard the pellet.  
 
Gel electrophoresis

described methods. If cell lysates were used, a BCA protein quantitative measurement is 
required in order to find out the protein concentration of each sample. Samples are then

respective protein levels. In addition to loading samples in the wells, loading of protein 
ladder to one or two separate wells is required which helps in identifying the molecular 
weight of the investigated proteins. Electrophoresis of gels takes place in 125V for 1-2 
hours. Among the different protein separation methods (zymography, western blotting 
etc.) variations exist in the used consumables and the steps following gel electrophoresis.

Results & Discussion 
In the initial stages of the project the main focus was to successfully induce MMP-
expression in ARPE-19 cells. As a pigment epithelium cell line, it is directly associated 
with diabetic retinopathy and if expression of MMP could be successfully induced under 
certain conditions and under the effect of specific stimulants, it could help identifying the 
MMP landscape in vitro. While animal studies provide a realistic option for protein 
expression, in vitro artificial systems are also important. In vivo systems are really 
complex and thus, it makes it difficult to identify the interactions between individual 
components and to explore their basic biological functions. In vitro work simplifies the 
system under study, so the investigator can focus on a small number of components. 
They permit a species-specific, simpler, more convenient, and more detailed analysis. 
(Gaffney et al. 2015) 
Initial experiments were focused around testing MMP expression in whole cell lysates of 
both cell lines. Supposedly, as a cancer line HT-1080 cells should vastly express 
gelatinases and thus, no stimulants were used, and no serum starvation was induced. On 
the other hand, ARPE-19 is not a malignant cell line and the usage of hyperglycemia 
associated stimulants was necessary. Therefore, HT-1080 cells served as a positive 
control that were just grown to confluency, lysed and then used in gelatin zymography 
experiments, while ARPE-19 cells before being used in such experiments were treated 
with inflammation/hyperglycemia stimulants that based on the literature would induce 
conditions similar to those of diabetic retinopathy (Coral et al. 2013; Roomi et al. 2017). 
In particular, ARPE-19 cells were grown to confluency and then treated with 25mM 
glucose and a range of different PMA concentrations alongside with their respective 
controls. Gelatin zymography results however, (Fig. 22) showed no expression of MMPs. 
A great deal of effort was put into producing cell lysates using detergents that will 
contain MMPs. Despite trying a whole series of different detergents (Triton X-100 and 
NP-40) and different lysing solutions the problem of no detectable MMP expression in 
both cell lines remained. Therefore, we

Results from nitrogen cavitation 
In an attempt to tackle the challenges of shear/detergent-based methods, an alternative 
and milder way of lysing cells was introduced. In detail, placing cells in a suitable high 
pressurized vessel that contains large amounts of oxygen free nitrogen results in the 
dissolution of the nitrogen into the cells. Releasing the pressure suddenly, forces nitrogen 
to bubble out of the solution and induces rupturing of the cell membrane and releasing 
of the cell components. This technique is called nitrogen cavitation and minimizes 
chemical and physical stresses that enzymes and subcellular compartments undergo 
during lysis. Lysing cells by nitrogen decompression is a rapid and effective way to 
homogenize cells and tissues, to release intact organelles, and to prepare cell membranes. 
Nitrogen cavitation is suitable for mammalian cells, fragile bacteria and generally 
organisms that are not protected by the rigid outer membrane layers that can be found in 
fungi spores and yeast. Additionally, it protects proteins and organelles from heat damage 
as the samples are kept cool during the whole process. (Simpson 2010) Therefore, any 
challenges of cell lysis experienced with detergents-oriented approaches until this point, 
should supposedly be overcome.

Western blot results from ‘nitrogen bomb’ cell lysates (Fig 23 & 24), showed expression

lacking signal. These findings arise two questions regarding the challenges experienced in 
the previous experiments. Either the MMPs that were detected with Western blot, were 
in a latent form that could not be detected in the zymogram or the signal bands 
correspond to fractions of the overall MMP structures (the antibodies used, detect the 
whole amino acid sequence of MMPs) as indicated by the slightly lower molecular weight 
of the bands. It should be pointed out that the active forms of MMP-2 and MMP-9 are 
identified at 63kDA and 82kDa, respectively. (Ren, Chen, and Khalil 2017) Taken into 
consideration the results from both detergent-based methods and nitrogen cavitation, we 
concluded that in cell lysates (intracellularly and on the cell membrane) gelatinases are 
expressed but they are in an inactive from that cannot be detected by gelatin zymography 
and that the signal bands taken from immunoblotting are representative of individual 
parts of the enzymes. 
 
Results from conditioned medium 
In spite of the fact that we managed to address the challenge of MMP expression in 
general, the problem of identifying their active forms with gelatin zymography was still 
evident. Bearing in mind that the whole project and the in particular the construction of 
stimuli responsive liposomes, relies on the cleavage of an MMP sensitive lipopeptide 
chain by the active forms of MMP-2 and MMP-9, it is obvious that their identification is 
crucial. As described in a previous section, MMPs are enzymes involved in the 
degradation of components of the extracellular matrix meaning that following their 
expression, they are excreted to the surrounding environment where they start to display 
their proteolytic activities. Therefore, a different approach was introduced in the 
experiments which relied on probing for MMPs in cell culture supernatants. These 
changes would eventually, improve our understanding for both MMP expression and 
their regulation based on their localization and existence in different timepoints of the 
cell cycle. 
 
The introduction of serum starvation to the cell cultures prior to any treatment with 
stimulants and protein separation methods, was necessary for having a successful 
outcome. First of all, it is obvious that supernatants of cell cultures need to be free of 
serum as it contains a variety of different proteins and enzymes. These components of 
serum may cross react with the excreted MMPs and induce their inhibition and/or their 
degradation. Moreover, by removing serum and its components, cells are kept in a 
condition that they are unable to proliferate and are synchronized in the same timepoint 
of the cell cycle. (Chen et al. 2012) Finally, lacking their source of nutrients, cells produce 
ubiquitously stress-related enzymes, such as MMPs.

Results taken from these experiments (Fig. 25) were in accordance with our proposed 
extracellular fate of MMPs. After a series of successful zymograms that showed MMP-2 
expression in culture supernatants for both cell lines, it was now suggested that MMPs 
when found intracellularly or on the cell membrane are still in a latent form that cannot 
be detected by gelatin zymography. However, expression of enzymes and their 
subsequent activation is not an absolute one-way interaction. The versatility of MMP 
possible activation mechanisms and the observed nuances of enzyme kinetics in vivo, 
provide further proof that the evolution of this class of enzymes has led to each 
individual member requiring specific conditions for its activation, a feature difficult to 
identify in the artificial in vitro conditions (Gaffney et al. 2015). One possible explanation 
of our inability to detect them in cell lysates, is the existence of an expression related 
equilibrium that totally inhibits or induces degradation of MMPs located intracellularly, 
after their active forms have been excreted in the surrounding environment. 
 
Being the turning point of our project, cell culture supernatants were solely used for the 
rest of the experiments. However, a range of conditions still needed to be investigated in 
order to determine the exact effect of different stimulants and the effect of the duration 
of serum starvation cell cultures. The next series of experiments were focused around 
inducing expression of MMP-9, which was absent until now, initially in HT-1080 and 
then in ARPE-19 cells. For this reason, PMA was further investigated as it has been 
shown that it can induce the expression of gelatinase B. Moreover, different confluency 
levels were tested and a more rational approach regarding the duration of serum 
starvation was followed. In detail, serum starvation arrests cells in the G0/G1 stage of 
the cell cycle. (Khammanit et al. 2008) This synchronizing effect however, depends on 
the duration of serum depletion which if it lasts more than the duration of a complete 
cell cycle has deleterious effects on cell survival leading to massive DNA fragmentation 
and other apoptotic related events. Therefore, is was agreed that each cell line was going 
to be treated in serum free conditions for half of the duration of their complete cell cycle 
(ARPE-19 24hours and HT-1080/12hours).

Results from liposomal measurements 
Determination of the zeta potential (charge) and size of the liposomal formulations used 
in this report, was crucial as they were exclusively designed for drug delivery systems. As 
mentioned earlier, nanocarriers exhibited a charge reversal that was taking advantage of 
the conditions of the diseased areas to induce enhanced cell uptake. In detail, 
formulations that contained the stimuli responsive lipopeptides (PCL-21) displayed a 
negative charge while for obvious reasons control liposomes (PEGylated stealth-like) 
presented a slightly positive /neutral charge, a needful difference for the introduction of 
cell uptake experiments.  
Being designed for the treatment of diabetic retinopathy liposomal formulations are 
needed to be injected to the CGN of the vitreous. The porosity of this network varies 
form 500 nm to 1μm and thus, it is safely concluded that the prepared formulations will 
be capable of diffusing between the pores of the CGN. (Table 4) Additionally, the CGN 
has been attributed a net negative charge due to the electronegativity of its 
glycosaminoglycans. Therefore, nanocarriers with positive charge and high cationic zeta 
potentials display strong electrostatic interactions with the pores and as a result they are 
being trapped and not diffused. (Eriksen et al. 2017)

Results from flow cytometry 
Experiencing a series of failing experiments in the initial stages of the project, resulted in 
committing a lot of time in trying to overcome the challenges that arose. Succeeding and 
reaching the initial goals, allowed the initiation of cell uptake experiments. In particular, 
cell uptake of MMP-sensitive liposomes and its regulation through different formulations 
was investigated.  
 
As shown in the materials and methods section, two different variations of liposomal 
formulations were prepared for the following experiments. In particular, the MMP-
sensitive liposomes contained the PCL-21 lipopeptide, that would take advantage of the 
excreted MMPs to induce cleavage of the outer leaflet, charge reversal and eventually cell 
uptake. The Pegylated stealth-like liposomes were designed to be used as the MMP-
cleavage control samples and thus, were expected to retain their lamellar composition 
and exhibit no charge turnover. The initial strategy followed, was relying on a 4 hours 
incubation of liposomes directly with the cell cultures that were in the process of serum 
starvation which as shown before, induces the production of MMPs. Incubation of the 
theoretically now cleaved by cell excreted MMPs liposomes with the cells was necessary 
to check if they are being actively uptaken. The expected mechanism relies on the fact 
that after the cleavage of the sensitive lipopeptide by MMPs, liposomes will reveal their 
cationic inner layer and will turn positively charged leading to enhanced interaction with 
the negatively charged cell membrane and eventually to cell uptake. On the other hand, it 
was expected that the PEGylated stealth-like control liposomes being neutrally or slightly 
positively charged, would show nugatory or minimum uptake.  
 
Results obtained (Fig. 32), showed minimum uptake of the MMP-cleavable liposomes 
while the PEGylated presented an uptake percentage of around 80%. While these results 
are completely reversed against the expected ones, it can be relatively easily explained 
what was neglected in the overall design of the experiment. Bearing in mind that the 
PEGylated liposomes are neutrally charged and that we investigated their cell uptake in 
an in vitro artificial system where they are practically ‘standing’ on top of the cells for 
around 4 hours and not circulating as happening in vivo, it is obvious that at same point 
they will eventually be taken up by the cells. At the same time, it was shown that MMP-

HT-1080 media exposure 
A novel approach of inducing charge reversal of liposomes was introduced by incubating 
the liposomal formulations with MMP-containing supernatants for 24 hours, taken by 
serum depleted cell cultures. As determined by the gelatin zymography experiments, 
these supernatants contained large amounts of MMP-2 and MMP-9 that should be 
sufficient for the induction of cleavage. In contrast to the direct incubation of the 
liposomes with the cell cultures, this approach exploits the full benefits offered by serum 
starvation without exceeding the time limit of a complete cell cycle and thus, the 
concentration of MMPs is more than enough to successfully cleave the MMP-sensitive 
chain. What is more, as shown by the thermolysin-induced cleavage, MMP-sensitive 
liposomes require longer incubation times to effectively reverse the charge. Briefly, the 
process includes incubation of liposomes for 24 hours in serum free - MMP containing 
medium that has been extracted from cell cultures, at 37oC, and then the 
MMP/liposomes solutions are added to the cell cultures for 4 hours to check their 
uptake status. Tested initially in HT-1080 cell cultures, this technique was found to be 
really successful and was later applied to ARPE-19 cells, as well.  
 
Results obtained from the Ht-1080 cell line, depict that improvising on an existing 
protocol can sometimes be really beneficious. Cell uptake for the PCL-21 constructs is 
immensely increased compared to the regular method of cleaving liposomes as shown in 
Fig 34,35. Control PEGylated liposomes still demonstrate increased uptake compared to 
the PCL-21 liposomes but this is as explained above, is a consequence of working with 
2D in vitro cell cultures and is not a matter of low MMPs concentration.

maximum uptake and immense fluorescent intensity compared to any other samples. 
This observation highlights the potency of thermolysin as an enzyme. Despite being 
characterized as a standard control for all the experiments that involve MMPs, the usage 
of thermolysin comes with disadvantages as well. Its extreme potency and wide range of 
possible substrates that it reacts with, is usually accompanied with increased cytotoxicity 
mainly cause by disruption of the cell membrane components. Additionally, bearing in 
mind that in this experiment fewer cells were ‘gated’ as alive, it is implied that 
thermolysin on the one hand is demonstrating a rapid and effective cleavage of the 
liposomal formulations but at the same time it promotes cell apoptosis. Therefore, future 
studies on the field that rely on the involvement of thermolysin or similar potent 
enzymes in cell-related experiments (i.e. flow cytometry) should be accompanied with the 
addition of toxicity controls. Moreover, the combination of thermolysin with the 
PEGylated stealth-like liposomes as well, would be beneficial in an attempt to define if 
this enzyme can have identical effects on the control samples as it does on the MMP-
sensitive liposomes. Following this strategy, would greatly contribute in determining the 
adverse effects of this family of enzymes on cell biology and eventually in the design of 
potentially successful strategies.

Conclusion 
In conclusion, the MMP-2 and MMP-9 expression profile in retinal pigment epithelium 
and human fibrosarcoma cells, was identified and all the problems experienced during 
the process were successfully overcome with extensive research done on the field and the 
applications of various protein separation methods such as gelatin zymography and 
immunoblotting. Additionally, it was shown that both cell lines were successfully 
stimulating the conditions of diabetic retinopathy by producing immense amounts of 
gelatinases. 
Liposomal formulations, PCL-21 and stealth-like PEGylated liposomes, were 
successfully prepared and displayed expected values in regard of their size and zeta 
potential. In particular, they presented a size average of 100 nm and charge respective to 
their lipids composition (negatively and neutrally charged, respectively). 
Furthermore, the MMP sensitive delivery system was shown to be able to undergo 
enzymatic processing and promote a charge reversal that was necessary for enhanced 
cellular association in vitro. Determination of its ability to be effectively uptaken by cells 
was achieved with the aid of flow cytometry method that initially contributed in 
discriminating dead from alive populations and cell uptake percentage by comparing the 
fluorescence intensity between MMP-sensitive and control samples.

Future perspectives 
There are several lines of research arising from this work which should be pursued, as 
different tests and experiments have been left as future work due to lack of time and 
arisen challenges. Despite having successfully determined the exact conditions required 
for MMP identification, extensive research is necessary to further determine their cellular 
fate and the regulation of their production. While protein separation methods are handy 
for the quantification of active molecules, they are not sufficient for determining the 
impact of other proteins and cell signalling on the expression and production of MMPs. 
Application of RNA sequencing techniques and studies on the effects of serum 
starvation on the cell cycle, might provide with further insight on these issues by studying 
protein availability from the level of gene expression. 
Although enzyme responsive liposomes have been successfully developed and tested in 
vitro, the translation into clinical studies is lacking. The complex design, sophisticated 
pharmaceutical development, and difficulties in scale-up make the translation of enzyme 
responsive liposomes from bench to bedside challenging. In addition, the efficacy of 
enzyme responsive liposomes, similar to other endogenous stimuli-responsive 
nanocarriers, may be different from one patient to another due to different levels of the 
trigger at the target tissue. Therefore, future studies need to address the applicability and 
effectiveness of these novel formulations in treatment approaches. 
Completion of the designed drug delivery system also requires the addition of drug 
agents that would either be encapsulated in the liposomal core or trapped in the lipid 
bilayer. Studies related to drug encapsulation efficiency, bioavailability and treatment 
potency and efficiency, are needed in order to identify the most suitable drug moieties in 
ameliorating the disease symptoms.
