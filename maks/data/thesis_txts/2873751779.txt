Abstract
Transmission Expansion Planning (TEP) plays a critical role in ensuring the long-term reliability,
efficiency, and economic viability of power systems. It involves strategic decisions about where
and when to invest in new transmission infrastructure to accommodate growing electricity demand,
the integration of renewable energy sources, and evolving network constraints. Traditionally, TEP
has been addressed through large-scale optimisation models that account for both investment
and operational decisions under a range of technical, economic, and regulatory constraints. These
models often solve the problem as a sequence of static sub-problems. However, when uncertainty
in future demand, generation availability, and renewable output is explicitly considered, stochas-
tic TEP formulations—particularly multi-stage models—offer a more realistic and robust planning
framework over a given decision horizon. These models typically rely on scenario trees to rep-
resent possible future states of the system, aiming to minimise the expected total cost across all
scenarios.
However, the computational burden associated with solving large-scale multi-stage stochastic TEP
problems grows exponentially with the number of scenarios and planning stages, often rendering
them impractical for real-world applications. To address this issue, recent research explores the
integration of Machine Learning (ML) techniques within the TEP modelling framework. ML offers
promising avenues to reduce computational requirements by learning to approximate complex
operational decisions as surrogate models, embedded within mathematical programming formu-
lations.
This work investigates such a hybrid ML-optimisation framework for stochastic TEP, focusing on
multi-stage formulations that capture temporal dependencies and long-term planning flexibility. Dif-
ferent ML architectures, feature representations, and training strategies were analysed to deter-
mine the affect on quality and feasibility of the resulting expansion plans. The findings demonstrate
that, when properly integrated, ML models can approximate key components of the TEP problem
fastly with high accuracy, leading to substantial reduction in solution time while maintaining close-
to-optimal performance.
This work contributes to bridging the gap between classical optimisation and data-driven learning
methods in power system planning, offering practical tools for managing uncertainty in large-scale,
real-world transmission networks. Specifically, it provides an end-to-end framework—from gen-
erating training data to testing the resulting model—using neural networks as surrogate models
in stochastic TEP formulations. This approach demonstrates notable computational advantages,
while also highlighting potential limitations in its general applicability across different systems.
Keywords : Machine Learning, Neural Networks, Power system planning, Stochastic Expansion
Problem

Introduction
The green transition, referring to the shift from fossil fuels to cleaner, renewable energy sources,
has posed major challenges over the past decade for energy infrastructure, supply chains, and
energy security. The electricity transmission grid is critical for enabling this transition. In fact,
modern transmission systems must adapt to integrate various renewable sources, accommodate
the decommissioning of old power plants and the addition of new generation, and ensure the
reliable delivery of power to consumers. Among the renewable sources, solar and wind energy
are especially considered alternatives to conventional fossil fuels (like gas or coal) due to their
easy access and scalability opportunities.
This trend is particularly noticeable in Australia. Australia has set a national target for 82% of its
electricity to be supplied from renewable sources by 2030, as part of an orderly transition to net
zero emissions by 2050. Achieving this 2030 target will help meet Australia’s commitment under
the Paris Accord of a 43% reduction on 2005 emissions levels by 2030.
In the meantime, as coal-fired power stations are going to retire in the next decades, renewable
energy (integrated into transmission and distribution networks, firmed with storage, and supported
by gas-powered generation) has become the lowest-cost means of delivering electricity to homes
and businesses. This approach supports Australia’s transition to a net-zero economy, according to
2024 Integrated System Plan [1]. For example, renewables accounted for nearly 40% of the total
electricity delivered through the National Electricity Market (NEM) in 2023.
1.1
Transmission Expansion Needs
To support the increasing penetration of diverse renewable energy resources, new and modernised
transmission and distribution networks are essential. Transmission infrastructure enables the deliv-
ery of electricity where and when it is needed, and enhances the power system’s overall reliability.
It is therefore important to make well-informed decisions about where and when to invest in the
grid. Since it began operating 25 years ago, the NEM has not experienced such a substantial pe-
riod of investment as it does today. These investments bring notable benefits to both consumers
and the broader economy.
The Australian Energy Market Operator (AEMO), operating the NEM, develops a whole-of-system
plan known as the Integrated System Plan (ISP). The ISP provides a detailed roadmap for the
efficient development of the NEM over a horizon of at least 20 years. It supports Australia’s complex
and rapid energy transition towards net zero emissions by enabling the delivery of low-cost, firmed
renewable energy and the essential transmission infrastructure needed to supply reliable, secure,
and affordable electricity to consumers across the NEM. It is updated every two years to reflect
emerging developments and refine assumptions. According to the 2024 Integrated System Plan:
• Nearly 10,000 km of new transmission lines will be needed by 2050 under the Step Change
and Progressive Change scenarios.
• Approximately 5,000 km of this transmission infrastructure is forecast to be delivered within
the next decade.
• Under the more transformative Green Energy Exports scenario, more than twice this amount
of transmission would be required, delivered at a much faster pace to support rapid economic
growth and export demand.

on technology costs and demand forecasts, to ensure they reflect a credible range of views about
Australia’s energy future. Candidate development paths were evaluated under three distinct future
scenarios projected through to 2050:

1.2
Challenges
Nonetheless, the unpredictable and irregular nature of such renewable power generation sources
gives significant challenges to their integration within existing electricity networks, where at any
time supply has to equal demand. The absence of current large-scale electricity storage facili-
ties poses an additional challenge, particularly for long-term infrastructure planning. Furthermore,
the uncertainty in the cost of these emerging technologies adds another layer of variability to the
planning process. As new solutions continue to evolve, their future cost curves remain difficult to
predict. This unpredictability can then also significantly impact long-term system design.
Here, transmission planners must take into account all of these considerations under a range of fu-
ture scenarios and events. To efficiently handle such short-and-long term complexities, modelling
approaches that employ stochastic optimisation are being preferred over traditional deterministic
methods.
Electricity transmission network investments are long-term strategic decisions, which form the
backbone of the electricity network. The objective of Stochastic Transmission Expansion Plan-
ning (STEP) problems is to minimise the total expected cost associated with both investment and
operation decisions over each scenario realisation, while satisfying constraints from the increas-
ing demand, imposed by technical and economic constraints. Therefore, the purpose of STEP
problems is to determine the optimal timing, location, and capacity of new lines to be built, as well
as any necessary upgrades to existing infrastructure. While this planning framework can also be
applied to generation capacity, that aspect is beyond the scope of this study.
A key advantage of stochastic solutions is that they account for a wide range of possible future
outcomes, typically represented in a scenario tree. By doing so, they aim to find a single cost-
effective strategy that performs well across all considered scenarios, rather than optimising for just
one possible future. As a result, the solution is providing greater reliability and reduces exposure
to risk under uncertainty at the lowest expected cost.
However, as stochastic planning problems continue to grow in size and complexity, particularly with
the expansion of scenario trees to capture more development pathways and long-term uncertainty,
the computational effort to solve such models increases drastically. In this context, the integration
of machine learning techniques is one promising direction to contribute towards enhancing com-
putational tractability. Data-driven approximation models — also known as surrogate models —

are developed to closely mimic the behaviour of the reference model using predictive techniques.
This approach enables the handling of complex decision-making problems under uncertainty with-
out incurring prohibitive computational costs or substantially compromising solution quality.
Thus, a hybrid use of stochastic optimisation frameworks and machine learning methods offers
one potential mean forward to tackle the problems induced by the increased variability and un-
certainty inherent in future power systems, and therefore their size and intractability. In partic-
ular, neural networks with Rectified Linear Unit activations functions can be reformulated as a
set of linear constraints, allowing them to be embedded directly into classical stochastic planning
models. When properly trained, such surrogate models can provide near-optimal solutions while
significantly improving computational efficiency, thereby maintaining tractability even as system
complexity increases.

2
Literature review
This section reviews relevant contributions to transmission expansion planning, with a particu-
lar emphasis on stochastic optimisation and the emerging role of machine learning in enhancing
decision-making under uncertainty.
2.1
Expansion Planning Problem
Expansion planning problems play a critical role in optimising power system operations by consid-
ering long-term investment strategies, operational costs, and renewable energy integration. Given
the uncertainty in renewables’ generation, cots of technologies, as well as demand growth, plan-
ners must adopt a flexible approach that allows present and future investments to guarantee long-
term reliability. Planners have also to consider a multi-year horizon and it requires developing a
long-term strategy that determines not only what and where to invest but also when to implement
those investments. This additional temporal dimension increases problem complexity significantly,
as it introduces a large number of new variables and constraints. Consequently, TEP often de-
mands substantial computational resources, particularly in large-scale power system applications.
A commonly used simplification in the 1990s was to break the time horizon down into a sequence
of static subproblems [2]. While this method reduces computational burden, it may fail to fully
capture the benefits of flexibility and optimal timing that are central to a truly dynamic stochastic
approach.
Therefore, to avoid inefficient solutions initially provided by deterministic approaches, recent stud-
ies on multi-stage stochastic models highlight the value of flexible and ‘wait-and-see’ strategies [3],
considering the planning horizon entirely. [4] showed for example why representation of long-term
uncertainty by a stochastic approach could provide greater flexibility for investment, and hence
reduces planning risks. Compared to more traditional (deterministic) methods such as the Least
Worst Regret (LWR) criterion currently used in Australia, a flexible investment plan derived from
stochastic analysis can embrace a wider range of future scenarios. As a result, the outcomes of
such a plan generally differ—both qualitatively and quantitatively—from those derived from de-
terministic approaches. On a further extent, [5] developed a stochastic model to assess the im-
pact of Distributed Energy Resources (DER) on power system expansion. Their findings indicate
that stochastic modelling yields more measured and robust results compared to deterministic ap-
proaches, particularly when analysing economic movements and investment strategies in renew-
able integration.
However, incorporating detailed operational flexibility while considering the entire planning horizon
poses significant computational and methodological challenges, often rendering real-life applica-
tions intractable. [6] introduced a column generation approach for solving generation expansion
planning problems, to iteratively add only the most promising variables (i.e. columns) to the re-
stricted master problem, leading to reduced computational burden. Their model incorporates unit
commitment and capacity expansion but does not consider transmission constraints. Building
on this, [7] formulated a multi-stage stochastic planning framework that integrates unit commit-
ment, energy storage modelling, reserve allocation, and transmission investments. By employing
a Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition and column generation algorithm, their study demonstrated an
86% improvement in computational efficiency through a column-sharing procedure among sub-
problems.
2.2
Machine Learning for Optimisation
Stochastic programming and decision-making under uncertainty involve nested optimisation prob-
lems, i.e. in which the objective function or constraints contain another optimisation problem.

==PAGE:15==
Nowadays, there is a recent movement toward tacking these problems using machine learn-
ing methods, as it offers ways to drastically reduce the computational burden.
For example,
optimisation-based Benders decomposition is well-suited for Two-Stage Transmission Expansion
Planning Problems. Its ability to disaggregate investment and operation constraints allows binary
investment decisions to be treated as complicating variables. However, its formulation rapidly
faces challenges, as the size of the master problem grows rapidly when all binary decision vari-
ables from the pricing problems are aggregated within the master problem, making it mostly in-
tractable. Therefore, two notable studies have focused on improving Benders decomposition using
ML models. [8] proposed a SVM-based classification approach to identify representative optimal-
ity cuts, enhancing computational efficiency by selectively adding only valuable constraints. [9,
10] extended this approach using a Random Forest algorithm to predict effective and ineffective
optimality cuts, leading to improvements in both upper and lower bound estimates.
Another promising avenue is scenario reduction, where ML techniques are used to select a repre-
sentative subset of scenarios from a large uncertainty space. [11] employ the Progressive Hedg-
ing algorithm, which decomposes the problem by scenarios rather than stages, leading to a more
scalable approach as each subproblem remains relatively small and does not grow excessively in
size. To further enhance computational efficiency, they leverage scenario reduction techniques via
a K-means clustering algorithm. This reduces the computational burden without severely compro-
mising the quality of the solution. [12] used a similar approach, grouping similar scenarios together,
to then solve a reduced-size surrogate problem. Similarly, [13] proposed selecting representative
time periods for power systems planning. While such techniques can drastically shrink the scenario
space, they risk over-generalising the uncertainty, potentially missing rare but extreme events.
Another class of machine learning-based approaches focuses on directly predicting optimal deci-
sions, typically in the form of binary or continuous solution vectors. For example, [14] investigated
direct decision prediction strategies. While these methods offer the potential for fast approxima-
tions, they often struggle with enforcing integrality constraints and ensuring strict feasibility, which
limits their general applicability in real-world optimisation settings. In the power system opera-
tion field, [15] proposed a unit commitment predictor based on a distributionally robust Support
Vector Machine (SVM) classifier. This model aims to enhance commitment decisions by explicitly
accounting for variability in input conditions. Similarly, [16] developed a machine learning-based
proxy model for economic dispatch that combines classification and regression techniques. Their
method improves computational efficiency while preserving solution quality, offering a promising
direction for accelerating real-time operations.
An other approach by [17] extended ML techniques to the AC Optimal Power Flow (AC-OPF) prob-
lem. Their deep neural network (DNN) maps system load features to generator active power and
voltage settings, enabling fast and feasible AC-OPF solutions with significant speed-ups compared
to traditional solvers. Building on this, [18] recently proposed a deep learning-based framework for
transmission expansion planning using a convolutional neural network (CNN). Trained to predict
optimal line construction decisions, their model demonstrates the potential of deep learning as a
surrogate for complex planning tasks, achieving both scalability and reasonable solution fidelity.
Building on the use of neural networks as surrogate models, a novel approach called Neur2SP was
introduced by [19]. This method trains a neural network to approximate the expected value func-
tion of a two-stage stochastic program. This model enables faster computation while maintaining
solution quality, offering a promising alternative to extensive formulations. Neur2SP typically illus-
trates the core idea of what a surrogate model is. While promising, this approach remains limited
to two-stage formulations and has not yet been extended to multi-stage decision-making problems.
Moreover, its application to complex infrastructure systems, such as transmission expansion plan-
ning, is still unexplored. As such, Neur2SP highlights both the potential and the current limitations
of ML-based surrogates in stochastic optimisation—underscoring the need for further investigation

Research Gaps
The above literature highlights a transition towards hybrid approaches that integrate traditional
optimisation techniques with Machine Learning. The reviewed studies demonstrate significant im-
provements in computational efficiency and decision-making fidelity, paving the way for more re-
silient and adaptive power system models. However, in the field of expansion planning and power
system operation, this has remained relatively unexplored.
In fact, using ML as a standalone tool to directly solve stochastic transmission expansion planning
(STEP) problems—without relying on dimensionality reduction—is a novel approach. Most exist-
ing applications focus instead on supporting sub-tasks such as scenario reduction or enhancing
Benders decomposition. This study aims to contribute to this emerging area by investigating how
ML models can be trained and embedded within optimisation frameworks to solve STEP problems
end-to-end.
The contribution of this master thesis is then threefold. First, it proposes a systematic framework
for generating training data and designing neural network architectures specifically tailored for
TEP applications. Secondly, it presents a novel formulation for the multi-stage stochastic TEP
model where operational costs are approximated via a Rectified Linear Unit deep neural network,
reformulated as mixed-integer linear constraints, reducing computational complexity. Finally, it
proposes an optimality guarantee assessment when obtaining investment decisions.

3
Stochastic Optimisation Model
The objective of transmission expansion planning is to determine the connections of the transmis-
sion lines within a power system in order to meet the growing demands for electric power while
ensuring safe, economic and stable operation of the system.
While deterministic TEP provides a basis for the identification of reliable and cost-effective trans-
mission network expansions, it often falls short when dealing with the inherent uncertainty of mod-
ern power systems. Real-world input parameters like electricity demand, renewables generation,
prices of fuel are highly volatile. Ignoring these uncertainties can lead to expansion strategies that
are suboptimal or even infeasible under future operating conditions.
STEP problems addresses this challenge by modelling uncertainty explicitly using scenarios and
probability distributions. Instead of optimising a single predicted future, STEP seeks to identify
transmission investments that perform robustly across a portfolio of plausible futures. This is typ-
ically achieved by formulating the problem as a two-stage or multi-stage stochastic optimisation
problem, in which the investment decisions are made in the first stage, while operational decisions
react to each realisation of uncertainty in subsequent stages.
The STEP approach improves the resilience and economic efficiency of expansion plans, espe-
cially in systems with high penetration of renewables, demand-side management, or evolving mar-
ket mechanisms. However, these benefits come at the cost of increased computational complexity.
The mathematical programming models obtained from the STEP approach can grow exponentially
with the number of scenarios and time stages modelled, often calling for decomposition techniques
or advanced solution algorithms for tractability [20, 21].
Despite such a challenge, stochastic modelling is increasingly regarded as relevant for long-term
power system transmission planning today. It ensures that investment decisions are made in con-
sideration of the whole range of uncertainties shaping the electricity market—effectively leading to
more robust, flexible, and cost-effective grid expansion methods [22, 23].
3.1
Problem Formulation
Two-stage stochastic programming is a widely used framework for optimisation under uncertainty,
where decision variables are split into first-stage (here-and-now) decisions and second-stage (re-
course) decisions, which are adjusted after the uncertainty is realised. The model accounts for
uncertainty by treating input parameters as random variables, denoted as ξ, influenced by the out-
comes of a stochastic process. This stands in contrast to deterministic models, which assume that
all input data is known with certainty and ignores variability in future conditions.

To reflect real-world transmission expansion dynamics, the model incorporates a construction lead
time and separates investment decisions from the availability of the lines. This precision affects
both the non-anticipativity constraints and the annuity calculations.
• Lead Time

between the decision and the line’s availability. However, in practice, new transmission lines
require time to be built after the investment decision is made. To model this, a lead time of
one stage (equivalent to τ = 5 years) is introduced between the investment decision and the
line becoming operational.

Learning Operational Decisions
The strategy explored in this work focuses on predicting yearly operational decisions instead of
the full vector of hourly operational decisions. This choice is motivated by two main reasons:
• Predicting hourly operational decisions is particularly challenging due to the highly non-linear
behaviour of merit-order dispatch. For example, an expensive generator may remain offline
for most of the year and only be dispatched during specific hours or days when variable
renewable generation (wind and solar) cannot fully meet demand. Such operating patterns
result in highly discontinuous and non-linear generation schedules, which are difficult for
machine learning models to capture accurately.
• To ensure fidelity to real-world system conditions, the model is trained using datasets cov-
ering complete annual operational data, compared to most of the studies using only rep-
resentative weeks [5, 27, 28]. This guarantees a comprehensive representation of system
variability and uncertainty throughout the year. Consequently, there is no need for scenario
reduction techniques, as the full dataset provides a more robust and representative training
basis.
The overarching goal is in the end for the machine learning surrogate model to replicate the
results of the optimisation problem, using the same input information that the optimiser would
see, but at a lower computational cost.

to generalise when exposed to realistic system variability.

a narrow band near zero. This made it difficult for the model to detect meaningful patterns during
training, resulting in poor performance.
Therefore, the target variables are left in its original scale to match the output range of the model.

4.3.2.2
Loss Function
A loss function is a function that compares the target and predicted output values, measuring how
well the neural network models the training data. The training process seeks to minimise this loss,
improving the model’s predictions.
Huber Loss Function
The Huber loss function has been chosen here for its desirable properties. Two commonly used
loss functions in regression are the squared loss (or L2 loss) L(a) = a2 and the absolute loss (or L1
loss) L(a) = |a|, where a = y −ˆy denotes the residuals, i.e. the difference between the observed
and predicted data value. The squared loss results in an estimator that is unbiased with respect
to the arithmetic mean, while the absolute loss yields a median-unbiased estimator.
However, each of these loss functions has limitations. The absolute loss is not differentiable at
a = 0, which can lead to slower convergence and less smooth training dynamics. On the other
hand, the squared loss is highly sensitive to outliers. When computing the total loss as P L(a), a
few large residuals can disproportionately influence the result, especially when the error distribution
has heavy tails (as for load shedding for example) [29]. In fact, load shedding refers to unserved
energy, i.e. situations in which the system cannot supply enough power to meet demand. Because
this represents a severe disruption to the electricity supply, it is treated as a last-resort emergency
measure, and therefore is very rarely occurring.
The Huber loss function addresses these limitations by combining the advantages of both the L1
and L2 loss functions. Near zero, it behaves like the squared loss, ensuring smooth gradients and
stable convergence. For large residuals, it transitions to a linear loss, which reduces sensitivity to
outliers.

4.3.2.3
Learning Rate
The learning rate is a critical hyperparameter in an optimisation algorithm that determines the step
size at each iteration while moving toward a minimum of a loss function. A well-chosen learning rate
balances convergence speed and stability [30]. While the descent direction is usually determined
from the gradient of the loss function, the learning rate determines how big a step is taken in that
direction. A too high learning rate will make the learning jump over minima but a too low learning
rate will either take too long to converge or get stuck in an undesirable local minimum.
Let θ denote the vector of model parameters, and let ∇θL(θ) represent the gradient of the loss
function with respect to θ. The parameter update rule for gradient descent is given by:
θ(t+1) = θ(t) −η∇θL(θ(t))
where:
• θ(t) is the parameter vector at iteration t,
• η > 0 is the learning rate,
• ∇θL(θ(t)) is the gradient of the loss function at iteration t.
A learning rate that is too small results in slow convergence, requiring many iterations to reach an
optimum. Conversely, a learning rate that is too large may cause the optimisation to overshoot the
minimum, potentially leading to divergence or oscillatory behaviour [31].
In order to achieve faster convergence, prevent oscillations and getting stuck in undesirable local
minima, learning rate schedules or adaptive learning rate methods (such as learning rate decay)
are often used. These strategies adjust η during training based on either the iteration count or
gradient statistics, allowing for faster training and improved stability [32].

4.3.3
Hyperparameter Optimisation
Every machine learning system has hyperparameters, and tuning them involves selecting a set of
optimal hyperparameters for a given learning algorithm. A hyperparameter is a parameter whose
value is used to control the learning process and must be configured before training begins [33].
Hyperparameter optimisation (HPO) aims to determine the set of hyperparameters that yields an
optimal model by minimising a predefined loss function (in this case, the Huber loss).
Historically, HPO has been performed via grid search, which exhaustively evaluates model per-
formance over a discretised subset of the hyperparameter space. While it is straightforward to

24
==PAGE:34==
implement, grid search becomes computationally infeasible as the number of hyperparameters
grows and often wastes resources evaluating uninformative regions of the space. A more efficient
baseline is random search, which samples configurations uniformly at random until a search bud-
get is exhausted. Empirical studies show that random search frequently outperforms grid search,
particularly when only a small subset of hyperparameters significantly affects model performance.
4.3.3.1
Bayesian Optimisation
A more advanced and sample-efficient strategy is Bayesian optimisation, which frames HPO as a
global optimisation problem over expensive black-box functions [34, 35]. Bayesian optimisation is
particularly effective when evaluation is costly, as it leverages past observations to inform subse-
quent sampling decisions. It operates iteratively using two components: a probabilistic surrogate
model (e.g., a Gaussian Process) that models the target function, and an acquisition function that
guides the selection of the next candidate hyperparameter configuration by balancing exploration
(uncertainty reduction) and exploitation (focusing on promising regions).
At each iteration, the surrogate model is updated using the history of evaluated hyperparameter
configurations and their corresponding loss values. Based on this model, the acquisition function
selects the most informative configuration to evaluate next. This optimisation process is repeated
for a total of 50 iterations. In each iteration, the selected configuration is trained for 200 epochs,
twice (i.e., the model is trained two times with the same configuration to reduce the randomness
inherent in the training process, such as that caused by weight initialisation. This helps to reduce
variance in the results and improves the overall efficiency of the Bayesian search). The config-
uration that achieves the lowest validation loss across all iterations is then chosen as the final
hyperparameter setting.
This HPO technique was applied to Generation Costs only, as its target distribution is well-suited
for optimisation (approximately bell-shaped). Practically, it was implemented using the
BayesianOptimization tuner from Keras.
4.3.3.2
Tree-Structured Parzen Estimator (TPE)
The Tree-Structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) is a sequential model-based optimisation algorithm
used as an alternative to Gaussian Processes (GP) in Bayesian Optimisation. TPE is particularly
well-suited for high-dimensional, complex, or noisy search spaces where GP-based methods may
struggle due to scalability or modelling assumptions [33].
In contrast to GPs, which directly model the objective function by estimating a posterior distribution
over possible functions, TPE adopts a different approach. It models the conditional probability of
hyperparameters given the objective value, splitting the observed data into two distributions:
• p(x | y < y∗): the distribution of hyperparameter configurations associated with good (i.e.,
low) objective values,
• p(x | y ≥y∗): the distribution associated with poorer-performing configurations.
By focusing on the ratio of these two distributions, TPE selects new hyperparameter candidates
that are likely to yield improvements, effectively maximising the Expected Improvement over the
best result observed so far.
In this study, this method is particularly beneficial due to the sparsity of the optimal Load Shed-
ding values obtained during the sampling process. In such sparse or discontinuous objective
landscapes, Gaussian Processes may fail to provide informative mean and variance estimates,
hindering their ability to balance exploration and exploitation effectively. TPE avoids this limitation
by relying on a density-based estimation rather than on smoothness assumptions. This method is
implemented using the Optuna hyperparameter optimisation framework [36].

4.6
Trustworthiness
In the context of critical infrastructure, such as power system planning, the integration of AI models
requires strong guarantees of trustworthiness, reliability, and transparency. Under the European
Union Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) [38], AI systems deployed in high-risk
domains—including energy systems—are subject to strict transparency and explainability obliga-
tions.
4.6.1
Explainability
To address these requirements and enhance the interpretability of this neural network surrogate
model, SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values were employed [39]. SHAP is a game theory
approach to explain the output of any machine learning model, and provides a unified framework
for feature attribution by computing the contribution of each input feature to a given model predic-
tion. It assigns each feature an importance value based on its marginal contribution to the model’s
output, averaged over all possible feature combinations. In the context of stochastic transmission
expansion planning, this interpretability is particularly valuable, as it allows to understand which
input features (e.g., which renewable generation source or line candidate) most influence cost
and reliability predictions. This supports trust in the surrogate model’s decision-making behaviour,
highlighting potential sources of prediction error, and can guide future model improvements or
scenario design. Moreover, SHAP values help validate that the surrogate model aligns with phys-
ical intuition or operational constraints, which is essential when embedding such models within
optimisation frameworks where transparency and reliability are critical.
4.6.2
Robustness and Distributional Resilience
In addition to explainability, robustness is a core pillar of trustworthy AI, particularly in high-stakes
domains such as power systems. Classical robust learning methods frame training data as uncer-
tain variables belonging to a joint uncertainty set and aim to minimise the worst-case loss over this
set. This approach aligns naturally with decision-making under uncertainty, such as in stochastic
transmission expansion planning. However, artificial neural networks (ANNs), while expressive,
are known to be vulnerable to adversarial perturbations—small, often imperceptible changes in
input features that can lead to disproportionately large errors in model outputs [40]. This sensitivity
can be particularly concerning in critical infrastructure contexts, where erroneous predictions may
lead to costly or unsafe operational decisions.
Recent advances [41] show that, under certain data conditions—such as dispersion for regres-
sion tasks—classical robust learning models are equivalent to distributionally robust optimisation
(DRO) models, which instead optimise over probability distributions within an ambiguity set. While
solving classical robust models directly can be computationally intractable in the absence of these
conditions, DRO formulations are more tractable and offer statistical interpretability. In the context
of this work, DRO-like behaviour emerges implicitly through the use of regularised neural networks
trained across a wide range of scenarios. This promotes both generalisation and resilience to in-
put variation, thereby mitigating some aspects of adversarial vulnerability. The resulting surrogate
model is thus better aligned with formal robustness principles and well-suited for deployment in
uncertain, safety-critical infrastructure planning environments.

4.6.3
Warm-Starting and Operational Trust
Another practical aspect contributing to the trustworthiness of AI-based decision support tools is
the use of warm-starting strategies. In optimisation contexts, warm-starting involves initialising the
solver with a set of variables that are known to be close to the optimal ones to accelerate conver-
gence. In the context of this work, they then could be predictions from a surrogate model. However,
while warm starts often reduce solution time by guiding the solver closer to the optimal region, they
do not always guarantee speed improvements. In some cases, warm starts may have little impact
or even slow down the solver due to additional overhead in processing the initial solution.
In the context of stochastic transmission expansion planning, investment decision warm starts
derived from robust surrogate models can guide the solver towards high-quality solutions more
quickly, even under varying scenario conditions, and by the same prove that the predictions ob-
tained are actually optimal.

5.1.2.1
Model Architectures
Let’s recall the three models under consideration:
• Joint Models (1NN - 1 Delta): A single neural network trained to simultaneously predict gen-
eration costs and load shedding using a unique custom loss function and only one threshold
parameter δ.
• Joint Models (1NN - 2 Deltas): A single neural network trained to simultaneously predict
generation costs and load shedding using a unique custom loss function with two threshold
parameters δ.
• Separate Model (2NN): Two independent neural networks, each trained to predict one output
(generation cost and Load Shedding) with dedicated loss functions.
These designs reflect trade-offs between compactness, interpretability, and task-specific perfor-
mance. The joint model offers a streamlined formulation with a single training phase and loss
function. However, its performance may be limited due to the heterogeneous nature of the output
distributions. In particular, load shedding is a sparse and highly skewed target, while generation
cost follows a near-Gaussian distribution. This discrepancy can result in suboptimal weight updates
during training, as the joint loss might not capture the most meaningful input-output mappings for
each target simultaneously.

Case Studies Conclusion
The two case studies presented in this work demonstrated the potential and limitations of the pro-
posed framework, testing both its predictive accuracy and adaptability across different systems.
Results show that the framework can deliver reliable operational decisions and near-optimal in-
vestment plans with significantly reduced computational time. A particularly interesting outcome
is that the original (unperturbed) instances were consistently predicted with high accuracy across
both study cases, effectively validating the core objective of the framework.
However, certain challenges were observed when allowing perturbation in the features—particularly
Machine-learning applications for transmission planning problems under uncertainty
55
==PAGE:65==
when target distributions, such as load shedding, became highly sparse. In such cases, the accu-
racy decreased.
For real-world applications, several key questions remain open. Notably, how can one ensure the
optimality of critical decisions—such as investments—when predictions are only approximations
of the true targets? And how close must these predictions be to preserve decision quality? Those
questions remain still open for now.
From a computational perspective, scalability is another concern. As more candidate lines are
introduced, the number of scenarios grows exponentially (N ×|S|×2|LC|), making exhaustive data
generation infeasible. While a naive sampling strategy was used in this study, more efficient meth-
ods for bigger study cases—such as filtering out infeasible or low-reliability configurations—could
be applied to reduce the search space. Additionally, the current framework is system-specific and
lacks efficient adaptability. Each new test case necessitates a complete re-generation of training
data and retraining of the model, which limits portability. This rigid structure could be alleviated
by investigating transfer learning techniques, allowing knowledge gained from one system to be
reused or fine-tuned for others.
Despite these challenges, both case studies highlight the strong potential of neural networks, when
formulated as sets of linear constraints, to serve as powerful surrogate models. When well trained,
they enable fast and accurate integration into stochastic TEP problems, offering a promising path
forward for tractable large-scale transmission expansion planning under uncertainty.

6
Conclusion
Transmission Expansion Planning currently plays a critical role in ensuring the long-term reliabil-
ity, efficiency, and economic viability of power systems. Traditionally, TEP has been addressed
through large-scale optimisation models that account for both investment and operational deci-
sions under a range of technical, economic, and regulatory constraints.
However, as the computational burden associated with solving large-scale multi-stage stochastic
TEP problems grows exponentially with the number of scenarios and planning stages, they often
become impractical for real-world applications, unless scenario reduction techniques are being
employed. Machine Learning offers therefore promising avenues to reduce computational require-
ments, by learning to approximate complex operational decisions as surrogate models embedded
within mathematical programming formulations.
This thesis demonstrates how machine learning techniques can be integrated into a stochastic
transmission expansion planning problem to enable fast approximations of operational outcomes,
such as generation costs and load shedding, in order to provide investment decisions. The pro-
posed approach presents a complete and practical pipeline, covering data sampling, surrogate
model training, embedding within an optimisation problem, and validating solution quality. By em-
bedding trained neural network surrogates within a multi-stage optimisation model, the proposed
approach significantly reduces computational complexity while maintaining near-optimal solutions.
However, two key limitations should be noted. Firstly, the framework has to be reapplied for each
new system considered, unless the renewable generation forecasts remain within the predefined
noise perturbation range (±25% in this case) across all scenario nodes on the scenario tree. Sec-
ondly, the computational benefits are mainly observed during the optimisation solving phase. In-
deed, the time required for data sampling and model training often exceeds the time saved during
optimisation, which limits the overall efficiency gains of the approach. Nonetheless, it is impor-
tant to note that training is a one-time cost. Indeed, once the surrogate model is trained, it can
be reused for multiple optimisation problems, making it especially valuable in cases where input
parameters vary slightly and therefore repeated evaluations are needed. In contrast, a classical
optimisation formulation would necessitate rerunning the entire model from scratch each time.
While ML methods offer promising capabilities in approximating complex system behaviours and
accelerating computational tasks, their adoption in highly regulated and risk-sensitive domains re-
mains limited. A major barrier is the lack of interpretability and formal guarantees on decision
quality of these models. This concern is increasingly relevant in light of emerging regulatory ef-
forts such as the European Union’s Artificial Intelligence Act [38, 44], which calls higher levels of
transparency and accountability in AI systems. This thesis therefore also highlights approaches
to interpret and validate the surrogate model’s predictions within the established understanding of
power system behaviour, providing initial steps toward building trust in such hybrid methods.
Nonetheless, future work should aim to provide formal investment decision optimality guarantees
by incorporating appropriate upper and lower bounds within the optimisation framework. This is
particularly critical when integrating machine learning models—such as the neural networks pre-
sented here—into decision-making pipelines for safety-critical applications like power system plan-
ning.
6.1
Future Research Directions
The work is an introductory work showing the capability of ML surrogate models in a Transmission
Expansion Planning framework.
Machine-learning applications for transmission planning problems under uncertainty
57
==PAGE:67==
Several directions for future work emerge to further develop this framework and enable its appli-
cation in real-world settings. Firstly, to increase trust and practical deployability, future research
should aim to establish formal optimality guarantees by incorporating provable upper and lower
bounds on the surrogate models’ outputs. Secondly, scaling this framework to larger and more
realistic systems will be crucial to rigorously evaluate its generalisation capabilities and practical
viability. However, as the number of candidate lines increases or the scenario tree spans a longer
time horizon, the number of configurations that must be evaluated during the sampling phase grows
exponentially, making the problem computationally challenging for both the sampling and training
phases. Therefore, efficient scenario selection will be key to managing this complexity.
From a modelling perspective, further improvements in training and inference efficiency — for ex-
ample, through the development of sparse neural networks, as suggested by [45] — are worth
exploring to ensure that the approach remains tractable as system complexity increases.
One other approach could also be to ’convexify’ the problem by relaxing the binary investment
variables into continuous variables in the surrogate model. Instead of representing investment de-
cisions as strictly ”built” or ”not built,” these variables can take real values. This relaxation could
then be extended to encode capacity information for the transmission lines, providing a more flex-
ible formulation and enabling afterwards stronger theoretical guarantees.
At the system level, extending the current formulation to incorporate additional operational con-
straints such as unit commitment (UC), battery energy storage systems (BESS), and more de-
tailed DC-OPF formulations significantly enhance the fidelity and applicability of the framework.
Such extensions would bring the surrogate-augmented approach closer to real-world system plan-
ning requirements, broadening its impact and demonstrating its relevance to modern, flexible, and
low-carbon grids.
In summary, this work provides an important and necessary step towards bridging the gap between
traditional optimisation and data-driven learning for power system planning, by demonstrating how
machine learning can be safely and efficiently integrated into complex decision-making pipelines.
