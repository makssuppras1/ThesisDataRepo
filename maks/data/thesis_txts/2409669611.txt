Abstract
In the resent years the design of footbridges becomes more slender. Increasing the slen-
derness of the footbridges can lead to issues of dynamic structural behaviour i.e. the
structure is more responsive to vibration issues induced by pedestrians when crossing
the bridge. Thus, the present studies targets the human-structure interaction where in-
ﬂuence from pedestrians on the dynamic properties of a structure is analyzed by use of
Operational Modal Analysis.
Verifying the identiﬁcation methods in Operation Modal Analysis in relation to harmon-
ics from pedestrian induced loads a Monte Carlo simulation is introduced. The force
model from the pedestrians is based on a model assuming a single and a freely walking
pedestrian. By use of a Monte Carlo time-domain model, loading from each ’unique’
pedestrian are simulated on a footbridge using characteristic stochastic force properties
of the pedestrians. The identiﬁcation of the simulated responses revealed that Opera-
tional Modal Analysis can identify the modal properties when a structure is subjected
to harmonics from pedestrian induced vibrations.
Furthermore, a footbridge is introduced to study any change in the modal parameters
of the bridge by performing full scale measurements with diﬀerent types of pedestrian
loadings. From the analyzed data of the full scale measurements a clear shift in the fre-
quencies and the damping ratios was obtained when pedestrians crossed the bridge, where
the frequency of the footbridge decreases and the damping increases when considering
pedestrian induced vibrations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Due to aesthetic reasons many architects design footbridges in the resent years more slender. An
example of a more modern footbridge with these aesthetic requirements is the ’Åbuen’ bridge in
Copenhagen, Denmark, see Fig. (1.1). The design of footbridges with these great slenderness is
obtained by using less materials, nevertheless, the modern calculation techniques allows engineers
to design structures with these design requirements. As the structure gets more slender issues of
dynamic structural behaviour can be introduced, meaning that the structure is more responsive to
vibration issues induced by pedestrians when crossing the bridge. Thus, in this project focus on
pedestrian induced vibrations on footbridges in the vertical direction is introduced.
Figure 1.1: ’Åbuen’ footbridge in Copenhagen, Denmark from 2008.
No vertical design guidelines have yet found any model for a crowd crossing a footbridge, but most
guidelines assumes a single pedestrian crossing the footbridge, where the load model is based on an
outdated model proposed by Blanchard et al. [5].

1.2
Objective
A crowd load model have not been provided by present design standards. Recent studies has intro-
duced the understanding of the vertical load related to a single pedestrian, however, there are still
uncertainties about the eﬀects of the crowd loading and group loading. The present studies target
the human-structure interaction where the inﬂuence from the pedestrians on the dynamic properties
is primarily analyzed by use of Operational Modal Analysis.
Verifying the identiﬁcation methods in Operation Modal Analysis in relation to harmonics from
pedestrian induced loads a Monte Carlo simulation is introduced. The footfall force model from the
crossing pedestrians is based on a model presented Bachmann et al. [12] for a single and a freely
walking pedestrian. By use of a Monte Carlo time-domain model, loading from each ’unique’ pedes-
trian are simulated on the Grevestien footbridge using characteristic stochastic force properties of
the pedestrians.
The Grevestien Footbridge in Greve, Denmark is introduced to study any change in the modal
parameters of a bridge by performing full scale measurements with diﬀerent types of pedestrian
loading and the results are analyzed by use of Operational Modal Analysis.
1.3
Disposition
In chapter 2 and 3 a general background of all analysis is introduced. This Master’s Thesis comprises
both physical experimental and numerical elements. In chapter 4 the numerical investigations are
presented and chapter 5, 6 and 7 contains physical experimental investigations. A more thorough
explanation of each chapter is presented in the following.
In chapter 2 the load eﬀect of a single pedestrian on structures is described by presenting the load
model of a single freely walking pedestrian. The various parameters of pedestrians such as dynamic
load factor and pacing frequency is incorporated in the model. Furthermore, the human-structure
interaction and human-human interaction will be introduced.
In chapter 3 a brief introduction of statistical information of random variables and processes is pre-
sented. A presentation of Operational Modal Analysis and the diﬀerent techniques, Time Domain
and Frequency Domain will be introduced.
In chapter 4 a presentation of human crowd loading simulation will be presented by random time-
domain process using characteristic stochastic force properties.

Chapter 2
Pedestrian Induced Loads
In recent years the slenderness of structures increases due to aesthetic reasons. With modern calcu-
lation techniques it is possible to design footbridges with less amount of materials and thus achieve
great slenderness. Nevertheless, when the stiﬀness of the structure decreases it can cause large vi-
bration problems in footbridges.
The load eﬀect of a single pedestrian or crowds on structures have been investigated by many re-
searchers. The pedestrian load depends on many various parameters such as body mass, pacing
frequency, crowd synchronization etc. Živanovič et al. [20]. Vertical, lateral and longitudinal forces
are induced by pedestrians when walking. Fig. (2.1) shows a diagram over the walking forces of a
single pedestrian.

produced by the human feet are exactly the same and periodic. A deterministic time-domain footfall
force model is developed by Bachmann et al. [12], see Eq. (2.1), where the load is given in terms of
truncated Fourier series.

2.1.2
Pedestrian Weight
When considering pedestrian induced load a fundamental parameter is the pedestrian weight. The
Danish Institute of Public Health have made a research about human body mass by conducting a
survey of 16,437 participants from the Danish population. The survey showed that the mean weight
of the Danish population is µW p = 74.0 kg with a standard deviation of σW p = 14.72 kg National
Institute of Public Health [5].

2.1.3
Pacing Frequency
Investigations can be found in many literature of the normal walking frequency. In Fig. (2.4) a
distribution of the pacing frequency of 505 Japanese people is estimated by Matsumoto et al. [21].
The investigation follows a clear Gaussian distribution with a mean pacing frequency of µfp = 1.99
Hz and a standard deviation of σfp = 0.173 Hz.
Figure 2.4: Pace distribution of pedestrians, Matsumoto et al. [21].
Several distributions of the pacing frequency has been investigated around the world e.g. the Eu-
ropean population where the mean pacing frequency is µfp = 1.83 Hz with a standard deviation of
σfp = 0.135 Hz examined by Ingólfsson [5].
In general any pacing frequency near the natural frequency of vertical modes of a given structure
can cause pedestrian induced vibration problems. Guidelines in general recommends that structures
with higher frequency than 5 Hz should not be examined for pedestrian induced vibrations British
Standard [1].

quencies, furthermore, the stride length has a stochastic variation dependent on each pedestrian.
A relationship between the stride length and the pacing frequency has been performed by John E.
Wheeler [5] where a cubic and a quadratic curve have been determined from previous results, see
Fig. (2.5). The discrete points in the ﬁgure is a physical measurement studied by Einar Ingólfsson
[5] where it was found that a linear ﬁt should be applied for the measurements, see Eq. (2.2).

2.2
Human-Human and Human-Structure Interaction
The presented formula in the previous section for the footfall force is only applied for single, freely
walking pedestrians. Hence, by using the model any interactions between pedestrians and the sur-
roundings are not accommodated. A crowd with a high density can aﬀect the pacing frequency and
velocity of each single pedestrian, this is described in many literature as human-human interaction.
This means that a fully correlated crowd can produce higher forces than a uncorrelated crowd. It is
proposed by Grundmann et al. [12] that an upper limit of the density for unconstrained free walking
is 0.3 peds./m2 and a limit for not being able to move anymore is 1.6 - 1.8 peds./m2 Bachmann
et al. [8]. It has to be mentioned that no studies has yet provided a quantiﬁed and comprehensive
knowledge about this matter. In this study the human-human interaction will be considered as
negligible, since such a high density is not investigated.

Human-structure interaction can be divided into two areas:
- The inﬂuence from crossing pedestrians on the dynamic properties of a footbridge.
- The inﬂuence from structural vibrations on the pedestrians walk.
The inﬂuence from pedestrians on the dynamic properties is primarily considered as added damping
and changes in the natural frequency i.e. pedestrian on the footbridge works as a dynamic system
consisting of a mass and a damper attached to the structural system Živanovič et al. [20]. Previous
studies have shown that the inﬂuence from the pedestrians causes a clear change in the damping.
These studies have shown that in most cases pedestrians acts like added damping, however these
studies have also shown cases of negative damping Jørgensen [13] and Ellis et al. [2]. It has to be
mentioned that no literature have yet found a clear correlation between the walking pedestrians and
the dynamic properties of footbridges. In this study any added damping from the pedestrian will
be incorporated in the structural damping of the footbridge.
Studies have shown that pedestrians ﬁnds structural vibrations in the lateral direction less acceptable
than structural vibrations in the vertical direction Živanovič et al. [20]. Vertical displacement higher
than 10 mm - 20 mm is considered as disturbing the natural walk of a pedestrian, Bachmann et al.
[8]. With an average pacing frequency for the European population 1.83 Hz given by Ingólfsson [5]
the corresponding acceleration gives 0.01m · (2π · 1.83Hz)2 = 1.32m/s2 to 0.02m · (2π · 1.83Hz)2 =
2.64m/s2. Further investigations of this eﬀect is not included in this study.

Load Model
A footbridges in a dense residential area will be subjected to large or small crowd loading from
crossing pedestrian, such as footbridges at bus stations or railway stations. Nevertheless, it is con-
siderably to analyze the responses of the bridge in a realistic manner whether the scenario occurs
for large or small crowding.
To analyze the stochastic pedestrian traﬃc situation in a realistic way the Monte Carlo simulation
technique is considered which will be applied to evaluate the responses.
The basic assumption by using the Monte Carlo simulation is that the crowd load is not a uniformly
distributed load. These pedestrian loading act as randomly pulsating point loads across the foot-
bridge with stochastic characteristic of each ’unique’ pedestrian, such as the pedestrian body mass,
the pacing frequency, etc. Another factor to be considered is the pedestrian arriving to the foot-
bridge per time unit is the average arrival intensity, λ. Footbridge can be in diﬀerent environments
extending from high density of pedestrian to low. In this study a representative load intensity is
chosen to be equal 1, λ = 1 peds./s, no further investigation of the local environment conditions

3.3
Operational Modal Analysis
An useful method applied by many engineers for analyzing modal properties of a given structure
under an excitation, such as wind and waves, or under artiﬁcial excitation is provided by the so
called Operational Modal Analysis (OMA). From test measurements on a speciﬁc structure the
correlations of the dynamic properties can be identiﬁed by use of mathematical models, where this
can lead to the identiﬁcation of the modal properties of a system. The whole premise of OMA is to
test a structure induced by excitations that have similar characteristics of white noise input i.e. the
energy is distributed over a broad range of frequencies, see Fig. (3.6), where the modal properties of
the structural system in interest should be within this frequency range. It is not fundamental that
the excitations have the same characteristics as white noise input, but it is fundamental that the
structure is excited suﬃciently, thus the contributions of all the modes in interest of the structure
can be captured in the signals. In the following diﬀerent techniques will be introduced in the Time
Domain and the Frequency Domain, we shall not cover everything in detail, but only present the
main ideas. A more detail description of the diﬀerent techniques can be found in Brincker et al [17].
3.3.1
Time Domain Identiﬁcation
In OMA the identiﬁcation in the time domain deals with free decays in therms of correlation func-
tions or similar random decrement functions, this approach is used to gain the physical information
of the structure from the correlation function. In this project the Poly Reference (PR) technique
and the Ibrahim Time Domain (ITD) technique is applied for the time domain identiﬁcation of the
modal parameters.
The PR technique assumes the correlation functions as free decays which means that the noise is
vanishing. This makes the method unbiased, due to the assumption of the noise approximating zero
is true, Brincker et al. [17].
One of the ﬁrst techniques developed in modal identiﬁcation techniques of multiple output systems is
the Ibrahim Time Domain (ITD) method, which makes it possible to take information from several
signals at the same time. The purpose of this method is to take OMA information from the random
responses of a structure by the random decrement technique, Brincker et al. [17].
3.3.2
Frequency Domain Identiﬁcation
In the frequency domain each mode has a small frequency band width where a mode dominates,
thus, by considering the frequency bands in which diﬀerent modes dominates it is possible to take
advantage of a "natural modal decomposition". It has to be noted that an inconvenience in the
frequency domain identiﬁcation is that the spectral density estimates have some problems with bias,
but as presented for the time domain free responses are considered meaning that minimum bias on
==PAGE:33==
CHAPTER 3. RANDOM PROCESSES
21
the free decays are obtained. In this project the Frequency-Domain Decomposition (FDD) technique
and the Frequency-Domain Poly Reference (FDPR) also called PolyMax1 (PM) technique is applied
for the frequency domain identiﬁcation of the modal parameters.
The Frequency-Domain Decomposition (FDD) technique was ﬁrst introduced by Rune Brincker.
The technique do not apply the natural modal decomposition method directly, but the FDD tech-
nique applies a similar approach. This approach considers small frequency bands, where the modes
outside the frequency bands have a reduced inﬂuence on the results. The FDD technique can an-
alyze closely spaced modes, since the method is based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of
the spectral density matrix. The technique is user friendly since it concentrates all the information
in one diagram of the spectral density matrix. The SVD do not completely correspond to the the-
oretical decomposition of the spectral density matrix, which makes the FDD approach based on an
approximate decomposition, leading to an approximate solution i.e. introducing bias, Brincker et
al. [17].
The PolyMax (PM) technique do not directly apply the natural modal decomposition method, but
like the FDD technique the PM technique applies a similar approach. This approach considers small
frequency bands, where the modes outside the frequency bands have a reduced inﬂuence on the
results. In the PM technique a stabilization diagram is implemented. The stabilization diagram is
in general an illustration of identiﬁed dynamic properties, such as the eigenfrequencies and damping
ratios, for diﬀerent curve ﬁtting model sizes. Using the stabilization diagram two main advantages
can be gained, ﬁrst, it can show how many modes there really is included in a frequency band,
second, by illustrating a stable pole estimate independent of the model size, which can conﬁrm that
the estimate is correct, Brincker et al. [17].

Modal Assurance Criterion
When performing modal analysis in OMA, it is often necessary to compare results from diﬀerent
experiment on the same examined structure or from diﬀerent identiﬁcation techniques using the same
collection of data sets. Comparing eigenfrequencies and damping ratio values is a straightforward
procedure since only single values is compared. But comparing mode shapes, the many degrees-
of-freedom at each examined position make it very challenging to compare these quantities, thus,
a correlation measure is applied. Considering the two mode shape vectors a and b, which is two
diﬀerent measures of the same test mode shape. Hence, the correlation between the two mode shape
vectors can be determined expressed as
MAC(a, b) =

(aHa)(bHb)

This is called the Modal Assurance Criterion (MAC), Brincker et al. [17]. If the mode shape vectors
are very alike the MAC value is close to unity.

7.4.1
Natural Frequency
It is evaluated that the actual natural frequency of the Grevestien footbridge is from a mean value
based on the ambient vibration test for multiple data sets, experiment H1 to H38 executed the
date 17/5-2017, due to the fact, that many ambient experiments have been performed on the bridge,
which gives a well deﬁned statistical description of the natural frequencies. The experiments with the
pedestrian loading were conducted two days earlier than the ambient vibration test with the multiple
data sets which can result in deviations of the natural frequency, due to thermal conditions and/or
air pressure ﬂuctuations and ground vibrations from the motorway traﬃc. Therefore, comparisons
are also made with the natural frequencies from ambient vibration tests performed on the actual
date, experiment A and F.
From the experiment H1 to H38 performed the 17/5-2017 a distribution of the identiﬁed natural
frequency from the diﬀerent OMA methods as presented in section 7.3 is established for each mode
of interest i.e. the bending and torsional modes. Fig. (7.16) to Fig. (7.22) is a diagram showing
the distribution of the natural frequency relative to the identiﬁed frequencies obtained from the
group of experiments consisting of ambient vibration tests and pedestrian induced vibration tests
performed the 15/5-2017. Where Poly Reference (PR) is denoted as ∗, Ibrahim Time Domain (ITD)
as □, Poly Max (PM) as ⃝and Frequency-Domain Decomposition (FDD) as ▽, furthermore, the
mean value of the diﬀerent methods is determined and illustrated as a line. For each experiment
the identiﬁed values is leveled. From Fig. (7.16) it is seen that the natural frequency for mode 1, 2
and 6 have shifted which means that the assumption of the thermal condition and/or air pressure
ﬂuctuations and ground vibrations from the motorway traﬃc is a possibility. Hereby, in this section
a comparison with the ambient vibration test before pedestrian loading measured on the actual day
will also be evaluated. As many literature have been stated a change in the frequency will occur,
due to pedestrian loading since the pedestrian is considered as added mass on the footbridge, which
is also clearly seen from the experiment performed in this study, see Fig. (7.17) to Fig. (7.21). It
has to be noted, that typically for mode 4 for some of the experiment this is not the case, where the
Time Domain technique have a tend to over estimate the natural frequency. Fig. (7.17) conﬁrms
that OMA estimates the frequencies with a great precision, since the identiﬁed frequency from the
responses of the Monte Carlo simulation almost gives the same as the mean value of the ambient
vibration test, which is the frequency inserted in the response calculations, as described in chapter 4.
In general the experiments shows a random shift in the frequency which lead to no linear relationship
between the amount of pedestrian on the bridge and the natural frequency obtained from the test,
this was not expected.

Chapter 8
Discussion
In this part of the project an overall discussion of the present thesis will be presented. A general
discussion of the assumptions, considerations and the results investigated in the study will be given.
8.1
Randomized Load Simulation
8.1.1
Vertical Pedestrian Walking Load
From experimental investigations it has been found that the excitation force from pedestrians are
not perfectly periodic as presented in chapter 2, Fig. (2.2). The footfall force model presented in
chapter 2 of a single pedestrian is based on a Fourier series expansion assuming a single static and
four harmonic components. This assumption will lead to a perfectly periodic excitation of the foot-
fall force of humans which is not true. By looking at the higher harmonics in the Fourier amplitude
spectrum Fig. (2.3) ’leakage’ of the energy into the nearby frequency region typically results in a
reduction in the responses compared to the load model assuming perfect periodicity, Brownjohn et
al. [11], but for the ﬁrst harmonic this eﬀect is of a limited extend.
From previous experimental studies Živanovič et al. [20] it has been found that there are a very well
correlation between the pacing frequency of the pedestrian and the dynamic load factor for the ﬁrst
harmonic as seen in Fig. (2.6), but this is not the case for higher harmonics where the results are
more scattered and the model uses a mean trend line describing the correlation between the DLF
and the pacing frequency. If a random distribution of the scattered DLF results have been performed
a more representative model could be provided in order to chose the DLFs α2 - α4 randomly from
the distributions. The same approach could be assumed for the relation between the stride length
and the pacing frequency, Fig. (2.5) and Eq. (2.2).

8.1.2
Crowd Load Models
Random Pedestrian Characteristic
For generating a randomized crowd loading a model for a single and freely walking pedestrian is
applied. The body masses and pacing rates of the ’unique’ pedestrian is selected randomly from
normal distributions for the Danish population. It is expected that the statistical values of the body
masses from National Institute of Public Health [5] is very representative, since these values are
obtained from a large statistical investigation of the Danish population. But the statistical values
of the pacing frequency from Ingólfsson [5] are of somewhat smaller statistical basis of the Danish
population. It should be mentioned, that the investigation of the pacing frequency performed by
Ingólfsson are conducted under an isolated condition in a lab, where the pedestrians walked at a
comfortable pace, but if these experiments have been conducted in a realistic condition, such as a
subway station with a high human traﬃc a higher mean value of the pacing frequency will have been
obtained. Thus, a higher value of the pacing frequency can lead to a shift in the response spectrum.
Poisson Distribution of Pedestrians
The pedestrian arrivals in the simulation is based on the assumption of a Poisson distribution as
presented by Matsumoto et al. [21]. The Poisson distribution is very well deﬁned, since Matsumoto
et al. performed a series of experiments in Tokyo, Japan, which consisted of counting the number of
pedestrians on a footbridge in 15 minutes with 15 seconds at rush hour. However, some shortcomings
are associated to the Poisson distribution, since assuming a rush hour at all times is unrealistic, as
the probability of such distribution in suburb environments are very limited and leading to higher
inter-arrival times. For considering this lack of assumption a possibility could be to implement the
population density of the given area in the Monte Carlo simulation. Another thing to be considered
is the grouping eﬀect where two or more pedestrians pacing velocities are correlated. The Poisson
distribution do not take grouping eﬀects into account, since only a single pedestrian enters the bridge
for each each arrival time.
Human - Human Interaction
In the Monte Carlo simulation any human-human interaction is not implemented. A future study
based on the human-human interaction could be for instance considering correlation between the
pedestrians in cases of high crowd densities, due to the fact that in a fully correlated crowd pedes-
trians can aﬀect each others pacing frequencies and velocities resulting in higher forces.

8.2
Modal Identiﬁcation of The Grevestien Footbridge
- Ambient Vibration Test
Ambient vibration test with multiple data sets have been performed on the Grevestien footbridge
to identify the modal parameters, where the ambient load consisted of constant loading from wind
gust and motorway traﬃc on the structure, described in the subsection 6.3.1. The ambient vibration
test consisted of a total of 38 measurements, subsequently the modal properties of the footbridge
have been identiﬁed by the method Operational Modal Analysis (OMA) using diﬀerent types of
techniques for the 38 measurements. A statistical calculation of the identiﬁed eigenfrequencies and
the damping ratios have been found for the ﬁrst six modes, since it is assumed that humans do not
excite modes with a frequency higher than approximately 8 Hz, see Fig. (2.3). Furthermore, a mode
shape estimation have been conducted by use of the commercial software ARTeMIS.
The objective of conducting a Monte Carlo simulation was to verify the identiﬁcation methods in
Operational Modal Analysis in relation to harmonics from pedestrian induced loads. The whole
premise of OMA is to test a structure induced by excitations that have similar characteristics of
white noise, but in this study strong harmonics from pedestrians is present in the responses, therefore
a simulation is made to verify if OMA can identify the modal properties with the added harmonics.
The identiﬁed mean modal properties and the mode shapes for the ﬁrst four bending and torsional
modes from the ambient vibration tests were implemented in the Monte Carlo simulation to obtain
the simulated responses of the Grevestien bridge in 38 sections. From the simulated responses an
identiﬁcation have been performed by use of OMA. These identiﬁed modal properties should result
to the same values as the inserted values. A comparison of the natural frequency of the bridge
between the ambient vibration test and the simulated responses have shown a maximum deviation
of approximately 0.1 %.
This small estimation of the deviation mean that Operational Modal
Analysis can identify the eigenfrequency of a given structure under pedestrian induced excitation
with a precision of 0.1 %.
But this is not the case for the identiﬁed damping factor, where a maximum deviation of approx-
imately 20 % was observed. It is seen that this large deviation is caused by the used frequency
domain identiﬁcation methods as presented in Tab. (6.4) and Tab. (6.5). One reason for the dif-
ﬁcult estimation of the damping ratios in the FDD and PolyMax approach is that they are based
on the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), which do not completely correspond to the theoretical
decomposition of the spectral density matrix, which makes the methods based on an approximate
decomposition, leading to an approximate solution i.e. introducing bias. Whereas in the time do-
main technique the approach such as the Poly Reference assumes the correlation functions as free
decays which means that the noise is vanishing. Thus, making the PR method unbiased, due to the
assumption of the noise approximating zero is true. It has to be mentioned, that the estimation of

Lastly, a Modal Assurance Criterion (MAC) was conducted between the experimental mode shapes
and the mode shapes obtained from the Monte Carlo simulation. As expected these MAC values
resulted in a quite good comparison, since the mode shapes used for the simulation was in good
agreement with the obtained mode shapes from simulated responses.

8.3.1
Field Experimental Pedestrian Characteristic
For each random crowd load test with 20, 25, and 33 pedestrians on the bridge a distribution of
the pacing frequencies have been conducted by the recorded videos, see Fig. (7.5) (b) - (d) using
the velocities of each pedestrian. For the European population the mean pacing frequency is µfp
= 1.83 Hz with a standard deviation of σfp = 0.135 Hz.
The mean pacing frequency from the
experiments corresponds well with the European mean pacing frequency, but the standard deviation
from the experiments is signiﬁcantly lower than the standard deviation of the European population.
This, is due to an incorrect execution of the experiments, since the pedestrians did not passed each
other, which led to approximately the same pacing frequency for each pedestrian, meaning that
the experimental obtained distribution did not follow the expected distribution of the European
population. Furthermore, a more realistic pacing frequency could have been obtained if the stride
length of the unique pedestrian where considered, but a resolution of too low quality and a too low
position of the camera made it impossible to decipher the pedestrians at the other end of the bridge.
8.3.2
Ambient Vibration Test
The ambient vibration experiments with multiple data sets were conducted in 18 consecutive hours,
this means that the identiﬁed natural frequencies may have been aﬀected by the thermal conditions
and/or air pressure ﬂuctuations and ground vibrations from the motorway traﬃc during the day.
Hence, to take these aﬀects into account for estimating a better eigenfrequency of the bridge a
diagram of the natural frequencies in relation to the thermal condition could have been conducted.
As the measurement devices did not recorded the exact time of each experiment, due to a reset in
the equipment watch this diagram was not possible to make. The aﬀect from the thermal conditions
can be seen from the diﬀerent ambient vibration test performed on two diﬀerent days, see Fig. (7.16)
in section 7.4. For this reason, comparisons were also made with the natural frequencies obtained
from the ambient vibration tests performed on the same day as the crowd vibration tests. For the
readers acknowledgement it has to be noted, that the ambient vibration test with multiple data
sets performed two days after the crowd tests has a signiﬁcantly better statistical description of the
natural frequency of the bridge in relation to the two ambient vibration tests performed on the same

of 38 experiments. Furthermore, the thermal aﬀects are of limited signiﬁcance as the experiments

8.3.3
Natural Frequency and Damping Ratio
From the analysis of the random crowd experiments it is observed that there is no clear relation
between the amount of pedestrians on the bridge and the identiﬁed frequencies and the damping.
According to many literature e.g. Živanovič et al. [20] a clear relation should be present between
the amount of pedestrians on the bridge and a decrement in the frequency.
8.3.4
Added Mass
In the subsection 7.4.4 the added mass was calculated in terms of the ratio between the natural
frequency of the bridge and the changed frequency of the bridge due to pedestrians. The calculation
showed that the pedestrians adds mass on the footbridge as expected, but the mean weight of the
pedestrians, µWp = 72 kg is not in accordance with the mean added mass obtained, see Fig. (7.34)
to Fig. (7.36). Using the calculation method for the added mass presented in section 2.4 it is seen
that this approach is very sensitive to the frequency ratio of the actual bridge frequency and the
changed bridge frequency due to pedestrians. It was presented in section 6.4 that OMA identiﬁes
the eigenfrequency of the footbridge with a precision of 0.1 %, changing the natural frequency of the
footbridge in the calculations of the added mass with 0.1 % will lead to a change of approximately
25 kg for the ﬁrst mode. This conﬁrms the high sensitivity of the approach, meaning that there is a
limited reliability in the obtained values of the added mass. Furthermore, it was observed that for
higher modes it was diﬃcult to identify the added mass of the pedestrians, a reason for this could
be that the pedestrians did not excited the higher modes.
8.3.5
Vertical Velocity-Proportional Force Coeﬃcient
It was observed in section 7.4.3 that the deviation of the velocity-proportional force coeﬃcient
between the experiments was relatively high, a possible reasons could be that the pedestrians in the
calculations was assumed to be evenly distributed over the footbridge and the pedestrians on the
bridge was modelled as Spring-Mass-Damper (SMD) systems assuming the system as a Two-Degree-
of-Freedom (2DOF) system.
Lastly, it is diﬃcult to state from the experiment B that adding more pedestrians on the footbridge
will lead to a proportional higher damping as seen in Fig. (7.31) to Fig. (7.33).

Experimental Duration
The methods in OMA assumes that a random response follows a Gaussian distribution, but it was
observed from the experiments with a duration less than 10 minutes that the measured responses of
the footbridge did not followed a Gaussian distribution quite well. Thus, the identiﬁed modal prop-
erties obtained from these experiments may contain some inaccuracies leading to higher deviations
in the calculations of the added mass and the velocity-proportional force coeﬃcient.

Conclusion
One of the objectives of this study was to identify the modal properties of a speciﬁc bridge by use
of Operational Modal Analysis. Hence, the Grevestien footbridge was investigated, where ambient
vibration test was performed on the bridge. From the experimental responses the modal properties
was identiﬁed showing that OMA estimates the natural frequencies with high precision as these
values was in good agreement with previous studies of the footbridge by M. N. Svendsen [12].
A presentation of human crowd loading simulation was presented by random time-domain process
using characteristic stochastic force properties to verify the identiﬁcation methods in Operational
Modal Analysis in relation to harmonics from pedestrian induced loads. It can be concluded that
OMA estimates the frequencies with a great precision, since the identiﬁed frequency from the re-
sponses of the Monte Carlo simulation almost gives the same as the mean value of the ambient
vibration test with multiple data sets. Furthermore, the analysis revealed that the identiﬁed damp-
ing ratios from the Monte Carlo simulation deviated from the identiﬁed damping ratios from the
ambient vibration test with multiple data sets. These deviations are caused by bias in the frequency
domain identiﬁcation techniques.
A full scale measurement on the Grevestien footbridge were conducted to study any change in the
modal parameters with diﬀerent types of pedestrian loading using OMA. The identiﬁcation of the
experimental responses from the pedestrian induced vibration test showed that OMA is in general
able to estimate the modal properties of the structure despite the presence of strong harmonics from
walking pedestrians. From the analyzed data of the full scale measurements a clear shift in the fre-
quencies and the damping ratios was obtained when pedestrians crossed the bridge. This is in good
agreement with previous literature, which claims that the frequency of a structure decreases and the
damping increases when considering pedestrian induced vibrations. The amount of the mean added
damping from each pedestrian for mode 1 was found to approximately 900 Ns/m this correspond well
with the investigation by Georgakis et al. [14], where it is suggested that the velocity-proportional

pedestrians adds mass, due to the method used in the study has too high sensitivity in relation to
the frequencies.
