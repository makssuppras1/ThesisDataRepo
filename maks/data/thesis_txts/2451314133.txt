Summary
The development of deep learning models in the latest years has been booming
and proven successful across multiple domains. One of the limitations that many
architectures suﬀer from is strong reliance on high volumes of labeled data. This
is particularly troublesome within medical domain, which is related to high costs
of data acquisition and annotation. This becomes even more problematic for
tasks such as semantic segmentation, which often require pixel-wise annotations.
Therefore, this work investigates approaches that could help reduce the amount
of needed data, while preserving the performance.
In order to do so, an existing fully-convolutional neural networks (DeepMedic)
is used for setting a baseline. The model is trained using various amount of
data, and employed for diﬀerent segmentation task. This model serves then
as a foundation for adversarial networks, which constitute a form of multi-task
learning. Then, the results of the two are compared with each other, both quan-
titatively in terms of performance metrics and qualitatively by inspecting the
visual outputs. Lastly, the adversarial setup is extended with another loss term
that allows utilization of unlabeled data. Due to training diﬃculties, the ar-
chitecture renders unsuccessful. Consequently, instead of comparing its output,
the analysis is shifted towards understanding why this is the case.
The study is performed on BraTS 2018 dataset, which is provided by the authors
of an annual challenge The Multimodal Brain Tumor Segmentation Challenge.
The results demonstrate the dominance of the adversarial setup, which is fur-
ther conﬁrmed by statistical tests. This can be explained by regularizing eﬀect
of multi-task learning which pushes the segmentations towards ones that look
natural and resemble ground truth annotations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and background
Brain tumors are one of the most dangerous and fatal types of cancer. They
are the eﬀect of undesired changes in the cells that lead to their uncontrolled
division and growth. Accurate localization and delineation of a tumor and its’
sub-components are essential steps in the treatment process.
They allow to
determine the best strategy, track disease development as well as assess the
therapy. However, carrying it out manually is a very time-consuming and chal-
lenging task, as the scans are three-dimensional, requiring the person to analyze
multiple cross-sections simultaneously. Moreover, in order to capture various
properties, the scans are often recorded in few diﬀerent setups, what makes
analysis even more complicated and prone to mistakes. Consequently, the anno-
tations must be performed by a trained specialist such as radiologist, rendering
the process very expensive. In order to visualize 3D scans, their cross-sections
need to be taken. This can be done in one of 3 orientations, what is depicted by
ﬁgure 1.1. Examples of a healthy brain and one containing a tumor, captured in
transverse orientation, are shown in ﬁgure 1.2. The diﬀerences in the intensities
are due to pre-processing, which for visualization purposes, has been applied
individually to each image.
Thankfully, nowadays to a considerable degree, the segmentations are done auto-

Machine learning is a broad ﬁeld, which is concerned with studying and build-
ing models that can learn to perform certain task using data instead of being
explicitly programmed to do so. One of its sub-domains is computer vision that
concentrates on processing and analysis of a visual data, such as image. This is
also the category that the problem at hand belongs to. More speciﬁcally, it is
semantic segmentation, where each pixel is assigned a class that it represents.
In other words, a picture is partitioned into semantically meaningful regions,
where each of them corresponds to certain object type. In this case it is tumors’
sub-components and characteristic areas.
Although the problem of tumor segmentation using magnetic resonance im-
ages (MRI) has been heavily studied [HDWF+17] [BPD+18] [SR18] [AVT+17]
[YZW+18], considerable amount of the researches focus on improving perfor-
mance of algorithm that are provided with a signiﬁcant amount of high quality
data. This is possible thanks to competitions and databanks such as The Mul-
timodal Brain Tumor Segmentation Challenge (BraTS) [fBIAS], or BRAINS
Imagebank [aEU]. The former one is the most popular and well-known compe-
tition within brain tumor segmentation and each year attracts numerous teams
from around the world. The attendees tackle the problem using provided high
quality data, which within the medical domain can be considered plentiful. A
more detailed description of the dataset is given in the chapter 4.1.3. This is
thus somewhat artiﬁcial setup that does not correspond very well to real-life.
Especially within medical domain, the data is often scarce, of questionable qual-
ity or lacking the annotations at all. The challenge has two sub-problems, being
the most accurate semantic segmentation and overall survival prediction which
is not part of this thesis. The competition aims to select an algorithm that
segments out the tumor and its sub-regions the most accurately.
The tasks
are segmentation of gadolinium enhancing core, that is active part of the tu-
mor; tumor core, which is composed of necrotic core and both enhancing and
non-enhancing tumor; and lastly the whole tumor.
1.2
Related work
One of the most commonly used algorithms within computer vision are con-
volutional neural networks, which have proven across range of tasks. Theses
are also the main subject of this thesis. However, they are not applicable to all
kinds of problems. This is due to their characteristics, that require high volumes
of data, making them unfeasible for certain problems within medical domain.
As a trade-oﬀ, people sometimes end up using more classical machine learning
approaches, that compromises the performance. The related work within image

segmentation in medical domain, can be thus considered on 2 planes. The ﬁrst
of them being more traditional approaches, that are still being used in real life
by people not being experts in machine learning. These might be for instance
scientiﬁc researchers who need to analyze some data but cannot aﬀord to del-

algorithms.
They also typically require higher volumes of the data that are
usually only available in case of big challenges or databanks. This is more of a
showcase of the capabilities of modern algorithms, that often are problematic
to employ in medical scenarios.
1.2.1
Classical approaches
According to survey [LLW+14] from 2014, many classical methods for semantic
segmentation were still very popular within medical domain few years ago. At
the bottom of this category lie traditional computer vision (CV) approaches. For
instance region-based methods such as simple thresholding or various ﬂavours of
region growing. On high-level, these methods are not based on trainable models,
but iteratively improve the solution until some predeﬁned conditions are met.
Moreover, these methods do not require labels but only deﬁnition of the initial
seed points.
Another approach, that is being used up to this day, is machine learning clus-
tering. One of the most known and common algorithms are k-means and fuzzy
c-means [BBSM18]. These methods are particularly useful for quick prototyping
and handling relatively simple problems. This could be for instance segmenta-
tion of the brain cells in the MRI scans, or ﬁnding the entire tumor. However,
these methods, in their standard implementation, require user to specify the
number of clusters.
On the other end of the spectrum of classical methods, are so-called traditional
machine learning classiﬁer-based approaches.
These methods as opposed to
above mentioned, can work not only with simple numerical data but more com-
plex vector-encoded data points. This opens up possibility for introduction of
some domain knowledge by feature engineering and ﬁnding better embedding
for the data. This category consist of algorithms such as: simple artiﬁcial neu-

is because the models learn the mapping from the vector space to the class.
1.2.2
Modern approaches
Since the years 2012, when the paper on deep convolutional neural network
[KSH12] has been ﬁrst published, the deep learning models became more and
more popular. In many ﬁelds they very quickly surpassed all other methods.
However, this was not the case in medical domain. This is due to two main
features. First, as already mentioned, lack of proper data. Second, the medi-
cal domain is rather conservative and generally relies on methods that are not
ad-hoc and can be explained or manually inspected. On the other hand, deep
learning models have been labeled as black-box algorithms, which are rather
problematic to interpret. Nevertheless, they have proven superior over the tra-
ditional machine learning algorithms in many task across diﬀerent domains.
Thanks to aforementioned BraTS challenge, the research for best algorithms
for semantic segmentation has been pushed forward. The approaches vary from
weakly- and semi- to fully supervised methods. It should also be mentioned
that great majority of the submissions are based on deep architectures.
A very interesting approach to semantic segmentation of infrequent lesions, such
brain tumor, is anomaly detection. The advantage of this method is the fact
that it does not require fully labeled data. Instead, it is enough to know whether
the image or a scan represents healthy brain or one with the tumor. It is thus a
form of weak supervision. This is exactly the approach the authors of [AVT+17]
have taken. To achieve that, they trained two networks. First an autoencoder
learns to reconstruct a healthy brain, what can be then used for detection of
brains’ lesions. The second network is a classiﬁer, that based on the restoration
error, identiﬁes the diﬀerent regions of the tumor. On high level, the method
works in two phases. First, the brains’ representation is learned in unsupervised
manner. This in turn allows to detect regions that are non-healthy brain and

Similarly, to the concept of artiﬁcial neural networks, also the idea of convolu-

ﬁrst presented back in 1968 by Hubel et al. in [HW68]. What it means is that
diﬀerent biological neurons that perform visual processing specialize in recog-
nizing certain characteristics and patterns in spatial manner.

Residual connections
Another approach that addresses the problem of vanishing gradients is intro-
duction of residual connections, also referred to as skip connections. Although
ReLU activation function already tackles this problem, it has been found ben-
eﬁcial to combine both methods.
This is because deep neural networks are
inherently more challenging to train.
As presented by the authors of Deep
Residual Networks [HZRS16a], although deep network should have better ex-
pressive power due to their capacity, they sometimes perform worse than the
shallow networks. In theory, the deep network could learn exactly the same as
its shallow counterpart and then in the subsequent layers apply simple identity
mapping. However, that is not the case in practice, revealing the shortcomings
of optimization process. A solution to this problem is introduction of residual
connections. The key idea behind them is to learn so-called residual function
instead of directly learning the actual mappings that the networks are to model.
A visualization of such connection is shown in Figure 2.9

to estimate. A residual function is formulated as follows F(x) = H(x)−x. Con-
sequently, in case of residual connection the mapping has form H(x) = F(x)+x.
In theory both functions are capable of approximating the true underlying map-
ping. However, because the optimization is done numerically, the optimization
process may strongly depend on the problem formulation yielding diﬀerent re-
sults.

2.2
Bayesian optimization
Machine learning models are typically composed of two kinds of parameters.
Trainable that are optimized throughout the training process and hyperparam-
eters that somewhat constitute architectural design and are chosen prior to the
training. The procedure of their selection is called hyperparameter tuning and
can be done in numerous ways. It is inseparable step in building machine learn-
ing model, that if not done properly may lead to network not being able to learn
anything. The most basic method is manual selection, that will almost always
be sub-optimal. Probably the two most common methods that can be found
equally often used are random and grid search. In the former, the values for the
hyperparameters are simply picked randomly within deﬁned ranges / options,
whereas the latter checks values that are grid-oriented.
Both methods work
somewhat reasonably with low number of dimension but become ineﬃcient as
their number increases. The visualization of both methods is presented in Figure
2.10. The ﬁgure points out shortcomings of the grid search, where unimportant
parameters are given the same amount of attention as the ones that have major
inﬂuence. Random search on the other hand, may be better in exploration but
may never exploit regions close to the best discovered parameters.
Since evaluation of a single set of parameters can be very time-consuming, some-
times taking even days, it seems reasonable to try to anticipate what would be
the next best set of parameters to test. This allows to spend more time on the
parameters that seem promising rather than waste it on the ones that either
do not have much inﬂuence or lead to poor performance. That is exactly what
Bayesian approach [SLA12] attempts to achieve. This is possible by creating a
probabilistic model, called surrogate. It estimates mapping from hyperparame-

2.3
Statistical signiﬁcance
A common scientiﬁc method for evaluating whether or not one method is supe-
rior over the other, are statistical tests. Although, the tests themselves may vary
greatly between each other, the framework is similar in all cases. First a null
hypotheses h0 and an alternatives hypotheses ha are posed. The ha is typically
a negation of h0. The test itself veriﬁes if there is enough, that is signiﬁcant,
evidence to reject the null hypothesis. What should be pointed out here, is
that the wording is very important. In fact, the h0 can either be rejected or
not rejected due to lack of evidence, what results in accepting ha. This means
that h0 is never really accepted, and can only be considered as not rejected.
Therefore, the hypotheses require careful formulation. An example of such is:
H0: the means of two repeated experiments for two diﬀerent methods are the
same, H0: they are diﬀerent. Consequently, if one is to show that one method
is better than the other, the null hypothesis of the equality of the results needs
to be rejected.
A standard way of performing the aforementioned test would be a 2-sample t-
test for means. This, however, works under particular assumptions, such as data
in each sample being normally distributed. This is rather problematic to assume
in case of deep learning. It is because the training of same model with diﬀerent
starting point may result in very diﬀerent networks, as the objective function is
typically very complex. This is why a Mann–Whitney U test [MY16] has been
applied in this work. Its key feature is the fact that it is non-parametric test that
does not make assumptions about the distribution of the data, what makes it
more suitable for the problem at hand. The high-level idea is that, the statistic
is based on the rank - ordering of the observations from smallest to greatest,
and not their absolute values. Once this is done, a value Ri is calculated that is
a sum of the ranks of the observations in sample i. Intuitively, if the two means
were to be equal, the R1 and R2 would be close to each other. This is then used

2.4
Convolutional Neural Network
Convolutional networks are simply networks that have at least one convolu-
tional layers. Most often, however, they consist of multiple such layers making
the models deep. Since their introduction by Krizhevsky et al. in 2012 [KSH12],
they became the most popular approach in various tasks within computer vision
ﬁeld. This is due to their capabilities and expressiveness. Namely, the convolu-
tional layers work as feature extractors, that ﬁnd alternative representation of
the image with by means of the learned ﬁlters. Those if stacked up together lead
to another interesting property, being hierarchical learning [KSB+10]. Specif-
ically, the ﬁrst layers specialize in learning low level features such as edges or
contours, whereas the deeper layers learn more elaborate feature being the com-
binations of these.
Eﬀectively the end layers are better at describing more
complex shapes or even whole objects. This is shown by Figure 2.11.

2.4.1
Fully Convolutional Neural Network
Although, the convolutional neural networks have been designed to handle
higher dimensional data such as images, in vanilla setup they are typically used
in conjunction with dense layers that perform classiﬁcation task. However, these
usually only appear at the end of the network where the dimensionality is greatly
reduced making it feasible to apply dense connections. In some task, however,
it is important not to down scale the input too much while performing convo-
lution and still be able to make some inference. Here, with a help come fully
convolutional networks.
They are essentially networks that only consist of locally connected layers. Ex-
amples of such are convolution, up-sampling or pooling. Accordingly, no fully-
connected also referred to as dense layers can be present. The reason for that
is the restriction that the dense layers carry with them. Namely, they require
speciﬁc sized input but also produce output of the pre-deﬁned size. This may
be perfectly suitable for a classiﬁcation task, where the output size corresponds
to the number of possible classes. Moreover, fully-connected structure gives the
network ability to have more holistic view on the input and learn not only local
but also global dependencies and relations which might be lost in case of locally
connected layers.
2.4.2
Networks for semantic segmentation
In case of semantic segmentation, instead of giving one label to entire picture,
each pixel of an input image needs to be assigned a label. Nonetheless, this
comes at extra computational cost, requiring the network to produce output of
the same size as the input. Consequently, this renders dense layers impractical
for tasks such as semantic segmentation, which would require very high number
of neurons. An example of semantic segmentation is shown in Figure 2.4.2.
An approach that became a common strategy in tackling semantic segmentation
are fully-convolutional neural networks (FCNN) that have been ﬁrst introduced
in 2014 by Long et al. in [LSD]. The key idea is to replace fully-connected with
convolutional layers that make the network able to work with arbitrary-sized
input data but also greatly reduce the number of trainable parameters. As a
result, FCNN can better utilize local spatial information which would be simply
discarded in vanilla CNN containing a fully-connected layer. That is because
of the fact that dense layers can only process vector inputs thus spatial input
would ﬁrst need to be converted into one by ﬂattening. Nonetheless, neither
this approach is perfect. Namely, it comes at a price of losing access to the

2.5
Adversarial networks
The adversarial setup has been ﬁrst introduced by Goodfellow et al. in Gen-
erative Adversarial Net [GPAM+14]. The key idea behind this framework is
that the architecture consists of two networks that compete against each other.
They are called generator G and discriminator D, and as their names suggest
and they belong to generative and discriminative types of algorithms respec-
tively. However, they can be implemented with various types of architectures.
Moreover, typically the network that is the end product is the generator, while
discriminator is only used during the training process to provide feedback to the
generator and discarded afterwards.
A vanilla CNN presented in the previous section, belongs to the group of dis-
criminative models. What it means is that a model, being a classiﬁer, learns
==PAGE:39==
2.5 Adversarial networks
25
conditional probability p(Y |X) directly by network training and adjusting the
parameters’ values. In other words, the model only learns how to categorize the
data into given groups - classes, by modeling the decision boundary, without
considering the underlying distribution.
A generative model on the other hand, tries to estimate the actual joint prob-
ability distribution p(X, Y ). Essentially, it attempts to model the distribution
that the training data originates from. This for example, allows to generate
new samples that come from that distribution.
Thanks to Bayes theorem,
p(Y |X) = p(X, Y )/p(X) = p(X|Y )∗p(Y ) it can also be used to perform the task
of categorization. The underlying idea is however very diﬀerent and typically
leads to diﬀerent results.
2.5.1
Generative adversarial networks
This subsection focuses on the brief introduction of the original setup of GAN
[GPAM+14], as it forms the basis for the architecture used in this work. A
conceptual graph visualizing GAN is shown in Figure 2.13. The goal of this
architecture is synthesis of new samples that come from the same distribution
as training data. In other words, generation of new images that would look
realistically and indistinguishably from the images that have been shown to the
model during the training process. To achieve that, the generator is fed with
a vector of random numbers z that constitutes a latent variable. That is then
mapped through the network into a new image G(z). This in turn is passed to
the discriminator that determines whether or not the sample looks real, which
typically is deﬁned as a probability of sample being real. The discriminator can
either take a generated sample G(z) or a real sample x. The loss functions for
both the generator and the discriminator are deﬁned as follows:
LG = −log(D(G(z)))
LD = −log(D(x)) −log(1 −D(G(z)))
(2.9)
The loss LG is also known as adversarial loss, as its only goal is fooling the dis-
criminator. Ideally for generator, the expression D(G(z)) would be 1 yielding
loss of 0. Discriminator on the other hand, has two terms, ﬁrst of which pun-
ishes for assigning anything else than probability 1 of being real to the actual
real samples, whereas the second term penalizes for not recognizing generated
samples as fakes.
The training of the GANs is inherently challenging process, as the only feedback

Moreover, two competing models have to be trained simultaneously what often

Also the evaluation is rather problematic, as the only measure of performance
is success rate in deceiving discriminator. In fact, the generator might be really
good at doing so while still performing poorly just because the discriminator
does even worse. Nevertheless, the ultimate goal is to have a generator that can
perfectly fool the discriminator, thus the discriminator yields probability of 50%
of being either real or fake regardless of the source of the sample. Consequently,
the discriminator is rather useless and is simply discarded.

Although, the adversarial setup for semantic segmentation originates from the

to original GAN, this architecture lacks a truly generative model. In fact both
networks have discriminative nature and are implemented as fully-convolution
neural network. For clarity, the ﬁrst model is no longer called generator but seg-
mentation network and the architecture is inspired by paper Adversarial Learn-

In contrast to original GAN, the ﬁrst network - segmentation - does not take a

Its output and the ground truth are then passed through

This time however, the network attempts to learn to distinguish ground truth
from produced segmentation. Also its output is diﬀerent. In original GAN, the
output was a binary decision stating the source of the sample. Here, the discrim-
inator produces a conﬁdence map, where each pixel is assigned a probability of
being a real sample. In addition to cross-entropy loss that underlies the semantic
segmentation process, also adversarial loss is applied. It has exactly the same
form and goal as described in Section 2.5.1. This somewhat combines the ideas
of both discriminative and generative approaches. The former being segmen-
tation, which is done by learning class boundaries with cross-entropy and the
latter restricting the output to match the target distribution. As a result, out
of possible segmentations yielding same errors, the adversarial loss should drive
it towards the ones that look the most natural and have the properties of the
ground truth. Interestingly, according to [HTL+], this could have a similar ef-

higher order structural consistencies. Moreover, this architecture represents a

form of multi-task learning [AEP07], which is an interesting problem on its own.
This strategy is often used in order to reduce the number of models that can
perform certain set of tasks, but in this case its goal is to support the training
process, by restricting the search space.
One may wonder, what is the motivation behind discriminator outputting a
conﬁdence map rather than a single binary classiﬁcation for the whole image.
Namely, this opens up a possibility of semi-supervised learning, idea of which
is utilization of both labeled and unlabeled data.
The conﬁdence map can
be interpreted as a measure of the quality of the segmentation for diﬀerent
regions. Since no ground truth information is available for unlabeled images,
they could not be directly used with cross-entropy. These are thus created with
an aid of discriminator.
If the probability of being a ground truth is above
certain threshold Tsemi, the segmentation is assumed to be reasonably good
and the initial segmentation is converted into actual ground truth ˆy.
This
kind of learning is known as self-taught scheme [RBL+07] as the model further
strengthens its own behaviour and habits. It may be surprising at ﬁrst, but
this methods has proven to be successful in certain cases [HTL+] [LPLN09] by
introducing additional variability preventing overﬁtting and enlarging data set
at very low cost. This is due to the fact that extra samples allow to ﬁnd better
representation and embedding. In original GAN, the ultimate objective was
fooling the discriminator every single time. Here, however, the desired result
is a good segmentation that can fool the discriminator which is implemented
as multi-task learning. Also, it should be mentioned that once segmentation
network gets very good at deceiving discriminator, the self-taught learning is
no longer applicable as discriminator does not provide valuable feedback on the
quality of the segmentation.

3.1.1
Architecture
This model has been created after analysis of the similar approaches for semantic
segmentation within the medical domain. As a result the authors have devised
an architecture that is at the same time eﬀective and eﬃcient in handing 3D
highly unbalanced data. The diagram of the architecture is presented in Figure
3.1.
This architecture made three main contributions. First, all the convolutional
ﬁlters have small spatial sizes of 33. This is a very important design feature
in case of 3D CNNs that helps keep the number of trainable parameters on
reasonable level. However, such design leads to having small receptive ﬁelds
that eﬀectively restrict the model to only extracting strictly local features. This
problem is mitigated by introduction of the dual pathway, which is the second
major contribution. The second branch processes the image in lower resolution
thus can focuses on more global dependencies and features. On the presented
diagram, it corresponds to the lower branch, that takes as input a patch that is
downscaled by a factor of 3. The method suggested by the authors is simply to
create a patch by sub-selecting every third pixel. Consequently a patch having
similar spatial resolution covers much bigger region, what results in loss of ﬁne
details but enhancement of more global structures. An example of how such
patches look like is shown in Figure 3.2.
Each branch consists of 8 convolutional modules after which their outputs are

The model made one more major contribution.
It is the training procedure
which is referred to as a hybrid scheme since it combines best of both dense
training and so-called patch-wise classiﬁcation. In this context, the word dense
does not relate to applying dense-layers, but rather processing the entire picture
at once without splitting it into smaller segments. Patch-wise, on the other
hand, means that a patch equal in size to the receptive ﬁeld is extracted and
passed through the network. As a result, in such setup, the network performs
a prediction only for the central voxel which is the consequence of downscaling
the input through applying convolution. In spite of being very straightforward,
this approach has a major drawback.
It is very computationally ineﬃcient.
For instance, in order to predict the classes of two adjacent voxels, all the
calculations would need to be repeated, even though they have overlapping
receptive ﬁelds. However, this strategy, allows to easily control and balance the
ratio of the training data with respect to the classes that they belong to. This
==PAGE:46==
3.1 DeepMedic
32
can be achieved by simply forming the batches in such a way that the samples
representing each class account for desired percentage. To avoid biasing the
model too much towards the rare classes, it has been suggested to sample the
patches such that 50% is centered on the background and the other 50% on
the foreground voxels, being tumor classes. This is particularly useful property
within the medical domain, where the classes are often highly imbalanced.
On the contrary, in the dense training scheme, the fed patches have the size of
the original images. It is the most computationally optimal approach as each
area of the image is passed through the network only once. This, however, is
often very impractical, especially in case of 3D data. It is due to the fact, that
the networks themselves grow big when 3D data is involved, as all the ﬁlters
need to be cached, ﬁlling most of the GPU memory. As a result, this approach
can not be employed in this form. Instead, as a combination of both, the created
patches have greater spatial size than the receptive, but small enough to ﬁt into
the GPU’s memory. Thanks to this, the calculations for overlapping patches are
shared what greatly reduces the amount of computations. At the same time,
it also makes it possible to control unequal classes distribution by sampling,
that is the feature of the ﬁrst approach. Moreover, combination of these two
training schemes has one more important property, that is self adjustment to
the classes distribution. Even though, the central voxels of the patches have
50% chance of being either background or foreground, it says nothing about the
remaining voxels of the patch, which might belong to any other class. This is
particularly visible for the patches that cover the classes boundaries. In case of
the central voxel being centered on some rare class, the patch is likely to capture
other voxels belonging to the same class - foreground, but also the background.
Eﬀectively, the distribution of the foreground classes in the training dataset gets
adjusted to their true relative distributions.
Lastly, the original setup also includes a conditional random ﬁeld (CRF) [KK11]
as a post-processing method which is applied on the output of the DeepMedic.
It has been constructed as a function of 2 potentials, being unary and pairwise.
This, however, has been omitted in this work as they only improve the perfor-
mance marginally while introducing extra complexity. Moreover, the main focus
of this thesis are end-to-end models.

not uncommon to train one of the networks for more iterations than the other,
in order to balance them. An example of such approach is Wasserstein GAN
[ACB17], that is an improvement of original GAN in terms of training stability.
Because this solution appeared to be a common strategy for handling such is-
sue, an extra hyperparameter has been introduced, that is named alteration. It
determines the number of iterations that the segmentation network should be
trained for, before doing one training iteration of the discriminator. Training
both nets alternately is a special case, where alteration = 1, and eﬀectively can-
cels the need for this parameter. Lastly, there is a lambda coeﬃcient, denoted
in 3.2 as λadversarial, that weights the importance of the adversarial loss. The
table 3.2 presents the chosen values together with their ranges.
The selected alteration = 6 may be considered somewhat high compared to
naive solution with training both networks alternately. However, it seems to
indicate that this parameter is in fact beneﬁcial and leads to better perfor-
mance. Moreover, it appears that the alteration is strongly correlated with the
lrdiscriminator. In fact, comparable results can be obtained using alteration of
9 or even 10, which require though accordingly higher learning rate, to compen-
sate for little training. Also, the winning λadversarial is not distinctly dominate
over other values, and diﬀerent coeﬃcients may work well with certain learning
rates and alteration.

Although, in this setup there were fewer parameters to be tuned in comparison
to DeepMedic, higher number of trials were needed due to lack of clear pat-

validation cross-entropy loss and not the total loss from equation 3.2. This was

3.2.3
Training remarks
Generally, training adversarial networks is much more challenging than a stan-
dard convolutional network.
This is no surprise, since the hyperparameters
not only have to be good enough for the network to train at all, but also be
robust such that they do not kill the progress of the other network. One of
the observations is that such setup is extremely sensitive to even small changes
of hyperparameters, what emphasizes the need for their proper tuning. This
became apparent when performing the hyperparameter search. During one of
the runs, in which the networks were strongly competing against each other,
preventing either of them from getting really good, the breakthrough happened
after 47 epochs. It was a point, when the discriminator’s loss went suddenly
drastically down, what further led to increase of the generator’s loss because of
high adversarial loss. Ultimately, the generator network started diverging and
the whole training was killed.
Furthermore, it is worth noting that one of the dangers stemming from multi-
task learning is destructive inference. The term originates from physic where
two waves interfere with each other in such a way, their sum is less than either
of them alone.
Similar phenomenon may happen when training network for
unrelated or weakly related tasks [ZLS+18]. This is a consequence of conﬂicting
gradients, such as having opposite signs thus leading to two opposite updates.
Training process has proven challenging for one more reason, that is its supervi-
sion. In case of a classical neural network with a single loss it is rather straight-
forward. The goal is to minimize a validation loss what ultimately ought to
maximize the performance. This can be easily tracked together with a train-
ing loss and nicely visualized with an aid of learning curves. Adversarial setup
is more complicated than that. The value of adversarial loss deﬁned by equa-
tion 3.2 does not necessarily indicate whether or not the performance improves.
This is because a better discriminator leads to higher adversarial loss, but in
fact both, the segmentation net and the discriminator might have improved. In
other words, two good networks might yield same adversarial and discriminator
==PAGE:53==
3.3 Semi-supervised adversarial networks
39
losses as two poorly performing networks because the losses are relative to their
opponents. They should be thus thought of as feedback signals for the train-
ing, but their absolute values are problematic to interpret. The goal during the
training was to have a discriminator that is better than random, but is neither
perfect. This was dictated by the ultimate purpose that the discriminator was
to serve. This has been brieﬂy described in section 2.5.2. Trivially, close to
random discriminator would be useless in providing any meaningful feedback
to the segmentation network. On the other hand, one that has nearly 100%
accuracy would almost never get fooled thus no unlabeled data would be used
for training. More on this topic can be found in subsequent section.
3.3
Semi-supervised adversarial networks
The last and most advanced solution is a further extension of the above presented
adversarial architecture. This setup investigates the idea of self-taught learning
[RBL+07] [PKD15] that enables to utilize unlabeled data, which is not possible
with neither DeepMedic nor adversarial networks from section 3.2. This is the
very reason for having the discriminator output a conﬁdence map rather than a
simple binary classiﬁcation for the entire image. A high score in the conﬁdence
map means that discriminator believes that the sample has been drawn from
the ground truth, which in turn can be interpreted as realistic looking and
plausible segmentation. All the voxels that have high scores associated with
them are then converted into ground truth and will be used for training. On
the other hand, low scores mean that the discriminator can tell voxels apart
from ground truth, so they are not that great and should not be used during
the training. Speciﬁcally, if the probability is above the threshold Tsemi, the
created segmentation is converted into ground truth ˆy, otherwise it is skipped.
As a result, only some regions of the segmentation are converted into labels
creating a masked label. An example of such conversion is presented in Figure
3.4.
This is a crucial contribution of the self-taught learning, as some way of assessing
the quality of segmentation is needed. Otherwise, if all of the segmentation
was converted into new labels, a lot of noise would be introduced. Potentially,
that would further lead to destruction of the network weights and performance
deterioration.

In order to evaluate models performance, an appropriate metric had to be cho-

over union (IoU), straightforward pixel-wise accuracy and a Dice-score, which
is also known as F1-score. In fact, the IoU and Dice-score are closely related as

what makes them particularly suitable for highly unbalanced datasets. IoU can
be seen as a strict metric that weights the same true positives as false positives
and negatives. It is thus very sensitive to any falsely predicted samples what
may be a desired property in case they are detrimental and should be avoided.
As a result, even seemingly good performing model may have scores far away
from ideal 1.0.
Dice-score on the other hand is less punishing, by rewarding the true positives
more. It may be considered a better option when the complete segmentation
is preferred over avoiding few more mispredictions. Consequently, it appears to
be more suitable for the task at hand. Additionally, it is one of the metrics that
have been selected by the authors of BraTS challenge what makes the obtained
results easier to compare with algorithms of other contestants.
4.1.3
Data
BraTS
As previously mentioned, BraTS is a dataset that contains brain multimodal
magnetic resonance images. More precisely, dataset from last edition (2018)
consists of 3D scans of pre-operative brains with gliomas.
A glioma is the
most common brain malignancy, although can also occur in the spinal cord,
that emerges from glial cells.
A glioma can be further classiﬁed either as a
low-grade (LGG) or high-grade (HGG). This is assigned based on the tumor’s
aggressiveness and ability to spread. The LGGs tend to be more heterogeneous
than HGGs, in a way that the diﬀerent regions are less distinctive.
HGGs are malignant tumors that can develop very rapidly and require imme-
diate treatment, often being surgery, chemo- or radiotherapy. They are life-
threatening formations with really high mortality rate.
LGGs, on the other
hand are considered less dangerous, due to slower growth rate but also should
not be neglected as they can convert into HGG.
For sake of completeness, it should be mentioned, that there are major diﬀer-
ences in datasets from 2017 onward. First of them is the fact that in the previous
years also the post-operative scans were included. Moreover, since 2017 all the
training data has been collected according to the same protocol. That was not
the case in 2014-2016 editions, where part of the data has been annotated with
highly performing algorithms from previous years and only visually veriﬁed by
humans [MJB+14]. Now, all the data has been manually labeled by one to four
annotators that subsequently has been approved by neuroradiologists to ensures
high quality.

In order to mitigate the problem of limited amount of data, that may easily lead
to overﬁtting, data augmentation has been employed. These, although rather
simple, are eﬀective methods for introducing variability and adding training
data at very low cost. The used techniques are:
• Flipping around the axes
• Gaussian noise addition
• Image rotation
All the transformations are applied with probability of 50%.
Pre-processing
Although the distributors of the data have taken the ﬁrst steps towards pre-
processing, further steps are required to make the data suitable for machine
learning models. Even though, one may argue that CNNs do not require much
pre-processing and can simply handle RGB images, intensities of MRI are not
standardized or bounded by any value making the transformation necessary.

This is typically addressed by normalization or standardization. The idea be-
hind the former is application of min-max scaling that brings the data into
bounded 0 −1 range.
However, because it relies on the maximal value, the
method is sensitive to outliers. This is a reason why standardization appears a
better choice in this case. The methods makes the data zero-mean unit-variance

methods of the MRI datasets [RMC+12]. For the completeness it should be
noted that, because of class distribution skewness, the pixels that do not belong

as they are not used during the model training anyway. Lastly, all these oper-
ations are conducted per channel, being the modality, as it typically is done in
case of RGB images.

Discussion
The experiment conﬁrms what was anticipated. The increase of the data leads
to better performance. Additionally, when the whole tumor or tumor core are
considered, the increase of the data decreases the standard deviation, making
the training more stable. The reason is that the tumors do not have ﬁxed, pre-
deﬁned shapes or location. Also they vary greatly in terms of their sub-regions
which may account for diﬀerent percentage of the whole tumor.
This is very nicely depicted by the red line, denoting the mean, that is consis-
tently going up, whereas the standard deviation is getting smaller and smaller
- the blue lines getting closer to each other. The learning curves also show that
the increase of the performance is not constant with addition of the new data. In
fact, it appears to plateau from 75%, with a rather small increase when trained
with all 100% of the data. One can also conclude that segmentation of tumor
core is the most sensitive to varying amount of data. This is depicted in 2 ways,
the steepest mean score curve, but also the the biggest change in the variance
between diﬀerent runs with various percentage of data. These trends can be
also noticed in two other sub-tasks but to a lesser extent.
At this point it should be mentioned that even though, the values reported in
other studies [KBF+17] [KNZ+] [WLOV] [ZWS+18], when trained with all of
the data, appear to be higher, they were obtained using 5-fold cross validation.
This reveals that not all of the data is equally useful either due to the varying
quality or representativeness. Namely, if that was the case, the performance
would rather be independent of the data split. This problem becomes apparent
when preforming evaluation on the test dataset, that is shown in table 4.3.
For instance, the rows 1 and 2 would indicate that the algorithm is capable of
performing well with respect to all classes, whereas rows number 7 or 10 tell
diﬀerent story. This is partly due to diﬀerences in annotations among labelers, as
well as the fact that LGGs are less distinctive than HGGs, and sometimes certain
regions are hard to distinguish or even localize. Consequently, the annotations
of LGGs are much more prone to having discrepancies or even miss certain
classes. In fact, both row number 7 and 9 scored 0 in segmenting enhancing
tumor. This is not due to poor performance, but simply lack of the given class
in the scan. As previously mentioned, it may seem misleading to include them
in evaluation and average score, as they will only lower the performance metric,
but this form of evaluation is consistent with other papers. After all, this will
be invariant between diﬀerent models as they all score 0 for given scans. The
table also shows that the scores for segmentation of the tumor core happen to
surpass the performance for the whole tumor, but its segmentation is less stable
and more variable. This is exactly the issue which has been discussed in section
4.1.3, that originates from the diﬃculty of the problem, combined with the fact

that the data comes from diﬀerent sources. When marking the whole tumor,
people are somewhat consensual, but when it comes to assigning the labels to
the sub-regions of the tumor, the boundary areas are not so clear. This can also

with only 10% of the data.

in the task of semantic segmentation when trained with all 100% of the data.
Another question that it is to answer, is whether the same performance can be
obtained with smaller portion of the data. Although, the posed questions seem
somewhat connected, the answers to them may diﬀer. These are interesting
points to examine, while the eﬀect of the extra loss might be diﬀerent when
used in conjunction with small and reasonably big datasets.

4.3.2
Discussion and analysis
The experimental results presented in table 4.4 and ﬁgure 4.4 show that the
average performance of adversarial setup is superior over DeepMedic. In fact,
this is the case for all the task and conﬁgurations, and the diﬀerence vary be-
tween 2.84 for tumor core segmentation when trained using 10% of the data,
to minimal diﬀerence of 0.04 for the same task when trained with 25% of the
data. Moreover, in case of DeepMedic it can be noticed that the performance,
for 2 out of 3 tasks, starts to plateau when training with 75% of the data. The
same can not be concluded for the adversarial setup. As a matter of fact, for
the same 2 tasks, being whole tumor and enhancing tumor segmentation, the
curves become more steep instead of ﬂattening. This might indicate that the
adversarial loss acts as a regularizer that helps during the optimization process.
Consequently, it appears that more training data could further lead to great
improvements, which does not seem the case for DeepMedic.
Although the average performance have increased, it does not necessarily mean
that the method is consistently superior. In order to verify this, it is important to
also analyze the standard deviations. This, at least to certain degree, indicates
how stable and consistent the methods is. Based on the table 4.5, it can be
immediately concluded that the adversarial training does not lead to decrease
of standard deviation in all the setups. On average, this seems to be the case
when 50% or less of data is used, and the opposite when trained with 75 or
100%. Nonetheless, if only segmentation of the whole tumor is concerned, the
standard deviation of the results of adversarial networks is lower for 4 out of 5
setups - except for when trained with 25%. Lastly, it should be noticed that the
adversarial setup never reaches as extreme deviations between the scores as the
DeepMedic. This is particularly visible for 10 and 25% of the data.
In order to properly determine whether or not one of the methods is superior
than the other, statistical tests have been employed. Even though, by inspecting
the values, one could conclude that DeepMedic does not perform as good as
adversarial network, it is not a valid way of making such decision. Instead, 15
tests were run, one for each setup, being combination of segmentation task and
percentage of data used for training. Out of all the tests, only 3 are signiﬁcant.
Two of them when used 100% of the data, and performing segmentation of either
whole or enhancing tumor. Since only 4 repetitions have been made for each
setup, generally it is not easy to obtain signiﬁcance unless one method is strictly
dominating. Also Mann-Whitney U test is a non-parametric test that does not
rely on raw data, disregarding the magnitudes of the diﬀerences between the
groups, it becomes even more diﬃcult to reject the null hypothesis with such
small samples.
==PAGE:73==
4.3 Comparison between DeepMedic and Adversarial Networks
59
Nonetheless, the test is a multiple hypothesis testing setup, the analysis of the
results need to be carried out carefully. This is due to the fact, that the proba-
bility of making a false inference increases when doing it multiple times. More
details on this problem can be found in paper named A general introduction to
adjustment for multiple comparisons [CFY17]. Eﬀectively, this indicates a need
for α adjustment or at least analysis of how many signiﬁcant results are selected
as such due to a chance. For this purpose, an analysis of false discovery rate
[S+03] has been conducted, which indicated that out of 3 signiﬁcant results,
none is expected to be false discovery.
The second test has been designed in such a way, that it does not suﬀer from
insuﬃcient number of observations. This does not allow to perform the test for
each conﬁguration, but instead to determine on a global scale, whether or not
the adversarial setup is better. If both models performed the same, the sample
mean µ, being diﬀerences between the scores, would be close to 0. However,
this is not the case, with µ = 1.4573, that according to the t-test is signiﬁcantly
higher.
Although the ﬁrst test, due to a small number of repetitions, did not have
enough statistical power to reliably conclude that adversarial setup is superior,
this has been done by the second one. Thus, this allows to answers previously
posed research questions. First conclusion is that adversarial learning indeed
boosts the performance when all 100% of the data is used for training. One
possible explanation for such eﬀect is that the addition of the adversarial loss
regularized the optimization process. This claim is supported by the shapes of
the learning curves that do not ﬂatten out at the ends. This is further inves-
tigated in section 4.5, where the qualitative results are presented. The other
question asked whether adversarial setup allows to reduce the amount of the
data used for training while preserving the same level of performance. This
could be veriﬁed with a similar statistical tests to the one conducted above. A
possible test would be a comparison of the performance for DeepMedic trained
with all 100% of the data, whereas adversarial setup only with 75%. However,
this would require many more observations, as the diﬀerences between the re-
sults are even smaller than in the performed test. Nevertheless, the average
scores for adversarial setup trained with 25% less data than DeepMedic appears
very comparable. This is particularly the case of whole and enhancing tumor
segmentation. This appears to hold through the whole range, even when the
adversarial networks are trained with 25% and DeepMedic 50% of the data.

4.4.2
Discussion and analysis
The ﬁgure 4.5 indicates a failure in training the model with self-taught learning.
This pattern has been reoccurring for most of the hyperparameters. The other
observed outcome was less drastic divergence of the losses which was a result of
the classical overﬁtting. This was not very interesting case, as it only occurred
due to lack of self-taught learning. This happened when either λself was very
small or mask_threshold high, which both ultimately led to the same eﬀect. In
fact, the values selected during the hyperparameter search follow this pattern,
what limits the eﬀect of self-taught learning. Nonetheless, even such conﬁgura-
tion had a destructive impact on the networks’ weights. In the shown example,
the lowest validation loss was recorded around epoch 101, which is right after
the self-taught learning has been enabled. However, in other trainings, it often
never surpassed the minimum loss at epoch 100. In addition, around epoch 104,
something critical happens in the network, which is reﬂected by sharp spike
in the curve. Both facts indicate a problem that is the result of addition of
self-taught loss.
A possible explanation of such behaviour is that the newly generated labels are
not accurate enough. This in turn introduces undesired noise and inconsistency
to the network.
In order to verify this claim, a snapshot of the network at
epoch 100 has been saved and used for creating new labels according to process
presented in ﬁgure 3.4. For this purpose, unseen images from testing subset have
been used as it was necessary to have the real ground truths. The test consisted
in generating new labels with an aid of discriminator and benchmarking them
against the true labels.
The results are presented with an aid of confusion
matrices in tables 4.9 to 4.12. The highlighted values are the correctly predicted
classes, whereas the last column is the voxels that did get assigned any class.
This is because the probability that they got assigned by the discriminator was
below the threshold.
The confusion matrices conﬁrm what was expected.
If a lower threshold is
being applied, there are indeed many misclassiﬁcations, whereas high threshold
results in disregarding most of the predictions. However, two classes appear
to be able to beneﬁt from this strategy.
They are the background (class 0)
being healthy brain and non-brain regions as well as enhancing tumor (class
3). Nevertheless, class 0 is heavily undersampled when creating the patches,
so it would be more reasonable to reuse real ground truth rather than obtain
extra data this way.
It can also be noticed that with lower thresholds, the
background is sometimes mistaken for edema (class 2), whereas tumor core
(class 1) is classiﬁed as enhancing tumor (class 3). This is likely because these
two pairs of classes appear next to each other and the boundary regions are often
unclear. Also the annotations made by diﬀerent labelers lack consistency. The

make the segmentation look more realistic, and resemble the ground truth. The
gaps are not really expected in the ground truths, especially between the se-
lected classes. Edema typically adheres to the tumor core, as it is caused by it.
Consequently, adversarial setup is expected to prevent the gaps from occurring.
Nevertheless, these might still happen, what can be observed in ﬁgure 4.8g. A
possible explanation for it is lack of agreement between labelers, and the way the
ﬁnal annotations are merged together, what may result in ragged ground truth.
Also, the gaps have diﬀerent characteristics than the one in the above example.
Previously, it was fully surrounded by edema from one and necrotic core from
other side. Here the gaps appear within edema. Consequently, it may seem
that adversarial setup is better, yet it is DeepMedic that yields higher score for
whole and enhancing tumor segmentation. In order to further analyze it, the
segmentation of edema, not being part of tumor core, is shown in ﬁgure 4.10.
In addition, the number of voxels classiﬁed as edema are counted, which are:
34595 for DeepMedic, 45342 for adversarial and only 11679 in the real ground
truth. This makes clear that the adversarial setup oversegments edema, what
ultimately lowers the score of whole tumor segmentation.
==PAGE:85==
4.5 Qualitative evaluation
71
Similarly to the ﬁrst example, also in ﬁgure 4.8, the adversarial setup has some-
what gap-closing eﬀect, when compared with DeepMedic. This is particularly
visible in edema, but also the enhancing tumor. Nonetheless, both methods
seem to produce very similar tumor core segmentation, which is a combination
of necrotic core and enhancing tumor - green and red respectively. A high level
of similarity could be expected due to the dice scores being on par with each
other. This, however, is not the case of edema. Both algorithms seem to take
somewhat diﬀerent approaches in marking it. DeepMedic has some tendency
to underdetect it, whereas adversarial setup seems to overdetect it in certain
regions. This can observed on the right side of the segmentations.
The last, third case is the most common scenario, where the DeepMedic turns
out to be inferior compared to adversarial setup. Also the performance of both
models is closer to the mean scores. Therefore, ﬁgure 4.9 represents a somewhat
typical case. This time, the problems are not only the boundaries of the classes,
but also their inner areas, which are not the same between segmentations and
ground truth. Despite many ﬂaws, this example shows dominance of adversarial
setup over the DeepMedic. This is the case in all 3 tasks that were measured.
First of them is whole tumor segmentation, where the adversarial networks pro-
duce much more complete result. The same applies to tumor core segmentation,
which is depicted by the last row. The last task is detection of enhancing tumor
only, thus the red marked regions.
Although, the adversarial setup is not always better than the DeepMedic, cer-
tain characteristics can be noticed. Namely, it appears to introduce higher-level
of consistency in the segmentation. This is reﬂected by gap reduction and edge
smoothing. However, it does not prevent all undesired artifacts and is far from
ideal. An example of such is presence of edema which does not appear in the
neighbourhood of the tumor core. These could probably be further minimized
with an aid of post-processing such as Conditional Random Fields or some
heuristic restrictions. Adversarial setup also produces more complete segmen-
tations, which sometimes comes at the cost of oversegmenting, compared to
the ground truth. This is especially the case for edema. On the other hand,
DeepMedic has a tendency of undersegmenting certain areas.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
5.1
Summary and discussion
In this thesis, a comparison of diﬀerent fully convolutional architectures for se-
mantic segmentation have been presented. The most basic model was DeepMedic,
which served as a foundation for further extensions. The model underwent mi-
nor improvements and full hyperparameter tuning using Bayesian Optimization.
Afterwards, it was trained multiple times with various percentage of available
data, in order to investigate the impact that the amount of data has.
These results were then compared with the scores returned by adversarial setup.
The main idea behind it was having two network-architecture, where the models
attempt to undermine each other. The ﬁrst one is DeepMedic-based segmen-
tation model, exactly the same as previously.
The second model is a fully
convolutional neural network that acts as a discriminator. Its task was to diﬀer-
entiate produced segmentations from the real ground truth. This was then used
to provide feedback to the segmentation network, what drove the segmentations
towards more realistic looking ones. This setup was inspired by other work, that
has been previously applied to RGB images presenting natural objects. In this
work, the data was 3D MRI scans, what required complete reimplementation.
Afterwards, an even more complex setup was created. The adversarial networks
==PAGE:87==
5.2 Future work
73
have been extended with an extra loss term, that introduced self-taught learning
scheme. However, the training did not succeed, and the analysis shifted towards
understanding why this has happened. This could be partly explained by the
dataset’s speciﬁcation. The training classes were in fact sub-classes of a tumor,
which sometimes could not be easily determined and distinguished. After further
investigating it turned out that also the human labelers would often disagree
on the boundary regions. Consequently, the labels generated with self-taught
learning scheme were noisy and error-prone. This eﬀectively led to destruction
of the network’s weights preventing from successful training.
As a result, the main focus of this work was comparison of the two ﬁrst architec-
tures. It started with discussion of empirical results, where adversarial setup has
proven superior. Then, the models were subjected to statistical tests in order
to conﬁrm advantage of adversarial model. Speciﬁcally, two diﬀerent kinds of
tests were carried out. The ﬁrst of them, due to small number of observations,
had limited statistical power. In order to overcome this limitation, the second
test has been designed. Instead of testing all the conﬁgurations separately -
diﬀerent segmentation tasks and percentage of data used for training - the test
has been modiﬁed such that the average performance in all conﬁgurations were
compared at once. This test had enough statistical power to reliably indicate
that adversarial setup indeed yields better results.
Lastly, the qualitative analysis of the results have been performed. This indi-
cated that the adversarial setup has one more merit. It introduces higher-level
consistency in the segmentations, which have smoother class boundaries but
also closing of small undesired gaps. This has thus somewhat similar eﬀect to
post-processing, which could be obtained with closing morphological operation
or CRFs. Nonetheless, the produced results are far from perfect, leaving a room
for future improvements. It has also be observed that DeepMedic leans towards
undersegmentation, whereas adversarial setup has a tendency of oversegmenting
the tumor. Consequently, this makes the outputs more complete and results in
higher average performance.
5.2
Future work
The work presented in this thesis shows the potential of multi-task learning
implemented as adversarial networks, for purpose of network regularization.
Although, this can be considered successful, due to improved performance, it
could be further improved. The next step in this research could be an attempt
to make the whole process more stable, such that the impact and performance
of the discriminator is more uniform between multiple trainings. For this reason
==PAGE:88==
5.2 Future work
74
more adaptive solution could be investigated. Instead of training both networks
for pre-deﬁned number of iterations, this could be done according to needs.
It would also be interesting to verify whether the self-taught learning in this
form would work in simpliﬁed 2-class setup, with tumor and background only.
Due to time constraints, this has been tried reusing the hyperparameters found
for 4-class setup. These, however, where not suitable and would need to be fully
tuned from scratch.
In case of 4-class setup, additional restrictions could be imposed on the labels
created with self-taught learning.
For the time being, it is purely based on
the feedback coming from discriminator. As long as the probability assigned
to a voxel by the discriminator is above given threshold, it is used for training.
However, it could be beneﬁcial to also check how certain is the segmentation
network about the class of that voxel. For this reason, the second threshold
could be applied. It would ﬁlter only the voxels which highest scoring class
has probability above certain level. Theoretically, at the moment, the highest
scoring class might have a probability of just above 0.25, with 3 remaining
classes being just below 0.25, and if the discriminator accepts it, it will be used
for training.
Lastly, it appears that this approach could further gain from post-processing or
additional regularization. Although the adversarial loss drives the segmentation
towards ones that look more realistic, they still contain numerous artifacts. An
idea would be to penalize for very small regions appearing on their own, away
from the rest of the tumor. Another option would be to add heuristics such
as: edema should always adhere to tumor core. This could be easily done in
post-processing step.
