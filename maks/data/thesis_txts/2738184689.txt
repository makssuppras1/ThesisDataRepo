Summary
This master thesis investigates the development of the startup CETO Innovation, with
a focus on its progression through the Innovation Readiness Level (IRL) framework.
The primary goal is to evaluate CETO Innovation’s preparedness for incorporation
as a startup and its potential for future growth and to understand the importance of
the Advisory Board in early-stage startups.
The project originated from the X-Tech Entrepreneurship course at DTU, spark-
ing the author´s interest in project management, team facilitating, innovative design
thinking within the dynemic environment typical for startups. Also the idea of cre-
ation for the better future, motivated the author to proceed with the project.
A key challenge addressed in this thesis is understanding the complexities and cur-
rent issues within Denmark’s District Heating (DH) market. The DH system is crucial
for the country’s green energy infrastructure but is plagued by a historically intricate
underground network. Today’s challenges include aging infrastructure, limited real-
time data, and reactive asset management, which leads to costly unplanned repairs
and operational disruptions. CETO Innovation distinguished itself by confronting
these challenges head-on and devising an innovative solution. By leveraging state-
of-the-art technology, CETO Innovation aims to provide comprehensive insights into
pipeline conditions, thereby enabling proactive maintenance and reducing operational
ineﬀiciencies.
The study aims to achieve two main objectives. Firstly, it seeks to validate the
early-stage startup, CETO Innovation, ensuring it reaches a level of maturity that
warrants company registration and can attract funding. Secondly, the study explores
the application of the IRL framework in supporting startup processes, focusing on
various critical aspects such as customer readiness, technology readiness, sustainable
business models, intellectual property rights, team readiness, and funding readiness.
Additionally, the research aims to determine the eﬀicacy of the IRL framework as a
guiding tool for Board meetings and to identify which axes within the framework are
most crucial for the startup’s success.
The methodology employed in this study includes a mix of qualitative and quan-
titative approaches such as interviews with startups and industry experts, literature
review, analysis of meeting agendas, minutes, and reports, as well as practical applica-
tions through workshops and strategic planning sessions aimed at driving innovation
and tracking progress.
Findings from this study indicate significant advancements in several areas: cus-

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Entrepreneurship is a crucial driver of innovation systems, providing fresh goods
and services to the market and fostering innovation among established businesses [1]
(Nielsen et al., 2021). The term ”startup” has emerged as a buzzword in contempo-
rary policy discussions and public debates. Remarkably, each year, approximately
100 million startups are launched worldwide, significantly impacting their respective
economies [2] (Ehsan, 2021). The widespread interest in creating startups is hardly
surprising, given their rapid growth potential and flexibility in pivoting—a contrast
to the inertia often observed in larger corporations, which typically lack the agility to
experiment and shift direction swiftly. Startups frequently originate within academic
environments, where ambitious, creative minds accumulate extensive knowledge. Fur-
thermore, the collaboration between universities and industry serves as a major source
of innovative ideas.
This thesis examines a startup conceived within the Technical University of Den-
mark, offering a detailed analysis of its innovation readiness.
It explores critical
influencing factors, such as the team, culture, strategy, product-market fit, and tim-
ing [3] (Blagburn, 2016), to determine their significance.
This study investigates
whether early-stage startups can be analyzed using the Innovation Readiness Level
(IRL) framework, which factors can be improved for a short period, and how boards
can be effectively guided through board meetings. These inquiries are addressed by
applying various research methods, including interviews, practical graphs, observa-
tions, and a comprehensive literature review. The research is conducted for 5 months,
accompanied by meetings, board guidance, and strategic planning.
1.1
Background on District Heating Challenges
The District Heating system stands as a pivotal component of Denmark’s green and
eﬀicient energy infrastructure. Not only does DH ensure a stable and reliable heating
supply for Denmark, but it also significantly aids in sustaining an environmentally
friendly energy sector and achieving the nation’s long-term energy policy objectives,
according to the [4] Danish Energy Agency.
The genesis of a collective DH sys-
tem during the 1920s and 1930s, leveraging waste heat from local electricity produc-
tion, marked a significant advancement. Subsequently, DH from Combined Heat and
Power systems saw expansion across major Danish cities.
By the 1970s, approxi-

resulting in heating disruptions lasting up to five days. Furthermore, this approach
undermines the effectiveness of annual replacement plans. The challenge is exacer-

the expansion and extension of the DH pipeline network. The growing reliance on
DH highlights the urgent need for innovative solutions that provide comprehensive
insights into pipeline conditions. This challenge prompted Fors A/S, a DH company,
to approach the Technical University of Denmark for an urgent solution. In response,
a team of engineers established the startup CETO Innovation to address this issue,
showcasing the continuous evolution and innovation within Denmark’s DH sector.

1.3
Importance of effective board meetings in
early-stage startups
In the context of early-stage startups, the significance of conducting effective board
meetings cannot be overstated. As highlighted in [10] ”Startup Boards: Getting the
Most Out of Your Board of Directors” by Brad Feld and Mahendra Ramsinghani,
these meetings serve as critical junctures where strategic decisions are made, opera-
tional issues are addressed, and the overall direction of the company is shaped. The
effectiveness of these meetings directly influences the startup’s ability to navigate
the challenges of its formative stages and to lay a solid foundation for future growth.
Effective board meetings facilitate clear communication between board members and
the management team, ensuring everyone is aligned with the company’s objectives
and aware of its operational realities. This alignment is crucial for early-stage compa-
nies, where strategic pivots and quick decision-making are often necessary to respond
to rapidly changing market conditions. Feld and Ramsinghani emphasize that these
meetings should not only focus on current issues but also anticipate future challenges
and opportunities, allowing the board to guide the company proactively rather than
reactively. Moreover, these gatherings are essential for maintaining the governance
of the company. They provide a structured opportunity to scrutinize financial perfor-
mance, evaluate management effectiveness, and ensure compliance with legal and eth-
ical standards. This oversight is vital to building credibility and trust with investors,
partners, and customers, which are critical for a startup’s survival and growth. Ad-
ditionally, effective board meetings encourage the involvement of board members in
a way that leverages their expertise, networks, and experience. This is particularly

guidance is high. Board members can offer insights into market dynamics, advice on
strategic partnerships, and mentorship to the company’s leaders, contributing to a
well-rounded strategic approach. In the scholarly article [11] ”Startup Governance”
by Elizabeth Pollman, a detailed framework is presented that elucidates the distinct

Conversely, startups lacking boards often encounter diﬀiculties in scaling due to the
absence of structured guidance and external accountability. Such startups are prone
to strategic misalignments and problematic investor relationships, which can jeop-
ardize their long-term sustainability. This comprehensive examination underscores
the critical influence of board governance in shaping the trajectory and viability of
startups.
1.4
The IRL framework and its potential benefits
The Innovation Readiness Level framework was developed by [12] KTH Royal In-
stitute of Technology as an adaptation and expansion of the Technology Readiness
Level (TRL) model originally introduced by NASA. While the TRL framework pri-
marily focuses on assessing the maturity of technological developments through spe-
cific stages, the IRL framework extends this concept to evaluate broader aspects of
innovation, specifically business and user readiness. The IRL framework builds on
the structured approach of TRL, which measures the progression of technology from
concept to deployment. This method has been pivotal in standardizing the evalu-
ation of technology within various industries. Recognizing the limitations of TRL
in addressing the comprehensive needs of startups, particularly those operating in
rapidly evolving markets, the IRL framework incorporates additional dimensions. It
assesses not only the technological aspects but also the readiness of business models
and user engagement strategies. This focus is crucial for startups, as they often need
to rapidly adapt and scale their innovations in response to real-time market feedback
and emerging opportunities. By integrating business and user readiness assessments,
the IRL framework offers a multidimensional evaluation of an innovation’s potential
for success. This holistic approach ensures that innovations are aligned with mar-
ket demands and user expectations, thereby enhancing their potential for successful
implementation.

1.5
Research problem and significance
Recognizing the intrinsic value of a startup beyond its ideation phase is a pivotal
concern in the entrepreneurial world. In 2023, the global startup economy, valued
at a staggering $7.6 trillion, underscored the tremendous potential for value creation
within diverse ecosystems globally.
This flourishing environment accentuates the
appeal of startups, showcasing their significant contributions to economic and social
frameworks. Yet, amidst this expanding popularity lies a critical question: How can
we determine if a startup moves beyond just being an idea to having real value? [13]
(Tóthová & Fiľa, 2023)
The allure of embarking on a startup venture is undeniable.
The prospect of
bringing an innovative idea to realization can be both exhilarating and, at times, over-
whelming, leading many to leap into the entrepreneurial fray with little consideration
of the complexities that lie ahead. It is this juncture of excitement and uncertainty
that necessitates a deeper exploration of a startup’s foundational elements. The cre-
ation of a tool by KTH, outlined in this project, serves as a guiding light through
these complex challenges. This innovation readiness tool offers a thorough framework
to assess the vital elements necessary for the growth and longevity of a startup.
By applying this tool, entrepreneurs and their teams gain invaluable insights into
their venture’s core strengths and weaknesses, enabling them to make informed deci-
sions about the feasibility and potential success of their business idea. It propels a
shift from mere curiosity to a structured analysis, guiding teams to scrutinize their
concept’s innovativeness, market viability, and overall readiness for the challenges
of the startup ecosystem. Furthermore, this tool also offers a transparent lens for
investors and stakeholders, presenting an objective measure of a startup’s worth and
its developmental stage.

1.6
Scope and Limitations
This study aims to investigate the development process of early-stage startups with
a particular focus on the influence of a board of advisors. The research will utilize
the Innovation Readiness Level tool to track the progress of startups over a period of
13 weeks. The study will identify which axes of development are affected by specific
factors and provide insights into the dynamic role of advisory boards in early-stage
startups. The research employs a mixed-methods approach, combining quantitative
data from IRL assessments and qualitative insights from interviews and observations.
Due to the observational nature of this study, the framework developed may not
be universally applicable to all startups but can serve as a potential basis for fur-
ther investigation and the creation of a more generalized framework. The reliance on
self-reported data in surveys may introduce response bias, potentially affecting the
accuracy of the findings. Additionally, this study was conducted within the academic
environment of the Technical University of Denmark, which may limit the generaliz-
ability of the results to other contexts or geographical locations. The specific focus
on startups in Denmark may also affect the applicability of the findings to startups
in different cultural or economic settings.

2.1.2
Technology Readiness Level
At the time of observation, CETO Innovation had thoroughly explored the technolog-
ical opportunities and challenges. Given the complexity of the technology, extensive
studies were conducted. Initially, the Double Diamond design thinking model was
employed to identify the problem and examine all possible solutions. The Double Di-
amond Model is divided into four stages: Discover, Define, Develop, and Deliver. The
Discover phase involves exploring problems, gathering insights, understanding user
needs, and generating ideas through divergent thinking. The Define phase focuses
on refining the problem and establishing a framework to address it, using convergent
thinking. In the Develop phase, solutions are created and explored, again employing
divergent thinking. Finally, the Deliver phase involves testing and evaluating solu-
tions, as well as improving or discarding components that do not work, following a
convergent thinking approach. [16] ( Wang et al.,2023) Additionally, comprehensive
market research was undertaken to determine which technologies were currently being
used to address the problem, which were available in similar industries, and which
were not suitable, thus eliminating those options.
As a result, the initial research phase reached the first level of achievement: iden-

3.2
Structure of advisory boards in startups
The rationale behind forming an advisory board for a start-up is clear and compelling.
Such a board provides access to a level of expertise and credibility that typically
surpasses that of a board of directors. Often, distinguished individuals may agree
to serve on an advisory board due to the absence of the legal responsibilities that
accompany a directorship. This allows founders and investors to establish a small
board of directors for legal and governance purposes while leveraging the knowledge
and connections of a broader advisory board and their networks.
Advisory boards, unburdened by the compliance and operational issues of a board
of directors, can offer the CEO and management team valuable insights, expert knowl-
edge, contacts, and enhanced credibility. Members of an advisory board act as key
nodes in a Granovetter-style weak ties network, thereby linking the emerging venture
to a diverse array of beneficial connections. Additionally, some advisory board mem-
bers might also be early seed investors. [24] (Morkel & Posner, 2002) Advisory Boards
usually consist of a group of individuals selected by entrepreneurs, the group provides
advice and helps the company succeed. They are separate from a formal Board of
Directors. Entrepreneurs can select advisors based on needed skills or voids to fill
within their company. [25] (Hoey, 2017) However, this was not the case for the CETO
Innovation. The current scenario was designed to provide learning opportunities for
both startups and advisory boards.
The venture established by DTU aims to uncover new opportunities for young
engineering students, as well as for experienced industry professionals who have gained
significant knowledge in the field and are now ready to share their expertise. Meetings
were scheduled bi-weekly, providing startups with the chance to explore and discuss
pertinent subjects.
In a real-world context, such meetings would typically occur
quarterly or as agreed upon, making it crucial to set clear objectives and agendas to
maximize the limited time available.
Understanding how to structure these meetings was essential to ensure they fos-
tered development rather than merely discussing general concepts. It was important
for the startup and advisory boards to challenge each other constructively while ad-
hering to all legal requirements. The idea of using IRL as a form of structuring the
Agendas for the Board Meetings was created.

Methodology
This chapter describes the methods employed during this study, offering a compre-
hensive overview of the nature of the master’s thesis—exploratory and qualitative
research. Exploratory research utilizes both quantitative and qualitative methods,
with a predominant emphasis on qualitative approaches. Quantitative methods en-
compass pilot studies, expert surveys, and secondary research, such as literature
reviews, case studies, and online sources. Qualitative methods include in-depth inter-
views, focus groups, and observational studies, enabling deeper insights into complex
phenomena and participant perspectives. The primary focus of this project is not
to provide definitive answers to research questions, but rather to observe and under-
stand progress and influential factors in the process, potentially paving the way for
future explorations and theories.

was conducted, and diary reports. It is particularly useful for understanding historical

Conclusion
The purpose of this master’s thesis is twofold as it was already mentioned in the
beginning of the project. First, to develop an early-stage startup (CETO Innovation)
providing enough validation to warrant company registration and gain funding to
support employment for its founders.
Second, to investigate the use of the IRL
framework to support the startup process through application in CETO Innovation.
The process of simultaneously developing the company and observing it from an
analytical perspective provided a unique opportunity to reflect on actions taken and
track progress in a structured manner.
Over the course of the 13-week program,
significant improvements were made in the company’s performance, allowing for a
clear identification of its strengths and weaknesses.
The primary goal of this thesis was to determine the viability of CETO Innovation.
The results indicate that the company has gained suﬀicient validation to continue its
development. Consequently, CETO Innovation is well-positioned to be registered in
the Danish market, paving the way for further growth and success.
As per the second matter, this project was aiming to understand and perhaps
answer the following questions:
1. Is the IRL framework a valuable tool for startup governance and strategy making
when used to facilitate board interaction between the Venture Board and Founding
team?
2. Are the axes within the IRL framework fully independent of each other in influ-
encing the innovation process in startups?
3. Are certain axes more critical/important for the success of early-stage startups?
To address RQ1, it was crucial to explore how the IRL framework could be ef-
fectively utilized to add value and assess its applicability for early-stage startups.
Initially, the framework was modified to streamline its structure and enhance com-
prehension. The next step involved determining how to implement it in Board Meet-
ings. Despite multiple attempts to present and discuss the tool with the Board, time
constraints due to packed meeting agendas prevented this from being successful.
An alternative approach was to use the tool as a parallel guideline for creating
meeting agendas. This method yielded positive results, as it clarified what topics to
include based on the product’s and team’s positioning on the readiness level scale.
Additionally, the documentation of agendas and meeting minutes after each meeting
facilitated great support to learn which factors were the most influential and why.

Conclusion
Having an Advisory Board with an extensive network willing to share its resources
was crucial. Board members also needed to demonstrate a commitment to the team
by actively contributing their time to the process. While experience and skills are
valuable, they become insignificant if not applied in collaboration with the founding
team. Therefore, it was essential to recognize that the Advisory Board for early-stage
startups acts primarily as mentors.
Therefore, the IRL framework proved to be valuable in this context. However, its
general applicability for other startups remains uncertain and would require further
validation.
To address RQ2 and RQ3 objectively, a more quantitative approach would be
necessary. However, due to the observative and qualitative nature of this project, the
answers are derived from a literature review and subjective analysis.
For example, the study in Administrative Sciences identifies the most critical
factors for startup success, highlighting the importance of the idea, CEO’s leadership,
business model, marketing approach, and the entrepreneurial team. The research
shows that while all these elements are vital, the idea and leadership play the most
significant roles, with cultural differences influencing the emphasis on each factor
across different regions. [34] (Sevilla-Bernardo et al., 2022), where the idea stands in
the first place of importance. However, a study of Hernande [35]Hernandez, on team
collaboration capabilities as a factor in startup success published in SciELO highlights
the importance of team collaboration capabilities, particularly in technology-based
startups. It emphasizes that effective teamwork and internal collaboration are crucial
for driving innovation and achieving success. [36] (Choi et al., 2021)
Through this timely observation of the team dynamic and their progress, it be-
comes evident that having a dedicated, hard-working team—or at least one passionate
member—is crucial for delivering innovation in a novel environment. This commit-
ment does not necessarily pertain to a specific idea or solution but rather to the
concept of innovation itself. It involves embracing uncertainty, taking risks, and hav-
ing the courage to fail. From the beginning, such a team or individual should set the
expectation that regardless of the end goal, the entire process is a lifetime learning
experience.
While technology plays a highly critical role according to various studies, filling
market gaps and bringing value to customers, its success largely depends on the
execution by a strong team. Without the right team to drive the technology, it may
remain unnoticed or unavailable to the market.
Moreover, the interdependence between the various axes of the readiness scale,
such as the Intellectual Readiness Level and technology, is noteworthy. Intellectual
Readiness is heavily dependent on the availability of technology since patents and
innovations cannot be created without technological foundations. This illustrates the
loop of interdependence: having advanced technology necessitates a skilled team, and
vice versa.
A sustainable business model, while necessary, is not critical. It is essential to
assess if the business can potentially be viable and sustainable, and whether the
temporary organization can create jobs in the market. However, the business model

is expected to evolve with the pace and movement of the startup and market demand.
Thus, adaptability and agility are core characteristics of a successful startup.
The funding axis creates a conducive working environment. For a startup with
a strong team and a good idea, funding provides the opportunity to expand more
rapidly. This is particularly important for deep tech companies focused on hardware
development, where substantial investment is required to bring innovative products
to market.
According to [37] Bill Gross and his research on why startups succeed, the most
crucial factor for startup success is timing, which is closely related to customer readi-
ness level. If there is a demand from the customer side and the market is actively
seeking new solutions or approaches, this creates an optimal environment for startups.
Regardless of how much funding a team has or how well-structured the business plan
is, without market traction, success is unattainable. Timing and customer readiness
are thus pivotal to the startup’s success.
In conclusion, this master’s thesis successfully validated the early-stage startup,
CETO Innovation, and demonstrated its readiness for registration and growth in
the Danish market. Additionally, the use of the IRL framework proved beneficial in
guiding the startup process i collaboration with Advisory Board, though its broader
applicability requires further study.
Key insights emphasize the importance of a
dedicated team, timely market entry, and the critical interplay between technology,
intellectual readiness, and effective execution.
