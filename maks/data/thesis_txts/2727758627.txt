Abstract
The deployment of the next generation of cellular mobile networks, 5G, is well underway,
bringing higher bitrates, lower latency and better coverage than the previous network
generations. However, along with all the technological improvements that 5G offers, a
significant increase in energy consumption of cellular networks is expected. This is due
to the increase in connected devices, data traffic and the required expansion of the cellu­
lar network infrastructure to meet the high data demands of modern applications, services
and users. The interest and need for energy efficient ways to run cellular networks have
therefore sparked an interest across the industry, as the cost of operating and environ­
mental impact of these networks are becoming worrying.
This report provides a comprehensive literature study on energy efficiency in cellular and
IoT networks. A thorough and detailed analysis of the energy consumption in these net­
works will be performed as well as providing useful metrics and measuring methods for
examining energy consumption in cellular networks. An overview of distribution of energy
consumption of network components is given and a discussion on patterns and challenges
regarding energy efficiency in cellular networks is provided.
Additionally, a complete rundown of beneficial strategies for energy efficiency is presented
alongside an overview of why and where to implement and deploy them in different gen­
erations of cellular and IoT networks. An evaluation and comparison of these energy ef­
ficiency strategies is performed and relevant case studies from the industry is presented,
showcasing the use of strategies.
Finally a discussion on the future of energy efficiency in cellular networks presents tech­
nologies and techniques under deployed throughout the industry.

Introduction
Cellular networks was introduced a few decades ago and have since then evolved rapidly
by building upon and enhancing the fundamental technologies seen in the first cellular
networks. It has changed the way we access information and communicate with the world
around us with the focus always being to provide greater coverage and capacity to the
users, as well as enabling the Internet of Things (IoT) development which has progressed
at an incredible rate over the last decade.
This development has however also significantly increased the power consumption of
these networks which leads to other challenges such as operational costs and environ­
mental impact. The Information and Communication Technology (ICT) sector is currently
responsible for approximately 2% of global carbon emission with mobile operators being
responsible for 0.2% which is only increasing [1]. Those numbers are expected to in­
crease significantly as it is estimated that by 2030, ICT will be responsible for 13% of the
global energy consumption and 3­5% of carbon emission [2].
The area of energy efficiency in cellular mobile networks has therefore gained more at­
tention from the network providers/operators, as they seek to minimize costs as wells as
limit the effects on the climate by switching to more sustainable methods.
Energy efficiency in networks refer to the ability of a network to live up to the demand of
high data rate while maintaining Quality of Service (QoS) requirements from regulators
and Quality of Experience (QoE) from customers while minimizing energy consumption.
This can be achieved by optimizing certain components or devices within a network in­
frastructure or manage energy usage more effectively.
The evolution from 2G to 5G have increased both the coverage and capacity of services,
but are now also seeing improvements when it comes to energy efficiency. Earlier, when
2G were the current standard, voice services were the focus and had no energy efficiency
measures embedded because of the simpler network infrastructure and lower data rates
[3]. This began to change when 3G was deployed as the increase in demand for services
requiring high data speeds resulted in more complicated and energy­intensive network
architectures.
With the launch of 4G, coverage, capacity and data speeds increased significantly which
enabled video streaming, mobile applications and other data­intensive services like never
before. To provide these services to the users, the network operators/providers had to
expand their network infrastructure to keep up with the increasing data demands, again
leading to more energy consumption.
This was also the case with the arrival of 5G as it provides ultra­low latency, incredibly
high data rates and exceptional connectivity which is possible by further advancing the
network infrastructure, resulting in even higher energy consumption. Numerous innova­
tive solutions are however in development and some are even installed already, showing
promising results when it comes to energy reductions in various cellular mobile networks.
The significant growth within the field of cellular networks is illustrated in Figure 1.1 [4],
which shows the exponential increase in mobile network data traffic currently and what
the future amount of data traffic is forecasted to be. The figure also shows that 5G is
expected to be responsible for most of the networks data traffic, further emphasising the
importance of energy efficiency in 5G networks.

2
Evolution of Cellular Networks
With the rising demand of communication services in recent years, the digitization of ev­
erything and the exponential growth of mobile communication, cellular networks have
gone through significant changes and development since its introduction a few decades
ago.
This chapter will take a closer look at the evolution of cellular networks throughout the
years and the different generations ranging from the second­generation (2G) Global Sys­
tems for Mobile Communications (GSM), the third­generation (3G) Universal Mobile Telecom­
munication System (UMTS), the fourth­generation (4G) Long Term Evolution (LTE) and
the latest being the fifth­generation (5G) New Radio (NR) [6].
The general network architecture has remained the same throughout the different gen­
erations of cellular mobile networks and consists of the same three main components/­
sections: the Radio Access Network (RAN), the Mobile Backhaul Network (MBH) and the
Core Network as shown in Figure 2.1 [7].
Even though the overall architecture has stayed the same, several changes and improve­
ments have been implemented whenever a new generation of mobile networks has been
introduced. Those improvements have been necessary to make sure that the cellular
networks can keep up with the growing demand of advanced data­intensive services and
mobile communication.
Seeing and understanding these differences and technological advancements, highlights
the limitations and possibilities of the different cellular network generations, providing a
better understanding of why some strategies for energy efficiency are more beneficial and
better utilized in certain generations.

2.1
Cellular Networks Overview
This sections serves as a brief overview of the general cellular network architecture where
the RAN, the MBH and the core network will be described and their individual responsi­
bilities and components will be presented.
2.1.1
Radio Access Network
The RAN, also known as the access network, is one of the three major components in the
cellular network and is responsible for connecting mobile devices, such as cellphones or
computers, to the rest of the network and the Internet [8]. Some add a fourth section to the
cellular network, being the User Equipment (UE) consisting of the users mobile devices,
but in this report, the UE is seen as a part of the RAN.
In the access network, areas of coverage are divided into cells which serves as base
station, also known as a cell tower, as illustrated in Figure 2.2 [9] with the different colored
circles and the black box being the processing components. Each base station is typically
responsible for three cells.
Figure 2.2: Base stations and cells infrastructure
Over the years, the RAN has improved and are now able to provide voice calls, text mes­
saging, and video/audio streaming to the increasing number of users which can include
many different devices ranging from mobile phones, IoT equipment or drones.
The RAN consists of base stations that uses antennas to provide coverage to all the
surrounding cells. The Antennas are positioned at the top of the base station and is
responsible for converting electric signals into radio waves [8]. A diagram of a typical
base station is shown in Figure 2.3 [8].
Other components of the base station are the radios and Baseband Units (BBUs). The
radios are responsible for ensuring that the correct frequency bands and power levels
are used for the transmissions as well as transforming digital information into wireless
signals. The BBUs are devices used to process baseband signals and are connected to
one or more Remote Radio Unites (RRUs) positioned near the antenna and works as a
control­center for the base station where uplink and downlink data traffic is processed to
make wireless communication possible [8].
For each new generation of cellular mobile networks, the RAN evolves by increasing data
rates, capacity and to support new technologies.

2.1.2
Mobile Backhaul Network
The mobile backhaul network refers to the transport network and ensures the connectivity
between the RAN and the core network. This enables the transport of user data and
signaling traffic across the network by linking the cell towers to wired networks elements
[10].
Central components and characteristics of the mobile backhaul network are:
• For data traffic transportation, the mobile backhaul network uses the different tech­
nologies copper, fiber and wireless for data transmission between its own compo­
nents as well as to and from the RAN and core network.
• The mobile backhaul network has devices such as routers and switches deployed
throughout its network, which are responsible for transmitting the data traffic be­
tween the base station and the core network,
• Scalability and capacity improvements is incredibly important for the mobile back­
haul network, as it must expand to support the higher bandwidth demands of modern
applications and services. As the amount of data traffic increases and the number
of connected devices continue to grow, the mobile backhaul network has to be de­
signed to manage it, as it must never be the bottleneck [9].
• A challenge faced by the mobile backhaul network is delivering acceptable QoS
to the users. QoS is ensured by categorizing the data traffic into types and then
allocating resources based on the importance of that data. This provides efficient
and reliable data transmission as the connectivity is consistent and the latency is
low.
• Another challenge in the mobile backhaul network is synchronisation which is cru­
cial, since having synchronization established enables certain network operations.
An example of such is smooth handovers for mobile users, as timing accuracy is
required for reliable communication for users moving in­between cells.
The mobile backhaul network therefore serves as a link between the RAN and the core
network and plays a crucial role in supporting the increasing demands of cellular networks
[10]. It provides reliable connectivity and effective data traffic transmission for mobile

2.1.3
Core Network
The mobile core network is essential for most activities performed on mobile devices, as
it is responsible for the connection between external networks such as the Internet, and
the mobile devices which allows the users to access various services.
It performs switching and routing functions for all services such as text messages, voice
calls and mobile data, as well as managing packet switching in 2G, 3G, 4G and 5G net­
works. It also manages both circuit and packet switching in 2G and 3G networks [11].
Key aspects and functionalities of the core network include [11]:
• A crucial task of the core network is the connectivity it brings between the inter­
nal network and other external networks. This enables users and other devices to
communicate and access services from outside their current network. Examples
of external networks are the Internet, Public Switched Telephone Networks (PSTN)
and other mobile networks.
• As all communication between the mobile devices and the external networks goes
through the core network, one of the main responsibilities of the core network is the
routing and switching of data traffic. It manages voice calls and data packets and
makes sure that these are directed to the correct destinations and does so based
on factors such as IP addresses or phone numbers.
• Location updates of the users is also a responsibility of the core network. By tracking
the movement and locations of devices within the network at all times, functions
such as handovers between base stations or different networks are possible, without
interruptions of any on­going communication occurs.
• Service authentication and security mechanisms are handled by the core network
as well. This is needed to make sure that only authorized devices and users have
access to the services provided by the network and the otherwise encrypted data.
• Network signaling is crucial for the operation of the core network as it enables net­
work functions such as call setup and call teardown. The core network manages
network signaling protocols to enable communication between nodes in the network
such as base stations and servers [12].
The core network is designed to provide access to services while handling the large
amounts of growing data traffic and the increasing number of users and devices.
To keep up with the increasing demands for faster data speeds, better connectivity set
by the users and the modern more advanced applications and services, the architecture
and functionalities of the core network has been upgraded and improved for each new
generation of cellular networks.
The RAN, the mobile backhaul network and the core network are managed and controlled
by network operators and administrators. They monitor the flow of data traffic and the
performance of the networks to make necessary configurations if needed. Management
and configurations are done using network management systems which enables control
and visibility of the entire network.

2.2
2G GSM
GSM was introduced in 1991 as the second generation of wireless communication sys­
tems and is despite being multiple decades outdated still used for many voice calls and
certain low data services [13].
Technology has advanced significantly since then, but GSM communication is still widely
used across the planet for text messages and remote sensors in certain equipment.
2.2.1
Overview
GSM was designed to advance the cellular network and provide better and more secure
performance than the previous generation [13].
One of the main goals of GSM was to create a system that had potential for achiev­
ing greater capacity than what was possible before and managed this by utilizing Time­
Division Multiple Access (TDMA), which is a channel access method that shares the same
frequency channel between multiple users by dividing the signal into different time slots.
By utilizing this technique, more users could be served by the available bandwidth. Addi­
tionally, to increase privacy, encryption of the digitally encoded voice calls was introduced
to prevent eavesdropping, which had been possible in earlier analogue cellular technolo­
gies.
The primary function of GSM was definitely voice calls, which was achieved by digitally en­
coding and later decoding the speech using a device called a vocoder. Different vocoders
were used for different scenarios.
Additionally, with data rates up to 9.6 kbps, other data services were supported as well
such as the one­way broadcast service VideoText and TeleTex, which was used to send
text to television screens [13].
Another service that has grown extremely popular and was introduced with GSM is the
Short Message Service (SMS). It was developed as part of the GSM specification and has
later also been incorporated into the newer generations of cellular networks. It allowed
bi­directional messaging, store and forward delivery as well as messages consisting of al­
phabetic letters and numbers, with a fixed length however. It quickly became an incredibly
popular service and still is to this day.
As the development of the GSM communication technology started, certain goals had
been set for the design and capabilities of the technology [13]:
• It should offer good voice call quality
• The phone or terminal costs should be low
• Handheld terminals should be possible
• International roaming should be supported by the system
• The spectral efficiency should be acceptable
• The system should offer Integrated Services Digital Network (ISDN) compatibility
When GSM deployed it was able to meet all those requirements, and with international
roaming possible as well as network devices from different manufacturers being able to
operate alongside each other, the technology was seen as a success.
Additionally, as GSM achieved 200 kHz Radio Frequency (RF) channels with TDMA and
Frequency­Division Multiple Access (FDMA) systems implemented, it was now possible
for up to eight users to access each carrier [13].

of the GSM technology.

GSM had initially been intended for deployment within Europe, but because it was seen

but are also utilized in 3G networks. It uses packet data rather than circuit switched

BSS
The BSS section is the RAN of the 2G GSM network architecture and is responsible for
communication with the mobile devices within its coverage area and connecting the users
to the overall network and consists of two components [14]:
• The Base Transceiver Station (BTS) is responsible for communicating with the
users and their mobile devices within the cells it provides coverage for. The BTS
consists of radio transmitter receivers and antennas which enables transmission and
reception of data to communicate with the devices. This communication is made
possible by the use of what is called the Um interface.
• The Base Station Controller (BSC) is the next element and controls a group of
BTSs and is often located closer to one of them than the others. The BSC controls
the radio resources and manages the handovers of users from one BTS to another
Sustainable Communication Networks
10
==PAGE:24==
while also allocating channels. The BSC communicates with the BTSs over an Abis
interface.
The BSS of the 2G GSM network used radio access technologies to provide services to
multiple users simultaneously. Each base station had several channels and with each
channel being able to support up to eight users at a time, a base station was able to serve
a large number of users at the same time [14].
To fully utilize the potential of those base stations, the network operators had to position
them thoughtfully throughout the coverage area to enable complete coverage for all users
within the area.
As overlapping cells covered by different base stations were unpreventable, the channels
used in one cell are not used in the next to limit interference as it would negatively impact
the call quality.
To make sure that the communication and connection between the different components
in the BBS were suited for reliable communication, a variety of technologies were used
to link the BTS to the BSC and the BSC to the core network, being the NSS [14]. With
the relatively low data rates used within the GSM network, technologies such as E1 or T1
lines were often utilized, especially for the link between the BSS and the core network.
To reduce the need for installation of specific lines, the remote BTSs were often linked
using small microwave links. This proved to be beneficial as the base stations often were
stationed in areas where using specific lines were challenging. Their main goal was to
provide great coverage, which usually required unusual placements, making microwave
link options seem like an excellent method for providing a data link for the network.
NSS
The core network of the 2G GSM network architecture is the NSS and consists of numer­
ous different components and is responsible for the main control and interfacing of the
whole mobile network.
All the components that make up the NSS is also illustrated in diagram from Figure 2.4
[9] and are described below [14]:
• The Mobile Service Switching Centre (MSC) is one of the main components of
the GSM network architecture and acts like a typical switching node within a PSTN
or ISDN, while also offering additional functionalities to support the demands of the
mobile users. Such functionalities include handovers, registration, authentication,
call location and call routing to a mobile user. MSC also provides an interface to
the PSTN which enables communication between mobile devices and phones con­
nected to a landline. Additional interfaces are provided as well which makes com­
munication between mobile devices on different networks possible.
• The Visitor Location Register (VLR) is usually integrated as part of the MSC but
are in some cases deployed as a separate component in the network. The VLR is
responsible for storing specific information from the HLR that enables the selected
services for each users to be provided.
• The Home Location Register (HLR) holds all the administrative information about
each user as well as their last known location which enables the GSM network to
route the calls to the proper base station. This is done by having the mobile de­
vice register with the network every time it is switched on. The network can then
determine which base station it is connected to and then route an incoming call to
that base station to make sure it reached the correct destination. To make sure that
the base station knows of the mobile devices current/latest location at all times, the
mobile devices periodically registers with the network while it is switched on and not
Sustainable Communication Networks
11
==PAGE:25==
active.
• The Equipment Identity Register (EIR) is responsible for deciding if a certain mo­
bile device is allowed access to the network and is done by looking at the Interna­
tional Mobile Equipment Identity (IMEI) of the specific device. This IMEI is prein­
stalled on every mobile device and is checked by the network when the device tries
to register. By looking at this identifier, the network can decide to either, allow the
device onto the network, deny access or monitor in case of problems.
• The Authentication Centre (AuC) is used for authentication and encryption of the
radio channel. It is a secure database storing the secret keys that are also stored
in the users SIM cards.
• The Gateway Mobile Switching Centre (GMSC) is in charge of securing the Mobile
Station Roaming Number (MSRN) from the HLR based on the Mobile Station ISDN
number (MSISDN) and then route the incoming calls to the correct MSC. The GMSC
is crucial for terminating calls as it is where that information is routed initially, as the
position of the user is not known yet.
• The SMS Gateway (SMS­G) is of course responsible for anything related to the
SMS service and is the term used for describing the two SMS gateways defined in
the GSM standards and handles messages transmitted in different directions. One
of those gateways are the Short Message Service Gateway Mobile Switching Centre
(SMS­GMSC), which is used for short messages sent to an user.
The other gateway is the Short Message Service Inter­Working Mobile Switching
Centre (SMS­IWMSC) used for messages coming from devices within the network.
All these components function and operate collectively to make up the core network of the
2G GSM network architecture. In some cases, the components were located somewhat
far from each other to make the network more resilient in case of failure, but they were
mostly positioned nearby [14].
Even though the GSM system was primarily a system designed for voice, all signal man­
aged within the core network were digital.
OSS
The OSS is responsible for controlling and monitoring the GSM network as well as control­
ling the traffic load on the BSS, which was possible as the OSS is connected to both the
components in the NSS and the base stations [14]. In some cases the maintenance tasks
were transferred to the base stations as the number of base stations usually followed the
increase in mobile users more­often than the OSS.
The 2G GSM network architecture illustrates basic functionalities and operations that were
needed for a mobile communication system to be able to provide voice and text­message
services as well as how the different components interact and depend on one another.

UMTS is the third generation of cellular networks and utilizes a completely different radio
interface built around the use of Direct Sequence Spread Spectrum (DSSS) as Code­
Division Multiple Access (CDMA) [15].
Even though the 3G UMTS network upgraded and changed the radio access standard,
the core network remained pretty much the same where circuit switched voice and packet
data were carried separately like in GPRS and EDGE.
UMTS came to use a wideband version of CDMA known as WCDMA that could occupy

2.3.1
Overview
By utilizing WCDMA as its radio transmission standard and the 5 MHz channel band­
width, it was now possible to support over 100 voice calls simultaneously or carry data at
a speed of up to 2 Mbps [15]. Later in the lifespan of UMTS with the deployment of High
Speed Packet Access (HSPA) it became possible to achieve data transmission speeds
up to 14.4 Mbps [15].
Several of the implementations seen as successful from GSM was carried over and im­
proved upon for UMTS, such as the Subscriber Identification Module (SIM) becoming
Universal SIM (USIM). Additionally, the network was designed so that both GPRS and
EDGE could be used for UMTS.
Furthermore, certain specifications were introduced in UMTS which allowed both Fre­
quency Division Duplex (FDD) and Time Division Duplex (TDD) modes. The first one of
those deployed was the FDD mode which had the uplink and downlink transmitting on
different frequencies with a spacing difference of 190 MHz [15].
The TDD mode, also sometimes referred to as Time Division CDMA (TD­CDMA), splits
the uplink and downlink in time with the base stations and then have the mobile devices
transmit in succession which can be beneficial for some applications. TD­CDMA also per­
formed well when utilized in cells of relatively small sizes. Since a guard time is needed
between the transmission and reception, having shorter distances being covered resulted
in smaller guard time as transit times were smaller.
Additionally, it was found that because of users surfing the internet and downloading
videos, far more data was carried in the downlink, which meant that it was more ben­
eficial to allocate more capacity to the downlink [15]. Being able to take advantage of
this imbalance in uplink and downlink traffic and allocating the resources improved the
efficiency of the network and made TDD systems highly efficient for carrying Internet data
in small cells.

PGW/SGW and other MME when a handover is performed and also manages Serv­
ing GPRS Support Node (SGSN) selection during LTE­to­2G/3G handovers occur.
Additionally, the MME is interfaced to the Home Subscriber Server (HSS) and the IP
Multimedia Subsystem (IMS). HSS is a database storing information related to users
and supscriptions while IMS is a standardized architectural framework for delivering
services such as voice, video and text messaging over IP networks [22].
• The SGW is responsible for packet routing, forwarding and buffering of downlink
packets and acts as a local mobility entity for handovers of devices within its network.
The SGW is interfaced directly to the base stations and manages downlink packet
marking whereas the base stations manage the uplink packet marking.
• The PGW manages all the operations related to IP packets such as packet inspec­
tion, UE IP address allocation and transport level packet marking for both uplink and
downlink.
The access to external networks is also handled by the PGW.
When 4G LTE first was introduced it provided significant improvements for the overall
performance of cellular networks and has since then only been improved even further. It
allows users the ability to interact with advanced applications and services while offering
high data rates and low latency.

Analysis of Energy Consumption
With the amount of energy consumed for maintenance and operation of cellular mobile
networks increasing each year, it is crucial to understand exactly what elements in the
network are consuming this power, and for what purpose.
This chapter provides a breakdown of how energy is consumed in cellular mobile net­

By better understanding how the energy is spent, methods for energy efficiency can be
deployed more effectively and offer better results.

Multiple varying factors play a significant role in evaluating performance and consumption
of cellular mobile networks. Several of these key metrics and measuring tools will be
presented and described below.
3.1.1
Throughput
Network throughput is the most common metric used in communication networks and
refers to the rate of data transmitted over a communication channel.
Throughput can be measured in several ways when used as a metric for network perfor­
mance, and an example is shown below in Equation 3.1 as throughput per unit energy,
which is established as a green metric [1]. The metric is then a ratio of effective through­

3.1.2
Coverage
Coverage is another important fundamental metric used to evaluate network performance
and is especially noticeable for the users, as poor coverage leads to e.g. slow download
speeds and interrupted video streaming.
The definition of coverage is the geographic area where the base station can commu­
nicate, and the mobile users ability to connect to a base station depends on the signal
strength in that specific location within the coverage area of the base station.
Coverage can be impacted in numerous ways as it can be boosted by higher transmission
power, better and taller or more antennas, or it can be weakened by surrounding environ­
ments such as buildings, mountains or interference from elsewhere [1].
The goal of the base stations is then to make sure that at least all users are constantly
receiving coverage and that coverage­related parameters such as antenna tilting or trans­
mission power is optimized if certain locations are experiencing low coverage.

The Carbon Footprint
The carbon footprint, also referenced to as carbon emission, is a metric used for measur­

company or country. When it comes to measuring the carbon footprint of cellular mo­
bile networks, there is no standardized approach, and it is therefore up to the individual
operator or company to define for their processes, configurations or products.
A methodology used by some companies is the Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) metric which
evaluates carbon emission of a product or task for the start of its production to the end of
its life [38]. The factors used to do this evaluation are raw material extraction, manufac­
turing, usage and disposal as well as use of resources, energy consumption and waste.
By utilizing LCA it is possible to get an overview and an estimate of the carbon emission
caused by a product or task.
In 2021, Ericsson proposed a method called Parameterized Embodied Emission Calcu­
lator (PEEC), which they mentioned as a streamlined approach to estimate carbon emis­
sions of telecommunication products, but particularly base stations [39].
PEEC is inspired by LCA and provides a fast approach for estimating the carbon footprint
of a product by utilizing a model, that translates the properties of that product into the
amount of carbon emission that it causes, based on factors such as materials and energy
consumption.

Energy Efficiency in Cellular Networks
As mobile data traffic continues to grow exponentially [4], it is essential that energy con­
sumption is optimized to reduce the costs of operation and limit the environmental impact
as much as possible.
This chapter provides an overview and detailed description of numerous energy efficiency
technologies and techniques for cellular mobile networks as well as an evaluation and
comparison of the different methods followed by examples of case studies from the in­
dustry.
By implementing energy efficiency strategies, network operators can save energy, reduce
costs and limit carbon emission.
4.1
Energy Efficiency Strategies
Strategies for energy efficiency combine different approaches such as network planning,
optimization and implementation of advanced technologies or techniques. The goal for
these strategies is to maintain or increase network performance while also minimizing
power consumption.
4.1.1
C­RAN
Cloud/Centralized Radio Access Network (C­RAN) is a centralized and cloud­based archi­
tecture that groups baseband processing functions of multiple base stations in the same
location. This is illustrated in Figure 4.1 [7]. This group of baseband processing is called
the Baseband Unit (BBU) pool and is placed further from the base stations compared to a
traditional Distributed RAN (D­RAN), where each base station has baseband processing
performed locally.
Each base station in C­RAN is equipped with a Remote Radio Unit (RU), also known
as Remote Radio Head (RRH). The RU/RRH is responsible for handling the Radio Fre­
quency (RF) functions, such as transmitting and receiving RF signals. The RU/RRH and
the base station are then connected to the BBU pool with high capacity optical fiber cables
[59].
Before reaching the BBU pool, the RU/RHHs goes through the fronthaul network. This
network provides high­speed and low­latency communication between the RU/RHHs and
the BBU pool and makes sure that data traffic and signals are exchanged effectively.
By centralizing the baseband processing, C­RAN prevents redundancy by not having pro­
cessing done at each and every base station site. By having the computing resources
centralized in the BBU pool, the resource utilization and optimization of hardware is in­
creased which results in energy efficiency and better power management [60].
Additionally, C­RAN can improve resource allocation and traffic management. Again be­
cause of the centralizing of the processing functions, C­RAN helps to enable dynamic
resource allocation that is based on real­time traffic, resulting in a reduction of energy
consumption in the network.
Furthermore, C­RAN more easily enables the implementation/deployment of other energy
efficiency techniques, one of them being Coordinated Multi­Point (CoMP) as it benefits
from the centralized processing of C­RAN’s architecture and can be implemented more
effectively [59].
The higher effectiveness of CoMP comes down to the centralized BBU pool, as it has ac­
cess to information from multiple base stations and enables better performance of CoMP
Sustainable Communication Networks

==PAGE:56==
Figure 4.1: D­RAN vs C­RAN architecture
functions such as coordinated beamforming and joint transmission.
Load balancing and traffic offloading can also expect an increase in effectiveness as the
centralized BBU pool can more easily and effectively distribute the data traffic among the
base stations. If a base station is experiencing large amounts of traffic, the BBU pool can
redirect some of that traffic to other nearby base stations with less load. This optimizes
resource usage and can reduce energy consumption even further.
Additionally, C­RAN enables the merging of baseband processing functions from mul­
tiple base stations into a reduced number of BBU pools. By doing so, the amount of
unnecessary hardware duplicates is lowered and the utilization of computing resources
is increased, leading to a reduction in power consumption.
The centralization of the BBU pools also enables the access to renewable energy sources,
as this was not as easily achieved at each individual base station. If the BBUs are placed
in specific locations, the access or possibility for renewable energy sources increases,
resulting in lower carbon emission.
C­RAN has the potential to deliver significant energy savings in cellular mobile networks
and have shown incredible results. The work in [44] compared C­RAN approaches to
traditional macro base stations and saw a reduction in power consumption of 68%.
The research done in [60] investigated and evaluated C­RAN under various network set­
tings and found significant improvements in network energy efficiency ranging from 47%­
53.8%.
4.1.2
CoMP
CoMP is a method used in cellular mobile networks to coordinate the functions and oper­
ations of multiple neighboring base stations to increase the performance of the network,
as shown in Figure 4.2 [61]. The goal of CoMP is to increase the spectral efficiency and
mitigate interferences as much as possible. This then improves the user experience,
especially if the users are positioned at the edge of a cell or just experiencing a lot of
interference [62].
To achieve this, nearby base stations work together by exchanging information between
Sustainable Communication Networks

==PAGE:57==
each other and then uses this data to make collective decision regarding resource allo­
cations and operations such as beamforming and interference management.
Joint transmission and coordinated beamforming are two techniques that are utilized by
CoMP to increase performance of the network [62]. Joint transmission is the technique
of making multiple base stations transmit signals to a specific user or group of users, at
the same time. This should result in an improved signal strength at the users location,
increasing the quality and reliability of the communication.
Coordinated beamforming, as the name implies, is the process of aligning the transmis­
sion beams from different base stations towards a specific user or group of users, result­
ing in better received signal quality while also lowering the interference from other base
stations [62].
As mentioned briefly in the subsection above, CoMP works very effectively if implemented
in networks using the C­RAN architecture, as the centralization of baseband processing
suits the operations of CoMP well.
CoMP can however also be implemented in traditional distributed networks and hybrid
networks.

CoMP is considered as a method for energy efficiency in cellular mobile networks because
it provides increased spectral efficiency as well as optimizing resource allocation of the
network [63]. By having the neighboring base stations work together and coordinate their
operations, CoMP can reduce energy consumption by limiting repetitions of transmission
and reception activities.
By optimizing transmissions and receptions, interference can be minimized which results
in a reduced need for retransmissions leading to higher energy efficiency and lower energy

Additionally, as CoMP involves efficient resource allocation and coordination between
base stations, CoMP can utilize network resources more effectively and avoid transmitting

4.1.3
D2D
Device­to­Device (D2D) communication is another technique for energy efficiency in cel­
lular mobile networks. D2D works by having the nearby mobile devices communicate
with each other, and by doing so bypass the base stations. D2D therefore allows mobile
devices to exchange data, messages or even services without having to route through
the network as illustrated in the diagram in Figure 4.3 [64].
D2D establishes direct peer­to­peer communication of devices in near proximity to each
other and operates in underlay mode and overlay mode.
• In underlay mode, the D2D devices communicate with each other while also com­
municating with the cellular network. The devices use a part of the cellular spectrum
to establish a direct connection with each other, while also keeping the connection
with the base station. Resource allocation and interference is still being managed
by the cellular network [64].
• In overlay mode, D2D communication operates on its own without being dependent
on the cellular network. It works by having the devices establish ad hoc networks
or clusters to connect to each other and communicate without relying on the cellular
network. The overlay mode is useful in situations where the cellular network is
down or unreliable, for example in very crowded locations or in areas with barely
any coverage.
To make D2D communication possible and enable it, the devices first needs to discover
each other and establish a direct connection. This can be done using multiple methods
such as Bluetooth, Wi­Fi Direct or even specific cellular network protocols that are de­
signed for D2D communication [64].
D2D communication is considered a strategy for energy efficiency in cellular mobile net­
works because of the direct communication between nearby devices, instead of transmit­
ting data through the rest of the network. Bypassing base stations and the core network
offers numerous benefits when it comes to energy efficiency.
The overlay mode is a great example of this, as D2D then operates independently by
having the devices communicate directly between each other, and by doing so, reduces
the energy consumption of base stations as the data traffic bypasses them.
The short ranges of the communication of D2D is also an advantage when it comes to
energy efficiency as the short distances results in reduced signal propagation require­

ments. The short transmission distances of D2D communication requires lower trans­
mission power than data transmissions that are done over long cellular links [65]. By
minimizing the transmission power, the power consumption is minimized as well.
Additionally, this peer­to­peer communication that is established between the devices can
in some cases reduce the need for other components of the network, such as base sta­

Communication that typically requires data transmission over long distances such as the

Femtocells
Femtocells are small­scale cellular base stations that are designed for indoor deployment
such as in office buildings or households to improve signal and connectivity in indoor
environments. They function as traditional base stations and provide the local area with
coverage up to a few meters as illustrated in Figure 4.4 [66].
For the end users, femtocells connect them to the Internet and the core network infras­
tructure of the service provider and then uses the operator’s network to register, so that it
can provide cellular services. When the femtocell has been registered it will broadcast a
cellular signal to its coverage area.
The femtocell sets up a connection by utilizing the user’s broadband internet connection,
which then allows the femtocell to route data traffic between its coverage area and the

operator’s network [67].
For some devices, it is even possible to utilize this set up to have smooth hand­overs
of ongoing voice calls or other services between the femtocell and the macro cell when
moving from one coverage area to another.
There are numerous advantages and benefits of femtocell, with the most potent one being
the significant improvement of coverage inside buildings and other indoor environments.
Various building materials are known for disrupting or directly causing weak indoor signal
quality, which then required the base stations to work even harder. This can be avoided
using femtocells, as the data traffic is routed through the user’s broadband internet con­
nection, resulting in higher data speeds and signal quality.
Another benefit of femtocells is the help, which it provides to the macro network as it
helps offload network traffic by routing it locally. This reduces congestions and improves
the overall performance of the network.

As the femtocells provide an intense cellular signal in a small indoor area, it minimizes
the amount of traffic that needs to be transmitted over long distances which requires a lot

energy consumption of data transmission.
By improving the indoor signal quality, femtocells reduce the amount of retransmissions

4.1.5
OFDMA
OFDMA is a newer multi­user version of the OFDM modulation scheme and is used to
efficiently allocate and share available frequency resources among multiple users at the
same time [68].
By dividing the available frequency band into multiple orthogonal subcarriers, it enables
each subcarrier to carry data for different users simultaneously. Since the subcarriers
are orthogonal and the frequency separation between the adjacent subcarriers are small,
they do not interfere or disrupt each other. An illustration of how OFDMA more efficiently
allocates and shares available frequency resources among the users is shown in Figure
4.5 [69].
The OFDMA system adapts in real time as the allocation of subcarriers to users is done
dynamically and is flexible. The requirements of the users, such as location and service
demands, changes constantly and the system will therefore allocate more subcarriers to
users that require higher data rates while also allocating fewer to users requiring lower
data rates.
Figure 4.5: OFDM vs OFDMA
Because of its ability to more efficiently allocate resources to the varying demands of the
users compared to its predecessor, OFDMA is recognized as an energy efficiency method
in cellular mobile networks as it offers several benefits on this front.
The spectral efficiency that OFDMA provides enables higher data transmission rates
which leads to an overall capacity increase of the network and by being able to serve
more users simultaneously, OFDMA reduces the need for additional base stations be­
cause of lack of spectrum [68].
Additionally, by being able to allocate resources dynamically and provide higher data rates
for more demanding users, OFDMA makes sure that each individual user receives the
necessary resources without wasting excess capacity in the process. Better optimizing of
resource allocation can improve the overall network efficiency and by doing so minimize
its energy consumption.

==PAGE:62==
Also, by being able to adjust the transmission power of individual or groups of subcarriers,
OFDMA is able to put certain subcarriers into sleep mode while the network experiences
periods of low activity and low data traffic. This reduces the overall energy consumption
of the network and also improves the energy efficiency.
OFDMA is an advanced technology and is seen as a fundamental in modern cellular mo­
bile networks and is used in 4G and 5G networks. The technology offers several benefits
where energy efficiency is one of them.
In [68], they sought to study the energy efficiency of OFDMA in 5G networks and found an
increase in overall network energy efficiency ranging from 27­58% and that having more
subcarriers in the network was beneficial.
4.1.6
Advanced Antenna Technologies
With base stations consuming most of the power in cellular mobile networks, implementing
energy efficiency methods that minimize their energy consumption and enables more effi­
cient resource usage is beneficial. Advanced antenna technologies such as beamforming
and MIMO does exactly this and can enhance energy efficiency in cellular networks.
Beamforming
Beamforming is a wireless communication system technique that works by focusing the
transmission signal towards a certain area or in a certain direction, to enhance the quality
of the transmission signal at that specific location. Figure 4.6 [70] shows an illustration of
beamforming. This is useful if the users are located in an area with interference or where
it’s difficult to provide acceptable coverage.
Beamforming works by calibrating the signal phases and amplitudes of individual antenna
elements to create this enhanced transmission signal, called the beam or beamwidth. The
direction of the beam will then experience increased signal quality as well as reduced
interference.
There are multiple beamforming techniques with analog beamforming, digital beamform­
ing and hybrid beamforming being the most commonly used [71].
• Analog beamforming works by sending the same signal from multiple antennas
using phase­shifters, meaning that the signal is set to different phases, which cre­
ates an antenna pattern that points to a specific location. The overall coverage is
improved as the signal phases of antenna signals are adjusted with the radio fre­
quency domain.
This type of beamforming should be simple to implement but offers limited adapt­
ability.
• Digital beamforming has different signals for each antenna in a digital baseband
with each antenna having digital receivers placed at the radiating elements. Dif­
ferent phases are then applied to different frequency bands, which allows digital
beamforming to be more flexible. The beams can then be guided in a specific di­
rection by a digital beamforming processor.
• Hybrid beamforming is a combination of the two beamforming techniques and uses
both analog beamforming alongside digital precoding to support multistream trans­
missions and form the transmitted patterns from an antenna array.
Hybrid beamforming can be used by 5G base stations.
Beamforming provides several benefits and can improve signal strength and quality as
well as extending the coverage range, which results in higher data rates, improved sys­
tem capacity and mitigating of interference by minimizing signal coming from unwanted
directions [70].

Beamforming is recognized as an energy efficiency method because of its ability to focus
the transmissions in a specific direction, which can increase energy efficiency in the net­

By focusing the signal towards the specific area/users, beamforming reduces the energy
that would otherwise be used to transmit signals in other directions, and improves re­

Also, by extending the coverage and range of the antenna without requiring high trans­

Beamforming is a beneficial technique for communication systems and is commonly uti­

DAS
Distributed Antenna Systems (DAS) is a network of multiple separated antenna nodes
that increases wireless network coverage and capacity in a given area as illustrated in
the diagram in Figure 4.8 [76]. The antennas are then managed by a central hub, which
they are all connected to, that handles the flow of traffic. The idea of DAS is to split the
transmission power between several nodes to provide coverage over the same area as
a single antenna normally would have, but with improved reliability and reduced power
consumption [76].
By dividing the coverage area into smaller zones to cover and dedicate resources to spe­
cific areas, DAS works well in areas that would otherwise be difficult for a single antenna
to provide coverage to. DAS is often deployed within large buildings, at stadiums or at
campuses, where the enhanced wireless coverage and capacity is beneficial and where
the multiple antenna setup works around the surrounding structures, such as walls or
buildings. This ensures better coverage, stronger signals and more reliability for users in
what would usually be hard­to­reach locations.
DAS supports multiple frequency bands and several wireless technologies and can man­
age services such as voice, data and video.
Figure 4.8: Single antenna systems vs DAS
DAS is considered an energy efficiency method because it reduces the need for high
power transmission over long distances, as the signal strength and quality is increased
in multiple areas with the antennas positioned closer to the user, which results in lower
energy consumption of the overall network [76].
Enabling localized coverage within buildings or stadiums also increases energy efficiency
as the antenna does not need to amplify the transmission signal to penetrate buildings
and walls to reach the area and users. This also minimizes the chance of packet loss and
therefore retransmissions, resulting in lower energy consumption.
Additionally, by improving network capacity and offloading traffic from the macrocellular
network, DAS reduces the amount of traffic that has to be handled by macrocellular in­
frastructure, which operates at higher power levels, therefore leading to better resource
utilization and energy savings.

Relay Assisted Networks
Relay­assisted networks is a wireless communication technology that supplements the
transmissions in a given area by deploying additional relay nodes throughout the coverage
area to assist the transmission of data from source to destination over long distances or
to help the transmissions reach users in difficult locations. This is visualized in Figure 4.9
[77].
The relay nodes are placed strategically in spots with weak coverage, throughout the
coverage area to make sure that the entire cell has strong reliable signal quality.
Relay­assisted networks operate in essentially three main phases, being the reception of
the signal from the source node, the processing of the signal meaning amplifying, decod­
ing and re­encoding and finally the transmission phase where the relay node transmits the
received signal to the destination node [78]. This process improves the signal strength
and the overall reliability of the network.
It is common for signals to decrease in strength over distance, which is caused by path
loss and fading, and that is why relay nodes are helpful, because they can reduce the
need for retransmissions since the signals that are received by the relay nodes are re­
freshed and transmitted repeatably.
This technology is also an excellent way of working around structural challenges, such as
buildings, walls and tunnels as the relay nodes can be placed at specific locations, making
the signal arrive from another angle while simultaneously also being stronger. This again
reduces the need for retransmissions and is an efficienct use of resources.
Relay­assisted networks offers high flexibility as it can be tailored to specific requirements
of a given network, with the number and placement of the relay nodes customizable based
on factors such as user density and signal patterns. This adaptability enables better re­
source allocation and optimization of energy consumption, and therefore offers energy
efficiency improvements.

Relay­assisted networks improves energy efficiency and lowers energy consumption in
wireless communication systems because of multiple reasons [77].
By assisting the source node with the long transmissions that usually requires very high

the relay nodes act as a middleman that carries the signals the rest of the way. Addition­

node and reduce the energy consumption of the overall network.

4.1.9
HetNets
Heterogeneous networks (HetNets) is a wireless communication system that uses multi­
ple types of base stations and access nodes in a given network to offer coverage to users
in an environment with a wide variety of coverage zones, such as outdoor locations, office
buildings and underground areas. The different types of base stations combined in Het­
Nets are macrocells, microcells, picocells and femtocells, as shown in Figure 4.10 [79].
This makes for a comprehensive infrastructure that can meet the diverse requirements of
users in different environments and traffic demands.
• Macrocells are the primary coverage layer which is provided by large base stations,
located at the center of the network that offer wide­area coverage. They serve as
the backbone of the network and are responsible for high­capacity traffic.
• Microcells are smaller versions of the base stations seen in macrocells and are
responsible for improving coverage and capacity in areas where the user density
is high. Microcells are usually deployed in locations where the traffic demands are
intense or in urban areas.
• Picocells are then even smaller base stations and are mostly used inside office
buildings, airports or other indoor locations to improve coverage in such areas, to
offload traffic from the macrocells as well as reducing the need for long high power
transmissions.
• Femtocells are as mentioned earlier really small base stations that are even smaller
than picocells and are deployed inside small buildings to improve indoor connectiv­
ity.
In HetNets, the operations of the different base stations have to be coordinated to ensure
that the network performance is optimized and that the users are handed over between
cells smoothly. This coordination is done by utilizing a Self­Organizing Network (SON),
which is an automation technology that is designed and used for mobile RANs to make
configuration, management, planning and optimization easier and faster [80].
As the traffic demand, services and user movements change over time, the network needs
to adapt in real­time to keep providing acceptable coverage. SON is therefore essential
as it reduces time and cost of deployment while self­optimization ensures maximum effi­
ciency in resource allocations, load balancing and interference management.
HetNets provide multiple benefits in cellular mobile networks and are also considered an
energy efficiency method because of several reasons.
The first reason is how HetNets reduces the need for long high­power transmissions from
the macrocell base station, as those transmissions are now handled by the smaller base
stations which are scattered around the coverage area to provide coverage to the areas
at the edge of the cell or just hard­to­reach areas [81]. As the amplifier in the base stations

to improve the overall energy efficiency.
Additionally, by offloaded traffic from the larger cells to the smaller ones, HetNets helps to
balance the network load and reduce congestion as well as optimize resource utilization,
as the traffic is redirected to and from the smaller cells instead of always going through
the macrocells.
Finally, by strategically placing the smaller base stations in locations, where they most
optimally can transmit data traffic to and from the users and provide strong and reliable
signal quality, HetNets reduces the chances of signal fading and packet loss, resulting in

HetNets as a strategy for energy efficiency have been well researched in recent years

4.1.11
Base Station Sleep Modes
The energy efficiency method of sleep mode/idle state is a low power mode for many
electronic devices and is utilized in base stations in cellular mobile networks to reduce
energy consumption and improve energy efficiency.
Since base stations can experience periods of low network traffic or low user activity, it
makes sense to lower the power consumption or deactivate base stations during certain
hours of the day. As shown and described in the previous section of this report, the traffic
handled by base stations can reach very low numbers, and that is why sleep modes are
so beneficial.
During these low traffic periods, certain base stations can be selected to turn off or just
operate at reduced power levels [43]. This is done by dynamically adjusting the transmit
power, turn off unused components completely or even shut down certain base station
radio frequency chains. Numerous factors are involved when deciding to activate sleep
modes, examples are traffic load, the time of the day or the number of active users within
the coverage area. Good examples for sleep mode activation is during the night or when
base stations are located in areas where it is only needed to provide coverage for a low
population.
If a base station is in sleep mode and not turned off completely, it can still operate to mon­
itor and handle incoming traffic. This is done by having the base station periodically wake
up or reactivate certain components to look for user requests or other kinds of signals
[48]. If nothing is detected, then the base station continues to stay in sleep mode to save
energy.
When traffic or user activity is registered, and the activity is increasing, the network man­
agement system will start the reactivation of the base stations to wake them up and exit
the sleep mode to return to their normal functionality. Figure 4.12 [40] shows a simplified
diagram of the reactivation of base stations based on the increasing registered incoming
traffic.
The reactivation trigger is usually predefined to a certain threshold and is carried out au­
tomatically.
The sleep mode technique is an excellent way of improving energy efficiency while also
reducing energy consumption of base stations, as well as providing other benefits.
By using and consuming as close to the needed energy as possible, sleep modes provide
significant increase in optimization of resource utilization while also increasing the lifetime
of the base station equipment. By reducing the operation of components and devices in
base stations, the stress on the power supply system as well as the components and de­
vices themselves are lowered, resulting in potentially reducing the cost of operation for
the network operators [40].

Sleep modes can also help to optimize resource allocation in the overall network by en­
abling resources such as processing, spectrum and power to be more efficiently allocated
to areas, where they are needed more because of higher traffic demands or more active
users.
Sleep modes have to be implemented carefully however, since it can in some cases lead
to increased latency, worse network coverage and a decrease in user experience. This
is because of the wake­up time it takes for the base stations to reactivate before they
are fully functional again. Sleep modes can be implemented with different states where
the wake­up time and energy efficiency are the two deciding factors [48]. Some states
provide a short wake­up time but with little energy efficiency while other states offer high
energy efficiency but with a long wake­up time.
Having proper network planning and traffic monitoring tools installed are therefore es­

service.

IoT for Energy Efficiency
With the amount of IoT devices increasing exponentially all around the world, deploying
strategies for energy efficiency is crucial to limit the energy consumption of the ICT sector.
By implementing advanced technologies and techniques for energy efficiency in cellular
IoT devices, it is possible to increase the lifespan of devices, reduce maintenance costs
and limit carbon emission, therefore creating a more viable IoT ecosystem.
This section presents a few strategies for energy efficiency in cellular IoT networks.
4.2.1
LPWAN
Low Power Wide Area Network (LPWAN) is a type of wireless communication technology
designed specifically for long­range communication at low bit rates between IoT devices
[88]. LPWAN networks is perfect for cellular IoT because of its ability to provide con­
nectivity over large areas while also requiring low power usage. The devices are able
to communicate over several kilometers and even provide coverage in challenging areas
such as indoors or urban areas.
Examples of LPWAN technologies used in cellular IoT networks are the already men­
tioned NB­IoT, which operates within the existing cellular network infrastructure and uses
licensed spectrum to provide reliable and secure connectivity as well as effective commu­
nication in underground or indoor areas [89]. Another technology is Long Range (LoRa)
which uses the unlicensed spectrum to provide low­power and wide­area coverage. This
is done by using the gateways of the LoRa networks to receive and forward signals from
devices deployed with LoRa.
Both technologies are ideal for IoT devices that need to transmit data over several kilo­
meters and from hard­to­reach locations. Some examples for usages of such devices are
for tracking, agriculture, monitoring and smart cities.
Operation with minimal power consumption in mind is the fundamental of LPWAN tech­
nologies and this is displayed in LPWAN networks ability to make IoT devices, connected
to the network, enter sleep modes when they are not transmitting or receiving data [89].
As explained in earlier sections, sleep modes are an excellent way to reduce overall en­
ergy consumption and energy efficiency in the network. For IoT devices in particular,
this enables them to save energy and therefore extending battery life, enabling long­term
functionality of the devices while minimizing the need for battery replacements.
Additionally, the long­range coverage provided by LPWAN technologies also reduces the
need for constant handovers between the base stations, which can lead to energy sav­
ings. An excellent example of the usage of the LWPAN technology LoRa is the work in
[90], where they found a reduction in power of public lightning in smart cities by 35%.
4.2.2
Adaptive Transmission Power Control
Adaptive Transmission Power Control (ATPC) is a technique for dynamically changing the
transmission power of IoT devices in cellular IoT networks, depending on the distance
between the IoT devices and the base stations or the strength/quality of the received

the communication reliable.
By continuously monitoring the strength and quality of the signals, the IoT devices can
adapt to the changes in the network and provide either support to the current signal or
reduce transmission power to lower energy consumption [92]. Examples of metrics used
when measuring and evaluating the received signal, are RSSI and SNR.
ATPC is utilized in cellular IoT technologies such as NB­IoT and LTE­M.
By being able to dynamically adjust the transmission power of the IoT devices and lower it
if needed, ATCP helps to extend the devices battery life, leading to fewer battery replace­

of course also reducing the energy consumption of the individual devices and therefore

Additionally, by ensuring reliable communication at long distances, if the signal from the

can lead to higher energy efficiency, depending on how much the transmission power in­

4.2.3
Efficient Protocols
By selecting energy efficient protocols to optimize data transmission, network operators
can reduce energy consumption and support the scaling communication in cellular IoT
networks with the use of Constrained Application Protocol (CoAP) and Message Queuing
Telemetry Transport (MQTT). Those two protocols ensures lightweight and reliable com­
munication between the networks IoT devices and the rest of the network infrastructure
[93].
CoAP
CoAP is based on the Representational State Transfer (REST) architecture and can run
on most devices that support User Datagram Protocol (UDP). It is designed as an efficient
resource utilization protocol for IoT devices with limited resources, as it provides efficient
data transmission with low overhead [94].
CoAP supports features such as multicast and caching, and is used in numerous IoT
devices deployed for smart homes, monitoring and sensors, as well as data exchange
between devices in smart cities for lighting, waste management and parking systems.
MQTT
MQTT is another lightweight messaging protocol that is designed for efficient communica­
tion between IoT devices and follows a publish­subscribe model, where devices publish
data that can then be received by interested subscribers [95]. It uses a broker­style ar­
chitecture, where a server receives all the messages from the senders and then routes
the messages to the receivers.
MQTT has a small packet size which reduces network overhead and can lead to energy
savings and is therefore often used in cellular IoT networks and especially in IoT devices
for monitoring and tracking.
Because of CoAPs and MQTTs compact message formats with minimal data overhead,
they are able to provide an increase in energy efficiency, because they reduce the size
of transmitted data packets and therefore the number of bits that need to be transmitted
over the network as well [93].
The fewer bits, and therefore the fewer packets that are transmitted over the network, re­
duces the overall traffic and can lead to less congestion which improves energy efficiency.
Additionally, CoAP and MQTT are designed with sleep mode mechanism in place for the
IoT devices, which is an excellent method for saving energy [93], as mentioned earlier
in this chapter. Both protocols easily enable the devices to effectively enter or exit sleep
mode when needed to ensure that they are available to communicate and handle all in­
coming traffic.

5
Future of Energy Efficiency in Cellular
Networks
With increasing demands on cellular networks regarding advanced applications and ser­
vice, more data traffic, larger number of users and devices as well as higher costs and
complexity of operating chose networks. Network operators therefore have to further
improve their networks as well as how they run them. This requires deploying and im­
plementing more advanced methods, which are beyond the technologies and techniques
presented in the last chapter.
As showcased in the case studies section from the previous chapter, AI based technology
is the future for improving every aspect of cellular networks as all the mentioned network
operators and companies from that section clearly states, that they are heavily focused
on AI based technology and have found promising results.
However, since AI based networks is a new technology and is still in its early stages, the
amount of research papers and articles regarding its usage for energy efficiency in cellular
networks is very limited.
This chapter will therefore serve as an introduction and overview of the technology, based
on the research paper [107] from 2022, which is a comprehensive survey on automation
solutions for 5G and 6G networks.
AI based technology within cellular networks refer to the implementation of AI for automa­
tion of network functions that enables efficient and self­managing operations, which limits
the need for humans to do it themselves.
Managing and optimizing cellular networks are traditionally done manually by the network
operators, but as the complexity and scale of moderns networks continue to expand, au­
tomated and more advanced methods for operation are needed, which is exactly where
technologies based on AI are ideal.
AI and ML algorithms can be used to collect, analyze and process large amounts of net­
work data to make optimal decisions to boost the overall network performance. As this
is done in real­time, it is possible for those algorithms to find patterns and even predict
network behavior, which enables better resource allocation, user experience and network
management.
Several attempts have been made in the past to automate and optimize operations of
cellular networks with a few examples being Active Networks (ANs), SON and Autonomic
Network Management (ANM). More recently, Zero­Touch Network Management (ZTM)
has emerged which is a technology for fully automating network management with limited
human involvement.
ZTM is able to analyze and understand its current state and will then provide recommen­
dations for possible reconfigurations for a human network operator to approve, before
making the changes to the network.
The introduction of ZTM can therefore be expected to play a key role in helping network
operators achieve high levels of automation and improved overall network performance.
Additionally, ZTM is expected to provide beneficial solutions for problems ranging from
energy­aware RAN resource allocation to automation of services.
Below is a brief descriptions of the other automation technologies used for network man­
agement:
Sustainable Communication Networks
67
==PAGE:81==
• Active Network Management is a network management system that enables pack­
ets transmitted through the network to contain programming codes that can modify
the network. Modern network technologies which revolves around centralizes net­
work management and separates the control plane from the data plane all originate
from ANM.
• Policy­based Network Management (PBNM) is another network management sys­
tem that uses policies to configure network elements and can operate in multiple
levels of a network. Policies are often defined as a set of rules that take certain
actions when specific conditions are met.
• Intent­based Network Management (IBN) is used to remove the manual human
aspect of configuring network devices and does so differently from policies, as intent­
based system works by having a set of goals for a communication network, without
having specifications for how to achieve them. When utilizing IBN, the network itself
is then responsible for reaching these goals and takes action accordingly.
• Self­organizing Network Management is an automation technology used through­
out the whole cellular network to make network planning, management and opti­
mization simpler and faster. It performs actions based on pre­defined rules and
solves tasks based on a specific set of inputs and outputs.
• Autonomic Network Management refers to the technique of configuration, moni­
toring and optimizing the network automatically without having a set of pre­defined
rules for the system to follow. The system can act on its own without humans or
other factors interfering.
To enable AI based technologies and ZTM in 5G networks and future generations of cel­
lular networks, other advanced technologies must be in place for them to function and
operate effectively and optimally.
These potential technological enablers can be categorized into programmatic control and
management, virtualization and orchestration, data analysis and closed loop control, AI
techniques for ZTM and finally building and testing tools.
Below is a brief description of the categories and their importance.
• Programmatic Control and Management refers to technologies that control and
manage wireless and mobile networks through software­based methods, such as
SDN. It is all about allowing operators to write software code that can perform man­
agement tasks and operations within the network, based on the available network
resources. Examples of tasks are troubleshooting of issues, resource allocation and
settings configurations.
• Virtualization and Orchestration is all about enabling ZTM with the use of virtu­
alization, which is a technology that mimics the functions of physical hardware in
virtual software to run multiple services and applications on a shared single phys­
ical machine. Orchestration is also needed and refers to the automation of man­
agement, scheduling and resource allocation to ensure efficient use of available
resources and that Service Level Agreements (SLAs) are maintained.
• Data Analysis and Closed Loop Control are services that cover both single and
end­to­end management domains and monitor components and services to provide
data on real­time performance of those network elements. This data is then used in
closed­loop automation to verify how the network reacts to changes. By implement­
ing data analysis and closed loop control, ZTM can be used for real­time network
Sustainable Communication Networks
68
==PAGE:82==
monitoring, smart decision making and automated optimization, overall improving
network management and the network.
• AI Techniques for ZTM is the use of AI and ML techniques for networks that is
designed to self­manage, self­adapt and self­react to changes or disturbances in
the network with minimal human involvement. Used AI and ML techniques are Su­
pervised Learning (SL), Unsupervised Learning (UL), Reinforcement Learning (RL),
Fuzzy Logic (FL), and Transfer Learning (TL). These techniques enables the net­
work to better analyze and predict to make smarter decisions on its own.
• Building and Testing Tools is all about better ML and networking validation envi­
ronments that enables less time spent on testbed setup as well as more powerful
and precise feedback of ZTM processes.
AI­based technology and ZTM promise significant improvements in many aspects of cel­
lular networks and will certainly help network operators improve the resource allocation,
optimization and power management of their networks.
With AI algorithms analyzing data traffic and patterns within the network, it will definitely
help increase energy efficiency and minimize energy consumption in cellular networks,
as energy efficiency methods will be implemented and utilized more effectively.

Conclusion
Throughout the recent years, cellular mobile networks have become an essential part
of our daily life, enabling communication, applications and services everywhere we go.
However, with the exponential growth of network data traffic and the increasing number
of connected devices, the energy consumption of these networks have increased sig­
nificantly, increasing costs of operation and environmental impact. Energy efficiency in
cellular mobile networks have therefore become a hot topic within the industry to try and
minimize environmental impact and lower costs.
Even though telecommunication companies and numerous studies across the industry are
focused on improving energy efficiency in cellular networks, there appears to be a lack
of standardized measuring methods for analyzing and comparing different components
and networks. A collection of relevant metrics and measuring methods have therefore
been presented, categorized into network performance metrics such as throughput and
coverage, channel quality metrics such as CQI and BER, and sustainable metrics such
as LCA and PUE. Other challenges within the field include available data and legitimate
data as companies tend to keep their data private for competitive reasons. Non­identical
components/devices from different manufactures and every network being different from
another is also a challenge, since it makes comparisons extremely difficult and less ac­
curate.
The breakdown of energy consumption and the distribution shows that the RAN and base
stations consume around 60­80% [47] of the total energy in cellular networks with the re­
maining energy being consumed by the mobile backhaul network at around 20% and the
core network at around 15% [42]. The RAN and base stations then spend approximately
50­80% energy on the power amplifier and around 10­30% on cooling systems [43] de­
pending on the base station size and coverage area, illustrating that focus should be on
the RAN and base stations considering energy efficiency.
By examining network data traffic patterns, ideal time­frames, being during the night and
early morning, were highlighted for optimally utilizing specific energy efficiency methods,
an example being sleep mode techniques.
Numerous strategies for energy efficiency in cellular mobile networks were presented and
described, promising increases in energy efficiency and energy savings for certain com­
ponents and sections within the networks. The different strategies were categorized and
it was explained where they are utilized and deployed within cellular networks. Network
architecture and technologies are methods such as C­RAN and HetNets which impacts
and changes the architecture and infrastructure of networks to increase energy efficiency
up to 60% [79]. Antenna and radio technologies include methods such as massive MIMO
systems and relay assisted networks that enhances signal quality to improve energy effi­
ciency by around 50% [77]. Energy managements techniques are methods that monitor
and control parts of the network to e.g. utilize the sleep mode technique which can pro­
vide energy savings of 90% [48]. The final category is carbon friendly techniques, which
uses energy harvesting to reduce the networks reliance on the power grid.
The case studies from the industry showed that massive MIMO systems and sleep mode
techniques and technologies are utilized at the major telecommunication companies, where
they have achieved fine results. Additionally, the case studies made it clear that the future
of energy efficiency in cellular mobile networks is AI based technology and ZTM, which
limits human involvement by automating network functions and operations to improve net­
work management and resource allocation and utilization.
