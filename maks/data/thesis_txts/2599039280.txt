Abstract
This thesis investigates a novel system of wheat straw pyrolysis production and its char, liquid and gas
products to investigate their energy potentials and thermal requirements. A basic ﬂowsheet system was
constructed with the intent of using partial oxidation to crack the carbohydrate compounds from the pyrolysis
into a simpler syngas composition, which would then use conventional methanol synthesis methodology.
Three diﬀerent cases were analysed using this base ﬂowsheet.
Case 1 investigated directing the liquid and gas components of the pyrolysis system and reported a
gas/liquid to methanol conversion of 71.3 % on a LHV basis and a carbon retention from biomass feedstock
to methanol and char products of 85.5 %.
Case 2 analysed the change in case 1 if only the gas mixture was processed through the methanol
synthesis.
This resulted in a capacity reduction of the ﬂowsheet by a factor of 3.33-3.8.
It reported a
carbon and chemical energy retention of 61.9 % and 70.5 % from partial oxidation process to the methanol
puriﬁcation.
Case 3 analyzed the consequences of adding H2 into the gas/liquid stream from the SOEC. This resulted
in an increase in carbon retention at the cost of reduced chemical energy conservation.
These kind of pyrolysis-based systems could prove to be an eﬀective method at a more sustainable
agriculture via biochar production as well as serving as a way of using ﬂuctuations in energy availability to
increase methanol production.

1
Introduction
With the current trend of securing alternate fuel sources from fossil fuels, a thermochemical bioreﬁnery plant
for the production of methanol for energy purposes using biomass poses a possible avenue towards that
trend. A bioreﬁnery is a facility which aims to generate chemicals and energy from biomass using sustain-
able means. This can be accomplished via gasiﬁcation of the biomass into syngas, a precursor gas for the
subsequent methanol synthesis. The current method for doing this is by a two-stage gasiﬁcation, which is
composed of a pyrolysis step at around 425-600 °C and a gasiﬁcation step at around 750 °C which produce
a product mostly composed of gaseous materials, most of which is composed of H2, CO, CO2 and CH4. (1) (2)
An alternate method to this would be to only use the pyrolysis step, which is the slow heating of biomass
which produces gas, char and liquid products. This would reduce the input energy required to create the
syngas volatiles, at the cost of increased amount of char formation in the reactor compared to the gasiﬁcation.
This char has been shown to be of use as a fertilizer for farming, due to its carbon enriching nature for the
soil. This has the advantage of creating a carbon cycle, where the biochar is used as a fertilizer for crops,
which will then go on to provide the feedstock for the pyrolysis process. The char also has an advantage in
that its production can employ feedstock conventionally considered too poor for bio-oil production, as well
as the feedstock being in low demand for other processes, such as agricultural waste.(3)
The syngas produces via this method will have smaller amounts of hydrogen, meaning that during
methanol synthesis, less amount of syngas will be formed. In order to alleviate this, the commonly used
method is to use the water gas shift reaction (equation 5) to rebalance the syngas to the ideal composition
between H2 and CO2. This will in turn produce additional amount of CO2, which will have to be vented into
the atmosphere or disposed of via carbon capturing methods. Alternatively, it is possible to add hydrogen to
the water gas shift reaction in order to form more CO while removing the CO2, resulting in higher methanol
yields and reduced CO2 emissions.
This additional hydrogen could be produced by way of solid oxide electrolysis cells (SOEC). While this
would increase the input energy of the plant design, it would also increase the chemical power recovery of the
biomass. The electrolysis also produces oxygen, which can be used for the partial oxidation (POX) of the
pyrolysis gas and for the pyrolysis chamber. The viability of this plan is dependent on grid electric energy
being low, due to its increased electrical requirements, as well as biomass prices being low. By utilizing
ﬂuctuation in energy prices over the daily cycle, the energy usage of the plant can be optimized by operating
the SOEC during the night when electrical prices are lower and storing the H2. This pairs well with the
ﬂuctuating energy production of solar and wind based energy as well as with ﬂuctuations in energy usage,
especially during the night. This would require storage of the hydrogen production, in order to capitalize on
the cost ﬂuctuations.
Bio char is a promising side product that can be harvested from the process. Its properties as a soil
enhancer oﬀer a partial solution to the climate impact of agricultural fertilizers while at the same time
improving fertility and agricultural produce by recycling carbon and other nutrients back into the soil. The
composition of the biochar heavily depends upon its feedstock and the type of pyrolysis, with diﬀerent
burning methods expunging diﬀerent compositions.(4) The reﬁned methanol product could then be used as
a power source for powering a generator. This would produce energy, waste heat for district heating and

reﬁne this methanol for the creation of advanced chemical products, such as jet fuel.
In this thesis, a preliminary look at a potential set up of a slow pyrolsysis based methanol plant with
biochar as a promising side product will be investigated with a focus on the lower heating values (LHV) of the
streams as well as heat integration on a surface level. The system will be simulated using Aspen Plus V8.8,

feeds into the partial oxidizer will be examined. The ﬁrst case is combination feed of the non-char products
of the pyrolysis, the condensable liquids (also known as bio-oil) and the uncondensable gas components fed
together into the partial oxidizer. The second case feed is the isolated feed of only the uncondensable gases.
Following that, a third case will be investigated which analyzes the eﬀect on the system hydrogen addition
has on the POX feed stream from case 1.

The conceptual layout of the plant begins with the drying of the biomass in preparation of the pyrolysis,
due to interference the moisture can have on the pyrolysis.
Secondly, the biomass will be fed into the

Methanol
Methanol is the simplest of the alcohols, consisting of a single methyl group linked to a hydroxyl group. The
chemical structure and physical properties can be seen in ﬁgure 2 and table 1, respectively (5; 6). Methanol
is a critical compound in chemical industry, being and important precursor of a range of chemicals such as
acetic acid, formaldehyde and multiple various combustible fuels, such as diesel, as well as solvents(7). These
multiple uses cause it to be produced all over the world in large quantities.

This reaction requires a large amount of energy to operate due to its endothermal nature, which can

An alternate method of forming the syngas is to use a partial oxidizer along with an extra supply stream
of oxygen to induce an autothermal partial oxidation, resulting in little to no heat generation, circumventing
the need for utilities. A ﬂaw with partial oxidation is its reliance on oxygen supply and the risk of potential

the operation of the POX. Section 5.6 goes into more detail on POX.
Methanol has been shown to be able to be used for combustion engines, with cars specialised in burning
it being available. It can also be mixed into conventional fuels for use in more conventional combustion
engines. One such way, as reported by , was the conversion of methanol into gasoline, processing a 96 % pure
methanol though a ﬂuidized bed reactor with a ZSM-5 zeolite catalyst to get 44% hydro carbon product, of
which 82% was gasoline, giving a total yield of 36,08% from methanol to gasoline(8).
3
Pyrolysis
Pyrolysis has multiple diﬀerent methodologies, with the main ones being slow, intermediate, fast and ﬂash
pyrolysis along with torrefecation. For the purposes of gas production, the ideal one is fast pyrolysis, while
for biochar production, a slow pyrolysis is preferable, due to increasing heating rates negatively aﬀecting char
production. Biomasses with high moisture are ill suited due to the water interfering with the controlled heat-
ing. Thus, pre-treatment via drying or choosing low moisture biomass is preferable for pyrolysis processes. (9)
Slow pyrolysis has a heating rate of around 10 ◦C/min and with operating temperatures around 400-
800 ◦C. This has traditionally been used for turning wood into charcoal. These kinds of setups have long
residence times and usually have high amounts of solid charcoal or char and low amounts of liquid fuels
relative to other pyrolysis methods(9). For the other methods of pyrolysis, fast pyrolysis is characterized by
high heating rates, heat transfers rates, careful temperature control from 425-600 ◦C and low residence times

while ﬂash pyrolysis has even smaller residence times and higher temperatures than fast pyrolysis, while
requiring extremely small granularity of the biomass feed which is usually accomplished through milling or
pelletizing. Torrefaction is operated similarly to slow pyrolysis except that it operates at temperatures around
200-300 °C, which causes the gas phase to mostly consist of CO and CO2, while retaining the carbohydrates
in the solid mass. Lastly, there is the intermediate pyrolysis, which has parameters between the slow and
fast pyrolysis and is good for waste wood, food and sewage sludge, and has a larger amount of biochar than
fast pyrolysis.(10)
Modern pyrolysis units often incorporate methodologies that crack the condensable products into syngas
in order to simplify the processing of the pyrolysis products, as well as circumventing the need of market-
ing/disposing of the liquid product and instead being able to concentrate the carbon balance into fewer
products (3).
While the pyrolysis process is an old and developed methodology, focusing on the production of char and
energy via more environmentally friendly feed stocks and methods is still in development. This is due to the
bio-oil being traditionally the more sought after product.
4
Pyrolysis Products
4.1
Biochar
Biochar is char derived from biomass feed stock and is the solid phase product of the pyrolysis process of a
biomass. Its primary usage is to act as a fertilizer for soil enrichment, by recycling key elements which were
extracted during the growth of the biomass, such as carbon, sulphur, nitrogen and phosphate. While the
application of char as a fertilizer for crops has been used for thousands of years, the way of seeing it as a way
of carbon sequestration and a method of ﬁghting global warming are relatively new ideas. This makes the
production of biochar promising for countries with large amount of agricultural residues, such as corn and
wheat straw. The main way this process combats global warming is by substitution of non-renewable fossil
fuels with renewable agricultural residues in the production of fuels, fertilizers and other various products.
While the fossil fuels and total classiﬁcations treatments of biomass may prove more energy productive in
the short term, this can lead to a non-regenerative cycle which would produce more energy in the long term
by sequestering carbon and other nutrients captured by the biomass back into the soil.(11)
The composition of the biochar heavily depends upon its feedstock and the type of pyrolysis, with diﬀerent
burning methods separating diﬀerent materials. The carbon ratio in it ranges from 50-55%. Nitrogen and
sulphur compounds usually volatilize between 200-375°C while potassium and phosphate volatilize at 700-
800°C. With the common pyrolysis methods being at 450-550°C, this means that the bio char will thus be
nitrogen and sulphur depleted. This can be combated by using nitrogen and sulphur rich feed stocks. Wood
and nuts have small amounts of phosphate and nitrogen, while manure, crop and food based bio chars are
richer in these nutrients.(12)
In summary, biochar promises to improve energy security via storage, mitigate greenhouse impact of
traditional fertilizes while also sequestering carbon back into the soil and increasing the sustainability of
agriculture(3).

4.2
Bio-oil
Bio-oil is the condensable part of the pyro gas mixture that is mostly composed of the same compounds as
the original biomass. In its liquid form it is a black/brown liquid which is often referred to as a tar. It has a
large amount of water compared to the other two products due to the moisture and water formed during the
combustion being allocated mostly into it. Like the other products, its composition and amount is dependent
on the methodology and the feedstock. For most pyrolysis methodologies, bio-oil is the largest share of the
resulting products. After the pyrolysis, it is usually in a gaseous phase and needs to be condensed into
its liquid phase. It can be used for numerous further reﬁnements, such as thermally cracking it for syngas
production and fuels, use it in the production of various diﬀerent chemical, reﬁne it into bio-diesel for
combustion or simply use it as a fuel for power generation and heating. (13)
For its use as a engine fuel, it has been shown that many engines can use processed bio-oil as a substitute
for diesel. If the more corrosive properties of the oil prove to be detrimental to the engine, the bio-oil can
also be mixed with diesel (14). A relative new technology is to use hydrotreating on the bio-oil in order to
produce jet fuels. This is done by ﬁrst removing water from the bio-oil, and then followed up by applying
hydrogen to the jet fuel in order hydrotreat it into hydrocarbon mixture which can then be separated using
fractionation to get the jet fuel (15).
4.3
Gas
The third product is the uncondensable gasses from the pyrolysis, which mostly consist of CO2, CO, methane
and higher order alkanes. This mixture is also known as syngas and is commonly used either as a source
of heat and energy via combustion or as a feedstock for the synthesis of ammonia, methanol and for fuel
production. (13)
5
Simulation description
In this section, a description of the overall design of the simulation and its individual systems, with the name
pyrolysis gas being used for the stream entering the partial oxidation in both simulations, whether it be the
gas only composition or the gas/liquid composition.
5.1
Design
The setup of the reﬁnery is divided into multiple major components. The planned capacity of the plant is
50 MW amount of wheat straw feedstock, calculated using its LHV. This small scale of the plant is in order
to reduce the amount of needed biomass, which can get costly to transport.
The ﬁrst is a pyrolysis unit, which produces a mix of gasses, liquids and biochar and is not simulated
in this design, but rather the values calculated from previous studies on the pyrolysis of straw (16). The
second is the partial oxidation, where the gasses and liquids are turned into a syngas, which is used as
the initial part of the simulation. Water is decanted from the syngas following the partial oxidation. This
is done in a heat exchanger by dropping the temperature, in order to remove undesirable water, which
can cause losses in methanol production in the later methanol reactor (see reaction 7). The gas cleaning
and conditioning for the subsequent methanol reactor loop, which is a well-studied process, follows this.
It includes an amine-based CO2 removal and a compression train in order to reach the required pressure
parameter of the methanol reactor. The partial oxidation step product is lacking in the hydrogen required

for the ideal methanol synthesis and thus we acquire that through a Solid Oxide Electrolyzer Cell (SOEC),
which electrolysis water into oxygen and hydrogen. Due to the ﬂuctuating price of electricity and thus price
of the SOEC, on-site hydrogen storage is required. The oxygen side product is used for the partial oxidation.
Following the methanol reactor, a simple gas liquid splitter is used to recycle the gasses back into the
methanol reactor and to retrieve the methanol. The methanol is then treated through a topping distillation
column in order to extract any gases in the product, leaving us with a slightly aqueous methanol product.
For this report, two diﬀerent feeds ﬂows into the POX were investigated. In the ﬁrst, the liquid was not
condensed from the gaseous product of the pyrolysis before being fed into the POX. In the second, the liquid
was separated and isolated into its own separate product, leaving only the gas to enter the POX. Following
that, a third case was investigated where hydrogen from the SOEC was added to the ﬁrst feed in order to
investigate its eﬀects on the system.
While it is not simulated in this design due to sulphur not being included in the material composition
of the ﬂows, a sulphur removal unit would have to be installed in order to prevent sulphur from entering
the system. A typical method of doing this is using a zinc oxide absorbant at temperature around 200-400
°C(17).
5.2
Electrolysis
A solid oxide electrolysis cell (SOEC) is used to make hydrogen and oxygen by using energy to separate the
two elements in water steam. The principle is similar to that of a Solid Electrolysis Fuel Cell (SOEF) except
it operates in reverse. Instead of taking in oxygen and Hydrogen and outputting electricity and water, it
takes in electricity and steam and outputs hydrogen and oxygen. This allows for the production of hydrogen
without the normally methane based steam reforming method at the cost of higher energy requirements. The
steam for the process is provided by the steam from the biomass drying process. The hydrogen production
required in the methanol synthesis is calibrated so that the molecular balance entering the methanol reactor
is two hydrogen moles per CO molecule.
A currently developing technology in the ﬁeld of electrolysis is high temperature steam electrolysis.
This requires less energy compared to the liquid water electrolysis due to the lower energy needed for the
dissociation of steam along with the higher temperature aiding it. This method can be preferable when
excess heat energy is available in the system, which can be used to produce said high temperature steam.
Steam electrolysis can have up to 92% eﬃciency from electricity to LHV H2 basis (18). This is a lot more
energy eﬃcient compared to the more traditional alkaline electrolysis methods eﬃciency of 70-80% (19). A
90% electricity to LHV hydrogen conversion will be assumed due to potential developments in the future
while also being a conservative estimate. The steam supplied to SOEC will be at 250 °C and at atmospheric
pressure.
While the theoretical upper eﬃciency of the steam is 100% from electricity to the LHV of hydrogen if the
only factors considered are the output of hydrogen and the input of electricity. This is reliant on enough heat
being applied to the steam, which in practice needs to be accounted for when the total energy eﬃciency of
the SOEC unit is calculated. The system does alleviate part of this energy requirement by providing its own
heating utility using the hot gas streams to provide heating for the inlet steam via. heat exchange. Another
reason is that the operational energy costs of the SOEC unit brings down its total eﬃciency. (20)(21).
The company Sunﬁre currently oﬀers a ﬂexible, modular SOEC unit which has a listed electricity to
H2 LHV ratio of 82%, which is smaller than the previously mentioned 92% eﬃciency, likely due to thermal

A water stream for the production of steam for the SOEC is added to the system, with the moles of water

5.6
Partial Oxidizer
The partial oxidizer is tuned at 3.96 bar pressure and 1200 °C in order to fully decompose the tars in the
liquid phase of the pyrolysis product as well as any methane. This system is based on tar removal POX
in Clausen et al. 2019(26). An oxygen stream is required in order to facilitate this process (this oxygen
stream is acquired from stored oxygen from the SOEC process). A Gibbs reactor is used to simulate the
chemical reactions in the oxidizer. This RGibbs reactor module works by minimizing the Gibbs free energy
of the feed under the speciﬁc temperature and pressure proﬁle, thus simulating what would happen in a
non-catalytic autothermal reactor. There are two primary chemical reactions happening inside the POX,
the partial oxidation of the methane,
CH4 + 1/2O2 ⇀

This is often done via catalytic converter which can help reduce the temperature requirements, with Ruthe-
nium Platinum noble metal catalysts being prominent candidates. Another method of partial oxidation is
to have it be followed up by steam reforming, which will not be examined in this simulation. By providing
the catalytic partial oxidation reactor with suﬃcient amount of oxygen, the partial oxidation can happen
autothermally, facilitating less heat formation in the reactor, reducing cooling amount and making up-scaling
easier. This is accomplished in the simulation by calibrating the O2 stream so that the heat duty of the
partial oxidizer is close to 0, so that no excess heat besides the one heating the streams entering the reactor
is formed. The oxygen also has the added side beneﬁt of removing coke that can contaminate the catalyst,

5.7
CO2 removal
The CO2 stripping step is used in order to reduce the CO2 levels in the methanol reactor, with lower values
meaning better conversion rate for methanol and reduced sizing of equipment for the subsequent methanol
synthesis loop. A standard set of amine adsorption/stripping columns is simulated via a splitter, with the
energy requirements of the stripper based on the amount of kg CO2 removed and the splitting calibrated to
set the CO2molar ratio entering the methanol reactor at 3%. The thermal energy requirements of the CO2
stripper were calculated using MJth/kg CO2 removed value of 3.5 (29).
5.8
Methanol reactor
The major problem with the methanol synthesis process is the large amount of heat produced during the
process. Two major ways are employed in getting rid if this heat. Either mitigating it with a catalytic
reactor or by employing heat transfer tubes/plates inside the reactor in order to transfer heat via the high
heat of evaporation of boiling water. The methanol reactor is an isothermal boiling water reactor with the
design restrictions that the H2/CO molar ration is at 2.02 and the CO2 molar fraction is at 3%. A REquil
reactor is used to simulate the two methanol related reactions:
CO + 2H2 ⇀
↽CH3OH
(∆H◦
298K = −90.55 kJ/mole)
(6)
CO2 + 3H2 ⇀
↽CH3OH + H2O
(∆H◦
298K = −49.43 kJ/mole)
(7)
These reactions are all simulated with a temperature approach of 15 °C. Note that equation 7 is equation
6 combined with the water gas shift reaction (eq.
5), thus incorporating it.
The REquil reactor unit
works by calculating the equilibrium of the chemical reactions that are enabled in the reactor with regards
to temperature and pressure. The temperature approach governs the temperature at which the reaction
equilibrium is calculated by adding it to the temperature of the reactor, thus simulating the temperature
proﬁle inside the reactor being higher than at the inlet/outlet. The reactor was set up in Aspen as a 270
°C reactor at 80.7 bar pressure. Following the methanol reactor, the temperature is reduced in order to
separate the methanol and water from the syngas with a simple gas/liquid splitter which is simulated via a
ﬂash drum. The syngas purge is then set to be 99%, due to low amount of methanol that reenters the loop.
The parameters of the MEOH reactor was based on work in Clausen 2015 (30).
5.9
Topping Column
The liquid product of the methanol reactor loop is then fed into a distillation column. The goal of this
distillation column, also known as a topping column, is to purge the methanol liquid of any excess gasses
leaving only the water and the methanol in the product. This is due to the fact that diﬀused gases can
interfere with further reﬁnement of the methanol into other products.
Achieving the commercial grade
purity of 99.85% (28) is not required due to the down stream processes not being expected to require that
level of purity. Distillation column was set up as a RADFRAC column with 10 stages and a partial vapor
condenser and with pressure ranging from 8 to 7.1 bar. The Reﬂux ratio of the column was set to 2 and
the bottoms rate was set to be equal to the methanol amount in the input stream. This way allows us to
adequately separate the gasses from the liquids. The parameters was based on work in Clausen 2015 (30).

5.10
Heat Integration
The reduction of utility cost by the usage of the heat of the available streams in the design will be looked
at in the following section. Five streams need to be cooled and three need to be heated. Five components
also have relevant heat requirements. The amine CO2 removal unit uses steam condensation to facilitate
heating for its operation which composes vast majority of its listed thermal requirements. The methanol
reactor and the distillation condenser both need cooling while the distillation reboiler needs heating. All of
them accomplish this via condensation/evaporation of steam. Finally, the pyrolysis unit requires heating
in order to operate. Thus, there are 7 units in need of cooling and 6 in need of heating. Using the cold
stream to cool some of the hot streams while also heating itself and vice versa would prove advantageous in
saving external heat energy requirements, which could prove to be a costly parameter. The component with
the greatest ﬂuctuations in thermal requirements is the SOEC unit, which has diﬀerent heat requirements
dependent on energy costs. An analysis will be done by performing a pinch analysis in order to identify the
systems overall heat requirements as well as identify the limit of its SOEC capacity.

shifted along the x-axis until its ﬁnal cumulative heat matched the hot stream, giving the graph seen on

6.5
Discussion
A critical factor which was not investigated in this thesis was the impact electricity prices can have on the
system, primarily with regards to the SOEC unit. This critical factor could shift the situation shown in
table 17 in favour of case 3(358) if the demand for methanol is high enough and the cost of electricity is
low enough. Likewise, another factor which was not factored in is the capitals cost of the plant and the
reﬁnement of bio-oil product in case 2. This bio-oil process would expand the potential heat integration
interactions of the plant. The smaller scale of the methanol system proposed in case 2 also makes it easier
to set up, although it comes with the issue of not being able to self-supply the heat requirements of the
pyrolysis reactor and itself.
Another factor that was not accounted for in the heat integration was the steam supply needed by
the steam drying of the biomass before the pyrolysis, which could prove to be an excellent avenue of heat
disposal. Another method of reducing heat requirements would be to employ some of the hot steam ﬂows
in the system as the SOEC feeds, although this would require them to be clean enough for the SOEC(25).
The heat integration which was performed in all three cases was surface level heat integration, with only
the major component heat exchanges looked at without regards towards reactor sizing and eﬃciency. The
electricity requirements of multiple sub systems such as compressors and other utility was not investigated.
Another part of the system that could be improved upon is the compositions data of the wheat straw,
which would help give a more accurate simulation of the system due to slight changes in composition changing
the results of the POX. Having a larger selection of compositional data of the gas phase composition of slow
pyrolysis would increase the accuracy of future investigations.
While the analysis in case 3 was not done on the gas only stream in case 2, it can be deduced that it
would have a larger impact on case 2 compared to case 1, due to the low H2 composition of the gas, reducing
the amount of carbon leaving the reactor as CO. Supplementing the POX with H2 could greatly increase

the POX CO production, at a larger relative energy cost increase compared to the example in case 3. A
preliminary investigation shows that methanol production would increase by 30.7 % at the cost of requiring
a 89.8 % increase in hydrogen production if all of the hydrogen were rerouted to the POX while still keeping
hydrogen entering the MEOH reactor at a 2.02 ratio to methane. This would increase the chemical energy
of the methanol to 10.1 MW, the electric energy cost of hydrogen up to 9.8 MW and a total energy eﬃciency
of the methanol against the inlet streams of 65.4%, which is lower than the result from case 2 (see 16)
where the second cases’ equivalent value was 70.57 %. The carbon retention in the system is 80 %, which is a
marked improvement from the original 61.68 %. These preliminary results conﬁrm the information from case
3 that the rerouting of the hydrogen stream increases carbon retention at the cost of a larger requirement
in hydrogen production.
7
Conclusion
This thesis has investigated a novel system of wheat straw pyrolysis production and its products. The ele-
mental and chemical composition of the straw and the pyrolysis products were estimated from measurement
data. Then a basic ﬂowsheet system was constructed with the intent of using partial oxidation to crack
the carbohydrate compounds from the pyrolysis into a simpler syngas composition, which would then use
conventional methanol synthesis methodology.
Three diﬀerent cases were analysed using this base ﬂow-
sheet. The ﬁrst investigated directing the liquid and gas components of the pyrolysis system and reported a
gas/liquid to methanol conversion of 71.3 % on a LHV basis and a carbon retention from biomass feedstock
to methanol and char products of 85.5 %. Composite curve heat integration was performed to analyze the
primary heat proﬁle of the system, which was found to generate a large amount of waste heat which could
be used for utility proposes outside the simulated system.
Case 2 analysed the change in case 1 if only the gas mixture was processed through the methanol synthesis.
There it was found that this would reduce the size of the ﬂowsheet section by factor of 3.33-3.8, reducing
capital and utility costs. This comes with the downside of producing less methanol and generating insuﬃcient
amount of heat in order to self-supply itself and the pyrolysis reactor, but comes with the added potential
of bio-oil reﬁnement.
Case 3 analysed splitting the hydrogen feed from the SOEC and feeding it into the POX feed and the
pre-methanol loop at the same time and looking at the changes to the system when hydrogen was shifted
from one to the other. From this it was found that by diverting more hydrogen to the POX feed, higher
carbon retention could be achieved at the cost of increased hydrogen production costs.
These kind of pyrolysis-based systems could prove to be a eﬀective method at a more sustainable agricul-
