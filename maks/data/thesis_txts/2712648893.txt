Abstract
Metamaterials are artiﬁcially structured composites. They can be used to modulate electromag-
netic waves. The structural dimensions must be determined by being smaller than the working
wavelength. Metasurfaces are multi- or single layer nanostructured surfaces. They can be used for
the application as polarization beam splitter or lens. As planar optical objects, metasurfaces can
substitute conventional optical elements. Also more complex applications are possible, e.g. lens
and polarization beam splitter combined and inducing an orbital angular momentum on a light
beam.
This project focuses on the fabrication of metasurfaces with the application as polarization beam
splitter. Introducing a phase gradient along a surface like a silicon-air interface enables the control
of the angle of diﬀraction. Varying the relative phase shift of adjacent meta-atoms in a manner
that there is a diﬀerent phase gradient resulting for orthogonal directions, allows to diﬀract ortho-
gonal states of polarizations at diﬀerent angles. Also right and left circularly polarized light can
be generated through a metasurface and diﬀracted into diﬀerent angles.
Design, characterization and fabrication of such devices requires precise metrology. Spectroscopic
ellipsometry is the main tool for optical characterization. It is a method relying itself on analys-
ing how diﬀerent states of polarizations are interacting with a sample. The working material for
metasurfaces fabricated in this project is amorphous silicon and also to be named - air. A high
permittivity contrast between these two allows that nanopillars are exhibiting waveguiding of light.
Transverse electric and transverse magnetic resonance modes can exist within the nanopillars which
are dependent or the pillars geometrical dimensions beside the refractive index. Based on these
eﬀects, one metasurface is for instance designed in a way that it shall diﬀract linear polarization of
a principle orientation at -6.74°while the orthogonal linear polarization to the ﬁrst on is diﬀracted
at 0°at normal incidence.
In order to realize such metasurfaces, a multi-step fabrication process needs to be carried out.
Therefore ”ISO 5” clean room work is necessary and the use of expensive machines like electron
beam lithography. A fabrication process is successfully established. Multiple issues need to be
solved through optimization of process parameters and the adaption; E.g. a chromium hardmask
is eventually used although it was initially not intended. The fabrication approach established here
can be directly transferred for the manufacturing of other amorphous silicon based metasurfaces.
Electromagnetic ﬁeld simulations are essential for the initial design of metasurfaces. These are typ-
ically non analytical computations and require some computational eﬀort. Diﬀerent commercial
and open-source software exist which are using a variety of methods. The design is carried out by
the project supervisor Morten Kildemo via ﬁnite element method. But also Fourier modal methods
are carried out in order to verify metasurface design by using another simulation method.

1
Introduction
Not just the properties of frequency and ﬂux can be assigned to light but also angular momentum.
Humans cannot see selectively decomposed polarization modes of for instance reﬂected sunlight but
some animals can. The desert ant ”cataglyphis” is taking advantage of polarized sun light and star
light during the night for its own orientation. A research team at from Aix Marseille University has
recently build a roboter inspired by the ants method of orientation. Therefore they are using two
rotating polarization ﬁlters and it resulted in a successful orientation of the robot with this support
(and counting steps) to navigate without the aid of any satellite positioning system (Dupeyroux
et al. 2019). Thus this technology reveals a possible redundancy system for e.g. drones using a
GPS sensor. As for any actively ﬂying object, reducing weight is an optimisation parameter hence
the polarized light detectors should be as small and light as possible. This is just one among other
possible ﬁelds of applications for metasurfaces.
Metasurfaces are thin layers consisting out of engineered structures within dimensions less than
the operation wavelength. Instead of conventional optical elements, metasurfaces can have similar
properties of light manipulation as e.g.
a lens has but rather as a thin object.
Objective of
this project is to nanofabricate metasurfaces proposed in a past publication (Arbabi et al. 2015)
and extend the design to further operating wavelengths. The motivation is to make an extensive
optical characterization of the metasurface and its wavefront manipulation using variable angle
spectroscopic Mueller Matrix ellipsometry. This characterization method essentially supports the
metasurface fabrication process. Ellipsometry, however, is not only an aid for monitoring, but also
for characterizing materials in order to be able to design metasurfaces accurately in the ﬁrst place.
The work chain has the following procedure: (1) Transmission and phase simulations of meta-
atoms. These are performed for diﬀerent wavelengths and are based on previously characterized
working materials.
Based on such simulations, the design of diﬀerent metasurface application
can be created. (2) A nanofabrication process approach has to be established and optimized. (3)
Optical characterization of the completed metasurfaces follows. The results of this can be compared
with simulation and fabrication results.

2.1.2
Orbital angular momentum
Orbital angular momentum (OAM) is a property of a light beam rather than of a single photon.
OAM is linked to the description of Vortex beams. Vortex beams are characterised by a doughnut
shaped intensity distribution of the beam cross-section. Moreover this cross-section, thus the spatial
distribution, is inhomogeneous in phase or polarisation. In case of Vortex beams being caused by
OAM, the phase is inhomogeneous across a beam cross-section and can exist independently of the
polarisation. Figure 2 visualizes helical beams carry OAM of diﬀerent topological charge. The
phase can be characterized by a factor eilθ where θ is the azimuthal angle and l is the topological
charge of OAM (J. Liu et al. 2020). Through this factor a helical wavefront arises which is quantised
by the topological charge l ∈Z (H. Zhang et al. 2019). The fact that l can also be negative is based
on the assumption that eilθ is not a unitary operator and θ as angle operator is not Hermitian.

2.2
Optical Properties of Solids
The description of electromagnetic waves and photons as given in Section 2.1 applies equally
for free space just as for space containing matter. The coeﬃcients for relative permittivity and
permeability are taking care of the description of electromagnetic waves in an environment of
atoms. These coeﬃcients are complex dimensionless values. They are depended on propagation
direction, polarization, frequency and material characteristics like elemental constitution, density
etc. Other properties, like the absorption coeﬃcient can be derived from these optical constants.
Thus these coeﬃcients are crucial for the description of the optical response of a material. As
mentioned the permittivity is not just dependent of the material and structure but also of the
frequency (dispersion). From now on the focus is just on the relative permittivity and the relative
permeability is approximated to unity.
In order to understand and model the permittivity, ﬁrst the Lorentz model is introduced which
is then extended to the ”free electron” Drude-model. The eﬀort of this theoretical description is
made in order to describe and understand metamaterials and metasurfaces.

The electric permittivity is in fact not a scalar measure but a second rank tensor (3x3 matrix).
The non-zero and symmetric components of the tensor are determined by the crystal system of

having three axes of same length and are all perpendicular to each other. There are just diagonal
non-zero elements of the permittivity tensor, assuming the principal axes of the crystal system
are aligned with the coordinate system. Due to the existing symmetries the permittivity tensor
its diagonal elements are all equivalent. The hexagonal crystal family and the tetragonal crystal
system are called uniaxial. Two diagonal elements are equivalent to each other e.g. ϵ11 = ϵ22.

polarized in that axis propagates slower, would be called slow axis. The remaining three crystal
systems (orthorhombic, monoclinic and triclinic) are characterised with axes all of diﬀerent lengths.
They are called biaxial crystal systems.

2.3
Light-Matter Interaction
In order to model light-matter interaction, a semi-classical model is applied: Light is treated as
electromagnetic wave based on Maxwell’s equations (Equation 7 - Equation 10). Light is typically
just the humanly visible spectrum of electromagnetic radiation but is used as representative term
respectively abbreviation for the whole inﬁnite spectrum here. Such an electromagnetic wave is
propagating on a null geodesics path through space-time and its wavefront can be represented
by a hypersurface which is element of the four-dimensional space-time following general theory
of relativity (Ueno 1953).
The Poynting vector of electromagnetic radiation (Equation 26) is
perpendicular to an inﬁnitesimal increment of the hypersurface and perpendicular to the time
vector. A hypersurface is an (n-1)-dimensional surface in an n-dimensional space (Cisneros-Molina
et al. 2021). From now on the time axis is assumed to be perpendicular to the three dimensional
space axes and thus a consideration of an explicit four-dimensional space-time vector space can be
neglected.
Making this assumption eﬀectively means a classical treatment of an electromagnetic wave. In
the following, diﬀerent models for light-matter interaction are described with an increasing level
of abstraction. In any case electromagnetic radiation is treated classically as previously described
while for the models of matter a transition is made from classical to quantum mechanical models.
This assumptions made have to be kept in mind to be aware of limitations on accuracy and validity
of the applied models. There is no concern of quantum electrodynamics (QED) in this thesis which
is why it is omitted. As relativistic quantum ﬁeld theory of electrodynamics, QED would be the
state of the art description of light-matter interaction. This theory is mentioned to point out the
whole range of levels of abstraction regarding light matter interaction respectively the variety of
models and approximations made.
Deﬁnitions of relevant terms in light-matter interaction are explicitly emphasized (referring to
photons but also applies to other particles). Starting with scattering, diﬀraction and deﬂection:
 Scattering: A photon can change its wave vector (momentum) due to interaction with mat-
ter (absorption and re-emission). It can be distinguished between inelastic (e.g. Compton)
scattering and elastic (e.g. Rayleigh) scattering (Adams 2017). The scattering interaction is
related to the matrix element (Equation 48).
 Diﬀraction: ”Any deviation of light rays from rectilinear paths which cannot be interpreted
as reﬂection or refraction (Sommerfeld 1912).”
11
==PAGE:24==
 Deﬂection: The change of the path of light. This can be due to the inﬂuence of a gravita-
tional force (S. S. Shapiro and I. I. Shapiro 2010).
The following terms are also deﬁned for the sake of clarity:
 Reﬂection: The change of a photons wave vector due to interaction with an interface back
into the originating medium (Lekner 1987)
 Refraction: If optical properties of a medium in which an EM wave is propagating change,
the EM wave experiences a change in momentum. If the incident EM wave has an angle
diﬀerent then 0° to the optical axis, the refracted EM wave experiences a new direction then
the incident one. This is called refraction which is dependent on the refractive index. Snell’s
law gives an mathematical description which can be derived from Maxwell’s equations while
Fermat’s principle of least time is a phenomenological explanation. Moreover refraction is also
dependent of a gradient in phase along an interface which is covered by the generalized Snell’s
law (Bloembergen and Pershan 1962) which also applies for metasurfaces as demonstrated
2011 (N. Yu et al. 2011). Refraction is frequency dependent (dispersion). Refraction is a
case of transmission.
 Transmission: Same as refraction; propagation of EM wave from one medium into another
respectively change of optical properties of surrounding matter. Characterized by transmis-
sion coeﬃcient. It is the counterpart of reﬂection.
 Absorption: EM waves propagating in matter are always damped. The attenuation can be
described by the Beer-Lambert law and is related to the extinction coeﬃcient κ respectively
absorption coeﬃcient α which is an intrinsic materials property but also dependent on the
perturbation itself (Beer 1852). More about that in Section 2.2.2. The photoeﬀect explains
absorption of EM waves quantum mechanically.

Waveguide modes:
Previously, a solution of Maxwell’s equations in free space has
been derived, which turned out to be a plane wave (Equation 15). This mode is called transverse

 Transverse electric (TE) mode is characterized by an electric ﬁeld normal to the direction

 Transverse magnetic (TM) mode is characterized by a magnetic ﬁeld normal to the

Eigenvalue equations need to be solved to ﬁnd the resonance modes which can propagate within a

Fabry-P´erot resonator:
A Fabry-P´erot resonator is an optical resonator. It supports
resonance frequencies depending on the refractive index and length of the cavity (Ismail et al. 2016).
Supporting means that it exhibits a frequency dependent transmittance.
2.3.2.3
Mie-Scattering:
The paper from Q. Zhao et al. 2009 gives a good overview about the
relevance of Mie scattering theory in the context of dielectric metamaterials. Scattering of EM
waves at small spherical particles can be calculated which is known as Mie solution of the diﬀrac-
tion problem (Bohren and Huﬀman 1998). Speciﬁcally coeﬃcients for the scattered electric and
magnetic ﬁeld can be obtained through a multipole series. Based on these coeﬃcients an eﬀective
permeability and permittivity can be calculated. The scattering ﬁelds depend especially on the
electric and magnetic dipole resonances of the scattering particle. Diﬀerent TE and TM modes can
exist within such a particle. Depending on the geometry, operating frequency and materials being
used, almost arbitrary eﬀective permittivity and permeability values can be obtained. Electric and
magnetic ﬁelds are mainly localized within the scattering particles. Not just dipole but also higher
order Mie resonances can occur.

2.3.3.1
Scalar theory of diﬀraction
A theory of diﬀraction has the goal to ﬁnd a ﬁeld distri-
bution resulting through an initial ﬁeld interacting with apertures. The phenomena of diﬀraction
has been observed 1665 by Grimaldi. He realized a gradual transition between light and shadow
on a screen after a small aperture. This contradicted the both so far known explanations of reﬂec-
tion and refraction which demanded a sharp transition. Several persons made previous signiﬁcant
contributions, like Christian Huygens (wave theory of light, 1678), Thomas Young (concept of
interference, 1804), Augustin Jean Fresnel (bringing wave theory and interference together, 1818),
J.C. Maxwell (light as electromagnetic wave, 1860), Gustav Kirchhoﬀ(solid mathematical found-
ation, 1882), Poincar´e in 1892 and Sommerfeld 1894 (for proving inconsistency in assumptions of
Kirchhoﬀhis Huygens-Fresnel principle) until Sommerfeld used Green’s function to eliminate
one of Kirchhoﬀ’s assumptions and presenting the Rayleigh-Sommerfeld diﬀraction theory
(Goodman 2005). The gap of the evolution of diﬀraction theory in the 18th century was caused
by the presence of Sir Isaac Newton and his believe into the corpuscular theory of light (and the
fact that his opinion was too dominant).
The assumptions which are made in both the Huygens-Fresnel principle and Rayleigh-Sommerfeld
theory are subsequently listed and limit the use of these theories to model diﬀraction pattern of
metasurfaces. The application of these theories can be summarized by the fulﬁlment of these two
conditions:
 The aperture size must be large in comparison to the transmitting wavelength.
 The diﬀraction pattern must be observed in suﬃcient distance to the aperture (Goodman
2005).
These two conditions are caused by the fact of simplifying an electromagnetic wave to a
scalar phenomenon while there is actually coupling between electric and magnetic ﬁeld vectorial
components following Maxwell’s equations. The property of polarization is irrelevant respectively
cannot be considered in a scalar theory. Further assumptions have to be made in order to yield
accuracy of model and real diﬀraction pattern:

2.4.1
Working principle of Metasurfaces
Mϵτα is Greek and means ”beyond”.
Metamaterials attain their properties rather from their
structure than from the material properties. They are artiﬁcial composites having properties that
are not found in nature and not observed in constituent materials. Having an artiﬁcial structure
makes the composite inhomogeneous and thus is described with eﬀective material parameters. In
order to homogenize the description of metamaterials the wavelength has to be much longer than
the size of the structural units (artiﬁcial atoms) (Tanaka 2019).
Metasurfaces are a special case of metamaterials respectively the planar counterparts. A metas-
urface consists of a single layer or a few-layers stack (Sun et al. 2019). It is referred to the work
by N. Yu et al. 2011 for initiating/driving a strong development of metasurfaces within the last
decade. Purpose of metamaterials is to modify an incident electromagnetic wave in a desired man-
ner. The artiﬁcial atoms in metasurfaces have a three dimensional extend but are translated just
two dimensionally. These artiﬁcial atoms form optical resonators and by varying the units in a
two-dimensional array a spatially varying phase response can be achieved. This leads to variety of
applications of metasurfaces, which are outlined in Section 2.4.2.
Each meta-atom ideally has an independent electromagnetic ﬁeld response unaﬀected by other
meta-atoms.
If the two parameters transmittance (respectively reﬂectance) and phase
can be arbitrarily modulated for a single meta-atom, then the outgoing electromagnetic ﬁeld can
be arbitrarily shaped (without any frequency modulation). Moreover, if the meta-atoms exhibit
birefringent properties, also a polarisation dependent control of the outgoing electromagnetic
ﬁeld can be obtained. Finally, the wavevector shall also be modulated. How to modulate each of
these properties is brieﬂy outlined:
Transmittance/Reﬂectance
can be modulated by changing the waveguide modes that can
exist in the structure.
But in the case of dielectric nanopillars, they can also be modelled to
work as low quality-factor Fabry-P´erot resonator (see: Section 2.3.2). Thus resonance modes are
key to reﬂection and transmission coeﬃcients (Arbabi et al. 2015). Resonances are eigenfunctions
constraint to the boundary conditions of the structure of a meta-atom.
These resonances are
caused by dipole and multipole excitement.
Ideally, the reﬂectance or transmittance can be arbitrarily modulated between 0 and 100 %. Large
forward scattering inside the pillars is important in order to have high transmission.
Phase:
The outgoing phase is dependent on the electric and magnetic ﬁeld response respectively
polarisation inside a meta-atom. This means the phase is dependent on dipole resonances which
a ﬂuctuating incident electric and/or magnetic ﬁeld induces. The impedance relates the electric
and magnetic ﬁeld with each-other Equation 21. A complex impedance value gives information
about the phase shift between electric and magnetic ﬁeld. A phase delay can accumulate within a
meta-atom. Varying impedance of meta-atoms causes varying phases of outgoing electromagnetic
waves.
Ideally the phase can arbitrarily be modulated between 0 and 2π. Strong magnetic dipole polar-
izations inside the pillars are important to achieve such a wide range of phase shifts.
Polarization:
In order to have a polarization dependent propagation, anisotropic properties are
required. This requires a system inheriting anisotropic meta-atoms. Natural materials seldom vary
more than 10 % in their ordinary and extraordinary refractive indices (Hu et al. 2020). Metasurfaces
can be superior in the contrast of refractive indices along two orthogonal axes.
Wavevector:
How a light beam propagates in the far ﬁeld is determined through the vectorial
components of the electric near ﬁeld. Evanescent modes in the near ﬁeld may not be regarded.
The far ﬁeld can be calculated using a theory of diﬀraction when having e.g. information about
18
==PAGE:31==
a phase and amplitude mask in the near ﬁeld. With the formulation of generalized Snell’s law in
2011 (Equation 58 and 59), the far ﬁeld can also be modelled as eﬀective refracted beams. In this
case information about the phase gradient is required. A phase gradient can be artiﬁcially in-
troduced through a gradual relative phase shift of adjacent meta-atoms along an axis.
Shall the beam transmitted through a metasurface be called refracted or diﬀracted?
Huygens
diﬀraction cannot fully explain the phenomena of refraction since it would allow multiple angles
of refraction. These multiple angles are called orders of diﬀraction. Intensity can vary between
these orders of diﬀraction. Metasurfaces in this project shall just diﬀract into one order at a one
principal polarization. This should be the case if the period between meta-atoms is smaller than
the operating wavelength (Shen et al. 2021). This means high-order diﬀraction is not occurring.
Hence a theory of diﬀraction can explain the transmitted light through a metasurface. Although
eﬀective refraction can describe the phenomena it is emphasized that the incident light beam is
not describing the path of a refracted TEM wave through the metasurface. A phase gradient along
the interface is essential for an angle of diﬀraction. A phase gradient term can also be found in
the generalized law of refraction.
It can be distinguished between plasmonic metasurfaces and all-dielectric metasurfaces. ”Multi-
resonance” and ”gap-plasmon” metasurfaces are example for the former category. They can be
realized by V-shaped antennas that support two eigenmodes respectively through a metal-insulator-
metal (MIM) structure that has strong nearﬁeld coupling between dipoles in the metal and an
antenna array in the top layer. The metasurface in this project belongs to the latter category and
is speciﬁcally a high contrast dielectric metasurface which supports the aformentioned multiple
Fabry-P´erot resonances. Magnetic dipole resonances are pronounced due to high refractive index
contrast and thus the electromagnetic ﬁeld is strongly conﬁned within each meta-atom (Hsiao et al.
2017).

Application of thin ﬁlm optics
Altering an incident wave through an interface of a metasurface opens a variety of possible applic-

already used while others are just a perspective for a possible future. Disadvantages from which
current metasurfaces are still suﬀering are a limited operational frequency range, low eﬃciencies
(e.g. low transmission) and fabrication of large-area metasurface at low cost and fast (Tong 2018).
Nanoimprint lithography could provide a fabrication technique for large-area and fast metasurface

Building a polarizing device has a variety of applications. Spectroscopic ellipsometry is used as

(Lalanne 1999).

possibility to split a light beam dependent on its angular momentum (Yin et al. 2013, Y. Liu et al.
2017).

3.1
Nanomanufacturing
This section describes a typical nanofabrication process and outlines speciﬁc methods used in this
project. But what is nanofabrication? ”Nano” refers here to the structural dimensions of less than
100 nm. In a traditional planar fabrication process, 2-dimensionally shaped structures are created.
Pattern transfer and patterning process steps are essentially used. The highest resolution obtained
by a 3D printing process is 500 nm using the two-photon polymerization process. Although this
is a relative high resolution for a 3D structure, it is rather microfabrication than in the nanoscale
(Geng et al. 2019).
The starting point of a metasurface is the choice of a substrate. Fused silica (SiO2) is used as
substrate in this project just as microscope slides (soda–lime–silica ﬂat glass sheets produced with
the ﬂoat glass process (Sustainability of Construction Materials 2016)). ”Microslides” are mainly
used as substrate precursors for experimental practising.
Structures out of a-Si on top of the
substrate are the active elements that constitute the metasurface. These structures cannot be
deposited directly but instead multiple steps of pattern transfer and patterning are required.
Mainly three diﬀerent approaches are pursued in this project: The ﬁrst approach starts with
thin ﬁlm deposition (Section 3.1.1), then a resist is transferred on the thin ﬁlm. Next step is
patterning of the resist with the (negative) projection of the desired structures. Lithography is
used as patterning method (Section 3.1.2). After the pattern is developed it is transferred via
etching (Section 3.1.3). Last step is stripping of the remaining resist layer (Cui 2017). These steps
are further outlined in the subsequent sections. In between these steps characterization is carried
out mainly for obtaining reference points and having a continuous quality control. Three principal
analysation methods are used here: Spectroscopic Ellipsometry to obtain information of optical
properties and thicknesses of layers (3.2.1), scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (3.2.2) and atomic
force microscopy (AFM) is used for imaging of the sample surface and also to achieve information
about the thickness of layers and geometry of structures (3.2.3).
A hardmask is used in another fabrication approach.
This means after resist development, a
material like chromium or aluminium dioxide is deposited. A lift-oﬀof the remaining resist covered
by the hardmask is carried out, then the pattern transfer into the desired layer takes place through
etching and ﬁnally the remaining hard mask is removed.
Figure 5 visualizes the workﬂow of
the entire fabrication chain of this approach since it is the most elaborated one. In a simpliﬁed
fabrication approach no etching is required at all. It has essentially just ﬁve steps: First the resist
is deposited directly on the substrate. Secondly, electron beam exposure. Thirdly development of
the resist. Fourthly deposition of the desired thin ﬁlm on the developed resist. At last lift-oﬀof
the remaining resist takes place.

3.1.1.1
PECVD:
General characteristics of plasma enhanced chemical vapour deposition are
a good ﬁlm uniformity, good ﬁlm density, 10 −100 nm ﬁlm grain size, substrate temperature of
200 −300°C and a moderate directionality. Dielectrics are the materials which generally can be
used in this deposition method (Cui 2017).
Working principle and set-up of a PECVD is the following: The substrate surface is cleaned and
placed on a platen in the instrument. The platen is automatically moved into a closed chamber.
The platen is heated and grounded. After achieving the desired level of vacuum the process gas
is inserted through a gas inlet. The pressure in the chamber is between 67 Pa and 267 Pa. A
radio frequency (RF, at 13.56 MHz) source and a low frequency source (LF at 50 −460 kHz)
are connected to an electrode above the platen. A plasma is created inside the chamber due to
the alternating electric ﬁeld. A deposition process starts due to a reaction of the ionized gas and
radicals with the substrate. ”PLasmaLab System 100-PECVD” is the PECVD model used, from
the manufacturer ”Oxford Instruments”. The longer the deposition process takes place, the thicker
the deposited ﬁlm.
Challenges in PECVD: In order to deﬁne challenges, an ideal result has to be deﬁned. High purity
and uniformity of the desired deposited thin ﬁlm should generally apply. Moreover control of the
desired ﬁlm thickness. Problems which can occur is ﬁlm stress due to a non-uniform deposition.
Furthermore adhesion problems to the substrate. A low surface roughness is usually desired.

3.1.1.2
Spin coating:
Thin ﬁlm deposition using spin coating refers basically exclusively to
the deposition of a photoresist respectively to an electron beam sensitive resist. It is usually de-
sired to have good adhesion of resist with the surface below, uniform thickness and no air bubbles
contained inside. A spin coater is a device for which acceleration and angular frequency can be set.
The sample is placed on a rotary table with vacuum suction to keep the sample steady through
applying negative pressure to the bottom of the sample. The sample center is covered by resist in
a fashion that at least 25 % to 50 % of total area is covered and no air inclusions are present. The
spin coating may start right away in order that the resist does not already start drying. The spin
coater used in this project is a model from ”SUSS MicroTec”. The data sheet of an e-beam resist
gives information about a spin curve. This gives a ﬁrst orientation for the corresponding angular
frequency to a desired resist thickness. Nevertheless the spin coating process may be customized.
For instance the surface which shall be coated can ﬁrst be treated with an adhesion promoter like
hexamethyldisilazane (HMDS) (Cui 2017). Moreover a ”thinner” for the resist can be used like
anisole (CH3OC6H5) to achieve thinner coatings. In the case of electron beam lithography, an
anti-charging layer on top of the e-beam resist might be desired.
Challenges to face in the spin coating process can almost all be tackled through a thorough
cleaning of the surface that shall be coated just as adapting the spin coating settings (acceleration
and angular frequency). Eﬀects of uncoated areas, pin holes, comet streaks, bubbles, swirl and
chuck marks can be caused by insuﬃcient volume of resist, air bubbles/particles, particles/spin
speed/acceleration settings, air bubbles in resist, too high spin speed/acceleration or ﬂuid not
centred and bend substrate respectively.
3.1.1.3
E-beam evaporation:
Working principle is an electron beam sweeps over a target
surface. Through the impact of the electrons and the low pressure in the chamber (10−7 Torr)
atoms from the target material are evaporating. A precipitation of these atoms takes place at the
chamber surface. In order to obtain a uniform coating, the sample can be rotated. In this project
the model ”Vacuum Classic 500” from the manufacturer ”Pfeiﬀer” is used. Not just metals (like
chromium) but also oxides, magnetic materials and dielectrics (like silicon) can be evaporated. The
acceleration voltage of the electron beam is 8 kV and the deposition rate can be adjusted between
0.1 nm/s and 20 nm/s. Multilayering recipes can also be run.
Challenges to face during E-Beam evaporation is like in any deposition process: Uniform and
well controlled thickness layer just like a pure and homogenous thin ﬁlm deposition without any
contamination. Challenges for deposition of a metal on an e-beam resist layer are emphasized:
cross-linking, blisters and bubbles can occur in the resist layer through present ions during the
evaporation process. Also a too high temperature can cause bursting of an underlying resist layer
(Volmer et al. 2021).
Especially cross-linking has a negative eﬀect on a successful subsequent
lift-oﬀ.
3.1.1.4
Sputter coating
is a deposition method for very thin layers usually between 1 and
20 nm. ”208 HR B” is the model used in this project from the manufacturer ”Cressington”. Typ-
ical target materials are platinum/palladium and gold. The working principle is the following: The
sample is placed into a closed chamber except with an inlet for an inert gas as argon and outlet
connected to a vacuum pump. The sputter coater is operated in diode conﬁguration. This means
that the target is placed at the cathode while the sample is placed on the anode. Free electrons
collide with argon atoms which become ionized. The Ar nuclei are accelerated towards the target
where they are sputtering oﬀthe target elements through physical impact. The target atoms a
precipitating everywhere in the chamber (Cui 2017). The deposition is controlled through the
oscillation of a quartz crystal just like during e-beam evaporation.
Challenges are to obtain a grain-free uniform coating. Since typical application of such a coating
process is to deposit a conductive layer for SEM investigations, the layer should be thick enough
to prevent charging (≳2 nm) but not too thick too maintain a high contrast (≲10 nm).

The sample is now prepared for the EBL. After the resist exposure, the sample has to be developed,

Challenges of EBL can be heating, stitching, fogging, and proximity eﬀects. Diﬀraction limit
is just a problem at structures with dimensions similar to the electron wavelength which is much
smaller than at photolithography. The problem of heating is caused by the highly concentrated
electron beam bombardment and can cause a critical dimensions distortion. It can be tackled by
using an algorithm which optimizes writing speed and avoiding to write adjacent areas. Stitching
is relevant in the technic of multiple EBL (MBL) where multiple electron beams are used at the
same time. The overlay from two beams at one writing area can have an error which causes a
defect area. Stitching can also cause a problem at the interface of two write-ﬁelds in a single beam
operation. Proximity eﬀect and fogging result in a deviation of the intended pattern size. It is
caused by forward scattered electrons in the resist and backscattered electrons at e.g. the backside
of the substrate. The latter two eﬀects can be tackled by dose correction and adapting the shape
of the pattern (Chang et al. 2015). Furthermore, an undercut might be desired for the subsequent
process step after resist development and hardmask deposition. It facilitates the lift-oﬀ. A resist
can have a predisposition for causing straight or inclined walls after development. Charging can
be a problem at presence of a dielectric layer below the resist. It can be tackled by using an anti-
charging layer. Just like any device operating with a focused or parallel beam, it is important to
reduce astigmatism, improve focus and operate at a narrow and steady bandwidth (beam current).
It is emphasized that pitch, current and dose-time has to be optimized dependent on the resist (and
structures). The beam current and aperture is related to the beam diameter and thus resolution.
The choice of dose (charge per area) is dependent on the beam current, pitch and exposure time
which is constraint constraint to the machine its minimum dose time. ”ELS-G100” is the model
used in this project from the manufacturer ”Elionix”. It operates at an acceleration voltage of

Etching:
It can be distinguished between wet etching and dry etching methods. In this project
a reactive ion dry etching method is chosen (RIE). More speciﬁcally inductively coupled plasma
- reactive ion etching (ICP-RIE) is used since it overcomes the problem of deterioration of the
etching selectivity in conventional RIE (Cui 2017). Etching selectivity refers to relative etch rates
of diﬀerent materials to each other (hardmask and thin ﬁlm here).
The instrument for ICP-RIE has a similar set-up as the one for PECVD (Section 3.1.1). The
etching takes place through a combination of physical impact and and chemical erosion of the thin
ﬁlm surface. ”Oxford Instruments” is the manufacturer of the ICP-IRE model ”Plasmalab System
100 ICP-RIE 180” used in this project.
Challenges in etching are a high and uniform etch rate and a challenge in the stripping process
is avoiding the collapse of structures. A high selectivity is generally desired. Moreover it can be
distinguished between directional and isotropical etching. While wet etching might has advantages
in selectivity above dry etching, dry etching can achieve better results in directional etching.
It is referred to the ”Bosch process” which allows high aspect ratio ion etching. The anisotropic
characteristic is achieved though alternating passivation, bottom ﬁlm etching and thin ﬁlm etching.
Passivation and thin ﬁlm etching is taking place in parallel in conventional ICP-RIE etching.
Loading eﬀects during etching are emphasized. Dependent on the absolute area present of surface
being etched, the composition of gasses in the etching chamber is aﬀected. This has an eﬀect on
the etch rate.
3.1.3.1
Etch rate determination
An accurate determination of the etch rate (in the unit
[nm/s]) is crucial in order to prevent an under- or over-etch. The etch rate is not just dependent
on the instrument and recipe but also from the carrier wafer and sample itself due to e.g. loading
eﬀects.
A speciﬁc etch rate determination can look like this: Amorphous silicon (a-Si) is deposited for

34 mins using PECVD on 1 µm SiO2 on a 4” crystalline silicon wafer. A layer of photoresist is
spin coated and after photolithography a reference pattern is developed. The wafer is sawed into
pieces of the size 400 mm2. A series of measurements is carried out on a sapphire (Al2O3) and
silicon dioxide (SiO2) 4” carrier wafer. Etch times vary between one and 300 s. Analysation of the
samples is made using three methods:
 Proﬁlometer: The undeveloped remaining photoresist is dissolved after the etching in acet-

 Ellipsometry: A developed pattern is not necessary in this method. Thus the photoresist

etching the sample for a speciﬁc time, it is analysed using spectroscopic ellipsometry. The

 Interferometry: Same sample preparation as for the ellipsometry method. Interference of
a reﬂected laser beam gives information about the change in layer thickness during the etch
process. More about that method in Section 3.2.5. An etch rate can even be determined in
situ. Moreover this method can be used as ”Endpoint Detection”. This means the point in
time when the layer of a-Si is etched through can be determined in real time.
The selection of the carrier wafer material is a choice dependent of etch rate control and etch
duration respectively suﬃcient high selectivity between mask and desired layer to etch. Using a
sapphire carrier wafer increases expectedly the etch rate due to reduced loading eﬀects, while it
makes the etch rate at the same time dependent on the exposed silicon area of the sample. A
silicon carrier wafer gives more control over the etch rate because it is rather independent from
the sample since exposed silicon on the sample is small compared to the wafer size. But due to
increased loading eﬀects, the etch rate for silicon is reduced, while it remains constant for the
resist thus reducing selectivity which requires a thicker resist layer. A thicker resist layer can cause
problems like proximity eﬀects and an undercut.
The etch rate is not just dependent on the formerly mentioned factors but also of the Fermi level
(Haller et al. 1988). The Fermi level is altered by doping compared to the intrinsic Fermi level
value. Gap states have likewise an eﬀect on the Fermi level which is e.g. dependent of annealing
temperature, hydrogenation and dangling bonds present in silicon.

Characterization
A variety of instruments are used to make diﬀerent measurements of the sample parameter in

3.2.1
Spectroscopic Mueller Matrix ellipsometry
Ellipsometry is the ultimate instrumentation technique of choice in consequence to measure all
desired properties of the metasurface at once. Optical eﬀective parameters are most crucial for the
function of the metasurface. Optical parameters are described in the theory section (Section 2).
Since optical parameters are a function of the frequency, the determination of the permittivity is
made by making a spectroscopic measurement. By obtaining Ψ (Equation 34) and ∆(Equation 35)
from the p- and s-polarized light, the permittivity just as ﬁlm thicknesses, crystallinity, anisotropy
and surface roughness can be determined (Arwin 2016).
An ellipsometer can be operated in reﬂection and transmission mode. Figure 7 shows a schematic
of the operation in reﬂection mode. The incident angle relative to the sample surface can be chosen
freely. The polarization state generator (PSG) manipulates a source light beam to have a speciﬁc

Co., Inc.”. Using spectroscopic ellipsometry works essentially in the way of measuring Ψ and ∆

compared with the measurements. A ﬁtting algorithm can be used of e.g. ﬁnding the ﬁlm thickness
with smallest mean squared error (MSE). If the model is accurate then the MSE is small and in
consequence optical properties and other material parameters of the model can be determined and
assumed to match with reality.
Challenges are to calibrate the ellipsometer properly and making an accurate model. But not just
the model has to be true to reality but also ”over-ﬁtting” may not take place. Therefore suﬃcient
parameters have to be known. For instance elemental composition and their atomic fraction can
be determined through energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy and serve for the construction of an
eﬀective medium model. Cross-section scanning electron microscopy can serve for the determina-
tion of layer thickness. Atomic force microscopy can provide information about surface roughness.
Proper material models are essential for a sample containing multiple materials.
3.2.2
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) can give high resolution images of the surface structure which
is possible with conventional light microscopy at this scale since the diﬀraction limit prevents cap-
turing structures in the manufactured size suﬃciently. A higher resolution can be achieved by using
an electron beam with a smaller wavelength than an optical beam. The set-up of the electron tube
is similar to the EBL set-up. The electron beam is accelerated (0.2-30 keV) and focused on the
surface of the sample. Since electrons are interacting relative strongly with matter, the air inside
the chamber of the SEM is sucked out to generate a vacuum. The energetic electrons are scattered
due to the interaction with the sample atoms. They can be elastically scattered with an angle of
greater 45° and be detected by an so called backscattered electrons (BSE) detector. The electrons
can be also scatter inelastically and transfer their energy on another electron of the sample. If the
energy transfer is suﬃciently big, the bound electron can be accelerated/ejected out of the atom
and can be detected with a secondary electrons (SE) detector. Figure 8 shows a schematic of the

3.3
Electromagnetic Field Simulations
Simulation methods used in this project are outlined in this chapter. Anything done by the author
is executed in a Python frontend.
The design of metasurfaces fabricated in this project is based on complex electromagnetic ﬁeld
simulations using the commercial software ”COMSOL Multiphysics” which uses the ﬁnite element
method. Phase and transmission for nanopillars with diﬀerent geometrical dimensions at diﬀerent
frequencies are derived. This work is preliminary carried out by the supervisor Morten Kildemo.
In order to validate phase shift and transmission values just as for calculating far ﬁeld diﬀraction
orders other simulation methods are applied. These ﬁnding can be compared with measured dif-
fraction orders and Mueller Matrices.
A scalar theory of diﬀraction is used as outlined in Section 2.3.3 for calculating diﬀraction pat-
tern and a modal method is used for computing complex vectorized electromagnetic ﬁelds as it
can be done in COMSOL. Typical alternative methods are ﬁnite-diﬀerence time domain (FDTD)
simulations as the commercial software ”LUMERICAL” oﬀers or in open-source implementations
like ’fdtd from ﬂaport’ (which is rather for educational purpose since it is slow and should rather
be implemented in C++ than Python) and ’meep from NanoComp’ that is an eﬃcient and com-
plete FDTD simulator (Oskooi et al. 2010). An alternative to the ﬁnite element method used in
COMSOL is the Discontinuous Galerkin Finite Element method (DGFEM).
3.3.1
Scalar diﬀraction simulation
The angular spectrum method is implemented in Python an open-source available from a GitHub
respository (Fuente 2021). It is able to compute diﬀraction pattern numerically. For the purpose
in this project a phase mask and the corresponding amplitude is necessary to calculate a spatial
intensity distribution in a speciﬁc distance. It is also taken advantage of the Bluestein method for
electric far ﬁeld simulation and also enables to reconstruct a phase mask in a speciﬁc distance to
the initial one (Hu et al. 2020).
Limitations of this method are that diﬀerent polarizations have to be analysed separately.
3.3.2
Rigorous coupled-wave analysis
Rigorous coupled-wave analysis (RCWA) is the method mainly used by the author to compute a
phase and amplitude mask of metasurfaces. The approach of a modal method as RCWA is that
32
==PAGE:45==
layers of uniform relative permittivity proﬁle are distinguished. The uniformity of permittivity
exists along the propagation axis (z-axis). In the mode expansion technique transverse eigenmodes
are calculated for each of these layers (Lavrinenko et al. 2018). Thus this method works well for
periodic structures. S4 is an implementation of the RCWA method (V. Liu and Fan 2012). It is
available in a LUA and Python frontend while the latter one is used in this project.
Limitations of a Fourier modal method as S4 is the numerical truncation of the Fourier series.
The electromagnetic ﬁeld is expanded into a Floquet-Fourier series and because of computation
eﬀort has to be truncated. A value for the truncation has to be chosen at which results start to
converge. Suﬃcient computational power is necessary.

4.1.1.4
E-beam resist dose test:
As explained in Section 3.1.2, ﬁnding an optimum in dose
for an e-beam exposure of a resist is crucial. Dose means electric charge per area at which a resist
is exposed. A threshold dose has to be surpassed to make a pattern developable. A dose is thus
highly dependent on the resist that is used but also forward and backward scattering (proximity
eﬀects) are contributing to an optimal value. Proximity eﬀect correction (PEC) is used, which is
why the term ”base dose” is used since the actual dose time varies. Figure 15 shows an overview
of four diﬀerent base doses on a pattern that is similar to the desired metasurfaces. Too high
base doses causes pillars to topple (Figure 15d) while a too low base dose causes the pillars to
be underexposed (Figure 15a). It appears to be that an optimal base dose lays between 210 and
240 µC/cm2.
Based on the results from the coarse dose test (Figure 15), a ﬁner dose test is carried out. A light
microscope recording is shown in Figure 16. No pillars are toppling in the chosen range of base
dose, nor underexposed pillars can be detected in images with higher magniﬁcation. Thus a base
dose of 220 µC/cm2 is chosen.

Amorphous silicon (a-Si) thin ﬁlm deposition takes place through plasma
enhanced chemical vapour deposition (Section 3.1.1). Depending on the deposition method and
recipe used, the permittivity of the a-Si can vary. One recipe is used for all ﬁnal metasurfaces.
Throughout the project, also a ”low frequency” PECVD recipe is tested which is supposed to
reduce surface roughness. E-beam evaporation for silicon deposition is also tested.
The substrate should ideally be immediately deposited with a-Si, after cleaning and without leaving
the clean room environment. The PECVD recipe used is called ”(OPT) Amorphous Silicon
250C”. It is provided by the PECVD manufacturer ”Oxford Plasma Technology”. The recipe has
ten distinguishable steps while a ﬁnal nitrogen purge is three times repeated:
1. Nitrogen purge
2. Pump
3. Pump to 5 · 10−5 Torr
4. Pre process pump 30 s
5. SiH4/Ar (Amorphous Si)
6. Repeat 3
7. Nitrogen purge
8. Pump
9. Loop
10. Pump to 5 · 10−5 Torr

amorphous silicon is the result of the deposition process but hydrogenated amorphous silicon (a-

at a temperature of 250° C and its duration determines the thickness of the a-Si layer. A deposition
rate is determined in paragraph 4.1.1.1 but it has a signiﬁcant tolerance respectively dependence
of the chamber condition and substrate used. An applied deposition duration of 32 min and 19 s
results in 706.14 nm which menas a deviation of -1.2 % to the desired thickness of 715 nm a-Si
of all ﬁnal metasurfaces with this pillar height. This value is ellipsometrically determined with an

Far ﬁeld diﬀraction:
The far ﬁeld of an electromagnetic wave propagating through a metasurface is calculated with
both Bluestein and angular spectrum method.
Since these two methods are applied as scalar
theories of diﬀraction, ﬁelds are calculated separately for diﬀerent states of polarization. A phase
and amplitude mask are used to calculate diﬀraction intensities. Phase and amplitude masks of a
metasurface are calculated with S4 in the radiative near-ﬁeld. Therefore a distance of approximately
λ0/4 is chosen.
Far ﬁeld diﬀraction results are computed with the open source module ”DiﬀractSim” from Fuente
2021. Figure 41 shows the intensity distribution at 5 cm distance of the mainly discussed ms1
design. A diﬀraction angle of -6.82° is determined for ExHy polarization. Generalized Snell’s
law (Equation 59) predicts a diﬀraction angle of -6.74° at 0°incidence.
The diﬀraction angle for EyHx polarized light is found to be -0.16°while generalized Snell’s law
predicts 0°. Hence the metasurface is theoretically designed as beam splitter for orthogonal states
of polarization.
Figure 42 shows the diﬀraction intensity for the ms2 metasurface type with same design parameters.
This results for ExHy polarized light in a main diﬀraction angle of -9.34°but if the entire metasurface
is rotated by -45°, it yields into a diﬀraction angle of -6.73°while the EyHx polarization results in
a diﬀraction angle of -0.01°. This means ms2 fulﬁls theoretically also its purpose. Section C.2
documents the user speciﬁc code for these diﬀraction simulations.
The metasurface type ms3 generates right-circular and left-circular polarized light (RCP and LCP)
while one type of spin angular momentum is supposed to have a diﬀraction angle greater than 0°.
The far ﬁeld diﬀraction is not simulated for this type of metasurface.

Discussion
Ideally, results of diﬀerent simulation methods, including the one performed for generating the
initial design of the metasurfaces, could be compared with optical characterisation results of all
fabricated metasurfaces. These characterisation results are not yet available. What can be done
is assessing whether the desired design matches with measured dimensions.
A signiﬁcant discrepancy between AFM, SEM and EBL fabrication is noted. While AFM meas-
urements are in correspondence with ﬁle data for EBL exposure, SEM measurements reveals a
systematic deviation. Further investigations need to be carried out in order to explain the cause
for the discrepancy. E.g. cross-sectional SEM measurements of ﬁnalized metasurfaces is desired.
AFM just as SEM measurements are veriﬁed multiple times with diﬀerent samples. Finally it
is relied on the AFM measurements - not because these match with the desired dimensions but
because it is ”more” direct. It is emphasized that this is a scientiﬁcally inadequate statement.
Although it is assumed for simulations that there are not density deviations of the amorphous
silicon layer (Section 2.4.3), which has an eﬀect on the permittivity, yet a thickness non-uniformity
of 1.72 % can be determined (ellipsometrically). PECVD is the cause for that. Other assumptions
made here can just be veriﬁed after ﬁnalising the optical characterization.
In accordance with a philosophy of science after Karl Popper, scenarios should be suggested which
can falsify assumptions and theories that are applied respectively which demarcate limits of the
theories that are used. Limitations for the application of scalar diﬀraction theories are especially
realised in case of modelling the metasurface of type ms3. Moreover if orders of diﬀraction cannot
be detected at simulated angles then multiple causes must be investigated. These possible causes
include the measurement method, fabrication and simulations for design and diﬀraction orders.
The fabrication process can be speeded up through eﬀectively optimising process parameters more
accurately and rapidly.
Therefore a high number of reference samples are indispensable.
The
risk of machine failure always exists which can be bypassed through fast fabrication work, having
alternative methods and process approaches.
6
Conclusions
The work on samples for this project includes 264 h of clean room laboratory work. 24 diﬀerent
metasurfaces are successfully fabricated. Hence all occurring fabrication problems are resolved.
For instance what was causing the toppling of nanopillars during the beginning of the project?
Radu Malureanu states the hypothesis of a pillar dimensions dependent etch rate and how back-
ward scattering at the sample interface of ”etching ions” during an ICP-RIE process might has
contributed to a conical shape and consequently lead to toppling. In order to prevent such an
overetch and even if occurring how to prevent toppling of pillars, multiple measures are carried
out: (1) Enhanced adhesion through Piranha substrate cleaning, (2) Using of a hardmask which
should decrease the tendency for an undercut, (3) dose optimization.
Another issue was rather polygon like shaped nanopillars than elliptical ones. This issue was solved
through two measures: (1) Optimising fracturing during .con ﬁle generation in the software beamer
and (2) using at same time a lower beam current and pitch during EBL exposure. (2) contributed
to an enhanced resolution. At the same time, both measures together are decreasing the EBL
exposure time by a factor of three.
Conclusions about metasurface performance cannot be drawn since the optical characterisation
has yet to be carried out.
Nevertheless Mueller Matrix ellispometry played a signiﬁcant role during sample fabrication. It
provides relevant data for the permittivity for the sample design and is an instance of control
during the fabrication.

Further Work
Variable angle spectroscopic Mueller Matrix ellipsometry measurements of metasurfaces need to
be performed. Such an optical characterization allows to analysis the performance of metasurfaces
and help to improve the design and fabrication of future metasurfaces.
A simpliﬁed fabrication process is attempted along the project.
Metasurfaces were fabricated
within just ﬁve steps, which does not involve any etching. This approach can be further optim-
ised.
The idea of making substrate embedded nanopillars is inspired from this approach.
Advantages of substrate embedded metasurfaces are that toppling of nanopillars is impossible and
in general such metasurfaces inherit a great robustness against contamination and mechanical im-
pact. This makes them interesting for applications in exposed environments. Drawback is that a
substrate like fused silica has a higher permittivity than air which reduces the permittivity contrast
compared to the dielectric amorphous silicon nanopillars.
