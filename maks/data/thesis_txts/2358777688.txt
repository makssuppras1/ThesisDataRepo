Preface 
 
 
 
This study serves as the final thesis project concluding the Master’s (MSc) degree in Petroleum 
Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The project was conducted over a 5 
months period in spring, 2016 (24th October, 2016 – 24th March, 2017) at Center for Energy 
Resources Engineering (CERE) in Chemical and Biochemical Engineering department under the 
supervision of Associate Professor Kaj Thomsen. The conducted research work is mainly theoretical and 
is based on electrolytic based thermodynamic modelling simulations. This emphasis on theoretical 
understanding has led to the development of a fast and robust equation of state model which predicts 
the precipitation of salt pretty well. 
 
This thesis consists of two introductory chapters. Chapter 1 is a short description of the thesis and the 
main motivation to do this kind of a study. Chapter 2 is a detailed over view of why there is such 
an emphasis on the studyand covers a wide range of topics right from how scales are formed to how 
it can be avoided. Chapter 3 has all the basic concepts of involved in this thesis including the 
thermodynamics. Chapter 4 includes the model and methodology used for Ext. UNIQUAC model, the 
modifications and parameters that were introduced to get fast and accurate results. Chapter 5 is the 
discussion section. Herein results from the model are compared with the actual experimental results 
analyzing why they are different when they are.  
 
The explanations given and critical correlations found are summarized and concluded in Chapter 7.  
Chapter 8 includes future scope of further work that can be done. Contribution made by other researchers 
has been cited at required places and a complete list of references is provided at the end of this 
research study.

Every year Oil and Gas companies lose millions of dollars because of scaling that occurs in the 
production well at deep well reservoirs. The interaction of the formation water with the injection brine 
is the main reason for these scales to precipitate. Apart from these the inevitable change in ambient 
pressure and temperature causes scales to deposit inside the wells leading to blockages and finally 
significant reduction in the production of crude. There is an estimated 4 million bbls per year loss 
incurred by BP as stated in a study. Apart from this a lot of Oil and Gas companies invest huge 
sums of money to remove already deposited scale depositions from the pipes. 
Keeping this need of predicting the scale deposition in sight this study is an attempt to use the 
Ext. UNIQUAC model, proposed by Thomsen et al., 1999, to model the scale depositions caused by 
the presence of Potassium ion in the brine. Further various reasons for scale depositions and measures 
to remove and avoid them have also been studied.  
Two parameters have been added to Ext. UNIQUAC model proposed by Thomsen to accommodate 
the pressure dependency of mineral solubility. This improved model is used to predict scales deposition 
of Potassium ion systems –including Ba2+ and Sr2+ ions. A study for the sulphide ions (H2S (aq), 
HS- and S2-) systems is also done to calculate the interaction parameters between them and other 
metal ions which like produce scale depositions.

2 Introduction 
 
 
What is scaling? 
Scaling is a particulate fouling phenomena in which there is a solid deposition of an inorganic 
substance mainly because of change in equilibrium conditions. There is change in the solid liquid 
equilibrium (SLE) of the solvent and salt system which causes the latter to precipitate and form solid 
blockages in the conduits. These disturbances in the equilibrium can either be because of change in 
concentration of the salts in the flowing media or the change in the temperature or pressure of the 
ambient surroundings. The amount deposited may further be dependent on supersaturation, kinetics, 
solution pH and CO2 content. (A.V. Garcia et al., 2005) [1]  
 
Why is there a scale formation? 
As suggested above the scale formation is due to the shift in the Solid-Liquid equilibrium which 
results in the precipitation of the dissolved solids from the carrier liquid. In this write up the case of 
scale formation particularly in the oil and gas and geothermal power production industry has been 
considered. In both of these cases the major minerals depositing are the sulphates and carbonates of 
Calcium, Barium, Magnesium and Strontium. In geothermal cases there is a report of deposition of 
antimony and arsenic sulphides as well [2]. Deep Subsurface water gets enriched in these ions through 
altercation of sedimentary minerals. This gives rise to complex fluids rich in ions, few of those are 
already at saturation limit for certain mineral phases. The carbonate and calcite formation gets enriched 
in Ca2+ and Mg2+ ions while the water in sandstone reservoirs gets rich in Sr2+ and Ba2+.(M. Crabtree 
et al., Fighting Scale- Removal and Prevention, Oil Field Review, SLB Resources) [3]    
 
 In both the cases, namely, Oil and Gas and Geothermal power, there is an incorporation of brine 
as a duty liquid, in geothermal energy it is used to increase its boiling point and heat capacity of

water and in the oil and gas industry the brine lowers the surface tension of the oil present in the 
reservoir making it easier to extract. Both of these industries are concerned with working at great 
depths. At greater depths there is an increase in pressure and temperature. When this liquid comes to 
the surface from the oil reservoir or the heat reservoir, there is a considerable decrease in pressure 
and temperature which renders the equilibrium imbalanced and thus there is a precipitation of dissolved

In oil reservoirs there is already a presence of formation water which has a high concentration of 
salts in it. Mixing it with injection brine cause an ‘incompatibility’ in the ‘waters’ present. This conflicting 
interaction of waters lead to precipitation of dissolved solids. The change in temperature and pressure 
generally causes carbonate deposition while the water incompatibility causes the sulfate formation.  
In reservoir fluids the total dissolved solids can reach a value of 400,000 mg/L. The exact value 
is a bit difficult to calculate since the amount of dissolved solids changes with the geological time and 
ambient conditions which the fluid is subjected to (encountered minerals and mineral diagenesis of the 
place). If an injection fluid is introduced with certain salinity, it is obvious that there would be a rather 
considerable dependence on it, since the conflicting interaction is caused because of solids dissolved in 
the carrier fluid. Figure 2 is an elaborate depiction of Strontium sulfate solubility with temperature at 
higher pressures calculated using ext. UNIQUAC model while Figure 3 shows dependence of strontium 
sulfate solubility in sea water with different salinities at different temperatures.

Another substantial cause of scale formation is the solubility shift of carbonate minerals because of 
acidic gas dissolution like of CO2 and H2S. Carbonate solubility increase with the fluid acidity caused 
by these gases. When formation water is in contact with carbonate rock and these gases, its carbonate 
content naturally increases. When subsequently the pressure drops in the flow line the CO2 gas evolves 
from the water causing a decrease in the acidity, increase in the pH and hence the precipitation of 
carbonate salts.   
Even though the major driving force for scaling to occur is temperature and pressure change, pH 
shift or water incompatibility issue, many times the super saturated water do not always produce scales. 
To explain this it is necessary to scope inside the bulk of water and understand how scaling occurs 
(Figure 5). 
 
 
Figure 5. Heterogeneous and Homogeneous Nucleation [2]

The initial development of scales begins from clustered atoms, a process called homogeneous 
nucleation. The atoms start clustering when it is triggered by local fluctuation in ion concentration. These 
atom clusters further concentrate into seed crystals. Crystals subsequently grow by ions adsorbing onto 
imperfections on its surface. The energy of this mechanism is provided by the surface energy of the 
crystal itself. Thus there is a huge energy reduction in the surface energy once the crystal size reaches 
a threshold radius called the critical radius. Meaning, the large crystals continue to expand (favorably) 
while the smaller crystals tend to dissolve back into the solution [6]. Thus if given a large enough 
degree of super-saturation, formation of one seed crystal would catalyze the formation of subsequent 
crystals, scale in this case. 
 
Crystal growth also tends to commence on the fluid boundary surface, a process called heterogeneous 
nucleation [7]. These sites can be imperfection on the pipes surface, joints or seams in the tubing and 
pipelines. Imperfection on the surface enables the atoms to form clusters which subsequently form 
crystals. Local turbulence caused by these imperfections causes local concentration fluctuation that result 
in rapid growth of scales.  
 
 
Economic Effect: the numbers and effects of scaling 
Scaling has proven to be one of the most loss incurring phenomena in oil and gas industry. Scale 
formation is a very common problem in oil and gas industry among other industrial processes. Oil and 
Gas industries, specifically, loses billions of dollars every year during production. It poses an even 
bigger problem in the future developments where there was a need to use far more saline water for 
reservoirs with larger depths and higher temperatures in the North Sea (Dyer and Graham, 2002) [8]. 
For a scale layer to build up, the supersaturated formation water has to come in contact with the 
walls of the production equipment. The tendency for scale to be deposited, therefore, will be low, if 
the crude has a low water cut and if the water is finely dispersed in the oil. The rate of scale 
deposition is approximately proportional to the rate of free water production. Depending upon where the 
formation water becomes supersaturated, scale may be deposited in the flow line only, in both flow 
line and tubing or in some cases even in the perforations and in the formation near the wellbore. 
Oilfield scales costs are high due to intense oil and gas production decline, frequently pulling of 
downhole equipment for replacement, re-perforation of the producing intervals, re-drilling of plugged oil 
wells, stimulation of plugged oil-bearing formations, and other remedial workovers through production 
and injection wells. As scale deposits around the wellbore, the porous media of formation becomes 
plugged and may be rendered impermeable to any fluid. 
 Among the most onerous of all scaling problems is that of sulfate scales, particularly barium sulfate 
scale. This is a difficult scaling problem because of the low solubility of barium sulfate in most fluids 
and the commensurate low reactivity of most acids with barium sulfate scale. Deposition of barium

sulfate into a continuous scale surface on production tubular exposes very little surface area for treatment 
by chemicals, and therefore this scale is almost impossible to remove once it is deposited. The most 
popular approach to addressing the barium sulfate scale problem has been to retard or prevent the 
formation of this scale in the first place.Scale inhibitors that are periodically pumped down production 
wells and into the producing formation for short distances around the wellbore have been developed 
and are widely utilized(McElhiney et al., 2001)[9]. 
Scale deposition causes serious damage in utilization systems and reduces flow areas. Thus there 
is significant reduction in production of crude oil. For example, BP alone loses around 4 million bbls 
annually in the North Sea (Graham and Mackay, 2003)[10]. There have been incidents reported where 
there was a deposition large enough to stop the flow of the entire production line and lead to the 
closing of the well (Zhang et al., 2001)[11]. Scaling leads to a lot of safety issues as well, such as 
blockages of subsurface control valves and affects the integrity of Electrical Submersible Pumps and 
hydraulic actuators [12]. Inorganic scale deposition leads to major losses incurred by the oil companies 
not only because of the flow reduction but also huge maintenance and replacement cost involved due 
to faulty running downhole utilities. Though altering the nature of injection water and surface coatings 
seem convenient, they are not very economically viable options [13].  
The following is a compilation of problems the various oil fields around the globe have had. It 
should thus give a fair idea about how prevalent the problem of scale deposition is and how long it 
has been continuing to haunt the oil companies. 
There have been reports of scale deposition in the Oil Fields due to water flooding in Saudi Arabia, 
Algeria, Indonesia and Egypt (El-Morgan Oilfields), where calcium and strontium scale deposition has 
been found in surface and subsurface production equipment (El-Hattab, 1982) [14]. 
Bezemer and Bauer (1969) [15] pointed to the main difficulties encountered in the South Sumatran 
fields (Indonesia) because of the deposition of calcium carbonate scales which have been restricting 
flow through tubing and flow lines, wear and abrasion of plungers and liners, and stuck plungers or 
wellhead valves. So far, the only scale prevention methods applied have been routine acidizing and 
well pulling. Further it was estimated that some 50 percent of the total well pulling effort was directly 
attributable to scale deposition. 
Brown et al. (1991) [16] mentioned about the presence of barium sulfate scale,which was a major 
problem andwould occur readily in the wellbores of the Forties field when produced water rich in barium 
ions was mixed with sulphate rich sea. Barium sulfate scale was found in the topsides equipment, 
production header and water handling plant.It was also found downhole, deposited on the production 
tubing and liner resulting in reduced bore sizes and associated loss of production. 
Todd and Yuan (1992) [17] stated barium sulfate scale deposition was a severe production problem 
in North Sea oil operations. Barium sulfate was often accompanied by strontium sulfate to form a 
different kindof scale called (Ba, Sr) SO4 solid solution.  
Bayona (1993) [18] indicated two major problems with seawater injection in the North Uthmaniyah 
section of the Ghawar field in Saudi Arabia.Firstly is the maintenance of acceptable water quality to

Asghari and Kharrat (1995) [19] mentioned water injectivity loss in the Siri field in Iran from an

Paulo et al. (2001) [20] described Sulfate scale deposition is a common problem in the Alba field

Voloshin et al. (2003) [21] portrayed a summary of all the scale problems encountered in Western

fluids to surface. Moreover, scale was one of the main reasons for failure of ESPs, which were 
prevalently used in the West Siberian oil fields. Further investigation showed that carbonate deposit was 
the main culprit, along with mechanical impurities and Iron deposits that were found. In 2003, many 
thousands of wells got compromised by scale deposition in Western Siberian oilfields. 
 
Moghadasi et al. (2003) [22] mentioned scale formation in the Iranian oilfields has been recognized 
to be a major operational problem causing formation damage either at injection or producing wells. 
Scale contributes to equipment wear and corrosion and flow restrictions, thus resulting in a decrease in 
oil and gas production. 
Strachan et al. (2004) [23] reported barium sulfate scale was a major problem in the BP Magnus 
field, even at low water cuts (<1%). In the late 1990’s, BP Magnus adopted a policy of executing 
pre-emptive scale squeeze treatments on newly completed wells to prevent scale deposition and maintain 
well productivity on water breakthrough. 
Study on Veslefrikk field in the Norwegian part of the North Sea gives a good insight on how 
scales can be a hindrance to the oil production and its economic effects can be related to other more 
contemporary scenarios. Tjomsland et al. in 2001, [24] studied two scenarios for the Veslefrikk field, 1. 
“No scale control” scenario and 2. “no scale” scenario. According to the study, the company 
(STATOIL) would have produced 9 million Sm3 less oil if no scale controls had been implemented. 
That amounts to 1100 million USD of NPV. Had there been no scale formation involved there would 
have been further production of 400 million USD equivalent of crude oil could have been achieved. In 
this case the money invested in scale control operations which includes inhibitor squeezes, re-perforations 
and mechanical and chemical stimulations, was approximately 6.3 million USD.

Management of scale formation and methods of prevention  
Over the years, with the acknowledgement of scaling as a problem, there has been a considerable 
advancement in technology to tackle scaling problem. Companies invest millions of dollars in studies to 
understand scaling better. A few of the conventional and unconventional methods to curb the scaling 
problem has been discussed in this section. [26] 
 
1. Reduction of scale potential: Efficient scale management strategy is necessary in the long run of 
the field and has a direct impact on the production viability. Sea water with high Sulphur content 
(sulphate ions) is a convenient and low cost source for injection water. High content of Sulphur 
makes it an accomplice in a scale attack on the well. Thus removing or reducing the Sulphur 
content in the injection water is the best bet for the companies to avoid scale formation. This 
is achieved by Sulphate removal plant (SRP), which de-sulphates water quite considerably. 
Desulphation removes only the Sulphur content in the water but the other salts remain intact. 
SRP removes quite a significant amount of sulphate ion reducing the sulphate ion concentration 
from 2700 ppm to 40-100 ppm (Kelland, 2009) [25]. SRP is quite a costly investment but 
still is the best option for large fields with severe predicted sulphate scale formation. Aquifer 
water and produced water are other water types that can be injected to avoid the risk of 
sulphate scaling. The sulphate contents of both water is negligible (<20 mg/l) in terms of 
sulphate scale formation potential (Olagire, 2015) [26].  It is to be kept in mind that inspite of 
==PAGE:21==

all these steps taken there is still a major risk of carbonate scale formation in the well thus a 
planned well production strategy is another way to go. It involves careful production of the well 
toavoid large pressure drops, or by choking back wells that produce water which may lead to 
mixing of incompatible waters (Kelland, 2009; Ramstad et al. 2005) [25, 27]. 
 
2. Scale prevention methods: One of the methods to prevent scale formation is the squeeze 
treatment. In this method chemical scale inhibitors are injected into the producers near wellbore. 
Scale inhibition is actually a chemical treatment used to control, delay or reduce scale from 
forming in the producing well. It is an economical and effective method for prevention of scaling. 
Scale inhibitors essentially reduced the rate of scale formulation significantly rather than fully 
eliminating its formation, as the word prevention so clearly suggests. The best inhibitors are 
a)stable in the conditions imposed b)do not interact with other chemicals incorporated in the oil 
field and c)be able to inhibit the scale in question, irrespective of mechanisms operating. These 
operate under two mechanisms: one that prevents the crystals to stick onto the well walls and 
the one which itself adheres to nucleating crystals and thus preventing further growth of crystals. 
There are different types of crystals: 
 
 
Conventional Inhibitors: These are hydrophilic in nature and dissolve in water. In the 
case of downhole squeezing its desired that the scale inhibitor gets adsorbed on the 
rock to avoid washing out the chemical before doing what is required. The most common 
inhibitors are inorganic phosphates, organophosphorous compounds and organic polymers. 
PPCA (polyphosphono carboxylic acid) and DETPMP (diethylenetriaminepenta (methylene 
phosphono acid))(Olagire, 2015) [26]. 
 
 
Green Scale inhibitors: With growing environmental awareness there are an increasing 
severity of norms and regulations to use more and more environment friendly methods 
in all the industries. Oil and gas industries are not left behind in this regard either. The 
growing need for environment friendly chemicals thus comes from increasing emphasis 
and rules laid down by the government on the oil industries to use eco-friendly chemicals 
in oil and gas extraction. Thus a need for eco-friendly scale inhibitors has risen. Though 
not a lot of work has been done in this field, a few works looks promising. A good 
eco-friendly inhibitor would be less toxic, highly biodegradable and shows no 
bioaccumulation. As a result the use of non-biodegradable phosphates and organopolymers 
has been limited. A few studies suggest the use of dibutylphosphorodithoic acid and 
carboxyhydromethylphosphonic acids to reduce the toxicity of inorganic phosphorous 
based inhibitors. Newer studies propose natural compounds and carboxylated plant poly-
saccharides such as carboxylated anulins and aspartate which are among the “greenest” 
scale inhibitors (Gupta et al., 2004) [28]. Polyaspartate serves as an inhibitor for both

as well (Inches et al. 2007) [29].  
 
 
Naphthenate inhibition: Naphthenate inhibition is very crucial in oil production where the 
crude oil and the water properties are prone to either calcium naphthenate formation or 
carboxylate formation. Naphthenate deposition only occurs when the pH exceeds the pKa 
of naphthenic acid in the oil phase. Thus injection of acids keeps the pH low inhibits 
naphthenate formation. Acetic Acid is the most commonly used chemical inhibitor for 
naphthenates. Acid demulsifiers consisting of acetic acid in an aromatic solvent mixture 
and non-acid demusifiers consisting of ethoxylates and alcohol have shown great results 
in reducing naphthenate scale deposition. Ethoxylates are highly attracted to the o/w 
interface rather than to naphthenic acid. As a result it contests with the naphthenic acid 
to reach the interface and react with the cation. The use of phosphoric acids and 
phosphorous based products have shown potential over acetic acid in carboxylate emulsions 
but environmental laws restrict their usage in the procedure. 
 
3. Scale Removal: Scale removal techniques should be quick, non-damaging to the immediate 
environment and efficient at prevent re-precipitation. The best way to treat the scaling problem 
is to understand it, its physical composition and chemical properties. A poor choice of removal 
method can promote reoccurrence of scale. Scale may occur as single mineral phase or mixture 
of similar compatible compounds. Sulfates of Calcium and Strontium depositions on sandstone 
formations are difficult to remove chemically and almost impossible to remove mechanically. 
 
 
Chemical Techniques: Chemical scale removal is the first and the cheapest approach to 
remove the scale formation in a field. It is the most handy when scale deposits in 
places where conventional mechanical removal methods are ineffective or expensive to 
deploy. Hard sulfate scales are very resistant to mechanical abrasion and when they 
precipitate in places where it is difficult to reach manually, strong chelating agents are 
poured in. These compounds break the acid resistance by encapsulating the metallic ions 
of the scale within their ringed structures. 
The first and the most widely used stimulation practice in the oil industry is the acid 
treatment. When the production or the yield of oil decreases due to scale deposition 
acid treatment is incorporated. Different carbonate scales and iron sulfides and oxides 
are soluble in acids. Hydrochloric acid (HCl) is most commonly used for this purpose, 
but because of its corrosive nature an additional corrosion inhibitor must be added. 
Furthermore at places when the high temperature and pressure is concerned there are 
different additives used to control the rate of the reaction. This makes this method of 
scale removal very costly. In cases like these organic acids are used which are less

corrosive but slow to dissolve the carbonate scales. Newer techniques involve using a 
mixture of HCl and organic acids to balance the limitations of each. As much as the 
acid washout technique seems convenient and simple it is of utmost important that due 
care should be taken regarding the strength and the amount of acid being injected since 
an overtreatment can cause the pores to collapse and reduce the well productivity instead 
of increasing it. Scales are generally covered with hydrocarbon and acids find it difficult 
to come in contact with the scale. The right kind of surfactant is thus used to increase 
the acid scale contact and not form an acid oil emulsion. 
    As mentioned above the 2nd kind of chemical stimulants are chelating agents, which 
solvate the metal ions and transport it to the surface by flowing the well or drive it 
deeper into the formation by injecting into the well. The slower rate of dissolution of 
the scale allows the chelatant to more evenly dissolve the calcite and maneuver itself 
through more pathways rather than just following the first fluid entry zone. The only 
downside of using chelating agents is that they are very expensive but Schlumberger 
has developed an improved EDTA based scale dissolver, called U105, a cost-effective 
alternative to conventional EDTA (Ethylene-diamine tetra acetic acid) and 
HEIDA(Hydroxyethyliminodiacetic acid) based chelating agents [2]. 
 
 
Mechanical Techniques: Mechanical methods are quite easy to understand and have been 
used to remove the scales since the very beginning to remove different types of scales. 
Nonetheless they come with several limitations and should only be considered as a last 
resort for any scale removal program. Firstly, it is very expensive especially when dealing 
with a deep well. Second, it is not very effective in re-stimulating the well because it 
only removes the scale from the well bore and not from the formation. There are a 
wide array of mechanical tools and techniques which are incorporated in the well bore 
scale removal schemes. To mention a few: Impact hammer, positive displacement mill, 
spinning jetting tool, sonic tool, turbine powered jetting tool, scale blasting et cetera.  
 In the early days explosives were used to rattle pipe and remove scales but this 
somehow tend to be overpowering for the tubulars to handle. As technology developed 
controlled milling tools were introduced which were used to break the hard scales which 
could not be removed by chemical wash. The scales on the tubing wall tend to be 
unevenly deposited because of which the motor cannot be supplied with right amount of 
power to mill through the scale every time which stalls the process.

Ever since the water injection method is being used for oil extraction during Improved Oil recovery 
(IOR), the incompatible mixing of formation water and the water being injected is resulting in scale 
formation. Removing the precipitated scale in already running wells is cumbersome, time consuming and

quite expensive and sometimes becomes impossible leading to rejection of the whole well. Thus, it 
seems, (and it always is) prevention of the scale formation is better than treatment of reservoir. One 
of the most common and efficient way to achieve this is using a thermodynamic model for prediction 
of scale formation potential in oil fields. An appropriate thermodynamic model should determine the type 
of the precipitated scale salt and maximum amount of precipitant in the mixed injection and formation 
waters. In recent years many research have been focused on this area[30]. Vetter et al[31] presented 
a model for prediction of simultaneous precipitation of barium, strontium and calcium sulfates. In this 
model, they assumed barium sulfate as the most insoluble salt which precipitates first and strontium 
sulfate tends to precipitate afterwards and finally calcium sulfate precipitates in the brine. He used only 
the solubility Product and mass balance of the salts ions from experimental data base to calculate the 
solubility and did not use any thermodynamic functions like activity and free energies. Yuan and 
Todd[32]developed a predictive model for calculation of sulfate salt precipitation due to incompatible 
mixing and pressure and temperature changes for oil reservoirs. The Pitzer[33] activity coefficient model 
was used to consider the non-ideality of solution at reservoir conditions. Moreover, the simultaneous 
precipitation and scaling tendency of sulfate salts including barium, strontium and calcium sulfates was 
considered. Haarberg et al[34]came up with an equilibrium model provided for prediction of sulfate and 
carbonate salts formation at reservoir temperature and pressure. The initial brine composed of Ca2+, 
Sr2+, Ba2+, CO32−and SO42−where CO2 and common organic acid could dissolve in so that the aqueous 
solution is in equilibrium with oil and gas phases. Moreover, the model took in consideration the 
equilibrium precipitation reactions, species mass balance and electro neutrality of brines. He calculated 
the Gibbs energy of solution was calculated using UNIQUAC, Brønsted[35]–Guggenheim[36] and Debye–
Hückel[37] models as short range, medium range and long rang term, respectively. By minimizing the 
Gibbs energy of the solution, the equilibrium calculation of the solid–liquid was carried out. Then using 
the initial and equilibrium concentration of species, the precipitation amount in the solution was obtained. 
Yeboah et al[38] developed a predictive model that calculates the scale formation potential and distribution 
in oil fields using the thermodynamic and kinetic data. The input data for modeling is the composition 
of the produced water and thermo-chemical data for determination of scale thermodynamic potential. 
When scale formation was predicted by using software, the distribution of the salt along the produced 
well is calculated based on the kinetic and well data. Scaling index (SI) for precipitation of barium, 
strontium and calcium sulfate and calcium carbonate salts were performed by Oddo and Thomson[39].  
 
The proposed SI was a polynomial relation which was a function of temperature, pressure and ionic 
strength. For the calcium carbonate SI, CO2 concentration in brine was also included. Thomas et al[40] 
developed a professional model for prediction of scale precipitation and formation damage in oil fields 
during production and rock–fluid interactions. Moghadasi et al.[41] represented a model based on the 
experimental data and semi-empirical relations which could describe scale precipitation in one of the 
Iranian oil field during water injection for pressure maintenance. The model was based on the incompatible 
mixing in aquifer of reservoir and considered the effect of temperature, pressure and pH of solution.

Scope of this study 
With such humongous loss incurred with the oil and gas companies dealing with the omnipresent 
phenomenon of scaling, it is of great importance that the process of scaling is understood to its very 
roots. It is seen that the scope of improvement in the subject is huge not just to understand the 
chemistry involved between the ions responsible for scaling but also to learn the interaction of inhibitors 
with these ions. The whole idea of doing research in this topic is not just to find solutions to the 
problems but make it more efficient and sustainable at the same time. 
This thesis is an attempt to reach a level of understanding of the ion interactions which leads to 
scale precipitations. This work will be focused on finding thermodynamic parameters of a few ions that 
are, in some way or the other, involved in scaling. In this work, the scale predicting model developed 
by Garcia et al. will be incorporated which takes in account the interaction parameters calculated by 
Extended UNIQUAC model. 
Scaling is not always undesirable. Sometimes scaling at micro level assists in oil production and 
helps increase the recovery during EOR or secondary recovery methods. One such case is the proposed 
theory of fine formation (Chakrabarty et al., 2016) [44].  
Initially, in water based EOR techniques like Smart water flooding and Water Alternating Gas, the 
precipitation of brine salts was not believed to have a positive impact on oil and gas recovery or 
sweep efficiency of the injected gas and brine. But, proven mechanisms of ion substitution on calcite 
surfaces leading to brine alteration increases the possibility of solids formations known as fine formation. 
Thus in recent studies, fines formation has gained increased researcher’s attraction towards enhancing 
the oil and gas recovery. In a hypothesis by Chakravarty et al., 2016, [44] they have suggested that 
it is possible for insoluble fines to have a similar behaviors in chalk reservoirs as in sandstone 
reservoirs. Their studies have shown that fines formation during Smart-water flooding (SmW) and 
carbon dioxide low salinity water alternating gas injection (LSCO2WAG) plays an important role in 
increasing the oil and gas recovery in carbonate reservoirs (Chakravarty et al., 2015; Garg, 2016) 
[45, 46].

3.1 
Solid Liquid Equilibrium  
 
Solid liquid equilibrium is the core for a model predicting the scale deposition in a scenario. In oil 
and gas industry scale deposition causes losses in millions to an Oil company and thus predicting its 
occurrence is the need of the hour. Solid-Liquid equilibrium study is the root of this kind of calculations. 
Solid liquid equilibrium is also important in designing separation processes that utilize either preferential 
dissolution or crystallization as a route to purification of a species. A variety of phase behaviors are 
exhibited by the systems which comprise of a single or a mixture of solids in dissolved in liquids. This 
particular section would deal with the basic thermodynamics dealing with the Solid liquid equilibrium in 
an electrolyte solution system.  
 
The very first work on the electrolyte thermodynamics was done by Arrhenius who proposed that an 
electrolyte would dissociate into positive ions and negative ions in water. If this system was in interaction 
with an electrical field these ions will move in the direction of oppositely charged polarity. He further 
suggested that the degree of dissociation was dependent on the type and its concentration of the 
electrolyte in the solution. Ever since Arrhenius proposed his theory there has been numerous studies 
on the aqueous electrolytic thermodynamics.

Solubility Product and saturation index. 
 
The thermodynamics of electrolyte solutions are further complicated by the fact that ionic reactions 
occur in the aqueous phase. Ions can react and be in one or more solid phases. Vast amounts of 
solid-liquid equilibrium data for many salts at varying temperatures exist in the literature. This section 
deals with how solubility product is connected with other thermodynamic relations. 
 
Solubility product:  
 
The solubility product of a salt solution is defined as the product of the activity of the ions it 
dissociates into and is denoted by Ksp. The value of the Ksp indicates the maximum concentration that 
can be achieved by the ions to remain dissolved in the solvent. 
  
If the product of the concentration of ions exceeds the Ksp then the solution is said to be super 
saturated and if it is below the value of Ksp it is unsaturated and remains dissolved in the solution 
otherwise it precipitates.

4 Model and Methodology 
 
 
 
 
 
Scale formation in wells is a very frequently occurring problem for Oil and Gas Industry while 
producing oil from reservoirs. As discussed previously the cause and the catalyst for its formation can 
be many like the degree of super saturation, pH of the solution, CO2 content, composition of formation 
and injection brine, reservoir conditions and kinetics of rock dissolution. As evident these scale formations 
have largely been responsible for major economic and efficiency loss not just in Oil and Gas industry 
but also in the geothermal industry. It has also been considered as a major cause of safety issues by 
blockage and failure of valves. 
Many mathematical models have been developed to understand these behaviors. The majority of the 
programs (Duan et al., 1996; Møller et al., 1998) [51] designed to predict scale tendency uses the 
framework provided by Pitzer (1991) [52]. For multi-component solutions, Pitzer’s approach requires 
both binary and ternary parameters. This requires both binary and ternary experimental data for all the 
species in the mixture. Such data are not available for some systems of importance in natural waters 
and are particularly scarce at the high temperature and pressure conditions like in Oil and Gas industry. 
As seen previously there are several models that attempt to predict this undesirable interaction between 
the ions that result in such a loss in money and pose danger to life. 
Most models are able to predict scale formation pretty accurately but are limited by the experimental 
data availability for different ions or for ions at different extreme conditions. So, a method requiring a 
limited amount of experimental data but leading to accurate predictions for the solid–liquid–vapour 
equilibrium and precipitation of electrolyte solutions is of great use. One such model is the Extended 
UNIQUAC model (Thomsen and Rasmussen, 1999) [53] which is the thermodynamic model incorporated 
in prediction of solubility in this study.

Equilibrium calculations 
Equilibrium calculations are required to determine if the solution forms a homogeneous phase and 
also fines formation prediction. The condition for equilibrium between two phases is that the chemical 
potential is identical in the two phases. To perform these calculations initially, speciation calculation is 
required i.e. degree of dissociation of different electrolytes. The speciation equilibrium can be formulated 
as a mathematical equation in terms of activity coefficients, concentrations, and standard chemical 
potentials. It can be performed alone or in combination with the solid-liquid, vapour-liquid and liquid-
liquid equilibrium calculations. As speciation calculation has been done, it should be checked if the 
chemical potential of the solid phase is less than its components. In this case, the solution will not be 
stable leading to precipitation of one or more solids. Similarly bubble point calculations can be performed 
to determine if the gases will evaporate or not i.e. vapour-liquid equilibrium calculations.

5 Results and Discussion 
 
This work mainly deals with the systems containing potassium ion. The reason behind this is the 
abundance of potassium metal ion salt that is present in the formation water or sea water which is 
used for water flooding. This whole work is centered to finding the concentration or how the presence 
of potassium ion affects the deposition of some commonly precipitating salts. Sometimes the data present 
in the literature is probable to have errors in the determination and have huge variations in many 
cases. As mentioned earlier reliable solubility data covering wide range of thermodynamic conditions is 
needed to accurately determine scale deposition or else the prediction cannot be totally reliable and 
might over estimate or even fail to show any deposition. 
 
Software 
A computer software, ESTIM is used for the estimation of parameters of extended UNIQUAC was 
developed by K.Thomsen and CERE dept. at Technical University of Denmark. The software includes 
implementation of Marquardt method for non-linear least square minimization from the Harwell subroutine 
library (Fletcher, 1971). The Nelder-Mead simplex search method as described by Fredenslund et al., 
1977 [63], was tooimplemented in the program as a supplement to the gradient based Marquardt 
method. Starting with the Marquardt and multiple runs of Nelder Mead iterations was found to be 
yielding good results. 
ESTIM calculates the osmotic coefficient, mean molal activity coefficient, degree of dissociation, apparent 
relative molal enthalpy, heat of dilution, differential heat of dilution, integral heat of solution, apparent 
molal heat capacity, heat capacity and solid liquid equilibrium of an electrolyte solution, based on the 
Extended UNIQUAC model. This software includes a number of options to be specified by the user in 
an input file: 
 
Identification of the parameters to be estimated 
 
The number of iterations performed in each of the Marquardt and Nelder-Mead iterations. 
==PAGE:55==
 
 
DTU | Modeling the solubility of Exotic Scales 
45 
 
 
A starting molality and step molality, tells the program only to include data with a concentration 
less than the starting molality in the estimation procedure. When the estimation is finished, 
the program increases the maximum molality by the step molality and starts a new parameter 
estimation with the parameters already obtained from the mole dilute solution as a starting 
guess. This continues till all data are included. 
 
A number of data points to dismiss, tells the program to perform a parameter estimation and 
dismiss the experimental data point that is responsible for the largest contribution to the 
objective function. The parameter estimation continues within the parameters obtained for the 
starting guess. This option tells the program how many times to repeat this pattern. 
 
Option to not perform optimization but just calculate the squared residuals and show the 
result in a file. This option makes it easier to identify a type of data or a certain data set 
with particular large deviations. 
When a run finishes, ESTIM creates a file with the estimated parameters, which is again used as 
an input file for the next run. The file created can be modified in case a new initial value to a 
parameter is needed to be given. 
Post getting parameters on ESTIM, the parameters are fed into another file which calculates the 
SLE curve and shows the solubility data for the required salt. This is an in house software from CERE, 
DTU called the SCALES which has been embedded into Microsoft EXCEL. This was used to get a 
graphic interpretation about the phases involved in a salts precipitation and also the thermodynamic 
properties involved.

As seen from the solubility curve Ext. UNIQUAC model calculations are in decent match with 
the experimental values. One particular data point by Grower et al. shows a large deviation from 
the trend and thus will not be considered as a reliable source of data. Another aspect of the plot 
shown in the figure is lower estimation of the model at higher concentrations of KCl in the solution. 
In the work by Davis et al., 1971, though process very well scrutinized does not take into 
consideration that there are other salts, all of them majorly strong electrolytes, present in the 
solution in an attempt to mimic the formation water composition. The main aim of the study was 
to check the inter relation of barite solubility with the ionic strength of the solution. The strong 
electrolytes tend to dissociate fully leading to a higher concentration of Cl- ions and also other 
metal ions in the solution. This may have led to the unintentional error in comparison of the two 
curves. The best way would have been possible if the concentration of other salts in the solution 
would have been given as well so as to help the model to calculate the interaction parameters 
between the potassium ion with barium as well as other metal ions in the solution.  
Khitarov’s group conducted a study to check the temperature dependence of Barite solubility in KCl 
solution. The ext. UNIQUAC model shows a good fit to the experimental data and shows an exact 
trend of first increase then a decrease in the solubility post 1800C. Khitarov did lots of experiment 
to study the solubility of barites in different chloride solutions. One of them was a 0.25M KCl 
solution which infact showed a trend and values very similar to its sodium counterpart. Khitarov et 
al. have done the experiment over the temperature range which is way more than our concern. 
Thus only 4 data points has been chosen for the validation.

solvent. Whereas the 400C curve shows the maximum deviation in the middle of the concentration 
range. Overall the trend follows that of barium carbonate in NaCl solution (Millero et al., 1984) 
[69], first increase and then decrease of solubility as the concentration of KCl increases. It is 
believed that it is the right trend because of the similarity between the ions of the salts, K+ and 
Na+.

6 Conclusion and future work 
 
 
 
 
 
The Extended UNIQUAC model presented by Thomsen and Rasmussen (1999) has been used to 
calculate solid-liquid equilibria in systems found to be detrimental to oil and gas production. The sulfates 
and carbonates of Barium and Strontium are commonly occurring scale minerals and their interaction 
with KCl is necessary since it is largely available in formation brine or injection water. Through this 
work one would not say that perfect interaction parameters for Potassium with strontium and barium 
have been found, since that would require huge amounts of experimental data and impeccable accuracy 
and consistency within them, but the present work comes close to predicting the concentrations of 
potassium ion, agreeably, in the solution that would limit the occurrence of scaling because of Barium 
or Strontium or favor it.  
Since the parameters of this scaling model (sulfates and carbonates of Barium and Strontium) in 
presence of NaCl has been determined in the previous work without considering the presence of KCl 
in the system, the addition of potassium ion comes as an add on to the already existing set of 
interaction parameters and not as an equal participant of the set for which the parameters are developed 
from scratch. Thus the tolerance of this potassium ion in this model is not as high as it is for the 
ions of already existing set. In order to get a more exact and precise estimation of interaction parameters 
of potassium one needs to be able to get a lot more data so as to be sure that the values regress 
and a better estimate is provided. In the case of VLE H2S in H2O where we had a plethora of data 
available the regression was successful and a good estimate for the interaction parameters as well as 
the volume parameters of each of the H2S(aq), HS- and S2- were obtained. However in the case 
where there was limited availability of data as in the case of K2S the regression failed to a sensible 
smooth curve. This was because of the wrong estimation of the sulfide parameter. The examples in 
this work and previous studies (Garcia et al., 2005) suggest that though there is a need to refine
