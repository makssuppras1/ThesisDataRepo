Abstract
In cycling, every second matters, and knowing exactly where the drag forces are gener­
ated is crucial for improving the geometries to take advantage of aerodynamics. Hence,
this thesis is dedicated to fully understanding the transition from laminar to turbulent flow
or boundaly layer detachment around the helmet and identifying the parameters that influ­
ence this transition, with a primary focus on the turbulence parameters of the inlet airflow.
To achieve the goal, a 3D CFD study will be conducted using Star CCM+ software, em­
ploying both the k­ω and the Gamma­ReTheta transition models. The simulations will be
carried out at typical velocities (54 km/h). Additionally, the study will investigate the im­
pact of the relative velocity of the wheels on the cyclist and whether this affects the flow
transition.
The helmet used in the simulations is also studied in the wind tunnel with a pressure
probe to determine, through the frequencies, where the transition takes place. This will
corroborate the results from the CFD simulations.
The simulations showed that the most important parameters are the turbulence intensity
and the simulation model. Depending on the simulation model, a difference of almost
6.2% in drag force is observed between the transition model and the non­transition model.
Additionally, a difference of almost 11% in simulation profiles is observed depending on
the turbulence intensity when it changes from 0.05% to 2%.
It is also observed that changing length scales within a fixed turbulence intensity does not
have as much influence as though in the beginning, neither the changes introduced by
the relative wheel speed.

Bicycle aerodynamics ­ history
Aerodynamics, derived from the Ancient Greek words αηρ (aero, meaning air) and δυναµκη
(dynamics), encompasses the examination of air movement, particularly when influenced
by solid objects like an airplane wing [1]. As a vital component of aeronautics, this area
unifies themes from fluid dynamics and its subset, gas dynamics. Although ”gas dynam­
ics” and ”aerodynamics” are sometimes used synonymously, the latter term covers the
movement of all gases, not simply air. Although key findings, including aerodynamic
drag, were made much earlier, the scientific study of aerodynamics in the modern sense
started in the eighteenth century. Otto Lilienthal achieved heavier­than­air flight in 1891,
marking the culmination of early aerodynamics efforts. Aerodynamics has been applied
in a variety of ways over time, including computer simulations, wind tunnel tests, empir­
ical approximations, and mathematical analysis. This has created a sound basis for the
development of heavier­than­air flight, as well as other technical advancements. Contem­
porary aerodynamic research emphasizes compressible flow, turbulence, and boundary
layers, increasingly utilizing computational methods [2].
The prevalent design for racing bicycles in contemporary times features a diamond­shaped
frame, a configuration first introduced by Humber in 1890. This enduring frame style, ex­
emplified in iconic world hour record bicycles ridden by Merckx in 1972, Moser in 1984,
and Campenaerts in 2019, has maintained its dominance for over a century. However,
past attempts to improve bicycle aerodynamics have led to significant modifications, de­
spite the diamond­shaped frame’s enduring appeal.
In the late 19th century and more recently, notable innovations emerged, challenging the
conventional design. The recumbent bicycle, first introduced by Charles Challand in 1895
but only widely recognized in 1932, introduced a laid­back position with the crank posi­
tioned in front of the cyclist. Francis Faure’s use of the recumbent bicycle in 1933 demon­
strated a significant aerodynamic advantage, breaking records until 1934 when new reg­
ulations from the Union Cycliste Internationale (UCI) excluded it from official recognition.
The streamlined enclosures, such as Bunau­Varilla’s Velo Torpille in 1913 and subse­
quent designs by Oscar Egg, Marcel Berthet, and Georges Mochet, aimed to improve
aerodynamics. However, by 1914, UCI restrictions prohibited the use of any external
aerodynamic devices.
These innovative designs, though not officially recognized, demonstrated the potential for
groundbreaking records, challenging the conventions of diamond­shaped frames. The
recumbent and streamlined bicycles offered glimpses into a future where unconventional
designs could redefine the boundaries of aerodynamic efficiency in competitive cycling.
Despite regulatory challenges, these historical endeavors continue to inspire discussions
and explorations into alternative bicycle configurations for optimal performance and effi­
ciency. [3]

Lately, one of the earliest type of calculations resembling modern CFD are those by Lewis
Fry Richardson, in the sense that these calculations used finite differences and divided the
physical space in cells. Although they failed dramatically, these calculations, together with
Richardson’s book Weather Prediction by Numerical Process, set the basis for modern
CFD and numerical meteorology. Computational fluid dynamics (from now on, CFD) is a
branch of fluid mechanics that uses numerical analysis and data structures to analyze and
solve problems that involve fluid flows. Computers are used to perform the calculations
required to simulate the free­stream flow of the fluid, and the interaction of the fluid (liquids
and gases) with surfaces defined by boundary conditions
1.1
State of art
In the ambit of CFD analysis for cyclist sport, numerous papers and studies have been
published in recent years, presenting significant results. This section summarizes the key
findings and advancements in this field.
1.1.1
Influence of Rotating Wheels
One area of focus is the effect of wheel rotation in the reference frame of the bicycle.
Studies have examined how the rotation of the wheels and their interaction with the road
impact the aerodynamic properties of the bicycle­rider system. Key findings indicate that
the drag coefficient value is reduced by approximately 1 to 1.8% due to the rotational
effect of the wheels. [4]
1.1.2
Simulation Profile and Accuracy Parameters
Another critical aspect of CFD analysis in this context is the definition of the simulation
profile and the parameters influencing the accuracy of the results. Essential parameters
include the mesh geometry, the wall y+ coefficient, and the mathematical models used
for the simulation. According to one study [5], for accurate simulations, the wall y+ value
should be below 1. Furthermore, the study highlights that the SAS (Scale­Adaptive Simu­
lation) and RANS T­SST (Reynolds­Averaged Navier­Stokes Transition SST) models are
among the most reliable for these simulations.
1.1.3
Influence of Yaw Angle of the Airflow
Some studies also focus on the influence of the yaw angle of the airflow. These studies
declare that at low yaw angles, less than 15º, the upper body of the cyclist predominantly
affects the aerodynamic forces. However, at the specific yaw angle of 15º, complex vor­
tices appear due to the flow separation at the hips, helmet, upper body, and bicycle, at a
height of 0.6H, where H is the total height of the ”wind tunnel”. [6]

1.1.4
Turbulence intensity and length scale influence
Defining turbulence intensity and eddy length scale at the intake is crucial because changes
in these parameters can significantly impact the transition zone and the drag forces expe­
rienced by the rider. Several studies have been conducted on a track and in a wind tunnel
to measure turbulence parameters and assess their effects on riders, whether they are
riding solo or in a group. The authors report that a solo rider can experience turbulence
intensities of 1.5% [7]. Another study indicates that a solo rider can encounter turbulence
intensities around 1% based on six­lap average measurements [8]. This latter study also
notes that although the length scale was not controlled during the experiment, it was mon­
itored, with values ranging from 0.11 m to 0.41 m. Moreover, a study by I.V. Morenko and
V.L. Fedyaev examined the influence of turbulence intensity on the drag and lift of a cir­
cular cylinder. They observed that the changes in drag were not linear with the changes
in turbulence intensity. In fact, the graphs resemble a parabola, and between 6% and 9%
turbulence intensity, the drag coefficient reaches its minimum. [9]
These findings underscore the importance of carefully considering both the physical phe­
nomena, such as wheel rotation and yaw angle, and the technical parameters, such as
mesh and model selection, in CFD analyses for optimizing the aerodynamic performance
of cyclists.
1.2
The present study
The primary aim of this project is to study the transition from laminar to turbulent flow be­
havior around the helmet of a cyclist. Understanding this transition is crucial for improving
the aerodynamic performance and overall efficiency of competitive cyclists. If transition
can be fully understood, one can adapt or modify the shape of the helmet to take advan­
tage of it and reduce the drag forces experienced for the rider. The specific objectives of
the project are outlined as follows:
1.2.1
Study of Flow Transition
The project will comprehensively investigate the transition from laminar to turbulent flow
around the cyclist’s helmet. This includes:
• Characterizing the Flow Regimes: Identifying and characterizing the different flow
regimes (laminar, transitional, and turbulent) around the helmet at various conditions
of intermittency and length scale of the eddies.
• Flow Visualization: Using advanced flow visualization techniques and computa­
tional fluid dynamics, such as real­time analysis, quantitative data and detailed in­
sight, to observe and analyze the flow patterns around the helmet.
1.2.2
Influence Factors on Flow Transition
This study will also explore the factors influencing the transition from laminar to turbulent
flow. These factors include:
• Helmet Design: Analyzing how the helmet design and surface features affect the
transition point and the flow characteristics.
• Environmental Conditions: Investigating how external conditions such as length
scales and atmospheric turbulence influence the flow transition.

Aerodynamics ­ Movement through air
Aerodynamics is the branch of mechanics that studies the movement of gases around
objects and their behavior when moving through the air. Before delving into the technical
concepts of aerodynamics, it is important to understand the behavior of air particles or
any other fluid in motion.
The atmosphere is a uniform mixture of gases with fluid­like properties subject to the laws
of fluid motion. These fluids move through a field and can exist in both liquid form (such
as water) and gaseous form (such as air). They are easily influenced by changes in static
pressure, density, temperature, and the velocity at which the fluid moves. When these
properties remain constant at any point in the fluid for a period of time, the airflow, or air
current, is considered uniform.
While the speed and direction of airflow particles may vary from one point to another
within the fluid, when focusing on a specific point, the speed of each individual particle
will always be the same. The path these particles take is called a streamline, and when
the flow is uniform, streamlines do not intersect each other.
Figure 2.1: Streamline of a particle in three dimensional space
A set of adjacent streamlines creates a tube effect of streamlines, almost like a tube with
solid walls. In constant airflow, the tube acts as a closed system, where both mass and
total energy remain constant. We can observe this by considering the operation of a
garden hose. Water moves along a tube, with particles displacing others until they exit.
This tube is referred to as a stream tube, and the literal translation is a tube of streamlines.
2.1
Continuity equation
Let’s divide the stream tube with two planes perpendicular to the airflow: the first section
A1 and the second section A2 (fig: 2.2). The mass of air passing through the tube can
be obtained by multiplying the surface area of the perpendicular section at that moment
by the velocity at that same instant. Since the mass remains constant at any point in the
tube, it can be inferred that the surface area and velocity are inversely proportional.
Mass = ρ · A · v
(2.1)
Where ρ is the density of the fluid, A is the transversal section and v is the flux velocity for
a given moment.

2.2
Reynold’s Number
Non­dimensionalization is a widely applied concept in various scientific and engineering
disciplines. Its primary purpose is to reduce the number of variables, facilitating mean­
ingful correlations of physical phenomena in scalable systems. One prominent example
is the Reynolds number (Re), which quantifies the ratio of inertial force to viscous force
in a flow. Flows can range from laminar, at low Reynolds numbers, to fully turbulent, at
high Reynolds numbers. The Reynolds number is described as:
Re = InertialForce
V iscousForce = ρV L
µ
(2.2)
In which ρ is the fluid density, V characterizes velocity defining the flow, L is a character­
izing length in the flow system, and µ is the fluid molecular viscosity.
The Reynolds number at which the flow transitions from laminar to turbulent is called the
critical Reynolds number, and it is affected by several factors, such as smoothness of the
flow surface and disturbance in the surroundings. Its value is not universal. For internal
flows with a smooth surface, it has a value of approximately 2,300, and for external flows
around an obstacle, it can be as high as 20,000. [10]
Why is this non­dimensional number important in CFD? Reynolds numbers are instru­
mental in interpreting computational fluid dynamics solutions. Turbulent and laminar flows
exhibit distinct characteristics that impact flow separation, stagnation behavior, as well as
drag and pressure drop attributes. Understanding the global Reynolds number proves
beneficial in analyzing various flow features and behaviors.
The applications of Reynolds numbers extend to fluid mechanics, particularly in calculat­
ing friction factors and drag for both internal and external flows. It holds significance in
hydraulic studies, airfoil design, wind tunnel testing, and climate science.
2.3
Boundary layer law
The boundary layer of a fluid is the region where its movement is disturbed by the presence
of a solid body in contact with it. The velocity of the fluid relative to the body in this zone
can vary between 0 and 99% of the undisturbed flow. Above the boundary layer, the
velocity of the flow is the same as it has in the infinity, as shown in the figure 2.3.

Figure 2.3: Basic boundary layer theory
Imagine a moving object; the air in contact with this object will adopt the same velocity as
the object. However, as it moves away from the object, this velocity gradually decreases
until it reaches 0, this phenomena creates a velocity gradient. This is due to the viscosity
of the fluid, which induces friction.
If we focus on a surface as a reference point, the airflow travels along the surface. How­
ever, due to viscosity, the velocity of the air in contact with the surface is completely 0,
and as it moves away from the object the velocity increases, creating a velocity gradient
(seen in 2.3). This increase in velocity reaches the speed of the object. This zone where
the air velocity increases from 0 to the object’s velocity is the boundary layer, as seen in
the preceding figure.
There are two types of boundary layers: turbulent and laminar. The laminar zone is iden­
tified when the air hits the object, right at the beginning. As it passes through the object,
it reaches the transition zone that begins to disturb the streamlines until entering the tur­
bulent zone, producing turbulence. The shape of the turbulence consists of completely
irregular streamlines without any guidance.
These turbulences are formed due to the velocity gradient.
Initially, we have a low­
pressure zone and the gradient will increases in size as it moves along the solid. This
causes the point where the velocity is 0 to also move away from the surface. When this
point separates, the wind will seek a way to find the zone of lower pressure. It is in this
zone that the wind adopts a negative velocity in the opposite direction; this is what is
referred to as the detachment of the boundary layer.
2.4
Flow Regimes
Flow regimes are distinct phases of fluid behavior that correspond to different sets of
boundary conditions. Typically, three primary flow regimes are recognized: steady state,
pseudosteady state, and transient state. Each of these regimes is characterized by spe­
cific conditions and behaviors exhibited by the fluid. The determination of the flow regime
relies on the imposed boundary conditions. One crucial parameter for identifying these
regimes is the rate of change in pressure with time. By analyzing this rate, engineers and
scientists can categorize the flow behavior and apply appropriate models and analyzes
depending on the specific regime encountered. Understanding flow regimes is essential
for accurately predicting and designing systems in fluid mechanics and related fields.
The steady­state flow regime characterizes a system in which the mass flow rate remains
constant at all points within the system, and the pressure exhibits no temporal variation
6
Helmets Aerodynamics
==PAGE:17==
(dP/dt = 0). In this regime, the fluid properties, such as velocity, temperature, and density,
reach a state of equilibrium, and their values do not change over time. This implies that
the system has attained a stable and unchanging condition, with no fluctuations in flow
parameters.
A system where the average pressure experience temporal variations is known as the
pseudosteady state regime. The system behaves as a closed system in this regime be­
cause the pressure changes at a constant rate (dP/dt = cte). Importantly, during pseu­
dosteady state conditions, there is an assumption that there is no fluid movement across
system boundaries. In a pseudosteady state, the fluid system exhibits a degree of equilib­
rium over shorter durations, and the variations in pressure are considered gradual enough
to warrant the quasi­steady assumption. This approximation proves valuable in scenarios
where the system is subject to rapid or periodic changes, offering a compromise between
the complexity of transient analyses and the simplicity of steady­state models.
The transient state, representing the final flow regime, is characterized by pressure changes
as a function of time (dP/dt = f(t)). Unlike the steady­state and pseudosteady state regimes,
the transient state acknowledges dynamic variations in pressure, emphasizing that the
pressure is not constant but rather evolves over time. Importantly, in this regime, there
are no restrictions on fluid movement, and the system is free to experience changes in
flow patterns. This regime is commonly encountered during transient events, such as the
startup or shutdown of a system, sudden changes in boundary conditions, or other dy­
namic scenarios where the fluid system is in the process of transitioning from one state
to another. [11]

Aerodynamics forces ­ Drag
In terms of the forces acting on the body, it is irrelevant whether the motion is caused by
the fluid, the body itself, or a combination of both. In fact, the relative velocity between the
body and the fluid will partially determine the magnitude of the resulting forces. This allows
for the study of the forces a moving object will encounter in a wind tunnel by creating an
airflow with a velocity matching the actual object­wind relative velocity.
Therefore, even though resistance is often discussed (when the object moves against the
fluid) or drag (when the fluid moves towards the object), the calculation of forces and their
magnitudes is entirely equivalent. For a long time, it was believed that the resistance to the
motion of a body in a fluid was solely due to its viscosity. However, air is a practically ideal
fluid (with almost negligible viscosity), and it is well known to create significant resistance
as the velocity of a body moving through it increases.
It was Prandtl in 1904 who, with the boundary layer theory, managed to provide an ex­
planation for this phenomenon and laid the foundations for its physical characterization.
The causes of the forces experienced by a body in a fluid can be classified into two types
based on their origin:
Frictional Resistance: This results from tangential stresses of viscous origin and therefore
occurs in areas within the boundary layer where these stresses are significant. In a flow
with a low Reynolds number (laminar), it is the primary cause of resistance to motion.
Simply recalling the definition of viscosity makes it easy to establish that its magnitude is
a consequence of viscosity value, velocity gradient, and the contact surface area between
the body and fluid.
Pressure Resistance: Its origin is entirely different from frictional resistance. It arises from
the pressure difference between the body’s leading surface (the first to come into contact

with fluid particles) and the region in the wake of the body. The resulting force opposes
the motion of the body (resistance) or drags it with the flow.
Since pressure resistance depends significantly on the separation of the boundary layer,
greater adherence of this layer to the body represents less resistance to motion. The
boundary layer is more resistant to separation as long as the flow is more turbulent.
Any body submerged in a fluid stream undergoes a series of forces and moments that
depend on the relative motion with respect to the fluid and its geometry. An asymmetric
object will experience forces in all three directions of space. To establish common ter­
minology, the reference direction is typically taken as the one following the undisturbed
fluid flow, with a positive direction downstream (always considering the fluid­body relative
motion; thus, if the fluid is at rest, and the motion corresponds to the body, the positive
direction will be against that movement).
The force opposing the forward motion of the body in the fluid (and therefore, according to
the positive direction of the recently defined axis) is called resistance or drag. The force
perpendicular to the resistance in the opposite direction to gravity’s acceleration is called
lift and can be negative (subtracting from the weight of the object). Forces perpendicular
to the two mentioned above are called lateral forces.

2.6.1
Wall Shear Stress
Wall shear stress refers to the tangential force per unit area exerted by a fluid on a solid
surface, typically a wall, in contact with the fluid. It represents the frictional force experi­
enced by the fluid molecules near the wall as they slide past the stationary surface. This
frictional interaction arises due to the viscosity of the fluid, which resists the relative motion
between the fluid and the wall.
In many engineering applications, wall shear stress plays a crucial role. For example,
in fluid flow through pipes, it determines the energy losses due to friction along the pipe
walls, influencing the efficiency of pumping systems. In boundary layer flows over sur­
faces, such as air flowing over an aircraft wing or water flowing over a ship hull, wall shear
stress affects the development of the boundary layer and the overall aerodynamic or hy­
drodynamic performance. Additionally, in heat transfer processes involving fluid flow,
such as convective heat transfer in a pipe or over a surface, wall shear stress affects
the rate of heat transfer by influencing the fluid flow patterns and the thermal boundary
layer.[13]

Turbulence, also known as turbulent flow, is the term used to describe the chaotic motion
of a fluid in the field of fluid dynamics. It is characterized by unpredictably changing flow
velocity and pressure. Laminar flow, in which a fluid flows in smooth, parallel layers without
interruption, is very different from this.
There are many commonplace examples of turbulence, including breaking waves, rushing
rivers, whirling storm clouds, and billowing smoke coming from a chimney. Turbulent flow
is a feature of most designed systems and natural fluid movements. It occurs when the

through.

3.2
Body aerodynamics
The long separation zones that cyclists find in the surrounding flow are referred to as
bluff bodies. In contrast to aerodynamic shapes such as airfoils, which have curved lead­
ing edges and a tapering body width, bluff bodies have sharp edges or a more sudden
narrowing toward the trailing surfaces. Due to this unique geometry, the boundary layer
experiences large adverse pressure gradients, which can cause flow separation that might
or might not reattach to the surface.
Cycling faces mostly pressure drag, as opposed to streamlined bodies where aerody­
namic drag is dominated by viscous tangential wall shear stress. Large low­pressure
vortices and a turbulent wake are produced as flow separates around bicycles. The pres­
sure difference between the high­pressure stagnation regions and the low­pressure wake
zones on the rider’s leading surfaces determines the size of the pressure drag. By inte­
grating the surface pressure distribution throughout its whole length, acting normal to the
body surface, one can calculate the resulting pressure force.
Presented in 2.3, the drag coefficient measures a body’s aerodynamic efficiency indepen­
dent of its size. In cycling, minimizing the drag coefficient and resisting forces is the goal
when the rider position is fixed (i.e., frontal area is fixed). Numerous parameters, such
as body shape, orientation, surface roughness, Reynolds number, and free stream flow
conditions, affect this coefficient.

• Fan
• Cooling
• Flow conditioning
• Test section

meticulous temperature control for both the air flow and the instrumentation. Hence, wind
tunnels of this kind are equipped with robust cooling systems, typically liquid­based, and
incorporate a radiator matrix through which the air passes.
Conditioning the airflow: To optimize the airflow at the test section, it is essential for the

8.3
Courant ­ Number
The Courant number, often denoted as Co, is a dimensionless parameter used in com­
putational fluid dynamics (CFD) to characterize the stability and accuracy of numerical
simulations, particularly those involving transient phenomena. It is defined as the ratio of
the flow velocity to the velocity of propagation of information through the computational
mesh within a single time­step. Mathematically, it is expressed as:
Co = u∆t
∆x
(8.1)
where:
• u is the characteristic flow velocity,
• ∆t is the time­step size,
• ∆x is the characteristic length scale of the mesh cells.
The Courant number reflects how far a particle can travel within a single time­step relative
to the mesh size. A Courant number greater than 1 indicates that information can prop­
agate through more than one mesh cell during a time­step, potentially causing numerical
instability. Therefore, for stability and accuracy, the Courant number is kept below a cer­
tain threshold, typically around 1. [22]

8.5
Wall y+
Another parameter is important in order to have a thin enough mesh, this is wall y+. The
wall y+ is defined as the dimensionless distance from a solid boundary to the first grid
point or cell center in a CFD simulation. It’s typically expressed as:
y+ = yuτ
ν
(8.2)
Where:
• y is the distance from the wall,
• uτ is the friction velocity (a characteristic velocity scale of the near­wall region),
• ν is the kinematic viscosity of the fluid.
It represents the ratio of the distance to the viscous sublayer (the region near the wall
where viscosity dominates) to the typical scale of the turbulent flow. [23]
In simulations with low y+ values, the near­wall region is well resolved. This indicates that
the first grid point is situated within the viscous sub­layer, allowing for accurate capture
of viscosity effects. This level of resolution is critical for precise prediction of quantities
like wall shear stress and heat transfer near solid surfaces. Conversely, in simulations
with high y+ values, the near­wall region is inadequately resolved. This results in po­
tential inaccuracies, particularly in regions with steep gradients, such as those near solid
surfaces.

Conclusions
This report’s goal was to fully understand the transition phenomena surrounding time­
trial helmets and pinpoint its location and timing. Furthermore, the report looks at which
factors affect the transition and, consequently, the drag resistance, measuring how much
of an impact they have. The study also looks at whether and how much drag resistance
is affected by the wheels’ rotation.
In more detail:
1. Transition Phenomena Analysis: This involves identifying the specific conditions
under which transition occurs around time­trial helmets. The goal is to pinpoint the
critical moments and locations of transition during different cycling conditions.
2. Influence of Parameters on Transition and Drag Resistance: Various parame­
ters that might influence the transition and drag resistance are tested. This includes
mainly airflow characteristics. The study aims to measure how significantly each
parameter affects the transition and drag resistance.
3. Impact of Wheel Rotation: The report also examines whether the rotation of the
wheels has an impact on the transition. The focus is on quantifying any changes in
drag due to wheel rotation, providing insights into its significance in overall aerody­
namic performance.
The very first result that has been taken is the difference between the models k −ω and
Gamma­ReTheta Transition model, which basically solves the simulation without and with
transition, respectively. The difference between them in the drag force is about 6.2%, with
the Gamm­ReTheta values being higher. These phenomena and results have been tested
on two different riders, Emma and Kasper, to ensure reliability.
Secondly, the comparison with the wheels rotating and setting velocity on the bottom wall,
simulating a road, shows that there is no significant difference because the percentile
difference remains below 1% for the total drag force and below 1.3% for the shear drag
force. However, the phenomenon that was reliable for the project is how this influences
the transition. The results show that there is flow separation as well, but no significant
changes are introduced with the fact that the wheels are rotating.
Regarding the parameters that characterize the turbulent airflow at the inlet, it can be seen
that as the turbulence intensity increases from 0.05% to 2%, the drag forces decrease by
up to 11% of the total value, with drag pressure being the most significant factor. Also,
this decreasing in the total drag force, it can be seen that the shear force increases as
the turbulence intensity grows. Related to this, also can be seen that the boundary layer
detachment is lower for higher intensities, this is so that after 1% of turbulence intensity
no transition nor boundary layer detachment is seen. Also, it has been seen that for
turbulence intensity values higher than 1%, no noticeable difference in the drag force is
obtained.
Tanking about the turbulence parameters, the eddy’s length scale has not a big influence
