Abstract
Business Process Management (BPM) plays a critical role in the effective design,
execution, and optimization of business workflows.
As an industry standard,
BPM encompasses various stages, with the initial design phase being particu-
larly crucial.
Accurate and unambiguous process descriptions are essential in
this phase to prevent misinterpretations that can lead to inconsistent process
executions and significant operational issues.
In recent years, Natural Language Processing (NLP) has emerged as a trans-
formative force in the BPM domain. NLP’s ability to understand, interpret, and
generate human language enables it to identify and resolve ambiguities in BPM
descriptions, thus enhancing clarity and eﬀiciency. The trend of integrating NLP
in BPM is driven by its potential to automate the detection ambiguities, ensuring
more precise and consistent process specifications.
This thesis explores the application of NLP technologies to detect ambiguities
in BPM descriptions. It includes a study of ambiguity definitions, data collection
and preprocessing of BPM description sentences, and the training and comparison
of various NLP models.
The research aims to uncover the potential of NLP in ambiguity detection,
contributing to the development of standardized, eﬀicient, and automated BPM
practices.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Business Process Management (BPM) is a systematic approach to improving
an organization’s business processes.
BPM aims to make business workflows
more effective, eﬀicient, and adaptable to changes in the business environment
[Dum+13]. It involves the management of processes such as design, modeling,
execution, monitoring, and optimization. BPM is crucial for organizations as
it enhances operational eﬀiciency, ensures compliance with regulations, and im-
proves overall business performance [BR08].
The lifecycle of BPM consists of several stages, each playing a significant role
in the successful implementation of business processes. These stages include:
Figure 1.1: Business Process Management Life Cycle.
• Design: This initial phase involves identifying existing processes, defining
new processes, and specifying the requirements and objectives for these
processes [Dum+13].
==PAGE:12==
2
1 Introduction
• Modeling: In this stage, the designed processes are modeled using various
techniques to visualize and simulate them [Wes07].
• Execution: Here, the modeled processes are implemented and executed
using BPM software tools [Wes07].
• Monitoring: The execution of processes is monitored to ensure they are
performing as expected [Dum+13].
• Optimization: Based on the monitoring results, processes are analyzed
and optimized for better performance [BR08].
The design phase is particularly critical because it sets the foundation for the
entire lifecycle. A well-defined process description ensures that following stages
can proceed correctly. However, the major challenge in the design phase is the
presence of ambiguity in process descriptions. Ambiguities in descriptions can
lead to multiple interpretations, causing inconsistencies in process implementa-
tion and execution [SJ15]. This can result in operational ineﬀiciencies, increased
costs, and failure to meet business objectives [SJ15].
Ambiguity in process descriptions can stem from unclear relationships between
process elements, vague references, underspecified requirements, and inconsistent
information [Fra+23]. Addressing these ambiguities is essential to ensure that
the designed processes are accurately understood and implemented. Resolving
ambiguities in the design phase can lead to clearer process definitions, reducing
the risk of misinterpretations and ensuring more reliable and eﬀicient process
executions throughout the BPM lifecycle [SJ15].
By reducing ambiguities, organizations can achieve better alignment between
their business objectives and process outcomes, enhance process performance, and
improve overall operational eﬀiciency [Fra+23]. This study focuses on leveraging
Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques to identify and resolve ambigui-
ties in BPM descriptions, thereby contributing to the clarity and effectiveness of
BPM practices.
1.2
Motivation
In the field of BPM, the precision of process descriptions are very important.
Ambiguities in these descriptions can lead to significant operational ineﬀiciencies,
increased costs, and the failure to meet business objectives [SJ15].
Current methods for detecting and resolving ambiguities in BPM descrip-
tions often involve manual review and traditional automation tools. However,
these approaches are limited in their ability to scale and adapt to the increasing
==PAGE:13==

complexity and volume of business process data [SJ15]. Manual reviews are time-
consuming and prone to human error, while traditional tools lack the sophistica-
tion to accurately parse and interpret nuanced language in process descriptions.
The advent of Natural Language Processing (NLP) offers a promising so-
lution to this problem. NLP techniques have advanced significantly in recent
years, demonstrating the capability to understand, interpret, and generate hu-
man language with high accuracy [Dum+13]. By leveraging NLP, it is possible
to automate the detection and resolution of ambiguities in BPM descriptions,
thus enhancing clarity and consistency [Van+18].
This research aims to use NLP technologies to capture ambiguity in BPM
descriptions. The goal is to develop and evaluate NLP models that can accurately
identify different types of ambiguities and suggest appropriate resolutions. This
study not only seeks to improve the precision of BPM descriptions but also aims
to have the chance to contribute to the field of automated process management.
This research has the potential for industrial usage. By improving the accu-
racy and eﬀiciency of BPM practices, organizations can achieve better alignment
between their business processes and strategic goals [BR08].
The findings of
this study could lead to the development of more robust BPM tools that sup-
port automated management and decision-making, ultimately enhancing overall
operational performance and competitive advantage [Wes07].
In summary, the motivation for this study is from the critical need to address
ambiguities in BPM descriptions, the limitations of current solutions, and the
promising potential of NLP technologies to transform BPM practices. This re-
search aims to apply advancements in NLP and practical applications in BPM,
contributing to the development of more eﬀicient BPM process.
1.3
Research Contribution
This study makes several contributions to the field of Business Process Man-
agement (BPM) by apply Natural Language Processing (NLP) to addressing the
challenge of ambiguity in process descriptions. The key contributions are outlined
as follows:
• Ambiguity Definition and Usage: This research provides a comprehen-
sive review and synthesis of existing definitions and examples of ambigu-
ity. By examining a wide range of ambiguity definitions, the study identi-
fies and formalizes four specific types of ambiguities to BPM descriptions.
These include different forms of descriptive ambiguities such as unclear re-
lations, unclear references, underspecifications, and inconsistencies. Each
type of ambiguity is illustrated with relevant examples, tailored specifically

for BPM contexts. This definition serves as a foundational framework for
detecting ambiguities in BPM descriptions.
• Dataset Collection and Processing: The study involves the collection
and preprocessing of a diverse dataset comprising 71 process description
cases, totaling 1040 sentences. The descriptions were sourced from various
authors and include both declarative and imperative types of descriptions,
ensuring diversity and authenticity. The preprocessing steps included ad-
dressing the complexities of original structures to prevent errors in sentence

This annotation framework is critial in the manual annotation process.

study systematically compares metrics such as precision, recall, F1-score,

future research and development in this area.
In summary, this research improve the understanding and dealing of ambigu-

robust annotation guidelines, and the comprehensive training and evaluation of
NLP models.
1.4
Scope Of The Study
This research focuses on specific aspects of ambiguity within the Business Process
Management (BPM) domain, with a particular emphasis on the design phase.
The scope of the study is delineated as follows:

• Focus on BPM Design Phase: The study only related to the design
phase of BPM. This phase is crucial as it involves the creation and specifi-
cation of process descriptions that set the foundation for subsequent stages
such as modeling, execution, monitoring, and optimization. By focusing on
the design phase, this research aims to enhance the clarity and precision of
initial process descriptions, thereby preventing issues that could propagate
through the entire BPM lifecycle [Dum+13].
• Descriptive Ambiguities in Process Descriptions: Ambiguities can
be found in various BPM artifacts, including models and logs, as well as
descriptions. According to the ambiguity taxonomy, this study only targets
descriptive types of ambiguities in process descriptions [Fra+23]. It does
not cover ambiguities in BPM models or logs. Furthermore, the research is
limited to textual descriptions of processes, excluding descriptions presented
in tables or images.
This focus ensures a clear and manageable scope,
allowing for a detailed examination of textual ambiguities.
• Exclusion of Rule-Based Approaches: Traditionally, NLP research
in this area has concentrated on grammatical issues, often employing rule-
based approaches such as Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging and regular expres-
sions (RE), which marks every word in a sentence with predefined parts-of-
speech (noun, verb, adjective, etc.) [SJ15]. However, these methods can be
time-consuming and labor-intensive, with a limited scope for covering the
wide range of ambiguities present in BPM descriptions. This study deliber-
ately excludes rule-based approaches and POS tagging, focusing instead on
sentence-level annotation and analysis. By annotating data at the sentence
level, this research aims to identify and classify sentences as either positive
or negative for specific types of ambiguities. (More information about other
studies will be given in Chapter 2. )
All models developed and evaluated in this study are trained at the sen-
tence level. The annotation process involves labeling sentences based on
the presence or absence of specific types of ambiguities, providing a binary
classification for each ambiguity type [VBM23]. This approach allows for
a more straightforward and scalable analysis of ambiguities within BPM
descriptions. The performance of different models is then compared to de-
termine their effectiveness in detecting various types of ambiguities. Met-
rics such as precision, recall, F1-score, and accuracy are used to evaluate
model performance comprehensively. (The methods of this study will be
explained in more detail in Chapter 3.)
In summary, this study is narrowly focused on addressing descriptive ambi-
guities in textual BPM process descriptions during the design phase. By avoid

2.1
Relevance study of Ambiguity
Ambiguity is a well-recognized issue in various domains, particularly in require-
ments engineering (RE) and Business Process Management (BPM). Numerous
studies have focused on defining and understanding different types of ambigui-
ties. This section summarizes the definitions and perspectives on ambiguity from
several key studies.
• A Characterisation of Ambiguity in BPM by Marco Franceschetti
et al. [Fra+23] Franceschetti et al. focus specifically on ambiguities in
BPM which make this especially suitable for this reseach. They categorize
ambiguities into descriptive and procedural types. Descriptive ambiguities
include unclear relations, vague references, underspecifications, and incon-
sistencies. Procedural ambiguities relate to the execution and operational
aspects of BPM. Their work underscores the importance of addressing these
ambiguities to ensure accurate process execution and to avoid operational
ineﬀiciencies.
• Ambiguity in Requirements Specification by Daniel M. Berry and
Erik Kamsties [EA04]. Berry and Kamsties’ work provides a detailed
analysis of ambiguities in requirements specifications. They categorize am-
biguities into lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic types.
Lexical
ambiguities arise from words with multiple meanings, syntactic ambigui-
ties from sentence structure, semantic ambiguities from unclear meaning,
and pragmatic ambiguities from context-dependent interpretations. Their
study emphasizes the impact of these ambiguities on the interpretation and
implementation of software requirements, highlighting the need for clear
and precise specifications [BK03].
• From Contract Drafting to Software Specification: Linguistic Sources
of Ambiguity by Richard K. Eaton and Christine F. Anderson
Eaton and Anderson’s handbook examines the sources of ambiguity in legal
contracts and software specifications. They identify linguistic sources of

ambiguity such as polysemy, homonymy, and syntactic ambiguity. Their
analysis is relevant to BPM as it highlights how linguistic ambiguities can
lead to misinterpretations and errors in contract and software specifications,
which parallels the issues faced in BPM descriptions
• Ambiguity in User Stories: A Systematic Literature Review by
Anis R. Amnaa and Geert Poels [AP20]. Amnaa and Poels conduct a
systematic review of ambiguities in user stories within agile software devel-
opment. They identify common sources of ambiguity, including imprecise
language and incomplete information. Their findings emphasize the need

• From Contract Drafting to Software Specification: Linguistic Sources

This handbook discusses the phenomenon of ambiguity in both software
requirements specifications (SRSs) and legal contracts. Both types of doc-
uments require precision, consistency, and completeness, but are prone to
ambiguities when written in natural language. The authors analyze the
sources of ambiguity from various perspectives, including linguistics, soft-
ware engineering, and law, and provide strategies for avoiding and detect-
ing ambiguities. The handbook highlights issues such as the use of com-
mon words like ”all,” ”each,” and ”every,” the positioning of terms like
”only” and ”also,” and the precedence of conjunctions like ”and” and ”or.”
It also discusses the role of proper language use in reducing ambiguities and
presents examples from both requirements and legal documents.

2.2
Relevance Solution to Ambiguity
In this section, we discuss various solutions proposed in the literature to address
ambiguity in different domains. We categorize the solutions into several classes,
such as machine learning approaches, NLP techniques, rule-based methods, and
crowd-sourcing frameworks. Each category includes detailed explanations of the
methods used and their advantages and disadvantages.
2.2.1
Machine Learning Approaches
• Detecting ambiguous author names in crowdsourced scholarly
data by Sun, X., et al.
[Sun+11] This study focuses on detecting am-
biguous author names in scholarly data using machine learning techniques.
The authors developed a model that leverages features derived from the
text to disambiguate author names. The main advantage of this approach
is its ability to handle large datasets eﬀiciently. However, it may struggle
with rare or unique names due to limited training data.

• Score-based automatic detection and resolution of syntactic am-
biguity in natural language requirements by Osama, Mohamed, et al.

This paper presents a score-based approach for detecting and resolving syn-

The model assigns scores to potential ambiguities based on their likelihood,

computational resources and extensive annotated datasets for training.

Natural Language Processing (NLP) Techniques
• Pragmatic ambiguity detection in natural language requirements
by Ferrari, Alessio, et al. [Fer+14]
Ferrari et al. use NLP techniques to detect pragmatic ambiguities in natu-
ral language requirements. Their approach includes semantic role labeling
and word sense disambiguation to identify ambiguous terms and phrases.
The main advantage of this method is its detailed semantic analysis, which
improves the accuracy of ambiguity detection. However, it requires complex
NLP pipelines and extensive linguistic resources.
• Addressing lexical and semantic ambiguity in natural language
requirements by Zait, Fatima, and Nacereddine Zarour [ZZ18]
This study addresses lexical and semantic ambiguities using NLP meth-
ods, including part-of-speech tagging and named entity recognition. The
approach enhances the clarity of requirements by resolving ambiguities at
both the lexical and semantic levels. The main advantage is the compre-
hensive coverage of different types of ambiguities. However, it may require
significant preprocessing and domain-specific adaptations.
• POSBIOTM-NER: a machine learning approach for bio-named
entity recognition by Song, Y., et al. [Son+04]

2.3
Summary for the Chapter
This chapter has provided a comprehensive review of the related work on ambi-
guity in various domains and the solutions proposed to address it. We conclude
this chapter by summarizing the key insights into the research on ambiguity, its
definitions, and the solutions proposed.
2.3.1
Definitions of Ambiguity
Ambiguity can be broadly categorized into linguistic ambiguity and software en-
gineering ambiguity:
==PAGE:23==
2.3 Summary for the Chapter
13
• Linguistic Ambiguity: Ambiguity that can be identified by any reader
with linguistic knowledge. Includes lexical (word-level), syntactic (sentence
structure), and semantic (meaning) ambiguities.
• Software Engineering Ambiguity: Ambiguity that is dependent on the
specific domain of the requirement. Can only be identified by readers with
suﬀicient domain knowledge. More prevalent in RE contexts. Includes con-
cept translation ambiguity, requirement document ambiguity, application
domain ambiguity, system domain ambiguity etc.
For BPM, the focus is more on linguistic ambiguity, particularly in how it af-
fects process descriptions. Unlike user stories and RE, BPM needs to concentrate
on avoiding confusion in the flow and logic of processes. Thus, BPM descriptions
need to ensure clarity in the relationships and logical sequence between entities
such as roles and activities. The primary concern is to prevent process confusion
and ambiguity.
2.3.2
Solutions for Ambiguity Detection and Resolution
The reviewed literature presents various solutions for detecting and resolving
ambiguities, each with distinct approaches, requirements, and effectiveness:
• Machine Learning Approaches: These methods, including fine-tuned
transformer models and score-based techniques, are effective in handling
large datasets and complex ambiguities. However, they require substan-
tial computational resources and extensive annotated datasets for training
[Osa+20; Alh22].
• Natural Language Processing (NLP) Techniques: NLP techniques
like semantic role labeling, word sense disambiguation, and named entity
recognition offer detailed semantic analysis, improving ambiguity detection
accuracy.
However, they require complex NLP pipelines and significant
linguistic resources [Fer+14; ZZ18].
• Rule-Based Methods: These approaches use predefined linguistic pat-
terns and rules to detect ambiguities. They are straightforward and ex-
plainable but may lack flexibility and scalability for complex or varied re-
quirements [OZ18; KP00].
• Crowd-Sourcing Frameworks: Methods like Crowdtruth leverage collec-
tive intelligence to gather annotated data, enhancing ambiguity detection
through diverse perspectives. However, they may face challenges related to
annotation quality and consistency [Ine+14; FG12].

• Hybrid Approaches: Combining rule-based and machine learning meth-
ods, hybrid approaches provide a balanced solution that leverages the strengths
of both techniques. They offer comprehensive coverage but can be complex

2.3.3
Current Trends In Ambiguity Detection
Traditional solutions for ambiguity detection include rule-based methods and
basic machine learning techniques. Rule-based methods use predefined linguistic
rules to identify ambiguous phrases. These methods are straightforward and easy
to implement but often lack flexibility and scalability. Basic machine learning
approaches, such as Naive Bayes, apply statistical techniques to detect ambigu-
ities. However, these models require extensive feature engineering and may not
perform well on complex or context-dependent ambiguities.
Current trending solutions leverage advanced NLP and deep learning tech-
niques, such as:
• Transformer Models: Techniques like BERT and GPT-3, which are pre-
trained on vast corpora, are fine-tuned for specific tasks like ambiguity
detection. These models capture context and nuances in text, significantly
improving detection accuracy. However, they require substantial computa-
tional resources and large annotated datasets [Alh22].
• Hybrid Methods: Combining rule-based and machine learning approaches
offers a balanced solution. Hybrid methods utilize the robustness of rule-
based systems and the adaptability of machine learning models, providing
comprehensive coverage and improved performance. However, they can be
complex to implement and maintain [Osm+21].
• Crowd-Sourced Data and Collective Intelligence: Leveraging crowd-
sourced annotations and collective intelligence frameworks, such as Crowdtruth,
improves the quality and diversity of training data. These methods harness
disagreements among annotators to refine ambiguity detection but face chal-
lenges related to consistency and quality control [Ine+14].
Designing automated and semi-automated tools for ambiguity detection is
also a growing trend.
These tools integrate advanced NLP models and user-
friendly interfaces to help practitioners identify and resolve ambiguities eﬀiciently.
Notable tools include:
• Ambi Detect: A tool that combines rule-based and machine learning
approaches to detect ambiguities in software requirements. It provides a
balanced solution by leveraging the strengths of both techniques [Osm+21].

4.2
Establish Definition Of Ambiguity
The concept of ambiguity in Business Process Management (BPM) is special and
critical. The specific definition of ambiguity used in this study is proposed from
the concept of Ambiguity Taxonomy [Fra+23]. provides a structured approach
to categorize ambiguities within BPM specifications. Ambiguities in the specifi-
cation of a process fall under the category of Descriptive ambiguity. This study
focuses on descriptive ambiguity, which refers to ambiguities in the textual de-
scriptions of processes. Descriptive ambiguities are classified into four distinct
types:
• Unclear Relations (T1): This type of ambiguity occurs when the rela-
tionships between different process elements are not clearly defined within
the process description. This can lead to uncertainty about whether tasks
should be executed in sequence, in parallel, or are mutually exclusive.
For example, a statement such as ”Prepare the meeting agenda and dis-
tribute it to participants” does not specify if these actions should occur
simultaneously or one after the other.
Clear Example could be: ”First,
prepare the meeting agenda. After the agenda is prepared, distribute it to
participants.”
• Unclear References (T2): This ambiguity arises when it is not clear
which part of the process a particular statement or specification refers to.
This can lead to confusion about the specific elements involved or affected
by a statement, potentially leading to erroneous process models or imple-
mentations.
For example,”Manager requires employees to submit files to the system,
he should review the document before approval” the sentence ”He should
review the document before approval” does not specify who ”He” refers to,
leading to ambiguity in the process model. The Clear Example should be
”(...) The project manager should review the document before approval.”
• Underspecifications (T3): This occurs when a process description delib-
erately or accidentally omits important details. Such omissions can be due
to the need for flexibility, limited domain knowledge, or oversight. This am-
biguity can result in incomplete understanding of roles, tasks, or required
actions within the process.
For instance, the process description ”Submit the report by the end of
the month.” The sentence has no subject, no object, better version could

4.5
Model Selecting And Training
In this section, we outline the methodology for selecting and training models to
identify ambiguities in Business Process Model (BPM) descriptions at the sen-
tence level. The task involves classifying sentences based on the presence of four
types of ambiguities: Unclear Relations (T1), Unclear References (T2), Under-
specifications (T3), and Inconsistent Specifications (T4). Our dataset consists
of approximately 1000 annotated sentences, with each sentence labeled for the
presence or absence of each type of ambiguity.
4.5.1
Training Strategy
The training strategy is designed to handle the classification of sentences into
positive and negative cases for each type of ambiguity. By training models to
recognize both the presence (true) and absence (false) of ambiguities, we aim to
improve the accuracy and reliability of ambiguity detection across the dataset.
The key aspects of our training strategy are as follows:
• Balanced Training Sets: Ensuring that the training sets are balanced in
terms of positive and negative cases for each type of ambiguity to prevent
model bias. Especially for some type ambiguity cases number is so low.
• Cross-Validation: Implementing k-fold cross-validation to validate the
models’ performance and ensure robustness against overfitting.
• Declarative and Imperative Descriptions: Training and evaluating
models separately on declarative and imperative descriptions to understand
their respective performance and adapt strategies accordingly.

Model Training
Each model undergoes a rigorous training process to optimize its performance in
detecting the four types of ambiguities. The process involves iterative training,
validation, and testing to refine the models and improve their predictive accuracy.
The key steps in the training process are as follows:
==PAGE:47==
4.5 Model Selecting And Training
37
• Data Preparation: The annotated dataset is split into training, valida-
tion, and test sets using K-Fold cross-validation to ensure robust perfor-
mance evaluation. This ensures that the models are trained on a subset
of the data and evaluated on unseen sentences to assess their generaliza-
tion capabilities. The labels for each ambiguity type (T1, T2, T3, T4) are
binarized for classification tasks.
• Model Training: Each model is trained on the prepared dataset by ad-
justing model parameters to minimize the loss function, which measures the
difference between predicted and actual labels. Training involves feeding
tokenized inputs into the models, performing forward and backward passes,
and updating model weights iteratively.
• Model Evaluation: Each model’s performance is evaluated using metrics
such as F1-score, precision, recall, and accuracy. These metrics provide
insights into how well the models can identify the presence or absence of
each type of ambiguity.
Cross-validation results are averaged to ensure
robust performance estimates.
• Iterative Optimization: Based on evaluation results, models undergo it-
erative optimization. This involves adjusting hyperparameters, fine-tuning
model architectures, and incorporating additional training data to enhance
model performance and reduce error rates.
4.5.4
Model Comparison
After training, the models’ performances are compared to identify the most ef-
fective approach for detecting ambiguities in BPM descriptions. The comparison
involves analyzing the F1-score, precision, recall, and accuracy for each model
across the four types of ambiguities. The key metrics used for evaluation are:
• F1-Score: Measures the harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing
a balance between the two metrics.
• Precision: Indicates the proportion of true positive predictions among all
positive predictions, reflecting model accuracy in predicting true ambigui-
ties.
• Recall: Indicates the proportion of true positive predictions among all
actual positives, reflecting model sensitivity to detecting ambiguities.
• Accuracy: Measures the overall correctness of the model by comparing
the number of correct predictions to the total number of predictions.

• Macro and Micro Averages: Used to evaluate model performance across
multiple labels, with macro averaging treating all classes equally and micro
