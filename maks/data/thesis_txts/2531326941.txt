Abstract
Room acoustics is the ﬁeld of study that investigates the interaction between sound waves
and a room. Room acoustic simulations are increasingly used in many diﬀerent applications
including architecture, video games, virtual reality and more. Depending on the application,
diﬀerent requirements relating to computation time, computational power and accuracy
are required.
In this thesis we have implemented a Discontinious Galerkin Finite Element Method solver
for the acoustic wave equation using the libParanumal framework. The developed solver
has several attributes that make it particularly suitable for room acoustics simulations.
It can handle a broad range of boundary conditions types: a) rigid (perfectly reﬂecting),
b) frequency independent absorption, c) locally-reacting frequency dependent absorption
and d) extended-reacting frequency dependent absorption, which is realized through a
novel method based on wave ﬁeld incidence angle detection to account for the angular
dependency of the extended reaction boundaries. Moreover to be able to simulate complex
geometries, support for curvilinear elements has been implemented.
In the thesis we have carefully validated the implemented code for aﬃne elements through
convergence tests that show correct spectral convergence, meaning we show to ability
to have a high-order convergence rate that gives opportunity for cost-eﬃcient room
acoustic simulations. The advanced boundary conditions have all been validated through
comparisons with existing validated time-domain acoustic solvers. We have also shown
that curvilinear elements leads to a large improvement in convergence compared to aﬃne
elements when simulating in curved geometries. This improvement is seen even without
using anti-aliasing methods to deal with the non-linearity caused by curvilinear elements.
The implementation is benchmarked on multi-GPU computational setups, and demonstrate
signiﬁcant gains speed for simulation compared to naive serial implementations. These
scaling tests have shown that multi-GPU support gives the ability to simulate large
rooms, over a broad frequency spectrum, both in terms of computation time and memory
requirements. We have also shown simulations on two non-trivial geometries, a star formed
room with a dome, and a auditorium. Overall this shows the viability of using a GPU
accelerated DGFEM solver for realistic large-scale room acoustics simulations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Room acoustics is the ﬁeld of study used to describe how sound waves interact with a
room. This can be applied to support the architectural design process for rooms and
buildings to ensure better acoustical properties, making said rooms better for diﬀerent
acoustic purposes. If an architect ﬁrm is contracted to design a new concert hall, the
acoustics of such a hall is obviously a very important factor of the designing process, as a
poorly acoustically designed concert hall would not be a pleasant experience for paying
concert-goers or musicians. This can be applied to many diﬀerent scenarios as well where a
pleasant acoustical experience is wanted, this could be everything ranging from a classroom,
open oﬃces, theatres, public venues, to large railway-stations.
Currently a lot of room acoustics simulations have been carried out by either scaled physical
models, Geometric Acoustics, or non-GPU-accelerated wave-based solvers.
Building a scaled physical model of the room one wishes to simulate can create a good
indication of the behavior of sound in a domain. However, building of scale models is often
very cumbersome and expensive. When using scale models, changing model parameters
such as boundary conditions, room layout etc., requires the building of a new scale model
or physical modiﬁcation of the old one, whereas in computer based modelling a lot of these
parameters can be changed with little to no eﬀort.
The technique for Room Acoustics called Geometric Acoustics (GA), is when the wave-
properties of acoustic waves are neglected and sound waves are assumed to behave like
rays. A wide array of these methods has been discussed in [SS15]. The assumptions
of the geometric methods holds quite well for high frequencies, as the wave-length is
short compared to the dimensions of the room or obstacles in the room. However, for
low-mid frequencies the approximation error, i.e. assuming ray-like behavior, increases as
wave-phenomena, such as diﬀraction and interference, tend to play a larger role. A recent
round robin test of several diﬀerent geometrical acoustics simulators found that none of
them were able to simulate room acoustics with a high degree of physical accuracy outside
a medium frequency range. Perceptually, simulations were always easily distinguishable
from measurements[BAA+19]. A huge advantage of GA is that it is much more ﬂexible
compared to the scale models, and GA is relatively cheap to compute when comparing to
the wave-based models, thus one has to leverage the importance of accuracy against the

computational costs.
The wave-based models solve the acoustic partial diﬀerential wave equations. These methods
are generally much more accurate than both the scaled physical models and geometric
acoustics, however they come with a high computational extra cost. As computers are
becoming much more powerful and the acquisition of high-performance tools, e.g. GPUs,
are becoming cheaper and therefore much more widely adopted, these wave-based models
are beginning to be practically feasible to solve the room acoustic problem for a broad
frequency range and large rooms. Therefore many are looking to the wave-based models
to gain the accuracy lost when using GA. These equations can be numerically solved in
a variety of ways. A commonly used wave-based method in room acoustic simulations
is the ﬁnite-diﬀerence time-domain (FDTD) method. While this method is relatively
computationally eﬃcient and straightforward to implement, it is ill-suited for handling
complex geometries[Bot95]. Another way is using a low order Finite volume method as
described in [Bil13], which can be used to overcome the limited geometric ﬂexibility of
FDTD. Low order methods are usually easy to implement, however often one can achieve
higher accuracy and a better accuracy-per-computational-cost by using a higher order
method. These are often more cumbersome to understand and implement, but the reward
is high order convergence and improved cost-eﬃency. One higher order method is Spectral-
Element-Method (SEM) solvers as demonstrated in [PEKJ+19]. SEM solver can quite
eﬀectively and accurately solve the time-domain acoustic wave equations, however when
one is interested in parallelizing the code for faster simulation time, Nodal Discontinuous
Galerkin Finite Element Method(DG-FEM) is often a better solution.
In DG-FEM
the solution on each element can be computed somewhat independently of every other
element, with communication only needed at element boundary such that the solution can
propagate between them. This semi-independence can be heavily utilized for parallelization
of the solver. By using DG-FEM we therefore gain all the accuracy of the wave-based
solvers, geometric ﬂexibility, high order convergence, and ﬁnally, but very signiﬁcantly, the
possibility of fast simulations through the use of high-performance tools by parallelization.
The aim of this work is therefore to introduce a high-performance computing approach
to the simulation of three dimensional room acoustics. This will be done through using
software written in C/C++ with Message Passing Interface (MPI) as its backbone, and
GPU accelerating the simulation itself through the parallel programming model Open
Concurrent Compute Abstraction (OCCA) for portable code development across diﬀerent
hardware architectures. This is exploited through the use of the parallelizable Nodal
Discontinuous Galerkin Finite Element Method.

1.3
Contributions of the thesis
In this thesis we have implemented support for advanced boundary conditions and
curvilinear geometry in the libParanumal[KCHW19][KCSW17][KW17][CW18] framework.
Combined this creates a GPU-accelerated DG-FEM solver capable of solving the time-
domain acoustic wave equation. Through convergence and scaling tests we have shown
that this solver is capable of simulating room acoustics in rooms of realistic size, with
complex geometry and boundaries.
We have also shown a general workﬂow that allows the end-user to be able to use tried
and tested tools for simulation. A design/CAD tool can be used to create the overall
geometry of the domain, gmsh can then be used for meshing of the created model as well
as specifying boundary conditions for every surface of the domain. This can then be loaded
into the solver hiding the speciﬁc software details.
The simulator has been tested in real and academic situations in the Room Acoustics
Group at DTU by a MSc student and a PhD student, details follow in chapter 14.

derive either the weak or strong formulation of the equations[WH08a]. Both of these
formulations give rise to a surface integral which leads to a good way of propagating the
solution between elements. While both of these formulations are viable choices, in this
thesis we will use the strong formulation of the equations.

2.4
Enforcing boundary conditions through numerical ﬂux
When on the boundary of the domain we wish to be able to enforce boundary conditions.
In DGFEM this is done through the numerical ﬂux term. On the boundary we do not have
a q+ from a neighbouring element, as this would be outside of the domain. We instead
determine q+ using the boundary conditions of the problem, and use this as a way to
weakly enforce the boundary conditions. We can look at this as having a ghost element
outside of the boundary of the domain, and we deﬁne the values in this ghost element to
enforce boundary conditions. Assuming we want to enforce Dirichlet boundary conditions
on the form
q = g(x, t) for x ∈∂Ω,
(2.33)
we have to determine q+ in such a way that these boundary conditions are enforced. The
most simple method to determine q+ is
q+ = g(x, t).
(2.34)
This corresponds to the ghost elements having the value of the boundary conditions, and
through the numerical ﬂux weakly enforcing them on the solution inside the domain. The
problem with this method happens if shocks can occur. While this is usually not a problem
in the acoustic equations, a more robust method is using the average of q+ and q−
q−+ q+
2
= g(x, t) ⇔
q+ = −q−+ 2g(x, t).
(2.35)
This is able to handle shocks at the boundary, and moreover even if no such shocks exists
at the boundary, this means that
q−≈g(x, t) ⇔
q+ = −q−+ 2g(x, t) ≈g(x, t),
(2.36)
and therefore eq. (2.35) can also handle non-shock boundaries.
Note that if no boundary condition is enforced on q, we simply let q+ = q−on the boundary
nodes.
We now have a way to handle boundary conditions using the numerical ﬂux in the DG-FEM
scheme.

3.4
Frequency independent boundary condition
Another boundary condition that we will use is the Frequency Independent(FI) boundary

accurately, compared to PR, models the real-world walls.

3.6

The LR boundary condition only considers the normal incidence surface impedance.
However, several surface types in practice exhibit a strong angular variation in the surface
impedance. This is called an extended-reacting boundary condition. Therefore if we
wish to take the angle of incidence into account we need a more advanced way of dealing
with the boundary. The Extended-reacting frequency dependent(ER) boundary condition

from normal to grazing, i.e. 0° through 90° from the normal, exploiting the symmetry
around the normal. What is essentially done is ﬁtting a rational function to each of the 91

3.6.2
Stiﬀness of equations
An issue with the diﬀerential equations that deﬁne both the LR and ER boundary conditions,
is that for some materials the ODEs can become stiﬀ. A stiﬀODE is characterized by
rapid changes in the solution over a short period of time. This means that numerical
methods often become unstable when solving these, unless very short time-steps are used.
Therefore it is important to handle such equations with care, to ensure a robust solver.
We will explore a solution to this later in section 6.2.

a 5 stage method compared to the ERK4 which is a 4 stage method. This is a trade-oﬀ
that can be weighted for each speciﬁc problem, i.e. if the right-hand side f is expensive to
calculate and the memory usage of ERK4 is not an issue, then the obvious choice is ERK4,
however if solving the ODEs on a ﬁne mesh with a large number of mesh nodes, ERK4 is
very memory hungry, thus making LSERK4 an excellent choice.

6.3
Time-step size
To ensure a stable solution of the ODEs we need to choose a time-step small enough such
that the solution does not blow up, but we also wish to choose an as large as possible time-

Curvilinear geometry
In earlier chapters we have assumed that all elements are aﬃne, but in real world problems
you often want to model curvilinear geometry. Curvilinear geometry is any geometry that
can not be exactly modelled using only straight lines. In two dimensions an example of a
curvilinear geometry could be a simple circle. If we mesh this circle with aﬃne triangles,
we essentially approximate the curvature of the circle with straight lines, see ﬁgure 9.1.
The approximation of the curvature becomes increasingly more accurate the as we make
the element side length h smaller, i.e. as h →0 the meshed circle approaches the true
circular geometry. This obviously is not feasible as this would require a ridiculous number
of elements to even approximate the circle within machine precision. Therefore we need a
diﬀerent method of solving this problem.
Figure 9.1: Circle meshed with aﬃne triangular elements, where the
dashes line shown the exact geometry of the circular domain.
An important property of DGFEM is that by increasing the polynomial order N we get

N does not lead to a better representation of the curvilinear geometry. Aﬃne elements
therefore often suﬀers large reductions in convergence rate due to geometrical errors, often
only reaching second order h-convergence. It is therefore useful to use higher order elements,
also known as curvilinear elements, where points are moved to ﬁt the geometry. This way
increasing the order N also improves the representation of the actual geometry, allowing us
to reach higher order convergence. The diﬀerence between aﬃne and curvilinear elements

9.2

As mentioned in the introduction of this chapter, a problem with using curvilinear elements
is that the Jacobian and normals are no longer constant, meaning we get a non-linear term
in the inner products used to compute the matrix based operators. This leads to aliasing
errors in the solution. One way to handle this is to use a method based on super-collocation,
as suggested in by Ensig-Karup et. al. in [EKEB16] and Kirby and Karniadakis in [KK03],
where we handle the integration part of the right hand sides using a higher order, in this
case 2N, in the curvilinear elements. Afterwards we then project back to lower order using
only the lower order modes. This is because aliasing errors accumulate in the higher order
modes, and this way one can get rid of a large part of the error. There are several steps to
handle this which will be outlined below.

Step 3 - Compute ﬂux and surface integral

aﬃne geometry
We have now gone through everything needed to implement a working DG-FEM solver
for room acoustics. In this chapter we will verify our implementation as well as show
results for aﬃne elements. For implementation we have used the libParanumal framework
developed by the Parallel Numerical Algorithms Group at Virginia Tech. libParanumal is
a set of ﬁnite element ﬂow solvers for heterogeneous (GPU/CPU) systems.
10.1
Implementation
The libParanumal framework contains a basic acoustics solver, however many parts that we
are interested in are missing, and have instead been implemented by us. The libParanumal
framework uses MPI (Message Passing Interface) to handle communication between CPUs.
Note, when we use CPU it refers to one single CPU core, while GPU refers to the entire
GPU. MPI works by treating each CPU core as its own entity, with its own separate
memory. Information can then be shared between CPU cores through a messaging system.
In practice this means that libParanumal splits the domain as seen in ﬁgure 10.1, and each
CPU core then only stores the simulation information needed for computations in elements
belonging to its own part of the domain. Though we as can clearly see, the CPUs share
an interface between them where information has to be shared for the acoustic wave to
propagate from one CPU to the next. This means that for some elements the numerical
ﬂux has to be computed based on neighbouring elements where CPU core 0, CPU0, does
not contain the information about these, which is then instead stored on CPU1, and vice
versa. In general this split of the domain means that many of our own implementations in
the framework requires the use of MPI communication. The same communication is used
when we run on GPUs, this is explained more in depth in chapter 12.

10.3
Vector ﬁtting
To be able to use Local reaction and Extended reaction boundary conditions we have to
obtain the poles λk, and α ± iβk, along with the numerical coeﬃcients Y∞, Ak, Bk and
Ck mentioned in section 3.5. This is done through ﬁtting of a rational function to the
complex, frequency dependent surface admittance Y (ω). Here we use the Fast Relaxed
Vector Fitting(FRVF) as implemented by Bjørn Gustavsen[GS99][Gus06][DMDdZ08][vec].
Note that to use this vectorﬁtting in a time domain simulation, the vectorﬁt has to be
passive. This means the vector ﬁt cannot generate energy when used in a time domain
simulation, as this will cause the solution to explode. In the vector ﬁtting toolbox, code is
provided that can be used to ensure that the model is passive.

11.2.1
Aliasing errors
In chapter 9 we described how curvilinear geometry introduces aliasing errors if not handled
well. Unfortunately we did not have time to implement the handling of aliasing errors
in libParanumal. In this chapter we will therefore have both results from libParanumal
without handling the aliasing errors, but also have results from a MATLAB implementation
where we do handle aliasing errors as described in section 9.2.
11.2.2
Verifying the code
When we did results for aﬃne elements we veriﬁed the model by comparing against
either the true solution or the veriﬁed SEM code depending on the boundary conditions.
Unfortunately for curvilinear geometry we do not have access to any of these. Moreover,
as we mentioned above, we do not handle the aliasing errors in the libParanumal code, and
therefore we would expect to see higher errors compared to when we simulate non-curvilinear
geometry. However there are a few things that can be done to verify the code. We can
verify the matrix based operators by using the Method of manufactured solutions(MoMS)
as described in section 11.2.3. However MoMS does not simulate the geometry, meaning
that it does not tell us if using curvilinear elements are actually improving the performance
of the simulation compared to aﬃne elements.
To determine whether the curvilinear elements are making a diﬀerence compared to using
aﬃne elements we can do the following:

Computational performance
As mentioned in section 10.1, the libParanumal framework uses MPI for communication in a
multi-CPU/GPU conﬁguration. Therefore information has to be shared between the CPUs
on the domain interfaces. This eﬀectively means that for each stage of the time-stepping
method we have to share information on the interfaces between CPUs. Communication
between CPUs is relatively costly, however major beneﬁt of using MPI is the incredible
ﬂexibility that it provides, i.e. the code has no assumptions of the number of CPU cores
available or if they are even on the same compute node. This means that in theory, the
code can be run on any number of CPU cores across any number of compute nodes.
This covers the CPU part of the communication. However, we are interested in performing
the computational costly parts of the simulation in parallel on GPUs. When running the
code on GPUs, the code is structured such that each CPU core is assumed to have one
GPU available. This means that only CPU0 can communicate directly with GPU0, which
means that the interface communication between CPU cores become a bit more costly.
This is because the same issue with neighbouring elements being on diﬀerent cores happens
for GPUs, as it did for CPUs. However, now this results in CPU0 having to copy all
interface information from GPU0 into RAM on CPU0, before it can then communicate this
information to CPUx, where 0 < x ≤N −1 denotes an arbitrary CPU core out of the total
N CPUs, through MPI. CPUx then has to copy the received interface information to its
connected GPU, GPUx. This means using GPUs introduces an additional communication
step, and therefore additional communication overhead, between CPU and GPU in each
time-stepping stage. This means that we do not expect to have as good scaling using
GPUs as when using only CPUs. However, we expect the actual simulation to be much,
much faster overall when using GPUs compared to CPUs. The communication structure
can be seen in ﬁgure 12.1, where the red arrows are the CPU and GPU communications
and the blue arrows are the MPI communications.

wave solver are numerous as mentioned in chapter 1. In this chapter we will introduce two

Boundary condition research by F. Pind
In the academic world new research is always performed in every ﬁeld, acoustics is no

therefore allowed for much faster analysis of the performance of these boundary conditions,
leading to less wasted time for the researcher.

Chapter 15
Conclusion
The theory behind time-domain room acoustics and in particular the wave-based approach
to room acoustics has been presented along with how to solve these partial diﬀerential
equations using the Discontinuous Galerkin Finite Element Method.
General theory
describing the DG-FEM approach to solving PDE’s have been described along with some
theory describing two diﬀerent time-stepping methods to solve the ordinary diﬀerential
equations that arise after the spatial discretization.
A low-storage explicit Runge-
Kutta method of 4’th order has been described including some of the limitations of
such a time-stepping scheme when used in conjunction with the possible stiﬀboundary
condition auxiliary diﬀerential equations that locally-reacting and extended-reacting require.
Moreover, an implicit-explicit Runge-Kutta 4’th order has been described and implemented
which is capable of handling the possibly stiﬀboundary ADEs.
We have demonstrated that our DG-FEM simulator is able to solve the time-domain acoustic
wave problem with a range of settings available to the user. We have implemented a set
of boundary conditions, Rigid, Frequency Independent, Locally-Reacting, and Extended-
Reacting boundaries, and made it possible for the user to mix the use of these boundaries
within the same geometry. All of the boundary conditions have been validated either
through a true physical solution, or by comparing to an existing validated SEM-simulator
written by F. Pind.
We have furthermore demonstrated that we are able to simulate on complex geometries,
and that the domain is only constrained by the capabilities of gmsh. Here we did see that
our solver introduced a small error, however as shown it is still possible to do simulations
on curved geometry with relatively high accuracy. The geometries can possible range from
very simple cube domains to complex rooms e.g. curvilinear geometries or surfaces with
benches. Meaning the solver is not constrained by any type of geometry.
We have shown that even high resolution simulations are possible, speciﬁcally we have
shown that rooms of size 5,000 m3 and 20,000 m3 can be simulated for 2 seconds with up
to ∼700 Hz with a PPW of about 8, in just 1.02 and 3.23 hours respectively. We have
also shown that one can use multiple GPUs to greatly speed up the runtime of simulations,
however multiple GPUs can also be a requirement due to memory requirements of large
problems.

15. Conclusion
15.1
Future work
15.1.1
Identify source of error when using curvilinear elements
As discussed in chapter 11 we introduce an error of size 10−7 when using curvilinear
elements. At the time of hand-in of this thesis, we have unfortunately been unable to
identify where this error is being introduced. Finding and eliminating this error should be
a top priority if continuing working with this solver.
15.1.2
Anti-aliasing in libParanumal
As we saw in chapter 11 the results using curvilinear elements with and without anti-aliasing
were very close. However, this is not necessarily true for all geometries and meshes, and
therefore anti-aliasing could be useful to implement. Though one also has to keep in mind
that the accuracy of acoustics simulations are often dominated by physical uncertainties
in estimation of boundary conditions. As anti-aliasing can be rather computationally
expensive, one therefore has to make a choice if the added accuracy is worth the extra
computation time. However, when implementing the anti-aliasing method described in
section 9.2, it could just be made an optional choice when running the simulation. That
way the user can choose between extra accuracy or faster computation.
15.1.3
Adaptive time-stepping
As discussed in section 6.3 our implementation uses a ﬁxed time-step which scales with the
resolution of the mesh. Here we found the constant C = 5 through trial and error. However,
for some setups a larger value of C is possible, which would lead to faster simulations.
Instead of using a ﬁxed time-step, it can therefore be useful to use adaptive time-stepping.
In adaptive time-stepping the size of the time-step is reevaluated during the simulation.
One method of doing this is using embedded error estimates.
Therefore, an adaptive time-stepping method can possibly greatly speed up runtime when
the problem allows for greater time-steps, and still have good accuracy by adjusting the
time-step when needed.
15.1.4
Local time stepping
Another issue that can decrease the time-step and therefore increase the runtime, is that to
guarantee stability, the time-step is constrained by the smallest element in the mesh. This
means that even if just a small part of a room model requires small elements to represent
the geometry, the time-step changes for the entire model. One way to deal with this is local
time-stepping, where larger elements can use larger time-steps than the smaller elements.
This however requires thought on how to handle numerical ﬂux when diﬀerent time-steps
are applied to neighbouring elements. An example of local time-stepping in DG-FEM can
be seen in [SGW+10], where a time-stepping scheme that allows for this is described.

==PAGE:115==
15. Conclusion
15.1.5
Realistic source function
All room acoustic simulations run in this thesis used a Gaussian pulse as initial condition,
which creates an omnidirectional pulse. However, in practice one is often interested in
using a directive source function to replicate directive sound sources such as the human
voice or loudspeakers. [ELP09] describes a generalized implementation of directive sources
on discrete-time methods.
15.1.6
More advanced boundary conditions
In this thesis we have implemented support for PR, FI, LR and ER boundary conditions.
More advanced boundary conditions, such as those where we have a coupling between
the air domain and boundary domain(s). For these boundary conditions we simulate the
porous absorber as a domain connected to the usual air ﬁlled room domain.

the amount of elements slightly, and therefore also giving us a larger time step in the
simulation.

A.4

2. Create a receiver locations ﬁle that has the receiver locations.
3. Create the vector ﬁtting ﬁle which contains the vector ﬁtting if using Local reaction
or Extended reaction boundary conditions.

A.4.2
Receiver Locations
The receiver locations are saved in as a .dat ﬁle. An example of such a ﬁle can be seen
below
2 #Amount of receiver locations
0.5 0.5 0.5 #x y z coordinates of receiver location 0
0.1 -0.1 0.6123 #x y z coordinates of receiver location 1
This data ﬁle simply has to have the name and be in the folder as determined by the
[RECEIVER] in the setupﬁle.
At the end of the simulation, the receiver location data will be outputted in
libparanumal/solvers/acoustics/data
and will have the names given in the [RECEIVERPREFIX] in the setupﬁle, with the suﬃx
_RecvPoint_xx.txt
where xx is the number of the receiver location e.g. 00 for the ﬁrst receiver location, 01 for
the second and so forth.
A.4.3
Vectorﬁt data
Vectorﬁtting is currently not implemented in libParanumal, so computing the vectorﬁts
has to be done in Matlab. A matlab script for generating vectorﬁt for LR and ER are
available in
libparanumal/solvers/acoustics/setups/setupdata/vectorfitDriverLR.m
libparanumal/solvers/acoustics/setups/setupdata/vectorfitDriverER.m
This also requires Matrix Fitting Toolbox available from https://www.sintef.no/
projectweb/vectorfitting/downloads/.
Now the scripts simply has to be run with the parameters we are interested in.
