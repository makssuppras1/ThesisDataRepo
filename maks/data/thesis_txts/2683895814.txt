Abstract
Quality control is an essential aspect of production. It ensures quality of goods before it
reaches the customer’s hands. Identifying and ensuring non-occurrence of visual defects is a
necessary part of quality control. The visual defect is a challenging problem to quantify as
the product’s defect could be of numerous types, size and colour.
In this thesis, an attempt is made to detect visual defects on Injection Moulded Plastics’
surface based on unsupervised methods.
Theoretically, The proposed method can detect
defects of any defect type without needing to define the characteristics of the defect.
A data collection system was built to create a custom data-set of Injection Moulded Plastic
Product.
The results show that the proposed method can differentiate defective products from non-
defective products to a certain extent. The result also provides visual identification of the
defect.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and Problem Statement
We are in a world where automation technologies are rapidly expanding and influencing every
dimension of life. One of the aspects which is most influenced due to automation is the way we
produce goods. Production methodologies have rapidly been adapted in automation technolo-
gies which have resulted in cheaper goods, eﬀicient processes, and reduction in wastage. It is
pertinent from the producer’s perspective to focus on continuous improvement of automation
technologies in order to provide high quality goods and improve production eﬀiciency.
One of the most important aspects of production is quality control. In manufacturing, quality
control is a process that ensures customers receive products free from defects and meet their
needs [1]. The importance of quality control could be understood from one of the recent inci-
dents involving Takata airbags which led to the biggest recall of automobiles in the automobile
industry [2]. The recall not only caused huge financial implications but also damaged the com-
pany’s reputation. Quality control strategies could be broadly classified into two categories
namely, Destructive testing and non-destructive testing. Non-destructive testing (NDT) is
an analysis technique used by industry to evaluate the properties of a material, component,
structure or system for characteristic differences or welding defects and discontinuities without
causing damage to the original part [3].
One of the subcategories of NDT is a visual inspection and it is one of the most common proce-
dures. However, as visual inspection is non-measurable and subjective, it has been hardest to
quantify. Visual inspection is an essential process where the product of interest is inspected
for visual defects before being packaged and introduced to the market. In manufacturing
quality control, automatically detecting flaws would be vital as it is not only less expensive
than recalling the product; But also, is critical for safety and company’s reputation. Most
of the visual inspections are carried out manually which are not optimal as they are usually
unfulfilling, repetitive tasks that require high amount of concentration for it to be performed
at an optimal level throughout the day. Not only this, but also it comes at a cost as humans
can perform inspections only at certain rates which could hamper the overall production time.
This has motivated researchers to develop a new automatic defect detection system with strin-
gent requirements because of the complexity and uniqueness of this problem. The developed
systems are material-specific and also vary with environmental conditions.

The current work aims to formulate an in-line defect detection system to identify defective
products specific to the plastic injection moulding industry. Injection moulding has become
the most popular plastic formation technique. It has established its application in a wide area
of industries. Production of custom-made plastic parts has created a gateway to fabricate
complex and intricate products at high quality with cost-eﬀiciency.
The applications of IMPP are widespread. From being utilised to make toys for babies to

employed to manufacture heart pumps, blood sugar monitors and heartbeat monitors. IMPP
occupy a prominent place in every household, one look around the kitchen and one could spot
containers, refrigerators, and kitchen utensils—all constituting parts made from injection
moulding machines. With its application over daily use products such as cars and handheld
gadgets, The plastic injection moulding industry has fuelled the modern consumer-driven
society’s growth.

Machine Vision has been popular technology of choice for identifying such defects on a pro-
duction line. Machine Vision acts as an eye of the computer, it enables the computer to

The Machine Vision usually constitutes of lighting

able as it is not influenced by fatigue and tiredness and Machine Vision could be deployed in

But there are certain downfalls for Machine Vision as they are susceptible to change in lighting,
colour and fail to identify undefined defects .

Objective
The objective for pursuing the project is to design and formulate a solution to identify the vi-
sual defects present on the surface of IMPP as they move across the conveyor belt of injection
moulding machine. The solution should overcome below-stated constraints in its implemen-
tation. The constraints are,
1. There could be more than one defect type occurring on IMPP’s surface.

Chapter 2
Related Work
A research was conducted to determine the most relevant methodology to achieve the objective
mentioned in Section 1.2. A Survey paper [7], formed the base of the research to explore the
problem of visual defect identification. In the paper [7], review of defect detection methods
spanning 220 scientific work was covered. The work mainly focused on visual defect detection
on materials such as textiles, metals and ceramics. Even though the present work mainly fo-
cuses on Plastic Injection Moulded Products, the technology from these congruent industries
could be utilised with appropriate customisation. The following paragraphs summarise differ-
ent methodologies used to address the problem of visual defect detection. The methodologies
are divided into three categories, represented by traditional methods, supervised methods and
unsupervised methods.
According to the survey [7], a significant amount of the scientific works encompassed statistical
and filter-based methods. Statistical methods utilise the spatial distribution of the pixels for
analysis. The statistical methods are composed of methods such as autocorrelation, Histogram
Statistics and Local Binary Patterns. Histogram Property-based methods have been proven
effective for their low computation cost [7]. They are also invariant towards translation, and
rotation [7]. Yuan et al. [8], proposed defect detection method based on Otsu Threshold
method. The proposed method was called Weight Object Variance. The work achieved a
detection rate of 94% and a false alarm rate of 8.4%. On the other hand, the filter-based
methods utilise the image features, such as edges, textures, and regions to identify the defects.
Filtering such features, especially edges, were part of the earliest attempts at image processing
[7]. Gabor filtering, in particular, was found to be the most commonly used methods for
defect detection according to the survey [7]. The Gabor Filter method aims at optimal joint
localisation in spatial, and spatial-frequency domains [9]. Among the several scientific papers
which utilised Gabor Filters, The work from Mak and Peng [10] achieved the best results [7].
The paper [10], investigated the problem of automated defect detection on textile fabrics.
However, the traditional computer vision solutions do not acclimatise well with variations
in the environmental conditions. Slight variation in brightness and natural light could alter
the algorithm’s behaviour largely. Although these traditional methods could be modelled to
detect defects of a particular defect type, the performance cannot be sustained simultaneously
on several defect types.

Deep learning-based surface anomaly detection has garnered much attention due to its ability
to extract image features. Deep learning has been applied to various applications to detect
anomalies, some of the works have been discussed below. Convolution Neural Networks have
proved their capability by delivering high accurate results for supervised problems such as
classification, target location , and semantic segmentation . In the work presented by Roohi
et al. [11], a deep convolution neural network to analyse image data for the detection of rail
surface defects was proposed. In this work, a Deep Convolution Neural Network (DCNN)
was trained with six classes of rail surfaces out of which five belonged to defect category,
and one belonged to non-defect. Yu et al. [12] proposed two separate Fully Convolutional
Networks (FCN) for the application of surface defect inspection in an industrial environment
. One of the FCN was coarsely positioned, whereas the other was finely positioned. The
experimentation achieved accurate results for the DAGM 2007 [13] dataset.
These works mentioned above were based on the supervised learning framework. The defect
detection methodologies based on supervised learning have seen great success with higher de-
fect detection rates, but the biggest pitfall for such methodologies would be the non-availability
of the data. In the current project, it is understood that the defects on the IMPP are of rare
occurrence; thereby, the number of defect samples are minuscule. It would be impossible to
train a model and acquire good results with small data size. The supervised methods need
labelled data for their training. In general, Generating a labelled dataset is a laborious task
and is not conducive for large scale industrial applications. Henceforth, it is challenging to
implement a defect detection system using supervised learning.
Unsupervised learning provides a solution for these common pitfalls as they do not require
labelled data set for their training. A prevalent methodology discovered through the literature
review was a design encompassing generative models such as GAN/autoencoder model built
solely on the product’s defectless samples. The model reconstructs the input defect sample
with the same pose without the defect. Furthermore, by the comparison of reconstructed
and original images; the defective region could be narrowed down. The model built on such
a methodology would be trained only on defectless samples. Thereby the need for sizable
defective samples would be eliminated. The defective samples are required for testing pur-
poses only. As an explicit defect is not modelled during the training phase, the methodology
could differentiate a defect less sample from a defective sample of any defect type. Thereby
satisfying the requirements for the project. This reconstruction based anomaly detection is
built on two popular models by name
1. Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)
2. Autoencoders
Even though there are no instances for using generative models to detect defects in the field
of injection moulding industry, the methodology has demonstrated its effectiveness in various
fields such as textile industry, steel industry, and medical industry to name a few.

Chapter 3
Analysis
The different techniques employed in visual defect detection discussed in the related work
chapter could be broadly classified into three categories: traditional methods, supervised
methods, and unsupervised methods.
Traditional methods are extensively used for the purpose of visual defect detection. Most of
the traditional methods are built based on statistical or filter-based methods. Nevertheless,
these methods are prone to environmental changes. Conventional methods generally cannot
provide generic defect detection capabilities as they heavily rely on the pattern, material, and
texture. It is a laborious process to build a generic algorithm which could be adopted to
detect defects of various defect types. Most of the times, the defects on IMPP are of non-
recurrent types. It is hard to build an algorithm to detect defects satisfying our constraints
using conventional methods considering these points.
Neural Networks have become a revelation in the field of image processing, especially for
solving classification problems. Neural Networks, based on Supervised methods have achieved
remarkable results. Supervised methods are well functioning, easy to implement and to use [7].
However, significant shortcomings for utilising supervised methods for visual defect detection
are as follows.
1. Lack of Defect Samples: The ratio of defective samples and non-defective samples are
vastly imbalanced. Thereby a model built on such imbalanced data would be greatly
unreliable. The type of defect which occurs on the IMPP are vast and are sometimes
not recurrent. A supervised model would not be able to classify the anomalies on which
they are not trained.
2. Manual Labelling is expensive: Supervised models are data-hungry. They require a vast
amount of data. As there can be different types of IMPP, creating labelled dataset for
each type would consume a lot of human resources.
In the view of reasons mentioned above, supervised learning methodologies fail to address
objectives of the problem statement.
Considering these factors, the methodology of a generative unsupervised model built solely on
non-defective samples and comparing the reconstructed image with the input to identify the
defection region could be considered most relevant for our problem statement. These models

require only non-defective samples which are available at large. The unsupervised methods
do not require manual labelling, thereby the process of generating data set is not cumbersome.
As the proposed methodology does not explicitly train on a predefined defect, the method
could distinguish defects belonging to any defect type. Replicating the methodology for dif-
ferent types of IMPP should be possible as the process remains the same barring the input
image fed to train the model. As seen in the related works section, there are mainly two

The GAN uses methodology of min-max two-player game and have shown promising results

compared to GAN. VAE are more likely to produce images closer to the trained data set. In

4.1
Supervised Learning
Supervised is one of the learning methods employed in the field of Machine learning, wherein
the ground truth is utilised in learning.
That is, the prior knowledge(label) of how the
output should be is already known, and the algorithm learns the function which could best
approximate the connection between the input (x) and output (y). Mathematically it could
be represented with as y = f(x). The naming of the method as supervised learning is intuitive
because one could imagine the learning process to be similar to a teacher who knows the right
answer supervising their students to obtain the right answer and learning could be stopped
once the pupils have achieved acceptable performance.
The supervised algorithms are primarily used to solve classification and regression problems.
In Summary, all the data utilised in this method should be labelled. The algorithm learns to
predict the output from the given input.
4.2
Unsupervised Learning
Contrary to supervised learning, unsupervised learning employs a methodology where labelled
data would not be necessary. The goal of the method is to model the underlying structure
present in the input data. It is similar to a situation where there are no right answers or
teacher supervision, yet pupils learn through exploration on their own. Unsupervised learning
is adopted in exploratory analysis, Density Estimation, and dimensionality reduction.

Activation Functions
Activation functions are a vital entity, contributing to the control of the transformation applied
during forward propagation and plays an essential role during backpropagation. During the
early days of neural networks, sigmoid and tangent hyperbolic were the most commonly
used activation function. Nevertheless, they have become obsolete due to the introduction
of activation functions with more eﬀicacy. The sigmoid function is still widely used as the
last layer of binary classification problems where its property of mapping the signals into the
interval of [0,1] is preferred.
For multi-class classification problems, utilising softmax function is suitable. The function
maps the signals into the interval of [0,1] and sums its output values to one. This helps in
representing the results as probabilities.
Recently Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) [29] has been the most popular choice for activation
function. This function maps the signal into the interval of [0, ∞]. The function is intuitive
and works eﬀiciently during backpropagation. As the weights cannot be re-initiated once set
to zero during backpropagation, it could lead to Dying ReLU. Different variants of ReLU
have been proposed over the years to tackle the issue of Dying ReLU. Leaky ReLU [30] is
one such example wherein the function maps the signal into the interval of [−∞, 0] in order
to circumvent the dying ReLU problem . A different variant of leaky ReLU was introduced
wherein the certain range of the negative slope is set randomly during the training phase.
This variant is called as Randomised Leaky Rectified Linear(RReLU) [31]. Due to the random
nature of the activation function, it could overcome the over-fitting problems.
Even though there are various variants of the ReLU available, it is still unclear if these
alterations could outperform the vanilla form of ReLU consistently.

Deep generative models
Deep Generative Models are unsupervised learning processes which aim to learn the true
distribution of the input data samples and generate new data point with some variations.
The Deep generative models could be categorised into two major groups based on how they
learn the true distribution. They are, explicit density-based and implicit density-based deep
generative models. Explicit generative models have an explicit likelihood function defined
which could be maximised, whereas implicit models do not need an explicit definition of a
density function. Famous examples for explicit and implicit deep generative models are VAE
and GAN respectively.

6.2.3
Data Augmentation
Data Augmentation is the technique to elevate the number of training data points artificially.
It also helps the model generalise better as it introduces the variations in the training data
set. Some of the data augmentation techniques include flipping the images horizontally and
vertically, rotating the image by certain degrees, cropping a part of the image. The purpose
of training the VAE is to help it learn the structure of the IMPP. Therefore data augmenta-
tion techniques which fail to convey the overall structure of the model were not considered.
The techniques such as Random Rotation and flipping the Horizontally and Vertically were
implemented as it would help the model generalise the IMPP in different orientations other
the ones in the training set. By Default re-sampling filter was set to Nearest, but irregulari-
ties in the edges were identified. Therefore, the re-sampling filter for data augmentation was
changed to Bilinear, which eliminated the issue.
6.2.4
Model Architecture
The architecture of the VAE contains two entities, an encoder which translates the input
image into a lower-dimensional space. Furthermore, a Decoder tries to reconstruct the Image
with the help of the lower dimensional representation. The latent variable (Z) in VAE is prob-
abilistic, unlike autoencoders. µ and σ are the approximates from the encoder network. The
Z is sampled from these parameters, as the sampling node does not support backpropagation.
A stochastic node N(0, I) is introduced, which enables backpropagation. This procedure is
known as Reparamatrisation trick and it was described in the Section 4.12.1.
The loss function for this model would be a combination of reconstruction loss and reguraliza-
tion term. The loss function aims to maximise ELBO. Reconstruction loss makes sure that
the reconstructed images are closer to the input image while the regularisation terms apply
constraints to make sure the learnt probabilistic distribution (Z) is similar to a prior distribu-
tion which is Gaussian distribution. The theory behind VAE and Variational Inference were
discussed in detail in section 4.11 and 4.12. Refer to these sections for a detailed explanation.
The encoder and decoder architecture were developed based on deep CNN models like VGGNet
and AlextNet. To create such a network, CNN were used as they work well for images. For
creating the network’s encoder architecture, Blocks of four Convolution Layer consisting of
4x4 kernel were created. The stride was fixed at 2 to achieve spatial down-sampling. This
was done instead of deterministic spatial function like Max-Pooling. Along with these, the
padding was set to one. All the convolutional layers were followed by a batch normalisation
layer along with an activation layer of ReLU. Two layers of fully connected layers then followed
the convolutional layers. The fully connected layer was used for µ and σ, to sample the latent
space variable z. The Fully connected layers were not followed by any activation function as
any non-linearity does not accompany σ and µ .
Four convolutional layers were created for the network’s decoder layer, each with a kernel
size of 3x3 and stride of one. The standard zero-padding were replaced with the replication
padding. The up-sampling layer consisted of the nearest neighbour method, which was scaled
by 2. Even the decoder’s layers are followed by batch-normalisation and an activation layer of
ReLU. The decoder’s final layer, which consists of the reconstructed image, does not have any
activation layer. The utilisation of setting the upsampling layers with the nearest neighbour,
replication padding and avoiding the use of max pool layers were adopted from the work
Hou et al. [41]. The work showcased stability in training the VAE and improved quality of
reconstruction. The overall architecture of the VAE along with the terms explained above is

Defect Detection
The aim of the project was to identify IMPP with visible defects on their surfaces.
The
following section explains the metric used to detect a defective product and the procedure

6.3.1
Anomaly Score
The ELBO is a combination of two terms: reconstruction loss and a regularisation term. The
reconstruction loss is mathematically expressed as ∥x −ˆx∥2. It is squared pixel-wise difference
between the input image and the reconstructed image. Simultaneously, the regularisation term
ensures that the learnt latent space is similar to the prior distribution; Gaussian distribution
in our case.
The defective regions of the IMPP would not be present in the reconstructed image. There-
fore the reconstruction loss of the defective image will on the higher side, as the number of
dissimilar pixels would be higher for the defective image. As the model has been trained on
the non-defective samples, The reconstructed image of the non-defective image would be very
similar to the input image. Therefore the number of dissimilar pixels would be comparatively
lesser for non-defective samples.

As ELBO is a combination of such two terms, it was ideal for being used as the anomaly score
to identify defective samples.
6.3.2
Defect Region Identification
Pixel wise subtraction of reconstructed image and input image was performed to identify
the possible regions of defects on the IMPP. This method worked mainly because of the
VAE’s capability to ensure the non-reconstruction of defective regions of IMPP. The intuition
behind the non-reconstruction of defective regions could be understood as follows. During the
training, the model would have learnt to map the most critical features of the IMPP to a lower
dimension. When a defective input image is provided as an input to such a network, The
model is unlikely to map the features containing the defects to the lower dimension. Thereby,
the decoder network would not be able to reproduce the defective regions.
Once the residual of the potential defective regions were obtained from the pixel-wise sub-
traction, the steps represented in the flow chart 6.8 were followed to make the residual more
visible and indicate the defects in an intuitive manner. The greyscale residual were hardly
visible, therefore to ensure pronounced residuals, A binary threshold was applied; and with
its application, all the pixels above the threshold acquired maximum a value and the pixel
below the threshold were fixed to zero. The highlighted region from the threshold image was
changed to a prominent colour red, as it is easy to spot the red region on the white background
of IMPP. Concurrently dark pixels were made transparent so that the residual region could
be highlighted on the original image. The flowchart describes these steps sequentially and
the images corresponding to the blocks in the flowchart represent the changes applied to the
IMPP during each process.

Anomaly Score to identify Defective IMPP
Figure 7.5: ROC curve for ELBO, Reconstruction Loss, and KL Divergence as Anomaly score
VAE provides three potential anomaly score parameters which could be used to distinguish
defective and non-defective IMPP. The potential parameters considered were ELBO, Recon-
struction Loss, and KL Divergence. Reconstruction loss is a metric that evaluates how well
the input image is reproduced. KL Divergence is a regularising term, and it provides insight
into if the given image belongs to the trained distribution. The ELBO is the term maximised
during the training of VAE and is a combination of Reconstruction Loss and KL Divergence.
ROC curves for each of these metrics were plotted, See Figure 7.5. Judging from the AUC
for each of the metrics, It could be concluded that KL divergence would not be an optimal
choice as an anomaly score. Nevertheless, it was hard to differentiate between ELBO and
Reconstruction Loss.
To further evaluate the metrics, the least cost line was found with the use of ROC curve while
keeping the cost of false-positive and cost of false-negative at 0.5. The optimal threshold for
ELBO was calculated to be 164 and 154 for Reconstruction Loss. The data points above this
optimal threshold could be considered as Anomalous IMPP. Corresponding error rates at the
optimal threshold were 10.2% for ELBO and 11.30% for Reconstruction Loss. The difference
of error rates between the two metrics was minuscule; with ELBO’s performance as anomaly
score being slightly better. For further implementation ELBO was considered as the preferred
choice for anomaly score. The IMPP with anomaly scores greater than the threshold would
be considered a defect.

Discussion
The aim of the project was to identify anomalous samples of IMPP. The solution was designed
considering the environmental conditions and surroundings of an injection moulding machine.
The project had to adhere to constraints mentioned in the problem statement to be practically
viable. The project had to overcome challenge of unbalanced data-set, and the solution had
to be generic towards different types of defects.
The solution encompassing unsupervised generative models was feasible as it offered answers
to most of the defined problems. The VAE could be trained only on non-defective samples,
so the need for extensive data set of defective samples was overcame. The method did not
need an explicit definition of a defect; therefore, the method could identify defects of any
type in theory. As the method required unlabelled data set, the data collection was easier
than generating labelled data set.
Thereby, the solution could be feasible for widespread
commercial applications. On top of these aspects, the method also offered a way to specify
the possible defect region to make it easier for the user to identify the defects. Even-though
the method has immense potential; it also has some glaring drawbacks and they are discussed
in the following paragraphs.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
A defect detection system based on a deep generative model to identify visual defects on the
plastic injection moulded was presented in the project. The project utilised a probabilistic
framework based on VAE to categorise a given sample into either defective or non-defective
distribution. The method utilised an anomaly score provided by the VAE for the classification.
The project further provides an intuitive way to localise the defects to guide the user visualise
the defective region.
The results showed that the method was able to separate the defective products from the non-
defective products to some extent. However, the method has critical shortcomings concerning
identifying small-scaled defects, irregular reconstruction and to visualise defect regions of
different defect types. Interestingly, there was a direct correlation between the model’s clas-
sification performance and the number of pixels representing a given defect.
Taking these aspects into consideration, Even though the methodology has some notable
shortcomings. The unsupervised method offers much potential. Further improvements to the
technique could make the methodology viable for various applications. The VAE utilised in
the formulation of the project is VAE in its basic form. Different variations of the VAEs have
been proposed in recent years. The variations of VAE inculcated in [20] and [19] could be ex-
perimented with, to conjure if these variations perform better for our use case. Such methods
could further improve the separation between the defective and non-defective distributions.
