Project Formulation 
Atom transfer radical polymerization (ATRP) is a controlled radical polymerization technique that 
has been extensively used to produce numerous polymers of tailored size and architecture [1]. For 
20 years, surface-initiated ATRP (SI-ATRP) has been the most popular and reliable method to 
produce polymers from a surface because of its high robustness and versatility [2], despite its 
dependence on a metal catalyst. An alternative metal-free route for ATRP using an organic-based 
photoredox catalyst has been proposed in 2014 [3] and adapted to surfaces by 2016 [4]. This new 
process suppresses an important problem that is intrinsic to any classic ATRP process, which is the 
presence of metal impurities at the end of the process [4]. Therefore, the absence of metal residues 
after the polymerization is advantageous for biomedical and electronic applications that are highly 
sensitive to metals [4], even at small concentrations.  
The general objective of the thesis is to investigate and establish a synthetic metal-free SI-ATRP 
procedure that enables the formation of copolymer brush films of high thickness (i.e. above 100nm).  
First and foremost, a literature study is performed to give an extensive overview about the state of 
the art of the ATRP, SI-ATRP and metal-free ATRP techniques, as well as reviewing what has been 
investigated in metal-free SI-ATRP so far in the literature. The aim of this study is to gain an 
extensive overview about the studied techniques and gain insight into the experimental conditions 
used and therefore identify appropriate conditions for the experimental work. 
Then, many routes will be investigated experimentally by varying the type of monomers or the 
experimental conditions used. The polymerizations will be initiated from silicon surfaces in the first 
instance.  The aim of this experimental work is to find the optimal conditions that lead to copolymer 
brush films of enhanced thickness. 
Finally, all the produced materials will be investigated by ellipsometry, x-ray photoelectron 
spectroscopy (XPS), FTIR and contact angle measurements.  
If time allows and if a successful route could be established, further experiments on other surfaces 
could be undertaken to investigate the extent to which the established route can be applied to other 
systems and thus be expanded to applications of interest such as biomedical devices or electronic 
materials.

Abstract 
 
This thesis explores the synthesis of polymers by metal-free atom transfer radical 
polymerization (ATRP) in water and their characterization. Fluorescein is used as a water-
soluble metal-free catalytic dye, thereby avoiding the use of transition-metal based catalysts 
that require an additional removal step after polymerization. The aim of the work is to establish 
an effective water-based metal-free atom transfer radical polymerization (ATRP) procedure and 
to investigate experimental conditions that could influence the process, for future applications 
in metal-free surface-initiated ATRP (SI-ATRP) reactions. The importance of establishing a 
water-based metal-free ATRP route is paramount not only for electronics and biomedical 
applications where the presence of metals is an issue, but also to pave the way toward a 
sustainable, environmental-friendly and facile method to build controlled polymers. 
Initial fluorescein-mediated metal-free ATRP polymerizations of methyl methacrylate (MMA) 
in dimethylformamide revealed polydispersity indexes above 1.3 and significantly higher 
experimental molecular weights than expected, suggesting a limited polymerization control and 
slow initiation. However, both lowering the light intensity and monomer concentration slightly 
improved the control over the polymerization.

1. Introduction 
1.1 – Atom transfer radical polymerization (ATRP) 
1.1.1 – Characteristics and mechanism of ATRP 
Atom transfer radical polymerization (ATRP) is a controlled radical polymerization (CRP) 
method [1,2] for the synthesis of narrow polydispersity index polymers (PDI<1.3-1.5) [1] of 
high and tailored number-average molecular weight and of well-defined architectures [1,7]. Its 
term is derived from the atom transfer reaction step that allows a steady and controlled chain 
growth upon synthesis of the polymer chains [2]. It is among the most popular and versatile 
CRP methods because of its easy experimental setup [7], its high versatility toward different 
types of monomers, solvents and reaction conditions [7] and the possibility of obtaining pure 
commercially available initiators and catalysts that are ready to use [7] without any required 
preparation. Beyond the synthesis of classic linear polymers of low polydispersity (i.e. linear 
polymers without branching), ATRP is also commonly used to synthesize functional polymers 
[8,9] and tailored polymers of complex architectures such as star-shaped polymers [1,6,9], 
block copolymers [1,9], graft polymers [9] or hyperbranched polymers [1,9]. The applications 
of polymers synthesized by ATRP are numerous, as shown in Scheme 1. 
 
Scheme 1: Polymer architectures possible by ATRP and their concrete applications in chemistry and 
materials science [9]. 
In ATRP, as seen in the mechanism shown in Scheme 2, the control of the polymerization 
process is managed by the presence of a stable dormant alkyl halide initiator or a halide-attached

growing polymer chain (Pn-X) whose chemical bond is reversibly cleaved through a redox 
reaction that is catalyzed by an organometallic complex consisting of a low oxidation state 
transition metal coordinated with multidentate ligands [2,3,4,7]. Upon alkyl halide (Pn-X) bond 
cleavage, the reduced low oxidation state (activator) organometallic complex (Mtm/Lz) is 
selectively seizing the labile halide from the growing chain, commonly a chlorine (Cl) or 
bromine (Br) [2,3] atom that can straightforwardly be coordinated to the transition metal (most 
commonly copper(CuI) [1-4] or Ruthenium(RuII) [5]) to form a higher oxidation state 
(deactivator) oxidized complex (X-Mtm/Lz) [11].  
 
Scheme 2: Mechanism of ATRP [3]. The Pn-X dormant species’ bond is cleaved to yield the reactive 
growing chain radical (Pn.) to be further reacted with a monomer (M) while the terminal atom (X) is 
transferred to the organometallic complex Mtm/Lz to ensure a controlled polymerization [2,3]. 
In general, the activating reaction (ka) is significantly slower than the reverse deactivating 
reaction (kd) that leads back to the dormant growing polymer chain (Pn-X), thus revealing that 
each individual growing chain is only active for a very short period of time before going back 
to an inactive dormant state [4,6]. Therefore, the equilibrium constant of the halide bond 
cleavage is highly shifted towards the reactants [11], i.e. the dormant growing polymer chain 
(Pn-X) and the reduced transition metal complex (Mtm/Lz) are predominant compared to their 
respective active radical growing polymer chain (Pn·) and oxidized transition metal complex 
(X-Mtm+1/Lz) species.  This consequently allows an enhanced control on the polymerization 
[4,6] where very low PDI [6], predetermined molecular weight polymers of tailored architecture 
can be obtained [6], because all polymer chains grow step by step at a controlled rate [11].  
Furthermore, in ATRP, as for all controlled radical polymerization techniques, the polymer 
molecular weight is found to proportionally increase with the ratio between the consumed 
monomer and the concentration of the initiator present initially in the polymerization reactor 
(i.e. the molecular weight increases linearly with respect to the conversion) [6,11,12] and the 
polymerization reaction is found to follow first-order kinetics in relation to the concentration 
of monomer [6].

Several experimental factors can influence the effectiveness and the efficiency of a classic 
ATRP reaction and require to be taken into consideration.  
First, as discussed previously, the equilibrium constant (KATRP) and the kinetics are the key 
elements that control and characterize the entire ATRP process. A small equilibrium constant 
indicates that the dormant growing polymer chain specie is present in excess in the reaction 
medium and therefore hinders any potential side-reactions to occur with its radical form since 
it is significantly “under-represented” in comparison to the dormant specie. Furthermore, if the 
deactivation process is kinetically faster than the activation (i.e. 𝑘𝑑≫𝑘𝑎), the growing polymer 
chain radical form is only “living” for a short time, before being reversibly deactivated again 
==PAGE:15==
4 
 
for a significantly longer time [6]. As a result, both contributions prevent a high availability and 
high lifetime of the radicals and hence effectively reduce most of undesired early chain 
termination, branching or chain-transfer reactions, yielding to very narrow PDI’s [6].  
Secondly, a significant amount of transition metal catalyst is required for a classic ATRP 
reaction to be effective [9] because of inevitable termination reactions that can irreversibly 
“immobilize” the catalytic complex in its inactive form and therefore gradually slow the 
reaction over time [6] since the immobilized catalytic complexes cannot react any more. If a 
high concentration of catalyst is added, then the fraction of irreversibly deactivated species is 
very low and does not significantly affect the polymerization rate.   
Then, the solvent should be cautiously selected so that it can dissolve the monomers, the 
catalytic complexes and the final polymer product, while being aware that the solvent does also 
affect the equilibrium constant and the propagation (kp) and termination (kt) rate constants [9]. 
As for the reaction temperature, it does influence the thermodynamics of the ATRP reaction and 
therefore dictates the reactivity of each specie [9]. The temperature also affects the activation 
rate constant (ka) and the stability of the catalytic complexes because of the exothermic nature 
of the ligand-transition metal complex formation in situ [9]. An increase in pressure can also 
enable the preparation of much higher molecular weight polymers [9] than at atmospheric 
pressure, primarily because the termination process is disfavored and the propagation process 
is favored when the pressure increases [9].  
Finally, the nature and the structure of both the ligands and the initiators as well as their 
concentrations do highly impact the equilibrium constant (KATRP of Eq.1) for the catalytic 
complex activation/deactivation reaction [10] and the deactivation rate constant (kd) [10], 
therefore determining the ATRP polymerization rate (the higher KATRP, the faster the 
polymerization) and the control of the polymer’s polydispersity index (the higher kd, the 
narrower the polydispersity index and the higher the control on the polymer architecture) [10]. 
The choice of ligands and initiators should be carefully performed, depending on the desired 
polymer molecular weight, reaction conditions and the type of monomers used [10,11]. 
Common examples of ATRP monomers include styrenes, acrylates, methacrylates, acrylamides 
and methacrylamides [2,4].

interesting observation is that most initiator structures contain a halide (Br or Cl) on a 𝛽 position 
relative to a carbonyl group, a cyano group or a benzene ring [13,14,48], i.e. alkyl halides.  The

1.1.2 – Evolution of ATRP

catalytic complexes in the purification step [7]. Considerable research has been undertaken to

concentrations are traditionally required because of termination side reactions that occur during 
the polymerization and of the limited catalytic activity of the transition metal complexes [18] 
that are observed in classical ATRP and that inevitably lead to an accumulation of the 
deactivator (i.e. the oxidized catalytic complex) with time [40]. If the catalyst loading is small,

The earliest developments for ppm-level transition metal catalyzed ATRP are activators 
regenerated by electron transfer ATRP (ARGET-ATRP) [39] and initiators for continuous 
activator regeneration ATRP (ICAR-ATRP) [40]. In both cases, the strategy was to design a 
system that could continuously regenerate the activator in-situ by the addition of a reducing 
agent [39,40] or a free radical initiator [40] respectively. In ARGET-ATRP, the presence of an 
external reducing molecule (i.e. ascorbic acid) allows the activator species to be continuously 
regenerated [13,39], whereas in ICAR-ATRP, the regeneration of the activators is achieved 
through a reduction of the deactivator with radical species that originate from a thermal 
degradation of an added initiator [40]. Consequently, in both cases, significantly lower amounts 
of transition-metal catalysts are required to operate the ATRP reaction [40] since the 
regeneration of the activator hinders the aggregation of the deactivator in the reaction mixture 
[42] and limits the decrease of the reaction rate with time.  
Further developments of low metal concentration ATRP systems are electrochemically 
mediated ATRP (eATRP) where a cathodic current from an external electrode is used to yield 
an effective reduction of the deactivator and regenerate the activator [42], a supplemental 
activator and reducing agent ATRP (SARA-ATRP) where an additional metallic wire or plate 
(such as Cu0) is used as both an activator and a reducing agent for the deactivator [13,41] and 
==PAGE:18==
7 
 
finally a photoinduced ATRP [43,44] where narrow bandwidth LEDs, UV light or sunlight 
effectively photoreduce the deactivator to regenerate the activator [13,43,44].  
Due to the active regeneration of the air-sensitive activator specie, eATRP is compatible with 
the presence of oxygen in the atmosphere and its reaction medium can be reused [13].  SARA-
ATRP is also interesting since no harsh reaction conditions are usually used and because it does 
not require inert atmospheres, nor thorough experimental procedures to remove impurities [13]. 
An interesting observation is that eATRP and photoinduced ATRP allow the activator to be 
accurately and selectively regenerated by external factors, respectively an electric current and 
light, that are not intrinsic to the reaction system and conditions [18]. However, the activation 
rates cannot be modulated by either an electric current or light, thereby indicating that the 
polymerization cannot be controlled by these external factors [18], i.e. the reaction cannot be 
selectively launched or stopped by an electric current or light [18].  
In conclusion, all these variations of ATRP, including the photoinduced ATRP system that was 
initially developed, still required a transition metal catalyst, usually Copper-based [43] or 
Iridium-based [44]. In order to find an alternative route without any metal catalyst, Hawker et 
al. published a paper in 2014 [17] in which they propose a photomediated metal-free ATRP 
route that conserves all the characteristics and advantages of a classic ATRP process, while 
eliminating the metal’s presence. This has been made possible by using an organic dye that 
effectively and selectively initiates and mediates a photoredox reaction upon light radiation 
[17].  
1.1.3 - Metal-free ATRP 
1.1.3.1 – Mechanism of metal-free ATRP 
After several years of research toward ATRP systems that operate on significantly lower 
transition-metal catalyst amounts, the development of an ATRP system by Hawker et al. [17] 
that can be run without any transition metal constituted a significant milestone in the field. An 
organic catalytic dye, 10-phenylphenothiazine (PTH, c.f. Scheme 3) was originally used as a 
substitute for the transition metal to mediate the reaction [17].  
The mechanism of (photoinduced) metal-free ATRP involves a three-step process, as shown in 
Scheme 3 [17,18]. The catalytic dye (Cat, i.e. PTH) is first excited by a photon to reach an 
excited state (Cat*, i.e. PTH*). Thorough investigations showed that the lifetime of PTH’s 
excited state (PTH*) is 4.5 ns [18], which is significantly less than for a transition metal catalyst

have been conducted by Y. Yagci and S. Jockusch and demonstrated that the triplet state takes 
precedence over the singlet state in the reaction mechanism [58]. The highly reducing excited 
state of the catalytic dye (Cat*, i.e. PTH*) reacts then with the growing chain (Pn-Br) to generate 
an active growing chain radical (Pn●) that can thus react with a monomer and an oxidized radical

polymer of increased molecular weight for a given irradiation time [18]. However, an important 
consideration to take into account is that free uncontrolled radicals may also directly emerge 
from a homolytic cleavage of C-Br bonds when high irradiances are used [18], that may lead to

especially for biomedical and electronic applications, few scientific papers have been published 
on the field, mainly because of its recent development. Another important observation is that 
no metal-free ATRP route has been investigated so far in water. Therefore, there are numerous 
opportunities for further improvements and research to be carried out to expand metal-free 
ATRP toward new horizons.

1.2 – Surface-initiated atom transfer radical polymerization (SI-ATRP)  
1.2.1 – SI-ATRP and polymer brushes 
Surface-initiated ATRP (SI-ATRP) is an ATRP reaction that is conducted from a surface. All 
parameters and mechanisms discussed in the previous section for ATRP reactions are valid for 
SI-ATRP as well [7]. The major difference of SI-ATRP with respect to classical ATRP is that 
the initiation occurs at the surface of a given substrate instead of happening in solution [14], 
thereby affecting the kinetics of the reaction [13].  
The result of a SI-ATRP – and SI-CRP in general – are polymer brush films where one chain-
end of each polymer brush is attached to a substrate’s surface by a stable covalent bond [13,14]. 
SI-ATRP enables a strict control over the composition, structure, molecular weight and film 
thickness of the polymer brushes [7,13]. 
Polymer brushes can be grafted in a surface by two techniques, namely grafting-to and  
grafting-from [13,14,15,16], as shown in Figure 4B [13]. The grafting-to method uses pre-
synthesized polymers that will be attached to the surface either by physical adsorption or by 
formation of a chemical bond with the surface, given that the pre-synthesized polymer possesses 
a surface-reactive end-group [14,16]. A disadvantage of this method is that it yields low grafting 
densities and poor layer stability and thickness because of steric repulsions between 
neighboring polymers at the surface [14,35,36]. These factors hinder the applications of these 
grafting-to polymer brush films as nonbiofouling surfaces [14] or as effective surface lubricants 
since low friction surfaces demand stable and thick polymer brush films [16]. The grafting-
from method, which is based on a direct in-situ surface-initiated polymerization (e.g. by SI-
ATRP) of free monomers on an activated substrate, yields significantly higher grafting densities 
[13] and the chain propagation is only diffusion-limited with no steric limitation [36], therefore 
enhancing the spreading of the polymer brushes toward the solution and leading to extended 
polymer film thicknesses instead of coiled up “mushroom” or “pancake” conformations 
(favored by polymer-polymer interactions) that are highly characteristic of low grafting density 
surfaces [14,15,16]. Therefore, by tuning the surface concentration of initiating sites, the 
grafting-from method allows the preparation of polymer brushes of controlled grafting density 
and architecture [7] and can be applied to a broad range surface types and shapes [7]. These 
positive characteristics of the grafting-from make SI-ATRP on a surface covered by ATRP-
compatible alkyl-halide initiators one of the most popular and most reliable techniques to form 
polymer brushes. Furthermore, ATRP, in contrast to anionic or cationic polymerization, does 
==PAGE:27==
16 
 
not require extremely dry experimental conditions [35]. The choice of SI-ATRP is further 
supported by its high robustness, versatility towards numerous types of monomers and tolerance 
to grow block copolymers of low polydispersity [14].

amphiphilic block copolymers. These polymers are composed of at least two blocks where one

nature, these block copolymer brushes that can be of interest for the fast-growing fields of 
organic electronics, bionanotechnology and molecular sensing [60]. However, the use of high 
concentrations of metal are problematic for such applications [17] and conventional SI-ATRP 
is therefore not a suitable route.

An important aspect of SI-ATRP is the control on the surface concentration of initiating sites, 
since the initiator density directly dictates the grafting density and thus impacts the reaction 
kinetics, as well as the thickness and the homogeneity of the polymer brush film obtained [13]. 
In comparison to classic ATRP, SI-ATRP has a relatively low concentration of initiating groups 
on the surface [13]. The immediate consequence of that situation is that the concentration of 
deactivator (i.e. the oxidized transition metal complex) is limited [13] since it requires a reaction 
between the growing chain and the activator (i.e. the reduced complex) for it to significantly 
increase. Strategies to overcome this issue and ensure a persistent radical effect that keeps a 
control on the chain growth and homogeneity are the addition of free “sacrificial” initiators in 
solution [13,37] or the direct addition of deactivators [36]. An excess of deactivator species 
with respect to activator complexes is necessary to reduce the rate of polymerization (c.f. Eq.1) 
and thus control the reaction while minimizing termination reactions by the persistent radical 
effect [13, 55].  
The initiator structure and the solvent used during its grafting on the surface directly impacts 
the grafting density [13]. Higher grafting densities and hence higher film thicknesses are 
obtained if triethoxysilane-terminated initiators are used instead of monochlorosilane-
terminated initiators [13]. If the length of the carbon spacer between the silylated head groups 
of the initiator structure increases, increased grafting densities and brush thickness are 
witnessed for the polymerization of MMA [13], although the highest molecular weight is 
obtained for a spacer length of 11 carbons atoms [13]. As for the solvent, the use of toluene 
instead of 1,2-dimethoxyethane yields a higher grafting density [13].

it.  Examples of procedures to introduce alkyl halide ATRP initiators before polymerization on

As the grafting density increases, higher rates of propagation are observed as a result of both 
an enhanced concentration of the activator species and an increased viscosity at the interface of 
the growing chain ends [13]. However, the steric hindrance originating from other neighbouring 
brushes may also limit the growth of polymer brushes in comparison to solution-grown

polymerization in SI-ATRP [57]. For curved convex surfaces, enhanced polymerization rates 
are observed in comparison to planar substrates, which is due to the decreased chances of chain-
end termination, i.e. the spacing between each chain-end increases as the polymer brushes grow, 
which decreases the probability of two chain ends to encounter themselves and terminate [13]. 
However, for curved concave or porous surfaces, lower molecular weights and lower 
polymerization rates are expected, as a result of a hindered diffusion of the reactants and of 
steric constraints due to confinement [13,59]. A decrease in pore size was found to significantly 
reduce the molecular weight of the polymer brushes [59].  
Each of these conditions have to be taken into consideration to optimize the SI-ATRP process 
and yield the desired outcome, i.e. polymer brushes of pre-established molecular weight, well-
defined thickness and low dispersity.  
Finally, the selection of the monomers to be used is critical since it dictates the physical and 
chemical properties of the obtained film, such as its hydrophilicity/hydrophobicity, its polarity 
or its architecture [31]. A rational selection of the monomers to be polymerized, combined with 
the possibility to construct well-defined architectures by SI-ATRP (c.f. Figure 5) is a formidable 
tool to form polymer brush coatings that give desired properties to surfaces that are originally 
not inherently present.

weight and polydispersity.  Polymer brushes cannot directly be characterized by these methods, 
except if they are cleaved from the surface or if sacrificial initiators have been added to grow 
polymers in solution [13], although this method may not accurately determine the molecular

1.2.2 – Evolution of SI-ATRP 
As for ATRP in solution, SI-ATRP also went through the same developments toward ATRP 
systems using significantly lower catalyst concentration with the emergence of SI-ARGET, SI-
ICAR, SI-eATRP, SI-SARA and SI-photoinduced ATRP systems [13,14].  
The mechanisms, as well as the (dis)advantages for these alternative surface-initiated ATRP 
techniques are similar to their counterparts in solution, but their kinetics are intrinsically 
different [13,14], as it was highlighted previously for classical SI-ATRP.  
Interestingly, the growth of polymer brushes is affected by the SI-ATRP system selected. As an 
example, polymer brushes grown by SI-SARA ATRP can reach a predetermined thickness in 
less time and decreased temperatures in comparison to classic SI-ATRP [13]. In SI-eATRP, the 
distance separating the active electrode and the substrate from which brushes are grown can 
influence the kinetics of the growth [13]. For photoinduced SI-ATRP, the polymer growth and 
polymerization rates were shown to be different, in comparison to classic SI-ATRP [13].  
Metal-free SI-ATRP has only been investigated since 2016, when Hawker et al. adapted their 
original metal-free procedure to surfaces [47]. As discussed in the previous section, only few 
research papers have been conducted in the field of metal-free ATRP and, specifically for metal-
free SI-ATRP, research has been even scarcer, thus leaving numerous opportunities for further 
improvements and investigations to be exploited. 
1.2.3. Metal-free SI-ATRP 
Surface-initiated metal-free ATRP on silicon wafers and nanoparticles has been first reported 
by C.J. Hawker et al. in 2016 [47], where the polymerization was shown to be well-controlled 
and with a linearly increasing brush film thickness and molecular weight with irradiation time 
[47]. PMMA homopolymer brushes (92.1nm thickness after 4 hours under 4.8μW/cm2) as well 
as PMMA-b-PTFEMA and PMTEMA-b-PTFEMA block copolymer brushes have been 
successfully synthesized in the work [47].  Ever since this work, only a few monomers have 
been utilized to build polymer brushes by metal-free SI-ATRP (c.f. Table 5). 
Like for classical SI-ATRP, the first step of the metal-free SI-ATRP process is to chemically 
graft the initiator onto the surface of the material, as seen in Scheme 8 (Step c). Since the 
initiator structures cannot always be commercially available, a synthesis is often required to 
produce it (Scheme 8, steps a and b). Once the initiator is grafted onto the surface, the 
polymerization can be started (Scheme 8, step d).

The initiation efficiency is paramount for SI-ATRP because it directly determines the grafting 
density [52]. BiB-based initiators display a lower reactivity than BPA-based ones, therefore 
leading to a decreased initiation efficiency which consequently yields low grafting densities, 
thereby compromising the structure of polymer brushes that collapse into mushroom structures 
[52]. Thorough investigations on silica nanoparticles confirmed that the use of BPA-based 
initiators did considerably yield higher grafting densities in comparison to BiB-based initiators, 
under the same conditions [52]. However, it is also possible to increase the grafting density by 
adding higher amounts of catalytic dye (PTH) that compensate for the low initiation efficiency 
of BiB-based initiators [52], although it increases the cost of the process.  
A key advantage of metal-free SI-ATRP is the ability to use photomasks to selectively grow 
polymer brushes in desired portions of the surface, since the polymerization does only occur if 
the catalytic dye is struck by incoming photons [47]. Thus, if a given surface is covered from 
light with a photomask, no polymers are grown from it, thereby leading to a spatially controlled 
polymerization pattern [47]. Another strategy would be to irradiate a surface in the absence of 
any monomer or catalytic dye prior to a polymerization with a photomask to yield a selective 
degrafting of the initiating sites on the irradiated area, thus also leading to spatially controlled 
polymerization patterns [14]. However, in this case, the polymerization would only occur in 
areas of the surface that were not exposed to light.  
Once the polymerization is complete, halide end-groups do remain on the polymer chains. Their 
removal is important to ensure a safe and reliable processing, as well as a stable end material 
with an extended lifetime [46]. If halide end-groups (such as Cl, Br, etc.) are left on the surface 
without any post-treatment, they tend to decompose at high temperatures (such as polymer 
processing temperatures or repeated exposure to sunlight), leading to the formation of highly 
acidic compounds (HCl, HBr) that can then chemically attack and damage the material [46], 
thereby hindering the use of high processing temperatures (that would be necessary to melt the 
polymers) and critically harming the long-term stability of the material [46].  
Inspired from their work on metal-free ATRP, the Hawker group designed a selective and 
versatile metal-free removal of halide end groups using PTH and light [46]. As shown in 
Scheme 11, the reaction converts reactive halide end groups to inert hydrogen end groups under 
mild conditions [46]. The operation had a positive impact on the thermal resistance of the 
materials, which have demonstrated a much higher thermal stability in their dehalogenated state 
in comparison to their initial halogenated state [46]. Besides, no halide end-groups remained at 
the surface after the treatment [46].

1.3 – Objectives of this thesis 
Whereas metal-free ATRP mediated by catalytic dyes in organic solvents has been extensively 
investigated with methacrylates since its discovery in 2014, no extensive research on a water-
based metal-free ATRP system has been conducted so far. Yet, the development of an aqueous 
route to polymers of controlled molecular weight and architecture by using an efficient dye that 
is not only highly reducing in its exciting state, but also entirely soluble and stable in water, 
constitutes an innovative and inspiring opportunity to expand the scope of metal-free ATRP/SI-
ATRP toward more sustainable and biologically-compatible systems. Thereby, this work seeks 
to investigate this aqueous metal-free ATRP pathway. 
Given that metal-free (SI-)ATRP is only at its early stage, not much has been experimentally 
carried out to adapt the method to an extended range of monomers, since most of the works 
focused on methacrylates (mainly methyl methacrylate). One of the objectives of this work is 
to provide an extensive study of metal-free ATRP for novel and water-soluble monomers.  
As for the polymer brush films synthesized by metal-free SI-ATRP, their thickness never 
exceeded 100nm and no extensive research on the optimization of polymer brush thicknesses 
by metal-free SI-ATRP has been conducted up to now. The investigation of an aqueous metal-
free SI-ATRP is of paramount importance, since water interacts with the metal-based catalysts 
in water-based transition-metal SI-ATRP. Indeed, water is known to isolate the halide atoms 
from the dormant species, the initiators and from the deactivating species [53,54]. This 
hindrance still constitutes a challenge since it produces polymer brushes of uncontrolled 
dispersity [53,54], requiring a thorough synthetic procedure with a high excess of monomer 
reactants [54]. Consequently, the investigation of a water-based metal-free SI-ATRP route that 
effectively yields high thickness (block co-) polymer brushes with low polydispersity is a 
unique opportunity to set a new state-of-the-art for the ATRP field and provide a convenient 
alternative to classical aqueous transition-metal SI-ATRP.  
Given all these current challenges and the need to advance metal-free (SI-)ATRP toward new 
horizons, this work seeks to investigate an aqueous metal-free (SI-)ATRP route that would lead 
to controlled polymers with predetermined molecular weights and polymer brushes with 
enhanced thicknesses for a wide range of monomers.

2. Experimental plan and strategy 
 
The initial experimental plan is to investigate metal-free ATRP in solution and in silicon wafers, 
using 10-phenylphenothiazine (PTH) and fluorescein as organic catalytic dyes.  
For polymerizations in solution, a two-step strategy is applied:  As a first step (c.f. Scheme 12a), 
PMMA (and PDMAEMA, which will be used for water-based systems) will be first synthesized 
following a reported procedure by Y. Yagci et al. [67] using fluorescein and PMDETA in DMF, 
to evaluate if the procedure can be reproduced with the available light source and experimental 
conditions and identify which condition changes would lead to highly controlled polymers. The 
choice of a fluorescein-based system as a catalyst is motivated by its high water solubility and 
by its reported ability to mediate metal-free ATRP [64,67]. Monomer concentration, the light 
intensity (at constant distance between the light source and the reactor) or the monomer to 
initiator ratio are examples of tunable parameters. After the optimal conditions are found for 
DMF-based polymerizations, water-based systems will be investigated (c.f. Scheme 12b). The 
polymerizations of water-soluble monomers such as DMAEMA or MPC will be carried out. 
The initial experimental conditions for this step will be based on the results and insights 
obtained by the first polymerizations carried out in DMF. But experimental conditions will also 
be varied to optimize the reaction and gain knowledge about their impact in water-based 
systems. This way, a complete overview of water-based fluorescein-mediated metal-free ATRP 
will be obtained. 
For polymerizations from silicon wafer surfaces (c.f. Scheme 12c), the initiator will be 
synthesized and grafted to the surface before the polymerization, that will be mediated by PTH 
(synthesized according to Hawker et al. [47]). The type of monomer, the reaction time and 
experimental conditions will be modified to investigate a method that generates high brush 
thicknesses. Finally, block copolymers will be grown to prove the chain-end fidelity and 
investigate what metal-free SI-ATRP conditions lead to controlled block copolymer brushes of 
high thickness. The polymer brushes will be cleaved at the end using hydrofluoric acid to 
investigate NMR and SEC of the cleaved brushes.

Most molecular weights from Table S2 are very high (Mn>20000 g/mol), which also correlates

seek ways to reduce the PDI value even further. Because of time constraints and of the priority 
to develop a water-based system, further investigations on this system have not been conducted.  
It is important to state that the calculated theoretical molecular weights from Tables 6 and S1 
are directly proportional to the conversion (c.f. Eq.7 from page 39), which was obtained by 
gravimetry. This method, although acknowledged and used in numerous scientific papers in the 
field, is not the most accurate way to determine the monomer conversion, mainly because the 
precipitation process may not efficiently isolate all polymers (including short polymer chains) 
from the solution, thus yielding an underestimation of the monomer conversion value. 
Consequently, in further experimental sections, the conversion will be determined by NMR, 
using an internal standard that is stable in water (trioxane) to monitor the decrease of the 
monomer’s signal peaks with time. Despite the uncertainty on the conversion values from Table 
6 obtained by gravimetry, the statement that the obtained polymers’ molecular weights are 
significantly higher than expected is still relevant. Indeed, the maximal theoretical molecular 
weight (i.e. at 100% monomer conversion) for PMMA polymers grown with monomer to 
initiator ratios of 50:1, 93:1 and 186:1 are respectively 5000, 9300 and 18600 g/mol. As 
mentioned above, all polymers obtained in Tables 6 and S1 display higher molecular weights 
(obtained by either NMR or SEC) than the maximal theoretical molecular weights values. 
Therefore, the experimental molecular weights are not strictly proportional to the monomer-to-
initiator ratio and indicate an issue with the initiation of the process, because of the 
unexpectedly high molecular weights acquired.  
==PAGE:46==

Several hypotheses may explain that behavior. First of all, a fraction of the initiator may be 
degraded under the reaction conditions applied. Indeed, if the sensitive C-Br bond is cleaved 
under the irradiation conditions used, the initiator would be degraded to form another chemical 
specie that would not be able to participate in the initiation step of the polymerization, thereby 
leading to a higher monomer to initiator ratio than expected which enables “virtually” higher 
molecular weights to be achieved. This hypothesis could be verified if lower experimental 
molecular weights would be obtained with lower light intensities since it would cleave fewer 
C-Br bonds. On the basis of the results displayed in Table S1, such a trend could not be clearly 
identified. Alternatively, the decomposition of the initiator could also be monitored by NMR 
(c.f. Figure A9 in Appendix A) by measuring the ratio between degraded (1.17ppm) and native 
protons (1.99ppm) adjacent to C-Br bonds in the initiator, since the signal peaks for these 
hydrogen atoms are shifted upon a C-Br bond cleavage. Unfortunately, the NMR peaks 
corresponding to the degraded initiator overlap with the peaks of the polymerized methacrylates 
(at around 1.1ppm, c.f. Figures A6.1 and A9 in Appendix A) and therefore do not allow the 
possibility to propose reliable interpretations on this matter.  
Another hypothesis would be that the initiation step is kinetically slow. This situation, 
especially if the propagation rate is substantially faster than the initiation rate, induces a fast 
molecular weight increase at low monomer conversions, since the monomers do propagate on 
a fewer number of chains than expected. Thus, the experimental molecular weights obtained 
would be significantly higher than expected at low monomer conversions, since less polymer 
chains would grow on activated initiators. Two explicit indicators for slow initiation could be 
straightforwardly identified. Either the experimental molecular weights obtained are shown to 
be consistently higher than the theoretical molecular weights for low conversions (when 
plotting the molecular weight against the conversion) or the experimental molecular weights 
are shown to decrease with time (or with conversion) while the PDI steadily increases as well. 
The latter observation may indicate that shorter polymer chains are being grown on initiators 
that were activated at late stages of the polymerization, which is inherited from a slow initiation.  
Finally, undesired chain terminations or chain transfer reactions may occur as a result of 
photochemical cleavage as well.  These can also modulate the average molecular weight of the 
obtained polymers. This hypothesis will be discussed later in relation with Figure 10. 
Because this work prioritizes the establishment of a controlled water-based system and since 
time was limited, no further investigations on the root causes for the witnessed high molecular 
weights were conducted. Nonetheless, the impact of the monomer concentration, the monomer

through kinetic studies (c.f. Entries I-4, I-5, I-6 of Table S1) to explore whether a controlled

As seen in Figure 8, at a light intensity of 3.3mW/cm2, the molecular weight increases

is consistent with a slow initiation. This behavior is not witnessed for the polymerization 
conducted with both lower monomer concentrations (1.55M) and light intensity (1.65mW/cm2)

reaction within the first hour of reaction. The explanation for this noteworthy observation can 
be reasoned by combining the knowledge acquired from all the different experiments. 
As discussed previously, a fraction of initiators in the reaction mixture could be degraded under 
UV light because of a light-induced carbon-halide cleavage that would form radicals which 
may undergo irreversible side-reactions, thus leading to low initiation efficiencies. This would 
also directly influence the true monomer-to-initiator ratio of the reaction mixture as well, thus 
leading to significantly higher molecular weights than predicted. But this behavior should in 
theory occur more frequently for higher light intensities, since more initiators would be 
degraded at high light intensity and therefore yield higher “virtual” monomer to initiator ratios 
and higher molecular weights.  This completely contrasts from what is observed from Figure 8 
because lower molecular weights are witnessed for 3.3mW/cm2 light intensity in comparison 
to polymers obtained under a 1.65mW/cm2 light intensity.  
But, despite this contradiction, it is also important to consider that if the stability of the initiator 
is compromised, then higher light intensities might not only trigger additional cleavage of the 
carbon-halide bonds from the initiators, but also enhance the cleavage of carbon-halide bonds 
from the active growing chains and yield termination of these, thereby leading to the generation 
of low molecular weight polymers. These termination reactions may explain why higher 
molecular weights are, in fact, not observed for 3.3mW/cm2 light intensities in comparison to 
1.65mW/cm2. However, this hypothesis will be questioned later along with the results obtained 
with Figure 10b. 
The observed decrease in molecular weight with time may also be attributed, as mentioned 
before, to a late activation of remaining unreacted initiators, thus leading to a late formation of 
short growing polymer chains which are significantly smaller than polymer chains that would 
have been active since the beginning of the reaction. This hypothesis is particularly relevant 
since initiator efficiencies were shown to be very low (c.f. Table 6), thus suggesting that 
unreacted initiators are abundant within the reaction mixture at later stages of the reaction, even 
though a significant fraction of them may be degraded. Therefore, available initiators may be 
susceptible to react and form short growing polymer chains at later times, thus inducing a 
decrease in the experimental molecular weight with time for polymers grown at higher light 
intensities of 3.3mW/cm2.  
==PAGE:49==
38 
 
As for the polymer grown at a lower light intensity of 1.65mW/cm2 (c.f. Figure 8), the molecular 
weight increases with time, but this increase becomes less significant as the reaction time 
increases. This decrease in the molecular weight increase slope might also be partly attributed 
to the late formation of short polymer chains, although lower light intensities (1.65mW/cm2) 
would be expected to generate fewer late initiations and chain termination reactions than 
stronger light intensities (3.3mW/cm2).  
Besides, it would also be reasonable to consider a decrease in the rate of polymerization in later 
stages of the reaction as a result of a decreasing concentration of monomer with increasing time 
(c.f. Eq.1 on Page 3). To this respect, the semi-logarithmic kinetic plot (c.f. Figure 10b) may 
help to bring light to this possibility. 
Finally, as seen in Figure 8b, the polydispersity index increases with time for all conditions 
investigated, which further supports the idea that short polymer chains are being produced over 
time due to late initiation or that undesired termination reactions occur.  From Eq.2 (on Page 
3), the polydispersity index is expected to decrease with time since it is inversely proportional 
to the monomer conversion. The results obtained in Figure 8 are thus inconsistent with what is 
expected and give further evidence of the uncontrolled nature of the carried-out 
polymerizations.

Numerous hypotheses have been proposed to explain the results and the plots obtained

light intensities yielded higher molecular weight polymers, the occurrence of termination and 
chain transfer reactions, a decrease of the rate of polymerization with time, a slow initiation or 
even a late activation of unreacted initiators. The curves of Figure 10b do not explicitly expose 
any explicit sign of termination, late initiation, nor implying any significant changes in the rate 
of polymerization. However, it is impossible to claim that these phenomena do not occur at all. 
Furthermore, the hypothesis of the late activation of unreacted initiators cannot be strictly 
verified with Figure 10b as well, because its contribution to the concentration of propagating 
radicals may be very small and insignificant in comparison to the amount of already active 
propagating radicals in the reaction mixture.

In conclusion, the polymerization of MMA in DMF allowed to gain precious insights on how 
several parameters can affect the system (e.g. the light intensity, the monomer concentration, 
etc.) and on the identification of the issues that may potentially arise for an aqueous system. 
The polymerization of MMA in DMF was not found to be controlled. The polydispersity index 
(PDI) values were rather high and even increased over time under all the conditions 
investigated. When compared to the literature results based on fluorescein-mediated metal-free 
ATRP (c.f. Table S2), the PDI values obtained in this work are however very satisfactory, even 
showing some improvement. A promising result is the linear trend of the molecular weight 
increase with respect to the conversion for low monomer concentrations (1.55M) and low light 
intensity (1.65mW/cm2) in comparison to higher concentrations (3.1M) and higher light 
intensity (3.3mW/cm2) which were not satisfactory. In the future, it would be recommended to 
investigate even lower monomer concentrations and light intensities, as well as varying the 
fluorescein and electron-donor amine concentrations, to potentially optimize the processes’ 
controllability. In this respect, a special focus on limiting the fast molecular weight growth is 
paramount.  Because of time constraints and since the polymerization of MMA in DMF does 
not constitute an exclusive novelty for the field, no further time was spent on it.

Metal-free ATRP of 2-(dimethylamino)ethyl methacrylate (DMAEMA) 
in dimethylformamide (DMF) 
Because of time constraints, no kinetic study for the polymerization of DMAEMA in DMF has 
been carried out. Nonetheless, two polymerization reactions were conducted to investigate 
whether fluorescein is able to effectively polymerize DMAEMA without the presence of any 
electron donating amine in DMF before carrying out the polymerization in water and, thereby, 
perform a more extensive investigation of the water-based system, which has never been 
characterized or reported in the literature before the beginning of this work1.  
 
Scheme 14: Polymerization of DMAEMA in dimethylformamide (DMF) using ethyl 2-bromo-2-

Therefore, an important observation is that DMAEMA was successfully polymerized in DMF, 
even leading to gelation at high monomer concentration for 18h (c.f. Entry II-1 of Table S3). 
This is a promising preliminary result since it demonstrates that DMAEMA, a water-soluble 
monomer, can be polymerized with fluorescein and without any electron donor under UV light. 
An interesting property of DMAEMA, which was one of the main reasons for its selection for 
the forthcoming experiments, is that it possesses an intrinsic tertiary amine group that could act 
as an electron donor as well, thereby theoretically eliminating the need of additional electron 
donor amines in the reaction mixture.  
It is however important to bear in mind that DMF as a solvent is not pure and that it may contain 
diethylamine traces (up to 0.05% [90]) that can also participate as electron donors. Although

Even though the polymerization of DMAEMA could be obtained in absence of any electron 
donor, it does not imply that the reaction is controlled.  As seen in Table S3 (c.f. Entry II-1) in 
section 5.5, the polydispersity index as well as the molecular weights achieved are very high, 
thereby raising questions concerning the control over the reaction and the uncertainty around 
the ability of DMAEMA to effectively act as an electron donor amine as well. This uncertainty 
remains an important fact to bear in mind for the forthcoming investigations in water-based 
systems. Because of time constraints and of the interest to engineer a water-based system with 
DMAEMA, no further research on this potential issue has been carried out in DMF.  
Indeed, investigating those issues would require some kinetic studies to enable an analysis on 
the effect of electron donor amine species’ addition (and lowering the monomer to initiator 
ratio) on the control of the polymerization. But the study has to be carefully designed because 
of the high concentration of DMAEMA involved. Indeed, if DMAEMA is effectively engaged 
as an electron donor, it is already present in huge excess in comparison to other designed 
polymerizations (such as MMA or MPC) where the concentration of the electron donor can be 
100x smaller (e.g. [M]:[I]:[Fl]:[e- donor] = 100:1:0.1:1]). By itself, this does not constitute any 
direct problem except that the MMA and MPC systems cannot be compared to the DMAEMA 
system, but it can be an interesting question to investigate how the electron donor’s 
concentration impacts the polymerization kinetics and control, since no study on fluorescein-
mediated metal-free polymerization of DMAEMA has been carried out in the literature.  
The effect of the addition of electron donor has been previously investigated in the literature 
for the fluorescein-mediated metal-free polymerization of MMA in DMSO [64]. The study 
highlighted a slight increase of the polymerization control with higher electron donor loadings 
[64]. More recently, C.Bian et al. [84] demonstrated that the addition of electron donor yielded 
an increase of the polymerization rate in water because of a higher activation rate, which implies 
a faster molecular weight buildup over time.  
If DMAEMA actively participates as both a monomer and an electron donor and if a high excess 
of electron donor constitutes an improvement of the polymerization’s control, then the 
polymerization would have been controlled and leading to polymers of narrow PDI, which is 
==PAGE:55==
44 
 
not what has been observed in Table S3. However, this lack of control may also be due to an 
enhanced activation rate yielded by DMAEMA’s electron donor property (if it can behave as 
one), or by an excessively high monomer concentration. Alternatively, it is also important to 
mention that only one PDMAEMA sample could be characterized with the available SEC 
system and that it is also highly probable that the observed peak may not belong to the 
PDMAEMA itself, but to an external high-molecular weight impurity that may originate from 
elsewhere. Indeed, if the SEC profile corresponded to PDMAEMA, there would have been no 
issue on reproducing the measurement for other similar samples. This aspect remains rather 
uncertain and thus, no trustworthy conclusions can be taken from the results obtained by the 
SEC analysis.  
In the future, investigations on the polymerization conditions and kinetics of DMAEMA or 
other amine-based monomers may enhance our understanding and knowledge of how 
fluorescein-mediated metal-free ATRP works and how the electron donating amine species (or 
its absence) influences the system, thereby allowing the possibility to rapidly tune the 
controllability of the polymerization and achieve optimized systems in a faster and more 
convenient way.  
In the meantime, the priority of this project is to investigate the polymerization of DMAEMA 
in water and optimize the process to achieve enhanced control over the molecular weight and 
dispersity of the obtained polymers.

recommended to use high amounts of fluorescein catalytic dye in solution to overcome any 
potential degradation, using the light source of this work. However, the lack of control 
witnessed in the previous sections cannot be solely attributed to the degradation of fluorescein, 
that is not significant enough to have a serious impact. As a way to further investigate and 
==PAGE:73==
62 
 
question the ability of fluorescein to be a suitable catalytic dye, cyclic voltammetry was carried 
out.  
Cyclic voltammetry constitutes a very efficient method to assess the suitability of a catalytic 
dye to effectively mediate a metal-free ATRP polymerization. Indeed, cyclic voltammetry 
allows the determination of the oxidation potential of the catalytic dye or even whether the 
radical cation/anion is stable in a given solvent and within specific conditions [18]. These 
features constitute important requirements for the controllability of the polymerization to be 
guaranteed, since a degradation or an instability of the radical cation/anion specie may 
constitute an important limitation to the controlled polymerization process [18].  
The aim of the cyclic voltammetry experiment presented in this work is to investigate whether 
fluorescein is a suitable catalytic dye to mediate aqueous metal-free ATRP processes. However, 
because cyclic voltammetry had only been carried out after the first polymerizations in water, 
cyclic voltammetry was also utilized as a way to investigate whether the lack of control in the 
conducted aqueous reactions could be due to fluorescein.   
 
Figure 19:   Cyclic voltammogram for an aqueous solution of fluorescein (0.1mM) and triethylamine 
(1mM) under argon atmosphere and irradiated at 1.65mW/cm2 (under similar conditions as for the 
polymerization mixture) as a function of the scan rate. The reference electrode is a standard calomel 
electrode (SCE), the working electrode a glassy carbon electrode and the counter-electrode a platinum 
electrode. The temperature is 21±1°C. 
The Figure 19 reveals a few interesting results. First, the peak for the oxidation of fluorescein 
can clearly be noticed between +0.25 and +0.75 V vs SCE (depending on the scan rate). Under 
inert atmosphere, this oxidation peak corresponds to the loss of electrons from fluorescein anion 
==PAGE:74==
63 
 
radicals to regenerate the ground state of fluorescein and deactivate the growing chain. A 
repetition of the experiment without triethylamine revealed a similar voltammogram, which 
excludes the possibility for this peak being related to the oxidation of triethylamine.  
The reverse reduction peak cannot be distinctly seen in the cyclic voltammogram at high scan 
rates (i.e. above 200mV/s). However, at 200mV/s and 100mV/s scan rates (red and black curves 
respectively), a small peak can be noticed at around -0.2V vs SCE, which may correspond to 
the reverse reduction reaction, i.e. the formation of the fluorescein anionic radical species from 
its excited state and the electron transfer from triethylamine.  
This result reveals that the formation of the anionic radical is not highly favored, leading to the 
impression that the electron transfer is irreversible. This behavior can be explained  by the fact 
that the preferred pathway for the fluorescein’s de-excitation is by fluorescence, since it has a 
fluorescence quantum yield of Φ=97% (in ethanol) [88], i.e. most of the fluorescein molecules 
quickly fluoresce from a short-lived (in the nanoseconds range) singlet state to get back to their 
ground state rather than undergoing an intersystem crossing toward a long-lived (in the 
milliseconds range) triplet state. 
As mentioned by Theriot et al. [89], one of the key requirements for an efficient metal-free 
ATRP mediating dye is a high rate of intersystem crossing [89], i.e. a favored triplet state 
formation, because of the enhanced lifetime of the triplet state which would enhance the 
probability of an electron transfer to be efficiently carried out [89]. Fluorescein is a disfavored 
candidate to this extent. Eosin Y, an alternative water-soluble catalytic dye, performs much 
better than fluorescein on that aspect, since its fluorescence quantum yield is narrowed down 
to only Φ=45% (in ethanol) [88]. Unfortunately, Eosin Y’s absorption spectrum [90] does not 
allow it to be efficiently excited below 450nm with the used UV lamp.  
Despite the high fluorescence quantum yield, a fraction of excited fluorescein molecules can 
undergo an intersystem crossing to generate triplet states that have an enhanced probability of 
receiving an electron from triethylamine to yield a anionic radical that activates the growing 
chain C-Br bonds, thereby allowing metal-free ATRP to be conducted. Furthermore, most 
catalytic dyes that have been investigated in the literature were reported to have relatively short 
excited state lifetimes (less than 10 nanoseconds) [18, 89] in their singlet states and their 
efficiency to mediate metal-free ATRP was also limited by their own fluorescence quantum 
yields [18].  
==PAGE:75==
64 
 
The key result from the cyclic voltammetry measurement is that fluorescein can effectively be 
converted to an active anion radical in presence of triethylamine, but that its production is 
hindered because of photochemical limitations related to the fluorescein’s fluorescence 
quantum yield. The consequence of this finding is that a high fluorescein concentration would 
be required for an efficient metal-free ATRP to be sustained over time, especially if it is in 
competition with UV-induced free radical polymerization (c.f. Section 3.6) as it was witnessed 
for the polymerization of DMAEMA.  
This statement correlates with the findings obtained in Figure 13, where an enhanced control 
over the polymerization could be obtained with increasing catalyst concentrations.  
As a final note, the light source’s emission spectrum has also an important contribution to the 
success of the polymerization, since a higher overlap between the emission spectrum of the 
light source and the absorption of the catalytic dye would promote the metal-free ATRP 
pathway. Thus, a light source with a red-shifted maximal intensity (with respect to the lamp 
used in this work) would also promote the efficiency and the effectiveness of fluorescein to 
mediate metal-free ATRP and thereby potentially enhance the control over the polymerization.  
In the future, it would be recommended to select a suitable light source-catalyst pair to optimize 
the overlap between their respective emission and absorption spectra and thereby allow metal-
free ATRP to be conducted under optimal conditions.

3.6. 
Mechanistic interpretation of fluorescein-mediated metal-free ATRP 
in water 
Both polymerizations of DMAEMA and MPC revealed some insightful results that may help 
to elucidate the mechanism of fluorescein-mediated metal-free ATRP and to understand how 
the system behaves. In the literature, apart from extensive studies about the kinetics of metal-
free ATRP [18,84] and about the characterization of catalytic dyes [18], no thorough research 
about the competition between UV-induced free radical polymerization and metal-free ATRP 
has been carried out. Yet, the polymerizations with DMAEMA revealed that UV-induced free 
radical polymerizations have a significant contribution and impact the control over the 
polymerization.  
Before any mechanistic interpretation is given, it is important to summarize the key findings 
that helped its construction:  
a) MPC could not be polymerized in absence of fluorescein and triethylamine (c.f. Entry 
IV-F of Table S7), as expected from a fluorescein-mediated metal-free ATRP process. 
However, DMAEMA significantly polymerized under the same conditions (c.f. Entry 
III-F of Table S5), revealing that a free radical polymerization occurs for DMAEMA in 
the absence of fluorescein and any electron donor, thus showing that the tertiary amine 
acts as an electron donor and induces free radical polymerization. 
b) The free radical polymerization of DMAEMA also originates from the monomer and 
not exclusively from the initiator’s C-Br cleavage, since DMAEMA polymerizes in 
water in the absence of fluorescein, triethylamine and the initiator (c.f. Entry III-H of 
Table S5). Neither MPC nor BMPHA did initiate any UV-induced free radical 
polymerization in absence of fluorescein and  (c.f. Entries IV-F and IV-I of Table S7).  
c) Increasing the concentration of fluorescein catalyst dramatically decreases the 
molecular weight (obtained by NMR) of the obtained PDMAEMA and increases the 
initiation efficiency (c.f. Figure 13), indicating that controlled radical polymerization 
(CRP) is in competition with UV-induced free radical polymerization and that an 
increase of fluorescein enhances the CRP process. Indeed, an increase in fluorescein 
catalyst concentration would hinder UV-induced side-reactions to occur and provide a 
more effective radical activation and deactivation process. 
d) A lower light intensity yields higher concentrations of propagating radicals and higher 
monomer conversions, but slightly lower polymer molecular weights in the case of the 
polymerization of DMAEMA (c.f. Figure 11).  
==PAGE:77==
66 
 
e) No DMAEMA polymerization is obtained in absence of light (c.f. Figure 12). Thus, 
both free radical and controlled radical polymerizations are indeed UV-mediated.  
On the basis of these observations, a straightforward relationship between the presence of a 
tertiary amine in DMAEMA monomers and the observation of uncontrolled polymers grown 
by UV-induced free radical polymerization can be made. Furthermore, amine radical cations 
have a reported chemical reactivity with radical species [92] that further supports the idea that 
the tertiary amine of DMAEMA may be reactive under UV light and thereby induce self-
polymerization and side reactions.  
Besides, the addition of fluorescein is shown to enhance metal-free ATRP. On one hand, the 
addition of fluorescence may significantly decrease the light transmission within the reaction 
mixture, thereby decreasing the probability of a photon reaching a monomer or an initiator to 
undergo UV-induced uncontrolled reactions. On the other hand, the addition of fluorescein 
promotes the reductive cycle because of the enhanced probability of participating in an electron 
transfer with the electron donor amine (c.f. Section 3.5 for the photochemical explanation), 
thereby favoring the metal-free ATRP pathway.  
Consequently, for the polymerization of DMAEMA in water, metal-free ATRP competes with 
UV-induced free radical polymerization and other photochemical side reactions, as shown in 
Scheme 19. As for the polymerization of MPC, no evidence of any competing mechanism has 
been noticed based on the control reactions and on the “on-off” experiment (c.f. Section 3.4).  
The Scheme 19 thus presents a mechanistic interpretation to help understand the observations 
witnessed in Section 3.3 for the polymerization of DMAEMA.  
Once UV light is applied to an aqueous solution of DMAEMA in presence of fluorescein, a 
fraction of the UV light is absorbed by the fluorescein, which thereby induces a controlled 
metal-free ATRP reaction that leads to PDMAEMA monomers of controlled molecular weight 
and architecture. However, a fraction of the UV light is still transmitted and can hit an initiator 
or a monomer to respectively yield a C-Br bond scission or generate radical species within the 
monomer structure. In both cases, undesired side reactions or free radical polymerizations may 
occur, which can lead to side products, inactive monomers (i.e. monomers that no longer 
possess an alkene group that is prone to undergo metal-free ATRP) or polymers of uncontrolled 
molecular weight and architecture. If such processes are significant, an SEC analysis would 
have revealed these undesired products, e.g. by monitoring the PDI or observing whether many 
peaks or bimodal peaks co-exist in a given chromatogram.

results.

The initiator functionalization is confirmed because of the appearance (in comparison with 
pristine silicon wafers) of two broad peaks: the first peak within 3200cm-1 – 2600cm-1 and the 
second peak within 2000cm-1 and 1300cm-1, corresponding to alkane and ester functional 
groups respectively. The broad and asymmetric nature of several peaks is due to the manual 
baseline that was applied to the spectra. A very high contact angle (around 90°) also indicates a 
successful grafting of the initiator on the surface (c.f. Table 7 on Page 73).  
The polymerization of MPC by PTH was also confirmed by FTIR, where the alkane peak (3200-
2600cm-1) and the ester peak (2000-1300cm-1) were significantly lowered, and where a broad 
and significant peak within 3600-3200cm-1 could be observed, corresponding to the O-H 
stretching of water. Indeed, PMPC is highly hygroscopic and the presence of the water O-H 
stretching peak is a reliable indicator of an effective polymerization, although it is also 
important to mention that a similar peak is also witnessed in the same region for initiator-
functionalized wafers. Given this observation, it cannot be concluded that MPC was indeed 
polymerized.  
However, a more noteworthy observation is the significant decrease of the 2350cm-1 and 
2100cm-1 peaks which are inherited from the pristine silicon wafers. Although these peaks could 
not be strictly identified, they are suspected to correspond to Si-O stretching modes from the 
pristine wafers. If this assumption is correct, then the observed decrease in signal intensities for 
these peaks in PMPC polymerized with PTH indicates a success of the polymerization, since 
the PMPC brushes would form films that “screen out” the signals from the underlying silicon 
wafer atomic vibrations. Furthermore, it also suggests that the MPC polymerized with 
fluorescein has failed, since no decrease in those peaks has been witnessed. This statement is 
in accordance with the optical microscopy results, which demonstrated that a clear pattern (i.e. 
a considerable polymer brush thickness) could only be seen for brushes grown with PTH (c.f. 
Figure 20).  
Contact angle measurements may also provide additional insights since the contact angle 
decreases when PMPC is polymerized on the surface of initiator-functionalized wafers, due to 
the hydrophilicity of the PMPC brushes. Thus, a decrease in contact angle would suggest a 
successful polymerization, although no information about the brush thickness can be obtained. 
As seen in Table 7, the contact angle decreases from around 90° (corresponding to the initiator 
functionalized silicon wafers) down to around 50° for both PTH and fluorescein mediated SI-
ATRP. Although this result might suggest that the surface polymerization of MPC was 
==PAGE:84==

successful with both PTH and fluorescein as catalysts, it is also important to point out that even 
a very thin layer of PMPC brushes may induce a significant decrease in contact angle, because 
of its enhanced hydrophilicity compared to the initiator-functionalized surface. Thus, these 
contact angle results show that both methods work, but both FTIR and optical microscopy 
suggest that significantly higher polymer brush thicknesses are obtained with PTH in 
comparison with fluorescein.

In the future, the optimization of the reaction conditions or the selection of a more efficient 
catalytic dye may lead to improved brush thicknesses and allow tailored (block co-)polymer 
brushes to be formed in a controlled way.

4. Conclusion 
 
The aim of this thesis is to expand the field of metal-free (SI-)ATRP to non-hazardous aqueous 
systems with water-soluble monomers for electronics and biomedical applications.  
Fluorescein has been selected as the catalyst, because of its excellent water solubility, suitable 
absorption spectrum with the available narrow bandwidth UV lamp, ready availability, low 
price and its known ability to effectively mediate metal-free ATRP in organic solvents [64,67].  
The polymerization of MMA in DMF was first investigated with fluorescein and PMDETA as 
electron donor. No control over the polymerization could be achieved with the investigated 
conditions, as very low initiation efficiencies, increasing PDI values with time and unsteady 
molecular weight increases were observed. Both lowering the light intensity and the monomer 
concentration slightly helped achieving enhanced control over the polymerization.  
DMAEMA, which was selected because of its water-solubility and its embedded electron-
donating amine group, could be polymerized without any additional electron amine in presence 
of fluorescein in DMF and subsequently in water. Furthermore, no polymerization was 
witnessed in the absence of light, thus confirming that the polymerization is exclusively 
mediated by fluorescein and light. However, DMAEMA polymerized in the absence of 
fluorescein and even without any initiator under UV light, indicating that UV-induced free 
radical polymerization competes with fluorescein-mediated metal-free ATRP. An enhanced 
control could be achieved with enhanced fluorescein catalyst loading (0.5% of monomer 
concentration) and with mild UV light intensity (1.65mW/cm2) according to NMR, but the 
absence of any SEC data does not allow to confirm that the polymerization was controlled. 
MPC, a water-soluble monomer, could also be polymerized with satisfactory control (according 
to NMR) at high catalyst loadings (0.5% of monomer concentration) under mild UV light 
intensity (1.65mW/cm2) and in presence of additional electron donor (20eq. of NEt3 with 
respect to fluorescein). No polymerization has been noticed either in the absence of light or in 
the absence of catalyst and electron donor, revealing that MPC selectively polymerizes by 
fluorescein-mediated metal-free ATRP. However, SEC results indicated high PDI values and 
the presence of low molecular weight polymer chains, indicating that the process is not as 
controlled as it was suggested by NMR.  
==PAGE:86==
75 
 
The very first aqueous metal-free SI-ATRP reactions were carried out with fluorescein as 
catalyst and triethylamine as electron donor, leading to a successful outcome, since the contact 
angle was shown to decrease from 90° down to 50° for PMPC homopolymer brushes. However, 
according to FTIR and optical microscopy, the polymer brushes obtained are very thin, 
revealing that either the reaction conditions or fluorescein are not effective for the synthesis of 
PMPC polymer brushes. 
In the future, the choice of suitable light sources, of water-soluble catalysts with high radical 
formation efficiency upon excitation and of light-stable monomers coupled to an optimization 
of the reaction conditions and of the catalytic dye may pave the way to efficient and highly-
controlled aqueous metal-free (SI-)ATRP pathways.

10-phenylphenothiazine (PTH) synthesis  
The procedure to synthesize the PTH dye is inspired from Hawker et al. [47]. To a Schlenk tube 
are added NaOtBu (134mg, 1.4mmol, 1.4eq.), phenothiazine (199mg, 1mmol, 1eq.), RuPhos

chromatography column with a 5% EtOAc/hexane eluent. TLC using a similar eluent was used 
to assess which collected fractions containing the product exclusively. These fractions are then 
assembled and dried under reduced pressure to yield the 10-phenylphenothiazine product 
(249mg., 90% yield). The NMR spectrum for the PTH catalytic dye is shown in Appendix A, 
Figure A3.

5.7. 
Surface functionalization with the 6-(chlorodimethylsilyl) hexyl 2-
bromo-2-methylpropanoate initiator  
Pristine laser-cut silicon wafers (water contact angle of 0°) are cleaned by sonication 
subsequently in acetone, ethanol and water for 5 minutes each. In between each sonication step, 
the wafers are thoroughly rinsed with ethanol. The wafers are then dried under reduced pressure 
in a glass reactor and three evacuate/refill cycles are performed. A solution of 6-
(chlorodimethylsilyl) hexyl 2-bromo-2-methylpropanoate initiator (80mg) in dry toluene 
(40mL) is prepared and injected to the glass reactor. The reaction is then conducted overnight 
under positive pressure of nitrogen to yield initiator-functionalized silicon wafers of high water 
contact angle (c.f. Scheme 21 in Section 3.7).

5.8. 
Surface polymerization of MPC  
Initiator-functionalized silicon wafers (from section 5.6) are covered with two small circular 
copper-based grid patterns (Agar Scientific Athene Grids Copper 3.05mm diameter, G2004 400 
Thin) and left under strong UV light intensity (Hamamatsu LC6, 365nm, 3500mW/cm2) for 10 
minutes to selectively cleave the initiators from the irradiated surfaces. After UV irradiation, 
the patterns are carefully removed from the wafers. The wafers are then transferred to a circular 
reactor.  
For PTH-based polymerizations (c.f. Table S9), a solution of 250mg (0.85mmol) of MPC in 
95μL of water is prepared in an Eppendorf tube and sonicated until all monomer content is 
dissolved. In parallel, a solution of 5.2mg (0.019mmol) of 10-phenylphenothiazine (PTH) in 
400μL of DMSO is prepared and sonicated until complete dissolution. The MPC solution is 
then transferred drop-by-drop to the PTH solution while shaking it to avoid the formation of a 
precipitate.  
For fluorescein-based polymerizations (c.f. Table S10), 250mg (0.85mmol) of MPC, 1.62mg 
(4.25μmol) of fluorescein and 6μL (4.35mg, 0.043mmol) of triethylamine are added to 0.5mL 
of water and sonicated for 1min.  
The resulting solutions are then carefully added on the surface of the wafer until 2/3 of the 
surface is covered. A thin microscopy glass slide is then applied on top of the solution to evenly 
distribute the solution over the entire wafer and to prevent unwanted coffee-ring effects upon 
polymerization.  
The circular reactor is then sealed to ensure an isolated atmosphere and nitrogen is flushed 
inside the reactor for 10 minutes, before the light source is turned on (3.3mW/cm2, 
Distance=1cm) to launch the polymerization for 1.5h.  
After reaction, the reactor is opened and the wafer transferred to a brine bath without removing 
the glass slide on top of it. After 30 minutes, the glass slide is carefully removed to avoid any 
shear stress on the brushes and the wafer is then thoroughly rinsed with brine and acetone and 
dried with a slight compressed air stream before the analysis of the wafers with optical 
microscopy, water contact angle measurement or ellipsometry.

Synthesis of water soluble 5-(2-bromo-2-methylpropanamido)-2-

The procedure used for the synthesis of this initiator is inspired from H.-A. Klok et al. [86]. In

brought to room temperature. After two hours of reaction, 1M HCl is added into the reaction 
mixture until the pH reaches 2. The reaction mixture is then extracted with 2x20mL of ethyl 
acetate. The organic phase is then washed with brine, dried with Na2SO4, filtered and 
concentrated with a rotary evaporator to yield a brown solid (312mg, 85.9% yield). The NMR 
spectrum of the product is shown in Figure A10 in Appendix A.
