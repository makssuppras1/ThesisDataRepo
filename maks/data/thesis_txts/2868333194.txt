Abstract
Our findings indicate significant improvements in language model performance on the Ice-
landic language comprehension tasks when models are fine-tuned with the tailored dataset.
Specifically, the fine-tuned models demonstrated enhanced understanding of syntactic nu-
ances and idiomatic expressions unique to Icelandic, which standard pre-trained models
often misinterpret. Furthermore, comparisons between fine-tuned and zero-shot models
revealed the fine-tuned models’ superior accuracy and eﬀiciency on domain-specific bench-
marks.

Introduction
Language models have advanced rapidly over the past decade, from chatbots to transla-
tion tools, with the GPT family of models from OpenAI paving the way [1]. However this
progress has mainly been focused on high resource languages and leaving other underrep-
resented languages for example Icelandic with limited digital support [2].
This master thesis at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) within the realm of
Human-Centered AI, under the guidance of Ole Winther and Nicki Skafte Detlefsen aims
to assess and enhance the proficiency of Large Language Models (LLMs) in Icelandic.
The project will presents an unseen dataset consisting of standardized high school reading
comprehension exams provided by Þorvaldur Páll Helgason from Miðeind 1, This initiative
not only tests the capability of LLMs to operate in Icelandic but also explores the potential
improvements through targeted fine-tuning of the models with this specific dataset.
The importance of this work are magnified by the rapid development and deployment of
LLMs globally, as seen with models like OpenAI’s GPT-4 [1], which Miðeind, along with
the Icelandic government, have already collaborated to foster understanding of Icelandic
2. Despite these advances, the Icelandic language’s representation in the digital dialogue
remains minimal, underscoring a significant gap this thesis aims to address.
Benchmarking efforts, such as HELM [3] (Holistic Evaluation of Language Models) to
those led by the Center for Research on Foundation Models at Stanford, have primarily
focused on more globally dominant languages. This project proposes a shift by creating
a structured benchmark specifically for Icelandic, recognizing the nuanced needs of lower-
resource languages.

2.1
Evolution of Natural Language Processing
NLP has a rich history that dates back to the early attempts at computational language
understanding in the mid-20th century. These early approaches were largely driven by rule-
based systems and symbolic AI that dominated AI research before the widespread adoption
of statistical methods and machine learning. This chapter explores the early methods
that formed the foundation of modern NLP. While these systems laid the groundwork
for future advancements, they faced significant challenges in accurately representing and
understanding the complexity of human language. Though the methods themselves are
of course completely outdated, their way of breaking down of language remain the same
today.
2.1.1
Rule-Based Systems
The rule-based systems, utilized handcrafted rules for processing language. These systems
were based on linguistic theory and attempted to formalize language through explicit syn-
tactic rules. Programs like ELIZA [6], one of the first chatbots, used rule-based pattern
matching to create simple, pre-scripted responses to user inputs. When looking at tran-
scripts of conversations between a human and this first chat-bot, the answers are somewhat
impressive and could in some cases even pass the Turing test. However, ELIZA lacked

any real understanding of semantics, as its responses were entirely based on pre-defined
templates rather than any comprehension of meaning.
Figure 2.1: Figure showing the bla [6]
Rule-based NLP systems were successful for narrow domains with limited vocabulary
and specific rules, but they struggled with the general complexity of human language.
Ambiguity, polysemy (multiple meanings for words), and context-dependency were major
hurdles. For instance, a rule-based system would often fail when a word had different
meanings depending on the context, such as ”bank” meaning either a financial institution
or the side of a river. Rule-based systems lacked the flexibility to learn from examples or
adapt to unseen input, making them impractical for real-world language processing tasks
[7].
2.1.2
Hand-Coded Grammars and Parsing
Another significant early approach to NLP was the development of hand-coded grammars
for syntactic parsing. Projects like SHRDLU [8] is an example of these efforts, where the
system could understand and manipulate a block world by interacting with users through
natural language commands. SHRDLU combined syntactic parsing with a defined domain
to enable simple conversational abilities, but its capabilities were limited by the narrow
context in which it operated.
Hand-coded grammars were painstakingly built by linguists to define the rules of language.
These systems used methods like context-free grammars to parse sentences and determine
their syntactic structure. While they represented a significant step forward, they were
ultimately inflexible and diﬀicult to scale.
Human language is rich in variability, and
manually defining all possible syntactic structures was a monumental task. Hand-coded
grammar approaches could not effectively handle unexpected inputs or adapt to the vast
linguistic diversity seen in different contexts, speakers and let alone different languages.

2.1.3
Symbolic AI and Knowledge-Based Systems
During the late 60s and 70s, became the dominant paradigm in NLP with systems like
LUNAR [9], which answered questions about moon rock samples using a database of struc-
tured knowledge, demonstrated the promise of symbolic approaches to language under-
standing. Symbolic AI attempted to represent human knowledge in formal logic, where
language could be understood through a series of rules and symbolic representations.
These systems were typically built on knowledge bases (such as the aforementioned moon
rock database), with the hope that explicitly modeling the world would allow machines to
effectively understand and reason about language.
The common limitation across these early NLP methods was their reliance on explicit
rules and manual programming.
As mentioned before human language is ambiguous,
dynamic, and context-dependent, which makes it challenging to encode all necessary rules
for understanding as well as in generating text. This is evident in the multiple nuances of
Icelandic language grammar where common mistakes children make are in fact based on
rules that apply to most words, but have exceptions. Like the irregular adjective góður
in its comparative form is betri, where many kids somewhat rightfully assume that you
should say góðari. The same exact irregularity in English is good, better, best instead of
..., gooder, goodest. Rule-based and symbolic systems lacked the capacity to generalize
from examples, which led to frequent failures when dealing with complex or novel input.
They also required a lot of manual labor to build and maintain, requiring expert linguists
and programmers to carefully define every nuance of grammar and meaning [5].
As a result, while these early systems demonstrated that computers could perform basic
language tasks, they were not scalable or robust enough for more general language under-
standing. This motivated a shift toward the data-driven approaches in the late 80s and
early 90s, where statistical methods and machine learning offered new ways to handle the
variability and complexity of natural language.

Statistical NLP

N-gram models were among the first statistical approaches applied in NLP. An n-gram
is a sequence of ’n’ items (typically words) used to predict the probability of the next

word based on the previous words. For instance, a bigram uses the preceding word to
predict the next, while a trigram uses the previous two words (Shannon, 1948).
This
simple approach captured short-term context and was used extensively in applications like
speech recognition and machine translation. N-grams improved upon rule-based systems
we discussed in the last chapter 2.1.1 by quantifying the likelihood of sequences in language,
making the models more adaptable and data-driven. However, they were limited by data
sparsity, as rare sequences often had little or no data to support accurate prediction, and
n-grams could only consider a small context window, which prevented them from capturing
long-term dependencies in language [5].

Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) were another key statistical method introduced to en-
hance NLP. HMMs are based on the concept of states and observations, where hidden
states (such as parts of speech) generate observable words.
They became particularly
useful for tasks like part-of-speech tagging, where each word in a sentence is assigned
a grammatical tag TODO: add reference (Rabiner, 1989). The probabilistic nature of
HMMs allowed them to model the sequence of states in language, thus providing a more
sophisticated representation than simple n-grams. HMMs also made it possible to model
longer word dependencies indirectly, through transitions between states.
However, HMMs still suffered from limited ability to fully understand complex linguistic
structures and had diﬀiculties handling non-local dependencies effectively.
While these statistical approaches improved the flexibility and adaptability of NLP sys-
tems compared to rule-based methods, they had notable limitations. Data sparsity was
a major issue, as even large datasets would fail to provide coverage for all possible word
sequences, especially for uncommon phrases. Additionally, n-gram models, HMMs, and
other methods lacked the capacity to capture long-term dependencies in text, as their
statistical nature typically confined them to modeling local context only. The inability to
encode deeper semantic relationships and nuanced meanings further highlighted the need
for a new approach that could generalize better from data.
The limitations of statistical models eventually led to the adoption of machine learning
and neural network-based approaches that allowed for more effective feature learning, the
ability to capture global context, and richer representations of language.

forward in NLP by providing a new architecture that addresses the shortcomings of tra-

allows Transformers to be significantly more eﬀicient in handling long-range dependencies

2.2.1
Transformer types
Transformer models can broadly be divided into three groups, encoder models, decoder
models and encoder-decoder []. We will now discuss these different types briefly and look
at the main models of each type.
Figure 2.6: High level structure of Transformers [14]
Encoder-decoder models, also known as sequence-to-sequence models, incorporate both
the encoder and decoder parts of the Transformer architecture. The encoder can access
all words in a sentence, while the decoder only accesses previous words. Pretraining these
models often involves complex objectives, such as T5’s method of predicting text replaced
by a mask. These models excel in tasks that generate new sentences based on input, like
summarization, translation, and generative question answering. Examples include BART
[15], mBART [16], Marian 1, and T5 [17].
Secondly are the most famous of the three, decoder models, which only utilize only the
decoder and allow each word to access only the preceding words in the sentence. Their
pretraining primarily involves predicting the next word. These models are ideal for text
generation tasks and include examples like CTRL[18], Transformer XL[19] and of course
the GPT[20] and preceding GPT models from OpenAI we all know and love.

2.4.1
Tokenization
In order for LLMs to understand text it must first be tokenized, this is done by a Tokenizer.
HuggingFace defines it as: ”A tokenizer is in charge of preparing the inputs for a model”
[26]. Along with each model there is a specially defined Tokenizer that must be used for
tokenizing input for the particular model. In Figures 2.14 and 2.4 the Inputs in the bottom
left are assumed to be tokens that are processed text by the respective tokenizer.
Language models typically process text by breaking it down into tokenized fragments, such
as subwords (similar to the n-grams we discussed earlier), which are assigned numerical
identifiers. This tokenization process uses a fixed vocabulary of fragments, often rang-
ing from 50,000 to 100,000 unique pieces, determined through statistical methods before
training [2]. Historically, these vocabularies have been biased towards English, featuring
many English-specific fragments while excluding common elements from languages like
Japanese or Hindi, and even more so from less widely spoken languages. This bias results
in longer input and output sequences for non-English texts, increasing inference costs due
to the computational demand, which scales with the square of sequence length.
Such
disparities affect both the eﬀiciency and the cost-effectiveness of model deployment for
underrepresented languages [2].

With the data generated in the process explained in the last chapter, a series of finetuning

The BERT [36] model, and models derived from it, were the base model of the first

5
Conclusion
This thesis has underscored the potential of fine-tuning LLMs for enhanced comprehension
of the Icelandic language, facilitated by the development and utilization of a new dataset
derived from Icelandic reading comprehension multiple choice questions. The dataset has
proven invaluable not only for improving model performance but also for establishing a
benchmarking standard tailored to the unique attributes of Icelandic.
Future Work
1. Expansion of the Dataset With Synthetic Data Generation: The dataset’s
structured format presents an opportunity for generating synthetic data. Future re-
search could explore automatic data augmentation techniques to expand the dataset
artificially, thereby enriching the training material available for model fine-tuning
without the need for additional human-labeled data.
2. Development of New Benchmarks: Leveraging the dataset, researchers can
develop new benchmarks that test various aspects of language understanding, such as
idiomatic usage, complex grammatical structures, and domain-specific jargon. These
benchmarks can serve as crucial tools for systematically evaluating the progress and
limitations of language models tailored to Icelandic and potentially other under-
resourced languages.
3. Fine-Tuning Additional Models: The current dataset offers a solid foundation
for fine-tuning a wider range of pre-trained models. Future projects could include
experimenting with different architectures and configurations to determine the most
effective models for Icelandic language tasks. This could also extend to testing hybrid
models that combine the strengths of multiple pre-existing models.
4. Cross-Linguistic Model Development: Building on the successes of this dataset,
similar approaches could be employed to create datasets for other low-resource lan-
guages. Cross-linguistic studies could use these datasets to fine-tune models capable
of multilingual understanding, which would be particularly valuable for languages
lacking extensive digital resources.
5. Interactive Learning Systems: The dataset could be used to develop interactive
learning systems that continuously evolve. By implementing user feedback loops,
these systems could dynamically improve their understanding and generation of Ice-
landic, adapting over time to changes in language use and user needs.
By focusing on these areas, future research can maximize the impact of the developed
dataset, advancing the field of language for Icelandic and potentially setting a precedent
for of other low-resource languages in artificial intelligence applications.
