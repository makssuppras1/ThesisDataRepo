Abstract  
The approaching antimicrobial resistance crisis poses a major threat to humanity. Many years of misuse 
and lack of development of novel antibiotics have resulted in a limited arsenal against advancing bacterial 
resistance. Microbial natural products (NPs) have long been a source of antimicrobial compounds. 
However, a major obstacle to investigating microbial NPs has been the cultivation recalcitrance of most 
bacteria. Novel and innovative cultivation methods are therefore needed.  
In this project, extracted bacterial cells from Bellevue and a Gram-negative fish pathogen, Vibrio. 
anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3*, were successfully encapsulated and cultivated using the PreciGenome 
droplet microfluidics system, which was employed for the first time in the MME laboratory.  
Additionally, the bacteria from the sandy sediment north of Rungsted harbor were screened for 
antimicrobial activity against V. anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3*. This was achieved by using three 
microfluidic operations: cell encapsulation, picoinjection with the fluorescent pathogen, and 
fluorescence-activated droplet sorting (FADS), with help from Novonesis. 
Based on the low fluorescence signal detected during FADS, 752 out of ~750 000 droplets across all 
emulations were sorted out, indicating the antimicrobial activity of those marine bacteria against the 
selected pathogen.  
Ultimately, the screening of the FADS droplets resulted in the isolation of several liquid cultures and 
colonies with antimicrobial potential properties against V. anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3*. The final 
data of this project demonstrated indeed the potential of droplet microfluidic cultivation as an innovative 
cultivation method. Overall, marine sandy sediment has shown great biosynthetic potential seen as a 
reservoir for antimicrobial activity exploration.

1 Background  
Since 1941, when the first patient was treated with the newly developed penicillin [1], antibiotics have 
saved millions of lives. However, decades after Alexander Fleming's groundbreaking discovery, 
humanity faces increasing threats from microbial infections again. Years of misuse, overuse, and lack of 
novel antibiotic discovery have resulted in a limited arsenal against the increasing resistance to the 
current antibiotics. It is estimated by WHO that by 2050, 10 mio. people will succumb to antimicrobial 
resistance (AMR) [2]. Multiple regulations and legal actions have been put in place to ensure a better 
and safer way of using antibiotics [3]. However, the approaching crisis cannot be solved solely through 
laws and regulations; the discovery and development of novel antimicrobial compounds play a critical 
role. Few novel antibiotics have been discovered since “the Golden Age” of antibiotic discovery in the 
1940s – 1960s[4]. Despite the hope and efforts, fully synthetic antibiotics have had relatively low clinical 
success [5][6]. Most antibiotics today are derived directly from microbial natural products (NP) or are 
semi-synthetic derivatives of NPs[4][5]. Turning our attention to NPs for discovering novel anti-infective 
agents would be advantageous. In the past, new antibiotics were discovered by screening the 
antimicrobial activity of environmental strains against pathogens via agar overlay inhibition assays[7]. 
However, the re-discovery of the same bioactive stains has resulted in the perception that the environment 
is an exhausted source of antibiotics. The rise of cultivation independent ‘omics’ technologies has, 
however, uncovered a tremendous biosynthetic capacity of not only terrestrial microbiomes, but also the 
underexplored marine environment. To this day, the terrestrial environment is the most explored 
environment, although the marine environment covers 70 % of the Earth's surface and hence represents 
the largest biome on the planet [8].   
Aside from knowing where to look, isolating the microorganisms with antimicrobial properties poses 
another major obstacle in antibiotic drug discovery; this is due to the fact that the vast majority of 
microorganisms are unculturable under standard lab conditions [9]. These uncultivable microorganisms 
have shown great potential as producers of antimicrobial compounds [10][11]. However, innovative or 
novel cultivation methods are needed to explore this potential.  Different innovative approaches have so 
far been developed, such as Ichip cultivation. This project will mainly focus on applying microfluidics

3 Introduction  
3.1 Marine environment and the antimicrobial biosynthetic potential of the marine 
sediment microbiome 
The terrestrial environment has historically been the primary source and focus on the discovery of novel 
antimicrobial compounds[4][12]. However, the vastly different conditions compared to the soil 
environment, along with the sheer amount of bacterial biomass of the marine environment makes it a 
good source for the discovery of novel bioactive compounds. Microorganisms of the marine environment  
have evolved to survive and grow in conditions of high salinity, pressure, low nutrient availability, and 
varying levels of oxygen[8] [13]. Due to this, the chemistry of many bioactive compounds produced by 
marine microorganisms differs from what is produced by terrestrial microorganisms[8]. A comprehensive 
analysis by Kong et al., 2010 showed that marine NP are superior compared to terrestrial NP in terms of 
chemical novelty and that 70 % of marine NP are not represented in the terrestrial environment [13]. For 
the last couple of years, more than 1,000 novel compounds have been identified from the ocean[14]. 
Recently, marine-derived NP with antimicrobial properties have attracted an increasing amount of 
attention[15].  
It has been estimated that marine microorganisms produce approximately 23,000 bioactive secondary 
metabolites. Actinomycetes are responsible for producing about 70%, fungi produce 20%, Firmicutes 
contribute 7%, and the remaining 1-2% are produced by other types of bacteria, such as 
Proteobacteria[16]. The marine sediment environment is especially interesting as it harbors as 5.39 × 
1029 prokaryotic cells on a global scale corresponding to 3.6 % of the total living biomass on earth[17].

Furthermore a study conducted by Bech et al., 2020 investigating the microbial communities of sandy 
sediments, reported a cell concentration of 3.09 × 107 ± 0.76 × 107 cells/g in sandy sediment, based on 
amplicon sequencing of 16S rRNA V3V4[11]. Aside from the high abundance of bacterial cells, marine 
sediment is also rich in polyketides (PKs) and nonribosomal peptides (NRP)[12][11]. PKS and NRPs are 
two major classes of secondary metabolites with wide clinical importance, as multiple compounds of 
these two classes are currently used as antibiotics[12]. Bioprospecting of environments often targets the 
adenylation domains (AD) of NRPs synthetases and keto synthase (KS) domains PKs synthases to 
approximate the biosynthetic potential and  its diversity in the investigated environment[12].  
3.2 Microbial Dark Matter  
Most bacterial diversity is unculturable under traditional cultivation methods, and these microorganisms 
are called “Microbial Dark Matter”. The cultivability of microorganisms from different environments 
does vary, but it is estimated that 85-99 % of microbial diversity cannot currently be cultivated[18][19]. 
In the 1930s, it came to light that not all microorganisms can be grown, with the work of the Russian 
Microbiologist, Razumov [20], five decades later,  the term “great plate count anomaly” was coined [21]. 
The great plate count anomaly describes the contrast between the number of colonies formed on agar 
plates from an environmental sample and the number of quantifiable cells through microscopy[21].   
Not one common factor seems to be the sole reason behind cultivation recalcitrance in the microbial 
world; on the contrary, multiple different factors may be at play at once, such as the need for specific 
growth factors, which can be at such a small concentration in situ that it cannot be detected. Temperature, 
pH, and nutrients are also essential factors to consider, as is the need for symbiotic partners. Some 
microorganisms are slow-growers, some form microcolonies, which makes them sensitive to competition 
and hard to detect by the naked eye[9][22]. 
With the rise and rapid advances in genome sequencing and omics- technologies, the view and 
understanding of microbial diversity have improved[9]. Furthermore, it is known that natural NPs of 
microorganisms are an excellent source of bioactive compounds ranging from antibiotics, hydrolytic 
enzymes, and anticancer agents and that the microbial dark matter is a rich reservoir for these bioactive 
compounds[8][23].

Aside from the industrial interest in discovering novel bioactive compounds, exploring microbial dark 
matter is essential to improving the fundamental understanding of bacterial ecology and evolution. A 
consequence of the cultivation barrier is that the current knowledge of bacteria and archaea is based on 
well-studied members of the culturable majority and reconstructed genomes from uncultured lineages of 
marine bacterial class globally in open ocean waters exhibits lower relative abundances to 
Gammaproteobacterial in cultivation-dependent studies [8].  
Technological advancements in genomics and metagenomics have brought significant insights into the 
overall functions and activities of environmental microbial communities. Moreover, these technologies 
have enabled the discovery of novel bacterial strains along with the identification of potentially 
industrially interesting biosynthetic pathways [8][24][25]. However, genome-driven discovery has 
limitations related to the identification of functional genes, making it difficult to predict the production 
of fully functional products and how different conditions affect their expression[9]. Metatranscriptomics 
and metaproteomics provide some insight into gene expression and protein profile. However, the 
generated data can be challenging to interpret without a fundamental understanding of the underlying 
physiology of a given organism[9]. 
As mentioned, traditional cultivation methods have their shortcomings in promoting the growth of the 
majority of microorganisms. However, it is essential to note that entirely novel enzymatic reactions and 
pathways were uncovered using cultivation-based approaches, which were otherwise undetected using 
cultivation-independent approaches [9][26]. Therefore, traditional cultivation and strategies (e.g., media 
plates, liquid cultures, etc.) are still essential tools in the biotechnology toolbox. However, much time 
and patience are needed to fully benefit from the traditional approach and methods, as many factors must 
be considered (e.g., nutrients, temperature, pH, etc.). The sterilization method for the substrate must also 
be considered, as some compounds can form toxic by-products, such as reactive oxygen species during 
autoclaving, which can inhibit or otherwise have a negative effect on microbial growth[9] [27]. A study 
has demonstrated that by replacing the otherwise popular gelling agent, agar, with gellan gum, the 
cultivability of seawater bacteria was improved, and a larger fraction of the seawater community was 
covered[8]. Different innovative approaches have been developed to cross the barrier of the yet-to-be-
cultured microorganisms, such as the Ichip and microfluidic droplet cultivation[9][28].

3.3 Ichip cultivation  
The Ichip, short for isolation chip, is an in 
situ cultivation method designed to tap into 
microbial dark matter. The Ichip consists 
of multiple well-like chambers where a 
single cell of an environmental sample is 
inoculated; each inoculated chamber is 
closed off with a membrane (20-30 nm in 
pore sizes) at the top and bottom opening 
of the wells (Figure 3.1). The inoculated 
ichip is then returned to the environment from which the cells originated. The Ichip facilitates growth 
promotion of microbial cells by reintroducing them to the naturally occurring growth factors, nutrients 
and other essential conditions in the environment[29][30]. The membrane ensures that the cells cannot 
escape the well, and foreign microbes cannot enter the chambers and thereby cause contamination.  
As mentioned, our lack of knowledge regarding the specific growth conditions for the majority of 
microbes greatly contributes to the existence of microbial dark matter. With the ichip, less time and 
effects has to be put into imitating the necessary conditions, as the microbes are grown in their 
environment[29]. The Ichip method is an excellent cultivation method for growing uncultivable 
microbes, as depending on the study, the Ichip cultivation increased the microbial recovery from 5- to 
300-fold, depending on the study[29]. Another benefit of the ichip technology is the seemingly 
domestication of the otherwise uncultivable species. Domestication is the process where microorganisms 
that beforehand could only be grown in situ are apparently adapted to the chosen media, which enables 
ex-situ cultivation[29]. Growing the microbes in the lab is essential for characterization and further 
analysis.  
In 2015, a new class of antibiotic, teixobactin, was identified from a soil sample cultivated using the 
Ichip [28]. No other novel antibiotic class had been discovered for many decades prior, making the 
discovery important and showcasing the importance of exploring and shedding light on microbial dark 
matter.

3.4 Microfluidic droplet cultivation 
Microfluidic droplet cultivation is another 
method with the potential to cultivate members 
of 
the 
microbial 
dark 
matter. 
Droplet 
microfluidics is a branch of microfluidics. 
Since its first description in the 90’s, the 
technology has gained much traction due to its 
high throughput rate and applicability for 
different scientific disciplines [31]. 
Like the Ichip technology, droplet microfluidics 
is also based on isolating single cells within their closed-off environment. Cells are encapsulated into 
tiny droplets, ranging in size from a few µm to hundreds of µm, corresponding to femto- to nanoliter[32].  
The droplets are made from mixing an oil phase and an aqueous phase to form emulsions. 
A wide range of oils can be used for droplets microfluidics. Fluorinated oil is generally used when 
encapsulating cell[33], which is the focus of this project. This is due to how respiratory gasses such as 
oxygen and carbon dioxide are highly soluble in fluorous fluids, which is important for survival of 
cells[34]. Additionally, fluorinated oil is less toxic toward cells compared to other oils and it is highly 
compatible with PDMS (Polydimethylsiloxane), which is the main material for microfluidic 
devices[34][35]. A vital component of fluorinated oil is surfactant. Surfactants are amphiphilic molecules 
containing both a hydrophilic and a hydrophobic group, forming a monolayer at the aqueous-oil 
interphase[33] (Figure 3.2). The surfactant molecules are essential later in manipulating the droplets, as 
they maintain the droplet structure and prevent droplet coalescence. Coalescence is the uncontrolled 
formation of a large droplet by merging two or more droplets by contact[33].  
Microfluidic droplet cultivation has many advantages. With the absence of competition in the single-cell 
droplets, the growth of slow-growing, microcolonies forming, and rare species are promoted. The 
technology also enables the generation, manipulation, and screening of millions of droplets in a highly 
parallel and high throughput manner. Additional, monodispersed droplets microfluid highly minimizes 
the use of reagents as volumes used per droplets varies form 10 fL – 1 µm while cultivation method using 
flask, dishes, tubes, plates and reactors generally utilizes volumes of 10 µL – 103 L per cultivation[36].

Different proof-of-concepts for droplet microfluidics have been conducted. Liu et al., 2009 demonstrated 
that from a mixed culture, the slow-growing Paenibacillus curdlanolyticus could be separated from fast-
growing E. coli, which would typically dominate and outgrow the slow-growing species[37]. Mahler et 
al., 2021 demonstrated that hitherto uncultured soil species could be grown from droplet cultivation. 
Furthermore, the bacterial diversity was significantly higher when compared with standard agar plate 
cultivation. As part of the study by Mahler et al., 2021, the cultivated microorganisms were screened for 
antimicrobial activity. From the screening, a potent antibiotic producer was isolated from whom several 
antimicrobial compounds were detected[19].  
Microfluidic droplet cultivation is an interdisciplinary method with roots in physics, chemistry, material 
engineering, coding, and microbiology[38]. The multidisciplinary nature of droplet microfluidics does 
present somewhat of a challenge for implementing the technology in non-specialized laboratories. 
However, more developers, such as Atrandi Biosciences or Precigenome, offer instruments for droplet 
generation and for pico-injecting, merging, sorting, etc. This project used the iFlow Droplet Generation 
System-Light Version from Precigenome to generate droplets for preliminary testing. Subsequently, 
instruments at Novonesis were utilized for droplet generation, Picoinjection, and FADS.  
 
3.5 Droplet generation and encapsulation  
When encapsulating cells, it is a common practice to follow Poisson distribution when deciding the cell 
occupation percentage of the droplets, especially when using the so-called passive encapsulation 
methods. The cell encapsulation per droplet for these passive methods are dictated by Poisson 
statistics[39]. The Poisson distribution is a probability distribution that deals with the frequency at which 
an event occurs within a specific interval.  
The passive encapsulation methods are the most used, and they are based on generating droplets at high 
rates by pumping fluids from external pressure-driven revisors through microfluidics geometries[39].  
The frequency of droplet formation is determined from the flow rates of the oil phase, aqueous media 
phase, and microfluidic geometry. Droplet generation frequency can reach over 10 kHz , corresponding 
to over 10.000 droplets per second [34]. The droplet sizes are determined by the ratio between the two 
flow rates, along with the dimension and design of channels [32].

The general concept of droplet microfluidic geometries is the 
creation of emulsions at the intersection where the aqueous 
media and oil flow channels meet. T-junction and flow-
focusing are the most commonly used geometries. With a T-
junction geometry, the media channel is orthogonally 
positioned to the oil channel. For a flow-focusing geometry, 
two oil channels intersect the media channel from opposite 
sides[32]. Flow-focusing is the best-suited geometry for high 
throughput screening as this geometry allows for high droplet 
generation frequencies going up to several kHz[40]. This 
project used flow-focusing geometry for all emulations. Aside 
from the monodisperse single emulsion (w/o), which has been 
explained so far, Emulsion can also be done as double 
emulsions, water-in-oil-in-water (w/o/w). w/o/w emulsion can 
be generated by re-injecting premade w/o emulsion into a 
media-based flow-focusing system. In the media-based 
system, two media channels intersect, and the oil channel 
contains the droplets. W/o/w emulsion can also be generated 
using an emulation chip with two consecutive flow-focusing 
junctions, where w/o droplets are directly encapsulated with 
media after formation[32] (Figure 3.3).  
Doublet emulsion droplets have different advantages, such as 
being stable for at least a year when stored at room temperature 
in an aqueous buffer, without showing signs of coalescence. 
Furthermore, despite heating, freezing, shrinking, or 
expanding by osmosis, the doublet emulsion droplets maintain 
their structural integrity. A significant advantage of the w/o/w 
droplets is how sorting can be done using standard FACS

3.6 Picoinjection  
Introducing new reagents, substances, or microbial cells to droplets is an important discipline in droplet 
microfluidics, as it increases experimental flexibility. There are a few different methods, with 
picoinjection being the focus of this section[41][42]. 
A picoinjection system generally consists of a main droplet channel that narrows down, forcing droplets 
into a line. Spacing between the droplets is achieved by adding oil from a side channel to the main 
channel.  The microinjector consists of a reagent channel and a positive and negative electrode. The 
droplet injection takes place at the intersection where droplets and reagents meet. Pico injection only 
happens when the electrodes are activated. The electric field destabilizes the aqueous media and oil 
interface, enabling the reagent to enter the droplet. When the droplet moves away from the electric field 
due to the flow of the channel, the droplet regains its stability, preventing the coalescence of the 
droplets[42] (Figure 3.4).  
Picoinjection is a very important and robust microfluidic operation, but is also an error prone technology  
[32][42].

interring the electric field, destabilizing the structure of the droplets, and allowing reagent to be injected. to the left, picoinjected droplets 
are gathered [42].

3.7 Fluorescence Activated Droplet Sorting (FADS) 
Sorting out droplets of interest is a very important part of droplet microfluidics. However, it is also one 
of the more challenging disciplines, especially regarding the identification and manipulation of individual 
droplets. These technological challenges are some of the reasons for the limited adoption of droplet 
microfluidics[43].   
As mentioned in section 3.5, only double emulsion can be sorted using standard FACS and no single 
emulations. This limitation is due to the FACS instrument's incompatibility with nonaqueous 
suspensions[38]. Single emulsions are, therefore, sorted using fluorescence-activated droplet Sorting 
(FADS). FADS follows the same concept as FACS, as droplets are physically sorted based on their 
fluorescence signal. The fluorescent signal in a droplet can be achieved though different means, such as 
injections of fluorescent dyes or GFP genetically modified organisms, into the droplets. Encapsulating a 
cell with fluorescent nucleic acid probes (FNAP) is another way of achieving a sortable fluorescence 
signal, where a signal is emitted from droplets with growing cells[43].  
The basic geometry for sorting droplets consists of a main channel, which splits into a waste channel and 
a collection channel. Detection of fluorescence takes place before the split. The waste channel has less 
resistance, causing the droplets to be directed to waste when sorting is not activated. The detection of 
fluorescence and the sorting mechanism vary depending on the approach. Dielectrophoresis (DEP) 
sorting uses two electrodes placed on the collection channel side. When the pair of electrodes are 
activated, the droplets that triggered the detection are pulled towards the collection channel. This project 
used DEP sorting. Other methods to achieve FADS are Acoustophoretic sorting and Hydrodynamic 
sorting.  Acoustophoretic sorting uses acoustic waves produced by an interdigitated transducer on the 
waste channel side to push droplets into the collection channel. Lastly, hydrodynamic sorting uses a 
pneumatic valve to close off the waste channel temporarily, thereby directing the droplets to the collection 
channel[32] (Figure 3.5).

4.1.1 Implementation of the PreciGenome droplet generation system - light version 
For the emulsions generated in-house, the PreciGenomes Droplet Generation system – light Version, was

4.1.6 Cell extraction of marine environmental samples from Bellevue using Nycodenz 
density gradient   
Cells from the Bellevue samples were extracted using a modified version of the Nycodenz density 
gradient protocol [44]. A mixture of 120 mL of Tween 20 (T20, 0.5% in PBS buffer) and 40 g of sediment 
were blended using an Ultra-turrax blender. This blender was cleaned with 70 % ethanol before use. The 
sediment solution was blended for one minute at 11,000 rpm and cooled on ice for one additional minute. 
This procedure was repeated two more times at 13,000 rpm. Next, the sediment slurry was kept on ice 
for 30 min. Then, 20 mL of sediment slurry was slowly added on top of 18 mL of 80 % (w/v) Nycodenz 
and the tubes were centrifuged at 15,000 x g for 40 min at 4° C. Additionally, the centrifuges were set to 
a slow acceleration and deceleration. The cell layer was identified and transferred to another tube. The 
obtained cells were vortexed and filtered through a 20 µm cell strainer into another tube. The filtered 
cells were centrifuged at 15,000 x g for 15 min at 4° C with slow acceleration and deceleration (value of 
1). The supernatant was discarded, and the pellet was resuspended with one mL of PBS.  
To quantify the number of cells using flow cytometry, a 100 µL sample of the extracted cells was 
transferred into a microcentrifuge tube for staining and analysis. The remaining cell extract was 
resuspended in  glycerol (20% final concentration) and stored at – 80° C until encapsulation.

4.1.8 Encapsulation of marine environmental samples from Bellevue  
The glycerol stock of the extracted Bellevue cells was thawed on ice and spun down twice at 5000 x g 
for 10 min and then 8000 x g for 10 min. The supernatant was discarded, and the pellet was resuspended 
in 500 µL MB, followed by a brief vortexing step. The sample was split in two with 250 µL in each 
Eppendorf tube. To both Eppendorf tubes, cycloheximide was added to a final concentration of 100 
µg/ml. Subsequently, 250 µL MB was added to one of the tubes and 250 µL 0.2 µm filter-sterilized 
seawater was added to the other.  
Three emulsions were generated using the same settings described in section 1.2.  (1) Droplets containing 
cells in MB supplemented with cycloheximide, (2) cells in MB:Seawater ( ratio 1:1 ) supplemented also 
with 100 µg/ml cycloheximide, and (3) Negative control droplets were prepared too from MB.   
During the droplet generation, changing between the three samples was done by continuing the droplet 
generation after the media reservoir was emptied. The droplet generation would be paused when 
approximately five cm of air was sucked up in the tubing. The tubing was then fastened to a new tube 
for collection of the following sample. The generation of droplets was resumed, and the air bubble was 
visually followed. When the air bubble reached the emulsion chip, all droplets were discarded until the 
air bubble had passed and all tubing only contained the second sample. The system was connected to 
MiliQ water after the second sample to let the entire sample run through the chip, as the droplet generator 
indicated leakage if the media reservoir was not filled with liquid.  
The three emulsions: (1) cells in MBcycloheximide, (2) cells in MB:Seawatercycloheximide, and (3) MB, were 
further incubated at 30° C.

5.1 Preliminary testing of PreciGenome droplets generation system – light version 
The preliminary testing was done in order to test the PreciGenome system, as well as assessing the

5.1.1 Generation of MiliQ water in fluorinated oil droplets  
It was essential for this project to test if droplets could be generated using the PreciGenome system, and 
if the generated droplets were uniform in size and structure (Figure 5.1). It was possible to generate MiliQ

were larger in size than expected from the PSI settings when comparing with the reference sizes of 46 
µm as informed from the PreciGenome droplet generation reference table (Appendix 9.1).  
While analyzing the droplets in the microscope a honeycomb-like structure was observed as a result of 
being too concentrated and squeezed down by the cover glass. Therefor it is essential to dilute one drop 
of droplets with sufficient amount of oil ranging between 25-50 µL.

6 Discussion  
The antibiotic resistance crisis is closer than ever before, and its effects will have tremendous 
consequences for humanity [2]. The need and demand for novel drug discovery has therefore prompted 
the exploitation of other environments aside from the extensively explored terrestrial environment, from 
where the majority of NP derived antibiotics originates from [4][12]. Notably, the marine environment, 
particularly sediment microbiomes, harbors substantial biosynthetic potential for a diverse array of 
antimicrobial NPs[11]. Tapping into this potential is severely limited by the uncultivability of most 
microorganisms, i.e. the microbial dark matter. This project aimed to use microfluidic droplet cultivation 
as an innovative method to gain access to the microbial dark matter within sandy sediment, and screen 
encapsulated bacteria for antimicrobial activity against the GFP tagged fish pathogen V. anguillarum 90-
11-286 gfpmut3*.  
In this project, microorganisms of marine sandy sediment from Bellevue and V. anguillarum 90-11-286 
gfpmut*, were successfully encapsulated and cultivated using the PreciGenomes Droplet Generation 
system – light Version. It appears that microfluidic droplet cultivation of the V. anguillarum species has 
not been documented previously.  
Moreover, encapsulation and cultivation of bacteria from Rungsted, followed by picoinjection and FADS 
of the droplets were successfully performed at Novonesis. This approach led to selection of marine 
bacteria with inhibitory properties against V. anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3*.  
Encapsulation and Cultivation: Regardless of the emulsion generation system used to encapsulate the 
marine microorganisms, a great morphological diversity could be observed from both the Bellevue and 
Rungsted droplets. This shows that microfluid cultivation can be used to cultivate different microbes, 
which is essential for exploring the microbial dark matter. The study conducted by Mahlar et al. 2021 
also observed a wide range of morphological droplets containing soil microorganisms, such as different 
sizes and cell densities of cocci-, rod-, and filamentous shaped growth[19]. Subsequent identification of 
their encapsulated cell and the parallel cultivation on solid media resulted in the assigned of 238 genera 
across the two cultivation methods, from where 93 genera were unique to droplets and three were unique 
to the plates. Additional analysis of the OTU reviled that out of 1687 OTUs, 983 OTUs was unique to 
droplets, while 27 was unique to plates[19]. Their results empathies that droplet cultivation is highly 
==PAGE:59==
Master thesis  
 
DTU 
Page 58 of 74 
 
efficient in cultivating a large proportion of microbial diversity, and thereby gives a good foundation for 
tapping into the microbial dark matter. 
Droplet manipulation: FADS. A total of 752 out of ~750000 droplets across all emulations (T1-T9) 
were sorted based on low green fluorescence signal, which corresponds to roughly ~0.1 %. The likelihood 
that the cell within the droplets having an influence on the growth of V. anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3*, 
is therefore large. Based on the observed morphological diversity and different cell density of droplets 
pre- and post picoinjection. The limited growth of V. anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3* resulting in a low 
GFP signal could have been influenced due to different reasons. Firstly and most interestingly would be 
inhibition due to the production of an antimicrobial compounds with mechanisms, such as inhibition of  
the cell wall synthesis, protein synthesis, nuclei acid synthesis or metabolic pathways[45].  The low green 
fluorescence signal within the droplets could also be the result of nutrient competition, i.e. competitive 
exclusion[25], which would not be surprising as high cell density can be observed in some of the droplets 
(Figure 5.12), Additionally, the low growth of the pathogen could also be the result of changes to the 
environmental conditions within the droplets, such as pH or oxygen levels, caused by the metabolic 
activity of the encapsulated marine cells[25].  
It must be noted that the morphology observations are based on the 12 available images of the sorted 
droplets, which may not be representative of the overall morphology diversity.  
A low GFP fluorescent signal could also be due to a lack of V. anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3* cells 
during the picoinjections. However, the counter dyes Texas red and Cascade blue were spiked in the V. 
anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3* culture to control of these events during sorting. Droplets with no form 
of fluorescent dye would therefore not be collected, preventing false positive hits.  
Screening of non GFP FADS bacteria on solid and in liquid media  
Liquid cultivation: From the screening for non GFP FADS bacteria in liquid media, multiple wells with 
growth but no fluorescence was achieved. Additionally, wells with high growth but a relatively low GFP 
signal were also observed. Wells with no GFP are highly relevant for further cultivation, verification of 
inhibitory properties and identification. The wells with relatively low fluorescence may not be directly 
relevant for further analysis, but the observation does indicate the presence of biomass in the wells that 
inhibits the growth of V. anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3*. As the number of wells occupied by growth 
==PAGE:60==
Master thesis  
 
DTU 
Page 59 of 74 
 
was higher than expected the likelihood of only a single cell being inoculated from the start was low, 
meaning that there is a risk that more than one cell was cultivated in the wells with growth.   
Solid cultivation: Multiple non-fluorescent colonies were observed on the MBG plates, which all would 
be interesting for further analyses. Especially interesting are the colonies of T1 which gained a dark 
brown pigment and odor between six and 20 days of incubation, the same pigment and odor was also 
observed from the liquid cultures of T1. This is of particular interest, as pigments often an indicate NP 
production[46]  
Another observation of the MBG plates was how fluorescent colonies and non-fluorescent colonies could 
seemingly growth in close proximity to one another, which again brings the mode of action of the 
inhibition in question. However, it is important to emphasize that some bacteria could produce bioactive 
compounds in liquid media and not on solid media and vice versa.  
By comparing the overall results from screening of non GFP FADS bacteria on solid and liquid media, 
T1, T2, T7 and T8 showed potential, as non-fluorescence growth was observed from both cultivation 
methods.  
Minimal or no growth in T4 (collected 160 droplets) and T9 (collected 210 droplets) might be due to 
errors during fluorescence-activated droplet sorting step, meaning that cells did not reach the collection 
tube or inaccuracies in samples dilution for CFU quantification, or the bacteria's failure to grow in liquid 
under the experimental set conditions. 
Verification and 16S rRNA gene sequencing of selected non-GFP FADS bacteria  
After the laboratory work of the projects had ended, 88 non GFP FADS cultures across both cultivation 
methods were tested for inhibitory properties against V. anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3* and identified 
via 16S rRNA gene sequencing. This work was performed by Iuliana Nita. The sequencing results 
revealed the identification of five strains with inhibitory properties against the chosen pathogen. The five 
strains were Vibrio splendidus, Vibrio rumoiensis, Vibrio lentus, Enterovibrio calviensis and Sulfitobacter 
marinus. V. splendidus is also a common marine pathogen, and may not be relevant for further 
analysis[47]. The four other strains could still be interesting for further analysis. Multiple Vibrio spp. are 
known to produce Bacteriocins, which are antimicrobial proteins or peptides with a narrow-spectrum 
activity against related species[48]. Bacteriocins produced by Vibrio spp. are called vibriocin. Due to the 
==PAGE:61==
Master thesis  
 
DTU 
Page 60 of 74 
 
good antibacterial activity of vibriocin against closely related Vibrio members, it has potential as a 
measurement against vibriosis in for example aquaculture[48][49].  Vibrio spp. have even been 
successfully used as probiotics in mollusk, crustacean and finfish aquaculture. However, the use of Vibrio 
spp as probiotics is not recommended due to their opportunistic nature and pathogenesis [48]. 
Sulfitobacter belongs to the Roseobacter group, from where the bacterium Pheaobactor can also be found 
[49][50]. P. inhibens produces the bactericidal compound Tropodithietic acid (TDA), which has 
previously shown significant inhibitory activity against V. anguillarum[49].  
During the search for bacteria with antimicrobial properties using droplet microfluidics, Iuliana Nita 
identified strains that, while not necessarily inhibiting V. anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3*, appear to 
may be novel at the species level (~97 % identity, including uncultured environmental sequences). These 
strains are closely related to Psychromonas marina and Psychromonas profunda or inhibiting strain 
Enterovibrio calviensis. These mentioned findings are not included in this report, but underscore the 
potential of this technique to discover new strains, expanding our microbial knowledge and opening paths 
for future research.  
The field of microfluidics droplet cultivation as an approach for cultivating hitherto uncultured microbes 
with antimicrobial biosynthetic potential, is relatively new. This project is important to the field as it can 
aid in broadening the understanding of the efficiency and applicability of the cultivation method as well 
as the use of picoinjection and FADS for investigating droplets of interest.   
Aside from the positive results of isolating bacteria exhibiting inhibitory activity against V. anguillarum 
90-11-286 gfpmut3*, the approach of this project did have some limitations. Firstly, the thresholds of the 
FAD sorting were created without knowing the fluorescence signal of the initially injected droplets. 
Making it harder to distinguish when inhibition had occurred. Secondly, quantification of the cells via 
flow cytometry did not perfectly correspond with observed growth in droplets, wells and plates, as 
sometimes the Flow cytometer seemingly underestimated the cell concentration and vice versa. This was 
not so much of a problem for the screen conduct on solid media as single colonies archived, however it 
did pose a problem when an accurate cell concentration was needed for the liquid screening. 
Autofluorescence or inaccurate gating could be the cause of this. Staining the cell and counting them 
==PAGE:62==
Master thesis  
 
DTU 
Page 61 of 74 
 
under the microscope could potentially be a better approach for a more accurate cell quantification for 
future work.  
The approach chosen for the screening of non GFP FADS bacteria also has limitations, as it is biased 
towards bacteria of high abundance as well as bacteria that can grow using traditional cultivation 
methods. The domestication of bacteria seen from Ichip cultivation has yet to be reported for droplet 
cultivation. Additionally, it would have been favorable to run the FADS cells through FACS prior to 
screening, as to decrease the noise of the remained V. anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3*. The project has 
been based on a trial-and-error approach, which is why, aside from possibly introducing FACS to this 
approach, several other alterations could enhance future work. This could for an example be the inclusion 
of the droplet dilution method were a bulk of droplets-of-interest are diluted using other droplets 
containing only media as dilution agent[43]. With the possibility of diluting droplets, investigating single 
droplets would become easier. Additionally, inspiration from Mahler et al., 2021 can be taken, were 
droplets were transferred to a spinning solid media plate at a slow pace, allowing the droplets to be spread 
out evenly on the plate[19]. Multiple colonies and liquid cultures with potentially inhibitory effects on 
the chosen fish pathogen, were screened.  The inhibitory effect was verified for five isolated strains from 
where at least four of them are interesting for further analysis, next cause of action could potentially be 
additional inhibition assays against other pathogens, (e.g. gram positives, human pathogen) to assess the 
antimicrobial range of the inhibitory property of the strains. Furthermore, analysis into the inhibitory 
mechanisms would also be essential. Additionally whole genome sequencing of the strains would be 
favorable to assess the novelty of the strains, as 16S rRNA sequencing is not reliable under the taxonomic 
levels of genera and species.

7 Conclusion  
To combat the approaching antimicrobial resistance crisis, it is crucial that novel antimicrobial 
compounds are discovered, as well as developing cultivation methods to grow the microorganisms that 
produce them. Studies suggest that droplet microfluids have the potential for tapping into microbial dark 
matter, which is rich in novel antimicrobial compounds. In this project, in-house droplets were 
successfully generated via the PreciGenome system, and growth of marine bacteria from Bellevue and 
the GFP tagged V. anguillarum 90-11-286 gfpmut3* were observed in the microscope. Utilizing droplet 
microfluidics, including cell encapsulation, picoinjection, and FADS enabled the sorting of 752 out of 
approximately 750,000 droplets with antimicrobial activity. Ultimately, leading to the identification of 
five bacterial strains with inhibitory properties against the GFP-tagged fish pathogen V. anguillarum 90-
11-286 gfpmut3*.  
Overall, this project has shown that droplet microfluidic cultivation along the droplet operations 
picoinjection and FADS has potential as an approach for cultivating microbes with inhibitory properties 
against the fish pathogen.
