1. ABSTRACT
MSc Thesis
1
Abstract
Maersk Line A/S operates thousands of refrigerated containers and have collected data
from journeys for a number of years with the purpose of improving the cold chain in various
ways. Better models of the cargo temperature is a starting point for reducing food waste,
faster contingency plans and potentially better control strategies, which would have a positive
economic and environmental impact. This thesis explores the potential of applying a mixed-
eﬀects framework to model the thermal response of a population of refrigerated containers
carrying bananas in what appears to be the ﬁrst time in literature. The framework proved
capable of reducing the unexplained variance and appears to be a promising approach. The
ﬁnal model is a non-linear mixed-eﬀects model based on simple diﬀerential equations, which
was derived using thermodynamics. The model is able to describe the main dynamics of the
two states using supply temperature and ambient temperature as inputs, though lacking the
capability of describing the system fully. The potential for using between-individual covariates
is explored and several candidates were found. One of the predicted random eﬀects indicated
a possibility of identifying faults in the cooling system based on the value of the physical
parameters. However, further work is required in order to explore the full potential as several
statistical assumptions of the model were not met at this point.

3. INTRODUCTION

Introduction
3.1
Problem statement
Transporting temperature sensitive cargo is done in refrigerated shipping containers called
reefers, which are containers equipped with integrated refrigeration units in one end of the
container, visible to the left on Figure 1.
Figure 1: Maersk reefers
These reefers are used to transport a variety of goods from fruits to pharmaceuticals. The
reefer shipping industry is of high value to A. P. Møller-Maersk A/S (Maersk) and an important
part of the global cold chain. Here the cold chain (temperature controlled supply chain) deﬁnes
the entire system of storing and transporting goods at the recommended temperatures from
manufacturer to consumer. The following facts highlight two important aspects of the business:
I: Reefers are only transporting ∼10% of the total cargo of Maersk Line A/S, but
account for ∼15% of the revenue. [3]
The reefer business area yields greater revenue than dry shipping, but does also require more
techincal knowledge. In order to get ahead of competitors in the future Maersk must utilize
the wast amounts of data to oﬀer more value to its customers. The reefer market is growing
and Maersk is a big player, who over the last couple of years have invested a large amount of
money in installing the so called Remote Container Management (RCM) system, which enables
Maersk to receive data from the reefers and to some extend control them online. This initiative
and the aquisition of Hamburg S¨ud, another strong player in the reefer market, underlines the

importance of the reefer business and the commitment of Maersk to become a data driven
company.
II: One third of world-wide food production does not arrive at the consumer in an
acceptable state. A major portion of these losses is due to variation in environmental
conditions during the cold chain.
[4]
Better understanding of the temperature dynamics of cargo could potentially reduce food waste
and enable more energy eﬃcient control schemes, both with a positive environmental impact.
Reefers carry many diﬀerent food items such as fruit, meat, dairy, seafood and pharmaceuticals,
which require a wide variety of conditions including temperature and atmosphere conditions:
From low volume pharmaceuticals worth millions of dollars per reefer to bananas worth thou-
sands of dollars per container. From shipping sushi grade tuna in -60 degrees Celsius to avocados
in humidity and CO2 controlled atmospheres. This thesis will use data from a collaboration
with the Dole Food Company Inc. and will therefore focus only on shipments of bananas, which
is the item that is shipped most of in reefers globally [5].
Understanding what happens to the cargo in these reefers is of high importance to both
Maersk and its customers.
This understanding serves as the starting point for improving
conditions and enables faster execution of contingency plans and cargo value repositioning
in certain cases. To improve Maersk’s use of the RCM data for predictions, this thesis will
attempt to establish the link between the measured temperatures in the refrigeration unit and
cargo temperature.
The chosen method is inspired by the approach taken in pharmacokinetics, which uses both
domain knowledge and statistics, potentially resulting in non-linear mixed-eﬀects models. The
aim is to use domain knowledge to create mathematical equations describing the system, either
in closed form or as diﬀerential equations, and use statistics to estimate physical parameters
and to decompose statistical variance into within- and between-individual components.
The proposed research question is concerned both with the theoretical population of reefers
and with how individual reefers diﬀer from this population. Ideally the variance would be
explained as much as possible, such that predicting temperature for individual reefers becomes
as accurate as possible.
Establishing this link between measured temperatures and the cargo temperature will provide
the basis to determine the cargo-speciﬁc thermal parameters enabling both individual predic-
tions with higher accuracy and potentially individually tailored control schemes.
3.2
Technical introduction to reefers
The working principle is that the cold air is blown in from the bottom of the reefer and the
hot air exits at the top. The supply air is entering a so-called T-bar ﬂoor, so it can ideally rise
uniformly in the total ﬂoor area, as shown in Figure 2a [6] and 2b [5].

Most of the air inside the reefer is re-cycled, but for maintaining a desired atmospheric
condition a fresh air intake can be adjusted manually prior to departure, but cannot be con-
trolled online in most reefers. It is adjusted to a certain posiiton which can be approximately
converted to a volumeﬂow in the range of 0 – 75 [m3 h−1].
Reefer types: 7 diﬀerent reefer types are beeing used currently. Some are being used for
a wide range of purposes, some are designed to ﬁt a few products and others are beeing fased
out of use. Three overall reefer types exist according to [7] and Maersk has all 3 diﬀerent types,
though not all are used for transporting bananas. From an overall perspective they are all
similar in the sense that cold air enters from the bottom and exits at the top. In this thesis
the diﬀerence between the types are therefore neglected in the physical modelling.
Cooling unit: The cooling unit itself will not be investigated in depth in this thesis, but a
brief introduction to the working principle seems to be in order. All reefer type cooling units is
basically a classic vapor-compressor refrigeration cycle with 4 main components: compressor,
condenser, expansion valve and evaporator. On Figure 3b [6] the cooling cycle is shown and an
example of practical implementation is shown on Figure 3a of MCI Star Cool model. Besides
the standard cooling components an economizer added to reduce power consumption. The
forced convection on evaporator and condenser is achieved by the two fans. Furthermore, there
is also an electric heating element placed near the evaporator (not shown), which can be used
when the ambient temperature is colder than the cargo should be. It should be noted that in
general the reefer is working in cooling mode.

Control system: A control system aims to keep the cargo at a ﬁxed temperature. Two
versions of this control system exists and both are focusing mainly on the cooling unit, and
they do not have a physical model of the cargo and container.
The information on which
control system is used for each is not readily available, but perhaps obtainable through diﬀerent
databases, which could be explored in future research. The two versions are
• QUEST 1: Keeping supply temperature equal to setpoint.
• QUEST 2: Kepping mean of supply and return temperature to setpoint.
Therefore Quest 1 operates without feedback from the cargo: the supply temperature is con-
trolled to reach the setpoint, without any considerations to the status of the cargo. Quest 2
does incorporate feedback from the cargo (return temperature), but does so without a physical
model of the cargo.
It is not fully understood how the fans are controlled.
Data: The cooling unit is logging SCADA data such as the measured temperatures, pres-
sures, voltage, power, etc together with GPS location and alarms, which is available for mod-
elling purposes. For most journeys the data is available online and updated hourly.
Besides this data set a collaboration with the banana company Dole Food Company Inc. have
supplied temperature measurements from within the cargo for around 1000 journeys. Both
datasets will be described in detail in Section 4.

Merging procedure:
The SCADA dataset contains information on many diﬀerent reefers
while the Temptale data set only contains data for banana shipments. As of this momment a
subset of the SCADA set have been used, but this contains 93 % of the journeys in the temptale
data set. Obviously the remaining 7 % should be included, but for now in this exploritive phase
it is deemed suﬃcient to proceed with.
Both datsets contain the variable container id, which can be used to merge the datasets.
They also contain information on the voyage (voyage code in SCADA and voyage number
in Temptale), however one is a string and the other an integer. Perhabs there is a conversion
mapping between them, but this was not readily available. Journey information is needed as a
container could be measured on multiple journeys, which needs to be split. This is done instead
by using the serial number and matching the timestamps between the databases.

Missing data:
This is an issue to be concerned with in this data set, especially for variable
ambient. For a fairly large amount of time series it appears that the ambient is missing
completely, which is driving the amount of missing data. This is problematic if this covariate
is to be used, and three diﬀerent options was considered:
1. Remove all time series that have 100 % missing value in ambient from the data set.
Without knowing the reason why these individuals are missing this covariate potentially
introduces a bias in the model, if say a certain reefer type or model version does not log
this measurement.
2. Remove the mathematical terms from the diﬀerential equations for these series.
This means that eﬀectively there would be two diﬀerent models used simultaneously,
which could make interpretation of remaining mathematical terms less clean.
3. Replace values in those time series with a constant average value such as the median or
mean.
Just choosing which average measure to use is somewhat unclear, and using a constant
value in place of an latent non-constant value could give confusing results.
It is chosen to use option 1. as there is a fairly large number of time series left to work with
and it would hopefully give the most interpretative results. The %-missing values decrease to
0.2 % after this operation and the remaining descriptive statistcs does not change noteworthy.
Investigating the reason for this missing ambient data is a relevant point to consider in future

The remaining missing values are treated diﬀerently depending on how the covariate with

These variables are implemented by an approximation function that interpolate values to

The MCAR assumtion is not ivestigated in depth and therefore the choice potentially introduce

more sophisticated methods that are statistically more sound, but the issue was not investigated

5. LITTERATURE STUDY
MSc Thesis
5
Litterature study
In this section the prior work in the literature is presented. It is split in two sections: Main
issues investigated and the diﬀerent modelling approaches taken.
5.1
Main issues
A number of challenges when shipping temperature sensitive cargo is presented in the lit-
erature and can be grouped in to two main areas relevant to this thesis:
1. Eﬀects of the atmospheric conditions such as temperature, humidity, CO, O2, etc. and
the eﬀects from the ripening process (generation of heat and CO2). In some cases the
coupling of the two is also considered.
2. Spatial temperature variations in the reefer as a result of packaging design and pallet
conﬁguration.
Several works investigate the causes of variation between the reefers, but there was not
found any work focusing on describing the variation between reefers as a population, eg.
in a mixed eﬀect model setting.
5.1.1
Atmospheric conditions and ripening
It is well known that controlling the atmospheric conditions of fruit is of high importance,
which is discussed by several papers:
[8] ﬁnds in laboratory settings that the temperature has critical impact on the greenlife of
bananas. This is done for diﬀerent enviroments in terms of O2 and CO2 levels which also has a
signiﬁcant eﬀect. Here ”greenlife” is deﬁned as the time span until the onset of a colour change
of bananas from green to yellow. [9] states that for certain apples the ripening time can be
increased from 1 day at 27 ◦C to 10 days at -1 ◦C.
[9] also investigate the combined heat and mass transfer problem, which is found to be a
highly coupled problem. They relate ripening heat generation to cargo temperature through
CO2 generation: the heat generated by ripening is a function of CO2 concentration and the CO2
concentraion is a function of cargo temperature. They also describe the modelling of thermal
properties such as speciﬁc heat, thermal conductivity as function of cargo moisture content,
which in turn is a function of air humidity and temperatures. In [10] the model is formulated
as an explicit ﬁnite diﬀerence model.
The heat generation can be formulated with at least two diﬀerent mathematical forms both

==PAGE:26==

of which are non-linearly related to temperature. In [11] they use ˙Q ∝T g, where ˙Q is the
generated heat, T is temperature and g is some cargo speciﬁc constant. This is based on the
model of [9], but not including the CO2 concentration. Another approach is using an exponen-
tial function ˙Q ∝exp(γT) used in [12], where γ is a cargo dependent constant.
[13] have through ﬁeld experiments estimated the heat generation of bananas for diﬀerent
conditions which are in the range of 20-200 ·[103W kg−1] with 50 ·103[W kg−1] in ”normal con-

Climacteric fruits, such as bananas, can create tremendous amounts of heat by res-

be removed by cooling, this creates hot spots, where the temperature increases beyond
control. In turn, the increased temperature accelerates the biological processes. [4].
5.1.2
Packaging design and pallet conﬁguration
Through state space modelling and experiments [13] investigate the inﬂuence of the pack-
aging design which signiﬁcantly aﬀect the ability to remove heat from the cargo. The actual
removal of heat can be reduced far below the nominal cooling capacity:
The airﬂow from the cooling unit can remove 60[·103W kg−1] for standard packing
conditions, or 1[kW] or the full container load, which is less than 10% of the nominal
cooling capacity of the refrigeration unit. This poor relationship indicates that the
airﬂow conditions inside a container are far from being optimal [13].
Trough CFD modelling [14] also investigates the eﬀect of packaging design and ﬁnd a large
eﬀect of both packaging design and pallet conﬁguration:
This study revealed the unsuitability of the package designs for cooling operation
conditions characterized by vertical airﬂow inside reefers. In addition, the orien-
tation/conﬁguration of pallets was found to inﬂuence in airﬂow distribution, fruit
cooling rate and temperature uniformity in a reefer, due to their eﬀect on networking
and channelling air within a pallet and from pallet to pallet. [14].
The ﬁndings of [14] can be summarized to the following general spatial variations:
1. ⇕Vertically up/down: Cooling within a pallet in the reefer varies primarily because the
bottom layer of the pallets cooled much faster than the middle and top layer. This is
because the cold air enters from the bottom.

5.2
Modelling approaches
The temperature distribution is governed by partial diﬀerential equations, which also could
be considered non-linear and/or coupled to other processes as discussed above. As a result
the modelling approach to this diﬀers depending on the focus area of the research. Four main
diﬀerent approaches were found in the literature, when modelling similar problems.
1. Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) approach
In 2006 [16] reviews the previous literature using various numerical techniques in reefers of
which the majority was CFD approaches. Among their ﬁndings are that: 1) Simplifying
the problem is popular, but impairs performance: The socalled porous medium approach
to simplify complex geometries are gaining popularity, but a unlikely to be accurate. 2)
CFD approaches in general have diﬃculty in accurately reproducing experimental results.
[17] investigates the porous medium approach compared to solid block and ﬁnd that the
solid block approach gives better results, but requires nearly 4 times more mesh elements.
[14], [11] are assuming constant thermal properties and thermal equilibrium at solid/ﬂuid
boundary, while the cargo is modelled as a porous medium. Essentially this means they
are using CFD to calculate the boundary conditions of a conduction problem.
They
are focusing their eﬀorts on describing the air ﬂow, and their model has high accuracy
compared to experiments in an integral sense, but they have average spatial errors of 18
% in temperature and 26 % in velocity.
Often the radiation eﬀects is not included in the CFD models, but [18] ﬁnds a considerable
contribution for a reefer placed in the sun at a harbor. Their ﬁnd signiﬁcant eﬀect of the
radiation in this situation.
2. Lumped mass approach
Several papers investigate the problem using a lumped mass approach (LMA), and thereby
simplify the problem signiﬁcantly by neglecting the spatial gradients.
In [6] and [19] a LMA model of cargo is build, with the main focus area of modelling the
vapor-compressor cycle in the cooling unit for future control optimization purposes. It is

heat generation eﬀects, but it should be noted that the cargo is not fruit, but meat which
might diﬀerent ripening characteristics. Other works such as [20] considers a coupled heat
and mass transfer problem, that is including the ripening process.
In the area of building temperature dynamics multiple works have been carried out such
as [21], [22], [23] uses so called grey-box-models, which are stochastic diﬀerential equations
whose structure is decided based on the physics of the system, while the parameters are

3. Analytical solutions
For battery temperature modelling some works are taking a more analytically approach to

and temperature gradient together with an assumed temperature distribution formulated
as 4 order polynomial. Both [24] and [25] are estimating some of the parameters from

Theory
This section describes the relevant theory, which have been divided in to two sections:

6.1
Physics
The following simpliﬁcations are made based on the literature review in Section 5:
1. It is chosen to model the uncoupled problem.
The heat generation process could be
included in a simpliﬁed form depending only on the temperature, but the mass transfer
and atmospheric concentrations such as CO2, O2 will not be modelled. The choice is
made partly because of a time constraint of the thesis and partly because several works
in the literature have chosen to investigate the uncoupled problem.
2. Modelling the spatial variations within the reefer (pallets placed in the front vs the back,
etc.) appears diﬃcult to neglect, but any model describing this cannot be validated as
there is only one temperature logger inside the reefer in the dataset. The dataset does
contain reefers which have been recorded on several journeys, where one could assume
that the sensor is placed diﬀerently each time. This might give some insight in to the
spatial diﬀerence by comparing these journeys for the same reefer, but it will not be
possible to attribute the diﬀerence to a known change in the spatial space, and it will not
be possible to control for other factors that also change from journey to journey.
3. No information on pallet conﬁguration or packaging condition are available in the dataset,
and these are of high importance for describing the ﬂow characteristics. Modelling the
airﬂow in the reefer in detail by the CFD approach or similar, is therefore not feasible.
4. Since several works are considering constant thermodynamic properties this will also be
done in this thesis. The absolute changes in temperature are in the order of 10[◦C] in this
modelling thesis.
5. Radiation was only considered in one work in the literature and here the reefer was placed
in the sun at a harbor. Potentially it could have an eﬀect for the reefers most exposed to
the sun, but the process will not be considered in this thesis.
Even when making these simpliﬁcations, the problem is still complex from a physical per-
spective as it is a combined conduction (temperature distribution within a cargo pallet) and
advection (cooling of air stream) problem.
The focus will be to establish the simplest possible physical modal that can describe the main
dynamics of the system. In order to do so knowledge of the governing physics must be estab-
lished and the theory presented below is the foundation for this.

Convection
can be confusing because of diﬀering terminology.
In the deﬁnition by [31] convection
consists of two sub-mechanisms
• Advection, in which the transportation of energy is caused by motion of a ﬂuid. The
energy is simply carried away.
• Diﬀusion, in which the transportation of energy is caused by temperature gradients. The
energy diﬀuses in a random motion of particles.
In general it will be assumed that forced convection are present, ie. ﬂuid motion driven by a
fan and not natural convection where the diﬀerence in density is causing the ﬂuid motion.
Newtons cooling (NC) is often used to describe convective heat transfer [29], which for example
could be the heat transfer from an object to a surrounding ﬂuid stream. NC is essentially a

h = f(T, ν, ρ, cp, v(x, y, z), ∇v(x, y, z), Ra, ...)

6.2.1
State space form
Dynamic systems are often represented on the state space form which consists of state
equations and observation equations [34]. Both continuous and discrete formulations exists. In
this thesis continous state equations will be used as the physical interpretation of parameters
are more direct, however the observations (data) is discrete in nature.
In the following representation the state equations consists of n continuous coupled 1st order
ordinary diﬀerential equations (ODEs), written with the time derivative on the left hand side
as a function of all states, all inputs and time. A deterministic system would be represented as
˙x1 = f1(x, u, t)
˙x2 = f2(x, u, t)
. . .
page 33 of 101
==PAGE:35==
6. THEORY
MSc Thesis
˙xn = fn(x, u, t)
Where x = x(t) are the states, u = u(t) are inputs. In the case of a linear system the equations
can be written in (autonomous) matrix form
˙x(t) = A(t)x(t) + B(t)u(t)
where the matrix A is denoted the state / system matrix, B the input / control matrix. The
state equations are coupled to the observation equations
y(t) = C(t)x(t) + D(t)u(t)
where C is the observation equation and D is the direct transfer / feed forward matrix. In
case of constant matrices A, B, C, D the system is said to be time-invariant (LTI) and if not
they are time-varying (LTV).
The state equations shown above are deterministic, so no randomness is involved, meaning
that given a certain starting condition the system will behave exactly the same every time. Also
the observation equation is observing the world perfectly, with no noise such as measurement
inaccuracy. Both cases are simple to work with, but more or less unrealistic, and therefore
random processes can be included in both state and observation equations. Below a simple
example of this is shown
˙x(t) = A(t)x(t) + B(t)u(t) + Bv(t)v1(t)
y(t) = C(t)x(t) + D(t)u(t) + v2(t)
Here v1(t) is denoted the process noise and v2(t) is the measurement noise, both of which
could be assumed Gaussian distributed white noise for simplicity. Regardless, the addition of
random processes to the state equation (v1(t) in this case) changes the system fundamentally
from ODEs to stochastic diﬀerential equations (SDEs), which have diﬀerent mathematical char-
acteristics, especially regarding integration.
For the sake of simplicity, this thesis will only explore deterministic modelling of the system.

could be a feasible approach. The mixed-eﬀects framework have several compelling features:
1. Flexibility: Handles unbalanced repetitions (diﬀerent number of samples for each individ-
ual) and continuous time (diﬀerent time points for each individual) as well as incomplete
data.
2. Interpretability: Knowledge of the physics can be used to create governing diﬀerential

3. Variance/Covariance: Parameters can be allowed to vary from ﬁxed value, thereby de-

4. Generalization: Estimating the statistical distribution of the parameters from the sample
will give better insight in to the entire reefer population.
In essence mixed-eﬀects models are models that include both ﬁxed and random eﬀects. Fixed
eﬀects are population parameters, whereas random eﬀects are associated with individuals ran-
domly chosen from the population that deviate from the population average. By convetion the
random eﬀects are denoted by roman letters, whereas the ﬁxed eﬀects are denoted with greek
letters. An exeption is that the residual error is denoted ϵ.

Non-linear mixed-eﬀects models
This section presents the framework of [36] for Non-
linear mixed-eﬀects models (NLME). The advantages of using non-linear models instead of for
example a polynomial (which is linear in the parameters) that could also model a non-linear
phenomena is in general: interpretability as equations are based on physical principles, parsi-
mony as fewer parameters are needed and validity beyond observed range of data [38]. This
will however make the inference process more diﬃcult, as shall be discussed later in this section.
The model framework have two types of covariates:
Within-individual covariates: Ui, which are needed to describe the response of this particular
individual and can be function of time. From a control-theory perspective one could think of
this as the input to the system, e.g. amount of cooling power delivered to the system.
Between-individual covariates: Ai, which are characteristics of individuals, but may not be a
function of time. They are relevant to how individuals diﬀer and could for example be the type
of reefer (40’ or 20’).
In the [36] these are sometimes collected in one Xi = {Ui, Ai}, which corresponds more closely
to the LME notation presented above, but this thesis will adopt the notation of handling them
separately to make it more clear where in the model they apply.

The closed loop data can be used in the direct method, but it is important that the 1) refer-

good results (in an unbiased sense) are: 2) using a good noise model, 3) low noise level and that
4) there is weak feedback. Given these reservations are fulﬁlled, the results should be unbiased
if one uses a error diﬀerence method.
In this thesis the four requirements are assumed to be fulﬁlled to a reasonable degree, so
that the indirect approach can be taken.

Subsequently in Section 8.2 the data will be investigated in a log-linear form by a linear mixed-

These two sections will be the basis for applying the non-linear mixed-eﬀects model. In Section

8.1.1
The governing physical processes
Conduction and forced convection
Conduction is governed by FL in Equation 2 ( ˙T = κ∇2T), but the diﬀusion constant κ is
unknown for a pallet containing bananas. Using that of water could be a reasonable guess since
bananas consists of 75 % water according to [43], however air is present between the banana
stems through which heat can transfer.
The situation is attempted to be illustrated on Figure 20 where two pallets are seen, each with
two packaging containers with 6 banana stems. The temperature logger is placed in the upper
left of these packaging containers in a distance L from the outer wall of the pallet.
In this situation it is assumed that the heat transfer (red wiggly lines) are going from the air
stream Ta, through the pallet, through the packaging, around the banana stems, and then it
reaches the sensor. In such a situation air is the primary conductor of heat, but of course it
must overcome the resistance of the pallet and packaging and it will lose some heat by warming
up the banana stems on the way. Therefore one could argue that the real situation is less
conductive than pure air.
Another aspect of the heat transfer problem is the forced convection by the supply air. The
supply air is entering from the bottom and some are channeled around the pallets, while some
is entering the pallets and packaging (indicated by dashed lines). Its obvious that if the sensor
is placed in an air corridor it will be cooled much faster than if it is positioned somewhere less
exposed to the forced convection.
In summary it is very diﬃcult to give a reasonable expected diﬀusion constant for such an
in-homogeneous material, when the local physical system is assumed to have a large impact on
the response measured by the sensor.

where neither conduction nor convection was negligible, but [6] assumes the conduction process
is negligible and obtains the same result as the Nusselt correlation.
Two hypothesis could explain this:
1. Conduction is actually negligible. Perhaps the ﬂow of air within the pallets (the dashed
lines in Figure 20) reduces the eﬀective length L so much that conduction is not important.
2. Had the temperature sensor in the experiment of [6] been placed in a diﬀerent position,
either closer to the center or closer to the surface of the pallet, the results would have
been diﬀerent. The value hA[6] therefore eﬀectively contains both the true hA-value and
the internal conduction resistance from the edge of the pallet to their sensor.
Without knowing more about the details of how the experiment was conducted in [6] it is not
possible to say something conclusively. Both the sensor placement, pallet conﬁguration and
packaging design could inﬂuence the results.

Real data and mixed-eﬀects:
The mixed-eﬀects modelling procedure of [38] was used as guideline, and is summarized in
a simple form below:
1. Gain insight in to the ﬁxed / random eﬀect structure by ﬁtting individual models to each
individual. From the variation of these parameters determine a structure of ﬁxed and
mixed-eﬀects.
2. Fit a mixed-eﬀects model with the decided structure. Check assumptions is not violated
and investigate random eﬀects for over-parameterization.
3. Investigate potential between-individual covariates that can explain random eﬀect varia-
tion. Fit new model with covariates.
4. Adding between-individual covariates to the model can change the random eﬀects, so one
will iterate between step 2 and 3.
The procedure was not followed strictly as step 1 and partly step 2 have been conducted in A
priori expectations Section 8.1 and in the log-linear model Section 8.2. It was expected that
the conclusions could be extended to the non-linear case: ωs, ωr varied across individuals and
should therefore be mixed-eﬀects. The same was found for the decay rate γ. One complication
is that the decay rate is decomposed in αs, αr and αr, but since all three would arguable depend
on the spatial placement of the sensor, it was decided to model all as mixed-eﬀects as well from
the beginning. Strictly speaking, this contradicts the bottom-up approach suggested by [38].
The thermal capacitance’s where kept at the values from previous section.

moving on to step 3 in the guidelines: exploring the variation of the predicted random eﬀects
using between-individual covariates. This aspect of the mixed-eﬀects framework illustrates an
interesting bridge between physical modelling and data science, and therefore it is chosen to
move on and present step 3, although it is strictly speaking too soon.
This exploration will only be done for the ICs wc, wr, by considering values at the time of
the departure. When there is no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the two SGs only SG3 will be
shown.

The variation of the a-values was not investigated extensively, but one interesting ﬁnd was
made in connection with manuel shipment evaluation, which contains a record of any
faults that are determined at the end of the journey.
In Figure 44 the a-parameters of SG2 are plotted agains the result of the manuel shipment
evalutation. There a noticable pattern that the faults Cool chain Latin, Power outage, Poor

online cargo temperature prediction setting this covariate cannot be used, but the connection is
interesting and invites one to investigate the potential connection between faults on the system
and estimated physical parameters at a later stage.

Conclusions on the non-linear model:
Diﬀerent starting guesses inﬂuence the converged values, though several converge to similar
ﬁxed eﬀects and diﬀer only on random eﬀects. The fact that they did not converge to the same
values indicate a non-convex optimization surface. The investigated SGs, that had diﬀerent
ﬁxed eﬀects, predicted remarkably similar for many individuals, both on a population and
individual level.
In total, these issues suggests that the model does not represent the system is completely at
this point. Whether this can be rectiﬁed by restricting the parameter space, udpdating random
eﬀects and variance structure or by changing the state space model has yet to be explored.
Assumptions on normally distributed random eﬀects are not fulﬁlled for several of the

parameters, and also independence was clearly violated for the two ICs, thus changes in the
variance structure is required. For SG3 one random eﬀect are a candidate for removal, indicating

The residuals of both states are autocorrelated and not normally distributed, indicating the
model does not explain the behavior of the system fully, however the model does seems ﬂexible

Between-individual covariates for determining the ICs have been explored and shows several

the journey based on the physical parameters for the individual reefer, though this was not
explored in depth.
The exploration of between-individual covariates have not been exhaustive and it would be
natural to explore this further, considering for example the age of container etc. However,
ﬁnding a covariance structure so the random eﬀects do not violate the assumptions should be
the priority as the ﬁxed eﬀects estimates are biased otherwise.
Next natural next step would be to add a correlation between the ICs and subsequently
adding the between-individual covariate location type. If convergence issues persists, one
might have to consider changes to the state space model as well.
8.4
Discussion
Section 8.1, 8.2, 8.3 contains what can be considered a ﬁrst iteration in the modelling
procedure: gaining physical understanding of the problem, ﬁtting individual functions, applying
a linear mixed-eﬀects model, applying a non-linear mixed-eﬀects model and exploring potential
between individual covariates.
After completing this ﬁrst iteration and having evaluated the statistical assumptions (mainly
on the non-linear model) it is relevant to remind one of the higher level assumptions and
simpliﬁcations that have been taking as well:
1. Hierachy in mixed-eﬀects model.
2. Neglecting diﬀerence in reefer types.
3. Assuming data is missing at random.
4. Closed loop assumptions: excitation of the entire spectrum of interest, good noise model,
low noise level, weak feedback and the estimation procedure holds as a error diﬀerence
method.
5. Assuming constant thermodynamic properties.
6. Neglecting mass transfer and atmospheric changes.

9. Neglecting temperature gradients (LMA approach).

From a mixed-eﬀects aspect one could argue that the hierachy of the non-linear model could be
extended. In the modelling hierarchy the journey was deﬁned as the individual level, meaning
that each reefer completing two journeys will be considered as two separate individuals. This
was done in order to include the expected variance arising from the spatial placement of the
temperature sensor within the cargo, pallet conﬁguration and packaging, as this was expected
to be very important. But this also means the model eﬀectively does not say anything about
how individual reefers diﬀer and this could be included as a nested random eﬀect. Reefer types,
manufacturer, etc. could also be included as a between-individual covariate.
From a system identiﬁcation point item 3 and 4 are still also relevant points for discussion.
Due to item 5 to 10, it also seems appropriate to question whether the governing physical
model is adequately describing the system, especially when combining this with the conver-
gence issues experienced in the non-linear model. However, it is diﬃcult to say conclusively
without working further on it and exploring the full potential. At this point in time the con-
clusion regarding the physical model is that it appears ﬂexible enough to model the majority
of the dynamics, but not all, eg. fast changes in Tr without changes in Tc.
One of the main phenomena that is not taken into account is the amount of air blown in to the
reefer by the evaporator fan (evaporator motor fan status), and the model essentially
cools the same when the fan is on or oﬀ. Both from physical reasoning and evaluation of Figure
8, this is appears not to be the case.
This represents several interesting decisions where one can choose to use either the statistical
or the physical toolbox to solve a problem: One could allow αs to be a time varying parameter
and use evaporator motor fan status as a within-individual covariate, thereby taking
in to account a ﬂuid mechanic phenomena by a regression approach, contrary to modelling it
explicitly in a physical sense, where one needs to include it in the diﬀerential equations. Taking
this approach one would not need to link the ”status” of a fan to a speciﬁc mass ﬂow rate
in physical units, which is diﬃcult even when knowing the Fan laws, Equation 3 without an
experiment.
Finally, the system was assumed deterministic but with the auto-correlated residuals, this is
perhaps not a reasonable assumption, and stochastic diﬀerential equations could be considered.

9. CONCLUSION
MSc Thesis
9
Conclusion
In this thesis the potential of using a mixed-eﬀects framework to model the thermal response
of a reefer population has been explored. This appears to be the ﬁrst time in the literature.
The theoretical considerations were presented and it was realized that the use of operational
closed loop data oﬀers some potential issues, which should be explored further.
The physical processes of the system have been discussed and it was concluded that nei-
ther convection nor conduction can be neglected when considering the estimated Biot number.
Works in the literature uses a lumped mass approach and this was concluded to be a reasonable
simpliﬁcation for a ﬁrst model, even though the theoretical justiﬁcations were not met entirely.
It was shown that the hourly sampling rate of the data set makes it unfeasible to consider other
thermal masses than the cargo.
The practical aspect of deciding on what software package to use was explored in a simula-
tion study together with the implications of going from an analytical solution to a numerical
integration of a diﬀerential equation. As expected using the true analytical solution yields
the best results, though it is limited in the sense that many physical systems are governed by
diﬀerential equations which not necessarily have analytical solutions. Of the tested methods
the solution of combining a numerical integration of the diﬀerential equations with the nlme
was preferable, as it oﬀered a compelling compromise between modelling ﬂexibility, unbiased
parameter estimates and computational speed.
By ﬁtting exponential functions to each journey it was shown that individual variations
are present, which implies the mixed-eﬀects framework is applicable in this context.
This
was supported by applying a linear mixed-eﬀects model which was capable of reducing the
unexplained variation of the log-transformed data.
The non-linear mixed-eﬀects model was applied using coupled diﬀerential equations of two
lumped masses, the so called ”two compartment model”. When estimating the parameters,
two diﬀerent potential issues emerged: 1) ﬁxed eﬀects estimation could be more diﬃcult if the
input signals are constant. 2) ﬁxed and especially random eﬀects random eﬀects are sensitive
to starting guesses.
Random eﬀects appeared to be violating the normality assumption in varying degrees. The
random eﬀects appear independent, except for the initial conditions that showed a correlation.
The residuals were very autocorrelated perhaps indicating the system is stochastic and the
deterministic approach is insuﬃcient. The residuals were also heavy tailed. It was evident that
the model is ﬂexible enough to capture the overall dynamics of the data by using the inputs,
but is clearly a simpliﬁcation of the true system.
The capability of splitting the variance was shown and the potential for introducing explaining
covariates was demonstrated for the two compartment model. In general the mixed-eﬀects
framework combined with physically derived diﬀerential equations seems promising, but require
more work.

9. CONCLUSION
MSc Thesis
Future work:
In addition to the steps mentioned above the future work includes continuing
work in the modelling procedure. At this point in time, only one iteration has been completed
and it seems reasonabl to continue iterating between updating the covariance structure and
adding explaining covariates in order to see how well the two compartment model can ﬁt the
data and predict new observations.
The model prediction performance was not tested in this thesis, partly because of the time con-
straint and partly because it was deemed more useful to explore the concept of the framework,
rather than asses a model that obviously needs to address the issues raised above. However,
this should be a part of the future work.
If the results of the continued work with the two compartment model is unsatisfactory, one must
consider the higher level assumptions and simpliﬁcations listed in the conclusion of Section 8.3.
One could consider adding a stochastic part to the diﬀerential equations or including other
physical phenomena such as heat generation. It is also possible that the physical model is too
simpliﬁed and one must consider completely diﬀerent diﬀerential equations, perhaps allowing
the placement the placement of the temperature sensor to be included directly in the model.
