Abstract
The main objective of this project is to reduce the oil consumption inside the combustion
chamber in a large two-stroke marine engine.
A high percentage of the power loss in a two-stroke diesel engine is taking place in the com-
bustion chamber, and the greatest power loss in the chamber is located between the contact
surface of the piston rings and the cylinder liner. This happens due to the high pressure
inside the chamber which pushes the piston rings toward the cylinder liner, whereof the
only thing separating them is a thin lubrication ’¼ülm. A study of the hydrodynamic be-
havior is done here by the use of Reynolds equation as the primary tool to calculate the
pressure distribution in the thin ’¼ülm ’¼éuid.
This report is conducted to investigate the lubrication behavior under di’¼Ćerent load and
velocity conditions, together with the in’¼éuence of the piston ring curvature. The analysis
is made for two simpli’¼üed piston ring shapes. The ’¼ürst is a linear case which can be solved
analytically and the second is a non-linear case which cannot be handled analytically, but
instead numerically while considering a parabolic shape.
In reality, many parameters do have an in’¼éuence on the ’¼ülm thickness, which is why
it is always a good idea to perform physical measurements. This is done here by a re-
ciprocating test ring equipped with multiple measurement devices like proximity sensors,
laser-induced Florence (LIF) and force transducers, etc. Additionally, the focus on the
LIF system is at high priority due to its sensitivity and complexity in terms of beam
adjustment, implementation of ’¼über optics and calibration.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Diesel engines are considered one of the largest contributors to environmental pollution
caused by exhaust emissions. Together with the high governmental requirements, forces
the engine companies to reduce emissions. Studies have shown that more than 50% of total
emission is particulate matter (PM) pollution, which can be seen as black smoke. This
smoke consists of heavy hydrocarbons which are partially derived from the lubrication oil
1 located between the combustion chamber and the piston. This oil plays an important
factor, as the piston rings need to be su’¼āciently lubricated since it is directly connected
to the engine e’¼āciency, in terms of friction forces which contributes to the power loss, oil
consumption and wear.
The lubrication of the piston rings for a two and four-stroke diesel engine is done dif-
ferently. The four-stroke has an oil sump that is pumped through the circuit of oil ways
to lubricate the bearing, piston, valves, etc. The circuited oil will then be ’¼ültered and
returned to the oil sump. With this system, the amount of oil needed can be accurately
prede’¼üned which keeps the waste level to a minimum. The two-stroke diesel, on the other
hand, has no oil sump but instead multiple oil inlets in the cylinder wall which injects oil
at each cycle. Here, an excessive amount of oil will end up being burned or an insu’¼ācient
amount increases the wear.
Ways to obtain a su’¼ācient amount of oil could be done by placing the oil inlet correctly
(this is not covered in this thesis) together with the right amount of oil needed at the right
time. This can be achieved for a known oil ’¼ülm thickness between the contact surface
of the piston rings and the cylinder liner. A common way to perform this measurement
is by the use of a proximity sensor, but the sensor itself may have some disadvantages
as there will be a change in the current across the cylinder liner. Another measurement
device that is newer than the proximity sensors, but yet not as used, is the Laser-Induced
Fluorescence (LIF). This device does not depend on the current, but instead the re’¼éection
of the ’¼éuorescence particles in the oil. It is known for its high sensitivity, giving it the
potential to perform measurements in micro-scale.

1. INTRODUCTION
1.1
Piston ring
A traditional diesel engine has between two and ’¼üve rings. Its functionality is to prevent
the combustion gasses from bypassing the piston and avoid contact with the cylinder liner
while maintaining the lowest friction possible. From ’¼ügure 1.1 it is shown that the upper
ring is a compression ring, which acts as a control pressure relief, allowing a smaller amount
of pressure to pass through a small groove. This is made to distribute the total pressure
drop to more than one ring.
Figure 1.1: Illustration of the piston ring
The high pressure from the combustion chamber pushes the piston ring against the piston
shoulder to prevent the pressure to escape. Furthermore, the backside pressure expands
the piston ring, where the only thing separating the pistons ring form the cylinder liner is
a thin oil ’¼ülm.

2.5
Geometrical Characteristics of Surfaces
The hydrodynamic lubrication theory underlies the assumption of a perfectly smooth sur-
face. This assumption cannot be true in real life as every surface has a texture, which in
this case could in’¼éuence the pressure development, load-carrying capacity, and wear. It is,
therefore, an important factor to be taken into consideration.
Figure 2.3: Characteristics of Surfaces
There are three types of geometric characteristics that describes the texture of the surfaces
and shape:
ŌĆó Waviness is usually associated with the machining tool, which can, for instance,
occur from unwanted induced vibration. The typical characteristics are relatively
long waves in a surface pro’¼üle.
ŌĆó Roughness occurs from the cutting tools and polishing process during production.
In contrast to the Waviness, the surface roughness is typically ’¼éuctuations in the
surface with shorter wavelength than the waviness sampling length.
ŌĆó Error of form is inherent in the manufacturing process. It is the gross deviation
from the nominal shape of a very long wavelength and is not considered as a part of
the surface texture but rather the shape of the surface. For instance the straightness,
circularity, sphericity, and cylindricity.

Conclusion
The su’¼ācient amount of lubrication between the piston ring and cylinder liner carried out
from by a theoretical and experimental approach.
A theoretical model was was conducted, from which a graduate advancement was made by
adding various parameters variables, in order to simulate the pressure pushing the piston
ring inner surface against the cylinder liner. The advancement of the COMSOL model
concluded with a transient load analysis of which the ’¼ülm thickness was obtained.
Tests from the reciprocating test rig have been performed together with the calibration
of the force transducers and proximity sensors as part of the hydrodynamic lubrication
study. The results form the proximity sensors were not able to measure the ’¼ülm thickness,
but they managed to show similar results when the lubricated is subtracted for the dry
measurements.
The LIF system included several collimator adjustments in terms of focusing and cen-
tering the laser beam which resulted in a focused green laser beam to be received by the
photomultiplier. Moreover, the implementation of the ’¼über optics was done together with
the LIF system extension to perform measurements at three locations. However, this in-
creased the noise level due to the limited amount of light compared with output 1. Hence,
it would be recommended to run with two laser outputs instead of three. A calibration
attempt of the LIF system was made under static and dynamic conditions. The result of
the static calibration with a pulsating laser beam showed similar tendencies as [4]. Unfor-
tunately, a high % error was addressed with the dynamic calibration, which was due to
the low amount of grooves and the assumption of a linear behavior within the range of 40
to 90 ┬Ąm.
Di’¼Ćerent load conditions, angular velocity, and shoulder height where studied using the
friction force, LIF, and COMSOL simulation. Here, a good correlation between the exper-
imented and simulated was found with a few exceptions like the 1 RPM LIF measurement
where the ’¼ülm thickness was expected to be constant.

Description 
MOTOREX HEAVY DUTY SAE 50 is an alloyed, mi-
neral-oil-based monograde motor oil. Selected base 
oils in combination with ideally coordinated additives 
provide optimal protection against wear and oxida-
tion. High-quality detergent additives dissolve dirt 
and deposits, and guide these to the oil filter with 
the help of selected dispersants. 
Advantages 
’éĘ 
Optimally formulated for classic vehicles 
’éĘ

’éĘ

’éĘ

Field of application 
MOTOREX HEAVY DUTY SAE 50 has been specially 
developed for classic vehicles. It is extremely well 
suited for use with petrol and diesel engines with 
pressure circulation lubrication and fine filter. It is al-
so ideal for use in engines with increased operating 
temperatures and under high loads.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The viscosity SAE 50 is designed for use in air-
cooled engines and for motorcycles. It can also be 
used in the event of overheating problems or increa-
sed oil consumption. 
Note 
MOTOREX HEAVY DUTY SAE 50 can be mixed with 
mineral-oil-based motor oils of the same perfor-
mance level. Oil must be replaced in accordance 
with the information provided by the vehicle manu-
facturer.
