Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate a direct and an indirect manner in which low¬≠
rank approximations can be leveraged in the efficient evaluation a Maximum Likelihood
based hyperparameter selection criteria, particularly in the context of linear, time invariant
system identification. While the Randomized Nystr√∂m (rNys) relies on random projec¬≠
tions to construct low¬≠rank approximations, the alternative Subset of Regressors (SOR)
approach proposed in this thesis relies on the explicit selection of the data points upon
which the approximation is built. The preliminary analysis indicates that the SOR and
the rNys approach have comparable results for smaller data sets. However, the inability
of the SOR Preconditioned Conjugate Gradient method to converge quickly indicates a
challenge in the scaling of this approach the data set of larger dimension.

Type II Maximum Likelihood Estimation
Supervised learning is a method of machine learning in which a set X ‚ààRn√óp of n input
data points are used to determine a mapping Œ∏ from the p¬≠dimensional feature space

model inevitably most take into account the probability that the model could have produced
the observed data. Thus, an essential consideration in model definition is how exactly
probability is to be definedÕæ for which two major paradigms of thinking exist. [16].
The first paradigm, referred to as the frequentist approach, defines probability as the mea¬≠
sure of the likelihood of an event based on its long¬≠run frequency. That is, the probability of
the occurrence of an event is equal to the limit of its relative frequency after a large number
of trials. Probability can therefore be used to quantify uncertainty due to randomness, but
not due to a lack of knowledge. Under this paradigm, model parameters Œ∏ are assumed
to be a fixed, unknown quantities, and are to be estimated purely from the observed data.
Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) is a frequentist approach to parameter selection
in which a logged likelihood function describing the probability of observing y under the

primarily focus on approximation methods for ÀÜy. In particular, this thesis will investigate
the efficient approximations of ÀÜy by means of low¬≠rank approximations of XK(Œ±)XT .
Simply put, a low¬≠rank approximation matrix of XK(Œ±)XT is a matrix with a smaller rank
than that of XK(Œ±)XT that can, to a certain degree, capture its underlying structure. To
understand exactly what this means, interpret XK(Œ±)XT to be the matrix of covariances
between each of the data points in X, and denoted KXX and referred to as a kernel
matrix. It is a fundamental result in linear algebra that the eigenvectors corresponding
to the dominant eigenvalues of KXX describe the directions in which the underlying data
exhibit the most variance. These dominant eigenvalues additionally convey the magnitude
to which the data vary in these principal directions. The rank of a square matrix is equal
to the number of non¬≠zero eigenvalues it has, and in the context of kernel matrices, are
indicative of the dimension of the subspace in which the underlying data lie. A low¬≠rank
approximation ÀÜKXX of KXX therefore approximates KXX by assuming that the data lie
in a low dimensional subspace.
It is a well¬≠known fact that the eigenvalues of kernel matrices tend to exhibit rapid decay.
Assuming that the underlying data is infused with observable patterns, this is a logical at¬≠
tribute to observe from such matrices. The data will likely not be equally spread out across
all dimensions, but will instead vary more across certain dimensions compared to others.
Additionally, as will be elaborated on later, kernel matrices are controlled by kernel func¬≠
tions infused with a ‚Äùsmoothness‚Äù assumption, also highly likely to produce a matrix with
quickly decaying eigenvalues. Being that their eigenvalues quickly tend towards zero,
kernel matrices can often times be well approximated by a matrix with a smaller rank.

Literature Review
The research conducted in this thesis builds upon several existing works, in particular the
Gaussian Process Regression approach to LTI system identification for the efficient eval¬≠
uation of multiple hyperparameter selection criteria, including the Profile Marginal Likeli¬≠
hood, see [5]. Here, indirect methods are proposed as a way to leverage both the Toeplitz
structure of input matrix Œ¶ as well as the factorization properties of applicable kernel func¬≠
tions so that such efficient evaluations are scalable to large data sets. Low¬≠rank approxi¬≠
mations play a pivotal role in the investigation towards scalability by means of defining a
preconditioner for the indirect methods.
Preliminary analysis indicates that the use of indirect methods to approximate the PML
criterion for the evaluation hyperparameter fit is comparable to the use of direct methods.
Additionally, they tend to scale better to larger datasets with slowly decaying impulse
responses. That is, evaluations of PML report comparable model fits as the order p of
the finite impulse response is increased. And as order p is increased, the run time of
evaluation the criteria PML by indirect methods tends to grow at a slower rate compared
to the use of direct methods.

Fast Fourier Transforms
Fourier analysis refers to the study of how functions can be decomposed into simpler
trigonometric or exponential functions with definite frequencies, and the decomposition
process itself is referred to as a Fourier transformation. [12]. Of particular interest in the
context of circulant matrices is the discrete time Fourier transform (DFT), a Fourier trans¬≠
formation of a discrete sequence of values [21]. Such transformations can be represented
in matrix form, that is, the DFT of a sequence x can be calculated via the matrix multiplica¬≠
tion Dx, where D is referred to as a DFT matrix. The direct computation of a DFT matrix,
however, can be quite expensive. The fast Fourier transform (FFT) is an algorithm that
efficiently applies a DFT matrix by re¬≠expressing the it as a product of sparse matrices.
As a result, the algorithm reduces the computational complexity of a DFT from O(n2) to
O(n log n).

Conclusion
Hyperparameter selection remains an important problem in the context of of LTI system
identification, for which maximum likelihood based methods, including Type II Marginal
Likelihood and ultimately the Profile Marginal Likelihood (PML), may be applied. The
methodology in this thesis leverages unique aspects of the problem, primarily the favor¬≠
able factorization of the stable spline kernel and the sparsity exhibited by the input signal
induced Toeplitz matrix to prioritize an investigation into approximating the PML via low¬≠
rank approximations that may be further applied to large data sets. The Randomized Nys¬≠
tr√∂m approach to a low¬≠rank approximation of the PML appears to perform comparatively
to the Subset of Regressors approach for a Toeplitz matrix of dimension 10, 000 √ó 1, 000.
However, the inability of the SOR Preconditioned Conjugate Gradient method to converge
quickly indicates a challenge in the scaling of this approach the data set of larger dimen¬≠
sion. Further research must be conducted regarding how the inducing points are selected
with respect to the distribution of the time instances of the true impulse response, and how
it may improve the scalability of the SOR approach.
