Abstract
This is a comprehensive study of all fast (≥500km/s) front-sided, Earth directed halo
Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs) from 1997-2017. The aim being to draw statistical con-
nections between false alarms and CME/ICME properties, CME source location, solar
flare progenitors and the semiannual variation. The advanced Drag Based Model (DBM)
is used to predict the arrival of each ICME at Earth which is used when probing the near-
Earth solar wind data. The solar wind environment near Earth during the predicted
arrival period of the ICME is allocated to a particular criteria; ICME hit, ICME glance,
ICME miss, CIR, unknown storm. Dst and Kp are used to determine whether a storm
occurred or not and subsequently whether the CME can be classified as a false alarm.
A miss CME is by default a false alarm. To determine the geoeffective potential of the
glance and hit ICMEs, they are subject to the ”BS/BZ/Newell effect” algorithm which
allocates a 1 or 0 if the ICME has storm potential or not. This study provides statistical
evidence of the existence of a semiannual variation for false alarms of Earth-directed halo
CMEs with the peak false alarm frequency at the solstices (January/July) and troughs
at the equinoxes (March/September). A correlation is apparent between the false alarm
and ICME miss semiannual variations which strengthens the axial theory and the im-
portance of the heliographic latitude of Earth with regards to the geomagnetic activity.
64.33% of false alarms were due to the ICME missing Earth, 21.64% due to a glance,
10.53% hit without a storm and 0.58% merged with a neighbouring CME. The statistics
affirms the well-known idea that fast, west limb CMEs deflect to the east. Preliminary
analysis suggest that a semiannual variation exists in the northern and southern hemi-
spheres for hits and misses that coincide with the heliographic latitude of Earth, however
more data points are needed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs) are some of the most hazardous phenomena in space
weather with the impact having the potential to be catastrophic. The ensuing danger of a
CME can cause damage to electrical power grids, satellite communication and navigation.
It is therefore important to analyse, understand and predict CMEs to prevent such
damage from having long-lasting economical and societal repercussions.
A CME has an array of different directions in which it can travel. The source location
does not necessarily indicate the direction of propagation due to deflection mechanisms
and non-radial eruptions. A combination of the Large Angle and Spectrometric Coro-
nagraph (LASCO) onboard the Solar and Heliospheric Observatory satellite (SOHO)
and images from STEREO Ahead and STEREO Behind, allows the acquisition of the
longitude, latitude, half-width and speed of a CME. The most concerning CMEs are
Earth-directed which appear in coronagraphs as a bright ring expanding into space,
hence the term halo CME. Figure 1.1 shows a halo CME on 2014-02-09 18:24:59 taken
by STEREO B, with the black line emphasising the halo feature. All Earth-directed
halo CMEs have the potential to create geomagnetic storms on Earth. A radially prop-
agating Earth-directed halo CME above the threshold speed of 500km/s is cause for an
alarm being issued. However, a CME does not always contain the correct orientations
of the magnetic field to activate a geomagnetic storm, therefore this hit CME would be
classified as a false alarm. A false alarm also occurs if the halo CME deflects away from
the Sun-Earth line leading to a glancing blow or a miss. The initiation of CMEs, such
as the acceleration and propagation phase (Chen 2008), alongside the source location,
effect the line of projection which alters whether the CME impacts Earth. A CME is
considered a false alarm if no geomagnetic storm occurred.
False alarms plague the world of space weather forecasting. Predicted arrival times
of shocks greater than a day after eruption have the tendency to be 50% incorrect, and
approximately 25% of shock arrivals are not predicted (Siscoe and Schwenn 2006). Wang
et al. 2014 found that 60% - 70% of all front sided halo CMEs were detected to have an
ejecta portion, and 50% of the halo CMEs were geoeffective. These statistics highlight

This study aims to investigate all Earth-directed halo CMEs from 1997 - 2017 above
the speed of 500km/s to identify the statistical properties of a false alarm. Each CME
is classified by whether it missed, glanced or hit Earth, or merged with another CME.
Through categorisation into these ICME-Earth and ICME-ICME interactions, the causal
pattern of false alarms will become more revealing. This investigative procedure also
involves comparing the ICME and CME properties of alarms with false alarms to fully
disclose whether there are any inherent difference that can lead to a false alarm. The
geomagnetic semiannual variation cycle is an important route to explore as it is exacer-
bated by solar events such as ICMEs. Does the false alarm distribution follow a similar
pattern? Finally, the relationship between the solar flare progenitors of CMEs and false
alarms are also probed with the intention to better improve the predictive authority of
space weather models in the future.

CHAPTER 2
Solar Activity
Solar events that occur on the sun manifest as solar wind signatures that emanate
through IP space changing through various solar cycles. This section will aim to highlight
solar activities such as CMEs, ICME, CIRs, and their ever-changing outcome to the
geomagnetic stability here on Earth. Furthermore, the solar activity discussed in this
section will be tailored such that a detailed probe into the physics of false alarms can
be conducted.
2.1
CMEs & ICMEs
2.1.1
Structure & Evolution
CMEs are defined as a noticeable change in the brightness, due to Thomson-scattering,
and defined structure of the solar corona. A CME is a large scale ejection of plasma
outwards from the solar corona which then propagates into IP space driving an electron
density wave beyond the solar surface.
The most accurate way to model a CME is
generally a contested topic, for example, whether the CME initiation is modelled as
either a driving force due to the energy stored within the magnetic field of the solar
corona, or that the CME initiates through photospheric currents (G. Forbes et al. 2007).
The contention on the specific physical mechanism all eventually leads to the overall
principle of cause and effect; what are the mechanisms at the Sun and how does this
influence geospace?
The CME originates from active regions whereby flares, filaments and magnetic po-
larity patches cause closed magnetic field lines to build energy and erupt. Generally, it
is possible to determine the progenitor of the CME, for example, faster CMEs are known
to be closely connected with eruptive flares. CME Progenitor and source location are key
observational tools used to determine the geoeffectiveness of halo CMEs (Gopalswamy
et al. 2007) and in turn the false alarm rate.
==PAGE:18==
4
2 Solar Activity
The CME morphology is governed by the generally conceded three-part structure
model. As the CME is formed out of the solar corona, a bright shell-like region emerges
resembling a light-bulb. This region encloses a dimmer cavity of low density plasma.
Finally, the core of higher density is contained by this dim region. This entire balloon-
like structure then ejects from the surface of the corona and propagates through IP space
therefore becoming an Interplanetary Coronal Mass Ejections (ICMEs). The duration
of this process tends to be in the order of hours at speeds from 100km/s to 3000km/s
(Manchester et al. 2017).
Figure 2.1: Schematic of the evolution of a CME from the solar corona (Ling et al. 2014)
Despite the contention regarding the CME initiation, the process of eruption is re-
garded as universally known. This understanding is based on the standard flare model
which suggests the accumulation of magnetic field lines leading to ongoing stress. The
current sheet formed beneath the seperatrix bubble is the location of frequent magnetic
reconnection creating a more advanced magnetic flux rope (Ling et al. 2014; Gou et al.
2019). This leads to the rise of the seperatrix bubble from the solar corona (figure 2.1).

The evolution of a CME can change during the propagation phase through interaction
with the IP medium and ambient solar wind. Both small and large scale structural
deformation can occur including expansion, deflection and rotation. The angular half-
width of the CME influences the evolution of the ICME through IP space. For the

studies have been conducted to model the 3D evolution of a CME included in which is the
angular half width. Isavnin 2016 included in the 3D representation of the CME evolution,
the flattening of the front of the CME due to drag from the fast CME interacting with

to the solar rotation and ”pancaking” distortion from the radial propagation causing
deformation globally around the CME. The ”pancaking” deformation is characterised by
the vertical and lateral half-width of the CME. The angular size of the CME is therefore
dictated by the ”pancaking” angle and the angular half width. Should neither change,
the CME size is considered uniform and conserved. This analysis of the evolution of
the CME is important when understanding false alarms as the CME can undergo major
deformations causing the CME to either miss or glance Earth, or the internal magnetic
structure can alter creating a non-geoeffective ICME.
2.1.2
Propagation and Interaction
The travel duration of an ICME is dependent on the ICME take-off speed, width and
mass as well as the ambient solar wind density and speed. The ICME properties dictates
what will affect the ICME transit time the most, for example, a narrow and massive
ICME will have a transit time most influenced by the ICME take-off speed (Vršnak et al.

gradient seen from the centre of the ICME to the flanks gives the concave shape seem
in many ICME schematics (Vršnak et al. 2010).

2.2.3
Parker Spiral & Cycles
The solar wind emanates from the sun being governed by its magnetic field and varying
cycles. Through the Ulysses mission, it was discovered that the solar wind speed has
latitudinal dependence with polar regions of the Sun experiencing an outflow of fast solar
wind in comparison to the equatorial region with a slower, more turbulent solar wind.
During the solar cycle the magnetic poles begin to reverse in orientation and it is during
solar maximum that the ordered solar wind is more chaotic as highlighted by figure 2.9
(b). The latitude of active regions is dependent on the solar cycle expressed by Spörer’s
law. During the start of the solar cycle, active regions are at high latitudes of up to
40◦and as the cycle continues, the active regions lose latitude reaching 20◦(Hathaway
2010) (Driel-Gesztelyi and Green 2015). Active regions are the main source location of
CMEs and a route cause into the deflection within the initial 4 solar radii.
The Sun’s magnetic field lines are far reaching due to the solar wind, and connected.
Meaning the direction of the field lines are reversed on either side of the HCS. The
magnetic axis and rotational axis of the Sun are different, depending on the solar cycle,
and therefore the HCS takes on the form of a ballerina’s dress. The Parker spiral is
an Archimedean spiral in which the Alfven ’frozen-flux’ magnetic field lines of the sun
are carried radially out. The rotation of the Earth creates the effect that a parcel of
magnetic flux carried out from the same source location on the Sun will form a spiral
shape, hence the standard analogy, ’the garden sprinkler’.
As mentioned, the interaction of a CME with the solar wind effects the direction of
propagation, and more notably, the magnetic structure of the Parker spiral will deflect
fast CMEs eastwards and slow CMEs westwards. This study looks at fast CMEs above
a certain threshold, therefore the easterly deflection affects false alarm probability of the
CME.

3.1

3.1.1
Structure & Dynamics
The magnetosphere is a region in geospace that is dominated by the Earth’s magnetic
field. The Earth’s magnetic field is complex, however in some respects, can be treated
as a simple dipole magnet with non-dipole elements. By subtracting the best fit dipole
model for Earth from the overall field observed, it is possible to isolate the non-dipole
field. Using spherical harmonic calculations, the overall dipole field and non-dipole field
of Earth are separated.
The magnetosphere acts as a shield against the charged particles that travel through
IP space within the solar wind.
As a result of the constant bombardment of solar
wind against the magnetosphere, the dayside experiences a compression relative to the
strength of the solar wind, and an elongation on the nightside as seen by figure 3.1. The
supersonic solar wind creates a bow shock ahead of the magnetopause and the elongated
stretching of the magnetosphere is known as the magnetotail. The magnetopause is
the boundary between the solar wind IMF and the Earth’s magnetic field. The magne-
topause stand-off distance varies with the speed of the solar wind. This metric is used

3.2
Variations
The basis of this study is to identify the link between geomagnetic false alarms and
solar activity, namely CMEs. To bolster the analysis, one must first understand the
semiannual variation of geomagnetic storms and that the stronger storms occur nearer
to the equinoxes and the weaker to the solstices (Vennerstrom et al. 2016). The equinoxes
are the points at which the celestial equator passes through the Sun’s apparent centre
and the rotational axis perpendicular to the Sun-Earth line. The solstices are the points
in which the inclination of the rotational axis are most extreme towards/away from the
Sun. Due to the inclination of the solar rotational axis and the Earth’s rotational axis,
the heliographic latitude of Earth changes as the Earth faces different parts of the solar
disk. The Russel-McPherron (R-M) theory indicates that the energy input from the
solar wind plasma is approximately 40% greater during the equinox than the solstice
(Vennerstrom et al. 2016). Whilst many theories existed as to the root mechanism of
this semiannual variation, Svalgaard et al. 2002 proposes the equinoctial hypothesis as
the primary cause which suggests that the angle between the Earth’s dipole and and the
solar wind modulates the coupling strength of the solar wind and magnetosphere. On the
contrary, Zhao and Zong 2012 determines the R-M method as being the most successful
in explaining the semiannual variation. The key argument being that the R-M theory
explains the varying southward IMF probability as a result of the difference between the
geocentric solar magnetospheric (GSM) and geocentric solar equatorial (GSEQ) systems,
which changes over time. Figure 3.3 shows the different orientations of the GSEQ, GSE
and GSM in the Y-Z plane indicating a southward GSM z-component.
Another competing theory is the axial hypothesis which describes the motion of
Earth’s orbit passing through the solar equatorial plane twice a year with direct relation
to the heliographic latitude of the Earth. During the equinoxes, the angular distance
from the Earth (GSE) to the GSEQ is at maximum (Cliver et al. 2004). As the Earth
passes through the solar mid-latitude it is more exposed to radially propagating CMEs
from active regions and therefore more prone to hits. Solar events such as CMEs will
exacerbate the effect of the semiannual variation of geomagnetic storms.

important to understand the geoeffective potential of the sheath portion of an ICME due
to the negative Bz that can occur, as mentioned in section 2. CMEs originate from areas
of active coronal features with the faster CMEs, that have a greater potential in being

The SOHO/LASCO catalogue aims to provide a complete and comprehensive list of

The Large Angle and Spectrometric Coronograph

progenitor of solar wind and the Sun’s internal structures (ESA 2003). This mission
sparked the initiative to create a single, uniform repository for universal access to the
required data sets.
The main CME catalogue is displayed such that the date and time of the CME
matches with first appearance within LASCO C2 field of view (FOV). The collection
of CMEs presented in date-time index form provides information on measured CME
parameters such as height-time measurements which in turn allows for the calculation of
linear and quadratic speed. Other measured parameters include angular width (degrees)
and Central Position Angle (CPA) (degrees). The importance of the CPA is to differ-
entiate CMEs that have close proximity eruptions. The main SOHO/LASCO catalogue
is used to identify a halo CME when the CPA value equals 360◦. The main catalogue
however does not distinguish between front-sided and back-sided halo CMEs. Figure 4.1
provides a snapshot of the SOHO/LASCO CME catalogue for date January 1997 and

and deprojected space speed.

The OMNI data is a collection of solar wind magnetic field and plasma information from
the satellites ACE, Wind, GOES and IMP-8. All data is time-shifted to the Earth’s bow

solar event nuances. The data is imported into the dataframe dfomnih, with the data

the index is assigned to a datetime format. The OMNI data is necessary to analyse the

extracting the solar wind data for 6 days after CME eruption and 1 day prior. This
drastically reduces the amount of OMNI data locally saved and therefore minimises
computational time. A CME with a speed greater than 500km/s takes no longer than
5 days to arrive, hence the choice of using 6 days post eruption to ensure all CME solar

4.1.3
Richardson & Cane
The Richardson & Cane catalogue provides all ICMEs in the near-Earth solar wind
through measurements of the solar wind magnetic field, plasma, ion compositions and
charge states (Richardson and Cane 2010). As all ICMEs are in the near-Earth vicinity,
they are considered as hits within this study, therefore confidence is taken into the
correctness of this catlog.
The data is imported and stripped of all unnecessary strings in order to transfer
the floats into a readable format for the Pandas dataframe, dfrch. Missing values are
presented as ”...” which is then changed within the import algorithm to NaNs. A column
in the Richardson & Cane catalogue gives the datetime of the associated CME launch
which is taken from the SOHO/LASCO catalogue. This datetime is then made the index
of the dfrch dataframe as now the ICME properties of the Richardson & Cane catalogue,
and the CME properties of the SOHO/LASCO catlog, dfslh, can then be compared
directly as they both have the same index. Not all ICMEs that hit are a result of halo
CMEs, therefore Richardson & Cane identify halo CMEs with an ’H’ which are sorted
and kept within the dataframe and all those without an ’H’ are disregarded.
Richardson & Cane determine the minimum Dst value best associated with the arrival
of the ICME between the period between the arrival time of the disturbance and the
end time of the ICME plasma (Richardson and Cane 2010). This same technique is used
for determining the Dst and Kp for all halo CMEs being studied.

The process of data acquisition and reduction is extensively explained in section 4. As

5.1
Criteria
Wading through the OMNI data is an arduous task if what is needing to be identified
does not present itself in an obvious manner. An example of this is the degree to which an
ICME impacts a satellite. Commonly, the ICMEs are identified by threshold parameters
in the OMNI data for the shock, sheath and ejecta with and without a magnetic cloud
within the ejecta. The satellite can pass through different parts of the ICME, therefore
different portions of the ICME structure may be seen in the solar wind data. Figure 5.1
gives the schematic of an ICME and satellites in 3 separate locations each of which causes
either a hit, glancing blow or miss. A hit is defined as the satellite passing through the
ejecta of the ICME, irrelevant as to whether it contains a magnetic cloud. A glancing
blow means the ICME shock and sheath portion impact the satellite whereas a miss
indicates that the satellite did not pass through any part of the ICME. The purpose of
creating this criteria is to identify whether a halo CME predicted to cause an alarm had
a different effect due to the portion of the CME that impacted the satellite.
As this study looks at determining the reason why a halo CME may not cause a
geomagnetic storm, the ability to know the degree to which the ICME impacted Earth
will identify whether deflection, impact location and direction of propagation of the
ICME are primary causes to the lack of Dst and Kp trigger to storm level. The criteria

5.1.1
Hit & Merger
A CME that propagates in the Sun-Earth direction as a front-sided halo has the
potential to hit Earth. A CME with a threshold speed above 500km/s that is Earth-

The identification of an ICME hit is through the analysis of solar wind data collected by
numerous satellites as well as geomagnetic indices such as Dst which is prominently used
in the Richardson & Cane identification method. ICMEs have characteristic signatures
in the near- Earth solar wind data which allows for potential identification, however
multiple parameters must be taken in order to provide confident results.
The Richardson & Cane catalog is the trusted source for determining whether the
ICME hit Earth. A more detailed analysis of the Richardson & Cane catalog can be
found in section 4. ICMEs in the Richardson & Cane catalog are considered hits as the
ejecta portion is identifiable in the solar wind data. The CME Richardson & Cane links

& Cane. For the case of multiple CMEs within this margin of acceptance, the algorithm
ensures the closest option to SOHO/LASCO datetime is chosen.

Earth (Wang et al. 2014). The glancing blow is still regarded as an important contributor
to geomagnetic activity. The studies performed by Yermolaev et al. 2010 and Echer et
al. 2013 concluded that 25%-35% of intense storms and 15%-25% of moderate storms
were as a result of the magnetic structure within the sheath plasma or behind the
magnetosonic shock (Lugaz et al. 2016). The cause of a geomagnetic storm is proven to
be complex especially with a multistep main phase. Wu and Lepping 2002 discovered

5.2
False Alarms
Once the CMEs are sub-categorised into the aforementioned criteria, they are then ready
for the identification of alarms and false alarms. Each CME in the dfslh dataframe is
assigned a maximum value for Kp and a minimum value for Dst within the 48 hour
window. Richardson & Cane provide a Dst index that they feel most associates with the
arrival of the ICME. The Kyoto dataset contains the Kp values every 3 hours. Padding
and resampling the data to hourly values allows for the maximum Kp value to be found
between the disturbance and ICME plasma end period for all hit CMEs. This method
can then be applied to all remaining CMEs in the dfslh dataframe giving the minimum
Dst and maximum Kp value from the Kyoto dataset within the 48 hour window of
predicted arrival. The caveat to this approach is that temporally neighbouring solar
events can effect the Dst and Kp value. To ensure this method provides reasonably
accurate results, the Dst and Kp values for all CMEs that missed are checked to ensure
they are below the thresholds for storm classification.
The classification of a false alarm within this study reflects whether the CME, during
the 48 hour predicted window of arrival, triggered Dst and Kp above their threshold
values. Each CME is therefore processed to identify whether it has a Dst < -50nT, a
Kp > 5 or both. Three new columns within dfslh reflects these outcomes for each CME,
respectively, and are populated with a binary result. A ”0” indicates an alarm and a
”1” indicates a false alarm. The outcome of this process leaves each CME with three
additional classifications; a false alarm (Dst), a false alarm (Kp) and a false alarm (Dst
& Kp).
For the purposes of analysis at a later stage, the minimum value of BZ, maximum
value of BS and maximum value of the Newell coupling function are determined for each
CME. BZ is calculated by applying a 60 minute rolling mean to the 48 hour predicted
arrival window in the OMNI data and finding the minimum value. Taking a rolling mean
smooths the data, preventing erroneous short-term spikes affecting the true value. BZ
is required to be negative for a duration of approximately 3 hours or more to instigate
a storm (B. Tsurutani et al. 1988) which is ensured through the calculation of BS. BS
are all the negative values of the BZ component with all positive values set to zero. The
rolling mean is taken over three hour windows and the maximum value is computed.
The basis of implementing both checks is to identify whether a false alarm was the cause
of a lack of negative BZ.
The Newell value assigned to each CME is calculated by taking the rolling mean over
==PAGE:62==
48
5 Methodology
a 6 hour window (Newell et al. 2007). Each window is required to have a minimum of
3 hours of data (B. Tsurutani et al. 1988), therefore excluding NaNs. The maximum
value is taken within the 48 hour period of the predicted CME arrival. The mean solar
wind Newell value is 6939.78. To understand whether a CME is geoeffective, the time
index of BS max, BZ min and Newell max for each CME are taken and used to give the
”BS/BZ/Newell effect”. For CMEs that hit, if each time index occurred within 36 hours
of the disturbance time, the ”BS/BZ/Newell effect” was considered positive; should one
of the indexes occur prior to the disturbance, it is considered negative. For glances, all
three time indexes must occur after the |B| shock for a positive result. For mergers, the
absolute difference of BS and BZ has to be less than 1.5 times the 40 minute sample
time which gives clear indication that there was a significant duration of geoeffective
solar wind. The outcome of identifying the ”BS/BZ/Newell effect” for each CME is to
deduce whether the CME false alarm was a result of the solar wind parameters and IMF
orientation.

travelling at a 45◦- 90◦MPA. Finally, the concentration of alarms are centred near the

alarms shown by the density distribution of ICME parameters such as space speed over
various longitudinal source locations.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
The aim of this study is to analyse fast Earth-directed halo CMEs and investigate
whether any statistical pattern of false alarms exists.
Thus far, we have looked at
false alarms through the lens of ICME and CME parameters, CME source location, the
semiannual geomagnetic variation, solar flare progenitor as a forecasting tool and the
criteria assigned to the CME depending on if it hit, glanced, missed or merged. This
section will aim to consolidate the information to explain the most plausible reason for
the CME false alarm. The key findings thus far are the semiannual variation of false
alarms, the relationship between false alarms and the ICME-Earth encounter, and the
link between the source location and the ICME-Earth encounter.
The semiannual variation of geomagnetic activity has been known by scientists in the
field for decades. The Earth’s magnetic field is modulated by the oncoming solar wind
and intensified by solar events. This semiannual variation is explained by one, or all, of
the following theories; R-M effect, axial theory and the equinoctial theory. The discovery
of the semiannual variation of false alarms bolsters the axial theory. Figure 7.1 and figure
7.2 clearly show the out-of-phase relationship between alarms and false alarms across
a monthly distribution using both Dst and Kp. As the Earth crosses the active source
regions on the solar disk during the equinoxes, the Earth is more exposed to a higher
frequency of CME eruptions, whether they be radially, or non-radially propagating.
Echer et al. 2013 found that 10.8% of moderate geomagnetic storms were caused by the
shock and sheath portion of an IP structure which would indicate the R-M effect within
the sheath portion of the structure.
Vennerstrom et al. 2016 argues that no single
mechanism is responsible for the semiannual variation pattern, especially at different
storm intensities. According to Svalgaard et al. 2001, the semiannual variation in larger
storm counts is a result of the equinoctial theory with a 40% increase in the frequency
of extreme storms being a result of the R-M effect and the axial theory.
The false alarm frequency distribution is closely linked to the distribution of misses,
highlighting a key finding that the majority of false alarms are a result of the outcome
of the ICME-Earth interaction and not coupling probability. To definitively prove the
==PAGE:94==

coupling importance with respect to the false alarm distribution, hit CMEs that triggered
a false alarm have to be analysed further. Unfortunately not enough data points exist
in this study to provide conclusive evidence of the hierarchical importance of the R-M
effect or equinoctial theory. The ability for the solar wind to couple the Earth’s magnetic
field, as stated by the equinoctial theory and R-M effect, explains the variation in storm
intensity, but does not affect the frequency distribution of CME false alarms. Had the
coupling had more importance on the semiannual variation, a larger number of hit halo
CMEs within this study would have triggered a false alarm during the solstice months
when coupling is least efficient as a result of BS.

It is rare for a CME to propagate radially from the source location due to deflection
in the solar corona and IP space (Wang et al. 2014; Kay et al. 2015; Kay et al. 2016).
During the declining phase of the solar cycle, the HCS is the region of lowest magnetic
pressure causing the CME to exhibit an equatorward deflection (Kay et al. 2015). How-
ever, active regions contribute more towards the deflection than the magnetic gradient,
making source location an integral part of understanding propagation (Kay et al. 2015).
Theoretically, a clear distinction between northern and southern hemisphere misses and
hits should be noticed during equinoxes and solstices due to the heliographic latitude

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
To conclude, this study has investigated the false alarms of geomagnetic storms from fast,
Earth-directed halo CMEs between 1997-2017. The CMEs were categorised by outcome,
i.e. miss, glance, hit or merge and were put under scrutiny to test their geoeffectiveness
using Dst, Kp, BS, BZ and Newell. Alongside the probing of false alarm patterns, known
space weather propensities are validated by the findings presented here and preexisting
theories are questioned. The concluding remarks are as follows.
• From all 343 Earth-directed halo CMEs studied, 32.07% missed, 28.57% glanced,
20.41% hit, 9.33% merged, 3.79% had a CIR arrival instead and 5.83% of storms
at predicted ICME arrival had an unknown solar wind signature.
• Using the Dst index as the geoeffective measure, 64.33% of false alarms were due
to the ICME missing Earth, 21.64% due to a glance, 10.53% hit but caused no
storm and 0.58% merged with a neighbouring CME. Whereas using the Kp index
to identify false alarms, 60.44% of false alarms were due to the ICME missing
Earth, 20.88% due to a glance, 8.79% hit but caused no storm and 1.1% merged.
• The frequency of glances for both alarms and false alarms are approximately equal
indicating that a glancing blow has equal probability of instigating a storm or not.
Mergers and hits are far more likely to instigate an alarm than not.
• The median space speed for a hit is 1255.5km/s, for a miss is 1097.0km/s and for
a glance is 1304.0km/s. It is still under deliberation as to why a miss has a lower
space speed than a hit. A glance requires a higher velocity to be more geoeffective
due to the angle of attack of the shock that makes contact with the magnetopause.
• The mean latitudinal CME source location is 15.68◦and -15.01◦which validates the
connection between Spörers law and CME eruption from active regions. The most
geoeffective hit ICMEs occur primarily on the west limb with misses becoming
more frequent the further eastwards on the solar disc. 87 of the hit CMEs erupt
from within ±45◦of (0,0) on the solar disk, 8 erupt on the west limb (≥45) and
3 on the east limb (≤−45). 25 misses have their source location on the west limb
==PAGE:98==
84
8 Conclusion
and 38 on the east limb. As suggested by literature, the evidence from this study
clearly indicates easterly deflection for fast halo CMEs
• A CME with a weak solar flare progenitor class has a higher probability to false
alarm than a strong flare. A ’B’ class flare will have 70.83% probability of being a
false alarm compared to that of a ’X’ class flare at 46.39%. The probabilities vary
with the semiannual variation, therefore during the solstice months, the false alarm
probability increases more than during the equinoxes. Far more data points are
needed to train the predictive tool in order to justify adding it into space weather
forecasting.
• The resounding find of this study is the statistical proof of a geomagnetic semi-
annual variation of false alarm Earth-directed halo CMEs. The majority of these
false alarms are a direct result of the CME missing Earth and not necessarily the
coupling capability of the ICME-magnetosphere system. Furthermore, the axial
theory of the semiannual variation holds greater strength against the competing
equinoctial and R-M theory in explaining this semiannual variation. Neverthe-
less, as these are all fast halo CMEs and therefore have added geoeffectivity, its
plausible that low speed CMEs will have a different result and put more empha-
sis on the coupling capability of the solar wind and the Earth’s magnetosphere.
Furthermore the ability for the ICME to couple with the magnetosphere would
have effected the statistics of false alarms as more effective coupling would occur
during the equinoxes and therefore lower rate of false alarms. This however would
be reflected in the hit ICMEs which requires more data points to validate.
• Analysing the northern and southern hemispheric source locations of the CMEs
provides preliminary support to the axial theory indicating that the heliographic
latitude of the Earth is important in whether the ICME hits or misses. In the
northern hemisphere, theoretically, during the spring equinox, more misses should
occur and during the autumn equinox more hits should. This theory is somewhat
represented, however further data points are required. In the southern hemisphere,
theoretically, during the spring equinox more hits occur and during the autumn
equinox more misses. This result is more clearly seen in the evidence provided in
this study with aid of a spline smoothing function.
Future work will involve further clarity into the definition of a glancing blow with
more quantitative information representing the degree to which an ICME glances backed-
up with evidence in the solar wind data. Furthermore, this study is to be repeated with

categorise the CMEs by geomagnetic intensity to understand the importance of the
semiannual variation and miss relationship. This will also help determine the weighting
of each semiannual variation theory with respect to the false alarm distribution.

The following is an analysis of why 60 minutes was chosen as the threshold to match the
SOHO/LASCO CME date time and the associated CME Richardson & Cane found
• When looking into how many of the Richardson & Cane ICMEs appear in the
SOHO/LASCO halo catalog, only 87 of the 132 do. Part of the apparent reason is
that when the data was reduced, for example the apparent speed had to be greater
than 500km/s, therefore some of those in Richardson & Cane aligned with the
data taken out. When the halo catalog is not cleaned, then there are 110 matches.
Taking away the Halos that Richardson & Cane think are from the Woods et al or
the DONKI list or are unsure, then 124 of the 132 remain in df_RCH meaning 14
of the ICMEs registerd by Richardson & Cane are not found in SOHO/LASCO.
An example of datetime mismatch is 2015/03/15 02:36 given as the datetime in
Richardson & Cane however the datetime in SOHO/LASCO is 2015/03/15 01:48.
It seems as if changing the threshold to 60 minutes means that 124 events are
found which is consistent with Richardson & Cane when only the ’H’ are included
The 56 out of 100 success rate for the shock finding algorithm using the 40 minute
sample time is due to; the occurrence of a CIR, the ICME is outside of the 48 hour
window, or the shocks for |B| and v are greater than the threshold.
