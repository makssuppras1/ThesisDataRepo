Abstract
In this thesis, the implementation of nonlinear model predictive control (NMPC) strategy
for domestic hot water (DHW) preparation in ultra-low temperature district heating
systems (ULTDH) is investigated.
The case study of this project is a heat booster
substation located in the Nordhavn area that provides DHW to 22 flats in a multi-
family building. The substation is supplied by ULTDH at a temperature of 40 ◦C – 45 ◦C.
A booster heat pump is used to raise the temperature level of the supply and it is
accumulated in a hot water storage tank.
The observed DHW consumption over a period of one year are studied to develop time-
series forecasting models to predict the DHW demand. Methods such as the seasonal
ARIMA method and Artificial Neural Networks are developed and their forecasting
performance is compared over time resolutions ranging from 10 minutes to 60 minutes.
A dynamic control-oriented model of the thermal stratification in the hot water storage
tank is developed and validated against measurements. Models for the heat pumps and
the heat exchanger are also developed in parallel and integrated into a cohesive dynamic
model of the substation for the purpose of investigating the model based predictive
control strategy.
The developed DHW demand forecast model and the substation system model are
integrated into the NMPC strategy with the purpose of achieving load-shift flexibility
and reducing energy costs by optimally scheduling the charging of the hot water storage
tank. The NMPC strategy is successfully implemented to optimally utilize the thermal
energy storage capability based on time-varying electricity price signals. The developed
NMPC strategy is compared with the current rule-based control strategy to evaluate the
performance and justify the additional effort of this advanced control strategy.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Renewable energy sources (RES), such as wind energy and solar energy, will increas-
ingly be deployed to improve the security of energy supply by drawing upon sustainable
natural sources and to significantly reduce the environmental impact. However, imple-
menting sustainable energy solutions for the future with a high penetration of RES is a
critical challenge for the present. Due to the intermittent nature of RES, it is imperative
to ensure their market availability to satisfy demand through the development of smart
and dynamic energy systems. The focus of these smart energy systems is mainly on the
integration of fluctuating renewable electricity into thermal grids to generate flexibility
on the demand side through utilizing various short and long term thermal energy storage
capability. [1]
The building sector is responsible for more than one third of the total energy con-
sumption and, especially in Denmark, two-thirds of the domestic heating demand is
provided by the district heating system [2]. Hence, district heating (DH) systems have a
very important role to play in future sustainable energy systems as the highly stochastic
electrical energy generated can be stored in the form of heat in the thermal distribution
network through electric driven thermal boosters. [3]
In addition, thermal storage capability of buildings can be utilized to actively provide
demand side flexibility through smart energy management systems [4]. A large potential
to shift the electricity consumption to adapt to the stochastic electricity generation is
identified through the utilization of a combination of thermal energy storage (TES) and
electrically driven heat pumps in building heating systems. By being connected to the
DH system, these heat pumps benefit from a large store of heat and can be utilized
intelligently to accumulate electrical energy in the form of thermal energy using the
storage capability of the TES to satisfy the heating and comfort requirements of the
residents in the buildings and thus, generating flexibility on the demand side through
shifting the electrical load.
However, to transition to such a smart energy system, the present district heating
systems must undergo a radical change towards ultra low temperature district heating
(ULTDH) networks. This is the driver of the concept of 4th generation district heating
(4GDH) proposed in Denmark [5], which aims to reduce the losses and increase energy
eﬀiciency of the total energy chain through utilization of lower supply temperatures.
Moreover, it aims to significantly increase renewable energy penetration by acting as

1.1
Objective
The case study of this project is a heat booster substation (HBS) located in the Nord-
havn area that provides domestic hot water to 22 flats in a multi-family building. The
substation is supplied by ULTDH at a temperature of 40 ◦C – 45 ◦C. An electrically
driven booster heat pump is used to raise the temperature level of the supply and it is
accumulated in a hot water storage tank. Further, a small circulation heat pump is used
to cover the heat losses in the DHW circulation loop.
The main objective of this thesis is to enable demand side flexibility through intelligent
use of thermal energy storage devices that can act as flexible loads to the power system
to increase the penetration of fluctuating renewable energy sources. Specifically, the
objective is to develop a model based predictive control strategy that optimizes the
charging schedule of the hot water tank based on time-varying electricity prices and on
the forecast of DHW demand.
To realize the objective, the following tasks are undertaken:
1. Develop mathematical models to forecast the DHW demand based on historical
consumption profiles.
2. Develop a control-oriented dynamic model of the HBS that includes modeling the
thermal stratification in the hot water storage tank, the heat pumps and the heat
exchanger.
3. Develop and implement the nonlinear model predicitve control (NMPC) strategy
to achieve load-shift flexibility and to reduce energy costs.
This study is part of EnergyLab Nordhavn1 - new urban energy infrastructures, a
multi-year project of research and development of future energy solutions. EnergyLab
Nordhavn utilizes the Copenhagen area of Nordhavn as a full-scale laboratory to test
future solutions for the smart integration and control of different energy systems (heating,
electricity and transportation) and the utilization of a large share of renewable sources.

CHAPTER 2
Methodology
This chapter describes the methodology and principles of time-series forecasting tech-
niques such as the ARIMA method and the Artificial Neural Network method. In the
latter part, the ideology of model based predictive control strategy is presented along-
with a brief mathematical framework.
2.1
Seasonal ARIMA
The Auto-Regressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) method is a well estab-
lished modeling technique for time-series data forecasting. The method applies to any
time-series that can be made to be “stationary”.
Concept of Stationarity
A time-series is said to be stationary if its statistical properties such as mean, variance
and auto-correlation are independent of time, i.e. a stationary time-series will exhibit
no long-term predictable patterns and its short-term patterns are random in a statistical
sense. Trends and seasonal components in the time-series will affect the value of the time-
series over time, thus making the time-series non-stationary. Hence, the non-stationary
time-series needs to be transformed into a stationary time-series.
Differencing
To stationarize a non-stationary time-series several data transformation techniques
are applied. One such technique is differencing. Differencing eliminates or reduces the
trend and seasonality in a time-series by stabilizing the mean of the time-series.[7]

Auto-correlation
The auto-correlation function (ACF) is a measure of the linear correlation between
a time-series and its lagged values. The partial auto-correlation function (PACF) is a
measure of the correlation of a time-series regressed with only the values of the time-
series at shorter lags, i.e. a “partial” correlation at lag k is the correlation between yt
and yt−k which is independent of the correlations at all lower lags (1, · · · , k −1).
The lag k auto-correlation and partial auto-correlation coeﬀicients (γk, ρk) are defined
in (2.6a) and (2.6b) respectively, where Y is the mean of the time-series. As the ARIMA
methods characterise the auto-correlations of a time-series, analysing the ACF and PACF
plots of the time-series provide insight about the number of non-seasonal and seasonal
orders required in the ARIMA model to enable it to forecast appropriately.
The order of non-seasonal AR and MA terms can be determined from the ACF and
PACF plot of the stationarized time-series. An AR signature is observed if there is a
sharp cut-off in the PACF plot and/or if the lag 1 auto-correlation is positive. The lag
at which the PACF cuts-off indicates the number of AR terms to be added. The reason
behind this is due to the fact that the AR terms act like “partial differences” in the
forecasting equation. Similarly, an MA signature is observed if there is a sharp cut-off
in the ACF plot and/or if the lag 1 auto-correlation is negative. The lag at which the
ACF cuts off indicates the number of MA terms to be added, with the reason being that
MA terms can act to “partially cancel out” an order of differencing in the forecasting
equation.[8]
The seasonal differencing order is determined by the number of times a time-series is
seasonally differenced in order to stationarize it. Usually, not more than one order of
seasonal differencing is recommended. The seasonal terms (SAR and SMA) are identified
in a similar fashion to the non-seasonal terms (AR and MA) with the difference being
that the lags of interest are the lags at multiples of the seasonality S.

2.2
Artificial Neural Network
Artificial Neural Network (ANN) models are an interesting and exciting approach to
model complex non-linear systems. The foundations for ANNs lie in developing computer
models that abstractly simulate the functioning of neurons in the human brain and to
then apply these self-learning models to model and simulate real world processes whose
exact mathematical function is very complex and is generally unknown.[9]
Feed-forward type neural networks with one or more hidden layers are employed to
approximate the unknown non-linear function between the inputs and the outputs. Each
hidden layer consists of some computing units known as nodes. Figure 2.1 shows the
network architecture of a feed-forward neural network with one hidden layer. Inputs
are fed to the corresponding nodes in the input layer of the network. The outputs of
these nodes become the inputs to the nodes in the next layer, i.e to the hidden nodes
in the hidden layer. Finally, the outputs of the hidden nodes are inputs to the node in
the output layer, i.e. to the output node. The output of the network is the output of
the output node. The nodes in adjacent layers are all inter-connected but there is no
connection between the nodes in each layer.
The input-output relation of each hidden node is determined by the connection weights
wk and and the threshold parameter bk. The inputs to each node are combined using a
weighted linear combination. For example, the inputs into the hidden node k (1 ≤k ≤p)
are combined to give the result zk. In the hidden layer, this result is then modified by
a non-linear function such as the sigmoid function to give the inputs to the next layer.
The parameters bk and wk are “learned” by the network during training on the past
data.[10]

In the context of time-series forecasting, ANN structures such as the non-linear auto-
regressive (NAR) network and the non-linear auto-regressive network with exogenous
inputs (NARX) have been widely applied with excellent results. The potential of these
networks to model complex non-linear data relationships is used to generate forecasts
of the time-series using the data relationships observed in the past values of the time-
series. The NAR network forecasts the time-series using one-dimensional data about
the past values of the time-series. The NARX network is an extension to the NAR
network, where the forecast of the time-series is performed through expanding the one-
dimensional time-series data to a multi-dimensional dataset using additional external
information to enhance prediction performance. NARX networks offer the ability to
integrate external knowledge that might improve the prediction accuracy at the cost of
higher complexity.[12]
A NAR network for time-series forecasting is formulated in the form of a discrete,
non-linear, auto-regressive model as:
y(t) = h(y(t −1), y(t −2), . . . , y(t −d)) + ε(t)
(2.8)
where the non-linear function h(·) is approximated through training the network
to find the optimal network weights and neuron bias. This function is then used to
predict the value of the time-series y(t) at time t using the past d values of the time-
series. The term ε(t) quantifies the prediction error of the forecast at time t. The past
d values of the time-series y(t −1), y(t −2), . . . , y(t −d) that are used as the predictors
are called the feedback delays. The network is trained using the Levenberg-Marquardt
back-propagation algorithm (LMBP).[13]

The topology of the network is determined by the number of hidden layers and the size
of hidden layers, i.e. the number of neurons per layer. The choice of network topology is
flexible and is optimized through trial and error to establish the network topology that
provides the best performance. It is important to emphasize that choosing a network
with few neurons might restrict the capability and computing power of the network
while at the same time including a large number of neurons in the network significantly
increases the complexity and the computational effort while providing only a marginal
increment in performance.
2.3
Nonlinear Model Predictive Control
The idea behind Nonlinear Model Predictive Control (NMPC) is to formulate a
control problem that captures the system dynamics and respects the various constraints
on the system states and inputs whilst numerically solving an on-line finite horizon open-
loop optimal control problem (OCP). The basic principle is to predict future dynamic
behavior of the system over a finite prediction horizon Tp and to determine a sequence
of control inputs over the control horizon Tc (Tc ≤Tp) that optimize the open-loop
performance objective function.[14]

If the prediction horizon is infinite and if the system is modeled perfectly with no
external disturbances, then the control input computed at time t = 0 can be applied

to the system for all times t ≥0. In reality, due to external disturbances and due to
the mismatch between the system model and the system itself, the predicted system
behavior is different from the true system behavior which necessitates a mechanism for
periodic system behavior feedback for the controller. Thus, the OCP is solved over a
finite horizon.
To enable feedback for the controller, the optimal control input is computed starting
with the measurement at time t and implemented only until the next measurement of the
system is available. Assuming measurements are taken every δ sampling time-units, a
new measurement at time t+δ is fed to the controller to compute a new optimal control
input over the forward-shifted prediction horizon which will be implemented until the
next measurement is available and the process is repeated.

CHAPTER 3
Demand Forecasting
In the first part of this chapter a statistical analysis of the domestic hot water (DHW)
consumption is performed to understand the consumption profiles of the residents at a
multi-family residential building. In the latter part, mathematical models are developed
to forecast the DHW demand and the techniques used to design, evaluate and compare
these forecasting models are described.
3.1
Data Preparation
The consumption data is collected from a smart communication meter installed at a
residential building1 that measure and record the DHW consumption at a sampling rate
of 10 seconds. The consumption dataset contains only measurements of the consumption
rate, during the period from June 2018 to June 2019, without any additional information
regarding the type of usage or the number of residents in the building. During this
period, there were times when the data could not be collected or was not recorded.
From the dataset, these missing data have been removed and only weeks with complete
data measurements (46 weeks in total) have been used in the estimation and validation
of the forecasting models. The raw dataset contains measurements in litre/h and it is
converted into units of kg s−1 for subsequent time-series analysis. The dataset is further
refined to time resolutions of 10, 15, 30, 45 and 60 minutes, wherein the volumetric
consumption rate is averaged over each time interval.
3.2

The analysis of DHW consumption patterns shows that it has both daily and weekly
seasonality with sharp peaks observed in the morning and evening during weekdays;
and on weekends, the consumption peaks are smaller and are spread out more evenly
throughout the day. This can be observed in figures 3.1 and 3.2 where the consumption
profiles are shown over three different weeks at time resolutions of 10 and 60 minutes

Summary
Initially, a statistical analysis of the DHW consumption profiles is performed at time

developed successfully to forecast the weekly DHW demand at these time resolutions.
The forecast performance of these models is evaluated over all the 46 weeks in the dataset

3.4
Weekday Holidays
The forecast of the seasonal-ARIMA models for the current day is strongly influenced
by the consumption profile of the same day in the previous weeks, i.e. the forecast
pre-sample dataset strongly influences the forecast. When the weeks in the forecast
pre-sample dataset and the current week deviate from normal weeks3 the error of the
forecast increases.
The effect of an unforeseen weekday holiday on the forecast performance can be ob-
served, for instance, in the days during Easter 2019, which occurred on 21 April 2019.
The observations in figures 3.9 and 3.10 show that from 18 to 23 April 2019 there is a
deviation of the DHW consumption from the usual weekly pattern and it follows the
profile of the consumption observed on holidays (weekends and weekday holidays). Gen-
erally, during holidays, there is a decrease in DHW consumption and the consumption
peaks are smaller and more evenly spread out. But the forecast model is unaware of
this, as it only relies upon the consumption in the previous weeks; and when there is
a difference between the previous week and the current week such as the occurrence of
unexpected weekday holidays, a greater error in the forecast is observed.

3.4.6
Summary
Through the developed neural networks and the daily-seasonal models, a better perfor-
mance in forecasting weekday holiday consumption is achieved compared to the weekly-
seasonal-ARIMA model by connecting the consumption profiles over the weekends to the
weekday holidays. The improved forecast performance of the NAR networks for week-
day holidays is quantified by the obtained RMSE values of 0.0134 kg s−1 – 0.0293 kg s−1
across the different time resolutions.
However, these methods assume knowledge of the occurrence of weekday holidays a
priori. A model to predict the pattern of holidays would need to be developed to enhance
the overall performance of the forecasts but this thesis refrains from further investigation
of such a model due to dataset limitations.

CHAPTER 4
Heat Booster Substation
In this chapter, the heat booster substation (HBS) studied in the project is described
in detail. Initially, the concept of the HBS along with the various components that are
part of the HBS are introduced. Subsequently, dynamic control-oriented models for these
components are developed and the associated methodologies are described. Finally, the
models are validated and the results are presented.
4.1
The Substation
The HBS is located in the Aarhusgade area of Nordhavn at a multi-family building
named Havnehuset.
The substation is operating with ultra-low temperature district
heating (ULTDH) with supply temperatures of 40 ◦C – 45 ◦C. To simulate ULTDH at
the HBS, a mixing loop was installed to mix the existing medium-temperature district
heating (DH) network supply, which is typically at 70 ◦C – 90 ◦C, with water from the
DH return line. The HBS is integrated into the existing heating and DHW installation
as shown in figure 4.1. Thus, the existing heating system in the building is operating in
the traditional way but with ULTDH temperatures. The HBS is supplying DHW and
DHW circulation to 22 flats and it is essentially comprised of four parts:
• HBS module that includes valves, flow-meters, temperature sensors, pipes, a heat
exchanger and a controller.
• Large heat pump to boost the ULTDH for hot water storage
• Small heat pump to boost the DHW circulation return temperature
• Two hot water storage tanks
Concept
The supply temperature of the ULTDH system is well below the temperature needed
for producing DHW at 55 ◦C through a direct heat exchange process. To raise or boost
the ULTDH temperature, an electrically driven heat pump is placed on the primary side
that utilizes the ULTDH supply as a heat source as well as a heat sink. A storage tank
for hot water accumulation is placed on the primary side to avoid legionella risk and
an instantaneous heat exchanger is used for heating the DHW when there is tapping.

The hot water storage tank is in reality two vertical cylindrical tanks, each having a
volume of 0.75 m3, connected in series. The bottom of the first tank is connected to the
top of the second tank. The tank is insulated by a 100 mm polyurethane foam.

Control Strategy
In this chapter, the control strategy currently implemented at the HBS is described.

evaluation is conducted and the results are displayed.
5.1
Current Control Strategy
The current control strategy implemented at the HBS is a traditional rule based on-off
control. The strategy is to limit charging the tank during the morning and evening
peak load hours; and during non-peak load hours the tank is fully charged whenever a
set-point is triggered.
The current control is designed in such a way that when the temperature set-point
T set−point
start
is triggered, the LHP is switched on. The LHP is switched off again only when
another temperature set-point T set−point
stop
is triggered. Two temperature sensors of the
five placed in the tank are chosen as the start and stop sensor. The LHP on-off control is
activated when the temperature measured in either the start or the stop sensor triggers
the corresponding start or stop temperature set-point. Additionally, two “operational
modes” are defined according to the time of day:
• Comfort mode := [ 00 : 00 →05 : 00] ∪[ 08 : 00 →16 : 00] ∪[ 18 : 00 →00 : 00]
• Setback mode := [ 05 : 00 →08 : 00] ∪[ 16 : 00 →18 : 00]

5.3.1
Remarks
An important consideration before implementing the NMPC controller should be made.
It is with regards to the optimization solver2 : that it can only numerically solve for con-
tinuous input variables. The implication for the control decision variable u1(t) (P LHP
comp )
is that it should be continuous i.e. it should take any value within 0 kW – 3 kW. In
reality, the LHP at the HBS takes only binary inputs, i.e. it is either on or off, which re-
quires the use of binary variables in the OCP. This issue can be tackled through the use
of mixed-integer nonlinear constrained optimization solvers which fall under the domain
of mixed-integer nonlinear programming (MINLP) [25]. Again, due to time limitations,
this avenue is not explored further.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In this thesis, the implementation of nonlinear model predictive control (NMPC) for
domestic hot water (DHW) preparation in ultra-low temperature district heating systems
(ULTDH) is investigated.
Initially, a statistical analysis of the DHW consumption profiles is performed at time
resolutions ranging from 10 to 60 minutes based on observational data of one year. The
DHW demand is then forecasted using weekly-seasonal-ARIMA models. The perfor-
mance of these methods is evaluated at the different time resolutions. It is found that
the ARIMA models predict the weekly DHW demand with great accuracy. With a range
of measured consumption data of 0 kg s−1 – 0.4717 kg s−1, the RMSE values obtained are
within 0.0197 kg s−1 – 0.0355 kg s−1.
However, it is found that the prediction error of the models increases due to the oc-
currence of unforeseen weekday holidays in the prediction week. To overcome this issue,
daily-seasonal ARIMA models and nonlinear auto-regressive networks (NAR) are devel-
oped to forecast the weekday holiday DHW demand. An improved forecast performance
is obtained through the NAR networks which is quantified by the obtained RMSE values
of 0.0134 kg s−1 – 0.0293 kg s−1 across the different time resolutions.
Dynamic control-oriented models to model the thermal stratification in the hot water
storage tank (1.5 m3) are developed and successfully validated against measurements.
Models for the heat pumps and the heat exchanger are also developed in parallel and in-
tegrated into a cohesive dynamic model of the substation for the purpose of investigating
the model based predictive control strategy.
The developed DHW demand forecast model and the validated substation system
model are integrated into the NMPC strategy with the purpose of achieving load-shift
flexibility and reducing energy costs by optimally scheduling the charging of the hot
water storage tank. The NMPC control strategy is successfully developed and the simu-
lation results show great potential in achieving load-shift flexibility and reducing energy
costs. The energy cost savings achieved vary depending on the controller parameters
and on the sampling time, but on average it is shown that it is feasible to reduce the
energy costs by more than 50%. During the winter season, increased energy cost savings
of upto 120 % are obtained.

6.1 Future Work
57
The results confirm that the NMPC controller is able to successfully optimize the
charging schedule by utilizing the thermal energy storage capability of the HBS based
on time-varying electricity price signals. In conclusion, the developed NMPC strategy
is compared with the current rule-based control strategy and the additional effort of
developing this advanced control strategy is justified.
6.1
Future Work
Due to time constraints, it was not possible to implement the developed NMPC con-
troller at the HBS. Nevertheless, a significant first step is made. Through the course of
developing this thesis, potential issues for further investigation have been identified and
are summarized below:
• Although models for forecasting the DHW demand on weekday holidays are devel-
oped with good accuracy, these models assumed the knowledge of the occurrence
of the unforeseen weekday holidays a priori. A model to predict the pattern of
weekday holidays would need to be developed to further enhance the overall DHW
forecasting performance.
• A significant barrier for developing a dynamic model for the large heat pump (LHP)
is the lack information regarding the variations in ULTDH supply temperature.
Developing a model of the ULTDH supply network would enable development of
an enhanced model for the LHP.
• In addition to load-shift flexibility, potential fuel-shift flexibility (shifting between
DH and electricity as energy supply) of the HBS can be investigated by varying the
evaporator outlet temperature of the LHP. To investigate the fuel shift capability,
a model of the heat pump that responds to the fluctuations in the DH supply
would need to be developed.
• An improved estimate for the mass flow rate and temperature of the flow leaving
the evaporator of the small heat pump (SHP) would be possible by modeling the
DHW circulation loop in the building system to gain information regarding the
temporal variations in the circulation return temperature and flow rate.
• The DH return temperatures from the heat booster substation are observed to be
higher than the ULTDH target of 25 ◦C. It is because the temperature of the flow
leaving the evaporator of the SHP is too high. In a future scenario with a higher
economic benefit for lowering the DH return temperatures, it becomes relevant to
consider replacing the SHP with a heat pump having higher capacity.
• Additionally, the increased economic cost of higher DH return temperatures can be
quantified and augmented into the NMPC cost function to optimize the operation
of the HBS with regards to reducing the DH energy costs as well.

investigated to integrate solvers (that can accept binary inputs) into the NMPC
