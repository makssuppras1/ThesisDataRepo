Abstract 
 
Metakaolin MK was investigated as a supplementary cementitious material, because of its 
apparent benefits of reducing the CO2 output of Portland cement. Kaolinite from Cameroon Africa 
was calcined at FLSmidth Dania using flash calcination at a temperature of 850 C. The calcination 
of kaolinite causes a dehydroxylation, which makes metakaolin. Using x-ray diffractograms, it was 
confirmed that a successful calcination had occurred. 30% of the rapid Portland cement RPC was 
substituted with metakaolin. This substitution causes a significant decrease in both early strength 
and flowability. The required amount of water to increase the flowability as a 100% RPC was 
investigated. It was investigated if the following sulfate sources could increase the compressive 1-
day strength of mortar prisms; calcium sulfate dihydrate, hemihydrate, anhydrite, sodium sulfate, 
and potassium sulfate. For calcium sulfate dihydrate, hemihydrate, anhydrite, it was also 
investigated if it could increase the 28-day strength.  
To test the flowability of mortar, the FLSmidth method, which is based upon ASTM C 1437, was 
used. For the compressive strength test, the DS-EN 196-1 standard was used.  
It was found that calcium sulfate dihydrate, hemihydrate, anhydrite, sodium sulfate, and 
potassium sulfate all increased the compressive strength of a 30% MK substituted mortar mixture. 
Sodium sulfate and potassium sulfate were the most efficient at increasing the 1-day strength, but 
the calcium sulfates also increased the compressive 1-day strength.  
It was found that calcium sulfate hemihydrate and anhydrite did not change the compressive 28-
day strength compared to a 30% MK reference specimen. At large dosages, calcium sulfate 
dihydrate reduced the compressive strength of the mortar, with 30% substituted MK.  
Calcium sulfate hemihydrate and anhydrite reduced the flow of the mixture. Calcium sulfate 
dihydrate and potassium sulfate did not change the flow. At large dosages, sodium sulfate 
increased the flow.

1. Introduction  
 
It is well established that the main driver of climate change is caused by the release of carbon 
dioxide from human activities. The consequences of this climate change are manifold but include 
detrimental effects on human life [1]. A significant contributor to the release of carbon dioxide is 
the cement industry. The cement industry accounts for up to 8% of global carbon dioxide 
emissions. From the cement industry, there are two primary sources of emission which is the CO2 
releasing reactions, and the supply of energy required to heat the raw ingredients to above 1000 C 
[2, p. 2]. The detrimental effects on human life create a motivation to reduce the CO2 emission 
from cement production and thereby making the cement production more environmentally 
friendly. To achieve this goal, the use of supplementary cementitious materials SCM has been 
under investigation as an option, because it can be used in the cement mixture instead of the 
Portland clinker. The SCM, which will be used in this paper, is metakaolin MK, which is derived 
from the calcination of kaolinite clay [3, p. 1]. MK has been determined to be a widely available 
product, which can be found at high volumes at a low cost and is more environmentally friendly 
compared to CEM 1 [3, p. 161-162]. 
The use of MK in a Portland cement mixture creates several problems. There are two main 
problems which will be evaluated in this project: 
- 
Reduction in early compressive strength  
- 
Reduction in flowability 
In a 30% MK and 70% ordinary Portland cement OPC, a reduction in the compressive strength of 
the mortar for the early strength is found [4]. In this paper, a 30% MK and 70% rapid Portland 
cement RPC mixture will be used. When RPC is used, similar reductions in early strength is 
expected, as when using OPC.  
The first main problem is that a reduction of early compressive strength is found when using MK 
as a substitution of RPC. A reduction in the early compressive strength is undesirable because it 
reduces the rate of productivity. The introduction of chemical activation has been proposed to be 
a solution to low early strengths. The chemical activators, which will be used, are calcium sulfates 
and alkali sulfates. The suggestion of using these chemical activators is supported by earlier 
studies, where the increasement of early strength was found using alkali sulfates in a metakaolin 
and Portland cement systems [5]. In a prior bachelor project, calcium sulfate hemihydrate was 
found to increase the early strength development in the described MK system [6]. These findings 
indicate that calcium sulfate dihydrate and calcium sulfate anhydrite could be used for this 
purpose. The cause of the increased strength from calcium sulfates and alkali sulfates is suggested 
to be mainly the sulfate component. A sulfate optimization will, therefore, be made because it is 
proposed that this is the main factor, which determines the compressive strength outcomes from 
these sources. It has also been proposed that a shift in the sulfate optimum can occur between the 
early and late term strengths. This effect was found in pure Portland cement systems [7].

The second main problem comes from the reduction in workability when MK is substituted into 
the powder mixture [3, p. 162]. Powder is in this project being defined as the sum of RPC, MK, and 
a potential sulfate source. The reduction in workability means that in a blended cement mixture, 
extra water is required to achieve the same flow as in a 100% RPC mixture. In a previous project, it 
was found that a 0.55 water/powder ratio in a 70% RPC and 30% MK was required to achieve the 
same flow in a 100% RPC with a 0.55 water/powder ratio [6]. This finding will be investigated in 
this project to confirm if this is reproducible. It will further be investigated if the use of different 
sulfate sources will change the rheology by using a flow measurement. This is to determine if 
changes should be made to the water/powder ratio after a sulfate substitution has been made. 
2. Problem assignment  
 
This project seeks to evaluate metakaolin MK as supplementary cementitious material. This 
product has been made from kaolinite-based clay using a flash calciner at FLSmidth Dania. In the 
powder mixture, 30% of rapid Portland cement (RPC) will be substituted with the produced 
metakaolin. 
The following problems will be investigated during this project: 
- 
What is the required water/powder ratio of a 30 % MK and 70 % RPC mixture, to achieve 
the same slump (flow) as a mixture of 100% RPC and a water/powder ratio of 0.5? 
 
- 
How does substituting calcium sulfate dihydrate, calcium sulfate hemihydrate, calcium 
sulfate anhydrite, sodium sulfate, and potassium sulfate change the flow of the powder 
mixture? 
 
- 
What content of calcium sulfate dihydrate, hemihydrate, and anhydrite should be 
substituted to an original mixture of 30% MK and 70% RPC to achieve the highest 1-day 
and 28-day strength of mortar prisms? 
 
- 
What content of potassium sulfate and sodium sulfate should be substituted to an original 
mixture of 30% MK and 70% RPC, to achieve the highest 1-day strength of mortar prisms?

3. Theory and background  
 
3.1  Portland cement  
 
Portland cement is classified as being a hydraulic cement, which means that it reacts with water 
and creates a water-resistant product. Portland cement consists mainly of Portland clinker and 
gypsum, but inorganic materials such as fillers can be added. 
There are two main parts to the production of Portland cement 
1. The production of Portland clinker 
2. The crushing of Portland clinker, while adding gypsum and other inorganic materials.  
The Portland clinker can be produced by wet, dry, or semi wet processes [8, p. 4]. The most energy 
efficient method is the dry process with an energy input of 800 kcal/kg clinker, while the wet 
process uses 1400 kcal /kg clinker [9, p. 205]. In Denmark, the semi wet and wet process is used to 
produce the cement products from Aalborg Portland. In both processes, the chalk (limestone) 
CaCO3 is ground and homogenized in a slurry that contains 30-40% water. To the mixture, a source 
of silica SiO2 is required. The preferred sources of silica are clay and shale rather than quartz, 
which is because quarzitic silica does not react easily with limestone. A source of alumina Al2O3 
and iron oxide Fe2O3 is also required to be added into the mixture. The primary source of alumina 
and iron oxide is clay.  
The slurry is transported to dry crushing in which the water in the slurry will evaporate. The heat 
which is used to evaporate the water in the slurry is using some of the heat from the preheater in 
the form of smoke gas. The powder is moved to the top of a cyclone where the smoke gas is 
separated from the powder. The smoke gas is moved to an electro filter, where particles from the 
smoke gas are removed. In the cyclone preheater, the material is preheated to 750 C. 
Next, the material is calcined in two calciners, where the temperature is 900 C. In this calciner, the 
chalk (limestone) is heated to a temperature in which CO2 is released from the carbonate CaO.

to 1400 – 1500 C. The rotation of the oven causes the crushed material to move down the oven. 
Because of the high heat, partial melting, and sintering of the material will occur, which creates 
the clinker minerals [8, p. 4].

The clinker is then pulverized in a ball mill to a particle size between 10 and 15 micrometers. In the 
ball mill, approximately 5% gypsum is added. This means that of the mixture, 5% is gypsum, and 
95% is Portland clinker [9, p. 206].  
Gypsum is defined as being calcium sulfate dihydrate. The water which is bound in the crystal 
structure can dehydrate due to heat. The heat can be generated from the warm Portland clinker 
and/or the heat generated in the ball mill. The extent to which gypsum is dehydrated is hard to 
determine, which makes it relevant to know how the degree of hydration influences different 
parameters. In this project, it is the compressive strength and flow. If knowledge is provided such 
that the outcomes of these parameters are known, then it can be determined if the heat should be 
managed.

3.3 Hydration of C3S 
 
Portland clinker is composed of four main components: C3S, C2S, C3A, and C4AF. In this section, C3S, 
which is also known as tricalcium silicate, will be examined for its properties during hydration. The 
specific form of tricalcium silicate, which is present in Portland clinker, is called alite. Tricalcium 
silicate is the main component of Portland clinker, which, to a large extent, controls the setting 
and hardening of Portland cement. C3S usually makes up 50 – 60% of the total mass of the 
Portland cement [8, p. 9]. 
The hydration of C3S is complex and is not fully understood in the literature. The hydration begins 
when C3S is brought into contact with added water, which creates an amorphous calcium-silicate-
hydrate phase, also known as C-S-H gel. Amorphous means that there is a lack of definitive form or 
shape, which is the case of a crystalline structure.  In the C-S-H phase, there will be a CaO/SiO2 
molar ratio of less than 3, where calcium hydroxide Ca(OH)2, also known as portlandite or in short 
CH, will be made. The full reaction that occurs can be simplified as below on equation 9 [10, p. 
159].  
3CaO ∙SiO2 + (3 + 𝑚−𝑛)H2O = 𝑛CaO ∙SiO2 ∙𝑚H2O + (3 −𝑛)Ca(OH)2 
 
 9 
The C-S-H gel is the most important strength giving component in cured concrete, and the rate of 
development plays a significant role in the development of early compressive strength. The fast 
rate of early strength development can be contributed to the fast reaction with water compared 
to C2S, which has a much slower strength development compared to C3S [8, p. 9].  
The rate of consumption of C3S can be influenced by different factors, which include the amount 
of C3A and calcium sulfate gypsum and calcium sulfate anhydrite [10, p. 161] [8, p. 9].

3.4 Hydration of C2S  
 
C2S, also known as dicalcium silicate or belite, makes up about 20% of the clinker minerals. The 
hydration product of belite is very similar to the C-S-H product made from C3S hydration, but there 
may be a slight difference in the ratios of the components that makes up the C-S-H gel [10, p. 176]. 
Belite has a much slower reaction rate, compared to alite, which means that hydration of belite 
influence the late term strengths to a more significant extent compared to alite.  
3.5 Hydration of C4AF 
 
C4AF, also called tetracalciumaluminatferrite, is one of the four main mineral compounds in 
Portland clinker. It makes up about 10% of the total mass and contributes only slightly to the 
strength development of the Portland cement, compared to the other three components C3S, C2S, 
and C3A. When C4AF reacts with water, it will create metastable hydrates, which later in the 
hydration can convert into a stable product. The reaction rate of C4AF is much slower compared to 
that of C3A [8, p. 10].   
 
3.6 Hydration of C3A 
 
C3A is also known as tricalcium aluminate, which usually makes up about 5-10% of Portland 
cement. C3A is unique in the sense that it reacts both with water and with the added gypsum in 
Portland cement. The reaction rate of C3A is very fast, which needs to be retarded to prevent flash 
setting. This is controlled using gypsum as a reaction retarding agent. In the presence of water and 
the absence of CS, C3A reacts to create a gel like material on the surface on the C3A particles. This 
material then turns into the metastable hexagonal crystal products; C2AH8 and C4AH19. These two 
products are also known as AFm phases. From the liquid phase, these two products will also 
precipitate. This reaction is expressed below in equation 10.  
 
2 C3A + 21H →C4AH13 + C2AH8 
 
10 
When the metastable hexagonal crystal products are made, a stiffening of the paste can occur in 
less than 10 minutes. This phenomenon is called flash setting and creates irreversible damage to 
the mixture. Flash setting can be controlled using a retarding agent such as gypsum. Later in the 
hydration, these two products will convert further into C3AH6, which has a cubic structure, and is 
the only calcium aluminate hydrate which is thermodynamic stable at the ambient temperatures 
[10, p. 135].  
From the hydration kinetics of C3A, it is known that the hydration rate is slowed down soon after 
mixing, which is caused by the formation of the previously described hexagonal hydrates C2AH8 
and C4AH19. After the conversion of these has been done to the stable product C3AH6, the reaction

speed proceeds again at high speed. This description of the hydration of C3A is in the absence of

To control the setting of the paste, a source of sulfate SO4 must be present in the mixture. In 
Portland cement, the sulfate source is either calcium sulfate dihydrate or calcium sulfate 
anhydrite.  Sulfate will modify the reaction to produce a calcium sulfoaluminate hydrate C6AS̅3H32, 
which is also known as ettringite (trisulfate). The reaction can be seen below in equation 11. 
 
C3A + 3CS̅H2 + 26H →C6AS̅3H32

When the ettringite is made, there will be a period where the hydration rate is slowed down. The 
length of this dormant1 period will depend upon the amount of calcium sulfate in the phase. When 
the available amount of calcium sulfate has been consumed, the hydration rate will increase once 
again and is associated with an increase in heat development. When the calcium sulfate is 
consumed, the ettringite will react with the tricalcium aluminate, which will create a more stable 
product called calcium aluminate monosulfate hydrate (monosulfate) [10, p. 177]. The product 
reaction can be seen below in equation 12.  
C6AS̅3H32 + 2C3A + 4H →3C4AS̅H12 
 
12 
When the ettringite is consumed, the AFm phase C4AH19 can also start to form.  
The cause of the dormant period, where the rate of reaction is slowed as a result of added sulfate, 
is not obvious. The most common theory that describes the mechanism behind this phenomenon 
proposes that the ettringite builds up around the surface of the C3A, which creates a barrier that 
causes a stop of further reaction. When there is a high presence of sulfate, the solubility of C3A is 
limited, and the ettringite is formed in a through solution and precipitate at the surface of the C3A 
particles. This theory has been questioned, but as this is mechanistic speculation, the outcome 
data is of higher value [10, p. 177].   
3.7 Gypsum and different degrees of hydration of calcium sulfate

If the conversion happens and hemihydrate is made, there will be no change to the retarding

In this project, the optimum gypsum content in a 30% MK and 70% RPC will be investigated. It is 
known that the optimum gypsum content may be different based on which properties are being 
measured by, e.g., strength at various ages and drying shrinkage. In this paper, it is investigated 
what the strength outcomes of the concrete with varying sulfate content, which means that the 
strength will be the determining factor for what “optimum” means. The optimum sulfate content 
may not be the same for a paste, mortar, or a concrete mixture, which means that this is a 
limitation on the project, as only mortar prisms will be used as discussed in Materials and 
methodology [12, p. 219].

These different types of calcium sulfate can have different properties with regards to compressive 
strength and the workability of the cement. With regards to compressive strength, factors that can 
influence the course of early hydration can be: 
- 
The relative amounts of calcium sulfate, aluminate, and ferrite phases. 
- 
The rate of relevant ionic species becomes available in or at the surface of cement grains.  
- 
Physical properties as the particle size distribution.  
The rate of which relevant ionic species becomes available differs depending on which type of 
calcium sulfate hydration is used. The relevant ions are Ca+2 and SO4-2, which is supplied by the 
calcium sulfate. The rate of supply can be changed depending on the degree of hydration of the 
calcium sulfate. Hemihydrate has the highest rate, followed by gypsum, and the slowest is

the particle size distribution of the sulfate source is not considered. This proposes a limitation, as

Sulfate can also be provided by clinker, especially as alkali sulfates or as calcium langbeinite. The 
calcium ions can also be provided by the inclusion of limestone [12, p. 219-220].  
An increase in the amount of sulfate can cause a progressive decrease in the shrinkage, which 
occurs in the dry atmosphere (50% RH or lower) and a progressive increase in the expansion, 
which occurs in water. A too large expansion can cause the concrete to disintegrate. The limit to 
the amount of sulfate in the concrete is set to be 2.5 – 4.5%. Even though this is a typical 
permissible amount, it rarely occurs under 5 – 6%, according to the studies [12, p. 220]. This 
shrinkage expansion will not be investigated during the project, but it should be made aware that 
this is a limiting factor of the amount of sulfate, which can be added. It should also be mentioned 
that this is not tested in an MK – RPC system.  
Calcium sulfate is said to influence the quantities of products, especially at an early age, and the 
microstructure of all ages. Calcium sulfate also increases the rate of hydration of alite C3S and 
belite C2S. The increasement of hydration of alite and belite can explain the increasement in 
strength at an early age, which can be observed through experiments. Because the addition of 
calcium sulfate influences drying shrinkage, it is suggested that the microstructure is modified. A 
modification of the microstructure can occur by changes in the micromorphology of the C-S-H gel 
or by the precipitation of ettringite throughout the gel. The ettringite could, to a limited extent, 
increase the strength through a decreasement of porosity. Ettringite only has this increasement in 
the strength of early age, because of the alternative products which would have replaced it. If too 
much ettringite was created or if it were created at a later age, a decreasement of strength could 
occur due to the localized expansion [12, p. 220-221].  
This project chooses maximum strength in mortar prisms as a definition for optimum sulfate 
content. This optimal strength differs depending on the clinker and the source of sulfate used. The 
amount, which is assumed to be as a percentage of the total powder mass, is generally 3-5% for 1-
day strength. Variations of this can be caused by the difference in alkali content and the ratio 
between the sulfate and alkali in the clinker. According to Taylor, the optimal sulfate content for 
late age is generally found to have a shift to higher contents. This change in the optimum for 
sulfate for different ages (1-day and 28-day) is a primary point of investigation in this project [12, 
p. 220].

3.8 Alkali sulfates  
 
The alkali content is an important component of Portland cement clinker. The alkali comes from 
two primary sources, which includes the raw materials that make up the Portland clinker and an 
added source to the cement. In the clinker, the alkali can be present in the forms of either sulfates 
and solid solutions and can be found in the silicate or aluminate clinker. If added to the Portland 
cement, it can be in the form of alkali oxides or alkali sulfates [13, p. 1]. In this project, there will 
be a focus on the alkali sulfates. 
The alkali, which has been incorporated into the clinker minerals, does not seem to significantly 
increase the hydration progress of C3S and the strength development of cement. It is found that 
the hydration of C3A is accelerated when using K2O doped clinkers and slowed when using Na2O 
doped clinkers. Other studies have found that alkali, in general, leads to accelerated hydration [13, 
p. 1].  
The mechanism behind the accelerated hydration effect of the alkali sulfate can be found in the 
increasement in the pH of the liquid phase [13, p. 1-2]. In the paper [13], the mechanisms behind 
how an increasement of the pH value is achieved are discussed. The first mechanism which the 
paper presented shows that when the alkali is added in the form of sulfates, some of the sulfates 
are dissolved and reacts with the aluminate to form ettringite AFt. During this formation, sulfate 
replaces the hydroxide ion OH- in the portlandite Ca(OH)2 to form CaSO4. The increasement of 
hydroxide ions will increase the pH of the solution. A part of the sulfate will be absorbed on the 
surface of the C3A particles, which also might promote a rise in the pH of the pore solution [13, p. 
7].  
Another mechanism of how the increased pH is found can be explained by the higher 
concentration of alkali metal in the liquid phase [13, p. 2]. It should be mentioned that alkali 
sulfate will not increase the pH value if just dissolved in pure water, but it seems to do so in the

3.10 Kaolinite and its conversion to metakaolin 
 
Kaolinite is a clay mineral and is the main component of the rock called kaolin. The molecular 
name of kaolinite is Al2Si2O5(OH)4, which also can be written as Al2O3·2SiO2·2H2O. Kaolinite 
composes of 39.8% alumina, 46.3% silica, and 13.9% water [14, p. 6]. Based on a material analysis 
of Danish RPC in appendix 1, it is found that RPC contains 19.21% SiO2 and 5.07% Al2O3. Kaolinite 
has based on this, a high content of aluminate and silicate compared to Danish RPC. Kaolinite has 
an alternating sheet of SiO4 and [Al2(OH4)]2 and forms a structure that has a hexagonal symmetry 
[14, p. 6].   
Kaolinite does not have any pozzolanic effect. The term pozzolan describes a material that 
possesses reactive silicate and/or aluminate. The pozzolan will, in the presence of water, react 
with calcium hydroxide to form a product which possesses cementitious properties. The products 
which are made through a pozzolanic reaction are calcium silicate hydrates and or calcium 
aluminate hydrates [16]. To achieve a pozzolanic effect, it must be converted to metakaolin 
through a dehydroxylation of kaolinite. A dehydroxylation changes the crystal structure of 
kaolinite, which becomes partially disordered. From the dehydroxylation of kaolinite, a hydroxyl 
group OH is released by the formation of water H2O [3, p. 154-155]. This reaction can be seen 
below in equation 14 and shows the conversion from kaolinite to metakaolin.

3.11 Pozzolanic activity of metakaolin 
 
When Portland cement is fully hydrated, it produces about 28% of its weight in CH. This number is 
theoretical, which means that in practice, it usually does not exceed 20%. The CH, which is 
produced, can reduce the durability of the concrete, and it is not clear if the CH that is produced 
contributes to the strength [17, p. 441]. It is, therefore, beneficial to reduce the amount of CH in 
the concrete. This can be done through the addition of MK, which reacts with the CH produced by 
the Portland cements. When the MK reacts with CH and water, it produces a cementitious 
aluminum C-S-H gel. This gel can contribute to the strength of the concrete or mortar. The 
reaction between MK and CH and water also creates crystalline products such as calcium 
aluminate hydrates and alumino-silicate hydrates. This can be products such as C2ASH8, C4AH13, 
and C3AH6 [17, p. 442].  
The reactivity of MK can be determined through a Chapelle test that can determine the rate of CH 
consumption through hydration. This determines the pozzolanic activity of the given pozzolan, 
which in this case, is MK [17, p.442-443]. In the paper [17], it was found that the reactivity of MK 
was 1050 mg Ca(OH)2/g pozzolan, compared to 875 for fly ash, and 427 for silica fume.  
The mixture which is used in this project is 70% RPC and 30% MK. This ratio was suggested by 
FLSmidth but is also supported by the literature. In [17, p. 443], the required amount of MK 
substituted until all the CH was consumed was found to be at 20% or between 30- and 40% 
depending on the specific paper. Higher substitution amounts than these would lead to unreacted 
MK, which is not desirable.

mixing was made at high speed, with the lid closed to prevent small particles from being removed. 
After the 60 seconds, the mixer was stopped, and the bowl was taken out where hand mixing was 
conducted. This was a necessary step because the mixer could not mix the powders properly. This 
was caused by the mixing head not reaching down to the bottom of the bowl. The hand mixing 
was done using a rotating motion with 1-2 rotations per second for 60 seconds, making sure the 
whole bowl was adequately mixed.  
 
4.5 Mortar mixing procedure  
 
Mixing of mortar will be carried out during the different tests, therefore a general explanation of 
the process. The method of mixing mortars will be according to the standard DS/EN 196-1:2016, 
Metoder til prøvning af cement – del 1: Styrkebestemmelse.  
Before the testing, the dry powder mixing procedure must have been made if two or more 
powders are used. The standard materials and amount used in the mixing of mortar are as follows; 
demineralized water 225 ± 1 g, 1350 g ± 5 standard CEM sand, and 450 ± 2 g of cement. For this 
project, there will be a variation in the amount of water and cement used. The standard also uses 
the term cement, where it in this report will be renamed to powder because of substitution and 
addition of pozzolans. For the mixtures, a whole bag of CEM standard sand will be used pr. portion 
of the mortar mixture. This is to prevent changing, e.g., the particle size distribution of the sand. In 
this project, there will be used both the substitution method and the addition method to create 
the mixture samples. These two methods differ slightly in the inter material proportions. The 
method of mixing is described in The substitution method and The addition method.  
The mixing steps are as follows: 
1. A bowl of powder will be placed in a mixer. To the powder mass water will be added. It is 
important to note the specific time of this occurring, as it is the zero time and is used to 
mark the time that the curing has begun.  
2. Immediately after the water has been brought together with the powder mixture, the mixer 
is started at low speed (turning button pointing at 1) for 30 seconds. After 30 seconds of 
mixing, the DS/EN 196-1 tells to add sand steadily during the next 30 seconds. For safety 
reasons, the machine is stopped, and the whole bag is poured into the mixture. The mixer is 
then started again at a low speed for 30 seconds. After this, the mixer is then set to high 
speed (turning button pointing at 2) for 30 seconds. 
3. After the 30 seconds, the mixer is stopped, and the mortar sticking to the sides, and the 
bottom is released using a scraper and placed in the middle of the bowl. The total duration 
of this stop should be 90 seconds.  
==PAGE:31==
Christian Hvirgel Hansen – s153352

4. The mortar mixture is then placed in the mixer and mixed at high speed for 60 seconds.  
The mixture tables for the compressive strength test are found in appendix 10, and the mixture 
tables for the flow test are found in appendix 11.  
 
4.6 Compressive test 
 
The compressive test method will be done according to DS/EN 196-1:2016, metoder til prøvning af 
cement – del 1: Styrkebestemmelse. It is previously described how the mortar mixture is made, 
which is done according to the same standard. A summary of this and changes made can be seen 
under mortar mixing procedure.  
Mortar prisms were chosen instead of concrete test subjects because of material limitations. It is 
assumed that the mortar prisms do not differentiate much from concrete. There is also the 
problem of handling large concrete cubes, which would require that more than 1 person must be 
working at a time for safety reasons. The mortar mixing table can be seen in appendix 2.  
Below a stepwise summary of the method described in DS/EN 196-1 is shown: 
1. The mortar mixture, which has been made based on the mortar mixing procedure, should 
immediately be transferred to the vibration table. A frequency of 50 Hertz on the table is 
used. A mold made according to the standard is used.  
2. The vibration procedure is done according to the A.3.2 compaction procedure using a 
vibration table, B found in the standard. 
3. The mold is transported to a climate room with a temperature of 20 ± 1 C and with a RH of 
90% 
In the standard, it states that the room which should store the mold shall be maintained at a 
temperature of 20±1C and have a RH of not less than 90%. The last condition could not be 
achieved because of the facilities available at DTU BYG, but it is not expected to have a significant 
influence on the development of strength. The temperature of the room was about 20.5 C and 
about 86% RH. The climate room data is shown in appendix 16.  
4. If the mortar specimen is going to be tested for 24-hour compressive strength, then the 
demolding will be done 20 minutes before the 24-hour mark of the zero time. If this step is 
true, skip to step 6.  
5. If the mortar specimen is a 28-day compressive strength test, it will be demolded between 
20-hour and 24 hours after the zero time. The specimen is placed under 20 C water. 
In the standard DS/EN 196-1, it is not a requirement to add calcium hydroxide to the water. It was 
chosen to do so because of standard practice on DTU. The amount of calcium hydroxide added to 
the water bath was according to ASTM C 511 – 03, Standard specification for Mixing Rooms, Moist 
Cabinet, Moist Rooms, and water Storage Tanks Used in the Testing of Hydraulic Cements and 
Concretes. According to this standard, 3 g/L of calcium hydroxide is added to the water bath.

electromechanical test machine. The bending test of the mortar specimen is first

4.8 The procedure of making the mixing tables 
 
In this project, several sources of sulfate will be used in a metakaolin – Portland cement system. 
Therefore considerations of how these sources are added should be considered. There are two 
methods of how to incorporate these sources, which is either the substitution method or the 
addition method. Both methods are used in the project.  
 
4.8.1 The substitution method  
 
The substitution method uses a base assumption that the powder mass, which is defined as being 
the sum of RPC, metakaolin, and the sulfate source, will be kept constant. The powder will be at a 
constant 450 g. The metakaolin is also kept at a constant 135 g, which corresponds to 30% of the 
total powder mass. If a sulfate source is introduced, it will then substitute some of the RPC mass. 
This is because calcium sulfate is already a component of portland cement. This reason is also 
extended to be the case for alkali sulfates. To calculate the amount of substitution which has been

The mortar specimens AN-1-FLS-SUB and H-1-FLS-SUB had about the same compressive strength.

be inefficient at increasing the compressive strength compared to  𝛽-anhydrite. 
The calcium sulfates all reached an optimum of compressive strength with a content of about 3.5% 
of powder mass. The peak that is found at about 3.5% could have shifted if more specimens had 
been made using a smaller increment of powder substitution. At each step of lower calcium 
sulfate hydration, the mass of the sulfate will make up a larger proportion of the calcium sulfate 
mass. Because the sulfate is the most important strength developing component in calcium 
sulfate, then this could give an advantage for anhydrite or hemihydrate compared to gypsum. A 
direct comparison of the sulfate will be shown in figure 13.  
There is a clear difference between the calcium sulfate strength, which has been made at 
FLSmidth, and those who have been made at DTU. The addition or substitution method describes 
how the mixing table is calculated. The addition method was used in this case when mixing the H-
1-DTU-ADD series, but the substitution method was used at FLS mixtures. The difference between 
these two methods was tested at DTU. It was found that using the substitution method, there was 
a compressive strength of mortar at 10.97 MPa, and for the addition method, a compressive 
strength of mortar at 11.05 MPa was found. This means that there is no apparent difference 
between using the addition method or the substitution method for when mixing the mortar. The 
temperature is required to be kept at 20 C, and changes to this curing temperature will change the 
rate of strength development. The temperature of the specimens at DTU was at a constant of 20 C 
during the curing period. The temperature and the relative humidity of the climate room where 
the mortar specimens were curing can be seen in appendix 16. The same procedure of mixing and 
testing the mortar specimens has been used in the two laboratories, which is DS/EN 196-1. From 
this, there is no explanation for the difference in strength besides the calibration of the test 
machines.

6. Conclusion 
 
It was found that a 0.55 water – powder ratio was required for a 30% MK and 70% RPC mortar 
mixture to achieve about the same flow as a 0.5 water – powder ratio 100% RPC mortar mixture.  
It was found that calcium sulfate dihydrate, hemihydrate, anhydrite, sodium sulfate, and 
potassium sulfate increased the compressive strength of an MK substituted mortar mixture.  
- 
An optimum of compressive strength of calcium sulfate dihydrate was found at about 
1.95% sulfate substitution or 3.5% powder substitution.  
 
- 
An optimum of compressive strength of calcium sulfate hemihydrate was found at about 
2.32% sulfate substitution or 3.5% powder substitution.  
 
- 
An optimum of compressive strength of calcium sulfate anhydrite was found at about 
2.47% sulfate substitution or 3.5% powder substitution.  
 
- 
An optimum of compressive strength of potassium sulfate was found at about 1.87% 
sulfate substitution or 3.39% powder substitution.  
 
- 
An optimum of sodium sulfate was not found as there was an increment to the 
compressive strength each time the sodium sulfate substitution increased.  
Calcium sulfate dihydrate had the least increasement of the compressive strength of the MK 
substituted mixture. Calcium sulfate hemihydrate and anhydrite the same increasement in 
compressive strength. Hemihydrate and anhydrite had higher compressive strength than 
dihydrate even after adjusting the water / powder ratio because of the reduced flowability.  
The 1-day strength optimization of the calcium sulfates required a higher amount of sulfate when 
the degree of hydration of the calcium sulfate was reduced.  
Calcium sulfate hemihydrate and anhydrite did not change the compressive strength of 28-day 
compared to a reference 30% MK substitution specimen. An optimum of compressive strength of 
calcium sulfates 28-day compressive strength was not found. It was found that calcium dihydrate 
decreased the compressive strength when large amounts were substituted in the mixture.   
It was found that calcium sulfate hemihydrate and anhydrite decreased the flowability of a mortar 
mixture at dosages, which gave an optimum 1-day strength. Calcium sulfate dihydrate and 
potassium sulfate did not change the flow of the mixture at dosages, which gave an optimum 1-
day strength. It was found that at large enough dosages, there was an increase in the flow when 
sodium sulfate was used.

7. Further research  
 
 
Potassium sulfate and sodium sulfate were found to be very efficient at increasing the compressive 1-day 
strength. Because of the extra alkali, which will be substituted into the mortar from the alkali sulfates, it is 
required to study the effects of ASR in an RPC and MK system. It is also relevant to study the long-term 
effects of the extra sulfate in the mixture in an RPC and MK system, which can cause ettringite formation.  
It has been suggested that a calorimeter can be used to determine the optimum sulfate content. If this 
method can be used for this purpose, then a sulfate optimization process can be done faster.  
Investigations of different MK substitutions should be made to optimize the MK substation degree.

Appendix 6 – The substitution method  
 
This method describes how the substation method is used for creating a mixing scheme.  
The CEM standard sand content will always be 1350 g pr. recipe. The water content will be either 
0.5 or 0.55. The water mass is calculated, as shown in the equation. 
w/p = water mass
powder mass →w/p ∙ powder mass = water mass 
 
 
 
 
Where the powder mass is the sum of RPC, metakaolin, and sulfate source.  
For the metakaolin, there will be a 30% substitution of the total powder mass, which for the 
substitution method will be kept at a constant 450 g. The amount of metakaolin, which will be 
used in every recipe, is 135 g and is calculated on the equation below.  
Metakaolin mass = powder mass ∙30% →450 g ∙30% = 135 g   
 
 
 
Metakaolin makes up 30% of the total powder mass during the substitution method, which means 
that the 70% consist of RPC and, if added, a sulfate source. The amount of sulfate source which is 
substituted is measured in the percentage of cementsub mass. The cementsub mass is defined as 
being the total mass of sulfate source plus RPC. This definition can be seen below in the equation. 
 
Cementsub =  RPC mass + Sulfate source mass 
 
 
 
For the definition of what cementsub mass is, the sulfate source mass can be found using the 
equation below. 
 
Sulfate source mass
= (powder mass −metakaolin mass) ∗% of Cementsub

Appendix 7 – The addition method 
 
This method describes how the addition method is used for creating a mixing scheme.  
For the method of addition, the amount of CEM standard sand, which is used pr. Mold is always a 
full bag 1350 g. For this method, 30% substitution metakaolin with RPC is used as well, but the 
main difference here is that the added sulfate source is not calculated into the cement mass. The 
water mass is calculated using the same equation as before [ HENVIS TIL water equation].  
 
0.55 = 𝑤𝑎𝑡𝑒𝑟 𝑚𝑎𝑠𝑠
𝑝𝑜𝑤𝑑𝑒𝑟 𝑚𝑎𝑠𝑠→0.55 ∗𝑝𝑜𝑤𝑑𝑒𝑟 𝑚𝑎𝑠𝑠= 𝑤𝑎𝑡𝑒𝑟 𝑚𝑎𝑠𝑠 
 
 
 
 
There will be a 30% substitution of RPC to metakaolin. This means that instead of using the total 
powder mass, only the total mass of RPC and metakaolin will be used. The total mass of 
metakaolin and RPC will be kept at a constant 450 g. The equation to calculate the metakaolin 
mass can be seen below, as well as the equation to calculate the RPC mass.  
𝑀𝑒𝑡𝑎𝑘𝑎𝑜𝑙𝑖𝑛 𝑚𝑎𝑠𝑠= (𝑅𝑃𝐶+ 𝑚𝑒𝑡𝑎𝑘𝑎𝑜𝑙𝑖𝑛 ) ∗30% →450 𝑔∗30%

The sulfate source, which will be incorporated into the mixture, is introduced through the method

The sulfate source is added as a percentage of RPC mass. This calculation can be seen below  
𝑆𝑢𝑙𝑓𝑎𝑡𝑒 𝑠𝑜𝑢𝑟𝑐𝑒 𝑚𝑎𝑠𝑠= 𝑅𝑃𝐶∗% 𝑎𝑑𝑑𝑒𝑑 𝑠𝑢𝑙𝑓𝑎𝑡𝑒 𝑠𝑜𝑢𝑟𝑐𝑒
