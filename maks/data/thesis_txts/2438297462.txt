Abstract
The ﬁeld of gene therapy has developed dramatically within the last two decades, however
new and improved methods to facilitate targeted delivery of therapeutic oligonucleotide
are highly warranted. The Low Density Lipoprotein receptor (LDLR) is a cell surface
expressed protein that binds and internalizes low density lipoprotein (LDL), resulting in
cholesterol being made available to the cell. Therefore, over-expression of LDLR leads to
LDL (cholesterole) being removed from the blood and preventing hypercholesterolemia.
Previous studies have shown that by targeting a long non-coding RNA for the LDLR
gene, LDLR-expression can be increased by transcriptional silencing of the LDLR reg-
ulatory lncRNA (BM450697) and its promoter. Also, it has been demonstrated that
siRNA-N-acetylgalactosamine (GalNAc) conjugates stabilize the silencing.
In this project, siRNA targeting lncRNA BM450697 (si5) and its promoter region
(si pro5) have been synthesized containing a 3’-alkyne handle enabling post-synthetic
incorporation of GalNAc. To make these conjugates, β-GalNAc-PEG3-azide was synthe-
sized and was applied together with commercially available α-GalNac-PEG3-azide, for
CuAAC-mediated click reaction to obtain α- and β-GalNAc conjugates of si5 R1 (sense
strands). Furthermore, a new bis-functional scaﬀold for terminal labelling of siRNA
therapeutics primed for TGS has been synthesized. By coupling of this bis-functional
scaﬀold to any oligonucleotide sequence, several drug delivery enhancers can potentially
be conjugated to increase delivery to target tissue.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Expansion of the biological pipeline
For the last hundred years, developments in drug discovery have had an enormous
impact on the quality of human health and lives [1]. Initially, scientists focused on small
molecules that bind speciﬁc biological macromolecules thereby altering the activity or
function of the target [2]. Few exceptions included proteins like insulin, a naturally
occurring hormone that could be isolated and puriﬁed to treat patients from either type
1 or type 2 diabetes [3]. The advantages of small molecule drugs are that they are easily
produced and formulated into a capsule or tablet, for oral administration [4]. In 1997,
Christopher Lipinski proposed a set of guidelines based on observations from existing
drugs on the market for predicting the likelihood of a compound to succeed as an oral
drug [5, 6]. He observed that a drug should exhibit the following properties,
• A molecular mass below 500 Dalton
• An octanol-water partition coeﬃcient (Log P) less than 5
• No more than 5 hydrogen bond donors
• No more than 10 hydrogen bond acceptors
However, in the last couple of decades there has been an increasing interest and focus
on biopharmaceuticals, so-called biologics [7]. Global pharmaceutical companies are
expanding their biological pipeline, and biologics represent an increasingly large share
of new drug approvals [8]. In 2017, 66 % of all FDA approved pharmaceuticals on the
market were small molecules, whereas biopharmaceuticals only represented 27 % [9].
Notably, when considering the distribution of pharmaceuticals in phase II/III in 2017,
62 % represented biologics while only 30 % were conventional drugs [9]. This tendency
shows that the pharmaceutical industry is moving towards biopharmaceuticals and away
form conventional drugs. Most progress has been made within protein based biologics,
like peptides and antibodies, though the ﬁeld of gene therapy has developed dramatically
within the last to decades [10]. Gene therapy is on a very early stage, having the ﬁrst
FDA approved gene therapeutic (Luxturna
TM, Spark Therapeutics) on the market by
2017 [11], never the less gene therapy is sought to have a key role in future treatments
[10].

1.2
Gene therapy
Gene therapy has become a popular technique within the medical ﬁeld due to its wide
possibilities within many genetic diseases [12]. In gene therapy synthetic genetic material
is transferred into the patient cell to treat any disease caused by genetic mutation in
the human genome. This technique makes it possible to treat diseases like Alzheimer’s,
cancer, and HIV etc. [13, 14, 15]. The advantages of gene therapy rely on targeting
disease on a transcriptional level - and endeavdor diﬃcult to achieve using small molecule
drugs [12]. Even in neurodegenerative diseases that are considered incurable, such as
amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), Parkinson and spinal muscular atrophy, therapeutic
RNA has shown promising results [16]. The route of administration for gene therapy
targeting CNS is spinal cord injection enabling the therapeutic to reach the CNS [12].
This is not the most convenient way of drug delivery, however systemic delivery has
not yet been successful as nucleic acid therapeutics themselves cannot cross the blood
brain barrier (BBB). Their high degree of anionic charge and their large size prohibit
them to eﬃciently cross the lipidic BBB by passive diﬀusion [12]. Moreover, natural
DNA and RNA sequences are not stable when injected intravenously (IV) due to rapid
excretion by the kidneys or degradation by enzymes, and it is now recognized that DNA
and RNA-based therapeutics need structural modiﬁcations in order to exhibit moderate
stability and half-life during circulation [17].
One main challenge in realizing the full potential of nucleic acid therapeutics is ef-
ﬁcient delivery of such therapeutics into targeted tissues and cells [18]. They must
overcome a billion years of evolutionary defenses designed to keep invading nucleic acids
from entering cells, before these therapeutics can become drugs [19]. Never the less,
delivery of RNA or DNA into nucleus is an exciting challenge due to limited permeability
of the nuclear membrane and intracellular mechanisms for eliminating exogenous DNA
and RNA sequences. As mentioned, gene therapy has developed tremendously within the
last 20 years, and some of the most promising types of gene therapy are described below.
Antisense oligonucleotide (ASO)
Antisense oligonucleotides (ASO) are synthetic single stranded DNA or RNA between
15 and 25 nucleotides in length that hybridize to a speciﬁc RNA sequence in cytoplasm
thereby interfering with gene expression [12]. Antisense-mediated gene silencing was ﬁrst
introduced by Stephenson and Zamecnik in 1978 [20]. Since then, modiﬁcations of the
backbone and sugar components have improved stability, binding strength and speciﬁcity
which have made ASOs suitable for therapeutic applications [12]. There are several ways
an ASO can modulate gene expression within the cell (Figure 1.1); a) Binding of ASO to
target mRNA can function as a steric block and prevent ribosomal translation of mRNA,
b) ASO binds to RNase H enzyme, which use it as a guide strand to speciﬁcally cleave
ASO targeted mRNA strand. Finally, ASO can interfere with RNA intermediates such
as splicing, secundary structure, capping etc., but RNase H mediated cleavage of mRNA
is by far the most explored mechanism of RNAi [21].
Interferring RNA
In 1998, Nobel prize laureates Andrew Z. Fire and Craig C. Mello discovered the
mechanism of interfering RNA. RNAi is a natural and powerfull tool for the organism to

Transcriptional gene silencing and CRISPR-Cas
The ﬁeld of RNA therapeutics is rapidly developing, with the focus of shifting from intra-
cytosol to intra-nuclear action mechanisms [22]. Two recently discovered mechanisms
for RNA based therapy, transcriptional gene silencing (TGS) and gene editing clustered
regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR)-Cas, interact directly with
chromatin in nucleus thereby acting on a transcriptional level [23, 22].
In transcriptional gene silencing either an ASO or siRNA enters the nucleus and bind
to transcript factors to prevent gene expression of a speciﬁc gene (silencing). TGS is
mechanistically diﬀerent from RNAi and results in long-term stable gene expression
modiﬁcations [22]. Instead of targeting protein coding mRNA in cytoplasm, siRAN or
ASO can be directed to target long non-coding RNA (lncRNA). Long non-coding RNA
and silencing proteins are located in the nucleus interacting directly with chromatin to
facilitate epigenetic repression [22]. Though, when either a lncRNA targeted ASO or a
lncRNA targeted siRNA are present in nucleus, they hybridize to lncRNA which inhibit
interaction of the necessary silencing proteins resulting in either activation or repression

1.3
Nucleic acids
Both DNA and RNA are built up by nucleic acids oligomers and constitute all genetic
material in living cells. In the nucleus, nucleotides are stored as deoxyribonucleic acids
(DNA), which fold up into chromosomes. While DNA stores information regarding the
whole genome of the living organism, ribonucleic acids (RNA) have several functions
within the cell. The main role of RNA is to transcript the genetic information from DNA
and translate it into proteins [24]. RNA carries out many functions related to protein
synthesis (ribosomal RNA and transfer RNA) or regulation of gene expression (miRNA
and siRNA). The most essential function of a cell is called the central dogma (Figure
1.3) and can be devided into three key functions; cell division (replication), DNA→RNA
(transcription) and RNA→proteins (translation). Compared with small molecular drugs
that often target proteins, gene therapy works on the translational and transcriptional
level.

The nature of RNA
Despite very few diﬀerences between DNA nucleotides and RNA nucleotides, the absence
of the 2’-hydroxyl group in DNA changes the stability of DNA with a 100 fold compared to
RNA. In basic conditions, the 2’-hydroxyl group can make an intramolecular nucleophilic
attack on the phosphate backbone resulting in cleavage of the RNA strand as seen
in Figure 1.7. Enzymes that use this mechanism are RNases. Thus, the presence of
2’-hydroxyl group on every nucleotide of RNA makes it labile, easily degradable and
diﬃcult to work with compared to DNA. From an evolutionary perspective, it makes
sense that RNA is inherently unstable. It is not designed to survive longer than to be
translated into protein, after which it serves no role. In gene therapy a 2’ modiﬁcation
(e.g. 2’-OMe) is often introduced to increase half-life of RNA therapeutics [17].

solid-phase technique has been widely used in many areas of the chemistry due to its great
advantages. In oligonucleotide synthesis, synthetic oligonucleotide strands are build up
from solid-support, nucleotide by nucleotide, from 3’ to 5’ end using the phosphoramidite
strategy. Every nucleotide has a phosphoramidite build into the 3’ while the 5’ end is
protected by 4,4’-Dimethoxytrityl (DMT) (Figure 1.9).

Puriﬁcation of oligosnucleotides
Next step was puriﬁcation of newly synthesized oligonucleotides. This process is important
in order to remove all possible impurities and failure sequences. The puriﬁcation is
usually carried out by high-pressure liquid chromatography (HPLC), where the molecules
are separated according to polarity and charge. For puriﬁcation of oligonucleotides
reverse phase (RP) and ion exchange (IE) HPLC are the most common techniques. In
RP-HPLC, the molecules are separated by polarity using a non-polar column material
(such as C18), and in IE-HPLC the separation is caused by diﬀerences in ion-exchange
aﬃnity to the column. A mobile liquid phase is run through a stationary phase with high
pressure over a gradient. The gradient can be adjusted to the oligonucleotides retention
time (Rt) in order to get the best possible separation. In this project IE-HPLC has been
applied.
If the olignucleotides are synthesised with DMT on 5’-end, two peaks usually appears
on HPLC, one with DMT and one without (cleaved oﬀduring the run). Furthermore,
on IE-HPLC the DMT-on oligonucleotide is expected at later retention times when

Bioconjugation and modiﬁcation of oligonucleotides
Bioconjugations is an important tool in many aspects of chemistry and pharmaceutical
industry [28, 29]. When a small molecule or a biomolecule are attached to another
biomolecule by a covalent bond, they form a new compound with properties dictated
by each molecule. This has proved a useful strategy in many contexts, such as in drug
delivery and molecular diagnostics. There are many types of reactions that can be
applied to connect biomolecules. Most essential is that the reaction is easy to per-
form in high yields and that it selectively reacts over other functional groups. With
this tool, oligonucleotides can be conjugated to a variety of proteins, carbohydrates
or lipids rendering a wide range of opportunities within the drug development [30, 19, 29].

as the name indicates, the reaction is just as easy as playing with LEGO. In 2001, K.
B. Sharpless reported this reaction as being high yielding, stereospeciﬁc, and simple
to perform [31]. Several reactions have been identiﬁed as click reactions, though one

Modiﬁcations
As mentioned, the stability of RNA oligonucleotides can be modulated by thiolation of
backbone, and by insertion of 2’ modiﬁcations such as 2’-OMe or 2’-F. These modiﬁcations
can increase the half life during systemic circulation, but too many phosphorothiolations
can be toxic and cause immunogenic reactions [33, 34]. To improve the stability and
delivery of the oligonucleotide, some of the biomolecules listed bellow can be conjugated
to enhance its cellular delivery,
• Cell penetrating peptides
• Galactosamine (GalNAc)
• Nuclear localization signal peptide
• Lipids
Cell penetrating peptides
Cell penetrating peptides (CPPs) are small peptides of 10 to 16 amino acids and are rich
in cationic amino acids, which adsorb to the phospholipids present in the cell membrane
thereby entering the cell by various possible endosytosis pathways [35]. Several peptides
have been investigated, where notably the Tat-peptide from HIV-1 -virus had a very strong
transmembrane capacity and therefore a great potential for drug delivery applications
[35]. Recently it was shown that ASOs tagged with arginine rich CPP were able to cross
the blood brain barrier and reached the brain of wild-type mice [36]. Though, some
major drawbacks of CPPs include toxicity and lack of speciﬁcity. The primary sequence
of Tat-peptide is shown bellow,
H2N −Gly −Arg −Lys−Lys−Arg −Arg −Gln−Arg −Arg −Arg −Pro−Gln−OH
N-acetylgalactosamine (GalNAc)
One of the most prominent modules for delivering siRNA and ASOs to hepatocytes is a
trimer of N-acetylgalactosamine (GalNAc), which bind to the asialoglycoprotein receptor
(ASGPR) that is predominantly expressed on hepatocytes [37]. ASGPR was discovered
by Gilbert Ashwell and Anatol Morell in 1965, and a few years later they found that
the sugar analogue with the highest aﬃnity for this receptor is GalNAc [19]. Since then,
GalNAc-siRNA conjugates have proven very eﬃcent and show 10-fold greater potency
than siRNA without GalNAc. ASGPR can bind 1-4 GalNAc but the trimer with mutual
distances of ∼20 ˚A, shows the highest aﬃnity [19].
When an siRNA-GalNAc conjugate binds to the ASGPR, they enter the hepatocyte
by receptor-mediated endocytosis. The siRNA is then released into cytoplasm where
the ASGPR gets recycled, the ASO associates with RISC, and the sense strand gets
degraded (Figure 1.22) [19].
Lipids
Another strategy is conjugation of lipids to oligonucleotides to enhance cellular delivery.
This mechanism is based on simple diﬀusion, where the lipid module facilitates the
delivery of a lipid-conjugate ASO over the membrane. Studies have shown that RNA in
complex with palmitic acid (C16) can enhances the ability of oligonucleotides to cross
the cell membrane [38].

Nuclear localized signal peptides
A nuclear localization signal (NLS) is a short peptide sequence that ’tags’ a cargo-molecule
for import to nucleus by nuclear transport. NLS sequences have shown potential ability
to improve nuclear delivery of DNA [39]. The NLS peptides facilitates nular transport by
binding to receptor and adaptor proteins to form a nuclear pore targeting complex. This
complex can dock into a nuclear pore channel, by which eﬃcient mediated intranucleus
transport can occure [40]. A variety of delivery enhancers have been reported in litterature
such as antibodies, liposomes, transferrine and nanoparticles, however these are not
discussed in further details.

Hypercholesterolemia is a state characterized by high cholesterol levels in the blood.
Hypercholesterolemia is a serious condition because of the increased risks of developing
atherosclerosis and other conditions. Cholesterol plays key biochemical roles and origi-
nates mainly from diet. The liver is able store cholesterol but transport it to other tissues
upon increasing exogenous levels of cholesterol. Cholesterol is transported in low-density
lipoproteins (LDL), which are recognized by LDL-receptor (LDLR) on the surface of all
cells (especially hepatocytes), where they are stored in Golgi apparatus [41]. Studies have
shown that by targeting a long non-coding RNA for the LDLR gene, LDLR-expression
can be increased by transcriptional silencing of the LDLR regulatory lncRNA (BM450697)
and its promotor [42]. Also it has been demonstrated that siRNA-GalNAc conjugates
stabilize the silencing of BM450697 [42].

In order to attach a cell penetrating peptide such as Tat, a lipid or GalNAc to the
5’-modiﬁed sense strands (KAS44 and KAS45), the initial idea was to install an alkyne
functional group by performing an amide coupling of the Fmoc-deprotected amine using
hexynoic acid. Incorporation of an alkyne into the oligonucleotide enabled subsequent
click reactions with GalNAc-PEG-azide.
The strategy of attaching hexynoic acid to the oligonucleotides is depicted in Figure
2.11) and was inspired previously reported work using LNA instead of RNA [49].
After solid-phase oligonucleotide synthesis of KAS44 and KAS45, strands were kept on
the column to enable amide coupling on solid-phase. Having the column material cen-
tralized in between two syringes, this approach enable eﬃcient and successive couplings
and washes.
Initially the 5’-modiﬁed oligonucleotide (KAS44 and KAS45) was added 20% diethy-
lamine (DEA) to convert the 2’-cyanoethyl phosphate triester into the corresponding
phosphate. This was done to eliminate the risk of conjugate addition between the free
amine and the acrylonitrile remnant formed during backbone deprotection [49]. Fmoc
was removed by standard procedure (20% piperidine in DMF) followed by wash with
DMF and EtOH. To ensure the Fmoc has been removed, the absorbance was measured

Chapter 3
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to synthesize six RNA oligonucleotide sequences to provide
modiﬁed sets of siRNA for targeting of lncRNA (BM450697) and its promoter in
hepatocytes. Satisfyingly, antisense strands (KAS42 and KAS43) and sense strands with
the 3’-alkyne handle Y were successfully synthesized using solid-phase oligonucleotide
synthesis. The antisense strands were modiﬁed with three phosphorothioate backbone
linkages resulting in a non trivial HPLC trace for both analogues (KAS42 and KAS43),
whose identity were conﬁrmed by MALDI. Furthermore, strands KAS40 and KAS41
were obtained in high purity (92.2 % and 96 %, respectively).
A β-GalNAc-PEG3-azide (S5) was successfully synthesized in three steps in an overall
yield of 8.2 %. Two siRNA-GalNAc conjugates were successfully produced using copper(I)-
catalyzed alkyne-azide cycloaddition (CuAAC) between α- and β-GalNAc-PEG3-azide
and KAS40. α-GalNAc-KAS40 was obtained in high purity (97 %) after IE-HPLC, while
β-GalNAc-KAS40 remains to be puriﬁed. Both conjugates remain to be tested in human
hepatocytes cell lines.
A novel bis-functional phosphoramidte S2 was successfully synthesized in two steps and
obtained in moderate yield (36 % over two steps) after optimization of reaction - and
workup conditions. Next, the aim was to couple S2 to KAS44 and KAS45 and install a
hexynoic acid moiety for subsequent attachment of azide-based biomolecules, notably the
Tat peptide. The ﬁrst attempt to couple S2 to KAS44 and KAS45 was unfortunately
not successful, and plans have been put forward to ﬁnish this part in near future.

Chapter 4
Outlook
For future perspectives RNA sequences should be synthesized with DMT-oﬀmode in
solid-phase oligonucleotide synthesis in order to purify on IE-HPLC. Alternatively RP-
HPLC should be considered.
To complete the siRNA-GalNAc conjugate set of RNA therapeutics targeting lncRNA
(BM450697) and its promoter, KAS41 needs to be clicked with both α- and β-GalNAc-
PEG3-azide like KAS40. These siRNA-GalNAc conjugates can afterwards be paired
with their antisense strands to have two full sets of siRNA and send to test in human
hepatocyte cell lines. Lastly, it would be interesting to synthesize KAS44 and KAS45
again to repeat the coupling of S2 and repeat the solid-phase approach to attach hexynoic
acid moiety. Initially, this attempt could be proceeded with a DNA sequence instead of
a RNA sequence as a prove of concept.

Cleavage, deprotection and TBDMS removal
Once synthesis of a RNA sequence was completed, the column was dried and emptied
into a PCR-clean vial (RNAse and DNase free) together with ﬁlters and incubated with
1 ml methylamine (33% in ethanol) at 65◦C for 2 hours. The mixture was cooled down
at rt for 30 min and left i fridge for further 30 min. The mixture was ﬁltered into 2 ml
PCR-clean Eppendorf tube where solid support and ﬁlters were washed with 1 ml of
50:50 96% ethanol and MilliQ water. The solution was spin down and evaporated with a
speed-vac and vortex every hour until pellet was dry.
After cleavage and deprotection of phosphate and nucleobases, TBDMS was removed by
adding 100 µl desilylation reagent (750 µl N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone, 375 µl triethylamine,
500 µl triethylamine trihydroﬂuorid) to the dry pellet and put on thermomixer at 67
◦C for 3-4 h. Then 10 µl quenching reagent (1000 µl 3 M NaOAc and 1 µl dietyl
pyrocarbonate) was added, followed by addition of 15 µl 5 M NaClO4 and µl 3 M NaOAc.
Cold acetone (1 ml) was added to precipitate the oligo and was centrifuged at 1000
rpm in 10 min, 4 ◦C and the supernatant removed from the pellet and another ml cold
acetone was added, centrifuged and supernatant removed. The wash was repeated 3
times in total and after the last wash, the pellet was dried on a speed-vac and dissolved

5.4
Procedure of solid-phase modiﬁcations of
oligonucleotides
The solid-phase procedure of hexynoic acid to modiﬁed KAS44 and KAS45 was inspired
by the literature [49]. KAS44 and KAS45 were kept on column to make the amide
coupling and washes more eﬃcient. Initially the column was added a 6 ml solution of
20% diethylamine (DEA) in dry acetonitrile and shaken for 5 min at rt. The resin was
washed with dry acetonitrile (3x5ml) and DMF (2x1ml).
Deprotection of Fmoc was carried out by adding a solution of 20% piperidine in dry
DMF (1 ml, rt, 20 min + 60min) to the resin. The liquid phase was removed from the
resin and absorbance measured at 301 nm. (100µl in 900µl DMF, zeroing the instrument
with DMF). The resin was washed successively with DMF (2x1ml) and ethanol (2x1ml).
Fmoc protected amino acid (30 eq., 3.3 mg), HATU (30 eq., 11.4 mg) and DIPEA
(60 eq., 7.8 mg) were mixed in anhydrous DMF (500 µl) and added to the resin. The
resulting suspension was shaken for 3 h at rt and mixed every 10 min in 5 min. The
liquid phase was removed and the solid support washed with anhydrous DMF (4x0.5
ml) and ethanol (2x1ml). The column was dried was cleaved from solid-support and
deprotected as described above.

5.5
Procedure for click reaction of α- and β-GalNAc to
si5 sense strand
The bioconjugation of α- and β-GalNAc-PEG3 azide was proceed as described in the
literature [51]. The α-GalNAc-PEG3 azide was bought at Sigma and β-GalNAc-PEG3
azide synthesised as S5. Both of them were made as stock solutions 5ng/50µl. To a 1.5
ml PCR-clean Eppendorf tube, 95 µl fresh MiliQ water is added followed by addition
of alkyne modiﬁed oligo (KAS40, > 80% purity) 12 µl from a 20 nmol stock solution.
The oligo solution is vortexed and spin down before use. Then 7.6 µl GalNAc-PEG3-
azide was added from the stock solution, though it should only be necessary to use 200
nmol, but 2000 nmol was added in this case to ensure reaction. Afterwards, 20 µl 1M
trietylammonium acetate (TEAA) is added as a buﬀer to stabilize pH ∼7, followed by
4 µl aminoguanidine (50 mM) and then 47.4 µl extra MiliQ water followed by degas
with argon ﬂow. 10 µl of the copper II reagent (CuTHPTA, 10 mM) was added, degas,
addition of 4 µl ascorbic acid (50 mM) and ﬁnalized with degas before closing the lid
and wrapped in paraﬁlm. The reaction was stirred for 24 h.
Workup
After reaction the concentration was measured and IE-HPLC run to see how the reaction
went (IE-HPLC only made for α-GalNAc-KAS40). Then the reaction was napped on
NAP-5 columns to remove salts and other impurities that can be removed by NAP.
NAP-5 was prepared by letting the buﬀer run through, add fresh MiliQ water (x 2) to
the edge of the coulumn, load the reaction mixture (200 µl) and let it pass through the
column. The Eppendorf tube is washed with 300 µl MiliQ and added to the column
and let that run through (Max load of column is 0.5 µl). To elute the reaction, 1 ml of
MiliQ water is added and eluent collected in new PCR-clean Eppendorf tube to obtain a
sample without salt.
