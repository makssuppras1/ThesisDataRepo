Abstract
It is well known that moving coil loudspeakers contain nonlinearities in the mechanical
system. These effects have previously been modelled by making the loudspeaker parameters
position dependent. It has been shown that there is also a level dependence, which can be
modelled by including hysteresis in the suspension of the loudspeaker model. In this report
the hysteresis is investigated, and many different measurement methods are used.
One measurement method was estimating static nonlinearities for the same loudspeaker
with different input signals. These estimated nonlinearities vary a lot, meaning that a static
nonlinearities are not a good model. Another measurement consist of measuring the level of
the harmonics generated by the nonlinearities over frequency and input level. The slopes of
the levels of the harmonics are not as expected for static nonlinearities.
Increased distortion is also measured when adding extra zero crossings in the velocity.
This increase in distortion is also measured at very low levels, meaning it has influence in
the normal use of loudspeakers. The hysteresis is also found to be a dominant distortion
mechanism in the frequency range above the resonance and up to a few hundred hertz,
meaning that further understanding of this effect, and how to avoid it, can improve the
linearity of loudspeakers.

Introduction
A loudspeaker is a device that transforms a signal from the electrical domain to the
acoustical domain. The objective is to take an electrical source, such as music signal, and
reproduce it in the acoustical domain. This transfer should preferably be completely one to
one, so that the signal is not distorted in any way.
A loudspeaker is designed in such a way that a mechanical system is excited by a force
produced by an electrical system. The mass, stiffness and damping of the mechanical sys-
tem can then be tuned to obtain the best transfer from electrical to acoustical domain, for a
given frequency range. measurements have previously shown that the mechanical system is
not very well described by a linear model. To make accurate predictions of the loudspeaker
response nonlinear models are needed. It has previously been shown in [1] that there is
position dependency in the parameters of the loudspeaker. In [2] the level dependence of the
parameters is investigated, and a model for the suspension is introduced. The model from
this study includes hysteresis, and in [3] measurements and simulations showed that this
model is capable of reproducing the level dependent behavior. These measurements relied
on finding the impedance of the loudspeaker at varying input levels, and the hysteresis model
showed similar changes to the loudspeaker impedance when the input level was changed.
In this report further measurements are made to show, that there is indeed hysteresis
in the suspension of the loudspeaker. In the previous study [3], the effects of including the
hysteresis element in the model were looked at from a level dependent impedance point of
view. In this report, care is taken to show that some of the effects seen in the time domain
measurements can only reasonably be explained by hysteresis, and not static nonlinearities
such as position or velocity dependent parameters. This is done through several measure-
ment methods. One of the measurement methods that will be used is estimation of the
static nonlinearities needed to reproduce a measurement, and then comparing these nonlin-
earties across measurements made on the same loudspeaker. Another method is looking at
the amount of distortion as a function of the input level. Together all these measurements
points to memory effects or hysteresis as the only real possible explanation, as many different
static nonlinearites are needed to model the same exact loudspeaker, when responding to
different stimuli.
As the suspension is the subject of interest, most of the measurements will be made at
frequencies below the resonance of the loudspeaker, where the influence of the suspension
nonlinearities should be the largest. Purifi Audio has supplied the drivers and measurement
setup used in this study. The drivers have been designed to have very low position depen-
dence in the parameters, so the distortion from the memory effects in the suspension should
be as easy as possible to see. At low frequencies the influence of electrical hysteresis should
be minimal, and care will be taken to make sure that any hysteresis measured is in the me-
chanical system, and not the electrical hysteresis. Three different kinds of rubber surrounds
will be tested, to see if the hysteresis is material dependent. In the end a measurement of the

Theory
In this section the theory needed to interpret the rest of this report is presented. A brief
overview of the standard lumped parameter model of a moving coil loudspeaker is given, as
well as how it can be modified to include nonlinear effects. Then two models of hysteresis
will be introduced. Next derivations for static nonlinear systems will be performed. If it
can be proven that the loudspeaker is not part of these systems, then some kind of memory
effect, such as hysteresis is needed. Lastly the setup for doing simulations of the loudspeaker
model with hysteresis will be derived and explained.
2.1
Lumped parameter model of loudspeaker
The standard linear model of a moving coil loudspeaker is the lumped parameter model.
This model is shown in figure 1. The input to the model is the voltage across the speaker
terminals, eg. The model consists of a mechanical system, which is a single degree of freedom
resonator, and an electrical system. The mechanical resonator consists of a moving mass
Mms, a linear damper Rms and a spring Cms.
These represent the moving mass of the
loudspeaker, the mechanical damping and the stiffness of the surround and spider. The
mass is accelerated by a force generated by the electrical part of the loudspeaker model. The
electrical model is a resistor Re, that models the resistance of the voice coil in series with an
inductor Le, that models the inductance of the voice coil. The current that flows through the
voice coil generates a force that accelerates the moving mass of the loudspeaker. The force
is proportional to the current, and the proportionality constant is called the force factor Bl.
The force factor is also used to find the back EMF from the movement of the voice coil.

To take nonlinear effects in the loudspeaker into account, the parameters of the model
can be made position dependent[1]. Usually the position dependent parameters are, the
compliance Cms(x), the inductance Le(x) and the force factor Bl(x). The position depen-
dent parameters causes nonlinear effects, as the position is dependent on the input voltage,
and the position dependent parameters are multiplied with voltage. This means that the
position dependent model is multiplying the input with itself, creating nonlinear distortion.
The position dependent parameters can be thought of as static nonlinearities. A static poly-
nomial nonlinearity of order n generates harmonics up to the order n. However due to the
feedback in the system, from the back EMF generated by the movement of the voice coil,
the harmonics generated by the polynomial nonlinearity are fed back into the nonlinearity,
creating even higher order harmonics. This is the reason why a loudspeaker model with low
order polynomial position dependence can generate very high order harmonics.

2.4
Geometrical interpretation of classical preisach model
The classical preisach model can be interpretted geometrically. As the parameters of
the model is the weight of each hysteron, the weighting function can be plotted in the (α, β)
plane, for interpretation. This is shown in figure 5. The weighting function is zero below
the line α = β, as α < β means there is no hysteresis. When the input increases to a value
of u(t) = a, any point in the plane, which is a hysteron with thresholds (α, β), that is below
the horizontal line α = a is turned on. The hysterons above the horizontal line stay in the
state they are in. When the input decreases to a value of u(t) = b every hysteron to the
right of the vertical line β = b is turned off. The output of the model is then the sum of the
weighting function in the area where the hysterons are on, minus the sum of the weighting
function where the hysterons are off. This is illustrated in figure 6.
The values of the weighting function can be found, by increasing the input to a value,
and then decreasing it again. This is achieved by realizing that increasing the input to a
value and then decreasing to another value makes a certain part of the weighting function
contribute positively to the output, and another part contributes negatively. By putting
the system in a state, where the on and off areas of the weighting function is known, the
weighting function can be sampled, by taking the difference between two outputs. In figure
6, the integral of the weighting function in the area that is the difference between the red
and blue outline can be found as the difference between the response at those two points in
time, as all the hysterons outside the outlined areas are in the same state at both moments
in time. In this way the weighting function can be sampled, and the resolution on the α
axis is given by the difference between the peaks of the triangular signal used to sample the

2.7
Envelope of harmonics
2.7
Envelope of harmonics
To quantify distortion, the harmonics generated by the nonlinearity can be examined.
The level of the harmonics is what is typically used for distortion measurements with for
example Total Harmonic Distortion (THD). In this report the use of first, second and third
harmonic is meant as respectively 1f0 , 2f0 and 3f0. This is clarified, as sometimes the first
harmonic is interpreted as 2f0. This means that the n’th harmonic has a frequency of nf0,
and this definition is what will be used for the rest of the report. This also means that the
fundamental is the same as the first harmonic.
One method of measurement is to present the loudspeaker with an amplitude modulated
signal. An example of an amplitude modulated signal could be a tone burst. Here the tone is
modulated by a smooth window function, such as a Hann window. To extract the envelope of
the harmonics the analytical signal version of the output is used. The process for getting the
analytical signal is to fourier transform the signal and then zero all the negative frequencies.
When the resulting spectrum is transformed back to the time domain, it is the complex
analytical signal. The magnitude of the analytical signal sa(t) is called the envelope and
the phase of the signal is called the instantaneous phase, see equation 27. ˆs(t) is the hilbert
transform of the signal s(t), and i is the imaginary unit.

To extract the envelope of each harmonic the spectrum of the analytical signal is shifted,

resulting signal is transformed to the time domain. This demodulates the signal, resulting
in an analytical signal for each harmonic of interest. This is done for each of the harmonics
of interest as well as the fundamental frequency. The magnitude of the analytical signals
for each of the harmonics is then the envelope of the given harmonic. These envelopes can
then be plotted in a log-log plot. The envelope of the input is plotted on the x-axis, and
the relative envelope of each harmonic can be plotted on the y-axis. The relative envelope is
the magnitude of the analytical signal for each of the harmonics, divided by the magnitude
of the input envelope. For a static nonlinearity it is expected that the relative amplitudes
of the harmonics increase with level. This increase is expected to be powers of the input
amplitude. For example it is expected that the relative amplitude of the third harmonic
grows with the input amplitude squared, as the absolute magnitude grows with the input
cubed. In a log-log plot of the relative amplitude, this results in a straight line with a slope
of 2.

3.1
Differences between Preisach and frictional element models
The main difference, that has been found, between the preisach and the frictional ele-
ment model, is the way the memory in the models work. The memory in the preisach model
works purely on the input signal. This is clear from the description given in equation 14 and
15, where the output of the preisach model is only dependent on the input. In equation 4
it can be seen that the output of the frictional model is dependent on δ and δin. These are
defined in equations 6 and 7, and in both of these the force is part of the definition, which
means that the output of the frictional element model is dependent on both the input and
output. This means that if presented with a pure tone, the response from the preisach model
is the same for the first cycle of the pure tone as for the last. This is because even though
the response is dependent on the original state of the hysterons, the input only changes the
state of the same hysterons during each cycle, so the response to each cycle is the same.
In the frictional model the memory variable is the force at the last change of direction. As
the input to the model is the displacement, and force the output signal, the memory of the
frictional element model works on the output. This causes the model to go through some
settling when presented with a pure tone, as each time the input changes direction the force
is slightly different, as it is dependent on the force at the last change of direction.
In order to explore the effects of this adaption over time, a test signal that consists of
a pulse with a tone on top can be generated. The idea was that if the loudspeaker could be
modelled by the classical preisach model, then the response at the end of the pulse should be
the same no matter if the tone is there or not. However if the frictional element is a better
model, then the tone on top of the pulse causes the settling of the frictional element, whereas
this settling would not happen with no tone present. This settling happens per reversal of the
direction. Adding the tone should allow the infinite impulse response-like frictional element
to settle around the top of the pulse. When the tone is not present, only a single reversal
of direction happens during the whole pulse. The difference of settling or not should then
cause a difference in the response of the loudspeaker at the end of the pulse. The start of
the signal is a negative pulse. This is included to bring the preisach model into a known
state. The input signal is shown in figure 13. The weighting function used in the preisach
model is obtained by simulating first order reversal curves on the frictional element model.
This ensures that the two models perform as close as possible to each other. The output
of each of the models can be seen in figure 14. This figure clearly illustrates the difference
between the models. The preisach model is only dependent on the input signal, whereas the
frictional model depends on the input and the output at the last change of direction in the
input. This dependence on the output causes the settling, which is not seen in the preisach
model.

3.3
Simulation of speaker with third order nonlinear stiffness
3.3
Simulation of speaker with third order nonlinear stiffness
To show the level of the harmonics over frequency some simulations were performed. In
these simulations a speaker with a single nonlinearity was simulated. The nonlinearity is an
even nonlinearity in the stiffness of the speaker, and the rest of the speaker is completely
linear. This even nonlinearity generates odd order harmonics. This simulation is done to
show how the level of the harmonics in the current drops off as the frequency is increased.
In figure 19 the level of the harmonics in the simulated current is plotted over frequency. In
the plot the resonance of the loudspeaker can be seen in the first harmonic as a dip where
the impedance has a peak. Towards high frequencies the slope of the third harmonic is 42
dB per octave, or 140 dB per decade. This is due to the second order lowpass from the
voltage to position transfer function. This second order lowpass is then cubed to generate
the third harmonic in the force, which means the rolloff in force is 3 times steeper. Then
the force passes through the force to velocity transfer function which is a second order
bandpass. Above the resonance the second order bandpass filter has a slope of 20 dB per
decade, so overall the total slope of the third harmonic is 140 dB per decade. This velocity
then generates back EMF which modulates the current, causing the harmonics to show
up in the current. The filtering is shown in figure 20. Note that for each power of s in
the denominator the frequency drops off with 20 dB per decade or 6 dB per octave. The
simulation was repeated with half the amount of linear stiffness and damping, to show that
the level of the harmonics is the same, and only the resonance frequency drops. This is due
to upper asymptotes of the filtering being controlled by the mass. The resonance can be
seen as a dip in the first harmonic in the current. This simulation is also shown in 19. Due
to the feedback in the system a fifth harmonic is generated. This fifth harmonic drops off
even faster over frequency, as it is generated by the input to a higher power, meaning the
dropoff is 220 dB per decade. As we shall see in a later chapter some loudspeakers exhibit
very different slopes of the harmonics over frequency. This is done through measurements,
and the results are quite different from these simulations, where the slope increases greatly
for higher harmonics.

4.1
Measurement setup
To perform the measurements for this report a measurement setup at Purifi Audio has
been used. During a measurement on a speaker, the current and voltage through and across
the speaker terminals is measured. The displacement of the speaker diaphragm is measured
with a displacement laser. The data obtained from these 3 measurement signals is used to fit
a position dependent generalized inductance Lgen(x) as well as a position dependent force
factor Bl(x) [8]. The force on the diaphragm is then computed from the measured current
as well as the fitted position dependent parameters, evaluated at the position determined by
the laser. In this way the force in each time step can be computed.
4.2
Test signals
4.2.1
Tone burst
The tone burst measurement signal is seen below. It is a pure tone with a smooth
window function applied. The idea behind using a tone burst, instead of a pure tone, was to
minimize the high frequency content that is generated at the start and end of a pure tone, due
to the abrupt start and end of the signal. By smoothly turning up the amplitude of the tone,
the transients are minimized. A single period from the middle of the tone burst can then be
used to plot the hysteresis-loop in a position-force plot. The way this signal is generated,
by modulating a pure tone with a smooth window fucntion, is identical to the amplitude
modulated pure tone. However the reason for amplitude modulating is different. The idea
with the tone burst is to minimize the transients, whereas in the amplitude modulation is
done to estimate the amount of distortion at different levels. Therefore the amount of periods
of the tone that are contained in the window is very different. The tone burst has less than
50 periods in a single burst, and the amplitude modulation has a lot more periods. The
amount of periods can be seen as increasing the resolution of the level dependent distortion
measurement, as the system spends more time at each input level. A plot of a tone burst is
shown in figure 21.

4.4
Measured hysteresis loops and fitted nonlinearities
The idea in the following section is to fit static nonlinearities to measurements made
on the same loudspeaker. If the assumption that the loudspeaker can be described by static
nonlinearities are correct, then all the fitted static nonlinearities should be similar. Different
kinds of variation in the input signal was measured. First the difference between a pure tone,
and a tone with an added third harmonic is explored. Then the phase of the third harmonic
is shifted, and finally different amplitudes of are investigated.
4.4.1
Measurement with and without subloops
The loudspeaker was measured with a signal consisting of a pure tone f0 with and
without an added harmonic at three times the fundamental frequency f0. The resulting force
and displacement was measured, and a static nonlinearity has been fit to each measurement,
using a least squares procedure. The measured hysteresisloops can be seen in figure 26, along
with the loops produced by the estimated static nonlinearties. From this figure it is again
visible, that the third harmonic adds a subloop to the hysteresisloop. To show that these
loops are produced by hysteresis and not static nonlinearities, both the damping and the
stiffness of the loudspeaker has been assumed to be static nonlinearities. If the loudspeaker
suspension is a static nonlinearity then the fitted nonlinearties for each measurement should
be close to identical. However the fitted nonlinearities are very different for the measurement
with and without a subloop. The resulting nonlinearities can be seen in figure 27. It is clear
that the fitted Kms(x) is similar between the two measurements. However the fitted Rms(u)
is clearly different. The Rms(u) from the measurement with the subloop looks more linear
than without the subloop.
Discontinuties at x = 0 and u = 0 were also fitted. These are shown in figure 28, where
the forces generated by the nonlinearities are plotted over displacement and velocity. A big
difference in the forces from the velocity nonlinearity is that the sign of the discontinuity at
u = 0 flips when the third harmonic is added. A discontinuity in the damping nonlinearity
Rms(u) also seems to be an indicator of hysteresis. The frictional element model that was
previously used was sensitive to changes in direction. A change in direction happens when
the u = 0, so the presence of this discontinuity can be seen as a sign of hysteresis.
To show the clear difference between the identified nonlinearities, the estimated non-
linearities has been used to compute the output of the other measurement, by putting the
velocity and position through the estimated polynomial nonlinearities. These estimates are
shown in figure 29, where it is clear that these static nonlinearities are not able to explain
the measurements. If the nonlinearity in a loudspeaker is static, then it should be able to
explain the response to all types of input, without changing the nonlinearity. This is not the
case for these measurements.

4.6
Measurement with series resistance
To test the hypothesis that the slowly growing harmonics observed in the amplitude
modulated measurements are from the mechanical system, a series resistance is added to the
loudspeaker. The idea is that with a large series resistance for example 10 times Re, the
current through the voice coil is controlled by the resistance, and not the magnetic system,
which can have magnetic hysteresis. To get the same displacement of the loudspeaker, a
much larger voltage is needed across the series resistance and the loudspeaker. To make the
measurements comparable, the amplitude of the first harmonic of the current is used for the
log-log plot. As the current is comparable, the force on the diaphragm should be about the
same for both measurements. The transfer function and Q values of the loudspeaker changes
when the series resistance is increased, but these effects are largest close to resonance.
It is expected that the harmonics in the position stay at the same level, and that the
harmonics in the current decrease in level, when the resistance is increased. This is because
the increased series resistance means that the back EMF from the movement of the voice
coil has less influence on the current. This is shown in equation 45.

DISCUSSION
5
Discussion
In this report a lot of measurements on loudspeakers have been presented. All these
measurements have been made to show that the observed behavior cannot be explained by
a static nonlinearity, and that the system needs some kind of memory. This memory means
that there is hysteresis in the system.
In the previous study on this subject [3], measurements of the loudspeaker impedance
were taken at varying input levels. This study showed that the changes in the loudspeaker
impedance could be explained by introducing hysteresis in the model of the suspension of the
loudspeaker. This previous study was not concerned with investigating whether hysteresis
was present, it only concluded that the time averaged effects, which an impedance measure-
ment is, could be explained by introducing these memory effects in the suspension. In this
report one of the main goals has been to show that there is hysteresis in the loudspeaker
suspension. This was done by taking a lot of measurements, that together point to hysteresis
being the only likely explanation.
A time domain measurement has been presented, which shows that the slope of the
velocity changes quite a lot when the force has a reversal of direction. This cannot be ex-
plained by a static nonlinearity, as the nonlinearity does not "know" which direction the
force is applied, only the magnitude of the force. Some kind of memory would be needed in
the system to explain this behavior.
To further examine this, several measurements were made on a loudspeaker, and for
each measurement static nonlinearities were estimated. These estimates varied a lot even
though all the measurements were made on the same loudspeaker. The fact that the static
nonlinearity changes based on the input means that it is not a static linearity at all. The
nonlienarities that were fitted to the measurements were Kms(x) and Rms(u). The same
measurements have been fitted with Kms(x), Rms(u) and Rms(x).
These results can be
found in appendix A. The results are of course not the same, as there are more parameters
to fit, but the overall interpretation is the same, as the same speaker has different position
and velocity dependant parameters depending on the type and amplitude of the input.
To further support the view that there is hysteresis in the suspension of moving coil
loudspeakers, the amplitude modulated measurements were made. The amplitude modu-
lated pure tone showed that the harmonics generated by the nonlinearity did not grow as
quickly with the input level, as would be expected for static nonlinearties. This is further
evidence that a nonstatic nonlinearity is needed to model the loudspeaker. It also showed
that some kinds of rubber surrounds generate more distortion than others. The amount
of distortion measured for the speaker with an IIR rubber surround was about double the
amount measured for the SBR70 and NRB70 rubber. This might be because the damping
seen in the rubber is actually nonlinear damping. More tests are needed, but it might be the
case that a rubber surround with less damping also has less distortion, due to the observed
Page 57
==PAGE:62==
5
DISCUSSION
damping being generated by a nonlinear mechanism.
The measurements with an amplitude modulated third harmonic shows that the growth
over amplitude of the harmonics is dependent on at which level an extra zero crossing in
the velocity is introduced. As the absolute level of the input signal was changed, so was
the level at which the harmonics started to grow. This points to hysteresis as the expla-
nation, as hysteresis is sensitive to changes in direction, and not the absolute level of the
signal. There is a very clear phase shift in the harmonics at amplitudes just above the thresh-
old for an extra reversal. This effect was measured, with the extra reversal at less than 0.1 V.
To show that the harmonics that were measured in all the measurements were in fact
from the mechanical system, a measurement with and without series resistance was pre-
sented. The hypothesis was that if the harmonics were generated by the mechanical system
then they should not change when a change was made to the system on the electrical side.
However as the harmonics on the mechanical side of the loudspeaker show up in the current,
because of the coupling of the mechanical and electrical systems, the amount of harmonics in
the current should be dependent on the electrical system. The added series resistance should
help suppress the current harmonics that are caused by the back EMF from the movement
of the voice coil. As the input voltage was increased when measuring with a series resistance,
the current in the voice coil was about the same magnitude. This means that the force on
the diaphragm is about the same, making the measurements comparable. The same force on
the mechanical side should generate the same amount of distortion, if the mechanical system
is the source of the distortion. This is exactly what was seen in the measurement. Another
point to be made here, is that the loudspeaker can be used as a sensor. The movement of
the voice coil produces back EMF, which changes the current. This change in current can
be used to measure the distortion on the mechanical side, provided a good model of the
magnetic system is known. The speakers that have been used in this study have very low
position dependence in the parameters, meaning this method can be used to measure the
mechanical distortion.
As all the previous measurements were made at frequencies below the resonance, any
distortion found here is not that worrying, as speakers are mostly used for sound reproduc-
tion above the resonance frequency. The reasoning for doing the measurements at these
frequencies is that it was speculated that the hysteresis would be easier to see in measure-
ments, as the suspension has the most influence below the resonance. A measurement of the
level of the higher harmonics was made as a function of frequency. This measurement shows
that the slope of the harmonics over frequency is less than expected, and the slope of the
third and fifth harmonic is very close to identical. This slow dropoff over frequency indicates
that the harmonics are generated by hysteresis. Especially the fact that the slope of the fifth
and the third harmonic is the same, and that this slope is lower than expected for a third
order nonlinearity. As the surround of the speaker was gradually cut away, the level of the
harmonics decreased. This happened along with a decrease in the resonance frequency, due
to more of the surround being cut way, meaning the stiffness and damping was decreased.
The same decrease in resonance frequency was seen in the simulation with decreased stiffness
Page 58
==PAGE:63==

and damping, however in the simulation the level of the harmonics stayed the same. The
decrease in distortion as the surround is removed points to the surround being the source of
the distortion.
Based on these measurements it is believed that hysteresis in the suspension of the
loudspeaker is a dominant distortion mechanism above the resonance, and up to a few hun-
dred hertz. This is within the range of frequencies that the loudspeaker is typically used to
reproduce, meaning that a removal of this hysteresis distortion could improve the linearity
of loudspeaker, in the range where it is typically used.
It has been observed that the distortion decreases with frequency. This might be be-
cause at high frequencies the movement is so fast that the rubber never settles into a resting
position, meaning there is no dry friction at each change of direction. Another possible ex-
planation is that there is a minimum threshold of movement needed to see the dry friction,
and at high frequencies the displacement is very small.
As this study shows that hysteresis is present in the suspension, the next step is to
model it. Other studies have found hysteresis in rubber, such as [10], where a model similar
to the fictional element is proposed. The two different models of hysteresis used in this report
have both been used for simulations. In the previous study [3], the numerical problems with
the frictional element model was explained.
The computation time was high, as a very
high sampling frequency is needed to accurately describe the nonlinear dynamics. In this
report the preisach model was investigated, and when the weighting function obtained from
the frictional model was used, this model too had some numerical issues. As the weighting
function has very high values in some locations, these hysterons switching on and off causes
a lot of noise, which is not present in the real world. Therefore a better model of hysteresis
in loudspeakers is needed. It was also not investigated which type of memory is the best
to model loudspeakers. The frictional element has a dependence on both input and output,
whereas the preisach only cares about the input.

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
In this report hysteresis in loudspeaker suspensions has been investigated. It has been
found that the suspension contains a memory effect, which leads to hysteresis distortion.
This conclusion is supported by many measurements, that each show that static nonlinear-
ities such as position or velocity dependence cannot explain the measured behavior. Time
domain measurements show clear changes in the response, when there are zero crossings in
the velocity and force. These measurents were mostly carried out beneath the resonance
frequency of the loudspeaker, as it is believed that they have more influence here, where the
suspension is dominating the response. A measurement of the level of the current harmonics
showed that the slope over frequency was not as expected for a static nonlinearty. The slope
was much lower than expected, and very similar for different order harmonics. Furthermore,
as the surround of the loudspeaker was cut way, the level of the distortion decreased. This
indicates that the distortion was generated by the surround, a conclusion that is also sup-
ported by the measurement with increased series resistance. It is believed that above the
resonance and below a few hundred hertz the hysteresis effect in the suspension is a dominant
distortion mechanism. The hysteresis was measured at input voltage levels of less than 0.1
V, which shows that this distortion matters at low levels as well. This means that unlike
position dependent distortion, the problem cannot be avoided by driving the speaker at a
low level. As the distortion is present above the resonance, it also affects the frequency range
where the speaker is used for sound reproduction, meaning that knowledge of this hysteresis
effect can help design more linear loudspeakers.
The hysteresis effect was found to be somewhat material dependent, and the trend
suggests that rubber with lots of damping has more distortion.
This was not examined
thoroughly, but a few measurements were presented that showed that IIR rubber performed
worse than sbr70 and nbr70 rubber.
Two models of hysteresis have been investigated in this report.
Both models have
numerical problems, meaning they might not be suitable to model this hysteresis. Another
problem that has yet to be solved is to figure out which kind of memory is best for modelling
loudspeaker hysteresis. The frictional element model has a settling effect, which is not seen
in the preisach model, however it has not been possible to measure which kind of hysteresis is
present in the loudspeaker, as the focus has been on proving the existence of hysteresis in the
suspension, and showing that it has an effect in the frequency range where the loudspeaker
is used.

FUTURE WORK
7
Future Work
• Find a good model for the hysteresis in the suspension.
The models presented in
this report have different properties, and it has not been investigated which model is
best for modelling the loudspeaker. Both models have numerical problems, so another
model might be preferable.
• Construct a mathematical proof that a static nonlinearity cannot produce the slow
growth of the relative level of the harmonics.
• Look into the material properties of rubber. Is the filler material in the rubber the
cause of the hysteresis? Find ways to minimize the influence of the hysteresis distortion,
in the frequency range where the loudspeaker is used for sound reproduction.
• Investigate why the hysteresis effect seems to dissappear above a certain frequency. Is
there a minimal amount of movement needed to excite the effect, or is there a frequency
dependence, due to the material in the suspension never returning to a rest position if
the movement is fast enough?
