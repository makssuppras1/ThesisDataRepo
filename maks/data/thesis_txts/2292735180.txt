Abstract
This thesis covers the development of a vision assisted navigation system with fault detection for an
Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) with autonomous vertical take off and landing capabilities. The sensor
fusion of the navigation system is expanded by adding a sonar range ﬁnder and an off-board camera sensor.
The vision system is implemented on an external computer that with wireless communication sends
position estimates to the UAV. The position is determined with computer vision algorithms that ﬁlter out and
identiﬁes colored markers placed on the UAV.
The fault detection covers faults in altitude measurements from the barometer, sonar and camera, where
a bank of Extended Kalman Filters (EKF) produces residuals from the estimated altitudes. The residuals are
fed to three GLRT detectors that detect the faults and reconﬁgures the main EKF to disable the faulty sensor.
A vertical take-off and landing procedure with setpoint control is implemented on an external computer
and evaluated in a lab environment.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAV), and especially multi rotor vehicles, have become a popular research
platform due to their low cost, high maneuverability and reliability. The small size and agile position control
of multi rotors make them suitable for rescue or surveillance missions in places where humans cannot
operate due to limited space or hazardous environments.
1.1
Motivation
Although a UAV is considered a reliable ﬂight system, faults may occur as a result of external disturbances
like wind or rain, or internal errors in the hardware/software structure of the navigation system and its
sensors. Faults can be more or less critical, with the worst cases resulting in a crash of the vehicle that could
cause personal and material damage.
A robust fault detection system must have redundant sensors that rapidly can replace the faulty ones.
Since UAVs are battery powered, the on-board sensing capabilities will limit the ﬂight time as more weight
is added to the unit. The ﬂight time can be maximized by applying redundant sensors to an external vehicle
with its own processing capabilities and power source.
To reduce faults caused by human errors, a multi rotor can operate in autonomous mode where it receives
off-board commands in the form of pre-planned mission waypoints or real time control of its position and
attitude.
1.2
Problem Statement
To attain a reliably working UAV that will not crash when a fault occurs it is necessary to detect faults
and isolate them as quickly as possible. Three ofﬂine GLRT detectors are implemented that can detect
whenever one of the height sensors fails. The residuals are formed from three Extended Kalman Filters
implemented on an offboard computer.
In order to move weight and processing power away from the UAV, an off-board vision sensing system is
developed and implemented on an external computer. The off-board computer handles the fault detection
and computer vision algorithms and sends the estimates and fault signals back to the UAV through a wireless
connection. In addition, the off-board computer controls the autonomous mode of the multi rotor where
take-off, landing and tracking of the vehicle are the objectives.

1.3
Literature Survey
Fault Detection and Isolation has become a widespread research area and especially fault tolerant control
of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles since these are required to be stable and safe in order to not inﬂict personal and
material damage. Several methods have been proposed in the literature to detect and isolate faults. Fauske
(2008) uses a bank of Kalman ﬁlters to detect and isolate faults by comparing the residuals of the estimates
pairwise. Another usage of a bank of Kalman ﬁlters is in Kobayashi (2003) where it is applied to actuator
fault diagnostics in a propulsion system using a weighted sum of squared residuals (WSSR). By exploiting
hardware redundancy, Brás (2012) is able to detect and isolate faults in inertial measurement units.
Visual tracking systems have been researched extensively with both off-board and on-board cameras
where the objective is to determine the position and heading of the vehicle. Carreira (2013) proposes a
vision-based autonomous control system that is using binary markers that is identiﬁed with an on-board
camera. A trinocular tracking system is proposed by Martínez (2009) where the known positions of three
off-board cameras are used to track a helicopter equipped with color markers. In a similar manner, the
Vicon system uses multiple cameras to precisely determine the position and heading of vehicles in an indoor
environment where Clark (2014) uses a 6-camera setup to control a swarm of quad rotors in a predeﬁned
space. As the Vicon system requires expensive equipment, Achtelik (2009) proposes an off-board stereo
camera setup with low cost web-cameras and color markers on the multi rotor.

Frames of Reference
In order to describe a 6 Degrees of Freedom (DOF) rigid body it is necessary to introduce some frames of
reference: Body-ﬁxed reference B-frame, ground vehicle inertial reference G-frame, camera center C-frame
and the local position NED-frame. The frames follow the right hand rule for orthogonal coordinate systems
and are deﬁned as:
B-frame
The body ﬁxed frame of reference is attached to the center of mass of the UAV with the following
conventions:
• The x axis points in the UAVs forward direction (roll axis)
• The y axis points to the right (pitch axis)
• The z axis points downwards (yaw axis)
G-frame
The reference frame of the ground vehicle is attached to the center of mass of the ground vehicle and is
deﬁned as:
• The x axis points in the Ground Vehicles forward direction
• The y axis points to the left
• The z axis points upwards
C-frame
The camera frame has its origin in the center of the camera and follows the same convention as the
G-frame with an offset rg in the positive direction of the G-frame z-axis.
NED-frame
The local position NED frame is attached to the center of mass of the UAV where the origin is set at its
starting point.
• The x axis points towards the north pole
• The y axis points to the east
• The z axis points downwards to the center of the earth

CHAPTER 3
Visual Tracking System
The Visual Tracking System (VTS) acts as a low rate aiding sensor for the on-board INS. In addition,
the system is used for tracking in order to make the UAV follow the movements of a Ground Vehicle while
keeping a synchronized heading and position.
3.1
Objectives
The requirements of the VTS are to
• Robustly determine the pose of the UAV
• Track the UAV from 1 to at least 5 meters distance
• Operate at a frequency of at least 5Hz
The combination of position and orientation is commonly referred to as the pose of a robot or vehicle. The
position is calculated and transformed from the camera frame to the local NED frame before it is sent to the
UAV. The yaw angle is calculated in radians and is used to synchronize the headings of the two vehicles.
3.2
Visual Markers
Computer vision algorithms can detect shapes, colors or patterns in an image, which in turn can be
used to determine the position of an object. We have evaluated color thresholding as a method for pose
determination.
By mounting colored objects beneath the UAV it is possible to ﬁlter out and detect the position of each
object in an image. Plastic balls of the colors blue, green, purple and red were evaluated by taking images
from 5.5 meters. The images were captured with a smartphone in a resolution of 3264×2448 to ensure a
high level of detail. In order to create an environment where optical noise sources were present, the images
were taken outdoors between a building and a tree. A color threshold ﬁlter was applied in order to separate
the markers from the background by using the MATLAB Color Threshold toolbox. The toolbox and ﬁltering
methods are further explained in Section 3.3.1.
For better visibility and higher resistance to illumination differences as a result of weather changes, a 1W
LED was mounted inside of each marker. The LEDs are powered with a CC/CV (Constant Current/Constant
Voltage) module since they require a reliable power source that is not affected by the voltage drop in
the on-board LiPO battery during operation. The results of the different color thresholds can be seen in
Figures 3.2 (blue, green) and 3.3 (purple, red), with the original image shown in Figure 3.1. The optimal

Color Space
Digital images have a color space where the information in each pixel is divided into channels. The most
used color space is RGB, where R stands for Red, G for green and B for blue, and each channel contains
information about the respective color. Another color space that is commonly used in computer vision and
image analysis is YUV where the color information, or chrominance, is represented in the U and V layers
and the Y layer represents the intensity, or brightness, of the image. Since this application is going to be
==PAGE:40==
18
CHAPTER 3. VISUAL TRACKING SYSTEM
used outdoors where the light conditions are different on a sunny day compared to a cloudy day, the intensity
of light plays an important part. While an RGB image has to be analyzed in each of the three channels,
each with intensity information present, a YUV image has the advantage of the separation between intensity
and colors. This means that a YUV color space is much more tolerant to changes in light and is thus more
appropriate for the outdoor application of the system.
Color Thresholding
Thresholding is a simple form of image segmentation where each pixel value is compared to an upper
and lower threshold in order to isolate an object or other information in a digital image. The thresholds for
ﬁnding the visual markers were as previously mentioned obtained by using the Color Thresholding tool in
MATLAB.
The tool has a graphical user interface that allows for selection of an area in an image that will be
separated from the background. Each channel in the color space has a range of 0-255 that is illustrated along
the x-axis of the histogram. Figure 3.7 shows the output of the tool when the thresholds in Figure 3.8 have
been applied to the original image. While adjusting the thresholds manually and observing the peaks in
the histograms that represents the concentration of pixels with a certain value on the separate channels it is
possible to ﬁnd the appropriate values for the purpose. The found thresholds are applied to each pixel in the

3.3.2
Erode
Erosion of an image is a simple way of removing disturbances such as single pixels that has been left
over from the thresholding. The erosion reduces the size of all regions that are represented by white pixels in
the binary image, resulting in a removal of single pixels and reduction of larger areas of connected pixels
since it erodes from the edges of the areas.
The ﬁlter is using a kernel with a center point, also known as the anchor point, that iterates through
the image pixel by pixel. If the anchor point is completely surrounded by white pixels, the value is left
untouched. However, if any of the pixels in the kernel is black, the anchor point is replaced by a black pixel,
resulting in the eroding effect. The kernel can have any shape, such as cross or star shaped, depending on the
==PAGE:42==
20

wanted output. The erosion function used in this project is a 3x3 matrix that is causing only a mild eroding
effect and is applied once. If needed though, the erosion can be applied in several iterations for more effect.
As can be seen in Figure 3.10, the number of iterations has a substantial effect on the outcome of the erosion.
The original image is heavily distorted by solar glares hitting at an angle that creates an area inside the
marker that the threshold cannot remove. By analyzing the results from a set of 10 images it was concluded
that one iteration is sufﬁcient for removal of small noise sources and that two iterations was removing too
much of the area inside the markers.

The erosion is as previously stated making collections of white pixels in the image shrink in size. In order

kernel. The ﬁnal output of the erode and dilate ﬁlters is shown in Figure 3.11 where it can be seen that the
small disturbances has been removed but there are still some black pixels left inside the markers. However,

to a reduction of the ﬁlter stability margins, the ﬁlter makes sure that the variances are positive and the
symmetry of the covariance matrix is withheld between each update.

correct the on-board estimations and provide redundandcy for the fault detection.
The sensor fusion is as previously mentioned performed sequentially, and only when a new measurement
is available. Since the ﬁlter is customised for the hardware, it was decided to keep the structure of the sensor
fusion and expand it with the redundant sensors.

CHAPTER 5
Fault Detection
This chapter describes the development process of a fault detection algorithm that uses a bank of
extended kalman ﬁlters as its residual generator. Tests were performed on real ﬂight data where faults were
injected into the system.
5.1
Objectives
The objectives for this part of the project are the following
• Development of a residual generator from which a fault can be detected
• Development of a robust fault detection scheme that can detect if a fault has occured in one of the
sensors
• Be able to distinguish in which sensor the fault has occurred
• Have as low as possible detection delay and false alarms
5.2
Residual Generation
5.2.1
Bank of Extended Kalman Filters
In order to detect that a fault has occurred in a system, be it a sensor or actuator fault, it is necessary to
have some data which can be analysed and estimate a fault from that. In this case we have a system using a
couple of redundant sensors, and that is especially apparent in the altitude measurements. A barometer, a
sonar and a camera are each used to measure the altitude. Should one of these sensors fail it is essential to be
able to exclude those measurements as fast as possible. The proposed scheme is to have a bank of Extended
Kalman Filters running in parallel and each fusing one aiding sensor (Figure 5.1), in this case a barometer, a
sonar and a camera. Each ﬁlter outputs its own estimate of the altitude ˆzi where i = [1, 2, 3]. Should one of
the sensors fail it is expected that one of the estimations will follow that failure closely. Should the others at
the same time be operational, the difference between the operational and the changed estimation should be
non-zero. These differences are called residuals and take the following form:
r1(t) = ˆz1(t) −ˆz2(t)
r2(t) = ˆz2(t) −ˆz3(t)
r3(t) = ˆz3(t) −ˆz1(t)
35
==PAGE:58==
36
CHAPTER 5. FAULT DETECTION
Each of these ri is then fed into a Generalised Likelihood Ratio Test in order to detect a change from
non-zero(healthy) conditions. There is one detector for one residual. Knowing if one of the residuals have
changed, means being able to identify which sensor had failed, this is called isolating the fault.
Figure 5.1: Diagram showing the bank of EKF’s with the GLRT detectors as well as the decision function
excluding measurements if a sensor had failed
When a fault has been identiﬁed a warning signal wi can be sent to a logical decision function which
in its turn sends a ﬂag to the main EKF on the vehicle with an order to reconﬁgure and exclude the faulty
measurements.
5.2.2
Implementation of the bank of EKFs on an off-board computer
The bank of kalman ﬁlters was initially meant to be implemented directly on the ﬂight controller in order
to maximise the operating frequency and minimise communication delays between sensors and estimators.
This setup had to be reconsidered since early tests showed that each ﬁlter was using up to 20% of the
available processing power. The system overloaded when 4 kalman ﬁlters (main + 3 in bank) were operating
in parallel resulting in a shutdown of high priority tasks such as attitude and position controllers as well as
lower priority tasks like logging and data streaming. The EKF bank was instead implemented as a RHD
plugin on an external computer where each ﬁlter was running in a separate thread. Polling of sensor data was
performed in a thread with a 200Hz update frequency from where each bank could access the data separately
via external functions. The ﬁlters are based on the same architecture as the on-board EKF and were written
in C++ exclusively.
5.2.3
Classiﬁcation of faults
There exist different type of faults which can affect a sensor. Following is a short list of some of the
faults:
• A bias in the sensor after some time or at the beginning of operation
• A slowly drifting and increasing bias
• Loss of accuracy or calibration errors
• A total failure of the sensor
In this project a total failure of the sensor has been considered. A total failure means that the sensor
stops giving out measurements and only gives out a constant zero. This might happen if a sensor has
been disconnected during operation, if some of the components inside the sensor become damaged or
short-circuited in some way etc.

5.3
Statistical Analysis of Residuals
Prior to developing a fault detection algorithm it is essential to determine the statistical behaviour of
the residuals, namely their distributions, in both a faulty and non faulty case. This initial analysis of the
residuals serves as the basis for choosing the type of detector to estimate a fault.
5.3.1
Height Residuals
The type of fault that was chosen for the statistical analysis was a total failure that can affect either of
the available altitude sensors (barometer, sonar or camera). The fault was simulated by multiplying the raw
measurements, before fusing in the EKF, with zero after some time had elapsed thus simulating a complete
failure of a sensor in mid-ﬂight. Three experiments were performed using the indoor test rig as shown in
Figure A.8 in appendix A in order to produce a set of residuals. Figure 5.2 shows the altitude estimations
from the bank of EKF’s and the simulated failure of the barometer occurring at t = tfailure, Figures A.2 and
A.3 in Appendix A show simulated failures of the sonar and camera respectively. The associated residuals
for a barometer failure can be seen in Figure 5.3, and for a sonar and camera failure in Figures A.4 and A.5
in Appendix A, where it is noticed that in the presence of a fault there is a change in the behaviour of two of
the residuals depending on which of the sensors has failed. A barometer failure causes a change in residuals
r1 and r3, whereas a sonar or a camera failure produces changes in r1 and r2 or r2 and r3 respectively. The
part of the residual before the change is denoted the non-faulty or healthy part and the part of the residual
after the change is denoted the faulty part. As previously mentioned and which can be observed from ﬁgures
5.2, A.2 and A.3, only two residuals are affected when a fault occurs in one of the sensors thus enabling
isolability of a fault to a speciﬁc sensor.

Requirements of the detector
The GLRT detector depends on some parameters as seen in algorithm 3, each of these have an inﬂuence
on the probability of detecting a fault and the delay from the time when the fault occurred to the time when
the fault is detected.
• M is the window/array size for the measurement vector Z to be tested.
• M0 and M1 are the array sizes used for estimating ˆθ0 and ˆθ1, they can be of equal size or different
sizes. The choice of the ratio of sizes has an inﬂuence on the decision function gri(Z) as seen in
Figure 5.14.
• W is the amount of new samples to be incorporated into the measurement vector Z at each iteration, it
has an inﬂuence on the detection delay.
• γ is the threshold used to distinguish between a fault and normal operating conditions. It only has an
inﬂuence on the amount of missed or false detections.
These parameters are up to the designer of the detection algorithm to choose and tune based on a set of
requirements. These requirements should ensure that the detector:
• Has a low probability of false alarms, hence a suitable γ should be determined.
• Has a low probability of missed detections
• Has a low detection time, which means that the delay τd between the fault and the detection is as low
as possible.
Selection of M, M0, M1 and W
The window size M should be carefully chosen, if M is too long the detection delay τd will increase
thus not suitable for detecting a fault, especially when it’s a critical failure. By choosing M too low the
designer runs into the problem of not having enough samples to represent the distribution that is assumed to
ﬁt the dataset. A case of choosing M too high is illustrated in Figure 5.14. Although there is a detection, it
is delayed because of the window size M = 600(35.4 s). In this illustrative example there is a threshold γ
deciding on when to switch from H0 to H1, further information on the choice of this threshold can be found
in the next section. If one was to lower the threshold there would be false alarms before and after the fault
has occurred thus making this choice of M undesirable. Choosing M too low will in its turn also increase
the rate of false alarms as seen in Figure 5.15. Not enough samples are saved in the array at each iteration in
order to match a Gaussian distribution. In the two examples it has been assumed and chosen that M1 = M0,
but if one was to choose M0 > M1 then gri(Z) would predominantly take on negative values hence making
the data adhere more to hypothesis H0. If on the other hand M1 > M0 the opposite situation will be true
and the hypothesis H1 would be true. The two situations are illustrated in Figure 5.16. Choosing M0 = M1
would therefore be a suitable choice since gri(Z) = 0.

Discussion of the results
The results presented so far have all been quite similar with similar experimental setup with the UAV
ﬂying at an altitude of 2 meters and experiencing a failure. For lower altitudes or even just after takeoff
the change in the mean of r1(t), r2(t) and r3(t) will be less noticeable and it might be that the detector
will miss the faults. Regarding the detection delays, we deem them acceptable, however since we have not
implemented the detection on the offboard computer we have no way to test if this is true. There also exists
transmission delays, transmission timeouts which would signiﬁcantly delay the alarm to the main EKF thus
allowing for fusion of bad measurements for a period of time. Some improvements can be done in order to
decrease the time delay,for example decreasing the window size W but that means sacriﬁcing the amount
of samples incorporated in each iteration which in turn could not ﬁt the assumed statistical distribution.
It would also increase the computational load since ˆθ0 is time varying. However one might try to keep
==PAGE:86==

ˆθ0 constant which reduces the computational load, this could especially have been done for the sonar and
camera since they don’t experience drift with temperature or ambient pressure. Increasing the sampling rate
of the offboard residual generation which means increasing the sampling rate of the bank of EKF’s could
also lead to a faster detection, however this is limited by the wireless transmission that links the offboard
computer with the UAV.

CHAPTER 6
Vertical Take-Off and Landing
This chapter describes the evaluation of setpoint control for an autonomous Vertical Take Off and
Landing (VTOL) procedure.
6.1
Off-board Control
The attitude and position controllers of the PX4 support off-board commands in the form of setpoints in
position, attitude, velocity or acceleration. When a given setpoint is reached, the controllers try to keep the
position until the setpoint is updated. The setpoints are deﬁned in the local or global NED frame depending
on the purpose of the ﬂight where global GPS setpoints are used for long ﬂights and the local NED frame is
used for shorter autonomous ﬂights. All coordinates and set points in this chapter are described in the local
NED frame as (x, y, z) = (N, E, D) in meters.
The setpoints must be streamed at a rate of at least 2Hz or the PX4 will go into fail safe mode. The fail
safe mode is conﬁgurable to either try to land the vehicle, keep it in the current position or switch to manual
control.
6.1.1
Flight modes
The PX4 has a number of ﬂight modes that are used for different purposes such as position, altitude, and
off-board control. Switching between the ﬂight modes is done in the software or with a physical switch on
the RC-controller that is conﬁgured in QGroundcontrol. We have chosen an approach where a 2-position
switch is assigned to change the manual mode to off-board mode and thereby excluding all other ﬂight modes
since they are not used.
6.2
Implementation
The off-board control software was developed as a plugin for the Mavlink protocol that is connected to
RHD where a function continuously reads the PWM (Pulse-Width Modulation) values from the switches of
the RC-controller in order to enable or disable the off-board mode. The hardware control of the modes is
implemented as a safety measure that enables a quick transition to manual control in case of unexpected
behavior.
The main structure of the off-board VTOL can be seen in Algorithm 5 where a "mission" is illustrated as
an example where the UAV stays at a preset altitude for a certain time t after which it lands on the GV. A
typical mission will have four main parts: Take-off, Tracking, Heading Synchronization and Landing.

6.2.1
Test setup
The VTOL tests were performed in a controlled environment where the UAV was attached to the ceiling
with a string that allowed for emergency shutdown of the vehicle while avoiding a crash. A string was
attached from the ﬂoor to the vehicle, restraining it from ﬂying into the ceiling. In order to be able to work
continuously, the vehicle was externally powered through a cable. The setup allowed the vehicle to hover
freely but caused a slight position drift caused by the weight of the strings and power supply. The position
drift was almost completely eliminated by adding an offset to the roll and pitch inputs from the RC-controller.
Figure A.8 in Appendix A shows the test setup where the landing pad is placed on the ﬂoor with the camera
mounted in the center.
The take-off velocity of the UAV is limited by the system parameter max velocity z in m/s. In order to
achieve a smooth take-off where accuracy is more important than time, the maximum allowed velocity in the
z-direction was set to 0.5m/s.

safety measurement. Figure 6.1 also shows the thrust level as the vehicle moves toward the set point where it
can be seen that a thrust level of around 35% of the maximum is sufﬁcient to lift the vehicle from ground
slowly. The setup of the take-off sequence illustrated in Figure 6.1 is as previously mentioned constrained

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
7.1
VTS
A Visual Tracking System was developed and implemented as a plugin for the local real-time servers.
Visual marker setups were evaluated and a system with red led-lit plastic balls was mounted on the UAV. The
system showed good results in the position and heading estimations where a high accuracy was achieved at
the wanted runtime frequency. The system did not deliver a perfect result when the vehicle was partially
visible in an image and we are therefore proposing a solution that could increase the accuracy and robustness
in Section 7.4.
7.2
Fault Detection
An offboard bank of EKFs for residual generation has been developed as a plugin. Each EKF accepts one
aiding height sensor and gives out one height estimation. Three ofﬂine GLRT detectors were then developed
which take each one set of residuals and estimates if a fault has occurred enabling isolability of the fault
to a speciﬁc sensor. Although the goal was to have a fully working online fault estimation of the UAV, the
ofﬂine fault estimation works for most of the cases where a total failure of the sensor was assumed with low
detection delay. Several improvements can still be made in the bank of EKFs as well as in the detectors.
More type of faults should be considered, and the current fault estimation should be tested at greater heights.
7.3
VTOL
The experiments that were carried out, take-off, heading synchronisation and tracking, were only partially
successful due to the position restrictions in the test facilities. Although the correct altitude was reached for
the take-off, a free ﬂight could have resulted in a loss of control and a possible destruction of the equipment.
The same goes for the tracking part, where the vehicle was keeping the wanted position for a short period of
time until the safety measures kept the vehicle from loosing control. It can be concluded that the autonomous
setpoint control is far from optimal in the current setup and needs more work in order to be implemented and
tested in a free ﬂight.
7.4
Future Work
7.4.1
VTS
In order to make the system more robust and accurate we propose the following additions:
71
==PAGE:94==
72
CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSIONS
• Hough line algorithm to detect the vehicle when only one marker is visible in the image. See
Appendix A.3 for example images and a short description of early tests.
• Automatic adaptive thresholding in order to decrease the illumination sensitivity.
• Adaptive current/voltage control of the marker LEDs according to current illumination levels
• Perform the algorithm in an ROI (Region Of Interest) in order to decrease the needed processing
power and increase the runtime frequency
7.4.2
Fault Detection
Three EKFs are running in parallel in the plugin this could be expanded to more EKFs taking additional
aiding sensors as input such as a GPS for fault detection in position estimation. The processing power of the
offboard computer is more than capable of handling more computational load. The offboard GLRT detectors
should be implemented on the offboard computer, this can be done by having them run in separate threads
and waiting for new residual data to arrive. The residual generation could also be done in a separate thread
where it is polling for new estimations.
7.4.3
VTOL
The setpoint control method suggested in this report could possibly be used but it needs more testing and
a better position estimation in order to function properly. We suggest the following improvements:
• Outdoor GPS tests with a high accuracy GPS unit
• An optical ﬂow algorithm with a downward-facing on-board camera for better position control during
landing and take-off
• A less powerful and smaller UAV that would provide accurate position control in exchange for raw
power
