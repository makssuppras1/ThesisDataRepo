Abstract 
Mackerel (Scomber scombrus) is a widely distributed, highly migratory species that spend their whole 
life in the pelagic environment. Knowledge of the vast distribution and the aggressive feeding behav-
ior of mackerel are required to understand the ecological impact in the North Sea, Skagerrak, and 
Kattegat. The stomach data were collected at night from the acoustic herring survey (HERAS) be-
tween 28 June and 8 July 2019. The present study aimed at the feeding composition, feeding time, 
and feeding strategy of mackerel over the areas.  Euphausiids were the most dominant food item both 
in terms of weight and occurrence, and in both Skagerrak and Kattegat while in the North Sea, fish 
prey dominated the diet in terms of weight, and euphausiid was most important in terms of occurrence. 
Mackerel stomach fullness was analyzed, scaled from 1 to 5 (from empty to the full stomach), and 
the stomach fullness of mackerel varied between the study areas and time of night.  While analyzing 
the mackerel stomach fullness, a lot of empty stomach mackerel were found during the late night 
compared to the early night. Mackerel was feeding mainly on larger prey items during the early night 
(dusk), whereas small prey items dominated the diet during the late night than early night. A linear 
regression model was made to analyze the prey selection. Larger prey (i.e., fish euphausiids and am-
phipods) showed a strong positive correlation with the size of the mackerel. In addition to that, re-
gression models were also used to test for the collinearity among the four focal prey groups (cope-
pods, crab larvae, euphausiids, and fish). Copepods and crab larvae showed strong relations, meaning 
that the mackerel most likely consumed those simultaneously (for example, they have occupied the 
segment of the zooplankton ingested during the filter-feeding events).  The result further indicated 
that mackerel switched their feeding mode between day and night depending on the prey concentra-
tion. Due to lack of irradiance at night, mackerel most likely chose filter-feeding during night, 
whereas, during the day, mackerel most likely adhered to particulate feeding behavior targeting large 
organisms, where available, displaying an overall opportunistic feeding strategy. 
Introduction 
Mackerel (Scomber scombrus) is a highly migratory and widely distributed species in the Northeast 
Atlantic (ICES, 2013). Generally, it extends its coverage from Morocco up to Norway and from 
Greenland to the western Baltic Sea on the eastern part of the North Atlantic (Iverson, 2002) and in 
the northwest Atlantic, mackerel are found from North Carolina in the south up to Labrador in the 
==PAGE:3==
2 
 
north (Sette, 1950). Mackerel spend their whole life in the pelagic environment, and they play a cru-
cial role in the ecosystem by feeding on zooplankton, pelagic fish larvae, and juveniles of some com-
mercially important fish stock (Trenkel et al., 2012). Traditionally the eastern Atlantic mackerel 
spawning area is divided into three components consisting of the southern (covers from Gibraltar to 
the southern Biscay), western (from Biscay to northwest of Scotland), and the North Sea spawners ( 
Jansen & Gislason, 2011). In the past decade, mackerel stock size increased by 170% from 2002 to 
2013 in the Icelandic water (Olafsdottir et al., 2016). Several factors influence the population dynam-
ics and growth of northeast Atlantic mackerel. A study by Olafsdottir et al. (2019) indicated that the 
geographical expansion of mackerel during the summer feeding in the Nordic Sea was pushed by 
mackerel abundance and the temperature preference to the selected habitat. Substantial changes in 
mackerel distribution and abundance exerted a significant effect on the ecosystem and economy (Jan-
sen, 2012). Mackerel migration is restricted by food availability and temperature (Sette, 1950; 
Castonguay et al.,1992; Jansen et al., 2019). Mackerel prefers a temperature of ≥  7°C.  However, it 
occasionally appears in colder water ( Castonguay et al., 1992). The gradual increase in temperature 
in the Icelandic water and decrease of zooplankton biomass in the Norwegian Sea may have triggered 
the migration of mackerel to Icelandic waters, creating a new feeding area during the summer ( Kvaa-
vik et al., 2019). Mackerel occupies a larger area of the different marine ecosystems and impacts the 
ecosystem through predator-prey interaction; and studies of mackerel diet and feeding mode are nec-
essary in order to understand the impact and interaction (Chipps & Garvey, 2007). Stomach content 
analyses can give information on fish feeding strategies and shedding light on many crucial ecological 
interactions. The study of feeding habits is often directed to determine the most frequently consumed 
prey, the relative nutritional importance, or to quantify the consumption rate of individual prey species 
( Manko, 2016). Analyzing fish diet and feeding mode of fish delivers the fundamental knowledge of 
trophic relations in aquatic food webs (Mahesh et al., 2019, Manko, 2016). Furthermore, the feeding 
ecology revealed the growth condition, feeding composition, and distributional alterations (Kvaavik 
et al., 2019).  The experience gathered through the stomach investigation and food habits are essential 
to decide on the fishery management and management of similar natural resources (Kido, 1996) and 
give a better understanding of the ecosystem structure, community composition, and population dy-
namics (Ahlbeck et al. 2012; Litvaitis 2000). 
Mackerel prey on a range of organisms, mainly zooplankton, during their early life stages, and grad-
ually change their feeding mode to a bigger sized prey as they grow (Pepin et al., 1987, Langøy et al., 
==PAGE:4==
3 
 
2006), relying strongly on the seasonal and annual food variability (Óskarsson et al., 2016; Trenkel 
et al., 2014). Previous studies showed that mackerel's stomach content consists of several food items 
that differ in terms of spatial, seasonal, and annual distributions. 
In most studies, mackerel feed on copepods ( mainly Calanus finmarchicus), euphausiids (Meganyc-
tiphanes norvegica), and amphipods and large prey items ( i.e., herring, sand-eel, capelin, and other 
juvenile fish) (Kvaavik et al., 2019; Mehl and Westgård, 1983). Mackerel feeds just about everything 
they come across, either by ingesting the whole prey ( i.e., particulate feeding), biting or filter-feeding 
(Macy et al., 1998). However, depending on prey concentration, mackerel may display prey selection 
(Manko, 2019). Stomach fullness increases mainly with increasing length and condition of the indi-
viduals, and prey selection tends to switch from smaller to larger prey as the mackerel grow. 
During the 1950s and 1960s, mackerel were significantly abundant in the North Sea (Browers, 1979), 
but in the 1970s, the North Sea stock collapsed (Jansen et al., 2012). Then gradually, mackerel ap-
peared in the Norwegian water in the 1990s (Jansen et al., 2012) and progressively extended its hor-
izontal distribution, and its abundance also increased in the North Sea, Skagerrak and Kattegat. The 
increase of mackerel in this region, both in terms of area coverage and abundance, can potentially 
impact the ecosystem and existing species composition. Therefore, the limited knowledge of macke-
rel stomach content, especially in Skagerrak and Kattegat, was the primary motivation for the present 
study in which I investigated the feeding ecology of mackerel based on stomach samples collected in 
the North Sea, Skagerrak and Kattegat. This project aimed to increase our understanding of the fol-
lowing: 
• 
What is the main prey composition of mackerel in the three regions? Knowing the prey com-
position of mackerels can tell us what type of organisms may be impacted, which could be 
essential information for ecosystem-based fishery management. 
• 
When do they feed? Some studies have suggested that mackerel feeding performance is 
strongly influenced by light (Macy et al., 1998). The studies have shown that during the day, 
mackerel are mainly particulate feeders on relatively large prey items due to the light availa-
bility, whereas lack of light tends to trigger filter-feeding behavior on small zooplankton. 
• 
What is the mackerel feeding strategy? Do they show prey selectively? In areas where con-
centrated small zooplanktons occur, mackerel open their mouth, swim slowly, and collect the 
small prey items through filter feeding. Whereas in areas where there is large prey, mackerel 
swim fast and get their food through particulate feeding. Depending on the prey availability, 
==PAGE:5==

mackerel larvae and juveniles are size and species selective feeders, while adults are more 
likely opportunistic feeders (Kvaavik et al., 2019). 
Methods and Materials

Data collection 
Mackerel was collected from the North Sea, Skagerrak, and Kattegat (Fig 1) on a field trip from 28 
June to 8 July with a survey vessel (scientific vessel, Dana) across 17 stations. Out of the 17 stations, 
seven-stations were from the North Sea, 5 stations were taken from both the Skagerrak and Kattegat. 
The depth range was between 29 m and 362 m, with a mean of 148 m. On each station, the sample 
was collected using a surface trawl two times a day during the nighttime (21:00 to 00:02). During the 
survey, a total of 622 mackerel samples were collected from the North Sea (318), Skagerrak (161), 
and Kattegat ( 143). The sample size was not constant among stations and between regions but aimed 
to have two samples of each size group ( measured on board ( to the nearest cm)). The sample was 
selected randomly. 506 stomachs were removed on board and stored in a zip plastic bag with station 
ID and size, before being immediately frozen at -20°C for later analyses. From the remaining 116 
mackerel, whole fish were placed in zipped plastic bags with station ID and size, before being stored 
==PAGE:6==
5 
 
at -80°C for later analyses. The sampling covered all available size groups (17cm to 43cm) caught 
during the survey. 
In the laboratory, two types of samples were analyzed. The whole fish that were preserved at -80°C, 
and the stomachs removed onboard and frozen at -20°C. Then, in the case of the whole fish sample, 
the fish was dissected lengthways on the ventral side, starting from the anus carefully to avoid cutting 
the prey. The stomach was weighed with the electronic balance, and the result was recorded to the 
nearest 0.001g. Afterward, the abdomen was opened by creating a shallow slice rigorously with a 
scalpel lengthways from the upper part to the lower end of the stomach. Hereafter, the food item was 
placed on a Petri dish, and the empty stomach was weighed. The weight of prey groups and the overall 
weight of the stomach content was then recorded. The larger prey was sorted out manually (especially 
fish) to measure their length( to the nearest mm) and weighed ( to the nearest 0.001g). The total prey 
weight was calculated by subtracting the empty stomach and the weight of unintentional food items( 
i.e., worms, plastic, and fish scales) from the whole stomach weight. Prey was identified with a ste-
reomicroscope to the lowest taxonomic group possible. Some of the identified species are included 
as photos in figure 2 and the appendix.  When the food item was squeezed, a little water was used to 
separate and to identify the group of species.

Prey composition 
After separating the stomach content into taxonomic groups, each group was weighed individually if 
that was possible; otherwise, crude percentage judgment was made. Fish prey was sorted out manually 
and weighed and measured separately. Fish prey that was impossible to measure (i.e., too fragmented 
and digested ) was calculated as a percentage weight from the total weight of the given items. Fur-
thermore, when two otoliths of the same fish were found in a digested or fragmented fish prey, it was 
considered as single fish. In terms of zooplankton, two types of methods were used. In method one, 
items of the same easily identified species were grouped and weighed together. In the second method, 
which concerned zooplankton, which was impossible to take directly measure, first, the food item 
was shaken to mix thoroughly, and then a 2 ml subsample was taken to calculate the percentage of 
the individual prey items. This subsample remeasurement was then used to calculate the total weight 
of the prey (total stomach weight* percentage% of the prey).

The stomach fullness was scaled from 1 to 5 based on visual inspection. Empty stomachs were given 
as1and full stomachs 5. Stomachs with very little content ( which is invisible before opening the 
stomach), little stomach content, but visible before opening the stomach, and partial full stomach 
without stretched the abdomen, were also scaled as 2, 3, and 4, respectively. The term "Other" was 
given for those who showed quantitatively less percentage after identified individually ( i.e., snail, 
bivalve, Appendicularia, shrimp, and lobster), and the term fish refers to all types of fish (i.e., adult, 
juvenile fish, and fish larvae and fish remain). 
Feeding time and feeding strategy 
Samples were grouped into two-time intervals (UTC): early night (dusk until midnight) from 21:00 
to 00:00 and late-night(midnight until dawn) from 00:00 to 00:02. To evaluate the variation of food 
items as a function of predator size, mackerels were pooled into centimeter size bins. Only those 
groups with a sample size ≥ 10 fish (which covered size groups 20- 39 cm) were included in the 
analysis.

Result 
A total of 22 mackerel stomachs were analyzed in the laboratory (DTU Aqua ), out of which 169 were 
empty, and 183 had very little stomach content, 128 had a medium stomach content. Only  58 and 84 
stomachs were recorded as 4 and 5 respectively ( near full or full on the stomach fullness scale re-
spectively), and overall, 73% of mackerel stomachs were recorded with food present in the stomachs 
(nearly empty to full stomach). Euphausiids were the most dominant food item contributor in Skag-
errak (87%) and Kattegat (84%), followed by copepods (6%) and fish(9%), whereas in the North Sea, 
fish and euphausiids were the main contributors (39 and 34% respectively). The statistical analysis 
indicated that as mackerel size increased, they selected larger prey on average. Conversely, copepods 
and crab larvae (small zooplanktons) failed to show a significant relationship as the mackerel size 
increases (Table 1). 
Prey composition

counted for a relatively small proportion of the stomach (i.e., %O, and %Wi was low) (Table 1).

stituted the highest proportion of diet in terms of weight(39%) followed by euphausiids(34%). Con-

Feeding time 
The stomach fullness was observed during two different time intervals (Fig 5). During the early 
night(dusk), 307 samples were analyzed, out of which 65 stomachs were empty, while category 1 and 
2 stomach ( empty or nearly empty)  constituted 145 stomachs. On the other hand, during the late-
night(dawn), 315 stomachs were analyzed, indicating that 104 were empty, whereas category 1and 2 
comprised 207 stomachs. The analysis mainly focused on four focal prey groups(amphipods, crab 
larvae copepods, euphausiids, and fish). The mean weight of fishy and euphausiids stomach content 
was higher during the early night in contrast to the late-night. Conversely, the mean weight of cope-
pods and crab larvae was higher during late-night compared to early night. However, the difference 
was not significant, according to a t-test.

Feeding strategy 
The statistical analysis indicates that fish, amphipods, and euphausiids showed a strong relation with 
predator size, whereas the copepods and crab larvae failed to show any relationship with mackerel 
size (table 2). The lower and upper sizes of the predator (<20cm and>39cm) were neglected in this 
analysis because they were rare. The same analytical steps were taken in figures 7 and 9. Linear 
models were used to examine the feeding strategy. The first was used to analyze the relationship 
between the predator size vs. the percentage with at least one of the focal preys in the stomach (Fig 
7), and the other model was used to look for relationships among prey groups (Fig 9). The linear 
model indicated that fish, amphipods, and euphausiids showed a strong relationship with the predator 
size (with a P-value of 0.0003, 0.0001, and 0.004, respectively). Conversely,  copepods and crab 
larvae failed to show any correlation to the predator size (with a P-value of 0.36 and 0.08, respec-
tively). At the same time, nonempty stomachs were also analyzed. However, it did not show signifi-
cant relation (P= 0.211), this indicated that small size mackerel could also feed on larger prey (espe-
cially euphausiids and amphipods), but probably due to the faster digestion rate, they are more likely

Discussion 
In the present study, the feeding ecology of mackerel was investigated, focusing, on prey composition, 
feeding time, and feeding strategy. Understanding the feeding pattern of mackerel can shed light on 
the ecological impact created by mackerel. Mackerel is an aggressive feeder that will eat any food 
item that it comes across if its small enough to be engulfed  (Jansen et al., 2019). The greedy feeding 
characteristics and a substantial increase in distribution and biomass in the North Sea, Skagerrak, and 
Kattegat, where mackerel studies are rare, have motivated the present study. 
Prey composition 
Generally, mackerel feeds on a variety of prey items (fish, zooplankton, mollusks, etc.), but mainly 
depends on the small zooplankton, which plays an essential role in mackerel's diet. In most of the 
study papers, copepods are the major players in mackerel diets. The availability of those organisms 
widely varies from region to region, season to season, and year to year. For example, copepods (es-
pecially C. finmarchicus) dominated mackerel stomachs in the Icelandic water during the summer 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
from 2009- 2013, constituting >60% of the stomach content (Kvaavik et al., 2019), whereas, in the 
southern Gulf of St Lawrence both copepods and euphausiids, in addition to fish dominated the stom-
achs of mackerel ( Darbyson et al., 2003). Copepods, euphausiids, and amphipods also dominated the 
diet of mackerel in the Irminger Sea (Jansen et al., 2016). In the present analysis based on stomachs 
from three different regions (the North Sea, Skagerrak, and Kattegat), euphausiids were the most 
dominant food item in Skagerrak and Kattegat in terms of weight (87% and 84% respectively) and 
occurrence (61% and 49% respectively). In the case of the North Sea, fish was the main prey item in 
terms of weight (39%), and euphausiids dominated stomach content in terms of occurrence (28%). 
Several other prey items were also found in the guts of the mackerel, especially in the North Sea. 
However, those prey items were highly variable in numbers and quantitatively insignificant ( for ex-
ample, Appendicularia, shrimp, bivalves, and snail constituted each <1%). Darbyson et al. ( 2003) 
illustrated that in a place characterized by few prey species but in higher concentration, mackerel feed 
on a few prey items. The present study revealed that in Skagerrak, the prey composition was less 
diverse compared to the North Sea and Kattegat ( table 1), and the mackerels fed mainly on euphau-
siids, especially at stations close to the Norwegian coast (Norwegian trench) and the central part of 
Skagerrak (Fig 1). Consequently, lower prey diversity and high concentrations of euphausiids in the 
Skagerrak may have driven the mackerel to feed mainly on euphausiids. Based on this observation, it 
could be concluded that when euphausiids are abundant enough, mackerel feed mainly on euphausiids 
and are less likely to feed on copepods 
The stomach fullness of mackerel has been evaluated using the scale 1to 5 (Fig 3) in the three study 
areas (the North Sea, Skagerrak, and Kattegat). In the North Sea, mackerel with nearly full and full 
stomachs accounted for 12%, whereas in the same numbers in Skagerrak and Kattegat were 32 and 
38%, respectively. Since euphausiids were the primary food item across all areas, the higher abun-
dance of euphausiids in Skagerrak and part of Kattegat, especially station (1362 and1374) (Fig 1), 
lifted the stomach fullness ratio in Kattegat and Skagerrak (i.e., stomachs were on average more full). 
Previous research has shown that mackerel exhibit the highest stomach fullness in midafternoon due 
to higher prey consumption during the daytime (Darbyson et al., 2003) 
Feeding time 
Mackerel is largely planktivorous using passive feeding techniques, where they filter the water 
through the gills retaining the food particles, or active techniques where they pick individual food 
items one at a time (i.e., particulate feeding techniques) (Kvaavik et al., 2019). At low irradiance, the 
==PAGE:18==

feeding rate of mackerel decrease (i.e., during the night ), and they tend to shift to filter-feeding mode, 
where copepods dominate the stomachs of mackerels ( Darbyson et al., 2003). Nevertheless, in the 
present study, during both time intervals,  early and late-night, the larger prey (especially euphausiids 
and fish) played a prominent role in the diet even though the larger prey showed a higher percentage 
during the early night (Fig 6). The more significant proportion of euphausiids and fish during the 
early night may have been due to the shorter time since mackerel could use the light and sight and 
chase those relatively large prey items. Furthermore, an increase in copepods (i.e., relatively small 
prey) in the mackerel stomach during late-night indicates a shift to filter-feeding mode during the 
night. Several mackerels were found with empty stomachs in late-night that could be due to the lack 
of light in which mackerel could not target larger prey, primarily fish and large zooplankton, or the 
small soft-bodied prey decomposed rapidly in the stomach. Euphausiids were found at a slightly 
higher proportion during the early night than the late night. During the day, euphausiids move to150-
200 m depth, and during the night, they move up to near the surface (Havforskinginstitute, 1969). 
This diurnal vertical migration of euphausiids allows mackerel to rely on euphausiids, especially dur-
ing summer where they can stay for a longer time in the upper surface water from 23:00-02:00 (Tar-
ling et al., 1998) and 92% of euphausiids appeared during the late evening and night (Jansen et al., 
2016). Older euphausiids arrive late to the surface and return earlier, while small euphausiids appear 
earlier at the surface and return late ( Tarling et al., 1998; Havforskinginstitute, 1969 ). If euphausiids 
stay in the upper water column for a longer time during mid-summer (time of the sampling program), 
this could be advantageous for the mackerel, explaining the dominance of euphausiids in the diet. 
As the sun goes down, the feeding technique of mackerel shifts from being predominantly active 
feeders during the daytime to passive feeders at night, due to the lack of irradiance (Darbyson et al., 
2003). Copepods and crab larvae were about the same size while analyzing the data in the lab, and 
both copepods and crab larvae were more abundant during the late-night, indicating that the crab 
larvae live among the copepods and are therefore simultaneously filtered out by mackerels during the 
night. In addition to that, copepods and crab larvae showed a strong positive significant relationship 
supporting this notion (Table 3). Jansen et al. (2016) suggested that the digestion rate between day 
and night is most likely the same, but it differs between the prey items, predator size, and different 
temperatures. Some food item sits for a longer time in the stomach, whereas others are digested rap-
idly. Prey with a soft body and small prey items are digested rapidly and therefore evacuated quickly 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
from the stomach, leading to a higher proportion of empty or nearly empty stomachs. The study re-
vealed a higher empty stomach occurrence during dawn (33%) compared to dusk (21.2%). A prey 
item has a limited time to stay in the stomach before it is evacuated, so the mackerel presumably ate 
the prey during the day, probably completed their digestion during the late-night, and increased the 
empty stomach ratio when approaching dawn. On the other hand, the result also indicated that macke-
rel with maximum stomach fullness had a slightly higher during occurrence the late-night (Fig 5). 
The reason could be that some of the mackerel enter patches with high prey concentrations and 
thereby get an opportunity to fill their guts and ultimately increase the ratio slightly by 0.1% during 
late night. 
Feeding strategy 
Mackerel practices both active and passive feeding behaviors, but mackerel with a bigger size prefer 
larger prey organisms when these are available, which requires a more active feeding strategy (Kvaa-
vik et al., 2019, Langøy et al., 2006). Active feeding is more profitable for a large predator, which 
could consume more large food items than small mackerel and actively prey on larger prey as to 
reduce the cost of energy appended by filter-feeding, especially in low concentration of the small size 
zooplankton (Gibson et al. 1992) and maximize the energy acquisition from larger prey (Kvaavik et 
al., 2019, van Deurs et al. 2015). Filter feeding is more energy demanding than particulate feeding or 
biting this due to the higher respiration rate when they are filter-feeding. For example, Cope anchovy 
(Engraulis capensis) exceeds their energy consumption by 3-10 times more during filter-feeding, and 
filter-feeding is also costly in herring, causing an increase in swimming speed of 20-60% compared 
to active hunting (Gibson et al. 1992). As shown in Fig 8, a few mackerel ate fish, but those who ate 
fish ate a lot of fish. Besides that, euphausiids were by far the most dominant food item in the study 
area, in which > 70% of the sampled mackerel ate euphausiids and encompassed a larger proportion 
in mackerel's stomach. These results highlight that mackerel select fish and euphausiids when they 
are available. Mackerels >35cm showed a higher proportion of fish prey in their stomach compared 
to mackerel <35 cm, as shown in (Fig 7). The present result showed that mackerel predominantly 
feeds on larger prey. Once they target larger prey (i.e., fish or euphausiids), it does not mean they do 
not eat the small prey; they most likely eat whatever prey item comes to their mouth. Results from 
linear regressions presented in table 2 showed that fish, amphipod, and euphausiids have a solid pos-
itive relationship with predator size, which indicates that mackerel gradually start to target a large 
prey (fish, euphausiids, and amphipods) as they grow. However, copepods and crab larvae failed to 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
show a significant relationship with predator size, meaning all size groups consume copepods and 
crab larvae in equal amounts (i.e., supporting the notion that they are opportunistic feeders). Samples 
with small size (small size predators) had less food items in their stomachs, a finding which could be 
due to the surface area per volume ratio. A higher surface area per volume ratio has a rapid digestion 
rate. Therefore, the small mackerel feed on tiny zooplankton and have a faster evacuation rate com-
pared to the bigger mackerel who eat large prey, which has a small surface area per volume ratio. 
Other linear regressions were conducted to see the collinearity between the focal prey items (Fig 9). 
Significant relationships were only found between euphausiids vs. copepods and copepods vs. crab 
larvae, indicating that when mackerel feeds on euphausiids, they are unlikely to target on other larger 
preys (i.e., fish and amphipods). Euphausiids and copepods showed a negative relationship, and this 
negative relation indicated that when there are more euphausiids out there, mackerels mainly feed on 
euphausiids and are less likely to feed on copepods. Alternatively, when there is a higher concentra-
tion of copepods, mackerel can change the feeding mode from particulate feeding to filter-feeding 
that may limit the consumption of bigger prey (i.e., euphausiids) and increase consumption of cope-
pods. The statistical model also showed a significant relationship between copepods vs. crab larvae; 
this could be because copepods and crab larvae have small sizes so that mackerel could filter them 
simultaneously when there are few larger preys around. 
The aggressive feeding behavior and high clearance rate of mackerel can have a substantial impact 
on the ecosystem (Kvaavik et al., 2019).  Apart from the broader distribution and increase in stock 
size,  mackerel are competitors with other species like herring for food and tend to overgraze the 
zooplankton community, which could lead to a potential decline in zooplankton abundance and alter 
the food web structure (Bachiller et al., 2018).  In most previous studies, mackerel fed mainly on 
copepods ( especially C. finmarchicus). However, in the present study, mackerel predominantly fed 
on euphausiids (especially Meganyctiphanes norvegica), mostly in Skagerrak, which could negatively 
impact the survival of the native organism and territorial species which primarily rely on euphausiids. 
Óskarsson et al. (2016)  suggested that 1.1 million tons of mackerel were estimated to consume about 
3.4 million tons of prey in the Icelandic waters, at trait which could create an ecological disturbance. 
Due to the furious feeding behavior, mackerel do not only have ecological impacts, but they also exert 
a huge economic impact by consuming a large amount of the commercially important species like the 
Norway lobster (Kvaavik et al., 2019).  In addition to that, herring spring spawners in the Skagerrak, 
Kattegat, and western Baltic Sea decrease the recruitment since the mid-2000s, which influenced by

rel's existence in the area could have a potential impact on the herring stock either by competing for 
the food or preying on the larvae and juvenile. Furthermore,  mackerel is one of the active predators 
on the forage fish ( i.e., herring, sprat, sandeel, and Norway pout). For example, 10% of mackerel 
food diet  comes from sandeel, and this could compete with predominantly rely on sandeel like mam-
