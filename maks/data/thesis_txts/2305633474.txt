The objective of this thesis is to investigate the effectiveness of additive manufacturing 
technology to produce injection mould inserts. With this process, the injection mould in-
serts designed and manufactured would improve the production process of plastic prod-
ucts. The type of additive manufacturing technology used is digital light processing (DLP) 
3-D printing. Prior to assessing the effectiveness of DLP 3-D printing, four different 3D 
printer manufacturers were reviewed to assess accuracy, speed, and precision of the prod-
ucts printed. Of these stereolithographic 3-D printers, it was concluded that the printer 
manufactured by EnvisionTEC produced the overall best parts therefore this machine was 
used exclusively for the purpose of this thesis. With the use of 3-D printing, surface quality, 
influence of structural forces, failure modes, inserts on both halves versus one sided, and 
various materials were evaluated, characterized and used to predict the performance of ad-
ditive manufacturing inserts. The various materials used include moulding polymer and 
mould base material which produced two interchangeable inserts measuring 80x60x10 mil-
limetres. Physical experiments and computational simulations were carried out at LEGO 
and DTU. 
  
  
Keywords: additive manufacturing, injection moulding, 3D printing, rapid prototyping

Introduction 
For many years, additive manufacturing (AM) has been a well-established production tech-
nology used mainly for rapid prototyping. With technological progress and the develop-
ment of new processes, AM technology now starts to become a serious option for mass 
production, especially in conjunction with other technologies in a manufacturing process 
chain. Production time and costs can be reduced significantly by using AM technology e.g. 
for the production of injection moulding (IM) inserts. 
 
IM is a well known replication technology for many years. It is one of the most used tech-
nologies for high volume production at low cost. It is capable of making very complex 
shapes of various sizes with good dimensional accuracy (Lantada et al., 2015). 
 
 
The demand for microproducts in the industrial market has increased over time. There has 
been a worldwide trend in various industries for the miniaturisation of products, for exam-
ple, patients needing medical devices such as hearing aids are requesting smaller products 
without the trade-off in quality. Automotive industries need to create microelectromechan-
ical systems (MEMS), such as airbag sensors. Electronics such as computers, smart watches, 
and phones are designed to be smaller and lighter while maintaining lightening speeds.  
 
This tendency towards miniaturisation of products implies that there is a great demand for 
a quick solution for the development of micro- and nano manufacturing technologies 
(Vaezi, Seitz, & Yang, 2013). 
 
With this demand, hardware components will need to be smaller than their current size in 
order to please consumers. Other industries requiring miniaturisation of products include 
optics and biotechnology (Vaezi et al., 2013). 
 
Some of the newer microproducts requires usage of different materials in complex three 
dimensional (3D) micro structures with high aspect ratio. 
 
A great challenge in the field of micromechanics is the high production demand for MEMS 
technology. Fabrication of micro-components and product assembly for MEMS products 
involves movement of small parts such as sensors, medical devices, and optical systems 
which is a great challenge (Vaezi et al., 2013). 
 
One of the recent popular ways of doing so is through 3D micro-Additive Manufacturing 
technologies.

2 Manufacturing methods 
For the production of products, more than one manufacturing method exists to achieve 
such a goal. Various manufacturing methods available to create components of a final prod-
uct can be grouped into the following six broad categories: casting, forming and shaping, 
machining, joining, finishing, and nano- and microfabrication (Kalpakjian & Schmid, 2010). 
 
In the category forming and shaping are polymer-processing processes described. Among 
these are injection moulding and rapid prototyping. In this project a rapid prototyping 
method are used for the production of IM tools. 
 
2.1 Additive manufacturing process 
Additive manufacturing (AM) is a manufacturing method that remains a well-established 
production technology mainly used for rapid prototyping. With technological progress and 
the improvement of current processes, AM technology now rises the question if it is pro-
gressive enough for mass production, especially in conjunction with other manufacturing 
processes. Prior to this advancement, AM had mostly been used for rapid prototyping of 
single units (Lantada et al., 2015). The advantage that AM technology offers includes sig-
nificantly reducing production time and costs. 
 
Additive manufacturing (AM) is a production technology process where material is added 
to a manufacturing process to constitute a final product. One of the most well-known AM 
processes is three-dimensional (3D) printing. This process was developed based on inkjet 
printing to build objects by adding melted material in layers (Vaezi et al., 2013). By using 
this process, the desired object can be created directly without the need of moulds. 
 
3D printing has gained popularity and has grabbed the attention from the public sphere. 
What is unknown to the public is that 3D printing is a type of additive manufacturing and 
includes various processing methods such as stereolithography (SLA), selective laser sinter-
ing (SLS), fused deposition modelling (FDM), and electron-beam melting (EBM) to name 
a few (Kalpakjian & Schmid, 2010). 
 
AM technologies are fundamentally different from other conventional methods of produc-
tion. Traditional manufacturing technologies requires the use of a subtractive method 
where material from a solid block is removed in order to achieve the desired object. Sub-
tractive processes is a type of machining process. Machining can be categorised into 3 major 
types: cutting, abrasive processes and advanced machining processes (Kalpakjian & 
Schmid, 2010). The machining process also includes surface modification processes of a 
workpiece after it has been produced.

As technology becomes more advanced and established, AM processes have been em-
braced by a much broader audience. Industrial prototyping and manufacturing processes 
now heavily relies on AM processes. Recently, this specialised technology has become more 
accessible for minor companies and individuals which has lead to a rapidly increasing ac-
quisition and adoption of more systems, materials, applications, and accessories. 
 
The benefits of using AM processes are many. Printed components can be produced with-
out tools and without assembly resulting in lower costs and shorter lead times. AM manu-
facturing makes complex geometry possible without extra costs. Last but not least it en-
courages innovation and gives unprecedented design freedom.  
 
 
2.1.1 AM for rapid prototyping 
AM has become a well-established production technology that is mainly used for rapid 
prototyping of single units. A downfall to AM technology is that the excessive cost and 
time required for rapid prototyping has created a desire for a more efficient process for 
mass production. Compared to the ease of injection moulding, another type of manufac-
turing process, the AM method is the less favourable of the two replication technologies 
(Lantada et al., 2015). 
 
Recent studies show that combining AM technology with mass production technology is 
ideal to increase the rate of efficiency of rapid prototyping. With the advancement of tech-
nology and development, AM processes offer the possibility of reducing production time 
and cost. 
 
In order to take advantage of the production processes available, the idea of using rapid 
prototyping in conjunction with mass production manufacturing would result in producing 
products with complex geometries while reducing the amount in spending. Although this 
is a great production process proposal, such technique is not yet available on a large scale 
but is a relevant need in the industry that requires more research and innovation (Lantada 
et al., 2015).

2.1.3 Stereolithography 
Stereolithography (SLA) is an AM technology where a light source (laser) radiates ultravio-
let (UV) light on photosensitive (photopolymer) resins which then begins a curing process 
that eventually solidifies the resin (polymerisation). The photopolymer is held as a liquid 
resin in a vat (basin). In the vat, there is also placed a movable building platform/stage on 
which the polymerised resin sticks to. For each solidified layer, the building platform moves 
slightly (a little bit) deeper down into the vat, whereupon the next layer can be created. This 
process is continued layer-by-layer until the entire object/part has been created inside of 
the vat  (Vaezi et al., 2013). 
 
Micro-Stereolithography (MSL) is SLA in microscopic scale (0,1 mm >X > 0,1 µm) 
(Kalpakjian & Schmid, 2010). It is an additive 3D micro manufacturing process with high 
accuracy that can produce objects with a layer thickness down to a couple of microns (1-
10 µm layer thickness). The resolution of the x, y, z translational motion stages, along with 
the diameter of the light source have a value of a few micro meters, which is smaller than 
in conventional SL (Vaezi et al., 2013). 
 
Some of the difference between SLA and MSL is presented by (Kalpakjian & Schmid, 
2010): 
• The laser is more highly focused (to a diameter as small as 1 µm, compared with 10 to 
over 100 µm in Stereolithography). 
• Layer thicknesses are around 1 µm, which is an order of magnitude smaller than in 
Stereolithography. 
• The photopolymers used must have much lower viscosities to ensure the formation of 
uniform layers. 
• Support structures are not needed in micro-Stereolithography, since the smaller struc-
tures can be supported by the fluid. 
• Parts with significant metal and ceramic content can be produced by suspending nano-
particles in the liquid photopolymer.

2.2 Injection moulding process 
Injection moulding is the most common method used to produce parts from plastic mate-
rials. This manufacturing process can shape and form parts with complex geometries with 
high accuracy in various sizes. Other characteristics are thin walls, high production rates, 
good dimensional accuracy, but also expensive tooling (Kalpakjian & Schmid, 2010). 
 
The machine used for injection moulding contains three main parts: an injection unit, a 
mould unit, and a clamping unit, see Figure 2.  
 
The injection (unit) system heats and melts the material to its suitable (processing tem-
perature) viscosity or flow ability and then it forces the material into the tool mould.  
The mould (unit) consists typically of two mould halves, a stationary (injection-side) and 
and a movable half (ejector-side) which are bolted onto large plates, called the platens. 
The clamping (unit) system holds the injection moulding tool and provides the required 
clamp force to hold the two sides of the mould together. 
 
Thermoplastic materials require the temperature of the mould to be cold and to be held 
below the melting temperature. This allows the product to solidify and subsequently be 
demoulded. In contrast to thermoplastic materials, thermoset materials and elastomers re-
quire the mould to be heated to higher temperatures to allow for the chemical process of 
crosslinking of the materials (Goodship, 2004). 
 
This project focuses specifically on thermoplastic materials and photopolymers.

2.2.1 Injection units 
In order for the injection moulding to work, two types of injection unit mechanisms are 
available. The mechanisms are a piston injection unit and a screw position injection unit. 
The screw position injection unit is a type of reciprocating screw which is the most com-
mon to use. The IM machine used for the purpose of this thesis comes equipped with a 
reciprocating screw. 
 
Depending on the unit used, the material forced into the mould cavity in the injection phase 
is being pushed by a ram/piston/plunger/screw. They all have the same function during 
the injection phase: to push the material into the cavity.  
 
2.2.1.1 Piston injection unit 
The piston injection unit was developed by a method originally used to mould rubber. The 
injection moulding machines only used external heat to heat the barrel. This was done in 
order to melt the plastic after which a plunger injected the molten material into the mould. 
However, there are some limitations associated with this type of injection unit compared 
to the reciprocating screw. The major limitation to this process is poor heat conduction. 
While the temperature of the plunger is uniform, the inner core of the plastic used is either 
too cold or not completely melted while the exterior was often too hot and resulted in 
plastic degradation (Goodship, 2004). 
 
 
Figure 3 - Piston (plunger) injection unit. 
 
2.2.1.2 The reciprocating screw 
The reciprocating screw injection system consists of the following five parts: the hopper, 
an externally heated injection barrel, a reciprocating screw inside the injection barrel,  a 
hydraulic motor and an injection cylinder, see Figure 4. 
==PAGE:17==

In the reciprocating screw injection unit, the moulding material is heated from two sources. 
Heat is transferred from hot cylindrical walls while frictional heat is delivered by the screw. 
The layout of the rotating screw system forces the polymer granules against each other and 
against the inside of the cylindrical walls of the injection barrel causing high friction. This 
process contributes greatly to the heating and melting of the polymer. Additional heat is 
provided by external heating bands that are wrapped around the injection barrel, therefore 
heating the barrel itself and the plastic inside of it (Kalpakjian & Schmid, 2010). 
 
 
Figure 4 - Injection moulding with a reciprocating rotating screw (left) and typical screw extruder and its 
features (right) (Kalpakjian & Schmid, 2010). 
The injection screw is typically divided into three different zones: (1) a feed zone, (2) a 
melting zone (also known as a transition zone), and (3) a metering zone. The outer diameter 
of the screw is constant in all three zones, but the flight depth of the screw decreases when 
moving from the feeding zone towards the metering zone. The design of the injection screw 
pushes out hot air as the polymers are transported and pushed against each other. This is 
shown in Figure 5 (Goodship, 2004). 
 
 
Figure 5 - Various injection screws and their zones (Goodship, 2004).

constant flight depth but is much smaller than then one in the first zone. The tip of the

2.2.2 IM process cycle 
An injection moulding cycle can be classified into three general stages. 
 
Plastication 
The first stage is responsible for heating the plastic up to an appropriate moulding temper-
ature. It is important to achieve a homogeneous melt. An advantage of the reciprocating 
screw unit compared with the piston unit is that the plastic comprises of only the space 
around the shaft of the screw and not the entire cross-section of the injection barrel. This 
leads to a more uniform temperature distribution of the melt. Important moulding param-
eters influencing this stage include cylinder temperature, screw back temperature and back 
pressure. 
 
Filling 
Molten polymer is injected into the tool during the filling stage. This step of the IM process 
has a significant influence on the residual stresses of the moulded part. The precision of 
the filling process can affect the surface quality of the final product and can possibly cause 
warpage. Therefore, it is important to find a good balance between the injection speed and 
injection pressure. Using an injection speed that is too high can cause jetting and degrada-
tion which will affect the mechanical properties of the product. In contrast, a low injection 
speed can lead to premature material solidification causing a thicker layer of frozen polymer

to form. To compensate for a low speeds, higher injection pressures can be used 
(Goodship, 2004). 
 
When producing parts with high aspect-ratio features, frozen layers develop more quickly 
because heat is lost more quickly in products with small dimensions in comparison to prod-
ucts with normal-sized cavities. This premature solidification becomes more evident when 
filling features on a microscopic scale. The heat loss from the polymer melt can be reduced 
by increasing the injection speed and mould temperature, but the practical processing con-
ditions are limited by the physical capabilities of the moulding tools  (Lucchetta, Ferraris, 
Tristo, & Reynaerts, 2012). 
Important parameters in this stage is the injection speed and injection pressure. 
 
Packing and Solidifying 
In the last stage after the mould cavity has been filled with material, it needs to be cooled 
and subsequently ejected from the mould. As the polymer cools and starts to solidify, its 
density decreases and causes the material to shrink. In order to compensate for this 
shrinkage, extra material is added to the mould cavity in the packing stage. Without this 
packing stage, the polymer will not only shrink but non-uniform cooling would occur and 
ultimately causing warpage. Packing pressure, packing time, and the mould temperature 
are extremely important parameters during this stage (Goodship, 2004) [ http://help.au-
todesk.com/view/MFIA/2016/ENU/]. 
 
Sequence of operation of injection moulding with the reciprocating screw: 
Sequence of operation of injection moulding: 
1. 
Plastic granules/pellets are fed to the injection barrel from the hopper. As the ma-
terial enters the injection barrel, it is pushed forward by the rotation of the screw 
that is powered by the hydraulic motor. 
2. 
At the tip of the screw is a one-way-valve that only allows material to flow towards 
the end of the nozzle. As polymer accumulates in front of the sprue bushing, the 
pressure increases and pushes the screw backwards as it turns and builds a cham-
ber of polymer in front of the screw. When enough material for the injection shot 
has been built up, the rotation stops. 
3. 
Then, the one-way-valve closes as the screw is being pushed forward by the injec-
tion cylinder via hydraulics.  This fills the sprue bushing, sprue, runner, gate and 
finally the mould cavity with polymer. 
4. 
After the cavity has been filled, the polymer is cooled down. The quicker the poly-
mer is cooled, the quicker the next product can be produced. Therefore, some 
types of moulds contain cooling channels filled with a cooling medium to speed 
up the process to solidify polymer quickly to reduce the cycle time. 
5. 
After the polymer is solidified, the moulds opens and the part is removed by ejec-
tor pins.

2.2.3.1 Moulds 
There are many different mould designs that exist which include two-plate mould, stripper 
mould, slide mould, and three-plate mould, just to mention a few. For the purpose of this 
project, a two-plate mould design was used.  
The two-plate mould is the simplest and most common design. It consists of two mould  
halves: a fixed half (cavity side) and a movable half (core side). Both mould halves are 
mounted inside the injection moulding machine. The fixed half is attached adjacent to the 
injection unit from which the molten polymer is injected. The movable part is attached next 
to the clamping unit and adjacent to the ejection system. When the mould is closed, the 
cavity between them comprises the part design where molten polymer can be injected into 
and form the desired part. When the two mould halves separate, the product remains at-
tached to the ejector-side of the mould. The reason being that the plastic when cooled, 
shrinks inward towards the core and squeezes tightly around the core half of the mould. 
The core half of the mould contains ejector pins to push the part off the mould once it has 
cooled (Goodship, 2004).

2.2.4 Pressure profile 
When a part or a series of parts are being produced there is typically a desire to manufac-
ture products as homogeneous as possible. But there is always a small deviation in the 
part quality including features like weight, dimensional consistency and surface character-
istics. This deviation can be caused by many different factors such as variation in the am-
bient temperature during the moulding process, machine start-up after a long period of 
time in which the machine has been idle, and change of colorant granules. There is there-
fore a great interest for achieving a highly controlled process, so these adverse factors can 
be minimized. 
One of the most critical process parameters which influences the part quality, is the cavity 
pressure in the mould. If the cavity pressure is held constant throughout all cycles the 
quality of the moulded parts will have a high consistency (Goodship, 2004). 
The cavity pressure inside the mould is not measured by the injection moulding machine. 
In order to measure the cavity pressure, a sensor needs to be integrated directly in the 
mould cavity itself, see Figure 9. 
 
“The internal cavity pressure can be measured by sensors within the mould, and can be 
indicated, or visually displayed, using an oscilloscope or a pen recorder.” (Goodship, 
2004) 
 
The pressure measured by the IM machine is the injection pressure, which is applied to 
the melt by the screw (temporarily acting as a piston). These parameters are typically 
measured by a sensor located at the tip screw (in the screw forward cavity), see Figure 9. 
 
However, the injection pressure is higher than the cavity pressure. There is a pressure 
drop during the filling caused by the flow resistance from the injection pressure location 
to the internal cavity pressure location. 
The level of the pressure drop depends on the viscosity of the melt, the injection speed, 
and the geometry of the flow path in the injection chamber, nozzle geometry and sprue.

2.2.6 Simulation 
Theoretical simulations of an injection moulding process are conducted in order to assess 
and verify the performance of IM inserts. 
Simulations of IM processes are frequently performed to obtain part and mould designs 
that are as ideal and robust as possible before finalising these. Through simulations differ-
ent layouts, configurations, features, etc. can be tested and exposed to different scenarios. 
Hereby shortcomings and limitations of such designs can be identified. 
 
Via simulations the most ideal process condition required to obtain the desired product 
can be predicted, minimising expensive and time consuming experimental setup and exe-
cution (Guerrier, Tosello, & Hattel, 2015). 
 
The tendency towards miniaturisation of products in the industrial market implies that 
there is a great demand in preforming accurate simulation for µIM. 
 
With µIM simulations it is possible to reduce the product time-to-market, the risk of 
costly re-engineering, and the development time of products (Marhöfer, Tosello, Hansen, 
& Islam, 2013). 
 
Approaches to validate Simulations and Experiments 
In order to assess whether a simulation is producing correct results, several methods can 
be applied (Guido Tosello, Costa, & Hansen, 2011): 
• a micro cavity partially filled in subsequent steps (the so-called short shot method) 
• flow-melt visualization method using a in-cavity high speed camera 
• the use of flow marker positions to trace the evolution of the flow front 
• the comparison of process parameters levels sampled during the process (i.e. injec-
tion pressure) 
 
When comparing experiments and simulations an important process parameter is the in-
jection pressure. However, simulation and IM machine pressure are not identical. In the 
simulation the injection pressure is calculated at the injection location, whereas in the ma-
chine the injection pressure is typically measured as the piston injection pressure. As ex-
plained in (224 pressure profile) there is a pressure drop caused by the flow resistance. 
Therefore, it is important to know the exact nozzle geometry and include it in the simula-
tion (Guido Tosello et al., 2011). 
 
It is important when simulating precision-IM processes to include the entire system 
around the part. Due to the size of the part at conventional IM, e.g. the feed system 
(sprue, runner, gate), has little influence on produced part. But as the part at µIM is so 
small the feed system influences the process. This also applies for small parts with micro 
features.

Relevant elements to include in the simulation are (Marhöfer et al., 2013): 
• actual cavity instead of part 
• feed system 
• mould 
• actual dimensions 
• actual material 
• real cooling channel layout 
• real cartridge heaters 
 
 
There exists a trade-off between accurate simulations versus long computational time. 
 
At a certain point you can invest a lot of computational time only improving the accuracy 
by a small amount. Therefore, the simulated model can ‘advantageously’ be simplified 
without major difference will occur in the results but saving a lot of time. It is important 
to consider what effect your simplification inflicts. Only features with low influence on 
the outcome of the simulation is appropriate to omit or change (Marhöfer et al., 2013). 
 
The mesh is important in obtaining an accurate simulation of micro parts. From the very 
beginning it is ideal to have a fine mesh of the parts. Parameters to take into account are 
(Marhöfer et al., 2013): 
• the global edge length for general accuracy 
• the chord height for the reproducibility of curved surfaces  
• the merge tolerance for micro features 
• the bias ratio for thin sections.

3 Production of  IM inserts 
There are numerous instances in which an insert can be positioned into a mould to create 
a specific part without having to manufacture an entirely new mould. Inserts are inter-
changeable and therefore easy to replace when needed.  
 
The following properties are required in order to achieve a quality mould insert (Yi Quin, 
2010) 
• The yield stress of the mould material at the maximum moulding temperature has 
to be significantly higher than the stress effected by the moulding force. 
• To avoid any bending and to ensure the greatest possible evenness of the mould, 
the residual stress inside the mould, caused by the fabrication process, should be 
reduced to a minimum. 
• The mould material should show chemical resistance against the polymer. 
• There should be a high heat conductivity of the mould material to reduce the heat-
ing and cooling times. 
• For cost effectiveness the lifetime of the mould should be extended over many cy-
cles. 
• To support successful demoulding, the surface roughness, especially of vertical 
sidewalls, should be reduced to an unavoidable minimum. 
• Demoulding angles are advantageous because they facilitate demoulding. In con-
trast, under- cuts prevent the successful demoulding of micro-structures. Even 
small undercuts in the sub-micron range can increase demoulding forces signifi-
cantly.

3.1 Conventional inserts 
There are several limitations associated with conventional manufacturing processes. Most 
of the technologies and methods are developed from human labour and are traditionally 
made by hand. This in itself trails for strong inherent path dependency that inhibits inno-
vation. Some of the constraints in conventional manufacturing technology include: 
• Expensive tooling 
• Fixtures  
• The need for assembly for complex parts 
• Expensive and time consuming 
o Change of machine (e.g. turning to milling) 
o Change of working tool (e.g. size of drill) 
o Change of part fixation 
• Up to 90% waste material 
 
Micro manufacturing of complex 3D microstructures proves challenging even though 
many processes are available for such tasks. Processes include soft lithography, laser pho-
toablation, localized electrochemical deposition and the LIGA process. Although all of 
these processes are capable of producing components with different 3D micro structures, 
improvements need to be made to optimize such processes. Attempts have been made to 
optimize these processes but some challenges remain. 
 
The processes mentioned are all capable of developing 2.5D microstructures with unsuc-
cessful attempts at producing true 3D structures. Of the four processes, the LIGA pro-

with the use of masked X-ray radiation. The disadvantage of use the LIGA process is that 
the X-ray radiation necessary for this process is expensive which is why is not as com-
monly used (Lantada et al., 2015).  
 
Electrochemical fabrication (EFAB) is a process developed which resembles an improved 
LIGA process (Vaezi et al., 2013). EFAB is capable of producing complicated 3D

The density of an object describes its mass per unit volume. The density can best be de-

air and secondly when fully immersed/covered in a liquid of a known density. 
 
HTM140 consists of up to 80% of acrylated monomer. Monomer is a molecule which 
combined with other monomers constitute/forms polymers. 
Polymers have low densities as they primary are made of light carbon and hydrogen at-
oms in low density amorphous or semi-crystalline packings. 
 
The mould density for HTM140 is given in its Safety Data Sheets (Appendix XX) and is 
1.08-1.12 g/cm3 at 25°C [LINK]. The mean/average value of the interval is used:

loaded in tension. Plastics materials can  usually resist more compressive load. 
The elastic modulus for a material is determined by the inclination of the stress-strain

7.1.2 ABS tests 
After confirming decent parts could be produced with the printed insert on the injection 
side of the mould using a LDPE, the material was changed into the stiffer amorphous 
polymer acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS) - the same material as conventional LEGO 
bricks are made of. ABS has a lower melt flow value than LDPE, and using a material 
with lower melt flow value for injection moulding applies more stress to the mould insert. 
 
ABS is an amorphous polymer which have lower melt flow values than semi-crystalline 
polymers like PE. Amorphous polymers have a board softening temperature (Tg, glass 
temperature) range that allows material to soften gradually, melt and flow without prema-
turely solidifying.  
The melting temperature of ABS is also higher than LDPE. This is generally valid for (the 
case) when comparing amorphous polymer with semi-crystalline polymer. The melting 
temperature of polymers depends of the structure of the polymer. Chain stiffness has a 
pounced effect and the stiffness is controlled by the ease of rotation about the chemical 
bonds around the chain. Double bonds in the backbone will increase the melt tempera-
ture, as will bulky side groups and polar grounds. 
 
A few incomplete shots using the same setup combination of one HTM140 and one brass 
insert halves were produced in ABS. Unfortunately, the sprue bushing in the IM machine 
broke after a few shots (see Figure 30). This was due to a too long circle time (couple of 
minutes) making the material in the nozzle start solidify. When the following batch was 
initiated the frozen polymer (material below solidification temperature) was pressed into

7.3.2 Conclusion 
The simulation process of Experiment 1 has been an iterative process where there gradu-
ally and continuously has been implemented more features and in-depth analysis options 
in ASMI.  
 
During the simulations additional/new dataset has been implemented continuously as 
more information of the process was obtained, such as specific IM machine parameters, 
IM machine geometry, and specific materials used.

with the experiment.

ever it is important to be aware of the potentials in the different mesh types so advanta-
geous simulations can be conducted. 
 
The fact that only few dataset was obtained from the experiment has resulted in a time-
consuming simulation process. At the same time, it has also been a very educational pro-
cess to examine the implementation options systematic in the attempt of obtaining im-
provements and an accurate simulation. Thorough preparation and detailed knowledge of 
all parameters is paramount to successful simulations.

8.1.1 Insert design changes 
The depth of the cavity of the injection-half and ejector-half was altered. This was done 
in order to ensure that after the cavity was filled with molten material, the final solid 
product would remain attached to the ejector mould half. 
[Figure showing parting line asymmetry] 
 
The gate and runner layout had been redesigned to exclusively being placed in the ejector-
half of the inserts. 
 
Instead of having a uniform inlet from sprue to part, the cross-sectional dimension of the 
runner was larger and few millimetres before the mould cavity of the medallion design 
was reached the cross-section dimension was designed smaller thus acting as an actual 
gate. This was done in an attempt to decrease the pressure within the insert by improving 
the material flow with the larger gate and runner design (Stratasys, n.d.). 
 
The purpose of the gate design was to ensure that the flow of the material was fast 
enough to fill the mould cavity as well as compensate for thermal shrinkage. The design 
used also allowed for rapid solidification of the product, short production time, and ease 
of demoulding from the ejector half of the mould inserts. Figure 42 shows the improved

8.1.2.3 Roundness error definitions 
In order to determine the minimum zone circle (MZC), two circles are drawn and used as 
a reference for measuring roundness error. 
One circle is drawn outside the roundness profile and the other circle is drawn inside the 
roundness profile. The roundness error is the difference between the radiuses of the two 
circles. 
 
Ideal number of roundness measuring points 
To determine the roundness of an object a number of reference points on the circular 
edge is needed. Normally one would run a series of measurements starting with measur-
ing e.g. 20 points to determine an object’s roundness. 10 cycles of measurements with this 
setting (20 points) would be conducted in order to take the machine’s margin of error 
into account. The number of measuring points would then be increased with 1 point hav-

8.3 Initial testing and adjustment 
Pre-adjustment 
Before the actual experiments could take place, it was observed that none of the inserts in 
HTM140 material fitted the master mould in the IM machine as they were received by the 
manufacturer. Time was lost due to adjusting the geometry of the inserts until they all fit-
ted the master mould. 
 
• The printed inserts’ holes at the corners to fasten the insert to the master mould 
were approximately 0,3 mm too small and misaligned with up to 0,5 mm in a 
wrong position (due to warpage from the printing process). These holes were 
therefore drilled in order to fit the mould. 
• The holes for ejector pins needed a draft angle for easy mounting. 
• The flatness of the top and bottom surface was uneven (due to warpage from the 
printing process) and both insert halves needed to be polished manually and with a 
water-based machine. 
• Pressure tape was applied between the insert and master mould surface to in-
crease lifetime and reduce pressure concentration. 
• The insert was mounted with low and wide rings between the screw and insert to 
decrease the tension and pressure on the insert.

pressure concentration, as well as to compensate for the reduced part thickness. The pres-
sure tape had a layer thickness of 0.2 mm. 
 
During the test of 1AB a reasonable process condition in terms of cooling time and cycle 
time was investigated. It was found out that with a cooling time of 50 seconds the parts 
were solidified enough to be ejected without major deformations and by having a cycle 
time of 300 seconds (5 min) a steady mould temperature of ~33,5°C was maintained just 
before the start of a new cylce. The mould. 
The mould temperature was measured at mould opening to be consistent within the range 
70,4 and 78,9°C. This was measured immediately after the mould opening which occurred 
55 seconds inside the cycle time. (Inj.Time=~2, Pack.Time=3, cool time =50).

8.5 Insert measurements 
The measurement results of the ejector-half insert are presented in this section. The 
measurements were made on a CMM as described in section 8.1.2.2 Ejector inserts.  
The measurements of the injection-half insert were not available and has thus not in-
cluded as explained in section 8.1.2.1 Injection inserts. 
 
8.5.1 Measurement insecurity 
None of the 10 3D printed inserts had a completely flat bottom or top surface.  
 
The CMM is measuring values by physical contact of a small and sensitive pinhead look-
ing sensor. The pinhead is moved around in 3 axes with very fine and precise control of 
movement. When the pinhead touches a surface to be measured it applies quite strong 
force. This might have resulted in a small and undesired displacement of the inserts with a 
non-uniform surfaces making a small insecurity in measurements. 
 
The exact reason of the uneven surfaces is not known, but is most likely caused by warp-
age during the printing or hardening process.

8.9 Results 
Some of the dimensional insert measurements carried out on the CMM machine had 
larger standard deviation than others. In order to minimise this insecurity of the measure-
ments, the inserts could have been fixed in a mount at the sides avoiding any possible 
movement caused by the uneven bottom surface on the inserts due to warpage. 
 
 
Reasons for uneven measurements:  
1. The inserts used for the first 4 experiments with low cooling temperature was pol-
ished manually with sandpaper. The inserts used for the 4 last experiments with 
high cooling temperature was polished with a water based polishing machine. The 
different polishing approaches might have had a different influence on the inserts 
resulting in different outcomes. 
2. The pressure tape attached between the mould and inserts leads to less efficient 
cooling transfer. 
 
It was observed that the E-tool material does not work with ABS as moulding material 
for injection moulding.

10 Conclusion 
In this project two different approaches have been conducted in order to investigate the 
potential in using AM technology to produce inserts for precision injection moulding: 
pragmatic experiments and theoretical simulations.  
 
Three pragmatic experiments have been carried out at a production facility of an indus-
trial partner. Subsequently theoretical simulations imitating the experimental scenarios 
were performed to evaluate, characterize and possibly predict the performance of the AM 
inserts. 
 
Through out this project 3D printed mould inserts have been used to injection mould a 
series of medallions with microscopic features. The printed moulds were then used with 
injection moulding. 
 
3D printed inserts 
The outer diameter of the CAD model of the medallion was Ø41,5 mm. The average 
value of the printed eight inserts before the experiments was measured to Ø41,12 mm 
this give a deviation of ~1. The same goes for the inner diameter with a CAD value of 
Ø35,00 mm and the average value measured to Ø34,86 mm. Again this gives a difference 
of ~1%. 
The same goes for the entire insert. There were deviations of 0,3 and 0,4 mm in the flat-
ness between the centre and the corners. 
These deviation was so significant that the all of the inserts in HTM140 did not fit in the 
mould block. Therefore, they needed to be post-processed 
This was surprising and unsatisfactory, as the ideal situation would be if there was no 
need for post-processing. 
 
It was also discovered that the material used for the medallions also had a noteworthy im-
pact on the insert. When ABS was used as a moulding material insert of HTM140 broke. 
Therefore, not many experiments were tested with ABS due to a limited quantity of in-
serts inserts. Therefore, more test is needed in order to conclude if ABS is feasible with 
HTM140 inserts. 
 
The rest of the experiment was conduced with LDPE as moulding material. Inserts sur-
vived at least 25 shots with a cycle time of 300 seconds. The long cycle time was caused 
by the poor heat conduction ability of the inserts. Compared with conventional IM pro-
cesses this is not very fast. A typical cycle time for steel inserts is a coupe of seconds. 
Through the experimental study conducted in IM experiment 2 the measurement/statisti-
cal analysis with Minitab shows, that the cavity of the printed mould insert decreases with

creased with an average of 0,3158% (0,1299 mm) and the inner diameter dimension with

Based on the results from this experiments inserts of HTM140 is not suitable as mass 
production tooling material. Further studies is needed in order to conclude something de-
