Abstract 
 
This study addresses a gap in computational pathology by developing an explainable and 
computationally efficient approach for lung cancer classification of two subtypes. We 
processed 14 WSI from two lung cancer subtypes using a workflow that integrates data 
preprocessing with explainable classification methods. By leveraging feature extraction 
techniques from specific (HistoEncoder) and non-specific (VGG16, InceptionV3 and 
ResNet50) pretrained models, our method combines unsupervised and supervised approaches 
to enhance interpretability. This research lays the groundwork for future exploration of feature 
extraction and model optimization, to enhance clinical decisions. 
Dimensionality reduction techniques, including PCA and t-SNE, were employed to analyze 
histopathological features, effectively distinguishing tile types and enhancing clustering 
accuracy with K-means, which identified two clusters corresponding to cancer subtypes. 
We trained and evaluated four supervised machine learning models: XGBoost, LightGBM, 
Artificial Neural Network and Random Forest. All models exhibited moderate to high 
predictive power and consistent classification decisions. Evaluation on an independent test set 
provided a thorough assessment of performance, generalizability and resistance to overfitting. 
We conducted explainability analyses of the supervised methods to elucidate the decision-
making processes of each model. Finally, we compared the supervised models with 
unsupervised methods, aligning our findings with existing literature to highlight the strengths 
and limitations of each approach.

1.Introduction 
 
1.1 Artificial Intelligence in Healthcare 
 
During the last decade, numerous advancements in Artificial Intelligence (AI) have shown significant 
success and promising results across various research fields, including medical imaging and healthcare 
(Borys et al., 2023). The challenges and demands brought on by the recent pandemic have further 
accelerated the integration of AI into healthcare systems, transforming the landscape of medical care. 
AI has had a profound impact on both research and the healthcare industry, particularly in medical 
diagnostics, treatment planning and bioinformatics. 
 
AI algorithms have demonstrated remarkable accuracy in analyzing medical images, such as MRI 
scans and X-rays, often surpassing clinician doctors detecting conditions like tumors and fractures 
(Chaddad et al., 2023). In bioinformatics, AI has been crucial in speeding up genomic research, 
identifying genetic markers linked to diseases, and optimizing drug discovery processes. 
 
As AI continues to advance and find practical applications in healthcare, including bioinformatics, it 
has the potential to revolutionize personalized medicine, enhance diagnostic accuracy, and streamline 
treatment pathways, ultimately leading to more effective and efficient healthcare delivery (Davenport 
& Kalakota, 2019). 
 
1.2 Explainable AI 
 
From a high-level point of view, Explainable AI (XAI) refers to a set of methods that aim to analyze 
the outcomes of AI systems making them understandable to humans. The primary goal of XAI is to 
ensure that the decisions made by AI models are transparent, interpretable, and can be trusted by users 
(Samek et al., 2019). 
 
Explainable AI (XAI) is becoming increasingly crucial as AI systems are more widely adopted in 
critical areas such as healthcare, finance, and autonomous systems. This is particularly important in 
domains like healthcare and finance, where the consequences of AI decisions can be significant, and 
understanding the reasoning behind these decisions is critical for ensuring fairness, accountability, and 
compliance with ethical standards (Ali et al., 2023). Furthermore, XAI is recently introduced to the 
computer science word, and thus is one of the most debatable topics (Ali et al., 2023). 
 
Given its significance, it’s important to clarify and differentiate between the terms Interpretability and 
Explainability. Interpretability refers to the ability to determine the cause-and-effect relationships 
within a machine learning model, or a model that can be understood by a human with any other aids 
such as a simple linear regression model. On the other hand explainability involves understanding how 
and which specific features influence a model's decisions and for that it requires additional techniques 
to be understood by humans (Stiglic et al., 2020).  
 
1.3 Importance of XAI in Health 
XAI is becoming increasingly vital in healthcare because it helps make AI systems more transparent 
and trustworthy. As AI tools start playing a bigger role in clinical decisions, such as diagnosing 
diseases or suggesting treatments, it's crucial that these systems provide clear reasons for their 
recommendations. This transparency not only aids doctors in understanding and validating AI

1.4 Histopathology and Digital Pathology 
 
Histopathology is the medical field that studies tissue characteristics obtained through biopsy and 
processes such as sectioning, staining, and multi-resolution microscopy  (Van De Rijn & Gilks, 2004). 
The output of this process is a tissue slide suitable for microscopy examination or digital analysis. 
 
Pathologists commonly use visual inspection of histopathology slides to evaluate the stage, type, and 
subtypes of lung cancer. However, in very technical situations visual inspection and subjective 
overview of clinicians can lead to misclassification. The two most common subtypes of lung cancer 
are differentiated through careful examination of WSIs, which in many cases can be difficult to 
distinguish (Coudray et al., 2018). 
 
The FDA approved PathAI’s and Philips' digital pathology platform for primary diagnosis of cancer 
on April 1, 2017. This approval marked a significant milestone, allowing pathologists to use digital 
slides for diagnosing cancer in routine clinical practice (Evans et al., 2018). In this foreground the 
application of AI in Medical imaging expanded rapidly, such us to transform the field of digital 
pathology by enhancing diagnostic accuracy, improving efficiency, and enabling more sophisticated 
analysis of complex data. In particular, AI algorithms and Deep Neural Network can analyze digital 
pathology slides to identify patterns indicative of different cancer subtypes, such as adenocarcinoma 
and squamous cell carcinoma, with remarkable precision (Coudray et al., 2018). 
 
All in all, deep learning algorithms, along with annotated maps that highlight specific areas within the 
images responsible for classification, can significantly aid pathologists in distinguishing between very 
similar cancer subtypes that require different treatment approaches. This advancement pushes 
precision medicine to a new level by enhancing diagnostic accuracy and ensuring more tailored and 
effective treatment strategies.

1.6 Whole Slide Images  
 
Whole Slide Imaging process involves the creation of high-resolution digital representations of entire 
tissue slides by scanning tissue biopsies at various magnification levels. The tissue samples are stained 
with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E). These images typically have a resolution that ranges from Χ 20 to 
Χ 40 magnification, producing files with sizes often exceeding several gigabytes. For instance, a WSI 
can be about 1 to 5 gigapixels in size, translating to an average width of 10.000 to 50.000 pixels, 
depending on the slide's resolution and the scanner used. WSIs are organized into multiple levels of 
detail, allowing zooming from low-resolution overviews to high-resolution microscopic views (M. 
Lee, 2023b). 
 
Processing these large and complex images poses several challenges. The big file sizes necessitate 
substantial storage capacity and computational power for both processing and classification. Image 
tiling and patch-based approaches are commonly used to handle the data, where the whole slide is 
divided into smaller, manageable pieces. However, this method can introduce issues related to 
maintaining context and ensuring consistency across patches. Variability in tissue staining and 
scanning artifacts further complicates the development of robust classification algorithms (Rizzo et 
al., 2022). 
 
The initial approach to this challenge is to apply deep learning to lung cancer WSIs, following a 
pipeline inspired by Wang et al. in Classification and Mutation Prediction from Non-Small Cell Lung 
Cancer Histopathology Images using Deep Learning (Coudray et al., 2018). A critical component of 
their technique involves practical image preprocessing, where large WSIs are partitioned into smaller 
tiles. Only tiles that contain at least 75% tissue are selected for further analysis. Tissue detection in 
this preprocessing stage is accomplished through histogram-based thresholding of pixel statistics in 
the gray scale. This preprocessing strategy helps in efficient management of the big amount of data 
generated by WSIs, facilitating more accurate and computationally feasible exploratory analysis and 
classification strategy of lung cancer.

1.7 Multiple Instance Learning 
 
In the context of histopathological analysis, WSIs are typically divided into smaller, more manageable 
regions known as tiles for computational tasks. This division enables the efficient processing and 
analysis of the images using machine learning models. Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) is a powerful 
framework particularly suited for this scenario, where each WSI is treated as a bag containing multiple 
instances, represented by the individual tiles. In MIL, the objective is to predict the label of the entire 
slide based on the aggregated information from its constituent tiles, rather than predicting labels for 
each tile independently. This approach is beneficial in medical imaging, where the presence of disease 
might be localized to specific regions within a slide, and not every tile may contain relevant 
pathological features (Gadermayr & Tschuchnig, 2024). Therefore, the MIL framework allows for the 
identification and aggregation of key pathological patterns across the tiles to make accurate predictions 
at the slide level, thereby enhancing the interpretability and accuracy of diagnostic models. 
 
1.8 Research Gap 
Effective diagnostic tools and transparent decision-making are crucial for improving the management 
and outcomes of lung cancer patients. Recent research has demonstrated the potential of XAI in 
enhancing the classification of WSIs for lung cancer diagnosis (Abdeltawab et al., 2022). While these 
advancements, most studies focus on supervised models or deep learning architectures like 
Convolutional Neural Networks. These approaches often prioritize accuracy but offer limited insights 
into the decision-making process of the model. Few studies explore the integration of unsupervised 
techniques with supervised methods to create a comprehensive framework for lung cancer 
classification. 
Accurate classification of pathological stages in WSIs remains challenging, particularly when features 
are unclear or coexisting lung conditions complicate the diagnosis. Early detection of conditions such 
as early-stage lung cancer or interstitial lung disease is critical, as these conditions are often linked to 
poor respiratory function and increased metastasis risk (Travis et al., 2015). In such cases, where timely 
and tailored decisions are crucial, a classification model that incorporates explainability could 
significantly aid in personalized medical management. 
Under this scope, despite the potential of unsupervised methods for pattern recognition, limited 
research has investigated how combining unsupervised and supervised approaches can improve both 
classification accuracy and model interpretability; an essential aspect for clinical decision making. 
Additionally, training these models can be computationally intensive, highlighting the need for more 
robust and agile options that maintain efficiency without sacrificing performance. Furthermore, the 
use of pre-trained models like VGG16 for feature extraction in this hybrid framework remains 
underexplored.

1.9 Aim 
This study aims to investigate the application of both unsupervised and supervised machine learning 
techniques for lung cancer classification using Whole Slide Images. Unsupervised methods, including 
clustering algorithms, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and t-Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-
SNE), will be employed for exploratory analysis and pattern recognition, alongside with feature 
extraction from pre-trained Deep Neural Networks. Supervised learning approaches such as eXtreme 
Gradient Boosting (XGBoost), Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM), Artificial Neural 
Networks (ANN) and Random Forest will be trained and validated on this dataset. The performance 
of these models will be assessed in terms of classification accuracy, generalizability, explainability 
and computational efficiency. All in all, the goal is to develop an accurate, efficient and interpretable 
model for classifying lung cancer in whole-slide images. Focusing on its clinical application as a 
reliable and explainable diagnostic tool that provides actionable insights for healthcare professionals.

2. Machine Learning and Computational Background 
 
2.1 Introduction to Convolutional Neural Networks  
 
A Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is a deep learning model mostly used for computer vision 
tasks. CNNs have revolutionized the field of computer vision by enabling machines to understand and 
interpret visual information with remarkable accuracy. CNNs leverage layers of convolutional filters 
to detect patterns, such as edges and textures, which are then combined to recognize more complex 
features in images (Sharma et al., 2018). This hierarchical approach allows CNNs to learn from raw 
pixel data and perform tasks such as image classification, object detection and segmentation. 
 
Convolutional layers are the fundamental building blocks of CNNs. These layers apply a set of 
different filters (kernels) to the input image, which slide over the image pixels to detect various 
features. Each filter is typically a small square, often 3x3 pixels in size, where the intensity of each 
pixel inside the kernel is learned during the training process through backpropagation. Initially, the 
filter contains random pixel values, but after training, it evolves to have optimal values that can detect 
specific features in the image. 
 
 
Figure 2.1. Convolutional process. 
To apply the filter to the input image, the filter is overlaid onto the image, and each overlapping pixel 
is multiplied with the corresponding pixel in the filter. The products are then summed to produce a 
single value, known as the dot product. This process is repeated across the entire image, and each 
resulting dot product is stored in a feature map; a matrix containing the convolved feature values. A 
bias term is also added to each value in the feature map. This operation of computing the dot product 
between the filter and the image and storing the results in a feature map is what gives CNNs their 
name, as the filter is said to be "convolved" with the input. 
 
Max-pooling layers follow convolutional layers and are used to reduce the spatial dimensions of the 
feature maps, making the network more computationally efficient while retaining important 
information. Max-pooling works by sliding a window (usually 2 x 2) over the feature map and taking 
the maximum value within each window. 
 
 
 
Figure 2.2. Max Pooling Process 
Finally, fully connected layers, typically placed at the end of the network, combine the features 
extracted by the convolutional and pooling layers to make the final predictions. In this stage, each 
max-pooled value serves as an input to the fully connected layer, similar to how a typical neural 
network operates. In these layers, every neuron is connected to the previous layer, enabling the model

output a probability distribution, identifying the most likely class. 
 
2.2 Feature Extraction 
 
Feature extraction is a key technique in machine learning and computer vision that involves pulling 
out important characteristics from raw data to simplify and enhance analysis (BÜYÜKKEÇECİ & 
OKUR, 2023). By transforming complex data into a more manageable set of features, this process 
allows models to focus on the most relevant aspects, improving their accuracy and efficiency. In the 
context of medical imaging, transfer learning has proven to be highly effective, as demonstrated in 
several studies (Salehi et al., 2023). 
 
Using pre-trained models takes this a step further. These models, which have been trained on large and 
diverse datasets, come with a set of powerful, pre-learned features that can be applied to new problems. 
This not only speeds up development but also boosts performance, especially when working with 
smaller datasets. In this context, incorporating feature extraction with pre-trained models will leverage 
these advanced features, making it easier to analyze the data and achieve better results (Al-Malla et 
al., 2022). 
 
To adjust this technique to digital image analysis pre-trained Deep Learning Models are explored to 
perform feature extraction. In deep learning, particularly with image data, feature extraction is the 
process of identifying and extracting important visual information from images to facilitate more 
effective analysis and classification. This involves using neural networks to transform raw image 
pixels into high-level features that capture essential patterns and structures. Pre-trained models, such 
as those developed using architectures like VGG, ResNet, or Inception, have already been trained on 
extensive image datasets like ImageNet. These models learn rich feature representations that can be 
directly applied to new image-related tasks. By leveraging the knowledge acquired from vast image 
datasets, we can extract rich representations that encode valuable information about the underlying 
visual content (Simon & V, 2020). All in all, this creates a latent space using as encoder the pre-trained 
DNN high representation of the original image but within a smaller dimensional space. Any ordinary 
pre-trained classification model like VGG or ResNet will extract different features of the image on 
each layer. While the earlier layers typically respond to more basic and simple features like edges, the 
deeper layers extract more specific features. 
 
Taking all this into account, three different pre-trained models for feature extraction in this stage of 
our project are analyzed. These models, which are well known deep neural networks (DNNs), have 
been trained on the extensive ImageNet dataset. Although they all benefit from this large-scale 
training, each model has a distinct architecture, which means they learn and represent different features 
from images. By examining these varied models, we aim to explore how their unique architecture 
changes the feature extraction on the classification task of lung cancer slides. More specifically, for 
this purpose the features from each tile via VGG16, Inception V3 and ResNet50 are extracted. 
 
Another interesting approach is to extract features from a model trained specifically on histopathology 
data, such as WSIs of various cancer types, and compare these features with those from pre-trained 
models developed on broader, less specialized datasets like ImageNet. The specialized histopathology 
model, with its focus on detailed tissue structures and cancerous patterns, offers insights tailored to 
medical imaging that might not be captured by general models. By contrasting the features and visual 
representations from these domain-specific models with those of pre-trained DNN trained on more

2.3 VGG16 Architecture 
 
Among the various CNN architectures, VGG16 stands out due to its simplicity and effectiveness. 
Introduced by the Visual Geometry Group (VGG) at the University of Oxford in 2014. VGG16 is a 
deep learning model designed specifically for image classification. The architecture was named 
"VGG" after the group’s name and "16" because it consists of 16 weight layers, including 
convolutional and fully connected layers. More specifically, VGG16 has three fully connected and 13 
convolution layers, carrying the ReLU tradition with AlexNet (Linkon et al., 2021). VGG16 
demonstrated that a deep network with a simple, uniform architecture could achieve state-of-the-art 
performance on the ImageNet dataset, which contains millions of images across thousands of 
categories. More specifically, VGG16 model trained on over 14 million images resulting in detecting 
effectively generic features from 1000 classes images. The model achieves 92.7% top-5 test accuracy 
in ImageNet, thus it can generalize to unseen data. 
 
The VGG16 model consists of 16 layers that include 13 convolutional layers and 3 fully connected 
layers. The convolutional layers use small 3x3 filters, which slide over the input image to detect various 
features, while the number of filters increases progressively across the layers, allowing the network to 
capture more complex patterns. After every few convolutional layers, max-pooling layers are applied 
to reduce the spatial dimensions of the feature maps, making the network more efficient while retaining 
important information. Finally, the fully connected layers at the end of the network combine all the 
extracted features to classify the input into different categories, with the last layer using a softmax 
activation function to output the probabilities of each class (Ferguson et al., 2017). This simple yet 
effective architecture was a breakthrough in computer vision, achieving state-of-the-art performance 
on image recognition tasks.

2.4 InceptionV3 Architecture 
 
InceptionV3 is a 48-layer deep convolutional neural network that has been pre-trained on more than a 
million images from the ImageNet database. This pre-trained model is capable of classifying images 
into 1.000 different object categories, including items like keyboards, mice, pencils and various

images. InceptionV3 takes images with an input size of 299 X 299 pixels. The network's architecture 
is divided into two main parts: the first part extracts general features from the input images, while the 
second part classifies these images based on the extracted features (Szegedy et al., 2016). Similar to 
how VGG16 is used, InceptionV3 is utilized for feature extraction purposes. The output feature vector 
contains 2.048 features and the last fully connected classification layer is removed. 
 
2.5 ResNet50 Architecture 
 
ResNet50 is a convolutional neural network with a different architecture that is part of the Residual 
Networks (ResNet) family, introduced by Microsoft Research. The key innovation in ResNet50 is the 
use of residual learning, which helps in addressing the vanishing gradient problem that can hinder the 
training of deep networks. ResNet50 consists of 50 layers, including convolutional layers, pooling 
layers and fully connected layers. A distinctive feature of ResNet is its use of shortcut connections that 
bypass one or more layers, allowing gradients to flow more easily during backpropagation (He et al., 
2015). This structure enables the construction of much deeper networks while maintaining efficient 
training and improving accuracy. ResNet50 has been widely used in various computer vision tasks due 
to its robust performance in image classification, object detection, and feature extraction.

2.8 Exploratory Analysis and Dimensionality Reduction 
 
Whole slide images, with their high resolution, represent a high-dimensional data space, as each image 
consists of two dimensional pixel values that together generate vast amounts of data. Due to this high 
dimensionality, effective analysis requires dimensionality reduction techniques. Dimensionality 
reduction is the process of transforming high dimensional data into a lower dimensional space while 
retaining as much of the original information as possible (Jolliffe & Cadima, 2016). This helps simplify 
data processing, reduce computational costs and mitigate the risk of overfitting in machine learning 
models. 
 
2.9 Principal Component Analysis 
 
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a widely used method for this purpose, aiming to reduce the 
dimensionality of the data while preserving as much of the original variance as possible (Ng, 2017). 
PCA works by capturing the linear structures within the data, identifying the directions, or principal 
components, along which the variance is maximized. These principal components are linear 
combinations of the original features, are orthogonal to each other, and are ordered based on the 
amount of variance they capture. PCA is a powerful tool in simplifying high dimensional data, 
capturing linear relationships (Jolliffe & Cadima, 2016). 
 
2.10 t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding 
 
Another widely used dimensionality reduction technique is t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor 
Embedding (t-SNE), which is particularly useful for exploring and visualizing complex and high 
dimensional data. t-SNE maps data into a lower-dimensional space, such as two or three dimensions, 
while preserving the local structure and relationships between data points (Meng et al., 2020). Unlike 
PCA, which focuses on capturing global variance in the data, t-SNE emphasizes preserving the local 
relationships between data points in the high-dimensional space. This allows t-SNE to reveal non-
linear patterns and clusters that may be hidden when using linear dimensionality reduction methods 
like PCA. 
 
2.11 Unsupervised Clustering 
 
Unsupervised clustering techniques play a crucial role in analyzing high dimensional data. 
Unsupervised clustering is a method used to group data points into clusters based on their similarities 
without prior labels. This approach allows for the discovery of inherent structures and patterns within 
the data. Common unsupervised clustering methods include hierarchical clustering, density-based 
clustering and K-means clustering. These methods help in understanding the data's underlying 
structure and can reveal insights that are not immediately apparent through other analytical techniques 
(Jain, 2010). The exploratory analysis offers valuable insights into how the tiles, and by extension the 
whole slide images, are organized in a lower dimensional space, as mentioned earlier. After feature 
extraction using pre-trained models and dimensionality reduction through techniques like PCA, it is 
common in image analysis to combine these methods with unsupervised clustering (Katkar et al., 
2015). This approach allows to assess how effectively the data can be grouped into distinct clusters.

2.12 K-means Algorithm 
 
K-means clustering is a widely used unsupervised learning algorithm that partitions data into a 
predefined number of clusters by minimizing the variance within each cluster. It iteratively assigns 
each data point to the nearest cluster center and updates the cluster centers based on the mean of the 
points assigned to each cluster. K-means is known for its simplicity and efficiency, making it suitable 
for large datasets, though it requires specifying the number of clusters beforehand and can be sensitive 
to the initial placement of cluster centroids (Ikotun et al., 2023).

difference between the average distance to points within the same cluster (a) and the average distance

2.14 Supervised Machine Learning Models 
 
In digital pathology, the classification of whole slide images is often framed as a binary classification 
problem, enabling the differentiation between various conditions such as LUAD. To effectively tackle 
this classification task, various machine learning algorithms can be employed as powerful tools.  
 
Among these, the Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM), Extreme Gradient Boosting 
(XGBoost), Feedforward Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs), and Random Forests have gained 
prominence. LightGBM and XGBoost are particularly valued for their capacity to handle complex data 
relationships and manage large high-dimensional datasets efficiently, achieving strong performance in 
terms of accuracy, scalability and time-efficiency (Chen & Guestrin, 2016; Ke et al., 2017). ANNs 
offer flexibility in modeling non-linear relationships within the data, while Random Forests provide 
robustness and interpretability through their ensemble approach, enhancing prediction accuracy (Sun 
et al., 2024). The selection of these models underscores their effectiveness in addressing various 
complexities in data and their proven performance in classification tasks, aiming to ensure accurate 
and reliable classification of WSI tiles. 
 
LightGBM is a gradient-boosted decision tree (GBDT) framework designed for high efficiency, 
especially with large datasets. It differs from traditional GBDT by using a histogram-based method 
that bins continuous features into discrete values, significantly speeding up computation. LightGBM 
builds an ensemble of decision trees sequentially, with each tree correcting the errors of the previous 
one. Its leaf-wise growth strategy enhances accuracy while reducing memory usage, making it well 
suited for large-scale, high-dimensional data. This speed and efficiency make LightGBM ideal for 
real-time applications in digital pathology. 
 
XGBoost is a powerful variant of gradient-boosted decision trees, known for its performance and 
speed. It uses a parallel tree boosting algorithm that leverages multiple cores for efficient training. 
Unlike LightGBM's leaf-wise growth, XGBoost uses level-wise tree growth. Its built-in regularization 
helps prevent overfitting, making it robust against noisy data. XGBoost’s capability to handle sparse 
data and missing values enhances its flexibility, making it a promising tool for classification tasks in 
digital pathology. 
 
Feedforward ANNs are a class of neural networks where information flows in one direction; from 
input to output; without any cycles. An ANN consists of layers of interconnected neurons, with each 
layer transforming the data through weighted connections and activation functions. This structure 
allows ANNs to capture complex, non-linear relationships in the data. While typically simpler than 
other deep learning architectures, ANNs are flexible and can be tailored for various tasks, including 
binary classification. In digital pathology, ANNs can effectively analyze image features and contribute 
to accurate diagnosis. 
 
Random Forest is an ensemble learning method that constructs multiple decision trees during training 
and merges their predictions to improve accuracy and control overfitting. Each tree is built using a 
subset of the training data and a random subset of features, which helps to create diverse models. The 
final classification is determined by majority voting among the trees, making Random Forests robust 
and interpretable. This model excels in handling high-dimensional data and is particularly useful in 
digital pathology for classifying WSIs, where it can effectively leverage the information from multiple 
image features to provide reliable predictions.

2.15 Training and Validation 
 
Nested validation is a technique for evaluating machine learning models that involves two loops: an 
outer loop for performance assessment and an inner loop for hyperparameter tuning. In the outer loop, 
the dataset is split into training and testing sets to evaluate model performance on unseen data. The 
inner loop further divides the training set for cross-validation to optimize hyperparameters. This 
approach reduces the risk of overfitting and provides a more reliable estimate of a model's predictive 
performance especially, with high dimensional data (Zhong et al., 2020). 
 
Leave-One-Out Validation (LOO) is a type of cross-validation where each observation is used once as 
a test set while the remaining data serves as the training set. This process is repeated for each instance 
in the dataset, maximizing the training data for model learning. While LOO is effective for small 
datasets, it can be computationally intensive for larger ones. Nonetheless, it offers a thorough 
assessment of model performance and predictive ability (Bradshaw et al., 2023).

2.17 Statistical Testing 
 
McNemar's test is employed to compare the performance of two classification models, specifically 
assessing the statistical significance of differences in their accuracy. This non-parametric test is 
designed for paired data, focusing on instances where the models provide differing predictions. By 
constructing a 2x2 contingency table, the test categorizes cases into four outcomes: instances where 
both models agree (correct predictions), where one model is correct while the other is incorrect, and 
where both models are wrong. McNemar's test is particularly valuable for evaluating classifiers on the 
same dataset, as it provides a statistical measure of whether the observed performance differences are 
likely due to chance (Sundjaja et al., 2024). From the contingency table we can compute the Mc-
Nemars’s score and thus the p-value into a confidence interval. A p-value > 0.05 indicates that we fail 
to reject the null hypothesis, suggesting that any observed differences in performance could be 
attributed to random variation rather than a significant discrepancy. Conversely, a p-value < 0.05 
suggests that the difference in performance is statistically significant.

3. Materials and Methods 
Developing a machine learning pipeline for WSI datasets is a challenging task, requiring significant 
engineering skills and thorough knowledge of model hyperparameters. The complexity of WSIs adds 
extra layers of effort in data retrieval, preprocessing and normalization, making it more demanding 
than typical image processing models used for standard classification tasks. 
For our research, we utilized custom scripts developed in Python programming language (specifically 
Python 3.11.4) to analyze the data. These scripts are uploaded in GitHub’s student profile under the 
Master Thesis repository (https://github.com/kgoras1/Master_thesis_repo), where all the details are 
presented in Section 7. This organization not only ensures transparency but also allows others to easily 
follow our methodology. 
3.1 Strategy for Dataset Creation 
 
For this study, a total of 14 WSIs from two different lung cancer types were analyzed. Seven WSIs 
were selected from LSCC and seven from LUAD, ensuring an equal distribution between the two 
classes (50% each). Despite the limited number of WSIs, each image was tiled into approximately 
20.000 smaller images of 512 x 512 pixels, captured in the Red-Green-Blue (RGB) color space. This 
tiling process significantly increased the dataset size, making it sufficient for training and validating 
machine learning models. 
 
All WSIs were sourced from publicly available repositories: the Cancer Imaging Archive (TCIA) and 
the Clinical Proteomic Tumor Analysis Consortium (CPTAC). Specifically, LSCC images were taken 
from the CPTAC-LSCC cohort, and LUAD images from the CPTAC-LUAD cohort. Data retrieval 
was facilitated using the Aspera Connect transfer tool. 
 
The WSIs used in this study contained no identifiable patient information, and no associated metadata 
(such as medical history or health status) was available. Consequently, only the images themselves 
were used for analysis, without any patient-specific details. 
 
3.2 Dataset Handling 
A WSI is produced by a microscope that scans a slide and stitches together smaller images to form a 
large composite image. This process can involve merging scanned square tiles or scanned strips into 
the final whole-slide image. At times, the individual smaller images can still be seen. These images 
are often saved in the SVS format, a file type not typically supported by common software tools. The 
average SVS file size is typically approximately 1 GB, making data handling and preprocessing 
essential. 
To manage the files size and format, we utilized the OpenSlide Python library (Version: 1.3.1), a tool 
specifically designed for reading and manipulating SVS images. To streamline the initial dataset 
exploration, we developed a custom script, "Thumbnail&slides_properties.py", which automates key 
preprocessing steps. This script uses OpenSlide to generate thumbnail images at 1/32 the original size 
and saves them as JPEG files for quick visualization. Additionally, it records key slide properties (e.g., 
dimensions and magnification levels) in a text file for future reference. This preprocessing step was 
vital in reducing the computational burden of working directly with large WSIs and helped ensure that 
the data contain meaningful content and document their essential image properties.

3.3 Generate tiles from WSIs 
To perform detailed analysis on WSIs, we divided each image into smaller, non-overlapping tiles using

3.4 Preprocessing 
To effectively analyze WSIs, it was important to first address how to filter out or discard tiles that 
contained little or no tissue content. Often, a significant portion of the image consists of background 
areas generated during the scanning process. To handle this characteristic, we implemented a 
systematic approach using a custom Python script, “Generate_Tiles.py” for generating deep zoom 
tiles from SVS files. This script helps identify and extract tiles with meaningful tissue content, focusing 
on three main goals: 1. discarding non-informative data (background or artifacts) to optimize 
computational resources, 2. creating a consistent dataset to reduce image variability, and 3. enabling 
more efficient downstream processing (Faghani et al., 2023) 
The first step involved generating tiles from a WSI with dimensions of 512 x 512 pixels and no overlap. 
Using the Python Pillow package, tiles were saved and visually inspected, allowing us to categorize 
them into three distinct types: 1. tiles consisting entirely of white space, which we refer to as blank 
tiles; 2. tiles with minimal tissue information, which we refer as partial tiles; and 3. tiles containing 
substantial tissue content, which refers to full accordingly.  This initial classification was essential for 
subsequent algorithm development aimed at filtering relevant tiles. Blank and partial tiles were 
discarded, while full tiles were retained for further analysis. 
For each 512 x 512 tile, the region was extracted and converted into RGB format for initial processing, 
generating arrays of shape (512, 512, 3), representing the rows, columns, and color channels (red, 
green, and blue). The RGB values, ranging from 0 (black) to 255 (white), allowed us to distinguish 
intensity variations across the tiles. Next, the tiles were organized into three separate directories based 
on their class: blank, partial, and full. This manual sorting process allowed us to explore the 
characteristics of each class, leading to better insights for fine-tuning the tile-filtering algorithm. 
For a more detailed analysis, we computed statistics for each tile, focusing on the grayscale content. 
Each tile converted from RGB tile to grayscale using the convert ('L') method, a function from the 
Pillow library, where 'L' stands for "luminance." This grayscale image simplifies the data by

3.9 Supervised Classification 
 
For this study, we implemented the XGBoost Classifier (XGBClassifier) from the XGBoost library

measure performance based on logarithmic loss. Similarly, the LightGBM Classifier was employed, 
configured with the same 'logloss' metric and utilizing default parameters, including 31 leaves, a 
learning rate of 0.1, and 100 boosting rounds. 
 
We also utilized the Random Forest Classifier from the scikit-learn library, which consisted of 100 
decision trees and a fixed random seed (random_state=42) to ensure consistency and enhance 
accuracy. An Artificial Neural Network (ANN) was designed using the Keras Sequential API, 
comprising an input layer, three hidden layers with 128, 64, and 32 units (with ReLU activation), 
dropout layers to mitigate overfitting, and an output layer with softmax activation for class 
probabilities. The model was compiled using the Adam optimizer and sparse categorical crossentropy 
as the loss function.

4.2 Generate tiles 
Tiling was essential for three reasons: 1. it facilitated effective data processing, as the images are too 
large to serve as a direct input for further analysis without this step 2. it allowed us to focus on 
biologically significant regions by filtering and retaining only tissue-relevant tiles 3. It reduced the 
input size of the data. 
To handle the SVS files and extract tiles for analysis, we selected to use a tile size of 512 x 512 pixels 
with no overlap. The choice of 512 x 512 pixels is primarily driven by a balance between computational 
efficiency and image detail. This tile size captures enough contextual information from the tissue and 
remains manageable in terms of memory and processing. Larger tiles would provide more detailed 
information but could lead to increased computational costs and longer processing times, while smaller 
tiles might lack sufficient context for accurate analysis. Additionally, choosing a no-overlap strategy 
simplifies the extraction process and reduces the risk of introducing redundant or duplicated 
information into the dataset. This approach ensures that each tile represents a distinct, non-overlapping 
segment of the image, which facilitates more straightforward analysis and classification. Although no 
overlap means that some fine details at the edges of tiles might be missed, it minimizes the complexity 
of managing overlapping regions and the potential artifacts that could arise from them. This strategy 
was selected to streamline the analysis while balancing the need for adequate contextual information 
and computational efficiency. Finally, some feature extraction models are capable of handling 512 x 
512 pixel images directly, so we did not downsample the resolution or add a resize step. This is not 
the case for the VGG16 model though. 
The initial step in our process involved generating tiles from one single WSI using a tile size of 512 x 
512 pixels with no overlap. To quantify the results, the initial tiling process yielded a total of 2.684 
tiles from a single WSI of 19.478 (original height) x 20.320 (original width) dimensions. After manual 
classification, the distribution of tiles across the directories was as follows: 1. the blank directory 
contained 1.718 tiles, making up 64% of the total, which highlights the large proportion of background 
content; 2. the partial directory included 90 tiles, representing just 3% of the total, with each containing 
roughly 10-50% tissue content; and 3. the full directory comprised 876 tiles, accounting for 33% of 
the total, with significant tissue information. The considerable number of blank tiles underscores the 
need for efficient filtering methods to focus on the more informative regions of the image.

4.3 Tiling Algorithm 
 
To improve the accuracy of distinguishing tissue from background, we introduced an approach that 
calculates tile statistics based on grayscale content. This method allowed us to simplify the complexity 
of the tiles by converting them from RGB to grayscale, which reduced the channels of the data by 
66.7%, while still preserving key information about pixel intensity. While this approach does remove 
color information, it is well-suited for our primary objective of distinguishing foreground tissue from 
background. By calculating the mean grayscale value and the percentage of white pixels in each tile, 
we assessed how much of each tile contained meaningful tissue versus background. This approach 
provided clearer insight into the tissue distribution across the WSIs, helping us refine the algorithm 
used to filter out non-informative tiles. Importantly, this method not only improves accuracy but is 
also crucial for scalability. For integration into larger preprocessing pipelines with many WSIs and 
tiles, the algorithm must be both accurate and efficient. By focusing on grayscale content, we aimed 
to strike the right balance between precision and speed, ensuring that the process remains fast and 
reliable as the dataset size grows. 
 
We thoroughly examined the distribution of mean values and average background values to assess the 
ROC curve and identify any potential issues. This analysis involved evaluating how these values were 
distributed across different tiles, which allowed us to identify any inconsistencies or anomalies such 
us class imbalance, outliers and noise (potentially from artifacts). By doing so, we ensured that the 
ROC curve was accurately reflecting the performance of our the algorithm, and we were able to address 
any underlying issues that could have affected the validity of the results. 
 
Figure 4.5. Left subplot shows the distribution of mean values for image tiles, with the x-axis representing the tile mean values and the 
y-axis indicating the frequency of those values. The right subplot illustrates the average background across the tiles, with the x-axis 
representing the proportion of background and the y-axis indicating their frequency. 
After calculating and examining the tile statistics, we plotted the ROC curve using the tiles' mean 
grayscale value and average background intensity. The mean value was treated as the threshold for 
classification, and Youden's J statistic was computed to determine the optimal threshold, maximizing 
the difference between the true positive rate (TPR) and false positive rate (FPR) for distinguishing 
between informative and non-informative tiles.

5 Discussion 
 
5.1 Key Findings, Literature Comparison and Limitations 
 
This study proposes a context-aware approach that integrates both unsupervised and supervised 
methods for the explainable classification of lung cancer in whole slide images. Unlike previous works 
that focus solely on classification accuracy, our study aims to address the gap by integrating 
explainable approaches, to improve both transparency and computational efficiency in lung cancer 
diagnosis. This approach contrasts with state-of-the-art black-box models that are difficult to interpret 
in clinical settings. 
 
 A key component of our workflow is the tiling algorithm, which effectively filters and retains only 
tissue-relevant tiles, achieving a precision of 95% and a recall of 91.1% based on a test set of one WSI. 
While similar approaches have been documented in the literature (Coudray et al., 2018), they often 
involve more complex methodologies without presenting corresponding test statistics. Our agile and 
robust method provides clear performance metrics; however, it is important to acknowledge the 
limitations of our algorithm, particularly concerning artifacts such as scanning noise and staining 
irregularities, which were present in other WSI in the dataset. These artifacts can appear in both cancer 
subtypes, potentially introducing noise into the dataset and complicating classification, as the models 
may misinterpret these artifacts as biologically relevant features. 
Another important aspect of our study was the feature extraction process, which combined both 
specific (VGG16, InceptionV3 and ResNet50) and non-specific models (HistoEncoder) to assess the 
==PAGE:66==
Explainable AI in cancer classification of Whole Slide Images 
 
 
66 
exploratory analysis. In the existing literature, researchers typically utilize the Inception V3 
architecture trained from scratch (Coudray et al., 2018) or employ models like ResNet50 for feature 
extraction (Shen et al., 2023). However, there has been a lack of exploration into the comparative 
effectiveness of specific versus non-specific models in this context. By addressing this gap, our 
approach enhances the understanding of how different feature extraction techniques contribute to the 
analysis of cancer subtypes. Our comparative analysis of feature extraction models reveals that VGG16 
provides the best balance of speed and efficiency. VGG16’s architecture allows it to effectively capture 
spatial hierarchies in images, making it more suitable for large-scale analyses where speed is crucial. 
Inception V3 offers a moderate approach, while ResNet50 excels in capturing intricate details but 
demands higher computational costs. HistoEncoder distinguishes itself by extracting highly relevant 
features with minimal complexity, positioning it as a strong candidate for differentiating cancer types 
within our dataset. However, none of the feature extraction models captured all underlying patterns in 
the data.  
The exploratory analysis reveals that the dimensionality reduction techniques applied to the extracted 
features have not been thoroughly explored in literature; the extent of variance retention depends on 
the model used, where retaining too much variance may compromise interpretability, while reducing 
it excessively risks losing critical information. In the context of PCA, we observed that non-tissue tiles 
tend to cluster together, whereas t-SNE captures more intricate details due to its non-linear nature. 
Notably, when projecting all tiles, a discernible clustering pattern into two classes emerged, albeit with 
some degree of overlap. Lastly, more complex models capture and cluster details such as artifacts. 
Understanding the spatial distribution of these classes can improve classification accuracy by 
highlighting areas for potential refinement in feature extraction, such as extending overlapping or 
artifact features. 
Furthermore, unsupervised clustering techniques in the existing literature remain largely unexplored, 
possibly due to the inherent complexity and variability of histopathological data, which can complicate 
the identification of meaningful patterns. Our analysis confirmed that K-means clustering identified 
an optimal number of clusters equal to two, validated by the silhouette score across various feature 
extraction methods. Mutual information scores indicated moderate clustering effectiveness; however, 
when applying majority voting and analyzing contingency tables, we observed that 10 out of 14 slides 
were correctly assigned to their respective clusters, regardless of the feature set used. Larger slides, 
which contain more tiles, tend to distribute more widely across different clusters possibly due to the 
presence of a significant amount of non-cancerous tissue, which may be common across all cancer 
types. This highlights the need for further investigation into the robustness and interpretability of 
clustering approaches within this domain and might include normal tissue sample as well. Training 
and testing using both LOO and holdout methods, K-means clustering on VGG16 features revealed 
that while LSCC tiles displayed clear separation, the dispersion observed among LUAD tiles suggests 
that VGG16 features may not be equally effective for all LUAD samples. Similarly, HistoEncoder 
demonstrated even greater challenges in effectively clustering LUAD tiles in one group. A reasonable 
explanation is that K-means assumes clusters are spherical and evenly distributed, which might not 
align with the irregular and complex nature of histopathological data. As a result, we observed that 
while LSCC tiles clustered well, LUAD tiles showed more dispersion, likely due to the higher 
variability in their patterns and the presence of overlapping normal tissue. 
Supervised learning using features from VGG16 and HistoEncoder, evaluated with various machine 
learning models and nested cross-validation, yielded promising results that align with existing 
literature on their performance. Specifically, (Coudray et al., 2018) utilized an InceptionV3 
architecture trained from scratch on a substantial dataset comprising of nearly 500 WSIs, achieving an 
overall slide-level accuracy of 87% on an independent test set. Also, they incorporated pathologists to

assess various insights from the tissue slides, defining weights for important features that contributed 
to their model’s performance. In contrast, our study attained a tile-level accuracy of approximately 
70%. This moderate accuracy indicates a reasonable level of performance for classifying individual 
tiles, although it suggests room for improvement when scaling to whole slide image classification. 
While the literature typically employs large datasets and deep learning models trained from scratch or 
utilizing transfer learning and fine-tuning to achieve high accuracy (Gupta et al., 2021), our approach 
using a minimal dataset and reduced features still demonstrates commendable results. 
In real-world applications, an accuracy of approximately 70% may indicate potential for classification, 
but clinical practice typically requires an accuracy above 85% to ensure reliable diagnoses, along with 
explainable approaches. Nevertheless, our method is particularly efficient in terms of computational 
resources, requiring less time to process large WSIs compared to traditional deep learning models. 
The results indicate that while supervised learning with features from VGG16 and HistoEncoder 
showed promising tile-level performance, challenges emerged when classifying whole slide images 
using majority voting. Notably, the ROC curves and MCC scores across inner folds displayed patterns 
of inconsistency, with all four models underperforming in folds 2 and 4 and showing slightly lower 
performance in fold 6 compared to baseline metrics; the VGG16 feature set achieved generally lower 
scores. For instance, ROC AUC values decreased by 30% to 50% from fold 1 to fold 2 across all 
models utilizing VGG16 features. Additionally, MCC scores experienced a decline of 75% to 80% 
between folds 2 and 4 when using HistoEncoder features, highlighting substantial variability across 
different cross-validation splits. These variations suggest that certain models, particularly Random 
Forest, may not generalize well across different data subsets, especially with smaller datasets. The 
overall WSI classification accuracy of 50% across all models using the VGG16 feature set suggests 
difficulties in effectively aggregating tile-level predictions for accurate slide classification. In contrast, 
the HistoEncoder feature set resulted in an overall classification accuracy of 1 for three out of four 
models, although variability in outer fold MCC scores for Random Forest underscores the need for 
consistent model performance across different data splits. 
In conclusion, ANN proved to be the most promising model, demonstrating consistent performance in 
both cross-validation and the test set. It also achieved a higher MCC compared to other models using 
the VGG16 feature set. While there is some risk of overfitting due to the small dataset, ANN’s overall 
performance remained robust. Both XGBoost and Light-GBoost demonstrated moderate performance 
with some extent of variability, but Light-GBoost's faster computation makes it a strong candidate for 
further investigation. Conversely, Random Forest, despite strong tile-level results, struggled with 
slide-level classification, particularly misclassifying a majority of LUAD_6 tiles, leading to an overall 
WSI accuracy of only 50% in both features sets. These findings emphasize the need for enhanced 
feature extraction methods and model strategies to improve both tile and slide-level classification 
accuracy, as well as to address the variability seen across data splits. 
In terms of explainability, while there have been efforts described in the literature to interpret model 
predictions, actual results and practical approaches for understanding model decisions in 
histopathology remain limited (Plass et al., 2023). Our approach sought to address this gap by tracking 
tile-level predictions from different models and feature sets to provide an intuitive, yet informative 
understanding of how specific regions within whole slide images contribute to the overall 
classification. This tile-tracking method, though simple, allowed us to find patterns and 
misclassifications across different models, offering a more transparent view of the decision-making 
process, especially when classifying complex cancer subtypes. However, deeper, model-specific 
explainability methods are still necessary to improve interpretability, as highlighted by the challenges 
noted in the literature. One promising direction is the use of saliency maps, which visualize the regions

5.2 Feature Perspectives 
 
Looking ahead, enhancing the robustness and performance of our models will require a combination 
of data expansion, model fine-tuning and improved interpretability strategies. 
One potential improvement for the workflow involves refining the tiling algorithm by incorporating 
more data for testing. Increasing the dataset will offer more variability, helping the algorithm 
generalize better to diverse cases. However, this approach would require additional manual work, 
particularly in labeling and classifying the tiles, to ensure that the algorithm accurately distinguishes 
between tissue-informative tiles. Additionally, expanding the algorithm to detect and filter out 
artifacts, such as scanning noise or staining irregularities, is essential to avoid their interference in 
classification. 
Given the limited dataset, data augmentation techniques such as rotation, flipping and cropping of WSI 
tiles could introduce additional variability and help reduce overfitting, particularly in models like the 
ANN. However, while augmentation can improve generalization, it may also increase the complexity 
of interpreting the model’s decision-making process. To further control overfitting, regularization 
methods such as dropout and L2 regularization can be applied to the deeper layers of the ANN, 
enhancing generalizability without sacrificing interpretability. 
A key feature perspective is to introduce to the dataset normal tissue slides for feature extraction and 
model training. Since WSIs often contain both cancerous and normal regions, relying on mixed data 
for feature extraction can introduce variability, as the proportion of normal tissue varies across slides. 
In cases where there is a high percentage of normal tissue, classification accuracy may be affected, as 
the model might be influenced more by features from non-cancerous regions. By focusing on normal 
tissue during training or feature extraction, the model can better differentiate cancerous from non-
cancerous areas, improving overall classification precision and reducing the risk of misclassification 
due to the presence of excessive normal tissue. Additionally, by introducing normal tissue, we can 
frame the problem as a multilabel classification task. This allows for better assessment of explainability 
by tracking which tiles are classified as normal within the slide and interpreting how the model 
differentiates these regions from cancerous areas. 
LUAD tiles exhibited more dispersion within clusters, likely due to their heterogeneity and the 
presence of mixed tissue patterns that are less distinct than those found in LSCC. This suggests that 
LUAD tiles may share visual characteristics with both normal and cancerous regions, complicating 
their classification. A feature proposal to address this, improving feature extraction techniques to better 
capture LUAD-specific visual signatures could help reduce this dispersion. For future work, more 
refined feature engineering, such as texture-based features or multi-scale feature extraction, could be 
explored to capture subtle distinctions between LUAD and normal tissues. Additionally, employing 
more sophisticated clustering algorithms like DBSCAN, which do not assume spherical clusters, could 
improve classification by identifying denser, more meaningful groups of tiles without being affected 
by outliers.

Another perspective is to gain deeper insights into the performance of various pre-trained models, 
either by incorporating additional data or testing alternative models. This evaluation can guide more 
informed decisions about model adaptation. If a pre-trained model consistently performs well, fine-
tuning its classification layer to better align with our specific dataset and task is a practical approach. 
Fine-tuning leverages the pre-trained model’s learned features, offering a more efficient solution than 
training from scratch, as it benefits from prior extensive training on related tasks. 
Transfer learning presents an additional method for addressing the dataset size constraint. By fine-
tuning the upper layers of pre-trained models, which are less specialized to any one task, the model 
can more effectively adapt to our dataset, reducing the need for large amounts of data while 
maintaining robust performance. Finally, an interesting direction would be to incorporate metadata, 
such as gene mutation stages and clinical information. By adding this layer of information, we can 
enrich the feature set used in classification, potentially improving the model’s ability to differentiate 
between cancer subtypes and predict outcomes (M. Lee, 2023a). This integration enables the model to 
leverage correlations between histological features and specific genetic alterations, which are crucial 
for understanding tumor behavior. Moreover, this approach can help identify patterns linking certain 
morphological characteristics to genetic profiles, ultimately enhancing the interpretability of the 
model's predictions and supporting personalized treatment strategies. 
6. Conclusions 
 
In conclusion, this study addresses a critical gap in computational pathology by developing an 
explainable and computationally efficient approach that balances performance with interpretability. 
We processed 14 Whole Slide Images from two lung cancer subtypes, implementing a comprehensive 
workflow that integrates data preprocessing with explainable classification methods. Our approach 
leverages feature extraction techniques from pretrained models, combined with both unsupervised and 
supervised methods, aiming to assist pathologists in lung tumor diagnosis effectively. 
 
While many state-of-the-art models achieve high accuracy, they often function as black boxes, limiting 
their clinical utility. This research emphasizes the necessity for models that are not only accurate but 
also interpretable and computationally efficient. A critical aspect of the workflow was the design and 
implementation of a tiling algorithm, which effectively filtered out non-informative background areas, 
focusing the analysis on biologically significant regions. This approach ensured that tissue-relevant 
tiles formed the basis of classification, greatly enhancing model precision by reducing noise and 
emphasizing meaningful data. 
 
Our findings demonstrate that both VGG16 and HistoEncoder serve as strong feature extraction 
models, each offering unique advantages. VGG16 balanced speed with computational efficiency, 
while HistoEncoder excelled in capturing domain-specific histopathological features, underscoring the 
importance of context-specific feature extraction in medical imaging. 
 
One important contribution of this project is that explored dimensionality reduction techniques, 
specifically PCA and t-SNE, to analyze features extracted from histopathological image tiles. Our 
findings revealed that PCA effectively distinguished between blank, partial, and full tiles, emphasizing 
the significance of early background removal for improved classification accuracy. As the dataset 
expanded, adjusting the number of principal components was necessary to balance performance with 
interpretability. t-SNE provided clearer clustering patterns, effectively capturing non-linear 
relationships that PCA could not.

K-means clustering yielded moderate results, identifying an optimal number of two clusters 
corresponding to the cancer subtypes, along with moderate MI scores. However, considering the 
substantial dimensionality reduction achieved through feature extraction and PCA, these results are 
promising. 
 
We evaluated four supervised machine learning models; XGBoost, LightGBM, an ANN and Random 
Forest; using features extracted from VGG16 and HistoEncoder. The models demonstrated moderate 
to high predictive power, providing a solid foundation for future optimization. However, the 
performance variability across models aligns with existing literature, indicating that further 
improvements in feature extraction and model tuning are necessary. 
 
The explainability analysis of LightGBM models using VGG16 and HistoEncoder features revealed 
significant overlap, with 82% agreement in classification decisions. The high consistency in LSCC_4 
tiles (792 correctly classified) highlight the robustness of both feature sets, though lower agreement in 
LUAD_6 tiles underscores challenges in certain regions. Despite differences in feature extraction, the 
overall alignment of the models reinforces the reliability and interpretability of their predictions. 
 
In conclusion, this research addresses a critical gap by developing an explainable and computationally 
efficient model that balances performance with interpretability. Despite the encouraging results related 
to feature extraction and classification, ongoing challenges remain, particularly in the areas of 
misclassified regions, clustering effectiveness and the limitations posed by the small dataset. 
Nevertheless, this work establishes a solid foundation for future research on feature extraction, model 
training and the integration of both supervised and unsupervised methods. By emphasizing 
explainability, it paves the way for the development of robust algorithms that can effectively leverage 
diverse datasets, including both labeled and unlabeled data. Furthermore, the insights gained from this 
research could inform innovative strategies for handling complex tissue patterns and improving 
clustering techniques. Overall, these advancements have the potential to significantly enhance clinical 
applications in lung cancer diagnosis, ultimately leading to more accurate and interpretable models 
that support pathologists in making informed decisions.
