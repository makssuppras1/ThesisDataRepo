Abstract
Hydrofoil vessels, especially catamarans, have been established in racing
sailing in recent decades. However, in their quest to exploit the advan-
tages of hydrofoils and record higher speeds, the sailing teams, naval
architects, and marine engineers are confronted with natural phenom-
ena that are neither easy to predict nor to overcome.
Ventilation and cavitation are the two most common phenomena that
adversely affect the hydrodynamic characteristics of surface-piercing hy-
drofoils at a high speed or high angle of attack.
This thesis aims to investigate the presence of these phenomena in open
sea conditions and to study the ’¼éow features of the Nacra 17 hydrofoil.
Due to the high cost of setting up a water tank experiment, numeri-
cal simulations were performed using Computational Fluid Dynamics
(CFD) methods as a good alternative. The commercial CFD software
Simcenter STAR-CCM+ was used to model the water-air and water-
vapor interface on a free surface.
Initially, assumptions were made in order to reduce the complexity of
the problem. The boat sailed without translational motion downwind
at a rotation angle. In addition, the buoyancy was considered negligible
because the lift force is high when the boat is in fully foiling conditions.
Moreover, due to the small variation in the heeling angle, the roll angle
was set to zero for all the study cases. Last, it was assumed that the
impact of the weight of the boat on the hydrofoils was minor in fully
foiling conditions, and the investigation was focused on only one of the
two hydrofoils.
Under these assumptions, a numerical setup was developed to gener-
ate the mesh for the geometry of the hydrofoil of the simulations and
to de’¼üne the numerical models with the correct initial and boundary
conditions. Since no experimental data were available to validate the
numerical setup, spatial and temporal veri’¼ücation were completed to
ensure solution convergence. Furthermore, various rotation angles were
tested to examine where ventilation occurs. In addition, a parametric
analysis was performed to study the in’¼éuence of the rotation angle on
the hydrodynamic characteristics of the hydrofoil.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Hydrofoils are used on recreational yachts, passenger vessels, and rac-
ing boats, both sailing and powerboats. In hydrofoil crafts, the hull is
lifted as the speed increases causing the vessel to ŌĆØ’¼éyŌĆØ out of the wa-
ter. Therefore, the signi’¼ücant wetted area reduction decreases drag and
increases speed [1]. For this reason, hydrofoils found a place in racing
sailing, making the sport even more challenging and exciting. In the
2013 AmericaŌĆÖs Cup, the pinnacle of yachting, foiling boats competed
for the ’¼ürst time. It was the AC72 wingsail catamaran made by Emi-
rates Team New Zealand [2]. In the 2024 Olympic Games in Paris, three
sailing classes that use hydrofoils will participate. These are the Nacra
17, IQFoil, and Formula Kite [3].
In 2011, Morrelli & Melvin, NACRA, and the builders CMI designed
and constructed a high-performance catamaran that satis’¼üed the criteria
required by World Sailing for the Olympic multihull 2016. After the
Olympic Games of Rio, the class and Nacra Sailing agreed to evolve the
boat into a fully foiling catamaran [4].
However, it has been observed that while hydrofoil crafts travel at high
speed, their lift is lost, leading the hull of the boat to lose support and
fall back into the water. As a result, the speed of the craft gets reduced.
A possible explanation for this loss of lift would be the occurrence of
two natural phenomena that provoke a change in ’¼éuid dynamic charac-
teristics around the hydrofoil, namely ventilation and vapor cavitation
[5].
Ventilation is the suction of air along the span of a hydrofoil at high
speed or high angle of attack when the air and water interface [6].
Whereas cavitation is the formation of vapor bubbles within a liquid
in low-pressure regions. These cavities of air collapse violently when
they encounter a region with higher pressure [7]. BernoulliŌĆÖs principle
describes the cause for the low-pressure appearance at high-speed [8].

Since the main aim of this project was to investigate whether ventila-
tion occurs on the hydrofoil of Nacra 17, several numerical experiments
were performed. First, all the study cases of Table 3.1 were tested with
a ’¼üxed Froude number, Fr = 4.1224, which is calculated based on the

The iso-surface is de’¼üned with the threshold of the water volume frac-

with water and consequently half ’¼ülled with air. As a result, this thresh-

surface.
For the cavitation identi’¼ücation, the concept was to run all the free sur-
face simulations until they converged and afterward to check the abso-
lute pressure of the hydrofoil. If the absolute pressure, which is the sum
of the static, atmospheric, and hydrostatic pressures, was less or equal
to zero, there were indications of cavitation inception.
For the cases

calculated values from the previous setup. The results were visualized
by plotting the volume fraction of the water vapor.

In this thesis, numerical CFD models were developed to examine the
occurrence of ventilation and cavitation and their impact on the ’¼éow
characteristics of the Olympic class sailing catamaran Nacra 17 hydro-
foil. Speci’¼ücally, 18 cases were studied for various rotation angles with
’¼üxed ’¼éow speed and immersion depth. The commercial CFD software
of Simcenter STAR-CCM+, which provides capable numerical models to
approach these phenomena, was used to simulate ventilation and cavi-
tation ’¼éow regimes.
Ventilation was observed in four out of the 18 study cases where the rota-
tion angles were [0ŌŚ”

lated. Ventilation was preceded by trailing edge ’¼éow separation, and
the ventilated cavities were stable. In the other study cases, the ’¼éow
regimes were fully wetted, but the base cavity formation was observed
in some cases.
Based on the parametric analysis performed to investigate the behavior
of the lifting force, it was concluded that for yaw angles higher than
10ŌŚ”and pitch angles equal or less than ŌłÆ7.5ŌŚ”, the conditions were ideal
for ventilation inception. In these cases, the pressure reduction on the
suction side made the conditions suitable for ventilation. In addition,
it turned out that in the ventilated hydrofoil, the lift was dramatically
decreased. The lift reduction on the ventilated hydrofoils occurred due
to the cavity formation that reduced the pressure around their suction
side. It was also shown that in the cases where the hydrofoil was venti-
lated, greater yaw angles led to a rise in the lift due to the higher load on
the hydrofoil. Furthermore, considering the Lift over Drag ratio and the
needed lift force on the hydrofoil for the lifting of the boat, the optimal
rotation angle of the study cases was [0ŌŚ”
ŌłÆ7.5ŌŚ”
5ŌŚ”].
Moreover, in the cases with a fully wetted ’¼éow regime, the lift, and the
drag increased by increasing the yaw angle and decreasing the pitch

Chapter 6
Future Work
Despite the wide range of studies carried out within the time limits of
this thesis, further analysis can be undertaken to better understand the
ventilation and cavitation phenomena on the Nacra 17 boat.
Firstly, executing the simulations with re’¼üned mesh or a smaller time
step would lead to better results, although this would require more com-
putational power and time.
Further, since only one case for which there was evidence for cavitation
inception was investigated, the same procedure should be applied to the
rest of the cases.
In addition, as mentioned in theory, the Froude number is a factor that
affects the occurrence of ventilation. This number depends on the speed
of the ’¼éow but also the immersion depth of the hydrofoil.
For this
reason, further simulations with varying depth and velocity could detect
differences in the aforementioned phenomena for the rotation angles
studied.
Another crucial factor that affects both phenomena is the presence of
waves on the free surface. A wave can change the immersion depth and
cavity closure angle, causing either initiation of ventilation or washout.
The Simcenter STAR-CCM+ software provides models to simulate the
liquid-gas interface, including surface gravity waves.
Also, there have been studies where they use fences to washout the
ventilation. It would be interesting to combine additional geometries
with the hydrofoil geometry and perform simulations for this study.
In this work, the hydrodynamic behavior of the boat was studied only
by rotating the hydrofoil. However, the three translation motions can
also be considered while the boat is sailing. Thus, dynamic simulations
must be performed regarding the translation motions.
