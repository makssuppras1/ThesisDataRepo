Abstract
This Master’s thesis investigates the high-temperature characterization of aluminosilicate,
specifically focusing on their application in DED-based 3D printing of lunar regolith struc-
tures. The study aims to establish a feasible process window for utilizing mullite as a
regolith simulant, leveraging its physical and chemical properties that closely mimic those
of actual lunar regolith.
Through a comprehensive series of experiments, the research characterizes the mullite
powder, identifies DED process parameters, and evaluates the quality of printed parts.
Key parameters such as laser power, scanning speed, and powder feed rate were opti-
mized to minimize defects and enhance mechanical properties. In-situ monitoring tech-
nique was integrated to provide real-time insights into melt pool dynamics, correlating
these observations with simulation outcomes to refine the process further.
The findings suggest that mullite is a viable candidate for lunar regolith simulant in DED
processes. The study also highlights the importance of substrate material and how it
affects the final part quality. Future work should explore additional substrate materials,
such as alumina, due to its thermal compatibility and successful application in existing
literature. Furthermore, large-scale experiments, and simulation of lunar environment
conditions to validate these findings for extraterrestrial applications are also advisable.
Finally, this research contributes valuable knowledge to the field of additive manufactur-
ing for space exploration, laying the groundwork for developing sustainable construction
techniques on the Moon.

Introduction
The Cold War era marked the beginning of space exploration as a primarily government-
led initiative, serving as a symbol of strategic dominance among nations. This period was
characterized by a competitive space race, notably between the USA and the USSR. The
1960s witnessed several ambitious missions targeting the Moon, achieving significant
milestones. Despite a subsequent decline in lunar mission interest, discussions about
establishing a permanent Moon settlement have persisted.
In recent times, the landscape of space exploration is shifting towards commercial in-
volvement. This change is evidenced by developments in space tourism, privately funded
scientific projects in space, and asteroid mining. Although state agencies still play a major
role as investors and consumers in the space sector, a gradual shift is occurring with the
development of space and lunar infrastructure, indicating a move towards more diverse
participation [1, 2].
Located merely three days away by spacecraft, the Moon stands as Earth’s nearest ce-
lestial neighbor, offering near-instant communication with our planet. In contrast, Mars,
another focus for human colonization, is about a year’s journey from Earth and presents
substantial communication challenges [1].
The goal of establishing a base on the Moon holds significant promise, mainly for the
potential it offers in gathering data and insights on survival in extraterrestrial environ-
ments. These initiatives are crucial for developing long-term plans for human settlements
in space and the exploration of other celestial bodies, such as Mars. Achieving this goal
requires engineers to innovate and build facilities capable of supporting life, designed to
withstand space’s severe conditions, such as micro-meteoroid impacts, intense solar and
cosmic radiation, and significant temperature changes. Current research explores vari-
ous strategies for constructing such habitats, leveraging underground spaces or utilizing
In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) to build with materials found on the Moon [1, 2, 3].
Additive Manufacturing (AM) technologies are highly regarded for their potential in ISRU
applications during extraterrestrial explorations [3, 4, 5]. They offer unparalleled flexibility
and design freedom by enabling the layer-by-layer deposition of complex-shaped three-
dimensional objects directly from a computer-based model, using local feedstock.
Among various laser-assisted AM methods, Direct Energy Deposition (DED) is particularly
noteworthy. It facilitates the creation of shell-like components, which are exceptionally
suitable for regolith-based shelters on the Moon. Additionally, one of the outcomes of
this research is the exploration of technology transfer from regolith-simulant processing
via AM technology to the processing of ceramics. This direction is particularly significant
as ceramics have not been widely exploited in AM manufacturing, primarily due to the
challenges associated with thermal transient stresses during melting and resolidification
processes [5]. Addressing these challenges could unlock new possibilities in ceramic
processing, expanding the applicability of AM technologies beyond regolith simulants to
a broader range of materials.
This Master’s thesis focuses on exploring the laser-assisted DED process using mullite
powder as a regolith-simulant material. The study starts with a detailed characterization
of the feedstock, followed by the establishment of a process window for part fabrication.
Subsequently, the produced samples undergo comprehensive characterization. In-situ

1.1
Project Plan
The project began with an in-depth literature review focused on the regolith-simulant ad-
ditive manufacturing (AM) process, emphasizing Direct Energy Deposition (DED) with
laser sources. This review was crucial in understanding the essential aspects of the pro-
cess window and the characterization methods pertinent to AM manufacturing of regolith-
simulants.
Following this initial phase, the project undertook the following steps:
1. Characterization of feedstock material: Mullite, in powder form at room temperature,
was characterized using Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM), optical microscopy,
X-Ray Fluorescence (XRF) analysis, and X-ray diffraction (XRD).
2. Characterization of printed parts: The analysis of the printed parts englobed the
evaluation of overall physical integrity, microstructural, and phase analysis to eval-
uate the quality of the manufactured components.
3. In-situ monitoring analysis: This involved studying the formation and stability of the
melt pool during the DED process, utilizing real-time monitoring techniques to en-
hance understanding and control of the manufacturing process.
The fabrication of samples was carried out using DED technology at the Research Insti-
tutes of Sweden (RISE), specifically at the Application Center for Additive Manufacturing
in Gothenburg. The characterization activities were conducted at the Technical University
of Denmark (DTU).
This comprehensive approach, from the initial literature review through to the characteri-
zation of materials and parts, in-situ monitoring, and simulation verification, is designed to
advance our understanding and capabilities in the AM process using regolith simulants,
contributing valuable insights into the field.

Theorical background
The Moon has been an object of curiosity for as long as human civilization has existed.
Through the observation of its phases and cycles, many theories have been established
about this mysterious celestial neighbor.
It has always been known that the Moon’s
phases affect Earth in various ways: from ancient mystical beliefs related to harvesting
and agriculture to the present scientifically understood tide shifts caused by the Moon’s
gravitational pull.
With the advent of space exploration in the mid-20th century, the Moon gained additional
significance as a political symbol, as nations competed to reach it during the Cold War.
This was exemplified by the Apollo program, during which the USA successfully con-
ducted crewed missions to the Moon. Since that historical milestone, other missions,
including subsequent Apollo missions, the Surveyor, the Soviet Luna program, the Chi-
nese Chang’e, and others, have landed on different areas of the Moon, exploring and
analyzing its surface [2].
Following the Apollo missions, interest in lunar exploration declined significantly due to
political shifts, high costs, and technological limitations. In recent years, however, ma-
jor national space agencies and commercial aerospace companies have refocused their
attention on the Moon [2].
The prospect of establishing a permanent settlement on the Moon has become increas-
ingly compelling. A lunar base would serve as a proving ground for technologies and
strategies essential for long-duration missions to Mars and beyond. The Moon provides
a nearby location where the challenges of living and working in space can be addressed,
yielding insights and innovations crucial for deep space exploration [1, 2, 3].
NASA plans to establish a long-term presence on the Moon through the Artemis program,
which aims to explore the lunar surface to develop the capabilities necessary for human
missions to Mars [6, 7]. Similarly, the Chinese National Space Administration (CNSA)
and the European Space Agency (ESA) have both expressed serious considerations re-
garding the establishment of a permanent lunar settlement [2, 8].
A lunar colony would require extensive multidisciplinary research and the development of
new vital technologies for deep space exploration. The Moon’s low gravity, high levels of
cosmic radiation, extreme temperature variations, and frequent micrometeorite bombard-
ments present significant challenges that must be overcome. Amongst these challenges,
the construction of shelters is one of the most critical steps in establishing a long-term
presence on the Moon [2].
One of the pivotal strategies discussed in recent literature is the In-Situ Resource Utiliza-
tion (ISRU). It involves harnessing local materials to support the needs of a lunar base,
thereby reducing the dependence on supplies from Earth. Given the high cost of launch-
ing each kilogram of mass from Earth, it is crucial to develop methods to minimize the
need for transporting raw materials [1, 2, 4, 3, 5]. A related concept is the In-Situ Fabri-
cation and Repair (ISFR), which, when combined with ISRU, would not only reduce costs
but also enhance mission flexibility and resilience [1].
The Moon’s surface could be roughly divided in two distinct areas: the lunar highlands,

2.1
Lunar Regolith
Lunar regolith is the fragmental and unconsolidated rock material on the moon’s surface.
This layer, which ranges in thickness from 4 to 15 meters, constitutes the uppermost
part of the lunar ground [9]. The regolith is composed of fine particles that have been
generated over millions and billions of years through processes of space weathering,
including meteorite impacts, solar wind, cosmic radiation, and thermal cycling, among
other factors [10]. The particle size distribution of lunar regolith typically falls within the
range of 60 to 80 µm, with some particles as fine as 10 nm [2].
The morphology of regolith particles differs from terrestrial minerals. Due to the absence
of erosional factors such as wind and rain on the Moon, lunar regolith exhibits irregular
and angular shapes with sharp edges, often containing glassy inclusions and porosities
similar to a ”Swiss cheese” [2]. These particles are highly abrasive and can be toxic if
inhaled [9]. Figure 2.1 displays images of lunar regolith agglutinates taken by NASA from
specimens brought to Earth during the Apollo 11 mission.

Source: NASA Photo S69-54827 [11]. (b) Scanning electron microscopy of agglutinate.

Furthermore, lunar regolith is predominantly composed of oxides, with silica (SiO2) being
the most abundant, comprising approximately 40 wt% to 49 wt%. Alumina (Al2O3) and
iron oxide (FeO) are also significant constituents, accounting for 12 - 30 wt% and 4 - 16
wt%, respectively [9].
Lunar regolith exhibits a substantial glass phase, primarily attributed to the frequent impact
of micrometeorites. The kinetic energy from these impacts is sufficient to melt the surface
material, forming a specific glass phase known as agglutinate. This process significantly
influences the regolith’s physical and chemical properties, contributing to its unique com-
position and behavior observed on the lunar surface [12].

2.1.1
Mullite as a lunar regolith simulant
Building on the understanding of lunar regolith and its current simulants is crucial to explor-
ing alternative materials that can serve as effective substitutes for regolith in experimental
settings. This subsection reviews the characteristics of mullite, examining its potential as
a lunar regolith simulant based on its properties.
Mullite is a ceramic material composed of alumina Al2O3 and silica SiO2, which are also
significant components of lunar regolith. Extensively used in traditional and technical ap-
plications, mullite is valued for its properties: low density, excellent fracture toughness,
low thermal conductivity, low thermal expansion, high thermal stability, and good mechan-
ical resistance [18].

shown in Figure 2.2. In the 2 Al2O3 · SiO2 composition, with 77.2 wt% of alumina, its

Mullite has a higher alumina content compared to silica, which contrasts with the com-
position of lunar regolith where silica is more predominant. This difference results in a
higher melting temperature for mullite due to the alumina’s influence, necessitating more
energy in the processing phases. This characteristic is particularly interesting because
it suggests that lunar regolith, with its lower alumina content, would require less energy
input than mullite for similar processes. Consequently, using mullite as a reference ma-
terial is advantageous; if processes are successful with mullite, they would likely succeed
with lunar regolith, potentially simplifying the energy requirements for lunar operations.
By leveraging these properties, mullite can effectively serve as a lunar regolith simulant,
providing a material that somewhat mimics the lunar soil. This ensures that experiments
and trials using mullite may yield applicable and relevant results to the research of lunar
technological development.
2.2
Additive Manufacturing of Lunar Regolith
Regarding ISRU and ISFR, Additive Manufacturing (AM) has been cited many times in the
literature as a promising means of production and construction for human settlement on
the Moon [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 23, 24, 25]. As a versatile manufacturing method, AM is capable
of producing parts in a wide variety of shapes and sizes [2].
Also known as 3D printing, AM creates components by progressively adding layers of
material in accordance with a digital blueprint [26]. This facilitates the fabrication of com-

plex parts with unique features while reducing the necessity for extensive component as-
sembly. It also accelerates the design-fail-fix cycle, reduces material waste and enables
on-demand production [25].
Furthermore, AM has been utilized across various industries over the past decades, in-
cluding aerospace, medical, automotive, and energy sectors [8, 24]. Specifically in the
aerospace, space, and defense industries, the global AM market is projected to exceed
$13 billion by 2028. They are critical for developing next-generation space systems [25].
AM technology generally requires minimal pre-processing of raw materials, as well as
a limited amount of auxiliary equipment for both preparation and post-processing. AM
processes can utilize various forms of material sources, such as filaments, slurries, and
powders [2, 24]. Some of the reviewed methods for 3D printing with lunar regolith in the
literature are exemplified below:
1. Binder jetting
2. Material Extrusion
3. Powder bed fusion
4. Direct energy deposition
Binder Jetting
Binder jetting (BJ) involves selectively depositing a liquid binding agent onto a powder
bed, creating a chemical bond between the binder and the ceramic powder. The final part
can either incorporate the binder or undergo thermal processing to remove it [2].
An example of the BJ principle applied to lunar regolith printing is the ESA’s study on
building lunar habitats with the D-shape technology [27, 28]. In this method, a complex
system of binder nozzles moves along a three-dimensional axis. Cesaretti et al. [28]
investigated the feasibility of BJ technology under lunar-like conditions by conducting ex-
periments within a vacuum chamber. They mixed a lunar regolith simulant with 25 wt%
of an inorganic binder, resulting in a hollow porous structure likely due to rapid binder
outgassing.
Material Extrusion
Another AM method is the so-called material extrusion, which selectively dispenses a
paste or ink through a nozzle. Taylor et al. [29] investigated this method with lunar regolith
ink to create micro-trusses. They prepared the ink by mixing the lunar regolith simulant
(JSC-1A) with polylactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) and solvents. The printed green bodies
were then subjected to thermal processing (pyrolysis and sintering) to remove the poly-
mer binder and sinter the regolith parts. The study demonstrated the feasibility of using
extrusion-based 3D printing and sintering to create structural elements from lunar regolith.

Laser Powder Bed Fusion
One more well-established AM method is the Powder Bed Fusion (PBF). This process
uses a high-energy heat source to fuse regions of a powder bed selectively [26]. The
most commonly used heat sources are lasers or electron beams. Some studies have
investigated using PBF to process lunar regolith simulants in the literature [3, 4].
Figure 2.3 presents a schematic representation of the Laser Powder Bed Fusion (LPBF)
process. In this example, the energy system, typically a laser, selectively melts the powder
material to form the fabricated object. Powder delivery is facilitated by a roller that evenly
spreads a thin layer of powder across the build platform. The energy system scans the
powder bed, melting the powder in specific areas according to the design of the part being
fabricated. Once a layer is complete, the build platform lowers, and the roller spreads a
new layer of powder. This layer-by-layer approach continues until the entire object is
formed.
Figure 2.3: LPBF schematic representation. Source: [31]
Goulas et al. [3] assessed the feasibility of using lunar regolith simulants with Laser-PBF
(LPBF). They tested various laser parameters to optimize the consolidation of the parts.
The study found that lunar regolith could be processed by LPBF. However, adjustments
in the process parameters and base plate materials are crucial for successful layer depo-
sition.
Caprio et al. [4] aimed to establish a feasible process window for producing multi-layer
samples of lunar regolith simulant (NU-LHT-2M, sourced from lunar highlands) using
LPBF. They explored the effects of different base plate materials, various laser powers,
and scan speeds. The study was conducted through three distinct campaigns: single-
layer, three-layer, and multi-layer. The objective was to identify the optimal process pa-
rameters for achieving stable and optimized deposition.
The researchers tested both carbon steel and refractory clay as base plate materials.
They varied process parameters, including laser power and scan speed, to determine
their impact on deposition stability. The key findings indicated that refractory clay was the
most suitable base plate material for achieving stable multi-layer deposition. The optimal
parameters identified were a laser power of 50W and a scan speed of 225 mm/s, which
resulted in stable deposition conditions without excessive energy input, thereby enabling
the production of multi-layer components.
Directed Energy Deposition
While LPBF has shown promise in the context of lunar regolith, there are other AM meth-
ods worth considering. One such method is Directed Energy Deposition (DED). In this
process, a thermal energy source, typically a laser or an electron beam, is used to melt

the material as it is being deposited. This method is particularly versatile, allowing for the
addition of material to existing components and creating new parts. It is also suitable for
producing large and complex structures [26].
Figure 2.4 presents a schematic representation of a DED process. The energy source in
this example is a laser beam, which is coaxial with the powder nozzles. The powder noz-
zles are responsible for depositing the powder material, which is then melted by the laser
beam. As the material melts, a molten pool is created directly onto the substrate plate.
Shielding gas is used to protect the molten pool and the deposited material from oxida-
tion and contamination. The laser beam moves along a predetermined path, allowing the
molten material to solidify and form a layer.

Balla et al. [5] conducted one of the most important studies to this day on the feasibility
of DED for the fabrication of lunar regolith parts. They used JSC-1AC and optimized
the process by varying laser power, scan speeds, and powder feed rates. Their research
successfully produced dense parts without macroscopic defects, demonstrating that DED
is a viable process method for lunar regolith simulant. Figure 2.5 presents their samples.

Although DED has not been extensively studied for lunar regolith simulants, it presents
significant potential due to its ability to erect freestanding structures without requiring ad-
ditives or binders [33]. Additionally, DED is well-suited for constructing large structures
without the constraints of a powder bed, as required in LPBF [34].

X-Ray Diffraction
X-Ray Diffraction (XRD) is a characterization method that allows for the study of the crys-
talline structure of materials. When a beam of X-rays strikes a crystal, the uniform spacing
of atoms within this crystal causes an interference pattern. If the X-ray beam contacts the
crystal at an angle θ, it reflects off the atomic planes at the same angle. These atomic
planes, spaced at a distance d apart, must reflect X-rays such that they constructively
interfere to generate a discernible interference pattern. Constructive interference hap-
pens when the path length difference between layers is an integer n, culminating to the
formulation of Bragg’s law:
nλ = 2dsinθ
(3.1)
The experimental setup used for the XRD measurements of mullite powder was a Rigaku
Smartlab X-ray diffractometer, from Rigaku Corporation, Tokyo, Japan. The X-ray tube
consisted of a 9 kW X-ray tube with a rotation Cu anode. The focusing geometry employed
was Bragg-Brentano, and the machine had two Germanium (220) monochromators. Fig-
ure 3.4 shows the XRD setup at DTU.
The conversion software used during the measurements was PDXL: Integrated X-ray
Powder Diffraction Software, from Rigaku Corporation, Tokyo, Japan. For the material
identification process, the Powder Diffraction File (PDF-4) database was utilized. The
output of the XRD analysis is a spectrum displaying various angles of analysis on the x-
axis and the signal output on the y-axis. The resulting data were analyzed using OriginLab
software from OriginLab Corporation, Massachusetts, USA.

Energy Dispersive X-Ray Spectoscropy
The SEM was coupled with Energy Dispersive Spectroscopy (EDS), which enables el-

both the surface and near-surface layers, yielding an average compositional analysis of
the volume affected by the electron beam. EDS operates by quantifying the energy and

beam of the SEM. This method enhances the capability to assess the elemental com-

X-Ray Fluorescence
X-ray Fluorescence (XRF) is a spectroscopic technique used to identify the elemental
composition of materials. XRF operates by directing an X-ray beam at a sample, which
excites the atoms and ejects electrons from their innermost orbitals. As electrons from
outer shells transition to fill these vacancies, they emit X-rays characteristic of their re-
spective elements. This emission, known as X-ray fluorescence, has a unique radiation
signature for each element, facilitating precise elemental analysis.
XRF measurements were conducted with an Epsilon 4 EDXRF, from Malvern Panalytical
GmbH, Malvern, UK. About 1.6 grams of powder mullite has been used for 10 grams of
flux, which in turn consisted of 67% Lithium tetraborate, 33% Lithium metaborate). The
measurements were done using the Epsilon software version 2.2, as well as the Omnian
application. The indicated oxides have been analyzed and summed to 100%.

improve the overall structural integrity of the specimens.

these temperatures. SWIR cameras capture images based on reflected photons, making

In the methodology of this study, a tailored approach was adopted for the experimental

X-Ray Computed Tomography
X-Ray Computer Tomography (XCT) is a measurement technique that offers valuable ge-
ometric characterization of internal features, which are challenging to measure without
damaging the sample. This non-destructive method was particularly useful for speci-
mens too fragile for other characterization techniques. XCT scanning generates a three-
dimensional computer model of a sample’s imaged volume. The contrast between dif-
ferent components within the model depends on their X-ray absorption rates, which are
primarily determined by their elemental composition and density. This technique exposes
the internal architecture of the specimens, including pores, inclusions, and other inter-
nal features. The XCT machine used was a Nikon Corporation model XT H 225, Tokyo,
Japan. Figure 3.11 displays the XCT machine utilized for these measurements. The
tomographic images were reconstructed using Nikon’s CT Pro 3D software, and post-
processing was performed using Avizo 3D, Thermo Fischer Scientific, Massachusetts,
USA.
Furthermore, the porosity of the cross-sectional views was analyzed using ImageJ (ver-
sion 2023.4.12, National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD). The images were converted
to 8-bit and then adjusting the threshold. By following the steps, the porosity area could
be measured.

Optical Fringe Projection
Optical fringe projection is a non-contact measurement technique employed for surface
profiling and 3D scanning of objects. It functions by projecting a pattern of light onto the
object, while a camera records the pattern as it deforms on the object’s surface. Using
principles of triangulation, the system analyzes the displacement and distortion of the
projected pattern in the captured images to compute the object’s surface geometry.
In this study, optical fringe projection was used to perform geometric characterization of
the printed samples. The equipment utilized was an optical scanner from 3Shape, Copen-
haguen, Denmark. Data acquisition was conducted using the Convince ScanWizard soft-
ware, model Q800. The primary goal of these measurements was to create 3D surface
maps of the samples, enabling the examination of the laser track shape and potential
deflection patterns that could be associated with process conditions.

environment, allowing for the examination of non-conductive samples without the need
for additional coating. Although the resolution of ESEM is generally lower than that of
conventional SEM due to the presence of gas molecules, it offers significant versatility in
sample handling. ESEM was particularly advantageous in this study as the samples were
made of non-conductive mullite, avoiding the buildup of charges on the sample surface

The dimensional analysis and warpage assessment of the single tracks produced on the
S-plate highlights the significant impact of both scanning speed and the importance of
material properties on geometric dimensions and structural integrity. The warpage ob-
served in these samples indicates a thermal mismatch between the mullite tracks and the
stainless steel substrate. Higher scanning speeds exacerbate this issue by increasing
thermal gradients and residual stresses.
This thermal mismatch is influenced by several key material properties:
• Thermal conductivity: Thermal conductivity determines how quickly heat is con-
ducted away from the material. Mullite has a lower thermal conductivity than stain-
less steel, leading to slower heat dissipation in the mullite tracks. This discrepancy
causes uneven cooling rates, with the stainless steel cooling more quickly than the
mullite, creating thermal gradients that may result in residual stresses and warpage.
• Coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE): The CTE defines how much a material
expands or shrinks with temperature changes. A significant mismatch between CTE
of mullite and stainless steel means that during the heating and cooling cycles, these
materials expand and contract at different rates. This differential movement induces
stresses at the interface and within the materials, causing the observed warpage,
particularly at higher scan speeds where thermal gradients are more pronounced.
• Heat capacity: Heat capacity is the ratio of heat absorbed by a material to the tem-
perature change. Variations in heat capacity between mullite and stainless steel
mean that these materials will absorb and release heat at different rates. Stainless
steel, with a typically higher heat capacity, can absorb more heat without a signif-
icant rise in temperature compared to mullite. This difference affects the thermal
dynamics during cooling, leading to thermal stresses and warpage.
• Bonding characteristics: The strength and nature of the bond between the mullite
and stainless steel influence how well the materials adhere to each other under
thermal stress. Poor bonding can lead to delamination or separation at the interface,
exacerbating the warpage. Effective bonding helps to distribute and mitigate thermal
stresses, but any weaknesses in bonding can result in localized deformation and
increased warpage.
By understanding and addressing these material property differences, it may be possible
to mitigate the warpage. Ideally, the material properties of the feedstock and the substrate
should be closer in value to diminish the thermal stresses.

with fewer and smaller pores. Moreover, reduced material flow at higher scan speeds

Conclusions and Future Work
The primary goal of this Master’s thesis was to explore the feasibility and process param-
eters for using mullite as a regolith simulant in the DED process for potential applications
in 3D lunar regolith printing. This research has provided significant insights and practical
findings that contribute to the broader field of additive manufacturing, particularly in the
context of extraterrestrial construction. The main conclusions are:
• Feasibility of Mullite as a Regolith Simulant: The comprehensive characteriza-
tion of mullite demonstrated its suitability as a lunar regolith simulant. The material’s
physical and chemical properties, including its high thermal stability and similarity
in composition to lunar regolith, make it an appropriate choice for experimental pur-
poses.
• Process Window Identification: Through a series of experimental campaigns, a
feasible process window for the DED of mullite was established. Laser power and
scanning speed were identified to produce multilayer tracks.
• Quality of Printed Parts: The characterization of the printed parts revealed that the
DED process could produce mullite components with acceptable physical integrity
and microstructural characteristics. The analysis identified the importance of the
substrate material in the final quality of the parts.
• In-situ Monitoring: The integration of in-situ monitoring provided valuable real-time
data on melt pool dynamics, which were correlated with the quality of the printed
parts. This approach enhanced the understanding of the DED process.
Building on the findings of this thesis, several avenues for future research are recom-
mended:
1. Exploration of Substrate Materials: Further research should explore other mate-
rials as substrates. Alumina would be advisable because it offers similar thermal
expansion properties to mullite, reducing thermal stresses and improving adhesion
during the DED process. Additionally, alumina’s high melting point may success-
fully avoid outgassing from the substrate during the deposition process. Further-
more, other researchers in the literature have successfully employed alumina as a
substrate for similar applications, demonstrating its effectiveness in enhancing print
quality and structural integrity.
2. Process optimization: Continued optimization of the DED process parameters
is necessary to further reduce defects and improve the mechanical properties of
printed parts. Further assessment with in-situ monitoring could enhance knowledge
on deposition dynamics, potentially increasing part quality.
3. Larger Scale Experiments: Conducting larger scale experiments with more com-
plex geometries and multilayer structures will help validate the process window and
provide insights into the scalability of the DED process for lunar construction appli-
cations.
4. Environmental Simulation: Simulating the lunar environment during the DED pro-
cess, including vacuum conditions and temperature fluctuations, will provide a more
accurate assessment of the process feasibility for extraterrestrial applications.
