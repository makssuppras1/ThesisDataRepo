Abstract
In the period between 1975 and 2015 the Greenland ice sheet has been associated with
increasing mass loss due to rising temperatures (Rignot and Kanagaratnam, 2006). The
northeastern Greenland ice stream extends far inland and is sensitive to changes oc-
curring at the margin (Christianson et al., 2014). Since 2003 coastal changes have been
propagating upstream (Khan et al., 2014), making a study of this area increasingly im-
portant. This thesis examines the observed changes during a 40 year period using var-
ious data sets. Ice velocities of the outlets glaciers in the northeastern drainage basin
are computed, using optical Landsat images and the ImGRAFT toolbox (Messerli and
Grinsted, 2015). The resulting ice velocities are utilized to compute the ice discharge
with the ﬂux gate method. Both the ice velocities and the ice discharge have increased
signiﬁcantly for Nioghalvfjerdsfjorden and Zachariae between 1975 and 2015, but de-
creased for the Storstrømmen glacier in the same period. Furthermore the surface
elevation changes revealed an extensive thinning not only at the margin but also far
inland along the northeastern Greenland ice stream. Radar data from 1978-1979 and
the newer CRESIS data from 2014 have been compared and by studying the location of
units of disrupted radio-stratigraphy a possible change in the width of the northeast-
ern Greenland ice stream is discussed. However, no changes have been observed in the
vertical extent of the basal ice units outside the northeastern Greenland ice stream.

Introduction
The goal of this study is to asses the changes that have happened in the Northeastern
Greenland Ice Sheet (GrIS) by using various geophysical datasets from 1975 up until
2015. In this 40 year period the GrIS has been experiencing increasing mass loss (Rig-
not and Kanagaratnam, 2006) due to a changing climate. The 5th IPCC report (Vaughan
et al., 2013) states that in the future predictions of sea level rise it is crucial to include
the contribution from the ice sheets, and currently the GrIS holds the equivalent of
a 7.3 m global sea level rise (Vaughan et al., 2013). Hence, it is of great importance
to obtain knowledge on what drives the ice dynamics. The Northeastern Greenland
Ice Stream (NEGIS) is exceptional important since it extends 700 km (Fahnestock et
al., 1993),(Joughin et al., 2001) inland, which enables coastal changes to propagate far
inland (Christianson et al., 2014) and according to Khan et al. (2014) it has been occur-
ring since 2003. As remote sensing techniques become more accurate, and more data
becomes available, analysis of ice sheets becomes easier and the overall current state
of the Northeastern GrIS becomes evident. By comparing data from the present with
data from the 1970’s the changes of Northeastern GrIS are surveyed.
Therefore, in order to asses the overall state of the Northeastern GrIS, different mea-

surements have been conducted, in order to compute parameters such as surface el-
evation changes, ice velocities, ice ﬂux, and changes in the internal structures in the
ice. These four different parameters are utilized to obtain an overall picture of the
state of the Northeastern GrIS and they provide a link between how changes in the
ice velocities can lead to enhanced thinning of the ice and many other interesting phe-

If the ice stream in the northeastern GrIS were to experience an increased dynamic
thinning, it will have a major impact on the mass loss of the Greenland ice sheet and
sea level rise, since the northeastern drainage basin (see Figure 2.3) covers 16% of the
ice sheet (Khan et al., 2014) and drains 20% of the ice sheet (Rignot and Kanagaratnam,
2006).
1.1
Scientiﬁc methods used
The four different parameters investigated in this thesis: surface elevation changes, ice
velocities, ice ﬂux, and changes in the internal structures in the ice are described in the
following.
A survey of the ice velocities is of great importance and is conducted in this thesis to re-
veal the transport of mass from the ice sheet. An ice stream can be thought of as a river,
which quickly transports ice towards the ocean. Therefore observing ice velocities of
the outlet glaciers quickly yields information about the condition of the ice sheet and
how it responds to changes in air temperature as well as changes in ocean temperature.
Ice velocities gives us information not only about the glacier but also about how it af-
fects the ice sheet further inland, when ice discharge increases or decreases. The out-
let glaciers in northeastern GrIS are accelerating according to Mouginot et al. (2015),
making the ice velocities paramount in understanding the changes in the northeastern
GrIS.
In this thesis, the accumulated ice sheet surface elevation change from 1992-2014 are
constructed in order to get an image of the trend in that period. Whether the accumu-
lated surface elevation change reveals a trend of thinning or thickening, it represents
the condition which the ice sheet is in. The surface elevation changes can additionally
be utilized to compute the volume changes and mass changes over time. Studying ele-
vation changes of the ice sheet is paramount in understanding how climate affects the

GrIS. It is particularly important to monitor the coastal areas where the outlet glaciers
are positioned, since these glaciers respond exceptionally rapid to variations in the
regional climate (Rignot and Kanagaratnam, 2006). Therefore the behavior of outlets
glaciers is an indisputably good indicator for the present climate situation.
The dynamic mass loss in an area, also denoted as the ice ﬂux, is calculated using the
ﬂux gate method, which is a method to obtain the amount of discharged ice. The ice
ﬂuxes of NEGIS from Messerli et al. (2014) are compared against calculated ﬂuxes from
2015 and eventually compared with the ﬂuxes computed from the 1970’s. The changes
in the amount of ice discharged could be triggered by a decrease in the amount of sea
ice in the fjords in front of the glaciers, which would lead to a speed up of the disinte-
gration of the glaciers tongues (Thomsen et al., 1997), (Khan et al., 2014). This would
cause a signiﬁcant impact on the mass balance of the northeastern GrIS.
In order to understand these changes it is necessary to also study what happens un-
derneath the opaque surface of the ice. This is done by utilizing Radio-Echo Sound-
ing of ice (RES). RES allows for acquiring more information about the ice-bed inter-
face, as this can provide information on past ice ﬂow (Panton and Karlsson, 2015).
RES is a method developed in the 1960s (Bingham and Siegert, 2007) for investigat-
ing the subsurface of ice sheets and ice caps. Some of the ﬁrst to take advantage of this
method over the GrIS was the Electromagnetic institute at DTU. They performed the
ﬁrst airborne RES in Greenland (Overgaard, 1984) in 1968 and continued in the follow-
ing years.
In this thesis the changes in the northeastern GrIS are explored during the past 40 years
through the above mentioned geophysical measurements conducted in the 1970’s and
in the present.

2.1
Glaciological concepts
An ice sheet is a glacier extending more than 50,000 km2 (NSIDC, 2016) therefore only
two ice sheets exists on Earth, the largest on Antarctica, which actually consists of two
ice sheets that have grown together, and the second largest on Greenland, the GrIS.
Ice sheets are continuously moving, and ice ﬂows in the downhill direction towards
the margins, with the lowest elevation, and away from the ice divide, with the highest
elevation. The ice moves exceedingly slow at the ice divide because the driving force
is gravity and that is determined by both bedrock and surface topography. Closer to
the margin the ice velocities can therefore reach rapid velocities (Cuffey and Paterson,
2010), as it can be seen on Figure 2.1. On Figure 2.1 the ENVEO ice velocity map of GrIS

The fast moving features in the ice are called ice streams and outlet glaciers (NSIDC,

The ice streams and outlet glaciers drains the ice from the GrIS, but only from the cor-
responding drainage basin. A drainage basin is deﬁned as an area of the ice sheet that
can be associated with a particular outlet glacier or ice stream that drains that speciﬁc
area (Cuffey and Paterson, 2010). In other words the NEGIS drains a particular area
called the northeastern drainage basin that is illustrated in Figure 2.3, where also the
NEGIS is visible inside the drainage basin.
2.2
The North East Greenland Ice Stream
In this study the focus is on the northeastern part of Greenland, or more speciﬁcally
on the northeastern drainage basin. The northeastern drainage basin covers 16% of
the GrIS and accommodate a vastly sizable ice stream that has a great inﬂuence on
the ﬂow of ice. NEGIS extends 700 km (Fahnestock et al., 1993),(Joughin et al., 2001)
into the ice sheet and reach all the way to the margin as it can be seen in Figure 2.1.
There are plenty of ice streams in Antarctica, but in Greenland there are just two large
ice streams: the Jacobshavn ice stream and the NEGIS. The Jakobshavn ice stream is
well known and drains about 7% of the GrIS(Rignot and Kanagaratnam, 2006), but the
NEGIS was ﬁrst discovered in 1993 by Fahnestock et al. (1993), and actually drains 20%
of the GrIS (Rignot and Kanagaratnam, 2006). Hence it is important to improve on the
knowledge available for the NEGIS, since it is likely to have a major impact on the fu-
ture ice discharge of the GrIS. According to Christianson et al. (2014) is the downstream
part of the NEGIS susceptible to ocean forcings that can rapidly be transported from
the coastal areas to the upstream part of the NEGIS. The ﬂow of NEGIS is thought to
be predominantly by sliding and to a less degree internal deformation. The sliding is
likely taking place due to the presence of subglacial water (Joughin et al., 2001) gen-
erated from a rather large geothermal heat ﬂux near the ice divide (Fahnestock, 2001).
The onset of the NEGIS has been suggested to be of a volcanic origin by Fahnestock
(2001).
The margin is where NEGIS divides and splits into three marine terminating outlets
glaciers (see Figure 2.4).

The three outlet glaciers are referred to as Nioghalvfjerdsfjorden glacier, Zachariae ice
stream, and Storstrømmen glacier. Together these outlets glaciers drains 20% of the
entire GrIS (Rignot and Kanagaratnam, 2006) and therefore there is a great interest for
understanding the changes they are undergoing.
According to (Khan et al., 2014) the Nioghalvfjerdsfjorden glacier and Zachariae ices-
tream have been found to be stable up until 2003. They are both marine terminating
outlet glaciers and have previously been conﬁned by sea ice, which has suppressed
their advance, and had a buttressing effect on the two glaciers (Khan et al., 2014),
(Messerli et al., 2014). As the climate have changed, bringing along warmer ocean tem-
peratures, the sea ice has declined which opened up the front of the two glaciers and

2.3
Mass Balance
The Mass Balance (MB) of a glacier, or an entire ice sheet, is determined by the snowfall
that adds mass (accumulation) and mass lost (ablation) by sublimation, melt-water
runoff, and discharge of ice into the ocean. If the gains and losses, or the accumulation
and ablation, are not the same, it leads to a change over time. A positive MB signiﬁes a
gain and therefore induces a thickening of the glacier. Opposed to a negative MB that
signiﬁes a loss, which therefore results in thinning of the glacier, and/or a retreat.
In Figure 2.5 the annual speciﬁc surface balance varies from positive to negative de-
pending on the location on the glacier. The altitude where the accumulation equals
the ablation is called the Equilibrium Line Altitude (ELA).
Figure 2.5: The annual speciﬁc surface balance varies from location to location on the
glacier. The ELA is where the accumulation equals the ablation. The Figure originates
from Cuffey and Paterson (2010).
In the accumulation zone above the ELA the balance is positive annually and below
the ELA, in the ablation zone, the balance is negative at the end of the year (Cuffey
and Paterson, 2010). The MB is the sum of all accumulation and ablation processes.

The variability between the transmitted signal and the received signal discloses details

When the radar signal reaches the surface of the ice sheet, some of it is reﬂected back,
but a part of the signal penetrates through the ice, due to volume scattering. The vol-
ume scattering in the ice causes the signal to be transmitted into the medium and to
be reﬂected several times at new interfaces.
The schematics in Figure 2.6 additionally demonstrate how the radar signal will be re-

be a volcanic ash layer or a layer with a different crystal orientation. In the upper parts
of the ice sheet, with snow, ﬁrn, and ice, the density changes can also result in a reﬂec-
tion. The further down in the ice the radar penetrates the more uniform the density of
the ice becomes.
These principles of radar makes it possible to obtain information about what happens
at the surface and underneath the opaque surface of the ice. The radar provide infor-
mation about the bed rock topography, the ice thickness, and the internal features in
the ice. The magnitude of the penetration depth of the radar signal depends on the
frequency of the signal. The frequency used varies between 60-230 MHz 2 depending
on the goal of the mission and the instrument. This method of using radar to reveal
details about the ice sheet is denoted RES, and is a commonly used method to provide
the observer with information about the internal structures of the ice. When utilizing
the method of RES, a crossection of the ice is obtained, denoted Echogram, which is
explained in detail in Chapter 6.

for, within a certain search region (see Figure 3.1 for the schematics). The program
does not search further away than the edge of the search region, hence it is important
to determine the width of the search region to be a little larger than the distance the

3.1
The Landsat Data
The Landsat missions consist of multiple satellites that ensure a continuous coverage
of the Earth. The ﬁrst Landsat satellite was launched by NASA in 1972, and in the fol-
lowing years repetitive launches have made a sustained daily coverage of the Earth
possible. Data from the Landsat 2 and 8 will be used in this study to investigate the
changes that have occurred in the last 40 years between 1975 and 2015.
Landsat 2 was launched in 1975 and the mission was put on standby in 1983 (LandSat2,
2016). The repeat cycle of Landsat 2 was 18 days, and the data from the Landsat 2
satellite is spread over 7 different spectral bands, and in this study band 7 will be used,
since it is the band with the least amount of lines crossing the image (see Figure 3.3).
The lines are artefacts and are a characteristic of some Landsat images. The images
have a wavelength of 0.50 - 0.6 micrometers, or in other words the images are obtained
in the visible part of the electromagnetic spectrum.
Landsat 8 was launched in 2013 and has a repeat cycle of only 16 days. The data from
the satellite extend across 11 different bands, and in this study band 8 will be used,
since it is the band with the highest ground resolution. It is called band 8 panchromatic
and has a wavelength of 0.50 - 0.68 micrometers, obtained in the visible part of the
electromagnetic spectrum (LandSat8, 2016).
The optical images from the Landsat missions are downloaded from the The Earth-
Explorer Web-page by (USGS, 2016). In this page every image is given an index num-
ber by which they can be identiﬁed, and along with the image, additional information
about the image is downloaded. For example, row and path, which is the numbers that
describes where the image was acquired along the satellite orbit, or the cloud cover,
which is of great importance in the visible part of the spectrum.
3.2
Data processing by ImGRAFT
In order to obtain the ice velocities of the glaciers, data-processing of the image pair is
performed using ImGRAFT. ImGRAFT is a MATLAB toolbox developed by Messerli and
Grinsted (2015) and it allows the user to easily generate the ice velocity ﬁeld, once a
suitable image pair is found.
Page 15
==PAGE:36==
Chapter 3. Ice Velocities

The process of locating the image pair covering the same area, within a short period
of time and with a low percentage of cloud cover, can be challenging. The images are
obtained in the optical spectrum, hence the cloud cover in the image will potentially
obscure the features being investigated. Since clouds, snow, and ice all appear white in
the image, the quality image can be utilized to distinguish between them. The quality
image shows different colors for clouds, snow, and ice and has been used to verify that
no clouds are blocking the glacier in question.
Preprocessing of the images is necessary in order to enhance the local surface texture
(Fahnestock et al., 2015). The method for preprocessing of the images depends on the
image. The Landsat 2 images inherently have lines crossing the image (see Figure 3.3),
making feature tracking more difﬁcult. Hence the lines are removed using a special-
ized ﬁlter provided by Aslak Grinsted. The Landsat 8 images are preprocessed with a
gaussian high-pass ﬁlter to enhance the features that are advancing due to ice ﬂow.
This improves the ability to retrieve the displaced features. The ice velocities are deter-
mined by utilizing feature tracking between the two images and if the time between the
two images is too long, no correlation is found between the two images, making fea-
ture tracking and thus ice velocity measurements unachievable. The period between
the images, before all features are obscured, is shorter in areas with high accumulation
rates and signiﬁcantly lower in areas with low accumulation rates (Fahnestock et al.,
2015). Therefore if a lot of precipitation has occurred between the two dates where the
images have been captured, it will conceal the features, making feature tracking im-
possible. The northeastern GrIS is an area with low accumulation rate and therefore is
it possible to perform feature tracking on images far apart in acquisition time.
The procedure of locating the image A and image B, and perform feature tracking on
them entails 4 steps and is described in the following, and is illustrated in Figure 3.2
below:
1. Image A is determined by using the EarthExplorer web-page by USGS. By select-
ing an area and a timeframe the images available will appear and the row and
path numbers of the image are noted. Once an image that has a low cloud cover
percentage is found, the image can be downloaded directly as a tiff ﬁle.
2. The image B needs to be selected. The requirements for image B are to have the
same row and path as image A, low cloud cover and snow cover.
3. Preprocessing of the images: ﬁltering of the images according to what is neces-

ﬁlter that removes the inherent lines but preserves the information in the image.
The newer images from Landsat 8 are ﬁltered with a gaussian high-pass ﬁlter.
4. Finally the two images can be used to generate the velocity ﬁeld by the means

Flux gate method
In this chapter the dynamic mass loss and thinning is investigated by the means of the
ﬂux gate method, in order to quantify the ice discharge from the three outlet glaciers
in 1975 and in 2015, respectively.
The ﬂux-gate method is a part of the “input-output” approach, that considers the MB
as the difference between mass gained through precipitation and mass lost by sublima-
tion and melt-water runoff (SMB), and discharge of ice into the ocean (D) (See Section
2.3). In this study the dynamic mass loss in the area, in the form of ice discharge (D), is
investigated, even though the entire mass loss consist of both discharge and the SMB of
the ice sheet. The ice discharge in the Northeastern GrIS is found to be the dominating
cause of mass loss (Kjeldsen et al., 2015) and is therefore the focus of this study.
The ice velocity maps generated in Chapter 3, are used to determine the ice discharge
by the means of the ﬂux-gate method which is explained in the following section.

Radio Echo Sounding of Ice
The method of RES involves using a low frequency radar, by which it is possible to
penetrate the ice and perform ice sounding. During the ﬁrst DTU missions in 1968
(Overgaard, 1984), the goal was to measure the ice thickness, but quickly other inter-
esting features inside the ice were discovered. One type of feature, found within the
ice, is denoted basal ice units (BIU) and can extend up to more than half of the entire
column of the ice. The BIU is lifting up the otherwise stratiﬁed ice in a way that cannot
be explained by the underlying bed topography, as it can be seen on Figure 6.1 to the
left. It is northern Greenland that has the largest amount of detected BIUs (Panton and
Karlsson, 2015). The margins of an ice stream are visible in the RES data as Units of Dis-
rupted Radio stratigraphy (UDR) as it can be seen in Figure 6.1 to the right. Both BIUs
and UDRs are investigated in this chapter and can reveal if any internal changes of the
ice have occurred in the period between 1979 and 2014. Therefore the method of RES
is considered an important and non-invasive source for obtaining crucial information
about the interior of the ice sheet.
Page 51
==PAGE:72==
Chapter 6. Radio Echo Sounding of Ice
Natalia Havelund Andersen
Figure 6.1: Examples of features found within the ice. To the left is a basal ice unit (BIU)
and to the right a unit of disrupted radio stratigraphy (UDR) can be seen. The images are
both from the CRESIS data, which is explained in detail in Section 6.2.
The BIU observed in the DTU RES data from the 1970s is assumed to be caused by
refrozen melt water on the bottom of the ice (Bell et al., 2014). The same category of
features has been found in the new CRESIS radar data from the NASA mission called
ICEBRIDGE (CRESIS, 2016). A more thorough explanation of the formation process
of these BIUs has yet to be found, but they often occur in the vicinity of fast ﬂowing
ice streams. The BIUs have been found to change direction as they move closer to a
fast ﬂowing ice stream. The closer they get, the more they appear to be aligned with
the ﬂow direction, according to the study by Panton and Karlsson (2015). This hap-
pens until the surface velocity becomes greater than 100 m/year, at which point they
vanish.
In this study the vertical extent of the BIUs is investigated at the locations where the
DTU radar ﬂight lines and the CRESIS ﬂight lines are intersecting, and where there is
a BIU present. These requirements limit the amount of locations where comparisons
can be made (explained in detail in the Section 6.3).
The RES radar data can also identify fast ﬂowing and slow moving ice, by observing the
internal layers. Locations where the otherwise continuous internal layers suddenly
become disrupted, a UDR, can be assumed to be at the edge of an ice stream (Bingham
and Siegert, 2007). Hence these locations are used as indicators for if the NEGIS has
moved or increased in size during the 35 year period between the two data sets were
acquired.

The onboard navigation system was an inertial navigation system (INS), where the

which is about 5 km (Overgaard, 1984). The INS can be recalibrated during ﬂight when
ﬂying over known features, but this is limited to the margin of the GrIS since almost no
recognizable features exist inland (Overgaard, 1984).

6.2
CRESIS radar data
NASA launched the airborne IceBridge Mission in 2009 to bridge the gap between two
satellite missions; ICESat (2002-2009) and ICESat-2 (expected launch in 2017). This
is done to ensure that the changing ice sheet is monitored continuously. The Ice-
Bridge Mission contains various instruments to perform the necessary measurements,
such as snow depth, ice thickness, and bed topography amongst many parameters.
The NASA Operation IceBridge Mission holds a Multichannel Coherent Radar Depth
Sounder with the abbrevation MCoRDS. The MCoRDS has a frequency range from 140-
230 MHz with a bandwidth of 20-40 MHz and a peak power of 200 W (CRESIS MCoRDS,
2016). This instrument can be used to measure the thickness of the ice sheet and map
the subsurface structures of the ice equivalent to the mission performed by DTU in the
1970’s.
The ﬂight paths obtained during the IceBridge Mission do not coincide very well with
the location of the old DTU radar data obtained in the 1970’s shown in Figure 6.5.

structures in the ice, discovered by the two missions, at one location, as seen in Figure
6.6. Comparison of the appearance of the structures requires ﬂight tracks in the same
orientation in order to decrease the spatial difference as much as possible, leaving only
the temporal difference to be a factor of variation.
The Center for Remote Sensing of Ice Sheets (CReSIS) has made the radar data available
at a ftp server for downloading. Before downloading the data it is recommended to

Summary of results
The mass balance in the northeastern GrIS has been close to zero up until 2003 (Kjeld-
sen et al., 2015), due to the conﬁning effect of sea ice in the area. Once the sea ice has
disappeared due to warming ocean temperatures changes have been observed in all
the methods employed in this study.
Ice velocities - The computation of the ice velocities, with ImGRAFT Messerli and
Grinsted (2015), depends on locating a suitable image pair. This is easier for Landsat 8
data and more demanding for Landsat 2 data due to the reduced amount of available
Landsat 2 data. Furthermore the available Landsat 2 data in the northeastern GrIS are
of poor quality due to clouds obscuring the features in the images.
Images from Landsat 2 are covered by crossing lines and affected by major map offsets
making preprocessing of the data imperative. Filtering and preprocessing improves
the ice velocity results for both Landsat 8 and Landsat 2.
The obtained ice velocities shows a signiﬁcant increase for both Nioghalvfjerdsfjorden
and Zachariae. The opposite was the case for Storstrømmen glacier which decreased
in velocitiy.

Surface elevation changes - The long term trend in the accumulated surface elevation
changes investigated in Chapter 4 revealed a thinning trend. The thinning trend is
dominating along the margin close to the outlet glaciers, except for the Storstrømmen
glacier, which is building up mass.
The mass changes in the drainage basin was computed below the ELA in order to en-
sure the correct density was used to compute the mass. Above the ELA not just ice, but
also snow and ﬁrn are present, which makes the mass calculation more challenging.
The location of the ELA was determined to be where the SMB was negative. An overall
declining trend in the mass change was found and the mass loss is therefore getting
bigger each year by −0.54Gt/year 2.
Flux gate method - The ﬂuxes are computed from various data sets collected in this
study. The calculation of the ﬂux entails ﬁnding the ice thickness by subtracting the
bed elevation from the ice surface elevation. Two models, with two different grid sizes,
of the bed and surface was used in order to estimate the empirical uncertainties. The
dense grid of the bed topography yields a larger ﬂux since the bed appears more ragged
and hence results in a larger amount of ice underneath the surface.
The mean mass ﬂuxes from Nioghalvfjerdsfjorden and Zachariae have increased con-
siderably during the 40 year period from 1975 to 2015 (See Table 5.3). Again, however,
the mean mass ﬂux from the Storstrømmen glacier has decreased.
Radio echo sounding of ice - When comparing the old DTU radar data with the new
CRESIS data no signiﬁcant change is found in the vertical extent of the BIUs. Opposed
to the UDRs where a possible change is detected. Searching for the same shear mar-
gins in the DTU data as in the CRESIS data only revealed the right and internal shear
margin. The left shear margin was not present in the DTU radar data.

Discussion
The results obtained in this thesis shows that the NEGIS has indeed experienced large
changes in the observed 40-year period. On the opposite hand no changes were ob-
served in the BIUs found outside the NEGIS. In the following each chapter of the thesis
is discussed.
Ice velocities - The ice velocities have been found to have increased immensely in
the observed 40-year period. The results in this study continues the same trend of
increasing ice velocities as found by Mouginot et al. (2015).
There is a seasonal bias on the computed ice velocities from the Landsat 8 data due to
the short period between acquisition times. However, since Rignot and Kanagaratnam
(2006) decided to neglect the seasonal bias, it is not accounted for here.
The uncertainty of the ice velocities obtained in Chapter 3 and presented in Table 3.2
could be estimated as an empirical uncertainty by locating many different image pairs
over the same area in the same period. This has not been done in this study because
only one image pair could be located in 1975 and 1976. However, when comparing
with the empirical uncertainty of the ice velocities obtained in the study by Messerli
Page 71
==PAGE:92==

and Grinsted (2015) which states around ±0.02 m/day, the computed ice velocities pre-
sented in Table 3.2 is well within that uncertainty. Even though it is expected that the
uncertainty on the Landsat 2 velocities are higher than the newer Landsat 8 velocities
due to an enhanced resolution on the Landsat 8 images.
Surface elevation changes - The accumulated surface elevation changes shows that
the northeastern drainage basin has been relatively stable until 2003, which is also in
agreement with the study conducted by Khan et al. (2014). After 2003 a more pro-
nounced thinning around Nioghalvfjerdsfjorden and Zachariae is happening, and the
thinning trend is beginning to propagate inland making the ice stream more narrow
and distinct. One probable cause for the thinning to continue upstream is due to the
warming ocean temperatures removing sea ice, hence relieving the buttressing effect
and letting the glaciers ﬂow faster (Kjeldsen et al., 2015). This effect can clearly be seen
on the Zachariae glaicer that has retreated quickly since 2003, as illustrated in Figure
3.7. This is the case along the coast except for the Storstrømmen glacier that have been
known to have a surging tendency(Reeh et al., 1994), and therefore responds differently
than the Nioghalvfjerdsfjorden glacier and Zachariae glacier.
The decreasing trend in the mass change reveals a mass loss in the area of 0.54Gt/year 2
on average, which is consistent with the results of general thinning in the accumulated
surface elevation data. This is, however, computed without correction for vertical up-
lift of the bedrock due to the declining load of ice on top. Another noteworthy aspect
is that the increased mass loss is, according to Khan et al. (2014); Kjeldsen et al. (2015),
driven by primarily dynamic mass loss and therefore is the ice discharge discussed
next.
Flux gate method - Quantifying the ice discharge from the three outlet glaciers in the
northeastern GrIS shows an increase in the amount of discharged ice. This is in line
with the increase found in the ice velocities and the enhanced thinning in the drainage
basin. The ﬂuxes are computed at three ﬂux gates at the three outlet glaciers, that all
are positioned at their approximate grounding line. This is done in order to ensure that
the ﬂux through the ﬂux gates are the dynamic ice discharge and does not melt after
passing through the ﬂux gate.
The surface from the 1970s could not be obtained directly therefore the measured ice
thicknesses from the DTU missions was used. The DTU ice thickness was compared
to the Bamber 2001 ice thickness. A conservative estimate of the surface elevation at
the margin was set to be 20% larger in the 1970’s than the Bamber 2001 surface. This

The ice velocities used in the computation are the surface velocities from Chapter 3,

the formation process of these BIU must be assumed to be larger than 35 years and
likely more than that, since no signiﬁcant change in the vertical extent of these BIUs
has been found.
That being said, there was only one location where a direct comparison of the BIUs
were possible due to the fact that the coverage of the DTU radar data does not match
the coverage of the CRESIS radar data.
It is therefore necessary to obtain more available CRESIS data from the same locations
as the DTU radar data in order to reach the full potential of the DTU radar data set.
This can be done by retracing the old ﬂight lines to both validate the coordinates of the
old DTU radar data and allowing for further studies of the vertical extent of the BIUs.
Furthermore the positions of the BIUs could also be observed, in order to evaluate if
they remain stationary or if they move in the direction of ice ﬂow.
The investigation of the UDRs is of great importance since it appear as if the left shear
margin was not present in the 1970s. If the left shear margin did not exist in 1979 it
would mean that the ice stream has increased dramatically in size since 1979, which is
a short time frame for such large glaciological changes.

Therefore, possible explanations for the missing UDR is elaborated on in the following.
The reason why the left shear margin is not visible in the DTU data can either be due
to the skewed angle of the ﬂight line, the error on the coordinates, the fact that it used
to be less prominent and therefore is not visible in the old DTU data, or the fact that it
simply was not there.
The angle of the ﬂight line could hide the dipping layers of the left shear margin, but
since both the internal and the right shear margin are visible at the skewed angle it
appears unlikely that this is the cause.
Secondly the coordinates of the radar data could be off-positioning the radar data by
many kilometers causing the entire cross section to shift. As found in Chapter 6 the
error needed for a shift large enough is 30 km. However, since the BIU structures in the
DTU radar to the left of the ice stream (see Figure 6.12 at the 10 km mark in the along
track distance) are similar to what can be seen in the CRESIS data (see Figure 6.9) then
a shift large enough is considered unlikely as well.
Finally the idea of a less prominent appearing shear margin seems like a reasonable
cause for the UDR to not appear in the DTU data.
In order to validate the real cause of the missing UDR it is necessary to retrace the
ﬂight line from 1979 in order to compare the bed topography, and thereby check if
the coordinates are correctly positioned. At the moment it is not possible to rule out
the possibility that the ice stream actually was thinner in 1979 than now, and abruptly
changed in size during the 40 year period. Further studies are necessary, with a better
coverage of the radar data, in order to validate the location of the radar track.

9
Conclusions
In this study the changes in the NEGIS have been investigated during a 40 year period.
The study has been conducted using various data sets in order to examine the changes
in the NEGIS on the surface of the ice and inside the ice. Four different parameters:
surface elevation changes, ice velocities, ice ﬂux, and changes in the internal structures
in the ice, have been used to obtain the overall picture of the NEGIS. It is not easily
determined how fast the northeastern GrIS is changing due to climate changes, but it
is surely changing, as it also was found by Khan et al. (2014).
Ice velocities computed from 1975 and 2015 show a signiﬁcant increase for the Nioghalvf-
jerdsfjorden glacier of 50.96% and the Zachariae icestream of 84.08% during the 40 year
period. However, a decrease for the Storstrømmen glacier of -67.78% was found, due
to the fact that it appears to be in a stagnant period without surging. However, the
ﬂow upstream the Storstrømmen glacier continues, which leads to a build up of mass,
according to Joughin et al. (2010).
This thesis also looked into the accumulated elevation changes in the period from
1992-2014 in the northeastern GrIS and a clear thinning trend is visible along with a

thinning trend propagating upstream the NEGIS. This makes the NEGIS appear more
narrow and distinct in the accumulated elevation changes. Further studies revealed a
mass loss with a clear decreasing trend of -0.54 Gt/year 2 in the area below the ELA in
the northeastern GrIS drainage basin.
Computation of the ﬂuxes from the three outlet glaciers was done using the computed
ice velocities from 1975 and 2015. The results showed an increase of discharged ice
during the 40 year period and the results from 2015 are in good agreement with the
results from Messerli et al. (2014). The ice surface model used was scaled down with
20% to represent the ice surface in the 1970s. Different ice surface and bed topography
models was used in order to compute an estimated uncertainty of the computed ﬂuxes.
The DTU radar data from 1978 and 1979 has been digitized prior to the data processing.
The BIUs outside the NEGIS, investigated here, showed no signiﬁcant sign of change
when compared with the CRESIS data form 2014. This leads to a formation process of
the BIUs that exceeds 35 years. On the other hand, the UDRs located in ﬂight path 07
1979 shows a possible change when compared with the CRESIS data from 2014. The
right and internal shear margin, as also found by Keisling et al. (2014), is visible in the
DTU radar data, but no left shear margin has been found. Several explanations for this
has been discussed, and it is possibly due to the large errors in the coordinates that
misplaces the radar track or a less prominent shear margin in 1979.
To conclude, an increase in ice velocities, thinning, and mass ﬂux is documented in
this thesis. Furthermore, a change in the location of the shear margin of the NEGIS is
discussed. The changes, that have occurred in the NEGIS during the last 40 years, have
been investigated and future studies have been suggested. It is paramount to obtain
knowledge about this area due to the large extent of the NEGIS and the possibility for
marginal changes propagating far inland.

After locating the interesting black and white ﬁlms with the ice-penetrating radar data

The setup for saving of each of the frames is governed by the numbers along the ﬁlm
strip, where the image id is decided to be the CBD number in the left bottom corner as
indicated be the red circle in Figure B.1 below. The CBD number will be refereed to as
the radar id number.

so the next image has id 178 in the left corner and 177 in the right corner. This enables
ud to later stitch the images together without any areas with no data.

B.2
The Coordinates of the radar data
Every time a measurement have been performed with the radar instrument, the cor-
responding coordinates have been measured as well. The coordinates are given in a
printed data catalogue for each year and the coordinates corresponds to the id num-
ber on the black and white ﬁlm strips with the radar data as shown in Figure B.1.
The data catalogue gives an overview of the obtained navigation data (Overgaard, 1984),
where the id of the coordinates relate to the id of the radar images as illustrated by the
red circle in ﬁgure B.1.
The coordinates in the data catalogue are given in degrees, minutes and 10th of a
minute, which is converted into latitude and longitude. The coordinates obtained
along the ﬂight path have a theoretical accuracy of 1-3 km (3 nautical miles = 5 km
(Overgaard, 1984)), however in practice it turns out that it is closer to 1 km when the
ﬂight paths are straight and a lot more than 5 km when the ﬂight changes direction.
The Inertial Navigation System (INS) was in the 1970s considered as a reliable instru-
ment and the aircraft in 1978 was equipped with such a system. During the ﬂight is was
only possible to adjust the INS system when ﬂying over known features, like a narrow
fjord, but it is almost impossible to adjust when ﬂying over the ice sheet due to the lack
of landmarks.
In addition to the navigation data the data catalogue also holds information about the
height over sea level and the ice thickness along the ﬂight path.
It is necessary to digitalize the coordinates by scanning the data catalogue and using
Optimal Character Recognition (OCR) software on the scanned image in order to get
the data in a .txt, which is readable. There are many different programs for perform-
ing the OCR, and in this study the program for MAC called Abbyy Finereader was used.
Other programs like Omnipage works well on DOS systems. The Abbyy Finereader pro-
gram can optimize the image and correct for noise in the image by adjusting the con-
trast before the OCR procedure starts. Due to the not optimal form of the rather old
printout of the data it is still not straightforward using the OCR software since num-
bers might be recognized as letters. Therefore the conversion cannot be performed
with out a certain amount of manual work. To test if the OCR software did the job cor-
rectly the data can be plotted in order to check if there is any major outliers along the
ﬂight track.
