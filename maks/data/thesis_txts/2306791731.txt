Abstract
An elastic-plastic analysis of size-eﬀects in granular ﬂow is carried out using the
ﬁnite element method. Focus will be on the dense inertial regime, for dry granular
material and ﬂow rates small enough, that inertial eﬀects can be neglected.
Nielsen and Niordson 2014’s visco-plastic numerical formulation of Fleck and
Willis 2009’s strain-gradient plasticity theory is implemented in 1D in Matlab, and
it is modiﬁed to better capture size-eﬀects seen in granular ﬂow.
First an inﬁnite shear slab between two inﬁnitely rigid plates with gravity is
implemented.
Then the model is extended to capture the eﬀects of ﬂow on an
inclined plane, where the top surface is free.
For the ﬁrst, displacement controlled model, qualitative reproduction of shear
bands is proven successful.
However the model of ﬂow on inclined planes is not successfully implemented.
More considerations have to be made regarding meaningful ratios between parame-
ters and the deﬁnition of the initial yield criterion.

Resumé
En elastisk-plastisk analyse af størrelseseﬀekter i granulært ﬂow udføres ved brug
af ﬁnite element metoden. Der fokuseres på det kompakt ﬂydende regime, for tørt
granulært materiale og lille nok ﬂowrate til at inertikræfter kan negligeres.
Nielsen og Niordson 2014’s viskoplastiske numeriske formulering af Fleck og
Willis 2009’s tøjningsgradient-plasticitetsteori implementeres i 1D i Matlab og mod-
iﬁeceres for bedre at fange størrelseseﬀekter set i granulært ﬂow.
Først implementeres et uendeligt lag granulært materiale udsat for forskydning
mellem to uendelige plader med tyngdekraft, hvorefter modellen udvides til ﬂow på
skråplan, hvor den øverste plade fjernes, så toppen af laget er fri.
For den forskydningskontrollerede model lykkedes det kvalitativt at reproducere
forskydningbåndet.
Imidlertid lykkedes det ikke at få modellen for ﬂow på skråplan til at virke.
Der skal gøres ﬂere overvejelser om bestemmelse af meningsfulde forhold mellem
parametrene og måden startﬂydespændningen skal deﬁneres på.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Granular materials can e.g. be geophysical (gravel, sand, soil), industrial raw ma-
terials, foodstuﬀs (rice, corn, cereal), pharmaceuticals (tablets, capsules, powder)
or powder for construction. It is the second most handled material in the industry
after water, as measured in tons (Duran 1996), but unlike standard ﬂuids and elastic
solids, granular matter lacks a well-understood theoretical foundation as no univer-
sal continuum model exists, that can predict its behaviour in an arbitrary geometry
with arbitrary loading. Hence empirical relations are being used.
One of the most accurate methods that has been used to model granular ﬂow, is
the discrete element method (DEM). But since it solves the equations of motion for
each individual grain, the method is too computationally heavy for larger domains or
longer time spans, such as in industrial and geological applications (Dunatunga and
Kamrin 2015). This is partly the motivation why a predictive granular continuum
model, that can correctly represent many millions of particles interacting through
dissipative, frictional contacts is needed (Henann et al. 2016).
One of the challenges in modelling granular media, is its ability to switch between
diﬀerent states. Imagine for instance an hourglass (see ﬁg. 1.1). The sand in the
bottom of the glass lies still and supports shear loads (solid like) from the sand
sliding down the sides of the pile (liquid like), while the sand falling down from the
top half of the glass separates and collides (gas-like).

Chapter 1. Introduction
Because of the multidisciplinary nature of this media, a combination of theory
from the ﬁelds of solid mechanics, ﬂuid mechanics and statistical physics are needed
to make a uniﬁed theory, which the Kamrin group is working on at MIT1.
Another challenge when considering granular media, using a continuum ap-
proach, is its nonlocal behaviour due to the cooperativity of grain motion, which is
scaled inherently by the size of the grains. That is, mechanical properties at some
location are inﬂuenced by the ﬂow taking place at other locations (Henann et al.
2016). Size-eﬀects can also be seen by the repose angle theory being contradicted
in experiments; when the layer thickness is small, the angle at which a granular
ﬂow comes to a stop, depends sensitively on the thickness of the layer (Kamrin and
Henann 2015, p181).
In this thesis the focus will be on a solid mechanics continuum approach, where
a strain-gradient plasticity theory (by Fleck & Willis), will be used to model dense
granular ﬂow in an attempt to take the nonlocality into account. Only dry mate-
rial will be considered, that can not support tension and in general, the particles
considered are larger than about 1µm, such that van der Waals forces and ordinary
thermal forces are negligible (Goddard 2014). The constitutive equations for dry
granular ﬂows, which govern how the material moves under shear stress, are still a
matter of debate (Jop, Forterre, and Pouliquen 2006).
Initially the material will be treated as an elasto-viscoplastic solid and then the
model will be extended to take into account pressure sensitivity through a modiﬁed
yield criterion.
The theory will be implemented in Matlab, where small strains and plane strain
is assumed. At ﬁrst the Fleck and Willis 2009 theory is implemented using the
Nielsen and Niordson 2014 discretisation, to test the implementation against known
results. Then it is expanded to take hydrostatic pressure into account.

Chapter 2
The Finite Element Method
The ﬁnite element method (FEM), is a numerical tool, that can be used to solve
boundary value problems for partial diﬀerential equations. In this project, it will be
used to solve for the spatial distribution of ﬁeld variables in a 1D domain, namely
displacement rates and later on also plastic shear strain rates. The method works
by dividing the domain into a ﬁnite number of sub domains, that are modelled by
elements of diﬀerent kinds, depending on the problem. In this case 3-node isopara-
metric quadratic elements will be used for the displacement domain, while 2-node
isoparametric linear elements will be used for the plastic shear strain domain.
Initially the FE formulation is derived for an elastic system, and later on, it will
be expanded to take plasticity into account and then again expanded to take strain
gradient plasticity into account through coupling with a domain of plastic shear
strain rates, in chapter 3 and chapter 4 respectively.

Chapter 4
Strain-Gradient Plasticity
As mentioned in the introduction, granular materials show non-local behaviour in
diﬀerent conﬁgurations. To try and capture this feature, the visco-plastic (rate-
dependent) version of the strain gradient plasticity theory by Fleck and Willis 2009
and its numerical basis by Nielsen and Niordson 2014 is implemented numerically.
A drawback to the method considered is that higher order stress quantities can be
discontinuous for certain inﬁnitesimal load changes (Nielsen and Niordson 2013).
In chapter 3, the plastic part of the strain rate, was easily determined by pre-
scribing 10% plasticity. In this chapter however, the plastic strain rate now has to
be solved for in a diﬀerent way, as the constitutive equations are more complicated.
As a higher order theory is used, a new domain and a separate set of boundary
conditions for the plastic strain ﬁeld are introduced.
The solutions to the ﬁeld variables of the two domains (the displacement rate
domain and the plastic strain rate domain) depend on each other. So assuming
that the displacement ﬁeld and the plastic strain ﬁeld are known everywhere in a
current state, the plastic strain rates can be determined from the Minimum Principle
I (Fleck and Willis 2009) (MPI). Afterwards, with the strain rates being known from
the use of MPI, the displacement rates can be obtained from the Minimum Principle
II (Fleck and Willis 2009) (MPII). The two solutions are used to update the current
state of the displacements and the plastic strains, and the two steps are solved
successively a given number of times.
To initialize the algorithm, the system is
solved for a small elastic step.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
An elastic-plastic analysis of some of the size-eﬀects in granular ﬂow was carried out
employing the ﬁnite element method. Focus was on the dense inertial regime, where
ﬂow rates considered were small enough, that inertial eﬀect could be neglected. The
granular material considered was dry and assumed not to support tension, why only
ﬂow, where the grains were in contact was modelled.
The numerical formulation of the visco-plastic version by Nielsen and Niordson
2014 of the strain-gradient plasticity theory formulated by Fleck and Willis 2009
was implemented in 1D in Matlab. It was modiﬁed to take the hydrostatic pressure
into account through the formulation of the initial yield stress.
The inﬁnite shear slab with gravity was implemented and qualitative reproduc-
tion of shear bands was obtained. However, the tendency of the thickness yielded
opposite dependence of the wall pressure of those seen in Kamrin and Koval 2012.
An eﬀort was made to extend the model to free surface ﬂow on an inclined plane.
However the model was not successfully implemented. More considerations need to
be made regarding meaningful ratios between parameters to prevent the stiﬀness
matrix from becoming singular, and the formulation of the initial yield criterion
needs to be thought through more carefully.
