Abstract 
The worldwide healthcare community are running out ways to fight the spread of bacterial infections as a 
rising number of species develop resistance against most antibiotics. The quorum-sensing system of S. 
aureus represents an interesting target for new type of antibiotics that would prevent the spread of MRSA. 
Knowledge about the quorum-sensing system and the associated autoinducing peptides (AIPs) are 
therefore of high importance.  
AIPs from different strains of S. aureus have been shown to inhibit the quorum-sensing system of each 
other and the AIP of S. schleiferi has shown great potency as an inhibitor of S. aureus. The primary 
sequence of the AIPs is well known, but little is known about their 3D structure, which is of great 
importance in the development of new antibiotics.  
This project investigates the 3D structure of AIPs through nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR). Internuclear 
distances were derived from 2D NOESY experiments and were used to select the set of structures that on 
average best matched the experimental data from a wide ensemble generated by computational 
modelling. The newly developed S3 HMBC experiment for accurate sign selective measurement of 
homonuclear and heteronuclear coupling constant were implemented at high field and used on the 
investigated AIPs.

Introduction 
 
In 2014 the World Health Organization, WHO, published a report warning about the rise of antimicrobial 
resistance worldwide and the very real threat of a post-antibiotic era beginning in the 21st century [1]. If 
infectious agents become resistant to antibiotics, we would see the return to the time before modern 
medicine, where minor infections and injuries can mean death.  
An example is strains of the bacteria Staphylococcus aureus developing resistance towards antibacterial 
drugs, termed methicillin-resistant S. aureus (MRSA). S. aureus can be a part of the normal flora of bacteria, 
but also cause a variety of infections and cases of MRSA has therefore mostly been found related to 
hospitalization [2]. In Denmark, a rising number of cases has been documented of people infected by 
community-acquired multiresistant MRSA in later years, with about 20% coming from abroad [3]. As the 
healthcare community are running out of effective antibiotics against MRSA, a great need arises for 
alternative ways to combat infections by S. aureus. 
Such an alternative is inhibiting the quorum sensing (QS) circuit required for the virulence of S. aureus [4]. 
QS is an intercellular communication process, where each cell secretes a signal molecule, the autoinducer, 
which accumulates in the surroundings with a concentration corresponding to the local population density 
of the bacteria [4]. The QS system includes the production of the autoinducer and the surface receptor it 
activates, resulting in a positive feedback circuit that will react to a concentration build-up in the 
surroundings. Once a sufficient population has been reached, the QS system can activate the production of 
virulence proteins. Inhibition of the QS system therefore becomes a desirable target in the search for new 
antibacterial drugs, as studies have shown that inhibition attenuates the spread of S. aureus [5]. Because 
the new potential drugs would only inhibit the spread of S. aureus, but leaving the actual killing of bacteria 
cells to the immune system, the present pressure on the bacteria to develop resistance would not ensue. 
Antibacterial drugs targeting the QS system could hereby be a long-lasting solution.    
For S. aureus, the signal molecule of the QS system is a small peptide, called the autoinducing peptide (AIP) 
and this has been the focus of this project. The primary sequence of the AIPs have been known for 20 years 
and extensive structure-activity-relationship (SAR) studies have investigated which structural features are 
important for binding to the receptor, but little is known about the conformation the AIP adopts in solution 
[5][6][7][8]. This project set out to explore the 3D structure of AIPs in solution through nuclear magnetic 
resonance (NMR) spectroscopy aided by computational modelling. Nuclear Overauser effect spectroscopy 
(NOESY) experiments provided data for calculations of internuclear distances and exact J-coupling 
constants were extracted from S3 HMBC spectra – a new in-house developed experiment, which 
implemented on 600 MHz and 800 MHz spectrometers as part of this project [9][10].

Quorum sensing in S. aureus 
The S. aureus chromosome contains the locus named the accessory gene regulator (agr), which controls the 
synthesis of a number of exoproteins [11]. The locus consists of the two neighboring operons P2 and P3, 
each having their own transcription promoter. The P2 operon includes four open reading frames AgrA, 
AgrB, AgrC and AgrD, which together makes an autocatalytic system and controls the activation of P3 
operon transcription into RNAIII as seen in Figure 1 [12].  
 
Figure 1: Overview of the quorum sensing system in S. aureus (reproduced from [4]). 
AgrD contains the peptide sequence that will become the AIP after proteolytic cleavage at two sites and 
cyclization with the formation of a thioester [13]. The N-terminal end of AgrD is an amphipathic α-helical 
region and is responsible for the peptide being membrane bound [14]. What will later become the AIP is 
placed in the middle of AgrD with the C-terminal end being a hydrophilic recognition sequence [14].  
AgrB functioning as an endopeptidase carries out the cleaving towards the mature AIP at the C-terminal 
site and is located in the cytoplasmic membrane with six transmembrane segments [15][16]. The residues 
from AgrB, His77 and Cys84, involved in the reaction are both placed on the inner surface of the membrane 
[15]. The peptide sequence of AgrB are highly variable and the interaction between AgrB and AgrD are 
group specific, but residues such as His77 and Cys84 are among those conserved across groupings [17].  
 
Figure 2: The interaction between AgrD and AgrB followed by the proposed process of transport  
and cleavage of the free AIP (reproduced from [18]). 
==PAGE:9==
Page 8 of 51 
 
The cleavage from the remaining AgrD occurs by activation of Cys84 by His77 followed by a nucleophilic 
attack on the last carbonyl of the AIP leading to formation of a thioester and the loss of the C-terminal end 
of the sequence. The AgrB-AgrD intermediate now facilitates the formation of the thiolactone ring by 
setting the scene for a thioester exchange with the cysteine within AgrD as seen in Step 3 in Figure 2 [18]. 
Before release of mature AIP, another proteolysis and translocation are needed and genes coding for the 
responsible enzymes are not found in the agr locus. Definite evidence for the order of events has not yet 
been found, but data supporting translocation as the first step has been provided, as a study showed that 
the general signal peptidase SpsB cleaves a peptide mimicking pro-AIP at the correct site [19]. As SpsB is 
placed on the outer face of the membrane and the N-terminal domain of AgrD has been found in the 
extracellular space [20], a model with initial translocation followed by proteolytic cleavage would fit very 
well to this system. The agent responsible for the transport of either AgrD-AIP or mature AIP through the 
membrane has yet to be identified. 
The free AIP diffuses until it meets the corresponding receptor AgrC, a dimeric histidine kinase (HK) [13]. 
AgrC codes for a 46kDa transmembrane protein with the N-terminal as the membrane-integrated sensor 
region and the C-terminal the HK domain shown in Figure 3 [21]. The dimer HK is activated via trans-
autophosphorylation by the AIP [22]. Due to a  highly variable N-terminal region and a conserved HK 
domain, it is to no surprise that residues in the second extracellular loop are responsible for AIP group 
selectivity [23]. The HK domain contains two subdomains, the dimerization and histidine phosphorylation 
(DHp) subdomain as well as the catalytic and ATP-binding (CA) subdomain [24].  
 
Figure 3: The structure of the QS receptor, AgrC, where the AIP binds to the extracellular sensor region  
and the P is the phosphoryl group bound to a histidine in the DHp subdomain (adapted from [25]). 
The DHp region is a hairpin of two helices, and dimerizes into a bundle of four α-helices. Between the 
sensor region and the DHp is a short helical linker, and this linker facilitates the activation of the HK by 
rotation of the helix causing changes in the dimeric DHp region, allowing for reposition of the CA domain 
facilitating the binding of ATP and phosphorylation of histidine [25]. Together AgrC and AgrA make up a 
two-component system with AgrA being the response regulator (RR) to the AgrC HK. AgrA consist of two 
domains with the N-terminal being the receiver module wherein the phosphorylatable aspartate amino 
acids lies. Upon phosphorylation the domain experience large conformational changes which mediates 
dimerization of the RR [26]. The C-terminal is the output domain responsible for DNA binding by 
recognizing specific sequence patterns separated by a 12 base-pair spacer [27]. AgrA binds to either the P2 
or P3 promoter regions with a higher affinity for P2 than P3 [28]. Herein lies the explanation on how the agr 
can differentiate its regulation of RNAII and RNAIII transcription; in case of shortage of activated of AgrA, 
RNAII transcription will dominate until the extracellular density of AIP is high enough for sufficient 
activation of the system. The quorum sensing system is therefore regulated by positive feedback.

Structure and groupings of AIPs 
Within the S. aureus the agr locus varies and based on differences in the coding of RNAII, four groups has 
been found, each producing different AIPs, AgrB and AgrC [5][29]. These groups were discovered due to the 
ability of one type of AIP to inhibit the agr response of the others.  
From here on, the AIP specific to group one will be designated AIP-I, group two AIP-II etc. Likewise, the 
receptors are named AgrC-I for group one, AgrC-II for group two etc. Of all the S. aureus strains, the largest 
part belongs to agr-I group [30]. Consequently, most studies have been done on this group and this project 
will focus on AIP-I as well as novel AIPs.  
The natural AIPs are small peptides of 7-9 amino acids of varying sequence [5]. All contain a central cysteine 
with the side chain thiol forming a thioester with the C-terminal, thus creating a cyclic structure and an 
exocyclic tail of varying length. 
 
Figure 4: The four AIPs with their similarities and differences in structure (reproduced from [31]). 
As indicated on Figure 4, all AIPs contains a 16-membered macrocycle, which has proven to be highly 
important for binding [7]. AIP-I and IV are largely matching structures, differentiated only at position five, 
being aspartic acid and tyrosine, respectively. Due to the high level of similarity between AIP-I and IV, each 
are able to cross-activate the receptor of the other, while all other cross-combinations has a deactivating 
effect on the system [4]. 
A number of SAR studies have tried to clarify the importance of each amino acid. One study did an alanine 
scan of AIP-I, which mostly resulted in a reduction of activity at all positions [7]. The exception is aspartic 
acid at position five; the replacement converted AIP-I into a potent inhibitor of all four agr [32]. This is 
interestingly also the position where AIP-I differs from AIP-IV. It was also found that exchanging the 
substituted alanine with glycine resulted in a huge loss of inhibitory effect [7].

Synthesis of AIPs 
Bengt Gless, from the group of professor Christian Adam Olsen (CAO) at KU, performed the synthesis of all 
AIPs used in this study. The synthesis is based on the formation of a N-acyl-benzimidazolinone (Nbz) 
intermediate and a solid phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) approach developed by Dawson and co-workers 
[35]. An optimized synthetic strategy was utilized based on an on-resin cyclisation/self-cleavage protocol, 
which afforded the target peptides in high yields and crude purity [34]. This chapter is merely meant as an 
overview of the process and the chemistry behind.   
Initial synthesis attempts were carried out according to a protocol used by Blackwell and co-workers, which 
uses an acid labile Rink linker resin loaded with a 3,4-diaminobenzoic acid group (Dbz) [35][36]. The first 
amino acid reacts upon activation by HBTU and DIEA with one of the two amino groups, exhibiting 
selectivity towards the 3-amino group due to the 4-amino being less reactive because of its position para to 
the amide as seen on Figure 5. 
 
Figure 5: Loading of the first amino acid on the resin with the Dbz group attached. 
To avoid cross reactions the amino acid contains the base labile fluorenylmethyloxycarbonyl (Fmoc) 
protecting group on the amine. After activation and coupling of the first amino acid, the Fmoc group is 
selectively removed by treatment with piperidine and the next activated amino acid can be coupled.  
Fmoc-SPPS is based on temporary protection of the amino functionality of the amino acids with the Fmoc 
protecting group to avoid cross-reactions in the amide bond formation step. 
 
Figure 6: Illustration of the chain elongation cycle with the last amino acid containing the Boc protecting group. 
The synthesis continues until the desired amino acid sequence is complete with the last amino acid 
containing the acid labile tert-butyloxycarbonyl (Boc) protecting group as drawn in Figure 6. After the 
peptide assembly, the Dbz-linker is activated by subsequent use of p-nitrochloroformate to generate the 
Nbz functionality, the formation of which is drawn in Figure 7 [35]. 
==PAGE:12==
Page 11 of 51 
 
 
Figure 7: Formation of the N-acyl-benzimidazolinone (Nbz) functionality. 
In the next step, the peptide was globally deprotected and cleaved from the resin by addition of 
trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) due to the acid labile properties of the Rink linker. Dissolution in phosphate buffer 
at pH 6.8 resulted in the thiolactone bridge as the cysteine thiol attacked the C-terminal Nbz functionality. 
The final product was then isolated by RP-HPLC. 
This synthesis protocol enables synthesis in low to moderate crude purities and yields depending on the 
AIP. With the 3,4-diaminophenyl linker, loading on the less activated 4-amino instead is possible, but of no 
difference since formation of the Nbz functionality, followed by cleavage by TFA can still take place at the 3-
amino group. Problems arise if both amino groups are acylated in either the first or any of the subsequent 
synthetic cycles, since that hinders the Nbz formation. These di-acylated by-products have proven to be 
difficult to remove on RP-HPLC [34]. This side reaction can be partially lowered by control of the 
concentrations in the loading phases, but it remains a challenge with amino acids like glycine due to their 
small steric bulk.  
A way of overcoming the di-acylation problem is making the 4-amino group even less reactive by steric 
hindrance. This led to the invention of a second generation Dbz-linker with a methyl group on the 4-amino 
group (MeDbz), which retains the functionality displayed by the first generation linker, while being more 
resistant toward double acylation in the coupling of amino acids [37].  
The synthesis was further optimized by performing the cyclization step with the peptide still attached to 
the resin as shown in Figure 8, which leads to cleavage of the desired cyclic peptide and leaves the by-
products from di-acetylation attached on the resin [34].  
 
Figure 8: The cyclization/cleavage process of the optimized synthesis with the Dbz group attached directly to the resin. 
==PAGE:13==
Page 12 of 51 
 
The MeDbz-linker is loaded directly on the resin without the acid labile Rink-linker, allowing deprotection of 
the peptide by TFA while remaining attached to the resin. When the cysteine thiol attacks the MeNbz 
moiety and forms the thiolactone, it cleaves the cyclic peptide from the resin while all unreactive by-
products stays on the resin, resulting in high crude purities of the reaction mixtures. The reaction mixture 
was directly purified by RP-HPLC and afforded the desired AIPs in moderate to high yields. This approach 
allows work at higher concentrations compared to solution phase cyclisation reactions and avoids the 
difficult purification of crude peptides containing di-acetylated by-products [34]. 
Analytical techniques 
Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy was used to access information of the investigated AIPs. 
A number of different experiments were acquired, each giving unique information in the AIP structure 
elucidation. Thus, each will be explained in detail in the following sections.   
Routine NMR experiments such as 1D 1H, COSY and HMBC aided in the structural assignments, while 
nuclear Overhauser effect (NOESY) spectroscopy provided information on internuclear distances and the 
new experiment Spin-State Selective (S3) HMBC resulted in very exact extractions of scalar coupling 
constant J, hereafter called J-coupling constants.  
In order to fully utilize the information from NMR spectroscopy, the theoretical behavior of compound 
structures was modelled in the program Maestro, through conformational searches resulting in low energy 
structures. The theoretical ensemble of conformations was compared to the internuclear distances 
calculated from the NOEs, and for the structures that fitted the data the best, theoretical JHH-coupling 
constants were calculated by density functional theory (DFT) calculations. This provided a complementary 
method of evaluating the conformations of interest by investigating the correlation to the results of the S3 
HMBC experiment.  
 
Figure 9: Overview of the analytical process and the interaction of the different techniques. 
The general approach is illustrated in Figure 9. The first step is the comparison between the experimental 
distances from NOE data and the conformations generated in silico by Maestro, which was achieved using a 
script written in Matlab by Casper Hoeck [38]. The script produces the ensemble of conformations that in 
average provides the best match to the intensity of the NOEs through an iterative process of a user 
determined number of steps.  
==PAGE:14==
Page 13 of 51 
 
The output of the script contained the conformations comprising the ensemble as well as the number of 
times each of the conformations were chosen for the iterative steps, which was used when comparing 
structures. The output also included the average deviation of the ensemble to the experimental input data 
along with the average deviation to each supplied NOE derived distance.    
When using the script, two different methods of identifying the best ensemble were tried. One method 
always chose the conformational ensemble with the best RMSD, providing a closer fit to the experimental 
data, but possibly resulting in many very similar conformations. Another method was based on the Akaike 
information criterion (AIC), scoring each step depending on the deviation relative to the experimental data, 
but adding a penalty for each new structure added to the pool of chosen structures [39]. It hereby becomes 
favorable to choose the same structure several times in the iterative process, resulting in a narrower 
structural ensemble. Redundant conformations are avoided at the cost of a slightly higher average 
deviation between the computational and the experimental data.  
If large disagreements were observed in the first comparison, reevaluation of the starting inputs, data or 
structural, was done. For the NOESY spectra that includes reassessment of the calculated distances and 
build-up curves, close examination of the assignment of the NOEs and uncertainty in the measurement of 
the NOE signal in the individual spectra. The structures from the conformational search also served as a 
point of reevaluation, leading to reassessment of the involved parameters in the calculations.

It was found desirable to use “realistic” experimental conditions, such as aqueous solvent at neutral pH and 
ambient temperature, for the results to describe the 3D structural ensembles of the AIPs in their native 
environment.  
Blackwell and co-workers reported 3D structures of the four native AIPs using NMR spectroscopy [40]. They 
acquired the spectra of the compounds in 70/30 H2O/AcCN mixtures with a pH of 3.65 due to a low 
solubility of the AIPs in water. Similar solubility problems were experienced in this project, when AIP-I was 
tried dissolved in water with visible precipitate present and next to no signal observed at 800 MHz. Thus, 
the organic solvent dimethyl sulfoxide, DMSO-d6, was chosen for the experiments, instead of trying e.g. a 
change in pH, and accept any possible changes in conformation following the change of solvent.

NOESY 
The nuclear Overhauser effect spectroscopy (NOESY) experiments utilizes the cross-relaxation mechanism 
by intramolecular dipole-dipole interactions, and as the interactions are through space and not bonds, the 
intensities of the cross-peaks can be linked to the internuclear distances between protons [42][43].  
In NMR, relaxation can take place by longitudinal (T1) or transverse (T2) mechanisms, of which T1 is of 
special interest in NOE experiments on small to medium molecules. The relaxation happens by energy 
exchange with the lattice, whose large degree of freedom means it offers all energy levels needed for 
exchange with any NMR transition [44]. As the exchange depends on interactions with the lattice, the 
molecular motion of tumbling is of relevance. For a small, fast tumbling molecule the NOE enhancement 
will be positive, whereas for a large, slow tumbling compounds the NOE enhancement is negative [44]. The 
tumbling rate is, in addition to the size, shape and flexibility of the molecule, dependent on temperature 
and viscosity of the solvent.  
 
Figure 11: Outline of the 2D NOESY experiment, where τm is the mixing time (reproduced from [42]). 
As seen on Figure 11, the evolution period of the 2D NOESY experiment begins with a hard 90° pulse, where 
the magnetization vectors become frequency labelled after the vectors are allowed to precess in the xy-
plane. The cross-relaxation takes place in the mixing period and the FID is recorded in the detection period

resonance, which subsequently is allowed to cross-relax during a fixed mixing time before an observation 
pulse [45][46]. As a selective technique, only the resonances in close spatial proximity to the excited 
resonance will give rise to a signal as a result of cross-relaxation.

diffuses to Hc and a cross-peak appears between Ha and Hc with a stronger intensity than the distance 
between them rightly would give [44]. Meanwhile, intensity is lost between Ha and Hb. From this, it is clear 
that the NOE intensities can no longer be trusted, when significant spin diffusion takes place.

Important experimental parameters  
A key parameter when setting up any NMR experiment is the duration of each pulse, also known as the 
pulse length or width. Normally the duration of all other pulses is defined relative to a 90° pulse, the 
calibration of which becoming all the more important. The 90° pulse is the time needed to flip the net 
magnetization e.g. from the equilibrium along the +z axis to the -y axis by rotation around the +x axis.  
The 90° pulse width depends on factors such as spectrometer, probe and the nuclei observed [50].   
The calibration can be carried out by recording signal intensity as a function of varying pulse lengths with a 
simple delay-pulse-acquire sequence. When the pulse length approaches the true 90° value, the signal 
intensity reaches a maximum, as all magnetization has been flipped to the xy plane. With increasing values 
the intensity drops and comes to a null point as the flip angle approaches 180° and the magnetization lies 
along -z [50].  
 
Figure 14: The dependence of signal intensity on the pulse width (reproduced from [50]). 
From an array as on Figure 14, the 90° pulse width can be found from the first maximum or calculated from 
the null point of 180°, 360° etc. [51].  
In the current automation routine, the 90° pulse width can be measured automatically.  
Due to the low intensity of 13C at natural abundance and long relaxation time, the 90° 13C pulse is not 
measured for each sample. Instead the pulse width is recorded with a labelled sample as a part of regular 
maintenance and the value stored.  
 
Between each scan in an experiment, an appropriate delay should be introduced. The spin-lattice relaxation 
T1 is the time constant for the return of the net magnetization along the +z axis. One can imagine that if a 
proper delay is not used and the equilibrium not reestablished, the following scan suffers from a lower 
intensity. 
The main component of the spin-lattice relaxation in liquids is the dipole-dipole relaxation mechanism, 
where the motion of the surrounding nuclei causes fluctuating magnetic fields at the observed nucleus [52].  
Theory links the dipole-dipole relaxation to the rotational correlation time τc, and leads to an inverse 
proportionality between τc and T1, meaning a bigger and slower tumbling compound will have nuclei with 
shorter T1 [53].   
==PAGE:19==
Page 18 of 51 
 
The T1 can be determined with the inversion recovery experiment, where an initial 180° pulse inverts the 
magnetization to lie along the -z axis. During the following delay τ2 the system seeks to reestablish the 
equilibrium through spin-lattice relaxation and the 90° pulse transfer the remaining magnetization to the xy 
plane for recording [51].  
 
Figure 15: Outline of the inversion recovery experiment for measurement of T1. 
For short τ2’s, the magnetization remains along -z, but as the delay is extended, the system has more time 
to relax and the magnetization will eventually start to build up along +z after crossing through zero. This is 
expressed in the signal intensity, which will start off as negative, but for a series of rising τ2’s, will drop to 
zero, only to rise as positive and stabilize as all magnetization is recovered. In the setup of the experiment 
τ1 is the delay between scans and must be set to a length of more than 5 times the highest expected T1 [51]. 
At 5 times T1 effectively all (99.33%) of the magnetization has returned to equilibrium [54].

Measuring J-coupling constants by S3 HMBC  
Coupling constants are normally measured as a line splitting between maxima within a peak as a result of 
the alpha and beta spin states, best known from the line-splittings observed in 1H 1D spectra. For more 
complex experiments, the lines representing each spin state can either be in-phase (IP) or anti-phase (AP) 
depending on the experimental setup. When both states are observed in the same spectrum, an error in 
the reading of the line-splitting is often observed, especially for small J’s, due to effects such as line 
broadening and a lack of resolution. IP cross-peaks generally leads to an underestimation of J due to 
overlap of the lines making the maxima seem closer, while AP may lead to an overestimation due to the 
opposite phases cancelling out, moving the maxima further away from the center.  
As these types of errors originates in the overlap of signals from the two spin states, they may be avoided 
by the separation of the states into different subspectra. This can be done by combining both AP and IP 
spectra into IPAP spectra, which can be overlaid for accurate readings of J.  
 
Figure 16: Principle of IPAP; separation of lines in a cross-peak from addition and subtraction from IP and AP spectra  
(reproduced from [9]). 
==PAGE:20==
Page 19 of 51 
 
This idea is the basis of a new set of experiments developed within the group, offering a more exact reading 
of J. The experiments are based on multiplicity edited HMBC spectra. HMBC correlates 1H and 13C spins 
through heteronuclear long-range coupling constants. When another 1H spin is attached to a given 13C spin, 
this passive 1H spin can be used for spin-state selective (S3) editing, effectively splitting the active 1H into 
two subspectra by the large 1JCH. The S3 HMBC homo- and heteronuclear experiments are based on two 
existing experiments, and utilizes a π phase difference in the multiplet structure with respect to JHH 
between the multiplicity-edited HMBC and the homonuclear J attenuated (HAT) HMBC experiments 
[55][56].  
The improved multiplicity-edited HMBC produces two subspectra separating correlations depending on the 
number of 1H spins attached to the 13C into even (C/CH2) and odd (CH/CH3) groupings. The subspectra 
appear by linear combinations of a standard HMBC with up-down datasets differing in a π phase with 
respect to 1JCH. This phase difference gives an inversion of the sign of the data sets, and with two 1H spins 
attached in methylene follows a double inversion, with quaternary carbons and CH3 groups following the 
same respective patterns based on the number of attached protons [57][58].  
The HAT HMBC experiment aims to show correlations between 1H and 13C spins separated by two bonds 
alike to H2BC, but relies on the long-range 2JCH instead of 3JHH [59][60].  The “hatting” procedure eliminates 
cross-peaks with vanishingly small JHH due to AP lines cancelling each other out, but has better sensitivity 
for a greater range of small JHH than H2BC. The experiment adds a π phase difference within the multiplet 
for each 1H spin attached to the 13C and by addition and subtraction of two complimentary data sets can 
likewise create two subspectra separating the C/CH2 and CH/CH3 groups [59].  
By combining the spectra from the multiplicity-edited HMBC and HAT HMBC, a series of subspectra are 
created separating the lines of the cross-peaks in the even/odd multiplicity spectra, see Figure 17.  
 
Figure 17: The principle of the S3 HMBC experiment as a combination of the multiplicity-edited  
HMBC and HAT HMBC (reproduced from [10]).

In practice, J is measured by taking a slice through the spectral row of a multiplet in both the add and 
subtract 2D subspectra in magnitude mode. By overlaying and shifting slices of the subtract onto slices of 
the add subspectra, J can be measured as the displacements between the two. The measurement includes 
the relative sign of J, something not usually measurable as other experiments have no way of tracking the α 
and β spin states. As the overlay is of two 1D spectra representing the two spin states, even very small 
values of J can be extracted.   
 
Figure 18: Cross-peak from S3 HMBC homo of strychnine (left) and 1D slice of the corresponding row (right). 
The S3 HMBC records spectra of the sub-experiments in an interleaved fashion, distributing any 
disturbances across all experiments so their respective intensities remain comparable. Similarly, the pulse 
sequences must be corrected to all having the same length and number of pulses, so all experience the 
same relaxation and sample heating. An AU program has been developed, which handles the processing of 
the datasets into the necessary subspectra [55].

which included previously unreported coupling constants [55][56].

Conformational optimization  
The structures of interest were modeled in Maestro and their conformations optimized by the application 
MacroModel, which uses force-field based methods to explore the conformational space by stochastic 
dynamics and Monte Carlo algorithms [61]. 
The structure is subjected to a random “kick” of one or several atoms giving an altered conformation, which 
is then minimized resulting in a possible new local minima conformation and serves as the new starting 
geometry for the subsequent perturbing step [62]. The generation of structures used mixed torsional/low 
mode sampling, where the perturbations were given as alterations of dihedral angles or shifting 
coordinates along low-energy paths on the conformational energy surface, hereby overcoming impossible 
large energy barriers between minima [10].  
The ensure that the entire conformational space was explored, the search used 10,000 steps and an energy 
cutoff of 50 kJ/mol. PRCG minimization optimized each step through 2500 iterations until the gradient 
converged with a threshold of 0.05. 
The relative energy depends on the chosen force field, which scores parameters such as bond lengths, 
angles and electrostatic interactions for each structure [62]. The calculations take the chosen solvent 
implicitly into account by treating the solvent as a continuous medium by adding a dielectric constant, 
which interacts with the molecule. Conformational investigations in this project used the force field 
MMFFs, which is useful for biopolymers and many organic molecules [63].

DFT-calculations 
Density functional theory, DFT, is a computational approach for calculating electron energies based on 
approximations of the electron energy via a set of functions describing the electron density of the system. 
DFT is here used to calculate theoretical J-coupling constants of different structures for subsequent 
comparison to experimental values. 
DFT-calculations use a basis set consisting of predetermined functions to describe molecular orbitals at 
each nucleus by linear combination of Gaussian type orbitals, GTOs. To the basis set, polarization or diffuse 
functions can be added to allow more asymmetry around the nuclei or better describe electron density 
further from the nucleus, respectively [62]. The basis set may be varied according to the modelled system. 
For the compounds in this project the basis set 6-31g(d,p) were used, which is a smaller set, but has been 
reported to give high accuracy with a low computational cost [64]. It is a Pople basis set, where the core 
orbitals are described by six GTOs, the inner valence orbital by three and the outer valence orbitals by one 
GTO. To this, polarization functions are added with a d-orbital to the heavy atoms and a p-orbital to the 
hydrogens, allowing the atom to have a more asymmetric electron density as a consequence of its bonding 
with other atoms [62].  
The functionals consists of a number of formulae, which use the basis set to describe and solve the electron 
density for the whole compound [62]. In this project, the DFT functionals used was the Becke Three 
Parameter Hybrid Functionals (B3LYP), which is widely used for small molecules with high accuracy in the 
results [64]. As a hybrid exchange-correlation functional, B3LYP include both exact exchange from Hartree-
Fock theory, local-spin-density and gradient correction [65].  
The DFT method is used to calculate the nuclear spin-spin coupling constant between nuclei as the second 
derivative of the electronic energy with respect to their magnetic moments [66]. Several mechanisms 
contribute to the coupling constant: the diamagnetic spin-orbit (DSO), the paramagnetic spin-orbit (PSO), 
the Fermi-contact (FC) and the spin-dipole (SD) terms [64]. The DSO and PSO components come from the 
magnetic moments created by the movement of electrons. The FC and SD contributions come from the 
interaction between the spin of the electrons and the local nuclear magnetic fields, called the spin 
hyperfine interaction. The FC term describes the interaction at the nucleus, while the SD term is the 
remaining part. For protons coupling constants the sum of the DSO, PSO and SD contributions are often 
very small compared to the FC term and it has been shown that these can be neglected for small molecules 
if the results are scaled appropriately [64].  
As the FC term dominates, higher accuracy can be gained if steps are taken to better describe the situation 
close to the nucleus. With this in mind, the function was augmented by four compact 1s functions [64].  
The DFT calculations returns the coupling constants in Hz, which then can be compared to the values 
measured from experimental NMR data.

A broad resonance at 5.25 ppm of lower intensity was attributed to the alcohol of either the serine or 
threonine or a combination of both, and as its dominant NOE correlation was to water, it was not 
investigated deeper. Neither the amine protons of the N-terminal or the acid proton of the aspartic acid 
could be ascribed to any peak due to exchange. However, the OH of the tyrosine gave a broadened singlet 
at 9.34 ppm with the same intensity as the amide protons.

AIP-S P2A 
 
Figure 23: Primary structure of AIP-S P2A with the primary sequence YAFCIGYF.  
The process of assigning AIP-S P2A followed the same steps as for AIP-I with the same use of spectra. All 
diastereotopic protons could be resolved and the spectra showed very little overlap considering the size 
and type of molecule. HMBC correlations from the methylene protons of Cys4 at 3.21 and 2.57 ppm to the 
thioester carbon of Phe8 at 200.2 ppm verified the macrocycle [68].  
The phenols of the tyrosines and the amine protons of the N-terminal were all assignable at 9.35, 9.20 and 
7.93 ppm respectively, with the amine appearing as a very broad singlet.  
 
Substituted amides can exist in either a cis or trans form with regards to the orientation of the amide 
substituent due to the partial double bond character of the amide bond. The majority of the natural amino 
acids exist almost solely in trans conformation due to the steric hindrance that would otherwise follow [69]. 
 
Figure 24: Difference in conformation of a trans (left) and cis (right) peptide bond between two amino acids  
Here green represents the side chains, and it is clear how they clash sterically in the cis form. 
As seen on Figure 24, a trans conformation results in a short distance between the amide proton and any 
protons on the side chain of the neighboring amino acid, whereas the cis conformation brings the alpha 
protons in close contact with each other and with any side chain protons [70].  
These differences give rise to different NOEs, making it possible to distinguish the two conformations by 
NMR. As no strong NOE correlations were seen between any alpha protons in either AIP-I or AIP-S P2A, cis 
conformation was ruled out for all peptide bonds in the two AIPs, as was expected.

AIP-S 
 
Figure 25: Primary structure of AIP-S. 
At first, the NMR assignment of AIP-S seemed straightforward as the 1D 1H spectra showed the expected 
signals belonging to amide, aromatic, alpha and side chain protons. The process of assignment followed the 
same steps as for AIP-I and a table of specific assignments can be found in Appendix 5. All diastereotopic 
protons could be assigned except for at the γ-position in proline, where the peaks overlapped and were 
part of a multiplet. The identification of the backbone positions was done through 2D NOSEY and DQF-
COSY correlations between the alpha and amide protons, except of course for the proline. The cyclization 
was again proven by HMBC correlations from the cysteine methylene protons at 3.21 and 2.58 ppm to a 
carbon at 200.3 ppm.  
A closer look did however reveal an extra set of signals of lower intensity “shadowing” the signals of the 
AIP, independent of sample batch.  
 
Figure 26: Selection of zooms from the 1D 1H spectrum of AIP-S. 
There was consistently roughly a factor of 3 between the main signals and their “shadows” as seen on 
Figure 26A. Figure 26B shows a zoom of two of the aromatic tyrosine signals, where the signal on the left 
shows a clear distortion from the expected doublet, while the doublet to the right shows no distortion but 
a higher intensity than expected from two tyrosine protons. Likewise, in Figure 26C the doublet of doublets 
to the left overlaps with what seems like another doublet of doublets while the apparent triplet at 2.58 
ppm has a smaller echo at 2.62 ppm.  
==PAGE:30==
Page 29 of 51 
 
This led to the question of whether the extra set of signals came from an impurity or from the compound 
having two stable conformations in solution. As neither MS nor UV data showed any impurities, the next 
step was looking for proof of the signals belonging to two conformations that would enable the distinction 
between them [34].  
Two stable conformations would be at an equilibrium with a certain rotational energy barrier.  
Here the proline was of special interest since it has been shown to have a slightly higher tolerance for a cis 
peptide bond than the others due to the steric constraints of the proline ring [69].  
 
Figure 27: Difference in conformation of a trans (left) and cis (right) peptide bond for a proline.  
Here green represents another amino acid side chain.  
For the cis proline, close contact between the alpha protons of proline and the neighboring tyrosine is still 
expected, while for the trans the δCH2 of the proline is brought near the tyrosine alpha proton as seen on 
Figure 27 [70].  
No strong correlations were seen in 2D NOESY between any of the dominant alpha protons so as for AIP-I, 
cis conformation was ruled out for all peptide bonds in AIP-S as the major component. When investigating 
the proline closer, strong correlations were seen between the δCH2 and the alpha proton of the neighboring 
tyrosine – a connection hard to imagine if the proline is not in a trans conformation.  
Is was impossible to make the same investigations of the “shadow” in the sample as the signals most often 
overlapped with the major set and were of lower intensity, so the possibility of a cis conformation could 
not be determined.  
A study of melittin, a peptide of 26 amino acids with a proline in the middle, showed that upon heating, 
signals of the cis and trans form shifted in their mutual intensity [71]. Heating influences the equilibrium 
between conformations and it is expected to see a merging of signals if the flip between conformations 
happens at a faster rate than the NMR timescale or the before mentioned shift in intensity as the 
equilibrium is dependent on temperature.  
 
 
==PAGE:31==
Page 30 of 51 
 
A similar study was undertaken, investigating the influence of rising temperatures on the chemical shift, the 
mutual intensities and multiplicity structure of the major and minor set of peaks.   
 
Figure 28: Overlay of 1D 1H spectra at rising temperatures, 298 K (blue), 308 K (red), 318 K (green), 328 K (violet), 338 K (yellow). 
Figure 28 shows the effect of heating the sample, with the triplet at 8.95 ppm and its neighboring “shadow” 
showing no signs of change in their mutual intensities. This is an unexpected result and indicates that the 
two sets of signals are not originating from a conformational equilibrium between two conformations. The 
peak at 8.6 ppm seems at first like it merges into a clean doublet, but at 338 K the signal is distorted again 
and leaves the impression of two peaks slowly crossing over each other as the temperature rises. 
Additional overlays of 1D 1H spectra can be found in Appendix 4.  
Another way of investigating the possibility of two conformations was through selective 1D NOESY 
experiments. Resonances of the two components were irradiated separately and the effect recorded.  
 
Figure 29: 1D 1H spectrum (blue) overlaid with 1D NOESY with selective excitation of the resonance at 8.95 (red) and 8.89 (green). 
In the overlay on Figure 29 it can be seen how selective irradiation of the peaks at either 8.95 or 8.89 ppm 
give rise to different signals further upfield in the spectra. More distinctly, there is no cross-relaxation 
between the two peaks and they appear totally independent of each other. It was expected that if they 
==PAGE:32==
Page 31 of 51 
 
originate from two conformations, cross-signals would appear between equivalent protons as a result of 
exchange between conformations [44]. 
A similar pattern was observed for all other selective excitations of resonances; the signals clearly favored 
keeping to their own conformer with no cross-excitations between them. The only exception was when an 
overlap of peaks from the two sets were irradiated, resulting in peaks from both sets being seen. Examples 
can be found in Appendix 4. 
Based on all the aforementioned experiments, it was concluded that the sample must contain two different 
compounds. These were probably epimers, since only one mass was seen with MS. Where in AIP-S the 
epimerization has happened was not apparent since the effect was seen throughout the AIP and not 
centered in one part of the peptide. Due to the inability to separate the compounds at the present, further 
work toward finding three-dimensional structure of AIP-S was halted. At current, the Olsen group is 
working on identifying the epimer [34].

As can be seen in Table 4, the tyrosine and phenylalanine aromatic protons have significantly longer T1’s 
than the other protons. The intensity of these signals will therefore suffer the most from inadequate delays 
between scans, to the incomplete recovery of magnetization along +z for the subsequent scan. This gives 
rise to an error when calculating internuclear distances from NOE intensities. 
For the values of T1 seen in Table 4, an adequate delay between scans would be 12-18 s, resulting in very 
time costly experiments. An overall more efficient approach can be to use a 30° instead of full 90° pulse. 
Hereby 70 % of the signal intensity is still obtained, but due to the lesser deviation from equilibrium, a 
shorter delay is needed before the magnetization is fully recovered along +z and data can overall be 
accumulated faster. 
Due to the similarities of the general structure of the AIPs, T1 was only measured for AIP-S P2A and it was 
assumed that the values of AIP-I followed the same pattern as seen in Table 4.

New conformational optimizations could be initiated hereafter with different combinations of constraints 
applied to the examined distances until a satisfactory agreement with the experimental data was found. 
Here caution should be kept in mind as narrow constraints could lead to ensembles of structures complying 
with the experimental data, but not resembling the native conformation(s) of AIP-I due to the constraints

protons and highly varied ensemble structures fitting the experimental data speak of a dynamic molecule, 
the motion of which could be quenched by ill-considered constraints.  
 
The group of Blackwell and co-workers has earlier reported 3D structures of the four native AIPs and 
several analogues based on distance restraints extracted from ROESY spectra [40]. As earlier mentioned, 
the group recorded spectra of the compounds dissolved in 70/30 H2O/AcCN mixtures at a pH of 3.65. Their 
structures were tried against the distances calculated from the NOEs recorded here in DMSO with an 
average deviation of 13.4 % compared to 4.1 % for the ensemble modelled in DMSO. This suggest that AIP-I

Conclusion 
 
The project included the implementation and evaluation of the newly developed NMR experiment S3 HMBC 
for measurement of homonuclear and heteronuclear coupling constants at high field. The results for 
strychnine showed a slight rise of RMSD from what was observed at 400 MHz, when compared to 
theoretical values from DFT calculations. At the same time, a lower sensitivity was noted by the 
disappearance of signals at higher field. The S3 HMBC was hereafter used in the investigations of the 3D 
structure of autoinducing peptides (AIPs) from the quorum sensing system in the bacteria S. aureus.  
For AIP-I, the major hurdle remains the low solubility in water, preventing studies of the AIP in conditions 
closer to its native environment. In this project, the AIP was investigated in DMSO and compared to earlier 
studies in H2O/AcCN, the results suggest that AIP-I behaves differently depending on solvent.  
From 2D NOESY experiments, internuclear distances were derived and compared to modelled structures in 
an iterative process, producing the ensemble of structures that in average best matched the experimental 
data. The analysis of AIP-I was made difficult by spectral overlap, preventing the extraction of key NOE 
correlations. A high degree of flexibility was seen regarding the orientation of the exocyclic tripeptide with 
no correlations to the macrocycle part and it was therefore excluded from investigations.  
The ensembles presented were evaluated in an iterative process by correlation to the experimentally 
derived distances between the endocyclic amide protons. Repulsive constraints were introduced as the first 
ensembles produced distances far shorter than the experimental data predicted. The investigation 
produced two families of structural ensembles with the major difference located in the orientation around 
the thioester. Both ensembles had certain amide protons oriented toward the inside of the macrocycle in 
agreement with an earlier study suggesting that exactly these protons were solvent shielded.  
Another cyclic AIP studied as part of the project was AIP-S P2A. The NMR spectra of AIP-S P2A showed well 
resolved signals with minimum overlap and the approach to the investigation of the 3D structure followed 
the general steps as for AIP-I. The resulting ensemble for AIP-S P2A indicated a highly ordered structure 
regarding the folding of the macrocycle backbone, which was especially noteworthy as the macrocycle 
sequence contained a glycine, whose lack of sidechain allowed a higher degree of freedom.   
The investigation of the structure of AIP-S were prevented due to the 1D 1HNMR spectrum revealing the 
presence of two sets of signals. Analysis of selective 1D NOESY and temperature experiments led to the 
conclusion that the sample contained two separate compounds. The identification of the unknown 
compound is an interesting subject for future studies.

Future Work  
 
 
• 
The investigations of the 3D structure of AIP-I should be expanded to new combinations of 
constraints and uncertainties to further examine the structure dynamics and explore whether 
ensembles can be obtained that correlates better to the experimental data.   
 
• 
The ensemble of AIP-S P2A suggested a highly ordered macrocycle backbone and the investigation 
could be expanded to include reflections of the dynamics of the exocyclic tail and the relative 
orientations of the side chains.  
 
• 
The unknown compound in the sample of AIP-S is likely the product of an epimerization occurring 
at some point during the synthesis. A systematic study where each amino acid is exchanged with 
their enantiomer in turn, could lead to the identification of the point of the epimerization as well as 
being of interest in a biological assay.
