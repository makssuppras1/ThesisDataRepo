Summary  
 
Waste food are valuable and renewable resources promising various end-products. Food 
processing wastes have environmental implications regarding its disposal and therefore its 
treatment, minimization and prevention needs to be assessed.  As demand for fossil fuels increases, 
driven by factors including over-population, there is a need to find alternatives to fight the threat 
of global warming and the scarcity of fossil resources. Biorefineries can play a vital role in this 
challenge by generating fuels and chemicals from available and renewable biomass.  
In recent years, the interest in organic acids derived from sugars has increased as part of efforts to 
develop biorefineries that produce not only biofuels, but also chemicals to replace those currently 
derived from petroleum. Therefore, the present project aims to conceptually design a process 
representing an integrated biorefinery converting Food Waste, more precisely citrus peel waste, to 
Galactaric acid (GA).  Although GA can be a strategic platform for chemicals for future green 
chemistry, it is still considered a fine chemical with its market limited to pharmaceutical and 
cosmetic industry able to account with costly manufacturing processes which are also not 
environmental-friendly.  
By means of literature research, it was possible to screen out unsustainable technologies and 
eventually select the best route taking into account reaction parameters such as conversion or yield, 
innovative methods and technologies and environmental-friendly requirements.  The selected 
route aims to produce 2000 tons/year of GA and comprises 3 steps: extraction of pectin using hot 
water and citric acid, hydrolysis of pectin in a continuous membrane bioreactor using 
Polygalacturonase enzyme from Aspergillus niger, and oxidation of D-Galacturonic acid to 
Galactaric acid catalyzed by resting cells of Escherichia coli. 
Then, as part of a technoeconomic analysis, the development of the process flowsheet, scaling-up, 
calculation of mass and energy balances, economical assessment and sensibility analysis were 
performed.  Then, by upgrading the value of solid waste residues to produce cattle feed as 
secondary product, waste from the first step of the process was reduced. Moreover, results from 
the economic analysis showed that profit increased from 8.69 to 22.51$/kg of GA and Environment 
E-factor was decreased by 70%. 
Finally, a sensibility analysis was performed to understand the bottlenecks of the process taking 
into account change in the yield of extraction, hydrolysis productivity and oxidation conversion, 
as well as, price of GA and raw materials, related to the plant location. Moreover, costs were 
assessed and it was concluded that the first step of the process was responsible for the largest 
percentage of total raw material costs. Moreover, the second step of the process is the most energy-
intensive due to heating-up of large amounts of diluted substrate solutions.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1. Food waste concern 
 
Food waste (FW) is a global concern and environmental problem. According to the European 
Project Fusions, FW is defined as “any food, and inedible parts of food removed from the food 
supply chain to be recovered or disposed”. The Food and Agriculture Organization of the United 
Nations (FAO) provides an alerting value of 1.6 billion tons of “primary product equivalents”, this 
is, e.g. milk, wheat, fruit, vegetables, meat being disposed. In parallel, about 21,000 people die 
every day of hunger or hunger-related causes. Moreover, FAO estimates that FW’s carbon 
footprint is 3.3 billion tons of CO2 equivalent of greenhouse gas (GHG) being released into the 
atmosphere per year1-3.  
Indeed, global food demand is driven by population and economic growth, and urbanization along 
with the materialist and consumerist mindset of nowadays society. Moreover, lack of effective 
waste management strategies leads to accumulation of FW. This surplus/waste happens at every 
step of the food chain: agricultural practices, post-harvest (handling and storage), and food 
manufacturing/processing, retail/wholesale and final consumption.  
Fortunately, awareness of food losses has increased globally at the national and international level 
where non-profit organizations try to fight FW. Also, environmental and governmental policies 
are changing the conventional waste destination and giving it different opportunities. Indeed, 
primary products waste carry nutritional valuable components. Thus, incineration and landfilling, 
as conventional options of “disposal” do not take advantage of “waste capacity”. Moreover, both 
options have environmental consequences mainly regarding emission of GHG. Biorefineries 
provide a different itinerary for the mentioned waste.

Figure 1 shows that the value of both plant and animal derived food waste can be upgraded. For 
instance, pectin can be extracted from plant waste, namely from fruit peel. Pectin is mainly 
composed by galacturonic acid (D-GALA). In fact, the interest in organic acids derived from sugars 
has increased in recent years as part of efforts to develop biorefineries that produce not only 
biofuels, but also chemicals to replace those currently derived from petroleum 5. 
 
1.2. Integrated biorefinery  
 
A biorefinery is a “facility that integrates biomass conversion processes and equipment to produce 
fuels, power, heat, and value-added chemicals from biomass”, as defined by the National 
Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL)6. The current categories/generations of biorefineries 
depend on the type of biomass chemistry: sugar and starch, lignocellulose and triglyceride 
biorefineries. Each category presents environmental and social concerns, for instance, the risk of 
creating a competition between food consumption and biorefinery processes for the same biomass. 
Nevertheless, advantages such as renewable, abundant and low cost feedstock can be stressed. 
Typically, biorefinery feedstocks are residues from other industries which are sought at the lowest 
possible price. However, logistics regarding handling, transportation, and inventory management 
can increase overall costs6.  
An integrated biorefinery may involve one or more types of integration such as: process 
integration (based on a holistic approach to design and operation), infrastructure integration 
(examples include the integration of a biorefinery with a petroleum refinery), feedstock and 
product integration (examples include the addition of bioethanol to gasoline, exploiting the

complementary characteristics of raw materials or products for a biorefinery with other materials), 
supply-chain integration (coordinates the resources and sequence of activities associated with the 
life cycle of a product, from feedstock operations through to delivery of the product portfolio), and 
policy and environmental integration (integrates the environmental performance of a biorefinery 
with the surroundings or with other processing facility in the same region and meets environmental 
regulations)6. Integration aims to optimize the life cycle impact of the process as well as create 
competitive advantage. 
When planning to start a biorefinery platform, it is also important to consider if it will be 
brownfield, greenfield or retrofit. A project is described as brownfield if it uses infrastructures, 
equipment, or land abandoned by another manufacturer. A greenfield is a project developed and 
implemented from scratch, and a retrofit project is an integration of new technology within an 
existing operating structure6.  
 
1.3. Process synthesis, design and technoeconomic analysis 
 
Chemical and biochemical route synthesis comprises the development of synthesis steps and 
selection of the best option. To recognize the best route, it is essential to screen out unsustainable 
technologies early in the development process. Then, as a part of a technoeconomic analysis, the 
development of a process flow diagram, calculation of mass and energy balances, and project 
economic and sensibility analysis are performed.   
In the early stages of process design, capacity-factored methods are mainly used. Capital costs and 
operating costs are estimated based on the actual costs of existing installations and on various 
factors and indices, such as scale-up factors.  
In petroleum-based as well as all biomass-based industries the key factors influencing the 
technoeconomic are feedstock, energy, capital cost and product portfolio. Indeed, choosing 
between bioprocess or chemical process, selection among alternative technologies, energy and 
water consumption, and waste production determine the technical and economic feasibility of the 
process6. 
 
1.4. Galactaric acid 
 
In this project, the production of bulk Galactaric acid (GA) from food waste is investigated.  GA, 
also known as mucic acid or meso-galactaric acid, is a symmetrical six carbon diacid – Figure 2. 
It is an aldaric acid with molecular formula C6H10O8 with a white to off-white crystalline powder 
appearance.

Besides skin care products with anti-aging properties, GA has the potential application in polymer 
synthesis as a platform chemical. For example, it can be reduced to adipic acid which is a precursor 
for Nylon or converted to 2,5- Furandicarboxylic acid, one of the twelve building block chemicals 
reported by the NREL8. Furthermore, it can be incorporated in the formulation of biodegradable 
detergents, for supporting separation processes of rare earths, as a surfactant or a chelating agent 
and to reduce the hardness of water 9-11. Thus, GA can be considered a promising renewable which 
allows the replacement of fossil-based chemicals.  
Although GA can be a strategic platform for chemicals for future green chemistry, it still 
considered a fine chemical with its market limited to pharmaceutical and cosmetic industry able 
to account with costly manufacturing processes reflected in the high market price of GA 
(35$/kg)12.  
GA was first isolated from Arabic gum in 1780. GA is a derivative from the oxidation of the 
external carbons of galactose, which gives first galacturonic acid as mono oxidized-product and 
finally galactaric acid. The main sources of GA are natural sugary compounds containing 
galactose. Currently, GA is commercially produced by oxidation of galactose with nitric acid or 
from D-GalA by electrolytic oxidation.  
The first recorded patent for GA production, based on nitric acid oxidation, dates back to 1929, 
and discloses a large scale process to obtain GA (90% purity) from larch aqueous extract rich in 
lactose. Nitric acid is added in proportion of 3:1 in a jacketed oxidation vessel with temperatures 
between 90-100ºC. Some drawbacks of the process are the production of nitrous gases which are 
toxic and have high impact for the environment, the problematic recovery of unreacted nitric acid, 
which leads to contamination of the products, highly exothermic reaction of oxidation. As this 
process is not sustainable, an optimized method was patented in 2008. Oxygen is provided for the 
oxidation and nitric acid simply acts as catalyst, introducing a better temperature control system 
and milder reaction conditions (20-45 ºC, for ca 8 hours), together with an efficient system of GHG 
abatement13. 
GA can also be produced through electrochemical ways: EDF-France applies an indirect 
electrochemical process employing a patented electrolysis cell as modified Grignard reactor (in 
presence of Br-/Br2 with 0.5M HBr) 14.

1.5. Pectin-rich biomass as feedstock for Galactaric acid production 
 
In order to exploit GA’s full potential, it is necessary to use inexpensive and available sources. An 
attractive source for GA production is D-Galacturonic acid. D-GalA is the most abundant monomer 
in pectin which is a component of plant primary cell wall and especially abundant in non-woody 
plant biomass such as in fruit peels. Figure 3 shows several pectin rich food waste streams such as 
residues from citrus fruit and sugar beet.

Citrus Processing Waste (CPW) biomass is available and currently poorly utilized. In the industry 
for production of orange juice, for instance, around 44–60% of the mass of processed orange fruit

1.5.1. Pectin 
Pectin is a co-polymer comprising 1,4-α-linked galacturonic acid and 1,2-linked rhamnose with 
side branches of either 1,4-linked β- D-Galactose or 1,5-α-linked L-arabinose and other sugars – 
Figure 4.

Some of the C-6 (carboxyl groups) of the D-GalA backbone are esterified with methoxyl groups, 
while others are present as uronic acid salt giving pectin dissolved in water excellent metal 
chelating properties 20. The esterification of galacturonic acid residues with methanol or acetic acid 
is an important structural characteristic of pectic substances as gelling agents. Overall the degree 
of substitution is known as the degree of esterification (DE). If more than 50% of the carboxyl 
groups are esterified with methanol the pectins are called high-methoxy pectins (HM), and if less 
than 50 % they are called low-methoxy (LM) pectins. This same principal applies to acetylation 
using the degree of acetylation (DAc).  
Pectin is mainly used as a hydrocolloid (a substance capable to trap water and to form gels at low 
concentration) by the food industry. Although its traditional usage is as gelling, thickening and 
stabilizing agent, it can also replace fat thus acting as a health-promoting ingredient 17. 
The pectin price ranges from $11/kg (HM) to $12/kg (LM). Six main producers responsible for 
most of the output are Herbstreith & Fox, Naturex/Obipektin, Danisco/Dupont, Cargill, CP Kelco, 
Yantal Andre Pectin  (members of IPPA: International Pectin Producers Association) 17. Cp Kelco 
is the world’s leading pectin producer with its brand GENU® Pectins. Products are made from 
citrus, including orange, lemon and lime peel. Cp Kelco imports dry citrus peel from south and 
Central America, and southern Europe (Italy) 20.

1.6.1. Pathway in which Pectin is an intermediate  
One possible pathway includes the pretreatment of the peels as first step. The pretreatment of the 
peels comprises washing in order to eliminate soluble solids and impurities which interfere in the 
process of purification. Then, the washed peels are dried in order to remove humidity and 
inactivate enzymes.  
The pectic enzymes include despolymerases of the main chain and esterases active in the metil and 
acetilesters of the galacturonic acid in the structures of homogalacturonan and 
rhamnogalacturonan. After milling and drying, the material size is reduced and stabilized. Some 
industries focus on this step and sell the dried material as cattle feeding for $0.04921. However, 
dried peels can be used for extraction of pectin and later hydrolysed to obtain D-GalA. The 
monomer is then oxidized to GA. 
1.6.2. Hydrolysis of waste 
Second pathway is the hydrolysis of wet citrus peel to produce D-GalA without including 
extraction of pectin. Recent researches, such as (Gómez et al., 2016) 22, propose a scheme that 
involves water-extraction of soluble sugars from lemon peels, enzymatic hydrolysis of the 
remaining solids (with commercial pectinases and cellulases) and later recovery of pectin-derived 
oligosaccharides including D-GalA. The focus of the study was to identify promising prebiotic 
candidates. Alternatively, (Asghar et al., 2012) 23, proposes enzymatic hydrolysis of orange peels 
wastes and makes a comparison between batch, fed-batch and continuous well-mixed reactors. 
Additionally, Zhang et al. describes the hydrolysis of sugar beet residues to release D-GalA and 
the successful conversion to GA. Engineered whole-cell E.coli was used as biocatalyst for the 
oxidation reaction. However, the focus of the experiment was further conversion of GA to adipic 
acid. 
1.6.3. Direct oxidation 
The third possible route is the direct oxidation of peels. Processing waste from orange juice 
industry was used as a substrate in submerged cultivations of engineered Aspergillus niger. As a 
result, GA was produced with 84% yield (dry waste). The process took 120h 24.

1.8. Motivations  
 
Due to climate change concerns, the search for renewable sources which can substitute fossil-
based feedstocks, and the design of processes with a neutral carbon foot-print is of imperial 
importance.  Organic acids derived from sugars play a major role as platform chemicals. One 
crucial use of these molecules is in the production of synthetic polymers. However, sugar acids 
such as D-glucaric acid and GA are being produced at low scales. Therefore, development of novel 
routes for producing GA will lead to a higher volume production and will help support the next 
generation of biorefineries.  
 
1.9. Scope of the project

2.1.1. Alternatives 
 
Some drawbacks of acid extraction are the big amounts of acid disposal and high temperatures. 
Some researchers support the use of microwave both for pretreatment of the raw material and 
extraction process 31. Subcritical water has also been studied for extraction of pectin from apple 
pomace and citrus peel. Although extraction happens for 5 minutes, highly dilute solution of wet 
peels is needed as well as use of HCl and high temperatures (>100 ºC) for extraction and drying 
(24h) 32.  
The advantage of an enzymatic extraction is higher pH (3-5) compared to acid extraction, and 
temperatures around 50 ºC, which is more beneficial in terms of economy and environmental 
impact. However, this alternative generates low yields of around 10.8% in case of citrus pectin 
and low galacturonan content. Cargill owns a patent in which the use of enzyme hydrolysis 
substitutes the use of acid 33,34. 
Considering the traditional extraction procedure, (Bogdanoff, Nicolás Mauricio, 2015) 28 obtained 
the diffusion coefficient for the extraction of pectin from orange peel with citric acid (instead of 
inorganic acids), which allowed the optimization of some variables of the process, such as: ratio 
of mass of solvent (acid and alcohol) to mass of fruit peel, time and  yield  of extraction. The yield 
obtained was 8.4% (dry weight of peel). 
 
2.2. Step 2- Hydrolysis of pectin  
 
The hydrolysis or depolymerization of pectin can happen chemically or biologically. Chemical 
hydrolysis of pectin requires prolonged treatment with mineral acids and high temperatures in 
order to achieve complete release of the monomer. Moreover, once released, D-GalA is subject to 
degradation, forming lactones in irreproducible amounts. Reported D-GalA recoveries within 48h 
using 0.2,1 and 2 M H2SO4 at 80 °C is, respectively, 2%, 10% and 12%. Alternatively, enzymes 
such as Polygalacturonase from fungi Aspergillus niger can be used to perform this reaction. 
However, product inhibition occurs because D-GalA is a competitive inhibitor of the enzyme 
(Baciu & Jördening, 2004; Journal & Chemistry, 2008)35,36,37.

3.1. Step 1- Chemical extraction with hot water and citric acid 
 
Comparing the alternatives presented in chapter 2.1.1., the standard route is better structured and 
already used in an industrial scale.  Besides the higher amount of available information, studying 
the traditional procedure is a chance to understand to which extent the conventional manufacturing 
process can be modified since pectin is not being produced for human consumption. Therefore, 
the first step of the process herein suggested follows the traditional route. However, the fact that 
the target of extracting pectin is not for pharmaceutical or food use, some operations can be 
disregarded such as ion-exchange. Furthermore, it is considered that the efficient pretreatment of 
the peels lead to a dry product with low degree of methylation and esterification. This prevents the 
need for de-esterification of the peels which is one of the steps of the traditional production of LM 
citrus pectin.  
Additionally, the results from the optimization of the conventional extraction of pectin from dry 
orange peel with citric acid are used  (see section 2.1.1). This is because an optimization prevents 
large amounts of acid waste and economizes a process. Table I, shows the similarity between the

Citric acid and water are used as solvents and the operation runs for 60 min at the solution’s boiling

large-scale extractions and the operational time is lower (1 hour).  Furthermore, using of citric acid 
(weak organic acid) instead of mineral acids is favorable in an environmental perspective. The

3.2. Step 2- Enzymatic Hydrolysis in a membrane bioreactor 
 
Among the alternatives found in the literature – Table II – the continuous hydrolysis of pectin 
happening in a membrane bioreactor is chosen for the suggested route. A membrane bioreactor 
defines a combination of a biological process and a membrane separation. In this case, 
Polygalacturonase enzyme from Aspergillus niger is used with citrate buffer pH 4.1 at 50 ºC. The 
choice is due to the use of mild operational conditions comparing with other alternatives as well 
as the fact that membrane (regenerated cellulose) is recognized as an environmental friendly 
separation. Moreover, continuous hydrolysis in a membrane bioreactor increases productivity 
compared to batch system. This is because low Mw molecules such as D-GalA, which is an 
inhibitor for the enzyme, are continuously permeated by the membrane. Additionally, enzymes 
which are retained in the membrane can be easily recycled. Experimental results for hydrolysis 
show that specific productivity is 9.7 g product/h g enzyme. Figure 11 shows the experimental set-
up of the membrane bioreactor for continuous pectin hydrolysis.

6. Wastewater treatment 
 
Waste management is a crucial step in any industrial process. Besides preventing environmental 
contamination and respecting waste treatment policies, the costs of purchusing materials are 
reduced due to stream recycling. Figure 19 shows a scheme for the waste streams management.  
 
Figure 19Waste management scheme. 
During manufacturing of pectin, s4 is a waste stream mainly containing solids remaining from the 
extraction of pectin with water and citric acid. To tread this waste, the separation of moisture can 
be done by filtration and later sent to wastewater treatment facilities. The solid residues are then 
disposed for landfills or for incineration, which adds costs for disposal of material. Alternatively, 
it can be dried and milled, resulting in an appropriate cattle feed or, instead, fermented to obtain 
bioethanol. Other alternatives will be assessed further in the report. 
Stream s8 is obtained from drying pectin gel. The separation of gas and liquid can happen in a 
flash drum releasing a liquid stream which is sent to the wastewater treatment facilities along with 
the waster purge. 
Regarding the hydrolysis, both streams s22 and s24 contain mainly water and soluble material. 
The reactants, pectin in s22 and D-GalA in 24, need to be recycled back to the corresponding 
equipment, membrane bioreactor and electrodialysis bipolar membrane. 
Stream s30 released from the recovery of GA in the oxidation step, contains a large amount of HCl 
which is a toxic material. The acid needs to be separated and recycled back to the crystallization 
tank.  
The present process comprises the use of large amounts of water. Therefore, the treated water from 
the wastewater treatment, should be recycled and reused in the process.

10. Results and Discussion 
 
10.1. Sensibility analysis  
 
10.1.1. 
Inclusion of cattle feed as secondary product 
Table XIV shows that the profit was increased by a factor of 159% when including cattle feed as 
secondary product. This means that the cost of disposing stream 4 is higher than that of the energy 
needed to treat this stream. However, although 1 more equipment (evaporator) is taken into 
account in the calculation of CAPEX, it should be noted that capital costs calculations only include 
the production of the main product. This brings uncertainties in the results because cattle feed 
account with almost 20% of the revenues.  
 
10.1.2. 
Break down of operational costs – raw materials 
Figure 21 shows that the step comprising extraction of pectin is the one accounting with largest 
percentage of the total OPEX of the process, and that the reason is the high cost of purchasing raw 
materials. Moreover, the step comprising hydrolysis of pectin is the one with higher cost of 
utilities. The reason is that large amounts of liquid are processed per hour in reactor R1. Indeed, 
the need for a highly diluted solution (2 g/L pectin) lead to large amounts of energy needed to heat 
up the feed-stream from 25°C to 50°C. Nevertheless, if it is assumed that pectin gel in stream s19 
is at 40 ºC after drying, profit is increased by 5%, from 22.51 $/kg GA to 23.56 $/kg GA. This 
means profit is highly sensitive to the energy demand for hydrolysis reaction, related to the 
temperature of the feed to R1. 
Additionally, Figure 21 shows that major percentage of the OPEX is the cost of raw materials, 
particularly in step1. Indeed, even though ethanol is being recycled, its costs are higher than that 
of dry citrus peel.  Furthermore, Figure 23 shows that citric acid costs are the highest among that 
of the other raw materials for the hydrolysis of pectin step. Indeed, this is because PG enzymes, 
with the highest price among the other materials, are assumed to be recycled and stable for 12 
months.   
Regarding the last step of the process, Figure 24 shows that NaOH costs account with 80% of the 
total raw material costs. The justification given above regarding PG enzyme, is as well applicable 
in this case.  
 
10.1.3. 
Break down of operational costs – utilities 
Figure 25 shows that step2 of the process has the higher percentage of total utility costs. As 
explained before, this is due to high energetic demands.

Influence of yield, productivity, recovery and conversion 
As shown in Figure 27, profit is directly proportional to yield of extraction. Moreover, it is also

raw material is needed to achieve the same annual production of GA.  
Regarding hydrolysis productivity and conversion of D-GalA, Figure 28 shows that if productivity 
of hydrolysis and recovery of D-GalA in electrolysis decrease by 20%, profit decreases by 
approximately 3%. Regarding conversion of D-GalA in the last sept of the process, for a change 
of -40%, corresponding to a new conversion value of 57.2%, profit decreases by a factor of 9%, 
approximately.  
 
10.1.5. 
Influence of price of raw material and main product and production of 
GA as a commodity chemical  
Figure 29 shows that the price of the main product has a greater influence on the profit comparing 
to that of the dry citrus peel. This is important when considering the possibility of a bulk production 
of GA. As explained previously, current production of GA is limited to industries able to account 
for the high costs of manufacture which is reflected on the market price of GA. However, to gain 
competitive advantage, the production of high volumes of the acid could allow a decrease of GA 
price (economy of scale) since fixed costs would because more “diluted”. Figure XV in chapter 
9.6. shows that production of GA as commodity chemical (300% increase of production) increases 
the profit by 303%. However, its price was not lowered. As will be discussed further, uncertainties 
in the calculations should be taken into account.  
 
10.1.6. 
Switching the location of the plant 
Indeed, location is a crucial factor to take into account when planning to start a biorefinery. The 
plant should be located next to the place where biomass is obtained/harvest to reduce the costs of 
transporting high volumes of material (with low density). Otherwise, if biomass is imported, the 
plant should be located in a strategic place where logistics regarding transportation, existence of 
competitors and costumers are considered. Figure 30 shows that raising the price of raw materials 
and utilities by 15% leads to a drop in profit of 5%. 
As a matter of fact, in most cases of bioreactions, highly diluted mixtures are required which 
implies the use of large quantities of water. Therefore, in a sustainable and ethical point of views, 
the plant should be located in a country where the consumption of water does not interfere with its 
accessibility by the population.

10.1.7  
Integrated biorefinery 
A key cost factor in biorefineries is integration possibilities. As mentioned previously in chapter 
1.2. there are different types of integration. Process integration including, for instance, heat 
integration and recycling of water would indeed optimize the process.  
Supply-chain integration regards biomass growth, harvesting, pre-processing, transportation, 
processing, usage, recycle, storage. Considering the present project, one possibility (disregarded 
during the selection of the route from citrus peel to GA) could be to locate the plant next to the 
juice processing industry in order to obtain wet citrus peel. In this case, the pretreatment of peels 
would need to be considered as well as the fact that methanol would be a by-product. 
Furthermore, additional synthesis of secondary products could be integrated in the present process 
which feedstock is dried citrus peel. For example, pectin can be extracted with simultaneous 
production of d-limonene, phenols and organic pigments such as carotenoids62-64. Moreover, waste 
stream s4 could be fermented to obtain ethanol65 which could be used in precipitation of pectin, 
reducing the high costs of raw materials in the first step of the process. Alternatively, this waste 
stream could be used to produce biogas. By converting this fuel to heat or power, the utility costs 
of the process could be lowered. Nevertheless, design of an anaerobic digestior and heat generation 
facility would affect the capital costs. 
Additionally, sugars in stream s4 can also be fermented to obtain citric acid which could be used 
for extraction of pectin, and in this way reduce costs of raw materials66,67.  
Ultimately, a retrofit project could integrate existing operating facilities for producing pectin with 
the technologies included in step2 and 3 of the present project.  
Although the alternatives listed above can be of interest, a technoeconomic analysis and 
environmental assessment are needed to understand to which extend the new design would 
increase the sustainability of the project which depends crucially on the boundaries of the reference 
(analyzed) system. 
 
10.1.8. Changes to the conventional extraction of pectin 
As shown throughout the report, some changes to the traditional process of pectin extraction were 
made: an organic acid is used; use of an optimized ratio mass of peels over mass of solvent allowed 
to disregard evaporation; de-esterification was disregarded because the products are not being 
produced for human consumption and also because it was assumed that LM dry peels is the 
feedstock; if an inorganic acid was used, stream s4 would need to be purified and dried before sold 
as cattle feed.

10.1.9.  
Uncertainties 
Uncertainties in the results are driven by the scaling-up procedure based on laboratory data which

concerning precipitation of GA it was assumption that the final product is 100% pure. 
Additionally, capital and operational utility costs have been estimated by use of existing scaling-
up factors and indices, increasing inaccuracy of the results. Nevertheless, in the early stages of 
process design it is impossible to avoid this kind of uncertainties.

11. Conclusions 
 
In the present work a biorefinery pathway was investigated to obtain GA from citrus peel 
processing waste, a second-generation biomass. Dry citrus peel is the feedstock, which is currently 
sold as inexpensive cattle feed purchased from an industry responsible for drying the waste peels. 
This way, high costs of transportation and pretreatment of biomass which is known to be a 
bottleneck of biorefineries are avoided. Moreover, buying this material creates a synergy between 
this two industries and permits locating the plant in a strategic place considering competitors and 
possible costumers.  
The route developed comprises 3 steps. The first step is chemical and the extraction of pectin with 
citric acid takes place. Although the high temperatures needed to hydrolyze the protopectin, the 
pH is higher than that in the conventional route, time of extraction is 1 hour instead of 3-4 hour 
and an organic acid is used. If a retrofit integration is applied to the process, existing facilities for 
extraction of pectin could be used to produce GA by the suggested route, by implementation of 
the additional technologies.  
The second step uses a continuous membrane bioreactor for the hydrolysis of pectin with use of 
PG enzymes (50 ºC, pH 4.1). By constant removal of product, the yield is increased in comparison 
to a batch hydrolysis. The last step uses resting cells of E.coli to catalyze the oxidation of D-GalA 
(37ºC, pH neutral) with conversion of 95.4%. 
By a technoeconomic analysis, a flowsheet of the process was drawn and a mass balance for 
producing 2000 ton/year of GA was performed. If including treatment of waste stream s4 to 
produce cattle feed as secondary product, the profit obtained is 22.5 $/kg of GA.  
From the sensibility analysis performed it was concluded that high costs of raw material are related 
to the first step of the process in which high amounts of ethanol are needed for precipitation and 
washing of pectin gel. Moreover, it was found that profit is highly sensitive to the energy demand 
for hydrolysis reaction, related to the temperature of the feed to the membrane bioreactor. This is 
because of the high quantity of water needed required for diluted subtract solution and which needs 
to be heat-up. Furthermore, it was found that the profit is more sensible to the price of GA than 
that of the dry peels.  
Comparing the suggested route to the conventional processes to synthesize GA, in an 
environmental point of view, oxidizing agents such as nitric acid are avoid. Also, using PG 
enzymes and catalytic resting cells of E.coli, respectively, in the second and third steps of the 
process allows the use of mild operational conditions. Also, although it was found that the 
membrane bioreactor is energy intensive, the complementary between reaction and membrane 
separation allows increasing the yields of reaction by avoiding product inhibition. Also, enzymes 
are retained in the membrane which simplifies the process of recycling the enzymes.  Never the 
less, this reactor needs to be implemented in a practical way for large scale processes in a way that 
high accumulation of material is avoided.  Regarding the last step of the process, HCl was used to 
==PAGE:60==
Food Waste upgrading by an integrated biorefinery process 
46 
 
precipitate GA and this is a drawback of the process. However, by recycling this acid, relatively 
low amounts are needed. 
It is important to consider uncertainties is the results. This are due to the scale up procedure which 
was done by proportional ratios from laboratory data to large-scale or empirically in the case of 
calculating the amount of catalytic cells of E.coli. Moreover, operational costs were also calculated 
by using scaling-up factors and indices available in the literature or based on existing facility costs. 
The fact that costs of wastewater treatment and electricity for bipolar electrodialysis membrane 
had not been considered, can also affect the profit. Nevertheless, the profit is calculated in a way 
that capital cost investment is being considered. Therefore, after the considered 10 years of pay-
back time, the profit is expected to be higher than the one calculated.  This leaves margins to 
account with cost of wastewater facilities and other costs not calculated. 
An important factor to consider is the possible competition with the pectin industry for the same 
biomass. Indeed pectin market is expected to increase due to the use of pectin for human 
comsumption. However, the price of GA is still higher than that of pectin and is expected to stay 
higher than 11$/kg. If GA is used as a platform chemical, then it will be able to compete with 
pectin industry due to increase in demand. 
Finally, due to lack of time to perform detailed simulations and due to unavailable data in the 
literature regarding some of the steps in the suggested route, enable one of making a proper 
sustainability analysis to the process. It is also of interest to integrate in the process the production 
of citric acid from waste streams containing pectin, D-GalA and other sugars, which can be 
fermented to citric acid. And in this way, the OPEX would be reduced.

12. Future work 
 
In future work, detailed simulation should be done accounting with batch times for extraction of 
pectin and oxidation of D-GalA, as well as, further sustainability analysis of the process. For such, 
available laboratory data is an essential requirement. However, information is scarce regarding GA 
precipitation with HCl. Indeed, it is important to find a more environmental-friendly way to 
recover GA. 
Also, after checking profitability of the process through a detailed analysis, experiments in pilot 
scale need to be done to verify yields.  
Integration of the process should be done in order to optimize it. Additionally, other alternative 
presented in chapter 2 routes should also be studied and compared to the suggested one.  
Furthermore, it is expected that developments in biotechnology will make it possible to find more 
efficient p of producing GA from Food Waste, such as highly selective microorganisms or 
enzymes able to catalyze a reaction allowing high yields in short periods of time.
