Abstract 
This thesis consists of a series of seven computational fluid dynamic (CFD) simulations to assess the 
smoke contribution of unprotected Cross-laminated Timber (CLT) surface elements in an enclosure. 
The simulations were modelled with a 250kW room-corner fire in a standard office compartment, 
initially with concrete linings and gradually increasing the amount of wood linings. The smoke 
contribution is investigated to determine the time to critical condition in a pre-flashover fire enclosure. 
The assessment is based on when the smoke reaches the critical height of 2m (acceptance criteria) in 
an enclosure height of 4m and is examined through the vertical profile of smoke layer height and 
temperature in Pyrosim (CFD fire modelling software). To simulate smoke from combustible 
surfaces, the material properties were configured, and the pyrolysis reaction rate equation was 
utilized. 
The first simulation compares the Zukoski theory on smoke filling in an enclosure with non-
combustible linings. The simulation resulted in a 53-second longer evacuation time, indicating that 
the Zukoski smoke-filling method predicts more conservative fire safety design results since it is 
based on simpler design fires. 
Six additional simulations were conducted to investigate the correlation between the quantity and 
location of combustible linings. The simulations demonstrated that wood surfaces can affect the 
critical time and contribute to smoke developing and increased temperature. The effect of wood 
linings is determined by their proximity to the source of the fire. Fire can spread to an adjacent 
combustible wall, triggering the pyrolysis process, which contributes to smoke and, as a result, 
reduces the time required to safely evacuate the enclosure. The simulations therefore revealed that in 
a pre-flashover fire, the quantity of combustible linings is only relevant if it is adjacent to the fire, but 
the impact is also limited due to the short time required for the enclosure to reach critical smoke 
condition. 
Finally, a study was conducted to examine the impact of using concrete vs. wood on the time it takes 
to reach critical smoke condition. According to the study, the Available Safe Egress Time (ASET) is 
reduced by 10–20 % depending on the number of combustible surfaces adjacent to the fire.

CHAPTER 1 
 
Introduction 
Concerns about the environmental impact of building constructions are leading to conventional 
building materials like concrete being substituted with wood, which can reduce the building’s CO2 
emissions by 50% [1]. However, wood often faces scepticism regarding its safety in the event of fire, 
since it is a combustible building material that may burn along with the fuel load present in an 
enclosure. This can affect the time until critical condition is experienced during the evacuation phase, 
and therefore impact safe egress and generate an unsafe environment for the building’s users. 
Existing fire safety engineering design approaches are mostly based on fire dynamics in non-
combustible enclosures. Since structural wood, such as cross-laminated timber (CLT), is becoming 
increasingly popular, it is more critical than ever to understand fire dynamics in combustible 
enclosures. For fire scenarios with non-combustible enclosure linings, the time until critical condition 
is reached can be found using hand calculations based on the criterion for the height of the smoke 
layer [2]. For combustible enclosure linings, the smoke development differentiates since the lining 
may contribute to the fire and create different smoke conditions in an enclosure. Since most deaths 
related to fire are associated with smoke inhalation [3], it is important to be able to calculate when 
critical condition occurs in an enclosure with combustible lining.  
The research on unprotected (exposed) wood linings is relatively limited. The existing research for 
enclosures with unprotected wood linings was identified through a literature search for this thesis. 
The relevant literature with related information is listed in Table 1 below. The methodology for the 
conducted literature search is presented in Appendix - A Literature Search. A logbook of the search 
strategy and findings is presented in Appendix - A.1 Search Strategy Document.

The dynamics governing pyrolysis and, subsequently, combustion of wood must be studied to allow 
fire-safe use of exposed structural timber elements. The reviewed literature on experimental and 
computer simulated studies observed and concluded that exposed timber in an enclosure will 
contribute to fire growth, resulting in a shorter time to flashover. Therefore, parametric models for 
enclosure fire dynamics must consider the contribution of unprotected combustible compartment 
linings. The studies vary in quantity and location of wood, which results in different effects on how 
fire spreads and its growth rate. This implies that the fuel position and distance to combustible linings 
potentially have an effect on the contribution to the production of pyrolysis gases.  
Multiple studies [4,6,8] found that whether or not the char layer of CLT falls off has a significant 
impact on fire dynamics of the enclosure. Fall off will result in exposing a fresh layer of timber, 
allowing for an increased fuel load and rapid pyrolysis in an enclosure. However, if the char layer 
does not fall off, the remaining layers will stay protected by the char layer, and the fire will 
automatically extinguish when all exposed combustible material is consumed. The experimental 
==PAGE:9==
3 
 
studies, therefore, highlight that wood has a complex pyrolysis process that may be unpredictable and 
vary from experiment to experiment. Aside from the increased fire load of exposed timber, the study 
on CLT panels [6] observed elevated smoke concentrations and earlier flashover. The development 
of smoke from CLT panels, on the other hand, was not investigated further. 
In comparison to a non-combustible ceiling in the enclosure, the experimental study of combustible 
ceilings [5] measured up to a 200°C difference in the fire growth phase. The temperature was 
discovered to be influenced by the opening factor, with a low opening factor resulting in a lower 
temperature during the growth phase. 
The computational study [7] examines the ability of a two-zone model (B-RISK) to account for the 
contribution of wood linings. The study addresses issues regarding CLT delamination, but more 
importantly, the two-zone model fails to account for the placement of exposed timber linings in 
relation to the fire. As a consequence, the model relies exclusively on the percentage of wood ratio 
rather than its positioning. 
The five studies identified and reviewed primarily focus on fire dynamics in relation to the second 
phase of fire, which means evaluating the structural fire safety of an enclosure with wood surfaces. 
McGregor’s thesis [6] do explore the first phase in some detail by examining smoke concentration, 
but the primary part focuses on delamination and the structural capabilities of wood. The literature 
review has shown a trend toward understanding the fire dynamics of exposed wood elements in order 
to better understand structural fire safety. There is an evident lack of research on the effects of 
unprotected wood on human safety. The reviewed literature observed increased fuel load and faster 
pyrolysis, implying a potential hazard for humans when evacuating and trying to obtain safe egress 
until critical conditions develop. This leads to the question of how the use of wood lining affects 
personal safety during an evacuation. And how is the smoke condition affected when concrete 
surfaces are substituted with wood?

1.1 Scope of Thesis 
Whilst the fire dynamics of utilizing wood surfaces to investigate structural safety have been 
documented, the impact on human safety is poorly understood. The aim of this study is to focus on 
the first phase of the fire in regard to using wood elements in an enclosure in order to assess the 
impact on human safety. Due to the numerous variables to consider in a fire scenario, as the literature 
search revealed, the complexity needs to be limited. The scope of this study is limited to 
understanding how the time to critical condition is impacted if some or all concrete elements are 
substituted with unprotected CLT. The study will be performed by analysing the smoke development 
until critical condition is reached.  
A compartment fire is controlled by the degree of ventilation (opening factor), fuel load, and thermal 
inertia. When non-combustible surfaces, like concrete, are substituted with combustible surfaces, like 
wood, it contributes to the fuel load and thermal inertia. Therefore, a fixed ventilated concrete 
compartment will be used as a reference, where different concrete surface linings will be substituted 
with CLT to establish a link between the quantity and location of combustible surfaces and their 
contribution to the fire.  
To study smoke development until critical condition is reached for the first phase of fire, a sequence 
of simulations, each increasing in wood surface area, are modelled. A fire simulation program 
(Computational Fluid Dynamics) will be used to compute the models. An enclosure fire with concrete 
linings will be modelled, analysed, and simulated with varying amounts of CLT substituting for 
concrete surfaces. The smoke development and height will be examined to determine wood surfaces 
effect on the available safe egress time.

CHAPTER 2 
 
Theoretical Background 
Prescriptive guidelines are used in the building codes to direct the design of a building toward 
particular levels of fire safety. The prescriptive criteria may differ depending on multiple variables 
such as size and occupancy, with the aim of reaching a similar degree of fire safety in different types 
of buildings. This affects the creative freedom in the design process by forcing it to comply with 
prescriptive rules for achieving fire safety.  
This is where performance-driven design comes into play. It enables higher flexibility, including the 
use of new technologies, allowing for more creative and complex designs. Timber construction 
designs that do not adhere to the prescriptive rules are typically created through a combination of 
prescriptive rules and performance-based design. Since modern construction elements, like CLT, are 
introduced in building projects, there are few prescriptive solutions available. As a result, 
performance-based design is heavily relied on. To use performance-based design, a better 
understanding of the fundamental fire principles is required.  
To study and investigate critical smoke conditions in an enclosure with exposed combustible 
structural wood; and compare it to a non-combustible enclosure, an understanding of the fundamental 
fire dynamics within an enclosure is presented. This includes predicting important fire phenomena, 
such as the time to critical conditions and smoke development, to ensure the fire safety design. This 
chapter will also cover the essentials of computational fluid dynamic simulations and how to use fire 
modelling competently.

2.1 Enclosure Fire 
A fire in an enclosure can be split into two phases: the pre-flashover and the post-flashover phase. In 
the pre-flashover phase the fire grows controlled by the amount of fuel present (fuel controlled) or 
the amount of air available through ventilation openings (ventilation controlled) up until flashover 
occurs. The fire safety design in the pre-flashover phase focuses on ensuring human safety.  
The post-flashover phase of a fire begins to decay as the fuel in the enclosure is consumed. The focus 
of fire safety design in this phase shifts to structural stability. Walton and Thomas [9] introduces a 
more detailed description of a compartment fire, which they divide into five stages based on 
temperature as a function of time, as illustrated in Figure 2.

2.1.2 Fuel Load  
The fuel load is the quantity of combustible material in an enclosure that releases heat at a rate 
dependent on the burning material. For different types of compartments, Eurocode 1 has defined the 
heat release rate (HHR) in kW/m2. 
The most reliable approach to model combustion, when simulating a fire, is to specify the HHR [11]. 
The HHR can be determined by either a calculation procedure, pre-existing data from experiments, 
or the predefined HHR from regulations. The simulation results of such a model have been proven to 
be successful in analysing smoke concentrations [11]. The chosen HHR is described in 3.1.2 Heat 
Release Rate.

2.2 Cross-laminated Timber 
Cross-laminated timber, CLT, is made up of several layers of wood glued together with an adhesive 
which, under high laminating pressure, forms a structural solid wood panel. The fibre direction 
changes in each layer due to the perpendicular arrangement of the layers, as shown in Figure 3. As a 
result, loads will be distributed in various directions, contributing to the high stiffness and strength of 
CLT elements.  
 
 
==PAGE:18==
12 
 
CLT is excellent for prefabrication, which allows for a quick and efficient construction process as 
well as easy disassembly once the building has outlived its usefulness. Furthermore, CLT has the 
advantage of being composed of wood, a renewable resource that, when compared to alternative 
materials like steel and concrete, delivers a positive CO2 accounting. Aside from the environmental 
benefits of using naturally sustainable material, CLT has also enabled construction projects to 
embrace the use of timber by offering new architectural possibilities and thus creating a greater 
demand for CLT.  
CLT is classified as Euro class D-s2, d0 by CLT Denmark [12], meaning it reacts to fire by 
contributing (D) and also creates limited smoke production (s2) but no droplets (d0). CLT can 
therefore be characterized as a combustible material that will be affected and begin to burn in the 
event of a fire. In general, timber releases a high amount of heat, and the heat of combustion is found 
to be around 17.5 ±2.5MJ/kg [9]. This has an impact not only on the structural integrity but also on 
human safety. It does, nonetheless, have low thermal conductivity and a high specific heat capacity, 
resulting in increased insulating capabilities.

2.2.1 Pyrolysis and Smoke Production 
Pyrolysis is the initial phase in the gasification or combustion process, during which a material 
undergoes chemical degradation. Degradation occurs due to the limited thermal strength of chemical 
bonds in materials, enabling the bonds to be broken with heat.  
Structural wood like CLT is able to combust due to an endothermic pyrolysis process where thermal 
degradation occurs, producing combustible gases through a loss in mass and cross-section. A 
substantial quantity of heat input is required, which is consequently absorbed by the wood, forcing it 
to burn through a gasification process. The gas is composed of H, CO, CO2, and hydrocarbons, with 
CO2 dominating at the start of the process. Smoke is produced when the evaporation of hydrocarbons 
from the wood into the ambient air happens and occurs when there is incomplete combustion. The 
smoke is composed of small particles of carbon and other components that are visible due to the 
compilation of unburned particles in the air [13].  
Up to 200°C, wood releases water, and between 200-280°C, gases begin to emerge. The actual 
pyrolysis begins at 280°C and continues to 500°C, producing substantial amounts of tar and gases. 
After 500°C the gas production is minimal and mainly consists of hydrogen [14].  
The temperature at which a wood surface ignites is usually at 250-300℃. It is influenced by several 
variables, including its shape, the quantity of oxygen in the air, the surrounding airflow, and the 
duration of fire exposure. So, since an enclosure containing unprotected CLT elements has the 
potential to ignite and enhance the heat release rate, exposed CLT is expected to affect the fire 
dynamics of an enclosure.  
 
2.2.2 Charring 
As wood burns, it produces a rigid carbon-rich char layer at the surface, which protects the rest of the 
wood (does not apply to thin pieces of wood, which can continue to burn) due to the low thermal 
conductivity of the char layer. The temperature of the exposed char layer is approximately the same 
as the fire, but the temperature of the wood layer behind it, known as the pyrolysis zone, is lower and 
approximately at around 200°C. Heat transfer reduces as the char layer depth grows, however heat 
penetration into the pyrolysis layer might increase depending on organic impurities and cracks in the

2.5 Computational Fluid Dynamics 
CFD is a numerical method for analysing systems that contain fluids (liquids and gases). CFD 
applications/models are based on numerical algorithms designed for predicting fluid flow, heat 
transfer, and mass transfer by solving mathematical equations through an analytical approach. The 
outcome of CFD analysis provides important engineering data for conceptual investigations of new 
designs. The method of computer simulation has the advantage of preventing costly errors and 
minimizing design risks. 
CFD operates by breaking down a model into a finite number of control volumes (cells), as illustrated 
in Figure 9. This defines the location of the flow field, which is then rendered by discretizing general 
conservation equations into algebraic equations and solving them using an iterative approach [13] 
[14].  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 9: Illustration of fluid region of smoke flow discretized into finite number cells 
The computational three-dimensional grid, also called a mesh, consists of multiple cells whose 
boundary conditions are generally defined by the geometry (linings). The mesh cells resemble blocks 
with the same length, width, and height; however, they can be non-uniform in size in any direction to 
modify the mesh's uniformity if required [11]. The level of detail of the model (the number of cells) 
is determined by the required accuracy needed. When there is a higher quantity of cells, more 
computer processing power is required to perform the calculations in each cell, but the outcome is 
more accurately represented.  
According to the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) own guidelines [11], who 
developed a CFD software called the Fire Dynamics Simulator (FDS), the mesh resolution size is 
determined by the objective. A study of the size can be conducted by creating an FDS input file with 
==PAGE:28==
22 
 
a coarse mesh and then refining it to see if there are any significant discrepancies in the findings. An 
independent grid resolution study is presented in Chapter 3 - 3.3.1 Grid Resolution.  
A numerical model necessitates an understanding of the assumptions applied, and there are a few 
factors to consider before initiating any computations. Fire growth is primarily determined by the 
thermal properties of the materials it is surrounded by (the enclosure). Even if all of the thermal 
characteristics are known, due to limitations in the model algorithms or numerical grid, the objective 
of interest may not be simulated correctly. Some materials have previously been examined and 
validated by NIST and have been added to FDS. To ensure a correctly computed simulation to 
examine the desired objective, it is necessary to first supply the material with thermal properties and 
then validate the effectiveness of the algorithm by comparing it to experiments. This is to ensure that 
the computed model contains the relevant principles and physics as the experiments. Only then can 
the computed model be relied upon to accurately estimate the results of a fire. According to the FDS 
Validation Guide [22], model validation is composed of three steps:  
 Examining model assumptions against experimental data 
 Analysing the differences in both measurements and model input 
 Determining whether the model is adequate for the task at hand 
The model validation required and possible for this thesis is described in chapter 3 - 3.4 Model 
Validation.  
 
2.5.1 Fire Modelling  
CFD can be utilized to analyse potential fire and smoke hazards, assess occupant comfort, and provide 
data on fire growth and smoke movement by enabling a "virtual airflow model” of a building or 
compartment. To model a fire, NIST developed the FDS software. The software uses numerical 
analysis to utilize the principles of the Navier-Stokes equations, which are ideal for low-speed, 
thermally driven flows like smoke and heat transfer from fires. The equations are solved iteratively 
by the simulation as a steady-state or transient condition [21]. FDS provides accurate and dependable 
data that has been confirmed, by calorimeter experiments and large-scale tests, as a computer tool for 
simulating fires [19] – [21].  
==PAGE:29==
23 
 
As an add-on software for FDS, Thunderhead Engineering developed Pyrosim. Pyrosim is a graphical 
user interface to provide 3D modelling through rendering of numerical calculations from FDS, 
making fire analysis more convenient and intuitive. Pyrosim allows you to construct or import a CAD 
model while the fire is created within the software. The simulation can then accurately predict smoke 
movement, heat release rate, and other fire-related values in the model based on the defined fire and 
boundaries.  
A fire in Pyrosim is governed by a created or pre-included reaction. The reaction can then be utilized 
by constructing a surface that acts as a "burner vent," allowing the reaction to take place. The burner 
is defined as a heat-conduction solid burning object of a homogenous material that burns at the surface 
with specified thermal properties and pyrolysis behaviour. Once the heat of combustion and the 
desired HRR are determined, FDS calculates the fuel mass flow rate from the surface to simulate a 
fire [26]. The reaction can be composed by using a "Simple Chemistry" model made up of C, H, O, 
and N that reacts with the oxygen to produce H2O, CO2, soot, N2, and CO.  
After constructing a model, a mesh is used to apply numerical algorithms to predict the outcome of a 
string of processes that commence from ambient conditions. Post-processing the outcome can be done 
in Smokeview, an integrated program allowing to view smoke, temperatures, and other FDS 
simulation outputs. For instance, if a fire compartment is generated in Pyrosim, Smokeview reads the 
FDS output files and renders animations to display smoke development throughout the simulated 
time. An example of smoke development in a compartment created in Pyrosim and displayed through 
Smokeview is shown in Figure 10. Pyrosim also provides data in csv files, which may be accessed 
within Pyrosim via historical plots or via an external program such as Excel. 
==PAGE:30==
24 
Figure 10: Smoke development in a compartment created in Pyrosim and shown through Smokeview 
Initially, the smoke is defined by the created reaction/fire in Pyrosim, as can be seen in Figure 10. To 
simulate smoke production for a specific surface/material that is exposed to the fire, pyrolysis 
parameters are assigned. To govern explicitly how the material burns, the solid surfaces are assigned 
thermal boundary conditions, and through the pyrolysis function, the heat of combustion and reaction 
is added. FDS uses empirical correlations to control heat and mass transfer to and from solid surfaces. 
In an enclosure fire, FDS calculates the position of the boundary between the hot upper layer (smoke) 
and the cooler bottom layer (air). It reads layers and calculates the smoke layer height using the 
"Layer-zoning Device" function. To calculate the smoke interface height, the device utilizes an 
algorithm based on numerical integration of the mesh along vertical lines [11]. Unlike two-zone 
models, which divide the zones into two, a CFD model like FDS simulates a continuous temperature 
profile, which can be used to determine the smoke layer height as a function of time. This can be used 
to determine when critical conditions for a specified enclosure occur.

3.3.1 Grid Resolution 
The output of CFD is determined by the numerical grid resolution, where the size is determined by 
the number of cells that make up the width, length, and height of the mesh. FDS recommends 
performing an independent grid resolution analysis depending on the objective (smoke layer height) 
to identify a suitable cell size for reliable CFD simulations [11]. This goal is to ascertain when the 
objective's outcomes have converged in order to establish the smallest mesh size that can be 
adequately used. 
The model setup used for the study is based on the earlier described setup in 3.1 Model Setup. 
According to the findings presented in Appendix - B Independent Grid Resolution Study, it is not 
possible to compute cell sizes that do not match the mesh size; only resolutions of 0.2m and 0.1m 
could be conducted. Typically, a cell size is deemed sufficient if the results have converged, but 
additional resolutions are not simulated due to the constraints in the zoning device. Based on the 
study, it can be concluded that there are only minor differences in smoke layer height as the smoke 
begins to stabilize for 0.1m and 0.2m, indicating that the coarse resolution is adequate. However, 
because one of the objectives to be researched is the quantity of combustible lining, a minor difference 
in smoke layer height has a significant impact on the final results. As a result, the cells used must be 
0.1m in size in order to maintain a high level of accuracy. An illustration of the cell size and mesh is

3.3.3 Smoke Layer Calculation 
With the height of the enclosure at 4m, the critical smoke layer height can be found using eq. (2.4): 
𝑧𝑧= 1.6𝑚𝑚+ 0.1 ⋅4𝑚𝑚= 2𝑚𝑚 
2 m is the value until critical condition is reached in the designed enclosure, and the value is the 
available egress time. The value is equal to half of the enclosure height and can be found in Pyrosim 
by generating a graph of the smoke layer height as a function of time for the computed fire 
simulations.  
The graph can, however, vary a lot and hit 2m after 60 seconds, but then rise and first stabilize at 2m 
later on. As concluded from the conducted grid resolution study, B Independent Grid Resolution 
Study, the data from the simulation in the beginning can be ignored until the smoke has stabilized. 
This is due to the fact that if the layer height decreases, it does not necessarily imply the existence of 
a layer, as there is no distinguishable layer at the beginning of the enclosure fire. The algorithm 
used by FDS is temperature dependent. Anything that leads the temperature across the vertical 
line, configured in the device, to differ from the ambient temperature can cause the layer height 
to change. For example, passing a jet of warm gas over the midpoint of the vertical line being 
measured would result in a layer height decrease even if no layer existed. 
A visualization in Figure 14 shows how the value for the critical smoke layer height can be determined 
for the enclosure. The results will be assessed with the layer temperature and the overall

3.4 Model Validation 
Fire models can vary in many ways, and it is not always certain that FDS can compute actual physical 
phenomena correctly. To determine how well FDS predicts the attribute for combustible materials, a 
model validation study was conducted. The validation method comprises of (1) comparing model 
settings to experimental measurements, (2) evaluating model uncertainty, and (3) determining 
whether the model is adequate for validating the attribute from combustible materials. 
Model validation for this thesis would ideally consist of verifying an experiment that studies the 
contribution of smoke from wood surfaces. Since no experiment of that kind is accessible, the next

best alternative is to validate an enclosure with combustible linings. Since it was not possible to find 
experimental reports with sufficient data to replicate the setup in Pyrosim, the third best option is to 
validate the pyrolysis process of wood. This is the most crucial component to be able to replicate in 
CFD for this study, as it validates the wood´s attribute to a fire. 
A model validation study has been performed by simulating a wooden crib fire and comparing the 
HHR to experimental data. NIST conducted the experiment in 2018 and later verified it using FDS, 
with the validation results published in the FDS validation guide [22] and the full experimental study 
in [30]. The model validation study conducted for this paper, through Pyrosim, is presented in 
Appendix – D Model Validation.  
The study reveals that replicating a fire with a combustible material like wood requires some pyrolysis 
parameters in the model which are not given in the experimental report. In the study, Pyrsosim´s 
ability to reproduce the experiment is illustrated by plots where the experimental results and the 
simulation results are compared. The uncertainties are discussed, and the findings discovered that the 
mesh resolution has a substantial influence on model validation. The 4cm resolution was found to be 
highly accurate in replicating the crib fire, with only minor deviations. As a result, the study concludes 
that the Pyrosim model can validate the fire attribute of wood elements and is an 81.8% match, with 
peak HHR at roughly the same time. And, particularly at the beginning, the simulation is more 
accurate, which is important in this study as the pre-flashover is being investigated.  
As a result, the model has been determined to be adequate for use in this study. However, it should 
be noted that this only validates the HHR attribute of wood and not smoke contribution of wood in 
an enclosure. As a consequence, unrealistic and even unreliable findings may be expected from the 
models.

Table 7 shows a 37.6% increment in the CFD method through FDS compared to the hand calculation 
approach developed by Zukoski. The hand calculation method is more conservative, resulting in the 
smoke reaching the critical height 53 seconds before the simulation. The results demonstrate that the 
conventional Zukoski smoke-filling approach may oversimplify the rate of smoke filling in an 
enclosure. When investigating why there is a 37.6% difference, the following factors play a part: 
 Heat losses to the surrounding boundaries are ignored when using the Zukoski method. When 
compared to the simulation that calculates plausible realistic conditions, the Zukoski method 
ignores the mass rate of the fuel and heat losses to surrounding walls that may have an impact 
on the smoke development rate.  
 
 Smoke takes time to travel from a point source to the ceiling and spread out over the surface 
region of the ceiling. The simulation takes this into account, whereas the Zukoski method 
==PAGE:50==
44 
 
considers the plume as if it were a pipeline, with smoke being transported to the ceiling and 
throughout the ceiling region in an instant. The method neglects the time it takes for smoke 
to travel and spread, which impacts the ASET. 
 
 The simulation is based on a t-squared constant fire, which approximates the heat release rate 
based on empirical data. After 73 seconds, the fire reaches a constant HHR of 250kW, whereas 
the Zukoski method's dimensionless heat release rate equation employs a constant HHR of 
250kW. This influences the rate at which smoke develops, which ultimately impacts the time 
it takes for critical smoke condition. By utilizing a constant HHR, the hand calculation does 
not represent a somewhat realistic fire behaviour; it is a hypothetical fire behaviour that 
predicts considerably more drastic smoke consequences.  
 
 The Zukoski method is based on a leakage in the bottom, while the simulation is modelled 
with two openings. The openings provide an opening factor of 0.02m1/2, which has an impact 
on the temperature development in the enclosure. The fact is, though, that the openings 
probably have a greater impact on the post-flashover phase (structural fire) and a lesser impact 
on the pre-flashover phase (personal fire safety). So, this is probably a smaller factor 
influencing the 37.6% difference.  
Multiple factors have an impact on the difference between the simple hand calculation method and a 
computer simulation. Ultimately, the Zukoski smoke-filling method predicts more conservative fire 
safety design results since it relies on simpler design fires and simple hand calculations to determine 
ASET. It is ultimately safer to use, yet an overly cautious design approach may result in costly fire 
safety measures.

4.2.1 Summary of the Simulation Results 
To investigate the effect of exposed wood surfaces on the safe egress time, six main simulations with 
combustible linings were conducted and one additional simulation. Patterns and connections were 
discussed and verified based on the findings. The results revealed that: 
- 
Data from S2-S7 showed that there is no direct relationship between the amount of exposed 
wood linings in an enclosure and the critical time for safe egress. The first two configurations, 
S2 and S3, demonstrated some association to quantity, but subsequent configurations with 
more wood resulted in higher ASET, meaning more time to evacuate the enclosure. As a 
result, there is no direct relationship between the quantity of combustible material in an 
enclosure. 
 
- 
A link was discovered between the position of wood linings and the ignition source, where 
wood surfaces adjacent to the fire have a significant impact on the time to critical condition. 
This demonstrates that the quantity of wood has an effect, but only if it is adjacent to the fire 
or another combustible wall close to the fire, as seen in S6 with a wood roof neighbouring a 
wood wall adjacent to the fire. 
 
==PAGE:59==
53 
 
- 
Based on the discussion and outcomes from the ASET vs. wood location, it was established 
that there is a limit to how much of the combustible lining near the fire can affect the critical 
time. This was observed from S7, which had two wood walls and a roof near the fire, yielding 
an almost equivalent ASET to S3, which only had two wood walls close to the fire. So, 
although a link between the location of wood linings and the ignition source was discovered, 
the influence on ASET appears to be limited.  
 
In general, Pyrosim achieved great success in simulating and accounting for the contribution of smoke 
from exposed wood surfaces based on the pyrolysis reaction rate. However, the impacts averaged 
around 20–25 seconds, which raises the question of whether the results are reasonable or not. In 
addition, minor deviations in critical time and temperature were detected for simulations with 
combustible surfaces not adjacent to the fire, revealing potential uncertainties in FDS and Pyrosim 
when simulating smoke contribution from combustible surfaces. However, smaller uncertainties were 
expected based on the model validation results in Appendix - D Model Validation. In the following 
section, a comparison with the concrete enclosure is made to further assess the credibility of 
simulating with combustible linings.

4.4 Uncertainties and Sources of Error  
Some uncertainties were discovered after reviewing the results and from the insights gained through 
the discussions. (1) In general, smoke is a complex and challenging by-product to accurately measure, 
particularly its height, which can vary from one end of the room to the other. The simulation evaluates 
it based on temperature, which explains why the results show smaller fluctuations. When looking at 
the three devices, there is a delay in the results, especially at the beginning, because the smoke spreads 
throughout the roof area and rolls down the walls and into "layers" throughout the enclosure. The 
inaccuracy in smoke height is estimated to be 0.1m, resulting in a ASET deviation of +/- 5 seconds. 
(2) FDS shows smaller inconsistencies, even when creating nearly identical simulations. The results 
from S2, with a half-wood wall adjacent to the fire, and the results from S8, with the same setup as 
S2 but an additional half-wood wall on the opposite side, lead to minor variations in results. Despite 
the fact that the extra half wood wall makes no difference for the duration of ASET, the critical time 
has a small deviation of 1.4% and the temperature progression has a small deviation of 0-3.25%. The 
error is caused by the CFD software’s numerical computation of the cells, solution algorithm, 
discretization system, and system approximations, which can exhibit disparate numerical results for 
the same CFD simulation.  
Overall, the uncertainties discovered after computing the models have been small. The results were 
heavily dependent on the outcome from layer-zoning device, which has proven to be a sensitive tool. 
Nevertheless, the device's results were consistent with the thermocouples and Smokeview, indicating 
that the data were reliable. The uncertainty is based on data fluctuations, which can affect the time 
determined for when the smoke reaches the critical height. 
The greatest level of uncertainty was encountered prior to initiating the simulations, during model 
validation. Although the validated experiment was not the most ideal experiment, it was still the best 
alternative available. The uncertainty emanates from the incapability to validate the smoke 
contribution from wood linings. The results and discussion in this chapter, on the other hand, suggest 
that the results are plausible since the results are clearly distinguishable, but the uncertainty is still 
existing. A discussion is formed in 5.1 Future Research, explaining how further work can be done to 
eliminate the uncertainty.

CHAPTER 5 
 
Conclusion 
The objective of this thesis was to understand the impact of CLT surface elements on ASET in an 
enclosure. The method was computationally oriented, with seven models simulated that differed in 
the quantity of wood surfaces. To simulate smoke from combustible surfaces, corrective parameters 
needed to be established in FDS/Pyrosim, as well as the variables for the pyrolysis reaction rate 
equation. To accurately simulate the models, a grid resolution and model validation study were 
undertaken prior to computing the models.  
The grid resolution study relied on an independent study that determined a cell size of 0.1m to be 
suitable. Due to the long processing time and limitations in obtaining data from layer-zoning devices, 
smaller cell sizes were not studied. Additionally, previous studies have shown that a mesh with a cell 
size of 0.1m is sufficient for this sized compartment and purpose.  
The model validation study was successful in reproducing the HHR from a burning crib experiment. 
However, the objective of this thesis was to investigate how CLT surfaces affect the time to critical 
smoke condition, and no equivalent real-life experiment could be found. This resulted in uncertainty 
before conducting the simulation, and the applicability of employing FDS in this thesis was 
questioned. The simulations conducted did not alter the mentioned uncertainty, but the results from 
the conducted simulations demonstrated FDS' ability to consider the impact on ASET if some or all 
concrete elements are substituted with unprotected CLT. And the fact that FDS/Pyrosim can establish 
the difference suggests that the results are plausible even though a more suitable validation has not 
been performed.  
 
The simulations revealed that the quantity of wood linings is only relevant in relation to their location 
to the fire source. S1 (reference), S2 (W1/2), S6 (WR), S3 (W2x1/2) increases in wood surfaces close 
to the fire and they show a clear effect on the time to critical condition. ASET is shown to be reduced 
by 10–20% depending on the quantity of combustible surfaces near the fire. One wall reduces the 
==PAGE:69==
63 
 
ASET by 10%, one wall and roof by 16% and two walls or more by 20%. The results were obtained 
using layer-zoning devices, which have proven to be a sensitive tool with smaller fluctuations when 
reading the ASET, but the data were found to be reliable. Furthermore, the findings revealed the 
following: 
- 
The Zukoski hand calculation approach for smoke-filling uses more conservative fire safety 
design and achieves a 37.6% higher ASET compared to the CFD simulation. The method was 
found to rely on simpler design fire and, as a result, is a more cautious design approach than 
CFD. 
 
- 
S4 with a full-length wood wall results in nearly identical outcomes as S2 with a half-wood 
wall. This means that a full combustible wood wall has the same impact on the smoke layer 
height as a half wall for the duration of ASET.  
 
- 
Combustible surfaces that are not adjacent to the fire will not contribute smoke as they will 
not undergo pyrolysis during ASET. This is demonstrated by simulation S5 with a 
combustible roof and a supplementary simulation S8 with a wall opposite to the fire. 
 
- 
There is a limit to how many combustible surfaces can affect the ASET. The result shows two 
combustible walls are the maximum, and three surfaces will not reduce the ASET further but 
will create more severe conditions moments after the ASET is reached. 
The impact of CLT surface elements on ASET in an enclosure has been found and investigated to 
understand the impact on human safety. The results obtained are limited to the size of the fire and the 
modelled enclosure. If a different size was investigated, the critical height and hence the ASET would 
be altered, implying that three combustible surfaces near the fire might shorten the safe egress time 
compared to two combustible walls. This would necessitate more research. The following section 
discusses how to conduct additional assessments. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:70==
64 
 
5.1 Future Research 
The research conducted in this study enlightens the effects of combustible lining quantity and location 
on safe egress time. Further assessments for varied enclosure sizes and fire scenarios are required to 
provide a more comprehensive understanding of the subject. This will evaluate the objective under a 
broader set of variables in order to fully understand the impact and to be able to refine the necessary 
parameters and assumptions in the model. The current study highlights the uncertainty of not being 
able to validate the model with an ideal experiment. Therefore, it is suggested that a full-scale 
experiment of the study be conducted to validate the model, which can either eliminate the uncertainty 
discovered prior to simulating or reveal that FDS is incapable of simulating smoke from combustible 
walls. With an ideal model validation further refinement of the simulation can be accomplish and a 
better understanding on the subject matter. The study revealed a limitation in the impact of several 
combustible surface linings adjacent to the fire. The limit, however, only existed during ASET, as S7 
showed a much higher temperature after ASET was obtained. This means that if different 
compartment sizes, fires, and openings were investigated, different results for simulations with three 
combustible surfaces could be obtained.  
To derive a similar calculation method as Zukoski smoke-filling method, more variables need to be 
investigated. The current study has investigated the quantity and location of combustible surfaces, 
but to be able to use the results, investigations of various compartment sizes and fires are required. 
The Zukoski smoke-filling method can be varied in HHR, room height, and floor area, and is only 
limited by the room being leaky (floor or roof leak). To derive a hand calculation method for rooms 
with combustible wood linings, a further study of different fires and room volumes is required.

before initiating new research. To discover and review literature on the matter, a search strategy is 
devised. The research approach starts with the generation of search terms, which is done by 
establishing the concepts that will serve as the basis for the search term analysis. This is performed 
by creating a comprehensive research question based on the thesis problem. Following the selection 
of terms from the research question, related terms are generated (synonyms, abbreviations,

expanding terminologies for the research area. Some of the most important terms used to find

B Independent Grid Resolution Study  
CFD's ability to numerically analyse each individual cell is determined by the resolution of the chosen 
grid. FDS suggests either to determine the required cell size, for a reliable CFD computations, through 
an independent grid resolution investigation based on the objective or by calculating the optimal size.  
The calculation is based on the non-dimensional expression D*/δx (D* fire diameter, δx size of a 
mesh) as presented in FDS Validation Guide [22]. The minimum cell size, based on the calculation, 
is approximately 01m. 
The calculated cell size is utilized as a finer resolution in this study, whereas a larger cell size is 
chosen for a coarser resolution. Pyrosim, by default, suggests a uniformly cells size 0.2m. The grid 
resolution study will therefore be based on a simulation with the suggested cell size (0.2m) and a 
simulation with a finer grid resolution (0.1m) for the computational domain. The cell size for the two 
simulations can be seen in Figure 30 and Figure 31.

B.2.1 Parallel Processing through multiple meshes 
Pyrosim has a feature allowing parallel processing a model with multiple meshed. The feature can 
e.g. allow one to refine a mesh around a fire while keeping the coarser mesh away from the fire. This 
solution can help to significantly reduce the time required to complete a simulation, while obtaining 
the results required.  
A test was conducted where are finer 
meshed were used in the upper 2m of 
the enclosure. This test is, therefore, 
a combination of test 1 and 2, with 
the upper mesh being 0.1m and the 
lower mesh being 0.2m (see Figure 
34). The simulation took 382min, 
which is an increment of 813% 
compared to test 1, but a decrement of 69.5% compared to test 2.  
The results obtained for the height of the smoke layer were inadequate. The smoke height was 
measured at 4 m by the layer-zoning device for the whole duration, with only a 0.01 – 0.03 m 
variation. As a result, it is concluded that the method of multiple meshes is impractical for determining 
the height of the smoke layer. 
 
B.3 Grid Resolution Conclusion 
Based on the findings, it is decided not to accept Pyrsosim´s suggested grid resolution (0.2m), because 
even minor differences can have an impact on the results when comparing models with differing 
quantities of combustible surface lining. The intention of estimating fire behaviour in an enclosure's 
fire safety design is to ensure that the fire safety performance is verified under certain suitably 
conservative assumptions. 0.2m is the slightly more conservative option since it achieves the required 
critical height slightly earlier than 0.1m. Furthermore, simulation with a coarser grid resolution 
requires considerable amount of processing time. However, due to the difference between the two 
resolution sizes chosen and the significance it may have for simulations to be performed in the report, 
Figure 34: Domain divided into two meshes (from Pyrosim) 
==PAGE:84==

in regard to small variations in combustible wood lining, the larger cell size cannot be justified. As a 
result, the appropriate cell size is 0.1m.  
The third test conducted, which involved parallel processing with multiple meshes, yielded incorrect 
findings and was thus declared unsuitable for use in this thesis. Cell sizes between 0.1-0.2m were also 
declared unsuitable due to incorrect findings via the layer-zoning device.  
In addition, according to the results of the grid resolution study, the simulations (tests 1 and 2) reveal 
inconsistencies in the smoke height at the beginning of the fire. This is because FDS employs a 
temperature-dependent algorithm; as a result, the coarser mesh oscillates more in the beginning since 
there is no distinguishable layer at the beginning of the simulation. Also, when the plume hits the 
ceiling, it spreads radially in the form of a ceiling jet and rolls down the walls which may impact the 
device readings. However, after a distinguishable smoke layer has developed, the data obtained has 
a reduced impact on the accuracy of the smoke layer, as the study has shown. As a result, the data 
obtained at the beginning of simulations conducted in this thesis will be ignored until stabilization is 
reached, at which point the critical smoke layer height can be determined.

materials to study the relation between the peak heat release rate and the quantity of combustible 
elements. The full report by NIST can be found in [30].  A validation study for one of the experiments

Guide show to be highly dependent on the grid resolution. Description of the model configuration in 
the validation guide is very limited, however the information will be integrated with past research of 
Pyrosim to recreate the experiment in a computer simulation.

fire: 
- 
Pyrolysis of wood is a complex process that differs from time to time. One of the reasons is 
that while the char layer may protect the rest of the wood, the formation of cracks might allow 
heat to penetrate the wood farther, resulting in the generation of additional heat. That is also 
why the heat of combustion for wood varies between 17-20MJ/kg and is defined as 16.7 
±0.5MJ/kg in the experiment. Because pyrolysis of wood is such a complicated process, 
Pyrosim has chosen to characterize it as a pyrolysis reaction rate, which is defined by a range 
of parameters which are not usually generated in experiments. As a result, getting the exact 
same outcomes in a simulation can be difficult. 
 
- 
The experimental setup differentiates from the simulation. The experiment takes place in a 
research facility and under a hood to measure the HHR, whereas the simulations utilize open 
mesh boundaries and numerical calculations for analysing the HHR. As a consequence, a little 
difference in atmospheric setting, as well as different measuring instruments, might have an 
impact on HHR data. 
 
==PAGE:91==
85 
 
- 
The fire source in the model differs from the one used in the experiment, causing some 
uncertainty in the ignition of the crib. Since it was not possible to recreate the same fire source 
as the experiment, a difference in the beginning (ignition) is expected.  
 
D.3 Conclusion 
In D.1 Setup, the model settings were compared to experimental measurements, and the factors that 
differed between the experiment and simulation were identified, which can have an effect on the 
results. In D.2 Results and Discussion, a review of the outcome is given in order to assess the 
uncertainties. Now it is possible to determine whether the model is adequate for validating the 
attribute from combustible materials. 
The findings revealed that mesh resolution has a significant impact on model validation, and that the 
finer the mesh, the better the validation. Secondly, the pyrolysis parameters, which are not defined in 
the experimental report but are a critical feature of Pyrosim, were found to be the source of the 
majority of the model settings uncertainty. The 4cm resolution is determined to be highly accurate in 
simulating the crib fire, and the difference in results between experiment and simulation is linked to 
the wood's complex pyrolysis process, experimental setup (atmospheric setting and measuring 
device), and fire source. 
It can be concluded that the fire attribute of a combustible material such as wood can be simulated. 
The peak HHR is determined to be an 81.8% match to the experiment and at almost the same time 
with a resolution of 4cm (95.6%). Given the level of uncertainty discovered, Pyrosim shows to be 
suitable for validating the attribute from combustible materials and, therefore valid to use in this 
thesis.
