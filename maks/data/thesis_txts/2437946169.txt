Perception of bio-based plastics 
i 
Abstract 
Finite oil resources and visible plastic pollution in our environment and marine systems is a 
widely discussed topic. However, in our economy plastics represent an essential and valuable 
material for a variety of applications. A promising alternative for policy makers which brings 
together both aspects are bio-based plastics (BBP). Companies recently started replacing 
conventional plastics with a bio-based solution. However, the perception and expectation of 
consumers towards this technology on the one hand and the business perspective of these 
aspects on the other hand are not clear yet. 
This study aims to extend the knowledge around the topic of BBP by investigating from these 
two perspectives. Based on the findings of a systematic literature review the consumers’ 
perception is captured in general and product specific by an online questionnaire in two 
different countries: Denmark and Colombia. Especially important are expectations towards the 
origin of raw materials and behavioural implications after the usage phase of products.  
Furthermore, a conceptual framework is developed to analyse publicly available information 
on how companies report around their efforts of BBP. 
The results show two main findings to contribute to academic literature. First, both 
stakeholders, consumers and companies, associate and report primarily positive aspects in 
regard to BBP. Second, the reasoning for these perceptions are contradicting in important 
elements. On the one hand, consumers have high expectations towards the products’ origin of 
raw materials and specific ideas for their disposal. In contrast, these aspects are not frequently 
included by companies when communicating their efforts in their sustainability reports and 
websites. Closing the discrepancy between the perspectives of both stakeholders needs to be 
addressed from two perspectives. First, that consumers must be aware of advantages and 
disadvantages regarding BBP. Second, companies need to formulate standardisation criteria in 
collaboration with policy makers to form a basis for a future development of the technology.

Introduction 
A recent communication of the European Commission (EC) (European Commission, 2018a) 
about a transnational strategy for the future of plastics in a circular economy (CE) highlights 
that there is a need for a change towards the so-called bio-economy (BE). This means that value 
is created by using raw materials which are obtained from a renewable source and transformed 
in biological processes instead of originating from a fossil source (Sleenhoff, 2016). The 
Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) defines the bio-economy 
as: 
“a set of economic activities related to the invention, development, and use of biological 
products and processes…. (They are) expected to improve health outcomes, boost the 
productivity of agriculture and industrial processes and enhance environmental sustainability” 
(OECD, 2009, p. 326). 
The definition above shows that policy makers draw different benefits from a transition towards 
a BE and different types of materials are involved. On the one hand it is seen as a driver for 
new jobs in regional and rural areas (European Commission, 2010) and therefore opens 
opportunities to mitigate migration movements. On the other hand, great hopes are associated 
that the BE contributes to the achievement of global goals when facing climate change 
(Spierling et al., 2018). However, potential shortcomings are mostly neglected, such as possible 
trade-offs between environmental impacts related to increased use of bio-based resources.  
Furthermore, the EC (2018b) has recently highlighted ecological problems resulting from the 
production, consumption and disposal of plastics. To address these, a special focus is set on 
BBP (also referred to as bioplastics, eco-plastics and green plastics) and the development and 
application of corresponding technologies as an important building block of the BE (Spierling 
et al., 2018). As a first response to this new policy in Europe, the French government has issued 
that all plastics shopping bags will be banned from supermarkets by 2020, and all disposable 
tableware and cutlery made of plastics will share the same destiny. An exception will be made 
for products which raw materials originate from a biologically or renewable source (FRANCE 
24 with AFP, 2016). In May 2018 the EC has followed on this track publishing a proposal to 
reduce the consumption of plastics by banning single-use plastic products in the whole 
European Union (EU) (European Commission, 2018c). The main motivation is to decrease the 
amount of plastics in the earth’s oceans. 
Although the technology of BBP has already existed for many years and policy-makers have 
put a lot of effort in its adoption the market share has been marginal (Soetaert & Vandamme, 
2006). In 2017, BBP accounted for around 2% of the annual plastic consumption (Nova Institue, 
2018). Nevertheless, the consumption and production are expected to increase drastically. The 
Nova Institute (2018) projects that the global capacity for bio-based building blocks and 
polymers will rise from around 2.5 Million tonnes in 2011 to around 5 Million tonnes in 2022. 
However, legislation is not the only important stakeholder in a BE. On the one hand, companies, 
as the key drivers for innovation in our society, are crucial for the development of new 
technology (Pitelis & Teece, 2009). To our knowledge the perspective and efforts of this actor 
remains to be explored. On the other hand, consumers play an important role in adopting 
changes and support the shifting of the economy towards a more sustainable future (European 
Commission, 2015).  
==PAGE:10==
2 
In this regard, different aspects have been identified on why this adoption is still limping. 
Scherer, Emberger-Klein, & Menrad (2017) have highlighted the lack of precise certification 
requirements and standardisation. Karana (2012) has mentioned that corporations which are 
involved in the production and application of BBP have failed to separate innovation efforts 
from their fossil-based counterparts. However, the consumers have been identified as the most 
important hurdle preventing the adoption of new innovations in the course of the BE. For a 
transformation many publications have highlighted the importance of public acceptance 
(Brockhaus, Petersen, & Kersten, 2016; Karana, 2012; Paula & Birrer, 2006; Sleenhoff, 2016) 
and its necessity to change policies for a favourable development (Lynch, Klaassen, & Broerse, 
2017). Also, a regulated market environment might ensure a stable and established market 
demand for BBP (Scherer et al., 2017; Singh, Ekanem, Wakefield, & Comer, 2003). 
However, in case of the consumer, up to date little research exists on how they perceive this 
type of products, especially in terms of characteristics and environmental benefits (Lynch et al., 
2017; Petersen & Brockhaus, 2017). 
 
1.1 Aim of the thesis 
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the topic of BBP in relation with two key stakeholders, 
namely consumers and companies. It intends to contribute to research by: (i) investigating the 
consumers’ perception of BBP, (ii) identifying and analysing publicly available information 
from business players which are involved in the BBP industry, and (iii) comparing the 
consumers’ perception with the companies’ narrative. The focus is set on the general perception 
of BBP and the perception of specific products made from BBP with focus on their 
environmental sustainability 
 
1.2 Structure of the report 
First, an overview of technological characteristics of BBP in distinction to conventional plastics 
is given. Section 3 describes how the literature review was conducted and presents the results 
in three areas, consumers’ perception of BBP, companies’ drivers and reported environmental 
benefits in regard to BBP and sustainability reporting. In section 4 the implications for this 
thesis including research gap and research questions are drawn. This is followed by the results 
of the consumers’ perception and the results of the analysis of the companies’ narrative for 
BBP. The thesis concludes with the discussion, conclusion and practical implications.

Bio-based plastics 
2.1 Background and definition 
Although BBP have a history of about 150 years, the term is still a mystery to many consumers. 
Furthermore, the application of different terminologies and definitions in the economy and 
legislation can be confusing to a wide range of stakeholders. Whereas, Pilla (2011) uses the 
term BBP and bioplastics as congruent, bioplastics may also cover other polymers. These may 
or may not be based on renewable materials to some extent and may or may not be 
biodegradable.  The term “renewable” is characterised by a feedstock originating from 
carbohydrate-rich plants with an annual crop.  For the characterisation as “biodegradable” no 
corresponding standards yet exist. In fact, materials can differ significantly in the time needed 
to degrade, the level of achieved biodegradation, and the required conditions necessary 
(European Bioplastics, 2018).  
In contrast, the term “bio-based plastics” clearly indicates that the raw materials for this type 
originates from a renewable source (see Figure 2-1). Similar characteristics account for 
biodegradable plastics, which can be derived from a renewable source to some extent or can be 
fully based on petroleum or gas (European Bioplastics, 2018; Shen, Haufe, & Patel, 2009; The 
Plastic Industry Trade Association, 2016) 
 
 
Figure 2-1: Categorisation of bioplastics, conventional and bio-based plastics (adapted from European Bioplastics, 2018) 
 
In the course of this thesis, focus is set on polymers, which originate from a renewable source. 
For this reason, the terminology of “bio-based plastics” is used for the following considerations. 
Since its discovery and an early application, BBP have always lacked in significance due to the 
availability of its petroleum-based counterparts. These have much lower costs in production 
and have had superior characteristics at that time. However, since the 1990s the technology has 
experienced a renaissance. The main drivers have been the increasing prices for crude oil and 
an increasing problem in regard to solid waste treatment. The amount of plastic waste 
contributed that almost the capacity of landfills in industrialised countries is exceeded  (Shen 
et al., 2009). However, large scale investments into the development of the technology have 
only occurred in the 21st century. This major change has been triggered by the envision of 
policy makers of a transition towards a BE. Dwindling oil supply and a growing concern of 
==PAGE:12==
4 
consumers together with an increasing demand for environmentally friendly products promise 
a significant market share for BBP in the future (Mülhaupt, 2013). 
Conventional petroleum-based plastics are based on hydrocarbons which are locked up in the 
earth for millions and millions of years. In contrast, in the industry three principal processes 
exist to produce BBP (see Figure 2-2). 
Regarding the origin of raw materials, it is differentiated between first, second and third 
generation sources. First-generation BBP are based on traditional agricultural products. These 
are derived from plants as a renewable source. These are mostly sugar cane, corn or soybeans. 
Second and third generation BBP are characterised by the utilisation of renewable non-food 
sources (e.g. switch grass, castor beans) or the utilisation of by-products resulting from the 
industrial processing of first-generation sources (e.g. husks, peels,) (The Plastic Industry Trade 
Association, 2016). 
 
 
Figure 2-2: Production processes of bio-based plastics (Jiang & Zhang, 2012; Shen et al., 2009)

which are used for the production of BBP and other materials and their characteristics see Table

2.2 Technological, functional and environmental characteristics 
Regarding the declaration of technological characteristics, the reality for BBP is blurry. The 
development of complete standardisation by governmental bodies has not been completed yet. 
However, several different eco-labels exist on the market, mostly awarded by non-
governmental organisations (NGOs). BBP which are described as biodegradable may or may 
not have similar requirements for a biological and enzymatic processing. For example, 
differentiates the OK Compost label between compostable (in industrial facility), home 
compostable (Vinçotte, 2018b), marine biodegradable, soil biodegradable and water 
biodegradable (Vinçotte, 2018a). On behalf of standards organisations, different specifications 
and norms exist. The ASTM D6400 covers “plastics and products made from plastics that are 
designed to be composted in municipal and industrial aerobic composting facilities” (D20 
Committee, 2012). The ASTM D6868 covers “materials and products (including packaging), 
wherein a biodegradable plastic film or coating is attached” and can be composted in the same 
way as products according to ASTDM D6400 (D20 Committee, 2017). The European Standard 
EN13432 regulates the biodegradability and decomposability of packaging under certain 
industrial conditions (Danish standards, 2006).  
In addition, in literature different opinions about functional properties exist (Hottle et al., 2013). 
Whereas Pilla (2011, p. 2) characterises BBP as “inferior (…) compared to their synthetic 
                                                 
1 Under certain conditions 
==PAGE:14==
6 
counterparts”, Hottle et al. (2013) highlight the potential of biopolymers to replace conventional 
plastics in almost every function.  
Same considerations account for the environmental performance. In literature many 
publications can be found giving contradicting results. Pilla (2011) describes BBP as 
sustainable and CO2 neutral with the potential to reduce the dependency of society on fossil 
sources. In contrast, Hottle et al. (2013, p. 1898) states that BBP “exhibit similar impacts 
compared to petroleum-based plastics”. Providing a different approach, Spierling et al. (2018) 
have analysed the available data of Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) studies to quantify possible 
environmental benefits on a global scale. For their analysis, they assumed that two-third of the 
world’s plastics consumption is substituted with a bio-based alternative. Calculations have 
primarily been conducted for the environmental impact category “global warming potential” 
(GWP). The results show that potential savings are around 241 to 316 Mio. T of CO2-eq. 
annually. However, due to lack of data from literature, assumptions about other environmental 
impact categories than CO2 emission, are hard to make.  
The considerations above show that no uniform and widely accepted framework for BBP exists. 
Not even a threshold that allow one to call a BBP, a BBP in regard to the amount of bio-based 
composition exists. Furthermore, in literature no consensus has been reached on the overall 
benefits and many aspects regarding the environmental performance have only been 
sporadically addressed in research.

3.2.1 Consumer perception of bio-based plastics 
Sijtsema et al. (2015) have conducted an exploratory study in 5 European Countries on how the 
term “bio-based” is perceived in general and how consumers have assessed the environmental 
properties of seven hypothetical bio-based products. It has shown that either people have rarely 
heard or are not familiar with the term and what properties corresponding products possess. 
These findings have been confirmed by other studies (Dupont-Inglis & Borg, 2018; Lynch et 
al., 2017; Sleenhoff, 2016; Walter, 2011). Although the perception of specific products made 
of bio-based materials have been overall positive in relation to the environmental sustainability 
there was still “much confusing regarding the term” and even negative and mixed associations 
for several characteristics (Sijtsema et al., 2015, p. 64). In this regard different types of 
questions have been raised by participants during the survey: What does biodegradable mean 
and how long does it take to degrade? Are the materials obtained from waste? Is there a food 
vs. material conflict? Are the materials recyclable? Are ecological systems endangered because 
of improper land use? Similar concerns have been identified by other studies in comparable 
investigations (Blesin, Jaspersen, & Möhring, 2017; Koenig-Lewis, Palmer, Dermody, & 
Urbye, 2014). 
In a different study Lynch et al. (2017) have investigated the perception of bioplastics, bio-
jetfuels and small-scale bio-refineries in the Netherlands. The findings show, that Dutch 
consumers associate these products with other words containing the term “bio” and 
environmental positive terms like biodegradable, biotechnology, organic, environmentally 
friendly, sustainable, etc. However, also negative concepts have been stated, like genetic 
modification, higher prices and improper land use. In a similar study Petersen & Brockhaus 
(2017) have observed the consumer behaviour and consumers’ reactions towards alternatives 
of headphones and garbage bags either made of petroleum-based, bio-based or recycled plastics 
and which have been presented in different design options in regard to their colours. Results 
show that for both products the bio-based choices are perceived as “environmentally superior” 
(Petersen & Brockhaus, 2017, p. 350). Moreover, the application of green colour for the design 
is associated with an environmentally more sustainable performance (Petersen & Brockhaus, 
2017). These findings are aligned with the results which have been obtained in Germany 
regarding the environmental sustainability of products made of bioplastics (Blesin et al., 2017) 
and sand toys made of altering proportions of bio-based materials (Scherer et al., 2017). 
In addition to these studies the sustainability perception of different packaging materials has 
been the subject to investigations in Denmark, Germany, and The Netherlands (Boesen, Niero, 
& Bey, 2018; Lindh, Olsson, & Williams, 2016; Steenis, van Herpen, van der Lans, Ligthart, 
& van Trijp, 2017). Orset, Barret, & Lemaire (2017) have captured consumers’ evaluation of 
different plastic bottles on the French market. Findings show that associations about 
environmental sustainability are primarily based on the material (Boesen et al., 2018; Lindh et 
al., 2016; Steenis et al., 2017) and what consumers can personally do at the disposal stage 
(Boesen et al., 2018; Herbes, Beuthner, & Ramme, 2018). Whereas petroleum-based plastics 
are seen as having the highest negative environmental impact, the bio-based and biodegradable 
counterparts are perceived as the most sustainable among many alternatives. In contrast, Herbes 
et al. (2018) have found out that recyclable or reusable choices are seen more environmentally 
friendly than biodegradable options. However, the specific materials of the recyclable and 
reusable options have not been explained in detail. This makes this study not easily comparable 
to other research in this area. Nevertheless, all these findings show that the consumers’ 
==PAGE:18==
10 
perceptions are not aligned with scientific results obtained from the LCA methodology (Steenis 
et al., 2017). 
Apart from the aforementioned articles in recent years many publications have appeared dealing 
with challenges and opportunities of the BE. In the early 2000s Singh et al. (2003) have focused 
their research on implications for university students and future product developers which 
would be the main drivers for an upcoming BE. And although there has been a broad consensus 
about the rapid success of this industry little knowledge of consumers hampered the growth 
(Soetaert & Vandamme, 2006). Recently, Brockhaus et al. (2016) have shown that, apart from 
scientists (Hottle et al., 2013), product developers question the superior environmental 
performance of bio-based products and are even afraid to apply them because of examples of 
Greenwashing accusations in recent years. For this reason, several studies have been concerned 
with the question of how to change consumer behaviour and perception in this respect.  
As a key task to increase the market share of BBP in the EU, the EC highlights the importance 
of the collaboration of different stakeholders and sufficient product information for the public 
(European Commission, 2018a). In general, consumers associate positive emotions with BBP 
because it makes them feel good to contribute to a more sustainable society (Jaca, Prieto-
Sandoval, Psomas, & Ormazabal, 2018; Koenig-Lewis et al., 2014; Lynch et al., 2017). 
However, participants of consumer surveys have stated that they would like to receive more 
detailed and specific product information about the background of these materials (Blesin et al., 
2017). These results are supported by findings of several other studies (Dupont-Inglis & Borg, 
2018; Sijtsema et al., 2015; van de Velde, Verbeke, Popp, & van Huylenbroeck, 2011; Walter, 
2011). Most important in this regard is the identification of knowledge gaps and taking concerns 
and suggestions of consumers into account (Dupont-Inglis & Borg, 2018; O’Rourke & Ringer, 
2016; Paula & Birrer, 2006; Sleenhoff, 2016). In this context, clearly the interaction of policy 
makers, the academic sector and companies is required. The communication of information 
needs to be compulsory, transparent, concrete and backed up with scientific research from a 
neutral source (Lopolito, Prosperi, Sisto, & De Meo, 2015; Lynch et al., 2017; Sijtsema et al., 
2015; van de Velde et al., 2011). 
As described above, literature has identified the consumer perception as an important factor for 
the development of the BE. However, different stakeholders have a significant influence on 
shaping this, namely legislation and companies. Private businesses are the key drivers of 
innovation in our economic system (Pitelis & Teece, 2009) and primarily responsible for the 
adoption and development of new technology. In collaboration with policy makers product 
specific standardisation criteria could create public credibility and have impact on basing 
consumer perception on recent scientific advances about impact and benefits of BBP (Priefer, 
Jörissen, & Frör, 2017). 
 
3.2.2 Sustainability strategies in bio-based plastics industry 
In the following the results of the analysis of literature regarding sustainability strategies and 
the BE is presented. Above all, companies which are active in the bio-based industry and which 
create disruptive innovations as alternatives to different conventional products find enormous 
growth opportunities (Aquilani, Silvestri, Ioppolo, & Ruggieri, 2018). However, the launch of 
several solutions has unveiled powerful obstacles for the maturation process of the industry. 
Disruptive technologies are frequently faced with scepticism and critic from the public because 
of the unknown impact (McDonald & Rolland, 2006). This can be mainly explained by the little 
==PAGE:19==
Perception of bio-based plastics 
11 
knowledge of consumers about origin, characteristics and adequate consumption guidance. This 
missing information from converters and legislation has created an uncertain market 
environment for the BE.  
In this regard, Iles & Martin (2013) have investigated how three big players in the plastic 
industry, DuPont, BASF and Braskem, integrated bio-based polymers into their product 
portfolio. Although BBP offer a great market potential due to their link to consumers’ 
expectations for a more sustainable way of packaging, they have failed in creating additional 
value yet. So far, they are only serving an environmental conscious niche market. DuPont’s 
initial motivation for developing BBP was a cost advantage because of ever increasing crude 
oil prices. However, at a later stage they saw the marketing opportunity as a “green” alternative 
to conventional plastics. In contrast, BASF entered the market because they identified a threat 
of competitors and changes in policies. Consequently, they developed a biodegradable polymer 
to initially serve a premium niche market. Braskem started research in this area as a strategic 
decision to exploit a future market opportunity.  
Since these three large organisations have integrated bio-based alternatives into their product 
portfolio other benefits and drivers to further invest in the new technology became important. 
Unfolding the new technological possibilities, the uncertainty of future changes in 
governmental regulations (e.g. regional GHG emission goals, reduction of negative 
environmental impact) forces managers to further pursue innovation in this area. Furthermore, 
fluctuating oil prices and the finite nature of resources have created a risk for the supply of raw 
materials. By integrating renewable material into production large companies have the 
opportunity to control their entire supply chain and meet environmental performance 
expectations of downstream converters. This was very difficult with the powerful oil industry. 
Although many things are technologically possible, the market has been stagnating. Yet, 
companies have missed the chance to develop disruptive business models for disruptive 
products in the BE by integrating different stakeholders in the development process (Iles & 
Martin, 2013). 
Focusing on small- and medium-sized enterprises in the rubber and paper industry, Bos-
Brouwers (2010) has investigated sustainability strategies in the course of innovation activities. 
She has identified the risk of resource scarcity as the main driver for companies to find 
alternative feedstock for their raw materials. Oil derivatives are still very important in the 
industry and in order to reduce costs and be competitive in the future switching to renewable 
resources is identified the only alternative in the long-term. Furthermore, new materials and 
new technologies open opportunities for innovative design and application options which can 
create a competitive advantage. Although the significance for a sustainable contribution of the 
economy to ecosystems is highlighted, the article points out that impacts on biodiversity are 
mainly overlooked by decision-makers yet. This lack of consciousness about consequences of 
an increasing agricultural exploitation has to be integrated into future research (Bos-Brouwers, 
2010). 
Another recent article considers the development of a sustainable supply chain in the course of 
the innovation of bio-PE (de Vargas Mores, Finocchio, Barichello, & Pedrozo, 2018). 
Therefore, the value chain of a Brazilian petrochemical company from the cultivation of raw 
materials (i.e. sugarcane) until the manufacturing in third-generation organisations has been 
investigated. The drivers to invest in bio-PE were a growing global demand for products from 
renewable sources, an ongoing debate on climate change and corresponding GHG emissions, 
==PAGE:20==
12 
strict governmental regulations and an intense global competition. The Brazilian market 
environment is identified favourable because of the availability of sufficient raw material and 
its present uneconomic use (sugarcane is primarily burned). Furthermore, the company has 
favoured the development of bio-PE due to the similar technological characteristics and features 
compared to conventional PE. No new distribution channel needs to be built up. Customers can 
substitute the conventional PE in their production processes. The short-term benefit for the 
petrochemical companies is the added value. The use of renewable resources increases its own 
brand value and industry-wide image. For their customers new marketing possibilities are 
opened. Nevertheless, the organisation faced challenges along the way. Missing policies forces 
companies to put much effort in reporting activities for bio-based products. In order to convince 
customers of the added value, third-party certification about the truly environmental advantages 
must be obtained. Therefore, the whole value chain including favourable logistic methods has 
to be considered to create a real sustainable product which attracts a sufficient number of 
customers and a public acceptance (de Vargas Mores et al., 2018). 
 
3.2.3 Sustainability reporting 
An important part of this investigation is the analysis of sustainability related reporting of 
companies which are active in the BBP industry. These represent a source of information and 
give the opportunity to evaluate corporate sustainability strategies. Therefore, the following 
section gives an overview of academic literature related to CSR-practices in general and what 
must be considered in the course of their analysis. 
To this date, important tools for corporations to align efforts and challenges related to BBP are 
their own CSR activities and their websites. By these kinds of communication, organisations 
legitimate their business making in regard to social and environmental impacts, increase their 
brand value, create a competitive advantage, apply benchmarking against other market players, 
increase transparency and motivate employees internally (Herzig & Schaltegger, 2006).  
Lozano, Nummert, & Ceulemans, (2016) state that reporting triggers a more sustainable 
business model inside of an organisation. It supports to create accountability in its own 
workforce and highlight the progress which is important to keep exploiting business 
opportunities (Iles & Martin, 2013). Presenting its own efforts to a broad audience of 
stakeholders becomes important during the maturity of sustainability efforts (Bartels, Iansen-
Rogers, & Kuszewski, 2008). However, at this stage monitoring of implemented operations and 
aligning suppliers’ activities helps creating an overall superior environmental performance over 
the whole value chain (Stubbs & Cocklin, 2008). This is of great importance regarding large 
organisations which are shifting towards a sustainable business model in course of changing 
management efforts. For their own benefit Lozano et al. (2016, p. 179) highlight the following 
main objectives of reporting activities: “(1) transparency of sustainability performance; (2) 
assessing sustainability; (3) promoting sustainability efforts; (4) fostering stakeholder dialogue; 
(5) improving sustainability reputation; and (6) fostering change”  
Although sustainability reporting can be important for stakeholders in the course of the 
transition towards a BE criticism occurs in academic literature. So far, differences in legislation 
their impact on society and the environment exists. Multinational companies around the world 
can join the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI), which are widely acknowledged guidelines for 
CSR reporting, on a pure voluntary nature. However, several countries in Europe and the 
European Parliament introduced regulatory initiatives about how sustainability reporting must 
==PAGE:21==
Perception of bio-based plastics 
13 
be carried out (Fombrun, 2005). Nevertheless, companies are mostly free to set their focus on 
what to publish and which aspects of business to highlight (S. Chen & Bouvain, 2009; 
Kleinschmit et al., 2014). Or as it is stated by Milne & Gray (2013, p. 17) and Searcy & 
Buslovich (2014, p. 167) respectively, reports contain “cherry pick elements of news” and 
reporting is the challenge “between sharing good and bad news”. In some cases, this leads to 
huge gaps in reporting practices because different stakeholders are tried to addressed (S. Chen 
& Bouvain, 2009). Another point of criticism is related to the widespread approach of the triple 
bottom line. By applying this methodology in sustainability reports corporations balance efforts 
towards a more sustainable future between social, environmental and economic aspects. The 
trade-off between these dimensions can lead to compromising with one of them (Stubbs & 
Cocklin, 2008). In our economic system the priority is often laid on economic concerns and not 
on the environment neither the society (Milne & Gray, 2013).  
In order to evaluate the importance of sustainability reporting in the BE not only the benefits 
for the authoring company need to be regarded but also which stakeholders are targeted by 
them. In general these are competitors, policy makers, consultancies, NGOs, labour 
organisations and the academic world (Bartels et al., 2008). For the development of a BE 
especially market competitors, policy makers, NGOs and the academic world are of great 
importance due to the lack of standardisation in the industry. Furthermore, it is important to 
integrate different perspectives to balance political, environmental and economic trade-offs in 
the course of further innovation (Kleinschmit et al., 2014; Searcy & Buslovich, 2014). This also 
includes the society which creates market opportunities in the first place. Without the 
engagement of the public into shaping a BE it is difficult for companies to communicate an 
added value and they might lose an already weak credibility of bio-based products by 
consumers (Hahn & Kühnen, 2013; Iles & Martin, 2013). Therefore, addressing the doubts of 
the public about benefits during the development of BBP needs to be subject in their reporting 
activities. These are primarily the usage of residuals and waste instead of creating a food vs. 
material conflict (Herbes et al., 2018; Meyer, 2017) and an ever-increasing exploitation of land 
which could lead to a loss in biodiversity (Bennich & Belyazid, 2017).  
In order to integrate the consumers’ opinion into the development of a BE the collaboration of 
companies with policy makers and NGOs is needed to regulate the rhetoric and content in 
sustainability reports (Burritt & Schaltegger, 2010). This is of great importance because they 
build the starting point for a profound discussion within the society (Deri, 2003), taking into 
account the significance of corporate sustainability reports for shaping the opinion of 
stakeholders (Bartels et al., 2008).

Implications for this thesis 
4.1 Research gap 
The EC is pursuing different strategies to change towards a sustainable economy. As the action 
plan for a CE (European Commission, 2015) and the European strategy for plastics (European 
Commission, 2018a) show, the BE and BBP in particular are seen as an important element in 
this regard. A central problem legislation and companies have identified is the insufficient 
knowledge of society about potential environmental benefits and behavioural implications for 
disposal. Consumers and their consumption patterns play an important role for shifting the 
economy towards a more sustainable future (European Commission, 2015); yet they have not 
adopted to a change towards a BE. The reasons for this are multi-layered and complex. 
However, more information and better communication is needed from different stakeholders 
(Nita & Castellani, 2017) which address knowledge gaps and doubts consumers have about 
BBP. In this way, views, motivation and expectations of legislation, private companies and 
society can be aligned. Based on these considerations, Figure 4.4-1 shows how an interplay of 
these expectations and actions could look like to decrease the mismatch of information 
regarding the BE. 
In order to better understand the consumers’ perspective, research has focused on exploratory 
and qualitative studies about their perception of hypothetical bio-based products or the 
comparative analysis of different types of packaging, including BBP. Moreover, many studies 
exist about corporate sustainability strategies and corresponding sustainability reporting, 
however the practices specifically in a BE are, to our knowledge yet missed out. So far, 
scientists have focused about the role and sustainability strategies of policy-makers on a 
national and European level. This has been reasonable due to the still young history of the BE; 
however, it is not sufficient if one considers which expectations politics has recently put into 
the technology. 
 
 
Figure 4-1: Interplay between consumers, companies and policy makers 
 
This study contributes to academic research by approaching the understanding of BBP from 
two angles. First, an investigation of how consumers perceive the environmental performance 
==PAGE:23==
Perception of bio-based plastics

of BBP is conducted. In particular, it is tried to capture the reasoning they have for this 
perception. A special focus is set on origin of raw materials and the disposal of products. These 
stages have been identified as most important for consumers when evaluating the environmental 
sustainability (Herbes et al., 2018). Furthermore, results are obtained and compared for two 
completely different cultural backgrounds: Denmark as a developed country and Colombia as 
a developing country. Research has shown that significant discrepancies in regard to sustainable 
behaviour across nations exist (Sudbury-riley, Hofmeister-toth, & Kohlbacher, 2014). 
However, the perception of developing countries is not well represented in academic literature 
yet, although their consumption of plastics and correspondingly the generation of plastic waste 
is expected to grow tremendously in the next years. 
In a second part the reported drivers and benefits in regard to sustainability performance of BBP 
are investigated from the perspective of companies. So far, research has mainly focused on the 
strategies of policy makers to make the BE attractive for investments. However, the analysis of 
companies’ drivers and which environmental benefits they draw from this development has 
been neglected. Special focus is set on where companies are located along the value chain; 
producers of BBP or converters. 
This study responds to a demand in academic literature by: (i) providing research about existing 
products made of BBP (Sijtsema et al., 2015), (ii) conducting quantitative follow up studies to 
confirm findings of exploratory qualitative investigations and (iii) testing these findings against 
the background of different cultures (Boesen et al., 2018; Brockhaus et al., 2016; Herbes et al., 
2018; Koenig-Lewis et al., 2014; Sijtsema et al., 2015).

5.1 Consumer perception of bio-based plastics 
In recent years several scientific articles have been published investigating the perception of 
bio-based products using explorative and qualitative approaches. These have been conducted 
for different target groups in primarily Western industrial nation. For this thesis a self-

different dependent and independent variables can be highlighted. Furthermore, the results of 
these quantitative research can be easily compared to other studies in future investigations

in two countries: Denmark and Colombia.

institutions. Furthermore, the survey was presented on campus and in-class at the Technical 
University of Denmark (Copenhagen, Denmark) and University EAFIT (Medellín, Colombia).

5.2.2 Data analysis 
For the analysis of company reports and websites a content analysis was undertaken, following 
the approach of Hrasky (2011). Very important in this regard is the coding process (Hsieh & 
Shannon, 2005). First, all descriptions from both sources in which organisations were

==PAGE:32==

mentioned the production and/or application of BBP were collected in a systematic manner. 
These extracts were then translated into content categories.  
On the basis of this procedure a descriptive analysis was undertaken, and results are presented 
in chapter 7. In order to identify patterns and correlations between the different categories, 
statistically significant differences between the categories are highlighted applying Pearson’s 
Χ2-test (Gilg et al., 2005). This method has been found robust for 2 x 2 contingency tables5 as 
they were almost exclusively present in this study (Camilli & Hopkins, 1978). The drivers, the 
environmental benefits, technological characteristics and availability of LCA results are tested 
against the control variables: (i) producers or converters, (ii) geographical origin and (iii) 
industry. After the results of the Χ2-test were obtained the combinations which violate the 
assumptions were neglected. These assumptions are that at least 80% of the cells should have 
an expected value of higher or equal to “5” and no cell should have an expected value of lower 
than “1” (McHugh, 2013). Since most of the tables in the investigation in this thesis only have 
four cells the first assumption is lowered to 75%.

In the same section as the technological characteristics participants were asked to evaluate BBP 
against conventional plastics in different stages of their product lifecycle, ranging from raw 
materials over production to disposal. The mean values of their answers on the 7-point Likert 
scale are shown in Figure 6-5. Accordingly, BBP are perceived as environmentally superior in 
all these categories compared to conventional plastics, except the impact on biodiversity. 
Especially significant this can be seen in the overall environmental impact (5.71) the production 
(5.63) and the disposal stage (5.71). Regarding water consumption, participants in Denmark 
marginally favour conventional, whereas people in Colombia identify BBP as superior. In the 
category of impact on biodiversity, in both countries conventional plastics are seen as less 
hazardous (3.73). In the question of competition with food supply, people are more or less 
undecided (3.99), which indicates a general consciousness for the topic. 
Asked about their perception of personal benefits by using BBP, participants from both 
countries have similar preferences. As shown in Figure 6-8 more than 78% feel that they 
personally contribute to a more sustainable society, followed by the reduction of their personal 
environmental footprint. Whereas, the majority of people in Colombia feel good when buying 
products made from BBP, participants in Denmark are more conservative (37.6%). Regarding 
overall benefits for the society, the pattern is slightly different in the two countries. For 
participants from Colombia, BBP contribute to a more sustainable economy (74.3%). For 
people from Denmark most important in relation to BBP is reducing the dependency of our 
economy on petroleum (69.6%). Also significant is that participants from Colombia see a bigger 
potential to help the economy through investments (50.5%) and a stronger focus on the 
agricultural sector (43,7%) and rural areas (44,7%). This might be due to the stronger 
dependency of the Colombian economy on this type of industry.

6.2.1 Research question 1: To what extent do consumers in Denmark and Colombia 
respectively perceive BBP as environmentally preferable? 
In order to answer RQ1 specifically questions about sustainability, environmental impact and 
production of BBP are important (see Figure 5-2). Accordingly, two hypotheses were 
formulated. First, hypothesis 1a is: Consumers have a positive attitude towards the 
environmental sustainability of BBP.  
The results presented in Figure 6-5 indicate that consumers in general perceive BBP as 
environmentally friendly. Earlier studies have shown that this favourable environmental 
opinion is highly correlated with the disposal stage due to the personal involvement of 
consumers (Boesen et al., 2018; Herbes et al., 2018). The analysis of this questionnaire supports 
these findings strongly (F=53.93, p=1.7594E-12). But also, environmental impact and the 
production are perceived as lower and more environmentally friendly respectively compared to 
conventional plastics. Nevertheless, concerns are raised in regard to the use of agricultural land. 
Participants of the consumer survey have a neutral opinion or partly agree with the statement 
that BBP have higher impact on biodiversity than conventional plastics.  
Apart from these arguments, consumers have a personal motivation to buy this type of products. 
Almost 80% and 70% of the participants respectively have reported that with purchasing BBP 
==PAGE:47==
Perception of bio-based plastics 
39 
they contribute to a sustainable society and make the economy more environmentally friendly. 
Also, the reduction of their environmental footprint and becoming independent from petroleum 
seems to be important factors. All in all, hypothesis 1a is accepted due to the overall positive 
environmental performance perception. Interesting in this regard is that consumers have a 
positive image of BBP although only 37.5% have actually bought a product which has been 
made of or which has been packaged in BBP.  
Second, RQ1 targets to reveal a difference in the perception about BBP between consumers in 
an industrialised country like Denmark and students in a developing country such as Colombia. 
Correspondingly, hypothesis 1b is: There is a cultural difference in regard to the environmental 
perception of BBP between Danish and Colombian residents. To answer this RQ especially the 
findings shown in Figure 6-5 and Table 6-7 are of interest. On the one hand, the column diagram 
visualises the discrepancies in the perception of both countries. On the other hand, the statistical 
analysis in the present study reveals that this difference is significant in the categories for 
general sustainability, lower environmental impact, production and disposal. Accordingly, 
Colombian residents have a significantly more positive perception in all of these categories. In 
the case of the impact on biodiversity, no differences are measured between the two countries 
(see Table 6-7). However, the results clearly support the aforementioned hypothesis and thus, 
it is not rejected. 
 
6.2.2 Research question 2: What is the reasoning for consumers to assess the 
environmental performance of bio-based plastics? 
RQ 2 deals with the reasoning for consumers in shaping their perception of BBP. As shown in 
section 6.1, residents of Denmark and Colombia have a general positive image. Hypothesis 2 
is: Consumers base their reasoning about the environmental sustainability performance of BBP 
on their perception of advantages of origin of raw materials and disposal stage. 
The comparison of answers in the different categories (see Table 6-7) show that consumers see 
the more environmentally friendly disposal compared to conventional plastics, followed by a 
more environmentally friendly production as most benefiting of BBP. This includes the origin 
as well as usage of raw materials. Emissions of CO2, water consumption and energy use are 
also perceived as favourable, but to a lower extend than the two other categories. Interesting is 
that in all these aspects statistical significant differences regarding country of residence exist. 
However, water consumption is the only case in the entire questionnaire in which there is a 
contrary opinion between cultures. Whereas, in all other categories Colombian residents have 
a stronger positive perception, in Denmark water consumption during the production of BBP is 
perceived as disadvantageous compared to conventional plastics.  
As described above the disposal and raw materials are seen as most beneficial for consumers to 
have a favourable opinion about BBP in comparison with conventional plastics. However, to 
answer RQ2 the correlation between the general perception regarding sustainability 
performance and the different criteria with the overall impression are consulted (see Table

p=4.1E-13 & r=0.55, p=3.1E-27) and production (r=0.52, p=1.1E-24 & r=0.60, p=1.1E-33) 
have the highest correlation. Thus, hypothesis 2 is not rejected.

6.2.3 Research question 3: What conclusions regarding origin and disposal of BBP 
do consumers draw from their perception (including product descriptions)? 
RQ 3 was designed to address what behavioural implications consumers have in regard to 
products made of BBP. Therefore, hypothesis 3 is: Consumers know little about content of BBP 
and corresponding behaviour implications for their correct disposal. 
The perception about characteristics and assumed way of disposal clearly highlights why 
consumers have a positive opinion about BBP and particularly for these categories. More than 
two third of the consumers think that raw materials are obtained from a renewable source (see 
Figure 6-2). For 57% these sources are plant-based, and almost half of the participants think 
that these are originating from residuals or are waste biomass of other industrial processes, thus 
are second or third generation BBP. Even one third of the consumers have special expectations 
toward the cultivation of raw materials, namely being conducted in a sustainable manner. 
Specifically, this becomes evident with the question in relation to the content of the IKEA 
ISTAD freezer bag. Although the product description clearly indicates that the bag is made of 
bio-PE and contains 85% bio-based materials, more than 20% think that the plant-based 
proportion is higher than 85% and only 37% think that the product contains conventional PE 
(see Figure 6-7).  
Regarding the disposal of products, the unawareness and misperception is reflected in the broad 
distribution of chosen waste options (see Figure 6-6). The participants of the survey have not 
only disagreed on how to handle these products after usage, but also the number of multiple 
answers is high. Almost one out of three chose more than one answer as the correct disposal 
option. For each product the producers were contacted in order to identify the correct choice. 
For the IKEA freezer bag, no answer has been received. However, as it is a drop-in BBP the 
disposal with other recyclable plastic waste seems most adequate. Though, 56% chose the 
correct option leaving almost half of the respondents which would dispose it incorrectly. For 
the tableware, an answer has been received indicating that the organic waste is the “best 
channel” (R. Hogarty, personal communication, June 5, 2018). Here, only half of the 
respondents chose this option. For the BASF transport box, no answer has been received. 
==PAGE:49==

However, it is made of the same type of BBP as the BioBag tableware: PLA. Accordingly, the 
same disposal option might be correct in this case. For the box, only 36% of the respondents 
chose this. Meaning, that almost two-third of the participants would dispose the product 
incorrectly. Due to the arguments above, hypothesis 3 is not rejected.

sources and projected amount of BBP.

on the sensible topic of sourcing materials which are not in competition with food supply

are not involved in the process of sourcing raw materials. They might only have an influence

7.6 Findings to research question 4 
In this section, we contrast the results from the consumer survey and the analysis of corporate 
communications by answering RQ4: Is there a difference between the companies’ reporting of 
and the consumers’ perception of environmental benefits and characteristics of BBP? 
The consumer survey allowed unearthing the general perception of BBP and how this 
perception is shaped among consumers, and the analysis of companies which are active in BBP 
industry shed light on their motivation, communicated benefits and associated characteristics. 
The results of the separate studies indicate several similarities and differences between the 
perception of consumers and the content of corporate reporting in characteristics and 
environmental benefits in regard to BBP which are detailed hereafter. Therefore, first the 
elements of the companies’ narratives that show alignment with the results of the consumer 
survey are indicated. This is followed by pointing out the aspects which reveal a mismatch. 
 
7.6.1 Matching elements 
Consumers in Denmark and Colombia perceive BBP as an environmentally friendly alternative. 
Accordingly, environmental benefits in different categories are associated if this technology 
replaces conventional plastics in various applications in our economy (e.g. reduction in GHG 
emissions). This also holds among companies, 74% of the sample in this study report at least 
one environmental benefit in their narrative.  
In particular, the perception highlights a lower environmental impact than conventional plastics 
and are generally sustainable. For 42% of the companies the reduction of their environmental 
impact and for 60% the reduction of their carbon footprint are the most frequently reported 
benefits of applying BBP. Although consumers perceive other categories as more benefitting, 
the emission of GHGs during production is seen as lower compared to conventional plastics. 
Furthermore, energy savings during production as a benefit of applying BBP instead of 
==PAGE:60==
52 
conventional plastics are reported by 25% of the organisations. This is in accordance with 
findings from the consumer survey: A lower usage of energy is seen as the third-most important 
benefit of BBP when comparing the values of the environmental perception questions.  
As the most important driver for companies’ involvement with BBP the decreasing in oil 
dependency is identified. This is reported by more than half of the sample and represents the 
most frequent aspect. This argument is also supported by consumers. Almost two out of three 
participants of the consumer survey see the potential of BBP to help reducing the economy’s 
consumption of petroleum.  
With regard to the origin of BBP, 68% of the respondents of the consumer survey indicated that 
they were derived from plant-based raw materials which matches indications from companies 
about the BBP they produce or use (78% of the companies analysed mention that BBP they 
produce, or use are made of plan-based raw materials). However, the investigation of comparing 
the characteristics of BBP is complex though. The individual analyses of the companies’ 
narrative are for a specific type of BBP, taking into account if it is biodegradable (e.g. PLA), 
recyclable (e.g. bio-PE) or not. In contrast, the consumers’ perception is captured in general. 
The participants were asked to assess different performance categories and potential 
characteristics, not focusing on a specific type of BBP. Therefore, for other aspects a 
comparison between both perspectives is not applicable. 
 
7.6.2 Contradicting elements 
The most significant difference between the consumers’ perception and the companies’ 
narrative is the focus on end-of-life aspects and resources. A more environmentally friendly 
disposal of BBP is perceived as most benefitting for consumers. In contrast, a reduction of 
waste in general and waste on landfills is only reported by 11% of the companies in this sample. 
Similar findings can be seen in regard to production and raw materials. For consumers, this 
element is perceived as much more environmentally friendly compared to conventional plastics. 
However, only 15% of the companies integrate aspects regarding resource protection into their 
narrative. 
Furthermore, 47% of the consumers perceive that the biomass for the production of BBP is a 
residual or waste of other industrial processes meaning that they only consider second and third 
generation BBP. This is reported by only 4% (7% if including non-food crops) of the 
organisations, as in almost all cases the raw materials are agricultural products for the purpose 
of BBP’ production. Another aspect of great interest for consumers is genetic modification. 
More than 80% do not consider GM plants being used for BBP. In contrast, only 12% of the 
companies describe their product as non-GM and even one producer specifically mentions the 
GM feedstock for their BBP. 
In regard to BBP’ benefits on a social level, consumers clearly see the potential to contribute to 
a more sustainable and environment friendly economy in general. More than 70% of the 
respondents perceive that these are the most important factors to buy and support this upcoming 
technology. In contrast, only 30% of the companies reported that sustainability has motivated 
them to invest in the development of BBP.

Discussion 
8.1 Consumer perception 
This study extends the knowledge about BBP in regard to two important stakeholders, namely 
consumers and companies. Moreover, to our knowledge this research is the first comparing the 
results between a developed and a developing country and consequently has to be tested in the 
future for other cultures.  
The results of the online questionnaire show that consumers, regardless their country of 
residence, perceive BBP products as more environmentally friendly than their conventional 
counterparts. Same results are obtained by Petersen & Brockhaus (2017) and Scherer et al. 
(2017) respectively. On the one hand, they directly link US residents’ environmental perception 
of products, to the used material. They conclude that BBP products are perceived as 
environmentally superior compared to conventional plastics and products with no material 
information. On the other hand, Scherer et al. (2017) show that German parents have more 
positive associations towards products with a higher proportion of bio-based content. 
Furthermore, previous research highlights the same findings for different countries, but by 
applying qualitative methods such as focus group discussions (e.g. Lynch et al., 2017; Sijtsema 
et al., 2015). Studies about the perception of different packaging material are also in line with 
these results. Boesen et al. (2018) and Steenis et al. (2017) show that consumers in Denmark 
and the Netherlands respectively perceive food packaging (soft drinks & beer and tomato soup 
respectively) made of BBP as the most environmentally friendly option.  
In addition to the general perception, this study reveals significant differences in perception 
towards BBP between consumers in Colombia and Denmark. Although the environmental 
performance of BBP is perceived superior compared to conventional plastics across countries, 
Colombian residents have a significantly more positive attitude towards BBP in nearly all of 
the asked categories. In contrast, Sijtsema et al. (2015) have investigated the perception of BBP 
in five European countries and their results show no differences between cultural backgrounds. 
Specifically, the present study unearths the unawareness of Colombian and Danish residents in 
regard to technological characteristics of BBP. This is aligned with results from previous 
research which shows that people are not familiar with the term neither with resulting 
characteristically implications (Dupont-Inglis & Borg, 2018; Lynch et al., 2017; Sijtsema et al., 
2015; Sleenhoff, 2016; Walter, 2011). This study also highlights the positive associations and 
the good feeling of consumers when buying this type of products. The feeling of contributing 
to a more sustainable society and economy is one reasoning for consumers to favour BBP over 
other materials. These findings are supported by Koenig-Lewis et al. (2014) and Lynch et al. 
(2017) who have investigated associations with sustainable packaging and BBP in Norway and 
the Netherlands respectively.  
The question then is, why do consumers have these associations and prefer a renewable 
alternative over conventional plastics? As the results show, this is primarily due to high 
expectations in regard to the origin of raw materials (e.g. waste biomass & sustainable 
cultivation of plants) and the disposal of BBP. As Lindh et al. (2016); Steenis et al. (2017), 
Boesen et al. (2018) and Herbes et al. (2018) point out, these two aspects are most important 
for shaping the consumers’ opinion in regard to the environmental performance of products.  
In contrast to the mostly positive perceptions this study also reveals ambivalent attitudes on the 
participants’ side. 44.7% and 39.3% respectively perceive that BBP have a higher impact on 
biodiversity than conventional plastics and its production is in conflict with food supply. 
==PAGE:62==
54 
Previous qualitative studies also show that same concerns are raised individually (e.g. Sijtsema 
et al., 2015). Furthermore, Lynch et al. (2017) show that consumers negatively perceive a 
potential of genetic modification of plants. Same results are obtained in Denmark and Colombia 
in the present investigation.  
However, many of the perceptions (e.g. energy savings) are not align with scientific research. 
Hottle et al. (2013) show that BBP do not always show better environmental performance when 
compared to fossil-based polymers. The same aspects are described by Steenis et al.(2017), who 
have come to the conclusion that the traditional plastic packaging solution obtains better results 
than the alternative of BBP. Although consumers perceive individual trade-offs such as the 
higher impact on biodiversity, their perception is not based on scientific investigations in 
general. 
 
8.2 Companies’ narrative of bio-based plastics 
For the analysis of the company’s perspective company reports and websites have been 
examined as the main tools for organisations to communicate sustainability efforts to a broad 
audience (Lozano et al., 2016). The results show, that the percentage of producers is 
significantly higher in the sample than converters. Though, the industry is changing rapidly, 
and many new and small actors are present in the market environment. As most important, 
companies in the present sample, regardless their location on the value chain, are driven to 
reduce their dependence on oil and change their production to a more sustainable manner. These 
findings are in accordance with the results of the analysis of corporate sustainability strategies. 
Iles & Martin (2013) and de Vargas Mores et al. (2018) show that four large chemical 
companies pursued same motivations when developing BBP.  
Furthermore, the results reveal that future market and growth opportunities make the 
investments into BBP appealing for companies. In particular, producers which are located at 
the beginning of the value chain, see this as an important driver to pursue related strategies. 
Same results are obtained by Aquilani et al. (2018) who investigated the sustainability strategy 
in relation to BBP from a general point of view as well as by de Vargas Mores et al. (2018) and 
Iles & Martin (2013) who focused their research on specific companies in individual case 
studies. This can be explained with the importance for these actors as it mostly represents one 
of their core businesses and crucial for their economic sustainability. Moreover, changing oil 
prices and the scarcity of fossil resources are aspects which are found important for companies 
in this study. These features are aligned with findings obtained by Bos-Brouwers (2010) and 
Iles & Martin (2013). 
In regard to environmental benefits which are directly correlated with a shift towards BBP the 
reduction of GHGs and the environmental footprint are identified most frequently. Accordingly, 
de Vargas Mores et al. (2018) identify an ongoing debate about climate change and connected 
GHG emissions as important aspects. Other aspects, such as energy savings and addressing the 
increasing waste issue have not been subject of investigation in academic literature yet. 
 
8.3 Comparison of consumers’ and companies’ perspective 
Moreover, the findings of this study show that many differences but also similarities in the 
perception of consumers and companies’ narrative of BBP exist. However, especially concerns 
related to an increasing production such as a material vs. food conflict and higher pressure on 
==PAGE:63==
Perception of bio-based plastics 
55 
our ecosystems were not emphasized in their communications (Bennich & Belyazid, 2017; 
Herbes et al., 2018; Meyer, 2017). Furthermore, the analysis reveals the discrepancies between 
the consumers’ expectations for raw materials and disposal of BBP and companies’ 
communication. Whereas, the opinion about the origin of raw materials and the correct waste 
streams for BBP is clearly deluded among consumers, less than two third and only 72% of the 
companies respectively report the plant source and the specific type of their BBP. To overcome 
these issues an integrated approach which addresses and facilitates a dialogue between these 
stakeholders is important to close the gap in perception. 
 
8.4 Limitations and future research 
The study conducted in the present master thesis presents several limitations. Overcoming these 
offers opportunities for future research in order to improve the understanding of consumers and 
companies in a further development of BBP. The current survey was conducted in two different 
cultural backgrounds. Due to difficulties in reaching participants on a volunteering basis the 
sample sizes have varied significantly. Thus, the results are not representative for the whole 
corresponding populations and must be seen critical for interpretations. Following 
investigations might extend the present data in the present countries and hypotheses should be 
tested in more countries to better understand differences in perception.  
Although the study tries to better understand the cultural difference by targeting a specific and 
comparable group of consumers results might be different for other social groups. As the mean 
age of the present sample size is low compared to similar studies (e.g. Orset et al., 2017), 
extending the target group might lead to different results due to changing value systems of age 
groups (Crosby, Gill, & Lee, 1984 cited by Sudbury-riley et al., 2014). Future research on 
quantitative consumer research in regard to BBP should therefore try to map the perception of 
the whole population by targeting different segments of society. 
Furthermore, the study deals with disposal implications of specific products. The selection of 
for these focuses on FMCG as they are held responsible for the contamination of marine 
ecosystems (European Commission, 2018c). However, Petersen (2017) and Luchs et al. (2010) 
have shown that differences in perception between product categories due to a lower level of 
personal involvement exist. Thus, future research might find different results when asking 
consumers about products with a longer usage phase. 
As of part of the company analysis, to our knowledge the present study represents the first of 
its kind to capture drivers and benefits in regard to BBP. Thereby, this approach is of an 
explorative nature. As the industry is changing rapidly the sample might change depending on 
the time. Furthermore, the number of companies reporting about their sustainability has been 
increasing steadily since the 1980s (De Bakker, Groenewegen, & Den Hond, 2005). The 
number of organisations applying GRI standards, as one of the most widely spread guidelines 
in sustainability reporting, has increased from 315 reports in 2005 to 5693 in 20179. Thus, the 
amount of information connected to BBP is expected to increase further. This might extend the 
data of companies and leads to an improved data processing. In the present study the small 
sample size led to several violations of assumptions in regard to the statistical analysis. Future 
research should address these with more available information.

Another drawback is the missing regulation for sustainability reporting. Huge differences in 
practices make it difficult to base a sophisticated analysis on the content of this information 
source (Milne & Gray, 2013; Searcy & Buslovich, 2014). Therefore, future research in that area 
might follow the same approach for consumers and companies. The perception of high 
representatives of corresponding organisations and consumers could be captured with the same 
questionnaire. This would allow to draw a more holistic view from the company’s perspective 
and would overcome difficulties in comparing the results of the two studies.

Conclusion 
The production and consumption of BBP is expected to increase significantly in the next years. 
With regard to this development, the study aimed to extend the understanding of two important 
related stakeholders. Findings indicate that both, consumers and companies primarily associate 
and report positive environmental aspects with this technology.  
Although the results of the survey among university students in Denmark and Colombia show 
that BBP are generally perceived as an environmentally friendly alternative to conventional 
plastics, the participants’ attitude in Colombia is significantly more positive in all investigated 
aspects. The reasoning for both target groups is primarily based on a more environmentally 
friendly perception about production and disposal of products made of or packaged in BBP. 
However, in both countries this is correlated with assumptions about characteristics (e.g. 
disposal in home compost) and expectations for the origin of raw materials (e.g. waste biomass) 
which are not aligned with current practice.  
On the other hand, the results of the analysis of company reports and websites show that 
especially these critical aspects are not well presented in companies’ communication to their 
stakeholders. Although industry primarily report benefits and positive associations with BBP 
their narrative addresses other elements in comparison with the consumers’ perception (e.g. less 
GHG emissions). These contradicting perspectives have led to a mismatch between the two 
stakeholders with regard to the environmental benefits of BBP. 
 
 
==PAGE:66==
58 
10 Practical implications 
The discrepancy implies two important aspects in regard to the two stakeholders and a further 
penetration of BBP as a consumption product:  
First, consumers have the responsibility to better inform themselves to eliminate existing 
misconceptions regarding origin of raw materials and post-consumer behaviour. These are two 
important characteristics for them to evaluate the environmental sustainability of products and 
this is exactly where the most significant discrepancies to companies lie. These aspects are also 
in the interest of policy makers to avoid contamination of existing waste streams and an 
additional burden on our ecosystems. 
Second, in collaboration between industry and legislation standardisation criteria needs to be 
developed to differentiate the various types of BBP and to distinguish these from conventional 
plastics. On the one hand, it is important to integrate the consumer’s perspective into these 
considerations in order to address the knowledge gap regarding raw materials. Information 
about the origin of these should give a clear indication if the product is made of first, second or 
third generation BBP. Thus, consumers can take doubts such as material vs. food conflict and 
loss of biodiversity into account when doing their purchase decision. On the other hand, 
instructions are needed to imply the correct disposal of products. This is especially important 
when no infrastructure in regard to industrial composting facilities exists.
