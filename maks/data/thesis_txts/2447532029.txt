Abstract
Department of Wind energy
Master of Science
Ice detection on wind turbine blades
by Panteleimon Stasinopoulos
The current master thesis investigates how ice distribution along wind turbine blades
can be predicted by implementing Artiﬁcial Neural Networks. For that reason this
thesis was divided in two parts, one including the experimental procedure and one
including the construction and evaluation of the Artiﬁcial Neural Network model .
In the experimental procedure the wind turbine blade was simulated as a cantilever
aluminum beam and it was excited for diﬀerent loading scenarios. As loading scenario
a diﬀerent equivalent ice load distribution on the beam is deﬁned. After performing
frequency analysis for all the loading scenarios three diﬀerent Artiﬁcial Neural Net-
work models with diﬀerent inputs and amount of data were constructed, trained and
evaluated in the end. The comparison between these three models showed that in
order to build an accurate neural network model a bigger amount of data is necessary.
Finally these three models were tested in prediction in an unknown dataset and the
results showed that even for a small amount of data the predictions can be accurate
enough if the problem is deﬁned correct.

Introduction
1. Introduction
The overall capacity of the world’s installed wind turbines, reached in 2017 539 GW
with a growth rate 10.8%(ﬁgure 1.1), according to preliminary statistics published by
World Wind Energy Association (WWEA)[6],127 GW of which are installed in cold
climate cites [7], which corresponds to 22% of the overall number.
Figure 1.1: Installed wind turbine capacity evolution
Cold climate sites (CC) for wind turbines are considered the regions in which,icing
occurs or the temperature is below the standard operational limits of wind turbines
(ﬁgure 1.2) (normal operational temperature for wind turbines −20o)[8].These speciﬁc
weather conditions lead the wind turbine to perform in a non optimal way, which has as
an outcome power loss up to 17%of the Annual Energy Production (AEP) and 20% to
50% reduction of the power coeﬃcient [9].
1
==PAGE:15==
Introduction
Figure 1.2: Deﬁnition of Cold Climate cites
On the other hand cold climate sites, which are normally high altitude sites, are to
considered to be more eﬃcient, for the installation of wind turbines, due to the fact
that the wind speed increases by 0.1m/s per 100m of altitude for the ﬁrst 1000m [10].
Furthermore in these regions, the available wind power is 10% higher than in regions
with lower altitude, due to the increase of air density at lower temperatures (the air
density is proportional to the power output)[11] .
1.1. Types of ice
The wind turbines located in cold regions are exposed to diﬀerent kinds of icing, from
which the most important categories are in-cloud icing and precipitation icing ([12]).
The in-cloud icing can be divided in rime, glaze or mixed ice, while the precipitation
icing can be divided in wet snow or freezing rain variants [13].
In-cloud icing occurs when the cloud is lower than the site elevation and the temperature
is less than zero degrees.The categories of in-cloud icing depend on diﬀerent parameters
(meteorological and atmospheric), like the liquid water content of the air (LWC), the
median volume diameter (MVD), the humidity of the air and the air density[14, 15].
1.1.1. Rime ice
Rime ice occurs when the water droplets freeze completely when they contact to a
surface.This happens in temperatures from −4oC to −12oC, while the size of the droplets
is approximately 30µm. Normally rime ice accretion density is between 100 −600kg/m3.
2
==PAGE:16==

1.1.2. Glaze ice
Glaze ice occurs in warmer temperatures and higher wind speeds than the rime ice.
The droplets size is approximately 0to 100 µm, while the temperature is from 0 to -4
degrees.In the glaze ice case not all the droplets freeze immediately, therefore some of
them maybe follow the streamlines and might freeze at the trailing edge. The density of
glaze ice accretion is approximately 900kg/m3.
1.1.3. Mixed ice
Mixed ice is a combination of glaze and rime ice and it can be created in cold environments
where the temperature is decreasing. Mixed ice presents the bad characteristics of glaze
and rime ice and can be formed expeditiously by creating a very rough accumulation
[16].

1.2. Ice accretion formation
Ice accretion is formed when supercooled water droplets impact a surface, such as the
surface of a wind turbine blade. Icing accretion is a function of time and the rate of
the ice mass accumulated on the surface, which can described through the following
equation:
dmice = α1 · α2 · α3 · LWC · U∞· A · dt
(1.1)
where α1 is the coeﬃcient that represents the collision eﬃciency, and normally the
number is smaller than 1 because small particles tend to follow the streamlines, while
larger hit the airfoil(ﬁgure 1.5), a2 represents the sticking eﬃciency and is an outcome
of the ratio between the droplets that stick to the airfoil and those who follow the
streamline and a3 symbolize the accretion eﬃciency, it is usually smaller than 1 and it
is the ratio of the ﬂux density of the droplets that stick to the airfoil and those who
freeze[17].
Figure 1.5: Collision eﬃciency, Line 1 represents the streamlines, line 2 the larger
particles that hit the object, line 3 the smaller particles that follow the
streamline
These three parameters act as a reduction factor to the actual mass of the ice, at the
speciﬁc time, which is represented by the liquid water content of the cloud (LWC), the
velocity of the droplet relative to a cross sectional area of the airfoil (U∞) and the cross
sectional area (A)[17].

1.3. Aerodynamics of wind turbine blades due to icing
Rotating wind turbine blades in icing conditions, tend to accumulate ice around the
stagnation point of the diﬀerent airfoils along the radius. As the wind speed increases
along the radius of the blade, more ice is created in the outer part of the blade. When
the ice accumulation increases the drag of the airfoil increases also and as a result the
power output diminishes. In addition the lift decreases due to the ice at the leading
edge of the airfoil[18]. In the following ﬁgure lift and drag coeﬃcients on NREL S826
airfoil, for diﬀerent ice accretions are presented.
Figure 1.6: Lift and drag coeﬃcients for a NREL S826 airfoil for diﬀerent ice accretions
[1]
Furthermore icing on the wind turbine blades increases signiﬁcantly their mass, fact that
causes an increase in their fatigue loads and changes in their natural frequencies[14]. As
the fatigue loads increase they aﬀect the design life of the wind turbine and ﬁnally the
whole economy of the project.

1.4. Anti-icing solution methods
In order to overcome the eﬀect of the ice accumulation on wind turbine blades, many
solutions have been proposed, which include both de-icing and anti-icing systems[13].
Anti-icing systems include all these approaches that have as a target to prevent the ice
formation on the surface of the wind turbine blades, while de-icing systems include the
systems whose target is to remove the ice from the surface of the blade after it is formed.
Both of these systems nowadays use heating technologies in order to decrease the icing
eﬀects on the wind turbine blades.
These technologies include either electrical heating elements, which are elements made
of mesh electrical wires or carbon ﬁbre and they are attached on the the surface of the
blade or hot air circulation which consists of hot air blowers which are located typically
on the root of the blade and their purpose is to circulate the hot air inside the blade
shell[19]. Both of these systems heat the entire blade and as a result they consume 1%
to 4% of the AEP, according to the ice severity[2].
1.5. Ice detection
The required power consumption, of these systems, can be optimized, if the location and
the quantity of the ice are estimated. Ice accumulation on wind turbine blades increases
their total masses and as an outcome it leads to a decrease in the natural frequencies
of the blades. This fact can be used as a main parameter in order to detect ice in the
wind turbine blades. Modern ice detection systems, such as BLADEControl system [20],
Wolfel IDD.Blade [21], detect the ice on the wind turbine through the aforementioned
deviations in the natural frequencies of the blade and automatically shutting it down,
in order the ice to be removed manually, and afterwards the turbine is automatically
restarted. The deviations in the natural frequencies of the blade are acquired through
vibration analysis. Vibration analysis is possible in installed wind turbines, due to the
fact that they are often exposed to turbulent wind[22]. These systems estimate the
status of the blade(no-ice,critical ice, no critical ice) and they don’t give any indications
about the distribution of ice along the blade.
For the prediction of ice distribution along the blade, artiﬁcial neural network(ANN)
models have been proposed[2, 3, 22].These models try to simulate ice as an additional

Aim of these models is, through vibration analysis, to create a dataset of natural frequen-

1.6. Problem statement
This project focuses in ice detection on wind turbine blades and for that reason it was
decided to perform a vibration analysis on a blade and afterwards construct and train
an ANN model. It is crucial to note here that the wind turbine blade was simulated as
a cantilever aluminum beam. The procedure followed, which is based on similar analysis
methods, explained in a previous section of this report, had the following steps:
1. Frequency analysis of the cantilever beam for diﬀerent equivalent ice load scenarios
2. Post process analysis of the signals, in order to acquire the natural frequencies
and the modal shapes of the beam
3. Construction, training and evaluation of the artiﬁcial neural network model
Every step will be analysed in detail in the following chapters of this report.

Experimental Procedure
2. Experimental Procedure
In this chapter of the report the experimental procedure is analysed, procedure which
includes the selection of the material the deﬁnition of the boundary conditions, the
ice load simulation and in the end the calibration of the experiment through a ﬁnite
model analysis software (ABAQUS). In the following sections of this chapter each of the
described steps is analysed in detail.
2.1. Experimental set up
As it is mentioned above, the wind turbine blade was simulated as a cantilever aluminium
beam. This approach was implemented, due to the fact that an actual wind turbine
blade was not available for testing and as a result it was necessary to be simulated with
a ﬂexible and light material such as an aluminium. Moreover, because a wind turbine
blade has both ﬂap and edgewise natural frequencies, it was decided the aluminum
beam cross section to be asymmetrical with dimensions b = 0.06m and h = 0.045m and
hollow in order to reduce the beam’s total mass (ﬁg. 2.1,2.2).

frequencies and for functionality reasons inside the working area.

3.2. Frequency Response Function
After extracting the impact time histories, it was necessary to deﬁne the methodology
out of which the natural frequencies of the beam will be calculated for each loading
scenario. For that reason it was selected to implement the Frequency Response Functions
(FRFs) in order to compute the natural frequencies.
The Frequency Response Function is a measuring function, which is used to identify the
resonant frequencies, damping and modeshapes of a structure[4]. The FRF is constructed
by the ratio of an input and an output value.
Furthermore, in a FRF curve the peaks represent the resonant frequencies, while the
width between the peaks represents the damping of the system. One more crucial
parameter of the FRFs is the phase, which is signiﬁcant for the estimation of the
modeshapes of the model. In the following ﬁgure a typical FRF is presented along with
its phase (ﬁg. 3.4).

pact and as a result they give precise overview of the experiment in the frequency domain.

3.2.1. Fast Fourier Transform
A Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) is an algorithm which uses Fourier analysis, in order to
transform a signal from time domain, to the equivalent signal in the frequency domain.
This is happening by calculating the Discrete Fourier Transform(DFT) of the signal.
In this procedure a sequence of values is converted into components with diﬀerent
frequency. FFT is using DFT’s matrices and simpliﬁes them in order to run a faster
transformation. Moreover for the calculation of the FFT of a signal, it is necessary to
deﬁne four parameters:
1. Signal length
2. Sampling frequency
3. Frequency resolution
4. Nyquist frequency
where:
Signal length (N): describes the overall number of sampling points that compose the
signal.
Sampling frequency (fs): is described as the number of sampling points per time.
25

Experimental post process methodology
Frequency resolution (∆f): is the ratio between the sampling frequency and the length
of the signal .
Nyquist frequency (fn): represents the half of the sampling frequency of the signal.

4.1.1. Machine learning categories
”Machine learning (ML) is the scientiﬁc study of algorithms and statistical models
that computer systems use to eﬀectively perform a speciﬁc task without using explicit
instructions, relying on patterns and inference instead”[25]. Machine learning consists
of three diﬀerent categories:
• Supervised learning
• Unsupervised learning
• Semi supervised learning
In supervised learning the model contains input variables (X) and output variable (Y)
and an algorithm is deﬁned in order to connect the input with the output by learning
the mapping function:
Y = f(X)
(4.1)

The target of supervised learning is to describe the mapping function so well, that for
any new input data (X) the output data (Y) can be predicted accurately [26]. The
algorithm stops the training when it reaches to an acceptable level of performance.
The second category is unsupervised machine learning in which there are input variables
(X) but not corresponding output variables. Target of unsupervised machine learning is
to model the structure or distribution data in order to know more about the data [26].
It is called unsupervised because there is not a deﬁned output and as a result there
are no correct answers and no supervisor. A good example of unsupervised machine
learning is clustering, in which we can acquire information about what groups the data
points construct when we apply the clustering algorithm.
Finally, the last category is the semi supervised machine learning, in which there is a
big amount of input variables (X) but only some of them have labeled output variables
(Y) [26]. A good example of semi supervised machine learning is a data set of images,
from which a small amount is labeled.
In ice detection methods, as it can be noticed from the description of the problem and

4.1.3. Activation functions
As it stated above, every input multiplied with some weight and an added bias passes
through the activation function in order to produce the output. As a result it can be
noticed that activation functions play a decisive role to a neural network, since they
decide whether an artiﬁcial neuron should be or not activated, in order to contribute to
the ﬁnal output.This fact can be justiﬁed from the deﬁnition of the activation functions
for neural networks, where activation function is the function that deﬁnes the output of
a neuron, given speciﬁc input data. Then this output data is elaborated as input for the
next neuron and the procedure continues until an acceptable solution of the problem is
being reached.
Furthermore, activation functions are responsible for the back propagation in a neural
network. Back propagation is a procedure in a neural network, in which the weights and
the bias are updated from the output error basis and then they are calculated again
until a speciﬁc error threshold is reached.
Purpose of the activation function is to introduce non linearity into the model. A neural
network can be described as a linear model if the activation function is not included.
The activation function transforms the input non linearly, making it capable to learn
and implement more complex tasks [27]. There is a huge variety of activation functions
and the selection of which is based on the researcher and the deﬁnition of the problem.
The most common are :
• Linear function
• Sigmoid function
• Tanh function
• Relu function

performs better is by comparing for each model the error it has from training with the
error or accuracy that it will be calculated for the same model for an independent data dif-
ferent from the training one(validation data). The model with the best performance will
be the one with the smallest error in respect to the validation data set. For that reason,
during training checkpoints are used in order to be able to identify and save this model.

Additionally, if the validation error starts increasing after a speciﬁc threshold deﬁned
by the user, between training sessions the network stops its training. This procedure is
called early stopping and it is deﬁned by the user, in order to avoid overﬁtting on the data.
Overﬁtted models are the models which, during training, learn every detail of the data
and as a result this aﬀects the models performance, since it cannot generalize in terms
of new data(ﬁg. 4.9)[33].
Figure 4.9: An overﬁtted model(green line) it is compared with a normalized model
(black line).The overﬁtted model depends a lot on the train data(blue dots)
and it possible to have a high error[5]
Overﬁtting can be stopped through early stopping, due to the fact that when the
model oveﬁtts the loss increases. On the other hand, if the training of the model is
insuﬃcient or if the model’s training stops early due to wrong criteria in the early
stopping, the model may be underﬁtted. These kind of models cannot train or general-
ize to new data. In the neural network, described in this report, early stopping have
been set with a 20% threshold in validation loss, deﬁned as patience in the Python script.

and the equivalent modal shapes. For the last model, as input data the data acquired
by performing 10 excitations per loading scenario scenarios, was used. For that model
only the natural frequencies were used as inputs. It is necessary to note here that the
model with 800 loading scenarios, was decided to be trained also, in order to validate
how much the amount of data aﬀects the accuracy of the network and to check what
the diﬀerences in prediction for the models will be.
Before analysing the results from the training of the 3 models, it is necessary to note
what accuracy and loss indicate for a neural network. Accuracy in a neural network
shows how many predicted values in each epoch are the same with the actual values
used for training. For example, if the predicted data of a model was [0, 1, 2, 3] and the
actual data was [0, 1, 2, 6], then the model’s accuracy would be 75%, score that indicates
that 75% of the predictions match with the actual values in the speciﬁc epoch. It should
be noted here that accuracy is not used for training, but is only a metric system of the
model which helps the researcher to evaluate it. On the other hand, loss presents the
error between the predicted and the actual values. This error is calculated from the
loss functions (Section 4.1.4 Loss functions) and it represents generally the mean value
between the error of the predicted and actual values of the whole dataset. For example
if the loss is calculated through the MSE function, for the same dataset used before,
it will be equal with 2.25, result that corresponds only for the error between the last
value of the dataset, since the error for the other 3 parameters is 0. As it can be noticed
loss has the same units as the units of the dataset and it is not expressed in percentage.
Finally loss in contrast with accuracy is used in the training with a procedure explained
previously(Section 4.1.3 Gradient descent for training).

Conclusions and further research
5. Conclusions and further research
The main purpose of this project was to investigate if ice distribution on wind turbine
blades, can be detected through an Artiﬁcial Neural Network. For that reason it was
decided to perform an experimental procedure in order to acquire the data and in the end
to train an artiﬁcial neural network with the speciﬁc data and validate its predictions.
As a result the project was divided in two big sections, one describing the experimental
procedure and methodology(which is subdivided in two parts) and one describing the
construction and evaluation of the ANN model.
In order to perform the experimental procedure two assumptions were made. In the
ﬁrst assumption the wind turbine blade was assumed that it can be simulated as a
cantilever aluminum beam, due to the fact that it was not possible to acquire a real
blade for testing, while in the second assumption the ice load on the blade was simulated
as consecrated force, represented by calibrated weights.
After acquiring the data from the vibration analysis of the beam, 3 diﬀerent ANN models
were trained. For the ﬁrst two models the input consisted of a data of 340 diﬀerent
loading scenarios. The only diﬀerence between these two models was that, for the ﬁrst
model only the natural frequencies were used as inputs, while for the second the natural
frequencies and the equivalent modal shapes were used as inputs. The third model con-
sisted of a dataset which included the 80 ﬁrst loading scenarios of the previous dataset,
with the only diﬀerence, that for every scenario 10 excitations per direction were made
instead of one. The input layer of the model consisted of two values corresponding to the
2 ﬁrst natural frequencies. It should be noted here that the last model was created in or-
der to investigate how the amount of data inﬂuences the performance of a neural network.
Furthermore, after the data for all of the three models was created, it was implemented
into the neural network in order to train the model. Each model in the end of training
provided two indications one for the accuracy and one for the loss of the model. After
comparing the results between the three models it was observed that the model with 800
data performed slightly better in terms of accuracy and loss than the other two. This a
logical conclusion, since in neural networks for bigger amount of data the predictions will
be better. The only drawback in this case was that the dataset included a huge majority
of same values and as a result it would be diﬃcult to generalize in terms of prediction
to an unknown dataset. An other observation was, that between the two models with
61
==PAGE:75==
Conclusions and further research
the same amount of data, the one with less inputs performed better. This lead us to
conclude that for a bigger amount of data describing the model, a bigger number of
tests is necessary, because if the neural network is considered as algorithm who tries to
create a connection between the inputs and the outputs, for the ﬁrst model it has to
connect two parameters with three outputs while for the second eight parameters with
three outputs.
Finally, all the models were tested in predictions in a dataset diﬀerent from the test
matrix. The results showed that the models react good enough in terms of prediction,
despite the discrepancies they have with the actual values Additionally, it was observed
that all the ANN models were able to identify accurately the section of the beam with
more added mass, for every loading scenario. That result indicated that ANN models
can be used as method for identifying ice distribution on wind turbine blades, but they
need a huge amount of data in order to more eﬃcient and reliable.
5.1. Further research
As it has been showed in this project, ANN models can be a solution in terms of detecting
ice distribution along a wind turbine blade. A huge drawback of neural networks is that
they require a huge amount of data in order to be able to describe the model accurately
and in the end to predict with a small error. For that reason it is necessary a huge
data set to be created and tested in the future in order to be able to conclude that the
network can estimate accurately the ice distribution on a wind turbine blade.
Furthermore, it is essential a real wind turbine blade to be used in the experimental
procedure and as a result the experiment needs to be redeﬁned. Additionally in the
new experimental procedure, except from the real wind turbine blade it is necessary
to use real ice, instead of calibrated weights and rethink about how the excitation of
the blade will happen. A good suggestion is this excitation to happen with a vibration
shaker instead of an impact hammer, in order the force to have a time history that
presents variations and it is more close to reality than a time history from an impact
which presents only one value the value of impact. Finally the possibility of constructing
a new test matrix with a diﬀerent strategy, than the one used in this report, may lead
to better and more accurate results.
