Abstract
Intimin is a bacterial adhesion factor belonging to the family of inverse autotransporters. Intimin is
responsible for the formation of attaching and effacing (A/E) lesions caused by enterohemorrhagic and
enteropathogenic Escherichia coli. A/E lesions are associated with severe diarrheal disease, which is
considered to be one of the leading causes of child mortality in low income countries. Recent studies of
intimin have lead to the hypothesis that the folding energy of the extracellular Intimin domain, IntD00,
could be inﬂuenced by sodium. Furthermore, it has been argued that the structure of IntD00 may be
conserved in many other inverse autotransporters. This study investigates the thermal stability of IntD00
with the use of nano Differential Scanning Fluorimetry (nanoDSF) and Differential Scanning Calorimetry
(DSC). Thermal unfolding of IntD00 was carried out in various concentrations of NaCl and KCl. The
experiments showed that IntD00 has higher stability in high salt concentrations of both NaCl and KCl.
The study further indicated that NaCl and KCl may stabilize IntD00 in two different ways. Putative
IntD00-like domains from three other inverse autotransporters, Invasin, FdeC and YeeJ, were puriﬁed with
Immobilized metal afﬁnity chromatography (IMAC). Crystallization experiments were conducted and
yielded structural information on the putative IntD00 domain from FdeC. This work contributes to the
characterization of IntD00. Further, the crystallization experiments contributes to the determination of
whether the IntD00-like structure is conserved among inverse autotransporters.
Resumé
Intimin er en bakteriel adhæsin, som tilhører familien af inverse autotransportere. Intimin er ansvarlig
for udviklingen af "attaching and effacing" (A/E) læsioner, hvilke forårsages af diarréfremkaldende
Escherichia coli infektioner. Disse infektioner regnes for at være en af de største årsager til dødelighed
blandt børn i udviklingslande. Nye studier af intimin har ledt til en hypotese om, at foldningsenergien af
et af intimins extracellulære domæner, IntD00, kan påvirkes af natrium. Herudover argumenteres der for,
at strukturen af IntD00 domænet kan være konserveret blandt andre inverse autotransportere. Dette studie
undersøger den termiske stabilitet af IntD00 vha. nano Differential Scanning Fluorimetry (nanoDSF) og
Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC). Analyserne af IntD00 blev udført under forskellige koncen-
trationer af hhv. NaCl of KCl. Eksperimenterne viste at IntD00 var mest stabil i høje koncentrationer af
både NaCl og KCl. Eksperimenterne kan muligvis også indikere, at NaCl og KCl stabiliserer IntD00 på
to forskellige måder. Dette projekt indebærer også oprensningen og krystalliseringsforsøg af formodede
strukturelle homologer af IntD00 domænet fra andre inverse autotransportere. Disse var Invasin, FdeC og
YeeJ. Det lykkedes at opnå strukturel data for domænet fra FdeC. Dette projekt bidrager til karakteris-
eringen af domænet IntD00. Krystalliseringens-eksperimenterne kan endvidere lede til en klargørelse af,
hvorvidt IntD00 struturen er konserveret blandt mange inverse autotransportere.

1
Introduction
1.1
Intimin contributes to lethal E. coli infections
Intimin is an adhesion factor which has long been studied due to its role in many lethal gastrointestinal
infections [7][26].
Some of the most widely known pathogens that utilize intimin for adherence to their host-cells are
enteropathogenic (EPEC) and enterohaemorrhagic (EHEC) Escherichia coli [7][26]. Intimin carrying
pathogens can infect the enterocytes of the intestinal epithelium [3]. This results in distinctive attaching
and effacing (A/E) lesions, which can lead to the severe bloody diarrhea [26][29]. EPEC infections are
well-known as one of the leading causes of child mortality in low income countries [15].
Whereas EPEC mainly causes severe and lethal infections in young children, EHEC infections severely
affects a larger age-group, including adults [13]. EHEC differs from EPEC because it, in addition
to creating A/E lesions, also carries the gene for shiga toxin [26]. This toxin is association with the
development of hemeolytic uremic syndrome (HUS) that involves clotting of bloodplatelets and kidney
failure. [13]. In a major EHEC outbreak in Germany, 2011, almost 4000 patients were reported infected
and 22% of these developed HUS [6].
Without doubt, Intimin carrying pathogens impose a signiﬁcant health threat worldwide.
1.2
Intimin causes intimate attachment between bacteria and host cell
Intimin is encoded by the eae gene, which is located on a pathogenecity island called "locus of enterocyte
effacement" (LEE) [26][7]. In addition to intimin, LEE also contains other genes neccessary for A/E lesion
formation. These include genes for the translocated intimin receptor (Tir), effector proteins, chaperones
and the type III secretion system [7][3]. Intimin and the other LEE encoded genes are responsible for the
strong and intimate attachment between the bacteria and host cell. The bacterial attachment and resulting
A/E lesion can be described in three overall steps as illustrated in Figure 1.
First, Tir is injected into the host enterocyte (intestinal epithelial cell) through the type III secretion
system, see Figure 1. A [7]. This secretion system is made up by proteins, which together forms a needle
like machinery, also called an injectisome [9]. The injectisome spans accross both the inner and outer
bacterial cell-membrane and extends into the extracellular space. The needle-tip forms a pore in the
eukaryotic host cell through which Tir and other effector proteins is directly injected [11][9].
Second, Tir positions itself in the host cell membrane and forms a dimeric hairpin-like structure. Tir
has both the N- and the C-terminal facing the host cell cytosol [21]. The hairpin loops protrudes into the

Third, Intimin, which extends from the bacterial cell surface, binds to Tir and A/E lesions begin to
form, see Figure 1.C. Both Intimin and Tir are dimerized and are believed to bind in interconnected
clusters [17]. The clustering of Intimin-Tir complexes results in an intimate attachment of the bacterial
cell to the host cell. Furthermore, Tir triggers host cell actin polyremization [3]. This consequently alters
the host cytoskeletal structure, which forms a raised actin-rich area, called a pedestal, at the site of
bacterial contact [11][14]. The Intimin-Tir binding leads to the A/E lesion symptoms. These involve
thinning (effacement) of the brush border microvilli and overall increased intestinal permeability [27].
The infection then progresses and causes mitochondrial damage, inhibiton of cell-division and instigates
host cell apoptosis [11]. This array of host cell damages dependents on LEE encoded effectors (although
not exclusively) [3]. All the implications of the A/E lesion leads to the characteristic EHEC and EPEC
associated diahrrea.
1.3
Intimin is an inverse autotransporter
Autotransporters are a group of transmembrane proteins that utilizes the type V secretion system to
transport themselves across the outer membrane of gram negative bacteria [22]. Autotransporters have
two regions: A transmembrane region and an extracellular region also called the passenger region. Some
also have a small periplasmic domain [22][17]. It is only the passenger which is transported through the
outer membrane, i.e "autotransported".
2
==PAGE:9==
The inverse autotransporters (type Ve) are named such due to their inverted orientation in comparison
to classical autotransporters (type Va) [16]. Whereas classical autotransporters have their extracellular
passenger regions in the N terminal, inverse autotransporters have their passenger region in the C-terminal
of the protein [23]. This difference in orientation as well as the overall structure of these types of proteins
is illustrated in Figure 2, which is a modiﬁed ﬁgure from a review by Leo et al. [18]
Figure 2: Illustration of the difference in classical (Va) and inverse (Ve) autotransporters which includes Intimin. The classical
autotransporters have an N-terminal passenger. The inverse autotransporter have their passenger region in the C-terminal of the
protein. The Figure is adopted from the review by Leo et al. [18].
Intimin belongs to the family of inverse autotransporters and serves as a prototype for understanding
the secretion and folding mechanisms for inverse autotransporters [18].
Intimin has three regions, each with a speciﬁc role: The intimin perisplasmic region is a small lysM
domain, which is illustrated as a grey pentagon in Figure 1 [17]. This domain has shown to aid a tight
binding to the periplasmic peptidoglycan and mediate the intimin dimerization that preceeds Tir binding
[17]. The lysM-peptidoglycan binding is argued to aid in resisting the physical strain posed by the
surroundings of the intestinal lumen [18].
The transmembrane beta-barrel consists of a closed beta-sheet, that forms a hydrophillic pore [19]. This
beta barrel facilitates the translocation of the passenger region across the outer membrane and serves as an
anchor for the protein [18]. Finally, the passenger region is the adhesive region, which is responsible for
binding to Tir [21].

1.4
The Intimin passenger region
The Intimin passenger region has ﬁve subdomains: D00, D0, D1, D2 and D3 [23][16], see Figure 3.
Figure 3: Illustration of the Intimin passenger region with the Big domains D00, D0, D1, D2 and the lectin-like domain D3. The
ﬁgure is copied from Weikum et al. [28].
The outermost C-terminal domain D3 is a C-type lectin domain, which together with the D2 domain
creates a rigid superdomain that constitute the Tir binding site [21]. This was shown through crystallization
of the complex between Intimin D1-D2-D3 and Tir [21]. The subdomains D00-D2 are tandem bacterial
Ig-like (Big) domains. It was previously believed that the Intimin passenger region only contained the
domains D1-D3. However, the presence of D00 and D0 has later been conﬁrmed through crystallization
and sequence analysis [28][19].
The Big domains are small sequences, of about 100 amino acids, which take on a immunoglobulin (ig)
like fold [2]. This fold consists of two stacked beta-sheets of anti-parallel beta strands [2]. The interior part
of the domain consists of hydrophobic sidechains, which forms a hydrophobic core [2]. It is important to
note that Big domains can have great structural resemblance, although their sequences may differ widely
[4].
The Big domains are further categorized into subgroups of structurally similar domains. The Intimin
passenger domains are classiﬁed as Big-1 (D0 and D1) and Big 2 (D2) [28]. These domain groups have
long been known to be typical for many inverse autotransporters [28][2]. The D00 domain which is
also called the juncture domain, was only recently crystallized and described by Weikum et al. [28].
Interestingly, the D00 domain displayed a structure which differed from the other known Big domains.

1.5
Intimin passenger transport may be driven by domain folding energy
The correct folding of the passenger domains is believed to be important for the successful passenger
transport across the outer membrane [19]. However, every molecular detail of said transport is not fully
elucidated [18][19].
Intimin, as well as other autotransporters, are initially transported across the inner membrane and
through the periplasmic space in an unfolded state [22]. The passenger transport across the outer membrane
cannot be driven by ATP due to the lack thereof in the periplasm [19]. Studies have indicated that the
transmembrane beta barrel is assembled and inserted into the outer membrane by the help of a beta barrel
assembly machinery (BAM complex) [8]. This machinery is known to be essential for the assembly
and insertion of other outer membrane proteins containing beta barrels [8]. Furthermore, it is shown
that other periplasmic chaperones, SurA, Skp, and DegP are needed for correct folding and initiation of
the passenger secretion through the beta barrel [8]. The overall transportation is believed to imply the
insertion of a hairpin-like loop of the N-terminal end of the passenger region into the barrel [16]. The
passenger region towards the C-terminal is subsequently fed through the barrel in an N to C direction.
This is illustrated in Figure 4. The ﬁgure has been copied from [16].
Recent studies have shown that incorrect folding of the passenger domains can halt the transportation
[19]. This suggests that the driving force of the transportation is the folding energy of the passenger
domains. One could assume, that the folding energy of the juncture domain D00 could drive the secretion
and folding of the remaining domains. However, a deletion of D00 did not affect the secretion [19]. This
indicates that the passenger is transported and folded sequentially one domain after another, independently
of D00. Nonetheless, the D00 domain may have an important function for the entire structure of the
passenger, as suggested by Weikum et al. [28].
Figure 4: An illustration of passenger domain transport across the outer membrane. The passenger is fed through the beta barrel
in an N to C direction. The Figure is copied and cropped from a paper by Leo et al. [16]

1.6
The structure of the D00 domain may be present in many inverse autotransporters
Weikum et al. describes that D00 differs from he typical Big domains of inverse autotransporters [28].
They also report that the link between the D00 and the D0 domains seems more rigid than links between
other Big domains. This rigidity has a stabilizing effect on the entire passenger domain. This allows
the passenger to attain a constantly extended conformation. Simulations indicated that the D00-D0 link
enforced rigidity increased the reaching range for the Tir binding domain. Therefore, the D00 domain
could be important for facilitating the most optimal extension and orientation of the passenger for enabling
Tir binding.
They further suggest that a D00-type domain is conserved in many other inverse autotransporters. This
hypothesis was based on structure predictions and sequence alignments of 18 different inverse auto-
transporters. Although the sequence identity was rather low, it is believed that the structures share high
similarity. An obvious method for conﬁrming this case could be to directly compare the Intimin D00 crys-
tal structure to crystal structures of the putative D00 regions of other inverse autotransporters. This thesis
aims at further supporting the stated hypothesis through crystallization of three inverse autotransporters;
Invasin, FdeC and YeeJ. Invasin is a Yersinia Pseudotuberculosis adhesin which is often compared and
discussed in relation to Intimin [28][18]. FdeC and YeeJ are both E. coli adhesins belonging to the inverse
autotransporters [28][18].
The putative D00-like domains in Inversin, FdeC and YeeJ will in this thesis be named InvD0, FdeCD0
and YeeJD0, respectively. The Intimin D00 domain will similarly be referenced as IntD00. Moreover, the
project will also include experiments with constructs that consist of the putative juncture domains linked
with the subsequent domain in the passengers of Inversin, FdeC and YeeJ. These "double domains" are
called InvD0D1, FdeCD0D1 and YeeJD0D1. The sequence alignment of the IntD00 region and the InvD0,
FdeCD0 and YeeJD0 regions is shown in Figure 5.
Figure 5: The sequence alignment of IntD00 and putative domains InvD0, FdeCD0 and YeeJD0. The sequence identity is
between 20 and 31 %. Prolin 459 is highlighted because it changes position dependent on sodium binding, cf. Figure 6. The
other conserved amino acids are highlighted with an asterisk *. The alignments was done with the EMBL Clustal Omega tool.

1.7
IntD00 could be inﬂuenced by sodium
Further analysis of the results documented by Weikum et al. indicates that the crystal structure D00 has a
site at which sodium can bind and thereby slightly alter the domain conformation (not yet published). This
alteration is speciﬁcally seen in the proline residue nr 459, which is highlighted in Figure 5, and shown in
the crystal structure of Intimin in Figure 6.
Figure 6: The superimposed structure of Intimin D00 domain with (blue) and without (green) the presence of sodium. left: the
entire domain shows no major change in domain fold. Right: The proline residue nr. 459 is highlighted and shows a shift
dependent on the presence of sodium.
Figure 6 shows the superimposed structures of the Intimin domain D00 with (blue) and without (green)
sodium bound. A slight shift of the highlighted proline residue is visible in the case of sodium bound
versus non-bound. The proline in the unbound state is in a more open conﬁguration than the proline in the
bound state. This ﬁnding has not yet been published, but it renders the question: could this sodium binding
site be of any importance? Weikum et al. points out that the D00 domain shares much similarity with the
extracellular domain of another class of adhesins, namely the cadherins [28]. Cadherins are known for
their dependence on calcium which causes changes in ﬂexibility/rigidity. Thus, the sodium binding site of
D00 could have similar structural importance.
7
==PAGE:14==
We recently hypothesized that the sodium ion contributes to the folding energy of D00 by making it
more stable when exposed to extracellular salt concentrations. Further, this may aid in the transport of
IntD00 through the outer membrane. The potential importance of this sodium site could be substantiated,
if the D00-like structure is present in other inverse autotransporters and if these also contains this
sodium binding site. These questions are addressed in this thesis by crystallization and thermostability
measurements in various sodium concentrations.
1.8
Theoretical background
The thermal stability of IntD00 will be analyzed with two methods: Differential Scanning Calorimetry
(DSC) and nano Differential Scanning Fluorimetry (DSF). Thermostability measurements aims to describe
the protein stability through analyses of unfolding characteristics, i.e the unfolding temperatures and
enthalpy. Both of these methods are brieﬂy described below. In addition, crystallization by vapor diffusion
is also described.
1.8.1
NanoDSF
NanoDSF indirectly measures the thermal stability of a protein through the intrinsic ﬂuorescence of
tryptophan [10]. Tryptophan emission (ﬂuorescence) occurs around 350 and 330 nm. The emission
wavelength is highly inﬂuenced by the polarity of the environment [12]. Hence, any changes in the
tryptophan environment will cause a shift in the emission wavelength [10].
The nanoDSF measurements are conducted by gradually increasing the temperature, which eventually
will cause the protein to unfold [10]. This can cause major changes in the environment of the tryptophan.
These changes can then be measured as the ratio between the ﬂuorescence of 350 nm and 330 nm [10].
This can be depicted as a graph with the 350/330 nm ﬂuorescence ratio vs. temperature. The unfolding
temperature is deﬁned as the temperature at which 50% of the native protein is unfolded [10]. High
unfolding temperatures indicates a high thermostability, i.e much energy is needed to denature the protein.
Since IntD00 has a tryptophan in its core, see Figure 7, this method can be applied for thermostability
measurements of IntD00. During unfolding, the tryptophan is expected to experience a major change in
environment polarity.

1.8.2
DSC
DSC measures the heat capacity of a protein sample [5]. It is therefore a direct measure of the folding
energy (stability) of a protein, whereas the nanoDSF indirectly measures stability through change in the
surroundings of tryptophan.
DSC measurements are based on difference in heat capacity between a protein sample and a reference
buffer sample [5]. During thermal unfolding the protein absorbs energy provided through temperature
increase. This absorption entails the disruption of the atomic forces in the native tertiary structure [5].
When sufﬁcient energy is provided the protein will no longer favor the folded state and begin to unfold.
The protein sample absorbs more energy than the reference buffer, i.e the heat capacity of the protein
sample is higher. The difference in energy needed to heat the two samples can be recorded. Consequently,
this yields a measure of the energy required for the protein unfolding. The energy is given as enthalpy
(∆H) with the unit as kJ per mole IntD00 (kJ/mol). A high enthalpy will entail a higher stability, since
more energy is needed to denature the protein. The unfolding temperatures can also be determined through
DSC measurements [5].

1.8.3
Crystallization by vapor diffusion
The descriptions in this section are based on knowledge from the book Crystallography Made Crystal
Clear, chapters two and three, by Gale Rhodes [24][25].
Protein crystals are composed of aligned arrays of individual proteins, which are organized to form
larger crystalline structures. If the crystals are sufﬁciently ordered, they can be used to gain information
on the tertiary protein structure through X-ray diffraction patterns. Depending on the diffraction quality,
these models can have varying resolutions. High resolution data is most desirable as they can differentiate
between features at a small scale, i.e. describe the protein structure more detailed than low resolution
structures. The crystalline forms varies between different proteins, and several different crystalline forms
may also be obtainable for one protein.
Protein crystals are achieved by reaching a supersaturated protein solution. This can be obtained with
various methods, but this thesis will only employ crystallization by vapor diffusion. When vapor diffusion
is used, crystals are obtained by enclosing the protein in an airtight chamber, see Figure 8.
Figure 8: Illustration of crystallization by vapor diffusion. Hanging drop and sitting drop is shown. The reservoirs contain a
buffer and precipitant. The droplets contain the puriﬁed protein and the same buffer and precipitant as the reservoirs. Vapor
diffusion, illustrated by arrows, causes an increase in protein and precipitant concentration in the droplets. This can lead to the
formation of protein crystals.
A small protein sample is mixed with a buffer that contains a precipitant. The protein is then placed as
a hanging drop or a sitting drop in the chamber (This is illustrated in Figure 8). The chamber reservoir
contains the same buffer and precipitant solution, but in much higher volume and at a higher concentration.
Since the chamber is airtight, the droplet and reservoir will equilibrate through vapor diffusion. This
implies that water will evaporate from the protein droplet, which in turn increases the concentration of
protein and precipitant in the droplet. If the protein concentration is increased sufﬁciently, the droplet will
reach a supersaturated state. If successful, this will lead to the nucleation and growth of protein crystals.
The buffer composition and precipitant, as well as other parameters like pH and temperature, are important
10
==PAGE:17==
for crystal formations. The various parameters may therefore be altered to obtain the optimal conditions
for crystallization. In order to ﬁnd an appropriate buffer composition, many different combinations may
be tested. This is easily done by using premade crystallization screens that are sold commercially. Apart
from the importance of buffer and precipitant composition, the initial protein concentration of the droplet
must also be high enough for the supersaturated state to occur.
When a protein crystal is obtained it must be collected and sent for diffraction. This can be done by
mounting the crystals on small loops and ﬂash freezing them in liquid nitrogen. Figure 9 shows a picture
of a mounted crystal. This process can however damage the protein crystal if the water forms ice crystals.
Therefore, it can be necessary to use cryoprotectants, which hinders the formation of ice crystals. Some
commonly used cryoprotectants are glycerol and ethylenglycol, which were also used in this thesis.

Results
4.1
All domains were successfully puriﬁed
All proteins were puriﬁed to ﬁnal yields of between 1 and 3 mg cleaved and pure protein per gram
harvested cell. Note that IntD00 puriﬁcation is not described here, since it was provided by former phD
student Julia Weikum. All domains were constructed with a His6-tag and a TEV cleavage site in order to
enable immobilized metal afﬁnity chromatography (IMAC) puriﬁcation and subsequent removal of the
tag. Thus, the full puriﬁcation comprised an inital IMAC puriﬁcation, followed by TEV protease cleavage
and ﬁnally a reverse IMAC (rIMAC) puriﬁcation.
The IMAC puriﬁcation is based on the strong binding afﬁnity between imidazole and the nickel resin
(stationary phase) of the chromatography column. His6-tagged proteins are selected for as the imidazole
sidechain of the six histidines causes tight binding to the nickel column. Other proteins, that have lower
afﬁnity for the column, will not be retained by the column to the same extend. Most proteins will therefore
simply ﬂow through and elude from the column. The His6-tagged proteins can afterwards be eluded
by increasing the imidazole concentration of the washing buffer (mobile phase). At sufﬁciently high
imidazole concentrations, the excess imidazole will replace the His6-tagged proteins and cause these to
elude. After TEV protease digestion the proteins are puriﬁed by rIMAC. As the proteins no longer has
their His6-tag, they will elude based on their intrinsic afﬁnity for the nickel resin and the cut off His6-tags
will bind to the column. Hence, the pure protein without tags can be collected.

4.2
NanoDSF measurements
NanoDSF measurements were used to get information on thermal stability of IntD00. It was hypothesized
that extracellular concentrations of sodium could contribute to the stability IntD00. This hypothesis could
be supported if high sodium levels would cause a visible increase in un- and refolding temperatures.
The effect of sodium and potassium on IntD00 stability was tested. This was done to elucidate whether
sodium has a distinct inﬂuence on IntD00, or if another monovalent ion,i.e potassium, could cause similar
effects, if any at all.
All nanoDSF results in this thesis are given in graphs that display the ratio of 350/330 nm ﬂuorescence,
and the ﬁrst derivative of this ratio. All graphs represent the average of triplicate measurements. The
estimated standard deviations are shown as light shadows for each colored graph.
4.2.1
Protein concentration inﬂuences thermostability
Initially, the stability of IntD00 in different concentrations were examined, see Figure 13. This was done
to determine an appropriate protein concentration, which would yield enough signal, for subsequent
measurements. The test was also used to understand whether the concentration of IntD00 could effect the
DSF measurements.
Four IntD00 concentrations were tested: 0.05, 0.2, 0.4 and 0.6 mg/mL. The sodium concentration was
200mM in all samples. The salt concentration was simply chosen as this was the stock-condition of the
provided IntD00.
Figure 13 leads to the assumption that all tested IntD00 concentrations both unfolds and refolds to
some extend. This is observed since the 350/330 nm ﬂuorescence ratio increases during heating and drops
signiﬁcantly during cooling. The degree of refolding seems to be related to the IntD00 concentration.
The 0.05 mg/mL (green graphs) exhibits the highest degree of refolding. The starting value (before
denaturing) has a 350/330 nm ﬂuorescence ratio of about 0.58 which is relatively close to the ﬁnal ratio of
about 0.68 (after refolding). The difference is about 0.1. In comparison the higher IntD00 concentrations
shows increasingly higher differences between the initial and ﬁnal 350/330 ratio. The highest IntD00
concentration of 0.6 mg/mL (red graphs) depicts only little unfolding. The lack of complete refolding may
be caused by aggregation of more concentrated IntD00 solutions.
The un- and refolding temperatures and their standard deviations are listed in Table 6. As expected,
the lowest IntD00 concentration of 0.05 mg/mL displays the highest degree of uncertainty. Though, the
ﬂuorescence signal is clearly detected even at this low concentration and the standard deviations are
deemed acceptable. Therefore, it was expected that 0.05 mg/mL IntD00 would give sufﬁcient signal for
later nanoDSF measurements.

4.2.3
NaCl and KCl have different effects on IntD00 unfolding
NaCl and KCl inﬂuences on IntD00 stability was examined at different ratios of NaCl and KCl and at
different IntD00 concentrations. Because the ionic strength of each test was constant it was believed to
elucidate any difference in salt effects. An overview of these experiments are given as tests 4, 5 and 6 in
Table 2, Section 3.3. Only test 4 with 0.05 mg/mL showed any new information. The graphs for this test
are shown in Figure 16.
Figure 16 shows a shift in the two-step unfolding of IntD00. This is best observed via the ﬁrst deriva-
tive of the 350/330 ﬂuorescence ratio (bottom part of the ﬁgure). These graphs portrays the slope of the
unfolding event. If a change in the 350/330 ratio occurs over small temperature range the slope is steep,
and the ﬁrst derivative will have a high value.
According to Figure 16; if the majority of available salt is NaCl then the second unfolding step is
steeper than the ﬁrst step. On the contrary, if KCl is most available; the ﬁrst unfolding step is steeper than
the second. This ﬁnding is also in agreement with the graphs shown in Figures 14 and 15. Both these
ﬁgures indicate the presence of a two unfolding steps. The ﬁrst derivative graphs of 50 and 100 mM NaCl
in Figure 14 depicts a small peak for the ﬁrst unfolding step, and a taller peak for the second unfolding
step. In contrary, the ﬁrst derivative graphs of 10 and 50 mM KCl in Figure 15 depicts a tall peak for
the ﬁrst unfolding step, and a smaller peak for the second unfolding step. Thus, it appears that NaCl and
KCl inﬂuences the unfolding rates of each step differently. KCl, in comparison to NaCl, causes the ﬁrst
unfolding event to be more sudden, i.e over a shorter range of temperature increase.
Figure 16 also indicates that KCl increases the unfolding temperatures more than NaCl. However, the
difference is small and may be due to inaccurate concentrations of NaCl and KCl. Further tests should be
conducted to resolve this issue.
In conclusion, if IntD00 unfold in two steps it appears that KCl and NaCl have different effects on the
two unfolding steps.

4.3
DSC measurements indicates that NaCl and KCl stabilizes IntD00
DSC experiments were carried out in order to obtain information on thermal stability and folding energy
of IntD00. The DSC measurements provide information on enthalpy and unfolding temperatures, which
are used as indicatives of protein stability.
The unfolding temperature is the temperature at which the maximum heat capacity is measured, i.e. the
transition peak maximum. The enthalpy (∆H) is calculated as the integral of the transition peak. The
area, which is displayed as a red line in all DSC-graphs, is deﬁned manually. The determined enthalpies
should not be considered exact quantiﬁcations, but rather a mean to compare different samples. Slight
uncertainties in area of integration caused enthalpy variations of about +/- 10 kJ/mol. Therefore, only the
overall tendencies and comparisons between graphs are discussed here. Each DSC graph depicts the molar
heat capacity as a function of temperature. The baseline (a blank buffer scan) is subtracted from each
graph. In order to achieve the molar heat capacity the graphs are normalized to the protein concentration
of each sample.
A total of 6 experiments were conducted in which 3 concentrations (5, 150 and 400 mM) of both NaCl
and KCl were tested. The protein concentration was 0.5 mg/ mL on all experiments. The temperature
was set to gradually increase from 15 °C to 85°C by 1 °C/min and subsequently to decrease back to
15°C, also by 1 °C/min. Both un- and refolding data could have been expected. However, the re-folding
graphs did not show any transition peaks, perhaps due to protein aggregation. The graphs that display
decreasing temperatures are therefore not included in this thesis. This section displays the unfolding
curves for IntD00 in 150 and 400 mM NaCl in Figure 17 and 18, respectively. The remaining unfolding
graphs are depicted in the appendix, Section 3. Table 8 summarizes the measured (∆H) and unfolding
temperatures for all DSC scans.
All DSC graphs, except the 5 mM NaCl graph, indicates that IntD00 fully unfolds. This is deduced be-
cause the molar heat capacity drops to a steady baseline before the heating is ﬁnished. This is exempliﬁed
in Figure 17. The unfolding graph of 400 mM NaCl (Test 1 in Table 8) shows an unexpected bump and a
sudden decrease in molar heat capacity, see Figure 18. Since there is no distinct transition peak, the graph
does not clearly show whether the IntD00 had fully unfolded or not. The odd curve may be caused by
bubbles within the sample or perhaps a faulty blank scan. An estimation of ∆H was attempted for this
scan, although the measurement is somewhat unreliable.

5
Discussion
5.1
Puriﬁcation
The transformation, expression and cell-lysis were successfully done for the isolation of all studied
proteins. It was expected that some protein would be lost during harvest and lysis, but this has not
been documented or quantiﬁed. The ﬁnal protein yields were sufﬁcient for all subsequent experiments.
Therefore optimization of the puriﬁcation methods was not necessary in this project.
The IMAC puriﬁcations were also successful, although the outcomes varied among the domains.
Some domains, especially InvD0, which was puriﬁed with a 1 mL IMAC-column, eluded at very low
imidazole concentrations. Since other unwanted proteins eluded simultaneously, the purity of the desired
proteins was deemed unacceptable in these elution fractions. This consequently lead to a lower protein
yield, as these fractions were not used in downstream puriﬁcation steps. It is believed that the early elution
was premature and not caused by a lack of afﬁnity for the column, but rather by overloading of the
IMAC-column.
Most domains eluded in multiple peaks at different imidazole concentrations, i.e. not all at once in a
single elution peak. This may be due to varying availability of the His6-tags. Due to buffer washing
between each peak, the desired protein was consequently diluted. This implied further loss of protein
because subsequent up-concentration was associated with protein loss. Future puriﬁcations should aim for
complete elutions contained in a single peak. This could be done by using columns with adequate binding
capacities and by limiting the number of imidazole gradients.
TEV cleaving was carried out for all proteins. In order to determine the most efﬁcient cleaving method
FdeCD0, FdeCD0D1 and YeeJD0 were cleaved in two different ways. It was found that TEV-cleaving
was best done during dialysis rather than in a still falcon tube with no stirring. Therefore, future cleavages
should be done during dialysis to minimize loss. The uncleaved portion of protein was not recovered and
thus lead to protein loss.
Reverse IMAC puriﬁcation was successfully carried out for all proteins. All proteins except InvD0
eluded as expected during the sample FT and initial washing with a low imidazole buffer of 10 mM. InvD0
however, did not elude before the high imidazole buffer was added to the column. The TEV cleavage of
InvD0 was shown to be successful on an SDS-PAGE. This means that only the intrinsic afﬁnity of InvD0
was responsible for binding to the nickel. Further it infers that InvD0 has a stronger intrinsic afﬁnity for
the nickel column than the other puriﬁed proteins. The adequate imidazole concentration for InvD0 elution
42
==PAGE:49==
may only be slightly higher than 10mM. This could be investigated in a future study, where rIMAC elution
is done with a gradual increase of imidazole concentration.
All puriﬁed proteins were deemed pure based on the SDS-PAGEs from rIMAC puriﬁcation. The
puriﬁed proteins were generally presented as clear single bands without smears or other bands. The purity
could have been further ensured by size-exclusion chromatography. However, a successful crystallization
of FdeCD0 substantiated the belief that the obtained purity was adequate for crystallization.
5.2
Thermostability measurements
The nanoDSF measurements indicated that the stability of IntD00 is inﬂuenced by the protein concentra-
tion. Therefore, all thermostability measurements were assumed to be sensitive to inaccuracies of protein
concentration. Two nanoDSF measurements showed slight discrepancies in unfolding temperatures, which
could be due to differences in protein concentration between samples. Inaccuracies in protein concentra-
tions may be attributed to uncertainties in nanodrop measurements annd general laboratory work. The
inaccuracies could perhaps be minimized if a bradford assay had been utilized to support the protein
concentrations found by the NanoDrop spectrophotometer.
Both nanoDSF and DSC measurements indicated that NaCl and KCl inﬂuence the stability of IntD00.
Higher salt concentrations caused increased unfolding temperatures and higher values of ∆H for the
unfolding peak. Thus, both techniques suggests that IntD00 is most stable in high salt concentrations.
The nanoDSF measurements revealed that IntD00 possibly unfolds in two steps. This was only
detectable in samples with low IntD00 concentrations, and only within physiologically relevant salt
concentrations, i.e below 200mM. It was further noted that the increase in salt concentration primarily
affected the ﬁrst unfolding step. At low salt concentrations (10 mM) this unfolding step occurred at
around 45-48 °C. The second unfolding temperature was around 62-63 °C. The ﬁrst unfolding temperature
increased by about 4°C upon a 40-50 mM increase in salt concentration. In contrary, the second unfolding
temperature stayed around 62-63 °C. A further increase in salt concentration caused the ﬁrst unfolding
step to disapear. This is most likely because the unfolding event occurs at a temperature very near the
second unfolding step. This implies that the two unfolding steps cannot be distinguished at high salt
concentrations. It also suggests that NaCl and KCl speciﬁcally stabilizes IntD00 before the ﬁrst un-folding
event. In summary, dramatic changes in unfolding temperature is mainly observed when both unfolding
steps are detectable.
As opposed to nanoDSF the DSC measurements did not yield two separate unfolding temperatures
for IntD00. Furthermore, the unfolding temperature was changed by less than 2 °C between the low salt
43
==PAGE:50==
(5mM) and high salt (400mM) samples. The "lack" of a second unfolding temperature could explain the
comparably small increase in unfolding temperature. It can be argued that the lack of a second unfolding
step is due to the high protein concentration (0.5 mg/mL) in DSC samples. The nanoDSF measurements
had shown that only low concentrations (0.05 and 0.1 mg/mL) displayed any indication of an intermediate
unfolding state.
The suggested presence of an intermediate unfolding state could be further investigated with additional
DSC measurements of samples with lower protein concentrations. Though, this necessitates that a sufﬁcient
DSC-signal can be obtained at these protein concentrations. Alternatively, circular dichroism spectroscopy
could also be used.
The nanoDSF experiments may further indicate that NaCl and KCl have different effects on the
two unfolding steps of IntD00. It appears that KCl, in comparison to NaCl, causes the ﬁrst unfolding to
occur over a smaller range of temperature increase. This could imply that KCl is less stabilizing than
NaCl, since the unfolding is more gradual in high NaCl concentrations. However, it should be noted
that the unfolding temperatures in KCl solutions generally are higher than in NaCl solutions of equal
concentration. Similarly, the recorded ∆H is generally higher for KCl than for NaCl. This could suggest
that KCl has a more stabilizing effect than NaCl at equal concentrations. However, it could also simply be
due to incorrect salt concentrations in the utilized buffers. Hence, additional experiments with new buffers
should be conducted.
In summary, although both salts increase IntD00 stability, they might do so in two different ways.
Another study have shown that high salt concentrations may have stabilizing effects on proteins by
decreasing the repulsive effects of charged surface groups [20]. Therefore, the overall stabilizing effects
of NaCl and KCl could simply be caused by the increase in ionic strength. The slightly different effects of
NaCl and KCl could perhaps be explained by the ability of IntD00 to bind sodium. It could be of interest
to disclose whether IntD00 also can bind potassium.
It could be hypothesized that if bound sodium or potassium contributes to IntD00 folding, then the
ion binding site ought to be shielded during transport of the unfolded Intimin through the periplasm.
A number of chaperones are believed to be important for avoiding folding and aggregation during the
periplasmic transport [8]. Future studies could involve binding assays of these known chaperones; SurA,
Skp, or DegP, which are all general periplasmic chaperones of E. coli [8]. One of these chaperones may
bind in the proximity of the ion binding site. This could indicate that chaperones are needed to shield the
ion-site in order to avoid premature protein folding.

Crystals

homologue of IntD00, thermostability measurements should be done. This could clarify whether the
ﬁndings of IntD00 also are applicable for FdeCD0 as well as other putative IntD00-like domains.
Due to issues with solving the InvD0 structure and statistical issues with the solved FdeCD0 structure,
both crystals could perhaps be improved. In order to improve crystal formation different variations
of precipitant composition could be attempted. The screening conditions that yielded crystals would
preferably be used as the foundation for ﬁnding the optimal precipitant composition. YeeJD0 yielded
micro crystals in one LMB screen. This screen could perhaps be used as a basis for YeeJD0 crystal
optimization. A suggestion of future optimization experiments are shown in Figure 9, in the appendix A.6.
All other proteins, InvD0D1, FdeCD0D1 and YeeJD0D1 did not form crystals and were largely
presented as clear drops without precipitation. This could be attributed to inadequate protein concen-
trations. Future crystallization experiments with these proteins could therefore be carried out at protein
concentrations of more than 40 mg/mL.

6
Conclusion
In this thesis, the juncture domain D00 from Intimin (IntD00) was investigated as a continuation of a
previous study conducted by J. weikum et al. [28]. This thesis aimed to contribute to the elucidation
of two different hypothesis; namely, that sodium can increase the folding energy of IntD00 and that an
IntD00-like structure is conserved in many inverse autotransporters.
IntD00 was biophysically characterized through thermostability measurements, which were conducted
with nanoDSF and DSC. The inﬂuence of sodium on IntD00, with respect to stability, was compared
to that of potassium. It was found that IntD00 is inﬂuenced similarly by NaCl and KCl. An increase in
either of the two salts results in higher unfolding temperatures and greater ∆H during thermal unfolding.
This suggests that both NaCl and KCl increases the stability and thus the folding energy of IntD00. Nan-
oDSF measurements also indicated that IntD00 may unfold in two steps. The measurements may further
suggest that NaCl and KCl speciﬁcally inﬂuences the ﬁrst unfolding event. It is yet unknown whether
the stabilizing effects of NaCl and KCl are caused by ion binding or simply imposed by high ionic strength.
It was shown that putative IntD00-like domains InvD0, FdeCD0 and YeeJD0 can be expressed in E. coli
and puriﬁed with IMAC. The double domains InvD0D1, FdeCD0D1 and YeeJD0D1 were also successfully
expressed and puriﬁed. Crystallization experiments were set up of for the above mentioned proteins. Crys-
tal formations were achieved for InvD0 and FdeCD0, but only FdeCD0 yielded good structural data. The
obtained FdeCD0 crystal allows for future structural comparison to IntD00. This could be the ﬁrst step to-
wards elucidating whether the structure of the IntD00 domain is conserved among inverse autotransporters.
The present study cannot conﬁrm nor deny the hypothesis stating that bound sodium can contribute to
the folding and transport of IntD00 through the outer membrane. Rather, this study indicates that NaCl
as well as KCl stabilizes the IntD00 native fold. The possible importance of the sodium binding site,
could be emphasized if other putative D00-like domains also contain this sodium binding site. Future
research should aim to compare other putative IntD00 domains with IntD00 through crystallization and
thermostability measurements.
