Abstract
Epilepsy is typically diagnosed by performing electroencephalography (EEG), a non-invasive
test that measures and records electrical activity in the brain. EEG is expensive as re-
quires the use of specialized medical equipment by trained professionals and neurologists.
However, the company BrainCapture is developing an affordable and mobile EEG record-
ing device that can be used with a mobile application, facilitating EEG data collection by
non-expert users.
One of the main challenges in EEG is the presence of artifacts, such as eye blinks or mus-
cle movements that can interfere with the EEG signal making it difficult to further interpret
the EEG data. Traditional artifact detection methods rely on manual inspection, which
can be time-consuming and subjective. Hence, given that BrainCapture EEG recordings
are conducted by untrained users, there is a need for an automatic method for artifact
detection during these recordings.
Over the last past years, there have been large research on the development of algorithms
for automated handling of artifacts, with a focus on deep learning-based models. Herein,
an evaluation of some of these methods is carried out in order to study the feasibility of
using these artifact detection algorithms into the BrainCapture framework.
The models are trained on the largest publicly available EEG artifact dataset, the TUH
EEG Artifact Corpus, and then transfer learning is applied on BrainCapture EEG record-
ings. The task mainly consists of a categorical classification of 6 artifact classes from
time-domain EEG data. Some preprocessing steps are applied to the data with the aim
of improving the performance of the models.
The results of this thesis suggest that deep learning models, such as DSCNN, are promis-
ing to be used for artifact detection within BrainCapture. DSCNN has demonstrated an
artifact detection rate of 80,90% and classification accuracy of 72,65%, averaged across
10 subjects and 6 artifact classes using leave-one-subject-out cross-validation. Addition-
ally, it has been demonstrated that its computational cost is feasible to be run in real-time
on a mobile device, which indicates that DSCNN could be used as a starting point for an
artifact detection method in BrainCapture.

Introduction
Epilepsy is a chronic brain disorder that causes recurring, unprovoked seizures, which
are brief episodes of abnormal electrical activity in the brain that can temporarily affect
its functioning. Seizures can have several symptoms depending on the part of the brain
affected. It affects around 50 million people worldwide, being one of the most common
neurological disorders in the world. About 80% of people suffering from epilepsy live in
low-and-middle-income countries (LMIC).
Treatment for epilepsy can help patients to have fewer or even become seizure-free by
taking anti-epileptic medication. It is estimated that up to 70% of people living with epilepsy
could live seizure-free if properly diagnosed and treated. However, according to the World
Health Organization (WHO) , around 75% of people with epilepsy in LMIC do not get the
treatment they need, even though the medicine is affordable [1].
Epilepsy can be diagnosed by a neurologist through electroencephalogram (EEG) tests,
which records and measures parts of the electrical brain activity and can show unusual
patterns of brain activity that happens in people suffering from epilepsy. To perform an
effective diagnose, expensive EGG machines and experienced professionals are needed.
However, LMIC have a significant lack of both things and, consequently, there is a gap to
cover in the required medical resources to diagnose.
Therefore, DTU spinout BrainCapture is developing an affordable and portable EEG equip-
ment that allows EEG recording from a mobile application to be used by untrained health-
care professionals.
One of the challenges in EEG recordings is the presence of artifacts that come from
sources such as eye blinks and muscle movements and contaminate the acquired sig-
nals significantly. These are normally detected by trained professionals taking the EEG
recordings, and used to give feedback to the patient to avoid the activity causing them.
This is specially challenging in the BrainCapture approach, where examinations at LMIC
are moved to local health centers and performed under non-optimal conditions by un-
trained staff. Moreover, the hardware used by the BrainCapture’s device to record EEG
signals, is simpler than the clinically-proved equipment used in hospitals and thus, the
collected data is more noisy and has a lower signal-to-noise ratio.
To overcome this challenge, there is a need to ensure the quality of EEG recordings while
the patient is being tested with the BrainCapture’s device, which could be achieved by
means of an algorithm that could automatically detect potential artifacts.
1.1
Electroencephalography
An electroencephalogram (EEG) is a non-invasive test that measures and records elec-
trical activity in the brain. EEG is one of the main diagnostic tests for epilepsy and other
brain disorders. It can show changes in brain activity that might be useful for identifying
mental diseases.
The brain electrical activity is recorded by attaching electrodes to the scalp of the patient,
which measure voltage differences between different points on the head. These elec-
trodes are connected to an amplifier that amplifies the brain waves and sends them to a
computer for recording.

EEG signals
The human brain is divided into four lobes: Frontal, Parietal, Temporal and Occipital. The
first is responsible for executive functions, such as moving, planning, problem solving and
making decisions. The Parietal integrates sensory information from different parts of the
body, including touch, temperature and pain. The Temporal lobe also contains regions
dedicated to processing sensory information, specially for hearing, language, emotion,
and creating memories. Lastly, the Occipital lobe processes visual information and per-

and gamma, each of them corresponding to different behaviour and mental states of the

1.1.2
EEG procedure
EEG recordings are usually conducted in hospitals by an EEG technologist. First, the tech-
nologist places the electrodes on the patient’s scalp. It is important that the electrodes are

nologist might scrub on each position with abrasive cream before placing the electrodes,
which improves the quality of the recordings.
The electrodes used for EEG are allocated on the scalp according to different international
standards. The most widely used arrangement of electrodes in EEG is the International
10-20 system. In this system, 21 electrodes are situated on the scalp as seen in Figure
1.3, where the distance between electrodes is either 10% or 20% of the total distance

O-Occipital and A-Auricular). The midline region is labeled with ’z’. Odd numbers indicate
the left hemisphere and even numbers are used to indicate right hemisphere [4].
The signals recorded by each electrode are then displayed on an EEG recording as dif-
ferent waveforms, called channels.

Other electrode configurations are extensions of the original standard 10-20 system, such
as 10-10, see Figure 1.4. They differ from each other in the number of channels, which
increases from 21 to 74. In order to account for an increased number of electrodes, the
number of locations have to be extended in all directions to keep an even spread of data
extracted from the patient’s scalp. However, 10-10 system is not as widely used as the
10-20 system.
An usual recording can last between 20 and 40 minutes and it takes about the same
amount of time to prepare for the recording, so the entire procedure usually takes 1 to 1½
hours. During this time, the technologist can ask the patient to perform several tasks such
as open and close their eyes repeatedly, look at flashing lights, breath rapidly or deeply,
relax, etc.
Meanwhile, the technologist is watching the signal in real-time and is able to recognize
any patterns in the EEG signal that can alter an EEG recording, such as movements, eye
blinking, etc. The aforementioned artifacts can create changes in signal that might hide
epileptic discharges and cause misinterpretation of the underlying neural information for
the diagnose. They are normally manually annotated on the signal by the technologist.

1.1.3
EEG artifacts
As mentioned previously, one of the commonly faced problems in EEG recordings is the
presence of artifacts that can contaminate the acquired signals significantly and might
affect the analysis and processing of EEG [5].
These artifacts can be caused by different sources: internal and external [6].
• Internal: due to phsysiological activities from the patient, such as eye movement,
muscular activity, etc.
• External: due to environmental inferences such as the power line and other elec-
tromagnetic noise from nearby, and instrumentation and recording equipment. For
instance, electrode pop or cable movement.

1.2
BrainCapture
BrainCapture approach for EEG scans is based on an affordable and portable electrode
cap and a Bluetooth amplifier that is connected to a mobile app that guides non-expert
users to perform EEG recordings. The final aim is to collect clean EEG data that can be
sent to a cloud and accessed by a neurologist to make the epilepsy diagnose remotely.
Here, the main problem is the lack of EEG experts performing the scans in local health
centers and uncontrolled environments. The chances to see artifacts are higher and it is
more challenging to detect and avoid them.
On the other hand, BrainCapture EEG data is specially noisy, as the hardware used for the
amplifier setup is simpler than the one used in EEG machines in most common hospitals.
Therefore, it is even more relevant to consider an artifact detection algorithm to ensure a
better quality of the collected EEG data.
Identifying and classifying these artifacts during the recording would allow users perform-
ing the EEG recordings to instruct patients to avoid the activity causing them, thus avoiding
interference in the signal enabling higher quality EEG data collection.

1.3
Thesis objective
In summary, the long standing problem of artifacts detection in EEG recordings is also
present in the BrainCapture’s approach. The complexity in BrainCapture is even higher
because of the noise in EEG data and the recordings being performed under non-optimal
conditions and by non-expert users. Consequently, in order to ensure good quality EEG
data collection that can be used for epilepsy diagnosis, there is a need to automatically
identify artifacts in real-time during the recordings.
On the other hand, the development of artificial intelligence approaches, such as machine
learning techniques, have had a revolutionary impact in many scientific areas. They have
shown the ability to learn from data and make predictions for a wide range of applications.
The use of these methods, from traditional machine learning approaches to deep learning
networks, such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) and Long Short-Term Memory
(LSTM), is also increasing within the EEG field and has the potential to be used for EEG
artifact detection.
Therefore, the aim of this thesis is to explore and evaluate current machine learning meth-
ods used for artifact detection, with a particular focus on deep learning techniques, and
study the feasibility of applying them within the BrainCapture framework.

Related work
For several decades, it has been claimed that progress in new technologies and medical
research are transforming the way to understand the mechanisms of our body’s behavior
in order to prevent, early diagnose and cure overcoming diseases. And without excep-
tion, in psychiatric research many progress has been done to transform the way mental
disorders are diagnosed, treated and managed.
One of these emerging technologies is EEG, which has been widely used for the scien-
tific analysis of neuropsychiatric disorders. However, EEG has a low signal-to-noise ratio
which limits its applications. This is mostly due to a variety of artifacts such as movement
artifacts, physiological artifacts and instrument artifacts. Therefore, even if EEG data is af-
fordable to collect, it is challenging to use in practice as it requires previous preprocessing
steps to detect and remove these artifacts [8].
Currently, artifact detection in EEG is performed by trained professionals who can meticu-
lously inspect and manually annotate EEG signals. This is a time-consuming and subjec-
tive process. Over the last past years, there have been large research on the development
of algorithms for automated handling of artifacts [9]. These techniques range from various
statistical to supervised and unsupervised machine learning approaches [10]. Neverthe-
less, there is still no universal solution available for this particular problem.
As of now, one of the most frequently used techniques is the Independent Component
Analysis (ICA), based on signal decomposition. It attemps to decompose various source
signals that are statistically independent and non-gaussian from a set of recordings in
which the source signals were mixed together in unknown ratios. For instance, the elec-
trodes in EEG simultaneously record a mixture of various brain activity (blinks, heartbeat,
muscular activity, etc.) and external contributions. As long as these signals are indepen-
dent and non-gaussian, ICA can usually separate and unmix the signals into a weighted
linear mixture of different signal sources, such as eye blinks or heartbeat. This way, it
provides a representation of the statistical sources contributing to the mixed recorded
signal.
In [11], Morup and colleagues used ICA for automatically identify multiple artifact classes
in EEG. They used multinomial regression to classify independent components of EEG
data using features from the temporal, spectral and spatial domain and proved that au-
tomatic separation of multiple artifact classes is possible, achieving a balanced accuracy
of 94.8%. They classified the artifacts into 6 classes: eye blinks, heartbeat, lateral eye
movements, muscle, and mixed neural and artifactual activity. Their classifier generalized
well across subject within studies but across-study generalitazion was more challenging.
Moreover, the use of artificial intelligence approaches is rapidly growing within various sci-
entific fields, such as computer vision and speech recognizing. Particularly, Convolutional
Neural Networks (CNN) have successfully been applied to perform automatic feature ex-
traction and classification tasks. Consequently, there is an increasing focus on using
machine learning algorithms to automatically detect artifacts in the EEG field.
For this thesis, a literature review of current studies about machine learning methods for
artifact detection is conducted following the PRISMA methodology [12]. The flow diagram
of the review process is shown in Figure 2.1. Special attention was given to studies that

gram (EEG) data by qualitative evaluating 30 studies that used either Convolutional Nueral

Their conclusion confirms the successful use of deep learning techniques on EEG data,
even though it can depend on the correct usage of testing procedures and the sufficient
characterization of the clinical populations used as subjects.

Problem statement
The previous literature research shows that many techniques for automatic artifact detec-
tion have been proposed recently. From traditional machine learning approaches such
as SVM, XGBoost and Independent Component Analysis to deep learning algorithms, for
instance Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), have demonstrated a good performance
in EEG artifact detection. Such good performances have been achieved by evaluating
the models on good quality EEG data recorded in hospitals, under optimal conditions.
Therefore, this study focuses on evaluating how the current artifact detection methods
can be used within the BrainCapture framework, which presents several challenges:
First of all, as mentioned previously, the BrainCapture data is more noisy because the
hardware used for recording the signal is simpler than in the hospitals. Additionally, the
EEG recordings are performed under non-optimal conditions and by non-expert users.
Second, it is important to consider how the performance of these methods is evaluated.
As it consists of a classification task dealing with medical data that will lead to a clinical
diagnose, the evaluation metrics should be carefully considered. In this case, it is relevant
to contemplate a combination of metrics that reflect both the model’s ability to detect
artifacts accurately and its ability to avoid false positive.
Last but not least, notice that the BrainCapture approach runs on a mobile phone device,
without internet connection and limited computing power, meaning that the proposed arti-
fact detection method needs to be adapted to the corresponding computational conditions
and limitations.
3.1
Research questions
Addressing the challenges presented to achieve the thesis objective, leads to the following
research questions:
• How can current machine learning algorithms – which are mostly used for high-
quality EEG data recorded in hospitals – perform with BrainCapture data?
• How should the performance of the models be evaluated considering the BrainCap-
ture case?
• Could these models be implemented and run on a mobile phone device?

4
Methods
The following section describes the procedure followed in this thesis to accomplish EEG
artifact identification within the BrainCapture approach using machine learning models.
The task mainly consists of training and testing the models to detect and classify multiple
artifacts, and assess the feasibility of integrating these models into the BrainCapture mo-
bile application. Therefore, the following sections cover specific aspects involved in this
task.
Firstly, it introduces the datasets used for training and testing the artifact detection models.
Secondly, it presents the preprocessing pipeline applied to the EEG data before being
input to the models. Then, provides a description of the machine learning models used
and their architecture. Finally, it explains the metrics and model selection approach used
to evaluate and compare their performance.
4.1
Datasets
In order to build successful machine learning models for artifact detection in EEG, large
amounts of data are required for training and testing the models. Despite EEG being
the most common test for mental disorders and considering the large number of EEG
recordings performed annually in clinical settings around the world, there is a lack of
public EEG data available to the research community, as it consists of medical and highly
sensitive data and its access is often restricted.
On the other hand, as mentioned previously, EEG data is traditionally annotated manually
by EEG technicians, which is very time consuming. For this reason, it becomes even
harder to find public annotated EEG artifact data that can be used for training supervised
machine learning methods.
BrainCapture is collecting some EEG recordings but, are still not enough to train robust
machine learning models. Consequently, this thesis used two main datasets: a public
EEG artifact dataset from Temple University Hospital and a small dataset of EEG record-
ings provided by BrainCapture.
4.1.1
The TUH EEG Artifact Corpus
The Temple University Hospital EEG data corpus [14] is the world’s largest open source
corpus of EEG data. It presents a subset of EEG artifact data, the TU Artifact Corpus
(TUAR), which contains 310 EEG files. In each recording, every artifact was annotated
by students who were trained in artifact recognition and followed an agreement in order
to ensure uniformity between annotations [18]. The files were recorded from 213 patients
over 14 years resulting on a total of 359.936 seconds of EEG data.
The artifacts present in the dataset are classified in five different classes:
• Electrode (elec): it is caused by electrode movement and interference or due to a
poor connection of the electrodes. It is shown in the EEG as high amplitude slow
waves.
• Eye movement (eyem): consequence of patient’s eye movement. It is seen as a
spike-like waveform specially on the frontal polar electrodes.
• Muscle (musc): corresponds to patient’s movement, it has high frequency and sharp
waves.

• Shivering (shiv): occurs when the patient shivers and is seen as sustained sharp
wave in all or most of the EEG channels. Shivering is also an specific subset of
muscle artifacts.
These artifacts can occur simultaneously, therefore other labels were used for encoding
overlapping events, such as eye movement and chewing (eyem_chew), eye movement
and shivering (eyem_shiv) and chewing and shivering (chew_shiv). Additionally, non-
artifact data was annotated as none.
The recordings in the corpus use different EEG channels, some use 30 channels, while
others use 32 and 36 and the sampling rate ranges between 250 Hz and 1000 Hz.
For this work, the dataset was filtered according the following conditions:
• Picked only the 21 EEG channels common in both the TUAR and BrainCapture EEG
setups.
• Excluded overlapping artifacts, such as eyem_shiv, to simplify the learning process
of the model and avoid confusion.
• Used only the recordings with a sampling rate higher or equal than 256 Hz, resulting
in 102 recordings.
4.1.2
The BrainCapture recordings
BrainCapture provided EEG recordings in two stages. First, an unnanotated set of 14
recordings collected with the BrainCapture device. This data is not annotated, and thus,
was used in later stages for visually evaluating the model predictions. This subset of
recordings is referred as Unnanotated BC dataset.
Second, BrainCapture shared a set of recordings from 16 subjects performed at the
Filadelfia hospital, a Danish hospital highly specialised in the field of diagnostics and treat-
ment of epilepsy. These EEG recordings were collected simultaneously by both the Brain-
Capture device and the hospital’s equipment, with the aim of data comparison. As a result,
two recordings were obtained from each subject: a good quality recording captured with
the hospital’s equipment and the second recorded with the BrainCapture’s device. The
subset of recordings obtained by the BrainCapture device is named BC dataset and the
one recorded by the hospital’s equipment is referred as the Filadelfia dataset.
A quality analysis was performed to evaluate the different recordings performed in Filadelfia
by the BrainCapture’s device, see more detail in Appendix A. It was observed that some
recordings had noisy EEG channels and also ECG leakage, meaning electrical signals in
the heart were interfering with EEG. This was inspected by visualizing the raw EEG and
the Power Spectral Density (PSD) for all channels in each recording, which measures the
signal’s power over frequency and can provide useful information about the distribution
of power across different frequencies in the EEG signals.
As a result of this analysis, 4 recordings were excluded due to poor quality, and two addi-
tional recordings were excluded due to having different annotations. Therefore, the final
annotated datasets used for this thesis consisted of 10 EEG recordings from the different
subjects that participated in the recording sessions at Filadelfia hospital.

the nurse taking the recording with the hospital’s equipment manually annotated artifacts,
as it is done in a real EEG recording scenarios.
The protocol for the recordings is show in Appendix B and consisted of four different
phases: resting stage, artifact exercises, focus stage and electrode pops. In the first
resting stage, the users were asked to relax with their eyes closed during 10 minutes, and
then to relax looking straight forward with their eyes open during 2 minutes. The next part
of the recordings consisted of performing several artifacts for 30 seconds each: eye blinks,
lateral eye movement, lateral head movement, chewing, jaw clenching and shivering.
Then, the users were asked to relax again with their eyes open for 2 minutes and repeat
again the artifacts exercises. Continuously, relax during 2 minutes with the eyes closed.
The following stage consisted of a focusing task in which the users were asked to perform
a mental arithmetic exercise: starting from 200, counting back by step of 7. Lastly, some
electrodes were intentionally moved to generate different electrode pops during around 3
minutes. In total, the recordings last around 30 minutes.
This work did not take into account the EEG signal recorded between exercises, as it was
deemed to be noisy and irrelevant because the participants were moving, talking, etc.
The recordings used the same setup with the 10-20 EEG montage, had 32 EEG channels,
and were recorded at a sampling rate of 256 Hz. To align the BrainCapture’s recordings
to the TUAR recordings, this thesis only used the 21 common EEG channels used in both
the BrainCapture and the TUAR setups.

4.2
Preprocessing
Before using the previously introduced datasets for training the machine learning models,
some preprocessing steps were applied to the raw EEG data, see Figure 4.11. For the
implementation of most of these steps, the Python package MNE was used [19]. It is an
open-source package for exploring, visualizing, and analyzing human neurophysiological
data, such as EEG. This section covers the preprocessing pipeline applied in this thesis.
Firstly, as mentioned before, only the 21 EEG channels defined in Figure 4.2 were picked
and their positions were set according to the 10-20 montage. Secondly, the common
signal processing pipeline for EEG data was applied [20] [21]. Figure 4.10, shows an
example of the power spectral density of 21 channels for a BC recording in each signal
processing step. Notice that the PSD of raw EEG data shows highest power at the lowest
frequencies, and drops off with increasing frequency.
As mentioned previously, a typical external artifact seen in most of EEG recordings is the
power line noise. It is caused by the electrical interference from the power grid. It is seen
as a peak at 50 Hz, corresponding to the frequency of the power line in Europe. One
of the methods to remove this external artifact is notch filtering. This involves applying a
band-stop filter, with a narrow band centered at the power line frequency, to remove the
power line noise from the EEG signal. Therefore, a notch filter at 50 Hz and 60 Hz was
applied. Notice that the power line noise can be seen in different frequencies, 50 Hz in
Europe and 60 Hz in the United States.

As mentioned previously, human brain activity is comprised in a range of frequencies typi-
cally from 1 to 40 Hz. For this reason, bandpass filtering was applied to preserve the band
of frequencies between a low-pass and a high-pass cutoffs, in this case chosen to be 0.2
and 40 Hz respectively, as commonly used in related work such as [15]. Nonetheless,
sources of noise can also be seen within these frequencies, such as head movement or
electrode wires in lower frequencies and muscle contractions or electromagnetic interfer-
ence in higher frequencies.
Both notch and bandpass filters were FIR filters with a Hamming window, being the default
paramaters in MNE’s, which have already been set to be optimal for most EEG data. After
applying this filtering, the EEG data was referenced to the average by subtracting to each
channel the average potential of all channels in each sample.
Thirdly, the annotations were set into the EEG data. In this step. the BrainCapture anno-
tations were translated into the TUAR artifact labels (eyem, chew, shiv, eyem, musc and
none), as shown in Figure 4.4.
Next, the EEG data was divided into epochs, equal-duration chunks of the EEG signal.
The duration of the epochs was set to be 2 seconds with a 50% overlap between epochs.
This value was considered to be enough time to represent both artifact and non-artifact
data, as the average artifact duration in the TUAR recordings was around 7 seconds.
Subsequently, in order to avoid ambiguity when training the model, some epochs were
excluded. First, the epochs that are annotated as combined artifacts in TUAR, such as
eyem_musc were excluded to avoid confusion detecting different kind of artifacts. Second,
if an artifact occurred in less than 50% of the epoch duration, these were also excluded
because were considered ambiguous epochs neither showing artifact or non-artifact data.
Finally, the data was resampled to 256 Hz and standardized. Much attention was drawn
to normalization, as it is a very relevant preprocessing step in machine learning. Specially
when train and test sets follow different probability distributions, this could lead the models
to poor generalisation performance. This issue was even more important in the EEG
context, where EEG signals are highly non-stationary both over time and between various
subjects.
In this project, EEG was first scaled by a factor of 106, to extend the sensitivity of machine
learning models, and then standardized channel-wise by subtracting the mean and divid-
ing by the standard deviation. This resulted in the data having a mean of 0 and a standard
deviation of 1. This approach was chosen because it allows to identify patterns of brain
activity that are consistent across multiple channels and for identifying specific channels
that may be particularly affected by certain artifacts.

4.3
Feature engineering
Once raw EEG has gone through the preprocessing pipeline, then feature engineering
was applied. This section explains the feature engineering used in this thesis.
Feature engineering consists of transforming data into specific descriptive features that
can be used to improve the performance of machine learning models. In EEG, features
can be typically seen in different domains knowledge: time, frequency and spatial do-
mains. In this work, time and frequency domain were used as explained in the following
sections.
Besides, artifact labels such as eyem and musc were translated into categorical variables
that could be feed to machine learning algorithms by using one hot encoding. This method
represents each categorical label as a binary vector, where each column corresponds
to a label and all values are 0 except for the value in the column corresponding to the
categorical label, which is 1. For instance, to represent eyem, the vector would have a 1
in the eyem column and 0 in all other columns.
4.3.1
Time domain
In this case, the data consisted of time domain EEG epochs resulting from the preprocess-
ing pipeline, where each epoch was represented as channels by time samples within the
epoch duration. Then, these epochs were concatenated resulting in three-dimensional
data of size (#epochs, #channels, #samples). The number of EEG channels was 21 and
one epoch had 512 samples, as epochs had 2 seconds duration at a sample frequency
of 265 Hz, see Figure 4.12.
Most of the related work, such as [5] and [16], achieved good artifact classification per-
formance by only using time domain data. For this reason, this thesis focused on accom-
plishing similar results with time domain data.
Figure 4.12: Time domain EEG input data. The figure represents the three-dimensional input data
to the models where each epoch is represented by 21 channels and 512 samples (2 seconds
duration). Then, all the epochs are stacked together.
4.3.2
Time-frequency domain
Another widely used approach for dealing with EEG is to use time-frequency domain fea-
tures, as it provides information about a signal both in the time and frequency domains
simultaneously.
One of the most frequently used methods for analyzing non-stationary time series data,
such as the neuroelectrical brain activity, in the time-frequency domain is Morlet wavelets

[23]. Spectrotemporal analysis is done through spectrograms, which visually represent
the spectrum of frequencies of a signal as it varies with time. Therefore, they show the
power of the signal in the time and frequency domain.
In this project, for each epoch, the spectrogram of each channel was computed by us-
ing Morlet wavelets in the frequencies from 0.2 to 40 Hz, see right plot in Figure 4.13.
Subsequently, these were concatenated resulting in images of size (#freqs × #channels,
#samples). Finally, the time-frequency images created for each epoch were stacked to-
gether and thus, the resulting data was also three-dimensional with the size of (#epochs,
#freqs × #channels, #samples), see left plot in Figure 4.13. This approach was inspired
by what was done in [24].

Models
This section presents the machine learning models employed in this thesis, which were
chosen based on the findings from the literature research. The research evaluated which
models in previous work provided the best compromise between artifact classification
accuracy and computational cost. As a result, the most appropriate models to address
the artifact detection task within the BrainCapture framework were found to be EEGNet,

Moreover, in [13] they also achieved successful artifact classification from the TUAR
dataset using traditional machine learning methods, such as Extreme Gradient Boost
(XGBoost). So far, it is one of the most used supervised machine learning algorithms,
due to its efficiency, easy implementation and successful accuracy. Therefore, XGBoost
was also evaluated in early stages of this thesis for comparison with the deep learning
models.
These models were implemented in Python using Keras [27], TensorFlow [28] and XG-
Boost [29].
4.4.1
XGBoost
XGBoost [29] is an open-source library that provides an improved implementation of the
Gradient Boost machine learning algorithm.
Gradient boosting consists of combining weak models, typically decision trees, and train-
ing them iteratively in a way that new models are trained to correct the errors made by
the previous ones. Therefore, misclassified records are given a greater weight to be cor-
rectly predicted in later models. These weak models are later combined, or also called
ensembled, to produce a single stronger learner.
XGBoost can not handle three-dimensional data, consequently, in order to implement
XGBoost in this thesis, the time-domain epochs data explained in previous sections were

as an array of length: number of channels by number of samples in one epoch. Finally,
all epoch arrays were concatenated together forming a two-dimensional matrix of size:
number of epochs by the epoch array length.

4.4.2
EEGNet
EEGNet is an EEG-specific deep learning model based on depthwise and separable con-
volutions for EEG feature extraction, presented in [30]. It has demonstrated to be capable
to learn a wide variety of EEG features and, in [17] it was used for artifact detection from
multi-channel EEG data.
Depthwise separable convolutions are a combination of a separable convolution and a
pointwise convolution. The main difference between regular 2D convolutions and a sepa-
rable convolutions is that the last, treat input channels differently, meaning that each input
channel is convoluted with a different filter. Then, the output feature maps are stacked
together and the pointwise convolution is applied.
Figure 4.22 shows the conventions for the depthwise separable convolution. Given that
the input data has M channels, M different filters of size Dk ×Dk ×1 are convoluted in the
first depthwise convolution. The resulting M feature maps, are then stack together with a
size of Dp × Dp × M, where Dp is the resulting size of convoluting Df with Dk. Then, a
pointwise convolution of size 1 × 1 × M is applied. If N filters are used, then the output is
of size Dp × Dp × N.
Figure 4.16: Depthwise Separable Convolution. Adapted from [5].
The main benefit of using depthwise convolutions is reducing the number of trainable
parameters to fit. On the other hand, using them in the EEG context, provides a way to
learn spatial filters for each temporal filter, thus enabling efficient extraction of frequency-
specific spatial filters.
The architecture of the EEGNet model consists of two blocks, as illustrated in Figure 4.17.
In this thesis, the input to the EEGNet network were the EEG epochs in the time domain,
of size (#epochs, #channels, #samples).
The first block includes a 2D convolutional layer with F1 filters of size (1, kernel_lenght),
in which the kernel_lenght was chosen to be half of the sampling rate, 128 in this case.
This is followed by a depthwise convolution layer with a filter size of (#channels, 1) and
a depth of 2. The depth parameter controls the number of spatial filters to learn from
each feature map. The output is then passed through a batch normalization layer and
a rectified linear (ReLu) activation function. Batch normalization is typically applied to
standardize the inputs to a layer and reduce the number of training epochs required to
28
Design of an IT system based on Deep Learning for EEG artifact detection with BrainCapture
==PAGE:37==
train deep networks. Next, an average pooling layer is applied to reduce the feature size
and finally, dropout is used to help regularize the model and prevent over-fitting.
In the second block, a separable convolution of F2 filters of size (1, 16) is applied again
followed by batch normalization, a ReLu activation layer, average pooling layer of size (1,
8) and dropout. Finally, the classification layers consist of a fully-connected layer of N
units with softmax activation function, being N the number of artifact classes to identify, 6
in this case. F1 is chosen to be 8 and F2 is determined by F1 × D, where D is the depth
of the filter, in this case 16.
The model was fit using the Adam optimizer to minimize the categorical cross-entropy
loss function and had 4.598 parameters. This model is publicly available at https://github.
com/vlawhern/arl-eegmodels. The model architecture is further shown in Appendix D.
Figure 4.17: Original EEGNet architecture from [30]. In their case, the EEG data had 64 channels
instead.
As the training datasets are imbalanced, meaning that the majority of observations are
non-artifact data, different weights were applied to each artifact label during training. This
way, the training algorithm considers the distribution of the artifact classes, giving more
importance to the missclassifications made by the minority class. This approach helps the
algorithm to balance the impact of different classes and prevent bias towards the majority
class, which can help to improve the performance of the model on minority classes.
4.4.3
DSCNN
In [5], they proposed another network architecture based on depthwise separable convo-
lutions that is slightly different from EEGNet, see Figure 4.18. This model also operates
on the time domain EEG data.
It consistes of a first traditional convolutional layer with F1 filters of size (#channels, 4).
This ensures that adequate spatial filter is learned in the first layer. Then, a depthwise
convolution is used with filter size (1, 32) and depth multiplier of 1. This is followed by an
average pooling layer with pool size of (1, 32). Next, a separable convolution is applied
with F2 filters of size (1, 32) and again followed by an average pooling layer with size (1, 8).
All the layers are followed by batch normalization and a ReLu activation function. Finally,
the output of the last average pooling layer is flattened into a single vector which is input
to a fully connected layer with N units, N being 6 again. More detail of the architecture is
shown in Appendix D. In this case, both F1 and F2 are chosen to be 16.
The model was trained using the Adam optimizer and the categorical cross-entropy loss
function. It had 2.918 parameters, almost half of the parameters used by EEGNet. Artifact
classes weights were also applied to each kind of artifact to consider the imbalanced data
during training.

The main difference between the EEGNet and the DSCNN model is in how they treat the

4.4.4
Transfer learning
One of the main challenges in machine learning, is that it requires large amounts of data
to train models that can achieve a good performance. This can be especially difficult when
working with EEG data, as there is few data available. Therefore, transfer learning can
be used.
Transfer learning is a machine learning technique where a model trained for solving one
task is then reused and applied to a different but related task. This way it transfers the
knowledge and features learnt from previous tasks, improving the learning process on the
new task. This approach reduces the resources and amount of data required to train new
models and typically provides better predictive performance.
In this case, in order to overcome the limited size of the BrainCapture dataset, transfer
learning was applied. The models were first trained with the larger TUAR dataset to learn
basic representations of EEG data and then, the last fully connected layers of the models,
used for solving the classification task, were retrained with the BrainCapture dataset, as
shown in Figure 4.19. The resulting model, was expected to have a better performance
on detecting artifacts in the BrainCapture dataset.

type of classification task dealing with, whether to give the same or more importance to
some classes.

data being classified as artifacts, making it harder to obtain high-quality recordings.

• False Positive Rate: to measure the amount of non-artifact data incorrectly classified

• Weighted F1 score: to measure the class-wise performance rather than the overall

account the imbalance between classes, where the weights were determined by the
number of samples available in each class.
• Recall by artifact: to measure how the model was able to recognize each kind of

4.5.2
Leave-one-subject-out Cross Validation (LOSOCV)
Cross-validation (CV) is a technique used in machine learning for validating the models
efficiency and evaluating how well the model generalizes on unseen data, not used for
training. It is specially relevant when working with small datasets, as it provides a broader
overview of the model performance on new data.
EEG data has a high intersubject variability and high intrasubject stability, meaning that
data can be very different from subject to subject. This variability is probably due to dif-
ferent psychological and physiological characteristics of each subject. Furthermore, each
recording session can also vary due to the used equipment or recording environment, etc.
Therefore, it is hard to build models that have a good performance among recording ses-
sions and individuals. In order to have an accurate estimate of the performance of the
models in this thesis, the evaluation was done subject-dependently bu using LOSOCV.
In LOSOCV, the training set is split into K different smaller sets, called ’folds’, where K is
the number of different subjects in the dataset. Therefore, the training dataset is split into
different subjects. Subsequenty, separate subjects are used for training and testing.
LOSOCV was used for evaluating the models in this thesis. The models were trained on
K-1 subjects and tested on the complementary subject. This was repeated K times, for
all possible combinations until the model had been trained and tested in every fold. Then,
the performance measures reported at every K-fold were averaged to provide an overall
effectiveness. Although this process is computationally expensive it provides a reliable
and unbiased estimate of model performance.

5.1
Feasibility to be run on mobile phone
The final application of the previously presented machine learning models is their use
within the BrainCapture app for automatically detecting artifacts during EEG recordings.
For this reason, the two main requirements are:
• The models should be able to run on a mobile device.
• The models should make predictions in real-time.
For the first requisite, TensorFlow offers a framework called TensorFlow Lite, to deploy
machine learning models on mobile devices (iOS and Android). Hence, TensorFlow mod-
els can easily be converted to TensorFlow Lite and then load into a mobile or embedded
device.
On the other hand, identifying artifacts real-time implies that the models should be able to
process the data faster than it is acquired, otherwise there may be a delay in the detection
of artifacts. For instance, 2 seconds epochs should be processed in less than 2 seconds.
The models presented in this thesis were converted to TensorFlow Lite and evaluated in
a computer, in terms of CPU and GPU load and computational time in order to asses the
suitability of their integration into the BrainCapture app.
5.2
Artifacts notification system
Integrating the artifact detection algorithm into the BrainCapture’s app would allow the
non-expert users performing EEG scans to be notified in real-time of the artifacts causing
noise in the EEG signals. This way, the users can help the patients avoid movements or
actions that cause interference and ensure good quality EEG data collection which will
facilitate an accurate diagnose.
BrainCapture would train the operators by providing them basic knowledge on how to
make high quality EEG scans, emphasizing the importance of guiding the patient to relax
and continuously observing the patient, to detect any visible artifacts such as eye blinks,
body movements, or muscle activity. Having this artifact detection algorithm would help
operators to identify artifacts that are not easily visible and that could be overlooked. For
instance, eye ball movement when having the eyes closed, eyelid shivering or tensing the
jaw.
However, there are several aspects that should be considered before putting into practice
the artifact detection algorithms in BrainCapture.
First of all, it is worth to mention that the artifacts considered by the models are limited
to the ones observed in the training datasets, which are electrode pops, eye movement,
chewing, shivering, and muscle artifacts. Even though the model was trained with a vari-
ety of EEG data, it is possible that it might not be able to detect all possible artifacts that
Design of an IT system based on Deep Learning for EEG artifact detection with BrainCapture
35
==PAGE:44==
can appear in the recordings. Every patient is different and thus can do several actions
that could lead to artifacts that have never seen by the model before.
For instance, in EEG recordings for detecting epilepsy patients are often asked to hyper-
ventilate. This kind of action typically generates several muscle artifacts because patients
might be exaggerating their movements. However, this type of muscle artifacts are not
seen in the BrainCapture’s recordings used for training the model, and therefore the model
might not be able to identify them. For this reason, the models should be continuously
updated and retrained with more diverse annotated EEG data to ensure that it can detect
a wide range of artifacts.
Second, it is unlikely to completely avoid artifacts during EEG recordings, as small unin-
tended movements can generate artifacts such as coughing, sneezing and shudder. For
instance, eye blink artifacts are unavoidable and will be seen in all recordings as patients
can not be asked to stop blinking their eyes. To address this issue, a hierarchy of artifacts
should be established to determine which artifacts would be worth notifying to the users
taking the recording.
One way to establish this hierarchy could be to prioritize artifacts based on their potential to
affect the overall quality of the recording. For example, eye blinks and eye movements are
considered to be relatively low-impact artifacts, as are mainly seen on few EEG channels.
On the other hand, large body movements or muscle artifacts can have a more significant
impact on the recording.
Another fact to be considered could be to prioritize artifacts based on their frequency
and duration, so that the most frequent and long-lasting artifacts are more relevant to be
notified.
In conclusion, even if the proposed models could detect and identify artifacts, further con-
siderations should be made by giving more or less importance to the model predictions
before being notified to the users taking the EEG recordings depending on the type, fre-
quency and duration of the artifacts.

Discussion
Overall, this thesis has demonstrated that current state-of-the-art models used for EEG
artifact detection are promising to be used for identifying artifacts in BrainCapture EEG
data. However, this section aims to discuss the contributions of this study by comparing
its results with related work.
Moreover, the work conducted in this thesis leaves room for further enhancement and
research work. Consequently, this section also presents some limitations to the current
study that could be addressed in future work, such as further fine tunning of EEG features
and preprocessing and trying new and more complex models. Additionally, it discusses
how these deep learning models could be integrated into the BrainCapture’s application
and proposes the implementation of an active learning approach by incorporating experts
knowledge.
All this suggestions aim to improve the artifact detection performance so that the proposed
models can be successfully used by BrainCapture in real-world scenarios.
7.1
Comparison with related work
In this section, the results from the related work in [13] and [5] are compared and con-
trasted in order to provide context for the contributions of this research. However, it is
relevant mentioning that the related works discussed in this comparison used different
datasets, EEG features, artifact classes, data sampling rate, preprocessing steps and
model evaluation methods. These factors can have a significant impact on the perfor-
mance and applicability of the presented methods and thus it is important to take them
into consideration when comparing the results, see Table 7.1.
For example, in [13], Roy et al. also used the TUAR dataset, but their approach to an-
alyzing the EEG data was different from the method used in this thesis. They used the
entire TUAR dataset, whereas this thesis only worked with the recordings with a sampling
rate equal or higher than 256 Hz, 102 recordings, and excluded the EEG data annotated
as combined artifacts such as eyem_shiv. Moreover, they computed Fast Fourier Trans-
form (FFT), to convert the EEG data into the frequency domain, for each second of the
EEG data with 75% overlap, while this thesis used the data in the time domain. This fact,
challenges the comparison between the two studies, as the FFT can highlight different
aspects of the EEG data than the time-domain representation.
On the other hand, in [5] they also worked with the EEG data in the time-domain, but they
used an unknown dataset with 64 EEG channels and 10 different artifact classes: jaw
clenching, move jaw, blink eyes, eyes leftward, eyes rightward, raise eyebrows, rotate
head, shrung shoulders and rotate torso. Regarding, the data preprocessing they only
mention to have applied a highpass filter at 1 Hz.
Furthermore, the approach used to report the results of the models in the related work
is different from the one used in this thesis. Roy et al. evaluated their results by testing
their models on 20% of the TUAR dataset, where the segregation of the TUAR dataset
was done at a patient level, so that the results show how well their models can generalize
across patients. On the other hand, Mohit et al. reported their results by training and
testing the models with data from the same patient, showing intra-patient generalization.

7.2
Future work
This section presents the several potential areas for further research in using deep learn-
ing methods for artifact detection in the BrainCapture’s approach.
7.2.1
Fine tuning
First of all, it would be interesting to perform further hyper-parameter tuning by testing the
models with new parameters, for instance the EEG epoch duration and overlap. In this
thesis, it has been demonstrated that increasing the epoch duration from 2 to 4 seconds
improves the model performance. Therefore, testing additional combinations of epoch
duration and overlap could help to further optimize the model performance and provide a
deeper understanding of how these parameters influence the results.
However, increasing the EEG epoch duration in BC dataset was not an issue, as the data
consisted of sequential exercises of provoked artifacts during a certain period of time,
typically 30 seconds. This means that longer stretches of consistent artifacts could be
observed in the BC dataset. Even so, EEG artifacts in real-world clinical settings do not
look like this. For instance, a muscle movement can occur in shorter timeframes, such as
Design of an IT system based on Deep Learning for EEG artifact detection with BrainCapture
53
==PAGE:62==
milliseconds. Consequently, increasing the EEG epochs duration might lead to a worse
performance when detecting artifacts during an EEG recording for epilepsy diagnose.
Secondly, the preprocessing pipeline could also be adjusted. Even though this thesis
applied the most common preprocessing steps for EEG data, other variations can be
seen in other works. For example, the band pass filtering could be applied to a broader
range of frequencies, as sometimes artifacts can also be seen in frequencies higher than
40 Hz. Therefore, it would be interesting to try how the models perform using a wider
frequency range.
There is also a large discussion about normalization techniques for EEG data. In this
thesis data has been standardized channel wise by subtracting the mean and dividing by
the standard deviation. However, other methods could be tried in future work, such as
normalize data by subject, or based on the entire dataset. For this reason, it would be
interesting to compare the model performance with different normalization approaches.
On the other hand, it would be relevant to investigate the effect of the different epoch re-
jection criteria on the model performance. In this thesis, EEG epochs containing artifacts
that were only present in less than 50% of the epoch duration were excluded. However, it
would be interesting to see how the model performance changes if only epochs where ar-
tifacts last the whole epoch duration are used. Notice that in this case, the amount of data
used would decrease, which could have a negative effect on the prediction performance.
Other studies have demonstrated a good performance of artifact detection in the fre-
quency domain, which was beyond the scope of this thesis. Therefore, it would be worth-
while to explore this approach as well.
Additionally, there is room for improvement regarding artifact annotations, as some of
the data used for training was not accurately annotated. For instance, EEG data from
the BC exercises such as Look straight forward, did not have annotations for the patient
eye blinks. This creates confusion when training the models to recognize eye blinks and
results in having a higher false positive rate, as eye blinks labeled as non-artifact are being
classified as artifacts. To address this issue, the annotation of the data should be further
improved, for instance, by using eye blink detection techniques when collecting EEG data.
Moreover, this work only considered a limited set of artifacts defined in the TUAR dataset,
so it would also be interesting to test the models on a wider range of artifacts.
Finally, another option could be to reduce the number of EEG channels used as input.
This could decrease the computational cost of the model, however, it may also result in
worse classification performance. It would be interesting to compare and evaluate the
trade-off between computational cost and performance when using less EEG channels.
7.2.2
Models
Another direction for future work could be to try other models or to increase the model
complexity. This thesis focused on using already existing models that demonstrated a
good performance on artifact detection. However, there are many other types of models
that could be relevant to try. For example, long short-term memory (LSTM) or transformer-
based models, which have been successful in other tasks and could be worth for artifact
detection in EEG.
Furthermore, variations of the model architecture, such as increasing or reducing the
filter sizes or the number of convolutional layers might lead to a better performance of
the models. Or even combining different architectures or models to create an ensemble
model could be an option.

7.2.3
Real case application
On the other hand, after seeing that the model could be used as a first version for detecting
artifacts within the BrainCapture approach, it would be worthwhile to finally implement it
within the app to ensure that it is efficient for mobile devices and evaluate its performance
during real-time EEG recordings.
This would involve to previously considering a further filter to decide which of the detected
artifacts should be notified to the user taking the recordings. In Section 5.2 some consid-
erations were discussed, such as defining an artifact hierarchy. This way, certain artifacts
would be considered more relevant than others for ensuring good quality data collection
and therefore given priority when being notified to the user.
Nonetheless, this should be further investigated together with BrainCapture to evaluate
the usability of the artifact detection algorithm in their mobile application for EEG record-
ing.
7.2.4
Active learning approach
Finally, a very interesting direction for future work would be to involve EEG experts knowl-
edge to manually evaluate the model predictions, which is similarly done in [32]. This
way, the resulting predictions would be shown to an EEG expert who could then indicate
whether the predictions are correct or incorrect. This process is known as active learn-
ing and would overcome the challenge of lacking annotated EEG artifact data to train the
models.
By using active learning, the model could learn from the experts feedback and improve
its performance over time. As mentioned previously, EEG artifacts can be highly variable,
therefore this approach could be conducted regularly in an iterative manner to continu-
ously update the model and adapt it to new and emerging artifacts.

8
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to evaluate how current artifact detection methods could be
used within the BrainCapture framework. While different approaches are utilized for arti-
fact detection in EEG, this thesis focused on evaluating the deep learning methods that
have previously demonstrated a better trade off between computational cost and classifi-
cation accuracy. The models were trained on the largest publicly available EEG artifact
dataset, TUAR, and then applied transfer learning on the BrainCapture data. The task
mainly consisted of a categorical classification of 6 artifact classes from time-domain EEG
data. Some preprocessing steps were applied to the data with the aim of improving the
performance of the models.
It has been shown that deep learning approaches, such as DSCNN, can identify arti-
facts from BrainCapture data, achieving 80,90% of the artifacts detected and 12,53% of
false positive rate with an overall accuracy of 72,65%, averaged across 10 subjects and
6 artifact classes using leave-one-subject-out cross-validation. Even though the model
encounters difficulties when identifying some types of artifacts, is able to detect a reason-
able proportion of artifacts, which could be used as a starting point for an artifact detection
method in BrainCapture. It was expected that the model would perform worse on Brain-
Capture data, but thanks to the preprocessing pipeline and transfer learning, the clas-
sification performance is comparable to those seen in previous work with higher quality
datasets.
The metrics that better represent how these models accomplish the artifact detection task
within the BrainCapture framework are accuracy, weighted F1-score, binary recall and
false positive rate. Accuracy provides an overview of the proportion of correct predictions,
while weighted F1-score gives an idea of how well the model performs on unbalanced
datasets, such as the used in this thesis. On the other hand, binary recall and false
positive rate indicate the percentage of artifacts that the model detects, showing the trade
off between detecting artifacts and avoiding false positives.
Finally, it has been demonstrated that, thanks to the TensorFlow Lite framework, it is pos-
sible to convert deep learning models to simpler models that can run in mobile devices.
Therefore, the model implemented in this thesis could be integrated into BrainCapture.
By using the TensorFlow Lite model, the computational cost is significantly reduced with-
out compromising the classification performance, detecting over 80% of artifacts with an
accuracy of 80,94% in one of the recordings provided by BrainCapture. Moreover, the
computational time of preprocessing and predicting EEG data are found to be sufficient
so that the models could be used in real-time during EEG recordings.
To conclude, the proposed model has shown promising results to be applied for artifact
identification while performing EEG recordings in real-time with the BrainCapture mobile
application.
