Abstract
Salting is an ancient technique used for preservation of various foods as well as for enhancing
the taste. Still, little is known about the salt concentration proﬁle in salted ﬁsh ﬂesh. In order
to obtain a better understanding of the diffusion patterns in food matrices, which might not ﬁt
into the commonly accepted framework of diffusion, the use of local concentration measurement
techniques is necessary. Preferably, these techniques should be able to be carried out in a fast
and non-destructive way.
The goal to achieve the development of a non-destructive methodology to meet these require-
ments was attempted on dry-salted Atlantic salmon and was based on the use of multispectral
imaging.
Although the development of the non-destructive methodology was unsuccessful, the un-
derlying destructive methodology was successful in terms of yielding concentration proﬁles of
both salt and water from local measurements on salmon ﬂesh. The salt concentration proﬁles
appeared to follow a Fickian diffusion pattern. The destructive methodology was based on the
successful development of a slicing procedure for the division of salmon pieces into separate
layers parallel to the skin. Modeling of the diffusion coefﬁcient and solubility limit of salt in
salmon ﬂesh was carried out by applying a set of model equations. Some discrepancies between
the predictions and the experimental data were found. The impact of the experimental errors on
the estimation of these parameters was evaluated, and the discrepancies were concluded to be
more related to the constraints on the models than to the experimental errors.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Salting is an ancient, though still widely used, technique for preservation of various foods, mainly
ﬁsh and meat products, and for the enhancement of taste. The preservative effect from salting is
dependent on an increase of concentration of soluble compounds at the expense of “free” water,
and, hence, a lowering of the water activity to such a degree that the growth of microorganisms
is inhibited. This is achieved by diffusion of salt into the product, which immobilises water
molecules and thereby lowers the water activity. [Hall, 2010]
Various salting techniques exist. One of them is dry-salting where excessive amounts of salt
are added directly to the surface of the meat product [Vestergaard, 2004]. The added salt induces
an osmotic pressure in the ﬂesh, and the water moving from inside the ﬂesh to the outside is
allowed to ﬂow away [Hall, 2010]. This extraction of water from the inside further contributes to
the decrease of water activity in the ﬂesh. After salting, a period of resting follows to allow for
diffusion of the salt uptaken and to allow for equilibration throughout the food matrix [Albarracín
et al., 2011].
Although diffusion, as understood by Fick [1855], is commonly referred to as the main mass
transfer mechanism when describing the salting kinetics, there are probably deviations from
this regime that could be explained if it was possible to experimentally determine concentration
proﬁles in food matrices. To obtain a full picture of the diffusion pattern, concentrations of the
diffusing substances should be known at all positions throughout the product. This necessitates
the use of a local measuring technique able to measure at speciﬁc points inside the product.
Concentration proﬁles can be determined either experimentally or by means of modeling
according to Fick’s laws of diffusion [Crank, 1975].
Modeling according to Fick’s laws can be done by applying proper conditions, which includes
considerations on the geometrical shape of the body in question and whether it should be treated
as ﬁnite or inﬁnite. For simple geometries, analogous solutions to heat transfer problems can be
used. [Vestergaard, 2004]
The experimental determination of salt concentration has been standardized by AOAC
[2000], which makes use of potentiometric titration with silver nitrate for the quantiﬁcation of
the chloride content equivalent to the sodium chloride content of the food sample. This method is

used worldwide and was therefore also used in this project. In order to be able to relate the water
concentration to the salt concentration, another standardized method based on oven-drying was
used for the determination of dry matter content for the deduction of water content [AOAC, 1990].
Both of the methods for salt and dry matter content analysis are destructive to the sample and
have usually been used for the determinations of average concentrations in the whole foodstuff,
i.e. global measurements.
To the best of the author’s knowledge it appears that no studies deal with the determination
of local concentrations of salt and water in ﬁsh, except for one study by Segtnan et al. [2009],
where fat and salt content at different positions of a salmon ﬁllet were determined by the use of
computed tomography and NIR interactance imaging. Barat et al. [2003] studied, among other
parameters, the changes in NaCl content in dry-salted and brine-salted cod, yet on a global basis.
Van Nguyen et al. [2010] studied the mass transfer of water and salt during brine salting of cod
loins, which also must be considered global measurements. Wang et al. [1998] developed a ﬁnite
difference model for the prediction of salt concentration in thin pieces of brine-salted Atlantic
salmon (Salmo salar). Nevertheless, that was not based on a spatial experimental proﬁle for the
salt content.
In the light of the foregoing, appropriate methods to perform local measurements in an
accurate and non-destructive way are yet to be explored and developed.
The original goal of this project was to contribute to the ﬁeld through development of a
non-destructive methodology to measure local salt and water concentrations in ﬁsh ﬂesh based on
multispectral imaging by the use of the VideometerLab 4 instrument designed and manufactured
by Videometer A/S (Hørsholm, Denmark, www.videometer.com). Atlantic salmon (Salmo
salar) was used as a source of ﬁsh ﬂesh for the experiments, and dry-salting was used as salting
technique.
The VideometerLab 4 is an instrument which is able to record reﬂectance bands obtained
by illuminating a sample at 19 different wavelengths spanning from the visible region to the
near-infrared region, and it can be used for the detection and quantiﬁcation of various substances
[Carstensen et al., 2006]. E.g. it has been used for the determination of water content in cookies
[Andresen et al., 2013] and for the determination of astaxanthin concentration in salmonids
[Dissing et al., 2011]. It is a fast and easy-to-use instrument, which gives rise to the potential
establishment of a model for the prediction of a spatial concentration proﬁle throughout the
entire foodstuff. This could for instance be used by the industry to control salting procedures for
an accurate degree of salting to ensure desired quality parameters such as taste and texture.
A need for calibration of the VideometerLab 4 instrument through correlation between
measured salt and water concentrations and the corresponding obtained spectra required the
access to concentrations at speciﬁc points inside the ﬁsh ﬂesh to be used as input for the
calibration, which in itself was destructive.
Unfortunately, the non-destructive methodology turned out to be unsuccessful as it was
impossible to acquire images of the salmon slices useful for the calibration. Nevertheless, the
destructive methodology resulted in concentration proﬁles of both salt and water in the salmon

ﬂesh. This methodology, in turn, required the development of a slicing procedure to produce
separated layers of ﬁsh ﬂesh perpendicular to the direction of diffusion. In the present case, this
involved slicing of the ﬂesh parallel to the skin, as salt was placed on the ﬂesh side to diffuse
through the ﬂesh towards the skin side. This requirement on the slicing turned out to be quite
challenging.
Apparently, to the best of the author’s knowledge, only one patent of an apparatus and method
capable of dividing a ﬁsh ﬁllet into separate layers parallel to the skin has been issued [Eide,
2000]. This involves the freezing of a ﬁsh ﬁllet onto a freezing drum and a band knife cutting
off a slice parallel to the circumference of the freezing drum, and hence, parallel to the skin of
the ﬁllet attached to it. Nevertheless, this apparatus was not available, and the freezing of a ﬁsh
ﬁllet to such a drum was undesirable in the scope of this project, as salted salmon pieces should
not be refrozen for the sake of comparability with other studies, and since freezing after salting
was speculated to introduce errors on the measured concentration proﬁles inside the ﬂesh. So
it appeared that slicing of unfrozen ﬁsh ﬁllets parallel to the skin apparently never had been
done before. Nevertheless, an alternative setup for a slicing machine tilted at an angle, and the
use of various aids eventually allowed for sufﬁcient control of the slicing procedure to produce
even slices. The thickness of the slices were measured with a Texture Analyser, as this was a
fast, easy, and non-destructive method. Nevertheless, additional testings of both a calliper and a
folding rule showed up to be useable, yet inferior, alternatives, since they were both destructive
methods and more time consuming. This was due to the fact that it was necessary to cut the
slices into bits in order to get access to the points at which the thickness should be measured.
The subsequent salt and water content analyses were also part of the destructive methodology,
although these were already standardized procedures and thus not developed in this project.
Since the usual way of investigating diffusion patterns is through global measurements,
the results based on the destructive methodology could possibly serve as improvement for the
description of diffusion by Fick’s laws.
Furthermore, the deﬁnition of two case models dependent on the progression of the salt
concentration at the interface between salt and ﬁsh ﬂesh and the application of these to the
experimental concentration proﬁles provided a basis for determination of both the diffusion
coefﬁcient and the solubility limit of salt in salmon ﬂesh.

Chapter 2
Materials and methods
2.1
Pretreatment
The Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) used in the experiments were Norwegian aquaculture ﬁsh,
harvested in 2016 and provided as frozen ﬁllets through O.H. Fiskeeksport A/S, Hirtshals, DK.
Frozen ﬁllets were kindly provided by Royal Greenland A/S and Skagerak Salmon A/S. The
ﬁllets were then vacuum-packed using a C 500 Multivac® vacuum-packer (Multivac, Sepp
Haggenmüller KG, D-87787 Wolfertschwenden, Germany) and frozen at −30 ◦C. Not until
right before further treatment of each ﬁllet, it was cut into pieces with either a KS-220 Reich
bandsaw (Karl M. Reich Maschinenfabrik, Nürtingen, Germany) or a regular saw and then stored
at −18 ◦C. Right before further treatment, salmon pieces were put in fridge (2 ◦C) for thawing
overnight.
2.2
Dry-salting
Salmon pieces were placed with the skin side down, whereafter as much salt (NaCl, “Groft,
Pioner, Rafﬁneret salt”, food grade, Brøste A/S, Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark) as possible was placed
on the top surface of each piece. The amount of added salt was noted by weighing the pieces
both before and after salt was added. The salmon pieces were then placed on a supportive grid in
a tray both made of plastic. The tray with ﬁllets was covered with a clear plastic ﬁlm and ﬁllets
were allowed to salt in fridge at 2 ◦C for different times ranging from 46 h to 933 h. After salting,
remaining salt was wiped off with a paper towel.
The solubility limit for salt in the salmon ﬂesh was tested with a long time salting experiment
(933 h ∼39 days) where extra salt was added after 266 h (∼11 days).
2.3
Contraction
Right before salting, salmon pieces were placed with the skin side down on a chopping board
and trimmed to be approximately rectangular as seen from above. Then, the length, width and

2.4
Slicing procedure
Salmon pieces were sliced on an AM-300 slicing machine (ATOM, Metalnord s.r.l., Italy) placed
in a horizontal position supported by a wooden box and with the nominal thickness set to 3.0 mm
(please refer to section 3.2.2 on page 13 for further details on the setup). Before each slicing
session, the cutting blade of the slicing machine was sharpened and a pair of layers of some 3M
CP-RL A-MED cleaning roll (Sanistål, Aalborg, Denmark) with some coarse sandpaper attached
to it were placed on the sample pusher plate of the slicing machine to go into contact with the
skin of the salmon piece. While slicing, a safety glove was worn and water was poured on the
cutting blade as an attempt to reduce the friction against the salmon piece. The last slice of each
piece was skinned with a sharp knife.
2.5
Thickness measurement
Thickness measurements of slices were measured with a TA-XT plus Texture Analyser equipped
with a 5 kg load cell (Stable Micro Systems Ltd, Vienna Court, Lammas Road, Godalm-
ing, Surrey GU7 1YL, UK) by using a P/10, cylindrical ﬂat-ended probe, 10 mm in diam-
eter. Slices were placed on an even chopping board resting directly on the platform of the
Texture Analyser, and the height was calibrated against the surface of the chopping board.
The ﬂat-ended cylinder was set to approach the sample at a pre-test speed of 1.00 mm/s.
The measurement was programmed through the use of the software Texture Exponent 32,
ver. 4.0.13.0 (http://www.stablemicrosystems.com) and set to start when a trigger
force of 0.0020 kg was reached. Then, the speed of the probe changed to 2.00 mm/s until a target
force of 0.0050 kg was reached, after which the probe retracted to the starting position. The
thickness of the sample was found from the point where the trigger force was reached and the
measurement started, which was interpreted as the position of the sample surface corresponding
to the thickness of the sample at the measured spot (see ﬁgure 3.8 on page 17 for a supportive
illustration of how the thickness measurements were carried out).

Salt content analysis was carried out according to AOAC [2000] using a 785 DMP Titrino titrator
connected to a 728 Magnetic Stirrer and a Ag-electrode (Metrohm Ltd., Herisau, Switzerland),
though with small modiﬁcations to the sample collection as follows: Samples were prepared

2.7
Water content analysis
Dry matter content analysis was carried out according to AOAC [1990] with the same sample
collection pattern as used for the salt content analysis (section 2.6). The collected samples were
placed in mufﬁn tins and oven-dried between 20 h and 24 h at 105 ◦C and the dry matter content
was determined. The water content was then determined on the basis of the mass evaporated
during drying.

2.8.1
Parameter ﬁtting and error propagation
The three presented solutions to equation (2.1) proposed by Crank [1975] were used to estimate
the diffusion coefﬁcient and/or the solubility limit or the parameters β or k (described according
to the case) by minimising the summed of the squared residuals between the experimental and
the predicted concentration proﬁles, by using the nonlinear least square minimisation routine
lsqnonlin, as predeﬁned by the MATLAB®software.
2.9
Error propagation analysis
A sensitivity analysis was carried out to study the inﬂuence of the errors of the thickness
and concentrations measurements on the determination of D and C∞for a ﬁxed interface
concentration case. This sensitivity analysis was performed by introducing noise to average
experimental concentration proﬁles obtained at the longest salting time (933 h) with a Monte
Carlo re-sampling algorithm (section 2.8.1) and by subsequently evaluating equation (2.2b).
The noise corresponded to a value randomly chosen within the limits of one standard deviation
for each parameter, either the concentration or the position. This algorithm was run 200 times.
The lower 5th and upper 95th percentiles of the resulting distributions of D and C∞were then
discarded to obtain the lower and upper conﬁdence bounds.

2.10
Multispectral imaging
Cross-sectional strips from both raw and salted salmon pieces were sliced off from the side of the
salmon piece perpendicular to the skin at a nominal thickness of both 3 mm and 6 mm using the
slicing machine standing in its normal position (as opposed to the horizontal setup when slicing
slices parallel to the skin as described in section 2.4 on page 5). Both cross-sectional strips and
slices sliced parallel to the skin of both raw and salted salmon pieces were trimmed with a knife
to ﬁt into plastic petri dishes (90 mm in diameter) and pictured in both unfolded and folded states
using a VideometerLab 4 spectral imaging instrument (Videometer A/S, Lyngsø Allé 3, DK-2970
Hørsholm, Denmark. Website: www.videometer.com). For slices sliced parallel to the
skin, this was performed after measurement of thickness. The VideometerLab 4 acquired high-
resolution images of the surface reﬂections from the sample at 19 different wavelengths ranging
from 365 nm to 970 nm based on Light Emitting Diode (LED) technology. The VideometerLab

3.1
Multispectral imaging
Reﬂectance spectra showed a trend towards an increase in reﬂectance with sample thickness
assessed by acquiring images of slices in both unfolded and folded states (single and double
folded, i.e. 2 and 4 layers, respectively) followed by comparison of the obtained reﬂectance
spectra. This trend indicated an incomplete interaction between the incident light and sample, i.e.
part of the light simply traversed through the sample without either being reﬂected or absorbed.
This would result in a low signal-to-noise ratio, and generation of inconclusive data in the
post-treatment of the spectra was therefore expected. Therefore, to assess whether a further
increased sample thickness would result in an upper level for the reﬂectance indicating a full
interaction between light and sample, image acquisitions of a slice of salmon in both its unfolded
state and folded states of 2, 4, 6, 8, and 12 layers were carried out. The 12-layered state was set
up by cutting the 6 layer-folded sample in two and then placing the parts on top of each other.
The resulting reﬂectance spectra are presented in ﬁgure 3.1 on the next page.
As seen in ﬁgure 3.1 on the following page the reﬂectance in the range around 550 nm to
900 nm keeps increasing with number of layers. Besides of that, the resulting larger thickness
from folding the sample resulted in the sample to come out of focus of the camera of the Videome-
terLab 4 instrument, which further introduced uncertainties on the extraction of meaningful data.
In addition, the apparent requirement of a fairly thick sample (∼2 cm to 3 cm) in order to achieve
a maximized reﬂectance would result in a large decrease in the possible number of slices to be
sliced off from each salmon piece, and, hence, also a signiﬁcant reduction in the amount of data
obtainable from the subsequent salt and water content analyses. Therefore, it was concluded
that a proper image acquisition of the salmon slices using the VideometerLab 4 instrument was
impossible to accomplish, and further experimentation was abandoned.

3.2
Thickness measurements
In order to be able to perform local measurements on ﬁsh samples, each piece of salmon ﬁllet
had to be divided into separated layers each representing a certain “depth” as seen from the top
surface of the ﬂesh side (i.e. opposite the skin side). This required a standardized procedure in
order to obtain even thickness and shape of each layer sliced off from the piece. Therefore, a
slicing procedure was developed as an attempt to meet these requirements.
3.2.1
Slicing with a knife
The initial attempt of slicing a salmon piece was carried out on the slicing machine. However,
since it was not possible to hold the piece ﬁrmly in place, the ﬁrst slicing attempts were carried
out with a knife, instead. This was done on narrow pieces, which were placed on the cross-
sectional side on a chopping board, and then thin slices were cut parallel to the skin starting from
the ﬂesh side. As seen in ﬁgure 3.2, the resulting slices were very uneven, even though the knife
was very sharp, and it was concluded that slicing with a knife in this way was not reproducible.
Another attempt with the skin resting on the chopping board while cutting parallel to the skin
was carried out. This resulted in apparently more even slices (ﬁgure 3.3). However, the slicing
was difﬁcult to carry out, and there was little control of the evenness and thickness of the slices,
so this approach was given up on in favor of trying to ﬁnd a feasible solution using the slicing
machine instead.

3.2.2
Slicing with a slicing machine
Since slicing with a knife did not prove to be feasible, further testing and improvement on the use
of the slicing machine were carried out. A piece of salmon larger than the one used for the initial
attempt, was sliced with the nominal thickness on the slicing machine set to 1.5 mm. However,
as the resulting slices turned out to be shredded and uneven, the nominal thickness was adjusted
to 3.0 mm, which resulted in more whole slices. After this, all subsequent slicing was tested at a
nominal thickness of 3.0 mm.
As cut slices were still uneven, the idea of placing the sample holder, the cutting blade and
the sample in a horizontal position was tested by building a supporting setup for the slicing
machine. This was done by using a wooden box as support (see ﬁgure 3.4a). This allowed
the entire surface of the ﬂesh side of the sample to be supported by both the sample pusher
plate and the supporting plate, as opposed to only being partly supported when placed in the
vertical position, since part of the sample would then fall into the gap between the side of the
sample pusher plate and the carriage tray (ﬁgures 3.4b and 3.4c). This setup also allowed for the
movement of the sample to be directed radially towards the center of the cutting blade, which
was speculated to result in less friction between the two, and hence less twisting of the sample.
In addition, as the last 1 cm to 1.5 cm of the piece could not be sliced, due to the fact that the
built-in gap between the sample pusher plate and the supporting plate restricted the application
of pressure on the sample, and, hence, the holding of the sample to be ﬁxed at the pusher plate
and the pushing of said towards the cutting blade, a pair of layers of cleaning roll were placed
against the pusher plate in order to ﬁll the gap and to provide some friction against the skin of
the sample while slicing (ﬁgure 3.4d). However, this horizontal setup still resulted in slicing of
skewed slices, i.e. slices sliced at an oblique angle relative to the skin. This seemed to be due to
the rotating cutting blade grabbing onto the sample and sometimes carrying it along resulting in
only partly sliced slices, which then sometimes had to be completed with a knife, anyway. So, in
order to decrease the friction between the cutting blade and the sample, the cutting blade was
sharpened before each slicing session, water was poured over the cutting blade while slicing, and
residues on the cutting blade from previous slicing were removed. On the other hand, in order to
attempt to increase the friction between the sample and the sample pusher plate to reduce the
risk of sample slipping, some coarse sandpaper was attached to the cleaning roll to be in direct
contact with the skin of the sample (not shown).
Furthermore, due to the curved proﬁle of the salmon pieces, a rough distinction between
the “thick part” and the “thin part” of each piece was made. The thick part seemed more evenly
shaped and also contained the vast majority of the ﬂesh, so for any subsequent slicing the extent
of the thin part of each salmon piece was determined by eye and cut off, and hence only the thick
part was sliced (see ﬁgure 3.5 on the next page).
Later, it was experienced that a slow and steady movement of the sample towards the cutting
blade while slicing resulted in both less slipping of the sample and even more evenly shaped
and uniform-looking slices, and therefore, this practice was also incorporated into the slicing

Chapter 4
Conclusions
The goal of this project was to develop a methodology to measure local salt (NaCl) and water
concentrations in ﬁsh muscle in a fast way. Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) was used in the
experiments, and the goal was attempted to be achieved in two steps: 1) Through the development
of a non-destructive methodology, and 2) through the development of a destructive methodology.
4.1
Non-destructive methodology
Initially, the goal was the development of a non-destructive methodology based on the Videome-
terLab 4 instrument, which proved to be unsuccessful because of the practicalities found in
section 3.1.
The establishment of this non-destructive methodology required a calibration of the Videome-
terLab 4 instrument. The steps required for the calibration of this resulted in a destructive
methodology itself which proved to be successful on producing data that could be used for the
determination of water and salt concentration proﬁles. Particularly, the methodology helped to
solve the problem of getting access to perform concentration measurements at speciﬁc points
throughout the thickness of the salmon piece, which was achieved by developing a systematic
slicing procedure that can probably be applied to other ﬁsh matrices with similar characteristics
(namely thickness) to salmon.
The methodology consisted of thawing and dry-salting of pieces of salmon ﬁllet followed
by division of pieces into thin slices, thickness measurements of slices, multispectral image
acquisition of slices, and eventually estimation of local salt and water concentrations of the slices
by the use of traditional analysis techniques.
As the attempt of image acquisition by the use of the VideometerLab 4 instrument turned
out to be unsuccessful, reasons for an attempt of development through the establishment of a
destructive methodology arose instead.

4.2
Destructive methodology
The attempt of establishing a destructive methodology proved to be successful in several ways
and was based on the following:
• Thawing and dry-salting of salmon pieces, although these steps were not part of the
development itself.
• Slicing of pieces. This included testing with a knife, which turned out to be too difﬁcult
to control in terms of obtaining even slices of uniform thickness. The decision of testing
a slicing machine tilted into a horizontal setup and the use of some cleaning roll and
sandpaper in order to both keep the sample in place while slicing and for making it
possible to slice the entire piece, instead of only partially, proved to be a feasible solution
for slicing apparently uniform slices parallel to the skin. In addition, sharpening of the
cutting blade prior to slicing and pouring of water on this while slicing further improved
the results in terms of each slice being evenly shaped.
• Thickness measurements. This included testing of the use of a calliper, a folding rule,
and a Texture Analyser. The Texture Analyser was found to be both easier and faster in
addition to being non-destructive as compared to the use of a calliper and folding rule,
which necessitated further cutting of the slice in order to be able to measure the thickness
at all spots on the slice. However, it was concluded that a calliper or a folding rule can be
used as a reasonable alternative if nothing better is at hand, as they both yielded results
similar to the measurements carried out with the Texture Analyser.
• Local salt and water concentrations estimations. These were based on sample collection at
speciﬁc locations, namely at the top surface and at the bottom surface of the slices, and
subsequently analysed by the use of traditional methods for salt and dry matter analysis.
4.2.1
Concentration proﬁles
One of the important outcomes of the destructive methodology was the ability to produce
concentration proﬁles of salt and water in salmon ﬂesh. The salt concentration proﬁles appeared
to follow a decaying exponential relationship with position inside the ﬂesh as would be expected
for a Fickian diffusion pattern, although this pattern was most easily observed for the shorter
salting times. The water concentration appeared to increase with position inside the ﬂesh for
shorter salting times as expected, and it seemed to follow an inverse relationship with the salt
concentration, whereas a decaying pattern similar for both water and salt concentrations versus
position at long salting times was observed.
The methodology also made it possible to estimate the solubility limit of salt, C∞, in the
salmon ﬂesh based on measured salt concentrations at the interface between salt and ﬂesh, Cint,
from a long time salting experiment. C∞in the ﬂesh was estimated to be 21.4 w/w%, although

the estimation was speculated to be inﬂuenced by insufﬁcient removal of crystalline salt at the
surface of the salmon pieces after salting, as this value exceeds the corresponding solubility limit
of salt in pure water (≈26.3 %).
4.2.2
Modeling
Another important outcome of the destructive methodology was the possibility to model the
diffusion coefﬁcient, D, through ﬁtting of a set of model equations to the experimentally
obtained concentration proﬁles. The salmon piece was considered an inﬁnite sheet expanding
from 0 < x < L with an initial salt concentration, C0. The use of three solutions to Fick’s
second law of diffusion led to the deﬁnitions of two cases dependent on the progression of the
interface concentration of salt, Cint, with time, which was limited by the solubility limit, C∞.
Case 1 was deﬁned by an interface concentration, Cint, varying linearly from the initial
concentration C0, i.e. of the form Cint = C0 + kt at shorter salting times, until a stationary point
equal to the solubility limit, Cint = C∞, at longer salting times.
Case 2 was deﬁned by an interface concentration, Cint, varying exponentially from the initial
concentration, C0, described by a function of the form Cint = C0 + C∞(1 −e−βt) for all salting
times.
A discrepancy between the estimations of the diffusion coefﬁcient from each of the regimes
comprising the ﬁrst case was found, as the estimations of the diffusion coefﬁcient for the regime
with a linearly varying Cint was found to be in the range 1.01 · 10−9 m2 s−1 to 1.90 · 10−9 m2 s−1,
whereas the estimation of the diffusion coefﬁcient for the regime with a ﬁxed Cint = C∞was
estimated to 4.81 · 10−11 m2 s−1.
Also a discrepancy between the estimated values for the diffusion coefﬁcient was found for
the second case, as they were found to be ranging from 9.64 · 10−11 m2 s−1 to 5.73 · 10−10 m2 s−1.
Although there appeared to be an agreement between the estimated values based on the modeling
of Case 1 and Case 2 for long salting times, it was concluded that neither of the predictions of
the concentration proﬁles were optimal and that a better model is needed in order to adequately
describe the experimental data.
The main reasons behind the observed errors and discrepancies related to the modelings were
speculated to be due to the constraints on the case models. Therefore, the inﬂuence of the exper-
imental errors in thickness and concentration measurements on D and C∞on the assumption
of a ﬁxed Cint = C∞was investigated by performing an error propagation analysis based on a
Monte Carlo re-sampling algorithm. The mean of D was found to be 5.37 · 10−11 m2 s−1 with
a 95% conﬁdence interval ranging from 4.85 · 10−11 m2 s−1 to 5.99 · 10−11 m2 s−1, and the mean
of C∞was found to be 19.7 w/w% with a 95% conﬁdence interval ranging from 18.4 w/w% to
20.9 w/w%. An overall good agreement between the predictions and the experimental data was
observed, as the experimental errors only had some impact on the prediction of C∞, which was
ascribed to the supposedly inadequate removal of salt from the surface of the salmon piece after
salting. This might be overcome by simply rinsing with water.

4.3
Perspectives
The developed destructive methodology may be applied to other ﬁsh species reminiscent of
salmon in terms of ﬁllet thickness, textural properties and/or composition in order for the slicing
to be successful with respect to obtainment of a sufﬁcient number of evenly and uniformly shaped
slices to get a detailed concentration proﬁle. Improvements on the sampling and estimation of
local water concentration would be desirable as this probably is the main source of experimental
errors. In addition, it could be a wise choice to only include salt and water concentrations from
the ﬂesh in further modeling, thereby omitting the skin, the fat deposits close to the skin, and the
dark muscles, which apparently showed a different diffusion pattern than the rest of the ﬂesh
most likely due to the different composition of these compartments.
The application of the methodology might give better insights into the salting kinetics in
salmon ﬂesh and perhaps also other ﬁsh species making it possible to develop models that yield
better predictions of the concentration proﬁle, also based on Fick’s laws of diffusion, but which
allow for a more realistic ﬁsh muscle matrix: water and/or salt concentration dependent diffusion
coefﬁcients, shrinking, fat heterogeneities, etc. This has been outside the scope of this project but
might also inﬂuence the diffusion pattern in various ways. The search for a numerical solution
could result in the answer to this and hence be a subject of further studies. In addition, more time
investment in the attempt of using the VideometerLab 4 instrument for proper image acquisition
of thin ﬁsh ﬂesh slices might show up to result in a powerful and versatile non-destructive
methodology for the prediction of salt and water concentration proﬁles in ﬁsh ﬂesh.
