Introduction 
Setting the Scene 
Energy transition will have a transformational impact on the UK electricity system, effecting power 
generation, demand and transmission. Fossil fuel is to be phased out and replaced by offshore wind, 
green gas and nuclear power plants, supplemented with European interconnectors. Electricity 
demand will increase as fossil fuels will also be phased out of domestic and industrial use and the 
transport sector.  
The key challenges for the transmission grid will be the growth in offshore wind energy and the 
replacement of fossil fuel gas with green gas. Currently the powerflow from North to South peaks at 
3 – 4 GW. In 2050 on a windy day up to 80 GW of wind power will be generated to meet the domestic 
and industrial demand augmented by the electricity demand of manufacturing green gas. When the 
wind drops, the loss of wind power will need to be compensated by the nuclear, interconnectors and 
green gas generators. This study will look at how this will change the powerflows though the system 
and how these powerflows can be accommodated by the transmission grid of 2050.  
Transition will also create opportunities for the UK. During the boom years of the North Sea oil and 
gas industry, the UK was a net exporter of energy. Due to the decline of oil and gas production, the 
UK is now a net importer of energy and electricity. Despite the impressive increase of the wind industry 
(24.0 GW capacity), UK still imports 5% - 10% of its electricity from Europe through interconnectors. 
Given the UK’s wind energy potential, the UK has the opportunity to become a net electricity supplier 
to Europe, providing that the transmission grid can accommodate these powerflows.  
 
Approach & Boundaries 
This study models and compares 4 different transmission systems for the year 2050. First the 2020 
transmission grid is modelled where the existing power generators and the actual peak demand are 
allocated to a grid of 40 sub-stations. The powerflow is calculated using minimum cost based 
powerflow optimization, where fictive relative costs are charged for the different power generation 
types. Wind energy is set at 10 E/MW. These 2020 results are compared with the actual powerflows 
to validate the model. The 2020 transmission system is then slightly upgraded to a full 400 kV system 
and expanded with 4 sub stations to connect the remote landing points of offshore windfarms. 
A plausible scenario for 2050 is made, which specifies the type, capacity and location of power 
generation as well as the magnitude and location of electricity demand. This single 2050 supply and 
demand scenario is used to analyse and compare 4 different transmission solutions:  
a) Upgrade and expansion of the 400 kV AC OHL system. 
b) Installation of an East Coast 800 kV UHV AC OHL line. 
c) Installation of an onshore East Coast UHV DC OHL line. 
d) Installation of a modular offshore East & West UHV DC cable. 
Each of these transmission cases are modelled under 4 sets of operating conditions, varying the 
electricity demand, availability of wind energy and the green gas reserves. Both the transmission cases 
and the operating conditions are end points of the spectra to highlight the differences in performance 
between the selected solutions. The cases are compared on various parameters, describing to what 
extent the grid can utilize the cheapest available electricity source and to what extent spare capacity 
can be exported to neighbouring countries. 
All modelling and analysis has been performed on a Python JupyterLab environment with Freeware 
packages (Att 1).

Boundaries.  
• 
Northern Ireland is not part of the UK grid system and the model is therefore limited to the 
countries England, Scotland and Wales. Existing and future interconnectors with Northern 
Ireland and other European countries are modelled as a DC generator at the substation (bus) 
of connection to the transmission grid. 
• 
The study focuses on the high voltage transmission grid. The distribution grid will have its own 
transition challenges, but is out of scope of this study. This means that power generation, 
which is connected at distribution voltage or used at source, such as smaller onshore 
windfarms and solar are excluded from the transmission power generators.  
• 
The study looks at the technical aspects of the different transmission options and does not 
include the costing and economic evaluation of the different options. Made up costs are used 
to steer the powerflow optimization routine, making one type of power generator more 
attractive than others. The ‘total cost’ therefore does not reflect a real cost. 
• 
The 2020 year is taken as the reference year for this study. For the transition year 2050 has 
been selected for 2 reasons. The year 2050 is often used by the UK governments and National 
Grid to describe the “end state” of the energy transition. More importantly by ignoring the 
difficult task of the actual transition, the focus on 2050 allows a look at blue-sky grid designs. 
• 
The powerflow provides a snapshot solution of the flow of electricity in the grid. Dynamic 
aspects of power quality, security  and safety are not addressed. The study is therefore limited 
to a sub-set of what is required to make a transmission grid and any selection would require 
these aspects to be studied as well.

UK Transmission Grid 
Technical Description 
Since the inception of the UK grid, the powerflow 
has always been from the North to the South. In 
the 50s Scottish hydropower exceeded local 
demand and in the 60s nuclear plants were 
predominantly located in Scotland and Northern 
England. The big demand centres are the 
traditionally industrial areas of London, North 
West England (Manchester, Liverpool) and the 
West Midlands (Birmingham). As service industry 
replaced manufacturing industry, people and 
electricity demand moved further towards the 
South (ref 1). 
The UK transmission system is based on 50 Hz, 
280 kV and 400 kV AC overhead lines with some 
132 kV overhead lines in remote areas. Buried 
cables are used to transmit the power into the 
larger cities for both domestic and industrial 
distribution. In the 70s the last major upgrade 
was made, where congested lines were uprated 
from 280 to 400 kV. Although 500 kV was 
preferred, the existing infrastructure (pylons – 
substations) could not be uprated to voltages 
above 400 kV (ref 2). In 2020 most of the 
transmission lines have been uprated to 400 kV 
and where capacity was lacking, parallel 
overhead lines were installed, as can be observed in Eastern Scotland (fig 1.1).

UK power sector has already made huge strides 
towards emission reductions; the 2030 Paris ac-

years and this will be completed by 2024. The 
coal plants have been replaced by gas plants,

existing nuclear plants are reaching the end of 
their lifetime and all existing plants will be closed

Organization & Policy 
The UK government sets the energy policy. This 
can be currently summarized as securing supply 
throughout the transition towards green energy 
at affordable costs, often referred to as the 
Energy Trilemma. Most recently the policy has 
been amended with the objective of self reliance 
and independence from Russian gas (ref 5).  
 
• 
Department for Business, Energy and Industrial Strategy (BEIS) is a governmental department 
responsible for defining and implementing the energy strategy of the country. It is advised by 
the Committee on Climate Change (CCC, greenhouse gas emission reduction, ref 4) and the 
North Sea Transition Authority (NSTA, security of supply and energy transition). In line with

CCC recommendations, the Offshore Transmission Network Review has been initiated, which 
will look at the technical and legal aspects of integrating the interconnectors, offshore 
windfarm connection and the national grid, promoting cost efficiency and enabling 
accelerated offshore wind development. In attachment 2 the OTNR Generation Map is 
presented, demonstrating the integration of offshore power generation and the transmission

• 
OFGEM is the energy regulator in the UK. It regulates grid operations (NGESO) and grid 
investments (NGET). Its mission is to ensure safe operations and fair pricing for consumers. 
• 
UK grid strategy is advised by the Smart Systems & Flexibility Forum (SSFF), which replaced 
the Electricity Networks Strategy Group (ENSG). While the ENSG used to work on the longer 
term SSFF is focussed on shorter term issues like the implementation of smart meters and 
accelerating the hook up of small scale renewable operators. 
• 
National Grid Electrical System Operator (NGESO) operates the GB transmission system and 
provides the 8 private distribution network operators in England, Wales and Scotland with the 
electricity required. Its operations and pricing are regulated by OFGEM. Every 5 years NGESO 
issues the Future Energy Scenarios with a planning horizon of some 10 years. Based on these 
scenarios and the existing bottlenecks it recommends a list of short term improvement 
projects, the Network Options Assessment (NOA).  
• 
National Grid Electricity Transmission (NGET) manages the upgrades of the transmission grid 
and executes the approved NOA projects. Its Innovation Strategy aims to deliver cleaner and 
cheaper electricity by promoting the development and application of new technology. 
Execution of the projects and cost recovery are regulated by OFGEM. 
• 
The Crown Estate and Crown Estate Scotland are owners of the UK foreshore and lease these 
areas out for any industrial use, such as offshore wind concessions and offshore cables. The 
Crown Estate has no authority to approve development programs and offshore (transmission) 
projects therefore require multi-tier approval from the Crown Estate and the Government. 
 
In the UK, plans for the transmission grid are therefore based on medium and short term 
requirements. OFGEM will break up larger projects into smaller projects, to ensure there is sufficient 
competition in the market and to spread the risks. As a result, the UK grid improvements are piecemeal 
and are aimed at providing the availability, quality and reliability at minimum cost to the consumers. 
China is currently the world leader in UHV AC & DC transmission lines, having installed 35 of these 
lines with as flagship project the Changji - Guquan line (3500 km, 10 GW, 1100 kV DC) (ref5).  
It is the opinion of the author that the different approach towards UHV power transmission between 
the UK and China is at least partially due to the difference in planning horizon. China creates a 
masterplan for the requirements in 30 to 50 years time. All approved projects, inc. pre-investments, 
contribute to the realization of this master plan.  
For the UK to consider the transmission options as addressed in this thesis, it would need to study and 
select such a 2050 (or 2070) transmission master plan. Currently the UK imports some 10% of its 
electricity through interconnectors. As will be shown, the UK has the opportunity to become a net 
exporter of green electricity. Realizing this opportunity will depend on the type of transmission grid 
the nation selects to build.

3.   
2020 Transmission Model 
 
Objective 
The objective of the study is to compare different transmission options in 2050. It has been assumed 
that the existing transmission system will not be replaced by 2050, but will be expanded by additional 
transmission lines to meet the increased capacity demand. This means that any model of the 2050 
system will need to contain a functioning representation of the 2020 transmission system. The results 
of this 2020 model can be compared with the existing powerflows and line loading throughout the 
country to verify to what extent the model represents reality and to identify the limitations of the 
model. 
There are 2 ways to run powerflow calculations.  
a. Powerflow. The manual way uses the generator voltage angles as input and calculates the 
flow of power through the network. This input resembles how the TSO operates the network 
and decides how much power each generator will deliver.  
b. Powerflow Optimization. The model is optimized for a function, such as minimizing cost or 
losses. The optimal voltage angles, power generation and demand are calculated within a 
given set of constraints. 
For this study the powerflow optimization was used, as the resulting powerflow is less influenced by 
the user input and therefore allows fairer comparison between the different transmission systems.  
 
Limitations 
Any model is per definition a simplification of reality and will therefore introduce errors. For the 
comparison of transmission cases it is important that the real power powerflows and line loading 
percentages are representative. Voltage angles and reactive power flows will not have to be matched, 
but discrepancies should be explainable. 
The powerflow optimization provides a snapshot or a steady state solution of the powerflow 
throughout the grid, i.e. there is no time dimension. This means that issues like power quality, 
redundancy, ramp-up times and operability are not evaluated. If this study would attempt to make a 
solid recommendation for the transmission architecture of the future, these operational aspects 
should be studied as well. 
 
Software 
It was decided to use Python as programming language in a JupyterLab environment and to only use 
Freeware software, to ensure the program could still be used after the study was completed. The 
powerflow optimization calculations are performed by the package PandaPower (Ref 1). This program 
has been developed by Klausthal university and has been verified against a series of grid definitions 
specified by CIGRE. For this study the powerflow optimization module has been used, which optimizes 
on costs. Additional functionality, such us power quality, redundancy and short circuit flows are 
available, but have not been used in this study.  
For geospatial data the program GeoPandas (Ref 2) has been used. GeoPandas works with shapefiles 
and both the British Government and Grid Operators make a lot of geospatial information available in 
this format. In the UK, the metric grid system British National Grid (BNG) is used, which is based on 
OSGB36 (ref 3). In PandaPower the numeric representation of this grid system is used (i.e. without 
block references) and has been applied with as base unit kilometres. 
Both PandaPower and GeoPandas use the python package Panda for their data structure. This means 
that the input and the output of both packages can be easily combined, manipulated with 
mathematical functions from NumPy, SciPy and Symbol and can be graphed with packages like 
MatPlotLib and Pyplot. Data columns like names, types and zones can be added to the DataFrame and 
results can be saved to a DataFrame and these files can be analysed / visualized with packages such 
as Panel. An overview of the Freeware packages used id provided in att 1.

Substations: PandaPower bus & external grid 
The nodes of the model are the substations. 
Existing substations were downloaded for 
England and Wales from the National Grid 
website. These areas were condensed to points by 
using the GeoPandas centroid function. The 
location of the Scottish substations were not 
available online and were digitized using Google 
Maps & Google Earth. The 2 data sets were 
combined and a common 4 letter abbreviation 
was assigned to all stations. 
 
After several iterations a representative subset of 
stations was selected using the 34 400 kV 
substations with the most connections and 2 280 
kV substations in Scotland (Spittal & Auchencrosh) 
which are the landing points of interconnectors.  
In addition a made-up substation was located in 
the heart of London as a load centre. In 
PandaPower a bus can only have a single voltage 
and additional buses were placed and connected 
with transformers to link the 280 kV lines with the 
400 kV grid. To run a powerflow optimization at 
least one external grid has to be defined. The 
substation Sellindge in the South East of the 
country was selected as the external grid with 0 
degree Voltage angle and 1.0 pu Voltage. The 
external grid is set to zero real and reactive power.

Cost Function: PandaPower polycost 
Powerflow optimization looks for the solution at the lowest costs. Costs are used as a relative driver 
(i.e. wind is cheaper than nuclear), rather than an absolute number where the cost of power 
generation reflects the true costs, as this is not required for the case comparisons. Accurate 
quantitative cost estimates would require  
• 
a definition of whose cost we are defining (grid operator, consumer, society). 
• 
a definition of the cost (capex, opex, financing costs, subsidies). 
• 
a definition of the cost for auxiliary services supporting  power quality and security of supply. 
• 
a definition of the contract and market for the delivered power. 
Such a quantitative modelling of the cost is beyond the scope of this study and not required to 
compare the transmission options. 
  
To drive the program the following costs are used: 
• 
Each generator type has a fixed cost/MW, between 10 and 20 E/MW. Wind is set at 10.0 
E/MW. The other renewables can not have the  same costs, as that could result in having 
multiple generators connected to the same bus with the same costs and this would prohibit 
convergence. Other renewables are therefore set at a slightly higher cost, as the study focuses 
on the utilization of wind power. The cost of interconnectors, fossil fuels and nuclear can be 
set and will vary. As a default the cost for real power is set: 
➢ Oil & Gas > Interconnector > Nuclear > Other Renewables > Wind 
• 
In general importing power will be more expensive than exporting and the interconnector cost 
is therefore adjusted with a slight curve (quadratic equation) where the cost per unit becomes 
progressively higher with increased import. This is a smooth function to ensure these variable 
quadratic costs do not result in convergence failure. 
• 
The optimization routine sometimes generates very high levels of reactive power to make a 
negligible reduction in real power. In the UK an industrial consumer is charged when they 
generate or require more than 35% reactive power. This has been included in the cost function 
to add a small quadratic cost to reactive power. This means that the cost will always be 
positive and that the optimization routine will try to spread out the required reactive power 
over multiple sub stations.

PandaPower works with a single file, usually called net. This file consists of a set of input DataFrames 
and after running the program also a set of result DataFrames. It is possible to define all elements of 
the net in Python, but this is not very practical for larger models with hundreds of elements. The input

PandaPower PowerFlow Optimization can result in convergence problems, even when a technical

5. 
2050 Generation, Demand & Transmission Models 
The grid will continue to adapt to changes in generation and demand. The government’s policy is often 
referred to as the Energy Trilemma, striking a balance between security of supply, decarbonization of 
the sector and providing affordable energy enabling economic growth. This study leaps over this 
challenging period of transition (analogy: rebuilding a plane in flight), and compares 4 book-end 
designs of the transmission system in 2050. Each of these transmission cases have been analysed and 
compared with the same power generation and demand model under different operating modes. 
During the study the government issued an update of the Energy Security Strategy (ref 1, 2), as a 
response to rising electricity prices and uncertainty regarding Russian gas supplies. The strategy is 
based on accelerating the decarbonization of the sector and becoming more self reliant. The 2050 
models in this study were adjusted and brought in line with this strategy update. 
 
2050 Demand Model 
When creating an estimate of the electricity demand in 2050 the phrase “how long is a piece of 
string?” comes to mind, which is echoed by the Government: 
 
Quote: UK Energy Security, April 2022 (ref 1, 2) 
Rather than making a forecast, a scenario has been developed based on a set of selected demand 
drivers. This mathematical model allows the generation of many scenarios, based on inclusion of 
certain drivers and assumptions.  
 
Demand Growth Drivers 
1. Population. In the 30 years from 1990 to 2020 UK’s population increased from 57. 2 to 67.2 
million people, a 17% increase. The government expects the population growth rate to slow 
down and reach some 74.0 million in 2050, i.e. a growth of 10% compared to 2020. Assuming 
a linear relationship between population and demand this results in DF_1 = 1.100. 
2. Road Traffic. Road traffic has 2 main drivers: the phase out of petrol / diesel and the growth 
rate of kilometres driven. As reference year 2019 has been used, avoiding the 2020 Covid 
lockdown dip. 
a. In 2019 less than 2% of the cars in the UK were electric or hybrid. For the scenario it 
is assumed that for road transport 2050 fossil fuels will be fully replaced by electric 
vehicles. In 2019 575 billion km were driven, where the growth rate was slightly higher 
than the population growth rate. The average electricity consumption is 0.2 kWh per 
km, which is biased towards low weight vehicles. Full transition of road transport 
would therefore increase average electricity demand by at least 13.1 GW in 2019.  
b. Over the last 20 years the growth rate of kilometres driven was 1.16% per year, i.e. 
more than twice the growth rate of the population. For this scenario it has been 
assumed that this growth rate will slow down (virtual working, less EU traffic, better 
public transport) and will be equal to the population growth rate. 
The combined road traffic will therefore increase average demand by DF_2 = 13.1 * 1.1 = 14.4 
GW. Note that the transition to electric vehicles on its own will increase total UK electricity 
demand by 50%! 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
 
UK Transmission Grid 2050 
Page 27 of 67 
FMK 2022, DTU 46W41 
3. Domestic and Industrial use of Fossil Fuels. In 2019 the use of fossil fuels for combustion (exc. 
transport) was 82.3 MTOE. This includes the conversion and distribution losses and excludes 
the hydrocarbons that were used as feedstock for the chemical and fertilizer industry. This 
compares with 56.6 MTOE which was used by the transport sector. Assuming a full transition 
away from fossil fuels in these sectors and a growth proportional with population, the 
domestic and industrial transition will increase demand by DF_3 = 82.3 / 56.6 * 13.1 * 1.1 = 
21.0 GW. 
4. Energy Efficiency. In the UK the annual average energy efficiency  improvement is reported to 
be 1.5% per year. This energy efficiency improvement is across al sectors. Major changes have 
been better heat isolation, energy efficient lighting, reduced idling losses, etc. It is assumed 
that this energy efficiency rate will reduce to 1.0% and this results in a demand reduction 
factor of DF_4 0.99 ^ 30 = 0.74.  
5. Peak Load Reduction: In 2020 the peak load is 150% of the annual average consumption. This 
takes place around 6PM on a cold wintery day. With the introduction of the smart grid, this 
factor between peak load and average load will be reduced: cars will be predominantly 
charged during the night hours and major appliances will kick in when electricity is cheaper. 
For this analysis it has been assumed that peak rate will be 125% of annual average  
consumption. 
6. Transmission / Distribution Load. Although small scale renewables will decrease the 
transmission fraction, this will have limited impact on the urban load centres. It was therefore 
decided to keep the 75% - 25% split as explained in the 2020 model.  
 
There are a number of drivers, which have been omitted from this scenario. The transport sector is 
focused on light vehicle road traffic. Trucking has the challenge that the weight of batteries render 
electrical trucks unattractive. Trucking  may therefore need to transition to hydrogen or ammonia 
combustion with lower efficiencies. Sea and air transport are even more challenging to become fossil 
fuel free.

For the transmission requirements both the amount of power demand and the location of power

2050 Power Generation Model 
For the coming decade the major power generation projects are specified with location, capacity and 
start-up year. Beyond this time frame, the energy transition is less well defined and often aspirational. 
There are different advisory bodies, such at the Climate Change Committee, which are tasked to advise 
government on policy. From time to time the government releases policy strategies to show to what 
extent the advice has been accepted and will be pursued as policy. Note that both these sources are 
coloured by politics. Green messages are highlighted and controversial messages are either hidden or 
skipped. As a result no consistent and comprehensive source of the make up of UK power generation 
in 2050 has been found. The following assumptions were used for the 205 power generators. 
 
Onshore Wind 
• 
Current capacity is 14 GW. The strategy aims to promote further expansion in amenable 
communities, although there is no subsidy available for this sector. Existing windfarms are 
being upgraded and it is assumed that onshore wind will grow to 20 GW. Note that only very 
large windfarms are connected to the transmission grid and growth in this sector will 
therefore have a small impact on the transmission requirements. 
 
Offshore Wind 
• 
There are firm plans to increase the existing 10 GW of offshore  wind by 20 GW (UK’s 4th Lease 
Round and the Scotwind Lease). The locations of these wind farms are part of the lease 
arrangement and are therefore known. The target of 50 GW has been brought forward to 
2030, with at least 5 GW from floating turbines. This will require fast tracking of the lease and 
approval process. For 2050 it is assumed that the total capacity of offshore wind will grow to 
70 GW. 
 
Other Renewables (Existing Technology) 
• 
The other renewables (solar, hydro, biofuel) capacity is currently 27 GW and small scale solar 
will be further promoted. Just like onshore wind the impact for the transmission grid will be 
small, as solar will be for own use or local distribution, rather than a source of the transmission 
grid. It is assumed that other renewables will grow to 40 GW by 2050. 
• 
Biofuel gets little mention in the referenced publications, mainly due to the controversy 
around air quality and the use of farmland for energy, rather than food. It is not envisaged 
that biofuel will become a major electricity source in the UK.

Green Gas - Hydrogen (Emerging Technology) 
• 
Green Gas will replace fossil fuel gas, as the flexible and reliable source of energy. When there 
is sufficient cheap energy (wind), this will be used in green gas manufacturing plants to split 
water into Hydrogen and Oxygen and the green gas will be stored in salt caverns. When there 
is insufficient wind / solar, the green gas will be burnt to supplement nuclear and inter 
connectors. Green gas therefore has 3 roles: it is a load when green gas has to be generated, 
the stock levels represent an energy storage system and when burnt it is a power generator.  
• 
The hydrogen target for 2030 has been set at 10 GW. There is no explicit mention of ammonia 
and it is therefore assumed that the hydrogen target includes ammonia as an alternative. It is 
uncertain if this target will be met and how this will grow after 2030.  
• 
In this study only the green gas for the energy sector is included. It is possible that the 
transport sector will also become a customer of green gas and this would result in an 
additional load. Assumed capacities are: 
o 2050 Green Fuel generator capacity: 42.0 MW 
o 2050 Green Fuel load capacity: 18.0 MW 
• 
It is assumed that the green fuel plants will be located on the sights of existing coal / gas power 
stations and will both contain the plants creating green fuel and the plants to burn the fuel 
and generate electricity. This would minimize environmental opposition and ensure continued 
employment opportunity in the areas where fossil fuel power stations are being phased out. 
 
Nuclear 
• 
Of the existing 7.8 GW capacity 75% will be retired in the coming years. There are specific 
plans in place to add an additional 8 GW of capacity in Hinkley Point C, Sizewell C and Bradwell, 
resulting in a 2030 capacity of 10 GW. The government strategy was used as an opportunity 
to announce a major expansion of the nuclear target and this has now been set to increase to 
24 GW by 2050.  
• 
Nuclear plants require access to water and are therefore often located near the coast and 
preferably near an estuary of a major river to have access to less corrosive sweet water. The 
location of the 24 GW capacity is assumed to be 50% at existing nuclear plants and 50% at 
suitable coastal locations where gas generation is being phased out. 
 
Quote: Location of Future Nuclear Power Plants (ref 1, 2) 
 
Interconnectors 
• 
Since 2020, IFA 2 (1000 MW, France) and North Sea Link (1400 MW, Norway) have become 
operational and ElecLink (1000 MW, Channel Tunnel) is to be commissioned in 2022. This 
increases the end 2022 capacity to 8.4 GW. Additional interconnectors under 
construction/consideration are NorthConnect (1400 MW, Norway), Viking Link (1400 MW, 
Denmark), Greenlink (500 MW, Ireland), bringing the capacity to 11.3 GW. The 1400 MW FAB 
link from France has been excluded from this list, as its planning permit was refused and is 
Russian owned. The target to increase the capacity to 18 GW was among the BEIS planning 
scenarios and this has been further increased in the model to 24 GW by 2050.  
• 
The government has called for more cost efficient ways to transmit offshore windpower to 
land. Currently every project builds its own landing cable and converter stations. As offshore

where excess power can be used to create green hydrogen factories.  
• 
Recent developments of high gas prices and the Ukrainian war has prompted the government

Transmission Cases 
In the previous chapters plausible scenarios were defined for UK electricity generation and electricity 
demand in 2050. There are different options to transmit this additional  power and the selection of 
the option will have a significant impact on how the power sector will develop in the UK and if the UK 
will again become a net exporter of power or will remain dependent on imported electricity when 
renewable capacity is low. 
 
4 Transmission cases have been evaluated and compared using the same model for supply and 
demand based on 4 operating conditions. In reality both the generation and demand side will be 
influenced by the selection of the transmission option: a flexible system will allow more export of wind 
power and a low investment option will reduce the utilization of wind power in remote areas such as 
offshore North West Scotland, which has one of the highest levels of wind energy in the world. 
 
A. Upgrade and Uprate. This is how the grid has traditionally been adapted to meet the changing 
demands. The 400 kV double circuit, bundles of 2  overheadlines will remain the backbone of 
the transmission system. Some lower voltage lines will be upgraded to 400 kV, most notably 
in Wales and Scotland to utilize the offshore wind capacity. In addition debottlenecking will 
take place to increase the transmission capacity by installing parallel double circuit OHL. 
B. UHV AC Backbone. Along the East coast of the country a new 800 kV overhead line will be 
added and connected with the 400 kV system with 8 transformer substations. This will require 
new pylons, cables and transformers at the substations. This 800 kV system will have almost 
5 times the capacity of the 400 kV OHL system. 
C. Onshore DC Overhead Line. A 6000 MW UHV DC overhead line will be installed along the East 
Coast. Where capacity allows this can be done by replacing one of the circuits from an existing 
AC overhead line with new DC bipolar lines.

D. Modular Offshore DC Cable. This will be the most expensive option, but will also create the 
highest flexibility. The DC transmission system will be integrated with the DC interconnectors 
and the DC power from the offshore wind farms. This will reduce the cost for offshore 
windfarms, as it eliminates the requirement for dedicated landfall cables and shore based 
convertor stations to tie into the net. It will also promote the export of UK windpower through 
the interconnectors

Operating Modes 
In 2020 the operating modes of the transmission system depended on the availability of wind power 
and if average or peak load was modelled. In 2050 the green gas adds an additional dimension to the 
operating modes. When there is plenty gas in storage, this gas will be used to generate power at low 
wind and at high wind the TSO will aim to maximize export. When there is insufficient gas in storage, 
at low wind the TSO will aim not to deplete the reserves by maximizing import and nuclear power 
generation, where at high wind the reserves are to be replenished by operating the green gas 
manufacturing plants. To keep the number of cases manageable it has been selected to run the low 
wind cases at peak load (assessing transmission constraints) and the high wind cases at average load 
(assessing export constraints).

TSO’s objective of how the system is to be operated.

Post Processing 
The simulation models create a PandaPower net file with all the input and the results for each of the 
16 cases. These are saved to file with transmission case and operating mode in the file name. In a 
separate program the 16 files are opened and selected results (generator results and line results) are 
read and aggregated in a DataFrame file, which again is saved to file. The generator file is loaded into 
an interactive panel program, which allows analysing the data per case and per generator type. A 
separate program uses this combined dataset to analyse a single parameter, such as the cost per MW, 
and displays this in a 4 x 4 matrix. The line data is analysed to calculate the boundary power flows, 
where the boundaries have been expanded to include the new AC lines and the DC lines. 
 
The fact that the programs PowerPanda, GeoPandas and Panel all work with Pandas DataFrames, 
greatly helped with combining the input and result data from one program to another.

Conclusions & Policy Recommendations 
 
Technical Conclusions 
1. In 2050, the 4 different transmission cases can all provide the average and peak transmission 
power flows as envisaged by the described plausible power generation and demand scenario. 
Not included in this study is to what extent the system can provide the reliability, dynamic 
flexibility and power quality required of the transmission system. 
2. In 2050, on windy days the North South powerflow can increase by a factor 5 to 17.7 GW, 
providing that a transmission system has been put in place, which can handle such a 
powerflow. The AC400 case is limited to less than a quarter of this powerflow, limiting both 
the use of the cheapest generation source and limiting the exports. Powerflow is therefore 
not a given quantity, which need to be accommodated, it is a measure to what extent available 
cheap power sources can be utilized. 
3. Every power system utilizing renewables needs a storage system. In the study this is provided 
by green gas. When power is cheap, energy is used to split water and the hydrogen is stored 
in salt caverns. When there are insufficient renewables, the green gas is burnt to provide 
electricity. This means that green gas is a load, a storage system and a power generator. The 
energy efficiency of the green gas cycle is at best 50%. A green gas system therefore adds to 
the electrical power demand and the required transmission capacity. 
4. The main differentiator between the cases is the extent to which they efficiently transmit the 
cheap wind power from the North to the demand centres in Central and Southern England, 
use the green gas sparingly and support the export of electricity through the interconnectors. 
a. Continuation of upgrading and uprating the existing grid to 400 kV system appears to 
be the least attractive, as power sources with 40% - 80% higher prices are utilized to 
meet the demands, while Sottish offshore wind capacity remains idle. When there is 
no wind, more green gas is required to keep the system running, which will place an 
additional burden on the capacity of green gas manufacturing plants and storage 
facilities. 
b. The Eastern 800 kV AC option performed well. From a reliability point of view, such a 
system would probably need to be expanded by a second North South UHV OHL.   
c. The flexible offshore DC cable system is technically superior. This case has as an added 
benefit that windfarm developers would be able to connect their DC power offshore, 
eliminating the need for landfall and onshore converter station permitting and 
construction. This case is however also by far the most capex intensive and it is quite 
likely that similar benefits can be achieved with a less ambitious plan. 
 
Observations 
• 
The energy transition will increase the powerflow in the UK, both due to an increase in 
electricity demand and due to the growth and shift towards the North of the Offshore wind 
industry. This is currently seen as a threat to security of supply and affordable electricity prices 
(2 aspects of the Energy Trilemma).  
• 
The energy transition will also result in a much higher and more dynamic price differential of 
energy sources. Wind energy and solar will be cheap when available; green gas electricity will 
be at least twice as expensive (opex) as renewable energy and the cost of imported and 
exported electricity will depend on where there are favourable wind and solar conditions. 
Relying on imports can be very expensive, when there is a lack of renewables over Europe. 
==PAGE:45==
 
 
 
UK Transmission Grid 2050 
Page 45 of 67 
FMK 2022, DTU 46W41 
• 
This price differential creates an opportunity. A high capacity and flexible transmission grid 
will be able to utilize the cheapest electricity sources for conventional demand and will be 
able to feed more cheap electricity to the green gas manufacturing plants. In addition a 
flexible high capacity grid will make better use of market opportunities regarding the import 
and export of power.  
• 
The decision on the grid of the future is therefore much more than a cost question. The type 
of grid the UK will put in place will shape the energy transition in terms of: 
o Size, location and speed of (floating) offshore wind farm development. 
o Size of green gas manufacturing, storage and combustion plants. 
o The position of the UK energy services sector in Europe. 
o The role of the UK in European power market. Net importer or exporter. 
 
Policy Recommendations: 
1. The current transmission policy of uprate and upgrade the 400 kV distribution system has 
served the country well. To prepare for 2050 continuation of this policy would hinder the 
development of esp. the offshore wind potential in the North of the countries. It is therefore 
recommended to prepare for an UHV system (AC or DC, OHL or cable) to support the 400 kV 
AC transmission grid and unlock the wind potential in the North. 
2. The UK was an energy exporter during the oil and gas boom, but due to the fossil fuel decline 
is now a net importer of both energy and electricity. The UK has the wind potential to again 
become a net energy exporter, with benefits to the energy sector, the economy and treasury. 
If becoming a net green energy exporter is justified and supported, than the government is 
recommended to express this as part of the vision for the energy transition. 
3. It is recommended to extend the transmission planning horizon from 10 years to 30 years and 
supported by the energy transition vision, to translate this into a 2050 transmission master 
plan, based on scenario planning and supported with economical analysis. Such a plan would 
form the blueprint of the transmission investments, to ensure that the individual projects 
required to keep the system running, contribute to efficiently realizing the master plan, rather 
than interfere with its implementation.  
4. Based on the transmission plan, offshore wind developers will have more certainty regarding 
the utilization of their generation capacity. Reduced uncertainty will reduce the hurdle to 
develop and support the government’s recent wish for accelerated (floating) offshore wind 
development. 
5. Some upfront investment in the different options for the UHV transmission system is most 
likely required to support the robustness of the selected options and ensure that British 
energy industry will be a dominant player in the energy transition and a net exporter of 
expertise and support services. Providing an efficient and flexible infrastructure will reduce 
the requirement of financial incentives to stimulate and accelerate further offshore wind 
development.

Electric Transmission Line Parameters 
To study the transmission system it is paramount to ensure the modelling of AC transmission lines is 
realistic. To achieve this the simulation of a single line with PandaPower is compared with the theory 
of overhead line  parameters (ref 1 – Ch 3). The theoretical aspects of this comparison are split into 
line parameters, transmission matrices and application of these for some example lines. 
An overhead line is represented by a single line, but in reality contain many lines to cater for the 3 
phases, line bundles and multiple circuits. The single line parameters are not just a function of the 
electric wire selected, but also of the wire distances in the bundles, the distances between the bundles 
and the distances between the bundles and the ground. Some of these parameters are also influenced 
by temperature and humidity.

a) Resistance R. This is mainly dependent on the number of cables, the materials used in the 
cable and the size of the cables. It is possible to calculate the resistivity based on the 
material(s) and cross sectional area , but it is more accurate to use the manufacturer’s tables, 
as this includes the increased path length of stranded wire and the Skin effect, which is 
dependent on the frequency. The temperature of the wire depends on the ambient 
temperature, ohmic heating of the line, heating by solar radiation and cooling by the wind. 
The recommendation to calculate the resistivity at 50 C is used for this verification.

general model. For power transmission capacity the temperature is often the limiting factor, 
which has been calculated for the most critical lines and are entered in the model as a current 
limitation. The resistance in the tables is per cable and will therefore have to be divided by 
the number of cables when a bundle is modelled.

• 
The Inductance and Capacitance are only dependent on the cable configuration and not on 
the cable material nor the power transmission.  The parameters GMD and GMR can be 
calculated to describe these configurations.  
• 
Where inductance increases with GMD and decreases with GMR, capacitance does the 
reverse. The distances within the bundle and the distances between the phases can therefore 
be optimized to ensure the energy in the electric and magnetic field are equal for the design 
power. 
• 
Such a line will not consume any reactive power and has a natural surge impedance load SIL. 
When power transmission exceeds this SIL, shunt capacitors are required to reduce the 
voltage drop and improve the power factor. When power transmission is below this SIL shunt 
reactors may be required to reduce the Ferranti effect and improve the power factor.

To improve the capacity of this overhead line the SIL needs to be improved and the most effective 
ways of doing is this to increase the Voltage and/or the number of cables in the bundle. Larger lines 
with less resistivity or hollow conductors will also improve the SIL. The SIL increases with spacing in 
the bundle and decreases with distance between the phases. UHV lines use bundles of 8 to 10 lines 
with large spacings creating bundles with a diameter of more than a meter. High voltage lines are 
limited by the clearance of the phase bundles and an optimal distance can be achieved by placing the 
bundles in a triangle, rather than in a horizontal line as was calculated in the text book example. 
Increasing the SIL will reduce the reactive power and improve the power factor when the load remains 
higher than the SIL. When the load or the available power reduces, the SIL will become much higher 
than the load and then significant amounts of reactive power will be generated in the line, which will 
need to be absorbed by shunt reactors. There is therefore a down side of oversizing an overhead line.

Attachment 6: Ferranti Effect Model Verification 
PandaPower runs resulted in surprising voltage and reactive power results. To better understand this 
and verify the results, simple line models were made with the dynamic Electric Circuit Simulation 
Program (ECSP), a freeware program, which has been built by Georg Schett (ref 1). 
 
The example below shows a 750 km powerline simulated in 5 sections to allow monitoring of the 
voltage along the line. When the load at the receiving end is low (i.e. lower than the surge impedance 
load SIL), the voltage along the line progressively increases, which can damage equipment. This is 
called the Ferranti effect. The observed anomalies were therefore not a program error, but were a 
result of a mismatch of the loads and the line characteristics. Power can flow from low to high voltage. 
 
 
 
The Ferranti effect can be suppressed by adding a reactor at the receiving end. 
 
 
Reference: 
1. Georg Schett, Power Engineering Tutorials, YouTube series & web dynamic simulator of power 
engineering.

Att 9: Managing Voltage Levels with transformers 
To maintain the Voltage in the model between 0.98 and 1.02 pu, booster transformers would be re-
quired at each substation in the model. PandaPower requires a fixed high voltage and low voltage bus. 
As the powerflow can reverse (see 2020 model without wind) the model would require 2 transformers 
at each substation, a step-down and a step-up trafo. This was modelled for simple networks, as shown 
below.  
Implementation of realistic voltage levels would require: 
• 
Additional substations in long lines, where the voltage drop exceeds 0.04 pu.  
• 
Tripling the buses in all substations. 
• 
Adding 2 trafos for each substation with automatic tap control settings. 
Attempts to model this for more complex networks resulted in convergence problems and it was 
therefore decided not to manage the system voltage within the realistic range of 0.98 and 1.02 pu.
