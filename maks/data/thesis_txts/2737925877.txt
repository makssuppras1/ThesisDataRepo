Abstract
The impact┬¡driven installation of a monopile foundation of an offshore wind turbine
is a very widespread method used in the industry. This operation applies signifi┬¡
cant dynamic loads to the pile that are transmitted to the soil, and that can affect
the leg load distribution and the final penetration of the adjacent jacked┬¡up spud┬¡
can. As pile diameters continue to increase rapidly, the dynamic loads required to
reach necessary depths are escalating, intensifying concerns about their impact.
The investigation was prompted by observed incidents of leg load loss during the
driving of monopiles, a phenomenon of considerable potential consequences for
the jack┬¡up unit. The study utilizes real┬¡life data for the soil properties, hammer
logs, and penetration records for both the spudcan and monopile to construct a de┬¡
tailed 3D Coupled Eulerian┬¡Lagrangian (CEL) finite element model using Abaqus/┬¡
Explicit. The primary aim of this thesis is to develop a precise numerical model
that accurately captures the observed phenomena and to benchmark it with em┬¡
pirical data. The simulations focus on the application of quasi┬¡static models with
the Mohr┬¡Coulomb Plasticity constitutive model for the problems of spudcan and
monopile penetration. A parametric study concerning different sand strength prop┬¡
erties is also launched in an effort to account for the inherent inaccuracies of the
input soil parameters collected from the offshore wind farm. The results indicated
that the Mohr┬¡Coulomb constitutive model accurately models spudcan penetra┬¡
tion, as verified by the industry guidelines. For the monopile installation, higher
friction angles showcased an increase in soil resistance, with localized stress ef┬¡
fects primarily around the monopile. However, the numerical models captured a
greater reduction in spudcan reaction forces than observed in real┬¡life scenarios,
suggesting the need for more sophisticated soil modeling to enhance predictive
accuracy.

Introduction
The global transition towards renewable energy sources is imperative to combat
climate change and reduce dependency on fossil fuels. Among renewable energy
technologies, offshore wind power stands out due to its potential for high energy
yield and relatively consistent power generation compared to onshore sources.
As a result, the offshore wind industry is experiencing rapid growth, driven by
technological advancements and increasing governmental support. However, this
expansion is not without challenges, particularly when it comes to the installation
of offshore wind turbines.
The development and deployment of offshore wind farms, in a short period of time,
are complex challenges. One of the critical aspects of offshore wind farm installa┬¡
tion is the setting of foundations, such as monopiles that is a very popular solution
for shallow to medium┬¡depth locations, which are commonly installed using im┬¡
pact driving techniques. According to Wind Europe 2021 statistics, the monopiles
constituted 80.5% of all installations in 2020 [1]. This fast┬¡paced development is
achieved by wind turbines with higher capacity, something that leads to increasing
monopile diameters in order to accommodate larger vertical loads and overturn┬¡
ing moments of the newer models. Alongside the pile diameters, the hammer
dimensions are also increasing, so it can be secured that the full penetration is
achieved, leading to more significant loads transferred to the soil. This method,
while effective, involves significant interaction with the seabed, where the impact
driving can cause cyclic degradation of the soilÔÇÖs properties. This degradation can
affect the stability and longevity of the structures, thus posing potential delays and
safety risks in the construction and operational phases.
Moreover, as the demand for offshore wind energy increases, developers are
forced to consider more challenging locations, including seismic zones and areas
with complex seabed compositions. These environments demand even greater
precision and innovation in installation techniques to ensure wind turbinesÔÇÖ struc┬¡
tural integrity and operational reliability. The industryÔÇÖs push into these new loca┬¡
tions highlights the need for advanced research in understanding and mitigating
the effects of impact driving on seabed soil dynamics.
The motivation for this research stems from real┬¡life incidents observed during
the installation of monopiles for an offshore wind turbine, which highlighted po┬¡
tential issues in current installation practices. The incidents involved a noticeable
leg load loss in each case, initially attributed to cyclic soil degradation due to the
dynamic loading imposed by the impact driving of the pile. This load loss, indi┬¡
cating additional penetration of the spudcan, could lead to significant damage to
the jack┬¡up unit. The redistribution of the loads on the other legs, if it exceeds the
design limit, can have, as a result, the overstressing of the legs or even potential
failure in extreme cases. Additionally, an unprecedented load loss over the limits
can be the cause of the hull experiencing uneven forces or even uneven forces in
the pinions of the jacking system. Such consequences might not necessarily lead

to failure in all instances but can be responsible for the wear and tear of the jack┬¡
ing system, reducing this way the life span of the jack┬¡up vessel or endangering
future operations. In order to ensure the realization of the sustainability goals that
we need to reach, the longevity of these vessels is of paramount importance.
This phenomenon prompted a detailed examination of a single location with such
behavior, using a combination of real┬¡world operational data and advanced simu┬¡

our understanding of the interactions between installation processes and seabed
soil properties, this study seeks to contribute to the broader goal of advancing
renewable energy technologies. This not only supports environmental and sus┬¡
tainability goals but also enhances energy security and supports economic devel┬¡
opment in coastal communities worldwide.
1.1
Objective of the project
This thesis aims to address these challenges by focusing on the effects of impact
driving on adjacent spudcan installations. By exploring this specific aspect, the
research contributes to developing more resilient and efficient installation tech┬¡
niques that could enhance the safety and stability of offshore wind structures.
The problem was simulated using the Abaqus/Explicit software to address the
complex nature of the soil┬¡structure interaction and the large deformations in┬¡
volved. This choice was guided by the need for a robust computational tool capa┬¡
ble of handling the Coupled Eulerian┬¡Lagrangian (CEL) method, which is partic┬¡
ularly well┬¡suited for scenarios involving significant deformations and interactions
between multiple bodies.
The simulation phase commenced with the development of a simplified numerical
model of spudcan penetration. This model served as a preliminary step to verify
the more complex, dual┬¡structure simulation involving both the monopile and the
spudcan. The objective of this initial model was to replicate the spudcan pen┬¡
etration during the pre┬¡loading phase to validate the accuracy of the simulation
parameters and methodologies before applying them to the more intricate system
dynamics of the actual installation scenario.
Once verified, the simulation was extended to a comprehensive model that in┬¡
cluded both the monopile and the spudcan. This model aimed to capture the
interaction effects and quantify the impact of various operational parameters on
the structural integrity and performance of the installation. By simulating the real┬¡
life conditions as closely as possible, the study sought to identify critical factors
contributing to load loss and propose practical solutions to enhance the reliability
and safety of offshore wind turbine installations.

Geotechnical Behavior and
Installation Process
The interaction of the soil structure in a monopile foundation through offshore
hammering operations leads to an incidence of damage in the marine floor pro┬¡
duced mainly by the generation and propagation of stress waves and the resulting
soil degradation. The current chapter attempts to present the geotechnical impli┬¡
cations of such phenomena due to these stresses, starting with the fundamental
physical processes and moving to wider consequences for soil behavior and the
properties of soils, as also to provide a brief presentation of the installation process
of the an offshore WTG foundation.
3.1
Wave Propagation during impact driving
A pile installed on the sea floor under a hammering action generates vibrations
seen as P and S waves in the soil medium. P┬¡waves are longitudinal, and the
motion involves the compression and expansion of the particles in the direction of
wave propagation. These are the fastest seismic waves and can travel through
any type of material, including fluids. In the context of offshore monopile installa┬¡
tions, P┬¡waves are significant because they directly alter the pore water pressure
and the soil particles. On the other hand, S┬¡waves are transverse and consist of
particle movement perpendicular to the direction of the wave propagation. These
will only travel through solids, as fluids do not support shear stresses. The trans┬¡
mission of S┬¡waves through marine soils could induce changes in soil structure,
mainly through the initiation of the generation of the already┬¡mentioned shear

intensity of these waves depend on hammer energy, pile geometry, and soil type.
As they travel through the ground, they generate cyclic loads and strains that put
the soil structure and properties through significant evolution over time. The dif┬¡
ference in how these two waves propagate is illustrated as Figure 3.1 by Kramer
(1996) [12].
The pile driving process, apart from the P and S waves, which originate from
the pile toe and expand outwards, leads to the generation of Rayleigh and Love
waves on the seabed surface═¥ they are combined on the seabed surface. The
Rayleigh waves cause an anti┬¡clockwise elliptical movement of the soil on a verti┬¡

of combined 1D compression and shear, while the Love waves force the soil to
a horizontal and transverse to the direction of propagation soil movement result┬¡

vibration propagation mechanisms become more complex in layered soil profiles,
where additional waves are generated due to reflections and refractions at layer

impact driving on ground vibrations. By using the average peak particle velocities

although it does not follow the same trend in all directions as the magnitude of the

3.2
Cyclic soil degradation during impact driving
Impact driving, as a monopile installation method in sandy soils, can significantly
influence the design and sustainability of foundations as it poses some very se┬¡
rious geotechnical challenges. Such an installation method is very widespread
in the offshore wind industry for monopile foundations and is realized by driving
piles into the ground using heavy blows from a hammer. It is a very efficient and
effective method for penetrating dense soil layers. However, it includes complex
interactions between the pile and the surrounding sandy soil.
The soil behavior during the monopile installation is different depending on whether
the pile is open┬¡ended or close┬¡ended. The former monopile category records a
higher rate of pile penetration in the soil than the rate of soil entering the inner part
of the pile, a behavior that can result in the soil┬¡plugging effect, mostly observed
for small diameter piles and in dense soil formations [14, 15]. In this instance, the
problem starts resembling the soil behavior under the penetration of close┬¡ended
piles, leading to more significant soil resistance and endangering the whole pro┬¡
cess if soil plugging has not been considered. Results of the numerical analysis
indicate that the jacked piles show a higher tendency to lead to a plug formation
rather than the dynamic installation methods, like impact and vibratory driving,
due to the more significant horizontal stress peaks captured inside the pile during
the installation process [14].

The maximum effective radial stress of the monopile shaft is achieved the mo┬¡

methods of pile installation, as it leads to more soil contraction than monotonic
shearing. These behaviors have been verified by using the Coupled Eulerian┬¡

monopile installation procedure is evident. Additionally, in this study, the effect
of friction fatigue, introduced by [17], is also explained and showcased from the
numerical results. This phenomenon describes the behavior of reducing radial
stress with increasing penetration depths. In a long┬¡term time frame, the reduced
radial stress may gradually increase again, although this is not linked to the pore
water pressure dissipation rather to the relaxation of initial high┬¡stress gradients
near the pile shaft.

This stress can then be used to calculate the soilÔÇÖs excess pore pressure and re┬¡

methodologies includes a certain degree of uncertainty.
In saturated soils, pile driving can generate excess pore pressures, potentially
reducing effective soil stresses, leading to soil strength loss and liquefaction. In
scenarios where significant spudcan settlement or liquefaction is possible, con┬¡
ducting a fully non┬¡linear, cyclically degrading numerical analysis with the most
advanced soil modeling technology is recommended. Some significant factors
that influence the generation and dissipation of excess pore water pressure in┬¡

from the piling operation [18].
3.3
WTG foundation installation procedure
The process of installing an offshore wind turbine generator (WTG) using a monopile
foundation involves a series of steps that are dependent on one another to ensure
the structural integrity and operational efficiency of the wind turbine. Upon arriving
at the installation location, the jack┬¡up vessel lifts itself above sea level using its
legs, providing a stable platform for installation activities free from wave and cur┬¡
rent interferences. This is achieved by driving the spudcans, the usually conical┬¡
shaped foundation of each leg, into the soil by applying a pre┬¡loading force larger
than the one that is expected to reach during the operational stage (i.e., founda┬¡
tion installation in this case). Once the vessel is safely jacked up, the monopile

Numerical Analysis
The installation of an offshore monopile is an inherently large deformation prob┬¡
lem. The classical FE Lagrangian formulations offer a very accurate depiction of
the interface of two parts for the whole duration of the analysis, due to the fact
that both the mesh and the material are deforming together since the movement
of the continuum is regulated based on the material coordinates and time. Such
properties could lead to excessive mesh distortions concerning a simulation with
large deformations, as investigated in this thesis. Another approach would be
the Eulerian analysis, where the mesh refrains from being distorted, allowing the
material to move freely through the Eulerian elements. This method is mainly ap┬¡
plied in fluid mechanics, and a possible drawback is the high chance of numerical
diffusion in the case of multiple Eulerian materials [21].
In an effort to combine the advantages of both methods, the Coupled Eulerian┬¡
Lagrangian (CEL) method was used. In the numerical predictions, using this CEL
formulation, the motion of Eulerian material along the mesh is tracked through the
computation of the materialÔÇÖs Eulerian volume fraction (EVF). This fraction rep┬¡
resents the volume occupied by material within an Eulerian element. When the
EVF is equal to 1, it means the element is completely filled. When the EVF is at 0,
the element is empty. The interaction between Eulerian and Lagrangian materials
is governed by a general contact mechanism that functions by a penalty contact
method. In this general contact approach, unlike in specific contact enforcement,
Lagrangian elements are allowed to pass through the Eulerian mesh freely. How┬¡
ever, they will meet an Eulerian element carrying any material, as a non┬¡zero EVF
indicates. [21]

dynamic analysis of solids and structures using explicit time integration. An ex┬¡

Unlike implicit methods, required in Abaqus/Standard, which needs a solution of
a set of simultaneous linear equations, this explicit method calculates the systemÔÇÖs
state at a future time, t + ╬öt, from the state at the current time, t. Integration steps

The analysis consisted from two steps, one where the geostatic stress state is es┬¡

a second one where the spudcan is being driven into the soil. For both steps, the

the normal velocity component to zero, the plane is fixed in that direction, while
movement parallel to the plane is allowed. This constraint is essential for accu┬¡

for maintaining the simulationÔÇÖs integrity. For the spudcan, all movements were
prohibited during the first step, and during the second, only the vertical movement
along the direction of axis Z was allowed.

explicit solution method has proven valuable in solving quasi┬¡static problems, but

it is mainly a true dynamic procedure originally developed to model high┬¡speed

solution, the event must be accelerated in some way. The problem is that as the

equilibrium in which inertial forces become more dominant. The goal is to model
the process in the shortest time period in which inertial forces remain insignificant
[22].

a smooth step amplitude curve can be applied using only the initial and final data

user to perform a quasi┬¡static analysis without generating waves due to disconti┬¡

5.1
Numerical challenges
The finite element (FE) simulations are very strong tools for understanding com┬¡
plex problems, like geotechnical interactions during large displacement offshore
installations, but they inherently include simplifications that introduce some dis┬¡
crepancies compared to real┬¡world behaviors. Additionally, there are some re┬¡
alistic limitations that concern the computational resources and duration of each
analysis, which also affect the accuracy of the results or the details of the simu┬¡
lation. This section outlines key challenges encountered during the simulation of
the spudcan and monopile penetration processes, as also some parameters that
could give better insight into the current topic:
ÔÇó The Mohr┬¡Coulomb constitutive model used in the simulations does not ac┬¡
count for the transient pore pressures that can significantly influence soil
behavior under dynamic loading conditions. This omission primarily derives
from computational constraints, as modeling these pressures would sub┬¡
stantially increase the simulation time and complexity. This limitation is very
important when considering that real┬¡life offshore installations usually inter┬¡
act with neighboring structures by the alterations in pore pressure condi┬¡
tions, as mentioned in chapter 3.
ÔÇó Studies, using more sophisticated constitutive models like the hypoplastic
one, have shown that the impact driving of monopiles causes considerable
changes in the sandÔÇÖs void ratio during the piling operation [25, 26, 27].
This behavior cannot be accurately captured with the Mohr┬¡Coulomb model,
simplifying soil behavior to plasticity without accounting for the densification
effects in real soils under cyclic loading.
ÔÇó The computational time of the analysis constrained the model by relatively
short boundaries, which can lead to wave reflections that inaccurately rep┬¡
Effect of Offshore Monopile Driving on the Adjacent Spudcan Penetration
29
==PAGE:40==
resent the semi┬¡infinite nature of real soil media. These reflections can po┬¡
tentially skew the results by introducing artificial stress waves that do not
occur in field conditions.
ÔÇó The accuracy of input soil properties is another critical limitation. The vari┬¡
ability of soil properties laterally across the site was not fully captured, pri┬¡
marily because cone penetration tests (CPTs) were conducted only at the
center of wind turbine locations, whereas the vessels are often jacked up
at some distance from these points (approximately 28m from monopile cen┬¡
ter to spudcan center for the investigated case). This discrepancy can lead
to significant deviations in the prediction of soil behavior under the spud┬¡
can and monopile due to potential differences in soil conditions between the
modeled and actual installation locations.
With these points in mind, the study aims to approximate quasi┬¡static conditions
through controlled loading rates, thereby mitigating but not entirely eliminating
dynamic effects. The adoption of larger elements near the boundaries serves
to dampen wave propagation effects artificially, enabling to focus on the primary
geotechnical behaviors without extensive dynamic complications. However, the
modelÔÇÖs absence of explicit damping mechanisms and reliance on the elementsÔÇÖ
size for wave attenuation necessitates a cautious interpretation of the simulation
outcomes, acknowledging the potential for non┬¡physical behaviors influencing the
results.

Conclusion
The results from the Abaqus/Explicit analysis using the coupled Eulerian┬¡Lagrangian
(CEL) method for simulating the penetration of a spudcan and a monopile provide
insightful conclusions regarding the soil┬¡structure interaction in offshore installa┬¡
tions and how a simplified constitutive model like Mohr┬¡Coulomb could capture
it. This study specifically focused on capturing the interactions between a jacked
spudcan and the monopile that was being driven at a distance of 18m.
The results of the spudcan penetration model proved to be in good accordance
with the established industry guidelines of InSafeJIP and ISO. In particular, until
the depth of the maximum bearing capacity area, all three approaches had similar
results, while after that, the InSafeJIP method underestimated the soil bearing ca┬¡
pacity. Both the ISO and the Abaqus results reached a bearing capacity that was
smaller than the actual one by approximately 9%, a deviation that is not alarming
considering the different soil profiles that existed in reality.
Concerning the model with both structures being simulated, apart from their inter┬¡
action during the monopile installation, the effect of different sand strength prop┬¡
erties to account for potential inaccuracies in the soil properties obtained from
the field was also investigated. The reaction forces experienced by the monopile
during its penetration exhibited an expected increase with higher friction angles,
indicating stronger soil resistance. For example, the model with a friction angle of
37o recorded a reaction force of 14.2 MN, which is 11% higher than that with a 33o
friction angle. This trend aligns with the understanding that higher friction angles
correspond to stronger soil characteristics, requiring greater forces for penetra┬¡
tion.
The stress contours for vertical and horizontal stresses around the monopile and
spudcan showed that the monopileÔÇÖs effect was localized near its vicinity, with
minimal impact on the spudcan located 18m away. The stress distribution patterns
indicated that higher friction angles resulted in deeper stress development, though
the overall influence remained localized around the monopile.
The horizontal and vertical displacements in the spudcan area were minimal, not
exceeding three millimeters, indicating that the monopile driving had a negligi┬¡
ble effect on the spudcan in terms of displacements. The horizontal and vertical
accelerations recorded near the spudcan showed residual effects from previous
steps, with higher friction angles resulting in slightly larger fluctuations and spikes.
The reduction in the spudcanÔÇÖs vertical reaction force during monopile installation
was captured effectively by the numerical models, though the magnitude of the
reduction was significantly higher than field observations. This discrepancy sug┬¡
gests that while the simplified Mohr┬¡Coulomb model can capture general trends, it
may not fully represent the complex soil behavior and interactions under dynamic
loading conditions.

for further refinement to enhance predictive accuracy.

Further Research
The findings of this thesis have illuminated several key aspects of soil┬¡structure in┬¡
teraction during offshore monopile and spudcan installations. However, the com┬¡
plexities inherent in geotechnical engineering and the limitations encountered dur┬¡
ing the simulations highlight the need for continued investigation. Further research
should address the simplifications and assumptions made in this study, particu┬¡
larly regarding soil modeling, simulation accuracy, and real┬¡world applicability. By
exploring advanced constitutive models, like the hypoplastic with the intergranu┬¡
lar extension (see Appendix), which allows the hypoplastic model to accurately
capture the soil behavior at small strains and enhances the modelÔÇÖs capability
to simulate cyclic loading by incorporating the effects of intergranular strain, the
model will be able to account for the effects of cyclic and dynamic loads more
effectively and capture the nonlinear response of soil, including both plastic and
elastic deformations.
The incorporation of the pore water pressure in the analysis, along with the use of
dynamic loading, will help to better resemble the real┬¡life scenario. It will enable
the capture of the main factors that lead to cyclic soil degradation since such a
model will be able to calculate the excess pore pressure and its dissipation (com┬¡
putationally expensive action). This way, more realistic soil mechanisms will be
activated, suggesting a numerical prediction closer to the actual case. Addition┬¡
ally, incorporating these factors will enhance the modelÔÇÖs ability to simulate the
time┬¡dependent behavior of the soil, providing deeper insights into the interac┬¡
tions between the soil and the structure under dynamic conditions.
Lastly, another step towards more accurate and reliable predictive numerical tools
would be the simulation of layered soil profiles═¥ despite all the numerical struggles,
it entails the accurate modeling of a monopile being driven through various soil
layers. The numerical difficulties include handling the discontinuities and varying
material properties at the interfaces between different soil layers, such as sand,
clay, and sand. Additionally, the complexity increases due to the need for more
sophisticated constitutive models to capture the distinct behaviors of each layer
under dynamic loading and the requirement for fine mesh discretization to rep┬¡
resent the stress and strain gradients accurately. These challenges necessitate
substantial computational resources and advanced numerical techniques to en┬¡
sure stable and convergent solutions. Future studies can build upon this work to
develop more for offshore foundation design.

Appendix ┬¡ Hypoplastic Model
This appendix presents the methodology and results obtained using the hypoplas┬¡
tic constitutive model, which were not included in the main body of the thesis due
to their unrealistic nature and the constraints of a tight timeline that did not allow
them to be fixed. The hypoplastic model was selected for its advanced capabili┬¡
ties in simulating the complex behavior of non┬¡cohesive soils under cyclic loading
and for a straight comparison with the Mohr┬¡Coulomb model, as the geometry
and the analysis steps were the same. The subroutines with VUMAT codes for
the implementation of the hypoplasticity were provided by Dr. Staubach, whose
results on similar large deformation problems can be found in [34, 35]. For this
specific project, these codes were provided modified so they excluded the hydro┬¡
mechanical coupling due to the increased complexity of such models but also in
an effort to be directly comparable with the results of the Mohr┬¡Coulomb models
presented in the main body of the thesis. The challenges that were encountered
highlight the need for further refinement and development of this model for the
current project. Documenting these findings serves as a foundation for future re┬¡
search aimed at improving the model and serves as a first step toward a more
accurate model that hopefully will be able to come closer to the actual results
obtained from the field.
A.1
Hypoplastic Model with Intergranular Strain
Extension ┬¡ Basic Principles
The behavior of granular materials, such as soils, is complex due to their grain
structure, which requires sophisticated numerical formulations to approximate their
mechanical properties. The hypoplastic model is a powerful constitutive model
used to simulate this behavior under various loading conditions, particularly for

Niemunis identified that the original hypoplastic model exhibited too low shear

accuracy under cyclic loading. [39]

strain [38].
