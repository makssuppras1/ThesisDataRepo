Abstract
This master thesis analyzes the possibility of European offshore wind developers to reduce their green-
house gas emissions through implementing green steel and green fuels in their projects. The purpose of
this thesis is to highlight the challenges and possible solutions to implement these technologies, thereby
giving insights to an offshore wind developer’s procurement and sustainability strategies towards 2030.
This is conducted through a gap analysis, which analyzes the current and desired state of utilizing green
steel and green fuels in European floating offshore wind projects. It also highlights the differences
between these states, what the consequences of these differences are, and actions to reduce those
consequences. The analysis is done through a comprehensive literature study. The literature study
presents an assessment of the market, policies and technical aspects of the current and estimated future
state of the steel industry, maritime sector, and the floating offshore wind industry. The feasibility of
an offshore wind developer implementing the technologies are based on this assessment.
To assess the current state and the potential of implementing green steel and green fuels for an offshore
wind developer, a Strength, Weaknesses, Opportunities and Threats (SWOT) analysis is conducted.
The SWOT analysis identifies the internal and external advantages and disadvantages an offshore
wind developer has in this regard. The analysis is based on the general attributes and factors of the
most established offshore wind developers in Europe.
The evaluation of the consequence of the gap between the current and desired state is presented
through a risk matrix.
The risk matrix identifies risks seen as obstacles to the potential of the
implementation of green steel and green fuels in floating offshore wind projects.
The risk matrix
evaluates the probability and impact of risks hindering the implementation of green steel and green
fuel. It also evaluates how much influence and control the developers have of the identified risks. The
risks and obstacles form the base for which mitigating actions are presented. The actions are divided
by direct and indirect initiatives, as well as initiatives by regulators.
It is concluded that it is unlikely that floating offshore wind developers can implement 100% green
steel and green fuels in their projects by 2033. The technologies will present an increase in capital
and operational expenditures, as well as uncertainty and risk. This would significantly reduce the
developer’s potential of winning auctions, and receiving funding and CFDs. However, with a smaller
share of the technologies, the risks have a minor impact on the project’s feasibility, and could be a
competitive advantage for the developers.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Floating Offshore Wind (FOW) is an emerging sector that has significant potential to provide green
energy for the world. The European Union (EU) has set out its goal of establishing at least 60 GW
of offshore wind by 2030 and 300 GW by 2050 (EU, 2022). These goals have a crucial impact on the
decarbonization strategy of the European energy sector in the long run. However, the carbon-intensive
processes involved in the manufacturing, installation, and operations of the wind farms are expected
to have a negative effect on the EUs carbon footprint. Furthermore, the sector is still in its early
stages compared to other renewable and conventional energy sources.
1.2
Research question
The primary drivers of Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions in a FOW project are the emissions asso-
ciated with steel materials, and fuel consumption during maritime operations. Achieving substantial
reductions in GHG emissions for FOW projects requires a critical focus on these factors. To analyze
this, the following research question has been defined:
Can a European offshore wind developer utilize green steel and green fuels by 2033 to reduce
their greenhouse gas emissions, whilst still establishing feasible projects?
1.3
Objective
This thesis aims to highlight the challenges and possible solutions to implementing green steel and
green fuels in floating offshore wind projects in Europe. This is presented through a gap analysis that
intends to highlight the differences between the current state and desired state of the steel industry,
maritime sector and the emerging floating offshore wind industry in Europe.
The results intends
to give insights for a floating offshore wind developer to expand and develop their procurement and
sustainability strategies towards 2030.

Gap Analysis
A gap analysis is conducted to determine the difference between the actual state and a desired future
state of a system (Gomm, 2009). A gap analysis could be used to assess several different interests for a
company. These include gaps in a market, product, profit, strategic, skill, and product development.
This thesis uses the market gap analysis as its base. A market gap analysis makes considerations
about the market and how customer’s needs will be in the future(Kim and Ji, 2018). The market
gaps and customer’s needs refers to the differences between desired share of green steel and green
fuels, and the existing share of these technologies. The market gaps are the differences between about
how the market situation for green steel and green fuels are as of now, whilst the customers’s need is
the developer’s desire to use the technologies. The consequence of the gaps is assessed through the
strengths and weaknesses of a developer and its potential to implement the technologies.
2.1
Steps
According to Kim and Ji, 2018, a gap analysis usually consists of four steps which are assessment
of current state, identification of ideal state, highlighting existing gaps, and implementing actions to
reduce the gaps. Renzi et al., 2021 also suggest to include the background as a first step, thereby
defining focus area and boundaries of the analysis, to understand the current situation and desired
objectives. This results in the following five possible steps in a gap analysis, which are the five steps
to be analyzed in this thesis:
• Step 1: Determination of focus area
• Step 3: Identification of ideal state for the market or company
• Step 3: Assessment of current state of the market situation and company
• Step 4: Highlighting the gaps that exist between the desired and ideal state
• Step 5: Defining actions to reduce the gaps
2.2
Tools
There are several tools that can be used to facilitate the gap analysis process. Two of these tools are
a Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities and Threats (SWOT) analysis and a risk matrix.
2.2.1
SWOT
A SWOT analysis is when a company evaluates its strengths, weaknesses, opportunities and threats.
These internal and external factors can help evaluate its current state, and the advantages and dis-
advantages to bridging the gap. The strengths refer to internal capabilities and positive aspects that
a business have for achieving their objectives. Weaknesses represent internal factors that could limit
or hinder a business to reach their objectives. Opportunities are external factors that could benefit
or support the business beyond the business itself. Threats are negative external factors that impede
or delay the business of reaching their goals. Consequently, strengths and weaknesses are consid-
ered internal elements, whilst opportunities and threats are considered external environmental factors
(Gürel, 2017).
2.2.2
Risk Matrix
There are different methods and risk assessments that gives the foundation of risk management. The
greatest representative for a qualitative method in risk assessments is the risk matrix. The risk matrix
is formed through three steps, as follows:

• Step 2: A value is applied to the risk of the severity of that risk occuring.

3.1
Project Phases in Offshore Wind Projects
FOW projects will progress through the same general phases, although individual elements can vary
significantly in each project, as there are numerous ways of designing and constructing a Offshore
wind farm. The usual life cycle phases and duration of these phases are as follows (Eatough, 2021):
• Auction preparation phase - 2 to 4 years
• Site auction phase - 3 to 12 months
• Development phase - 2 to 5 years
• Construction and installation phase - 2 to 4 years
• Operation and maintenance phase - typically set to 30 years
• Decommissioning phase - unknown
3.1.1
The Auction Phase
In the auction phase, the regulator initiates a project tender, and invites interested developers to
submit their bids.
The tender encompasses the licensing criteria that must be met.
Developers
engage in the process by formulating their bids, constructing financial models that incorporate key
cost and revenue factors. This enables them to assess the value of the development rights. Following
the submission of all bids, the regulator evaluates each proposal and selects the developer with the
most compelling bid to be awarded the project (Eatough, 2021). The different aspects of the auction
phase will be described more in detail in Section 5.3.
3.1.2
The Development Phase
The development phase is defined as all activities that a wind farm developer performs before making
a Final Investment Decision (FID) to proceed with the construction of a wind farm. This phase takes
approximately 2 - 5 years, and covers the design and engineering of a wind farm. Key steps include
the following:
• Feasibility studies
• Front-End Engineering Design (FEED) studies
• Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)
• Resource assessments
• Geotechnical and geophysical surveys

3.1.4
Operation and Maintenance Phase
During the life-cycle of a FOW farm, there will be a range of scheduled and unscheduled maintenance
tasks to be done as part of the Operation and Maintenance (O&M) phase. The scheduled tasks will
be done both in regards to statutory requirements, and the developer’s ambition to maximise the
economic viability of the project. The unscheduled tasks will be related to component failures or
alarms that have been activated through to the wind farm’s monitoring systems (Eatough, 2021).
The O&M phase also depends on the chosen substructure typology and logistricts strategy.
Scheduled Maintenance
For the turbines, it is likely that the scheduled maintenance is going to be aligned with the requirements
of a fixed bottom wind turbine. These activities relates to annual maintenance, blade inspections,
electrical system inspections and statutory inspections. The substructures and mooring system will
likely to be required scheduled maintenance at least every 5 years (Jiang, 2021). The maintenance
work, availability, complexity and occurrence will depend highly on the type of substructure and
mooring system.
The cables are critical to the wind farm, and is highly vulnerable to damage.
Therefore, the cables will be subject to close inspections and monitoring throughout the operational
life of the wind farm (Eatough, 2021).
Unscheduled maintenance
If a component fails or damage is identified, the operator will be required to perform unscheduled
maintenance. These activities will depend highly on the location of the failure, the size and lifting
requirements. There will also be differences as to the turbines will be towed back to port to perform
these repair work or do the work on site. The availability of towing back to port depends on the type
of substructure and its sensitivity to draught (Eatough, 2021).
Substructure typology
The chosen substructure typology of a FOW project will impact the O&M phase. A spar substructure
has the disadvantage that heavy lift vessels might be required, as well as deep water ports and shelteres
areas for repairs. Semi-submersible substructures on the other hand has the advantage of a simpler
unhook from the mooring system.
No heacy lift vessels are reuires, and helicopter access is also
possible. It can also be towed to port for major repairs. TLP substructures on the other hand are
difficult to unhook from the mooring system, which requires onsite repairs. In addition, the mooring
system is highly susceptible to failure (Jiang, 2021).
Logistics strategy
There are two strategies that can provide access to the FOW farms; either using a Crew Transfer
Vessel (CTV) or a Service Operation Vessel (SOV). The CTV is a smaller vessel that contains a team
of technicians to conduct work on the wind farm. These vessels are usually easier to hire on a short
term charter, and can accommodate between 12 and 24 technicians per trip. A SOV is much larger
than a CTV and can accommodate around 60 technicians at a given time. These vessels are more
viable at sites that are further from shore and can enable technicians to spend greater portion of their
shift to do actual maintenance activities on site due to shorter transit times. A CTV is limited to
distances which are less than 50-60km from shore to the wind farm (Jiang, 2021).

Decommissioning
There is limited experience in decommissioning FOW farms and its components. However, the de-

disadvantage that it will have to be partially decommissioned in deep water and require heavy lift ves-

The future demand for European steel is highly uncertain. There are several scenario forecasts for

One of the biggest users of steel is in the automotive and aviation industry. Due to the changing
patterns in mobility and behavioural changes in travel, it is estimated that the steel demand of these
sectors will decrease by 2050. While these industries are set to decrease their demand, it is presumed
that the steel demand for renewable electricity will increase (IEA, 2022). According to the EUs 2030
Climate Target Plan Assessment the demand for steel from the wind industry (both onshore and
offshore) is assumed to be 3.7 Mt/year. However, in comparison the total actual steel consumption in

4.1.2
Decarbonizing Steel Technologies
There are several options to reduce the CO2 emissions related to the production of steel. In the
short term, a switch from fossil fuel to low CO2 energy sources can enable some limited CO2 miti-
gation. Emissions can be further reduced by using Carbon Capture Utilisation and Storage (CCUS)
technologies. Somers, 2022 show that this can be achieved through technologies such as:
• Substituting biomass or hydrogen for coal/coke in the blast furnace.
• Using new types of smelting reactors that replaces several energy intensive steps and can be
combined with CCUS
• Capturing and recycling the emitted CO2 to produce other basic chemicals and synthetic fuels.
Many European steelmakers opt to fully replace existing processes with breakthrough technologies
that rely on electricity or hydrogen to reduce iron ore, making the production have little to no CO2
emissions. This would require to replace existing steel processes with new steel plants. This can be
achieved by using some key technologies such as (Somers, 2022):
• Hydrogen - Direct Recution Iron (H-DRI), which avoids the use of fossil fuels directly.
• Electrolytic processes. Iron ore is reduced solely through electricity.
• Hydrogen plasma. The smelting reduction of iron ore to steel with fossil free inputs.

This plan expects to have 30% of the EU primary steel production decarbonized with
renewable hydrogen by 2030(EU, 2022).
In order to reach the goal of reducing the emissions by 55% by 2030 in Europe, EU has introduced two
systems that European industries are to implement the next decade. This is the Emission Trading
System (ETS) and Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM). The ETS is a system for which
a cap is set on the total amount of GHG emissions that can be released by certain industries. With
this system, the EU has created a market mechanism that gives CO2 a price and creates incentives
to reduce emissions in the most cost-effective manner. Companies have to buy or receive allowances
corresponding to their CO2 emissions, thereby making green options more attractive. This is only
operated in all EU countries, as well as Iceland, Liechtenstein and Norway. To prevent carbon leakage
by companies moving their production outside of EU to avoid the cost of ETS, the CBAM has been
introduced. The CBAM imposes a fee on imports of products based on their carbon content, which
would be equivalent to the cost that EU companies pay to the ETS. Hence, a level playing field for
EU companies and protection from unfair competition with companies with weaker climate policies
is achieved (Nissen et al., 2022).
Nissen et al., 2022 presents that the steel sector has received free pollution permits from the ETS to
prevent carbon leakage. It is intended that these free ETS allowances should be phased out between
2026 and 2035, by reducing the free allowances with ten percent each year. CBAM is intended to
replace the carbon leakage protection from ETS. The financial obligations from the CBAM will only
apply to the excess emissions costs.

4.2.2
Policy Aspect
In July 2021, the European Commission put forward the "Fit for 55" package. Fit for 55 is a package
of legislative proposals that aims at ensuring the success of the European Green Deal.
Thereby
reducing the emissions in the EU of 55% by 2030 relative to 1990, and net zero by 2050. There are
three distinct EU maritime shipping regulations that are introduced: FuelEU Maritime, EU ETS and
EU Energy Taxation Directive (ETD) (Soone, Jaan, 2023). The targets are also supported by the
International Maritime Organization (IMO), which aims to reduce the total annual GHG emissions
globally by at least 50% by 2050 (IMO, 2023). Solakivi et al., 2022 presents an estimate of when
and with what assumptions greener maritime fuels could be competitive against conventional fossil
fuels in Europe. This analysis also examines what would be the cost impact on an individual vessel.
Planned regulations and incentives created by the EU is thereby of high importance for the premise
of the analysis. This analysis, in conjunction with the relevant articles provided by the EU, will serve
as the primary resource utilized to inform the policy and cost projections within the maritime sector
of this thesis.
FuelEU Maritime is a proposal that mandates that ships improve their GHG intensity of the energy
used on board. The intensity are set by targets over time compared to a base year of 2020. This
proposal is for commercial vessels which are above 5000 gross tonnage. Emissions related to voyages
within EUs jurisdiction are accounted for 100% and by half if the start or end of voyage is outside
EUs jurisdiction. The GHG intensity is evaluated and adjusted every five year, Reductions of -2%
in 2025, thereafter -6%, -13%, -26%, -59%, and -75%, respectively until 2050. Shipping companies
are responsible to report specified voyage information and their Well-To-Wake (WtW) emissions.
The companies are also given some flexibility which allows for the companies to bank or borrow
compliance surplus or pool a group of ship’s compliances together to meet the set targets. If the
compliance deficits, the companies are required to pay penalties (Solakivi et al., 2022).
EU ETS has included the shipping sector in their newest revision. Similarly to the FuelEU Maritime,
this applies for vessels larger than 5000 gross tonnage and accounts for 100% or 50% based on if the
voyages are within EUs jurisdiction or if either end of the voyage is outside, respectively. Like with
the steel sector and other industries, the EU ETS for the shipping industry will set a limit on GHG
emissions. The emissions will be included partially between 2023 and 2026. This scheme will include
20% in 2023, 45% in 2024, 70% in 2025 and 100% of the emissions from 2026. The amount will be
based on the emission factor of the fuel and the amount of fuel that has been consumed (Solakivi
et al., 2022).
EU ETD for the maritime industry is a proposal that introduces a minimum tax rate based on the the
energy content on various fuels. This would be given in an amount of EUR per GJ, with a higher tax
on fuels that are more polluting. In order to incentivize the use of alternative fuels, including biofuels
and biogas, low-carbon fuels, and advanced sustainable fuels should have a minimum tax rate of zero
for 10 years (Solakivi et al., 2022).

moderate and ambitious case. Solely based on the cost, utilizing green steel instead of conventional
steel could reduce the emissions drastically at a marginal extra cost. However, the marginal cost also
incorporates more risks and uncertainties that the developer has to take. Developers already have an
uncertainty by being dependent on other industries. Deciding to use green steel would also increase
their dependencies of hydrogen production, renewable energy production, developments of new or
improved steel mills. This increases the overall risks and uncertainties. The technology that has the
most promising potential for reducing emissions in steel production and has the most attention from
European steel producers is from H-DRI. It is thereby set as the goal for both the moderate and
ambitious case that this technology is to be used. As presented in section 4.1.3 EU predicts that
renewable energy will be the fastest-growing market in demand for steel in the next decades. They
also expect that 30 % of the primary steel production will be decarbonized with renewable hydrogen
by 2030.

the developer would need less resources with regards to the construction, installation and maintenance
of the wind farms. The developers would also be able to establish the offshore wind farm with less
environmental impact of the seabed and marine ecosystem. These impacts have not been studied in

5.3.1
Policy
The EU has set out its goal of establishing at least 60 GW of offshore wind by 2030 and 300 GW by
2050. EU has also stated that they want to reduce their emissions by 55 % by 2030 as a stepping stone
towards net zero by 2050. One of the most important factors to reaching these targets is by retiring
coal and gas plants, and replacing them by clean energy (Levin et al., 2023). To reach net zero, it
is important that also the industry will become greener. The EU has established the Green Deal
36
==PAGE:47==

Master thesis
Industrial Plan which is in line with the REPowerEU plan. These plans set out to boost industrial
competitiveness, and tackling dependencies by transforming industrial processes through a massive
speed-up and scale-up of renewable energy (European Commission, 2023).
In order to establish renewable generation, the most common support mechanism that governments in
Europe have established are Contract for Difference (CFD). A CFD contract is a contract between the
electricity producer, and the government for which secures the difference between the strike price and
reference price for the electricity it produces during the contract. In the UK, these contracts have been
established for 15 years. The CFD is also called a two-sided CFD which means that it reduces the risk
overcompensating, and splits the risk between the developer and the government. The few countries
in Europe that utilizes one-sided CFDs usually has a high share of bottom-fixed wind projects. The
contracts are allocated through a series of auctions, and provide the developers with a guaranteed
strike price in EUR/MWh for the years that the duration of the contract (Winje et al., 2022). If the
price that won the auction for a two-sided CFD was 50EUR/MWh, and the prices that the developer
sells to the wholesale electricity market are higher than 50EUR/MWh, then the developer has to pay
the difference back to the government. If the prices are lower than 50EUR/MWh, the government
has to pay that difference to the developer. In the auction, it is usually the developer that could
provide the cheapest alternative that wins the CFD contract. The lower a FOW project’s projected
LCOE is, the more likely the developer is to secure the contract. The LCOE is calculated by dividing
the CAPEX and Operating Expenditure (OPEX) with the energy production that system will have.
Thereby the developers are incentivised to lower their CAPEX and OPEX as much as possible, whilst
increasing their energy production as much as possible (Martinez and Iglesias, 2022). A disadvantage
by having CFD contracts is that the developer is not incentivezed to increase the value of the power,
but rather the amount of power produces. Thereby. producing more when the prices are high will
not make a difference to the developer, or to find other buyers of the power, through for instance
Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)s (Norwegian Ministry of Petroleum and Energy, 2023). PPAs are
long-term contracts to buy the energy in agreed volumes and at prices that meet the needs of both
the developer and the consumer (Mendicino et al., 2019). Another possibility is to have qualitative
criteria, either instead of price auction or in combination with a price auction. Norway has used a
combination in its first floating offshore wind project, where applicants will need to enter a qualitative
competition for which they will be granted a site that they later on will enter in an auction for a
two-sided CFD. In the qualitative competition, cost level in 2030 weighs 30 %, execution capability
weighs 30 %, innovation and technological development weighs 20 %, positive local benefits weighs 10
%, and sustainability weighs 10 % (Norwegian Ministry of Petroleum and Energy, 2023).
Thereby, in the current market, offshore wind developers compete to supply electricity based on the
cost of energy they produce.
Generally they are not incentivized to reduce their emissions.
The
revenues of the projects are determined by a fixed compensation arrangement, which means that de-
velopers aim to build FOW farms with as low possible cost to protect their margins. Thereby having
higher cost to the material such as green steel, would directly impact the developer’s profitability.
It is therefore important that utilizing green steel is promoted and incentivized by relevant govern-
ment to achieve CO2e savings. There are several ways that government could support this. Tender
requirements, mandates and financial incentives could be provided in the auction phase to motivate
developers to use green steel. It is also possible that government could set green steel as a prerequisite
for further developments in the sector, to contribute to the sustainability and emission goals set by
the EU (EU, 2022). Material-Economics, 2021 addresses the challenge that green steel supply and
demand must match in terms of geography, steel grades and volume for the trade flow to increase
smoothly. As there are high differences in the quality and properties of steel, this can be challenging
for many industries. It is presented an alternative process that could mitigate those challenges for a
given buyer, which is a green steel certificate. Similar to a renewable energy certificate, this allows for
the CO2 abatement to be traded separately from the actual physical delivery of the product, while
providing certainty to buyers that volume of green steel truly has been produced and used somewhere.

Steel and Marine Operations
Currently, the main production methods of steel are BF-BOF and EAF. Although there is a lot of

steel makes having alternatives and greener technologies an opportunity to have a higher cost on their
products. Having big companies signing pledges such as FMC to order at least 10% of near-zero
products, the steel producers recognize the demand. These opportunities are especially in early stages
when the supply of low emission steel still is low. Due to EU importing steel, the legislation of CBAM
is detrimental to securing level playing field between European and other regions’ steel producers.
Although it is currently at a low TRL-level, steel producers seem to favour the development of green
steel made from H-DRI. Since there is no specific certification or standardization put forward to
what can be categorized as green steel yet, there could be more developments in lower emission steel
such as increased use of renewable energy in EAF, CCUS or bioenergy that would be cheaper for
the steel producers whilst still complying with the incentives and regulations put forward by the EU.
This could lead to that more new steel producers focuses on new technology such as H-DRI, whilst
established steel producers rather focuses on utilizing their existing steel mills with minor alterations
or buying green certificates instead. This could lead to that the cost of H-DRI becomes even more
expensive and would take longer time to develop. It is also important to note that due to the great
interest and developments in green technologies, new regulations, advancements and improvements
could significantly change the evaluation of the TRLs of the different steel technologies and projections
going forward.
There are hardly any ships running on green fuels as of today. With 1.2% of the current ships in the
world running on alternative fuels, there is not any ships available today for the marine operations
for a FOW project. When looking at the percentage of gross tonnage the green fuel such as battery
only accounts for 0.06% of the world fleet. However, when looking at the ships that are currently in

LNG also has a smaller GHG intensity which makes it a valuable fuel for shipowners, given that it
also presents low costs.

in FOW projects. By having more data and information on the implementation of the technologies
for FOW projects, the desired state could be divided into several sub-objectives that are concrete
and specific steps required to attain the main objective. Thereby, this section will highlight the gap
between the desired state and current state, as well as assessing the obstacles that a developer has in
order evaluate the consequence of this gap. Thereafter these results will be discussed.
6.1
Results and Discussion
The desired state is using 100% green steel and green fuels in FOW projects. Currently there are no
FOW projects that utilizes green steel and green fuels. Thereby it is a large gap between the desired
and current state. As the technologies are relatively new, this major gap is as expected.
However, to assess the consequence of this gap for an offshore wind developer, it is conducted an
assessment based on a risk matrix.
This assessment has been conducted to identify the critical
obstacles. The risk matrix assessment that have been conducted analyzes identified risks and obstacles
that a developer would have, that could impact the feasibility of utilizing green steel and green fuels
in their projects. The risk assessment have been assessed relating to its probability and impact. In
addition to the risk assessment, the amount of influence and control a developer would have on the
risks has been evaluated. The probability is assessed based on how likely it is for the risk or obstacle
to occur, and will be assigned a value ranging from 1 to 5. The values for the probability are defined as
follows; 1 - very unlikely, 2 - unlikely, 3 - possible, 4 - likely, 5 - very likely. The impact is assessed based
on how much it could negatively affect the feasibility of a developer implementing the technologies.
The values for the impact are defined as follows; 1 - insignificant, 2 - minor, 3 - moderate, 4 - major,
5 - severe. The risk score is calculated by multiplying the probability with impact. The risk score
refers to its risk of hindering a developer of utilizing green steel and green fuels for its projects. The
amount of influence a developer would have on the risks are also assigned a value ranging from 1 - 5.
The values for the influence are defined as follows. 1 - much control, 2 - control, 3 - moderate amount
of control, 4 - limited control, 5 - no control. The total score has been calculated by multiplying the
risk score with the influence. This provides that the higher the total score is, the higher the risk or
obstacle could negatively affect the feasibility of developers achieving the desired state. The results
are presented in table 8.

In this section, actions that developers can take to mitigate the risks and challenges that may hinder
their progress towards their desired state are presented. Due to many of the risks being outside of the
developers control, it will also highlight what possible actions policymakers and producers could do
to develop green steel and green fuels for the offshore wind industry by 2030. The findings are based

7.1
Results and Discussion
Direct initiatives
One of the biggest uncertainties that steel producers and ship owners have in regards to green steel
and green fuels is the urgency and demand from their customers. There is a lot of political incentives
to make the steel and maritime industry greener, but at what rate these will transform also highly
depend on their customers and market. Thereby, one of the most important thing a developer could
do to realise the possibility if implementing these technologies are to engage in buyers’ club initiatives
such as FMC and SteelZero. Implementing green steel and green fuels for any industry is complex,
thereby a collaborative effort to coordinate between stakeholders and send sufficiently strong signals
in regards to the volumes the industries require would make steel producers and ship owners to
recognize the market demand and urgency of the products. Developers also have an advantage of
having developed experienced partnerships with OEMs and supply chains from the O&G industry.
The developers could thereby send requests and demands to the direct steel buyers and ship owners
to engage in bilateral value-chain cooperation initiatives. They could thereby incentivize them to
see the commercial benefit of being early movers ahead of their competitors. This could require a
change in the procurement practices that would alter the practices from a criteria-based exercise to
strategic supply chain development. Thereby develop an innovative and green value proposition for
their customers, which could give them a competitive edge.
Indirect initiatives
For steel producers and ship owners, one of the critical actions to develop the technologies will be
to engage with high volume customers in order to develop the necessary value chain. As there are
a lot of different developers that are competing for developing offshore wind projects, it would be
important to have more collaboration and coordination with other developers in regards to the supply
chain and procurement standards as an indirect initiative to develop these technologies. This could
make the offshore industry be more united and seen as more important customers for steel producers
and ship owners. This could benefit the understanding, challenges and opportunities better, both
from a developers point of view, and from steel producers and ship owners. However, it would also
be better for the overall climate targets to have steel producers and ship owners specifically focus
on projects with higher emission reduction potential than offshore wind. For instance it could take
less than 200 days for a wind farm as proposed in Table 5 to produce the amount of hydrogen and
electricity necessary to produce the amount of green steel necessary for constructing the wind farm.

whilst the remaining energy would provide additional renewable energy. A regulator or government
could consider it more important to focus on implementing green steel in other industries instead of
increasing the risk of making offshore wind farm projects unappealing.
Another indirect initiative that developer could do is to utilize its opportunity to promote more
PPAs in suitable projects. Thereby getting involved in power-to-X projects that could increase the
production of green hydrogen and ammonia. This could benefit both the developer’s long term strategy
as well as steel producers and ship owners. One of the biggest risks and uncertainties for developing

Discussion and Further Work
8.1
Overall Discussion
As presented, there are a lot of uncertainties and risks for a developer to implement green steel and
green fuels.
The risks and uncertainties have been found and discussed based on the the desired
state of using 100% green steel in the materials of a offshore wind farm and use 100% green fuels
in the maritime operations. The desired state may be overambitious when assessing the obstacles,
current weaknesses and threats a developer will have to implement these technologies. It is found
that one of the most important actions that a developer could take is to sign pledges such as FMC to
minimize the risk and promote the proposed technologies. These pledges all have smaller objectives
for the utilization of green steel and green fuels. By signing a pledge of using 10% green steel and 5%
green fuels it would decrease the financial risks and project feasibility risk significantly. Instead of the
technologies being obstacles for the developers in auctions, having a lower share of green steel and
green fuels in the project could be of insignificant importance for investors, and could also be seen as
a competitive edge compared to competing developers.
Another important note is that there are a lot of uncertainties and developments in the industries that
have been discussed. The floating offshore wind industry is still at a pre-commercial phase. Although
the steel and maritime industries are well-established, the proposed technologies are new to these
industries as well. As this thesis has been established mainly in literature reviews, the main findings
and assumptions that have been made throughout the thesis could change continuously throughout
the next decade as more quality data and experiences will be gained.
8.2
Further work
It became clear that many of the risks and obstacles for offshore wind developers in their pursuit
of reducing their emissions through utilizing green steel and green fuels are outside of their control.
It was found that one of the most important risks was due to the revenues being determined by a
fixed compensation rate for which incentivizes the developers to cut the CAPEX and OPEX, whilst
maximizing the energy production as much as possible to secure a contract.
This thesis mainly
focused on the issues of CAPEX and OPEX with regards to implementing green steel and green
fuels. Another way of analyzing a developer’s potential of reducing emissions would be to analyze
its potential to increase the lifetime of an offshore wind farm. If the lifetime increases, the developer
would increase their potential energy production, decrease its environmental impact due to longer
lifetime with production and reduce the financial risks and uncertainties for investors. This could
also be a cheaper alternative for the developer to develop, rather than taking on the high risks and
increased costs required to implement green steel and green fuels.
Increasing the lifetime of the
project’s components is assumed to be more in the developers control and could be more beneficial
for the developers to analyze in the near term future. This is thereby recommended to be analyzed
further.
As mentioned, the industries that have been assessed in this thesis are relatively new. There are
thereby limited quality data that are available for further analysis. Thereby, forecast and foresight
analysis are of high importance to get better understanding of the industries and technologies. As
there are so many new pledges, regulations and initiatives from the EU and other policymakers and
stakeholders, there is a need for analyzing when the cost of green steel and grey steel would be
assumed equal. There was high differences in the studies that were presented in Figure 4.4, which
implies high uncertainties. It is recommended that whilst obtaining more information, experience,
data and technological developments for green steel, forecast studies would provide less uncertainty.
Such forecast studies would be very relevant for industries such as offshore wind. Whilst obtaining
more precise data, these could be used further in prediction models. There was a cost forecast of

Conclusion
The overall objective of this thesis has been to assess the research question: Can a European offshore
wind developer utilize green steel and green fuels in 2033 to reduce their greenhouse gas emissions,
whilst still establishing feasible projects.
In order to answer this, a thorough gap analysis have been conducted. This analysis have assessed
the desired state, current state, obstacles and risks to reaching the desired state and actions to
mitigate the risks. The desired state was found to utilize 100% green steel in the materials of the
wind farm and using 100% green fuels. This is however found to be unlikely, due to its many risks,
uncertainties and lack of incentives. The research bases of a developers point of view and how they
may affect the implementation of utilizing these technologies. To assess the developers potential to
utilize these technologies, a SWOT analysis have been conducted to establish and identify its positive
and negative impacts. It was found that the weaknesses, threats and obstacles are of such significance
that implementing 100% green steel and green fuels are deemed unlikely for a offshore wind project
with a COD in 2033.
However, when looking at the market trends through pledges such as FMC, companies and industries
tend to pledge for a lower share of green steel and green fuels by 2030. By implementing a lower share
of these technologies such as the FMC pledge requirements of 10% green steel and 5% green fuels, it
is deemed suitable for floating offshore wind projects. The obstacles mainly refer to the disadvantages
of having a high increase of CAPEX and OPEX through uncertain and high-risk technologies such as
green steel and green fuels. Thereby implementing these technologies would significantly reduce the
developer’s potential of winning auctions, receiving investments and CFDs. But with a smaller share
it is concluded that these risks would have a much smaller impact and could in turn be of high value
to the developers as they could use this as a competitive edge to their competitors. It is also noted
that the obstacles for the implementation of these technologies are mostly outside of the developer’s
control. Thereby it is also concluded that further investigation in to increasing the projects’ lifetime
would be advantageous for developers to reach their targets of mitigating emissions of their offshore
wind projects.
