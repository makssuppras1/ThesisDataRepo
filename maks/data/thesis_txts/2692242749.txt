Abstract
As energy production becomes variable and decentralised, a reform of the energy sec­
tor seems appropriate, especially concerning grid infrastructures, which are adapted to
controllable and predictable amounts of energy. The question of cost recovery, espe­
cially for grid operators, is raising significant challenges because most of the costs are
sunk. This MSc Thesis aims at studying the impact of tariffs design on a Danish energy
district while allowing cost recovery for operators, in the context of DER availability, heat
electrification and district heating connection. A 2­level Python model, representing the
interaction between a prosumer, modelled by a linear cost minimisation problem, and the
DSO, represented by a cost adjustment sequence, has been implemented. Multiple tariff
structures, combining volumetric with or without net­metering, capacity and fixed shares
are studied based on their impact on the flexibility they can trigger at the household level,
their sunk cost allocation fairness, the carbon saved and their economic efficiency. Sen­
sitivity analyses on key parameters are conducted, as well as the implementation of a
Critical Peak Tariff (CPT) scheme. Results show an opposition between environmental,
economical and flexibility performances: capacity­based tariffs allow for investments in
flexible equipment, fixed tariffs return the least cost solution for society, and volumetric
with net­metering tariffs allow for large carbon cuts while being profoundly unfair. Non­
Net­Metering (NNM) designs provide intermediate performances. An increase in carbon
cost is expected to slightly increase potential flexibility for the concerned tariffs, while an
increase in sunk costs should reduce business opportunities for heat pumps. A change
in prosumer proportion has the ssame type of results but to a lower magnitude, and NNM
tariffs prove to be very robust to this parameter. An increased heat consumption reduces
the investments in batteries only. Finally, only CPT schemes without net metering can trig­
ger large flexibility with volumetric charges, and prove to effectively reduce usage of the
network during congested periods. This study shows the crucial importance of knowing all
parameters before designing network tariffs, and the unavoidable political dimension of all
changes, since reforms are bounded to impact people differently regarding their capacity
to react to price signals.
Keywords: Distribution grid tariff – Sunk cost recovery – Linear cost minimisation – Flex­
ibility

Vocabulary precision
In the following, the vocabulary will be used as described on Figure 1. The sum of 6 costs
forms the total cost for a household. They represent all the different aspect of what a
consumer is paying at the end of the year. An overall cost is defined as the sum for all
consumers of this cost component: e.g. the overall network cost is the sum of the cost of
the network for all users in the energy district. Also, the overall total cost is representing
the total cost for society (both terms are equivalent in this paper), while the sum of the
overall network cost, the overall investments costs and the overall O&M costs form the
system cost.

1
Introduction
As climate change becomes a more urgent topic every day, human society has to adapt to
comply with the Paris agreement targets, signed to offer a chance to limit global warming
below 2°C [1]. One area being studied, because responsible for a lot of CO2 emissions,
is the energy sector [2]. Technological progress has made it possible to produce energy
while being more environmentally friendly, especially for electricity with the development
of Variable Renewable Energy sources (VREs). Nevertheless, these technologies are
for the most decentralized, unpredictable, and variable, putting the demand­side and the
electricity distribution grid under much pressure. Indeed, the regulatory framework for
the electricity grid and the price signals sent by the tariff to the end­users are adapted to
conventional systems, built on centralised and dispatchable technologies.
The boom in variable renewable energy creates thus a flexibility challenge: if green tech­
nologies are bound to be uncontrollable, therefore the use of the energy should be as
flexible as possible to adapt to this fluctuating energy production. On the demand side,
technological progress in electricity storage, notably with the development of electric ve­
hicles (Li­ion batteries e.g.), flexible power­to­heat technologies (heat pumps e.g.), digi­
talization and smart appliances bring new solutions to address this challenge.
One way to bring flexibility into the network is by giving people enough incentives to con­
sume the most efficiently possible. This process can be done by giving them price signals,
indicating the best time to consume. Based on these signals, consumers are then able to
consume at the best time and receive economic incentives to drive investment into on­site
generation technologies or controllable equipment. These signals are represented on the
energy bill, composed of the electricity price, the network tariff and other policy charges
(taxes e.g.). If the cost of energy is based on market principles and taxes are handled by
the political agenda of countries, network tariffs are regulated by regulatory authorities.
This tariff reflects several network costs that can be composed of energy charges (€/kWh),
capacity charges (€/kW) and fixed charges (€), which technically vary through time and
location to reflect congestions and local network architecture. These components are
combined to allow the network operator to recover its total costs, including its sunk cost
for existing assets, which, in a network industry, constitutes the main share of expenses
that cannot be attributed to a specific grid user, its operation costs such as maintenance
or losses and its future investment [3].
Coase’s theory suggests a 2­part tariff that is reflective of the distribution of cost between
the capital and operational expenditures (CAPEX and OPEX) borne by the Distribution
System Operator (DSO) [4]. In other words, a fixed charge should be applied to recover
the sunk costs, and a volumetric charge based on the marginal cost used to recover the
cost of use of the network (losses and other operational costs) and give signals that new
investments are needed where bottlenecks appear.
Because of limited political and social acceptance of fixed charges that are considered
discriminatory and perceived as unfair, tariffs are in practice built differently where most
of the network costs are covered via a volumetric charge and where the fixed charge is
limited [5].
As explained earlier, current regulation schemes must be reformed to send signals that
support better flexibility, while also ensuring the entire cost recovery of the grid for past and

==PAGE:24==
future investment. This takes place in a context where end­users become prosumers and
invest in distributed generation, storage equipment and flexible electric equipment, and
where these bidirectional flows (consumption and generation) are increasingly subject to
net metering where only the difference between import and export is billed by the DSO.
At the difference, when no net­metering is applied, the sum of import and export is billed.
Many studies, detailed in Chapter 3, have investigated various new tariff designs and
identified some trends. Volumetric charges with net­metering are an implicit subsidy for
PV panels, but create a massive distortion of the cost of the network, solely borne by pas­
sive users. Capacity charges are good to limit prospective costs, as they further reduced
peak usage of the network, and they incentivise the installation of responsive loads, in­
creasing available flexibility. Nevertheless, they are not suitable to recover sunk costs
and can create an unfair situation too. Furthermore, static tariffs are identified as no good
to transmit efficient signals of the use of the network. Therefore, Time of Use (ToU) or
dynamic tariffs would be an improvement, but they also place a higher burden on irrespon­
sive consumers, often affiliated with the low­income class. Competing effect of some tariff
schemes have also been identified, between solar panels and electric vehicles e.g.
With literature often taking some simplified tariff schemes in their models (1­part tariff,
50/50 tariffs), there is a lack of a more thorough overview of the impacts of different tariff
designs on different parameters, such as the investment level for end­users to invest in
flexibility and generation, the fairness of cost allocation of the network, the total cost for
the society and the carbon emissions. In the same way, many studies purposely suppress
some costs, like investment and Operation and Maintenance (O&M) costs in Distributed
Energy Resources (DER) or disregarded the electrification of heat to the profit (or not) of
electric vehicle roll­out. Some models also do not consider variable prices of electricity.
Therefore, in the process of assessing the impact of multiple tariff structures ranging from
100% capacity tariff to 100% volumetric tariff to 100% fixed tariff, as many costs as possi­
ble are included in the simulation process, as well as heat pumps. The tested designs are
evaluated regarding their influence on the level of investment in generation and flexible
technologies, the fairness of the network costs allocation and the amount of tax avoided
by the prosumer, the carbon emissions that are cut and the total cost borne by both pro­
sumers and passive users during a year. Since investments from end­users in Renewable
Energy Sources (RES) and flexible technologies is at the heart of the thesis, each tariff
is tested with and without net­metering. Some sensitivities are executed to refine the
findings and further capture the impact of the initial situation on the results.
To present policy and technical recommendations to improve energy districts in a cost­
efficient way in the optic of a sustainable transition, the results of the simulations are
studied and analysed. Trends in technologies, available policy leverage and regulatory
framework are identified to try to answer this question. The thesis is organised as follow.
First, a brief introduction of the general context is given. A thorough literature study is
presented next to evaluate the state of the art in the domain of designing grid tariffs.
Following is a presentation of the modelling process and the model realised and used
in this work, as well as the data collection. The results are then analysed before some
sensitivity analyses are conducted. Results are then discussed to open towards possible
orientations for future work. Finally, some conclusive remarks are made.

General context
Along with increased climate change awareness, recent technological progress made
possible and necessary a change in the energy sector. Thanks to DERs, consumers can
become actors and react to price signals to consume efficiently, bringing energy genera­
tion and flexibility to the network. The following chapter is composed of a brief introduction
of the trends in technological change, the electricity bill and the price signals.
2.1
From consumer to prosumer, trends and changes in the
energy mix
To achieve the goal of carbon neutrality by 2050 set by the Paris agreement and the ob­
jective of 32% of renewables in the energy production mix by 2030, the roll­out of greener
technologies is essential [6]. Thanks to support policies and technological progress, the
objective of 20% by 2020 has been achieved, but it is not guaranteeing the fulfilment of
2030 objectives.
Energy production is currently changing on different levels. First, there is an increase
in the use of renewables, non­controllable and non­predictable energies, such as pho­
tovoltaic panels or wind power. Indeed, wind energy deployment is exponential in the
world, and in particular, in Europe1, it went from 86 GW in 2010 to 213 GW in 2020. This
is mainly due to the high capacity installed in Germany (62.4 GW), Spain (26.7 GW) and
the United Kingdom (25.1 GW) [7].
Second, some energy sources are getting available at the household level. This is the
case with some heating technologies, such as geothermal pits or solar collectors, but also
on the electrical level with photovoltaic panels, as shown in Figure 2.1a. In Europe, the
residential PV capacity installed went from 5.3 GW in 2010 to 31.6 GW in 2020 in a steady
way [7].
Third, this study is made in the context of district heating deployment, as shown in Fig­
ure 2.1b. In Europe, the number of kilometres in use has gone from around 150 000 km in
2005 to more than 200 000 km in 2019. The leading countries are Sweden (38 000 km),
Denmark (33 000 km) and Germany and Poland (22 000 km each) [8]. The heat is still
relying heavily on natural gas, but an increasing share is produced through renewables,
averaging at 33% in Europe in 2020 [8].

2.2
Electricity bill components and economic signals
The main assumption behind the development and use of price signals is that consump­
tion can be made rationally through smart handling to allow a maximization of the con­
sumer utility. In this context, it means that consumers, or their smart devices, are expected
to use the information they receive from the energy bill to minimize their costs over time.
It is possible to decompose the bill into different categories, representing different costs
that the consumer has to bear. In Europe, [16] shows that 37% of the costs paid by the
consumer are for energy purposes, while 25% are for the network in 2019. The rest of
the costs are split between levies for renewables policies (14%), Value Added Tax (VAT,
14%) and other charges including for the promotion of energy efficiency or CO2 taxes
(10%). This repartition can vary greatly among European countries, as shown in Fig­
ure 2.2. These costs are distributed over the average 21.6 c€/kWh that households pay
for electricity. Again, this value varies widely, between 29.8 c€/kWh in Germany and 9.8
c€/kWh in Bulgaria.
Figure 2.2: Electricity bill breakdown [16]
The energy price is determined through market mechanisms and is highly dependent on
the production mix. Indeed, the marginal cost of production can vary greatly between
the technological mix in use. In particular, renewable energies like wind or hydro, when
available, can lower the equilibrium price on the market because of their low marginal cost
[17]. The amount to which the price decrease also depends on the replaced technology.
In addition to that, since wind, solar, hydro and even nuclear have low marginal costs and
low carbon content, cheap energy is often correlated with greener energy [17].
The second main costs for the consumer are the network costs, resulting from the DSO’s
activities, and passed on to the users connected to the network. DSOs are responsible
for connection from the transmission lines to the consumer [18]. By doing so, they are
also responsible for the adequacy of the capacities of the network, its maintenance, and
the connection of new consumers to the grid. As they operate as monopolies on their
districts, they are regulated by national entities (NRA), and the amount of cost they can
recover is strictly supervised. There are only a few maintenance operations, resulting in
low operation costs mainly caused by energy losses. Consequently, most of the costs
are considered sunk, creating a risk of utility death spiral since DSOs need to recover

their cost even in case of a change in consumption [19]. Costs can also be prospective,
meaning that they rely on the coincidental peak usage of the network [5]. These costs are
variable and represent the capacity to commission or decommission based on the use of
the network. These costs are then passed to the consumer and signal congestion, need
for additional capacity and peak use. A large body of literature is dedicated to the study
of networks costs and is reviewed in Chapter 3.
The third component of an energy bill is composed of the taxes and charges imposed
by the state, billed on a “per­unit” method. They encompass levies for renewable en­
ergy projects, consumption tax, environmental taxes, value­added tax for example. The
amount dedicated to these costs can vary widely across Europe, as shown in Figure 2.2.
In the following, the term “tax” will encompass the levies for renewable projects. As part
of the energy bill, taxes have a significant impact on the actors of the energy system.
For example, when the energy price is too low, consumers do not have any incentives
to reduce their consumption, and producers do not change toward more efficient produc­
tion means. Therefore, using taxes, the price of energy can be increased to incentivise
positive change [20]. On the other hand, high taxes would deter investments in P2H tech­
nologies since the energy bill would increase [21]. In the same way, taxes are a political
issue. As sources of revenues, changes in their design can have a significant impact: oil
and electricity taxes do not subsidise the same program, they are not handled at the same
administrative level, they impact people from different social group differently… [22]. Even
the implementation of a tax plays a role: aggressive carbon taxing could lead to better
carbon reduction by limiting their emissions straight away, while a progressive deploy­
ment could help adjust the trajectory over time [23]. Consequently, the use of taxes as
tools to propagate the right price signals is complex.
Finally, taxes are a critical aspect of the energy bill. According to [24], they should, by
their design, stimulate positive behaviour, incentivise innovation, not deter consumption,
but also be simple to understand and transparent. At the same time, taxes should not
impede price signals created by energy price and grid tariffs. Otherwise, it would create
inefficiencies in the consumption and deployment of technologies. Even more, as for the
other component of the energy bill, it is a political tool in the sense that a change in its
design would necessarily make winners and losers.
2.3
Economic signals and policy­making process
As explained previously, the energy system is deeply changing by the technologies in­
volved, requiring a change in the policies surrounding it. Therefore, regulation agencies,
policymakers and other authorities have a crucial role as they are supposed to guarantee
an efficient transition. Price signals are one of many tools that can be used to influence
the use of the network, the production and consumption of energy in society. Table 2.1
gives a brief recap on price signals that the consumers receive through the energy bill.
These signals are crucial to guarantee the efficient use of the network. Assuming that the
prices are not distorted, and according to economic theories, they are supposed to allow
consumers to consume in the most socially optimal manner.
The mission of regulators is then to allow these signals to pass through to the consumer,
which will react to them, and to design measures, or regulations, to meet climate, social
or economic goals. Nevertheless, some difficulties could arise. First, [20] explains that
multiple measures can interact with one another when targeting the same group, based on
similar mechanisms and identical timing. They can either have a reinforcing or a mitigating
effect.

3
Network tariffs design review
As explained earlier, network operators are by essence natural monopolies. Therefore,
they need to be regulated and their earnings defined. This is the role of Network Regula­
tory Authorities (NRA). Then, the revenues have to be collected, which is done through the
grid tariff presented in the energy bill. DSO and/or NRA decide on a tariff design, meaning
that they define the methodology for the revenue collection [25]. Several possibilities ex­
ist. Indeed, volumetric, capacity or fixed charges can be applied to the consumer. As part
of the energy bill, and with the roll­out of new technologies and the challenge of climate
change mitigation, this design is becoming very important, since it can harness important
flexibility [26].
The following section is dedicated to network tariffs. First, a presentation of the guiding
principle in their design is given. Then, the link between components of grid tariffs and
economical performances is given. Finally, the question of DER installation, flexibility and
policing is dealt with in the last part of this section.
3.1
Guiding principle
A grid tariff is not only a set of cost components put together. Their design is framed by
precise rules and principles, which are presented in this section. In the second part, the
tariff is decomposed and described part after part.
3.1.1
Design principle
Numerous articles and reports describe the guiding principles for an efficient tariff design
[3], [19], [25], [27], [28], [29]. First and foremost, tariffs must be cost­reflective. Not
particularly linked to distribution tariff, this principle is mainly connected to basic economic
theory. In other words, a consumer, no matter his size, should always pay exactly the costs
of the service he receives. These costs in electricity networks depend on energy losses,
peak usage, the time of use or other specific costs [3]. This founding principle is enforced
by Article 18(7) of Regulation (EU) 2019/943. Cost­reflectiveness enables relevant price
signals to be transmitted to the consumer, who is then able to react accordingly. Thus, it
is easily understandable that the overall efficient use of the network relies on it [3]. This
need for signals also points out the relevance of a rapid roll­out of smart meters, which
are essential to collect data that allow to better associate individual usage and net cost
reflectiveness [3]. Indeed, smart meters are used by DSO to bill consumers according to
their consumption pattern and add these costs to the one created by the data processing,
the sunk costs and prospective costs.
As sub­elements of the cost­reflective principle, [27] states that tariffs should also follow
the 3 following guidelines:
• Cost­additivity: final tariffs should be the results of all the aggregated costs of the
DSO.
• Symmetry: injection and consumption of energy should carry the same value in
reward or charge at a specific time.
• Robustness to the aggregation: when a cost does not depend on the aggregation
of loads, then the sum of individual costs should equal the aggregated one.

Overall, these elements can be summarized in a global principle of cost­efficiency based
on non­distortive and cost­reflective methods [25].
Regarding network users, tariffs should also respect some principles. Numerous charac­

• Transparency [3], [19], [25]
• Predictability [3], [19], [25], [27]
• Simplicity [3], [19], [25]
• Non­discriminatory [3], [25]
• Stability and consistency [25]
These should support the establishment of signals the consumer can react to. By allowing
all type of consumers to calculate, estimate and predict their costs, these principles are
made to allow a greater response from the consumers.
Furthermore, [27] states that consumers should be billed upon the burden they cause to
the network in terms of how much and when meaning that OPEX and congestion costs
should be directed to the one responsible for them. In the same way, tariffs signals should
only reflect costs and not guide consumers towards investment in a specific technology,
meaning that tariffs should be technology­neutral. This aspect can be linked to article
18(1) of Regulation (EU) 2019/943, stating that unrelated policy objectives should not be
included in the tariff design. In this situation, as a policy preference for a technology is
not something the consumer is responsible for, no costs can be allocated to him.
On the operator’s side, tariffs should allow cost recovery for past [3], [19], [25], [27] and
future [19] costs.
Nevertheless, it is to mention that all these principles can have opposing effects. Indeed, a
perfectly cost­reflective tariff would certainly be complex, thus not simple, not transparent,
and probably hard to predict. Even more, prices would be very volatile [3]. Therefore, in
reality, a balance is struck between guiding principles to obtain the best tariff possible. It
also explains why no tariff is best for all situations.
3.1.2
Cost components
There are three types of cost component that DSO can use to charge consumers [3]:
• Fixed charges (in €/year): they are monthly or yearly flat tariffs, often based on the
type of consumer (households or industry e.g.) or their connection voltage to the
grid. They are used to recover sunk costs or other costs not directly linked to grid
usage.
• Capacity charges (in €/kW): also called power charges, they are based on the peak
use of the network. This price can be defined through contracts before the use of the
network (ex­ante), or afterwards through measurements of the actual peak usage
(ex­post).
• Volumetric charges (in €/kWh): also called energy charges, they charge the con­
sumer regarding the use of the system during their energy consumption. The costs
covered are mainly O&M, losses, and other services to the system that the distri­
bution of energy requires. They can be either flat (same rate all the time) or time­
dependant (either predefined band tariffs or dynamic). In the case of DER being
10

==PAGE:33==
installed by a consumer, the energy charges can be net­metered (NM) or non­net­
metered (NNM). When net­metering is applied, the energy exported to the network
is counted backwards by the meter, resulting in only the net import being charged by
the operator. Without net metering, exports and imports are billed by the operator.
Tariffs are designed as one, or a combination of these components. Historically, purely
volumetric tariffs have been used because of the low volatility of energy production. Con­
sidering that energy demand was the only unknown variable for operators [30] and the
strong correlation between power and energy demand [3], these tariffs were working satis­
factorily. Since then, designs have evolved mainly toward a combination of fixed and volu­
metric charges for household consumers1 [31]. If many countries across Europe adopted
this structure, the relative share of each cost components still varies from one to another.
Although, some common trends can be observed, like the fact that the volumetric charge
represents at least 62% of the overall tariff, except for Estonia, Sweden, and the Nether­
lands. Romania even uses a 100% volumetric tariff, as seen Figure 3.1. Some other
countries prefer other methods, like France which uses a 3­part tariff, Finland which has
a capacity­energy scheme or the Netherlands which uses a fixed­capacity design.

As tariffs vary in time and space, DSO may charge consumers differently within the same
country, in accordance with their respective NRA [3]. This spatial differentiation can be
the consequence of multiple operators being in charge over different areas, or like in Ire­
land where different rates are applied to rural and urban consumers [31]. More generally,
the drivers for such discretisation are the consumer density, the distance between trans­
mission lines and consumption point, and the network characteristics and its use [3].
Regarding the time dependency of grid tariffs, different strategies can apply. Hungary,
Romania or the Czech Republic for example have a static tariff, meaning that the rates
and cost components are set for the year and do not change at some point in time [31].
Other countries use ToU tariffs, meaning that the price of the network can vary regarding
the moment it is used. These moments can be set in advance in the form of time­bands

A first intuition to develop a more dynamic tariff is the Critical Peak Tariff (CPT) method,
which consists of applying a largely higher price signal when the local grid is congested to
support “congestion flexibility” and more efficient use of the network. It is an intermediate
solution between flat and fully dynamic tariffs, which sends critical price signals to reduce
congestion effects and is also simpler to design and implement than dynamic tariffs.
3.2
Tariffs and economic results
The impact of tariffs on the economy can be evaluated through different parameters. On
one hand, by their ability to promote efficient use of the network, they are responsible
for some costs to the society when comparing to what would be socially optimal. On the
other hand, they are also responsible for distributional effects by their designs. Some
billing methods could lay a heavier burden on some social groups, increasing inequalities
and raising fairness issues.
3.2.1
Cost for society
The cost for society is defined as the sum of the network cost, the energy cost, the invest­
ment and operation of DER equipment, taxes, carbon, and other charges. All costs are
not considered in every study. As a general ruling, a minimisation of this sum is perceived
as a maximisation of social welfare. In this context, the economic efficiency of one tariff
is to be understood as its ability to minimise the cost for society.
As explained in the previous section, tariffs can be designed in numerous ways. Tariff
design can have a significant impact on the overall efficiency of network operation, and
the welfare that the population can benefit from. Correspondingly, studies have shown that
some changes in tariff design could increase social welfare. For example, [4] shows that
the adoption of a Coasian 2­part tariff would increase the welfare up to 18% by reducing
inefficiencies. This tariff consists of energy charges based on the marginal cost of the
network and some fixed charges tailored to the sunk costs.
[25] studied the impact of different tariff designs on the overall price paid by a household
(including energy price, investment in PV panel or curtailment technologies without any
capital cost, and network payment) and the price of the network including reinforcement
costs. It concludes that both volumetric and fixed tariffs are expensive, both regarding
the overall price of the network and what is paid by the consumer. Capacity charges give
a better outcome than the two previous, but the method resulting in the lowest total sys­
tem cost (defined as the sum of the cost of energy, DER investments and operation, and
the revenues for the operator) is combining fixed charges, distributed according to the
12

Ramsey­pricing principle2, and a share of “Peak Coincidental Network Charges” (PCNC),
meaning that the charge is attributed to the consumer regarding forward to their contribu­
tion to the peak. This method also has the consumer paying less (in investment, energy,
and network) than when facing fixed or volumetric charges.
Going further, [5] shows that, under the assumption that 100% of the costs to be recovered
by the DSO are sunk, fixed charges give the best outcome in term of costs for the society,
including here network costs, DER investments, energy costs and “other” costs (invariant).
The least­cost solution is also the one maximising social welfare. When 100% of the
costs are deemed prospective, meaning that they are not sunk but driven by the peak
consumption, then capacity tariffs give the least costs for the society, since consumers
are incentivised to reduce their peak use of the network, making the needed capacity
decrease. When the costs are half­sunk half­prospective, a two­part capacity and fixed
charge would be preferred. In the same way, [33] shows that purely non­net metered
volumetric tariffs are more efficient than volumetric with net­metering charges or capacity
charges, regardless of the combination of high or low investment cost for PV panels and
batteries. This scheme also has the advantage of being robust to an increased percentage
of prosumers. Since this article has been used for the development of the model, more
details are given in the next section.
3.2.2
Fairness
As pointed out by [27] and numerous other articles, making the overall system better off
does not mean making consumers better off, even less making all of them better off. In­
deed, modifying the tariff design or the weight of each cost component will result in a
redistribution of the network cost that may affect differently different categories of con­
sumers and may result in discriminatory effects. Such a link between tariff and fairness
is discussed by many authors, especially in the situation of DER development. For ex­
ample, [34] points out that under volumetric with net­metering tariffs, and capacity to a
lesser extent, prosumers can free­ride the distribution network, letting passive users bear
the cost of the network.
[33] reaches the same conclusion, in that that net­metered volumetric charges and ca­
pacity charges are unfair toward passive consumers because they end up subsidising the
use of the network for prosumers. Nevertheless, it points out that a rather good fairness
performance can be reached using pure volumetric without net­metering charges. [32]
also explains that using net­metering volumetric charges leads to a transfer of welfare
from passive users to prosumers. These three articles show that the prosumers, by their
ability to adapt to price signals, can pay less the network and make passive users bear
its charge.
From a broader point of view, [28] explains that ToU tariffs can hurt even more passive
users since they would be unable to react to price signals. Indeed, during peak hours,
when prices become high, the difference would be even greater with prosumers who
switch their consumption at a more affordable time. In [15], the network tariffs are pre­
dicted the day before, based on the use of the network. Based on a stepwise approxi­
mation of the marginal cost of the network, this billing method is expected to provide the
right price signals for energy production and self­consumption but might as well creates
cross­subsidies when smart meters are not fully rolled out.
Finally, it is also to be noted that fairness is a matter of how perceived a situation is.
Therefore, switching from volumetric to fixed charges would be seen as deeply unfair, as

3.3
Tariffs, flexibility and policy advice
More than an impact on the economy and people’s finances, tariffs can, through price
signals, have an impact on how consumers behave. By reacting to it, users can become
prosumers and invest in DER to generate energy, transform it from electricity to heat or
store and consume it flexibly. This section is dedicated to the study of the link between
the tariff design and these investments, and then on the policy recommendations that can
be made considering the full picture.
3.3.1
Investment in DER and flexibility equipment
As explained earlier, network tariffs are important in transmitting price signals to con­
sumers. By doing so, they can trigger investments from prosumers in DER and flexible
technologies, such as heat pumps, electric vehicle or energy storage, thereby reducing
congestion effects on the distribution network, and delaying reinforcement investment
[35]. They can also incentivise investments in generation technologies, such as PV pan­
els.
Regarding energy production, the considered technologies are mainly solar panels. They
are incentivised by volumetric tariffs with net­metering [27], [28], [33], [36], [37]. Indeed,
by producing energy and selling it back to the network, meters run “backwards”, creating
an implicit subsidy from passive users to prosumers [33]. At the same time, this mech­
anism does not incentivise batteries, since storing the energy would expose to energy
losses during charging, discharging and the storage phase. Hence, because of these
losses, the fluxes of energy needed to satisfy the load are greater than without energy
storage, creating higher costs. Therefore, volumetric tariffs provide an incentive for de­
centralised generation, but not for flexibility. When considering volumetric charges with­
out net­metering, or “bi­directional” tariff, the generation capacities are not incentivised as
much, but storage can be triggered [32]. Indeed, producing more than what can be stored
or consumed incentivises the consumer to export the energy, which is billed by the DSO.
Capacity tariffs are incentivising battery roll­out [37]. More generally, all responsive loads
are incentivised by capacity charges [27]. Indeed, as the peak created by the prosumer
consumption would be critically responsible for the price paid to the DSO, prosumers
have high incentives to delay their use of the network to reduce their bill. Therefore,
it incentivises largely flexibility and reduces the potential need for reinforcement of the
network [25]. Furthermore, [37] shows that net­pricing methods also incentivise batteries
and reduce the peak load of the consumer, even more, when combined with capacity
tariffs. This method consists of keeping a net­metering principle, but the selling price for
the consumer is lower than the buying one.
Fundamentally, fixed tariffs do not stimulate any behavioural changes since they do not
carry any price signal. Consequently, the prosumer invests in technologies regarding
other parameters present on the energy bill, such as the electricity price and other policy

charges. For example, a very volatile electricity price could trigger flexible load tech­
nologies, and the higher the electricity price, the higher the generation capacity would be
installed [36]. Nevertheless, the design of the network tariff is not responsible for such
investments.
Furthermore, [25] points out that PCNC pricing gives the right signals, triggering the right
investment in DER capacities for efficient use of the network. Indeed, compared to flat
tariffs, ToU or dynamic tariffs encourage even more to invest in responsive load or genera­
tion capacity by charging higher price at the time of congestion. For example, considering
industrial consumers, [29] shows that time­varying signals lead to a significant increase
of electric boiler in the generation mix up to 17%, increasing, therefore, the flexibility of
the electricity network. CPT pricing is therefore a good way to increase the flexibility case
brought up by P2H technologies. Going further, [38] explains that non­coincidental peak
pricing, defined as pricing the peak use of the consumer without regard to the contribution
to the overall peak, is “obsolete” because it incentivises people to reduce their use of the
network over all hour, and not to specifically reduce it when congestion happens on the
network.
In the end, it is to be noted that flexible capacities do not necessarily lead to reduced peak
usage of the network. [11] showed that when combined with real­time electricity pricing
(based on the market price) and a fixed cost component, some capacity tariff design can
lead to higher peak usage of the network while reducing energy procurement costs overall.
[11] explains that using global signals could lead to a synchronised use of the network.
Therefore, in the case of responsive load being installed, new peak usage could appear.
3.3.2
Policy consequences of tariff design
In the end, all decisions are political, in the sense that re­designing tariffs will affect people
differently, regarding their ability to react to price signals, thus their social group and in­
come class [4], [33], [39], [40]. Therefore, the targets pursued by a change of tariff design
should be clearly defined.
Intending to roll out large capacities of solar panels, volumetric with net­metering seems
to be the appropriate solution, but at the expense of low flexibility and serious fairness
questions regarding the financing of the network [37]. When targeting increased flexibility
on the network, capacity charges [27], coincidental peak pricing [32], ToU or dynamic
tariffs [3] give enough incentives to trigger the installation of responsive loads. In the
meantime, [32] shows that tariffs enhancing the flexibility on the network also hinder self­
consumption, which could be a goal in itself for policymakers. In the same way, [34] shows
that the more a policy incentivises PV panels, the less it incentivises electric vehicle.
More generally, the higher the tariff, the higher the installation of DER [36]. Indeed, the
more expensive the electricity is, the more rentable would energy generation be, and the
more energy storage would be useful to avoid paying during the very high price period.
In contrast, it is not true for P2H technologies. Indeed, the more expensive the electricity
is, the less affordable such technology is since its use would increase the load for the
consumer [29]. Therefore, there is a dilemma of electrifying heat as recommended by the
EU [12] or promoting solar panels and other decentralised energy production means.
Another constraint that must be taken into account by policymakers is the cost recovery
for the DSO. Indeed, sunk costs tend to cause revenues shortfall [40]. If fixed charges are
the best solution to recover sunk costs, they drive away consumers making such tariffs
unworkable in real life [14], [15], [28]. Capacity tariffs [32] or volumetric charges [27] are
not ideal to recover the sunk costs, on which the consumer has no impact. By trying to

recover the costs through these methods, prosumers are then able to install some DER
to minimise their own costs, creating a lack of revenues for DSOs, who are then forced to

users, while prosumers are even more incentivised to install more DER. This results in
less interaction with the grid, fewer revenues, potentially ending up in the fatal utility death
spiral [19]. As a consequence, tariff design has to be thought through in the long term,
balancing the effects of the different cost components.
Finally, it is also to understand that all results presented here could be applied in real life
only if smart meters are rolled out sufficiently [15].

Modelling: process and description
Based on the body of literature presented Chapter 3, it has been decided to create an op­
timisation model to study the influence of the design of the network tariffs on the flexibility,
economic and environmental outcomes of the network. To do so, some basic rules have
been followed to have the best formulation possible. Indeed, inspired by recent literature,
the mathematical formulation has been developed in compliance with some principles to
be as transparent as possible: even though no protocol for writing exists at the moment,
it appeared important to follow some basic guidelines that can inspire other works and to
give a better understanding of the process of thoughts which guided the modelling. After
such considerations have been thought through, the model presented in [33] has been
used as a source of inspiration to develop the algorithm used in the present work. A short
presentation of their model will introduce the presently used program, which is introduced
at last in this section.
4.1
Guiding principles and thinking process
In this section, some rules introduced to create replicable conditions are presented. These
rules are given and put into the context of this work, describing the process followed during
the modelling and the choices made. It is done to describe the guiding line behind the
model. General good practices are also given.
4.1.1
Design process
Identified and explained by [41], certain steps have been implemented in the design pro­
cess to be as consistent as possible. Indeed, it has been considered that following a
protocol when redacting the model could only help transparency and ease the under­
standing of the whole process. Therefore, inspired by [41], the following steps have been
considered in the whole project.
• The formulation of the question: this very first step is necessary to have an efficient
modelling process. It consists of mapping the issue at stake and deciding on the pre­
cise answer which is to be given at the end. It also implies determining the audience
which is targeted, the goal of the project, the information that is needed, the organi­
sation of the outputs, the specificity of the results… At this point, some ground rules
are also to be set to ensure the efficiency of the work. In this work, as presented be­
fore, the goal is to formulate policy and technological recommendations to network
operators regarding the optimisation of their tariff design to increase flexibility and
ensure their cost recovery. The data needed are presented in Chapter 5.
• Boundaries: the objective here is to set the limits of the model in term of temporal
precision and horizon and the geographical area targeted while ensuring the mini­
mum complexity required. These considerations are not trivial since a poor design
can lead to significant inefficiencies and imprecisions. Indeed, too high a time res­
olution could lead to high computation time and the sacrifice of other parameters
to make the whole simulation process feasible. In the same way, increasing or de­
creasing the spatial scope could influence greatly the computational burden. Here,
it has been decided to use hourly time series, as they allow to grasp the impact
of time signals without carrying too heavy a computational constraint for a yearly
simulation. For similar reasons, the technologies available for one purpose have
been reduced to one, assumed to be representative of all that are currently on the

In general, the authors show that the more prosumers there are in the energy district, the
more inefficient and unfair the tariffs are, no matter their design. This is caused by the
appearance of a cross­subsidy from passive users to prosumers. The extent to which
these issues would raise is hard to predict since the share of the prosumer in the energy
district directly impacts the outcomes of the model. Indeed, it is pointed out that under
capacity tariffs, the more prosumers there are in the energy district, the larger investments
in batteries are. This effect can lead, for example, to the “utility death spiral”: the more
prosumers there are, the more the cost coefficient need to be increased to ensure the
cost recovery, the more incentives there are to install more DER capacities, resulting in a
trend for prosumers to go off­grid.
[33] also points out that under capacity and volumetric without net­metering tariffs, the
prosumers can end up having a higher overall cost at charge than if no one was investing
in any DER at all. This situation, interpreted as a “Nash equilibrium”, is resulting from the
non­cooperative decision­making process used to model consumers. A Nash equilibrium
is obtained when players know the strategies from one another, but do not have any
incentives to change their own. In this precise case, a consumer would install neither more

nor fewer capacities because the costs at stake do not justify it. But if the decision was
taken by all consumers altogether, they would install less capacity, reaching an optimal
solution. The model applied in [33] does not allow for such behaviour.
Finally, [33] shows with this study that net­metering can be interpreted as an implicit sub­
sidy for PV adoption since it reduces the costs implied by the adoption of this technology.
Furthermore, it can be characterised as a cross­subsidy since the costs are borne by
passive consumers.
4.2.3
Model limitations
The work conducted in [33] served as a base for the development of the model used in
this project. Nevertheless, some limitations have been identified.
Firstly, the tariff designs studied in this paper are only pure 1­part tariffs. As pointed out
by numerous articles ([4], [25]...), presented in Chapter 3, it appears that 2 or 3 part tariffs
could lead to a large improvement of the current situation.
Secondly, the use of daily time series for electrical load has been identified as a potential
source of approximations. Even if the appendix section of [33] presents longer time series,
they still do not cover a full year. This could limit the impact of the investigation. Indeed,
using “stylised” data do not allow a thorough representation of what could happen over a
full year, therefore creating some biases and limitations on the flexibility investigations.
Thirdly and finally, in the same way as for the previous point, the use of “stylised example”
of Europe load profiles can fail to represent a variety of situations. As said in [33], “the
intention of this paper is not to analyse the impact of tariff design on consumers from a
specific region”. Nevertheless, grids are inherently different from one region to another,
creating different input parameters that an “example” cannot grasp. For example, Den­
mark has a lot of district heating network and a high share of wind turbines, while France
electric mix relies on nuclear power and Poland’s on coal. These variations could lead to
different results, and the development of a model able to grasp such a granularity seems
important.
These limitations lead to the need to develop a different model to answer the question
raised in this project.
For completeness purposes, it is to be noted that [5] is answering some of the limitations
identified, but for different purposes. By using advanced mathematical conditions, they
transform the previous model in a single resolvable bi­level non­linear optimisation pro­
cess, allowing them to study multi­part tariffs. This paper has been used as a helping
hand to understand all the ins and outs of the mathematical formulation of the previously
exposed paper. Nevertheless, no such conditions have been used in this Thesis because
of the complexity of such a mathematical formulation. Linear programming has been pre­
ferred to study the issues at stake.
4.2.4
Contribution of the thesis
As presented in the previous sections, there is still a lot to do in this field to improve
the network tariff design and provide more flexibility to the grids. This work is a step
toward filling this gap. Indeed, the goal of this paper is to study the impact of the design of
network tariffs on an energy district. Here are studied the deployment of DER, the flexibility
they can provide to the network, the carbon emissions avoided, but also the fairness and
economic efficiency of such measures. Also, it is made to highlight the impact of some
precise parameters to allow policies recommendations and comparisons to be made.

First, the present work includes a larger technological set. As pointed out by [12], the

different process for attributing the cost coefficient for the grid operators is created to
include multi­part tariffs designs. In the process, the proportions to apply to the design

model are to be used to make policy recommendations for real­life DSO.
Third, this work aims at studying full­year situations. Indeed, average values creating
some potential biases, dataset covering a full year with hourly time steps are used. As
they do not create too high a computational burden, they allow simulations run to execute
quickly enough, and they give higher precision to the results than average ones.
Fourth, this work aims at studying the impact that some parameters could have on the
outcome of the model. Real life being always changing and evolving, some assumptions
or values taken as valid to represent the actual situation might be outdated in the coming
future. Therefore, as [33] studies the impact of the price of technologies, some input
variables are to be questioned to make enlightened policy recommendations.
Finally, data are collected to represent local circumstances, as they are expected to vary
widely. Of course, it is not possible to study all the possible situations, but the model
developed is usable with any type of data as long as they have the right format. The
study of precise situations allows making precise policies recommendation to precise grid
operators, without taking the risk of basing them on biased average European values. The
data collection is presented Chapter 5. In the same way, the model is built to be easily
adaptable: by selecting­deselecting some variables, a large set of local circumstances
can be studied.

6.1.1
Simulation protocol
As identified earlier, many articles only present a few tariff designs. As it appeared unclear
that variations from one design to another would give predictable results, many combina­
tions of parameters are tested and represented. Nevertheless, since the computational
burden is too large for continuous variations of possible designs, only a discrete set of
combinations could be tested. Therefore, some choices were made to strike a balance
between a good coverage of possible designs and a reasonable simulation time.
On the same note, a design relies on a set of 4 variables (the volumetric share, the capac­
ity share, the fixed share, and the net (or not) metering). Since 3 variables are linked by
Equation (4.4), this set can be reduced to three. It is also to note that α, β, and the fixed
share are bound between 0 and 1 and that their sum must equal one. As a result, the con­
figurations shown in Table 6.1 have been tested, both in the configuration of net­metering
and without net­metering
All results of the base case scenario are presented using a heatmap following the previous
template (Table 6.1). It is to understand that for the point (0,0), the tested tariff is, therefore,
a pure fixed tariff. Therefore, tariffs on the bottom line and first column of the tables
are 2­part tariffs except for the angles which represent 1­part tariffs. 3­parts tariffs are
represented on the rest of the table, as represented in Table 6.1, with the share of the
fixed tariff being calculated as 1 – α – β.
Finally, it is to be noted that the bottom line is not changing between maps representing
designs with net­metering and the one without. Indeed, as the volumetric share is null,
the results are the same according to Equation (4.7).

pays more than a passive user, while below 0.5 a passive user pays more. Accordingly,
since only sunk costs are considered, a fairness of 0.5 would be the prime target for a fair
cost attribution.

6.3
Discussion of the results, policy implications
From the previously presented results, it is possible to take out some lessons regarding
the tariff designs made to recover the sunk costs. If it is impossible to state that one design
is better than the others, some trends can be identified. To paraphrase [40], there is no
silver bullet that could solve everything. Again, all mentioned correlations are presented
in Appendix B.
Firstly, there seems to be an opposition between economic and environmental perfor­
mances. Indeed, fairness of the network costs attribution is negatively correlated to the
CO2 avoided (­0.60 correlation coefficient). This phenomenon is mostly due to carbon
savings being mainly linked to the installation of PV panels. Yet solar panels are mainly
incentivised if energy exports are rewarding the prosumer, thus it results in unfair situa­
tions. Correspondingly, there is a high negative correlation of ­0.78 between fairness and
PV capacities. Hence, there is a general tendency for fair tariffs not to be environmentally
efficient, the opposite being also true. Considering all costs, a connection seems to exist
(intermediate coefficient of ­0.5) between the fairness of network cost allocation and the

networks, therefore higher economic efficiency. At the same time, as explained in Sec­
tion 6.2.4, the fairer the design the less total cost difference between passive users and
prosumers. In the end, there might be political choices to make, between economical
performances (fairness of network cost allocation and total costs, economic efficiency…)
and environmental results.
Secondly, based on the data and assumptions used in this scenario, flexibility can be
challenging to trigger. First, as shown before, heat pumps are not flexible in the way they
are used here. To increase their flexibility, they should be combined with heat storage
and their capacity should be increased. Policymakers may consider artificially incentivis­
ing prosumers to invest more in bigger heat pumps. This could be a good solution to
increase flexibility on the network and reduce carbon emissions, even more since as of
now the results shows that heat pumps are not correlated with any fairness issue, peak
load increase or changes in total costs. Nonetheless, a drawback would be that it would
allow prosumers to avoid more taxes, which may lead to a revenue shortfall for the state,
which would require to be corrected ex­post. Another difficulty would be that incentives
could not be made by manipulating the cost components of the tariff design since it seems
that there is no significant correlation between the amount installed and the volumetric,
capacity, or fixed component (all three correlations are below 0.36 in absolute value).
Theoretically, low V NP should incentivise heat pumps investments. Second, investment
in battery storage might be easier to trigger to provide flexibility to the network as they
are highly correlated to the capacity cost component (0.91). Economically, batteries are
not correlated to large fairness issues or tax avoidance, but they would likely increase
the costs for prosumers, thus reduce the global economic efficiency. At the same time,
manipulating the capacity cost component should not result in too much change for other
technologies since they are rather uncorrelated (all below 0.33 between CNP and PV,
heat pump and heat storage). Finally, with decreasing costs of technology and science
progress, flexibility might just increase naturally thanks to higher profitability. For now, the
amount of flexible capacity provided remains low, the bigger battery installed being way
smaller than the one for an average electric car (2.33 kWh against 35 kWh). All in all, it
is difficult to trigger some flexibility at the household level.
Thirdly, results obtained show that there should not be utility death spirals under any cir­
cumstances considering these data and assumptions. The constraints forbidding negative
net import and the one preventing solar curtailment are undoubtedly partly responsible for
this result, as they limit the size of panels that can be installed by prosumers and force the
production of energy. As a result, energy is produced at the maximum rate all the time,
forcing prosumers either to store or export it. By putting a non­negative net­import con­
straint, the size of solar panels is capped. Batteries being still too expensive, the cheaper
and best solution for prosumers is then to remain connected to the grid. In practice, even
without these constraints, the solar profile from Denmark is unlikely to allow prosumers to
go off­grid. Again, these results are specific to the assumptions studied in this scenario.

For example, [57] or [58] explains that load defection is already high in California and that

For the heat network, it is completely different since the roll­out of heat pumps is promot­

heat pumps, shown in Figure 6.4. It creates a large reduction in business opportunities
for actors of the heat sector.
Fourthly, it seems important to study the impact of tariff designs and DER technologies
on the total cost for society. Results show that the least overall total cost is reached for
purely fixed costs. It confirms the result presented in [5], which states that when costs
are sunk, fixed charges are the cheapest way around. It is also interesting to notice that
there is a large correlation between batteries and overall total cost (0.71). Being the most
expensive technology in the model, this result is making sense. It is interesting to notice
that it is particularly true for NNM designs (0.92), while the correlation is lower for NM tariffs
(0.66). It could be explained by the larger PV panel capacities installed in the second case,
counterbalancing the investment costs by highly reduced yearly energy costs. Regarding
causes for high overall total costs, it is strongly correlated to peak load reduction for NNM
tariffs while it is uncorrelated for NM ones. In the end, this can be explained by the very
nature of the billing method: with NNM schemes, the more energy is circulating, the bigger
the price for the prosumer, no matter the direction of the fluxes. Finally, on that matter, it is
to notice that there is no apparent correlation between the overall total cost and the total
cost for either prosumers or passive users when considering NM tariffs, but that there
seems to be a link when considering NNM tariffs (coefficient of correlation of 0.66 and
0.97 respectively).
To recap it seems that batteries are the main technology that could bring flexibility to the
network at the household level, heat pumps being used inflexibly. By artificially incen­
tivising heat storage, more flexible behaviour could be obtained. Environmentally, heat
pumps are also necessary, as electrification is decarbonising the heat. All in all, PV pan­
els are participating greatly in these emission cuts, and looser constraint on their use
could help increase the level of investment. Nevertheless, this would further increase the
need for flexibility, and it would risk the introduction of more considerable inequalities be­
tween prosumers, expected to be wealthier [59], and passive users. Finally, if fixer tariffs
are more cost­efficient (lower cost for the society), volumetric with net metering is more
environmentally performant and capacity­based tariffs allow for more flexibility.

7
Sensitivity analysis
The following chapter is dedicated to giving some insights into variations of the baseline
scenario in parameters that are likely to change between locations or in the coming years.
First, changes in the price of CO2 are studied. Second, a sensitivity analysis is made to the
amount of sunk costs that the operator needs to recover, as it could carry a deep impact
on the level of investments and that it is not the same everywhere. Third, the percentage
of responsive consumers is the parameter that varies, since it appears imaginable that
this value is likely to change at some point, the climate concerns growing in people’s mind.
Fourth, a study on the heat profile is made, as it is expected to carry an important impact
on the electrification of heat. Finally, a scheme of Critical Peak Tariff is implemented with
two variations.
For a purpose of conciseness, only variations in investment level are represented here, as
they are directly responsible for variations in other parameters. For example, the level of
investment in PV panels drives the fairness of the sunk cost allocation, and not the other
way around. Some quick insights are given for the other parameters. Greater represen­
tations are given in Appendix F to Appendix J. Also, when average values are expressed,
they represent the average value for all tariff designs. When relative changes are ex­
pressed, the change compared to the base case scenario are expressed, in the form of a
relative variation.
Note: Scales can vary from one sensitivity (subsection) to another for representation pur­
poses but remain the same intra­figure. It is designed that way to better represent the
variations caused by one parameter, not between parameters. Heat maps are read the
same way as in the previous chapter.
7.1
Carbon sensitivity
7.1.1
Scenario variation
The value of the ton of carbon emitted in the atmosphere is, in Europe and for intensive
industries, in particular for the power sector, defined by the European Emission Trading
Scheme (EU­ETS) market. Here, emission quotas transit to reach an equilibrium accord­
ing to market driving forces and economic theories. In the base case, the price of 20 €/ton
is used. As going forward, this price is not expected to decrease, higher values have been
studied. The first one is 40 €/ton since the EU­ETS price is currently fluctuating between
20 and 40 €/ton. The highest value taken is 100 €/ton, as it is used in different documents
as a possible target for an efficient valuation of carbon. For example, France is explicitly
aiming at increasing the price of CO2 emissions up to 100 €/ton for 2030 in the climate
law passed in 2016 [60].

Economic performances
The average fairness of network costs is slightly decreasing with an increased carbon
price, of around 0.3% for NM tariffs and 0.2% for NNM ones in absolute values. It is to
be linked with the increase in investments in solar panels, as they have been identified as
one of the main reasons for fairness changes in the previous chapter. Nevertheless, it is
also to be said that the median fairness is increasing at the same time, of 0.04 and 0.3%
respectively. From that it is to be understood that variations are not uniform across tariffs:
increased investments in PV panels tend to reduce the fairness of sunk cost allocation
while decreased battery investments (for pure capacity tariffs) have the opposite effect.
All in all, the pattern observed in Figure 6.6 is not changed because changes are very
low, with a magnitude of 0.003 · 404 = 1.2 €/pers/year. The maps are represented in
Figure F.1 in Appendix F.
On tax avoidance, maps being represented in Figure F.2 in Appendix F, the changes
are the other way around: because of higher investments in heat pumps, tax avoidance
increases, and more noticeably for NNM than for NM designs. Nevertheless, the same
assessment as for fairness can be made, in that that tax avoidance remains below 4% in
absolute value, and that NNM designs still allow for fewer savings on that matter, despite
the higher relative increase.

Carbon insights and total cost
An increased carbon price is allowing prosumers to reduce their emissions, except for
tariffs which do not trigger any response from the prosumers under any circumstances
(purely fixed tariffs e.g.). Relatively speaking, average changes are steeper for NNM de­
signs (+9.4% of carbon avoided against 5.5%), but the savings remain well below NM

7.1.3
Main takeaways and policy conclusions
At the household level, and only considering energy consumption, an increase in the CO2
value can trigger some interesting changes. It increases the carbon savings to larger
extents than the other parameters tested and can make batteries profitable for more tariff
designs. More importantly, the combination of a high carbon price and electricity storage
would allow for consumption at times when the energy is cleaner. This phenomenon could
help accelerate the energy transition. On the downside, it makes the cost for society rise.
All in all, the absolute changes remain low per household. Such a measure could have
larger effects if bigger energy consumers were considered. Furthermore, with no net­
import constraint, a larger increase in solar panel investments would occur, enhancing
the benefit from higher carbon prices. It also has the critical advantage of being easily
feasible and applicable.
Moreover, it is not to forget that an increase in carbon price is likely to have an impact
on the overall society, thus it would be reductive to consider that the only impact of such
a measure is made on the energy consumption at the household level. The trends ob­
served here could likely be transferred to other parts of the society and have a ripple effect
consequently, especially regarding carbon emissions.
Also, as a limit to this study, there is no link between the carbon price and the electricity
mix (energy price and carbon intensity). In practice, such a connection is expected to
exist. Further studies should look in this direction.

By increasing the amount of sunk costs to recover, Equation (4.41) implicitly gives a
greater value to each “unit of energy circulating” and “each unit of exchange capacity”. In
other words, both V NP and CNP values are greater.
As a result, heat pumps, which are increasing the electric load, are affected by such a
change, as shown Figure 7.4. In general, for NNM designs, the increase of the V NP
value is causing a decrease in heat pump investment. For NM designs, the increase in
V NP value is less causing a decrease in heat pump investment because solar panels
can compensate by powering them or exporting energy, but two intermediate designs are
still well affected. The observed decrease is mainly caused by an increase in heat storage
investment in this case, even if the installed capacities remain below 0.16 kWh. All in all,
the more fixed the tariff, the fewer variations in investment because the less the consump­
tion and the network costs billed are linked. Also, the capacity invested in remain modest
and below 0.7 kW­h for all tariffs.
Finally, investments in heat pumps using NNM designs are more affected than NM ones
because without net metering all the energy exchanged between the network and the
users is billed.
Battery investments, correspondingly to Figure 7.5, are also changing with changing sunk
costs. With reduced sunk costs, only a pure capacity tariff gives enough incentives to trig­
ger investments in batteries. On the contrary, when sunk costs increase, investments in
batteries are bigger and more accessible for volumetric tariffs. It is observed that the more
peak load carries an important weight on the final bill, the more it is interesting to flatten
the consumption curve, thus investing in batteries. With increased sunk cost, the CNP
value for some low capacity­based tariffs becomes high enough to trigger investments in
battery capacity: under NNM designs, designs with 2/3 of the tariff being volumetric can
make electricity storage attractive. The investment level almost reaches 2.4 kWh at most.
For NM designs, the large investments in PV panels represented Figure 7.6 do not allow
for energy to be stored, since exporting it grants bigger savings than storing it for later.
Figure 7.6 shows that the investment level in PV panels is also regarding the amount
of sunk costs. The average value for NM tariffs goes from 3.5 to 4.3 to 4.5 kW installed,
while it goes from 0.7 to 1.2 to 1.7 kW without net metering. The increase in PV investment
can be explained by the increased V NP value: solar panels allow for greater savings,
thus are incentivised more. Nevertheless, the maximum capacity is still capped at 6.7kW
because of the Equation (4.37), thus the variations are not uniform across all tariffs1.
Without this constraint, investments in solar panels are expected to increase largely for
highly volumetric NM tariffs.
As a general rule for NM tariffs, the more volumetric, the more PV panels are profitable,
the amount of sunk costs only exacerbating the phenomenon. For NNM tariffs it is a bit
different since the amount of sold energy is also billed by the DSO. Consequently, with
increasing sunk costs, investments in PV panel rise more for designs with a high share
of capacity component (along with battery investments), excessing the level of purely
volumetric designs, as shown in Figure 7.6.

avoided carbon is noticeable. On the contrary, where a large increase of PV panel in­

better environmental performances with increasing sunk costs, while others have worse
results. On average, NM designs still perform better than the respective NNM ones, and

Changes in the average total costs are close to linear with sunk costs. The noticeable thing

in use. Results show that this variation is impacting the overall total cost almost equally
in both cases, but that in the NNM case, the consequences are more shared between
prosumers and passive users, being a total cost reduction when the amount of sunk cost
is reduced, or a total cost increase the other way around. It makes echo to the fact that
total costs are closer between prosumers and passive users when using NNM instead of

costs, only exacerbated: differences between users are bigger with higher sunk costs as
shown in Figure G.4 and in Figure G.5 in Appendix G.
7.2.3
Main takeaways and policy conclusions
This sensitivity analysis highlights the counter effects that are applying to carbon emis­
sions. On average, the higher the overall network cost, the lower the investments in heat
pumps but the higher in solar panels. These two technologies being responsible for car­
bon cuts, there is a therefore a middle ground where average emission avoided are at a
maximum. Nevertheless, this statement should be treated cautiously because it is based
on average values. Indeed, when looking closely, it is noticeable that the save emissions
increase for certain designs and decrease for others. Therefore, policymakers should in­
vestigate thoroughly the situation upon which they deliberate and the exact value of the
sunk costs that must be recovered. A design fit for inexpensive networks is not necessarily
for expensive ones.
Finally, considering that heat pumps do not behave flexibly, it could be said that more
expensive sunk cost enable bigger flexibility, as batteries deployed are bigger. Again, it
is not true for all tariffs, thus the need for a precise knowledge of the fixed costs to design
properly the tariff to apply.

Prosumer proportion
7.3.1
Scenario variation
As the population, social groups or meteorological conditions can vary greatly from one

expected to change. In particular, 93% of Europeans see climate change as a ”serious
problem” and already have taken at least one action to mitigate it [61]. Therefore, com­
bined with increasing affordability in DER, an increasing number of prosumers could be
expected. As testing 0% and 100% seemed exaggerated hypothesis, intermediate cases
have been tested, knowingly 25 and 75%.

7.3.2.2
Economic performances
As a consequence of greater investments in solar panels, the fairness of network costs
allocation is decreasing (Figure H.1 in Appendix H). Correspondingly to the precedent
numbers, the fall is greater for NM tariffs than for NNM, where it remains almost stable
(1.4% reduction between 25 and 75% of prosumers). In the case of NM tariffs, the de­

investments in batteries for highly capacity tariffs. For NNM designs, the changes are
concentrated for capacity­based tariffs: bigger batteries allow a prosumer to cut his peak
use to reallocate sunk costs to the passive users.
With an increased share of prosumers connected to the network, prosumers are less likely
to avoid taxes, correspondingly to the lower investments in heat pumps, but in absolute
values, variations have an order of magnitude of 0.01%. Again, the trends identified pre­
viously guide the trends observed in fairness and tax avoidance, as shown in Figure H.2
in Appendix H.
7.3.2.3
Carbon insights and total cost
In general, CO2 emissions cuts are very small, correspondingly to modest variations in
investments. For capacity­based designs, the increase in battery allows for more carbon
savings (90kg). For the rest, emission changes have a magnitude of less than a kilogram.
For NM designs, the more prosumers there are, the more carbon is avoided per prosumer.
As shown in Figure H.3 in Appendix H, the amount of carbon avoided increases where the
investment in PV increase, but decreases where the investments in heat pump decrease.
All in all, the average changes are very small (less than 3.2% in total) but have to be mul­
tiplied by the number of new prosumers to get a grasp on what it represents. Therefore,
even a small change could lead to considerable change.
In terms of cost for society, an increase is to be noted both for NM and NNM schemes in
Figure H.4 and in Figure H.5 in Appendix H. Nevertheless, NNM tariffs prove to be very
robust, since the relative changes are very uniformly small, and of the same magnitude for
both prosumers and passive users (around 2% of relative average increase respectively
between 25 and 75% of prosumers). It results that these tariffs more economically efficient
than NM ones.
With NM tariffs, the total costs are very stable for prosumers. For passive users, total
costs increase regardless of the design. For mainly capacity or fixed based designs,
the total cost varies between 1400 and 1600 €/pers/year. For volumetric tariffs, the total
cost skyrocket, from 1487 to 2564 €/pers/year. In this situation, if the fairness is not
changing significantly, fewer passive users must compensate the cost of more prosumers,
explaining the heavier burden they have to bear.

7.3.3
Main takeaways and policy conclusions
The main fact that this sensitivity analysis points out is the robustness of NNM designs. In­
deed, the variations are very small for most of them over changes in prosumer proportion.
This should allow policymaker to take measures applicable in the long run, regardless of
the share of prosumers. It reduces the global uncertainty surrounding the reform of tariff
designs, which might be advantageous.
On the contrary, NM tariffs present the advantage of increased environmental perfor­
mances per person with an increased share of prosumers connected to the network (ex­
cept in some very precise cases). Therefore, multiplied by the number of prosumers, it
could have a significant effect. Economically, the increased share of prosumers also in­
creases the poor performances of NM designs, making the unfair tariffs even unfairer and
the overall total cost higher, reducing their economic efficiency and social welfare. The
only positive aspect on that matter is the reduced amount of tax avoided by prosumers
but at the expense of lower electrification of heat, which is not necessarily desirable.

7.4.2.2
Economic performances
Fairness is increasing on average, as shown Figure I.1 in Appendix I. For NM designs,
fairness is better for tariffs with a capacity component, since the increased size of panels is
making the peak load largely bigger for the prosumer. Consequently, the pure volumetric
tariff still allows prosumer to not pay the network, while 3­part tariffs with low volumetric
share reach fairness level slightly over 50%.
For NNM tariffs, low volumetric tariffs show the same increase in fairness, as the difference
between designs is small. For highly volumetric tariffs the fairness is largely increasing:
because of higher load, the amount of “units of energy” exchanged between the prosumer

the passive user. For the most volumetric design, prosumers end up paying for around

respectively reaching 11.9 and 11.7%, as shown in Figure I.2 in Appendix I. For the biggest
floor area, it represents up to 100€ of taxes that a prosumer is not paying compared to a
passive user.

Carbon insights and total cost
Thanks to an increased amount of heat pumps invested in, the amount of avoided carbon
is broadly increasing too, by 252 and 275% for NM and NNM designs, reaching 3471
and 3250 kg/year on average respectively, as shown in Figure I.3 in Appendix I. PV pan­

consumption is strictly the same as with lower floor area (in other words, the amount of
electricity that is self­consumed for electricity purposes is the same). Essentially, the pat­
tern observed with the lower floor area is just transposed to higher levels, with higher
savings where more panels are installed and lower savings where fewer heat pumps are
installed. If emissions at the grid level were considered, the amount of carbon saved
would be bigger with higher heat consumption since the size of PV panels is bigger.

7.4.3
Main takeaways and policy conclusion
It results from this sensitivity study that the greater the heat load for consumers, the more
prosumers are expected to install heat pumps, the more carbon can be saved. A larger
diffusion of greener distributed energy resources is incentivised; thus it would allow a
greater reduction of carbon­intensive sources in the energy mix at the grid level. Nev­
ertheless, flexibility is also reduced because of lower battery investments and the use of
the heat pumps as a base heat provider. The increased size of heat pumps offers some
potential for flexibility, but they should be coupled with great heat storage to really offer
some to the network. In the present scenarios, it is not the case.
On the economic aspect, two main aspects arise. On the one hand, prosumers, which
can be assimilated to wealthier social groups, could end up financing more the network
than passive users with the increased electrified heat load. At the same time, they can
reduce their overall bill. This could be both politically arguable and socially acceptable. On
the other hand, the bigger the electrified load, the more taxes are avoided by prosumers,
creating a revenue shortfall for the state.

lighting the difficulty to select an optimum tariff design. As general trends, it can be no­
ticed that NNM tariffs always have, on average, better performances on the economical
ground (better fairness, lower total cost for the society and total system cost) but are less
environmentally friendly than NM designs. On the flexibility ground, it is also shown that
heat pumps are not used as flexible heat provider as they are used as baseload no mat­
ter the variations in parameter. Hence, batteries are the only real source of flexibility,
heat storage being installed in too low capacities to have a significant impact. Never­
theless, storage capacities are mainly installed for capacity­based tariffs, except using a
CPT mechanism without net­metering. In this situation, volumetric charges also incen­
tivise the use of batteries. Extremely expensive networks or very high carbon price could
also trigger investments in batteries for highly volumetric NNM designs.
At the same time, there still is a competition between environmental and economical per­
formances, in every case. For example, while an increase in carbon price is leading to
more carbon emissions avoided, it is also leading to poorer fairness and bigger inequalities
over the total cost that prosumers and passive users have to bear, even if both categories
have to pay more. Considering that prosumers are often wealthier, an increase in carbon
price is therefore hurting more low­income classes. More generally, carbon savings are
the results of investments in PV or heat pumps. These investments are increasing the
total cost for the society and the system cost and are very often allowing prosumers to
reallocate some of the sunk costs to passive users.
In the end, some limitations to the results are to be pointed out. The variations studied
are only theoretical, thus are the effect. In practice, prosumers are unlikely to adapt to
some variations, for example, if the carbon price is indeed increasing on a steady and
long­term basis (which is already unlikely), it is improbable that prosumers would adapt
the installed capacity of PV to match with the new price. In the same way, if a prosumer
increases his heat consumption, he is very unlikely to reduce the storage capacity installed
in his household. Even more, investments would be made only for feasible situations, for
people being aware of potential gains, able to pay the upfront costs and willing to invest in
different technologies at the same time. Consequently, these results only display trends
of what could be expected with changing situation.

8
Discussion and limitations
As with every modelling project, assumptions and data are potential sources of bias and
can influence the results. As a consequence, it appears crucial to present and discuss
identified sources of potential limitations. First, a discussion about the interpretation of
the results is carried. In the second part of this chapter, limitations about the data and
the assumptions made are identified and discussed. Finally, general observations and
limitations are discussed.
8.1
Interpretation limitations
First, the results presented in this paper are only theoretical. For example, PV panels
require a case­per­case study before investments are made, and the general parameters
used here lack this representation. Even inside a region, weather conditions or the land­
scape can make investments unfeasible. In the same way, no particular attention is given
to the area required for the installation of DERs. If solar panel need a certain amount of
exposure to the sun to provide the required power, heat pumps and batteries also need
some space to be installed. Many parameters can be considered, such as easy access
to provide maintenance to the facilities, or a sufficient air source for the heat pump to be
efficient. All these parameters make such an investment impossible to generalise in real
life.
Second, when analysing the results, it is important to remember that only sunk costs are
represented here. This assumption is motivated by the fact that sunk costs are the main
reason for issues for operators in their cost recovery dilemma. Indeed, as explained in
the literature review, they have “no cause” directly linkable to the consumer, and fixed
charges, which would be the most efficient billing method, are not well seen. Neverthe­
less, prospective costs are also a reality and could be considered in further studies. Cost
of energy losses could be added in the form of volumetric charges and would act the same
as additional energy taxes since being paid “per­unit”. This would reduce the penetration
of heat pumps since electricity would be more expensive, but also incentivise investments
in PV panels. Prospective costs could be added to, in the form of an additional capac­
ity charge based on the coincidental peak. It could lead to lower fairness, as prosumers
would be able to reduce yet another cost, but also allow higher investments in battery
units.
Finally, no rebound effect is considered in this study. It would not lead to increased in­
vestment in DER in the short term, but it could lead to bigger consumption for prosumers.
It would certainly lead to a bigger cost for society since more energy would be consumed.
Assuming that consumption would not increase for passive users, fairness could also
increase.
8.2
Assumptions and data
First and foremost, a discussion should be made on Equation (4.37), forcing net­import
from the grid to be positive. The prime idea had been to prevent the network from pay­
ing the prosumer at the end of the year, especially when using net­metering designs.
But it had been rapidly identified that a constraint just forcing the network costs for the
prosumers to be at least positive would have a major flaw. Indeed, as soon as a vol­
umetric share is included in the design, prosumers would install a large amount of PV
Technical and policy recommendations regarding the optimisation of an energy district
79
==PAGE:102==
panels to export energy and therefore benefit from the cross­subsidy identified in numer­
ous articles like [33] and pay the network way less. This constraint putting a limit on what
prosumers could eventually do, it has been deliberately chosen to put a stringer constraint
onto them by the use of Equation (4.37). This constraint limits the amount of PV panels
that prosumers can invest in even though it would be profitable to them. As explained in
Section 6.3, this constraint is therefore improving the fairness for NM highly volumetric
tariff designs and limits the risks of grid defection. Nevertheless, with lower technology
prices or higher energy prices, this constraint could prove to have the opposite effect and
drive prosumers toward energy autonomy.
In the same way, if curtailment of solar energy was allowed, instead of Equation (4.14),
bigger investments in PV panels could theoretically be made. In practice, it could hurt the
business opportunity for batteries in some cases, since not producing the energy could
prove to be cheaper than storing it.
Other hypothesis and assumptions that were taken in the modelling process are to be dis­
cussed. One of them is the absence of any feedback loop regarding the carbon intensity
of the electricity mix in real­time. Indeed, when a reasonable amount of people install a
reasonable amount of PV panels, it is reasonable to assume that no significant change to
the carbon intensity would occur. This justifies the assumption used here. Nevertheless,
it could be interesting in future work to study the scenario where the energy produced is
also consumed locally, by neighbouring households or businesses. With this hypothesis,
the carbon intensity reduction onto the energy grid is to consider carefully as it would have
a greater impact locally.
In the same way as the feedback about the carbon intensity, there is no feedback on
the peaks either. They are considered as an exogenous factor in the critical peak pricing
scheme, and the reaction and behavioural change from the prosumers on these peaks are
not considered here. Again, it is relevant to keep this hypothesis here when considering
the whole distribution network, but it would not stand when considering smaller scale
districts. It would be also particularly relevant to consider this factor in further studies.
Regarding peak pricing, it is noticeable that this model does not consider coincidental
peaks in the attribution of capacity charges. The capacity considered is the maximum
one used over the year. As stated in [40], such a method is not fit for representing the
costs that one consumer creates since there is no guarantee that the peak usage of one
consumer is happening at the same time as the network is under the most pressure.
The implementation of such a billing method would lead to better cost reflectiveness, but
would undoubtedly hurt passive users, especially combined when combined with CPT.
Nevertheless, as the profiles used for prosumers and passive users are the same, there is
no real issue here, as the approximation of the responsibility for the needs in transmission
capacity is not unrealistic. In any case, it would be interesting to adjust the presented
model to consider this constraint.
Also, the model is built to correspond to the description of Denmark. Therefore, to use it
on a wider basis, it should be adapted. This means that other data should be gathered,
regarding the price of energy, tax rates, carbon intensity, consumption profiles… It also
implies that some parameters should be changed. For example, in countries where district
heating is not an option, a shift in technologies should be made. To that matter, the model
is built to be easily adapted to different situations, by “switching on and off” some variables
which would not be relevant in new cases. Nevertheless, other options could be selected
and would be relevant to study in further work, such as the possibility to use heat pumps
at the district heating heat generation level. This way, it could allow economies of scale.
In this present paper, the goal was to study the flexibility that households could provide
80
Technical and policy recommendations regarding the optimisation of an energy district
==PAGE:103==
under different tariffs schemes; therefore, this option has not been considered. In practice,
a thorough study should be made to study the amount of flexibility this new set­up could
bring to the network to allow a good comparison. It is to be noted that, as pointed out by
[62], high investment cost and low operation cost incentivise a non­flexible use of heat
pumps, which corresponds with the results found in this study at the household level.
8.3
General limitations
Another limitation that needs to be highlighted here is the fact that only “immediate” consid­
erations are made. In other words, no commissioning, no decommissioning, no prospec­
tive costs are used here. Life­cycle analyses (LCA) on solar panels show that producing
one unit of energy is still responsible for some carbon emissions when considering the en­
tire life of the product [63]. In the same way, batteries are known to have a non­negligible
impact on the environment during the early and late phases of their life [47]. Neverthe­
less, none are considered in this model. Therefore, the model used in this report is not
representing correctly the entire picture, which is yet critical to address, especially when
dealing with consequences from climate change. On the same note, [63] points out that
widespread use of such technologies raises the question of real sustainability because
of the use of rare materials without substitutes and of their end of life. It is important to
keep in mind that large use of rare materials could put their provision line under much
pressure, create scarcity, and provoke a price increase. This could considerably change
the assumptions taken here.
Widespread use of rare materials has also an impact on human rights and living condi­
tions, as for now, most of the ores necessary to build batteries are extracted in appalling
conditions. Taking the famous example of cobalt mining in the Democratic Republic of
Congo, it has been proven that there are numerous dramatic issues such as child labour,
high prevalence of infectious diseases or high mortality rates, to only give some few exam­
ples [64], [65]. But since cobalt is crucial in the making of batteries, it raises the question
of a real sustainable and desirable future from the widespread use of such technologies.
Finally, and on a more general note, this model relies on unproven assumptions [66], in
particular the hypothesis of rational investments in and utilisation of DERs for personal
benefit. Indeed, rationality in behaviour is critically convenient, easing the modelling pro­
cess and the simulations. It is also not accurate. Humans can react differently to stimulus,
thus assuming a common answer from prosumer is at best inaccurate. As explained by
[67], people, because of their very own cognitive process, could react differently to cli­
mate change effects and incentives to mitigate it, based on the distance from which they
perceive climate change. Already in 2008, [68] states that ”standard economic approach
to climate change policy, with its focus on narrowly rational, self­regarding responses to
monetary incentives, is seriously flawed”. This article concludes that the use of models
which puts greed and self­profit over cooperation and altruism are not fit to describe and
comprehend human behaviour. Consequently, the model developed here relying on such
assumptions for practical reasons is necessarily creating inaccuracies. Including these
components is a significant challenge, both for regulators and modellers.

Conclusion and future work
9.1
Conclusion
As grid tariffs are expected to be updated to adapt to greater variable and distributed
energy resources, this report aims at identifying trends in flexibility, environmental and
economic gains that could be expected from various tariff structure. Based on linear cost
optimisation from individuals, operators and regulators can gain insights into the potential
effect a reform of the tariff design can have on prosumers response. Indicators, such as
the amount of carbon avoided or the total cost for society, are extracted and analysed to
give policymakers better understanding of the ins and outs of the dynamic behind tariff
designs.
This thesis studies a hypothetical energy district, based on data representing Denmark.
In the base scenario, 50% of the network users can react to price signals by investing
in PV panels, batteries, heat pumps, heat storage or gas boiler. Other consumers are
only connected to the grid and district heating. Based on a double level optimisation, con­
sumers try to minimise their total cost, composed of energy, network, investment in DER,
O&M of DER, taxes and carbon cost, while the DSO reacts to their optimisation to adjust
the cost coefficient used to recover the sunk costs amounting to 404 €/pers/year. The
reached equilibrium is expected to represent the optimised situation, where prosumers
pay the less possible and the operator recovers the sunk costs.
This study shows that, in general, heat pumps are an efficient way for prosumers to re­
duce their bills while reducing their carbon footprint. Capacities invested in can almost
cover the peak heat demand. The coupling of heat and electricity could trigger large
flexibility potentials, but simulations show that they are used as baseload provider, thus
inflexibly. Nevertheless, it opens opportunities for authorities to improve this flexibility by
incentivising heat storage, which is not incentivised enough under the assumptions used.
Gas boilers are, in every case, too expensive to be installed.
This study also highlights that flexibility through electricity storage is incentivised by ca­
pacity tariffs. By encouraging prosumers to reduce their peak use, this cost component
push for investments in batteries which can be used to delay imports (or exports) of en­
ergy. Nevertheless, the designs incentivising flexibility do not trigger large investments,
as battery capacities remain below 3 kWh in all circumstances tested, far from the ones
of electric vehicles. These tariffs, by triggering investments, increase the cost for the so­
ciety and put a larger share of the sunk cost onto passive users. Globally, the cost for
society is minimised with fixed tariffs because no investments are incentivised. This is a
consequence of the costs being sunk.
The results display that highly volumetric designs behave very differently regarding if they
are used with or without net­metering. With net metering, serious issues of fairness are
created by the cross­subsidies from passive to responsive users highlighted in this study.
The introduction of a positive net­import constraint limits the effects by capping the invest­
ments in solar PV at 6.7 kW. Nevertheless, substantial amounts of energy are exported
and costs are re­allocated to passive users. No flexibility is triggered either.
Without net metering, good fairness can be achieved because energy exports are billed
by the DSO, resulting in more ”units of energy” being charged for the prosumer. As a
consequence, investments in PV panels do not exceed 3kW. If flexibility is more easily
attained, a substantial share of capacity tariff is still required in the tariff design. Globally,
Technical and policy recommendations regarding the optimisation of an energy district
83
==PAGE:106==
NNM designs are cheaper for society, and total costs differences are smaller between
users. Nevertheless, environmental performances are poorer since the amount of PV
panel installed is significantly lower, and that investments in heat pumps can be limited.
More generally, it underlines the competitions opposing environmental and economical
performances of tariff designs.
This study shows that an increase in the cost of CO2 could lead to important reductions in
carbon emissions for tariffs where investments in heat pumps were limited by increasing
the profitability of electrifying heat. In general, it increases the burden of the sunk costs
onto passive users. It also does not create substantial improvement to the flexibility.
A more expensive network is likely to conduct to lower investment in heat pumps but could
lead to a large increase in flexibility through bigger batteries installed for capacity tariffs.
these are the results of the increasing value of V NP and CNP. This study shows how
important it is to know the value of the sunk costs when settling for a design, as changes
can vary widely from one to another with different sunk costs, especially regarding carbon
emissions.
NNM designs are very robust to an increased proportion of prosumers. Consequently, this
type of tariff could prove to be stabler in time than NM ones. Generally, an increased share
of prosumers reduces the investments in heat pumps but increases the one in batteries.
Overall, fairness is expected to decrease as more prosumers are putting more cost to
fewer passive users. Nevertheless, NM tariffs improve environmentally with increasing
prosumer share. This in particular could prove to be very interesting since changes are
quantified “per person”. When multiplied by the actual number of people becoming actors,
the improvement could be substantial.
An increase in the heat consumption would lead to bigger investment in heat pumps,
keeping them in their role of baseload provider. As a consequence, the amount of carbon
avoided increase largely. Moreover, the capacity of solar panels would be increased,
and fairness would rise largely, making prosumers pay more than half the costs of the
sunk costs in some cases (especially NNM highly volumetric tariffs). Nevertheless, large
shares of taxes would be avoided by prosumers and flexibility would be reduced largely
on the network.
Finally, the introduction of a CPT mechanism for prosumers could lead to large improve­
ments in flexibility for a large set of NNM designs, and without hurting the use of heat
pumps. The effective use of the network would be greatly reduced during peak hours,
leading to less congestion and lower needs for increased network capacity. The study
also shows that it could reduce the total cost for passive users by increasing fairness.
Nevertheless, higher investments also lead to higher cost for society.
To conclude, this study gives some results that could be used by policymakers to design
grid tariffs for energy districts. If no tariff is expected to give a best solution which can
solve everything, political choices have to be made in favour of some defined target.
Flexibility is difficult to trigger at the household level, and capacity based tariffs are the
only available choice to allow investments in batteries. The opportunity presented by
CPT tariffs is offering a good case for battery use, but only when used in a NNM scheme.
This method could prove to be efficient, especially considering that no variable costs are
considered here for the network. All in all, DSO and NRA need to know precisely the
configuration of the energy district they operate on, as changes in some parameters could
lead to significantly different results.

9.2
Future work and suggestions
The model created in this study is only representing a part of a complete energy district.
To completely grasp the impact of a tariff design, the representativity should be enhanced.
For example, prospective and variable costs of the network could be added to the cost
decomposition. In the same way, solar heating panels, electric vehicles and every other
technology mature enough to be on the market could be added. Different set­up could
also be used, such as the case without any district heating, or pre­existing infrastructures.
The code is built to be easily adaptable and improvable with that respect.
Without changing the model, it would be interesting to change the data selection and use
future values, to get insights of how a tariff would behave under new circumstances. It
would definitely be interesting to know it before redesigning a network tariff.
On a more general basis, wide opportunities arise for a complexified carbon handling of
the model. Impacts of the commissioning and decommissioning, the emissions from cra­
dle to grave, feedback loops are numerous opportunities that are relevant, and probably
crucial to consider. In an era where global effects should not be overlooked anymore,
answering these questions should be a priority. Therefore, the study of the larger or more
diverse district could be studied. The interaction of different designs on different districts
at a country scale could also be an opportunity for development, presenting incredible
challenges but non­less important opportunities.
Other opportunities lie in a deeper study of the interactions between electricity and heat
network. If [12] expresses both the electrification of heat and the deployment of district
heating as two critical development, a thorough joined study should be conducted. The
deployment of heat pumps and batteries (or other electricity storage, such as flywheels or
pumped hydro) at the district heating generation level is interesting for several reasons. It
could trigger economies of scale and allow for greater flexibility potential considering the
larger fluxes that the network is handling compared to a household. The deployment of
other renewable energies at the grid level could be studied jointly.
Finally, with the announced biodiversity crash and global warming, countries like Iceland
and New Zeland start drifting away from ”economy first” policies [69], [70]. Therefore, the
question of the relevance of a purely economic optimisation seems legitimate. Models
based on the minimisation of carbon emissions could be built to give first insights at how
tariffs should be designed to target well­being over efficiency.

for more volumetric based tariffs, the peak load increasing which results is hugely different.

being installed but without storage whatsoever. With NNM tariffs, the peak load increase
is moderate compared to the previous one, because the energy sold is billed the same
way by the DSO as the energy imported. Therefore, there is a limitation to the incentives
to install solar panels.
