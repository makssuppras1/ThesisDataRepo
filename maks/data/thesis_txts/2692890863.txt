Abstract
The aim of this project was ultimately to develop a working prototype device for measuring
LPWAN performance. The coverage of four LPWAN technologies, LoRaWAN, Sigfox,
LTE-M, and NB-IoT, was examined. More specifically, the primary signal performance
indicators used for data analysis and visualisation in this project was RSRP, RSRQ, and
RSSI. Other signal performance indicators were also collected such as SNR, number of
gateways receiving the message, etc. This thesis explains and discusses the development
of a client device capable of collecting signal quality and strength data for LoRaWAN,
Sigfox, LTE-M, and NB-IoT networks. Furthermore, the implementation of such a device
is presented and the utility of the device is analysed and showcased. As a result of this
project, a working prototype is produced.
It was found that the developed client device is a useful tool for anyone wishing to deploy
a LPWAN technology in an outdoor area. The client device and the back-end developed
during this project allows one to gain a more profound understanding of the coverage of
each of the aforementioned LPWAN technologies in a given area. This enables decisions
and recommendations for specific scenarios to be made on well-founded understandings of
the technologies’ performances and capabilities.
Examining the data collected by the client device, shows that several factors can impact the
functionality of such a product, such as duty cycle limitations (LoRa, Sigfox), attachment
changes for different technologies, boot-up procedures, etc.

Introduction
The amount of Internet of Things (IoT) devices has been steadily increasing and still
continue to do so. With the emergence and evolution of IoT, and the need for these end-
devices to be connected, not only is there a need for short-range communication, but also
long-range communication. While solutions which are based on cellular communication,
such as 2G, 3G and 4G can provide a larger coverage and thus longer range.
These
technologies consume excessive amount of energy, and a number of these end-devices may
be deployed in locations with no power source and are required to operate for years.
Therefore, these IoT application requirements has led to the emergence of a new type of
wireless communication technology, which is referred to as Low Power Wide Area Network
or LPWAN for short. This technology, as the name suggests was developed to provide a
low power option, for cheap end-devices of low complexity, while still ensuring long ranges
of up to 10-50 km in rural zones, and 1-5km in urban areas while allowing for battery
lifetime of upwards of 10+ years[1].
LPWAN is essentially a new paradigm of IoT, and is still a relatively new concept. The
LPWAN technologies has shown great promise and continue to do so, and the LPWAN
market size is according to recent market research set to surpass that of $80 billion by
2027[2]. LPWAN technologies are introduced by the 3GPP, which operate in the licensed
band, but also proprietary technologies exist, which are operating in the unlicensed ISM
band. Each LPWAN technology will have its pros and cons, depending on the application
of the system one wishes to deploy. Some LPWANs are designed to allow for ultra low
device complexity, at the cost of a lower data rate, while others are designed with aspects
such as mobility or indoor coverage in mind. The demand for LPWAN devices are in-
creasing, and that goes for a number of the different technologies, such as LoRa, Sigfox,
LTE-M and NB-IoT. According to a market research from 2019, done by Techno Systems
Research, the market for major LPWAN standards, which includes the four previously
mentioned LPWANs, will grow by approximately 81% YoY. A prediction of LoRa contin-
uing to be the best selling standard was also made, however, NB-IoT shows a significant
amount of growth[3]. The market forecast is shown in Figure 1.1.
When deploying a system, which is utilising LPWAN, there are numerous factors which
should be considered. Depending on the application and the geographical location of the
deployment, different LPWAN standards should be considered, in order to get the most
optimal technology for your solution. It is however also extremely important, that at the
location where one wishes to deploy the end-devices, a satisfactory coverage and signal
quality is provided for the given technology. Aside from just ensuring that coverage is
ensured, one may also wish to determine the quality of the coverage, such that the end-
devices can operate without too many implications. Having bad coverage can not only
limit the data rates, which the end-devices can provide, but it can also cause an undesirable
increase in power consumption, as well as messages being lost in transmission. While there
are theoretical ways to attempt to asses the coverage of a given LPWAN standard, it often
proves to be very diﬀicult to provide a precise result. Often times it may be necessary to
perform coverage measurements in order to get an accurate idea of the quality of service,
at a given location.
This thesis, will look into four of the LPWAN standards, namely: LTE-M, NB-IoT, Sigfox
and LoRa. Here, LTE-M and NB-IoT are derived from LTE and of the 3GPP standard,
while LoRa and Sigfox are both proprietary solutions. Both the design of the LPWANs

Scope
The primary scope of this project is the development of a LPWAN measurement device
and creating a proof-of-concept that satisfies the goals set for the project. The results
generated by the use of the developed client device, serve as the proof for the client device
functionality, and as such, the primary focus of this thesis will be on the development,
design and implementation of the device.
The results presented and discussed in this thesis are based on measurements of the cov-
erage and network quality of LPWAN technologies. However, the data gathered by the
client device in its final state during this project, is not necessarily suﬀicient to explain the
signal qualities and strengths measured by the client. Meaning, that other supplementary
information might be necessary to explain and properly investigate the causes of the signal
behaviours. The data, however, is satisfactory in order to generate coverage maps and
measure signal qualities and strengths. As stated in the title of this project, the project
device is designed as a ’RF performance measurement device’. In this project, the signal
strength and quality was used as the performance parameter as the basis for any analysis
and comparisons. It is important to note that the project client device is a tool that can
be used to gather coverage data and signal strength / quality data for different LPWAN
technologies, but the client device itself does not compare any technologies or data.

LPWAN technologies
While different LPWAN standards/technologies are designed with certain principles in
mind, they all have a number of elements in common. In this chapter, a brief introduction
of the general goal and design principles of Low-Power Wide-Area Network will be given.
Following this, each of the four LPWAN technologies chosen, will be presented. Finally,
a summary will be given of the most relevant aspects of each technology and a look into
what parameters will be interesting to look at when determining coverage for each of the
LPWANs.
3.1
LPWAN characteristics
Low Power Wide Area Network (LPWAN) is a term for wireless technologies designed to
provide coverage in large areas with low bandwidth and offering power eﬀicient commu-
nication for the end-devices using the network. The end-devices utilizing this network are
often ultra-low-power sensor nodes and other IoT devices. The end-devices using the LP-
WAN technology will be connected to a base-station. The LPWAN technologies may use
either a licensed or unlicensed spectrum. NB-IoT and LTE-M are examples of technologies
operating in the licensed spectrum, which means they run on public cellular networks that
are using a licensed radio spectrum. LoRAWAN and Sigfox are examples of technologies
operating in the unlicensed spectrum. The unlicensed spectrum may be used by anyone
which could potentially produce interference between different networks.
Depending on the application of the LPWAN technology, there may be different trade-offs,
due to the architecture and requirements. Generally, there are 4 major characteristics and
performance parameters to LPWANs:
• Coverage
• Capacity
• Power consumption
• Cost
These 4 characteristics will typically have different priorities depending on what scenario
the LPWAN should be applied. It may be, that it is necessary for one network to be
able to reach end-devices which are far away in rural areas, and that the end-devices
should have a low power consumption, to ensure the end-devices are able to function for
an extended time. Meanwhile, other networks may require less coverage, but instead a
large amount of end-devices must be connected, and thus ensuring a high enough capacity
is of the essence. Typically, trade-offs between the 4 categories are made, and should be
taken into consideration when deploying the technology[5].
3.1.1
Coverage
Simply put, the geographical area of which the network is reachable from an end-device, is
the coverage of the network. Some LPWAN networks may need to provide indoor coverage,
while others may require end-device mobility and tracking.
3.1.2
Capacity
The capacity of a network is usually described as the amount of traﬀic the network is able
to support at a given moment. This metric is useful since networks consisting of end-
devices that rarely generate traﬀic, are able to sustain a higher number of end-devices,

without these experiencing loss of service, than a network with devices that do generate
high amounts of traﬀic. The network capacity varies from network to network and between
network technologies, since it is very dependent on operator equipment, distance between
user equipment and base stations/gateways, number of devices in network etc.
3.1.3
Power consumption
The power consumption of a LPWAN can be divided in two: the power consumption of the
network and the power consumption of the end-devices. The network draws the biggest
amount of power, since it needs to power base stations, servers, routers, etc. However, the
network is almost always connected to a power grid, ensuring adequate power at all times.
The end-device also requires power, but this is minimal compared to the network. However,
the end-device is rarely connected to any power grid, and usually operates on batteries.
This creates a demand for low power end-devices, since end-devices far away or in hard to
reach conditions might be expensive if a technician needs to change battery etc.
3.1.4
Cost
There are operator costs and end-user costs, when talking about the costs associated with
a LPWAN. The operator costs includes a multitude of elements, such as: network license(if
in licensed band), end-device development costs(in some cases), base station cost, network
running costs (electricity, maintenance, etc.)
End-users can experience several costs: price of the end-device, network services, subscrip-
tions, etc.
3.1.5
Link Budget
The link budget of a given system is used to determine how well the signals can be received.
The link budget is calculated by adding all the gains from the system and subtracting the
losses of the propagation channel. Thus, the link budget is dependent on many variables,
that might vary depending on the specific use case. A general equation for the expected
power incident of a wireless signal might be expressed as:
PRX(dBm) = PTX(dBm)+Gsys(dB)−Lsys(dB)−Lch(dB)−Mfade(dB) = Link budget[6]
(3.1)
• PRX = calculated incident power/signal strength at receiver
• PTX = transmit power
• Gsys = signal gains achieved by the system
• Lsys = signal losses introduced by the system
• Lch = anticipated signal losses introduced by the propagation channel (approxima-
tion calculated by use of models or collected data)
• Mfade = fading margin (difference between calculated received signal strength and
receiver sensitivity) [6]
It is diﬀicult to calculate the exact link budget for a scenario due to the many factors
impacting the signal loss in the propagation channel, meaning that the models and or
data used to calculate channel loss are of utmost importance in order to create a good
estimates for the link budget. Link budgets can be useful when planning a roll-out of a
given technology in an area, since it allows the service provider to plan the location of
gateways/base stations.

LoRa
It is important to note the difference between LoRa, the physical layer technology, and Lo-
RaWAN, a MAC layer protocol. LoRa as a physical transmission medium is developed by
Semtech [7], while the LoRaWAN protocol is developed and maintained by LoRa Alliance
[8].
4.1
LoRa physical layer
The LoRa physical layer is not open source, which increases the diﬀiculty of verifying the
specifications.
LoRa is used for long range, low power communication. In order to achieve this reliable,
long range, low power communication, LoRa utilises its own proprietary modulation, as
well as forward error correction (FEC). Since LoRa is a physical layer and modulation,
it should theoretically be possible to apply it in any desired frequency band, however the
available documentation only specifies the 868 MHz (EU) unlicensed band as well as the
2.4 GHz band.
Only the 868 MHz band will be discussed in this project, due to the
product of this project only applying LoRa in this frequency band. Since the 868 Mhz
band is unlicensed, a duty cycle limitation of 1% is imposed in this band.
4.1.1
LoRa modulation
The proprietary modulation that LoRa uses, is part of the reason why LoRa can commu-
nicate over such long distances with minimal power usage. The proprietary modulation
technique used in LoRa is derived from Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS) modulation. Spread
spectrum modulation uses the principle of spreading the data signal across a frequency
spectrum, increasing the robustness of the signal. Several elements are integral to LoRa
modulation, which are needed in order to understand exactly how the modulation is func-
tioning.
A chirp is a signal that is spread across frequencies over time, either increasing, called
an up-chirp, or decreasing, called a down-chirp. In the case of LoRa modulation, these
chirps are cyclically shifted and the data is encoded onto the chirps with frequency jumps.
In LoRa, the bandwidth of the chirp is equivalent to the bandwidth of the whole signal,
meaning that a chirp spans the entire allowed bandwidth.
An example of unmodulated and modulated LoRa signal can be seen in Figure 4.1, in
which an unmodulated LoRa carrier signal consisting of three up-chirps followed by one
down-chirp is seen on the left. On the right, the same signal is seen in a modulated state,
where data has been encoded onto the carrier signal.
The Spreading Factor (SF) describes the number of chips per symbol, defined as 2SF
which is also the length of the symbol. Furthermore the SF directly translates to the
number of data bits that can be encoded by a symbol, i.e. Symbol = SFbits. From this it
can be seen that by operating with a SF of 7, 2SF = 27 = 128 chips are used per symbol,
while the symbol represents 7 bits of data. This is the amount of spreading code applied
to the data signal, when transmitting. The symbol time (Ts), is described by
Ts = 2SF
BW
This shows that the symbol time and thus the transmission time (and time on air) is
directly dependent on the spreading factor as well as the bandwidth.

LoRa range
The modulation implemented in LoRa enables long range communication, Semtech adver-
tises up to 48km in rural areas[10]. Naturally the range will be impacted by the area and
obstructions e.g. urban, rural, etc. As well as the equipment specifications, e.g., antenna
configuration etc.
4.1.4
LoRa power consumption
In the physical layer of LoRa, several techniques are employed to provide high robustness
and reduce power consumption.
The LoRa nodes do not require a stable local clock, a power consuming and expensive
component. The proprietary LoRa modulation provides equivalent timing and frequency
offsets between the receiver and transmitter.
The LoRa physical layer parameters mentioned earlier, (spreading factor, code rate, band-
width), can be adjusted to prioritise either throughput, range, robustness, or energy con-
sumption [11].
4.1.5
LoRa MAC layer
The MAC layer of LoRa, commonly known as LoRaWAN, is a protocol which imple-
ments several important features that enables many more use cases for this technology.
LoRaWAN and its specification is maintained by the LoRa Alliance [12].
4.2
LoRaWAN features
The LoRaWAN network protocol enables LoRa end-devices to connect and communicate
with a LoRa enabled network with several features such as:
• Device classing for easy and aligned end-device configurations
• Security mechanisms for secure and private communication
• Power consumption control features for minimising power consumption
• Roaming, both passive and handover roaming

• End-devices, e.g., sensors connected to the gateways

• Network server, the central control server that manages the network and connects

• Join server, an entity responsible for deciding whether an end-device should be con-
nected to the network and if so, which application server it should be assigned to.

4.2.2
Device classes
The LoRaWAN protocol defines three device classes:
• Class A: Bi-directional end-devices (lowest power consumption)
• Class B: Bi-directional end-devices with scheduled receive slots
• Class C: Bi-directional end-devices with maximal receive slots (highest power con-
sumption)
Class A LoRaWAN end-devices operate by having two downlink receive windows after
an uplink transmission has been completed. This results in a low power consumption, but
at the expense of possibly very long response times. This is due to downlink messages
having to wait until an uplink transmission has occured, until they can be delivered to the
end-device. These end-devices feature the lowest power consumption, but consequently
also has the highest worst-case latency of all device classes.
Class B LoRaWAN end-devices are similar to class A end-devices, in that they share the
two downlink receive windows after an uplink transmission. Class B end-devices also have
additional receive windows, which are scheduled together with the gateway by having the
gateway send time-synchronised beacons to the end-device.
Class C LoRaWAN end-devices allow for downlink messages to be received at all times,
except when the end-device itself is transmitting. These end-devices exhibit the highest

power consumption but provide the lowest latency communication of all device classes
[15].
4.2.3
Power consumption
Due to the device classing in LoRaWAN, the power consumption can vary from end-
device to end-device. Furthermore the power consumption will be influenced by the LoRa
modulation parameters such as spreading factor, bandwidth, etc. since these directly affect
the time on air for LoRaWAN packets.
Class A LoRaWAN end-devices also work in an asynchronous fashion, with scheduled re-
ceive windows after each up-link transmission. This helps reduce power consumption, since
no synchronisation between the transmitter and receiver is necessary. Class B end-devices
do utilise synchronisation with scheduled receive windows, and class C end-devices have
almost completely continuous receive windows, only interrupted by up-link transmissions
[15].

Sigfox
Sigfox is a proprietary narrowband communications technology developed by Sigfox, a
global network operator whose networks are based on Sigfox technology. Sigfox is best
described as a LPWAN technology, which focuses primarily on long distance, low power

5.1
Sigfox physical layer
The Sigfox physical layer operates in the 868 MHz (EU) unlicensed band, similarly to
LoRa, meaning that Sigfox also has to comply with a duty cycle limitation of 1%. Sigfox’s
bandwidth is only 192 kHz, with each message only taking up 100 Hz inside this spectrum,
using what Sigfox refers to as Ultra-Narrow Band modulation. Sigfox data rates are limited
to 100 to 600 bps.

5.3
Sigfox Features
5.3.1
Device registration
Each Sigfox end-device contains a unique identifier (Device ID) and a PAC (Porting Au-
thorisation Code), which are both needed for activating an end-device on a network. The
PAC is a one-time code which changes once it has been used, it is used to prove ownership
and should be kept secret. In the Sigfox back-end, the end-device needs to be registered
and tied to a contract before use. Here, the PAC and Device ID is needed to authorise
and identify the end-device. Each end-device needs to be on an active subscription. Each
subscription has a number of tokens attached to it and each end-device consumes one
token once connected [20].
5.3.2
Random access
Due to the asynchronous nature of the transmissions from the end-devices to the base
stations, Sigfox deploys a feature called ’Random access’, which has the end-device send
its message on a random frequency. Following the original message, two duplicates are
sent on different frequencies and at different times. This increases robustness, and tries
to ensure that a base station will be listening when at least one of the messages are being
sent.
5.3.3
Cooperative reception
In order to deliver a good quality of service, Sigfox does not associate and attach end-
devices to a specific base station. Instead, messages are sent out by the end-devices and can
be picked up by all base stations within range. Coupling random access and cooperative
reception, allows Sigfox to achieve a high quality of service.
5.3.4
Sigfox messages
As mentioned, Sigfox only has a throughput of around 100 to 600 bps, which combined
with the low power requirements and duty cycle limitations, has lead to Sigfox message
sizes of at most 12 bytes. Due to the time on air for Sigfox messages being around 2
seconds and the duty cycle limitation of 1% in the 868 MHz (EU) frequency band, this
leads to end-devices being able to send 6 messages of 12 bytes per hour. Sigfox message
consists of a header, payload, MAC (message authentication code), and a CRC value.
5.3.5
Energy consumption
Due to the absence of synchronisation and pairing of end-devices to base stations, no
unnecessary energy is spent on synchronising or pairing before sending data messages.
Furthermore, Sigfox chips usually consume 10 to 50 mA during transmission.

Long-Term Evolution
Long-Term Evolution (LTE) is also known as the fourth generation mobile network (4G),
which is defined by the 3GPP standard. One of the major changes LTE brought, compared
to its predecessor (UMTS) is the adaptation to an all-Internet Protocol (IP) approach.
This means that the network almost entirely relies on an IP-based core network. This
adaptation also means that the design and implementation of the air interface etc. was
greatly simplified [22].
While Long-Term Evolution in itself is not a LPWAN technology, it facilitates the possi-
bility of deploying LPWAN technologies such as NB-IoT and LTE-M on top of the already
existing LTE infrastructure. These LPWANs rely on the infrastructure from LTE, and
thus makes it easy to deploy and scale these newer LPWANs, since most countries has
already rolled out LTE infrastructure. The following section will introduce some of the
common aspects and design principals of LTE, which both NB-IoT and LTE-M are derived
from in order to get a better understanding of these two LPWANs.
6.1
Mobile Network Architecture
Before progressing into more LTE specific topics, the general idea of a mobile network
architecture will be presented. A mobile network consists of Base Stations (BS), which
is what the User Equipment (UE), typically mobile phones, are connected to. Each base
station will cover a certain area, and what determines which UE is connected to which
base station, depends on where the phone is located and the signal quality. Generally
for mobile networks, the geographical area which a base station can support UEs within
is known as a cell. Typically a base station has 3 antennas, which translates into three
different cells. This is illustrated in Figure 6.1

The two essential requirements in a mobile network is the coverage and the capacity. The
Coverage is the geographical area, where a UE can connect to the mobile network, while
the capacity determines the ability to which the mobile network can handle traﬀic from
users. Since LTE cells are running on the same frequency, it means that coverage and
capacity are heavily linked since an increase in UE activity will also cause the cell radius
to shrink due to interference.
6.2
LTE Architecture
The basic network architecture of LTE consists of mobile devices, which in the LTE spec-
ification are reffered to as User Equipment (UE). The UEs are then connected to base
stations, which are referred to as eNode-Bs. This connection is an over the air connection,
on the air interface (Uu). eNodeBs are connected to each other over the X2 interface.
They are also connected to the Evolved Packet Core (EPC), which among other things
acts a gateway to other networks, handles mobility management for UEs etc. The Evolved-
UMTS Terrestrial Radio Access Network (E-UTRAN) is the air interface where the UEs
and eNode-Bs are operating. The LTE network architecture is illustrated in Figure 6.2

6.2.1
eNode-B
The eNode-Bs are as previously mentioned the Base Stations of the LTE network. Each
eNode-B consists of antennas, a radio module which handles modulation and demodulation
of transmitted and received signals on the Uu interface. They are also equipped with a
digital module, which can process the radio signals received over the air, and interface
these signals to the core network. The eNode-Bs are fairly autonomous, in the sense that
they are responsible for numerous aspects of the network, such as[22]:
• Ensuring Quality of Service
• Managing and scheduling air interface resources
• mobility management
• load balancing
The connection between an eNode-B and the EPC is referred to as the S1 interface. This
interface is split into 2: The User Plane and the Control Plane.
The User Plane is used by the eNode-Bs during a handover, meaning a UE is in connected
mode, e.g. in a call, and is required to change eNode-B. This ensures that no data is lost
during the call.

The Control Plane is used for control messages, such as letting the network know that it’s
still operating, by sending status messages etc. It’s also responsible for configuring logical
connections when UEs wants to communicate using the LTE network, this also includes
supplying encryption keys for data on the air interface.
Since the eNode-Bs are required to handle numerous aspects of the network, they require
a connection between them, this is referred to as the X2 interface. This interface can
also be utilized for handover, if the two eNode-Bs are connected via the X2. The X2

this is necessary because in LTE, base stations use the same carrier frequencies, and thus
around cell edges a UE may receive signals from multiple base stations.
6.2.2
Evolved Packet Core (EPC)
The Evolved Packet Core network represents the core of an LTE network.
The EPC
consists of multiple sub-components. The entities of the EPC can be seen in Figure 6.2.
Mobility Management Entity (MME)
While the eNode-Bs do autonomously handle UEs and their radio bearers, the overall

This is necessary to provide
the network with an overall database of user information, which can be accessed from
anywhere in the network as the UEs are roaming around the network. The MME is used
to authenticate users joining the LTE network, selection of gateway router to the internet,
as well as mobility management.
Serving Gateway (S-GW)
The S-GW manages user data tunnels from the eNode-Bs and the Packet Data Network

Packet Data Network Gateway (PDN-GW)
The PDN-GW is the gateway to the Internet, and is thus one of the entities ensuring
data packets coming from the UEs are forwarded to the Internet, as well as ensuring
packets from the Internet are forwarded to the S-GW. The PDN-GW is also responsible
for assigning IP addresses to the UEs. When the MME has authenticated a UE it will
poll the PDN-GW for an IP address to assign the UE.

The HSS is essentially a database of network operator’s subscriber information. Data such
as the user’s International Mobile Subscriber Identity (IMSI), the Mobile Country Code
(MCC), Mobile Network Code (MNC) is stored. Additionally, the HSS is also responsible
for information used to authenticate subscribers as well as encryption keys.

LTE-M
Long-Term Evolution Macine Type Communication or LTE-M for short is a Low-Power
Wide-Area Network standard which is aimed for Internet of Things applications. Like
other LPWANs, this technology is used for interconnecting end-devices such as sensors,
IoT devices or other industrial end-devices. LTE-M is a standards-based network, which
operates in the licensed spectrum.
The LTE-M technology is designed to facilitate a wide range of end-devices, including
low-cost IoT systems. LTE-M also ensures a power-consumption effective way of com-
municating, while having deep signal penetration capabilities to ensure coverage in even
remote places[27]. LTE-M was also designed to support more demanding features, such
as mobility support for roaming end-devices, as well as voice services etc. Comparative to
other LPWAN technologies, LTE-M provides relatively high data rates, while maintaining
a relatively low power consumption and wide coverage. The LTE end-devices which may
utilize LTE-M are categorized as Cat-M.
The network architecture is similar to that of LTE, as explained in chapter 6. LTE-M
devices are referred to as UEs or devices. These are connected to the base stations referred
to as eNode-Bs. The eNode-Bs will in turn relay the information from the UE to the EPC.
7.1
Physical layer of LTE-M
LTE-M is designed such that it relies on the already existing LTE network infrastructure,
and thus coexist with the regular 4G LTE network. This means that the uplink and down-
link transmission schemes are also similar to that of the LTE network, i.e. Single-Carrier
Frequency-Division Multiple-Access (SC-FDMA) in uplink, and Orthorgonal Frequency-
Division Multiplexing (OFDM) in downlink. Similar to what was presented in the LTE
section 6.4, Resource Blocks are also used in LTE-M for scheduling of resources to devices.
In other words, an LTE-M device and a smart phone for instance can coexist in the same
LTE cell on the same carrier, and thus have the resources be shared between the two
devices.
LTE-M devices are running on similar bandwidth as standard LTE devices, see Table 6.1
for more. LTE-M also support a large number of frequency bands, see Figure D.1.
7.1.1
Duplex modes in LTE-M
In LTE-M both Frequency-Division Duplex (FDD) and Time-Division Duplex (TDD) is
supported. FDD as well as TDD are both supported in Full-Duplex (FD) and Half-Duplex
(HD) mode.
A device which supports FD-FDD will be able to transmit and receive
simultaneously, on different frequencies on two different arriers, one carrier for uplink
the other for downlink. A HD-FDD will have to do transmissions in 1 subframe, and
reception in another subframe, in other words transmission and reception can not occur
simultaneously for this type of device. While both Half-Duplex and Full-Duplex may be
available for LTE-M, an important aspect of all LPWAN technologies is the ability to
maintain a low device complexity and device cost. Therefore the most common mode is
the Half-Duplex mode for LPWAN applications, since the implementation is simpler than
the Full-Duplex implementation [5].
7.1.2
Coverage Enhancement
One of the primary goal of LTE-M as well as other LPWANs is to have exceptional
coverage.
However, most LPWAN devices, including LTE-M devices will also have a

rather simple device complexity, which may come at costs such as only having a single-
antenna receiver as well as having lowered the maximum transmission power. For these
reasons, the device simplifications may actually lower the coverage of LTE-M devices, and
therefore, coverage enhancement techniques to improve the coverage must be implemented,
in order to get on par or above regular LTE devices, which doesn’t suffer as much from
low device complexity. Another aspect to keep in mind, is that LPWAN devices such as
LTE-M may often be deployed in places where coverage may be challenging.
To address these concerns, two CE (Coverage Enhancement) modes are introduced for
LTE-M devices: CE mode A, which is the most typical mode for LTE-M devices, which
enhances coverage beyond the normal LTE coverage and CE mode B for an even deeper
coverage, when a device is deployed in locations where coverage is very challenging.
One of the techniques used for enhancing the coverage is repetitive transmissions. More
specifically it’s called TTI bundling, which means that the UE will transmit multiple
subframes in a row, thus ”bundling” the data.
This is done in order to increase the
chances of successful data reception at the eNodeB. This technique especially improves
coverage near cell edges. Another technique is simply lowering the modulation to QPSK,
this in turn will increase the coverage but will however lower the bandwidth, since a lower
modulation means a lower spectral eﬀiciency[5].
To indicate the coverage, the Maximum Coupling Loss (MCL) is often used, which de-
scribes the limiting value of the coupling loss at which a given service can be delivered
[28]. Without the coverage enhancement, LTE systems would tolerate up to approximately
144 dB coupling loss. With the CE techniques of repetitive transmissions, lowering the
modulation, etc. an increase of the LTE-M MCL to 164 dB can be expected[29].
The two CE modes: CE mode A and CE mode B both utilize the same techniques, but
where as CE mode A can use up to 32 repetitions, CE mode B can utilize upwards of 2048
repetitions. CE mode A is as previously mentioned the default mode. CE mode B greatly
extends the coverage compared to CE mode A, but does however not provide mobility

7.3
LTE-M connected and idle mode procedures
in LTE-M, a number of the procedures that allows for devices to connect to the network or
a cell, change between cells, and more, are inherited from LTE. When a UE doesn’t have
data to send, it’s generally considered to be in idle mode. When the UE is transmitting
data or communication is happening, it’s referred to as connected mode. Next some of
these procedures both in idle mode and connected mode will be covered.
7.3.1
Cell selection
When an LTE-M device is powered on, it will like other LTE devices have to decide which
cell it should connect to. Typically there will be numerous cells to chose from, both from
different eNode-Bs but also from different operators. The procedure for cell selection can
be broken down into steps[27]:
1. The UE searches the Primary Synchronization Signal (PSS), which allows the UE
to get information about system timing and the frequency offset. This then allows
the UE to synchronize to the cell
2. Search the Secondary Synchronization Signal (SSS) in order to identify the duplex
mode, the Physical Cell ID (PCID) etc.
3. Now that the UE is synchronized, it can extract information about the downlinky
system bandiwdth and scheduling information of the LTE-M specific Signal Infor-
mation Block 1 (SIB1), by decoding the Master Information Block, containing this
information.
4. Depending on the information acquired from the SIB1, a UE can determine whether
it’s allowed to camp on the cell. A more detailed description can be found in Ap-
pendix E.
Having successfully performed the cell selection procedure, the UE is then connected to a
cell.
7.3.2
Cell reselection
As the UE moves around, the current cell that it’s connected to might not remain feasible
due to a weak signal, or perhaps another cell is more suitable. In order to always provide
the best quality of service, the UE will be evaluating the neighbouring cell’s signals. Once
the serving cell doesn’t uphold the threshold criterias, the UE will look to connect to a
different cell. The primary values determining whether a cell reselection should occur are
the RSRP and RSRQ values, which describes the received power from the serving cell,
and the serving cell power relative to neighbouring cells respectively. These concepets will
be introduced later in chapter 9. Depending on what CE modes the device supports, the
requirements of RSRP and RSRQ to perform a cell reselection will vary.
7.3.3
Paging
Paging is performed when UEs are in idle mode. When there is downlink data for a UE in
idle mode, paging is performed to re-initiate communication with the UE. When a device
is idle it will periodically monitor paging occasions in the downlink, in case communication
is attempted to be established. When a UE receives a paging transmission, it will verify if
one of the transmissions matches its identity. This device identity is typically described as
the System Architecture Evolution Temporary Mobile Subscriber Identity (S-TMSI). When
a device needs to be paged, the MME in the core network will inform the necessary eNodeB
to attempt to page the UE using the MPDCCH channel . For stationary devices the MME
can provide a Paging Coverage Enhancement level which can be useful for stationary
devices located in places with a weak coverage.
This level determines the number of

with these devices. If the device is however not statically located, the MME will not be

For end-devices it can be rather power consuming to periodically check for paging trans-

Mobility in connected mode
After the device has synchronized with the network and access has been ensured, the
device will perform a Random Access procedure. This also occurs when responding to a
paging transmission. This procedure will help facilitate scheduling for uplink transmissions
and further scheduling information. Once this procedure has been performed, the device
is connected and will go from being in idle mode to connected mode. LTE-M supports
handover, which is similar to cell reselection, however handover occurs when the device is
in connected mode. The handover allows the device to seamlessly switch to a new cell,
while staying attached to the network. This also allows the LTE-M to be rather power-

Since LTE-M supports handover, it provides an excellent mobility support which is ideal
for devices which are mobile.

8
NB-IoT
Narrowband-Internet of Things is a 3GPP standardized LPWAN technology. NB-IoT is
utilizing the licensed band. Similar to LTE-M, which is also operating on the licensed
band, both of these standardized technologies are built on the already existing Long-Term
Evolution architecture. NB-IoT was launched with the focus of providing long range, low
data rate and the possibility of ultra-low power consumption, while also ensuring wide
coverage and massive amounts of connections. Another aspect which NB-IoT attempts to
improve upon compared to other LPWAN technologies, is the lack of security. Lastly, an
importance on low device complexity was crucial, since NB-IoT aims at having low cost
devices. This is partly achieved by only having one transciever for both the downlink and
uplink transmission, as well as not supporting higher-order modulations. With the low
data rates supported by NB-IoT, a big emphasis on memory is not necessary, which also
in turn lowers the device complexity as well as the cost.
8.1
Physical layer of NB-IoT
Compared to LTE and LTE-M, the architecture of NB-IoT is very similar to these, since
it is derived from the LTE architecture.
The NB-IoT devices are referred to as UEs
or devices, which are connected to eNode-Bs, which in turn relays the data to the core
network.
The uplink and downlink transmission schemes are also similar to that of LTE and LTE-
M, which as previously mentioned means that SC-FDMA is used in the uplink direction,
while OFDM is used in the downlink. NB-IoT devices however only require support of
half-duplex, meaning a device is only required to either transmit or receive at a given time,
not simultaneously.
8.1.1
Operation modes
While a number of aspects of LTE/LTE-M and NB-IoT are fairly similar, something that
does differ NB-IoT from LTE-M and LTE is the deployment flxibility, where NB-IoT has
3 modes of operation, that is: stand-alone, in-band, and guard-band.
Stand-alone
mode can be used in a new frequency carrier, such as GSM. This in turn
can help better utilize otherwise unused resources. Essentially, GSM carriers can be re-
farmed into NB-IoT carriers, this does however require the introduction of guard-bands.
In-band
mode works by reserving a single LTE Physical Resource Block (PRB) for both
uplink and downlink
Guard-band
mode is similar to In-band mode operating within LTE carriers, however,
instead of utiizing a PRB, the LTE guard-band is utilized. The guard-band mode utilizes
the fact that only about 90% of the LTE bandwidth is used by the LTE signal, leaving
5% on each side for guard-bands. Either of these guard-bands is then used for NB-IoT
carrier. This mode is only possible in the 5 MHz bandwidth and above[29].

LTE. Meanwhile, the standard 15 kHz subcarrier spacing will however remain orthogonal

strength is limited, while the multi-tone frame is used to provide higher data rates for
devices.

modulation schemes were also introduced.

8.2.1

As in LTE-M, in order to synchronize and connect to the network, the device has to

when the device is in idle mode. For NB-IoT there are two types of cell selection: initial
cell selection and noninitial cell selection.

have to calibrate and search for a channel raster. Until release 15, this was essentially brute

to that of the one described for LTE-M in chapter 7.

the current cell has failed and thus a new cell has to be selected. At this point the device
is already synchronized to the network, so this process is much faster. The noninital cell
selection is also performed when the device wakes up from sleep.

the RSRP threshold value, also known as Narrowband RSRP (NRSRP) for NB-IoT. It
should be noted that depending on the CE level, the NRSRP threshold will differ. The
cell reselection is the main procedure to ensure idle mode mobility support.

Determining signal quality
Determining the signal quality can be done in numerous ways. A number of different
parameters may play in to what causes a good or bad quality of service for an end-
device. Depending on the LPWAN technology, the parameters to determine the quality
of the signal may differ substantially. In this section each of the LPWANs and the factors
playing into the quality of the signal will be introduced, to give a better understanding of
how determining coverage and overall signal quality can be approached.
Generally for all wireless technologies it is useful to examine the max coupling loss, which
describes the maximum power loss between the base station and the end-device antenna,
for the data to still be delivered.
9.1
LoRaWAN signal quality
For determining the signal quality of LoRa and and the coverage of LoRaWAN networks,
RSSI is used. This is the main signal quality / strength indicator that is available, along
with the SNR. Another metric included in the measurements collected in this project is
the number of gateways that received the message. It could also be relevant to look at
what modulation parameters are used for each RSSI measurement, i.e. spreading factor,
code rate, bandwidth, since modulation changes will affect the throughput and time on
air of the LoRa signal. RSSI values are the primary signal strength indicator used in
this project for LoRa signals. Determining ”good” and ”bad” RSSI values for signals is
diﬀicult, since LoRa is able to operate below the noise floor and not much information is
available on different RSSI levels for LoRa signals [35]. However, for the Nemeus module
used in the client device, the receiver sensitivity is -137 dBm[36].
This indicates that
signals with a RSSI below this threshold will not be able to be received by the Nemeus
module. It should be noted that the LoRa calculator, which can be found at [37], provides
a tool for calculating the receiver sensitivity dependent on the modulation parameters.
An example of this can be seen in Figure 9.1, where an example has been calculated with
the parameters as: 125 kHz bandwidth, spreading factor 7. In this example the receiver
sensititivity is calculated to be -123 dBm.
It is also relevant to look at the SNR, since this indicates how close the signal was to the
noise floor around it.

Since LTE-M and NB-IoT is essentially dervied from LTE, the approach to determining

reports to the eNode-B it’s connected to. These measurement reports will not only include
the current signal reception of the eNode-B it’s connected to, but also neighbouring eNode-

thus make decisions on what eNode-B should be serving the UE. Some of the parameters
included in these measurement reports sent from the UE will now be described.

extracted by the UE. More specifically some of the resource elements within the resource
blocks are Reference Signals (RS). in Figure 9.3 illustrates how the Reference Signals can

9.3.5
LTE coverage quality summary
Having described some of the parameters that are used in order to determine the overall
quality of the signal in LTE, it is important to also discuss the utilisation of these. For
LTE, the RSSI value is not necessarily great on its own, since it doesn’t take much into
consideration other than the total power received from all sources. The RSRQ is a good
indicator, since it describes the strength of the serving cell compared to all the other cells,
however, this does not necessarily imply a good connection. Even if the RSRQ value is
good, it could still be the case, that the signal strength is poor, it might just be that the
serving cell is relatively much stronger than the neighbouring cells, yet still the serving cell
signal may not be suﬀicient for a good connection. The RSRP value is thus parimarily used
to determine the coverage / signal quality, since it directly describes the power received
from the serving cell. The RSRQ does however still play a big role, and the overall quality
of the signal quality can be better described using both RSRP and RSRQ in LTE.

9.5
Radio Propagation
When a radio wave is travelling through the air from one point to another, the radio
waves will be affected by factors such as reflection, diffraction, absorption, scattering and
Doppler effect[42]. The loss that occurs between the transmitter and the receiver is referred
to as the path loss. Numerous factors may have a say in how well a signal is received,
both the antenna, the transmit power, the base-band frequency and the terrain will play
major roles. Especially for LPWAN technologies, increasing the transmit power is not a
very valid option since they are often required to operate for many years without anyone
attending to them, and are typically relying on battery power.
It can be diﬀicult to exactly predict the path loss of a system, however there are path loss
propagation models developed to help predict the path loss for a system given a set of
parameters.
9.5.1
Free Space Path Loss model
One of the most simple path loss models is the Free Space Path Loss model. This model is
a rather naive model and assumes a free space between the receiver and the transmitter.
36
Developing a RF performance measuring device for comparing LPWAN technologies
==PAGE:46==
It does however provide an idea of how these models are constructed. the FSPL model
can be expressed as:
FSPL(dB) = 20log(d) + 20log(f) + 32.44
Where d is the distance between the receiver and the transmitter in km, while f is the
frequency of the signal in MHz. The FSPL model is rather naive, but can be a good
baseline, and other models are built around the same approach[43].
9.5.2
Other path loss models
The Okumura-Hata model is a very commonly used path loss model. It is a path loss
model which incorporates 4 different environments, which are: rural areas, suburban ar-
eas, medium-small cities and large cities. The model is an empirical model, which is based
on extensive measurements in different urban and rural landscapes in Tokyo, where fre-
quencies from 200 MHz to 2GHz were tested. The different variants of the Okumura-Hata
model can give a more accurate path loss model for different environments. The Okumura-

Client device design
This project focuses mainly on the development of the device developed during the project
in order to obtain measurements of different LPWAN technologies. However, some features
beyond the client device were needed, such as; remote storage and data analysis. For this
reason the design part of this thesis is divided into the client device design, the back-end
design, and a short front-end design part.
Like most other wireless communication technologies, when deploying them, it is essential
that the location which they are placed, or intended to roam, has a suﬀicient coverage
within that area. The signal strength of each LPWAN technology may vary significantly,
depending on the geographical location, network provider as well as being indoor or out-
door.
Most LPWAN technologies already have established their own coverage heatmaps, show-
casing the quality of the signal for a number of locations. Sigfox has heatmaps of the
coverage, determined by the number of base stations which received a message in a given
area. This information is available for different operators and product class of the Sigfox
device[46]. LoRa-Alliance has also got a coverage map[47], showing which countries has
deployed Global, Public as well as Community LoRa networks. Additionally it also shows
which operators are prevalent in each country.
For NB-IoT and LTE-M individual operators will also typically have their own coverage
map. An example of this is Telia’s coverage map[48]. This map does however not contain
much information about the quality of the coverage in different locations, but merely the
fact that coverage is guaranteed to some extent.
A large number of the coverage maps available to the public does not contain a lot of
specific information about the quality of the signal for different places. They are often
only illustrated at the means of a color indication, or rather simple aspects such as number
of base stations reached. Another aspect of this is that these coverage map are typically
self-reported by the network operators etc., while this system can provide a third party
take on the measurements.
One of the goals of this project is to provide more in-depth coverage information and a
coverage map, which contains additional information about a more detailed perspective of
each LPWAN technology for different geographical locations. This may help give a more
detailed insight into the exact signal quality for numerous LPWAN technologies at once,
and in turn help make a decision on which LPWAN standard might be most feasible to
deploy for a given location.
This chapter will go into detail about the overall system and its design. The different
parts of the system will be introduced, and the design choices made will be explained.

a coverage map can be generated. The coverage map should be a heatmap, which will
make the information easily digestible as well as easily allowing end users to quickly scan

10.2.1
STM32 design
The STM32 will essentially act as a master, while the rest of the modules will act as slaves,
awaiting commands from the STM32. This means that the overall program flow of the
client system is orchestrated by the STM32. Before moving on to the design of the STM32
program, a list of requirements for the STM32 has been compiled:
1. Poll modules for coverage and location data
2. Control flow of communication in the client system
3. Aggregate coverage data
4. Pass coverage data to ESP for storage
These are the overall requirements that the STM32 program design must achieve for the
system to be coherent.
In order to ensure these requirements, the STM32 will have to send various commands
to for instance ensure that the modules are connected to the correct LPWAN technology
for measurements. Once a module is successfully connected or attached to the desired
LPWAN technology, the STM32 will have to poll the module for LPWAN coverage mea-
surements. Once this process is finished, and the STM32 has successfully polled the data
necessary, it will need to poll the GPS module to have a coordinate which will match
the signal quality data measurements that it just polled. Once a GPS coordinate and
corresponding signal/coverage quality measurements are collected, a coverage data point
is thus generated. Once a coverage data point has been collected, it will be transmitted
to the ESP in order to be stored.
This is essentially the process that will occur for each of the LPWAN technologies. In
Figure 10.3 a state diagram is illustrated with the general idea of the program flow in the
STM32. Here it can be seen that upon the start of the STM32, the peripheral modules
will be initialised such that they are ready for transmission, and then one by one connect
to each LPWAN and poll measurements, while pairing these measurements with GPS
location data, such that a coverage data point can be made. Practically, the STM32 will
Developing a RF performance measuring device for comparing LPWAN technologies

==PAGE:53==
first send commands to the SARA module (on the SODAQ), which will connect to LTE-
M. Once that connection is established, the STM32 will send commands to collect signal
quality data. Having received the signal quality data, a GPS coordinate will be polled
and the coverage data point is aggregated and sent to the ESP for storage. This process
will occur for the three remaining LPWAN technologies. Once all four LPWAN’s coverage
data has been polled, as well as transmitted to the ESP for storage, the STM32 will loop
back and start polling each LPWAN again, one by one.
Initially, the frequency at which coverage data should be collected was considered.
A
threshold value could be introduced, for when measurements should be made. The two
options considered were a timer, such that a measurement for each technology should
happen every x amount of time. The other option was to have measurements triggered
once a certain distance had been travelled. However, since the two modules (SARA &
Nemeus) each support two LPWAN technologies, it means that each module have to
switch between the two LPWAN technologies which in itself is time consuming. Thus, a
more straight forward, sequential approach of simply performing measurements for one
LPWAN technology, then connecting to the next LPWAN technology to perform the next
measurements, etc., was ultimately chosen.

The STM32, more specifically, the STM32F103C8T6, also referred to as the blue pill, is a

to assist with advanced C/C++ development with peripheral configuration, as well as
providing debugging features. It’s specifically designed for the family of STM32 micro-
controllers.
Ontop of the STM32 CubeIDE, the HAL driver API is used to provide some higher-level

Having implemented the flow of the overall system in the STM32, a revolution of the main
loop, i.e. having performed coverage measurements of all four LPWAN technologies takes
roughly 40 seconds on average. In other words a coverage data point is being generated
every 10 seconds. Walking with an average speed of 5 km/h, this means that each LPWAN
technology will have a data point for roughly every ~55 meters, and each coverage data
point being generated every ~13 meters.
Sigfox Limitations
Since the Nemeus module doesn’t actually provide any AT commands which provide mean-
ingful information about the signal quality of Sigfox, another measure had to be taken.
The only real possibility is to extract the information from the Sigfox Backend, where each
message is stored with its own signal quality information. This means that in order to get
any meaningful coverage data from Sigfox, it will have to be extracted from the Sigfox
Backend. The information stored in the backend does, however, not have any information
about the geographical location of where the message was transmitted from, and thus, a
coverage data point can not be extrapolated. In order to combat this, a payload contain-
ing two parts is sent with every message: a session ID, which is randomly generated at
the start of the device bootup, and a sequence number which iterates with every message
transmitted. The ”coverage data” that is sent to be stored is a GPS coordinate with the
matching payload (session ID and sequence number). This then makes it possible to match
the signal quality from the sigfox backend with the coordinate stored in the database, thus
making it possible to derive coverage data points for Sigfox.
The duty cycle limitation
is another limitation of Sigfox. According to [56] a maxi-
mum of 140 messages can be transmitted per day, which corresponds to roughly a message
every 10 minutes. This greatly limits the possibility of providing coverage data for Sigfox.
The way in which the Nemeus Sigfox module handles the duty cycle timer is in the RAM,
which is volatile memory. In other words a simple power cycle of the device will reset the
timer, allowing for messages to be transmitted more often. This work-around of power
cycling the Nemeus module was implemented and tested to illustrate that it is indeed pos-
sible to perform coverage measurements for Sigfox like the other technologies. In practice,
this workaround is however not ideal, as it goes against some of the regulations for public
radio frequencies, also known as ISM bands.
It should be noted, that the duty cycle limitations affect all devices operating in the 868
MHz range, which means that LoRa is also affected by the duty cycle limitation. However,
sending link check requests does not appear to count as a ”message” per say. This means
that Link Check Requests can be transmitted continuously without having to reboot the
device to reset the duty cycle limitation. It is not immediately obvious to the authors,
why the Link Check Requests, seem to be unaffected by the duty cycle limitations, and the

In order to analyse and visualise the collected measurements in a meaningful way, the data
is needed to be loaded onto the machine doing the analysis, This can be done by utilising
the API ’getData’ function and saving response data locally. Python was used preparing
and analysing the data, as well as generating the coverage maps.

important ones. Note that a Google maps API key is necessary for using the gmap Bokeh
functionality. This provides an interactive map, such that users can move around the map
to examine the coverage points.
The procedure of generating coverage maps for each of the four LPWAN technologies can
be generalised to the following:
1. Load LPWAN data pandas dataframe
2. Convert values if necessary (e.g. RSRP, RSRQ, RSSI to dB and dBm)
3. Create empty map for the given area and setup map functions (e.g. hover to display
details)
4. Add data from dataframe to map
Due to the Sigfox data being held in both the project back-end and the Sigfox back-end,
combining these two datasets is necessary, in order to generate a meaningful coverage
map for Sigfox. This is done by loading two CSV files into two different dataframes, then
combining the wanted columns, by using the sequence number and session ID generated
by the client device.

Result generation and collection
Having designed and implemented the client device, as well as the back-end API, the
system was tested by performing measurements while walking. The results are generated
continuously as the client device is running, and are stored on the SD-card. When needed,
the client device is powered up again and the measurements are sent to the back-end,
which inserts them into the database. It is then possible to extract the measurements via
the API function ’getData’ for data analysis.
For the analysis of the results, the measurements are pulled from the database, and cleaned
up. This entails removing invalid measurements, e.g., where coordinates are erroneous,
measurements with 0-values, etc. Due to the signal quality data and other meta-data
being stored in the Sigfox back-end, the Sigfox meta-data is downloaded as a CSV file
from the Sigfox back-end and combined in the data analysis process together with the
Sigfox data from the project back-end. Before analysing the data, the values for RSSI,
RSRQ, and RSRP from the SARA module for LTE-M and NB-IoT are converted to dB
and dBm, as per the conversion charts found in [51].
For all technologies except Sigfox, the signal measurements obtained in this project are in
the downlink direction meaning that it is the module (SARA R410, Nemeus MM002) on
the client device, which calculates the signal strength from the received signal. For Sigfox
the signal measurements are collected in the uplink direction.
The specification for the Nemeus module does not specify which direction the LoRa signal
measurement belongs to (uplink/downlink). However, Figure 17.1 shows the RSSI of the
link check reqeust which is -109 dBm, while Figure 17.2 shows the RSSI in the Cibicom
back-end for the link check request to be -100 dBm. Since these two values differ it is
found that the RSSI from the link check request is measured by the module and is thus
in the downlink direction.

Example use case
The use case for a client device like the one developed during this project is primarily
for businesses that would want to deploy a LPWAN network in some area. The client
device could then be used to test the four different LPWAN technologies and be able
to collect data that could be utilised by said business in order to generate a coverage
map for each technology. This would help the business evaluate the technologies from a
more advanced and better informed perspective. A number of other factors such as cost,
throughput, battery life, etc. would also need to be taken into account before making a
clear recommendation for a specific case.
22.1
DTU Lyngby Campus
Imagining a scenario where a LPWAN should be deployed at DTU Lyngby Campus, this
client device along with the measurements included in this thesis could be used to make a
recommendation. It is necessary to state that all measurements were collected outside and
not indoors, meaning that no recommendations can be made for a LPWAN technology to
use in indoor or indoor/outdoor scenarios.
Looking at the four different coverage maps for each technology in Figure 18.3, Figure 19.1,
Figure 20.1, and Figure 21.1, it’s clear that for the most part all four LPWANs have cover-
age. As to the extent of the quality of the coverage, LTE-M appears to be very consistent.
If discarding the ”faulty” NB-IoT measurements, the NB-IoT appears to receive a very
strong RSRP value in most locations measured. Slightly stronger than that of LTE-M.
If the ”faulty” measurements in NB-IoT are not discarded, it does however seem very
inconsistent, while LTE-M has very consistent measurements.
The LoRa measurements were fairly consistent, some measurements were missing but
overall the RSSI measurements were fairly good. The lack of Sigfox measurements make
it hard to determine the coverage. For some of the locations the coverage was measured,
the quality did however appear to be good. Perhaps if the measurements are taken while
not moving this would be very different.
As such a deployment of LTE-M devices in this area could definitely be recommended. The
reasoning behind this, is that LTE-M shows to provide the most consistent coverage in the
areas covered in Figure 20.1. However, as previously mentioned, such a recommendation
should definitely be made on the basis of a specific use case with clear specifications such
as; needed throughput, number of total devices, budget, etc.
In the end of the day it can however be diﬀicult to exactly say which technology would
be the ”best”, as comparison can be diﬀicult. The results may be better for determining
which LPWANs are a possibility and which are not.
The amount of measurements collected for this project is not necessarily a satisfactory
amount of measurements to base any recommendations on, and thus it would be greatly
beneficial to perform more measurement collection. This would increase the quality of the
coverage maps and help immensely in providing the best recommendation.

23
Discussion
23.1
NB-IoT inaccuracy
Certain NB-IoT measurements were showing RSRQ values of 255, which is the SARA
module’s way of saying that the RSRQ value is ”not known or not detectable”. The exact
reason for this is unkown, but it’s suspected that despite the device stating that NB-IoT is
connected, the connection is not fully made and thus the RSRP measurements are perhaps
not from the serving cell. And if a proper connection has not been made to a serving cell,
the RSRQ value will be impossible to measure as it is the relative received power from the
serving cell to neighbouring cells. It is also possible that NB-IoT simply has some holes in
the coverage where measurements were done, but this is fairly unlikely given the amount
of base stations and how well the closely related LTE-M performed. A solution to help
combat this would be to give the SARA module additional time to connect to NB-IoT
before performing the measurements. Or perhaps keep polling for NB-IoT signal quality
data until the RSRQ value is viable.
23.2
Comparison of LPWANs
Having gathered the coverage measurement results, one can not necessarily compare the
results of different LPWANs directly. This is due to some values not being suited for direct
comparison, i.e. RSRP for LTE-M and RSSI for LoRa. The results from the client device
should thus more be seen as whether a LPWAN technology is adequate for a location given
the coverage quality. In case of more than one LPWAN technology providing adequate
coverage at a given location or area, the decision on which technology to deploy, should be
made on the basis of other aspects and features such as data rates, power consumption,
etc.
23.3
Limitations
23.3.1
Sigfox limitations
As was discussed in chapter 14, there are a number of limitations to the Nemeus module
/ Sigfox technology. One of the issues is the limitation of the signal quality data that
can be retrieved directly from the module. This means that one has to extract the data
from the Sigfox Backend to determine the coverage. This was shown to be possible by
ensuring that the payload contained an ID, such that the messages can be uniquely defined,
allowing for signal quality in the Sigfox backend, to be combined with a GPS coordinate in
the database. Nemeus/Sigfox doesn’t support a command such as Link Check Request
like in LoRaWAN, or other techniques to directly read signal quality. Because of this, a
payload has to be transmitted, and that causes the issue of easily hitting the duty cycle
limitation, which means that at maximum 6 messages can be transmitted an hour, which
in other words are 6 Sigfox measurements an hour. This is extremely limited compared to
the three other LPWAN measurements, which for every hour will approximately perform
100 coverage measurements an hour. This ultimately leads to a very ”patchy” coverage
map for Sigfox. While it was shown possible to simply power cycle the Nemeus module to
circumvent the duty cycle limitation, it is not an ideal solution as it is against the ISM band
regulations. Ultimately, both a version with Sigfox and without Sigfox was implemented.
An alternative solution could be to add a simple button to the device, which could decide
if Sigfox measurements should be performed, and then settle for at most 6 measurements
an hour.

23.3.2
GPS limitations (no indoor signal)
One of the selling points of some LPWANs is their ability to provide coverage in not only
remote locations, but also in indoor locations as well as basements or underground. Like
many GPS modules, the EVA-8M struggles getting a GPS signal when indoor or under-
ground. This limits the device when it comes to indoor coverage since a GPS coordinate
is not guaranteed. The signal quality will however still be measured (given there’s still
coverage of course), but no GPS coordinate will be attached to the measurement.
23.4
Explored frameworks/setups
A significant amount of time was spent, both utilizing different frameworks such as Li-
bOpenCM3.
Different modules were also tested, such as the stand-alone SARA-R412
module and other versions of the STM32 family. There did however prove to be some
incoherence in how some of these STM32s and boards communicated over the UART.
Much more thorough description about the issues experienced are dailted in Appendix G.
For the STM32 implementation, the UART polling mode was used for implementation.
This mode causes the device to be in ”blocking” mode, which means the device is waiting
for UART reception.
Another possibility would be to use the interrupt mode, which
simply starts a thread for a callback method, which is called once UART data is received.
This means that the main program can continue to run. This could potentially allow for
more eﬀicient measuring, since it would be possible to start LTE-M attachment, perform
LoRaWAN measurements, and then when LTE-M is ready the program would perform the
LTE-M measurements and so on. This means that the client device wouldn’t have as much
”downtime” waiting for modules to attach to networks etc. One would however have to
implement this very carefully, especially considering the SODAQ device both have the GPS
module and the SARA module, meaning that these devices share the UART connection
to STM2, and the callback function would have to differ between these messages.
Ultimately, the HAL library proved to be very strong driver in combination with the
STM32.
23.5
Device and technology pairing
Having only two modules to perform LPWAN measurements for four LPWAN technologies
may lower the complexity of the device, but it also means a significant increase in the
amount of time the modules have to connect to the network, which is fairly time consuming.
Having a device for each LPWAN technology could greatly increase the frequency of
signal measurements being done.
This also means that triggers for when to perform
measurements could be implemented, such as a timer or when a certain distance has been
travelled.

24
Conclusion
The goals set for the client device developed during this project were; collect signal quality
data for the four LPWAN technologies chosen, collect GPS coordinates for each signal
quality measurement, locally store coverage data, wireless transfer of coverage data to
remote storage.
These goals were all met by the client device in its final state, as described in this thesis.
The resulting client device developed during this project is a working prototype that fulfills
its goals and requirements. The client device achieves the goal of collecting signal quality
data by sequentially polling both the Nemeus module and the SODAQ / SARA module, in
order to receive measurements for each of the four LPWAN technologies surveyed in this
project. Furthermore, the SODAQ module is polled in order to retrieve GPS coordinates
for each signal measurement, thus satisfying the goal of localising each measurement.
Finally, the ESP8266 is used to both store the measurements locally on an SD card and
wirelessly transfer these to a remote database, hosted in the Google cloud environment.
Furthermore, goals were set for the project in general; enable remote access to stored
coverage data, visualise the coverage data and present the results. The cloud back-end
solution implemented in this project satisfies the goal of enabling remote access to the
data stored in the MySQL database. Furthermore, the data was analysed and from this
several coverage maps were generated and presented in Part V.
The produced client device enables for easy signal measurement collection for the four
LPWAN technologies; LoRaWAN, Sigfox, LTE-M, and NB-IoT. Furthermore, it automat-
ically uploads the measurements to a cloud-hosted database. The API enables both for
the client device to insert data into the database, as well as allowing for easy extraction of
coverage data from the database. This makes it possible for users/customers to integrate
the coverage data into their own products and solutions.
The various relevant signal quality parameters for the four LPWANs were also analysed
and discussed. From this, the primary signal performance indicators were chosen, which
was what the heatmap primarily showed.
The heatmap was designed to help give a
better overview of the coverage. Here the primary value chosen for LTE-M and NB-IoT
was RSRP, while for LoRa and Sigfox the RSSI was chosen. The heatmap also contains
additional signal quality parameters.
While Sigfox did prove to have some limitations when it comes to polling signal quality
data for coverage measurements, a workaround was designed and implemented. Alterna-
tive solutions for gathering Sigfox coverage data that obeyed the duty cycle limitations
were also discussed. It should be noted that a final client device or product should of
course comply with the duty cycle limitations imposed on the frequency bands in which
it operates.
The LoRa signal quality measurements collected by the client device were verified using
the Adeunis field tester device, by comparing the RSSI values of these two devices and
ensuring the values were in the same range.

Future works and improvements
There are numerous areas of improvement for the client device, which,could be interesting
to explore and implement.
A number of these have been selected to be outlined and
discussed.
25.1
Improvements for entire system
There are a few improvements to the whole system, which would produce even better
results. These improvements are primarily focused on the type of data that is collected.
The signal quality data collected in this project could be well-supplemented with:
Throughput data
for each technology, implemented by sending messages at a specific
location and measuring the throughput (bits/sec) that can be achieved at each unique
location.
This poses a few challenges and would require drastic changes to the whole
system, but primarily in the client device itself. One of the challenges of implementing
such a feature would be the increased time usage per location, which is expected to increase
drastically if measuring the throughput for each technology.
Power usage data
is another feature that might be useful would be to measure the
power usage for each technology per location, since this could be an important metric
when choosing what LPWAN technology to deploy.
Modulation configuration parameters
would be great supplement to the data, since
in the current implementation, technology specific modulation configurations such as LoRa
spreading factor, bandwidth, LTE-M / NB-IoT CE mode, etc. are not available or stored
in the back-end. This should be prioritised if the project is to be developed further, as this
would enable deeper data analysis that could increase the understanding of the coverage
of each technology. It would hereby help highlight technology specific advantages and
disadvantages.
25.2
Client device and peripheral improvements
For the client device and peripheral modules, some improvements can be made to both
optimise the overall client device performance. These improvements can be divided into
categories, based on the technology or board that they belong to.
25.2.1
STM32 "blue pill" improvements
An obvious place to start improving and optimising is the place where the most of the
computation takes place and where the majority of the developed code is running. With
the STM32 being the heart of the client device, it links together the most peripherals,
does most of the communication and is responsible for keeping track of measurements etc.
A list of the improvements that would be prioritised given more time:
• Delay optimisation
• Additional tasks and threading with FreeRTOS
These two areas of improvement are closely related and it is expected that using multiple
FreeRTOS tasks with would help improve the amount of delay currently in use in the
102
Developing a RF performance measuring device for comparing LPWAN technologies
==PAGE:112==
STM32. The FreeRTOS library makes it possible to create so-called ’tasks’, which are a
way of implementing a form of threads in micro-controllers.
Currently, when the STM32 issues a command to a peripheral device, it will wait (delay)
until an answered has been received from the queried peripheral device. Utilizing multiple
tasks could mean that while the STM32 is waiting for an LPWAN module to respond/-
connect to the network, another task could be running, performing measurements for the
other LPWAN module. This could increase the frequency of coverage measurements being
computed. Since both the GPS and SARA module are mounted on the SODAQ board,
semaphores might be necessary to implement for this critical region, as the UART mes-
sages received in the STM32 from the SODAQ board may be mishandled. This approach
could also allow for different peripheral devices to be queried at different rates, e.g., devices
with duty cycle limitations would be queried at a lower rate than others.
Another approach could be to use the UART interrupt mode instead of the polling mode,
allowing the main program to continue, until a UART message is received. As was also
previously briefly discussed.
25.3
Back-end improvements
The back-end developed in this project have some improvements that could be further
improved upon:
• Trigger for filling clean tables. Currently the clean tables in the database are filled by
manually executing queries, this would have to be automated by using sql triggers.
• Automatic conversion of RSRP, RSRQ, and RSSI values received from the SARA
R410 module.
• Using Sigfox back-end API to incorporate Sigfox signal data in own database
Data aggregation
Since numerous measurements might be performed for the same technology, for the same
coordinates, it might also be necessary to aggregate this data. In other words, multiple
measurements with the same coordinates and LPWAN technology can be aggregated,
and an average for that location’s data can be computed. This can in turn provide the
end user with a more clear idea of what the quality at a coordinate may be. Currently
the GPS device gives value to the sixth decimal, which is a precision of roughly 10 cm.
These coordinates could be rounded down to 4 or 5 decimals, which still would provide a
reasonable precision, and several measurements could then be aggregated and an average
for the signal quality parameters could be made.
25.4
Front-end development / improvements
Since there is currently no developed front-end/UI for the client device, this would be a
welcome addition, which would make the project a more complete package, rather than
a developer-oriented implementation device. Currently the back-end offers an API for
integration and collecting data to local machines/applications. This allows for developers
or potential customers to easily integrate the client device into existing solutions. However,
no presentation platform e.g. app, webpage, etc. has been developed.
As mentioned in chapter 12, a front-end design has been made, seen in Figure 12.1.
However, it has not been implemented as a functional part of the complete system. Since
the backend implementation already provides an API for data to easily be extracted, the
only missing link is the user interface. This would however increase the ease of use for
end-users.

contains is the cell barring and cell r eservation information. Lastly the RSRP and RSRQ
values are also used to determine if a cell is suitable for the UE.

G
LibOpenCm3 and alternative modules
implementation attempt
Utilizing the open-source library LibOpenCm3 was the initial plan. It seemed like a fairly
versatile library which supported a large amount of devices, including the STM family.
Initially it appeared to be working as intended. UART connections were set up between
the STM32 and ESP8266, which worked without issues. The STM32 was also successfully
connected to the NEMEUS module and the Teseo-liv3f module, without encountering
any major issues. When attempting to connect the SARA-R412 module, however, there
appeared to be a number of issues. These issues included missing certain bytes of data and
the overall flow of the program being stuck while waiting for UART data to be received.
The first hurdle when connecting the STM32 to the SARA-R412 module, was getting
the power cycle right. The timing of it appeared to be slightly different from what the
documentation stated.
The next issue was receiving a message after transmitting an AT command.
Here it
appeared that the first couple of messages received from the SARA-R412 was received
correctly and relayed on to the ESP. However, after a couple of these messages being
relayed, an issue occurred which would either cause:
A complete hault in the program after a couple of messages received correctly from the
SARA-R412 module.
The other case was that after a couple of messages received correctly, there would appear
to never really be received anything substantial on the STM32. Different USART flags
were looked into, in order to determine if there was actually data ready to be received
in the UART buffer. Even though the program would continuously poll the SARA for
a response, there still seemed to be issues. We could see that the AT command from
the STM32 was being transmitted correctly, and that a response message was correctly
sent to the STM32 (checked via TTL). However in the program when investigating the
LibOpenCm3 flags such as USART_SR_RXNE, which is a flag indicating whether the receive
data register is empty. According to https://github.com/libopencm3/libopencm3/issues/918
the RXNE flag was never set once Overrun errors occured on the UART port. This lead to
an investigation of checking whether overrun errors would occur, and the program would
indeed experience Overrun errors occasionally. As a way to try and fix this issue the RXNE
flag as well as the ORE flag (overrun error flag) was checked, i.e. only reading from the
RX buffer if USART_SR_RXNE && USART_SR_OR was true. This did however not seem to
solve the issue. Furthermore, since the program experienced overrun errors, other erros
were also tested. It appeared that certain Framing Errors also were occuring occasionally
when attempting to receive data over UART from the SARA-R412.
Thorough testing with both the HAL library utilizing the CubeIDE, as well as STduino it
appears that the USART connection was still not optimal. Rather frequently (2 correctly
received, 2 corrupted messages, 2 correctly received, etc.), the received message from the
SARA-R412 appeared to be corrupted or entirely missing. The main difference seems
to be that the Arduino and HAL library handles this rather well, and without further
modification to the code / setup, the programs didn’t come to a hault or get stuck in a
state where nothing was ever correctly received from the SARA-R412. While the USART
connection to SARA-R412 worked rather well with both Arduino and HAL, LibOpenCm3
Developing a RF performance measuring device for comparing LPWAN technologies
121
==PAGE:131==
had similar issue, the main difference was that once the first corrupted message was re-
ceived, a correctly received message was never received again. This might be because some
extra steps or modification has to be taken in the LibOpenCm3 implementation. Perhaps
a certain flag has to be set again or similar. It was however not resolved, even though
multiple attemps were made to clear/set flags that may have been of concern.
A git-issue was opened on the LibOpenCm3 github, explaining the issue, however at
the time of writing no response or clarification on the issue was received. Additionally, a
stackoverflow post was also posted and a few answers came in, which were all implemented
and tested, but caused no progress or clarification on the issue, rather the contrary.
Additionally an attempt of getting the STM32 to receive data properly from the SARA-
R412 was proposed, where multiple FreeRTOS tasks were created, each handling either
transmitting or receiving to the ESP/SARA. These tasks would utilize 4 FreeRTOS queues.
1 FreeRTOS queue for ESP tx, 1 for ESP rx, and the same goes for 2 FreeRTOS queues
for the SARA module.
The idea was to store the data in a different way, and avoid
immediately transmitting data byte for byte once a message was received on the UART
port. This however, didn’t change the outcome and we experienced similar issues with
this approach.
Having tested numerous differen’t solutions and approaches to this issue, we were never
able to exactly pinpoint the problem.
It is however certain, that the SARA correctly
receives the AT command from the STM, and correctly sends a response back to the STM.
However, once this message is received by the STM, the way we handled the message was
the problem. What exactly caused this problem is uncertain, but the current blief is,
that the buffer might overflow or experience some other error. Flushing the buffer with
the libraries we were using for this approach seemed like quite the challenge, and would
require some ”bare-metal” programming.
Instead a different approach of using the HAL library in combination with the FreeRTOS
was taken. Essentially the code that was written for using the LibOpenCm3 was simply
emulated in the HAL library combined with the CubeIDE appeared to be working more
or less flawlessly for SARA as well as the Nemeus. The Teseo GPS module did however
prove to cause some issues, and an alternate GPS module was used, which seemed to work
right away.
Code examples of some of these attempts can be found in [53].
