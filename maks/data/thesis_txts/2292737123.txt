Abstract
The aim of this thesis, is to investigate the application of a built-in webcam on a Remotely Operated
underwater Vehicle (ROV), in order to localize a given two dimensional (2D) feature. From the obtained
information extracted from the captured images, the orientation and position of the ROV relative to the
feature, can be determined based on the position and the size of the 2D feature in the image.
Further investigations are made of which 2D feature is suited for the task. The 2D feature must have a
such a geometry, that the prediction of the ROVs orientation and position relative to the feature becomes as
easy as possible, and with as little computational effort as possible.
The 2D feature is representing a Docking Station (DS), where the ROV could for example recharge
its batteries and/or receive new missions. The DS is a simple structure composed of one 2D feature for
localization purposes and a docking mechanism. The docking mechanism is a connector composed of a
electromagnet and a couple of metal plates. There are two of these connectors, one placed at the DS and one
on connected to the ROVs chassis. As the ROV approaches the DS, the two electromagnets will attract the
metal plates on the opposite connector and the ROV will dock at the DS.
When the 2D feature is localized the orientation and position of the ROV can be determined. With this
information, the position of the ROV is used for navigation and controllers are designed to locomote the
ROV from its starting point to the DS.
The controllers are designed from the physical model of the ROV, where the physical model is found
experimentally, by ﬁnding the force of the thrusters and the frictional drag opposing the ROV as it moves
through the water.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter will give an overview of the motivation and background for this thesis. An introduction to
underwater robotics with the focus on underwater robotics activities. A description of the SIRI oil platform
is presented and the problems that will be considered in this thesis are formulated. Furthermore a project
vision is given on what the future goal is. Finally, the scope and limitations of this thesis are outlined.
1.1
Motivation and Background
As the offshore underwater installations for oil-rigs, oil-storage, various pipelines, wind-turbines, com-
munication, etc. only keep on growing in numbers and complexity, so does the demand for maintenance
and hence the need for inspection. To ensure these installations are fully operational and to protect the
environment, regular inspections of these installations are needed.
These inspections usually require huge amount of resources, in form of on-site ships, manpower, various
experts in various ﬁelds, Unmanned Underwater Vehicles (UUVs), etc. These tasks can vary from days to
several weeks, and these inspections can be planned and executed on regular bases, or launched after for
example a bad storm in a given area. The favourable outcome of these inspections are that nothing abnormal
is found, because these inspections can unfortunately become a crucial factor in which determines if a given
project should continue its operation or not.
Inspection of underwater installations are usually made by divers or UUVs, depending on the environment,
temperature, depth, etc. where these inspections are to be made.
There are two main subcategories to the UUV, which are the Remotely Operated underwater Vehicles
(ROVs) and the Autonomous Underwater Vehicles (AUVs).
The advantages concerning the ROV, is that it is a tethered vehicle which links a human operator with
the ROV, and therefore the operator can control the ROV and get data feedback in real-time. This allows for
solving complicated tasks with the ROV, and if the ROV also is powered via the tethered cable, it can stay in
water indeﬁnitely. The disadvantage concerning the ROV, is that it easily can get entangled with obstacles in
the environment, and if it should venture into some underwater structure, it must get out, the same way it got
in.
The advantages concerning the AUV, is that it can be programmed in advance and does not need any
supervision by a human operator, in order to perform the preprogrammed task. It has no tethered connection
and can move freely in and around any obstacles in any environment. The disadvantages are that all
information collected by the AUV must be stored within the AUV, and no real-time feedback or control is

possible. The AUV must also carry its own power in form of batteries, which limits the time it can perform a
given task and be on the ﬂy.
The use of ROVs and AUVs in various underwater activities such as mapping of the seaﬂoor, inspection
of underwater installations or exploring underwater caves and coral reefs, depends on the assignment and
which of the two are best suited for the task. Complicated task are usually requiring a human operator, since
there could be unforeseen circumstances that need human intervention, and hence the ROV will be used. For
mapping, continuous inspection and collecting various data that can be preprogrammed, the AUV will be
used.
In order to optimize the inspection of these underwater installations, to insure that they are fully operational
at all time and avoiding any environmental catastrophes, these inspections should be continuous and
autonomous.
1.1.1
Siri Platform
The Siri Platform is an oil-rig located in the North Sea, operated by DONG Energy. The Siri platform is
a part of the oil ﬁeld called The Siri Centre, which is composed of four oil-rigs: Siri, Nini A, Nini B and
Cecilie. The Siri Platform includes an oil-storage tank located underneath the platform at the seabed, and all

1.2
Problem statement
For a permanent underwater multi-robot inspection system to be operational, its essential for these robots,
that there is a robust and easy way to localize and dock at a Docking Station (DS), where the robots can
recharge their batteries, send inspection data to the home ofﬁce to be processed and/or to receive data about

When a robot is running low on batteries, it must return back to base and recharge its batteries at some
DS. The DS must be localized by some built-in censors that all robots have, or at least some of the robots,

all the robots can be docked at the same DS and/or docked with any other robot in the system. The DS must
also be equipped with some recognizable feature, that the transport robots are able to localize and hence

The ROVs three brushless DC motors are equipped with ﬁxed pitch propellers. Two of the three motors
physical conﬁguration is such that they have the same (x, z)-value and the same orientation, with the two
torques pointing each in the opposite x-direction, with opposite pitched propellers. The two motors are
rotating in opposite directions, to counteract the induced moments of inertia. This allows the ROV to Surge,
or move forward and backward in its x-direction. This conﬁguration also allows the ROV to Yaw, or rotate
around its z-axis, turning to starboard and port. The third motor is placed approximately 1cm away from the
rotational z-axis in the x-direction of the other two motors, with the torque pointing in the ROVs z-axis. This
allows the ROV to Heave, or move up and down in the z-direction. The induced torque by this motor leads
to rotational acceleration of the ROV around its z-axis and must be counteracted using the other two motors.
With this physical conﬁguration of the brushless DC motors the ROV has 3 degrees of freedom, Surge,
Yaw and Heave.

3.5
Hough Transform
The Hough Transform Algorithm (HTA)(Horn 1986) is a feature recognition algorithm. The HTA was
originally designed to ﬁnd lines in images, but has been extended to also ﬁnd arbitrary functions and shapes
in images. I order to ﬁnd a line in an image, the image must ﬁrst be processed with an edge detection
algorithm, for example the CEDA.
Figure 3.5: The ﬁgures represents an edge of a inﬁnite thin line, detected with the CEDA.
The HTA is searching through the processed CEDA image, if a pixel is found that has a higher value than
zero then it represents a part of an edge. An edge can be a part of any arbitrary function or shape in the
image, but in this case the HTA are searching for a straight line. When a non-zero pixel is found the HTA
assumes that this pixel could be a part of a straight line, but this line can have an inﬁnite possible orientations,
in digital images this orientation will usually be between −89o to 90o in a certain resolution. Instead of
representing these speciﬁc lines, they are represented as vectors from the origin orthogonal onto these lines.
The vectors are in polar form, given with the length ρ from the origin orthogonal onto these lines, and θ as
the angle between the x-axis and the vector, where the angle is positive in the Clock Wise (CW) direction
and negative in the Counter Clock Wise (CCW) direction. The HTA will calculate ρ for all possible angles
from −89o to 90o for each non-zero pixel value found by:

5.2
Testing the manoeuvrability and docking with the designed connectors
The designed connectors for the DS and the ROV are made out of plastic, and there are four metal plates
equally distributed around the connectors, as shown on Fig.5.3. When the connectors are locked together,
the force of electro magnets are holding on to the metal plates. The connectors have one electro magnet
each, and they are connected by a wire to a simple circuit-board each with a 12V battery, a button and a
LED. When the button is pressed the LED is turned on, and the electro magnet looses it grip of the metal
plates. The ROV and the DS are equipped with one connector each, and the connectors will be rotated such
that the electro magnets are placed on opposite sides of the connectors, when the ROV is docked at the DS.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Using the OpenROV software it was not possible to conﬁgure the camera settings or install the OpenCV
library, therefore the newest release of the Linux OS Debian was installed.
The ROVs on-board BBB is capable to process continuous images, and extracting information from them.
All the image processing is done with the BBB, which is running multiple tasks while it is executing the
designed Feature Localization Algorithm (FLA), the update interval of the algorithm is on average 210ms.
This could probably be optimized, if some form of task scheduling was made in the Linux OS.
In order to control the ROVs three degrees of freedom, the ATM microcontroller must be conﬁgured such
that it responds to commands via the USART from the BBB. Then a program on the BBB is designed sending
commands to the ATM, in order to manually control the ROV, and another program for the autonomous
control.
The FLA was very good in locating the feature. Testing the FLA in different environments with different
colour representations and light intensities, the FLA gave some good results. Testing the FLA in tracking the
feature while manually Heaving/Yawing and manually locomoting the ROV from a starting position and
docking at the DS, the FLA gave some good reults in tracking the feature.
The ROVs physical system is quite complex and its the lack of symmetry makes it hard to control, especially
when the connector was mounted on the ROV. The thrusters direction of force are not pointing directly at the
Centre Of Buoyancy (COB), and therefore inducing a Pitch torque moment around COB. In order to achieve
a little bit better Surge control, the connector should be placed right in front of the camera, the the drag force
would oppose and ﬁnally cancel the induced moment when the ROV reaches its terminal speed. The vertical
thruster should be placed right on top of the COB, or adding a vertical thruster symmetrically on the other
side of COB, and opposite pitched propellers and rotating in the opposite direction, cancelling the induced
Yaw moment of the vertical thruster.
The designed Heave controller could not control the ROVs zG-position, because it was oscillating up and
down, due to the induced Pitch moment of the vertical thruster. The designed Surge controller probably
works, but because of the induced Pitch moment of the drag force on the connector the ROV is diving. Since
the Heave controller does not work properly, it is not possible to test the Surge controller. The designed Yaw
controller apparently worked, but there is no data to support it.

CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
6.1
Future Work
Reaching the overall goal in designing a autonomous underwater multi-robot system there must be a
application-speciﬁc software framework with a generic functionality that can selectively be changed in
order to achieve best results regarding this speciﬁc multi-robot system. The selected or designed software
framework should be common to all its users and its future candidates.
When the ROV is docked at the DS, there must be designed a system that can recharge the ROVs batteries.
The batteries could be charged by induction and this also opens up for the possibility to transfer data, adding
the data signal to the inductive charging signal. Inductive charging and data transmission is also practical in
this case, because it can be isolated from the erosive sea water, no moving electronical or mechanical parts
and no wearing of the installed equipment.
When the camera is to far away from the DS and can’t located the feature, there must be some other way for
the ROV to navigate it self to the DS. This could be acoustic beacons placed in some speciﬁc order, around
the area of interest, such that the ROV could navigate via these beacons.
The ROVs physical system could also be reconﬁgured or redesigned, such that it becomes more symmetrical.
Reconﬁguring the thrusters such that the forces exerted by the thrusters are directly on the ROVs COB, or
adding weights to move the COB closer to the the acting forces. A solution to the ROVs average density,
such that its buoyancy is always neutral compared to water, possibly adding and removing weights manually
or automatically by for example pumping water in or out of a contain ﬁxed on the ROV. Finally making sure
that the ROV is completely water proof, because when it has been submerged in water for some time, water
vapour condenses in front of the camera, obstructing the camera view.
