Abstract 
The brown seaweed Saccharina latissima contains high levels of iodine, which in excess intake can 
cause adverse human health effects. Norwegian cultivated S. latissima were blanched to attempt 
reduction of iodine content to ensure a safe food product. Two experiments were executed, one 
with focus on the two blanching methods; steam and water at the three temperatures 60, 80 and 
95°C for 300 seconds. Second experiment focused on the process variables; time and temperature 
within water blanching. The process temperatures were 30, 45, 60 and 80°C and processing time 
were 0, 30, 120 and 300 seconds, where 0 seconds were an immerging into the water. Moreover, 
the nutritional compositions together with quality compounds (amino acids and total phenols) 
were screened to evaluate their loss during processing. Water blanching was more efficient to 
reduce iodine than steam and lowered the iodine content below the threshold value of 2,000 mg 
I/kg dry weight. The process variables interacted with each other from which it could be concluded 
that both process time and temperature has an influence on the iodine reduction, although 
around 350 mg I/kg dry weight was the reduction going towards a constant level. The water 
blanching at the tested process variables affected water, ash and calculated carbohydrates, but 
not lipid or protein concentration significantly. It was found that a processing time above 30 
seconds would decrease the total phenolic content significantly. Regarding protein quality was no 
significant changes found when evaluating by essential amino acid to amino acid ratio and amino 
acid score. However, a significant decrease of the amino acids alanine, aspartic and glutamic acid 
occurred, which could lead to a change of flavor. Low processing temperature and time such as 
45°C for 30 seconds is recommended due to nutritional and quality aspects of the final product, 
additionally due to economical and environmental impacts. At these process variables the safe 
intake of S. latissima is 4.3 g wet weight according to recommended intake of iodine for adults.

1. Introduction  
Macroalgae (seaweeds) refer mainly to any large marine algae with multicellular structure, and are 
different from higher plants, as they are composed of thallus not possessing roots, stems, or 
leaves (Baweja et al. 2016; Ghosh et al. 2011). They are divided into three classes; Rhodophyta 
(red), Chlorophyceae (green), and Phaeophyta (brown). This thesis is exploring Saccharina 
latissima (Linnaeus) C.E. Lane, C. Mayes, Druehl & G. W. Saunders, which belongs to the group of 
brown seaweeds. The trend of utilizing seaweeds for human nutrition is a new concept in the 
Western part of the world. FAO has registered the yearly worldwide harvest of cultivated 
seaweeds for final consumption since 1950, however harvest of brown seaweeds in Europe was 
not reported until 1985. Saccharina latissima was first reported as cultivated and harvested in 
2007 by Spain (FAO 2017). Spain produced in total 21,000 tonnes wet weight (ww) in 2007 and in 
2015 the total European production was more than doubled to 49,510 tonnes ww produced by 
Norway, Denmark and Spain (FAO 2017). Saccharina latissima has shown promising cultivation 
results, both because of its remarkable growth rate, but also due to its chemical composition 
which is suitable to human consumption.  In 2016 a report by DTU Aqua  (Petersen et al. 2016) 
described that the most important business potential for seaweed cultivation was for human 
consumption due to its minerals, polysaccharides and several bioactive compounds. In brief, the 
bioactive compounds such as phenols, specific polysaccharides and pigments perform antioxidant, 
anticancer, and antiobesity activities etc. (Holdt & Kraan 2011). The increase of commercial 
cultivation and utilization the recent years, makes seaweed interesting for research and industry 
to find out more about seaweed as a food in Western countries, to optimize production and 
processing, and to look into food safety and regulations.  
Seaweed Energy Solutions AS is a Norwegian innovation and business development company 
established in 2006. The establishment was built on the vision to enable large scale farming of 
seaweeds for food products among other. They cultivate, harvest and process high quality 
seaweed on a commercial scale and are interested in the reduction of the iodine content in S. 
latissima due to safety aspects of consumption, since it is one of the market barriers.

2. Aim, objectives and hypotheses  
The current demand for the brown seaweed species S. latissima and its valuable nutritive 
compounds leads to an interest in reducing the high iodine content in the species for making it a 
safe product to consume. Thus, this thesis and its experimental research are based on answering 
the following research question: 
 
From this research question the following aim and objectives are derived. The aim of the study is 
to decrease the iodine content by two blanching methods and determining the possible loss of 
nutrients and bioactive compounds.  In detail the objectives to study are: 
Review: current available literature about the chemical composition and the influence 
of processing on iodine and other nutritional components in S. latissima are reviewed 
and compared to the results of this study.  
Process variation: the possible changes in content of iodine and other compounds in S. 
latissima due to different blanching methods and the process variables; temperature 
and time.  
Protein quality and potential bioactives: assessed by specifically evaluating amino acid 
composition and score, and total phenolic content.  
Mass balances and losses: evaluating the losses of product- and valuable chemical 
components after blanching by mass balances and calculating the amount retained 
after blanching.  
Risk assessment on iodine: comparison of the detected iodine contents to the 
recommended daily intake and tolerable upper limit established by the Nordic Council 
of Ministers (2012) and the French recommendations (CEVA 2014) on iodine in 
seaweed products, by calculating the amount of seaweed for safe human  
consumption. 
Recommendation: evaluation of the blanching methods including the processing 
variables for industrial application in terms of retaining beneficial nutrients and 
potential bioactives as well as decreasing the iodine content to comply with 
recommendations for safe human consumption.  
“How does blanching influence the high iodine content in 
Norwegian cultivated Saccharina latissima and does it influence the 
nutritional value and potential bioactive compounds?”

3. Theoretical background and literature review 
3.1. History of seaweeds as foods 
Seaweeds are currently a new trend for food consumption in Europe. They have barely set 
footprints in European history as they are merely mentioned for local parts of Europe in the 
literature. These small footprints in history signify that the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) 
does not classify some seaweed as a novel food. A novel food is a food that has not been 
consumed to a significant degree by human in the EU before 15th of May 1997 (EFSA 2018). 
Although some seaweeds are listed in the EFSA novel food catalogue, marked as “non-novel”, 
which means that if a company wishes to trade a particular seaweed, which was not commonly 
used and not accepted at the novel food catalogue, an application needs to be made. 
But what marks have seaweeds set in the European consumption’s history? The footprints go 
back to the Icelandic sagas, which mention Palmaria palmata used as a food product on Island 
around year 950, and 500 years later in a dried grounded format in Norway for bread and 
porridge, although these traditions have not been kept (Indergaard 2010c; Chapman et al. 2015). 
Traditions of seaweed as food are also found in old Celtic culture. The Celts exploited Chondrus 
crispus for thickening of dairy products and utilized Porphyra sp. for the recipe “laver bread”, a 
product which is still produced in Welsh regions (Indergaard 2010c; Delaney et al. 2012). In the 
19th century France, seaweed was gathered for production of iodine until World War II. Iodine 
production changed to other materials, which replaced seaweed and the extraction of alginate 
acid begun (Delaney et al. 2012).

The history of seaweed consumption in Asia has set some greater foot prints and traditions 
compared to the European history as it goes back more than 2,000 years. Already in the years 
before the common era countries such as China and Japan were eating seaweed for nutritional 
purposes (Baweja et al. 2016; Indergaard 2010c; Murty & Banerjee 2012). In the 1600s Japan 
started cultivating Porphyra sp., commonly known as “nori”. In the 1920s China commercially 
produced seaweed by setting out stones, concrete elements or by exploding cliffs and rocks. When 
the rope cultivation method was implemented, the production increased even further (Indergaard 
2010a).  In 1940s the interest of seaweed started for researchers, as studies on seaweed biology 
and nutrition were conducted as well as establishing cultivations in Japan and exploiting their 
potential for hydrocolloids (Baweja et al. 2016).

In 1993 the consumption of seaweed in Europe was close to 70 tons dry weight giving 
approximately 0.145 g dw ∙ person-1 ∙ year-1 versus 97,000 tons dry weight in Japan giving 782 g dw 
∙ person-1 ∙ year-1 (Fleurence et al. 2012; Hansen 2013; World Bank Group 2016). These different

inspired by the Asian seaweed consumption culture and the fact that seaweeds are not 
categorized directly as a novel food, the possibilities of seaweed to grow as an industry in Europe 
has potential.   
3.2. Feeding the world 
In 2009 FAO convened a Meeting of Experts and a report titled “How to Feed the World in 2050” 
was published after the meeting. The report describes their expectation of a rise in the human 
population to 9.1 billion in 2050, compared to 7.4 billion in 2016 (World Bank Group 2016). 
Although in spite of this expected rise of human population, the experts of the meeting were in 
agreement that in 2050 it would be possible to have a good food security for all. However, to be 
able to meet this prospect, new resources and new types of food must be investigated. They also 
concluded that there could be a problem with fresh water reserves and supplies and that for 
growing the food production an expansion of cropland was needed (FAO 2009).  
In January 2016 United Nations (UN) presented 17 sustainable development goals to be 
achieved before 2030. The term sustainable development was described in 1987 by the World 
Commission on Environment and Development also known as the Brundtland report, and was 
defined: “development which meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of 
future generations to meet their own needs” (Drexhage & Murphy 2010). Three goals are 
important in the context of this thesis: the second, the twelfth and the fourteenth. The second 
goal is “end hunger, achieve food security and improved nutrition and promote sustainable 
aquaculture”. Number one health risk worldwide is hunger and malnutrition. In 2014-2016 ~11% 
of the world population was undernourished. The 12th goal is “ensure sustainable consumption 
and production patterns” and goal 14 called life below water is described as “conserve and 
sustainably use the oceans, seas and marine resources for sustainable development”. Seaweeds as 
food fit with these goals, as they are a new unexploited resource in the Western part of the world, 
and have potential to be developed into new nutritious food products. Moreover, they do not 
need fresh water supply, nor do they take up land area (Murty & Banerjee 2012), which is very

positive in the context of the hurdles that FAO indentifies in the future food security. Lastly by 
planning the cultivation of seaweeds with a sustainable mindset they have the potential to 
conserve the life below water by turning organic pollutions into biomass, which can be used for 
human consumption.  
3.3. Saccharina latissima and its nutrient components  
3.3.1. 
Saccharina latissima 
All brown seaweeds belong to the class Phaeophyceae which is characterized by the brown/golden 
color, mainly due to the carotenoid pigment fucoxanthin (Baweja et al. 2016). Brown seaweeds 
are mostly common in cold to temperate waters, which is also the case for Saccharina latissima 
(Dawes 2016; Murty & Banerjee 2012). Saccharina latissima is a kelp species, which is generally 
known for its fast-growing large structure. Its morphology of the thallus can be seen in Figure 3.  
The leafy part is called a frond or blade, which is crinkly-formed at the edges and can grow up to 
about four meters long length. The lower part of the frond is the meristematic tissue, thus the part 
where new growth of the frond arises. The stipe or the stem is round and smooth. Both the frond 
and the stipe are flexible, which allow them to bend with wave actions. The lower part is the 
holdfast, that is a huddle of branching, root-like growths that the seaweed uses as an anchorage 
holding on to rocks or the sea floor (Murty & Banerjee 2012; Ghosh et al. 2011), and the entire 
thallus is responsible for nutrient uptake and photosynthesis.

3.3.2. 
Chemical composition of Saccharina latissima 
In the search for new novel sources of food, it is important to quantify and evaluate the chemical 
composition for safety and nutritional aspects. Several studies have determined the composition 
of S. latissima and an overview of the reviewed literature can be found in Table 1. It is worth 
mentioning that the chemical composition is influenced by extrinsic factors such as season, 
geographical location, salinity, temperature, light intensity, and nutrient supply, which can be the 
reason for the great variety (Rioux et al. 2017; Manns et al. 2017; Holdt & Kraan 2011; Misurcová 
2011),.  
In general, water is the main component of the tissue, and it is much higher (usually 75-90% 
ww) compared to terrestrial plants (20-40% ww) (Indergaard 2010b). In the studies review, the 
range of water content was 45.2-94.0% ww.  
Minerals are an essential part of the human diet, as the human body is not able to form them.

Carbohydrates include everything from monosaccharides to polysaccharides such as dietary 
fibers. The carbohydrates in brown seaweeds can be sugars; glucose, fucose, mannitol and uronic

total carbohydrate content in S. latissima, as some of the results might be underestimated.  
Nutrient-wise are proteins the essential building blocks of all living organisms. They are present 
in seaweeds in various forms; intracellular, in the cell-walls, as enzymes, and bound to pigments or 
polysaccharides (Rioux et al. 2017). The literature reviewed had a range of protein from 1.1-21% 
dw. There are several methods for determining protein e.g.; Bradford protein assay, nitrogen-to-
protein conversion factors (5.0, 6.25 or species-specific ones), Lowry protein assay, and by 
summing up total amino acids residues. Four of the studies determining protein used three 
different analytical methods. The different analytical methods have their own flaws and 
assumptions. FAO (2003) and Angell et al. (2016) recommend summing up total AA residues if 
equipment such as HPLC is available. The only study from Table 1 using the summing of amino acid 
residues method is Nielsen et al. (2016). Other studies focusing on protein in S. latissima has also 
been conducted. E.g. Ortind (2015) found a variation of protein content from 4.0-16.1% dw and 
Marinho et al. (2015) 1.3-10.8% dw. When considering the sum of AA residues method, the total 
protein variation ranges from 1.1 to 16.1% dw including the study by Nielsen et al. (2016) 
The quality of proteins in a foodstuff can be determined by evaluating the essential amino acids 
(EAA). EAA are amino acids (AA) that can’t be synthesized by the human organism and must be 
supplied in the diet. For humans the EAA are: leucine, isoleucine, valine, lysine, threonine,

important when evaluating the quality: the EAA to AA ratio and the amino acid score. The ratio 
explains how big an amount of the amino acids is essential. In seaweeds essential amino acids can 
count up to half of the total amino acids (Cerna 2011).

3.4. Iodine and human intake 
3.4.1. 
Iodine and human health  
Minerals are the components that are left as ash after combustion of an organic material. They 
can be divided into groups depending on the concentrations that human needs; main elements 
(>50 mg/day), trace elements (<50 mg/day) and ultratrace elements (small quantities (µg/day)) 
(Belitz et al. 2009). Iodine is a natural occurring non-metallic trace element that primarily occurs in 
nature as the oxidation states iodide (I-) and iodate (IO3-) (Ar Gall et al. 2004). Iodine is very 
abundant in the oceans, which is why the human nutritional access of iodine mainly is through 
fish, shellfish and seaweeds. Cereals, fruits, vegetables, dairy and meat usually consists of 20 to 50 
µg kg-1 whereas seafood contain the most – 100 to 1500 µg depending on species (Moreda-Piñeiro 
et al. 2011; Coultate 2002).  
Iodine is a trace mineral meaning that it is an essential nutrient for the human body. It is a 
structural and functional element of the thyroid hormones, which are necessary for energy 
yielding metabolism and growth but also neuronal- and nervous system development and 
cognitive functions especially in 15 week old embryos to 3 year old children (EFSA 2014; Moreda-
Piñeiro et al. 2011; WHO 2009; WHO 1998). Iodine from food and water is 92 to 100% bioavailable 
thus is almost totally absorbed and it is reduced to iodide in the gut and absorbed by the intestine. 
The kidney excretes the iodine with urine (Moreda-Piñeiro et al. 2011; WHO 1998).  
When mentioning iodine, it is almost impossible not to bring up the world situation of iodine 
deficiency. Since 2005 and every third year the World Health Organization (WHO) has reported the 
==PAGE:24==
 
Page 24 of 83 
 
global iodine status. The last report was from 2016 (Gizak et al. 2017). Zimmermann (2011) 
reported that 50% of the European inhabitants have a mild iodine deficiency and that on average 
pregnant women have an insufficient intake (Gizak et al. 2017). Some of the iodine deficiency 
disorders are goitre (enlarged thyroid gland), hypothyroidism (low levels of thyroid hormones in 
the blood), spontaneous abortion, delayed physical and mental development of fetuses, infants 
and children. Even a mild and moderate deficiency for a pregnant women can lead to these 
consequences for her unborn (WHO 2007; Nasjonal Råd for Ernæring n.d.; Zimmermann 2011).  To 
overcome the iodine deficiency global efforts have been initiated. The Universal Salt Iodization 
(USI) program has been performed for the last two decades where WHO recommends a 
fortification of 20 to 40 mg iodine kg-1 salt (WHO 2009; Gizak et al. 2017). Denmark has 
compulsory iodization of table salt and salt used in industrially produced bread whereas Norway is 
not part of this program although iodized table salt is seen (Nasjonal Råd for Ernæring n.d.).  
Ironically the aim of this thesis is to reduce iodine concentration in seaweed for food 
consumption. But too high intake of iodine is also possible. It can be harmful to the thyroid and 
also induce goitre and hypothyroidism by inhibiting the synthesis and release of the hormones 
(Nasjonal Råd for Ernæring n.d.) and it can also trigger thyroid autoimmunity, which can lead to 
thyroid cancer (WHO 1998; WHO 2009; EFSA 2014). In 1994 WHO stated that “Daily iodine intakes 
up to 1 mg appear to be entirely safe.” (for adults), and people that are regularly exposed to high 
amounts (10-200 mg/day) from e.g. medicine, dairy products, over-iodization of salt and seaweed 
kelps can live with no adverse chronic health effects.   
3.4.2.

intake (RI), average requirement (AR), lower intake level (LI), and upper intake level (UL) of iodine 
have been set. The RI refers to the iodine concentration that maintains a good nutritional status. 
The AR is defined as the lowest intake level over a long time that will retain a well nutritional 
condition. The LI indicates that an intake below this level could lead to iodine deficiency diseases. 
The UL is defined as the maximum level of a chronic daily intake that is unlikely to cause any 
adverse health effects (Nordic Council of Ministers 2012). The recommendation for adults from

3.5. Thermal processing 
3.5.1. 
Hydrothermal processing methods 
Blanching is a hydrothermal processing method, which is described to relate to producing safe 
foods by heating (Holdsworth & Simpson 2016). They also define the word process and describe it 
as a time-temperature relation, where temperature relates to the heating medium and the time 
for which it is applied. The heating media is important for the performance of the blanching 
treatment but other variables influence the effect as well. The efficiency of blanching depends on 
the heat-transfer coefficient, temperature gradient between product and heating medium, the 
thermal conductivity of the product and the ratio of the product and heating medium (Reyes de 
Corcuera et al. 2004). The process variables temperature and time also depends on the desired 
result. All in all there is not one overall design of blanching systems which is effective for all 
products, as it depends on the final use of the product and the effects looked for. After blanching 
the product is usually cooled in water to avoid a cooked taste, to inhibit thermophilic bacteria and 
to cool down prior further processing. 
Hot water and steam are the most common heating media for blanching in the industry as they 
are well researched, easy to set up, operate and optimize. Water blanching is typically carried out 
at temperatures from 70°C to 100°C, but studies on lower temperature together with long time 
blanching have also been conducted (Reyes de Corcuera et al. 2004). The blanching time depends 
on the wanted end product and its use, times between 1-40 min. have been reported (Reis et al. 
2017). An industrial water blancher consists of a cooking chamber cylindrically shaped, insulated, 
and with a heating jacket around. Inside of the chamber it can have a screw, chain conveyors or a 
==PAGE:27==
 
Page 27 of 83 
 
rotary drum to transport the product forward in the chamber while water is added to blanch (Reis 
et al. 2017; Reyes de Corcuera et al. 2004). 
 
Figure 4: Illustration of an industrial cylindrical water blancher (Reis et al. 2017) 
Steam blanching is usually conducted with 100°C steam or superheated steam (above 100°C)  
(Reyes de Corcuera et al. 2004; Xiao et al. 2017). Condensing steam has a higher heat transfer 
coefficient than hot water. The efficiency of steam blanching depends on the flow rate of the 
steam.  
Studies comparing steam and water blanching showed that steam was able to preserve 
nutrients better than water blanching and that it required 75 to 90% less energy for the same 
product characteristics. Water blanching utilizes more resources such as energy and water 
compared to steam blanching. Although it might seem that steam is the beneficial method, water 
often performs a more uniform blanching, meaning lower processing temperatures can be used. 
Comparing sensory qualities such as texture steam blanching often results in softening of the 
tissue. Also for a close to uniform steam blanching, the product must be thin and layered out in 
mono layers, as thick layers of product will lead to an non-uniform blanching and an increased 
blanching time (Xiao et al. 2017; Reyes de Corcuera et al. 2004). 
3.5.2. 
Hydrothermal processing applied industrially on vegetables 
Only a few studies touched upon hydrothermal processing of seaweeds, and no one studied the 
leach of nutrients from the seaweed. Therefore a review of nutrient loss due to hydrothermal 
processing on vegetables was conducted. Hydrothermal processing or blanching has mainly been 
commercially applied in the vegetable industry and therefore has research mainly focused on 
blanching vegetables. The purpose of blanching vegetables is to inactivate enzymes, eliminate 
==PAGE:28==
 
Page 28 of 83 
 
microorganisms, modify texture, remove pesticide residues, preserve color, flavor and nutritional 
value (Reyes de Corcuera et al. 2004; Melnick et al. 1943; Reis et al. 2017; Xiao et al. 2017). 
Especially the concern of inactivating enzymes has been the main focus of vegetable blanching 
prior to freezing as enzymes can cause off-flavors and off-colors during frozen storage.  
Studies show that nutrients are lost during blanching of vegetables, especially during water 
blanching. The nutrients lost were found to be proteins, phenolic compounds, vitamins, minerals, 
sugars and carbohydrates (Reyes de Corcuera et al. 2004; Xiao et al. 2014; Xiao et al. 2017). Table 
6 is a summary of the literature study about nutrient loss in various vegetables after water or 
steam blanching. 
The amount of nutrients lost depends on the blanching method and their process variables. 
Castro et al. (2011) studied water blanching of yellow bell peppers with the process conditions: 70, 
80 or 98°C for either 60 or 150 seconds. They found the protein content to decrease 13 to 24% 
where the biggest loss was observed when increasing temperature and time (Castro et al. 2011). A 
review completed by Adams (1981) showed a 12% protein loss in water blanching of peas and 8% 
in potatoes. 
York cabbage water blanched at 80 and 100°C for 2 to 14 min. all showed a reduction of total 
phenolic content (43.5 to 76.5%), while the higher temperature and longer processing time lead to 
a higher reduction, although no further reduction was observed after 6 min. The study discussed 
that the reduction could be due to blanching damaging the cell walls and causing solubilisation of 
the phenolic compounds. This causes them to leach out of the biomaterial, thus resulting in loss of 
total phenolic content and therefore antioxidant capacity (Jaiswal et al. 2012; Reis et al. 2017). 
Ahmed & Ali (2013) blanched cauliflower and found the lowest losses to be 16.6% observed for 3 
min. steam blanching and highest 37.7% for water blanching for 3 min. 
The ash content in a food is the total measure of minerals present. Mineral loss during 
blanching is caused by them leaching into the heating media (Fennema et al. 1996). Ahmed & Ali 
(2013) found a loss of 16.8% for water blanching at 100°C for 3 min.

4. Materials and Methods 
4.1. Seaweed material and preparation 
Saccharina latissima was collected by the company Seaweed Energy Solution AS (SES) from their 
cultivation site at Frøya, Norway (N63° 42.279' E8° 52.232’). Two batches of seaweed were 
harvested: approximately 1 m long thallus at February 13th 2018 and April 23th 2018. After harvest 
the seaweed was stored in aerated tanks with flow through seawater at 7-8 °C for 2-3 days until 
processing was carried out. Stem and holdfast was kept on the samples. If hydroids or other 
fouling organisms were present they were removed prior to processing to prevent them from 
influencing the results from chemical composition analyses.  
4.2. Processing of Saccharina latissima 
Two blanching experiments were conducted; 1) comparing steam and water blanching. The 
chemical composition of fresh material, hot water blanching, and steam blanching of S. latissima 
at various process temperatures (60, 80 and 95 °C). And 2) evaluating the influence of process 
variables on the chemical composition. The variables used were processing temperatures (30, 45, 
60 and 80 °C) and processing times (0, 30, 120 and 300 seconds). An overview of the experimental 
designs of the two experiments can be seen in Figure 5 and Figure 6.

processing with the various variables, as well as fresh and freezing, were conducted in triplicates 
(n=3).  The order of processing was randomized to ensure that observer bias was not influencing 
the results.

Water and ash content 
Water content was determined by vaporizing water at 105°C 20-24 hours. Ash content was 
determined by ignition in a muffle furnace (Nabertherm) at 600°C for 15-20 hours. Both analyses 
were determined gravimetrically and were performed in duplicates (n=2). 
4.3.3. 
Total iodine 
Iodine was extracted and prepared for inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) 
according to the standard DS/EN 17050:2017 2017. For the seaweed samples 0.15-0.20 g freeze 
dried milled homogenized powder was weighed into Kimax® glass centrifuge tubes. 5 ml of Milli-Q® 
water and 1 ml of 25% tetra methyl ammonium hydroxide (TMAH) were added and mixed. The 
tubes were sealed and placed in a preheated oven at 90±3°C for 3 hours followed by cooling and 
diluting to a final volume of 20 ml with Milli-Q® water. To remove coarse particles, the samples 
were centrifuged at 10,000 g for at 20 min. The supernatant was filtered through a 0.45 µm filter 
and for the seaweed powder, samples were diluted 50 times prior to analysis. Samples were 
stored in metal free plastic tubes for a maximum of 5 days prior to ICP-MS analysis. 
The iodine quantification (n=1) was performed by ICP-MS using a Finnigan ELEMENT-2 (Thermo 
Scientific) combined with a SC2 DX auto sampler and a prepFAST auto dilution system (Elemental 
Scientific). The parameter settings were 15.5 L/min coolant gas, 1.1 L/min auxiliary gas, and 0.75 
L/min nebulizer gas. Isotopes monitored were 127I and 185Re was used as internal standard. The 
limit of quantification (LOQ) for iodine was 0.1 µg/g and the limit of detection (LOD) was 0.005 
µg/g. Blank extracts were analyzed and subtracted from the results if within detection limit. No 
reference material was available for S. latissima, but is to be established for the lab for future 
detection.  
4.3.4. 
Amino acids hydrolysis and HPLC 
50 mg homogenized and freeze dried S. latissima was hydrolyzed in 1 ml 6 M HCl in a 105°C heat 
cabin for 22 hours and performed in duplicates (n=2). Prior to quantification the samples were 
neutralized by NaOH and HCl to pH 7.0±0.5 and filtered through a Whatman glass microfiber filter 
GF/C using suction to remove coarse particles. The samples were then diluted 1:100 followed by a 
filtration through 0.22 µm and then determined by high-performance liquid chromatography 
(HPLC).  
==PAGE:35==
 
Page 35 of 83 
 
Composition of amino acids were detected by HPLC. Reverse-fase HPLC (Dionex UltiMate® 3000 
HPLC+ focused, Dionex UltiMate® 3000 Autosampler, Dionex RF Fluorescence Detector, Thermo 
Scientific, USA) including precolumn derivatization of the amino acids with o-phtaldialdehyde and 
Nova-Pak column (Nova-Pak® C18 4 μm) were used.  
Under acidic hydrolysis with HCl asparagine and glutamine are completely converted to aspartic 
acid and glutamic acid, respectively. Tryptophan is completely destroyed, tyrosine is partially 
destroyed and cysteine only indirectly detectable. A loss of 5 – 10% of serine and threonine also 
occurs (Fountoulakis & Lahm 1998), which is not revised for in this study. The chromatographic 
peaks can gather in one, which was the case of glycine and arginine, therefore an average of their 
molar masses were used to calculate the content of these two amino acids.  
4.3.5. 
Total protein and protein quality 
Determination of the total protein content was calculated by summing up the total moles of 
amino acids as suggested for algae by Angell et al. (2016) and for general food stuffs by FAO (2003) 
and then subtracting the water mass (18 g H2O/mol amino acid), which were integrated during the 
disruption of peptide bonds (Diniz et al. 2011) in the acid hydrolysis.  
The amino acids analyzed represented both the free and combined amino acids, but it was assumed 
that all were from protein sources when determining protein content and amino acid profile. To 
evaluate the amino acid quality the sum of total essential amino acid (EAA) ratio to total amino acids 
(AA) and the amino acid score were calculated, although not including bio availability or digestibility. 
The reference pattern was for children at the age of 3 to 10 years, as recommended by (FAO 2011) and 
was established by WHO/FAO/UNU (2007).

4.3.8. 
Extraction of phenols 
Phenols were extracted according to the method described by (Stévant et al. 2017). The extraction 
solvent consisted of a mixture of 60% ethanol and 40% demineralized water. It consisted of two 
extraction steps both followed by decanting. 10 mg/ml freeze dried seaweed powder was mixed 
with the extraction solvent and placed in an ice bath for 2 hours following by a decanting. The same 
procedure was applied to the pellet left behind and the supernatants were pooled together. Extracts 
were kept out of light and stored at -20°C until processing.

4.5. Data processing and statistical analysis  
The data is expressed as means ± standard deviation unless otherwise stated. The raw data 
treatment, calculations and modeling were executed in Microsoft Excel 2007. SigmaPlot 13.0 was used 
to illustrate the results as figures. For statistical tests IBM SPSS Statistics 24 was used. To test for 
significant differences between freshly harvested seaweed in February and April, a T-Test was used. 
The test run to define the statistically significant difference between the means of the groups (fresh, 
frozen, and blanched material) was a one-way ANOVA with Tukey’s post hoc test. General Linear 
Model (GLM) was used to test for significant interactions among factors (processing time and 
temperature) also with Tukey’s post hoc test for multiple comparisons between and within groups. 
Means were considered statistically significantly different when levels of p < 0.05 were obtained.

6. 
Discussion 
6.1. Comparison of nutritional composition of fresh Saccharina 
latissima cultivated in Norway to other studies  
The comparing of the chemical composition from this study with other studies (Table 1) is to be 
discussed in brief. Generally, is Saccharina latissima a biological living organism, with a chemical 
composition varying due to both intrinsic and extrinsic factors such as geographical location and 
date of harvest. Within the geographical location, each organism might also have slightly different 
growing conditions or genetics, which could influence the final chemical composition. The study is 
divided into two experiments, experiment 1 which was on S. latissima harvested in February 2018 
and experiment 2 which was harvested in April 2018. 
Water content was found to be 90.8±0.1 and 90.7±0.4% ww for February and April, 
respectively. These concentrations were in the higher end of the literature study but within the 
found range of 45.2-94.0% ww (Table 1). The ash content were 49.2±2.2% dw for February and 
44.5±1.1% dw for April, both in the high end and even a bit outside the literature review (8.7 to 
45% dw), which gives that the Norwegian cultivated S. latissima have a high mineral content 
relative to current European published studies and significantly higher in February compared to 
April. 
The protein concentration quantified by summing up total AA was 7.1±1.1% dw in February and 
6.8±2.7% dw in April. The protein concentrations from the review also quantified by total AA 
varied from 1.1 to 16.1% dw, which meant that the protein concentration in Norwegian cultivated 
S. latissima was within the protein concentrations generally found.  
Even though the carbohydrates were quantified differently in the reviewed studies but 
calculated by subtracting water, ash, lipids and protein in this study, they were still compared. The 
review had a range of 20.0-76.0% dw and the calculated carbohydrates represented 43.4% dw in 
this study, which is within the range. 
The lipid concentration from the literature study ranged from 0.2-2.1% dw, but was mostly in 
the lower end. The lipid concentration was found to be 5.3±2.9% dw in the S. latissima harvested 
in April. This concentration is more than double as high as the highest in the reviewed literature. 
The standard deviation of the three process replicates is almost 3%, which is very high, but 
==PAGE:58==
 
Page 58 of 83 
 
subtracting it from the average will still lead to a high lipid concentration compared to the 
reviewed studies. It could be interesting to quantify how much polyunsaturated fatty acids 
represent these 5.3±2.9% dw to investigate the quality of the lipids. 
The iodine content of the unprocessed S. latissima from the reviewed European studies had a 
big variation from 510 to 6,568 mg/kg dw. Other studies from Norway had contents of 2,930 
mg/kg dw in April, 3,802 mg/kg dw in May and 1,655 mg/kg dw in June. This current study found a 
content of 5,739.4±105.2 mg/kg dw in February and 4,605±275 mg/kg dw in April, thus contain 
high amounts of iodine. The contents were also higher when directly comparing to the Norwegian 
cultivated S. latissima from other studies. April is usually the harvest month for SES where the 
iodine content is significantly lower than February, which is beneficial for reducing the iodine 
content to produce a safe product for human consumption.   
To sum up had Saccharina latissima cultivated by SES in Norway a high water, mineral, and lipid 
content compared to the reviewed studies. As mentioned earlier is it possible to have variations of 
chemical composition within same species due to both intrinsic factors such as genetics, but also 
by extrinsic factors such as geographical location, season of sampling, and environmental 
conditions. Some studies have looked upon the seasonal variation and environmental conditions 
such as salinity and depth of growth, which have indicated that a relation between these factors 
and the chemical composition occurs. This could be the reason why the water, mineral and ash 
concentrations were higher. It is not possible to be more specific, as these factors were not tested 
for.  
6.2. Lowering iodine content in S. latissima by blanching  
Experiment 1 with S. latissima harvested in February compared steam- and water blanching at 
three different temperatures (60, 80 and 95°C) at 300 seconds. Comparing the results of iodine 
content from experiment 1 to the French recommendations of 2000 mg I/kg dw it was with no 
doubt that water blanching was sufficient and steam blanching was not. This was the one and only 
reason to continue exclusively with water blanching in experiment 2.  
However, the iodine content after processing at 80°C steam for 300 seconds was significantly 
different from fresh S. latissima and had a relative loss of 14.5%. It may be possible that steam 
blanching would be able to lower the iodine content to 2,000 mg/kg dw or below that threshold 
==PAGE:59==
 
Page 59 of 83 
 
with an initial lower iodine content in fresh material and a longer processing time. It is important 
to mention that the results also depend on the circumstances of the machinery. Steam blanching 
is not one specific method, it can also be optimized and its efficiency depends on many factors. In 
this particular, study the amount of steam and the velocity during processing were unknown. If a 
directly steaming onto the product with several air blast valves above or below the product had 
been applied, or super heated steam with temperatures above 100°C was used, then the final 
iodine content would most likely be lower. It could be interesting to look into optimization of the 
steam blanching method, as steam usually is gentler to other compounds such as minerals and 
bioactive compounds, as shown in the review about blanching of vegetables. In addition less 
energy and water are utilized, which is equal to production costs.  
All three processing temperatures with process time of 300 seconds in experiment 1 were 
sufficient to reduce the iodine content below the threshold of 2,000 mg/kg dw. Therefore were 
both of the processing variables lowered in experiment 2. Thus the four temperatures (30, 45, 60 
and 80°C) together with the processing times of 0, 30, 120 and 300 seconds were chosen. The 
processing time of 0 seconds was applied as a control to test if immerging S. latissima into the 
blanching water would have any effect on the iodine content, also indicating how big an influence 
temperature had on the iodine reduction.  
Freezing is commonly used to preserve the seaweed after harvest, and therefore it was also 
studied. No significant difference was found between fresh and frozen biomass. It can therefore 
be concluded that freezing is not reducing the iodine content and thus additional processing is 
needed. The cooling step of the blanching process was also analyzed, but no replicates were 
performed for the cooled seaweed, and can therefore only be used as an indication. The iodine 
content of the cooled seaweed was between the average ranges of fresh and frozen material, 
which indicated that cooling did not have any influence on the iodine reduction. 
Significant interactions among process variables mean, that there was no linear relation 
between time and temperature, although, it can be seen that the iodine is reduced when 
increasing both process time and temperature. If again the iodine contents are compared to the 
French recommendation of 2,000 mg/kg dw, it is seen that 30°C for 0 seconds was not sufficient at 
all. In fact, it can be seen that the average content is higher (5,157±201 mg/kg dw) than the initial 
content of fresh material (4,605±275 mg/kg dw). This difference could be due to biological or 
==PAGE:60==
 
Page 60 of 83 
 
extrinsic factors among seaweeds or the fact that only three replicates were analyzed. Although if 
the blanching time was set to 300 seconds, the content was lowered to 1014±349, which is below 
the threshold value given by the French recommendation. In addition, immerging at 45°C was not 
sufficient, but already at t = 30 seconds the content was reduced sufficiently. This was not the case 
for neither 60 nor 80°C, where immerging was sufficient. At longer processing times than the 
above mentioned, the iodine content was going towards a constant content of approximately 350 
mg/kg dw. This constant iodine level could possibly be due to iodine on various forms (speciations) 
and bound differently to the biomass. Dividing the process by temperature, the following process 
variables used in this study were sufficient when the target is the threshold value defined of 2,000 
mg/kg dw: 
 30°C for 300 seconds 
 45°C for 30 seconds 
 A dip in 60°C 
 A dip in 80°C  
When deciding on the process variables it is important to look into the economical aspects; is it 
more economical or environmentally costly to have high process temperatures or processing 
times, or something in between?  
The processes of this current study was much more efficient in the relative iodine content after 
blanching compared to the other studies focusing on same problem. The most effective study 
found a relative iodine content of 12.2% (Stévant et al. 2018) by soaking the biomass at 32°C for 1 
hour. This treatment together with boiling 10 and 20 minutes lowered the iodine content below 
the threshold value of 2,000 mg/kg dw to a relative iodine content of 24.0 and 48.7%, respectively. 
Lüning & Mortensen (2015) boiled dried seaweed, which in dried format had an iodine content 
below the threshold value prior to boiling. They were able to reduce to an amount of 95 mg/kg dw 
for both 20 and 30 min. of boiling. When running statistics on a split dataset, no significant 
differences were found between seaweed treated for 30, 120, and 300 seconds when looking into 
the three groups of temperatures 45, 60 and 80°C. This means that after 30 seconds for those 
three mentioned process temperatures, a constant content of iodine was achieved. Similar cases 
were found in the studies by both Lüning & Mortensen (2015) and Korsvold (2017). The reason of 
different effectiveness between studies could be due to several factors. As earlier mentioned, the 
==PAGE:61==
 
Page 61 of 83 
 
iodine can be differently bound in the seaweed. If the iodine is in other forms or tighter bound in 
the seaweed studied by Korsvold (2017), Stévant et al. (2018) and Lüning & Mortensen (2015), it 
could be the explanation for the difference in the effect of reducing. Additionally, could the initial 
iodine content of the seaweed prior to process have an influence on the reducing effect, if a 
higher quantity were to be removed.  
6.3. Preservation of biomass and nutritional compounds 
In Table 13 the nutritional composition of water blanched Saccharina latissima is listed. The 
protein content of blanched biomass was significantly higher compared to fresh S. latissima, which 
was also the case of experiment 1. It is beneficial for the company, that the protein content for 
e.g. 80°C at 120 seconds is 13.1 % in dry seaweed, compared to 6.8 % in dry fresh seaweed. This 
increase of protein concentration is not equal to a sudden synthesis or uptake of protein during 
blanching as there were no proteins in the heat media. This change is due to a loss of other 
compounds. To find out exactly which compounds are lost during blanching, the product loss in 
mass must be evaluated. Therefore, will Table 13 only illustrate the nutritional overview of the 
product after blanching.  
When selecting the process variables it is indeed important to consider the product loss. The 
results (Table 11 and Figure 11: Biomass loss during water blanching defined by weighting. Expressed in g 
loss ∙ 100 g-1 ww processed S. latissima.Figure 11) showed significantly that the bigger process 
temperature the bigger product loss, although with no significant differences between 60 and 
80°C. Processing at 30°C had on average no loss of biomass whereas 45°C had an average of 
10.6%, although no significantly differences were observed. For 60 and 80°C the average loss was 
approximately 20%. Loss of biomass during processing is equal to an economic loss, thus it is 
recommended to process the seaweed at low temperatures such as either 30 or 45°C. 
Combining the analyzed concentrations of the blanched seaweed together with the product 
loss during processing, retentions for each component were calculated Table 15. Some of the true 
retentions were found to be above 100%, which could be due to errors. When diving into the 
group of 80°C the retentions increased for protein with increasing processing time, and as 
significant differences were not tested for, it is not possible to know if the retentions among 
processes are significant. Although the statistics were not tested for, the true retentions showed 
that the biggest loss of components was for water, ash and carbohydrates and not for protein and 
==PAGE:62==
 
Page 62 of 83 
 
lipids. The carbohydrates were depended on the other compounds, as they were calculated on the 
basis of the other concentrations. Since S. latissima is known for its high quality protein and good 
lipid profile, the losses of lipids and protein were especially important. The retentions were overall 
high for both lipids and protein, indicating that the loss of these two components were low. 
Therefore, suggesting process variables regarding loss of these two compounds, any of the 
variables used in this study will be a safe choice. Although the ash fraction could contain some 
valuable minerals, which are lost during processing, as well for carbohydrates could some 
important dietary fibers leach during blanching. It seems from the retentions and also when 
statistically evaluating total loss of biomass, that the retention of both carbohydrates and ash are 
higher at lower processing temperatures, thus preserving these components better.   
The experimental design was based on literature studying blanching of vegetables Table 6. The 
main reason was that no studies had looked considerable into the nutrient or bioactive losses of S. 
latissima or other seaweeds to the author’s knowledge. Therefore it was expected that the same 
relative losses could occur for S. latissima. Protein losses for bell peppers, peas and potatoes 
varied between 8 to 24% (Castro et al. 2011; Adams 1981). This significant loss was not found in 
any of the blanched samples. The vegetables were blanched for 60, 150 and 180 seconds, which 
were within the processing variables of this current study. A reason for S. latissima to hold onto its 
protein compounds could be the ability of the polysaccharides to bind to the proteins.  
6.4. Quality aspects of blanched Saccharina latissima 
The quality of a product can be many aspects other than nutritional value or safe human 
consumption. Appearance such as color is very important for the consumer perception when 
entering a product in the food market. Due to processing temperature a difference on color was 
found. No colorimetric analysis was performed, but it was visible that the product changed color 
during processing. This is important when choosing the process method and variables. If the initial 
brown/golden color of S. latissima wants to be kept, then the processing temperature should not 
exceed 60°C, although if a deep green product color is desired, then 80°C will be a good choice. 
The green color achieved at 80°C could look more interesting to the consumer and increase the 
acceptance of a new product.  
Another quality measure is the total phenolic content, which was determined since phenols are 
known to have antioxidant capacities (Jiménez-Escrig et al. 2001). The total phenolic content of 
==PAGE:63==
 
Page 63 of 83 
 
this study was very low compared to an earlier study, which used the same extraction and 
determination method. This current study had a content of 0.75±0.30 and 0.47±0.07 mg PGE/g dw 
for fresh and frozen S. latissima, respectively, whereas the other study by the author (Wirenfeldt 
2018) had a content of 1.63 to 1.75 mg PGE/g dw in frozen and freeze dried S. latissima. It was 
difficult to compare the total phenolic content to other studies, as studies have shown a great 
variety between different extraction methods. Mostly have the extraction solutions consisted of 
water, ethanol or a mix of both. Sabeena Farvin & Jacobsen (2013) studied several seaweed 
species and their total phenolic content. They concluded that S. latissima was one of the species 
with lowest content compared to others. Even though the low phenolic content of S. latissima is 
taken into consideration, it the content found in this current study relatively low compared to an 
earlier study by the author (Wirenfeldt 2018). This difference must be due to some unknown 
factors such as extrinsic factors, but it could also be possible that the extraction yield was lower 
for the current study compared to the earlier study, even if the same extraction method was 
applied. Even though the results of the current study is not within range of the other study, the 
relative loss between fresh and blanched biomass can conclude, that there is a significant loss of 
total phenols due to blanching. The variable processing time is the significant influence on the loss 
of relative total phenolic content, which signifies that the higher process time, the bigger loss of 
antioxidant capacity. 
The quality of the protein was described by EAA to AA ratio and the AA score, which were 
calculated for both experiment 1 and 2. EAA/AA ratio in fresh S. latissima was 46±5 and 42±1% 
and the score was 96±32 and 101±5% for February and April, respectively. Although no statistically 
significant differences were found. The EAA/AA ratio from other studies were 21-42% in Danish 
waters when combining results from Marinho et al. (2015) and Nielsen et al. (2016) and 30 to 52% 
in Faroese waters (Bak et al. n.d.; Ortind 2015).  The ratio was higher than the Danish S. latissima 
but had same trend when compared to the Faroese S. latissima. However, the AA score was high 
in both month relative to the above mentioned studies, that found a variation of 17 to 69% in 
Danish waters (Marinho et al. 2015) and 18 to 106 % in Faroese S. latissima  (Bak et al. n.d.; Ortind 
2015).  The high AA score indicates that the protein of Norwegian cultivated S. latissima by SES has 
a high quality compared to others. It is important to mention that the bioavailability of the amino 
acids in this current study and the reviewed studies mentioned above were not accounted for in 
==PAGE:64==
 
Page 64 of 83 
 
the AA score. Consequently can the quality by AA score not be compared to other industries 
directly, as the true quality highly depends on the access to amino acids.  
Each individual AA was divided by the total AA to find out which amino acids were lost during 
processing. Statistics showed a loss for alanine, where steam blanching was the gentlest to alanine 
compared to water blanching. Since alanine is not an essential amino acid. The same procedure 
was conducted for experiment 2, where the significant losses found were for alanine, aspartic and 
glutamic acid. All three are not essential amino acids, therefore it needs to be considered if these 
significant losses are important for the final product and its nutritional quality. As earlier 
mentioned is product quality also the sensory aspects such as taste. The taste of the seaweed is 
related to the amino acids, especially the glutamic acid, which is the primary stimulatory agent in 
the fifth basic taste; umami. With a decrease of these three amino acids and especially glutamic 
acid, the taste of the product is likely to change. This could lead to another perception of the 
product for consumers, but if it is towards a benefit or disadvantage for the product is unknown.

moment. Skaaning (2017) described an unpublished Danish average daily consumption of 0.1 g dw 
per person per day and a high intake level of 1.0 g dw per person per day (pers. comm. S. L. Holdt). 
If these values are considered, then for the average intake all of the blanching methods would be 
safe and within the RI. For the high intake level, an excess intake of iodine would take place when 
comparing to both the RI and UL, but not for the average of the high reducing processing 
variables. All in all a goal of a daily consumption must be set if the process parameters should be 
==PAGE:66==
 
Page 66 of 83 
 
chosen on the base of human consumption. If the S. latissima consumption levels of the blanched 
biomass are met for individuals or generally a population, the fortification of iodine in table salt 
and other products must be reconsidered as the possibilities of en excess intake from various food 
sources could be possible.  
7. Conclusion 
The total iodine content of fresh Saccharina latissima analyzed from the two experiments were 
5,739±105 mg/kg dw and 4,605±275 mg/kg dw for February and April, respectively. The French 
recommendation of iodine concentration of seaweed products has been established to be 2,000 
mg/kg dw, thus the content of the two fresh biomasses exceeds this threshold. The first 
experiment showed that water was more sufficient to reduce the iodine content, and it reached 
below the threshold level, whereas steaming did not. The second experiments looked into the 
influence of various processing times, which did not indicate that one or the other of the factors 
had a bigger influence on the iodine reduction than the other. All in all, the higher process 
temperature and time the bigger reduction. Although when reaching a concentration of 
approximately 350 mg/kg dw the concentration appeared to be constant even though processing 
time or temperature was increased.  
The cultivated S. latissima from the current study had higher water, ash and carbohydrate 
concentrations when comparing to other studies. The water blanching affected water, ash and 
calculated carbohydrates but not lipid or protein concentration significantly. Regarding the protein 
quality of the blanched S. latissima, it was evaluated by EAA to AA ratio and AA score. No 
significantly changes were identified due to blanching, although it reduced the content of alanine, 
aspartic acid, and glutamic acid significantly. The overall total phenolic content was low compared 
to another study on S. latissima, but the relative contents showed a loss when processing time 
was 30 seconds or more.  
For economical and environmental impacts low processing temperatures and times are desired. 
The processing at 45°C and 30 seconds had no color change and was sufficient to reduce the 
iodine content below the French recommendation. If an averagely daily target intake of S. 
latissima for a person is approximately 0.23 gram dw or 4.32 gram ww, these processing variables 
are highly recommended.

Page 67 of 83 
 
8. Future work 
This study indicates that blanching can reduce the iodine content of Saccharina latissima 
sufficiently for safe daily human consumption of 2.3 to 8.6 gram ww depending on blanching 
method. However, this conclusion is based on an assumption of 100 % bioavailability and 
bioassessibility. As mentioned earlier the iodine from food and water is usually 92 to 100% 
bioavailable, but this might not be the case for Saccharina latissima and a study on the 
bioavailability and bioassessibility could help understand the magnitude of iodine as a health risk 
in sugar kelp. Furthermore, a speciation of iodine in S. latissima is also important when assessing 
the human safety for seaweed consumption. 
For the second experiment four processing times and four temperatures were chosen. It could 
be interesting to study if other temperatures between 30°C and 45°C and processing times of 10 
seconds or some in between 30 and 120 seconds would be sufficient as well. Moreover, this study 
was more efficient than others, and it could be interesting to find out what other variables could 
have an influence such as the concentration of seaweed per liter of water or a stirring during 
processing. 
The loss of valuable compounds such as lipids, amino acids and total phenols were evaluated. 
Other compounds such as fucoxanthin, other antioxidant capacities, and a spectrum of the specific 
minerals and trace elements lost during processing would be interesting to look into. Also if a loss 
of other undesired compounds would take place such as cadmium and inorganic arsenic.  
The blanching process affected the product color, which would become green when blanched 
at high temperatures. The perception of a food product is important for the consumer. The 
blanching could also change other sensoric aspects such as texture and taste. These parameters 
would be interesting to study, as the perception of the product could also change to something 
more positive, as seaweeds for human consumption is new to many in Europe. Blanching is used in 
the vegetable industry to preserve the products from deterioration by enzymatic activity and 
microbial growth. The heat treatment would probably also affect these activities in seaweed and it 
would be interesting to evaluate the preservation effect from blanching.
