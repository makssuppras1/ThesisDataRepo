Abstract 
Antibiotic resistance is a growing problem and alternatives are in urgent need. Different ribotypes of 
Clostridium difficile have been shown to be resistant to different antibiotics and the symptoms range 
from mild diarrhea to pseudomembranous colitis and death. The surface layer of C. difficile is 
composed of high and low molecular weight proteins which is involved in the adhesion to host cells. 
An example of such an adhesion factor is SlpA. It is believed that by binding sdAbs to SlpA, the 
adhesion of C. difficile could be prevented. Therefore, as an alternative to antibiotic treatment of C. 
difficile - which often disrupts the microbiota and leads to recurrent infections – the discovery of 
sdAbs against SlpAs from different clinically relevant C. difficile ribotypes will be assessed. This will 
be conducted by cloning, expression, and purification of the adhesion factor protein SlpA from the 
C. difficile ribotypes 12, 18 and 27. These SlpA proteins will be used for phage display experiments 
to identify human sdAb binders from the Predator library.  
Another aim in this study is to evaluate two different ELISA screening methods “polyclonal cloning 
followed by protein-based screening” and “monoclonal phage screening”.  
The cloning, expression, and purification of SlpA from the C. difficile ribotypes 12, 18 and 27 was 
successfully conducted. It was possible to identify and sequence 18 clones expressing sdAbs with 
high affinity to SlpA-18 from which 15 shared the same sequence. It was only possible to identify 
one clone expressing sdAbs against SlpA-27 and it was not possible to identify any interesting sdAb 
binders against SlpA-12.  It was also concluded that the “polyclonal cloning followed by protein-
based screening” approach was the superior ELISA screening technique since more binders were 
identified.

Introduction 
Ever since Alexander Fleming introduced penicillin in the 1940s many bacteria species have 
developed resistance towards antibiotics, and some have become the multidrug resistant bacteria we 
see today. This phenomenon is seen due to overuse of antibiotics and thereby an exerted selection 
pressure (Monroe & Polk, 2000). Antibiotic resistance is a growing problem and in 2016 it was 
estimated that 700,000 people worldwide died each year due to resistant infections. Unless action is 
taken it is estimated that this number will increase to 10 million people each year by 2050. This 
scenario will cost the global economy 100 trillion USD and the consequence is that every third second 
a person will die due to antibiotic resistance (O´Neill, 2016). Therefore, new methods are needed to 
keep treating infectious diseases.  
Clostridium difficile 
An example of an antibiotic resistant bacteria is Clostridium difficile (C. difficile) also known as 
Clostridioides difficile. C. difficile is an anaerobic, gram-positive, and spore-forming bacillus, which 
is the most common cause of healthcare-associated infectious diarrhea. A C. difficile infection range 
from asymptomatic carriage to mild diarrhea to a severe life-threatening infection with sepsis, toxic 
megacolon, and transmural pancolitis (De Roo & Regenbogen, 2020). 
The pathogenetic effects of C. difficile are mainly due to the production of the exotoxins TcdA and 
TcdB. TcdA and TcdB disrupt the cytoskeletal structure and the tights junctions of the host cells and 
induce apoptosis and necrosis together with inflammation. Some C. difficile strains do also produce 
a binary toxin called C. difficile transferase (CDT) (Di Bella et al., 2016).   
Antibiotic use, especially clindamycin, third-and fourth generation cephalosporins, fluroquinolones, 
or combination of antibiotics is the most common risk factor for C. difficile infections. The mortality 
rate of C. difficile infections ranges from 2 to 6%, but mortality is significantly higher in patients 
suffering from inflammatory bowel disease and patients admitted to intensive care units (De Roo & 
Regenbogen, 2020). Approximately, 18 to 35% of patients treated for C. difficile infections 
experience at least one recurrent infection within 2 to 8 weeks of the initial infection. A potential 
mechanism for susceptibility to recurrent C. difficile infection is persistent disruption of microbiota 
diversity by antibiotics which allows spores to germinate (De Roo & Regenbogen, 2020; JY et al., 
2008).  
C. difficile displays an array of proteins on the cell surface known as the surface layer (S-layer). 
Several proteins within the S-layer are involved in the adhesion to the host and thereby the

colonization of C. difficile by adherence to the epithelium mucosa. An example of a S-layer protein 
(SLP) involved in the adhesion of C. difficile to the host is Surface Layer Protein A (SlpA) which is 
important in the adherence to Caco-2 cells. Since SlpA is surface exposed it could be a good drug 
target to avoid adhesion of C. difficile (Bradshaw et al., 2018; Janoir, 2016).    
C. difficile was initially thought to be a nosocomial pathogen, but today the level of asymptomatic 
carriers is increasing and 35% of the C. difficile infections that occur in the community, has led to 
additional proposed fecal-oral transmission sources, such as compost, manure, food and zoonotic 
sources (De Roo & Regenbogen, 2020).  
In the United States nearly half-a-million C. difficile infection cases occurred in 2011 with 
approximately 29,000 deaths with the vast majority of these being adults over 65 years. The financial 
burden of C. difficile infections in the US is estimated at 1.9 to 7 billion USD annually because C. 
difficile  prolongs hospitalization (De Roo & Regenbogen, 2020). 
 
There are several different ribotypes of C. difficile. Some ribotypes are classified as hypervirulent 
and they differ in resistance to antibiotics, toxin production and thereby disease severity and mortality 
rates (Bradshaw et al., 2018; De Roo & Regenbogen, 2020).   
Ribotyping 
PCR-ribotyping is a way to distinguish between different C. difficile strains by targeting the intergenic 
spacer region between the 16S and 23S rRNA genes. The intergenic spacer region of C. difficile differ 
in length and PCR amplification with a single primer pair results in a pattern of bands unique for a 
specific PCR-ribotype (Janezic et al., 2014). Ribotypes are geographically distributed by nation and 
region, but also by hospital. The ribotype population is expansive but dominated by a few major types 
such as ribotype 27 (De Roo & Regenbogen, 2020). The wild type ribotype of C. difficile 630 is 
ribotype 12 (Kandalaft et al., 2015).    
Ribotype 27 is considered an epidemic strain due to an increased incidence and severity in the US, 
Canada, Mexico, and Europe. Furthermore, it has increased toxin A and B production together with 
production of CDT. It is resistant to multiple antibiotics, particularly fluoroquinolones, and relapses 
are also more common for ribotype 27 compared to other ribotypes (Carman et al., 2011; De Roo & 
Regenbogen, 2020).  
Ribotype 18 also identified as smz, is a predominant strain in some Italian, South Korean, and 
Japanese hospitals and it tends to affect older patients producing higher levels of the toxins TcdA and 
TcdB. Ribotype 18 has a gyrA C245T mutation that confers high resistance to fluoroquinolones.

Furthermore, antibiotic resistance to erythromycin, clindamycin, and moxifloxacin have also been 
identified in some strains of ribotype 18 (De Roo & Regenbogen, 2020).  
Differences between ribotypes have also been observed in the adhesion factor SlpA which is a part 
of the S-layer in C. difficile (Bradshaw et al., 2018).  
S-layer 
Many prokaryotic species have a S-layer which typically consists of a single protein that forms the 
outermost surface of the cell, but an S-layer may also allow surface presentation of proteins anchored 
deeper in the cell wall. S-layer proteins account for 15% of the total protein of a cell and some of its 
functions include transport or storage of nutrients, host cell adhesion and/or invasion, immune system 
evasion, and protection from competing microorganisms (Bradshaw et al., 2018).  
The S-layer of C. difficile, is largely heterodimeric, see Figure 1. The S-layer is formed by the low 
and high molecular weight proteins (LMW SLP and HMW SLP) which are coded for by a single 
gene slpA. HMW SLP is formed of three cell wall binding domains while the fold of LMW SLP is 
potentially unique to C. difficile by which these two proteins form a heterodimer on the surface of the 
cell with HMW SLP forming a lower layer and LMW SLP forming an upper layer that is exposed to 
the surface (Bradshaw et al., 2018). According to Qazi et al., 2009 the S-layer does not normally 
appear to by glycosylated, even though Dingle et al., 2012 has identified a glycosylation gene cluster 
in some strains. Kirk et al., 2017 has identified strains of C. difficile that lack an S-layer and found 
that these strains showed significantly increased susceptibility to lysozyme and antimicrobial peptide, 
an inability to produce symptoms of infection in hamsters and a decreased toxin release. Furthermore, 
they showed a reduction in sporulation and heat resistance. This shows the importance of the S-layer 
in several processes, but also that it appears not be essential to the survival of C. difficile (Bradshaw 
et al., 2018).

The primary component of the C. difficile S-layer is SlpA. SlpA is also heterodimeric and consist of 
a HMW SLP and LMW SLP. Karjalainen et al., 2001 have shown that antibodies against HMW SLP 
from one ribotype retain activity against HMW SLP from another ribotype and concludes that HMW 
SLPs are conserved between ribotypes. On the other hand, LMW SLPs are not always recognized by 
antibodies raised against another ribotype due to a large variability between ribotypes. This large 
variability is likely to have arisen in an attempt for C. difficile to evade the host immune system 
(Bradshaw et al., 2018; Cerquetti et al., 2000; Karjalainen et al., 2001). SlpA is a known adhesion 
factor of C. difficile which has been investigated by Merrigan et al., 2013. They found that pre-
treatment of host cells with crude or purified SlpA subunits, or incubation of vegetative bacteria with 
anti-SlpA antisera significantly reduced C. difficile adhesion. They also found that SlpAs from 
different ribotypes mediated host-cell attachment to different extent.   
    
Since SlpA has been shown to be an adhesion factor of C. difficile and that anti-SlpA antisera has 
been shown to reduce the adhesion of C. difficile this study tries to discover antibody fragments that 
block adhesion of C. difficile to the host. Antibiotic treatment disrupts the microbiota of the patient 
and the idea of blocking the adhesion of C. difficile with specific antibody fragments is to avoid that. 
Specific binding will only prevent the colonization of C. difficile and keep the microbiota of the 
patient intact. By keeping the microbiota intact, the possibility of recurrent infection is lowered. 
Another aspect of the idea is that several C. difficile ribotypes show resistance to antibiotics which 
can be circumvented by using antibody fragments instead of antibiotics. One way to discover 
antibodies and antibody fragments is by phage display.    
Phage display 
Phage display is a high throughput in vitro selection method used to discover antibodies specific to 
different antigens. It was first demonstrated in 1985 by George P. Smith (Smith, 1985). He 
incorporated foreign DNA encoding peptides into the chromosome of M13 phages. Five years later 
antibody phage display selection was first described by McCafferty et al., 1990 who incorporated 
genes encoding an antibody binding domain into M13 by which an antibody library was created for 
the selection of antigen specific antibodies.  
To fully understand the phage display technique, one must have knowledge about antibodies, the 
M13phage, and phage libraries. These topics will therefore be enlightened in the following sections.

Antibodies 
Antibodies are Y-shaped biomolecules that can bind with high specificity to a wide range of antigens 
which include both protein and non-protein targets. An antibody consists of two identical light-chains 
and two identical heavy chains with a variable and a constant domain. The antigen-binding site of an 
antibody is composed of six complementary determining regions (CDRs), (Figure 2). Three within 
the heavy chain variable domain and three within the light-chain variable domain (Hoogenboom, 
2005). The immune system creates a large collection of antibody binding sites by combinatorial 
assembly of germline-encoded segments. By doing this a huge repertoire of naïve B-cell lymphocytes 
each expressing a unique antibody on their surface is created. Exposure to antigen select those 
lymphocytes from this repertoire which produce antigen-reactive antibodies and trigger the 
incorporation of somatic hypermutation which allows subsequent selection of mutations that improve 
the affinity of the antibody for antigen. By these approaches, the immune system in humans is able 
to create thousands of millions of different antibodies from which suitable antigen-binding antibodies 
are rapidly selected. This is a very powerful system that scientists have been investigating for decades 
and tried to find methods that could recreate the system using recombinant approaches. One of these 
methods is phage display (Hoogenboom, 2005).

A unique type of antibodies was found in the serum of camels called heavy chain only antibodies 
(HCAbs). The variable domains of these HCAbs are called variable heavy chain (VHH) also known 
as nanobodies, or single-domain antibodies (sdAbs) and they are the type of antibody fragment used 
in this study, (Figure 2). sdAbs represent the smallest naturally derived antigen-binding functional 
fragment and they maintain their affinity and specificity comparable to full-size antibodies. sdAbs 
are small (15kDa) and they are comprised of 4 conserved sequence stretches which surround three 
hypervariable CDRs: CDR1, CDR2, and CDR3. CDR3 is the major contributor to antigen binding 
since at least 60-80% of the antigen contact is through this region. Due to their small size sdAbs are 
also able to reach receptor clefts or binding pockets more easily (Steeland et al., 2016). 
The small size also allows rapid tissue penetration of sdAbs which includes the blood-brain barrier 
in comparison to full size antibodies. Another advantage of sdAbs compared to full size antibodies is 
that the overall structure is more hydrophilic which contributes to high stability, solubility, and 
resistance to aggregation. Due to this hydrophilicity and the single chain structure sdAbs can be 
efficiently produced in bacteria, yeast or mammalian cells which enables large-scale production and 
makes them more economical to produce compared to full size antibodies. sdAbs are resistant to high

M13 phage 
The M13 phage (Figure 3) belongs to a group of phages referred to as Ff phages which only infect 
Escherichia coli strains that express the F pilus since the adsorption of the phage requires binding of 
a phage coat protein to the tip of the F pilus on the bacterium. The M13 phage establishes a chronic 
infection in the bacterium and continuously release new phages. The genome of the M13 phage 
consists of single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) with a length of 6407 bp that consist of 9 genes encoding 
11 different proteins. Five of these proteins are coat proteins G3P, G6P, G7P, G8P and G9P. G3P is 
an attachment protein involved in the adsorption and extrusion of the phage and the capsid protein 
G8P is the most abundant protein which forms an envelope around the chromosome. The rest of the 
proteins G1P, G2P, G4P, G5P, G10P and G11P are involved in the replication and assembly of the 
phage (Ledsgaard et al., 2018).  
 
 
The first stage in the M13 infection is the adsorption process where the N2 domain of the G3P protein 
binds the tip of a F pilus on the surface of the E. coli host. This binding allows the N1 domain of the 
G3P protein to bind TolA which functions as a co-receptor on the surface of the bacterium and 
mediates the uptake of the phage genome (Ledsgaard et al., 2018).

Once the chromosome of the phage has been injected into the bacterium the host DNA synthesis 
machinery will start synthesizing a DNA (-) strand complementary to the ssDNA (+) strand of the 
M13 phage chromosome. These two strands will form a supercoiled double-stranded DNA phage 
chromosome termed the replicative form. The G2P protein binds the 5´-end, and the genome is 
replicated from the 3´-end using the (-) strand as template. The (+) strand is re-circularized and ready 
to be converted into the replicative form or to be packed into new M13 phages. G5P will bind ssDNA 
causing a more rod-shaped appearance of the ssDNA. Then a pore is formed by a complex of the 
G1P, G11P and G4P proteins in the membrane and the phage genome is translocated through this 
pore, while the phage coat G8P is assembled. At last, the G3P and G6P proteins will collaborate in 
the release of the bacterium (Ledsgaard et al., 2018).   
 
Several different antibody fragments can be displayed on the surface of the M13 phage, (Figure 2), 
which permit selection of antigen-recognizing phages. These antibody fragments are fused to the G3P 
protein of the M13 phage with a trypsin cleavage site and are thereby displayed on the surface of the 
phage. By cloning large numbers of genes encoding antibody fragments it is possible to create large 
phage display libraries from which antibody fragments can be selected (Kristensen & Winter, 1998; 
Ledsgaard et al., 2018).      
 
In the beginning antibody genes were cloned directly into the phage genome with all the genes needed 
for infection, replication, assembly and budding together with the antibody-G3P fusion to create 
phage libraries. Three to five copies of the G3P protein are normally incorporated on the phage and 
multivalent display of antibody-G3P proteins could therefore occur.  
An alternative to this approach is phagemid libraries. Phagemid libraries consists of phagemid vectors 
which use three elements: an antibiotic marker for selection and propagation of the plasmid, the gene 
encoding the antibody-G3P protein, and the regions of the M13 chromosome required for 
amplification and the production of the (+) DNA strand which can be packed into new phages. To 
produce functional phage particles that display the antibody-G3P complex, E. coli with a phagemid 
need to be infected with a “helper phage” that contains the complete M13 genome encoding all the 
proteins needed for capsid production, assembly, replication and budding of the phage. The “helper 
phage” is thereby providing the phagemid with the proteins needed for amplification of new phages.  
Upon infection, wild type G3P from the helper phage will compete with the phagemid encoding the 
antibody-G3P protein for incorporation on the phage. 90% of the phage population will be “bald

phages” and not display the antibody-G3P protein. The majority of the phage particles that do display 
an antibody-G3P protein will only have a single copy (Ledsgaard et al., 2018).  
Another advantage – despite only displaying a single antibody-G3P copy - of phagemid libraries is 
that higher transformation efficiencies can be achieved with phagemid vectors compared to phage 
vectors. Phagemids are therefore more typically used in libraries because larger libraries can be 
constructed (Ledsgaard et al., 2018).      
  
“Bald phages” contribute with noise when doing selection and they are therefore made non-infectious 
by introducing a trypsin cleavage site in the G3P protein in the “helper phage” that make “bald 
phages” non-infectious when treated with trypsin. On the other hand, phages carrying the antibody-
G3P protein will be resistant to trypsin cleavage since the trypsin cleavage site is located between the 
antibody and the G3P. Upon trypsin cleavage the binding between the antibody and the G3P protein 
will be disrupted and the phage with an intact G3P protein will be infective and still carry the 
phagemid encoding the binding antibody. Thereby, there is a direct relation between the phenotype 
and its cognate genotype through the phage (Kristensen & Winter, 1998; Ledsgaard et al., 2018). This 
relation is very important in phage display, and it is important to consider when constructing phage 
or phagemid libraries since a high diversity of phages that encode and display different antibodies are 
desired.      
 
Phage libraries 
A phage or phagemid library is a collection of phages displaying antibodies with a high diversity on 
the surface. As described earlier the antibody binding specificity is predominantly located in the CDR 
regions and the diversity of antibody libraries are therefore focused within these (Mandrup et al., 
2013).  
There are different categories of libraries ranging from immunized over naïve and synthetic libraries. 
An immunized library is based on the natural affinity maturation of the antibodies by the host, and 
one would therefore expect that immunized libraries contain multiple high affinity binders against 
the antigen used in the immunization (Mandrup et al., 2013).  
On the other site naïve libraries have not undergone antibody affinity maturation but are based on 
genetic material obtained before exposure to immunogenic material. A naïve library is not supposed 
to contain bias for binders against any antigen and should also in theory improve the chances of

isolating a wider variety of binders including antibodies directed against the host´s own molecules 
(Mandrup et al., 2013). 
In synthetic libraries, CDR diversity has been introduced artificially by which it is possible to 
circumvent any bias introduced by the host immune system by having full control of the amino acid 
composition in the CDRs. This approach also allows specific introduction of diversity at defined 
positions known to be important in antigen-antibody interactions. Semi-synthetic libraries with 
different combinations of synthetically and naturally host derived diversity have also been made 
(Mandrup et al., 2013).      
 
In this study a synthetic sdAb phagemid library named Predator created by Mandrup et al., 2013 was 
used. This library is based on the HEL4 domain antibody and has been constructed with restricted 
randomizations at 4 positions in the CDR2 and 7 positions in the CDR3. A unique feature of this 
library is that the CDR3 region mimic the natural human immune response by designating amino 
acids known to be prevalent in functional antibodies to the diversity in the CDR3 region. No 
randomization was introduced in CDR1 because of the importance of certain amino acids regarding 
aggregation and the folding characteristics of HEL4 (Mandrup et al., 2013). HEL4 is an isolated 
human heavy chain variable domain (VH) with properties similar to those of VHH which are well 
expressed in bacteria (Jespers et al., 2004).

The randomizations were introduced by the use of trinucleotide synthesized oligonucleotides. The 
library was designed to have a diversity which reflect the amino acid composition of CDR regions 
from known functional human antibody clones. Additionally, knowledge of binding characteristics 
from specific amino acids were also included in the diversity design of the library, (Figure 4). The 
library was also designed to be modular due to unique restriction sites e.g., in the surrounding regions 
of all three CDRs. The library was based on the HEL4 domain antibody scaffold which is known to 
be aggregation resistant and to have a high solubility (Jespers et al., 2004). A hydrophilic mutation at 
position 29 was also introduced to improve the solubility of the antibody. The Predator library was 
constructed in the pHEN1 vector which means that the library includes an ampicillin resistance gene, 
the f1 phage origin of replication (f1 is a Ff phage almost identical to M13 (Webster, 1999)), most of 
the G3P and the origin of replication from the original pHEN1 vector, (Figure 5). Phage particles 
were produced by infection with KM13 helper phage and the functional diversity of the Predator 
library has been shown to consist of 6.2 x 107 unique clones (Mandrup et al., 2013).

Phage display and different selection methods  
Now that antibodies, the M13phage, and phage libraries have been enlightened the performance of 
phage display with different selection methods can now be explained.  
 
As described earlier phage display is a way to discover antibodies specific to different antigens, 
(Figure 6). The first step is to add a phage library to a vial where antigen is presented. Antigen can 
be presented by direct coating where hydrophobic regions of the antigen bind the vial by which it is 
immobilized. Another option is to immobilize the antigen by a capturing system such as streptavidin-
biotin where the antigen is tagged with biotin that binds the streptavidin coated vial. The next step is 
the binding of phages which has the highest affinity sdAb to the antigen. The vial is thereafter washed 
to remove nonbinding phages and the binding phages are eluted with trypsin. The trypsin digestion 
also makes bald phages non-infective. The eluted binding phages infect an E. coli (e.g., TG1) culture 
together with the “helper phage” by which the binding phages are amplified. If further rounds of 
selection are performed, the binding phages are precipitated, and a new round of selection is initiated 
together with the following steps. Usually, 1-3 rounds of selection are performed with the amplified 
Figure 6. The steps in a phage display selection experiment. (A) addition of phage library
to an antigen coated vial. (B) binding of phages that display high affinity sdAb. (C)
unspecific binders are washed away. (D) antigen specific phages are eluted using trypsin
digestion. (E) eluted phages infect E.coli together with the “helper phage” by which a new 
panning round can be initiated to further accumulation of phages or the binding can
evaluated by ELISA. (Ledsgaard et al., 2018).

phages to accumulate binding phages. The results can be evaluated by ELISA (enzyme-linked 
immunosorbent assay) (Ledsgaard et al., 2018).  
Another way to perform selections is to do in solution selection. To isolate high affinity sdAbs 
attached to phages by this selection method, an antigen with a tag such as biotin is required to pull 
down and bind the phage. By in solution selection the antigen maintains proper folding and 
conformation as the native antigen, which is not always the case when doing direct coating. Direct 
coating could also diminish the accessibility of relevant epitopes (Ledsgaard et al., 2018).    
 
As described earlier one way of evaluating binders after the selection is ELISA. ELISA can be 
conducted in several ways. One way is polyclonal ELISA where a mixture of clones is screened. 
Polyclonal ELISA can be used to evaluate the titers after each round of selection. Another way is 
monoclonal ELISA where single clones are screened. ELISA can both be conducted with the 
sdAb:phage complex or with sdAb protein alone. This is done by cloning the phagemid into a vector 
and express the protein.

Aim of the project 
The overall aim of this study was to identify sdAbs that can bind adhesion factor SlpA and thereby 
block the adhesion of C. difficile ribotype 12, 18 and 27 to the host. To identify sdAbs phage display 
experiments with the Predator library was conducted. SlpAs from the different ribotypes were 
recombinantly expressed in E. coli, purified by IMAC and then used as antigen in phage display 
selections. Another aim was to evaluate two different ELISA screening methods “polyclonal cloning 
followed by protein-based screening” and “monoclonal phage screening”.

Purification 
Osmotic shock 
To disrupt the outer cell membrane to release periplasmic proteins osmotic shock was used. 
A post induction aliquot was stored for SDS-PAGE and the O/N culture was centrifuged at 6000g for 
10 minutes. The pellet was resuspended in ice-cold hypertonic solution and incubated on ice for 1 
hour. To prevent sedimentation the tube was inverted every 5 minutes. The supernatant was collected

but inverted every 5 minutes. The supernatant was collected (fraction 2) and stored by centrifugation 
at 8000g for 10 min at 4 ℃ (an aliquot was saved for SDS-PAGE). The two supernatant fractions 
were pooled. 
Dialysis 
To remove small molecule impurities from the protein after the osmotic shock, and to introduce the 
buffer used in IMAC, dialysis was used. The 3,500 molecular weight cutoff (MWCO) dialysis 
membrane was hydrated for 2 minutes in binding buffer then the pooled fractions were added to the 
membrane and excess air was removed. The membrane was left in 2L binding buffer with stirring for 
2 hours at room temperature, then the binding buffer was exchanged with 2L new binding buffer and 
left with stirring O/N at 4℃.

His-tagged recombinant SlpA proteins and a buffer containing imidazole was therefore used.

Selection on Maxisorp tubes

at 220 rpm until an OD600 at 0.4 was reached.

precipitation was done (see sections further down) and these sub-libraries were then used for the next 
selection. It should also be mentioned that no negative selection was conducted during the second

Different ELISA screening techniques  
One aim of this project was to test and optimize a new protocol for the screening process of potential 
sdAb binders, “polyclonal cloning followed by protein-based screening”, and compare it with a more 
established one, “monoclonal phage screening”. This test was only done with SlpA-18 and only with 
one round of selection where the washing conditions was 10xPBS. All the SlpAs were screened with 
the “polyclonal cloning followed by protein-based screening” approach.     
Monoclonal phage screening  
Day 1 – Monoclonal colony picking: 
Single colonies on the plates from the phage display selection were picked and inoculated in 96-
deepwell plates containing 600 µL 2xYT with 2% glucose and 100 µg/ml ampicillin. Some wells 
throughout the plates were not inoculated to function as negative controls. The plates were sealed 
with gas permeable seal, and incubated at 37℃ 800 rpm O/N. 
 
Day 2 – Monoclonal phage rescue:  
2 µL of the O/N culture was transferred to new 96-deepweel plates containing 200 µL 2xYT with 2% 
glucose and 100 µg/ml ampicillin. The plates were grown for 2 hours at 37℃ 220 rpm. After 2 hours 
growth 25µL 2xYT with 2% glucose, 100 µg/ml ampicillin and KM13 helper phage was added to the 
plates and the plates were grown for one more hour. Then the plates were centrifuged at 1800g for 10 
minutes and decantated. The pellet was resuspended in 2xYT containing 100 µg/mL ampicillin and 
50 µg/mL kanamycin then grown for 42 hours at 30℃ 800 rpm.  
 
Day 3 – ELISA coating

in PBS and incubated O/N at 4℃.

Phage display  
Now that it was confirmed that the correct SlpAs were expressed and purified the phage display 
experiments with the Predator library could be initiated to discover sdAb binders for the SlpA-12, 
SlpA-18, and SlpA-27 antigens. Since SlpA-18 was the protein that there were expressed and purified 
the most of, that protein was the first protein chosen for phage display.  
 
For the first round of selection 100µg/mL of SlpA-18 was used for positive selection and for the 
negative selection (random His-tagged protein) 80µg/mL. the washing conditions was 10x PBST20 
0.1% + 5x PBS. By doing that too few colonies were observed. The negative selection could be the 
problem and therefore a new selection was made this time with 100µg/mL of negative antigen. The 
washing conditions was the same, but this time two selections were made. One with negative selection 
first and one without negative selection. The result from this experiment was that more colonies were 
observed on plates where there had been negative selection first which was not as expected. The 
conclusion was that a mistake had happened or that the antibiotic was not well distributed in the agar 
plates. Therefore, new plates were made, and the experiment was remade, but this time green colonies 
were observed due to a contamination. A new TG1 culture was grown and reinfected with the eluted 
phages from the previous experiment. Different 2xTY plates with ampicillin were also tested together 
with 2xTY plates containing carbenicillin, but green colonies would still appear. A contamination 
source was identified to be the PBS, and a new experiment with sterile PBS and same antigen 
concentration and same washing conditions was initiated. Only 5 colonies were seen, but no green

sdAb-based ELISA screening  
After three rounds of selection with SlpA-12, SlpA-18, and SlpA-27 respectively, the obtained 
phagemids were cloned into the expression vector pSANG10-3F and transformed into BL21(DE3).

was used to screen the binding of monoclonal clones. A secondary HRP conjugated anti-FLAG 
antibody was used in the sdAb-based ELISA. The maxisorp plates were coated with 10µg/mL of the 
SlpA antigens. To verify that the sdAbs only bound the SlpA antigens two additional ELISA 
screenings were made. One where the maxisorp plates were coated with 1µg/mL of the negative 
antigen and another one where the maxisorp plates were coated with 2% MPBS. By doing these 
screenings true binders that only bound the antigen were easier to select for further investigation.

Discussion 
Since antibiotic resistance have been observed in several different C. difficile ribotypes new treatment 
methods are needed. In this study, an alternative treatment method has been suggested. The idea is 
that adhesion of C. difficile can be prevented by blocking the adhesion factor SlpA with sdAbs. 
Antibiotic treatment disrupts the microbiota of the patient and the idea of blocking the adhesion of C. 
difficile with specific sdAbs is to avoid that. Specific binding will only prevent the colonization of C. 
difficile and keep the microbiota of the patient intact. By keeping the microbiota intact, the possibility 
of recurrent infection is lowered. 
The reason for choosing sdAbs is that they are small, which – together with their robust chemical 
entities – give raise to rapid tissue penetration including the gastro-intestinal tract. The fact that sdAbs 
can be administered in the gastro-intestinal tract make them ideal candidates in this study. 
Furthermore, sdAbs can be produced more efficiently in different organisms compared to full size 
antibodies. sdAbs are also easier to engineer and can be linked together or with other molecules for 
the creation of multivalent molecules  (Steeland et al., 2016; Wu et al., 2017). 
 
In this study, the discovery of sdAbs against the adhesion factor SlpA from the C. difficile ribotypes 
12, 18 and 27 have been investigated. 28 clones expressing sdAbs against SlpA-18 have been 
identified and from these it was possible to sequence 18 clones. It was observed that 15 clones shared 
the same sequence, sdAb-SlpA18-1, 2 clones shared the same sequence sdAb-SlpA18-2, and that the 
control sdAb-SlpA18-3 had another sequence. This means that despite the large diversity of the 
Predator library it was possible to accumulate good binding clones by doing 3 selections with slightly 
harsher washing conditions, as expected. sdAb-SlpA18-3 share its CDR3 region with sdAb-SlpA18-

be a non-binder to SlpA-18 and was chosen as a control. Regarding the binding of these clones in the 
ELISA dilution assay, it was noticed that sdAb-SlpA18-1 had a good binding to SlpA-18 and did not 
bind the negative antigen or MPBS. sdAb-SlpA18-1 is considered as a suitable strong binder to SlpA-
18 and would therefore be the best candidate for further specificity and affinity determinations. The 
cross-reactivity related to different ribotype structures should also be evaluated. sdAb-SlpA-2 had a 
decreased binding to SlpA-18 and is considered a weaker binder to SlpA-18. sdAb-SlpA18-3 had a 
more decreased binding than sdAb-SlpA18-2 to SlpA-18 as expected. This confirms that the CDR3 
region contributes the most to antigen binding of sdAbs since sdAb-SlpA-18-3 has a different CDR3 
than sdAb-SlpA18-1, but the same CDR2 as sdAb-SlpA18-1 (D’Angelo et al., 2018). sdAb-SlpA18-

Unfortunately, it was not possible to identify the same amount of binding clones against SlpA-12 and

high phage concentration was added in the second round of selection.  
For SlpA-12 six clones were analyzed in an ELISA dilution assay. They all showed similar limited

introduction of noise because of too long development time or any other variation an ELISA can 
introduce such as insufficient blocking, non-homogenous coating, or differences in the washing step.

as a binder to SlpA-27, since it also showed an increased binding to SlpA-27 compared to negative

different CDR2 and CDR3 regions.  
If more clones had been screened there is a possibility that more clones expressing sdAbs with affinity

after the first round of selection. That would have been very antigen demanding, but by doing that, 
no diversity would have been lost. By doing 3 rounds of selection with harsher washing conditions 
the amount of good binding clones should be enriched, by which the chance of picking a good binding 
clone should be higher.     
 
There has previously been investigated in the discovery of sdAbs against SLPs. Kandalaft et al., 2015 
has isolated a panel of specific sdAbs against SLPs. They immunized a llama with C. difficile ribotype 
12 and 27, isolated the lymphocytes from which a phage display library was made. They used phage 
ELISA to identify individual phage displaying sdAbs with specificity to SlpA-12 and SlpA-27. The 
phagemids were cloned, expressed, and purified by IMAC and the binding of the sdAbs to 
immobilized SlpA-12 and SlpA-27 were determined by surface plasmon resonance (SPR). Whole 
cell ELISA was used to determine the ability of each sdAb to bind different C. difficile ribotypes.

They found that all sdAbs bound SlpA-27 and two of them bound SlpA-12. Furthermore, they 
identified one sdAb which was able to recognize all different SLPs tested.  
Since the sdAbs identified by Kandalaft et al., 2015 comes from a llama immunized phage library the 
change of creating an unwanted immune response is higher compared to a humanized library as the 
Predator library that is based on the HEL4 domain.  
 
This study has also investigated a screening technique called “polyclonal cloning followed by protein-
based screening” and compared it with a more established screening technique “monoclonal phage 
screening”. Both techniques are ELISA screening techniques and they both screen monoclonal sdAbs, 
but the “polyclonal cloning followed by protein-based screening” approach uses expressed sdAbs 
from BL21(DE3) as the primary antibody whereas in the “monoclonal phage screening” approach a 
sdAb-phage complex is used. Expressed sdAbs resemble the binding of SlpAs better by not being 
attached to a phage since sdAbs attached to phages can lead to conformational changes of the sdAbs.  
By doing the cloning and the expression first, as in “polyclonal cloning followed by protein-based 
screening” we know that all binders found by this approach can be expressed in BL21(DE3). By 
doing “monoclonal phage screening” the cloning and expression are done after the ELISA, and there 
is a chance to find a binder which is hard to express. If binders are found and expressed another 
ELISA should be initiated which means that two ELISAs are needed when doing “monoclonal phage 
screening” compared to “polyclonal cloning followed by protein-based screening” where only one 
ELISA is needed. This also suggest the use of “polyclonal cloning followed by protein-based 
screening” instead of the “monoclonal phage screening” approach.   
Another advantage by using the “polyclonal cloning followed by protein-based screening” is that the 
secondary antibody used in this approach is anti-FLAG which has a better binding to the tag compared 
to anti-M13. This binding is important to have a correct detection of sdAbs. 
Before this study was initiated, there were downsides of the “monoclonal phage screening” approach 
which was that the reproducibility of the phages was dissimilar and the phage concentration in the 
wells were thereby different which led to unreliable results. To accommodate this problem the phages 
in this study grew for 42 hours. 
Another problem was that other group members in our group have had trouble with the reproducibility 
of their results when using the “monoclonal phage screening” method. The hope was that these 
problems could be solved with the “polyclonal cloning followed by protein-based screening” 
approach, but this screening technique just introduced a new similar problem. Instead of not knowing

the phage concentration in each well, the new problem introduced was that the protein concentration 
was unknown due to differences in the expression of the proteins.  
Another problem by using the “polyclonal cloning followed by protein-based screening” is, that by 
doing PCR amplification of the phagemid pool bias is introduced. An alternative to circumvent this 
problem could be to use a restriction enzyme-based cloning - which will not introduce any bias - 
instead of PCR amplification. The problem with this approach is that the cloning efficiency decreases 
which could result in the loss of binders. Despite the fact that PCR amplification is introducing bias 
it could compensate for the loss of binders by using the restriction enzyme based cloning approach. 
The restriction enzyme cloning approach has not been tested yet.  
 
In general, it is uncertain how well proteins are expressed in 96-deepwell plates. One way to achieve 
a more homogeneous expression is to use autoinduction media instead of IPTG induction. By using 
autoinduction media there is no need to monitor the cell density since the induction will start when 
the cultures have reached a certain cell density which can differ in time dependent on how well the 
bacteria grow. By manually induction with IPTG the induction happens at different metabolic states 
throughout the bacteria cultures which leads to unequal protein expression (Fox & Blommel, 2009). 
The reason for not using an autoinduction system in this study was that other group members have 
had trouble with the expression of proteins with such settings.  
An optimized way of screening sdAbs in ELISA would be to express the sdAbs in a setting that 
provides the cultures with optimal amount of aeriation and to induce the cell cultures at the same 
metabolic state either by monitoring the cell density of all cultures and induce at the same metabolic 
state or to use autoinduction media. To further optimize the ELISA screening the proteins should be 
purified and the concentration of sdAbs should be the same.  
During this study there have been several realizations regarding the optimization of both phage 
display and ELISA. It is important to use autoclaved PBS during phage display to avoid 
contamination. The washing stringencies when using the Predator library should not be so harsh 
compared to other libraries (or at least when using the antigens in this study). The same antigen 
concentration should be used when doing positive and negative selections in phage display, and in 
ELISA screenings. It is also important to include both positive and negative controls when doing 
ELISA screenings including dilution assays.

The ELISA screening method has the advantage of being highly specific and sensitive because of the 
antigen-antibody reaction, but it also has some disadvantages. One disadvantage is the risk of false 
results due to insufficient blocking of the microtiter plate. Another disadvantage is that the ELISA 
screening method is a multistep process, which requires more work compared to other methods. In 
addition, the ELISA screening technique is not as efficient as other methods since it requires a lot of 
antigen (Sakamoto et al., 2018; Vaisocherová et al., 2009). This is a problem when screening several 
samples e.g., when the binding of several clones from a phage display experiment is screened. This 
is also the reason for using different antigen concentration in the phage display selections compared 
to the ELISA screenings. In this study 100 µg/mL of positive antigen was used in the phage display 
selection whereas only 10 µg/mL was used in the ELISA screening. This could influence the 
discovery of binders since less signal would be detected at a lower antigen concentration.       
As an alternative to ELISA SPR could be used. SPR is a technique used to characterize biomolecular 
binding in terms of their kinetics, affinity, and epitope specificity. It is a label-free technique that 
allows measurements of native molecules as opposed to the ELISA screenings in this study, where a 
secondary anti-FLAG-HRP antibody was used. Additionally, SPR is an automated high throughput 
method with a low antigen consumption compared to ELISA. Which allows the screening of many 
binders without the need of large amounts of antigen (Knowling et al., 2020).    
 
The next step in this project would be to express the sdAb-SlpA18-1 shown to be a strong binder to 
SlpA-18 from one of the clones that has that sequence. The sdAb should be purified with protein A 
and a new ELISA dilution assay with prober controls should be made with known sdAb concentration 
for apparent KD measurement. The specificity and affinity of sdAb-SlpA18-1 should also be 
determined, together with the cross-reactivity related to different ribotypes. 
Another next step will be to do a whole cell ELISA with C. difficile to see if the sdAb-SlpA18-1 
would still bind the SlpA protein in its whole cell environment and conformation. This would give 
an indication for the binding of sdAb-SlpA18-1 in a natural environment, but the actual binding and 
the ability to prevent C. difficile infection in patients will need further in vitro and in vivo models. 
  
Whole cells could also be used when doing phage display. Instead of doing selections with 
recombinant expressed SlpAs, another possibility is to do a whole cell panning with C. difficile where 
whole cells are immobilized instead of a protein. This approach would increase the likelihood of 
discovering sdAb binders that would bind to SlpAs in a whole cell environment and conformation,

but it would also increase the change of finding irrelevant binders (Alfaleh et al., 2017). This can only 
be done in a class 3 laboratory which was not available in this study.    
It is also a possibility to use the toxins TcdA and TcdB or the spores from C. difficile as antigen in 
the selection process when doing phage display. If such sdAbs were discovered by these approaches 
the outcome in patients could be to decrease the pathogenicity of the toxins and limit the reoccurrence 
of C. difficile (Hussack et al., 2011; Pizarro-Guajardo et al., 2017).       
Another phage display strategy is to find cross-binding sdAbs that can bind several SlpA molecules 
from different ribotypes. This could be done by doing a selection with a SlpA protein from one 
ribotype, do phage rescue and phage precipitation and with this sub-library do a new selection with a 
new SlpA protein from a different ribotype. This process could then be repeated until the number of 
desired SlpAs that the sdAb should cross-bind to is reached.  
Such a sdAb would be expected to have low affinity for the different SlpA proteins. A way to 
circumvent that is to link single sdAbs together into one molecule. Each of them should have high 
affinity to one SlpA protein from different ribotypes. Such a molecule would have high affinity to 
several SlpA proteins. This approach could be conducted with the sdAbs in this study.

Conclusion 
It can be concluded that it was possible to clone, express and purify the adhesion factor SlpA from C. 
difficile from the ribotypes 12, 18, and 27 and use them for phage display with the predator library to 
obtain human sdAbs. A comparison of two different ELISA screening methods “polyclonal cloning 
followed by protein-based screening” and “monoclonal phage ELISA” have been assessed. The 
conclusion was that the “polyclonal cloning followed by protein-based screening” was the superior 
ELISA screening technique since more binders were identified. It can also be concluded that it was 
possible to identify and sequence 18 clones expressing sdAbs with high affinity to SlpA-18 from 
which 15 shared the same sequence. It was only possible to identify one clone expressing sdAbs 
against SlpA-27 and it was not possible to identify any interesting sdAb binders against SlpA-12.
