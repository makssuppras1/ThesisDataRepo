Scope of Thesis 
This project will describe physico-chemical models applying in bio-kinetics and Wastewater treatment 
(WWT) systems.  Acid-base chemistry is the common physico-chemical model that is applied in the two 
systems. Therefore, the project is divided in two parts. In the first part prediction of acid-base equilibria is 
performed in a bio-catalytic process, more precisely the catalysis by a transaminase enzyme of the 
transfer of an amine group from a donor molecule to a pro-chiral acceptor ketone for producing chiral 
amines. The work of the first part will comprise the following tasks:  
 
Introduction of Monitoring and Control describes briefly chiral amine production process and the 
position of acid-base equilibria in the process.  
 
Background theory of acid/base chemistry provides the basic theory of pH calculation with respect to 
ideal solution.  
 
Methods of theoretically model development in MATLAB program regarding titration processes 
of hydrochloric acid and tree specified amines. The theoretically model are validated by manual 
titration experiments.  
In the second part the physico-chemical modeling is performed focusing on wastewater treatment system. 
The acid/base chemistry model regarding non-ideal solution is applied to describe pH variations in 
wastewater treatment systems. Further, the physico-chemical models are applied to predict cationic/anionic 
behavior in terms of dissolution/ precipitation processes in wastewater treatment systems. The main 
objective of the second part of this project is to develop and apply physicochemical models to investigate 
and simulate chemical precipitation processes with the aim of wastewater treatment. The work of the first 
part will comprise the following tasks: 
 
Introduction regarding physicochemical models and development based on the literature review. 
 
Methods and materials describing the experiment procedures for validation of physicochemical 
modelling applied in simulation.  
 
Physicochemical modeling task describing the model development with respect to reactions in liquid 
and liquid-solid phases. The model corrects for ionic strength via the Davies approach to consider 
chemical activities instead of molar concentration. In addition, the proposed approach also takes into 
account the ion pairs important to wastewater treatment. The general acid-base equilibria as well as total 
species contribution balances are formulated as a set of differential algebraic equations (DAEs) and

solved simultaneously with the model's ordinary differential equations (ODEs) using a new multi-
dimensional version of the Newton-Raphson algorithm.  
 
Results of a base case precipitation process is introduced and discussed followed by seven scenarios 
describing the effect of changing selected parameters.

Abstract 
Most of important chemical reactions are occurring in aquatic environment. Chemical equilibria and particularly 
acid-base equilibria play a key role in aquatic chemistry. This project provides a comprehensive treatment of 
aquatic solutions and specially acid-base equilibria. In this project the importance of acid-base equilibria is 
investigated in two different industrial case studies. In the first case study, the application of acid-base equilibria 
is investigated for a continuous synthetic process of a chiral amine as a key organic component in the 
pharmaceutical industry. The amine must be stored at the pH level of 3 in the last unit of the process. For this 
aim, the fundamentals of acid-base chemistry, and the pH calculation procedures are described and the titration 
procedure is modeled to predict interactions of hydrochloric acid and tree specified amines, i.e. 1-methyl-3-
phenylpropylamine (MPPA), isopropylamine (IPA) and methylbenzylamine (MBA). The specified amines are 
modeled as titrators for titration of HCl in different concentrations. Then, the models that have been derived are 
validated by using experimental data. Furthermore, a model is also developed to predict the required amount of 
acid corresponding to the input of amine to maintain the acidity of the reservoir at the desired pH level of 3. In 
the second case study a generalized physicochemical modeling framework, including acid-base equilibria, is 
applied aiming at multi-minerals precipitation processes in the wastewater treatment (WWT) field. The relevant 
physicochemical models are describing liquid phase equilibrium reactions and liquid-solid phases ion-exchange 
kinetic reactions. According to the non-ideal behavior of wastewater, the Debye-Hückel theory is applied to 
calculate chemical activity of ions. Equilibrium governed reactions are defined mathematically by a set of non-
linear algebraic equations (AEs) that include one law of mass action for each reaction and one species

context of dynamic titration of an aqueous solution containing calcium, phosphate, magnesium and inorganic 
nitrogen. The model predictions indicate that struvite followed by dicalcium phosphate dihydrate  (DCPD) are 
the main precipitated solids under the base case conditions. The base case pH, saturation indices and ion 
concentration profiles are validated by available experimental data from the literature. Additional scenarios are 
defined to investigate in detail the influence of the base case parameters, such as pH, supersaturation, initial 
concentration of ions and presence of foreign ions, on the precipitation process.

Part 1: pH Monitoring and Control  
1.1. Introduction of Monitoring and Control  
In this section a brief introduction of pH monitoring and control aimed for chiral amines storage is provided. 
Optically pure chiral amines are important chemical building blocks which are basic components for organic 
synthesis. These components are mostly used in the pharmaceutical industry for imparting desirable biological 
activity to chemical entities (Said 2002). Optically pure chiral amines can be synthetized via chemical 
transformation and bio-catalytic processes. Transaminases (also known as aminotransferases) are biocatalysts 
which have proven to be suitable to catalyze the transfer of an amine group from a donor molecule to a pro-
chiral acceptor ketone for producing chiral amines (Committee 2010). ω-Transaminase (ωTA) is a biocatalyst 
that facilitates the transfer of an amino group of an amino donor such as isopropylamine (IPA), alanine (Ala) or 
methylbenzylamine (MBA) to a ketone, for example benzyl-acetone (BA), to produce 1-methyl-3-
phenylpropylamine (MPPA) as an optically pure chiral amine (Yun & Kim 2008). The reaction mechanism 
using isopropylamine (IPA) and alanine (Ala) is illustrated in Fig. 1.1 
 
Fig. 1.1: Synthesis of 1-methyl-3-phenylpropylamine (MPPA) using ω‐transaminase as 
biocatalyst and benzylacetone (BA) as amine acceptor substrate. Isopropylamine (IPA) or 
alanine (Ala) acts as amine donor, which forms acetone (Ace) or pyruvic acid (pyr), 
respectively. pyridoxamine 5’-phosphate (PMP) and pyridoxal-5’-phosphate(PLP) are co-
factors in the reaction (Figure courtesy of Søren Heintz). 
Application of in-situ product removal and in-situ substrate supply strategies enhance the amine product yield by 
shifting the equilibrium position. The latter strategies are needed due to several limitations like low solubility, 
inhibition by co-products and unfavorable thermodynamics (Committee 2010) . In practice, in-situ product

1.3. Background Theory of Acid/Base Chemistry  
For the first time, Svante Arrhennius (Hoff & Bel 2002) recognized the essential nature of acids and bases. 
Based on his electrolyte dissociation idea, he postulated in 1884 that acids produce hydrogen ions in aqueous 
solution, and bases produce hydroxide ions. Although these ideas are fundamentally correct, it applies only to 
aqueous solution and allows for only the bases that contain a hydroxide ion. In 1923, a more general definition 
of acid and base comes to life independently by Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted in Denmark and Martin Lowry in 
England. In terms of the Brønsted- Lowry definition an acid is a compound that can donate a proton, and a base 
is a compound that can receive a proton. Compounds that donate proton are Brønsted-Lowry acids and 
compounds that accept a proton are Brønsted-Lowry bases. The Brønsted-Lowry definition refers to the 
formation of conjugate acids and conjugate bases, produced by the transfer of a proton from the acid to the base. 
Once an acid has given up a proton, the remaining part is a conjugated base, and the conjugated acid is formed 
when the proton is transferred to the base. In 1923, the American physical chemist G.N Lewis provided a 
modified theory to describe acid and base (Zumdahl 2009). In his theory the acid receives pairs of electrons and 
the base donates pairs of electrons. In light of this definition a Lewis acid is any substance that can accept a pair 
of nonbonding electrons and a Lewis base is any substance that can donate a pair of nonbonding electrons. The 
new Lewis definition expanded considerably the field of acid and base to the extent that it incorporated 
solvation, heterolysis, coordination, halogen-bond formation, hydrogen-bond formation and electrophilic and 
nucleophilic reactions. The advantage of Brønsted-Lowry related to the Lewis definition is that it is possible to 
establish a universal order of acid or base strength because the proton exchange is used as the reference and it 
enabled the strength of several thousands of Brønsted-Lowry acids and bases to be measured (Zumdahl 2009).

1.4.2.  Model Validation 
The discussed model from the previous section has been validated by making titration experiments in the 
laboratory. The model was implemented, by using amine as titrant. However, it was not possible to use amine as 
titrant in the laboratory, due to the cost of the amine and safety issues related to handling larger quantities of the 
amine. Therefore, reverse experiments have been performed, where acid chloride was used as titrant for titration 
of amines.  
1.4.2.1. Experimental Procedure  
Experiments were based on manual titration of MPPA, IPA and MBA with different concentrations of HCl. In 
manual pH titration, each amine solution was titrated from a high pH to a low pH by addition of HCl along the 
way. The pH was reduced gradually until equal molar HCl concentration related to the amine has been added. At 
this point the titration profile reached the equivalence point. The pH value was then reduced rapidly by adding a 
trace volume of HCl until leveling off with the addition of excess acid.

Part 2: Multi Minerals Precipitation Modeling 
2.1. Introduction  
The global rising of domestic and industrial wastewater disposal often results in high nutrient loading of the 
aquatic environment.  Excessive nutrient concentrations such as phosphorus and nitrogen – in wastewater have 
serious environmental impacts. Therefore, the requirement of achieving wastewater treatment with the aim of 
nutrients and toxic metals removal to acceptable limits is necessary and vital. 
Physicochemical models have been developed for many years in the wastewater treatment field and have 
become very important tools in design, control, teaching and research of wastewater treatment processes. The 
models demonstrate the main characterization of the chemical and biochemical systems, and improve the 
fundamental understanding of the system behavior. The models can predict the future behavior of the system 
according to a change of conditions. The use of such models typically results in a reduction of experimental 
costs and time consumption for process design/development (Stratful et al. 2001). The fundamentals of the 
physicochemical reactions are explained comprehensively in aquatic chemistry text books (Werner Stumm 
1995). PHREEQC version 2 is a computer program for simulating chemical reactions and transport processes, 
applying the basic principles to introduce more complex and accurate models in natural or polluted water 
(Parkhurst & Appelo 1999) .  
In 1982 the International Association on Water Quality (IAWQ) appointed a task group in order to create a 
generalized common platform. The aim of the platform was to facilitate the application of practical models for 
chemical oxygen demand (COD) and nitrogen (N) removal with a minimum of complexity for design and 
operation of biological wastewater treatment systems. In 1987 the task group (Henze M., Grady C.P.L. Jr., Gujer 
W. 1987) published the Activated Sludge Model No. 1 (ASM 1) to predict performance of oxidation of organics, 
nitrification and denitrification under steady state and dynamic conditions. The ASM1 has been considered as 
the state-of-the-art as well as reference model, since this model led to the general acceptance of wastewater 
treatment modelling. To cover enhanced biological phosphorus removal, ASM1 has been expanded into ASM2 
and further to ASM2d including denitrifying by polyphosphate accumulating organisms (PAOs) (Henze et al. 
2000).The main limitation of the ASM models is the only use of the alkalinity state (SALK) to determine the net 
acid-base state of the system (Henze et al. 2000). Alkalinity indicates the ability of water to resist the decrease or 
increase of pH from neutrality. For example, the nitrification process consumes alkalinity and consequently 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
 
 26 
 
lowers pH in the activated sludge mixed liquor, and oppositely processes such as ammonia release will increase 
alkalinity. 
Acid-base chemistry, which is a fundamental physicochemical property in aqueous solution chemistry, is 
important in chemical neutralization methods in waste water treatment to reduce the impact of discharging the 
effluent to the environment. Further, many biological processes are very sensitive to pH change which will 
influence process stoichiometry and kinetics (Serralta et al. 2004). Therefore, it is essential to implement a 
model to perform accurate pH prediction during the process. Gernaey et al.(Gernaey et al. 2001 ) proposed an 
extension of ASM1 including free proton production in the biological process by replacing proton concentration 
instead of alkalinity concentration. Later, Serralta et al. (Serralta et al. 2004) proposed an extension of ASM2d 
including a chemical model able to calculate the pH value in the biological processes. 
The described deficits of ASM models according to pH prediction got an IWA task group to develop the 
anaerobic digestion model No. 1 (ADM1) for mathematical modelling of anaerobic digestion processes(Batstone 
et al. 2002). It is an advanced model used for full-scale industrial plants design, systems operational analysis and 
control. The model has contributed technology transfer from research to industry and is now a common basis for 
further model development and validation studies. The model covers a set of physicochemical processes, 
including acid–base interactions as equilibrium governed processes, as well as the stripping of gaseous species 
(Serralta et al. 2004). However, the main limitations of the model are ion pairing (at high conductivity) and 
precipitation modeling (Batstone et al. 2012). A further limitation is the lack of activity corrections at low 
conductivity that results in pH prediction errors (Ganigué et al. 2010). It can be concluded that existing models 
need to be extended and modified to comprise all relevant physicochemical and/or biochemical processes for 
given applications (Ganigué et al. 2010). Mineral precipitation, particularly phosphorus precipitation such as 
struvite (magnesium ammonium phosphate) formation, is among the important reactions occurring in wastewater 
processes. Phosphorus precipitation can have positive or negative impacts depending on the area in which the 
reactions are occurring. A well-defined modelling framework will probably aid the investigation of non-
biological chemical reactions in wastewater treatment. It provides a realistic perspective of precipitation 
processes and affords ability to prevent or promote solid minerals formation.

2.2. Objectives  
The main objective of the second part of this project is to develop and apply physicochemical models to 
investigate and simulate chemical precipitation processes with the aim of wastewater treatment. Based on the 
physicochemical models, the pH of the system will be predicted with respect to a dynamic titration approach and 
cation/anion initial concentration of the aqueous phase. Prediction of cation/anion behavior in terms of ion 
paring reactions and precipitating with respect to pH change will be performed. Accordingly, saturation 
conditions of the aqueous solution with respect to different solids are an important issue to be analyzed. The 
influence of anion/cation initial concentration and presences of foreign ions on the precipitation process will be 
analyzed critically.  
2.3. Materials and Methods  
The experiments are performed by Kazadi Mbamba and co-workers (Mbamba et al. 2015).The experiments are 
carried out in a batch setup where titration causes a change of the wastewater pH over time and results in 
precipitation of minerals. These experiments are done so that robust and reliable dynamic behavior of the system 
can be represented. This representation allows estimation of the kinetic model parameters statistically. 
2.3.1. Sample Materials  
Sealed containers at room temperature are used to keep wastewater samples. This is in order to minimize the 
carbon-dioxide stripping and avoid unwanted precipitation before the experiments are carried out. Supernatants 
are decanted after 2 days (i.e. they are used in the experiments and after the wastewater samples settled for this 
time). All sample characterizations are performed before the experiments for the majority of dissolved elements 
using Coupled Plasma Optical Emission Spectroscopy (ICP-OES), Flow Injection Analysis (FIA), Ion 
Chromatography (IonC) and Total Carbon Analysis (TCA) (see Analytical techniques). 
2.3.2. Apparatus  
The dynamic titration experiments used an auto-titrator (T70, Mettler-Toledo, Greifensee, Switzerland) with a 
pH sensor (Model DGi115-SC, Mettler-Toledo, Greifensee, Switzerland). The titration vessel used was a 1 L 
stirred glass crystallizer. During a titration, the test sample to which titrant was added was stirred with a 40 mm 
teflon-coated magnetic bar stirrer at 300 rpm. The volume of each titrant added, the pH and the temperature were 
recorded over time through a LabX Light Titration Software interface (Mettler-Toledo, Greifensee, Switzerland). 
==PAGE:28==

2.3.3. Experimental Procedures   
Continuous pH titration tests with synthetic wastewater were carried out, to test the competition of various major 
divalent cations with higher concentration of phosphate, in the absence of inorganic carbon. The initial pH of a 1 
L aliquot of test wastewater was adjusted to a desired value using concentrated sodium hydroxide (NaOH) or 
hydrochloric acid (HCl) solutions, with the amount of added acid or caustic recorded. A 2 M aqueous solution of 
NaOH (5-10 mL total) was then quantitatively added at a predetermined fixed rate and pH was continuously 
measured. 100 mg/ L of struvite crystal seed was then added at a time point in the experiment where the solution 
was estimated (using equilibrium predictors) to have transitioned from undersaturated to supersaturated 
conditions with respect to struvite. 
All the experiments were carried out at measured room temperature (20 to 25 _C). For chemical analysis in each 
experiment, 3 mL samples of the titration vessel contents were collected at specified time intervals, immediately 
filtered through 0.45 mm cutoff syringe filters (PES), diluted with deionized water to prevent post-precipitation 
and stored at 4 _C until analysis by ICP-OES, FIA, IonC and TCA (see directly below). 
2.3.4. Analytical Techniques  
The major elements (Ca, Mg, Na, K) were analysed with ICP-OES (Perkin Elmer Optima Model 7300DV, 
Waltham, MA, USA) after nitric acid digestion. Ionic concentrations N - NH4 and P - PO3 were quantified with 
a Lachat QuickChem flow injection analyser (Lachat Instruments, Loveland, CO, USA) using standard method 
31-107-06-1-A. Total Inorganic Carbon (TIC) concentrations were quantified using a Total Carbon Analyser 
(Shimadzu TOC-L CSH Total Organic Carbon Analyser, Kyoto, Japan). Chloride, sulphide, thiosulfate, sulphite 
and sulphate concentrations were measured by IonC using a Dionex ICS-2000 IC System fitted with an AS50 
Autosampler and AD25 Absorbance Detector (Dionex, Sunnyvle,CA). A RFIC IonPac AS18 column was used 
with the instrument. The sample was eluted with a KOH gradient (12 to 52 mM) and absorbance was measured 
at a wavelength of 230 nm. The IC analyses were performed at 35 ºC. The pH in all experiments was measured 
with a pH sensor (Model DGi115-SC, Mettler-Toledo, Greifensee, Switzerland), and it is especially noted here 
that a highly precise and reliable pH sensor is essential for minerals precipitation studies. Total suspended solids 
(TSS) were determined as a difference between total solid (TS) and total dissolved solid (TDS). TS was 
measured by evaporating and drying a wastewater sample at specified temperature (103 to 105 ºC). TDS was 
determined as for TS but on wastewater samples filtered through 0.45 mm cut-off syringe filters (PES).

transformation reactions. Therefore, the model structure consisted of two parts that describe the equilibrium 
derived reactions of the aqueous phase and contain kinetically derived expressions for precipitation/dissolution

expressions. Those processes are modelled by following the Ostwald rule, which means that the least

when corresponding constituent ions of a specific solid are combining to form nuclei or so-called crystal 
embryos.  Crystal growth refers to the development of nuclei resulting into visible solids, which continues until 
equilibrium is reached. The precipitation process highly depends on the degree of supersaturation of the solution.

2.4.3.  Implementation details  
The physicochemical models in the previous section were introduced as equilibrium condition for reactions in 
the aqueous phase and kinetic condition for the ion exchange between the liquid-solid phases. The equilibrium 
reactions were introduced as a set of non-linear and linear algebraic equations (AEs) and the kinetic reactions as 
set of ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The numerical solution procedure can perform by two approaches; 
ODE approach and differential algebraic equation (DAE) approach.  
In the ODE approach proposed by (Loewenthal R. E. and v. R. Marais 1976), aqueous chemical equilibrium 
reactions are expressed kinetically and are solved simultaneously with liquid-solid ion exchange reactions. In the 
ODE or kinetic-based modelling approach the model is constructed in such a way that weak acid/base equilibria 
and ion pairing reactions are described in terms of the kinetics of the forward and reverse reactions. Accordingly, 
the kinetic vector of the all involving reactions was defined. With respect to species present in the system, very 
high values of the kinetic rate constants are selected to ensure that the corresponding chemical reactions reach 
equilibrium effectively instantaneous. However, the large range of the time constants in this system make the 
numerical simulation stiff, which leads to extremely long simulation time and possibly numerical instability can 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
 
 35 
 
occur during model simulation.  Therefore, utilization of a stiff Matlab/Simulink solver will be necessary to 
solve the mixed system by the ODE approach. However, the stiff solvers are very sensitive to dynamic input and 
step changes.   
To overcome the stiffness problem, the differential algebraic equations (DAE) approach is utilized as an 
alternative numerical solution. In the DEA approach, the fast reactions are calculated separately algebraically 
and the slow reactions are calculated by means of an ODE at each iteration step. The simulation procedure 
performs sequential iteration among the ordinary differential equations obtained from the kinetic expressions of 
the precipitation model and a sequential iteration among the nonlinear algebraic equations obtained from the 
equilibrium process of aqueous models. The algebraic system solves the total concentration of each component 
including proton as function of time through the series of differential algebraic equations by using the Newton-
Raphson (NR) approximation method. The NR method iteratively solves the implicit algebraic equation set 
obtained from mass action laws and mass balance equations (section 2.2.1) as shown in equation 2.12:  
Zi+1 = Zi − G(Zi)

2.6. Discussion  
2.6.1.  Determination of Precipitation Parameters 
The usability of the developed codes can be elaborated, for instance for determination of the precipitation 
parameters. Mbamba and co-workers (Mbamba et al. 2015) had designed different experiments to estimate the 
𝑘𝑐𝑟𝑦𝑠𝑡 (parameter. The value was estimated by non-linear fitting lsqcurvefit in MATLAB. Furthermore an 
objective function was set up, to generate confidence regions for nonlinear parameter identification. They 
suggested that a large kinetic coefficient 𝑘𝑐𝑟𝑦𝑠𝑡 could be used when measuring data are insufficient.  
2.6.2.  Linkage with Biochemical models 
The applied physiochemical model of the project is generic and can be linked to many biochemical models and 
other processes. As an example, the general acid-base equilibria models can be used to predict pH during a 
fermentation process. In this section, some of the processes, that are applying the generic physicochemical 
models, will be introduced briefly.  
In biochemical industry application of physicochemical properties are indispensable especially acid-base 
equilibria models. Many of biological reactions are involving proton production or consumption that affects 
directly the pH of the medium.   According to microbiological anaerobic fermentation of lignocellulosic biomass 
the physicochemical models are applied to predict pH, concentration of free ammonia ions, inorganic carbon. 
Further, the physicochemical models are applied to calculate liquid-gas transfer of the main gas components 
such as methane, carbon dioxide and hydrogen (Lübken et al. 2010).  
In the dairy industry, the physicochemical models are used to predict the behaviors of minerals in milk solution 
(Rosmaninho & Melo 2006) with aim to pasteurization and productions of milk proteins. Normal bovine milk 
contains approximately 3-3.5% of protein. Approximately 80% of milk protein is arranged as casein in casein 
micelle and the remaining 20% is arranged in serum (whey). Casein micelles stand as colloidal particles of 100-
300 nm in diameter. The particles contain in additional ions such as calcium, phosphates and relatively smaller 
amounts of citrate and magnesium (Guo et al. 2003). Accordingly, due to presence of calcium and phosphate the 
possibility of precipitation in milk is high in sufficient conditions as descript in the working project. The 
precipitation of calcium phosphates leads to scale formation on the surface of devices and pipes that have 
negatively economic and hygiene consequences.  
==PAGE:68==
 
 
 
 68 
 
Renal calculi (urolithiasis) or kidney stone is a urinary disease known from ancient time. Calcium 
oxalate(CaC2O4), struvite, DCPD and uric acid are the main types of kidney stones. Supersaturation of urine 
with respect to the mentioned solids and precipitation conditions obey the physicochemical models that were 
descripting in the working project. Calcium oxalate is covering more than 70 % of the urinary stones. Struvite, 
uric acid (C5H4N4O3) and DCPD are covering about 10-20%, 5-15 % and 5% of the urinary stones, respectively 
(Han et al. 2015). The precipitation of calcium oxalate occurs by presence of calcium and oxalic acid(H2C2O4) 
in the pH range of 5.5 to 6.1(International et al. 1999). Formation of struvite is due to urinary tract infection that 
converts urea (NH2CO) to ammonia and further protonation of ammonia to ammonium (Kotak & Road 2009).

Conclusions 
In the first part of the project the physicochemical model approach was used to predict acid-base equilibria for 
bio-catalytic process of chiral amines production. General theory of acid-base chemistry was used to develop a 
theoretically titration model for HCl as acid and MPPA, IPA and MBA as amines. The titration models of each 
mentioned amine and HCl with several concentrations were validated by manual titration experiments performed 
in laboratory. An additional model was developed to predict the required amount of acid corresponding to the 
input of amine to the second reservoir to maintain the acidity of the reservoir at the desired pH level of 3. The 
model showed that the required volumetric flow rate of HCl increases as the concentration of MPPA increases, 
due to the increased MPPA flow rate. 
In the second part of the project the physicochemical equilibrium and kinetic models were applied to predict and 
simulate the precipitation processes of multi minerals synthetic waste water. The precipitation modeling 
approach was validated via dynamic titration experiment of synthetic waste water from low to high pH by 
Kazadi Mbamba et al. (2015).  Calcium, phosphate, magnesium, ammonia and hydrogen were the essential ions 
involving the base case theoretical models and experimental of the synthetic waste water.  
The base case model was suggested that precipitation of struvite was the predominated followed by DCPD, 
where ACP predicated in relatively small amount. Although the theoretical model and validated experimental 
data were suggested that solution was supersaturated in sufficient degree with respect to OCP, precipitation of 
the solid was not occurred according to the theoretical model. The precipitation of HAP and newberyte neither 
were occurred in accordance with the related saturation indices. Precipitation of DCPD was occurred at the pH 
between 6 and 7.5 followed by ACP at the pH between6.4 and 9. The sufficient pH range required for the 
precipitation of struvite occurs was found to be between 7.8 and 8.5 according to the theoretical model.   
The influence of some crucial parameters on precipitation processes of the base case was investigated as well. 
Accordingly, seven scenarios were descripted and discussed. In scenario (i), the influence of the titrant (NaOH) 
flow rate was investigated. The predicted model indicated that as the titrant flow rate was increased the pH 
profile of the synthetic waste water was increased earlier and more extensive. Subsequently, the solution 
saturation index increased more extensive from undersaturated toward saturated and supersaturated conditions. 
According to the concentrations of ions the decreasing profiles initiation time were promoted in accordance to 
promotion time of saturation indices. Accordingly, induction times of the predicated solids were decreased 
significantly. Scenario (ii) was indicated that initial concentration of magnesium ion had a direct influence to 
struvite precipitation. The predicted model showed that the total mass precipitation of struvite increased 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
 
 70 
 
significantly as the initial concentration of magnesium increased. The investigation of phosphate ion effect in 
scenario (iii) showed that initial concentration of the ion had a direct influence to precipitation competition 
between DCPD and ACP. It was concluded that the total mass precipitated of ACP was increased significantly as 
the initial concentration of phosphate ion was increased. Inversely, total mass precipitated of DCPD was 
decreased significantly. The effect of changing initial concentration of ammonia was investigated in scenario (iv) 
and showed to be similar to the effect of titrant change in scenario (i). The predicted model indicated in scenario 
(v) that initial concentration of calcium has a key factor to precipitation of DCPD. It was illustrated that the total 
mass precipitation of DCPD was increased in high extent according to increase of initial calcium concentration. 
Scenario (vi) indicated that supersaturation of solution with respect to calcite, argonite and magnesite did not 
occurred despite presence of carbonate ion in solution. In scenario (vii) presence of potassium ion in the base 
case indicated that precipitation of k-struvite was not occurred.  
The predicted model indicated that as initial concentration of a cation such as magnesium and calcium was 
increased the pH of the synthetic wastewater was increased earlier and more extensive comparing to the base 
case scenario. The effect of pH profile change on saturation indices, decreasing initiation time of the ions 
concentrations and precipitations induction time were similar to the effect of titrant flow rate change. 
Conversely, as initial concentration of an anion such as phosphate and carbonate was increased the pH of the 
synthetic waste water was increased slower comparing to the base case scenario. Consequently, time to solution 
was reached saturation point and induction time of precipitated solids were increased comparing to the base case 
scenario. Further, decreasing initiation of ion concentrations were increased as pH profile was increased in less 
extensive comparing to the base case scenario. 
Further working 
The titration processes of the first part of the project should be performed by auto-titration to reduce deviations 
of the experimental data regarding to model. Furthermore, the described amines should be diluted sufficiently 
and used as titrator for titration of acid.  
According to second part, it is an advantage to run some lab experiments to validate the discussed scenarios. The 
influence of organic acids such propionate, butyrate and valerate on base-case scenario can be investigated. The 
effect of sulfate, aluminum and iron on the base case scenario can also be investigated.
