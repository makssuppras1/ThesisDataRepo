Abstract
This thesis investigates the effectiveness of MILP models for financial portfolio optimiza­
tion, integrated with various scenario generation techniques. The primary objective is to
assess the robustness of portfolio optimization models in realistic investment scenarios
that incorporate transaction costs and periodic rebalancing. Initially, three optimization
models (MAD, CVaR and Minimax) were evaluated under in­sample conditions using his­
torical stock return data from S&P 500 and DAX 40 indexes. Scenario generation meth­
ods were used, including Bootstrap (BS), Monte carlo simulation using normal (MCN)
and t­Student distributions (MCT), with extensive parameter tuning conducted to iden­
tify optimal configurations. The analysis was extended to include both fixed and pro­
portional transaction costs. Dynamic rebalancing strategy were implemented and com­
pared against a static buy­and­hold strategy to determine their effectiveness considering
additional incurred transaction costs. Out­of­sample analysis revealed that transaction
costs have minimal impact on returns for a buy­and­hold strategy, indicating robustness
in performance across different scenario generation methods, particularly evident in DAX
40 data. The results showed that CVaR model outperformed MAD in terms of returns,
but with higher volatility, especially evident for S&P 500 data. Conversely, MAD model
demonstrated consistent performance with lower volatility, making it more suitable for
risk­averse investors. Rebalancing strategy, although beneficial in terms of adaptability
to market shifts, did not manage to outperform static strategies, possibly due to increased
transaction costs. Overall, this research confirms that MILP models combined with sce­
nario generation tools are a viable option for driving financial decisions, highlighting critical
considerations such as transaction costs, risk tolerance of the investor and rebalancing
frequency.

Introduction
Investing is a challenge faced by both individuals and financial institutions. Over recent
decades, the variety of instruments available to investors has grown significantly. Beyond
traditional stocks and bonds, investors may now choose to allocate their resources in mu­
tual funds and derivatives. While each asset class exhibits its own risk­return profile, the
main task is to find a way to select a portfolio of assets that either maximizes the return for
a tolerable level of risk or minimizes risk subject to a target return. This thesis focuses on
equity portfolios. Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT), introduced by Harry Markowitz in 1952
[1], set the foundation for single period portfolio optimization. By treating portfolio risk as
the variance of returns, Markowitz showed how diversification can produce an efficient
frontier of portfolios that optimally balances expected return against risk. In single period
portfolio optimization an investor allocates his/her resources at the end of the investment
period, among n available assets by choosing for each asset j a weight xj, which is a
fraction of the total monetary value available for investment. At this point, assets are only
purchased and the portfolio is constructed based on the investor’s expectations about fu­
ture asset performance. The portfolio is then held unchanged until a future moment called
target time. The set of assets available for investment, is denoted by N = {1, 2, ..., n}.
For each asset j the rate of return is modeled as a random variable Rj with a given ex­
pected value µj = E[Rj]. Given the weights of the assets, the rate of return of portfolio x
is modeled as R(x) = Pn
j=1 Rjxj and its expected value is E[R(x)] = Pn
j=1 E[Rj]xj .
The primary goal of rational investors is to maximize the expected return of the portfolio
and minimize the associated risk. In general, portfolios that offer higher expected returns
also tend to carry higher risk. The objective of portfolio optimization is to efficiently man­
age this trade­off between expected return and risk. The risk associated with a portfolio
is often expressed through dispersion measures to be minimized, which are functions
that quantify how much the portfolio’s return is likely to deviate from its expected value.
While risk is a standard way of modeling uncertainty, alternative approaches focus on
safety measures to be maximized, which are probability estimates of achieving a mini­
mum desired level of return. More information about dispersion and safety measures will
be provided in chapter 4. To further illustrate the trade­off between risk and return, the
efficiency frontier is depicted in figure 1.1

In the figure above, portfolios A and B belong to the efficiency frontier, while portfolio C
does not. The reason for that is that A dominates C in terms of yielding the same re­
turn for a lower amount of risk, while B dominates C in terms of yielding higher return
for the same amount of risk. Single period portfolio optimization relies on simplifying as­
sumptions, those assumptions include the usage of historical data as sole indicator for
future performance of asset returns, the absence of transaction costs and static portfolio
composition throughout the investment horizon. To address the above limitations, one
parametric and two non­parametric scenario generation methods are introduced, each
scenario represents a realization of the multivariate random variable representing the
rates of return of all securities. These methods are evaluated based on their ability to
provide realistic inputs for portfolio optimization models in terms of their out­of­sample
performance. However, it is often required to generate a large number of scenarios to get
a representative sample set with similar properties as the original distribution as stated.
Solving a MILP problem at that scale could significantly increase computational time. To
construct the optimal portfolio three different mathematical models proposed in literature
are introduced. These models at first omit transaction cost and are used to establish
out of sample performance on different scenario generation methods. The two best per­
formers are selected and reformulated to include both fixed and proportional transaction
cost to simulate real trading environment. Finally, to overcome this buy and hold strategy
in which the selected portfolio is remained unchanged throughout the entire investment
horizon, a dynamic strategy is chosen that periodically re­optimize the portfolio to incorpo­
rate new market information. Key challenges in implementing this dynamic strategy are
the rebalancing frequency and transaction costs, which can significantly impact portfolio
performance over time.
1.1
Objective and Research Question
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the challenges of translating market uncertainties
into realistic inputs for portfolio optimization models, while at the same time accounting for
real trading conditions and potential market shifts over time. Different scenario generation
methods are assessed on whether they produce reliable input scenarios for portfolio op­
timization when measured on out sample performance. Scenario reduction methods are
used primarly to reduce computational time, but investigation should also be conducted
on the effect they have on the performance of the selected portfolio in out of sample
data. Next, transaction costs are incorporated into the models to observe to what extent
those costs alter the optimal portfolio allocation and performance. Finally, the classic buy
and hold strategy is extended by introducing periodic rebalancing at different frequencies
to assess whether this transformation will improve overall performance against buy and
hold strategy and also weight the benefits of rebalancing against the additional transcation
costs. Therefore, this thesis will address the following question
• Are the proposed models, incorporated with scenario generation techniques, a reli­
able tool for supporting financial decisions ?

2
Literature Review
In 1952 Markowitz [1] laid the groundwork for optimizing portfolio selection by introducing
Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT). Markowitz concluded that investors are primarly inter­
ested in the expected return and the variability of returns of the portfolio and he showed
that these dimensions could be traded off against one another through diversification. By
treating each asset’s return as a random variable and examining the portfolio variance
as a quadratic function of the asset weights, he formulated a single period optimization
model called Mean­Variance (MV) model that either maximizes expected return for a given
variance threshold or minimizes variance for a given target return. However, the pro­
posed models has been criticized for two reasons. First variance leads to a large­scale
quadratic programming problem, expanding the models by incorporating real features like
transaction costs and cardinality constraints could result in a computationally challenging
problem. And second, model’s reliance of quantifying risk using variance could lead in
penalizing both upside and downside deviations.
Therefore, for practical applications, linear or mixed integer linear programming formu­
lations have been proposed in literature. As stated in Young [3] framing the portfolio
selection process as a linear optimization problem also makes it feasible to constrain
certain decision variables to be integer or binary. To address MV model’s limitations, re­
searchers have suggested portfolio LP­formulations that are able to capture the trade­off
between return and risk while at the same time remain computationally tractable. In 1990,
Konno and Yamazaki [4] introduced the Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD) model. By re­
placing variance as risk measure with the absolute deviation from the expected return,
MAD is reduced to an LP problem that is more efficient from a computational point of view
compared to MV model which results in a quadratic programming problem, while at the
same time it retains MV model structure . Under the assumption of normally distributed
returns, MAD and the MV model become mathematically equivalent. Subsequent work
by Ogryczak and Ruszczyński [5] showed that MAD also respects second order stohastic
dominance, ensuring that its solution aligns with standard axioms of investor risk aversion.
MAD model has been widely tested accross diverse markets, including mortgage­backed
securities in Zenios and Kang [6] and extended in different forms, including the Semi­MAD
which considers only the negative deviations from the mean as proposed in Feinstein and
Thapa [7]. Another risk measure that is aligned with MAD properties is the Gini’s Mean
Difference (GMD) introduced by Yitzaki [8] which captures the average absolute differ­
ence between all pairs of returns. The GMD assuming that rate of returns are normally
distributed becomes proportional the standard deviation and therefore is equivalent to
the MV model. Another LP formulation was introduced by Young in 1998 [3]. Young in­
troduced the Minimax model which uses the worst­case realization of returns as a risk
measure for protection against extreme losses. Should the rate of returns be normally

Indexes
Stock market indexes aggregate the performance of selected groups of assets, reflect­

The S&P 500 index is a market capitalization weighted index comprising of 500 publicly

The DAX 40 Index represents the performance of 40 large and liquid German companies

Frankfurt Exchange. Originally comprising 30 companies, the index expanded to 40 com­

economic performance [23].

Data Limitations
In this thesis, limitations are primarily related to data availability and consistency. The

3.5
Scenario Generation Methods
In LP and MILP models formulations, asset returns are modeled as discrete random vari­
ables. To preserve reliability of the models, it is essential to represent uncertainty through
a finite set of possible outcomes, known as scenarios. A scenario consists of one re­
alization of the rate of return for each of the n assets available. Mathematically, this
corresponds to a vector of length n, where each element represents the return of an as­
set under that scenario. A total of t mutually exclusive scenarios, each associated with
a probability pt are generated. Scenario generation is the process of constructing sets
of possible future return realizations. The quality of this process has a direct impact on
the robustness and reliability of portfolio optimization outcomes, that is why it is important
that a sufficiently large number of scenarios be generated to ensure statistical significance
and to capture the variability of asset returns. In this thesis it was chosen that different
number of scenarios be generated with a minimum number of 1000 scenarios, each sce­
nario will be assigned equiprobable probability to be realized and it will represent yearly
rate of returns. Among the techniques proposed in literature, Bootstrap and Monte Carlo
Simulation techniques were chosen to be analyzed. The following subsections present
the scenario generation methods employed in this thesis, providing detailed description
in the context of portfolio optimization.
3.5.1
Bootstrapping Technique
The bootstrapping method involves repeated random sampling with replacement from the
original dataset, assigning each scenario an equal probability of selection. Each scenario
generated corresponds to the joint realization of returns for all assets, preserving the
inherent correlation among assets. At each step, an integer is randomly drawn from a
uniform distribution, selecting the the corresponding historical scenario and included it in
the expanded scenario set. This resampling process continues iteratively until the desired
number of scenario sis achieved, resulting in an expanded dataset that preserves the
statistical characteristics and correlations of the original observations. The bootstrapping
technique for scenario generation in financial applications, has been utilised by Consiglio
[24] in studying asset­liability management problems and Kouwenberg [25]. In this thesis,
where the evaluation period is chosen to be 1 year, individual scenarios are constructed
by randomly selecting weekly returns observations with replacement, with the number of
weekly observations corresponding to the total weeks in the evaluation period. These

3.5.2

Monte Carlo simulation is a parametric method to generate scenarios by repeatedly sam­

create a large number of possible outcomes for asset returns. A critical aspect of this
method is the selection of appropriate multivariate distribution to represent the joint real­

is the multivariate normal distribution, characterized by zero mean (µ) and identity covari­

sampled from a multivariate Normal distribution N(µ, Σ) where mean rate of returns and
covariance matrix are estimated from historical return data. However, as mentioned in
Mandelbrot [27], multivariate normal distribution fails to capture prominent characteristics
of financial returns such as the presence of heavy tails. To overcome this limitation, the
multivariate t­Student distribution is often mentioned in literature as an alternative for mod­
eling heavy tails and capture market volatility. The heaviness of the tails in multivariate
t­Student distribution is expressed by the degrees of freedom parameter (ν). Glasserman
in [28] suggested assigning discrete values ranging from 3 to 7 to the parameter ν for suf­
ficiently capture the heaviness of the tails of most markets, as empirical evidence had
showed. Same as in section 3.5.1, the simulated returns are compounded to match the
evaluation period. In the context of this thesis, both Normal and Student’s t­distribution

Transaction Cost Models
When constructing realistic optimization models for portfolio management, transaction
costs are an important factor to consider. Transaction costs refer to expenses incurred

each time an investor buys or sells an asset, they include broker commissions and other
fees charged by financial institutions. Such costs have a direct impact on net returns, as
they reduce the profitability of transactions and the available capital for future investment
decisions. Generally, investors tend to hold a relative limited number of assets within
their portfolio, due to the associated transaction costs. Each adjustment to the portfolio
incurs costs, investors must carefully balance the benefit of asset reallocation against
potential reduction in returns resulting from expenses. Next, an explanation is going to be

literature studies have been conducted in MILP problems accounting for transaction costs
in Mansini [30], Kellerer [14], Angelelli [31] and Baumann [32].
4.2.1
Transcaction Costs Explained
In general transaction costs are incurred when either buying or selling an asset, however
the implemented strategy so far is buy and hold, therefore, assets are only bought. When
dealing with transaction costs, the asset weights xj, for j = 1, ..., n will be expressed

4.3
Rebalancing Models
Early foundations acknowledging portfolio rebalancing were laid by Markowitz, who rec­
ognized the importance of intermediate portfolio adjustments. Smith in [17], expanded
Markowitz’s single period framework to account for transaction costs, proposing a se­
quential period­by­period adjustments. In previous sections, single period period portfolio
optimization models formulated as LP or MILP have been discussed. An assumption un­
derlying these models is that at the initial investment time, the investor possesses capital
but does not already hold a portfolio. However, in real life applications, investors typically
already own portfolios, which they periodically rebalance in response to evolving market
conditions or additional capital availability. For example, when market trends become
negative, they seek portfolio modifications to minimize potential future losses or even in
positive market conditions, they might rebalance their portfolios to enhance returns. This
portfolio adjustment process is known as portfolio rebalancing. Two important consid­
eration in portfolio rebalancing is the presence of transaction costs and the rebalancing
frequency. Every reallocation of assets incurs transaction costs, which impact portfolio
components and returns. Relevant literature research on the impact of transaction costs
in rebalancing models can be found in Pogue [33] and in Gennotte and Jung [34]. In this
section, the concept of portfolio rebalancing is explained, considering transaction costs
and an optimization models using CVaR as safety measure is utilized for addressing multi­
period investment decisions. Relevant studies on rebalancing based on LP computable
risk/safety measure can be found in Konno and Wijayanayake [20] and in Guastaroba et
al [21].
4.3.1
Rebalancing Explained
Unlike buy­and­hold strategy, where investors choose their initial portfolio and maintain
it throughout the entire investment horizon, rebalancing allows portfolio adjustments in
specific intervals. These adjustments involve purchasing additional shares, selling ex­
isting holdings or reallocating investments among different assets, those adjustments in­
cur additional transaction costs with the expectation of improved portfolio performance.
The rebalancing strategy is defined where the initial optimal portfolio is subsequent re­
optimized monthly until the end of the evaluation period is reached. The initial portfolio
is determined by solving the basic optimization model, while each of the subsequent 12
rebalancing decisions is obtained by solving the rebalancing model at monthly frequency.
In particular, weights x0
j are obtained by solving the CVaR model with transaction costs
using a 2­year in sample data from 03/01/2017 to 25/12/2018. The selected portfolio is
then evaluated for the whole 1 year evaluation period (buy and hold strategy). For the re­
balancing strategy, at the end of the first month in the out of sample period, the in­sample
window is rolled forward by 1 month, dropping its oldest month and appending the most
recent out­of­sample month, then the dynamic CVaR model is employed which uses as
input the x0
j weights obtained previously, to obtain the new x1
j weights. The new portfolio
is evaluated over the next month. This monthly sliding window continues over a 12­month
out­of­sample period, yielding monthly rebalanced portfolios. This rebalancing strategy is

Evaluation
In this chapter, extensive computational experiments are performed to assess the pro­
posed models. The analysis is divided in two main sections. First, in­sample analysis is
performed, which involves evaluating the performance of the models under different pa­
rameters and scenario generation methods. Specifically, this phase serves as a bench­
marking procedure, where multiple combinations of parameters and scenario generation
methods are tested to identify those that yield the best performance based on risk­return
trade­off. On the other hand, after having established the optimal parameter settings,
the out­of­sample analysis is conducted, where those settings are applied to evaluate the
performance of the models on unseen data. By conducting the out­of­sample evaluation,
conclusions can be reached on whether the models generalize well on unseen market
conditions.

Out­of­Sample Analysis of Transaction Cost Models for

In the out­of­sample analysis for the optimization models incorporating transaction costs,
the performance of the portfolios is assessed, constructed by the MAD and CVaR models
with transaction costs over a 52 week horizon following the initial selection date. These
models extend the basic formulations by integrating fixed and proportional transaction
costs, to simulate real world trading environment. The evaluation aims to test the robust­
ness of these models under realistic conditions, using parameter configuration, identified
as optimal in the in­sample analysis. For the MAD model with transaction costs, two con­
figurations were selected for each index, one with 1000 scenarios and minimum required
return µ0 = 0.05, and one with 1000 scenarios and µ0 = 0.05. For the CVaR model with
transaction costs, the optimal configuration found during in­sample analysis, uses 1000
Financial Portfolio Optimization using Mixed Integer Programming
43
==PAGE:53==
scenarios with a confidence level β = 0.15. Transaction cost models, introduce additional,
real world parameters, for a more detailed explanation on those refer to section 5.2. To
evaluate out­of­sample performance, cumulative portfolio returns were calculated over
52 week horizon and compared to the corresponding market index. Let R1, R2, ...., Rm
denote the weekly ex­post portfolio returns. These are computed by first converting the
portfolio weights xj, which now represent the number of shares purchased for each asset
j, into investment weights. This is done by multiplying each xj by the corresponding asset
price and then dividing by the total capital invested. The resulting values represent the
fraction of capital allocated to each asset. The ex­post portfolio return for each week is
the obtained by taking the weighted sum of the out­of­sample asset return using these
investment weights. The cumulative return is then calculated using the equation 3.3.1.
Same as in the evaluation of the basic models on out­of­sample data, a comprehensive
set of performance metrics is computed, which includes the same metrics as before and
additionally it includes, average transaction costs paid (Avg. TC), min transaction costs
paid (Min. TC), max transaction costs paid (Max. TC) and profit at the end of the out­
of­sample period (Profit), which is expressed as Capital ∗Cumulative return because
as explained in previous chapter the transaction costs are deduced from the return. The
inclusion of transaction costs, provides insights into the trade off between risk,return and
costs incurred. The out­of­sample analysis of transaction cost models aims at the evalu­
ation of the models across scenario generation methods and to the comparison of trans­
action cost model performance compared to their basic counterparts.

constructs a portfolio at the beginning of the investment period and holds it unchanged un­
til the end. This strategy benefits from minimal transaction costs, as it involves solving the
optimization problem only once. Conversely, the rebalancing strategy is a characteristic
of a dynamic investor who periodically adjust the portfolio to respond to changing market
conditions. Particularly, the rebalancing strategy is defined as one where the portfolio is

2 year in­sample window. Each subsequent rebalancing is carried out using a rolling
window approach, where the in­sample period is updated monthly by dropping the old­
est month and incorporating the most recent out­of­sample data point. More information
about how rebalancing works can be found in 4.3.1. Finally, the rebalancing model is
tested for 10 different seeds and operates under the same transaction costs parameters
and is evaluated at the start of the 2nd month of out­of­sample period and under the same
performance metrics introduced in 5.4.

6
Discussion
In this thesis, three distinct portfolio optimization models (MAD, CVaR and Minimax) were
investigated, two based on safety measures and on based on a risk measure across
two major financial indexes the S&P 500 and DAX 40. Additionally, the impact of three
scenario generation techniques (Bootstrap (BS), Monte Carlo using Normal Distribution
(MCN) and Monte Carlo using t­Student Distribution (MCT)) was examined. To identify
the most effective parameter setting for each model an extensive in­sample analysis was
conducted. Given that only one of the models utilized a risk measure (MAD), its inclusion

Conclusion
This section focuses explicitly on the out­of­sample performance of transaction cost mod­
els. The reason for this emphasis is that the in­sample analysis served primarily as a
calibration phase, aiming to identify the best performing parameter configuration for each
model. Furthermore, it is important to clarify the the out­of­sample evaluation of the basic
models, does not replicate a real trading environment, but it is used to assess the impact
of incorporating transaction costs. When it comes to the rebalancing model, it was pre­
viously explained in chapter 6 that although it consistently outperformed the benchmark
index, it did not surpass the performance of the static transaction model. Therefore, the
conclusions presented in this section will focus exclusively on the findings derived from
the transaction cost models.
The out­of­sample results for the transaction cost models suggests that the choice of sce­
nario generation technique has a limited effect on portfolio performance. This is particu­
larly evident in the case of DAX 40 data, where all scenario generation methods yielded
nearly identical results for different models. Hence, it can be concluded that any of the
three methods can be reliably used without significantly affecting the outcome. For the
DAX 40 index, both MAD and CVaR models consistently outperformed the benchmark in­
dex, offering high returns with low volatility. In contrast, the results obtained for S&P 500
varied. The MAD model provided stable, low volatility returns and outperformed the index
in approximately half of the evaluation periods. The CVaR model, although outperforming
the index in most weeks, exhibited more volatile returns. In summary, for DAX 40, both
MAD and CVaR models are reliable and effective. However, given that both exhibit low
volatility and the CVaR model achieves higher return, it would be more suitable for a risk
averse investor to choose the CVaR model, focusing also on returns. For S&P 500, the
MAD model is recommended for risk­averse investor due its stable performance. While
the CVaR model demonstrates stronger performance in terms of returns, its increased
volatility may not align with a risk profile. In figure 7.1 and in figure 7.2, the performance
of the different models is illustrated under different financial indexes. The optimal config­

Future Work
The research presented in this thesis has explored the integration of various portfolio opti­
mization models with different scenario generation techniques, incorporating transaction
costs and rebalancing strategies. While the results provided valuable insights, further
research can be conducted in the future.
1. Parameter and transaction costs sensitivity analysis While this study analyzed spe­
cific values for critical parameters such as the minimum required return µ0 and the
confidence level β, future research should conduct more extensive sensitivity anal­
ysis. Particularly, additional values for µ0 and β should be explored, for example
one can try to explore the trade­off between risk and return when those parame­
ters, receive extreme values. Additionally, investigating a wider range of fixed and
proportional transaction costs, found from major brokerage institutions or defining
those costs based on the country of origin of the index, for example different costs
should be use for assets traded in the US than assets traded in Germany.
2. Modeling of transaction costs Currently, transaction costs were modeled simply as
fixed and proportional components deducted from returns. Future studies could
investigate alternative approaches to modeling transaction costs, including concave,
convex or piecewise linear cost structures.
3. Inclusion of more real­world constraints Cardinality constraints, which limit the num­
ber of securities selected in a portfolio, transaction lot constraints, requiring invest­
ments to occur in predefined trade sizes, could significantly influence portfolio deci­
sions and the associated costs.
4. Exploration of rebalancing frequencies and strategies Rebalancing frequencies could
significantly affect transaction costs and overall portfolio performance. Future re­
search should investigate various rebalancing intervals, such as weekly, quarterly
and annually, to understand how the timing of portfolio adjustments affects perfor­
mance. Furthermore, dynamic rebalancing strategies triggered by specific market
indicators could be explored, providing enhanced responsiveness to changing mar­
ket conditions.
5. Multiobjective portfolio optimization In the present work, the optimization models fo­
cus on a single objective approach, either minimizing risk or maximizing the worst
scenario realization. Future research could extend the proposed models into a mul­
tiobjective optimization framework, employing techniques such as pareto frontier
analysis, where simultaneously maximize return and minimize risk.
6. Another possible future research could involve examining the performance of the
proposed models under various market conditions, such as bull, bear and volatile
market phases. Assessing the robustness of portfolio optimization models in dif­
ferent market environments can provide insights into the adaptivity of the model in
different market conditions.
