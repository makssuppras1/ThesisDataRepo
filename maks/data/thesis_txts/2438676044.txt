Foreword 
 
The studies regarding the airport project in Ilulissat commenced from the spring semester of 
2016 during the Arctic semester carried out in Sisimiut, Greenland. The report Extension of 
Airport Runway in Ilulissat of the course Infrastructure Constructions in the Arctic, dealt with 
geometrical design problems, aviation regulations, but also geodetic and geothermal data 
analysis. The following semester, the special course Classification and oedometer testing in 
frozen clay samples consisted of laboratory work and data analysis of frozen samples drilled and 
retrieved by boreholes located at the new airport area. Finally, the MSc thesis is the last piece of 
the puzzle completing the Arctic studies about the airport project in Ilulissat. 
The thesis summarizes laboratory and modelling work testing the thermal behavior of frozen clay 
layer overlaid by crushed rock embankment. It also deals with the problem of climate change 
and the way it may affects the thermal stability of permafrost.  
The dissertation was carried out at the department of Civil Engineering of the Technical 
University of Denmark (DTU) in cooperation with the consultant company COWI A/S during the 
spring semester of 2018. The project was performed in both DTU laboratory facilities and in the 
offices of COWI A/S in Lyngby. The latter has been chosen as the principal consultant company 
for the construction of the airport in Ilulissat providing valuable data of the project to the author 
of the report.

Abstract 
 
The geotechnical design of the new runway embankment in Ilulissat has already been completed 
by COWI A/S in April of 2018. It has been decided from the client and design companies to 
remove the frozen sediment deposits and replacing them with crushed blasted rock in order to 
secure the stability of the embankment and avoid future settlements caused by the presence of 
thawed permafrost and ice lenses. Although this solution can assure a safe and reliable structure, 
the required cost is exceptionally high.  
On the contrary, the current report develops a scenario with the construction of an embankment 
over the existing permafrost without material replacement.  A series of laboratory tests and 
modeling of the embankment in software was utilized in order to ensure a reliable outcome. 
A model of frozen clay underlain by bedrock was generated in FEM (Finite Element Method) 
software PLAXIS 2D and tested the soil thermal variation throughout an entire year period. The 
outcome of the model was compared with the soil temperature values from field work 
performed by DTU Associate professor Thomas-Ingeman Nielsen at the same period. In this way, 
the parameters of the model were validated before the addition of the embankment at the new 
model.  
The problem of thawing permafrost especially during the summer months, when the air 
temperature in Ilulissat can exceed +8oC, is coped with the utilization of an embankment (in two 
different geometries) which acts as insulator for the permafrost decreasing the soil temperature 
and stabilizing it at below zero values securing the stability of the structure.  
Climate change has a considerable impact in the Arctic and especially at the structures built 
directly on permafrost. The level drop of active layer may cause differential settlement and 
material failure at the structures.  Thus, a prediction of climate change until 2050 by DMI is used 
to model the thermal variation of permafrost due to air temperature rise at the following years.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Location and Climate of Greenland 
 
Greenland is the largest island in the world located between the Arctic and North Atlantic Ocean, 
northeast of Canada, while it belongs geographically in the Arctic region and the North America 
with geographic coordinates 72 00 N and 40 00 W. It’s part of the Danish Realm since 1953, but 
also it is self-ruled since November 2008. The total area of Greenland is 2,166,086 km2 of which 
410,449 km2 are ice-free and 1,755,637 km2 are covered by ice (CIA, 2017). In other words, the 
country is about 81% ice-capped and only 19% of the total area is ice-free (see figure 1). The 
Greenland ice sheet is the 2nd largest in the world after that of Antarctica. The land area of 
Greenland makes the country the 13th largest country in the world, whereas it is the most 
sparsely populated with 38,833,708 m2/capita. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The last official mensuration of population was 56,186 in 2015 (Worldometers, 2017). The people 
live along the coast, mostly in the West, in small settlements and towns. The total length of the 
coastline, which is mainly rocky, is 44,087 km providing immense fishing opportunities to the 
local people. Furthermore, almost one quarter of the population lives in the town Nuuk, capital 
of Greenland.  
 
The climate in Greenland is Arctic and forest doesn’t exist in the area, while the country is 
characterized by cold winters and cool summers. The latter differs only a few degrees from the 
North to the South because of the midnight sun in the summer. However, long periods of

1.2. Location and Climate of Ilulissat 
 
Ilulissat is in the west coast of the country only 250 km north of the Arctic circle at approximately

icebergs (see figure 2). The place has an outstanding universal value and it is included in the 
UNESCO World Heritage Site list.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The population of the town is approximately 4,530 residents and it is the 3rd largest town after 
capital Nuuk and Sisimiut (Government of Greenland, 2017). The mean annual temperature is 
about -3.3 oC (Ingeman, et al., 2008) and the average precipitation is 263mm (climatemaps, 
2017), while the mean elevation is approximately 39m (Cappelen, et al., 2001)

2. Literature Review  
2.1. Permafrost Conditions at the present airport area 
 
Investigations regarding the permafrost conditions at the town of Ilulissat have been carried out 
since 1980, when Niels Foged and the Danish Geotechnical Institute brought to light the first 
series of data before the construction of the existing airport in 1982 (Foged & Ingeman, 1980). 
A total number of 48 boreholes were drilled between 1978 and 1979 showing (at the area of the 
current airport) bedrock overlaid by marine clay and silt deposits at maximum depth of 12m 
below the ground surface (b.g.s) situated in a discontinuous permafrost zone. The salt content 
of these deposits was so high that the pore water was found partly unfrozen even at ground 
temperatures equal to -4oC.  In other words, the high salinity of soils along with the increasing 
number of fine-grained particles decreases the temperature in which the water begins to freeze. 
Another parameter which can cause freezing point depression at fine grain deposits is the 
capillary tension in the pores (Foged & Ingeman, 1980). A general view of the salt content in the 
fine-grained deposits can be obtained by the figure 6 indicating the chloride content versus 
depth.

It should be mentioned that chloride is an indication of salinity and not the actual salinity which 
includes other substances as well (fluoride, bromium, nitrat and other anions). As it is shown in

can also be observed that in a depth close to 7m, the chloride content reaches a maximum of 20

According to Niels Foged, in small depth equal to 3-5 meters b.g.s., it is seen ice rich frozen clay 
deposits with presence of ice lenses but with much lower chloride content. This means the 
existence of fresh pore water at the surface with gradual change with depth to pore seawater at 
depths >5m (Foged & Ingeman, 2008).  Furthermore, the active layer thickness varied from 0.5m 
to 1m consisted of muskeg and clay. Figure 7 shows the discrete layers as described above and 
presents the variation of chloride content and ground temperature depending on the depth.  
 
2.2. Construction of a test rock fill 
 
A test fill was built during the construction phase (1980-1984) of the existing airport in Ilulissat, 
located close to the present safety zone (south east of the runway), while Instrumented 
boreholes with deformation gauges and temperature sensors were established as well (see 
figure 8).  The materials used were blasted rock fill and some graded coarse on top of the rock 
material resulting to a total thickness of 2.5m (Foged & Ingeman, 2008). 
It should be noted that the deformation gauges were installed at the top and bottom of the 
permafrost layer and at the initial ground surface below the test fill. Initially, before the 
construction of the test fill, the ground temperature was found -4oC at 2m depth and slightly 
higher at -3 oC at a depth of 4 and 5.5m (1980-1982).  Between 1982 and 1984, after the 
construction of the test fill, the data showed a critical drop of the ground temperatures and more 
specifically, the new temperature values recorded were -5.5Oc and -3.5Oc at a depth of 2 and 5m 
respectively. In other words, there was a decrease of ground temperature of 1-1.5oC below the 
new construction of the test fill. Consequently, the test fill worked as an insulation layer for the 
ground below allowing the heat transfer during the winter period but also preventing the 
conductive heat in summer (Foged & Ingeman, 2008). 
In 1993 Søren Frederiksen, MSc student of DTU, downloaded from the data logger a series of 
data between 1984 and 1987. The new data showed settlements of 3cm at the original surface 
below the test fill with the bottom and top boundaries of the frozen clay to remain unaffected.

results indicate a small deformation at the centre of the test fill since the surface has deformed 
only 15 cm since 1993 and 30 cm since 1982 (figure 10). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Thus, the data analysis of the rock test fill indicates valuable information for this report, even if 
the test fill was constructed at the area of the present airport. Beyond doubt, the results denote 
that the ground temperature conditions at the frozen clay are improved after the construction 
of the test fill but also the rise of the frost table is considered an encouraging outcome. 
Furthermore, the gauges installed at the boreholes showed minor settlements at the ground 
below the test fill but significant settlements at the top surface of the fill. These results lead to 
the conclusion that since the initial frozen ground presented almost no settlements, the 
settlements at the top surface of the test fill could be caused by compaction problems. In 
addition to this, the test fill did not experience any maintenance from 1982, when it was 
constructed, until the last time that it was visited by the mentioned above professors in 2007. 
Therefore, the lack of maintenance in conjunction with the climate change at the area might 
cause the failures at the active layer and the horizontal displacements.  
In the bottom line, the outcome of the test fill is a concrete proof that the construction of a 
blasted rock embankment over a frozen fine-grained ground can be effective.

of heat transfer models.

2.8. Thermal Parameters 
Specific heat capacity (Cs): 
The amount of heat or energy of a soil needed in order to increase its temperature 1 degree is 
called heat capacity and it is expressed units of energy per unit of mass per unit of temperature 
(J/kg/K) (Andersland & Ladanyi, 1994).The ratio of the heat capacity to that of water is the 
specific heat capacity at the same units. A rise of specific heat capacity means an increase of 
energy needed to increase the material temperature. 
The product of the solid density (ρs) or the density of the soil particles with specific heat capacity 
is equal to the total heat storage of the solid material. The units of solid density are mass per 
unit of volume. The heat storage consists of the heat storage of soil particles as well as of pore 
fluid.   
The frozen clay material is a phase transition case in which the specific heat capacity is given by:

(21)
Where the indices i, w and v denote property of ice, water and vapour respectively (PLAXIS, 
2016).

The energy or heat transported in the solid material is described by the thermal conductivity. An 
increase of thermal conductivity means an increase of the transferred energy to the material and 
as a result the heat travels transports faster in the material. It should be noted that materials 
with low thermal conductivity are used for insulation purposes. The thermal conductivity is given 
by equation (20).

The thermal expansion coefficients (αx,αy,αz)express the degree of expansion (or contraction)

Latent heat of water 
The water needs a specific amount of energy to change from liquid to solid state (ice) but also to

Vapour diffusion coefficient 
The diffusion of vapour in the material is indicated by the vapour diffusion coefficient Dv (PLAXIS, 
2016). The mass flux of vapour can be deactivated by setting Dv=0. It should be noted that in the 
current project, vapour is not considered.

by the pore pressure variation. As it was mentioned above, vapour is not part of the model 
calculations at the present report, consequently the thermal enhancement factor has been set 
equal to zero.

3.  Laboratory tests at frozen clay samples 
 
A series of classification and oedometer tests were carried out at frozen samples retrieved by 
the town of Ilulissat. The tests were implemented by the author and the Msc student Malvina 
Lola and supervised by associate professor of DTU Thomas Ingeman Nielsen and mr Niels Foged, 
at the laboratory facilities of Technical University of Denmark (DTU) (Kandiliotis & Lola, 2017). 
The cores were transferred at a frozen state from Ilulissat to DTU and stored in a controlled 
temperature container, located at the DTU campus, at constant air temperature of -10oC. 
 
3.1. Classification Tests 
 
A large amount of excess material is remained during the specimen preparation which should 
not be discarded in order to be used for classification tests. The cuttings were stored in small air 
tight bags minimizing the water sublimation and oxidization of the samples. The latter were kept 
in controlled temperature conditions and, in case of transfer, they were carried inside insulated 
package. The tests were implemented in triplicates in order to minimize the error of the results, 
while the average values were used for further calculations and analysis (Ingeman, 2017). 
The following classification tests were implemented to determine significant properties of the 
samples: 


Organic content

The classification of the samples which tested with the oedometer method are presented in

3.1.2.  Determination of Atterberg limits 
 
The Atterberg limits consist of the liquid, the plastic and the shrinkage limit. The latter has not 
been tested and it is not presented at the report. The liquid limit test determines the water 
content that corresponds to the state when a soil changes form from liquid to plastic. There are 
two methods that can be used in order to define the liquid limit: 
  
 
The fall-cone method and the 
 
Casagrande method 
 
The most preferred and accurate method is the fall-cone. However, in lack of sufficient material 
for the fall-cone method, in the present project, the liquid limit was determined with Casagrande 
method. As plastic limit is defined the lowest water content at which the soil is plastic. Usually, 
the liquid limit test is carried out along with the plastic limit and their results can vary depending 
on the competence of the personnel performing them.

3.2.  Incremental Oedometer test 
3.2.1.  Introduction 
 
Every type of soil will settle, if it is under a load process causing settlements at the structure 
which is founded on them. The type of settlements can be either uniform or nonuniform. The 
latter type of settlement is also known as differential settlement and it can cause serious damage 
in every structure reducing significantly its design life. A settlement has three different phases: 
immediate or elastic compression, primary consolidation and secondary consolidation or creep. 
It should be mentioned that the last two phases are of great importance (Budhu, 2011).  
Primary consolidation is the volume change in fine grained soils due to discharge of water from 
the pores. Furthermore, in primary consolidation, the excess pore water stress is transferred to 
the particles of the soil. The secondary consolidation or compression begins when the primary 
consolidation is finished. The former comprises the volume change of a fine-grained soil due to 
the readjustments of the soil particles after the completion of the primary consolidation phase 
(Budhu, 2011) 
However, the consolidation test in permafrost samples presents some differences comparing to 
the classic method of consolidation. In the classic method, the strains are caused only by the load 
application, while on permafrost samples, the strains are also caused by the temperature 
variation during the thawing phase. In the current project, the thawing phase is carried out at 
200kPa constant load with a gradual thermal decrease (1oC step) of temperature from -10oc to 
0oc and then direct increase to +5oC. Consequently, it is obvious that for the consolidation of 
permafrost samples, a temperature-controlled room is essential for the operation of the test.  
It should be noted that strains occurred at the samples during the consolidation process only at 
the vertical axis. The strains at the horizontal direction are equal to 0, since the metal ring placed 
on the perimeter of the specimen does not allow the development of strains at the horizontal 
axis. 
In addition to this, special equipment is needed (PT100, sensors) for the consolidation test on 
permafrost samples in order to log the temperature at the specimen but also the temperature 
at the room where the test is carried out and the rooms of which their temperature can affect 
the test (usually rooms adjacent to the test room). On the other hand, the classic method of 
consolidation test is not related to the temperature of the sample and temperature sensors or 
thermistors are not utilized. 
The stress level and the temperature values for each step are listed in table 20. The total mass 
placed onto the loading arm for each step and the mass added in order to reach the stress level 
for each step are presented at the last two columns of the table. Although the minimum duration 
is displayed typically at 24 hours, the critical parameter for the completion of a loading step is 
the stabilisation of the strains. Consequently, there are several steps (mainly in thawing phase) 
with a duration more than 24h (Ingeman, 2017).

process commence. Subsequently, a reverse loading process was carried out at standard

A large number of mechanical, groundwater and thermal parameters are required to be inserted

4.2. Validation of parameters 
The uncertainty of the chosen parameters needs to be validated setting as reference data the 
soil temperature values measured in the field between March 2017 and February 2018 by DTU 
Associate professor Thomas Ingeman Nielsen. The thermal data are derived from borehole 
B16019T (see figure 13) with the two samples subjected to classification and consolidation tests, 
as it was presented in chapter 3, were derived from the same borehole.  
The geometry of the subsurface consists of a 6m depth frozen clay layer underlain by gneiss 
bedrock. The field data were measured up to a maximum depth of 10m (4m depth in bedrock). 
However, since bedrock is part of the thermal calculation, an assumption is made for a total 
depth of gneiss equal to 14m (see figure 25).

The phreatic level is set at the surface (h=0.0m) resulting in a fully saturated frozen clay cluster 
of ice and unfrozen water depending on the soil temperature. Contrary to this, at the cluster 
below the clay, the gneiss material is assumed as non-porous. The assumption is based on the 
void ratio of gneiss which according to Niels Foged, it can reach a maximum of 0.01 and 
consequently it can be neglected.   
The groundwater boundary conditions are closed on the left, right and bottom side of the model 
with only the surface defined as an open boundary condition. In other words, if there is any water 
in liquid state at the pores of the clay layer, it cannot be discharged and consequently the 
phreatic level cannot drop. It has to be noted that vapour assumed to be neglected for the 
thermal calculations of the model.  
Accordingly, the thermal boundary conditions of the model are also closed at the left and right 
border lines but the bottom boundary is set at a standard soil temperature of 270.7K (or -2.4OC). 
The field measurements showed that the soil temperature at the gneiss cluster remains almost 
stable at app. 270.7K for the entire year.

5. Numerical modelling 
 
The construction of an embankment directly on permafrost is examined at this section and its 
effect at the soil temperature beneath the ground surface. Two different embankment 
geometries have been chosen for analysis of their effect at the underlain frozen soil layer. The 
first embankment geometry consists of 150m length, 2m height and an embankment slope of 
1:1.5. The second geometry consists of the same length and slope with a height of 10m. 
5.1. Model set up – 2m height embankment 
A plain strain model was chosen to simulate the subsurface and the construction of the 
embankment. The left vertical border line of the model is a line of symmetry denoting that the 
same set up occurs at the left side of the vertical symmetry line. The model consists of three 
different clusters with each cluster representing a different material. The soil is simulated by two 
soil materials, the gneiss bedrock and the clay material of which the thermal behavior is of high 
importance, while the structure material of the embankment is crashed rock of gneiss. The 
embankment is assumed a dry cluster since according to Niels Foged the water content is 
expeted to be less than 2%. In this way, both clusters of bedrock and embankment were assumed 
dry while the clay layer is fully staurated with the phreatic level set at the initial ground surface 
(see figure 34). 
 
Figure 34: Drainage behavior of the model material sets. 
The construction of the 2m height embankment  over the clay layer is assumed to occur in March 
of 2017. The selection of this time period is of critical importance in order to avoid the  formation 
of active layer at the initial  ground surface. Furthermore, according to DMI, this month was the 
==PAGE:54==

coolest month of the year (average air temperature of -14.2oC) and consequently the soft soil 
and the pore water (in a vety high percent) are remaining at frozen state.  
More specifically, the thermal gradient of the soil effects directly the state of water in the pores. 
The water occurs in the pores in both liquid and solid state. The ice saturation indicates the 
percentage of pores covered by ice, as it is shown in figure 35 (at the initial phase). The maximum 
ice saturation of 92.85% appears close to the ground level, where the soil temperature reaches

5.4. Thermal effect on permafrost due to climate change 
 
Permafrost areas cover about one quarter of the Earth’s land area. Climate change scenarios 
predict that global warming will be intensified at the polar regions with resulting in high decrease 
of permafrost geographic range. Infrastructure systems developed over ice-rich permafrost may 
experience significant damage and settlements caused by thawed ground. Alaska, Canada, 
Siberia are some of the countries that have developed substantial infrastructure system and are 
in great risk the following years. (Nelson , et al., 2002) 
In this way, it is essential to model the effect of global warming to the active layer thickness of 
permafrost and the frozen fine-grained basins overlaid by the embankment structures in Ilulissat 
airport.  Stendel, (DMI scientist) et al.  published in 2007 a report regarding the regional climate 
change for Greenland.  More specifically, they used a climate simulation which shows the 
seasonal changes in temperature between 2021-2050 comparing to the temperature between 
1961-1990. The future climate evolution for 2021-2050, in west coast of Greenland, describes 
warmer winters of about +6oC, a rise of 4oC in Spring and an increase of 2oC during summer and 
spring with respect to the mean air temperature from 1961 to 1990. The mean monthly 
temperature values at the period between 1961-1990 are demonstrated in figure 49 (Stendel, et 
al., 2007).

5.5. Displacements due to climate change. 
 
The climate change will cause rapid thawing of the soil and consequently degradation of 
permafrost increasing the active layer thickness. On the other hand, as it has been proved at the 
chapter 5.4, the embankments prevent the thawing of underlying soil. The displacements are 
expected at the embankment slope caused mainly by the thawing of the underlying permafrost.  
The results show that the maximum displacements occur in August. The values of the first year 
(2017) and the last year of the prediction (2050) are presented in table 30. Nevertheless, these 
values should be used with caution since the model consists of linear elastic materials. The 
deformations are increasing during the thawing period, while the rest months of the year remain 
stable. In other words, the deformations are caused mainly by thermal variation through the 
seasons and not due to stresses.

6. Conclusions  
 
Infrastructure constructions in the Arctic underlain by permafrost suffer by differential 
settlements due to the phase transition of permafrost throughout the year. However, if 
permafrost material can be retained in solid state, those problems can be diminished 
considerably.  
The construction of a runway embankment directly on the frozen clay material showed that 
it can reduce the soil temperature extensively and especially during the thawing period. Both 
of tested embankments (2m and 10m height) demonstrated substantial resistance against 
the air temperature and retained the subsoil in frozen state for the first year.  
The rise of air temperature until 2050 due to climate change showed that the soil below the 
embankments will become warmer but still in freezing level. Contrary to this, the frozen clay 
approaches the thawing point in 2050 below 2m height embankment and if the temperature 
keeps rising due to climate change, 2058 will be the critical year when active layer will be 
arisen below the embankment. In other words, 2m height of embankment are not enough 
to secure the stability of the structure in the future. 
The 10m height embankment demonstrated excessive thermal resistance keeping the soil 
temperature away of the thawing point. In contrast to the 2m height embankment, the 
scenario of global warming did not change the effectiveness of the 10m height structure 
concluding that it is the most efficient structure between the two geometries. 
According to the model results, the structures are vulnerable at the embankment slopes and 
especially close to the embankment toes. The small height of the embankments at that point 
is unable to insulate adequately the subsoil resulting in the presence of the frost line below 
the ground surface. Furthermore, the problem is amplified the coming years due to climate 
change introducing insecurity at the structure.   
All the mentioned-above outcomes lead to the final conclusion that the construction of an 
embankment over the existing permafrost can alleviate the thermal conditions at the 
subsoil, but it cannot prevent the presence of the active layer below the embankment slope. 
The construction of an embankment needs to be associated with other methods and 
technologies that will engage the problem below the embankment slope.  
Artificial ground freezing, thermosyphons, ventilated ducts passing through the 
embankment are some of the tested technologies in the Arctic (e.g. in China (Dong, et al., 
2010), Tibet (Bo, et al., 2003) and Russia (Ibragimov, et al., 2013)). Further investigations 
need to be done for the optimisation of those systems and the development of other 
technologies which will allow the construction of infrastructures at permafrost areas with 
the smallest possible risk.
