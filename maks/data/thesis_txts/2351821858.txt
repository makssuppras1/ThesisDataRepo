Abstract
All large auditoria at the technical university of Denmark (DTU) are to undergo an
extensive renovation as the facilities are out-dated and expensive to maintain. The ren-
ovation of building 303A finished in September 2016 and building 116 is to undergo a
similar transformation in 2018. This study investigated the current indoor environment
of the newly renovated building 303A and the old building 116. The investigation in-
cluded, among other things, measurements and surveys during two weeks in September
and November 2016.
Compared to previous measurements of the indoor environment, new measurements
showed that the indoor air quality of building 303A had improved, but the thermal envi-
ronment had worsen as too high temperatures were recorded in several rooms. Results
from the questionnaires, filled in by the occupants, showed that the indoor air quality
was overall well perceived and 95% of the respondents found the thermal environment
acceptable. In general, the perception of building 303A had improved in regards to
personal comfort, health symptoms, and ergonomics since undergoing a renovation. In
Building 116 the temperatures and CO2 concentrations were mainly within the limits of
Category III according to DS/EN 15251 (2007) during the two weeks of measurements.
The building therefore performed well considering the construction period. However,
despite the low CO2 concentrations, the air quality was perceived as poor. The old ven-
tilation system was evaluated as a possible source of pollution, which would explain the
poorly perceived air quality. The ventilation system also resulted in complaints about
draught, which was likely caused by over-ventilating.
Computational Fluid Dynamics calculations were used to investigate the performance
of a mixing ventilation system compared to a displacement ventilation system. The
result showed that mixing ventilation created a uniform thermal environment with low
temperature differences between the front and back row, and low percentage dissatisfied
due to vertical temperature differences. However, displacement ventilation had lower
draught rates and remarkable lower CO2 concentrations in the occupied zone (between
800 ppm to 500 ppm). Displacement ventilation is therefore recommended for creating
the best indoor air quality.
By changing the ventilation design in building 116 from a CAV to a VAV system with
a heat exchanger, energy performance estimation by simulation, showed an expected
energy consumption reduction of 83% to 86%, depending on the auditorium type. The
estimated energy savings were based on different control systems for winter and summer,
and the instalment of a CO2 and temperature control system.

Resumé
Alle store auditorier på Danmarks Tekniske Universitet (DTU) skal igennem en omfat-
tende renovering, da auditorierne er forældet og dyre i drift. Renoveringen af bygning
303A blev færdiggjort i september 2016 og renoveringen af bygning 116 påbegyndes i
2018. Dette studie undersøgte det nuværende indeklima i den nyrenoverede bygning
303A og den forældede bygning 116. Undersøgelsen inkluderede bl.a. brug af målinger
og spørgeskemaer i løbet af to uger i september og november 2016.
Sammenlignet med tidligere målinger af indeklimaet i bygning 303A, viste de nye
målinger at luftkvaliteten var blevet forbedret, men at det termiske indeklima var blevet
forværret, da for høje temperaturer var blevet registreret i adskillige rum. Spørgeske-
maer, der var blevet udfyldt af brugerne, viste at luftkvaliteten blev betragtet som ud-
mærket og at 95% af de adspurgte fandt det termiske indeklima acceptabelt. Generelt
blev bygning 303A opfattet som forbedret i h.h.t.
personlig komfort, helbredsymp-
tomer og ergonomi. I bygning 116 var temperaturerne og CO2-koncentrationerne hoved-
sageligt inden for grænserne i h.h.t. Kategori III ifølge DS/EN 15251 (2007). Bygningen
klarede sig derfor godt, taget i betragtning af bygningens opførelsesperiode. På trods af
de lave CO2-koncentrationer, blev luftkvaliteten betragtet som værende dårlig, hvilket
formegentlig skyldes det gamle ventilationsystem, som kan være en forureningskilde i
sig selv. Flere af de adspurgte klagede over træk, hvilket kan skyldes overventilering af
auditorierne, som sjældent var fyldte.
Computational Fluid Dynamics beregninger blev brugt til at undersøge præstationen
af opblandingsventilation sammenlignet med fortræningsventilation. Resultaterne viste
at opblandingsventilation klarede sig bedst i forhold til at skabe et ensartet termisk
indeklima, hvor der ingen forskel var i temperatur mellem forreste og bagerste række og
kun en lille forskel i vertikaltemperatur. Fortræningsventilation viste sig dog at have
lavere risiko for træk og de laveste CO2 koncentrationer i opholdszonen (mellem 500
ppm til 800 ppm). Fortræningsventilation anbefales derfor som løsningen til at skabe
den bedste luftkvalitet.
Energiberegninger for bygning 116 viste, at energiforbruget kunne reduceres mellem
83% og 86%, afhængig af auditorietypen, ved at ændre det nuværende CAV-system
til et VAV-system. Beregningerne inkluderede ikke forbedringer af U-værdierne af kon-
struktionen, og det vurderes derfor at besparelserne potentielt kunne være større. Disse
forbedringer bygger på, at der bruges forskellige indstillinger af systemet for vinter og
sommer samt, at der installeres en varmeveksler og CO2- og temperatursensorer.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The auditoria at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) were build in the 1970’s,
where the focus on the indoor environment and teaching methods was different than to-
day. Since then, the indoor environment and its effect on humans have received greater
attention. In the 1980’s the term ”Tight Building Syndrome” or ”Sick Building Syn-
drome” (SBS) arose as buildings were built increasingly more air tight. This was done
in order to lower the heat loss and thereby the energy consumption of buildings. Oc-
cupants started to feel unwell and studies showed that the indoor environment had a
significant impact on the human health and mental performance. Today, more recent
studies show that improving the indoor environment increases the human performance
and learning abilities. This have lead to an increased focus on improving teaching facil-
ities.
This shift of focus have made the auditoria at DTU out-dated and in need of improve-
ment. By renovation it is possible to make the indoor environment more comfortable,
but also save energy on heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC). DTU aspires
to do so and have developed a 10-year-plan to perform extensive renovation on all the
big auditoria at DTU. As a part of this plan, the newly renovated building 303A was
finished in September 2016 after a major renovation. Next auditoria to be renovated are
in building 116.
Aim
The aim of this project was to focus on the indoor environment of building 116 and the
upcoming renovation. Therefore, an investigation of the physical and perceived indoor
environment of building 116 had to be carried out. In order to evaluate the effect of a
renovation, investigations of the newly renovated building 303A also had to be included.
By the use of these investigation and studies of different ventilation designs, suggestions
to a feasible design proposal of the future building 116 had to be made.

Problem Statement
The problem statement was drawn up in August 2016 - nothing has been changed or
added to the description since.
DTUs auditoria were built at a time, where focus on teaching methods, building materials,
and indoor environment was less than today. Over the years, the indoor environment
and its effect on humans have received greater attention. This shift of focus has made
the auditoriums at DTU outdated and in need of improvement.
As a solution to the outdated auditoriums, DTU have made a 10 year-plan in order
to renovate and improve all the auditoriums at DTU. Here the challenge is to create
a good indoor environment that meets the demand of both students, teachers, and the
owner of the building.
This project will focus on the renovation of building 116. Building 116s auditoria are
outdated both in regard to the indoor environment and the ventilation design. For in-
stance, the auditoria have no access of daylight and no view to the outdoors for the
users, due to the placement in the center of the building. These are important param-
eters in regards to a good indoor environment. The ventilation system is an outdated
CAV system that often provide too high air changes, when the auditoria are only partial
filled or completely empty. The over dimensioned ventilation system also results in a
high energy demand, because of the unnecessary use of resources. Major changes in both
the visual and technical design have to be made, in order to improve the auditoria both
in regards to indoor environment and energy consumption. Ideas for renovation of b.
116 will be based on knowledge acquired from earlier renovation at DTU and current
guidelines for indoor environment and state-of-the-art teaching facilities e.g. by the use
of a previous renovation and measurements of b. 303. The results from previous projects
will be included in order to develop a solution to a good indoor environment suitable for
building 116. New measurements and surveys will also be carried out on building 116,
in order to establish knowledge about the exact problem points of this particular building.
More specifically, the indoor environment and user perceptions and ideas for upgrading
of b. 116 will be characterized by measurements and questionnaires distributed among
students in b.116.
The outcome of the project will describe the current state of the indoor environment
and user perceptions in the auditoria in b. 116 and a proposal of elements that should
be particularly considered in the renovation of b.
116.
Building simulation methods
will also be developed in order to estimate the best possible solution in regards to energy
consumption and indoor environment.

2.1
The Indoor Environment
The indoor environment has long been known to influence human work performance.
However, many different dependent and independent variables influence the perception
of a good indoor environment. As an example, Lee et al. (2012) investigated the indoor
environment quality (IEQ) at a university in Hong Kong. Parameters such as air quality,
lighting conditions, aural experiences etc. were measured and compared to the students
self-evaluation of their work performance. Overall the study showed a good correlation
between the number of complaints and learning performance. This shows the possibility
of evaluating the subjective work performance based on objective and subjective mea-
surements. In the following, different parameters of the indoor environment and their
effect on humans are presented.
2.1.1
Thermal Environment
A literature survey by Frontczak and Wargocki (2011) showed that in office and teaching
buildings, thermal comfort was ranked with higher importance than visual and acoustic
comfort, and good indoor air quality. Same study concluded that thermal comfort had a
higher impact on the overall satisfaction with the indoor environment than other indoor
environment factors.
A test performed on children in the age of 10 to 12 by Wargocki and Wyon (2007),
showed that children performed better in numerical and language based tests, when
the temperature was lowered from 25 ℃to 20 ℃. This improvement of performance
by lowering the temperature, was also found in adults by Lan et al. (2011). The study
investigated the impact of thermal comfort/discomfort on the work performance. Perfor-
mance was measured through different tasks e.g. typing and visual learning at different
air temperatures. The study concluded that thermal discomfort lead to reduced per-
formance, while the optimum performance could be obtained at a thermal sensation
slightly below neutral. All three studies highlight the importance of thermal comfort in
the indoor environment.
2.1.2
Air Quality
A study by Seppänen et al. (2006) showed a significant increase of performance in office
work, when the ventilation rate was increased. The relative performance as a function of
the ventilation rate, can be seen in Figure 2.1. The increase of ventilation rate reduced
the indoor pollutant concentration, but at high ventilation rates the indoor pollutant
would reach the outdoor concentration and the performance would stabilize. The study
also showed that too high ventilation rates could create draught and a high noise level,
which could diminish the performance.

Increase of performance by increase of the ventilation rate was also investigated by
Haverinen-Shaughnessy et al. (2011). The study tested 5th grade students by standard-
ized tests. A significant increase in the proportion who passed the test was found, when
the ventilation rate was increased.
Increasing ventilation rates have also been found to have a negative effect on the human
performance. Wargocki et al. (2004) investigated the ventilation rate in regards to the
use of old and new filters in the ventilation system. The performance of the employees
in a call-center was monitored. The study showed that increasing the ventilation rate
in systems with old filters, reduced their performance. If the filters were replaced with
new ones, the performance increased with increasing ventilation rates. This underlines
the importance of a good air quality.
CO2 is commonly used to evaluate the indoor air quality, as it is a good indicator
of human bioeffluents in the indoor environment (Petersen, 2005). However Satish et al.
(2012) showed that CO2 alone can effect the human performance. Compared to a CO2
concentration of 600 ppm, a concentration of 1000 ppm showed a significant decrease
in six of nine decision-making performance test. These decreased further for CO2 con-
centrations above 2500 ppm. High CO2 concentrations should therefore be avoided by
proper ventilation.
Lowering the concentration of pollutants not only affects the perceived air quality but
also the prevalence of sick building syndrome (SBS) symptoms. Wargocki et al. (2000)
investigated the perceived air quality and SBS symptoms in a group office. By increasing
the ventilation rate the percentage of dissatisfied decreased and the sensation of dryness
in mouth and throat was also improved significantly. It also made the subjects feel sig-

2.1.3
Lighting
The preferred level and type of light in a work place highly depends on the type of work
that has to be performed. According to a literature study by Moeller et al. (2011), light
with high color temperatures increase the alertness and activity. The study suggest that
the lighting-system should not be designed to focus on increasing the work performance
alone, as the circadian rhythm could be disturbed and cause health problems (Boyce,
2006).
A study by Borisuit et al. (2015) compared the exposure of artificial and natural light in
a classroom with healthy subjects between 20-30 years of age. The study discovered that
the subjects overall preferred natural light over artificial light, despite having no direct
outside view. Even a short exposure to natural light have proven to have a positive
effect on humans. Kaida et al. (2006) carried out a study on 16 females, with the object
of examining the exposure of natural bright light on afternoon sleepiness. The study
concluded that brief exposure to bright natural light decreased afternoon sleepiness.
Although natural light without a view to the outdoors, is preferred over artificial light,
the use of artificial natural light cannot replace windows. Offices without windows have
proven to cause a higher degree of tension and anxiety in the occupants (Chang and
Chen, 2005). Same study also showed that a landscaped view caused less nervousness
than a cityscape view. Indoor plants also had a similar effect, but was not as effective
as the instalment of windows.
2.1.4
Acoustics
Although thermal comfort is ranked of higher importance by occupants than the acoustic
environment (Frontczak and Wargocki, 2011), the acoustic environment is still impor-
tant to the perceived indoor environment. Lee et al. (2012) investigated the IEQ at
university-teaching rooms. The study showed that the acoustical environment was the
major determining factor for the overall IEQ. The IEQ votes showed to be particular
sensitive to the aural experience by the occupants.
Many different sound sources effect the acoustic experience and depending on the activ-
ity, the acoustic comfort varies (Meng and Kang, 2016). For teaching rooms or auditoria
in particular, there are three main sources of sound: The teacher or lecturer, the back-

2.1.5
Auditoria in particular
Auditoria or theatres are rooms, where special requirements to the ventilation applies
due to size of the room and the high occupant density. Auditoria frequently operate at
high occupancy level, which creates high sensible heat loads. These high loads have to be
removed to keep a comfortable indoor temperature, and since the air has to be distributed
over a wide area, design solutions have to be found that can create a comfortable indoor
environment. Todt (1987) introduced three types of ventilation suitable for auditoria:
• Displacement ventilation
• Mixing ventilation
• Wall ventilation (A composition of displacement and mixing)
These are known ventilation types, but found suitable because they could abide by
the special requirements for the high ceiling and large number of occupants. The concept
of displacement and mixing ventilation are well described by Petersen (2005) and can
be seen in Figure 2.2. The implementation of these ventilation concepts in auditoria are
described in the following.

Displacement Ventilation
In auditoria the seating is often fixed, making it ideal to install fixtures below or near
the seats. This way the air is supplied directly into the occupied zone. This can be done
either by installing inlets just below the seat or in the desktops (Todt, 1987), Figure 2.3.
The exact placement and type of diffusers will depend on the chosen design.
Figure 2.3: Concept of displacement ventilation used in Auditoria with fixtures integrated
below the seats (left) or in tables (right)
A literature study by Kosonen and Tan (2004) showed that displacement ventilation
overall leads to an improvement of indoor air quality compared to mixing, when used in
offices and classrooms. Displacement ventilation proved effective in removing pollutants,
and had a ventilation effectiveness as high as 150-200%. However, Kavgic et al. (2008)
showed that displacement ventilation used in a theatre with inlets at feet level, leads to a
higher number of complaints of discomfort caused by cold draught at the occupantsf́eet.
In order to avoid draught, the velocity and temperature of the supply air will have to
be carefully controlled.
Mixing Ventilation
Mixing ventilation typically supplies air through the ceiling, which creates a free open
space. This type of ventilation is suitable in multifunctional rooms where large floor
space is needed or for movable seating arrangements. However, based on the principle
of only mixing the air, the ventilation effectiveness can only reach 100 % at full mixing
(Kosonen and Tan, 2004).
Mixing ventilation has also been shown to cause risk of
thermal discomfort due to draught in several studies (Kavgic et al., 2008) (Shan et al.,
2016). Although mixing ventilation creates a free floor area, the choice of diffuser and
ventilation rate has to be carefully dimensioned to avoid draught problems.

Wall Ventilation
Wall ventilation can either be a concept of displacement or mixing ventilation depending
on the placement of the diffusers and the properties of the inlet air. Wall ventilation (like
mixing) creates an open space but the risk of draught near the diffusers is high. Similar
to mixing ventilation, the placement of occupants should therefore not be taken without
consideration. A study by Shan et al. (2016) investigated the use of wall ventilation
in tutorial rooms at a university. The results showed that displacement ventilation by
wall diffusers lead to a significantly higher draught risk and colder sensation in the lower
body level compared to mixing, which caused an overall draught sensation.

2.2
Energy Performance
The use of non-sustainable energy in the past have put the world into global warming,
and the urbanization of the increasing population around the world, have also created
a high demand of resources. The desire to create a good indoor environment challenges
the buildings’ energy consumption. In Denmark, buildings alone constitutes up to 40%
of the total energy consumption (”Bygningsministeriet”, 2014). The distribution of the
energy within the building should therefore be done with consideration of limiting the
energy use, but without jeopardising a comfortable indoor environment.
Several approaches can be used to create a comfortable thermal environment in build-
ings. During heating season in Denmark it is most common to use a water based heating
system such as radiators or under-floor heating. In building 116 at DTU the thermal
environment solemnly depends on the air supply, which cause a high energy demand,
as there is no heat recovery. Implementing air-to-air heat recovery in the ventilation
system would lower the energy consumption of axillary heating/cooling significantly
(Besant and Simonson, 2000). Depending on the number of heating degree days (HDD)
and the type of system, the use of a heat-exchanger in residential buildings have a

mean of a 20 minute interval. Only data recorded on weekdays from 8:00-17:00 were

registered number of occupants. The four time-intervals were: 8:00-10:00, 10:00-12:00,

4.4 CFD
4.4.4
Model Validation
Model validation was done by monitoring the residuals for the pressure, velocities (x,y
and z), kinetic energy and dissipation turbulence, temperature, and concentration. The
calculations were run at maximum 2000 iterations, if not converged. If the model did
not converge, the result with the lowest value of residuals was used.
The model was also validated by checking if the room temperatures stabilised. The
room temperatures were monitored by placing monitor points behind each occupant at
0.1, 0.6, and 1.1 m above ground. These monitor points were also used for analysing
the temperature, airflow patterns, and CO2 concentrations within the occupied zone.
One monitor point was also placed at the exhaust. This was used to adjust the wall
temperatures if the heat load removed from the model didn’t correspond to the internal
heat loads. Heat load calculations can be found in Appendix G.

In this chapter the findings from the six previous chapters are discussed in regards to the
current indoor environment in building 116 and 303A. This chapter also discusses the
use of building simulation tools to evaluate the future indoor environment and energy
performance of building 116.

10.1
Indoor Environment of Building 303A
Before building 303A was renovated, measurements of the indoor climate were carried
out by Kristensen (2015). Results were obtained for Auditorium 41, 42, and 44 during
one week in February and April 2015. The study showed that the building performed
well in regards to the thermal environment, when considering the type of building, con-
struction period etc. In regards to the IAQ, the building did not perform well, when
considering the CO2 concentrations. It was concluded that ineffective ventilation was
the main reason for the poor performance.
After the renovation, the IAQ seemed to have improved.
Only Auditorium 42 had
significant problems with too many hours of high CO2 levels. The thermal environment
however, seemed to have worsened, as too high temperatures occurred in several rooms.
Prior to the renovation, the ventilation system was a CAV system. The new VAV system
uses as low a ventilation rate as possible to provide satisfactory temperature and CO2
levels. However, there is some delay in the control system, which creates a risk of reach-
ing too high temperatures. Despite the many hours of overheating, most occupants felt
neutral to slightly warm. Overall a large proportion still found the thermal environment
comfortable, between 80% to 95% depending on the room. For the entire building as a
whole, 95% found the thermal environment acceptable.
The statistical comparison with building 116 showed that building 303A was perceived
better in regards to thermal comfort, acceptability, air quality, light, sound, and clean-
liness. All which showed that the personal comfort was improved by the renovation. In
particular, many resources were used to improve the electrical lighting and the view to
the outdoors in building 303A. This was proven to be significantly better than building
116 and studies show that artificial daylight improves the mood, alertness and well-
beeing compared to ordinary electrical lighting (Borisuit et al., 2015).
Building 303A also performed better than building 116 in regards to health symptoms.
Linear regression showed that a temperature decrease lead to a higher score of irritated
eyes. This means that the elevated temperature, which was observed in some auditoria,
would lead to less eye irritation. An inverse relation was found by Smedbold et al. (2001)
between temperature and irritated eyes. Although Smedbold et al. (2001) also found
that relative humidity and alternation of indoor microbial flora had an effect on irritated
eyes. Hence the results might not be conclusive.
It was expected that the temperature also had an effect on thermal comfort, but this
was only the case if the significance level was raised to 10%, which would increase the
risk of wrongfully accepting a correlation. According to Lan et al. (2011) the alertness
and work ability would also be influenced by the temperature. This was not found to
be significant conclusive in this study.

Indoor Environment of Building 116
In building 116 too low temperature occurred during September, with temperatures be-

temperatures could then be explained by over-ventilating outside occupancy. During oc-
cupancy the temperatures were usually within Category III according to DS/EN 15251
(2007), which is recommended for existing buildings.

most of the occupants in Auditorium 81 felt neutral. Most occupants in Auditoria 82
and 83 felt slightly cool, even though the temperatures in these auditoria were within
Category III (19 ℃-25 ℃) and even Category II (20 ℃-24 ℃). Building 116 uses mixing

compared to the thermal sensation, as measurements showed a temperature difference

quality was not perceived as good by the occupants. This contradiction was confirmed by

and the perceived air quality. However CO2 is only an indicator of human bioeffluents,

old ventilation systems may be source of pollution itself (Pejtersen et al., 1989) (Burge
et al., 1990). A study by Wargocki et al. (2004) even showed that if the air change was
increased the perceived air quality would decrease, due to the dirty filters. Building 116
is almost 50 years old and the HVAC system is similar to the original. Hence, it could
be the reason for the poor perceived air quality.

10.3
Indoor Environment by CFD
This sections briefly discusses the CFD findings in regards to which ventilation system
were most suitable for the different auditorium layouts.
Mixing Ventilation
Overall, mixing ventilation created an even distribution of air temperature. This re-
sulted in a low PD due to vertical temperature difference for both the traditional and
new auditoria layout. The temperature difference between each row was insignificant
for both the traditional and new auditorium layout. Mixing ventilation had to supply
air at low temperatures in order to obtain a comfortable temperature range within the
occupied zone. This is a disadvantage during cooling season, where a high supply tem-
perature is preferable in order to save energy on cooling. During heating season, low
supply temperature can be an advantage, as energy on heating can be saved. In the new
auditorium layout higher supply temperatures could be used, as the internal heat load
was lower than with the traditional layout.
Low supply temperatures created some draught problems in the traditional layout, even
though the average air speed was within Category I. Thermal discomfort due to draught
is a known problem with mixing ventilation (Kavgic et al., 2008)(Shan et al., 2016).
However the draught rate was still below 10%, which was within Category II. Draught
should not be a problem in the new auditorium layout, as the draught rates were below
the limits of 5%.
The disadvantage of mixing ventilation was seen in the CO2 concentrations, where high
air flow rates had to be used, in order to keep the CO2 concentration below 900 ppm.
In both the traditional and new auditoria the CO2 concentration was above 900 ppm
at minimum ventilation rate (7.4-7.8 l/s/pers.).
At maximum ventilation rate (13.7
l/s/pers) the CO2 concentration came just below 900 ppm. This is due to the ventila-
tion effectiveness of mixing ventilation, which can only reach 100% at best (Kosonen
and Tan, 2004).
Displacement Ventilation
Displacement ventilation clearly showed an uneven distribution of the air temperature.
Since displacement ventilation is buoyancy driven, this was not unexpected.The uneven
temperature distribution created small differences in the temperature between the front
and back rows of approx 2 ℃. This difference was also found in the measurements in
building 303A, which used the same ventilation type. This mean that occupants in the
back row could be exposed to higher temperatures than the front row, hence the percep-
tion of the thermal environment will differ, as shown in Chapter 6.

10.3 Indoor Environment by CFD
The buoyancy driven flow created a large number of PD (7%-30%) due to vertical tem-
perature differences, which was worst in the traditional layout. The PD was significantly
lowered by increasing the air flow, therefore a change of diffuser size could defeat the
problem. However, previous studies have shown that displacement ventilation may cause
a cold sensation in the lower body level (Shan et al., 2016). Kavgic et al. (2008) inves-
tigated thermal comfort in theatres, and concluded that a high number of complaints
of cold discomfort, due to cold air around occupants feet. Some of the respondents in
building 303A even complained about cold feet in the questionnaires, Chapter 6. The
vertical temperature differences are therefore an important matter to consider, when
using displacement ventilation in auditoria.
In contrast to mixing ventilation, displacement ventilation used high supply temper-
atures (up to 23 ℃) to create comfortable room temperatures in the auditoria. This is
an advantage during the cooling season, as cooling is often expensive. During heating
season heat sources would have to be installed in the room (e.g. water radiators) in
order to use low supply temperatures.
The air velocities and the draught rate were within Category I for both auditorium
layouts. The displacement ventilation used theatre diffusers with a low air supply for
each inlet, hence the air velocities are small when leaving the diffuser.
One of the benefits of displacement ventilation is the high ventilation effectiveness (Koso-
nen and Tan, 2004). This could be seen in the CO2 concentration within the auditoria.
At minimum ventilation rate the CO2 concentration was well within Category II and at
maximum ventilation the average CO2 concentration was just below 500 ppm. However,
because the ventilation is buoyancy driven the CO2 concentration was higher at the
back rows than at the front. Again, this could also be seen in the measurements carried
out in building 303A. Changing the exhaust to the front of the room, did not seem to
eliminate this factor, as a thermal stack still occurred in the room, creating an increase
of temperature with height.

10.4
Energy Performance
The energy performance calculations by IDA-ICE showed that by changing the venti-
lation type from a CAV to a VAV system with a heat exchanger reduced the energy
consumption by 79% to 90%, depending on the auditorium. This showed how much
energy is wasted on heating and over-ventilating by the use of a CAV system in build-
ing 116. However, changing the ventilation type alone did not create a comfortable
indoor climate, which could uphold the requirements from Building Regulation (2015)
nor DS/EN 15251 (2007). In particular, full occupancy lead to too high room temper-
atures and reduced occupancy lead to too low room temperatures. A heating system
had to be installed in order to create a more stable and comfortable thermal environment.
Overheating was reduced by night ventilation, which meant that the traditional and
new audotria upheld the requirements by Building Regulation (2015). Too many hours
of overheating still occurred in the large auditorium 81 on a yearly basis. However, sim-
ulation was run at full occupancy for a year and the registration of occupancy during
September and November revealed that auditorium 81 was only at max. 80% capacity.
It could therefore be considered to lower the number of seats in the large auditoria and
thereby reduce the hours of overheating. By improving the furniture and the auditorium
layout, a reduction of occupancy per m2 was implemented in the large Auditorium 42 in
building 303A. Similar changes to Auditorium 81 would reduce the occupancy and also
improve the interior.
The use of a cooling coil reduced the number of hours with overheating for all audi-
toria. However, this also increased the energy demand between 2.0% to 3.1% depending
on the auditorium. For the smaller auditoria the requirements for DS/EN 15251 (2007)
could be met without a cooling coil, but with night ventilation. For Auditorium 81 night
ventilation was not sufficient and a cooling coil would have to be implemented to meet
Category II in DS/EN 15251 (2007).
Overall, the yearly energy demand of Auditorium 81 was expected to be reduced by
83% with the use of a cooling coil. For Auditoria 82 and 83 the energy reduction was
expected to be approx. 84% if the traditional layout was kept. If the new layout was
implemented, the energy consumption would be reduced from 380 kWh/m2/year to 51.2
kWh/m2/year, which is a reduction of almost 87%. These reductions corresponds to
findings by Scnell and Inthout (2001) who estimated that up to 80% could be saved on
energy, alone by chaining the VAV system to a demand controlled system.

10.5 Assumptions and Methods
10.5
Assumptions and Methods
This section discusses some of the inaccuracies and uncertainties related to the methods
that were used throughout this study.
Measurements of the indoor climate were only carried out during a period of two weeks
in September and November. These periods were used as representative for heating
and cooling season. However, yearly measurements should be carried out in order to
ensure representative data for heating and cooling. Especially during heating season
as November was warmer than the average according to DMI (2016a). The occupancy
will also change from season to season, as the auditoria are used for different courses
during spring, summer, and autumn which might expose some other issues in the indoor
environment.
In order to get results that were representative for the occupied zone, the equipment was
placed at table tops, near the occupants. This created a risk of direct interference of the
data by the occupants, either by breathing onto the equipment, touching the equipment,
or moving the equipment. All which might have resulted in misleading data for the
temperature and CO2 concentrations. Since the equipment wasn’t monitored, the effect
of this variable was unknown. The placement of the equipment also meant that some
of the data was insufficient, because the logger was unplugged from the electrical socket
by accident. This was avoided to some extent, by installing two loggers in some rooms.
This was not done in all rooms due to limited equipment available.
The use of an on-line questionnaire had some disadvantages.
Although it was easy
for most occupants to answer the questionnaire, it also meant that it was not possible to
ensure that the occupants were present in the room while answering. In order to obtain
as many answers as possible, the questionnaires were sent out to the teachers, who then
had to inform the students. This passing of information meant that not all students
were informed correctly about when the questionnaire should be filled in. This was a
particular problem during September, where it was assumed that the occupants were
present in the room, when answering the questionnaire between 8:00-17:00. This could
have given a misleading results, especially in regards to personal comfort.
The linear regression analysis assumed that data residuals followed a normal distribu-
tion. However, the qq-norm plot showed that the models could have been improved. In
general the correlation coefficients were low, between 1% to 5% for most of the simple
regression lines. For multiple linear regression the correlation coefficients were stronger,
indicating that several factors influenced the occupants perception of different variables.
Non linear-correlations could also be investigated to see if different correlations existed.
In regards to the CFD model, only two types of ventilation designs were investigated
for each room. A combination of the two ventilation designs have previously been in-

vestigated by Clausen and Ravn (2015). However, the use of two different supply types
could be expensive, and would also take up space. If both ceiling and theatre diffusers
had to be installed, the ceiling height would be reduced significantly, hence eliminating
the benefits of a large air volume per occupant. Because of this, a solution with both
DV and MV was not investigated.
Far from all CFD models had a converged solution, meaning that there were some
uncertainties in the results. DV is buoyancy-driven ventilation, which can create some
problems in the turbulence model. Several studies show that a k-epsilon model fails for
CFD simulations with displacement ventilation. Gilani et al. (2016) showed that the
choice of turbulance model had a great impact on the age of the air, which ultimately
changes the temperature distribution. Cehlin et al. (2014) described how the use of a k-
epsilon model generates to high eddy viscosity and therefore damps out the unsteadiness
of the flow, which should occur with DV, due to the buoyancy and momentum forces.
A solution to improve the model, could be to improve the diffuser design.
Dis-
placement/theatre diffusers are very complicated and should not be treated as a simple
opening in CFD (as done in the model). Zhang et al. (2009) suggest to make some
changes to the grid, by blocking some cells in front of the inlet. The study showed an
agreement in the computed profiles and the measured ones.
Most importantly, when using CFD to simulate the indoor climate, is to consider that
the model calculates a steady-state result. In reality many parameters changes during
the course of a day or a year. Therefore, it was important also to carry out yearly
simulations by another simulation tool, in this case IDA-ICE. However, the CFD still
gave a detailed idea of how the thermal environment and air flow would be like for the
different types of ventilation in building 116.
Simulations of the energy performance provided an idea of the yearly energy demand.
However, the improvements to the different auditoria did not include an improvement
of the building envelope or the efficiency of the different ventilation components, such
as the fan and heat exchanger. These would most likely be improved, which should
lower the actual energy consumption even further. Also, the yearly energy consumption
included full occupancy from 8:00-12:00 and 13:00-17:00, which may not be the case for
the future building 116. However, this provided a ”worse-case” result, as a reduction of
occupants would reduce the ventilation rate and thereby the energy consumption. The
reduction could also result in a higher heat demand, but the result showed that the
energy saved on ventilation was higher.

CHAPTER 11
Conclusion
Overall, the current indoor environment in building 116 was well perceived and was
within Category III in regards to thermal environment and CO2 concentration (DS/EN
15251, 2007). However, there were occupants who complained about draught and felt
cold despite acceptable room temperatures. The occupants also found the indoor air
quality poor, despite that the measured CO2 concentrations were mainly below 700
ppm. This unexpected result could be explained by the old ventilation system, which
can be a source of pollution itself. Hence, the high ventilation rate was not able to lower
the air pollution.
Building 303A performed significantly better than building 116 for most parameters
related to personal comfort, health symptoms, and ergonomics. In particular, between
94% and 96% of the occupants found the thermal environment acceptable in building
303A. This was approx. 10%-points higher than building 116. Occupants also felt signif-
icantly better, more alert, and more concentrated in building 303A. All are factors which
will improve the students work ability. Previous investigations of the indoor environment
revealed problems with poor air quality (Kristensen, 2015). This was no longer an issue
after renovation, as the overall perception of the air quality had improved. However,
too high temperatures and CO2 concentrations were recorded in some rooms during full
occupancy, which meant that Category II (DS/EN 15251, 2007) was not fully met.
By CFD calculations both mixing and displacement ventilation was investigated for two
different auditorium layouts in building 116. The CFD model showed that keeping the
existing mixing ventilation system could create draught risk at low supply temperatures
and high CO2 concentrations. Displacement ventilation performed better in regards to
draught risk and the CO2 concentration, which was far below the limit of 900 ppm.
However, due to the buoyancy driven air flow, vertical temperature differences lead to
a high percentage dissatisfied (up to 30%) during low flow rate. It was estimated this
could be avoided by using a smaller inlet size. Based on this the displacement ventilation
is recommended in order to obtain the best indoor air quality.
Energy performance calculations showed that by changing the current ventilation sys-
tem, from a constant air volume to a variable air volume system, the yearly energy
consumption for buidling 116 was reduced significantly. For the large Auditorium 81
the energy use was reduced from 400 kwh/m2 down to 64.4 kwh/m2, which is a reduction
of approx 83%. For the smaller auditoria, the yearly energy consumption was estimated
to be reduced from 380 kwh/m2 down to 51.2 kwh/m2 or 61.8 kwh/m2, depending on

Ventilation Calculations
Minimum ventilation rate was calculated according to DS/EN 15251 (2007) Category
II for low polluting buildings.
For traditional layout:
q = 7l/s/pers · n + 0.35l/s/m2 · A = 7l/s/pers. · 130pers. + 0.35l/s/m2 · 159m2 = 966l/s
For only half a room, the ventilation rate would be 483 l/s or 1735 m3/h.
For new layout:
q = 7l/s/pers · n + 0.35l/s/m2 · A = 7l/s/pers · 70pers. + 0.35l/s/m2 · 159m2 = 546l/s
For only half a room, the ventilation rate would be 273 l/s or 1001 m3/h.
