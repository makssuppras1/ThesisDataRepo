Abstract
Beyond the traditional usage of seaweeds for human, animal consumption and additive pro-
duction, the unique characteristics and composition of macroalgae oﬀer new possibilities for
the production of bio-based products such as biofuels and commodity chemicals. Advantages
over other biomass, such as, the lack of necessity of land, fertilizers or freshwater for growing,
are recognized in macroalgae, making it a promising source. Specially, brown seaweeds oﬀers
signiﬁcant potential for the future development of bio-based products through fermentative
pathways due to their high carbohydrate content. However, the technologies required for its
processing are currently immature and the research in this ﬁeld is mostly oriented towards the
production of biofuels. This leads to fail in the leverage of all its potential when producing
commodity chemicals, as lactic acid (LA). The brown algae comprises of several carbohydrates
such as laminaria, mannitol and alginate, not present in other biomasses, that are unequally
metabolized. The monomers that constitute laminaria, cellulose and mannitol are assimilated
by the bacteria lactobacillus casei, following the Embden-Meyerhof-Parnas (EMP) glycolytic
pathway producing lactic acid. However, despite the research on sacchariﬁcation of alginate,
microorganisms able to ferment it to lactic acid have not been found yet.
With these boundaries, the production process of LA from brown algae is designed with a
capacity of 11 tonne/hour of LA so that its viability is comparable with exiting production
scales LA plants. Thus, four potential scenarios are simulated by rigorous Aspen Plus models
for polymer-grade LA production, starting from wet Laminaria macroalgae. This simulations
give as a result the inventories that subsequently are required to perform the techno-economic
(TEA) and environmental assessment (LCA).
Techno-economic models are established according to mass and energy balances in order to in-
vestigate the feasibility of these new process technologies. Conﬁgurations for heat recovery are
suggested to maximize the productivity and estimate minimum lactic acid selling price (MSP)
and maximum feedstock price (MFP). These are calculated in order to select best design among
several possible conﬁgurations. Subsequently, sensitivity analysis is applied on the scenarios
which present improved feasibility and the factors which have highest impacts are determined.
The utility usage appears to be a relevant factor which makes the plant location critical due to
high regional price diﬀerences in the various parts of the world. Moreover, the feedstock com-
position is another critical factor, since depending on the percentage of utilizable carbohydrates
the proﬁtability completely changes. The process does not recover the investment when using
an average composition of Laminiaria sp. and the diﬀerence between the MSP and the current
selling price (SP) is 10%. On the other hand, when using L. digitata, whose composition of
carbohydrates is higher, the diﬀerence between the MSP and the SP is a 2%. Besides, if the
hypothetical fermentation of alginate is considered, the capital invested is recovered before the
11th year. This result shows that investment in microorganisms able to metabolize alginate to
LA would be justiﬁed.
Finally, the mass and energy balances of the simulations are appraised for a LCA, performed as
a complementary study to the TEA. The LCA results of the scenarios which were considered
most feasible from the economical perspective, challenge the results of the economic scenar-
ios conducted. Although being economically imperious does not automatically result in them
having a better environmental performance.

Introduction
Humans have been using fossil fuels for decades. However the concern about the depletion
of these resources, together with the eﬀects of their usage (e.g.
climate change), is rising
in the society. All fossil fuels are ﬁnite and non-renewable and the ecosystems assimilation
capacity is limited. Moving towards the mitigation of these factors and the development of an
environmentally, economically and socially sustainable global economy, bioreﬁnery appears as
a solution.
Bioreﬁnery entails a range of technologies able to produce products, fuels and chemicals from
biomass sources.
The concept oﬀers wide possibilities for the production of bioenergy and
chemicals through the depolymerization and deoxygenation of the biomass; thermochemical,
biochemical, mechanical and chemical. This approach leads to a radical change in the industry
that will require new technologies for processing the biomass. Thereby, economic studies to
check the feasibility of the processes as well as an environmental analysis of the new bio-based
products impacts will be needed. [1]
1.1
Transition to biobased products
Targeting the shift from fossil to bio-based raw materials following the bioreﬁnery concept,
the research is focused towards ﬁnding plant or organic wastes able to substitute the non-
renewable resources used in the traditional reﬁneries. Thus, diﬀerent feedstocks are considered
as potential alternatives; starchy, lignocellulosic and algal biomass. However, they widely diﬀer
in composition (Table 1) and therefore, the technologies and processes required to obtain bio-
based products from them are very speciﬁc for each of the biomasses.

Thus, in order to obtain the maximum valorization of the biomass, bioreﬁneries carry out
integrated processes which produce not only bio-based products (biofuels and chemicals) but
also secondary energy carriers (power, heat). However, the production of biofuels as alternatives
for the conventional transportation fuels is leading the development of the sector.
On the
other hand, the chemical sector has also a high importance from the economic point of view.
In petroleum reﬁnery, while just a 15% of the oil barrel goes to the production of chemical
products, it accounts for around 50% of the proﬁt, 30% of the energy consumption and 20% of
the greenhouse emissions. Thus, the bio-based chemical production from biomass is considered
a promising niche whose market share will grow from 2% in 2008 to 22% in 2025. [3]

1.2
Various feedstock categories for bioreﬁneries
The ﬁrst attempt for substituting the fossil-fuel commodities for bio-based products was by
means of starchy biomass or food crops. They have a high sugar or oil content making them easy
to convert into high-value products. The biofuels bioethanol and biodiesel, whose feedstocks
are seeds and grains such as wheat, corn and rapeseed are the most manufactured. The agri-
cultural crops composition (mainly cellulose and starch) makes the conversion into bio-based
products relatively easy, bio-fuels and bio-based chemicals. Their technologies and procedures
are already well developed and have been analyzed through Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) in
order to point out their environmental performances. These studies have mainly shown a net
reduction in global warming emissions and fossil energy consumption. However, when study-
ing other environmental aspects (e.g acidiﬁcation, land use, etc) most of the assessments have
concluded that these parameters are highly aﬀected by assumptions and the substitution of
fossil fuels by biofuels leads to increased negative impacts. In addition to the environmental
impact, the skepticism about its carbon balances and the competition with food for agricultural
land, leading to the rise of food prices, has prompted the deviation of the studies towards other
feedstocks. [1], [4]
The limitations discerned in the products from starchy biomass are expected to be partially
overcome with the lignocellulosic biomass. Lignocellulosic biomass entails agricultural, indus-
trial and forest residues and non-food crops. The lignocellulosic materials can be considered
as the most abundant bio-renewable source on earth. LCA studies done in terms of land used
eﬃciency and environmental performance show their advantages over starchy biomass.
Be-
sides, lignocellulosic feedstocks do not interfere in the food supplies since utilize waste or the
non-edible portion of the plant for producing value-added products. However, this feedstock is
considered hard to convert due to the complexity of its plant cell, which is a mixture of the
polysaccharides: (i) hemicellulose, (ii) cellulose, and (iii) lignin. Hemicellulose and cellulose are
chemically linked to the lignin, making diﬃcult the processing and depolymerization into their
respective monomers. Thus, for obtaining the monomers, expensive pretreatments, with high
cost and energy demand are required. [1],[5],[6].
Therefore, the interest in algal biomass is growing. Algal biomass represents a diverse group of
photosynthetic organisms that can be converted into fuel, power or value added chemicals. The
interest in this feedstock is increasing due to advantages over terrestrial crops such as growing
faster, having higher energy yield per area and not being in direct competition for land with
food crops [7].

1.3
Scope of the project
Bioreﬁnery is understood as a potential alternative to the fossil fuel-based market that is
developed without aﬀecting the economy and ecosystems. Thereby, in order to evaluate the
feasibility of the replacement of any existing product, one needs to calculate the total economic
and environmental impact of the newly developed product.
Techno-economic assessment (TEA) and life cycle assessment (LCA) are tools that are being
currently used in order to assess the feasibility of the new alternatives to fuel-based products.
TEA is an approach to understand the commercial viability of the new feedstocks regarding
factors such as process design, mass and energy balance and capital and project cost. On the
other hand, LCA is a method that determines quantitatively the impact of a product life cycle
on the environment taking into account factors such as global warming potential, eutrophica-
tion or land use, among others. Thus, in order to perform LCA to processes at an early stage,
reliable inventories are required. The latter can be achieved by tools as Aspen Plus which by
means of rigorous calculation of mass and energy balances populates such inventories.
The scope of the study is to investigate the possibilities of algal biomass as feedstock for biore-
ﬁneries, specially for the production of bio-based chemicals. The main focus lies on the design of
a process which produces a commodity chemical (lactic acid) with polymer-grade purity through
a fermentative pathway and the performance of a techno-economic evaluation. Diﬀerent con-
ﬁgurations are designed and economically assessed for eventually determine the feasibility of
the process. Besides, after evaluating the diﬀerent options, the inventories of commodities and
utilities of the most economically feasible scenarios are allocated to perform an environmental
assessment. The evaluation is done by means of LCA and it shows how these two tools can be
combined by using the same inventories, obtained from the simulation, to assess the economical
and environmental impacts, homogenizing boundaries.

2
Literature review
Algal biomass denotes a key feedstock for the expansion of the bio-based energy and chemi-
cal production. Its usage for bioreﬁnery purposes is attracting great interest due to the attempt
to deal with the limitations detected in biomasses of ﬁrst and second generation [8].
Algae can be classiﬁed as micro and macroalgae according to their physical and chemical prop-
erties.
Physically, they diﬀer widely in size: microalgae are microscopic (5-100 µm) while
macroalgae can grow up to 70 meters [9]. Referring to composition, they also diverge. Microal-
gae have higher lipid content and are rich in oil (up to 80% of dry weight in some species) used
for biodiesel production [10]. This is one of the reasons why there has been more research in 3rd
generation biofuels from microalgae. On the other hand, macroalgae have higher percentage of
carbohydrates, higher biomass production rates (per volume) and biomass densities [11].
Macroalgae can be classiﬁed according to their color as green, red and brown algae. They have
a particular composition that strongly diﬀers from other feedstocks, with a water content up to
90% and carbohydrates (manan, ulvan, carrageenan, agar, laminarin, mannitoal, alginate, and
fucoidin) that neither appear in the composition of starchy and lignocellulosic biomass nor in
microalgae [2]. This high water and sugar content makes them very suitable so they are suitable
for the production of fermentation products by hydrolyzing the carbohydrates into sugars [12].
2.1
Algal-based bioreﬁnery
Macroalgae, also called seaweeds, have been cultivated and harvested for centuries with
domestic purposes (food and feed). However, the global concern for climate change and the
drawbacks found in starchy and lignocellulosic biomasses are shifting this purpose towards in-
dustrial applications. Currently, the share of macroalgae intended for food is still the 83-90%
of the total seaweed industry, accounting for $5 billion. In the other fraction, the production of
algae hydrocolloids has economic importance generating $132-240 million worldwide.
Within macroalgae, brown algae rise as a good alternative in bioreﬁnery since it is the largest
seaweed source with yearly production of 9.72 millions tons (DW) [13], having high carbohydrate
compositions (with a 50-60% carbohydrate in dry bases), lacking of lignin in their composition
[14], [15] and being cultivated using techniques known for the last 50 years [8].
Thus, thermochemical, chemical and biochemical pathways have been regarded in order to ob-
tain bio-based products. Figure 1 depicts diﬀerent processes carried out in a seaweed bioreﬁnery.
The processes in the lower part represent thermochemical conversion through catalytic conver-
sion and fractionation while the upper part processes correspond to chemical and biochemical
conversion. Due to the macroalgae high water content, microbial conversion gives better results
than direct combustion or thermochemical pathways, lowering the capital costs. [8], [13]

Carbohydrates in fresh harvested brown seaweed account for 15-20% of the total weight. This
amount, coupled with the lack of lignin in their structure, makes them a suitable substrate
for microbial conversion without the requirement of harsh pretreatment for releasing the fer-
mentable sugars. Despite the brown algae high carbohydrate content, just a 30-40% of them are
hexose-based polysaccharides while the rest are C-5 sugars. Although so far just hexoses have
been used as substrate for microbial processes, new advances towards C5 sugar fermentation
allow up to 90% of carbohydrates. [8]
However, the diﬀerent carbohydrates that form the brown algae are not extracted following the
same procedures. While laminaran and mannitol can be easily extracted by water, alginate
that is present in a 30-40% is not. Laminarin ((C6H10O5)n) is a β-1,3-linked glucan that can be
hydrolysed to glucose by laminarinase [endo-1,3(4)-β-glucanase]. Manitol ((C6H14O6)n) is an
alcohol formed by mannose sugars that needs to be oxidazed to fructose by mannitol enzyme
before the fermentation. Cellulose ((C6H10O5)n) is a linear chain of D-glucose linked by β(1→4)
that can be released by cellulases. On the other hand, alginate is a polymer of 1,4-glycosidically
linked -L-guluronic acid (G) and β-D-mannuronic acid (M). Its degradation is suggested to be
catalyzed by alginate, mannuronate and gulunonate lyases via a β-elimination reaction. [17]
Diﬀerent conﬁgurations and technologies for pretreatment, hydrolysis and fermentation have
been tried. It is seen that the harsh pretreatments, with high temperature and addition of acid
used in second generation biomasses, do not give good economical results, since they increase
the capital costs and use of energy and they do not increase the hydrolysis substantially.[13]

2.3
Lactic acid production
Lactic acid (LA) is one of the most important and widely used organic acid worldwide due
to its multifuncionality in industrial and biotechnological applications. It is used in the food
industry for synthesis of base chemicals and for the production of the biodegradable plastic
polylactic acid (PLA). The synthesis of lactic acid can follow two diﬀerent pathways, chemi-
cal and microbial (Figure 2). The chemical pathway consists in the hydrolysis of lactonitrile,
derivative from petrochemicals, that produces the racemic mixture of D- and L-lactic acid. On
the other hand, though the microbial pathway, pure lactic acid either L- or D- is synthesized
depending on the microorganism used. Thus, the most industrially used method worldwide is
the microbial.[22]
Figure 2: Schematic representation of lactic acid production
The fermentation process is commonly carried out in a batch reactor since higher yields of LA
than in continuous process are reported. Conversely, with continuous mode higher productivity
was found. Nevertheless, the results were not conclusive and further studies have shown that
feed-batch and semicontinuous mode have higher yields than batch. [23]
Even when using the biotechnological production pathway, the ultimate eﬃciency and economics
of LA production are still in doubt since the process requires a high product speciﬁcity. Thus
the cost of the raw materials play an important role. Although lignocellulosic biomass is a
cheap source, the harsh pretreatments needed have high cost. Therefore, as Table 2 depicts,
macroalgae biomass rises as a good alternative since it seems to have a good potential for lactic
acid production high production yield. However, it will be required research in organisms able
to produce lactic acid from the sugars available in the algae composition. [8]
Hwang et al. (2011) compare the fermentative production of lactic acid from seaweeds and
terrestrial plants using diﬀerent Lactobacillus bacterial strains. The study shows that whereas
the terrestrial plant carbohydrates can be almost fully fermented, in the case of brown seaweeds,
currently it is just the 61.6% of carbohydrates. However, lactic acid yields obtained for terrestrial
and aquatic plants are similar (0.18-0.19 g/g biomass and 0.16-0.17 g/g biomass respectively),
which suggest that if the research is oriented towards ﬁnding new technologies able to process
these non-fermentable sugars, the yields of lactic acid for seaweeds biomass will increase whilst
for lignicellulosic biomass the sugars are already utilized in the maximal extent. The study also
reﬂects the importance of choosing a proper strain of Lactobacillus since the sugars that form
the biomass are not equally fermented by all the strains. [24]

Besides, as it has been shown, a vast majority of the studies are focused in the production of
biofuels, mostly bioethanol from macroalgae and biodiesel from microalgae. In order to ﬁnd
studies that assess the sustainability of chemical production through biochemical pathways, the

Thus, it was seen that the viability of bio-based products from second generation biomasses has
been broadly studied. However, these studies are also mostly focused in biofuels. Two speciﬁc
studies were found where both, TEA and LCA, are directly addressed for the production of
lactic acid from lignicellulosic biomass [43], [44].
In Daful et al. (2017), six cases for lactic acid production were designed and compared address-
ing their economical and environmental performance, having as functional unit ”1 tonne of LA
produced”. The environmental assessment was made applying a LCA cradle-to-gate consider-
ing the following environmental impacts: global warming potential, abiotic depletion, terrestrial
acidiﬁcation, ozone depletion, fresh water ecotoxicity, terrestrial ecotoxicity, photochemical ox-
idant formation, human toxicity and freshwater eutrophication. The economic evaluation gives
the installed equipment cost ($), the total direct cost (TDC), the total indirect cost (TIC), the
ﬁxed capital investment (TIC), total capital investment (TCI), operating cost (OPEX) and the
revenue. Odd scenarios use hemicellulose while even scenarios use cellu-lignin. Besides, the two
ﬁrst scenarios use Ca(OH)2 for neutralizing the fermentation that has as byproducts gypsum
and solid waste, the 3rd and 4th scenarios do not use base for neutralizing the fermentation
but an acid-tolerant thermophilic bacterium (Bacillus coagulans) and the two last scenarios use
Mg(OH)2 for neutralizing the fermentation and then is recycled. Thus, the study shows how the
processes which use cellulose have greater production of LA using the same technology (even
scenarios) and, thus, they are economically more proﬁtable. From the environmental perspec-
tive, the two ﬁrst scenarios show the worst performance. Eventually, the study exposes that the
two last scenarios are the most advantageous from the economic and environmental perspective,
being the sixth the best one [43].
In Mandegari et al. (2017), an assessment of the diﬀerent product alternatives (ethanol, LA
and electricity) through biochemical pathway is performed. In this way, four diﬀerent scenarios
are designed ((i) ethanol and electricity production, (ii) lactic acid and electricity production,
(iii) ethanol from cellulose, lactic acid from hemicellulose and electricity production and (iv)
ethanol from hemicellulose, lactic acid from cellulose and electricity production) and compared
having as functional unit ”1 tonne LA produced”. The economic evaluation gives the total
capital investment (TCI) (M$), the variable operating cost (M$/year), the ﬁxed operating cost
(M$/year), the ethanol, LA and electricity cost (M$/year) and the internal rate of return (IRR)
(%). The environmental assessment is performed following a LCA cradle-to-gate considering
the following impact categories: abiotic depletion, global warming potential, acidiﬁcation, eu-
trophication, ozone depletion, photochemical oxidation, terrestrial and aquatic eco-toxicity, and
human toxicity. Thus, the study concludes that whereas the lactic acid production has the
best economic performance, the bioethanol production has the minimum IRR and the lower
environmental impacts across all impact categories [44].
These studies manifest the potentials of biochemical pathways for producing bio-based products
beyond the production of biofuels.

2.5
Knowledge gap
This literature review aims giving a general outlook of the potential of macroalgae as feed-
stock for producing bio-based products through fermentative processes. Speciﬁcally the study is
focused on evaluating the feasibility of producing lactic acid from brown algae applying techno-
economic analysis. Therefore, the research goes towards ﬁnding previous manuscripts that have
performed a TEA on 3rd generation of biomass to produce commodity chemicals.
Besides,
since including the environmental impact perspective helps to ﬁnd possible trade-oﬀs between
economic feasibility and environmental performance, when designing new processes and tech-
nologies, publications where both tools are addresses together are investigated.
The research shows brown algae and, concretely, Laminaria species as a promising feedstock
due to its carbohydrate content, availability and high production yields. However, since it is a
recent concept, the processes are not well deﬁned, there are not eﬃcient harvesting and process-
ing methods nor microbes able to produce the desired end-products, what leads to uncertainty
and expensive processes.
To the best of our knowledge, no previous TEA performed on 3rd generation of biomass to
produce non-biofuel bio-based product has been published yet. And therefore, studies which
assess macroalgae viability for producing the latter appealing TEA and LCA, have not been
found either. TEA and LCA have been independently applied in biofuels production processes,
but they have not been implemented yet in analysis of process for chemicals production. In
order to see how these two techniques are applied together to check the feasibility of non-biofuel
bio-based products manufacture, the scope of the research had to be widened to lignocellulosic
feedstocks.
Thus, the objective of this master thesis is to design the production process of pure lactic acid
from brown algae and to perform a techno-economic analysis for evaluating it. Besides, an
environmental evaluation of the ﬁnal design is performed in a complementary study by means
of a LCA using the same inventories than for the TEA analysis. The results from the TEA and
LCA will be comparable since they have been build form the same inventories. This leads to a
novel procedure, since the production of a non-fuel bio-based product from macroalgae and the
subsequent techno-economical evaluation had not been addressed before.

Process design
A process model is developed for the production of lactic acid from brown algae by means
of the software Aspen Plus v8.8. Rigorous material and energy balances are accomplished in
order to obtain an accurate model that can be used for performing a precise techno-economic
assessment of the process. The procedure is designed on basis of the national renewable energy
laboratory (NREL) designs for ethanol production from lignocellulosic and microalgae biomass
[45], [46]. However, steps such as pretreatment or downstream have important diﬀerences due
to the usage of a diﬀerent feedstock and the production of a diﬀerent ﬁnal product, respectively.
3.1
Design basis
The ﬁrst step for designing the process model consists of the selection of chemical components
and a thermodynamic model. While some of the chemicals where available in the Aspen Plus
libraries, few components present in the algae structure were not available in the databanks.
Hence, for the missing components the physical properties were implemented manually based
on literature data.
The list of components used in the simulation is provided in Table 30,
where the speciﬁcations introduced for deﬁning the components not present in Aspen Plus are
included. Selection of a proper thermodynamic model for the phase equilibra and the physical
properties of the simulation is of great importance in order to ascertain whether the model
simulated represents the reality. Thus, the model selected was NRTL due to its capability in
handling combinations of polar and non-polar compounds up to very strong non-ideality.
3.1.1
Feedstock
The composition of the feedstock used has a great inﬂuence on the design of the various
steps in the model such as pretreatment and fermentation. Thus, the selection of the brown
macroalgae Laminaria digitata is made owing to its high content of carbohydrates in the solid
fraction, what boosts the lactic acid production through fermentation. However, as it has been
previously mentioned, Laminaria digitata is just available with good carbohydrate composition
in the period April-August. Therefore, for ensuring the continuous process of lactic acid pro-
duction throughout the year, the usage of other brown algae is considered if L. digitata is not
available in enough quantity.
Thus, the composition of the raw material implemented in the model does not come just from
L. digitata but from average values of macroalgaes from Laminaria specie. The composition
outlined in Table 3 is adjusted for simulation purposes as Table 8 shows.
As Table 8 shows, the fucoidan is not implemented in the model. The reason for not including
it is the lack of references with its physical properties. Therefore, since its amount is very small
in comparison with the other carbohydrates, it is decided to make the same assumption as
in Fasahati et al. (2012) [13]. Its composition is equally distributed between laminarian and
ash since it is considered that it half reacts (as the glucose of the laminaria) and half remains
unreacted (as the ash). Likewise, the ash has many components but for the simulation, only
the ones in a percentage higher than 3% were included.

3.1.2
Plant size
Traditionally, lactic acid production was oriented towards food and pharmaceutical industry.
However, lately, its demand is growing due to the interest in producing bioplastics using the
biocompatible and biodegradable poly-lactic acid (PLA).
In 2012, the production of PLA was estimated to grow from 180000 tonne/year to 800000
tonne/year by 2020. While Naturworks, largest producer, manufactures 140000 tonne/year,
smaller producers cover the rest of production [47]. Thus, the design of the current plant has
the objective of covering one share of this capacity to reach the targeted PLA production.
Thus, taking a seventh of this capacity, the design of the production site is done in regards to a
production of 11 tonne of lactic acid per hour, considering 8000 operating hours per year (92.6
% optimal).
3.1.3
Microorganism selection
The process described in the study produces lactic acid from brown algae through a fer-
mentative pathway. Thus, the microorganism chosen for fermenting the biomass is of great
signiﬁcance since the lactic acid needed for the production of poly(lactic acid) requires high
purity of L(+)-lactic acid and not a racimic mixture of steroisomers L(+)/D(-). Besides, as the
fermentation carbon source is a mixture of diﬀerent carbohydrates such as alginate, laminaria,
mannitol and cellulase, the microorganism must be chosen carefully so that is able to ferment
as much feedstock as possible in order to maximize the productivity of the process.
There is a large number of microorganisms able to produce lactic acid, such as diverse species
of bacteria, fungi or yeast. These microorganisms metabolize monosaccharides, like glucose and
fructose, into cells and LA. As mentioned before, laminaria and cellulose are polymers of glucose
that after being hydrolyzed give as a result the fermentable monomers. Mannitol, though, be-
fore being fermented must be oxidazed to fructose by mannitol deshydrogenase, enzyme which
appears in microorganisms such as Lactobacillus (Figure 3).

3.2.1
Pretreatment
The handling of the biomass before entering the fermenter can be diﬀerentiated into physical
and chemical pretreatment. The aim of the physical pretreatment is conditioning the biomass
in order to improve the reaction eﬃciency in the next steps, whereas the chemical pretreatment
hydrolyzes the carbohydrates into sugars ready for being fermented.
Physical pretreatment
As previously set, the case considered as baseline consists in the scenario where drying the
biomass is performed. Thus, the base case physical pretreatment consists of washing, drying
and crushing of the biomass as Figure 6 shows. The washing step involves drenching the biomass
with freshwater in order to remove the high content of salt, sand and other traces which can
eﬀect the eﬃciency of the microorganisms during the fermentation [50]. This is due to the nature
of Lactobacillus bacteria species, which is a non halotolerant microorganism whose growth is
limited with salt concentrations higher than 5% [51]. Thus, the biomass entering the system
is assumed to be washed with water in a ratio 1:1, decreasing the concentration of salt below
the limit. Then, a drying step is performed in order to reduce the high moisture content of
macroalgae. Thus, dried biomass can be stored and transported in a cheaper way rather than
having a high water content (moisture content reduced to around 20% [52], [13]). Moreover, as
Table 4 shows, the feedstock is not available with the desirable composition throughout the year
and is harvested just during the few months. Thus, enabling drying, provides the possibility
of storing the biomass in the desired composition conditions and utilizing it during the year.
For drying, a rotary drier which brings the algae with high water content in contact with hot
air, as the one used by Davis et al. [46], is chosen. This operation is conducted at a maximum
temperature of 85o C in order not to damage the biomass [53]. Finally, the biomass was crushed
reducing the particle size to <0.005 m [54], increasing the surface area and the eﬃciency during
the fermentation. A screen is used in order to separate the particles with size larger than 0.005
m and is recirculated to the crusher again.
Figure 6: 1st physical pretreatment: Washing, drying and crushing
The second scenario is similar but without the drying step, as Figure 7 illustrates. The high
water content in the feedstock makes it very suitable for fermentation processes whereby the
drying step seems not to be required, being able to skip this stage in the physical pretreatment.
Besides, in this case, the usage of a screen after the crushing is not considered due to the high
wetness of the biomass.

Thermo-chemical and enzymatic pretreatment
The chemical pretreatment step particularly diﬀers from lignocellulic to algae biomass due to the
particular structure and composition of the seaweeds. In particular, the lack of lignin, a polymer
responsible of the diﬃcult conversion into products of the second generation biomass, in brown
algae structure leads to consider that such harsh pretreatment as 2nd generation biomasses
would not be needed.
As shown in the literature, experimental research has tried diﬀerent
procedures with the aim of obtaining sugars from the algae biomass able to be fermented by
microorganisms to obtain lactic acid. Thus, following the study of Fasahati et al. (2015) for the
production of bioethanol from brown algae [13], two scenarios are considered in this assessment;
(i) diluted acid pretreatment (ACTH), which it is also applied in second generation biomass,
and (ii) hot water wash (HWW).
Diluted acid pretreatment consists of adding sulfuric acid in an autoclave at 121 oC with a 20%
solids loading solution in order to reach a pH 1 (22 g H2SO4/kg dry biomass)[45]. The second
chemical pretreatment scenario described as a ”soft pretreatment” based on the addition of hot
water at 50oC (HWW) until reaching a solid loading of 20% [13]. However, for the scenarios
without the drying step, addition of external water is not considered, since the the solids are
already diluted.
Thus, the reactions occurred in this step are the mild hydrolysis of laminarin and cellulose
into glucose [17] but these thermo-chemical pretreatments lack the ability to fully hydrolyze
the algal polymers laminarin and cellulose. Therefore, an enzymatic treatment is performed in
a separate reactor by adding industrial cocktails of diﬀerent enzymes. Although, it must be
pointed out that for simulation purposes, the common industrial enzyme cellulase is considered
for performing the sacchariﬁcation step (20 g cellulase/(kg cellulose+laminarin)). On the other
hand, mannitol extraction does not require previuos pretreatment for its extraction. Due to its
high solubility in water, it is considered to get dissolved, going from the cells wall to the water
phase.
However, before including the enzymes in the sacchariﬁcation step, a conditioning step is re-
quired in order to adjust the ﬂow to speciﬁc conditions for the enzymes to work as intended pH
5-6 and temperature 48o C. This step diﬀers between both pretreatments described. In dilute
acid pretreatment, the temperature needs to be lowered from the 121oC in the hydrolysis reactor
and, therefore, a heat exchanger is needed. Then, in order to neutralize the H2SO4 added in
prior reactor and rise the pH to 6, ammonia is added in a ratio of 4.8 g/(l of hydrolysate) as
depicted in Figure 8 [45].

3.2.2
Fermentation
Fermentation is a critical step of the process since it is the operation when glucose, hy-
drolyzed in the previous step from laminarin and cellulose, and mannitol, oxidized to fructose
as shown in Figure 3, are fermented to lactic acid by the bacteria Lactobacillus casei. The study
of Kaavessina et al. presents the dependence of the bacteria performance on the concentration
of sugars (glucose and fructose) in the broth that leads to diﬀerent lactic acid yields [49]. In
each scenario, the concentration of sugars after the pretreatments is, although diﬀerent, lower
than 159 g/l (concentration shown in the study as the optimum). Therefore, before the fermen-
tation the hydrolyzed slurry is taken to an evaporator which concentrates the sugars to ≈150
g/l (Figure 10).
The fermentation requires the addition of inoculum in order to have enough cells for the fer-
mentation to happen. According to Kaavessina et al., an inoculum of 10% of the fermentation
volume (v/v solution) is added to the reactor [49]. To begin the process, an initial amount of
inoculum needs to be added to the fermenter. Subsequently, it will grow and not more seed
strains from a lab will be required. Thus, for simulation purposes, in order to provide the
required inoculum to the fermenter, 10% of the stream coming from the evaporator is sent to
a fermenter dedicated to seed production, as it is described in Humbrid et al [45], for then
taking it to the main fermenter (10). The conditions in both reactors, seed production and
main fermenter, are the same, the temperature is kept 37 o C with cooling water and pH 6
is maintained by the addition of calcium carbonate (150 g/l broth [56]). Calcium carbonate
acts as a buﬀer controlling the pH drop due to the acid production by reacting with the lactic
acid, producing calcium lactate. This addition is required since a lower pH would inhibit the
production of lactic acid in the fermenter.
The cells growth in fermentation requires the addition of nitrogen and phosphorous nutrients.
However, in this case, the proteins present in high amount in the feedstock are considered the
nitrogen source whereas diammonium phosphate (DAP) is added as P source, as before shown
in Davis et al. [46].

Concentration
Before going to the downstream, the solids present in the solution must be removed. These
solids consist of the non-reacted fraction of the algae such as alginate, lipids, proteins and cells.
Thus, these solids are separated according to their particle size. Firstly, the cells, whose particle
size are bigger are removed with a ﬁlter (micrometers scale). The recirculation of these cells
is not considered as it is stated in the lactic acid fermentation process presented in IHS 2012
Process Economics Program Yearbook [57].
Then, the solids with smaller particle size (alginate, lipids, proteins, non-reacted laminaria,
mannitol and cellulose) present in the solution are removed with a decanter (nanometer scale).
However, at this point, it is important to notice that the calcium lactate formed in the fermenter
is in solution, since it is in a concentration lower than 8.5 g/100 ml [58]) and so it does not
decant, avoiding the loss of lactate.
Then, in order to recover the lactate in form of calcium lactate, this aqueous solution is acidiﬁed
by the addition of sulfuric acid, which reacts with the calcium lactate producing lactic acid and
calcium sulphate salt as Table 11 shows. This salt, also known as gypsum, is subsequently
separated by a decanter, leaving the solution free of solids before the separation step.

3.2.4
Separation and puriﬁcation
The ﬁnal stage of the process is meant to separate the lactic acid from the fermentation broth
and purifying it to its polymer grade variant (99 wt% dry basis [59]). The components that
reach this stage are mainly lactic acid and water, although there are also some sugars which did
not react in the fermentation stage (glucose and fructose). However, the lactic acid puriﬁcation
presents some diﬃculties since it is a nonvolatile compound (its boiling point at atmospheric
pressure is 210oC) that is usually mixed with nonvolatile impurities and other organic acids and
bears self-polymerization at high temperatures. Thus, several methods such us crystallization,
solvent extraction, evaporation, adsorption, electrodialysis, membrane separation or reactive
distillation have been applied in order to facilitate this separation. Although all of them present
pros and cons, the two-step process consisting of esteriﬁcation of lactic acid with alcohol followed
by the separation of the lactate ester by distillation and its hydrolyzation to lactic acid rises as a
good option in order to obtain high purity lactic acid [59], [60]. Thus, although performing this
method requires high capital cost, it can be improved by executing these steps simultaneously
using a distillation column.
Thus, the present work implements this conﬁguration. The reactive distillation is simulated with
a RADFRAC unit in Aspen Plus which is fed from the bottom part with the liquid phase from
the evaporator (60 wt. % lactic acid) and methanol. The column is designed in the way that
the estereﬁcation reaction takes place in the lower stages of the column (Table 12, reaction (1))
whereas the upper ones are for the separation of the products of the reaction, water and methyl
lactate (distillation section). This presents a continuous system where the products leave the
reaction section towards the separation stages as soon as they are produced. According to Le-
Chatelier’s principle, the equilibrium reaction shifts towards enhancing the product formation.
The hydrolysis reactor ensues the reactive distillation column. There, methyl lactate reacts
with water giving lactic acid and methanol that are ﬁnally sent to a ﬁnal distillation column
which separates the methanol giving as a result pure lactic acid.

3.3
Process economics
The aim of performing a thorough process design, accomplishing a simulation model with
accurate mass and energy balances is to eventually complete a techno-economic assessment of
lactic acid production from brown algae. The cost obtained will position the production process
of lactic acid with this pathway in the market: This will allow to assess whether changes in the
process could improve the economics or if more research is needed.
Firstly, the detailed simulation allows obtaining the total capital investment by means of the
costing software Aspen Process Economic Analyzer V8.8. Then, the total cost of production is
calculated with the ﬁxed and variable operating costs for ﬁnally doing the cash-ﬂow analysis.
This cash-ﬂow analysis provides an overview of the resources required and the earnings over
the project lifetime. The procedure and assumptions done for calculating the economics are the
ones shown in Towler et al. (2013).[62]
3.3.1
Capital cost
The capital cost account for the ﬁxed one-time expenses related with the start of the activity
of plant, construction of the plant itself, equipment purchase and installation and capital for
holding the activity of the plant.
The cost related with procuring and installing the equipment, also called inside the battery
investment (ISBL), is taken from Aspen Process Economic Analyzer V8.8. This software is
a tool provided in the Aspen package which provides good estimations for equipment prices
when the ones from the providers are not available, as it happens in this case. Aspen Process
Economic Analyzer V8.8 can directly take the design information from the process simulation
and, making assumptions, calculate and provide the equipment sizes and costs. However, it
also gives the option of implementing manually the design details making the calculations more
accurate. Besides, in order to obtain the cost of some units which handle solids whose properties
are not available in Aspen Plus, the simulator SuperPro Designer is used instead, since it is
consider to be a good software for biochemical processes.
Thus, the ﬁrst step of the process is sizing the equipment. Although Aspen Process Economic
Analyzer V8.8. provides this information, in order to obtain the costs as precise as possible,
some equipment is sized according to the method shown in Biegler et al [63]. This is the case
of reactors and ﬂash columns whose sizes are obtained taking into account the ﬂow and the
residence time. In Aspen Process Economic Analyzer V8.8, the residence time is set as default
to 5 minutes for its calculations. However, the reactors used for pretreatment and fermentation
in the current study have much longer retention times and the ﬂash columns shorter. Hence,
reactors and ﬂash columns are sized as a vessel according to Equation 1 [62].
Volume = 2 (FL τ)/ρL

3.3.2
Operating costs
The operating costs can be clearly divided in variable and ﬁxed operating costs. The variable
operating cost are proportional to the production rate of the plant and they refer to the costs
of raw materials, utilities, consumables and eﬄuent disposal. On the other hand, the ﬁxed
operating costs are independent to the output of the plant since they refer to the labor and
maintenance costs, the overhead charges and the taxes.
The variable operating costs (VCOP) are usually a big share of the total cost of production and
thus, optimizing their usage in the process is a main target in the process design. Decreasing the
amount of raw materials and consumables without aﬀecting the production is, in many cases,
something not susceptible to change. However, the usage of utilities and the eﬄuent disposal
can be managed in the way the cost are reduced. Thus, in order to reduce the amount of
utilities, heat integration is a practice done in most of the chemical plants. The implementation
of the heat integration in this design is discussed in the optimization section. In the case of
the eﬄuent disposal, the possibility of recovering this streams as fuel must be considered. This
can be done by just incinerating this waste and using the heat produced or by sending these
streams to anaerobic digestion. These two cases are considered in the design of the LA plant
and are also addressed in the optimization section.
The ﬁxed capital cost of production (FCOP) refers to the labor and maintenance costs, the
overhead expenses and insurances and thus, do not vary according to the amount of product
generated by the plant. The labor cost depends on the number of shift positions, on the number
of operator per shift and on the location of the plant for calculating the operator salary. This
process describes a ﬂuid and solids continuous process and so, it will require 3 shift positions +
1 for every solid handling and the general assumption of 4.8 operator per shift position is taken.

removal of the non reacted solids. Since the amount of non reacted material is substantially
high, instead of just dispose it, it is thought to give it a second value by incinerating it. Hence,
the incineration of this biomass will produce energy that can be considered as a byproduct of
the process.
In Towler et al. (2013), the value of this waste stream is estimated as shows the equation below
[62].
PWFV = PF x Ho
C

Where PWFV = waste value as fuel ($/kg), PF = price of fuel ($/MMbtu or $/GJ) and HoC is

Thus, the waste stream that comes out from the decanter has non reacted carbohydrates, lipids,
proteins and ash. Knowing the ﬂow of the waste stream (taken from each of the simulation in
kg/h), the price of the fuel (7.81 $/MMbtu ??) and the low heating value (LHV) of Laminaria

factor 9.48E+04 MMbtu/MJ. LHV is used since there is not energy to release due to the water
evaporation. This process is a assummed to happen in a burner with eﬃciency 100%.
The waste stream ﬂow is higher in the scenarios with hot water wash pretreatment since the
hydrolysis and sacchariﬁcation is less eﬀective and, thus, they have higher amount of carbohy-
drates and other solids non reacted that will produce more energy when incinerated.
However, the incineration unit is not modelled individually but it is assumed to be included in
the OBLS cost of the project.
3.4.3
Anaerobic digestion
Although the incineration already gives an extra value to the waste, the anaerobic digestion
is supposed to maximize this outcome. As in the incineration case above mentioned, the valuable
solids non reacted in fermentation are separated in a decanter to recover them as carbon source
to produce biogas for heat and power beneﬁts.
The waste stream from the decanter already comes at 37oC, the temperature of the fermentation,
which is considered a proper temperature for the anaerobic digestion and, therefore, a heat
exchanger is not needed. Thus, the stream goes directly to the digester were the material is
assimilated by the AD microorganisms producing biogas (methane and CO2 in a 50%/50%
molar ratio) and some solids with high nitrogen content.
Once the biogas is produced, it is conduced to a compressor where its pressure is risen for then
going to the turbine generator. There, the biogas is combusted generating electricity and heat,
combined heat and power system (CHP). The heat is not produced directly but it requires
a heat recovery steam generator. Thus, water is introduced and heated until it reaches the
conditions of low pressure steam (124 oC) that then is recirculated in the system. Besides, the
ﬂue gas that exits the heat exchanger is at very high temperature and, in this case, it is also
considered as utility, being integrated in the system. However, since this stream is mostly N2

When regarding the availability of the biomass, the annual production of brown algae is esti-
mated to be 9.42 MT (dry basis) [13]. The main farmer countries are in Asia; China produces
more than 4 MT/year of Laminaria seaweed where around to the 90% is cultivated in aquacul-

with the set capacity, it is noticed that it would require a big percentage of the annual brown
algae production. Thus, in order to ensure this process, obtaining higher amount of biomass
from European countries which have barely implemented macroalgae cultivation techniques [64]
would be required.
Besides, the large amount of algae needed is aligned with the fact that just the 12% of the
biomass is solid fraction, and the 28.5% of it is ash (Table 8). Thus, although the carbohydrate
content in brown algae is higher than in lignicellulosic biomass, the yield of lactic acid from
brown algae turns to be very similar to the yield of 269 kg LA/(dry ton) obtained by Liu et al.
(2015) when using corn stover as feedstock [65]. This is direct consequence of not being able to
metabolize alginate to lactic acid with the microbial strains currently available.
Apart from the global output gleaned from the amount of biomass required and the yields ob-
tained, table 22 gives an overview of the four base cases simulated. The two cases with dilute
acid pretreatment present higher yields of lactic acid production than the two with hot water
wash pretreatment. The yield diﬀerence is owing to higher polymer hydrolysis achieved when
using the acid pretreatment compared to HWW, as it is illustrated in Table 9. This leads to have
a higher amount of sugars available to be metabolized to lactic acid in the fermentation. On the
other hand, scenarios with electrodialysis applied to the downstream conﬁguration, resulted in
a lower capital cost. This agrees with the fact that the reactive distillation needs two distillation
columns and one reactor while the REED/EBDS scenario needs fewer equipment; the system
with two membranes connected to an electric ﬁeld and a distillation column. Thus, according
to these results, the scenario with dilute acid pretreatment and electrodialysis downstream are
determined to be the best option. However, when checking the production costs, it is noticed
that the variable cost are considerable high for this scenario, while for the for the scenario with
how water wash pretreatment is lower. This agree with the fact that the scenario with dilute
acid pretretament used more consumables (more sulfuric acid and ammonia) and utilities (it
reaches higher temperatures) tha the scenario with hot water wash.

4.3.1
Physical pretreatment: dryer or not dryer
Conﬁguring the process with or without dryer has more inﬂuence than saving a dryer unit
in the physical pretreatment. The drying process is a step that leads to an increase of the
capital cost and the variable cost of production, since it is a step that requires high amount of
energy in order to evaporate water present in the algae. Thus, since the process carried out is
a fermentation, which requires water it seems logical that the drying step is not required.
However, not drying the biomass has some eﬀects. Firstly, if the algae is not dried before entering
to the thermo-chemical pretreatment step, in order to produce the same amount of lactic acid,
the equipment required is bigger. When drying the biomass, the solid content in the hydrolysis
step is 20% while in this case is 12%. Thus, the reactor for hydrolysis and sacchariﬁcation would
have larger volumes what leads to an increase in the capital cost. Nevertheless, the high water
content remains just until the fermentation step. At this point, the fermentation requirements
set a sugar concentration of ≈150 g/l [49] homogenizing conditions until the end of the process.
Thus, both scenarios with hot water wash pretreatment are simulated and the techno-economic
evaluation is performed in order to obtain the MSP. As it is illustrated in ﬁgure 25, the heat
integration for hot water wash scenarios does not lead to economic improvement of the process
and therefore, in this case the minimum selling price is just calculated in the base case and in
the base case with anaerobic digestion and CHP system. The results obtained are summarized
in ﬁgure 30 where in order to see which scenario performs better, the minimum selling prices
obtained without the dryer are compared with the lowest minimum selling price obtained with
the dryer. The lowest minimum selling price in both cases with the dryer, reactive distillation
and REED/EMBS, is obtained in the design with the anaerobic digestion implemented.

4.4
Environmental Assessment
When creating a process for a new bio-based product, the economic feasibility of the design
is, currently, the main driver of the decision making process. However, disregarding the en-
vironmental impacts can lead to overlook possible trade-oﬀs between economic feasibility and
environmental performance. Likewise, when performing the environmental assessment of a pro-
cess, in order to do a proper assessment, accurate inventories of the process are required. Thus,
when the process assessed is still in its ﬁrst steps and not commercialized, software simulators,
as Aspen Plus, can provide the latter inventories.
Performing both assessments aims to shape future production of LA from brown algae. Thus,
once the processes are designed and the simulations are performed in Aspen Plus, the inventories
created, with information on the commodities and utilities used in the process, are extracted
and used for the evaluation of the environmental impact of the latter process. Thus, instead
of using libraries as ecoinvent as sourse of inventories, the process of LA from brown algae
simulated in Aspen will populate the required inventory
The evaluation of this processes are addressed in the complementary study ¨Ogmundarson et al.
(2018) by means of Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) [66]. The software SimaPro is used applying
the methodology ReCiPe Midpoint hierarchist (H).
The functional unit used in this consequential LCA is ”production of 1 kilogram of lactic acid
per hour” and the goal is to assess the environmental impact of the scenarios which perform
best according to the TEA. Two boundaries are described: (i) cradle-to-gate and (ii) gate to
gate.
As this study is the ﬁrst attempt of combining TEA and LCA to a product coming from 3rd
generation biomass, only impacts on global warming are considered. This could be a limitation
of this study as other impact categories might be relevant when considering bio-based products
(e.g. water depletion, ecosystem quality ,etc.) [67]. The impact on global warming are expressed
on kg CO2-eq of lactic acid. As a ﬁrst approach the study is made not country speciﬁc, so that
global average values were used.
In order to compare the result of TEA and LCA, a contribution analysis is performed. The
same contributors are selected in the LCA and TEA analysis and they are normalized to one
so they can be contrasted; utilities (low pressure steam, electricity and hot air), commodity
chemicals and the chemical plant itself.
Figure 34 shows the results when cradle-to-gate boundaries are applied. It illustrates how TEA
and LCA have diﬀerent highest contributors. In the LCA the highest contributor is the hot air
utility, whilst in the TEA is the feedstock purchased. This analysis is interesting because while
in the TEA, the hot air utility was not standing out, with the LCA study is seen that is a factor
to evaluate further. However, it is also considered that the deviation of the results may come
from the process used for simulating impacts of the feedstock handling before the plant gate.
This process takes into account just the impact of the oil required for harvesting, disregarding
other impacts such as the transportation from the place the algae is collected to the production
plant.

However, it must be pointed that this LCA study is still in its ﬁrst steps and this accounts just

is only used in the dryer for the macroalgae. This high impact could not be foresee before
applying the LCA and it shows that this unit operation should be further investigated and the
improvement the process may be required.
Besides the option of just removing the drying step, that would require to increase the production
of Laminaria sp. in order to meet the current requirements of the plant, other options are suggest
for improving the process. The set temperature for the dryer is 85oC, which is within the range
set for heat sensitive product (49-132oC)[68]. The temperature of the air is risen when entering
in the dryer and it leaves with high moisture content due to the algal water evaporation. As
design is modelled, the energy used for heating this air is lost. Therefore a way not to lose all
this energy would be to decrease this temperature of the air with high moisture content to the
water dew point, so the water condensates and the air does not need to be heated from the
ambient temperature. This would lead not only to a environmental improvement, reducing the
CO2 equivalent emissions, but also economic.

Conclusion, limitations and future work
5.1
Conclusion
In the current study, the design and subsequent economic evaluation of the production pro-
cess of polymer-grade lactic acid is investigated. Diﬀerent design conﬁgurations are simulated
in order to be able to assess which scenario has better economic feasibility. Finally, two thermo-
chemical pretreatments and two downstream schemes are considered.
The two pretreatments performed are aligned with the transition from lignocellulosic to algal
biomass. Firstly, an acid pretreatment is performed following the procedure used for lignocel-
lulosic biomasses. However, since brown algae lacks lignin, an approach in which the addition
of acid and high temperatures are not considered is performed.
Regarding the downstream, a conﬁguration suggested in several studies for the separating lac-
tic acid is reactive distillation. However, the relatively high amount of non-reacted sugars in
this process that does not evaporate with methanol or water, hinders reaching a high purity
lactic acid, and leads to use a separator unit to achieve the polymer-grade purity. The other
simulation option consists of using a Reverse Retro Enhanced Dialysis and Electrodyalisis with
Bipolar Membrane system which separates the sugars from the lactic acid by applying an elec-
trical current to the membrane. This method emerges as a good option regarding eﬃciency as
well as economics.
Coupling diﬀerent designs, four base case scenarios for the production of LA from brown al-
gae are simulated by Aspen Plus and subsequently, the techno-economic evaluation is made,
separately, for each of those, ﬁnding the MSP and the MFP. Then, for each scenario two heat
recovery conﬁgurations are implemented; (i) heat integration and (ii) anaerobic digestion of
the solids non-reacted in the fermentation. Techno-economic evaluation is performed on the
optimized conﬁgurations and the new MSP and MFP are compared with the base case values.
The heat optimization shows that while in the scenarios with acid pretreatment, heat integra-
tion is economically worthy, in the scenarios with hot water wash pretreatment, it leads to
an increment in the capital cost not justiﬁed by the savings in utilities. Hence, the anaerobic
digestion in the acid pretreatment is applied to the conﬁguration with heat integration and in
the scenario with hot water wash, to the base case.
Thus, taking into account the set initial considerations, the scenario which performs the best is
the one which applies hot water wash pretreatment, REED/EDBM downstream and anaerobic
digestion to the non-reacted solids from the fermentor. Thus, although the acid pretreatment
implies better yields, the techno-economic analysis shows that extra cost in utilities, chemicals
(variable cost) and capital cost (extra equipment required for conditioning after the acidiﬁca-
tion) is not justiﬁed. Thus, the MSP is 3.113 $/kg and the MFP is 50 $/kg for the optimal
process design.
Although there are no previous studies addressing the production of lactic acid from brown
algae, a similar study that produces LA from corn stover, and one study that uses brown algae
for producing ethanol was found. Thereby, when comparing yield, MSP and MFP, it is observed
that the values obtained in this study are relatively worse than in the previous studies. This is

When considering factors such as location and feedstock composition, the model displays its

and anaerobic digestion to the non-reacted solids from the fermentor, in the USA and China

rope. Therefore, the fact of not being feasible in the USA and China shows that the electricity
and steam in these countries are not expensive enough for the AD/CHP system installation
being justiﬁed. Hence, the model is very sensitive to the utilities price. Besides, in order to
compare the performance of the plant in each country, since the SP of LA is region dependent,
the comparison is performed between percentage diﬀerence of the MSP of the best conﬁguration
and the SP are compared. In this regard, Germany emerges as the best location to construct a
LA plant since the diﬀerence is the lowest, 10.3 %.
Concerning the design parameters, a process is designed disregarding drying the biomass, sim-
ulated and assessed resulting in a slightly lower MSP than the respective base case. Therefore,
although it increases the capital cost, since the equipment required is bigger, the savings in util-
ities (hot air) and the purchase of dryer unit compensates. This implementation is uncertain
since the composition of macroalgae strongly depends on the month of harvesting. Therefore,
in order to ensure the supply of biomass with the highest carbohydrate composition possible,
its storage is required, and when water content is high, the biomass is more susceptible to be
degraded.
Subsequently, the eﬀect of the feedstock composition is assessed. It is shown in two diﬀerent
ways, using as feedstock a brown algae (Laminaria Digitata) with higher laminaria and man-
nitol content or by including the fermentation of alginate. In both cases the feasibility of the
plant is improved. In the case of using Laminaria Digitata, by just increasing the percentage
of laminaria from 16.5 to 18 %, and the mannitol from 12 to 15 %, the MSP decreases from
3.113 $/kg to 2.845 $/kg, which is very close to the current SP (2.7933 $/kg). However, annual
production of this algae is not enough for constructing a LA plant with the current set capacity.
The capacity was set in order to construct a plant comparable with real LA plant supplied by
other feedstocks. When considering alginate, the results completely change and just keeping
the the SP as it is, 2.7933 $/kg, the investment is recovered in 10.5 years.
Finally, the attempt of comparing the results of TEA and LCA based on common inventories,
shows the possibility of developing new biotechnological processes by simulating the processes
with software tools like Aspen Plus. Thus, by means of material and energy balance, inventories
of processes at ﬁrst stages can populated as it has been done in this case.

5.2
Main limitations
The ﬁrst major limitation of the study is modelling a process which lacks of speciﬁc ex-
perimental data to be based on. Explicit data of fermentation experiments of brown algae to
lactic acid have not been found on the public domain. Therefore, to model the process, the
data is obtained from two diﬀerent sources. For the hydrolysis and sacchariﬁcation of the algal
carbohydrates [13]. On the other hand, for the fermentation of lactic acid, the data is obtained
from an experiment where the lactobacillus casei metabolizes glucose and fructose to lactic acid.
In order to perform the sacchariﬁcation of the biomass, experimentally, very speciﬁc enzymes,
such as laminarinase [endo-1,3(4)-β-glucanase] for the laminarin, are required. However, in the
current study the enzyme price used is based on cellulase. This diﬀerence may have a signiﬁcant
eﬀect in the overall economy of the plant.
The data for hot water wash pretreatment is obtained from the control samples used in experi-
ments done for dilute acid pretreatment, to monitor the rate of sacchariﬁcation [13]. It emerged
as a valuable new pretreatment approach but its optimal conditions, such as the temperature,
have not been evaluated.
In the sensitivity, designs where drying of biomass is not considered are simulated. In these
cases, the solids dilution rate is 12%, since the biomass enters to the system with the original
88% of moisture. Therefore, the solids are more diluted than the %20 moisture in gate stated in
Fasahati et al. (2015) [13]. However, in the simulation, the same hydrolysis and sacchariﬁcation
conversion are assumed.
The capital cost of the facility does not account for the storage tanks for the biomass nor the
commodities.
The non-reacted sugars removed before reaching the last distillation column in the downstream
process, are not accounted in the model. The price of the hot air is found in literature so that
it is estimated for the base case as an value between the electricity and the low pressure steam.

5.3
Future work
In order to have a more accurate model, performing speciﬁc experiments monitoring the
hydrolysis, sacchariﬁcation and fermentation of algal carbohydrates to lactic acid are required.
This would show the actual conditions such as temperature and retention time that lead to
more realistic conversion and yields.
Investing in research oriented on microbial strains able to ferment alginate to lactic acid may
lead to an economic proﬁtable process, as shown in the sensitivity analysis.
Among the assessed ones, the hot water wash emerges as a good option for macroalgae pretreat-
ment although it has been tested just at 50 oC while being used as a control for monitoring the
acid pretreatment. Although it shows better economic results than the dilute acid pretreatment,
the yield achieved is lower. Performing experiments with a range of temperatures will show the
relation between the temperature and the yield, without the addition of acid. In this manner,
proper trade oﬀ, which provides the optimal condition for this pretreatment, could be found.
Since, the sensitivity analysis shows that the design without the drying of biomass is more
feasible than the one with dry biomass, further studies on this possibility must be performed.
However, the limitation of obtaining biomass with the optimal composition during the whole
year hinders continuous process of the plant without taking into account the storage of biomass.
Since storing the wet biomass may lead to degradation of the feedstock, ﬁnding a ratio wet
biomass / dry biomass may prevent the usage of extra energy for the drying and the degrada-
tion of the feedstock when storing the algae with high moisture content.
When comparing the results given while doing sensitivity analysis on the scenario without dry-
ing drying biomass and with drying the biomass, the capital cost is aﬀected due to the diﬀerent
sizes of required equipment.
Also, the usage of the utilities is minimized since less cooling
and heating are needed. Thus, performing more experiments addressing lower dilution rates
will show if the yield of the process would be aﬀected while the capital and utility cost are
decreased.
A scenario with the REED/EDBM system connected to the fermentor could be implemented.
This conﬁguration consists of a in-situ separation of the lactic acid. . This design prevents the
pH drop related to the acid production so that the addition of CaCO3, as buﬀer, is not required.
Avoiding the addition of CaCO3 leads to considerable savings in the commodities cost.
The non-reacted sugars removed from the process before arriving to the distillation column
could be recirculated to the fermentor. Thus, the production of lactic acid is expected to be
increased.
Develop further the simultaneous TEA and LCA, extending the analysis to other impact cate-
gories disregardes in this analysis. Also, create more speciﬁc processes in SimaPro which address
the designed process steps.
