Abstract
The objective of this thesis was to assess the feasibility of waste heat utilization using an organic Rankine
cycle at the Kraﬂa Geothermal Power Plant in Iceland using two different optimization methods. The
effectiveness of the two methods in predicting the potential performance of multiple working ﬂuids was
assessed along with the current performance of the power plant.
The feasibility of the organic Rankine cycle was determined by using a model of the cycle with the net
power output as the objective function. Multiple working ﬂuids were used in the optimization and their
performance compared, both using the power output and a post processing of the results. The purchased
equipment cost and the total capital investment of the cycles was calculated. The investment feasibility was
assessed using the net present value method and the payback period of each cycle. The current performance
of the Kraﬂa Geothermal Power Plant was assessed using an exergy analysis of the plant and the results were
compared to the literature.

1.1
Low Temperature Waste Heat Utilization
A few methods have been suggested that are able to efﬁciently utilize low temperature waste heat. One
of these methods is the Organic Rankine Cycle (ORC). It uses the same setup as the conventional steam
Rankine cycle, however it utilizes organic working ﬂuids which have a lower boiling point than water and
are therefore better suited to utilizing low temperature heat sources. The working ﬂuids used are typically
hydrocarbons or refrigerants. The working ﬂuid has a big impact on the performance of the cycle and
therefore has to be chosen with a number of criteria in mind. The optimum setup of the cycles can also impact
the effectiveness, along with the temperature of the cycle and whether the heat source outlet temperature
from the boiler is limited or not.
In order to analyse the potential of the organic Rankine cycle a model of the cycle has to be constructed,
and based on that model a design can be implemented. However in order to construct the model a few
assumptions have to be made. The performance of the cycle can be greatly affected by these assumptions
and therefore it is logical to compare the performance predicted by the model with different assumptions.
This report will focus on two methods that have been suggested in order to model and optimize the organic
Rankine cycle.

1.1.1
ORC Optimization Methods
The traditional way of modelling and optimizing an ORC for a given heat source is assuming a minimum
pinch point temperature in the heat exchanger. However this method can have its drawbacks when the
temperature gradient of the ﬂuids in the heat exchanger don’t match evenly leading to a large loss in the
heat exchangers when condensation and evaporation take place. Therefore an alternative option of ORC
optimization has been suggested, the constant UA method, where UA is the product of the overall heat
transfer coefﬁcient of the heat exchanger and the heat exchanger area. This method assumes an overall UA
value for both the boiler and the condenser and the distribution of the UA values between the boiler and
condenser is an added optimization variable.
The pinch method has been analysed in multiple papers. Heberle et al. [7] used the method for low-enthalpy
geothermal resources with zeotropic mixtures and concluded that the non-isothermal phase change of the
zeotropic mixtures lead to a performance increase compared to pure ﬂuids. Gawlik et al. [8] showed that the
levelized purchased equipment cost can be reduced by the use of mixtures instead of pure ﬂuids. The method
of specifying a total heat exchanger area, A, per unit capacity in order to maintain a constant load in the
heat exchanger despite the capacity variation of different working ﬂuids, was suggested by McLinden et al.
[9]. They concluded that in order to account for the effect of the heat exchanger on the performance of the
cycle a constant total heat transfer coefﬁcient is preferred to assuming a pinch point temperature. Högberg et
al. [10] used three different methods for two mixture pairs; a equal pinch point temperature, equal mean
temperature difference and equal heat transfer area. They concluded that the predictions of the capacity of
the cycle and comparison of multiple working ﬂuids should be made using the equal heat transfer areas.
Andreasen et al. [11] compared the methods for use in marine engines and concluded that the UA based
method is more consistent in comparing multiple working ﬂuids since it is able to ensure a comparable mean
temperature difference in the heat exchangers.
This study will present a comparison of these two methods for a wider range of working ﬂuids than previous
studies available to the author. In addition the power output and cycle efﬁciency is not the only performance
criteria. The comparison rather includes the post-processing of the results with regards to seperation of the
overall heat transfer area, A, and the overall heat transfer coefﬁcient, U, of the heat exchangers. Finally
the heat exchanger cost will be used in order to assess the usefulness of the two methods in predicting the
potential performance of the optimized organic Rankine cycle.

2.1.1
Ideal Rankine Cycle
The organic Rankine cycle operates on the same principles as the Rankine cycle. The Rankine cycle is
an idealised steam cycle for power plants. The simple cycle consists of four main components, the pump,
evaporator, turbine and condenser. The working principals are as follows with reference to ﬁgure 2.2 with
water as the working ﬂuid
• Stage 1-2: Isentropic compression The low pressure water from the condenser is pumped to a higher
pressure before entering the boiler
• Stage 2-3: Isobaric heat addition The high pressure water evaporates in the boiler using the heat
from the heat source
• Stage 3-4: Isentropic expansion The high pressure steam is expanded in the turbine which turns the
axis for the generator
• Stage 4-1: Isobaric heat rejection The low pressure steam is condensed in the condenser with heat
rejection to the cooling medium [17]
The T-s diagram of the ideal Rankine cycle can be seen in ﬁgure 2.1. The water enters the pump at stage 1 as
a saturated liquid after the condenser. The pump compresses the water isentropically and thereby increases
its pressure to the boiler operating pressure. The increased pressure leads to a decrease in the speciﬁc volume
of the water and therefore increases the temperature slightly. A diagram of the cycle can be seen in ﬁgure
2.2
After the pump at stage 2 the water enters the boiler or the high recovery steam generator (HRSG). The
heat for the boiler is usually obtained from coal, combustion gases or nuclear energy. The boiler pre-heats,
evaporates and super-heats the water before it enters the turbine at stage 3 in an isobaric process.

2.1.2
Organic Rankine Cycle
The ORC is based on the Rankine cycle but uses an organic working ﬂuid which enables the cycle to
utilize lower temperature heat sources because of the working ﬂuids lower boiling point. The ﬁrst working
ORC was built in 1961 [18]. The working principle of the ORC is essentially the same as for the simple
Rankine cycle. The working ﬂuid is pumped to the working pressure of the boiler, expanded in the turbine in
order to produce work and then condensed in a condenser before entering the pump again. The ORC has
been suggested as a mean to utilize waste heat in a future where climate changes are of increasing concern.
Therefore the implementation of an ORC as waste heat recovery is able to effect the energy intensity of
many industrial processes positively. The ORC is also able to produce electricity from renewable sources
even if the heat source has low temperature.
Quoilin et al. [19] did a summary of the various advantages and drawbacks of ORCs. The following were
found to be the main differences between the two cycles
==PAGE:35==
2.1. ORGANIC RANKINE CYCLE PRINCIPLES AND RESEARCH
13
• Low temperature: The ORC is able to recupaterate heat at a considerably lower temperature than
steam due to the low boiling point of the working ﬂuid.
• Superheating: Since organic ﬂuids generally remain superheated after the expansion process there
is no need to superheat them speciﬁcally in the cycle. This can lead to decreased corrosion on the
turbine blades and therefore a longer lifetime.
• Component size: The steam Rankine cycle requires larger equipment due to low ﬂuid density in the
low pressure side of the cycle. In addition the high volume ﬂow rate demands larger diameter of pipes,
larger heat exchanger and turbine.
• Boiler: The small difference in density between liquid and vapor states of organic working ﬂuids
enables the use of a once-through heat exchangers. This is in contrast to the steam Rankine cycle
which has a relatively high density difference between liquid and vapor state and can therefore have
very different heat transfer characteristics.
• Expander inlet temperature: Steam Rankine cycles require high expander inlet temperatures in
order to avoid condensation in the expander. This is not the case in the ORCs due to the superheating
requirement.
• Pump: The pump work compared to the output power of the expander is considerably larger in ORCs
compared to steam Rankine cycles. This can be expressed as back work ratio (BWR). The power
consumption of the pump is in relation to the volume ﬂow rate. In addition the pump work compared
to the expander output power is related to the critical temperature of the working ﬂuid, i.e. with higher
critical temperature one can expect lower BWR.
• Working ﬂuid advantages: Water is generally a convenient working ﬂuid due to high availability, low
cost, low environmental impact, non-toxicity, low viscosity, low hazard level and chemical stability.
The organic working ﬂuids are therefore generally not able to compete with water within these criteria.
However water needs more treatment in order to avoid corrosion due to oxygen in the cycle.
• Expander: In steam Rankine cycles the pressure and enthalpy drop over the turbine is much greater
than when using an organic ﬂuid. This has a consequence of more expansion stages and therefore
higher cost in the steam Rankine cycle. In contrast the enthalpy and pressure drop in ORCs is much
lower and therefore single or two-stage turbines are usually employed. In addition the comparatively
lower enthalpy drop in the expander leads to lower rotation speed of the turbine which is able to
decrease the stress on the turbine blades, simplify the turbine design and enable a direct translation of
shaft work from the turbine to the generator without any additional gearing.
• Efﬁciency: The thermal efﬁciency of ORCs is generally lower than that of steam Rankine cycles due
to lower temperature heat source.

2.1.4
Transcritical and Subcritical Cycles
A transcritical cycle is a thermodynamic cycle where the working ﬂuid reaches both supercritical and
subcritical states. A subcricital thermodynamic cycle is one where the working ﬂuid is only in the subcritical
area. A few studies of the effect of subcritical and transcritical cycles on the ORC performance have been
performed. Daniël Walraven et al. [20] investigated the effect of transcritical and subcritical cycles on the
overall performance. The result was that the transcritical cycles obtained the highest performance. Andreasen
et al. [15] also investigated the performance of transcritical and subcritical cycles in ORCs and the result was
that transcritical cycles achieved the highest power output compared to subcritical cycles. The heat recovery
effectiveness was found to be higher for the transcritical cycles than for the subcritical ﬂuids. This report
will focus on subcritical cycles due to the lack of availability of heat exchanger correlation of transcritical
heat exchangers compared to subcritical ones.

2.1.5
Working Fluids
Some research has been made on the working ﬂuids of ORCs for example by Chys et al. [21] and
Heberle et al. [7] who researched the potential of zeotropic mixtures in ORCs and found a potential cycle
efﬁciency increase. The importance of the working ﬂuid in the cycle is high. The overall efﬁciency of the
Rankine cycle depends on the heat source, heat sink, components of the cycle and the properties of the
working ﬂuid. The working ﬂuid will effect the performance of the cycle and will have a big impact on the
design of the cycle components.
Working Fluid Performance Criteria
Quoilin et al. [19] made a guideline of parameters and indicators which have to be taken into account
when selecting working ﬂuids.
• Thermodynamics: The thermodynamic properties of different working ﬂuids can vary signiﬁcantly.
The aim of the working ﬂuid selection should be to maximize the power output and/or the efﬁciency
of the cycle. The thermodynamic performance of the working ﬂuid can depend on a variety of ﬂuid
properties, e.g. the density, critical point, speciﬁc heat etc. Therefore it is critical to compare multiple
working ﬂuids in order to obtain a valid optimization.
• Saturation curve: The saturation curve of the working ﬂuids can determine whether the working
ﬂuid needs superheating or not. A ﬂuid with a negative slope of the saturation curve is referred to as
a wet ﬂuid and can condense in the later stages of the expander leading to decreased efﬁciency and
damage to the turbine. The T-s diagrams of pentane and water respectively can be seen in ﬁgure 2.5.
The ﬁgure shows a) a positive saturation curve for pentane and b) a negative saturation curve for water.

• Vapour density: The vapour density of the working ﬂuid can indirectly affect the size of the heat
exchanger. With decreased density the volume ﬂow rate must increase. This leads to a larger heat
exchanger in order to limit the pressure losses which in turn leads to higher costs.
• Viscosity: By utilizing a ﬂuid with a low viscosity it is possible to decrease the losses that occur due
to friction and they in turn have high heat transfer coefﬁcients.
• Conductivity: The heat conductivity of the working ﬂuid can directly effect the size and effectiveness
of the heat exchangers.

• Evaporation pressure: Higher pressure system can increase the cost of the boiler and the thermal
stresses within it. Therefore a working ﬂuid with a lower critical pressure can have an advantage over
a working ﬂuid with a higher critical pressure.
• Condensation pressure: In the case of sub-atmospheric condensation pressure air can inﬁltrate the
cycle. Therefore the condensation pressure should be above the atmospheric pressure in order to avoid
this.
• Stability: The stability of organic working ﬂuids varies at high temperatures. There are risks of
chemical decomposition at high enough temperatures.
• Freezing: In order to avoid freezing of the working ﬂuid the melting point of the ﬂuid should be
higher than the lowest environment temperature.
• Safety: The safety aspect of the working ﬂuid selection is important. The two main factors are toxicity
and ﬂammability. The safety of different refrigerants can be categorized according to the ASHRAE 34
safety group.
• Warming potential: The Global Warming Potential (GWP) is deﬁned as a relative measure of how
much heat is trapped in the atmosphere by a greenhouse gas [22]. It uses the GWP of CO2 as the unit
value. The legislature regarding the release of these greenhouse gases locally has to be taken into
consideration.
• Ozone depletion: The Ozone Depleting Potential (ODP) is the comparative potential of depletion of
the working ﬂuid with trichloroﬂuoromethane (R-11) ﬁxed at unity [23]. Again the local legislature
has deﬁned the limits of the ODP of the ﬂuids.
• Cost and availability: The cost of the working ﬂuid is an important aspect of the working ﬂuid
criteria. If the optimium working ﬂuid is difﬁcult to obtain locally and needs to be imported in great
amounts with low stability in availability then the cost of obtaining it can be derisive.
In light of these parameters it is not certain a working ﬂuid that reaches the highest performance is the
most suitable for operation both due to economical unfeasibility, environmental viewpoints and hazard
consideration. Both pure ﬂuid selection and binary mixture ﬂuid selection for ORCs have been studied
although less focus has been on the binary ﬂuids [15].
Pure Fluids and Mixtures
A zeotropic mixture is a mixture of two or more combined working ﬂuids that have different boiling
points that never have the same composition of liquid or vapor at the vapor-liquid equilibrium line. The
potential of zeotropic mixtures in ORCs was studied by Chys et al. [21] and Heberle et al. [7] and showed
that by using zeotropic mixtures up to 20% increase in electrical production was obtained. The main
shortcoming of the pure ﬂuid optimization of ORCs is the ﬂat evaporation proﬁle of the pure ﬂuid compared
to the slope of the heat source. This is a source of large irreversibilities but can however be somewhat
resolved by zeotropic ﬂuids according to the results of Chys et al. [21]. Andreasen et al. [15] also concluded
that for multiple mixtures a power increase was possible while simultaneously reducing the pressure of the
heat exchangers.

2.2.1
Economical Feasibility and the Icelandic Power Market
Economical feasibility is important when considering waste heat utilisation. The investment has to be
able to be proﬁtable in the long term. The Icelandic power market has a history of low electricity prices.
This can be explained by being an isolated market, with no opportunity to sell to a higher bidder and the fact
that electricity is relatively cheap to produce from renewable sources, such as hydro power and geothermal
power. This has attracted numerous industries which require a large amount of electricity for its production,
e.g. aluminium smelters and ferrosilicon producers.
A common method used to determine the feasibility of an investment is the net present value (NPV) method.
The net present value method is the present value of cash ﬂows with the purchase price subtracted. The
payback period of the power plant is also of great interest to the investors.

2.4
Exergy Analysis Background
The deﬁnition of the second law of thermodynamics states that a process occurs in a certain direction
and has quality as well as quantity. Exergy analysis was ﬁrst developed as a concept by J.Willard Gibbs
in 1873 [25]. The name is derived from the Greek words ex and ergon and directly translates as "from
work". Exergy analysis is based on the second law of thermodynamics and can provide an alternative way
of thermodynamical analysis compared to a conventional energy analysis. It quantiﬁes the quality of the
available energy. Energy can not be destroyed, however the concept of exergy destruction is considered
useful when analysing the exergetic efﬁciency of a system. Exergy analysis is able to show the causes and
locations of inefﬁciencies in the thermodynamic system. It is a more reliable way of quantifying how the
performance of a thermodynamic process approaches an ideal process. Therefore it is possible to use the
results of an exergy analysis in order to optimize or improve designs of thermodynamic cycles [26].
Exergy analysis as a tool to analyse the performance of thermodynamic cycles has gained acceptance and is
now recognized internationally as a useful method by the industry and academia in recent years [26]. The
purpose of this section is to review the fundamentals of exergy analysis and the work done in the ﬁeld.
2.4.1
Fundamental Deﬁnition of Exergy
The fundamental deﬁnition of exergy is the maximum theoretical useful work obtainable between two
systems at two different states when put in contact with each other. The useful work refers to shaft or
electrical work. When an environmental reference state has been deﬁned it is possible to quantify the exergy
and assign it a value [27].
Environment and Dead State
The theoretical deﬁnition of an environment is that it is in a stable equilibrium and is both a sink
and a source for both materials and heat. The environment is considered without irreversibilities. All
irreversibilities are considered to be located within a system which is located within the environment. Many
models have been suggested in which the environment is modelled. The environment is usually considered
to be composed of certain common materials in the earths crust and the atmosphere which are in equilibrium
and have no possibility of producing work, neither from physical or chemical interactions. In this thesis the
environment is considered to be a large incompressible system with temperature T0 and pressure P0. The
reference temperature T0 will be considered to be the lower value of the average of the atmospheric and
cooling water temperature used for the power plant condenser [27].
The dead state is deﬁned as the state where the chemical, mechanical and thermal properties of the system is
in full equilibrium with the reference environmental properties. The system has in addition no elevation or
velocity in reference to the dead state. This means that no interaction can happen between the system and
the environment [27].
Exergy classiﬁcation
Classically the total exergy components of a system are divided into four categories, neglecting nuclear,
magnetic, electrical exergy and surface tension. The notation in this chapter will follow the notation
suggested by Bejan et al [27] and consider the following exergy classiﬁcations
• Physical exergy
• Chemical exergy

the model. Therefore the expander inlet temperature was added as an optimization parameter to the genetic
algorithm. This allowed the construction of a new energy balance for the boiler and therefore satisfy the
number of degrees of freedoms in the system.

ORC Optimization Description
Two different optimization methods were used for the pinch point method and the UA method, the
fminsearch and genetic algorithm respectively. These methods are both available in Matlab and are desribed
in the next two sections. The optimization parameters and solving parameters for the respective methods can
be seen in table 3.1.
The optimization uses a two level approach. The ﬁrst level uses either the genetic algorithm or the
fminsearch functions for the UA method and pinch point method respectively. The inner optimization uses
the fsolve function available in Matlab in order to obtain the power output of the cycle. An overview of the
process can be seen in ﬁgure 3.2. The optimization parameters are the parameters of the outer optimization
and the solving parameters are the parameters deﬁned in order to solve the ORC model.
The optimization parameters of the outer optimization for the pinch method are the turbine inlet pressure,
Pexp,in, the expander inlet temperature, Texp,in and the mole fraction of the mixtures. The solving parameters
of the inner optimization is the condensation pressure, Pcond with the pinch point temperature difference as
the objective function.
For the UA method the turbine inlet pressure, Pexp,in, the turbine inlet temperature, Texp,in, the ratio of UA
between the heat exchangers, UAratio and the mole fraction of the mixture, Y . The solving parameters
of the inner optimization are the condensation pressure and the heat source outlet temperature, Th,o. The
objective function is the UA values of the heat exchangers.

UA Optimization Method
The constant UA method was considered by Baik et al. [35] [36]. The purpose of using constant UA
value for comparison of different working ﬂuid is that the cost of the overall cycle is closely related to
the size of the heat exchanger. Therefore by using constant UA values when comparing different ORCs
the overall cost of the plant has been taken into consideration. The division of the total UA value of the
condenser and evaporator was optimized. The result showed a higher power output was obtained with
transcritical cycles when the total overall conductance of both the evaporator and condenser was above 35
kW/K and with subcritical cycles when the conductance was below that.
The optimized solution of the UA method is obtained by using the genetic algorithm function, ga, which
is available in the Global Optimization toolbox in Matlab [29]. The genetic algorithm (GA) is a model
for solving constrained or unconstrained optimizations based on the process of natural selection. They
were invented by John Holland in the 1970’s. Genetic algorithms are a subset of adaptive heuristic search
algorithms. They are designed to simulate processes in natural systems and are essentially based on the
survival of the ﬁttest. The GA uses an analogy of genetic structure in a population of individuals. Living
organisms consist of cells which in turn consist of chromosomes which consist of genes. These genes contain
a trait which can be essential to survival in a natural environment. The combined set of all chromosomes
is called a genome. The initial population is randomly created and uses an iterative process where each
iteration is called a generation. By evaluating the ﬁtness of each individual, i.e. the possibility of success in
life, in a generation the algorithm is able to select the ﬁttest individuals and discard the weakest and modify
the genome of the selected individuals and form a new generation. This is then repeated in each iteration.
The algorithm then converges to a solution either when a maximum number of generations is reached or the
ﬁtness of the population has reached a satisfactory level [37] [38]. A ﬂowchart of the GA process can be
seen in ﬁgure 3.3
Figure 3.3: A process overview of the genetic algorithm. Initially a population of individuals is created. The
individuals are evaluated, the ﬁttest individuals are selected, genes are crossed over and a new generation
created. This is an iteration process which will terminate when the maximum number of generations is
exceeded or the ﬁtness of the population has reached a satisfactory level.

Economical Model
The economical analysis of a thermal project is vital if it is to be a success, i.e. to make a proﬁt. The
feasibility of the investment can be calculated by estimating the total cost of the product, in this case the
electricity. This section will present the models used in order to calculate the cost and earnings of the ORC
for each working ﬂuid. The cost estimation is vital in the implementation of a project. The method used is
called the revenue requirement method and is detailed in Bejan et al [27]. It uses four steps in order to obtain
the cost of the ﬁnal project
1. Total capital investment (TCI) estimation
2. Determination of the economic, ﬁnancial, market input and operating parameters used for the detailed
cost calculation
3. Total revenue requirement estimation
4. Levelized product cost estimation
However in this case only the TCI was calculated in order to keep the calculations simple and since those
calculations should provide enough information in order to calculate the feasibility and the payback period
of the investment with different working ﬂuids. The feasibility was estimated using the net present value
(NPV) method where the cycle with the highest NPV was chosen as the most feasible one. This can be seen
in chapter 3.3.20. All values are in US dollars (USD). In addition the payback period of the cycles was also
calculated.
This section will describe how the purchased equipment cost was calculated for each component. The calcu-
lation of the heat exchanger area is of special concern, and heat exchanger correlations and considerations

Total Capital Investment Estimation
The total capital investment cost is a one time cost needed in order to build the plant. The total capital

direct costs consist of all permanent equipment and resources needed to build the plant. Indirect costs are not
used directly in the fabrication of the plant but are vital in order to complete the work. Other costs include
the startup costs, working capital, allowance for funds during construction, i.e. the interest that is incurred
during the construction of the plant. The breakdown of the TCI costs can be seen in table 3.7.

optimization the area can be calculated. The model used is based on the shell-and-tube heat exchanger model
by Kærn. et al [47]. The heat exchanger has the working ﬂuid inside the tubes in order to avoid leakage
[48]. The models used are based on the models used by Andreasen et al. [49] and modiﬁed in order to
obtain the area of the boiler and condenser. The heat exchanger is designed as TEMA (Turbular Exchanger
Manufacturers Association) [50] E type shell-and-tube heat exchangers with a single tube and shell pass.
The layout of the tubes is in a rotated square. The modelling conditions are shown in table 3.9.
Shell-and-tube heat exchangers are a series of tubes inside a larger parallel shell. This allows for one ﬂuid to
ﬂow on the inside of the tubes and another ﬂuid to ﬂow inside the shell around the tubes. The major parts
of a shell-and-tube heat exchanger are the tubes, bafﬂes, the shell, tubesheets, front-end head and rear-end
head. In Shah et al. [51] some guidelines are provided for the selection for each component and the most
important considerations will be listed here. These considerations were used as a guide for the input of the
area calculation models.
Tubes The geometry of the tubes is important to the overall heat transfer since it takes place across the tubes.
Usually the tubes are plain without ﬁns. When compensating for a low heat transfer a low ﬁnned
==PAGE:60==
38

tubing can be used. Shah lists the most common plain tube sizes as 15.88, 19.05 and 25.40 mm. By
using a smaller diameter the heat transfer coefﬁcient can be increased and it can decrease the size
of the heat exchanger. Smaller tubes are however harder to clean and tubes smaller than 19.05 mm
require chemical cleaning since mechanical cleaning is not practical.
The number of tubes in a heat exchanger are dependent of ﬂow rates of the ﬂuid and the available
pressure drop. The number of tubes are chosen in order to keep the velocity of the ﬂuids in the heat
exchanger within certain limits. Smaller tubes lead to increased velocity and vice versa. The lower
velocity limit is in place to decrease fouling and the higher limit is in place to decrease corrosion.
Tube passes refer to the number of times the ﬂuid inside the tubes passes through the shell ﬂuid. The
number of tube passes possible are related to the acceptable pressure drop. One tube pass is used in
the calculations in order to keep the pressure drop low and the calculations simple.
In this analysis a tube size of 15.88 mm is used and velocity constraints are according to table 3.9
which are based on the velocity constraints in [51]. The tube thickness is chosen as 1.651 mm which
is a standard thickness [51] for a 15.88 mm tube. The material is chosen as stainless steel with a heat
transfer coefﬁcient of 16 W/(m K) [52].
Tube layout The tube pitch, i.e. the centre-to-centre distances between adjoining tubes, can be deﬁned as a close
pitch with a low value of pitch to outer diameter ratio and an open pitch with a high value of the same
ratio. With a low value the shell-side heat transfer and compactness can be increased and with a large
value the ease of cleaning is higher and the likeliness of shell-side plugging decreases. The minimum
value of the tube pitch to the outside diameter is given by Shah as 1.25 and the maximum as 2.00. In
the calculations a value of 1.5 will be used. The standard layout of the tubes can be split into two
categories, the equilateral triangle and the square as shown in ﬁgure 3.5.
Figure 3.5: The tube layout arrangements. The two square layouts are either at a 90◦or 45◦angle and the
triangular layouts are either at a 30◦or 60 ◦angle.
The equilateral triangle is commonly oriented in a 30◦or 60◦angle compared to the ﬂow direction
and the square is commonly at 45◦and 90◦. A square pitch is usually not used because cleaning is not
feasible with a ﬁxed tubesheet design. In this analysis a rotated 45◦square layout will be used.
Bafﬂes The bafﬂes direct the ﬂow of the shell ﬂuid and support the tube bundles. In Shah the practical bafﬂe
spacing is set to 1/5 to 1 shell diameter and suggests that the optimum spacing could be from 2/5
to 1/2. In the calculations a value of 2/5 will be used. With lower allowed pressure drop the bafﬂe
spacing increases.
Shells The shell used for the calculations is a TEMA E-type shell. This shell is the most common of the
shell-and-tube heat exchangers due to both low cost and good reliability and simplicity. It is used for a

The heat exchanger model used was the one used by Andreasen et al. [49] and was designed for

Contingencies
Contingencies are the factor in which the cost estimates can vary. The estimates are often made without
satisfactory information and are therefore based on assumptions. However factors such as weather conditions,
equipment cost, shipping etc. could adversely effect the real cost of the project. Therefore cost contingencies
are put in place to take these uncertainties into consideration. Bejan suggests that the the contingency factor
ranges between 5-20% of the ﬁxed capital investment or 8-25% of the Engineering and supervision and
construction costs. This depends on the complexity, size and uniqueness of the plant. In this case the plant is
small, built on the site of an existing plant. However little experience in ORC’s is available which could
affect the cost. Therefore this category was considered to have a value of 15% of the engineering and
supervision and construction costs.

Startup Costs
The startup costs are mainly inﬂuenced by design changes of the plant that happen after the plant has
been built but before the system is able to operate at design conditions. The costs include the loss of revenue
while the plant is not running, labor, material and overhead costs. The startup costs are considered to be part
of the indirect costs of the plant. These costs are suggested to be about 5-12% of the ﬁxed-capital investment
by Bejan. Pierobon et al. suggest that the cost is about 4% of the FCI. By taking that into consideration the
startup costs were considered to be 5% of the FCI in this model.

Costs related to the research, licensing and development of the system must be added to the TCI. Pierobon
et al. suggest a value of 7.5% of the FCI which was used.
3.3.19
Allowance for Funds Used During Construction
It takes a certain amount of time from when the system is originally designed and when the plant is up
and running. The investment must cover all expenses during this period without any revenue. The investment
money can come from either loans, or directly from the company or a combination of both. However since
no return on the investment can be expected for a period of time the time value of the money used must be
considered. This can be done by considering the interest rate and is equal to the weighted cost of capital.
Again Pierobon et al. suggest 7.5% of the FCI which was used in this case.

than R141b and pentane separately, while the pure ﬂuids give the lowest power output. The binary ﬂuids
always give a higher power output compared to their pure ﬂuids counterparts. However the mixtures also
show an increase in the UA value. The pure R32 cycle and a mixture of R32/R134a give the highest output
of 3.90 MW and 3.91 MW respectively.
There are two transcritical cycles in the results, the pure R32 cycle and the mixture of R32/R134a. It can be
noted that while the net power output of R32/R134a is 28.2% higher than that of R141b the total UA value
UAtot is 60.3% higher which could indicate increased cost of the heat exchanger over-weighing the gain in
net power. In addition the pressure levels in the transcritical R134a/R32 and R32 cycles are considerably
higher than for the other analysed cycles. As mentioned, in section 2.1.5, a higher evaporation pressure level
could increase the boiler cost and the stresses with in it. Therefore a cycle with a lower evaporation level
could be desired. The condensation pressure is also an important parameter. As noted in, section 2.1.5, it is
preferred to have a condensation pressure above the atmospheric pressure in order to avoid air inﬁltrating the
cycle. Sub-atmospheric pressures are found in pure R141b, the R141b/pentane mixture and pure pentane.

In this section the results of the cost analysis and feasibility analysis is presented.
4.2.1
Cost Analysis
The cost of each component can be seen in table 4.4 along with the purchased equipment cost (PEC)
adjusted with inﬂation. The cost of the transcritical cycles was not calculated. The table shows that for both
methods the PEC of R141b is the highest. However this can not be explained solely by the heat exchanger
cost. The volumetric ratio between the inlet and the outlet of the expander has a large impact on the cost of
the turbine. As explained in section 3.3.2 the volumetric ratio, ˙Vratio should not exceed 4 in order to keep
the expander single stage.
For the pinch point method only R32, propane, R134a, propane/butane and butane have a volumetric ratio
below 4.No ﬂuid achieves a ratio below four in the UA method. This means that the PEC is not an ideal
parameter to compare the optimization methods, the cost of the heat exchanger is preferable due to the high
cost of the turbines and difference in turbine volumetric ratio. The volumetric ratio does however effect the
results of the feasibility study.
Propane has the lowest PEC for the pinch point method and the mixture of propane/pentane for the UA
method. These ﬂuids not only have a low heat exchanger cost but the turbine cost is low compared to other
ﬂuids with an turbine volumetric ratio above 4. The highest combined heat exchanger cost for the pinch
point method is for the propane/butane cycle and R141b for the UA method. For the pinch point method the
PEC of R141b is more then twice the PEC of propane although by looking at at table 4.1 the power output of
the R141b cycle is 3.05 MW while the power output of propane is 3.27 MW, representing a 7% more power
output for propane compared to R141b. For the UA method the PEC of the R141b cycle is also around twice
the cost of propane/pentane. The cost of the generator and the pump is not a signiﬁcant portion of the total
PEC, only representing a few percent of the total. For the cycles with a turbine volumetric ratio below 4 the
cost of the heat exchangers and the turbine are found to be similar.
4.2.2
Investment Feasibility
The total capital investment, net present value and payback period of each case can be seen in table 4.5
for both optimization methods. The table shows that the highest net present value is obtained for propane
and propane/butane for the pinch point method and propane/pentane and propane for the UA method. The
contrast between the methods with regards to the cost of the ﬁnal cycle can be clearly seen in the table.
For the pinch method the propane/pentane cycle has the highest net present value. However the NPV of
propane/pentane is negative using the UA method. The lowest net present value is obtained for R141b which
also has the lowest overall heat transfer coefﬁcient.
The net present value is positive for a number of ﬂuids for both cycles suggesting that the investment is
feasible, provided a suitable ﬂuid is used. However like mentioned in the introduction there are a few
considerations regarding the heat source ﬂuid that have not been taken into account.
It is interesting that the ﬂuids that have the highest net present value are ﬂuids that are in the middle in terms
of power output for both optimization methods. Therefore the power output of the cycles does not give a
strong indication the feasibility of the investment.
The payback time does range from 6.5 years to 16.2 years depending on the working ﬂuid and the optimization
method. This suggests that the added ORC would not take too long in paying back the original investment.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
In this chapter the results are discussed further, trends are observed and analysed and a broader perspective
is given on the results and how they are related. The results are also related to the objectives of the report.
Further comparison to the literature is made in order to validate the results and the observations.
5.1
Exergy Analysis
The calculation of the exergy efﬁciencies can be a powerful tool in visualizing the losses in the cycle
compared to the actual maximum performance achievable. One of the objectives of the thesis was to: assess
the performance of the Kraﬂa Power Plant compared to other conventional geothermal power plants in the
literature. As well the following objective was deﬁned: an assessment of the plant performance is calculated
with and without an ORC utilizing its waste heat. This comparison and assessment is shown in the following.
5.1.1
Exergy Efﬁciency
By comparing the exergy efﬁciency of the Kraﬂa Power Plant to literature analysis of binary ﬂash power
plants an estimation of the effectiveness of the plant can be performed. Bodvarsson et al considered the
exergetic efﬁciency of a double ﬂash geothermal power plant with a 250◦C heat source and a heat sink of
40◦C. They estimated the efﬁciency of the double ﬂash cycle at 49.0% [66]. Kanoglu however calculated the
actual exergetic efﬁciency of an existing binary power plant, and found the efﬁciency to be 29.1% of the
geothermal power cycle but rose to 34.2% using a waste heat recovery system [67]. Using this as a reference
to the Kraﬂa Power Plant the exergetic efﬁciency of the plant seems to be at a reasonable level compared to
the literature. By looking at table 4.6 the exergetic efﬁciency of the separator station and the condensers is
noticeably low.
Aljundi [68] made an exergy analysis of the Jordan power plant Al-Hussein. Al-Hussein uses heavy fuel
oil to power a rankine steam cycle. The results of the analysis with regards to exergy efﬁciency were quite
similar to the results in Kraﬂa. The turbine was found to have an efﬁciency of 73.5% compared to the 70.9%
efﬁciency of Kraﬂa, resulting in a percentage point difference of 2.6. The condenser was found to have
an exergy efﬁciency of 26.4% compared to 32.6% of Kraﬂa, a difference of 6.2 percentage points. The
total efﬁciency of the cycle was found to be 24.8%, although that includes a boiler in which 76.75% of the
exergy destruction took place. Aljundi also calculated the thermal efﬁciency to be 26.0%, which although is
considerably higher than that of Kraﬂa can be explained by a closed Rankine cycle and a considerably higher
cycle temperature. The exergetic efﬁciency of Kraﬂa therefore seems comparable to exergetic efﬁciencies in
the literature.

5.2
ORC Optimization
The two methods compared in the cycle are the constant pinch point method and the constant UA
method. One objective of the thesis was to: assess how useful two different ORC optimization methods are in
predicting the potential performance of an ORC. By using the results of both the net power optimization and
the consequent area, overall heat transfer coefﬁcient and cost calculations it is possible to observe general
trends for both methods and thereby compare them with each method’s advantages and disadvantages.
5.2.1
Comparison of the Pinch Point and UA Method
The main objective of the thesis was to compare the the effectiveness of two optimization methods in
predicting the potential performance of an organic Rankine cycle. Although the optimization objective was
net power output the methods also have to be comparable with regards to cost and feasibility. By decoupling
the U and A value of the heat exchangers an area estimation was performed for each cycle as the cost of the
heat exchangers are a pivotal part in estimating the total cost of the cycle and the cost is directly related to
the heat transfer area.

better in comparing the potential of each working ﬂuid without a necessary increase in cost.
The cost of the cycles optimized using the UA method is however not constant. Therefore it seems that in
order to make a full comparison between working ﬂuids with regards to cost as well as power output the cost

performed in addition to the power output optimization. The cost does need to be ﬁxed when comparing the

The results show that the selection of the working ﬂuid is important to the ﬁnal power output and the ﬁnal
cost of the cycle. The performance, in this case the power output, of the ﬂuid does not necessarily imply that
the cycle is the most proﬁtable.

5.3
Cost and Feasibility
Another objective was to: assess the feasibility of an ORC utilizing the waste heat of Kraﬂa power plant
using the payback time and net present value as the feasibility parameters. A discussion about the cost
of new geothermal power plants in Iceland and other criteria one has to keep in mind when assessing the
feasibility of power plant will be in the following sections.
5.3.1
Cost Comparison Per MW to Established Power Plants in Iceland
A comparison of the cost of installed power plants in Iceland can be made in order to evaluate how
expensive an additional ORC is compared to new power plant projects in Iceland. Hydropower is responsible
for 72% of the produced electricity while geothermal is responsible for 24%. Hellisheidarvirkjun is a
geothermal combined heat and power plant built in 2006. It produces 303 MW of electricity and 133 MW of
district heating. The estimated cost of the plant was 560 mill. USD [69]. Another geothermal power plant,
Theistareykir, now being built with has an estimated power production of 45 MW and an estimated cost of
184 mill. USD [70]. Therefore the cost of each MW of electricity is 1.84 mill. USD and 4.1 mill. USD per
MW for Hellisheidarvirkjun and Theistareykir respectively. This represents a large difference between the
two power plants, however the ﬁnal cost of Theistareykir is not yet determined and could yet increase or
decrease.

5.3.2
Hazard and Global Warming Potential
The safety classiﬁcations of the pure ﬂuids can be seen in table 3.5. The safety of the ﬂuids is determined
by both their toxicity and their ﬂammability with A1 being the least toxic and least ﬂammable and B3 the
most toxic and ﬂammable. Propane, butane and pentane all fall into the A3 category. This means that
they are not toxic but are highly ﬂammable. This means that these working ﬂuids are possibly not ideal,
depending on the safety regulations at the site. R134a is a common ﬂuid in commercial ORCs and has the
safety classiﬁcation of A1, meaning that it is not particularly toxic or ﬂammable, making it an ideal candidate.
Both R245fa and R141b also fall under this classiﬁcation. R32 is classiﬁed as an A2 ﬂuid meaning that it is
more ﬂammable than the other refrigerants considered. Finally no classiﬁcation could be found for R1234ze
since it is still pending evaluation from ASHRAE.
The global warming potential of the hydrocarbons, propane, butane and pentane, is relatively low compared to
the other considered ﬂuids with a GWP value of 3.3. The refrigerants mostly have a GWP value considerably
higher than this with R134a obtaining a GWP rating of 1300. R1234ze is however considered a low GWP
refrigerant and only has a GWP rating of 7. With increased concern about the pollution from geothermal

5.4.3
Economical Analysis
The results of the cost analysis can be compared to results of similar studies. Salas [72] made a
comparative study of purchased equipment cost for organic Rankine cycles. The results show that the
speciﬁc cost of an ORC with a resources temperature of 120◦C is approximately 950 USD/kW. The
purchased equipment cost of the optimizes cycles in this study vary from 403 to 1099 USD/kW with an
average value of 759 USD/kW. This is on average quite lower than the results of Salas, a difference of 20.2%.
This can be explained by difference in working ﬂuid, and the capacity of the cycle.
5.4.4
Total Capital Investment
Salas also performed a literature review of the speciﬁc capital cost (SPC) for organic Rankine cycles.
According to EPRI [73] the speciﬁc capital cost is between 2481 to 3848 USD/kw. The results of the pinch
point method estimate the speciﬁc capital cost as 1875 to 4054 USD/kW and 1787 to 3643 USD/kW using
the UA method. The average for the pinch method is 2826 USD/kW and 2451 USD/kW. The difference can
be explained by the difference in capacity of the cycles and a difference in the working ﬂuids used. The
results of the speciﬁc capital cost are generally in accordance with the literature. A better estimation of the
capital costs could be obtained by consulting experts in the ﬁeld and receiving information from already
built power plants regarding costs.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions and Further Work
Here the conclusions obtained from the work and discussion are presented. The chapter also contains
suggestions of further work in the ﬁeld.
6.1
Organic Rankine Cycle Optimization Effectiveness
A comparison of the effectiveness of two organic Rankine cycles optimization methods, using a variety
of working ﬂuids, to predict the potential performance of the cycle is performed. The methods are based on
a constant pinch point temperature in the heat exchanger and a constant UA value of the heat exchangers.
A calculation of the heat exchanger cost and total capital investment was performed for each cycle. The
results show that the constant UA method shows greater potential in comparing a variety of working ﬂuids
when the heat exchanger cost should be considered. The constant UA method does not ensure constant heat
exchanger cost, however the potential of each working ﬂuid can be compared on a uniﬁed bases using the
UA method.
The results conﬁrm that a ﬂuid mixture improves the net power output of the cycle compared to its pure ﬂuid
counterparts. This is showed to be the case for both methods for the selected ﬂuids.
6.2
Exergy Analysis
An exergy analysis of the Kraﬂa Power Plant is performed using measurements from the plant. The
results are compared to results from the literature in order to obtain the relative performance of the plant.
The results show that the exergetic efﬁciency of Kraﬂa is 30.6%. This is comparable to other geothermal
power plants found in the literature. The main source of exergy destruction is found to be in the steam
separator station, a total of 76.8%. The exergetic efﬁciency increase of adding an ORC in order to utilize the
waste heat from the separators is found to be from 1.69% to 2.16%.
6.3
Organic Rankine Cycle Feasibility
The total capital investment and net present value of the optimized organic Rankine cycles is calculated
along with the payback period of each cycle.
The results show that in most cases the investment is feasible with regards to a positive net present value.
The payback period of the cycles is found to be in the range of 6.5-16.2 years. The price per MW of the
optimized cycles is calculated for two new geothermal power plants in Iceland and compared to the results

of the optimization. The comparison shows that the price per MW of the optimized organic Rankine cycle is
in many cases comparable to the price per MW for the new power plants. The results therefore show that the

6.4
Further Work
In order to make a comparison between working ﬂuids with regards to performance on a constant cost
basis one needs to add the cost as an optimization variable. This means that the organic Rankine cycle
needs to be optimized as done in this study simultaneously with an optimization of the cycle’s components.
This could incur a computationally heavy optimization but would ensure an equal basis with regards to the
performance of the ﬂuids.
The potential for waste heat recovery of the Kraﬂa Power Plant can be researched further while taking the
potential disadvantages of the geothermal ﬂuid into consideration. In addition a more detailed cost analysis
of the production and installation of the cycle can be performed.
The potential of utilizing an ORC for electricity production in low temperature ﬁelds around Iceland is an
interesting study. As mentioned in the introduction there have been identiﬁed around 250 low temperature
ﬁelds in Iceland. To the authors knowledge only one low temperature ﬁeld has been used for electricity
production, a Kalina cycle in Husavik, in northern Iceland. The potential for electricity production with an
ORC could therefore be an interesting study, especially in the rural areas of the country.
