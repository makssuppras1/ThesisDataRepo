Abstract
Given that people are spending a lot of time indoors where they are exposed to potentially
harmful pollutants, ventilating buildings is essential to ensure the occupants’s health and
comfort. To reduce the high energy consumption due to heat losses or fan operation,
smart ventilation systems have started to see the light. These systems are using sensors
to measure metrics such as a pollutant’s concentration or the occupancy to adjust their
airflows and save energy. However, most of the standards and regulations are imposing
fixed minimum airflows instead of focusing on the actual indoor air quality they provide.
There is therefore a need to develop a performance­based approach that can easily be
used by the manufacturers to assess new ventilation systems before putting them on
the market or by building designers to chose the most appropriate strategy for a given
project. The aim of this thesis was to develop a set of indicators based on the pollutants
of concern in Danish dwellings by going through relevant literature and based on previ­
ous work on performance­based approach. Ten indicators were designed to assess the
performances of ventilation systems regarding carbon dioxide (CO2), Relative humidity
(RH), formaldehyde and fine particulate matter (PM2.5). Their usability was then demon­
strated on a typical Danish case simulated in CONTAM by comparing the performances
of a humidity­controlled system with a constant air volume (CAV) system. The indicators
were first normalized by taking the CAV system as a reference. This allowed to draw
some conclusions with the humidity­controlled system performing better regarding CO2
and high RH and the CAV system performing better for formaldehyde and low RH. The
implementation of thresholds for each indicator then showed that none of the system was
actually providing an acceptable Indoor Air Quality (IAQ) for the occupants and therefore
highlighted the importance of having agreed thresholds or reference systems to be able
to use these indicators for performance­based evaluation.

1
Introduction
People are spending, on average, 60% to 90% of their time indoor (home, school, work)
[1] where they can be exposed to harmful concentrations of pollutants that can lead to
cardiovascular and respiratory diseases or cancer for example. In fact, household air
pollution was responsible for about 117 200 deaths in 2012 in Europe as stated by the
World Health Organization (WHO) [2]. Ventilating buildings is therefore essential to ensure
the health and comfort of the occupants.
But ventilation doesn’t come without consuming energy (heat loss, fans) and new types
of smart systems start to see the light. The AIVC [3] defines the smart ventilation con­
cept as follow: Smart ventilation is a process to continually adjust the ventilation system
in time, and optionally by location, to provide the desired IAQ benefits while minimizing
energy consumption, utility bills and other non­IAQ costs (such as thermal discomfort or
noise). This means that sensors are used to assess metrics such as pollutants’ concen­
tration, condensation risks or occupancy to adapt the airflow rate and ensure a healthy
environment without using unnecessary energy. As shown by Mortensen [4], heating and
electricity savings associated with demand controlled ventilation (DCV) are assumed to
be reduced by 30% compared to a CAV system with heat recovery. DCV systems are
defined as a specific type of smart ventilation systems that are continuously and automat­
ically adjusting the ventilation rates according to the pollutant loads [1].
However, standards are mainly imposing prescriptive requirements with fixed minimum
airflow rates which is not adapted to those systems. In fact, the aim of ventilation in a
building is to control the pollutants so it would be more relevant to focus on their con­
centrations to assess the performance of a ventilation system. To implement this kind of
performance­based method, appropriate indicators must be developed by targeting the
most critical pollutants for the occupants’ health.
Some projects have already been conducted to develop such indicators and a few coun­
tries like France or Belgium are already using performance­based method in their stan­
dards but this is not a common approach in many other countries.
A literature review was conducted to have an overview of what has been done in the field
of performance­based evaluation and better understand the needs in order to target this
thesis’ objectives.

Background
2.1
Previous studies on performance­based approach
The development of smart ventilation systems has raised concerns on how to assess their
abilities to provide a good air quality for the occupants without functioning at a given con­
stant airflow rate. Previous studies about performance­based approach were reviewed to
better understand what has been done on the subject.
Guyot et al. [1] conducted a review on performance­based approaches used in five differ­
ent countries in Europe and the US. The common thread in all the methods studied in the
paper is the use of, at least, the exposure to a pollutant generated indoors (often CO2)
and the condensation risk. There are some similarities among the indicators used in the
different countries: cumulative CO2 exposure over a certain limit, mean CO2 over a given
period, time above a certain RH threshold and simple pre­calculated multipliers for use in
energy calculations. However, the targeted values are different from country to country
so there is a need for a common evaluation method to facilitate the development of smart
ventilation system and the validation process that the manufacturers need to go through.
Poirier et al. [5] reviewed relevant papers and standards dealing with IAQ performance
indicators to select a reduced set of indicators considering other pollutants than just CO2
and humidity. After having identified the pollutants of concern in the buildings, they an­
alyzed the corresponding indicators and their thresholds. Finally, a set of five indicators
with their thresholds was chosen and tested on a low­energy house to compare the per­
formances of three ventilation systems. The chosen indicators were the following:
• maximum cumulative exceeding CO2 exposure over 1000 ppm
• maximum cumulative occupant formaldehyde exposure
• maximum cumulative occupant PM2.5 exposure
• maximum percentage of time with RH higher than 70% (condensation risk)
• maximum percentage of time with RH outside a range of 30–70% (health risk)
The application on the low­energy house case demonstrated that this method made it
possible to compare the performance of several ventilation systems. One of the main
observations made was the poor performance of all the systems regarding formaldehyde
and PM2.5. In fact, the thresholds were largely exceeded for those pollutants, meaning
that there was a health risk for the occupants. This highlighted the need of considering
other parameters than just CO2 and humidity when assessing the performance of venti­
lation systems.
In her paper, Justo Alonso [6] also exposes what needs to be done to improve DCV
and its evaluation. By performing measurements in offices and houses and a literature
review, the paper shows that measuring CO2 and temperature is not sufficient to control
ventilation systems and that pollutants not linked to room occupancy might rise beyond
the health recommendations even if the first two parameters are kept in the right ranges.
The conclusion to the measurements was the need to measure 3 different parameters:
• Pollutants related to human activity (CO2)
• Pollutants that infiltrate from combustion or traffic outdoors (PM2.5)

• Parameters related to other “non­combustion­related activities” indoors, heat sources
and moisture loads
Another parameter to consider is the ability to measure the different pollutants. In fact,
sensors can be very expensive and low­cost ones are just becoming deployed for other
parameters than CO2 and temperature, their accuracy is therefore limited, so a calibration
methodology was developed by Justo Alonso [6]. Finally, co simulations between CON­
TAM and Energy Plus were performed to compare different control strategies by consid­
ering IAQ and energy consumption. The results showed that all DCV control strategies
led to reduction in energy use compared to the typical control strategy based on CO2.
It was also highlighted that considering PM2.5 in ventilation control strategies was more
complicated than CO2 but it was clearly justified in polluted urban areas.
2.2
Pollutants of concern
As explained in the previous studies on performance­based evaluation, there is a need
to measure other parameters than just CO2, temperature, and RH to control and assess
the ventilation systems. A literature review was conducted in this thesis to highlight the
most concerning pollutants in residential buildings, first at an international scale and then
focused on Denmark.
2.2.1
International scale
For this part, three papers were reviewed: the International Energy Agency (IEA) report
on Annex 68­subtask 1 [7], the AIVC technical note about residential ventilation and health
[8], and and Mortensen’s PhD report about demand­controlled ventilation for multi­family
dwellings [4].
IEA­EBC (Energy in Buildings and Communities) Annex 68 aims at gathering data on
pollution sources in buildings and finding the methods to optimize ventilation and air­
conditioning. Subtask 1 is setting up the metrics to assess the performance of low energy
buildings by ensuring a good IAQ with high energy performance. A list was constructed
by the IEA­EBC [7] with the pollutants that are known to have an impact on the occupants’
health. To define this list, the pollutants described as harmful for humans by cognizant
authorities for long­ and short­term exposures were considered, and their concentrations
were then checked in dwellings to see if they were exceeding the recommended levels.
The AIVC technical note [8] is focusing first on the indoor quality in buildings and its ef­
fects on occupants’ health and is then exposing the control strategies that can be used
to improve the IAQ. One of the chapters is focusing on the prioritization of the pollutants
by reviewing relevant standards and papers based on different methods: hazard assess­
ment, cumulative risk assessment, impact assessment exposure, cost benefit analyses
of ventilation, impact of specific sources. An overview is then given with the priority pol­
lutants for chronic exposure and the potential acute exposure concerns.
Mortensen’s PhD report [4] is focusing on the ventilation rates and design of DCV systems
after having identified the priority pollutants in indoor environments. To identify them, three
publications were reviewed and the pollutants that were considered as hazardous were
listed.

The pollutants listed in table 2.1 are quite numerous and they shouldn’t all be measured to
assess ventilation performances. To select the most harmful ones for the occupants, the
DALY approach can be used. The DALY acronym means Disability­Adjusted Life Years
and is defined by the WHO as follow: ”One DALY can be thought of as one lost year of
”healthy” life. The sum of these DALYs across the population, or the burden of disease,
can be thought of as a measurement of the gap between current health status and an ideal
health situation where the entire population lives to an advanced age, free of disease and
disability” [7]. This metric allows to quantify and compare the impact of various pollutants
and each disease induced which is very interesting in the case of assessing the impact of
IAQ on occupants. Two methods can be used to estimate the DALYs lost for air pollutants:
the intake­incidence DALY approach based on epidemiological data to quantify disease
incidence or the intake­DALY method that uses animal toxicity literature to calculate the
health impact.
Figure 2.1 shows the ranking of pollutants using their DALYs. It can therefore be con­
cluded that particulate matter, formaldehyde, and nitrogen dioxide are the most concern­
ing pollutants in buildings.

Once the pollutants of concern are identified, there presence can be measured in many

regarding whether they can be dangerous at chronic or acute exposure.
2.2.2
Danish context
To elaborate indicators adapted to smart ventilation systems in Denmark, it is important to
know what pollutants are the most concerning in Danish dwellings. The available literature
has been investigated and the results are summarized in this part.
To start with, Skovgaard et al. [9] indicates that Danes spend on average 80% to 90% of
their lives indoors with 16 hours per day at home and that 22,000 DALYs are lost every
year due to poor indoor climate in Denmark (which equals to about 376 DALYs per year
per 100,000 persons).
The main health issue found during the research is the high prevalence of mite asthma in
Danish population due to the growth of house dust mites in dwellings that thrive the most
at temperatures between 17 and 32°C and RH between 55% and 75% ([10], [9]). RH
should then be kept at lower values to avoid mould growth and prevent the development
of these mites. However, the humidity level shouldn’t be too low to avoid problems such
as thinning of eye tear film for RH<30%, nasal or throat dryness for RH<10% or spreading
of influenza virus for RH<40% (more relevant for public spaces).[11],[12]
Another great concern in residential buildings is the exposure to particulate matter. In
fact, they are responsible of 2/3 of the annual loss of healthy life years in Europe. It
is commonly thought that particles come from outdoor air brought inside via aeration or
ventilation. However, 76% of the particles in homes come from indoor sources and are at
least as dangerous as outdoor ones. Cooking and candles burning are the most important
sources. In fact, a study has been conducted on 7,000 Danes showing that 50% of them
light candles at least 4 times a week. Therefore, it seems that the population is unaware
of the health risks associated with this activity. In fact, particles emitted from candles are
quite similar to diesel pollution that is known to increase the risk of lung and cardiovascular
disease. As for cooking, it represents almost 30% of the particles’ emission. [9]

Finally, the exposure to building generated pollution shouldn’t be forgotten. In fact, volatile
organic compounds can react with ozone and form species that can be potentially harm­
ful for the occupants. Moreover, recent measurements of formaldehyde concentrations
in 20 newly built Danish homes (built between 2001 and 2007) showed a tendency of
higher formaldehyde concentrations in the newest and biggest homes and the guideline
set by the WHO for formaldehyde concentration was exceeded for two dwellings. Their
can be various sources of formaldehyde in buildings that still need to be identified but
wooden panels or oil­treated wooden floors (that are widely used in Denmark) are known
to emit this pollutant [13]. Measurements were then made on possible sources such
as wooden boards, insulation materials, textiles, carpets, paints, and cleaning products.
Fiberboard was identified as the strongest formaldehyde source and further measure­
ments showed that, even if the wooden boards were complying with the Building Regula­
tion’s requirements, the formaldehyde concentrations in small rooms could reach values
six times higher than the WHO recommendations for indoor air [14].
The indicators chosen for Danish dwellings therefore need to consider the concerns de­
scribed above to ensure smart ventilation systems are properly assessed in the sake of
the occupants’ health.

and comparing them exist. Certifications and standards have been studied to evaluate
how performance­based assessment was conducted in different countries.

The certification schemes BREEAM [17] and DGNB [18] have also been studied to see
what indicators were used to assess the air quality of the building. For both certifica­
tions, sample measurements are conducted in the real building and are mostly done to
assess the emissivity of the construction and not directly the ventilation system so this is

construction and before occupancy. The formaldehyde concentration must not exceed
100 µg.m−3, averaged over 30 minutes and the total volatile organic compounds (TVOC)
concentration must not exceed 300 µg.m−3, averaged over 8 hours. DGNB is also focus­
ing on those pollutants and buildings with a TVOC (total) concentration greater than 3,000
µg.m−3 or a formaldehyde concentration greater than 100 µg.m−3 cannot be certified.
Finally, the updated Danish building regulation [19] is stating the following requirement for
ventilation systems: ”In living spaces as well as in the dwelling as a whole, there must
be an outdoor air supply of at least 0.30 l/s per m² of heated floor area. However, if the
ventilation system is able to regulate the outdoor air supply according to satisfactory air
quality and humidity conditions in the dwelling by measurement, it is allowed to reduce the
outdoor air supply to 0.15 l/s per m² for a longer period over the day if the dwelling is not
in use.”. This criteria is more in balance with smart ventilation systems than the previous
version imposing a minimum flow rate of 0.30 l/s per m² of heated floor area but it is still
imposing a minimum air flow rate instead of focusing on the actual IAQ of the building.
2.4
Objectives of the thesis
The background study about smart ventilation and performance­based evaluation high­
lighted the following needs:
• Other pollutants than just CO2 and humidity should be considered when assessing
the IAQ in a building (formaldehyde, PM2.5, ...)
• A generic method with indicators and thresholds should be implemented to facilitate
the development and the evaluation of smart ventilation systems worldwide
• Standards should be adapted by using such indicators actually assessing the IAQ
instead of requiring minimum flow rates
Starting from these needs, the objective of the present thesis was to select a set of
performance­based indicators adapted to the Danish context and needs to be able to
assess smart ventilation systems. Simulations of a Danish dwelling were then performed
with the software CONTAM to demonstrate the use of these indicators by comparing a
DCV system with a constant air volume system.

To evaluate the usability of these indicators, they were tested on a CONTAM simulation

concentrations simulated for the heating period (15/10 to 15/04) for 4 different ventilation

mechanical humidity­controlled exhaust­only ventilation (MEV­rh), and balanced mechan­
ical ventilation with heat recovery with room­based control (MVHR­rb) [23].

3.2.1
Presentation of CONTAM
As described in the CONTAM user guide [25], CONTAM is a multi­zone pressure­driven
model developed by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) and de­
signed to help the user determine:
• Airflow Rates: infiltration, exfiltration, and room­to­room airflow rates in building sys­
tems driven by mechanical means, wind pressures acting on the exterior of the
building, and buoyancy effects induced by the indoor and outdoor air temperature
difference.
Performance based approaches to evaluation of residential ventilation
13
==PAGE:24==
• Contaminant Concentrations: the dispersal of airborne contaminants transported by
these air flows; transformed by a variety of processes including chemical and radio­
chemical transformation, adsorption, and desorption to building materials, filtration,
and deposition and resuspension to and from building surfaces, etc.; and generated
by a variety of source mechanisms.
• Personal Exposure: the prediction of exposure of occupants to airborne contami­
nants for eventual risk assessment
CONTAM works with a pressure­based code which means that the air flow rates are cal­
culated using the pressure difference between the zones based on the principle of conser­
vation of mass. The air flow rates calculated are then used to determine the contaminants
concentration in the different zones.
This software was chosen because it allows to simulate the emission of and exposure to
other pollutants than just CO2.
The following limits can however be highlighted:
• Each zone is assumed to be well­mixed, so the contaminants emitted in a zone at
a certain time are diluted in its entire volume within one time step.
• There is no thermal model, which means that the temperature is considered constant
inside a zone.

3.2.4
CO2 emissions
CO2 is emitted by all the occupants and its emission rate depends on their age, weight,
and level of activity. Persily and de Jonge [31] propose a method to estimate CO2 gen­
eration rate based on human metabolism and exercise physiology using recent data and
thus allowing more accurate results than the values often found in the literature that have
been used for decades.
This approach is divided in several steps. First, the Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) of the
person needs to be determined using their age, sex, and body mass. Then, the value of
physical activity can be estimated and the product of those two values is used to calcu­
late the oxygen consumption (1 kcal of energy consumption leads to 0.206 L of oxygen
consumption) and the CO2 production (based on a respiratory quotient of 0.85). This con­
version results in 1 MJ/day in energy use corresponding to 0.00048 L/s of CO2 production.
The CO2 generation rate can be expressed in L/s as follow at an air pressure of 101 kPa
and a temperature of 273K. BMR is in MJ/day and M in met.
VCO2 = BMR · M · 0.000484
(3.18)
A simplified table is proposed by Persily and de Jonge [31] to easily estimate the CO2
generation rate by only knowing the age of the occupant and their activity level. This
table was reprinted in the appendix (fig. A.1).
Figure A.1 gives the CO2 emission rates for an air temperature of 273 K. However, the

to be used to convert the values to the right temperature. The pressure is kept at 101
kPa.
VCO2,T = BMR · M · T

(3.19)
This formula can then be expressed with the table values:
VCO2,T = VCO2,273K · 0.003661

Formaldehyde emissions
VOC emissions are often hard to predict, and the models don’t often correspond to real
life measurements. Johnston et al. [36] developed two emission models for formaldehyde
by performing a regression analysis on data measured in Danish semi­detached homes.
The model for normal emissions was chosen to approximate the emissions for the simula­
tions in this thesis. The model uses the air change rate, the absolute humidity, and the air
temperature because increasing one of those parameters will increase the formaldehyde
emission rate as shown in this equation:
EHCHO = −1.340·10−2+2.627·10−5·N +9.019·10−5·T +3.498·10−7·AH −1.518·10−7·T 2
(3.23)
With EHCHO the emission rate estimated by models derived from regression analysis in
mg.s−1.m−2), N the ACH in h−1, AH the absolute humidity in g.m−3, and T the absolute
temperature in K.
An accurate way to use it would be to run the simulations with CONTAM a first time to
obtain the values of air change rate and absolute humidity for each time step and then

4.4
Suitability of the RH indicators
Originally, the RH indicator proposed by the Annex 86 was gathering the high and the low
limits of relative humidity. For this thesis, it has been chosen to separate it into two indica­
tors: one focusing on low humidity to assess the occupant discomfort, and one focusing
on high humidity to assess the mould formation risk in the building. Figure 4.9 illustrates
the relevance of this separation in regard to performance­based evaluation. In fact, the
Annex 86 indicator is hiding the information shown by ERHo and ERHr. For example, in
the case of the family, the general indicator indicates that the CAV system is performing
better in terms of relative humidity whereas it is in fact performing better regarding low
humidity and occupant’s comfort but not for high humidity and mould formation risk.
Regarding ERHo, the values obtained in table 4.2 are lower than what was expected for

4.5
Implementation of thresholds
As explained earlier in the report, the choice was made here to compare the humidity­
controlled system to a reference CAV system by normalizing the indicators according to
this latter. This method can be very useful to compare different systems and choose the
one that is more adapted to the needs of a specific household. However, without thresh­
olds, it is hard to determine if the systems compared are actually providing a sufficiently
healthy IAQ for the occupants. If the performance­based indicators are to be used as a
tool to allow a ventilation system to be put on the market they should be compared to
commonly agreed thresholds or to a reference system that is already known to provide
an acceptable IAQ. To illustrate how this can be implemented, a short study have been
conducted at the end of this thesis period and thresholds have been set for the indicators.
ECO2 can be interpreted as how much higher than 1000 ppm is the CO2 exposure con­
34
Performance based approaches to evaluation of residential ventilation
==PAGE:45==
centration on average. The threshold was chosen at 200 ppm which means that, in the
worst acceptable case, the CO2 exposure concentration would always be at 1200 ppm
which corresponds to the requirement for Category II for indoor environment quality (IEQ)
in EN 16798­1 [44].
PCO2 indicates how high the CO2 concentrations are rising. In households the concen­
trations are usually not reaching harmful concentrations for the occupants but setting a
threshold on this value can allow to detect a malfunction of the ventilation system if the
occupant is exposed to very high values for a limited time when being in a certain room
for example. As stated by Zhang et al. [45] physiological effects can be observed above
3000 ppm so this value was chosen as a threshold for this indicator.
ERHr is the percentage of time when the relative humidity is higher than 60%. Therefore,
an accurate way to set a threshold would be to calculate what surface RH it corresponds
to in the room and, regarding the materials used, find the time taken by mould to form
on it. A simpler way was used here by taking the values given in the French requirement
[15] to avoid condensation. Since the indicator is taking the maximum RH among all
rooms and that the bathroom is the most humid here, the threshold was set according to
the bathroom requirement: maximum of 1000h above 75% of RH over the heating period
which corresponds to 23% of the time for this thesis’ simulations. ERHr is designed with an
upper limit of 60% RH but the threshold was kept as calculated by the French requirement
for 75%, keeping in mind that the relative humidity at the surfaces is higher than in the
room air.
ERHo can be interpreted as how much lower than 30% is the exposure relative humidity
on average. To determine the threshold, an extensive literature review conducted by
Wolkoff [11] was used. His paper summarizes numerous results from studies made on
animals and humans regarding the effects of low RH (dry eyes, irritated airways). Looking
at the different results, it can be seen that most of the first symptoms are observed at RH
between 15% and 25%. Therefore, a threshold of 5% was chosen for this indicator. It
means that, in the worst acceptable case, the exposure RH would always be at a value
of 25%.
Finally, for the PM2.5 and formaldehyde indicators, the exposure limit values (ELV) pre­

indicators) and a long­term ELV (used for the basic cumulative indicators and the DALY

the combination of IAQ and energy indicators could be used to find the best compromise
when selecting a ventilation system.

4.7
Use of the DALYs indicator
In this thesis, the calculation of the DALYs indicators was only showing the value obtained
over the entire simulated time for comparison ease. However, as intended by De Jonge
and Laverge [21] when creating this indicator, calculating the dynamic DALYs at every
time step would be an interesting way of detecting the periods where pollutants cause
most harm to the occupants. Figure 1 from De Jonge and Laverge [21] illustrates this
phenomenon by showing, with a theoretical and extreme case, how the dynamic DALYs
are evolving compared to the pollutant concentration an occupant is exposed to. For this
case the occupant is only present for two hours a day and is engaged in high polluting
events during that time (cooking for example). The figure shows that the dynamic DALYs
curve evolves with a steep slope when the concentration of pollutant is peaking and stays
at a constant value when the exposure concentration is null. A steeper increase of the
DALYs indicates that the indoor air the occupant is exposed to at that time is more harmful
than when the increase is less steep. This method can therefore be used to adapt the
airflow rate of a ventilation system when the indoor air is harmful for the occupants.
Comparing the values used in the study [21] with the ones obtained in this thesis revealed
rather large differences with DALYs twenty­five to one hundred times lower in their cases
compared to this thesis. A first explanation can be the difference of emission rates. It was
difficult to compare their values with the one used in this report since they use different
emission rates for the different materials whereas in this thesis a general value was set per
m2 of floor area. Overall their emission rates seem to be five to ten times lower than the
one used in this thesis which can partly explain the low DALYs. This reinforces the need of
creating an agreed method to model the formaldehyde sources. The second explanation
to the high differences in DALYs values can be that the values of the airflow rates in their
simulations might be higher than for this thesis which would explain a better removal of
the pollutants like formaldehyde and thus lower values for the DALYs. However, since
the values used were not clearly stated in their paper, this is more a conjecture and this
is the main reason why the comparison between their system and the one used in this
thesis was complicated. Finally, another explanation could be that, in the Annex 86, and
therefore in this thesis, a simplified effect factor was used in the DALYs calculation (see
eq. (3.2)) whereas De Jonge and Laverge used eq. (4.3)

5
Conclusion
The main objective of this thesis was to select a set of performance­based indicators
adapted to the Danish context and needs to assess the IAQ provided by smart ventilation
systems. The literature review highlighted the pollutants of concern at an international
and Danish scale and the following were kept: CO2, RH, formaldehyde and PM2.5. A
set of indicators developed by the IEA­EBC Annex 86 was used as a basis and some
of them were modified after use to obtain the final set composed of 10 indicators. To
demonstrate their usability, they were tested on CONTAM simulation results to compare
a constant airflow ventilation system with a humidity controlled one. The simulations were
performed with a floor plan from a typical Danish apartment and the occupancy schedules
and pollutants emission scenarios were created based on relevant literature and adapted
to the Danish context.
The ventilation systems were first compared by normalizing all the indicators according to
the reference case which was the CAV system here. This allowed to draw some conclu­
sions with the humidity­controlled system performing better for ECO2 and ERHr and the
CAV system performing better for EHCHOacute and ERHo. Moreover, using the indicators
to compare the results with and without the kitchen hood highlighted the importance of this
modelling choice and provided a better insight on how it was affecting the IAQ by really
differentiating short­ and long­term exposure indicators. However, even if the indicators
were proven to be useful to compare different systems between them, it can be hard to
determine if the one compared are actually providing a good IAQ for the occupants. To
cope with this issue, thresholds were set for each indicators and both systems were com­
pared using them as the reference. The results show that none of the systems actually
provided an acceptable IAQ for the occupants with the PM2.5 and formaldehyde indica­
tors being two to three times higher than the thresholds. This highlighted the importance
of having agreed thresholds or a reference system that is known to provide an acceptable
IAQ to assess the performance of other ventilation systems.
In conclusion, the indicators were easy to use on the simulation results and allowed to
detect the strength and weaknesses of both ventilation strategies as well as the areas of
improvement regarding the inputs used in the simulations.

6
Further work
As explained above, the comparison of the ventilation systems by using thresholds was
begun at the end of the thesis. Therefore, even if the values chosen are based on relevant
literature, an extensive research could be conducted by investigating more literature or
performing real­life measurements. Implementing agreed thresholds would make the use
of these indicators more reliable to decide if a new smart ventilation system should be put
on the market, for example.
Regarding the modelling choices, two areas of research have been identified. The first
one is the modelling of the formaldehyde emission rate that was simplified as a constant
one in this thesis and that lead to results that were not in accordance with the chosen
model. To tackle this issue, research should be conducted to be able to implement more
realistic emission rate scenarios in CONTAM by taking into account the variations due to
air change rate and absolute humidity. The second line of research regarding modelling
choices concerns the kitchen hood. In fact, in this thesis, a capture efficiency of 75%
was assumed and only 25% of the cooking emissions were actually emitted into the zone
air. The results obtained by comparing the scenarios with and without the kitchen hood
highlighted the importance of modelling it. However, as this was an idealized way of
modelling this system, extensive research should be conducted on the kitchen hoods
available on the market to better understand their operation and be able to perform a
more realistic modelling.
Concerning the DALYs indicator, it has been discussed earlier that this can be a useful
tool to assess the harm caused by the indoor air on the occupants. However, this method
is still in development and the comparison between the different studies are complicated.
There is therefore a need to do more research on the topic to develop a common method
to calculate this indicator and be able to use it to assess ventilation systems.
Finally, the indicators were tested only on simulation results here but their use could be
extended to assess ventilation systems that are already in operation by using real­life
measurements. As most of the indicators use the exposure concentrations of each oc­
cupant this would probably be more challenging with real­life measurements. In fact, it is
easy to follow the exposure concentrations of an occupant with CONTAM but, in real­life,
it would imply to have the sensors carried by the occupants all day to follow them. A
thorough study could therefore be conducted to adapt the use of these indicators to the
challenges of real­life measurements.
