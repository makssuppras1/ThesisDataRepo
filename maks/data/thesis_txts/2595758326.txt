Abstract
With the recent discovery of the two-dimensional ferromagnetic material CrI3 there has
been a rise in interest of two-dimensional magnetism. Two dimensional magnetism is in-
herently diﬀerent from three dimensional, as magnetic order can only exist in the presence
of magnetic anisotropy, and thus the anisotropic Heisenberg model is used to describe the
essential physics. The parameters entering in the Heisenberg model are determined using
an energy mapping scheme and density functional theory (DFT). The critical temperatures
are then estimated with classical Monte Carlo simulations. The materials considered are
Janus monolayers, which has a broken inversion symmetry, leading to interesting prop-
erties such as an out-of-plane dipole moment. 54 materials are considered across three
crystal structures, where 14 materials are predicted to exhibit magnetic order of which
three are predicted to be ferromagnetic and 11 anti-ferromagnetic. Furthermore the eﬀect
of Hubbard U correction, in the framework of DFT+U, is studied and found to have a
signiﬁcant impact on the magnetic properties. The U correction expands the list from 14
materials to 20 materials predicted to exhibit magnetic order.

Introduction
Magnetism in two and three dimensions are inherently diﬀerent. Magnetism in three di-
mensions arises from spontaneously breaking rotational symmetry. The Mermin-Wagner
theorem [1] states that no continuous symmetry can be spontaneously broken in two
dimensions, and the rotational symmetry has to be broken explicitly. Therefore two di-
mensional magnetism can only occur if magnetic anisotropy is present, which breaks the
rotational symmetry explicitly.
The interest in two dimensional magnetism has been on the rise ever since the ﬁrst
experimental realisation of CrI3, which exhibits ferromagnetic order with a Curie temper-
ature of 45 K [2]. Two dimensional magnets have the potential to be used for numerous
application, e.g. magnetic skyrmions that can be used in magnetic storage, and are pre-
dicted to exist in serveral Janus monolayers[3][4]. Furthermore two-dimensional magnets
can also be used in spintronics [5]. To be able to use two-dimensional magnets in devices
they need to be magnetic at higher temperatures, ideally above room temperature. There-
fore there is a need to ﬁnd materials with critical temperatures above room temperature.
The materials investigated in this work are Janus monolayers. Janus monolayers are
named after the Greek god with two faces, one pointing to the past and one to future.
Like the Greek god Janus monolayers have two faces. They consist of a layer of metal
atoms sandwiched between two diﬀerent layers of halogens. The Janus monolayers have
broken inversion symmetry, which leads to many interesting properties [6]. 54 diﬀerent
monolayers are investigated across three crystal structures, namely the transition metal
dichalcogenides (TMD) H-phase, TMD T-phase and the CrI3 prototypes. The metal atoms
selected are Fe, V, Mn, Co, Cr, and Ni, and the three halogens are I, Br, and Cl. All the
monolayers are on the form MXY, where M is the metal atoms and X, Y is the halogens
with X ̸= Y.
The anisotropic Heisenberg model is used to describe the most essential physics. From
spin-wave theory of the anisotropic Heisenberg model, a criterion can be derived for which
magnetic order can exist, namely the spin-wave gap. The spin-wave gap is the lowest
excitation energy of the spin-wave spectrum and has to be positive for magnetic order
to exist. When performing the screening of the materials it can be used as a descriptor
for magnetic order. The parameters entering in the anisotropic Heisenberg model can be
determined by combining a classical energy mapping scheme and density functional theory
(DFT) total energies calculations. Afterwards comes the non-trivial task of solving the
anisotropic Heisenberg model, which cannot be done analytically. Thus classical Monte
Carlo simulations are used to estimate critical temperatures.

Spin-orbit coupling and magnetism
2.1
Spin-Orbit coupling
The spin-orbit coupling (SOC) is a relativistic eﬀect that arises in solid state systems. In
a semiclassical picture the SOC can be understood by taking the reference frame of the
electron, where the nucleus moves around the electron in a loop. As the nucleus is charged
it induces a magnetic ﬁeld that the electron feels through the Zeeman interaction. The
SOC Hamiltonian can be written as
H = λL · S,
(1)
where L is the orbital angular momentum and S is the spin angular momentum [7]. λ is
the coupling strength of the SOC, which can be shown to scale with the atomic number
as Z4, and thus the SOC is larger for heavier atoms.
2.2
Exchange interactions
Exchange interactions arise as a consequence of the Pauli exclusion principle and Coulomb
interactions [7]. The Pauli exclusion principle forbids two electrons to occupy the same
quantum state. Therefore if the two electrons are located in the same orbital they must
have opposite spin, thus anti-aligning their spin magnetic moments. The electrons can
also minimise their coulomb energy and occupy diﬀerent orbitals and have the same spin.
This competition between Pauli exclusion and Coulomb interactions determines whether
the electrons prefer to align their spins or anti-align their spins, i.e. being ferromagnetic
or anti-ferromagnetic. The exchange interactions can be described by the Hamiltonian

2.3

Anisotropy is essential for magnetism in two dimensions. Anisotropy means that the spins
have a preferred axis to align with called the easy axis.
Single ion anisotropy is a consequence of charges in the orbitals, which carry magnetic
moments, interacting with the potential set up by the atomic sites of the crystal lattice.
The crystal ﬁeld quenches the orbitals along a speciﬁc crystal directions. Due to the SOC,
the spins want to be aligned with the orbital angular moment, and thus the spins aligned

Spin-wave theory and the Mermin-Wagner Theorm
5.1
Mermin-Wagner Theorem
It was long believed that magnetic order could not exist in two dimensions. This fact comes
from the Mermin-Wagner theorem [1], which states that no continuous symmetry can be
spontaneously broken in two dimensions, and broken rotational symmetry is required for
magnetic order. However there is a loop hole, namely anisotropy. Anisotropy can break
the rotational symmetry explicitly and therefore it is not required spontaneously to be
broken and magnetic order can arise. The anisotropy has to be out-of-plane because this
fully breaks the rotational symmetry.
If the anisotropy is in-plane there is rotational
symmetry in-plane, and there is an easy-plane instead of an easy-axis.
The magnetic order parameter is the magnetisation, and when it goes to zero mag-
netic order is lost. Spin-wave excitations, i.e. magnons, of the system will decrease the
magnetisation. The number of magnons can be shown to diverge in two dimensions, if the
spectrum is not gapped at T > 0. The number of magnons is given by

and the magnons will decrease magnetisation and thus magnetic order is lost. If there is
a gap in the magnon spectrum the limits of the integral changes and it does not diverge,
and magnetic order can exist. It turns out that the magnon gap arises from anisotropy
both in the ferromagnetic and anti-ferromagnetic case. The expression for the spin-wave
gap can be derived from spin-wave theory.

number. If (2S −1)A > NnnSB the spin-wave gap is real and positive, it is negative if
(2S −1)A < NnnS(2J + B), and otherwise imaginary. If the anti-ferromagnetic spin-wave
gap is negative the system will have ferromagnetic order, which is possible if B is large
even with J < 0, and if it is imaginary the spins will be in-plane. Only in the case of a
positive and real spin-wave gap does it correspond to a physical gap. It is not possible for
single ion anisotropy to open a spin-wave gap for S = 1/2, and anisotropic exchange is
required.

Density functional theory
Density functional theory (DFT) is a tool used to solve the many-body electronic problem
from ﬁrst principles. The main principles is that instead of using wavefunctions the elec-
tron density n(r) is used to describe the electronic structure [14]. This greatly reduces the
degrees of freedom of the problem. DFT provides a good comprise between accuracy and
computational expenses. However strongly correlated phenomena cannot usually be cap-
tured by DFT. Magnetism is an example of a strongly correlated phenomena and therefore
the Heisenberg model is used to describe physics. DFT is good method to calculate total
energies, which can used to determine the Heisenberg parameters as described in chapter
4.

Workﬂow
In order to easily screen a large number of materials a workﬂow has been made. The
workﬂow ensures that all the calculations are done with the same code and parameters to
make the calculations consistent. The workﬂow used to determine the magnetic properties
of the Janus monolayers is seen in ﬁgure 11. The ﬁrst step is the generate all the magnetic
states of the monolayers and relax the structures. The structures are relaxed until the
maximum forces on the atoms are less than 0.01 eV/˚A. For relaxing a k-point density of 6
˚A−1, a plane wave cut oﬀof 800 eV, and a Fermi smearing of 0.05 eV is used. For systems
with convergence issues, a k-point density of 9 ˚A−1 is used.
Next the ground state is calculated. For the ground state calculation, a k-point density
of 12 ˚A−1, and the same Fermi smearing and plane wave cut oﬀas for relaxing, is used.
From the ground state calculation a number of properties can be determined, such as the
out-of-plane dipole moment and band gap.
The heat of formation, ∆H, describes the thermodynamic stability. The heat of for-
mation is the energy of the material with respect to the standard states of elements of the
material, which is given by [19]

disintegrate into its constituent elements or remain in the given structure. Furthermore

Hubbard U corrections
The entire workﬂow has been run again on the Janus monolayers but with a Hubbard
U correction. The values of the U corrections are found in chapter 7. By including the
Hubbard U some materials, otherwise predicted to be metallic, opens a band gap. Starting
from the 54 materials 38 materials are predicted to thermodynamic stable and insulators.
This is an increase of 14 possible magnetic materials compared to not including a Hubbard
U. The Heisenberg parameters are calculated and illustrated in ﬁgure 15. The full list of
parameters calculated with a Hubbard U correction are found in appendix A.

CrI3 prototype
The Hubbard U correction increases the exchange couplings J of the Janus monolayers
containing chromium, which leads to an increase in Curie temperature of 8-9 K. The Hub-
bard U closes the spin-wave gap of Cr2Cl3I3 and thus completely changes the prediction
of magnetic order.
Likewise is there an increase in the exchange couplings of the anti-ferromagnetic mate-
rials containing iron. The weaker exchange couplings results in a decrease of the Neel tem-

Longer range exchange interactions corrections to the critical tem-
perature
The above results are based on the assumption that only the nearest neighbour couplings
contribute signiﬁcantly to the critical temperatures. To see if the assumption is justiﬁed
the exchange couplings up to third nearest neighbours are calculated for two materials
and Monte Carlo simulations are made to see the corrections to the critical temperatures.
The calculation of the longer range exchange couplings is discussed in chapter 4.1.
10.5.1
Cr2Br3I3
For Cr2Br3I3 the isotropic exchange coupling has been calculated up to third nearest neigh-
bour interactions as described in chapter 4.1. The exchange couplings are J1 = 2.64, J2 =
0.85, and J3 = −0.06. The exchange coupling decreases with longer range interactions,
and as the third nearest neighbour is two orders of magnitude lower than the nearest
neighbour coupling, longer range interactions should be negligible. The next nearest ex-
change interactions is about a third of the nearest neighbour interaction, but as Cr2Br3I3
has a honeycomb magnetic lattice there are twice as many next nearest neighbours. Fig-
ure 16 shows the heat capacity as a function of temperature for Cr2Br3I3 with ﬁrst and
up to third nearest neighbours exchange interactions. By including up to third nearest
neighbours exchange interactions, there is a signiﬁcant increase in the Curie temperature,
namely from 28 K to 72 K, which is more than a doubling in the critical temperature.

anisotropy. It is not clear why there is no clear correlation between the anisotropy and

Discussion
The parameters entering in the anisotropic Heisenberg model are obviously crucial for the
predicted properties of the materials. The energy mapping used to determine the param-
eters is based on total energy calculations obtained from DFT and a classical mapping of
the Heisenberg model. The total energy calculations is at T = 0K , where the quantum
eﬀects probably play an important role, and the classical energy mapping cannot captures
these eﬀects. Furthermore the total energies also depend on which exchange-correlation
functional is used in the DFT calculation, which will inﬂuence the calculated Heisenberg
parameters.
The inclusion of a Hubbard U term penalise localisation of the electrons and can
provide correlations not captured by the PBE exchange-correlation functional. As seen
in section 10.4, it can quantitatively change the prediction of the magnetic properties of
a material. The Hubbard U can close and open spin-wave gaps changing the predicted
magnetic properties. In general it appears that the exchange coupling J increases with
U. Thus anti-ferromagnetic materials tend to lower Neel temperatures with larger U, and
for ferromagnetic materials they tend to higher Curie temperatures. However there are
exceptions where the exchange coupling decreases with U. In this work the values of U
is taken from ref [17], but in general the value of U cannot easily be evaluated for the
individual systems, and the predictions rely heavily on the value chosen for U.
One of the motivations for examining Janus monolayers was that the anisotropy would
correlate to the dipole moment and in turn lead to increased critical temperatures. Al-
though the correlation is only clear for the TMD H-phase, for the sake of the argument
lets assume the dipole moment and anisotropy are correlated. As the anisotropy is also
dependent on SOC, which is larger for heavier atoms, changing a layer of heavier atoms
to lighter atoms to induce the dipole moment will lead to a weaker SOC. Thus a compe-
tition between the strength of the SOC and dipole moment can have a inﬂuence on the
anisotropy. This argument is based on the assumption that the correlation is present, and
in the case of CrI3 prototype, where the correlation seems not to be present, there is not
an increase in the Curie temperatures of the Janus monolayers Cr2X3Y3 compared to the
Cr2X6 monolayers. Speciﬁcally there is a decrease in the Curie temperatures of the Cr2X6
if one of the faces is switched with a layer of lighter atoms and vice versa if a layer is
switched with heavier atoms, which might be solely due to SOC. In this case the Janus
monolayers does not exceed the Curie temperature of CrI3. In general there is not an
increase in critical temperature of Janus structures compared to the reference systems.
Illustrated by the examples of Cr2Br3I3 and Mn2Br2Cl3 it is clear that the assumption
of only including nearest neighbours exchange interaction is not valid. The calculation
showed a correction of over a factor two to the critical temperature by including longer
range interactions. However the longer range interactions makes screening much more
computationally demanding.
The screening including only nearest neighbours interac-
tions can be used to make a rough screening of many materials, and the most interesting
materials can then be further investigated by including longer range interactions.

Conclusion
A computational screening of 54 Janus monolayers in the search for magnetic two-dimensional
insulators is carried out. The materials are mapped onto the anisotropic Heisenberg model,
as anisotropy is crucial for two-dimensional magnetism. The Heisenberg parameters are
calculated using a combination of total energy calculations from DFT and a classical en-
ergy mapping of the Heisenberg model. The spin-wave gap can be expressed in terms of
the Heisenberg parameters, using spin-wave theory, which has to be positive for magnetic
order to exist. The spin-wave gap is thus used as a descriptor for magnetic order. The
critical temperatures are then calculated for materials, with a positive spin-wave gap, us-
ing classical Monte Carlo simulations, which was shown to be in a good agreement with
exact diagonalisation.
Without a Hubbard U term, 14 materials were predicted to host magnetic order.
Three materials order ferromagnetically and the rest anti-ferromagnetically, with critical
temperatures ranging from 13 K to 200 K. In general the Janus structures does not improve
the critical temperatures compared to the reference systems. The reference system V2Br4
was found to have a Neel temperature of 315 K thus exceeding room temperature.
The eﬀects of inclusion of a Hubbard U term through DFT+U on the Heisenberg
parameters was examined. The inclusion of a Hubbard U opened band gaps for several of
materials extending the list of magnetic insulators from 14 to 20. In general the Hubbard
U seems increase the Curie temperatures of ferromagnetic materials, and decrease the
Neel temperature of anti-ferromagnetic materials. With the Hubbard U correction anti-
ferromagnetic three materials are predicted to exceed room temperature, making them
ideal candidates for technical applications. It was further found that longer range exchange
interactions than nearest neighbours are important for the critical temperatures and should
be included for an accurate prediction of the critical temperature.
