Abstract
Event sourcing is a powerful architectural pattern that enables auditability, traceability,
and replayability by recording state changes as events. However, its adoption in small
companies remains limited due to technical complexity, steep learning curves, and in-
frastructure demands. This thesis investigates these barriers and explores how event
sourcing can be simplified without compromising its core benefits.
Using the Design Science Research methodology, a simplified method and lightweight
framework were developed for small company contexts. Key adaptations include a CRUD-
based repository abstraction, synchronous execution, minimal setup, and built-in support
for projections, event replay, and personal data management. The artifacts were de-
veloped, demonstrated, and evaluated through a real-world case study involving a Spot
Quote application at CON-LINQ.
Results show that the approach significantly reduces perceived complexity and improves
developer usability, as reflected in a high SUS score of 89. Core benefits of event sourcing
were preserved and found directly valuable in practice. This thesis contributes both a prac-
tical artifact and actionable design knowledge for adapting event sourcing to constrained
environments. While the solution improves accessibility, it introduces trade-offs, such as
reduced scalability and limited domain expressiveness, that limit its suitability for more
complex or large-scale systems.

Introduction
Event sourcing captures all changes to an application’s state as a sequence of immutable
events [1]. Each event represents a meaningful state change, and the chronological log
of these events becomes the system’s single source of truth, as it contains all necessary
information to reconstruct the current state from the sequence [2]. Unlike traditional state-
based approaches that store only the current state of data, event sourcing preserves the
full history of changes [3].
Event sourcing has emerged as a powerful architectural pattern in modern software de-
velopment and is increasing in popularity [4] (p. 1), offering significant advantages for sys-
tems that require observability and replayability, debugging and diagnostics, and scalabil-
ity [5] (p. 382-384). This approach also unlocks new possibilities for resilience, improved
auditing, and reliability [5] (p. 385-386) [6].
1.1
Problem Context
Event sourcing has found widespread adoption in complex, large-scale systems and en-
terprises such as Netflix and Walmart where its benefits of reliability, flexibility, and scalab-
ility justify its architectural overhead [4] (p. 1). As Overeem explains, ”Event sourcing is
applied in enterprise applications (...). This is in accordance with the community from
which event sourcing originated, which focuses on enterprise applications.” [4] (p. 4–5).
However, despite these proven advantages in enterprise contexts, adoption in small com-
panies appears to be limited. Small companies often operate with constrained resources [7],
smaller development teams, and simpler system requirements compared to large enter-
prises. These constraints can create a challenging environment for adopting architectural
patterns like event sourcing, because it is frequently associated with increased complexity,
a steep learning curve, and architectural overhead [4] (p. 1, 9-10) , which may discourage
smaller teams from implementing event sourcing.
1.2
Research Gap
While event sourcing is widely adopted in large-scale, enterprise systems [4] (p. 1, 4),
its adoption in small companies remains rare and underexplored. Existing literature and
tools largely focus on complex, distributed architectures, often assuming access to spe-
cialized development teams, robust infrastructure, and support for microservices. These
assumptions rarely align with the realities of smaller companies, which typically operate
with limited resources [7], limited personnel, and simpler system requirements.
Overeem et al. highlight that “event sourcing can be applied in systems of any size: both
smaller and larger systems benefit from the pattern” [4] (p. 14), yet they also observe that
“there is little academic material available when it comes to the topic of event sourcing” [4]
(p. 2). This signals both an opportunity and a gap in understanding how event sourcing
can be practically and effectively applied in small-scale contexts.
In addition, literature often emphasizes event sourcing’s complexity, citing steep learning
curves, the need for architectural rethinking, and challenges with concepts like immutable
logs and eventual consistency [5] (p. 384-385). These technical hurdles can discourage
small teams from even considering event sourcing, contributing to its limited adoption
despite its potential benefits.

An important gap remains in understanding the specific barriers that prevent small com-
panies from adopting event sourcing, and in identifying practical methods to reduce its
complexity. Addressing this gap requires research into how event sourcing can be ad-
apted, simplified, and made more accessible for smaller teams without compromising its
core benefits.
1.3
Problem Statement
Event sourcing is becoming increasingly popular, particularly in large-scale systems, due
to its benefits such as auditability and replayability. Yet, its adoption among small com-
panies remains limited. This suggests that certain barriers, such as technical complexity,
steep learning curves, and a lack of tailored guidance, are preventing adoption, even
though the approach may hold substantial value. These barriers are not well understood,
and without identifying and addressing them in the context of resource-constrained teams,
event sourcing is likely to remain inaccessible to smaller organizations.
1.4
Research Aim and Objectives
The aim of this thesis is to understand the barriers that prevent small companies from
adopting event sourcing, and to explore how the approach can be simplified to better
suit their needs. This involves the development and evaluation of a tailored method and
lightweight framework that preserve core benefits while reducing complexity.
The research objectives are:
1. To investigate why event sourcing remains rarely adopted in small companies, identi-
fying the specific technical, organizational, and perceptual barriers they face.
2. To explore how event sourcing can be adapted to fit small-company contexts without
losing its core benefits.
3. To design a simplified method and lightweight framework based on the identified
needs and constraints.
4. To demonstrate the practical use of the proposed method and framework through a
real-world case study.
5. To evaluate the method and framework in terms of implementation effort, usability,
and their ability to deliver core event sourcing advantages such as auditability and
replayability.
1.5
Research Questions
To address the aim and objectives, the research is guided by the following questions:
1. What barriers prevent small companies from adopting event sourcing in practice?
2. In what ways do small company characteristics affect the perceived complexity and
applicability of event sourcing?
3. What simplifications or adaptations can make event sourcing more accessible to
small companies without sacrificing core benefits?
4. How does the proposed method and framework perform when applied in a real-world
small company context?
5. To what extent does the proposed method and framework reduce complexity and
increase usability while preserving key event sourcing benefits such as auditability
and replayability?

Literature Review on Event Sourcing
This chapter reviews the concept of event sourcing, focusing on its core principles, be-
nefits, and challenges. It explores how event sourcing differs from traditional state man-
agement approaches. Related patterns such as CQRS and event-driven architecture are
also discussed to situate event sourcing within modern software architecture. Finally, the
chapter evaluates existing frameworks and tools, highlighting their strengths and limita-
tions.
2.1
Introduction to Event Sourcing
This section defines what event sourcing means in the context of this thesis. The descrip-
tion presented here is synthesized from key perspectives in the literature (Section 2.2)
including foundational contributions from Fowler, Young, Kleppmann, Overeem, and oth-
ers, and serves as the conceptual basis for all subsequent discussion and analysis.
Event sourcing is an architectural pattern in which all changes to an application’s state
are recorded as a sequence of immutable events. Unlike traditional CRUD-based sys-
tems, which overwrite the current state in a database, event sourcing treats each state
change as a first-class, append-only event that is persisted chronologically in an event
log. These events serve as the single source of truth and reflect what actually happened
in the system.
This model introduces a fundamentally different approach to state management. Rather
than storing only the latest state, the system reconstructs current state dynamically by
replaying past events. As such, the state becomes a derived concept, not a stored arti-
fact. This separation between events (what happened) and projections (how it looks now)
aligns with modern demands for transparency and traceability.
This approach enables a range of powerful capabilities that are difficult or impossible
to achieve reliably in conventional systems. Most notably, it provides a complete and
provable audit trail, supports precise state reconstruction at any point in time, and enables
advanced debugging techniques such as time-travel debugging. For example, if a bug is
reported, developers can replay the exact series of events that led to the issue, recreate
the exact conditions, and identify the root cause with confidence.
Additionally, event sourcing supports decoupled components through projections, and
flexible system evolution. As business requirements change, new read models or views
can be introduced without modifying the original event data. This makes the system in-
herently extensible and adaptable.
These advantages, however, come at a cost. Event sourcing introduces complexity in
areas such as event modeling, schema evolution, consistency management, and per-
formance optimization. Systems must be carefully designed to handle the growth of event
logs, ensure backward compatibility, and manage eventual consistency, particularly in
distributed environments.
The following sections explore the foundational concepts of event sourcing, its practical
benefits, common challenges, and its relationship to related patterns. Together, they offer
a comprehensive view of how event sourcing reshapes state management in modern
software systems.

2.1.1
Core Concepts
Event sourcing is built upon a small number of powerful concepts that together reshape
how application state is managed and understood. These concepts include events, the
event store, event replay, snapshots, and projections. Together, they enable a shift from
storing current state to persisting the history of state changes.
Events
At the heart of event sourcing is the event. An event represents a domain-specific fact
that describes something that has occurred within the system. These are not technical
updates like ”row updated” but meaningful business actions such as QuoteCreated, Ship-
mentUpdated, or BookingDeleted.
Events are immutable and append-only. Once written, they are never modified or deleted.
This immutability guarantees that the full history of state changes is preserved, enabling
auditability, traceability, and system reconstruction.
Entities
In this thesis, an entity refers to an event-sourced aggregate, a domain object. Examples
of such entities might include core business concepts like Quote, Shipment, or Booking.
They encapsulate identity, state, and behavior relevant to a specific bounded context.
Entity state is constructed from events.
Event Store (Event Log)
Events are persisted in an event store, a central component that functions as an append-
only log. Unlike traditional databases that capture only the current state, the event store
captures the full timeline of state transitions. It acts as the single source of truth from
which all derived views or states are computed.
Event Replay
Since the current state of an entity is not stored directly in event-sourced systems, it
must be reconstructed by replaying the full sequence of events associated with that entity.
This involves loading all recorded events for a specific aggregate and applying them in
chronological order to derive its most recent state.
Event replay is not only essential for recovering application state but also unlocks capab-
ilities such as time-travel debugging. By replaying events up to a specific point in time,
developers can recreate the exact conditions under which an issue occurred. This allows
for precise bug reproduction and diagnosis, even long after the original incident, without
relying on fragile logs or guesswork.
Projections
Projections, also known as read models, transform raw events into queryable views.
While the event store is optimized for recording events, projections are optimized for
application-level querying. Projections allow different perspectives on the same data,
enabling the introduction of new business views without rewriting the event model, and
decoupling of read and write concerns.
Snapshots
A snapshot is a saved point-in-time representation of the system. Rather than replaying
thousands of events, the system can load the most recent snapshot and apply only the
newer events. This significantly reduces load times and improves runtime efficiency.
Snapshots are optional and are typically created periodically (e.g., every 1,000,000 events)
or on demand. Importantly, they do not replace events, they complement them for per-
formance purposes.

Immutability and Truthfulness
At the core of event sourcing lies the principle of immutability. Events are not just log
entries, they are immutable records of what actually happened in the system. Once an
event is recorded, it cannot be modified or deleted. This immutability turns the event log
into a verifiable and incorruptible source of truth.
Such a foundation makes the system inherently more transparent and trustworthy, qual-
ities that are essential in domains requiring auditability, regulatory compliance, and his-
torical accountability. However, to uphold this guarantee of truthfulness, careful consid-
eration must be given to how the event store is implemented and physically secured.
Factors such as storage integrity, access control, and tamper-proof mechanisms play a
critical role in preserving the reliability of the event log over time. These concerns lie
beyond the scope of this thesis.
2.1.2
How It Works
Based on a synthesis of multiple sources discussing event sourcing from different per-
spectives [1, 3, 8, 9, 10], this section outlines a structured approach to event sourcing,
which involves capturing, storing, and processing events to dynamically reconstruct ap-
plication state. A full implementation example is provided in Chapter 5.
The process begins by capturing domain events. Whenever an action occurs that alters
state, a corresponding event is generated to describe the change. These are domain-
specific and reflect business operations, such as OrderPlaced or PaymentProcessed,
rather than low-level database operations. Each event includes a unique identifier, time-
stamp, and relevant payload, forming an accurate and immutable record of what occurred.
This log of events can later be replayed to reconstruct system behavior at any point in time.
Once generated, events are stored in an event log. Unlike traditional databases that store
only the current state, the event store retains all state changes as immutable, append-only
records. Events are stored in chronological order, ensuring nothing is lost or overwritten
and supporting full reconstruction of past states.
Event storage must ensure both immutability and long-term scalability. Since events can-
not be changed or deleted, the system must accommodate schema evolution, commonly
handled through versioning, where older formats are adapted at replay time. To manage
large event volumes, strategies such as compaction and archival can be applied.
Finally, application state is reconstructed by processing events. Instead of retrieving state
directly from a database table, the system replays all events related to an entity to rebuild
its latest state. For example, an order might be reconstructed by applying OrderPlaced,
PaymentProcessed, and OrderShipped events in sequence.
To avoid performance issues when many events exist, snapshots can be introduced to
capture the entity state at a specific point in time, so only newer events must be replayed.
Additionally, projections transform raw event data into optimized queryable views. These
enable efficient access to frequently needed data without reprocessing the events.
2.1.3
Design Characteristics
Event sourcing lies at the intersection of design and architectural patterns. As Gupta
explains [11], design patterns solve recurring problems at the component or module level,
while architectural patterns shape the overall structure of an application, defining how
components interact and manage data system-wide.
At first glance, event sourcing resembles a design pattern. It prescribes how state changes
are captured as immutable events, stored, and replayed to reconstruct current state. This

often within a single service or bounded context.
However, when applied more broadly, event sourcing assumes architectural significance.
It defines how data is persisted and state derived, how failures are managed, and how
auditability and observability are achieved. The central use of an append-only event log
influences infrastructure and data flow.
In this thesis, event sourcing is used as the primary mechanism for state management.
Because it shapes system-wide storage and query behavior, it is treated as an architec-
tural pattern within this work.
2.2
Perspectives from Literature
Event sourcing has been conceptualized in various ways by practitioners and research-
ers, each highlighting unique aspects of its design, benefits, and challenges. This section
synthesizes foundational perspectives to explain how the pattern is understood in liter-
ature and practice. These perspectives form the conceptual foundation for how event
sourcing is interpreted in this thesis.
2.2.1
Formal Descriptions and Historical Context
One of the earliest and most widely cited formal descriptions of event sourcing comes
from Martin Fowler, who defines it as a pattern that “ensures that all changes to application
state are stored as a sequence of events” [1]. Fowler emphasizes auditability, debugging,
and scalability as key advantages. By storing a complete log of state transitions rather
than only the latest state, systems gain the ability to analyze past behavior and reproduce
application state with precision.
Greg Young, a central figure in promoting event sourcing, emphasizes that the core ideas
behind the pattern are not new. In a 2014 talk, he traces the concept back to mainframe
computing. He argues that recording and replaying events to reconstruct state has long
existed and remains foundational to accounting and banking systems [12].
Young defines event sourcing as a model in which every state transition is treated as
an immutable fact. Events are stored chronologically and serve as the system’s single
source of truth, with state reconstructed on demand. He emphasizes that current state is
merely a derived view, transient and disposable, while the event log represents the real,
permanent record. As he puts it, “every state transition inside of your domain… should
be a first-class concept inside of your system” [12].
2.2.2
Auditability, Traceability, and Information Preservation
Young underscores that a major benefit of event sourcing is its ability to retain complete
historical information. This allows capabilities such as retroactive reporting, replaying
system behavior, and reproducing bugs by time traveling to a specific moment. In his
view, event sourcing uniquely avoids information loss entirely [12].
To highlight the limitations of traditional audit logging, Young uses the tale of Hansel and
Gretel as an analogy. In the story, the children attempt to find their way back by dropping
breadcrumbs, an improvised audit trail. However, animals eat the crumbs, making the trail
unreliable and ultimately leading to their misfortune. Young suggests that an unreliable
audit log can be worse than none at all, as it creates a false sense of traceability. In
contrast, event sourcing integrates the audit trail directly into the system’s core model,
making it verifiable and tamper-proof [12].

Microsoft’s description aligns with this perspective, framing event sourcing as a pattern
that ensures every state change is explicitly captured. This allows precise reconstruction

Kurrent, a company specializing in event-native data platforms, similarly defines event

2.2.3
Enterprise Value and Real-World Use
In his landmark book, Designing Data-Intensive Applications, Kleppmann presents event
sourcing as a powerful tool for building scalable, auditable, and fault-tolerant systems.
He highlights how the pattern supports accurate recovery and auditability, particularly in
environments with complex data flows and regulatory constraints [14] (ch. 11).
2.2.4
Practical Challenges and Limitations
Despite these strengths, event sourcing also introduces technical and organizational chal-
lenges. Overeem et al. highlight difficulties related to steep learning curve, schema evol-
ution, projection rebuilding, data privacy, and the general lack of mature tools [4] (p. 1).
Richman adds that managing the ever-growing event log requires careful infrastructure
planning.
Handling versioning, ensuring consistency across distributed systems, and
building efficient replay mechanisms are non-trivial engineering tasks [2]. However, Young
offers a counterpoint to concerns about log growth. He argues that the size of the event log
is not inherently problematic, so long as storage demands scale below the rate predicted
by Moore’s Law. In other words, if the rate of data generation stays within the bounds of
expected hardware improvements, the accumulation of events remains manageable over
time [12].
2.2.5
Conceptual Summary
Collectively, these perspectives emphasize that event sourcing is not merely a storage
strategy but a fundamental rethinking of how state is modeled, derived, and preserved.
Its core principles, immutability, auditability, and replayability, make it particularly suited
to domains requiring precision and transparency. However, the pattern is complex and
requires thoughtful implementation. This thesis adopts the view that event sourcing is
best understood as an architectural pattern where changes to the state of an application
are captured as a sequence of immutable events.
2.2.6
Benefits of Event Sourcing
Event sourcing has been widely recognized as a powerful architectural pattern, primarily
for five reasons: auditability, flexibility, scalability, development of complex systems, and
its growing adoption trend [4] (p. 5). While various sources highlight different benefits of
event sourcing, Malyi and Serdyuk conducted an in-depth analysis of its advantages and
challenges. Their study systematically identifies seven key benefits, supported by multiple
references, consolidating common themes across existing literature [5] (p. 382-384):
1. Observability and Replayability: Event sourcing ensures that all state changes
are recorded in an immutable log. This allows for complete observability of how
the system reached its current state. Developers can replay historical events to
reconstruct past states or diagnose issues efficiently [5] (p. 383).
2. Debugging and Diagnostics:
The ability to trace events back to their origins simplifies debugging. Developers
8

==PAGE:17==
can pinpoint the root causes of issues by examining the sequence of events that led
to a problem. This time-travel debugging allows for accurate problem reproduction
[5] (p. 383).
3. Reliability:
Since events are immutable, they cannot be altered once stored. This provides a
single source of truth, ensuring data consistency and reducing the risk of corruption.
The event log serves as a robust mechanism for maintaining system reliability [5]
(p. 383).
4. Scalability:
By separating event storage, processing, and state projection, event sourcing allows
different components to scale independently [5] (p. 383).
5. Multiple Views on Data:
Event logs enable the creation of multiple read models optimized for specific queries.
This decoupling of the read side from the write side allows different perspectives on
the same data without affecting the primary event store [5] (p. 383).
6. Extensibility:
Developers can introduce new features or modify existing ones with minimal impact
on the system. New read models or projections can be created without altering the
core event store, allowing the system to evolve seamlessly over time [5] (p. 383).
7. Decoupling:
Event sourcing promotes a modular architecture by separating command handling,
event storage, and state projections. This decoupling reduces dependencies, al-
lowing independent development, testing, and scaling of system components [5] (p.
384).
2.2.7
Limitation and Challenges of Event Sourcing
While event sourcing offers numerous advantages, it also presents several challenges
that must be addressed to ensure successful adoption. Malyi and Serdyuk identified key
limitations, which are commonly referenced in research and industry experiences. The
main challenges (described in detail below) include the steep learning curve, rebuilding
projections, eventual consistency, dependencies on external systems, and event version-
ing [5] (p. 384-385). Additionally, Overeem et al. highlight two further challenges: the
availability of supporting technology and concerns around data privacy [4] (p. 10-11):
1. Steep Learning Curve:
Adopting event sourcing requires a fundamental shift in mindset from traditional
CRUD-based systems. Developers must become familiar with new architectural
concepts, such as event storage, command handling, and state projection. This
learning process can be time consuming, especially for teams unfamiliar with event-
sourced systems. Additionally, ensuring proper event modeling and aggregation
strategies requires a deep understanding of domain-driven design principles, which
can further delay onboarding and initial productivity [5] (p. 384).
2. Rebuilding Projections:
One of the operational challenges in event sourcing is the need to reconstruct read
models by replaying the entire event log. As the number of stored events increases,
this process can become computationally expensive and slow. Maintaining efficient
projections is particularly challenging when schema changes occur, as updates may
require additional processing to ensure that older events remain compatible with

9
==PAGE:18==
new data structures. Without careful design, these factors can lead to performance
bottlenecks [5] (p. 384).
3. Eventual Consistency:
Unlike traditional transactional databases that enforce strong consistency, event
sourcing often operates on an eventual consistency model. Since event processing
and read model updates happen asynchronously, there may be temporary discrep-
ancies between what is stored in the event log and what is available for querying.
This can introduce complexity in application logic, requiring additional mechanisms
to manage stale data, resolve conflicts, and ensure that users receive accurate in-
formation despite minor delays in data propagation [5] (p. 384). This behavior,
however, is not inherent to event sourcing itself but rather arises from specific imple-
mentation choices, particularly when asynchronous processing is used for updating
read models.
4. Dependencies on External Systems:
Integrating an event sourced system with external dependencies can pose signific-
ant challenges, particularly when those systems do not adhere to event sourcing.
If external data influences event processing, maintaining the ability to replay histor-
ical events may become problematic, as referenced information might change or
become unavailable over time [5] (p. 385).
5. Event Versioning:
As systems evolve, the structure of event types may need to change to support new
features or altered business requirements. However, modifying event type defini-
tions without breaking existing processing logic or read models is a non-trivial task.
Strategies such as upcasting, maintaining multiple versions of event types, or in-
troducing transformation layers must be carefully planned and consistently applied.
Poorly managed versioning can lead to data inconsistencies, increased technical
debt, and significant maintenance overhead [5] (p. 385).
6. Lack of Standardized Technology and Tooling: A significant hurdle is the lack of
mature frameworks and infrastructure support. While event sourcing can be imple-
mented without specialized tools, many practitioners find existing solutions insuffi-
cient [4] (p. 10).
7. Data Privacy and Regulatory Compliance: Regulations like GDPR pose a chal-
lenge to event sourcing’s core principle of retaining all data. Compliance requires
solutions such as event anonymization, selective data removal, or separating per-
sonal information from event storage. These approaches ensure that privacy oblig-
ations are met without compromising system integrity [4] (p. 11).
2.2.8
Mitigating Challenges in Event Sourcing
It might seem that the trade-offs of event sourcing are just as great as its benefits, but some
of these challenges can be effectively mitigated with best practices, ensuring that the
advantages of event sourcing outweigh its challenges. Some key best practices include:
• Event Creation: Designing well-structured and meaningful event types that capture
business intent rather than low-level state changes is essential. Event types should
represent domain-driven changes (e.g., ”OrderShipped” instead of ”StatusUpdated”),
making them easier to understand and maintain [13].
• Snapshots: Periodically saving the current state of entities to avoid replaying a full
event history, improving performance while maintaining historical accuracy [1].

• Versioning Strategies: Using techniques such as schema evolution, and backward-
compatible transformations to handle changes in event structure over time [15].
• Read Model Projections: Creating optimized read model projections to efficiently

• API Gateway: They facilitate integration between event sourced systems and ex-

2.2.9
When to Use Event Sourcing
While event sourcing is not a solution that will fit all, it still provides valuable benefits
despite its challenges. So when should you actually use it and when not to use it?
Other than just looking at the benefits of event sourcing, it can be particularly useful in the
following scenarios:
• High Performance and Scalability Needs: Event sourcing is quite suited for sys-
tems where performance and scalability are top requirements. It can handle high-
load systems efficiently by improving throughput through append-only operations
and decoupled event handlers [3]. However, this is more because of its close rela-
tion with event-driven architecture, which is discussed in Section 2.3.2.
• Systems Requiring Strong Audibility and Traceability: Event sourcing provides
a complete, immutable history of all changes made to the system, which is essential
for audit trails and compliance requirements [6].
• Complex Business Workflows with Event-Driven Requirements: For applica-
tions involving complex business processes, event sourcing facilitates the tracking
of business events and workflows, which improves error traceability and enabling
easy rollback or replay of events [16] (p. 80-81).
• Event-Driven Architectures and Microservices: Event sourcing naturally fits well
with event-driven architectures and microservices, where they need to communicate
asynchronously [2].
• System That Require State Reconstruction: Event sourcing allows the recon-
struction of past states at any point in time, which enables features like state recov-
ery after failures and time travel debugging [1, 13].
• High Fault Tolerance and Recovery Capabilities: Since all events are stored
immutably, systems can recover from failures by replaying events to restore the
state. This enhances fault tolerance after failures [3, 6].
But, generally it is not recommended for simple applications with minimal data complexity,
systems where real-time consistency is mandatory, and when the overhead of managing
event stores outweighs the benefits [3].

teract with it in various architectural contexts. Understanding these relationships provides
a broader perspective on how event sourcing fits within modern software design and how
different approaches can enhance or influence its implementation.

2.3.1
Command Query Responsibility Segregation
CQRS is a design pattern that separates command (write) and query (read) responsibilit-
ies, allowing each to be optimized independently. In contrast to traditional CRUD systems,
which use a single model for both, CQRS avoids performance bottlenecks by decoupling
state modification from data retrieval [8, 9, 17]. The write model processes commands
and enforces business rules, while the read model supports efficient querying.
CQRS pairs naturally with event sourcing, where commands produce events that are
stored and used to update the read model through projections. This enables fast queries,
supports independent scaling, and allows new read models to be added without modifying
core logic. However, maintaining separate models introduces complexity and requires
careful planning.
Together, CQRS and event sourcing offer strong support for systems with complex busi-
ness logic and traceability. While event sourcing defines how state is persisted and re-
constructed, CQRS structures the separation of concerns, ensuring both reads and writes
are handled effectively.
2.3.2
Event-Driven Architecture
EDA structures systems around the generation, transmission, and handling of events.
When meaningful state changes occur, producers emit events that are consumed asyn-
chronously by one or more consumers. This decouples components, enabling scalability,
responsiveness, and loose coupling [2, 18].
EDA consists of producers, consumers, and event channels. Producers trigger events,
which are transmitted to consumers via channels.
Consumers process events using
business logic and may generate new events in turn. A common model include publish-
subscribe with multiple consumers per event.
EDA is common in distributed and real-time systems, such as IoT, finance, analytics, and
microservices, where components must react quickly and independently. Its asynchron-
ous nature improves resilience but also introduces challenges like ensuring message de-
livery and managing eventual consistency.
While often used together, EDA and event sourcing serve different purposes.
Event
sourcing persists all state changes as immutable events, while EDA focuses on how com-
ponents in a system communicate and react to events. However, event-sourced systems
can naturally emit events into an EDA system, bridging the two approaches.
Implementation often relies on message brokers, which decouple producers from con-
sumers and manage delivery guarantees. Brokers support different patterns like publish-
subscribe and queuing, and technologies include RabbitMQ, Apache Kafka, AWS SQS,
and Azure Service Bus [19, 20].
2.3.3
Domain-Driven Design
DDD, introduced by Eric Evans, is a development approach centered on modeling soft-
ware around the business domain. It promotes a shared language between developers
and domain experts and divides complex systems into bounded contexts for manageab-
ility [21, 22].
Core concepts include entities (with unique identities, e.g., customers), value objects (de-
scriptive but identity-less, e.g., addresses), and aggregates (clusters of entities and value
objects treated as a single consistency boundary). The aggregate root controls access
and enforces rules within the aggregate.

DDD aligns naturally with event sourcing, as both emphasize capturing domain beha-
vior and business logic. Supporting patterns include domain services for business logic
outside entities, and repositories for managing aggregate persistence without exposing
internal data structures.
2.3.4
Sagas, 2PC & Non-2PC Transaction Management
In event-driven and event-sourced systems, especially microservices-based ones, tradi-
tional transactional boundaries across services often do not apply. Yet, business pro-
cesses still require coordination to ensure that multiple actions either succeed together
or are compensated if one fails. Strategies like sagas, two-phase commit (2PC), and
non-2PC transaction models offer different approaches with varying trade-offs.
The 2PC protocol provides strong consistency by coordinating a prepare and commit
phase across all participants [23]. However, it introduces tight coupling, blocking be-
havior, and fragility in the face of partial failures, making it unsuitable for event-driven,
asynchronous systems.
Non-2PC approaches, such as the LEAP protocol, avoid global coordination by keeping
related data local to one system [24]. This improves performance and fault tolerance but
sacrifices immediate consistency and requires upfront knowledge of transactional bound-
aries.
Sagas offer a more natural fit with event sourcing. They model distributed transactions
as a series of events, where each step can trigger the next, and failures are handled
by compensating actions. Each of these steps is recorded as an event, aligning with
the principles of event sourcing: immutability, traceability, and append-only correction.
Sagas maintain consistency over time without global rollback, making them well-suited
for systems using event sourcing [25, 26].
While sagas, 2PC, and non-2PC strategies all support distributed consistency, sagas
share a particularly strong conceptual overlap with event sourcing. Both treat system be-
havior as sequences of meaningful, durable events and provide full audit trails of business
activity.
2.3.5
Reactive Manifesto
The Reactive Manifesto emphasizes building systems that are responsive, resilient, elastic,
and message-driven, which aligns more closely with EDA than with event sourcing. EDA
naturally supports responsiveness through real-time reactions to events. Its decoupled
structure enhances resilience by isolating failures within components, while elasticity is
achieved through scalable, distributed services [27].
In contrast, event sourcing focuses on recording all state changes as immutable events,
providing strong auditability and state reconstruction capabilities. While this immutability
supports resilience, event sourcing does not inherently promote responsiveness or elasti-
city. Replaying events to rebuild state can introduce latency, and scalability depends on
the performance of the event store rather than flexible, distributed components.
2.3.6
Materialized View
A materialized view is a physical, precomputed table created from one or more existing
tables to improve read performance [28]. For example, rather than repeatedly joining
customer and product tables, a materialized view stores the combined result, allowing
faster queries. These views are periodically refreshed, either fully, incrementally, or on-
demand, to reflect changes in the source data.

Materialized views are conceptually similar to projections in event sourcing. While mater-
ialized views derive from relational tables, projections are built from event logs that reflect
system changes. Both aim to provide efficient, read-optimized views, with projections
typically updated in real time as events occur like incremental refreshes.
They also resemble snapshots in event sourcing, which store the latest state of an entity
to avoid replaying all past events. Like materialized views, snapshots store derived data
to speed up access and reduce computation overhead.
2.4
Existing Approaches to Event Sourcing
Event sourcing frameworks vary widely in terms of architectural assumptions, complexity,
and intended usage. To better understand their characteristics, they can be classified into
three broad categories:
• Enterprise-Scale Frameworks for Distributed Systems
• General-Purpose Frameworks with Developer Abstractions
• Specialized Event Stores
2.4.1
Enterprise-Scale Frameworks for Distributed Systems
These frameworks are tailored for high-scale, distributed environments and are typically
used in microservices architectures. While powerful, their complexity may make them
unsuitable for small companies with limited infrastructure.
Eventuate
Eventuate is a platform designed to support the development of transactional, event-
driven microservices using event sourcing and CQRS. Its primary goal is to address a
fundamental challenge in distributed microservice architectures: how to atomically up-
date state and publish events without relying on 2PC, which is often impractical in modern
systems [29].
To tackle this, Eventuate provides two main solutions: Eventuate Tram for traditional
JDBC/JPA-based applications, and Eventuate Local, which is tailored specifically for event
sourcing. Since this thesis focuses on event sourcing, the discussion will center on Even-
tuate Local and its capabilities.
Eventuate is an open-source, on-premise platform designed for self-hosted systems with
a strong focus on event sourcing. It uses a MySQL database as the event store and Kafka
for event publishing. A Change Data Capture (CDC) component monitors the database’s
transaction log and publishes events to Kafka topics, enabling reliable, asynchronous
event delivery without compromising consistency.
For instance, in a traditional approach, an OrderCreated row might be inserted into the
database, followed by publishing a message to Kafka. If the system crashes after the
insert but before the publish, consistency is lost. Eventuate avoids this by combining the
two steps into a single atomic operation using its event store and CDC mechanism.
This architecture ensures that each state change and its corresponding event are handled
atomically within a single transaction. Eventuate provides robust support for auditability
and replayability. However, it also introduces notable infrastructure complexity, requiring
Kafka, Zookeeper, and the CDC service, making it more appropriate for systems needing
microservice coordination rather than simpler applications.

Akka
Akka is a toolkit for building highly concurrent, distributed, and fault-tolerant applications
based on the actor model. It provides an event sourcing mechanism through Akka per-
sistence, which allows stateful actors to store their state as a series of immutable events.
The sum of all the actors would represent the state of the application. This makes it
particularly well-suited for applications that require reactive and resilient event-driven pro-
cessing. Akka’s actor model enables efficient message-driven concurrency, allowing indi-
vidual actors to communicate asynchronously while maintaining their state independently.
This results in a loosely coupled system where failures can be isolated, and self-healing
mechanisms can be implemented to improve system resilience [30, 31, 32].
Akka persistence enhances the framework’s event sourcing capabilities by providing built-
in support for event replay and snapshotting. This ensures that application state can
be reconstructed efficiently, preventing performance degradation over time. Additionally,
Akka’s seamless integration with other reactive technologies makes it an attractive option
for applications that require elasticity and responsiveness.
For example, an actor in Akka might represent a specific customer’s order process. In-
stead of a central service updating a database, each actor manages its own lifecycle and
state, receiving messages like OrderCreated, OrderCancelled, and storing corresponding
events. While this allows for concurrency and isolation, it requires developers to rethink
traditional object-oriented or functional design.
However, the complexity of Akka presents a significant challenge for smaller teams and
companies that do not require large-scale distributed processing. Much of this complex-
ity comes from its actor-based concurrency model, which requires developers to adopt a
different mental model for structuring applications. Instead of traditional function calls or
shared state, developers must manage message passing between independent actors,
understand actor lifecycles, handle asynchronous communication patterns, and imple-
ment supervision strategies for failure recovery. This approach can be powerful but is
difficult to grasp without prior experience, leading to a steep learning curve. As a result,
even though Akka is highly effective for building reactive and distributed systems, its ad-
vanced concurrency and fault-tolerance features may introduce unnecessary overhead in
simpler applications that do not need that level of scalability or resilience.
Axon Framework
Axon Framework is an event-driven microservices framework that provides end-to-end
support for event sourcing, CQRS, and distributed messaging. One of its key advantages
is its comprehensive toolset for handling event-driven application development, making
it easier to implement complex event-sourced architectures without requiring extensive
boilerplate code [33, 34, 35].
Axon’s event-driven approach enables developers to decouple business logic from stor-
age and communication mechanisms, allowing for greater flexibility and scalability. The
framework includes an advanced event store that simplifies event persistence and re-
trieval. Additionally, it supports distributed messaging, ensuring that different components
of an application can communicate asynchronously and process events independently.
A developer might annotate a method in an aggregate with @CommandHandler, and
Axon will automatically route incoming commands and persist resulting events. While
convenient, this abstraction can obscure underlying control flow and make debugging
difficult.
However, while Axon offers powerful abstractions, its benefits come with trade-offs. The

Axon’s opinionated structure and reliance on annotations and configuration can obscure

2.4.2
General-Purpose Frameworks with Developer Abstractions
These frameworks are designed to bring event sourcing to mainstream development en-
vironments, by providing helpful abstractions and reducing boilerplate. They often strike
a balance between flexibility and simplicity but may still introduce infrastructure overhead.
Revo
The Revo Framework is a modular, open-source application framework designed for de-
veloping C#/.NET applications that use event sourcing, CQRS, and DDD. Its architecture
is organized into distinct modules that handle core functionalities, infrastructure, and ex-
ternal integrations. The core modules provide foundational components such as event
handling, command processing, domain modeling (aggregates, entities, value objects),
and data access abstractions that are decoupled from persistence. The infrastructure
layer facilitates integration with various storage mechanisms and messaging systems,
offering support for technologies like Entity Framework and RabbitMQ. The framework
includes an asynchronous processing model that ensures event handling in distributed
environments [36, 37].
Revo’s extensive feature set can introduce significant complexity and overhead, making
it challenging to manage in practice. Its broad range of modules with event sourcing,
CQRS, DDD, sagas, projections, and integrations with various databases and messaging
systems can lead to unnecessary bloat, especially in projects that do not require all of
these capabilities. This results in a steep learning curve as developers must understand
and configure a lot of components to get even simple applications running. This feature-
rich approach can introduce more complexity than necessary, reducing the benefits of the
abstraction in more simple use cases.
EventFlow
EventFlow is a .NET framework designed to facilitate the practical implementation of
event sourcing in application development. It provides a structured way to handle core
components such as aggregates, commands, events, and event stores, ensuring that
all state changes are captured as immutable events. EventFlow reduces much of the
boilerplate associated with event sourcing, offering out-of-the-box support for event per-
sistence across various storage backends like SQL Server, PostgreSQL, MongoDB, and
EventStore. The framework focus on modularity and extensibility, allowing developers to
define custom behaviors while maintaining a clear separation of concerns. It supports ad-
vanced event sourcing features such as snapshots to optimize aggregate loading, event
upgraders for handling changes in event schemas over time, and metadata enrichment
for adding contextual information to events. EventFlow also integrates seamlessly with
distributed systems through event publishing mechanisms, making it suitable for event-
driven architectures [38, 39].
While EventFlow streamlines the implementation of event sourcing, it does not fully elim-
inate the complexity associated with distributed systems. Challenges such as managing
event consistency, ensuring reliable message delivery, and coordinating state across ser-
vices still require significant effort.

2.4.3
Specialized Event Stores
These tools focus exclusively on the storage, retrieval, and projection of events. While
they do not provide full frameworks, they are often foundational components in event-
sourced systems and are optimized for performance.
Kurrent
Kurrent is a specialized event store designed specifically for event sourcing applications.
Unlike general purpose databases, it is optimized for storing and retrieving event streams,
ensuring high performance reads and writes. Its architecture allows for efficient event
querying and projections. One of Kurrent’s core strengths is its ability to handle large-scale
event streams with minimal performance degradation. It provides support for complex
event projections, which allow applications to generate read models without modifying
the underlying event data [40, 41].
Despite its strengths in storing and retrieving event streams efficiently, Kurrent focuses
primarily on event storage, not on simplifying the broader event sourcing experience. It
does not provide abstractions for aggregates, command handling, or projection manage-
ment, leaving those concerns to be implemented entirely by the developer. This makes
it well-suited for low-level event storage, but it does not help solve many of the prac-
tical design challenges of event-sourced systems, such as event modeling, versioning
strategies, or integration with application logic. As a result, while powerful, Kurrent is
a better fit as a backend component rather than a full framework for simplifying event
sourcing.
2.4.4
Implications for Small Companies
Most existing event sourcing frameworks are designed for large-scale systems, assuming
the availability of distributed infrastructure, advanced tooling, and experienced develop-
ment teams. This creates a barrier for small companies, which often require simpler,
lightweight solutions. The frameworks reviewed above reveal a gap between the capab-
ilities of current tools and their accessibility to smaller teams. More critically, they all rely
heavily on explicit event and command handling, adding to the complexity and develop-
ment overhead in environments with limited resources.

Research Implications
Although the literature emphasizes event sourcing’s strengths, such as auditability, trace-
ability, and replayability, it also exposes the significant complexity and development over-
head it introduces. Much of this comes from the tight coupling between event sourcing
and event-driven architectures, and demand of explicit handling of commands, events,
and projections. These patterns can be difficult to manage, particularly for small teams
lacking the resources to build and maintain complex infrastructure. As a result, there is a
clear research need for simplified, developer-friendly alternatives that preserve the core
benefits of event sourcing without the burden of full event-driven orchestration and explicit
event handling.

To investigate the barriers that limit the adoption of event sourcing in small companies

3.1
Justification for Using Design Science Research
Design Science Research (DSR) is a problem-solving methodology that focuses on cre-
ating and evaluating artifacts to address real-world challenges while contributing to both
practical and theoretical knowledge [42, 43]. It is particularly suited for research at the in-
tersection of technology and socio-technical systems, where artifacts must function within
human and organizational constraints [42] (p. 12–13).
This thesis adopts DSR to develop and evaluate a simplified event sourcing method and
framework tailored to small companies with limited technical capacity. DSR not only sup-
ports artifact creation but also their contextual evaluation, enabling reflection on utility,
usability, and trade-offs.
Alternative approaches, such as standalone case study research, offer deep contextual
insight and are commonly used to explore socio-technical systems [44]. However, case
studies are primarily explanatory and less suited for systematically designing and validat-
ing new solutions. DSR, by contrast, emphasizes solution-oriented knowledge develop-
ment through artifact design and empirical validation, making it a better fit for this thesis.
The case study is therefore used not as an independent research method but as an in-

Event Sourcing in Practice
To motivate the simplified approach proposed later in this thesis, this chapter outlines how
conventional event sourcing is typically implemented in modern systems. While event
sourcing fundamentally involves recording state changes as immutable events, it is often
combined with EDA that introduce additional complexity, such as asynchronous mes-
saging, eventual consistency, and separate read models.
Much of this complexity comes not from event sourcing itself, but from architectural pat-
terns layered on top, including message queues and event buses. For small teams or
simpler domains, this added infrastructure can become a barrier to adoption.
To illustrate these challenges, the chapter presents a representative implementation using
a shopping cart domain. This example highlights the key components, aggregates, com-
mands, events, handlers, and projections, and provides context for the design choices
behind the simplified framework developed in this thesis.
5.1
Conventional Implementation Workflow
Figure 5.1 shows an example from Microsoft with a typical event sourcing workflow in con-
ventional architectures. The presentation layer queries a read-optimized, asynchronously
updated data store to populate the user interface (1) [3].
When a user performs an action (e.g., adding an item to a cart (2)), a command hand-
ler retrieves the relevant entity’s historical events from the event store and replays them
to reconstruct its current state in memory (3). The command is then validated, and if
successful, one or more domain events are raised (4) [3].
Rather than triggering side effects directly, these events are typically published to a mes-
sage queue or event bus, decoupling producers from consumers. Subscribed event hand-
lers (5) respond by persisting the new events (5a), updating the read model (5b), and
triggering external processes (5c) [3].

be prohibitive for small teams or simpler domains, and are not inherently part of core event
sourcing. This complexity highlights the need for a simplified approach, which is explored

Event handling in event sourcing typically follow one of three main strategies: (1) handled
within domain objects, (2) self-processing events, or (3) event processors. A hybrid of
these is also common. While they differ in structure, they all qualify as event sourcing
so long as application state is derived exclusively from events and not from direct state
mutation.
1. Domain Object Event Handling
In this approach, domain objects replay and apply their own events to maintain state.
This preserves encapsulation and traceability, as the logic lives where the state resides.

6
Conceptual Design of the Framework
The previous chapter illustrated how conventional event sourcing, while powerful, intro-
duces significant complexity in practice, particularly for smaller teams. Much of this over-
head does not come from event sourcing itself, but from its typical coupling with event-
driven infrastructure, such as message queues, read model projectors, and asynchronous
event handlers.
In response, this chapter introduces the conceptual design of a simplified event sourcing
framework developed in this thesis. The framework seeks to preserve the core benefits
of event sourcing while exposing a CRUD-style interface familiar to most developers.
By abstracting away explicit event handling and infrastructure concerns, the goal is to
reduce cognitive and operational barriers, making event sourcing more accessible for
teams accustomed to traditional architectures.
6.1
Design Goals and Overview
Before introducing the conceptual foundation of the simplified event sourcing framework,
it is useful to briefly restate the core benefits and challenges outlined earlier in this thesis,
both of which the proposed framework aims to preserve and address, respectively.
Benefits (from Section 2.2.6): observability and replayability, debugging and diagnostics,
reliability, scalability, support for multiple data views, extensibility, and decoupling.
Challenges (from Section 2.2.7): steep learning curve, rebuilding projections, eventual
consistency, dependencies on external systems, event versioning, lack of standardized
technology and tooling, and data privacy and regulatory compliance.
The simplified framework builds on the core principles of event sourcing but streamlines its
implementation by reducing infrastructure overhead, abstracting complexity, and aligning
more closely with how developers are used to working with state. Conceptually, it favors
a CRUD-like experience, embeds projections into the normal flow, and avoids reliance on
external messaging infrastructure.
The rest of this chapter outlines the design and architectural decisions behind the sim-
plified event sourcing framework. It begins with an architectural overview and the core
storage components. This is followed by a CRUD-based repository abstraction, with a
subsequent section on transactional consistency to support transactions and concurrency
control. The chapter then covers support for event replay and snapshots, event and en-
tity versioning, GDPR-compliant data handling, and strategies for managing external de-
pendencies. It concludes with a summary of how the approach addresses the identified
challenges, preserves key benefits, and which trade-offs are introduced.

6.4
CRUD-based Repository Abstraction
This section introduces the core approach of this thesis: a repository abstraction that
exposes a CRUD-style interface while internally managing event generation and persist-
ence.
6.4.1
Making Event Sourcing Approachable
To lower the barrier to adopting event sourcing, this framework introduces a repository
abstraction that mimics the familiar CRUD programming model. The CRUD mindset em-
phasizes consistent and synchronous interaction with persistent state: developers re-
trieve an object, modify it, and persist the changes. This model is simple and predictable,
particularly in small-team or traditional application contexts where transactional opera-
tions are expected to be immediate and linear.
The abstraction simplifies the mental model for developers familiar with CRUD while pre-
serving key benefits of event sourcing. Although read operations do not generate events
(since they do not alter state), their inclusion in the repository interface supports the CRUD
mindset and reinforces a consistent interaction model. The implementation is aligned with
CQRS principles, separating read and write paths, while maintaining a read-optimized en-
tity store for current state access, supporting scalability and decoupling.
6.4.2
Repository Structure and Consistency Model
Each operation follows a consistent pattern:
• Create: When a new entity is created, a CreateEvent is automatically generated
based on the entity’s state. This event is then persisted to an event store, and the
current version of the entity is saved to an entity store.
• Update: Updating an entity results in an UpdateEvent, capturing the updated it in
the entity store.
• Delete: Deleting an entity creates a DeleteEvent, which removes the entity and
updates the persisted state accordingly.
• Read: Reading an entity retrieves it directly from the entity store, without interacting
with the event store, since the current state is already persisted and readily available
for fast access.
This setup also addresses a key limitation in traditional event sourcing: eventual consist-
ency. In many event-sourced systems, the event is first written, and then asynchronously
processed to update read models or projections. That can lead to temporary inconsist-
encies between writes and reads. By updating the current state synchronously alongside
the event log, this framework ensures read-after-write consistency, reducing complexity
and improving the developer experience. The same mechanism applies to Update and
Delete operations, ensuring that both the event store and the entity store remain in sync.

Entity State Management
Entities are created outside the repository, typically through constructors or factory meth-

When using ReadById, developers receive the current version of the entity from the entity
store. These entities are mutable, but any changes must be explicitly persisted by calling
Update. Modifications made without doing so will not be reflected in the event store or
entity store.
This introduces an important contract: entities retrieved from the repository should be
considered session-scoped. While this model assumes developers act responsibly within
a session, it does not enforce concurrency control. A solution could be to introduce optim-

the immediate context of their retrieval are not automatically tracked. It is the developer’s
responsibility to explicitly resubmit modified entities to persist updates. This pattern can
lead to concurrency issues, such as stale writes, if an entity is read, modified, and updated
after a delay during which another change has already occurred. These challenges are
discussed further in Section 6.5.2.

6.4.5
Enforcing Valid State Transitions
To ensure basic lifecycle integrity, the repository enforces a simple constraint: entities
must exist before they can be updated or deleted.
Specifically, an Update or Delete
method call on the repository will check whether the entity exists in the entity store based
on its id. If the entity does not exist, either because it was never created or has already
been deleted, the operation will fail explicitly. This guards against stale or invalid updates
and provides a minimal safeguard for entity lifecycle consistency. It also allows the re-
pository to serve as a basic validation layer, ensuring that operations follow an expected
flow from creation through mutation to deletion.
Furthermore, this constraint ensures that no invalid event can be emitted. Since events
represent the source of truth in an event-sourced system, allowing an operation to emit
an event for a non-existent or already deleted entity would compromise the integrity of
the event log. By verifying that an entity exists before processing updates or deletions,
the repository guarantees that all emitted events reflect valid state transitions. This aligns
with one of the core principles of event sourcing: events must be trustworthy records of
legitimate domain actions that could have occurred. Invalid operations that could lead to
inconsistent or illegal system states are therefore proactively prevented.
For instance, consider a case where a list of spot quotes is displayed in the frontend.
A user selects a quote, modifies the destination address, and submits the change. The
updated SpotQuote object is then passed to the backend, which calls the repository’s
Update method. Before applying the update, the repository checks if the entity exists in
the entity store. If the entity has been deleted or was never created (e.g. due to stale
client data or a bug), the operation is rejected and no event is emitted. This prevents
illegal state transitions and ensures that only valid updates are recorded in the event log.
It is important to note that while the repository ensures structural validity, such as prevent-
ing updates to non-existent entities, it does not enforce domain-specific business rules.
Responsibility for validating business logic (e.g., ensuring a quote cannot be updated after
expiration) remains with the developer.

Trade-offs and Summary
This approach makes event sourcing accessible to developers who are more comfortable
with traditional, synchronous CRUD operations. The repository acts as a familiar interface
while maintaining the event log and current state under the hood. This significantly lowers
the adoption barrier, particularly for teams not yet familiar with event sourcing patterns.
However, the abstraction comes with a trade-off. By using generalized event types like Up-
dateEvent<T>, the system loses the expressiveness found in rich, domain-specific events
such as SpotQuoteFromAddressUpdatedEvent. These more specific events provide clearer
audit trails and domain insight, which are useful for debugging, analysis, and understand-
ing business workflows. The choice to prioritize simplicity sacrifices some semantic clar-
ity in the event log. As discussed in Section 2.2.8, established best practices in event
sourcing recommend modeling events that reflect meaningful, domain-driven changes.
This approach is often justified on the basis that such events are easier to understand,
maintain, and reason about. However, in the context of this framework, where event
types are abstracted away and not defined manually by developers, this argument holds
less weight. Since developers are not directly managing event definitions, the benefits
of semantic clarity are somewhat diminished, making the trade-off for simplicity more ac-
ceptable in this specific case.
This trade-off reflects a deliberate design decision: to make event sourcing simpler and
more maintainable, particularly for small teams and systems where overhead and com-
plexity must be minimized.
In line with this simplification, the framework also removes event handlers. Traditional
event-sourced systems often rely on handlers to apply events, update projections, or
trigger side effects. While powerful, this model introduces boilerplate and conceptual
overhead. In contrast, this framework centralizes behavior in the repository: events are
automatically generated and applied when calling Create, Update, or Delete. Developers
no longer need to implement or register custom handlers for state persistence.

Although this design limits the ability to react to domain-specific events or build event-

Developers who require domain-specific behavior can still layer logic externally using

6.5
Transactional Consistency
Traditional databases offer transactional guarantees, ensuring that operations either com-
plete fully or not at all. This atomicity is critical in workflows like booking both a flight and
hotel, where partial success would cause inconsistencies.
Event sourcing complicates such guarantees: events are immutable and persisted indi-
vidually, with no inherent support for atomic multi-entity operations. To address this, the
framework introduces transactional consistency by grouping repository operations into
atomic units of work.
6.5.1
Transaction Model
The framework introduces a transaction manager that defines a scoped unit of work.
Within this scope, developers can queue multiple Create, Update, or Delete operations,
which are buffered in memory rather than executed immediately. Upon commit, these op-
erations are applied as a conceptually atomic batch to both the event store and the entity
store. This approach preserves consistency while remaining aligned with the principles
of event sourcing.
A distinguishing feature of this model is that newly created or modified entities become
instantly available within the transaction scope. This enables composition, such as cre-
ating an address and referencing it in a booking within the same transaction, even before
persistence.
If the transaction is committed successfully, all queued operations are persisted. In the
event of a failure during commit, the system emits compensating events to reverse pre-
viously applied operations. Since the event log is immutable, rollbacks are achieved by
appending semantically inverse events (e.g., a DeleteEvent following a CreateEvent, or
an UpdateEvent restoring prior state).
If the transaction is rolled back before commit, no operations are persisted, and the in-
memory state is discarded entirely. This ensures transactional determinism while aligning
with event sourcing constraints.
This design resembles a localized Saga pattern: compensating events are used to re-
store consistency, but execution remains within a single bounded context, avoiding the
complexity of distributed coordination.

6.5.2
Concurrency Considerations
Beyond transactional safety, the framework must handle concurrency to prevent conflicts
during simultaneous access. For example, when two users attempt to book the last avail-
able seat, only one operation should succeed.
To address this, the framework uses optimistic concurrency control. Each entity carries a
version, and updates verify that the stored version matches the one read. If a mismatch
is detected, indicating concurrent modification, the operation fails with a version conflict.
This check applies to both transactional and standalone operations, enabling consistency
without pessimistic locking, which was avoided due to its performance costs and potential
for contention in concurrent environments. While scalable and non-blocking, this model
requires developers to implement retry or conflict resolution logic.
6.5.3
Guarantees and Limitations
The transactional model in this framework provides several important guarantees that
closely align with traditional expectations around transactional behavior, but with some
notable differences due to the constraints and philosophy of event sourcing.
Atomicity
At a high level, the framework aims to provide atomicity: either all operations in a trans-
action are successfully committed, or none are. However, due to the immutable nature of
events, true rollback (as in undoing changes in place) is not possible. Instead, the frame-
work achieves atomic-like semantics through compensating events. If a failure occurs
mid-commit, any partial changes are reversed by emitting semantic inverses.
Consistency
The framework supports consistency at the application level by allowing developers to
define and enforce validation rules and invariants before commit. It uses optimistic con-
currency control to prevent lost updates. However, unlike traditional ACID-compliant data-
bases, it does not automatically enforce referential integrity across entities. Ensuring such
38
Streamlining Event Sourcing for Small Companies
==PAGE:47==
consistency is the responsibility of the developer, typically implemented via custom repos-
itory decorators or domain logic.
Isolation
Each transaction operates on an isolated in-memory view of the system state. Changes
made within a transaction are not visible to others until the transaction is committed.
Durability
Once a transaction is committed, the resulting events are persisted to the event store,
which acts as an immutable log. This guarantees that committed changes are not lost,
even in the case of system failure. Durability is also ensured by writing to the entity store
as part of the commit sequence.
Limitations
This approach does come with limitations:
• No true rollback: Since events are append-only and cannot be deleted or modified,
rollback is always logical rather than physical.
• No distributed transactions: The framework does not coordinate consistency
across external systems or services. If cross-service guarantees are needed, an
external orchestrator or saga pattern must be introduced.
• Eventual resolution, not strict atomicity: If a commit partially succeeds before
failing, compensating events are required to restore correctness. This means the
system may temporarily enter an inconsistent state, which is later resolved.
• Developer responsibility: The framework does not enforce relational integrity,
global constraints, or consistency guarantees across multiple entities. Ensuring cor-
rectness is delegated to the developer, who must implement domain validation and
handle version conflicts.
Comparison with ACID
While the framework captures the spirit of ACID transactions, it does not offer full ACID
compliance as found in traditional relational databases:
• Atomicity: Simulated via compensating events and not guaranteed at storage level.
• Consistency: Enforced through versioning and validation, but bounded to the do-
main model.
• Isolation: Provided within a transaction’s in-memory scope.
• Durability: Ensured through event log and entity store persistence.
The absence of full atomic rollback and the reliance on compensations mean that trans-
actional boundaries are best treated as eventual rather than instantaneous. This design
trade-off simplifies implementation and aligns well with the event sourcing philosophy,
while still offering meaningful safety and predictability for most application-level workflows.
6.5.4
Importance of Transactional Guarantees
Transactional consistency is essential for maintaining system reliability, particularly in
workflows involving multiple related changes. Use cases such as booking multiple ser-
vices, transferring funds across accounts, or creating interdependent records require that
all operations either succeed together or not at all. Without atomicity, failures could leave
behind incomplete or orphaned data.

6.6
Event Replay and Snapshots
A key advantage of event sourcing is the ability to reconstruct system state by replaying
a log of immutable events. In this framework, the event log serves as the source of truth,
while current entity state is cached in an entity store for fast access. This hybrid approach
avoids costly replays during normal operation while retaining full history for debugging,
auditing, and recovery.
However, traditional event-sourced systems face two major challenges when replaying
events. First, performance: replaying the full event log to rebuild entity or system state
becomes increasingly slow as history grows, affecting responsiveness.
Second, and more critically, replays often trigger unintended side effects. Events are
re-emitted and handled by components that update projections and may also perform ex-
ternal actions, such as sending emails, initiating payments, or interacting with third-party
systems. Reprocessing these events can lead to duplicated side effects, inconsistent
state, or corrupted downstream data, making replays risky and difficult to manage in pro-
duction environments.
To address these issues, this framework introduces a side-effect-free replay model com-
bined with snapshots. Snapshots allow fast recovery of recent state without replaying the
full event history, while side-effect-free replays ensure historical inspection and debugging
remain safe, deterministic, and isolated from external systems.
6.6.1
Side-Effect-Free Replays
This framework uses a deliberately side-effect-free replay model, reflecting its non-reliance
on EDA, i.e., it does not listen for events to trigger external processes. During replay,
events serve only to rebuild internal state, without invoking workflows, integrations, or
asynchronous actions. This makes replays safe, predictable, and ideal for production
debugging, historical analysis, and recovery.
State reconstruction is handled entirely within a replay service via the repository abstrac-
tion, which re-applies past events to the entity store. This supports both targeted rehydra-
tion and full-system restoration using only the event log, with no dependence on external
systems or queues.
While this ensures a simpler, deterministic architecture, it also means side effects like
emails, payments, or API calls must be manually retriggered if needed. This trade-off
prioritizes clarity, control, and safety over automated orchestration, which can add risk
during replay.
For example, if a production bug blocks a SpotQuoteUpdated event, intended to trigger a
booking, the event can be replayed to restore state, but the side effect (e.g., sending the
booking) must be performed manually.
This is a deliberate design choice that prioritizes operational safety and debuggability
over automation, particularly in systems where simplicity and control are important. The
framework adopts a streamlined, isolated replay model focused exclusively on internal

state reconstruction and debugging, avoiding the complexity and risks associated with
side effects.
6.6.2
Use Cases and Benefits
The replay capabilities built into this framework provide strong support for several crit-
ical scenarios. For debugging, they allow developers to precisely reproduce and step
through past behaviors to identify and resolve issues that may only surface under specific
conditions. For auditing, the ability to replay events enables verification of how and why
certain changes occurred, offering a clear, inspectable trail that supports compliance and
accountability.
Replay also plays a role in logic evolution. Developers can test new or revised business
rules against historical data in a safe, isolated environment, enabling experimentation
without risking the current system state. Finally, in disaster recovery situations, the sys-
tem’s reliance on an immutable event log means that even if the entity store is lost or
corrupted, it can be fully rebuilt from scratch. This ensures consistency and recoverability
under failure scenarios.
The replay engine is designed to make these operations deterministic and isolated, mean-
ing they can be safely executed even against live production data without impacting on-
going operations or introducing unintended side effects.
6.6.3
Replay Modes
To support a variety of operational and development scenarios, the framework defines
three distinct replay modes. Each mode offers a different level of isolation and beha-
vior, catering to specific use cases such as state inspection, local testing, or production
debugging.
Strict Mode
Strict mode allows the system to restore its historical state by replaying events, but it
prevents the emission of any new events. This mode ensures complete isolation from the
active system, no changes are made to the event log or the current entity store. It is best
suited for passive inspection, such as verifying the state of a SpotQuote at a specific point
in the past or confirming the result of a sequence of operations. In practice, this mode is
primarily useful for compliance audits, rather than everyday debugging, since it prohibits
any interaction beyond observation.
Sandbox Mode
Sandbox mode enables developers to interact with a historical version of the system while
allowing changes to be made under strict constraints. In this mode, any operations per-
formed may emit events, but these are captured in memory only and never persisted to
the event store. This prevents tampering with the official event timeline and ensures that
any exploration or experimentation remains isolated.
This mode is particularly valuable during development. For example, a developer working
locally might replay events up to a customer-reported issue and try different ways to fix
it or simulate alternate business rules. Since the changes are not persisted, there’s no
risk of polluting the production timeline. The sandbox uses the connected (typically local)
entity store, allowing quick iteration while preserving historical integrity.
Debug Mode
Debug mode is designed for deeper, production-oriented analysis. Unlike sandbox mode,
which reuses the current entity store, debug mode creates a full copy of the current entity

==PAGE:50==
state and replays events in a completely isolated context. This makes it ideal for repro-
ducing bugs reported in production environments.
For example, if a SpotQuote failed to update correctly due to an edge case, a developer
could launch a debug session that copies the state of the production entity store, replays
all relevant events up to the incident, and tests the problematic update, all without affecting
live data or the event store. Like sandbox mode, any emitted events remain in memory
and are discarded after the session ends.
Together, these replay modes provide a robust toolkit for examining the evolution of sys-
tem state in a safe, deterministic, and reproducible way. Whether investigating a bug,
validating a new feature, or reviewing historical operations, developers can choose the
most appropriate mode based on their level of access, risk tolerance, and intent.
6.6.4
Snapshots
Snapshots in this framework capture the full state of all entities at a specific point in time.
Rather than representing isolated entity-level snapshots, this approach captures a full
system state, enabling efficient and consistent replays across the entire domain.
When initiating a replay to reconstruct the state as it was at a particular moment, either
for debugging, auditing, or analysis, the framework first locates the latest snapshot that
precedes the target time. This snapshot serves as the starting point for the replay. Only
the events that occurred after the snapshot and before the desired timestamp are then
reapplied. This reduces the volume of events that must be processed and significantly
speeds up replay operations, especially in systems with long event histories.
Importantly, replays in this framework are never performed directly on the live produc-
tion system. Instead, replays are executed in isolated environments, either in-memory,
on a developer machine, or within a sandboxed context. This ensures that the current
production state remains untouched and safe.
To further support this safety, the framework also captures a snapshot before any replay
session begins. This snapshot allows the system to restore the current working state
almost instantly once the replay is complete, even within development environments. This
makes experimentation with past data both reversible and low-risk.
The snapshot strategy is ultimately designed to reduce replay time. This makes snapshots
a crucial mechanism for supporting efficient replay capabilities in the framework.

6.7
Event and Entity Versioning
Over time, software systems evolve. Fields are renamed, types are split or merged, and
data structures are optimized. In event-sourced systems, this creates a fundamental chal-
lenge: how can we maintain compatibility with historical events and entities that were
created under outdated schemas, without violating the immutability of the event log?
Without proper versioning, any schema change risks breaking the ability to replay old
events or read historical entity state. This would compromise both data integrity and
replayability.
6.7.1
Event and Entity
In this framework, events and entities are tightly coupled through the CRUD-based repos-
itory abstraction. Each event contains standard metadata, such as an ID and timestamp,
and also embeds the relevant entity instance that was created, updated, or deleted.
Because the entity is stored directly within each event, versioning primarily concerns the
entity itself. When replaying older events, the embedded entity may need to be migrated
to the current version to maintain compatibility with the latest application logic. The full
structure of events and entities are described in detail in Section 7.2.
6.7.2
Dynamic Migration
To solve this, the framework introduces a versioning mechanism that supports forward
evolution. Rather than modifying old events, which would break immutability, each entity
is annotated with a version number. This version serves as a marker for the schema under
which the data was originally created.
When old data is read, either during replay or normal operations, the framework checks
its version and applies a registered transformation function to migrate it to the current
schema. This preserves compatibility while ensuring that the latest application logic al-
ways operates on the expected data format.
6.7.3
Versioning Schemas and Migrations
Each entity class includes a SchemaVersion field to capture the version used at the time
of creation.
When reading entity from an event, the framework determines whether its version matches
the current expected version:
• If the version is current, the data is deserialized directly into the up-to-date class.
• If it is outdated, the framework first deserializes into the corresponding historical
version class and then upgrades it using a registered migration function.
Migration functions are defined explicitly by developers. For example, in Listing 6.4, an
older version of the SpotQuote entity used Address objects for its From and To fields. In
the newer version, these have been replaced by integer foreign keys referencing stored
addresses. A migration function converts the old structure into the new one.

Common techniques in traditional event-soruced systems include publishing both old and
new event schemas in parallel, transforming entire streams into a new schema version,
appending summary events that act as checkpoints, or keeping aggregates short-lived so
they can be deprecated [46]. While these approaches offer strong guarantees in distrib-
uted settings, they also introduce significant complexity and break event sourcing a bit. In
contrast, this framework avoids such overhead by applying upcasting dynamically during
deserialization. This keeps version handling centralized, avoids breaking immutability,
and removes the need to maintain multiple schema versions in parallel.
6.7.5
Trade-Offs and Responsibilities
While this model is simple and robust, it places the responsibility of defining versions
and migrations on the developer. Poorly written migration logic could lead to subtle bugs.
Furthermore, migration logic must be carefully tested and versioned alongside application
code to ensure that the system remains compatible across deployments. Nonetheless,
this approach strikes a balance between forward flexibility and historical fidelity, allowing
the system to evolve without rewriting history.
6.8
GDPR Considerations
Ensuring GDPR compliance in event-sourced systems presents unique challenges due
to the immutable nature of the event log. Unlike traditional CRUD-based systems, where
personal data can be modified or deleted in place, event sourcing records every change
as an append-only event, preserving personal data indefinitely for purposes such as trace-
ability and auditability.
This immutability stands in direct tension with GDPR requirements like the right to erasure.
Since events cannot be altered or removed without compromising the integrity of the log,
44
Streamlining Event Sourcing for Small Companies
==PAGE:53==
compliance requires alternative design strategies. The challenge becomes even more
complex when personal data is replicated across projections, increasing the number of
locations where data must be purged or anonymized.
Several conceptual approaches have been proposed to solve this conflict. Oskar Dudycz
outlines techniques such as data segregation, log compaction, forgettable payloads, and
crypto shredding [47]. These strategies aim to balance legal compliance with the design
constraints of event-sourced systems. Data segregation involves isolating personal data
from events into separate storage, allowing for independent management and deletion.
Log compaction reduces the volume of stored data by retaining only the latest event in a
stream, though this may not fully satisfy legal deletion requirements. Forgettable payloads
externalize sensitive data, replacing it with references that can be invalidated independ-
ently of the event stream. Crypto shredding encrypts personal data within events and
relies on destroying the associated keys to make that data permanently inaccessible.
The framework described in this thesis adopts a developer-controlled variation of data
segregation and forgettable payloads. Rather than embedding personal data directly in
events, developers annotate sensitive fields, which are then extracted and stored in a
dedicated personal data store before the event is persisted. Upon retrieval, the system
rehydrates the event by reattaching the associated personal data, enabling full system
behavior without storing sensitive information directly in the immutable log.
This approach preserves the integrity of the event log while supporting GDPR-related
requirements. However, it is not without trade-offs. It depends on developers correctly
identifying and annotating personal fields, which introduces the risk of oversight or incon-
sistency. Unlike lower-level strategies such as crypto shredding, this method relies on
careful convention rather than enforcement. It also adds operational complexity: the sys-
tem must maintain consistency between the event store and the personal data store, and
it must handle rehydration failures gracefully, for example, by returning null values when
personal data has been deleted. Responsibility for defining what constitutes personal
data and implementing appropriate retention or deletion policies remains at the applica-
tion level.
6.9
Managing External Dependencies
External dependencies introduce significant complexity in event-sourced systems, par-
ticularly during event replay. In event sourcing, where system state is reconstructed by
replaying past events, reissuing the same external request can yield different results, due
to changes in external systems, time-sensitive data, or business logic. It may also fail
(e.g., if the external service is unavailable) or cause unintended side effects, such as
duplicate payments or order confirmations.
A widely adopted solution to this problem is the use of an API Gateway, as described
by Martin Fowler [1]. The gateway acts as a single entry point for all outgoing requests,
decoupling the core system from external integrations. In this framework, the gateway is
extended with the API response store, that stores and retrieves responses. This enables
deterministic and reproducible behavior during event replay.
When a component needs to call an external service, it routes the request through the API
Gateway. If the system is replaying and the same request has been made previously, the
gateway returns the stored response. Otherwise, if not replaying, it performs the request
and records the result for future use. This ensures consistency between the original and
replayed execution paths.

==PAGE:54==
To accommodate different operational scenarios, the gateway supports three configurable
access strategies:
• CacheOnly: Only previously recorded responses are used.
If no matching re-
sponse exists, the request fails. This strategy is ideal during replay scenarios where
exact state reconstruction is critical. For example, if a booking was submitted at a
specific point in time, this ensures the same response is returned, preserving con-
sistency and avoiding unintended side effects.
• ExternalOnly: Every request is executed live, bypassing the cache entirely. This
strategy is always used during normal (non-replay) operations to ensure that the
system retrieves the most current and accurate data. It is particularly important for
interactions that produce side effects, such as bookings, payments, or rate queries,
where the external call must reach the intended live endpoint to reflect the actual
state of the external system.
• CacheThenExternal: The system checks the cache first and performs a live request
only if no cached response exists. This is useful for backtesting or experimenting
with new logic on historical data. For instance, developers can go back in time and
selectively trigger new external calls (e.g., from a different rate provider) to see how
the system would behave under updated conditions.
During normal operation, the gateway operates in ExternalOnly mode to ensure fresh data
and trigger side effects. For example, when requesting current freight rates, relying on
cached responses would undermine accuracy and correctness. Moreover, actions like
order submissions must occur once, as part of the intended business flow, not during
replay or recovery.
Consider a scenario where the system requests a buying rate from an external freight
provider. In live mode, the gateway records the response (e.g., EUR 850) alongside
the request metadata. Later, during a debugging session, the same request is issued
in CacheOnly mode, and the previously stored response is returned, ensuring that the
replayed behavior matches the original.
By centralizing and controlling access to external services, this approach ensures determ-
inism in replay while maintaining flexibility during development and operations. It mitigates
the risks of side effects and inconsistency when interacting with external systems during
replay.
6.10
Benefits, Challenges, and Trade-Offs
This section summarizes how the framework simplifies event sourcing while retaining its
core benefits. It outlines the primary challenges addressed, the preserved benefits, and
the trade-offs accepted to achieve this balance.
6.10.1
Addressed Challenges
The framework specifically addresses common adoption barriers for event sourcing.
Steep learning curve is reduced by introducing a CRUD-based repository abstraction.
Instead of requiring developers to define and process events directly, the framework al-
lows interaction through familiar CRUD operations. Internally, these actions are translated
into events, letting developers benefit from event sourcing without needing to fully under-
stand its underlying mechanics.
Rebuilding projections is simplified through the inclusion of an entity store and auto-
mated snapshotting. This avoids the need to replay long event histories during startup or
46
Streamlining Event Sourcing for Small Companies
==PAGE:55==
debugging, making projection maintenance more manageable and system performance
more predictable.
Eventual consistency is avoided by executing all operations synchronously and in-process.
Unlike traditional event-driven systems that rely on asynchronous messaging, this frame-
work processes events and projections within a single method, ensuring consistency.
External dependencies are managed via an API Gateway that distinguishes between live
execution and replay scenarios. During replay, cached responses are served to maintain
determinism and prevent unintended side effects such as duplicate API calls or repro-
cessed payments.
Event versioning is handled through entities with versions and upcasting. When older
events are replayed, the framework can transform the entity on the event into a format
compatible with the current domain model, enabling forward evolution of both entities and
logic without losing access to historical data.
Lack of standardized tooling is addressed by offering a lightweight, standalone frame-
work. The system requires no message brokers, distributed logs, or specialized infrastruc-
ture, making it easy to adopt incrementally and operate in low-complexity environments.
Data privacy and regulatory compliance (e.g., GDPR) is achieved by separating per-
sonal data from persisted events. Sensitive fields are extracted and stored in a dedicated
personal data store at write time, allowing them to be deleted or anonymized without al-
tering the immutable event log.
6.10.2
Retained Benefits
Despite its simplifications, the framework retains the essential benefits of event sourcing.
Observability and replayability are preserved by storing every state change as an im-
mutable event. This enables full traceability, auditability, and the ability to reconstruct
application state at any point in time. Developers and operators can inspect the exact se-
quence of changes leading to a given outcome, facilitating transparency and post-incident
analysis.
Debugging and diagnostics are supported through deterministic event replay.
De-
velopers can recreate historical states in isolation, enabling time-travel debugging and
reproduction of complex issues that would otherwise be difficult to track down using tra-
ditional logs or snapshots alone.
Reliability is maintained through the event log, which acts as a consistent and append-
only source of truth. Since all state transitions are recorded in order, system state can
always be recovered by replaying events.
Scalability is supported through the separation of command and query responsibilities,
following the CQRS model. Create, update, and delete operations write events to the
event store and update the current state in the entity store. Read operations are served
from projections maintained in the entity store, which can be scaled independently of the
event store. This allows the system to accommodate increased load or support special-
ized data views without requiring changes to core logic.
Support for multiple data views is achieved through flexible projection mechanisms
and the current state maintained in the entity store. The event log provides a consistent
source of truth from which diverse read models can be derived, allowing the system to
serve different application needs without modifying historical data.

Extensibility is facilitated by the append-only nature of the event log. New features,
business rules, or projections can be introduced without rewriting existing events, and
updates to domain entities are supported through versioning and migration. The system’s
modular design encourages incremental evolution with minimal disruption.
Decoupling is achieved through several design choices. Read and write paths are separ-
ated via the CQRS model, while the API Gateway isolates external integrations from core
logic. Modular components, such as the event store, entity store, API response store, and
personal data store, can evolve independently. Versioned entities further reduce coupling
by supporting schema changes and extensions without disrupting existing workflows.
6.10.3
Trade-Offs
In simplifying event sourcing, the framework makes several trade-offs that limit some ad-
vanced capabilities.
Reduced event semantics: By hiding event modeling behind CRUD abstractions, the
system loses some domain richness. Developers no longer explicitly design events that
reflect business intent, which may reduce expressiveness and audit clarity in complex
domains.
Synchronous execution: Processing all operations within a single process simplifies
consistency but reduces horizontal scalability. The architecture is best suited to small-
scale services rather than high-throughput, distributed systems.
Limited reactive behavior: As the framework does not rely on event-driven workflows,
it may offer less flexibility for real-time event propagation or loosely coupled reactive sys-
tems.
Manual versioning and migration: Supporting schema evolution requires developers to
write migration logic. This adds maintenance overhead, especially in long-lived systems
with frequent changes.
No event-driven side effects: The framework deliberately avoids triggering side effects
(e.g., API calls, emails) in response to events, including during live operation. Events
serve solely to reconstruct internal state. While this simplifies architecture and ensures
deterministic replays, it limits automation and requires external workflows to be managed
manually or through separate mechanisms.
Conflict handling with optimistic concurrency: The system uses version checks rather
than locking. This is scalable, but puts the burden of conflict resolution on developers,
who must handle edge cases and retry logic explicitly.
Summary
The framework provides a practical path to event sourcing for teams that need its benefits
without its traditional complexity. By simplifying the developer experience and minimizing
infrastructure requirements, it makes event sourcing more accessible, while still delivering
core capabilities like auditability and replay.

Realizing the Conceptual Design
This chapter describes how the conceptual framework was implemented in practice. It
outlines the translation from theory to code, detailing how the event sourcing architecture
was realized and how its key features were constructed.
The implementation follows Clean Architecture principles to maintain separation of con-
cerns, support modular development, and enable long-term adaptability. The framework
is organized into subsystems that address core concerns such as persistence, event re-
play, snapshots, version migration, and external integration.
The chapter also includes notes on testing strategies and performance considerations.
The complete implementation is available in the GitHub repository referenced in Ap-
pendix A.2.
7.1
Architecture
The framework adopts a layered architecture inspired by Clean Architecture [48], with the
primary goal of maintaining a clear separation between domain logic and infrastructure
concerns. This approach ensures that core functionality remains stable, testable, and
independent of external technologies.
A central motivation for this architecture is flexibility. By isolating infrastructure concerns,
including storage, API integrations, and snapshot mechanisms, the framework enables
these components to be swapped or extended without altering the core application or
domain logic. For example, while MongoDB is used in the current implementation (as
described later), the same framework could support relational databases or technologies
like Entity Framework by modifying only the infrastructure layer. This design allows the
system to retain its full range of use cases while adapting to different technical environ-
ments.
The system is divided into four conceptual layers:
• Core: Contains essential domain types (e.g., events, entities) and contracts for per-
sistence. It has no external dependencies and forms the foundation of the system.
• Application Abstractions: Defines interfaces for application services that use in-
frastructure (e.g., snapshot interfaces for replay mechanisms), allowing the applic-
ation logic to remain decoupled from specific technologies.
• Application Layer: Implements use cases, such as replay and personal data work-
flows, using only the abstractions provided by the inner layers. It knows what needs
to happen, but not how or where.
• Infrastructure Layer: Provides concrete implementations of the required services,
such as the stores, repository and API gateway. This is the only layer that depends
on specific technologies like MongoDB.
By isolating each concern into a dedicated layer, the architecture promotes modular devel-
opment, simplifies testing, and supports long-term adaptability, allowing individual parts
of the system to evolve or be replaced independently.

Personal Data
The Personal Data subsystem, illustrated in Figure 7.7, addresses privacy concerns by
ensuring that personally identifiable information is isolated from the immutable event log.

their values to null. These stripped values are stored separately in the PersonalDataStore.
The attribute may be applied to any editable public field or property.
While the framework provides storage isolation, it does not enforce encryption or access
control. These are left to the developer and deployment environment. If personal data is

7.3.8
Smart Repository
The SmartRepository decorates the base repository to introduce transaction manage-
ment. When a consumer requests an IRepository<T> (where T implements IEntity), the
dependency injection container returns a SmartRepository instance. This enables trans-
actional behavior to be applied seamlessly to all repository operations.
Before diving into how the SmartRepository works, it is important to understand the Trans-
actionManager, which powers the transactional functionality.
Transaction Manager
The TransactionManager coordinates transactional operations across the event and entity
stores, ensuring that changes are applied atomically or rolled back in case of failure. It
is registered as a scoped service, meaning a new instance is created for each request,
guaranteeing isolation and non-overlapping execution between concurrent operations.
Internally, the TransactionManager tracks a queue of commit and rollback function pairs.
When a transaction is committed, the commit functions are executed sequentially. If any
step fails, previously executed commits can be rolled back using the rollback functions.
To support this behavior, a secondary rollback queue is maintained in parallel.
The transaction state includes a unique TransactionId, an active flag, and two dictionaries
for tracking entities marked for upsert (create/update) or deletion within the transaction
scope. These structures allow the manager to maintain a consistent in-memory view of
all modifications, ensuring that no entity is simultaneously scheduled for both deletion and
upsert. For example, if an entity is first marked for deletion and then updated, it will be
automatically removed from the deletion queue.
To begin a transaction, the Begin method must be called explicitly. This initializes all
internal data structures and ensures that no other transaction is currently active. From
this point onward, operations such as entity tracking or action registration are permitted.
Entities are tracked through TrackUpsertedEntity<T> and TrackDeletedEntity<T>, which
update the internal dictionaries based on type. These tracked entities are not persisted
until the transaction is committed.

Actions within the transaction are registered through an enlistment mechanism, where
each operation is associated with both a commit and a rollback function. This design
makes it possible to define compensating logic for any step that may fail during the trans-
action.
On commit, all actions are executed in order. Each corresponding rollback function is
stored during execution, enabling reversal if an error occurs after partial completion. If
all commits succeed, the transaction is cleared and considered complete. If an exception
is thrown during this process, the system remains in a consistent state by invoking all
registered rollback functions in reverse order via the RollbackAsync method.
Importantly, rollback is only necessary if the transaction fails during the commit phase. If
the transaction fails before committing, no changes are applied, and rollback is unneces-
sary since all operations are in memory.
The TransactionManager also exposes read-only access to its tracked entities, allowing
other components, such as repositories, to query which entities are affected during the
current transaction lifecycle.

A key motivation for introducing snapshots is to improve replay performance, particularly
as the number of events grows. The results confirm this. As shown in Figure 7.17, replay

The Event Sourcing Method
This chapter presents a high-level, 10 step, conceptual method for adopting the simplified
event sourcing framework. The goal is to lower the technical and cognitive barriers that
often discourage small teams from using event sourcing by focusing on key concepts
rather than low-level implementation. Each step in the method reflects a core activity
or decision in applying the framework. For detailed implementation guidance, see the
handbook in Appendix A.4. While the method is primarily intended for new applications,
its principles can also support gradual adoption or migration in existing systems. The next
chapter demonstrates this method in practice by building the Spot Quote app, complete
with code examples and practical implementation details.
1. Add the event sourcing framework
Integrate the framework by first installing the required NuGet package:
EventSourcing.CRUD.Infrastructure
This provides all the necessary components for event sourcing support. Alternatively, indi-
vidual NuGet packages, such as Core, Application, or others, can be installed as needed.
Links to all available packages are listed in Appendix E.1.
Make sure a MongoDB instance is available for all required stores. Optionally, each store,
such as the event store, entity store, personal data store, and API response store, can
use its own dedicated database. However, for simplicity, they may also share the same
MongoDB instance. These connections will be configured next.
Define the necessary configuration in a centralized file such as appsettings.json. This
includes the MongoDB connection strings and optional features like snapshotting and
personal data separation. You can also control operational thresholds, such as when to
trigger snapshots and how long to retain them. Full template can be seen in Appendix E.2.
Finally, register the framework in your startup code (e.g., Program.cs) by passing in the
configuration. This ensures that the necessary services are added to the dependency
injection container and made available throughout your application.
2. Identify and model key domain entities
Begin by defining the primary business objects in your domain. These should repres-
ent real-world concepts such as quotes, customers, or shipments, and contain the data
needed to support the business logic they are responsible for. They must inherit from the
abstract Entity base class. You do not need to define any events yourself. These are auto-
matically generated by the framework (e.g., QuoteCreated, QuoteUpdated, QuoteDeleted).
Define relationships between entities using the Id field inherited from the base Entity class,
referencing related entities by ID rather than embedding them. Begin with one or two en-
tities and expand gradually to reduce complexity and learning overhead. The framework
supports partial adoption, enabling event sourcing to be introduced incrementally without
requiring a full system overhaul.
3. Register your entities
Each domain entity must be explicitly registered with the framework so that it can be prop-
erly persisted, versioned, and managed. Registration enables the framework to automat-
ically generate events, track changes, and associate each entity with the correct database
collection. This step also ensures support for future schema evolution and migration.
Streamlining Event Sourcing for Small Companies
71
==PAGE:80==
4. Interact with your domain through repositories
Domain entities are accessed and manipulated through a repository abstraction that sup-
ports standard create, read, update, and delete operations. This approach encapsulates
event handling behind a familiar CRUD interface, enabling intuitive development while
maintaining an event-sourced history. All operations are exposed as asynchronous meth-
ods, allowing flexible integration patterns. Awaiting these calls is considered best practice
in CRUD to maintain consistency, though not strictly required.
Interacting with the repositories can either be done through generic repositories or by cre-
ating domain-specific repositories. Generic repositories can be injected directly into ser-
vices for straightforward data access and CRUD operations. In more complex scenarios,
creating domain-specific repositories that wrap the generic interface enables encapsula-
tion of domain logic, improves modularity, and promotes clearer separation of concerns.
This abstraction layer also provides a convenient point for introducing transactional be-
havior through the framework’s transaction manager, allowing for simulated atomicity.
5. Define projections and read models for optimized queries
To support efficient querying, define projections or read models tailored to specific use
cases. These are built using filtering and projection techniques, allowing lightweight, fo-
cused data retrieval without overfetching. This improves performance and enables more
scalable and responsive read operations.
6. Use the API gateway for all your HTTP calls
All outbound HTTP requests should be routed through the framework’s API gateway ab-
straction. This ensures deterministic behavior during replay scenarios, enables full trace-
ability of external interactions, and supports testing and debugging without affecting pro-
duction integrations.
7. Use replay and debugging tools to analyze and evolve your system safely
The framework supports time-travel debugging, historical state restoration, and controlled
event replay between specific points in time. These capabilities assist in diagnosing fail-
ures, testing new business logic safely, and validating outcomes without impacting pro-
duction. All state changes are traceable, allowing complete visibility into the evolution of
each entity.
8. Evolve your domain model using versioning and migrations
As the domain model changes over time, use the built-in schema versioning and migration
strategy to maintain compatibility with existing data. Older events remain replayable, and
legacy entities can be migrated to align with the current schema, ensuring consistency
and long-term maintainability.
9. Handle personal data securely and in compliance with regulations
Mark sensitive fields with the [PersonalData] attribute to exclude them from event logs
and store them separately. This supports compliance with data protection regulations
such as GDPR. A preliminary analysis should identify which fields or combinations could
lead to personal identification. Once identified, they can be annotated to ensure secure,
compliant handling throughout the system.
10. Implement operational logging and auditing as needed
The framework maintains a complete, immutable event store that captures all changes
to domain entities. However, it does not include built-in logging or audit trail exposure.
Logging and audit trails should be implemented based on the specific needs and compli-
ance requirements of the domain. The event store can be queried via the event store to
retrieve recent or historical events, making it possible to surface relevant actions in logs,
dashboards, or audit reports.

Integrating the event sourcing framework is straightforward, as it is provided via NuGet
packages:
• The Core layer requires the EventSourcing.CRUD.Core package to enable entities
to inherit from Entity.
• The Application layer uses the EventSourcing.CRUD.Infrastructure.Repositories
package to access repository functionality.
• The Web layer integrates the EventSourcing.CRUD.Infrastructure package to re-
gister all necessary services.
The services are registered by adding the following to Program.cs in the Web project:
builder.Services.AddEventSourcing(builder.Configuration,
(schema, migrations, migrator, mongoDbRegistrationService) => {});
Once registered, all services provided by the framework can be injected and used wherever
needed.
A MongoDB instance is available via Docker.
2. Identity and model key domain entities
Identifying the core domain entities in the Spot Quote application was straightforward due
to the existing CRUD implementation in the CON-LINQ platform. However, transitioning
to an event-sourced architecture introduces certain modeling changes, most notably, the
use of IDs to reference related entities, rather than relying on embedded relationships.
To keep the implementation focused on the architectural goals of the framework, some
simplifications have been made. For example, CON-LINQ validates all master data such
as countries, zip codes, and cities. In this version, only country validation is preserved,
purely to demonstrate that master data validation is still compatible with the event-sourced
model. More extensive validation would add implementation noise without contributing
meaningful insight into the design of the framework.

10.2.1
Spot Quote History, Traceability, and Debugging
This part of the demonstration shows how the method and framework enables traceability,
auditability, and safe debugging through historical replay.
The session begins with a typical scenario: a spot quote is updated by adding an extra
container, and new buying rates are fetched the a mock API. The quote is then saved. In
the event log view, every system change appears as an immutable event, CreateEvents
for the locations and buying rate, and an UpdateEvent for the spot quote, all grouped in
a single transaction. Each event includes payload and metadata that can be inspected.
The entity history view shows a timeline of events related to a specific entity and its result-
ing state after each change. While the MVP lacks authentication, the explanation includes
how a user ID could be associated with each event for audit trails.
To demonstrate debugging, a support scenario is simulated: a customer encountered an
issue while updating a quote the previous day. Using the replay tool, the system is rolled
back to the relevant event number or timestamp. The quote is restored to its earlier state,
allowing the issue to be reproduced. Any API calls made during this session return the
same responses as before, thanks to cached HTTP results.
After the debugging session, the replay log shows which events would have been emitted.
Once the replay stops, the system reverts to its original state, confirming that no changes
are persisted during replays.
This demonstrates how the method provides full historical insight, supports reproducible
debugging, and isolates time-travel analysis from live operations.
10.2.2
Backtesting Business Logic
This part of the demonstration shows how the method and framework enables safe backtest-
ing of business rule changes using real production data without affecting the live system.
It begins with an example rule: the system blocks customer deletion if any spot quotes
are associated. This ensures data integrity under normal operation. Next, a scenario is
introduced where this rule is relaxed. The validation is temporarily removed, and the app
is restarted.
Rather than testing the change live, a debug mode replay is used. This mode uses real
production events but routes changes to a separate entity store, creating a safe sandbox.
In this replay session, deleting the customer succeeds, reflecting the new behavior. When
the session ends, the original system state is restored, confirming no live data was altered.
This demonstrates how developers can safely validate logic changes against real data,
improving confidence while avoiding production risk.
10.2.3

framework to treat them securely.

excluded from the immutable event log. Instead, they are stored in a separate personal

Discussion
The aim of this study was to understand the barriers that prevent small companies from
adopting event sourcing, and to explore how the approach can be simplified to better suit
their needs. As stated in the problem statement, while event sourcing is widely used in en-
terprise systems, its adoption in smaller companies remains limited. This is largely due to
factors such as technical complexity, steep learning curves, and a lack of implementation
guidance tailored to the constraints of small teams.
To address this problem, this thesis formulated five research objectives: (1) to investigate
why event sourcing remains rarely adopted in small companies, identifying the specific
technical, organizational, and perceptual barriers they face, (2) To explore how event
sourcing can be adapted to fit small-company contexts without losing its core benefits, (3)
to design a simplified method and lightweight framework based on the identified needs and
constraints, (4) to demonstrate the practical use of the proposed method and framework
through a real-world case study, and (5) to evaluate the method and framework in terms of
implementation effort, usability, and their ability to deliver core event sourcing advantages
such as auditability and replayability.
This chapter analyzes the key findings in light of those research objectives and the guiding
research questions. It also reflects on the artifact developed through the design science
research (DSR) methodology, outlines the theoretical and practical contributions of the
work, and discusses potential threats to validity.
11.1
Addressing the Research Questions
This section interprets the findings in relation to each of the research questions.
11.1.1
RQ1: What barriers prevent small companies from adopting event
sourcing in practice?
The literature review identified several core barriers to adopting event sourcing: a steep
learning curve, the complexity of rebuilding projections, managing eventual consistency,
integrating with external systems, handling event versioning, and ensuring personal data
compliance. These challenges are often compounded in small companies, where teams
operate with limited time, personnel, and technical resources. Some difficulties also come
not from event sourcing itself, but from its common pairing with EDA, which introduces
additional system complexity.
One of the most significant contributors to the learning curve is the departure from tradi-
tional CRUD-based thinking. Rather than updating state directly, developers must model
and persist events that represent state changes. For teams unfamiliar with this pattern,
this shift introduces cognitive overhead and increases the perceived complexity of imple-
mentation.
The evaluation provided additional context specific to small company environments. While
participants acknowledged the conceptual value of event sourcing, they raised concerns
about practical adoption. A major obstacle was the perceived lack of a viable path for
incremental migration. Retrofitting event sourcing into an existing architecture was seen
as requiring a full rewrite, a difficult task for teams with deeply embedded relational data-
bases. Although the framework developed in this thesis uses a NoSQL backend (Mon-
goDB), this is not a fundamental requirement of event sourcing.
88

It should also be emphasized that this thesis focuses on enabling event sourcing in new
applications rather than migrating existing systems. Nevertheless, the findings suggest
that broader adoption may require improved support for relational databases and more
accessible paths for incremental adoption.
11.1.2
RQ2: In what ways do small company characteristics affect the
perceived complexity and applicability of event sourcing?
Small companies typically operate with lean infrastructure, small development teams, and
broad developer responsibilities. These structural traits shape how architectural decisions
are evaluated, not just in terms of technical feasibility, but also perceived risk and effort.
Participants found the proposed method and framework approachable for new projects,
largely because it adopted familiar patterns like those used in Entity Framework. Its min-
imal setup, clear abstractions, and built-in guidance reduced perceived complexity and
made it accessible even to those without prior event sourcing experience.
However, concerns remained. Several participants expressed hesitation about MongoDB,
reflecting a broader reliance on relational databases. Additionally, the prospect of main-
taining a less conventional architecture raised questions about long-term sustainability in
small teams with limited time for deep specialization.
In such environments, where only a few developers must build and maintain entire sys-
tems, technologies that align with widely known tools and mental models are more likely
to be adopted.
Unless event sourcing frameworks closely resemble mainstream ap-
proaches, their perceived complexity, regardless of actual implementation effort, may
deter adoption.
11.1.3
RQ3: What simplifications or adaptations can make event sourcing
more accessible to small companies without sacrificing core
benefits?
This thesis introduced a simplified event sourcing method and framework designed for
small companies. Key adaptations included a CRUD-based repository abstraction that
remove the need for complex event modeling and handling, projections are built auto-
matically, synchronous execution to simplify infrastructure, support for data privacy, and
a minimal setup that avoids the need for message brokers. These changes aim to lower
the learning curve and reduce cognitive and technical overhead. The solution successfully
addresses all the major barriers identified in the literature.
Despite these simplifications, the framework retains all core event sourcing benefits: ob-
servability and replayability, debugging and diagnostics, reliability, support for multiple
data views, extensibility, and decoupling.
However, the design introduces trade-offs. These include reduced event semantics, syn-
chronous execution, limited reactive behavior, manual versioning and migration steps,
and the absence of event-driven side effects. While these constraints reduce architectural
flexibility, they are acceptable in the context of small teams, as they significantly lower im-
plementation complexity while preserving the essential advantages of event sourcing.
11.1.4
RQ4: How does the proposed method and framework perform
when applied in a real-world small company context?
To assess practical viability, the proposed method and framework were used to build the
Spot Quote app, modeled after CON-LINQ’s existing Spot Quote module. The goal was
to demonstrate how event sourcing could support the same business needs while offering

added benefits such as traceability and replayability. The implementation showed that the
framework was capable of supporting a fully functional application.
The Spot Quote app was then used throughout the business value evaluation to demon-
strate core event sourcing features, including history tracking, event replay, backtesting,
and personal data handling. These capabilities were integrated into the app using the
framework’s abstractions.
Overall, the method and framework performed effectively in a realistic small company
context. They enabled the development of a business critical application under typical
constraints, confirming not only the framework’s relevance and feasibility, but, most im-
portantly, its practicality for building real-world solutions.
11.1.5
RQ5: To what extent does the proposed method and framework
reduce complexity and increase usability while preserving key
event sourcing benefits such as auditability and replayability?
The evaluation results indicate that the proposed method and framework substantially
reduce the perceived complexity typically associated with event sourcing, while improving
usability for developers in small company contexts. Participants consistently described
the structure as intuitive and approachable, aided by familiar patterns such as repository-
based CRUD operations. This usability was quantitatively supported by a high average
SUS score of 89, placing the method in the excellent usability range.
Qualitative feedback further reinforced that core features, such as replay, traceability, and
personal data separation, were not only preserved but viewed as valuable. Replay func-
tionality, in particular, was identified as the most impactful feature, enabling time-travel
debugging and support scenarios that are otherwise difficult to implement in conventional
systems. The ability to inspect full entity histories and isolate personal data for GDPR
compliance were also recognized as practical benefits.
While the evaluation did not measure these capabilities in high-load or distributed environ-
ments, participants found them directly applicable to day-to-day development and support
needs. The simplified model trades reactive features and fine-grained event semantics
for a more accessible and manageable implementation. In the scope of small companies,
this trade-off appears justified and effective.
In summary, the evaluation suggests that the method achieves its goal of making event
sourcing more accessible by lowering complexity and improving usability, while retaining
the benefits that make the pattern valuable in the first place.

11.3
Contribution to Knowledge
This thesis contributes new insights into how event sourcing can be adapted to the real-
ities of small company contexts through deliberate simplification of both method and im-
plementation. While event sourcing is typically associated with complex, enterprise-scale
architectures, this research demonstrates that all of its core benefits, such as traceability,
auditability, and replayability, can still be retained within a substantially simplified archi-
tecture, at least in small-scale use cases.
The primary contribution is a conceptual method and lightweight supporting framework de-
signed to eliminate the adoption barriers such as the steep learning curve. By abstracting
away low-level mechanics such as event persistence, replay handling, and projection re-
building, and exposing familiar CRUD-style interfaces, the framework enables developers
with limited experience in event sourcing to work productively and confidently. This is sup-
ported by empirical evaluation, including a high average SUS score (89) and consistent
positive feedback from developers during usability and business value assessments.
A secondary contribution is the identification and explanation of key design trade-offs
made to improve accessibility. For example, using synchronous in-process execution re-
duces infrastructural complexity and improves developer approachability, but limits scalab-
ility and reactive capabilities. Likewise, adopting CRUD-like abstractions eases onboard-
ing and aligns with familiar development models, but may obscure event semantics and
reduce domain expressiveness over time. These trade-offs provide practical guidance
for others seeking to simplify event sourcing in constrained environments, highlighting
the balance between simplicity and fidelity in socio-technical system design.
In addition, this work contributes to Design Science Research by demonstrating how con-
textual factors, such as limited team size, infrastructure constraints, and reliance on fa-
miliar tooling, shape the requirements and design of technical artifacts. The framework
and method are not intended as universal solutions but rather as context-specific inter-
ventions that directly address barriers identified in the literature. In doing so, the research
offers actionable knowledge for both practitioners and researchers seeking to apply event
sourcing in environments where conventional solutions may be infeasible.

11.4
Threats to Validity
While the evaluation provided valuable insights into the usability and perceived benefits
of the proposed event sourcing method and framework, several limitations must be ac-
knowledged.
Participants were guided through structured tasks using a pre-configured template and
examples, potentially influencing their responses. The presence of the facilitator, also
the author, may have introduced unintentional bias, making it difficult to isolate whether
positive feedback came from the artifacts themselves or the evaluation setup.
The study was conducted within a single small company (CON-LINQ) with a limited num-
ber of participants, reducing generalizability. Since the same case was used for both de-
velopment and evaluation, potential bias and context-specific influence cannot be ruled
out. Broader validation across different teams and domains is needed.
The evaluation focused on basic features and did not test the method under complex or
long-term operational conditions. The use of examples and step-by-step instructions likely
improved the onboarding experience but does not fully represent how real-world adoption
would unfold.
Finally, both interviews and SUS scores captured short-term, subjective perceptions. No
extended follow-up was carried out to evaluate the maintainability or sustained usability
of the framework over time.
These constraints suggest that while the findings are promising for small company con-
texts, further research is required to confirm the method’s broader applicability, scalability,
and durability over time.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to investigate the barriers that hinder small companies from adopting
event sourcing and to explore how the approach could be simplified without compromising
its foundational strengths. Through the use of Design Science Research, a conceptual
method and supporting framework were developed, demonstrated, and evaluated in the
context of a real-world case study.
The findings confirm that many of the known challenges in event sourcing, such as steep
learning curves, architectural complexity, and infrastructure demands, are particularly
acute in small company environments.
These were addressed through a set of tar-
geted design choices, including the CRUD-based repository abstractions, synchronous
processing, built-in replay support, and lightweight, standalone deployment. These adapt-
ations significantly improved usability and accessibility, enabling developers to implement
and understand event sourcing more easily.
Evaluation results, including a high average SUS score and positive qualitative feedback,
indicate that the method and framework made event sourcing more approachable without
sacrificing essential benefits like auditability, traceability, and replayability. However, the
study also identified trade-offs. Simplifications such as synchronous execution and re-
duced domain expressiveness may limit scalability, long-term flexibility, and semantic
richness. These trade-offs reflect a conscious design focus on small-scale applicability,
but they also point to potential limitations for broader or more complex use cases.
While the artifacts performed well in their intended setting, the evaluation’s scope, limited
to one organization, one system, and short-term use, means that broader generalizabil-
ity remains uncertain. The work offers an encouraging foundation, but further research
is needed to explore how the approach performs under more demanding, evolving, or
distributed conditions.
In total, this thesis contributes both a practical tool and actionable knowledge for simplify-
ing event sourcing in constrained environments. It highlights that meaningful simplification
is possible, if carefully designed, and that even in small teams, the core benefits of event
sourcing can be made accessible through thoughtful abstraction and alignment with de-
veloper needs.

13
Directions for Future Work
This thesis focused on simplifying event sourcing for new systems in small company con-
texts, but several areas remain for future exploration.
First, the framework could be extended to support the migration of existing systems, es-
pecially those built on relational databases. Many small teams already use SQL-based
infrastructure, and introducing event sourcing alongside existing tables, rather than repla-
cing them, could make adoption more feasible. Adding support for relational persistence
and better integration with widely used ORMs such as Entity Framework would align the
framework more closely with typical small team technology stacks.
Second, while the current framework prioritizes simplicity through synchronous execution,
introducing optional support for asynchronous messaging and pub-sub mechanisms could
enhance scalability and reactive capabilities. This would allow more advanced use cases,
such as triggering side effects or handling background processing.
Third, broader validation is needed to test the robustness and generalizability of the frame-
work. The current evaluation was limited to a single organization and a relatively simple
use case. Future research should involve more diverse domains, increased complex-
ity, and long-term usage to assess maintainability, adaptability, and performance under
evolving conditions.
Lastly, improved developer tooling, such as scaffolding features to generate boilerplate
code and managing replay, could further lower the entry barrier. These tools would sup-
port faster onboarding and more consistent adoption, especially for teams new to event
sourcing.

F.1
Evaluation Introduction Text
Event sourcing is a way of building systems where every change to application state is
stored as an immutable event, rather than simply updating records in place. Instead of
saving only the latest version of an object, as in traditional CRUD systems, you cap-
ture a chronological sequence of events such as QuoteCreated, QuoteUpdated, and
QuoteDeleted. This enables complete reconstruction of an entity’s history, supports ad-
vanced debugging through time-travel, and allows for safe reprocessing of events when
business logic changes.
The key benefits of event sourcing include full traceability, auditability, historical debug-
ging, and the ability to test new features without affecting the live system. Despite these
advantages, event sourcing is often seen as too complex for small teams due to steep
learning curves, unclear implementation guidance, and heavy infrastructure demands. It
can be difficult to know where to start, and many existing solutions require deep know-
ledge of domain-driven design or event-driven architectures.
This framework is designed to remove those barriers. It requires only MongoDB, remains
lightweight, and introduces clear patterns for defining domain entities, migrations, and
projections. Sensitive data is handled, and tooling is included for replay, snapshots, and
debugging. The goal is to make event sourcing accessible without losing its core bene-
fits by abstracting away low-level event mechanics and exposing a familiar CRUD-style
interface for everyday use.
