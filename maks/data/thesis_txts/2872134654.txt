Abstract
This thesis investigates the integration of variable wind power into small, isolated
power systems, using the Icelandic transmission grid as a case study. With rising
electricity demand and a growing interest in wind energy, understanding the grids
ability to accommodate variability has become essential. A simpliﬁed but represen-
tative model of the Icelandic system was developed in PowerFactory and validated
against operational data. Two 120 MW wind farms were added at Blanda and Búr-
fell, and 33 years of hourly wind data were used to simulate realistic production
variability.
Results show that Cut IIIB and Cut VI are the systems most critical bottlenecks.
Cut IIIB is already overloaded under base conditions, and variability at Blanda can
cause power ﬂow excursions up to ±108 MW, exceeding its already raised 170 MW
limit. Cut VI is also frequently congested. These ﬁndings highlight the need to
reinforce the northern transmission corridor by upgrading it to 220 kV. However,
reinforcements alone may not suﬃce.
The study evaluates demand-side ﬂexibility as a mitigation strategy, introducing
controllable loads at FLJ and BRE. Two control approaches are tested: one-to-one
tracking and selective proportional control. While the latter performs better, no
tested conﬁguration fully prevents constraint violations under all conditions. Results
also show that adding load at BRE is often counterproductive, increasing stress on
both Cut IIIB and Cut VI. A distributed and well-placed load strategy, ideally near
wind generation sites, is necessary.
In conclusion, integrating wind energy in isolated systems requires both infras-
tructure upgrades and coordinated, adaptive ﬂexibility strategies to manage vari-
ability and maintain stability.

1
Introduction
The transition to a carbon-neutral energy sector demands a signiﬁcant increase in the
production of renewable energy, particularly wind and solar power. However, the inherent
variability and intermittency of wind generation present challenges for grid stability and
reliability, especially in smaller, isolated power systems. Unlike larger systems that beneﬁt
from interconnections with neighboring grids, islanded systems face unique constraints,
which limit the applicability of traditional solutions and can weaken system stability, for
example in terms of frequency and voltage. Addressing these challenges requires speciﬁc
strategies to ensure a stable integration of wind energy without compromising the overall
reliability of the power system.
In this context, Iceland serves as a particularly relevant case study due to its fully is-
landed power system and its dependence on renewable energy. In recent years, Iceland has
expressed a growing interest in diversifying its renewable portfolio, particularly through
the development of wind power to meet the increasing demand driven by electriﬁcation,
decarbonization eﬀorts, and rising industrial demand. The main objective of this thesis is
to quantify the impact of integrating wind power into Icelands transmission network, with
a particular focus on its hourly variability, and to explore how ﬂexibility could be used to
mitigate resulting operational stresses.
To this end, a simpliﬁed but representative model of the Icelandic transmission system
was developed from the ground up in PowerFactory, based on national data sources and
inputs provided by Landsnet and Eﬂa Engineering. A Python-based automation frame-
work was implemented to carry out parametric studies and simulate various scenarios of
wind power dispatch and load ﬂexibility. 33 years of hourly wind speed time series were
used for the scenarios. Then two wind farms are added to the system, and the propaga-
tion of their hourly production variations is analyzed with respect to system constraints,
including line loading, voltage limits, and critical transmission cuts. The load ﬂexibility
is used to absorb variability and enhance the system stability.
This thesis combines: data-based modeling of a real power system, sensitivity anal-
ysis of local injections and withdrawals, application of linear superposition principles to
variability propagation, evaluation of demand-side control strategies using ﬂexible loads.
By comparing diﬀerent compensation strategies, with diﬀerent degrees of complex-
ity, this work aims to provide insight into the technical limitations and possible control
solutions for integrating variable renewable generation in small islanded systems.

The Icelandic power system
2.1
System Overview and Rising Electricity Demand
The Icelandic transmission grid is owned and operated by Landsnet, the national
Transmission System Operator. In 2022, the total energy fed into the grid was approxi-
mately 20 TWh, while the installed capacity was around 2,800 MW. Electricity in Iceland
is produced exclusively from renewable sources, with 70% of the energy coming from
hydropower and 29% from geothermal sources in 2022 [1].
The grid is divided into three main categories: the primary transmission system op-
erating at 132 kV and 220 kV, respectively in red and green on Figure 1, which forms a
ring around the island; smaller regional systems operating at 132 kV, 66 kV (in blue), and
33 kV (in yellow); and individual connections to major industrial consumers. At the end
of 2023, the Landsnet network included 86 substations and 92 delivery points, of which
20 were power plants, 10 for directly connected large consumers, and 62 for distribution
utilities. The southwestern part of the system operates at 220 kV, linking the Þjórsá pro-
duction area with the high-demand zones in the Reykjavik Region and the industrial area
in Hvalfjörður. From the Capital Region, there is currently a single 132 kV connection
to Reykjanes. The ring-shaped grid, primarily operating at 132 kV, connects the various
consumption and generation areas around the island. Built between 1972 and 1984, this
infrastructure signiﬁcantly improved the reliability of the power supply at the time. From
the northern part of the ring at Hrútafjörður, a 132 kV line supplies electricity to the
entire Westfjords, terminating at Mjólká. In the eastern region, two parallel 220 kV lines
connect the Fljótsdalur power station to the Alcoa aluminum smelter in Reyðarfjörður [2].

The Icelandic power system
The main production company in Iceland is Landsvirkjun ,the national power com-
pany, that accounts for 73% of the electricity production as presented in Figure 3. Iceland’s
abundant water resources, fed by glacial meltwater, make hydropower the largest contribu-
tor to the country’s renewable energy portfolio. These glacial rivers help maintain a steady
water supply for the hydropower system. As seen in Figure 2, Hydropower accounts for
approximately 70% of Iceland’s installed power capacity, with generation concentrated in
three key areas: the East, North, and South-West. The system includes both large and
small plants, some of which have reservoirs that allow for energy storage, making them
highly ﬂexible.
Iceland is also rich in geothermal resources, thanks to its location in an active volcanic
zone with over 200 volcanoes. Geothermal production is concentrated in three main areas:
the Northeast, the Reykjanes Peninsula, and the Capital Region.
In 2022, the total
installed geothermal capacity was around 760 MW, or 29.4% as shown in Figure 2 .

A signiﬁcant portion of Icelands energy production, 78%, is consumed by energy-

Planning the future transmission system poses signiﬁcant challenges, particularly in
meeting the growing demand for power transmission. Growth in electricity demand is
driven by new large-scale users such as food production, materials processing, and the ex-
pansion of data centers, while no signiﬁcant increase is expected from aluminum smelters.
Figure 5 shows that by 2050, electricity demand for general use, large-scale users, and
energy exchange powered directly by electricity is projected to reach approximately 29.3
TWh an increase of 8.8 TWh from 2024. The transition to a carbon-neutral energy sector
requires signiﬁcant changes in the energy market, and a signiﬁcant increase in generation
[2].
2.2
Need for New Generation and Grid Reinforcements
In order to meet rising electricity demand driven by electriﬁcation of transport, new
industry, and climate goals, Iceland plans to increase its installed capacity. While hydro
and geothermal remain the backbone of the system, signiﬁcant investments in wind power
are expected.
As of 2022, Icelands installed wind energy capacity was only 1.8 MW,
accounting for a mere 0.03% of total energy production, Figure 2. In the "Master Plan
for Nature Protection and Energy Utilization", [4], 18 wind power projects are being
considered in the fourth phase of development. These projects would add approximately
3,300MW of capacity, distributed over all the territory as shown in Figure 6, reﬂecting
Iceland’s commitment to diversifying its energy mix and incorporating more wind power
into its renewable portfolio [5].

Figure 6: Geographic distribution of potential wind projects under Phase 4 of the Master

Nevertheless, the eﬀective integration of this additional wind capacity remains a chal-
lenge due to the structural limitations of the existing grid. The Icelandic power system
relies on a ring-shaped transmission network that connects areas of high generation with
those of high consumption. Unfortunately, this grid often operates with congested lines,
bringing the system close to instability and making the integration of wind power, with
its inherent ﬂuctuations, particularly challenging [6].
To address these constraints, Landsnets long-term strategy includes reinforcing the 220
kV backbone of the system. One key goal is to complete the ring at 220 kV, notably by
establishing a southern connection between Fljótsdalur and Suðurland, from FLJ to SUL
on Figure 1, and another one in the north connecting Rangárvellir to Reykjavík, from
RAN to REY on Figure 1. This would help alleviate existing bottlenecks and provide the
necessary capacity to integrate new renewable generation, especially in remote or wind-rich
regions. Other planned developments include substation upgrades, smart grid initiatives,
and targeted ﬂexibility strategies.
While detailed studies of power system cuts and ﬂow limits are addressed later in
this report, it is important to note that multiple bottlenecks already limit the system’s
operational ﬂexibility. Without proper reinforcements, new generation capacity may be
curtailed or delayed due to insuﬃcient transfer capability between production and demand
zones.

Theoretical framework
Having introduced the main characteristics, challenges, and future development goals

3.1
Fundamentals of Power Flow Analysis
The power ﬂow problem is the calculation of the voltage magnitude and phase angle
at each bus of a power system operating under balanced three-phase steady-state condi-
tions. It also includes the determination of active and reactive power ﬂows throughout
the network components such as generators, loads, transmission lines, and transformers,
as well as the associated system losses. The power system is ﬁrst represented as a single-
line diagram, and the required input data include bus speciﬁcations, transmission line
parameters, and transformer characteristics.
Each bus k in the system is associated with four key variables: the net active power
Pk, the reactive power Qk, the voltage magnitude Vk, and the voltage phase angle δk. For
each bus, two of these quantities are speciﬁed as known inputs, while the remaining two
are determined through the power ﬂow calculation.

PGk corresponds to the active power injected by a generator into the bus, following the
generation convention, while PLk refers to the active power consumed by a load, in the
load convention. The same applies to reactive power.
Depending on which quantities are known before running the load ﬂow, three diﬀerent
types of buses can be identiﬁed:
• Load bus (PQ bus): P and Q are speciﬁed, while V and δ are calculated. If the
bus has no generation, then Pk = −PLk is negative, and for a typical inductive load,
Qk = −QLk is also negative.
• Voltage-controlled bus (PV bus): P and V are given as inputs, and Q and δ
are computed. This type of bus is commonly associated with generators, switched
shunt capacitors, or static var systems. The model also requires the maximum and
minimum reactive power limits that the equipment can supply. If one of these limits
is reached, the reactive power is held at the limit, and the bus is treated as a PQ
bus. This category can also include buses with tap-changing transformers, in which
case the tap setting is computed during the power ﬂow.
• Slack bus (swing bus): V and δ are speciﬁed, usually set to 1̸ 0, and P and Q
are determined by the solver. There is only one slack bus in the system, acting as
the global reference for voltage magnitude and angle.
The transmission lines are represented using their Π equivalent model, as shown in
Figure 8. Each line is deﬁned by its series impedance Z′ and its total shunt admittance
Y ′, which is equally split between both ends of the line. The model also includes the
maximum power rating of the line in MVA, as well as the identiﬁcation of the two buses
it connects, noted (k, i).

In steady-state simulations, as the ones realized in this study, loads are typically mod-

sented as constant active power sources with ﬁxed voltage magnitude at their correspond-
ing bus. At least one generator bus must be deﬁned as a slack bus to account for active
power losses in the system. It also serves as the voltage magnitude and angle reference
for the power ﬂow calculation.
In contrast, during transient analysis, synchronous generators are modeled using dif-
ferential equations that describe the dynamics between ﬂux linkages and stator/rotor
currents, based on the machine’s initial state.
3.2
Nodal power ﬂow formulation
A fundamental step in solving the power ﬂow problem is the mathematical formula-
tion of the electrical network using nodal analysis. However, classical nodal analysis is
not directly applicable to power system studies, as the components are modeled diﬀer-
ently. In particular, loads are speciﬁed by their active and reactive power rather than
by impedances, and generators are represented as power sources rather than voltage or
current sources. While traditional linear circuit analysis leads to linear algebraic equa-
tions that can be solved using direct methods, the power ﬂow problem involves nonlinear
algebraic equations due to the constant power characteristics of loads and generators. As
a result, the nodal analysis must be adapted, and the solution typically requires iterative
numerical methods, such as the Newton-Raphson algorithm.
As in any nodal approach, a nodal matrix must ﬁrst be deﬁned, the bus admittance
matrix Ybus. This matrix is constructed using the system’s transmission line and trans-
former data. The diagonal elements Ykk represent the sum of all admittances connected to
bus k, while the oﬀ-diagonal elements Ykn (for k ̸= n) are the negatives of the admittances
between buses k and n. An example of the contribution of a single transmission line, as
described in Figure 8, to the admittance matrix is shown in Figure 10.

3.4
Frequency Control and Primary Frequency Regulation
In the context of this study, which investigates the hourly variability of power gen-
eration, understanding power-frequency control is key to examining how the system can
maintain stability under changing generation conditions.
Frequency is a system-wide parameter that reﬂects the balance between active power gen-
eration, consumption, and system losses. In a power system, all synchronous machines
rotate at the same speed, ensuring that the frequency is uniform throughout the network.
Consequently, in large interconnected systems such as the European grid, a change in
demand in Spain can instantaneously aﬀect the operating conditions of generators as far
as Northern Germany.
Maintaining the power equilibrium and system frequency close to its nominal value is
essential to avoid equipment damage and ensure reliable system performance. Signiﬁcant
frequency drops can lead to increased magnetizing currents in transformers and induc-
tion motors, potentially causing overloads. Furthermore, if not quickly addressed, such
drops may trigger the disconnection of loads and generators, worsening the imbalance and
potentially leading to blackouts.
Traditionally, frequency stability is maintained by generators adjusting their output
to compensate for active power imbalances. This process is structured into several layers
of control:
• Primary Frequency Control:
This is an automatic, decentralized response that aims to stabilize frequency within
30 seconds following a disturbance.
The frequency may settle at an oﬀ-nominal
but acceptable value. This response is mainly provided by the speed governors of
synchronous machines.
• Secondary Frequency Control:
Also automatic, this control restores the frequency to its nominal value over a pe-
riod of several minutes by deploying system-wide frequency containment reserves. In
large interconnected grids, decentralized frequency restoration is implemented, en-
suring each Transmission System Operator (TSO) restores its own generation-load
balance and reestablishes scheduled power exchanges with neighboring TSOs.
• Tertiary Frequency Control:
Activated manually and centrally by the TSO control center, this control restores
the operating reserves and anticipates forecasted imbalances. It can also substitute
secondary control if unavailable. Activation times range from 15 minutes to several
hours.

Wind Variability and Integration Challenges
The previous chapter introduced the theoretical framework used to model power ﬂows
and system dynamics, with a focus on frequency control and operational behavior in iso-
lated grids. Building on this foundation, the next chapter explores the speciﬁc challenges
posed by the integration of wind energy, particularly its variability and system-wide im-
pact.
This analysis begins with a description of how wind energy is converted into electrical
power and how wind turbine behavior inﬂuences production patterns. It then moves to a
statistical characterization of Icelandic wind data and discusses the implications for grid
stability in small, weak power systems.
4.1
Wind Power Conversion and Turbine Characteristics
Wind turbines convert the kinetic energy of moving air into electrical energy. As the
wind ﬂows over the rotor blades, it creates a pressure diﬀerence that causes the rotor to
spin. This mechanical rotation is transferred to a generator, producing electricity. The
power output of a wind turbine can be estimated using the following equation:
P = 1
2ρAV 3
(21)
where:
• P is the power extracted from the wind [W],
• ρ is the air density [kg/m3],
• A is the swept area of the rotor blades [m2],
• V is the wind speed [m/s].
This cubic dependence on wind speed shows the importance of site selection and local
wind conditions. Modern wind turbines are designed to operate eﬃciently within speciﬁc
wind speed thresholds. For the Enercon E-138 EP3 (4.26 MW), used in this study, power
generation begins at a cut-in speed of 2 m/s and reaches its rated capacity at 13 m/s.
From 13 to 20 m/s, the output remains constant, after which it decreases gradually to
protect the turbine, ceasing completely at the cut-out speed of 28 m/s.

4.3
Challenges of Integrating Intermittent Renewables in Small
and Weak Power Systems
In large and strongly meshed continental grids, renewable variability can often be
absorbed and redistributed over multiple paths and regions.
However, in smaller and
weaker systems like Iceland’s, the structure is far more constrained.
The grid is not
strongly meshed, with only a few major routes for power transfers between production
and demand centers. This makes the system more vulnerable to local imbalances.
Integration of intermittent resources like wind can cause large ﬂuctuations in power
ﬂow, voltage, and frequency. Due to the limited number of directions in which power can
be transferred, variability at a given location can aﬀect a large portion of the system. The

weak system strength (low short-circuit power) also limits the ability to stabilize these
ﬂuctuations quickly.
This challenge is ampliﬁed when wind production increases rapidly in one region while

can lead to stressed operating conditions, increased losses, or even curtailments.
The
results of the variability analysis in this study show that such situations are likely to

and distributed ﬂexibility strategies.

5
Development and validation of the model
The previous chapter highlighted the characteristics and impact of wind variability on
small and weak power systems such as Icelands. It also emphasized the need for models
and control strategies capable of capturing the operational eﬀects of integrating variable
renewables. To address these challenges, this chapter outlines the structure and develop-
ment of the transmission system model used in this study. Built from national data and
reﬁned with input from Landsnet, the model enables system-level analysis by aggregating
the grid into representative zones and validating against real operational scenarios.
The model was developed in several stages, starting with the identiﬁcation of major
generation and consumption areas across Iceland, along with their approximate location
and size. These were grouped into aggregated zones to form a simpliﬁed yet representative
structure.
Each zone was assigned to a hypothetical bus, and selected transmission lines were used
to connect them based on the actual grid. Two control room snapshots were then used to
deﬁne realistic dispatch conditions and reﬁne the model further. These diagrams helped
align the simpliﬁed structure with actual infrastructure and introduced new elements such
as reactive compensation devices. The imaginary buses were mapped to real substations
to enhance spatial realism.
These control room scenarios were also used to validate the ﬁnal PowerFactory model.
By comparing simulated power ﬂows and electrical quantities to those observed in the
control room, it was possible to conﬁrm that the model accurately reﬂects the operational
behavior of the Icelandic power system.

Use of PowerFactory and Python API development
The simulations presented in this thesis are performed using PowerFactory 2024, a
commercial software developed by DIgSILENT for advanced power system analysis. Pow-
erFactory oﬀers a comprehensive set of functionalities, including load ﬂow calculations,
short-circuit analysis, dynamic simulations, and stability studies, making it particularly
suitable for both transmission and distribution network applications.
In the context of this work, PowerFactory is used to represent a simpliﬁed version of the
Icelandic transmission system, incorporating wind farms, aggregated loads, transformers,
and transmission lines. One of the main objectives is to investigate the impact of wind
generation variability on system operation. To support this, a fully automated simulation
framework was developed using PowerFactorys Python scripting interface.
The set of
scripts created allows the software to perform parametric studies in which generation
and load levels, as well as other relevant parameters, can be systematically varied across
multiple scenarios.

6
Methodology development
This section presents the methodology used to evaluate the impact of wind power
variability on the Icelandic transmission system. It builds upon the validated base case
model by introducing two wind farms, each composed of 28 turbines rated at 4.26 MW,
amounting to 119.28 MW per site, located at the BLA and SUL/BUR buses, as described
in section 4. Since the consumption in Iceland is mostly industrial and highly limited by
the generation available it has been decided that the extra generation provided by the
wind farm would be consumed by two new loads matching the size of the wind farms and
located in FLJ and BRE. These two locations are currently being considered for power-
to-X projects involving electrolyzers, and could enable the type of demand-side ﬂexibility
investigated in this study.
Before simulating wind ﬂuctuations, operational limits and system security constraints
are deﬁned, notably through the use of critical transmission cuts and voltage thresholds.
These constraints form the basis of the feasibility criteria applied throughout the analysis.
This part of the study represents a key development stage of the thesis. The core
objective of this methodology is to assess how wind variability propagates through the
system and to identify feasible control strategies for managing its eﬀects.
Two main
regulation approaches are considered: one in which variability is absorbed by the system’s
existing power-frequency regulation (i.e., absorbed by the existing synchronous machines),
and another in which dedicated ﬂexible loads (FLJ and BRE) are controlled to follow wind
generation ﬂuctuations.
To achieve this, a step-by-step methodology has been followed. First, a sensitivity anal-
ysis is conducted to determine how active power variations at the four key locations,BLA,
BUR, BRE, and FLJ, aﬀect critical system cuts. This step helps deﬁne local variation
margins for each node and each cut. However, since these results only reﬂect isolated
variations, they must be complemented by an analysis of combined eﬀects.
Therefore, the next step is to verify whether combinations of variations can be handled
using the principle of linearity. Once validated, this linearity is exploited using time series
of wind power variations to quantify their impact on the system, ﬁrst under the assumption
that variability is absorbed by regulation, and later under the hypothesis that a controller
directs the new loads to follow the wind farms’ ﬂuctuations.
6.1
Operational ranges and security constraints
6.1.1
Deﬁnition and role of transmission Cuts
In assessing the stability of the Icelandic power system, Landsnet relies on a series of
transmission bottlenecks called cuts (sníð), which simplify the evaluation of large-scale
power ﬂows between geographically distinct regions. These cuts play a crucial role in both
planning and operational decision-making, as they allow for the monitoring of directional
ﬂows and early detection of system stress, particularly during contingencies or shifts in
production and load. Each cut is deﬁned by a directional power ﬂow through a selection
of transmission lines, and it measures the net active power transfer between two areas. A
maximum admissible power ﬂow has been determined for each cuts based on operational
experience and several technical constraints, including thermal limits of the transmission
lines, voltage stability, frequency stability, equipment ratings, N-1 security criteria or
operational status of wide-area control schemes (WACS), [2]. They are particularly useful
for simplifying the evaluation of global stability, as they oﬀer a simple view of how power
ﬂows across critical boundaries. In this project, cuts IIIb, IV and VI are considered and

6.2
Nodal sensitivity analysis for wind and load injection
In this section, a sensitivity analysis is conducted to assess the systems response to
local variations in generation and load. The objective is to evaluate how small changes in
speciﬁc nodes impact the overall network ﬂows, cut values, and stability indicators. The
overall goal is to see how much wind can be added to the system and how much variability
can be handled without crossing the voltage, line loading or cuts limits.
Four scenarios are analyzed individually:
• An increase of 10MW in wind power production at Blanda (BLA).
• An increase of 10MW in wind power production at Búrfell (BUR).
• An increase of 10MW in load consumption at Brennimelur (BRE).
• An increase of 10MW in load consumption at Fljóstdalur (FLJ).
For each case, the base case corresponds to Scenario 1 from the control room. The
method consists in introducing the variation at the target node while keeping all other
operating conditions unchanged. This approach allows for analyzing both the local impact
and the propagation of the eﬀects throughout the system. The variations are deliberately
limited to 10 MW to remain within the linear operating range, thereby avoiding the acti-
vation of operational constraints or non-linear phenomena. This ensures that the results
accurately reﬂect the system’s sensitivity under normal conditions.

This section presents the key ﬁndings of the study, structured around two main areas
of analysis. First, the impact of wind variability on critical transmission cuts only compen-

in wind power injections. This analysis highlights the transmission corridors most sensitive
to ﬂuctuations and identiﬁes the main bottlenecks under current system conditions.
Second, the analysis looks at how load ﬂexibility could help ease congestion on critical
cuts. Two control strategies are tested to see how eﬀective they are in mitigating the
impact of variable renewable generation. This provides a ﬁrst look at how demand-side
actions might support system operation in the face of increasing variability, and highlights
some of the current networks limitations.

7.2
Evaluation of load ﬂexibility for mitigation
In this section, the two new loads installed in BRE and FLJ are used to compensate
for the additional wind power production or its variability, rather than relying on power-
frequency regulation. This strategy assumes the existence of a load control system that
enables these loads, data centers or electrolysers for instance, to dynamically follow wind
power ﬂuctuations.
The main goal is to improve system stability while also allowing for higher electricity
demand, especially in isolated systems like Iceland. Based on the previous results, two
key operational priorities emerge: reducing loading on Cut IIIB and reducing the loading
on Cut VI.
Given that the system responds approximately linearly to variations, the eﬀects of
wind generation and load adjustments can be combined using linear superposition. This
principle allows us to evaluate combinations of wind generation changes (at BLA and
BUR) and load adjustments (at BRE and FLJ) that relieve critical cuts.
The analysis uses the impact coeﬃcients from Table 10 and Table 11. For each of the
four wind variation directions (increase/decrease at BLA and BUR), we determine the
corresponding load variation that minimizes stress on the system.

Two types of control strategies will be presented to assess how the new loads can help

• One-to-one tracking: Each wind farm is paired with a single load, which is as-

from the wind farm at BLA, while the load at FLJ tracks the one at SUL.
• Selective proportional control: This strategy distinguishes between positive and
negative variations, allocating them to the most beneﬁcial load. For example, pos-
itive deviations may be assigned primarily to FLJ, and negative ones to BRE, in
accordance with the previous analysis.

This scenario causes critical overloads in several areas of the grid:
• The line RA1–BL2, connecting BLA to RAN, reaches 123% loading after the vari-
ability event.
• The line KR2, between KRA and FLJ, hits its maximum capacity at 100%.
• The lower ring, consisting of lines TE1–HO1–HP1–PB1–SI4 experiences a combined
loading up to 144%, which represents a serious operational risk for the system.
Alternatively, if the variability is absorbed entirely at BRE, the overload shifts to other
critical paths:
• The lines between BLA and HRU exceed 113% of their thermal limit.
• In addition, the voltage at node HRU drops to 0.91 pu, below voltage stability limits.
These results demonstrate that compensating all the wind variability in a single load
location, whether FLJ or BRE, can lead to unacceptable operating conditions.
This
reinforces the need for a distributed or coordinated control strategy that considers both
the spatial distribution of variability and the network’s capacity to absorb it without
triggering additional constraint violations.

Results and discussions
These results conﬁrm that selective proportional control oﬀers a more eﬀective and ﬂex-
ible alternative to one-to-one tracking. While trade-oﬀs between cuts remain inevitable,
the tested strategies, especially Case F, demonstrate that a well-designed sharing of vari-
ability between ﬂexible loads can signiﬁcantly reduce overload risks on the most critical
parts of the network.
From an implementation perspective, Case F appears easier to apply, as it does not
require identifying which wind farm experiences the variation. A system frequency mea-
surement could in principle be used to trigger proportional responses, similarly to primary
frequency control through droop. However, relying on frequency alone does not allow
distinguishing the location of the variability. As such, implementing Case F would not be
feasible using a fully decentralized approach and would instead require a more centralized
and complex control scheme, similar to secondary control, to enable this type of selective
response.

Conclusion
8
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the integration of variable wind power into the Icelandic trans-
mission system, focusing on the challenges that arise in small, isolated power grids. Ice-
lands ring-shaped network and high share of renewable energy make it a particularly
relevant case for studying how wind variability propagates and aﬀects system stability.
A simpliﬁed yet representative model of the Icelandic grid was developed from scratch
in PowerFactory, incorporating two 120 MW wind farms at Blanda (BLA) and Búrfell
(BUR), and validated against operational control room data. A Python-based framework
allowed for automated scenario testing based on 33 years of hourly wind speed data. The
model was used to assess both the propagation of variability and the capacity of load-side
ﬂexibility to mitigate its eﬀects. The absorption of wind variability by power-frequency
regulation and by load-side ﬂexibility was compared.
The results highlight two critical transmission bottlenecks: Cut IIIB and Cut VI.
Cut IIIB, linking the northeast to the main consumption center in the west, is already
frequently overloaded under current conditions. Without any ﬂexibility, wind variability
at Blanda can cause excursions of up to ±108 MW, which may push the ﬂow well beyond
the current 170 MW limit of Cut IIIB, already raised from its original 130 MW threshold,
thereby signiﬁcantly stressing this constraint. Cut VI, which supplies the southwestern
industrial region, may be overloaded around 92% of the time depending on the initial
dispatch, though the relative severity of the overloads is generally lower than on Cut IIIB.
These ﬁndings clearly underline the need to reinforce the northern ring, in particular by
upgrading 132 kV lines to 220 kV and raising the Cut IIIB limit to 250 MW. However,
this reinforcement alone may not be suﬃcient, and additional upgrades on the southern
corridorespecially across Cut VIshould also be considered.
To complement these infrastructure upgrades, the study explored the potential of
demand-side ﬂexibility using two controllable loads located at FLJ and BRE. The objec-
tive was to dynamically adjust local consumption to track wind variability and alleviate
stress on critical transmission cuts. The results show that targeted ﬂexibility can be highly
eﬀective: increasing the FLJ load during wind surges and reducing the BRE load during
production drops consistently mitigates overloads on both Cut IIIB and Cut VI.
Two control strategies were compared. The ﬁrst, and simplest, is one-to-one tracking,
where each wind farm is paired with a single load. While this approach was somewhat
eﬀective at relieving Cut IIIB under certain conﬁgurations, it failed to address congestion
on Cut VI and sometimes worsened the loading on other cuts such as Cut IV. The second,
more adaptive strategy, selective proportional control, assigns wind variability to diﬀerent
loads depending on the direction of the deviation: positive deviations are mostly absorbed
by FLJ, and negative ones by BRE. This strategy proved to be the most eﬀective. One
conﬁguration (Case F) successfully reduced the maximum deviation on Cut IIIB to just
3 MW and kept Cut VI at its lowest observed level of 648 MW. Although this setup
caused a minor overload on Cut IV, it occurred only 0.04% of the time, making the trade-
oﬀacceptable.
However, none of the control strategies tested in this thesis were able to fully maintain
all deviations within secure operational limits under all conditions. This highlights a likely
need for local absorption mechanisms that is, having ﬂexible loads directly connected at or
near the wind farm sites to reduce the amount of variability propagating into the grid. The
placement of ﬂexible loads appears to be a crucial factor. In particular, the study shows
that adding extra load at BRE is not an eﬃcient solution, as it tends to increase congestion
on both Cut IIIB and Cut VI due to BREs already high baseline demand. Simulations
where all variability was compensated at a single location, either FLJ or BRE, also resulted
61
==PAGE:72==
8
Conclusion
in severe local line overloads and voltage drops. These results conﬁrm that compensation
must not only be ﬂexible, but also spatially distributed and coordinated with the systems
topology to avoid creating new bottlenecks. Future studies should consider alternative
locations for ﬂexible loads, ideally closer to generation sites or more evenly distributed
across the grid.
Another promising direction would be to implement an adaptive control scheme, which
adjusts the distribution of variability across the loads depending on its amplitude.
A
strategy combining load-side ﬂexibility with partial support from synchronous machines
was also tested during the study, but did not lead to improved results and was therefore
not detailed in this report.
Despite the promising results, this work has some limitations that should be acknowl-
edged. First, having a larger number of dispatch scenarios would have allowed for a more
comprehensive representation of the diﬀerent operating conditions that can occur in the
Icelandic grid, and would have provided a broader set of base cases for analyzing vari-
ability. Second, due to the use of an aggregated and simpliﬁed model, a certain degree
of accuracy is inevitably lost. Some transmission cuts are not represented in the model,
which means that part of the system’s constraints and potential issues may not be fully
captured.
Overall, this work shows that successfully integrating wind power in small islanded
systems like Icelands requires a combined approach. Grid reinforcements are essential, es-
pecially in the northern corridor, but operational solutions such as demand-side ﬂexibility
and intelligent control strategies play an important complementary role. While no control
strategy can relieve all constraints simultaneously, proportional and distributed ﬂexibility
result being a practical and eﬀective tool for managing wind variability, provided it is
coordinated across the network.
