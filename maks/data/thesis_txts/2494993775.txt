Abstract
The baking industry has been growing steadily for the last several years, and it is es-
timated that the baking market will keep increasing in the future. Ohmic heating is
a technology that can help improve some of the biggest challenges in the cake baking
industry regarding production time and energy savings. In order to have a crust forma-
tion the ohmic heating needs to be combined with conventional heating. The industrial
application of using ohmic heating in the baking industry is limited and more research
is required.
This project investigates the potential use of combined ohmic and conventional heating
processes for semisolid food baking. The voltage, time in the ohmic heater and time
in the conventional oven are investigated using Full Factorial Design and Response Sur-
face Methodology (RSM) in order to evaluate their effects on five key cake parameters.
The studied parameters are color formation, moisture content, expansion, hardness and
springiness. The results showed that voltage have no significant (p>0.05) influence on
any of the parameters. However, more applied voltage to the cake means it is baked
faster. The ohmic time and oven time affects the moisture content, expansion, hardness
and springiness significantly. The color formation on the crust is only determined by
the conventional oven.
Ohmic heating is up to 42% more energy efficient than conventional ovens. Combining
ohmic heating and conventional heating can reduce the baking time up to 64.3%. Ohmic
heating technology looks promising for cake baking but more research is required on
different recipes. The ohmic heating and conventional heating needs to be combined in
one unit to achieve the advantages of combining ohmic heating and conventional heating.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The European bakery market is one of the most vital bakery markets and has a size of
approximately 40 million ton products and €210 billion in sale. Approximately 8 million
ton of cake is consumed in Europe per year. It is estimated the European baking market
will increase by 3.3% till 2023 (Mahadew [2018]). In USA the baking industry has been
growing steadily in the past several years. Sales of cupcakes and cakes are expected to
increase by an average of 7.2% per year, totaling $754.8 million in 2021 (Business [2018]).
Conventional heating is a slow heating process and it takes a certain amount of time
for the heat to reach the center of the batter. One of the biggest challenges in the
cake baking industry is to minimize energy consumption and increase mass production
(Conforti [2015]). Ohmic heating is a high energy efficient heating process as almost
all of the electrical power supplied is transformed into heat. Ohmic heating is a fast
heating method compared to conventional heating because heat is generated within the
food material. Therefore, ohmic heating can reduce the baking time for cake and the
required energy for cake baking (Sensoy [2012]). However, using ohmic heating for cake
baking will give the cake a wet surface and no crust formation. Brown crust is one of
the main attributes of a cake. In order to meet consumer expectation the solution is to
combine ohmic heating with conventional heating.
The use of ohmic heating in food processing has existed since the early 1900s. However,
the use of ohmic heating slowed down in between the late 1930s to the 1960s due to
the cost of electricity and the lack of suitable electrode materials (Sensoy [2012]). The
interest in ohmic heating has reemerged and is used commercially for treating mixtures
of large particles and liquids effectively. However, the scientific research on the use of
ohmic heating in solid food is still limited.
The main objective in this study is to determine the possibility of using a combination of
ohmic heating and conventional heating for cake baking. Specific objectives include the
investigation of the influence of applied voltage, time in the ohmic heater, time in the
oven in regards to the process time and the quality of the cake. The baking process is
optimized in order to obtain the most desirable cake and a short baking time. The energy
requirement for baking a cake using ohmic heating is estimated in order to determine
the viability. In appendix A.1 a Gantt chart is available to show the overall timeline of
the project.

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
2.1
Cake baking
Bakery products, particularly bread, has a long history and may have started as early
as 23000 years ago due to people discovering wheat and being able to bake it on stones
heated by fire (Zhou et al. [2014]). For centuries cake baking changed very little and
it required long hours of labor. Today a lot of different cakes are available. The cakes
range a lot in complexity from consisting of simple ingredients to more advanced cakes
containing numerous components. Today, cakes are often characterized by a sweet taste,
pleasant flavors and a tender texture (Conforti [2015]).
2.2
Ingredients
A basic recipe of pound cake utilizes flour, sugar, eggs, butter, milk, baking powder and
vanilla extract. Each ingredient contribute to a particular aspect of the quality of the
cake, e.g. flavor, texture and moisture content (Conforti [2015]).
2.2.1
Flour
Flour is a compound made by milling grains. Flour provides structure, texture and taste
to the cake. Flour contains starch which is a polysaccharide. The starch is insoluble in
water, but can however absorb water and cause the granules to swell. This process is
called starch gelatinization and it is an irreversible process when a certain temperature
is reached. Starch gelatinization is affected by the type or starch, temperature and the
availability of water (Lund [2009]).
In flour there are two important proteins called gliadin and glutenin. When these pro-
teins are mixed with water they form an elastic network called gluten.
This elastic
property of gluten then works with the gases produced by leavening agents. The gases
inflate the batter like a balloon, which is what causes the batter to rise. When the
batter is baked it hardens in its inflated state, giving the cake its structure. The protein
content depends on the type of grain used, and a low protein content is usually preferred
in cakes (Conforti [2015]).
==PAGE:16==

2 Literature review
2.2.2
Sugar
Granulated sugar is also sometimes known as white sugar, or “regular” sugar and it plays
an important role in cake baking. Sugar contributes to the sweetness of the cake but
also influence cake volume, moisture content, tenderness, crust colour, and the calorie
content of the cake (Conforti [2015]). When sugar is creamed with butter it increases
the volume of the cake during baking by incorporating air into the fat. Sugar helps to
brown the crust through caramelization and Maillard reaction. Sugar weakens the gluten
development because sugar limits the availability of water in the batter. During baking
sugar raises the temperature at which gelatinization and coagulation of proteins occur,
leading to formation of more air bubbles which results in a finer, more even texture
(Conforti [2015]).
2.2.3
Eggs
Eggs are multi functional and therefore play an important role in cake baking. Eggs
add moisture to the cake due the the large amount of water in the eggs. The eggs helps
with aeration because the egg whites can entangle large quantities of air when whisked.
Eggs influence the structure of the cake due the presence of proteins in the yolk and the
egg white that coagulate when heated. The egg yolk gives a yellow color to the cake
(Conforti [2015]).
2.2.4
Butter
Fat is an important ingredient in achieving a good texture and mouth feel of the cake
(Davidson [2016a]).
2.2.5
Milk
Milk performs several functions in a cake recipe. Milk dissolves the ingredients, especially
sugar, during mixing of the batter. Milk hydrates the starch which causes gelatinization
when baked. Milk adds flavor, proteins, B-vitamins, and calcium to the cake. (Conforti
[2015])
2.2.6
Leavening agents
In cake baking chemical leaveners are important for their carbon dioxide producing
ability which gives volume and crumb structure to the cake. CO2 is produced when
baking powder is used. Baking powder consists of sodium bicarbonate (baking soda)
and sodium dihydrogen phosphate. When these compounds come in contact with liquid
and heat, a chemical reaction occurs that produces CO2 (Conforti [2015]).

2.3 Baking process
5
2.2.7
Mixing the ingredients
The purpose of mixing the ingredients is to make a homogeneous batter and to incorpo-
rate air into the batter. The first step is to mix butter and sugar where large volumes
of air is incorporated in the batter in the form of minuscule cells in the fat phase. When
mixed with water, protein from the flour forms an elastic network known as gluten. In
cake it is desired to have a weak gluten network to help trap gas so the final cake has
a soft texture. Mixing time is important because mixing for to long can cause a loss of
air and result in a heavy cake. The mixing affect the structure and volume of the cake,
and should therefore be carefully designed (Conforti [2015], Manohar [2014]).
2.3
Baking process
Baking is the key unit operation in cake baking where the batter develops in to the
desired product.
During baking the product changes characteristics such as volume,
color, texture and flavor. Several physical and chemical changes take place during the
baking process including volume expanding, vaporization of water, formation of porous
structure, gelatinization of starch, formation of crust and color (Manohar [2014]). In
figure 2.1 an overview of the changes are summarized. These changes are dependent on
temperature and process time and they may overlap and interact.
Figure 2.1: Overview of what happens during a baking process (Davidson [2016a]).
The gluten absorbs water and starts to swell at temperatures between 30-50◦C. When
denaturation of proteins happen at 50◦C moisture is released and it contributes to starch
gelatinization. Starch gelatinization may continue until the batter reaches a tempera-
==PAGE:18==

ture of 95◦C. The cake rises rapidly at around 60◦C due to the formation of CO2 from
the baking soda (Davidson [2016a]).
There are several factors that contribute to the coloring of the cake surface. After the
moisture has evaporated from the surface, the color will change from around 150◦C.
There are three processes that contribute to the coloring of the cake. (Davidson [2016a])
• Caramelisation is caused by the breakdown of sugars (110◦C for fructose and 160◦C
for glucose and sucrose).
• Dextrinisation is the breakdown of starch into dextrins. Dextrinisation occur at tem-
peratures from 100-200◦C.
• The Maillard reaction is a chemical reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars.
The Maillard reaction is a non-enzymatic browning that consists of several complex re-
actions. As sucrose is heated and inverted to glucose and fructose, the two reducing
sugars react with amino acids via the Maillard reaction to produce browning and flavor
compounds of importance in baked goods. Lactose is also a reducing sugar and can par-
ticipate in the Maillard reaction where it can produce a reddish color. High pH favors
the Maillard reaction (Zhou and Hui [2014]). The Maillard reaction will occur at around
150◦C. The Maillard reaction, caramelisation and dextrinisation will also have an affect
on the final taste of the cake (Davidson [2016a]).
In a baking oven the heat is transferred to the food through conduction, convection
and radiation. The conventional oven relies on hot air for heating food, primarily by
convection. The hot air is circulated in the oven by a fan. In the cake baking industry
tunnel ovens are most commonly used because they minimize energy consumption and
increase mass production. The tunnel ovens baking chamber is divided into several zones.
Each zone can be independently controlled which gives more flexibility in cake manu-
facturing (Conforti [2015]). In a baking process the batter temperature slowly increases
from the outside to the inside. In pound cake baking the crust is sometimes finished
before the center is fully cooked due to the internal resistance in the cake. In order to
make cake baking more efficient alternative technologies are investigated that exclude
significant temperature and moisture gradients during cake baking (Deleua et al. [2019]).

2.4 Ohmic heating
7
2.4
Ohmic heating
Ohmic heating (OH) also called joule heating is a process where an electric current is
passed through a food product with the purpose of heating it. Ohmic heating of food
products has existed for many years. In the nineteenth century ohmic heating was used
as a pasteurizing technology for milk. However, due to the high processing cost and the
lack of suitable inert electrode materials other pasteurizing technologies were chosen.
In the 1980s the interest in ohmic heating for the food industry returned. This is mainly
due to the short time it takes to reach a high temperature in food products. Today elec-
trodes can be made of stainless steel instead of titanium, and thereby avoid the chemical
reaction between electrodes and food during ohmic heating (Chen [2014]).
Figure 2.2: Schematic overview of ohmic heating.
The principle behind ohmic heating is simple as seen in figure 2.2. The food product is
made part of the electrical circuit. When designing the ohmic heating unit it is impor-
tant that the electrodes are in contact with the food throughout the process. Heat is
directly generated inside the food when an electrical power is applied and the current
passes through the food. The heating rate is dependent on the electrical voltage and the
electrical conductivity of the food item.
Ohmic heating offers advantages such as rapid uniform heating, reduced surface fouling,
high energy efficiency and high quality food products. Ohmic heating technology has
the potential to be a big factor in the food processing industry (CORDIS [2016]). How-
ever, ohmic heating has some disadvantages such as lack of research and it is difficult
to manage and control (Sakr and Liu [2014]). Ohmic heating also has some application
limits if the food product doesn’t contain dissolved ionic salts.

2.4.1
Electrical conductivity
The electrical conductivity σ is an important parameter in ohmic heating. The electrical
conductivity is the measure of a materials ability to allow the transport of an electric
charge. It is defined as the current transferred across an area per unit of electrical field,
see equation 2.1 (Sakr and Liu [2014]).
σ = L · I
V · A =
I
E · A
(2.1)
• σ Electrical conductivity (Sm−1)
• L is the gap between the two electrodes (m)
• I is the current passing through the material (A)
• A is the cross-section area of the sample in the heating g cell (m2)
• E is the electrical field V/L (V/m)
The electrical conductivity in food usually increases with temperature. The electrical
conductivity of both solid foods and liquids is often considered to be a linear function
of temperature, see equation 2.2 (Goullieux and Pain [2005]):
σ = σin · (1 + m · (T −Tin)
(2.2)
• σin is the initial electrical conductivity
• m is the proportionality constant (◦C−1)
• Tin is the initial temperature
However, a non-linear relationship can sometimes be observed during ohmic heating pro-
cessing if there is a change in viscosity during processing. The reasons could be starch
gelatinization, decrease in moisture content or air in the product (Chen [2014]).
The electrical conductivity for food products can be categorized in three groups (Goul-
lieux and Pain [2005]):
• Good conductivity (σ > 0.05 S/m)
• Low conductivity (0.005 < σ < 0.05 S/m)
• Poor conductivity (σ < 0.005 S/m)
Examples of food products with good conductivity are eggs, milk desserts and fruit juice.
Margarine has a low conductivity whereas fat has a poor conductivity. The conductivity
of food products usually increase with water content (Goullieux and Pain [2005]).

2.4.2
Temperature measurement during ohmic heating
Temperature measurement during ohmic heating is a challenge but necessary. Thermo-
couples offer reliable measurements and have low cost. However, using thermocouples
during ohmic heating can lead to problems such as electrical discharge and signal per-
turbations. For thermocouple type K it is observed that it may give errors due to the
interactions between the temperature gradient and the magnetic field experienced by
the thermocouple (Zell et al. [2009]).
2.5
Cake quality
The cake quality is very important in cake manufacturing in order to produce cake that
meets the consumer expectation. The cake should look good and be attractive on the
outside while being enjoyable to consume. The quality of a cake is dependent on sev-
eral factors such as ingredients, mixing and baking procedures. In a good quality cake
it is important to keep track of tenderness, structure, moisture and flavor (Conforti
[2015]). Texture Profile Analysis is a double compression test for determining the textu-
ral properties of foods. However, no sensory characteristics using consumer testing will
be investigated in this study.
2.6
Summary of literature study
The literature study showed that ohmic heating technology provides several benefits such
as rapid heating, high quality products and energy savings. More research investigating
the application of using ohmic heating for cake baking is required.

3.4
Quality measurement
To investigate the quality of the cake four tests were performed.
3.4.1
Moisture content
The moisture content is measured in the cakes to ensure a high quality cake. If the cake
has a low moisture content the cake might be dry and inedible. If the cake has a high
moisture content it might not be fully baked and the cake can fall apart when eaten.
The moisture content is determined from the loss of product weight during drying by
measuring the change in mass before and after drying. The drying oven is constant at
105◦C and the samples will dry in the oven overnight until no more change in weight is
observed. All the samples are triplicated.
3.4.2
Texture Profile Analysis (TPA)
Texture Profile Analysis (TPA) is a double compression test for determining the textural
properties of foods. In cakes the tenderness and structure are important and it can be
investigated using TPA. During a TPA test samples are compressed twice to provide
knowledge about how samples behave when chewed, see figure 3.6. The TPA tests were
carried out using Texture Analyzer XT.Plus (Stable Micro Systems Ltd., UK) with 30
kg load cell. A cake piece is cut into a cube (2x2x2cm) before doing the TPA. The pieces
are cut from the center of the cake to exclude crust on the sides. The crust on the top is
included on all of the samples. All samples are triplicated and the TPA experiments are
done approximately 18 hours after baking. A flat probe with 50mm diameter, a speed
of 5 mm/sec and 70% strain is used.
Figure 3.6: An example of a Texture Profile Analysis graph ( [TTC]).
==PAGE:28==
16
3 Materials and method
The TPA can measure a lot of characteristic ( [TTC]):
• Hardness: The maximum force of the 1st compression.
• Fracturability: The tendency of a material to fracture at the first peak.
• Cohesiveness: How well a product withstand a second deformation relative to the
first deformation (Area 2/Area 1).
• Adhesiveness: The negative force to overcome the resistance when pulling the
probe back from the sample (also called stickiness). Not shown in figure 3.6.
• Springiness: How well a product physically springs back after it has been deformed
during the first compression (Distance 2/Distance 1).
• Resilience: How well a product fights to regain its original height (Area 4/Area
3).
• Gumminess: Characterizes semi-solid foods (Hardness · Cohesiveness).
• Chewiness: Chewiness is the energy required to chew a product until is ready to
be swallowed (Hardness · Cohesiveness · Springiness).
The TPA program has the option to do a macro which means the values can be directly
calculated. This project will investigate the hardness and the springiness of the cakes
because they are good at describing the texture of cake. The results from the other
measurements are available in appendix B.
3.4.3
Color measurement
The crust color is an important quality of the cake. It is often the first impression a
consumer gets and it therefore has to look appealing. The color is measured using L,a,b
values. The color is determined by transmitting light on the cake. The reflection from
the sample is measured through filters and optical fibers by which the color is deter-
mined. Two different methods to determine the color are used. The color is measured
using Minolta CR-200 and VideometerLab 2.
The Minolta CR-200 is calibrated on a Minolta standard-white reflector plate before
measuring the color of the cakes. The Minolta can only measure the color in a small
spot.
The VideometerLab 2 has 18 LED sources with a range from 375 to 1050 nm which gives
an image size of 2056 x 2056 pixels (Videometer). The VideometerLab 2 is preferable if
the color of a larger area has to be determined. The image is analyzed in Matlab and
the average L,a,b values are returned.

3.5
Design of Experiment (DoE)
Design of experiments (DoE) is a strategy used to gather empirical knowledge based on
analysis of experimental data. DoE is a practical solution to explore multifactor and
how they affect the responses. The goal of DoE is to understand the influential variables
(factors). The statistical software, JMP Pro, is used for designing and analysing the data.
A full factorial design is used for investigating the response of two or more factors and
the interactions between the factors. A full factorial design includes all the possible
combinations of the levels of the factors. The sample size is the product of the levels of
the factors, see equation 3.1. The full factorial design in this study was constructed with
3 factors with 2 levels. The levels Xi are coded from -1 to 1, where -1 is the minimum
setting and 1 is the maximum setting.
Sample size = Levelfactor = 23 = 8
(3.1)
Every sample is duplicated which results in a total of 16 experiments. The run order
was randomised to balance the effect of uncontrollable conditions that may impact the
results. The factors investigated are voltage, ohmic heating time and oven time because
they have a big influence on the quality of the cake.
A Response Surface Methodology (RSM) was also constructed which add center points
to the full factorial design. The RSM finds the best response allowing quadratic effects
(curvature). The RSM required 8 additional experiments (16 experiments in total). The
RSM with a Face Centered Central Composite Design (CCF) was chosen because it can
improve the estimation and optimal process settings (NIST/SEMATECH [a]) .
The linear regression model for full factorial design (NIST/SEMATECH [b]):

4.5
Energy
In this section the process energy requirement for ohmic heating will be investigated.
The amount of electrical power required (Ptotal) equal the energy to heat the batter
(Qheat), the evaporation of water Qevap) and energy losses, see equation 4.14.
Ptotal = Qheat + Qevap + loss
[kJ]
(4.14)
The amount of energy required to heat the batter from 20◦C to 100◦C is calculated using
equation 4.15.
Qheat = cp,batter · ∆T · mbatter
[kJ]
(4.15)
Specific heat capacity (cp,batter) defines the amount of energy the batter needs to change
the temperature of 1 kg batter 1 K [kJ/(kg·K]. The specific heat capacity of the batter
is calculated based on ingredients in the batter, see appendix E. The specific heat ca-
pacity varies only little with temperature and it is assumed the specific heat capacity is
constant throughout the baking process. m is the mass of the batter. The specific heat
capacity is approximately 2.48 kJ/kg for the batter at 20◦C.
The energy required to evaporate water is calculated in equation 4.16
Qevap = ∆Hlatent · mwater
[kJ]
(4.16)
∆Hlatent is approximately 2250 [kJ/kg] which is the required energy per kg water to
evaporate water at 100◦C. mwater is the amount of water evaporated.
In order to calculate the power supplied from the system, the resistance in the batter
should be calculated using equation 4.17 (Ohm’s law). R is resistance (Ω), U is voltage
(V) and I is the current (A).
R = U
I
(4.17)
Using Joule’s first law the power generated by the supplied system can be calculated,
see equation 4.18. P is the thermal energy (W).
P = I2 · R
(4.18)
Because the resistance and current varies during ohmic heating the total power supplied
by the system can be calculated as an integral of P(t). The integral equations can be
seen in appendix E.

Gally et al. [2017] conducted a study about energy needed to reach an expansion ratio of
3 for dough using ohmic heating. Their results show an increase in energy requirement
for a higher voltage. The authors suppose that the higher amount of energy comes from
higher energy losses but it needed further investigation.
Ghnimi et al. [2007] have investigated the energy efficiency in a ohmic heating fluid jet
setup. Based on the thermal balance, the energy efficiency of the Ohmic cell is close to
96% and the energy efficiency of the power supply is up to 90%.
Davidson [2016b] has conducted a study where he investigates the oven efficiency in a
conventional oven during biscuit baking. The overall oven efficiency is approximately
56%. It is estimated that the heat loss through the burner flues can be recovered up to
50% and be used in a heat recovery system thus increasing the efficiency of the oven.
Comparing the literature results with the results in this project means that ohmic heating
can be up to approximately 42% more energy efficient than a conventional oven. However,
the conventional heating that is required for crust formation after ohmic heating is not
included and therefore the energy efficient might be a little bit less.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
Cake baked in a combined conventional and ohmic heating process proved to be feasi-
ble. In the preliminary experiments, it became clearer which qualities the cake should
possess after baking. The cake should have around 20% moisture content and have a
crust lightness at around 48 measured by Minolta CR-200.
Design of Experiment was used to investigate the influence of three parameters (applied
voltage, time in the ohmic heating and time in the oven). The results showed that higher
voltage increases the heating rate and thereby can reduce the baking time with up to
64.3%. However, the voltage does not have a significant effect on the quality of the
cake. The ohmic heating time and the time in the oven have a significant effect on the
moisture content, hardness, springiness and expansion on the cake. The crust formation
happens in the oven and therefore the color formation is only determined by the oven.
From the knowledge gained in preliminary experiments and Design of Experiment it is
possible to optimize the process and get a desirability of 95.3% compared to to sponge
cake from the danish manufacturer Dan Cake. Energy calculations estimate that ohmic
heating can save up to 42% of the required energy, compared to an industrial convection
oven.

CHAPTER 6
Future work
In this chapter some of the issues with ohmic heating that have not been investigated
in this paper will be discussed.
More recipes and different cake types should be tested to broaden the application range
of ohmic heating. It would be interesting to see how shredded nuts, carrots and choco-
late affect the ohmic heating for example. It could also be interesting to replace butter
with vegetable oil and see how it affects the conductivity. Dan Cake uses vegetable oil
in their carrot cake and sponge cake which probably is because vegetable oil is a much
cheaper ingredient than butter.
The setup used in this project where ohmic heating is combined with conventional heat-
ing is not ready to be deployed in a cake production line. It is not time efficient that
the cake has to be moved from the ohmic heater to the conventional oven. It would be
a huge advantage to have the crust formation during ohmic heating to reduce the total
baking time. It should be investigated if it is possible to do the ohmic heating while the
cake is in the conventional oven.
With the ohmic heating setup used in this project it is possible to change the length
and height of the cake - but it has to be a square cake. It would be interesting if other
shapes like a round cake or triangle cake would be possible to bake using ohmic heating.
If other shapes of cake can be produced using ohmic heating, it could increase the po-
tential market size.
Oven temperature and fan speed also affect the formation of crust color. Their effect
was not tested in this study, but in future research it would be a good idea to investigate
their effect.
Future research should also include a sensory analysis using consumer testing in order
to assess the quality of the ohmic heating cakes.
