Abstract
Electrification can be considered as one of the main pillars of the energy transition, but for some
sectors, it is for now diﬀicult to start the transition, for technological or economic reasons. The
aviation, freight or shipping, or some chemical industries still need fuel and at this point, eFuels
can be a potential solution. Liquid eFuels have an advantage comparing to gaseous eFuels as
they are easier to store and transport due to its physical phase. In this respect, eMethanol is
emerging as a viable solution. The global demand for methanol is significant and is expected
to grow in the coming years.
To improve the global production of eMethanol, investments in Power-to-methanol plants are
necessary. These plants produce hydrogen through the electrolysis of water, which reacts after-
wards with CO2 to produce methanol. In this context, hybrid power plants (HPP) could play
a vital role providing the necessary green electricity. Combining two or more power generation
units, HPP can reduce the power generation fluctuations caused by intermittent renewable
energy sources, and thus ensure a more steady supply of power to the electrolyser.
Thus, in this work, the operation of a HPP augmented with eMethanol production is optimized
to maximize the revenue of the plant owners and prioritize the production of green methanol.
HyDesign tool for the design and operation of utility-scale HPP is further developed to integrate
the Solid oxide electrolyser cell (SOEC) to produce hydrogen, Direct Air Capture (DAC) unit
for capturing CO2 from the atmosphere, reactor for the methanol synthesis, and a methanol
storage tank to assess the plant’s performance and operation.
The operation of such a plant is managed by an energy management system (EMS) with an
objective to maximize the revenues, by selling electricity to the grid in the spot market and
methanol at a fixed price. Installation and operational costs during the plant lifetime are sub-
tracted from the revenue. Additionally, EMS’ objective function also uses a penalty cost to
prioritize green methanol production over grid methanol production. Moreover, each technol-
ogy used in the system has associated capital expenditures (CAPEX) and operational costs
that impact the financial metrics of the plant, such as the profitability defined as Net Present
Value (NPV) over CAPEX, or the break-even price of green methanol that is resulting in a
NPV equal to zero.
This work focuses on assessing the plant’s performance on integrating Power-to-Methanol in a
HPP located at Ørnhøj, Denmark, the site is known for a high potential of wind resources due
to its proximity to the North Sea. First, the impact of the integration of Power-to-Methanol
in a HPP on this site will be examined. Further analysis will include (1) sensitivity analysis
on the parameters that are critical to assess the plant’s performance: variations in the price of
methanol, the cost of purchased electricity, the CAPEX and OPEX of the plant’s technologies
and the grid capacity to see how these values impact the profitability of the plant, (2) case
studies across different locations across Europe: Westerstede in Germany, a wind-dominated
site, Chaulieu in France, where the wind resources are high. These comparisons aim to show
the plants’ strategies to maximize its revenues in different geographies with different weather
conditions and electricity prices. Finally, an optimal design of the plant will be determined,
through a sensitivity analysis on the plant’s technologies’ capacities.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Most of the countries’ strategies regarding climate change tend to electrify many sectors, replac-
ing fossil fuel by green electricity. This can be explained by different reasons: the electrification
of many sectors increase resiliency and improve the systems cost and eﬀiciency. The potential is
huge, as many sectors already started the transition such as the automobile sector, or industrial
and space heating. However, for other sectors, it is for now diﬀicult to start the transition,
for technological or economical reasons. The aviation, freight or shipping, or some chemical
industry still need fuel. At this point, eFuels have a potential. They offer a sustainable alter-
native to conventional fuels, and partly fits with the existing infrastructure as mentioned in
[21]. Liquid eFuels have an advantage comparing to gaseous eFuels: they are easier to store and
transport due to their physical phase. In this regard, eMethanol seems like a good opportunity.
Liquid at ambient temperature, it is a relatively easy to store and transport fuel. What’s more,
Methanol’s global demand is significant and the forecast show a growth. [5] claims that the
global production of Methanol was equal to 100 Mtons in 2020, and forecasts that it would
reach 500 Mtons in 2050, with half of it being eMethanol. In comparison, the same organiza-
tion claims in this article [6] that the global production of Hydrogen, in 2022, is equal to 75
Mtons/year. This shows that Methanol is also an already established fuel among the others.
On a smaller scale, the Danish strategy is to reach 80% of electrified means of transportation,
as claims [9]. This shows that 20% will still need to be supplied by fuels. The Danish Energy
Agency (DEA) has analyzed the Fit-for-55 package, a CO2 reduction program which purpose is
to regulate transport’s and industry’s emissions. This involves the promotion and development
of Power-to-X (P2X). This consists in the green electricity conversion to eFuels, using the sur-
plus of electricity generated by renewable technologies, generally when the generation exceeds
the demand. According to the DEA, the displacement of fossil fuel by P2X fuels would lead to
a decrease of 0.5 tonCO2/year in the danish transport sector [9]. In this context, eFuels is used
as a direct source of energy. However, in a more electrified scenario, eFuels still have a potential.
The intermittency of renewable resources makes it diﬀicult to control the power load of re-
newable electricity production assets. In this context, the need for storage is essential. The
international Energy Agency has shown that between 2015 and 2020, the Li-ion battery was
by far the main technology used to store electricity [15], as shows Figure 1.
Figure 1: Energy storage technology mix, 2015-2020 [15]
Lithium resources are not infinite, and there is a real need to diversify the electricity storage

==PAGE:12==
means. Liquid eFuels seem to be a good alternative for electricity storage through electrolysis
and fuel cells. In terms of volumetric energy content, under atmospheric conditions (1 bar,
and 20 ◦C), Methanol seems a better solution than Li-ion batteries, with a volumetric energy
density of 15.8 GJ/m3 against 0.9-2.4 GJ/m3, according to [5]. Compared to marine gas oil
(36.6 GJ/m3), Methanol seems less interesting.
The diversity of possible applications makes Methanol and eMethanol an energy carrier or
source of energy that needs to be relied on for a more sustainable future.
1.2
Scope of the work
This Master thesis proposes to optimize a HPP in order to improve its eﬀiciency and produce
green hydrogen for synthesizing eMethanol. Firstly, it investigates the potential and challenges
of eFuels in the current and future energy markets. The study then looks at the available
processes used to make eMethanol. The thesis discusses how methanol production through
green hydrogen and carbon within a HPP model affects the economy. The study also seeks to
identify the trade-off between revenue maximization and eMethanol production maximization,
with respect to the purchase of electricity from the grid in terms of operation, sizing and
financial metrics. Key considerations in the synthesis of eFuels are identified, with an emphasis
on optimizing processes to maximize production and minimize costs. In addition, the research
evaluates the optimal operation on case studies.
A sensitivity analysis of the model is be
conducted to understand how the HPP is sensitive to these parameters. Besides, the study aims
to find the optimal balance between eFuel and electricity production over the HPP’s lifespan.
Ultimately, it seeks to propose an optimal design for an HPP that effectively incorporates eFuel
production, ensuring economic viability.
1.3
Structure of the report
The structure of this thesis is organized into five sections. After this quick introduction, a
literature review is carried out in Section 2 to have a better understanding of the context
of eMethanol, the challenges it can face and the existing production technologies. Section 3
shows the methodology used in this work to optimize the operation and design of the HPP.
Afterwards, the case studies are developed in Section 4. The operation optimization tests are
developed in Section 5, before having a discussion on the results of the optimization problems
in Section 6.

2.1
eMethanol context and challenges
2.1.1
Current situation
To reach the potential developed in Subsection 1.1, important investments are needed. The
most conventional way to produce methanol is through steam-reforming natural gas. Accord-
ing to [19], this technology accounts for around 65% of the global production. In this case,
methanol is considered as grey methanol.
Around 35% of the production comes from coal
gasification, which creates brown methanol. Finally, green methanol, produced by renewable
energies (biomass for bioMethanol and electrolysis for eMethanol), only accounts for around
1% of the global production. This shows the huge room for improvement of this sector, in a
need to mitigate CO2 emissions.
One opportunity for improvement is the overall eﬀiciency of the production of eFuels. As they
are a secondary source of energy carrier, producing eFuels lead to higher losses in the chain. [21]
claims that eFuels’ overall eﬀiciency range between 10% and 35%. The difference of application
of these fuels explain the large range. This same article explains that a direct electrification
alternative needs 2-14 times less energy. As said earlier, some field won’t adapt to a full elec-
trification, such as aviation, or freights for instance.
The development of eMethanol plants is not really spread worldwide, as shows Fig. 2.
Figure 2: EMethanol plants in the world [14]
The plants are mainly concentrated in western Europe and China. This embodies a challenge,
to export the technology worldwide. This can also be seen as a political potential of co-operation
between regions. [5] says that a co-operation exists between Morocco and European Union for
the deployment of Power-to-X facilities. This co-operation undergoes the transmission of feed
stock production strategies or technology exportation, and would lead to the creation of new

2.1.3
Investments
The development of eMethanol on a large scale goes through important investments in P2X.
Different countries started developing strategies to increase their P2X capacity or their ability
to develop the technologies to make it more eﬀicient.
Among Europe’s strategies to decarbonise several sectors, the European Green Deal, there is
a path to decarbonise hydrogen and thus fuels. [11] states that this path is structured around
three steps:
• From 2020 to 2024, the first step is to develop and invest in renewable hydrogen electrol-
yser to increase the total capacity from 1 GW to 6 GW.
• From 2024 to 2030, the second step is to reach a total capacity of at least 40 GW
of renewable hydrogen electrolyser and to make hydrogen an integral part of energy
system. This means that green hydrogen should be used in several sectors, such as the
steel production, industrial chemistry and the production of renewable fuel such as green
methanol.
• From 2030 to 2050, renewable hydrogen should be able to supply any sector where the
decarbonisation process is hard to do with other alternatives such as electrification for
instance.

It is clear that this strategy shows Europe’s will to invest in the development of green hydrogen
and renewable fuels.
In this context, Denmark has a major role. According to [9], Denmark is the leader in terms
of patent publications. This is non negligible in the development of P2X, as the technology
is relatively young and there is still room for improvement. 344 million DKK have also been
earmarked by Denmark for the research in innovative green technologies. Besides, Denmark
is making important investments (1,25 billion DKK) to achieve a total electrolyser capacity of

at a low cost, especially in Europe, where the goal is to reach 40 GW of P2X capacity in 2030,
according to [10]. To produce eMethanol, there is also a need to have a source of CO2. To be
aligned with the goals of mitigation of the carbon impact, the Danish strategy also claims that
strong investments in Carbon Capture, Utilization and Storage (CCUS) are carried out. This
aims to increase the negative emissions and to allocate resources for the production of eFuels
that contain carbon, such as eMethanol. This important strategy could lead to a decrease of
the price of eMethanol which would shift from 250 DKK/GJ to 170 DKK/GJ, still according
to [9].
2.1.4
Legislation challenges
Some challenges remain, in terms of legislation for instance, where some progress needs to be
done. There is a need for a robust legislation to frame the development of P2X. As the growth
is strong, the regulations did not have time to be developed in the same time. This leads to
high delays in the deployment of P2X plants for instance. A clear legislation would also help
to attract investors.
Lately though, Germany started to implement policies to accelerate the expansion of renewable
energies. According to [12], the full-scale invasion in Ukraine sped up their will to be more
energy independent, regarding their the natural gas the country historically purchase in Russia.
Two measures of this policy that came into effect on the January 1st 2023 show the desire to
push for a better legislative framework :
• ”Accelerate planning and approval procedures.”
• ”Promote financial participation by municipal authorities.”
These measures would lead first to an accelerated development of P2X plants, and to a higher
production capacity of eMethanol, and second to more investments.
Accelerating the development of eMethanol goes also through the development of sustainable
policies in sectors where Methanol can be used. In the transport sector for instance, there is
a potential to promote eMethanol, as the existing infrastructure can fit to this type of fuel as
said before. [5] says that some countries start introducing a blended Methanol/Gasoline type
of fuel, where both product has a different proportion. This would help having a smoother
transition where a use of Methanol-only (and thus eMethanol-only) type of fuel. On the leg-
islation side, the Renewable Energy Directive from European Union requires at least 14% of
renewable energy used in transport in 2030. This pushes the member countries to introduce
quotas in the blended fuels and to promote eMethanol among other eFuels. Obviously, social
considerations need to be embraced to make it accessible to most of the population.

On a technical point of view, this makes sense. Although, methanol has a low volumetric energy
density compared to conventional fuel (around half of gasoline and diesel fuel according to [5]),
blending it with conventional fuel can enhance the performance of the obtained fuel. Indeed,
the high octane rating of methanol makes the engine able to reach better performances.
2.1.5
Comparison with bioMethanol
As stated before, eMethanol and bioMethanol are the two types of renewable fuels on the mar-
ket.[5] says that low-carbon methanol exists from decarbonising the production of methanol
from natural gas steam reforming. eFuels and eMethanol have some advantages compared to
bioFuels, in terms of sustainability. Even though the biomass can be generated in a sustainable
way, the resources are limited. What’s more, when biomass comes from crops, large green
areas are affected. Increasing the production of those biofuels would increase the need for more
agricultural lands, and would need more areas. This also raises societal questions of acceptance
of using this lands for the production of biomass crops.
This can embody a feedstock availability limitation, especially in the case of bioMethanol among
bioFuels. Indeed, as [2] states, there are no bioMethanol plants currently under operation. Some
used to exist, but have been replaced by natural gas. This shows that some improvement need
to be done in the supply of the feedstock.
However, in terms of cost, biomass remains a good compromise, and is cheaper compared to
DAC technology, that is developed and modelled later in this paper. For the biomass, the
carbon dioxide cost is included between 12-22 USD/tonCO2, while for the DAC the cost is
included between 300-600 USD/tonCO2, according to [5]. Obviously, DAC is a relatively very
new technology, which can explain this high cost.
This leads in a higher price for eMethanol supplied by carbon dioxide from DAC only, compared
to the production of eMethanol supplied by carbon dioxide from any renewable source.
2.2
Existing eMethanol production technologies
The production of eMethanol needs both green hydrogen and renewable carbon dioxide. After
a quick summarize of the existing electrolysis technologies, this section focuses on the specific
direct air capture technology among the CCUS technologies. Finally, the methanol synthesis
is described.
2.2.1
Electrolysis technologies
As said before, electrolysis is using electricity produced by renewable sources, such as wind or
solar. This technology plays a crucial role in the energy transition. So far, it is the sustainable
hydrogen production mean that is the most developed.
Three technologies are leading the electrolysis market : the alkaline electrolysis, the proton
exchange membrane (PEM) electrolysis, and the Solid Oxide electrolysis.
• The alkaline electrolysis usually uses a KOH solution as electrolyte, with OH– anions
being the ionic conductors.

• In the PEM technology, a membrane is used where protons are passing through a mem-
brane and combine to hydrogen.

• The Solid Oxide electrolysis, still at a research phase, differs from the two others, having a
high operation temperature. This implies less need for electricity from, as the reaction is
thermally activated by the heat. It is expected that at full-development, this technology
will have a higher eﬀiciency than the two others, according to [8].

2.2.2
Direct Air Capture technologies
In the context of the Paris Agreement, CCUS technologies’ importance is non neglected. Able
to sorb, store and utilize carbon it has a major role in the carbon emission mitigation strategies.
Among the CCUS technologies, DAC plants are relatively very new, and the IEA indicates that
there were, in 2019, 15 operational DAC plants worldwide. According to [16], a rough estimate
of the carbon dioxide removal is around few MtonsCO2 for the DAC technologies. The goal is
to reach the GtonsCO2 scale in 2050, and this goes through a yearly growth rate of 50%.
This technology is based on the extraction of CO2 from the air to either store or utilize it. In the
context of eMethanol production, the CO2 is directly used. This technology contains a sorbent
that, in a first time adsorb or absorb CO2 from the atmosphere, under specific temperature and
pressure conditions. In a second time, and by changing these conditions, the sorbent is able to
desorb the CO2. Two types of sorbents are used in the industry:
7
==PAGE:18==
• Solid sorbents are characterized by their porosity, that helps to separate the CO2 from
the gas-mixture adsorbed in input of the system (air) [16]. Amine surfaces can be added
to the sorbent to make it more selective towards CO2.
• Liquid sorbents are characterized by a strong basic hydroxide solution to be able to absorb
CO2.

Methanol synthesis technologies
Different processes exist to produce methanol.

As seen in the previous section, natural gas through the steam-reforming process is the main
technology to produce methanol. Even though this technology is cheaper compared to other
production way, its carbon impact is high. In this mechanism, natural gas (such as methane
for instance) forms with steam a syngas, that, after compression, reacts with carbon dioxide to
produce methanol. A fluid-separation step is then carried out to divide the by-products and
increase the purity of the obtained methanol.
In a constant research of mitigating the carbon impact, further researches have been made to
produce green methanol.
According to [8], a way to produce green methanol is the hydrogenation of CO2 through the
following reaction. This leads to eMethanol, which process of production is more detailed in
the report.

To provide green methanol, the combination of green hydrogen obtained from the electrolysis
of water thanks to green electricity, and carbon dioxide obtained from DAC is interesting.
The CO2 and H2 gas flows are pressurized before entering the reactor. This step is important,
as the pressure of the inner gas has consequences on the optimal cost and performances of the
reactor. According to [22], increasing the operating pressure increases in a first time the heat
generated in the by the reactor, that can be then reused according to the integrated system.
[22] states that this increase also results in a decrease of the heat exchanger area, that would
result in a lower cost spent in that. In the same time, increasing the pressure of the reactor
requires a higher thickness of the components, that increases also the cost. This shows that an
optimization of the design of the reactor itself is relevant, to make it work at an optimal cost
for the wanted performances.
In the reactor, a catalyst helps to activate the reactor. Usually CuO/ZnO/Al2O3 is used as a
catalyst for this reaction according to [5]. In the case of the eMethanol production, this cata-
lyst is slightly modified, as it is in contact with more water than in the conventional methanol
production process.
To make sure that the methanol yield is high enough, the products obtained from the reactor
goes first through a flash that is dividing the vapour and liquid phase [22]. At the flash op-
erating temperature, hydrogen goes then through the flash’s vapour stream and is recycled in
the reactor, while methanol goes through the flash’s liquid stream. After this step, it is usual
to resort to a dividing wall column. As [22] states, this component is responsible for a higher
purity of methanol, thanks to the distillation part, but it is also responsible for a higher yield,
recycling carbon dioxide that didn’t react earlier.

3.1
HyDesign model
HyDesign is an open source tool developed by DTU researchers [17]. This tool can be used to
optimize the sizing of hybrid power plants. This nested optimization is well developed in Fig.
6:
Figure 6: HPP sizing as a nested optimization. XDSM diagram. [17]
The XDSM diagram described above is made for a very basic case of HPP, where a wind farm,
a solar farm and a BESS is modelled. The model described in this paper adds more technologies
to produce eMethanol.
In a first time, the system is optimizing the operation of the HPP. By giving in input data,
wind power time series, solar power time series, electricity market prices, the capacities of each
of the plant’s components and other input technological parameters related to each component,
the tool is first using linear optimization to optimize the revenues of the HPP. First, the EMS
operation optimization is made on a single batch. This single batch optimization is a first step
to validate the model, by doing analysis on a short amount of time. The batch size has a
high importance though: initial and final conditions are set, for the BESS and Methanol tank.
Having a one-day batch, or a one-month batch then has a high impact on the final operation.
In this work, the batch size is set to one week.
In a second time, a long-term EMS operation is optimized. The EMS model gets as input one-
year electricity prices, wind data and solar data. The year is then divided in different batches:

The P2MeOH plant operation becomes a sub-model of HyDesign, which is then assembled with

In this work, a sensitivity analysis is conducted on the different technologies’ capacities of the

3.2.2
SOEC
The choice to implement a SOEC is motivated by two main reasons. Despite this type of
electrolyser still being at a research stage and not commercialized at a high scale such as PEM
or Alkaline electrolysers, it shows a higher eﬀiciency. This is due to the fact that less electrical
power is needed to produce hydrogen, as the SOEC works under higher temperature conditions
(600◦C-800◦C) and heat is compensating. Another reason to use an SOEC, is the fact that
the methanol synthesis reaction through the hydrogenation of CO2 is an exothermic reaction.
Excess heat from this reaction can be used to supply the SOEC. This reaction also produces
H2O that can be supplied to the SOEC in the vapour form, and reduce the cost of water. Thus,
it is assumed, in a first time, that the excess heat from the reactor is high enough to both heat
up the reactants before the methanol synthesis and supply the SOEC with the required heat to
reach the maximum eﬀiciency possible. It is also assumed that all the water produced during
the methanol synthesis is supplied to the SOEC.
The LHV is used in the hydrogen production rate rather than the HHV, mainly because the
SOEC operates at high temperature, and the water produced is already vaporized, which means
that there is no need to provide more energy for the vaporization. Unlike the HHV, the LHV
is not taking in account the latent heat of vaporization of water.
The SOEC chosen in this work follows the experimental model from [18]. In this model, dif-
ferent SOEC technologies are modelled. The technology chosen in our case includes the “free
stream” in the calculation of the SOEC eﬀiciency. This means that external heat is supplied,
which can help reaching higher eﬀiciencies. This makes sense in our model, as this external
heat is modelled by the methanol synthesis excess heat. Low-pressure (LP) and high-pressure
(HP) electrolysers are modelled with this “free stream”: LP-SOEC is already available but has a
lower eﬀiciency as some electricity is used to feed auxiliary compressors. HP-SOEC has a higher
eﬀiciency but is not available yet. In this model, a LP-SOEC is modelled. It is supposed that
the SOEC works under a temperature equal to 750◦C. The eﬀiciency and hydrogen production
rate are then normalized, and it is assumed that this model can be scale to a bigger electrolyser.

3.2.3
DAC
It is assumed that the DAC system adsorbs and desorbs, for each hour t, a certain amount
of carbon dioxide, in such a way that the reaction with hydrogen doesn’t lead to a limiting
reactant. Thus, the amount of carbon dioxide desorbed in the system is proportional to the
amount of hydrogen produced. Thanks to that, there is no need to store any extra hydrogen or
carbon dioxide. To simplify the optimization problem, the adsorption and desorption process
are modelled together in one equation.
In order to work, the DAC requires electrical power. The power is supplied by the renewable
source as stated before. It is assumed that this requirement is proportional to the amount of
carbon dioxide desorbed in the system.
3.2.4
Methanol synthesis
The reaction’s yield is assumed to be equal to 0.9999. This is explained by the fact that the
eMethanol synthesis system developed in 2.2.3 is characterized by a recycle process.
The reactor requires a certain amount of power to supply the compressors. It is assumed that
this amount of power required at a time t is proportional to the quantity of methanol produced
by the reactor at the same time t.
The methanol synthesis is exothermic.
In this model, it is considered that the reactor is
thermally self-suﬀicient, meaning that all the heat produced by the reaction is consumed in
inlet to heat up the reactants, and no external heat is needed.
3.2.5
Methanol tank
The methanol tank is modelled similarly to the battery model. For each time t, it either stores
or supplies methanol. Another solution would have been to model two input and output that
are different and enable both the supplying and storing at a time t. This would have lead to
an extra decision variable. In order to simplify the optimization problem, the first solution is
kept.
It is considered that the methanol tank’s self-discharge eﬀiciency is neglected. This can be
explained by the fact that at the storage temperature and pressure conditions, the methanol is
liquid and relatively stable, which limits the losses. The restitution eﬀiciency of the Methanol
tank is equal to the charging and discharging eﬀiciency, considering that both are equal.
It is assumed that there is no change in pressure or temperature between the reactor and the
tank. Thus, no compressors are added, and the tank doesn’t need any extra electrical power.
What’s more, inside the reactor, it is assumed that the temperature and pressure are constant.
Thus, no contraction or dilatation of the liquid inside the tank is modelled, to simplify the
model.

4.1
Introduction
Four case studies are examined in this report. First, the report aims to assess the performance
of a HPP that includes the production of eMethanol. To do that, the same HPP’s performances
are evaluated in two scenarios: one with and one without eMethanol production. The HPP
are both located on the same site, and the electricity production means have the same capac-
ity. In a second time, a sensitivity analysis is carried out on the parameters that describe the
plant: variations of the price of methanol, the cost of purchased electricity, the CAPEX and
OPEX of the technologies, and the grid capacity to see how these parameters are impactful
on the evaluation of the system. A third case study is the comparison of the P2MeOH plant
described in the first case study with the implementation of the same plant in Westerstede,
in Germany, a wind-dominated site. It is also compared with the implementation of the same
plant in Chaulieu, France, where the wind resources are high. These comparisons aim to show
the plants’ strategies to maximize its revenues in different geographies and different weather
conditions. Ultimately, in a last case study, an optimal design of the P2MeOH plant, located
in Ørnhøj, that ensures economic viability and/or green methanol production maximization is
determined. To carry out this sizing optimization, it is assumed that the methanol demand
doesn’t necessarily need to be met. Indeed, to exactly meet the demand, the plant’s needs to
have a specific design.

that, the more the grid capacity increases, the less the curve becomes linear. This is explained
by the fact that the curve tends to zero when the grid capacity tends to very high values. Even
for relatively small grid capacities, the GUF remains relatively low (0.2). This is of course
explained by the fact that the plant uses electricity to produce methanol, and that selling elec-
tricity to the grid is not the only priority.

Optimization of the size of the P2MeOH plant’s economy

carried out on three scenarios to see how they impact the design of the HPP. Three cases where
the price of methanol changes are observed, and for each case, sensitivity analysis on the SOEC
capacity, the battery capacity and the grid capacity are made.
The first sensitivity analysis on the SOEC capacity, for the three cases, is described in Fig.
28. In the first case, where the price of methanol is equal to 1 €/kg, the optimal design would
lead to a plant that does not include Power-to-Methanol. This is related to the fact that the
revenues generated by the methanol are not high enough to make the SOEC investment prof-
itable. When the price of methanol increases, the optimal SOEC capacity also increases. It is
observed that when the price of methanol is set to 4 €/kg, the
NPV
CAPEX ratio reaches really high
values. This case is in reality hard to implement, as this methanol price is too high compared to
the current methanol market price. The optimal SOEC capacities found in this first sensitivity
analysis are used in the second one, as fixed parameters.

where the Power-to-Methanol is not included to the plant, a higher
NPV
CAPEX is reached when
there is no battery. This can be explained by the fact that it is more profitable to curtail

the case of 2 €/kg, there is a need to have a higher battery capacity to store more electricity.
The high amount of revenues in the case of a high price of methanol also gives flexibility to the
plant owner to size a bigger battery.

Conclusion and Further Works
7.1
Conclusion
The fuel market is highly dominated by the fossil fuels, for decades now. However, technologies
to produce sustainable fuels see the light, and start being industrialized at a higher scale, as
shown the beginning of production of Topsoe’s biggest world’s SOEC factory in Herning, Den-
mark [1], in early 2025. Other investments need to be done to meet the sustainability goals
set internationally, and nationally. Denmark and France have already published hydrogen and
Power-to-X strategies that show a will, and an awareness that it won’t be possible to electrify
every sectors.
Among eFuels, eMethanol has a high potential. Already used as an alternative fuel for the
shipping industry, or blended with gasoline, it is already implemented in the transport sector.
What’s more, liquid at ambient temperature and pressure, this fuel has an advantage compared
with gaseous fuels. The production of eMethanol requires green hydrogen produced thanks to
the electrolysis of water. The leading technologies in this sector are the Alkaline and PEM
electrolysers. The SOEC could endorse a major role in the next year, as it works under higher
temperature than the two technologies explained before, and can achieve a higher eﬀiciency.
The carbon source to produce eMethanol also needs to be sustainable, and can come from
biomass (to produce bio-e-methanol), or from a relatively new technology: direct air capture.
Both reactants are compressed to produce the final product, eMethanol, in a reactor powered
by renewable energy. The reaction needs a catalyst to be activated.
As the technologies to produce eMethanol require renewable electricity to work, including a
Power-to-Methanol plant in a HPP, able to supply a more or less steady electricity power
flow, would be relevant. the evaluation of a HPP that includes the production of eMethanol,
compared to the same HPP that produces only electricity, has been carried out in Ørnhøj, Den-
mark. The result show that the plant that includes Power-to-Methanol generates more profits,
thanks to a high price of methanol. Including the eMethanol production requires electricity.
The study shows that it helps the plant reducing the curtailment level, and reducing the LCOE,
by producing more electricity for a same total cost of production.
The sensitivity analysis demonstrates that some plant’s parameters have a remarkable impact
on the plant’s performances. Changing the price of methanol shows that the input price used
in the study (2 €/kg) can be reduced and the plant’s owner would still have profits, which gives
him flexibility. However, the cost of the technology used are responsible for a the high price of
methanol compared to the current market price (around 0.3 €/kg). It can be expected, in the
future, that the price of eMethanol will get closer to the market price and make the product
more attractive [5]. According to the study, the SOEC and its operational cost has the highest
impact on the Power-to-Methanol plant’s performances and a decrease of this cost in the future
would increase the profits of such a plant. Changing the grid capacity also has an impact on
the plant, and it is necessary to find a good balance between a low grid capacity that implies
lower cost, but more curtailment, and a higher grid capacity that implies the contrary. The
geographical factors, including local electricity prices and local wind resources, definitely have
an impact on the plant’s performances. High wind resources helps the plant produce more green
methanol, and save money from buying electricity from the grid. High local electricity prices
may imply high PPA that also penalizes the plant owner when it comes to purchase electricity
from the grid.
Finally, the optimal size of the plant, in a scenario where no electricity is purchased from the
41
==PAGE:52==
grid, shows that the current price of technology makes it to expensive to invest in a large SOEC,
when there is no demand to meet. A high methanol price needs to be considered to make the
plant with SOEC profitable. The size of the battery is driven by the sale of methanol in the
case studied. The higher the price of methanol is, the more methanol is sold. In this case, the
size of the battery is bigger, to be able to supply the highest amount of electricity as possible to
the SOEC. Finally, the optimal grid capacity is higher when no methanol is sold, as expected,
because more electricity is sold to the grid in this case.
7.2
Further Works
A first way to improve this work would have been to find the optimal design of the plant, having
an optimization problem that optimizes the ratio
NPV
CAPEX , and that has every plant’s technology
capacity as decision variables. HyDesign is able to do so, through a surrogate optimization, as
this problem is computationally really demanding.
After this study, the further works could be carried out to have a more detailed understanding
of a HPP that includes Power-to-Methanol. This work could take a direction that would detail
the plant and its technologies, or an opposite direction that would give a systemic comprehen-
sion of this kind of plant, and how they affect the energy markets.
Existing software such as Aspen Plus are able to model the technologies used in this study,
such as the electrolyser or the methanol reactor. Having a more detailed model that would also
include the heat flows, or the catalyst used in the reactor for instance, would help to have a
better understanding of the most important parameters of the plant, and how to implement it
in real life.
On the opposite, models such as Balmorel would be relevant to understand the impact on the
energy markets of a HPP that includes Power-to-Methanol. Different scenarios on the cost of
technologies and on the demand of methanol could be carried out. This would help understand
the potential of such a plant on a national or continental point of view.
