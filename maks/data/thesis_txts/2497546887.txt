Abstract
The present project presents the technical implementation, testing and validation of a soil
moisture retrieval algorithm in Python using C-band Sentinel-1 data at high incidence
angle (∼42°). The retrieval algorithm is based on the alpha approximation. The alpha
approximation utilizes the dense temporal coverage of the Sentinel-1 mission, assuming
that changes in backscatter between subsequent acquisitions are only due to variations
in soil moisture, such that vegetation and roughness can be neglected. The area used for
testing the algorithm was chosen to be the region surrounding the Foulum test center for
agricultural studies in Denmark, due to the availability of time series from 2018 of in situ
soil moisture measurements to be used for validation. Masking of too densely vegetated
areas have been performed using the cross-polarized component of the SAR backscatter,
which have been validated using NDVI maps.
Auxiliary data, including land cover maps and parcel borders enable the computation
of backscatter ﬁeld means, signiﬁcantly reducing the impact of speckle noise and thus
decreasing uncertainty of the estimated soil moisture. Consequently, the results have ﬁeld
scale resolution (i.e. ∼0.1 km). The permittivity to soil moisture inversion is performed
using the Hallikainen polynomial model, where a soil texture map provide the information
necessary to obtain precise results.
Further work will aim toward applying a change detection algorithm in order to detect
sudden temporal changes in vegetation and surface roughness, as the alpha approximation
is inherently sensitive to such sudden changes.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This project will be concerned with the estimation of soil moisture over agricultural land,
using Polarimetric SAR C-band images with dense temporal coverage, i.e. using Sentinel-
1 data. This will be part of the MOIST (Managing and Optimizing Irrigation using
Satellite Tools) project in an eﬀort to optimize irrigation strategies through estimation
of crop water stress and evapotranspiration, for which soil moisture is a crucial factor.
Many methods for estimating surface soil moisture (SSM) exist, hence the ﬁrst step
of the project will be to evaluate current SSM retrieval algorithms applied to vegetated
(agricultural) areas and to choose an appropriate method.
In particular, the alpha approximation method proposed by [1] shows promise as it
has the capability to decouple the eﬀect of soil moisture from surface roughness and
vegetation by considering the ratio of co-polarized backscatter coeﬃcients from SAR ac-
quisitions made on consecutive days. This assumes that surface roughness and vegetation
eﬀects change much slower than soil moisture, such that they can be assumed invariant
between consecutive acquisitions. As with many other SSM retrieval methods, the alpha
approximation also assumes that the backscatter is dominated by the attenuated surface
scattering in the Water Cloud Model (WCM).
The latter assumption is tested in the later work by [2] who develops a simple thresh-
olding algorithm to classify farmlands as dominated by either volume or surface scattering
using the cross-polarized backscatter component.
Therefore, in this project the thresholding method by [2] is implemented along with
the alpha approximation method. An important aspect is the validation of the classiﬁca-
tion performed by the thresholding algorithm. Ways to do have been investigated, where
the initial idea was to use optical remote sensing images and its derivatives e.g. NDVI
to compare with.
In the following sections, some of the basic concepts related to and important for soil
moisture retrieval from space-borne platforms are introduced. Afterwards an outline for
the structure of the report is given.

1.1
Synthetic Aperture Radar
Synthetic Aperture Radar is an imaging radar (RAdio Detection And Ranging) technique
that builds upon the concepts of the Side-Looking Airborne Radar (SLAR). In a SLAR
system, the radar antenna is mounted on an airborne moving platform, where it transmits
radar pulses, and records the returned radar echoes (backscatter). The antenna direction
is called the range direction, while the perpendicular direction (direction of the along-
track movement of the platform) is known as the azimuth direction. In the SLAR system,
the resolution in the range direction is determined by the bandwidth of the transmitted
radio signal, while the azimuth resolution is inversely proportional to the diameter of the
antenna [3], and linearly depends on the range, such that, ρaz = λR/D, where ρaz is the
azimuth resolution, λ is the wavelength, and R and D are range and aperture diameter
respectively. Thus, in order to obtain a good azimuth resolution at long ranges, a large
aperture is necessary, which makes this technique ill-suited for space-borne platforms.
Since multiple pulses hit the target as the platform moves along-track, these echoes can
be added, as if they were all recorded by one synthetic aperture. This process, along
with focussing of the obtained images due to the Doppler shift of the signal, results in a
theoretical azimuth resolution of ρaz = D/2, and is thus independent of the distance to
the target [4].
Along with measuring the intensity and frequency of the received signal, a SAR sys-
tem can also be used to measure the phase and polarization of the signal. The phase
can be used in Interferometric SAR (InSAR or SARIn) techniques, to detect ground dis-
placement (e.g. after earthquakes, land subsidence in coastal cities) or to make precise
elevation maps. The polarization is measured in Polarimetric SAR (PolSAR) systems,
where the antenna transmits either vertical or horizontal polarization, and records the
received vertical or horizontal polarization. Thus, four polarization combinations are
possible, i.e. VV, HH, VH or VH, where the ﬁrst letter denotes the transmitted polariza-
tion and the last letter denotes the received polarization. When a SAR system has the
capability to measure two polarizations simultaneously (usually VV+VH or HH+HV) it
is referred to as dual polarization, while the system is called fully polarimetric if all four
conﬁgurations are available. PolSAR is an important way to classify land areas, since the
signal polarization carry information about the physical and electromagnetic properties
of the ground.
In particular, soil moisture has a large eﬀect on the relative permittivity of the surface.
If other eﬀects on the signal polarization can be modelled, it is therefore possible to
estimate soil moisture from PolSAR images.
1.2
Soil moisture
Many methods for soil moisture retrieval exist, and they can in general be divided into
methods for optical, thermal infrared [5], [6], [7] and microwave passive and active sensors
[5], [6], [7], [8].
The following will focus on active microwave soil moisture retrieval
techniques based on [8].
==PAGE:12==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
3
Within active microwave remote sensing a wide number of soil moisture retrieval
methods exist. Common to them all is, that they all seek to estimate the soil dielec-
tric constant εs, from which the soil moisture can be inverted using a class of models
known as Dielectric Mixing Models (DMM). The active methods can be further divided
into physical, empirical, semi-empirical and change detection approaches. The physical
models model the backscatter based on the physical interaction between the SAR signal
and the ground target, and can also sometimes synonymously be referred to as Radiative
Transfer (RT) models. Examples of physical models are the well-known Small Pertur-
bation Model (SPM), the Small Slope Approximation (SSA) and the Integral Equation
Model (IEM). These models generally use as input, parameters characterizing the surface
(roughness, correlation length, etc.), frequency, incidence angle, and of course the dielec-
tric constant, to model the SAR backscatter. They are also, in general, only applicable
to bare soil, and thus do not account for the contribution from vegetation.
Semi-empirical models seek to alleviate some of the weaknesses of the physical models
by applying calibration parameters, mainly to account for surface roughness conditions,
which can not always be known, if the model is to be applied over a large area.
Empirical models, reject the basis in physical understanding altogether, and describe
a direct functional relation between backscatter and soil moisture. It is noted in [8], that
most empirical models prescribe a linear relationship, while some includes an exponential
term. Though they may describe local conditions very well due to extensive calibration,
they do so at the expense of having to be calibrated at every new location, requiring
suiting in situ observations. Another type of empirical model may be the model utilizing
neural networks (machine learning) to establish a backscatter - to - SM relation, such as
the model used in [9]. While this type of model may be eﬀective, the lack of foundation
in physical processes, and the black box nature of neural networks makes it intuitively
unattractive.
The last class of models is the change detection approach, which seek to utilize the
multi-temporal nature of modern space-borne SAR systems. Some of the most common
change detection algorithms are the delta-index method [10], the alpha approximation
method [1], and the TU Wien method [11]. Common to these methods is that they
seek to factor out the inﬂuence of roughness and vegetation by utilizing the consecutive
acquisitions. The alpha approximation is attractive, due to its ease of implementation
and that it only has a singe calibration parameter, and thus it was chosen for this project.

As mentioned, the above algorithm is applied to the VH backscatter. The rationale
behind this, is due to the observation, that vegetation tends to depolarize the signal.
Thus, the backscattered signal is expected to contain both horizontal and vertical com-
ponenets, regardless of the transmitted polarization. Contrarily, backscatter from bare
surfaces, is not depolarized to the same extent [2]. Hence, the backscatter from a bare
or sparsely vegetated surface is expected to be dominated by the co-polarized compo-
nent of the signal, while the backscatter from densely vegetated areas is expected to be
dominated by the cross-polarized component. Using this approach, [2], is then able to
adaptively determine a threshold for the VH component, and then classify areas of VH
above that threshold as dominated by volume scattering and vice versa.
From knowledge of the phenological stages and temporal evolution of the backscatter
from various types of crops, [2] is furthermore able to determine a constant threshold
of −14 dB, that well separates crops dominated by surface or volume scattering. This
constant threshold can be used in the cases where a bimodal distribution is not present
in the data, and thus an adaptive thresholding method would not be applicable.

In this chapter, the processing steps taken to obtain soil moisture are described. De-

The S-1 images that have been used in the implementation are of dimensions 1600 ×

4.1
Initialization and ﬁltering
The SAR images are stored in ENVI format, and are loaded into a numpy array in Python
via the implemented function read_binary. Along with the images themselves, parcel
borders, land cover and incidence angle maps are loaded as well. The incidence angles ϑ
were used to obtain the gamma backscatter coeﬃcient γ0 = σ0/ cos ϑ, as recommended
by [2], in order to reduce the inﬂuence of varying incidence angle over the scene.
The multichannel ﬁlter (MF), as given by equation 2.28, is then applied to the VV and
VH images, using the implemented function multich_simplest. This method does not
perform any spatial averaging, and thus preserves resolution, better than a simple uniform
Moving Average (MA) ﬁlter. However, the local means σi still need to be estimated from
a window centered at the pixel being ﬁltered (i.e. σi is just the result after MA ﬁltering),
for use in equation 2.28.
This results in some loss of spatial resolution, but not as
pronounced as with a MA ﬁlter. Examples of the ﬁltered images after using the MF
and MA ﬁlter is seen in ﬁgure 4.1. Note that the ENL is roughly the same, while the
multichannel ﬁlter preserves edges better than the simple MA ﬁlter.

4.2
Masking (Thresholding)
After the images have been ﬁltered, the segmentation into attenuated surface and volume
scattering classes was performed. As mentioned in section 2.2, this requires a bimodal
distribution. Figure 4.2 shows the histogram for the AOI after removal of non-agricultural
areas (forest, lakes, buildings, etc.) at doy 162. As seen, this distribution is not bimodal,
and the adaptive thresholding method is not immediately applicable. As noted in [18],
this can be due to the presence of highly unequal population sizes. To investigate further,
two crop types of roughly equal population size, each expected to belong to diﬀerent
scattering classes is considered. It is noted in [2], that rape is expected to exhibit volume
scatter due to its ramiﬁed structure after full development, while cereals (barley, wheat,
rye, etc.) is expected to be dominated by surface scattering year round. From table 3.1,
we see that winter rye and winter rape have roughly the same population sizes. Therefore,
these two crop classes are used to see if they can generate a bimodal distribution, which
can be seen in ﬁgure 4.3. As seen from ﬁgure 4.3, the rye and rape ﬁelds are well separated
at doy 66 (7 March) and 162 (11 June), while they almost perfectly overlap at doy 282

4.3
Alpha approximation
This section describes the alpha approximation implementation, which is the core of this
project.
The input data to this part of the retrieval process, is the ﬁltered VV images from
section 2.3. Equation 2.15, which is used to determine αV V is fairly simple to apply,
however the important part is to determine the assumed period N where no changes in
surface roughness and vegetation occur, as well as the parameter αmin.
[12] and [31] report using N = 4 in their study of the DEMMIN site in Germany
using ENVISAT/ASAR images with an average revisit period of 10 days. This implies,
an assumption of 40 days without change in surface or vegetation conditions. Making
the same assumption, this is equivalent to using N = 6 for the S-1 data with 6 days
exact revisit period. Initially in this project, it was decided to also use N = 4, but later
we will see how changing this parameter will impact the results. The other parameter of
importance, αmin, is a scaling parameter that the retrieved α value is proportional to,
and thus will have a big inﬂuence. Optimally, it should be tuned according to the time
of year, as well as spatially, to account for spatial and temporal variability.
During the development and testing of all the parts of algorithm, it was decided to
use αmin = 0.5 for all pixels and days of the year. This made it easier to focus on the
other parts, such as bug ﬁxing etc. The value αmin = 0.5, was chosen by assuming a
minimum percentage of soil moisture of mv,min = 0.03 and inserting that into equation

4.4
Fresnel inversion
The inversion of the retrieved αV V values is performed as described in section 2.1.1, by
inverting equation 2.17, by rewriting it into a fourth degree polynomial.
The implemented function fresnel_quartic_coeff is ﬁrst applied, and computes
the polynomial coeﬃcients as given by equation 2.19 from inputs of αvv and incidence
angle.
The computed coeﬃcients are then given as input to the other implemented
function quartic_solver, which computes the four solutions to the quartic equation as
described in appendix A. Between zero or all of the four solutions may be real, however
it is expected that two will be real and two will be complex [Balenzano, private comm.].

Chapter 5
Results and discussion
This chapter present the results from validation and testing of the implemented retrieval
algorithm.
Firstly, the sensitivity of retrieved soil moisture to the choice of the two
parameters αmin and N is examined in sections 5.1 and 5.2. Afterwards, the resulting
VH masks are presented, and it is studied if masks indeed agree with NDVI. Uncertainty
maps are generated, in order to clarify if algorithm assumptions have been met. This
leads to the analysis of said uncertainty maps with comparison to crop calendars, in
order to assess the underlying physical reason for the invalid assumptions. Finally, the
retrieved soil moisture maps are compared to precipitation data.

conditions.

obtain retrieved SM from the synthetic data.

Chapter 6
Future work
This chapter discusses how the individual parts of the work in this thesis could be con-
tinued.
Further work on optimizing the masking of vegetated areas is needed. As was seen in
some of the results, anomalously high soil moistures are retrieved for individual ﬁelds and
crop types, which most likely is caused by changes in vegetation or soil roughness condi-
tions. This work could include a study on how to better mask using the VH backscatter,
since the analyzed scene was imaged at high incidence angles (42° −43°), which might
justify revisiting the constant VH threshold of −14dB. This is because in [2], the −14dB
is based on images acquired at incidence angles between 23° −33°. Although the γ0
backscatter is less sensitive to incidence angle, a diﬀerence of around 10° is probably
still enough to result in a signiﬁcant reduction in backscatter. Thus, a densely vegetated
ﬁeld with γ0 above −14dB at 30° incidence angle, may have γ0 below −14dB at higher
incidence angle and therefore not be masked by a −14dB threshold.
The capabilities of the implemented algorithm have been demonstrated, and it has
been shown that the results are inﬂuenced by the choice of the parameters αmin and N.
Though in situ measurements of soil moisture are available, it was not possible to use it
for calibration to a satisfying extent, due to it spanning the year 2018, while the images
used are from 2017. Thus, further work within this topic is important so as to correctly
calibrate the αmin parameter. As discussed in section 2.1, it would be interesting to use
low resolution soil moisture maps derived from ESA’s SMOS or NASA’s SMAP in order
to obtain αmin values that calibrate the results both spatially and temporally. The SM
maps computed by the SMOSAR algorithm [31], which also uses the alpha approximation
were also available over AOI for comparison, but they were not used. This was because
it was decided that it would not make sense to compare the absolute levels of SM maps
that were calibrated and some that were not calibrated. However, in the future, where
the SM maps of this thesis have been calibrated, this comparison would be interesting.
The analysis of sensitivity to soil texture is also a point for future studies. Detailed
soil texture maps are available in Denmark but that is not necessarily the case in other
countries. An idea could be to apply diﬀerent DMM’s for this purpose.
Initially in this project it was thought that the alpha approximation algorithm could

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This thesis presents the implementation and testing of a soil moisture retrieval algorithm
based on the alpha approximation method of estimating the dielectric constant of the
soil. The present report thus describes the necessary theory along with the data used for
testing.
The main body of work in this thesis, lies in the implementation phase, where the
algorithm elements all have implemented from the bottom in Python, based on theory in
the literature. Thereafter, has been the testing of the generated results, as described in
the Results chapter, in which the capabilities of the current implementation are demon-
strated and it is described how the retrieved soil moisture maps are sensitive to the
parameters αmin and N. Here, it was seen that the retrieved soil moisture is more sen-
sitive to positive oﬀsets in αmin than to negative oﬀsets. Additionally, it is observed
that assuming a longer period of roughness and vegetation invariance N result in higher
values of SM.
The retrieved SM maps are not usable in their current state, due to the lack of
calibration using ground measurements. Therefore it is concluded that said calibration
is necessary for the SM maps to have practical usefulness, as discussed under the Future
work chapter.
Furthermore, it is observed that the masks derived from the cross-polarized compo-
nent of the backscatter coeﬃcient do not mask all the anomalous ﬁelds, particularly in
the summer. It is therefore concluded that further work on this topic is needed.

def fresnel_vv(eps,th):
""" Evaluate the Fresnel equation for VV polarization.
Input:

th : incidence angle (rad), Should be in the interval [0,pi/2[
Output: Fresnel coefficient (alpha_vv)
"""
sint2 = np.sin(th)**2
cost = np.cos(th)
num = (eps-1)*(sint2-eps*(1+sint2))
den = (eps*cost+np.sqrt(eps-sint2))**2
return abs(num/den)

""" Performs the simplest multichannel filtering, assuming no
