Abstract
Solid oxide electrolysis cell (SOEC) is a technology to convert H2O into H2 using
renewable electricity from the intermittent renewable energy from solar and wind. It has
a higher conversion efﬁciency compared to other electrolysis technologies such as pro-
ton exchange membrane electrolysis cell (PEMEC) and alkaline electrolysis cell (AEC),
due to its high operating temperature. The main cell components of SOECs are mostly
YSZ and Ni, which are based on abundant materials on earth rather than noble metals
for PEMECs. However, SOEC is still an immature technology in the research and devel-
opment (R&D) phase. To enhance its performance and scale-up to commercialization,
modiﬁcations like improving the processing method of electrodes, using mixed ionic and
electronic conducting materials, or increasing the surface area of electrochemically active
phases on especially fuel electrode have raised great interests.
This thesis focuses on studying the performance of inﬁltrated cells tested by previous
work, and improving the performance of electrodes by inﬁltrating with different electro-
chemical materials. The electrochemical performance of the developed electrodes were
mainly evaluated through electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) and polarization
curves (iV curves). EIS analysis was performed by ﬁtting with equivalent circuit model
using complex nonlinear least square (CNLS) method and distribution of relaxation time
(DRT) plots using RAVDAV package. Post-mortem methods such as X-ray diffraction
(XRD) analysis and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) were also applied, in order to
see the microstructure change of nano-particles on inﬁltrated fuel electrode before and
after the durability test.
Cell performance of 4 previous tests was analyzed in Chapter 3. Two cell types are
used in these tests, with cell type 1 with LSCF/CGO as the oxygen electrode while cell
type 2 using LSC/CGO as the oxygen electrode. Besides, both cells consist of a Ni/3YSZ
support layer, a Ni/8YSZ active layer, an 8YSZ electrolyte, and a CGO10 (Ce0.9Gd0.1O2)
inter-diffusion barrier layer. Active area of all cells are 4×4 cm2. 4 cell tests, namely,
11test104, 11test108, 3test141, and 3test153 were inﬁltrated with different amount of
CGO or NiO-CGO solutions into the Ni/YSZ electrode, and being tested under either
800 oC or 750 oC (11test108) with 22.2 L/h of H2O+H2 (H2O/H2=90/10) to the Ni/YSZ
electrode and 50 L/h O2 to the LSCF/CGO or LSC/CGO electrode. 11test108 cell, which
was inﬁltrated with 250 mg/cm3 CGO per active Ni/YSZ backbone, outperforms all other

tests using cell type 1, with the voltage degradation of 22.68 mV/kh and ∆ASR of 24.92
mΩcm2/kh. This result proves that the CGO electrocatalyst and the relative loading on

To separate the impedance response into several peaks according to their characteris-

Introduction
This chapter aims to introduce the basic principles of solid oxide electrolysis cells
(SOECs), including a brief review of the role of electrodes, the most commonly used
materials, and fabrication. Degradation mechanisms under high current densities and
development through an inﬁltration approach are also introduced.
1.1
Principles of SOECs
Solid oxide cells (SOCs) are electrochemical devices capable of storing energy under
high temperatures (typically 600-900 oC). They can be operated either in electrolysis
mode (solid oxide electrolysis cells, SOECs) to convert H2O (steam) and/or CO2 into H2
or CO using surplus renewable energy, or fuel cell mode (solid oxide fuel cells, SOFCs)
to generate electricity from chemical energy (Fig. 1.1).
Fig. 1.1. Schematic of the renewable electricity storage system based on SOCs [2].
Compared to other low-temperature electrolysis cells such as alkaline electrochemi-
cal cells (AECs) and polymer electrolyte membrane cells (also known as proton exchange
membrane cells, PEMECs), SOECs are extremely advantageous by elevating the operat-
ing temperature. Not only does it avoid using expensive noble metal catalysts like plat-
inum (as for PEMCs), it also uses less electrical energy balanced by more thermal energy.
Moreover, operating at higher current densities (above -1 A cm−2) has raised great interest
in order to upscale the SOEC technology and reduce the investment cost [3]. However,

1.1.1
Oxygen electrode materials
Oxygen electrodes require high electronic conductivity, matchable thermal expan-
sion coefﬁcient (TEC) with other cell components, high catalytic activity for oxygen re-
duction or evolution reactions, and chemical and mechanical stability at high operating
temperature. Besides, a porous microstructure is required for the transport of gaseous O2
[5].
It is worth mentioning the concept of triple-phase boundary (TPB) to better explain
the reaction mechanism and kinetics. TPB is the place where oxide ions from the elec-
trolyte, electrons from the electrode, and the gaseous species from the porous interface
meet. It is considered as the active site in the SOC where the charge transfer reaction
occurs. The length of TPB has a strong inﬂuence on the electrode polarization resistance
in the electrodes.
Doped lanthanum manganite (LaMnO3) is the most studied material for the oxygen
electrode. By substituting La with low-valence elements such as strontium ((La, Sr)MnO3
LSM), a large amount of Mn4+ will form, thus enabling excellent p-type electronic con-
ductivity. However, due to its low ionic conductivity and slow surface oxygen exchange
kinetics, other desirable electrode materials are to be found [5].
Mixed ionic and electronic conductors (MIEC) have received great attention due to
the increase of TPB active sites. Mechanism of charge transfer in an MIEC is shown in
Fig. 1.2.
Fig. 1.2. TPB mechanism and MIEC mechanism of charge transfer. Note that O2– can
transfer through the entire MIEC instead of TPB only [6].
In general, with lanthanum on the A site and transit metals such as cobalt or nickel
on the B site in a perovskite structure (Fig. 1.3), materials like (La, Sr)(Co, Fe)O3 show

kinetics [9].
1.1.2
Fuel electrode materials
The requirements for the fuel electrode materials are similar to those for the oxygen
electrode, which have excellent catalytic activity, high stability at operating temperatures
and in reducing conditions, sufﬁcient porosity, matchable TEC with that of electrolyte
and interconnect materials, chemical stability, and cost-effectiveness. Currently, Ni-YSZ
cermet is the most commonly used fuel electrode material, where Ni plays the role of
being a catalyst as well as an electrical current conductor. Optimization of Ni-YSZ elec-
trode was studied by Anne et al. for high performance and long-term stability operated
at high p(H2O) (p(H2O/p(H2)=90/10)) and high current densities (-1 and -1.25 A cm−2).
The SOEC cells with ﬁne particle size and dense microstructure in the active Ni/8YSZ
electrode showed a degradation rate of 55 mV/kh and 310 mV/kh when operated at -1 A
cm−2 and -1.25 A cm−2, respectively [11]. Poisoning by the silica impurities in the inlet
gas can highly affect the durability of Ni-YSZ based SOEC cell, as reported by Ebbesen

1.1.3
Electrolyte materials
Yittria-stabilized zirconia (YSZ), with a typical yttria content of 8 mol%, is the most
common material for the electrolyte. It provides long-term stability and reasonable ionic
conductivity under high operating temperatures. It also possesses high fracture toughness
among all the metal oxides but little electronic conductivity [13]. On the other hand,
gadolinium-doped ceria (CGO or GDC), with the same ﬂuorite structure as YSZ, shows
signiﬁcant ionic conductivity as well as electronic conductivity (Fig. 1.5). Optimal dopant
concentrations for CGO are found in the range of 10-20mol%. The main factor that gives
CGO higher conductivity than other materials is the similar sizes of the dopant ions and
primary ions. It was reported that to increase the oxygen ion conductivity, it is desirable
to have the size of dopants as close as possible to that of the host ions [13].
In addition, some perovskite structured materials like La1–xSrxGa1–yMgyO3–δ (LSGM)
and yttrium-doped barium zirconate (BZY) are also considered as promising electrolyte
materials due to their excellent ionic conductivity and long-term stability [5].

been reported that impurity poisoning of the active sites of Ni/YSZ TPBs is the main
cause for the degradation. It may be avoided by cleaning the feed stream before entering

that the main degradation is due to the internal pore formation in the YSZ electrolyte and
inhomogeneties of the LSCF anode [17].

1.3
Electrode activation via inﬁltration
Inﬁltration is gaining increasing attention as a promising approach for modifying
the electrodes in SOCs [20]. It includes the following steps as shown in Fig. 1.7: i)
preparation of porous electrode backbone, ii) inﬁltration of the liquid solution or sol which
contains the stoichiometric metal salt precursors into the backbone, iii) heat treatment to
form the desired morphologies [21].
Commonly, metal salt precursors are prepared with surfactant. In most cases, metal
nitrates are used and dissolved in distilled water as the inﬁltration solution. The function
of the surfactant is to reduce the surface tension and facilitate the solution to penetrate into
the porous backbone [22]. Multiple inﬁltration cycles are normally required to achieve
the required loading on the electrode.
The inﬁltration method will impact both activation polarization originating from the
charge transfer resistance at the electrochemical reaction sites, and concentration polar-
ization due to the resistance of mass transfer through pores [23]. Electrode inﬁltration
with electrocatalyst solutions enhances the TPBs by introducing various electrocatalysts
into the electrode, thus reducing activation polarization of the electrode thereby enhanc-
ing the electrode performance [20, 24, 25]. On the other hand, it was reported by Lu
et al. that inﬁltrating Ni nanoparticles into the Ni/YSZ electrode backbone can theoret-

1.4
Motivation and objectives of the study
Although the SOEC technology has shown great potential in energy storage and
raised interest in recent years, the commercialization of SOEC is still subject to insuf-
ﬁcient durability. Fuel electrodes play a key role in the stability and durability of an
SOEC, and its performance can be enhanced through inﬁltration. This thesis aims to
study and further develop SOEC electrodes with improved stability and durability at high
current densities through inﬁltration. Since wet-chemistry material synthesis has proved
to increase the TPB density without being calcinated at high temperatures, four electro-
catalysts candidates with high conductivity and catalytic properties are selected to be
inﬁltrated into the Ni/YSZ electrode in this work. There have already been a considerable
amount of published papers on most of the inﬁltrated materials, but inﬁltrating proton
conductor into the Ni/YSZ fuel electrode has not been studied before.
In Chapter 2, inﬁltrated candidates (Ni, Ru, M-CGO, BZCY) and rig test informa-

2
Materials and methods
Electrode materials and testing conditions affect the performance of an SOEC cell.
This chapter introduces detailed information on two cell types, inﬁltrated materials, and
cell test set-up. The characterization techniques to analyze the cell performance, includ-
ing electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) for electrochemical characterization,
scanning electron microscopy (SEM) for the post-test microstructural analysis, and X-ray
diffraction (XRD) to examine the chemical composition of the nanoparticles, are also
illustrated in this chapter.
2.1
Cell information
2.1.1
Cell type 1
Cell type 1 consists of a NiO/3YSZ (3 mol.% Y2O3 stabilized ZrO2) support layer
(~300µm in thickness), a NiO/8YSZ (8 mol.% Y2O3 stabilized ZrO2) active fuel electrode
(~10µm), an 8YSZ electrolyte (~8µm) and an LSCF/CGO (LSCF: La0.6Sr0.4Co0.2Fe0.8O3–δ)
oxygen electrode (~25µm) with a CGO10 (Ce0.9Gd0.1O2) inter-diffusion barrier layer
(~5µm) in between the electrolyte and the oxygen electrode [26]. A 4×4 cm2 LSM
((La0.75Sr0.25)0.95MnO3–δ) current-collector layer (CCL) was screen-printed on top of
the LSCF/CGO electrode.
2.1.2
Cell type 2
Cell type 2 is a 2.XG cell, with Ni/YSZ (=50/50) as the fuel electrode, YSZ the
electrolyte, LSC/CGO (LSC: (La0.6Sr0.4)0.99CoO3–δ) as oxygen electrode with a CGO10
inter-diffusion layer [27]. Lanthanum strontium cobalt oxide (LSC: (La0.6Sr0.4)0.99CoO3–δ)
is applied on top of the oxygen electrode to function as a CCL, with an active area of 4×4
cm2.

ium doped ceria, also known as GDC or CGO. It is of great interest to study this ﬂuorite
crystal-structured-based material owing to its high oxide ion and electronic conductivity,
appreciable electrocatalytic activity, and excellent chemical stability under reducing con-
ditions [28, 29]. The optimum dopant concentration for Gd in CeO2 is in the range of
10-20% [13]. In this work, Ce0.8Gd0.2O1.9 is selected as the inﬁltrated material, with the
ionic conductivity of 0.055 S cm−1 at 800 oC [30].
Ovtar et al. has reported the 3000 h durability test results on a Ni/YSZ based cell
with CGO inﬁltrated into the fuel electrode [1]. The cell was tested at 800 oC and -1.25
A cm−2 with 22.2 L/h H2O+H2 (H2O/H2=90/10) to the fuel electrode and 50 L/h O2 to
the oxygen electrode. The degradation rate was decreased to 66 mV kh−1 compared to
the cell with bare Ni/YSZ electrode, which was 699 mV kh−1. They also reported that
the CGO inﬁltration changed the mechanisms for steam reduction reaction. Not only are
the Ni/YSZ TPBs active reaction sites, Ce-containing particles on the Ni as well as on
the YSZ grains are also active reaction sites. These are both crucial points for the steam
electrolysis.
2.2.2
Ru
Ru has high activity and improves carbon tolerance on the fuel electrode of an SOC
[31, 32, 33]. It was reported by Kim et al. that Ru-doped SYTRu (Sr0.92Y0.08Ti0.98Ru0.02O3+/δ)
electrode has higher activity and stability than bare SYT for methane dry reforming due
to the improved oxygen mobility [31]. Sun et al. reported the performance of a fuel cell
under the direct utilization of ethanol after being wet-impregnated with RuCl3 solvent
[34]. A nanostructured Ru layer was formed with nano Ru particles uniformly dispersed
on both electrode surface and the inner surface of Ni and YSZ particles. Therefore TPB
area on the Ni/YSZ electrode was highly extended. Moreover, nearly no carbon deposi-
tion with negligible degradation was found with Ru-Ni/YSZ cells, with the max power
density of 369 mW cm−2 for a Ru-Ni-YSZ cell and that of 312 mW cm−2 for a bare
Ni-YSZ cell at 750 oC.

2.4
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM)
It is possible to investigate the micro- and nanostructure of the cells by using scan-
ning electron microscopy (SEM). It produces images by scanning the surfaces with a
focused beam of electrons. When the accelerated electrons hit the sample, they will inter-
act with the sample material and thereby emitting Auger electrons, secondary electrons
(SE), backscattered electrons (BSE), characteristic X-rays, and continuum X-rays differ-
ing with energies (Fig. 2.4). These emitted electrons and X-rays can be converted to
digital images after being detected by the speciﬁc detectors [46].
SEs have low energies (<50 eV) and can only escape from the top few nanometers of
the sample surface. Signals from SEs are localized at the point of impact of the primary
electron beam, and they provide beneﬁcial information on the sample surface topogra-
phy and morphology [47]. BSEs have higher energies than SEs emerging from deeper
locations from the sample surface, therefore the resolution is less than SE images. They
occur due to the collisions of the electrons with atoms and cause the change of the elec-
trons’ track. They can provide the distribution in multi-phase samples, composition, and
crystallography information [48].
In this work, high resolution images were obtained using a Zeiss Merlin microscope.
It is equipped with HE-SE2 (for topography), In-lens (for surface structure and Ni per-
colation), EsB (Energy Selective Backscattered detector, for pure material contrast), AsB
and STEM detectors [49]. Images were taken at acceleration voltages ranging from 15
kV to lower than 1 kV to detect the microstructure and phase distribution with secondary,
back-scattered, and in-lens secondary electron detectors. A tabletop SEM (HITACHI,
TM3000) was also used for lower resolution images.

X-ray diffraction (XRD) is a non-destructive technique to characterize the atomic

A regular array of electrons produces a regular array of spherical waves, and they
can be added constructively in some speciﬁc directions. These constructive interferences
by X-rays can be observed if they satisfy Bragg’s law, determined by:

here θ is the incident angle (2θ is the angle between the incoming and outgoing beam

Complex nonlinear least-square (CNLS) method
A complex nonlinear least-square method (CNLS) based on the algorithm developed
by Marquardt and Levenberg is used to ﬁt equivalent circuits for different processes [58],
as Fig. 2.8 shows. If the impedance spectrum can be ﬁt in a CNLS mode, it will be
possible to further extract information about the reaction kinetics and performance of the
cell.
Fig. 2.8. Equivalent circuit model used to ﬁt the measured data of the impedance spectra
for: (a) data measured under OCV and under current load and (b) data measured under
current load with a low-frequency inductance loop [1]
In a CNLS model, electrical elements typically consists of inductance (L), ohmic
resistance (Rs) in series of polarization resistance (Rp). Hence, the impedance diagram
can be separated into different frequency ranges. As is shown in the CNLS model (a),
the impedance spectrum tested by this work is ﬁtted with an equivalent model named:
L −R0 −R1Q1 −R2Q2 −G −R4Q4 −R5Q5, consisting of an inductor L, R0 the ohmic
resistance, four resistors in parallel with constant phase element (CPE) Q, and a Gerischer
Impedance G. Q is used to describe a non-ideal capacitive behavior, which is widely used
to ﬁt the impedance diagrams. The CPE impedance is expressed as:
ZCPE = 1/Y0(jw)n
where Y0 is the adjustable parameter containing the diffusion coefﬁcient, and n is the
frequency-independent CPE model parameter [3, 59, 60].
Nyquist plot of a resistance in parallel with a CPE is a depressed semicircular arc
with the angle of (n−1)×90o. The meaning of CPE varies with different n values. When
n = 1, CPE represents a pure capacitor with Y0 = C, and when n = 0, CPE corresponds
to an ideal resistor with Y0 = 1/R. In the CNLS model, the smallest value for n is 0.5,
representing a special case where the impedance is created by the diffusion resistance to
current ﬂow carried by electroactive species [3]. Therefore, depending on the n value

3.2.1
11test104
Initial performance
11test104 cell was tested at 800 oC with a total CGO loading of 125 mg/cm3. Fig.
3.1 illustrates the EIS data recorded under OCV with varying gas feeds to two electrodes.
Nyquist plot shows that with lower steam (20%) fed to the fuel electrode, Rp has a greater
value than that of with 50% steam. Besides, when pure oxygen was fed to the oxygen
electrode, the cell has the lowest Rp. From the Bode plot can be seen that low frequency
Rp has the most signiﬁcant change. This implies that gas conversion and gas diffusion
processes are affected most by the gas composition supplied to both electrodes. Rs should
keep the same value in principle since it originates from the oxygen ion transport across
the electrolyte, and the electrons transport in the electrodes. Other factors that might lead
to Rs change could be the temperature change, or the cell testing set-up [1, 16]. In this
case, since Tcenter of the cell under all three test conditions are 807 oC, it is a bit uncertain
about the reason causing the Rs change.
DRT method is applied to identify the frequency ranges for the different processes,
as is introduced in Chap 2.5.2. Five peaks can be identiﬁed from the DRT plot from high
frequency to low frequency, representing ﬁve different electrochemcial processes (P1-P5)
[1, 11, 63, 64], the following can be concluded: process P1, which is the ionic transport
process in the electrodes, is independent of the gas change supplied to two electrodes.
P2 represents the electrochemical process at the TPBs of the Ni/YSZ electrode, which
shifts to a lower frequency with less steam fed to the fuel electrode. P3, P4 and P5 all
exhibit difference when switching the gas supply from pure oxygen to air, representing a
visible dependency to the p(O2) change. P3 presents the electrochemical process at the
oxygen electrode. P4 and P5 relate to the mass transport in the porous electrode and gas
conversion process, illustrating the highest sensitivity to the gas fed on electrodes. With
less steam supply to the fuel electrode, P5 from the gas conversion process shifts to lower
frequency with an increased resistance value. It seems that the gas conversion process is
the most sensitive to the steam content change at the Ni/YSZ fuel electrode.

4
Optimization of CGO inﬁltration
and other electrocatalysts into the
Ni/YSZ electrode
As mentioned in the previous chapter, inﬁltration of electrocatalysts solutions on the
Ni/YSZ electrode affects the cell performance during the durability test of high current
density steam electrolysis. Therefore, how different electrocatalyst affects the electro-
chemical performance on the Ni/YSZ electrode is of great interest to study. This chapter
introduces the experimental results of inﬁltrating different electrocatalysts, i.e. CGO, Ru-
CGO, and BZCY solutions into the Ni/YSZ electrode. However, due to the equipment
failure, the test of BZCY inﬁltrated cell (11test129) was terminated without any valid EIS
data. Cell performance with different inﬁltrated nanoparticles is analyzed and discussed
through electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), post-test microscopy imaging on
the cell cross-sections (SEM), and XRD analysis on the Ni/YSZ fuel electrode side.
4.1
Experimental
4.1.1
Cell type information
The tested cells used in this work are cell type 1, as described previously in Chapter
2.1. This type of cell has a footprint of 5.3 × 5.3 cm2, consisting of a Ni/3YSZ support
layer (~300 µm in thickness), a Ni/8YSZ active fuel electrode (~10 µm), a dense 8YSZ
electrolyte (~8 µm), a CGO10 inter-diffusion barrier layer (~5µm), and an LSCF/CGO
oxygen electrode (~25 µm). A 4 × 4 cm2 LSM current collect layer was applied on top
of the LSCF/CGO electrode by screen-printing.
4.1.2
Solution preparation
CGO
0.3M CGO solution was prepared by adding measured amounts of Ce(NO3)3 · 6H2O
and Gd(NO3)3 · 6H2O (molar ration of Ce3+:Gd3+= 4:1) to the distilled water, and mixed
using the magnetic stir until all salts are dissolved. Afterwards, surfactant P123 (Poly(ethylene
glycol)-block-poly(propylene glycol)-block-poly(ethylene glycol, EO19PO69EO19)) with
1.4 wt.% per weight of solution was added in the solution to reduce the surface tension
75
==PAGE:86==
4.1. EXPERIMENTAL
of the solution, and to increase its spreading and wetting properties [84, 1]. The solution
was stirred using a magnetic stir for a couple of days before use.
Ru-CGO
0.5M Ru-CGO solution was made by dissolving the appropriate amount of RuCl3 · 3H2O
into an already prepared 0.5M CGO solution without adding surfactant. Ru is 2wt% of
the total solution (weight of Ru/CGO=2/98). The solution was stirred for two days before
use.
BZCY
0.5M BaZr0.1Ce0.7Y0.2O3–δ solution was prepared by dissolving a weighed amount
of Ba(NO3)2 in deionised water and stirred at 80 oC, then adding a measured amount
of solid citric acid (∑metals/citric acid molar ratios= 1:2). Ammonia was then added
dropwise to adjust the pH value in the range of 9-10. In a separate beaker, stoichio-
metric amounts of ZrO(NO3)2 · 2.35H2O, Ce(NO3)3 · 6H2O, and Y(NO3)3 · 6H2O were
dissolved with an appropriate amount of water. After being dissolved, it was added drop-
wisely to the previously prepared Ba(NO3)2 solution to prevent precipitation (see Fig.
4.1). The ﬁnal solution was stirred at 80 oC overnight and ready to be further calcined at
elevated temperatures at 600 oC, 700 oC, 800 oC, and 900 oC, respectively, for 3 hours at
air or safety H2 condition (N2/H2=95/5). After calcination, all the powders were grounded
and characterized by XRD analysis to determine under which condition can form the tar-
geted BZCY single phase.

4.1.3
Inﬁltration procedure
Before inﬁltration, the weight of each cell is measured to determine the catalyst
loading after each inﬁltration cycle. Then, a few drops of the respective metal nitrate
solution were dropped onto the Ni/YSZ electrode surface and left for approximately 30
seconds for the backbone to absorb. The residual solution after the absorption was gently
wiped off using tissue paper. The distribution of inﬁltrated material inside the electrode

backbone is shown in Fig. 1.7 in Chapter 1. After the inﬁltration, the cell was calcined
at 300 oC in the air for 12 minutes to decompose the nitrates/chloride. The heating ramp
is set as 5 oC/min. After the calcination, the cell was weighed again to determine the
catalyst loading after each cycle. This procedure was repeated several times to obtain the
desired catalyst loading.
For the CGO inﬁltrated cell, the inﬁltration and calcination processes were repeated 3
times. Thus, the total loading is around 47 mg CGO per 1 cm3 Ni/YSZ backbone (Ni/YSZ
electrode + Ni/YSZ support). 0.5M Ru-CGO solution was inﬁltrated on the Ni/YSZ elec-
trode with 3 cycles, with a total loading of 100 mg/cm3 (per Ni/YSZ backbone). For the
BZCY inﬁltrated cell, the inﬁltration procedure was repeated 3 times, achieving the total
loading of 94.3 mg/cm3 (per Ni/YSZ backbone). Information of these inﬁltrated cell are
summarized in Table 4.1.
4.1.4
Cell reduction and durability test set-up
All cells were mounted in the alumina house and tested in rigs. Before inﬁltrating,
the cell need to be reduced ﬁrst in the test house to reduce the NiO/YSZ fuel electrode to
Ni/YSZ (Fig. 4.2). The cell was heated up to 850 oC at a temperature ramp of 1 oC/min
while supplying 20 L/h Argon gas to the fuel electrode and 20 L/h air to the oxygen
electrode. Once the temperature reached to 850 oC, gas supply was switched from Argon
to safety H2 (N2/H2=95/5) to the fuel electrode, and increase the airﬂow to 50 L/h to the
oxygen electrode for 2 hours. After that, supply 24 L/h H2 and 0.5 L/h O2 to the fuel
electrode and increase the airﬂow to 140 L/h to the oxygen electrode. 4% steam was
generated by combusting 24 L/h H2 and 0.5 L/h O2 to the fuel electrode side. After 2
hours, the test was shut down and cooled to room temperature at a ramp of 1 oC/min. The
cell was reduced and ready for the inﬁltration treatment.
After inﬁltrating with different electrocatalyst solutions (except for the reference
cell), the cell was mounted with the same procedure in the alumina house and tested in
the rig. Different from the reducing procedure, cell was heated up to 800 oC under safety
H2 with an airﬂow of 50 L/h. Once the cell has reached the temperature, keep the cell
under the safety H2 (N2/H2=95/5) condition for 1-2 hours to decompose the nitrates/chlo-
ride from the precursors. After that, pure hydrogen with 4% H2O (24 L/h H2 + 0.5 L/h
O2) was supplied to the fuel electrode and 140 L/h of air to the oxygen electrode. Initial
performance was carried out with 4%, 20%, and 50% H2O to the fuel electrode and 50
L/h O2 or air to the oxygen electrode. After that, apply the current steply to 20 A with
22.2 L/h H2O+H2 (H2O/H2=90/10) to the Ni/YSZ electrode and 50 L/h O2 to the oxygen
electrode, followed by the 100 h durability test. The cells were then brought back to OCV

4.3
Conclusion
In this chapter, different electrocatalyst, i.e. CGO, Ru–CGO, and BZCY solutions
were inﬁltrated on the fuel electrode of the Ni/YSZ-YSZ-CGO-LSCF/CGO cell, and the
results are compared with 11test104 cell. Unfortunately, due to the equipment failure,
the test of BZCY inﬁltrated cell was terminated without valid EIS data. However, XRD
analysis was carried out to further determine the BZCY phase on the Ni/YSZ electrode
after being operated at 800 oC for a few hours.
When operated the cell under steam electrolysis with 22.2 L/h H2O+H2 (H2O/H2=90/10)
to the Ni/YSZ electrode and 50 L/h O2 to the LSCF/CGO electrode at 800 oC under -1.25
A/cm2 current density, signiﬁcant differences can be observed between the non-inﬁltrated
reference cell and the inﬁltrated cells. It was found that the CGO inﬁltrated cell has better
initial performance under different gas conditions compared to the reference cell, with a
lower resistance at Ni/YSZ electrode. During the up-current process, it was found that the
biggest difference between these cells is the electrochemical processes occurring at high
frequency (~104 Hz). The electrochemical process at Ni/YSZ electrode of the reference
cell is more sensitive to the increased current than that of the inﬁltrated cells, in compar-
ison, the Ru-CGO inﬁltrated cell shows the least sensitivity to the applied current. Cell
voltages at OCV and at -1.25 A cm2 of the inﬁltrated cells are also compared. Among the
three tested cells, Ru-CGO inﬁltrated cell has the lowest OCV, i.e. 878.17 mV, and closest
to the theoretical EMF. However, it has the most increased cell voltage after applying the
current densities, with the value of 418 mV, compared to the CGO inﬁltrated cell (287
mV) and 11test104 (251 mV). This indicates that the Ru-CGO inﬁltrated cell degrades
drastically while applying the current densities.

5
Summary and outlook
5.1
Summary
SOEC technology has great potential in energy conversion and storage. Therefore,
modifying the conventional SOEC cells for high current density electrolysis applications
is being explored to obtain more efﬁcient performance. In this thesis, the wet-inﬁltration
method was studied, and the performance of the long-term durability tests of 4 previous
inﬁltrated cells was analyzed. In the experimental part, inﬁltrated cells and a reference cell
each had a 100 h durability test conducted. All the studies yield the following conclusions.
In Chapter 3, the performance of four inﬁltrated cells operated under high tempera-
ture at harsh current densities was analyzed. According to the EIS characterization, it was
found that the resistance at high frequencies (103-104 Hz), corresponding to the electro-
chemical process at Ni/YSZ electrode, were mostly affected by the inﬁltrated materials.
Besides, gas conversion and gas diffusion processes are temperature independent and have
little changes among the inﬁltrated and non-inﬁltrated cells. After ~6000 hours operation
at -1.25 A cm−2, the inductance loop at low current densities occurred on 11test104,
11test108 and 3test141, due to the reduction of YSZ in the Ni/YSZ electrode because of
the electrode polarization. The appearance of the low-frequency inductance loop might
be postponed by increasing the operation temperature, yet it needs to be clariﬁed in fu-
ture work. From the CNLS ﬁttings it can be concluded that resistance contributing to Rp
from all processes increase with time in general, with Ni/YSZ electrode having the most
signiﬁcant increase. However, resistance from the gas diffusion process decreases with
time, possibly due to the increases of pore size with time at high temperature, thereby
decreasing the resistance of the gas diffusion process, or due to the error from the ﬁtting.
Rs, however, increases with time for all four tests.
XRD analysis of the 11test104 cell showed the occurrence of monoclinic ZrO2 and
Ce containing secondary phases on the Ni/YSZ electrode after the test. NiO also oc-
curred, indicating the oxidation of the Ni, possibly due to the sealing deterioration. These
unexpected materials contribute to a lower ionic conductivity and the volume change of
the particles, leading to a weaker structure or even the delamination/fracture of the fuel
electrode and the electrolyte interface. SEM images show the porosity difference in the
Ni/YSZ electrode, with the active electrode layer (~10µm) having the largest porosity
92
==PAGE:103==
CHAPTER 5. SUMMARY AND OUTLOOK
and the neighbouring support layer having the smallest. This conﬁrms the Ni particles
migrate away from the electrolyte/electrode interface to the neighbouring support layer.
By further quantifying the porosity for the three pieces located at the inlet, outlet, and
middle of the cell, it can be concluded that Ni migration is more signiﬁcant at the inlet
than at the middle and outlet due to the difference in the steam supply direction, current
and temperature distribution on the cell under the testing conditions. Moreover, CGO
nanoparticles can impede the loss of Ni percolation, compared to the non-inﬁltrated cell
from the previous study. It further conﬁrms that the Ni/YSZ electrode performance can
be improved through CGO inﬁltration.
In Chapter 4, three cells were tested and compared with 11test104, i.e., the non-
inﬁltrated reference cell, CGO inﬁltrated cell, Ru-CGO inﬁltrated cell. The EIS data
analysis shows that the inﬁltrated materials mostly affect the electrochemical process on
the Ni/YSZ electrode, with lower resistance at high frequencies (103-104 Hz) and less
sensitive to the applied current. During the 100 h durability test, the reference cell had
the largest cell voltage degradation and Rp degradation among all the tested cells, with
the values of 18.3 mV/kh and 17.3 mΩcm2. This further proves the positive effect on
the cell performance with the inﬁltrated electrocatalyst. The cell voltage, Rs and Rp for
the Ru-CGO inﬁltrated cell, on the other hand, decrease with time. A similar situation
was also found in 3test153 and previous studies by others. This might be due to the cell
activation at the beginning of the durability test. From the high-resolution SEM images
the distribution of the inﬁltrated CGO and Ru-CGO nanoparticles on Ni/YSZ grains can
clearly be noticed. However, because of the short time durability test, no signiﬁcant
difference in Ni percolation or the microstructure change between the reference cell and
the inﬁltrated cells could be observed.
In summary, the ﬁndings reported in this thesis demonstrate the potential of the inﬁl-
tration method to enhance cell performance, especially from the electrochemical process
on the Ni/YSZ fuel electrode. Moreover, the choice of electrocatalyst materials and the
corresponding loading on the Ni/YSZ electrode also affect the cell performance.
5.2
Outlook
More work needs to be done to further improve the performance of the SOECs,
inspired by the results from this thesis.
The degradation mechanism of the fuel electrode could result from the migration of
Ni particles away from the electrolyte-active electrode interface to the active electrode-
93
==PAGE:104==
5.2. OUTLOOK
support interface [86]. This will destroy the percolating of the Ni network nearby the
electrolyte, thereby decreasing the electrocatalytic TPB active sites and affect the elec-
trode performance. Therefore, anchoring Ni particles in the fuel active layer is the key
issue to be solved. This might be achieved by adding dopants or by inﬁltrating nanostruc-
tured Ni particles on the fuel electrode.
Reducing the ohmic resistance (Rs) of the cell can improve the performance. Though
Rs does not increase much during the durability test, it is still higher than the expected
resistance. For the experimental part of this thesis, Rs of some tests show much higher
values than the others. For example, the expected resistance for an 8 µm thick YSZ
electrolyte and a 5 µm thick CGO barrier layer is ~0.013 Ωcm2 at 800 oC [87]. However,
for all results in this thesis, Rs have higher values. Ohmic loss can be affected by a lower
ionic conductivity of the electrolyte due to the interdiffusion of the elements from the
electrodes [87]. Therefore, reducing the inter-diffusion of CGO from the barrier layer or
Ni from the fuel electrode should be explored. This can be achieved by decreasing the
thickness of the YSZ electrolyte or using scandinum-doped zirconia with a better ionic
conductivity [5].
Optimization of the inﬁltration method is to be explored. Though this thesis has an-
alyzed the performance of cells with different electrocatalyst solutions (different types
of solutions or different loading) tested under -1.25 A cm−2 with 22.2 L/h H2O+H2
(H2O/H2=90/10) to the Ni/YSZ electrode and 50 L/h O2 to the oxygen electrode, optimiza-
tion of the catalyst loading on the fuel electrode, details of the precursor solutions (con-
centration, type, and weight percentage of surfactant), and heat treatment details (time,
temperature, heating ramp) are not yet optimized. A few changes, such as changing the
heat treatment temperatures from 200-350 oC to 150-300 oC, changing the heat ramp from
240 oC/h to 300 oC/h, and changing hold time from 30 min to 12 min have been tried in
the experiments. Despite this, the optimal method has not yet been found. For example,
since CGO has a great catalytic effect on the fuel electrode, ﬁnding its optimal loading
would be interesting to study. In addition, optimization of the weight percentage of Ru or
NiO in CGO solutions would also be useful to ﬁnd.
BZCY inﬁltration is of great potential on the SOC cells since BZCY172 is a great
proton conductor candidate. It was exciting to see the single-phase of BZCY formed after
being calcined at 900 oC at air condition for 3 hours. However, this condition harms
Ni/YSZ fuel electrode due to Ni oxidation. Therefore, other cell types, for example, with
STN (Sr0.94Ti0.9Nb0.1O3), or PSCM ((Pr0.75Sr0.25)1–xCr0.5Mn0.5O3–δ) as fuel electrode,
