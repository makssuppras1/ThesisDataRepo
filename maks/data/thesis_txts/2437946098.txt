ABSTRACT
In this study a novel Magnetic Coupling (MC) system was simulated and tested using Finite
Element Methods (FEM). The case study was a wind turbine yaw system, which exploits
the potentials of the MC technology, to cope with the wearing eﬀect of its transition system.
The analysis started from an experimental conﬁguration which was modeled in COMSOL
simulation environment. The topology was evaluated over its nominal and overspeeding
performance, as well as for its thermal characteristics. From the gathered data, a new
reference model aroused in order to be incurred to a parametric analysis. Under this
analysis several geometries were investigated, with the results focusing on the torque and
magnetic ﬂux density characteristics. The overall results were afterwards analyzed, under
the scope of cost minimization and material utilization. Finally, a new model proposition
emerged, with its feasibility validated from a thermal study.
Keywords: Finite element analysis, magnetic couplings, yaw applications, synchronous
magnetic couplings, asynchronous magnetic couplings, eddy current couplers, slip couplers,
permanent magnets, torque performance,

INTRODUCTION
Initial cost and maintenance expenses are prime considerations in the selection of any
system. Comparing permanent magnet couplings (PMCs) with some mechanical adjustable-
speed drives, the coupling will have generally higher initial cost. However, a PMC can
transfer high torque without physical contact with that securing maintenance free, long
service solutions.
PMCs are getting a lot of attention, in a continuously expanding application spectrum.
PMCs couplings are most often used for liquid pumps and propeller systems, since a static,
physical barrier can be placed between the two shafts to separate the ﬂuid from the motor
operating in air [4]. PMCs in pumps are valuable, since mechanical power is transmitted
from the dry to the wet side without mechanical contact. Some diver propulsion vehicles
and remotely operated underwater vehicles also use magnetic coupling to transfer torque
from the electric motor to the propeller.

Problem statement
TechnoFlex Aps is a Danish company, with its base in Silkeborg, Jutland. With many years
of expertise, TechnoFLex has provided numerous coupling solutions for various applications.
Currently, its focus was centered on a new magnetic couplings system which could be
employed in wind turbine yaw drivetrains. A typical scheme of the yaw mechanism in
wind turbines is presented below.

Research scope and objectives
This assessment was made with the intention of developing a suitable magnetic coupling
prototype for a wind turbine yaw system. During the project’s execution, the potentials of
FEM were explored. Various geometric and material implementations were employed, with
their characteristics depicting new directions. Main goal was the achievement of feasible,
state of the art solutions, with respect on the performance needs and manufacturing costs.
Some of the key remarks and research milestones during the progress, are listed below.
• Development of an analytical 2D FEM methodology, for the designing of SMC and
AMC topologies.
• Implementation of a computational scheme, for stationary and time-dependent
studies.
• Conﬁguration of a parametrization model, for the variations in the geometries and
the other speciﬁcations.
• Inception of a routine for the quantiﬁcation of the energy dissipation and temperature
increase.
• Establishment of a validation method, for the acknowledgment of the solutions
feasibility.
1.4
Thesis layout
This MSc thesis is consisted from four chapters including the present. The topics covered
in each chapter are summarized below.
• Chapter 2. In this chapter the theoretical background of PMC technology is given.

• Chapter 3. This chapter is dedicated to the simulation’s setup. In the beginning

material utilization. At last, the study ﬁnishes with two model propositions and
their validation for their overall performance.

PERMANENT MAGNET COUPLERS
A Permanent Magnet Torque Coupler (PMC) is a device usually placed on the shaft
between two rotating mechanical systems. This type of couplings operate using strong
magnetic ﬁelds, which are governed by the laws of electromagnetism and electromagnetic
induction. PMCs were originally made from bulky and heavy electromagnets which made it
diﬃcult to implement without using slip rings and power supplies. The ﬁrst PM materials
like the ferrite magnets weren’t strong enough to be used in MCs. Today with the recent
developments in stronger and cheaper rare-earth materials like NdFeB, new applications
and topologies have emerged [6].
The use of permanent magnets in PMC couplers have the advantage of having smaller
radial and axial dimensions resulting in a coupler with lower mass. They also contribute
to bigger air gaps for easier manufacturing and a higher torque per mass ratio. Currently
some of the most popular and most recent types of PMC include the Synchronous, Eddy
Current and Multiphase couplers. In this study the focus will be placed on the ﬁrst two
categories [7].
2.1
Synchronous torque couplers
Synchronous type magnetic torque couplers, as shown in 2.1 consist of two hubs, each
ﬁtted with permanent magnets on its surface. An electrical drive is connected, through its
axis to one of the hubs, whilst the other hub is following the rotation at the same speed
due to the attraction and repulsion magnetic forces. The magnetization of the magnets
should be constant over time and rotational speed. Rare earth magnetic materials provide
high coercive forces, suitable for these applications [8].

2. Permanent Magnet Couplers
2.2
Eddy current couplers
Eddy current torque couplers, have existed since the 1960’s and have been popular in
industrial settings as brakes and damping mechanisms. Much like the synchronous couplers,
these couplers were originally ﬁtted with ﬁeld windings that produced the magnetic ﬁeld.
The ﬁeld windings are now replaced with permanent magnets which had a big eﬀect on
the renewed research interest of these couplers. Like the other couplers mentioned, the
eddy current coupler has two rotor hubs as shown in ﬁg 2.4. The primary rotor consist of
permanent magnets that produce the magnetic ﬁeld. The secondary rotor is simply a solid
conductor and generates torque when currents are induced. The conductor is typically
made from a material with low electrical resistivity like copper or aluminum. Currents are
induced, when there is a slip diﬀerence between the primary and secondary rotor [9].
Figure 2.4: Radial axis PM eddy current torque coupler.
The transmission of permanent magnet eddy current coupling is based on the relative
rotation between the outer rotor and the inner rotor. When the permanent magnet eddy
current coupling starts working, the outer rotor rotates with the drive shaft at a ﬁxed
rotational speed. In the meantime, the inner rotor connected to the loaded shaft stay still,
which causes a huge relative speed between the outer rotor and the inner rotor. Thus
the alternating magnetic ﬁeld and the eddy current are generated in the copper rings of
the outer rotor [10]. Lenz’s law shows that the coupling eﬀect between the eddy current
induced in the magnetic ﬁelds of the permanent magnetic ﬁeld and the original magnetic
will generate a magnetic transmission torque, which will make the inner rotor rotate with
the outer rotor rotate in phase. Moreover, since the outer rotor rotates at a ﬁxed speed,
the speed diﬀerence between the rotor and the outer rotor gradually decreases, which
makes the generated magnetic transmission torque between the two gradually reduced.
Eventually, the inner and outer rotors maintain stable operation at a ﬁxed speed diﬀerence.
Finally the energy transfer without mechanical connection between the loaded shaft and
the drive shaft is achieved [11, 12].
The amount of torque transmitted by the coupling is controlled by the amount of magnetic
ﬂux generated in the conductor, regardless of whether the two coupled parts are stationary
or rotating. Since slip will immediately result, if the torque capacity of the MC is exceeded,
it is important to carefully consider any overload that may be expected in any given
application [13].

2.3
MC design considerations
2.3.1
Synchronous torque couplers
For the SMCs, the topological variations are typically focused on the PMs’ design. Except
their height and width, which are the main inﬂuencing parameters, formation and shape
have been proved to aﬀect also their performance [14].
Based on the lamination principle which is applied in the back irons, this technique can
be also useful in the case of PMs. That because, the electrical conductivity is reduced
and thus the eddy currents are subsequently limited. In that sense, power losses and heat
generation are decreased and the PMs’ working life is extended [15].
Another aspect of the PMs design is centered in their shape. Rectangular PMs are one of
the most common shapes found in electromagnetic applications. Their simplicity allows
them to be used in various ways and their manufacture is easier. Besides that though,
their application may not be always easy. In most cases, the application of rectangular
PMs would require the modiﬁcation of the back iron in order to receive them. Besides
the increase in the design complexity, another important factor that is aﬀected is their
protection, with the danger of them tearing away being higher [16]. Hence considering
the above, curved PMs were preferred and chose to be employed at the present study. A
schematic representation of the two conﬁgurations is given below.

3.3
Mesh
The mesh settings determine the resolution of the FE mesh used to discretize the model.
The FE solver divides the model into small elements of geometrically simple shapes, in
this case triangles.
As it is shown in the ﬁg. 3.3, the mesh elements become smaller and more dense around
the area of the airgap. A high mesh density in the air gap is important since the last
one represents the boundary between the stationary and rotating part of the model and
the mesh elements must be aligned with each other across the boundary in order to get a
realistic result. It is worth noting that the mesh density, undesirably increases around sharp
edges and corners thing that makes the simulation more diﬃcult to converge. Another
indicator of the signiﬁcance of a proper geometric conﬁguration.
Having a ﬁne mesh leads to a more accurate approximation of the solution, but takes more
time and computational power to solve. For this study a customized mesh was used, in
order to cover the accuracy needs without unnecessary computational expense refmp.

3.3.1
Heat losses calculation
Being a slip device, the MC must of necessity to reject power loss in the form of heat.
During nominal operation the relative speed between the two hubs is almost zero. At that
point, with no slip occurring the generated power is considered negligible [25].
18
==PAGE:27==
3. Simulation Setup
The produced power losses, which are generated under slip, are mainly magnetic and
occur in both the SMC and AMC models. This value if quantiﬁed, can give a good
estimation of the local heat increase. The equation which connects the energy losses with
the temperature increase in each material is:
Qi = ci · mi∆T
(3.13)
, where for i depicting each individual material, Q is the heat added, c the speciﬁc heat
factor, m the mass and ∆T the change in temperature. With the volume Vi being easily
extracted from the geometry and ρ, from the material speciﬁcations, the mass can be
calculated as mi = Vi · ρ.
Regarding the energy quantiﬁcation, that was employed through the FE solver, by time
integration of the generated power losses.
In this study, using the possibilities of the FE solver, the power generation in each
component was measured. This value, integrated for a speciﬁc time frame, delivered the
energy production for each material.
3.4
Optimization constraints and methodology
In general, the application of PMC is limited by its cost and mass. The importance of
reducing the weight and the material cost, while still keeping the system mechanically as
simple as possible is clear. Increasing the performance is not under the scope, since the
application has speciﬁc torque characteristics that have to be respected. On the contrary
increased torque should be avoided, since that could result unnecessary stress when the
coupler slips.
A limitation should be placed on the maximum current density in the MC, in order to
prevent excessive temperature rise. This signiﬁcantly aﬀects the lifetime of the PMs, as
well as the adhesive holding them to iron surfaces. In such devices, since current only
exists in a solid conductive-sheet without any insulation, the current density can be much
higher than the conventional electric machines (usually a value between 30 A/mm2 and 50
A/mm2 would be reasonable). Analytically, the list of the physical design constraints and
speciﬁcations are given in table 3.2.

4.2.2
Parametric analysis of the AMC
Similarly as before, the prototype model used as a reference point for the AMC. The
veriﬁcation simulations outlined the original model’s performance, which was not proved
suﬃcient. Both the torque capability and temperature increase were not into the desired
framework.
The evaluation of the results suggested to follow a new methodology. For the determination
of the new approach, several geometries were considered. AMCs, as mentioned, operate
very similarly to the induction motors. Thus, the designing directions were centered to
squirrel cage implementations [26].
Under the new scopes, the Aluminum bar along with the Alnico conductors were neglected
from the AMC design. The slip coupling implementations, discussed in section 2.3.2, seemed
attractive alternatives and were employed. Overlapping topologies, generally achieved
satisfying results. The simplicity of their design and the inclusion of the conductors into
the IHs, presented durability and high performance. Nevertheless, in intense overspeeding
simulations the temperature reached critical points and the interpolated layer of steel
covering the conductors proved unsuitable.
Hence, the investigation concluded on a non-overlapped SCIM implementation, consisted
of rectangular conductive bars. The bars were modelled as solid conductors and were
interconnected through end-ring sections with ﬁnite impedance. Following the working
standards of the original model, a new reference model emerged.

should be avoided. The other important factor is the saturation potential. The increase

• The inﬂuence of conductive bar depth, on magnetic ﬂux density and axial

Eddy currents, as mentioned in previous chapters, tend to ﬂow around the edges of
conductive surfaces. In cases of rotating machineries, this eﬀect gets stronger towards
the center. In this analysis the inﬂuence of the conductor’s thickness over the system’s
performance was investigated.
Increasing the conductive bars’ depth, as can be seen in ﬁg. 4.19b, increases the AMC’s
torque production. This action makes the skin eﬀect more obvious and subsequently also
the current density, leading to a better transmission capacity. Too deep conductors though,
will again result in magnetic saturation in the IH yoke area, which will degrade AMCs
transmission performance. This phenomenon can already be noticed in the present case
in ﬁg. 4.19a, where the MFD between the conductors almost reaches the limit of 1.8 T.

From a mechanical point of view, the smaller the bar becomes, the more diﬃcult to
construct it, due to the problem of adequately ﬁxing the bars to the end rings. The
connection of the bars with the end ring is vital and if the contact resistance becomes too
large, the torque performance and eﬃciency will be signiﬁcantly reduced [19].
Another important considering over the bar size is the magnetic saturation. Increasing the
bars’ width too much will lead to saturation in the steel yoke separating the bars, while
reducing it too much will cause internal saturation in the bars. Therefore, the size of the
conductive bars should be moderate, to the extent that the material is well utilized and
extreme temperatures are prevented.

4.3
Implementation proposals
After the initial modeling and parametric analysis conduction, the next step suggests
the development of a new functional prototype system. The models were assessed under
the scope of manufacturing cost minimization. With the mechanical speciﬁcations being
constant, increase in the performance would incongruously stress the drive system which
operates at certain performance points. Thus, this cost eﬃciency evaluation was mainly
focused on active mass reduction and material utilization. The results of this assessment
are presented in this section.
4.3.1
Proposed SMC model
Under the scope of minimizing the manufacturing cost, while maintaining the desired
characteristics, a new SMC implementation emerged. The designated outer characteristics
were respected and the airgap length remained to the operating safe value of 2 mm. By
taking advantage the material utilization oﬀered from the high number of poles, the new
SMC model is consisted of 20 magnetic poles. The torque increase which was provided
by that, allowed the downgrade of the PMs classiﬁcation to a less expensive N33SH type.
Regarding the size of the PMs, their thickness was selected at 5 mm and the magnetic
pitch ratio at 0.87. This value of magnetic pitch adequately meets the speciﬁcations, since
the torque ripple eﬀect, that it would suggest, is limited by the high number of poles.

CONCLUSIONS
This MSc thesis was focused on the development of a magnetic coupling system, for the
needs of a wind turbine yaw drivetrain. The study was conducted in collaboration with
the danish company TechnoFlex Aps and facilitated in Powerlab-DK. The laboratory was
supplied with an original prototype from the company, which was used as the foundation
of the undertaken study.
The analysis started with the modeling of the experimental prototype using FEM and
the software COMSOL Multiphysics. The initial model was split into two parts, which
were assessed separately under the principle of superposition. The performance of the
these parts, synchronous and asynchronous, was tested under nominal and overspeeding
conditions. The results for the synchronous model proved satisfactory for the nominal
operation, but concerning in thermal aspects, under slip. Regarding the asynchronous
model, the analysis pointed out the necessity for an alternative topology where performance
and heat tolerance are increased.
After the evaluation of the initial results, the two models were substituted to a parametric
analysis. The investigation included parametric variations over the geometry and the
material speciﬁcations. More speciﬁcally, for the case of the synchronous part, the focus was
placed on the permanent magnets and the general sizing characteristics. Some indicative
conclusions were, that the number of magnetic poles are favoring the torque production,
as well as the overall permanent magnet characteristics. For the case of the asynchronous
part, the attention was centered on a new designing consideration, where the inner half
coupler was replaced by a squirrel cage substitute. The analysis there, underlined that
the number of conductors along with their size aﬀects the torque production, yet heat
limitations have to be accounted.
The overall results gave new perspectives in the conception of magnetic couplings with slip
characteristics. From the evaluation of the gathered data, two proposed designs emerged,
one for the synchronous and one for the asynchronous conﬁguration. Both models were
developed under the scope of cost minimization for the required performance, and got
validated from a temperature increase study. Analytically, the characteristics of the overall
proposed implementation are summarized in the table 4.5.

Recommendations and future work
With the completion of this work, several topics and thoughts came to the surface. Among
those which present interest for further investigation are:
• A laboratory test, with the construction of the proposed models. The results could
be compared with the initial model’s, as well as with the FEA, underlining the
simulation’s accuracy.
• A FE 3D veriﬁcation of the models, parallel to the 2D. The results would include
the axial leakages and verify the assumption that they could be ignored.
• A thorough design optimization using numerical algorithms. The examination could
include one or more objective functions, for the minimization of cost and active mass,
or the maximization of performance.
• An analytical thermal study employed with FEM, for the accurate estimation of heat
losses and the evaluation of the calculation method from this study.
• The exploration of diﬀerent designs and materials for the PMs, yokes and conductors.
• The expansion of the study to larger applications, where the torque needs range over
hundreds of Nm.
