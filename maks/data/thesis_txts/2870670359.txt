Abstract
Pseudomonas aeruginosa is a metabolically flexible opportunistic pathogen
and a leading cause of chronic lung infections in people with cystic fibrosis
(pwCF). Its persistence in the cystic fibrosis (CF) airway is driven by multi-
ple mechanisms, including metabolic adaptation and immune evasion, allowing
the pathogen to survive despite host defenses and prolonged antibiotic treat-
ment.
In this thesis, we investigated the role of mutations in the aceE and
aceF genes, which encode components of the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex
(PDHc), in shaping bacterial metabolism and infection. Using a combination of
growth assays, metabolic profiling, and infection studies in an air-liquid interface
(ALI) epithelial model, we characterized how these mutations influence bacterial
fitness, immune activation, and persistence mechanisms.
Our results show that mutant strains harboring mutations in the aceE and
aceF have a distinct metabolic profile, secreting large amounts of pyruvate since
it cannot be intracellularly processed into acetyl-coenzyme A (CoA) due to the
malfunction of the PDHc. The secreted pyruvate can neutralize reactive oxy-
gen species (ROS) which might provide protection against immune mediated
oxidative stress. Additionally, we observed that acetate that is generated as a
byproduct of pyruvate reaction with ROS is assimilated by P. aeruginosa, which
might compensate for the loss of PDHc function and sustain increased bacte-
rial growth. These strains also exhibit reduced virulence, as evidenced by lower
epithelial damage, and show a decreased induction of immune recruitment by
interleukin-8 (IL-8) secretion from the epithelium in the ALI infection model.
This metabolic adaptation appears to be a novel persistence strategy facilitating
chronic infection.
These findings suggest that metabolic rewiring in P. aeruginosa contributes
significantly to immune evasion and long-term adaptation in the CF lung. This
study highlights the importance of using physiologically relevant infection models,
such as ALI cultures, to capture bacterial phenotypes that are not observed under
standard in vitro conditions. Understanding the interplay between metabolism,
immune suppression, and oxidative stress resistance could lead to new therapeutic
strategies aimed at disrupting chronic P. aeruginosa infections in pwCF.

Introduction
P. aeruginosa is a highly adaptable and metabolically flexible bacterium with the abil-
ity to thrive in a wide range of environments, including soil, water, and host-associated
habitats such as animals, humans, and plants. In pwCF, chronic lung infections caused by
P. aeruginosa remain the leading cause of disease progression and mortality. Although ad-
vancements in CF treatment have significantly extended patient life expectancy, persistent
P. aeruginosa infections remain resistant to eradication. It has been shown that these in-
fections are typically clonal in origin, but as the disease progresses, the dominant bacterial
strain undergoes extensive diversification, giving rise to distinct morphotypes and specialized
subpopulations(8).
1.1
Cystic fibrosis
1.1.1
Genetic basis and pathophysiology of CF
The respiratory system is the main interface between the external environment and
systemic circulation. The human aiways not only allow for the exchange of O2/CO2 but
also offer a protective barrier against external airborne pathogens(9). The respiratory tract
contains a series of different cell types such as basal epithilial cells, cilliated epithilial cells,
secretory cells, mucus producing cells, goblet and clara cells(10). Furthermore, the airway
system has in place defense mechanisms including mucociliary clearance and innate immune
capabilities, like secretion of antimicrobial peptides and cytokines by epithelial cells and
resident leukocytes in order to recruit and activate the immune system.
CF is a genetic disorder inherited in an autosomal recessive manner, caused by muta-
tions in the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator (CFTR) gene. Clinically,
pwCF commonly exhibit symptoms such as persistent cough, wheezing, recurrent lung in-
fections, difficulty in breathing, steatorrhea, and salty-tasting skin(11). The CFTR gene
encodes a protein essential for regulating chloride and bicarbonate ion transport across the
apical membrane of epithelial cells. In pwCF, this protein is either absent or dysfunctional,
leading to the accumulation of thick, sticky mucus in the lungs and other organs. This
disrupts mucociliary clearance in the respiratory tract, increasing susceptibility to chronic
bacterial infections. CF also affects the digestive system by impairing nutrient absorption
and commonly causes male infertility. The severity of CF varies widely, depending on the

specific mutations in CFTR(12).
The CFTR protein consists of 1.480 amino acids with a molecular weight of approx-
imately 170 kDa.
It belongs to the ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporter superfam-
ily and is structurally composed of two transmembrane domains (TMD1 and TMD2), two
nucleotide-binding domains (NBD1 and NBD2), and a unique regulatory domain (RD) (Fig.
1A).
Figure 1: Cystic fibrosis genetic basis and pathophysiology.(A) 3D representation of
the tertiary structure of CFTR protein(1). (B) Mucus ion transport in CF airways(2).
The transmembrane domains form the channel pore through which chloride ions pass,
while the nucleotide-binding domains bind and hydrolyze ATP to regulate channel opening
and closing. The RD modulates protein activity through phosphorylation. Proper folding
and function of CFTR require interdomain interactions that stabilize its complex confor-
mation and ensure efficient ion transport. Mutations affecting these domains can disrupt
protein folding, impair channel gating, or reduce ion permeability, contributing to disease
progression(13; 1).
The absence of functional CFTR impairs bicarbonate secretion, resulting in airway
surface acidification and increased mucus viscosity. Reduced chloride ion transport also limits
water movement into the airway lumen, leading to airway dehydration and abnormal mucus

osmotic pressure and viscoelasticity (Fig. 1B)(14). The static mucus, resulting from CFTR
dysfunction, creates an ideal environment that promotes bacterial colonization, particularly
by P. aeruginosa, as well as inflammation caused by the oxidative burst of the recruited
innate immune cells, such as neutrophils and macrophages(15). This leads to persistent lung
infections that drive progressive respiratory decline(16). The link between CFTR dysfunction
and chronic infections highlights the importance of targeted therapies aimed at restoring
CFTR function.
CFTR modulators, such as elexacaftor/tezacaftor/ivacaftor (ETI), are
designed to enhance chloride transport by correcting misfolded CFTR proteins or improving
their activity, offering new therapeutic strategies to manage CF lung disease(17).
Beyond its role in ion transport, CFTR plays a critical role in maintaining airway surface
liquid homeostasis, regulating cilia function, and modulating inflammation. As a chloride
channel, it facilitates ion flux across the epithelial membrane, contributing to fluid balance,
cell signaling, and proper muscle function. In airway epithelial cells, CFTR is expressed on
the cilia, where it helps regulate their coordinated beating to remove mucus from the lungs.
1.1.2
Pulmonary infections in pwCF
Due to dysfunction of CFTR protein a thick layer of mucus is accumulated in the respira-
tory airways creating an enviroment prone to microbial colonization, by several opportunistic
bacterial pathogens and fungi. Initially intermittent, these airway infections eventually be-
come chronic and harder to treat.
A vicious cycle of airway obstruction, infection, and
infammation leads to irreversible structural airway damage and respiratory failure. Once es-
tablished, these pathogens form aggregated structured microbial communities encased in an
extracellular matrix that protects bacteria from immune clearance and significantly reduces
antibiotic penetration, making treatment highly challenging(16).
P. aeruginosa is a well-characterized opportunistic pathogen and a major cause of noso-
comial infections, particularly in immunocompromised individuals. In pwCF, P. aeruginosa
infection is typically acquired in childhood, originating either from environmental sources
or through patient-to-patient transmission. Its presence in CF airways is associated with
an accelerated decline in lung function and an increased risk of premature death. Conse-
quently, newly detected P. aeruginosa infections are aggressively treated with antibiotics in
an attempt to eradicate the bacteria. However, due to its remarkable adaptability, P. aerug-
inosa is able to evade immune defenses and develop multidrug resistance, leading to chronic

infection despite repeated therapeutic interventions. Persistent P. aeruginosa infection is
a major contributor to acute pulmonary exacerbations (PEx), decreased likelihood of lung
function recovery following a PEx episode, and a more rapid decline in lung function over
time(16). Among CF pathogens, P. aeruginosa is the most prevalent and clinically signifi-
cant, as it plays a central role in lung function decline, persistent inflammation, and AMR
development(18; 19; 20; 21; 22). Other oportunistic pathogens causing respiratory infection
include, the Burkholderia cepacia complex, Stenotrophomonas maltophilia, Achromobacter,
Staphylococcus aureus and Nontuberculous mycobacteria among others(16).
1.1.3
Airway mucus composition
The thick and viscous sputum in CF lungs creates a nutrient-rich environment that
supports bacterial growth, consisting of both host- and microbe-derived compounds(23).
However, the composition of mucus varies significantly among patients, reflecting individual
differences in metabolism and disease progression.
Among the most abundant nutrients
available to P. aeruginosa are amino acids, which serve as essential building blocks for
biomass synthesis or can be broken down to generate reducing equivalents for cellular energy.
Glucose is also present in both CF and non-CF mucus, though its concentration fluctuates
between patients and across different disease stages, with levels generally being higher in CF
compared to other respiratory conditions. Another important metabolic substrate found in
CF mucus is lactate, which accumulates at concentrations between 3.6 and 15 mM. This is
primarily attributed to neutrophils, which release lactate under anaerobic conditions - an
environment typical of CF lung infections(23).
In the lungs, gel-forming mucins are secreted by both surface goblet cells and submucosal
glands, playing a crucial role in airway protection. While goblet cells produce both MUC5AC
and MUC5B, submucosal glands exclusively secrete MUC5B(24). The maturation of mucins
involves O-glycosylation of serine and threonine residues within the endoplasmic reticulum,
a modification essential for defining their biological properties. The specific oligosaccharide
composition and terminal sugar charges influence mucus hydration through electrostatic
interactions while also facilitating pathogen trapping via molecular binding mechanisms(25).
In CF airways, chronic inflammation leads to goblet cell and glandular hyperplasia,
significantly increasing the production of both MUC5B and MUC5AC. Additionally, high
concentrations of extracellular DNA from dying inflammatory cells accumulate within mucus,

symptoms(26). Oxygen availability is severely restricted due to the dense mucus layers, as
well as oxygen consumption by immune cells and other microbial species residing within
the lung microbiome. These factors create a dynamic metabolic landscape that shapes the
survival and adaptation of P. aeruginosa in CF airways(6).
1.1.4
Model systems for CF investigations
The complex composition of the sputum layer of CF lung and the specific nutritional
availability poses a significant challenge for accurately recreating the CF lung environment
for infectious disease studies. As a result, there have been various investigations with the
aim of analyzing the patient’s sputum composition in order to construct a defined medium
that nutritionally mimics CF sputum(27). As P. aeruginosa physiology shifts depending on
the environment, a synthetic CF medium (SCFM) resembling the nutritional composition
of CF sputums has been developed(28). However, SCFM lacks any host cell, limiting the
possibility of studying host-pathogen interactions.
Due to the significant effects of the respiratory exposure to the external environment
on human health and disease, there has been growing interest in establishing reliable and
predictive in vitro models. One widely used system is the ALI model, which closely mimics
airway physiology. In ALI models, a porous membrane insert separates two compartments:
the upper apical side exposed to air and the lower basolateral side in contact with a nutrient
medium. This system is designed to closely replicate the physiological conditions of the
epithelial barrier in human lungs. Initially, basal cells are seeded onto the membrane and
expansion media is added to the basolateral and apical compartment, while cells are allowed
to expand to ensure proper contact with neighboring cells. After a few days, the medium
is removed from the apical compartment, and the media in the basolateral compartment is
changed to the media that induces differentiation leading to the development of a pseudo-
stratified epithelial cell layer with secretory, goblet, clara, and ciliated cells (Fig. 2)(29; 30).
The ALI model is a widely used in vitro system for studying airway epithelial cells under
physiologically relevant conditions. By allowing epithelial cells to differentiate into a struc-
ture with mucus production and cilia, the ALI model provides an in vivo-like environment
for investigating host-pathogen interactions, drug responses, and disease mechanisms both
for healthy and diseased airways(10; 31; 32). Immune cells are not normally included in

1.2
P. aeruginosa characteristics and metabolism
1.2.1
P. aeruginosa characteristics
P. aeruginosa is an heterotrophic, Gram-negative, rod-shaped bacterium belonging to
the kingdom Monera, phylum Proteobacteria, class Gammaproteobacteria, order Pseudomon-
adales, and family Pseudomonadaceae. The bacterium measures approximately 1-5 µm in
length and 0.5-1.0 µm in width(33).
P. aeruginosa is exceptionally adaptable and can colonize diverse environmental niches.
It thrives at temperatures ranging from 4°C to 42°C, with optimal growth at 37 °C(6). The
bacterium possesses a single polar flagellum, enabling motility, and can metabolize a wide
variety of organic compounds, contributing to its ecological success as both a saprophyte
and opportunistic pathogen(34).
Although commonly found in the environment, P. aeruginosa is a significant opportunis-
tic pathogen in humans. It is associated with acute infections in burn wounds, immunocom-
promised patients, as well as chronic respiratory infections in pwCF, chronic obstructive
pulmonary disease (COPD), and primary ciliary dyskinesia (PCD). P. aeuginosa can also
infect multiple hosts such as insects, plants and mice using a wide variety of virulence fac-
6
==PAGE:18==

tors, including exotoxins, quorum sensing (QS)-regulated biofilm formation, and secretion
systems that enable host invasion and immune evasion(35).
P. aeruginosa exhibits significant antimicrobial resistance (AMR), posing challenges in
clinical treatment. Resistance mechanisms include (a) acquisition of resistance genes via
horizontal gene transfer and (b) mutations in genes already present in the genome, leading
to up-regulation of efflux pumps, β-lactamases or changes in porins, among others(33).
Carbapenem-resistant P. aeruginosa strains are particularly concerning and are listed as
critical priority pathogens by the World Health Organization (WHO)(36).
Two commonly used laboratory strains of P. aeruginosa are PAO1 and PA14. PAO1,
originally isolated from a chronic wound infection in the 1950s, was the first sequenced P.
aeruginosa genome (6.3 Mb, 5570 predicted open reading frames). PA14, a highly virulent
strain, was initially identified from plant infections but has also been isolated from human
burn wounds. The genome of P. aeruginosa encodes a high number of transcriptional reg-
ulators and metabolic genes, contributing to its adaptability in diverse environments(33).
Public genomic resources such as www.pseudomonas.com and Pseudomonas Metabolome
Database (PAMDB) provide curated datasets for functional and metabolic studies.
1.2.2
P. aeruginosa metabolism
P. aeruginosa exhibits a hierarchical preference for carbon sources(37). In laboratory
conditions, tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle intermediates and amino acids are the preferred
carbon sources for P. aeruginosa strains(27). The TCA cycle is central to P. aeruginosa
metabolism, not only generating ATP through oxidative phosphorylation but also supplying
key intermediates for biosynthetic pathways. Interestingly, glucose is not a primary carbon
source, despite being present at millimolar concentrations in CF secretions. Instead, glycerol
appears to be favored by CF-adapted isolates during exponential growth, though the reason
for this remains unclear(4). One possible explanation lies in the abundance of phosphatidyl-
choline (PC) in CF airway surfactant. P. aeruginosa secretes lipases capable of hydrolyzing
PC, yielding phosphorylcholine, glycerol, and long-chain fatty acids (FAs) such as palmitate.
The released glycerol is metabolized through enzymes encoded by the glp operon, while FAs
undergo β-oxidation, producing acetyl-CoA, which is then funneled into the TCA cycle for
energy production or into the glyoxylate shunt for gluconeogenesis and biomass synthesis
(Fig. 3)(38).

The primary energy-generating mechanism in P. aeruginosa is respiration, which relies
on a proton motive force to drive ATP synthesis. Like many other bacteria, P. aeruginosa
possesses a highly adaptable and intricate electron transport system, allowing it to utilize a
diverse range of electron donors and acceptors. Its genome encodes at least 17 different respi-
ratory dehydrogenases, which facilitate electron transfer from metabolic substrates into the
quinone pool. Among these are three NADH dehydrogenases and a succinate dehydrogenase,
which play key roles in energy metabolism(39).
To complete aerobic respiration, P. aeruginosa employs five terminal oxidases respon-
sible for the four-electron reduction of molecular oxygen to water. Three of these are cy-
tochrome c oxidases, which channel electrons through the cytochrome bc1 complex and
c-type cytochromes, while the remaining two are quinol oxidases, which receive electrons
directly from ubiquinol. In addition to aerobic respiration, P. aeruginosa has the capacity
for anaerobic growth, as its respiratory chain is linked to denitrification enzymes that reduce
nitrogen oxides. This metabolic flexibility allows it to survive and grow under low-oxygen
conditions by utilizing nitrate or nitrite as alternative electron acceptors (Fig. 4)(5).

1.3
Bacterial adaptation during respiratory infections
1.3.1
Adaptive evolution during respiratory infections
Upon infection P. aeruginosa faces several challenges that hinder its colonization and
growth. Such challenges include the presence of antibiotics for eradication of the bacteria,
the nutritional composition of the infection site, innate oxidative stress in the respiratory
system, and the immune system. The infected epithelium recruits the immune system via
the secretion of IL-8, among others. This cytokine belongs to major chemokines to stimulate
migration of neutrophils, monocytes and macrophages into the inflammation sites. This
chemokine has a C-X-C motif and consists of 72 amino acids with molecular weight of
8.8 kDa(42). Macrophages are often the first immune cells to combat invading pathogens.
They engulf pathogens, dead cells and cellular debris by phagocytosis and they degrade the
content of the phagolysosomes. Recognition of invading bacteria by innate immune cells leads
to ROS production into the extracellular space. The antimicrobial activity of macrophages
consists of this oxidative burst with nox, the enzyme family of NADPH oxidases, being the
exclusive source of the extracellular ROS produced by macrophages in response to bacterial
infection(43).
Nox enzymatic activity varies and includes antimicrobial defense, vasoregulation, hor-
mone synthesis, and regulation of gene expression, cell proliferation, and differentiation. In
particular Nox2 can produce large ammounts of ROS, which are crucial for the antimicrobial
10
==PAGE:22==

activity of phagocytes, including macrophages. Other sources of ROS in the human organism
include mitochondria, xanthine oxidases, peroxisomes, cyclooxygenases and lipoxyogenases,
and cytochrome P450(44).
ROS are reactive chemical molecules deriving from molecular oxygen (O2), with at least
one oxygen atom that is more reactive than O2. This group of molecules consists of two sub-
classes: (i) highly reactive free radicals, including the superoxide anion (O2•−), the hydroxyl
radical (•OH), alkoxyl (•OOR), and peroxyl radicals (•OOH) and (ii) nonradical species
such as hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), singlet oxygen (1O2), ozone (O3), and the hypochloride
anion (OCl−). O2 is the common precursor of ROS. Nox by oxidatively transferring one sin-
gle electron (e−) from NADH to O2, quickly produces O2•−which is then quickly dismutating
to H2O2 by the transfer of another e−and two protons (H+). This process is either catalyzed
by superoxide dismutases or in the presence of water. H2O2 is then dismutated to •OH, by
the uptake of one e−and the release of hydroxide (HO−), which subsequently transforms
to water by the transfer of one e−and one H+. O2•−and H2O2 are the two most abundant
ROS in cells(45). The term ROS scavenger describes any small or large molecule that is
capable of detoxifying one or more ROS subspecies by different mechanisms defined by the
chemistry of the scavenger and the ROS subspecies. A ROS scavenger does not detoxify
only radicals but can also scavenge for nonradicals, like H2O2(46).
Common ROS scav-
enger include, N-acetylcysteine, ascorbic acid and its anion ascorbate, vitamin E(44), and
pyruvate(47). P. aeruginosa, possesses three distinct monofunctional catalase genes-katA,
katB, and katE (also referred to as katC) - each with specialized evolutionary adaptations,
in order to combat the oxidative stress of the environment(48).
The stressful environment that bacteria encompass in the respiratory tract drive its
adaptive evolution.
In the case of P. aeruginosa, this process mainly occurs via muta-
tional changes in preexisting genes. These pathoadaptive mutations lead to host adaptation
through remodeling of regulatory networks and central metabolism, acquisition of antibiotic
resistance or loss of virulence factors, allowing bacteria to establish chronic infections that
can endure up to several years(49).

Virulence factors of P. aeruginosa play a key role in the initial colonization process.

1.3.3

P. aeruginosa exhibits significant metabolic heterogeneity, adapting its nutrient utiliza-
tion profile based on environmental conditions. The availability and diversity of nutrients
differ considerably between the external environment and the CF lung, driving metabolic
adaptations throughout infection. Studies on clinical P. aeruginosa isolates have revealed

flexibility is closely linked to broader phenotypic and genotypic diversity, which is most pro-
nounced during the early years of infection, when selective pressures drive physiological ad-
justments in response to the CF lung environment. Notably, many clinical isolates develop
auxotrophies for methionine, leucine, and arginine, which correlate with non-synonymous

Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex and its role in metabolic adaptation
The PDHc plays a central role in P. aeruginosa metabolism by catalyzing the conver-
sion of pyruvate to acetyl-CoA. This multi-enzyme system consists of three enzymatic com-
ponents: pyruvate dehydrogenase/decarboxylase (E1p), dihydrolipoamide acetyltransferase
(E2p), and dihydrolipoamide dehydrogenase (E3). The PDHc is primarily regulated through
allosteric mechanisms and product inhibition, ensuring metabolic balance (Fig. 6)(7).
In P. aeruginosa, the PDHc forms an octahedral E2p core, and its encoding genes are

undergoes nucleophilic attack by lipoamide, forming a tetrahedral intermediate, which then

The acetyl-CoA produced by PDHc fuels the TCA cycle, which is central to P. aerugi-

TCA cycle also supplies key biosynthetic precursors, linking PDHc activity to broader

Notably, mutations in aceE and aceF have been linked to metabolic specialization
and impaired virulence, as observed in longitudinal collections of P. aeruginosa clinical
strains(53). The aceE and aceF mutant strains indeed, are considered pathoadaptive genes
and their function is negatively selected during CF infections. Furthermore, metatranscrip-
tomic analysis in CF mucus, revealed down-regulation of the PDHc(53). Strikingly, adapted
strains appear to sacrifise full TCA cycle functionality, either by accumulating mutations or
by down-regulating the PDHc, shifting their carbon source preferences(53). This drastic re-
configuration of metabolic pathways further supports the idea that P. aeruginosa undergoes
long-term metabolic adaptation as a trade-off for survival and persistence in the CF lung
environment.
1.4
Hypothesis and objectives of the thesis
In this master’s thesis, we investigated how central carbon metabolism contributes to
bacterial growth and influences host immune responses. Central carbon metabolism supplies
bacteria with both energy and essential building blocks, but some of its intermediates also
have unexpected effects on the host’s immune system, including the reduction of immune
cell recruitment and the limitation of inflammation during infection. The project focuses
on understanding how P. aeruginosa uses cross-feeding of pyruvate through accumulated
mutations in the PDHc to influence the airway epithelium and evade the innate immune
cells. To explore these interactions, an ALI model system of airway epithelium was used
to study bacterial infections with laboratory strains of P. aeruginosa. This setup allowed
the characterization of immune evasion mechanisms, which may play a critical role in the
progression of respiratory infections. The project’s learning objectives included: (a) under-
standing the role of central carbon metabolism in providing energy and building blocks for
bacterial growth, (b) performing bacterial infections using laboratory strains of P. aeruginosa
in an ALI model system, (c) identifying specific intermediates of central carbon metabolism
that influence the host immune response, (d) characterizing the molecular mechanisms of

Strains and media

H2O2 killing assays

3.1
Pyruvate dehydrogenase mutations provide protection from H2O2
We hypothesized that the mutations observed in the P. aeruginosa aceEF operon,
encoding the PDHc-during a chronic infection in pwCF(53), lead to pyruvate secretion as a
protection mechanism against ROS due to the ability of pyruvate to react with H2O2(60; 61).
20
==PAGE:32==
Stefanos Lampadariou - s230275
Chapter 3
To assess this hypothesis, we used mutant strains previously constructed in Pedersen et al(53)
harboring a point mutation in the aceE gene and a mutant strain harboring a 4 base-pair
(bp) insertion in the aceF gene. Those strains were named aceE and aceF, respectively.
We then measured the growth rate of the wild type strain PAO1 and the two mutant
strains in SCFM media in presence of 5 mM H2O2 and/or 5 mM pyruvate, to asses the effect
of ROS on bacterial growth (Fig 7A). We observed a significant reduction in the growth
rate when H2O2 was added. However, the addition of external pyruvate led to a complete
recovery of growth rate indicating that pyruvate is able to provide protection against H2O2.
Importantly, all strains showed no significant changes in growth rate when pyruvate was
added alone, in comparison to the SCFM control.
This protective mechanism was further supported by the effect of H2O2 on lag time (Fig.
7B). As shown in Figure 7B, the lag time was increased for PAO1, aceE, and aceF strains in
the presence of H2O2, with lag times of 8.5, 11.4, and 15 hours, respectively. The addition of
pyruvate fully restored the lag time for PAO1 and aceE, while it was significantly decreased
for aceF.
To further validate these findings with pyruvate secreted by the different strains, we
conducted a H2O2 killing assay using the supernatants from each strain (Fig 7C). We incu-
bated normalised overnight cultures at OD600nm of 0.5 for 30 minutes with 5 mM H2O2 and
quantified the resulting CFUs to assess the protection provided by the pyruvate secreted in
the supernatant by each strain. A clear trend in survival percentage was observed for each
strain with the survival of aceF being significantly higher than both PAO1 and aceE.
We then used the supernatant from the aceF mutant to test its protective effect on
the PAO1 strain. As it is shown in Figure 7C, we observed a similar survival percentage
as the aceF mutant. To confirm that protection was provided by pyruvate we quantified
the pyruvate in the supernatants of the laboratory strains (Fig 7D). As expected, PAO1
did not secrete any significant amount of pyruvate while the aceE mutant strain showed
a pattern of secretion (around 2 mM) followed by re-assimilation, while the aceF mutant
strain continuously secreted pyruvate reaching a concentration of around 8 mM after 24h.

3.2
Acetate by-product assimilation by aceF mutant strain
As mentioned above, a point mutation was introduced in the aceE strain, resulting in
a missense mutation in the PDHc. This mutation likely causes reduced enzyme activity
while retaining partial functionality.
Instead, the insertion of 4-bp to the aceF mutant
rendered the PDHc non-functional, as the reading frame of aceF gene was altered. This
causes the inability of PDHc to transform pyruvate to acetyl-CoA, preventing the TCA cycle
from functioning. Importantly, the protective reaction of pyruvate against H2O2 produces
acetate(59), which P. aeruginosa can assimilate for acetyl-CoA production and TCA cycle
activation(4) (Fig. 8A).
When comparing the growth rates of the PAO1, aceE, and aceF strains in SCFM and
LB media we observed a significant increase in the growth rate in LB medium (Fig 8B). As
the latter is an undefined medium which contains acetate as one of the carbon sources, we
hypothesized that acetate could be assimilated by the aceF mutant, likely contributing to
TCA cycle activation. To test this hypothesis, we measured the maximum growth rates of the
strains in SCFM medium supplemented with acetate. Acetate was either generated via a 30-
minute pre-incubation reaction between pyruvate and H2O2 or obtained commercially (Fig.
8C). No significant differences in growth rate were observed for PAO1 and aceE, while aceF
exhibited a clear trend of increasing growth rates. These findings support our hypothesis
that under oxidative stress, the protective role of pyruvate leads to acetate production, which
can then be assimilated and incorporated into the TCA cycle.

the observed decrease in epithelial damage and inflammation does not appear to be solely
a consequence of the aceF mutant’s growth deficit, as aceE demonstrated similar bacterial
growth to aceF over a 24-hour period despite exhibiting different infection dynamics.

induced the immune response of the epithelium by adding LPS and tested the effect of
pyruvate in the immune system recruitment (Fig 9D). Interestingly, we observed a significant
reduction of IL-8 release in the presence of pyruvate, supporting our hypothesis that pyruvate
acts additionally as an immunosuppressor.

Discussion & Conclusions
Metabolism is a fundamental process that is dynamically regulated and optimized based
on nutrient availability and the cellular needs of an organism. In P. aeruginosa, clinical
isolates have been shown to undergo metabolic specialization during infection, suggesting
an adaptive response to the host environment(55; 54; 6). These metabolic shifts serve as
compensatory mechanisms to offset the fitness costs of other phenotypic traits but they are
also actively selected to enhance bacterial persistence during infection(53). In this project,
we evaluated mutations in the aceEF operon as a non-conventional mechanism of persistence
in chronic infections of P. aeruginosa in pwCF. We investigated how bacteria by cross-feeding
of pyruvate evade the immune system and adapt in the CF lung environment.
Strains carrying mutations in aceE and aceF, which encode components of the PDHc,
exhibit characteristics typical of chronic infections, including down-regulation of the T3SS,
up-regulation of the type 6 Secretion System (T6SS), increased phenazine and alginate pro-
duction as previously reported(62; 53), and in some cases reduced growth rates.
These
features suggest that such mutations may provide a selective advantage within the host.
Additionally, these adapted strains secrete significant amounts of pyruvate, a metabolite
known to influence inflammatory responses in the host(63). The effect of the aceF mutation
appeared more profound relative to the aceE mutation. This might depend on the concen-
tration of pyruvate secreted, but also on the specific type of mutation. This is in line with
the fact that several mutations have been identified in clinical strains, with two different
patterns of secretion (re-assimilation vs continuous secretion) providing different levels of
protection in the host.
Introducing clinical aceE and aceF mutations into a well-defined laboratory strain
(PAO1) revealed that aceF mutation leads to decreased immune activation and both aceE

and aceF strains exhibited reduced penetration of the epithelial layer compared to the wild-
type PAO1 strain, which retains a highly virulent phenotype characteristic of acute infec-
tions. However, the aceF mutant showed a more substantial decrease in virulence-associated
traits, including IL-8 suppression and TEER preservation. This decrease in infectivity could
be explained by down-regulation of the T3SS. Previous analysis have indeed shown a down-
regulation of the T3SS in PDHc mutants(53). These findings align with the concept that
within-host evolution in pwCF favors strains with attenuated virulence, as a strategy to
minimize tissue damage and evade immune detection(64).
The above mentioned mechanisms of persistence such as (i) down-regulation of virulence
factors and (ii) reduced growth rate are a common trait in several adapted clinical isolates.
Nevertheless, the secretion of pyruvate that is observed in this subgroup of late clinical iso-
lates reveals a new mechanism of persistence of P. aeruginosa. Beyond its central role in
carbon metabolism, pyruvate serves as a potent scavenger of ROS and has been explored as a
potential anti-inflammatory agent in various diseases(65). In CF airways, chronic neutrophil
activation and dysregulated epithelial homeostasis result in excessive ROS production(66),
which bacterial pyruvate secretion likely helps neutralize. Additionally, the reaction of pyru-
vate with H2O2 produces acetate which could be then re-assimilated by PDHc defective
mutant strain and introduced to the TCA cycle compensating its growth deficit.
Inhibition of the PDHc and rerouting of pyruvate metabolism confer protection against
H2O2, thereby enhancing bacterial resistance to innate immune cell-mediated killing, suggest-
ing that the selective pressure for these mutations is the oxidative stress in the CF respiratory
tract. Additionally, this new persistence mechanism which confers tolerance against ROS
could be beneficial to the entirety of the pulmonary microbial community in pwCF.
Furthermore, pyruvate appears to actively suppress the host immune response by in-
hibiting IL-8 secretion.
This immunomodulatory effect may contribute to bacterial per-
sistence in chronic infections by dampening innate immune cells recruitment and limiting
epithelial inflammation. Consequently, the reduced immune recognition of aceE and aceF
mutants appears to be directly linked to bacterial pyruvate secretion, which modulates in-
flammatory responses in both epithelial cells and immune components.
These observations suggest further investigation into how metabolic rewiring contributes
to bacterial persistence in chronic infections. Future studies should explore whether pyru-
vate metabolism interacts with other pathways crucial for long-term adaptation, such as QS

tive advantage for aceEF mutants within the CF lung should also be assessed. Moreover,
understanding whether other pathogens within the polymicrobial CF lung environment ben-
efit from P. aeruginosa pyruvate secretion could highlight potential cooperative interactions
between species.
Importantly, the reduced growth rate of aceE was only observed in the ALI model
and not in standard in vitro conditions. This suggests that bacterial phenotype is highly
dependent on host-like conditions and highlights the importance of physiologically relevant
models. The ALI model more closely mimics the lung environment, particularly in terms of
nutrient availability, epithelial barrier function and cytokine signaling. This study highlights
the necessity of using infection models that accurately mimic the CF lung environment to
capture bacterial phenotypes that would otherwise remain undetected in standard in vitro
conditions.
In conclusion, the PDHc mutant strains exhibit lower production of T3SS and increased
secretion of pyruvate to their surrounding (Fig 11). During a regular infection, P. aeruginosa
produces high amounts of T3SS, damaging the host. The host then responds with cytokine
production in order to recruit cells of the immune system. The innate immune cells, such
as neutrophils and macrophages, arrive at the infection site where they secrete ROS. ROS
then kill the bacteria by nucleophilic attacks in DNA, proteins and other biopolymers (Fig
11A). On the other hand, aceE and aceF mutants exhibit a distinct infection dynamic due to
T3SS down-regulation and pyruvate secretion. These phenotypic traits result in a decrease
in airway epithelium damage and also a decrease in the host response. By producing lower
amounts of cytokines the host’s immune recruitment is attenuated with fewer innate immune
cells arriving at the infection site. The H2O2 released by neutrophils and macrophages is
neutralized by the pyruvate in the surrounding environment leading to the production of
acetate. Acetate can be assimilated by the mutants with non functional PDHc for fueling
the TCA cycle (Fig. 11B). This new mechanism, illustrated in figure 11, highlights the
importance of the metabolic adaptation in persistence and immune evasion.
Overall, these findings suggest that metabolic interventions could represent new ther-
apeutic strategies to disrupt chronic P. aeruginosa infections in pwCF. Understanding the
interactions between bacterial metabolism, immune suppression, and oxidative stress resis-
tance could be critical in designing targeted treatments to improve patient outcomes. Further

4.1
Limitations of the study
There are potential limitations to this study. All the assays evaluating the activity
of pyruvate for down-regulating the immune system and neutralizing H2O2 were conducted
with recombinant strains and not with clinical strains, which may not fully capture the
in vivo metabolic adaptations observed in patient-derived strains. Additionally, the H2O2
killing assay in the ALI model was performed with PAO1 supplemented with commercially
acquired pyruvate rather than pyruvate naturally secreted by clinical or recombinant strains.
Since pyruvate can act as a carbon source for PAO1, it remains unclear whether mutants with
non-functional pyruvate dehydrogenase would exert identical results. Overall, further studies
incorporating clinical strains and host-derived metabolites will be necessary to confirm these
findings in physiologically relevant conditions.
