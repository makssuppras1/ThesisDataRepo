Abstract
This work explores how machine learning (ML) can improve the current Common Grid Model
Alignment (CGMA) process at Nordic RCC and evaluates the downstream impact of the CGMA
predictions on Flow-based Market Coupling (FBMC). The thesis is divided into two parts.
In Part I various ML models were developed to predict cross-border (CB) electricity flows and
net positions (NP) from the day-ahead FBMC for the 12 Nordic bidding zones (BZ). A substan-
tial and diverse dataset, consisting of weather forecasts, generation and transmission outage
information, and market data lags, was constructed. The best performing model for predicting
CB electricity flows was a stacked ensemble model, consisting of a Neural Network (NN) with
a customized loss function, an XGBoost regression and XGBoost classification model. On the
test data, the Ensemble model reduced the mean squared error (MSE) from 289.335 MW2 to
173.393 MW2, compared to the current CGMA model at Nordic RCC. Part I demonstrated
the substantial potential of replacing the current CGMA methodology with an ML-based ap-
proach, not only due to the observed improvements in predictive performance, but also due
to the preserved level of model explainability, an essential requirement for Nordic RCC given
their responsibility for critical infrastructure.
Part II builds on a simulation tool that simplifies Nordic RCC’s processes from CGMA pre-
dictions through FBMC, ending with a model to assess system security. Using a test system,
the analysis showed that CGMA prediction errors can significantly degrade both economic
eﬀiciency and system security.
Two pipeline setups were compared: a Base Case without CGMA predictions and a CGMA
pipeline where NP predictions from CGMA were fixed as input. The Flow-Based Model Error
(FBME) increased by roughly one-third in the CGMA pipeline compared to the Base Case.
Further analysis revealed that CGMA errors were sometimes corrected downstream, however
causing PTDF approximation errors, while in other cases, they led to suboptimal market out-
comes. Overall, Part II highlights the value of improving CGMA predictions, particularly for
enhancing the security of the power system.
Given the insights from Part I and II, a key question for Nordic RCC is whether it is more ben-
eficial to predict CB flows or NPs and fix them in subsequent processes. This study highlights
a trade-off: NP predictions offer higher accuracy but result in less alignment in later processes,
whereas CB flow predictions are less accurate but lead to better alignment downstream.

1.1
Context
1.1 (i)
Electricity Trading, Day-ahead market, and Flow-Based Market Coupling
The European power system is highly interconnected and electricity is constantly traded across
different markets and timeframes. The most relevant electricity market for this thesis is the
day-ahead (DA) market. Here, electricity is traded for delivery each hour the next day.
Every day at 10:00 CET the available capacities on the interconnectors and in the grid are
published. Buyers and sellers then have until 12:00 CET to submit their bids to the Nomi-
nated Electricity Market Operators before the market closes and is cleared for the next day
[47]. The day-head market clearing process is done through a common European optimiza-
tion algorithm called Euphemia. Euphemia integrates the different European markets into one
coupled market, allowing electricity transactions to occur across different regions by utilizing
cross-border (CB) transmission capacities. Regions are divided into zones called bidding zones
(BZ) which corresponds to geographical areas to which network constraints are applied [24].
As shown in Figure 1, the Nordic power system has 12 BZs. Five in Norway, four in Sweden,
two in Denmark, and one in Finland.
Figure 1: Nordic Bidding Zones [71]
Euphemia ensures that electricity production and demand are balanced to maximize overall
socioeconomic welfare across Europe while taking network constraints into consideration [47].
The optimal market clearing output of Euphemia contains:
1. Electricity prices for each BZ
2. Net positions (NP) (export minus import) per BZ
3. CB electricity flows between BZs.
It is the responsibility of the transmission system operators (TSOs) to model the network and
calculate the transmission capacities used in Euphemia. When defining these, TSOs must find
1
==PAGE:12==
an optimal balance between economic eﬀiciency and operational grid security. Grid security
must always be the top priority. Yet, operating the grid overly conservative is suboptimal
since it introduces excessive limitations that prevents the grid from being operated at its full
potential.
This balance has become increasingly important as the share of variable renewable energy
(VRE) sources has continued to grow. Utilizing renewable energy (RE) when it is available is
critical for ensuring cheap and clean electricity production. However, the uncertainty related to
the intermittent nature of RE has introduced challenges in predicting the electricity generation
and power system state in advance. Ultimately, this makes it harder for the TSOs to provide
secure operational capacities while optimizing socioeconomic welfare. Accordingly, there has
been a need for a more precise modelling of the available transmission capacities sent to the
market [26].
In the Nordic power system, this has lead to the implementation of the Flow-Based (FB)
capacity calculation process which went live on the 29th October 2024 [55]. While FB will
be discussed in greater detail in the following sections, its main objective is to maximize the
transmission capacity made available for cross-zonal exchanges in the DA market coupling - a
process now often referred to as Flow-based market coupling (FBMC) [12]. The FB capacity
calculations are performed in collaboration between the four Nordic TSOs (Energinet from
Denmark, Svenska Kraftnät from Sweden, Statnett from Norway, and Fingrid from Finland)
and the Nordic Regional Coordination Centre (Nordic RCC).
1.2
Nordic RCC and its Processes
Nordic RCC is an independent company owned by the four Nordic TSOs. Nordic RCC supports
the four Nordic TSOs in maintaining the operational security of the Nordic power system [27].
Its independent status enables the TSOs to strengthen collaboration and operate the system
in a fair and integrated manner, ultimately leading to a more eﬀicient utilization of the Nordic
grid and RE resources. Nordic RCC provides a wide range of services to the TSOs, however,
most important for this thesis are the three Nordic RCC services:
1. Common Grid Model Alignment (CGMA)
2. Common Grid Model (CGM)
3. Flow-Based Capacity Calculations

1.2 (i)
CGMA
The starting point for the entire process timeline is the CGMA. Simply put, the CGMA is a
predictive algorithm that generates two types of output:
• D-2 predictions of NPs for the 12 Nordic BZs
• D-2 predictions of power flows across all 30 CB transmission lines in the Nordic power
system.
Two days before (D-2) the Energy Delivery Date (EDD) Nordic RCC receives forecasts from
the TSOs of load and in-elastic (price insensitive) production for each Nordic BZ. A historical
pool of Nordic CB flows and NPs from the last 96 hours of Euphemia market results are then
collected. This data compose the input to the CGMA process at Nordic RCC.
The fundamental principles of the current CGMA methodology follow a rather intuitive ap-
proach:

1.2 (ii)
D-2 IGMs and CGM
The D-2 predictions of NPs and CB flows from the CGMA are sent back to the TSOs. Each
TSO then use these predictions to establish their own D-2 Individual Grid Model (IGM).
Through D-2 IGMs, the TSOs try to represent the best possible forecast of transmission system
conditions using the predictions of the CGMA as starting point [18]. The IGMs describe the
power system elements (generators, loads, substations, transmission lines) at each control area.
The IGMs are therefore highly detailed models, covering a vast number of network elements
3
==PAGE:14==
in the transmission system, accounting also for planned outages and thermal limits. The IGM
structure allows for high-fidelity alternating current (AC) load flow simulations to define voltage
levels, active and reactive power.
Once all four D-2 IGMs are established, they are sent to Nordic RCC and merged into a D-2
CGM by getting all IGMs to converge and reach a balance in terms of production and load in
line with the D-2 NP predictions [12]. The D-2 CGM is then the best available D-2 prediction of
the Nordic grid and serves as the foundation for subsequent processes at Nordic RCC, including
the FB capacity calculations.
1.2 (iii)
Flow-Based
The technical aspects of the FB calculations will be explained later in Part II Section 9.4,
where FB calculations are performed on a test system. This section merely provides a high-
level overview of the FB process and purpose.
The goal of the FB capacity calculation process is to integrate information from the physics of
the power system into the Euphemia market coupling. The FB capacity calculations are based
on a direct current (DC) approximation of the nodal D-2 CGM AC power flow solution. This
power flow solution represents a deterministic snapshot of the power system based on predicted
generation, load, and network topology. However, Euphemia needs to adjust the zonal NPs
(not nodal power injections) to match both economic and physical constraints in the market
coupling process. Thus, FB can be understood as a linearization around the D-2 CGM solution
determining the feasible operational space where Euphemia can adjust NPs before transmission
line capacities are violated.
For each hour, the TSOs select a set of Critical Network Elements (CNE) (overhead lines, un-
derground cables and transformers) that they deem most important for capacity calculations
because they are expected to have an influence on the amount of power that can be exchanged
between BZs [53]. The selection of CNEs is based on a combination of operational experience
and numerical analysis. FB capacity calculations are only conducted on these CNEs and thus
not on the rest of the network elements. CNEs can be both internal elements inside a BZ or
CB elements between BZs.
For each CNE and for each hour, a remaining available margin (RAM) is then calculated. The
RAM of a CNE represents the maximum transmission capacity that can be implicitly auctioned
in the DA market [57]. That is, the transmission capacity left after accounting for the D-2 CGM
flows, reliability margins, and other operational parameters. For the exact definition of RAM
used by Nordic RCC, see Article 17 in [53].
However, FB does not only determine the RAMs. It also provides linear sensitivities, Power
Transfer Distribution Factors (PDTFs), of how changes in zonal NPs changes the flows on trans-
mission lines. So, when a BZ’s production or consumption changes in FB, the corresponding
changes in power flows are determined assuming a linear relationship between changes in NPs
and flows [26]. Note that this implies that FB adopts a zonal rather than nodal assumption on
how generation and load changes translate into flows.
Together, the RAMs and PTDFs form the feasible space (the FB domain) in which Euphemia
can operate within. It is therefore through the FB domain that information from the D-2 CGM
is transferred to the Euphemia market coupling, ensuring that Euphemia can optimize the Eu-
ropean market solution while respecting the physical transmission limits of the Nordic CNEs.
The ambition is that this approach enhances market eﬀiciency, security, and the integration of
renewable energy sources (RES) compared to the previous capacity calculation methodology
[26].

==PAGE:15==
1.2 (iv)
D-1 IGMs and CGM
Inevitably, the linear approximations and the nodal to zonal transformation during the FB
capacity calculations and in Euphemia introduce errors. Additionally, the market is only cleared
using the set of CNEs as network constraints. Consequently, this demands a need for another
full simulation of the IGM and CGM AC power flow simulations, now using the D-1 market
solution as the starting point. This is done to check all network elements for possible overloads
that may not have been captured in the simplified FB market coupling process. Nordic RCC
then runs N-1 security analyses to conclude if any remedial actions need to be taken. However,
this thesis will not include N-1 security analyses as part of its scope.
1.3
Motivation
A concern with the current CGMA methodology is that as the renewable production capacities
continues to increase, a historical pool limited to 96 hours may not always contain NPs and
flows from power system states that closely match the new D-2 TSO forecasts. For instance, if
the last four days has experienced high wind production, but a rapid drop is expected, there is
a risk that the CGMA predictions may significantly deviate from actual system outcome. As
a result, it has become a priority of Nordic RCC to explore state-of-the-art methodologies to
enhance the CGMA process. Consequently, this task serves as the focus of Part I of this thesis,
which investigates how modern machine learning (ML) models can improve D-2 predictions of
NPs and CB flows compared to the current CGMA implementation.
Simultaneously, the recent go-live of FB capacity calculations has generated a huge interest
from both TSOs and Nordic RCC in evaluating the FB quality and its impact on the market
results. As a part of this FB quality assessment, understanding the influence of CGMA on
the FB market coupling process has become increasingly important. Accordingly, Part II of
this thesis is dedicated to analyzing the impact of CGMA on FB and the resulting market
outcomes.
This analysis aims to provide insights into how improvements in CGMA could
enhance socioeconomic welfare and system security.

Introduction to Part I
Part I of this thesis explores how ML models can serve as an alternative to the current CGMA
implementation at Nordic RCC. Both BZ NPs and CB flows will be predicted. Yet, the main
focus is on predicting CB flows — a much more complex task, but one that offers very high
informational value to Nordic RCC and the TSOs.
2.1
Introduction to the Prediction Task
CB electricity exchanges are derived from the generation and load both within and outside of
the Nordic power system. For any BZ, the following balance equation must hold at all times,
as energy cannot accumulate within the power system:
Production −Load −Export + Import = 0
(1)
Based on this, a NP can be defined as either:
NP = Production −Load
or
NP = Export −Import
(2)
So, NPs essentially show whether a system has an energy surplus or deficit based on production
and load (domestically) or exports and imports (in terms of trade).
Given that each of the 12 Nordic BZs has multiple possible import and export connections,
there are potentially infinite combinations of imports and exports that can achieve the same
NP. For example, in a given hour, DK2 may experience a surplus of wind energy, resulting in
a NP of 1000 MW. One solution to achieve this NP could involve DK2 exporting 333 MW to
DK1, 333 MW to SE4, and 334 MW to DE-LU. Alternatively, DK2 could export 2000 MW to
SE4 while importing 500 MW from both DK1 and DE-LU.
Thus, even if the NPs of all 12 Nordic BZs are known, the exact flow distribution are not given,
as there are multiple options for electricity trading within the Nordic power system as well as
with Central Europe and the Baltics. Predicting DA CB flows is therefore a complex task that
requires an understanding of the factors influencing these flows.
The 30 Nordic DA CB flows result from the Euphemia market coupling algorithm based on
merit order curves, where the cheapest generators are prioritized to meet demand, and CB
capacities are utilized to cover load at the lowest possible cost through CB trading [24]. The
30 CB flows depend on factors such as:
1. Availability of weather-dependent resources for renewable production
2. Load levels in various zones and how weather and time factors influence them
3. Identification of the cheapest producers and their locations
4. How the physical characteristics of electrical grid components affect flow distribution
7
==PAGE:18==
5. Capacity in the grid on both CB lines and on internal lines within BZs
6. The topology of the network, including temporary outages of generators or transmission
lines that alter the structure of the power system
Changes in these factors from one hour to the next may lead to sudden price fluctuations
between BZs, causing the flow distribution to change drastically. This volatility makes it very
challenging to predict flows, as they become high-frequency variables with an output that can
appear relatively step-like since flows often reach their maximum line capacity. Outages further
complicate predictions, as these topology changes can have substantial and unexpected impacts
on flow distribution, while the timing of outages is diﬀicult to predict.
So, to reliably make predictions of the 30 Nordic DA CB flows, it is essential to have access
to various types of data to address the above factors for each hour. Additionally, a suﬀiciently
long historical period is necessary, as only then will a ML model have enough information to
learn how flows in the system distribute given a range of NPs and different topology changes.

3
Literature Review on ML for Power System Forecast-
ing
The electricity system combines the stochastic nature of electricity consumption and VRE
production with the physical requirement that production and consumption must always be
balanced. As a result, accurate predictions have long been a priority, and therefore power
system forecasts are a well-studied field in constant development [43].
To scope our research, the following search criteria have been applied:
• Neural networks (NN) for power system forecast
• DA price forecast NN
• Nordic power system forecast NN
• Load forecast using transformers
The primary aim of Part I of this thesis is to predict Nordic DA CB flows. However, the
primary body of literature revolves around predicting load, DA prices, and RE generation [30].
This is because CB exchanges are in fact a derivation of the spatial distribution of load and
production and not the other way around. It is therefore more fundamental to predict load
and renewable production rather than CB exchanges, why the research on predicting CB flows
is quite limited.
To our knowledge, the use of CB flows as a target for prediction has only been explored in
Wallin’s work in 2016 [70], which did not employ NNs but rather utilized multiple linear re-
gression and Tobit models to constrain the output. Another closely related study is by Abdel-
Khalek [1], who used NNs to predict CB power transmission capacities which is the output of
the Central European FB model. However, the fact that the primary body of literature revolves
around the prediction of load, DA prices, and RE generation is not an issue since the problems
are highly similar, meaning that the same model types and input data will be applied to a
large extent regardless of the output being load, renewable production, or CB flows. Therefore,
the subsequent literature review will focus on time series forecasting using ANNs in a general
power grid context.
Traditionally, statistical tools such as the ARIMA method were used for load and price fore-
casting. However, with advancements in computational power and increased availability of
data, deep learning has become increasingly prevalent in the field [41].
ML offer the advantage of autonomously discovering relationships within data, particularly
in capturing non-linear dependencies between features and the target variable. Consequently,
deep learning algorithms have been extensively applied to load forecasting as well as wind and
solar power forecasting. Initially, fully connected NNs with a few hidden layers were used for
prediction tasks [13]. However, as the problem is inherently a time series task, more advanced
methods that incorporate prior system states have been explored. A widely used approach
has been recurrent neural networks (RNNs), which are capable of identifying temporal depen-
dencies in data by considering the previous state at each time step. Noman Shabbir et al.
[59] showed how an RNN model was used to outperform a Linear regression, an SVM, and a
Tree-based regression for residential load forecasting.
A significant drawback of RNNs, however, is the vanishing or exploding gradient problem,
where the gradient either diminishes or explodes for data points that are chronologically far
away from the one being evaluated, especially in long sequence forecasting [58]. To address
this issue, long-short-term memory (LSTM) networks have been widely adopted as they are
9
==PAGE:20==
more effective at capturing long-term temporal dependencies. In Deivamani et al. [13], the
baseline LSTM was refined by developing a bi-directional LSTM, which processes information
both forward and backward, unlike the standard LSTM that processes in only one direction.
However, this modification only showed minimal improvements in the load forecasting task,
over the baseline LSTM when evaluating the root mean squared error.
A strong competitor to LSTM is the convolutional neural network (CNN). Although CNNs
are primarily known for image processing, a 1D version, referred to as temporal convolutional
networks, has proven applicable to time series forecasting. Pavićević et al. [49] investigate
the effectiveness of an RNN, LSTM, and CNN in forecasting DA electricity prices using data
from the Hungarian HUPX market and weather variables. Here, the authors found that a CNN
combined with a dense layer marginally outperformed RNN, and LSTM models by being better
at predicting outlier cases.
One of the most recent developments in time series forecasting is the introduction of attention
mechanisms, and more specifically, transformers. The transformer architecture, first introduced
by Vaswani et al. [69], was originally designed for natural language processing. More recently,
transformers have been applied to time series forecasting and have shown significant promise.
For load forecasting, transformers have demonstrated strong performance. In L’Heureux et
al. [41], the transformer model was tested on multiple datasets with various prediction hori-
zons, particularly excelling at larger output windows of up to 36 hours with hourly predictions.
However, as indicated in Latisos et al., transformers are not a universal solution. In their DA
electricity price prediction case, a combination of CNN and a gated recurrent neural network
(a simplified version of LSTM) outperformed the transformer model when evaluated using the
mean absolute percentage error.
During the research into various NN architectures for power-related forecasting tasks, we repeat-
edly encountered the XGBoost model. Although XGBoost is not a type of NN, it is a tree-based
learning algorithm widely used for both regression and classification tasks [14]. Since its intro-
duction in 2016, XGBoost has gained significant popularity, particularly in machine learning
forecasting competitions as desribed in Pinson et al. [50] and Sonawane et al. [64].
In addition to its general strengths, such as handling missing data, scalability, and computa-
tional eﬀiciency [64], XGBoost has shown strong performance in a range of power forecasting
applications. For instance, it has been successfully applied to short-term load forecasting by
Aguilar Madrid et al. [2], and to wind power production forecasting by Xiong et al. [73].
However, it is important to note that XGBoost is not always the top performer. In some cases,
such as in the study by Singh et al. [62], LSTM models outperformed XGBoost in short-term
load forecasting tasks.
In summary, our review of the literature on NNs for power grid forecasting indicates that the
optimal network architecture has not been definitively established in the current body of work.
The optimal NN configuration is dependent on variables such as computational capability,
number and types of features, and size of output window. Moreover, XGBoost has proven to
be a strong candidate when exploring alternatives beyond neural network-based architectures.
Aside from Wallin’s work [70], to our knowledge, no research has specifically addressed the
forecasting of CB flows, indicating that the optimal ML framework for this task has yet to be
established. Consequently, an exploratory approach will be taken to forecast CB flows, testing
a range of model architectures to evaluate their respective strengths and weaknesses.

Specifically, the data represents Scheduled Commercial Exchanges from the DA market clear-

The transmission line data includes both high voltage direct current (HVDC) and high voltage
alternating current (HVAC) lines. As illustrated in Figure 4, the behavior of these two types of

The AC line NO1→NO5 exhibits continuous and highly variable flow patterns, ranging from

(NTC) in both directions, as shown in the right plot. The following are two likely reasons for
this behavior:
1. DC lines are typically more costly and the resulting dimensioning can therefore lead to
frequent congestion and flows hitting the capacity limit.
2. DC lines often span longer distances between areas with large price differences, making
their usage more binary (max import or max export).
Figure 55 in Appendix C, shows the histograms of all 30 market flows. The histograms confirm
that, in general, the DC lines exhibit less diversity in terms of unique flow values compared
to the AC lines. I.e., DC lines tend to be utilized at certain levels. Very frequently they are
used at either full export, full import, or not at all. The AC lines show smoother and more
continuous distributions, with values spread across their entire capacity ranges, indicating more
diversity in the patterns of how they are utilized.
These patterns present challenges in predictive modeling, requiring consideration of both the
DC and AC characteristics in the prediction models, as well as the dynamic nature of available
capacity due to planned transmission outages.

4.1 (ii)
Data: Time Attributes
Based on the assumption that CB flows encompass some cyclical nature, for instance, the hour
of the day or whether it is a work day or not, several time-related features were generated.
The hour of the day and the day of the year were extracted directly from the timestamp index
of the data. To capture cyclical trends, sine and cosine transformations were applied to these
values. Specifically, the hour of the day was normalized over a 24-hour cycle, while the day of
the year was normalized over a 365-day cycle, producing sin hour, cos hour, sin day, and cos
day features (see equation (10) to (13)). These transformations allow the model to recognize
periodic patterns, such as the fact that hour 0 is very close to hour 23 and that the end of one
year transitions to the beginning of the next, allowing the model to learn the daily and seasonal
variations in electricity flow.
To account for non-working days, holiday data for Denmark, Sweden, Norway, and Finland
was generated using the holidays package [68]. Each date was then checked to determine if it
fell on a weekend or a national holiday for each country. For each country, a binary feature
was created (DK non working day, SE non working day, NO non working day, and FI non
working day) to indicate non-working days, with weekends and national holidays marked as
non-working.

4.1 (iv)
Data: Outage Information
One key challenge in forecasting CB transmission flows is the ever-changing grid topology.
Fluctuations in flow can be attributed not only to RE production or electricity consumption
but also to planned and unplanned outages on both generation assets and transmission lines.
These outages are accounted for using data from ENTSO-E’s Transparency Platform [21].
For the entire 2024, outage data was collected from each BZ in the countries listed in Table 2,
as well as from France. Despite France having no direct transmission lines to any Scandinavian
BZs, its market size suggested that outages there could still have an impact on the target
variables.
The raw data on generation and transmission outages required significant processing to make it
usable, both ensuring correct format, filtering out unnecessary information or data that would
not be applicable in a real-world scenario.
The outage data was categorized into planned and unplanned outages.
Unplanned outage
messages were excluded for the first 36 hours after being reported, as it was assumed that the
prediction would be created at least 36 hours before the actual time of use.
For generation outages, a new feature was constructed for every unique combination of BZ and
generation type. This resulted in 33 new features, representing, for instance, nuclear outages
in SE3, hydro reservoir outages in NO2, and biomass power plant outages in FI. These features
were expressed such that, for example, a value of 100 for biomass outages in FI indicated a
total outage of 100 MW for biomass power plants in Finland during that specific hour.
Transmission line outages are even more critical, as they can directly explain reductions in
19
==PAGE:30==
flows, as illustrated in the left plot of Figure 9, unexplainable to any of the other features
implemented. The features were constructed such that the baseline value for a line’s capacity,
like DK1→NL, was the maximum NTC, obtained from [20]. Outages would then reduce the
feature’s value for specific timestamps. As shown in Figure 9, transmission outages are not
necessarily symmetrical, so two features are constructed for each line, each representing a flow
direction. However, since some lines did not experience outages, not all lines were assigned two
transmission outages.
Figure 9: Example of capacities aligning and not aligning well with D-1 Market Flows
However, as shown in the right plot of Figure 9, transmission outage data do not always align

and maximum constraint in the prediction model. Nevertheless, because flows and capacities

penalty factor in the loss function, as described later in Section 5.1 (i) .
Additionally, the RAM features were constructed based on the transmission outage data. For
each line, the flow lags (described in the upcoming section, 4.1 (v) ) and the estimated capacity

4.1 (v)
Data: DA Lags from ENTSO-E
In lack of open-source D-2 power system forecast, the latest available D-1 market data from
ENTSO-E are used as an alternative. By using these forecasts two days before EDD, system
forecasts 24 hours before the target time are obtained. This effectively creates 24-hour lags of
price, generation, load, flow and NP forecasts.

These lags may become useful for the ML models since power system data typically exhibit time
series behavior with daily patterns. While the system state is never completely identical to its
state 24 hours earlier, there is still a noticeable correlation between an hour and its 24-hour lag,
as shown in Figure 10. This strengthens confidence in using the lags as a reasonable substitute
for actual D-2 forecasts.

most influential among the lags that are allowed to be used for our D-2 predictions.
All five plots have their 24th lag outside of the shaded area surrounding the x-axis.

load and generation of many other BZs. In addition, each NP can correspond to countless
combinations of flows. This confirms the importance of holistically capturing the overall power

1. Prices, Generation, Load: SE1, SE2, SE3, SE4, NO1, NO2, NO3, NO4, NO5, FI, DK1,

2. Flows: The 30 Nordic CB flows
3. NPs: The 12 Nordic BZs
Like for the weather data and generation outage data, the aim is to include the zones surround-
ing the Nordic system to get a clear picture of their forecasted load, generation, and prices,

since these will have a huge impact on the exchanges on the borders of the Nordic power sys-

the hope is that, together, these lagged features can enable sophisticated ML models to learn
interactions and the complex power system dynamics known from domain knowledge.
An example of such a power system dynamic is the relationship between BZ prices and flows. A
general rule of thumb for CB exchanges is that electricity flows from low-priced zones to high-
priced zones, although this is not always the case in the FBMC process where non-intuitive
flows sometimes exist [46]. The transmissions between DK1 and NO2 provide a clear example
of this dynamic, as illustrated in Figure 11. When wind resources are abundant in Denmark,
the Danish electricity prices drop, and DK1 exports to NO2. Conversely, Norway provides flex-

surrounding zones, the features account for internal and external influences on CB exchanges,
which hopefully allow the ML models to capture the relationships between prices, generation,
load, flows, and NPs.

4.1 (vii)
Data: Nordic RCC CGMA D-2 Input Data
Due to the confidentiality of the Nordic RCC CGMA D-2 input data, time series or distributions
of the data cannot be shown in this thesis. However, it has been allowed to qualitatively describe
the data, to emphasize the importance of using the data for developing the ML models.
The Nordic RCC receives the CGMA D-2 input data from the four Nordic TSOs and uses it as
direct input to the CGMA process. This data is very valuable for developing our ML models
to predict DA market flows in D-2, as such D-2 forecasts of power system data are, at least to
our knowledge, not available open-source. The data consists of forecasts of inelastic generation,
load, and transmission capacities two days ahead.
In this context, inflexible generation refers to the Nordic generation that is price-insensitive
and thus typically dispatched in the DA market clearing. The generation forecasts include:
1. Wind: DK1, DK2, FI, NO1, NO2, NO3, NO4, SE1, SE2, SE3, SE4
2. Solar: FI, DK1, DK2
3. Run-of-River Hydro: NO1, NO2, NO3, NO4, NO5
4. Small Scale Production: SE1, SE2, SE3, SE4
5. Nuclear: FI, SE3

Wind, solar, run-of-river hydro, and small scale production are classic examples of renewable
power production that are inherently price-insensitive. Their production depends on weather
conditions, and their operating costs are very low, which means they are utilized as much as
possible whenever available. Nuclear power plants on the other hand are in a gray zone. For
many operative and regulative reasons in the Nordic power system, they can be denoted as
price-insensitive because they are constantly operating as base-load producers [72]. This can
also be seen in the D-2 CGMA where nuclear power plants in FI and SE3 are always forecasted
to produce at at least 40% capacity. In contrast, hydro storage plants are highly price-sensitive
and are used flexibly to cover demand that inflexible generation cannot meet. Therefore, hydro
storage among other conventional generation types are not included in the D-2 CGMA input
data.
The D-2 CGMA input data also contains load forecasts for all 12 Nordic BZs, which are all
used as input in our ML models.
The D-2 capacity forecasts, however, are not used as input in our ML model. The D-2 capacity
forecasts that Nordic RCC gets from the TSOs are link capacities, which are also adjusted
based on the AC grid limitations. Consequently, these capacities do not align with the DA CB
flows targeted by our ML models. Instead, the ENTSO-E capacities, described in Section 4.1
(iv) , were preferred, as the DA flows conformed more closely with these than with the D-2
CGMA capacity forecasts.

4.2
Missing Data
In the entire combined input dataset, around 6% of the rows had one or more cases of features
with missing values. The initial aim of this project was to develop a variety of ML models,
including time series models. Therefore, an ARIMA model was designed with the purpose of
imputing missing values in each feature to preserve the 24 hours block structure in the data.
However, after discontinuing the development of time series models like RNN and LSTM, it was
decided to remove all rows with missing values instead of imputing. This choice was made to
minimize our impact on the input data, since our model development had shown that suﬀicient
data was available even after removing 6% of the data.
Consequently, the combined input data X has the dimensions (6084,2057), and the target
matrix y has the dimensions (6084,30).
4.3
Partitioning into Training, Validation, and Testing Data
It was decided to design a flexible partitioning strategy that could split the data as closely as
possible to specified proportions X%, Y%, and Z% for training, validation, and testing. The
daily block structure in the data was preserved at the points where the splits occurred between
training, validation, and testing.
Ultimately, the last 14 days of the data (equivalent to 336 hours or approximately 5% of the
data) were put aside as test data. The rest of the data were then divided into training and
validation sets using an 80/20 split, as visualized in Figure 13.
25
==PAGE:36==
Figure 13: Illustration of the partitioning into training, validation, and testing data
The reasoning behind using only 14 days (approximately 5% of the data) as test data is that
topology changes appear to have a significant impact on the data.
Predicting too far into
the future increases the risk of the models encountering flows limited by outages that were
not represented in the training or validation data.
While this issue seems unavoidable to
some extent, this approach offers a more fair evaluation of the models’ performances given
the relatively short dataset available (9 months). It would likely be meaningful to expand the
dataset with data from 2023 as well. However, Viking Link from DK1 to GB was first launched
on the 1st of January 2024, which is why this date was initially chosen as the starting point to
ensure the models were trained on the current system’s main topology. It is also worth noting
that if Nordic RCC decides to implement ML models as a replacement for their current CGMA
model, the intention is to retrain the models continuously and only predict a 24-hour block for
D-2. This justifies the design of a model that predicts a relatively small amount of data within
the near future.
As previously mentioned, it was observed in the data that there were transmission outages
in the validation and test datasets that did not occur during the training data period. This
made it very challenging for our models to effectively use features such as capacities, RAM,
and generation outages, as it lacked a basis to properly associate them with flow limitations
during training.
The discontinuation of time series model development allowed us to address this problem by
shuffling the training and validation data, thereby breaking the 24-hour blocks, as illustrated
in Figure 14. This approach allowed the models to utilize the topology related features more
effectively, which could clearly be seen from subsequent feature analyses.

XGBoost Theory
Concept of XGBoost
The development of an XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) model in this thesis was mo-
tivated by its recent popularity and state-of-the-art performance in various ML competitions
and applications for ML tasks using structured or tabular data [64]. XGBoost is a tree-based
supervised learning algorithm developed in 2016 that can be used to solve both prediction and
classification tasks [14]. In Gradient Boosting, an ensemble of weak models are combined to
improve the predictions, and in the case of XGBoost, these weak models are simple gradient
boost decision trees [29]. XGBoost initializes with a single tree. Then, it sequentially adds
new trees that predict the residual errors made by the previous tree until a stopping criteria is
met. XGBoost applies the gradient descent algorithm to minimize loss, by only adding models
in the negative gradient direction [29].

5.1 (iii)
Stacked Ensemble Model
The concept of a stacked ensemble model is rather easy to understand. In a stacked ensemble
model, the predictions of multiple base models are fed into a more simple but regularized model,
the meta-model. The meta-model is then trained on the predictions of the base models. In
this way, the base models are effectively combined through the meta-model which can then be
used to make the final predictions [65]. Making a stacked ensemble model is therefore only
appropriate when the base models are diverse, so that the predictions (or errors) made by the
models are uncorrelated [10]. Because when errors are uncorrelated, the ensemble model can
use the unique strengths, while avoiding potential weaknesses, of each base model, thereby
optimizing the overall performance.
One of the advantages of stacking therefore lies in its
ability to integrate very diverse models to solve complex prediction tasks.
In a Nordic RCC operational context, the most important aspects for any flow prediction
algorithm, in addition to performance, is the reliability, transparency, and interpretability of
the models and results. This makes ensemble models very suitable for two reasons. In terms
of reliability, they generalize well. A simple and regularized meta-model prevents overfitting
to the base line predictions [17]. Secondly, during operations, Nordic RCC would not depend
solely on a single model. Instead, Nordic RCC can analyze the outputs from both the base
models and the stacked ensemble model. This multi-model approach minimizes the risk that
downstream Nordic RCC processes are compromised by significant individual model errors. In
terms of transparency, and interpretability, a simple meta-model is easy to interpret and Nordic
RCC can afterwards explore how the base models each contribute to the final predictions made
by the meta-model.
In this thesis, the stacked ensemble model has been implemented using an elastic net regression
model as meta-model. An elastic net regression model is a simple linear regression that uses
penalties from both the lasso and ridge techniques to regularize [67]. Figure 20 shows a chart
of the implementation and will later be referred to as the Ensemble model.

5.1 (iv)
Feature Importance Methods
This thesis employs two methods for assessing feature importance: Shapley values and Feature
Permutation. These methods were used for two different purposes. First, to reduce the number
of features in our dataset as described in Section 5.2. Second, they function as a post-analysis
tool to explain how feature inputs impact the output of our complex models, as done in Section
6.3.
Shapley values
The Shapley value is an important tool within the field of explainable AI. The concept of Shapley
values originates from cooperative game theory [61]. While the mathematical foundation of
Shapley values is out of scope of this thesis, an explanation can be found in [42].
For every prediction generated by a ML model, there is a set of Shapley values equal to the total
number of input features. These Shapley values are a decomposition of the model’s predictions
and can be understood as how each feature contributes to the outcome for a specific prediction
relative to the model’s average prediction [42]. In other words, by summing up all features’
Shapley values for a particular prediction and then adding the average of all predictions, the
model’s predicted value can be reconstructed. Shapley values can therefore both be positive or
negative, depending on their contribution to the prediction. A positive Shapley value indicates
that the feature pushes the prediction above the average prediction. Vice versa applies when the
Shapley value is negative. The higher the absolute magnitude of the Shapley value, the more
impact the feature has on the model’s prediction. This makes Shapley values a powerful tool
39
==PAGE:50==
for explaining the behaviour of complex ML models at high granularity by providing insights
into how each feature impacts the final predictions.
Feature Permutation
Despite the many strengths of Shapley values, the Shapley encounters exponential complexity
as the number of features increases in a dataset, why methods to approximate the Shapley
may be needed as described in Zhang et al. [74]. Running trials computing Shapley values
for our XGBoost and NN models showed a much lower computational time for the XGBoost
compared to the NN. So, it was decided to continue using Shapley values for the XGBoost
but seek other options for the NN. Thus, for accessing the feature importance within the NN
model, the importance method of feature permutation was used. This method involves shuffling
the values of one feature while keeping all other features constant, and then comparing the
original prediction score (e.g., MSE) to the new prediction score with the shuffled feature.
Thus, the relation between the specific feature and the target variable is broken. This method
is thoroughly described and applied by Huang et al.
[33] in a short-term load forecasting
problem. In short, a notable drop in performance (prediction score like MSE) indicates that
the feature is important for the model’s prediction, whereas a negligible drop suggests that the
feature is less important. This process is repeated for all features to calculate each feature’s
importance score, allowing them to be ranked based on their predictive importance.
5.2
Integrated Hyperparameter Tuning and Feature Reduction
Due to the very large dataset, consisting of 2057 features with many being strongly correlated
(as seen with the weather features in Figure 7), there was a strong incentive for feature reduc-
tion. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was omitted due to a request from Nordic RCC for
explainable ML, as PCA makes it diﬀicult to explain the contributions of individual features.
Instead, the approach shown in Algorithm 2 was adopted. First, the full model with all features
is trained with hyperparameter optimization using Optuna. Then, each feature is assigned a
feature importance score, and a specified number of features are removed in each iteration.
This process is repeated until a predefined threshold of features is reached.
Due to computational limits, different parameters were chosen for feature reduction for XG-
Boost and the NN as seen in Table 6. As mentioned previously, the Shapley value calculations
were much faster for the XGBoost compared to the NN. However, training the NN with feature
permutation as importance score was much faster than training the XGBoost with Shapley
value calculations. Therefore, it was decided to reduce the number of features removed per
iteration to 10 for the NN, while keeping the corresponding value for XGBoost at 50.
The feature reduction was run on DTUs HPC using 24 cores. The runtime of the integrated
hyperparameter tuning and feature reduction loop was roughly 70 hours for both XGBoost and
the NN. Due to the confidentiality of the CGMA input data, it was excluded from the HPC
process as it could not be stored on an external server. After feature reduction, the CGMA
features were added back to the models.

NN Penalty
As discussed in Section 4.1 (iv) , the CB lines experience outages or maintenance work, resulting
in time-varying capacities. However, as previously shown in Figure 9, the capacity data and D-1
market flows do not always align. Consequently, a soft penalty that nudges the flow predictions
to stay within the capacities is more appropriate than imposing a hard constraint that forces
them to stay within the line capacities.
To address this, a NN with the same hyperparameters as the original NN model, listed in
Table 7, was constructed, but with a penalty term added to the loss function, as presented in
Equation 20.
This introduces a new hyperparameter, λweight, which determines the weight of the penalty
term. To find an optimal value of λweight, 51 values within the range from 0% to 100% (2%
step size) were tested. The test MAE and MSE of these runs are shown in Figure 24. While
some fluctuations are observed, the results indicate that the optimal λweight lies between 40%
and 60%.
The final NN model with penalty was chosen as the model that performed best in this analysis
with respect to both MSE and MAE. This model has a λweight of 46%, i.e., the penalty term for
exceeding the line capacities is weighted 46% in the NN loss function, while the MSE accounts
for the remaining 54%.

5.3 (v)
Stacked Ensemble Model
Previously, the Ensemble model was described as one single model.
Implementation wise,
this is not the case. An elastic net regression only has one alpha (L1) and one lambda (L2)
regularization parameter. So, if a single elastic net regression were used to predict flows for
all 30 lines simultaneously, it would require a single set of alpha and lambda values that fit all
lines collectively. This conflicts with the aim of tailoring each ensemble model to the specific
characteristics of a line. Thus in practice, 30 unique ensemble models were created, i.e. one
for each line, instead of a single multi-output model. Each ensemble model effectively acts
as a linear regression with 90 weights (corresponding to the 30 predictions from each of the
three base models), using both L1 and L2 regularization. This setup allows unique alpha and
lambda values to be optimized for each line. These sets of hyper parameters can be seen in
Table 25 in Appendix B. Combining all 30 ensemble models gives a weight matrix of 90x30.
46
==PAGE:57==
This matrix can be interpreted as a mapping of how the prediction for a given line (row) from
a base model impacts the final Ensemble prediction for the same or another line (column).
This weight matrix provides the opportunity to analyze how each base model contributes to
the final Ensemble model. By summing, normalizing, and grouping the absolute coeﬀicients,
each model’s proportional contribution to the Ensemble can be calculated. In Figure 25, the
contribution of the final base models to the Ensemble model have been calculated and visualized.
Figure 25: Model Contribution to the Ensemble Model
In Figure 25 each bar represents a specific line in the dataset, and each segment of the stacked
bars shows the proportional contribution of each base model to the Ensemble model’s overall
prediction. The varying proportions across lines shows that the Ensemble model utilizes the
base models differently depending on the line being predicted. As mentioned previously, the
contributions shown in the plot are based on the validation predictions of the base models. So,
if a base model slightly overfits to the validation data, its contribution to the Ensemble may be
inflated because it appears more important to the Ensemble model than it actually is. However,
this again is the main reason for choosing a simple meta-model. By doing so, overfitting to the
validation predictions is minimized, ensuring that the Ensemble model generalizes well on the
unseen test data.

Description of Evaluation Metrics
The performance of the models will be evaluated based on five metrics: MSE, MAE, PAP, PSE,
and DAC. These metrics are picked to provide a broad evaluation of the models’ accuracy, error
severity, and how well their predictions follow the trend of the true D-1 market flows. Short
descriptions of the metrics are given below.

errors due to the squaring of the errors. As a result, MSE is useful for highlighting if models
tend to have cases of high prediction errors. For flow predictions, the unit of MSE is MW2.

6.2
NP Predictions
Nordic RCC are responsible for predicting both D-2 flows and NPs for each of the 12 Nordic
BZs. So far, only flow predictions have been discussed. As mentioned previously, these CB
flows can be grouped by BZ and summed up to effectively calculate the NPs of each BZ. This
means that Nordic RCC automatically fulfills their data delivery requirements by predicting the
flows directly. However as shown, the flows can be very complex and tricky to predict. On the
other hand, predicting NPs is a lower-granularity task compared to predicting CB flows. For
Nordic RCC, it is therefore worth exploring whether directly predicting NPs improves accuracy
compared to summing up flow predictions. If direct NP predictions significantly reduce errors,
it could justify a shift in approach, where NPs are predicted first and then distributed into
flows, rather than the current method of predicting flows and summing them into NPs.
The XGBoost classification is not suitable for the NP prediction problem, since the step-like
functions are not observed for the NPs. The NN with penalty is neither applicable due to NPs
very rarely exceed the total export or import line capacity for a BZ. Consequently, there are
not enough available models to make appropriate use of the Ensemble model. Therefore, the
following NP prediction analysis is merely a comparison between CGMA and the XGBoost
regression model. Figure 30 shows the time series of the XGBoost regression model versus

predict compared to flows when comparing the XGBoost NPs predictions in Figure 30 with the
XGBoost flow predictions in Figure 26. An interesting example is DK1. The XGBoost model
performs well in capturing the overall trend and making accurate NP predictions. However, it

smaller changes in local production, consumption, or outage events may cause slight shifts in

events or changes in local production or consumption can lead to congestion that requires
different use of DC lines, resulting in a completely different flow distribution. Thus, these

flows is inherently more complex than predicting NPs. NPs can be quite robust to internal

Feature Analysis
With the CGMA predictions being a part of the critical process chain leading to the Euphemia
market clearing, the explainability of model predictions is highly important. Fortunately, by
using Shapley values, the impact of input features can be traced all the way through to the
final predictions of our ML models.
In Figure 31, the number of features from each feature category represented in the top 100 most
important features for the XGBoost regression model is shown. The importance is measured
using Shapley values as described in Section 5.1 (iv) . As expected, the D-2 forecast from the
CGMA data is the most present category in the top 100, with 26 CGMA features among the
top 100, accounting for 48% of all CGMA features. Similarly, D-1 lags account for 23 of the top
100 features. Interestingly, 21 weather features are among the top 100, showing that there is a
clear value in the weather forecasts despite the presence of D-2 RE production forecasts. Yet,
21 is only around 1% of the original 1700 weather features. This suggests that starting with
over 1700 weather features is excessive. This was also shown in Figures 23 and 22. Another
aspect to point out is the proportional representation of the time features at 88%, showing
there is a clear time-dependent nature of the flow on the transmission lines.

wind_power features, where a high value, meaning high wind production, leads to export from
DK1 to NO2.
Looking at the time features, such as the non_working features, it is seen that when it is week-
end or holiday in Norway or Denmark, there is a tendency of export from DK1 to NO2. This
tendency could be explained by the typically lower electricity consumption during weekends
and holidays. During such times, DK1 is more likely to export to Norway, since Norway’s
dispatchable hydro power functions as a battery for Denmark by exporting to Denmark when
consumption, and therefore prices, are typically higher. The CB flow is also hour-dependent,
as shown with the sin_hour being the 9th most important feature for this line. Due to the
sine cosine transformation, the interpretation is a bit more diﬀicult. The Shapley plot reveals
that when the sine feature is positive, corresponding to hours between 6 p.m. and 6 a.m., it
pushes the prediction towards export from DK1 to NO2. At the same time, a negative value
for the cosine feature (cos_hour), which represents hours between midnight and 12 a.m., also
contributes to an export prediction. Together, these indicate that the model has learned a
pattern where export from DK1 to NO2 is more likely between midnight and 6 a.m..
Lastly, we can look at the RAM features, which represent how far a flow was from the export
(max) or import (min) capacity 24 hours ago. For example, the NO2→NO5_RAM_min fea-
ture reflects how far the flow was from minimum capacity (maximum export capacity from NO5
to NO2) 24 hours ago. A high value (red) indicates electricity flowed from NO2 to NO5, which
impacts the model’s prediction toward export from DK1 to NO2. This makes sense, since if
export from NO2 to NO5 was observed 24 hours ago, it is probably due to low prices in DK1
being transferred up through Norway, leading to export from NO2 to NO5. This confirms that
the 24-hour lags are useful when predicting flows, as also highlighted in Figure 53, which shows
the correlations between CB flows and their corresponding 24-hour lags.

Conclusively, this short analysis shows how Shapley values can be used to explain model predic-

enables deeper insight into why certain predictions are made and helps improve the model’s
explainability, and thus the overall transparency of the ML pipeline.
6.4
Discussion of Results and Recommendations to Nordic RCC
Throughout Part I of this project, it has been shown that utilizing ML models can improve the
results of Nordic RCCs CGMA process drastically. However, this would require Nordic RCC
to extend their pool of CGMA input data to include features such as weather forecasts, time
attributes, outage information, and a wider range of D-1 lags. Finding relevant open-source
data has been a main priority for the ML model development in this thesis. Yet, these features
may still prove diﬀicult for Nordic RCC to use immediately. Because of the criticality of their
processes, Nordic RCC are limited to very few data channels to ensure that data is always
available. Making an operational tool such as CGMA dependant on, e.g., weather forecasts
from Met.no may therefore prove legally diﬀicult because high responsibility is unknowingly
put on YR. Still, the ML analyses in this thesis has made a proof of concept and a benchmark
for how much CGMA can be improved by implementing ML instead of the current CGMA
methodology.
The NPs prediction problem is evidently easier to tackle using ML than the CB flow problem.
Yet, even aggregating outputs from the flow models resulted in better NP predictions than
60
==PAGE:71==
the current CGMA could produce. Thus, regardless of the type of prediction problem, the
ML models improved the CGMA results. It is therefore up to Nordic RCC to decide whether
to prioritize NPs as the main prediction target or to emphasize the added value of the more
detailed and granular flow predictions.
When talking to employees at Nordic RCC, they deem reliability and trustworthiness to be the
most important areas of improvement for the CGMA besides prediction accuracy. Consequently,
the arguably biggest perk of introducing ML instead of the current CGMA methodology is in
terms of avoiding severe prediction errors. Fundamentally, the CGMA will always be limited
to pick flows and NPs within its historical pool. Thus, when data in the historical pool are
unable to decently describe the system state of the near future, it can lead to large errors in
the CGMA predictions. For example, a historical pool after a long period with very low Nordic
wind production is expected to result in severe CGMA forecast errors if the wind production
all of a sudden drastically increases. In contrast, ML models are much more flexible when it
comes to utilizing input data. Instead of relying on a relatively small historical pool, the ML
models are trained on vast amounts of data enabling them to predict very adaptively.
This makes the ML models intelligent in a way the current CGMA is not, which ultimately
increases the trust and reliability of the ML models. The Ensemble model fits into this narrative
as well. When using the highly regularized Ensemble model, Nordic RCC will never have to
rely on the output of a single model but rather on the weighted output of multiple models.
As shown in this thesis, such approach can ensure better overall generalization and thereby
enhance the reliability of the output even more. Another trait of the Ensemble model is that it
provides Nordic RCC with the opportunity to develop new types of models, such as time series
LSTMs and transformers, and easily incorporate them in their pipeline. Even non-ML models
like the current CGMA methodology could be incorporated to improve the final predictions.
As a final remark, using ML to replace the current CGMA methodology would fall under the
category of ”High-risk AI” (Management and operation of critical infrastructure) in the new EU
AI Act [25]. Therefore, certain obligations will apply for Nordic RCC if they want to incorporate
ML in their process chain. Among these obligations are the ”Transparency Obligations”: Under
the EU AI Act, transparency means that AI systems are developed and used in a way that allows
appropriate traceability and explainability (...) [23]. It was a priority of this thesis to create
an ML solution which was highly explainable and where the impact of input features could be
traced all the way through to the output. This was achieved by utilizing Shapley values for
the base models and by selecting an easily interpretable elastic net regression for the Ensemble
model. The approach undertaken in Part I of this thesis is therefore intended to serve as a
source of inspiration for Nordic RCC, should they decide to develop an ML-based CGMA that
complies with the Transparency Obligations outlined in the EU AI Act [9] [22].

7
Conclusion of Part I
The CGMA prediction constitutes the foundation for many downstream processes at Nordic
RCC, including the Flow based capacity calculations. Improving the CGMA is expected to
positively influence the outcomes of these processes, resulting in better capacity estimations
for CB lines in the Nordic power system. Therefore, Part I focused on improving CB flow
predictions through the application of ML models.
To make accurate predictions, a significant part of the work involved gathering appropriate
data. As shown in Table 1, extensive Met.no weather data was extracted for the Nordic area,
based on the locations of RE plants. In addition, data from ENTSO-E’s transparency platform
on outages of CB transmission lines and generation assets supplemented the weather data.
Due to the lack of open-source D-2 forecasts for production and consumption, DA lags from
ENTSO-E were added to the dataset, based on identified correlations between these lags and
events occurring 24 hours later. Lastly, water reservoir levels from ENTSO-E and the confi-
dential Nordic RCC CGMA data were included.
With the dataset constructed and following data partitioning and normalization, two base ML
models were developed. A base XGBoost and NN were chosen as ML models, and each model
underwent feature reduction and hyperparameter optimization. Both the XGBoost and NN
models encountered two key challenges: adhering to the capacity limits of the transmission
lines and capturing the stepwise nature of the flows. To overcome these issues, two additional
models were developed. First, a NN with an added penalty term was introduced to account
for predictions that exceeded capacity limits. Second, an XGBoost classification model was
designed to better represent the stepwise behavior of flows.
Overall, the XGBoost regression model performed best across most metrics. However, deeper
analysis showed that each model had its advantages depending on the line being examined or
the aspect being focused on. To exploit the differences between models and their strengths, a
stacked ensemble model was built, combining the XGBoost regression, NN penalty, and XG-
Boost classification models. As presented in Table 14, the Ensemble model outperformed the
individual models on four out of five metrics.
The industry partner Nordic RCC described how some TSOs use predictions for each CB line,
while others use the aggregated NP predictions. Therefore, an XGBoost regression model was
developed to directly predict NP values, to evaluate whether direct NP prediction offered ad-
vantages or whether aggregating CB flow predictions was suﬀicient. Aggregating the flows from
the XGBoost regression flow model performed better than the CGMA NP prediction; however,
significant improvements were achieved by constructing a dedicated XGBoost regression NP
model. This analysis highlighted the complexity of predicting flows, while showing that the
lower-granularity NP problem was easier to capture with ML regression models.
Given that the CGMA process is part of critical infrastructure, explainability is of high im-
portance for Nordic RCC to prevent the processes from becoming black boxes.
Therefore,
significant effort was put into analyzing the models and the contributions of individual fea-
tures to the predictions. Figure 25 illustrated the relative contributions from each model in
the Ensemble. Further analysis of feature importance for the XGBoost regression model high-
lighted the value of CGMA data, as well as the importance of weather and ENTSO-E data.
For instance, a Shapley summary plot for the DK1→NO2 line showed the dynamics of how
specific feature values influenced predictions. Features related to wind, for example, indicated
that high wind values led to predictions of export from DK1 to NO2.
The results of this analysis demonstrate the significant potential of ML approaches for pre-
dicting CB flows in the Nordic region. In particular, the Ensemble model effectively leveraged
62
==PAGE:73==
the strengths of individual models, significantly outperforming the original CGMA process at
Nordic RCC. Additionally, a high degree of explainability was preserved, ensuring that the ML
predictions remain transparent and interpretable rather than becoming entirely opaque black
boxes.
Consequently, the approach undertaken in Part I of this thesis can hopefully serve as a source of
inspiration for Nordic RCC, should they decide to develop an ML-based CGMA that complies
with the Transparency Obligations outlined in the EU AI Act [9] [22].

Introduction to Part II
In Part I, the potential of applying ML techniques to the CGMA process in Nordic RCC was
demonstrated by outperforming the current CGMA implementation. However, as shown in
Section 1.2, the CGMA process is only one component of the broader Nordic RCC operational
pipeline. Moreover, implementing new methods requires significant resources and time, so it is
essential to ensure that these efforts are worthwhile.
The goal of Part II is to analyze how CGMA prediction errors propagate through the processes
in Nordic RCC and ultimately affect the FBMC process.
To achieve this, a simulation tool that mimics the processes in the Nordic RCC has been
developed. Ideally, the Ensemble model from Part I should have been tested on the entire
Nordic RCC pipeline using historical data to evaluate the impact of improved predictions
on subsequent processes. However, due to confidentiality constraints and the fact that some
processes fall under the jurisdiction of Nordic TSOs (the IGMs), this was not possible. Instead,
a simplified pipeline was constructed, based on the test system from Schönheit et al. [57]. This
pipeline partially follows the structure and code of their paper and GitHub repository [56].

8.1 (i)
Comparison of the Base Case and CGMA Pipelines in the Simulation
Tool
Each box in the simulation tool will be thoroughly described in Section 9, but a brief overview

that both include FB calculations and the following three optimization models:
1. A nodal D-2 Common Grid Model (D-2 CGM)
2. A zonal D-1 Flow-Based Market Coupling (D-1 MC)
3. A nodal D-1 Common Grid Model (D-1 CGM)
In both pipelines, the optimization models are solved in windows of 24 hours. The light-blue
boxes represent the time-varying parameters of wind, solar, and load. Throughout the pipeline,
there is perfect foresight, so the parameters remain the same across all optimization models
and in both pipelines.
The difference between the Base Case pipeline and the CGMA pipeline is the inclusion of a
CGMA. The starting point of the Base Case pipeline is the D-2 CGM, which is solved as an
optimal nodal market clearing, using wind, solar, and load forecasts as the only time-varying
inputs. In the CGMA pipeline, the starting point is a CGMA (in this case a NN), which
tries to predict D-1 MC NPs directly. These predicted market NPs are then used to fix the
NPs in the nodal D-2 CGM, after which the remaining steps of the pipeline remain identical to
those in the Base Case pipeline. As a result, the initial NPs of the CGMA pipeline is based on
NP predictions of a simplified system with zonal prices, in contrast to the Base Case pipeline,
where the initial NPs are obtained from the market clearing of a nodal system with nodal
prices.
This setup allows us to, conduct a three-fold assessment of CGMA’s impact:
1. Isolate the impact of introducing a CGMA in the first place, by assessing the effect on
FB capacity calculations, market results, and overall system security
2. Assess how errors in CGMA predictions propagate through the pipeline
3. Analyze the added value of improving CGMA in terms of its impact on the FBMC.
8.1 (ii)
Real-World Applicability of Findings
Since the effects of CGMA in Part II are simulated on a test system, they are not directly
transferable one-to-one to the true Nordic RCC processes. As mentioned earlier there are three
major differences.
First, IGMs have been removed from the process pipeline.
Second, the
CGMs in our pipeline are solved using DC-OPFs, while the CGMs at Nordic RCC rely on AC
load flow simulations. Third, the test system itself differs significantly from the Nordic power
system in terms of complexity and network topology.
Yet, despite these simplifications we consider it reasonable to expect that many of the effects of
CGMA observed in the CGMA pipeline will also be present in the Nordic RCC process pipeline.
In fact, conducting simulations on a controlled test system removes noise that would otherwise

would be possible in the inherently less ”clean” and more complex environment of a real-world

9.3
D-2 CGM
Although the D-2 CGM is included in both the Base Case pipeline and CGMA pipeline, its
inputs and formulation vary slightly between the two.
In the Base Case pipeline, the D-2 CGM only takes load and renewable generation as input,
assuming perfect foresight of the next 24 hours. It then solves a nodal DC-OPF to determine
the optimal dispatch of conventional generators.
Since the D-2 CGM operates as a nodal
market clearing, all grid lines are included in the optimization, covering the entire network
as depicted in Figure 34. In this way, the D-2 CGM serves as a D-2 full system power flow
solution, outputting generation, flows, and nodal prices. The resulting D-2 CGM flows directly
feed into the FB calculations, while the D-2 NPs serve as inputs for the D-1 MC.
In the CGMA pipeline, the D-2 CGM takes the predicted CGMA NPs as additional input.
These are then fixed in the D-2 CGM before solving the nodal OPF. This means that the
CGMA predictions will influence the resulting generation, flows, and nodal prices since the D-2
CGM must adhere to the NPs, although the model allows for some flexibility as described later
in this section.
As highlighted in the Part II Introduction, the Nordic D-2 CGM is an AC load flow simulation,
whereas in our pipeline, it is implemented as a DC-OPF. Consequently, our D-2 CGM is less
accurate in replicating real-world electricity flows but still fulfills its purpose of providing input

data from the test system, which made an AC-OPF infeasible. Additionally, a DC-OPF ensures

computer.

9.4
Flow-based Calculations
The D-1 MC must be allowed the flexibility to adjust the NPs of the D-2 CGM for two main
reasons:
1. Coupling different markets and regions can lead to a new global optimum
2. The optimal solution in nodal OPFs may differ from the optimal solution of zonal OPFs.
As described in the FB introduction in Section 1.2 (iii) , FB defines the feasible space in which
the D-1 MC can adjust the NPs before transmission line capacities are violated. It does so
by providing RAMs and PTDFs that describe how these adjustments impact power flows in
the system. Since FB are linearized around a deterministic snapshot of the D-2 CGM, FB
essentially integrates information from the nodal D-2 CGM into the zonal D-1 MC. For sim-
plicity, the details of the nodal to zonal transformation were previously left out. However, this
transformation from the detailed nodal representation in the D-2 CGM to the simplified zonal
structure in D-1 MC is actually one of the most important aspects of the FB process [57].
Because of this fundamental shift in representation, several assumptions and approximations
must be made which essentially shapes the final FB results.
To understand the transformation one can take a starting point in the nodal CGM represen-
tation described in the previous section. Here, Equation (34k) and (34l) shows that the power
flow on each line and nodal injections are determined using voltage angles, the line susceptance
matrix H ∈R|L|×|N|, and the nodal susceptance matrix B ∈R|N|×|N|, respectively. In FB, flows
are calculated differently. Instead of explicit flow computations, PTDFs derived from H and B
are used as linear sensitivities to estimate the power flows on each line.
Here, it is important to distinguish between the nodal PTDFN ∈R|L|×|N| and the zonal
PTDFZ ∈R|L|×|Z|. Each entry in the PTDFN and PTDFZ represents the fraction (ranging
between -1 and 1) of power injected at node n or zone z that contributes to the flow on line l.
A positive PTDF value means that a power injection increases flow on line l, while a negative
value indicates a reduction. A PTDF of 0 means that the power injection does not have an
influence on the flow on that particular transmission line.
The PTDFN can be computed as the matrix multiplication of H and B−1.
PTDFN = HB−1
(35)
To ensure the mathematical solvability, node 68 is chosen as the slack node. Before performing
the matrix multiplication, the slack node column is removed from H, and both the row and
column corresponding to the slack node are removed from B. Once PTDFN is computed, the
slack node is reintroduced as a column of zeros at the appropriate index, ensuring that changes
73
==PAGE:84==
in slack node injections do not affect power flow distributions.
However, since the D-1 MC operates with zonal NPs and not nodal injections, one must convert
PTDFN to PTDFZ to define how changes in NPs translate into changes in line flows. Practically,
this is achieved using a GSK matrix with shape |N| × |Z|, where N is the set of nodes and Z
is the set of FB MC zones. GSKs describe how a change in NP is linearly mapped to nodal
injections. Intuitively, this can be understood as how a change of NP within a BZ is allocated
to individual generators in the entire system [4]. The mathematical expression of the mapping
between PTDFN and PTDFZ is thus:
PTDFZ = PTDFNGSK
(36)
The formulation of GSKs therefore greatly influences the quality and dynamics of FB calcula-
tions (and subsequent market results) as it embeds some heavy assumptions into the PTDFZ
on how flow distributes. Since the ’true’ mapping depends on the merit order of the market
clearing, GSKs are predictive estimations that TSOs must select in advance. Consequently,
different GSK strategies exist and the choice of GSK depends on the TSOs expectation of which
generators will act as marginal producers and adjust their output to cover the last increment
of NP change within a BZ [4]. From a market perspective, this is because only price responsive
generators will respond to price changes in a BZ and thus participate in a NP change.

using PTDFZ is reasonable. Therefore, one may only want to focus on the CNEs and not all
line elements when trying to explain the influence that the NP adjustments in the MC have on
the flows. Finally, the simplicity of performing FB calculations on a subset of CNEs increases
the transparency of the results for all market participants by making it easier to understand
and forecast BZ prices and dispatch. So, when selecting CNEs, TSOs must find a balance in
the trade-off between market eﬀiciency and security.

CGM NPs, the greater the flow distribution assumptions of the MC deviate from those of the
CGM, leading to less realistic market outcomes.

moved further away from the initial set point that FB linearizes around. Figure 46 confirms

11.2 (i)
Subconclusion: Base Case vs. CGMA Pipeline Analysis
Table 20 and Table 21 highlighted, that even within the CGMA pipeline and Base Case pipeline,
the choice of GSK strategy plays a role in terms of both economics and security. This arises
from the mathematical differences in how the resulting PTDFZ distributes the flows and im-
pacts the CNE selection. Consequently, the choice of GSK strategy plays a significant role in
how CGMA errors affects the FBMC outcome. Since GSKs determines the FBMC’s assump-
tions of how NP changes leads to changes in flow, their impact becomes more pronounced when
the D-1 MC substantially alters NPs. The analysis shows that FBME is higher when using
the Pmax Sub compared to the Flat strategy. This makes sense since Pmax Sub assumes that
the marginal producer is either a hard coal or a gas power plant. Thus, such assumption will
not hold for large NP changes between the D-2 CGM and D-1 MC (e.g., 2000 MW). In such
cases, the change in generation is not limited to the marginal producer but affects multiple
generators, leading to larger discrepancies between the CGM and FBMC. So, when CGMA
errors are large, and the D-1 MC substantially alters NPs, the Pmax Sub strategy may lead to
a larger the mismatch between the CGM and FBMC compared to the Flat strategy, which can
be measured as the PTDF approximation part of the FBME.
Selecting more CNEs makes the FBMC topology more closely resemble the CGM, which im-
proves the security of the market solution.
This is favored from a security point of view.
However, as mentioned previously, there is a value of simplicity in performing FB calculations
on a subset of CNEs as it increases the transparency of the results for all market participants
by making it easier to understand and forecast BZ prices and dispatch.
Overall, the findings support the conclusion that when the CGMA errors are low, the DA
market outcome is more favorable in the CGMA pipeline than in the Base Case. However,
from a security perspective, CGMA integration creates a bigger mismatch between the flow
distribution and congestion in the D-2 CGM and the D-1 MC, compared to the Base Case.

The analysis has also shown that assessing the impact of CGMA integration on the system can
be very complex. The integration of a CGMA introduces errors through its predictions that
do not exist in the Base Case pipeline. However, as shown in Figure 45 and Figure 46, the D-1
MC can, in some cases, mitigate the propagation of CGMA errors. When RAM constraints
allow it, the D-1 MC can find a solution identical, or at least very similar, to the original
optimal solution, by altering the NPs accordingly. This means that a high CGMA prediction
error does not necessarily lead to an economically worse market outcome. Instead, it depends
on the flexibility that the D-2 CGM provides the D-1 MC through the RAM constraints. Yet,
the more the D-1 MC adjusts the NPs, the further the solution is moved away from the initial
set point that FB linearizes around. In a congested system, this increases the risk of finding
solutions in the D-1 MC that are economically suboptimal or infeasible in terms of security.
When the D-1 MC is unable to adjust the NPs, the CGMA error propagates throughout the
entire pipeline. Consequently, the CGMA predictions heavily influence the final market out-
come since the market must optimize within the constraints of the predicted CGMA NPs. In
such cases, the market outcome may still be feasible and secure within the CGM, since the
NPs in the D-2 CGM, D-1 MC, and D-1 CGM remain identical. However, because the pre-
dicted CGMA NPs are inherently economically suboptimal, the final market outcome is also
suboptimal. Accordingly, a large CGMA error under these circumstances can have quite a big
influence on the final market outcome. So, when NPD−2 CGM = NPD−1 MC, a large CGMA
error may lead to a market outcome that deviates further from the economic optimum, with
greater errors resulting in increasingly ineﬀicient solutions. When NPD−2 CGM ̸= NPD−1 MC, a
large CGMA error increases the likelihood of finding a market solution which is insecure within
the CGM.
Ultimately, the results indicate that improving the CGMA may give better conditions for the
market to find a security-wise feasible solution with a high economic value, though the impact
of CGMA errors depends on multiple factors. In an attempt to provide a more general picture
of the value added by improving the CGMA, the next section is dedicated to quantifying the
exact impact that the current CGMA has on the FBMEs in the system.

11.4 (ii)
Using Shapley Values to Study the Isolated Topology Error
Having isolated the topology error in the perfect CGMA, the aim is now to explore the under-
lying causes of this error in more detail. Inspired by the explainability analyses in Part I, the
dynamics within the test system can be studied using Shapley values. Practically, this can be
achieved by developing an XGBoost model for each GSK that utilizes D-2 data to predict the
absolute mean FBMEs in the two perfect CGMA scenarios. Shapley values can then be used
to identify the D-2 features that have the greatest impact on FBME. Appendix H presents the
predictions of the XGBoost models, demonstrating that both models predict absolute mean
FBMEs with high accuracy, reinforcing confidence in the corresponding Shapley values. Figure
49 illustrates the top 10 most important features for explaining FBME for the perfect CGMA
with Flat GSK. The corresponding plot for Pmax Sub can be found in Appendix H, showing
similar dynamics.
By analyzing the Shapley values in Figure 49, we identify a key dynamic contributing to topol-
ogy errors and, consequently, FBME. The most influential feature is Zone 2 (Renewables),
which represents the capacity factor for Zone 2 in a given hour. A high capacity factor (i.e.,
high renewable generation) leads to increased FBME according to the Shapley value. Referring
to Figure 34, Zone 2 is predominantly composed of PV plants. As a result of the high PV
generation in Zone 2, the NP for the conventional Zone 1 is low, due to the import of cheap
PV power from Zone 2. However, in the D-2 CGM, this import flow causes congestion on
transmission lines in the area of Zone 1 near Zone 2. To mitigate this, the model dispatches
the hard coal generator at node 83, which is more expensive than the lignite generator at node
35.
In contrast, the D-1 MC does not capture this congestion effectively due to fewer CNE lines
(see Figure 38a). As a result, it dispatches the cheaper lignite generator at node 35 instead of
the hard coal generator at node 83, failing to anticipate the resulting congestion and overloads.
Consequently, the topology error arises from the D-1 MC being less constrained and thereby
able to redispatch especially generators at node 5, 83, 35, 34, 59, and 68, to find a more ’opti-
mal’ solution under the same NPs. Referring back to Figure 41, the observed flow differences
between the D-2 CGM and the D-1 CGM now make a lot of sense, as they occur precisely
around these nodes. Despite having equal NPs in both models (npD−2 CGM
t,z
= npD−1 MC
t,z

The Perfect CGMA case study demonstrates that significant improvements in the CGMA NP

Conclusion of Part II
Part II of this thesis analyzes the impact of CGMA prediction errors on the FBMC process.
By comparing a Base Case and a CGMA pipeline, it has been shown how CGMA prediction
errors propagate through the process pipeline, influencing market outcomes both in terms of
economic value and security. Additionally, sensitivity analyses and a case study of a ”perfect
CGMA” have quantified the value of improving the CGMA predictions in terms of overall sys-
tem performance.
One of the fundamental differences of implementing a CGMA compared to the purely nodal
OPF-based Base Case pipeline is that the D-2 CGM is already from the beginning guided to-
ward a zonal solution for the subsequent FBMC by fixing NPs based on CGMA predictions.
Theoretically, this approach provides value since the optimal nodal solution in the D-2 CGM
is not necessarily the optimal zonal D-1 MC solution. However, this benefit comes at the cost
of introducing CGMA prediction errors, which do not exist in the Base Case pipeline. These
errors propagate throughout the market clearing process, influencing both the economic and
security-related outcomes.
As shown in the analysis, the D-1 MC’s ability to adjust NPs may mitigate this propagation of
CGMA errors. When the optimization domain of the FBMC is suﬀiciently large, the D-1 MC
has flexibility in optimizing NPs. This can effectively correct for CGMA errors, by allowing the
D-1 MC to converge back to the original optimal solution, or at least a very similar solution.
However, when RAM constraints limit this flexibility, the CGMA errors persists and propagates
throughout the entire pipeline. In such cases, the market must optimize within the constraints
of the predicted CGMA NPs, resulting in economically suboptimal outcomes. Specifically, when
NPD−2 CGM = NPD−1 MC, a large CGMA error may lead to a market outcome that deviates
further from the economic optimum, with greater errors resulting in increasingly ineﬀicient
solutions. When NPD−2 CGM ̸= NPD−1 MC, a large CGMA error increases the likelihood of
finding a market solution which is insecure within the CGM. Ultimately, the results show that
improving CGMA predictions enhances the conditions for the FBMC to find both economically
eﬀicient and security-feasible solutions.
The choice of GSK strategy (Flat vs. Pmax Sub) also plays a significant role in how CGMA af-
fects the FBMC outcome. Since GSKs determines the FBMC’s assumptions of how NP changes
leads to changes in flow, their impact becomes more pronounced when the D-1 MC substan-
tially alters NPs. The analysis shows that FBME is higher when using the Pmax Sub compared
to the Flat strategy. This makes sense since Pmax Sub assumes that the marginal producer
is either a hard coal or a gas power plant. Thus, such assumption will not hold for large NP
changes between the D-2 CGM and D-1 MC (e.g., 2000 MW). In such cases, the change in
generation is not limited to the marginal producer but affects multiple generators, leading to
larger discrepancies between the CGM and FBMC. So, when CGMA errors are large, and the
D-1 MC substantially alters NPs, the Pmax Sub strategy may lead to a larger the mismatch
between the CGM and FBMC compared to the Flat strategy, which can be measured as the
PTDF approximation part of the FBME.
From an economical point of view, the implementation of CGMA slightly reduces electricity
prices but does not consistently lower system costs. When CGMA errors are low, the system
costs in the CGMA pipeline are generally lower than in the Base Case. However, when errors
are high and the D-1 MC is constrained, the CGMA pipeline can lead to a less optimal market
outcome with higher system costs.
Security-wise, the integration of a CGMA introduces a greater mismatch between flow distri-
butions in the D-2 CGM and the D-1 MC. This often happens because the CGMA prediction
99
==PAGE:110==
error leads to ineﬀicient D-2 CGM dispatches that the D-1 MC wants to improve by altering
the NPs. Consequently, CGMA errors impacts the PTDF approximation part of the FBME,
resulting in increased FBME and overload occurrences. The sensitivity analysis showed that
a 1 MW increase in CGMA NP prediction error could lead to an average increase of 0.0192
MW in FBME per CNE in the worst-case scenario. This highlights the importance of accurate
CGMA predictions in maintaining system security.
The case study of the Perfect CGMA highlighted that eliminating prediction errors, decreases
the FBME with approximately one third compared to the normal CGMA pipeline. Addition-
ally, the Perfect CGMA lead to a reduction in curtailment of RE, leading to higher integration
of renewable generation into the electricity mix. The Shapley value analysis further indicated
that topology errors mainly arise from the D-1 MC being less constrained and thereby able to
redispatch. Especially generators at node 5, 83, 35, 34, 59, and 68, are redispatched to find
a more ’optimal’ solution under the same NPs, causing a flow discrepancy between the CGM
and FBMC.
Conclusively, while the integration of a CGMA may benefit the final market outcomes, its
effectiveness is highly dependent on prediction accuracy. Improving the CGMA does not only
improves economic outcomes but also strengthens system security by decreasing FBME.

Synthesis and Discussion of Findings in Part I and
Part II
Part II has identified key considerations for designing a new CGMA at Nordic RCC. This section
integrates these insights with findings from Part I on developing an ML model to predict CB
flows or NPs and explores potential paths forward.
13.1
ML or Mathematical Model as CGMA
As illustrated in Figure 2, the CGMA plays a central role in the harmonization of the IGMs
of the four Nordic TSOs. The analysis in Part I showed that both NPs and CB flows can be
effectively predicted using ML as a replacement for the current CGMA algorithm. However,
an interesting insight from the Base Case pipeline developed in Part II is that alignment of
the IGMs of Nordic TSOs could also be achieved by computing NPs or CB flows through a
mathematical model, similar to the D-2 CGM. This suggests that an ML-based approach, as
explored in Part I, is not the only viable path: mathematical modeling presents an alternative
method for generating CGMA output.
A mathematical model offers several advantages over ML. It provides transparency, as all vari-
able values can be extracted, and ensures compliance with constraints such as the requirement
for FBMC zones’ NPs to sum to zero. Furthermore, mathematical models avoid the high-
frequency regression challenges seen in ML, where predicting rapid shifts, especially on HVDC
lines, proved diﬀicult.
However, mathematical models also present challenges. They are highly sensitive to input data
quality, particularly when modeling the Nordic power system as a DC-OPF, where accurate
generation cost data is essential but often unavailable. While the Base Case pipeline assumed
perfect foresight for capacity factors and load, real-world data comes with significant uncer-
tainties. Furthermore, the Nordic power system is heavily influenced by external dynamics
from the Baltic, Great Britain, and Central European grids, raising the question of geographic
scope, should the model extend to all SDAC (Single Day-ahead Coupling) members within the
Euphemia algorithm or only a subset?
We argue that ML is a more robust approach for predicting NPs or CB flows, particularly
in relation to the input data given. With an Ensemble model, Nordic RCC can continuously
refine predictions by integrating new and improved ML models. While a mathematical model
may yield highly accurate predictions in some cases, it is also more susceptible to large errors
due to its heavy dependence on specific input data.
These large errors, as noted in Section 11.2 (i) , can have two effects. Either the NPs pre-
dicted by the CGMA are not adjustable throughout the pipeline, leading to ineﬀicient market
outcomes, or they are corrected later in the process, causing the FB linearization setpoint to
deviate significantly from the actual solution. Therefore, minimizing extreme prediction errors
is crucial, an area where the Ensemble model in Part I excelled. This highlights the value of
the Ensemble model, which notably outperformed the current CGMA at Nordic RCC in terms
of error metrics such as MSE and PSE.
13.2
Predicting CB flows or NPs in the CGMA
While Part I demonstrated that, relative to their scale, NP predictions generally have lower er-
rors due to their more aggregated nature and smoother functions, CB predictions provide more
granular information, which can significantly impact subsequent processes. A key question in
101
==PAGE:112==
designing an improved CGMA at Nordic RCC is whether it is more beneficial to predict NPs
or CB flows.
Afterall, it depends on how the predictions are used in the D-2 CGM. If NP predictions are
fixed, the D-2 CGM becomes quite flexible in optimizing the corresponding dispatch since mul-
tiple CB flow combinations can satisfy a given NP solution. In contrast, fixing CB flows imposes
stricter constraints on the D-2 CGM, effectively guiding it toward a more market-consistent
outcome by reducing the domain of feasible flow solutions. However, this approach assumes
that CB flow predictions are suﬀiciently accurate to be worth adhering to.
This trade-off defines the choice between predicting NPs or CB flows. NP predictions offer
higher relative accuracy but have a weaker influence on the subsequent pipeline. Conversely,
CB flow predictions impose stronger constraints on subsequent processes, ensuring greater
alignment with CGMA predictions. However, since CB flows are more challenging to predict,
their relative accuracy is lower compared to NP predictions. Nonetheless, given that the role
of CGMA is to improve the alignment of IGMs across Nordic TSOs, it would be desirable to
use CB flow predictions instead of NPs, provided that prediction accuracy is not excessively
compromised.
Another argument for using CB flows instead of NP predictions in the CGMA is to minimize
localized optimizations within subsets of the overall system. When optimization occurs solely
within the Danish grid, for example, the solution may be optimal for Denmark but not nec-
essarily for the Nordic or European context.
By fixing CB flows instead of NPs, we limit
optimization at the national level more, helping to maintain a Nordic-optimal solution, or even
a European-optimal solution, since the predictions are trained on CB flows resulting from the
Euphemia market coupling.
In summary, while predicting CB flows is a more complex task and leads to greater predic-
tion errors relative to NP predictions, its value lies in better aligning subsequent processes and
promoting a European-optimal solution rather than a set of national-optimal solutions.
13.3
Future Work
While the findings in this study provide significant insights into the feasibility of an ML ap-
proach to the CGMA at Nordic RCC, the analysis is not exhaustive. Several areas warrant
further investigation to refine a new CGMA and to better understand its effects in the following
processes.
13.3 (i)
General improvements
Naturally, one could explore other and more advanced ML models, such as graph neural net-
works, LSTMs, or transformer-based models. Similarly, there is potential to enhance the ML
models by identifying better input data, both in terms of dataset size and quality.
However, the above points can be seen as incremental improvements. From a more theoretical
perspective, it would be particularly interesting to evaluate how a mathematical model would
perform if it replaced the current CGMA at Nordic RCC, as discussed in Section 13.1, rather
than adopting an ML-based approach as the new methodology.
Another key theoretical consideration is whether to fix CB flows or NPs, as discussed in Sec-
tion 13.2. A valuable insight would be to quantify the trade-offs between CB flow and NP
predictions: is the reduced prediction accuracy from fixing CB flows compared to fixing NPs
outweighed by the benefits of having less optimization in the D-2 CGM and more aligned results
throughout the pipeline?

13.3 (ii)
Using the PyPSA-Eur Model as Test System for Part II
As previously mentioned, due to confidentiality constraints, it was not possible to test the
Ensemble model developed in Part I on the actual Nordic RCC processes. Instead, the next
best approach was to develop the Base Case and CGMA pipeline using the test system from
[57] to analyze dynamics of the CGMA. However, a key limitation of this approach is that some
of the system dynamics in the test system may not accurately reflect those of the Nordic power
system. For instances, although topology and PTDF approximation errors also occur in the
real world operations, the specific underlying causes can be diﬀicult to compare between the
test system and the actual Nordic system due to differences in topology.
Therefore, testing CGMA prediction dynamics on a system that more closely resembles the
Nordic power system could provide significant value. Due to time constraints, this was not
feasible within this project, but future research could explore testing these dynamics on the
open-source PyPSA-Eur model [31]. Using a system based on the European power network
would offer additional benefits, as it would better capture interregional interactions, allowing
for an analysis of whether the effects of CGMA differ across the Nordic BZs. This would enable
testing the ML models developed in Part I on a pipeline similar to the one in Part II but using
the Nordic power system as the test system, thereby integrating Part I and II within the same
framework.
13.3 (iii)
Predicting FBME to improve RAM calculations
The Shapley analysis of the topology error in Part II demonstrated that the FBMEs were
predictable using D-2 data.
This suggests that it may be possible to anticipate the errors
introduced in the FBMC process before the RAMs are calculated. This allows one to incorporate
FBMEs in the RAM calculations, effectively functioning as a dynamic FRM that adjusts up or
down, rather than being fixed at a constant 5% of the line capacity. By doing so, Nordic RCC
would be able to recognize the errors made in the FBMC by:
1. providing the market more capacity on a CNE in cases where the FBMC are expected to
overestimate the flow
2. reducing the capacity on a CNE given to the market when the FBMC are expected to
underestimates the flow.
In doing so, this change in RAM calculations would enhance both market eﬀiciency and the
security of market outcomes.
Assuming that FBMEs are also predictable in the real-world operational pipeline of the Nordic
RCC, incorporating them into RAM calculations could offer a simple yet highly effective im-
provement to the current FB capacity calculations.

Final Conclusion
Throughout this thesis, it has been explored how the Common Grid Model Alignment (CGMA)
process at Nordic RCC can be improved by utilizing machine learning (ML) techniques (Part
I). Furthermore, the impact of CGMA prediction errors on the market eﬀiciency and system
security of the subsequent Flow-Based Market Coupling (FBMC) process has been analyzed
(Part II). Together, these insights contribute to providing a holistic perspective on designing a
future CGMA methodology at Nordic RCC.
Part I of the thesis focused on developing ML models to improve the CGMA predictions of
Nordic cross-border (CB) flows and bidding zone net positions (BZ NPs).
A ML pipeline
including feature engineering, feature reduction, hyper parameter tuning, and ensemble learn-
ing, significantly improved prediction accuracy compared to the existing CGMA methodology.
The Ensemble model, combining XGBoost regression, a neural network (NN) model with cus-
tomized loss function, and XGBoost classification models, outperformed individual models in
predicting CB flows and BZ NPs. The results highlighted that BZ NPs are inherently easier to
predict using ML, whereas CB flows exhibit high-frequency variations and stepwise behavior,
making them more challenging to predict. Yet, summing up CB flow predictions from the ML
flow models still resulted in better NP predictions than the current CGMA at Nordic RCC
produces. Thus, regardless of the type of prediction problem, the ML models improved the
CGMA results.
The EU AI Act emphasizes transparency in AI-based decision-making - especially in processes
related to critical infrastructure. To ensure explainability, Shapley values were computed and
analyzed to enhance the transparency and trustworthiness of the ML-based predictions and
show how future regulatory and operational expectations may be met by Nordic RCC.
Part II of this thesis analyzed the impact of CGMA prediction errors on the FBMC process.
By comparing a Base Case and a CGMA pipeline, it was shown how CGMA prediction errors
propagate through the process pipeline, influencing market outcomes both in terms of economic
value and security. Additionally, sensitivity analyses and a case study of the ”perfect CGMA”
have quantified the value of improving the CGMA predictions in terms of overall system per-
formance. The analyses in Part II showed, that when the optimization domain of the FBMC
is suﬀiciently large, the FBMC can flexibly optimize NPs. This may effectively correct for
CGMA errors by allowing the optimization to converge back to the original optimal solution
that the CGMA initially tried to predict. Thus, the ability of the FBMC to adjust NPs de-
termines whether propagation of CGMA errors are mitigated or persist. Yet, the more NPs
are changed in the FBMC, the bigger the Flow-Based Model Errors (FBMEs) in the system
become. Thus, in cases where the FBMC is able to flexibly optimize NPs, a large CGMA error
increases the likelihood of finding a market solution which is insecure within the subsequent
CGM. Oppositely, when the FBMC is very constrained, a large CGMA error may lead to a
market outcome that deviates further from the economic optimum, with greater errors result-
ing in increasingly ineﬀicient FBMC solutions. The sensitivity analysis showed that a 1 MW
increase in CGMA NP prediction error could lead to an average increase of 0.0192 MW in
FBME per CNE in the worst-case scenario. This highlights the importance of accurate CGMA
predictions in maintaining system security. The case study of the perfect CGMA highlighted
that eliminating prediction errors, decreases the FBME with approximately one third, reduces
overload occurrences, and increases utilization of renewable energy, compared to the normal
CGMA pipeline. Ultimately, the results show that improving CGMA predictions enhances the
conditions for the FBMC to find both economically eﬀicient and security-feasible solutions.
Combining the findings from both parts of the thesis forms a holistic perspective on designing
a future CGMA methodology at Nordic RCC. While NP predictions are generally more accu-
104
==PAGE:115==
rate, they have a weaker influence on the subsequent processes. CB flows are more challenging
to predict but provide greater granularity, ensuring better alignment in subsequent processes
and with the rest of the European power system. Thus, the choice between the two depends
on the trade-off between prediction accuracy (NP) and better alignment between subsequent
processes (CB). By leveraging ML models while addressing the trade-offs between CB flow and
NP predictions, Nordic RCC can develop a more accurate and robust CGMA methodology,
enhancing the overall eﬀiciency of the Nordic power system.
