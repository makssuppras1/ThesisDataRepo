Abstract 
In automobile-oriented cities, surface public transport systems can appear unattractive and 
ineffective due to traffic congestion, particularly during peak hours. Some innovations of public 
transport as well as alternative traffic modes have been proposed and developed in order to 
relatively reduce both the influence and dependence of automobile-oriented, such as intelligent 
transport system, bus rapid transit and light rail transit. In this study, regarding mobility 
assessment for transport infrastructure expansion, the light rail transit system in Copenhagen is 
evaluated. Total travel time is regarded as a significant criterion of urban mobility, the length of 
total travel time not only affects urban mobility, but also determines passengers’ travel behavior. 
This project will be evaluated to clarify effects/impacts of light rail and how travelers’ total 
travel time would be changed (shortened or lengthened) once the light rail service is available. 
Two scenarios (baseline and light rail scenario) will be created in order to highlight the traffic 
conditions before and after light rail system. In particular, bus service 300S and 330E are 
selected as the reference scenario to compare with the light rail service in the light rail scenario, 
since their bus corridors are very similar to the light rail corridor. After that, the analysis how the 
light rail impacts the mobility is rolled out. It encompasses and details the variations of three 
time components (walking time, waiting time and in-vehicle time) as well as total travel time by 
analyzing the effects of light rail, finalizing the impacts of the light rail on urban mobility. 
Furthermore, the comparison on accessibility level is developed, in order to analyze the impacts 
of light rail on urban accessibility.  
Due to the fact that the Copenhagen light rail is still under construction at the moment, the data 
used in this study are predictions from a transport simulation model implemented in MATSim. 
The final results indicate that 37.72% trips can save time as they contain light rail compared with 
62.28% light rail users spending more travel time, although in-vehicle time of light rail is shorter. 
There are some possible external reasons leading to this happened, such as long walking time to 
light rail stations, long waiting time when transforming another traffic mode. As a result, it is 
better to combine other kinds of public transport for light rail system.

1 Introduction 
 
1.1 Background 
Recent decades, the increasing urbanization will be a huge challenge to cities all over the world, 
and the demand for new innovation solutions within urban mobility is growing [1]. On the one 
hand, with the increase of automobiles, traffic conditions have to confront more and more 
congestions gradually; on the other hand, it is undeniable that the increasing urbanization would 
produce a series of environmental problems, such as greenhouse gas emissions and air pollution, 
resulting from the increased number of automobiles. Hence, the performance of automobile-
dominated cities have largely aroused people’s concerns and interests in the field of public 
transport, such as in transportation planning and management of urban regions [2]. It appears 
necessary to promote alternative modes of travel that contrasts automobile-dominated urban 
travel markets [Taaffe, Gauthier, & O’Kelly, 1996]. 
 
In fact, public transport system is thought to be an acceptable alternative of automobile in terms 
of reducing traffic congestions, accidents as well as improving air and life quality, so it should be 
developed and encouraged. Besides, public transport system is able to accommodate more travel 
demands at each travel. In order to enable public transport system to become a practical and 
attractive alternative to individual mobility, it has to be efficient enough, such as in frequency, 
travel speed, reliability, etc. For example, some transport innovations have also been researched 
and then come to use with little or no reduction on service quality, such as travel time [3]. 
 
Since the growth of traffic flows is likely to influence the degree of traffic congestions, new 
transport infrastructure is designed and expected to address the predicted levels of increased 
travel demand. Moreover, the new transport infrastructure can be a reinforced part of creating a 
productive transport network, connecting the existing infrastructures, in order to largely ensure 
urban mobility smooth. It is expected that a light rail system would be a potential and appealing 
option. According to the empirical experiences in some cities which are already equipped with a 
light rail system, the performances are prominent, such as in Bergen, Norway, the passenger 
ridership was 50% higher than expectation when the first 10km section was come to operation in 
June 2010 [4]. Light rail system indeed plays a positive role in public transport conditions.

Conversely, some experts in the U.S. pointed out that the data of light rail system could not 
detect any reductions in traffic speed or travel time on the same corridor [5], which implies that 
light rail system is not conducive to relieving car traffics. 
 
As a result, it is proved that not every city is suitable for developing light rail system in urban 
development. It depends on urban planning, public transport network and residents’ travel 
patterns, etc.     
 
1.2 Problem Statement 
Light rail systems have been conducted in many cities all over the world, but the possible effects 
brought in can be either positive or negative. In fact, the first light rail system in Greater 
Copenhagen is under construction currently and planned to open in 2025. Since the light rail 
system is expected to become an efficient solution to transport system, it is worth investigating 
and analysing its prospective effects so that it is available to obtain a better understanding of 
relevant impacts on urban mobility. Due to the fact that the Copenhagen light rail is still under 
construction at the moment, so all the dataset used and analyses made in this study derive from 
the prediction of MATSim1 and its output. In addition, this study will also adopt the PTAL2 
(Public Transport Accessibility Level) model with which so far no analysis has been done for 
Copenhagen to analyze the urban accessibility level based on MATSim prediction and its output.  
 
1.3 Aim 
This study aims to make a contribution to analyse urban mobility, proposing the effects of 
transport infrastructure expansion (the Greater Copenhagen light rail system) and assess its 
entailing urban mobility. Specifically, it presents a framework containing two scenarios (baseline 
scenario and the light rail scenario) to study urban mobility on the basis of MATSim prediction 
and its output, such as the possible changes to travel time by using different traffic modes. As a 
result, the following workflow is related to this aspect: 
-- Baseline scenario (current urban mobility analysis): urban mobility can be defined as the 
movement of people or freight in urban areas [6] and it involves transport system and service.

1.4 MATSim and relevant dataset introduction 
As stated in the problem statement, all the datasets involved and analyses made in this study are 
derived from MATSim and its output. Due to this, it is necessary to understand the background 
and algorithm of MATSim before rolling out corresponding analyses. 
 
The Multi-Agent Transportation Simulation Toolbox (MATSim) was started around 2006 with 
the goal of generating traffic and congestion patterns by following individual synthetic travellers 
through their daily or weekly activity programme [7]. It is composed of several simulation tools 
to model travel demands, traffic flows and mobility, etc. It is an agent-based fast dynamic traffic 
system designed to deal with large-scale agents and transport network, including both private and 
public transport [8]. Simply, MATSim starts with an initial travel demand, the simulation is an 
iterative process between travel activity re-scheduling and traffic assignment [9], in which re-
3 
 
==PAGE:10==
 
scheduling means schedules need to be modified and updated towards the more optimal ones. 
This algorithm is called Genetic Algorithm. At the beginning of a new iteration, the new 
schedule is generated by choosing two existing schedules as “parents”, randomly selecting 
activities from them, and changing start time and durations of the activities [9]. To use this 
algorithm, MATSim is able to keep not only one schedule for each agent, but a set of possible 
schedules generated out of the original agent database. However, this study will neither develop 
the theory about MATSim deeply nor interpret to deal with the MATSim model, but it bases on 
the outputs of MATSim to roll out relevant researches and analyses.

encompass 159,765 agents (residents) with 583,588 trips. The total number of agents and trips 
are considered as the 10% representatives. The real number of agents and trips would be 10

modelled trip, representing 10 trips. The following tables (Table 1.1 – Table 1.9) are the

1.5 Disposition 
This thesis consists of 8 chapters and they can be generally divided into 5 parts: introduction, 
method, theory, analyses and results, and final conclusion. 
 
The second part (Chapter 2) is a literature review of the themes analysed and methods adopted 
in this study (mobility analysis, accessibility, light rail system and PTAL). 
 
The third part (Chapter 3) describes the general methodology showing the structure and 
workflow, as well as how the datasets of MATSim are used in this study. 
 
The fourth part (Chapter 4 to Chapter 7) covers a description of analyses and their results. 
Chapter 4 focuses on current mobility introduction and analysis, chapter 5 is about accessibility 
analysis, particularly public transport accessibility level. Chapter 4 and 5 constitute the 
knowledge of current mobility analysis. Chapter 6 proposes the introduction of light rail system, 
not only analysing the light rail scenario, but also comparing with two bus lines (300S & 330E), 
while chapter 7 concludes the results of two scenarios and studies the impacts of light rail system 
on urban mobility and accessibility. 
 
The final part (Chapter 8) is about the conclusion of the mobility assessment of Copenhagen 
light rail system.   
 
1.6 Limitation 
This thesis aims to study and assess the effects on mobility due to transport infrastructure 
expansion, and thus the construction of light rail system in Copenhagen is selected as a case 
study.  
 
In the mobility analysis, the dataset is merely theoretical since the light rail system in 
Copenhagen is still under construction, so all the dataset come from MATSim prediction and its 
output. Additionally, although MATSim is used as a transport simulator for analyzing transport 
conditions all over the world, “the uncertainty of it is a well-known and inevitable problem by 
using stochasticity/simulation for choice set generation, leading to producing the additional 
8 
 
==PAGE:15==
 
variability in the output” [10]. Because of these, the results of analyses in the study may not 
reflect the tangible urban mobility (for example: the number of residents using light rail service, 
in Chapter 6) and travelers behavior to some extent.  
 
In addition, based on the impacts on mobility, there are substantial elements (internal or external) 
and environment that may more or less change mobility as well, probably leading to the distinct 
effects and results. However, this thesis would mainly concentrate on the performance of 
residents’ total travel time which is considered as an important measure of mobility. Apart from 
that, the analyses are also based on a macro-level so that they do not allow to get into the details 
of each public transport line.

2 Literature review 
 
2.1 Mobility research 
Mobility is a broad definition. In the context of transport planning, it has been defined as the 
potential for movement, the ability to get from one place to another [11], and the capacity of 
moving or of being moved readily from place to place [American Heritage Dictionary 2000]. As 
a matter of fact, in 1885, E.G. Ravenstein Havenstein highlighted, for the first time, important 
patterns and regularities of population movements which can be regarded as the prototype of 
mobility [12]. 
 
2.2 Background of urban mobility 
In the US, the Texas A&M Transportation Institute’s (TTI) 2010 Urban mobility Report reported 
that in the 498 urban areas studied, there were approximately 56 billion passenger miles of travel 
on public transportation systems in 2011 [13]. What if these amounts of residents did not 
commute by public transport? It would have produced a huge number of traffic flows on the 
road, even traffic congestion and delay, and so on.  
 
However, as the continuity of civilization and modernization, more and more vehicles have 
appeared all over the world, particularly in urban area, which would cause a series of acute 
consequences. Traffic condition and environment would be the first two predictable 
consequences arising probably, such as traffic congestion and accidents, global warming, climate 
changing, etc. As a result, in order to build up a sustainable city, the target of urban transport 
planning should be focused on shortening journey distances, but also minimizing dependence of 
private motor vehicles. Admittedly, public transport is encouraged to substitute automobile. For 
instance, in many cities in Europe, the U.S. and even developing counties, the introduction of 
new type of traffic mode, such as light rail transit and bus rapid transit, as well as promotion of 
bicycle are in progress [14]. The main intention is not only to improve urban public transport, but 
also provide public transport service as an alternative mode to the vehicles.

2.3 Mobility in Copenhagen 
Copenhagen prioritises mobility, and has a target that 75% of all movement will be on foot, by 
bike or by public transport [15]. In fact, if Copenhagen is going to meet this target, it needs to 
increase the portions of public transport and bicycle use, and pedestrians. To facilitate this, the 
City of Copenhagen is putting considerable efforts into providing an integrated, efficient and 
green transport network [15], such as the new metro line and light rail project. The Metro City 
will open in 2019 and more than 100,000 extra passengers are expected to use public transport in 
Copenhagen every day [15], while the Greater Copenhagen Light Rail is planned to open in 
2025, “around 13-14 million passengers are expected to use the Greater Copenhagen Light Rail 
on an annual basis” [16]. 
Apart from the expansion and construction of traffic network, the city of Copenhagen is also 
working closely with the traffic system, including optimising traffic flow and adopting services 
for public transport users [15]. 
 
2.4 Light rail systems 
1) Light rail systems in Europe 
Although light rail systems was firstly introduced and began to operate in North American in 
1978 [17], Europe not only has completed more light rail systems all over the world, but also the 
light rail system in many European cities has shown it is an effective and successful tool to 
change the urban mobility from automobile-dominated cities. For example, in Bergen, Norway, 
it introduced a completely new system of light rail after facing major challengers related to 
accessibility, congestion and environment pollution [18], to prompt a higher use of public 
transport in the dense city downtown. In Graz and Innsbruck in Austria and Olomouc in the 
Czech Republic, the achievements of light rail system turn out that it is able to provide an 
efficient and environmental friendly means of public transport as an attractive alternative to 
private cars [19]. In fact, Figure 2.1 [20] tells us that the length of light rail in Europe is 
overwhelmingly higher than other countries and regions within two decades, but also climbs up 
to 12000 km from 9000 km in 2016.

2) Effects of light rail system (positive and negative existing) 
Several studies have explained a series of benefits of light rail, arising from lower transportation 
costs, changing development patterns, higher property values, and reducing air pollution [21]. 
Specifically, still in Bergen, thanks to the existing of light rail system, the daily travel behaviour 
of residents, who live along the line, have changed significantly, and thus boost the rate of transit 
use in the areas where light rail system is available [22]. There was relevant information proving 
that public transport use had increased both in volume and in market share since the opening of 
the light rail system in Bergen in 2010 [4]. Likewise, in Montpellier, France, it exemplified how 
the volume and vehicle speed were changed after the light rail system in 2000, coupled with 
traffic congestion, travel time, air pollution, noise and accident cost reduced to some extent [19]. 
 
However, light rail system indeed has positive advantages in public transport, but it does not 
mean that light rail system suits every city confronting traffic problems. As Todd Litman (2015) 
pointed out, it is hard to claim that traffic congestions would be severe without light rail system 
as traffic congestions tend to increase with city size or inhabitant growth [23]. The Urban

Mobility Report in 2002 stated that traffic congestions in American cities both with and without 
light rail transit has steadily increased since the 1980s [24]. In St. Louis and Portland, they have 
suffered from increasing traffic congestion, even though both of them were equipped with light 
rail system [25]. Likewise, Shefali Ranganathan (2016) in Washington Policy Centre, although 
investing a large amount of money for light rail, it has nothing to do with reducing travel times 
and improving people’s mobility. But that if we do nothing, traffic jams will still doubt. 
 
2.5 Accessibility 
Accessibility refers to the ease of reaching desired goods, services, activities and destinations, 
which together are called opportunities for interaction and exchange [26]. For example, grocery 
store provide access to food, libraries and the Internet provide access to information and 
knowledge. Consequently, the quality of accessibility directly or indirectly influences people and 
their communications. In transportation field, the notion of accessibility is a significant link 
between transport planning and land use. According to one theory, accessibility is the ultimate 
goal of most transportation activity, and transport planning should be based on accessibility [26], 
so it can be considered that accessibility has become a critical element in evaluating the 
efficiency of transport system. Besides, an accessible transport system is also essential to ensure 
equal opportunities for all people in society [27].  
 
However, it is a given that there are many factors affect accessibility, such as transport demand, 
mobility, user information, integration (the degree of integration among transport system links 
and modes), affordability (the cost to users relative to their incomes), land use factors, transport 
network connectivity, prioritization. Because of these existing various accessibility factors, they 
derive corresponding evaluation approaches to measure the effects or impacts of accessibility 
from different aspects. For instance, in 2006, El-Geneidy and Levinson used detailed data on 
land use, travel behaviour, population demographics and such information to evaluate 
accessibility in a particular situation for the Access to Destination study. It aimed at measuring 
accessibility for certain activities and locations so as to compare accessibility for different 
groups. Besides, there are some specific examples of accessibility evaluation developed and 
applied in different countries: 
13 
 
==PAGE:20==
 
Access to Destinations: It developed tools and datasets to quantify overall accessibility, taking 
into account multiple modes and land use development patterns. 
Access Scores: It used GIS mapping tools to measure people’s mobility demands, their ability to 
access work and common non-work activities, indicating how specific transportation system 
changes will affect accessibility.  
Accessibility Evaluation Tools: it linked people and places, developing tools for measuring urban 
accessibility for various demographic groups, trip purposes, modes, times and costs. 
Measuring Transit Accessibility in Ahmedabad: It showed public transport accessibility levels 
(PTAL) taking into account average walk speed, distances to transit stops and transit service 
frequencies. Besides, PTAL demonstrates that such tools can function in developing as well as 
developed countries [26]. 
 
In conclusion, various approaches, applications and tools can be used to measure accessibility, 
but they vary broadly in scope depending on the perspectives, research purposes and input, 
including modes, impacts and geographic scale. 
 
2.6 Public Transport Accessibility Level (PTAL) 
For this study, it will apply Public Transport Accessibility Level (PTAL) to the accessibility 
analysis, measuring locations’ public transport network accessibility. In fact, the methodology of 
Public Transport Accessibility Level was developed in 1992 by the London Borough of 
Hammersmith and Fulham, taking into account walk access time and service availability 
(Transport for London, April 2010). Later on, the PTAL model has been thoroughly reviewed 
and tested, and has been agreed by the London Borough-led PTAL development group as the 
most appropriate for use across London [28]. 
 
PTAL model measures the accessibility level for a specific given point (origin place) by 
considering the public transport service (public transport frequency) for all available transport 
modes and the accessibility index (distance, walking time, schedule waiting time, etc.) to that 
point. Simply, PTAL mode takes into account the relevant information about entering each 
public transport access point rather than exiting. 
 
14 
 
==PAGE:21==
 
As a result, PTAL model reflects [28]: 
1) Walking time from the point-of-interest to the public transport access points; 
2) The reliability of the service modes available; 
3) The number of services available within the catchment; and 
4) The level of service at the public transport access points – i.e. average waiting time 
 
However, it does not consider [28]: 
1) The speed or utility of  accessible services; 
2) Crowding, including the ability to board services; or, 
3) Ease of interchange. 
 
So far, PTAL model was also applied to Ahmedabad, India, in 2014, in order to: 1) improve 
existing public transport systems by recognizing areas with accessibility; 2) formulate parking 
policies; 3) integrate land use zoning with public transport access [29]. Besides, PTAL was used 
as a fundamental for different research purposes. For example, Wu and Hine have mapped PTAL 
against the deprivation index for Belfast in Northern Ireland, finding the deprived wards were 
located in the “very poor/poor” PTAL zones [30]. By using a very detailed time based walking 
network [31], PTAL assessment was used for Christchurch, New Zealand. Finally, there are 
similar methods to PTAL model, such as the Greater Manchester Accessibility Level (GMAL). It 
considers “crow flies” distance to Service Access Points [29].  
 
In conclusion, PTAL is one of the well-known and widely used measures all over the world. Its 
details and calculation processes are specifically developed in Chapter 3 (methodology chapter).

relevant calculations to walking distance between a point of interest (household) and the nearest

Methodology of PTAL 
The methodology of PTAL is briefly described below. It is mainly broken into 3 crucial steps: 
Step 1: Define the stations that can be reached from the location of each household. 
Step 2: Calculate the accessibility index of PTALs model for each household (point of interest).   
It is mainly transformed by the walking time and waiting time. 
1) Accessibility index is mainly transformed by the access time, so the distance, which is 
the fundamental of access time, between all households and all stations must be 
calculated. As stated above, this study is deployed based on the outputs of MATSim, 
while the walking distance of MATSim is calculated by applying the Euclidean distance 
multiply 1.3, where the calculation formula in two dimensions is evolved as: 
 
                        D (p, q) = ඥ(𝑝1 −𝑞1)2 + (𝑝2 −𝑞2)2 ∗1.3                     (1) 
 
On top of that, this formula is applied to measure the distance between each POI and all 
SAPs over this study, selecting the stations that can be connected to the chosen POI.  
 
According to the PTALs principle and its application in London, the maximum distance 
from one POI to SAP is 640 metres for bus and 960 metres for rail [28], but these two 
criteria have to be adapted by multiplying 1.3 to this case study in order to adopt the 
outputs of MATSim, keeping data coherent. As a result, the maximum distance between 
one POI and SAP is increased to 832 metres for bus and 1248 metres for rail. Finally, 
any SAPs (bus and rail stations) beyond these two criteria of distances will be deleted 
for that particular POI. 
 
Similarly, in order to be consistent with the outputs of MATSim as well, the walking 
speed estimated at 72 m/min (1.2 m/s) in MATSim is adopted in this study instead of 80 
m/min used for the PTAL application in London, where formula is: 
                        
                            Walking time = distance / walking speed                               (2) 
 
19 
 
==PAGE:26==
 
At the end of this process, a matrix of the distance from each household to all matched 
SAPs within the hypothetical areas of 832 metres and 1248 metres with corresponding 
walking time is obtained.   
 
2) Identify valid routes (lines) at each SAP and calculate average waiting time. In reality, 
operation routes vary in different time periods. For this study, the specific time period is 
set at the morning peak, particularly from 08:00 to 09:00. Besides, for each POI, one 
route is only considered once. Where a route takes place twice or even more, since it 
serves more SAPs within a hypothetical area of one POI, thus the SAP that is nearest to 
the POI is selected. 
 
When it comes to the average waiting time. It is defined as the period from when a 
passenger arrives at an SAP to the arrival of the desire service (bus or train) [28]. In the 
calculation, the average waiting time is estimated as half of the interval between 
services plus the “reliability factor” K. This reliability factor K relates to the reliability 
of public transport service, which may be derived from observed survey data at any time 
of a day. If services are operating to schedule then k = 1 [32]. However, in the absence 
of services schedule data from MATSim, this reliability factor has to agree with the 
application in London, k = 2 mins for bus and k = 0.75 mins for rail respectively. The 
formula 3 is: 
                           Schedule Waiting Time = 0.5 * (60 / Frequency) + k                   (3) 
 
, where service frequency is the summation of how many buses/trains arrive at one SAP 
per hour. Because the specific time period is set at the morning peak hour (08:00 – 
09:00), so frequency here represents the number of buses/trains arriving at a particular 
SAP over this time period. 
 
3) Calculate the total access time for each valid route at each SAP by adding walking time 
and schedule waiting time. 
 
Total Access Time = Walking time + Schedule Waiting Time                        (4) 
20 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
4) Convert total access time into equivalent doorstep frequency (EDF). This is done by 30 
divided by total access time. The reason of conversion is that EDF can be comparable to 
bus/train service frequency, but taking into account the additional walking time taken to 
reach the station along with reliability [33].  
 
Equivalent Doorstep Frequency = 30 / Total Accessibility Time                       (5) 
 
5) Finalize accessibility index for each POI by aggregating all EDF. In this process, the 
route with the highest frequency is assigned the weight factor of 1, while other routes 
are assigned the weight factor of 0.5. As for one out of four transport modes (there are 
four transport modes estimated, bus, train, metro and light rail), the accessibility index is 
calculated as: 
 
                    Accessibility Index one transport mode = 1 * EDF max + 0.5 * ∑ EDF all other routes      (6) 
 
               Finally, the accessibility index of one POI is calculated as follow, where the       
accessibility index only considers public transport service (bus, train, metro and light rail): 
 
                                  Accessibility Index POI = ∑ Accessibility Index public transport mode                   (7) 
 
Step 3: Map PTAL, visualizing the values of PTAL on a map of Copenhagen by ArcGis.

Mobility analysis is an important and helpful tool to understand current traffic conditions and

4.1 Methods 
In fact, the Legs dataset contains information for each trip. It not only tells residents’ origin 
places and destinations, but also their specific starting time and end time, plus traffic mode 
preference. As a result, it is possible to work on the Legs dataset in order to comprehend 
mobility condition before the construction of light rail system. Besides, the analysis of public 
transport movements is focused on the morning between 08:00 am and 10:00 am. The analysis of 
car movements is based on the same time period. 
The principles behind these analyses are: 
1) The frequency shows where the public transport service is more/less frequent and where 
is attractive; 
2) The capacity shows where “public transport offer” [12] is higher and where is lower; 
3) The PT movements show how most of public transport services move; 
4) The movements of cars show the origin places and destinations of car users, in order to 
investigate the main reasons why they prefer using cars. 
 
The Point Density tool is mainly applied to illustrate the current mobility conditions in this 
chapter. It calculates the density of point features around each output cell. Conceptually, a 
24

neighbourhood is defined around each cell centre, and the number of points that fall within the 
neighbourhood is totalled and divided by the area of the neighbourhood [35]. 
 
As for frequency and capacity of public transport, both of them are derived from the 
DepartureLoadings dataset describing the specific departures of public transport within a day. In 
particular, PT frequency comes from the number of PT arrivals at every corresponding stop and 
aggregating all of them to each stop, while PT capacity is to indicate the level of public transport 
offer. According to the attribute of Point Density tool, one item’s value determines of the number 
of times to count the point. For example, an item with a value of 3 would cause the point to be 
counted as three times [35]. It means that the value of one StopId depends on the number of PT

Finally, the output values are gained by searching points in the defined search radius, and 
aggregating all searched points before dividing by the area of defined search radius. 
 
Since the Legs dataset is composed of details of every agent’s trips, it is used to study the public 
transport movements as well as the origin places and destinations of car users via sorting out and 
grouping by their FromStopId and ToStopId. Similarly, the search radius is set as 300 metres 
with output cell size of 50.

5 Accessibility analysis 
 
Apart from the mobility analysis made in previous chapter, the aim of this part is to illustrate the 
accessibility performance in the catchment area based on the location of each household that is 
regarded as point of interest (POI). As a result, the Public Transport Accessibility Level (PTAL) 
is applied to show the accessibility level as it is a detailed and accurate measure of the 
accessibility of a point to the public transport network, taking into account walk access time and 
service availability [28]. In addition, the outputs of MATSim that this study bases on are largely 
applicable for the steps of calculating PTAL. 
 
5.1 Introduction 
The choice of PTALs model is because of several reasons,  
1) It was developed in 1992, and then it has been not only thoroughly reviewed and tested, 
but also agreed by the London Borough-led PTAL development group as the most 
appropriate for use across London [28]. 
2) It displays the distribution of relevant density of the public transport network. 
3) It considers the access time that is composed of walking time and waiting time. 
4) The number of services available within the catchment [28]. 
5) The output of MATSim can be matched the processes of PTAL calculation, furthermore 
its results are relatively easy to interpret.  
 
The following steps are to roughly show how the result of PTAL for each POI is defined and 
obtained, and the specific workflows have been detailed in Chapter 3. 
Step 1: Define the stations that can be reached from the location of each household. The 
point here focuses on the distance that people are willing to walk between their house (point 
of interest, POI) and the public transport stations (service access points, SAP), since PTAL 
model takes into account the walking time between these entities. 
 
      Step 2: Calculate the accessibility index of PTALs model for each household (point of 
interest).   It is mainly transformed by the walking time and waiting time.

whether using public transport; the other is the PT service. Both of these two factors affect each 
other as well. It can be presumed that the more proximity to public transport, the higher 
likelihood of using public transport. Simultaneously, this likelihood is closely related to PT 
service that appeals to residents, such as its frequency, reliability, travel time and safety.

have shown that more number of people will move to these cities in the near future. It calls for a 
transport system along and between these fingers. The proposed light rail addresses the situation 
by running across all the five fingers outside Copenhagen and linking a number of lines in the

After the selection of segment corridor of bus & light rail service, the passenger density (average 
and maximum passenger density) between two consecutive stops (stop-to-stop) would be 
specifically calculated, in order to analyse how the public transport use changes, and which 
sequence of stops is the most crowded part of the corridor.  
 
As a matter of fact, passenger’s density of public transport contains two aspects, one is the 
passenger density in public transport and the other is the density of passenger waiting at a station 
[34]. However, this section will just concentrate on the passenger density of selected segment 
corridor, showing a more specific and detailed results. The following workflow is used: 
 
1) Dividing a day into 6 periods, emphasizing morning peak and evening peak hours, so 
00:00 – 06:00, 06:00 – 10:00 (morning peak), 10:00 – 13:00, 13:00 – 16:00, 16:00 – 
20:00 (evening peak) and 20:00 – 23:59; 
2) Sorting out DepartureLoadings dataset, aggregating the number of bus line (300S & 
330E) departures and light rail departures according to 6 time periods, which is based on 
their specific departure time; 
3) Multiplying the bus/LRT capacity with the total number of bus/LRT departures obtained 
from Step 2; 
4) Aggregating entire carried passengers for bus line 300S & 330E and LRT service over 
segment corridor; 
5) Dividing Step 4 by Step 3, and then the final result is the average passenger density.  
6) Sorting out the highest number of passenger carried between two consecutive 
stops/stations among all the departures for bus 300S & 330E and LRT service over 
segment corridor; 
7) Dividing Step 6 by bus/LRT capacity, then the final result is the maximum passenger 
density between two consecutive stops.

6.3 Discussion 
In this chapter, it proposes an analysis on the light rail system and its entailing effects. Although 
the light rail project remains under construction in reality, the analyses are developed by 
simulating and predicting residents’ possible travel behaviours after LRT system on MATSim 
and corresponding effects and impacts. On top of that, according to the present real traffic 
network, the corridors of bus 300S & 330E are most likely to be changed or substituted. 
Consequently, the simulation and analyses of LRT scenario are at the premise of the substitution 
of those two bus lines, in order to largely comply with the real traffic condition. 
 
The LRT section gives an overview on market share of every traffic mode firstly, and then 
focuses on its effects and impacts on accessibility level, ending up the comparison with two bus 
lines. However, it does not consider other aspects that probably occur, such as the surface space 
utilization, environmental problems and even transport policy, and so on. 
 
The market share indicates that the growth of light rail use mainly because of the reduction of 
bus use, probably coming from bus 300S & 330E users before. On the other hand, according to 
the PT conditions over segment corridor, light rail service is not only able to attract more 
passengers than both compared bus lines, but also higher frequency and less running time

through segment corridor. Although the spanning distance of segment corridor of bus 300S is 
longer, it needs 22 bus stops to cover, in contrast to 15 stations covering 13,488 metres of light 
rail service. Therefore, fewer stations of light rail service means effectively saving time, such as 
dwell time and running time.  
 
As stated before, although the LRT system is still under construction, by analysing its features 
and effects, the prospect of light rail service is seemingly bright, positively contributing to higher 
frequency with less waiting time, more passengers carried, fewer stations, and so on.

7 Light rail impacts on mobility 
 
In the previous chapter, an introduction of light rail system, including its features and 
comparison with two bus lines, has been developed to evaluate the prospective effects. They 
appear to give positive answers out of the results. In order to evaluate the potential impacts on 
this case study, it is necessary to analyse the consequences on urban mobility and accessibility. 
Admittedly, travel time is regarded as an important measure to evaluate urban mobility. In order 
to make the analyses on urban mobility more realistic and specific, travel time are categorized 
into three time components for agents’ trips, walking time, waiting time and in-vehicle time 
(time spent on public transport service). Therefore, this chapter will mainly encompass agents’ 
travel time, including three time components, and the difference of public transport accessibility 
level (PTAL) to develop.  
 
7.1 Methods 
Travel time is regarded as an important measure to evaluate urban mobility. Its variations before 
and after light rail system are able to reflect the effects of light rail use in this study, concluding 
either negative or positive impacts on urban mobility. Therefore, relevant methods applied in this 
chapter are as follows:  
1) Based on two scenarios (baseline and LRT scenario), sorting out which trips and how 
many trips contain bus line 300S & 330E, and LRT service. 
2) Beginning with the analysis on those who used to be bus line 300S & 330E users and 
have already become LRT users now, then sorting out their time spent on walking, 
waiting and in-vehicle, ending up with aggregating their total travel time. 
3) Processing those currently are LRT users, not shifting from bus line 300S & 330E.  
4) Specifying and illustrating the variations of three time compositions for entire LRT trips, 
then concluding the total travel time based on the time variations. 
5) Analysing trip distribution after trips contain LRT service, concluding how many of them 
take up less time over a trip and how many of them take up more time. 
6) The comparison of PTAL model is based and developed on the accessibility analysis 
obtained from chapter 5 & 6 before and after light rail system.

7.4 Discussion 
In principle, total travel time is regarded as the main aspect to reflect the impacts on urban 
mobility. As a result, this chapter is rolled out by 3 time components (walking time, waiting time 
and on PT time) of travel time to evaluate the effects on urban mobility before and after LRT 
system based on the outputs of MATSim.  
 
First of all, after analysing the proportion of trips containing bus line 300S & 330E or light rail 
service, their performances (Table 7.2) on 3 time components shows that the results of light rail 
service are better. It not only can attract 300S & 330E users, but also save 145,656 seconds in 
total for entire 265 trips. 
 
Apart from trips deriving from bus line 300S & 330E, there are rest of 1127 trips used to be 
made by other traffic mode, now containing LRT service. Based on total 1392 trips containing 
LRT service, the analyses of time variation are developed. They aim at explaining specifically 
how residents’ travel time change after using LRT service by comparing with their travel time 
spent on initial travel mode. As a result, the results show that those who were car and bike users, 
pedestrians have to spend extra time on walking and waiting, in order to use LRT service, mainly 
concentrating on 1000s – 2000s and 0s – 500s respectively. Taking into account the “in-vehicle 
time”, this time component represents the time spent on public transport, and thus the result 
shows over 400 trips that were car trips, now would take up more PT time, which means that 
group of residents have to spend more time on public transport in contrast to car driving over a 
trip. Conversely, as for a certain group of PT users, they are able to save time on public transport 
as they contain LRT service, generally between 0s and 2000s. However, as for those saving more 
than an hour of travel time, it is presumed as the consequence of the stochasticity of MATSim 
prediction, which is thought somewhat unrealistic and unreasonable in real life. In addition, 
because of heterogeneous cases to entire 1392 trips, the result of total travel time shows 
rationally, some trips containing LRT service can be shortened total travel time more or less, 
whereas some are more likely to take up more.  
73 
 
==PAGE:80==
 
Finally, Table 7.4 is about the distribution of LRT trips taking up how much time on 3 time 
components and total travel time. It displays that around 83.62% and 78.59% of LRT trips take 
up more time on walking and waiting, as opposed to they take advantage in “in-vehicle time”, 
more LRT trips can save residents’ time on public transport. However, in realty, it is unrealistic 
to evaluate the effects of mobility only focusing on “in-vehicle time”. The description of 
mobility is to describe one movement in a period over a complete trip, which implies the total 
travel time should contain walking and waiting time as well. Under this circumstance, there are 
about 37.72% LRT trips are capable of obtaining benefits, saving total travel time. In other 
words, light rail service indeed plays a positive role on mobility to that range of trips, and save 
LRT users’ travel time. On the contrary, approximately 62.28% of light rail trips would take up 
more time in total travel time, although the main reasons are not completely related to LRT 
service itself. For instance, as long as initial car users shift to use light rail service, it will take 
their certain time to walk to light rail stations and destinations afterwards, which is reasonable 
and rational. In any case, total travel time is one indispensable and objective factor of mobility 
and also the criterion or measure to evaluate effects on mobility. As a result, concerning travel 
time aspect, light rail service plays a negative role on mobility to those 62.28% LRT trips to 
some extent. Even though LRT system cannot bring in positive effects for most trips in terms of 
travel time consumption, it attracts a range of car users to use more public transport instead of 
their cars, which is beneficial to environment and sustainable city though.  
 
In addition, the analysis of PTAL mode shows an expected and rational result. Firstly, there are 
more POI raising their rate of accessibility level than those of reducing in general. Secondly, 
along LRT corridor, the accessibility level of most POI rise as well, where can deduce that LRT 
system indeed improves the accessibility condition in its corridor areas.

8 Conclusion 
 
This study aims at making a contribution to the studies on public transport efficiency, proposing 
transport infrastructure expansion to the surface pubic transport, further investigating and 
evaluating the effects on urban mobility. However, since the Copenhagen light rail system is still 
under construction at the moment, this study is based on the MATSim prediction and its outputs 
to evaluate the possible impacts on urban mobility after light rail system is available. 
Consequently, the unexpected stochasticity of MATSim prediction may influence the analyses 
and results of this study to some extent, such as comparing the same agents’ (residents) 
individual travel behavior before and after LRT system. Besides, the analyzed agents and those 
obtained results of trips are the 10% representatives of real number of agents and trips. For 
example, each shown trip in the result represents 10 trips.  
 
Analyzing the baseline scenario allows to understand the general traffic condition before the 
light rail is available. In particular, the market share tells that car-using dominates the largest 
proportion (74.10%) and public transport only takes up about 8.75%. Based on the market share, 
the analysis on passenger density of public transport shows the use/crowdedness of it is at a low 
level, implying that public transport is not only capable of accommodating more passengers, but 
also helping plan future transport network connection, etc. From the analyses of public transport 
(frequency and capacity of public transport, PT movements), they reflect that public transport 
system is greater developed in Copenhagen centre areas than other areas, such as offering more 
service frequencies and capacities. Consequently, the movements of public transport highly 
concentrate in centre areas. As for the analyses of origin/destination of cars, they result that car 
movements more concentrate in centre probably due to the high density of school/working areas.  
 
Analyzing the urban accessibility allows to examine the access level of different households to 
public transport. Consequently, PTAL model is applied to analyze the urban accessibility. 
Simply, PTAL model reflects: 1) the walking time and distance from a point of interest to all 
possible public transport access points within a hypothesis area; 2) the reliability and frequency 
of public transport mode; 3) the level of public transport service at that public transport access 
point. The results highlight that in Copenhagen centre, the accessibility level is greater than other 
75 
 
==PAGE:82==
 
areas, and it gradually becomes power as moving away from city centre. This is consistent with 
the results of urban mobility. These results also reinforce that the more the public transport 
system is efficient and easy to access, the more likely to be used. The study of integration 
between PTAL and school/work activity shows that the good accessibility areas cover the 
highest school/work density ones. 
 
The introduction of light rail scenario is to obtain the rough knowledge after the light rail system 
is available, such as passenger density and passengers’ initial waiting time at stations. However, 
the market share tells that car remains accounting for the largest proportion, around 74.02%. 
Although the overall percentage of public transport is nearly as same as before, the rate of bus is 
decreased, as opposed to the growth of light rail with 2.06%. In addition, the analysis on segment 
corridor of both bus line 300S & 330E and light rail system is rolled out. The comparison aims at 
detailing and understanding the differences between them in order to evaluate the effects of light 
rail system on urban mobility. The results of comparison show that light rail system gives better 
answers in terms of service frequency, the number of passengers carried, passengers’ average 
waiting time and in-vehicle time. 
 
In the study of light rail impacts on mobility, it mainly encompasses residents’ travel time, 
including three time components (walking time, waiting time and in-vehicle time), plus the 
difference of PTAL model. The results show that regarding those 265 trips which are finished by 
LRT service from bus service 300S & 330E, their total travel time are reduced by nearly13%. In 
addition, there are 1392 LRT trips in total, in which those were car and bike users, pedestrians 
have to spend extra time on walking and waiting in order to use light rail service. The time 
mainly concentrate on 1000s – 2000s and 0s – 500s respectively. Taking into account the “in-
vehicle” time, over 400 trips used to be car trips, now take up more in-vehicle time, which 
implies a group of residents have to spend more time on public transport in contrast to car 
driving over a trip. However, regarding those saving more than 5000s, it is thought that the result 
of “in-vehicle time” is somewhat unrealistic/unreasonable due to the stochasticity of MATSim 
prediction. When it comes to those PT users, most of them are able to save time on public 
transport as their trips contain LRT service, generally between 0s and 2000s. In general, the 
result turns out that not all users are capable of gaining benefits (saving travel time) after their 
76 
 
==PAGE:83==
 
trips contain LRT service, approximately 37.72% (525 trips) spending less time, as opposed to 
62.28% (867 trips) spend more time than their initial travel mode used. Apart from the analyses 
of light rail impacts on mobility, the result of the difference of PTAL shows that along LRT 
corridor, the accessibility level of most POI increase, which means light rail system improves the 
accessibility condition in its corridor areas. 
 
Finally, for this case study, transport infrastructure expansion of light rail system, it brings in 
positive effects to a certain number of passengers to some extent, shortening their total travel 
time. Residents’ time reduction indicates their mobility is improved by using light rail service, 
which is based on the definition of travel time being the criterion or measure to evaluate effects 
on mobility. Unfortunately, for about two third residents, they have to spend more travel time to 
complete a trip. In a conclusion, even though this study is somewhat theoretical and based on the 
MATSim prediction with several unexpected stochasticity, the results give interesting insights 
into the light rail system. The results help us understand the light rail system indeed play a 
positive role for some residents, especially those living nearby LRT corridor, in terms of urban 
mobility and accessibility.
