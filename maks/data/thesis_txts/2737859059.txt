Abstract
The increasing demand for sustainable energy solutions necessitates the advancement
of technologies like fuel cells, which are integral to energy storage systems. This the­
sis explores the application of machine learning (ML) techniques to predict and mitigate
degradation in fuel cells, specifically Solid Oxide Fuel Cells (SOFCs). By leveraging ML,
the research aims to develop predictive models that can accurately forecast degrada­
tion patterns, optimize maintenance schedules, and enhance the operational efficiency of
SOFCs.
The study involves the development and validation of Gaussian Process Regression
(GPR) models, trained with various operational data to predict short­term voltage drops
and overpotential in SOFCs. Different models are compared, including those trained with
fluctuating operational data and upgraded with epsilon improvements to enhance predic­
tion accuracy. The integration of active learning techniques further refines these models
by iterative updating predictions based on new data, thus improving robustness and re­
liability. Model D was improved into Model D+, both predicting the difference between
voltages in different time steps, but still needed a paradigm shift in order to be able to put
it to use, as different operational conditions in time make it hard to be able to see through
the voltage difference the degradation in fuel cells. Then, the SOFC simulation code was
modified in order to get more data information between time steps and get enough data
information to compare the SOFC at each time step in order to see degradation directly
into voltage drop, so we applied this idea into Model E, even though we need more data
to train this Model in order to validate it. In parallel with this we developed Model F keep­
ing another idea in mind. Using the big amount of data already used in Model D, even
though we had the operational conditions constant change, we targeted overpotential as
the predicted feature, knowing that overpotential is one of the parameters we are inter­
ested in reducing in order to mitigate degradation in fuel cells. This was explored in Model
G even with more detail as we divided the different overpotentials a SOFC experiments
and predicted the ones that are most directly involved with degradation.
Results indicate that the ML models developed can effectively predict degradation, of­
fering a proactive approach to maintenance that extends the lifespan of fuel cells and
reduces operational costs. The findings underscore the potential of integrating ML into
energy management systems, paving the way for more efficient, reliable, and sustainable
energy solutions. This research contributes to the broader goal of achieving an AI­driven
virtual power plant, enhancing the management of energy storage systems, and support­
ing the transition to a more sustainable energy future.

1
Introduction
Machine learning (ML) stands at the forefront of transformative technologies, offering un­
precedented opportunities for innovation and advancement in various fields. One area
where ML demonstrates immense promise is in the realm of sustainable energy solu­
tions, particularly in harnessing the potential of storage energy systems, such as fuel cell
technology, electrolysers or batteries. Machine learning applied to energy storage sys­
tems offers the capability to develop accurate and sophisticated predictive models. These
models can forecast energy demand, optimize energy management, and anticipate usage
patterns, enabling more effective planning and operation of storage systems. Further­
more, machine learning enables the identification of complex patterns in data collected
from storage systems. This can lead to a better understanding of the factors impacting
system performance and degradation, facilitating the implementation of predictive and
preventive maintenance strategies. By harnessing available data and developing accu­
rate predictive models, we can optimize energy management, enhance the integration of
renewable energy sources, and move towards a more sustainable energy future.
1.1
Enegy Storage systems
Various energy storage technologies have been developed over the years, each with its
unique characteristics and applications. Batteries, fuel cells, and electrolyzers are promi­
nent examples of these technologies, each playing a vital role in enabling a more sustain­
able and efficient energy landscape.
Batteries encompass a diverse range of electrochemical devices designed to store and
deliver electrical energy. From lithium­ion batteries commonly found in consumer elec­
tronics to advanced lead­acid batteries used in automotive applications, battery technol­
ogy continues to evolve to meet the demands of various sectors, including transportation,
renewable energy integration, and grid stabilization.
Fuel cells, on the other hand, represent a diverse group of electrochemical devices that
generate electricity through the reaction of fuel and oxidants. These devices operate
across a spectrum of temperatures and employ different types of electrolytes, leading to
a wide array of fuel cell variants tailored for specific applications. For instance, Proton
Exchange Membrane Fuel Cells (PEMFCs) excel in low­temperature operation, making
them ideal for mobile applications such as transportation and portable electronics. In
contrast, Solid Oxide Fuel Cells (SOFCs) operate at high temperatures and are well­suited
for stationary power generation and combined heat and power systems.
Electrolyzers are another critical component of energy storage systems, facilitating the
conversion of electrical energy into chemical energy through the process of electrolysis.
By splitting water into hydrogen and oxygen gases, electrolyzers enable the production of
clean hydrogen fuel for various applications, including fuel cell vehicles, energy storage,
and industrial processes.
Each of these energy storage technologies offers unique advantages and challenges, and
their integration into the energy ecosystem requires careful consideration of factors such
as efficiency, scalability, and environmental impact.

Solid oxide fuel cells degradation
Solid oxide fuel cells (SOFCs) show promise for use in both stationary power plants and
mobile applications, such as large ships, thanks to their high efficiency and adaptability to
various fuels. However, their widespread adoption is hindered by factors like high costs
due to low volume production, mechanical failures leading to crack formation and creep,
and degradation issues limiting their lifespan. Degradation can occur through mechanical
or chemical means, including interconnect oxidation, carbon deposition, nickel particle
coarsening, and poisoning of electrodes by substances like chromium and sulfur.
While degradation in single cells has been extensively studied under controlled conditions,
understanding degradation within a SOFC stack is more complex due to temperature,
flow, and concentration gradients. Analyzing degradation comprehensively is crucial for
improving long­term performance, but predicting degradation accurately is challenging
due to the multiphysics nature of the phenomena.
Various studies have investigated degradation mechanisms, such as chromium poisoning
of the cathode, nickel particle coarsening at the anode, and oxidation of the interconnect.
These studies delve into factors like electrode and electrolyte compositions, current den­
sity, humidity levels, and concentration effects on degradation rates.
Experimental evidence suggests that chromium from the interconnect migrates to the
cathode, affecting its performance, while nickel particle coarsening reduces the active
surface area of the anode. Models have been developed to simulate these degradation
processes, aiding in predicting long­term trends and understanding interactions between
different phenomena.
Developing a multiscale degradation model for full SOFC stacks allows for simulation of
stack lifetimes with reduced computational resources.
In this project we will use a degradation SOFC model originally developed by Omid Babaie
Rizvandi and Tomás Wierzba Farcy[2].

3.1
GPR models
The purpose of introducing Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) is to provide the reader
with an overview of how this model was utilized in the thesis project.[13]
Think of a Gaussian process as a way to describe a distribution over functions. Instead of
dealing with individual functions, we’re looking at the entire space of possible functions.
When we do calculations or make predictions with Gaussian processes, we’re actually
working directly with this space of functions. Moreover, it is frequently possible to modify
the properties of Gaussian processes by adjusting specific parameters. A full predictive
distribution is the format in which predictions are produced by a GP model.
A Gaussian process is a stochastic proccess fully characterized by its mean function and
its covariance function as we can see in Equation 3.1.1. A stochastic process consists of
random variables indexed by a set X, typically representing the input space with dimension
d. In our context, X corresponds to the input space.
f(x) ∼GP(m(x), k(x, x0))
(3.1.1)
The primary benefit of GPs is that they are non­parametric models, which do not con­
dense the training set into a parameter vector with a finite dimension. Semi­parametric
models, like neural networks, occupy an intermediate position, allowing the number of hid­
den units (k) to grow as n does. Results pertaining to universal consistency are possible
in this situation under specific technological parameters and rates of increase on k. This
is crucial because there could be an endless number of possible function shapes when
attempting to fit an unknown non­linear model to a piece of data. Indeed, GPR determines
the likelihood distribution across all permissible functions that satisfy the data.
GPR is a probabilistic regression technique that models the underlying relationship be­
tween input and output variables using a Gaussian process. The posterior mean in GPR
is the output variable’s expected mean value based on the observed data. In figure 3.1
we can see a visual example from an article involved in uncertain prediction for slope
displacement time­series using Gaussian Process Machine Learning [14].

3.3
Adam Optimizer
Adam optimizer is a more advanced form of stochastic gradient descent (SGD)[16], which
is commonly used in deep learning applications such as computer vision and natural lan­
guage processing, is the Adam optimizer, which was first introduced in 2014 and pre­
sented at ICLR 2015. Adam, who got his name from the phrase ”adaptive moment esti­
mation,” uses estimates of the gradient’s first and second moments to modify the learning
rate for each weight in the neural network. It just needs first­order gradients, making it
memory­friendly and efficient. Adam frequently performs better at generalizing than ear­
lier adaptive optimization methods like AdaGrad and RMSP. On the other hand, scientists
have seen situations in which Adam is unable to reach the best answer; in these situa­
tions, returning to SGD produces superior generalization results.[17]
Adam uses the average of the second moments of the gradients to adjust learning rates
rather than RMSP’s average first moment. By adjusting the decay rates of the moving av­
erages using the parameters β1 and β2, this technique computes the exponential moving
averages of gradients and squared gradients. Adam accelerates optimization and adjusts
learning rates to the magnitudes of recent gradients by combining two gradient descent
techniques: Momentum and RMSP.

3.4
Active learning
Active learning (AL) revolutionizes the traditional approach to machine learning by strate­
gically selecting data points for annotation, rather than relying on randomly labeled sam­
ples. This paradigm shift is particularly advantageous in scenarios where labeled data is
scarce or expensive to obtain. By actively querying the most informative data points, AL
accelerates the learning process and improves the efficiency of model training.
AL encompasses a variety of strategies for selecting data points to be labeled. These
strategies leverage different criteria, such as uncertainty estimation, diversity maximiza­
tion, or model disagreement, to identify the most informative samples. Uncertainty sam­
pling, for example, prioritizes data points where the model’s predictions are least confi­
dent, aiming to reduce uncertainty in regions of the input space with sparse or conflicting
information.
Diversity sampling, on the other hand, focuses on selecting samples that cover a wide
range of features or represent different clusters within the data. This approach helps
prevent bias and ensures that the model learns from a diverse set of examples, improving
its generalization performance across various input distributions.
Another popular AL strategy is query synthesis, where the model actively generates syn­
thetic data points for annotation based on its current knowledge. This approach is par­
ticularly useful in domains where collecting real­world data is challenging or expensive,
allowing the model to bootstrap its learning process by generating informative samples.
AL finds applications across various domains, including computer vision, natural language
processing, healthcare, and industrial quality control. In medical imaging, for instance, AL
can assist radiologists in identifying rare anomalies or pathologies by prioritizing challeng­
ing cases for annotation. Similarly, in manufacturing processes, AL can optimize quality
control by selectively sampling defective products for inspection, minimizing downtime
and production costs.
In the context of this thesis, AL plays a crucial role in improving the performance of the
model while minimizing the annotation effort or the number of measurements required.
By actively selecting informative data points from the input space, we aim to build a more
accurate and robust model while mitigating the risks associated with sampling from unsafe
regions.

4.1.1
Inlet temperature
In this investigation, we focus on three key temperatures: fuel temperature (Tfuel), air
temperature (Tair), and cell temperature (Tsolid). To simplify our analysis, we adopt a
single inlet temperature for all three variables. This uniform inlet temperature, denoted as
T0, remains consistent across all simulations and serves as the initial temperature at the
first node of the cell. Our study explores a range of T0 temperatures spanning from 650
to 800 [°C]. This temperature range is chosen deliberately, considering the operational
constraints of solid oxide fuel cells (SOFCs). While temperatures above 800 [°C] are
feasible for SOFC operation, they often lead to rapid oxidation of the metal interconnect
and a subsequent decline in chromium content. Moreover, temperatures below 650 [°C]
result in significant activation overpotentials in the electrodes [7]. Therefore, our analysis
focuses on this temperature range to strike a balance between operational efficiency and
material degradation concerns.
Achieving commercial viability below 650°C for solid oxide fuel cells (SOFCs) remains a
challenge despite significant attempts to decrease the operating temperature of SOFCs.
There is potential for significant cost reductions in operations and maintenance and capital
expenditures (CAPEX) when operating at lower temperatures. These savings result from
decreased balance of plant expenses and lower material prices, respectively. However,
activation overpotential in the cathode and ohmic losses in the electrolyte are the two
main physical processes that present substantial obstacles to temperature reduction.
Maintaining ideal cell performance becomes difficult when the temperature is lowered
because it aggravates the activation overpotential in the cathode and raises the resistance
in the electrolyte. It is extremely difficult to find an electrolyte that is stable beyond 650°C
and has a high oxygen ion conductivity below that temperature range. Because of their
relative stability and compatibility within the required temperature range, zirconia­based
electrolytes—typically yttria­stabilized zirconia (YSZ)—are used in the majority of SOFCs
even though they do not have the highest ionic conductivity [20].

4.1.3
Current density
Selecting the right current density is essential for SOFC performance while running in
galvanostatic mode, where a constant average current density is maintained throughout
time and the voltage adjusts accordingly. DTU Energy’s Frandsen[22] et al. conducted a
study wherein the performance of SOFCs was examined at three distinct average current
densities: 0.15, 0.25, and 0.35 A/cm². The results showed that when current density
increased, there was a noticeable increase in cell voltage drop.
Using an average current density of 0.5 A/cm², another study[7] investigated the advan­
tages of reversible SOFC operation. It is suggested that average current densities greater
than 1 A/cm² are unlikely to result in optimal SOFC operation. The ohmic overpotentials
are still above 240 mV at this point, suggesting losses above 20% in relation to the open­
circuit voltage (OCV), which is normally between 0.9 and 1 V.
As a result, 1 A/cm² is the maximum current density examined in this investigation. On
the other hand, because of the low power output per cell (low power density), a minimum
average current density of 0.1 A/cm² is taken into consideration. It would not be feasible
to fall below this number.
4.1.4
H2 inlet composition
The inlet molar fraction of hydrogen in a solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) is a critical factor
affecting its performance and operation. Hydrogen is the primary fuel used in hydrogen
fuel cells, and its molar fraction in the inlet stream can vary based on several factors,
including design requirements, operating conditions, and available hydrogen sources.
The hydrogen molar fraction in the inlet of SOFCs can vary significantly depending on the
hydrogen source used. If the hydrogen source is a gaseous fuel such as natural gas or
syngas, the hydrogen molar fraction in the inlet may be relatively high, although it will also
contain other components such as methane, carbon monoxide, and carbon dioxide.
The inlet molar fraction of steam can take almost any physically possible value as its value
is selected from a range going from 0.0001 to 0.9999.
4.1.5
O2 inlet composition
The molar composition of oxygen in a solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) inlet plays a significant
role in determining its performance and efficiency. The oxygen partial pressure, which is
influenced by the molar composition of oxygen in the inlet, directly affects key parameters
such as the open­circuit voltage (OCV) and cathode overpotential.
In addition to influencing the OCV and cathode overpotential, the molar composition of
oxygen also impacts the cathode concentration overpotential. This is because the con­
centration of oxygen at the cathode significantly affects the electrochemical reactions oc­
curring within the fuel cell.
In the context of SOFC operation, oxygen concentrations below 21% (molar­wise) are not
typically considered, as this is the molar fraction of oxygen in atmospheric air. Therefore,
the range of oxygen inlet compositions considered in this study typically starts from 0.21
and extends up to 0.9999, representing nearly pure oxygen environments.

4.1.9
Initial conditions
The simulation time spans from one to two days (24­48 hours), with a time step ranging
from one to two hours. Initially, the current degradation state of the cell is established
by randomly selecting initial conditions from a pool of 15 thousand long­term simulations
provided by Thomas thesis[2]. This ensures that the chosen initial conditions are phys­
ically realistic. These initial conditions consist of three structural parameters of the cell,
which vary spatially across the nodes, as we can see in Figure A.2.
The first parameter is the area­specific resistance of the interconnect (ASR), represent­
ing the current oxidation state of the interconnect. The second parameter is the radius of
the Nickel particles in the anode, indicating the current level of degradation in the anode,
particularly related to the anode activation overpotential. Lastly, the length of the cath­
ode’s triple­phase boundary represents the degree of degradation of the cathode, directly
influencing the cathode activation overpotential.
After randomly selecting these degradation parameters from the long­term simulations,
the short­term simulations are conducted. This process is repeated 15 thousand times to
account for various initial conditions and simulate a wide range of scenarios.
Finally from this simulation we get the data to train the different predictive models.

From Thomas’ thesis [2], various degradation models were analyzed, including those
focusing on long­term degradation outcomes such as cell lifetime in hours or total energy
produced in kWh. However, the model that inspired this thesis was specifically developed
to address the rapid short­term prediction problem to assist real­time operation of the
SOFC.
The GPR model is trained on short­term transient data, which involves fluctuating operat­
ing variables over time. In this approach, each simulation is assigned a time step between
one and two hours, resulting in 24 time steps per simulation. Short­term simulations have
a uniform time step frequency (∆t [hr]), unlike long­term simulations where the step fre­
quency may vary. Short­term simulations are less likely to encounter high­degradation
situations compared to long­term simulations due to their shorter duration. Instead, the
likelihood of reaching high deterioration levels in short­term simulations depends more on
the randomly chosen starting degradation condition than on the specific time step used.

increases, the ohmic overpotential decreases due to the reduced voltage drop across
the cell components. Also, higher cell voltages can facilitate more favorable reaction

concentration overpotentials), thereby contributing to a reduction in total overpotential.

electrochemical reactions at the cathode and anode. When Jav is increased, the rate of
electrochemical reactions at the electrodes also increases. This can lead to higher acti­
vation overpotential because the reactions need to overcome greater kinetic barriers to
sustain the higher current density. Also, we have other overpotentials as concentration
overpotential which is influenced by the availability of reactants (e.g., oxygen and fuel) at
the electrode surfaces and when Jav is increased, there can be limitations in the trans­
port of reactants to the electrode surfaces, resulting in higher concentration overpotential
as the demand for reactants exceeds their supply or ohmnic overpotential, that as cur­
rent density increases, these resistive losses also increase, contributing to higher overall
overpotential.

lies in understanding and managing the degradation processes affecting the anode side.
Nickel particle coarsening is identified as the primary degradation process, wherein the
Ostwald ripening mechanism reduces the length of the triple phase boundary (TPB) and
electronic conductivity of the anode electrode. However, the YSZ backbone plays a crucial
role in inhibiting nickel coarsening by stabilizing and preventing excessive agglomeration
of nickel particles. By comprehending and predicting these degradation mechanisms,
effective maintenance strategies can be devised to prolong the lifespan of SOFCs and
optimize their performance. Model G’s predictive capabilities enable anticipation of an­
ode activation losses over time, thereby facilitating proactive maintenance planning and
operational efficiency enhancement in practical applications.
On the other hand, developing a predictive model for cathode activation losses in SOFCs
holds significant importance for understanding and mitigating degradation processes af­
fecting the cathode side. One prominent degradation mechanism is Cr poisoning, wherein
chromium traces from air or fuel flows react to form solid Cr2O3, blocking reaction sites
in the triple phase boundary (TPB) of the cathode. The reduction in TPB length over time
due to Cr poisoning is described by a differential equation, with the rate of CrO2(OH)2 re­
duction being heavily influenced by cathode activation overpotentials and temperatures.
Finally, it is also important to develop a predictive model for overpotential losses in the
interconnect. Developing a predictive model for interconnect degradation is crucial. In­
terconnects play a vital role in SOFCs but are prone to oxidation over time, leading to
increased resistance and performance decline. Predicting this degradation is essential
as it allows for proactive maintenance planning and optimization. By anticipating degra­
dation, interventions can be scheduled effectively, minimizing downtime and maximizing
system lifespan. Additionally, optimization strategies can be devised to mitigate degrada­
tion effects, ensuring long­term reliability and efficiency of solid oxide fuel cells.

5
Optimization of Solid Oxide Fuel Cells
for enhanced performance
Our main goal in this section is to optimize essential operating parameters for solid oxide
fuel cells (SOFCs) in order to minimize overpotential losses, a significant factor influencing
the performance and efficiency of these devices.
In order to develop this, we will work with Model F:Training GPR with fluctuating operation
short­term data and total overpotential target value seen in section 4.5.
Optimizing the operational and design variables of SOFCs is crucial for minimizing these
losses and improving their overall efficiency. By doing so, not only can profitability and
sustainability of the technology be increased, but also practical implementation across
various industrial and commercial sectors can be facilitated. [27]
This model­based optimization approach holds significant relevance in industrial applica­
tions as it provides a systematic methodology for identifying optimal operating conditions
of SOFCs, enabling companies to maximize energy efficiency and reduce associated op­
erational costs. Ultimately, this research has the potential to drive widespread adoption
of solid oxide fuel cells as a clean and efficient energy source in a variety of industrial and
commercial applications.[28]
5.1
Optimization Method
The optimization strategy is based on the genetic algorithm (GA), a heuristic search tech­
nique motivated by genetics and natural selection[29]. The GA functions by keeping track
of a population of ”individuals,” or potential solutions, each of which could represent a dif­
ferent set of input parameters for the SOFC system. These parameters, which include
variables like temperature, pressure, and reactant concentrations, are stored as a list of
floating­point values as we have seen in section 4.1.
The genetic algorithm iteratively evolves this population of individuals over a series of
generations[29]. At each generation, individuals are evaluated for their fitness, which is
determined by how well their corresponding set of parameters performs according to the
predictive model[30]. In this case, the fitness is inversely proportional to the overpotential
losses predicted by the model, as the objective is to minimize these losses.
During the evolution process, individuals are selected based on their fitness to serve as
parents for generating the next generation[29]. This selection process, along with ge­
netic operators such as crossover and mutation, introduces variation into the population,
allowing for exploration of the solution space and adaptation to different environmental
conditions.
The optimization process continues for a specified number of generations, after which the
algorithm converges towards a set of optimal input parameters that minimize the overpo­
tential losses and maximize the efficiency of the SOFC system[30]. The best individual
found at the end of the optimization represents the optimized set of parameters, which
can be used to improve the performance of real­world SOFC applications.

5.2
Optimization Process
The optimization process begins with configuring the genetic algorithm (GA) to suit our
specific problem of minimizing overpotential losses in a solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) [31].
The genetic algorithm operates on a population of candidate solutions, evolving them over
successive generations to find the optimal set of input parameters [32].
The first step is defining the fitness function. The fitness function is the core of the GA,
guiding the selection of the best candidates. For this problem, the fitness function evalu­
ates the predicted overpotential losses using the trained predictive model. The objective
is to minimize these losses, so the fitness function is defined to return the predicted loss
as the fitness value.
Then, the next step is setting up the genetic algorithm components. First of all we have
individuals and population. Each individual in the population represents a potential solu­
tion, encoded as a list of parameter values. The population is initialized with a set of these
individuals.
The GA uses crossover (mating) and mutation to generate new individuals [33]. These op­
erators introduce variation, allowing the algorithm to explore the solution space. Crossover
mixes two parent solutions to create new ones while mutation makes random small changes
to individual solutions to keep them diverse.
Then a selection is done in order to find the best individuals based on their fitness to form
the next generation. A tournament selection method is used, where several individuals
are randomly chosen, and the best among them is selected.
Once the GA components are set up [32], the algorithm is executed. First of all the popu­
lation is initialized with a specified number of individuals, each representing a potential set
of SOFC input parameters. The algorithm evolves the population over a defined number
of generations. In each generation each individual is evaluated using the fitness function,
the best individuals are selected to form the next generation and genetic operators are
applied to produce new individuals.
After the final generation, the best individual, in this case the one with the minimum pre­
dicted overpotential loss, is selected. This individual represents the optimal set of input
parameters for minimizing the SOFC’s overpotential losses.
The results include the optimized input parameters and the corresponding minimized over­
potential loss. These results are compared to the original values to demonstrate the im­
provement achieved through the optimization process as we can see in table 5.2.

6.1
Short­term model D and D+­prediction of voltage drop
Model D was the first step of this project and its results were found by Thomas in his
project [2]. This model seems to performs poorly and yet has room for improvement is
the Model D. Model D only represents a part of the cell voltage, notwithstanding the low
quality of the voltage change predictions. As a result, model D can be used to some
extent, but its outcomes need to be carefully considered.
In order to reduce the uncertainty shown in Model D Figure 4.12 we created Model D+
where we analyzed the reason of this high uncertainty and try to reduce it applying some
strategies.
Even though the results for prediction in Model D and Model D+ are not bad, we have
seen that when it involves target data values near 0, in this case voltage drop of the cell
close to 0V values, this gives some problems to the model uncertainty.
If we take a look at how this voltage drop of the cell is defined, considering that this is
the target value and the main idea of this model is to predict degradation in SOFC for
assisting real­time operation we can detect some misconception.
Model D, and therfore Model D+, are trained to predict in each step the voltage drop we
will have. But this voltage drop is defined as the difference between voltage of the cell
in the current step and the voltage of the cell in the previous step. This would make
sense in terms of degradation as long as the operational conditions remained the same,
as you can see in Model C in Thomas Thesis[2], but in this case, voltage of the cell is
constantly changing sharply due to changing operational conditions, so this makes this
voltage drop value just a difference between voltage cell in different operational conditions
and is difficult to interpret from here the degradation.
Therefore this leads us to explore new prediction models where we can train the model
to predict target values where to interpret the degradation.
6.2
Short­term model E­prediction of real voltage drop
Model E was developed to build upon the established ideas in Model D but from a different
perspective. The primary goal of Model D was to predict the voltage drop to assess
degradation in terms of voltage in the SOFC. However, a significant challenge in Model D
was the continuous change in operational conditions at each timestep of the simulation.
While this approach was useful for understanding the impact of short­term operational
changes on degradation, it hindered direct comparison of voltage differences between
timesteps. Consequently, it became difficult to clearly discern how degradation affected
voltage since both the voltage and operational conditions varied at each timestep.
To address this issue, we proposed a solution involving minor modifications to the 1D
SOFC model to extract more information. Specifically, we introduced a new simulation
point where operational conditions remain unchanged after an hour of operation. This
modification allows us to observe the impact of degradation on voltage drop more directly.
In this modified approach, we maintain the same operational conditions from the first hour
to the second hour. This enables a direct comparison of voltage differences within a stable
Machine learning for fuel cell degradation

==PAGE:90==
operational context, providing a clearer understanding of degradation effects. From the
third hour to the fourth hour, the operational conditions change again, and so on.
This structured approach in Model E allows us to isolate the effects of degradation on volt­
age drop without the confounding influence of changing operational conditions, thereby
offering a more precise analysis of SOFC degradation over time.
While Model E offers a refined approach to understanding the impact of degradation on
SOFC voltage drop, it still faces significant challenges. One of the main issues is the
need for more data to train the model effectively and improve its predictive accuracy.
Insufficient data can hinder the model’s ability to generalize and make reliable predictions,
thus impacting the overall performance of the optimization process.
Additionally, as observed in Figure 4.20, the voltage drop of an SOFC after an hour of
operation is relatively small. The model must therefore work with very small values, which
can introduce further complications. These small changes in voltage can be difficult to
predict accurately and may be susceptible to noise and other minor variations in the data.
This sensitivity can affect the reliability of the model’s predictions and the subsequent
optimization outcomes.
So, knowing this, some future work done with this model could include the following ideas:
• Data Augmentation: Increase the amount of training data by incorporating data from
additional experiments or simulations. This can help the model learn better patterns
and improve its accuracy.
• Improved Data Collection: Enhance the precision and frequency of data collection
during experiments to capture more detailed information about the SOFC’s perfor­
mance.
• Advanced Modeling Techniques: Employ more sophisticated modeling techniques,
such as ensemble methods or deep learning, to handle small voltage changes and
reduce the impact of noise.
• Regularization Methods: Implement regularization methods to prevent overfitting
and improve the model’s generalization capabilities.
• Validation and Testing: Conduct thorough validation and testing of the model with
independent datasets to ensure its robustness and reliability.

degradation. Analyzing Section 2.2, we decided to split the overpotential model into the
three most significant overpotentials in terms of degradation: anode activation losses,
cathode activation losses, and interconnect losses. We followed the same strategy for
three cases, as the data distribution for the three overpotential losses was logarithmic.
The training for the models followed the same process as seen in the other models, but
we encountered some unstable behavior in the anode activation losses, which could be

resources.

losses in the interconnect model, where the training strategy should be re­evaluated to
find better methods for training and achieving more accurate results.
For future work, the main goal would be to improve the oxidation losses in the interconnect
model and select the data more accurately to ensure meaningful results. By refining
these models and training processes, we can achieve better performance and reliability
in predicting degradation­related overpotentials.
