Abstract
This work included the study of corrosion mechanism in electronic devices under humid and gases
exposure. The experiments focused on electrochemical migration (ECM), together with the effects
of pitch distances and applied voltage. The experiments were carried out using three gaseous envi-
ronments: H2S, SO2, and CO2. Additionally, tests in normal atmospheric conditions (i.e., with-
out gas exposure) were included as reference. Real-time leakage current was measured alongside
in-situ droplet imaging for selected samples. After the test, surface characterization was done in-
cluding; optical microscope (OM), scanning electron microscope (SEM), X-ray diffraction (XRD),
and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). The results showed that the pitch distance strongly
influence the time to failure, with smaller distances leading to faster droplet coalescence and ear-
lier dendrite formation. Additionally, the experiments underH2S showed that this gas significantly
enhances corrosion, and promotes the formation of copper sulfide (CuS) when reacting directly
with copper. On the other hand, SO2 showed a limited impact under the experimental test condi-
tions used in this work. Experiments under CO2 atmosphere modified the wettability properties
of the surface, delaying droplet coalescence. However, once droplets merged, the corrosion rate
was increased due to lower pH and higher conductivity of the electrolyte. The in-situ images of the
droplet evolution accurately predicted the start of the transient region (where droplets coalescence
initiates electrochemical migration), validating it as an important tool for predicting critical times
for failures.

Introduction
Electronic devices form part of our daily life, and are widely used across every industry. Over the
years, there has been a higher demand for making devices smaller, like in the case of smartphones
for example. However, this miniaturization involves not only external changes but also internal
modifications. Electronic devices rely on printed circuits boards (PCB) through which electrical
connections enable their functionality. In order to reduce the size of PCBs, it has been necessary
to increase the packing density, meaning that the components on a PCB are closer to each other.
Depending on their application, electronic devices may be exposed to different environmental con-
ditions. One of the most important issues found in electronics in related to humidity. When devices
are exposed to conditions where temperature and humidity fluctuate, the risk of failure increases.
This is because when the surface of PCB interacts with moisture in the air, water molecules are
adsorbed into the surface forming water monolayers. While monolayers alone may not be harmful,
problems arise when the critical conditions, specifically humidity, dew point and surface temper-
ature promote condensation to occur. Condensation is a transient event, typically observed during
day/night shifts, which create the critical temperature and humidity conditions for water to con-
dense.
Condensed water initially forms as droplets, which can merge depending on their size and shape,
and eventually forming a continuous water layer. This water layer can connect two biased metals,
creating a conductive path. Higher packing density increases the risk of failures because in this case
smaller droplets are sufficient to bridge two conductor. Once a water layer is formed, the formation
of a electrolytic cell occurs, and corrosion process starts. On clean surfaces, the formation and
spread of the droplets takes place at a slower pace. However, surface contamination accelerates
water layer formation, decreasing the time for failure. Additionally, other surface characteristics
like roughness and wettability also influence droplet behavior. It is important to note that the
formation of water layer is critical in order for corrosion to occur, therefore preventing droplet
coalescence can significantly enhance electronic reliability.
Once an electrolytic cell forms, electrochemical migration (ECM) can occur which is one of the
most important corrosion related failure in electronics. ECM is primarily driven by the electric field
formed between the conductors, making it highly influenced by factors such as applied voltage and
pitch distance. While voltages vary according to device function, increasing the pitch distance has
been shown to delay the time for failure, [1]. During ECM, typical metal dissolution and reduction
reactions take place. Additionally, other reactions such as oxygen and hydrogen reduction occur,
generating a pH gradient between the conductors. This pH difference plays an important role,
as it defines the stability of specific species, and therefore the final composition of the corrosion
products.
Electrochemical migration is not the only form of corrosion found in electronics. Others include
galvanic corrosion and gaseous corrosion. Galvanic corrosion arises from the presence of different
metal present in PCBs, which under high packing density may be in contact, forming galvanic
cells. The exposure of electronics devices to gaseous environments containing H2S, SO2, or CO2
represents a significant challenge. The combination of corrosive gases and a condensed water layer
significantly influence corrosion mechanisms. When any of these gases dissolve in the water layer,
they release ionic species increasing the conductivity of the electrolyte and enhancing the corrosion
rate. However,the severity of corrosion depends on the specific gas. H2S is a corrosive gas which
promotes the formation of both copper oxides and copper sulfides, and it can directly react with
the copper. SO2 and CO2 are also important gases which their effect on corrosion depends on

the water layer formation, and gas concentration. H2S is frequently used in single-gas testing of
IGBT modules, and also in mixed gas testing with SO2, [2][3]. CO2 have been widely studied in
the context of carbon capture and storage (CCS), [4].
1.1
Motivation for the thesis
Electronic devices are widely used in many industries which leads them being exposed to harsh
conditions that damage the integrity of components due to corrosion-related failures. Electronic
devices are widely used in industries such as industrial plants, outdoor installations, and even in
carbon capture facilities. During their service, they are exposed to humid conditions, but also to
corrosive gases. These gases can include; H2S, SO2, and CO2 between others.
Additionally, the miniaturization trend in electronics, which leads to higher packing densities,
makes these devices more sensitive to humidity, specifically to condensation conditions. When
critical conditions are met, condensation can occur triggering corrosion mechanism such as electro-
chemical migration (ECM). Despite extensive research on corrosion in electronics, studies related

important parameter that also affect ECM like pitch distance and applied bias voltage. The inter-

of PCBs under humid and gaseous environments was studied using gases which represent an issue

Literature review
2.1
Printed Circuit Boards (PCBs)
Printed Circuits Boards (PCBs) are an essential part of any electronic device from common ob-
jects like phones to more complex systems, including medical devices and military equipments, [5].
Their importance lies in providing structural support for the assembly of electronics components,
and enabling electrical connections through conductive paths, [6]. However, many reliability is-
sues in electronics are directly related to problems with the PCBs themselves. For example, it has
been reported that approximately 26% of the failures in power electronics systems are due to PCB-
related issues, [7]. During their service life, devices are often exposed to different environmental
conditions that make PCBs vulnerable to stresses such as, humidity, condensation, and corrosive
gases. Additionally, the fabrication process and the ongoing need for miniaturized electronic pack-
aging provide conditions that promote corrosion, and ultimately failure of the device.
2.1.1
PCBs Structure and Materials
Modern PCBs can vary significantly in complexity, ranging from single-layer designs to advance
boards with as many as 20 to 30 layers, [8]. However, they primarily consists of the following
parts, [5]: (1) a non-conductive substrate or laminate, (2) a conductive printed circuits, (3) a solder
mask, and (4) mounted components.
The manufacturing process of PCBs involves many steps, beginning with the selection of the lam-
inate material. FR-4 (Flame Retardant 4) is the most valuable used substrate. It is composed of
fiberglass reinforced epoxy with fire retardant, [5][9]. On this substrate, the conductive circuits
are created, primarily using copper. The desired copper areas that are needed for conductive pur-
pose (traces and pads) are protected with an etch-resistance material. The remaining, unprotected
copper is removed using an etching solution, leaving behind the desired circuit pattern, [5].
After the circuit pattern is formed, the laminate is coated with a thin solder mask layer, which is
basically a green-colored polymer coating. While this layer gives PCBs their characteristic color,
its main purposes are to prevent shorts circuits and the oxidation of the copper traces. Specific
areas, such as pads where components will be soldered, are left exposed,[7]. It can be seen like this;
the copper circuits define the electrical pathways, while the solder mask acts as a protective barrier
over them. However, defects in the solder mask or changes in its wettability can significantly
affect condensation behavior on the PCB. This is, the formation, distribution and spreading of the
condensed water droplets can be altered, and potentially reducing the time to failure. This topic
will be discusses in more details in later sections.
The final step in PCB production includes the assembly of all electrical components which are held
in place by soldering which not only has the function of connect the components physically but also
electrically, [10]. In PCB assembly two main mounting methods are used namely; Through-hole
Technology (THT), and Surface Mount Technology (SMT). The first one as its named suggest, it
consist on drilling holes through the board, and insert the leads of the components through them,
by finally fix them in their place by a soldering process, more specifically wave soldering. On the
other hand, the SMT technique consist on mounting the components directly on top of the Cu pads
and soldering them using a technique called reflow soldering. This technique is highly used since
supports the demands for miniaturization, [8]. Fig.2 illustrates a typical PCB assembly.

Although copper pads can be used without any additional surface treatment, its direct exposure
to different service environments can lead to oxidation issues. Therefore, some surface finishes

during the assembly of components. Some common surface finishing techniques include: Hot Air

2.2
Humidity and Condensation dynamics on PCBs
Understanding the moisture and condensation dynamics on the PCBs is essential for effectively as-
sessing the reliability of electronic devices. The humidity-related issues in electronics arise from
the exposure of the devices to varying climatic conditions together with the trend of device minia-
turization which leads to an increase on the component packing density. The presence of moisture
can result in various failure modes due to corrosion with the severity depending on both environ-
mental conditions and device characteristics. Therefore, the formation of a thin water layer on the
PCBs surface is a critical factor affecting reliability under humid conditions, [15].
2.2.1
Humidity characteristics
The interaction of water with the PCB surface depends on several factors, including the amount
of moisture in the air and the temperatures of both the air and the PCB surface. For a water layer
to form on the surface of the PCB, certain environmental conditions must first be met. Therefore,
understanding the behavior of water in air, and key concepts such as absolute humidity, relative
humidity, and dew point need to be considered.
Absolute Humidity
The absolute humidity (AH) refers to the actual amount of water vapor present in the air. It is
expressed as the ratio of the total mass of water vapor (gaseous form of water in atmosphere)
(mH2O) to the volume pf air (Vair): AH = mH2O/Vair(g/m3). An important aspect of the AH is
6
Corrosion Mechanism in Electronics under Humid and Gaseous Environments
==PAGE:19==
its direct relationship with the temperature. As the temperature increase, air can hold more water
vapor, resulting in a higher AH. This temperature effect plays an important role in the impact of AH
on corrosion in electronics. This is because at higher temperatures, the interaction of water vapor
with hygroscopic residues on the PCB surface is intensified, and the faster transport of moisture
through the device packing toward the PCB can be accelerated [16].
Relative Humidity
The relative humidity (RH) can be considered as the ratio between the actual amount of water vapor
in the air and the maximum amount of water the air can hold at a specific temperature. This value
is expressed as a percentage, accordinf to: RH = (pw/p0).100, where pw is the partial pressure
of water vapor and p0 is the saturation water vapor pressure at that temperature, [16]. RH can also
be understood as an indicator of how close the air is to being saturated at a given temperature. For
example, 100%RH means the air is saturated where air is holding all the water vapor it can at a
specific temperature. If a 50%RH is now considered, it means that the air is holding only half of
its moisture capacity at the same temperature. During temperatures variations, when temperature
decreases, the amount of water that the system can hold is reduced. As a result, any excess in
moisture condenses into liquid water. The condensation is critical in the context of electronic
failures, as the condensed water layer on PCB surfaces can lead to corrosion issues. For this
reason, the RH provides important information about the conditions required for condensation,
and this value is the one used in experiments in which different temperatures and humidity values
are used to study the corrosion mechanisms in different atmospheric conditions.
Dew point
The Dew point (DP) indicates the temperature to which the PCB surface must be cooled to provoke
condensation on its surface. As previously explained, condensation happens when the air reaches
its saturation point, i.e., 100%RH. Therefore, the dew point represents the critical temperature at
which this saturation condition is achieved. Along with the relative humidity, the DP is a crucial
factor for electronic reliability, as it directly affects both the thickness of the condensed water layer
and the rate at which it forms. The magnitude of the temperature drop required to reach the dew
point depends significantly on the RH level and temperature. Under certain conditions, only a min-
imal decrease in the temperature is sufficient to promote condensation, [16]. A common tool used
to determine when saturation level is reached, according to the temperature and humidity levels
is the mollier diagram. A typical diagram is shown in Fig.3. This diagram is a graphic tool that
relates the absolute humidity, relative humidity, and dew point, [16]. The red line in the diagram
indicates the saturation (100%RH) or dew point for different temperatures and humidity condi-
tions, indicating the temperature needed for condensation. For example, by tracing a temperature
of 25◦C and 80%RH (shown by the green lines), it is possible to calculate the temperature at
which saturation is reached. In this example, it corresponds to approximately 20◦C (indicated by
yellow line).
Changes in the temperature of the PCB can result from different factors, such as usage conditions or
fluctuations in the surrounding environment. However, the PCB surface may not always respond
immediately to these changes, which can be due to the thermal mass of components, leading to
a temperature lag. This mismatch gives place to transient conditions that promote condensation,
and increase the risk of corrosion-related failures. Additionally, condensation can occur even at
lower RH levels when residues such as flux residues, dust, corrosive gases are present on the PCB
surfaces. Due to their higher hygroscopic nature, these contaminants can lead to the water layer
buildup at low humidity levels, [15][16][17][18]. More details about the effect of contaminants
will be discussed in more details in 2.2.4.

2.2.2
Interaction of Humidity with the PCB surfaces
So far, the conditions necessary for moisture buildup on the surface of the PCBs have been dis-
cussed, emphasizing the role of relative humidity and dew point in starting condensation. Addi-
tionally, it has been highlighted that the formation of thin water layers on PCBs can significantly
impact the reliability of electronics systems. However, just the presence of water does not always
represents an immediate risk. A real concern arises when the water bridges two biased conduc-
tors forming an electrochemical cell, for example. As shown in the literature, leakage current, a
corrosion-related failure mechanism linked to a reduction in surface insulation resistant, requires
the biased electrodes to be completely covered by the condensed water layer to initiate, [20]. There-
fore, the way moisture interacts with the PCB surface plays a critical roles in determining whether
water buildup will lead to reliability issues. The formation and effect of water depend on many
factor including environmental conditions, and the design and materials characteristics of the PCB
itself, [16]. For this reason, it is necessary to understand the different mechanism by which water
interacts with solid surfaces.
As illustrated in Fig.4, moisture can interact with surfaces through several mechanism, such as,
adsorption, capillary condensation, and deliquescence, the latter occurring when hygroscopic ionic
contaminants are present on the surfaces. Additionally, at a bulk level, absorption and crystal
hydrate formation can also occur. For this research, only the interaction of water at a surface level
and condensation are of interest, and these will be discussed in this section.

Adsorption
Adsorption of moisture refers to the adherence of water molecules to the surface of a solid, primar-
ily through hydrogen bonding, leading to the formation of a monolayer of water. This process can
occur even under non-condensing conditions. Once the first monolayer is formed on the surface,
additional water molecules can interact with it, resulting in the formation of a thin water layer, ,
as shown in Fig.5. As the humidity increase, the amount of water vapor that can interact with the
adsorbed water molecules increase, [16].
Figure 5: Water adsorption on solid surfaces, [22].
The extent of water adsorption is influenced by the type of material, climatic conditions as well as
the cleanliness of the surface. For example, polymers materials, used in PCBs, have relatively low
affinity for water molecules, resulting in moderate water adsorption. On the other hand, materials
with a high surface energy, like metals, are more hydrophilic. These materials often adsorb an
additional water monolayer compared to those with low surface energy, [16]. This highlights the
complexity of water adsorption on PCBs, which usually are composed of different materials, and
each of them interacts with water at different intensities.
Capillary Condensation
Capillary condensation can be described as the process by which water vapor condenses within
confined spaces, such as pores or other surface defects even when the relative humidity is be-
low saturation conditions, [16]. As previously described, water molecules initially adsorb on the

surfaces forming water monolayers, As humidity increases, these develop into water multilayers,
as shown in Fig.6, this process being primarily governed by the moisture adsorption mechanism.
However, in reduced spaces like pores or grooves, where water molecules are closer to each other,
the vapor pressure required for condensation decreases. As a result, condensation in confined
spaces is faster compared to larger ones. Additionally, the condensation in these localized areas
can expand either by adsorbing additional water vapor or by merging with adjacent confined re-
gions, forming a continuous water layer, as shown in the last step for capillary condensation in
Fig.6. As it is known, surfaces ,specially those of the PCBs, are not completely flat. Instead,
they exhibit certain roughness and other features which act as preferred sites for condensation via
capillary, [15].

Development of condensation
It is often assumed that the formation of a water layer occurs uniformly across the surface of the
solid, but that is not the case. Fig.7 illustrates the different stages of the water layer formation.

these water islands grow and expand (Fig.7b). With further increases in humidity, the higher wa-

path that can connect biased conductors (Fig.7c and d). The time required, and the number of
water monolayers needed to form these conductive paths, depend on the distance between conduc-
tors, emphasizing the role of design parameters in reliability. Ultimately, a uniform water layer is
formed, covering the expose surface (Fig.7e), [16].

2.2.3
Effect of surfaces topography on Condensation dynamics
As discussed so far, the interaction between humidity and the surface of solids plays a crucial role
in the formation of water layers. When the temperature of the surface drops below the dew point,
condensation can occur. However, water accumulation can also take place under non-condensing
conditions through capillary condensation, particularly when cavities or pores are present on the
surface of the sample. Therefore, environmental conditions alone do not fully determine the extent
and behavior of water buildup. Surface characteristics, particularly surface roughness, significantly
influence how water forms, spreads, and interacts with the surface. Beyond the physical roughness,
the chemistry of the solder mask is also a key factor, since it determines the surface energy which
directly influences how easily water molecules adsorb and spread over any surface, [15]. In this
section, surface roughness of the PCBs will be explored, along with its effects on the nucleation and
spreading of condensed droplets. The role of surface wettability and contact angle measurements
will also be discussed.
PCB topography
In PCBs, the surface roughness originates from both the FR-4 laminate and the solder mask applied
on top of it. The FR-4 laminate is inherently rough and porous, resulting in an irregular surface
characterized by valleys and peaks, [23]. Fig.8a shows a SEM image of a laminate without solder
mask. However, the laminate is always coated with a solder mask, which helps to smooth out
the rough surface of the laminate. However, even though this creates a more uniform surface, the
solder mask introduce its own roughness. The roughness of the solder masks is related to the filler
material used in it, including the size, shape, and concentration, [16][13]. Additionally, the PCB
surfaces can also contain defects such as pores and pinholes, which arises from the manufacturing
process like poor curing or uneven application of the solder mask, [15]. As a results, PCB surfaces
can vary significantly in roughness, even when coated with solder mask. Fig.8b and c illustrate
examples of different toughness levels. The surface defects in PCBs act as preferential nucleation
sites for the condensation and spreading of the water droplets resulting in a faster formation of a
continuous water layer increasing surface wettability.
Figure 8: SEM images of a) laminate material (no solder mask), b) solder mask with a low degree
of roughness, and c) solder mask with a higher roughness, adapted from [23].
Fig.9 shows how condensation behavior differs based on surface roughness. On rough surfaces,
droplets form with irregular shapes and grow and merge more easily. In contrast, on smoother
surfaces, droplets have a concentric shape and are isolated across the surface of the PCB, therefore it
takes more time for this droplets to coalesce. This highlights the importance of surface topography
on the droplets formation. Once droplets coalesce, they can form continuous paths between biased
electrodes leading to short circuits and corrosion.

findings show that rough, hydrophilic solder masks promote earlier and more extensive water layer

examined the effects of solder mask roughness and pad design on electrochemical migration using
water drop and thermal humidity tests. Their work emphasizes how surface morphology and PCB
design impact condensation-related failures.
Wettability
It has been mentioned that changes in the roughness of the sample change the wettability of the
material. However, so far it has not been talked about the term wettability. Wettability refers as
how easy it is for a liquid to spread over the surface of a solid. This property can be evaluated
by measuring the contact angle. This angle is formed between the surface of the solid and the
tangent line drawn at the boundary of the droplet. A small contact angle (< 90◦), indicates good
wettability where water can spread easily on the surface of the sample. In contrast, a large contact
angle (> 90◦) indicates poor wettability and limited spreading. Surfaces with contact angle greater
than 150◦are considered superhydrophobic, as illustrated in Fig.10, [16].
Wettability is closely related to surface energy. An hydrophilic surface (high surface energy) have
a high affinity for water molecules and thus promote good wettability. Hydrophobic surfaces (low
surface energy) repeal water and display poor wettability. It might be intuitive to think that in-
creasing the roughness of a hydrophobic surface would improve its wettability. However, that is
not the case. In fact, when the roughness of a hydrophobic surface is increased, the wettability is
decreased. On the other hand, increasing the roughness of a hydrophilic surface can enhance its

wettability even more, [16].

Thus, both surface roughness and surface energy are critical parameters in determining wettability
and, as a result the risk of failures due to condensation. The surface energy is governed by the
chemical composition of the solder mask, which affects how water molecules interacts with the
surface. The roughness, as previously discussed, determines how water droplets nucleate, spread,
and coalesce, particularly into cavities where capillary condensation occurs. These factors together
influence whether water can form conductive paths between electrodes, contributing to corrosion
and electrochemical migration, [15][16]. Although surface roughness was not a controlled variable
in this study, some level of roughness can exist due to the manufacturing process.
2.2.4
Effect of contaminants on condensation dynamics
As mentioned earlier, the detrimental effects of water condensation on PCB surfaces arise when
condensed water droplets grow large enough to coalesce and form a continuous water layer. This
water layer can bridge two biased conductors, providing a conductive path and triggering electro-
chemical reactions. However, for condensation to occur, the surface of the PCB must be below
the dew point temperature. When this is not that case and the PCB is only exposed to high hu-
midity without condensation, water may adsorb onto the surface, forming a few water monolayers.
The thickness of these monolayers is insufficient to support electrical conduction. However, this
applies to clean PCB surfaces, which is rare in real world applications.
The surfaces of the PCB can contain contaminants coming from various sources throughout their
lifecycle. These contaminants significantly affect condensation dynamics, being the soldering pro-
cess one of the most critical sources. No-clean flux systems, though designed to eliminate post-
solder cleaning, still leave residues on the surfaces. Additionally, chemical treatments used in PCB
fabrication, human handling (e.g. fingerprints or skin secretions), and exposure to particles like
dust or de-icing salts can all contribute to surface contamination. Furthermore, environmental ex-
posure to corrosive gases such as NOx, SOx, COS, H2S, also represents a risk where these gases
can react directly with exposed metal pads in humid conditions, [25].
These contaminants represent a risk because they promote earlier and more extensive condensation
and facilitate corrosion, leading to a shorter lifespan of the device. In contrast to clean surfaces,
which require significant cooling below the dew point in order for condensation to occur, in con-
taminated surfaces, condensation can start even at lower humidity levels. This behavior is primarily
due to the hygroscopic properties of contaminants. A hygroscopic nature refers to the ability of
a substance to absorb moisture from air even below the supersaturation conditions, [26]. There-
fore, drastic changes are not necessary in order to form a water layer on contaminated surfaces. In
this context, the term of deliquescence should be included. Deliquescent contaminants can absorb
enough moisture to dissolve into a liquid solution. This accelerates the formation of water layer

==PAGE:26==
and increases the risk for failures, [15][16][17]
In this work, al experiments were carried out using a clean surface. However, since in study II
and III clean samples were exposed to atmospheres with CO2, H2S, and SO2, only contamination
caused by exposure to these gaseous environments will be discussed. While contamination from
other sources as manufacturing residues or flux is not included.
Deliquesce
The concepts of hygroscopicity and deliquescence were previously introduced. However, under-
standing how these two are interconnected, and how contaminants promote higher condensation
levels, requires considering a combined mechanism than involves adsorption, capillary condensa-
tion, and eventually deliquescence. As mentioned earlier, under humid conditions, surfaces adsorb
water molecules forming a few monolayers. In case of the presence of contaminant on PCB sur-
faces, these ones due to their hygroscopic nature, readily attract and adsorb moisture accelerating
the initial water buildup. As the humidity increases, confined spaces such as pores or other surface
defects can further promote capillary condensation, as explained in 2.2.2. As this process con-
tinues, water gradually accumulates. Once the critical relative humidity value for that particular
contaminant is reached, the residue starts to dissolve into the liquid, resulting in the formation of
a conductive water layer marking the onset of deliquesce, [16][18].
The critical RH at which deliquesce takes place is specific to each ionic residue, depending on its
chemical structure and solubility. Some residues have a stronger affinity for water and dissolve at
lower RH levels. Once a residue deliquesces, it retains the absorbed water for extended periods,
even if the ambient RH drops below the critical. Therefore, when the water needs to be remove
from the dissolved residues, simply reducing the humidity back below the deliquescence threshold
is not sufficient. In fact, lower RH levels are required, [25]. In summary, hygroscopicity and
deliquescence not only promote a faster water layer formation, but also increase the conductivity
of those water layers by dissolving ionic residues into them. Since these residues have a tendency
of retain water for a extended time, they significantly increase the risk of leakage current, corrosion
and eventual device failure, [15].
Gaseous Contaminants
Electronic devices are often exposed to various gaseous contaminants throughout their service
life. In the presence of humidity, these gases can significantly contribute to corrosion. Only a
brief overview of these gases is provided in this section, as a more detailed discussion, particu-
larly of those used in this study, is present in section 2.4. Among these gases, hydrogen sulfide
(H2S) is considered one of the most corrosive and toxic atmospheric pollutants. Other relevant
contaminants include sulfur dioxide (SO2), nitrogen oxides (NOx), and carbon dioxide (CO2).
When a thin water layer is formed on the PCB surface. these gases can dissolve and dissociate
into ionic species, increasing the electrolyte conductivity and prmote corrosion, [27]. In partic-
ular, copper (Cu) surfaces are susceptible to sulphidation, a reaction between copper and gases
containing sulfur, which results in the formation of copper sulfides. The extent and rate of this
reaction depend not only on the concentration of the gas but also on the RH of the environment,
[28]. Therefore, the formation and extend of corrosion products can vary significantly between a
clean surface and one that already contains contaminants. Additionally, some corrosion products
can also act as hygroscopic residues. In real world conditions, PCBs are often exposed to transient
humidity cycles. If corrosion products form and later get dried, they remain on the surface. When
high humidity conditions return, these corrosion products reabsorb moisture differently to how it
would happen on a clean sample. Therefore, understanding the role of gaseous contaminants, and
their interaction with condensation dynamics is also important for electronics reliability.

2.3
Corrosion Mechanism on Electronics
In this section, an introduction to the corrosion mechanism commonly found in electronics devices
is presented. The discussion starts with a brief introduction into the basic of corrosion, followed
by more specific corrosion mechanism relevant to electronics, including galvanic corrosion, con-
ductive anodic filament (CAF), electrochemical migration (ECM) and gaseous corrosion. More
emphasis is given to the last two mechanisms, ECM and gaseous corrosion, due to their relevance
to the experimental conditions studied in this work.
2.3.1
Galvanic and Electrolytic Cells
Basic principles of Corrosion
First at all, it is necessary to define the term corrosion, which can be described in simple terms as
the degradation of a material structure due to its interaction with its environment, [29]. Corrosion
can affect different materials like metals, polymers and ceramics, but for the purposes of this work,
the focus is on metal corrosion.
Electrochemical corrosion involves charge transfer reactions, encompassing both oxidation and
reduction. These are half reactions where electrons are produced at one location (oxidation), while
the other consumes those electrons (reduction). All electrochemical cells must have four essential
components, [29][30]:
1. anode: the site where oxidation occurs, and where the positive metallic ions are releasing
into the solution.
2. cathode: the site where electrons are consumed by the reduction of species from the envi-
ronment (e.g., oxygen or hydrogen ions).
3. electrolyte: a medium (typically a liquid) that that allows ionic conduction between the
electrodes.
4. electron-conducting connection: an electrical pathway between the anode and cathode that
allows electron flow.
In the context of electronics, these electrochemical cells can form on the PCB surfaces when a thin
water layer connects two biased metals. The formed water layer by itself is a poor electrolyte due
to its low conductivity, therefore the presence of dissolved ionic contaminants (from residues or
gases) plays a crucial role to enhance the electrolyte´s conductivity, and facilitate current transport.
Galvanic and Electrolytic Cell
Two key types of electrochemical cells are relevant in electronics corrosion: galvanic and elec-
trolytic cells. Both cells can form on PCBs under specific conditions.
• A galvanic cell is form when two dissimilar metals are electrically connected in the presence
of an electrolyte. The reactions are driven by the inherent electrochemical potential differ-
ence between the two metals. The more reactive (less noble) metal becomes the anode and
corrodes, while the more noble metal act as the cathode. Fig.11a illustrates an example of a
galvanic cell. This type of cell can occur on PCBs due to multi-material combinations.
• On the other hand, an electrolytic cell, shown in Fig.11b, are formed when two biased con-
ductors are connected through an electrolyte. In this scenario, corrosion is driven by the
externally applied voltage rather than a spontaneous redox potential difference. In this case,
the positively biased electrode works as the anode (oxidation), and the negatively biased one
becomes the cathode (reduction), [29][30][31] .

2.3.2
Galvanic Corrosion
As mentioned earlier, galvanic corrosion takes place when two different metals are physically and
electrically in contact. The driving force for corrosion mechanism is the difference in electrochem-
ical potential between the two metals. The severity of corrosion depends on the magnitude of this
potential difference. Therefore, a larger difference results in more significant corrosion. Addition-
ally, other factors like the area ratio between electrodes also influence both the rate and severity of
galvanic corrosion, [29].
In PCBs, galvanic cells can easily form due to the different materials used during their assembly.
A clear example includes the Electroless Nickel Immersion Gold (ENIG) surface finish, which is
commonly used in electronics. This surface finish includes three metal layers: gold (Au), nickel
(Ni), and copper (Cu). The Cu is the pads. Then, a layer of Ni is deposited directly on this Cu,
where it is suppose to act as a barrier between the Cu and the Au. Finally, a thin layer of Au is
deposited on top of the nickel. This layer is very thin of approximately 50nm. However, it is
typically porous, which leads to exposure of the Ni layer to the environment. Due to the large
electrochemical potential difference between Au and Ni, galvanic corrosion is triggered where
the Ni acts as the anode and the Au as the cathode. The corrosion of Ni can continue until it also
expose the Cu material, which is then corroded. Real world failures related to this phenomenon has
been reported including the corrosion in mobile phone key-pads, [29][32][33]. Another common
example is found in immersion silver (ImAg) surface finishes, where copper (Cu) is coated with
a thin layer of silver (Ag). If the silver layer is porous or if there are issues with the solder mask
coverage, the Cu may be exposed to the environment. since silver is more noble than copper,
galvanic coupling leads to the accelerated corrosion of the exposed copper, [29].
2.3.3
Electrochemical Migration (ECM)
Electrochemical migration (ECM) is a critical failure mechanism in electronic devices. It is charac-
terized by the growth of metallic filaments (dendrites) bridging two biased conductors. The funda-
mental process of ECM includes ionic migration, therefore ECM requires two key conditions: the
presence of an electrolyte and an applied bias voltage, [34]. This configuration essentially forms
an electrolytic cell as described in Fig.11b, where typical electrochemical reactions like metal dis-
solution at the anode and reduction at the cathode take place. However, ECM mechanism also
includes additional stages that ultimately lead to dendrites formation and failure of the device.

ECM Mechanism
The mechanism of electrochemical migration can be divided in four stages including; electrolyte
formation, anodic dissolution, ionic transport, dendrite formation, [35]. Fig.12 illustrates these
four stages mechanism in ECM.
• Stage 1: Electrolyte formation: This initial step involves the formation of a water layer
that connects adjacent biased conductors. This water later can form through adsorption and
condensation as discussed earlier in section 2.2, Where it was mentioned that factors like
relative humidity, contaminants, and surface conditions strongly influence the formation of
this water layer on the PCB surface. The formation of the electrolyte is an important step in
ECM, since without it, ionic migration can not take place, and ECM can not proceed, [34].
• Stage 2: Anodic dissolution: Once the water layer is formed, and under an applied bias
voltage an electrochemical cell is stablish. As explained in section 2.3.1 one materials will
act as the anode and other as the cathode. The metal, working as anode, oxidizes and releases
metal ions (cations) following the reaction:
M →Mn+ + ne−
(1)
If the applied voltage exceeds 1.23V water splitting can take place. During water splitting,
oxygen evolution occur at the anode by reaction 2, while at the cathode hydrogen evolution
occurs by reaction 3, [34][36].
Oxygen evolution at the anode
2H2O →O2 + 4H+ + 4e−
(2)
Hydrogen evolution at the cathode
2H2O + 2e−→H2(g) + 2OH−
(3)
These reactions creates local pH gradients where the area bear the anode becomes acidic
due to the production of hydrogen ions (H+), while the cathode becomes alkaline due to
the formation of hydroxyl ions (OH−). The formation of pH gradients is important since it
influence the type and solubility of corrosion products which may affect the dendrite com-
position.
• Stage 3: Ionic transport: once the metal ions are released in the electrolyte, they migrate
towards the cathode. Their movement is primarily influenced by the electric field, but also
by diffusion and convection. The electric field (E) is generated between the electrodes when
a voltage is applied, and it is magnitude is a determined not only on the bias voltage (V), but
also of the distance between the electrodes (d), according to;
E = V/d
(4)
Therefore, the velocity at which the ions migrate is determined by the magnitude of the
electric field and the ion mobility. Where the ion mobility depends on the properties of the
ion such as charge, shape and size, and the properties of the electrolyte including pH and
temperature. In addition to the electric field, diffusion also contributes to ion transport due
to the formation of concentration gradients which promotes diffusion.Additionally, the ions
transport by convection can be related to the collapse of hydrogen bubbles derived from the
hydrogen reduction at the cathode. However this effect is more notable on condensation
conditions when a thick water layer has been form, and at higher voltages, [34].

• Stage 4: Dendrite formation: This last stage corresponds to the metal deposition and subse-
quently dendrite growth. When metal ions reach the cathode, they are reduced and deposited
as metallic forming a dendrite which grows towards the anode, described by:
Mn+ + ne−→M
(5)
The dendrite form with direction to the anode because the tip of the growing dendrite is
closer to the anode, compared to the rest of the connector, resulting in a stronger electric
field. Therefore the tip of the dendrite becomes a preferential site for further ion reduction
at that point. In the moment the dendrite reach the anode, it leads to a short circuit causing
sudden rise in current, and subsequently failure of the device, [29][35][37]. At the end, the
morphology and composition of the dendrites can vary according to the local environment.
The chemical composition of dendrites can go from pure metallic to a mixture including
metal hydroxides, depending of pH and other factgors, [29].

under certain pH conditions. As discussed above, water splitting reactions generate OH−ions

are helpful tools for evaluating the thermodynamic stability of metal species under different pH
and potential values. However, this diagram is a equilibrium diagram and shows the stability of
the species from a thermodynamic point of view, in real cases other parameters like kinetics play
an role in when these products are formed, [29][35].

2.3.4
Conductive Anodic Filament (CAF)
The conductive anodic filament (CAF) formation is often broadly categorized as an electrochem-
ical migration (ECM) phenomenon, however it differs from traditional ECM, which, as earlier
explained, involves the growth of metallic dendrites from the cathode toward the anode. In con-
trast, CAF involves the formation of a conductive filament from the anode to the cathode within
the PCB laminate. This filament is composed not of pure metal but of corrosion products, primar-
ily cooper-based compounds. The CAF mechanism starts with the absorption of moisture into the
laminate. This happens due to the degradation of the material or inherent properties of the laminate
that allow water uptake. The absorbed moisture provides an electrolytic path for ionic migration.
Then, when an bias voltage is applied, electrochemical reactions similar to those seen in ECM
systems are initiated, [41][42].
Similar to the ECM, the reactions at the anode and cathode create a pH gradient along the migration
path. Due to this pH gradient and relatively slower mass transport within the laminate, corrosion
products such as copper salts can precipitate, giving place to a gradually formation of a filament
bridging both electrodes. Once this conductive filament reach the cathode, it results in shorting of
the system. Therefore, the formation of CAF is highly influenced by the amount of moisture that
can be absorbed by the laminate material, which is related to its capability for water absorption.
CAF is specially important for multilayer PCBs laminates due to their multiples interfaces (e.g.,
Cu-resin, epoxy-glass) that can degrade under high humidity and temperature allowing moisture
to ingress, [29] .
2.3.5
Gaseous Corrosion
Gaseous corrosion refers to the degradation of a material when it is in contact with a gaseous
environment. The main factors influencing gaseous corrosion are: humidity and corrosive gases.
These corrosive gases primarily include sulfur-based gases like hydrogen sulfide (H2S), sulfur
dioxide (SO2), as well as nitrogen-containing compounds such as ammonia (NH3) and nitrogen
dioxide (NO2), [29].
Corrosion caused by gases typically occurs when water is adsorbed onto the surface. When cor-
rosive gases get in contact with water, they react forming acidic species, which further drive the
corrosion process. For example, when hydrogen sulfide dissolve in water dissociate forming hy-
drosulfide (HS−) and sulfide (S2−) ions. This ions will react with the metal forming some form of

sulfide compound. Therefore, the water contribute in two ways; adsorption of the corrosive gases
and the dissolution of metal, involving oxidation and reduction reactions, [29].
To study gaseous corrosion in electronics, various specialized tests are employed, often combin-
ing corrosive gas exposure with controlled environmental factors like temperature and humidity.
Mixed flowing gas tests are a common method for studying corrosion mechanisms where a mixture
of gases like H2S, SO2, NO2, and Cl2. This kind of test allows to study corrosion mechanism
maintaining the concentration of gases close to what it can be found on the real world applications.
Additionally, a control on the temperature and humidity levels is also possible. Single-gas tests
are also performed when specific gases wants to be studied, [3].

the exposure of hybrid power module to sulfur gas exposure which is an environment commonly

2.4.1
Hydrogen sulfide (H2S)
Hydrogen sulfide is a corrosive gas that originates from both natural and industrial sources. Its
presence in the environment represent a risk not only for electronic devices, but also for human
health, [44]. H2S can be found in places such as swamps and sewers, and even in volcanic gases,
with geothermal wells emitting between 10 - 30 ppb H2S into the atmosphere, [27][44]. Addition-
ally, it can be found as by-product from industrial applications such as oil and gas plants, mining,
and wastewater treatments, [45]. In all these environments, different devices are exposed to such
harsh conditions than can significantly reduce their lifespan.
As mentioned earlier, in the presence of a water layer, H2S dissolves and dissociates to form H+,
HS−, and S2−which enhance the electrical conductivity and decrease the pH of the electrolyte.
This will be reflected in an increase in the reaction rates including metal dissolution, and therefore
dendritic growth, [27][35]. Variations in the pH are also important, as pH defines the stability of
chemical species, and therefore influences the final composition of the corrosion products. These
products typically consist on both metallic sulfides and oxides. In the case of copper exposed
to H2S, common products include copper monosulfide (CuS), and copper sulfide (Cu2S), [46].
However, it has also been reported the formation of other type of copper sulfides like geerite Cu8S5
or digenite (Cu1.8S), [44]. Alongside copper sulfides, at high humidity levels copper oxides such

Technique principles
The fundamental principles governing light microscopy includes image formation, magnification,
and resolution. The image formation starts when the specimen to be analyzed is placed at position

which is influenced by the diffraction of visible light. In order to obtain higher resolution, it is
necessary to use shorter wavelengths. However, the optical microscope use visible light whose

An introduction into the principles of scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and energy-dispersive
X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) is prided, followed by information related to the sample preparation,equipment,

Technique principles
The scanning electron microscope (SEM) generates high-resolution images by scanning the surface
of a sample with a focused electron beam. This is done by using electromagnetic lenses. When the
beam interacts with the sample, different type of signals are produce within a pear-shape interaction
volume, as seen in Fig.22. The size of this interaction zone depends on the accelerating voltage
(energy of the incident electrons), [60].
Figure 22: Interaction volume for Scanning Electron Microscope, [60]
The emitted signals provide different information according to the scattering mechanism. The two
main mechanism include elastic scattering which produces backscattered electrons (BSE). This

electrons provide information that reveal compositional contrast where heavier elements will ap-
pear brighter due to their higher atomic number. On the other side, secondary electrons (SE) forms
from inelastic scattering near the surface of the sample. These electrons provide the topographical
information of the surface. The contrast is influenced by the orientation of the surface with respect
to the detector. This study primarily used secondary electron imaging for morphological analysis.
While SEM is a powerful tool for characterization of materials, sample preparation is important.
One of the main challenges is the surface charging, specially for non-conductive samples. This can
cause distortion of the image. Therefore, samples should be properly prepared in order to prevent
any charging effect, [60].
Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) is a technique used alongside SEM to determine the
elemental composition of materials. When the electron beam interacts with the sample, some
electrons can be ejected from the inner shell, leaving behind vacancies and atoms in an unstable
state. To return to a stable condition, an electron from a higher-energy shell fills the vacancy,
resulting in the emission of an X-ray photon. These emitted X-rays have well-defined energies
which are unique for each element, and received the name of characteristic X-rays. This signals
are detected by an EDS sensor integrated to an SEM. This techniques allows the identification of
most elements in the periodic table with exception of hydrogen and helium, [61].

the samples were coated with a thin carbon layer.

3.4.4
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS)
Technique principles
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) is considered a tool for surface analysis which provides
insights about the chemical states of elements within a depth of 10 nm. The fundamental mecha-
nism of XPS is based on the photoelectric effect. As other characterization techniques, the samples
are exposed to X-rays in an ultra-high vacuum environment. When the X-ray hits the material, it
ejects electrons from the inner shell of the atoms called photoelectrons. A detector collects these
photoelectrons and measures their kinetic energy. Then, the binding energy is calculated by sub-
tracting the measured kinetic energy of the photoelectron from the energy of the incident X-ray,
which in this work an Al kα source was used. The obtained binding energies are unique for each
element and its chemical state. At the end, a spectrum for each element is obtained plotting the
counts per second versus the binding energy (eV). It should be understand that even though the
XPS is used for surface analysis, the X-ray actually penetrate much deeper that 10nm. However,
only those electrons closer to the surface of the sample scape the material without losing energy,
and therefore they provide reliable data, [64].
This technique is used in many disciplines. In the case of corrosion, since many of the reactions
occur at the surface, forming thin corrosion product layers that due to their thickness they may be
beyond the detection limit of other techniques. In these cases, the surface sensitivity of XPS is

like stainless steel, aluminium, and titanium alloys. However, the surface sensitivity of XPS also
introduces certain challenges particularly related to surface contamination. Any presence of con-
tamination like adventitious carbon or adsorbed moisture can hide the surface of interest, resulting
in lower signal intensities and affecting the analysis. This issue was actually encountered during
the analysis in this study, where no copper signal was initially detected likely due to surface con-
tamination. Therefore, like in this case, a pre-cleaning using argon ion beam can be used before
the measurement to remove surface contaminants, [64].

In-situ Image and Image Analysis
As previously mentioned, the in-situ monitoring of droplet evolution and dendrite formation was
done using a Dino-Lite camera. For all the test, images were captured every 2 minutes throughout
the duration of the test. These images served two purposes: first, as a qualitative tool to correlate
the leakage current measurements with the physical behavior observed during the test. Secondly,
as a basis for quantitative image analysis.
For the image analysis, selected images were analyzed to determine the size of the water droplets
formed on the surface of the samples. This analysis provided information on droplet diameter
growth over time, which is relevant for predicting the critical time to failure as a function of the
pitch distance between the Cu pads. The image analysis was carried out using ImageJ, an open
source image processing which provides many post-processing tools, [70].
The key parameter extracted from the images was the Feret diameter, defined as the longest dis-
tance between any two points along the droplet boundary, [71]. Therefore, this measurement is
particularly suitable for assessing the potential of a droplet to connect the two pads. The image
processing procedure in ImageJ follow these steps:
1. Set the scale: since each image may have slightly different magnification, a reference length
(the size of the Cu pads) was used to calibrate the image.
2. Convert to grayscale: the image was converted to a 16-bit grayscale to prepare it for thresh-
olding.
3. Thresholding: a binary thresholding was applied to isolate the droplets from the back-
ground. The thresholding was manually adjusted to make sure that only the droplet areas
were highlighted.
4. Fill holes: to ensure accurate droplet detection, the ”Fill holes” command was applied. This
step fills internal regions within the droplets that may be incorrectly interpreted as empty.
5. Watershed separation: when adjacent droplets were too close together and detected by
the software as a single object, the ”watershed” function was used to separate them into
individual droplets.
6. Measure Feret Diameter: once the droplets were properly segmented, measurements were
performed by selecting Feret diameter as one of the analysis outputs.

4.1
Study I: Effect of pitch distance
As a result of the ongoing miniaturization in electronics design, pitch distance has become an
critical factor on reliability of electronic devices, [72]. Reducing spacing between conductors,
together with bias potentials (applied voltage), directly affects the intensity of electric field which
is a primary driving force for electrochemical migration (ECM), [73]. Therefore, understanding
the interplay between pitch distance and voltage is essential for predicting the time for failure and
assessing electrochemical behavior under different conditions.
In this section, the leakage current response under different pitch distance and applied voltage is
presented. To have a better interpretation of the current behavior, in-situ images of the droplets
formation are analyzed, allowing correlation between the electrical response and the physical pro-
cesses taking place on the sample surface. Additionally, image analysis is used to estimate the
critical time to failure based on the droplets growth. Finally, the morphology and chemical com-
position of the formed corrosion products are also present.
4.1.1
Leakage current measurements and in-situ water droplets condensation
Fig.26 shows the leakage current versus time plots corresponding to the four experimental condi-
tions carried out in this study: samples with pitch distances of 1 mm and 3 mm, each subjected
to an applied voltage of 2 V and 10 V. Three repetitions per condition were conducted, and the
results are plotted to asses repeatability. The results are divided by voltage level, starting with 10
V, followed by 2 V.
4.1.1.1
Samples under an applied voltage of 10V
Fig. 26a and b shows the results for current response under 10 V for 1 mm and 3 mm pitch distances,
respectively. As observed, the three repetitions per condition show a high degree of consistency,
confirming the reproducibility of the test setup. For both samples, the current evolution follows a
similar tendency which can be divided in three distinct regions: a stable region, a transient region,
and a failure region. These stages align with the condensation dynamics taking place on the sample
surfaces (higher pitch distance).
First, the stable region mainly corresponds to the early hours of the test, during which current
remains low and steady. This stage last approximately 2 hours for the 1 mm pitch sample and about
5 hours for the 3 mm sample. The low leakage current is attributed to the high resistance across
the system due to both insulating solder mask and the absence of a continuous water layer. As
observed in the in-situ pictures, Fig.27 a1 and b1, within the first hour some concentrical droplets
had formed, but their size and distribution does not provide a path connecting the solder contacts.
Therefore, the conductivity is still extremely low. Additionally, the 3 mm sample shows a longer
stability stage which it is related to the greater separation between the Cu pads.
In the transient region, the current starts to increase as water droplets grow and coalesce. The
initiation of transient region happens earlier in 1 mm pitch distance sample, around 2 hours into
the test (Fig.26a), compared to the 5 hours for the 3 mm sample (Fig.26b). As seen in Fig.27a2,
by hour 4, droplets have grown large enough to cover the Copper pads and barely have connect
the electrodes. Even a small dendrite is already visible, indicating ion transport has taken place.
However, an small gap between the droplet and the pad interrupts further growth likely caused by
uneven condensation. This behavior illustrates how reduced pitch distances enable faster coalesce
of water droplets and accelerate electrochemical migration. On the other hand, for the 3 mm sam-
ple, Fig.27b2, droplets distribution remains more dispersed, and coalescence has not occurred yet

37
==PAGE:50==
even after 4 hours into the test. This correlates with the current profile in Fig. 26b where the leak-
age current remains low indicating that the system is still in the stable stage. The transient stage
for this sample starts once the water droplets grow large enough to initiate bridging. It can also be
observed that irrespective of the pitch distance, once the transient region starts, the duration of it
is almost the same for both samples, that is around 3 hours.
The failure region is marked by a sharp and sustained increase in current, indicating the formation
of continuous conductive paths like growing dendrites. The dendrites due to their composition
provide a much lower electrical resistance compared to the water layer and insulating solder mask.

Samples under an applied voltage of 2V
Fig.26c and d, shows the leakage current profiles for the 1 mm and 3 mm pitch distance samples,

corresponds to approximately the first 2 hours of the test, the leakage current remains at low levels.
During this stage, small concentric droplet are formed on the sample surface, as seen in Fig.27c1.
These early droplets do not yet connect the electrodes, preventing significant current flow.
As the condensation test progresses, the sample enters the transient region, marked by a slightly
increase in leakage current. This increase is attributed to the growth and spread of water droplets,
which coalesce connecting the electrodes and provide ionic conduction paths. Fig.27c2, shows the
formation of multople droplets of varying sizes formed between the electrodes. Although it is dif-

leading to a moderate increase in current. At this point, no dendrites are observed.

covering both electrodes. However, dendritic growth is still absent. However, some black products
are observed lying along the cathode (left pad) suggesting ionic migration and localized reduction
reactions. These black products indicate copper ion transport from the anode (right pad), but due
to the low applied voltage, the energy is insufficient to drive full dendrite formation. This matches
with the leakage current profile in Fig.26c, where the current remains relatively stable through this
stage.
Around the 9 hours mark, a sudden increase in leakage current, exceeding the 10−2mA is observed,
indicating the onset of the failure region (Fig.26c). Indicating that after this time dendritic bridge
have finally formed enabling low-resistance conduction and shorting of the system. Interestingly,
the leakage current level is similar to that observed under 10 V, but the time to failure is significantly
delayed. This behavior reflects the influence of the applied voltage where a low voltage results in
a weaker electric field between the electrodes, and consequently slower electrochemical reaction
rates. As a result, the formation of dendrites under 2 V occurs slower, requiring more time to reach
the failure stage.
On the other hand, for the 3 mm pitch sample, only two distinct regions can be identify: the stable
region and transient region, see Fig.26d. The leakage current behavior is similar to that of the
corresponding 10 V case in therms of timing, with the stable region lasting for approximately 5
hours. After this, the sample enters the transient region, during which the leakage current gradually
increases. However, it does not go exceed 10−2mA throughout the entire 15 hour test. Therefore,
it is considered that a failure stage was not reach in this case. This is further corroborated by
the in-situ images, Fig.27d4. which shows that by 12 hours a continuous water layer has formed
connecting both electrodes, however no dendrites are observed. Instead, faint black products are
visible, but these do not have a typical dendritic characteristic. Rather, they appear as diffused
formations and do not extend to the anode (right pad). Therefore, the increased in leakage current
observed in this stage is likely due to the formation of the water layer, but because dendrites were
not formed, the system did not short and failure did not take place. The absence of dendrites even
after prolonged exposure is attributed to both the large pitch distance of the sample and the low
applied voltage. As it is observed, the increased distance requires longer time and stronger electric
fields for dendrite formation.

5
Discussion
Electrochemical migration (ECM) severity and rate are influenced by the interplay of different pa-
rameters, including pitch distance, applied voltage, and exposure to corrosive atmospheres (con-
taminants). For this reason, the study of corrosion mechanisms in electronics is a complex task.
Understanding how these parameters affect corrosion is essential for the development of preven-
tive strategies against electronics failures. For this reason, this work started with a study of the
effect of different pitch distances under humid conditions, using both low and high applied bias
voltage. These experiments were carried out under normal humid conditions in order to understand
the interaction between pitch distance and voltage in the development of ECM. Subsequently, two
additional studies were conducted using controlled exposures to H2S and SO2 atmospheres, in
order to understand how corrosive gases influence corrosion mechanism on PCBs. Finally, exper-
iments were also carried out under CO2 conditions, not with the goal of studying in details the
corrosion mechanism, though some will be discussed in this section, but rather to assess the fea-
sibility of conducting such experiments in the in-house built setup. A discussion of the obtained
results for each study is presented in this section.
5.1
Effect of pitch distance on ECM
For this study, two pitch distances of 1 mm and 3 mm were used in combination with two applied
voltages; 2 V and 10 V. As shown in the leakage current results, Fig.26, the current profiles for the
tests can be divided into three distinct regions: a stable, transient, and failure part. In general, it is
evident that the pitch distance significantly influences the time for failure (TTF) irrespective of the
applied voltage. This time corresponds to the moment in which dendrite reach the anode shorting
the system, and providing a path for current to flow.
Starting with the high voltage (10 V) samples. The time needed for the transient part to start is
higher for the 3 mm pitch sample. This region corresponds to the point at which droplets coales-
cence connecting the electrodes, and marks the onset for electrochemical reactions which subse-
quently lead to dendrite formation and electrical shorting. As mentioned earlier, an electrolytic
path, connecting the electrodes, must form in order for corrosion to start. Thus, until this happens,
electrochemical reactions can not occur. Therefore, the time for the transient part reflects the time
needed for water to condense, and grow in size in order to connect the two biased electrodes. As a
result, more time is needed for the larger pitch distance of 3 mm compared to the shorter one. This
also means that any factor that enhances condensation behavior, like surface contamination, will
have a negative impact on the start of the transient region.
Interesting, once the transient region starts, its duration is almost the same for both pitch distances
under a high voltage (10 V). This transient region mainly involves the metal dissolution, and ion
migration. Theoretically, the pitch distance influences the ion migration due to its effect on the
electric field. According to the equation 4 the magnitude of the electric field (E), considering same
applied voltage, is stronger for the shorter pitch distance (1mm), promoting faster ion migration and
dendrite growth. Yet, in this case, the transient part is similar, suggesting that high voltage values
override the pitch distance effect. Additionally, voltage influences the availability of metal ions,
since higher voltage results in higher corrosion rate, accelerating metal dissolution. According to
a study by Reiss et al, [82], the time to failure due to ECM is inversely proportional to the electric
field strength.
Even though this transient zone is similar for both pitch distance samples, one notable difference
can be observed for the behavior during the failure region. The current for the 1 mm sample sharply

increase reaching high current values almost immediately after failure, whereas the 3 mm sample
shows a more gradual increase in current. This is likely due to the amount of formed dendrites.
Since it takes less time for ion migration in the 1 mm pitch distance, a faster and more extensive
dendrite growth take place in this sample. While for the 3 mm pitch distance sample, the other
way around occurs. This can also be observed in the in-situ images. Fig.27b3 shows the in-situ
image for the 3 mm pitch distance at the moment the failure stage starts. At this point, only a thin
branched dendrite is in contact with the anode. Nevertheless, the maximum current values for both

For the samples under an bias voltage of 2 V, the results show a clear difference compared to the
10 V samples, highlighting the role of the applied voltage. First of all, the time needed for the
transient stage to start is identical to those under higher voltage. This remark that this time is
governed by the pitch distance, and voltage does not have any effect on it, without considering
other conditions like temperature, and relative humidity. On the other hand, the transient region
is significantly affected by voltage. For the 1 mm sample, the transient region increase from 3
hours (at 10 V) to 7 hours (at 2 V). This is because a low voltage results in slower corrosion rates,
meaning that the amount of metal ions available for dendrite formation also decrease. Additionally,
dissolution reactions are not the only reactions taking place in the formed electrolytic cell which
also can include water splitting. Since the applied voltage (2 V) is just above the water splitting
threshold (1.23 V, [36]), reactions like oxygen and hydrogen evolution, which generates H+ and
OH−respectively, may occur at a slower rate. Therefore, less ionic species are present on the water
layer lowering its conductivity, and affecting ionic migration. The fewer metallic ions combined
with the low conductivity, slow dendrite formation rate, increasing the TTF. For the case of the
sample for 1 mm pitch distance, dendrite formation eventually occur, and the final current values
reach similar levels to those obtained at 10 V (102mA).
In contrast, for the 3 mm sample at 2 V, only a stable and transient region were observed. It is
possible that the duration of the tests was not enough in order to reach the failure region. In this
case, the combination of low voltage, higher pitch distance (weaker electric field), lower metal ions
concentration, and lower electrolyte conductivity, contribute to decrease the dendrite formation
rate. Although, in the in-situ images it is possible to observed the formation of corrosion products
extending from the cathode towards the anode, they do not have a dendritic shape. Additionally,
small amount of corrosion products are also observed on the anode as black products. This suggests
that some ions precipitated likely influenced by local pH and solubility limits.

explain the different dendrites morphologies observed through SEM. These are form through the

5.2
Effect of CO2 and air atmospheres
For this series of experiments, the two pitch distances of 1 mm and 3 mm were also used, however,
in this case, only a high applied voltage of 10 V was used. The condensation tests were carried out
under two different atmospheric conditions, by flowing CO2 and air. The first general observation
for all results, regardless of pitch distance and gas exposure, is the randomness in the time to failure
(TTF) which in this case correspond to the moment in which the maximum limit current of 1mA
is reached.
As expected, for both CO2 and air conditions,the 1 mm pitch samples reached the maximum cur-
rent limit (1mA) faster than for the 3 mm samples. As discussed above, this is expected since
larger pitch distances delay water coalesce between the electrodes, and therefore delaying failure.
Another consistent observation is that samples exposed to air failed faster than those under CO2,
irrespectively of the pitch distance. Similarly, in the long exposure experiments, failure takes
place earlier under air conditions than under CO2. From the in-situ images, it was observed that
condensation under CO2 exposure was slower compared to air condition. This is likely due to
CO2 lowering the humidity levels in the chamber, which affects the dew point, therefore there is
a smaller difference between the temperature of the surface of the sample and the dew point. This
results in droplets forming at a slower rate.
Additionally, under CO2, it takes more time for droplets to coalesce into a continuous water layer.
Often, large droplets form separately on each copper pad, but they remain slightly separated, pre-
venting an electrolytic bridge. This highlight that coalesce of droplets is critical for electrochemical
reactions to start, even when the droplets are extremely close but not connected. An example of
this can be observed in Fig.39b3. This behavior is supported by the wettability measurements. The
contact angle values measured under CO2, both in dry and humid conditions, were significantly
higher (>> 15◦) than for a clean surface. While for both air and CO2 experiments used samples
with a clean surface, CO2 exposure modified the surface wettability of the samples. As a result,
for the CO2 experiments, it takes more time for droplets to spread and merge, delaying the forma-
tion of a conductive water layer. On the other hand, the samples exposed just to air had contact
angles of 76.53◦which is close to the clean surface value of 70.62◦. This would explain why the
condensed droplets spread and coalesced faster in air conditions leading to shorter TTF.
In order to understand the real effect of CO2 itself, and leaving aside the condensation dynamics,
the time from droplet coalesce to failure (reaching 1mA) was measured. Since the electrochemical
reactions start only after the droplets connect the two electrodes, by doing this, the variability due
to condensation is removed, and the focus is only on the influence of gas atmosphere on corrosion
behavior. These results are shown in Fig.40. Based on the results, the time for failure from shortest
to longest is as follows: 1 mm sample CO2 < 1 mm sample under air < 3 mm sample under CO2
< 3 mm sample under air. The fact that samples exposed to CO2 failed faster after coalescence
suggests that the conductivity of the water layer increases, but also the pH decrease. These two
factors enhance the corrosion process, and a faster ion migration takes place. When CO2 dissolves
in water, it form carbonic acid (H2CO3), which then dissociates into bicarbonate (HCO−

Conclusion
In this work, three different studies were carried out which include the evaluation of parameters
and atmospheric conditions in which real world electronic devices can be exposed. Therefore,
from those studies the following conclusions can be obtained:
Effect of pitch distance
• The pitch distance plays a crucial role on determining the TTF. Low voltage and long pitch
distance is a perfect combination for prolonging the failures due to electrochemical migra-
tion.
• Larger pitch distance consistently increase the time for failure, regardless of the applied
voltage.
• The starting point of the transient region, when droplets coalescence and chemical reactions
start, is delayed for 3 mm pitch samples, as more time is needed for droplets to grow and
connect the electrodes.
• Image analysis of the condensed water droplets allows to successfully predict the time for
transient region to start.
• For samples under an applied voltage of 2 V and 3 mm pitch distance, failure stage was not
reach within the test time, suggesting that a combined effect of low electric field and low
concentration of metal ions delay electrochemical migration mechanism.
• The corrosion products consisted on Cu(OH) and CuO, with varying dendrite morpholo-
gies.
Effect of CO2 and air atmospheres
• The exposure to CO2 atmospheres shows a significant impact not only on the corrosion
mechanism but also on the condensation behavior compared to air conditions.
• The interaction between CO2 makes the surface of the samples more hydrophobic (higher
contact angles), resulting in a delay of droplet coalescence and TTF. While samples exposed
to air conditions shows contact angle close to those of the clean surface, explaining the faster
coalescence of droplets.
• Once water layer is formed, the presence of CO2 accelerates the corrosion process due to
an increase in the conductivity of the water layer, and decrease in pH due to the formation
of bicarbonate, carbonates and hydrogen ions.
• Corrosion products under CO2 exposure, primarily consist on Cu2O and CuO. While air
exposure result in Cu2O, CuO, and Cu(OH)2. Therefore, the gas composition influences
not only the time for failure but also the type of corrosion products.
• These findings help to validate the effectiveness of the experimental setup, but also highlights
the effect of environmental conditions on ECM.
Effect of H2S and SO2 atmospheres
• The exposure to H2S significantly affects corrosion mechanism under humid conditions
with leakage current gradually increasing with each cycle.
