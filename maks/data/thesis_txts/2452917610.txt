ABSTRACT
The Nordic power system has been undergoing a huge change in terms of power system augmentation
and renewable energy inclusion. Energy policies and research studies have been studying the eﬀect of
a more carbon-neutral power system in the Nordic power system in the years to come. Specially the
incorporation of large wind power farms is a major ongoing change in the Nordic-power system. Power
system stability concerns over large scale integration of full converter based type-4 wind turbines in
the Nordic region has been an important area of ongoing academic and industrial research. This thesis
is aligned to address the concerns in this direction of power system stability in the futuristic Nordic
power system.
More focus on small signal stability concerns with high wind power penetration has been given in this
report. Inter-area oscillation modes at diﬀerent levels of wind energy inclusion in the system has been
studied in detail. Modal analysis techniques has been used in this work and appropriate power system
stabilizers to damp out these inter-area oscillations has been studied. The eﬀect of type-4 wind farms
on oscillation modes in the Nordic power system was studied and appropriate controller designs have
been proposed.

POWER SYSTEM STABILITY-AN INTRODUCTION
A “stable power system” should remain in operating equilibrium under normal system conditions
and should re-establish itself into an acceptable state of equilibrium after a certain disturbance in
the system [1]. The concept of stability in power system arises due to the interaction of diﬀerent
opposing forces. The basic understanding of stability in any power system is the “state of synchronism”
between synchronous generators. Synchronous generators interconnected synchronously means that
the generator armature magnetic ﬁeld is interlinked with the rotor magnetic ﬁeld (both rotor and
stator magnetic ﬁelds rotate at synchronous velocity). In fact the rotor magnetic ﬁeld leads the
stator (armature) magnetic ﬁeld. In this steady state condition, the input mechanical power from the
turbine-governer is equal to the electrical power output of the machine.
During any disturbance, the electrical power changes and there is a mismatch between the input
mechanical power and the machine electrical power which either accelerates or decelerates the rotor
following the laws of rotational dynamics. Restoring forces in the system also called synchronizing
power comes into play. The extent of these forces depends on the non-linear power-angle relationship.
Instability results when the rotor angle deviates so much that the power transfer decreases beyond
that and the restoring forces are no more suﬃcient to maintain synchronism. In this condition the
rotor rotates at speeds which produce frequencies diﬀerent from the power network frequency. This
disruption of interlinking between the stator magnetic ﬁeld (system frequency) and rotor magnetic
ﬁeld also called as “slip” leads to transient behaviour of machine ﬂux and power [1].
In power systems, disturbances in network (large or small) is reﬂected in the electrical torque output of
synchronous generators. This torque is comprised of synchronizing and damping torques. A disturbance
where the machine electrical torque has insuﬃcient synchronizing component, the rotor angle behaviour
exhibits a non-oscillatory or aperiodic instability. In case of insuﬃcient damping torque, the rotor
angles shows an oscillatory instability. This type of instability study is done by small signal stability
study. It involves small perturbances introduced to the power systems like variations in loads and
generation. The capability of power system to maintain synchronism under such small perturbances
is termed as small signal stability [1]. This allows linearisation of synchronous machine and power
network equations around the initial operating conditions and facilitates a linear analysis.
Small signal oscillatory instability involves intra-plant oscillations (local oscillations), inter-area
oscillations (global oscillations), Control oscillations and torsional oscillations [1]. Oscillations between
wind turbines and power systems come under the category of control oscillations or SSCI (Sub-
synchronous control oscillations) [2]. However this thesis work focuses in detection of inter-area modes
in Nordic-32 model using modal analysis and damping them out with appropriate stabilizers with a
high penetration of wind power.
Power system stability studies could be classiﬁed according to the associated system parameters
aﬀecting the opposing forces in the power system. Figure 1.1 shows the broad classiﬁcation of power
system stability study.

The Nordic power system geographically is the North European power network comprising of Sweden,
Norway, Finland and Denmark. Latvian and Estonian power network has not been considered because
they are connected through HVDC lines. The Nordic-32 model proposed in the January 1995 CIGRE
Task force report-“Long term Dynamics-Phase-II” [12] has been used in this thesis to analyze the
small signal stability for the Nordic power system with a high share of renewable energy in future.
The possible share of renewable energy penetration in the Nordic power system as on 2035 has been
envisioned based on relevant literature and incorporated in the Nordic-32 model.
To understand the Nordic Power system in terms of renewable penetration levels by 2035 it was

Commission, EnergiNet, Swedish energy agency, Svenska kraftn¨at (Swedish TSO), Stattnet (Norwegian

1. Global climate action: Limiting global temperature rises.

Dynamic Wind turbine models
PSS®E uses both generic and vendor speciﬁc wind turbine models. This report however has only

proprietary model used to emulate the same characteristics and behaviour of a speciﬁc type of wind
turbine. The Western Electricity Coordinating Council (WECC) created the WT4 generic model to
simulate the performance of a FSC wind turbine connected to the grid [25]. These generic models are
suﬃcient to study bulk grid integration studies however these are less accurate for speciﬁc analysis like
frequency responses, synthetic inertia, wind curtailment studies etc [26].
PSS®E Type-4 Wind turbines generic models are representative of two types of turbines :- 2.5 MW GE
turbine and 2.3 MW Siemens turbine. This report however considers the 2.3MW turbine parameters as
mentioned in PSS®E documentation [27]. In PSS®E, the type-4 full converter interfaced wind turbine
generic model has two modules [28].
1. Generator/Converter model (WT4G2).
2. Electrical (Converter) control model (WT4E2).
The distinctive aspects regarding PSS®E generic wind turbine models from the perspective of modeling
and stability studies are:
• Mechanical state variables like rotor angle are not present in WT4 generic models.
• Transient behaviour of synchronous generators is dominated by rotor ﬂux behaviour. However
rotor ﬂux dynamics are not present in WT4 models instead the converter response is modeled in
response to converter controls.
• In PSS®E, during dynamic simulation, the conventional synchronous generator is modeled as a
Norton current source (ISORCE) whose values changed instantaneously with rotor ﬂux linkages.
In case of WT4G2 also, it is modeled as a ISORCE whose value depends on active current
commands (IP,cmd) and reactive current command (IQ,cmd) from WT4E2.
• P and Q control in WT4 models are completely decoupled. Mostly the dominant power control in
Type-4 wind turbines is the Reactive power control. As the turbine and generator are completely
decoupled from the grid, it can operate over a wide wind speed range and the rotor side converter
operates at variable frequencies.

MODAL ANALYSIS OF NORDIC-32 MODEL
This section shall investigate upon the Eigenvalue (Modal) analysis of the Nordic-32 system using
PSS®E NEVA platform. These analysis shall be the baseline for the optimal placement and tuning
of the Power system stabilizers of the poorly damped inter-area modes in the Nordic-32 model with
increasing degree of wind farm penetration.
The analysis in this section has been carried out for three cases:
• Case-1: Modal analysis of Nordic-32 without PSS and no wind farms.
• Case-2: Modal analysis of Nordic-32 without PSS and south grid thermal generators converted
to wind farms.
• Case-3: Modal analysis of Nordic-32 without PSS and all thermal generators converted into wind
farms.
PSS®E NEVA works inside PSS®E NETOMAC 15.0 and it calculates all the modes, Eigenvectors
(controllability and observability), Participation factors etc. Figure 6.1 shows the complex plane
representation of modes in NEVA.

The Nordic-32 model in this case only has hydro-generation in the North and thermal generation in the
south and central regions. The modal analysis was done in NEVA. At this stage, only electro-mechanical
oscillations are of interest. The following methodology for segregating electro-mechanical oscillations
has been adopted in this report.
• Loading the PSS®E .sav and executing load ﬂow to ensure the normal state of the Nordic-32
system. Loading the .dyr ﬁle and then clicking on “Launch Eigenvalue analysis” under Dynamics
tab in PSS®E.
• A total of 175 modes were listed. Modes denoted with green circles (as per ﬁgure 6.6) are the
stable modes and the ones in red color are considered insuﬃciently damped or unstable.
• In a particular mode, if rotor angle state variable has highest participation factor can be considered
as an electro-mechanical oscillation. If the oscillation frequency is within 0.1 - 0.9 Hz it has been
considered as global (inter-area oscillation) and if within 0.9 - 2 Hz then it is a local oscillations
(Inter-plant or inter-machine).
• Phase and magnitude of right Eigenvectors of rotor angle state variable in NEVA are calculated
and the oscillating generators are determined from the mode shapes. Right Eigenvectors give the
mode shape which is the amount of activity of a state variable has when a particular mode is
excited. These mode shapes shall be helpful to determine groups of oscillating generators and
their nature of cohesion ( oscillating with or against each other).

PLACEMENT OF POWER SYSTEM STABILIZERS
Linear control theories are widely used in PSS control system analysis and tuning. Before this, the
source of power system oscillations and the possible damping strategy should be discussed. Power
system oscillations occurs as dynamics set in motion due to mismatch of generator shaft mechanical
power and electrical power produced.
The generator produced electrical torque constitutes two
components:
• Synchronous torque: the component generator electrical torque proportional to change in rotor
angle (δ).
• Damping torque: the component generator electrical torque proportional to change in rotor speed
(ω).
The ultimate aim of the PSS is to add an auxiliary signal to the exciter module in phase with rotor
speed (ω) which will as a result produce a damping electrical torque. Also it is supposed to generate
a signal which has a leading phase in order to compensate for the negative phase introduced by the
generator-exciter system. This has been shown in ﬁgure 7.1.
Figure 7.1. Heﬀron-Phillips Model of Single machine inﬁnite bus small signal model [32][33]
This concept shall be revisited in detail for the PSS tuning in the following chapter. This chapter shall
be about the optimal placement of the PSS which shall be found with the help of residues. A brief

PSS TUNING
After the determination of PSS placement, this chapter explains the tuning of power system stabilizers.
PSS are supposed to damp out small rotor angle excursions and they are not intended to enhance the
transient stability (large rotor angle stability) of the system [43]. As discussed in chapter 7, the whole
idea behind stabilizing power system oscillations in the range of 0.1-1.9 Hz is by injecting an auxiliary
signal inside the excitation system which is in phase with rotor speed and adds to the electrical damping
torque. Ideally, a PSS should have good performance for local modes and inter-area modes under all
operating conditions [37]. Usually the Root-locus and phase compensation techniques are used in PSS
tuning [37]. But in this project the stabilizers shall be more focused on the inter-area modes using
phase compensation technique. Tuning a PSS is done in two steps:
• Estimating the generator-exciter frequency response characteristics for deciding lagging phase
characteristics.
• Estimating the parameters of PSS ﬁlters, washout ﬁlter, lead lag network to give appropriate
response at target frequencies.

CONCLUSION
This thesis presents the small signal stability analysis of Nordic-32 model with high inclusion level of
wind farms using PSS®E software. The penetration level of wind power in the Nordic-32 model was
taken at the maximum possible level and also it was found that the present energy policies directed
the inclusion of renewable energy. Statistics showed that among the Nordic countries, Denmark has
the highest share of renewable penetration. It was observed that future Nordic power system would be
a more inclusive and distributed power system with high share of wind energy.
It was observed that during load ﬂow simulation of Nordic-32 system, due to conversion of conventional
synchronous generators (thermal power plants) into wind power plants, reactive power insuﬃciency
sometimes leads to non-convergence issues. Voltages at the load buses in the south grid fell drastically.
Therefore planning for suﬃcient reactive power in the grid for maintaining suﬃcient bus voltages is a
critical aspect. Usually wind turbines inject reactive power according to their power capability curves
and they maintain the PCC voltages as an obligation under ancillary services.
Certain large signal disturbances like generation outages, line outages etc were implemented in Nordic-
32 model. It was found that it is already a stressed system. For example, outage of 1200 MW gen-4047
leads to frequency collapse in the system. Also outage of 400kV line between bus-4031 and bus-4041
line leads to almost central and North grid getting islanded. The eﬀect of over-excitation limiters on
ceiling the ﬁeld current injection above a certain upper limit and its implication on system stability
was observed.
In transient cases with wind farms, the issue of spikes in the power and voltage output plots was
observed. It was found that real current command of WT4G2 model is curtailed and reactive current
command is increased to a user-deﬁned limit “Max_FRT_Iq” during faults (situations of low voltages).
This is how FRT is implemented in WT4G2 and WT4E2. Also during faults, the converter current
limiting controllers switch from P-Priority to Q-Priority mode. It was found that the spikes were
considerably reduced by lowering the voltage threshold for FRT known as “FRT_Thres” in PSS®E
WT4E2 model. Also the spikes were considerably reduced when the total current capability of the
converter denoted as “I_MAX,TD” in PSS®E WT4E2 model is increased. No conclusive eﬀect of
dynamic simulation step size with numerical instability of wind turbine models were found.
In the modal analysis of Nordic-32, unfortunately wind turbine states were not found in the PSS®E
NEVA module output data. Three cases with varying degree of wind penetration were considered. It
was observed that with increasing wind power in the system, the inter-area mode frequency increases.
A 0.949 Hz inter-area mode was observed between the equivalent grid and North grid generators in
case-3 with all thermal generators converted into wind farms. Also the number of inter-area modes
decreased with increasing penetration of wind farms.
A speed input stabilizer model STAB1 was tuned for damping out these inter-area modes. PSS®E
NEVA was used to ﬁnd out the residues for deciding the placement of PSS. Unfortunately the Residue
angles of generator-exciter transfer function were either inconsistent or could not be deciphered. Four
PSS designs (PSS-1, PSS-2, PSS-3 and PSS-4) were tested. PSS-4 with the largest leading phase had
the maximum damping eﬀect on generator rotor oscillations and power oscillations. However the rotor
swings with PSS-4 also increased which suggested with PSS designs, a trade-oﬀbetween transient
stability and small signal stability has to be done. The optimal PSS design would be the one with the
accurate phase compensation damping out certain speciﬁc modes without compromising the transient
stability of the machine. In this regard, it is necessary to limit the PSS output to take care of these
80 of 94

aspects. Although due to the fact that it could not be ensured that whether the PSS designs were
compensating the actual generator-exciter lagging phase characteristics due to inconsistent PSS®E
NEVA residue angle data, the perfect PSS design could not be suggested. However considering the
simulations done in this report, PSS-3 design seems to be the better choice.
