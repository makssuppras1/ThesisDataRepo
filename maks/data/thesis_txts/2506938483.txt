Abstract
Pneumatic systems have been around for many hundreds of years and oﬀer
a way to transmit power with a clean medium that is readily available as air
is all around us. Even though pneumatic actuators and proportional valves
have been around for a long time they are rarely used for servo systems such
as positioning systems. Diﬃculties in controlling such systems arise from the
inherently nonlinear dynamics that are associated with pressure dynamics, air
compressibility, and air ﬂow through valves. This thesis explores ways to model
and control pneumatic servo systems and aims to gain a better understanding
of their behaviour and how this knowledge can be used to predict and improve
the performance of such systems. This is done by reviewing the state of the
art in research literature and examining methods for modelling and controlling
pneumatic servo systems. Based on these ﬁndings a nonlinear mathematical
model has been developed that accurately predicts the behaviour of a combined
pneumatic system consisting of a directional proportional valve and a linear
pneumatic actuator. Several methods for estimating the model parameters
are reviewed and applied to an experimental setup that is used for model
identiﬁcation as well as validation of the mathematical model. A sliding mode
controller has been developed and has successfully been implemented on the
experimental setup.

1
Introduction
1.1
Brief overview
Pneumatic systems have long been used in a variety of industrial processes. Together
with hydraulic and electrical systems, pneumatic systems is one of the most common
ways to transfer power in industrial settings. While compressed air systems have
been around for many hundreds of years modern pneumatics probably started in
the 1800’s where compressed air was produced and distributed on industrial scales.
The advent of computers in the late 1900’s has made digitally controlled pneumatic
components possible which has now become a standard for automated industrial
systems.
Before the mid 1900’s, pneumatic actuators had largely been used only with on-oﬀ
valve-control between endstops and few attempts had been made at continuous valve
control. The work conducted by Shearer [1] is one of the ﬁrst to study the dynamic
characteristics of proportional valves for pneumatic actuators with the purpose of
continuous control. Even though servo-/electropneumatic systems have been on
the market since the 1990’s [2], pneumatic servo technology only found limited use
outside research laboratories in the late 1990’s [3]. Today, microcontroller-based
systems are also available. Modern research in electropneumatic systems is largely
driven by the availability of cheaper and faster electronics, development of nonlinear
control methods, interest in automated industrial systems and robotics. Even so,
motion control of pneumatic systems is rarely done and is generally deemed diﬃcult
to accomplish due to the very nonlinear behaviour of such systems.
1.2
Brief outline of current research
Using pneumatic actuators for precise control of force or positioning is a topic that
has been actively researched since the 1950’s with various works by Shearer conducted
at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology ([1], [4]). The 1990’s saw a resurgence of
research on continuous control of pneumatic actuators in low pressure servo systems
which is still an actively studied topic. As pneumatic cylinders themselves are
relatively simple components most research focus on the valves that control the ﬂow
of air in the pneumatic systems. The examined literature generally ﬁtted into one or
more of the following categories: 1) modelling and experimental studies for better
analysis and optimized design, 2) applications of control theory for better dynamic
performance, 3) control optimization to minimize air consumption, 4) new materials,
hardware and applications. Much of the current research falls into one or more of
these categories. Some of these articles are brieﬂy reviewed here.
1.2.1
Modelling and experimental studies
A very elaborate model for a pneumatic system controlled by a single proportional
control valve is developed in [5]. The model is mostly based on physical laws and
neglects few things in the system. As many sub-components are included, the model
is complex with respect to computation but is a good reference model to understand
the diﬀerent parts of the system. In addition to the piston dynamics, the model also
includes a theoretical model for ﬂow rates through the valve, time delay eﬀects and
the ﬂow attenuation through the tubes connecting valve and cylinder.
5
==PAGE:10==
The dynamic behaviour of a pneumatic cylinder have been tested in a hydraulic
test machine by [6] who propose using a linear second-order model for the pressure
dynamics and another nonlinear model when the cylinder is used as a pneumatic
spring.
Other similar systems use solenoid valves which are either open or closed. These
valves are simpler than proportional valves and can be controlled with pulse-width-
modulation (PWM) to adjust the ﬂow through the valve. [7] and [8] propose diﬀerent
models for these PWM-controlled solenoid valves, which allows the use of analytical
methods for designing the control system.
Many studies concern the use of pneumatic muscle actuators which have applica-
tions in robotics and orthotics due to the low component weight and compliance of
the air. The muscle actuators are generally characterized by being two end pieces
that are connected by a ﬂexible membrane. When the volume is ﬁlled with air the
membrane expands laterally which contracts the distance between the end pieces
thereby actuating a motion. In [9] a model and control law is developed for a tracking
system that uses two pneumatic muscle actuators and 5/3 directional proportional
valve. Such a system is very similar to using a rodless cylinder.
Special dynamic eﬀects inherent to linear pneumatic actuators for positioning
have also been well studied. One is the ”sticking and restarting” phenomenon [2]
which is a variant of the commonly known stick-slip phenomenon. The main point
in the article is that if the mechanical subsystem (pneumatic cylinder) has reached
an equilibrium the pressure dynamics may still evolve and restart the motion; even
if the target position is reached with no static error. The article tries to establish
theoretical conditions for this phenomenon.
Characterization of the oriﬁce area/sonic conductance of the valve requires
knowledge of the mass ﬂow rate. Direct measurement for all positions is a very work
intensive procedure that also requires additional equipment. Alternative methods
for determining the mass ﬂow rate through the valve are presented in [10], [11] and
[12] where the mass ﬂow rate is estimated using pressurization curves.
1.2.2
Group 2: Controllers
Many of the advancements in nonlinear control methods from the late 1900s have
incited new research that tries to apply them to various nonlinear systems; pneumatic
systems included. The methods are well developed and comprehensively described
in [13] and [14]. Both books are good references for the topic. Advantages of
applying nonlinear control methods include consideration of nonlinear dynamics,
increased performance and robustness. This often comes at the cost of needing more
computation, increased complexity, lack of ﬂexibility and need for more sensors.
Sometimes a few sensors can be replaced by state observers at the cost of increased
time to meet the control objective.
Many apply new control approaches for pneumatic actuators, such as feedback
linearization [15], cascaded control [9], backstepping [16], sliding mode control [8, 17],
or combinations hereof [18]. Naturally, linear theory is also frequently applied [19].
1.2.3
Group 3: Energy eﬃciency
As pneumatic systems are relatively simple the main costs are associated with
operation. Some researchers have studied how to use the air more eﬃciently in
6
==PAGE:11==
open-loop controlled motion tasks and thereby saving energy. In [20] ﬁve 2-way
control valves are used in a Wheatstone bridge structure to completely control the
ﬂow directions. They employ optimization to minimize the use of air from the supply.
The authors report that the main part of air savings comes from better usage of the
expansion energy. In [21] other valve arrangements are tested and typical motion
objectives are included as well. The authors report between 27%-29% air savings in
comparison to a meter-out controlled system.
The research indicates that simple open-loop motion tasks can beneﬁt from
applying optimized valve switching in terms of compressed air savings. The main
reason is the better usage of expansion energy.
1.3
The work in this thesis
A review of the literature indicates that control systems for pneumatic actuators
have come a long way for a variety of applications. Studies have reported that the
inherent nonlinear behaviour of pneumatic actuators are best dealt with by applying
nonlinear control methods that can account for the uncertainties in the model. Most
research have tested at most two controllers for the same setup.
In all the reviewed literature, air temperature is dealt with as a constant. Depend-
ing on the sensitivity to temperature variations, this assumption should be validated.
Humidity is dealt with by drying the air after compression. This is already done in
most industrial systems to increase the service life of the components.
The work in this thesis focuses on understanding the system through analysis of a
mathematical model, developing a controller for tracking a sinusoidal trajectory and
validating the model and controller through experiments. This is done by developing
a reference model of a linear pneumatic actuator, mainly based oﬀthe work of [5].
The model parameters are estimated for an experimental setup, which will be used
for validating the model and testing various controllers.
The impact of parameter variation due to temperature is theoretically investigated
by using the obtained model.
Linear control and nonlinear control approaches are considered and implemented
on an experimental setup for comparison.

2
Mathematical model
The pneumatic system shown on ﬁg. 1 consists of a double-acting cylinder and a 5/3
proportional valve. As two of the ﬁve ports are meant as exhaust ports, such a valve
is often called a four-way valve. Two pressure sensors measure the pressure at the
inlets of the cylinder, and another sensor measures the position of the piston inside
the cylinder.
Figure 1: The pneumatic system.
A basic model of the pneumatic system consists of an equation describing the
forces acting on the piston-rod, two equations to describe how the pressure varies in
each chamber, and a relation between the valve input, e.g. voltage or current, and
the mass ﬂow rate of air through the valve.
One could in principle characterize the whole system by doing experiments on
the separate components and ﬁtting parameters to the observed dynamics. The
pressure dynamics are usually modelled as a ﬁrst-order system as the equations
predict, but one could use a second-order system as suggested in [6]. A complete
experimental characterization is complicated, however, as the response depends on
several parameters which may vary during the process, such as the gas temperature
at several stages.
For model-based control, a model based on physical principles is usually of more
use to the designer as it gives insight into the dynamics and certain dependencies
inevitably show up. The eﬀects from simplifying the equations may even be analysed.
For this system, a basic model can be derived by applying Newton’s second law
to the piston-rod, and applying the ideal gas law, conservation of mass/continuity
equation and the energy equation on a control volume to describe the pressure
dynamics. The air ﬂow through the valve can be modelled using a variation of the
oriﬁce equation or characterized empirically.
Several approaches have been taken in diﬀerent studies. Even though many are
concerned with a system largely identical to the one presented in ﬁg. 1, few of the
models are identical. The main diﬀerences are in how the pressure dynamics are
treated and how the air ﬂow through the valve is modelled. The derivation done
in [5] is among the more detailed and presents the key aspects in modelling such
8
==PAGE:13==
systems from a theoretical standpoint. The model derived here is largely similar with
some simpliﬁcations and alterations, e.g. diﬀerent model for the mass ﬂow rate. The
ﬁnal model consists of four diﬀerential equations: Two for the piston-load dynamics
and one for each air chamber to describe the pressure dynamics.
2.1
Piston-load dynamics
The double-acting pneumatic cylinder can be modelled considering the piston-load
dynamics as well as the pressure dynamics in both chambers.
Figure 2: Axial forces acting on the piston.
The piston is modelled as a mass, mp, that can move in one dimension relative to
the housing. Therefore only the axial motion and forces are considered. The position
x has been taken from the middle of the stroke with positive direction outwards (in
extension). Figure 2 shows the axial forces acting on the piston-rod. The piston-rod
has an additional rigidly connected load mass, mL, and the load exerts a force FL
on the piston. The load force can be gravitational load, additional friction e.g. from
guide rails. The chamber pressures are assumed to be uniform in each chamber. The
pressure p1 acts on the circular area A1 and the pressure p2 acts on the annular area
A2. The ambient pressure Pe acts on the rod area Ar outside the cylinder. The
pressures are absolute pressures. Friction forces are present at the seals of both the
cylinder-piston interface and at the cylinder-rod interface. The sum of these friction
forces are denoted Ff, which represent dissipative forces such as viscous damping
and dry friction.

2.3
Valve ﬂow models
A simple model of the 5/3 proportional spool valve consists of an equation relating
the mass ﬂow rates to the spool position and an equation relating the spool position
to the electrical input. The latter equation may include the spool dynamics such as a
mass-spring-damper actuated by a force. The control chain is that the spool position
deﬁnes the oriﬁce areas that throttle the air ﬂow. The limitation of using only one
valve is that both the intake and exhaust areas are controlled simultaneously by a
single input.
The mass ﬂow rate through the valve can be modelled in several ways. The
general oriﬁce equation is often used to relate the ﬂow rate to the pressure drop
across an oriﬁce. This works especially well for incompressible ﬂows. For pneumatic
components, the ﬂow can reach high velocities and the equations vary depending
on whether the ﬂow is choked/sonic or subsonic. Theoretical models exist for some
simple oriﬁces, which have inspired empirical models that are better able to describe
pneumatic components. Among these are the standards ISO 6358, JIS B 8390
and IEC 60534. These also specify possible test procedures for use in the industry.
All of the models describe the steady state behaviour of the ﬂow rate and rely on
experimental data to determine the parameters for the speciﬁc component.
A few of the possible models are presented in this section.
Instead of specifying mass ﬂow rates directly in units of mass per time, they are
usually converted to the equivalent volumetric ﬂow rate at a speciﬁed temperature,
pressure and gas constant. The volume is often referred to as either standard liters
or normal liters. Here the term normal liters will be used and the ﬂow rate given in
normal liters per minute ([Nl/min] or [Nlpm]). The ISO 6358 reference conditions
are p0 = 1 bar, T0 = 20 ◦C and R = 288 J kg−1 K. The conversion from volumetric
ﬂow rate at speciﬁed conditions to mass ﬂow rate is done with the ideal gas equation

2.3.3
Input-aﬃne ﬂow model
For control purposes, the two previous models have the disadvantage that the valve
input (voltage or spool position) is not directly present in the equations, but is
nonlinearly related with the sonic conductance or oriﬁce area. Being able to directly
invert the function to solve for the desired input voltage that results in a certain ﬂow
rate can be of advantage. This model is used in [23] to ﬁt measured ﬂow rates to
polynomial functions. The form has been chosen such that the input appears linearly
(aﬃne input). The model is of the form
w(Pp, u) = φ(Pp) + ψ(Pp, sgn(u)) u
(18)
where both functions φ and ψ can be polynomials or another ﬁtting function. In the
article, the functions are ﬁtted for a speciﬁc supply and exhaust pressure. Pp is the
pressure at one of the working ports of the valve, and u is the voltage deviation from
the midpoint. The function φ(Pp) represents the leakages when the valve is closed
at u = 0. The function ψ(Pp, sgn(u)) is a piecewise function that together with u
represents how the ﬂow rate depends on the port pressure and voltage.
Depending on how this model is used it can oﬀer advantages or disadvantages. A
disadvantage of the model is that it is purely empirical so the physical characteristics
of the ﬂow are not represented. This means that the inﬂuence from parameter
variations can not be examined. For implementation, the validity of the ﬁt heavily
depends on the quality and amount of data and the ﬁtting function used. In [23],

2.7
Model summary
The model has a few critical assumptions. The ﬂow dynamics in the chambers
have been ignored as each chamber is treated as a homogeneous volume of air. The
pressure changes in the chambers have been assumed to be separate polytropic
processes for charging, discharging and volume change due to piston motion. The
error introduced by this assumption is bounded but is not necessarily constant.
The incoming air is assumed to be at the same temperature as the chamber air, so
there is only one temperature term. The ﬂow rate model is based on steady state
considerations and does not account for dynamic eﬀects of the ﬂow. For the steady
state pressure where ˙pi = 0, the input and output ﬂows, wi,in and wi,out, are not
necessarily zero but might cancel each other when leakages are signiﬁcant. As this
leakage ﬂow is internal in the valve and there is no actual ﬂow through the port, the
mass ﬂow function may not be valid. This can of course lead to an increased error
between the dynamical response and the steady state response.
For the connecting tubes, the time delay is around 0.3 ms for each 10 cm of tubing.
Therefore it is deemed reasonable to neglect the time delay for shorter tubes. In the
test setup, the tubing are generally kept to less than 30 cm between the valve and
the cylinder which is deemed short. For the setup used in this work, the attenuation
coeﬃcient has been examined and found to take a minimum value of φ = 0.74. This
only happens for a very brief moment, while during operation it is mostly φ > 0.96.
Therefore it is also deemed negligible.
Based on bandwidth considerations, the valve spool dynamics can be neglected
when the goal is slow motion tracking.
The supply pressure will be assumed constant, which means that the dynamics
of the pressure regulating valve are neglected. Fast variations may occur just when
the ﬂow rate jumps from 0 to a high value or vice versa. However, in applications
where the supply pressure is digitally monitored it may easily be incorporated.

Model identiﬁcation
In this section, the various parameters in the model are identiﬁed for the experimental
setup. Some parameters can be found from data sheets, such as the piston-rod mass
or nominal values for the mass ﬂow rate through the valve under speciﬁed conditions.
Load masses can often be measured directly on a scale or estimated in CAD software.
Dynamic friction between the cylinder and piston has been estimated with
two experiments. Initially, the friction coeﬃcients have been determined in a test
where the piston is roughly moving at a constant velocity. Afterwards, the friction
coeﬃcients are estimated during tracking operation with a proportional controller.
The magnitude of static friction is initially estimated in a simple experiment where
the piston-rod is pulled with a calibrated spring.
Leakage eﬀects are examined by measuring the steady state pressures in the
cylinder chambers at various supply pressures.
The sonic conductances are estimated in two separate experiments.
In one
experiment the ﬂow rates are measured using ﬂowmeters while varying the pressure
at the work port of the valve. In the other experiment the pneumatic cylinder is used
as two constant volume chambers, and the time series of the charging/discharging
curves are used to estimate the sonic conductances.

3.2
Measurement uncertainties
The overall uncertainty of the measurements are aﬀected by all the uncertainties in
the signal path from sensor to DAC. In this section, the accuracies of the components
are examined and the impact on the measurements is analyzed.
The AD module (NI 9229) has a bit depth of 24 bit over the range ±60 V. This
corresponds to a resolution of (120 V)/224 ≈7 µV. With the pressure calibrations in
eq. (75) this results in a pressure resolution of 0.7 Pa. The pressure sensors have a
general accuracy of ±0.3%FS (typ.) which is roughly ±2 kPa. The actual accuracy
has been found to be more in the range ±0.2 kPa or better.
The acceleration data is limited to accuracy of the handheld calibrator which
outputs a sinusoidal signal with an rms acceleration of 10 m s−2 ± 3% at an angular
velocity of 1000 rad/s ± 0.02%. Values below the calibration level therefore have up
to ±3% accuracy, whereas values above the calibration level have a worse accuracy.

3.4
Static friction
The static friction is important to know as it causes stiction. If the static friction
is large enough it may cause problems for both setpoint control and tracking. In
the former case it can cause the piston to stop too early which results in a non-zero
error. In the latter case it can cause hysteresis together with the dynamic friction.
As the speciﬁc contact area is unknown, it is more feasible to estimate the friction
force (Fs) itself rather than a friction coeﬃcient.
A simple test has been carried out to estimate the static friction. The purpose is
not to do a full statistical analysis of the friction force, but to get a quick estimate
of its magnitude. This has been done with the tubes disconnected from the cylinder
and the cylinder ﬁxed horizontally. A spring balance up to 2500 g has been mounted
to the rod and force applied manually by hand. The force has been increased at
a slow steady rate with the force applied coaxially with the piston. The scale is
observed visually and the last value before the stiction is overcome is the measurement
point. The measured mass value has been multiplied by g = 9.82 m s−2 to yield the
equivalent weight (F = mg).
The friction has been examined at the endpoint x1 = −50 mm and at the midpoint
x1 = 0 mm. Due to the constraints of the setup, the force can only be applied
outwards/in extension. Using these two positions may indicate what magnitude of
friction is to be expected.
Some results are shown in table 2, where the mean force is shown together with
minimum and maximum measured mass values to give an indication of the range.
The data for the midpoint suggests that the static friction also depends on whether
the piston is returned to the midpoint from the negative or positive direction. This
indicates that the static friction is not constant for the cylinder. At the midpoint
the static friction ranges from roughly 13 N to 16 N. For the cylinder, this roughly
corresponds to a pressure diﬀerence of 9 kPa = 0.09 bar away from equilibrium

3.6
Pressurization test - step inputs
An experiment has been set up where the piston-rod can be ﬁxed at a certain position.
The setup is outlined in ﬁg. 15. Practically, the piston-rod has been ﬁxed to a slotted
cast iron table using a right angle bracket and an end piece for the rod end. During
tests the rod moved at most 0.1 mm which corresponds to a negligible diﬀerence in
chamber volumes. The chamber volumes V1(x1) and V2(x2) are essentially constant.
The piston seals of nitrile rubber are found to not leak air between the chambers.
The supplied (dry) air is at around 10 bar, which is dropped to 7 bar or below by
the pressure regulating valve. The pressures at both cylinder inlets are measured.
Figure 15: Test setup with ﬁxed piston-rod.
This setup can be used to examine steady state pressures and pressurization times
for a ﬁxed position, various supply pressures and various lengths of tubing. The
steady state pressures are here also called dead head pressures as the working ports
are ”dead-headed” into a closed-oﬀchamber. For an ideal proportional spool valve
with no leakages, the pressures will eventually reach either the supply pressure or
the exhaust pressure. A real proportional spool valve, however, has leakages due to
necessary clearance between the spool and bore. The internal ﬂow paths then result
in a region where the pressures are in between the supply and exhaust pressures. The
dead head pressure curve can also be used to ﬁnd the valve’s null position (where
the two pressures are equal).
29
==PAGE:34==
During measurement the time series of U(t), p1(t), p2(t) and x1(t) have been
recorded. The time series of the position can be omitted as the variation is negligible.
The input voltage is stepped back and forth between U < 5 V and U > 5 V, but only
after the pressures have reached steady state.
In initial tests it was quickly found that the proportional valve has a region at
U ∈[4, 6]V where leakages are more signiﬁcant. For this reason, more measurements
have been made in this region.
To determine the operating characteristics of the valve and pressure dynamics an
experiment has been made with the piston-rod ﬁxed. In such a setup the motion
dynamics can be ignored and the only inﬂuence of the position is on the chamber
volumes Vi(x1). Practically, this has been accomplished by mounting a ﬁxture to the
rod end and then ﬁxate it to the test bench.
During the experiment, the valve setpoint was varied between U < 5 V to U > 5 V,

setpoints close to the mid-position U ∈[4, 6]V, the steady state pressure does not
reach the supply pressure on either side. Instead, the pressures reach intermediate
values which are congruent with the leakages.

3.7
Pressurization test - Sinusoidal input
Inspired by two articles [24] and [12], another experiment is set up exploring another
way to determine deadhead pressures. It is in most aspects similar to the pressur-
ization test with a ﬁxed position. Instead of a stepped input signal, the setpoint
voltage is swept using a sine wave with a large period. In the aforementioned articles,
they employ a slow sinusoidal signal with the goal of examining the dead zone of the
valve. They have tested it with periods of 100 s and 40 s respectively. This test has

3.8
Flow rate - direct measurement
The most obvious method to estimate the sonic conductance is to directly measure
the mass ﬂow rates through diﬀerent ports of the valve for various spool positions
(setpoint voltages) and various pressures. It is impractical and expensive to do
this for every possible combination of ports, pressures and spool positions. Instead,
measurements can be done between the supply port and the working ports and
perhaps also between working ports and the corresponding exhaust ports.
The measurements are as close to operational conditions as possible and have
been made for port 2. The results can be used as a reference and likely extrapolated
to port 4. The test setup for measuring the ﬂow characteristics of the valve is shown
on ﬁg. 21. The air supply pressure, which is about 10 bar, is regulated down with
two pressure regulators. The upper one in the diagram is used to regulate the valve’s
supply pressure at port 1, which is kept at 7±0.005 bar. The other pressure regulator
is used to adjust the pressure at the working port for the measurements with negative
ﬂow rate; otherwise it is shut oﬀ. The ﬁlter regulator in the MS4-LFR is redundant
as the air has already been ﬁltered. An adjustable ﬂow control valve is used in
conjunction with a silencer to increase ﬂow resistance and increase the pressure at
port 2 for positive ﬂow rates. The absolute pressures at port 1 and 2 of the valve are
measured with pressure transmitters.
Variable area ﬂow meters are used to measure the ﬂow rates. These actually
measure the volumetric ﬂow rate but the scale is calibrated to measure mass ﬂow rate
at a speciﬁed pressure and temperature. Data sheets for variable area ﬂowmeters

The sliding mode controller has been implemented in LabVIEW for the test setup.

reference; albeit with a slight lag. While the deadzone compensated proportional
controller is currently able to track the reference with less lag, this may soon change
as the implementation is reﬁned and the gains have been adjusted.

6
Conclusion
In this work a model has been developed for a pneumatic system consisting of a
5/3-way directional proportional spool valve and a linear pneumatic actuator. The
ﬁnal model consists of a second-order diﬀerential equation for the piston dynamics,
two ﬁrst-order diﬀerential equations for the pressure dynamics in the chambers of
the cylinder, and an extra equation describing the mass ﬂow rate through the ports
of the valve. The model has been found to capture the overall behaviour of the
system well, especially outside of the valve’s deadzone. The cushioning mechanism
at the ends of the cylinder stroke has not been modelled which makes simulations
at the last 22 mm of stroke diﬃcult. The inﬂuence of temperature variations in the
chambers has been considered and found not to give any signiﬁcant errors. Multiple
approaches for describing the mass ﬂow rate has been considered and the ﬂow rate
function from the ISO 6358 standard has been found to be the most useful as it
captures the characteristics of the valve.
The main part of the work has been related to the experimental identiﬁcation of
the model parameters. Friction in the actuator and the parameters in the mass ﬂow
rate model has been found to be the most critical and troublesome parameters to
identify. The mass ﬂow rate through the valve has been investigated both directly
and indirectly. By measuring the ﬂow rates at a working port, the sonic conductance
(oriﬁce area) in both directions have been shown to have the same magnitude and
agrees with the manufacturer’s nominal values. The mass ﬂow rates have also been
estimated indirectly by charging and discharging chambers of ﬁxed volume and using
the modelled pressure dynamics to solve for the mass ﬂow rate. This method has
been found to oﬀer many advantages compared to direct ﬂow measurement. However,
52
==PAGE:57==
the indirect estimates of the sonic conductances does not agree perfectly with those
acquired from direct measurement. A signiﬁcant variation has been found between
the sonic conductances for the charging and discharging processes. This may perhaps
be repeated in further work.
Dynamic friction has been estimated during operation with constant velocity tests
as well as sinusoidal motion. For the pneumatic cylinder in the current experimental
setup the friction force has been shown to exhibit signiﬁcant hysteresis.
This
has presented diﬃculties in estimating friction parameters. The viscous friction
component has been found to vary signiﬁcantly from 107 to 230 N s/m depending on
the method of identiﬁcation and the frequency of the tracking.
The model has been adjusted for use with nonlinear control methods and a sliding
mode controller has been derived for tracking purposes. Conditions have been set up
that can be used to tune the gain of the switching term for disturbance rejection.
The control law of the sliding mode controller has been implemented in LabVIEW,
though further tuning is required to reﬁne its performance. Proportional control
alone has been found to have poor performance if the spool overlap is signiﬁcant.
Implementing compensation for the deadzone has been found to be an easy way to
decrease the tracking error when piston changes direction.
In the future it may interesting to examine if observer models can be implemented
to assist in estimating the model inaccuracies. Such methods have already been
tested for other systems, so it may be interesting to see how they can apply for this
system. Such methods may perhaps be used to further increase the accuracy of the
parameters in the ﬂow function or alter the friction parameters.
In regards to the indirect ﬂow measurements, one idea that has not been ex-
plored for determining the sonic conductance (or oriﬁce area) is to combine the
charging/discharging with the deadhead pressure curves. These two procedures done
separately are still less time consuming than measuring the ﬂow rate. If the constant
volume charge/discharge experiment is done with both exhaust ports closed, the sonic
conductance from the supply port to work ports, Ci,in, can be estimated fairly well.
With the exhaust ports open, the deadhead pressure curves can be used to determine
the ratio between the input and output sonic conductance Ci,out/Ci,in = wi,in/wi,out.
Combining these methods, all four sonic conductances may be estimated to an
accuracy given by the charge/discharge experiment.
