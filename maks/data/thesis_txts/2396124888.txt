Abstract  
Probiotics have been suggested as a possible tool to prevent diseases in fish farms, which encompass risk of 
infections by pathogenic microorganisms. A potential probiotic is the marine bacterium Phaeobacter 
inhibens. The bacterium produces the antibiotic tropodithietic acid (TDA) as a secondary metabolite, and 
importantly, resistance towards TDA has been shown to be difficult to develop. This study focuses on 
secondary metabolites produced by Phaeobacter inhibens, specifically on a novel compound, methyl TDA 
(MeTDA).  
Analysis of LC-MS data from an extracted P. inhibens culture identified and characterised MeTDA. A 13C-
labelling experiment showed that MeTDA was a secondary metabolite and not an artefact. MeTDA was 
purified with SPE and semi-preparative HPLC. NMR experiments and a synthesis of MeTDA from TDA 
elucidated the structure as a TDA derived compound with a methyl substituent on sulphur. MeTDA 
inhibited Vibrio anguillarum in the same concentration range as TDA. This showed that MeTDA was an 
antibiotic like TDA.  
Inspired by the discovery of MeTDA, additional sulphur containing metabolites were looked for with 
Dynamic Cluster Analysis. Six sulphur containing compounds were identified, and comparison to the 13C-
labelling experiment showed that three were biosynthesised by P. inhibens. Four compounds were purified 
in small amounts and tested against Vibrio anguillarum. Inhibition zones were observed which suggested 
that P. inhibens produce more bioactive compounds than MeTDA and TDA. A purified compound with 
similar polarity to MeTDA was studied further. Structure characterisation indicated a molecular formula of 
C15H12O4S2 and preliminary structure elucidation investigations proposed a substituted TDA derived 
compound.  
Altogether, this master’s thesis has shown that MeTDA as well as several unknown bioactive sulphur 
containing metabolites likely related to TDA biosynthesis are produced by P. inhibens.

Resume  
Probiotika er blevet foreslået som et muligt redskab til at forebygge sygdomme i fiskelandbrug, der har 
risiko for infektioner af patogene mikroorganismer. Den marine bakterie Phaeobacter inhibens er et 
potentielt probiotika. Bakterien producerer antibiotikummet tropodithietic acid (TDA) som en sekundær 
metabolit, og det er blevet vist at resistens mod TDA ikke udvikles nemt, hvilket er vigtigt. Dette studie 
fokuserer på sekundære metabolitter produceret af Phaeobacter inhibens, specifikt på det nye stof, methyl 
TDA (MeTDA).  
MeTDA blev identificeret og karakteriseret ved analyse af LC-MS data fra en ekstraheret P. inhibens kultur. 
Et 13C-mærkningsforsøg viste at MeTDA var en sekundær metabolit og ikke et artefakt. MeTDA blev 
oprenset ved hjælp af SPE og semi-preparative HPLC. NMR eksperimenter og en syntese af MeTDA fra TDA 
pegede på at strukturen var et TDA afledt stof med en methyl substituent på svovl. MeTDA inhiberede 
Vibrio anguillarum i samme koncentrationsinterval som TDA. Det viste at MeTDA var et antibiotika, ligesom 
TDA er.  
Opdagelsen af MeTDA inspirerede til at lede efter yderligere svovlholdige metabolitter med Dynamic 
Cluster Analysis. Seks svovlholdige stoffer blev identificeret, og 13C-mærkningsforsøget viste at tre af 
stofferne blev biosyntetiseret af P. inhibens. Fire stoffer blev oprenset i små mængder og testet mod Vibrio 
anguillarum. Inhibitionszoner blev observeret hvilket antydede at P. inhibens producerer flere bioaktive 
stoffer end MeTDA og TDA. Et oprenset stof med polaritet magen til MeTDA blev undersøgt yderligere. 
Strukturkarakterisering indikerede en molekylformel på C15H12O4S2, og indledende strukturopklarings-
undersøgelser foreslog et substitueret TDA afledt stof.  
Alt i alt har dette kandidatspeciale vist at MeTDA såvel som flere ukendte, bioaktive, svovlholdige 
metabolitter, sandsynligvis relateret til TDA biosyntese, bliver produceret af P. inhibens.

1 Introduction 
Traditionally, the supply of fish has come from the capture of wild fish; however, during the last couple of 
decades there has been a significant rise in farmed fish. The rearing of fish in farms, aquaculture 
production, is a rapidly growing industry. In 2014, aquaculture production exceeded the amount of 
captured fish according to the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO, 2016). 
Overfishing has caused a decrease in the number of wild fish, and the supply of fish is therefore increasingly 
more dependent on aquaculture production. However, rearing of fish in fish farms encompasses risk of 
infection by pathogenic microorganisms which are often introduced with the feed (Olafsen, 2001). 
Previously, antibiotics were used to prevent diseases, but due to the increasing occurrence of resistant fish 
pathogens, it is not a sustainable solution (Cabello, 2006). Successful vaccine programmes have been 
developed for fish, but they cannot be applied to the fish larvae because their adaptive immune system has 
not developed at this stage (Bentzon-Tilia et al, 2016). Instead, probiotics have been suggested as a 
possible tool to prevent diseases. Several definitions of a probiotic have been formed, which in general 
suggest that a probiotic consists of live microorganisms and provides a desirable effect when added in a 
certain amount (Probiotics in food Health and nutritional properties and guidelines for evaluation, 2001).  
Bacteria of the Roseobacter group have been shown to antagonise bacterial fish pathogens such as Vibrio 
anguillarum, which is one of the Vibrio species that causes the greatest economical losses (Toranzo et al, 
2005). Roseobacters are highly abundant in coastal areas, and they occur naturally in aquacultures. 
Importantly, this means that they are able to sustain in these environments, which is the main criteria for 
their use as a probiotic in fish farms (Bentzon-Tilia & Gram, 2017). The Phaeobacter genus is a member of 
the Roseobacter group, and addition of these bacteria to aquaculture productions have shown to reduce 
the rate of mortality of fish larvae (D’Alvise et al, 2012). This probiotic effect is most likely due to the 
production of the secondary metabolite, tropodithietic acid (TDA), which has been shown to have an 
antibiotic effect (Brinkhoff et al, 2004; Tsubotani et al, 1984). The stage at which the probiotic bacteria are 
introduced is important for the mortality rate. Adding the bacteria before the pathogen gives the best 
results (D’Alvise et al, 2013), hence, future greater scale cultures will lower the mortality rates most 
efficiently if the probiotics are added early and are used to prevent the pathogens rather than adding them 
during the infection. Normally, this would not be a good solution due to the risk of resistance development; 
however, resistance towards TDA has been shown to be difficult to develop (Porsby et al, 2011). Overall, 
this makes Phaeobacter a promising probiotic. With the great potential that lies in Phaeobacter, it is 
interesting to study whether it produces other secondary metabolites with antibiotic effects. Besides TDA, 
only a few other metabolites of Phaeobacter have been found, where TDA is the most studied one of them. 
This study focuses on secondary metabolites produced by the bacterium Phaeobacter inhibens, specifically 
on a novel compound, methyl TDA.

2 Introduction to Phaeobacter inhibens  
2.1 Classification  
All living organisms are specified with an overall classification system. The kingdom of bacteria contains all 
bacteria, and beneath this level, a hierarchy of ranks are used to specify each of them. The most important 
ranks below kingdom are phylum, class, order, family, genus, and species (Ruggiero et al, 2017). In this 
project, the strain DSM17395 of the species Phaeobacter inhibens is studied.  
The classification system has changed several times over the years, and reclassification of an organism 
often happens when further analysis is performed. When P. inhibens was first identified, it was named 
Roseobacter gallaeciensis (Ruiz-Ponte et al, 1998). Later, the genus of the bacterium was reclassified as 
Phaeobacter due to noticeable differences in genes, pigmentation, antibiotic production, amongst other 
things (Martens, 2006). Finally, the bacterium was given a new species name when it was discovered that 
two strains both initially classified as Phaeobacter gallaeciensis actually represented two distinct species. 
Hence, the bacterium DSM17395 is at the moment classified as Phaeobacter inhibens.  
2.2 Symbiosis between P. inhibens and Emiliania huxleyi 
P. inhibens is a gram-negative marine bacterium (Ruiz-Ponte et al, 1998). In the oceans, P. inhibens often 
lives in symbiosis with the microscopic algae, Emiliania huxleyi. E. huxleyi can form great blooms in the 
ocean (Holligan et al, 1983), and  bacteria from the Roseobacter group are often co-occurring during these 
blooms (Gonzalez et al, 2000). From this correlation, an interaction between the algae and the bacteria was 
hypothesized (Seyedsayamdost (a) et al, 2011).  
The symbiosis consists of two parts, a mutualistic phase and a parasitic phase (Seyedsayamdost (a) et al, 
2011; Wang et al, 2016). In the mutualistic phase, P. inhibens produces the antibiotic tropodithietic acid 
(TDA), which protects the algae from pathogens. Furthermore, the bacteria make phenylacetic acid, which 
works as growth promoter for E. huxleyi. E. huxleyi produces dimethylsulfoniopropionate (DMSP) that the 
bacteria can use as a carbon and sulphur source. Also, the algae provide a surface for the bacteria to form 
biofilm on.  
When the alga grows old, it releases a breakdown product p-coumaric acid (pCA). At some concentration of 
pCA, P. inhibens gradually starts to produce roseobacticides instead of TDA (Seyedsayamdost (a) et al, 
2011; Seyedsayamdost (b) et al, 2011). Roseobacticides are potent algaecides, hence, the relationship 
changes to parasitic.

2.2.1 Discovery and Properties of Tropodithietic Acid 
Thiotropocin (Figure 1, structure (1)) and its antibiotic properties were identified in 1984 when it was 
isolated from the bacterium Pseudomonas sp. CB-104 from soil (Kintaka et al, 1984; Tsubotani et al, 1984). 
In 2003, TDA (Figure 1, structure (2)) was found as a secondary metabolite from marine bacteria of the 
Roseobacter group (Brinkhoff et al, 2004; Liang, 2003). At this stage, it was thought that TDA and 
Thiotropocin were two different compounds. Pseudomonas and bacteria from the Roseobacter group are 
classified under the phylum Proteobacteria, but hereafter they belong to distinct classes, which mean that 
they are phylogenetically not closely related. Later, computational studies showed that Thiotropocin and 
TDA exist as a pair of tautomers that can exchange easily via a [1,5]-hydrogen shift (shown in Figure 1) 
(Greer et al, 2008). Thiotropocin and TDA contain a flat seven-membered ring which is aromatic due to its 
delocalised π-bonds. A third structure, Troposulfenin, is possible (Figure 1, structure (3)) but the 
computations showed that it was less stable due to localised bonds, and a puckered ring.

Figure 1 shows the two structures of the antibiotic, thiotropocin and tropodithietic acid (TDA). Computational studies have 
shown that they exist as a pair of tautomers that exchange via a [1,5] hydrogen shift. In the same studies, a third possible 
structure was drawn, Troposulfenin, but it was shown to be less stable than TDA and thiotropocin due to localised bonds and a 
puckered ring.  
 
The antibiotic properties of TDA have been shown to be caused by its ability to collapse the proton motive 
force (Wilson et al, 2016). The proposed mode of action describes that TDA can diffuse through the cell 
membrane, where it releases its acidic proton in the cytosol because of the neutral pH (Figure 2). In the 
cell, TDA chelates to a monovalent cation, such as potassium, which it diffuses out of the cell with. Outside 
the cell, there is a high concentration of protons. Here, TDA releases the cation, binds a second proton, and 
repeats the cycle.

2.3 Natural Products 
All organisms have to convert the nutrients available to them into the metabolites that are essential for 
their survival and growth. Degradation of nutrients to building blocks and energy is known as the primary 
metabolism (Dewick, 2009). An example of this is the glycolysis, where carbohydrates like glucose are 
degraded to useful building blocks. The primary metabolism has a high level of similarity between 
organisms; however, they differ in what is called their secondary metabolism.  A certain secondary 
metabolite is specific to a group or an individual organism, and hence, there is often a reason for the 
production of a certain compound. A species might produce a toxin to enhance its survival, or a coloured 
compound to look as a threat. Distinctively, a secondary metabolite might only be produced under certain 
circumstances.  The secondary metabolism of an organism has developed over time and has had an 
importance for its survival. Hence, this is also the area where most of interesting novel compounds are 
found. Secondary metabolites are also called natural products. The organisms obtain the building blocks for 
the secondary metabolites from their primary metabolism. There are four major groups of compounds, 
which can form most secondary metabolites when combined and modified. The acetate pathway produces 
fatty acids and polyketides, while the shikimate pathway through shikimic acid forms aromatic amino acids.

Terpenoids are made of C5 units which are made through the mevalonate and methylerythritol phosphate 
pathway. Lastly, a group of nitrogen containing compounds is called alkaloids, which are mostly found in 
plants. P. inhibens utilises the shikimate pathway for the production of TDA, hence, this pathway will be 
explained thoroughly.   
2.3.1 The Shikimate Pathway  
The shikimate pathway is utilised by microorganisms and plants to construct aromatic compounds, such as 
aromatic amino acids (Dewick, 2009).  The pathway is shown in Appendix 1. In the first step of the pathway, 
phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) from the glycolysis is connected to D-erythrose 4-phosphate by the enzyme 
DAHP synthase. The reactions in these pathways are enzyme catalysed, which lowers the activation energy 
and speeds of the rate of the reaction.  The product, 3-deoxy-D-arabino-heptulosonic acid 7-phosphate 
(DAHP), is then turned into a cyclic intermediate, 3-dehydroquinic acid by 3-dehydro quinate synthase. 
Then, the enzyme 3-dehydroquinase dehydrates this to 3-dehydroshikimic acid, which through reduction 
by shikimate dehydrogenase forms shikimic acid. This is the central intermediate, since a range of different 
compounds can be formed from here. One possibility is that a second molecule of PEP is added, followed 
by elimination of phosphoric acid to form 5-enolpyruvylshikimic acid 3-phosphate (EPSP).  This is performed 
by the shikimate kinase and EPSP synthase. EPSP can then be transformed into chorismic acid by 
chorismate synthase. Hereafter, chorismate mutase can perform a rearrangement which gives prephenic 
acid. Prephenate dehydrate then decarboxylates this to phenylpyruvic acid. Phenylpyruvic acid can then be 
transaminated by phenylpyruvate aminotransferase to phenylalanine with the use of pyridoxal phosphate 
(PLP). Otherwise, phenylpyruvic acid can be decarboxylated and oxidised to phenylacetic acid (Thiel et al, 
2010). Phenylacetic acid and phenylalanine are both known starting products for P. inhibens’ production of 
TDA (Berger et al, 2012; Teufel et al, 2011, 2010).  
2.3.2 Labelling of Metabolites 
Elucidating biosynthetic routes can be difficult; a tool for studying this is feeding the organism isotopically 
labelled compounds such as 13C-labelled glucose. Glucose is the starting compound of the glycolysis, from 
where the different pathways described earlier originate. In this way, the resulting products will have the 
13C-isotope incorporated if it follows a pathway where it breaks down the labelled starting compound. The 
difference between a labelled and a non-labelled compound can be detected with mass spectrometry 
where monoisotopic masses can be detected. This will be explained more thoroughly later.  
Labelled compounds can also be used to prove that a certain compound is built by an organism, while 
elucidation of a biosynthetic pathway might require several labelled feeding experiments.

2.3.3 Biosynthesis of TDA 
The biosynthesis of a compound is a stepwise scheme that explains the formation of a metabolite produced 
by a certain organism. TDA is biosynthesised from phenylacetic acid (PAA), which comes from the shikimate 
pathway (Berger et al, 2012; Teufel et al, 2011). The biosynthesis is shown in Appendix 2, and the numbers 
in the brackets refers hereto. The first part of the pathway is encoded by common genes that also are 
present in E. coli and it was unknown for a long time until revealed by (Teufel et al, 2010). The metabolic 
intermediates and functions of each enzyme were found with labelling experiments. First, the enzyme 
phenylacetate-CoA ligase PaaK turns PAA into phenylacetyl-CoA (2). Then, the aromatic ring is turned into 
an epoxide (3) by the enzyme ring-1,2-phenylacetyl-CoA epoxidase encoded by the genes paaABCDE. An 
isomerisation converts the epoxide into an oxepin (4), which is a seven membered ring with an oxygen 
atom in it. This is performed by the enzyme ring-1,2-epoxyphenylacetyl-CoA isomerase PaaG. Next, the 
enoyl-CoA hydratase (ECH) in the C-terminus of PaaZ adds water which leads to ring opening (6). The paaZ 
gene has a single mutation (paaZ-E256Q) that hence affects the function of the N-terminus; the pathway 
differs from E. coli from this point. The next steps were deduced from a bioinformatics analysis by Brock, 
Nikolay and Dickschat, 2014. After the addition of water, ring closure takes place and forms a seven-
membered ring (7). tdaE encodes for a dehydrogenase enzyme, which through a 1,4 elimination forms (8). 
Hereafter, water elimination by the dehydratase TdaC results in an aromatic tropone derivative (9). The 
intermediates (8) and (9) were proposed based on the detection of the two shunt products (8b, 9b). 
Hereafter, (11) is formed by the addition of (S)-thiocysteine (10) by the enzymes TdaB, and then, TdaF and 
flavine mononucleotide (FMN) transfer it into (12) with a loss of (11b) (Brock et al, 2014). These two steps 
take place one more time resulting in (14). The dithiol intermediate is oxidised to (15), which is hydrolysed 
by the thioesterase TdaD to give the final product TDA.

3 Introduction to Analytical Chemistry 
3.1 Extraction  
Extraction can be performed on a mixture to isolate desired compounds or to simplify it (Harris & Lucy, 
2016). Often, an organic solvent is utilised to extract compounds from a solution of water, which contains 
the analytes of interest. The organic solvent has to be immiscible with water, which means that the two 
phases after mixing will separate and form two layers. During an extraction, the solute will be most soluble 
in the solvent with a polarity most similar to itself.  Further, the pH of the extraction can be important if 
acids or bases are present. Normally, charged compounds will be more soluble in the water phase. For 
instance, weak acids will be negatively charged at the pH of water, and they will stay in the polar water 
phase. Lowering the pH with acid below their pKa will make them neutral, and available for extraction into 
the organic phase. On the other hand, if the weak acids are wanted in the water phase, the pH can be 
increased to make sure they stay in this phase.  
In this study, P. inhibens will be grown in a water-based medium; hence, the extraction solvent has to be 
immiscible with water. Also, the solvent has to have similar properties to the compounds of interest in the 
culture. The known metabolite TDA is a weak acid, which means acid is necessary to make it neutral and 
thereby go into the non-water phase. Since, this study is looking for secondary metabolites, preferably 
related to TDA, the nonpolar solvent ethyl acetate will be used because it is immiscible with water and 1% 
formic acid (FA) will be added to lower the pH in the hope of extracting weak acids. 
3.2 Chromatography 
Chromatography is a separation technique, which utilises two phases. The analytes will equilibrate between 
the phases, which will result in separation if they interact differently with the phases. Chromatography 
consists of a mobile phase, which can either be a liquid or a gas, and a stationary phase, which is bound on 
a surface, most commonly within a column. The column can either be packed with stationary phase 
particles inside, or be open tubular with stationary phase bonded on the sides of a hollow capillary with a 
very small diameter.  
A wide range of different chromatography techniques have been developed. Gas chromatography (GC) has 
a gaseous mobile phase, and the separation takes place in an open tubular column, where the stationary 
phase is bound. The column has a small diameter (0.10-0.53mm), can be up to 100m long, and is placed 
inside an oven (Harris & Lucy, 2016). The analytes have to be volatile in order to be analysed with this 
Page 13 of 94 
 
==PAGE:15==
method. Liquid chromatography (LC) has a liquid mobile phase and takes place in a packed column with 
small particles. This method can be adjusted to fit most analytes. Size exclusion chromatography (SEC) 
utilises size for separation. The stationary phase consists of particles with small pores. Small molecules are 
able to enter the pores, while the larger molecules are not. Hence, the larger molecules will elute first.  The 
smaller molecules have a longer pathway through the column and elutes afterwards in decreasing size.  
Ion-exchange chromatography uses charges as a separation tool. An anion exchanger has a stationary 
phase with positive charges, which will bind anions such as acids, while a cation exchanger is negatively 
charged. The charges can either be strong which means they are permanent, or weak, which means that 
the surface is charged in one pH interval, and neutral in another.  
All these different kinds of chromatography are useful in separation science. In this project, LC has been the 
main method.   
3.2.1 Liquid Chromatography  
Normal-phase liquid chromatography was the first to be developed (Harris & Lucy, 2016). Here, the 
stationary phase is made of microporous, silica particles. The silica particles have silanol groups on the 
surface, which makes them very polar. The stationary phase is most often within a column, which can have 
different dimensions. The mobile phase is nonpolar to begin with, and can go towards being more polar, 
when the separation is performed to enhance the elution strength. This is a type of adsorption 
chromatography, where the analytes and solvent molecules compete for spots on the silica surface. The 
more similar the solvent is to the stationary phase, the easier it will displace the adsorbed analytes. Hence, 
polar solvent molecules displace analytes more easily, and have higher elution strength. With this method, 
the nonpolar analytes elute first, and the most polar elute later.  
Later, a nonpolar stationary phase was developed. This stationary phase has carbon chains attached to the 
silica surface, most commonly octadecyl (C18) chains, which make the surface nonpolar. A polar solvent is 
used to begin with, and afterwards, the elution strength can be enhanced with a less polar solvent.  This is 
called reverse-phase chromatography and is the most frequently used. It is also called partition 
chromatography, because the analyte will be dissolved in either the solvent or the stationary phase. The 
analytes bind to the stationary phase through Van der Waals interactions. The mobile phase used in the 
beginning of the separation is used to prepare the stationary phase in the column, after which the sample is 
loaded. If the same mobile phase is used throughout the run, it is called an isocratic elution, while a 
gradually shifting mixture of two solvents is called a gradient elution. Here, it is important that the two 
solvents are miscible. With this method, the polar analytes elute first and the most nonpolar in the end.  
Page 14 of 94 
 
==PAGE:16==
LC has been advanced further over the years. High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) and ultra-
high-performance liquid chromatography (UHPLC) are advanced from LC, here; pressure is used to improve 
the technique. The different elements of the process will be explained in more detail below and the full 
system is shown in Figure 3.  
 
Figure 3 shows a HPLC system. The figure comes from Waters (Waters, 2017) 
Modern systems have an autosampler, where the sample is placed. The system controlling the autosampler 
is told the position of the sample, and the injection needle will automatically go to the sample, take out a 
small volume and inject it. There are two pumps, which make it possible to run a mixture of two solvents. 
The pumps produce the flow rate of the solvents through the system. The solvents are mixed in a mixing 
chamber before it runs onto the column. When the sample is injected, the flow of the solvents goes 
through the needle. First, the flow goes through a guard column. This is small column made of a similar 
stationary phase as the main column. Here, compounds that adsorb strongly to the stationary phase will be 
retained. The guard column is cheaper than the main column and, therefore, it is cheaper to shift this once 
in a while. From here, the flow goes to the main column, which is normally heated. This decreases the 
viscosity of the solvents, which lowers the needed pressure. The column is only a few millimetres in 
diameter and can have a range of lengths (3-30cm). The particles of the stationary phase are of great 
importance. Smaller particles lead to increased resolution and better separations. Resolution is used to 
describe the efficiency of separation, and good resolution is obtained when two peaks do not overlap and 
are separated with baseline in between. After the analytes have eluted from the column, they reach a 
detector, which together with a computer, creates a chromatogram. The eluent then goes to waste. The 
chromatogram shows a graph with the detector response as a function of time. The time it takes an analyte 
to pass through the system is called the retention time.

The analyte usually elutes as a Gaussian shaped peak due to the way an analyte moves through the column. 
A sharp eluting peak is desired, but the longer an analyte stays on the column, the broader the peak 
becomes. This is mainly caused by diffusion where molecules move from an area with a high concentration 
to an area with a low concentration. Overall, this can be described with the term plate height, H (Harris & 
Lucy, 2016). Plate height is a measurement of the efficiency of a column, and it depends on the distance a 
compound has travelled and the variance of the band. The variance of the band in the column depends on 
how fast the specific analyte diffuses. In order to obtain sharp eluting peaks, a small plate height is desired 
because it leads to narrower bands in the column. The van Deemter equation describes how different 
parameters influence the plate height,

before going back into the mobile phase because the pores of the particles will be smaller on smaller

3.2.2 Analytical and Semi-Preparative HPLC 
Analytical HPLC is utilised to gain information about the analytes in a sample. A small volume is injected, 
which is not regained. Instead, it is often fragmented in a connected mass spectrometer in order to obtain 
the most information. Hence, it is important that only a small amount is needed for the measurement so 
only a small fraction of a sample is lost. In general, the column diameter is often 0.1-0.8cm, and the 
particles are 1.7-10 µm (Waters, 2017). A small diameter of the column, small particles, a heated column, 
and pressure enhances the separation and lowers the time of the method.  
So far, the focus of LC has been analytical, but LC can also be used preparative. In a preparative HPLC run, 
the eluting peaks are collected. If successful, a collected peak will contain a purified analyte. In general, 
when the amount of sample increases, a larger column is needed. The term Semi-Preparative is normally 
used for purification up to 0.5g (Waters, 2017). Here, the column diameter can be 1-4cm, and the particles 
5-15 µm.  
3.2.3 Solid-Phase Extraction  
Solid-Phase Extraction (SPE) is a chromatographic technique, which has its stationary phase in a cartridge. 
Also, it is a sample preparation technique that uses chromatography to prepare a sample for further 
analysis.  
Analysis of a complex sample can be difficult when the compounds of interest are amongst other 
substances. Also, a compound of low concentration can be very hard to detect if it is in a complex sample. 
Here, SPE can be used to simplify a sample by separating the analytes into fractions, before further 
purification or analysis. By using a different stationary phase to the one used for purification or analysis, the 
possibility of the same compounds co-eluting twice can often be prevented. 
Before SPE is started, the stationary phase is conditioned and equilibrated. The conditioning with an 
organic solvent wets the pores, while the equilibration prepares the column for the extraction by replacing 
the organic solvent with the solvent that will be used in the beginning (Arsenault, 2012). Hereafter, the 
sample can be loaded onto the stationary phase. The analytes enter the pores and, if they are more soluble 
in the stationary phase than the mobile phase, they will be retained. First, a wash is performed and then a 
number of elution steps. This can be modified to fit the individual sample.  
One kind of SPE is called Oasis MAX. It is a strong anion exchanger with reverse phase properties 
(Arsenault, 2012). It is a strong anion exchanger, because it is positively charged at all pH values. The 
positive charge consists of a tertiary amine. At higher pH values, acids will be negatively charged. If a 
Page 17 of 94 
 
==PAGE:19==
sample is loaded in this stage, the acids will bind strongly to the anion exchanger. The reverse phase 
properties can be used to elute the neutral analytes in a number of fractions. Then, the pH can be lowered 
which will make the acids neutral. In a second reverse phase, the acids can be eluted into a number of 
fractions (Arsenault, 2012). 
A different kind of SPE has a stationary phase with normal phase properties and attached diol groups 
(Biotage, 2017). Here, the silanol groups of the stationary phase have been modified to contain diols. 
Overall, this is useful for separation of polar analytes from nonpolar compounds. The polar analytes will 
bind to the stationary phase through hydrogen bonds.

3.3 Spectroscopy 
3.3.1 Ultraviolet Spectroscopy  
Molecules that absorb light in the visible (VIS) region (400-750nm) appear coloured (Lampman et al, 2010). 
When a molecule absorbs parts of the white light, which contains all colours in the visible spectrum, the 
complement colour to the absorbed wavelengths will be seen. The ultraviolet (UV) region has lower 
wavelengths (approximately 190-400nm), and molecules that absorb light in this region will not be 
coloured, but absorptions in this region can still be detected.  
Absorption of light is a result of a transition from a lower energy state to a higher energy state (Lampman 
et al, 2010). In order for the molecule to perform this transition, the applied energy has to correspond to 
the exact difference between the energy levels. Light sources are used to irradiate the compound. Typically, 
a deuterium lamp is used for the UV region, and a tungsten lamp for the visible region (Lampman et al, 
2010). In a LC system, the light from these two lamps is merged and shone onto the eluent. The light that 
comes through is split into several beams which are detected by a diode array detector (DAD). This has a 
series of photodiode detectors adjacent to each other. Every diode records a small interval of the UV/VIS 
region. In this way, a full spectrum of the UV/VIS region is recorded. The obtained spectrum will be a Total 
Wavelength Chromatogram (TWC), which has absorbance as a function of time. If a certain wavelength is of 
interest, an extracted wavelength chromatogram (EIC) can be formed. If a certain peak within one of these 
Page 18 of 94 
 
==PAGE:20==
chromatograms comes from an analyte of interest, the peak can be integrated and an absorption 
chromatogram for this time interval can be extracted. This will display absorbance as a function of 
wavelength, and is called an UV spectrum.  
An UV spectrum recorded of a certain compound can provide useful information. It can suggest which 
functional groups might be present, and a comparison between two different UV spectra can be used to 
predict if two molecules are likely or unlikely to have the same chromophore. The chromophore of a 
molecule is the part that absorbs light (Harris & Lucy, 2016). Conjugation of double bonds lowers the 
required energy for a transition because the energy levels move closer together (Lampman et al, 2010). In 
general, compounds that absorb light in the visible region are likely to have a conjugated system.   
3.3.2 Mass Spectrometry 
Mass spectrometry (MS) is an analytical tool where the mass-to-charge ratios (m/z) of ions are detected. It 
is often coupled with HPLC. After chromatographic separation, the analytes are ionised with an ionisation 
source, and their mass-to-charge ratios measured with a MS analyser. Overall, a mass spectrometer has to 
be able to ionise the analytes, separate the ions according to their mass-to-charge ratios, detect the ions 
and count them, process the data from the detector, and additionally, fragment the ions (Hoffmann & 
Stroobant, 2007). Several mass spectrometers exist, such as a Triple Quadrupole (QqQ), which is mostly 
used for quantitative analysis, and a Quadrupole Time-of-Flight (QTOF). A QTOF coupled to a UHPLC will be 
used in this study.  
3.3.2.1 Electrospray Ionisation 
Before the compounds can be analysed in the mass spectrometer, they have to be ionised. A range of 
techniques exists; the soft ionisation method electrospray ionisation (ESI) is used for this study.  In this 
process the LC flow is directed out of a small capillary, forming small droplets (Figure 5). A counter flow of 
drying gas evaporates the solvents, forcing any charges to aggregate with the non-volatile analytes. As the 
charge density increases the ionic repulsion causes the drops to explode until the analytes are present as 
isolated gas phase ions. A strong electrical field is present in the spray chamber, which directs the ions into 
the ion capillary of the spectrometer.  
The ionisation can be run in positive or negative mode, which makes the ions positively or negatively 
charged, respectively. In general, positive mode will ionise most analytes.  
Page 19 of 94 
 
==PAGE:21==
 
Figure 5 shows electrospray ionisation.  The eluent from the LC system enters the spray chamber through a nebulizer. Here, 
there is atmospheric pressure and a nitrogen drying gas. The eluent will form charged droplets that will break into smaller 
droplets until individual ions are left. The ions will then pass through a capillary and enter the mass spectrometer.  The picture 
comes from (Agilent Technologies, 2015).  
3.3.2.2 Focusing, Filtering, and Fragmentation  
After the analytes have been ionised, the ions are led through an octopole (Figure 6), which has a wide 
mass range but low focussing power (Hoffmann & Stroobant, 2007). It consists of eight parallel rods, and 
leads the ions within a broad range through; hence, it is also called an ion guide. Ions with a too high mass 
will not come through, but will get lost on the rods.  
Hereafter, the ions enter a quadrupole (called Quad Mass Filter in Figure 6), which works as an ion guide if 
no selection of precursor ions is applied. A precursor ion is an ion, which fragments or changes its charge in 
the collision cell described below. The quadrupole consists of four parallel rods. If a certain ion is of special 
interest, the voltage applied in the quadrupole can be chosen to select a very narrow range, which means 
that only ions herein will come through.  
Then, the ions enter the collision cell, which is a hexapole with nitrogen gas present. If no collision energy is 
applied, no fragmentation takes place and the ions are led through. This is called MS mode. If energy is 
applied, the precursor ions fragment into fragment ions, also called product ions. Some of these can be 
neutral fragments, which will not be detected. Overall, this mode is called tandem mass spectrometry 
(MS/MS).

Figure 6 shows the different components of the QTOF analyser. After the analytes have been ionised by the ESI, they enter the 
mass spectrometer. First, the octopole guides the ions into the quadrupole where a certain mass range can be chosen. Then, the 
chosen ions are led into the collision cell, where fragmentation takes place if an energy is applied. The product ions are then 
arranged into a parallel beam by the ion beam shaper. At last, the ions enter the field-free flight tube, where the ion pulser 
accelerates them into the tube. The ions are reflected in the end of the tube towards the detector. From the time it takes the

Afterwards, the ions enter an ion beam shaper, which adjust the ions into a parallel beam. Then, they pass 
through a slit to the analyser, where there is full vacuum.  
During these different stages, the pressure is gradually lowered more and more in order to obtain vacuum 
in the Time-of-Flight analyser.

3.3.3 Nuclear Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy  
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) is an important method for structure elucidation. In 1946, NMR signals 
were observed for the first time (Bloch et al, 1946). 
1H chemical shifts can be detected with a one-dimensional (1D) 1H-NMR experiment, where the multiplicity 
of a peak and the coupling constants express the neighbouring protons. 13C chemical shifts can be 
measured with a 1D 13C-NMR experiment, but due to the low natural abundance of 13C, it has a lower 
sensitivity than 1H. After the development of 1D NMR, a range of two-dimensional (2D) methods were 
introduced (Aue et al, 1976).   
Double-Quantum-Filtered Correlation Spectroscopy (DQF-COSY) (Piantini et al, 1982) provides information 
about the spin systems. It measures 1H-1H correlations over two or three bonds. A 1H chemical shift scale is 
present in both dimensions, which along the diagonal gives rise to diagonal peaks corresponding to the 1H-
NMR spectrum. A cross peak arises between the diagonal peaks of two protons if they couple. In this way, 
spin systems can be outlined. Heteronuclear single-quantum correlation spectroscopy (HSQC) detects one 
bond correlations from 1H to 13C or 15N (Bodenhausen & Ruben, 1980). A 13C chemical shift range is present 
in the first dimension and 1H in the second. This experiment is utilised to elucidate which carbon the 
protons are attached to. If the two protons in a methylene group have different proton chemical shifts, this 
experiment will correlate them to the same carbon. Heteronuclear multiple-bond correlation spectroscopy 
(HMBC) (Bax & Summers, 1986) can detect two, three, and four bond couplings from 1H to 13C, where the 
three-bond coupling is most commonly present.  A 13C chemical shift range is present in the first dimension 
and 1H in the second. HMBC gives valuable information about how the structural fragments are connected. 
For larger molecules, the two bond correlations from 1H to 13C provide valuable information; for this a 
heteronuclear two-bond correlation (H2BC) experiment has been developed, where three and higher bond 
correlations are suppressed while two bond correlations are enhanced (Nyberg et al, 2005).

3.4 Data Processing 
3.4.1 Dereplication 
In natural products chemistry, the main focus is novel secondary metabolites. When working with an 
organism, the search for new metabolites can be very time consuming. Some organisms produce the same 
secondary metabolites, and in order to save time, already known metabolites might be found with 
dereplication.  
Dereplication is the process of identifying already known metabolites in a short time, which gives more 
time to look into unknown, novel compounds. LC-UV/VIS-MS is a method that can be used for 
dereplication, where the MS detector determines the accurate masses. The data file of a crude exact 
obtained with this method can be analysed and interpreted to find known or novel metabolites. The 
obtained chromatographic data will be a Total Ion Chromatogram (TIC), optionally with MS/MS. Often it is 
easier to look at the Base Peak Chromatogram (BPC), which can be extracted from the TIC. A BPC shows the 
most abundant ion at each time point. If a certain ion is studied, an extracted ion chromatogram (EIC) can 
be extracted from the TIC for that specific ion.  
A peak of interest in the chromatogram is studied by looking at its mass spectra. A mass spectrum can be 
extracted as an average of the full peak in the chromatogram. At the top of the peak, the detector might be 
saturated, which will be indicated with a star next to the ion in the mass spectra. If that is the case, a mass 
spectrum can be extracted from the front or tail of the peak where it is not saturated. In the mass 
spectrum, differences between ions help determining the molecular ion and adducts. The molecular ion is 
the mass of the analyte plus a hydrogen when run in positive mode, and it is shown as [M+H]+. Due to 
presence of sodium and potassium ions, the ionised analytes can form adducts with these ions, which is 
described as [M+Na]+ and [M+K]+. If the molecular ion and adducts are present, the differences between 
these can determine the mass of the analyte. These exact differences between the molecular ion and 
adducts, and the exact mass of common losses were stated by (Nielsen et al, 2011) and a selection is shown 
in Table 1.  
After identification of the molecular ion, the molecular formula can be calculated e.g. with the formula 
calculator in MassHunter. If a lot of formulas are possible, some might be excluded by looking at the 
isotope pattern. The most counted isotope, A, will consist of the most abundant isotope of each element. 
The peak one mass unit higher, A+1, will for small molecules have a much lower count due to low 
abundance of isotopes such as carbon 13, oxygen 17, and nitrogen 15. Further, there can be a peak two 
mass units higher, A+2. This is normally due to either a halogen or sulphur if it is a smaller molecule. If it is a 
Page 24 of 94 
 
==PAGE:26==
higher mass molecule, A+1 will become larger than A, since the presence of one carbon 13 in the molecule 
will be more common than a molecule with just carbon 12. This is due to the 1.1% abundance of carbon 13. 
For sulphur, the most abundant ion is sulphur 32, but 4.22% of all sulphur is sulphur 34 (Hoffmann & 
Stroobant, 2007). Hence, the presence of sulphur will be visible in the isotope pattern. Overall, the isotopes 
of different elements and their abundances will determine the look of the isotope pattern.  
The formulas can be utilised to search for known compounds in databases, where the formulas are used as 
the entry.  If the compound is UV active, its UV spectrum can be extracted from the Total Wavelength 
Chromatogram, which can be compared to the UV maxima stated in the database if these are available. In 
this study, the databases Antibase (Laatsch, 2012) and MarinLit (“MarinLit,” 2012) were utilised. Antibase is 
a large database for natural products, while MarinLit is specific for natural products from marine organisms.  
The dereplication process can be complicated, when more compounds are eluting at the same time. To 
figure out if two or more ions belong to the same compound, deconvolution can be performed. EICs are 
performed for the ions of interest, and by overlaying them, it can be determined if they belong to the same 
compound. If they have the same retention time and peak shape, this is the case. Otherwise, it is two or 
more compounds, which are co-eluting. This analytical procedure will be applied for the later analysis.

3.4.2 Dynamic Cluster Analysis (Andersen et al, 2016) 
Crude extract chromatograms can be very complex, especially if there are a lot of media components 
present. It can be difficult to identify new natural products by manually studying a chromatogram and its 
peaks. A systematic way to analyse a chromatogram is Dynamic Cluster Analysis (DCA) (Andersen et al, 
2016). DCA is a tool to look for compounds containing sulphur, chlorine, or bromine. As mentioned earlier, 
P. inhibens produces several sulphur containing compounds with TDA being the most studied one. Due to 
this, it is interesting to study if the bacterium produces other sulphur containing compounds.  
Dynamic cluster analysis (DCA) is a programme that identifies compounds with sulphur, chlorine, or 
bromine in data obtained from high resolution LC-MS. The technique is able to find 97.4% of molecular 
formulas with one of these three elements according to (Andersen et al, 2016). It utilises specific algorithms 
that divides a data set into three groups; one that contains the halogens chlorine and bromine; a second 
that contains sulphur but no halogens; and a third without halogens and sulphur, called the carbon group. 
The algorithms are based on the difference between the A+1 and A+2 isotope and the A:A+2 intensity ratio. 
The uppercase A is the lowest mass in an isotope pattern, where A+1 and A+2 are the isotopes one and two 
Dalton higher, respectively.  Sulphur has three different isotopes; sulphur 32, 33, and 34, which account for 
95.03%, 0.75%, and 4.22%, respectively (Hoffmann & Stroobant, 2007). Sulphur 34 contributes to the A+2 
peak, but this is a minor peak, especially if there is only one sulphur present.  Chlorine and bromine have 
two isotopes each; chlorine 35 (75.77%), chlorine 37 (24.23%), bromine 79 (50.68%), and bromine 
81(49.31%) (Hoffmann & Stroobant, 2007). Here, the A+2 isotopes are much more abundant than sulphur 
34, which results in higher A+2 isotopes. Hence, these halogens have higher A:A+2 ratios, and the 
distinctive difference in the ratios is what the algorithm that divide the halogen and the sulphur groups is 
based on. Similarly, if a compound does not contain any of these elements, the A+2 will be absent or small 
depending on the size of the molecule. Therefore, it is also possible to distinguish sulphur containing 
compounds from the carbon group. Furthermore, the difference between the A+1 and A+2 isotope also 
shows whether a compound contains sulphur or halogens due to the mass defect. The A+1 to A+2 
difference for these elements are usually lower than 1.001 u (Andersen et al, 2016), and, it is also possible 
to differentiate between halogens and sulphur.  
The binding energy that holds a nucleus together causes a mass defect which means that the isotope mass 
of an element differs from the mass of the individual protons, neutrons, and electrons it consists of. The 
monoisotopic mass is an isotopomer consisting of a single combination of isotopes. In MS, the 
monoisotopic mass is only observed for the A peak, which consists of the most abundant isotope of each 
Page 26 of 94 
 
==PAGE:28==
element. On the other hand, the observed A+1 and A+2 peaks consist of several ions with different 
combinations of isotopes as explained previously.  
From a database of different molecular formulas, algorithms were developed that are able to distinguish 
these differences. All the algorithms lie within the programme, and they require high resolution MS data. 
Overall, when a data set is analysed, the A+1 to A+2 difference and the A:A+2 ratio is calculated for peaks 
with counts over a certain threshold, which then places the ion or adduct into one of the three groups.  
DCA has shown to be capable of detecting compounds with one sulphur atom, which other methods often 
cannot do due to the low abundance of sulphur. In the programme, the data can be filtered to show 
compounds containing halogens (Cl and Br), sulphur and no halogens, or all A+2 results. For the later 
analysis, the sulphur filter will be utilised mainly.  
3.5 Aim of Study 
The aim of this study is to find and characterise novel secondary metabolites produced by P. inhibens, 
which are related to TDA.  This will be performed with cultivation and extraction of P. inhibens in order to 
obtain mass spectrometry data to look for novel compounds with dereplication and dynamic cluster 
analysis. The crude extract will be utilised to purify chosen metabolites with chromatography. The 
metabolites will be characterised with mass spectrometry and their structures elucidated with nuclear 
magnetic resonance. Finally, it will be tested if they are biologically active.

5.1 Dereplication of TDA in the Crude Extract 
The base peak chromatogram of the extracted culture was overlaid with the base peak chromatogram of 
the extracted media (Figure 7). The overlay showed a lot of similarities, which meant that numerous media 
components were still present in the crude culture extract. It made dereplication more difficult, because 
the secondary metabolites produced by the bacteria were not easily seen. As mentioned earlier, P. inhibens 
is mostly known for its ability to produce the antibiotic TDA. TDA has the molecular formula C8H4O3S2, 
which in a mass spectrum would result in a molecular ion at m/z 212.9675, theoretically. Since the LC-MS 
normally runs within an error of 5 ppm, an extracted ion chromatogram (EIC) for this mass-to-charge with 
±5 ppm expansion was formed. The EIC showed a peak eluting at 7.17 min (Figure 8, B). It was checked 
whether the detector was saturated at the top of the peak, since this was not the case, a mass spectrum 
was extracted of the peak in the time interval 7.11-7.26 min (Figure 9). The molecular ion was present at 
m/z 212.9674, which deviated 0.47 ppm from the theoretical value. A neutral loss of water from the 
molecular ion of TDA corresponded to the fragment ion at m/z 194.9570. The mass difference was close to 
the general values listed in Table 1 (Nielsen et al, 2011).  Deconvolution showed that the ion belonged to 
TDA because it had the same retention time and peak shape (Figure 8, C).

5.2.1 
13C-Labelling Experiment showed Methyl group originated from P. inhibens 
The purpose of the experiment was to show whether the methyl group in MeTDA was produced by P. 
inhibens, or if it originated from a solvent. The bacterium was grown in minimal media, where the only 
carbon source was glucose. In order to study the origin of the methyl group, one culture was feed normal 
D-glucose, and another 13C-labelled glucose. The extracted cultures were analysed with LC-MS.  
A merged EIC of the previously present adducts and dimers of TDA and MeTDA were extracted of each of 
the cultures where the changes due to carbon 13 were taken into account (Figure 14, A). First of all, it 
showed that MeTDA eluted over a long time (3.5-6.5 min), indicating special properties making purification 
difficult, while TDA eluted around 6.9 min. From the chromatograms, mass spectra were extracted of TDA 
and MeTDA. TDA has eight carbons and hence a difference of, (8*(13C - 12C)) = 8.0268, was expected 
between a labelled and a non-labelled molecule of TDA. In Figure 14, B), it is seen that a difference of 
8.0267 was found.  MeTDA has nine carbons and hence, the predicted difference between a labelled and a 
non-labelled molecule of MeTDA was (9*(13C - 12C)) = 9.0302. In Figure 14 C), this difference was present for 
the sodium adduct and values very close to this for the molecular ion and the potassium adduct. The dimer 
of MeTDA contained 18 carbons, and the observed difference of 18.0606 was very close to the theoretical 
value of 18.0604 (Figure 14, D). Overall, this meant that P. inhibens produced MeTDA itself since it 
incorporated the 13C from the labelled glucose in MeTDA. If methylation of TDA had happened in a solution 
afterwards, the carbon of the methyl group would have been 12C, mostly. Hence, the difference in mass to 
charge would have been 8.0268 ((8*(13C - 12C)) instead.  
It was interesting to observe that the bacterium was able to grow under these conditions, and that TDA and 
MeTDA were produced. This suggested that MeTDA was a major metabolite, since it also was produced 
under these limited nutrient conditions where it had to produce everything from glucose. Biosynthetically, 
it showed that the bacteria were able to produce its secondary metabolites from glucose which indicated 
that P. inhibens was able to use the glycolysis and the Shikimate pathway as explained in the introduction.

5.3 Method development for Purification of MeTDA and TDA 
After showing that MeTDA was a metabolite produced by P. inhibens, purification of the compound was 
necessary in order to obtain NMR data of the molecule and to test its antibiotic potential.  
5.3.1 Solid-Phase Extraction of the Crude Extract  
The crude extract contained a lot of interesting metabolites produced by P. inhibens along with media 
components. This complicated the purification process. In order to simplify the extract, solid-phase 
extraction was implemented. The overall goal was to separate MeTDA from TDA without too much loss of 
each product in other fractions. Further, choosing a different stationary phase to the preparative 
purification afterwards could prevent that the same compounds co-eluted twice.   
First, a strong anion exchange column with reverse phase (OASIS MAX) was tried out. After sample load, 
the reverse phase properties of the column were used by elution with increasing concentrations of MeOH. 
Hereafter, the pH was lowered to 2. The low pH meant that weak acids would not be charged anymore, and 
Page 43 of 94 
 
==PAGE:45==
hence, would not bind to the positive charge on the column. At this pH, the reverse phase properties were 
used a second time. A lot of the media components eluted in the first fractions, whereas TDA and MeTDA 
bound to the charged column and did not elute until the pH was lowered. This method was useful for 
separating media components from MeTDA and TDA. TDA and MeTDA eluted in the same fractions after 
the pH change together with a number of other compounds. The recovery was low, possibly due to the 
chosen pH. TDA has a pKa value of 2 (Porsby, 2010), hence, it might only be partially protonated at the pH 2. 
A second experiment was performed with a pH of 1, but a satisfactory recovery was still not obtained. 
Hence, other methods were turned to.   
Secondly, a diol normal phase column was utilised with a five step gradient of heptane and ethyl acetate. 
Here, TDA mostly eluted in the second fraction, while MeTDA primarily eluted in the third. Based on this, 
more steps were applied to get a better separation between the two. Next, seven fractions were collected. 
TDA eluted mostly in the second fraction (15% EtOAc), while MeTDA eluted in the fourth fraction (45% 
EtOAc). This was a satisfactory separation since each compound mostly eluted in one fraction, and each of 
them was only present in small amounts in the surrounding fractions. This method was replicated in order 
to obtain enough for further purification.  
5.3.2 Purification of MeTDA with Semi-Preparative HPLC  
The preparative HPLC had a UV detector, and hence, fractions were collected based on absorption. From 
the LC-MS analysis, it was known that TDA absorbed at 304 nm and MeTDA at 290 nm. Hence, an 
absorption chromatogram as a function of time for 300 nm was displayed during the runs. Fractions were 
collected when peaks absorbing at this wavelength eluted. Additionally, a chromatogram at 220 nm was 
shown, which indicated if other compounds were co-eluting. Last, a momentary absorption chromatogram 
as a function of wavelength was watched during the run as the fractions were collected.   
The fourth SPE fraction containing MeTDA was used for the following separation. The chromatogram 
obtained from the analytical LC-MS (Figure 15, A) showed that the fraction contained a lot of compounds. 
For the first run, a C18-column was utilised. Analysis of the collected fractions with LC-MS showed that 
MeTDA eluted over a longer period and not as one peak as desired.

5.5.1 Synthesis of MeTDA from TDA 
When TDA was first discovered (Tsubotani et al, 1984) limited analytical techniques were available for 
structural elucidation. NMR was performed, but because only 1D NMR existed at the time, several 
derivatisation reactions were also performed to help elucidate the structure. Here, TDA was reacted with 
methyl iodide, which methylated TDA (Figure 20).  
 
Figure 20 shows the reaction performed by Tsubotani et al, 1984. 
In this study, the same reaction was performed for a purified sample of TDA, which did not contain any 
MeTDA. The sample is seen in Figure 21, A), where only a TDA peak is present at 6.9 min, and no peak is 
present at the retention time for MeTDA at 5.8 min. This sample was reacted with methyl iodide, and the 
result is seen in Figure 21, B). By comparing this to the exacted sample of MeTDA, Figure 21, C), it was seen 
that the synthetic MeTDA had the same retention time. The fragmentation of the synthetic and exacted 
MeTDA was compared for 10 eV, 20 eV, and 40 eV, and high similarities were seen. The fragmentation 
patterns for 20 eV for synthetic and exacted MeTDA are seen in Figure 21, D), and E), respectively.  
Consequently, it was very likely that the synthetic and exacted MeTDA had the same structure, and possibly 
looked like the product of the reaction in Figure 20.  
Although it was demonstrated that the natural MeTDA and semi-synthetic MeTDA were likely to be the 
same compound, a full structural elucidation was necessary due the aforementioned analytical limitations 
in 1984. This was especially important given the ambiguity between the thiotropocin tautomer originally 
identified.

5.5.5 Theoretical Calculations of NMR shifts and Comparison to the Chemical Shifts of TDA 
The theoretical values for the product shown in Figure 20 were calculated with Advanced Chemistry 
Development (ACD) I-Lab. The values are listed in Table 3. The calculated shifts corresponded very well to 
the measured values for this compound. This confirmed 178.7 ppm as a carbonyl, 167.8 ppm as an ester, 
and 176.4 ppm as an aromatic carbon. The last shift was high due to the very strained position, and it 
proposed the 5-membered ring where the ester group binds to the sulphur. Additionally, a structure with 
just the 7-membered ring and a free carboxylic acid would have more protons and thereby have a higher 
mass than the one observed in MS.  
Comparing the 13C chemical shifts of TDA to MeTDA showed an important difference for C4. C4 was 
observed at 15.0 ppm higher for TDA, which could be caused by its strained position in the four-membered 
ring. Hence, a lower chemical shift is observed for C4 in MeTDA because it is not strained.  
Overall, this showed that the structure shown in Figure 22 was reasonable. The exact structure could be

5.5.6 MeTDA inhibited Vibrio anguillarum  
A bioassay with V. anguillarum was performed to study if MeTDA was an antibiotic like TDA. The bioassay 
was performed with this pathogenic bacterium, because it is often present in fish farms, where it can cause 
high rates of mortalities, especially in larval fish as they do not have developed immune systems. The 
bioassay is shown in Figure 26, and the description of the well numbers and concentrations are listed in 
Table 4. MeTDA was present in well number 7, 8, and 9, as a dilution series. In 9, MeTDA was present in the 
lowest concentration, approximately 0.3-0.6 mM, and it had an inhibition zone of 1.6 cm at the widest 
diameter. TDA was present in well number 3, 4, and 5, also as a dilution series. In 5, TDA had a 
concentration of 0.3-0.7 mM, approximately, and an inhibition zone of 1.8 cm. Comparing MeTDA to TDA, 
MeTDA obtained a slightly smaller inhibition zone, but it inhibited V. anguillarum within the same 
approximate concentration range as TDA.  
Overall, this shows that MeTDA is an antibiotic with great potential. It supports that P. inhibens has a great 
potential as a probiotic, since it produces more than one antibiotic.  
MeTDA might be able to collapse the proton motive force like TDA even though it has a different structure. 
If it follows the current hypothesised mode of action (Figure 2) (Wilson et al, 2016), MeTDA might diffuse 
over the cell membrane like TDA. In order to bind a cation, the five-membered ring might open which 
would form a carboxylic acid like TDA (Figure 25). If this is indeed the mode of action, MeTDA have to be 
biotransformed to become bioactive (similar to prodrugs administered to humans). However, a yet 
unknown mode of action is also feasible. More studies will be needed to elucidate this.

5.5.7 Biosynthetic Analysis of MeTDA 
Most likely, P. inhibens produces MeTDA by methylation of TDA or from an intermediate in the TDA 
biosynthesis. Commonly, methylations are generated with S-adenosylmethionine by a methyltransferase 
(Dewick, 2009), but bioinformatic analyses have not detected any methyltransferase encoding genes close 
to the tda genes.    
A sulphur-methyl group (SMe) was also found in a new group of compounds, Roseochelins, produced by P. 
inhibens (Wang & Seyedsayamdost, 2017). This addition was enzyme-catalysed, and it could be the same 
enzyme adding SMe to a TDA intermediate.  
A proposed biosynthesis toward MeTDA is shown in Figure 27. In the biosynthesis of TDA (Appendix 2), the 
intermediate (13) has one sulphur attached. Instead of going through a second addition of (S)-thiocysteine, 
the S-methylation enzyme might add a SMe-group, after which the thioesterase in TdaD hydrolyses the 
thioester to a carboxylic acid. At last, the carboxylic acid and the closest sulphur can form a five-membered 
ring under oxidation. On the other hand, MeTDA could also be a precursor for TDA, if the enzyme cleaved 
off the SMe group of MeTDA, and hereafter added another sulphur atom to form TDA.  
 Determining the actual biosynthesis will require more studies.  
 
Figure 27 shows a proposed biosynthesis of MeTDA starting from intermediate (13) from Appendix 2. Intermediate (13) has 
already been added the first sulphur, but the second sulphur and the methyl group could be introduced with the enzyme found 
by (Wang & Seyedsayamdost, 2017). Then, the thioesterase in TdaD could hydrolyse the thioester to a carboxylic acid. Lastly, 
oxidation can form the five-membered ring, which gives the final structure of MeTDA.  
5.6 Structure Elucidation of Compound (Y) 
During purification of MeTDA, another fraction was collected due to its strong absorption at 300 nm, peak

molecular ion was determined to m/z 321.0252 from its difference to the sodium adduct. From the isotope

5.7 Study of Sulphur Containing Compounds Produced by P. inhibens 
5.7.1 Dynamic Cluster Analysis 
Sulphur containing molecules are often bioactive as was the case with TDA and MeTDA; thus, new sulphur 
containing compounds were looked for with Dynamic cluster analysis (DCA). DCA offered a systematic 
approach, and was employed for SPE fractions 2-4.  In general, compounds eluting between or close to 
MeTDA and TDA were focused on. 
First, SPE fraction 3 was studied, which was the fraction between the main fractions of TDA and MeTDA. 
The BPC of fraction 3 is shown in Figure 32, A). The area of a smaller peak of interest in the chromatogram 
was measured to 175,000, hence, an area cut-off in DCA of 150,000 was chosen. The required rate of 
adducts that must contain sulphur was kept at 50%, which was proven successful by (Andersen et al, 2016). 
This gave 12 results, where seven of them eluted in the range of MeTDA to TDA or closely after. The 
numbers indicated below correspond to their number in the table (Appendix 13) and in Figure 32, A).

The first result (1) was MeTDA, which was identified with its sodium adduct and sodium dimer adduct. The 
second result (2) had a monoisotopic mass of m/z 262.9811 and eluted at 7.32 min. A MS spectrum was 
extracted of the peak to determine the molecular ion. Here, the ion had an average monoisotopic m/z of 
262.9808. A difference of approximately 16 was found to m/z 278.9543, which corresponded to the 
difference between a sodium and a potassium adduct, and similarly, a difference of 22 identified the 
molecular ion at m/z 240.9988 (Figure 32, B). The molecular ion was used to calculate possible formulas 
within 5 ppm in the formula calculator, which gave one formula, C10H8O3S2 with a DBE of 7. The formula 
was used to search for known compounds in Antibase and MarinLit, but no results were found. The 
compound had the same number of oxygens and sulphurs as MeTDA and TDA, and compared to MeTDA 
the difference was CH2, just as the difference between MeTDA and TDA. Due to this interesting relation, the 
compound was chosen for purification.  
The next result (3) in the analysis was TDA which was identified from just its molecular ion. Hereafter, a 
result (4) with m/z 264.9422 eluting at 8.16 min was identified. In the mass spectrum, this ion was 
identified as the sodium adduct with a difference of 22 to the molecular ion at m/z 242.9605 (Figure 32, C). 
The molecular ion was used to search for possible formulas, which gave C9H6O2S3. No known compounds 
were found in the databases. This formula had a different number of oxygens and sulphurs compared to 
TDA, but was still of interest due to its three sulphur atoms. Hence, this was also chosen for purification.  
The fifth result (5) at 8.41 min was identified with two adducts, m/z 276.9967 and m/z 531.0039 by DCA. 
The first ion had a difference of m/z 1 to its A+1 isotope, which meant that the ion with a high mass was a 
dimer. In an extracted MS, a difference of 22 was found to the molecular ion at m/z 255.0151, which meant 
that the second adduct was a dimer with sodium. The molecular ion was used to find possible formulas. 
One formula was found, C11H10O3S2, with the same number of oxygens and sulphurs as TDA and MeTDA. 
The formula was used to search for known compounds in Antibase and MarinLit, but no results were found. 
The difference between this formula and MeTDA was C2H4. This was also chosen for purification.

fitted the best. None of these gave any results in the databases, why it was also chosen for purification.

5.7.2 DCA did not identify Compound (Y) in Fraction 4 
DCA worked well in terms of finding new sulphur metabolites as described above. Though, it did not 
identify compound (Y) in Figure 15, which contained two sulphur atoms. The BPC of fraction 4 was studied 
further.  Here, the compound (Y) eluted in the tail of the large MeTDA peak, which would have made it 
difficult to identify manually (Figure 33). In order to understand why DCA did not detect the compound, 
DCA was studied further and some of the parameters were discussed.  
 
Figure 33 shows the BPC of fraction 4 with an overlay of the peak of compound (Y). It shows that the compound would have 
been difficult to notice manually.  
Before DCA can be performed, the data file recorded on the LC-MS has to be extracted into a certain 
format (.mzdata), after which the data is filtered and fitted to another format (.CSV) in RStudio. The filtering 
is performed by a file that comes with DCA. The compound and its ion were searched for in the CSV-file. For 
the molecular ion only A and A+1 were present, and only A for the sodium adduct. Hence, DCA did not 
identify the compound due to the missing A+2 isotopes which is used as a parameter in DCA. This was due 
to the low counts of the A+2 isotopes. In the filtering, counts below 5000 had been removed. This 
parameter was changed to 2000, and in the new CSV-file, A, A+1, and A+2 were present for the molecular 
Page 63 of 94 
 
==PAGE:65==
ion, and A, and A+1 for the sodium adduct. DCA was performed again, but the compound still was not 
identified. Studying the retention times of the isotopes showed that isotopes of the molecular ion were 
measured 1.14 seconds apart. Hence, the result was filtered off because the allowed retention time shift 
was chosen to ±1 second. This was raised to 1.5 sec. Now the compound was identified with its molecular 
ion. If this parameter is increased too much, there is a risk of pairing two ions that are not a part of the 
compound.  
Instead of lowering the count cut off, another LC-MS run could be performed with a higher injection 
volume to obtain higher counts.  The low counts are due to a low concentration of the analyte in the 
sample. A higher injection volume increases the risk of saturation of the detector for other analytes that are 
present in higher concentrations. The analysis showed that parameters in the filtering and in DCA have to 
be taken into account when using the programme in order to obtain the desired results. If high abundant 
compounds are in focus, the parameters can be adjusted to only include the certain results. If minor 
metabolites are desired, the parameters can be altered to be softer, such as allowing fewer counts and a 
lower area cut off. Overall, Dynamic Cluster Analysis was useful and time saving for identification of 
possible metabolites in a complex sample. 
5.7.3 Purification of DCA Results with Semi-Preparative HPLC 
The third SPE fraction was used for purification of the DCA results. The desired compounds where (2), (4), 
(5), (6), and (7). In the analytical LC, they eluted in the range of 53.2-63.5% MeCN in water. From previous 
runs of MeTDA, it was known that the compounds elutes earlier in the preparative HPLC. Hence, a method 
with a gradient of 25-35% MeCN in water over five minutes, followed by 35-50% over the next 20 min was 
applied.  In order to collect the analytes based on their absorption, UV spectra were extracted for (2), (4), 
(5), and (7) (Appendix 14). They all absorbed around 300 nm, hence, this wavelength was displayed for 
collection. Due to overlap from surrounding peaks, an UV spectrum of (6) could not be obtained. Co-eluting 
peaks might have added to the extracted UV spectra.  
The obtained absorption chromatograms for the run are shown in Figure 34. The peaks from MeTDA to the 
end were collected. Collecting the peaks based on absorption was problematic, since TDA absorbed 
strongly in even a small amount, which made it difficult to see the other analytes. Hence, future work could 
be improved if complete separation from TDA was performed first. Also, the analytes of interest eluted in a 
very short time range, which complicated the purification further.  
From LC-MS analysis of the fractions, it was shown that pure fractions were obtained for (2), (5), and (7). (6) 
was collected in the TDA fraction, which would have required further purification, but due to time

5.7.4 Results pointed towards a Number of Unknown Metabolites 
Small amounts of the compounds (2), (5), and (7) were obtained. They were tested against V. anguillarum, 
where inhibitory activity was observed for all three (Figure 26). It suggested that P. inhibens might produce 
other bioactive molecules than MeTDA and TDA; however, a dilution series of each compound against V. 
anguillarum would be needed to compare how potent they are compared to TDA. In the 13C-labelling 
experiment (4) and (7) were biosynthesised by P. inhibens. All DCA results had similar polarity to MeTDA 
and TDA. Also, the extracted UV spectra were similar to the UV spectra of MeTDA and TDA. Overall, it 
suggested that the identified compounds could be related to TDA.  
Altogether from the DCA analysis and finding of compound (Y), six sulphur containing compounds were 
identified. Three of them were biosynthesised by P. inhibens, and the four purified samples showed 
bioactivity. It pointed towards several bioactive, sulphur containing metabolites produced by P. inhibens. 
Further characterisation and elucidation of these could help understanding the biosynthesis of TDA. 
Additionally, it would be important to understand whether the bacterium produces other bioactive 
compounds in order to apply it as a probiotic in greater scale in fish farms. Understanding the bioactivity of 
TDA related compounds could also help elucidate the mode of action.

6 Conclusion  
The novel antibacterial natural product Methyl TDA (MeTDA) was discovered. A feeding experiment with 
13C-labelled glucose showed that MeTDA was biosynthesised by P. inhibens, and that MeTDA was not 
formed during extraction. A synthesis of MeTDA from TDA suggested the structure was TDA derived. The 
structure was characterised with LC-MS, where a characteristic loss of CO2 showed that the methyl group 
was not positioned on the carboxylic acid. Furthermore, elucidation with NMR substantiated the presence 
of a seven-membered aromatic ring similar to TDA. Comparison of the chemical shifts of MeTDA to TDA 
showed a distinctive difference for a carbon bonded to sulphur, which suggested an alteration in the 
structure at this position. HMBC correlations from the methyl group to the aromatic ring determined the 
methyl group’s placement on the sulphur atom. Also, MeTDA inhibited Vibrio anguillarum in the same 
approximate concentration range as TDA, which revealed antibacterial properties of MeTDA. This 
strengthened the great potential of P. inhibens as a probiotic for aquaculture, since this adds to the 
bioactivity of the bacterium. Successful purification methods for TDA and MeTDA were developed. MeTDA 
was difficult to purify due to its special properties; however, pure fractions were obtained with diol SPE 
followed by semi-preparative HPLC purification on a hexyl-phenyl column.  
A secondary metabolite with a molecular formula of C15H12O4S2 and a polarity similar to MeTDA was 
identified. Analysis of the NMR experiments indicated that the compound was related to TDA. The chemical 
shifts of the additional fragments indicated that there was neither a second aromatic ring nor alkenes. The 
compound inhibited V. anguillarum at a concentration of 9-22 mM, and was biosynthesised by P. inhibens.  
Several interesting compounds were identified in the SPE fractions with Dynamic Cluster Analysis. Small 
amounts of three compounds were purified and tested against V. anguillarum, where inhibition zones were 
observed. Two results were present in the 13C-labelling experiment, which showed that they were 
biosynthesised by P. inhibens and were not artefacts. Characterisation of the compounds with LC-MS 
together with UV data and polarity suggested that the compounds could be related to the TDA 
biosynthesis. All the results identified in the analysis were searched for in the databases Antibase and 
MarinLit, where no known compounds were found. Overall, the analysis pointed towards a number of 
putative metabolites, which in further studies could help elucidate the biosynthesis of TDA. For this, 
Dynamic Cluster Analysis was an advantageous method, which saved time and identified compounds that 
would have been difficult to identify manually in a complex sample.  
Altogether, the discovery of a new antibiotic illustrated the great potential that lies in marine environments 
for finding novel antibacterial compounds, which are needed to fight pathogenic bacteria.

7 Perspectives 
The bacterial strains that produce TDA have been studied for a long time. In this study, MeTDA was a major 
metabolite in the chromatogram, and it is therefore curious why it has not been noticed before, especially 
due to its bioactivity. From the LC-MS runs, it was seen that MeTDA ionises very well; hence, in the future it 
could be used as a marker for the presence of Phaeobacter in marine samples.  
It would have been interesting to study the minimal inhibitory concentration of MeTDA against different 
bacteria and compare the results to TDA. This would have indicated if MeTDA is more or less potent than 
TDA. In order to study if the additional metabolites identified in this study could be antibiotics, a dilution 
series of each tested against V. anguillarum would show if they were active in the same range as TDA or if a 
higher concentration would be required for an effect. For the compound, C15H12O4S2, further purification 
and NMR experiments like H2BC and DQF-COSY could solve the structure of the compound. Determining 
the biosynthesis of MeTDA and its mode of action was out of scope of this study, but it would be interesting 
to look into in future studies. The biosynthesis could be studied with gene knockout experiments where the 
tda genes are knocked out. If the bacterium is able to biosynthesise MeTDA when feed TDA, or TDA when 
feed MeTDA, it would show the existence of a methyltransferase protein encoded for in another part of the 
genes. On the other hand, if it cannot perform this change, partial gene knockouts of the tda genes would 
be needed to locate the part of the tda genes that stands for the methylation. It is also possible that the 
biosynthesis differs from TDA at an earlier stage. The mode of action could be studied by re-extracting the 
bioassay and analysing whether MeTDA is present only or if other MeTDA derived compounds are present 
in the agar of the inhibition zone, such as TDA or MeTDA with a free carboxylic acid. This would show if 
MeTDA has changed during the mode of action, or if it is bioactive in itself.  
Secondary metabolites produced by P. inhibens are of great interest due to the bacterium’s potential as a 
probiotic. It will be important to know all antibiotic compounds that P. inhibens produces and to determine 
all the probiotic effects before applying it in greater scale. Further studies are needed to determine if the 
bacterium produces additional antibiotic compounds, and which conditions the bacterium produces the 
individual metabolites under, especially considering an aquaculture setup. Certain conditions might be 
required for the bacterium to produce the metabolites that it produces during nutrient rich conditions in 
the laboratory, which are somewhat artificial. The conditions in the environment where P. inhibens would 
be applied as a probiotic might not fulfil this.

9.15 Appendix 15 
Small amounts of the compounds (2), (5), and (7) were obtained. Due to their similarities to TDA and 
MeTDA in their UV absorption and molecular formulas, 1H-NMR was performed. The aim was to elucidate 
their structures by comparing with the chemical shifts of TDA and MeTDA. However, the obtained spectra 
were not possible to analyse due to the absence of signals. Instead, the compounds are discussed on the 
basis of obtained LC-MS data, and their inhibition on the bioassay.   
9.15.1 Characterisation of (2) 
The analyte had the molecular formula C10H8O3S2 and a DBE of 7, which suggested it was TDA derived. It 
was not possible to determine the structure or fragments from MS and MS/MS.  It was tested in the 
bioassay as one well (10) (Figure 26). It had an inhibition zone of 1.3 cm, which showed that the compound 
inhibited growth of V. anguillarum. A dilution series would be needed in order to tell if the compound 
inhibits at lower concentrations.  
A search was performed for the formula in the data file for the 13C-labelling experiment. The compound 
was not found which meant that the compound either was not formed during minimal growth conditions 
or that it was formed during the extraction and purification process. From the formula, it could be 
ethylated TDA which could have been formed from during extraction with EtOAc.  
9.15.2 Characterisation of (5) 
The molecular formula of (5) was C11H10O3S2 and it had a DBE of 7. In MS/MS, it had a similar fragmentation 
pattern to TDA, which suggested that the additional C3H6 was lost as one fragment (Figure 35). On the other 
hand, it fragmented differently to MeTDA. It was tested in the bioassay as one well (11) (Figure 26). It had 
an inhibition zone of 1.6 cm which showed that the compound the inhibited growth of V. anguillarum. A 
dilution series is needed to determine if the compound inhibits at lower concentrations.  
A search was performed for the formula in the data file for the 13C-labelling experiment. The compound 
was not found; similarity to (2), it meant that the compound either was not produced during minimal 
growth conditions or that it was formed during the extraction and purification process.

9.15.3 Characterisation of (7) 
The molecular formula was C9H10OS3 with a DBE of 5. In the bioassay it was added in well (12) (Figure 26). It 
obtained an inhibition zone of 1.0 cm where the well was 0.8 cm. It showed that the compound had a 
minimal inhibitory effect at V. anguillarum, but the sample was only visible as a small droplet before being 
dissolved for the bioassay, so it might be due to addition of a very small amount.  
This compound was found in the data file for the 13C-labelling experiment as a minor peak. This meant that 
the compound was biosynthesised by P. inhibens. This increases the interest of the compound, but further 
studies are needed in order to elucidate the structure and its inhibitory effect.  
9.15.4 Characterisation of (4) and (6) 
Compound (4) and (6) were not purified due to time limitations. An optimised method would be needed. 
EICs for the compounds were extracted from the 13C-labelling experiment, which showed that (4) was 
present and (6) was not. Hence, (4) is a metabolite made by P. inhibens which would be interesting to study 
further. On the other hand, (6) might only be produced under nutrient rich conditions as in the Marine 
Broth.
