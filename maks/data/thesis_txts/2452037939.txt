Abstract
The main goal of this project is to evaluate the effectiveness and efﬁciency of introducing
pins connection between tempered glass elements. The great advantages of using pins in
tempered glass come from the compressive residual stresses, which are accumulated in the
outer layer of the tempered glass element, implying that even a connection with a small
pin is capable to carry a heavy load. Furthermore, the drilling procedure in tempered glass
implies a redistribution of the residual stress which doubles the capacity of the element.
The conclusion of the project is the deﬁnition of an optimal drilling depth which makes
the steel pins yielding before the failure of the glass, leading to an elastoplastic behavior
of the pin connection. Furthermore, a model is implemented to describes the reaction
of the glass to the load applied by the pins, demonstrating that the maximum stresses
recorded in the glass are of the same kind of the redistributed residual stresses.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction and hypothesis
1.1
The subject
Glass has been using as a construction material since centuries, just from an aesthetic and
energetic-saving point of view. Since decades, the interest in using the glass as a structural
material has been increasing and in turn the related researches. The glass itself does not
suit the most important structural characteristic, since it is a brittle material and it does not
behave plastically, implying a sudden rupture and loss of structural capacity. The glass
used for structural design is usually tempered, which is the results of a heat treatment
explained widely in the following chapters. The treatment increases the capacity of the
glass implying a wider possibility of use. From a user point of view, the tempered glass is
quite problematic because the manipulation of the elements after the tempering process
is usually avoided due to its difﬁculties. Besides, the partial drilling of the glass after the
tempering process leads to a redistribution of the stresses and in turn to the high capac-
ity of the hole, which overcomes the disadvantages of the brittle behavior and makes the
glass useful for structural design and for being a carrying element.
The present work aims to prove that the tempered glass can be considered in every way a
structural material, which offers high performance from the design and the aesthetic point
of view. The experiments aim is to deﬁne some ideal characteristics of a joint between
tempered glass elements made by steel pins, where the failure of the steel elements occurs
before the glass one, leading to an elastic-plastic behavior of the connection.

1.2
State of the Art
Multiple types of research have been carried out by the Civil Engineering Department of
the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), aiming to describe precisely the behavior
of the tempered glass, the post-tempering drilling process and its effects.
One of the initial research is focused on the possibility to partially drill the tempered
glass to allow the assemblage of mechanically attached brackets [Nielsen, 2012]. Ini-
tially, the ﬂaw distribution of the glass surface is neglected and a plate is studied, ending
up with the result that the drill must not exceed the compressive zone (external layer),
which has a depth of 20-25% of the total thickens of the plate. It is noted that the drilling
process implies a redistribution of the stresses around the hole surface, which leads to
an increase of residual tangential stresses around the surface of the borehole up to 100%.
During the analysis It has been discovered that the higher is the diameter of the hole the
more pronounced is the increase of stresses on the surface of the boreholes. Due to lack
of measurement tool, to deﬁne correctly and precisely the redistribution of the stresses
on the hole a numerical model of the drilling is created with ABAQUS CAE. The stress
distribution of the tempered glass is reconstructed in the model by the thermal expansion
coefﬁcient and in turn the thermal strain, leading to the stress state through the thickness
of the glass. The Fig. (1.1) and (1.2) described the results of the current paper, pointing
out the increment of residual stresses implied by the drilling process with to different
maximum depths.
Figure 1.1: Contour plot of tangential stresses when drilling in tempered glass. The plots
are from an ax-isymmetric model of a 19mm glass where the center of the hole is on the
left side and the depth of the drilling, d, ranges between 0 and 1.2mm.[Nielsen, 2012]

1.4
Research method
The present project is based on experimental analysis, where several specimens are tested,
monitoring their behavior under a certain applied action. An initial analytical analyse is
carried out to deﬁne the possible capacity of the element, the optimal specimen’s dimen-
sion and the equipment needed during the execution. Secondly, the chosen system is
implemented in a Fine Element Program where the specimens are tested in a similar sit-
uation of the actual physical experiment. Along with the implementation of the system
in the FEM program, the basic geometric, physic and chemical properties are measured
on the specimen, ending with a statistical analysis of the specimen family. Afterword, the
specimen is tested and the results are compared to the analytical and FEM’s ones.

2.1
Structural Glass: composition and physical
properties
Composition and chemical properties
Glass is an inorganic product of a fusion process characterized by a cooling rate which
leads to a solid condition without crystallization. The process leads to a material that does
not have a regular geometrical crystals network, but it is characterized by an irregular
network of oxygen, silica and soda atoms. In order to reach this amorphous composition,
during the cooling process the viscosity is constantly controlled until solidiﬁcation. The
solidiﬁcation temperature is deﬁned as transition temperature, even though the liquid to
solid transition happens gradually and not suddenly, as is deﬁned by a single temperature
value. There are different kinds of glass depending on the composition, such as soda-lime-
silica glass and Borosilicate glass. The former is mainly used for common structures and
it is the one used in the experiment at the base of this master’s project. For silica glass, the
transformation temperature, Tg, is about 530 ◦C and its chemical composition is described
precisely by Tab. 2.1.

Physical properties
One of the crucial reason why glass is used so widely in the construction and the design
ﬁelds is its optical properties, which depends on the dimension, chemical composition,
and applied coating. The glass absorbs a large percentage of UV radiation remaining
completely transparent, furthermore, long-wave infrared radiations are blocked by the
Si-O group. On the other hand, the most important reason why glass is avoided in the
construction is that its perfect elastic and isotropic behavior lead to a brittle fracture mode.
Which means that local stress concentrations are not redistributed. The plastic behavior of
a common glass beam can be reached by applying a metal bar on its bottom, assembling a
mechanical reinforced beam [J.H. Nielsen, 2007]. The tensile strength of the glass is quite
low, around 30 MPa, compared to the compressive one, around 800 MPa. Furthermore,
the presence of ﬂaws on the glass surface decreases drastically the tensile capacity. Glass
elements reach the tensile failure when the critical stress at the tip of one ﬂaw is reached.

2.2
Glass production
There are two common production methods: ﬂoat glass and cast-glass/cast-proﬁle. In the
latter, the molten glass is poured continuously between two rollers which are positioned
at such a distance to create the necessary thickness of the panel, anyway this method is
outdated because the ﬂoat glass gives glass panels with higher precision in thickness and
less imperfection on the surface.
Float glass production
The major advantages of this production, besides the ones above mentioned, are low
cost, optimal optical quality, wide availability and the high reliability of large panes pro-
duction. Those are the reason why about 90% of glass panes are produced with this
method nowadays. The prices is divided in three steps: melting (1600 −1800 ◦C), form-
ing (800−1600 ◦C) and cooling (100−800 ◦C). The solid glass is melted in a furnace and
then poured in a shallow pool of molten tin, to lead the liquid glass to spread and form
a ﬂat surface. Afterward, it is gradually cooled and drawn on to rollers before entering
a long oven, called lehr. The annealing lehr cools slowly the material to avoid the intro-
duction of residual stresses within the glass. The possible thickness produced with this
method varies from 5 to 25 mm, depending on the speed of the roller before the annealing
lehr. The production process is continuous and wastage-less because all the unwanted or
broken pieces are re-melted back in the furnace. The two sides of the panes are not com-
pletely identical, because during the process one side is in direct contact with the melted
tin and some tin atoms goes into the glass through diffusion. It has been discovered that
the side exposed to the tin bath is slightly weaker than the other one, anyway the reason is
not the tin bath itself but the contact between the rollers and the panes, which also gives
the slightly undulated section. The production process is schematically described by Fig.
(2.1).

2.3
Tempering process
The tempering process aims to create a residual stress state featuring compressive stresses
near and on the surface and tensile stresses in the core of the glass panes. Since in the
core, there is no presence of ﬂaws an induced tensile stress state does not inﬂuence the
reliability of the glass. On the other hand, the surface of the glass is covered by ﬂaws,
hence creating a compressive stress state on the surface limits their growth, increasing the
reliability of the element. The tempering process is composed of three steps:
• Cleaning
• Heating
• Quenching
There are two different types of tempered glass: fully tempered and heat strengthened
glass. The main difference between the tempered glass and the annealed glass is the
residual stresses and in turn the fracture pattern, which is described by Fig. (2.2).
Figure 2.2: The fracture pattern of different types of glass, from the left: annealed, heat-
strengthened and tempered glass. [Matthias Haldimann, 2007]
The fully tempered glass is characterized by the highest residual stresses level and
small dimension of the pieces when it breaks, about 1 cm2. After being properly cleaned,
the glass is heated up to 610−675 ◦C and then rapidly cooled down. This procedure leads
to a fastest cooling process on the surface of the pane and slower in the middle. The heat
strengthened glass is produced in the same way as the fully tempered but with a lower
cooling rate, which leads to lower residual stresses and bigger fragments in the fracture
pattern. Therefore, the remaining load-carrying capacity after fracture is higher than in
the case of the fully tempered thanks to the fracture behavior closer to the annealed glass.
Another innovative method to produce tempered glass does not involve the thermal ef-
fects of the glass and consists of the exchange of sodium ions in the glass surface by

The experiment consists of a 4-point bending test. This speciﬁc conﬁguration has been
chosen to apply a state of pure bending at the joint location, avoiding shear stresses and in
turn having a uniform load applied on the pins. Furthermore, the pin characteristics and
the residual stresses of the tempered glass are assumed to be constant, so each pin reacts
in the same way. The experiments are carried out along the total duration of the master
thesis and it is subdivided into different steps:
• 1st Phase: the initial phase of the experiment deals with the deﬁnition of the set-
up, general overview of the tests and the initial analytical considerations. Then,
the deﬁnition of the specimens and the basic characteristic useful during the post-
analysis are measured, such as the dimension and residual stresses.
• 2nd Phase: the following phase consists of the drilling process of the glass. This
process is not easily deﬁnable along with the timeline because the drilling is car-
ried out alongside with every single test. Which means that not all the specimens
are drilled at the same time. Some of them are drilled in the initial phase of the
experiment and others just before the last tests.
• 3rd Phase: The third phase consists of the physical assemblage of the beam and the
deﬁnition of all the necessary measurement tools. Then, two pilot tests are carried
out to have an overall view of the experiment procedure and post-analysis of the
data.
• 4th Phase: this speciﬁc phase of the experimental work is the actual tests, carried
out for 20 specimens
• 5th Phase: Post-processing of the data recorded during the test and FEM analysis.

pins than what is applied in reality. This conclusion is based on the fact that the diameter
of the hole, 1.7 mm, does not match perfectly the diameter of the pin, 1.5 mm, implying
that in any conﬁguration the probability of the pins all working together is almost zero.

In other words, the dimension of the hole is larger than the diameter of the pin leaving the
pins able to move slightly and affecting the contact between the pins and the surface of
the hole. In order to tackle this problem, an adhesive layer is utilized between the glass
and the pin. To some extent, the real forces might be extrapolated by the experiment in
case the working pins are localized and counted.
First, the Consistent Rotation of the beam is seen in specimens: GB04, GB05, GB13,
GB17, GB18, and GB19. There are two different causes for the beam rotation: one is
the failure of the external layer of the glass, which implies a free movement of the pin,
secondly is the Consistent Yielding of the pin. The former is experienced with GB04,
where the drilling depth is just 1 mm. The latter occurs in GB05, GB13, GB17, GB18,
and GB19. The rotation is easily seen in Fig. (4.5), where the curves after reaching the
maximum load values start a gradual unloading. This state is given by the continuous ap-
plication of the displacement on the beam, but since the pins have failed, the connection
is not able to carry anymore load and the curves drop until reaching a state of zero load
or close to it.
The Consistent Rotation is a direct effect of the Consistent Yielding of the pins. The
higher is the amount of pins yielded the higher is the rotation of the joint. When the
yielding stress is reached in the steel element its deformation is particularly pronounced,
implying, in turn, a pronounced rotation of the joint. The tests where the Consistent
Yielding of the Pin is appreciable are GB05, 10, 12, 13, 15, 16, 17, 18 and 19. The
effect is visible when the L-D1 curve changes from a linear path to a curved one or an
almost horizontal path. Due to the different behavior of the glass (linear-elastic) and the
steel pins (elastoplastic) the majority of the L-D curves are characterized by two distinct
zones: in the ﬁrst one, the pins are not yielded yet and the L-D curve is a straight line
following the elastic behavior of the pin and the perfectly elastic-brittle one of the glass;
the second one instead is characterized by a curved L-D displacement, which points out
the plastic behavior of the pin. In the nine cases mentioned above the plastic range of the
pins is reached, in the other the glass fails ﬁrst.
Following the above paragraph, it is concluded that the tested specimen has two types
of failure: brittle, due to the failure of the glass before the failure of the pin and vice

and nothing is holding the pins in place. This is desired as a local failure of the glass. The
compressive stresses generated by the tempering process allow the glass to break locally
on its surface while the rest of the external layer holds the entire element together.
To conclude, it is demonstrated that the optimal drilling depth in the case of a 10 mm
tempered glass specimen is above 1 mm and below 1.5 mm, since the yielding capacity
of the pins is not activated.

4.3
Optimal Drilling Depth
The results of the test clearly point out that while proceeding with this type of connec-
tion, there is a range of drilling depth which optimizes the capacity and particularly the
failure type of the entire beam. While using a connection between steel and tempered
glass element is always expected that the brittle failure of the glass is reached before the
plastic one of the steel. This makes the use of those two materials particularly hard and
underestimated, because in any kind of construction the ﬁrst goal is the safety of the ﬁnal
user and in any case the failure of the glass must be avoided, particularly the one of the
tempered glass, which is explosive and might be way more dangerous than the simply
ﬂoated one. Trough the experiments carried out is demonstrated that while using this
kind of innovative connection there is an optimal drilling depth which makes the joint
more safe and resistant, leading to the failure of the steel before the one of the glass. This
speciﬁc rage of depth activates the yielding capacity of the steel pin before reaching the
failure of the glass, ending in an elastoplastic behavior of the entire beam, characterized
by high rotation and avoiding the glass failure.

starting point of any sort of research. The criterion used to describe the common rule
while drilling in tempered glass is described by the following parameter:

4.5
Possible improvements
The experiment results and execution are completely based on the ability of the user to
execute each phase, which means that some improvements might be carried out in future
studies.
First, the depth of the holes is executed with a vertical driller which is maneuvered man-
ually with a tolerance of ±0.05 mm, meaning that probably the depth varies slightly for
each hole. Secondly, the diameter of the hole is 1.7 mm and the diameter of the pins is
1.5 mm. The gap of 0.2 mm between the pin and the hole makes possible the assemblage
of the beam since the position of the holes is usually a bit shifted from their expected
position. Lastly, the pin is composed by two elements, the actual pin which is inserted in
the glass and a second element which ﬁxes the pin to the steel plate. The assemblage is
particularly problematic because when the pin is inserted in the second element it usually
bent slightly while subjected to the compressive force. This might lead to a concentration
of the stress in the hole because the edge of the pin is pressing on the glass surface, affect-
ing the capacity of the joint. Furthermore, the number of pins that are actually working
is unknown in the present project. In future studies will be interesting to know the exact
amount, in order to deﬁne the real force acting on the pin and to deﬁne the stiffens of the
specimen related to the amount of working pin.
All these problems might be easily avoided utilizing a high precision machine during
the assemblage of the pins and particularly during the drilling of the glass.

A Fine Element Method analysis is used to describe the distribution of the stresses in the
tempered glass while subjected to a pin load. The analysis is focused not on the entire
behavior of the pin conﬁguration, but just on one of the holes. The study is based on the
assumption that the pins are far enough from each other to avoid the overlapping of the
effect, meaning that the stresses applied by the pin on the glass in one hole location do
not reach either the other hole or the other hole’s inﬂuence area.
The model is based on the data of the test on the specimen GB13, characterized by
the maximum force applied to the pin equal to Fpin = 1033.15 N and a surface residual
stresses of σres.comp = 82.21 MPa
5.1
Explanation of the numerical model
Elements and Boundary Conditions
The numerical model is composed of 1 solid element and 1 shell elements. The shell
represents the glass beam and the solid represents the portion of the glass where the pin
is inserted. The two different elements are considered to simplify the model as much
as possible while keeping it as close as possible to the real experiment, allowing the
bending of the entire beam and reducing the number of nodes utilized in the analysis.
The shell is used for the portion of the beam where a precise analysis along the thickness
of the element is not needed. Instead, the solid at the pin-hole location is used to get a
precise result of what happens through the thickness of the element. The two-element

Conclusion
In the present project, an innovative steel pin connection between tempered glass ele-
ments is analyzed through experimental tests and Fine Element Method implementation.
This kind of connection is considered since it takes advantage of the redistribution of the
residual stresses on a tempered glass specimen due to its partial drilling. The project aims
to deﬁne some ideal characteristics valid for this type of connection.
Firstly, an initial analysis of the tempered glass specimens is carried out, deﬁning the
distribution of the residual stresses through their thickness. This initial part leads to the
conclusion that there is a depth limit while drilling in tempered glass, which is the 20 % of
the thickness. The exceeding of this limit would cause the breakage of the glass element
and in turn the failure of the joint. Furthermore, it is described how much this limit varies
in different points of the specimen, ending with the conclusion that to have a reliable con-
nection, the drilling depth in the tempered glass must be kept as far as possible from 20 %
of the thickness.
Secondly, the experimental test points out that the optimal drilling depth, for the spe-
ciﬁc specimen considered in the project, is rage from 12% to 13% of the total specimen’s
thickness. This depth range allows the steel pins to yield before the failure of the glass,
leading to an elastoplastic behaviour of the connection. The results also identify some
major problems during the production and assemblage of the specimen, such as the high
precision required while drilling the tempered glass specimen. Furthermore, the tests
emphasize that not all the pins are working and carrying the applied load, leading to a
connection weaker that the ideal case, where all the pins work simultaneously.
Lastly, a Fine Element Method analysis is carried out utilizing a simpliﬁed model, to
understand which are the major effects on the glass subjected to a pin load. The model

Residual stresses Measuring phase
There are different ways to measure the residual stresses in the tempered glass, the chosen
one in the current experiment is the SCALP-04, which is a compact scattered light polar-
iscope for depthwise stress measurement in architectural glass panels. The theory behind
the SCALP is base on the fact that when a beam of light traversing space meets with some
obstacles, these obstacles become secondary sources of light and emit light in all direc-
tions. This effect is due to the incident light which induces a vibration of the electrons in
the secondary source in response to its vibrating electric vector. In solid materials, such
as glass, the scattering is essentially due to in-homogeneity. In case of tempered glass,
the in-homogeneity is less than 0.2λ, where λ is the length-wave, hence the scattering
==PAGE:72==
60
APPENDIX A. EXPERIMENT SET-UP AND CALIBRATIONS
is called Rayleigh Scattering. In this particular scattering, the intensity of the scattered
light varies inversely as the fourth power of λ. [Hödemann, 2014] A ray of light that is
incident to a birefringent material is coupled rays: ordinary ray, o-ray, which is linearly-
polarized in the principal stresses direction, σ1 and the extraordinary ray, e-ray, linearly
polarize on the principal stress direction, σ3. In our speciﬁc analysis the two principal
stress directions are deﬁned as:
σ1 = σx
(A.1a)
σ2 = σy
(A.1b)
Since the o-ray and the e-ray have different wave fronts in birefringent material an optical
retardation between the two direction is created, which implies a different stress recorded
in the x and y direction in any point of the element.
The tools used during the measurement are:
• SCALP-04
• Immersion liquid - alcohol
• Specimens
• Glass element positioned just below the tested specimens in order to increase the
accuracy of the measurement closed to the bottom face of the tempered glass beam
The measuring procedure is fairly simple and consists of a few steps. Firstly, the glass
element is placed on the table, while on top is applied some alcohol. Secondly, the glass
beam is placed on top of the glass element, in turn, the surface, where the scalp is applied,
is cleaned and some immersion liquid is spread on the surface. Then the scalp connected
to the PC is applied on a speciﬁc point on the glass beam. Lastly, any source of light is
turned off and the measurement is recorded. The procedure is repeated for each specimen
with the SCALP applied in the same point, recording in x and y-direction.
To collect the data of both direction, the scalp is rotated with an angle of 90 deg on the
glass beam, Fig. (A.6) shows the position of the scalp during the x-direction measure-
ment. Fig. (A.6) describes how the immersion liquid is applied on the specimen and
the glass element and points out that the laser beam must go trough both layer of liquid,
otherwise the measurement on the bottom side of the specimen will be imprecise.

Drilling phase and Assemblage
As it has been already stated the drilling phase is particularly difﬁcult to locate in the
timeline of the experiment as a start-and-end phase since the drilling is carried out al-
ways before the speciﬁc test. The advantage of drilling separately the specimens is that
the procedure might slightly change between the ﬁrst drill to the last one, to model the
drilling process based on the experience win with the experiment executions. The drilling
procedure and assemblage is composed of the following steps:
1. Cleaning of the work plane and specimen
2. Marking of the hole position with a small drilling tip on both side
3. Drilling
4. Glue and tape application (in the considered case)
5. Assemblage
Any time that the tip of the vertical driller used in the process touches the glass, a high
amount of water is needed to avoid the tip warming and its malfunction, and to clean
the hole from the waste powder of the glass. Furthermore, to create a smooth and clean
==PAGE:75==

surface, without the propagation of big ﬂaws the drilling is done gradually. The second
step is carried out with the help of a steel element especially created for this experiment,
see Fig. (A.8).
Figure A.8: The sketches of the steel element used during the drilling process.
The element is placed on the glass and used as a guide for the drilling patter.
The holes diameter is constants for all the specimen, equal to 1.7 mm, on the contrary,
the depth varies (1.7 −1.5 −1.3 −1.2 −1.0 mm) in order to analyze the different
behaviours. During the test 4 different signals are recorded and analyzed, which are:
• Applied displacement by the testing machine
• Applied load on the specimens
• Displacement LVDT-000
• Displacement LVDT-044
All signals are in voltage and they must be translated in mm or N, depending on their
identity. Before the assemblage of the joint, some glue is applied to the holes. The curing
time for the glue is at least 16 hours, meaning that from the assemblage of the pin to its
test there must be a gap of almost 1 day.

the specimen the machine would still try to reach the load speciﬁed in the setting, leading
to the malfunction of the machine. The tests are composed by 10 steps each, increasing
the load from zero to 10 kN in the ﬁrst, TEST1, and to 1 kN in the second, TEST2. Ba-

GB10
The GB10 is characterized by a drilling depth of 1.2 mm, the Square conﬁguration, S,
consistent yielding of the pins and brittle failure of the specimen. The graph on the
left of Fig. (B.38) describe the behaviour of the beam, pointing out the initial linearity,
the late non-linearly and the brittle failure. In this test, the yielding capacity of the pin
is activated around 1000 N, when the graph shows the change from the linear to the
non-linear behaviour. After the maximum applied load is reached, the curve drops and,
afterwards, the load increase again. The ﬁrst drop is the consequence of the local failure
of the glass or the pins in one location and the sudden increase of capacity just after the

any point of a material, which depends linearly on normal stress in the same plate. The
yield surface is a composite of a shear criterion, Mohr-Coulomb surface, and an optional
tension cutoff criterion, Rankine Surface.

touches the Mohr circle for the state of yield stress in the plane of the minimum and
maximum principal stresses, see Fig. (C.6).
