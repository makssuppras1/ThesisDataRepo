ABSTRACT 
In general, the conditions of Danish primary schools are poor, especially the indoor climate. A good indoor 
climate is important to ensure health, wellbeing and performance for pupils. A good building is defined by its 
overall performance, including the indoor climate, aspects of cost, the level of maintenance, energy usage, 
etc. When renovating primary school different proposals are considered and it is complicated to make an 
overall comparison to decide among the proposals, considering relevant aspects of the initiatives.  
A multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) approach was utilized and tested for supporting and improving the 
decision-making. A number of MCDM approaches and calculation methods exist. In this study the AHP, SAW 
and TOPSIS methods were tested. The AHP method was utilized to determine weights, expressing the 
importance of each criterion. The SAW and TOPSIS methods were used to make the MCDM evaluation utilizing 
data of indoor climate parameters, for instance CO2 concentration, and cost, among others. An alternative 
TOPSIS method was developed to handle indoor climate parameters, like temperature, where an ideal 
temperature is defined. 
Weightings of criteria expressing the relative importance of the criteria were assigned through subject 
responses of different questionnaires. Main criteria were assigned by project managers from ‘DTU Campus 
Service’ and ‘Københavns Ejendomme’, where wellbeing was assigned highest weight. Detail criteria of mainly 
indoor climate qualities were assigned by students and professors of DTU with great knowledge on the indoor 
climate. Thermal conditions were given highest priority, followed by air quality, acoustic conditions and finally 
lighting conditions. All criteria weightings were organized and distributed in a systematic hierarchy. A selection 
of criteria weights was used in different case studies where relevant.  
The MCDM approach was tested through three different case studies. One case study dealt with an overall 
evaluation of classrooms based on indoor climate qualities of air quality, thermal-, acoustic-, and lighting 
conditions as well as functional aspects of i.e. window properties, and volume per pupil. Measurements carried 
out for 122 classrooms in 43 Danish primary schools, from a previous study, were included in the MCDM 
evaluation. The results supplied great overview for comparison and communication as well as for supporting 
decisions for prioritizing renovation initiatives needed for different classrooms.  
The second case study referred to a study made on comparing different ventilation approaches, including 
relevant criteria of indoor climate parameters, cost and usage. Criteria of cost and pupil performance 
dominated the MCDM evaluation as large variations existed between the alternatives in combination with 
weights assigned for these criteria. The final MCDM evaluation indicated a preferable solution of using 
automatic windows with exhaust fans, closely followed by a decentralized mechanical ventilation system.  
The fourth case study was based on a fictional case, considering different retrofit alternatives for a school, i.e. 
general upgrade on conditions, or additionally on functionality aspects, or finally demolishing the school and 
build new. Two MCDM evaluations were used to compare the different alternatives indicating which 
renovation alternative was more preferable. One evaluation was based on simple qualitative scales and 
another on more detailed evaluation utilizing quantified data. The different approaches are ideal at different 
stages of the project process, depending on the level of information on different scenarios.  
The MCDM approach provided an opportunity for objectively and systematically comparing and deciding 
among different alternatives by prioritizing relevant aspects to improve the conditions on primary schools. The 
method allowed for including indoor environmental qualities quantitatively or qualitatively in the evaluation.

RESUMÉ 
Det er velkendt, at de fleste danske folkeskoler er gamle bygninger med forringet almen stand og nedsat 
vedligeholdelse og dårligt indeklima. En bygnings præstation er kendetegnet ved bl.a. et godt indeklima, 
funktionalitet, sund økonomi, minimal vedligeholdelse, lavt energiforbrug, m.fl. Da et sundt indeklima er vigtigt 
for at styrke sundhed, trivsel og præstation hos skoleelever synes dette aspekt væsentligt i forbindelse med 
restaurering af bygninger for at kunne evaluere bygningens samlede præstation.  
Dette speciale undersøgte anvendelsen af en matematisk tilgang i form af multi-kriterie beslutningstagen 
(Mult-Criteria Decision-Making, MCDM) med henblik på at støtte beslutningstagen i forbindelse med 
renovering af danske folkeskoler. Der findes forskellige fremgangsmåder og beregningsmetoder for MCDM, 
hvor dette projekt tog udgangspunkt i følgende tre metoder: AHP, SAW og TOPSIS. AHP metoden blev anvendt 
i forbindelse med at tildele vægtning af forskellige kriterier, som udtrykker deres relative betydning og 
prioritering i forhold til hinanden. SAW- og TOPSIS metoderne blev anvendt til helhedsvurderinger, hvor bl.a. 
indeklima parametre, såsom temperaturer, indgik i vurderingen sammen med andre parametre som pris. 
TOPSIS metoden blev modificeret, således indeklimaparametre med ideelle værdier blev tilgodeset.    
Der blev foretaget undersøgelser af vægtning af forskellige kriterier ved brug af spørgeskemabesvarelser. 
Kriterier blev systematisk inddelt i et hierarki for hoved-, del- og detaljekriterier. Hovedkriterierne blev 
vurderet af projektledere fra ’Københavns Ejendomme’ og ’DTU Campus Service’, hvor velvære fik angivet 
højest vægtning. Del- og detaljekriterier for bl.a. indeklimaparametre blev vurderet af professorer og 
studerende på DTU, hvor termiske forhold blev tildelt højest vægt, efterfulgt af luftkvalitet, akustiske forhold 
og til sidst lysforhold. De forskellige parametervægte blev anvendt i tre forskellige casestudier.  
Den første case omhandlede helhedsvurderinger af 122 klasselokaler fordelt på 63 skoler i Danmark med fokus 
på indeklimaparametre og funktionelle forhold. Helhedsvurderingerne gav overblik til sammenligning af de 
forskellige klasselokaler, som dermed kunne indikere klasselokaler med behov for forbedring samt hvilke 
aspekter der burde forbedres.   
En anden case var baseret på et tidligere projekt, hvor målinger af bl.a. luftkvaliteten i klasselokaler med 
forskellige ventilationseftermonteringer indgik i en samlet vurdering sammen med pågældende priser for 
forbrug for de forskellige systemer. Ud fra MCDM metodens resultater, blev automatisk vinduesåbning med 
udtræksfaner vurderet til den bedste løsning efterfulgt af decentral ventilation.  
Et tredje casestudie tog udgangspunkt i at sammenligne tre renoveringsalternativer til forbedring af en fiktiv 
skole. Der blev i den forbindelse foretaget en simpel MCDM beregning, hvor kvalitative bedømmelser af 
udvalgte kriterier indgik. Ligeledes blev der foretaget en mere detaljeret vurdering, hvor også kvantitative 
inputs blev anvendt. Den simple beregning rangerede en ny bygning højest, mens den detaljerede beregning 
vurderede en større transformation højest. Forskellene i inputs påvirkede resultaterne og medførte forskellen. 
Hver MCDM metode blev vurderet brugbar men på forskellige tidspunkter i processen - den simple i de 
indledende idefaser og den detaljerede i de senere faser, hvor der er samlet mere information om de 
forskellige løsninger.  
MCDM metoden gav mulighed for beslutningsstøtte ved objektivt og systematisk at sammenligne forskellige 
renoveringstiltag for folkeskoler for at forbedre forholdene. Gennemsigtigheden ved sammenligning kan også 
forbedre kommunikation mellem beslutningstagerne. Til sammenligningen af forskellige renoveringstiltag 
giver metoden desuden mulighed for at inkludere kvantitative og kvalitative indeklimaparametre i 
bedømmelsen.

1 INTRODUCTION 
Danish primary schools in general seem outdated and are in need for renovation. The quality of the indoor 
environments is poor, which especially affects wellbeing, health, absenteeism and performance of pupils. As 
pupils spend large amounts of their time in these environments they should be improved. Actions to renovate 
schools have been taken, often with initiatives to reduce the heat loss and thus the energy usage, indicating 
priorities of cost savings. These initiatives tighten the building envelope and may reduce the air change rate, 
compromising the indoor climate further. Recent studies indicate that initiatives to improve the indoor climate 
have not been undertaken or the effect of the initiatives has not been visible.  
Wellbeing is expressed through i.e. Indoor environmental qualities, including air quality, thermal, acoustic and 
lighting conditions. Wellbeing is part of social aspects of a building expressing soft values, which also includes 
aesthetics, cultural conditions, functionality, flexibility, to name a few. These soft values are difficult to quantify 
and express through economic perspectives thus often not included in the overall decisions on which 
renovation initiatives to carry out. However, all the soft values support the activities carried out in a building 
and it is the activities carried out which creates profit.   
For existing primary schools, wellbeing, functionality, and the technical condition of the school are important 
aspects to consider when planning renovating. Other factors of economic and perhaps environmental 
character need consideration as well to choose sustainable solutions. It is difficult to objectively and 
systematically evaluate different renovation initiatives considering all relevant criteria.  
Multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) is an approach which is used in many contexts, also in the building 
industry, i.e. the DGNB certification. An MCDM approach can be based on mathematical calculations, which 
through normalization and weighting distributions between different criteria delivers a final score, comparing 
all alternatives included in the evaluation. The purpose of the MCDM methods in general are to make overall 
evaluations and comparisons of different alternatives or to indicate an optimal alternative.  
The purpose of this study was to improve and support decision-making for renovation initiatives for Danish 
primary schools through a multi-criteria decision-making approach by integrating indoor environmental 
qualities in the overall assessment.

2 BACKGROUND 
2.1 PRIMARY SCHOOLS IN DENMARK 
Danish Primary Schools have been constructed over a large period of time. It is stated that around 90 % were 
built before the year 2000 and that about 52 % were built in the period 1960s-1970s (Berg et al., 2015; Larsen 
et al., 2016). With the usual wear and tear of school buildings, this suggests that most of the schools are in 
need of renovation, depending on the level of renovation done until now. Nevertheless, not only the state of 
the construction is important, also the physical framework needs an upgrade to fit new teaching principles 
and methods. Many schools were built at a time where teaching occurred in classrooms and was based on 
quiet surroundings for concentration and where breaks happened at the same time for everyone. Today 
however, teaching is more varied both where, when and how it is carried out (Clausen et al., 2017b) 
Besides the technical condition of the primary schools, an important reason for renovating schools is the 
indoor environment, which has caused large concerns. Great efforts have been made in recent years to 
increase awareness on the indoor environment in Danish primary schools. Pupils in Denmark spend more than 
30 % of their time in classrooms (Toftum et al., 2017), where the majority offer a bad indoor environment.  
A study from 2015-2016 where measurements were made in 250 classrooms at 60 schools, showed that in 
47% of the occupied hours the CO2 concentration was above the recommended 1000ppm (Clausen et al., 
2017b). A study showed that CO2 concentrations in classrooms were proportional with absenteeism as an 
increase in average CO2 concentration of 100 ppm resulted in 0,2% increased absenteeism, which is equivalent 
to half a school day per year (Clausen et al., 2017b). Further, the study indicated that by increasing the 
ventilation rate by 1 l/s per person in a classroom the absenteeism was reduced by 1,6 %.  
Another study showed that by increasing the ventilation rate in classrooms and improve the regulation of 
temperature speed and precision for tasks requiring concentration was increased between 2,2% and 15% 
(Clausen, et al., 2017b). Intensity of light, colour temperature and dynamics of light also have an effect for the 
wellbeing of pupils as well as their performance in tests of concentration (Clausen et al., 2017b). Insufficient 
lighting can cause symptoms as headache, fatigue or even make you fall asleep (Larsen et al., 2016). Acoustics 
are also important as it affects hearing perception, voice recognition, understanding of speech, and abilities 
to reflect. All these abilities are reduced in noisy surroundings. A study in 355 primary schools in Finland found 
that the most frequent daily inconvenience in the classrooms were noise (Larsen et al., 2016). The auditory 
working memory and comprehension were negatively affected by noise.  
Children are more vulnerable to bad indoor climate as their lungs are not completely developed and they 
breath in higher volume compared to their body mass (Larsen et al., 2016). Thus, poor indoor climate of the 
primary schools affects health, learning ability and general wellbeing of pupils. In addition, improved indoor 
climate will not only benefit the pupils but also the national economy as research shows that better performing 
pupils will be more productive and become more well educated (Berg et al., 2015). 
“If we in Denmark have to rely on the bright minds of the future, we cannot place our 
children in environments which degrades abilities to concentrate and learn”  
(Jastram, 2017). 
Actions to renovate Danish schools have been made and in 2009 a quality improvement fund was established, 
consisting of 50 billion DKK, which were to be invested in 2009-2018, where 22 billion were dedicated the 
physical environment. The mass experiment in 2009-2014 showed no significant improvement in indoor 
climate.  In 2009 56% of 330 schools all over Denmark had CO2 concentrations above the limit of 1000ppm 
==PAGE:12==
Page 12 | Background  
and in 2014 60% of 262 schools had CO2 concentrations above 1000ppm despite the focus on importance on 
sufficient venting in the classrooms (Berg et al., 2015). These results indicate that no initiatives to improve the 
indoor environment had been carried out or had no visible effect. Upgrades of schools are often motivated to 
reduce energy consumptions (Berg et al., 2015) and most renovation initiatives for schools can be classified as 
initiatives with a higher financial viability (Larsen et al., 2016). This could indicate that initiatives are prioritized 
and carried out if an economic profit exists.  
An evaluation carried out by The Danish Rational Economic Agent Model (DREAM) showed effects on the 
lengths of pupil’s education, effects on sick leave among teachers, and effects on pupil’s test scores and 
thereby productivity by improving the indoor climate. The evaluation showed that an annual saving of 121 
million DKK (at 2011 level) on the primary government budget could be made. The savings were among other 
based on fewer sick days for teachers, higher average PISA score by pupils, thus increasing labour productivity 
(DREAM, 2012).  
Throughout the entire life span of a building the cost of activities in the building, i.e. salaries of workers, are 
much larger than the construction cost (Clausen et al., 2017a). However, it is important to remember that it is 
also the activities in the building which create value and profit. Relative costs and profits throughout a building 
process is illustrated in Figure 2.1 (Clausen et al., 2017a).  
 
FIGURE 2.1: ILLUSTRATION OF THE RELATIVE DISTRIBUTION OF COST AND PROFIT OF A BUILDING PROJECT 
The relative profit of activities is large, thus prioritizing good conditions, i.e. wellbeing for the activates of a 
building is profitable. This also means prioritizing the indoor climate. For instance, a study made by World 
Green Building Council showed that noise can reduce the performance by employees by 66% (Clausen et al., 
2017a). A study made in the period 2015-2017 highlighted the need for prioritizing the indoor climate. It 
suggested that the importance of the indoor climate should be communicated. ‘Good indoor climate’ should 
not be considered an additional investment but instead a ‘bad indoor climate’ is the loss of profit or an 
additional cost. In addition, “the users should realize the need for a good indoor climate and thus demand it”  
(Clausen et al., 2017a). 
2.2 RELEVANT ASPECTS TO PRIORITIZE FOR RENOVATION OF PRIMARY SCHOOLS 
As buildings have long life span, the decisions we make today should have long-term perspectives (Birgisdottir, 
2015). Long-term considerations are expressed through sustainability. There are many ways of defining 
sustainability and one is the ability to sustain a condition during a long period of time (Mortensen et al., 2010). 
A building offering good overall performance through a holistic perspective throughout its entire lifetime is a 
==PAGE:13==
Page 13 | Background 
sustainable building (Green Building Council Denmark, 2013). A sustainable building thus provides good 
conditions for wellbeing, satisfaction, and productivity. This also means creating environments with good 
indoor climate (Realdania, 2017). Sustainability is expressed through three main aspects, social, economic, and 
environmental. These are shortly described in the following and illustrated in Figure 2.2.  
SOCIAL Social sustainability includes aspects of comfort, health, functionality, user satisfaction 
and architectural qualities (Green Building Council Denmark, 2013). The social qualities 
are often referred to as ‘soft values’ and are often expressed in a qualitative manner 
compared to economic and environmental aspects often expressed quantitatively.  
 
ENVIRONMENT Environmental aspects consider impacts of usage and materials, evaluated through a 
life cycle assessment. In addition, it considers use of raw materials and water and the 
effect of actions on local and global environments (Green Building Council Denmark, 
2013).  
 
ECONOMY Economic aspects consider life cycle cost as well as potential for future economic 
development. The life cycle costs include cost of product, installation, maintenance 
and usage and thus considerations on life time for materials needs consideration 
(Green Building Council Denmark, 2013).

Besides criteria of cost and profit, environment and wellbeing, incorporated values of cultural historic quality 
expressing cultural inheritance in existing building are of importance. Cultural qualities include cultural historic 
values as well as aesthetic and architectural values. These qualities are important to consider through a 
==PAGE:14==

sustainable perspective.  The qualities are also specifically important for existing buildings in Denmark. 
Interests for preserving buildings and cultural environments arose as a reaction to large demolitions during 
the post-war period (Slots- og Kulturarvstyrelsen, 2017). Today many buildings in Denmark are assigned a 
preservation value by the SAVE method, reflecting its aesthetic, architectural and cultural values (Slots- og 
Kulturarvstyrelsen, 2017). Depending on the preservation value the renovation initiatives are constricted to 
preserve the qualities integrated in the buildings.    
In most renovation cases of primary schools, the municipality is responsible. The municipality of Copenhagen 
for instance, is in charge of 66 primary schools. The department called ‘Københavns Ejendomme’ (KE) is 
responsible for management of all school buildings in the municipality of Copenhagen. It is also KE who 
suggests a school for renovation, where the politicians make the final decision (Københavns Kommune, 2012).

3 PROJECT OBJECTIVE 
The purpose of this project was to explore the possibilities for improving and supporting evaluations and 
decisions of different renovation initiatives through a multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) approach where 
indoor environmental parameters are included in the evaluation.  
The study further questions quantifications, scales and weights of various parameters, i.e. indoor 
environmental parameters as well as other parameters included in the renovation decisions.  
The MCDM approach has been utilized and tested on case studies of different scales and with different aims 
in order to challenge and test its abilities for different issues.

4 THEORY 
4.1 INTEGRATED DESIGN PROCESS 
In general, a design process is referred to as an iterative, cyclical process. This also applies the integrated 
design process (Figure 4.1) (Heiselberg, 2007). A proposal is generated on behalf of set goals, the proposal is 
further developed, evaluated and afterwards the steps are continued until the result is satisfactory.  
 
FIGURE 4.1: CYCLICAL PROCESS OF THE INTEGRATED DESIGN PROCESS 
The traditional process of building design began with the architect developing a concept and the engineering 
role contributing at the end of the process with the main purpose of redesigning for safety and buildability. 
Architects thus control the earliest design stages with the perspective of creating coherence between usage 
and cultural qualities of a building, however for the owner and society it includes many other aspects (Jensen 
et al., 2011). This traditional process meant that technical-scientific knowledge would not be introduced 
before late in the process. It is stated that about 80 % of the project cost is settled in the first phases (Rosendal, 
2009). In Figure 4.2 is illustrated the connection between cost and influence throughout the time of a design 
process. The opportunities to make changes decrease significantly and the costs to change concepts increase 
along the process (Green Building Council Denmark, 2016; Zimmerman, 2004). As it illustrates, it is a good idea 
to prioritize and invest in the early stages to ensure the best solutions from the beginning.   
 
 
 
==PAGE:18==
Page 18 | Theory  
 
FIGURE 4.2: INFLUENCE AND COST OF PROJECT THROUGHUT THE PROJECT PERIOD 
The integrated design process is a result of increased number of demands for buildings. Demands for energy 
consumption were introduced after the oil crisis in the 1970’s (Berg et al., 2015). Requirements meant that 
scientific knowledge would need to be introduced earlier in the process in order to fulfil the requirements. In 
1990s an increased focus on the building’s symbiotic relationship with the exterior climate originated. This 
lead to  the development of numerous simulation programs in order to get rather fast and precise 
documentation on the design at hand, as well as enhancing transparency for all design team members to 
better understand and follow the process (Jensen et al., 2011). Transparency is a very important factor when 
working in multidisciplinary design teams, as all should understand the effect of the decisions made to better 
understand and challenge them (Jensen et al., 2011).

4.2 MULTI-CRITERIA DECISION-MAKING (MCDM) 
Multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) is a complex approach for identifying a preferable alternative, classify 
alternatives or rank alternatives in a subjective preference order. The term MCDM concerns all existing 
methods, which help make decisions, in cases with more than one criterion. The MCDM approach makes a 
joint assessment of a number of different parameters and support and improve the decisions by being 
efficient, objective, rational, and explicit. In recent years, the interest in MCDM approaches has risen 
exponentially (Mardani et al., 2015).  
For all building design processes, a large number of different criteria are taken into account. Building designers 
have to consider all of them in order to develop good solutions, which can be challenging. It is complicated to 
keep track of all criteria and to decide which solution is the preferable solution, as they might all have different 
pros and cons. This is where an MCDM approach might be useful.  
The MCDM method deals with complex issues by breaking them into smaller components that are afterwards 
reassembled to present an overview to the decision makers. For instance, a simple example of choosing 
between three alternative ventilation systems is shown in Table 4.1. Each alternative is divided into smaller 
components (criteria).

The MCDM approach utilizes assessments and criteria weights to calculate ranking of the alternatives. Based 
on the rankings the decision makers take decisions on which alternative applies best to the problem 
(Søndergaard et al., 2017).  
A very important aspect of MCDM is its ability to illustrate a complex decision through its shared, transparent 
framework (Ezzat et al., 2016). It helps keeping a systematic and wide perspective through a palpable tool, 
which directly highlights issues to be considered and handled with consideration.  
Most MCDM methods deal with alternatives that only vary discretely across different criteria. The criteria can 
consist of both cardinal and ordinal problems and their input can vary between quantitative and qualitative 
assessments in exact values and intervals (Mardani et al., 2015). 
In order to have an optimal solution, all alternatives must be evaluated by common criteria. The criteria are 
defined by weights expressing the relative importance of each of the criteria. Weights are determined by 
involving stakeholders. Relevant stakeholders depend on the case and include persons who can or perhaps 
==PAGE:20==
Page 20 | Theory  
will be affected by the decision (Søndergaard et al., 2017). A major issue is the aggregation of all criteria since 
it has to deal with different kinds of evaluations, scores, values, units, etc. (Mardani et al., 2015). The 
aggregation is important as it sums up a ‘score’ for each alterative, indicating a preferable rank for the 
alternatives.   
A number of different calculations methods exist within MCDM. Each MCDM method has its own strategy for 
selecting the best alternatives (Mardani et al., 2015). In general, they all have four main steps; select criteria, 
assign importance to each criterion (weight), normalize against the same scale and evaluate the overall 
performance expressed in a numeric value. Some of the mathematical approaches are AHP, SAW, and TOPSIS 
(Verney et al., 2011). AHP, which is referred to as Analytical Hierarchy Process, is based on pairwise 
comparisons (Ezzat et al., 2016). SAW stands for Simple Additive Weighting. SAW summarizes weighted criteria 
for each alternative (Tupenaite et al., 2010). TOPSIS ranks all scenarios according to their relative closeness 
towards an ideal solution, based on a set of criteria each with its own weight of importance. The ideal solution 
is a theoretical solution scoring highest on all criteria. These three highlighted methods are very different and 
will be used and tested in this study and thus explained further.

5.2 CALCULATION METHODS 
A number of different mathematical decision methods exists within MCDM. The main steps are the following 
(Roszkowska, 2011): 
STEP 1 
System evaluation criteria that relate system capabilities to goals are established (criteria, 
weights and goal) 
 
STEP 2 
Development of alternative systems for attaining the goals (generate alternatives) 
 
STEP 3 
Alternatives are evaluated in terms of criteria (inputs) 
 
STEP 4 
One of the normative multiple criteria analysis methods are applied (normalization) 
 
STEP 5 
Weights are multiplied for normalized inputs (weighted normalization) 
 
STEP 6 
Scores of each alternative are summarized according to the utilized method (results) 
 
STEP 7 
One alternative is accepted as “optimal” (preferred) 
 
STEP 8 
If the final solution is not accepted, gather new information and go to the next iteration of 
multiple criteria optimization 
 
Three variations of MCDM methods are considered and tested. These include the AHP method, the SAW 
method, and the TOPSIS method. The AHP method is based on pairwise comparisons on a set scale, whereas 
the SAW and TOPSIS methods both have the ability to utilize quantified data, i.e. from measurements, for 
instance CO2 concentrations, temperatures, relative humidity, etc.  
In the following, the steps and fundamentals of each calculation method are explained. They are all explained 
by use of the same school example as was introduced in the previous section; what actions to take for a 
primary school. First the two MCDM evaluation methods of SAW and TOPSIS are explained and compared with 
a slight modification of the TOPSIS method. Afterwards the DR and AHP methods for assigning weights are 
explained.

5.2.6 DETERMINING WEIGHTS 
Assigning weights through groups of people utilizes subjective opinions and converts them into objective 
weighted ranking of preference. In most cases a set of experts are asked about their opinions on importance 
of different aspects of a building project and a set of shared opinions are made. Depending on the group of 
experts as well as the case at hand, group decisions are rather complex as it might involve many conflicting 
factors such as conflicting individual goals, inefficient knowledge, validity of information, individual motivation, 
personal opinion, or power (Roszkowska, 2011).  
For a renovation case, a project group responsible for the build and the relating decisions, could be asked to 
give their opinions. Hence, a set of shared weights could be determined. This would in addition represent and 
define a shared set of expectations and thus indicate focus points of priority for the specific project. This step 
is very important in an integrated design process as it lays a groundwork as well as being a navigator for 
designing the optimal solution in the following stages of the process.    
Two different methods for assigning weights were used in this study, the DR method and AHP method. These 
are explained in the following.  
5.2.6.1 
THE DR METHOD 
A simple way to collect weightings is to ask a relevant group of people to rank criteria according to importance 
of the criteria, for instance between 1 and 9 on a set of criteria. This is referred to as Direct Rating method 
(DR), which uses ”direct numerical ratio judgements of relative attribute importance” (Roberts et al., 2002). 
The method can be implemented in different ways, one way is that the decision maker assigns an arbitrary 
importance of highest value, for instance 9, to the criteria considered most important. Afterwards values are 
given to the second most important etc. The same weight can be given to more than one criterion if they are 
considered equally important. The numerical raw ratings, 𝑤𝑖

criteria sum to one.  
5.2.8 SUMMARY 
The SAW and TOPSIS methods have the abilities to utilize collected data and thus the only subjective inputs in 
the calculation are the weights. However, the SAW method cannot contain input that are equal to zero, which 
reduces its applicability. The SAW method is ideal if inputs are given in intervals, for instance values between 
1-9. Still, none of these two methods provide for weight elicitation and consistency checking of judgements. 
In this study the AHP method was used for assigning weights. The AHP method could also be used for making 
the MCDM evaluations, though not by utilizing quantified data and thus not utilized in this study. The AHP 
method, is based on subjective opinions through pairwise comparisons, including consistency checking. AHP 
is significantly restrained by human capacity and limitation (Roszkowska, 2011). Hence, it would be suitable 
for cases with large numbers of criteria and alternatives and where objective quantified data is provided to 
utilize the SAW or TOPSIS methods. In addition to SAW or TOPSIS, the AHP method could help compute 
weightings (Afshari et al., 2010), through its systematic approach as well as consistency abilities. The AHP 
method assigns weights based on subjective opinions that are translated into objective preferences 
(Roszkowska, 2011). In addition, the DR method was also useful for assigning weights. The AHP method was 
considered more thorough and includes consistency checking, however depending on the number of criteria, 
the AHP method might be too comprehensive. The variation on weights are dependent on the scale chosen 
for the DR method and thus this scale should be considered before selected.  
Despite using the same input and weights, the SAW and TOPSIS methods do not necessarily suggest the same 
ranking of alternatives. The variations occur when results for two alternatives are very close and are caused 
by the calculation approaches of the normalizations and the expression of results. In situations where results 
of two alternatives are close to one another, additional evaluations for the alternatives should be carried out 
to support the results for one alternative or the other.  
Variations of input used in the SAW and TOPSIS methods will not affect the results as long as they still keep 
their shared scaled relationship. However, when alternating the input to an integer value of an interval, the 
results are affected. Similarities are found, but the variation in scale might change the rank between 
alternatives. The differences are caused by slight variations between inputs, which are however increased by 
two different intervals. Thus, slight differences might be neglected or increased. 
An alternative TOPSIS method can be utilized when dealing with indoor climate parameters defined by ideal 
values and thus not optimal at either a maximum or minimum value. The SAW method, however cannot deal 
with this obstacle.  
For both the SAW and TOPSIS methods the results are found by a combination of all input for each alternative 
through the normalization. All inputs thus have the ability to affect the results of other alternatives. Thus, any 
==PAGE:53==
Page 53 | The MCDM Approach 
value of input is relevant and cannot be neglected. If one input for one alternative is neglected, the calculation 
of TOPSIS assumes a value of zero (SAW cannot make this calculation). This value of zero can affect the others, 
as it might define the optimal or ideal value. This means that inputs for all criteria and for all alternatives are 
necessary in order to make these MCDM calculations.

6 CASE STUDIES 
Three different case studies utilizing the MCDM approach were carried out. They are shortly presented below.  
 
CASE 1:  
DANISH PRIMARY 
SCHOOLS  
 
 
A large study was carried out in the period of 2015-2017, including evaluations of 
60 Danish primary schools. The evaluations were carried out for about 250 
classrooms and included measurements of indoor climate parameters as well as the 
physical framework (including information on windows, surface materials, etc.). 
The data expressing the state of all the classrooms were extensive, and an MCDM 
evaluation was used to compare the classrooms.  
 
Aim: To compare the quality of each classroom based on included criteria through 
a MCDM calculation was carried out. Thus, indicating classrooms in need of 
renovation and in addition which improvements are needed compared to other 
classrooms.  
 
Type of MCDM evaluation: Comparison of different alternatives (large scale) 
 
 
CASE 2:  
VENTILATION 
RETROFITS 
 
 
 
When a school is to be renovated it might be helpful to compare different 
renovation alternatives. A study was made on ventilation retrofits in schools, 
comparing costs, the indoor environment, heating- and energy use and 
performance of students, in order to find the optimal solution.  
 
Aim: To use the MCDM approach to find the preferable solution, all relevant results 
from the study included.  
 
Type of MCDM evaluation: Decision among alternatives (small scale) 
 
 
 
 
CASE 3:  
SCHOOL 
RENOVATION 
 
 
Alternatives of different renovation initiatives for an entire school often have 
different advantages and disadvantages of different aspects.  Fictional alternatives 
were conducted for comparison on a number of different criteria, including 
wellbeing, functionality, economy, environment and the technical condition of the 
existing building.  
 
Aim: To make a comparison between a number of renovation alternatives through 
a MCDM calculation and thus finding a preferable alternative, considering all 
relevant criteria. 
 
Type of MCDM evaluation: Decision among alternatives (large scale)

6.2 CRITERIA WEIGHTINGS 
A well performing building indicates a sustainable solution. The three main parameters of sustainability are 
social, economy and environment. The main criteria were re-evaluated and slightly modified to increase focus 
and awareness for renovation cases of existing primary schools. Social aspects include wellbeing, functionality 
and cultural qualities, containing very different impacts in its one score. These three parameters are very 
different and vary in importance for a primary school. Thus, it was chosen that these three parameters should 
be evaluated separately eliminating the joined main social aspect and instead divide it into three main criteria. 
In addition, a sixth main criterion was highlighted, expressing the technical condition of an existing building. 
This was considered important for renovation cases, where the state of the existing building should be taken 
into account. An overview of relevant criteria (main, sub and detail) used in this study is shown in Figure 6.3.

The indoor environment comprises many different parameters. In many studies the indoor climate quality of 
schools is represented by the general criteria of air quality, thermal conditions, acoustics and light conditions.  
For simplicity and as a starting point, these four main characteristics are mainly included in the case studies 
carried out. A short description of each of these criteria is given here.  
AIR QUALITY 
Air quality is often expressed by the concentration of CO2, measured in ppm. CO2 is 
the indicator of pollution, where humans are a significant source of contamination. 
This is the main parameter expressing air quality utilized in this study.

CONDITIONS 
 
Thermal conditions include air temperature, radiant temperature, air velocity and 
relative humidity. It is sometimes evaluated by the predicted mean vote, PMV and 
predicted percentage dissatisfied, PPD. In this study temperature and relative 
humidity were mainly included.

CONDITIONS

Lighting is expressed as daylight, artificial lighting and colour temperature. It is also 
affected by reflectance and glare. In this study daylight factor and mean illuminance

6.3 CASE 1 – DANISH PRIMARY SCHOOLS 
The purpose of this case was to compare the quality of classrooms by use of an MCDM approach including 
indoor environmental qualities. Today buildings in Denmark are classified based on their energy consumption 
through Be15, and perhaps also on their sustainability through classifications like DGNB or Nordic Ecolabel 
(Svanemærket). These classifications encourage building owners to aspire to build quality buildings. A 
tendency among particularly professional building owners is seen for having their building certified 
environmentally and sustainably. “They do not necessarily do this because they are idealists, but instead 
because they want to make long term investments” (Realdania, 2017).  
However, a concern has been raised about the chance that energy savings, environment and sustainability 
certifications take away the focus from the good indoor environment. There is a risk that reducing energy 
happens on behalf of a good indoor environment (Realdania, 2017).  
An extensive study was carried out in the period 2015-2017 on classrooms conditions in Denmark (Clausen et 
al., 2017b). The study included information on 250 classrooms on 60 schools and included measurements of 
indoor climate parameters as well as the physical framework including room size, volume, surface materials, 
etc. Some of these measurements and observations will be used in this case study.   
Classroom characteristics of the presented study were evaluated by the MCDM approach utilizing the 
alternative TOPSIS approach. This approach was selected due to its ability to assign ideal values for 
temperature and relative humidity. The included criteria were in the main criteria groups of wellbeing and 
functionality. Thus, this case study does not take into account cost, perception, pupil performance, energy 
usage, environment, or condition of constructions.  
For some schools of the presented study some parameters would be measured in one classrooms as a 
representative for other classrooms or perhaps the entire school, for instance for lighting conditions. For 
schools where only one classroom had information on light conditions, these conditions were assumed for the 
rest of the classrooms of that school, the same for sound measurements. For schools which had more than 
one classroom with light or sound measurements, the classrooms without either light or sound measurements 
were excluded from the study, to avoid risk of wrongful assumptions. Hence, 122 classrooms on 43 schools 
were included in the MCDM evaluation.  
Some classroom characteristics, such as orientation and surface material were not included in the MCDM 
evaluation. These criteria were neither positive nor negative attributes and thus the variations of these criteria 
could not be compared for being better or worse than other classrooms. For instance, the orientation of a 
classroom was neglected as the orientation does not make a classroom better or worse compared to others. 
Classrooms towards south might have more challenges to uphold a good indoor climate, but this can be 
obtained for instance by use of solar shading. The criteria of an MCDM analysis should be independent, thus 
these criteria were excluded. The criteria included in the evaluation are shown in Figure 6.17.  
The MCDM evaluation was used to compare the different classrooms and thus indicating classrooms with low 
overall quality by the results and hence classrooms in need of improvement.

6.5.3 SUMMARY 
Through the simple SAW evaluation, the new building is more preferable and through the detailed TOPSIS 
evaluation, the transforming alternative is preferable. The inputs were chosen on a similar basis, however the 
value and spread of values for each method varied. In the SAW method integer values between 1-9 were used. 
In the TOPSIS method values varied on larger scales. The large scale supplied an opportunity to gain larger 
difference between alternatives. The large difference gives an opportunity for criteria with large variations to 
have greater impact on the evaluation, compared to the integers between 1-9.   
The detailed evaluation has the opportunity to affect the evaluation directly through quantified values. 
However, in a real-life project collecting all the information would be time consuming. The choice between 
the two methods should consider the purpose of the evaluation, i.e. is it to give an early indication and to 
communicate initial arguments, or is it later in the project stage, where actual information has been collected 
and can be compared directly.  
The selection of criteria is also important. In this example a selection of criteria was chosen for its recognition 
from previous cases as well as incorporating other relevant criteria. In some cases, for instance in early stages, 
and through a simple evaluation not all detailed criteria are needed and maybe only main criteria are 
necessary. The evaluations represent the issues discussed in a project group and can help communicate as 
well as compare different solutions in a systematic, objective, and logic manner, utilizing relevant criteria at 
that specific stage.

7 DISCUSSION 
7.1 CASES 1-3 
The case studies in this study varied in purpose, scale and context. On a smaller scale the overall best 
ventilation retrofit was found and on a larger scale a preferable renovation proposal for an entire school was 
found. A third MCDM variation evaluated a large number of classrooms to indicate which classrooms were in 
more need for upgrade than the others. For all variations of scale and purpose the MCDM method provided 
efficient and systematic evaluation to support decision-making, by also including indoor environmental 
parameters.  
For evaluations where no direct use of MCDM calculation is used it is comprehensive to compare large data 
sets and scenarios systematically, effectively, and objectively. The number and priorities of criteria complicates 
the evaluation, in addition relevant criteria might be difficult to compare. The MCDM method was useful for 
evaluating large data sets as it breaks up the inputs into smaller parts and comparing them individually. In case 
study 1 a matrix consisting of 122 alternatives measured on 15 different criteria was rather simply translated 
into relative values to easily compare the 122 classrooms. In addition, the method provided detailed results 
to identify which parameter lack quality compared to the others.  
The approach of this case study showed potential for providing a method of comparison for a municipality to 
evaluate different schools. The method can indicate poor conditions and thus which schools to prioritize for 
renovation. In addition, the method highlights the qualities that support an alternative compared to the others 
and thus which qualities need improvement.  
For the fictional school example two variations of MCDM approaches were used for choosing between 
different renovation initiatives. A simple evaluation utilizing semi-quantitative inputs based on qualitative 
assessments on scales from 1-9 for all criteria indicated that the preferable solution was demolishing the 
original school and building a new. A detailed evaluation utilized quantitative data for some criteria and semi-
quantitative inputs for soft values, i.e. architectural quality preferred the alternative of transformation 
upgrading the general condition of the building as well as functionality aspects the most preferable solution, 
followed by renovation on the general condition. The difference between the two variations was the variation 
of inputs, since scales from 1-9 might not be able to represent the large differences on for instance 
environmental and economic impacts which varied greatly by quantified data for the detailed evaluation. 
Potential for a simple evaluation was considered as it can be utilized in early stages of a project. The project 
group can evaluate scenarios considered by semi-quantified evaluations. The detailed evaluation is more 
appropriate on a later stage where detailed quantified data for different solutions are known. For instance, 
early in the process it might be useful to make a fast comparison between a number of alternative concepts 
and then later in the process when i.e. two more detailed proposals are made a more detailed evaluation could 
be carried out. Both variations seem useful for a project group as it can evaluate different possibilities (through 
semi-quantitative or quantitative arguments discussed in the group) relatively fast through its systematic and 
objective manner, while increasing communication through its transparency. 
Criteria containing relatively large spread of inputs for different alternatives had big impacts on the results. 
For instance, product cost for retrofit ventilation systems of case study 2 varied from 0 DKK to 60.000 DKK 
between two alternatives and had large impact on the results.

7.2 WEIGHTINGS 
Another important factor significantly affecting the results are the weights expressing the relative importance 
of each criterion. When assigned weights were employed, a very different ranking of preferable alternative 
was obtained than when equal weighting of criteria was applied. This has also been observed in other case 
studies (Søndergaard et al., 2017).   
Weights of relative importance were assigned by different experts. Main criteria were assigned by project 
managers while sub- and detail criteria of indoor environmental qualities were assigned by students and 
professors from DTU with expert knowledge on the subject. Main criteria were defined through a sustainable 
concept and modified to fit renovation issues of primary schools.  
For all assigned weights slight variations between the expert responses were found, indicating that no 
exclusive set of prioritized weightings might exist. Average calculated weights were utilized in the MCDM 
evaluations. Thermal conditions were assigned highest priority among sub criteria followed by air quality, 
acoustic conditions and finally light conditions. A previous study found that thermal conditions of temperature 
and relative humidity significantly affected the perception of air quality. Air was perceived less acceptable with 
increasing temperature (Fang et al., 2000), which supports the results for prioritizing the thermal conditions. 
Another study also estimated that increased satisfaction with temperature provided the greatest 
improvement in self-estimated job performance, followed by increase satisfaction with noise and air quality 
(Frontczak, 2012).    
The spread of weights between subjects on indoor climate qualities might represent the fact that indoor 
climate is evaluated differently by different persons. Indoor environmental qualities are often based on 
‘average person’ and groups of for instance pupils can be more sensitive to impacts than others (Larsen et al., 
2016). A study carried out in 2005 found similar results as no consensus on which improvements should be 
prioritized were found, when there is a limited budget, since subjects did not agree on which environmental 
factor was the most important to improve (Clausen et al. 2008). In the study a group of subjects were given 
individual choices to improve 50% budget conditions of the indoor conditions (air quality, thermal conditions, 
acoustic- and thermal comfort. The study showed that all possible combinations were preferred by somebody. 
The study further showed that the possibility to individually chose which parameters to improve within a given 
budget had a positive effect on acceptability with the overall indoor environment, indicating that to improve 
acceptability in general, more individual choices should be made (Clausen et al., 2008).  
For DGNB weightings (Green Building Council Denmark, 2013) thermal conditions have higher weight 
compared to weights assigned in this study, lowering the summarized weights of light and acoustic conditions 
(Figure 7.1). Lighting conditions also have higher weight for DGNB compared to this study, which results in a 
significantly lower weight for acoustic conditions compared to the weightings found in this study. The DGNB 
weightings are not specified for school environments, but represents various building types. This could indicate 
that acoustic comfort in teaching environments is given higher priority compared to others.

detail-criteria pair-wisely, which is complicated and increases the risk of uncertainty and inconsistency for the 
results. Instead, knowledge on the hierarchic systematic structure on criteria should be utilized and 
implemented. Thus, comparisons should only be made for a set of criteria within one criterion group. In other 
words, main criteria can be compared with one another, as they were by the project managers, sub criteria 
can be compared with one another, however detail criteria should be compared for each sub- or main criterion 
group. For instance, relative humidity should only be compared with other criteria expressing thermal 
conditions and thus not with parameters expressing acoustic quality. This hierarchic structure was not 
considered when the questionnaires were created and thus not implemented.  
Weights are dependent on the group of persons assigning them and it is important to select the persons 
carefully also considering how representative they are (Søndergaard et al., 2017). In this study the numbers of 
responses should be increased compared to the number of responses in this study to get a more thorough 
and general, wide perspective and indication on weights.  
7.3 MCDM EVALUATION  
It is important that the decision makers consider the criteria they include in a MCDM evaluation carefully. In 
this study the selection of criteria was based on inputs and results from previous studies and thus not chosen 
for general usage. Indoor climate quality is expressed through many different parameters. In this study, for 
instance CO2 concentration was expressed through mean values of operational hours and number of hours 
above certain limits. Another possibility could be expressing it through number of hours in certain intervals. 
Besides, some indoor climate parameters, for instance light, was in this study expressed as a positive attribute, 
thus the higher the illuminance, the better the quality. However, some considerations on boundary conditions 
should be made, as for instance extreme values might change the opinion of that criterion, for instance a too 
high level of illuminance can cause glare. The MCDM evaluation does not analyze and evaluate the inputs, 
instead it carries out an objective calculation based on the inputs and definitions it is given. The MCDM 
evaluation thus expresses the quantified data and definitions for criteria and thus the output of the evaluation 
is only as good as the inputs given.  
In reality, conditions of one criterion might affect the perception of another. For instance, the perception on 
air quality is often affected by the temperature. In addition, perception and opinion on combinatory effects 
might be felt, but they are not included in the overall MCDM evaluation. Combinatorial effects for different 
criteria are not included in the MCDM evaluations, as all criteria are evaluated independently. Thus, the MCDM 
evaluation does not represent the perception of the overall soft values, instead it evaluated the conditions of 
each criterion independently and summarizes the results. Further, knowledge of combinatory effects is limited 
for indoor environmental aspects and thus this limitation of the method does not affect the studies.  
Another limitation is the need for input data for all alternatives for all criteria. For the ventilation case study 
some inputs were not included in the original study and in order to make the MCDM evaluation some 
assumptions had to be made. The assumptions were based and compared with the other retrofit alternatives, 
thus not actual values from measurements or simulations.

Page 91 | Conclusion 
8 CONCLUSION 
The MCDM approach provided a method for evaluating and comparing a large number of alternatives, 
supporting and improving decision-making for different renovation alternatives.  
In this study, classrooms were evaluated, through many different criteria, by using different characteristics of 
the classrooms. Through the comparison the worst classrooms were identified and arguments on which 
initiatives are needed for renovation was provided. The evaluation is useful for a municipality as they can 
prioritize renovation initiatives among different schools through the comparison.  
The MCDM approach also compared different renovation initiatives on small and larger scales. On a small scale 
retrofit ventilation alternatives were compared considering relevant criteria of cost and indoor environmental 
qualities. On a larger scale the MCDM method was used to identify a preferable renovation alternative for an 
entire school. Two methods of different levels of detail were used and compared. A simple evaluation seemed 
ideal for the very early stages when quantified data for different solutions is limited, whereas the detailed 
evaluation is ideal later in the process when more information is known. The output of an MCDM evaluation 
express the input and thus the more detailed the input the more precise the results. The evaluation can further 
highlight aspects supporting a renovation proposal and oppositely highlight disadvantages for a renovation 
proposal. The distribution result on contributions can be useful in the design stages of development of 
proposals as the proposals can be modified and improved on the identified disadvantages. The earlier in the 
process the bad solutions are identified and dropped, the better the potential for the final solution. 
Weights of different criteria are important. For existing primary schools, the condition of the building was 
assigned high priority. This criterion cannot be neglected for renovation situations and was selected as one of 
the main criteria to consider along with general main criteria of economy, environment and social aspects. In 
addition, social aspects were divided into wellbeing, functionality and cultural qualities, as especially wellbeing 
and functionality were considered of high priority for primary schools. Thus, explicit weight of each of these 
main criteria were identified. Wellbeing, functionality and technical condition were assigned highest weight 
among the main criteria. These weightings vary greatly from weightings for a general DGNB certification, 
indicating that aspects to consider for renovating a primary school is different from aspects for sustainably 
certifying buildings in general.  
A more thorough study on weightings should be carried out to find a set of general weightings for school 
renovations. The questionnaires in this study should be modified by elaborating explanations for criteria 
definitions to minimize misunderstandings. Further, criteria comparisons should be divided between different 
groups of criteria (main, sub, and detail).   
Through the case studies the MCDM approach supported and improved decision-making of different 
alternatives in an effective and systematic manner. The method was further considered useful for project 
groups to increase communication through its general transparency. The MCDM approach has potential for 
making overall, objective evaluations for comparison of classrooms and renovation alternatives for buildings 
including primary schools while including indoor environmental parameters together with other relevant 
criteria.

forventes at værktøjet kan bidrage og opfordre til kommunikationen mellem de involverede parter ligeså 
vel som at kunne holde status og overblik over de mange inputs/fordele/ulemper der vil være ved

ABOUT THE PROJECT: 
“Danish Primary Schools – A study in multi-criteria decision support within renovation with focus on indoor 
climate” 
The project investigates the use of a multi-criteria tool in the early stages of a renovation project concerning 
Danish primary schools. The tool utilizes a mathematical approach, where a number of renovation proposals 
are evaluated according to a set of criteria (i.e. functionality, economy, indoor climate, sustainability). These 
criteria will in most cases weight differently. As an example, functionality might be considered more 
important in schools compared to sustainability, but then again it could be different. In order to get an idea 
of the weightings on priorities, you are asked to note your opinions in this questionnaire. The tool is 
considered to be used in the decision processes within the project group. In addition, the tool is expected to 
increase communication between the people involved, as well as keeping an overview on the arguments 
between the different renovation proposals.
