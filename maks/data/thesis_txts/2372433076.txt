Abstract 
This thesis serves the objective of evaluating the potential for early design phase Computational Fluid 
Dynamics (CFD) simulation. A tool called Butterfly was introduced, which is connected to the 3D 
modelling tool Rhinoceros, in conjunction with the parametric scripting tool Grasshopper. To obtain 
this objective, first a validation study was carried out for the most widely used turbulence models in 
the world of CFD. The validation study consisted of two parts: First an isothermal test case, and 
second, a case with heat transfer from a human body. This was done to validate whether the CFD 
code of Butterfly could be trusted. The validation study shows promising results, which are in line 
with both experimental data, and data obtained from other CFD codes. Secondly, Butterfly was 
utilized for an application study, where the main objective was to make a parametric framework that 
could automatically calculate boundary conditions. The application study was carried out with a case 
(Villa GUG) provided by BIG (Bjarke Ingels Group). A parametric model was implemented 
successfully, and made it possible for 27 different CFD simulations to run independently. This 
process amounted to a total of 102 hours. The purpose of the application study was to inform a design 
team on what strategies could be used to obtain a satisfactory thermal environment for the worst-case 
scenario with the highest heat gain of the year.  
 
The biggest problem surrounding CFD as an early design phase tool is time consumption. One of the 
most important factors in a parametric design tool is that it can provide visually impactful results at 
a fast rate. However, with the future of cloud computing, and overall increase in processing power, 
this problem seems limited. Secondly, the main problem with the early design phase is that engineers 
are not always a common part of it. However, the number of engineers in the design teams are 
increasing rapidly, and in few years, the culture of all-architect design teams will be eliminated.   
 
The potential for CFD in the early design phase is high. It is the belief of the author of this report that 
CFD can already be applied to early design phase projects, and that within 3-5 years it will be used 
as common practice in an early design phase.  
 
For future work, efforts should be made to validate all parts of Butterfly. Furthermore, tools should 
be implemented to perform automatic mesh independency studies and residual controls. Overall, the 
majority of manual processes of classic CFD should be automated and computer-generated to 
accommodate an early design phase CFD tool.

1. Introduction 
Thermal comfort in buildings has seen an increase of interest by the building industry over the past 
decades. This interest manifests back to the 1970’s, where P.O Fanger constructed a climate chamber 
on the campus of DTU (Technical University of Denmark) to study the effects of thermal environment 
on human test subjects (Andersen & Gyntelberg 2011). As indoor environment is well associated 
with the energy performance of buildings (Hovorka et al. 2012), it should be optimized with the 
energy performance as a constraining variable. Thus, it should provide a satisfactory indoor 
environment for the occupants while being energy efficient. It is a known fact that building 
optimizations made in the early design phase will determine its overall environmental impact 
(Basbagill et al. 2013), namely the energy consumption. Therefore, BPS (building performance 
simulation) tools should be applied to the early design of buildings. In doing so it can be quantified 
whether a given design decision will impact the building’s energy consumption and indoor 
environment as expected. Such an initiative already exists in the form of computational design. 
1.1. 
Computational design 
Computational design implies the use of CAD (Computer-Aided Design) to create a building design 
that satisfies within a solution space determined by a set of constraining parameters (Schumacher 
2009). This enables the designer to reinforce a given solution with scientific proof that the building 
functions as originally intended. While CAD merely describes the aid that computers can add to the 
design process, a vast amount of different efforts have been made to incorporate it into a specific tool. 
All of which operates within different domains, such as Life-Cycle Analysis (LCA), Energy 
modelling, Structural stability, etc. The common denominator for computational design and the 
attraction for most building designers is the possibility to make design variations in an early design 
stage and observe what makes the design more robust.  
 
The use of computational design tools in indoor environment is far from new. In fact, it is so well 
established that in some countries it is required by regulation to prove that the building in question 
will live up to certain rules. The Danish building regulation for domestic buildings requires for the 
room with the highest heat gain within the building that the temperatures does not exceed 27°C for 
more than 100 hours of the year. This should be proven with an energy simulation of the building 
(Dansk Standard 1995). As described by Sun & Wang in (2010), the comfort of the occupants in the 
indoor environment can be evaluated with many different already existing tools. However, they all 
have the same assumption that the air in the room is fully mixed, thus providing no temperature 
gradient in the room. In ventilation design not all airflows are fully mixed specially during the past 
decade where more advanced HVAC room systems have been introduced, for instance the 
displacement ventilation system. The efficiency of such ventilation system is highly dependent on 
the stratification created by natural buoyancy forces. A model with the assumption of fully mixed air 
would fail to predict the temperature gradient of the room, thus jeopardizing the thermal 
comfortability of the occupants (Griffith & Chen 2003). 
 
To predict the temperature or velocity distribution in a room, more advanced tools must be applied. 
In the article CFD and ventilation research by Li & Nielsen in (2011), the application of CFD

(Computational Fluid Dynamics) in ventilation design was discuessed. Amongst many possibilities, 
they clarify the significance of using CFD to predict the velocity profiles and temperature distribution. 
Moreover, they state that “In CFD, ventilation parameters such as room/building geometry, supply 
and exhaust openings, and physical variables can be flexibly changed. CFD would allow scientists 
to test scenarios that were not possible using experimental methods...”  
1.2. 
 Computational Fluid Dynamics 
CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics) is a modern method devoted to solving complex fluid 
mechanical problems by utilizing computational power. CFD has been well established for many 
decades and has been applied to many fields such as mechanical engineering, civil engineering, 
chemical engineering, astrophysics, and many more. Part of this popularity and increased use is, as 
stated by Li & Nielsen (2011), due to computer processing power experiencing almost an exponential 
climb. They even suggested that in 10 years from 2011 ventilation systems will be verified on a city 
scale, rather than on a room scale.  
To understand why computational power is essential to CFD, one must understand the core principles 
behind it. CFD consist of solving the fundamental flow equations, involving continuity, three 
momentum equations (x-, y-, and z direction), and the energy equation (Nielsen 2007). However, 
solving these equations can introduce an infinite number of unknowns. For instance, the velocity will 
fluctuate in all directions, resulting in infinite degrees of freedom. This makes it impossible to solve 
the Navier-Stokes equations, since they are all coupled (dependent) on each other, like pressure-
velocity and temperature-velocity. Therefore, for flows of non-laminar nature, a Reynolds 
decomposition should be applied. This is called the RANS (Reynolds Averaged Navier Stokes) 
model. As the fluctuating nature of a three-dimensional flow has been described with the Reynolds 
decomposition, we have introduced 9 additional unknowns (6 Reynolds stresses and 3 turbulent 
fluxes). To model these, we introduce turbulence modelling (Saad 2011). For indoor environment 
simulations this is often modelled with the use of turbulent viscosity, solved by the two-equation k-ε 
turbulence model, k being the turbulent kinetic energy and ε being the dissipation (Nielsen 2007). 
Another well-known approach to solve the fluctuations within the fluid is to use LES (Large Eddy 
Simulations). LES does not time average the results, and therefore it must be a transient solution, thus 
causing a much higher computational time cost. Due to the increased time-cost, LES will not be used 
in this project, even though it has proven to be superior in capturing advanced flow characteristics, 
that the RANS model was incapable of.  
 
CFD introduces a concept that no longer ignores the flow characteristics of a room, and enables the 
user to predict the differences of air temperature and velocity with high precision. However, as 
discussed earlier, assumptions of the nature behind the equations are essential. Hence, it should be 
underlined that CFD is not necessarily a “correct” solution. As Li & Nielsen (2011) described it, CFD 
does not represent a substitute for experiments and theoretical knowledge in the ventilation field, but 
rather creates a platform for researchers to run simulations and to verify design options before testing 
them. As seen in Figure 1, CFD is normally used to work around few parameters in the numerical 
domain. Changing parameters is a tedious process, and often changes all the boundary conditions.

This means that these boundary conditions will have to be recalculated and revaluated by the engineer 
every time one parameter is changed.

For a project that is aimed at investigating hundreds or even thousands of possibilities, this process 
would be time consuming for a CFD engineer to go through. Therefore, a methodology more 
susceptible to design changes, should be investigated.  
 
Attempts within the field of CFD have been carried out in the aerospace industry. Imlay (2012) 
defined one of the main problems with using a parametric approach is that the requirements of the 
system, component, or process must be clearly stated. These are the terms that the computer will take 
into consideration when creating an optimal solution. If no requirements are given to the computer 
(i.e. infinite degrees of freedom) it will never converge towards a solution. This can be carried over 
to the civil engineering industry as well. A building design takes shape based on the impending 
constraints of a given site. Involving parametric CFD in building design could enable the use of CFD 
in the early design phase of the building, since time spent making design changes would be 
significantly less. Thereby, the design team would be able to predict airflows of rooms, a result that 
will be of high informative value about an overall optimized design of the building. However, since 
users of CFD should persist a background knowledge of the underlying concept, a framework should 
be developed for the architect or engineer to utilize when needed. Such a framework should specify 
the constraints of parametric CFD. Furthermore, a sensitivity analysis should be performed to discuss 
how much the individual variables will change the overall validity of the given CFD solver.  
 
Butterfly is a new open source tool that is under development. Butterfly runs with R/GH 
(Rhinoceros/Grasshopper) as interface, with a connection to the Linux based open source CFD tool 
called OpenFOAM. R/GH is a famous computational design tool for architects and engineers with 
many different uses. The main feature of R/GH is the possibility to parametrically try out various 
design propositions, and inform the user why one design is better than the other. Furthermore, 
Grasshopper can “pipeline” information, which means that changes to a domain will inform the 
upstream components on the updated domain. This is an incredibly powerful tool to make parametric 
design changes. With this new connection to OpenFOAM, as seen in Figure 2, incentive is given for 
the design team to try out the power of CFD in their early design phase. As stated earlier, there are 
many fall pits in the world of CFD, and therefore the risks of using such tool without the underlying

2. Room air conditioning 
2.1. 
Heat transfer fundamentals 
For air conditioning in buildings, two kinds of heat are relevant: 
 
• 
Sensible heat 
• 
Latent heat 
 
Sensible heating is described as the process that causes the temperature of an object to rise, whereas 
the latent heat is the heat removed in order for an object to change phase, like water changing from 
liquid to vapor (Daikin 2017a). It is common in indoor climate to account only for the sensible 
heating, since it is the cause of temperature changes in a room, and thus a change in flow.    
 
Following this explanation of sensible and latent heat, it should be stated that conduction, convection, 
and radiation can be deployed as both sensible and latent heat.   
2.1.1. 
Conduction 
Conduction is heat transfer caused by direct molecular collision. It happens between two different 
solids. Higher velocity particles collides with slower velocity particles, and the kinetic energy of the 
slower particles increases, and as a result, heat transfer happens. An example of conduction in the 
indoor environment could be the feet of an occupant pressed against the floor. The closer the 
molecules get, the higher the heat transfer coefficient will be. This is also the reason why two different 
floor materials will feel colder or hotter at the same temperature.   
2.1.2. 
Convection 
When a fluid (air, etc.) is heated, its density becomes lower and the buoyancy forces make it ascent. 
A classic example is a space heater in a room. When the air gets heated, it rises to the ceiling, and 
thus forces the colder air to fall. The colder air then reaches the space heater and gets heated, which 
is a classic example of a convection current. 
Another example in a room, as seen in Figure 5, is when the solar heat gain is high through a window. 
This causes the internal surface of the window to be heated. The window now has a higher 
temperature than the rest of room air. The air passing by the window gets heated, and therefore rise 
to the ceiling.

Convection also happens for occupants in a room. The air around the occupant heats the space and 
therefore a “heat plume” rises above their head. 
2.1.3. 
Radiation 
Thermal radiation is the result of the emission of electromagnetic waves. An object possesses a 
temperature, and from that it emits waves that carry away thermal energy. Like previous heat 
deployment methods, the amount of radiative heat transfer is based on the temperature difference of 
two different objects.  
An example of radiation is when iron is being cast by the blacksmith – the temperature of the iron is 
so high that it can be felt when holding a hand near it – and if the temperature is high enough it will 
even “glow” with heat.  
For the indoor environment, radiation is felt from the surrounding surfaces of the walls. The 
temperature is felt based on the view factor that the occupant has to that surface. 
2.2. 
Thermal comfort 
In ASHRAE Standard 55-2010, six factors that affect the human thermal comfort are listed: air 
temperature, mean radiant temperature, humidity, air velocity, clothing, and metabolism. The two last 
are occupant dependent factors. Each category is explained separately here.  
2.2.1. 
Mean Radiant Temperature (MRT) 
The mean radiant temperature is described as the radiant heat exchange that occupant experiences 
with the surrounding enclosure. Radiation can be hard to design according to, since most air 
conditioning systems are based on air movement, and thus the radiant temperature can be quite high. 
MRT can be easier to account for with the use of radiant systems, such as chilled floors or ceilings.  
 
The mean radiant temperature can be calculated by using a view factor calculation, see Danish 
Standard 7726 Annex C for further information (Standards 2001).   
2.2.2. 
Air temperature 
The air temperature is defined as the actual temperature of the air in a room. The air temperature is 
often the factor which is being controlled by the ventilation systems, ignoring the radiant properties 
of the room.

2.2.4. 
Personal factors 
The personal factors include the factors that the occupants control themselves. One is the clothing 
factor, the other the metabolic rate. The clothing rate specifies how much clothes the occupants are 
wearing, and the metabolic rate how much activity they are performing. For further elaboration, see 
Danish standard 7730 (Dansk Standard 2006) 
2.2.5. 
Comfort indices 
Comfort indices are used to classify the quality of a thermal environment. This section of the thesis 
will be short and concise, since this information is widely available in the Danish Standards.

3.1. 
 Mathematical background 
3.1.1. 
Schemes 
The selection of numerical scheme can greatly influence the solution. For this thesis, the purpose was 
not to meticulously describe each scheme in detail, but rather to make a point of when to select one 
scheme over another. The discussed schemes involve: 
 
• 
1st order upwind 
• 
2nd order upwind 
• 
QUICK 
 
1st order upwind: The 1st order upwind scheme, as seen in Figure 10A, assumes a cell surface value 
w based on the upstream point W. With large differences between each grid point, this can cause 
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
major numerical diffusion, as it was previously explained (Nielsen 2007). While being the less 
accurate numerical scheme, it can offer faster grid convergence, thus a more stable solution.  
2nd order upwind: The 2nd order upwind scheme shares the methodology of the 1st order upwind 
scheme. However, instead of only using the upstream point W to estimate the cell surface value w, it 
uses both WW and W to extrapolate the cell surface value w, as seen in Figure 10B. The greater 
accuracy will come at the cost of the overall stability and computational time. 
QUICK: The QUICK scheme uses the values of the 2nd order upwind scheme to create a polynomial 
fit through the points to estimate the cell surface value w, as seen in Figure 10C. It can be considered 
as the most accurate of the numerical schemes, but again it takes more computational power to solve.  
 
 
Figure 10 - A) 1st order upwind B) 2nd order upwind C) QUICK. Reprinted from Nielsen (2007)  
Overall, it is recommended to use a numerical scheme of 2nd order if it is available and if grid 
convergence can be obtained (Naulin & Nielsen 2003; Nielsen 2007). If grid convergence is 
unobtainable, the solution can be initialized with the use of the 1st order upwind scheme, and be 
continued with the use of the 2nd order upwind scheme.  
3.1.2. 
Turbulence modelling 
A brief overview of turbulence modelling was offered in the introduction of the thesis. This section 
will delve deeper into the consequence of choosing one turbulence models over another, and what 
effect it has on the overall solution. Furthermore, the most commonly used turbulence models for 
indoor environment will be introduced.  
RANS 
In general, two characteristics of a flow can be present – laminar or turbulent. However, for 
ventilation design, most flows depend on mixing and therefore they seldom present a laminar flow 
case. Therefore, this thesis will only discuss the models to handle turbulent flows. The turbulent flow 
is characterized as a flow with turbulent fluctuations, that can create additional shear stress of the air 
(fluid). The turbulent shear stress can be solved with the use of a turbulence model. In the following 
sections, the turbulent viscosity models of k-ε, k-ω, and the SST model, will be elaborated further.

3.4.1. 
Quality control 
As a part of CFD, it is important to be able to validate the numerical solution given by the CFD tool 
in question. The categories can be divided into: 
 
• 
Convergence 
Discusses whether a solution has reached convergence to a steady-state solution. This can be done by 
monitoring the key variable residuals, or by monitoring the average temperature or velocity of the 
domain throughout the iterations. 
• 
Grid independence 
==PAGE:31==
30 
 
The solution may be dependent on the given grid. If the solution changes when the grid is refined 
further, then it is grid dependent, and therefore not acceptable. When a grid is refined, the overall 
result of the solution should not change drastically.  
• 
Heat/mass balance 
The numerical domain is subject to a mass and heat flow balance. If more air is added to the domain, 
than removed, the mass balance is not achieved. This can happen for heat as well.

4.3. 
 Summary 
From Validation study 1 and 2, it can be concluded that the results compare well to the benchmark 
studies, including both experimental data and CFD simulations.  
 
Validation study 1 consisted of an experimental wind tunnel study carried out by Nielsen in (1991), 
that laid the basis of the CFD benchmark study carried out by Olmedo & Vilhelm in (2010). The data 
obtained from this validation study of Butterfly in broad terms, confirm that the results obtained 
thereof, can be trusted with similar settings as specified in the benchmark study. However, it should 
be mentioned that this validation study did show some inconsistencies in predicting flows with high 
vorticity i.e. complex flow structures. This was explicitly evident in the near zone of the outlet, as 
seen in Figure 27. Figure 24 shows that the turbulence model predicts the turbulent kinetic energy 
well compared with the benchmark test. Therefore, it can be concluded that the standard k-epsilon 
model works well for isothermal flows, and predicts the nature of the flow well. Though the overall 
flow is predicted well, for complex flow structures with high vorticity, the a k-epsilon model with 
wall functions (RNG, Realizable, low Re, etc.) should be applied. Furthermore, a finer grid should 
be used for zones that induce complex vorticity.  
 
Validation study 2 consisted of an experimental study carried out by Nielsen et al. in (2003). The 
study aimed to give a benchmark study for future CFD prediction to be compared with it. The 
validation study applied the RNG k-epsilon model, since it works well with indoor airflows. Both the 
velocity and temperature profiles of the rooms were predicted with minor discrepancies to the 
experimental study, as seen in Figure 31 and Figure 32. The data was also compared to a similar CFD 
validation study, which showed similar discrepancies in temperature and velocity, as compared to the 
experimental study. Therefore, it was concluded that the discrepancies observed were caused by the 
data inconsistencies of the experimental study’s boundary conditions.  
Following the validation of the code, a grid precision study was carried out. Here 4 different grid 
resolutions were calculated and compared to the original validation study results. This was done to 
confirm grid independency, and to investigate to what extend the grid resolution could be decreased 
to, without compromising the results of the CFD model, thus spending less time on simulating the 
model. Using a grid with lower resolution overall increases the uncertainty for the prediction of the 
draught rating. This was calculated as a difference of 1.5% for draught rating. Since this is a minor 
difference, it will not be discussed further in this project.  
 
Overall, it can be concluded that the RNG k-epsilon turbulence model works well for free convection 
flows. However, the uncertainty is increased when close to a wall region. Therefore, measurements 
of any nature (velocity, temperature, etc.) should not be carried out close to solid walls. If this is 
needed, a SST k-ω model should be used, with a y+ ≈ 1, meaning a much denser mesh in the region. 
Since early design phase CFD seems to focus more around the general situation of the room, this 
would normally not be necessary. Figure 36 showed that the results for G2 were almost the same as 
the original validation test case. Therefore, it can be concluded that the mesh cell size of 
0.05×0.05×0.05 m for this case was appropriate to predict a precise airflow in the free zone, as seen 
==PAGE:47==
46 
 
in Figure 38. However, it should be mentioned that this may vary with the difference in geometry in 
the specific case.

5. Application study – Villa GUG 
In the previous validation studies Butterfly has been validated for the use on indoor climate CFD 
simulations. In this case study, a framework for early design phase CFD is presented. Following the 
presentation of this framework, it is tested directly on the case study of Villa GUG. Figure 39 shows 
the proposed framework. Starting a project in this phase is caused by the design team of a building to 
raise a question to the current design of an indoor space – for example: “Would a glass façade of this 
size create a problem for the indoor environment?”. Then a benchmark CFD simulation is carried 
out for that case, and it can be identified whether the design will impact the indoor environment 
negatively. If yes, then the problem is followed up – “Why is the indoor environment being impacted 
negatively by the glass façade?”. This will lead to some theories of why the problem persists, but 
more importantly it can be discussed what might solve the problem. Normally, possible solutions are 
mostly decided upon empirical knowledge of the design team, rather than scientific data. In this case, 
every new design proposition is laid out as a different strategy for how to solve the bad indoor 
environment. In our working example, 3 different strategies are shown. Then, following the 
simulation of the strategies, they are compared in terms of Danish Standard 7730 for thermal comfort. 
This might lead to a “best” solution, but will in general give the design team an idea of how to solve 
their problem. It should be emphasized that these simulations are aimed on fast and responsive results, 
not on precision. Therefore, the strategy should be reevaluated in the detailed design phase.  
 
 
Figure 39 - Framework for Strategic early design phase CFD 
==PAGE:49==
48 
 
As discussed in Section 3.3, it is problematic to simulate different cases since the boundary conditions 
are constantly changing and are dependent on each other. Furthermore, by standard methods they 
would have to be determined manually every time a new design proposal is suggested. In this case 
study, a “pipeline” method will be implemented, using Rhino/Grasshopper in connection with 
Butterfly, to automatically update the upstream components of the CFD model.

5.4. 
Solution strategies 
Strategy 1: The strategy was focused on the passive mean of decreasing the solar heat gain coefficient 
of the window, thus decreasing the high solar heat gain in the room, as seen in Figure 50. This lead 
to lower surface temperatures, but it should also be noted that the lower the SHGC becomes, the less 
clear the window is, and thus the view thereof will be compromised. The strategy is aimed to vary 
the SHGC from 0.5, as in the benchmark study, to 0.1. Each step uses the current SHGC – 0.05, and 
there are 9 steps (SHGC 0.5, SHGC 0.45, SHGC 0.4, …., SHGC 0.1).  
 
 
 
Figure 50 - Strategy 1, decreasing the solar heat gain coefficient 
Strategy 2: The strategy is focused on removing as much of the sensible heat gain from the solar 
radiation directly on the floor as possible (Figure 51). Though this is a smart to remove the solar heat 
gain from the direct solar radiation, it should be considered that the floor temperature cannot be 
decreased too much since it can cause discomfort for the occupants. Furthermore, in cases with little 
direct solar radiation, this system will have a maximum capacity of 40 W/m2, as it is purely radiative 
for the surrounding walls. In cases with direct solar heat gain, the maximum cooling capacity can 
exceed 100 W/m2. The system works very well with the MRT of the occupants, since the occupants 
will have a higher view factor to the floor. The surface temperature of the floor is the variable in this 
strategy, varying from 25˚C – 21˚C (Floor temp. 25˚C, 24.5˚C, 24˚C, …, 21˚C, in total 9 steps). The 
SHGC was kept at 0.5 for all the simulations in this strategy.   
 
 
Figure 51 – Strategy 2, floor cooling strategy for Villa GUG 
Strategy 3: The strategy is focused on cooling the ceiling so that the air rising from the heated window 
is cooled as soon as it reaches the ceiling (Figure 52). This strategy deals better with the mixed air in 
the room, and the maximum cooling capacity is 100 W/m2. This was thought to lower the air 
==PAGE:55==
54 
 
temperature better than the floor cooling strategy, but the floor temperature is relatively high because 
of the direct solar radiation on the slab. Therefore, this might not be just as good at dealing with the 
MRT of the room, thus the radiant environment might prove to give a radiant asymmetry discomfort 
for the occupant. Also, the view factor to the ceiling was much lower for the occupant, and hence the 
chilled ceiling has a smaller impact on the MRT than the floor cooling system. The ceiling 
temperature will be varied from 21˚C – 17˚C (Ceiling temp. 21˚C, 20.5˚C, …, 17˚C, a total of 9 steps). 
The chilled ceiling is implemented as a passive chilled ceiling.

floor. Since Strategy 2 also was thought to work better for a winter situation, where a large amount 
of heating is needed on the floor, it was selected as the superior solution of the 2 active cooling 
strategies (Strategy 2 and 3).

worked well for decreasing the total draught rating in the room, therefore they were combined.

6. Discussion 
When performing CFD simulations, the precision of the results should be discussed in detail. For this 
report, it was carried out independently for the validation studies and application studies in their 
respective sections.  
 
This section aims at discussing the potential for early design phase CFD. This statement should be 
emphasized greatly – the thesis is about an early design phase, not a detailed design phase. The 
purpose of the early design phase is to give the building design team a qualified guess of how to 
design a building, and give a better starting point for the detailed design.  
6.1. 
Implementation to an early design project 
An early design phase demands that the objectives can be changed dynamically as the project is 
analysed further in detail. During this thesis, the objectives of the validation study and application 
study were clear.  
 
In the application study, 3 strategies were proposed in the original case. These 3 studies were set up

6.2. 
Validation tools for CFD 
One of the biggest problems encountered when applying CFD to an early design phase was the 
validation of the model. In other terms, it is a time consuming process to simulate a new case with 
higher grid resolution to validate that the current case is grid independent. For instance, in Validation 
study 2, it took 3 hours of simulation time to obtain a model with an independent grid, and to be able 
to validate it with the current result. This proves problematic since it is the job of the engineer to do 
it manually, and then to estimate whether the solution is independent. If this was applied to 
Application study 1, this would increase the simulation time with 27 hours, an increase of 25% to the 
calculation time. Every time the engineer has to spend valuable and expensive hours on such 
validation, as seen in Figure 81, the value of the program descends. Furthermore, it would mean that 
an architect would have to consult with an engineer between every single variable change carried out.  
 
 
Figure 81 - Engineer required between each change of variable, spending a total of 9 hours on validation 
However, as discussed earlier, the computational power is available, and will only improve in the 
future. Therefore, an automatic tool for mesh validation should be applied. Not only would it optimize 
the workflow of the parametric model, but it would also remove human error, as discussed in previous 
study (Hensen 2004). New ways to evaluate the “correctness” of a model in a more qualitative way 
are also quoted to be in the demand.

6.3. 
Design team composition 
Butterfly has potential for usage in a design team. However, the professional background of the 
design team members should be taken into account for this statement to be true. 
 
For many years, the design team composition has been under development and revision. In the past, 
architects would usually propose a design, and then engineers would work for that design to be 
realized, making the decisions based purely on aesthetics, and not based on performance of structural 
stability and/or indoor climate. This out-of-date design team composition made it impossible for early 
design phase CFD in the past.  
 
However, as design teams have been revised over the years, an engineer often constitutes a crucial 
part the team, consulting on the early design project. With an increasing interest in making this sort 
of multidisciplinary design team, the potential for early design phase CFD increases even more.

7. Conclusion 
It can be concluded that the potential for early design phase CFD is big. This is manifested in two 
major changes in the building industry. 1: Engineers are becoming a common part of the early design 
phase, and this development will continue throughout the years, and 2: The computational power is 
increasing and the future of cloud computing is becoming a reality. With continued development on 
Butterfly, and with the implementation of modules that eases the workflow, Butterfly has the potential 
to make CFD a common part of the early design phase in the civil engineering industry.  
 
In terms of the user for the early design phase CFD tool Butterfly it can be concluded that with 
restricted access, it can be used by architects to change already implemented variables in the system, 
even at the current state of the program. However, as soon as the system needs new modules 
implemented, a skilled CFD/HVAC engineer is needed to validate the methodology.  
 
The Butterfly code that connects with the CFD tool OpenFOAM works as expected within the 
confines of this project. However, it should be emphasized that Butterfly is still only a beta-released 
product, and will require much more meticulous testing in order to validate the entirety of the code. 
For future studies, the different implementations of turbulence models and solvers should be 
continuously validated to secure the precision of the tool.

9.1. 
 Appendix A – Diffuser selection 
To find a diffuser that would fit the throw of the Case Study, Villa GUG, some hand calculation was 
needed. The diffuser needed for the inlets was to be a standard diffuser mounted on the wall, with a 
grille. First, we need to calculate the heat we need to remove for the combined solution.  
 
A solution with a 24˚C floor surface was chosen, with a SHGC of 0.4. This amounted to a total heat 
gain 13517 W through the window, with a cooling capacity of 59.5 W/m2 for the floor cooling. 
Therefore, the flow rate was calculated as 0.491 m3/s. With 5 diffusers, this will be ≈98 l/s pr. 
Diffuser. 
 
The throw length of the diffusers should be approximately 8 m, since that is the distance to the 
window from the diffuser. Using the Lindab catalogue for AC Grilles, a grille with the size 600 mm 
× 100 mm was chosen. The overall throw length for this grille is 5.9 m. However, when the grille 
placed with 300 mm of the ceiling, the Coanda effect happens. From the LINDAB theory section 
(Lindab 2011), it can be seen that a correction factor of 1.4 should be multiplied with the throw length 
of the catalogue, when the Coanda effect is present. Therefore 5.9 × 1.4 = 8.26 m. This is calculated 
for an isothermal jet. Therefore, if the supply air is colder than the room temperature, the forces of 
buoyancy will overcome the momentum of the initial jet earlier. The Lindab catalogue states that the 
throw should be reduced by 1.5% per degree of the temperature. In our room, the air temperature is 
≈26˚C and the supply air is 20˚C. Therefore, this amounts to a total reduction factor of 1.5% × 6˚C = 
9%. This corrects the throw length from 8.26 m to 8.26 × 0.91 = 7.5 m. The overall effective area for 
the 600 × 100 mm grille, was 0.02 m2. This was assumed as a size of 400 mm × 50 mm.  
 
Finally, a classic problem in CFD, when specifying inlet boundaries, is that the jet doesn’t spread as 
much as it should. Figure 84 shows that Inlet 1, which is specified as a single velocity vector, shows 
incorrect jet characteristics. Therefore, Inlet 2 is created in the CFD model. The inlet is divided into 
5 sub surfaces, each with a different vector, that has the same magnitude. The actual difference in 
angle might differ from case to case, but in this project, it was specified as -20˚, -10˚, 0˚, 10˚, 20˚. 
This way, the jet characteristics are maintained, and imitated, as in a real case.

Appendix B – Direct solar load analysis 
In the application study, the solar heat load was simplified as a load being distributed on the entire 
floor surface. However, in terms of physical reality, this is not how it looks. The solar load enters the 
room as a ray, that hit only a part of the floor, as seen in Figure 85. 
 
 
Figure 85 - Solar distribution for, left: assumed in validation study, right: physical reality of solar rays 
Overall, the heat balance of the room doesn’t change, due to this fact – the total heat entering and 
exiting the room, remains the same. In terms of radiation, it will change the MRT in room for an 
occupant, based on where the occupant is in the room. Also, it should be mentioned, that if the 
occupant is being hit by the direct solar radiation, the MRT will be much different – but that could 
happen, since there is no solar shading in the room.  
 
This asymmetry could affect the temperature of the floor, when the floor cooling has been installed, 
as seen in Figure 86.
