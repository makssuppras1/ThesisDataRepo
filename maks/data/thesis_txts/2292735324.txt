Abstract
The attenuator style wave energy converter concept KNswing has
been subjected to model testing and numerical analysis. The con-
cept features 40 OWC chambers on a long ’¼éoating barge. The nu-
merical analysis has been done using WAMIT with the extension
of generalized modes to model pressure in the OWC chambers
and structural de’¼éection. The performance and mooring forces
of the WEC have been measured in model test of regular and
irregular sea states. Along with operating conditions, two dif-
ferent mooring concepts have been tested in survival conditions.
The conclusion to the model test and numerical analysis is that
the concept will survive the largest of expected waves and that
a reinforced concrete structure will su’¼āce. The performance is
again established to be good. The concept still needs detailed
design, but has been further proofed.

Chapter 1
Introduction
With an increasing desire to move away from fossil fuels in energy production, the
hunt for renewable energy sources intensi’¼ües. With this intensi’¼üed search, new al-
ternative energy sources become interesting.
The ’¼ürst serious move towards renewable energy began in the seventies with the
oil crisis. Among other ideas, this gave birth to the danish wind energy industry, but
a lot of other, less successful, alternative energy concepts were also conceived during
this crisis. Among these, the ’¼ürst ideas for harvesting energy from ocean waves, an
abundant and readily available resource, saw the light of day.
In recent years, the wish for renewable energy has grown even more dire with the
realization of man made global warming and geo-politcal considerations. This has
driven the development of renewable energy technology to new areas of interest and
rekindled interest in some of these old concepts. This has meant that wave power
has received renewed attention. For many reasons, it seems that wave power may
’¼ünally be within reach of becoming commercially viable. Key factors include the
rising oil price, synergies with oŌåĄshore wind energy facilities established in recent
years, another is new synthetic material technology for mooring oŌåĄshore structures.
Lastly, wave energy is being re’¼üned by many new concepts in ongoing development.
1.1
Introduction to the KNswing
This work revolves around the Wave Energy Converter (WEC) concept KNswing.
The KNswing is an attenuator style WEC using Oscilating Water Column (OWC)
chambers to convert wave energy to electricity. The advantage of the KNswing is
that no vital mechanical parts will have to be submerged in sea water. By using
OWC chambers, wave power can be converted without a direct mechanical interface.

1.2
Content of this work
This master project revolves around the KNswing and seeks to bring it one step closer
to realization. This has resulted in ’¼üve, to some extend, separate topics, which all
aim at providing new insights to this device. First topic is the description of a new,
second series of model experiments. Second topic is the analysis of the data recorded
in these experiments. The third topic is an estimate of the bending moment, the
KNswing will experience from wave loads combined with OWC damping. This will
be done as an extension to the existing WAMIT model, with a generalized bending
mode. The aim is to provide con’¼üdence in the structural integrity of the device.
The fourth topic is a separate experiment, which was carried out to validate
the pressure sensors used in the previous model experiment, and in the new series
together with some initial single chamber experiments. The description of this ex-
periment and the background for it, is contained in Chapter 5.
The ’¼üfth topic is a theoretical exercise which seeks a time domain solution to
the existing frequency domain solution of the pressure mode radiation diŌåĄraction
problem. The complete solution to this problem turned out to be out of scope for
this project, but a simple calculation was possible, to try and bring the solution a
step closer. This eŌåĄort is covered in Section 4.3. The ’¼ürst three topics are the main
subjects, which entitle them to some more introduction.

1.3
Intro to the Portaferry experiments
As mentioned, a second round of model experiments were granted by the Marinet pro-
gram. These experiments were conducted in the QUB (QueenŌĆÖs University Belfast)
Portaferry wave tank [1]. The previous experiments, by Pors and Simonsen [13],
were carried out on a water depth of 1 m, which depending on the ’¼ünal size of WEC,
will correspond to deep water. The wave tank in Portaferry is only about half this
depth, 0.54 m, making it ideal to test more shallow water conditions.
The new experiments aim to both extend and validate the data already collected.
Aside from establishing the eŌåĄect of water depth by running similar experiments, as
the ones run previously in 1 m depth, the new experiments in Portaferry also covered
the investigation of a turret mooring setup for the KNswing. The currently tested
mooring system was a spread mooring system where the WEC is moored with three
lines attaching at diŌåĄerent point on the WEC. One aft, one left and one right. This
means that the WEC is kept in roughly the same position and that the heading is
kept constant. The new idea, with a turret mooring, is to allow the WEC to rotate.
this is done by mooring a central buoy to which the WEC attach with a single
line. By allowing the WEC to rotate, the mooring load should be lowered as the
risk of huge side ways waves is minimized. This new rotating situation then brings
new focus to the eŌåĄect of side ways waves, both on performance as well as mooring
loads. These will determine if it is bene’¼ücial to let the WEC rotate. It is estimated
that mooring will be a considerable expense in instating and maintaining a ’¼éoating
oŌåĄshore WEC [8]. As a consequence, the mooring requirement of the device is likely
to be a key parameter in the ’¼ünal cost per kW from a concept like the KNswing.
The cost of the mooring system generally scales with the mooring load.
1.4
Structural loading
Another point which becomes critical once an acceptable performance is achieved,
is the structural integrity of the device. This part of the design has not previously
been addressed for the KNswing.
The structural design of the KNswing poses new challenges, as the eŌåĄect of the
OWC chambers is new. Here the WAMIT model has a unique possibility to include
both the OWC damping and structural deformation in the same simulation. This
is done through the use of so called generalized modes.
The standard radiation
diŌåĄraction solution has already been extended with pressure modes by Bingham
et al. [3]. The addition of structural deformation modes will enable estimation of the
structural stresses under wave loading. These result will be compared with a simple

Chapter 2
Scope and Objective
The overall objective with this work is to bring renewable ocean wave energy closer
to realization. The contribution to this higher goal is sought through development
of the KNswing wave energy converter concept. This concept still has some way to
go before a full scale commercial implementation can be realized. The current state
of development has been described in the introduction, and this work takes oŌåĄhere.
The ’¼ürst and most clear objective of the second round of experiments was to
cover a gap in measurements of the performance around the resonance period for the
OWC chambers. This gap was noted by Ducasse [4] and Bingham et al. [3], and is
vital both because this is were performanec is supposed to be best and the actual
design period, but also because of a large peak in the numerical WAMIT results for
these frequencies. But this is not the whole motivation for more experiments.
The second objective of the experiments was to investigate the eŌåĄect of shallow
water. The water depth is suspected to aŌåĄect almost all aspects of the concepts. But
of interest to the new set of experiments is the eŌåĄect on mooring and performance.
The third objective is to investigate the use of a new mooring system, the turret
mooring system. The objective is to be able to compare this with the existing spread
mooring solution, but also to investigate how the spread mooring will behave in
shallow water.
Testing was also done with short crested waves. The eŌåĄect of this has not been
analyzed. Mainly because the important area of angled waves has not been touched
upon yet. The current WAMIT model is not able to handle this as the xŌłÆz symmetry
breaks down. Experimentally, the regular approach to angled waves also requires
extra pressure sensor on the leeward side. This investigation has been left for future
work.

To extract the wave amplitude and frequency from the wave probe reading, har-

Harmonic analysis
The idea of harmonic analysis is to do a ’¼üt of a sum of har-
monic functions to the signal in question. How many harmonics to sum is then a free
choice. Here 5 harmonics were summed. From nonlinear wave theory any frequency
present in the signal is either an instrumentation error, paddle error or a multiple of
the base harmonic frequency. With this reasoning, the signal can be reconstructed
from a sum of harmonics. The amplitude of the lowest harmonic can then be taken
as the amplitude. The amplitude of the higher harmonics can be interpreted as the
degree of nonlinearity of the wave.

Chapter 7
Summary and conclusions
In this chapter the conclusion will be presented, but ’¼ürst a short summary.
7.1
Summary
A scale model of the wave energy converter concept KNswing has been tested in the
shallow water wave tank at the QUB, Portaferry. The result obtained here has been
analyzed. The performance of the WEC in shallow water has been established, for
both irregular and regular waves. Two diŌåĄerent mooring solution have been tested
and compared. Further new numerical results for bending of the WEC structure have
been computed. These computations were made by extending the existing WAMIT
model with a bending mode.
7.2
Conclusion
From the report, it is clear that many results have been obtained through this work.
The results span from the expected absorbed power to a design criteria for the
concrete structure.
First of all the performance has again been proven to be satisfactory, and the
eŌåĄect of a shallower water depth has been established. This eŌåĄect is not huge, but
for longer waves the CWR is higher in shallow water. The eŌåĄect of angled waves
has also been brie’¼éy touched, and the indication is that the peak in the CWR curve
become more pronounced, with the WEC performing better over a wider spectrum of
wave length when the waves come in, at zero angle. The performance has also been
predicted in irregular waves using regular experimental results, and the agreement
77
==PAGE:84==
CHAPTER 7. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS
78
is good, proving that regular model experiments can be used to predict wave energy
production in an arbitrary measured spectrum.
Two mooring solutions have been tested in the Portaferry shallow water tank,
turret mooring and spread mooring. From the results, the two systems experience
similar stress. The spread mooring has its weak point when larger waves, attack at an
angle. The turret system is exposed when the wave direction align with a mooring
line. In both cases large mooring loads can arise, and in both cases the solution
seems to be adding more lines at the critical angles. The turret mooring however,
gives the better CWR, should a broad spectrum of waves come in out side the main
direction. The basis for this conclusion is a bit thin, as only one experiment with
angled linear waves was completed. Another vital part of the mooring setup is its
ability to restrict the motion of the WEC. This data has not been treated su’¼āciently
to give experimental data, but with the elasticity being similar for the two setups,
there is not much reason to suspect much diŌåĄerence. In the end of the day the best
choice of mooring will also be heavily in’¼éuenced by the cost, and as this has not
been treated it is hard to produce a ’¼ünal recommendation.
The numerical tool WAMIT has been used to predict the response of the structure
in bending.
This result has been obtained by extending the WAMIT model by
Bingham et al. [3] with a structural deformation mode, governed by simple beam
bending modes. This response has been used to predict stresses in the structure.
From this result, the proposed steel reinforced concrete structure seems to be capable
of withstanding the structural loads. A detailed design of the concrete structure will
need expert knowledge, but the concept seems to be realistic.
To round up the conclusion, some more aspects of the KNswing concept has been
treated, and the concept still seems like a good idea.
Future work on this project include detailed design of the full size concrete struc-
ture. This work will probably need to sophisticate some of the crude assumptions
made in this work. A detailed structural design will also have to take stability of the
’¼éoating WEC into account. Further work also needs to decide on the PTO system, if
rectifying valves and a manifold are bene’¼ücial for the power output and cost eŌåĄective
enough. The mooring also needs a more detailed design. In this work the mooring re-
quirement has been investigated, but how the actual mooring should be constructed
is still completely open. Should more performance measurements become possible,
a better resolution of angled waves would also be bene’¼ücial as real ocean waves are
not long crested.
