Abstract
Antimony Sulphide (Sb2S3) has recently emerged as a promising candidate for nextôÙ
generation thinôÙfilm photovoltaics due to itãs lowôÙcost, earthôÙabundant elements and
favourable optoelectric properties. Itãs suitable band gap, high absorption coefficient, and
good stability have led to a rapid rise in research into Sb2S3ôÙbased devices for solar cell
applications. This material exhibits a quasiôÙ1D crystal structure which results in highly
anisotropic electronic properties.
Correct orientation of the crystal lattice is therefore
paramount in achieving highôÙefficiency absorber layers. This challenge has contributed
to the low efficiency of fabricated Sb2S3ôÙbased devices, with the highest achieved PCE
of 8% falling significantly short of itãs high theoretical limit. This work aims to develop a
novel method to fabricate a [hk1]ôÙorientated Sb2S3 absorber layer for use in solar cells.
The effects of sputtering and annealing parameters were investigated with Raman and
photoluminescence spectroscopy, SEM imaging, and EDX analysis, and shown to play
a pivotal role the absorber properties. Additionally, silver doping was used to study itãs
effects on layer morphology and final device performance. A champion device with a
measured PCE of 1.38% was successfully fabricated.

Introduction
Developing efficient and lowôÙcost photovoltaic (PV) systems is a vital step towards comôÙ
bating the environmental and energy crisis we face today. The replacement of fossil fuels
with renewable energy sources is paramount if we are to reduce our greenhouse gas
emissions and reduce the severity of climate change impacts [1]. The PV industry ofôÙ
fers a rapidly growing production of renewable energy. In 2022 the cumulative installed
photovoltaic electricity generation capacity increased to over 1 TW, only 10 years after it
reached the 100 GW level in 2012 [2]. However, by 2023 solar PV still only met 4.5% of
global electricity demand, while over 70% is provided by nonôÙrenewable sources [3]. This
represents the continued need for cheaper and more efficient PV technology.
Currently, the most widespread solar cells are fabricated from silicon (Si), which is an
earthôÙabundant material, and have reached a market share of >90% of the total PV proôÙ
duction [4]. Both monoôÙ and polyôÙcrystaline Si modules have matured to reach close to
their theoretical highest efficiency limit of 29.4% [5]. High quality crystals are required in
order to produce these high device efficiencies [6], raising the production costs of these
modules. Furthermore, Si is an indirect bandgap material and consequently requires a
relatively thick material layer in order to absorb sufficient sunlight to perform efficiently [7].
These factors have spurred research in the direction of thinôÙfilm semiconducting materials
for solar cell applications, which are conversely direct bandgap materials and therefore
boast much higher optical absorption than indirect bandgap materials [7],[8]. This allows
for much less material use in their manufacture. Additionally to being considered alternaôÙ
tives to Si solar cells, thinôÙfilms offer great potential as upperôÙcell candidates in tandem
solar cells where absorber layers are stacked in order of increasing bandgap so as to
capture a greater range of the solar spectrum.
A promising candidate in thinôÙfilm technology is stibnite, also referred to by itãs chemical
name, antimony sulphide (Sb2S3). The first breakthrough on Sb2S3 as absorber material
in solar cells was reported by Savadogo et al in 1992 [9], and the technology developed
over the next two decades to realise Sb2S3ôÙbased single junction solar devices measured
at a 7.5% efficiency [10]. Since then, research failed to produce a device outperforming
the 2014 record until as recently as 2022, when Wang et al. reported on a 8% Sb2S3
singleôÙjunction device [11]. Earlier this year, Li et al. used silver doping to improve the
efficiency of their pristine cells by 20%, developing a 7.73% efficient champion device
[12].
The bandgap of Sb2S3 thinôÙfilms has been reported in the range of ã1.7 eV, which corôÙ
responds to a ShockleyãQueisser efficiency limit of 28.64% for a singleôÙjunction device
[13]. Coupled with a conventional Si bottomôÙlayer, highly efficient Sb2S3 films could theôÙ
oretically produce device efficiencies of over 40% in a tandem configuration [14]. The
underôÙperformance of reported devices demonstrates the need for further development
of the technology if Sb2S3 is to become a competitive part of the future PV industry.
While publications on Sb2S3 thinôÙfilms has grown exponentially over the last few years
[15], the majority of devices make use of chemical deposition or thermal evaporation techôÙ
niques in the fabrication of the Sb2S3 layer [16],[17]. Little research has been published
on the development of Sb2S3 films fabricated through a magnetron sputtering technique
[18], [19], [20], which shows good promise in large production scalability. Amongst the

2.1
ThinôÙfilm solar cells
Although firstôÙgeneration siliconôÙbased solar cells make up more than 90% of the global
PV market share [4], they are close to reaching their theoretical efficiency limit of 29.4%
[5]. This is a major driving force for research and investment into alternative absorber maôÙ
terials. ThinôÙfilm solar cell materials boast massively higher absorption coefficients than
their côÙSi counterparts, lying in the range of ã¥105 cmã1 [8]. Their use as an absorber
layer in solar cells therefore require very little material, potentially providing significantly
decreased costs and energy consumption of the manufacturing process [22]. Another
advantageous feature of thinôÙfilm absorber layers are flexible and tunable bandgaps [23],
making them suitable for use in tandem cells. Finally, due to their low thickness they
can be implemented in flexible device architectures and can be used in rollôÙtoôÙroll manôÙ
ufacturing methods, further reducing energy consumption in the manufacturing process
[22].
The two common deposition techniques used in thinôÙfilm manufacturing are physical and
chemical depositions. Physical deposition can either be evaporation, the most common
being vacuum thermal, electron beam, and laser beam evaporation, or sputtering deposiôÙ
tion which can be either direct current (DC) or radio frequency (RF) sputtering. Chemical
deposition methods include chemical bath deposition (CBD), chemical vapor deposition
(CVD), and spin coating [24].
Cadmium Telluride (CdTe), öÝôÙSi, and Copper Indium Gallium diôÙselenide (CIGS) are the
three most widely commercialized thin film solar cells [25].
öÝôÙSi cells have reached efficiencies of 13.6% and have very low manufacturing costs due
to the abundance of silicon and itãs high production scale [26]. They also have a high
absorption coefficient of 1.74 x 106cmã1. öÝôÙSi technology has been widely implemented
in electrical devices such as watches, clocks and calculators as a reliable energy source
since the 1980ãs. However, research has shifted towards the newer thinôÙfilm technologies
due to their perceived greater potential of reaching higher cell efficiencies.
CdTe is very chemically stable and robust, and can therefore be manufactured in a large
variety of deposition techniques allowing for good scalability and large production areas
[27]. Research level cells have reached efficiencies of 22.1%, and largeôÙarea production
line manufactured modules have reached as high as 19% [28][29]. However, Cadmium
toxicity and Tellurium scarcity both contribute to the undesirability of industrial scale comôÙ
mercialisation of this technology.
CIGS solar cells have recently surpassed laboratory scale efficiencies of 23%, and modôÙ
ule scale efficiencies have reached 18.6% [30]. They exhibit highly tunable bandgaps
between 1ôÙ2.6 eV, and are intrinsically stable during operation. Commercial production
is limited due to scalability issues, and both indium and gallium are relatively rare and
expensive.
Due to the limitations outlined above, research into new and emerging technologies is onôÙ
going. Recent notable technological advances have been made in perovksite solar cells,
Copper zinc tin sulfide (CZTS) solar cells, and quantum dot (QD) solar cells, reaching
efficiencies of 26.1%, 19.1%, and 14.9% respectively [31].

2.2
Antimony Sulfide for thinôÙfilm solar cells
In the last decade, antimony sulphide (Sb2S3) has attracted the attention of solar cell
researches due to itãs high optical absorption coefficient of ã¥1.8 x 105cmã1 [32] and the
earthôÙabundance and nonôÙtoxicity of itãs constituent elements. Reported direct bandgaps
of ã1.7 eV [33] also make them very suitable candidates for the top subôÙlayer in SiôÙbased
tandem dualôÙjunction solar cells.
Sb2S3 solar cell configuration exisits in two architectural types: planar solar cells and
mesoporous sensitized solar cells.
MesoporousôÙSb2S3 solar cells evolved from dyeôÙ
sensitized solar cells by replacing photoactive dyes with small crystallites of Sb2S3 [15].
A typical mesoporous Sb2S3ôÙsensitized solar cell is made using a TCO film, a thin semiôÙ
conducting layer that prevents short circuits, a mesoporous semiconducting oxide layer,
a light harvesting Sb2S3 layer, and a holeôÙtransporting material (HTM). These HTMs were
found to severely suffer from inefficient collection of holes, and hence research into this
form of cell architecture has been almost inactive since 2014, when Choi et al achieved
the record efficiency of 7.5% [10]. This literature review will therefore focus on planar
Sb2S3 solar cells, which differ from their mesoporous counterparts by the exclusion of the
mesoporous nanocrystalline semiconducting oxide layer.
Fast progress has been made in planar Sb2S3 solar cells , with Boix et al publishing their
results on a CBD fabricated Sb2S3 solar device with a PCE of 1.43% in 2012 [34], to
Wang et al achieving a record high PCE of 8% in 2022 [11]. Various deposition methods
have been employed such as CBD [35],[36], spinôÙcoating [32], thermal evaporation [37],
VTD [38] and sputtering [19]. Sputtering is a promising deposition technique, as it is
capable of producing uniform films over large areas [39],[40]. This offers the possibility for
competitive industrial scaleôÙup. Gao et al, succesfully fabricated solar cells with a device
configuration FTO/Sb2S3/CdS/iôÙZnO/ITO/Al and achieved a PCE of 2.41% by sputtering
and postôÙsulphurization by optimising the postôÙannealing parameters [18]. Wang et al.
recorded a PCE of 2.81% with their Mo/ Sb2S3/CdS/ITO/Ag device with selfôÙassembled
quasiôÙvertically oriented films. They were able to control the crystal orientation through
careful optimisation of the sputtering and sulphurization processes [19].
Low carrier concentration and high bulk and surface defects in Sb2S3 lead to poor elecôÙ
trical properties such as low conductivity and low mobility and this has been linked to itãs
oneôÙdimensional crystal structure [41]. Sb2S3 exhibits a quasiôÙ1D crystal structure which
results in highly anisotropic electronic properties and this phenomena has largely conôÙ
tributed to the struggles of researchers in achieving higher PCEãs in Sb2S3 solar cells.
In the crystal structure, Sb and S atoms are strongly bound by covalent bonds to form
(Sb4S6)n ribbons that orientate along the [001] direction, whilst the ribbons themselves
are held together by weak van der Waals forces in the [100] and [010] directions [42],[19].
Figure 2.1 shows a schematic diagram of this ribbon structure. The carrier transport along
the [hk0] direction is inefficient because the carriers need to hop between the ribbon chains
[43],[21], while the carrier transport along the [hk1] direction is much more efficient due to
a much lower recombination probability [44].
Much interest has been shown in determining the effect of crystal orientation on the elecôÙ
trical and magnetic properties of Sb2S3 films. Roy et al. confirmed that the conductivity
along the [hk1] direction was close to 2 orders of magnitude higher than that along the
[hk0] direction [45]. Additionally, the surfaces orthogonal to the [hk1] orientation have the
lowest surface energies and no dangling bonds, and therefore reduce the surface trap
sites in the Sb2S3 films [46],[15]. It is therefore evident that controlling the crystallographic
orientation is paramount in achieving higher solar cell device performance. Recently, dopôÙ

2.3
Doping Strategies
Efficient doping strategies could adjust Sb2S3 material physical and electrical properties
by producing benign defects, regulating the crystallinity, and affecting the intrinsic strucôÙ
ture [41].
Ito et al. used 5 at% titanium doping of their CBD fabricated Sb2S3 absorber layer and
achieved an increase in device PCE from 3.8% to 5.7%, but did not conclude on the mechôÙ
anisms behind this improvement [47].
Jiang et al.[48] used multiple alkali metals (Li, Na, K, Rb and Cs) to dope the absorber layer
in their FTO/côÙTiO2/Sb2S3/SpiroôÙOMeTAD/Au solar cells, again using the CBD method.
They identified that the doping atoms were not incorporated into the crystal lattice, but
rather resided in grain boundaries or the film surface. They discovered that these dopants
then reacted with sulphur at the surface, creating sulphur vacancies in the lattice. The
found that Li and Na doping caused cracking at the film surface, while K and Rb proôÙ
duced more compact films with fewer pinholes. Cs doping produced greatly enhanced
uniformity with smooth, large grain size. With respect to their pristine control device with
a PCE of 4.2%, Li and Na doping was detrimental to performance, while K and Rb slightly
improved the PCE. Their CsôÙdoped device PCE saw an improvement to 6.6% They conôÙ
cluded that the improved concentration of sulfur vacancies resulted in an increased Fermi
energy level and provided better nôÙtype conductivity and an increased charge carrier conôÙ
centration. Tang et al. observed similar results by using a 4.8% Zn doping concentration.
They similarly concluded that the doping led to increased sulphur vacancies which in turn
increased the carrier concentration within the Sb2S3 lattice [49].
Godines et al. succesfully used CBD to incorporate Ag ions into the Sb2S3 lattice. They
used an Ag concentration of 7.5mol%, which resulted in more uniform and dense films
as compared to the nonôÙdoped films. They observed a narrowing of the bandgap energy
from 1.75eV to 1.66eV, and an increased dark conductivity by an order of magnitude [50].
More recently, Yang et al. have successfully tailored the crystallographic orientation of
their Sb2S3 thinôÙfilms by using extremely low Ag doping concentration (>1%) and sulfurôÙ
izing an Ag/Sb bimetallic precursor film deposited using dualôÙsource electronôÙbeam (EB)
evaporation. Through XRD analysis they confirmed a completely [hk1] orientated crystal
orientation. The [hk1] oriented crystal structure exhibited lower defect density, a higher
conductivity, and a faster carrier mobility in comparison to those of the randomly oriented
unôÙdoped film, which contributed to a higher separation and injection efficiency of photoôÙ
generated charge carriers [21].

in an increased device PCE from 6.4% to 7.73%. They concluded the crystallinity and

Sb and Ag/Sb films were deposited onto the Glass/FTO/CdS substrates using radio freôÙ

films require annealing in a sulphur atmosphere to induce crystalinity and to incorporate

The sputtering chamber as seen in figure 3.2 was used for the deposition of the Sb and
Ag/Sb layers. The substrates were loaded through the loading chamber into the main

the sulphur powder. The tube pressure was maintained at a constant value of 175 mbar

pressure and should provide a uniform atmosphere inside the box. This setup is shown
in figure 3.3

Raman Spectroscopy
Raman spectroscopy is a useful tool for providing information about the vibrational modes
of crystal structures. It relies on the principal that when light interacts with molecules
in a sample most of the photons are elastically scattered by a phenomenon known as
raleigh scattering, but very low amounts of the light is scattered inelastically. This inelastic
scattering means the scattered photons have a different energy than the incident light,
and this difference or ãshiftã can be measured quantitively. This difference in energy of the
incident and scattered photons is what is known as the raman shift. Plotting the intensity
of this light as a function of the energy shift creates a raman spectrum. Each peak on this
spectrum corresponds to a certain vibrational mode and is unique to each material [54],
[55]. Raman spectroscopy can be used to help identify different phases that can exist in a
material [56], provide information on levels of mechanical stress and strain [57], and help
detect defects and impurities [58].
A typical raman setup consists of an excitation laser source which passes through a beam
splitter and a Raleigh rejection filter, which blocks the elastically scattered light. The reôÙ
maining light travels through the spectrometer entrance slit onto a diffraction grating which
splits the light into several beams. This dispersed light is then captured by a CCD camera

Photoluminescence spectroscopy
PL spectroscopy relies on the phenomena of light emmision due to carrier recombinaôÙ
tion in certain materials. When incident light is absorbed in a sample, electrons in the
valence band are promoted to the conduction band, creating eletronôÙhole pairs. When
these charge carriers recombine they emit photons with an energy level that corresponds
to the bandgap of the material [61].
A PL spectrum is a plot of this emitted light as a function of photon wavelength or energy.
The same confocal Raman spectroscopy system as used for the raman measurements
was used to gather the PL spectra, with the grating adjusted to 300 gr/mm. Again, an
acquisition time of 10 s for each frame and five frames averaging was used to capture the
spectra.

5
Discussion
5.1
1st Iteration
Throughout the sample spread raman peaks were consistent, appearing at the typical
band positions for stibnite reported in literature. Comparing the measured spectra, the
most notable discrepancy was that of the gas flow annealed sample at 350ãÎC, whose
peak intensities were substantially lower than that of the other samples at all of the peak
positions. Furthermore, the peak at 314cmôÙ1 attributed to symmetric stretching of SôÙSb(2)ôÙ
S bonds was less defined and broader. Broader peaks may indicate a lower crystalinity
or a higher state of disorder [68],[69].
For the graphite box annealed sample at 300ãÎC, we see the peak at 159cmôÙ1 shifts to a
lower wavelength. Pure antimony exhibits a characteristic peak at 150cmôÙ1 [70], so this
shift could suggest a sulphur deficient film.
Regarding the PL spectra of the samples, we observe decreasing intensity with lower anôÙ
nealing temperatures. High PL intensity typically indicates high crystal quality with fewer
nonôÙradiative recombination centers, while reduced PL intensity can signal the presence
of a significant number of defects [71]. This implies that increasing the annealing temôÙ
perature of the samples leads to higher crystalline quality. The absorption peaks are poôÙ
sitioned very closely, which is expected due to the similar fabrication parameters across
the samples.
The SEM imaging provides an insight into the surface morphologies of the samples. For
the gas flow annealed films, the 300ãÎC sample shown in figure 4.3 appears somewhat
amorphous, lacking distinct grain boundaries. From this it can be inferred that the temôÙ
perature is insufficient for proper crystallization of the film. Although the film becomes
smoother with defined grain boundaries after increasing the temperature to 350ãÎC as
seen in figure 4.4, pinholes are observed as well as a buildôÙup of some form across the
surface. The film annealed at 400ãÎC was completely delaminated and only the CdS layer
remained on the substrate. These observations led to the discontinuation of using the
gas flow configuration for the annealing step and further annealing was performed with
the graphite box configuration.
SEM images of the graphite box annealed samples reveal an initiation of cluster sites
above 300ãÎC, with these clusters forming bubbleôÙlike structures at 400ãÎC. The low PL
intensity of the 300ãÎC samples, and large bubbleôÙlike clusters seen in the 400ãÎC led to
350ãÎC being the chosen annealing temperature to use for the second iteration of batches.
5.2
2nd Iteration
The second fabrication iteration provided a large spread of silver doping concentrations.
This allowed the characterization results to be compared at different doping concentraôÙ
tions, with the rest of the fabrication parameters remaining constant.
The EDX analysis of the sample before and after annealing revealed inconsistent incorôÙ
poration of silver throughout the sample. Analysing the silver to antimony ratio before and
after annealing (see figure 4.9), we expect to see a linear relationship. However, no such
linear relationship is evident, with the spread of results appearing in a random manner.
Furthermore, sulphur content throughout the annealed sample remained almost constant
at 46%, revealing a homogeneous sulphurisation across the surface. The causes for this
are unclear, but could be attributed to an nonôÙhomogeneous heat distribution inside the

box leading to uneven nucleation sites across the sample. Another possible reason could
be the presence of impurities originating from the porous vacancies within the graphite,
deposited by previous annealing experiments.
The measured raman spectra maintained the same overall structure throughout the samôÙ
ple spread. The peak intensities generally declined with decreasing silver amounts down
to a Ag/Sb ratio of 0.96, after which the intensity rose with further reduction of silver. The
1.19 (blue), 0.88 (green) and to a lesser extent the 0.96 (gold) samples show evidence of
peak formation around 291cmôÙ1, which is a dominant peak assigned to amorphous Sb2S3,
meaning these samples could have a lower degree of crystallization. Comparing the meaôÙ
sured raman spectra to that of pristine Sb2S3, the doped samples still exhibit the typical
stibnite peaks at 285cmôÙ1 and around 312cmôÙ1, with the additional Ag3SbS2 peaks also
present. This could represent a mixture of phases throughout the sample. The stibnite
peaks at 285cmôÙ1 and around 312cmôÙ1 were overall less dominant in the doped samples
than that of the undoped samples. This decrease in intensity could be due to the decrease
in phonon correlation lengths caused by local crystal lattice disorder induced by doping
[50].
Considering the PL spectra across the sample, the large variation in intensity with no
apparent correlation to doping concentration could relate to the nonôÙuniformity of atomic
ratios and phases across the film. Interestingly, the peak positions as seen in figure 4.12b
do not differ by more than 1.7meV. The incorporation of the larger Ag atoms into the Sb2S3
lattice should theoretically shift the PL absorption peak to higher wavelengths, representôÙ
ing a lowering of the optical bandgap [50], [72]. The lack of evidence of this could suggest
an inefficient incorporation of silver atoms into the Sb2S3 lattice, and further supports the
notion of phase separation throughout the bulk surface, with secondary phases being
contained to the film surface.
Analysing the film morphology through SEM imaging, we clearly observe the initiation and
growth of large crystals, increasing in size with higher doping concentrations. The indisôÙ
tinct yet evident grain boundaries seen in the 11.3% sample seem to be covered with a
webôÙlike film, which is aggravated with higher doping. Both of these phenomena could
further indicate phase transition and separation throughout the surface. The high silver
content of the formed crystals and flakes could suggest that excess silver not incorporated
into the stibnite lattice would build up and separate, leading to the formation of these preôÙ
cipitates. This, coupled with the variation of raman and PL spectra, suggested the doping
in this case was excessive and led to the decision to reduce the doping concentration for
the third iteration of sample fabrication.
5.3
3rd Iteration
As per a report by Dong et al. [73], the crystalization temperature of Sb2S3 is signifiôÙ
cantly reduced with the introduction of Ag doping. Two annealing temperatures of 300ãÎC
and 350ãÎC were therefore assessed during this iteration. Assessment of the EDX meaôÙ
surements of the samples revealed very SbôÙrich films for the 300ãÎC samples, suggesting
incomplete sulfurization and crystallization. This is supported by the SEM images, where
these samples can be seen to exhibit an amorphous morphology. Increasing the temperôÙ
ature led to S/Sb ratios close to 1.25, much closer to the stoichiometric 1.5 ratio of pure
stibnite. The sulfurization of these samples was much more consistent than with the preôÙ
vious iteration, as the silver doping concentration after sulfurization was consistent with
the variation of sputtered silver.
The raman spectra of the samples could not be used to make definitive claims about the
structure of the layer, as variations in the spectra were observed across the same samôÙ
==PAGE:41==
ple surface. The spectra shown in figure 4.16 were closely representative of the average
spectrum for the corresponding sample. The raman spectra of the films again show a
dominant peak around 292cmôÙ1, with the exception of the 007 sample where this peak
is shifted down to the more typical 285cmôÙ1 of crystalline stibnite. After analysis of the
crossôÙsection SEM of these films, this peak shift to 292cmôÙ1 could be representative of
the amorphous bottom subôÙlayer of the double layer structure of the absorber layer.
The SEM topôÙviews confirm the nonôÙuniformity across the sample surfaces, where in all
three samples regions of large, distinct and smooth grains existed in localised spots within
the bulk surface, which consisted of smaller, irregular grain sizes.
Regarding the doubleôÙlayer seen in the crossôÙsection SEM images, reports of a doubleôÙ
layer formation in thinôÙfilm absorber layers have previously been reported in literature,
specifically in the case of CZTS films [74], [75]. Zhang et al. found the double layer
distribution to hinder the solar cell performance by causing an interfacial barrier with high
series resistance [75].The existence of this amorphous subôÙlayer could additionally be the
cause of irregularities across the top surface of the films. Consequently an attempt was
made to avoid the formation of this double layer by drastically reducing the film thickness
for the fourth iteration of sample fabrication.
With respect to sulfurizing metallic stacks in this way, where layered films are deposited
sequentially on top of one another, the stacking sequence can affect the sulfurization
pathway and nucleation dynamics. This has recently been studied in depth with respect
to CZTS films by Lai et al. [76], and to a lesser extent by Chalapathi et al. with regards to
pyrargyrite (Ag3SbS3) absorbers. In reference to these works, the sputtering sequence
of Ag/Sb should be investigated further to potentially enhance the film morphology.
5.4
4th Iteration
Through the SEM imaging both the doped and undoped samples appear similar in morôÙ
phology. Although the top surface of the films still contain some irregularities in grain
size and smoothness, the films exhibit good uniformity. The crossôÙsection images reveal
no evidence of the doubleôÙlayer formed in the previous iterations, although the absorber
layer is not as uniform and smooth as the uppermost subôÙlayer seen in the third iteration
crossôÙsection images. The occurrence of pinholes seems vastly reduced, however a few
can still be seen to occur at the interface of the CdS and absorber layers. This could
suggest that this interface plays a significant role in the nonôÙhomogeneous grain growth
of the absorber layer. Nonetheless these samples were chosen for the fabrication of solar
cells as per the methodology outlined in section 3.2.
The best performing cell exhibited an openôÙcircuit voltage of 460mV, a shortôÙcircuit current
density of 8.71mA/cm2 and a record PCE of 1.38%. Cells 10a2, 9a1 and 9b1 all showed
irregular IV curves with numerous peaks and troughs. Possible explanations for this could
be impurities or dislocations in the crystal lattice acting as recombination centers for the
charge carriers, shunt pathways created by pinhole occurrence in the films, or defects at
the interface between different layers of the cell which could create traps or barriers to the
charge carriers. Furthermore, nonôÙuniform doping across the absorber layer can create
regions with differing electrical properties. These are all possible causes of the fluctuating
IV measurements. The fact that a shadow mask was not used during the measurement
could also contribute to the fluctuations. Although most of the charge carriers outside of
the contact area would recombine before reaching the contacts, there may still exist some
nonôÙuniform current collection caused by these carriers, giving rise to fluctuations.
It is evident that the undoped cells all exhibit higher PCEãs than the doped samples. The

doped samples exhibit significantly higher openôÙcircuit voltages than the undoped samôÙ
ples, but show substantially lower current densities, giving rise to the poor PCE perforôÙ
mance. This higher VOC could be a consequence of the silver dopants passivating some
material defects within the absorber layer. The lower current densities could arise from
the nonôÙuniform doping within the layer leading to variations in the crystallinity across the
film, this could negatively impact the charge carrier collection, reducing the overall current
collected.

6
Conclusion
The analysis carried out in this work makes evident the need for careful optimisation of the
parameters used for fabricating Sb2S3 absorber layers. Through the various characteriôÙ
sation methods employed, precursor thickness, postôÙdeposition annealing temperatures,
and doping concentration and uniformity were found to all play significant roles in layer
morphology and optical properties, as well as final device performance. Furthermore,
without quantifiable characterization of crystal grain orientation, hypotheses were drawn
based on previous literature studies on the impacts of these fabrication parameters.
The Sb2S3 absorber layer was found to exhibit a doubleôÙlayer structure for the early iteraôÙ
tions where sputtering deposition times were 45 and 30 minutes. SEM analysis revealed
these films to be ã¥1ôçm thick. This doubleôÙlayer had been reported to create a high series
resistance at the back contact, and therefore hinder device performance. The sputtering
deposition time was consequently reduced, resulting in an absorber layer thickness of
roughly 300 nm with no doubleôÙlayer occurrence.
Two sulfurization configurations were initially tested: a graphite box configuration, and a
2ôÙtemperature zone gasôÙflow configuration. The gasôÙflow configuration produced samples
showed some form of precipitate formation at grain boundaries, and completely delamôÙ
inated layers at elevated temperatures. The graphite box tests produced samples that
exhibited decent uniformity across the absorber surface, albeit with signs of cluster forôÙ
mation. Annealing temperatures for both configurations was adjusted in steps from 300ãÎC
to 350ãÎC and finally 400ãÎC. The samples annealed at 300ãÎC exhibited low PL intensities,
while the films at 400ãÎC all showed visible defect signs. 350ãÎC graphite box annealing
was chosen as the preferred sulfurization method moving forward.
To first analyse the effect of Ag doping, a screening sample was fabricated. EDX anaysis
of the precursor sample showed a smooth gradient of doping concentration across the
surface ranging from 11.2% to 25.9%. Post sulfurization the sample exhibited a seemingly
random spread of Ag/Sb ratios across the surface. Phase seperation was evident, and
Raman characterization suggested the coexistance of both Sb2S3 and Ag3SbS3. Large
crystals and flakes across the surface were observed via SEM, and EDX analysis revealed
their composition to be mainly silver with traces of sulphur. No major shifting of the peak
positions of the PL spectra was observed throughout the screening, suggesting inefficient
incorporation of the dopant.
The next iteration of sample fabrication produced films with Ag concentrations of 1.7ôÙ
3.9%. Although the films possessed localized areas of preferential large, smooth and
distinct grains, there was a high level of nonôÙuniformity across the surface. The doubleôÙ
layer morphology was still present in these samples, with pinholes observed across the
surface and at layer interfaces.
The final iteration of sample fabrication resulted in uniform absorber films with few pinôÙ
holes and no occurrence of the doubleôÙlayer observed previously. EDX analysis of the
doped samples revealed no quantifiable presence of Ag, proving the Ag concentration
was below the detection limit of 0.1 wt%. These samples were used for device fabricaôÙ
tion of FTO/CdS/Ag:SbS/SpiroôÙOMeTAD/Au solar cells. The resultant champion device
possessed a measured PCE of 1.38%, which was fabricated from an unôÙdoped absorber
layer. Fluctuating IôÙV curves were generated over many of the samples, though ISC and
VOC could still be extracted. The doped devices possessed VOC values up to 28% higher

==PAGE:44==
than the undoped cells, but had significantly lower ISC values and consequently lower
PCEãs.
Recommendations for the continuation of this research would be to further optimize the
annealing parameters, specifically investigating the interplay between duration and temôÙ
perature. Adjusting the sputtering deposition rates may also allow for more control over
the doping procedure. The influence of precursor sequence should also be investigated to
further understand the formation dynamics of the crystal lattice. Furthermore, an inôÙdepth
XRD study would be a natural next step, enabling the quantification of crystallographic
orientation.
The work presented herein contributes to a fundamental understanding of this novel dopôÙ
ing strategy, and presents further avenues of exploration in the field of tailored crystalloôÙ
graphic orientation of Sb2S3 thinôÙfilm solar cells.
