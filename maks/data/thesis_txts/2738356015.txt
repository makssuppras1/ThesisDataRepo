Abstract
The global wind energy sector has seen signiﬁcant growth, yet faces challenges due to the
variability of wind production and its impact on electricity prices. This research explores
the use of a value-based metric, Annual Energy Value (AEV), to optimise wind farm
layouts and enhance revenue, addressing limitations of the traditional Levelised Cost of
Energy (LCOE).
A multi-objective optimisation framework incorporating AEV and Annual Energy Produc-
tion (AEP) was applied to wind farm scenarios with ﬁxed electricity prices, characteristic
of Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs), and volatile electricity prices, typical in countries
with high wind energy penetration. Using the Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) algo-
rithm in TOPFARM, the study compared dense and sparse wind farm types.
Results show that ﬁxed electricity prices do not reduce revenue, whereas volatile prices
cause signiﬁcant revenue loss, with value factors dropping to 77 % for dense and 80 % for
sparse wind farms.
To optimise for revenue, a focus on volatile electricity prices and dependencies on weather
conditions such as wind speed and wind directions should be prioritised. The ﬁndings
indicate future research should reﬁne the AEV metric, and consider real-world case studies
using value-based metrics to improve the economic viability of wind energy projects.
These eﬀorts aim to better integrate renewable energy sources and address climate change
challenges.

Introduction
The global wind energy sector witnesses remarkable growth in the 21st century, driven by
technological advancements and increasing recognition of wind power as a key component
in combating climate change. Historic development of global wind energy installations
from 2001 to 2023 highlights a consistent upward trajectory in new installations [1].
Notably, 2023 sets a record with nearly 120 Gigawatts (GW) of new installations. Various
factors contribute to this signiﬁcant milestone [1]. For example, the US government
expands wind energy through stable regulatory policies and ﬁnancial incentives [2], while
Europe enhances oﬀshore energy competitiveness by reducing oﬀshore wind capital costs,
thereby increasing the oﬀshore energy share [3].
As the wind industry expands and the wind energy production share in the grid increases,
the electricity market faces signiﬁcant challenges related to the alignment of supply and
demand [4]. Wind production is inherently variable and depends primarily on weather
conditions, leading to periods when generation does not match energy demand [5]. This
misalignment becomes more critical as the wind industry grows and a larger fraction of
the generation portfolio consists of renewable energy such as wind [6]. Peak wind energy
generation often does not coincide with peak demand, complicating the balancing of
supply and demand and introducing signiﬁcant volatility into the electricity market [6].
Government subsidies such as feed-in Tariﬀ(FIT) for wind farms are gradually reducing
as the share of wind energy grows [7]. This shift moves towards an open electricity market.
It has been observed that countries experiencing volatility in electricity prices due to wind
energy generation, where wind farms participate in an open market, often ﬁnd a negative
correlation between wind energy production and electricity prices [4]. For example in
Figure 1.1 a negative correlations between wind speed and electricity price was found at
Østerild site in Denmark, a country with a wind energy share of 54 % [8]. This trend,
as wind energy development penetration grows, is anticipated to intensify. The cause of
this negative correlation lies in the before mentioned misalignment between supply and
demand, resulting in high wind energy production coinciding with low electricity prices
and vice versa, leading to revenue loss for wind farms [4].
This issue is further pronounced for wind farms with Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)
that ﬁx electricity prices over the operational lifetime of the farm, using the Levelised
Cost of Energy (LCOE) metric [9]. While fundamental to the rapid growth of the wind
industry, this metric fails to account for electricity price volatility, undervaluing the wind
farm [6]. Consequently, as wind farms with PPAs operate in an open energy market, they
experience revenue loss due to this oversight.

2.1
Value-based metrics in wind energy
LCOE and AEP have been cornerstone metrics in designing energy systems, enabling the
rapid growth and development of the wind industry [10]. In the design of wind farms,
they are typically optimised to minimise the LCOE or maximise the AEP [10, 4]. LCOE
measures the cost per unit of electricity generated over the lifetime of the wind farm,
whereas AEP determines the total AEP [6]. However, both metrics fall short in considering
the volatility of market prices in which wind farm developers with a high share of wind
energies are participating [11]. Loth et al. [6] criticise LCOE for not considering the
mismatch between energy production and market demand, resulting in an overvaluation
of energy systems like wind and solar energy.
As wind and solar energy penetration increases, studies have discovered that electricity
prices show a negative correlation with production [4]. In other words, when wind farm
production is high, with high wind speeds, the electricity price will be low or even negative,
resulting in small revenue or even paying for the production. Gronheit et al. [12] examined
the impact of increasing wind energy share on market prices in western Denmark between
2009 and 2021 and discovered often lower market prices during high production periods
and vice versa.
One solution for wind farms confronting ﬂuctuating electricity prices could be optimising
by maximising the revenue concerning the value rather than minimising the cost of energy
==PAGE:17==

[11]. However, metrics that can be implemented to carry out such optimisation are not
fully studied yet, and the wind energy industry is still exploring the best solution. One of
the main problems is that the proposed value-based metrics require complex electricity
price models that take into account several factors [6]. Furthermore, these metrics need
to consider the interactions of the whole energy system [6]. Due to its complexity, the
implementation of other or new systems is elaborately or infeasible [6].
Given the widespread integration of metrics such as AEP and LCOE in energy system
design, their simplicity and success in developing variable renewable energy systems are
evident [10]. The goal now is to deﬁne new metrics that are equally simple but also account
for the value of variable renewable energy systems, such as wind energy, to maximise
their revenue. Two proposed metrics that could fulﬁl this are the Cost of Valued Energy
(COVE) and the AEV. These metrics aim to provide a more comprehensive evaluation by
incorporating both the cost and the value aspects of renewable energy production.
Simpson et al. [13] introduced COVE as a simple single metric that adjusts generation
according to normalised hourly prices. This ensures that the economic viability of a system
is determined by minimising the ratio of costs to revenue [6]. Meanwhile, ﬂuctuating
commodity prices are inﬂuenced by the balance between supply and demand [6].
Bechmann et al. [4] introduced AEV as a simple and practical replacement for the AEP
metric, designed to incorporate electricity price ﬂuctuations using wind distribution instead
of time-series data, making it computationally eﬃcient. Therefore, it can be integrated
into current design methods and utilised as a new objective function for optimising wind
farms [4].
In the case studies of the paper from Loth et al. [6], "Why we must move beyond
LCOE for renewable energy design," three distinguished methods were carried out to
integrate price models in COVE. Data from Germany, representing a high wind energy
share, and from California, where solar energy penetrates, were analysed [6]. The results
show that electricity grids with high renewable energy penetration experience signiﬁcant
generation devaluation, where variable renewable energies generate less value due to time
misalignment with market demand [6]. In comparison, the COVE metric consistently
resulted higher than the LCOE, indicating that LCOE undervalues the cost of integrating
variable renewable energies [6].
Bechmann et al. [4] demonstrate the practical application of the new AEV metric in the
Danish market, where the wind energy share penetrates, by analysing the impact of price
volatility on wind turbine revenue. Findings showed that wind turbines can lose over 15%
of their revenue due to price volatility. Hence, designing wind turbines and wind farms
to generate more energy at lower wind speeds can signiﬁcantly increase their revenue [4].
The AEV metric oﬀers an eﬀective approach to evaluating the value of wind turbines and
wind farms in dynamic pricing scenarios. It addresses price volatility by incorporating a
new price curve into AEP calculations [4]. This price curve can be derived from electricity
price data collected during the wind measurement campaign conducted for the design of
the wind turbine or wind farm [4].
This study aims to enhance the value and revenue generated by wind farms by optimising
wind farm layouts concerning a value-based metric, where AEV stands out due to its
simplicity and ease of integration. While COVE oﬀers a detailed and comprehensive ap-

2.2
Financial support instruments in wind energy
FIT and PPA have helped to accelerate the development and growth in global installation
of renewable energies such as wind energy [10].
In this section, both price support
instruments concerning wind energy are described, detailing how they contributed to the
growth and highlighting their limitations.
2.2.1
Feed-in tariﬀ(FIT)
A FIT is a ﬁnancial mechanism from the government implemented to support the expansion
of wind energy [5]. This is realised by guaranteed ﬁxed rate payments to the wind farm
producers for the electricity they generate for a certain period [5]. These subsidies help
develop wind energy and attract investors [5]. The FIT rates are designed to ensure a
stable revenue stream, providing a ﬁnancially viable asset [5]. It represents a dominant
support policy implemented in 83 countries for integrating renewable energies into energy
systems. However, a trend of declining electricity prices in the wholesale electricity market
associated with increasing renewable energy share and FITs was found, indicating a
decrease in proﬁts [5]. A wholesale electricity market is a marketplace where electricity is
bought and sold in large quantities by various market players [14]. It includes three main
types of markets, such as the intraday market or balancing mechanism that is responsible
for real-time adjustments and maintaining grid balance [14]. Second, the day-ahead
market trades electricity one day in advance for the next 24 h or 48 h of the next day [14].
Finally, the forward market is where market buyers can buy and sell electricity for future
delivery [14]. Moreover, further studies showed a market distortion due to high renewable
energy production with FITs and negative wholesale electricity prices [5]. Furthermore, it
denotes an increased price volatility due to its natural variability in production.
As the share of wind energy increases and becomes an established energy producer
contributing signiﬁcantly to the electricity grid, the subsidies from FITs were reduced
and eventually shifted to an auction system [7]. This resulted in wind energy producers
participating in the open electricity market, where they bid to oﬀer the lowest price for
generating electricity [7].
2.2.2
Power purchase agreement (PPA)
In contrast to the FIT, a PPA is a contract between an electricity generator and a utility
that represents a seller and buyer respectively [9]. The aim is to establish a fair and
risk-controlled agreement for the purchase and sale of energy [9]. In the wind energy
industry, PPAs were fundamental for growth and development by providing a stable and
predictable revenue [9]. This is carried out by a ﬁxed electricity price for the generated
energy by the wind farm over its entire lifetime [9]. Hence, it provides investors with
a stable revenue with reduced ﬁnancial risk [9]. However, to determine the electricity

operational costs of a wind farm. As discussed before, in terms of renewable energy, the
LCOE undervalues the cost of integrating renewable energies.
To conclude both price support instruments, the FIT is a government policy mechanism
that sets ﬁxed payments for wind energy production, with standardised terms and
government-set rates. A PPA is a commercial contract between a buyer and seller, where
terms and a ﬁxed price are negotiated between both parties, providing ﬂexibility and
customised agreements. Even though PPAs and FITs have successfully increased the
share of wind energy in countries worldwide, one common problem can be identiﬁed with
the increased share of renewable energies, which results in volatile electricity prices. For
that reason, the adaptation of renewable energies and consideration of the ﬂuctuating
electricity prices in electricity markets are of great importance.
2.3
Introduction to wind farm layout optimisation
Optimising the position of the individual wind turbine within the farm is crucial to
mitigate the wake eﬀect, which reduces the wind speed and increases the turbulence
intensity. Thereby, energy production is maximised, and loads on the wind turbine are
reduced. This subsection explains the basic principles of the wake eﬀect and provides an
overview of the key points. Afterwards, wind farm layout optimisation is introduced, and
the primary objectives are discussed.
2.3.1
Basic principles of wake eﬀects
Wake eﬀects are an important factor in wind farm performance, as the turbulent airﬂows
created by upstream turbines can impact the eﬃciency of downstream turbines [15]. This
subsection delves into the theory of the wake eﬀects. By understanding and mitigating
these impacts, the layout of the entire wind farm can be optimised.
Wake eﬀects occur due to the disruption of the airﬂow behind a wind turbine, as illustrated
in Figure 2.1, leading to regions of reduced wind speed and increased turbulence [16].
With the wind direction wake eﬀect changes as shown in Figure 2.2. The wind turbine
operates by extracting kinetic energy from the wind to generate electricity [17]. This
extraction of wind speed in front of the wind turbine causes a reduction of wind speed in
the wake of the turbine and an increase in turbulence intensity [17]. In the analysis of
the wake, two main phenomena are of interest: velocity deﬁcit and increased turbulence
intensity [16]. The velocity deﬁcit, resulting from the extraction of kinetic energy, leads
to a reduction in the power output of downstream wind turbines [16]. Meanwhile, the
increased turbulence caused by the wake induces unsteady loading and elevated fatigue
loads on downstream turbines [17].
The wake behind a wind turbine is divided into two distinct regions: the near wake and
the far wake [17]. The near wake region starts immediately behind the wind turbine and
continues until 2-4 rotor diameters downstream [16]. The airﬂow in this region is mainly
deﬁned by the blade geometry, number of blades, blade aerodynamics, and tip vortices
[17]. Therefore, the near wake region is dominated by rotor-induced turbulence [16].
==PAGE:20==

Beyond the near wake, the far wake extends further downstream [16]. In this region, the
wind speed gradually recovers, and turbulent intensity starts to reduce. One reason for
accelerated wake recovery is the large-scale atmospheric turbulence, which enhances the
mixing of turbulence within the wake [16]. The velocity deﬁcit in the far wake region
resembles a Gaussian proﬁle, which is axisymmetric and self-similar, meaning the ﬂow
pattern maintains a consistent shape as it progresses downstream [16, 17].
The wind turbine or wind farm is aﬀected by the wake in several ways; however, there
are two main points: the performance and lifetime of the wind turbine [19, 20]. One is
the reduced power production due to the lower wind speeds aﬀected by multiple turbines
in a large wind farm [19, 20]. Another is the increased mechanical stress due to the
increased turbulence intensity within the wake that impacts the downstream turbines
with ﬂuctuating loads, which can lead to higher fatigue damage compared to freestanding
turbines [20]. This unsteady loading can reduce the operational lifespan of the turbines.

2.3.2
Fundamentals of wind farm layout optimisation
To mitigate the wake eﬀect, wind farm layout optimisation algorithms are implemented
when planning a wind farm. This involves selecting the optimal location, adopting an
accurate wake model, deﬁning a realistic objective function, and arranging the wind
turbines to maximise power production [15].
The development of applying a wind farm layout optimisation started in the late 1970s
when the wind turbines were positioned in a grid-like conﬁguration, without focusing
on the spacing of the wind turbines. Afterwards, when the ﬁrst electricity-generating
wind farm was installed in the early 1980s, the ﬁrst engineering wake models were carried
out. This wake model was built on the principles of ﬂow momentum conservation and
assumptions of far wake expansion, aiming to optimise spacing between wind turbines in
a linear array. It only resulted in an improvement of 1.29 % power production; however,
this showed that by rearranging the wind turbines’ positioning, an improvement could be
achieved [15].
The scientist G. Mosetti introduced in 1994 the ﬁrst wind farm layout optimisation,
utilising a Genetic algorithm [21]. Through iteration, this algorithm reﬁnes a population of
conﬁgurations, methodically moving toward an optimal layout [21]. This layout maximises
energy production at the lowest cost while exploring the wide-ranging possibilities of wind
turbines. This is followed by numerous new algorithms for wind farm layout optimisation,
for example, Gradient-based optimisation, Monte Carlo optimisation, and Stochastic
Evolution, to name a few [15].

2.4
Wind turbine power curve determination
The power curve, which illustrates the relationship between wind speed and power output,
is essential for evaluating turbine eﬃciency, optimising wind farm layouts, and AEP. The
IEC 61400-12-1 Edition 3.0 standard [22] outlines a comprehensive methodology for the
measurement, analysis, and reporting of wind turbine’s power performance, ensuring
consistency and accuracy.
The determination of the power curve is fundamental for assessing the performance of
a wind turbine exposed to varying wind conditions, which is crucial for manufacturers,
operators, and investors. Furthermore, accurate power curves allow the optimisation of
wind farm layouts for the desired output. In addition, the power curve plays a vital role
in ﬁnancial modelling by determining the AEP, thereby estimating the revenue of a wind
farm.
To compute the power curve and consequently, the AEP, wind speed and power output
measurements are required. For the ﬂow distortion of the measurements, various prerequi-
sites for the test site must be evaluated. First, assess the position of the wind measurement
equipment. Next, deﬁne a suitable measurement sector. Additionally, determine if site
calibration according to standards is required and, if so, establish ﬂow corrections through
measurements. In the evaluation of the site, particular attention should be given to
==PAGE:23==
2
LITERATURE REVIEW
11
topographical variations and roughness, as well as other wind turbines and obstacles such
as buildings and trees.
The wind speed needs to be measured at hub height using measurement equipment
according to IEC standards. Accurate wind speed measurement is essential, as it directly
inﬂuences the power curve’s precision.
For consistency and the possibility of comparison, normalisation procedures are carried
out. Typically, the measurements are normalised for air density to account for atmospheric
variations. Further normalisation can include adjustments for turbulence intensity and
vertical wind shear.
The power output is measured using calibrated power transducers, ensuring high accuracy
as speciﬁed by IEC standards. Power data is averaged over 10-minute intervals to align
with wind speed measurements.
Further instructions are given for data collection and processing. The data acquisition
should span a wide range of wind speeds, ideally from cut-in to cut-out speeds, ensuring
a comprehensive dataset with at least 180 hours of valid data. Adequate data points
are crucial for a statistically signiﬁcant power curve. Additionally, data from periods of
turbine shutdowns or curtailments are excluded to prevent skewing the power curve.
The power curve is established by grouping the wind speeds and power output into 0.5
m/s wind speed bins. This is carried out by calculating the average wind speed and power
output for each bin.
Due to expected uncertainties, a comprehensive uncertainty analysis of the power curve,
considering factors such as measurement device precision, environmental variability, and
data processing methods, is required. These uncertainties are expressed as standard
deviations and included in the power curve presentation.

2.5.2
Cost of Energy (COE)
The Cost of Energy (COE) is a metric implemented into an optimisation problem where
the objective function is to minimise the cost but maximise the total power production
[24]. For a logical categorisation of the objective functions, the COE metric is analysed
instead of LCOE. The diﬀerence is that COE provides yearly costs, whereas LCOE oﬀers
normalised costs over the lifetime of the wind farm. Thus, COE is directly comparable
with AEP, AEV, and COVE, which are non-normalised metrics. Other than the AEP,
the COE captures the costs of the energy that is produced with a wind turbine or wind
farm [25]. Therefore, in the concept of the optimisation landscape, the COE is located on
the edge of the triangle between production and cost, which denotes a balance between
both aspects.
To calculate the COE, the total annual costs are divided by the AEP. According to Stanley
et al., the annual costs of a wind farm are comprised of several components [25]. These
include the ﬁxed charge rate, which covers the ﬁnancing costs, the initial expense of the
turbines, the costs associated with setting up the infrastructure, and the ongoing operation
and maintenance costs [25]. This method of deﬁning the COE allows for a comprehensive
metric that accounts for both the energy production and the associated costs, providing a
clear picture of the wind farm’s economic performance [25].

Another metric introduced by Loth et al. [6] considers the value by incorporating revenue
instead of AEP into the existing metric, the COE. This metric, known as COVE, provides
a more comprehensive understanding of a wind farm’s economic performance by taking
into account the revenue [6].
The calculation of COVE involves dividing the annual costs by the revenue of the wind
farm, which is deﬁned as AEV. Therefore, the COVE is located on the edge of the triangle,
showing a balance of cost and revenue.
In scenarios where the electricity price remains constant, this term simpliﬁes to include only
the power, aligning with the AEP. This simpliﬁcation ultimately leads to the derivation
of the COE, providing a consistent and comparable metric for assessing wind farm
performance.
By exploring these subsections, this section aims to provide a thorough understanding of
the elements involved in value-based metrics integrated into wind farm layout optimisation.
The knowledge gained here lays the groundwork for deﬁning the methodology in the next
chapter 3 to enhance the value and revenue of wind farms performed in this study.

In this study, a multi-objective analysis is conducted to optimise the wind farm layout.
The objective functions investigated are the AEP and AEV. While computing the AEP,

3.2
Optimisation framework for multi-objective analysis
In this research, the goal is to identify a trade-oﬀbetween AEP and AEV in a wind
farm layout optimisation, to maximise the revenue of a wind farm. This is carried out
by performing a multi-objective analysis, and in this section, the framework for the
multi-objective analysis is deﬁned. Therefore, the utilised Pareto method is ﬁrst described
and explained. To compute potential optimal solutions and analyse them with the Pareto
==PAGE:29==

method, the implementation of weighting factors and random initial starts is described in
the next step. Finally, the Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) algorithm applied in this
study is presented.
As various solutions are expected, not only one optimal solution is achieved because no
objective can be improved without sacriﬁcing another [27]. If one solution is better in
both objectives, it is considered non-dominated, meaning no other solution can dominate
this solution [27]. If a point is non-dominated, it is called a Pareto optimal solution [27].
As various points can achieve this, a collection of Pareto optimal solutions is computed,
which is called the Pareto set [27]. The Pareto front, on the other hand, is the set of
objective function values corresponding to the solutions in the Pareto set [27]. Thus, the
Pareto set contains the top choices based on the criteria, and the Pareto front displays
their performance [27]. In this case, the Pareto front provides a set of optimal solutions
where no objective can be improved without sacriﬁcing the other, which provides the
opportunity to explore the balance between maximising energy production and enhancing
revenue [28].
3.2.1

is employed. This approach combines the objectives into a single function that balances
AEP and AEV using a weighting factor ⁄. Thus, the objective function can be deﬁned as

3.2.3
Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) optimisation algorithm
SGD is an optimisation algorithm useful for optimising functions where the objective
represents an average over a large number of data points and calculating the gradient
of the entire dataset is computationally expensive. SGD performs this by iteratively
updating the parameters based on the gradient computed from a randomly selected subset
of the data. By randomly sampling the data, it allows the algorithm to handle large
datasets more eﬃciently.
In wind farm layout optimisation, the AEP represents the stochastic value due to its
variability in atmospheric conditions such as the probability density function of wind
speed and wind direction. The deﬁned constraints of the optimisation problem, such as
wind farm boundaries and minimum inter-turbine spacing, are deterministic.
In this study, the SGD optimisation algorithm provided in the wind farm optimisation
tool TOPFARM described in section 4.7 is implemented.

The objective of this section is to evaluate the impact of the number of iterations
and threshold parameters on the AEP and computation time utilising box plots. By
analysing these box plots, we can observe the median, interquartile range (IQR), whiskers,
and outliers [39]. This analysis helps in understanding the trade-oﬀs between output
performance and computational eﬃciency, allowing for informed decisions in the main
investigation cases. First, the number of iterations is examined, followed by the threshold
parameters. It is anticipated that by increasing the number of iterations, the variability in
determining AEP will decrease due to providing the optimisation algorithm with suﬃcient
iterations, which also results in an average increase. However, computation times are
expected to increase with the number of iterations because more iterations require more
processing. For the analysis of the threshold, variation in the AEP calculation is expected
as the optimisation algorithm terminates the simulation, for example, with a 0.1 threshold
in advance but with a 0.02 threshold later. This also provides the reason for longer
computation time.

Recommendations
This chapter encapsulates the key ﬁndings and insights from the multi-objective opti-
misation analysis of wind farm layouts. The implications of incorporating both ﬁxed
and volatile electricity prices into the optimisation process are discussed. Additionally,
recommendations for future research and practical applications are provided. These con-
clusions oﬀer a comprehensive overview of the study’s contributions and suggest potential
directions for ongoing advancements in the ﬁeld of wind farm layout optimisation.
6.1
Conclusion
This research investigated the value-based metric AEV, introduced by Bechmann et al.,
which addresses the need to consider the value of energy produced by wind farms. This
metric is crucial for tackling the challenges posed by volatile electricity prices, arising
from the misalignment of supply and demand, often resulting in negative electricity prices
in markets with a high share of wind energy. The AEV was compared with the AEP
metric in a multi-objective optimisation analysis of wind farm layouts using TOPFARM
with a Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) algorithm.
The primary objective was to enhance the value and revenue generated by wind farms
through optimised layouts based on value-based metrics. The conventional Levelized
Cost of Energy (LCOE) function was substituted with a value-based metric, enabling a
comparative analysis of energy production and value. This was achieved by developing a
method for optimising wind farm layouts that can adapt to the ﬂuctuations of electricity
markets and investigating how various market conditions and price correlations inﬂuence
the optimal conﬁguration of wind farms.
In the Methodology chapter, the AEP, AEV, and electricity price models were deﬁned
to create an optimisation framework for multi-objective analysis. Anticipating various
optimal results, a trade-oﬀbetween production and revenue was considered, implementing
the Pareto approach to determine this trade-oﬀ.
The framework was applied to deﬁne wind resources, electricity prices, wind turbines,
wake models, and conﬁguration parameters for the multi-objective optimisation analysis.
Fluctuating electricity prices were derived from a site in Østerild, Denmark, representing
a country with a signiﬁcant wind energy share of more than 50%. The wind resources for
the optimisation were taken from the Horns Rev 1 oﬀshore site, close to Østerild.
A wind farm consisting of 64 15 MW oﬀshore wind turbines, creating an approximate
capacity of 1 GW, was investigated. To explore the impact of volatile electricity prices,
==PAGE:71==
6
CONCLUSION & FUTURE RECOMMENDATIONS

ﬁxed electricity price scenarios were compared with moderate, high, and extreme volatility
scenarios for both dense and sparse wind farm types. The high volatility scenario represents
conditions in Denmark. Before the main analysis, the optimisation baseline parameters
were deﬁned, exploring the impact of the number of iterations and various thresholds
on the AEP and optimisation time. The analysis proceeded with 200 iterations and a
threshold of 0.1, considering the limitations of the optimisation algorithm.
The ﬁndings revealed that in scenarios with ﬁxed electricity prices, there was no observed
reduction in revenue, reﬂecting conditions in countries with low wind energy shares or
wind farms with ﬁxed-price Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs). However, in scenarios
with volatile electricity prices, signiﬁcant revenue loss was detected due to the negative
correlation between wind energy production and electricity prices. Increased price volatility
led to substantial revenue loss, with value factors „V s dropping to approximately 77% for
dense wind farms and 80% for sparse wind farms, indicating a potential 25% revenue loss.
Regarding wind farm layouts, sparse wind farms generally achieved slightly higher value
factors „V compared to dense wind farms in volatile price scenarios, suggesting that
sparse designs might mitigate some revenue losses, though they still experience signiﬁcant
devaluation. The optimisation algorithm prioritised wake eﬀects over high electricity
prices, indicating that a redesign focusing on wind speed and direction concerning high
electricity prices could enhance revenue despite potential increases in turbine loads.
The current algorithm did not identify a trade-oﬀbetween AEP and AEV, with no Pareto
optimal solutions found. However, extreme volatility scenarios showed tendencies towards
a Pareto front, suggesting room for algorithm improvement. Enhancements could involve
optimising parameters such as iterations, thresholds, learning rates, and inter-turbine
spacing to better explore solution spaces and reduce overﬁtting risks.
Future research is needed to reﬁne the algorithm and incorporate more comprehensive
data, considering wind speed and direction’s impact on electricity prices. This could lead
to clearer distinctions between AEP and AEV solutions, potentially uncovering more
optimal wind farm layouts to maximise revenue. Adjusting the algorithm to prioritise
electricity price optimisation over wake eﬀects may result in diﬀerent, more proﬁtable
wind farm designs.
6.2
Future Recommendations
Several challenges were faced in this research, which limited the study and its outcomes.
To further investigate and explore the objective of this study, the following suggestions
are provided:
1. Reﬁnement of the AEV Metric:
• Conduct further studies to reﬁne and enhance the AEV metric, ensuring it
accurately captures the revenue ﬂuctuations due to electricity price variability
and their impact on wind farm revenue. This may involve the use of historical
data on electricity prices and wind farm revenue for thorough validation.
2. Optimisation Algorithm Enhancements:
==PAGE:72==
6
CONCLUSION & FUTURE RECOMMENDATIONS

• Smart Start Algorithm: Implement a smart start algorithm to provide better
initial solutions, potentially improving the overall eﬃciency and eﬀectiveness
of the optimisation process.
• Increased Iterations: Increase the number of iterations in the optimisation
algorithm to ensure a thorough exploration of the solution space and potentially
uncover more optimal conﬁgurations.
3. Exploration of Electricity Price Dependencies:
• Wind Direction: Explore the dependency of electricity prices on wind direc-
tion to understand how directional variations aﬀect revenue and optimisation
strategies.
• Number of Wind Turbines: Investigate how the number of wind turbines
impacts the revenue and subsequently inﬂuences the optimisation of wind farm
layouts.
4. Real-World Case Studies:
• Implement and validate the proposed optimisation models in real-world case
studies that represent diﬀerent electricity price scenarios.
By addressing these recommendations, future research can build on the ﬁndings of this
study, leading to more eﬃcient, reliable, and economically viable wind energy projects.
These eﬀorts will contribute to the broader goal of integrating renewable energy sources
into the global energy mix and mitigating the challenges posed by climate change.
