Abstract
This thesis uses static and dynamic whole-body positron emission tomography to
map the spine and determine if load-bearing exercises can stimulate an increased
remodelling response and if it is localizable. The data contains subjects with no long-
lasting lower back pain in the last three months. Subjects were scanned two times,
interrupted by a load-bearing side-bending exercise. For each scan, subjects were
injected with the 18F-labelled radiotracer NaF. Low-dosis attenuation-corrected com-
puted tomography acquisitions were used with the TotalSegmentator tool to generate
a segmentation of the spine, which was used to create a regional map, separating an-
terior, posterior, left, right, superior, and inferior parts of the vertebral body from
each other. Standard uptake values were calculated based on the static whole-body
acquisitions from the first and second scans, with the residual tracer concentration
of the first scan subtracted. Kinetic modelling was applied using the weighted least
squares method to obtain the kinetic rate constants.
The load-bearing exercise would only be performed with the weight in one hand
denoted as the active side and the other as passive. SUV measurements indicated an
average increased uptake of 20% for all vertebrae and subregions in the spine and a
significant increase in the difference between the active and passive sides.
Rate constants K1, Ki and Vb showed a significant increase difference for posterior
regions, combined with an insignificant increase difference for SUV measurement;
this indicates a vascular vasodilating response. Rate constants based on only two
subjects showed significant differences from the available literature, suggesting that
larger populations are required for kinetic modelling.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In Appendix A the initial project plan can be found.
1.1
Motivation
Back pain is a common condition experienced by the adult population, taking up
doctor’s appointments and increasing the number of sick days [1].
1.2
Aim/Scope
This thesis sets out to achieve the following milestones:
• Acquire 10-15 PET-CT scans from healthy subjects
• Generate subject-individual regional spine map
• Calculate SUV
• Generate PET tracer kinetics modelling
• Assess the remodelling changes in the spine after load-bearing exercises
• Determine whether regional differences in the spine are present and localizable
The goal is to generate a load mapping of the spine and determine whether it is pos-
sible to detect a response to load-bearing exercises in the spine and if it is localizable.
The project set out to examine whether a regional map can be created based on gen-
eral automatic segmentation and whether the quality of the regions is adequate to be
used in determining local load response in the spine.

Background Material
The following section briefly introduces the background theory of bone, spine anatomy,
and the methods used in this project.
2.1
Bone remodelling
Adult bone tissue appears in two types: cortical (compact) and cancellous (spongy)
bone. The prior makes up 80% of the skeleton, and with a densely packed intercellular
matrix, it acts as an outer shell of the bones. In contrast, the latter found in the
interior of the bones is made up of trabeculae and spicules forming a lattice-like
pattern [2]. The proportions of compact and cancellous bone depend on the bone
type, but it is found that cancellous bone makes up to 80 % of the vertebral body
[3]. The bone is covered by the periosteum, which forms an outer fibrous layer and
an inner layer that contains osteoprogenitor cells.
The bone matrix is maintained by four cells: osteoblasts, which synthesize and secrete
bone components; osteoclasts, which resorb surplus bone by dissolving bone mineral
and extracellular matrix; osteocytes, which make up the osteoid bone tissue; and
osteoprogenitor cells, which serve as precursors to all bone cells except osteoclasts
[4], [5]. The osteocytes, in particular, are of interest in this study as they are known
to be pressure-sensitive and orchestrate the acute response of bone tissue to loading.
This response has recently been shown to include a perfusion increase in the tibia and
knee but has not been studied in other bone tissues [6], [7].
The bone receives blood supply from nutrient or perforating arteries. The nutrient
arteries supply the marrow space and the inner half of the cortex. Perforating arteries
extend inward from the periosteal arteries on the external periosteum surface.
The vertebral column comprises 33 vertebrae separated by intervertebral discs acting
as shock absorbers as shown in Figure 2.1. They are separated into five sub-groups
based on their structural characteristics and location. The cervical vertebrae are in
the neck region numbered C1 to C7, and their smaller size and higher flexibility allow
a great range of motion. Thoracic vertebrae in the mid-back region are numbered
T1 to T12, are larger and more rigid, and are attached to the rib cage, ensuring

upper-body stability and protection of the heart and lungs. Five Lumbar vertebrae,
L1 to L5, are the largest of the vertebrae and are the major load-bearing vertebrae.

2.2
PET
Positron Emission Tomography, abbreviated as PET, is characterized by the sequen-
tial occurrence of positron pairs emission events.
When unstable atoms undergo
decay, particularly β+, it happens because too many protons are present in the atom
nucleus. Either positron decay or electron capture can achieve stability. In the latter,
an electron from one of the inner shells combines with a proton in the nucleus, con-
verting it to a neutron. An outer shell electron will take its place and emit the shell
energy change as an X-ray. In the prior, a proton is converted into a neutron emit-
ting a positively charged electron (positron) together with a neutrino. This positron
will travel a short way through surrounding matter, which will excite and ionize the
==PAGE:22==
6
2 Background Material
atoms. As it loses its kinetic energy, the positron chances of colliding with an electron
increase until it inevitably happens and both are annihilated, combining their mass
and converting it into two opposite-directed photons with equivalent energy to their
original mass [8].
Because of the simultaneous and opposite directed properties of the annihilation pho-
ton pairs, PET detectors need no collimator to determine the original position and
direction of the photons. This dramatically improves the sensitivity of PET
over Single Photon Emission Computed Tomography (SPECT) since these
only absorb photons travelling perpendicular to the collimator. When two
detectors register coincident photons on opposite sides of the annihilation event, an
imaginary line can be drawn between the detector, namely the line of response LOR.
Some uncertainties in detecting and timing registered photons arise, and the following
part will shortly describe them. Sometimes, only single events are registered, which
can happen when either a non-annihilation photon is registered, a photon from a pair
leaves the plane of detection, or the other photon gets absorbed or scattered in the
surrounding medium. These events are rejected. However, there is also the risk that
two-photon pairs are generated simultaneously at separate locations, and one from
each pair leaves the plane of detection, giving the impression that the remaining two
photons are from an actual coincidence event. These are referred to as random events.
Finally, photons can be scattered, altering their path to be registered on an incorrect
detector. These events’ coincidence increases with increasing count rates [8].
Detektor/Crystal
The crystals convert gamma photon energy into light photon energy, which is detected
by the photomultiplier tubes PMT that generate an electrical signal proportional to
how close the PMT is to the site of impact [8]. Different crystal materials have been
tested continuously, improving their scintillation capabilities. A desirable improve-
ment property is a higher light yield and a shorter dead time. The greater the photon
output, the better energy and spatial resolution, which improves the distinguishabil-
ity of low-energy scatter from high-energy annihilation photons. Dead time is the
time needed for the excited crystal atoms to return to the ground state. One detector
block comprises 4 PMTs arranged with 6 × 6 crystals [8].
Time of flight
When the annihilation event occurs off-centre in the detection ring, there will be a
short time difference proportional to the difference in distance travelled. This allows
determining their time of flight TOF and calculating the event’s position along the
LOR. This helps constrain the reconstruction algorithm, improving image quality. [8].
Attenuation Correction
When photons travel through a medium and are subjected to scatter or are absorbed
so that they fail to keep a straight line or reach the detector, they are attenuated.
This is independent of the annihilation position, which enables the possibility to pre-

7
correct for this effect arithmetically using data from transmission scans [8], [9].
Scatter & Random events
Energy thresholding is implemented for the cases where photons have been heavily
scattered compared to their initial 511 keV. For other cases, simulated scatter models
are used to correct for scatter [9]. Since random events are detected from separate an-
nihilation, they only add noise with no spatial information, reducing image contrast
[9]. These can be avoided by either a delayed or coincidence time window [9]. In mod-
ern PET scans, corrections for single, random, and scatter events are quite suﬀicient,
avoiding significant errors in examinations such as those in the present study.
Resolution
Annihilation events close to the periphery of the PET detector ring are prone to
interact with several detectors because of their oblique angle to the detector block.
This is called the parallax error and can be encountered with a larger detector ring.
2.2.1
SUV Uptake
When the scan is finished, and the raw data with all counts of true annihilation
events have been reconstructed, each pixel of the final output image is represented
as Becquerel per millilitre Bq mL−1 effectively the number of decays per second.
The distribution and intensity of tracer uptake give an idea of focal locations with
increased intensity. However, this measure is highly affected by the injected dose and
the relative size of the medium or body it is present in. This gives the introduction
of the Standardized Uptake Value (SUV), which is a widely used PET quantifier
calculated as a ratio of tissue radioactivity concentration at time t and administered
dose at the injection time divided by bodyweight in grams [10]. The equation is as
shown below:

equation above will be [g mL−1], assuming the body has the same density of water at

2.2.2
Compartment Modelling
Compartment modelling is the process of decomposing the physiological system of
dynamic processes into several interacting compartments [12]. The idea is to consider
a chemical in a physical space with the assumption that it is evenly distributed, and if
there is a difference, the compartment will be divided. In general, PET information
content is noisy and inadequate to tell enough about the pharmacokinetics of the
tracer, which supports the use of models with certain assumptions to simplify the
truth [13]. Thus, compartment models are. As stated, the first assumption limits the
model to time variations but not space; secondly, the tracer will be present in the tis-
sue in different states and can travel through these assigned as compartments. These
states can be described linearly in first-order ordinary differential equations (ODE).
The system requires an input drive corresponding to the measured tracer concen-
tration in the blood supply over time [13]. This introduces the three-compartment
(two-tissue) model, with a blood supply compartment, an extracellular tissue com-
partment, and a tracer bound/metabolized compartment in the tissue. The model is
shown in Figure 2.4.
Figure 2.4: Two-tissue compartment model.
The model utilizes samples at times in the dynamic PET acquisition at certain frame
times and uses activity present in the voxels within a region of interest (ROI) over
time this creates a voxel time activity curve (TAC).
The rate constants control the rate at which the radiotracer transfers through the
pathways between the compartments [12]. They can be described as the following:

• K1 is the rate at which the tracer from the blood/plasma is transported into
the first tissue compartment.
• k2 is the rate of transport back to the plasma.
• k3 represents a fraction of tracer bound in the tissue.
• k4 is the fraction of tracer which returns from bound tissue C2 into being free

with larger residuals [19]. The linear regression is as follows:

3.3
TotalSegmentator
Developers
TotalSegmentator is a publically available, easy-to-use whole-body CT segmentation
tool developed by Wasserthal et al. [22]. It is trained to identify 104 anatomical
structures with a test Dice score of 0.943. They created their annotated data by
implementing an iterative workflow of annotating a few patients and then training a
preliminary model to lay the groundwork for annotating and refining the output seg-
mentation. If a given structure had a publicly available existing model, that model
would be used to refine the first segmentation.
They did this for 5, 20, and 100
patients, retraining the model after each iteration of segmentation refinement. The
final refined predictions were used as the final ground truth to train a new model to
reduce bias [22]. The model had a training, validation, and test dataset of 1082, 57,
and 65 patients.
Architecture
The model is based on the nnU-Net model developed by Isensee et al., which continues
the work based on the U-Net [23], [24]. The U-Net architecture has become a bench-
mark in medical image segmentation, and Isensee et al. sought to improve its advan-
tages by presenting a robust and self-adapting framework. With this, Wasserthal et al.
created a generalized model that could predict 104 anatomical structures, where the
framework would automatically configure all hyperparameters based on the dataset
[22].
==PAGE:33==
3.4 Region Extraction

Usage
TotalSegmentator serves its purpose in this project by segmenting the whole-body
ultra-low-dose CT images acquired. The tissues of interest are the aorta and all ver-
tebrae. A specific model segmenting only vertebral bodies is used to easily separate
the vertebral arch from the body of each vertebra. This model is locked by a license
number, which developers can acquire. It is noted that TotalSegmentator mixes neigh-
bouring vertebrae classes occurs in 1% of cases [22]. Since a CT scan is acquired for
pre- and post-scans, the segmentations achieved are subject to similarity evaluation
to ensure the reproducibility and quality of the resulting ROI PET SUV. The tool
has a built-in Python API to be implemented in the final processing pipeline.
Output
The output is set to return all generated tissue masks with unique indexes for each
bone as one single file in the Neuroimaging Informatics Technology Initiative NIfTI-
1-format. For general purposes, a segmentation for all available tissues is made so
they are available for future projects. The tissues of interest are masked out at the
beginning of the processing by their respective label number: vertebrae = 26-50 and
aorta = 52. The vertebral body model returns a single binary NIfTI-1 file with all
vertebral bodies segmented.
3.4
Region Extraction
The goal is to examine whether it’s possible to determine unilateral molecular changes
in the vertebrae of the spine after load-bearing exercises and to determine if such
changes differ regionally within the vertebrae. This requires all the vertebrae along
the spine to be segmented into left and right regions, anterior and posterior regions,
and superior and inferior regions. TotalSegmentator performs the first steps in seg-
menting all vertebrae and their bodies; after that comes creating the subregions in
the vertebrae for pre- and post-exercise scans. The simplest would be to split each
vertebral body along the middle index across all three axes. Still, a different approach
is proposed because of the spine curvature and the varying structural properties. The
first attempt at creating this region map was based on the total spine curvature to
consider lordotic, kyphotic, and possible scoliosis present in the spine. This method
would fit a basis spline (B-spline) through the coordinates of the total spine mask.
The performance depended on the number of knots given to the model fit, and the
degree was kept below three to avoid overfitting. The model fit was suﬀicient on the
coronal axis splitting left and right but had trouble following the curvature on the
sagittal axis splitting anterior and posterior regions. Different parameters were tested
and validated visually, but the performance of the fit would always change once it
was performed on a new subject. It was chosen to go with a much simpler approach
of fitting a linear regression plane through the coordinates of each vertebra, as seen

Discussion
This project hypothesized that load-bearing exercise would induce changes in NaF
kinetics and uptake, primarily related to a vascular response K1. It further hypoth-
esized that there would be regional differences in the response. A significant and
regionally diverse response to exercise was observed in the spine. This study used a
segmentation model and generated regional maps of the spine. Each vertebra was
investigated by sub-regions, and their response to the exercise was evaluated, whether
side-specific loading would be localizable and an increased vascular response was mea-
surable.
5.1
SUV
The SUV measurements were compared to the literature values determined by (Sab-
bah et al.)[11], which indicated significant differences for SUV mean in the cervical
spine and lesser differences in the thoracic and lumbar spine. SUV max Given the
population size of 9 in this project compared to the population of more than 129
in Sabbah et al. and the age average of 41 ± 17 compared to 61 ± 15[11], there is

and post-exercise SUV have the same relationship, as the two scans are performed
shortly after one another and on the same scanner, the found SUV measurements are

Observing SUV in Table 4.1, there is a close-to significant difference of about 3.4 %
between the passive and active regions. The difference in SUV mean observed in the

exercise-induced increases in uptake favoured the weight bearing side (active). The

generally increased uptake and higher increase for active regions gives great promise
to the hypothesis. The inter-subject analysis provides an overview of possible outlying
subjects and insight into inter-subject load response variation, where some subjects
displayed a significantly higher response than others. This could be age-related or
loading stress-related, depending on how well the exercise is performed, which will be

5.3
Regional distribution in the response
Overall, there was an average 20 % post-exercise increase in SUV for all spine re-
gions, indicating a load-bearing exercise-induced response in vertebral bone tissue.
With specific vertebrae in active and anterior regions having more uptake than their
counterpart, this shows a regional differentiation in the exercise-induced response.
Given the structural composition of the spine, the distribution of vertebrae that have
more increased uptake than others raises questions about whether this is related to
their structural properties, location on the spine, or how the load was distributed.
Beginning with the five lumbar and the one sacral vertebrae, with their placement at
the bottom of the spine, their main distinction is load-bearing properties. Given the
exercise is load-bearing of 5 kg, this gives reason to expect a significant difference in
uptake change between active and passive regions. However, this was not the case;
they were very similar in uptake with no distinct difference. As for thoracic verte-
brae, T11 and T10 would mark the placement where the regional differences would be
notable and increase along the remaining thoracic vertebrae. The thoracic vertebrae
are bound to the rib cage, making them rigid and less flexible. In turn, the rib and
spine of the thorax together serve as a crane bearing the weight held by the shoulder.
Finally, the cervical vertebrae are the most flexible but showed a distinct shift in
uptake difference, going from more active regional uptake to passive regional uptake,
even more so for the anterior and posterior regions.
With the expectation that movement friction between the vertebrae will cause an

having a load-bearing nature, a side bend of 5 kg lifted a total of 50 times could be too

weight down the entire spine, applying stress to each vertebra’s upper and lower parts.
Again, considering the curvature, future work could be put into splitting anterior,
posterior, superior, and inferior into four combined regions, e.g. looking at anterior
superior regions in the thoracic vertebrae with the most kyphotic curvature such as

is expected to add measurable changes for superior and inferior regions. However, this
is speculative and only based on the curvature of the spine and not on the load-bearing
properties, but it is still prone to testing.
The shift seen around the cervical vertebrae in which region would have the most

would be the limitation of the scanner FOV, since not all acquisitions were taken above
the shoulders. This would limit sub-region data, thus making the measurements more
uncertain, also indicated by the significant differences between the atlas literature
values and the measured SUV [11]. However, the increased posterior response could
also have a mechanical explanation in which there is a natural response to keep the
head lifted and backward when bending downwards. It is speculated this response
could have an effect, but the missing regions serve as a more valid explanation for
now.

5.4
Load-bearing exercise mechanics
The side-bending exercise was estimated to be simple, with enough strain to induce
a suﬀicient load response on the bending side. The SUV results showed this with
an average increase of 20 %, which confirms the expectations. However, with all the
moveable vertebrae in the spine, this introduces many degrees of freedom, limiting
the precision of modelling the loading. This margin of uncertainty is amplified by
inter-subject differences in upper body posture while performing the exercise. Some
might be more bent forward while others backwards; some have higher flexibility and
can bend lower, such as young subjects, compared to other more rigid backs as for
older subjects. Future considerations could include three weights to evaluate and map
differences in response related to load; however, this would require sharply regulated
movement and more subjects to ensure comparability between subject responses.
A great addition to this project would be to video record the exercise and generate
a biomechanical finite element model (FEM) using an open-source tool named Open-
Cap [28]. This would relate the regional uptake measurements to a physical model
and combine physical stress points with actual uptake measurements, resulting in
a complete load mapping of the spine. However, the increased degrees of freedom
and non-limited spine motion render it challenging to achieve a suﬀiciently accurate
biomechanical model. More work is required in the field, as stated in the literature re-
view by (Naoum et al.)[29], in which this project hopefully will serve as a motivational

Limitations
Limitations had to be made throughout the project, altering the scope and detail;
this short section will list these limitations and how they have been accounted for.
Initially, 12 scans were planned within the project’s timeframe; three of these had

This leaves nine usable subjects for data processing. Each subject has dynamic and
static acquisitions from the lower back and the spine depending on which scanner,
and a static WB acquisition for all of them. SUV was possible to determine on all
subjects, leaving these results the most reliable; however, regarding kinetic modelling,

challenges resulted in only two usable subjects for kinetic modelling, which eternally
reduced the results’ validity and shifted the main focus to SUV .

Conlusion
This project aimed to investigate the effects of load-bearing exercise on the human
spine by using SUV measurements and kinetic modelling to determine if it would in-
duce an increased and localized response. This was achieved through scan acquisitions
performed on two scanners using the radiotracer NaF and a third-party segmentation
tool to create regional maps, which served as mapping of the load distribution in
the spine. The SUV measurements were corrected for residual tracer concentration,
and the tracer constants were determined through the weighted least squares method.
The measured SUV was significantly different from the available literature values,
based on a much larger older population than this project, indicating some uncer-
tainty. Rate constants differed considerably from the available literature values; yet
again, based on two subjects, this project shall continue to examine the spine’s load
response.
Results showed a positive response for all subjects and all vertebrae. SUV measure-
ments indicated patterns of increased activity in activity-stimulated regions, specifi-
cally regional in the thoracic vertebrae. Increases in K1, Ki, and Vb were significant
for posterior regions, compared to insignificant changes in SUV back measurements,
indicating a vascular vasodilating response.
The uncertainties following the load-bearing are expected to express a significant error
margin for future work. Thus, biomechanical modelling is expected to be added as
a complementary addition to the mapping of the spine’s load response. Future work
should also focus on developing a robust regional map, with intervertebral discs and
facet joints as additional sub-regions, to improve load-mapping of the spine. This
would shed light on essential aspects of the spine, which is expected to respond to
load-bearing exercises. Expanding the subject population to contain more dynamic
acquisitions is expected to improve the significance of the difference measured in the
rate constants.
This project shed preliminary light on the spine’s exercise-induced load response by
using static and dynamic hybrid PET-CT acquisitions. The results presented in this
project shall serve as preliminary until the regional mapping is improved and the
population size is increased.
