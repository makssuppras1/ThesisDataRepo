Abstract
Grammar-based compression consists in replacing a long string by a small context-free
grammar that generates that string. In this thesis, we present our study on Re-Pair
grammar-based compression that allows random access to any character without de-
compressing the string. Let S be a string of length N compressed with Re-Pair into a
ﬁnal sequence of m symbols and n grammar rules. We show the experimental results
of our solutions to the random access problem which achieve either O(n) time and
O(m log n) + O(n log n) space or O( n log n
b
) time and O(m log n) + O(r0(1 + 1/b) + nb)
space where r0 is the total number of bits required if we represent every symbol ri of the
grammar rules with ⌊log ri⌋+ 1 bits. We compare our solutions with gzip and we con-
clude that, for several scenarios, we can perform random access on the grammar-based
compressed string faster than decompressing the gzipp-ed data and then accessing di-
rectly the original text at the cost of ∼1 bits/char more than the compression rate
obtained by gzip.
Keywords: Grammar-based compression, Re-Pair, Random access

Chapter 1
Introduction
Data stored in modern database systems, e.g. biological data and World-Wide Web
data, is usually huge and saved in a compressed format [16]. No features in a database
oﬀer as many direct beneﬁts as data compression and fast answers to the queries.
Achieving these two features is challenging and requires answers to questions such:
how to compress the data eﬃciently in term of compression rate and how to enable
fast access on the data. There are practical cases in which, the typical approach of
decompressing the data and then performing the access is unacceptable for at least
two reasons: 1) the size of the uncompressed data represented into memory can be up
to an order of magnitude bigger than the compressed form [16], 2) the decompression
of the data is too time consuming.
We consider these challenges in the context of grammar-based compression, where
we can replace a long string S of length N with a small context-free grammar G
composed of n production rules [16]. We then consider the random access problem,
where we want to provide fast random access to any of the 1 ≤i ≤N characters of
S by directly accessing the compressed data G.

1.1
Related work
Given a string S with length N, we can solve the random access problem in the naive
way using O(N) space and accessing any character in constant time. Alternatively, we
can use a grammar-based compression algorithm to compute n grammar rules (where
n ≪N) that generate the string S. This, achieves O(n) space and allows us to
perform a top-down access on the grammars rules in O(h) time, where h is the height
of the parse tree [16]. Note that, the worst case for a top-down access is O(n), that
is the case when we have to access all the rules. Any SLP of size n can be converted
into a SLP having parse tree with height O(log N) [11] at a cost of increasing the
space to O(n log N). Claude and Navarro [5] reduced the space from O(n log N) bits
to O(n log n) + n log N bits at the cost of increasing the time to O(h log n). Bille at
al. [16] achieved O(log N) time and O(n) space and pre-processing time on the RAM
model (near-linear on the pointer machine model) [16].
1.2
Roadmap
The rest of the thesis is organised as follows. Chapter 1 continues with some basic
concepts and notations regarding text compression.
Chapter 2 provides a formal description of Grammar-based coding and shows some
examples of its use of representing long strings with small grammars.
In Chapter 3 we describe the Re-Pair algorithm, in particular the time complexity
and the data structures used and we show examples of the grammar rules produced
by the algorithm.
Chapter 4 begins with the description of the random access problem. We then
discuss how to perform random access on compressed string produced by Re-Pair and
we describe diﬀerent solutions with their time/space complexity.
In Chapter 5 we explain the problem of enabling direct access to a sequence of
encoded symbols and why solving this problem can reduce the compression rate of a
Re-Pair Grammar-based compressed string. In particular, we describe Wavelet Trees
and Directly Addressable Codes (DACs).
Chapter 6 describes the experimental results of our solutions. We show the com-
pression rate and the random access time for diﬀerent solutions and we compare our
best solutions with the gzip/gunzip tools.
The conclusion follows in Chapter 7. We discuss our main results and we list some
ideas of future works.

1.3
Text compression
In the modern era of computer technology we are used to a continuous increase in
storage and transmission capacity. Despite this, we want to avoid waste of disk space
and the challenges posed by the growing volumes of “big data” require to continuously
look for new ideas and solutions able to solve the problem of representing information
eﬃciently.
The goal of data compression is to represent a given data using as little space as
possible: the new compressed data should take less space than the original and we
should be able to reconstruct the original data from the compressed one.

We can divide the data compression techniques in two main families, lossless and
lossy compression: while in the former we are interested in the exact reconstruction
of the original data, in the latter we can tolerate small changes or a noise in the
reconstructed data. Usually, in the speciﬁc ﬁeld of text compression we are interested
in lossless compression techniques.
In 1948, Claude E. Shannon formulated a basic theory for data compression and
established a fundamental limit to lossless data compression, called entropy: the av-
erage amount of information required to represent any symbol of a message. Shannon
proved that the entropy value depends on the statistical nature of the source and that
it is mathematically impossible to achieve better compression rate than the entropy
[4].
In the following section we deﬁne the zero-order entropy of a string S as well as
higher-order entropy. The following deﬁnitions will be used especially in chapter 5.

Chapter 2
Grammar based codes
Grammars are widely used in computer science and engineering: typical applications
are compiler designs, speech recognition, image understanding, language modeling
and lossless data compression [10]. Since the topic of the thesis is accessing grammar
compressed strings, we are interested in grammar-based coding in the ﬁeld of lossless
data compression. The idea of grammar based compression is straightforward: given
a long string S of length N over an alphabet Σ = [1, σ], replace it by a small grammar
G which generates S.
2.1
Context-free grammars
We are interested in context-free grammars (CFGs) which can be deﬁned as a quadru-
ple G = (V, T, P, S0) such that
• V is a ﬁnite no-empty set of variables
• T is a ﬁnite no-empty set of terminal symbols
• P is a ﬁnite set of productions rules where left members are from V and right
members are from (V ∪T)∗
• S0 ∈V , it’s called the start symbol
As example the following production rules
S0 →BBA

B →aA
produce the string abaababa. The production rules can be also called grammar
rules and in this thesis we will often use the second term.

Chapter 3
Re-Pair
Re-Pair - for recursive pairing - is a dictionary-based compression algorithm developed
by Larsson and Moﬀat [15] in 2000. The compression scheme has characteristics which
make Re-Pair a good choice when compressed data has to be accessed directly. The
ﬁnal output produced by the algorithm is composed of the ﬁnal compressed message
and a set of hierarchical grammar rules - also called dictionary of phrases.
The
approach used by Larsson and Moﬀat is oﬀ-line, meaning that the full message (or a
large block of it) is used to infer the complete dictionary: intuitively, the advantage
of oﬀ-line approach is that, being possible to access to the entire message, it should
be possible to optimize the choice of phrases to maximize the compression rate [15].
The goal of dictionary based modelling is to derive a set of phrases such that
replacing the occurrences of these phrases with references to the rule producing them
decreases the length of the message.
3.1
Algorithm
The idea of Re-Pair compression algorithm is straightforward and consists in replacing
all the occurrences of the most frequent pair of symbols in the message with a new
symbol until there is no pair which occurs at least twice (see Algorithm 1).
while there is a pair of symbols occurring at least twice do
1. Identify symbols a and b such that ab is a the most frequent pair of
adjacent symbols in the message;
2. Introduce new symbol A and replace all occurrences of ab with A.
end

We distinguish between the general term symbol as new symbol introduced by
the algorithm as well as an input character, while we restrict the use of the term

3.1.1
Best compression achievable
The best case complexity of compression achievable by Re-Pair is O(log N), meaning
that a string S of length N can be compressed up to an order of the logarithm of its
length. Comparing with other popular compression techniques, we have LZ77 with
self-referencing having O(1) best case complexity and LZ77 without self-referencing
O(log N) [1].

a string composed by the same character repeated x times. After the ith pair replace-
ment, the string is reduced to a new string with length N

3.2
Data structures
Our implementation is based on the data structures described in [15]. The three main
data structures used during the pairs replacement process are the following:
1. An array of symbols, initially containing the input message. Each element of
the array contains three ﬁelds: two pointers and a symbol.
2. An hash table storing the pairs still under consideration for replacement and
containing a pointer to an element of a priority queue.
3. A priority queue, implemented as array of ∼
√
N linked lists, where the ith
linked list stores the information about the pairs occurring at most i times and
the ﬁnal list records the more frequent pairs. Each node of a linked list stores
a pointer to the ﬁrst occurrence of the pair in the sequence and holds a counter
indicating the number of occurrences of that pair.

The two pointers of each element in the array of symbols are used to link elements
together and in combination with the hash table we can access all the positions of
the array that hold a given pair.
Algorithm 2 describes more in detail the operations performed by Re-Pair on the
three data structures.
1. Locate the ﬁrst or next sequence entry associated with ab. Identify the
adjacent symbols x and y to establish the context xaby;
2. Decrement the counts of the adjacent pairs xa and by. If any of the pairs
reaches a count of one, delete its priority queue record;
3. Replace ab in the sequence, leaving xAy;
4. Increase the counts of the pairs xA and Ay.

Chapter 5
Encoding Re-Pair
A crucial point of the Re-Pair algorithm is the encoding of the ﬁnal sequence of
symbols as well as the grammar rules produced by the pair replacement operations.
The solutions described in chapter 4 are based on ﬁxed-length coding where each
symbol of a sequence over an alphabet Σ = [1, σ] is represented using a ﬁxed number
of log σ bits. Since we are interested in improving the compression rate we want to
use other existing solutions which outperform the ﬁxed-length coding technique.
Variable-length encoding is the heart of data compression [14][9] and is based on
statistical compression methods which assign a shorter codewords to more frequent
symbols. There are several encoding techniques, based on variable-length encoding,
which can be applied on the output of Re-Pair and some of them are described in
[15].
The bad news is that using typical encoding techniques, such the one described
in [15], doesn’t properly ﬁt our scope of performing an eﬃcient random access on
compressed data. If we encode the ﬁnal sequence s with a common variable-length
encoder and we want to access the ith symbol of the encoded sequence, we have
to decode all the previous i-1 symbols preceding it, which makes random access
extremely slow.
For this reason, we want to encode the output of Re-Pair enabling fast direct
access to any element of the encoded data. In the following sections we describe three
possible solutions.
5.1
Sparse sampling
Sparse sampling [14] is the classical solution to the problem of enabling direct access
to an encoded sequence of symbols.
19
==PAGE:31==
The idea is to sample the sequence storing the absolute pointers to each hth
sampled elements of the sequence. Accessing the (h + d)th element, for 0 ≤d < h,
can be performed decoding d codewords starting from the hth sample.
Given an encoded sequence of n symbols and length N bits, sparse sampling
achieves O(N) + ⌈n
h⌉⌈log N⌉bits and O(h) access time, assuming we can decode any
symbol in constant time.
5.2
Wavelet trees
A wavelet tree[13][9][7] is a data structure designed to represent a sequence of symbols
S = s1s2...sn over an alphabet Σ = [1, σ] and answer eﬃciently to some basic queries:
• access(S, i) returns si.
• ranka(S, i) returns the number of occurrences of the symbol a in S[1, i].
• selecta(S, j) returns the position in S of the jth occurrence of symbol a.
The wavelet tree has log σ levels and at each level i the symbols of the alphabet
are partitioned in two subsets which are contained respectively in the left and right
child of the next level i + 1. The wavelet tree is a balanced binary tree with σ leaves
and σ−1 internal nodes each of which holds a bitmap. Since there are log σ levels and
each of them stores bitmaps having at most n bits, the worst case space complexity
of the data structure is O(n log σ).
Figure 5.1: A wavelet tree constructed for abracadabra (Wikipedia)
Note that the O(n log σ) space complexity can be obtained if we remove O(σ log n)
bits for the extra pointers needed for the topology of the tree. As explained in [13],
this can be done by grouping all the leaves to the left and considering that all the
20

bitmaps at all the levels belong to consecutive nodes and they can all be concatenated
into one large bitmap.
In addition of the space required to represent the tree, each of the bitmaps needs
extra space for supporting rank and select operations. There are several solutions for
supporting rank and select operations on bitmaps [13][7][12] which requires o(n) bits
of extra information to be stored.
Accessing si can be done with log σ rank operations on the bitmaps of each level

Chapter 7
Conclusion and future work
Given a string S of length N we have shown how to perform random access on any
character of S by accessing the grammar-based compressed string. In particular, we
have studied the performance of diﬀerent grammar-based solutions using the Re-Pair
algorithm which compresses a string S into n grammar rules and a ﬁnal sequence of
m symbols. We have shown the experimental results of our solutions to the random
access problem which achieve either O(n) time and O(m log n) + O(n log n) space or
O(n log n
b
) time and O(m log n)+O(r0(1+1/b)+nb) space where r0 is the total number
of bits required if we represent every symbol ri of the grammar rules with ⌊log ri⌋+ 1
bits. We have compared our solutions with gzip and we have concluded that, for
several scenarios, we can perform random access on the grammar-based compressed
string faster than decompressing the gzipp-ed data and then accessing directly the
original text.
For example, we have compressed DNA sequences of size 100MB into ∼37MB and
we were able to access up to either 100KB of data, ∼200 milliseconds (28%) faster
than the gzip-gunzip solution or 300KB of data, ∼100 milliseconds (16%) faster or
400KB of data, ∼50 milliseconds (8%) faster. We have also compressed English text
of size 100MB into ∼40MB and we were able to access up to 100KB of data, ∼50
milliseconds (8%) faster than gzip-gunzip.
We have shown that our repair rc solution requires ∼1 bits/char more than the
compression rate obtained by gzip. We have encoded the symbols of the grammar rules
with the DACs technique which have further decreased the compression rate up to
∼0.15 bits/char and we have encoded both the ﬁnal sequence and the grammar rules
with DACs which have further decreased the compression rate up to ∼0.25 bits/char.
40
==PAGE:52==
We have shown the trade oﬀbetween the compression rate and the random access
time. In particular, we have shown that applying encoding techniques such DACs
manages to improve the compression rate but slows down signiﬁcantly (2-3 times)
the random access time.
In general, our solutions have achieved better results in terms of random access
problem for DNA sequences in comparison to English text.
Our solutions could have practical application on huge data (Gigabytes or Ter-
abytes) from where we only need to retrieve a small portion at a time (Kilobytes or
few Megabytes), e.g. biological data as Genome databases, which store thousands of
sequences and in the case where we only need to retrieve few sequences at a time.
There are several ideas for future work on this topic:
1. Apply techniques such [8][16] to provide access to substrings of length m on the
grammar-based compressed string faster than the naive solution of performing
m random access queries.
2. Combine our results with fast algorithms for uncompressed approximate string
matching and study the performance of approximate string matching on grammar-
based compressed strings without need of decompression [16].
3. Investigate the performance using alternative grammar-based compression al-
gorithms. Grammars such SLPs capture many popular compression schemes
including the LempelZiv family and Sequitur [16].
