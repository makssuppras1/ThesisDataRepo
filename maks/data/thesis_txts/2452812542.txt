Abstract
Among Renewable Energy Sources, wind turbines are currently the most advanced technol-
ogy able to supply a large share of the electricity demand of the global market. Although
wind turbines contribute to the global electricity production in a ”green” way (especially
in Denmark), many people are still adverse to them, and complain when a wind turbine
installation is planned nearby their house. One of the main reasons of complaint is the
noise made by the turbines.
DecoWind is a three-year Danish project with the main purpose of investigating whether
the noise emitted from wind turbines can be reduced in a smarter way, so that it would be
less troublesome for the neighbors. This thesis provides a contribute do the DecoWind
project, by accomplishing one of its planned tasks. In particular, this work is focused on
the measurements and on the analysis of the data obtained during the ﬁrst measurement
campaign near the city of Drantum (DK). The goal is to estimate the acoustic ground
impedance of at least two types of ground placed between a wind turbine located in the
ﬁelds nearby and the city dwellings.
To carry out the measurements of the acoustic ground impedance, a ”template” method
called NordTest method (NT ACOU 104) was adopted. Diﬀerent measurement tests have
been performed over four diﬀerent types of ground. Then, all the recorded data have been
analyzed and discussed in this document.
Finally, the acoustic ground impedances of two grounds were determined through an
indirect method. In the end, from the analysis of the results obtained from the tests
carried out during this work, some relevant conclusions about the NordTest method have
been drawn.

Introduction
1.1
Present and future of Wind Energy generation
Over the past few years, climate change has become a crucial and widely discussed topic.
The need to cut down CO2 emissions in the environment has to be balanced with the
ever-increasing global demand of electricity[1].
Renewable Energy Sources (RES) seems to be the best and most practical solution to
cope with this issue, especially thanks to the constant improvement of knowledge and
technology in the last two decades. Among RES, wind turbines are currently the most
advanced technology able to supply a large share of the electricity demand of the global
market. As a matter of fact, Figure 1 shows the cumulative wind power installations in
Europe for the last ten years. In particular, the image describes how much wind energy
power generation grew when compared to other energy sources: just last year (2018), wind
energy reached a production peak of 11.7 [GW] (14% of EU electricity demand) [2].
This rapid growth in the wind energy sector is greatly inﬂuenced by the EU vision for
the future of green energy production. In fact, the European Wind Energy Association
(EWEA) has estimated that around 230 [GW] of wind capacity will be installed in Europe
by the end of 2020, which would produce between 15 to 17% of the EU’s electricity demand,
avoiding 333 million tonnes of CO2 emissions per year and saving e28 billion a year in
fuel costs [3, 4].
Figure 1: Total power generation capacity in the European Union 2008-2018 [2]
Since the 1970s, Denmark has always been one of the pioneer countries in developing
commercial wind power. This allowed it to reach the world record in percentage of wind
energy generation over total energy production in 2015 [5]. This record was surpassed in
2017, when Danish wind turbines were able to deliver power equivalent to 43.6 percent of
Denmark’s electricity production [6] as shown in Figure 2.
The Danish state had identiﬁed huge potential expansions in wind energy since several
years ago. As a point of fact, in 2012 the Danish government had agreed a new set of
1
==PAGE:14==
goals designed to wean the country oﬀoil and gas. The plan proposed to see Denmark cut
its greenhouse gas emissions by 34% and to increase the share of electricity production
from wind to 50% by 2020 [7].
Figure 2: Electro-production from wind energy in Denmark 2005-2017 [6]
Although wind turbines deliver a steadily increasing share of the Danish electricity supply,
that does not mean that the number of turbines increases. On the opposite, nowadays
there are about 20% fewer wind turbines in Denmark than in 2001, when the number of
wind turbines peaked [8].
The reason behind is that wind turbines have become bigger and more eﬃcient due to the
constantly improvements in design and technology in wind energy sector. Hence, replacing
old wind turbines with newer, more eﬃcient ones, allowed to reduce the number of turbines
per occupied area, without decreasing the wind energy production.
1.2
Noise of wind turbines
Although wind turbines contribute to the global electricity production in a ”green” way
(especially in Denmark), many people are still susceptible and complain when a wind
turbine is supposed to be installed nearby their house. The main reasons for complaint
are noise [9] and visual impact [10, 11, 12] of wind turbines. The fact that turbines’ size
has increased considerably in recent years does not help with regard to these issues. In
Europe, which is more densely populated than many non-EU countries, these two topics
are particularly concerning for public acceptance of wind energy.
2
==PAGE:15==
Psychological factors can have an inﬂuence on the acoustical and visual perception of
wind turbines, and to the attitude towards wind energy in general. Some people are
more susceptible than others depending on physical health, personality, mood, etc. The
particular kind of noise emitted from wind turbines has been the cause of some reported
incidents, where citizens complained of the noise being an ”annoyance” or ”nuisance”
[13, 14]. This is due to the fact that wind turbine’s noise is diﬀerent from other noise sources
[15], especially for its sudden changes in the sound propagation and in low-frequencies due
to the turbulence in the wind itself.
In order to control general noise pollution, most countries introduced regulations that
deﬁne upper bounds for the noise to which people may be exposed. These limits depend
on the areas they interest and are diﬀerent between daytime and nighttime. In Denmark,
the Danish Environmental Protection Agency has published recommended noise limits for
most types of environmental noise. As a general rule, the recommended noise limits are
based on the perception of noise, as evaluated by interviewing large groups of people. In
particular, for what concern wind turbines, the legislation issues requirements regarding
the minimum distance between turbines and dwellings, and noise limits [16].
At the moment, the latest normative published by the Statutory Order [17] is described
in the IEC 61400-11 standard [18], which declares that the total noise load from wind
turbines must not exceed the following limit values:
• at the noisiest point in outdoor living areas no more than 15 meters from residential
area in the open country:
– 44 dB (A) at a wind speed of 8 [m/s]
– 42 dB (A) at a wind speed of 6 [m/s]
• at the noisiest point in areas for noise-sensitive land use:
– 39 dB (A) at a wind speed of 8 [m/s]
– 37 dB (A) at a wind speed of 6 [m/s]
The last update to the ”Støjbekendtgørelse” (translated: ”Noise ordinance”) document,
regarding the regulation of the sound emitted by the wind turbines, dates back to December
2015. However, considering the increasingly rapid development of wind energy, the Danish
country is keen to keep up with these improvements: for this reason, the DecoWind project
was born.
1.3
DecoWind project
DecoWind is a three-year Danish project managed by Technical University of Denmark
(DTU), Department of Wind Energy in collaboration with Siemens-Gamesa Renewable
Energy (SGRE), EMD International A/S and Force Technology (acting under the brand
of DELTA).
The main purpose of this project is to investigate whether one can reduce the noise emitted
from wind turbines in a smarter way, so that the noise will hardly be so troublesome for
the neighbors. These noise reduction improvements could also have positive repercussions
3
==PAGE:16==
on the overall production of energy [19]: quieter wind turbines can be placed closer to
residential centers, thereby increasing the potential area of installation and thus the number
of turbines. A larger wind farm will then produce more electricity, while keeping noise
production within acceptable limits.
A secondary goal of this project is to look more closely at the modelization of noise
produced by wind turbines, to validate currently available noise models, and possibly to
improve on them.
In this project, the involved members from DTU will also try to evaluate in detail the
noise perceived by people nearby a wind farm in order to update the current regulations.
This will allow an optimization of the distribution of wind turbines over Denmark, without
increasing the annoyance of the neighbors.
As previously mentioned, DecoWind is a 3-year project. During this period, the partners
involved planned to measure noise emission at diﬀerent sites. Since the project focuses
on noise propagation of wind turbines, all the sites were nearby wind turbine or on wind
farm areas. The ﬁrst measurement campaign (performed in the ﬁrst semester of 2019)
was designed to be close to a wind turbine located in the ﬁelds around Husumvej 11, in
Drantum (DK).
In general, to measure a source’s noise emissions, one or more microphones at diﬀerent
locations are employed as receivers: the microphones are all connected to a central data
acquisition unit via cables. The collected data is then analyzed to obtain a measure of noise.
To accomplish this task, the project partners outlined the locations of the microphones
and suggestions for cable-ways on the map below Figure 3.
Figure 3: Drantum site, microphones position and cable-way suggestion
On the map, power cables are marked in yellow, while the microphone signal cables are
marked in blue. The green dots represent the two data acquisition units (DAQ1 and
4
==PAGE:17==
DAQ2), placed in a pallet box. Mic 1, Mic 2 and Mic 3 are microphones placed on
the ground on top of a round wooden plate. According to IEC 61400-11, these three
microphones should be placed around the wind turbine at a distance of 180 [m] (white
line in Figure 3), corresponding to the sum of the height of the tower (109 [m]) and the
radius of the rotor (71 [m]) of the wind turbine. The remaining microphones are mounted
on a tripod, approximately 1.5 [m] above the ground and secured with pegs. They were
used to measure the long-range noise propagation.
Since the period granted for this thesis (ﬁve months) is much shorter than the period
designated for the DecoWind project (three years), it is obvious that the candidate could
not participate to all the campaigns deﬁned in the project. Therefore, the object of this
thesis will be oriented on the measurements and on the analysis of the data of the ﬁrst
campaign in Drantum.
1.4
Noise measurements
The DecoWind project describes three main measurement tasks to be performed at the
ﬁrst location. They are:
1. Flow and wake measurements: they measure inﬂow, humidity, heat ﬂux and
wake ﬂows in a wind farm with a cluster of wind turbines, under diﬀerent operations.
These measurements should be carried out using LIDAR, Sonics and Met-mast.
2. Noise measurements: under the same conditions as the previous task, these
measurements are aimed at the noise emitted by the cluster of wind turbines, as
well as the acoustic impedance between the turbines and nearby dwellings. These
measurements should be carried out using microphones placed around turbines and

Background Theory
Before explaining the methodology used in this project,some acoustic theoretical deﬁnitions
are introduced for the reader. This background theory will be useful in order to better
understand the next chapters.
First of all, when talking about acoustics, it’s necessary to understand what sound is, and
how it is represented in physics.
2.1
Sound wave
2.1.1
Deﬁnition
In 1960, the American National Standards Institute (ANSI) (the current American National
Standard on Acoustical Terminology), deﬁned sound as ”(a) Oscillation in pressure, stress,
particle displacement, particle velocity, etc., propagated in a medium with internal forces
(e.g., elastic or viscous), or the superposition of such propagated oscillation. (b) Auditory
sensation evoked by the oscillation described in (a).” [20]. Hence, sound is a sequence
of waves of pressure which propagates through compressible media such as air or water
(sound can also propagate through solids, but there are additional modes of propagation)
[21]. During their propagation, waves can be reﬂected, refracted, or attenuated by the
medium. In general, unwanted sound is called noise.

2.2
Sound propagation
2.2.1
Properties
As previously mentioned, to be transmitted from a sound source to a receiver, a sound
wave requires a medium through which to propagate. As clearly described in [21] and in
[23], all media have three main properties which aﬀect the behavior of sound propagation.
1. A complex relationship between density and pressure. This relationship, aﬀected by
temperature, determines the speed of sound within the medium.
7
==PAGE:20==
2. The motion of the medium itself. Independently from the motion of sound through
the medium, if the medium is moving (for example due to wind), this movement
may increase or decrease the absolute speed of the sound wave depending on the
direction of the movement.
3. The viscosity of the medium. This determines the rate at which sound is attenuated.
For some media, such as air or water, attenuation due to viscosity tends to be
negligible.

To analyze a sound source, the frequency spectrum of the sound source has to be determined.

The frequency range is divided into several bands. The sound level is determined for each

3
Acoustic Ground Impedance
3.1
Introduction
In outdoor conditions, sound propagation is mainly aﬀected by three factors: geometrical
spreading, atmospheric condition and excess attenuation [33]. First and foremost, the
propagation path of sound waves could be physically inﬂuenced by geometric obstacles,
either natural or artiﬁcial, that act as a barrier and impede the regular sound propagation.
Secondly, the atmosphere is generally not homogeneous and several parameters could
inﬂuence the outdoor sound propagation: temperature, wind speed, turbulence, humidity,
density. Lastly, sound waves interact with the ground over which they pass. These factors
weigh diﬀerently on the sound level, proportionally to the distance between sound source
and receiver [34].
In long-range propagation, i.e. for distances of around one kilometer or more, the factor
that aﬀects the most outdoor sound propagation is the atmospheric condition. Instead, for
short-range propagation (distances of one hundred metres or below), sound propagation
is mainly inﬂuenced by ground eﬀects. The acoustical properties of the ground surface
play an important role on the total attenuation of the sound propagation especially for
low frequencies. Indeed, a suﬃcient acoustic descriptor of those properties is the complex
surface normal impedance, which depends on frequency [35].
Since this project focuses on the acoustic impedance of ground surfaces and its measuring
methods, long-range propagation and atmospheric attenuation will not be taken into
account.
In most cases, if we consider a sound source and an observer positioned at a close distance,
the sound pressure signal emitted by the sound source expands spherically. Therefore, the
receiver will perceive a complex sound signal composed of a direct sound wave and a sound
wave reﬂected by the surface of the ground. This ground reﬂection is strongly dependent,
in magnitude, phase, and shape, on the real and imaginary parts of the acoustic impedance
of the ground [36], especially at oblique angles of incidence. The acoustic impedance is
not simply related to just the material composition of the ground; rather, it describes the
complex interplay between all of the ground characteristics and the propagation of the
sound waves that interact with it.
In order to measure the acoustic ground impedance, various methods can be used. The
diﬀerent measurement procedures can be grouped in two categories: direct and indirect
methods, described in subsection 3.2 and subsection 3.3. In addition to those, a theoretical
method could also be used to calculate the acoustic ground impedance analytically: this
method is described in subsubsection 2.2.4.
3.2
Direct methods
All the measurement techniques that use the deﬁnition of impedance directly, without the
need for a propagation model, are included in the direct method category. These speciﬁc
procedures are mostly restricted to laboratory environments, since they either require that
samples be cut from the material under investigation, or use an experimental set-up or
facilities which can’t be easily used outside of a lab.
15
==PAGE:28==
3.2.1
Kundt’s tube
Most of these techniques refer to the oldest and longest used procedure for measuring
reﬂection coeﬃcients of material samples: the Kundt’s tube. Along with it, another
common measurement method used in laboratory is the impedance tube. Both methods,
used for the determination of the acoustic properties of a material, make use of a stationary
wave tube. This is because, in a tube, the mathematical model for sound propagation
becomes one-dimensional (for a ﬁxed bandwidth): sound waves can only propagate in one
direction along the tube. This makes the experimental set-up relatively simple and small.
Figure 10: Schematic representation of two measurement techniques in an impedance tube.
In Figure 10 Kundt’s tube is shown on the left, while the impedance tube is shown on
the right. In both cases, a loudspeaker emits a sound wave (ˆpB) that travels forward and,
after passing through the material of interest, is reﬂected on the backing plate placed at
the end of the tube. The reﬂected sound wave (ˆpA) can be measured separately through
the movable microphone (for Kundt’s tube) or the two microphones placed in the center
of the tube (for the impedance tube). Thus, heeding certain constraints, the reﬂection
coeﬃcient of the test sample at the end of the tube can be determined.
Although these methods are simple and eﬀective, they are too inaccurate for in-situ
acoustic impedance measurements [34, 37]. Hence, they will not be discussed in this thesis.
3.3
Indirect Methods
Due to the limitations of direct methods explained before, it is not always easy to directly
measure the acoustic impedance of the ground. A more suitable solution seems to be the
use of a simpliﬁed modelization of acoustic propagation above a surface of given impedance
[38]. The ground impedance can be obtained by ﬁtting the model using the value of some
other, easier to measure, parameter.
All the methods that measure the ground impedance using outdoor sound propagation
models are grouped in the indirect methods category. These measurement techniques
exploit multiple parameters that characterize the ground impedance, which in turn aﬀect
sound propagation.
In this way, the models allow to predict the attenuation of the
sound produced at a given point, which can then be compared with the experimental
value obtained during the measurement at that speciﬁc point. The ground impedance is
then expressed as a function of frequency and other soil parameters via diﬀerent existing
propagation models. These impedance models diﬀer according to the number of parameters
required.
16
==PAGE:29==
All indirect methods can be used to estimate ground characteristics over a vast region,
compared to the limited scale of laboratory experiments. The impedance results will be
an average of the impedance of all the components of the wide region considered. As can
be expected, the inconvenience of these methods is that the impedance of the ground
calculated through impedance models is an approximation. Among the existing models,
though, some yield a better ﬁt to the experimental data, depending on which factors were
taken into account, and on how accurate the initial hypotheses are with regard to the real
measurement conditions [37].
Although a one-parameter semi-empirical model (see subsubsection 3.3.1) is the most
common model and it has been used widely for outdoor sound prediction, there are also
many other impedance models for the acoustical properties of ground surface impedance.
In [39], 14 of these impedance models are described and compared. Since some of these
models require values or estimates of many parameters, which are typically unknown
for outdoor ground surfaces, only the most important models are chosen for further
investigation. A more elaborated overview can be found in [31, 34, 39].

4
NordTest Method
4.1
Description
The most widely used ground impedance measurement methods are ”template methods”:
they compare sound pressure level diﬀerences measured above ground surfaces, with
level diﬀerences obtained by calculation. There are two main template methods that are
recognized worldwide: the Nordtest method [47] (mainly adopted in the EU), and the
ANSI S1.18 method [43] (mainly adopted in the USA).
It’s common knowledge that the best way to measure the ground surface impedance
in-situ is by a direct measurement of the complex reﬂection coeﬃcient followed by a
suitable signal processing step [48, 49, 50]. Directly getting the complex impedance of the
ground allows for a stricter estimate measurement test of ground impedance than does
comparisons based only on pre-calculated short-range level diﬀerence values (as required
by the template methods). However, these kind of direct in-situ measurements are quite
sensitive to measurement errors, particularly for the required outdoors measurements of
the phase diﬀerence [51].
The Nordtest method and ANSI S1.18 are template methods that allow for a very con-
venient determination of the surface impedance with only basic knowledge of acoustical
measurements and without complicated mathematical routines. They are often imple-
mented as standard method when an assessment and management of environmental noise
is carried out, principally due to their simplicity.
Since this project is executed in Denmark, after a brief discussion with my supervisor, it
was a common agreement to select the Nordtest method (see Appendix B) as template
method for this work. In this chapter, some elements of the measurement method will
be brieﬂy described next to provide the necessary background for an explanation of the
results to be presented.
4.2
Methodology
The Nordtest method indicates a speciﬁc procedure for the determination of the normalized
speciﬁc acoustic impedance of ﬂat outdoor ground surfaces in-situ. As default, the template
method is based on the one-parameter impedance model (subsubsection 3.3.1), thus it
illustrates how to proper measure the only parameter involved in this model: the ﬂow
resistivity class σ. As a result of the measurement procedure explained in this method, one
or several ﬂow resistivity classes are identiﬁed for the ground surface under investigation.
These results are then embedded in the impedance model in order to calculate the complex
acoustic impedance of the ground as a function of the frequency, in a range between
200-2500 [Hz]. Higher frequency bands are avoided as these may be more sensitive to the
size of the loudspeaker opening, the microphone stand and other disturbances. Lower
frequency bands are prevented due to the low accuracy of the method, especially for
hard surfaces. Thus, it is possible to deduce that the Nordtest method is not suitable
for evaluation of ground impedances below or above the frequency range deﬁned in the
template.

made by recording the time-averaged sound pressure level of each one third octave band
within the frequency range of interest for at least 15 seconds. At least four independent
measurements are needed, and between each measurement translation and/or rotation of
the source and the microphones to an adjacent piece of identical ground is recommended

4.3
Reviews and updates of the method
The Nordtest method has been tested by four diﬀerent measurement teams, each carrying
out four measurements on each of four diﬀerent test sites. The results show good agreement
between the diﬀerent measurement teams. The measurement uncertainty is less than or
equal to one ground class, withal laid in the uncertainty range deﬁned in the template
method [53].
Nevertheless, how previously mentioned in subsubsection 3.3.1, the one-parameter model
does not always oﬀer suitable results of ground impedance values. In spite of this model
has the advantage of using only a single parameter to predict the acoustical properties of
ground surfaces, several studies ([52] [54] [55]) showed diﬀerent disadvantages of the same
impedance model. In particular, Attenborough in [55] summed up two main deﬁciencies
of the Delany and Bazley’s model. These two defects are listed below.
• The Delany and Bazley impedance model predicts non-physical values for the real
part of the surface impedance of a layer (i.e. negative values of the real part) when
extrapolated to low frequencies.
• For a porous material with known ﬂow resistivity, it overestimates of the imaginary
part of the propagation constant and porous material.
It follows that, despite the fact that the Nordtest method is a robust and easy to use
procedure, it is restricted to the chosen absorber model(s).
Research ([34] [46] [51])
suggested an alternative default choice of impedance model, in order to replace the
one-parameter with a more physically based model (i.e. law of Darcy).
Among others, after eight years from the oﬃcial publication of the Nordtest method, in 2006,
a revision of the same was proposed by the Nordic Innovation Center [46]. The NT ACOU
104 template method has been overhauled taking into account updated developments, new
measurements and with some improvements to extend the range of grounds consider in
the original method. The recommended major changes are the following:
• for measurements of hard surfaces, an alternative measurement distance (8.75 [m]
instead of 1.75 [m]) is introduced related to a variation of the frequency range (from
200-2500 [Hz] to 800-6300 [Hz]);
• for porous road surfaces and ground with layer appearance, the Hamet 3-parameter
(subsubsection 3.3.5) impedance model is presented;
• level diﬀerence tables of reference have been revised, corrected and elaborated in
order to simplify the evaluation of the new impedance values.
This project was carried on based on the latest and revised version of the Nordtest method
(see Appendix C). This choice was determinate by the results of a comparison of the
reference tables of both versions of the template method. The error for each ﬂow resistivity
class was proved to be lower and more realistic in the updated version of 2006.

Overview

identiﬁcation of the complex acoustic ground impedance of at least two diﬀerent types of

of the ground, is strictly related to the ground eﬀect. The ground eﬀect can be deﬁned as

sound exhibits a series of interference maxima and minima. The Nordtest method uses a
parameter-ﬁtting technique which exploits and ﬁts the ground eﬀect by using the spectrum
of the diﬀerence in levels between vertically separated microphones at a short range from
a point source above a ground surface [56].

Results and Discussion
6.1
Data processing
Before presenting the measurement results for ﬂow resistivity class and acoustic ground
impedance value, a short description of how data is stored and processed is given.
6.1.1
Data format
As explained in the previous chapter, the sound emitted by the loudspeaker was recorded
by two diﬀerent microphones at diﬀerent heights, on four channels for each microphone.
The recorded data was then stored on the DTU network server as a collection of .tim
ﬁles. The acronym .tim stands for ”Time-Domain Waveform Data”: these text ﬁles store
data along diﬀerent columns, with a row for each sample. The ﬁrst column stores the
timestamps of each sample, while the following columns store the data for each channel.
The format is similar to that of .tsv ﬁles, in that the columns are separated by tabs and
there is a header that names each column.
Each recorded ﬁle represents 120 seconds of continuous measurements: whenever a ﬁle
has stored 2 minutes’ worth of data, it is saved to disk and a new ﬁle is created without
interrupting the measurement process. This means that an hour of recorded measurements
corresponds to 30 diﬀerent ﬁles. Each ﬁle contains the data for the signal recorded by a
single microphone: since the microphones measure the sound emitted by the loudspeaker
in terms of voltage, the stored values are all expressed in milliVolts [mV]. Therefore, in
order to convert this voltage information into the desired sound pressure level diﬀerence
between the two microphones, a data processing step is needed. This was accomplished
through the numerical computing software MATLAB.
6.1.2

sound signal information in terms of voltage, and by knowing the transfer factor of
each microphone (Table 10), it’s possible to convert said information from audio voltage
(milliVolt [mV]) to sound pressure (pascal [Pa]) via Equation 22a.

Once the signal is expressed in terms of sound pressure value, it’s necessary to convert its
representation from the time domain to the frequency domain. In addition, the frequency
39
==PAGE:52==
band should be converted from the standard narrow-band to 1/3-octave band. The
frequency-domain representation is required to analyze the frequency spectrum of the
signal as a whole, irregardless of speciﬁc time periods; moreover, ground acoustic impedance
is expressed in the frequency domain as well. In addition, since the narrow-bands in a range
between 200 [Hz] and 2500 [Hz] are very diﬃcult to visualize separately, the 1/3-octave
bands are adopted instead. These bands, also required by the Nordtest method, are wide
enough to be easily distinguished, thus reducing uncertainties and errors when analyzing
the data.
To describe the spectrum of a signal, two components are needed: the ﬁrst is the afore-
mentioned signal after conversion to the frequency domain; the second component is the
signal’s Power Spectral Density (PSD). The power spectrum of a time signal represents
the distribution of the signal’s total power into the signal’s frequency components. Mathe-
matically speaking, the PSD is the absolute value of the square of the Fourier transform
of the same signal.
There are several functions in MATLAB that could be applied to convert a signal from
time domain to frequency domain. The most common function is FFT which computes a
Fast Fourier Transform on the input signal. Then, the PSD of the signal can be obtained
from the FFT result, using the above deﬁnition. Finally, a 1/3-octave band ﬁlter has to
be applied in order to convert the data from narrow-band to 1/3-octave band. This can
be done through a function called ”NarrowToNthOctave”. It has to been noted that this
function is not included in the software, but it can be downloaded from the MathWorks
website [58].

Summary
The main purpose of this project was to determine the ﬂow resistivity class of at least
two diﬀerent types of ground located nearby the wind turbine placed in Drantum. Even
if the total error E was higher than the limit imposed by the Nordtest method, when
considering the comparison between DELTA speaker and DTU speaker (Table 19, Test 2
and Test 3), the results seem to be acceptable for almost all the tests. In fact, when using
the DTU speaker the obtained error is about 4 [dB] lower than the error obtained when
using the DELTA speaker. It appears clearly then, that having used the DTU speaker
for the measurement tests of ground #1 and #2 too, the overall errors would have been
within the limit of 15 [dB]. The same cannot be said for ground #3, which presents a
larger error (almost 9 [dB] over the limit of Nordtest) due to the diﬀerent geometry used.

measurement error E seems to give acceptable results. A summary of the results obtained

6.4.1
Theoretical solution
As suggested by the Nordtest method, an analytical solution was also calculated and
compared to the measurement results. The adopted procedure agrees with the theoretical
method presented in subsubsection 2.2.4.
By comparing the calculated sound level diﬀerence obtained by the analytical method
to a reference table, the ﬂow resistivity class of the ground under investigation could be
identiﬁed by the lowest error E. That is, the sound level diﬀerence can be calculated,
rather than measured.
Although the analytical method could in theory allow for the calculation of the level
diﬀerence for any impedance model (as speciﬁed in the Annex D of the Nordtest method),
the theoretical model (from Equation 6 to Equation 9) is only constrained by two variables:
the geometry and the acoustic impedance Z. This means that, keeping the same geometry,
the only ”free parameter” in the model will be the acoustic ground impedance obtained
through the impedance models presented in subsection 3.3. It follows that, when using
the one-parameter model to express the acoustic impedance as a function of the frequency,
57
==PAGE:70==
the only variable will be the ﬂow resistivity class σ. Having only a single free variable
might not allow the model to properly represent the environmental conditions, therefore
causing a loss of accuracy.

7
Conclusions
The goal of this thesis was to collaborate to the DecoWind project, by assessing the
acoustic ground impedance of at least two types of ground located nearby the wind turbine
in Drantum. Ground #1 was chosen to be a grass lawn; ground #2 was chosen to be an
agricultural ﬁeld.
Following the procedure outlined by the template Nordtest method, the acoustic ground
impedance of ground #1 and ground #2 was determined indirectly from in-situ measure-
ments.
From the analysis of the results obtained from the tests carried out during this work, some
relevant conclusions have been drawn. They are listed below.
• Changing from dry to wet conditions results in a higher ﬂow resistivity class for the
same ground type. In practical terms, this implies a higher ”resistance” to sound
propagation, especially for the sound wave reﬂected on the soil surface.
• Small geometry changes in the conﬁguration of the measuring instrumentation can
have a large impact on the ﬁnal error. In particular, it was noted that a shift of only
10 [cm] in the position of the lower microphone results in an increase of the error E
of about 33% (9.1 [dB] on 27.6 [dB]). This magnifying eﬀect could also lead to a
wrong result for the ﬂow resistivity class of the ground.
• The use of a windscreen for low to moderate wind conditions does not aﬀect much
the accuracy of the measure. For wind speeds of around 5 [m/s] in the vicinity of the
ground, the use of a windscreen on microphones accounted for an error reduction of
about 7% (1.4 [dB] on 19.9 [dB]).
• The eventual roughness of the soil surface does not aﬀect much the accuracy of the
measure. Cutting the grass in the measurement area to obtain a ﬂatter surface leads
to a decrease of the error of around 1.4% (0.2 [dB] on 14.2 [dB]).
• The model and especially the age of the source loudspeaker has a large impact on
the accuracy of the results. When comparing the error for a measurement using a
new B&K omnisource speaker type 4295 to the error for a measurement using an
older Dodecahedron loudspeaker type Nor276, an improvement of 29.28% (4.1 [dB]
on 14 [dB]) was reported. This substantial change in error was enough to result in a
diﬀerent ﬂow resistivity class estimation for the same ground type.
• It was not possible to evaluate with certainty if an increase in the number of free
parameters for the model of acoustic impedance corresponds to a higher accuracy of
the result. Even though this intuition is asserted in the literature [35, 39, 46], the
provided values of porosity (Ω= 0.45), depth of the porous layer (L = 0.1 [m]) and
pore shape factor ratio (sp = 0.75) for the considered ground types are inconsistent
with the measurement results. Rather, it is suggested in the literature to artiﬁcially
”tune” these values to provide the best ﬁt for the measured acoustic impedance. It is
debatable, then, if these default parameters could be reused for similar ground types:
a more correct approach, although less practical, would be to measure the value of
these parameters for each test that is conducted. When that solution is not feasible,
63
==PAGE:76==
a comparison of the results obtained for the four models on ground #1 and ground
#2 (Figure 35 and Figure 36, respectively) suggests that using the one-parameter
Delany-Bazley could still be a suﬃcient approach to estimate the ground acoustic
impedance.
• To obtain the Power Spectral Density in a 1/3-octave band, the poctave MATLAB
function is recommended. After comparing eight diﬀerent candidate functions, mainly
diﬀering in the size of the window used to sample the signal, several tests carried
out during the course of this work have shown that poctave was the most suitable
and accurate for the purpose of this project.
• To conﬁrm the validity of the results obtained by the Nordtest method, the analytical
method has been considered. However, the theoretical solution has not brought
beneﬁts, but has, instead, evidenced a remarkable inconsistency in the analytical
resolutive approach.
In particular, we observe that changing the ground type,
maintaining the same geometry and considering the same one-parameter model
(Delany-Bazley), the ﬂow resistivity class (and consequently the acoustic impedance),
does not change. This results in a discrepancy between the solution obtained by the
analytical method and the solution obtained by the measurements and the use of the
Nordtest method. Therefore, while it is not excluded that some errors in calculation
could have occurred due to the inexperience of the author, on the other hand it is
advisable to revise the Nordtest method and the analytical solution recommended in
it.
Following the above conclusions, some suggestions for future work are outlined below.
• It is advised to deﬁne clearer and more thorough speciﬁcations in the Nordtest
method, especially for what concerns the measurement instrumentation used. For
example, the method does not mention the stands/tripods to be used for loudspeaker
and microphones, even though they might also aﬀect the propagation of sound by
vibration [62].
• It is suggested to explain how the values in the reference tables in the Nordtest
method were calculated. This is in order to create more intermediate values in the
ﬂow resistivity class range, and therefore improve the accuracy of the measurements,
and to double check the results of the method in case the error E is above the
imposed limit.

6
TEST PROCEDURE
6.1
Principle
The method uses two fixed microphone positions, the
bottom and the top microphone respectively.
The sound pressure level is measured at two different
heights above the ground at a specified distance from a
loudspeaker at a specified height above the ground. The
measured data are used to obtain a best fit to one of a
number of calculated curves. The result is the flow resistivity
class.
6.2
Test site
The test site shall be flat, with surface unevenness not ex-
ceeding ±50  mm, and no vertical reflecting objects are al-
lowed within 10 times the distance of separation between
source and receiver. The ground shall be in the condition
desired for the measurements. It can be dry, wet, snow
covered, frozen or in any other condition provided that there
is an accurate description of it in the test report.
Note: It may be difficult to comply with the qualification require-
ments of clause 6.8 if the ground surface is concave or
convex over the test site.

due

1 
Scope and field of application 
 
4.1 
General 
This NORDTEST method specifies how to determine the normalised specific acoustic 
impedance of flat ground surfaces outdoors in situ. It yields one or several flow resistivity 
classes the values of which can be translated into the real and imaginary part of the 
specific acoustic impedance to use for outdoor sound propagation calculations.  
 
Depending on the ground impedance to be measured this NORDTEST method evaluates 
the impedance in one third octave bands for the range 250 to 2500 Hz or 800 to 6300 Hz. 
 
4.2 
Measurement uncertainty 
The measurement uncertainty is estimated to be + 1 flow resistivity class. 
 
Note 
The measurement uncertainty has been estimated from comparison measurements 
carried out on 4 different ground surfaces by 4 different Nordic laboratories.

7 
Instrumentation 
7.1 
General 
Within the frequency range of interest the sound source shall be a loudspeaker, 
omnidirectional within 1 dB within + 30°. The microphones shall be omnidirectional 
within 1 dB within + 45°. Both microphones shall be fitted with windscreens during the 
measurements. 
 
The measurement equipment shall meet the requirements of a class 1 instrument 
according to IEC 61672-1 and the filters shall meet the requirements of IEC 61260. 
 
7.2 
Calibration 
During each series of measurements, apply a sound calibrator with an accuracy of ±0,3 
dB (class 1 according to IEC 60942) to the microphone for checking the calibration of the 
entire measuring system at one or more frequencies over the frequency range of interest.  
 
Verify the compliance of the calibrator with the requirements of IEC 60942 once a year 
and the compliance of the instrumentation system with the requirements of IEC 61672-1 
at least every two years in a laboratory making traceable calibrations. 
 
Record the date of the last check and confirmation of the compliance with the relevant 
IEC standard.

General

9 
Test procedure 
9.1 
Principle 
The method uses two fixed microphone positions, the bottom and the top microphone 
respectively.  
 
The sound pressure level is measured at two different heights above the ground at a 
specified distance from a loudspeaker at a specified height above the ground. The 
measured data are used to obtain a best fit to one of a number of pre-calculated curves. 
The result is the flow resistivity class. 
 
9.2 
Requirements on the test site 
The test site shall be flat within + 50 mm and no vertical reflecting objects are allowed 
within 10 times the distance of separation between source and receiver. The ground shall 
be in the condition desired for the measurements. It can be dry, wet, snow-covered, 
frozen or in any other condition provided that there is an accurate description of it in the 
test report.  
 
Note 
It may be difficult to comply with the qualification requirements of clause 6.8 if the 
ground surface is concave or convex over the test site. 
 
9.3 
Requirements on meteorological conditions 
To minimize turbulence due to thermal and wind gradients it is recommended to carry out 
the measurements when the sky is overcast and the wind speed low. If there is a stable 
wind direction locate the measurement line at an approximately right angle to the wind 
direction, see figure 1.

9.9

If max(sDj) exceeds 4 dB try to repeat the measurements on a better test site. 
 
If e exceeds 15 dB for measurement distance 1 and the frequency range 250-2500 Hz or 
25 dB for measurement distance 2 and the frequency range 800-6300 Hz try to apply 
another impedance model described in this Nordtest method. If the measurements still do 
not qualify the impedance cannot be evaluated with this Nordtest method. An alternative 
method is described in Annex E.

A.2.2

In some situations, like snow covered ground, the one-parameter model may not yield any 
good results. Then an alternative approach is to use a two parameter model. That means 
we have to minimize the error with respect to two parameters, and then calculate the 
impedance from them. If one of the parameters can be measured, like the layer depth in 
the model below, we can use the same principle as for the one parameter model. 
 
In the case of a soft layer on top of a hard ground, such as snow on frozen ground, use the 
model (2) described in 5.3, via the tables B.3 - B.8 depending on layer depth and 
temperature. If the layer is thicker than 17.5 cm, use the one-parameter model. If not, use 
the table corresponding to the depth which is closest to the real value, and determine the 
best fit class with the method described for the one-parameter model. When presenting 
the result, also indicate measured layer depth, and the layer depth used in the 
comparisons.

Annex C   Level difference tables for measurement 
distance 8,75 m (Normative) 
C.1 
Field of application 
This annex is primarily applicable to comparatively hard surfaces with flow resistivities 
exceeding 1 MPas/m2. The tables presumes measurement geometry A.2 that is a distance 
of 8,75 m between source and receiver. Table C.1 and C.2 are to be used for 
comparatively hard, non-absorbing road surfaces where the one-parameter model could 
be expected to yield reasonable results whereas table C.3-C.5 are to be used for porous, 
sound-absorbing road surfaces.

Flow resistivity class 16, etc. 10 indicates a flow resistivity of 10 kPas/m2, 16 16 kPas/m2, 
etc. The table is in MPas/m2.
