Preface
This thesis was prepared at the Department of Electrical Engineering at the
Technical University of Denmark (DTU) as a partial fulﬁllment in the require-
ments of achieving a Degree in Master of Science in Medicine and Technology.
The project aims at developing a real-time SensoriMotor Rhythm (SMR) based
Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) spelling application customized for a patient
suﬀering from Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS). The volunteering patient
is characterized by a severe degree of locked-in-syndrome (LIS) with an intact
cognitive function and a moderate control of the eye muscles.

Abstract
This project aims at developing a SMR based BCI spelling application for a
voluntary patient suﬀering from ALS.
An experimental setup and BCI paradigm for data acquisition is constructed
to record an EEG from a total of two subjects including the motor disabled
patient and a healthy subject. Furthermore, data set 1 from the BCI IV com-
petition is included to serve as a comparison to the acquired data in this project
and as a validation of the proposed algorithm.
An algorithm for classifying up to three diﬀerent motor imagery tasks, left
hand, right hand and feet, is developed based on three types of features in-
cluding ERD/ERS quantiﬁcation, a power ratio and a PLV. Especially the two
ﬁrst features demonstrate the bestability to discriminate between the three
classes of motor imagery,while the last feature delivers a poor performance.
A SVM classiﬁer is applied whereby accuracies above 80% are obtained for
both subjects as well as for the BCI IV competition data set. By only dis-
criminating between two classes of motor imagery the patient and the healthy
subject reach even higher accuracies above 85 %.
A real-time implementation is not accomplished within the project time an-
dremains a future perspective.
Based on the ﬁndings in this study it is believed that the enrolled patient owns
the ability for performing SMR based control and that the proposed algorithm
has a potential in a future spelling application.

Summary
Dette projekt sigter mod at udvikle en SMR baseret BCI stave applikation til
en frivillig ALS patient.
Der konstrueres et eksperimentelt setup og BCI paradigme til optagelse af et
EEG fra to individer, den motorisk hæmmede ALS patient samt et raskt in-
divid. Endvidere inddrages datasæt 1 fra BCI IV konkurrencen med henblik
på sammenligning med de optagne data i projektet samt til validering af den
foreslåede algoritme.
En algoritme udvikles til klassiﬁcering af op til tre forskellige motor imagery
funktioner, venstre hånd, højre hånd og fødder, baseret på tre feature typer,
ERD/ERS kvantiﬁcering, et power forhold og en PLV. Især de to første fea-
tures demonstrerer den bedste evne til at diskriminere mellem de tre motor
imagery klasser, mens den sidste feature har ringe performance.
En SVM klassiﬁer benyttes, hvorved en klassiﬁcerings rate på over 80% opnås
for begge individer, såvel som for BCI IV datasættet. Ved kun at diskriminere
mellem to motor imagery klasser opnår patienten og det raske individ endnu
højere klassiﬁceringsrater på over 85 %.
En real-time implementering blev ikke fuldført indenfor projektets tidsramme
og udestår derfor som fremtidigt projekt.
Baseret på resultaterne i dette projekt, vurderes det, at den medvirkende pa-
tient udviser evne for at kunne udføre SMR baseret kontrol og at den foreslåede
algoritme har potentiale til at indgå i en fremtidig implementering af en stave
applikation.

Introduction
The technology of Brain Computer Interface (BCI) intends to function as an
extra link between the brain and the surrounding environment and hereby
opens the possibility of either replacing, restoring, supplement or enhancing
a lost or still existing function of the user. Over the last couples of decades
BCI systems have proven to support people suﬀering from motor inability in
a broad variety of functions as for example by a robotic arm, a word speller
and by restoring lost neural outputs by implanting electrodes stimulating the
paralyzed muscles.
Especially SensoriMotor Rhythm (SMR) based BCI has received a lot of at-
tention within the ﬁeld of BCI research for years due to the known changes in
the activity of the SMR associated to execution of movement and imagination
of movement. Studies have demonstrated that healthy adults in general are
assumed to be able to control and use SMR based BCI applications. Despite
the limited amount of data it is also suggested that the majority of patients
suﬀering form Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS) also hold this possibility
even though the performance tends to be lower and training rates seem to be
higher compared to healthy people.
Although SMR based BCI spelling applications controlled by patients with
ALS and other SMR based BCI applications previously have been developed,
several improvements are still needed before this type of BCI applications will
gain their true entry in assisting motor disabled patients. Many of these im-
provements are related to speed, accuracy, robustness and adaption of the BCIs
to the individual user. Additionally the ﬁnancial cost is another important and
often contradicting factor necessary to consider in order for these systems to
become accessible for the disabled users.
The objective of this project was to develop a real-time functioning SMR
based BCI spelling application based on the imagination of three diﬀerent
movements for a volunteering patient suﬀering from ALS and characterized by
almost complete Locked-In-Syndrome (LIS). The motivation for this project
is driven by two key factors. First of all the developed system is intended to
be customized for the subject enrolled fulﬁlling the needs due to his condition,
second a low cost setup is prioritized.

Background
Present BCI systems utilize the electrical or magnetic signals of the brain to
decode the intend of the user. BCI systems based on electrical signals are far
more common than systems applying magnetic signals, due to the low cost
and ease related to acquisition of the former compared to the latter. Systems
applying magnetic signals are currently primarily constricted to research set-
tings [1].
A typical BCI system is basically divided into three functional blocks; signal
acquisition, feature extraction and feature translation, see Figure 1.1. These
functional blocks form the overall structure and process in the development of
SMR based BCI spelling application.
This chapter will provide an overall description of the diﬀerent types of sig-
nal sources applied in BCI systems with an especially detailed review of SMR
based BCI systems and the associated physiological background.

1.1. Electroencephalography
Chapter 1. Background
1.1
Electroencephalography
A electroencephalography (EEG) is a method of measuring and visualizing
the electrical activity of the brain. Noninvasive electrodes are placed along
the scalp to measure the electrical potential between the electrodes and a
reference. The detectable electrical potentials mostly arise from the alternating
ioniccurrents within the pyramidal neurons when action potentials emerge,
due to the fact that these neurons are large and located perpendicular to the
cortical surface. When a suﬃcient amount of neurons exhibit the same ﬁre rate,
these neurons display a synchronously oscillating behavior and their currents
are summed whereby they become measurable at the scalp.
An EEG signal is according to the standardized accepted terminology analyzed
in several distinctive frequency bands, see Table 1.1. These frequencies are
known to be present in certain topological areas of the scalp or related to
speciﬁc biological events. Most likely the terminology has occurred to support
a joined accepted nomenclature for the most applied EEG content in clinical
practice. It should be noticed that the exact frequency ranges for the various
bands tend to diﬀer in the literature. Notice how the µ frequency band overlaps
with the α activity. α activity is most common during rest and detectable over
the visual cortex while µ activity is related to motor behavior and detectable
over the SMR. The clear distinction between these overlapping frequencies
tend to be used diﬀerently in previous literature why both bands (µ and α)
are referenced in relation to motor behavior.

1.2. Sensorimotor rhythm in EEG
Chapter 1. Background
Visual evoked potentials are initiated by a visual stimuli such as a ﬂashing light
or the display of an image. These events often appear as negative or positive
deﬂections in the EEG time-locked to the speciﬁc stimuli and most promi-
nently in the area of the visual cortex. Steady-State-Visual-Evoked-Potentials
(SSVEPs) are evoked by repetitive visual stimuli, which consequently will in-
duce steady state oscillations in the visual cortex that can be detected by e.g.
simple frequency analysis [3].
P300 is an ERP elicited by the process of making a decision. It is often re-
ﬂected by a visually positive deﬂection in the EEG occurring 250-400 msec.
after the stimuli.
Over the last couples of decades especially SensoriMotor Rhythm (SMR) based
BCI systems have received a lot of attention and have become a highly inves-
tigated area within BCI research.
SMR based BCI systems are based on the detection of changes in the rhythmic
activity of SMR. In contrary to EVPs evoked by an outer stimuli, the changes
in SMR are internally generated as a result of the intend of the user. This
implies that these events are not time-locked to an external stimuli making
them more diﬃcult to detect.
SMR based BCI systems are furthermore unlike most other based BCI types
independent of eye gaze control which in particular enables these systems to
support persons suﬀering from severe loss of motor function with a low level
of motor control of the eyes.
According to literature SMR based BCI systems are suggested to provide the
highest level of control in regards to the degrees of freedom compared to other
BCI signal sources [4].

of the brain is advantageous.
The cortex of the brain is subdivided into several areas according to their
function and control, as demonstrated in Figure 1.2a.

Chapter 1. Background
(a) Lateral view of the brain outlining the speciﬁc areas of the cortex associated to
motor function and processing. [5]
The primary motor cortex (PMC) and the medial premotor area/supplemen-
tary motor area (SMA) all function in the process of voluntary movement. The
PMC contains large neurons having long axons running down to the spinal
cord, where they further interact with the muscles. The initiation of a vol-
untary movement already starts in the SMA, where it is determined which
muscles to contract, in which order and to which degree before further signal-
ing is emerged to the upper neurons of the PMC, wherefrom the movement
actually is initiated. Each hemisphere of the PMC is characterized by a very
signiﬁcant and ordered representation of the neurons controlling the diﬀerent
body parts under the voluntary control, see Figure 1.2b. Muscles controlled by
a large amount of motor units are likewise represented in a relatively large area
in the cortex, which consequently provides a more precise movement. Muscles
aﬀected by fewer motor units are instead represented in smaller areas and will
not exhibit the same level of precise control.[7]

Penﬁeld (1949) all demonstrated detectable SMR changes related to motor
behavior [11]. Several studies have speciﬁcally described how ERDs appear
in the µ and β bands over the sensorimotor cortical areas during voluntary
movement.
µ rhythms are believed to exhibit two diﬀerent ERD patterns; First, a topo-
logical non-speciﬁc ERD pattern in the low frequency range of the µ rhythm
band (8-10 Hz) generated during all types of motor behavior and located over
the whole surface of sensorimotor cortex. It is most likely to be a preparation
of the motor area. Second, a topological speciﬁc ERD pattern in the higher
range of the µ rhythm band (10-13 Hz), located to a restricted topological area
of the sensorimotor cortex dependent of the speciﬁc task.
This topological localized ERD behavior will not only occur as a single event
but is often followed by localized ERS behavior in the neighbouring areas in
the PMC. This phenomenon has been termed a focal ERD/surround ERS in-
troduced by Suﬀczynski et al. [12]. Many examples exist for these speciﬁc
events, an example is illustrated in Figure 1.4.

Frequency analysis of the SMR signals of the PMC is an obviously choice
for detecting ERD and ERS events induced by execution or imagination of
movement.
The classic approach is band pass ﬁltering of the EEG signals recorded from
various recording sites of the PMC.
A standard ERD/ERS quantiﬁcation proposed by Pfurtscheller and Aranibar
[9] involves the steps of bandpass ﬁltering each trial, squaring of each sample
and averaging over multiple samples. The relative power decrease (ERD) or
increase (ERS) are then quantiﬁed with respect to a chosen reference period
a few seconds prior to the event. This approach positions as one of the most
applied methods in several studies discriminating between diﬀerent imagery
motor tasks [19, 9, 20, 21].
Graimann et. al.[20] demonstrate how the ERD/ERS quantiﬁcation proposed
by Pfurtscheller et. al.[9] is applied for investigating and visualizing the ERD
and ERS behavior in several frequency bands. Time-frequency maps are cre-
ated by the classic ERD/ERS quantiﬁcation in combination with a bootstrap
method only detecting signiﬁcant ERD/ERS behavior, see Figure 2.2. This
is a simple approach to retrieve the most active frequency bands representing
the desired ERD/ERS behavior in both the temporal and frequency domain.

2.3. Spatial ﬁltering and classiﬁers
Chapter 2. State of the art
2.3
Spatial ﬁltering and classiﬁers
SMR based BCI systems applying Common Spatial Patterns (CSP) are among
the best performing in diﬀerentiating between various motor imagery tasks
[27, 28, 29].
The CSP is a procedure to decompose multidimensional data into sub-components
with maximum variance between two classes. The multidimensional data is
projected into a subspace of lower dimensions by linear transformation to max-
imize the variance between the classes.
An Adaptive Laplacian (ALAP) ﬁlter was compared to other spatial ﬁlter
approaches including Common Average Reference (CAR) , small Laplacian
(LAP), large LAP and CSP in a study by Wolpaw et. al. [27]. In settings
with a large amount of channels the ALAP proves to be superior to the other
applied spatial ﬁlters. However, in settings with fewer channels restricted to
the motor area the ALAP and CSP perform equally.
In general the eﬃciency of spatial ﬁltering is to a large extent controlled by
the number of electrodes applied. The large LAP evidently provides a better
topological resolution of the EEG compared to a small LAP but does at the
same time require more channels. The choice of spatial ﬁltering and electrode
setting will become a trade oﬀbetween a better spatial resolution of the EEG
versus a lower number of channels.
Even though high performance is obtained by the CSP algorithm, a few draw-
backs limits its eﬃciency, especially in real-time functioning BCI systems. The
number of estimated parameters increases with number of electrodes making
it a quite complex algorithm in some cases. Another limitation of the CSP
algorithm is due to its supervised nature making it more prone to noise and
the risk of over-ﬁtting when only a limited amount of data is available. This
makes CSP a less attractive choice for real-time processing applications.
SVM classiﬁers are in general a popular choice when working with EEG signals
and hence also a classiﬁer used in BCI applications. The SVM is less com-
plex than classiﬁers such as Neural Networks and FCMs [22]. Furthermore the
SVM diﬀers from other classiﬁcation algorithms by its margin maximization
and regularization term providing a high generalizability and additionally its
insensitivity in relation to over-ﬁtting [30] [31].

Determination of subject-speciﬁc frequency bands
A very important concern in relation to quantifying ERD/ERS events is the
determination of the upper and lower limits for the applied frequency bands
of interest known to vary between subjects as stated by Pfurtscheller et. al.
[14]. It was chosen to investigate if a pattern of subject speciﬁc frequencies
was visible in the two data sets. Two diﬀerent approaches inspired from the
literature were used. Both methods apply a statistical procedure to identify
signiﬁcant frequency bands.
Comparison of short-time spectra

power spectra calculated in the baseline period, R, and in the activity period,
A, after the cue. The diﬀerence between the logarithmic power of the two
spectra provides an estimate of the most diﬀerentiating frequency bands in
relation to the reference period and the activity period, respectively.
Instead of using one second, two second power spectra were applied with the
reference power spectra calculated 2 seconds before the cue and the activity
power spectra calculated 0-2 seconds after the cue, see Figure 5.2. Using a
95% conﬁdence level (α = 0.05) signiﬁcant frequency content was identiﬁed
exhibiting either ERD or ERS behavior in the activity period, A, relatively to
the baseline period, R.

Signiﬁcant ERD/ERS patterns by bootstrapping
Another approach by Graiman et. al. [20], as mentioned in Section 2.2, com-
putes time-frequency resoluted ERD/ERS maps identifying signiﬁcant events
by a bootstrap based method.
ERD/ERS time courses for overlapping frequency bands covering the entire
frequency range of interest are combined in one map for one channel. The
ERD/ERS time courses are determined according to the classic approach as
described in Equation 5.1.
A frequency range of 7-34 Hz and a 1 Hz frequency resolution is considered
adequate to capture most of the ERD/ERS patterns in EEG. Graiman et. al.
proposes that a bandwidth of 2 Hz is applied for frequencies lower than 16 Hz.
In the frequency range 16-23 Hz a bandwidth of 4 Hz is recommended and
for higher frequencies a bandwidth of 8 Hz [20]. For one channel this provides
several ERD/ERS time courses which are combined as rows in a matrix to
form a single map.
In order to identify the most signiﬁcant patterns and associated frequencies
in each map a bootstrap technique is introduced, a suitable alternative to
asymptotic methods when the number of trials is limited.
The bootstrap
method estimates the unknown distribution by replacing the unknown pop-
ulation with data from the known empirical distribution [37]. A t-percentile
bootstrap method is proposed for estimating the conﬁdence intervals for the
computed ERD/ERS values.

5.2
Phase Lock Value
Most of the typically applied signal features within BCI utilizes frequency band
ﬁltered power from a speciﬁc recording location. Recent studies instead focus
on the relation between diﬀerent recording sites and proposes that these rela-
tionships possess a potential within SMR based BCI systems.
Studies by Wang et. al.[25, 26] suggest, among others, how measurement of
the synchronization by the PLV between the PMC and SMA can be employed
as a robust feature for discriminating between diﬀerent motor imagery classes.
Wang et. al. demonstrated an increased contralateral level of synchronization
between the PMC and the SMA in the µ rhythms similar to the ERD distri-
bution. By these ﬁndings it is anticipated that the usage of signals originating
from the SMA as a reference potentially can enhance the power diﬀerences
between the local recording sites within the PMC [25].
5.2.1

An almost identical implementation of the single trial large-scale PLV feature
investigated by Wang et. al.[25] was reproduced in this project.

a reliable quantiﬁcation of the phase coupling.

The preceding chapters concerned the feature extraction and feature selection.
The next step as speciﬁed in the functional block of a BCI system in Figure 1.1,
presents the feature translation. Many diﬀerent types of models from simple
linear models to more complex classiﬁcation algorithms are appropiate for this

The SVM classiﬁer is a highly applied supervised machine learning method
used for class discrimination also in several BCI aspects. The following chapter
presents the application of a SVM classiﬁer with the extracted features and

a better basis for comparison with the two-class BCI data set. Furthermore, it
was interesting to investigate if two speciﬁc classes were more separable than
others and whether this would be subject speciﬁc.

7.2
Cross-Validation
Cross-validation is a validation technique for estimating the predictive per-
formance of a model. The cross-validation scheme determines a test set for
validation during the training of a model whereby the risk of overﬁtting is re-
duced and a more generalized estimate of the performance is obtained. Cross-
validation methods are subdivided into diﬀerent types according to the speciﬁc
splitting of the data set into a test set and a training set, respectively. A k-fold
cross-validation procedure was applied dividing the data set into 20 equal-sized
folds. One fold is retained for validation while the remaining 19 folds are used
for training. This procedure is repeated 20 times ensuring that all folds are
tested once, see Table 7.1. The 20 fold performances are then averaged to
obtain an overall estimate of the generalizing performance.

Discussion
This last chapter will provide a critical examination of the diﬀerent ﬁndings
of this study and additionally an evaluation of the applied methods and exe-
cutable alternatives in a future perspective.
8.1
Experimental Setup and Preprocessing
One of the main activities of this project included the development of an ex-
perimental setup for data acquisition of three performed motor imagery tasks
including left hand, right hand and feet.
G.tec’s USBampliﬁer in combination with the g.tec.GAMMA box were applied
to record simultaneously EEG from 16 active electrodes densely placed over
the PMC. As an extension of the aim in limiting the cost related to the ex-
perimental setup it would be interesting to investigate how a reduction of the
number of channels would aﬀect the performance.
A BCI paradigm with close resemblance to the paradigm applied in the BCI
IV competition data set 1 was implemented in order to gain the best foun-
dation for comparison between the BCI data set and the acquired data set.
Small modiﬁcations of the implemented paradigm were made to adapt to the
personal needs of the disabled patient such as longer time periods during the
trials and the implementation of a visual moving object as cue instead of a
stationary visual cue to compensate that the disabled patient was aﬀected by
cataracts on both eyes during the project period.
The paradigm was not directly feedback regulated even though it could be ar-
gued that the moving object could simulate a feedback to the user and have a
placebo eﬀect evoking a better performance as seen in real feedback regulated
paradigms.
Based on reporting from the two subjects included in the the acquired data
set the lack of feedback made it a bit exhausting to complete a whole session
without loosing focus and concentration. This could possible be avoided by
the implementation of a feedback to the user during each task an important
improvement to consider in a future perspective.

8.1.1
Outlier Detection
The EEG signals were visually inspected before the sessions were started with
a noise inspection program to ensure the quality of the signals as stated in
Section 3.2.2. Any outlier detection or visual inspection of the recorded sig-
nals was however not executed after the recordings. This is obviously a very
important concern to address. Especially in relation to which consequences
this may have induced for the further processing of the signals.
The desire of removing outliers is always dependent of the objective and aim of
the speciﬁc task. Outliers represent abnormal behavior from the distribution
which in some aspects constitute the main interest of the data and in other
cases these are considered artifacts that should be removed. Speciﬁcally within
unsupervised classiﬁcation is detection of outliers highly applied to investigate
if data contains any separable but unknown classes within the distribution.
In accordance to this speciﬁc project it would have been beneﬁcial to im-
plement an outlier detection. Considering the fact that two types of power
features are extracted from the data outliers can have an inﬂuential eﬀect on
the resulting power. Outliers could easily be detected as data samples where
the associated power value exceeds a pre-determined threshold. The challenge
arise however in how the inﬂuence of the outliers should be eliminated. If the
outliers simply are removed this would destroy the continuity of the signals
which further would aﬀect the following processing. A more conservative but
secure approach is based on an all-or-nothing principle which simply implies
that all trials aﬀected by outliers should be removed. Whether this would
reduce the number of trials critically is another associated risk and trade-oﬀ
to evaluate.
It is, however, one of the most important concerns to clarify in a future aspect
of this study in order to truly validate the quality of the data recorded as well
as the associated ﬁndings in this study.
8.2
Feature Extraction and Feature Selection
The development of an automatized algorithm preprocessing the raw EEG sig-
nals and extraction of features for ﬁnal classiﬁcation constituted the next main
activity in relation to this project.
Three types of features were extracted from the preprocessed EEG signals in-
cluding single trial ERD/ERS time courses, single trial PLVs and single trial
power ratios. A statistical approach testing the signiﬁcance of the variances
between the diﬀerent classes for each feature was used to select optimal features
anticipated to provide the best discrimination between the diﬀerent motor im-
agery tasks.
The selected subject speciﬁc features determined in Section 6.1.1, improved
the classiﬁcation performance for the majority of the subjects during both two

class and three class discrimination compared to the utilization of all features
. The beneﬁt of applying a statistic approach for feature selection rather than
a principal component analysis (PCA) is explained by the fact that the signiﬁ-
cance in relation to the speciﬁc tasks are evaluated. A PCA identiﬁes features
explaining most of the variance of the data which not necessarily are related
to the speciﬁc tasks and consequently not an advantageous method for this
purpose. A close related method to the PCA is the supervised Fishers Linear
discriminant analysis also called Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) which
is applied in both classiﬁcation tasks and feature reduction. The LDA specif-
ically aims at increasing the separation between classes making it a feasible
method for feature selection. Because of the multi-class discrimination in this
project a simple statistic test was preferred rather than the LDA to minimize
the complexity.
A limitation associated to only testing the signiﬁcance of the variances is the
risk of discarding relevant features. Features not exhibiting a signiﬁcant vari-
ance could still contain characteristics able to separate the diﬀerent classes.
This could be mitigated by the inclusion of additional statistics but was not
further elaborated in this project.
Another and more relevant issue is related to the speciﬁc statistical approach
utilizted to test for equality of the variances. The applied Bartlett’s test is
sensitive to observations deviating from a normal distribution. In these cases
a Levene test is less sensitive and a more suitable choice. The possible presence
of outliers in this project increase the risk of non-normal distributed data. As
a consequence of this a Levene test was performed for comparison, see Sec-
tion 10 in Appendix. Few features became signiﬁcant with the Levene test
which not became signiﬁcant with the Bartlett’s test. However the portion of
signiﬁcant features in the Bartlett’s test not occurring as signiﬁcant with the
Levene test was greater. In other words, this could imply that the Bartlett’s
test beside signiﬁcant features furthermore has identiﬁed redundant features
compared to the optimal features selected from the Levene test. How the op-
timal features found in the Levene test aﬀects the obtained performances in
this project has not been further investigated and must follow in a future work.
The majority of the single trial ERD/ERS time courses and the single trial
power ratios both computed in the three diﬀerent frequency bands represent-
ing µ, β and γ became signiﬁcant for most of the included subjects. The single
trial ERD/ERS time courses and the single trial power ratios are both power
features closely related why it was expected that both of them would exhibit
signiﬁcance rather than just one of them.
Only few examples of signiﬁcant single trial PLV features occurred and pri-
mary in the BCI competition data set. This was however expected based on
the poor variation observed between the PLVs during the diﬀerent motor im-
agery tasks as discussed in Section 5.2.
According to literature ERD/ERS quantiﬁcation is a widely used and well

Furthermore, it is suggested that the determination of subject speciﬁc fre-

Two statistical approaches were applied to investigate whether it was possible

signiﬁcant ERD/ERS maps by a bootstrap technique. The obtained signiﬁ-

The ﬁrst presented a great variation across subjects, sessions and channels and

It was assessed that the two methods as they were applied here could not de-

the methods incorporating a voting principle it would be possible to obtain
subject speciﬁc bands applicable for all channels. This was, however, not fur-

robust statistical method and its ability of visualizing both time and frequency
resoluted signiﬁcant ERD/ERS behavior.
The use of spatial ﬁltering was not prioritized in this project again to keep
the complexity of the algorithm at a minimum. Spatial ﬁltering is, however,
a frequently used approach according to literature to enhance the localized
activity [42], as stated in Section 2.3. It is possible that a prior spatial ﬁl-
tering of the data would improve the performance specially in the single trial
ERD/ERS time courses and hereby possibly provide averaged ERD/ERS time
courses more similar to those seen in literature.
The complexity of the most applied spatial ﬁltering methods vary as do their
performances. A large LAP ﬁlter provides a high enhancement of the localized
topological activity but increases at the same time the number of necessary
channels thus not supporting a low cost experimental setup.
The CSP is
among the best performing spatial ﬁltering methods but is also prone to over-
ﬁtting when the amount of training data is limited and is furthermore sensitive
according to the selected channels [43]. CAR is a more simple spatial ﬁlter
method also providing a reasonable enhancement of the localized activity and
hereby a possible ﬁrst choice to further investigate if spatial ﬁltering should be
incorporated in a future perspective of the this project. Subsequently if the
CAR method does not improve the performance the CSP would probably be
the best alternative based on its good performances, however, given that the
necessary precautions to avoid overﬁtting are made.

A possible addition to the presented features and a not prior introduced ap-

of the Movement Related Potentials (MRPs). The RP is located in the lower
frequencies 0-5 Hz [44], as one slowly decreasing potential appearing with max-

A study by Wang et. al. [45] combines analysis of RPs and ERDs for dis-

Classiﬁcation and Performance
A cubic SVM classiﬁer was trained for each of the classiﬁcation tasks (with the
exception of speciﬁc cases where a Quadratic SVM and Fine Gaussian SVM
delivered the highest performances) using a 20-fold cross-validation to estimate
a generalizing accuracy and thus reduce the risk of overﬁtting.
In many classiﬁcation aspects it is normal procedure to split the data set into
a training set used to train a model and a validation set for estimating the ﬁnal
performance of the trained model. In this project non of the data was retained
for a ﬁnal validation set but all used for training due to limited amount of
data. This was however compensated using a 20-fold cross-validation scheme.
It could however be argued that the obtained performances of this studies are
limited and not truly representative due to the absence of a validation set of
unseen data.
All subjects and individual sessions, where applicable, were subject to two
class and three class discrimination according to the performed motor imagery
tasks.
The performances of the acquired data set increased over the three
sessions complying with ﬁndings in the literature suggesting that the ability
of SMR control improves with training. In the three class discrimination the
disabled person (subject 5) exhibited an initial performance of 66.3 % during
the ﬁrst session and reached a performance of 85.2 % during the third session
corresponding to an increase of 28.5 %. In comparison the healthy person (sub-
ject 6) only obtained an increased performance of 1.6 % in the third session.
Previous studies found that people with ALS in general are characterized by
a lower performance rate and higher training rate than healthy users as also
seems to be the case for subject 5 during the ﬁrst session. Remarkable is, how-
ever, the great diﬀerence in their performance improvement over the sessions.
This is most likely explained by the time period over which the sessions were
made. The sessions of the disabled person were recorded over a total period
of 2 months while the sessions of the healthy person were recorded in one day

with the same experimental setup though with breaks between the individual
sessions. This pattern was also generalizes for the two class discrimination of
the ALS patient and the healthy person.
Higher accuracies were obtained in the two class discrimination compared to
the three class discrimination for the ALS patient and the healthy person. The
ALS patient (subject 5) achieved the highest averaged performance during left
and right hand motor imagery, while the healthy person (subject 6) reﬂected
the highest averaged performance during right hand and feet motor imagery.
The diﬀerences between the averaged performances between the three combi-
nations of two class motor imagery for both subjects are, however, relatively
small. This makes it diﬃcult to drag any conclusions suggesting that two spe-
ciﬁc motor imagery tasks performed better than others.
The BCI IV competition data set (subject 1-4) in general obtain lower per-
formances than the two class performances of the enrolled patient and the
healthy subject (subject 5 and 6). One explanation could be the diﬀerences
in the technical speciﬁcations and equipment applied between the two data
sets. During the downsampling of the ERD/ERS time courses the diﬀerent
trial lengths of the two data sets were not compensated. This further induced
that the acquired data set was characterized by more features than the BCI
data set which possibly could aﬀect the performances.
Another explanation could be related to possible outliers. There are no re-
ports stating whether the BCI data set has been subject to outlier detection.
It is however anticipated that the quality of the data must has been revised
before distributed as a free public available data set. With this in mind is it
further possible that the performances of the BCI data set in fact are more
representative than the performances of the acquired data set which instead
could be randomly aﬀected by outliers.
The initial idea behind the inclusion of the BCI data set was for it to serve as
a validation of the proposed algorithm and furthermore as a comparison to the
acquired data set. Based on the diﬀerent technical speciﬁcations of the two
data sets many diﬀerent factors could inﬂuence the ﬁndings. Further implying
that any conclusion between the two data sets must be made with precaution.
The performances of the BCI data set are however still reasonable supporting
the ability of the proposed algorithm in this present project.

Future Perspectives
This project aimed at developing a real-time functioning SMR based BCI
spelling application for the enrolled target subject. However, due to the lim-
ited time frame a real-time implementation was not achieved. Instead this
project covered an oﬄine analysis of motor imagery and proposes a low com-
plex algorithm able to discriminate up to three class motor imagery.
As elaborated in the previous sections some central areas still need further clar-

iﬁcation before the full potential of the proposed algorithm can be determined.
Assuming that these topics will be covered in a future work and conﬁrm the
present ﬁndings, the proposed algorithm is believed to possess the ability for
discriminating up to three class motor imagery for the enrolled patient.
The best performances were obtained by the two class discrimination thus sug-
gesting that two-class motor imagery would provide the best foundation for a
future real-time functioning SMR based spelling application for the enrolled
patient. Even though discrimination of three classes would deliver a higher de-
gree of freedom two classes are still adequate to navigate in a spelling system
as is demonstrated e.g. in the famous Hex-O-Speller [17].
The current implementation of the algorithm operates as a synchronous system
referring to the fact that the system is time controlled. In a future real-time
implementation a key task remains in extending the algorithm to function as
an asynchronous system implying that the timing is determined by the user.
In this case the algorithm must further include an automatic detection of the
target events of the signals.
Due to the variation of the setup from session to session an initial calibration of
the system is needed prior to a session. This calibration could be constructed
as a synchronous system as done in this project whereby a number of time
controlled trials are recorded and used to train the model.
An accuracy of 70 % is considered suﬃcient to control a Language Support
Program according to literature [41] [46]. This accuracy could be applied as a
threshold value to estimate a required calibration time for a future real-time
implementation by using the obtained data in this project.
A further future perspective in a real-time environment would be the imple-
mentation of a continuously adaptive system adjusting to the user during a ses-
sion. This is in general a highly investigated topic within current BCI research
and an essential factor for the eventual value of SMR based BCI systems.

Conclusion
This project aimed at developing a SMR based BCI spelling application based
on three motor imagery tasks for a voluntary participating patient suﬀering
from ALS. The key objectives of the project were targeted against developing a
real-time functioning system adapted to the special needs of the patient due to
his condition and, furthermore, a low cost experimental setup was prioritized.
The project was decomposed into three main activities including the construc-
tion of the experimental setup, development of a low complex algorithm for
discriminating between up to three motor imagery tasks and ﬁnally a real.time
implementation of the proposed algorithm. .
Data set 1 from the BCI IV competition was included in the project for com-
parison and validation of the proposed algorithm. An experimental set up and
BCI paradigm almost similar to the BCI paradigm of the BCI IV competition
data set 1 were developed for execution of three sessions of data acquisition
with the disabled patient and an additionally included healthy subject.
An algorithm was developed for discriminating between up to three classes of
motor imagery including; left hand, right hand and feet. Three types of low
complex features including a power feature quantifying the ERD/ERS behav-
ior, a power feature expressing a power ratio and ﬁnally a feature based on
the synchrony between channels measured by the PLV. A SVM classiﬁer was
utilized for both two class and three class discrimination.
Signiﬁcant features were identiﬁed for each subject and each individual ses-
sion from where it was seen that the signiﬁcant features primarily constituted
the two power features. The feature based on PLVs did not prove signiﬁcant in
its ability to discriminate between the diﬀerent motor imagery tasks. This is
not in compliance with the reportings of Wang et. al. [25] demonstrating how
the analysis of relative power values with the inclusion of PLVs increases the
performance classifying two motor imagery tasks. The performances revealed
that utilization of signiﬁcant features only, compared to the inclusion of all
features, provided better accuricies in the main for all subjects.
The patient demonstrated a general tendency of increasing performance over
the sessions supporting the convential anticipation that disabled persons like
healthy persons are able to perform SMR control by training. Considering
67 of 115
==PAGE:79==
Chapter 9. Conclusion
the classiﬁcation accuracies of the BCI IV competition data set and the last
sessions of the disabled patient and healthy subject the proposed algorithm
provides performances above 80 . If only two class motor imagery is examined
for the last session of the patient and healthy subject considering signiﬁcant
features only the algorithm performs better than 85 %.
Assuming that possible outliers are removed from the acquired data and it is
validated that the lack of an outlier detection has not inﬂuenced the obtained
performances in this project, it is suggested that the proposed algorithm is
able to discriminate up to three diﬀerent motor imagery tasks of the disabled
patient.
Unfortunately a real-time implementation was not further assessed within the
time of this project and must remain as a future perspective where one of
the main task relies on the converting the proposed algorithm from a current
synchronous system to an asynchronous system.
The presented project concludes that the enrolled patient suﬀering from ALS
demonstrates the ability of SMR control and, furthermore, it is believed that
the proposed algorithm, able to discriminate between three motor imagery
tasks, could serve as a foundation towards a future real-time functioning SMR
based BCI spelling application.

Phase{m} = phase(per10:length(phase)−per10);
