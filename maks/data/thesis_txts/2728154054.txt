Abstract
The present study focuses on the early stage development of biohybrid Pickering
foams composed of partially deacetylated chitin nanofibres (cht), chitosan (chs), and
bioactive glass (BAG) for bone tissue engineering applications. The investigation
involved the test of formulations containing different concentrations of cht, chs,
crosslinkers, and BAG, ultimately leading to the selection of a base formulation
with 6wt% cht and 6wt% chs in combination with 0.6wt% BAG or 0.06wt% BAG.
Gas bubbles (N2O and CO2) were added to these formulations through the use
of a foam dispenser. Mechanical tests of the developed foams showed promising
results, with formulations displaying characteristics properties relatable to bone
tissue support. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) imaging of the foams reveals
highly porous structures, demonstrating the potential for efficient nutrient diffusion
and cell proliferation. Ion leaching from the foams, as determined by Inductively
Coupled Plasma-Optical Emission Spectroscopy (ICP-OES), is comparable to existing
literature, ensuring biocompatibility. Additionally, immersion of the scaffolds in
simulated body fluid results in the growth of hydroxyapatite (HA) on the surface,
further affirming their potential for bone tissue engineering. Differential Scanning
Calorimetry-Thermogravimetric Analysis (DSC-TGA) provides valuable insights
into the thermal stability of different scaffold compositions. Preliminary rheology
tests are promising, indicating the potential use of these formulations as inks for 3D
printing, facilitating precise scaffold fabrication. In conclusion, this study presents a
significant step towards the development of biohybrid Pickering foams as potential
scaffolds for bone tissue engineering. The successful incorporation of chitin, chitosan,
and bioactive glass, along with the advantageous mechanical, morphological, and
biocompatible properties, makes these foams promising candidates for further research
and applications in tissue engineering.
Keywords Chitin nanofibres, Chitosan, Bioactive glass, Pickering foams, Bone tissue
engineering, Scaffolds

Introduction
In the European Union (EU), about 23 million people are currently at high risk of
developing osteoporotic fractures [1]. Financially speaking, in 2019 in Europe alone
the excess cost related to osteoporosis and its linked fragility fractures amounts to
€56 billion each year. In view of the current global situation and the future increase
in complex bone damages, it is only reasonable to find new approaches like tissue
engineering, which has the potential to become a valuable solution [2].
Tissue engineering is a growing sector, with a global market that reached ap-
proximately US$10 billion in 2019 and an estimated compounded annual growth
rate (CAGR) of 14.2% between 2020-2027 [3]. Considerable advances have been
made in the field of tissue engineering, enabling the harvesting of cells from patients,
their multiplication in cell culture, and subsequent integration into biomaterials or
scaffolds to address organ, skin, and musculoskeletal deformities or damages. Bones
are hierarchical structures capable of regenerating and repairing themselves, although
the efficiency of bone repair depends on the extent of the damage [4–7]. Severe bone
defects resulting from trauma, congenital diseases, tumours, and infections often
require surgery and bone grafts for proper healing. The cost of bone graft surgeries
is a significant burden on healthcare systems, estimated at around US$2.5 billion
annually worldwide [4]. For small defects, autologous bone grafts are considered the
standard treatment [4, 6–8]. These grafts involve transplanting bone tissue from the
patient own body to the damaged site. Autologous grafts possess osteoconductivity
and osteoinductivity since they contain native bone extracellular matrix (ECM),
osteoblasts, and growth factors. However, a major drawback is the limited availability
of graft sources and the potential damage to the donor site. Alternatively, allografts
can be used, but they carry a higher risk of disease transmission and can lead to
adverse immunogenic response. Moreover, the present demand for donor organs and
tissues for transplantation exceeds the actual availability [3, 9]. Metal implants are
also commonly used [10]
Considering these factors and the ageing population, the demand for bone grafts
is expected to rise. Hence, tissue engineering focuses on creating engineered grafts or
scaffolds that can be implanted in the patient and support native tissue growth or be
seeded with autologous cells and then implanted in the damaged area [4, 6, 7, 9, 11–14].
Ideally, synthetic grafts should mimic the bone extracellular matrix, be biocompatible,
biodegradable, and display suitable mechanical properties. They should also have
appropriate porous structure and surface area that would enable angiogenesis, cell
adhesion, migration, proliferation, and osteogenic differentiation. In the case of bone
==PAGE:10==
2
tissue, scaffolds should preferably be osteoinductive and osteoconductive as well.
Cells are commonly cultured on two-dimensional (2D) scaffolds in vitro, which limit
cell population and viability, whereas three-dimensional (3D) scaffolds can support
larger tissue growth and mimic the vascular morphology of native tissue [9]. Three
main strategies of tissue engineering can be described (Figure 1a)[15]:
1. Cell therapy, which comprise of the harvesting cells from the patient or a donor
and their subsequent in vitro expansion for the final injection in the patient;
2. Acellular grafts, which comprise of the implantation of either donor-derived or
synthetic biomaterial scaffolds without any cellular component in the patient
body to promote native regeneration;
3. Cellular grafts, which utilise a top-down approach where autologous/allogenic
cells being scaled-up in vitro, subsequently seeded on a scaffold and matured
in bioreactors and finally implanted in the body (Figure 1b).
Top-down approaches are favoured in traditional tissue engineering, but it has been
challenging to obtain larger tissues with both high metabolic requirements and cell
density (Figure 1b)[16–18]. Biocompatible polymeric materials have been investigated
for bone graft substitutes since the 1940s, with a focus on achieving appropriate
mechanical properties and promoting tissue ingrowth [19]. Biomaterials used for
the production of scaffolds include synthetic polymers and natural polymers, and
bioceramics. Specifically, bioceramics promotes the regeneration of the bone tissue
since they are osteoconductive [11]. However, they are inherently brittle and their
biodegradation rate is often quite low [20]. In addition to their biocompatibility and
non-toxicity, the mechanical properties and degradation rates of natural polymeric
materials can be tailored to a certain extent. Consequently, it is favourable to create
blends between these biomaterials [2, 4, 5, 8, 12–14, 21, 22].

1.1
Biohybrid Pickering Foams
As aforementioned, porous materials have diverse biomedical applications, such as
porous biomedical devices, prostheses, scaffolds for cell culture and tissue engineering,
particulate foams for drug delivery and diagnostics, and 3D culture platforms for
studying cancer development and drug responses [23]. Considering the need for a
highly porous system in creating the right environment for cell attachment, commu-
nication, and nutrients/waste products flow, it is understandable why foam system
are so attractive.
Foams are regarded as multiphase systems, wherein air constitutes one of the
phases.
Surfactants are commonly used as stabilising agents [24].
Apart from
surfactants, foams can also be stabilised by particles. These particle-stabilised foams
are referred to as Pickering foams. The main difference between this stabilisation
methods is the amount of energy required for the compounds to enter and leave the
system. For instance, surfactants easily reach the interface between air bubbles and
solution, and they leave the system as smoothly. Particles require more energy to
enter the system; consequently, they tend to be more strongly if not permanently
adsorbed ( Figure 2).
==PAGE:12==
4
Figure 2: Illustration of Pickering foams (particle stabilised foams). The figure was
reproduced with permission from Hunter et al, 2008, Copyright (2008) Elsevier.
Foams can be characterised by either an open-cell system or a closed-cell system
[25]. These terms describe the arrangement and connectivity of the cells within the
foam material. Open-cell foams are characterised by an interconnected network of
cells throughout the material. The cells are interconnected with each other, creating
pathways for the flow of gases, fluids, and other substances. This open-cell structure
allows for the absorption and transportation of liquids, as well as the passage of gases
through the foam. Open-cell foams are often more compressible and flexible. On the
other hand, closed-cell foams have isolated and sealed cells that do not interconnect
with each other. This closed-cell arrangement makes the foam material less permeable
to gases and liquids. The lack of interconnected pathways provides greater resistance
to the flow of air, water, and other substances. Closed-cell foams are typically more
rigid and have higher structural integrity. In terms of biomedical devices, foams
should be characterised by an open-cell type of system, which can sustain nutrient
passage and cell communication.
Pores can be further classified depending on their size as micropores, mesopores,
and macropores [11].
Micropores have pore sizes below 2 nm, mesopores have
pore sizes ranging from 2 nm to 50 nm , and macropores have pore sizes greater
than 50 nm. Clearly, biomedical foams must possess the above listed properties,
such as biocompatibility, biodegradability, processability, sterilisability, and tailored
mechanical properties [26].
The performance and application of biomedical foams depends on controlling
their features and functionalities through material selection and fabrication processes
==PAGE:13==
5
[23, 26]. The porous structure - i.e. surface-to-volume ratio, pore size, and intercon-
nectivity - plays a crucial role in controlling cell behaviour and tissue development in
porous scaffolds for tissue engineering. Various processing techniques allow precise
control over the morphology, micro- and nano-features, and degradation of biomedical
foams. Advances in non-toxic and low-temperature processes facilitate the controlled
integration of bioactive substances.
1.2
Chitin, Chitosan and Bioactive Glass
1.2.1
Chitin and Chitosan
Within biopolymers, chitin (cht) and its derivative chitosan (chs) have shown
outstanding potentials as biomaterials for scaffolds [2, 8]. Natural fibres like cht, chs,
and cellulose have several advantages over synthetic polymers, namely less complex
preparation methods, biocompatibility, and higher sustainability [10]. Cht is an
interesting biopolymer commonly extracted from shellfish exoskeleton [2]. These
polysaccharides are non-toxic, biodegradable, biocompatible, osteoconductive, and
are able to sustain the deposition of the matrix rich of minerals by osteoblasts [5, 8, 27].
Furthermore, the inherent cationic nature of chs confers the biopolymer antibacterial
properties. Both cht and chs can be easily processed into films, membranes, gels,
nanofibres, sponges, and micro and nanoparticles and are being used in a plethora of
biomedical applications.
cht is the second most abundant natural polysaccharide after cellulose [8, 28,
29]. Its annual availability is estimated to be of over 10 Gigatons [8]. It is mainly
extracted from shellfish wastes through various biotechnological approaches, but can
also be found in the cell walls of fungi and yeast and in the exoskeletons of other
arthropods [2, 8, 28–30].
Chemically, cht is a linear polysaccharides composed of β-1,4-linked N-acetyl-2-
amino-2-deoxy-D-glucose units (N-acetylglucosamine) (Figure 3)[8, 29, 30]. chs,
the deacetylated form of cht, is a linear heteropolysaccharide composed of N-
acetylglucosamine and 2-amino-2-deoxy-D-glucose units at different concentrations [8,
29, 30]. The biopolymer is commonly recognised as chs if the degree of deacetylation
(DD) is above 60% [8]. The molecular weight (MW) of chitin is around 1 million
Da, whereas chitosan ranges from around 300 to 1000 kDa depending on the source
and processing [5, 8]. Cht is structurally similar to cellulose but with acetamide
groups at the C2 position. The abundance of hydroxyl groups (-OH), one primary
in C6 and one secondary in C3, and N-acetylated or amino groups (-NH2) in C2
==PAGE:14==
6
lead to the formation of intra and intermolecular hydrogen bonds that confer the
crystallinity to the structures [8, 29, 30]. The N-acetylated groups are responsible
for the higher strength of cht and its insolubility in common organic solvents and
water. On the other hand, cht with a lower number of N-acetylglucosamine units
is soluble in acidic , neutral, and alkaline solvents [28]. The higher solubility of
chs offers greater versatility in terms of processability and applications [2, 29]. The
β-1,4-linkages confer order and linearity to the crystalline nanofibers.
There are three allomorphs of cht: α-, β-, and γ-chitin [8, 31]. The former form
is the most abundant, generally found in hard structures, and its characterised by
an antiparallel arrangement of the chains. β-cht and γ-cht are less common and
are found in more flexible structures. The chains in the β-cht are parallel to each
other, whereas in the γ-cht they are display two parallel chains and one antiparallel.
In case of chs, both degree of deacetylation and molecular weight determines its
physicochemical and biological properties [8, 28]. The mechanical stability, versatility,
biocompatibility, non-toxicity, and biodegradability of these polymers make them
highly sought-for materials for a variety of applications [8].
Regarding the biomedical field, both cht and chs have been widely studied
as potential biomaterials for tissue engineering, gene therapy, drug delivery, and
wound dressing [2, 8, 28, 32]. It is a highly coveted biomolecule due to its net
cationicity and multiple functionalities. The cationic nature of chitosan is crucial for
interactions with anionic biological macromolecules like glycosaminoglycans (GAGs),
a main component of the extracellular matrix (ECM) of bone and cartilage [5, 33].
Furthermore, the hydrophylicity of cht make it a good support for the adhesion and
proliferation of cells.
The degradation of chs and cht in vivo occurs via non specific enzymatic hydrol-
ysis by lyzozymes, enzymes which hydrolyses the β-1,4-glycosidic bonds between
glucosamines, glucosamine and N-acetyl-glucosamine, N-acetyl-glucosamines [5, 34].
The results are oligosaccharides that can be excreted or assimilated into the GAG
or glycoprotein metabolic pathway. The products of cht and chs degradation have
different biological activity (e.g. anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antimicrobial) [34].
The degradation of chs is inversely proportional to the degree of deacetylation; there-
fore, an higher degree of deacetylation relates lower degradation rates [5, 35]. This
can be explained with the structure crystallinity [5, 34]. For instance, more ordered
structures are more stable and overall more complicated to degrade. The molecular
weight also influence the degradation rate of the polymer, with higher molecular
weight leading to slower degradation. Consequently, appropriately modifying the
==PAGE:15==
7
degree of deacetylation and molecular weight of chs can be easily tuned to host bone
growth.

1.2.2
Bioactive Glasses
BAGs are a group of bioactive ceramics containing various concentrations of SiO2-
Na2O-CaO-P2O5, with SiO2 less than 55%, that have received increasing attention
since their discovery in 1971 (Figure 4) [12, 36–40]. This class of silicate glasses
was discovered by Larry Hench, which also identified the renowned 45S5 Bioglass®
(44.5 wt% SiO2) . These ceramics were classified as Class A bioactive materials [12,
36, 38]. They demonstrate exceptional bioactivity and can establish bonds with
both soft and hard connective tissues. Class A bioactive materials exhibit both
osteogenic and osteoconductive properties, whereas Class B bioactive materials - such
as hydroxyapatite (HA) - are only osteoconductive [12]. BAGs display time-dependent
surface modification following implantation [21]. Upon contact with physiological
fluids, BAGs stimulate the formation of a bone-like HA layer which encourages
collagen interaction on their surfaces [12]. The favourable cellular responses and
facilitated rapid bone formation is due to the release of essential ions such as silicon
(Si), calcium (Ca), phosphorus (P), and sodium (Na) from BAGs. They stimulate cell
division, ECM synthesis, and mineralisation by triggering a synchronised sequence of
genes in osteoblasts. Depending on the type, they have also displayed antibacterial
properties, stimulated angiogenesis, and reduced inflammation [38, 41].
Apart
from Bioglass®, other compositions have gained growing attentions thanks to their
outstanding outcomes in clinical trials: 13-93 and S53P4 (Bonealive®) [40, 42].45S5
and S53P4 are FDA-approved BAGs and they are commonly utilised in clinical
applications as granules or particles [38, 39, 43].
The use of the BAG s53P4 has been approved on certain clinical applications.
==PAGE:16==
8
It is currently being utilised for craniofacial surgery procedures, spinal fusion and
depressed tibial plateau fractures, as bone graft after benign tumour resection, and
in the treatment of osteomyelitis [42, 44]. The S53P4 is the most frequently utilised
BAG composition in current clinical practice . There is no clear explanation as per
why this particular glass is more extensively used in clinical practice compared to the
older 45S5 one. The main difference between these two BAGs results to be the slower
degradation rate of the S53P4 composition. Moreover, the extent of the antibacterial
capabilities of the 45S5 BAG are not yet fully understood.
In their review on the use of BAG S53P4, van Gestel et al. (2015) display
compelling evidence that support the efficacy of said BAG as a clinical treatment
for bone defects in a variety of scenarios achieving long-term outstanding results
[44]. Although complete resorption may not always be achieved, the use of S53P4
bioactive glass has shown significant benefits in craniofacial surgery applications
and the grafting of benign bone tumour defects, particularly in situations where
autologous bone grafting was hazardous or unfeasible. All in all, the use of the BAG

Background
Williams (1927) defined biomaterials as "a nonviable materials used in a medical
device, intended to interact with biological systems" [37, 50]. Research have been
broadly focused on the synthesis, characterisation , and foreign-body reaction of
biomaterials. The biological interaction between the host and the material is mea-
sured in terms of biocompatibility, which is defined as "the ability of a material
to perform with an appropriate host response in a specific application". It is cru-
cial for implantable biomaterials to be biocompatible., i.e. lead to an appropriate
host-material response [10, 23, 51]. In terms of tissue engineering, the clever use of
biomaterials is pivotal to develop ideal devices able to interact with biological systems
without resulting in negative body response. Scaffolds can be fabricated using a
variety of synthetic and natural materials, and their careful assembly. The purpose of
creating these scaffolds is the chance that cells will adhere, migrate, proliferate, and
differentiate on them creating new viable tissue. They provide a 3D solid support for
cell attachment, proliferation, migration, and differentiation, acting as reservoirs for
controlled release of bioactive molecules or drugs that aid in the regeneration process
[52]. Ideally, biomaterials should display suitable physicomechanical and engineering
properties depending on their intended applications. In addition, they should be eas-
ily functionalised with bioactive molecules if necessary [10, 23, 51]. The degradation
by-products of biomaterials used in tissue engineering should also be biocompatible
and non-toxic. The characteristics of the scaffold will ultimately regulate cellular
behaviour. Therefore, it is important to carefully fine tune the properties of each
scaffold according to the desired application. Ease of processability is another factor
that influence the choice of biomaterials. Both the internal structure and external
shape of the scaffolds should be obtained relatively easily and be replicable to a
certain extent. The scaffold should also be sterilisable using appropriate techniques.
The following paragraphs describe in more details the different types of biomateri-
als used in tissue engineering. Furthermore, cht, chs, and BAG are further described
both in terms of materials and in the specific field of bone tissue engineering. The
principles of bone tissue engineering and bone biology will be described briefly. Both
biological and structural requirements of porous scaffolds will be listed and examined.
The advantages and drawbacks of using foams is also addressed. Lastly, various
manufacturing processes for the creation of scaffolds are described, with emphasis on
3D-printing.

2.1
Biomaterials for Tissue Engineering
2.1.1
Bioactive Ceramics
Alumina, zirconia, calcium phosphates, bioactive glasses, glass ceramics, and
carbon are commonly used biomaterials in the group of ceramics [23]. In particular,
zirconia and aluminia are used in total joint prostheses and dental implants since
they are bioinert, strong, and have high wear resistance and low friction. On the
other hand, calcium phosphate-based ceramics, such as hydroxyapatite (HA) and
tricalcium phosphate, have received significant attention since they resemble the
inorganic composition of bone and display bioactive and osteocunductive properties
[23, 53]. These materials are defined as bioactive ceramics. HA, being the main
inorganic component of bone, is particularly employed in ceramic implants for
osteogenesis and plasma-sprayed titanium alloys [23, 37]. Tricalcium phosphates
(TCP) are also widely used as bone cement and implantable materials since they
are more soluble than HA. Both HA and TCP are commonly used either as porous
ceramics, or in combination with polymeric substrates, or as cements.
BAG are a set of bioactive ceramics containing different concentrations of SiO2,
Na2O, CaO, P2O5, and sometimes other compounds. Of these, silicon dioxide is the
predominant. BAG have been extensively used in the field of bone tissue engineering
due to their osteoinductive properties. When exposed to biological fluids, they
form a carbonated HA layer which creates an interface that promotes the binding
between the scaffold and the surrounding bone. Notably, the silica content in these
glasses influences their resorption rates and the formation of new bone. For instance,
Silica-rich scaffolds display better bone-forming abilities.
The mechanical fragility of ceramic remains the greatest shortcomings of these
biomaterials, particularly in case of load-bearing applications [23]. In spite of the
drawbacks, the extensive study of bioactive ceramics, especially HA, has yielded a
substantial body of literature highlighting their potential in bone healing and various
medical applications [37].
2.1.2
Decellularised Extracellular Matrix
Decellularised ECM is a promising biomaterial in the field of tissue engineering [23,
51]. It is the result of a long process where all the cells and nuclear components are
removed using various detergents. The result is an acellular matrix characterised by
important physical and chemical properties otherwise challenging to reproduce. The
ECM act as the main structural component for cells, being a complex net comprised
==PAGE:20==
12
of fibrous proteins, proteoglycans, and glycoproteins [23]. The ECM influence cells
migration, proliferation, differentiation, and functions by transmitting signals via
receptors on the cells surface. The use of decellularised ECM offers the advantage
of avoiding chronic inflammation, immunological reactions, and toxicity that are
often associated with synthetic polymers inertness. Research have been focusing
on both allogenic and xenogenic ECM [23, 51]. In particular, decellularised ECM
has already been used for repairing small intestinal submucosa, heart valves, and
skin bladder [51].There are currently several commercially available Food and Drug
Administration (FDA) approved options in terms of decellularised ECM, such as
Alloderm and LifeCell for the dermis, Synergraft and Cryolife as porcine heart valve,
Urinary Bladder Matrix (UBM) and Acell as porcine urinary bladder, and Axogen
and Acell as processed nerve allografts. In addition, decellularised ECM can be
used as powder component in hydrogels to include chemical cues that influence the
behaviour of the seeded cells.
The decellularisation approach is preferable for regenerative purposes since al-
logeneic and xenogeneic antigens commonly lead to detrimental inflammatory and
immunogenic response and, ultimately, rejection [51]. The decellularisation follows a
general protocol that consists physical, enzymatic and chemical degradation steps,
although the specific process is ultimately governed by the type of tissue . Despite
of the thorough cleaning of the ECM, it is almost impossible to wipe clean the
ECM and some traces of DNA and nuclear and cytoplasmic materials stay on the
matrix. Still, the decellularisation of ECMs is quite promising in the field of tissue
engineering. The utilisation and combination of single purified ECM components,
e.g. collagen, hyaluronic acid and fibrin, allows for the creation of more controlled
and uniform materials that would display a structure similar to the native ECM and
lower immunogenicity [23].
2.1.3
Polymers
Polymers are mainly composed of organic elements and are an important class of
biomaterials. They share macromolecular characteristics similar to key functional
components within the biological environment, such as lipids, proteins, and polysac-
charides[23, 54]. Their advantages are remarkable; namely simple processability
and chance to be utilised in a wide range of biomedical applications, ranging from
non-degradable to degradable implants and devices. In particular, degradable poly-
mers are of high interest in biomaterial applications such as drug delivery platforms,
degradable sutures, and temporary implants. Depending on their structure, they
==PAGE:21==
13
can degrade in diverse environments and rates without generating toxic by-products,
resulting in reduced recovery time, lower infection risks, and decreased healthcare
costs. Furthermore, the degradation rate of these polymers can be controlled to a
certain extent. The biocompatibility of the degradation components and the possible
side effects of fast and slow degradation have to be carefully assessed. However, it
is possible to design degradable polymers that produce non-toxic by-products by
altering their structure, chemistry, and functionality.
Biodegradable polymers can be either synthetic or natural[23, 54]. Generally,
synthetic polymers are advantageous in terms of controlled synthesis, tunable mate-
rial properties, and precise degradation. Natural materials, on the other hand, do
not tend to cause immunogenic nor display toxicity responses commonly associated
with synthetic polymers, i.e. they have a higher biocompatibility [55]. A comprehen-
sive understanding of synthetic and degradable polymers is crucial, including their
properties, degradation processes, design considerations, to obtain the desired char-
acteristics. In spite of their low immunogenic response, one of the main drawbacks
of biodegradable synthetic polymers remains their low bioactivity, which may lead
to an adverse immunogenic response and result in their rejection. Moreover, there
are concerns regarding the degradation mechanism of polymers like poly-l-lactic acid
(PLLA) and polyglycolic acid (PGA), which generates acidic monomers through
their hydrolysis leading to a decrease in local pH [56]. This acidic environment can
potentially cause necrosis in cells and tissues.
Synthetic Polymers
Non-degradable synthetic polymers are generally used as biomaterials when
mechanical and corrosion resistance are required[23]. These polymers are commonly
used in load-bearing surfaces for total joint arthroplasty, acetabular cups in hips, and
knee prostheses. On the other hand, biodegradable synthetic materials, especially
saturated aliphatic polyesters, are commonly used for the creation of porous scaffolds
[51].
The latter offer several advantages over natural materials. Firstly, biodegradable
synthetic polymers are quite easy to synthesise. Secondly, polymers like polylactide
(PLA), polyglycolide (PGA), and poly(caprolactone) (PCL) allow for precise control
over their mechanical properties and degradation rates. Consequently, they can be
tailored for specific applications. Lastly, they present chemical properties that lead to
their hydrolytic or oxidative degradation, specific rate and process depending on the
chemical structure and MW of the polymer, respectively. All in all, the utilisation
==PAGE:22==
14
of these biodegradable polymers and copolymers in the field of tissue engineering
is favoured due to their ease of synthesis, controllable degradability, and minimal
inflammatory response. Some of these biodegradable synthetic polymers can be
processed with techniques like electrospinning and 3D printing, which allow for the
creation of aligned structures that mimic native ECM. In addition, their versatility
is enhanced by the possibility of utilising them as hydrogels networks, which are
formed by crosslinking in solution. Another benefit is that some of these materials
are already approved by regulatory authorities like the FDA.
Natural Polymers
Natural polymers such as proteins and polysaccharides are extensively used in the
field of tissue engineering and drug delivery [23, 51, 55]. They offer several advantages,
namely high biocompatibility, non-toxicity, and biodegradability. In addition, they
have bioactive motifs that promotes local remodelling and the spreading of cells
[55]. Protein-based natural polymers like collagen, fibrin, gelatin, and silk fibroin
are commonly employed in various tissue engineering applications [51]. For instance,
collagen is the most abundant protein in the human body providing physical support
and signalling cues for cell behaviour. These characteristics make it suitable for
engineering artificial skin, bone, and cartilage. Collagraft is a composite of fibrillar
collagen, HA, and TCP approved by the FDA which is used as a biodegradable
bone graft substitute. Fibrin act as the structural component of blood clots and it
allows for cell migration during wound healing by providing a transitory matrix. It is
currently being studied for the regeneration of various tissues and as potential carrier
for bioactive molecules. Gelatin, a derived of collagen, has applications in the field
of cartilage, bone, and skin regeneration due to his minor antigenicity [23, 51]. In
addition, gelatin gels at low temperatures which makes it utilisable in extrusion-based
3D printing processes. Silk fibroin exhibits versatility in composite scaffolds due to
its renown high strength, low weight, and slow degradation.
Polysaccharides like chs, alginate and hyaluronic acid have also been extensively
studied in tissue engineering [23, 51]. Alginate is mainly obtained from brown seaweed
and is widely used due to its biocompatibility, gelation process, easy modification,
and its hydrogel texture resemblance to the native ECM one. Hyaluronic acid is
a polysaccharide present in cartilage and skin tissues that plays a structural role
and promotes tissue repair. While natural materials offer advantages, they also have
limitations such as purification challenges, high cost, low mechanical properties, and
difficulty in creating homogeneous and reproducible structures [51, 56]. Advances in
==PAGE:23==
15
the field have addressed the drawbacks associated with natural polymers, allowing for
the exploration of their full potential in developing biomaterials that closely mimic
the ECM [55].
2.2
Principles of Tissue Engineering for Bone Regeneration
As mentioned in the Introduction, autografts and allografts are commonly used
in the repair and regeneration of bone [57, 58]. Moreover, metallic pins, screws,
and ceramic-based particulate bone fillers are generally utilised in the treatment
of bone defects [5]. Their use however has several shortcomings mainly linked to
low availability and compatibility concerns [5, 57, 58]. The presence of osteoblasts,
osteocytes, osteoclasts, cytokines, hormones, growth factors, and bone morphogenetic
proteins (BMPs) contribute to the activation of metabolic pathways and tissue
regeneration in bone osteogenesis. Mimicking the structural organisation of natural
bone is crucial considering the invasion of soft/fibrous tissue before proper bone
regeneration is a common challenge with bone grafts, whether natural or synthetic
[2]. Synthetic TCP-based ceramics, BAG, and synthetic and natural polymers have
been extensively investigated for scaffold fabrication in bone tissue engineering [5, 57].
These materials exhibit osteoconductivity, high porosity, and appropriate mechanical
properties, allowing for cellular infiltration, attachment, nutrient diffusion, and bone
ingrowth. Scaffold design requires consideration of pore size, porosity, mechanical
strength, degradation rate, and the incorporation of biologics such as mesenchymal
stem cells (MSCs) and growth factors to enhance bone regeneration. The combination
of polymers and minerals in composite materials has shown promise as hard tissue
substitutes.
In osteogenic tissue engineering, the combination of poly(propylene fumarate)
(PPF) with ceramics such as HA or biomacromolecules like fibrin is commonly
employed to create scaffolds with enhanced bioactivity and mechanical strength [51].
The use of synthetic calcium phosphate-based ceramics, including biphasic calcium
phosphate, beta-tricalcium phosphate (β-TCP), and HA, has become popular due
to their similarity to the mineral composition of bone. Additionally, various other
ceramics such as bioactive glasses (BAG), glass-ceramics, silica, and titania have been
utilised. Synthetic polymers, including PCL, PLA, PGA, and poly(lactic-co-glycolic
acid) (PLGA), have garnered significant interest [5]. Furthermore, natural polymers
like alginate, chitosan, collagen, gelatin, silk fibroin, and glycosaminoglycans (GAGs)
have also been investigated.
Materials used as bone scaffolds have specific requirements to effectively fulfil
==PAGE:24==
16
their intended function, which will be further elaborated in the following paragraphs
[5]. First of all, the scaffold should be biodegradable and should not generate toxic
degradation by-product [43]. Secondly, they should be osteoconductive, facilitating
the recruitment and migration of osteogenic cells, while also serving as a template
for new bone formation. Furthermore, the scaffold must possess a highly porous
structure that allows for cellular infiltration, attachment, neovascularisation, bone
ingrowth, and efficient diffusion of oxygen, nutrients, and waste products [5]. The
interconnected porosity of the scaffold is crucial, with an ideal pore size ranging from
200 to 600 µm. In addition to this, microstructural features such as microporosity
or surface roughness can enhance the interactions between cells and the scaffold,
promoting cellular activities. The mechanical properties of the scaffold are also
of paramount importance.
It is necessary to strike a balance between scaffold
porosity and mechanical strength, as these two factors are closely intertwined. In
non-load-bearing defects, the scaffold should possess sufficient strength to maintain
its structural integrity during and after placement within the defect site. For polymer-
based scaffolds, the desired compressive strength typically falls within the range of
cancellous bone strength, which is generally reported as 1–12 MPa. Achieving this
strength level is critical to ensure the scaffold can effectively support the healing
process and withstand physiological stresses.
Overall, the principles of tissue engineering for bone regeneration involve the
implementation of innovative biomaterials, porous scaffolds, and appropriate me-
chanical and biodegradable properties to mimic the natural bone structure and
promote cellular functions necessary for successful bone tissue regeneration [2, 5, 40,
43, 57, 58]. By satisfying these material requirements, bone scaffolds can create an
optimal environment for tissue regeneration and contribute to successful bone tissue
engineering applications.
2.2.1
Bone Tissue Biology
Bone is the dynamic and mineral-rich component of the skeletal system [19]. It
is a fascinating and intricate tissue that exhibits a complex hierarchical structure,
consisting primarily of calcium phosphate apatite crystals and collagen fibres [5, 19].
The skeleton system has various functions, including providing mechanical support,
storing minerals, and protecting vital organs [19, 57]. Additionally, bone serves
as the primary source of mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) in the body and serves
as a site for blood cell production, specifically in the bone marrow in a process
called hematopoiesis. Bone tissue is vascularised and innervated. Bones are both
==PAGE:25==
17
rigid and flexible, allowing the attachment of muscles and enabling movement while
preventing fractures [57]. Depending on the place and function, bones are named flat,
long, short, and irregular. Long bones are larger and characterised by a diaphysis
(shaft), epiphyses (ends), metaphysis (area between epiphysis and diaphysis), and a
medullary cavity (filled with bone marrow). Short bones have similar dimensions
in diameter and length. They typically form movable joints. Flat bones are thicker
and have two layers which provides protection and support. Irregular bones, like the
name suggest, lack a regular shape.
Chemically wise, approximately 60% of bone mass consists of minerals, predomi-
nantly calcium, phosphate, and a small amount of carbonate [19]. The remaining
40% is made up by organic components within the ECM, mainly composed of type
I collagen and noncollagenous proteins like. These proteins also play a crucial role
in bone growth, development, remodelling, and repair. The inorganic matrix of
bone is mainly composed of HA crystals, along with bicarbonate, magnesium, potas-
sium, sodium, and citrate ions. The presence of these minerals contributes to the
mechanical strength and properties of bone.
Depending on its density, bone can be broadly categorised into two types [5, 19,
47, 57, 59]:
• cortical (compact) bone, which forms the outer part of bones, is enveloped
by a protective layer called the periosteum, and has a porosity range between
1-100 µm. It is characterised by regular sheets called lamellae. Thickness and
arrangement of these lamellae vary depending on the bone and its specific
location. Bones like femur that bear the weight of the body are characterised by
more abundant, thicker lamellae compared to the bones in the upper extremities.
Cortical bone is prevalent in the shaft of long bones, but it is also present at
the proximal and distal end of bone tissue in the inner part of the trabecular
bone. It counts for approximately 80% of the total skeletal mass.
• cancellous (trabecular/spongy) bone, is characterised by a mesh-like, highly
porous (100 to 500 µm), irregularly arranged lamellae and collagen fibres
structure. It is predominantly found in the metaphysis, epiphysis, and medullary
cavity at the ends of long bones, as well as in short bones. Spongy bone has
a larger surface area; therefore, it plays a vital role in the body like mineral
homeostasis and acid/base regulation.
Trabecular and cortical bone exhibit porosity, with trabecular bone having a porosity
range of 50% to 90%, while cortical bone has a lower porosity range of 3% to 12%
==PAGE:26==
18
[60].
Bone growth occurs longitudinally through the physis, which consists of cartilage
cells and matures after adolescence [5, 57]. The widening of bones occurs through
apophysis. The organic and inorganic ECM of bone can regenerate through the
activity of cells known as osteoclasts, osteoblasts, and osteocytes. These cells play
essential roles in maintaining bone structure and function. Specifically, osteoblasts
play a key role in bone formation by producing osteoid - an unmineralised matrix -
and regulating mineralisation. Osteocytes, which derives from osteoblasts, form a
complex cellular network within the bone structure. These cells act as mechanosensors
by detecting mechanical forces and directing remodelling activities through paracrine
signalling. Lastly, osteoclasts are responsible for resorbing and removing mineralised
bone tissue. They play an essential role in bone remodelling and maintaining the
integrity of the skeletal system.
2.3
Biological and Structural Requirements of 3D Porous
Scaffolds
Generally designing polymeric scaffolds requires for three specific biological and
structural requirements to be achieved [10, 12, 52]. Specifically, biocompatibility,
biodegradability are considered biological requirements, while porosity, mechanical
performance, and structural morphology are regarded as structural ones. Biocom-
patibility determines the suitability of the material for in vitro and in vivo use.
Physicochemical properties and bioactivity can be tailored to optimise tissue re-
generation depending on the targeted tissue. More specifically, scaffolds should
possess biodegradability, appropriate porosity, matching mechanical properties, and
structural similarity to the target tissue. In addition, the scaffold material should
stimulate neovascularisation, which is the formation of new blood vessels, all while
serving as a functioning template for new tissue formation for an appropriate period
of time.
Regarding scaffolds for bone tissue engineering, the materials used should also be
osteoconductive, osteoinductive, and osteogenic [19]. Osteoconduction refers to the
ability of the scaffold to promote the attachment and growth of osteoblasts on its
surface and throughout its structure, osteoinduction refers to the ability to stimulate
the differentiation of mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) into osteoblasts, ultimately
leading to the formation of mineralised tissue. Lastly, osteogenicity refers to the
ability of the scaffold to facilitate the formation of new bone tissue, even without
==PAGE:27==
19
the invasion of host cells. Osteogenicity itself is a property of cells, therefore, the
scaffolds can become osteogenic only if it is seeded with cells before implantation.
In the case of bone tissue engineering, high porosity is essential to facilitate
cellular infiltration, attachment, nutrient and waste diffusion, neovascularisation, and
bone ingrowth [5, 10]. The pores should not only form an interconnected network
but should also exhibit an optimal pore size. A pore size ranging from 120 to 325
µm is desired in terms of bone tissue engineering since it has been proven to enhance
osteoblast adhesion and proliferation. Features at microstructural level, like surface
roughness, can enhance cell-scaffold interactions. Balancing mechanical properties
and scaffold porosity is crucial, especially in the case non-load-bearing defects for
which the scaffolds should maintain the structural integrity.
Currently, one of the main challenge in tissue engineering is the ability of creating
vascularised system [42, 51, 61]. Commonly, oxygen and nutrients are supplied to
organs and tissues through a complex network of blood vessels and capillaries. Current
efforts to engineer vascularised tissues involve two main strategies: (i) promoting
neoangiogenesis using endothelial cells (ECs); (ii) focusing on the properties of
tubular structures using biologically derived or synthetic scaffolds [61].
2.3.1
Biocompatibility and Biodegradability
Biocompatibility and biodegradability are crucial factors to consider when de-
signing scaffolds for tissue engineering, specifically in case of bone regeneration [10].
Biocompatibility refers to the ability of the scaffold to function properly throughout
its intended use and while exhibiting minimal toxicity [10, 52]. In terms of biodegrad-
ability, it is pivotal that the rate of degradation is tuned to the regeneration of the
tissue [10, 12]. This requirement derives from the fact that bone undergoes constant
remodelling [19]. If the scaffold is nonresorbable or nondegradable, it would hinder
the natural reshaping process and prolong the time needed for the organ to regain its
natural function. Considering the requirements for the scaffolds to gradually degrade
and be replaced by native tissue, it is essential to assess the cytotoxicity of degraded
components [52]. Generally, scaffold degradation in vivo occurs through hydrolysis,
oxidation, or enzymatic activity, depending on its chemical structure. Biodegradable
materials often contain hydrolysable bonds such as esters and amides. Polymers
like PLGA, PCL, and polyurethanes (PUs) are an example. Natural polymers like
collagen, gelatin, and polysaccharides undergo enzymatic degradation by cell-secreted
proteases present in the ECM.
Considering the degradation rate of scaffolds should match the regeneration rate
==PAGE:28==
20
of surrounding tissue, it is advantageous to use polymers since this property can be
modulated by adjusting the hydrophobicity and MW [10, 12, 52]. Overall, achieving
biocompatibility and biodegradability is essential for successful scaffold design in
bone tissue engineering .
2.3.2
Porosity, Structural Morphology, and Mechanical Parameters
Porosity
Porosity can be defined as the the amount of empty space relative to the volume
of the structure [10]. It is a crucial characteristic of scaffolds in tissue engineering.
Being able to fine tune the porous 3D architectures of scaffolds while maintaining
their structural integrity is a crucial aspect in their design. Additionally, it is essential
for the pores to form an interconnected network in order to allow nutrient diffusion,
waste removal, cell infiltration, and blood vessel growth, ultimately enabling the
formation of new tissues. Scaffolds which exhibit both high porosity and a hierarchical
morphology have proved to promote early stage osteoinduction.
The size of the pores influences cell behaviour, osteoinduction, and the growth of
blood vessels. Studies have shown that scaffolds with larger pores ranging from 200
to 600 µm promote better osteoblast adhesion and proliferation, although the highest
number of cells are detected at mean pore sizes ranging from 120 to 325µm [5, 10].
Moreover, pore size should not be smaller than 30-40 µm, which is the threshold
for nutrient diffusion, and pores ranging in sizes between 160 and 270 µm enhance
neovascularisation. However, a balance must be struck between pore size and specific
surface area to achieve optimal nutrient flow and ligand binding density [9].
In bone tissue engineering, scaffolds with interconnected porosity above 90% and
diameters between 300-500 µm are generally recommended for cell penetration, tissue
ingrowth, vascularisation, and nutrient delivery [12, 52]. Scaffold degradation rate
is also influenced by pore size and wall thickness. A highly porous scaffold with
interconnected pores offers a large surface area for cell attachment, oxygen, and
nutrient transport, as well as waste removal [62].
Different pore sizes trigger diverse tissue responses, with bone in-growth favouring
pores larger than 300 µm. Maintaining a balance between pore size and surface area
is critical for cellular adhesion, infiltration, and vascularisation. The optimal pore size
for bone tissue engineering is still debated, but it appears that pores exceeding 300
µm contribute to osteogenesis and capillary formation [10]. However, the mechanical
properties of the scaffold can be compromised by highly porous structures and
excessively large pores.
==PAGE:29==
21
Overall, scaffold pore structure is a key factor in tissue engineering, impacting
not only cell behaviour and nutrient flow, but also scaffold degradation [60].
Structural Morphology
The structural characteristics of scaffolds play a crucial role in promoting cellular
functions in tissue engineering [10, 60]. One important aspect is scaffold roughness,
which has been found to influence protein adsorption and cell adhesion [10]. Smooth
surfaces tend to stimulate the differentiation of multipotent cells into specific cell
types like fibroblasts, while rough surfaces promote the differentiation of cells involved
in bone formation. This highlights the significance of tailoring scaffold surfaces to
guide cell behaviour. On a microscale level, surface roughness of implants contributes
to the process of bone healing.
The macrotopography of scaffolds plays a crucial role in implant integration
and long-term stability [60]. Various visible geometries, including ledges, flutes,
indentations, and grooves, can be incorporated to enhance integration and improve
scaffold stability. Increased roughness has been shown to promote cell and tissue
integration, as well as enhance interfacial and shear strength. By increasing the
surface area of the scaffolds through texture and roughness, there is more space for
cells to attach and facilitate tissue genesis.
In addition to surface roughness, surface coating is often employed to enhance
the interaction between cells and synthetic scaffolds [52]. By applying a chemical
treatment, the scaffold surface can be functionalised with active domains that facilitate
the attachment of bioactive molecules, such as minerals for osteoconduction and
osteointegration, ECM components, and drug molecules.
Considering bone tissue engineering, being able to cast or create a scaffold that
match the measures of the targeted defect is as essential as designing its microstruc-
ture. In summary, both scaffold design and implant macrotopography have significant
impacts on cellular functions and integration. Scaffold roughness and surface coat-
ing influence cell behaviour and enhance cell-scaffold interactions, while implant
macrotopography facilitates implant integration and stability. These considerations
are essential in promoting successful tissue engineering and favourable implantation
outcomes.
Mechanical Parameters
In addition to their structural characteristics, the mechanical performance of
scaffolds is also very important [10]. The choice of biomaterials plays a decisive role
==PAGE:30==
22
in achieving good mechanical performances. Additionally, it is essential to achieve
a balance between porosity and mechanical strength since increased porosity can
compromise mechanical properties [5, 10]. Research has demonstrated that as pore
size and overall porosity decrease, the compressive strength of scaffolds tends to
increase. This can be attributed to the higher density and thickness of pore walls in
scaffolds with smaller pores. Conversely, scaffolds with larger and more interconnected
pores exhibit a higher void volume, leading to lower mechanical strength. Furthermore,
the degradation rate of a scaffold is closely linked to its mechanical strength, with the
strength decreasing as the scaffold undergoes degradation. To enhance mechanical
stability while allowing biodegradation, various covalent and noncovalent crosslinking
techniques can be employed.
It has been proven that the stiffness of the substrate significantly impacts cell
function (Figure 6) [52, 63, 64]. Cell migration and proliferation are also influenced by
substrate stiffness, with different cell lineages responding differently to varying stiffness
conditions. Specifically, microenvironment stiffness influences the commitment and
differentiation of MSCs, with softer matrices promoting neurogenic or myogenic
differentiation and stiffer substrates supporting osteogenic differentiation.
When it comes to polymer-based scaffolds, the target compressive strength
typically falls within the cancellous bone range (1-12 MPa) [5].

2.4
Manufacturing Techniques
As aforementioned, the characteristics of a scaffold play a pivotal role in tissue
engineering by producing the matrix and promoting various cellular functions such
as attachment, differentiation, and maturation [10, 52]. The high level of control over
scaffold porosity allows for enhanced tissue regeneration, enabling cells to migrate
throughout the material beyond its surface limitations. These features surpass the
capabilities of traditional grafts and metal implants.
Fabrication methods for constructing porous scaffolds in tissue engineering have
evolved greatly and now a variety of options is available, (e.g. electrospinning, phase
==PAGE:31==
23
separation, freeze drying, and self-assembly) [10, 16]. These methods create scaffolds
with nanoscale architecture that, in case of fibrous biomimetic, can mimic the native
ECM while providing an initial space for the regeneration of new tissue [16].
The design and properties of scaffolds heavily depend on the relationship between
structure, processing method, and material properties relationships. It is crucial to
understand this relationship in order to obtain constructs that meet both biological
and structural requirements of tissue engineering [10].
Traditional approaches include methods like particulate leaching, freeze drying,
gas infusion, and phase separation are used for the developing of porous scaffolds [51].
These techniques generally involve casting the scaffold in moulds with specific designs
to generate interconnected pores. The introduction of particles or bubbles that are
then removed is often used as a technique to obtain interconnected porosity. However,
these methods have limitations such as uncontrolled pore size and connectivity, weak
mechanical strength, and residual solvents or porogens. An alternative approach is
hydrocarbon templating, which allows for control over scaffold thickness and pore
structure. However, it is complicated to get rid of the organic solvents used in this
process, which poses challenges for biological applications.
More novel methods include extrusion-based techniques, like 3D printing and
electrospinning which enable more control over the architecture of the scaffolds [10].
Using these methods, it is possible to obtain scaffolds with interconnected macropores,
a beneficial aspect in terms of nutrient diffusion.
2.4.1
Porogen Templating
Porogen templating is a technique in which gas bubbles are generated within an
aqueous dispersion through high intensity stirring or air sparging [10]. The obtained
foam is then gelated or cured. Solvent exchange is often used as an intermediate
step followed by oven drying. This method produces foams with average pore sizes
of approximately 200 µm without the need of using solvents [10, 52].
Porogen templating is a simple and scalable process compared to other methods,
the lack of control over the morphology of the bubbles lead to lack of control the
size and shape of the formed pores [10]. Furthermore, it is important to prevent
coalescence or partitioning of the unstable gas bubbles during scaffold formation and
drying to maintain mechanical stability. Anisotropic materials helps to minimise
coalescence compared to suspensions of isotropic particles.
==PAGE:32==
24
2.4.2
Gas Foaming
Gas foaming is a technique similar to porogen templating [52]. Specifically, in
gas foaming the expansion of a gas is used to create highly porous scaffolds. The gas
commonly used in this method is carbon dioxide (CO2) due to its safety, reusability,
and cost-effectiveness. The advantage of using CO2 is that it can reach a supercritical
state at relatively mild temperature and pressure conditions.
The process of gas foaming entails the saturation of the solution with CO2
[52].
Upon controlled depressurisation, CO2 nucleates and expands.
Pores are
left behind as the gas is evacuated. Size and density of the pores can be altered
by manipulating the temperature, pressure, and rate of depressurisation.
The
modification of depressurisation rate influences the creation of dual pore populations
at the nano- and microscale. The mild processing temperature of gas foaming enables
the incorporation of biological molecules in the scaffolds.
2.4.3
Freeze Drying, Emulsion Freeze-Drying, and Critical Point Dry-
ing
Freeze-drying is another solvent-free method for creating highly porous scaffolds
by freezing and sublimating an aqueous or organic solution under low temperature
and pressure conditions. Unlike processes involving high temperatures, freeze-drying
allows for the inclusion of heat-sensitive components in the scaffold [16, 52]. The
structure of the scaffold can be adjusted by varying the parameters of the solution
used to cast the frozen mould. Complex architectures can be achieved by controlling
moulds and processing parameters, including the growth speed and orientation of ice
crystals, enabling the definition of pore orientation and the creation of unidirectional
porous scaffolds.
Cooling rate and freezing temperature have a great effect on the final structure of
the scaffolds [52]. Rapid cooling rates lead to more uniform pore structures, whereas
lower freezing temperatures decrease the average pore size. Consequently, annealing,
can increase the mean pore size since it involves increasing the temperature of the
frozen material. The size of the pores can also be regulated by polymer concentration
and molecular weight.
Emulsion freeze-drying is an alternative approach that allows for the preparation of
scaffolds with specific pore surface areas by dissolving a polymer solvent, emulsifying
it with a nonsolvent, pouring the resulting emulsion into a mould, and quenching it
at a low temperature before freeze-drying to remove the solvents [52]. Effectively
==PAGE:33==
25
producing hierarchical structures remains a challenge with this method.
Critical point drying is a technique that involves the dehydration of the sample
in ethanol and its subsequent replacement with liquid CO2. The supercritical CO2 is
then released through heating [65]. This method is faster than freeze-drying.
2.4.4
Solvent Casting Particulate Leaching and Gel Casting
Solvent-casting particulate leaching (SCPL) is a cost-effective method for creating
highly porous scaffolds [52]. It involves the dispersion of insoluble porogens and the
subsequent solvent evaporation, forming a composite material that is finally washed
to remove the porogen particles. SCPL allows for the production of scaffolds with
high porosity and controllable pore architecture. However, it can result in poor
interconnectivity and the use of organic solvents limits bioactive molecule incorpora-
tion. The mechanical properties of the scaffold resulting from these techniques are
generally below the requirements of load-bearing tissues.
Gel casting is an alternative technique that uses aqueous polymeric solutions ,
foaming, and in-situ polymerisation to create scaffolds with porosity above 90%, high
interconnectivity, and strong mechanical properties [52]. Gel casting is suitable for
polymeric, ceramic, and composite scaffolds, offering a versatile approach in tissue
engineering applications.
2.4.5
Phase Separation
Phase separation is a technique used in the fabrication of porous scaffolds for tissue
engineering applications [16, 52]. This process can be categorised into solid-liquid
phase separation and liquid-liquid phase separation [16]. There are two approaches
to achieve phase separation: the addition of a nonsolvent and the thermally induced
phase separation (TIPS) method [52]. The addition of a nonsolvent leads to scaffolds
with heterogeneous pore structures. In contrast, the TIPS method involves the
destabilisation of a polymeric solution through small temperature changes, resulting
in a phase separation process that yields a phase polymer rich and one polymer-lean
[16, 52]. The polymer-rich phase solidifies to form a matrix, while the polymer-lean
phase transforms into pores upon solvent removal.
The TIPS method offers control over the morphology and microporous structures
of the scaffolds by adjusting process parameters and polymer characteristics (e.g.
polymer concentration, MW, solvent/nonsolvent ratio, cooling path) [16]. This
technique is particularly attractive for cell spreading and migration due to its ability
to achieve high interconnectivity. Various synthetic and natural polymers, either
==PAGE:34==
26
alone or in combination, can be used to create highly porous scaffolds with controlled
structures [52].
Phase separation methods have been applied to fabricate synthetic polymeric
nanofibers for tissue engineering [52]. Phase separation holds great potential for
fabricating 3D nanofibrous scaffolds with uniform pore structures through dual or
multiple phase separation processes, offering advantages over processes like electro-
spinning [16]. At present, the biggest drawbacks are the limited material selection
and poor final resolution.
2.4.6
Electrospinning
Electrospinning utilises high voltages to stretch polymer solutions into nanofibers,
resulting in porous scaffolds with a high surface area [2, 10, 51, 52, 66]. During
the electrospinning process, high voltage is applied to a capillary tube filled with a
polymer solution or melt. The charge repulsion within the solution or melt overcomes
surface tension, forming a jet that solidifies into fibres as the solvent evaporates [16].
Electrospinning enables the creation of fibrous structures similar to natural collagen
fibrils and offers control over fibre orientation [2, 10, 51, 52, 66]. However, controlling
the distance between fibres, which impacts cell migration, can be challenging. In
addition, it is still challenging to obtain complex structures and homogeneous pore
distribution.
By adjusting process parameters and polymer solution properties, the structure
of the scaffolds can be modified [13, 52]. Parameters such as polymer concentration,
applied voltage, and needle-to-collector distance can be adjusted to modify the
diameter and morphology of the fibres. Intricate porous structures can be achieved
using a movable collector plate and software templates. Both natural and synthetic
polymers can be used. While solution electrospinning is commonly employed to
create degradable scaffolds mimicking the ECM and serving various purposes, it
requires potent solvents that can affect sensitive molecules [66]. Melt electrospinning
is a type of electrospinning that eliminates the need for solvents, reducing toxicity
and simplifying post-processing [52].
2.5
3D Bioprinting
3D bioprinting is a promising technology that allows the precise layer-by-layer
deposition of living cells and biomaterials to create functional tissues and organs [10,
67, 68]. It utilises computer-aided design (CAD) and fabrication techniques to achieve
==PAGE:35==
27
high precision and spatial control in tissue engineering. It has the advantage of
precise control over the spatial organisation of cellular components and biomaterials,
enabling the fabrication of complex tissue structures.
The process involves pre-processing, processing, and post-processing [67]. In the
pre-processing phase, imaging data is transformed into a blueprint for bioprinting
using specialised software. The processing phase involves selecting a printing method
and formulating a combination of materials which influence the interaction and final
outcome of the printed tissue. The post-processing phase includes steps to mature
the bioprinted tissue, often using bioreactors. Successful bioprinting relies on three
key variables: the cellular component, the scaffolding or supportive biomaterials, and
the bioprinting device [10, 67]. The biomaterials used serve as a bridge between the
cells, bioinks, and hardware.
Overall, 3D printing techniques are significantly advancing the fabrication of
tissue-engineered scaffolds, providing enhanced complexity, patient-specificity, and
reproducibility [52].
2.5.1
Extrusion Printing
Extrusion printing is a 3D printing process that involves the continuous extrusion
of shear-thinning, thixotropic materials, which can be either biomacromolecules or
cells, through a nozzle using pneumatic pressure [68]. While extrusion printing is
a cost-effective method for printing constructs with encapsulated cells, its printing
resolution is constrained by factors such as the diameter of the print head, the
viscosity of the biomaterial, and the size of the incorporated biological components.
The use of shear-thinning biomaterials is limited as they need to maintain their
strand shape during the fabrication process. Compatible bioinks for this deposition
method include gelatin, fibrin, alginate, mixtures of hyaluronic acid (HA), and
mixtures of silicate nanoplatelets. This method allows for the engineering of chemically
and structurally complex tissues more easily by combining multiple materials through
different printing cartridges.
2.5.2
Inkjet printing
Inkjet printers, utilise controlled small volumes of biomaterials to obtain 3D
structures [68]. Known as drop-on-demand printers, inkjet bioprinters employ non-
contact techniques to deposit successive drops of bioink onto a substrate, replicating
a computer-aided design (CAD) project. These printers offer high resolution in
bioink deposition and fast printing speeds. However, the process of droplet synthesis
==PAGE:36==
28
through thermal or mechanical means can potentially damage cells due to instant
heat exposure and shear stress.
Inkjet bioprinting offers advantages such as high speed, availability, and relatively
low costs [68]. However, compared to other bioprinting methods, it lacks precision
in droplet size and placement. The requirement for low viscosity bioink also limits
the range of suitable bioinks that can be used with this method. Additionally,
inkjet bioprinters have been associated with issues like nozzle clogging and cellular
distortion.
2.5.3
Stereolithography
The stereolithography (SLA) technique involves the use of photopolymerisation
[52, 68]. Specifically, UV light or laser is directed over a liquid polymer that can be
crosslinked to form a solid layer. This layer-by-layer approach allows for the creation
of a 3D structure by gradually lowering the printing platform into the polymer
solution. This technique allows for high-resolution patterning. SLA is particularly
advantageous when using curable acrylics and epoxies as the photopolymerisable
material.
At present, the main limitation of SLA is the requirement for intense ultraviolet
radiation during the crosslinking process, a lengthy post-processing time, and limited
material choice [68].

Chitin Nanofibres
α-cht nanofibres were obtained from previously purified cht from the shells of

a microfluidiser using a single pass at 1500 bar pressure. The obtained concentration
of the dispersed chitin nanofibres was 0.4 wt%. The solution was further concentrated

Rheology
To analyse the rheology of formulations containing different concentrations of
BAG (5wt% and 50wt%), oscillatory tests were carried out at two temperatures
(25°C and 37°C). This was also done to assess the elastic and viscous behaviour of
the formulations for the possible application in 3D printing (Figure 18). During an
oscillatory test, the response of a sample over time can be evaluated by changing the
frequency of the applied strain within a deformation range [74]. Storage (G’) and
loss (G”) moduli are recorded over the frequency. G’ indicates the amount of stored
deformation energy, while G” describes the deformation energy that is dissipated
through internal friction during flow [75]. Higher frequencies indicate shorter time
scales, while lower frequencies indicate longer time scales. The frequency test offers
insights into both behaviour and internal structure of the material. Hence, it allows
a comparison between crosslinked and uncrosslinked polymers. In particular, the
analysis was carried to assess the crossover point of the formulations. The crossover
point is critical in rheological analysis since it refers to the point where the formulation
transitions from a liquid state (where G’<G") to a solid state (where G’>G") [75,
76]. For instance, it is the point at which the storage modulus (G’) and the loss
modulus (G”) become equal or start to intersect. In terms of 3D printability, this
point is crucial as it determines the formulation ability to be extruded effectively.
The 3D printing, specifically direct ink writing (DIW), operates by extruding and
depositing a material through a nozzle onto a substrate [77]. The inks used can be
liquid, viscous, or viscoelastic. The ability to retain a shape upon printing depends
on the mechanical properties of the material. To print 3D structures, viscoelastic
materials are employed to ensure shape fidelity. Viscoelastic materials exhibit solid-
like or elastic behaviour when no stress is applied but rearrange when subjected to
stress due to the interactions between particles and polymers. The shear stress (τ)
experienced by the material is determined by the force (F) exerted over the area
(A), resulting in strain (γ) that depends on the height of the material (h) and total
deflection (s). The mechanical properties of the material become apparent when
applying shear stress at different shear rates (γ̇). The viscosity (η) is influenced
by both shear stress and shear rate. The printability of an ink is influenced by its
shear-thinning and thixotropic behaviour. The initial viscosity of the ink determines
the stress required for it to flow. This shear stress increases with higher pressure and
larger nozzle inner diameter. In this case, fluid shear stress is mainly influenced by
pressure and nozzle geometry, whereas the viscosity of the ink plays a negligible role.
However, higher viscosity inks require higher pressures for extrusion, while lower
==PAGE:53==
45
viscosity and shear-thinning inks, combined with nozzles with larger inner diameters
(>200 µm), can be extruded at lower pressures, resulting in lower fluid shear stress.

(when G’ and G” are independent on the strain) to perform the oscillation frequency
are displayed in Appendix A8.

4.2
Discussion
As described in the Background, chs is more widely utilised as a biomaterial in
tissue engineering than cht. For the present study, cht was chosen as the base material
for the scaffolds due to superior mechanical properties shown by the obtained cht
nanofibres [48]. The cht fibres exhibited a notably high electrostatic charge, with
a ζ-potential of +95 ± 3.5 mV, leading to remarkably stable colloidal suspensions
==PAGE:54==
46
in water [48]. Moreover, the process to obtain the cht nanofibres results in fibres
with lengths of approximately 50 µm and widths as narrow as 50 nm with. The
aspect ratio of this fibres reached values as high as 500, which is one of the largest
obtainable in terms of nanomaterials. The aspect ratio (s) is defined as the ratio of
length to diameter of the fibre. The increase in aspect ratio is generally linked to
the increase in both compressive and tensile strength of the composite. The aspect
ratio is directly connected with the stress transfer length, which is described as the
length over which the strain in the fibre builds up to a plateau value, i.e. the load is
carried more efficiently. The aspect ratio of the fibre largely influences the elastic
stress transfer [78, 79].
Chs is subsequently added to enhance the compatibility and bioactivity of the
scaffolds. Being more hydrophobic, cht would supposedly stabilise bubbles quite well
in the system by itself. However, it is possible that chs acts as further Pickering
stabilising agent [80–82]. Figure 19 illustrate the theoretical structure of cht, chs,
and BAG. In this scenario, cht nanofibres form the primary network structure
of the scaffolds, providing mechanical support and stability to the overall system,
whereas chs surrounds the lamellae of the pores [80–83]. By doing so, chs acts as a
stabilising agent, helping to further prevent the aggregation and coalescence of the
cht nanofibres. The presence of chs in the interstitial spaces and around the lamellae
of the pores enhances the stability of the scaffolds by providing a protective layer,
reducing the surface tension, and facilitating better dispersion of the cht nanofibres
within the aqueous solution. Overall, this theory suggests that the combined presence
of cht and chs allows for the formation of stable and structured scaffolds, with cht
forming the main network and chs stabilising it. Furthermore, colloids typically
exhibit percolation at reduced mass fractions, facilitating both material structuring
and enhancing its strength [84].
Assessing the flow properties, mechanical strength, and microstructural charac-
teristics ensures that the chosen formulation meets the specific requirements and
performance expectations of the intended application. Considering the possibility of
employing the foam formulations in 3D printing, the viscosity of the wet state plays
a vital role in the formulation application and handling. A lower viscosity indicates a
more fluid consistency, making it easier to work with and apply. It allows for better
flow and penetration into substrates, ensuring uniform coverage. On the other hand,
a higher viscosity may offer improved stability and reduced shrinkage. Ultimately,
specific viscosities can be targeted depending on the requirements of the application
and the desired properties of the final product.

4.2.1
Uniaxial Compressive Test
Compressive testing offers valuable insights into the mechanical strength of
materials and their ability to withstand compression forces. Higher compressive
strength signifies enhanced load-bearing capacity and resistance to deformation. This
criterion becomes particularly critical in applications where materials are subjected
to heavy loads or pressure. Furthermore, evaluating compressive test results aids in
determining the suitability of formulations for appropriate cellular differentiation, as
discussed in the Background section.
Based on the mechanical results presented in Figures 11a and 12a, increasing
concentrations of cht and chs resulted in progressively stiffer and stronger samples.
Consequently, the formulations containing the most concentrated cht fibres in a 1:1
ratio with chs (0.6wt% cht and 0.6wt% chs) were chosen as the BF, with E of 170
kPa and yield strength of 25 kPa. To further enhance the mechanical properties
of the scaffolds, MC and HPMC were added to the formulations. However, when
adding MC and HPMC, a significant increase in viscosity was observed, leading to
the easier entrapment of bubbles in the system. Samples with high amounts of MC
and HPMC (up to 6 times the amount of cht) became challenging to handle due
to their high viscosity, resulting in scaffolds that were too strong for analysis by
the texture analyser device. Therefore, these samples were excluded from further
analysis. Lower concentrations of the crosslinkers (50wt% and 100wt%) yielded
more compliant samples that did not overload the device. As depicted in Figure
11b, HPMC led to stiffer samples, with the modulus doubling when the crosslinker
concentration was doubled. Regarding strength (Figure 12b), higher concentrations
of HPMC resulted in stronger samples compared to those containing MC, whereas
at lower concentrations, samples with HPMC exhibited greater strength. However,
==PAGE:56==
48
samples with HPMC as the crosslinker exhibited less fluctuation compared to those
with MC. This behaviour is likely attributed to MC being a stronger foaming agent
than HPMC, resulting in increased porosity, which in turn decreases the mechanical
properties of the samples. Consequently, HPMC was ultimately selected as the
crosslinker for the addition of BAG.
BAG was added to both the BF and a formulation with a constant concentration
of HPMC (25wt%). The wet systems remained stable for only two days due to
the pH increase following the addition of BAG, resulting in phase separation due
to fibre aggregation. Scaffolds with 5wt% and 25wt% BAG were less strong but
stiffer than their HPMC-containing counterparts, while the addition of 50wt% BAG
resulted in both stronger and stiffer scaffolds (Figures 11c and 12c). This behaviour
may be attributed to the increased porosity in samples containing HPMC, which
would subsequently lead to weaker mechanical properties compared to the denser
structure formed by formulations without the crosslinker [5, 10]. Considering these
results, formulations with only BAG were selected for the further addition of gas
bubbles.
Overall, the addition of gas bubbles decreased both the stiffness and
strength of the samples (Figures 11d and 12d). This outcome was expected due to
the increased porosity of the scaffolds. N2O resulted in smaller bubbles compared
to CO2. Mechanical results were similar between samples with high concentrations
of BAG, with scaffolds containing N2O being slightly stronger (13 kPa) and stiffer
(72 kPa) than their counterparts. Except for the scaffold with 5wt% BAG and
CO2, the increase in BAG concentrations did not seem to significantly influence the
mechanical properties of the scaffolds when gas was added. However, it is worth
noting that scaffolds with low concentrations of BAG and CO2 displayed considerable
fluctuations. To obtain a comprehensive understanding of the mechanical results for
this particular sample, further analysis of additional scaffolds is required. Overall, all
scaffolds, except those with the addition of gas bubbles, exhibited a stiffness in the
range of collagenous bone 6. Despite the lower mechanical properties of the scaffolds
obtained with the addition of gas bubbles, these systems still exhibited a compressive
modulus higher than 30 kPa, which is should promote cellular differentiation toward
bone tissue [52, 63, 64].
4.2.2
SEM for Microstucture and Bioactivity
Ideally, the scaffolds should display an environment of interconnected pores.
While SEM imaging is a valuable technique for analysing the microstructure of
porous scaffolds, X-ray micro-computed tomography can give more information on
==PAGE:57==
49
the interconnectivity of the structure. In this study, SEM was used to visually assess
the porosity of the scaffolds.
Generally, an increase in the concentration of cht and chs resulted in scaffolds
with reduced porosity (Appendix A6a and A6b). This decrease in porosity can be
attributed to the denser packing of the cht and chs fibres at higher concentrations,
limiting the formation of larger pores. MC and HPMC were added to the formula-
tions to enhance the structure and porosity of the foams other than enhancing the
mechanical properties.
The addition of MC (Appendix A6c and A6d) and HPMC (Appendix A6e and
A6f) improved the scaffolds structure by promoting the formation of more porous
architecture . SEM images revealed a more open and porous architecture compared
to formulations without MC and HPMC. Notably, scaffolds containing 50wt% of
MC and HPMC exhibited more suitable foam structures compared to formulations
with 100wt% crosslinker. The addition of BAG in foams containing HPMC resulted
in foams with well-defined and interconnected porosity characterised by pores of
various sizes. (Appendix A6g and A6h). In contrast, the scaffolds with BAG but
without HPMC did not exhibit such well-defined and interconnected porous structures
(Appendix A6i and A6j).
This prompted the addition of gas to introduce stable bubbles in the systems,
thereby enhancing the overall porosity and pore connectivity. Generally, by utilising
gas bubbles the foams achieved a more desirable porosity. As expected, the scaffolds
obtained with the use of N2O, exhibited a highly porous and heterogeneous structure,
whereas the scaffolds with CO2 were characterised by a less porous, more uniform
architecture. The porosity of the scaffolds should be further assessed in order to
understand if it could host the attachment and growth of cells. Considering the
mechanical properties of these scaffolds, it would be interesting to understand if
the porosity obtained by forcing bubbles in the system could overcome their lower
stiffness.
SEM imaging of scaffold immersed in SBF for 7 days at 37 °C exhibited the
typical cauliflower-like structure and crystals of HA (Figure 14) [13, 85]. The surface
coverage of HA was more homogeneous in samples with higher concentrations of
BAG (Figures 14a and 14b) compared to lower amount of BAG (Figures 14c and
14d). Interestingly, scaffolds with 5wt% of BAG resulted in more developed HA
crystals compared to the scaffolds with 50wt%. To further assess the presence of HA,
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XRD) could be used [13]. This method could
also give further insight into the surface’s chemical bonding and structures of the
==PAGE:58==
50
scaffolds before immersion in SBF.
4.2.3
FT-IR
FT-IR analysis offers valuable insights into the chemical composition and bonding
characteristics of materials. In the case of chitin-chitosan-BAG systems, a comparative
analysis of FT-IR spectra for different compositions can provide significant information
about their molecular structures and the incorporation of BAG.
The samples containing chs and cht exhibit characteristic peaks commonly as-
sociated with these polysaccharides [48, 86, 87]. Specifically, broad peaks can be
observed between 3500-3200 cm−1, corresponding to O-H and N-H stretching vi-
brations. Peaks between 3000-2800 cm−1 represent symmetrical and asymmetrical
stretching vibrations of CH2 in polysaccharides. The amide I bands are found be-
tween 1700-1600 cm−1, while a peak in the range of 1600-1500 cm−1 corresponds to
the amide II band. Additionally, three peaks between 1200-1000 cm−1 are related
to asymmetric C-O-C stretching, symmetric C-O stretching, and C-O-C bridge
vibrations, respectively. According to Liu et al., prominent peaks between 2950 and
2930 cm−1 (C-H stretching) provide strong evidence of chemical crosslinking between
the cht nanofibres [49].
Comparing the FT-IR spectra of BF+BAG systems with 50wt% and 5wt% BAG,
differences can be observed in the peaks associated with BAG incorporation [13]. One
noteworthy region to focus on is the Si-O-Si stretching region, typically occurring in
the range of 1000-1100 cm−1. Enhanced peaks within this frequency range indicate
the presence of the silicate network, which can be observed in both samples containing
BAG and serves as evidence of successful incorporation of glass in the cht-chs matrix
(higher peak in case of 50wt% BAG compared to 5wt% BAG). In contrast, when
analysing the FT-IR spectrum of the BAG powder itself, characteristic peaks related
to Si-O-Si stretching vibrations are expected to be more prominent and well-defined.
This is due to the BAG powder predominantly consisting of silicate compounds.
4.2.4
ICP-OES
The presence of the additional phase of BAG is essential for effective bone
regeneration since cht and chs framework alone does not provide ions that kick-
start bone growth. The results of the ICP-OES spectroscopy confirmed a series of
events involving the BAG that aligns with the findings described by Ferreira et al.
about the mechanism of mineralisation [13]. As described in the Background, BAG
forms a hydrated surface layer when in contact with bodily fluids or water, which
==PAGE:59==
51
facilitates the exchange of ions between the glass and the surrounding environment.
This ion exchange process helps initiate a series of biological reactions, including the
formation of a biologically active HA layer on the surface of the glass, which promotes
integration with surrounding tissues [12, 22]. The release profiles of Na+ and Ca2+
are similar to the one described by Ferreira et al. in their article [13]. In their
analysis, the ions reached a plateau around 12 hours after immersion, suggesting that
the vitreous structure of the mineral phase within the scaffolds was maintained. The
subsequent step of mineralisation involved the loss of soluble Si and the consequent
formation of a gel layer rich of SiO2. It is plausible that the BAG behaviour of
the scaffolds analysed in the present study would behave in a similar manner. It is
noteworthy that the composition of the BAG utilised in the present study and the
one used by Ferreira and colleagues slightly differ [13]. In particular, BAG S53P4
has a higher SiO2 concentration and lower concentrations of the other components.
The lower concentrations of P2O5 is probably the cause why it was not traced in
the samples analysed. However, it is possible that P ions could be detected after a
longer submersion time.
4.2.5
DSC-TGA
The DSC curves provide insights into the thermal behaviour and phase transitions
of the samples, while the DTG curves reveal the decomposition and weight loss
patterns.
In the DSC curves, characteristic peaks and endothermic or exothermic transitions
can be observed. These peaks represent specific thermal events such as melting,
crystallisation, or degradation. The temperatures at which these events occur provide
information about the thermal stability and behaviour of the samples.
The DTG curves, derived from the TG data, show the rate of weight loss as a
function of temperature. Peaks in the DTG curves indicate the temperature ranges at
which significant weight loss or decomposition occurs. These peaks can be associated
with the degradation of the biopolymers or glass. Observing Figure 17b, the peaks
below 200 °are related to the evaporation of water moisture physically adsorbed on
the surface of the scaffolds [13]. This could be explained by the hygroscopic nature
of cht and chs. This peak is lower for samples containing 50wt% BAG suggests
chemical interaction between the surface Si groups of BAG particles and the OH of
chs and cht. Interestingly, the samples containing only 5wt% of BAG resulted to
have a higher water evaporation peak. The higher peak at lower temperatures of the
sample containing 5wt% BAG after immersion in SBF may be linked to the sample
==PAGE:60==
52
not being fully dried after submersion in SBF.
According to the literature, alterations in the thermal degradation characteristics
of polymers are recorded after incorporation of inorganic particles into polymeric
matrices [88, 89]. In Figure 17, 2 peaks can be observed between 200 and 400 °C.
These peaks can be associated with the degradation of chs and cht, respectively
[87]. The peaks around 300 °C and 400 °C for the scaffold with 5wt% BAG before
immersion in SBF resulted more similar to the ones of the control sample, whereas
it is clear that higher concentrations of BAG and immersion in SBF influenced chs
and cht degradation greatly. Cht degradation is particularly affected by this. It is
plausible that greater concentrations of BAG increases the diffusion of heat to the
sample, resulting in an accelerated cht degradation [13]. Lastly, by addressing the
residual mass of the samples (Appendix A7) it seems that an increase amount of
BAG and the presence of HA after immersion in SBF led to a decrease in mass loss
[90].
4.2.6
Rheology
Generally, if the stress applied during oscillatory measurement is lower than the
yield stress, the network properties of the material are analysed [76, 77]. In this
case, the observed G’ is greater than G”, indicating a behaviour more similar to that
of a solid. However, when the applied stress exceeds the yield stress, the crossover
occurs, and G” becomes larger than the G’, indicating a more liquid-like behaviour.
To understand the mechanical properties of the printed materials, amplitude sweeps
were conducted at 25 °C and 37 °C. At both temperatures, the formulations have
exhibited a dominantly elastic behaviour, as suggested by the predominant G’ (Figure
18). Samples with lower concentration of BAG resulted more viscous than their
counterparts (higher G’). Considering the results from the rheology tests, it appears
that the formulations could be used in 3D printing but will likely not retain the
printed shape once extruded from the nozzle due to their low viscosity.
Overall, Various parameters should be taken into considerations when assessing
the formulation for 3D printing. The rheology analysis carried out for the present
study is just a preliminary assessment of the formulations properties.

Conclusions
The present study focused on exploring the use of cht-chs-BAG Pickering foams
for scaffold creation in the field of bone tissue engineering. Through extensive research
and experimentation, several important findings have been revealed. In summary,
mechanical testing results demonstrated that the addition of BAG in the formulations
led to better mechanical properties compared to the BF, but addition of gas in the
foaming process led to scaffolds with reduced stiffness and strength. However, the
scaffolds still mechanical properties that fall within the range suitable for bone tissue
growth.
The average porosity and interconnectivity of the scaffolds emerged as critical
factors that require further analysis. The determination of average porosity can be
assessed using techniques such as the Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) method or
by imaging software, while X-ray micro-computed tomography can provide valuable
insights into the interconnectivity of the scaffold structure. It should be noted
that different freezing and drying methods may influence the resulting porosity and
interconnectivity of the samples, and should be considered as future path of analysis.
The ICP-OES results gave an insight on the ion leaching and possible mineral-
isation process on the surface of the scaffolds. Furthermore, after immersing the
scaffolds in simulated body fluid for 7 days, SEM imaging revealed the formation of
structures resembling HA on their surfaces. To confirm the presence of HA, XRD
analysis should be conducted. This bioactivity would indicate the potential ability of
the scaffolds to support bone regeneration and would highlight their suitability for
integration into the natural bone tissue. The utilisation of characterisation techniques
such as FTIR, SEM and DSC-TGA has provided valuable evidence of the strong
influence of BAG on the scaffolds. These analyses have shed light on the chemical
composition, surface morphology, and thermal behaviour of the scaffolds, supporting
their potential for bone tissue engineering applications.
To successfully utilise these formulations for 3D printing applications, further
rheological studies should be undertaken. Understanding the rheological properties
of the formulations will be essential in ensuring their printability and maintaining
structural integrity during the printing process.
In conclusion, it is important to acknowledge that this study represents an early
stage exploration in the use of cht-chs-BAG Pickering foams for scaffold development
in bone tissue engineering. While significant findings and insights have been obtained,
there is still much to be studied. Considering the challenging emerging field of tissue
engineering, there are inherent challenges and limitations that need to be addressed.
==PAGE:62==
54
Factors such as scalability, reproducibility, and cost-effectiveness must eventually
be considered for the practical implementation of these scaffolds in clinical settings.
Collaboration among multidisciplinary teams, including engineers, material scientists,
biologists, and clinicians, will be vital to overcoming these challenges and advancing
the field of bone tissue engineering.

A1: Chitin Purification Procedure
Glutaraldehyde cross-linker solution (25%) and 100% acetic acid were purchased
from Sigma-Aldrich [48, 49, 69]. pH 2 buffer solution and pH 11 buffer solution
were purchased from VWR Chemicals, Belgium. All chemicals were used as received
without further purification. The purification process of chitin involved soaking the
residual biomass in 1 M HCl for 12 hours, followed by treatment with 1 M NaOH
for 12 hours, repeated at least three times. The resulting solid was decolorized using
0.5% NaClO2 and adjusted to pH 5 with acetic acid, then heated at 70 °C for 2 hours.
The purified chitin solid residues were obtained after washing with distilled water
and crushed using a household blender. The wet chitin solids were stored at 4 °C.
A2: Reagents and Ion concentrations of SBF
Reagents used for SBF (in distilled and ion-exchanged water) [73]:
1. sodium chloride (NaCl)
2. sodium hydrogen carbonate (NaHCO3)
3. potassium chloride (KCl)
4. di-potassium hydrogen phosphate trihydrate (K2HPO4 · 3 H2O)
5. magnesium chloride hexahydrate (MgCl2 · 6 H2O)
6. calcium chloride (CaCl2)
7. sodium sulfate (Na2SO4)
8. Tris-hydroxymethyl aminomethane ((HOCH2)3CNH2) (Tris)
9. 1M (mol/l) Hydrochloric Acid, 1M-HCl,
10. pH standard solution, (pH 4, 7 and 9).
