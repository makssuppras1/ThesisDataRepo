Abstract
Enzyme immobilization is known for its favorable effects on enzyme stability and reusabil­
ity and for enabling a transition to continuous processes. Furthermore, it leads to the cre­
ation of entities displaying both catalytic and separation properties, lowering therefore the
need for downstream purification processes. In the present work, the specific case of lac­
tate dehydrogenase immobilization on membranes through polycation co­deposition and
interfacial polymerization has been studied for the conversion of pyruvate to lactate. Pyru­
vate is a degradation product of lignin and a by­product of succinic acid fermentation and,
therefore, constitutes one of the compelling raw materials for future lactate production.
The novelty of this project does not reside in the immobilization of lactate dehydrogenase
itself but in the chemistry used for the latter immobilization. Polycation co­deposition and
interfacial polymerization have indeed, to our knowledge, never been used for lactate
dehydrogenase immobilization.
This work led to the development of an immobilization method demonstrating high en­
zyme loading, lactate yield, membrane permeability, stability over time, and negligible
enzyme leakage. The immobilization technique was also proven to broaden the optimal
temperature range. However, it did not decrease the enzyme sensitivity to pH, but it also
did not exacerbate the issue.

1
Introduction
The present project focuses on developing a novel technique for the immobilization of
lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) on ultrafiltration (UF) membranes based on polyelectrolyte
co­deposition and interfacial polymerization.
Enzymes are green catalysts displaying high substrate and product selectivity and en­
abling the use of mild reaction conditions. Nonetheless, the use of enzymes is limited by
their low stability and reusability. These two downsides can be tackled through enzyme
immobilization, which mainly consists of fixing enzymes to a support and/or entrapping
them in a confined environment.
One of the supports highly researched for enzyme immobilization is membranes. Mem­
branes are permeable interfaces able to transport chemical compounds at different speeds.
Such velocity differences justify the use of membranes as separation units. In recent
years, membranes have also been considered suitable enzyme supports due to, among
others, their tuneable properties, high enzyme loading, and high mechanical stability.
Besides, the combination of enzymes and membranes leads to the creation of an entity,
if well­designed, exhibiting high catalytic properties and separating, to a large extent, the
reaction products.
The novelty of this study resides in the methods used to perform the immobilization of
LDH. Indeed, to our knowledge, polyelectrolyte co­deposition and interfacial polymeriza­
tion have never been used to bind LDH to a membrane. Therefore, the effects of adsorp­
tion on LDH induced by polyelectrolyte co­deposition have not been researched. The
same goes for the impacts of covalent bonding and entrapment on LDH resulting from
interfacial polymerization. Given that each enzyme possesses specific properties, the
outcomes of both immobilization techniques for LDH were thus yet to be discovered.

2
Literature review
The current section gives a general overview of membrane technology and the different
techniques used for enzyme immobilization. In addition, L­lactate dehydrogenase and the
considered reaction, i.e., conversion of pyruvate to lactate, are investigated in detail. A
thorough investigation of the enzyme properties and the reaction substrate and products
is of high interest when designing the immobilization method and studying the catalytic
properties of the immobilized enzyme.
2.1
Membrane technology
Membrane technology comprises every process involving membranes, e.g., reverse os­
mosis (RO), microfiltration (MF), nanofiltration (NF), ultrafiltration (UF), and electrodialysis
(ED). Baker [1] defines a membrane as ”nothing more than a discrete, thin interface that
moderates the permeation of chemical species in contact with it”. Mulder et al. [2] de­
scribes it as ”a permselective barrier or interfase between two phases”. A membrane is,
therefore, a permeable material displaying the ability to transport chemical compounds at
different speeds. The velocity difference from component to component enables the use
of membranes in separation processes. It is important to mention that such separation al­
ways requires a certain amount of energy to offset the entropy of the mixture. In the case
of membrane technology, the requisite energy can be provided in the form of pressure,
electrical current, or heat [1, 2].
Selectivity and flux are the key parameters used to specify the performance of a mem­
brane. In general, high selectivity and flux are desired, as the resulting separation process
is, thus, both fast and efficient [3]. Nonetheless, in some particular cases, low fluxes can
be beneficial, e.g., to maximize the contact between catalyst and reactants in biocatalytic
membranes.
Membrane technology displays numerous advantages and disadvantages, more or less
constraining depending on the studied application. Instances of the latter are displayed
in Table 2.1.

2.1.1
History and current applications
When looking into membrane history, a clear distinction can be made between scientific
findings and industrial applications. Indeed, the first discovery of membrane­related phe­
nomena dates back to the late 18th century with the definition of osmosis by Abbé Nollet
in 1748 [5]. On the other hand, the first significant application of membranes relates to
drinking water testing at the end of World War II. The discrepancy originated from four
main problems of membranes, i.e., the unreliability, the slowness, the low selectivity, and
the price. Each of these issues were addressed and tackled to reach a broad use of
membrane processes [1, 2].
One of the breakthroughs that led to the implementation of membranes in industrial pro­
cesses is the Loeb­Sourirajan process (1963) [6]. Before the latter, membranes displayed
uniform composition and structure throughout, i.e. isotropic membranes. Since the main
driving force for separation is the pressure when considering membranes (1­100 bar [7]),
the membrane needed to display a certain thickness (10­200 µm [2]) to withstand the me­
chanical constraints. Nonetheless, as the flow passing through the membrane is inversely
proportional to the membrane thickness, such thickness greatly hindered the flow. The
ingenuity of the Loeb­Sourirajan process resides in applying a thin selective layer (thick­
ness < 0.5 µm [2]) on a much thicker support (thickness ∼50­200 µm [2]), providing the
mechanical strength and not slowing down the flow as highly porous. In other words, Loeb
et al. [6] invented what is today considered typical anisotropic membranes and therefore,
finally allowed to stop compromising between selectivity and flow, i.e., to speed up the
membrane separation while maintaining the same product purity. The difference between
isotropic and anisotropic membranes will be discussed in further detail in Subsection 2.1.3
[1, 2].
Afterward, the development of membranes started to speed up, as well as their respec­
tive implementation in the industry. Around 1980, MF, UF, RO, and ED became well­
established processes in the industry, followed by gas separation processes in the 90s,
and finally, the development of reliable and efficient water treatment solutions at the end
of the 20th century [1, 2].
Nowadays, membranes are more and more omnipresent in sectors like water purification
and wastewater treatments, the food and feed industry, gas separation, the pharmaceu­
tical industry, and even the electronics industry [1, 8]. The membrane market was worth
around 8 billion USD in 2021 and is expected to grow with a compound annual growth rate
(CAGR) of around 8% til 2027/2028 [8, 9]. Seeing that society steers toward more sustain­
able ways of producing and consuming, membranes could play a decisive role. Membrane
energy consumption is indeed low compared to other separation processes. Furthermore,
membrane offers a wide range of applicability due to the variety of membrane structures
and compositions. The latter characteristics enable membrane technology to be relevant
in numerous fields. As developed by Sazali et al. [10], membrane technology could take
part in exhaust gas purification, more precisely in carbon dioxide separation. Membranes
could therefore enable CO2 capture and partially prevent the emissions of one of the most
discussed greenhouse gases. Briceno et al. [11] also highlight the fact that membranes
could play a role in hydrogen purification, the latter fuel often cited as a key element in
future energy solutions [12].

2.1.2
Fundamentals of membrane technology
As previously mentioned, membranes are used as separation processes. A concentra­
tion difference is, therefore, observed from one side of the membrane to the other and
results from the difference in permeation rates. The numerous particles retained by the
membrane barrier accumulate along the membrane, creating a concentration gradient.
The latter phenomenon is called concentration polarisation and occurs in the near vicinity
of the membrane surface in a zone named boundary layer δ. Concentration polarization
impacts membrane retention and induces a diffusive flow from the membrane surface to
the feed bulk. On top of the concentration polarisation decreasing the flow, feed com­
pounds can through, e.g., adsorption, pore blocking, precipitation, and cake formation,
further hinder the flux through the membrane. Such further flux hindrance is defined as
fouling [1, 2].

Several models can be used to describe membrane­based separation processes. Some
are mechanistic models relating the separation to the membrane structure, whereas oth­
ers are based on a phenomenological approach and thermodynamics. Two often­used
models to characterize the transport of solute and solvent through membranes are the
solution­diffusion model established by Lonsdale et al. [13] and the irreversible thermo­
dynamic model developed by Kedem and Katchalsky [14]. The former is a mechanistic
model derived from simple and basic principles, and the latter is an example of a phe­
nomenological model [1, 2, 15].
One difference between the solution­diffusion model and the Kedem­Katchalsky model is
the introduction of the parameter called reflection coefficient σ representing the membrane
imperfections. As for the solute flux, the main difference between the two models is that
the irreversible thermodynamic model considers convection in addition to diffusion [13,
14].
As previously voiced, a driving force of some type is necessary to forcefully push a feed
through a membrane and obtain the desired separation. Two operating modes exist for
membranes processes, i.e., dead­end or cross­flow mode (see Figure 2.3). In the dead­
end configuration, the feed is perpendicular to the membrane surface with an accumu­
lation of retained particles on the membrane and a flux named permeate traversing the
membrane. The cross­flow mode, on the other hand, considers a feed flow along the
membrane surface splitting up into two different fluxes, i.e., the permeate and the reten­
tate. The permeate corresponds to the flux crossing the membrane, and the retentate is
the remaining flux not crossing the membrane and, therefore, staying on the membrane
side where the feed was injected [1, 2].
It is of interest to mention that the designed experiment considers a dead­end config­
uration. In such a configuration, no retentate flux is obtained. Nonetheless, the word
retentate will still be used in the following to describe the remaining liquid on top of the
membrane surface at the end of a filtration treatment in dead­end mode.

2.1.3
Membrane types and classification
A multitude of membranes exists and may be classified according to their composition,
structure, and applicability range.
Membrane material can be manifold. The most common membrane material is polymers
due to their satisfactory properties (e.g., chemical, mechanical, thermal stability, perme­
ability) and low price. Ceramics can also be employed for membrane manufacturing and
were introduced because of their extended operating lifetimes and higher thermal and
chemical stabilities. The latter properties make them particularly suitable for food, phar­
maceutical, and biotechnological applications. Materials like metal, zeolite, carbon, and
specific liquid phase can also be used for given membrane applications [1, 3].
Membranes are categorized into two main types when investigating their structure, i.e.,
isotropic and anisotropic. Whereas the composition and structure of isotropic membranes
are identical throughout the entire membrane, anisotropic membranes are constituted of
multiple layers whose structure and properties are different. As previously mentioned in
Subsection 2.1.1, membranes obtained through the Loeb­Sourirajan process are typical
examples of anisotropic membranes with a thin and dense surface layer, enabling selec­
tivity, sitting on a thicker support layer with a highly porous structure, allowing for the high
flow and mechanical strength. It should be noted that isotropic and anisotropic mem­
branes can also be called respectively symmetric and asymmetric [1, 3]. Finally, even
though the development of anisotropic membranes enabled the growth and expansion of
membrane technology, isotropic membranes are still studied and researched [17, 18, 19].
Membranes are also classified based on the smallest particle size not crossing the mem­
brane barrier. In other words, membranes are categorized based on their pore size and
the subsequent particle size they can or not retain. As displayed in Figure 2.4, to each of
the membrane class can be attributed an applicability range based on target particle size.
For instance, the applicability range of MF goes from around 0.1 to 10 µm [1, 7, 2].

2.1.4
Fabrication methods and modules
As previously pointed out the vast majority of membranes used are polymeric, the present
section will, thus, concentrate on the principal fabrication methods employed to produce
polymeric membranes. The method principles and the obtained membrane types will be
looked into for each fabrication process.
To produce isotropic microporous polymeric membranes, three methods are at one´s dis­
posal, i.e., track­etching, stretching, and template leaching. Track­etching is a two­step
preparation process that consists in exposing the membrane first to an energetic ion beam
and then to a batch of aggressive chemicals. The energetic ions cross the polymeric mem­
brane, breaking polymer chains and leaving a ”track” in the material. The latter track is
more susceptible to chemical attacks. Therefore, when subjecting the membrane to harsh
chemical reagents, it will be preferentially attacked along the sensitized tracks, leading to
the formation of pores. The induced pores are extremely uniform, with a tortuosity close
to one (see Figure 2.5a). The obtained membrane is, thus, akin to a sieve and displays a
quite low porosity(≤5% [1]) to minimize the formation of double­size holes resulting from
too close tracks. It should be noted that the track can also be induced by fission particles
or other radiation sources. Stretching involves the extrusion of an oriented film of poly­
mer, e.g., polypropylene. The polymeric film is then stretched. The elongation induces
the formation of slit­like pores (0.025­0.25 µm wide [1]) in between the polymer crystallites
(see Figure 2.5b). Finally, template leaching considers the preparation of a homogeneous
mixture of the membrane material and a leachable compound. The mixture is applied as a
film, and the leachable component is then removed thanks to a suitable solvent, resulting
in pores formation whose size relates to the leachable component size (see Figure 2.5c)
[1, 3].

2.2
Enzyme immobilization techniques
Biocatalysis and enzymes have been increasingly accepted and used by various sectors
of the industrial world due to their mild reaction conditions, biodegradability and sustain­
ability, substrate and product specificity, and the like. Nevertheless, the use of enzymes
is hampered by their lack of stability, reusability, and recovery. Enzyme recovery from
a reaction mixture without degrading or inactivating the enzyme in some ways is indeed
highly complex, affecting, thus, negatively their cost­efficiency. These downsides have led
to the development of immobilization techniques. Immobilizing enzymes allows to over­
come most drawbacks mentioned here­above and displays other pros such as enabling
the use of continuous processes and facile product separation. Nonetheless, the cost of
enzyme immobilization has to be borne in mind, as well as the potential non­negligible
decrease in enzyme activity [33, 34].
Enzyme immobilization methods can be classified into three main categories, support
binding, entrapment, and cross­linking. Support binding involves attaching the enzyme
to the surface through physical, ionic, or covalent bonds. Depending on the bonds con­
sidered, the enzyme is more or less bound to the support. A weaker bond between the
enzyme and support often rhymes with a more cost­efficient and simpler immobilization
process. However, such an arrangement is subject to extensive enzyme leaching and
so, rapid enzyme loss during operation. The reciprocal is generally correct, i.e., stronger
bonds mean more complicated processes and lesser leaching. However, stronger chem­
ical bonds also come with a high risk of enzyme denaturation, leading to a decrease in
enzyme activity. Entrapment is a technique that implies trapping the enzyme within a net­
work. The enzyme is, therefore, not attached but physically constrained in the network
due to a pore size smaller than the enzyme itself. The present technique relies on the fact
that both substrate and product can enter and exit the network and do not accumulate
inside the web. It is, thus, mandatory for the substrate and the product to be smaller than
the enzyme. Additionally, an added mass transfer resistance should be expected when
opting for this second approach. The last method, i.e., cross­linking, is an irreversible
method associated with the preparation of carrierless macroparticles. In other words, the
enzymes, with the help of a binder molecule, bind together to form 3D cross­linked ag­
gregates. Cross­linking is related to high enzyme loading and minimal enzyme leakage.
Nonetheless, as for covalent bonds but to a greater extent, severe enzyme conformational
changes are observed, causing activity losses [33, 35].

2.4
Lactate dehydrogenase
This section studies, in greater detail, the enzyme considered in the present report, i.e.,
lactate dehydrogenase (LDH). It first reviews the main characteristics of the enzyme be­
fore dwelling on its structure, optimum conditions, and isoelectric point.
LDH is a prevalent enzyme enabling the conversion of sugar into energy in living cells [69].
LDH belongs to the oxidoreductase enzyme class (EC 1), the latter catalyzing oxidation­
reduction reactions facilitated by cofactors like nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phos­
phate, flavin adenine dinucleotide, and flavin mononucleotide as electron carriers [70, 39].
More precisely, LDH catalyzes the reversible conversion of pyruvate, i.e., one of the end
products of glucose degradation, to lactate in the presence of NAD+/NADH as cofactor,
as displayed in equation 2.1 [71, 72].
As previously mentioned in Subsection 2.3.2, lactate is a chiral molecule and, therefore,
consists of two enantiomers, i.e., stereoisomers that are mirror images of one another

tween L­specific and D­specific LDH. In other words, two different LDH enzymes exist,
each producing from pyruvate a given enantiomer of lactate. Such specificity enables the
obtention of a solution composed of one given enantiomer and not of a racemic mixture.
The present study focuses on L­lactate dehydrogenase (L­LDH, EC 1.1.1.27) catalyzing
the formation of L­lactate, as only a few lower microorganisms produce D­specific LDH

2.4.2
Optimum conditions and inactivation
Jackson et al. [81] researched both optimum reaction conditions and stability of L­LDH.
Optimum reaction conditions consider activity measurements at different experimental
conditions (e.g., changing pH or temperature). On the other hand, stability examines
enzyme incubation under experimental conditions and then performs the activity mea­
surement at standard conditions, investigating, therefore, the enzyme degradation when
exposed to certain conditions. The pH optimum was determined through enzyme activity
measurement at numerous pHs and 25°C with a 0.8 mM pyruvate concentration and a 0.2
mM NADH concentration. The study considered a 25 mM sodium acetate buffer for pH 3­
5, a 25 mM sodium phosphate buffer for pH 6­8, and a 25 mM sodium bicarbonate buffer
for pH 9­11. Jackson et al. [81] reported an optimal pH between 6.0 and 7.0. The latter
publication also looked into optimum temperature at pH 7.0 (sodium phosphate buffer)
with a 0.8 mM pyruvate concentration and a 0.2 mM NADH concentration. A range of
optimal temperatures between 45­55°C was identified. The pH stability was then studied
through enzyme incubation at different pHs for one hour. The same buffers as the ones
mentioned here above were used. Maximum activity was observed at pH 7­8, indicating
that little to no enzyme degradation occurs in the latter pH range. Finally, the thermal
stability of LDH was investigated at 50°C and 55°C for several incubation times. After
one hour of incubation at 50°C, the residual enzyme activity measured corresponded to
more or less a fourth of the initial activity. Jackson et al. [81] also determined the half­life
of L­LDH at 55°C to be worth 2 min, backing the high sensitivity of L­LDH to tempera­
ture. Therefore, even though the optimum temperature was found to be around 50°C, it
is impractical to use such a temperature when considering possible enzyme reuse.
Bai et al. [82] also looked into thermal stability/inactivation of L­LDH. The publication con­
sidered a reaction mixture containing 0.66 mM pyruvate, 0.25 mM NADH, and 0.002 µM
L­LDH in 0.1 M phosphate buffer, pH 7.5. The latter reaction mixture was exposed to six
different temperatures, i.e., 37, 45, 56, 58, 60, and 62°C, and the substrate conversion as
a function of time was monitored through spectrophotometric measurements. Figure 2.15
displays the results obtained by Bai et al. [82]. From Figure 2.15, it can be noted that the
progress curve at 37°C is linear, allowing for the computation of the initial rate of reaction.
Temperatures higher than 37°C lead to non­linear progress curves tending towards lower
and lower final substrate conversion, the latter implying that the higher the temperature,
the higher the enzyme inactivation. It can, therefore, be concluded that for the reaction
conditions studied by Bai et al. [82] and for reaction times of around 10 min, temperatures
higher than 40 °C should be avoided to prevent enzyme inactivation and thus, obtain er­
roneous activity measurements. After complete inactivation of enzyme samples, Bai et
al. [82] considered cooling them down to 4°C for 24 h to determine whether the activity
loss was reversible or not. The cooling process only restored 1­3% of the original activity,
indicating that the inactivation is irreversible to a large extent. Finally, Bai et al. [82] re­
ported that pyruvate, i.e., the reaction substrate, protects to a certain extent the enzyme
against thermal inactivation through binding at the active site.

crose (0.1 ­ 0.5 M) and possibly other small solutes against enzyme denaturation induced
by pH conditions.

Isoelectric point
Lluis [76] studied the isoelectric point of each of the four isozymes contained in rabbit
muscle L­LDH. Assuming that the isozymes are present in identical proportions in the
considered tissue, the isoelectric point of rabbit muscle L­LDH is 7.2. The latter computa­

for rabbit muscle L­LDH. Nonetheless, Sagrista et al. [77] also studied the isoenzymatic
composition of rabbit muscle L­LDH and documented that M4 and M3H isozymes are the
predominant isozymes (see Figure 2.16).

a tetrameric enzyme, L­LDH is, therefore, composed of a combination of four peptide

2.5
Summary and project novelty
This project researches the immobilization of LDH on a membrane to catalyze the con­
version of pyruvate to lactate, i.e., a building block of future green chemistry.
The benchmark membrane considered in this study is GR51PP, a polysulfone 50 kDa
UF membrane developed by Alfa Laval. In essence, it is a polymeric asymmetric UF
membrane with a polysulfone skin layer and a polypropylene support. The benchmark
membrane is modified through polyelectrolyte co­deposition and interfacial polymeriza­
tion. The pore size is, therefore, decrease due to the above­mentioned treatments, and
properties between the ones observed for UF and NF membranes are expected.
Figure 2.17: Schematic representation of the GR51PP membrane developed by Alfa Laval, i.e., a
polymeric asymmetric UF membrane with a polysulfone skin layer and a propylene support.
Polyelectrolyte co­deposition and interfacial polymerization are the two techniques stud­
ied for the developed enzyme immobilization on flat sheet membranes. Their combination
leads to an enzyme immobilization protocol based on support binding and entrapment,
two of the enzyme immobilization techniques developed in Section 2.2. Polyelectrolyte
co­deposition is used to enhance the electrostatic activities between LDH and the support.
Moreover, as explained in Section 4.1, the considered polyelectrolytes are composed of
basic functional groups, e.g., ketones and amines. Adding these polyelectrolytes to the
membrane leads to the generation of positive charges, which can favorably affect the en­
zyme conformation and thus its activity. As the polyelectrolyte co­deposition is optimized
to maximize charge interactions with the enzymes, this first immobilization procedure re­
lies on an adsorption­type support binding. An interfacial polymerization layer is added
on top of the co­deposited coating layer (cf. Subsection 2.1.4). The interfacial polymer­
ization forms a cross­linked network entrapping the enzymes but also binding with the
enzymes. The binding between the enzymes and the interfacial polymerization stems
from the amine groups present in LDH [72]. The chemistry behind the latter phenomenon
is further developed in Section 4.1. Thus, the considered interfacial polymerization immo­
bilizes the enzyme through entrapment and covalent­type support binding. Polyelectrolyte
co­deposition and interfacial polymerization, and especially their combination have, to our
knowledge, never been tested for LDH immobilization.

Several membrane modifications were performed and discriminated by evaluating the

Membrane pretreatment
A pretreatment step is required to use GR51PP membranes. The pretreatment was com­
pleted before the use of each membrane considered in this report.
Membrane coupons of around 7 cm x 7 cm were cut and placed in a beaker. The beaker
was filled with 0.1 wt% NaOH to cover the membranes. It was then placed on a rotary
shaker for 30 min at 30 rpm. Afterward, the NaOH solution was discarded and replaced
with water. The beaker was once more placed on a rotary shaker for 30 min at 30 rpm.
The previous step with water was repeated two more times. Finally, the membranes were
stored in water in the fridge (6°C) until use.

The pretreated membrane was set in a frame with a 6.5 cm diameter hole, with the skin
layer side facing upwards. The membrane fastened in the frame was placed on a rotary

Results and discussions
4.1
Membrane modification selection
4.1.1
Preliminary study
The benchmark, i.e., pretreated GR51PP, and the four modified membranes were pro­
duced and characterized according to the protocols described in Subsection 3.2.1. The
membranes’ WP, NADH retention, and ZP curves are displayed in Figures 4.1­4.3.
WP is of interest in the present study as it is a general characterization of the unit speed.
Moreover, given that this study looks into biocatalytic membranes, WP also represents
how long the reactants stay in the membrane and, therefore, the time they have to react
with the enzymes. The NADH retention is a useful property to contemplate for potential
cofactor reuse. The studied enzyme immobilization was developed to be coupled with
another enzymatic reaction consuming NAD+ as a reactant and thus regenerating the co­
factor needed for the conversion of pyruvate to lactate, i.e., NADH. The lower the NADH
retention factor, the higher the amount of the cofactor that can be recirculated and reused
within the process. Finally, the ZP scan will give insight into the possible attraction/repul­
sion forces between the membranes and other charged compounds. Membrane charac­
terization through ZP is also a must to optimize the developed enzyme immobilization.
Indeed, the enzymes are added to the membrane after the polyelectrolyte co­deposition
step. Therefore, the amount of enzyme first immobilized on the membrane is mainly gov­
erned by adsorption. The attraction/repulsion forces between the electrolyte layer and
the enzymes are, thus, to be tuned to maximize enzyme deposition and binding to the
membrane.

Finally, one can explain the third comment, i.e., the ZP decrease induced by the TMC

TMC network. TMC contains three chlorine atoms, the latter being good leaving groups.
TMC can, thus, react with amine and hydroxyl functional groups, leading respectively to
the formation of amides and esters [109, 110, 111, 112, 113, 114, 115]. Recall that the
two polycations co­deposited contain amine groups (cf. Figure 4.6). When in contact
with water or the polycations deposited on the membrane, TMC thus starts to polymerize,
forming a cross­linked network. Figure 4.7 display the main reactions occurring during
the network formation.
Esters and amides are compounds with a lesser reactivity. One of the only reactions pos­
sible in the present conditions is a transformation into carboxylic acids, the latter being
improbable, especially in the case of amides where heating and a long time are required
[108]. They are, therefore, assumed not to impact to a large extent the overall charge.
Conversely, the carboxyl functions formed at the ends of the TMC network when in con­
tact with water considerably impact the membrane charge, i.e., a ZP decrease. Indeed,
carboxylic acids, as their name suggests, are acid (pKa ∼0­5 [108]) and thus, lead to
the formation of negative charges. Furthermore, one can expect the benzene rings to
stabilize the possible negative charges, making the present compound a quite reactive
acid due to its corresponding base stability.

When combining the NADH and ZP results (see Figures 4.2­4.3) and recalling that the

the membranes, as all membranes have negative ZP values at pH 8.5. However, low
NADH retention factors were obtained even for the most negatively charged membranes.
An example of the latter is the benchmark membrane exhibiting the second­lowest ZP

could originate from the size difference between the component and the membrane pores,
GR51PP pores being roughly 50 times larger than NADH. This size difference enables
NADH to cross the membrane without being in the nearby environment of the membrane
surface, where the repulsion forces could start having an impact. In the present case,
it can thus be assumed that, to a large extent, the NADH retention is independent of
the membrane charge. Furthermore, given the high relative size difference, the NADH
transport through the membranes could be expected to be negligibly hindered.

Two parameters, i.e., the impact of the TMC treatment duration and the influence of PDA,

TMC treatment duration
To study the effect the TMC treatment duration has on the membrane properties, two other
treatment durations were tested, i.e., 2 min and 30 min. Recall that the latter treatment
for the base case lasts 10 min. Once more, for the reasons pointed out at the beginning
of the section, the membranes were discriminated through WP, NADH retention, and ZP
evaluations. Figures 4.9­4.11 display the obtained results.
The WP and NADH retention results do not seem to display a trend (see Figures 4.9­
4.10). The same goes for the ZP curves, which are similar for a given polycation (see
Figure 4.11). From this experiment, it was thus concluded that the duration of the TMC in­
terfacial polymerization treatment does not have a significant impact on the studied mem­
brane properties. One possible explanation for the limited influence of the TMC treatment
duration arises from the reaction mechanism. The interfacial polymerization occurs only
at the interface between the remaining aqueous solution contained in the membranes
pores and the TMC/hexane solution, as the two solvents are immiscible. The thickness
of the TMC network is, therefore, microscopic (≤0.1 µm [1, 2]). Furthermore, due to
the high reactivity of TMC [116], the cross­linked network is most likely formed in the first
few seconds/minutes after the two solutions get in contact. More information on interfa­
cial polymerization is available in Subsection 2.1.4. As the TMC treatment duration did
not appear to have an impact, the membranes subsequently modified through interfacial
polymerization used a 2 min TMC treatment instead of a 10 min one.

4.1.3
Membrane modification choice
No detrimental WP or NADH retention values are observed in the previous experiments.
The membrane modification choice is, therefore, mainly guided by the necessity to have
significant adsorption of the enzyme on the polyelectrolyte coating layer. Indeed, as pre­
viously mentioned, the enzymes are added after the polyelectrolyte deposition and before
the TMC interfacial polymerization. The initial amount of enzymes bound to the membrane
is, thus, determined by the attraction forces between the enzyme and the polyelectrolyte
coating layer, as no reactions between the two are expected.
As discussed in Subsection 2.4.3, L­LDH isoelectric point is 7.8 [76, 77]. Therefore, the
enzyme is positive at pHs lower than 7.8 and negative at pHs greater than 7.8. Further­
more, the magnitude of the charge increases the further from the isoelectric point the pH
is. As the enzyme undergoes inactivation for pH /∈[7.0 ; 8.0] [81], it was concluded that
the pH at which the enzyme immobilization should be performed is 7.0. Therefore, the
chosen modified membrane after polyelectrolyte co­deposition should have a negative
ZP at pH 7.0. From Figures 4.3 and 4.14, it was inferred that the polyelectrolyte leading
to the most negative charge was PLL in combinaison with PDA. Therefore, the chosen
membrane modification consists of a PDA/PLL co­deposition and a 2 min TMC interfa­
cial polymerization. It should be mentioned that the latter modification step will only be
considered if needed for enzyme stability and to minimize leakage.

Results summary
All in all, one concludes from this study that the optimum pH and temperature of L­LDH are

Enzyme immobilization temperature
The impact of temperature is studied in the present section. For this purpose, two 4­hour
enzyme immobilizations were carried out in a cold room (4°C). The three key character­
istics used in the previous section, i.e., NADH conversion, membrane permeability, and
enzyme immobilization efficiency, were computed for the 4°C experiments and compared
to the values obtained for the 4­hour immobilization at room temperature (22°C). One
should note that only the immobilization itself was performed at 4°C. The two following
rinsing steps with pH 7.0 phosphate buffer (0.1M) were carried out at room temperature.
From this study, it was expected to discover an increased enzyme immobilization at low
temperatures. Brownian motions being less important at low temperatures, it was ex­
pected that the enzymes would smoothly bind to the membrane and that barely any en­
zyme, after being immobilized, would return to the bulk enzyme solution.
From Figure 4.21, it can be seen that the observed trend is the polar opposite of what
was expected. Instead of decreasing with temperature as foreseen, the enzyme immobi­
lization efficiency increases from 0.13 µg/cm2 to 1.36 µg/cm2 for a 4°C­22°C temperature
increase. These unexpected results may arise from a reduction in affinity between the en­
zyme and the membrane surface when decreasing the temperature. Gagliardi et al. [117]
and Morel et al. [118] report that temperature does affect acid­base properties. Hence, a
temperature decrease could drive an increase in the membrane ZP values and, therefore,
reduces the attraction forces between LDH and the modified membrane. Recall that LDH
is positively charged at pH 7.0, which guided the membrane modification choice towards
a negatively charged membrane after modification.
Quite logically, an NADH conversion decrease is also observed when considering 4°C
instead of 22°C (see Figure 4.22). Indeed, less immobilized enzymes result in a lesser
activity of the biocatalytic membrane as the amount of catalyst at one’s disposal is lower.
However, the activity decrease appears not to be proportional to the enzyme immobiliza­
tion efficiency decrease. Indeed, the enzyme immobilization efficiency at 4°C corresponds
to a tenth of the one at 22°C, whereas the conversion only decreased by a third of its initial
value. When having a closer at the results, it appeared that it is the rinsing solutions that
contain large amounts of enzymes and thus lower enzyme immobilization. The enzyme
losses could therefore stem from the temperature change between the immobilization and
the membrane rinsing. These odd results could also be caused by the low precision of
the Bradford protein assay, previously pointed out.
Finally, the membrane permeability is higher by around 20% at 4°C than at 22°C, which
could stem from a lesser flux hindrance from the enzymes as fewer are bound on the
membrane surface and pores (see Figure 4.23). Incidentally, it is complicated to infer any
trend over the filtration cycles for the NADH conversion and the permeability.

Enzyme immobilization optimization summary
First, from the optimization focusing on enzyme immobilization duration, it can be con­
cluded that 4 hours is the optimal duration in the present conditions. Indeed, even though
an increase in enzyme immobilization efficiency is observed when moving from a 4­hour
to a 24­hour immobilization, the increase is considered not large enough to justify an
additional 20 hours of immobilization.
The results of the second optimization concentrating on temperature are unambiguous
and lead to the adoption of a 22°C temperature for the rest of the experiments. In a sub­
sequent study, researching how temperatures higher than 22°C affect biocatalytic mem­
branes and the developed immobilization technique would be of great interest. Nonethe­
less, enzyme inactivation should be taken into consideration when conducting the latter
research as inactivation appears for temperatures as low as 37°C (cf Subsection 2.4.2).
Finally, it could be noted that no matter the enzyme immobilization studied, the conversion
decrease over time is low. For this reason, no additional TMC layer is considered, as it
would only decrease the membrane permeability.
4.3.2
Immobilized enzyme stability
The properties of the optimal immobilization technique, i.e., 4­hour immobilization at 22°C,
are further studied through an enzyme stability analysis. Two different types of stability are
researched in this section, i.e., stability over time and pH and temperature stability. The
stability over time aims at understanding the evolution of the biocatalytic membrane prop­
erties with time. The second stability tries to investigate how the biocatalytic membrane
properties are influenced by the reaction conditions and to which extent.
Stability over time
The immobilized enzyme stability is studied over one month for two immobilization proto­
cols, i.e., 1­hour enzyme immobilization at room temperature (22°C) and 4­hour enzyme
immobilization at room temperature. The stability study over time also researches the
1­hour immobilization in addition to the optimal one as it was considered of interest to
determine whether enzyme loadings impact or not enzyme leakage and degradation with
time.
Over one month, the NADH conversion and the membrane permeability of two replicates
of each kind were measured. The generated data is plotted in the here below figures
(4.24­4.25). As no considerable trend could be observed for the different filtration cycles,
it was agreed that the results related to the different filtration cycles could be considered
replicates of the same membrane conditions, i.e., immobilization technique and storage
time.
From Figure 4.24, it can be observed that the conversion evolves similarly for both im­
mobilization techniques. In the first storage week, a drop of around 17% is noted. In the
following weeks, the NADH conversions kept decreasing, but to a lesser extent. It, there­
fore, appeared that the starting conversion value does not impact its evolution with time.
At the end of the one­month storage, no activity was left for the membranes consider­
ing the 1­hour immobilization at room temperature. Nevertheless, the NADH conversion
related to the membranes subject to the 4­hour immobilization was still higher than 30%
after a month. In other words, the optimal immobilization still carried 55% of its initial ac­
tivity at the end of the one­month period. The persisting activity after a one­month storage
period substantially contrasts with the free LDH properties. As a reminder, it was found
that in around two hours, 50% of the enzyme activity was gone when employing free LDH
at pH 7.0 and 22°C (see Figure 4.17).

chosen to discard pH 5.0 in the present study as NADH is noticeably degraded at that pH.
Processes considering NADH as a co­factor would therefore not operate at pH 5.0. The
results of this study are plotted in the form of a 3D graph displayed in Figure 4.26.
From Figure 4.26, it can be noted that the influence of pH is considerably higher than the
one of temperature. No sharp difference in the activity values can be seen for the tem­
perature range studied. On the other hand, a steep decrease in conversion is observed
with the pH. The maximum conversion is observed at pH 6.0 with a 25%/min value. It is
not feasible to determine the optimum temperature as the conversion values are highly
similar at pH 6.0 for the temperature range studied. Comparing the latter results to the
ones obtained for free LDH, a shift of the optimum toward lower pHs is noted (see Fig­
ure 4.15). Moreover, the impact of temperature appears to be smaller for the immobilized
enzymes than the free ones. For free enzymes, the temperature activation can balance
the severe enzyme inactivation/degradation with time (cf. Section 4.2), whereas negligible
differences are observed with temperature for the immobilized enzymes.

Immobilized enzyme stability conclusion
The stability study highlights considerable differences between free enzyme and immo­
bilized enzyme activity. First and foremost, the drastic enzyme inactivation/degradation
with time vanishes almost completely when immobilizing the enzymes. After one month,
the NADH conversion of the optimized immobilization method is still worth more than 50%
of its initial value, a record in comparison to the 50% activity decrease of the free enzymes
in two hours. Besides, the optimal reaction conditions are altered with the immobilization.
A substantial temperature activation was observed for the free enzymes, leading to an
optimal temperature of 45°C. It should be noted that no temperature higher than 45°C
is tested and that the actual optimum temperature may thus be higher for the free en­
zyme. Contrarily, the immobilized enzyme activity is nearly not affected by temperature,
making it feasible to utilize the immobilized enzymes in a broader range of temperatures
and potentially save energy. In conjunction, LDH optimum pH shifts from 7.0 to 6.0 when
performing the immobilization. The activity of the immobilized enzymes considerably de­
creases when moving away from the optimal pH, similarly to what is observed for the free
enzymes. It can thus be inferred that the immobilization does not enable the use of a
broader pH range, but it does not exacerbate the issue. Finally, even though the immo­
bilization appears to display substantial benefits, there are also downsides to the present
immobilization, e.g., NADH is more stable at pH 7.0, and the immobilization is associated
with higher costs.

4.3.3
Comparison with the benchmark
After concluding the optimization and stability study of the immobilization on a membrane
modified through PDA/PLL co­deposition, it was decided that comparing the results of
the previous sections to the ones obtained when performing the same immobilization on
the bare benchmark would be interesting. As developed in Section 4.1, the PDA/PLL
co­deposition on pretreated GR51PP mainly led to an increase in the membrane ZP val­
ues and to adding amine and ketone functional groups, i.e., basic functional groups, on
the membrane. As enzymes are complex proteins displaying both positive and negative
charges throughout their structures, adding basic functional groups was expected to po­
tentially have a good impact on their conformation and, thus, their activity. On the other
hand, an increase in the membrane ZP could lead to less enzyme immobilization as the
attraction forces between the enzyme and the membrane decrease.
Enzyme immobilization on the benchmark (pretreated GR51PP) was evaluated based on
three criteria, i.e., enzyme immobilization efficiency, NADH conversion, and membrane
permeability. Figures 4.27­4.29 display the results obtained for the benchmark and the
results related to the membrane modified through PDA/PLL co­deposition.
One can observe from Figures 4.27­4.28 that the enzyme immobilization efficiency in­
creased by 35% for the benchmark in comparison to the membrane modified through
PDA/PLL co­deposition. However, the computed conversions are, strictly speaking, the
same for the two membranes. These two contradicting properties could stem from the
properties reminded here­above, i.e., the membrane ZP values and the addition of posi­
tive charges.
Figure 4.29 reveals that the membrane permeability of the benchmark is greater than the
one of the membranes modified through PDA/PLL co­deposition. The higher permeability
of the benchmark can be easily explained by the absence of an additional layer hindering
the flux.
All in all, it appears that the benchmark is also a suitable support for LDH, enabling high
enzyme immobilization and activity and having a permeability greater than 125 L/(m2·h).
However, it can also be inferred that the developed immobilization considering a PDA/PLL
coating layer leads to a higher activity per immobilized enzyme. Indeed, even though
the enzyme immobilization efficiency increases by more than a third when moving from
the membranes modified through PDA/PLL to the benchmark, the NA0DH conversion is
identical. It can thus be concluded that each enzyme immobilized on the benchmark is,
on average, less active than the ones immobilized on the coating developed in this report.
This increase could stem from the favorable impacts that the positive charges of PLL have
on the enzyme conformation.

Conclusion
This project investigated the immobilization of LDH on a membrane and its impacts on
LDH activity and stability.
The studied immobilization considered polyelectrolyte co­
deposition and interfacial polymerization as the latter two methods have, to our knowl­
edge, never been used to immobilize LDH. Two polycations were researched, namely
PEI and PLL, and the interfacial polymerization was performed with TMC.
First, a study of the immobilization methods without enzymes was performed to under­
stand how the coating affects membrane properties. It was found that none of the coat­
ings tested led to detrimental permeability and NADH retention values. On the other
hand, strong variations in the membrane charge were noted. As the initial amount of en­
zymes deposited on the membrane is governed by an adsorption mechanism, maximizing
the attraction forces between the enzyme and the support became the focal point of this
first study. It was, thus, established that the membrane modification considering a 1­hour
PDA/PLL co­deposition was optimal as it allowed for the highest attraction forces between
the enzyme and the support. It should be mentioned that TMC interfacial polymerization
was researched as considered valuable to minimize enzyme leakage. However, leakages
essentially equal to zero were measured for the here­above cited modification. Therefore,
the additional TMC layer was considered unnecessary.
The second part of this research focused on the properties of free LDH. The study of
free enzyme properties is indeed required for subsequent comparison with immobilized
enzyme properties. It was determined that free LDH optimum conditions are pH 7.0 and
45°C. Besides, free LDH is highly sensitive to pH and temperature and rapidly inactivates
with time. A 50% activity decrease was indeed noted when moving by 1.5 from the optimal
pH. A drastic enzyme activity decrease of 50% in two hours was also measured. The latter
inactivation over time was counterbalanced by the temperature activation, highlighting
thus the significance of temperature activation for free LDH.
Enzyme immobilization with the selected membrane modification was then performed and
optimized. Two parameters were looked into during the optimization, i.e., duration and
temperature. It was shown that a 4­hour immobilization is optimal for the present reaction
conditions. Indeed, even though the amount of immobilized enzymes increased with time
for durations as high as 24 hours, it was considered that the activity increase was not
high enough to justify an additional 20 hours. When studying the impact of temperature
on immobilization, it was expected to observe a decrease in enzyme binding on the mem­
brane with temperature, as Brownian motions intensify with temperature. Nevertheless,
the opposite was observed, i.e., an enzyme immobilization tenth times higher was ob­
served at 22°C in comparison to 4°C. It was hypothesized that the decrease could stem
from a variation of the acid/base properties of the membrane with temperature, leading
to lesser attraction forces between the enzymes and the support. All in all, the optimized
enzyme immobilization method, i.e., 4­hour immobilization at 22°C, demonstrated a high
enzyme immobilization efficiency of around 1.35 µg/cm2, a 60% NADH conversion, and
high constant membrane permeability.
The immobilized enzymes were characterized by two types of stability, i.e., stability over
time and pH and temperature stability. It was proven that over one month, the biocatalytic
membrane activity decreased by less than 50% of its initial value. The latter results con­
stitute a drastic change in comparison to the free enzymes losing half of their activity in

two hours. Moreover, it was found that the developed immobilization enabled to obtain a
broader optimal temperature range (30­45°C). Regarding the optimum pH, a shift from pH
7.0 to 6.0 was observed when immobilizing the enzymes, which may be detrimental to the
activity as higher NADH degradation is observed at pH 6.0. It was also noted that the pH
sensitivity did not decrease with immobilization, i.e., significant activity losses are noticed
when moving away from the optimal pH. However, the latter issue was not exacerbated
by the immobilization.
Finally, the optimized enzyme immobilization technique (4­hour immobilization at 22°C)
was performed on the benchmark membrane to see whether the selected membrane
modification did have an impact. It appeared that the benchmark is also a suitable support
for immobilization. Nevertheless, it was also inferred that the activity per immobilized
enzyme was higher when considering the selected membrane modification. The latter
phenomenon could originate from the favorable impacts that the positive charges of PLL
have on the enzyme conformation.
In summary, the main findings of the research were:
∗Low free LDH stability over time, which did not stand out when performing the liter­
ature research.
∗Considerable increase in stability over time resulting from the immobilization.
∗Broader temperature range for immobilized enzymes rendering the process more
versatile and enabling energy savings.
∗No broadening of the optimal pH range after immobilization and shift to pH 6.0 at
which the NADH degradation is higher.
The present study is a step forward in developing upcycling methods for succinic acid
fermentation’s by­products and in using more efficiently biomass waste such as lignin. It
adds up to the present knowledge on enzyme immobilization on membranes and could
be a starting point for the development of continuous processes using LDH. Additional
studies could try to implement the developed biocatalytic membrane in a pilot­scale pro­
cess and look into how the upscaling impacts the membrane and enzyme properties.
A more precise characterization of the free and immobilized enzyme activity through a
Michaelis­Menten curve could also be studied.
