Validating the Theory of Visual Attention experimentally in conditions where
visual targets and distractor are perceptually indistinct
Sylvester C. Reventhal Abelsen
Abstract
In the application of the theory of visual attention (Claus Bundesen 1990), it is often assumed that 
separating targets from distractors is an instantaneous process. We examine the case where targets and 
distractors are perceptually indistinct so that this assumption no longer holds true. We also include the 
case for perceptually distinct target and distractor to compare with. Additionally, we compare our 
results with an analogue experiment in the auditory domain to see if the theory works in both sensory 
domains.
Observers were able to identify letters and distinguish colors in both the distinct and indistinct 
experiment, however targets were reported in the wrong order to a greater extent in the indistinct 
experiment than the distinct experiment, so making targets indistinct from distractors had a noticeable 
impact on the performance of the observer. The same trend is seen in the auditory experiment, where 
the analogue to color is the direction of the sound.
The theory of visual attention describes the indistinct results well, so the theory is valid when targets 
are indistinct from distractors. Since it also describes the results in the auditory experiment, it works as 
a theory for both visual and auditory attention and indicates that it might work as a theory of attention 
in general.

Introduction
Summary of the theory of visual attention
In the theory of visual attention (Claus Bundesen, 1990), visual recognition and attentional selection 
consists in making perceptual categorizations of elements in the visual field. A perceptual 
categorization is made if and when it enters working memory and at this time, the element is 
recognized as a member of a particular category.
The theory presents a mechanism for attentional selection, but does not discard the notion that 
attentional selection is governed by an intelligent agent. It seeks to relieve the burden on the intelligent 
agent by providing a powerful mechanism at its disposal. Coupled to a front-end system that supplies 
the measures of strength of sensory evidence concerning the nature of elements in its visual field, the 
mechanism does the task of filtering (selection of targets rather than distractors) and pigeonholing 
(classification of selected elements with respects to categories that are relevant for action).
The theory gives a computational account of attentional selection via two formulas. v(x, i) gives the 
probability that a perceptual categorization “x belongs to i” is made at time t , provided that the 
perceptual categorization has not yet been made:

Description of visual experiment
The visual experiment presents an observer with letters of different colors under different conditions, 
each with their own set of rules. The observer is tasked with identifying 1 or 2 of the letters presented. 
The letters that are to be identified, denoted as targets, can be distinguished via their color and letters 
that need not be identified, denoted as distractors, can be distinguished by being of a different color 
than the target.
One simplification when applying the theory of visual attention is that separating targets from 
distractors in an instantaneous process. The aim of this experiment is to validate the theory of visual 
attention when targets are indistinct from distractors so that making categorizations of “x belongs to i” 
is then expected to take some time. 
The experiment comes in two variants, distinct and indistinct, the main comparison between the two 
being how often distractors are mistaken for targets (see the condition partial report below) or how 
often targets are reported in the wrong order (see the condition whole report below). In both variants 
the letters are the capital letters A, E, I, O and U1, but the colors are different between the variants. For 
distinct, the colors are red and green, while indistinct uses two colors that are a mix of red and green, 
with one being a little more red than green and the other similarly being a little more green than red. In 
relation to the theory of visual attention, the shapes of the letters and the colors represent the perceptual
categories that are relevant.
Providing an answer (not necessarily a correct answer) to a target is denoted reporting and reports are 
forced choice. The conditions are single report, partial report and whole report, each coming in 2 
variants, one for red and one for green.
In single report, there is only 1 target and no distractor. In the red version, the target is red and in 
likewise in the green version, the target is green. The aim of this condition is to give us the baseline for 
how well the observer can identify targets of a given color.
In partial report, there is 1 target and 1 distractor. The target and distractor is displayed 
simultaneously and the distractor will always be a different letter than that of the target. This condition 
shows us how the observer performs when attention is divided between two objects, but with lower 
pertinence value for the color of the distractor, than that of the target. The pertinence value of the 
distractor is still expected to be > 0, since identifying the letter and color of the distractor increases the 
odds of guessing the target, if the observer has not identified the letter of the target and then has to 
guess (guessing 1 out of 4 choices instead of 5). The red version has a red target and green distractor, 
while green likewise has a green target and red distractor.

In whole report, there are 2 targets, no distractors and the targets must be reported in a specific order. 
For the red version, the red target must be reported first, denoted as primary target, and the green 
version reported second, denoted as secondary target. In the green version, the primary target is instead 
green while the secondary target is red. This condition shows us how well the observer performs when 
the attention is divided between two objects with similar pertinence values for their color categories.
Completing a condition takes a series of 120 trials (see trials section under procedure), where a trial 
consists of displaying a fixation cross followed by displaying targets and distractors simultaneously for 
a given period of time, denoted the exposure duration. When the exposure duration ends, targets and 
distractors are replaced by masks that are composed of various shapes in a roughly even mix of red and
green color. A trial is completed when the user has made reports for all targets in the trial. Trials are 
evenly distributed among 6 different exposure durations from 2 – 7 screen frames, with a frame lasting 
1/85 seconds.
Description of the auditory experiment
The visual experiment comes as a visual analogue to a similar auditory experiment. The auditory 
experiment likewise has the conditions single report, partial report and whole report with the same set 
of rules, except color is replaced by sound direction and the letters are conveyed through headphones 
instead of displayed on a screen. The letters used are vowels A, E, I, O and U, the same as in the visual 
experiment. The sound directions are left and right and represent the auditory analogue to red and green
used in the visual experiment.
The auditory experiment comes in two variants, distinct and indistinct and the same rules for two letters
apply: if two letters are played, they will not be the same letter and each is assigned a different sound 
direction. In distinct, the left sound direction means a sound is only played through the left speaker and 
likewise a letter assigned the right sound direction is played through the right speaker. 
In indistinct, sound direction is conveyed through inter-aural time difference, that is, if a sound 
physically was traveling towards the listener from the listeners right side, it would first reach the 
listeners right ear and after a brief moment (less than a millisecond), reach the left ear. Through 
processing, the brain can use this time difference to determine which direction the sound came from. As
an example in the auditory experiment for the left version of partial report, the target (here assigned the 
left sound direction) is then initially played through the left speaker and the distractor through the right 
speaker. Then after less than a millisecond, the target is played in the right ear and the distractor in the 
left ear. For longer exposure durations, the listener should still perceive the target as coming from the 
left and the distractor from the right, but as the exposure duration is shortened, the listener may not 
determine the sound direction correctly.
While the auditory and visual experiment may differ in exposure duration and number of trials, they are
otherwise meant to be analogues to each other.

Procedure
The participant is given time to read the instructions before starting the test and are seated 
approximately 0.5 meters from the screen.
The test is split into 2 main parts, distinct and indistinct. Distinct is executed first and indistinct last. 
Parcitipants can take breaks when needed, but are asked to do so at the end of a condition rather than 
during a condition.
Both distinct and indistinct have 3 different conditions: single report, partial report and whole report, 
which are executed in the the order single → partial → whole. Each condition comes in two variants, 
one for each color, which defines the color of the primary target as well as the color of the secondary 
target or distractor. Which color variant comes first is balanced among participants, so half of the 
participants will start with red, then green while the other half will start with green followed by red.

Stimuli is displayed on a CRT monitor at 1280x1024 resolution and 85 Hz refresh rate. Reports are

Stimulus
The letters presented are capital A, E, U, I and O at ~1 cm from the bottom of the letter to the top of the
letter. Single report has only a single target, partial report has both target and distractor while whole 
report has both a primary and secondary target. If a secondary target or distractor is present, it is a 
different letter than the primary target.
The fixation cross is the + character displayed as grey (rgb = 0.4 * [1 1 1]) in the center of the screen.
The background color is dark grey (rgb = 0.25 * [1 1 1]).
Distinct red has a rgb value of [0.4, 0, 0]. Indistinct red has a rgb value of [0.6, 0.4, 0].
Distinct green has a rgb value of [0, 0.4, 0]. Indistinct green has a rgb value of [0.4, 0.6, 0].

Printed Handout (red first version)
Dear participant.
You will be presented with red and green letters. First the task will be to identify the red letter for a 
session of about 5 minutes, followed by an identical test where the task is to identify the green letter. 
Later the task will be to identify both letters in a specific order. The testing itself takes a little over an 
hour plus some time reading instructions and a 5-10 minute break.
During the test, you will see a + sign in the center of the screen, please keep your eyes on it for the 
duration of a test and maintain a fixed distance to the screen. The letters will appear briefly after which 
you can giver your answer. You will get a shorter practice session before each type of test, to show you 
what the test looks like. The practice session is overall a little easier than the test after, but please don't 
worry too much if some trials are difficult as some are meant to be barely doable.
The tests are as follows and in the order they are presented:
Distinct single RED: identify a single red letter
Distinct single GREEN: identify a single green letter
Distinct partial RED:  both red and green letters are presented, but answer only what the red is
Distinct partial GREEN:  both green and red letters are presented, but answer only what the green is
Distinct whole RED-GREEN: answer both, starting with the red, then the green letter
Distinct whole GREEN-RED: answer both, starting with the green, then the red letter
As you can see, whether the test name has RED, GREEN, RED-GREEN or GREEN-RED in the name 
indicates how many letters you must answer and in what order. Should you be unsure of which one to 
answer for, please wait a little and a question appear, indicating which one to answer for.
After this, you should take a 5-10 minute break and then you revisit the tests where the colors are 
harder to tell apart, red is shifted towards orange and green is shifted towards yellow-green.

Results
Model
In this section the odds of a trial leading to a hit for a particular condition is modeled via the summing 
the probabilities for the cases that are possible in that condition. Primarily, the effect of guessing is 
shown here as well as short descriptions of why the guessing odds are assigned as they are.
The cases to consider is whether or not the target is identified, whether or not the distractor/secondary 
target is identified and whether or not the color is identified. For color, it is an all or nothing ordeal 
since there are only 2 colors and at most 2 letters, so if the color of one letter is identified, the color of 
the 2nd letter is implied to be the color the 1st letter is not.
In the following, the state of the observer is described in terms of having identified the letters and/or 
their colors or not. L1 represents that the observer has identified the 1st letter, L2 that the observer has 
identified the 2nd letter and C that the observer has identified the color. If the observer has not identified
the 1st letter it is shown as !L1 and similarly not having identified the 2nd letter is shown as !L2 and not 
having identified the color is shown as !C. The observer then must provide an answer(s) for the trial 
and will have to guess at L1, L2 or C if either have not been identified. 
For each of the cases listed, the probability shown is the probability that, if the observer is in the state 
given by the case, the trial will be a hit for that condition. In case 2 of partial report, for example, the 
state of the observer is that both letters are identified, but the color is not. The only way this can be a hit
for partial is if the observer correctly guesses the color and gives L1 as the answer. The odds of 
guessing the color here is 50%. If the observer instead incorrectly guessed the color and answered L2, 
this would be a hit for partial reversed (matching case 2 of that condition). The probability that the 
observer will both be in case 2 in partial and make a hit for partial is then  P(L1) * P(L2) * P(!C) * 0.5, 
where P(L1) is the probability of observing the 1st letter, P(L2) the probability of observing the 2nd letter
and P(!C) the probability of not observing the color. P(L1) , P(L2) and P(C) is modeled using the 
logistic function f (e)=
1
1+e
(−k (e−e0))
, where k is the steepness of the curve, e0 the midpoint and e the
exposure duration. P(L1), P(L2) and P(C) each has it's own set of k and e0.
While not shown here, each of the conditions has both a red and a green version. For example, in the 
red version of the whole – primary + secondary target  condition, L1 is the red primary target while L2
is the green secondary target. Similarly in the green version of the partial condition, L1 is the green 
target and L2 is the red distractor.

Single
In single report, there are 2 cases to consider: The target is either identified or not. A hit is if the 
observer answers L1.
case 1: L1. Odds of a hit is 100%.
Since the target is identified, this is a hit.
case 2: !L1. Odds of guessing leading to a hit is 20%.
The target is not identified, leaving the observer to guess the target at 20% odds.
P(single)=P(L1)+P(! L1)⋅0.2
Partial – L1 is the target, L2 is the distractor
In partial report, there are 8 cases to consider: the target is either identified or not, the distractor is 
either identified or not and the color is either identified or not. A hit is if the observer answers L1.
case 1: L1, L2, C. Odds of a hit is 100%.
The observer has identified L1 and knows it is the target.
case 2: L1, L2, !C.  Odds of guessing leading to a hit is 50%.
The observer has identified both target and distractor, but doesn't know which is which, so the odds of a
hit is 50%.
case 3: L1, !L2, C. Odds of a hit is 100%.
The observer has identified L1 and knows it is the target.
case 4: L1, !L2, !C. Odds of guessing leading to a hit is 50%.
The observer has identified the target, but doesn't know it is the target. Guessing this is 50%.
case 5: !L1, L2, C. Odds of guessing leading to a hit is 25%.
The observer hasn't identified the target but knows what the distractor is. Since that leaves 4 possible 
letters for the target, the odds of a hit are 25%.
case 6: !L1, L2, !C. Odds of guessing leading to a hit is 12.5%.
The observer has identified the distractor, but doesn't know it is the distractor. The color must be 
guessed at 50% odds and then the target out of 4 possibilities at 25% for 12.5% odds of a hit.
case 7: !L1, !L2, C. Odds of guessing leading to a hit is 20%.
The color is identified, but without identifying any letters, this does not help and the target must be 
guessed at 20% odds.
case 8: !L1, !L2, !C. Odds of guessing leading to a hit is 20%.
Nothing is identified so the target must be guessed at 20% odds.

Quality of the fit
As there are several local minimums to be found for the fit, a good guess has been given as a starting 
point for each k and e0, so each k has had a starting value of 100, each e0 a starting value of 0.05 and 
each lapse a starting value of 0.1. 
There is no guarantee that the result represents the best solution, but judging from how close the curves
are to the points in indistinct, that does represent a good solution. The quality of the distinct fit is lower 
as some curves struggle hitting the points at lower exposure duration, possibly because green was 
easier to detect than red as seen in the higher number of only green hits in whole report (both green-red 
and red-green variants) as well as green distractors mistaken for red targets in the red version of partial 
report. Since the rest of the conditions are more similar between red and green variants, the fit must 
balance between matching green-only hits and the rest of the conditions.
The high number of hits in whole only-green made it necessary to provide a good guess as a starting 
point for the fit function, as starting with values between 0-1, for example, would find a local minimum
with a good match for whole only-green, but struggle at meeting the points in other conditions and the 
current fit represents a better overall representation.

Comparison with auditory experiment
The auditory experiment does have some general trends in common with the visual experiment. In 
distinct it is easier to distinguish between sound direction like it is easier to distinguish between color, 
compared to indistinct. The gap between both correct and both correct in reverse for whole report is 
bigger in distinct than in indistinct for both auditory and visual results. The same trend is likewise 
apparent in partial report as well in both cases.

Conclusion
The observers where able to correctly identify letters and colors in both distinct and indistinct 
experiments, yet when the colors where indistinct, distractors were perceived to be targets to a greater 
extent than in the distinct version. The same trend was true for whole report in red-green as well as 
green-red, where targets were reported in the wrong order to a greater extent in indistinct than for 
distinct.
These trends when comparing partial report and whole report in distinct versus indistinct are also 
present in the auditory experiment.
Given that separating targets from distractors was evidently not an instantaneous process in neither the 
visual nor auditory experiment we can conclude that one cannot make the assumption that targets can 
be instantaneously separated from distractors, should they be indistinct and the exposure durations 
sufficiently short.
Given that the derived psychometric functions describes the indistinct results well, the theory of visual 
attention is valid when targets are indistinct from distractors. Since this is true for both the visual and 
auditory experiment, this does indicate that the theory of visual attention does work as a general theory 
of attention, or at least as a theory for both visual and auditory attention.
Complications
Ideally, there would have been more participants, two different people participated in the test. Also 3 of
the tests were performed by the same individual that calibrated the test. On the upside, this means I 
know all participants made an effort to perform the test as intended.
A neck brace was considered to ensure consistent distance from the monitor, both for the individual 
participant during the experiment, but also among all all participants.  This was not used, so there may 
have been a small variation in distance between the two participants, but since both participants have 
been well aware that this is necessary, variance in distance during an individual test should be minimal.
Psychtoolbox 3 is excellent for ensuring accurate timings of the stimuli, but did take some time to get 
working properly. Specifically, the timings were not fully accurate on windows and it became 
necessary to switch to linux. On linux, psychtoolbox reported that timings were accurate and all results 
presented here are from linux, specifically ubuntu 16.04 LTS.

Glossary of Terms
Condition
A set of rules that determine the goal of the participant and thus the outcome of a trial.
The conditions are single report, partial report and whole report. In single report, there is 1 target and 
the goal is to report what it was. In partial report there is 1 target and 1 distractor and the goal is to 
report the target. In whole report there are 2 targets and the goal is to report both targets in a specific 
order.
Distinct
If two objects are distinct, they are clearly different in 1 or more ways. Here the objects are letters and 
in the distinct variant of the experiment, the letters are distinct with respects to color.
Distractor
A letter that the participant does not need to identify, but is displayed at the same time as the target.
Exposure Duration
The amount of time the stimuli is displayed.
Frame
A single rendition of the screen. The CRT screen used updates at 85 Hz, so a single frame lasts for 1/85 
seconds.
Lapse
The observer was unattentive during a trial and did not have a proper chance to observe the stimuli.
Indistinct
If two objects are indistinct, they are not clearly different in 1 or more ways. Here the objects are letters
and in the indistinct variant of the experiment, the letters are indistinct with respects to color.
Mask
A collection of shapes in a mix of the colors two colors used (red and green for distinct, mostly-red and
mostly-green for indistinct) displayed after the stimulus. The purpose here is to remove the observers 
internal representation of the stimuli, making the exposure duration more reliable.
Stimulus
Collectively the targets and distractors that are to be displayed at the same time during a trial.
==PAGE:32==
Target
The letter the participant needs to identify.
Trial
The sequence of events from onset of the focus, display of stimulus, display of mask to reports by the 
participant.
