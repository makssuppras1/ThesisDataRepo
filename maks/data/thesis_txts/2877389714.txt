Abstract
Background: Early detection of neurodevelopmental disorders and visual impairments in chil-
dren is essential, yet current diagnostic pathways in Denmark are often lengthy, fragmented, and
heavily reliant on subjective observations. Eye-tracking technology offers a non-invasive means of
capturing objective indicators of visual attention, but its practical role within existing diagnostic
journeys remains unclear.
Methods: A design-oriented research approach based on Design Thinking was employed.
The study combined literature review, desk research, stakeholder interviews and surveys, and user
journey mapping to analyse existing practices and inform protocol development.
Results: The user journey analysis revealed key pain points, including long waiting times and
limited use of objective screening tools. This work proposes a conceptual protocol and identifies
contextual requirements; no empirical validation of performance or time reduction was performed.
Keywords: Neurodevelopmental disorders, visual impairments, eye-tracking, early detection, di-
agnostic pathways, Denmark, protocol development

Chapter 1
Introduction
According to Danish national registry data, among 1,789,463 children born from 1993 through
2020, the most frequently diagnosed neurodevelopmental disorder was attention-deficit/hyperactivity
disorder (ADHD), affecting 53,828 children, followed by autism spectrum disorder (ASD), diag-
nosed in 40,307 children, all before the age of 18 (Madsen et al., 2024; Scandurra et al., 2019). In
comparison, the current prevalence of childhood visual impairment in Denmark is clinically sig-
nificant as well, estimated at 1.6 per 1,000 children under 18 years, based on 45 years of data from
the National Danish Registry of Children with Visual Impairment (Kessel et al., 2024). Several
neurodevelopmental disorders have been observed to peak in late adolescence, particularly among
girls, suggesting delays in being noticed, detected, or diagnosed (Dalsgaard et al., 2020). De-
spite these relatively high incidence and prevalence figures, many children and adolescents across
Denmark experience substantial waiting times for psychiatric assessment and treatment (The Dan-
ish Dream, 2025). According to Danske Regioner, the regional authorities are facing significant
challenges in reducing the long waiting lists for child and adolescent psychiatric assessments, as
progress requires alignment between several factors. In the North Jutland region, children may
wait as long as 3.5 years to receive evaluations for conditions such as ADHD, autism, or anxiety
(The Danish Dream, 2025). Marianne Skjold, director of the Danish Mental Health Foundation,
has described the situation as “hopeless and incredibly urgent” (The Danish Dream, 2025).
The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, United States (CDC) emphasizes that early
identification and intervention during the first few years of life is crucial, as early intervention
can significantly improve a child’s developmental trajectories (Centers for Disease Control and

Prevention, United States, 2024).
The referral to child and adolescent psychiatry in Denmark can indeed be initiated by general
practitioners, other physicians, or municipalities (often via PPR, the pedagogical-psychological
counselling service), particularly when developmental or learning difficulties are noticed at daycare
or school, or are suspected by parents (Capital Region Psychiatry, 2025). However, the diagnostic
process is critically hampered by its reliance on subjective observations from parents or teachers,
combined with extensive waiting lists that delay diagnosis and intervention.
Recent studies suggest that eye-tracking can provide objective biomarkers for early detection
of neurodevelopmental disorders. In children 14–48 months, a composite eye-tracking biomarker
distinguished autistic from non-autistic children with 78% sensitivity and 77% specificity. When
combined with the primary care provider’s diagnosis and confidence level, accuracy improved to
91% sensitivity and 87% specificity (Keehn et al., 2024). Similarly, another study using a brief 2-
minute Gazefinder eye-tracking protocol with clinically referred infants aged 9–14 months showed
that the tool was highly feasible and well-tolerated, with strong data capture. Infants displayed ex-
pected social attention patterns, and importantly, greater autism-related behaviours were linked to
increased gaze toward non-social geometric scenes over social scenes—suggesting early, specific
visual-attention markers of emerging autism (Chetcuti et al., 2024). Extending beyond autism,
eye-tracking measures collected during continuous performance tasks have been shown to reveal
characteristic attentional control differences in children with ADHD—namely reduced fixation,
decreased central gaze, and heightened gaze variability. These metrics improve diagnostic dis-
crimination and point to eye-tracking as a feasible and promising tool for ADHD screening and
assessment (Lee et al., 2023). Together, these findings suggest eye-tracking could augment current
diagnostic processes by reducing reliance on subjective reports and accelerating detection.
Since the methods for identification and diagnosis vary significantly from country to country,
and even between regions for different disorders—such as dyslexia—this can create challenges for
educators, parents, and professionals who rely on consistent assessment practices. One factor con-
tributing to this inconsistency is the wide range of professionals involved in dyslexia evaluation.

Assessments may be conducted by teachers, school-based specialists, educational psychologists,
certified diagnostic assessors, or, in some cases, medical practitioners. This diversity in asses-
sors leads to further variation in how the disorder is identified and measured (Dyslexia Compass
Project, 2022). Therefore, it is essential to ground this research in a specific healthcare system. So,
focusing on children aged 5 to 12—a critical period when the transition into formal schooling acts
as a developmental stress test that makes subtle challenges apparent—this research will employ

seamlessly integrated into primary care or developmental clinics to support earlier and more equi-

this research, empirical experimental studies need to be conducted for the development of various
eye tracking protocols based on the digitalisation of existing methods or devising novel methods
for appropriate for different ages and learning difficulties.

Definitions of Key Concepts
1.1.1
Neurodevelopmental Disorders
The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition (DSM-5) is a com-
prehensive classification system for mental and behavioural disorders, developed and published by
the American Psychiatric Association (APA) in 2013 (Association, 2013).
Although Denmark’s clinical and administrative healthcare system is based on the ICD-10 (and
switching to ICD-11), the DSM-5 was used as it provides standardised diagnostic criteria that guide
research, clinical assessment, and treatment across disciplines. The DSM-5 builds upon earlier
editions by integrating dimensional assessments and updated definitions, reflecting contemporary
research in neuroscience and developmental psychology (Kupfer et al., 2013; Regier et al., 2013;

Sundhedsdatastyrelsen, 2021).
According to DSM-5, neurodevelopmental disorders are a group of conditions that typically
manifest early in development, often before the child enters grade school. These disorders are
characterised by developmental deficits that cause impairments in personal, social, academic, or
occupational functioning (Association, 2013). The main neurodevelopmental disorders include:
• Attention Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) –is a behavioral syndrome defined in
the DSM-5-TR as "characterized by the persistent presence of six or more symptoms involv-
ing (a) inattention (e.g., failure to complete tasks or listen carefully, difficulty in concentrat-
ing, distractibility) or (b) impulsivity or hyperactivity (e.g., blurting out answers; impatience;
restlessness; fidgeting; difficulty in organizing work, taking turns, or staying seated; exces-
sive talking; running about; climbing on things) (American Psychiatric Association, 2022)".
• Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) – According to the DSM-5-TR, ASD is “characterized
by persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction across multiple con-
texts, as well as restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities” (American
Psychiatric Association, 2022)
• Specific Learning Disorders (SLDs) – The DSM-5-TR defines SLD as “difficulties learning
and using academic skills, as indicated by the presence of at least one of the following: inac-
curate or slow and effortful word reading, difficulties with spelling, difficulties with written
expression, difficulties mastering number sense, number facts, or calculation, or difficul-
ties with mathematical reasoning” (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). An estimated
80% of those with learning disorders have an impairment in reading in particular (commonly
referred to as dyslexia) (Kohli et al., 2018)
• Communication Disorders - According to the DSM-5, communication disorders include
language disorder, speech sound disorder, childhood-onset fluency disorder (formerly known
as stuttering), and social (pragmatic) communication disorder. Someone may also be diag-
nosed with an unspecified communication disorder when they present with persistent prob-

lems in language and speech but do not meet the criteria for any specific disorder. Auditory
processing disorder is not included in the DSM-5 but is sometimes considered a communica-
tion disorder. Auditory processing disorder is included in the ICD-10, another widely used

• Motor Disorders - Motor disorders include developmental coordination disorder; stereo-
typic movement disorder; and the tic disorders of Tourette’s Disorder, persistent (chronic)
motor or vocal tic disorder, and provisional tic disorder. Motor disorders are malfunctions
of the nervous system that cause involuntary or uncontrollable movements or actions of the
body. These disorders can cause lack of intended movement or an excess of involuntary
movement. Symptoms of motor disorders include tremors, jerks, twitches, spasms, contrac-

• Intellectual Disabilities - DSM-5 defines Intellectual Disability (ID) as a disorder involving
significant limitations in intellectual functioning and adaptive behavior, originating during
the developmental period that affect three major domains: (1) the conceptual domain, which
includes skills in language, reading, writing, math, reasoning, and memory; (2) the social
domain, which involves empathy, social judgment, communication, and the ability to form
relationships; and (3) the practical domain, which covers self-management, personal care,
job and money management, and organization of daily activities. Intellectual disability is
chronic and may co-occur with other mental conditions such as depression, ADHD, or autism
spectrum disorder (American Psychiatric Association, 2022).
• Other Neurodevelopmental Disorders -
This category applies to presentations in which symptoms characteristic of a neurodevelop-
mental disorder that cause impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of
functioning predominate but do not meet the full criteria for any of the disorders in the neu-
rodevelopmental disorders diagnostic class. The other specified neurodevelopmental disor-
der category is used in situations in which the clinician chooses to communicate the specific

the heterogeneity of symptoms and reliance on subjective observation, which can delay appropriate
interventions (Elsabbagh et al., 2012; Lord et al., 2020; Thapar & Cooper, 2017).
1.1.2
Visual Impairments
Visual impairments encompass a range of conditions that reduce visual acuity, visual field,
or visual quality, and can significantly affect developmental, cognitive, and learning processes in
early childhood. According to the International Classification of Diseases, 11th Revision (ICD-
11), disorders of the visual system include refractive errors, structural abnormalities, and diseases
affecting ocular or neurological pathways (World Health Organization, 2022). Early identification
is essential, as many childhood visual impairments are preventable or treatable when detected
promptly. Common visual impairments relevant to early developmental assessment include:
• Refractive Errors – WHO identifies refractive error as one of the leading global causes
of vision impairment (World Health Organization, 2022). The U.S. National Eye Institute
(NEI) describes refractive errors as conditions where the eye cannot properly focus light on
the retina, resulting in blurred vision (National Eye Institute, 2020e). They include:
==PAGE:15==
1.1. DEFINITIONS OF KEY CONCEPTS
7
– Myopia (nearsightedness) – difficulty seeing distant objects.
– Hyperopia (farsightedness) – difficulty seeing close objects.
– Astigmatism – caused by uneven corneal curvature, producing distorted or fuzzy vision.
• Amblyopia – Defined by NEI as decreased vision in one eye due to abnormal visual devel-
opment in early childhood, even when corrective lenses are provided (National Eye Institute,
2020b). Amblyopia commonly arises from strabismus or untreated refractive errors and can
lead to persistent visual deficits if not managed early.
• Cataract – WHO classifies pediatric cataract as a clouding of the lens that prevents clear
light transmission and may substantially disrupt visual development (World Health Organi-
zation, 2022). NEI notes that cataracts can cause blurry vision, difficulty reading, and, in
severe cases, vision loss (National Eye Institute, 2020c).
• Glaucoma – Childhood glaucoma involves elevated intraocular pressure leading to progres-
sive optic nerve damage. NEI highlights symptoms such as tearing, light sensitivity, and
cloudy corneas in infants (National Eye Institute, 2020d). Without early treatment, glau-
coma can lead to irreversible vision loss.
• Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD) – While typically seen in older adults, early-
onset or hereditary forms can appear in childhood or adolescence. NEI describes AMD
as a condition affecting the macula, impairing central, detail-oriented vision (National Eye
Institute, 2020a).
• Strabismus – The American Academy of Ophthalmology (AAO) defines strabismus as a
misalignment of the eyes, which may present as inward turning (esotropia), outward turn-
ing (exotropia), upward turning (hypertropia), or downward turning (hypotropia) (American
Academy of Ophthalmology, 2021). If untreated, strabismus can lead to amblyopia due to
suppression of input from the deviating eye.

Visual impairments can influence key developmental domains, including attention, motor co-
ordination, early literacy, and socio-communicative interaction. WHO and NEI emphasize that
conditions such as refractive errors, amblyopia, and strabismus are highly responsive to early in-
tervention, while delayed detection may contribute to long-term functional limitations (National
Eye Institute, 2020e; World Health Organization, 2022). This underscores the necessity of routine
childhood vision screening and systematic assessment in both clinical and educational settings.
Research confirms a significant overlap between visual impairment and neurodevelopmental
disorders. This high rate of comorbidity means many children with neurodevelopmental disorders
may also have undiagnosed vision problems that can worsen their symptoms (Fazzi et al., 2012).
The link is particularly strong in Autism Spectrum Disorder, where the risk of ASD is over 30
times higher in children with visual impairment compared to the general population (Goh et al.,
2017). Similarly, a recent meta-analysis confirmed that individuals with ADHD are more likely to
have specific eye conditions like convergence insufficiency (difficulty for eyes to work together up
close) and astigmatism (Gc et al., 2023). A key bridge between these conditions is Cerebral Visual
Impairment (CVI), a brain-based vision issue common in children with intellectual disabilities and
autism, whose symptoms can mimic behavioral problems (Chokron & Dutton, 2020). This overlap
is also seen in specific syndromes; for example, in Down Syndrome, refractive errors and focusing
issues are the norm rather than the exception, but often go uncorrected (Woodhouse, 2016).

Research Questions
RQ1: In Denmark, who are involved in the process of diagnosing neurodevelopmental disor-
ders and visual impairments among children, and how are these conditions identified?
a. Which stakeholders (e.g., parents, teachers, pedagogues, general practitioners, specialists)
play a role in the initial identification and referral processes?
b. What diagnostic methods are currently applied in healthcare and educational settings, and
what are their typical time frames and resource demands?

c. What challenges or strains do these diagnostic processes impose on children, families, edu-

RQ2: How could eye-tracking expedite the diagnostic process for Neurodevelopmental Disor-

eye-tracking protocol as an early support tool for identifying neurodevelopmental disorders in
children?

Dyslexia: Definition, Prevalence, and Identification Pathways in Denmark
Individuals with dyslexia have difficulties learning to read written words because they struggle
to connect letters with their corresponding sounds.
Dyslexia is commonly defined as a specific learning difficulty characterised by persistent prob-
lems in learning to read written words, primarily due to difficulties in connecting letters with their
corresponding sounds. It is estimated that approximately 5–7 percent of the population is af-
fected by dyslexia. In Denmark, the identification of dyslexia is standardised through the National
Dyslexia Test (Ordblindetesten), developed by the Danish Ministry of Children and Education in
collaboration with the Social Services Agency (Socialstyrelsen), the Centre for Reading Research
at the University of Copenhagen, and the School Research Programme (IUP) at Aarhus University.
This test is designed for use across a wide age range but has a clearly defined lower age limit.
In the Danish public school system (Folkeskolen), the National Dyslexia Test (Ordblindetesten)
may be administered no earlier than March 1st of third grade. Prior to eligibility for the Ordblinde-
testen, pupils in kindergarten and first grade who show signs of reading difficulties, as well as those
with a family history of dyslexia, may be identified through early screening, risk assessment, and
pedagogical observation. In addition, parents have the right to request a dyslexia risk assessment
for their child during first grade. Based on these early assessments, responsibility for initiating
appropriate educational support and interventions lies with the school. The decision to administer
the Ordblindetesten is ultimately made by the individual school or educational institution, based
on professional judgement and a well-founded suspicion of dyslexia (Center for Læseforskning,
Københavns Universitet et al., 2024; “National Dyslexia Testing - Eurydice”, 2025).

ADHD: Definition and Structural Bottlenecks in the Danish Diagnostic Pathway
"Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is a neurodevelopmental disorder character-
ized by a persistent pattern of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that is developmentally
inappropriate and impairs functioning across multiple domains, including academic, social, and
occupational settings. It is recognized as one of the most common neurodevelopmental disorders,
with a worldwide prevalence estimated at approximately 5% among children and adolescents. The
disorder typically manifests before the age of 12, as specified by the Diagnostic and Statistical
Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition (DSM-5) and the International Classification of Dis-
eases, Eleventh Edition (ICD-11), which require symptoms to be present in more than one setting
and to cause significant impairment. [...] Comorbidities are common, with anxiety disorders,
learning disorders, and oppositional defiant disorder frequently co-occurring [...] Genetic studies
indicate high heritability for ADHD, with estimates ranging from 70%–80% in both children and
adults, and gene-environment interactions contributing to neurodevelopmental risk." 1
Long waiting times for ADHD diagnosis in Danish child and adolescent psychiatry have inten-
sified alongside a sharp rise in diagnosed cases, increasing from about 1,000 children and adoles-
cents in 2001 to around 8,000 in 2011 (“National clinical guideline: ADHD / National Board of
Health”, 2025). Many children wait several months, and in some cases years, for an assessment,
delaying access to early intervention and educational support. Although Danish law guarantees
assessment within 30 days of referral, this requirement is often not met. The National Audit Office
has reported that around 70 percent of the assessment processes in child and adolescent psychiatry
exceed the statutory time limit. This shows a clear gap between legal rights and clinical practice.
When the 30-day limit is exceeded, families should be offered assessment through the expanded
free hospital choice. In practice, the option for free hospital choice is limited, which causes delays
to persist despite formal safeguards. The main cause of long waiting times is a sharp increase
in referrals for suspected ADHD, combined with a shortage of specialized professionals. At the
same time, services prioritize children with acute psychiatric conditions, often placing ADHD as-

ASD: Definition and Care Pathways for Diagnostic Assessment in Denmark
Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is characterized by two core behavioral dimensions: enduring
differences in social communication and the presence of restricted and repetitive behaviors, inter-
ests, or activities, emerging in early development and leading to meaningful functional impact.
Research increasingly highlights substantial variability in how these dimensions present across
developmental stages, sex, language abilities, and cognitive profiles (Leisman & Melillo, 2025).
Differences in social communication may involve reduced social reciprocity, atypical use of non-
verbal cues, and variations in peer relationships, but are often better understood as qualitatively
different patterns of interaction rather than simple deficits. Restricted and repetitive behaviors en-
compass motor stereotypies, a strong preference for sameness, sensory reactivity differences, and
highly focused interests; analytic studies suggest these features cluster into distinct subtypes with
different associations to anxiety, attention-related traits, and adaptive functioning. Behavioral pre-
sentation is further influenced by co-occurring conditions, with high rates of attention difficulties,
anxiety, mood symptoms, and sleep problems commonly observed alongside ASD (Leisman &
Melillo, 2025).
Autism spectrum disorder is one of the neurodevelopmental conditions with the strongest ge-
netic basis, as twin studies repeatedly report heritability estimates ranging from 64% to 91% (Leis-
man & Melillo, 2025). Evidence from family and sibling studies further supports this, showing
recurrence rates of approximately 10–20% among siblings, which underscores substantial familial
clustering (Leisman & Melillo, 2025).

Gaze Based Assessment Using Eye Tracking Devices & Techniques
Eye tracking is a well-established method in developmental research, as it is expected to give
objective and time-resolved measures of visual attention and oculomotor behaviour. In contrast
to traditional assessment approaches that depend on observer reports or retrospective accounts,
eye tracking captures children’s visual engagement with stimuli directly and in real time. This
capability makes the method particularly well-suited for investigating neurodevelopmental and
visual conditions, in which differences in attentional allocation, social orienting, and patterns of
visual exploration are core features.
In this review, two closely related strands are explored. The first focuses on identifying gaze
patterns and behavioral markers associated with specific neurodevelopmental conditions. The sec-
ond translates these findings into structured eye-tracking–based assessment tools intended to sup-

port early screening and supplementary clinical evaluation. The following subsections review these
strands in sequence.
2.2.1
Gaze Patterns
Gaze patterns, as measured through eye-tracking techniques, have been widely investigated in
relation to neurodevelopmental conditions in children and adolescents.
A review of 74 studies on eye-tracking in children and adolescents with ADHD demonstrates
correlations between eye-tracking metrics and ADHD-related symptoms. These metrics are cate-
gorized gaze-related measures into five groups: (1) fixation measures, including number of fixa-
tions, fixation duration, time to first fixation, fixation frequency or rate, and fixation latency; (2)
saccade measures, such as saccade latency, amplitude, accuracy, number, duration, velocity, and
peak saccadic velocity; (3) regression measures, including the number and duration of regressions;
(4) scanpath measures, reflecting overall scanpath characteristics; and (5) eye-vergence measures,
particularly the angle of vergence (Le et al., 2025).
Atypical gaze allocation patterns have also been consistently reported in children with autism
spectrum disorder (ASD) when compared to typically developing (TD) peers, particularly in re-
sponse to socially salient stimuli. Eye-tracking studies have shown that toddlers and preschool-
aged children with ASD exhibit reduced fixation time percentages to predefined areas of interest
(AOIs) across a range of stimulus types. Developmental differences have been observed specif-
ically in fixation to the eye region during face-processing tasks, suggesting altered visual atten-
tion strategies. Moreover, fixation-based gaze features have demonstrated discriminative potential
when analyzed using machine learning approaches, enabling classification between ASD and TD
children, with higher classification accuracy reported in toddlers than in preschool-aged children
(Kong et al., 2022).
These studies indicate the breadth of gaze metrics used in neurodevelopmental disorder re-
search.

information could be identified regarding the company’s current operational status. At the time of

Website not found” error. However, according to a LinkedIn conversation with co-founder Gustaf
Öqvist Seimyr of Optolexia, they have changed the company name to Lexplore and have become
more specifically focused on assessing children’s reading ability, which can be analysed in less

Dyslector is a newly developed application created by a Russian university’s HSE AI Research

2.3
Large-Scale Datasets in Pediatric Eye Tracking
Eye-tracking datasets are increasingly used in clinical and neurodevelopmental research to
address limitations and to complement traditional diagnostic approaches. The combination of
eye-tracking data and machine learning has significant potential for developing new ways for early
screening and diagnosis (Kong et al., 2022).
Large-scale longitudinal datasets further enhance the utility of eye tracking. One prominent ex-
ample is the "YOUth Cohort Study" by Utrecht University and UMC Utrecht, which tracks Dutch
children from pregnancy through early adulthood to understand how biological and environmental
factors influence neurocognitive and behavioral development. This cohort integrates eye-tracking
data with multimodal measures, including neuroimaging (EEG, sMRI, fMRI), behavioral assess-
ments, biological samples, and extensive questionnaires completed by children and their parents.
Data have been collected from nearly 4,000 participants across multiple age groups (e.g., ages 5–
7: approximately 250 participants; ages 8–10: approximately 1,350 participants; ages 11–16: ap-
proximately 500 participants). The dataset is available for research use through GDPR-compliant,
managed-access procedures (“YOUth Cohort Study - Utrecht University”, 2025).
Peekbank is an open, large-scale repository designed to support research on early language
development through developmental eye-tracking data, with a particular focus on children’s real-
time word recognition. The repository primarily aggregates data from looking-while-listening
paradigms, a widely used experimental method for examining how infants and toddlers process
spoken language as it unfolds (Zettersten et al., 2023).A key motivation behind Peekbank is to
address long-standing methodological limitations in developmental science, particularly the re-
liance on small, isolated datasets that hinder cumulative theory building. By pooling trial-level
==PAGE:36==
28

eye-tracking data from multiple laboratories and standardizing data formats and metadata, Peek-
bank enables large-scale, cross-study analyses that would otherwise be infeasible. In addition to
the curated datasets, Peekbank provides open-access tools and documentation that support data
reuse and reproducibility. These include conversion pipelines, analysis utilities, and a web-based
interface that facilitates data exploration and visualization. Together, these resources lower tech-
nical barriers to entry and promote collaborative, reproducible research practices across the field
(Peekbank Project, 2023).
OneStop Eye Movements is a notable step-change in publicly available, naturalistic reading
data: it aggregates 152 hours of eye-movement recordings from 360 participants reading English
newswire texts (2.6 million word tokens), enabling robust estimation of individual differences and
text-driven effects that are difficult to study with smaller corpora (Berzak et al., 2025). Beyond
scale, the dataset is distributed with documentation and curated files via the project’s public repos-
itory/landing pages, which support reuse for psycholinguistics and NLP evaluation (e.g., cognitive
validity tests for language models) (LACC Lab, 2025).
The European Learning Disorder Dataset (ELDD) is described as a large-scale collection of
4,243 eye-movement time-series recordings from children across Europe, compiled to support
data-hungry models (e.g., deep neural networks) for screening dyslexia and related learning dis-
orders. The recordings span both reading and non-reading oculomotor tasks (including saccade,
vergence, and reading tests), which is important because dyslexia-related oculomotor differences
can manifest beyond reading alone. In practice, this multi-task design can improve model robust-
ness by capturing complementary signatures of oculomotor control and reducing over-reliance on
any single task paradigm (El Hmimdi et al., 2024b; Nilsson Benfatto et al., 2016).
A key strength of ELDD is its clinical, multi-site character: data are reported to come from
collaborations with multiple clinical centers using standardized eye-tracking/analysis technology
(e.g., Remobi/AIDEAL), which better reflects real-world variability (noise, calibration drift, het-
erogeneous cohorts) than tightly controlled lab-only datasets (El Hmimdi et al., 2024b). This
realism is particularly relevant because domain shift and acquisition variability are frequent fail-

ure modes for medical ML systems (El Hmimdi et al., 2024b). At the same time, the dataset is
reported as not publicly available and accessible only upon reasonable request to the correspond-
ing author, which can limit reproducibility and independent benchmarking across research groups
(El Hmimdi et al., 2024a). Future value would be increased by clearer documentation of site-wise
protocols, stimulus materials, and recommended evaluation splits, as well as (where ethics allow)
a de-identified release or a controlled-access mechanism.
Despite their potential, large-scale pediatric eye-tracking datasets remain limited. Many avail-
able datasets include fewer than 100 or 200 participants. However, the small dataset size limits the
effective training of data-intensive algorithms and likely contributed to overfitting during model
evaluation, thereby reducing the reliability. Collecting high-quality eye-tracking data from chil-
dren presents practical challenges, including calibration difficulties, fatigue, motion artifacts, and
reduced accuracy or precision in fixation and saccade measurements. Data quality is strongly in-
fluenced by the expertise of the operator, highlighting the importance of training and standardized
data collection procedures (Hessels & Hooge, 2019),(Wass et al., 2013).
Moreover, ethical and regulatory constraints further limit data collection and sharing in pedi-
atric eye-tracking research. Research involving children raises ethical considerations for physician-
researchers, parents, clinical staff, and society at large. Such studies require informed consent
from legal guardians, age-appropriate assent from participants, and strict compliance with data
protection regulations. These requirements are particularly salient when working with sensitive
populations, such as children with neurodevelopmental disorders, where risks related to privacy,
stigmatization, and data misuse must be carefully managed (Engelhardt, 1979), (Webster, 1984).
Taken together, these practical and ethical constraints underscore the need for standardized,
ethically compliant eye-tracking protocols.
This includes clearly defined task designs, child-
appropriate calibration procedures, explicit data quality criteria, comprehensive documentation,
and robust governance frameworks. Establishing a standardized data collection protocol is espe-
cially important in the Danish context, where language specificity, cultural norms, and coordination
among multiple stakeholders (e.g., clinicians, researchers, educators, and parents) play a critical

role. Such protocol-level contributions are essential for ensuring data quality, reproducibility, and
responsible research practices, particularly when large-scale pediatric eye-tracking data collection
is difficult or not feasible.
2.4
Constraints for Eye-Tracking-Based Screening Tools
Implementing eye-tracking tools or eye-tracking-based data collection tools in school settings
or PPR, or any current Danish healthcare setting, requires digital literacy or training among the
users, e.g., educators and PPR employees, as studies show that eye-tracking data quality is known
to be affected by operator expertise (Hessels & Hooge, 2019).
2.5
Key Takeaways and Scope of Contribution
The literature shows clear progress in eye-tracking research and its use in supporting the di-
agnosis of children’s neurodevelopmental disorders in several countries. In Denmark, however,
eye-tracking is not part of the early diagnostic process. Early assessment is still mainly based on
observations, with limited use of objective digital tools.
There is limited evidence on how eye-tracking could be used in existing Danish healthcare
settings for children, such as schools, PPR services, or preventive health examinations. It is also
unclear how eye-tracking results could support early referrals without replacing clinical assess-
ment.
In addition, current research lacks standardized and age-appropriate protocols, especially for
children aged 5–12 years. Studies differ in tasks, calibration, duration, and data quality require-
ments, making results difficult to compare and unsuitable for real-world use outside laboratories.
Based on these findings, this study focuses on understanding stakeholders’ pain points, needs,
challenges, and expectations regarding the use of eye-tracking in Danish educational and health-
care pathways. The perspectives of parents, pedagogues, PPR professionals, general practitioners,
and experts are used to explore how eye-tracking could be realistically integrated into early assess-
ment and referral processes as a supportive tool rather than a diagnostic replacement.

3.2.1
Desk Research
Desk research is another name for secondary research, which involves reviewing existing stud-
ies, reports, and documented findings produced by others. It is not only about collecting data;
rather, previous research findings are reviewed to gain a broad understanding of the field (David,
2016).
Desk research was conducted as an initial stage to support the overall research process. Since
the use of eye tracking for early detection of neurodevelopmental and visual impairments was a
relatively new area for me, it was important to first gain background knowledge before engag-
ing in in-depth discussions with stakeholders. This helped build familiarity with key concepts,
existing approaches, the Danish context, and ongoing challenges in the field. Then, it was revis-
ited iteratively, and new in-depth desk research was conducted after getting more information from
stakeholder interviews and surveys, allowing literature findings and qualitative data to complement
each other throughout the study.
The selected search terms included early childhood screening, neurodevelopmental disorders
(with a particular focus on autism spectrum disorder (ASD), attention disorders such as ADHD,
dyslexia, and visual impairments), as well as eye tracking and gaze patterns. The search encom-
passed work from Denmark, the European Union, and international contexts.
Sources were accessed through Perplexity and Gemini web searches to academic databases,
including Google Scholar, PubMed, IEEE Xplore, and DTU Findit, as well as online blogs, news
articles, and Danish websites.

In the Ideate phase, the idea was translated into a structured exploration of the concepts. This

In this study, the POV statements were derived from the Rich Picture, User Journey Map, inter-

During the Ideate phase, the POV statements were used as reference points to guide and assess
emerging concept directions.

A set of “How Might We” (HMW) questions was formulated to reframe synthesized challenges

In this thesis, prototyping is defined as developing a conceptual protocol artifact rather than

The protocol is iteratively refined from documented stakeholder materials (interviews, prior

mark’s decentralized system (state, regions, municipalities) and describes how responsibilities are
distributed across early childhood education, schools, municipal services, and healthcare. In rela-
tion to pediatric visual screening, the thesis synthesizes EU-Screen–based tables of who conducts
screening across age groups (e.g., youth health nurses and GPs) and which tests are used (e.g.,
fixation/cover tests at younger ages; visual acuity screening in the 3–7 years age group).
Gaze markers as potential screening signals.
The literature review organizes eye-tracking mea-
sures used in NDD research and highlights that gaze metrics commonly include fixation-related
measures, saccades, regressions, scanpaths, and vergence measures. For ASD, the review em-
phasizes atypical gaze allocation in response to socially salient stimuli, including altered fixation
to faces/eye regions, and notes reported discriminative potential when fixation-based features are
analyzed using machine-learning approaches.
Tools, datasets, and implementation gaps.
The desk research includes examples of eye-tracking
based tools (e.g., EarliPoint as an FDA-cleared autism diagnostic aid within a narrow age range),
and synthesizes broader limitations: scarce large-scale pediatric datasets, lack of standardized age-
appropriate protocols across contexts, and limited evidence about integration into Danish practice.
These gaps directly motivate RQ4 (standardization, ethics, feasibility) and shape the scope of the
conceptual protocol in this thesis.

4.6
Synthesis: Answers to the Research Questions
This section synthesizes the findings presented in Chapter 4 to provide integrated answers
to the four research questions (RQ1–RQ4). The synthesis is grounded in stakeholder interviews
and surveys, desk research, and design-oriented analysis artifacts developed through the Design
Thinking framework. The answers reflect the exploratory and conceptual nature of the study and
do not claim empirical validation beyond the documented evidence.
RQ1: Stakeholders, methods, and diagnostic pathways.
The findings indicate that early iden-
tification and diagnosis of neurodevelopmental concerns in Denmark is a distributed, multi-stakeholder
process involving parents, educators, municipal Pedagogical Psychological Counselling (PPR),
general practitioners, and specialized child and adolescent psychiatric services. Initial concerns
typically emerge in everyday educational settings, where pedagogues and teachers observe behav-
ioral, learning, or social difficulties and initiate documentation and dialogue with parents. PPR
functions as a central municipal gatekeeper, coordinating observations, administering standardized
assessments, and determining whether referral to specialized psychiatric services is warranted.
Across stakeholder accounts, diagnostic practices rely predominantly on observational methods,
questionnaires, and resource-intensive standardized tests, with limited use of objective digital

61
screening tools. The pathway is characterized by extended time frames(over years), multiple han-
dovers between institutions, and substantial administrative effort across sectors.
RQ2: Potential role of eye tracking in early screening.
Based on stakeholder perceptions and
synthesis of existing literature, eye tracking is identified as a potentially supportive screening ap-
proach rather than a diagnostic replacement within the Danish context. The study does not demon-
strate empirical effects of eye tracking on reducing waiting times, improving diagnostic accuracy,
or expediting referrals. Instead, the findings suggest that eye tracking could plausibly complement
existing identification and referral pathways by providing short, non-invasive, and objective signals
that may support earlier clarification and triage when interpreted alongside professional judgment.
Its potential role is therefore conceptual and conditional, grounded in perceived feasibility, ac-
ceptability, and prior research evidence rather than demonstrated outcomes in real-world Danish
educational or healthcare structures.
RQ3: Stakeholder experiences of the diagnostic journey.
Stakeholders experience the current
diagnostic journey as lengthy, fragmented, and emotionally and administratively burdensome. Par-
ents report stress, uncertainty, and limited guidance while navigating prolonged waiting periods,
whereas educators and PPR professionals describe sustained documentation workloads, capacity
constraints, and delays before assessments or referrals are completed. These experiences indicate
that strain accumulates across multiple stages of the pathway rather than at a single clinical point.
Despite these challenges, stakeholders express cautious openness toward eye tracking as a support-
ive tool, provided that it does not increase workload or child burden, is comfortable and acceptable
for children, and is clearly communicated as decision support rather than a standalone assessment
or diagnosis.
RQ4: Key considerations for a standardized and ethical eye-tracking protocol.
The study
identifies several key considerations for developing an ethically grounded and contextually appro-
priate eye-tracking screening protocol. These include short session duration, child comfort and

ent data governance and informed consent procedures, and explicit safeguards against misinterpre-

Conclusion
5.1
Outcome of This Study
This thesis addressed four research questions (RQ1–RQ4) through a design-oriented process
informed by the Design Thinking framework. Empirical input consisted of desk research and lit-
erature review combined with limited stakeholder engagement (interviews and qualitative surveys
across a small number of stakeholders). The main outcomes are therefore best understood as ex-
ploratory and conceptual rather than as validated evidence of effectiveness.
First, the thesis provides an initial Denmark-context mapping of the early identification and
referral journey for children with suspected neurodevelopmental concerns. The mapping inte-
grates desk research with stakeholder perspectives to describe roles, handovers, bottlenecks, and
points where strain accumulates (e.g., coordination delays, long waiting periods, and administra-
tive workload). Given the limited stakeholder sample, the mapping should be interpreted as a
structured synthesis of available evidence rather than as a representative account of all municipal-
ities or professional roles.
Second, the thesis consolidates relevant literature on gaze-based markers, eye-tracking–based
tools, and implementation constraints. Rather than demonstrating clinical performance, the re-
view identifies what types of gaze measures and task paradigms have been reported, where ev-
idence is strongest (often in younger age ranges and controlled settings), and where gaps re-
main—particularly around standardization, operator dependence, and real-world feasibility for
children aged 5–12.

CHAPTER 5. CONCLUSION
Third, the thesis proposes a conceptual eye-tracking screening support protocol specification.
The protocol is explicitly framed as a supportive aid, not as a diagnostic instrument. It trans-
lates stakeholder constraints and literature-based considerations into protocol-level requirements
(e.g., short duration, child comfort and engagement, trained administration, feasible setup con-
ditions, transparent governance and consent, and safeguards against misinterpretation including
visual-impairment confounds). Because no empirical testing was conducted, the protocol should
be treated as a requirements-driven design artifact intended to support future piloting and refine-
ment.
Finally, the thesis illustrates how a design-oriented approach can be used to connect pathway
analysis with protocol design under real-world constraints. The value of this approach in the
current work lies in traceability from stakeholder concerns and literature gaps to specific protocol
requirements. Future work is required to operationalize and validate the protocol through empirical
studies and implementation evaluation.
5.2
Limitations
This project has several limitations that should be acknowledged. These limitations affect how
the results can be understood and how the proposed ideas can be used in future work.
Although many stakeholders were contacted, only a small number of them were able to partic-
ipate. This means the journey maps and reflections are based on limited input, and they may not
represent the full variation of experiences across different municipalities or professional roles in
Denmark.
The study did not include full data collection with children. Because no eye–tracking sessions
were carried out during the thesis period, it was not possible to measure calibration rates, data
quality, or children’s reactions to the tasks. As a result, the protocol remains a design proposal
rather than a tested method.
Many conclusions in this thesis are based on published studies and reports. Since equipment,
age groups, and methods differ from study to study, the findings cannot be transferred directly to
==PAGE:73==
5.3. FUTURE WORKS
65
a Danish school or health setting without further testing. This also means the proposed protocol
may need adjustments once real data are collected.
Eye–tracking has shown promising results, but its use in everyday screening is still new. Most
evidence comes from controlled laboratory environments. Schools and clinics may face challenges
such as lighting conditions, children’s movement, glasses, or limited staff training. These factors
could reduce data quality and were not tested in this project.
The protocol has been designed to fit into short time slots and to be easy to use. However,
practical issues such as the time needed to set up equipment, the availability of devices, and privacy
rules for storing gaze data may limit how easily the protocol can be used in real settings. These
aspects require further work and were not examined in detail.
The project focuses on children aged 5–12. This is useful for school settings, but many early
signs of neurodevelopmental conditions appear in younger children. Because of this, the findings
do not apply to screening in infancy or preschool age, where eye–tracking has been studied more
often.
Eye–tracking patterns are not unique to one condition. For example, reduced attention to faces
or unstable gaze can appear in several developmental or visual difficulties. Since no data were
collected to compare different groups, the study cannot show how specific or sensitive the proposed
tasks would be in practice.
Overall, the thesis provides a first step toward designing a child-friendly eye–tracking protocol,
but further work is needed to test the tasks, adjust the procedure, and confirm that the protocol fits
well into Danish schools and health services.
5.3
Future Works
This thesis is an exploratory study that proposes a conceptual eye-tracking–based screening
support approach informed by stakeholder insights and literature.
Because no empirical eye-
tracking data were collected and stakeholder participation was limited, the findings should be un-
derstood as a first design step rather than a validated solution. Based on these limitations, several
==PAGE:74==
5.3. FUTURE WORKS
CHAPTER 5. CONCLUSION
directions for future work are identified.
First, the proposed protocol needs to be operationalized and tested with children aged 5–12.
Future studies should define concrete tasks, stimuli, calibration procedures, data quality thresholds,
and output measures that are appropriate for this age group. Practical challenges that were not
examined in this project—such as children’s movement, short attention spans, use of glasses, and
suspected visual impairments—should be explicitly addressed.
Second, a small feasibility pilot should be conducted in Danish school or PPR settings. Since
no eye-tracking sessions were carried out in this study, such a pilot is necessary to evaluate setup
time, calibration success, data quality, child acceptance, and staff training requirements. This
would help determine whether the protocol is realistic outside controlled research environments.
Third, the added value of the protocol should be evaluated by comparing its outputs with ex-
isting assessment tools already used in Danish practice. This is important because eye-tracking
patterns are not specific to a single condition, and without comparative data it is not possible to
assess sensitivity, specificity, or practical usefulness.
Finally, ethical and implementation aspects require further work. Consent and assent proce-
dures suitable for school settings, data handling and storage practices, clear communication of
results, and safeguards against misinterpretation or labeling should be tested in practice. Atten-
tion should also be given to how the protocol fits into existing workflows and whether it can be
implemented fairly and consistently across different settings.
Overall, future work should focus on moving from a conceptual design to empirical testing and
real-world evaluation, to determine whether the proposed approach can responsibly support early
identification processes in Danish schools and health services.

Me: What do you usually check during these routine visits?
GP01: We check whether the baby has two ears, normal hands and legs, ten fingers, and whether

Me: Do you ask parents about concerns or worries?
GP01: Yes, we ask if there is anything the parents are worried or concerned about regarding the

sits idle. When children attend vuggestuer or børnehaver, we ask whether they interact with
other children. We also ask about the relationship with siblings, if they have any, and the
relationship with the parents.
Me: What do you focus on when children are four or five years old, or are there other key ages?
GP01: Especially at four and five years of age, we are very concerned with understanding the
child’s social and mental development. We look at whether the child can try to write, sing a
rhyme or a song, or communicate with other children. However, in most cases, these issues
are noticed by pedagogues, who then contact the kommune and involve a social psychologist.
Me: Can you share an example case from your experience?
GP01: For example, a four-year-old child came a few days ago. The parents said the child was

clearly from about three meters away and whether the child made the eyes very small while
using an iPad. We also try to notice whether the child makes loud sounds while speaking,
such as “vel, vel,” which may indicate hearing issues.

GP01: Even if someone comes with neurodevelopmental issues, we as general practitioners can-

GP01: I am not sure, but before children move from vuggestue to børnehave, pedagogues assess

and if we think the issue is not related to psychiatry, we refer the child to a general children’s
hospital, not a child psychiatric hospital. We mainly document the case and advise parents
to talk with the kommune, and we provide notes when requested.
