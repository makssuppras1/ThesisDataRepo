Abstract
DTU Wind Energy
Kaimal-based constrained turbulence for loads predictions with lidar
by Aikaterini Mitsakou
This thesis investigates the ability of a constrained-turbulence technique to recreate
turbulence from Lidar measurements, with the goal of improving the accuracy of aeroe-
lastic loads validations. The constrained-turbulence methodology is based on the Kaimal
spectrum with exponential coherence, and is used by a tool which is called PyConTurb.
Two Lidar types whose names are WindVision and ZX-TM, are being evaluated based
on a four-beam and circular pattern geometries respectively. The project focuses on
two research questions which are based on adjusting the constrained turbulence to more
realistic conditions for the Lidar technology. The ﬁrst question considers the low fre-
quency of Lidars, and how it should be handled with respect to reconstruction. The
second research question addresses the blade blockage in the signal, and its eﬀect on the
reconstructed turbulence. These research questions are also evaluated in terms of the
impact on aeroelastic simulations of the DTU 10 MW Reference Wind Turbine through
use of the HAWC2 simulation tool.
The ﬁrst question is handled by extrapolation of the spectrum and the second one via
using a Least Squares Spectral Analysis method for spectrum reconstruction. In general,
the results show that the methods that are used are quite suﬃcient, and is also veriﬁed
by the HAWC2 results. In addition, the ZX-TM Lidar is proved to be more eﬃcient
with respect to the reconstruction of results when compared to WindVision. The project
is carried out in conjunction with researchers from Siemens-Gamesa Renewable Energy
(SGRE).

Chapter 1
Introduction
The general scope of this thesis derives from the large interest within the Wind Energy
industry toward the stochastic wind turbulence ﬁelds, and their eﬀect on the mechanical
loads that stress the wind turbines.
Although there is a great progress in the wind
measurement techniques and technology, the wind speed measurements are not suﬃcient
enough in order to evaluate the turbulent ﬂow. This is due to its extremely complex
nature in connection with the large volumes and length scales.
In numerical loads
simulations, the turbulent wind is traditionally modelled as a random three-dimensional
Gaussian ﬁeld with spectral properties, which is deﬁned by a turbulence model. The
most popular and eﬀective ways to model the wind turbulence are through the Kaimal
spectrum and the Mann turbulence model.[4]
On that premise, the current thesis focuses on the ability of a constrained-turbulence
technique to recreate turbulence from Lidar measurements with the goal of improving the
accuracy of aeroelastic load validations. The main tool that is used for the simulations is
called PyConTurb, and its constrained-turbulence methodology is based on the Kaimal
spectrum with exponential coherence.[5] The two Lidar types that were chosen for the
simulations are called WindVision and ZX-TM, and they are based on a 4-beam and a
circular pattern, respectively. The wind turbine that was selected for the simulations is
the DTU 10MW Reference Wind Turbine.
The basic purpose of this project is to investigate two main research questions which
derives from the combination of the Lidar technology and the PyConTurb reconstruc-
tion method. The research questions are deﬁned together with our collaborators from
Siemens-Gamesa Renewable Energy (SGRE).
The ﬁrst question considers the low frequency of Lidars, and how it should be handled
regarding reconstruction.
Most of the Lidar models that are used for wind turbine
monitoring purposes, operate with relatively low sampling frequency.
However, the
turbulence boxes dimensions that are used in constrained turbulence techniques have a
higher time resolution. Thus, there is a need to adjust a low frequency signal to a high
frequency turbulence box in order to be able to compare the synthetic unconstrained
turbulence ﬁeld with the constrained turbulence and hence evaluate the examined Lidar
type.

The second question addresses the blade blockage in the signal, and its eﬀect on the
reconstructed turbulence. Both Lidar technologies that are used in the simulations are
mounted on the nacelle, and as a result the measurements are aﬀected substantially by
blade blockage. At ﬁrst, calculation and determination of the amount to which the signal
is aﬀected by blade blockage is conducted, with respect to the technical characteristics
of DTU 10MW. Finally, a methodology needed to be determined in order to handle the
signal and thus evaluate to what degree the reconstruction is aﬀected.
Finally, the last section of the report is based on using the HAWC2 aero-elastic code
on the DTU 10MW RWT. The purpose of the HAWC2 simulations is to evaluate the
aeroelastic behavior of the wind turbine in response to the constrained turbulence boxes
that were extracted from PyConTurb.

Chapter 2
Lidar analysis and Line of sight
This chapter presents the background of the lidar models that will be used in this thesis
and the conversion methodology to line-of-sight.
The principle known as lidar (LiDAR - Light Detection And Ranging) was invented in
the 1960s.[6] With this remote sensing technique, aerosols (tiny particles) in the air are
used to reﬂect an emitted laser beam. These particles are assumed to have the same
velocity as the wind itself.[1] In ﬁgure 2.1 a schematic view of this principle is presented.
Figure 2.1: Schematic representation of lidar system [1]
A wind lidar relies on the Doppler eﬀect (a slight change in the frequency of the back-
scattered light), which is caused from the moving air-borne particles that pass through
the laser beam’s focus point in the atmosphere. The Doppler frequency shift gives direct
information on the wind speed component along the line-of-sight of the beam. Due to
the ultra low power levels of the typical back-scattered light associated with the lower
atmospheric boundary layer, a very sensitive optical detection scheme must be employed.
This detection method must have a relatively high powered and narrow-line width laser
to be eﬀective.[6]
2.1
Lidars geometry and characteristics
The lidar models that were chosen to be examined in the following simulations are the
WindVision and ZX-TM. The primary reasoning for this selection is that both lidar
types share a fair amount of common features, this enables them to be comparable and
ensures consistent results. Furthermore, another reason is that these lidar models are
also of great interest for the collaborators from SGRE. There is a detailed description
for each of the chosen lidars in the following subsections.

2.1.1
WindVision lidar
The WindVision, which could also be described as a four-beam lidar, is a continuous
wave laser, manufactured by the company Windar Photonics. WindVision has a lot of
potential to enhance the wind turbine operation through operations management. Some
of these are wake, turbulence, gust and shear detection, and also yaw correction. The
lidar features four beams that are arranged in 18◦half-cone angle, which are then able
to measure in both the vertical and horizontal plane. The WindVision lidar operates at
a wind speed range of 2m/s - 75m/s, with a data output rate of 4Hz (1 full scan per
second).[6]

2.2(b). The diameter of the circle includes the lidar points and is deﬁned by the upwind
distance between the wind turbine and the measurement points (which is mentioned in
table 2.1) and the 18◦half-cone angle.

2.1.2
ZX-TM lidar
The ZX-TM, which could also be described as a circular lidar, consists of a three-
dimensional continuous wave lidar optical head and is manufactured by the company
ZX Lidars. This lidar model samples the measurements in a circular pattern with either
15◦or 30◦half-cone angles.[7] The simulations in this report use a circular pattern with
15◦half-cone angle. ZX-TM operates at wind speed range of 0.5m/s - 45m/s (with
loading survivability up to 70m/s), with a data output rate of 50Hz (1 full scan per
second).[7]

PyConTurb simulations
This chapter will analyse the procedure that was executed to generate the turbulence
boxes with the PyConTurb tool, and how the main research questions were handled in
accordance with PyConTurb speciﬁcations.
The ﬁrst tool that was used in the simulations is called PyConTurb, whose abbrevia-
tion is derived from Python’s Constrained Turbulence application. PyConTurb is an
open-source tool which eﬃciently generates turbulence boxes, which are constrained by
measurements at multiple points in multiple directions. The above-mentioned tool uses a
novel algorithm based on the Kaimal Spectrum with an Exponential Coherence (KSEC)
simulation technique, and it plays a pivotal role in loads validations.[5]
PyConTurb is also available in the following git repository:
https://gitlab.windenergy.dtu.dk/pyconturb/pyconturb.
The scripts that have been used for the current simulations are available in the following
git repository: https://gitlab.windenergy.dtu.dk/pyconturb/katerina-thesis
3.1
PyConTurb methodology
The KSEC method, is one of two main constrained turbulence simulation techniques,
together with the Mann turbulence model technique. Through these tools the turbu-
lence simulation is constrained in a speciﬁc way, so that it resembles the real measure-
ments. The turbulence generation in PyConTurb occurs in the Fourier domain, where
the Fourier magnitudes are deterministic and deﬁned according to the provided Kaimal
power spectrum. At ﬁrst, all the phases are randomly sampled, while afterwards the
ones corresponding to the longitudinal turbulence are correlated using the Cholesky de-
composition of the coherence matrix.[5] The PyConTurb simulations used in this thesis
employs the default IEC 61400-1 parameters.
An important advantage that comes with the use of the KSEC method, is that it al-
lows PyConTurb to be computationally fast, while also avoiding the memory problems
associated with the Mann turbulence model. However, the PyConTurb tool comes with
two major drawbacks when compared with the Mann model. The ﬁrst of these issues
10
==PAGE:20==
Chapter 3
11
is that it does not follow the conservation of mass, and the second is that the stan-
dard KSEC model deﬁned in IEC 61400-1 does not feature coherence in the lateral and
vertical components (in the y and z axes). These could be considered signiﬁcantly un-
acceptable assumptions as real wind has coherence in both the v and w components.
Hence, the lack of cross-component coherence could potentially aﬀect the resulting load
calculations, since the information from v and w turbulence components are ignored. [5]
PyConTurb oﬀers a variety of possibilities for the generation of turbulence boxes. How-
ever, other than generating turbulence (being the main operation), PyConTurb also
includes functions in order to regulate the mean wind speed proﬁles and to deﬁne the
turbulence standard deviation and turbulence power spectrum variation. [8]

3.4.2
Spectral extrapolation methodology
In this section, there is a need to adapt the baseline scenario to more realistic conditions
regarding frequency. The chosen frequency in the baseline simulations is 13.653Hz, (as is
mentioned in table 3.1) and does not correspond to a representative value of the chosen
Lidar models. Both WindVision and ZX-TM measures with a frequency of 1Hz for a full
scan, as is referred in subsections 2.1.1 and 2.1.2. Therefore, it is necessary to ﬁnd a way
to extrapolate the u vectors of the turbulence box and create a constrained turbulence
box, which will be comparable in size to the unconstrained one.
One possible technique to create a full turbulence box with less sampling points than
the necessary ones for a speciﬁc simulation period, is by extrapolating the spectra of
the equivalent time-series. The most eﬀective way to do this is by ﬁtting a -5/3 power
law line to the higher frequencies of the spectrum, or by ﬁtting a Kaimal spectrum to
the entire data spectrum. As previously mentioned in the beginning of this chapter,
PyConTurb is based on the Kaimal spectrum. Hence, by ﬁtting the Kaimal general
equation to the spectrum for the full frequency range, we could get a ﬁnal spectrum
very close to the original one. Since the Kaimal spectrum follows the trend of the -5/3
power law, a possible -5/3 extension at the higher frequencies has also the potential to
provide satisfying results.
Therefore, for the simulations in this thesis the method of -5/3 spectrum extrapolation
was chosen for implementation. The demonstration of this method will focus on one
single u vector for simplicity.

calculated from PyConTurb for 8192 sampling points and a simulation period of 600s.
Then, the signal is downsampled to a lower frequency sampling rate. For example, 1
in 8 measurements corresponds to 1024 points, which is close to approximately 1 Hz of
sampling frequency. More speciﬁcally, the frequency obtained is (8192/8)/600 = 1.7Hz.

3.5
Summary of simulations
To summarise, the PyConTurb simulations begin with a baseline scenario where the
basic variables and conditions that have been deﬁned throughout the thesis’ description
are being used (i.e. Lidar types, wind turbine type etc.) without further restrictions.
These kinds of simulations are a good chance to examine how the results of constrained
turbulence are in more ideal conditions.
Another opportunity for these simulations
would be to conduct a sensitivity analysis to examine how much the randomness of the
seed numbers for the turbulence generation would aﬀect the results.
In the downsampling scenario, a certain number of measurements are sampled in order
to investigate to what degree a much lower frequency could aﬀect the constrained turbu-
lence. Since downsampling u vectors are characterized by equally spaced measurements,
one way to approach this issue is by extrapolating the power spectrum and using the
Fourier transform to reconstruct the time-series.
Finally, in the blade blockage scenario, the primary diﬀerence with downsampling is
that the measurements are not equally spaced. This characteristic makes it impossible
to use the same method of reconstruction as is used in downsampling case. Thus, the
PSD is approached with the Least Squares Spectral Analysis method, and then further
extrapolated in the same way as in downsampling scenario.

4.3
Downsampling simulations
A primary issue involved in increasing the sample time is that a signiﬁcant amount of
information is lost from both the spectrum and the time-series.
Two main ways to
manage this issue is to ﬁt a −5/3 power law to the higher frequencies or by ﬁtting a
Kaimal spectrum to the original spectrum as is also described in subsection 3.4.2. For
the simulations of this thesis, the spectrum is chosen to be extrapolated with the -5/3
power law.
In this subsection, the Vlos vectors of Lidar points from the original turbulence wind
ﬁeld are ﬁrst selected.
The unconstrained (original) box and Lidar points have an
original sampling frequency of 13.65Hz. The downsampling method in these simulations
is determined by choosing one for every eight points (1 in 8), which results in a lower
sampling frequency of approximately 1Hz (1.7 Hz). Afterwards, the Power Spectrum
Density (PSD) is ﬁrst calculated and then extrapolated with the -5/3 power law. This
extrapolation occurs until it reaches a high enough frequency that represents the original
wind ﬁeld given by 13.67Hz, as described thoroughly in chapter 3.4.2.
What is of most interest to be investigated in this subsection, is an error analysis of the
Vlos vectors. While extrapolating the Vlos spectrum and creating the Fourier vector,
the ﬁrst part of the vector is calculated with the original spectrum. However, the second
remaining part is computed via equation 3.2, where the magnitudes are given by the
extrapolated spectrum and the phases are chosen randomly between 0 and 2π through
use of a seed number. In the current simulations the random seed number that deﬁnes
the phases is examined. More speciﬁcally, an error analysis is conducted for diﬀerent
random seed numbers to investigate how the downsampled and extrapolated Vlos vectors
are aﬀected by the random seed number with respect to their phase.
The process of Vlos vector extrapolation is presented in ﬁgures 4.8 and 4.9 for WindVi-
sion and ZX-TM respectively.

4.4
Blade blockage simulations
The blockage addition represents the second area of interest included in this thesis after
downsampling. This section includes all the simulations correspond to blade blockage,
whose methodology is described in more detail in subsection 3.4.3. The main diﬀerence
between downsampling and blade blockage is that the measurements of the ﬁrst are
equally spaced while the measurements of the second are not.
This means that the
Fourier transformation method which is used in the reconstruction of the time-series in
downsampling is not an option in blade blockage, hence an alternative solution must
be found. One possible technique that was used is the Least Square Spectral Analysis
==PAGE:53==
Chapter 4
44
(LSSA) method for the calculation of PSD. Then, the PSD is extrapolated with the -5/3
power law, as is described in subsection 3.4.2.
The blade blockage addition is applied in Vlos vectors in much the same as in the
downsampling case described in section 4.3. The blade blockage in this thesis is deﬁned
through methodology which is based on the DTU 10MW geometry, and is described
in subsection 3.4.3.
According to the aforementioned blade blockage method, it has
been concluded that a percentage of 87% of the total measurements are blocked. Hence,
after the blade blockage is applied on the Vlos vectors, the LSSA method is used to
reconstruct the PSD for the unblocked measurements. Then through usage of a spectral
extrapolation the reconstructed time-series of Vlos vectors are created. Finally, it is
worth mentioning that the initial reconstructed time-series from the LSSA method would
have a diﬀerence in both the standard deviation and mean value. This is also presented
in detail in the methodology subsection 3.4.3 located in ﬁgure 3.13(a). Hence, a scaling
is applied according to the measured values in order to adjust the reconstructed Vlos
vectors.
In ﬁgure 4.11, there is a more detailed demonstration of blade blockage in Vlos vectors
for the WindVision and ZX-TM Lidars after scaling. In ﬁgures 4.11(a) and 4.11(e),
there is a representation of the measured and reconstructed Vlos after the LSSA and
spectral extrapolation for WindVision and ZX-TM. It can be seen that the measured
values are relatively poor in regards to providing information.
This is because the
unblocked measurements are unequally spaced. Then, ﬁgures 4.11(b), 4.11(c), 4.11(d)
and 4.11(f), 4.11(g), 4.11(h) present the time-series in the full simulation time and in
the more speciﬁc time-frame of 0 - 50s. Furthermore, they also display the PSD’s with
blade blockage after reconstruction with the LSSA method and spectral extrapolation,
and are compared with the original Vlos for WindVision and ZX-TM respectively.
The time-series for the full simulation time illustrate that the LSSA method works quite
well for reconstruction. However, in the focused time-series, it seems that there are more
diﬀerences than the equivalent time-series with downsampling as shown in ﬁgures 4.8
and 4.9. This is the case even though the percentage of measurements is similar in both
downsampling and blade blockage of approximately 13%. The most possible cause for
this diﬀerence in results is that the reconstruction of PSD with the LSSA method is
conducted for a small range of frequencies. While the spectral extrapolation using the
-5/3 power law is not very successful at low frequencies since the PSD usually has a
noisy trend that shifts the extrapolation, hence the discrepancy observed.

This section concludes with a comparison of all the most important error results that
were presented in this chapter. After all the error analyses that were conducted during

the mean values of MAE and MxAE are presented in ﬁgures 4.16 and 4.17. Where

As mentioned in sections 4.3 and 4.4, there is an interest to examine how the Vlos
reconstruction with spectral extrapolation is aﬀected by the seed number that is used
for random phases in the calculation of Fourier vectors. Furthermore, there is an interest
in how the downsampling and blade blockage aﬀects the Vlos reconstruction in general.
From ﬁgures 4.16(a) and 4.16(b), it can be concluded that the mean values of MAE
and (to a smaller degree) that of MxAE, are not too aﬀected by the seed numbers
that is used in phase generation. When comparing downsampling with blade blockage,
the diﬀerences of error mostly derive from the spectral extrapolation which starts a
lot earlier in the frequency range for blade blockage (ﬁgures 4.11(d), 4.11(h)), rather
than for downsampling (ﬁgures 4.8(f), 4.9(f)). This means that the noise trends that
are experienced in the spectrum shifts the extrapolation, and increases the error of the
blade blockage reconstruction results. That is also the reason for the larger diﬀerence
between WindVision and ZX-TM in downsampling when compared with blade blockage.
The reconstructed spectra of WindVision and ZX-TM in blade blockage are similar to
each other since the frequency range is small. Regarding downsampling, the diﬀerence
in error between WindVision and ZX-TM is derived from the Vlos calculation and the
bigger half-cone angle of WindVision which creates stronger noise within the higher
frequencies and as a result poor spectral extrapolation (ﬁgures 4.8(f), 4.9(f)).

Chapter 5
HAWC2 simulations and results
The aeroelastic code HAWC2 is mainly used for calculating wind turbine response in
the time domain.
It has ﬁrst been developed since 2003 at the aeroelastic research
programme at Risoe in National laboratory of Denmark. [2]
The turbine is modelled by an assembly of bodies, which are mainly the tower, the shaft
and the blades, and are connected with constrained equations that allow only certain
movements between the bodies. A main body consists also of several bodies each with
a node distribution, this allows data at these particular points within the system. The
aerodynamic part of the code is based on the Blade Element Momentum theory. The
control of the turbine is performed through one or more DLL (Dynamic Link Library)
ﬁles. [2]
5.1
Simulations set-up
The model that is used in the current thesis is based on the virtual DTU 10-MW reference
wind turbine designed as part of the Light Rotor project which is a collaboration between
the Wind Energy Department at the Technical University of Denmark and Vestas.[3]
The main input is an htc ﬁle which contains all the information of the model together
with the time simulator. Then through use of the htc ﬁle, a number of other ﬁles are
also being called. These include: the data of the system (.dat ﬁles), the control data
of the system (.dll ﬁles) and the turbulence box which is generated from the respective
PyConTurb simulation which is examined. The data and control ﬁles, which are used in
the current simulations, represent the ”10-MW Reference Wind Turbine v 9.1” version
found from the following website: https://www.hawc2.dk/download/hawc2-model/
dtu-10-mw-reference-wind-turbine/hawc2_9_1
The simulations are conducted by calling the HAWC2 executable from a Windows Com-
mand Prompt on the input .htc ﬁle, and the outputs are two binary ﬁles. There are
diﬀerent options of outputs that can be selected for HAWC2 simulations. However for
this thesis two ﬁles were generated as outputs: a data ﬁle with the standard extension
.dat, and another one with the same name but the extension .sel.

Chapter 6
Final discussion and future research
The ﬁnal results of the thesis indicate that the methods that have been employed have
worked suﬃciently well. However, there is still room for improvement, and a need for
further research and investigation which will be discussed in the following list:
• Within this thesis, it was decided to use the Lidars in accordance to the manu-
facturers technical speciﬁcations in order to investigate their capability. However,
another approach would involve a reconstruction analysis with diﬀerent half-cone
angles and Lidar patterns. This would optimize the reconstruction success rate
and test the limits of PyConTurb.
• In the downsampling case, the spectrum is extrapolated by ﬁtting a -5/3 power
law to the higher frequencies within the spectrum.
Since PyConTurb is based
on a Kaimal spectrum and our synthetic time-series are produced by PyConTurb,
another possible technique for spectrum extrapolation could be a Kaimal spectrum
ﬁtting to the downsampled power spectrum. This suggested method is reinforced,
since the results of the -5/3 power extrapolation from this thesis show that the
method is not fully successful since the high amplitude of spectrum shifts the
extension from the targeted position. Thus, it would be of great interest to explore
other methods such as the Kaimal spectrum ﬁtting in order to achieve better
performance in reconstruction.
• A lot of variables are used as constants through all the simulations (i.e. wind
speed, standard deviation, etc.)
in order to investigate speciﬁc questions and
conduct diﬀerent sensitivity analyses. However, the wind speed is a deterministic
variable and ideally, it would be more appropriate to have a series of simulations
where the wind speed changes in order to also explore the other regions (regions
1 and 3) of power curve of DTU 10MW in the aeroelastic simulations.
• The error techniques that have been used in this thesis show some important results
that in general make sense. However, when a signiﬁcant event like blade blockage
is applied, a smaller increase than expected of the mean of the Mean Absolute error
(MAE) and/or the Maximum Absolute error (MxAE) is experienced compared to
the baseline scenario (where no special restrictions are applied). That is because
the Lidar aﬀected area is quite small in relation to the entire turbulence box.
59
==PAGE:69==
Chapter 6
60
Thus, it would be a wise idea to test additional error calculation methods, and
also calculate the error in speciﬁc focus areas within the turbulence box in order
to be able to investigate in more detail the results of each method.
• Due to the fact that in blade blockage the measurements are not equally spaced, the
Least Squares Spectral Analysis (LSSA) method is used in order to reconstruct the
spectrum. This method has proven to be very reliable, even in the extreme cases
where 87% of measurements are blocked. However, the fact that it uses a small
range of Nyquist frequency could be considered a drawback in Vlos reconstruction.
Ideally, an exploration of additional relevant techniques could be beneﬁcial to result
in an even more successful Vlos reconstruction with a higher frequency range (i.e.,
Nyquist-like limit) where they are sensitive to. One suggested alternative could
be the Lomb-Scargle Periodogram, which is a well-known algorithm for detecting
and characterizing periodic signals in unevenly sampled data [13].
• Finally, as mentioned several times during the report, the KSEC method is one of
the two main constrained turbulence techniques together with the Mann turbulence
model. So, a one-on-one comparison with real or Lidar synthetic measurements
could be another very interesting topic for future research.

Chapter 7
Conclusions
The main purpose of this thesis was to investigate the ability of a constrained turbulence
technique to recreate turbulence from Lidar measurements through use of the PyCon-
Turb tool. The main questions that were chosen to be investigated derived from the
combination of Lidar technology coupled with the PyConTurb reconstruction method.
These questions focus on the low frequency of the Lidars, and the blade blockage that af-
fects the Lidar measurements abilities. Both Lidar types that were selected (WindVision
and ZX-TM), showed an overall satisfactory performance regarding the reconstruction
results. However, the ZX-TM displayed slightly better results in both the constrained
turbulence boxes and for the DTU 10MW aeroelastic response.
The ﬁnal conclusions from the research that was conducted in this thesis are:
• The size of half cone angle of the Lidars has both positive and negative aspects.
When the half cone angle is small, a lot of information from longitudinal com-
ponents u in Vlos calculations are obtained, which are considered the dominant
vectors in turbulence. Furthermore, the power spectrum presents smaller noise
and thus it is easier to achieve a successful spectral extrapolation. However, a
small half-cone angle results in smaller contribution of the lateral and vertical
components v and w in Vlos, and thus some of their information is lost. Also a
small half-cone angle focuses on a relatively small area of the turbulence box which
indicates that the peripheral points are not evaluated.
• The seed number selection in the constrained turbulence does not aﬀect the mean
values of the errors between original and reconstructed turbulence box in any
signiﬁcant way. However, it does more so for the maximum and standard deviation
values. This indicates that the general picture of the constrained turbulence box is
similar for diﬀerent seeds, and the seed number may be responsible for diﬀerences
in the homogeneity of turbulence.
• The error results from the Vlos reconstruction show that the spectrum extrapo-
lation is not always very successful. In cases that the spectrum presents a noisy
pattern, the extrapolation is shifted away from the targeted position and thus the
reconstructed time-series presents a higher error than expected.
61
==PAGE:71==
Chapter
62
• The error analysis determined from the comparison between turbulence boxes for
baseline and blade blockage cases shows that ZX-TM is better than WindVision
for both cases. The ZX-TM lidar shows a 21.5% and 20.5% improvement when
compared to a reference error for baseline and blade blockage, while for WindVision
it shows a 18% and 16.8% diﬀerence respectively.
• The Least Squares Spectral Analysis (LSSA) method showed very promising results
in spectrum reconstruction of unevenly sampled data (i.e. blade blockage). Even in
extreme cases of blocked measurements (87%), it approached the original spectrum
at a high rate.
• The aeroelastic response of the DTU 10MW wind turbine for the constrained
turbulence boxes with WindVision and ZX-TM Lidars and blade blockage, show
that both Lidars work to a suﬃcient level with respect to loads validation.
