ABSTRACT
This master’s thesis focuses on structural optimization with strength constraints in the form of
strain and von Mises constraints. The optimization problems were derived and implemented in
a developed plane stress FEM code and in the optimization module of the beam cross sectional
software BECAS. The developed plane stress FEM model was validated against analytical
solutions and the implementation of the optimization problem formulations were validated
against a ﬁnite difference check. The optimizations were solved using gradient based methods
with the open source solver IPOPT. Constraint relaxation in the form of ε−relaxation and
constraint aggregation with both the lower and upper bound Kreisselmeier-Steinhauser function
were used.
Optimization performed in the plane stress code on benchmark structures revealed the
upper limit on the number of local constraints IPOPT can handle before crashing to be around
2500. When using constraint aggregation, it was found that only small aggregation parameters
could be used before numerical issues occurred. The interval is much smaller than what is used
in the relevant literature in the cases where the optimization solver MMA is used. The limit on
the aggregation parameter limited the reproducibility of the results from relevant literature, but
the obtained designs show some similarities.
In BECAS, topology and multi material distribution optimization were performed on a
square cross section for simple load cases using local displacement and strain constraints. The
obtained topologies and material distribution were as one would expect based on the structural
response of the cross section, however, in the material distribution some unexpected material
choices were observed in transitions zones between regions of elements with the same material.
BECAS was used to perform topology optimization of a cross section in the DTU 10 MW
reference blade using aggregated displacement and strain constraints. The obtained results
were as expected based on the ﬁndings from the analysis of the square cross section. It is noted
that speciﬁc components in the displacement and strain vector do not pass the ﬁnite difference
check for the aggregated constraints when a shear force is applied. The bug causing this issue
was not found.

1.1
Wind Energy
Since the industrial age, the demand for energy has been increasing and with the economic
development and the increase in population of large countries such as China and India, the
demand is increasing rapidly, [Bevan 2010]. In scientiﬁc communities, it is generally accepted
that the climate is affected by human behaviour and the energy sector is a signiﬁcant contributor
to human made greenhouse gas emissions. A part of the solution is to replace fossil fuels with
renewable sources; wind power being one of them. In Denmark, wind power has been a hot
topic since the oil crisis in the 1970s. In 2015, more than 40% of the energy consumption in
Denmark was powered by wind and by 2020, the Danish Energy Agency expects this ﬁgure to
rise to 50%, [Breum 2016].
As the wind turbines increase in size to harvest more energy from the wind, the structural
and dynamic challenges increase due to the added mass and ﬂexibility of the long blades. It
is therefore relevant to structurally optimize the cross section of the blade to obtain favourable
attributes such as high strength and low weight.
1.2
Thesis Goal and Overview
This master’s thesis focuses on structural optimization using strength constraints. The contri-
bution of this thesis is the addition of strength constraints in the beam cross section analysis
tool BECAS, which is developed at DTU Wind Energy, [Blasques 2011]. Adding strength con-
straints enables control of the strength performance of composite beams in general and wind
turbine blades in particular. The objectives of the thesis are the following:

Overview of the Project
In the ﬁrst part of the project, a plane stress ﬁnite element code with Q4 and Q8 elements
(called the 2D code) was created. The purpose of this was to learn about topology optimization
and derivation of strain and stress constraints along with methods to reduce the computation
requirement and reaching converged solutions. It was used as an intermediate step before im-
plementing displacement and strain constraints in the development branch of the beam cross
sectional analysis toolbox, BECAS. The 2D code focuses on isotropic material while BECAS
can handle multiple materials and optimize their location. As strength constraints are compu-
tationally expensive, the goal is to optimize a single cross section of DTU’s 10MW blade.
This ﬁrst chapter provides an overview of the report layout, thesis goals and a short intro-
duction to structural optimization and why it is interesting to use strength constraints. Chapter
2 presents the implementation and validation of the 2D FEM model along with an introduc-
tion to the theory used in BECAS. Next, in chapter 3, the optimization problem formulation
implemented in the 2D code is presented along with their sensitivity analysis. The issues with
singular optima and high computation expense when using strength constraints are also dis-
cussed. In chapter 4, the optimized designs obtained using the problem formulations in chapter
3 are compared to results from relevant literature. In chapter 5, the displacement and strain
constraints implemented in BECAS are presented and subsequently validated in chapter 6 and
used to perform topology optimization and material distribution optimization on a square cross
section. In chapter 7, topology optimization of two airfoils located 3/4 along the length of the
10MW DTU Reference blade is presented. The thesis ﬁnishes in chapter 8 with ﬁnal remarks
and ideas for future work.

1.3
Introduction to Structural Optimization
In the ﬁeld of mechanics, structural optimization using numerical tools is relatively new. It was
ﬁrst introduced in 1960 by Schmit, [Vanderplaats 1993], and has since then been a very active
research ﬁeld. Structural optimization can be used as a conceptual tool during the design phase
of a product. The goal is to ﬁnd the optimal structure able to sustain user deﬁned loads and
boundary conditions. A structure is understood as an assembly of materials, [Gordon 1978].
2
==PAGE:13==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Figure 1.1: Design domain under load.
In ﬁgure 1.1, a plane design domain is subjected to a load, q, and is constrained by
two supports. Using the available domain, the goal in structural optimization is to answer the
question "what is the optimal structure able to transfer the load to the supports?". To answer
this question, the meaning of optimal must ﬁrst be deﬁned. An optimal structure is understood
from a mechanical point of view, and could therefore be the one with the smallest mass or the
one that yields the smallest displacement in a point in the structure. To ﬁnd a well-deﬁned
solution, the optimal structure must fulﬁl some design criteria such as making the total volume
smaller than or equal to a maximal allowed volume, having the strain or stress somewhere in the
structure below the yield strain or stress of the material or having speciﬁc natural frequencies.
The optimization problem can be written in a general form as
minimize
x∈Rn
f(x)
subject to
gi(x) = 0
i = 1,...,I
hj(x) ≤0
j = 1,...,J
(1.3.1)
The objective function is f, which must be minimized while fulﬁlling the constraints gi and
hj. The equality constraints, gi, could represent the equilibrium equations of the model which
must be fulﬁlled. The inequality constraints, hj, are used to limit the optimizer. This could
be a limit on displacement, stress, volume and so forth. The design is altered by changing the
design variables x. In truss optimization, the design variables are often the cross sectional area
while in continuum optimization, the design variables can be the relative density of an element
which alters the material stiffness.
Most structural optimization methods ﬁt into four categories. The ﬁrst one, material
optimization, deals with the material itself. This can be used with composite structures where
one ﬁnds the optimal ﬁbre angles and orientations. The remaining three optimizations deal
with the material distribution and are called sizing, shape and topology optimization. In shape
optimization, the outer boundary of an existing topology is improved with no new boundaries
formed. Similarly in sizing optimization, an existing topology is altered by changing geometric
parameters such as height and width of a beam or the thickness of a plate. Lastly, in topology
optimization, no predeﬁned topology is used which offers the most design freedom, as the
whole design domain is available for the optimization algorithm, [Verbart 2015].
Motivation to use Strength Constraints
Optimization with strength constraints is the practically attractive topic of, for instance, min-
imizing material use while fulﬁlling strength requirements for the structure. The strength re-
quirement could be a stress such as Von Mises equivalent stress or components of strain or
stress. For composites, it could be the maximum stress or strain. Including strength criteria
directly into the optimization can reduce the post processing of the design, as the optimized

design will fulﬁl, or be close to fulﬁlling, the strength requirement set by the designer.
Much research has been done on structural optimization with strength constraints regard-
ing the two fundamental issues these types of constraints have. The ﬁrst issue is the so called
singular optima, [Duysinx & Bendsøe 1998], where the optimization solver cannot reach the
optimal solution due to constraint violation. One can help the solver by relaxing the constraints
by, for instance, a method called ε−relaxation, [Cheng & Guo 1997], which is presented in
chapter 3.4. Using this method, one alters the optimization problems slightly because the up-
per limit of the constraints are increased, i.e. relaxed. The second issue is that optimization
with strength constraints is computationally expensive due to the many local constraints. For
instance, one could, for an isotropic material, constrain the von Mises stress in each element.
This means that the number of constraints and elements are equal. This can cause memory issue
and large computation time as most FEM model meshes are very reﬁned to ensure a converged
FEM result. Minimizing the number of constraints by approximating the maximum constraint
value with one or a few functions, i.e. constraint aggregation, [Martins & Poon 2005], is a
possible solution. The method is discussed in chapter 3.5.
The use of strength constraints can ease the post-processing but is still a tool to be used
in the conceptual design phase with the beneﬁt of having a better starting point when making
the ﬁnal design, and one has to remember that the optimized design is only optimized for the
given load and boundary condition, and that a change in these during operation could lead to
failure of the structure.

In practise, for a given barrier problem in the outer iteration j, the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT)

Hessian of the Lagrange function, L (x,λ,z) = f(x)+c(x)λ−z where z = µ/x. This second
order derivative with respect to the design variables can be user provided or, as was the case

According to [Kawajir et al. 2010], IPOPT converges with fewer iterations if the user provides
the analytically derived Hessian, but in case the computation time per iteration of the Hessian
is large, using the approximation could be a better choice.

The strategy works well but is sensitive to the choice of ini-

is decreased slowly.
The monotone strategy is the default strategy in IPOPT, but the user can also choose
the alternative adaptive update strategy, where µ is varied for each internal iteration and the
barrier parameter is calculated so that it reﬂects the scale of the problem. This makes the

Local minima
Structural optimization problems are often non-convex and a way to check the convexity of the
problem is to calculate the Hessian matrix of the objective and constraint functions. If both
of these are positive semideﬁnite, i.e. the eigenvalues of the Hessian matrices are all positive,
then the optimization problem is convex, [Christensen & Klarbring 2009]. If not, the problem
is non-convex. For non-convex problems there is no guarantee that IPOPT ﬁnds the global
minima and most likely it converges to a local minima of which there can be several. In this
thesis, it was found that different solutions can be obtained by changing the model parameters
slightly such as the design variable bounds, the initial design x0 and the choice of barrier update
strategy.

2.1
Introduction to FEM
This section provides a brief introduction to the ﬁnite element method (FEM), [Cook 1994].
FEM is a widely used numerical tool to perform analysis of, among others, solid mechanics,
ﬂuid dynamics or heat transfer. The domain of interest is discretized using ﬁnite elements of
which there are several types. The choice of element depends on the problem being solved, the
geometry of the domain and the desired accuracy. The elements are connected via shared nodes,
and by interpolating the nodal values, one can obtain the ﬁeld quantity of interest anywhere in
the domain. The connectivity of the nodes results in a large number of coupled algebraic
equations - one for each degree of freedom of which there can be several thousands in a typical
analysis. The obtained solution is an approximation which can be improved by using smaller
7
==PAGE:18==
CHAPTER 2. STRUCTURAL MODELS
elements in the discretization of the domain. This comes at the cost of increased computational
time.
In this thesis, the ﬁeld quantity of interest is displacement from which the strain and
stresses of the structure can be calculated. The effects of temperature are not considered, and
the initial stress and strain state of the structure is null. Additionally, the material behaviour is
assumed linear and the displacements and rotations small.
In structural analysis, the equation being solved describes the static equilibrium and is
given by the system of equations Ku = q. Here, K is the stiffness matrix, u is the nodal dis-
placements which are the unknown quantity in the equation system. Lastly, q is the external
load which cause the nodal displacements. Because the material behaviour is assumed lin-
ear, and the strains small, the stiffness matrix is independent on the nodal displacement. The
equilibrium equations can be derived in several ways. A common method is the principle of
virtual work. Here, the equations are obtained by equating the gradient of the potential energy
of the system, with respect to the nodal displacements, to null, [Sigmund 2016]. This is done
due to the the principle of stationary potential energy which states that an equilibrium to the
system is found when the potential energy takes a minimum value for any small admissible
displacement variation. The total potential energy of the system is a sum of the strain energy
contained in the elastic body due to distortions and the potential energy of the externally ap-
plied loads, [Cook et al. 2001]. This means that the equilibrium equations for the system are
found by calculating the partial derivative of the potential energy, Π, with respect to the nodal
displacements u.

The Q4 element is a relatively simple continuum element. Its shape functions are linear
with respect to the coordinates x and y. This creates a problem when the element is experiencing
pure bending. In this load case there exists no shear stress, σxy, but because the displacements
along the boundary of the element are interpolated using linear functions, it creates an angle
shift between the nodes. As a result, the element appears too stiff in bending due to the spurious
shear stress. This results in the model underestimating the nodal displacements. A solution to
this problem is to use higher order elements such as the Q6 or Q8 element. Alternatively,
using a ﬁne mesh also reduces the issue as the angle shift in each element reduces when the
discretization is improved.

2.3
BECAS Introduction
This section provides an introduction to the baseline program BECAS (BEam Cross section
Analysis Software). In this thesis, version 3.2 was used. The optimization module of BECAS
is under development, and a task was to expand it with strength constraints in the form of strain,
see section 1.2. BECAS is developed by DTU Wind Energy, [Blasques 2011], and is based on
formulations presented by [Giavotto et al. 1983]. The purpose of BECAS is to calculate the
stiffness properties in a cross section of arbitrary shape consisting of isotropoic or anisotropic

materials. Combining BECAS with a beam model, one can model complex beams such as
wind turbine blades. Here, each cross section is represented by a node in the beam model
and from the beam’s point of view, BECAS provides the 6 × 6 stiffness matrix for each node
which is then assembled in the beam model to a global matrix. A beam model named FRANS
has been developed by [Blasques 2014] and is distributed with BECAS. Figure 2.10 illustrates
BECAS’ interaction with FRANS. Together, they are a computationally more efﬁcient solution

2.3.1
Assumptions
The theory behind BECAS, [Blasques 2011], assumes that the cross section is part of a slender
beam with no abrupt variation in the cross sectional geometry, material, structural properties
or loading. Furthermore, the displacements, strains and stresses are assumed small with a
smooth variation i.e. their gradients are small along the length of the beam. In regards to the
cross sections; there are no restrictions on their the level of anisotropy however the material is
assumed to behave in its linear elastic domain. The cross section geometry can be arbitrary.

3.1
Modiﬁed SIMP Interpolation Scheme
In the following sections, three problem formulations for density based topology optimization
are presented. This method of topology optimization, where the relative density of the element
is the design variable, is in the literature on structural optimization called the solid isotropic ma-
terial with penalization method, [Sigmund 2007]. The design variables operate on the Young’s
modulus of the material and thereby regulate the element stiffness. The Young’s modulus for
an element is given in the so called modiﬁed SIMP approach as

In this approach singularity of the stiffness matrix is avoided when the design variable is 0, as
the void areas are given a small stiffness such as Emin = 10−9 Pa which is independent of the
design. The alternative is the standard SIMP scheme Ee = xp
eE0 where a lower bound on the
designs variables, xe ∈]0,1], ensure a non-singular stiffness matrix. In both cases, the penaliza-
tion factor, p > 1, enables generations of black and white design, as intermediate densities are
penalized and become less favourable. Based on the choice of p, such as p = 3, elements with
low density are less stiff making them a "waste" of material, and elements with high density
have high stiffness, i.e. as xe →1 then Ee →E0. The SIMP approach is an artiﬁcial material
law at intermediate densities, but is considered acceptable because the densities are expected
to converge, due to the penalization factor, to the lower and upper bounds where the material
is correctly modelled, [Duysinx & Bendsøe 1998].

3.4
Addressing Singular Optima
The next two optimization problems have strain and von Mises stress constraints. Using these
constraints, a common issue is the so-called singular optima. This issue occurs in constraint
optimization methods, where the global optima is not reached without violating one or more
constraints. In the literature on structural optimization, it is mostly presented as an issue when
using stress constraints, but nevertheless it is also an issue when using strain constraints. In
[Sved & Ginos 1968], they presented a three bar structure subjected to stress constraints with
the objective to minimize the volume. The design variables were introduced as the cross sec-
tional areas of the bars. It was found that, if one removes one of the bars before the optimization
begins, one obtains an optimized structure with a smaller volume than if the optimization was
performed using all three bars. Both design fulﬁl their stress constraints. Inserting the known
solution, where one cross sectional area was zero, into the original 3 bar optimization problem
resulted in a constraint violation on the bar whose area was set to zero. This suggests that
the optimization does not, by itself, ﬁnd the optimal solution because removing a member by
setting the area to zero violated the stress constraint of that member.
[Duysinx & Bendsøe 1998] discuss that singular optima also occurs in optimization of
continuum structures as local stresses (the constrained stress) tend to ﬁnite values for zero
density elements. This results in an optimal design, where the optimization algorithm is unable
31
==PAGE:42==
CHAPTER 3. OPTIMIZATION PROBLEM DEFINITIONS IN THE 2D CODE
to remove low density regions completely. A remedy for this is ε−relaxation proposed by
[Cheng & Guo 1997]. Here, one relaxes the constraints slightly so instead of having ge ≤0 one
has ge ≤ε where 0 < ε << 1. This enlarges the solution space (feasible domain) and makes
the global optima accessible. According to [París et al. 2009b], the relaxation parameter has
values in the interval of ε ∈[0.001,0.1].
The relaxed problem is an approximation to the original problem which it approaches
as ε →0. Therefore, one could make use of a continuation scheme, where the problem is ﬁrst
solved for a large ε which is gradually reduced where, for each reduction in ε, one use the pre-
viously found optimal design as the next starting point. However, [Stolpe & Svanberg 2001]
showed, using truss examples, that using a continuation approach does not necessarily result
in the global optimum as the trajectory of the global optimum, as ε is decreased, can be dis-
continuous. This means that even using small steps of ε, with the optimal design being the
starting point for the next iteration, does not necessarily lead to the global optima in the unre-
laxed problem. The use of constraint relxation is shown on the von Mises stress constraints in
section 3.7.
Another difﬁculty using strain or stress constraints is the large number of local con-
straints because strain and stress are local quantities. Thus, when using strain or stress con-
straints, the number of constraints is at least equal to the number of design variables. As the
mesh in the FEM model is reﬁned, the memory requirement and computation time increases
signiﬁcantly. A way to minimize the number of constraints dramatically is called constraint
aggregation and is presented in the next section.
3.5
Constraint Aggregation
Constraint aggregation is a tool to reduce the number of constraint functions. Using the method,
one differs between local and global constraint functions, and the essence of the method is
to combine all the local constraint functions into one or a few global constraint functions.
For instance, having three local strain constraints for each element in the structure, i.e. 3Ne
constraint functions in total, can be combined into 3 global constraint functions which reduces
the memory requirement.
Several aggregation functions exist which approximate the maximum of the local con-
straint functions, max(h). In this thesis, the Kreisselmeier-Steinhauser (KS) function is used,
[Kreisselmeier & Steinhauser 1979]. Alternatives to the KS-function are for instance the P-
norm or P-mean functions, [Le et al. 2010, Verbart et al. 2016]. The KS-function was chosen
because the local constraints need not be strictly positive as in the P-norm or P-mean func-
tions. This is a requirement in order to avoid the possibility of generating complex numbers
as the constraint value. Therefore, the P-norm and P-mean functions are often used to aggre-
gate the strictly positive von Mises stress. The KS-function does not have this issue, hence
the KS-function can be used for both strain and stress constraints as well as ε-relaxed con-
straints which was convenient for this project. Several versions of the KS-function exist. In
[Yang & Chen 1996], the upper bound KS-function is given as

Validation of Sensitivities
In this section, the implementation of the analytical sensitivities presented in section 3.3, 3.6
and 3.7 are validated by comparing them to numerical derivation using the central difference
method. Deﬁning a random design, xmin ≤x0 ≤1, the analytical sensitivities for the objec-
tive function and constraint function were calculated. Next, the numerical derivatives of these
functions were calculated using the central difference method. To do this, each design variable
was perturbed one by one by a small factor 0 < β << 1. The numerical derivatives were cal-
culated for each perturbation and stored. Then, the difference between the matrix of numerical
derivatives and the analytical derivatives was calculated. To get an estimate of the size of the
error matrix, the Frobenius norm was used. Then, the perturbation factor β was changed, and
the process repeated. The result is a plot of the Frobenius norm of the error matrix for each
perturbation factor β.

the step size, α, in the backtracking line search procedure when calculating the new values for
the design variables. This solved the issue in some cases but only for small aggregation fac-

in [Martins & Poon 2005] and [Verbart et al. 2016], which both use MMA as the optimization
solver. IPOPT has two barrier update strategies which is brieﬂy discussed in section 1.4. In
order to reduce the aggresiveness of the design variable change by IPOPT the two strategies
were tested. The default update strategy used in the examples is the adaptive which was found
to lead to faster convergence. Both the adaptive and monotone were tested on the cantilever
beam example in section 4.2.4.

stants are small, the lower bound KS-function underestimates the local constraint value. This
means that, even if the maximum von Mises stress violates the local constraint, the KS-function
underestimates the constraint value which, evidently, end up not violating the constraint, and
the optimization converges without constraint violations. The approximation of maximum lo-
cal constraint value improves as µ increases, and therefore the maximum stress decreases for
increasing µ. Additionally, increasing µ decreases the constraint relaxation the lower bound
KS-function provides, [Verbart et al. 2016]. As the constraints become less relaxed, the peak
stress comes closer to the user deﬁned stress limit. An argument would then be to use the upper
bound KS-function, so one is sure to obtain a solution whose peak stress does not surpass the
user deﬁned stress limit. However, using the upper bound KS-function would also require con-
straint relaxation in order to avoid the issue with singular optima, so the same issue of obtaining
too large stresses compared to the user deﬁned limit would occur again. A more viable solution
is to choose the stress limit which take the inaccuracy of the KS-function and the magnitude
of the constraint relaxation into account. One could estimate the error based on the number
of aggregated constraints and the aggregation parameter which is constant throughout the opti-
mization, see ﬁgure 3.2. Otherwise one would do this by examining the maximum stress at the
end of a trial optimization and then adjust the stress limit for the next optimization.
The two barrier update strategies yield similar designs for µ = 1 and µ = 2 but seems
to diverge to two very different solutions for µ = 3.5. There is a tendency for the monotone
strategy to use no material at the lower right corner of the design domain where instead there is
an incline bar attached to a vertical bar where the load is applied. This feature is also observed

feature but instead have a straight horizontal bar lying in the bottom spanning the full length
from left to right of the design domain. For µ = 1 and µ = 2 the adaptive strategy creates the

5.1
The Starting Point
BECAS has been the subject of several dissertations with [Carstensen 2017] being the latest.
In this thesis, the latest clean version of the optimization module for BECAS is used where
clean means that the module does not contain any of the recent work from other students, but
is the latest version made by José Blasques at DTU Wind Energy. The work done in this the-
sis is therefore not a continuation of earlier dissertations but offers new additions to BECAS’
optimization module which has been used in [Blasques & Stolpe 2012] and [Blasques 2013].
Because of this, the optimization module already has several functions implemented such as
compliance, volume and speciﬁc components of the compliance of cross sectional stiffness ma-
trix. These can either be used as the objective function or constraint function. The module uses
version 3.2 of the baseline BECAS to perform the cross sectional analysis which is presented
in chapter 2.3.
BECAS supports anisotropic materials which yields the possibility of doing topology
optimization or material optimization which has already been implemented in the optimization
module. If there is only one material available, and the volume of the structure is used as
the objective or constraint function, it corresponds to doing topology optimization where an

element is allowed to become void. If there are several materials, material optimization can
be done by replacing the overall volume constraint by material constraints for each element
which force 100% material in the elements. The task is then to ﬁnd the best way to combine
the materials in each element in order to minimize the objective function.
In the mentioned articles on BECAS, the Greek letter ρ ∈RNe×Nm is used as the notation

by solving multiple problems individually, where the only change is the mesh and possibly the
topology of the cross section if the material distribution is the design variable.

5.5.2
Constraint Aggregation
The use of local constraints become increasingly problematic when the mesh is reﬁned. The
increase in elements lead to a rapid increase in the number of constraints and design variables.
As an example, a 30 × 30 mesh where all the strain components are constrained and with 5
candidate materials resulted in 11700 constraint equations. Some of these constraints are the
linear material constraints which have a high level of sparsity based on the ﬁlter radius. Due
to this, it seems that IPOPT did not have issues with these constraints. However, from the 2D
code it is known that IPOPT has difﬁculties with the non-linear constraints which result in a
full Jacobian matrix of the constraint functions. Trying to run the 30 × 30 mesh with several
materials and strain constraints made IPOPT experience a fatal error.
If the end goal is to analyse wind turbine blades, which require a highly discretized de-
sign domain due to the relatively complex shape of the cross sections, the number of constraints
had to be reduced. This section presents the derivation of the aggregated constraints where the
sensitivity analysis is performed using the adjoint method. The purpose is to limit the number
of constraints while also speeding up the sensitivity analysis.
Each element has its own displacement vector we which contains the nodal warping
displacement ue, strain parameter ψe and so forth, see equation 2.3.18. Each of these quantities
have several components such as ux, uy et cetera. Additionally, each element has six strain
components. In the implementation, the user should be able to pick speciﬁc components of a
given quantity. This means, for instance, that out of all the displacements, one should be able to
constrain a single component ux or several components ux,uy,uz. Each constrained component
should be represented by its own aggregation function. If the components to be constrained are
ux,uy,uz in all elements, three aggregation functions would be made. Then, because there are
two constraint types; s1 and s2 in equation 5.5.2, there should be two aggregation functions for
each constraint type. In total, six aggregated constraints are made. These functions are made
using loops where and inner loop runs through the components and the outer loop runs through
the two constraint types. The aggregation function is calculated and stored inside these loops.
This aspect is a matter of implementation, and therefore this section will focus on constraining
a single component.

Topology Optimization
In this section, converged results for the topology optimization runs are presented. The problem
formulation is given in equation 5.5.2 and the settings used for the model and IPOPT are shown

elements. Three optimizations were considered; minimization of nodal warping displacement,
minimization of strain and curvature due to rigid body motion and rotation, and minimization
of strain evaluated at the element center. All was done using local constraints. Three load cases
were tested; a bending moment around Mx, a twisting moment Mz and a shear force Fy which
also results in a bending moment Mx. The load was adjusted for each optimization in order to
obtain converged designs. The applied load is written in the ﬁgure captions of the optimized
designs. Additionally, the active components for the converged solutions are also shown in
the ﬁgure captions. Here, active means that the ﬁnal objective value is equal to that speciﬁc
component’s displacement/strain/ curvature, depending on which quantity that is constrained.
Bending Moment
In ﬁgure 6.2, the warping of the cross section is shown when it is subjected to unit a positive
bending moment around x. This load would result in a negative tip displacement of the beam,
where the upper part of the beam would experience tension and the bottom half would expe-
rience compression. This is seen in the mentioned ﬁgure as the upper part is compressed due
to the Poisson’s ratio, i.e. as the upper part of the beam extends due to the tension, the area of
the upper part of the cross section decreases locally. Similarly, in the bottom, the cross section
is squeezed due to the lower part of the beam being in compression which results in a local
increase of the in-plane cross sectional area. As a result, the warping displacement ux is largest

bending case, reducing the in-plane warping displacements reduces the curvature and therefore
also εzz. The strain distribution for the initial structural response, table 6.5, for the two active

attains extreme values at the mid side along the left and right sides of the cross section. Due to
this, it seems reasonable that there is material at the sides of the design domain.

6.3.2
Discrete Material Optimization
Discrete material optimization deals with the task of distributing a list of predeﬁned candidate
materials in the best possible manner. The materials used are the isotropic and orthotropic
materials listed in table 6.1. The amount of material in each element is constrained to be 100%
at all times, and because the design variables are penalized when calculating the element and
material stiffness matrix, see equation 5.4.4, the elements of the optimized structure will for
the most part consist of one material. The orientation of the orthotropic material is treated
as different candidate materials, and the material set available to the optimizer is therefore
the isotropic material and the orthotropic material in predeﬁned directions. Consequently, the
design variables are not the ﬁbre angles but the scaling factors of the stiffness matrix for each
candidate material. An illustration of how the orthotropic material can be orientated in an
element is shown in ﬁgure 6.9, where the same angle deﬁnitions as in [Blasques 2013] are
used. Since the model only considers the cross section of the beam, the ﬁbre angles have been
ﬁxed at αf = 90◦while the ﬁbre plane is available in several directions. This corresponds to
having the ﬁbres orientated at different angles in the cross sectional plane xy.
The optimization settings are shown in table 6.4, and it is noted that, initially, each
element is ﬁlled with 20% of each of the ﬁve candidate materials. The mesh discretization was
decreased from 40×40 to 20×20 in order to signiﬁcantly decrease the size of the optimization
76
==PAGE:87==
CHAPTER 6. NUMERICAL EXPERIMENTS IN BECAS
problem. The same solver settings as in section 6.3.1 is used, see table 6.4. Lastly, the model
does not consider buckling of the ﬁbres. This means that the composite is assumed to be equally
strong in tension and compression. For most composites such as kevlar, carbon or baron-epoxy,
this is not the case, [Jones 1999].
All simulations are performed using local constraints. The material orientation is shown
using lines whose angle with respect to the ﬁrst axis represent the ﬁbre plane orientation.
Isotropic material is represented by a circle. The value of the volume fraction for the given
element and material is represented using the line thickness. In the plots, only material densi-
ties larger than 0.25 are plotted in order to enhance readability of the ﬁgures. Some elements
are found to consist of several materials which indicate the presence of intermediate densities.
This occurred for a penalization factor of p = 3 using the adopted SIMP parametrization pre-
sented in section 5.3.1. Generally, it was observed that regions exist in which the elements
consist of the same material, and the intermediate density elements are located in the transition
zone between these regions. The intermediate densities are interpreted as a sign of the opti-
mizer not having the optimal material available, and thus a combination of several materials
turned out to be better than only one material. It is reasoned that the presence of intermedi-
ate densities would decrease if the number of candidate materials was increased. This was
not tested, but it seems valid if one considers a material catalogue with an inﬁnite number of
candidate materials. In this case, there should always be an optimal material, and due to the
penalization, it is favourable to only use one. It is possible that the designs are more deﬁnitive
if a continuation approach is used on the penalization of the design variables. One could also
consider trying the method used in [Lund & Stegmann 2004]. Here they mention the inability
for the adopted SIMP approach to push the design variables to their bounds and use an alterna-
tive weight function instead of just using the design variables. The obtained results contain no
intermediate densities resulting in manufacturable designs. For future versions of BECAS, one
could consider trying this parametrization to obtain more deﬁned material distributions with
fewer intermediate densities.
In the following, the results obtained for the three difference load cases are discussed.
The obtained designs are presented in ﬁgure 6.10.
Torsional Moment
Two optimizations were run to completion in this load case where the torsional curvature, κz
and the shear strains εxz,εyz were minimized. The shear strains are the angle change in the
elements when observed from the xz and yz plane respectively. The distribution and a visual-
ization of the plane shift are given in ﬁgure 6.4. The discussion of the mechanical behaviour is
identical to that given in section 6.3.1 and will not be repeated here.
It is expected that the ﬁbres should be oriented along the direction of the shear strain sim-
ilarly to how the cross section was ﬁlled with material at the locations of extreme shear strain
in section 6.3.1. One would thus expect to obtain similar optimized designs when minimizing
either κz or εxz and εyz as the two are interconnected and were found to give similar optimized
topologies in section 6.3.1, see ﬁgures 6.7d and 6.7e. The optimized designs are shown in
ﬁgures 6.10a and b. They look very similar and the largest difference is in the corners. In the
min(max(|εxz|,|εyz|)) design, ﬁgure 6.10b, the optimization chose to use a combination of the
isotropic material and the orthotropic material with 45◦ﬁbres. The reasoning behind the use
of the isotropic material is unclear, as the material is weaker in all regards compared to the
orthotropic material. It was investigated whether the isotropic material is chosen based on its
mechanical properties. This was done by decreasing and increasing the stiffness, shear modu-
lus and poisson ratio dramatically. It was found that the material properties did not play a role
77
==PAGE:88==

as long as it was less stiff than the orthotropic material and that the isotropic material was still
chosen to be part of some elements. Then, it was tested whether the initial design caused the
presence of the isotropic material since the initial design had 20% isotropic material in each
element. By setting the volume fraction of the isotropic material to 0%, and the volume frac-
tion of the orthotropic material with horizontal ﬁbres to 40%, it was found that the isotropic
material still showed up in the ﬁnal, fully converged design. It is noted that the optimization
problem does not consider material cost or weight. This implies that the optimizer gains noth-
ing by using the isotropic material. It is plausible that this is the effect of converging to a bad
local minima, and by using a better parametrization as suggested in [Lund & Stegmann 2004],
one would perhaps not obtain a solution where the isotropic material is used.
Bending Moment
As shown in table 6.5, a pure bending causes in-plane warping displacement, curvature around
x and in-plane shear strain and normal strains in all three directions. All these quantities have
been minimized in this load case.
In ﬁgure 6.10c, the curvature κx, which was also minimized in the topology optimiza-
tion, section 6.3.1, has been minimized. The material distribution is symmetric and mostly
consists of horizontal, i.e. 0◦, ﬁbres which ﬁll 50% of the domain. In the corners, the elements
are ﬁlled with incline ﬁbres of ±45◦. The horizontal ﬁbres in the top and bottom resist the
compression and stretching of the cross section, see ﬁgure 6.2, as the ﬁbres are assumed to
have the same mechanical properties in both tension and compression. This corresponds well
with the horizontal bars observed in the topology optimization, see ﬁgure 6.7a. The location of
the incline ﬁbres match the transition from maximum ux to a maximum numerical value of uy.
The material placed in the middle is assumed to serve no special purpose other than fulﬁlling
the material constraints.
Next, in ﬁgure 6.10d, the nodal warping displacement ux and uy are minimized. Hori-
zontal ﬁbres are placed at the top and bottom to reduce the compression and stretching of the
cross section. This indicates that the horizontal bars at the top and bottom decreases the min-
imum value of uy by decreasing the warping displacements in the x−direction. Vertical ﬁbres
are placed along the sides where the warping displacement uy has its maxima. The middle re-
gion of the cross section consists mostly of vertical ﬁbres. The transition zone from the middle
region to the sides of the cross section consists of intermediate densities. Here, the isotropic
material is present in several elements. It is unclear why this material is used instead of any of
the orthotropic candidates which are much stiffer.
In ﬁgure 6.10e, all strain components are minimized. Here, the horizontal ﬁbres at the
top and bottom are present as expected along with vertical ﬁbres at the sides where uy obtains
its maximum values. The corners are ﬁlled with inclined ﬁbres. These were also present in the
design where κx was minimized, see ﬁgure 6.10c. From the deﬁnition of ϵ, equation 2.3.11, it
is found that the curvature κx is included in the εzz component. It is plausible that this link is
what causes the inclined ﬁbres in the corners.
Shear Force
Lastly, the shear force case is analysed on a cross section in the middle of the beam. The cross
section is again decreased from 1×1 m to 0.1 × 0.1 m to follow the assumption of the beam
being slender. In ﬁgure 6.10f, the warping displacements are minimized. The design domain
has horizontal ﬁbres in the top and bottom which is expected due to the bending moment Mx.
Along the sides, there are vertical ﬁbres. This corresponds well to the topology optimization in

Remarks
This concludes the discussion of the material optimization results. Overall, the optimized de-
signs are somewhat as expected. Since the cross section and loads are symmetric, one of these
expectations was that the material distribution should also be symmetric. The feature was
found in all designs. The most complex case of symmetry is for the min(max(|ux|,|uy|)) under
pure bending case. Here, the ﬁrst and third quadrant and the second and fourth quadrants are
pairwise symmetric (the origo of the cross sectional coordinate system is in the middle of the
cross section). All other designs are symmetric about the x and y axis. The second expectation
was that there should be no use of the weak isotropic material. There are some intermediate
density elements with isotropic material and it is unclear why this is a good choice. The most
signiﬁcant appearance of the isotropic material was in the optimization min(max(|ux|,|uy|)) un-
der pure bending where two elements located in a transition zone had 56% isotropic material.
The next step in the analysis of this would be to apply continuation of the optimization where
the penalization is gradually increased and the obtained design from the previous solve is used
as the initial design for the next run with a larger penalization constant.

7.1
DTU 10MW Reference Wind Turbine
The DTU 10 MW RWT is a design basis for the next generation of wind turbines with a
capacity of 10 or more MW. It was designed in a collaboration between DTU Wind Energy
and Vestas Wind Systems. The nacelle supports three blades with a mass of approximately 41
tonnes each. The blades are 89 m and made of glass ﬁbre reinforced composites with balsa
wood as the sandwich material, [Bak et al. 2013].
The complex shape of the airfoils are best discretized using irregular meshes, however,
the version of the optimization module used in this thesis did initially not support this. In the
thesis by [Carstensen 2017], this support was added to the version of the optimization module
used in that thesis. This code was added to the optimization module used in this thesis. How-
ever, the density ﬁlter implemented in the optimization module is build on the assumption that
all elements have the same area. This is false for irregular meshes and especially the irreg-
ular meshes made by Carstensen. While it was possible to generate reasonable results using
the density ﬁlter, the usage of Carstensen’s irregular meshes of the air foils was abandoned
due to this inconsistency. Instead, the meshes were generated from a rectangular mesh, where
the airfoil is cut out of the rectangle. Matlab has a convenient built-in function for this called
inpolygon. This function can ﬁnd elements within and on the boundary of a perimeter. This
way, a regular mesh of rectangular Q4 elements was made which naturally represents the con-
tour of the airfoil very crudely compared to an irregular mesh. However, the level of crudeness
can be decreased by improving the discretization. Elements outside the perimeter are not con-
sidered in the analysis as the element and node numbering is updated once the elements inside
the perimeter have been identiﬁed. The code to perform this action was already included in
the optimization module. The contour of the cross sections in the blade are shown in ﬁgure
7.1. The red cross section marks the cross section used for the optimizations with simple load
cases, section 7.2. The mesh of the cross section shown in ﬁgure 7.2 is used which consists of

8.1
What has been done
This thesis has focused on deriving and implemented optimization formulations for structural
optimization in two codes. First, a plane stress FEM code was created and optimization for-
mulations of minimum compliance, minimum volume were implemented along with strain and
von Mises stress constraints. The sensitivity analysis can be performed using the direct or ad-
joint method. The code served as a primer before moving the beam cross sectional analysis
tool BECAS.
In BECAS, displacement and strain functions were implemented and used in a bounded
problem formulation where one can minimize the absolute value of a quantity such as nodal
warping displacement. The problem formulation can be run using local constraints for small
problem sizes or aggregated constraints for large problem sizes. The functions were also made
to support material distribution optimization.
8.1.1
On Using Strength Constraints - Issues
The optimizations were performed using the non-linear solver IPOPT. During numerical ex-
periments in the plane stress code (2D code), it was found that IPOPT experiences fatal crashes
if the Jacobian matrix of the constraints has a size of ≥2500 rows and columns. The error was
reproducible in BECAS. This puts an upper limit on the number of allowable number of local
constraints and therefore constraint aggregation or the use of an active set strategy is required
for larger problems. This thesis employs constraint aggregation by approximating maximum of
the local constraints with the Kreisselmeier-Steinhausser function. It was found that the range
of usable aggregation parameters was limited as the function would be evaluated to inﬁnity
87
==PAGE:98==

presumably due to large changes in the design variables causing large local displacements,
strains and stresses. This aggressive design variable change was observed for both types of
barrier update strategies. Because of this, the usable range of aggregation parameters was rel-
atively limited compared to the literature on strength constraints, where MMA is often used
as the optimization solver. Overall, it can be concluded that constraint aggregation is neces-
sary in order to perform optimization of wind turbine blade cross sections but the accuracy of
the approximation is low due to the small aggregation parameters and large number of local
constraints.
In the implementation of the aggregated constraints of the bounded problem formulation
in BECAS, there is a bug in the constraint aggregation function. It was found that applying
shear stress to the cross section made the calculation of the gradients of some components
faulty. This was discovered by checking the implementation with the central difference al-
gorithm. The bug was not found and thus limits the use of aggregated constraints to a few
components of the displacement and strain vector.
In the bounded problem formulation used in BECAS, only one auxiliary variable was
used. This limits the optimization to only consider the largest of all components being mini-
mized. It would create more specialized designs if more design variables were introduced so
that each component had its own design variable.
8.1.2
On the Obtained Results
In the 2D code, typical benchmark structures used in structural optimization were optimized
in the form of minimization of the structures volume under von Mises stress constraints. The
obtained structures were comparable to those found in the literature on structural optimiza-
tion. The restriction on the aggregation parameter did result in non-uniform stress distributions
which are not a sign of optimality which was observed for the hinge example. However, this
agrees with the ﬁndings in the literature employing constraint aggregation, where the unifor-
mity is improved for increasing aggregation parameters larger than those used in this thesis.
In BECAS, minimization of nodal warping displacement, curvature and strain was per-
formed on a square cross section in the form of topology and material distribution optimization.
Additionally, topology optimization of an airfoil was performed for different load cases. The
results are deemed to be as intuitively expected while some features of the designs are unclear.
One such feature is the use of the isotropic material in the material distribution optimization.
8.2
Future Work
This section offers some suggestions for future work.
The 2D code
• It would be interesting to change the optimization solver from IPOPT to MMA to see
if this allows the use of larger aggregation factors and thereby enables convergence to
designs similar to those in relevant literature. If this is the case, one might consider also
implementing MMA in BECAS.
• One could also look more in to the the implementation of the method presented in
[Le et al. 2010] with the goal improving the approximation of the KS-function even for
small aggregation constants.

The optimization module in BECAS
• The bug in the aggregation function needs to be solved in order for the implementation
to be used to its full potential. Possible future users of code developed throughout this
thesis are referred to section 6.1 for a list of observations regarding the bug.
• Material optimization of the wind turbine where void is allowed is an interesting analysis
which was not performed in this thesis. This can be done by allowing the lower bound on
the multi material constraints, equation 5.5.3 to be less than or equal to 0. Additionally,
for it to make sense to have void elements, the materials must be constrained. This could
take the form as a mass constraint where the material density formulation given in section

It would also be practical to include a price constraint as some materials are more ex-

constraint using the material density.
• The version of the optimization module in BECAS used in this thesis does not have a
beam model implemented. However, another version of the optimization module exists
where the beam model, FRANS, is already implemented. It has been used in several
master theses with [Carstensen 2017] being the most recent. From a practical standpoint,
it would be interesting to include the displacement and strain constraint functions in this
version with the beam model.
• One could consider moving BECAS to Fortran to increase the speed of the computations.
This would be beneﬁcial if one is using strength constraints on several cross sections
connected in a beam model, as suggested in the above point. The increase in computation
speed would however also include a decrease in plotting options and debugging tools
which Matlab provides.
• One could consider changing the parametrization of the constritutive matrices from the
SIMP approach to the parametrization suggested in [Lund & Stegmann 2006]. The pur-
pose of this is to obtain a more deﬁned optimized material distribution with fewer in-
termediate density elements in the transition zones between regions of elements with
identical materials.
• The subject of a new master thesis could be the implementation of fatigue life as an
optimization option, where one could maximize the life time under a volume constraint
or strength constrains.
General thoughts
• The penalization constant has been deﬁned as 3 for all simulations made in this thesis.
It would be interesting to try different values for this parameter and use a continuation
approach, where the penalization is gradually increased as the optimization runs.
