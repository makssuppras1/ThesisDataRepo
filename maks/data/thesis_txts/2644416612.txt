Abstract
The Danish healthcare system has a growing focus on improving the patient experience by focusing on
coherence in continuity of care. At the same time, the centralised hospital structure is facing future
challenges with staff shortage caused by the growing elderly population that is connected to an increase
of hospitalised patients with multimorbidity. Collaboration between nursing staff across specialisations
is believed to create more flexibility and improve the health care quality in the bed wards. While new
hospital buildings are under construction in Denmark, new work systems must be designed to solve the
future challenges.
The aim of this master’s thesis is to design a nursing work system supporting collaboration across
bed wards at North Zealand Hospital. To answer the research questions, we use a design thinking
approach and including an exploratory case study. Our process follows the Regulative Cycle, where we
in each step of the process apply theories of social capital, relational coordination and communities of
practise. During the process we have involved relevant stakeholder from different organisational levels
through interviews, observation, workshops and surveys. The collected data has been analysed through
contextual work models and the solution has been developed through iterations.
Our results from the case study show that by implementing a work system that focuses on combin-
ing nursing staff members from two specialisations in a team to collaborate on a defined patient group
increases the relational coordination. By facilitating formal and informal meetings in the team the rela-
tionships were strengthened and communication and knowledge sharing between the bed units increased.
The thesis concludes that the work system in bed wards is highly influenced by culture and social
elements as trust, justice and sense of belonging. To design a work system for bed wards there must be
accounted for these cultural and social aspects by involving the nursing staff in the design process. To
do this, a balance between bonding and bridging social capital must be met and relationships between
the bed wards must be built by facilitating interaction across boundaries. We found that developing
the work system in a bed ward while the nursing staff is confronted daily by staff shortage and a heavy
workload was challenging.
The thesis presents a suggested solution including a work system design and a design process for the
bed wards to use in the process of implementing a work system that supports collaboration with their
surrounding bed wards. The solution is adjustable to meet different needs and conditions in the bed
wards at North Zealand Hospital.

Glossary
Bed ward: The part of a medicine specialisation that manage and treat hospitalised patients.
Bed unit: A sub-department within a bed ward.
Multimorbidity: The coexistence of multiple health conditions in an individual.
Nursing specialisation: The medical area of specialisation the nursing staff is grouped within.
Nursing staff: Nurses and assistants who work in the bed wards to nurse and treat patients.
Health care professionals: The group of professionals who work to treat hospitalised patients. This
includes doctors, physiotherapists, nurses, assistants etc.
LIA:Pulmonary Medicine and Infectious Diseases ward at North Zealand Hospital.
0651: Infectious Diseases bed unit in LIA at North Zealand Hospital.
0655 Pulmonary Medicine bed unit in LIA at North Zealand Hospital.
LIAM: Emergency department of LIA at North Zealand Hospital.
SP: The Healthcare Platform in which the nursing staff update the patient chart.
FBA: Collective meeting in the bed ward between nursing staff to share knowledge and discuss pa-
tients in the beginning of their shifts.
Joint patients: The defined patient group that that two nursing specialisations can benefit from
collaborating on.
Bed community: The area in which the joint patients are placed throughout our pilot in LIA at
North Zealand Hospital.
Covid-19: The name of the infectious disease caused by novel coronavirus which by WHO is declared
to be a pandemic.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the thesis project by clarifying the project background and scope followed by
a presentation of the purpose statement and research questions. In continuation of this, the chosen
design approach and thesis process will be presented and explained.
1.1
Project Origin
The project originates from the project teams’ mutual interest within user involvement, optimisation
of work systems and innovation of the health care sector. We find complex organisations interesting to
explore as well as user involvement and human factors. Since one of us works as a student assistant at
North Zealand Hospital a list of projects were proposed from the Hospital whereas one of the projects
was concerning development and improvement of collaboration in bed wards for the future hospital.
This project was motivating to us, since it matched our areas of interests and had a real-world problem
definition. Additionally, the healthcare system is undergoing big changes these years with new visions
for the future as well as dealing with uncertainties due to Covid-19. Going into the project, we were
motivated by academic curiosity, and strong empathy towards the context of the problem.
1.2
Background
Hospitals are big and highly complex organisations. They consist of several different departments and
professional groups with each of their own interests, work system and routines (Bendix et al. 2012).
The combination of the hospitals being very political influenced, involving several different stakeholders
and owned by the state makes it complex and challenging to develop [A1].
1.2.1
Danish hospitals are aiming towards centralisation
For the last 100 years, Danish hospitals have been aiming towards deeper specialisation and central-
isation to ensure better treatment of patients. This has resulted in hospitals being divided into silos
by medical specialisation area. Each silo treats patients within a certain medical specialisation area
and has specialised doctors and nursing staff connected to this area. On this basis, the staff in one
ward is highly specialised and experienced in this one medical area. The silo structure has increased
the professional quality of treatments (Center for Sundhed 2019). However, the silo structure does not
accommodate treatment of patients with more than one illness who needs competences from different
specialisations throughout their hospitalisation (Samuel and Jylling 2017).

1.3
The New Hospital Structure in Denmark
To accommodate the challenges in the health care sector, the government has initiated projects to
modernise and centralise the Danish health care system by building new and improved hospitals. The
budget involved building 16 new constructions around the country (Wang 2018). The new hospitals
must be innovative to ensure a framework that can accommodate the future challenges benefiting pa-
tients, relatives and employees and at the same time increase productivity. The new hospital buildings
create an opportunity to design hospital systems that are prepared for the future challenges and oppor-
tunities in the healthcare system (Center for Sundhed 2019).
The common goal is to build flexible hospitals supporting better and more coherent continuity of care,
increased patient safety, efficiency and a higher professional quality (Danske Regioner, Danmark 2020).
With the new hospitals, the Danish Regions will modernise and develop the existing hospital structure.
The Regions concentrate the treatments in fewer hospitals in order to increase the professional quality
and improve the utilisation of resources (Center for Sundhed 2019).
1.3.1
Challenges at Danish hospitals
Studies show that the amount of elderly people will increase in Denmark in the near future. Elderly
people are likely to suffer from multimorbidity meaning that the total amount of people with more than
one long term chronic disease will grow. Today citizen with multimorbidity accounts for 33 percent of
the growing population in Denmark (F. Larsen 2017). A growing amount of multimorbidity will increase
the pressure on the health care sector since these patients are more complex and more demanding to
treat than patients with one disease within a single specialisation. Additionally, the readmission rate
for patients with multimorbidity is 12 times bigger than for patients with only one disease (Statens
Serum Institut og National Sundhedsdokumenation og - IT 2015).
Multimorbidity demands more coordination and focus on the continuity of care. It is a paradox that
while multimorbidity is growing and requires a more holistic effort, the hospital structure is still going
towards more specialisation and fragmentation. According to Brostrøm (2017), the systems at the hos-
pitals are not developing sufficiently. It is an organisational and professional challenge which the health
care sector must adjust to. Brostrøm suggests trying out new organisational structures and types of
collaborations with a focus on balancing between generalist competencies and specialist competencies
when setting up teams. He emphasises the importance of a strong management and a necessary change
in culture for collaborating on multimorbid patients (Brostrøm 2017).
At the same time, as activity and tasks are growing in the health care sector, hospitals are under
constant work pressure. This pressure shows in tight restrictions to save resources as well as a continu-
ous lack of employees. The activity at the Danish hospitals have increased with 25 percent from 2009
to 2015. During this time interval the number of employees has only increased with 4 percent. This
development will result in future hospitals being under pressure lacking employee resources. Addition-

for the staff (Samuel and Jylling 2017). Brostrøm (2017) suggests that the solution is not to decentralise
the hospital, but to strengthen the collaboration across the professional specialisation areas.
1.3.2
Collaboration in Danish Hospitals
While the hospital structure in Denmark is developing, the internal organisational structure is addition-
ally reconsidered. To accommodate the future challenges, more hospitals in Denmark work on ways to
implement more collaboration in the bed wards. Through initial fieldwork we investigated how three
Danish hospitals work on improving the collaboration across specialisations. At the Neurological ward
at Aarhus University Hospital, they work together in teams across specialisation on a certain patient
group to save resources during night shifts [A2]. At Regional Hospital West Jutland they place the
wards who share competences next to each other to encourage knowledge sharing and collaboration
across specialisations. This is to facilitate interaction across specialisations and enhance the flexibility
[A3]. At North Zealand Hospital we visited five different bed wards. They emphasised the challenges
in collaborating across specialisations due to the wards’ differences and need of a sense of belonging to
their own area of specialisation [A4].
The silo structure in the hospitals results in the nursing staff being grouped homogeneously based
on their medical specialisation (Kaas, T. Larsen, and Andersen 2017). This means that most nursing
staff is very specialised in one area. Studies discuss the balance between specialised knowledge and
general knowledge within the nursing staff group and how there should be more general knowledge to
complement the specialised knowledge since the balance between the two is ideal for cross functional col-
laboration (Kaas, T. Larsen, and Andersen 2017) (Fogh et al. 2015) (Ingerslev et al. 2016). Even though
the nursing staff is very specialised in their knowledge, 70 pct. of their work require general competences
they share across specialisations [A34]. However, the culture in the wards can be very different from
each other and our initial fieldwork showed the importance of including social perspectives, perceived

1.4
Hospital’s work system
The workplace at a Hospital can be approached as a work system with interconnections between several
elements. These work-related elements can be physical settings, organisation, participants, information
flows, work tasks, technology and tools (Alter 2006). The hospital’s work system is a complex system
with a number of different elements (Wilson 2013).
Approaching the hospital as a work system enables a systematic analysis of the different elements,
how they interact and where breakdowns or potential for improvement appears. This analysis helps to
redesign the work system and clarifies how changes in the work system’s design will affect the overall
system (Alter 2006). If changing elements in the isolated work system, it may result in difficult im-
plementation. Therefore, an essential starting point for improving a system design is to evaluate the
existing system. Appropriate evaluation can generate knowledge on which design requirements can be
improved (Hignett et al. 2013).
1.5
North Zealand Hospital
One of the Danish hospitals undergoing changes in the work system, is North Zealand Hospital. North
Zealand Hospital will move to the New North Zealand Hospital in 2024. The new hospital creates
opportunities for a number of changes. By now, North Zealand Hospital has started preparing and de-
signing the work systems for the new hospital by initiating different projects in the departments [A5][A6].
One of the visions for the New North Zealand Hospital is to create a collaborating hospital which
amongst other initiatives includes collaboration across areas of specialisation. The physical settings
will potentially enable cross-functional collaboration and sharing of knowledge between specialisations
within the hospital with an aim to increase the patient experience and improve the work environment
for the staff. North Zealand Hospital has defined a number of organisational principals in order to
facilitate collaboration [A7] (Nyt Hospital Nordsjælland 2019):
1. Maximise sharing features for using resources and facilities optimally. When sharing multiple
functions, it is easier to think as a whole rather than in delimited categories. When people are
brought closer together, new communities, interdisciplinary synergies, knowledge sharing, and a
greater understanding of the whole emerge. In this way, the hospital’s physical settings are utilised
to strengthen the collaboration.
2. Right of use rather than ownership is a principle describing how the hospital needs to work together

Scope
The scope of the thesis is the bed wards as a work system and the nursing staff in the bed wards as
the main user of the work system. The work system is limited to the actions and work practices of
the nursing staff when collaborating to treat the hospitalised patients. Collaboration is defined as ”the

Theories from community of practise, social capital and relational coordination will be applied to create
the theoretical framework of the thesis. Elements from these three theories will complement each other

the participants’ learning relies on how they are informally bound in their work practise. Social capital
describes social elements in organisations which we use to investigate barriers and possibilities for de-
veloping the work system. Relational coordination provides concrete tools to develop the work practise
and measure the impact of the changes.

In the following, the theoretical framework is presented. The different theories is used for drawing
parallels to the collected data. In this way, theories are used for processing the data, resulting in a more

3.1
Social Capital
There are several understandings of the term social capital (Pierre 1986) (Coleman 1988) (Putnam
2000). In the following the focus will be on social capital in a work context. This perspective sug-
gests that organisations with a high level of trust, justice and the ability to work together enable the
members to jointly solve the company’s core tasks. Organisational social capital is important for organ-
isations’ performance, team creativity, knowledge sharing, innovation, employee well-being and health
(Prætorius and P. Hasle 2015) (Olesen et al. 2008). With a high degree of social capital, the core task
is solved with increased quality and productivity. Conversely, social capital is also increased through
collaboration on the core task (Moltke and Graff 2014).
Social capital describes the characteristic that enables employees in an organisation to collaborate
on the organisation’s core task to their own and the user’s satisfaction. To solve the core task, it is
necessary that employees work well together and that this collaboration is based on a high level of
trust and justice. Being able to collaborate, trust each other and feel justice at work are key elements
regarding the well-being of employees (Kristensen, P. S. Hasle, and Pejtersen 2008) (Moltke and Graff
2014). Trust and justice are closely linked to collaboration on the core task, since the individual’s
experience of whether these two factors are present in the relationships is closely linked to whether one
experiences the collaboration as successful and productive (Moltke and Graff 2014).
3.1.1
Ability to Collaborate
The ability to collaborate is connected to collaborating on the core task. When collaboration is under
pressure, it is often due to the fact that those who collaborate have different professional goals or dif-
ferent experiences of what is important in relation to the work (Moltke and Graff 2014). The ability
to collaborate is based on developing norms for mutual responsibilities and creating a framework where
collaboration can exist. This includes time, meeting facilities and communication channels. In order for
employees to reflect on and, in practice, act towards common actions and goals, they need the skills to
coordinate and collaborate. These skills should be developed in a context that supports and develops
the employees’ opportunities and abilities to collaborate. The ability to collaborate develops in rela-

tionships and interactions. The individually competencies must be taken into account in the practical
collaboration between colleagues, management and customers. The employees learn to cooperate and
understand each other’s situation and reaction patterns in the ongoing interactions (Olesen et al. 2008).
3.1.2
Trust
Trust in an organisation applies to the internal trust between the employees, trust between management
and employees as well as internal trust in management (Olesen et al. 2008). Trust is about whether
or not you can rely on each other. In a workplace, this means in particular that the employees trust
what colleagues and managers say, and that the management trusts that the employees do their job as
good as they can. Trust is built in the interaction between people, where they acknowledge and respect
each other’s differences and are open to and interested in the way the other sees and understands their
behaviour (Moltke and Graff 2014). Trust is an important factor in terms of quality of work, job sat-
isfaction and health. Mutual trust is crucial for the ability to work together and for organisations to
develop and implement changes (Olesen et al. 2008). In an organisation, especially two types of trust
is essential: Social trust and individual trust.
Social trust is about trusting people without knowing them. Social trust is a necessary form of to-
getherness that creates the opportunity to interact and create relationships with other people. With
social trust, the complexity is reduced by daring to act and respond to actions of others without know-
ing the consequences. In the workplace, social trust is necessary because it enables collaboration across
groups, even if they do not know each other (Moltke and Graff 2014).
In the workplace, individual trust is built between people in relation to some specific work tasks. Indi-
vidual trust is expressed when a person is met with trust from another person and they create a mutual
relationship of trust and a possible cohesion. To create individual trust, one must give and show trust
through actions. Therefore, trustful relationships develop over time through situations that show trust
as being reciprocated. The more experiences with accommodated trust, the stronger the relationship
(Moltke and Graff 2014).
Goals, outline agreements and rules in organisations are all management tools built to trustfully ensure
that the tasks will be solved as well as helping to coordinate and decide. Management tools are useful
when dealing with collaboration between different actors. The tools seem satisfying, motivating and
clarifying because work tasks become more manageable when there is a guide. However, management
tools can remove focus from the core task or express a lack of trust from the management in allocating
responsibility. Solving the core task requires diverse competences which must be coordinated and allow
flexibility, since the situation can differ and change spontaneously. Thus, precise rules or established
contracts can limit the collaboration and needed flexibility (Moltke and Graff 2014).

3.1.3
Perceived Justice
Justice is not only about the individual experience of justice, but it is also about creating an experi-
ence where the organisation strives to create procedural justice in relation to decisions, changes and
conditions at the workplace. In organisations, there is always a certain distance in power between man-
agement and employees. In addition, differences in status and conflicting interests often appears. Thus,
a system of justice may involve inequalities in form of different positions and functions with different
requirements, but justice is about creating transparency in the system and communicating the reasons
for the deviations. An openness about the basis for decisions improves the experience of justice for the
employees. Decisions about change processes that require managers and employees to act differently,
require in particular openness about the arguments for the decisions in order to increase the experience
of justice (Moltke and Graff 2014).
In order to secure social capital, the management focus should be on the procedural justice. According
to Lind and Tyler (1988), procedural justice is the extent to which employees experience having had a
voice and opportunity to express their opinions, the extent to which the forum is perceived as neutral,
in which the employees have the opportunity to express themselves, and the extent to which employ-
ees feel respected and dignified treated with credibility. To create procedural justice, managers must
plan decision-making processes and involvement processes in relation to key themes and decisions in the
workplace and justify decisions with the underlying considerations. Though it is not only management’s
responsibility to create the experience of procedural justice, employees must contribute as well to ensure
an experience of justified processes for their colleagues. Creating a reflective culture of dialogue is a
prerequisite for succeeding in building social capital through procedural justice. In a reflective culture
of dialogue, principles and rules are continuously held up against their effect and furthermore the in-
volvement or lack of involvement in decisions are justified (Moltke and Graff 2014).
Justice helps to create social capital in the organisation because the employees’ experience of being
treated fairly helps to promote the employee’s desire and willingness to collaborate and that the em-
ployees orient themselves towards common actions and goals in the organisation. Justice can help ensure
that the common benefits, values and benefits are developed and established. Furthermore, research
has shown that lack of justice increases the risk of mental health problems for the employees and has a
negative effect on the motivation, commitment and involvement in the workplace (Olesen et al. 2008).
3.1.4
Bonding and bridging social capital
In addition to the organisational understanding of social capital, the sociological theorist Robert Put-
nam also emphasises the collective element which is relevant when an organisation is in focus. He has
defined the two terms bonding and bridging, describing how groups of people are bonding within the
group and bridging across groups (Moltke and Graff 2014). Putnam (2000) understands social capital
as the features of an organisation, such as trust, norms and networks between individuals, that promote
coordination and collaboration for the common good. He believes that individuals needs social inter-

action and therefore it is important for people to be included in relationships with other people and
by that create a social network which will lead to an accumulation of social capital. This will satisfy
the individual’s need for social interaction and by that improve the well-being within a given community.
Bonding social capital is an expression of whether a group or a department succeeds in collaborat-
ing internally to solve the core task. A high bonding social capital is created when people work closely
together, have an overview of their working conditions, easily coordinate around customers or work
tasks and know what is important to the team-members and their work. A high bonding social capital
increases the group members’ experience of trust and justice. The collaboration will often be charac-
terised by frequent communication and it is knowingly, that a group member can count on help if needed
and is not afraid to ask for it. The communicative patterns, the content of the communication, and the
group’s relationships determine the group’s performance. The bonding social capital can develop into
a challenge if groups become introspective networks that delimit themselves for others. This can create
distance between people and lead to increased mistrust between groups and hence poorer ability to
work together. Often the core task will require working together across departments. Therefore, a team
or department must consider the competencies and capacity of other departments (Moltke and Graff
2014). Knowledge of each other’s experiences, conditions and interests can create strong relationships
and increase the bonding social capital. Bonding social capital it is based on concrete trust between
people who know each other and includes in beneficial networks (Hegedahl and Rosenmeier 2007).
Bridging social capital is an expression of the extent to which groups or departments succeed in collabo-
rating with other groups or departments, and in that way strengthens the solution of the common core
task in the organisation. It is about managers and employees understanding that their department’s
tasks are related to other departments, and that the connection is crucial for the customer. When dif-
ferent departments succeed in working together and they each experience that the others are willing to
help, the experience of bridging social capital increases. In this situation, the departments get to know
what is important to each other to be able to carry out their contribution to their work (Moltke and
Graff 2014). Bridging social capital is based on social trust, which is trust to people you do not know.
The bridging social capital consist in moving outside one’s social, political and professional networks
with people unlike oneself (Hegedahl and Rosenmeier 2007).
Collaboration, coordination and communication are greatly influenced by whether employees are grouped
according to their function or the overall task they contribute to.
Grouping by function typically
strengthens the bonding social capital, where greater trust is built between members of a group with
participants reminiscent of each other. Grouping by task creates bridging social capital, where a greater
understanding of each other and each other’s work tasks is created across professional and departmental
boundaries (Walker and Lorsch 1968).

Relational Coordination
Relational coordination supports social capital within organisations. Gitell et al. (2007) suggest a rela-
tional work system that enhances organisational performance by creating a framework encouraging the
development of social capital between the employees. They clarify that the adoption of a relational work
system predicts high levels of social capital among the employees in the form of relational coordination
(Gittell, Seidner, and Wimbush 2007).
Gittell (2012) describes relational coordination as coordination of work processes that occurs through
frequent and high-quality communication supported by relationships with common goals, common knowl-
edge and mutual respect. Relational coordination indicates that to collaborate it takes relationships that
internally coordinate how to solve the core task. Therefore, relationships are essential to create a collec-
tive identity in an organisation and to enhance effective coordination of work (Gittell 2012). Relational
coordination is documented to be particularly important for the quality and productivity in work pro-
cesses that are characterised by interdependency, a high degree of unpredictability and a certain time
pressure (Hornstrup 2014).
Relational coordination is a positive self-perpetuating concept that occurs when communication and
relationships around the work supports each other (see figure 3.1). By strengthening the relationships
and communication, the relational coordination will be strengthened (Hornstrup 2014).
Figure 3.1: The positive self-perpetuating effect of relational coordination
Common goals ensures that employees see the goals of their own task as part of the common goals
across professional functions and departments. Common goals are important for the problem-solving
communication. Common knowledge is about the employees understanding how their own performance
of the task has an impact on the others’ performance of their part of the task and the overall outcome
of the work process. This common knowledge has an impact on the ability to communicate in a timely
manner to the right people and improvise coordination when something unexpected happens. Common
goals and knowledge make sure that employees immediately coordinate to solve the task in the best
possible way and precisely because they have the same goals and know each other’s tasks. The mutual
respect ensures that the employees consider that other employees respect their work as part of the over-
all goal. This is important to actively communicate and coordinate with each other about performance

across differences since the respect indicates that the employees trust each other’s assessments and that
it supports the common goal (Gittell, J. H. and Seidner, R. and Wimbush, J. 2009).
The positive relationships based on common goals, common knowledge and mutual respect are sup-
ported by the positive communication practices. Frequent communication indicates that the communi-
cation between the employees in the work process is sufficiently frequent and to a sufficient extent. The
frequently communication also support the timely communication which describes that the important
knowledge is communicated in a timely manner. Ensuring that the communication take place when
necessary and when there is a need for it enable the employees to act wisely. Also, the information that
the employees give to each other during the work process must be correct in order to be possible to
coordinate wisely. Precise communication facilitates actions because it can immediately be understood
by the recipient. Lastly, the communication must be problem-solving in order for the employees to
solve problems together when complications and unexpected events occur without blaming each other
(Gittell, J. H. and Seidner, R. and Wimbush, J. 2009).
A result of relational coordination is that communication becomes more uniform. Fewer opportuni-
ties for error arise, which increases the quality level. Also, relational coordination reduces the time
spent on unnecessary communication, unclear information or waiting for responses from colleagues, al-
lowing organisations to utilise their resources more productively and efficiently. Relational coordination
enables employees to more effectively coordinate their work with each other, thus increase the produc-
tion possibilities to achieve higher-quality outcomes while using resources more efficiently. The effect
of relational coordination in hospitals is that it enables hospital workers to achieve better patient care
quality and shorter hospitalisations (Gittell, J. H. and Seidner, R. and Wimbush, J. 2009). Therefore,
relational coordination can improve the performance as it has a positive effect on the patient care qual-
ity. In addition, relational coordination can also improve employee job satisfaction because possessing
the necessary resources to be able to perform one’s job is crucial for one’s job satisfaction. Also, good
relationships in the workplace are a source of well-being (Gittell 2012).
Relational coordination is about developing a culture where routines and workflow support a good
collaboration and where it is rewarded to be able to act appropriate and flexible if it encourages solving
the core task (Moltke and Graff 2014). By focusing on work practices, relational coordination can
support the development of social capital. Relational coordination depends on the adoption of the work
practices, the intensity of its adoption and the degree to which it reach across all relevant employee
functions. Gittell et al. (2009) describes the effective work practices through cross-functional concepts.

Cross-functional meetings: Meetings give participants the opportunity for real time coordination. Face-

immediacy, and ability to build connections among participants through nonverbal cues.
Informal

Communities of Practice
Wenger (2004) presents a way of understanding how people learn in context from which he developed
the concept and theory ’Communities of Practise’. Communities of practice are important to the func-
tioning of any organisation, but especially to those recognising knowledge as a key asset. Through
engagement, people learn when participating in practises where they interact with each other and the
world. This can be within and across practise boundaries. Based on these interactions, people negoti-
ate and align their meaning and sense of belonging. This is done in a practise that is connected to a
==PAGE:43==
24
3 Theoretical Framework
community which is why it is called communities of practise.
Through communities of practise, learning is unavoidable and a phenomenon that happens continu-
ously but on different levels. Wenger connects learning to everyday experiences and one’s participation
in the world. Learning is about actively participating in a community’s social practice and by this
construct identities in relation to the community. A community of practice works as a local system of
competences. In order to make learning possible in practise, one must have an experience of meaning
when interacting with this system of competences (Wenger, E. 2004).
3.3.1
Participating in Communities of Practise
A community of practise consists of participants who develop routines, processes and tools that are
needed to get their work done. People working in organisations learn through their participation in
communities made up of people with whom they interact with on a regular basis (Wenger 1998). Being
a member in a community of practise is a question of mutual engagement in maintaining the social
community and acknowledging each other as participants. Membership is based on participation rather
than an official status, organisational title or hierarchy. The community of practise has to work con-
tinuously on the mutual engagement for keeping the members motivated and willing to invest in their
work and each other (Wenger, E. 2004).
All communities of practise have a common repertoire which includes common routines, words, tools,
stories, actions etc. This repertoire have been produced or taken in by the community during its exis-
tence and has become a part of the practise. The common repertoire makes sure that the community
has a common language of practise and it works as a resource to negotiate meaning (Wenger, E. 2004).
Participating in a community of practise means that one is on familiar territory, can manage com-
petences and is acknowledged as a competence. Some people cannot participate in a certain community
of practice because they do not have the required competences. It is in the interaction between the
outsider and the participant where new experiences and competences are necessary in order to create
new knowledge. When people find themselves in unfamiliar practises, this practise will show as a sym-
bol on one’s lacking competences. The outsiders will not know how to collaborate, will not know the
procedures and will lack common references. The confrontation with unfamiliar contexts will affect the
confidence in one’s work (Wenger, E. 2004).
Homogeneity is not a necessity for the members in a community of practice. Engagement does not
only rely on one’s own competences, but just as much on others’. A community of practice takes ad-
vantage of what one does and knows, but also on what one doesn’t do and doesn’t know. This appears
when people connect in order to learn. When solving a task as a team, it often makes sense to include
different competences that overlap or complement each other. In this case it is more important to know
how to give and achieve help instead of trying to know everything oneself (Wenger, E. 2004).

3.3.2
Boundaries between Practises
Boundaries clarify the distinction between communities of practise and how they differ. Boundaries
arise because the participants develop close relations and engage in each other internally in their commu-
nity of practise in a way that makes it challenging for outsiders to access (Wenger, E. 2004). However,
a community of practise cannot be seen isolated from other communities of practise, as they are con-
nected. Communities of practise structure learning potential through the knowledge they develop, but
they also structure it through interactions with other practises at their boundaries (Jørgensen, Edwards,
and Ipsen 2008). Boundaries are the most likely place to produce and share new knowledge and are
important to pay attention to. The boundaries are where the unexpected can happen and innovation
exists. Activity at the boundaries support learning, since new practises often arise at the boundaries
(Wenger, E. 2004).
To cross the boundaries between communities of practise it takes mediators and boundary objects.
Leaders often mediate between more communities of practise and they apply elements from one practise
to another, which demands coordination and adaption of perspectives. Participants also use boundary
objects to meet across communities of practise and to coordinate perspectives from each of their work.
Boundary objects can be physical objects as well as invisible concepts as language and systems that
connects communities of practise (Wenger, E. 2004).
Wenger describes different ways for the participants to meet across boundaries depending on the wanted
repercussion and outcome (see figure 3.3). One way is for participants to meet face-to-face. This en-
hances the possibility to be honest and more private in the connection, though it will only include
subjective perspectives from the participants’ communities of practise. Another way is to visit another
community of practise. This gives a wide access into an unfamiliar community and will give more
true and real insights into the practice across the boundary. Leaving one’s own practice behind while
visiting, makes one more receptive to new learning. However, the result is best if both communities
of practise visit each other. Lastly, another way to meet is by having delegates from each community
of practise meeting up to negotiate the meaning of their practices across boundaries. Each community
of practise will get the opportunity to understand and learn about other practices. This connection
ensures an equal two-way communication (Wenger, E. 2004).

start to arise (see figure 3.4). This practise works to take care of the boundaries and maintain a con-
nection between other practises by dealing with conflicts, harmonise perspectives and develop solutions.
Boundary practises are often seen in interdisciplinary organisations. However, a boundary practise risks
becoming a self-standing community of practise which will prevent it from creating relations between

3.3.3
Negotiation of Meaning
Wenger describes how people’s engagement in the world is a constant negotiation of meaning. Since
the world is constantly moving and conditions changing, every practise has to continuously reinvent
itself. Changes in strategy and in the internal structure and procedures are often related to introducing
new participants, which means new perspectives, meanings and competences. Though a practise is
familiar, the meaning of it is still renegotiated by its participants over and over again. This demands
adjustments from the participants where the combination of impressionability and resilience is a mean.
Learning includes a close connection between order and chaos. When people interact they recognise
parts of themselves in each other which they respond to and which is due to a mutual ability to negotiate
meaning (Wenger, E. 2004).
Identity is closely connected to practise.
Building up an identity includes negotiation of meaning
concerning the experiences of membership in a community. Identity in practise explains how learning
changes who people are and how they experience their role in a practise. Identity is a negotiation of
ways of being a person in the relevant context. Reversely, the formation of communities of practise
is also a negotiation of identities. Identities are defined through the practises one engage in, but also
through practises one do not engage in. Identities are defined by who you are, but also who you are
not (Wenger, E. 2004).
Negotiability is based on participants owning their own meaning as well as their ability to take re-
sponsibility for negotiating this meaning. Engaging in negotiation includes a production of meaning
==PAGE:46==
3.3 Communities of Practice
27
proposals as well as acceptance of these proposals. Learning depends on participants’ ability to con-
tribute to the collective production of meaning, since it is through this process that experiences and
competences affect each other. When participants are not able to engage in negotiation of meaning, they
can choose to trust that meaning still exists. This meaning does not belong to them, but presumably
to someone else. Participants understand that their ability to negotiate can be limited and they accept
that they do not necessarily understand everything they do in practise, but they still imagine that the
meaning exists to stay motivated in their work (Wenger, E. 2004).
3.3.4
Sense of belonging
Another result of being part of a community of practise is the creation of a sense of belonging. Any way
of belonging involves compromises, and these compromises is what makes people capable of compensat-
ing for each other’s lack of competences. The combination of engagement, imagination and adaption
results in a reflecting practise which can engage but also distance itself from others (Wenger, E. 2004).
If people engage in a community of practise they often feel a sense of belonging to it.
Although,
there is a limit to how many different contexts a person can belong to and engage in. This is due to
limits concerning time, geography and energy. This constraint is the strength as well as the weakness
concerning engagement as a way of belonging. Through imagination a practice can identify with an
unknown organisation and at the same understand how the organisation looks from an external point
of view.
When exploring discontinuities, the community of practise will be forced to rethink their
individual positions, ask new questions and develop new competences. It is through imagination that
people envisage new opportunities of development and investigate alternative strategies. Imagination
ensures adjustments to the changing needs, especially when instructions are unclear. Lastly, people
find a way to belong through adapting). When people adapt to social norms and rules, they do not
need engagement or imagination. They do what it takes to do the work in order to take part in an
organisation. Adaption increase the efficiency of work. It is about controlling, managing and power.
Adapting in a community of practise results in identifying with having no power (Wenger, E. 2004).
3.3.5
Design for Communities of Practise
Though communities of practise are self-organised and not described as being something that can be
designed intentionally, it is possible to design social infrastructure that can enhance learning. Learning
can be defined as a reorganisation of experience and competence. This reorganisation can be designed
in order to affect learning. A community of practise cannot be a result of a design but a respond to
design. When designing a process, a course or a system it is highly relevant to include the affected
communities of practise (Wenger, E. 2004). However, a community of practise does not necessarily
need institutional infrastructure, but more time and space for the members to collaborate. Therefore,
the best way to design for a community of practise is to support it with resources and connections, but
be careful not to overwhelm it with institutional management (Jørgensen, Edwards, and Ipsen 2008).
==PAGE:47==
28
3 Theoretical Framework
Design for learning is aiming to combine different competences, in order to make people educate each
other. A design is not only a specification but also a boundary object, working as a communication
object, from which communities of practise can negotiate their contribution, their position and their
adaption. According to the theory, design must support three types of infrastructure. Firstly, designing
for engagement is a matter of activity, developing of community, inventiveness, social energy and emer-
gent competence. This infrastructure must facilitate mutuality, competences and continuity. Secondly,
designing for imagination includes dealing with a wider context. The infrastructure of imagination
must facilitate orientation, reflection and investigation. Lastly, designing for adaption can affect and
contribute to doing work that does not include engagement. This infrastructure must facilitate conver-
gence, coordination and jurisdiction (Wenger, E. 2004).
Wenger defines organisations as social designs directed towards practise.
Communities of practise
are a key to an organisation’s competences and to the development of competences. The organisation
consists of two parts: the institutional design and the practise that give life to the organisation. The
institutionalisation is a necessity but also a root to challenges. Many communities of practise are cre-
ated as a result of realisation of an institutional design. Without institutionalisation, the organisation
cannot be held together. With too much institutionalisation, the organisation risk coming to a halt if
the practise start working for the institutional design instead of the other way around (Wenger, E. 2004).
Design of an organisation connects communities of practise in an organisation by crossing boundaries.
Thus, it is as important for the design to create channels of communication, that is to create insti-
tutional abstractions. The main principal in designing for communities of practise is to connect and
combine different competences that exists in a constellation of practises. Organisational design should
not aim for defining one competence, that comprises all others, but it should aim to coordinate several
different competences in an organisational learning process. To have the same vision is by that about
being able to see each other and envisage common goals (Wenger, E. 2004).

formal and informal during a conversation. The informal conversations includes discussing their private
lives, discussing personal opinions on patients or sharing stories. The informal communication shows
how the nursing staff commit to each other.

During a shift the nursing staff continuously communicate with their nurse manager. This is mainly
face-to-face meetings where they enter the nurse manager’s office or it can be in the staff area if the nurse
manager takes part in formal meetings. The conversations between the nursing staff and the nurse man-
ager include coordinating the staff schedule, expressing concerns about their work or discussing specific
situations that has occurred. If the communication between the nursing staff and the nurse manager
does not happen face-to-face they call each other using the work phones. The nurse managers addition-
ally hang up general information on paper in the staff area and send out written updates in the weekly
newsletter by e-mail. The communication between nurse manager and nursing staff depends on mutual
respect when they try to meet each other in solving a situation. The nursing staff trusts that the nurse
managers work for their best interest which motivates them to share their concerns and ideas with them.
During every shift the doctors show up for the ward round which is a face-to-face meeting between
the doctors, the nursing staff and the patient. This is to share knowledge on the patient and discuss
the status and treatment given. If the nursing staff needs a doctor during a shift outside ward round,
they use their work phone. Sometimes the doctors and the nursing staff can disagree on decisions made
concerning patients which can lead to a decrease of their mutual respect.
The nursing staff has ongoing contact with the patients. This is always face-to-face meetings in the
bedrooms. The face-to-face meetings include going through routine checks, talk about how they feel
and accommodate their needs. The patients can page the nursing staff by pushing a button from their
bed and then they trust a nursing staff member to come see them.

4.2.5
Cultural Model
The cultural model reveals what formal and informal patterns that influence the work in the bed ward of

towards collaboration across the units (see figure 4.8).
The atmosphere in the bed wards changes depending on whether the nursing staff is next to the patients
or in the staff room. The atmosphere around the patients is professional, yet kind and casual. Whereas
in the staff room, it is more relaxed, personal and humorous. The nursing staff kindly teases each other,
tells stories about patients and their private lives. In emotional situations, the nursing staff comforts
each other. If the nursing staff is under pressure the tone can get strict and even harsh. The nursing
staff fear the pressure of being inadequate if they are responsible for more patients than they can cope
with. This is expressed in the injustice they feel when management demands too much from them and
they feel insufficient even though they do their best.
The nursing staff members are all dressed in uniforms during their shift. Occasionally they add per-
sonality and fun into their work by e.g. wearing an elf hat and decorating the hallways in the occasion
of December. They buy bread or cake to share and they arrange presents and surprises for each other.
This is examples of how they engage in the community of practise they belong to and make an effort
to sustain the community and contribute to a positive atmosphere.
The nursing staff follows several policies to do their work.
They use professional policies to follow
procedures of patient treatment and practical policies to organise and time responsibility and tasks.
Their focus is on the patient care quality and experience, where it is important for the nursing staff
to feel confident and competent in their work. They prefer to ’learn by doing’ from experienced staff

Teams
A team consists of two nursing staff, one from 0651 and one from 0655. The team must include at
least one nurse, but can be combined of one nurse and one assistant or two nurses. In the team, one
nurse must be experienced in the LIA bed ward ensuring that a new nurse always collaborates with an
experienced. This is to ensure the professional competence.
Shifts
Only day shifts and evening shifts are handled by the team. Bed ward 0655 is responsible for the night
shift. Preferably, two shifts are placed in extension of each other ensuring that the same team has two
days in a row and follows the same patients. A team never has more than two days in a row in the bed
community.
Responsibility
The responsibility in the team is divided into a ’1st’ and a ’2nd’. The ’1st’ has the main responsibility
for the joint patients and is not responsible for other patients. It is the ’1st”s responsibility to coordi-

’2nd’ can also be responsible for patients in one’s own bed ward. These patients are light, stable, and
self-reliant patients. When the ’2nd’ is from 0651, the ’2nd’s other patients are as close as possible to
the bed community. If more help is needed, a ’3rd’ is added to the team. The ’3rd’ is inserted in case
there is a need for the ’2nd’ to be responsible for only joint patients (see figure 4.13).

4.8
Case Conclusion and Recommendations
The bed community has been a way to establish a boundary practise between the two bed units. It has
facilitated meetings between the two bed units which has increased their understanding of each other’s
communities of practices. Before the implementation of the bed community the bed units believed
that they were very different from each other professionally and culturally. Their collaboration through
the pilot showed a number of overlapping competences between the nursing specialisations as well as
a patients focused common goal. They recognised the potential in sharing knowledge and learn from
each other’s competences when working on the joint patients.
The collaboration agreement worked as a boundary object that described the work system model and af-
fected the nursing staff’s regular work routines which was something they had to adapt to. The nursing
staff’s reaction to this varied from person to person. The nursing staff who works in LIAM engaged in
the bed community with a positive attitude whereas the ones who do not take shifts in LIAM opposed
to work on the joint patients. This showed that relationships and familiarity is important for successful
collaboration.
Since the bed community was physically placed in 0655 and the joint patient group was Covid-19
patients the feeling of being isolated and on unfamiliar ground increased throughout the pilot. This
resulted in the nursing staff from 0651 lacking a sense of belonging when working in the bed community
which decreased their motivation to engage in the community. Due to a lack of transparency in the
allocation of resources, the nursing staff from both units felt that they sacrificed more than the other
unit and they did not experience justice. The collaboration agreement limited the flexibility that was
needed in order to adjust to the changing needs in the bed ward. The lack of flexibility resulted in a
lower prioritisation of the patients outside the bed community, while the patients in the bed community
were satisfied.
The pilot showed that time and resources must be prioritised when developing a work system. To
prepare for this a process must be planned and underwent before implementing the work system. The
nursing staff managed to adapt to the new work system, but they never engaged fully in the work
around the bed community which prevented the development of a new community of practise.
By
agreeing on roles and coordinate the tasks, both bed units recognised and experienced a meaning in
crossing their borders and help each other to achieve the common goal. Working as a team in the bed
community developed the relationship between the nursing staff and created a repertoire including a
common language, knowledge and understanding of each other.
The relational coordination increased with 0.35 points. By continuously adjusting the work system
model and by clarifying roles and expectations the communication in the team became more uniform
which resulted in fewer frustrations and more trust in the team. Focusing on building relationship and
strengthen the communication in the team affected the overall connection between the two communities
of practice and created more activity at their borders.

Recommendations
• Facilitate face-to-face meetings between the nursing staff across the nursing specialisations
in order for them to build relationships.
• Make sure nursing staff only has main responsibility of patients within their own nursing

• Place the joint patients in bedrooms close to the collaborating neighbour unit to ensure

• Make sure all nursing staff members know their responsibility when working on the joint

Discussion
The thesis has resulted in a number of findings when designing and implementing a work system sup-
porting collaboration. In the following, these findings will be discussed by including initial fieldwork,
perspectives from selected literature and theory of relational coordination, social capital and communi-
ties of practise.
6.1
Differences in bed wards
Wenger (2004) explains in his theory how people who fully participate in the core of a community of
practise are in familiar territory where they can navigate competently and feel acknowledged. This is
where they feel safe and comfortable, when they identify with their context. Our case study showed
that when nursing staff believe they are very different from each other socially and professionally, they
tend to define their own identities by what they are not instead of what they are. They are not like the
others [A30]. They constantly negotiate ways of participating in their practise by negotiating their iden-
tities. The nursing staff internally agree that they are different from ’the others’ and this strengthens
the group’s internal bond. This social distance between them enhances their self-interest and disturb
their focus around the common goal. The nursing staff needed social trust to ’the others’ in order to
be able to collaborate with someone outside their own community of practise (Moltke and Graff 2014).
The experience of justice is an essential factor in this dynamic. If the nursing staff feels justly treated it
enhances their willingness to collaborate on the common tasks and goals (Olesen et al. 2008). The fact
that the nursing staff feel different from each other is a factor in not being able to imagine how and why
they should collaborate. They need imagination to welcome different perspectives across boundaries.
This imagination can only be realised if they have the right engagement to act on it (Wenger, E. 2004).
It is valuable for communities of practise to enhance collaboration across boundaries to connect dif-
ferent competences and knowledge. Developing communities of practise is not based on homogeneity, it
is based on mutual engagement in which differences are as important as similarities. The difference in
competences can work as a valuable resource (Wenger, E. 2004). The nursing staff’s positive attitude
towards development of competences across specialisation contradicts with the resistance they show
towards expanding their area of specialisation. If they are supposed to collaborate on shared patients,
they see a risk in compromising the quality of their specialised work by becoming more general in
their competences [A2][A4]. Lindberg (2020) comments on the theme and shows concern regarding the
nursing staff becoming more attenuated in their professionalism. A large part of the nursing staff’s
identity lies in their specialisation. In the hospitals there is a need for specialised competences among
the nursing staff to treat the patients, but for a number of specialisation areas, it makes sense for the
==PAGE:81==
62
6 Discussion
nursing staff to go across and help each other. A nurse manager from North Zealand Hospital further
states that it is important to keep the professional specialisations and avoid neutralising them. The
nursing staff need their specialised knowledge, but they also have a lot of general skills they share
across specialisations [A4]. Ingerslev et al. (2016) acknowledge that today’s patients are more com-
plex requiring knowledge from different specialisations. Therefore, nursing teams need to have both
general and specialised knowledge. But working with a balance between the two types of knowledge
only makes sense if the nursing staff is willing to share knowledge and learn from each other. If they
can do this, it will create more flexibility and make them less vulnerable when treating complex patients.
Though differences can be valuable, it became clear throughout the pilot (see section 4.6: Iterations)
that the nursing staff has a number of overlapping competences and that they share the same goal in
their work which is the patients. The more the nursing staff collaborated, the more similarities in their
work they discovered. This indicates how the work system facilitated relational coordination where the
nursing staff gradually developed relationships and created a common knowledge and mutual respect
about each other’s work (Gittell, Seidner, and Wimbush 2007).
Conversely, Kaas et al.
(2017) explains how feeling misunderstood or met with mistrust results in
people keeping their experiences to themselves and avoid asking for help. This can be due to a work
culture, where everyone only takes care of their own responsibilities. Frøjk et al. (2015) furthermore

order to collaborate with other wards. Head nurse at LIA supports this and believes that there is a
central task in looking into the level of social capital in the ward before initiating any projects with

have any barriers concerning their social environment [A34]. By adapting the relational coordination
work practises of cross-functional meetings and cross-functional boundary spanners in the bed ward,
the culture can develop and it can enable the nursing staff to build relationships based on trust and
commitment (Gittell, J. H. and Seidner, R. and Wimbush, J. 2009).
6.2
Developing and operating a bed ward
Our pilot showed how the wards are challenged in having to engage in developing projects while they
need to maintain the daily running of the ward. The nursing staff cannot control the patient flow
and they must deal with incoming patients regardless of time and situation. The daily tasks must be
accomplished while implementing new work systems in the wards and this showed to be challenging
in practise. The fact that the joint patients where Covid-19 patients, we assume to have affected the
nursing staff’s attitude towards working in the bed community. The head nurse of LIA explained that
due to Covid-19 the nursing staff had for a longer period been under additional pressure and therefore
they were less tolerant towards changes [A34].
Gleerup (2008) discusses how the daily management of an organisation requires a lot of administra-

tion, which can compromise the development. He states the importance of creating space and time to
calibrate during changes, in order for artefacts to turn into symbols and settle in the basic assumptions.
Otherwise, it will have negative consequences for the operation and quality of the core output. Wenger
(2004) agrees to this explaining how the short term demands in the daily work are conflicting with the
long term demands on developing competences. This challenge can be examined in literature, where
Wadel (2008) distinguish between developing management and administrating management. Since hos-

Thus, we found a challenge in developing and implementing a nursing work system
while the bed ward constantly had to be operated because the patients’ needs could not be ignored

Therefore, implementing innovation must be planned at a time when the necessary resources are avail-

planning, testing, implementation and monitoring. The theory of relational coordination suggests to
establish cross-functional boundary spanners in order to develop the work system (Gittell, J. H. and

being more motivated to change and to adjust their work procedures to the different situations [A3].
When involving the employees in the development processes and decisions their experience of justice
is increased (Moltke and Graff 2014). The nursing staff is generally open to learn and develop their
competences, but it is important for them to be professionally prepared. They need time to be educated
in what they need to do practically. This is preferably through ’learning by doing’ where they follow
the procedure from experienced nursing staff until they learn it themselves (see subsection 4.7.2:Focus
Group Interview)
6.3
Coordination before collaboration
The aim of implementing our work system model was to support collaboration between the bed wards in
LIA. The definition of collaboration is ”the situation of two or more people working together to create or
achieve the same thing” (Cambridge Dictionary 2020). To further elaborate on the term collaboration,
Keast and Mandell (2012) explains how collaboration can be ranged from cooperation to collaboration
with coordination in between. Though these terms are often used interchangeably they are increasingly
understood to be analytically distinct. The difference between coordination and collaboration is inter-
esting to discuss concerning the implementation of our work system design.
==PAGE:83==
64
6 Discussion
Ovretveit (1993) and Keast (2012) define how coordination brings together interdependent parts in
an ordered relationship to solve the common task. Organisations can remain independent departments
but jointly contribute to an agreed shared outcome. Collaboration on the other hand is described by
Mandell (2001) to be more stable and long-term by having high levels of interdependence. Innes and
Booher (1999) follows this saying that in collaboration the involved people’s success relies on the other
part’s success which means that it is followed by big risks. Collaboration is often focused on processes
and future strategies rather than single tasks that have to be done.
Based on the above-mentioned descriptions of collaboration and coordination, it can be discussed which
of the two that is most relevant to use for describing the nursing staff’s work on the join patients.
Though the pilot aimed to make the nursing staff collaborate across bed wards, what they actually did
was to coordinate. The nursing staff did not feel equally invested in developing their relationships and
future strategies and the work on the joint patients turned out to be more about solving the single tasks.
They stayed independent but contributed with their respective competences to the work on the joint
patients. We learned that at this stage coordination was preferred within the provided period of time
to implement the work system while ensuring the nursing staff’s sense of belonging. The coordination
can develop into collaboration with time if collaboration is the final goal.
6.4
Bonding and bridging in a boundary practise
Wenger (2004) describes how boundary meetings can lead to mutual engagement and develop a bound-
ary practise. The pilot has been a way to establish a boundary practise between the two bed units. It
has connected the units with an aim to intentionally create a new community of practice including both
units. Since the bed community was placed physically in 0655 and mainly the nursing staff from 0655
initially knew how to treat the joint patient group, the bed community facilitated boundary meetings
where members from 0651 visited the community of practise in 0655. Seeing the work on the joint
patients as a boundary practise showed to be valuable since a boundary practise work with connecting
two practises by coordinating work, harmonising perspectives and solving common challenges by con-
tributing with each of their different competences (Wenger, E. 2004).
A boundary practise ensures both bridging and bonding social capital which is valuable according
to theory of social capital. Although, we learned through the pilot, that it has consequences to com-
promise the bonding social capital in order to build bridging social capital. The nursing staff was not
willing to risk their sense of belonging to build relationships to the other unit. If they do not feel a
sense of belonging and thrive in their own ward, they will not be willing to make an effort to help
the other wards [A26]. This emphasises the importance of bonding social capital in order to achieve
bridging social capital. The sense of belonging increases the bonding social capital and the nursing
staff’s experience of individual trust and confidence (Moltke and Graff 2014).
However, Putnam (2000) describes how too much bonding can result in isolation and challenge the
==PAGE:84==

bridging social capital. The bed community developed internal bonding which weakened the bridging
social capital between the units and isolated the bed community from the two units it was supposed to
connect, which is furthermore a risk of boundary practises (Wenger, E. 2004). A way to accommodate
this is presented by a quality consultant from Regional Hospital West Jutland who explains the impor-
tance of standardising and sharing the physical settings in order to increase the bridging social capital

The pilot never reached a state where a new community of practise developed. It can be expected
that by facilitating boundary meetings between the units, by time new communities of practise can ap-
pear involving members from both units. However, we believe that implementing a boundary practise
will improve the bridging social capital at the same time as holding on to the bonding social capital
which by itself will strengthen collaboration.
6.5
Organisational management and employee involvement
Our case study has emphasised the importance of involving the nursing staff in the development of
the work system. The communities of practise are built on mutual engagement between the partici-
pants which is connected to active involvement in the development of their practise (Wenger, E. 2004).
Danielsen and Holscher (2019) concludes that a pattern emerges indicating that employee well-being
increases when the employees themselves are involved in designing their work system and planning the
implementation in their work. Kaas et al. (2017) furthermore concludes that involving employees in the
development process strengthens a common innovation competence amongst the employees and gives
them faith.
Going into the pilot we believed, based on findings from our initial fieldwork, that involving nursing
staff by giving them space to negotiate their conditions would make them more engaged and motivated
in the project. However, the nursing staff demanded concrete guidelines from the management as well
[A26]. This emphasised the importance of the management’s role. The results of the case study show
that even though the nursing staff wants to be included in the process, they also find it necessary to
have concrete guidelines that explain a clear purpose. It is important for them to know exactly why
they need to follow the instructions and what is expected from them. Most of the nurses accept the
management changing the work system but why, how and what has to be clarified (see section 4.8:
Conclusion and Recommendations). Guidelines are a management tool that motivates the employees
and clarifies the meaning with their work. In this way, the guidelines help the employees to coordinate
and make decisions when collaborating (Moltke and Graff 2014).
Albæk et al. (2009) clarifies that implementation of organisational changes have the greatest chance
for success if the changes are connected to the employees’ perceptions of professional relevance and
communicated on the basis of this focus. The balance between management and employee involvement
is necessary, and both perspectives need to be taken into account when going through changes. More
==PAGE:85==
66

projects agree on this. Edwards and Tewers (2015) describe how management created the framework
for changes while the employees created the solutions which ensured a high level of engagement and an
increasing chance for successful implementation. Kaas et al. (2017) furthermore concludes that accom-
modating and managing change is not only relying on the individual employees, but it is a question of
whether or not the managers create a space for dialogue.
Our case study showed that even though we created space for dialogue between the bed wards to
involve the nursing staff in the negotiation of the work system, they did not engage as expected in
the evaluation and negotiation of the work system. The nursing staff has not been used to have much
influence during changes and therefore it felt new to them to be in a position where they were encour-
aged to negotiate and influence the work system like they were asked to do in our pilot. Wenger (2004)
explains that when the space for negotiation changes, it will change what the participants recognise as
lying within their area of competence. If this is suddenly up for negotiation, it will change the way they
see themselves in the community of practise, because it is something they can influence. It is through a
collective negotiation of meaning that learning exists and based on this learning, the work system can
be improved. This can explain the nursing staff’s lack of engagement in negotiating the bed community.
By being acknowledged as influential participants in the community of practise, the nursing staff was
expected to engage and negotiate instead of just adapting, but we did not see this.
6.6
Design for collaboration
The pilot was an experiment in which we wanted to facilitate collaboration by designing a nursing work
system. It is interesting to discuss the limitations and possibilities of designing sustainable relationships
and collaboration in the bed wards.
For creating a common understanding of each other and build relationships between employees Fogh
et al. (2015) recommends establishing spaces for dialogue. This helps to emphasise the quality in each
other’s specialisations and to identify common gains and challenges together. Wenger (2004) suggests
doing this by sending delegates from each department, who will meet and negotiate meaning in their
own community as well as their common community. This is what we saw the head nurses do, when
they met to discuss their daily work and future plans for the wards. They see themselves as mem-
bers of their own community, but also of a common community where they mediate between the two
units [A26]. This is an example of planned formal meetings which are necessary for communication and
knowledge sharing that encourage collaboration (Gittell, J. H. and Seidner, R. and Wimbush, J. 2009).
To accommodate and implement more collaboration in the bed wards, the nursing staff describe the
importance of the physical settings and the proximity to each other. The physical settings have to
be designed for enhancing collaboration making sure that the collaborating parts are placed nearby
each other [A26]. According to Allen (1977), co-localisation and physical proximity are important as
it has an impact on the ability to innovate, collaborate and coordinate. Proximity enables informal

cross-functional meetings which are effective for building relationships. Prætorius and Hasle (2015)
also clarifies the importance of establishing settings for increasing social interactions, a common under-
standing and information. These meetings are central, as they refer to forums where the involved can
meet and contribute to solve the common task together. And by that the co-ownership and engagement
among the nursing staff will increase. The nursing staff at Aarhus University Hospital agrees to this
and explains how a common office across area of specialisation increases the feeling of community. In
this way, the employees from different wards will meet spontaneously and interact with each other and
build relationships [A2].
In continuation of this discussion, the balance between organisation structure and flexibility can be
discussed. The collaboration agreement showed to serve the pilot both negatively and positively. The
agreement made sure that the staff knew their roles and responsibilities concerning the bed community,
which kept discussions to a minimum. However, the written down agreement worked against the need
for flexibility in a changeable work environment. Therefore, the collaboration agreement did not help
developing a culture where the nursing staff was rewarded to act flexible if it encourages to solving the
core task (Moltke and Graff 2014). However, nurses from similar projects at Aarhus University Hospital
explains how they have learned that it is important to have a defined framework and agreement when
collaborating across wards. This way, the specialisations can agree on what and how the collaboration
should work and fit it to their environment and needs [A2]. Theory of relational coordination further-
more supports the effect of writing down common agreements, saying that it increases the common
language and understanding across wards. Furthermore, it helps to ensure an accurate communication
among the employees avoiding misunderstandings and mistrust (Gittell 2012).
Our initial fieldwork showed that no bed wards are identical and even though they follow the same
goal and principles, there is no universal work system for all wards [A1][A2][A3]. To accommodate this,
Hasle et al. (2017) suggest that adjustable dimensions can be used to find the local situational solutions
that are most optimal. It is the adaptation to the local context that is crucial to design for collaboration.
The adaptation results in local ownership and there are always local conditions that need to be taken
into account. Therefore, it can take relatively long time for departments to make the individual organ-
isational initiatives to work as intended. The affected employees must be involved, different solutions
must be tested, and routines must be incorporated. It often requires several initiatives and repetitions
to ensure internal coherence in the ward. Hasle et al. (2017) explain that the collaborating hospital can
benefit both patients and staff, but this is not a strategy that can simply be rolled out over the entire
hospital system, because then the local commitment is not achieved which is crucial for persistence.
Instead, the strategy requires that the local managers in collaboration with their employees design the
local work system for collaboration. This is reflected in our final solution’s design process that enables
the different wards to adjust the work system design to their specific needs.
Our results showed that the relational coordination improved with 0.35 points by implementing the
work system in the bed ward. By organising the nursing staff working in the bed community, it enabled
==PAGE:87==

the nursing staff to easily coordinate knowledge and actions around their common goal (Moltke and
Graff 2014). Designing a work system focusing on a specific patient group helped to clarify the common
goal for both units. However, since relational coordination is very sensitive for adjustments, it is uncer-
tain if the work system will have the same effect when defining a different patient group as the joint
patients (Edwards 2014). It is essential to focus on the work around the patients when measuring the
relational coordination. In our case study, the patient group was Covid-19 patients which is a narrow
group concerning the medical tasks because the patients in most cases need the same type of treatment.
This made the work procedure in the bed community simple and stable without many variations which
we believe have influenced the increase in the relational coordination. When other bed wards define
their relevant patient groups to collaborate on, it will not necessarily have the same effect in relational
coordination if the patient group is broader or their final work system allows for more variation than
the one, we tested in our case study.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Through our case study in a bed ward at North Zealand Hospital we have investigated the existing
nursing work system and identified challenges and possibilities for changing the work system. Based on
our findings from the case study, we have designed a nursing work system that supports collaboration
across bed wards and a design process that can encourage the bed wards to start the design process,
involve the nursing staff and adjust the work system to their local settings.
Supported by theory of social capital our analysis of the existing work system showed that the silo
structure in the hospital has resulted in bed wards with a very strong bonding social capital that builds
on trustful and justified relationships. This is expressed through the nursing staff’s continuous com-
munication and mutual commitment internally in the bed ward. The nursing staff’s varying workday
require flexibility in their work system as well as structured communication to get their work done. The
work system is highly influenced by the culture in the bed ward which is dominated by the nursing
staff’s need for a sense of belonging to their own specialisation. We learned how the culture affected the
nursing staff’s willingness to engage in the implementation of new initiatives and that they required a
meaningful purpose in their daily tasks. These dynamics were as well linked to the demanding workload
the nursing staff must deal with due to limited resources in terms of staff and time. The dominating
bonding social capital in the bed wards limited the bridging social capital to other bed wards. Before
we changed the work system, the nursing staff did not realise the potential in collaborating across their
specialisations because no daily interactions between bed wards were facilitated in their work. The
lack of interactions across the specialisations has resulted in limited insight in each other’s work which
hinders collaboration and establishment of relationships.
The results from our case study further support theory of communities of practise and show how facili-
tating boundary meetings across communities can establish a boundary practise that connects the bed
wards and increases knowledge sharing and learning. This contributes to balance general and specialised
knowledge in the nursing staff team which is valuable to accommodate future challenges in hospitals in-
cluding an increasing number of patients with multimorbidity. According to our results and supported
by theory of relational coordination the creation of a boundary practise that facilitate collaboration on
joint patients between neighbouring bed wards increases the relational coordination. Throughout the
case study we have discovered that coordination accommodates the flexibility required in the nursing
staff’s changeable work. When working across bed wards on a joint patient it requires information
sharing, planning and decision-making, but our case study showed that also belonging to a speciali-
sation area is critical to the nursing staff. When aiming for coordination rather than collaboration
between the bed wards, they can remain independent and specialised while contributing with their rel-
==PAGE:89==
70
7 Conclusion
evant competences to neighbouring wards when they need other competences to treat the joint patients.
Based on our recommendations from the case study, we created a final work system design. By defining
a joint patient group between the two neighbouring bed wards, the bed wards can implement the work
system. When patients are assessed to be a joint patient it is marked in the patient chart. The facili-
tation of a formal meeting with the nursing staff from each bed ward who is responsible for the joint
patients enables them to share knowledge and coordinate the work on the joint patients. The nursing
staff is only responsible for joint patients within their own area of specialisation to ensure confidence in
their competences and a sense of belonging.
The fact that the New North Zealand Hospital building will have standardised physical settings, shared
facilities and locate specialisations who share competences in continuation of each other, will encourage
the bed wards to implement a work system that facilitates collaboration with their neighbouring bed
ward. To make our final work system design adaptable to the individual bed wards and their differ-
ences, we discovered that a design process that involves the nursing staff has to be underwent before
the implementation. By testing the design process through the case study, we learned the importance
of clarifying the purpose for the nursing staff as well as engaging them in decisions and adjustments of
the work system design. Through the case study we found that it was challenging to implement a work
system in the bed ward while the nursing staff had to manage their usual tasks in parallel. This proved
that prioritising time and resources for development is essential for implementation of work systems to
become successful.
Though it takes time and engagement to build relationships across communities of practise, our thesis
shows that it is possible to design a work system that supports the development of such by facilitating
meetings across bed wards and account for the cultural leverage on the work system. Facilitating co-
ordination between neighbouring bed wards on a joint patient group is the first step towards creating
collaboration across nursing specialisations in the hospital.
7.1
Considerations for the hospital
The bed wards at North Zealand Hospital will have to prepare themselves for the new work systems
at the new hospital. Based on our study, we recommend all wards to initiate the first steps of the
design process as early as possible by facilitating boundary meetings across nursing specialisations to
enhance their understanding of each other and acknowledge the potential for collaboration. This will
ensure that the nursing staff will be prepared and engaged in the work systems at the new hospital. In
continuation, it will be interesting to investigate what effect the implementation of the work system
has on the doctors’ role in the work system and whether or not they should be included in the design
process and the work system design. Though the project scope has been focusing on North Zealand
Hospital, we believe that other Danish hospitals can also use our final solution as inspiration for how
they can begin a process of implementing more collaboration across bed wards.

CHAPTER 8
Project Reflection
Retrospectively, it is relevant to reflect on our choices and given conditions throughout the thesis project.
In this chapter we will reflect on the strengths and limitations of our chosen approach and explain how
some given conditions for the project has affected the result.
8.1
Data Collection
For collecting data our predominantly methods have been qualitative consisting of participant observa-
tions and contextual interviews in the bed ward of LIA. We will reflect on how we observed in practise
and what affect our presence and approach had on the nursing staff and patients.
We started by doing the participant observations and contextual interviews in the bed wards together
the two of us. This was to get two perspectives on the situation as well as double eyes and ears to
observe and ask the right questions. Eventually we realised the effect of splitting up. Being only one
person to follow the nursing staff around the bed ward showed to be more efficient, since we could talk
to double as many employees within the same period and also it seemed as if the nursing staff had a
more relaxed attitude towards only one person instead of two. For the rest of the project, we split up,
one following 0651 and one following 0655. We made sure that it was the same person who followed
the same unit, since we became more familiar with the employees along the way. Although, this also
meant that we risked losing important points, experiences and findings by not being present both of
us. But by writing everything down and meet for a short follow up during the session, we made sure
to share knowledge and document everything of importance.
We only did evaluation, observation and contextual interviews at the hospital on Friday day shifts.
This meant that we ended up including many of the same nursing staff members in our evaluation and
rarely met the nursing staff who tended to work night or evening shifts. The staff schedule changes
every week and we always met new staff, but it would have been interesting to observe if anything was
different during the evening shifts. Yet only being present on Fridays gave us a structure and made
sure that the nursing staff knew what to expect from us. Additionally, it made sense to be there on
Fridays, since the newsletter comes out on Fridays and we would add changes and comments on the
work in the bed community in the newsletter to implement changes the following Saturday.
We experienced how our relationship to the nursing staff developed throughout the pilot. In the begin-
ning the staff was confused about us being there not knowing the purpose of our presence too well. Along
the way they got use to us which was reflected in their attitude towards us and their willingness to talk.

This meant that the observations and conversations we did before the pilot was done while not knowing
the staff very well and therefore, they might have been more closed and private about details when
talking to them. Additionally, we did not have much time to collect data for the analysis of the existing
system since the pilot was to begin shortly after we were included in the project. It would have been
valuable to have had an even deeper look into the existing work system based on a longer period of time.
The primarily focus of the pilot was the collaboration between the nursing staff.
Therefore, obser-

including coordination and collaboration between the nursing staff when treating the patient that we
have not witnessed. Collaboration across specialisation includes collaborating on the patient and there-

they help each other treating patients. Additionally, we learned from the case evaluation (see section
4.7.2: Focus Group Interview) that the pilot compromised the treatment of the patients outside the bed
community. This perspective was not realised before the pilot ended and therefore it was not included
throughout the pilot, which would have been valuable for the ongoing adjustments of the model.
For our evaluation we used questionnaires that we sent out to the nursing staff before and after the
pilot to compare their answers and investigate the effect of the pilot. We sent out the survey for all 80
nursing staff members to respond and had 37 respondents before and 34 respondents after. Though it
was almost the same number of staff who responded we cannot be sure that it was the same individuals
who responded which can question the strength of the comparison between before and after.
Our approach to the data collection has resulted in us gaining insights into the actual nursing work
system since we followed the nursing staff personally in the context of their work. We involved actors
from different levels of the hierarchy, including patients, nursing staff, nurse managers, the head nurse,
developers and administrators. This has been a strength for the validity of our final solution. Retro-
spectively, it might have given us more clear results to have had a more structured approach setting
up more semi-structured interviews instead of observing, but we found participant observations and
contextual interviews more suitable for the work system in a bed ward.

Our Role in the Project Group
The fact that we became a part of the project group affected the division of roles and responsibility in
the group. Before we entered the project group, it was already comprised of nursing staff from 0651 and
0655, though they had not started the process yet. When entering the project group, we agreed to be
responsible for formal tasks as defining the process, describing the model and evaluate it every week in
order to improve it. The project group was originally defined to develop the concrete model that should
describe the work system in the bed community. As a part of the project group, we participated in the

sensed that they expected us to do it. Since we intentionally planned a workshop in order to develop the

8.3
Importance of Communication
During the pilot, communication has shown to be critical. Already in the planning phase it was dis-
cussed in the reference group how the pilot should be communicated clearly to avoid misunderstandings
and negative rumours. Therefore, a communication plan was made. To avoid rumours, it was decided
by the nurse managers that there should be no information given to the nursing staff before the informa-
tion meeting where all nursing staff was invited to receive information on the pilot. Since this meeting
was held during work hours only 20 nursing staff members out of 80 were present. This meant that only
25 pct. of the nursing staff received the necessary information. Information on the pilot was added to
the Friday newsletter and posted in the nursing staffs’ respective Facebook groups. Afterwards, it was
learned that far from all nursing staff reads the newsletter. We quickly observed that the communica-
tion plan was not enough to provide the necessary information to the nursing staff before starting the
pilot. The information came too late. When the pilot began, there was confusion about the purpose
of the bed community and the responsibilities. This resulted in misunderstandings and conflicts and
had a negative effect on the nursing staff in both units. It was learned that the importance of timing
and correct communication is even more crucial than expected. It was experienced how face-to-face
communication between the staff worked the best, since it was more honest.
Furthermore, the information about the importance of filling out evaluation cards and giving feed-
back after their shift in the bed community showed to be insufficient. Less than half of the nursing
staff filled out the evaluation cards and we found it challenging to come up with methods to motivate
them apart. A way to ensure a higher answer rate could have been to use stronger motivational tools
as nudging or rewards. The low answer rate resulted in us excluding the evaluation cards from our
evaluation of the pilot.
The nurse managers were not a part of the project group which meant that they were not involved
in designing the pilot and the ongoing changes. This caused issues since the nurse managers did not
take a part in mediating the project to the nursing staff. They knew the importance of the project
but did not feel as a part of it. Their role and influence showed to be more important regarding the
==PAGE:93==

communication plan than expected. It was learned how the staff follows their managers and expect
important information on changes from them. There was a discontinuity between the nurse managers
work with coordinating and allocating the bed community and not being part of the decisions behind

managers had been more involved in the information of the project and acted as stronger advocates.
8.4
Covid-19 Limitations
The Covid-19 pandemic has existed throughout our entire thesis process. Fortunately, we were allowed
to execute a case study with a bed ward dealing with Covid-19 patients. Though almost every process at
the hospital was affected by the pandemic and they expected the situation to get worse during autumn,
the LIA department wished to follow through with the project on collaboration across their units and
it furthermore motivated them when we were connected to the project as a resource. Originally, the
patient group who was supposed to lie in the bed community during the pilot was pneumonia patients.
Due to the risk of the number of hospitalised Covid-19 patients rising, it was decided to change the
patient group to be Covid-19 patients instead of pneumonia patients. This made sense because Covid-19
is an infectious disease affecting the lungs. Furthermore, the change in patient group meant that though
the number of Covid-19 patients would rise during the pilot, the project could still continue since the
pilot would now consist of a work system able to deal with a rise in hospitalised Covid-19 patients.
Though the change of patient group meant that we could actually follow through with our project,
it affected the nursing staff’s attitude towards the project. The nursing staff was from the beginning
tired of dealing with Covid-19 related issues and it was clear that the pandemic took a lot of their
attention and energy already. Furthermore, the risk of being infected by the disease and the exhaust-
ing work carrying the mandatory isolation equipment was an essential factor for the nursing staff and
their attitude towards working in the bed community. These factors surely affected the nursing staff’s
willingness and motivation to collaborate in the bed community and it was difficult for us to distinguish
between barriers due to covid-19 and barriers due to the implementation of the new collaborating work
system.
Additionally, the Covid-19 patients are placed in isolation areas, that were already defined before the
pilot, to be located in the middle of 0655’s ward unit. This meant that it was not possible to place the
joint patients in the middle of the two units, which was intentionally the plan in order for the units to
feel the same level of ownership of the area. Placing the bed community in 0655 created a big imbalance
between 0651 and 0655 meaning that the nursing staff from 0651 working in the bed community tended
to feel as a guest and lost their feeling of belonging to their own unit. This meant that only 0651 got
insights into their neighbouring unit and not the opposite way around. Also, it decreased the trust and
experience of justice between the two, since they compared who helped each other the most and who
was under biggest pressure.
