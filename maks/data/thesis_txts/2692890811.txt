Abstract
Structural dynamics are a vital part of civil engineering. The engineering field studying the modal
properties of systems under ambient vibrations is called Operational Modal Analysis. The accurate,
continuous prediction of structural vibrations creates new opportunities in structural health monitoring.
This project thus aims to develop a new method for the Operational Modal Analysis of systems based
on short time histories utilising the Random Decrement Technique (RDT).
The method, once developed, is applied to three different types of systems: Time Invariant, Time Vari-
ant, and Non-Linear Systems.
The first Time-Invariant System investigated consists of two quadratic hollow tubes welded together
to form a T-shape. The second Time-Invariant System is the 15 storey high Heritage Court Tower
in Vancouver, Canada. The Time Variant Systems are derived from a Finite Element Model (FEM)
of the aforementioned steel structure, whose frequency variations are modelled through State Space
Simulations. Finally, a 12-DOF system subjected to a severe earthquake event is analysed.
It is hoped that with these tests, a thorough understanding of the performance of the algorithm can
be reached. For this, a sensitivity analysis involving a wide range of parameters has to be performed,
and a similar algorithm implementing the Direct Correlation Function Estimation is implemented for
comparison.
Furthermore, the method is developed to work in conjunction with several techniques. Three identifica-
tion techniques are investigated with regards to RDT based short time history OMA: The Time-Domain
Poly-Reference (TDPR) with a varying number of autoregressive (AR) coeﬀicient matrices, the Ibrahim
Time-Domain (ITD) and a higher order implementation of the ITD.
And finally, the benefits and disadvantages of the addition of a Hilbert-Transform of the signal to the
algorithm are investigated.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The modelling of structures subjected to dynamic loading forms a vital part of any structural analysis.
The significance of this field has grown with the increasingly ambitious projects of our time, the chang-
ing environment, as well as the fast-rising number of offshore structures in operation across the seas.
A classic approach to Dynamic Modelling is to calculate the Stiffness and Mass Matrices for a given
structure from its known dimensions and material properties, and to obtain the expected modal prop-
erties from these values. However, it is important to note that the material properties of a structural
element gradually change over time, both due to degradation from exposure to the environment, and
plastic deformation. Whereas the elastic part of the strain response of a structure is recovered after
loading stops, the plastic part is not, which over time leads to what is commonly known as strain hard-
ening. Furthermore, the significant stresses often occuring during construction mean that the assumed
ideal conditions can become moot before normal operations have even commenced. As the physical
properties of the building materials change, even if the mass and shape of the structure remain largely
intact, the stiffness matrices nevertheless will no longer reflect reality. As such, the accuracy of such
models begins to decrease. To investigate the changes in a structure and update any models, vibration
testing of the structure in question becomes necessary.
Two main paths exist for this purpose, both of which allow for the verification of the analytical models
and the investigation of their dynamic properties. Experimental Modal Analysis, henceforth referred
to as EMA, requires precise knowledge of the input, with the magnitudes, location and direction of all
the forces affecting the test subject. It is thus necessary in this approach to attain almost clinical test
conditions, so that the response measurements, e.g. to a hydraulic shaker test, are not contaminated by
the surroundings. The investigated structure thus cannot operate normally during these tests. These
controlled environments can be hard or even impossible to attain, when e.g. an off-shore structure is
concerned.
This is where the greatest strengths of its alternative, the Operational Modal Analysis (OMA) come
into play: OMA does not require the input forces to be known. Instead, as shall be discussed in greater
detail in section 2.2, they are assumed to be unknown.
Liberated from this requirement, it is no longer necessary to artificially excite the structure in a con-
trolled environment.
If one chooses to do artificial excitations, one can do this with much milder
methods. For example, the test data used for one of the investigated structures, which shall be dis-
cussed later on, were performed by scratching the structure with a pencil. However, one can also simply
use the structures’ response to natural, ambient loading for analysis. Wind, waves, or even simply the
occupants of the structure going about their daily routines all differ from each other significantly, but
as excitation forces they are all valid inputs, as long as the excitations are significant enough to be
discernible from the noise.
The structures investigated can likewise be very different. Many of the algorithms utilised in this thesis
were not investigated with civil engineering in mind. For example, the Random Decrement Algorithm,
which is the focal point of this thesis project, was developed at NASA in the late sixties and early
seventies with space flight in mind[1][2].

This description, however, also make the downsides of OMA apparent. It is not quite straightforward
to implement, requiring sensory equipment of a high enough standard, as well as suﬀicient computation

possibilities, especially regarding Structural Health Monitoring.
1.0.1
Short Time Histories
The fact that OMA allows for the investigated structures to continue their operations normally during
vibration testing opens the door for continuous, almost real-time monitoring of structures. Rather than
evaluating the data of a single, long time history after testing has taken place, it is possible to perform
the analyses continuously on short time histories as time progresses. In this way it can help speed up
the response to critical damage and allow for the prolongation of a life time extension by allowing for
action before further damage occurs. It can also complement more traditional methods where visual
inspection is diﬀicult.
These short time histories will hereafter be referred to as windows, and the estimation of the necessary
sample duration to reliably predict the eigenfrequencies takes a central role in the code developed for
this project.
1.0.2
Random Decrement Algorithm
A key part of Operational Modal Analysis is the estimation of correlation functions for the measured
responses. The Random Decrement technique (RDT) approaches this task in an eﬀicient manner. A
triggering condition is applied to a response signal. The time steps, where this threshold is crossed,
function as the starting points of the time segments to be investigated. The Random Decrement (RD)
signatures are then found by simple averaging.
At every time instant t in the time segment, the response consists of three parts: the response to the
initial displacement, the response to the initial velocity, and the response to the accelerations due to
the random input loads between the initial state and the time instant in question.
==PAGE:15==
1.1 Literature Review
3
When a large enough number of samples for a Gaussian signal are averaged, things tend to even out.
For the sampled time segments this means that, as they all share the same initial conditions, but all
were subjected to random input loads after, the influence of these random input loads will disappear.
The resultant RD signature is then simply proportional to a corresponding correlation function.
Compared to the direct estimation of correlation functions, one can expect the random decrement al-
gorithm to outperform with regards to calculation time.
1.1
Literature Review
The nonlinear dynamic behaviour of structures has been subject to a number of scientific studies over
several decades. Clinton et al.[3] analysed the changes in natural frequencies of the Millikan Library,
which had been damaged by the 1971 San Francisco earthquake, and found the structure to have sus-
tained a 25% eigenfrequency reduction in the north-south direction during the earthquake, followed by
a slow and moderate partial recovery over many years. The article noted that measurable changes in
the natural frequencies of structures not only occur due to extreme events, such as strong earthquakes,
but also due to more moderate ones, such as forced vibrations, minor earthquake shaking, as well as
wind, rain and temperature extremes. The authors pointed out that the duration of recovery cannot be
contained, as it can vary between minutes and years depending on the cause of damage. The degree of
stiffness recovery of the structure likewise varies. The structure can, provided that the frequency ranges
of excitation and structure did not coincide, i.e. no resonance phenomenon occurs, return to a state
with eigenfrequencies very close to the original. These findings were corroborated by Calvi et al.[4] with
the analysis of a number of experimental and measured data, as well as the results of numerical models.
Montejo et al.[5] tracked the frequency changes of a shake-tested concrete column through simulated as
well as experimental data, and concluded that damage assessment based on measurements from before
and after can lead to an underestimation of the actual damage suffered by the structure, as the structure
will have recovered some of its frequency drop by the time the post-event measurements are initialised,
making the continuous tracking of frequency variations of high value structures in earthquake prone
areas desirable.
With much of the mathematical basics instrumental to OMA originally developed for other branches of
engineering, such as mechanical and electrical engineering, there are many papers dealing with individ-
ual aspects of the subjects of this thesis. A good introduction to the field of Operational Modal Analysis
itself, as well as the Random Decrement Technique, is given in Brincker’s Introduction to Operational
Modal Analysis[6], which is referenced frequently throughout the report, and especially in chapter 2.
With the research in Random Decrement dating as far back as the sixties, with the first publication
being Cole[2], one might expect this field to be fully explored. The rd signature was, however, misun-
derstood in the beginning, as a correlation function, and as a free response of the system. The latter
only holds true if the filtering is considered part of the system, as any filtering will inadvertently affect
the signature as well. The mathematical basis for the RDT was finally provided by Vandiver[7] in 1982,
and the same paper also clarified the exact meaning of the RD signature as being proportional to the
correlation function.
The RDT as developed by Cole was implemented as the auto RD exclusively, with the triggering condi-
tions and the averaged time segments all stemming from the same response signal. This is exemplified
by his application of it to the failure detection on a space shuttle wing. A cross referential implemen-
tation of the RDT, where the triggering conditions are defined on one signal, and the time segments
from other but simultaneous response signals, was first demonstrated by Sam Ibrahim in 1977[8]. Due
to Ibrahim’s leading role in the development of the RDT as well as the Ibrahim Time Domain (ITD)
identification technique, the two have likewise been strongly connected, but, as shall be demonstrated

here, other identification methods work similarly well. Given that the RDT takes place firmly in the

Many variations and possibilities in the application of the Random Decrement Algorithm likely remain

With regards to the tracking of changes in the dynamic properties of structures, a number of pa-

The specific

Hilbert-Transforms have enjoyed continuous interest due to their potential contribution to many signal

identification method and showing a consistency between the two methods. However, the Hilbert Trans-
form there as well as in a number of other articles by the same authors were performed on the Power
Spectral Density. No papers specific to the application of the Hilbert Transform in the Time Domain
of short time histories OMA were found.
As such, this thesis project is to my knowledge novel insofar as it adds to the understanding of the
potential of various short time history OMA schemes.
1.2
Objectives
The main aim of the project was the investigation and validation of the viability of the Random
Decrement Algorithm for short time histories based OMA. For this main goal, the following targets
were also pursued:
• Implementation and optimisation of a Random Decrement Algorithm for a short time history
OMA
• Verification of the algorithm by implementing it on various time invariant, time variant and
nonlinear structural systems
• Investigation of the potential of RDT compared to the direct estimation of the Correlation Func-
tion
• Assessment of the impact of various parameters on its computational eﬀiciency and results
• Implementation of a Hilbert Transform to the measured response to obtain the undamped response
• Investigation the ability of the RDT based OMA to properly operate on these undamped responses
• Assessment the viability of this combined approach
The thesis topic can be further distilled into the following two questions:
Can the Random Decrement Technique be used to reliably estimate the modal properties of a signal
using short time histories?
And can the Hilbert Transform play an effective role in such an algorithm?

2.2
Basic Principles of OMA
Operational Modal Analysis is the engineering field that investigates the modal properties of struc-
tural systems under ambient vibrations. These normal operating conditions however mean that it is
impractical to know the precise nature of the forces affecting the structure. The noise is instead being
treated as Gaussian white noise having gone through a loading filter to become the unknown excita-
tion forces affecting the structural system, with the output from the latter being the measured responses.
Figure 2.1: Structural System.
[12] proves that the modal parameters of the structural system and loading filter remain separable
and intact after the combination. With the frequencies and damping factors remaining unaffected, the
individual load contributions thus do not actually have to be Gaussian. The resultant responses will,
regardless of the kind of loading, almost always adhere to the Central Limit Theorem and follow the
”normal”, Gaussian distribution.
The Gaussian distribution means that the mean value of the signal is not needed, with all the informa-
tion concentrated in the second-order properties, which can be described by either spectral densities or
correlation functions (see section 2.5). Both approaches are utilised.
Having measured the responses and having ensured their suﬀicient quality through filtering, the next
step is to estimate a correlation function to obtain the free decay of the system.

will be explained in subsection 2.5.1, and as the main focal point of the thesis, the Random Decrement

Finally, a variety of Time Domain Identification techniques allow for the estimation of the eigenfrequen-

2.3
Signal Processing - Filtering
The randomness of the measured signals negatively affects its quality. It is thus necessary to process
the signals before carrying out the actual Analysis for the obtained information to be reliable.
One cardinal rule of OMA must always be adhered to with regards to filtering: The same filtering must
be applied to all channels of data.
This is because as long as the same filtering has been applied on all channels, the modal parameters
remain unaffected. If on the other hand this rule is not upheld, one can expect the modal parameters
to be affected.
This approach holds also true for the ”loading filter”, the concept illustrated in Figure 2.1 allowing for
the analysis of the responses due to a coloured force input.
Of the filtering schemes featured in this report, four require some considerations to be made as to what
frequency range one is interested in. Three of these, the low-pass, high-pass and band-pass filters, take
place exclusively in the digital realm and are depicted in Figure 2.3.

The relationships between the Nyquist frequency fn, Sampling Frequency fs and Time steps ∆t is given
by

A limit has thus been set for the data, before the actual recording of the data has even been started.
The frequency band ranging from 0 to the Nyquist frequency containing the allowable frequencies is
referred to as the Nyquist band.
2.3.2
Detrend Filter
The term ”Detrending” refers to the removal of any offset of the direct current by forcing the signal to
have zero mean. As such it is an important tool to ensure the accuracy of the signal especially in the
lower frequency range and was always performed on all data sets except for the Nonlinear earthquake
event.
2.3.3
Decimate filter
The higher the sampling frequency, the lower the noise floor. It is thus often chosen to oversample
the signal, and then filter the signal by what is frequently referred to as down sampling or decimation.
Whether this filter is useful or not, depends entirely on the data available. The number of data points
in a signal is reduced by a factor r, and the time steps between these data points increases accordingly
to ∆t · r.
The decimation filter can be very useful for data sets with a high sampling frequency, but disadvanta-
geous for those with large time steps. As such it was chosen not to perform any downsampling on the
nonlinear case, as the time steps were already quite large.
2.3.4
Low pass filter
For measurement data with too wide a Nyquist band compared to the frequency range of interest, it
can prove advantageous to implement a low pass filter with a cut-off filter and a slope as pictured in
section 2.3(), to limit the impact of higher frequencies on the estimates regarding the lower ones and
remove the Direct Current (DC) drift.
As the frequencies of interest in all investigated cases for this thesis project were found in the lower
frequency range, the low-pass filter was featured in a time-variant case for which the band-pass filter
was unsuited.
2.3.5
High pass filter
The high pass filter, pictured in 2.3(a), is the counterpart to the low pass filter, removing the influences
of frequencies below a certain threshold for improved accuracy on the higher modes.
However, two crucial considerations led to this being the only digital filter method that was never uti-
tilised for the project:
For one, the lower modes are almost always the most critical and thus the most worthy of investigation,

as well as the ones where a suﬀicient excitation was almost always ensured.
And secondly, higher modes correspond to higher frequencies and shorter periods. These shorter peri-
ods tend to make the higher modes noncritical when it comes to determining the minimum window size
required for reliable short time history OMA.
The high pass filter was thus the only digital filter method never to be utilised for any of the structural
examples presented in this project.
2.3.6
Band pass filter
The Band pass filter, pictured in 2.3(b) can be described as a combination of the low-pass and high-pass
filters. It features two cut off frequencies, reducing the frequency band of a signal on both sides to a
smaller range with fewer modes.
2.3.7
Modal filtering and modal decomposition
Most algorithms and functions implemented and utilised in this project are capable of processing mul-
tiple response signals at once, to expedite the computations and compartmentalise the challenges, the
main scripts and functions were written to perform on one mode m and the corresponding response
vector ym(t).
Furthermore, it was deemed expedient to perform the analysis on the modal coordinates, rather than
the responses themselves. The relationship between the measured signals y, the mode shape matrix B
and the corresponding modal coordinates q(t) are given by:
y(t) = Bq(t)
(2.31)
From which the equation for the modal coordinates can easily be derived by multiplying the measured
response with the pseudo-inverse of the mode shape matrix B+:
q(t) = B+y(t)
(2.32)
Good OMA testing practice should be adhered to by keeping the number of measurements than the
number of modes of interest. I.e. the mode shape matrix B should contain more rows than columns. It
is also important that any modal decomposition only take place after the modal coordinates have been
estimated from the responses.

2.4.1
Fourier Transform
Having been introduced in 1822 by Jean-Baptise Joseph Fourier, the Fourier Transform allows for the
Decomposition of functions depending on absolute time into functions depending on frequencies and
thus remains absolutely essential for any dynamic modelling. With all data in real cases being discrete,
the version of the Fourier Transform needed for signal processing in the context of the OMA is always
the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT).
The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm was utilised heavily in this project and an algorithm that
excels at computing the the DFT in a very quick and eﬀicient manner, and as such it should not come
as a surprise that MATLAB features the fft function as the primary native tool for the obtainment of
discrete Fourier Transforms. An in-depth explanation of the Fourier transforms would be beyond the
scope of this report and the reader is assumed to already be familiar with them. However, their import
==PAGE:24==
2.4 Signal Processing - Transforms
12
to OMA cannot be overstated, and as such it is worthwhile describing their role in the algorithms
implemented here.
Several functions based on the FFT from the OMAtoolbox by Brincker et al. utilised in this project
include:
• fftint1, for the integration of signal matrices via Frequency response functions, ultimately relying
on MATLAB’s own fft function.
• fftsim, for the simulation of responses from the random Gaussian excitations one modal coordi-
nate/DoF at a time,
• fftspec, for the obtainment of spectral density matrices as well as the plotting of spectral densi-
ties, by which the eigenfrequencies were identified,
• fftfilt1, for the filtering of data,
• fftseg, for the segmentation of data utilising the Hanning window with 50% overlap,
• and fftsyn for the obtainment of synthetised time signals from segmented time data.
One primary role of the Fourier Transform of the data was to convert the data from the Time Domain to
the Frequency Domain and vice versa with no loss of information. In this project, plots of the spectral
density matrices in the frequency domain were frequently used to estimate the frequency poles visually
in the preprocessing phase, whereas the computations themselves were for the most part performed
in the Time-Domain, as both the direct correlation function estimation and the random decrement
technique in their classic versions are firmly rooted in the time domain as well.
Frequency Domain implementations do exist however, such as the Random Decrement Frequency Do-
main Decomposition (RD-FDD) scheme, which allows for the evaluation of RD functions spectra with
the FFT algorithm, provided that spectral leakage is taken into account by ensuring that the RD
functions are long enough to allow for a total decay.

CHAPTER 3
Methodology
This chapter pertains to the implementation of the algorithms, the theoretical background of which
were described in chapter 2.
The computations were almost exclusively carried out in MATLAB, whose vast number of in-built
tools were complimented by the OMAtoolbox developed by Rune Brincker, and supplemented with
additional functions and scripts by Sandro Amador.
These tools were used as the foundations, on
which the developed algorithms were built.
3.1
Signal Preprocessing
Before performing the actual short time histories OMA with the algorithm created for this project, it’s
adviced to first perform a few steps to obtain proper, unbiased results.
One must first consider the modes m of interest, and for each of these modes, the corresponding mode
shape bm needs to be determined to then be collected into the mode shape matrix B, which will be
necessary to perform the modal filtering on the measured responses.
These mode shape matrices can be estimated from a Finite Element Model, or obtained by performing
an OMA on a preliminary long time history.
Secondly, one ought to look at the spectral densities and determine the eigenfrequencies for the data, as
well as the modal participation factor and approximate damping for each of the modes. And on these
grounds, one should then decide on the digital filtering settings best suited to obtain unbiased results,
and matching the frequency range of interest, as explained in greater detail in chapter 2.
3.2
Overall script architecture
Specialised project files were created for each case to extract modeshapes and eigenfrequencies from the
files, as well as create the simulated responses utilising the Fast Fourier Transform.
A dedicated main file was set up for the loading of the various .mat-data files, and then calling the
various matlab scripts performing the short time history OMA. The main file was written in such a way
as to allow for the best possible comparability between the various functions implemented in the script.
This was done by allowing the user to implement various computational settings as a vector, in which
case they would be performed one after the other, with the results being saved in cells. The

mentation without Hilbert Transformation (HT0), with Hilbert Transformation of the whole response
pre-short-time-history-OMA (HT1) and Hilbert Transform of each signal window (HT2), whilst fixing
all other options at one value. The script would then run all 2 × 3 × 3 = 18 possible combinations for

3.6 Implementation of the direct Correlation Function

3.6
Implementation of the direct Correlation Function
The direct estimation of the correlation function with subsequent identification of the modal properties
mirror the approach described in algorithm 1. However, it is even simpler, as only ever one correlation
function is estimated, and as such, it did not require the distinction between the various signatures,
which were done for the Random Decrement case.
3.7
Stability Criteria
The stability criteria generally come in two forms: The failure can either lead to a specific window being
excluded, or to the windowsize being considered insuﬀicient, with the windowing repeated for a larger
window size.
3.7.1
Modal Coordinates Criterion
To ensure reliable results, it was necessary to ensure that the excitation of the structure has been
suﬀicient to ensure reliable results in spite of the presence of noise. This has been done by calculating
the absolute standard deviation σ1(w) for the modal coordinates present in each window w, which were
subsequently normalised for the largest magnitude of the response in modal coordinates q(t) found in
each window. The normalised standard deviation for each window was then juxtaposed to the modal
coordinates criterion, which was for the most part kept at 0.3. The results were excluded, where this
criterion was not met, to account for the larger influence of noise at lower excitations.
3.7.2
Fitting Criterion
The second stability criterion is implemented by fitting the estimated correlation functions/free re-
sponses onto synthesised free decays. For the direct correlation function estimation algorithm, this was
explained in subsection 2.5.1. For the random decrement signatures, the free decay formulae developed
in subsection 2.1.2 for zero initial displacement and in subsection 2.1.1 for zero initial velocity were
utilised to fit the first four and last four signatures respectively.
In both cases, the synthesised functions Rqs are fitted to the estimates obtained in each window. If the
maximum standard deviations between them exceed the standard deviation criterion, the identification
is considered a failure, and the windowing is repeated again for a larger window size.
σ2 = std(Rq −Rqs)
(3.6)
However, one should remember that the Random Decrement algorithm implemented actually supplies
8 free response estimates.
The implementation here is thus such that the windowing is considered
failed, if none of the signatures was able to meet the standard deviation criterion. If several signatures
manage to meet the criterion at the same step, the signature with the lowest standard deviation from
the estimated free decay is considered the best one and used moving forward.
3.7.3
Damping Criterion
A third failure criterion is proposed to complement the fitting criterion in subsection 3.7.2.
It is generally not possible to reliably assess the damping ratio for short time histories. The damping
ratio ζ can thus often take extreme values. However, significant deviations of the damping ratio reflect
nonphysical values, and a correlation between the error in the frequency estimation and the magnitude
==PAGE:40==

of the damping can be observed.
As such, two methods of utilising excessive damping as a failure criterion are proposed:
1. The first approach is to simply exclude the values with too high damping values.
2. The second approach is to increase the window size further if the damping criterion was exceeded
by any of the windows.
Both approaches were implemented, and shall be discussed further in subsection 5.1.5.

Modal Coordinates Criterion
The Modal Coordinates Criterion serves the purpose of eliminating windows with insuﬀicient excitation,
as the effect of noise on weakly excited windows can lead to false results. Beyond that, however, there

criterion was thus ultimately chosen to be σq = 0.3 as a compromise, as well as for the results to remain
comparable to the others. The test on the T-structure was considered suﬀicient for all other cases and
kept.

CHAPTER 5
Time Variant Systems
In line with the conclusions of the article by Clinton et al.[3], two fundamental types of time-variant
changes were simulated utilising the state space simulation procedure as set out by Bart Peeters in
his PhD thesis[15] and explained in section 2.7. The first being a gradual sinoidal frequency variation,
much like what one could expect from temperature shifts in a day-night cycle, as well as some other
weather phenomena, and the second a sudden significant frequency drop, much like what one could
expect to happen during a significant earthquake event, followed by a partial recovery.
It should be noted that the fourth and fifth mode were considered both noncritical (as the window
lengths required by them are shorter) and trivial (as they are easier than the first three modes), and
are thus for the most part kept out of this chapter for brevity’s sake, as there was little to show with
either of them that couldn’t be demonstrated with the third mode as well.
Finally, the addition of 2% normally distributed noise to the State Space Simulation was initially
implemented, but ultimately removed from this thesis project. As shown by Krogenes Solheim’s thesis[9]
and expected on the grounds of the central limit theorem, the impact of such noise was usually very low,
as the windows already had to include several seconds worth of time steps. With the sampling frequency
usually set to 1600 Hz as had been done in Krogenes Solheim’s thesis, the gaussian distribution of this
noise was practically guaranteed.
5.1
Sensitivity Analysis
Many of the conclusions drawn from the sensitivity analysis for the time-invariant system were consid-
ered to be transferrable to the Time-Variant system as well, such as that of the modal criterion and
the signature length. For example, the decimation factor was kept at r = 4, and the signature lengths
continued to be tied to the frequency of the mode being investigated.
A number of even fundamental assumptions, however, were not necessarily transferable and thus reeval-
uated.
5.1.1
Window Lengths
A minor assumption in need of readdressing relates to the window lengths and stems from the fact that
in the previous chapter, the analysis was done in the knowledge that the system would change very
little over the course of the measurements. Increasing the window lengths would thus inevitably lead
to the point from which onwards all windows would remain unconditionally stable.
For the Time Variant system, this is no longer necessarily true. If the window lengths become too
large, they end up containing more change than they can reasonably process, and as such the windows
can become unstable again, before potentially stabilise once more. The term and concept of the Un-
conditionally Stable Window (USW) will be continued and keep the same name for simplicity’s sake,
even though the unconditionality is compromised. But as excessively large windows are of no particular

interest to short time histories OMA, those can be disregarded. However, when reading the term USW
in this chapter and the Non-Linear chapter 6, this qualification of the term should be kept in mind.
5.1.2
Filter settings

One also has to be more careful with regards to band-pass filters when analysing time-variant or nonlin-
ear system. If the varying frequency moves out of the frequency band, the algorithm will no longer be
able to track it. An admittedly exaggerated example of this can be seen in Figure 5.1. The bandpass
settings were thus widened for all modes, and this inevitably led to somewhat larger error margins. The
standard deviation was in turn changed from 0.2 to 0.15 to counter this.
5.1.3
Identification Methods
The various identification methods were likewise investigated once more, and it was expected that these
would have to be changed to higher order versions. Surprisingly, however, it was found that all identi-

the TDPR with na = 3 and the third order ITD. This is illustrated well with the RMFE plotted for
the first mode in Figure 5.2, but also by the second and third mode in section D.1.
It was thus ultimately decided to stick to the TDPR with na = 2 used in chapter 4.

5.1.4
Sampling Frequency
The sampling frequencies are in a real world scenario usually implemented such that the Nyquist fre-
quency band encompasses all the modes of interest. Rune Brincker in ”Introduction to Operational
Modal Analysis”[6] recommends that the Nyquist frequency be separated from the highest mode of
interest by approximately 20%, such that any issues are avoided. As such, and given that the highest
mode with some added tolerance for the frequency variation is at around 100Hz, this gives a minimum
permissible sampling frequency of around 240Hz, which can be rounded up further to 250 Hz.
To
keep everything else comparable, the number of data points in each segment as well as the number of
segments themselves were kept the same.
The outcome was as expected. The much reduced time step size of now 4ms forces the algorithm to
compensate for this loss of information by increasing the length of the window Similar had already been
observed for the HCT with a time step of ∆t = 2.5 ms compared to the T-structure with a time step of
∆t = 0.605 ms and a sampling frequency of 1653 Hz. However, in the time-variant case, this is the rare
occasion where the highest modes become the hardest to process, as they then have too low a resolution
to be accurately estimated, as unlike in the Time-Invariant scenario, the allowable length for a window
is limited.
It can thus be concluded that, compared to the time invariant cases, the time-variant requirements with
regards to the time step size are more strict.
Given that there is little else of interest to be gained from low sample sizes apart from observing the
algorithm compensating for this loss of information, and in order to keep the results comparable to the
Time-Invariant T-structure, the sampling frequency was thus set to 1600 Hz for the both Time Variant
cases.

5.1.5
The Role of Damping, and the Damping Criterion
An additional, optional criterion that had been scarcely talked about so far except for subsection 3.7.3,
was damping. It should be noted once more that the damping itself cannot be reliably or precisely be
estimated from short time history OMA. The relative mean damping errors were significantly worse
than those for the frequencies, as shown for the time-invariant cases in Figure B.1 and Figure B.2. And
yet, it was found that extreme damping values were a reliable indicator for a bad frequency estimate as
well.
This was found to be particularly valuable with regards to the Hilbert Transformed OMA, which suffered
from outliers due to distortions from the normalisation to its envelope, which were misinterpreted by
the algorithm as high damping, but it is useful as well for non-Hilbert Transformed signals. In the
Time-Invariant case, where larger window sizes always mean better identification, a damping failure
criterion can be implemented to reject window lengths which yielded nonphysical results.
A more
moderate solution is to treat these extreme outliers as unidentified windows, and simply exclude them
from further analysis.
Ultimately it was decided, in order to not over-complicate any comparisons with other works, to neither
exclude the results nor utilise the implemented damping criterion (the code of which is nonetheless listed
in ), but to merely highlight the frequencies with a damping of 75% or higher of the maximum damping
ratio attained for that specific window length. One can quickly observe in the frequency variation figures
for Time-Variant systems presented hereafter, that the damping extremes almost always coincide with
either outliers, which ought to be discarded, or very significant frequency changes in that specific
window.

5.3
State Space Simulation of earthquakes
The second State Space Simulation emulated an earthquake scenario. The State space simulation was
thus set into three parts. First, an initial flat period to simulate the time before the event. Then, a
very considerable 50% drop. And finally, after 10 seconds, a quick partial recovery of 50%, such that
the frequency for the remainder of the response is at 75% of the original. The overall length of the
response was kept the same as for the first case.
The goal set out here was quite simple: Would it be possible to not only extract the frequencies for
the first, undamaged, and the third, partially recovered part, but also the middle part where the steep
drop had occured? One big problem, as explored by Clinton et al, was that by measuring the structure
before event and after the recovery had taken place would often lead to an underestimate of the actual
damage that had occured. Being able to observe this change would be a great selling point for the
developed method. The MRMFE was thus for once considered to be of secondary import.
The bandpass filter was customised for each investigated mode to have the center of the bandpass at
75%, and the band-pass width at a bit over 50% of the initial eigenfrequency.
Treating all modes uniformly in the state space simulation was a bit unrealistic, as the lower frequencies
tend to be hit the hardest by the frequency drops. However, treating the higher modes differently would
have trivialised them further, when they already were quite easy to compute.
Ultimately, the algorithm proved capable of measuring the frequency drop at a range of window lengths,
although the remarks on the ”unconditional” stability of the windows proved highly relevant. A sequence
of stable window lengths would often be followed by another sequence of failures, which in turn were
again succeeded by good results. This clearly correlated to the ratio between the window lengths and

CHAPTER 7
Analysis
The goal of the thesis project had been the development of a short time history based OMA with the
Random Decrement Algorithm, and the assessment of its viability. The algorithm was to contend with
three distinct types of systems and handle them well. It was to support various Identification techniques
and to compete with the direct estimation of the correlation function.
With this versatility in mind, the choice was made at the midpoint in the semester to completely re-
work the code from several similar, but independent scripts into one system. Overall, the scripts and
functions developed from this performed quite well and fulfilled these ambitions. The user could specify
the parameters in the main file, as well as choose which parameters he wants compared. The script
would run the OMA based on these settings, and, if several values had been chosen for some of them,
it would run them in sequence and store the results. It was not necessary to even tell the script what
kind of system it was dealing with. The only part of the script that needed the information, the plot-
ting section at the end, could tell that automatically from the loaded response file. With two different
correlation/free response function estimation techniques, three different identification methods and a
vast number of other settings, the possibilities were limited only by the RAM, and, if too many figures
had been asked for at the end, the graphics card. The flexibility often allowed for multiple avenues
to verify the results, and that one element could be switched out for a better one with little diﬀiculty.
The main take away of this is that it was the Random Decrement Technique, which allowed for all of
this adaptability, and much that has been observed regarding the RDT can be transferred to where it
is used for the analysis of short time histories.
The RDT is often described as the fast and eﬀicient method compared to the direct estimation of the
correlation function, which in turn is a bit more precise, and this observation also held true with regards
to short time histories and the cases investigated here.
With regards to the time invariant systems, it was able to handle them very well, even where compli-
cations, such as tightly spaced modes, made the separation diﬀicult. In time-invariant scenarios, the
algorithm was able to overcome most issues by simply increasing the window size. This was especially
noticeable with regards to the Heritage Court Tower, where the USW for the second mode was found
to be around 21 seconds.
However, proper preparation of the data was always the key to success and, even where not strictly
necessary to attain useable results, proper filtering often both improved the accuracy and shortened
the computation times significantly, such that less than 4 seconds were enough to attain unconditional
stability for all five modes of the T-structure.
The band-pass filter proved to be the most useful of all filter settings, and was used for most cases.
The low-pass filter was also implemented and tested out for the first two modes of the Time-Invariant
system, but ultimately replaced by the better performing band-pass.
The high pass filter was never utilised, as it never fit the problem. The hardest modes to identify
were almost always the first modes. In the only case, where it might have been useful to eliminate
the domination effect of the first mode, it was hard to implement, as the frequency variations led to
==PAGE:81==
7 Analysis
69
overlaps between the modes in the frequency bands.
The decimation (or down-sampling) of the signal proved to be a double edged sword, as it increased
the length of the timesteps, leading to poorer performance where the conditions didn’t allow for it.
However, under proper circumstances it could improve the accuracy of the results.
This thesis thus clashes with the observation in Krogenes Solheim’s thesis, that the decimation factor
of 8 was not possible for the T-structure. The algorithm was, given the very small original time step
of 0.605ms, not perturbed by the 8fold increase, and even saw a small gain in accuracy compared to
r = 4, but not significantly enough to make it worthwhile, unless neither band-pass nor low-pass filter
had been applied.
The proper Random Decrement length very much depended on the frequency investigated. Where the
signal was too long, it led to the inclusion of the noise tail, which negatively affected results, as was
shown in figure 4.12(b). The low frequencies of the first modes, however, required a large enough sample
size to cover a full cycle. The outcome of that sensitivity analysis was thus to make the various signal
lengths dependent on the mode being investigated.
The modal coordinates criterion on the other hand was largely left intact. The criterion serves an im-
portant function by removing weakly excited modes, whose results are more tainted by noise than the
windows with higher excitations. However, increasing the criterion too much simply resulted in fewer
windows being analysed and information being lost. When the differences between smaller increments
were investigated, the changes in the results were rather limited. The Modal Coordinates Criterion was
thus only reduced for the Non-Linear case, as the high fluctuations during the event made the algorithm
less well than before.
The standard deviation criterion on the other hand was frequently adjusted depending on circumstance.
It was set to 0.2 for the Time-Invariant cases, 0.15 for the Time-Variant cases, and 0.25 for the non-
linear case, as it made intuitive sense to give more leeway during such a volatile event.
The author maintains that a damping criterion can be a good addition to the two other criteria. Whilst
the damping estimates are seldom good, extreme values tend to signify a nonphysical measurement.
For time-invariant systems, it can be used as an indicator to signify that a longer window length might
be appropriate. For time-variant and non-linear Systems, on the other hand, the recommendation is
to simply filter the frequency measurements related to high damping estimates out. The two most
significant outliers in Figure 6.4, for example, are both identifiable through this method. This approach
could be explored further however, as it wasn’t considered relevant enough for the thesis topic to be
expounded on in any detail for this report.
With regards to Identification Techniques, the Time-Domain Poly Reference with na = 2 Autoregressive
coeﬀicient matrices and the standard Ibrahim Time Domain performed similarly well. The higher order
ITD and the TDPR with a larger number of AR matrices only became viable under highly specific cir-
cumstances where the author hadn’t prepared the data well, or chosen the right parameters. Whenever
the house was in order, the standard ITD and TDPR2 were unmatched by the others.
Finally, the Hilbert Transform did not meet the expectations. It was rarely the case that it produced
better results than the non-Hilbert Transformed RDT, and rarer still for this improvement to have been
significant enough for the effort to have been worthwhile.
==PAGE:82==
CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
In the Introduction, the objective of this thesis project had been formulated as two fundamental ques-
tions:
Can the Random Decrement Technique be used to reliably estimate the modal properties of a signal
using short time histories?
And can the Hilbert Transform play an effective role in such an algorithm?
In the course of the project, it was found that the answer to the first question was a definite Yes.
The Random Decrement Algorithm as implemented was generally well able to perform with both time
invariant, time variant and nonlinear structural systems, and was further customiseable with a wide
range of parameters and tools.
The performance generally hinges on the ability of the user to choose the right filter settings and pa-
rameters, and this requires the user to do some optimisation. But for the investigated systems, the
obtained results proved, for the most part, to be very good.
Two time-invariant systems had been investigated in-depth, and the obtained results had been of high
quality. For the T-structure, not even the outliers exceeded a relative mean frequency error of 0.5%,
and for the Heritage Court Tower, with less than 1.5%, but for larger window lengths, the performance
was also very good.
The Time-Variant Systems investigated weren’t quite as good, but this was very much due to them
deliberately having been made rather diﬀicult. The algorithm was nevertheless able to cope with an
instantaneous frequency drop of 50%, identify the frequency drop within a very limited time, before
the recovery set in.
Regarding the Non-Linear system, the results were likely close to as good as they could be given the
circumstances. A very large time step size contributed to the problem.
Whilst the Random Decrement Technique was rarely observed to best the Correlation Function in terms
of precision to a significant degree, it made up for a, depending on the circumstances, very slight reduc-
tion in accuracy with eﬀiciency.
Depending on the circumstances and the task at hand, this might well make it the best choice. In a
real world application, where the measurement system would continue running for years and years, this
eﬀicacy can outweigh the advantage of having yet another zero added after the comma of the margin
of error.
The second question however, cannot be answered in the same way. The Hilbert Transform as imple-
mented by the author can neither in the complete nor windowed form contribute effectively to the short
time histories Random Decrement algorithm.
==PAGE:83==
8.1 Further perspectives
71
8.1
Further perspectives
The algorithm as implemented in the course of this thesis project proved itself capable of delivering
frequency estimates with high accuracy, and many aspects of it have been explored in depth. However,
there is much room left for exploration. For example, both of the Time Variant and the Non-Linear
System all required for the band pass filters to either be widened or completely abandoned. This could
be changed by having the filtering be performed in each window of the signal, and have the algorithm
communicate with a finite element model, in conjunction with which it might be able to determine
whether an observed extreme measurement is just the result of noise, or an actual frequency shift.
Similar can be said regarding the mode shape matrices, which also do change over time, albeit only to
a small degree[19]. Furthermore, the signal was normalised into the modal coordinates based on the
entire signal, which is not realistic. Implementing it on the individual windows, as had been done here
with regards to the Hilbert Transform, might present an interesting and promising endeavour.
