Abstract
The administration of biopharmaceuticals through traditional routes is faced with several
challenges due to their large size, waterôÙsolubility and degradation.
The buccal muôÙ
cosa has emerged as a potential drugôÙdelivery site, however, transdermal drug delivery
is severely limited by the tight junctions in the buccal epithelium. Microneedles are a
promising technology for transdermal drug delivery due to their potential biocompatibility
and controlled drug release properties. In particular the properties of hydrogel microneeôÙ
dles make them suitable for buccal drug delivery.
This thesis aims to explore the potential of using microôÙmoulding coupled to the rollôÙtoôÙ
plate system and other strategies for the fabrication of releasable hydrogel microneedles.
Releasable microneedles can help circumvent some patient compliance issues stemmed
form the discomfort of a large buccal patch. Additionally, the fabrication of releasable
microneedles ensures the drug loading is confined to the needle, optimizing the drug
loading.
To obtain releasable microneedles, a two polymer system was designed. Utilizing soluble
Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) films for the patch and Poly(ethylene glycol) diacrylate (PEGDA)
for the microneedles. The work was conducted iteratively, optimizing different aspects of
the fabrication, and adressing the challenges faced. This work is a continuation of two
master thesis that aimed at exploring different hydrogel formulations and introduced the
rollôÙtoôÙplate system as a potential fabrication step.
In conclusion, this work succesfully optimized the application of the rollôÙtoôÙplate system for
the fabrication of releasable hydrogel microneedles, while identifying key challenges that
affect its efficiency. Additionally, a new alternative fabrication method with high potential
for efficiency and scalability was developed.

Introduction
1.1
Biopharmaceuticals and Drug Delivery
The discovery of insulin and its first use as diabetes therapy in 1922 marked a breakôÙ
through in medicine, biotechnology and laid the foundation for the development of bioôÙ
pharmaceuticals. Over the decades, advancements in biotechnology have revolutionized
the production of therapeutic proteins, enabling the largeôÙscale manufacture of recombiôÙ
nant insulin through genetic engineering techniques [1] [2]. These innovations improved
the safety, efficacy, and availability of insulin as well as paved the way for the developôÙ
ment of other biopharmaceuticals such as monoclonal antibodies, vaccines, and gene
therapies. The biopharmaceutical industry has grown exponentially in recent decades,
currently making up 20ôÙ25% of the pharmaceutical market, with continued growth exôÙ
pected [3].
However, biopharmaceuticals which are typically proteinôÙderived are hundreds of times
bigger than other nonôÙbiological pharmaceuticals.
Chemically synthesized molecules
used for therapeutics are usually less than 700 Da while insulin for example is around
5,8 kDa. This proves to be a challenge in the administration of biopharmaceutics causing
low bioavailability [4] [5]. The following are the main administration routes.
1.1.1
Routes of administration
Oral administration
Oral administration refers to the delivery of drugs into the gastrointestinal tract via the
mouth. It offers several advantages such as its convenience and low cost. It is also suitôÙ
able for selfôÙadministration, and its nonôÙinvasive nature makes it well tolerated by most
patients. However, this administrations route is limited by the low bioavailability caused
by the firstôÙpass metabolism [6]. Drugs, especially biologics can be subjected to degradaôÙ
tion in the gastrointestinal tract. Additionally, the epithelial barrierãs permeability can also
limit the bioavailability of macromolecules and biopharmaceuticals that cannot cross this
barrier easily [7] .
Intravenous administration
Intravenous administration refers to the direct administration of the drug into the bloodôÙ
stream through a vein. Intravenous (IV) administrationãs main advantage is the bypass
of the absorption stage, reaching full bioavailability. Additionally, first pass metabolism is
skipped [8]. Although IV administration is specially useful in acute care applications, it reôÙ
quires skilled healthcare professionals for the administration while also being an invasive
route that can cause discomfort for the patient [7].
Transdermal administration
Transdermal administration involves applying the drug formulation on the skin of the paôÙ
tient for systemic absorption. This route provides a steady release and absorption of the
drug while avoiding first pass metabolism. Patient compliance is very high due to the conôÙ
venience of cream and transdermal patch formulations [9]. However, this administration
route is severely limited to lipophilic low molecular mass molecules (under 500 Da) due
to the skin barrier [10].

It is comprised of nonôÙ
keratinized stratified epithelium covered by mucus, the basal lamina and the lamina propia,

The buccal mucosa has a set of characteristics that make it a site of interest for drug
delivery systems. It is a highly vascularized tissue, which allows for quick absorption into
the bloodstream while also being easily accessible. The epithelium layer is around 500
to 800 ö¥m in thickness and connected by tight junctions, which can limit the permeability
of certain large molecules. However, the underlying tissue facilitates the absorption of
drugs into systemic circulation entering the internal jugular vein, bypassing the first pass
metabolism in the gastrointestinal tract. Additionally, the buccal mucosa has potential for
both rapid release and controlled release formulations. Nitroglycerin buccal sprays are
used to treat angina pectora which requires immediate therapeutic effect, and the rapid
absorption in the buccal mucosa allows for quick action [12]. On the other hand, patch
and tablet formulations can be used for drugs that require a sustained release [13].
Despite the advantages that the buccal mucosa offers as a drug delivery site, there are
still certain limitations. It has a relatively small surface area, thus limiting the total dose
that can be delivered. Additionally, although its epithelial barrier is not as restrictive as
other keratinized epithelial barriers, it still severely restricts the delivery of hydrophilic and
macromolecular drugs.
To circumvent the limitations in delivering large hydrophilic molecules in the buccal muôÙ
cosa, several formulation types have been developed. Polymeric mucoadhesive agents
have been widely used in different formulations taking advantage of the swelling properôÙ
ties of certain polymers to adhere the formulation to the mucus layer. Polymers such as e
polyacrylic acid (PAA), polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), sodium carboxymethylcellulose (NaCMC)
and sodium alginate are commonly used for these types of formulations [14].

Microneedles are a promising solution to overcome the challenges of transporting hyôÙ
drophilic molecules across the biological barriers in the buccal mucosa.
While other
formulations rely on prolonged contact with the mucosa to induce passive diffusion, miôÙ
croneedles are able to penetrate the epithelial barrier facilitating the transport of the drug.

Microneedles have sizes ranging from 250 to 1000 ö¥m. This allows them to penetrate
the epithelium layer without reaching the blood vessels or nerves ensuring that no pain
or discomfort is caused to the patient [15]. Typical microneedleôÙbased delivery systems
consist in a small array or patch of multiple drugôÙloaded needles assembled onto a base.

1.3.1
Types of Microneedles
There are several types of microneedles that can be classified by material, mechanism of
action.
Solid microneedles
Solid microneedles the first type of microneedle introduced, typically consisting in sharp
projections of materials such as silicon, steel or titanium [16]. These types of needles
are not loaded with drug. The delivery system consists in a twoôÙstep process, where the
needles act as a tool to prepare the delivery site. The needles first disrupt the epithelial
barrier by forming channels then the drug formulation such as a cream or gel is applied
over the treated area [17]. Although, when introduced, this approach showed promise, its
use decreased overtime as other microneedle technologies that didnãt require a twoôÙstep
process were introduced.
Hollow microneedles
Hollow microneedles are designed to resemble microscopic syringes with a hollow interior
through which a liquid formulation is delivered by diffusion or by pressureôÙdriven flow
[18]. In this approach, the microneedle patch can function as a reservoir for the drug,
thus allowing for treatment that requires high dosage. However, the complexity of the
fabrication process and the potential for clogging during use are the main challenges
[19].
Coated microneedles
This approach involves coating the tip of the microneedles with the therapeutic. Once
the microneedles are inserted into the skin, penetrating the epithelium layer, the drug
formulation dissolves within the tissue [20]. Coated microneedles are typically used for
treatment that requires small and precise dosage, as their main limitation is dose size.[21].
Disolving microneedles
Disolving microneedles are typically fabricated with biodegradable materials, including
saccharides and polymers such as maltose or polyvinyl alcohol respectively [19]. The
drug is encapsulated into the needles during the fabrication process. Upon entering the
tissue and contact with the interstitial fluid, the needles hydrate and start dissolving, thus
progressively releasing the drug. The release rate can be tailored to the therapeutic needs
by modifying the materials [22].
Hydrogel microneedles
Hydrogel microneedles or Hydrogel forming microneedles (HFM) consist in a network
of crosslinked hydrophilic polymers capable of absorbing large amounts of water [23].
Similar to dissolving microneedles, HFM are typically drug loaded during the fabrication
process which will get released upon insertion into the epithelium. HFM technology takes
advantage of the mechanical properties of Hydrogels when dehydrated to penetrate the
epithelium barrier. Upon entering the tissue, the hydrogel will swell with the interstitial
fluid due to the hydrophilic nature of the polymer chains causing the crosslinked network
to expand [24]. The swelling of the polymer network with the interstitial causes the drug
to diffuse passively into the tissue [25].

1.3.2
Hydrogel Microneedles for buccal drug delivery
The unique characteristics of hydrogels, including biocompatibility, high drug loading caôÙ
pacity and the ability to finely customize matrix properties for controlled drug release,
makes hydrogelôÙbased microneedles ideal for developing controlled drug delivery sysôÙ
tems for buccal drug delivery [26].
Hydrogels consist of a threeôÙdimensional network of crosslinked hydrophilic polymeric
chains capable of absorbing large amounts of water or aqueous solutions into their netôÙ
work [27]. Hydrogels can be found in two main states: the gel state and the glass state.
The glassy state is rigid, brittle and provides enough mechanical strength to potentially
disrupt the epithelial barrier in needle form. The glassôÙtoôÙgel transition happens when the
Hydrogel in the glass state comes into contact with an aqueous solution (Interstitial fluid
in the case of hydrogel microneedles) [28]. In this process the polymer chain network
relaxes, and the mesh size increases, making the hydrogel more flexible. This increase
in meshôÙsize facilitates the diffusion of molecules such as therapeutic agents allocated in
the network to diffuse out of the gel [27].
The glassôÙtoôÙgel transition as well as the extent of the capability of the hydrogel to absorb
water is directly correlated with the crossôÙlinking density of the matrix. Higher crossôÙlinking
density results in a tightly packed matrix with smaller pores that can absorb less water,
in turn the mechanical properties tend to be higher. Lower crossôÙlinking densities lead
to higher waterôÙuptake, larger pore size and an overall softer hydrogel with lower meôÙ
chanical strength [29]. This property of hydrogels can be tailored to the needs of the
application by modifying the monomer molecular weight, the polymer concentration in the
aqueous solution and the crosslinking method (photo initiator concentration in the case
of photoôÙcrosslinking) [30][31]. Finding the balance between these properties and obtainôÙ
ing an optimal crosslinking density is crucial for the application of hydrogels in fabricating
microneedles.

Releasable microneedles
The overall size and geometry of the patch is an important parameter in the fabrication of
a microneedle patch. In hollow microneedles, the patch itself can act as a reservoir for
the drug [18]. However, in hydrogel microneedles, the patch only serves as a support for
the needles. Traditional patches can feature a large base for the needles, this can cause
patient compliance issues when applied to buccal drug delivery. A large, nonôÙdegradable
patch can cause discomfort to the patient, difficulty speaking and swallowing. This can
potentially lead to poor adherence to the treatment. A patch with releasable microneedles
can help circumvent this issue while also optimizing drug utilization by confining drug
loading on the needle.
By designing a patch with a soluble backing layer, upon application the patch can dissolve
in the saliva releasing the needles embedded in the mucosa. This approach can greatly
increase patient compliance, eliminates the need for patch removal reducing patient disôÙ
comfort while ensuring localized and controlled administration [38].

There are many different fabrication techniques that have been successfully applied for

2.1
Introduction
The first step in microôÙmoulding is generating a masterôÙmould which will serve as a temôÙ
plate for the final microneedle patch. It is a critical step in the fabrication, as the quality of
the masterôÙmould will directly affect the quality of the subsequent steps. There are sevôÙ

applications and needs, such as lithographyôÙbased techniques, microôÙmilling techniques
and additive techniques such as 3D printing [40].
3D printing offers several advantages over other methods when developing a masterôÙ
mould for a microneedle patch. 3D printing enables for fast design iterations, allowing
for a quick prototyping process while being a oneôÙstep procedure [41]. Additionally, 3D
printing allows for a wide range of design freedom and complexity while maintaining high
resolution in the prints [42].
For this project the BMF (Boston Micro Fabrication) s140 printer was selected. It is a high
precision printer designed for additive manufacturing on the microôÙscale utilizing PôçSL
(Projection Micro Stereolithography). PôçSL consists on projecting a UV light pattern onto
a liquid photopolymer resin curing each layer with high precision [43]. With this technolôÙ
ogy, BMF s140 can reach a resolution of around 10 ôçm, making it ideal for generating
microneedle moulds, which are in the micron range and require sharp tips and edges.
PôçSL was favoured over other 3D printing technologies, such as traditional StereolithogôÙ
raphy (SLA) due to the ability to fabricate more intricate microstructures. PôçSL utilizes a
digital micromirror device to project entire layers at once, achieving resolution not possible
with traditional SLA [44].
Multiple rounds of printing were carried out to optimize the printer settings, familiarize with
the equipment, and refine the overall printing process. These initial trials were crucial for
understanding how to best use the printer and to fineôÙtune parameters such as exposure
time, layer thickness, and resin distribution. However, for the purposes of this section,
only the rounds that yielded significant results, providing valuable insights into necessary
adjustments to the model and parameters will be discussed. These key iterations directly
informed the design changes and process improvements that contributed to the successôÙ
ful outcome described.

PDMS Moulding
After the fabrication of the master mould, a negative mould is fabricated. This mould
serves as an inverse replica of the designed microstructures and allows for the accurate
transfer of these features from the master mould into the final patch. Polydimethylsiloxane
(PDMS) is one of the most used materials to generate negative moulds in microôÙmoulding.
PDMS is an elastomer known for its flexibility, chemical inertness, and ability to replicate
microôÙscale details with high fidelity [46]. SYLGARD 184 Silicone elastomer kit was used
for all the PDMS moulding experiments.
3.1
Initial PDMS Moulding Process
In this project, PDMS was prepared by mixing silicon elastomer with curing agent at 10:1
(w/w) ratio and was mixed thoroughly to ensure homogeneity. To remove air bubbles
generated in the mixing process, the mixture was centrifuged at 1000 rpm briefly. PDMS
was then poured over the masterôÙmould that was placed inside a 6 wellôÙplate. After reôÙ
moving any generated air bubbles in the vacuum desiccator for 5 minutes, the samples
were placed in the oven at 70ô¤C for 2 hours for the curing and solidification of the PDMS.
After the crosslinking process the solid PDMS can be demoulded from the masterôÙmould.
To facilitate the removal of the PDMS moulds from the 6 wellôÙplate, two small holes on
the bottom of the plate were drilled.

Releasable Hydrogel Microneedles
4.1
Introduction
In traditional microôÙmoulding protocols for generating hydrogel microneedles, the hydroôÙ
gel is cast directly into the PDMS mould (after surface treatment) and UV crosslinked.
However, this project aims to obtain a microneedle patch where needles are indepenôÙ
dently attached to the patch/backing layer. It is hypothesized that by utilizing the R2P
equipment, it is possible to crosslink PEGDA microneedles on a soluble PVA film to obôÙ
tain a microneedle patch where all needles are independently releasable. Upon applying
the patch in the buccal mucosa, the PEGDA microneedles swell from the interstitial fluid
starting the drug release process while the PVA patch that is in contact with the saliva
dissolves releasing the microneedles within the mucosa.
In particular, this section of the study aims to optimize the R2P equipment to obtain reôÙ
leasable needles on a PVA patch in a consistent reproducible way. In order to obtain
releasable needles, the R2P rolling cylinder must remove any excess PEGDA from the
surface of the mould, so no PEGDA film remains preventing the needles from acting inôÙ
dependently.
4.2
RollôÙtoôÙplate Equipment
The R2P equipment is a UV/rollerôÙbased nanoôÙimprinter designed to imprint nanostrucôÙ
tures in curable resins. It features a moving platform and a LongLife 395 nm LED UV
lamp within a rolling cylinder. The equipment allows the user to control different variables,
including platform movement speed, cylinder height (distance between cylinder and platôÙ
form), force applied by the cylinder and UV intensity. This equipment is not designed
for the fabrication of hydrogel microôÙstructures, thus the fabrication process of releasable
hydrogel microneedles assisted by the R2P system was a highly iterative process.

Height Optimization
Another parameter affecting the formation and retention of the microneedles in the PVA
patch is the height of the cylinder ( i.e. distance between platform and rolling cylinder).
The distance between the cylinder and the platform must be small enough for the sample
to be in contact with the cylinder while still allowing the sample to pass under the cylinder.
All PDMS moulds fabricated have a thickness of 5 mm. In initial rounds of experimentaôÙ
tion, it was observed that making the cylinder height equal to the PDMS mouldãs thickôÙ
ness, the mould resulted in insufficient contact for the equipment to apply force. This led
to patches with nonôÙreleasable needles where a PEGDA film can be observed between
the PVA patch and the needles. Thus, for the force optimization process, the height was
fixed at 4 mm.
To determine the optimal cylinder height, multiple moulds were tested at 1 mm, 2 mm, 3
mm, and 4 mm. In these experiments the force was fixed at 2, as earlier trials indicated
that despite inconsistencies, this force setting resulted in the highest needle count.
Moulds rolled at 1 mm height offered too much resistance, preventing the sample mould
from passing under the cylinder and UV lamp, resulting in failed runs. In the other hand,
moulds rolled at a 2 mm cylinder height displayed the highest needle retention percentage.
However, this setting also displayed high variability, obtaining needle retention ranging
from 90% to 20%. Moulds rolled at heights 3 and 4 mm displayed comparable needle
retention percentages ranging from 20% to 50%. While these display higher consistency
than 2 mm height moulds, it appears that a cylinder height slightly lower than the mould
thickness allows for the cylinder to apply the necessary force to obtain most of the needles
in the final patch.

Discussion
The RollôÙtoôÙplate used in this study has shown promise in the fabrication of releasable hyôÙ
drogel microneedles, he combination of a moving platform with a UV lamp inside the rolling
cylinder allows for the quick fabrication of microneedle patches and could potentially be
amplified for larger scale operations. However, several limitations affect the consistency
of the resulting patches.
The R2P equipment is designed to operate with UV curable resins for embossing miôÙ
crostructures. These resins have a much faster crosslinking rate than the hydrogel forôÙ
mulation used in this project. This discrepancy is a severe challenge in achieving consisôÙ
tent microneedle formation. With faster curing resins, the equipment is allowed to operate
continuously without the need for to stop the platform underneath the UV lamp. The hyôÙ
drogel used in this study required higher exposure time thus the platform needed to be
stopped with the mould under the UV lamp. This introduces inconsistency in the rolling
process due to the difficulty of halting at the same position every time. Leading to inconôÙ
sistent needle counts. This issue could be circumvented by increasing the photoôÙinitiator
concentration in the hydrogel formulation. However, an increase in PI concentration can
in turn cause toxicity.
Another issue that was observed with the R2P equipment was the uneven force distribuôÙ
tion. This would often cause the PVA film to crimple slightly leading to a loss of contact
between the needles within the mould and the PVA patch. This issue may be directly
caused by the size of the rolling cylinder compared to the size of the mould. A bigger
cylinder would ensure that all the mould is in contact with the PVA film. However, this isôÙ
sue appears to only manifest when the protocol requires the sample to be stopped directly
under the cylinder.
Despite these challenges, the technology remains promising for releasable hydrogel miôÙ
croneedle fabrication. This study serves as a proof of concept, demonstrating that the
RollôÙtoôÙPlate system has the potential to produce functional microneedle patches, even
with the current limitations. With further optimization of the equipment, such as improving
the UV curing process and addressing the force distribution issues, the process could
become more reliable and scalable.

Conclusions
This study aimed to design, fabricate and characterize releasable hydrogel microneedles
for buccal drug delivery through microôÙmoulding coupled with the R2P system. In particuôÙ
lar, the study aimed at optimizing the different steps in the microôÙmoulding methodology.
In the printing stage of the microôÙmoulding process, it was observed that printed moulds
would undergo resin shrinkage during the printing resulting in needles with insufficient
length. This issue was successfully addressed by modifying the needle design, increasôÙ
ing the length of the needles to compensate shrinkage. Additionally, the implementation
of adaptative slicing not only optimized printing times but also contributed to mitigating
shrinkage effects. As a result, highôÙfidelity microneedle patches, ideally suited to serve
as masterôÙmoulds for microôÙmoulding were successfully fabricated.
High fidelity PDMS negative moulds were generated from the masterôÙmoulds, however,
several challenges were encountered in particular in the demoulding process. A key issue
identified in the negative moulding stage was the strong adhesion between the residual
resin in the printed master moulds and the PDMS. This interaction would often lead to
inconsistent demoulding, generating silicone residue on the masterôÙmoulds and often deôÙ
stroying both template and negative mould. This issue was successfully circumvented by
generating an optimized protocol for the PDMS casting. Additionally, an effective way of
generating flat PDMS moulds suitable to operate on the R2P equipment was found.
This study successfully demonstrated the feasibility of utilizing the R2P system to fabricate
releasable hydrogel microneedles patches, while expanding on the findings of previous
master student Christina Richelsen Schulz. Different settings on the R2P were optimized
to obtain independent needles. The results indicated that the force applied, the height
and crosslinking time play an important role in crosslinking the hydrogel within the mould,
while also eliminating any excess hydrogel. Moreover, it was observed that the R2P offers
advantages over a more direct approach like the CNI tool. While the CNI tool provides exôÙ
cellent needle retention, the needles obtained lack the ability to act independently, unlike
those in the R2P system.
While the R2P system showed potential, several limitations were identified with the proôÙ
tocol. The low crosslinking rate of the hydrogel formulation selected forced to operate
in nonôÙideal conditions for the R2P. The need to halt the platform under the cylinder for
UV curing significantly affected reproducibility and prevented the equipment from applyôÙ
ing uniform force across the mould. A different hydrogel formulation, with higher photoôÙ
initiator concentration could potentially circumvent the crosslink rate issue, at the cost of
increased toxicity, which can impact the biocompatibility of the microneedles.A separate
study focused on optimizing the formulation could achieve a balance between crosslinking
efficiency and biocompatibility, ultimately improving the effectiveness of the R2P equipôÙ
ment for obtaining releasable microneedles.
Additionally, an alternative preôÙhumidification approach for obtaining releasable PEGDA
needles on a soluble patch was explored. Initially conceived as a method to salvage
microneedles from failed R2P runs, this technique demonstrated promising results. While
not fully explored in this study, a dedicated strategy focused on demoulding crosslinked
microneedles via preôÙhumidification could offer an efficien scalable alternative for large
scale patch fabrication.

Future Perpestives
Building on the findings of this study, several key areas require further investigation to
enhance the functionality and scalability of the development of releasable microneedle
patches for buccal drug delivery. One crucial aspect is further characterization of the miôÙ
croneedle patched, in particular the mechanical properties and ability to penetrate biologôÙ
ical barriers such as the epithelium. Although penetration studies were originally planned,
it was not possible to carry out due to time constraints. Future research could focus on
evaluating the mechanical properties of the microneedles using artificial or exôÙvivo skin
models to determine their penetration capabilities. Additionally, techniques such as conôÙ
focal microscopy could be applied to assess penetration depth and the behaviour of the
needles within the skin models.
Another area that requires further investigation is the drug release properties of the miôÙ
croneedles. Conducting an inôÙdepth drug release study to determine the kinetics and
efficiency of the delivery system is crucial. Drug release studies were originally planned
for this thesis but could not be carried out due to time limitations. Parallel with this study,
another masterãs student focused exclusively on drug release studies for hydrogel miôÙ
croneedles obtained with similar fabrication strategies. However, future research should
investigate drug release profiles in physiological conditions with different drug models.
This could give insight into the types of therapeutics that are suitable for drug delivery
through hydrogel microneedles obtained with the methodologies described in this study.
Additional research can also be conducted on optimizing the hydrogel formulation, which
can influence both drug release properties and fabrication efficiency using the RollôÙtoôÙ
plate equipment. The slow crosslinking rate of the current formulation proved to be a
limiting factor in the application of the R2P introducing variability. Exploring hydrogel
formulations with higher photoôÙinitiator concentrations could improve efficiency and reôÙ
producibility. These modifications must be balanced to avoid affecting the bioavailability
of the needles.
Further research into refining and optimizing the preôÙhumidification method as an alterôÙ
native approach for the fabrication could also be highly valuable. Developing a strategy
for crosslinking microneedles independently within the mould, followed by the controlled
demoulding through the preôÙhumidification process, could improve consistency and effiôÙ
ciency. Evaluating parameters such as humidity and the drying conditions, along with the
PVA film properties, this method could be optimized to maximize needle retention on the
patch.
