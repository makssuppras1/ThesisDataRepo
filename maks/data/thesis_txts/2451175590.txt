Abstract
The optimal design of trusses, and especially with respect to the inclusion of stability
considerations, has been subject to much attention. Due to the simple numerical formu-
lation associated with the ground structure approach where the bars areas, but not the
nodal positions, are included as design variables, most works on the subject are based on
this. As a consequence of this approach, however, the optimality of the resulting geome-
tries is limited by the mesh density. Furthermore, the proposed problem formulations are
generally non-convex meaning that the advantages of convexity are lacking.
In this thesis, a method to solve the truss optimization problem with local and global
stability constraints and variable nodal positions is proposed. The optimization problem
is formulated for a linear elastic and a rigid plastic model, respectively, where global sta-
bility is introduced as a semideο§¹nite constraint while local stability is introduced in terms
of the Euler buckling load. To permit the use of convex solvers, the problem is formu-
lated as a semideο§¹nite program by using linearized expressions. Hence, the problem is
formulated in terms of incremental changes from an initial state and solved iteratively.
The formulation is derived for 2D truss structures and subsequently expanded to 3D.
Two simple two-bar structures are used to validate the the 2D formulation, and an example
with a 2D truss structure is used to demonstrate the impact of the stability constraints
and variable nodal positions on the solution geometries. An example with a 3D truss
structure is used to investigate the effect of different choices of initial structures on the
optimized material volume, showing that the initial guess can have a signiο§¹cant effect
on the result. Finally, the methodβÄôs eligibility in practical applications is demonstrated
through an example with a simpliο§¹ed model of a deck section of a cable-supported bridge.
Overall, the examples demonstrate the methodβÄôs ability to ο§¹nd more optimal structures
in terms of material volume by allowing changes in the nodal positions. Also, inclusion
of the nodal positions as variables does not signiο§¹cantly increase the computation time,
but practical applications of the method generally require long computation times.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In addition to designing safe structures, one of the main concerns for structural engineers
is to make efο§¹cient use of the available resources. Consequently, the amount of mate-
rial used in a structure in combination with its stiffness and load carrying capacity are
generally decisive factors in the context of preliminary design. By these criteria, truss
structures are attractive candidates for many structural designs, and due to their simple
and transparent nature they have been applied within structural design for centuries.
From the problem of determining an efο§¹cient design, a natural extension is the prob-
lem of determining the most efο§¹cient or optimal design. In the start of the 20th century, the
subject of optimal design of continuous trusses was treated in (Michell, 1904) where the
truss weight was minimized without exceeding the allowable stresses, and later the sim-
ilar, but more practical, problem regarding discretized trusses was discussed in (Prager,
1974). Since then, the numerical optimization of trusses has received a great deal of
attention, and several different formulations have been proposed.
The optimal truss geometry is described by the position of the joints (or nodes) and
the cross section areas of the interconnecting bars. Traditionally, the approach has been
to base the optimization problem on a ο§¹xed ground structure (deο§¹ning the positions of
bars and nodes) with the bar areas as variables such that the optimal nodal positions are
indirectly determined through the choice of bars with non-zero areas. This leads to a
simple numerical formulation based on either material volume or compliance that can
be solved efο§¹ciently with algorithms based on mathematical programming (BendsΟΗe and
Sigmund, 2003).
In particular, the inclusion of stability in the optimization problem (which is essential
to avoid unstable structures) has received much attention due to the difο§¹culties arising
with it as outlined in (Zhou, 1996) and (Rozvany, 1996). Stability problems can generally
be divided into local stability (buckling of individual members) and global stability (buck-
ling of the system as a whole). These are conventionally handled by different means; local

stability is frequently handled in terms Euler buckling (Achtziger, 1999a,b; Tugilimana
et al., 2018) while global stability has e.g. been included as a non-convex semideο§¹nite
constraint (Ben-Tal et al., 2000; KoΥ΅cvara, 2002; Tugilimana et al., 2018). An alternative
approach is to include both types of stability implicitly by use of corotational elements
(Madah and Amir, 2017).
While the ground structure approach has some desirable features, the degree of op-
timality of the resulting structures is limited by the maximum mesh density allowed by
available storage capabilities and/or computation times. For that reason, the problem
of simultaneous optimization of bar areas and nodal positions has been addressed as
well (Achtziger, 2007) and expanded to include local and nodal stability (Descamps and
Filomeno Coelho, 2014).
Common for the works referred to above is that the proposed optimization problem
formulations are all non-convex, i.e. the advantages in terms of computational efο§¹ciency
and a guaranteed global minimum that come with convex formulations are missing.
Finite element limit analysis (FELA), which is based on ο§¹nite element analysis (FEA),
is a convex linear optimization problem assuming rigid plastic material behaviour. The
formulation without stability constraints was introduced with linear programming in (An-
derheggen and KnΟΕpfel, 1972) and has in recent years seen development within optimiza-
tion of the limit load of concrete structures in particular (Herfelt et al., 2016a,b, 2017;
Poulsen and Damkilde, 2000). FELA has also been applied to trusses (Damkilde and
HΟΗyer, 1993), and in a recent MSc thesis (Mikkelsen, 2018) and an ongoing PhD project
by M. Baandrup at DTU, a convex formulation of the truss optimization problem includ-
ing global and local stability is treated. These works do, however, not consider changes
in the nodal positions.
In this thesis, a method to efο§¹ciently solve the truss optimization problem with stabil-
ity constraints and the nodal positions as design variables is proposed. A formulation of
the truss optimization problem is made based on traditional FEA and another one based
on FELA. In both cases, the problem is formulated as a semideο§¹nite program to keep the
advantages of convexity. The general approach is to linearize all non-linear expressions
such that the problem can be solved in an iterative manner.
First, the problem formulations are derived for 2D trusses and validated by applying
the formulations on two simple two-bar structures. The applicability of the formulations
is further demonstrated for a plane truss structure subjected to a single load case. Sub-
sequently, the formulations are expanded to three dimensions, and their applicability is
demonstrated for a 3D truss structure subjected to two load cases. The main purpose of
these examples is to demonstrate the methodβÄôs ability to ο§¹nd structures with lower ma-
terial volumes than for ο§¹xed geometries. Finally, the potential and challenges associated
with the formulation in relation to practical applications are demonstrated through an

Truss optimization
The objective of the truss optimization problem considered in this thesis is to minimize
the material volume while satisfying
βÄΔ equilibrium of the structure,
βÄΔ stresses within (or on) the yield surface,
βÄΔ global stability of the structure, and
βÄΔ local stability of the members.
For truss structures, the material volume can be expressed as a vector product and the
conditions can be formulated as a set of vector or matrix equalities and inequalities, in this
thesis by assuming either linear elasticity or rigid plasticity. This is done in the following
where the linear elastic formulation is based on the traditional FEA formulation, see e.g.
(Cook et al., 2002) or (Zienkiewicz et al., 2013) for a general introduction. The rigid
plastic formulation is based on the FELA formulation as presented in (Olesen et al., 2017).

5.3
Short elements
If the positions of two nodes converge towards the same point, the length of the element
between those nodes will go towards 0. In this case, the constraint on the change in
nodal positions (5.2.2) will effectively keep the nodes ο§¹xed although their optimal (joint)
position might lie elsewhere. Here, this effect is handled by removing elements with
lengths smaller than a given threshold. Since signiο§¹cant changes in the structure could
cause the problem to become infeasible, the threshold should be set low enough to avoid
this. The appropriate value of the threshold does, however, depend on the topology.
5.4
Obtaining convergence
Since the minimum of a linear function is negative inο§¹nity, the objective function will
cause the variables to seek towards the constraint limits in each iteration. In the case of

the nodal positions are included as variables. As a consequence, the solution will oscillate
around the true minimum with a precision corresponding to the magnitude of the variable
constraints as illustrated in Figure 5.1.

CHAPTER 11
Conclusion
A formulation of the truss optimization problem with constraints on global and local sta-
bility and variable nodal positions has been proposed based on a linear elastic (FEA) and
a rigid plastic (FELA) model, respectively. Global and local stability were handled with
a semideο§¹nite constraint on the system stiffness and a constraint on the Euler buckling
load, respectively. By means of linearization the problem was formulated as an SDP, al-
lowing the use of efο§¹cient convex solvers (here: Mosek). The formulation was validated
by comparing the results from tests on simple 2D structures with those obtained from
analytical expressions.
Through 2D and 3D examples, the method has been demonstrated to lead to reduc-
tions in material volume compared to a formulation with ο§¹xed nodal positions. For a 2D
structure, the effect of the stability constraints and variable nodal positions were demon-
strated by gradual introduction. The two formulations were shown to converge towards
relatively similar material volumes within 100 iterations, although the convergence speed
and ο§¹nal geometries were found to be sensitive towards changes in the variable con-
straints.
For a 3D structure, the effect of the choice of the initial geometry on the reduction
in material volume was demonstrated. Generally, a step-wise approach where the level
of non-convexity is gradually increased proved to give better results than simply using
the optimal ο§¹xed-geometry structure as the initial structure. However, the use of a scaled
linear elastic solution as the initial structure proved in multiple cases to lead to geometries
with either signiο§¹cantly lower or slightly higher material volumes than that of a structure
with ο§¹xed nodal positions. This effect seems to be largely dependent on the load case and
to be smaller when multiple load cases are considered. The computation time was shown
to increase super-linearly with respect to the problem size and the plastic formulation was
generally seen to lead to shorter computation times than the elastic formulation.
Finally, an example with a simpliο§¹ed model of a deck section of a cable-supported
77
==PAGE:90==
78
CHAPTER 11. CONCLUSION
bridge with constraints on symmetry and domain boundaries was used to demonstrate the
potential and challenges associated with the use of the method in practical applications.
Material optimization with the plastic formulation showed that an additional material re-
duction of approx. 4% could be obtained by including the nodal positions as variables
compared to a model with ο§¹xed nodal positions. The computation time for 50 iterations
was 46 hours for ο§¹xed nodal positions and 47 hours for variable nodal positions (using
20 CPUβÄôs in parallel). Hence, practical applications of the method require long compu-
tation times, but they are not signiο§¹cantly increased by including the nodal positions as
variables.
Overall, the examples demonstrate that the inclusion of the nodal positions as vari-
ables can lead to more optimal designs than for ο§¹xed nodal positions. However, the
method does face challenges in terms of the computation time required for practical ap-
plications as well as the fact that only a local minimum, which may lie relatively far from
the global minimum, is guaranteed.
11.1
Further work
Due to the challenges arising with the method proposed in this thesis, it does open up for
further development. One of the main challenges is that, while the linearization approach
does lead to a locally convex problem, the overall problem is still non-convex. Thus,
the found minimum can be a local one that, as demonstrated in the examples, may lie
relatively far from the global minimum. Since none of the investigated approaches were
seen to consistently lead the lowest material volume, a method to arrive at the global
minimum is desirable. Given that the largest source of non-convexity in the problem
presumably is the concave constraint on local stability, a possible approach is to extent
the formulation to beam elements such that local stability can be treated in terms of global
stability by inclusion of one or more nodes between the element ends.
Another main challenge is the computation times for large structures. Since the bulk
of the computation time can be attributed to the global stability constraint (the number
of equations and slack variable entries depends quadratically on the number of unsup-
ported degrees of freedom), signiο§¹cant improvements in terms of computation times can
presumably be obtained by solving the dual problem instead of the primal problem.
Finally, since numerical issues rendered the elastic formulation incapable of deliver-
ing a result in Chapter 10, the cause of and solution to these issues should be investigated
further.
