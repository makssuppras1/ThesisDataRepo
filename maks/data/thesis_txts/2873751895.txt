Abstract
Robust 3D object detection represents a critical capability for autonomous driving sysôÙ
tems, requiring effective fusion of complementary sensor modalities to achieve reliable
performance across diverse environmental conditions. This thesis addresses the chalôÙ
lenge of integrating 4D imaging radar and camera data for 3D object detection through
a novel selfôÙsupervised preôÙtraining approach that reduces reliance on manually annoôÙ
tated training data. We present a MultiMAEôÙbased framework that extends Masked AuôÙ
toencoder principles to handle heterogeneous multimodal data. The approach tokenizes
camera images using Vision Transformer and 4D radar point clouds through a PointMAEôÙ
inspired architecture optimized for sparse radar measurements. CrossôÙmodal fusion is
achieved through a correspondence loss that enforces geometric consistency between
spatially aligned tokens from different modalities during masked reconstruction. For 3D
object detection, we develop three progressive architectures: a theoretical optimal fusion
baseline, a twoôÙstream enhancement addressing imperfect fusion, and a PETRôÙinspired
approach incorporating explicit depth reasoning. The work contributes novel insights into
selfôÙsupervised multimodal learning for autonomous perception, providing a foundation
for future research in radarôÙcamera fusion systems that address the annotation scarcity
challenge, while effectively handling the sparse and irregular characteristics of 4D radar
measurements. Implementation code is available on SAVAãs Github.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Autonomous driving systems represent one of the most challenging applications in modôÙ
ern computer vision and robotics, requiring robust perception capabilities that can operôÙ
ate reliably across diverse environmental conditions, while being operated in realôÙtime.
The fundamental task of 3D object detection is to accurately identify and localize obôÙ
jects in threeôÙdimensional space, forming the backbone of safe autonomous navigation.
CameraôÙbased perception systems offer rich visual information with detailed texture and
semantic content at relatively low cost. However, cameras inherently capture only 2D
projections of 3D scenes, losing critical depth information essential for accurate spatial
reasoning. Traditional approaches to recover depth information, such as stereo vision or
monocular depth estimation networks, suffer from precision limitations and computational
overhead that can compromise realôÙtime performance requirements. While significant
progress has been achieved using LiDARôÙbased systems, the high cost and limitations
in adverse weather conditions have motivated exploration of alternative sensor modaliôÙ
ties. 4D imaging radar technology has emerged as a compelling remedy, offering several
key advantages for autonomous driving applications. Unlike traditional rotating radars,
4D imaging radars provide enhanced spatial resolution while maintaining the inherent roôÙ
bustness of radio frequency sensors to adverse weather conditions, including fog, rain,
and dust. Most importantly, 4D radars directly measure Doppler velocity and provide
precise distance information through timeôÙofôÙflight principles, addressing the fundamental
depth limitation of cameraôÙonly systems. The collaboration of AgriRobot and the SAVA
(Safety in Autonomous Vehicles in Agriculture) project at DTU aim to tackle this challenge
by leveraging stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart selfôÙsupervised learning methods given the scarcity of data
inherent in the field.
1.2
Problem Statement
Since 4D radar technology has only recently become viable for autonomous driving, exôÙ
isting fusion methods for 3D object detection are predominantly designed for dense LiôÙ
DAR data and may not optimally handle the extreme sparsity typical of radar measureôÙ
ments. Fusion methods, specifically designed for cameraôÙradar fusion, are supervised
approaches requiring extensive annotation and handôÙcrafted architectures tailored to speôÙ
cific tasks. Despite the complementary nature of camera and 4D radar, the effective fusion
of these heterogeneous data sources remains a challenge.
1.3
Research Directions
This thesis addresses the challenge of radarôÙcamera fusion for 3D object detection through
a selfôÙsupervised learning approach to mitigate reliance on annotated training data while
effectively handling the heterogeneous data sources. The primary research objectives
are:
1. SelfôÙSupervised MultiôÙmodal Fusion: The research explores a preôÙtraining frameôÙ
work based on Masked Autoencoder (MAE) principles that can learn robust crossôÙ
modal representations from radar and camera data without requiring manual annoôÙ
tations. This approach aims to address the limited data problem inherent in superôÙ
vised 4D radar datasets.

Literature review
To address these research directions, it is essential to understand the current state of
multiôÙmodal fusion techniques and selfôÙsupervised learning approaches in 3D object deôÙ
tection. This chapter provides a theoretical foundation for the work conducted in this
thesis, including sensor information and characteristics with various implementations for

2.1
Sensors
2.1.1
Camera
RGB cameras provide rich visual information, which is essential for scene understandôÙ
ing and object detection, offering high spatial resolution and detailed texture information.
Given the extensive documentation of their methodological strengths in the existing literôÙ
ature, a thorough review of these advantages falls outside the scope of this analysis. One
aspect that has to be pointed out is that monocular cameras capture only 2D projections
of 3D scenes, losing critical depth information, necessitating sensor fusion approaches
for robust 3D object detection.
2.1.2
Radar
RADAR (RAdio Detection And Ranging) calculates distance and doppler velocity by transôÙ
mitting radio waves. In autonomous driving, the wavelength of the transmitted radiowaves
is around 1ôÙ10 mm, making it robust against smaller, comparable sized objects such as
rain, fog, and dust. 4D radar is usually a MultipleôÙInput MultipleôÙOutput (MIMO) having
multiple transmitters and antennae to simultaneously transmit and reveive multiple radio
signals. Although having much higher spatial resolution compared to the rotating radar,
it lacks in coverage. The working principle is sound, based on the Time of Flight principle
the distance of the object from which the wave reflected can be calculated. Also, given
the frequency shift of the outgoing and incoming radio wave the relative Doppler velocity
can be calculated. This additional component of radars are alone worth investigating,
raising excellent indicators for 3D object detection in dynamic scenarios. It is worth menôÙ
tioning that only the radial component of the velocity is known by this calculation and not
the tangential meaning the true velocities of objects are not known. Another key charôÙ
acteristic of radars are their RCS (Radar Cross Section) indicating how well the target
reflects radio signals. Low RCS values represent low reflective properties and High RCS
values the contrary. The output of the radar sensor through ADC, Fourier transforms and
other signal processing methods that will not be discussed here, is a pointcloud with 3
spatial coordinates and many other components depending on the sensor (RCS, Doppler
velocity etc.).

2.3
Object detection
Object detection represents one of the most fundamental challenges in computer vision,
with particular significance for autonomous systems where accurate, realôÙtime perception
is safetyôÙcritical.
2.3.1
CameraôÙbased detectors
CameraôÙbased object detection methods from classical approaches to modern transformerôÙ
based architectures have proven highly successful and accurate. Although, SVMs, CNNs,
RôÙCNN variants and the YOLO family have been widely used for 2D object detection given
the nature of their extracted features, the current trend has been shifting towards extendôÙ
ing detection into three dimensions putting transformerôÙbased approaches into spotlight
for reasoning with attention.
Classical methods
While classical methods are rarely used in the stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart, their contribution to comôÙ
puter vision is undoubtedly significant.
Support Vector Machines
The foundation of modern object detection can be traced
back to classical machine learning approaches, particularly Support Vector Machine (SVM)
based methods that dominated the field before deep learning came into play.
The classical SVMôÙbased detection pipeline operates through a carefully engineered feaôÙ
ture extraction and classification framework. The core principle is the Histogram of OriôÙ
ented Gradients (HOG) feature descriptor which captures local gradient information through
spatial binning of orientation histograms. The main principle of the HOG descriptor is to
calculate horizontal and vertical gradients for each pixel then grouped together into gradiôÙ
ent histograms. From the generated features SVMs are used to fit the optimal hyperplane
that achieves the minimum distance to data points (support vectors). With applying a slidôÙ
ing window approach and calculating the HOG+SVM bounding boxes can be predicted.
After applying NMS (NonôÙMaximum Suppression) the redundant boxes can be eliminated.
Even though SVMs showed promising results in the early object detection era, it exhibited
huge limitations as the exhaustive sliding window approach required substantial compuôÙ
tational resources.

Limitations and Evolution Beyond Classical Approaches
While classical methods
like SVMs with HOG features established the foundation for object detection, their fundaôÙ
mental limitations become critically apparent when extending to 3D detection tasks, parôÙ
ticularly in multimodal radarôÙcamera scenarios. Classical methods were designed for 2D
image analysis and lack inherent mechanisms for processing threeôÙdimensional spatial
information [17]. SVMs with HOG features operate on gradientôÙbased texture descriptors
that capture local appearance patterns but provide no depth understanding [18]. This
limitation becomes prohibitive for autonomous driving applications where accurate 3D loôÙ
calization is safetyôÙcritical [19]. The exhaustive sliding window approach employed by
classical detectors creates fundamental scalability issues for 3D detection. As demonôÙ
strated by Dalal and Triggs [17], the computational complexity increases exponentially
when extending from 2D to 3D search spaces. For autonomous driving scenarios reôÙ
quiring realôÙtime performance, this computational burden becomes infeasible [20]. The
reliance on manually engineered features (HOG, SIFT, SURF) prevents classical methôÙ
ods from learning optimal representations for complex 3D scenarios [21].
Convolutional networks
The rise of deep learning has revolutionized computer vision
especially in object detection tasks. Deep learningôÙbased object detection methods can
be categorized into twoôÙstage and oneôÙstage detectors, each with their architectural adôÙ
vantages. With the introduction of transformers, object detection challenges were tackled
even better than convolutional methods. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) repôÙ
resent a fundamental paradigm shift in computer vision, moving from traditional handôÙ
crafted feature extraction methods to learned hierarchical representations. A ConvoluôÙ
tional Neural Network is a deep learning architecture specifically designed to process
gridôÙlike data, such as images, by exploiting spatial relationships through localized feaôÙ
ture extraction. Unlike traditional fullyôÙconnected networks, CNNs use convolution operaôÙ
tions to detect local patterns and features across an input image through learnable filters
(kernels). Through multiple convolution layers, pooling layers and activation functions the
network can learn low to high level features such as edges, corners, shapes, scenes. This
hierarchical approach mimics human vision where simple features are combined to form
increasingly complex representations.

Despite the impact of CNNs on 2D object detection, several fundamental limitations neôÙ
cessitated the need for transformerôÙbased architectures for 3D multimodal detection tasks.

TwoôÙstage detectors break down detection into two distinct stages: region proposal genôÙ

potential areas where objects of interest might be present in the image and the second
stage takes these proposed regions and classifies objects within those regions.

SelfôÙsupervised learning was introduced in deep learning with the aid to the fieldãs emergôÙ

4.1
Initial Approaches for Point Cloud Reconstruction
4.1.1
PointNet++ encoding and MLP decoding
The first approach considered PointNet++ [36] for hierarchical point cloud feature extracôÙ
tion, implementing a threeôÙstage set abstraction hierarchy. The architecture employed
progressively coarser sampling, using ball query neighborhoods of increasing radius.
Each set abstraction layer consisted of MLPs. The tokenization process involved several

key steps: point cloud coordinates were transformed to channelôÙfirst format for PointNet++
processing, hierarchical features were extracted through three set abstraction layers with
progressively reduced point counts, and tokens were generated by sampling from the
highestôÙlevel features (512ôÙdimensional) and projecting to the transformer token dimenôÙ
sion. After applying masking the masked tokens then were processed through the main
transformer.
For reconstruction, a simple multiôÙlayer perceptron decoder was implemented consisting
of multiple MLP blocks. The decoder projected points through the set of MLPs to generate
a fixed number of points (1024). This implementation was based on the number of points
observed through the analysis of the dataset and made point generation much simpler.
Initial Insights and Discussion
While this output adapter successfully captured the overall structural characteristics of
radar point clouds, it failed to achieve precise pointôÙlevel reconstruction. The limitations
observed indicated that the reconstruction quality stemmed from the simplicity of the outôÙ
put adapter.
Although multiple attempts were made to address these limitations, experimenting with difôÙ
ferent decoders from the literature, such as the FoldingNet [53] decoder and TransformerôÙ
based decoders, no significant progress was achieved. Ultimately, the encoding strategy
was assumed to be the main culprit, leading to the exploration of simpler, proven encoding
methods.
4.1.2
GridôÙbased Tokenization
A shift toward gridôÙbased methods was motivated by the desire to create structured, fixedôÙ
size representations similar to ViT image patches. PointPillarôÙinspired [37] tokenization
was implemented, which discretized the 3D space into regular voxels and aggregated
point features within each voxel.
Insights and discussion
While PointPillar achieved much faster encoding, reconstruction quality still remained unôÙ
satisfactory, indicating a fundamental misconception how feature extractors should be
incorporated in the MAE framework.
During this phase, a critical insight emerged: the tokens generated solely through PointNetôÙ
style [30] aggregation lacked explicit positional embeddings, making it difficult for the
transformer to develop spatial awareness. The transformer struggled to understand the
geometric relationships between different spatial regions without explicit positional conôÙ
text.
4.1.3
Breakthrough: PointMAEôÙinspired Architecture
The final breakthrough came with the adoption of PointMAE [52] principles, which fundaôÙ
mentally shifted the architectural philosophy. Rather than relying solely on hierarchical
feature extractors to capture spatial relationships, this approach explicitly provided posiôÙ
tional information to the transformer while using lightweight PointNet encoders for basic
feature extraction.
Key Insights
While feature extraction is very powerful for point cloud modalities, the sparsity of radar
points makes it highly challenging. Simple methods proved efficient for reconstruction,
pushing the transformer itself to find relationships between features. The exploration of
encoding methods indicated the significance of positional embeddings for transformerôÙ
based architectures.

Final Point Cloud Tokenization method
The radar point cloud data is tokenized using a PointMAEôÙinspired architecture that proôÙ
cesses spatial patches as sets. The tokenization process involves several key steps:
1. Patch Generation: Farthest Point Sampling (FPS) selects patch centroids from the
point cloud, followed by KôÙNearest Neighbors (KNN) to identify the nearest points
for each centroid.
2. PointôÙwise Feature Extraction: A lightweight PointNet architecture processes inôÙ
dividual points within each patch. Better feature extractors do exist in the literature
(See Section 2.3.2), but the transformer encoder is assumed to do the heavyôÙlifting.
The pointôÙwise MLP consists of three linear layers (input channels ã64 ã128 ã
256 dimensions) with ReLU activations, transforming raw point features into higherôÙ
dimensional representations.
3. PatchôÙlevel Aggregation: Max pooling aggregates pointôÙwise features within each
patch, followed by a patchôÙlevel MLP (256 ã512 ãtoken dimension) that produces
the final patch embeddings.
4. Normalization: Points within each patch are normalized relative to the patch cenôÙ
troid and scaled by standard deviation to improve convergence stability.
5. Positional Encoding: The extracted tokens are positionally embed with 3D sinuôÙ
soidal PEs similar to the image features. While learnable PEs can be used , since
points have specific geometric structure fixed PEs have slightly better performance
than learnable PEs (see Section 4.3.1).

4.2.2
Masking Strategy
The masking approach follows MultiMAE principles with Dirichlet distributionôÙbased token
allocation between modalities. For both image and point cloud modalities, an aggressive
masking ratio of 80% was applied, consistent with recommendations from both MultiMAE
[51] and PointMAE [52] literature.
The masking information (which tokens were visible for the encoder) is preserved for
subsequent loss calculation, as Masked Autoencoding requires loss computation only on
tokens that were unseen by the encoder during training.
4.2.3
Transformer Encoding
The transformer encoder maintains consistency with the SAVA projectsãs design specôÙ
ifications. The architecture employs 12 transformer layers with 384ôÙdimensional token
representations and 6 attention heads. The MLP expansion ratio is set to 4.0, creatôÙ
ing feedôÙforward networks with 1536 hidden dimensions. Layer normalization is applied
before attention operations (preôÙnorm architecture), with dropout rates of 0.1 for both atôÙ
tention and feedôÙforward components. GELU activation functions are used throughout
the network.
Masked tokens from both modalities are concatenated to form a unified token sequence
fed into the transformer encoder. A single global token facilitates crossôÙmodal information
transfer during the encoding process.

Point cloud Decoder
Point cloud reconstruction is based on an inverse PointNet architecture, mirroring the

Image decoder

construction. The decoder consists of three main components:
1. Token Projection: Encoded tokens are projected from encoder dimension to deôÙ

A systematic experiment was conducted to determine optimal hyperparameters for the

object distance: proximate objects may be represented by dozens of measurement points,

applications.
The theoretical hypothesis driving this investigation was that increasing the number of
patch centroids would improve reconstruction performance by providing finer spatial granôÙ

lost in larger patches.

Query Initialization and 3D Reference Points
The detection framework initializes learnable object query embeddings through standard
embedding layers with Xavier uniform initialization. Each of the learnable object queries

within the defined scene boundaries of [ôÙ20, ôÙ2, 0] to [20, 5, 100] meters in x, y, z directions,

Reference point generation creates a regular 3D grid with uniform distribution across the
operational space, providing initial spatial priors for object localization.
Reference Point Refinement
Between consecutive decoder layers, 3D reference points undergo iterative refinement
through dedicated linear heads that predict coordinate offsets. Refined reference points
are clamped to valid scene boundaries and used to generate updated positional embedôÙ

MultiôÙScale Prediction and Auxiliary Supervision
Each decoder layer incorporates independent classification and bounding box regression
heads, facilitating intermediate supervision during training. Classification heads produce
logits for 14 object classes plus background, while regression heads predict 7ôÙdimensional
3D bounding boxes including center coordinates (x, y, z), dimensions (width, height,
length) and orientation angle.
The architecture outputs both primary predictions from the final decoder layer and auxiliary
predictions from all intermediate layers, enabling deep supervision and improved gradient
flow throughout the training process.
5.3.2
Loss Formulation and Training Objectives
All detection variants employ standard DETR loss formulation requiring bipartite matching
between predictions and ground truth objects, followed by classification and regression
loss computation on matched pairs.
Hungarian Algorithm Matching
The Hungarian algorithm establishes optimal oneôÙtoôÙone correspondence between preôÙ
dictions and ground truth by minimizing total matching cost. The cost function combines
classification cost (negative logôÙprobability of predicted class) and regression cost (L1
distance between predicted and ground truth bounding box centers). Cost weighting emôÙ
ploys 2x for classification and 5x for regression, prioritizing geometric accuracy during
assignment.
Classification Loss
Classification loss utilizes focal loss with öÝ = 0.25 and ö° = 2.0 to address inherent class
imbalance where background predictions significantly outnumber object detections.
Bounding Box Regression Loss
The 7ôÙdimensional regression loss decomposes into geometrically meaningful compoôÙ
nents with tailored weighting. Center coordinates receive 10û weighting through smooth
L1 loss, object dimensions use 2û weighting with logarithmic scaling for size invariance,
and orientation employs sineôÙcosine decomposition with unit weighting to handle angle
discontinuities. The complete regression loss combines all components ensuring balôÙ
anced optimization across spatial position, dimensions, and orientation parameters.

Conclusion
6.1
Research Contributions
This thesis addressed the challenge of 3D object detection using 4D radar and camôÙ
era fusion through a novel selfôÙsupervised learning approach. We successfully extended
the MultiMAE framework to accommodate 4D radar point clouds alongside camera imôÙ
ages. Through systematic experimentation, we developed a PointMAEôÙinspired tokenizaôÙ
tion strategy specifically adapted for sparse radar data. The results demonstrated that
selfôÙsupervised learning can generate meaningful multimodal representations for radarôÙ
camera data, addressing the annotation scarcity problem. However, the fusion quality
achieved was imperfect, necessitating enhanced fusion methods.
We systematically developed three detection architectures of increasing complexity: a
baseline assuming perfect fusion, a twoôÙstream approach addressing fusion imperfecôÙ
tions, and a PETRôÙinspired architecture incorporating explicit depth reasoning. We esôÙ
tablished that transformerôÙbased detection architectures can effectively leverage selfôÙ
supervised multimodal representations, though precise 3D spatial reasoning remains chalôÙ
lenging for radarôÙcamera combinations. The research highlighted the potential of the apôÙ
proach while pinpointing specific areas requiring further investigation.
6.2
Limitations
Due to the scope and timeline constraints of this masterãs thesis project, several initially
planned investigations could not be completed within the available timeframe. The focus
was prioritized on developing and validating the core MultiMAE fusion framework rather
than exploring precise point cloud filtering strategies that could improve data quality for
fusion.
The sophisticated nature of transformerôÙbased multimodal architectures presents sigôÙ
nificant complexity in terms of optimal configuration and fineôÙtuning. Given the project
timeline and the extensive hyperparameter fineôÙtuning required for transformer systems,
comprehensive architectural optimization remains an area for future investigation. The
current implementation provides a foundation for understanding the potential of these apôÙ
proaches while acknowledging that with deeper architectural exploration improved perôÙ
formance could be achieved.
