Abstract 
10 District Heating connected buildings in Denmark which included 1 single-family home, 6 row 
houses and 3 apartment complexes having low return temperatures from their substations 
were investigated. During the visit, the substation components were identified, and their set 
points were noted in a registration form. The residents and the building managers were 
interviewed and asked questions about building renovations, thermal comfort and their heating 
behavior.  
Representations of the substations were prepared using standard P&ID symbols on Microsoft 
Visio. The substation description with the components, their set points and their corresponding 
parameter values has been provided. A graphical representation of the substations’ measured 
monthly average supply and return temperature has been provided.  
Three building sites, number 1, 2 and 3 were selected and design heat loss calculations have 
been performed according to DS 418 and DHW calculations according to DS 439 standards. The 
design heat loss calculations have been compared with the SH system power to determine 
whether the SH system can satisfy design heat demand. Similar comparison has been made for 
DHW demand and the power of the storage tank or instantaneous HEX. From the heat loss 
calculations, it was determined that the radiators were oversized, and this promotes cooling of 
the water.  
After analysis it was determined that all variations of substations can provide low return 
temperatures when controls and settings of the components are set according to 
recommendations and good heating practices are followed. For the single-family home, the row 
houses and the apartment building, the return temperatures were 23 C, between 23 C to 28 
C and between 31 C to 39 C respectively. These low return temperatures have been 
attributed to setting TRVs between 2 and 4 and avoiding maximum setting, setting return 
thermostats between 1-3 on the scale, avoiding bypass connections for row houses, having a 
return line radiator/ floor heating for bathroom comfort to mitigate effects of bypass flows, 
installing pre-settings and setting them between 3-5 in apartments for better hydraulic balance 
and operation.

1. Background and literature review  
District heating is a method of generating heat in a central location as hot water and 
distributing it with a network of pipes to the demand centers like households, commercial 
buildings etc. which utilize the heat for space heating and preparation of domestic hot water. 
District heating technology in Denmark has seen various transformation and development since 
the establishment of the first waste to heat plant in Frederiksberg in 1903 [1]. In the early 
stages of development, steam was used as heat carrier in concrete pipes, marking the first-
generation DH.  
DH technology saw major expansion and growth in Denmark after the oil crisis of 1973. 
Denmark and neighboring countries were impacted as 90% of the energy consumption was 
derived from imported oil due to lack of homegrown natural resources [2]. This crisis led to the 
central government cracking down on the use of imported oil and finally established the first 
long-term energy policy which stressed the importance of fuel independence and 
improvements in energy efficiency in Denmark [3][4]. 
The household sector, responsible for 1/3rd of the final energy consumption [5] constituted a 
major component in achieving energy efficiency and saw upgrades in heating supply and 
consumption methods. Plans to expand and improve district heating systems were laid out, and 
over the course of four decades the technology has developed and has become a corner stone 
of the Danish energy system. 
The current DH industry in Denmark is well developed, high quality and cost effective. DH 
networks have become extensive, connecting over 63% of private homes [6]. It can be divided 
into six large DH areas and 400 small and medium areas generating around 122 PJ of heat 
annually [6]. 50% of this heat is generated from renewable sources and another 8% is from 
biomass. The rest of the heat generation is from oil, natural gas and coal [6] with 68% of heat 
being generated in cogeneration plants.  
Now, with international attention being given to the effects of global warming, the focus is 
shifting towards more efficient and sustainable energy systems having minimum greenhouse 
emissions. The EU commission has set an ambitious target of achieving near zero emissions 
(compared to 1990 levels) by the year 2050 [7] to ensure a healthy environment for habitation. 
The district heating sector has an important role to play in order to ensure the target is met. It 
has resulted in the DH systems across Europe slowly phasing out coal and other fossil fuels and 
integrating locally sourced renewable resources to generate heat.  
The integration of renewable sources in the DH grid is feasible due to the transition into fourth 
generation DH, where the network operation temperatures are dropping to an estimated 50C 
==PAGE:9==
2 
 
supply and 25 C return. The 4GDH has known benefits such as reduced distribution losses, 
improved efficiency, smaller prefabricated pipe dimensions and the ability to include low 
energy sources such as heat pumps, solar and geothermal heat in the heat generation mix, 
making the system more flexible and sustainable [8].  
The consumers of DH are also undertaking building improvement projects that promote energy 
savings and bring down the final energy demand. New buildings from the next decade, under 
law, are to be built as zero energy buildings ensuring they have minimum energy demands 
reducing the heating demands of future building stock [9] which ensures the systems are 
compatible with low temperature DH. However, the challenge for the implementation of 4GDH 
is to operate the existing building stock, where the heating systems were designed for high 
temperatures, with low temperatures. Several studies of such buildings show that these 
buildings will not face thermal discomfort problems due to reduced temperatures [10][11][12]. 
Typically, the SH systems are designed based on extremely low outdoor temperatures and no 
heat gains leading to oversized systems for most of the heating season. Hence, low 
temperatures can be used to operate the SH systems and ensure the required comfort [11]. 
The first step in the transition towards LTDH is achieving low return temperatures. While DH 
operators control the supply temperatures in the networks, the return temperature depends 
on the end-users and how their heating systems are operated and controlled. Having low return 
temperatures has advantages such as reduced pump and pipe sizes, increased capacity of DH 
network, reduced insulation costs, flue gas condensation and increased efficiency [13]. Reduced 
return temperatures not only improve efficiencies but also have economic benefits which was 
documented in [14], where the Boras district heating generation system estimated the annual 
value of lower temperature to be 0.3 million SEK (Swedish Krona) per C resulting in high 
annual savings. Another study [15] comparing three distribution temperature levels of low 
(55/25 C), ultra-low electric boosting (45/25 C) and ultra-low heat pump boosting (35/20 C) 
technology reported significant operational savings. Though the economic benefits of reduced 
return temperatures vary with different networks, a study of 27 networks in Sweden found that 
typically 0.15 Euro/MWhC savings can be obtained [16].   
As seen from the aforementioned studies, it is very advantageous to have low return 
temperatures in the DH network which depend mainly on individual substation design, 
operation and heating practices. Hence, to have high cooling of supply DH water, it is important 
to follow principles of well documented substations having effective design and operation. 
However, the existing knowledge is limited when it comes to substations that have 
documented consistent low return temperatures. One study which documented seven low 
temperature DH networks recorded the return temperature variations between 32 C and 44 C 
[17]. Other accounts of low temperature district heating having supply temperatures of 50-55 
==PAGE:10==
3 
 
C supplied to groups of detached houses and low energy terraced houses in Denmark also 
show resulting return temperatures above 30 C [18]. A simulation case study [19] records 
return temperatures in the range of 17 C to 21 C however, this is only a result of changing the 
network pipes to three-pipe system, installing apartment substations for multi-family buildings 
and mounting of HEXs with high thermal lengths which require significant changes to existing 
systems. Records of existing DH systems having annual average return temperatures below 30 
C are lacking and so is the documentation of factors responsible for such reduced 
temperatures [19].  
This report presents the results of a study focused on the operations of heating systems in 
existing buildings connected to DH networks. In collaboration with local DH companies in 
Copenhagen, several existing buildings were investigated. It was documented that return 
temperatures below 30 C can be achieved in well operated heating systems.

2. Methods 
In this section, the investigation process of 10 well-functioning heating systems has been 
described and the standard Piping and Instrumentation diagram symbols used to prepare the 
schematics of these sites have been mentioned. The procedure for design heat loss calculations 
according to DS 418 standard performed for three sites from the set has been described. The 
DHW calculations procedure according to DS 439 has also been described.    
2.1 Site visits 
The first step of the study was to visit the single-family homes, row houses and apartment 
buildings having a well-functioning heating system installed. The locations were determined by 
contacting some of the District Heating companies of Copenhagen and obtaining information 
about of the substations having low return temperatures in their network. The homeowners 
having these substations were then contacted and were requested to participate in the study. A 
total of 10 homes and apartment buildings agreed to participate and were consequently visited 
and inspected from the end of January to late February 2019. Due to EU regulations, the 
locations and details of the participants will not be disclosed in the report and they will be 
simply referred to by a number.     
During the visits, a procedure was followed which began with inspecting the substation, usually 
located in the basement. A visual inspection was made to determine all the components 
installed in the substation. A rough diagram was prepared which represents these components. 
Pictures of the substation were also taken which include the entire system, individual 
components, energy labels, product specifications and information labels. The settings of 
control valves and pumps were also noted, and the pipe dimensions and insulation were 
measured. If balancing valves were present, they were also inspected for type and setting. 
Details about weather compensation were also noted if applicable. 
The space heating systems were also inspected, and the type of system installed, single pipe, 
two pipe or reversed return pipe was noted. If radiators were installed, the radiator type and 
the dimensions were measured and recorded as well as the position of the TRVs and their 
settings. Similar steps were performed for under floor heating system installations.  
Apart from the substation and the SH system, the windows were also inspected. The layers and 
glazing were noted. Any renovation performed by the residents on the house was also 
recorded. 
All this information was recorded in a registration form, which has been attached in the 
Appendix. As part of the survey, a short interview was conducted with all the residents about 
their thermal comfort and if they had any complaints about the heating system. Questions were

also asked about their behavior with respect to temperature preferences in the house, night set 
back etc.  
Finally, the data regarding the heat consumption and the monthly average supply and return 
temperatures from the substations was obtained from the District Heating companies and have 
been represented graphically. In some cases, the data was incomplete due to issues with 
measuring instruments and where relevant it has been highlighted.

2.4 Heat loss Calculation 
Different building types were inspected in this project: single family houses, row houses and 
apartment blocks. Among all sites inspected, three buildings, Building site 1 (a single family 
house), Building site 2 (a row house) and Building site 3 (an apartment block), were selected as 
references cases and for these sites, detailed heat loss calculations were performed considering 
specific building fabric. Heat losses occur due to temperature differences between the indoor 
and outdoor environment, creating a need for space heating when the outdoor environment is 
colder. The space heating is predominantly required during cold winter period, starting from 
early autumn and up to late spring. 
The calculations of heat losses from buildings typically follow national standards and guidelines. 
In Denmark, DS 418 is the standard document followed for performing heat loss calculations 
which considers the Danish climatic conditions and building and energy codes. While using this 
standard, the heating systems are designed for worst case scenarios to ensure the heating 
demand is met even for the coldest winter spells. Hence the systems are said to be 
dimensioned for “design load” conditions. According to DS 418, the dimensioning temperatures 
to be considered while making the heat loss calculations are represented in Figure 1.

The temperature driven heat losses from an indoor space can be broadly classified into two 
types, transmission losses and ventilation losses. In addition, infiltration losses also occur in 
buildings due to joints of construction, cracks or openings etc. which depend on the quality of 
workmanship. These losses are considered while determining the heat transfer coefficients or U 
values of construction elements, which represent the effectiveness of the elements as 
insulators.

3. Results 
In this section, the 10 investigated sites have been summarized. For each site a short 
introduction along with the substation schematic is presented. The components of the system, 
their operation and their set points have been reported. The data gathered from DH companies 
regarding the monthly supply and return temperatures have been represented in graphs.    
The DHW and heat loss calculation mentioned above has only been performed on three 
building sites. The sites selected are Building Site 1 which is a detached single-family home, 
Building Site 2 which is a row house and Building Site 3 which is an apartment complex. These 
three sites are representative of three types of dwellings and the procedure applied to these 
dwellings can be extended to other similar sites to determine their heat losses.  
The three Building Sites, 1, 2 and 3 are described and details required to perform heat loss 
calculations are provided. The details of the construction elements, their materials and U values 
have been summarized in tables along with the transmission areas. In a separate table the floor 
areas have been described to calculate the ventilation losses. Details regarding the dimensions 
and type of radiators/floor heating systems are also mentioned to calculate the maximum 
power output by the space heating system.  
3.1 Building Site 1 
Building Site 1 is a single-family home from the late 20’s consisting of a basement, main floor 
and a first floor covering a total floor area of 196 m2. The house was inhabited by an elderly 
couple having a keen interest and extensive knowledge about their heating system, which was 
being regularly monitored and serviced by them.  
The building site has been examined by an energy consultant and has been given an energy 
label marking of “D”. In Denmark, energy labeling of buildings ranges from G (least saving) to 
A2020 (good saving) which has now become a law and helps to visualize the energy 
consumption of a building and also suggests profitable improvements that can be implemented 
to achieve CO2 reductions and savings on heating and electricity bills. This building can be 
reduced to “C” level by implementing energy savings renovation as suggested by the 
consultant. The energy labels of buildings and other general information can be collected from 
boligejer.dk by entering the address of the building.

For DHW preparation an instantaneous plate heat exchanger, Redan Akva Vita, manufactured

directs DH supply water into the heat exchanger during tapping periods and the desired DHW 
temperature can be set by turning the adjustment lever mounted on the PM regulator. The 
FJVR thermostat is a self-acting bypass valve and regulates the return DH temperature. It 
provides a constraint to the flow on the primary side of the HEX to ensure the return 
temperature corresponds to the set point.

the basement, ground floor and the first floor of the house. Radiator control was achieved by 
self-regulating TRV valves manufactured by Danfoss mounted on the supply lines. The head of

the main space and no weather compensation or night setback was implemented.

3.3.1.a Space Heating  
For meeting the space heating needs, two large insulated shell and tube type heat exchangers

exchangers was switched off during summer when the outdoor temperatures rose.  
Danfoss ECL 9300 weather compensation unit was used to control the space heating system. 
The ECL unit established control with the help of regulating valves of type AMV 523 installed in 
the return line of the heat exchangers and group of sensors connected to strategic locations 
such as the DH supply and warm water exit points of the HEX, the regulating valves, and the 
pump to receive timely data. An outdoor temperature sensor is also part of the unit which 
helps to control the flow temperature of the circulated warm water according to the outdoor 
temperature. A setting knob on the unit is used to set the flow temperature of the circulating 
warm water. Depending on the thermal comfort required, the knob can be turned up for higher

In all the investigated building sites, a combination of different factors, both design and control 
are responsible for good system performance. However, some common elements can be 
detected in most of the building sites and are mentioned below.  
i) 
TRVs: TRVs were installed in all homes with radiators to achieve temperature 
control. The TRV set points ranged between 2 (15 C) usually in basements and 
bedrooms and 4 (21 C) usually in living room. Setting TRVs to maximum set point of 
5 (26 C) must be avoided as they are not designed to maintain such high indoor 
temperature. Maximum settings also allow the valve to be completely opened and 
let the water flow through the radiator without sufficient regulation. This leads to 
high return temperatures which is not favorable. Care should also be taken to 
ensure multiple radiators in the same room have the same set point of TRVs for 
better operation.  
ii) 
Return thermostats: Return thermostatic valves are mounted on the return line of 
radiators and regulate the temperature of the water leaving the radiators. As 
highlighted in Table 36, four sites had Danfoss FJVRs set in the range 1 (20 C) to 3 
(35 C), which promote adequate cooling of supply water. However, these valves can 
cause thermal discomfort as they do not adjust according to changing room 
temperature like TRVs. Hence using them in combination with TRVs as set up in Site 
1 can be useful to regulate SH operation and ensure low return temperatures. 
Another potential issue with the return thermostats is that the users may have high 
settings (>3) which can lead to high return temperatures. Hence, care should be 
taken to ensure the valve is positioned at low set points during operation.  
iii) 
TRVs with Pre-settings:  Pre-settings on radiator TRVs were found in all apartment 
buildings. Usually there are 8 different pre-setting positions, 1-8 and an additional 
position N. Position N allows the valve to operate at maximum capacity and is used 
while flushing the system. Set points between 3 and 5 were observed during site 
visits and are recommended along with pump pressure head of 5-10 kPa in critical 
radiator [22]. Pre-settings limit the maximum flow through the valve and maintain 
hydraulic balance in the system even when TRVs are set at maximum values and 
cannot provide flow regulation. In apartment buildings, pre-settings avoid overflow 
in radiators and promote lower return temperatures in combination with a pump 
operating at low flows.  
iv) 
Weather compensation: All apartment buildings were installed with weather 
compensation control. Weather compensation plays an important role in optimizing 
the supply temperature of the system with dynamic changes in the outdoor 
temperatures. Proper control of supply temperature reduces the chance of 
overheating which might lead to high return temperatures. For building site 9, the 
==PAGE:76==
69 
 
weather compensation curve was recorded. The supply temperature for design 
outdoor temperature of -12 C was 69 C and for higher outdoor temperatures the 
supply is gradually decreased. If the SH elements have a certain degree of over sizing 
then the compensation curve can be further reduced or optimized while still 
ensuring the same thermal comfort with lower operating temperatures.  
v) 
Bypass valve: Bypass connection enables supply water to be redirected to the return 
pipe without cooling in the summer when the heating demand is low, leading to 
increased return temperatures. An estimated 4 C increase in average return 
temperatures can be expected when 10% of the supply water is bypassed [23].  
Hence, bypass flows have a huge impact on return temperatures. In the row houses 
investigated, the street pipeline passes through the customer’s basement leading to 
short connections to the HEX and hence no bypass is needed. This is one of the 
major reasons to have low return temperatures from substation located in row 
houses.  
vi) 
Return line radiator: One unique factor of the study was the implementation of a 
return line radiator in the basement of Building site 1. The DH return water from the 
HEX was passed through the radiator before leaving the substation. This is analogous 
to having a floor heating system in the bathroom where the return DH water is 
directed all along the year. This promotes cooling down of bypassed water in 
summer, reducing the return temperatures.  
vii) 
Oversized Radiators: Buildings sites 1, 2 and 3 were used as examples to perform 
heat loss calculations. In all these sites, the radiators were found to be over 
dimensioned for design load conditions. Larger radiators result in large heat transfer 
areas and hence have higher potential for lower return temperatures [24]. However, 
design conditions rarely occur and by considering heat gains, radiators are operating 
under part load for most of the year. Thus, these radiators can be operated under 
LTDH temperature levels and the desired thermal comfort can still be experienced 
without necessarily renovating the buildings [11]. 
viii) 
Direct SH connection: Another design factor is the direct connection of space 
heating system to the DH network for single family homes and row houses. This 
aligns with [21], which states that direct connections such as the ones in Denmark 
are responsible for lower return temperatures compared to the Swedish heating 
network commonly having indirect connection. The internal connection was the 
commonly used two pipe system which ensures the same temperature is available at 
each radiator. 
ix) 
Differential pressure control: Another important requirement is the differential 
control valve when the SH systems are directly connected to the DH pipes as seen in 
Denmark. The presence of the differential control valve ensures a well-adjusted 
==PAGE:77==
70 
 
system with low oscillations in pressure, protecting the components of the 
substations. This promotes proper sizing and functioning of control valves and also 
improves their lifetime [25], leading to reduced faults during operation and 
consequently low return temperatures. In apartment buildings, the differential 
pressure control also maintains hydraulic balance of the SH system so that even 
distribution of supply water is available at each radiator and thermal comfort is 
experienced by the consumers. 
x) 
Night setback: In all the investigated building sites, night setback was not 
implemented. This strategy is used to reduce indoor temperatures at night to 
achieve some energy savings where the heat energy stored in the building’s thermal 
mass is utilized for comfort. This practice leads to having higher peak loads in the 
morning when the SH system is expected to warm up the indoor air as well as the 
building’s thermal mass. This practice will increase the costs of operation when peak 
boilers are used for production of heat and also cause radiators to work at higher 
part loads for longer periods resulting in higher return temperatures. Hence, 
according to [16], night setback has no value for sites which are well insulated and 
have long time constant or thermal storage capacity.   
xi) 
Consumer interest: Consumer interest and heating behavior dictates whether the 
system operates effectively and ultimately affects the return temperatures. 
Although deep knowledge about heating systems is unnecessary, good heating 
practices such as properly setting TRVs/FJVRs, following company recommendations 
during installation of substation and occasional servicing was found to be favorable 
for achieving low return temperatures. Most of the single-family homes monitored 
their system, kept track of their energy bills and took steps towards having more 
energy savings, mainly by increasing the insulation of their homes and replacing old 
windows with newer and more efficient ones.

5. Conclusions and further work 
Ten well-functioning DH home substations in Denmark were identified in collaboration with DH 
companies and were investigated. These substations were installed in 1 detached single-family 
house, 6 row houses and 3 apartment buildings and were characterized by low return 
temperatures. These building sites consisted of both old and recently built dwellings and give a 
good representation of a typical mix of dwellings connected to the DH network. During the site 
visits, the components and control elements installed in the substation were identified and 
details regarding the manufacturer, type and the set points were noted. The details of the SH 
system were also recorded. If radiators were installed then the location, type, dimensions, TRV 
position, set point and pre-settings (if any) were recorded. For floor heating, the location and 
set points were recorded. Pictures of the substation, the energy/manufacturer’s labels on the 
components and the SH system were taken and a rough sketch of the substation connections 
was made. Apart from the substation and the SH system, the building was also inspected and 
details regarding the window type, layers and details of renovation carried out were also noted. 
During the visits, the homeowners and the building technicians were interviewed and asked 
questions about thermal comfort and complaints or specific issues experienced to get a better 
understanding of their system. Their involvement and knowledge about the heating system 
were also noted. 
After the site visits a detailed schematic was prepared for all the substations using Microsoft 
Visio, a diagramming tool using standard P&ID symbols using the sketch prepared during the 
site visit as a reference. The schematic represents all the components of the substation and the 
control elements. Data regarding these components was gathered from manufacturer’s 
product catalog and technical specification documents. Description of the components, their 
set points, their corresponding values and manufacturer recommendations has been provided 
in a table below the schematics.  
For Sites 1, 2 and 3 design heat loss calculations and DHW calculations were performed. These 
sites were selected as they represent three different types of dwellings, single family, row 
house and apartment building and the procedure applied for heat loss calculation to these sites 
can be extended to other sites. For heat loss calculations, the transmission, ventilation and total 
heat losses were calculated using details from building plans, energy label documents and the 
DS 418 standard. This design heat loss was compared with the maximum power of the SH 
system calculated by inserting details of radiators/floor heating into a Microsoft Excel 
calculation tool developed by DTI. These calculations and comparisons were made to determine 
whether the SH system was over dimensioned and if this plays a role in achieving low return 
temperatures. Similarly, DHW calculations were also performed to determine if the 
instantaneous HEX/storage tank can sufficiently meet hot water requirements as per DS 439 
==PAGE:79==
72 
 
standards. These calculations also give an idea about the compatibility of existing heating 
systems with future LTDH systems.  
After site visits and the analysis of the substations the following learning points were derived.  
For the single-family home (Site 1), an old building from the 20’s, the average return 
temperature was 23.8 C. The substation consisted of an instantaneous HEX for DHW and a 
direct connected SH system with radiators. The low return temperatures from this site can be 
attributed to TRVs which were positioned between 2 (in bedroom, basement) and 4 (in living 
room) on the scale which is recommended and also consisted of return thermostats set around 
a position of 2 to control the return temperatures from the SH system. It is advisable to avoid 
maximum setting on these valves which can easily increase the return temperatures. The site 
also consisted of a bypass valve, but the return water from HEX was directed into a radiator in 
the basement before connecting to the DH street pipe, hence cooling down the water. This 
arrangement or similar floor heating arrangement for bathroom comfort is recommended for 
reducing return temperatures especially in the summer due to bypass.   
Row houses (Sites 2, 4, 5, 6, 7 and 8) investigated, were buildings built between the late 60’s 
and the early 70’s. The average return temperatures ranged between 23 C and 28 C. Most of 
these houses consisted of HEXs for DHW preparation and directly connected SH system with 
radiators for space heating. However, two of these sites had unique air heating (Site 8) and 
ceiling heating systems (Site 7) and still managed to attain low return temperatures, reinforcing 
the fact that different systems can provide low return temperatures provided they are properly 
controlled.  For the rest of the systems with radiators, similar patterns were observed as seen in 
the single-family house where the TRVs and the return thermostats were set correctly. 
However, the absence of the bypass valve in the row houses due to short service pipes play a 
major role in reduced temperatures highlighting the importance to find technical solutions in 
the future to mitigate the impacts of bypass flows. Floor heating was also present in two houses 
(2 and 4) and may lead to a slight increase in return temperature in the summer when the set 
point on the scale is high. 
Apartment buildings consisted of buildings from the 20’s (Site 9), 30’s (Site 3) and 00’s (Site 10) 
and the return temperatures ranged between 31 C and 39 C. These temperatures were 
possible due to proper hydraulic balance in the system obtained by balancing valves and TRVs 
with pre-settings set in combination with low flow through the system. Pre-setting values of 3-5 
are recommended for good hydraulic balance and control. Weather compensation also plays an 
important part and the correct curve must be selected depending on the building’s energy label 
and the existing SH system. For site 9, with an energy label of C and properly balanced heating 
system, a supply temperature of 69 C was selected for outdoor temperatures of -12 C which 
is standard.  
==PAGE:80==
73 
 
Apart from these learning points it was also found that radiators which are oversized have a 
higher potential for lower return temperature and also increase the system’s compatibility with 
LTDH [11]. This along with a direct connected SH system with two pipe arrangement is 
favorable. Care should also be taken to incorporate differential pressure control to avoid over 
pressurization and allow for efficient operation of control valves.  
Among these investigated buildings sites, apartment buildings were found to be more difficult 
to control and the average return temperatures were significantly higher than single family/row 
houses, especially building site 3 which had a return of 39 C. Since this return temperature is 
based on instantaneous values and not monthly averages, it may be subject to change. 
However, as the heating system was renovated in 1994 and all the radiators have pre-settings, 
this building has great potential to achieve lower average return temperatures by simply 
improving the control of the system. An investigation following the detailed hourly 
measurements for operating temperatures and SH and DHW energy consumptions can lead to 
a better understanding of the system and thus, lead to improvements of the weather 
compensation curve, pump operation and charging of the storage tank to achieve return 
temperature in the range of 25-30 C without any extra costs or improvement of the building 
envelop in the future.
