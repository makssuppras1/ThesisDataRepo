Abstract
The increasing integration of renewable energy sources, particularly through inverter-
based resources (IBRs), is fundamentally changing the dynamics of modern power sys-
tems. IBRs differ fundamentally from synchronous generators in terms of dynamic behav-
ior and system interaction. Grid-following (GFL) converters rely on control loops measur-
ing and synchronizing with the grid voltage. They require sufficient grid stiffness (system
strength) to keep synchronism and ensure stable operation. As the penetration of GFL
converters continues to rise, it is essential for grid operators to evaluate system strength
and take preventive actions to mitigate potential stability issues.
Traditionally, system strength has been evaluated using the short circuit ratio (SCR), a
metric based on physical network parameters. However, SCR does not adequately reflect
the dynamic interactions between IBRs or the complexity of converter-dominated grids.
The Equivalent Short Circuit Ratio (ESCR) has been proposed as an extension of SCR
to better capture the impact of nearby converters through an interaction factor.
This thesis investigates the suitability of the ESCR as a methodology to quantify system
strength in IBR-dominated grids. A Python-based tool was developed to automate ESCR
calculation in PowerFactory. Various methods for the determation of the interaction factor
have been implemented and compared in a simplified four bus test system. Electromag-
netic transient (EMT) simulations are used to interpret the ESCR values and check how
well they reflect the actual system stability and converter behaviour under fault conditions.
The results show that while ESCR offers a practical and fast screening approach to iden-
tify relatively weak areas in large grids, it has significant limitations. Its accuracy depends
on assumptions about converter fault behaviour, which lack standardization and do not
have a clear physical interpretation. Dynamic phenomena which are determined by the
converter’s control system, are not captured in the ESCR value. Furthermore, no univer-
sal ESCR threshold could be identified for the test system that reliably indicates instability
or a critical weak grid.
Despite these limitations, the ESCR proves useful as a pre-screening tool for identifying
potentially weak areas that require more detailed stability analysis. The thesis highlights
the need for further refinement of the ESCR methodology, better standardization of pa-
rameter assumptions, and testing in larger and more realistic system models. This thesis
project contributes to the ongoing effort of enabling reliable system strength assessment
in future converter-dominated power systems.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background and problem statement
The renewable energy share in electricity generation has grown significantly and is further
driven by European and National policies [1]. The integration of emission-free energy
sources into the power grid requires significant system changes and the implementation
of new technologies.
Variable renewable energy technologies, particularly variable sources such as wind and
photovoltaics, rely on power electronics for grid integration and are thus commonly called
inverter-based resources (IBRs). IBRs, alongside the benefits and flexibility, bring new
challenges to grid stability and its power quality dimensions such as voltage regulation,
harmonics, active and reactive power control [2]. Compared to conventional synchronous
generators, IBRs differ in their dynamic behaviour, including inertial response, voltage
stability characteristics, and fault ride-through behavior [3]. The dynamic behaviour of
synchronous generators is mostly determined by their inherent physical properties, such
as rotating mass and electromagnetic characteristics. In contrast, the response of IBRs is
dominated by their control system. Proper design and tuning of the control systems allow
converters to emulate desired characteristics of synchronous generators and comply with
grid code requirements.
Grid-following (GFL) converters rely on control loops measuring and synchronizing with
the grid voltage. They require sufficient system strength to keep synchronism and ensure
stable operation [4]. As the penetration of GFL converters continues to rise, it is essential
for grid operators to evaluate system strength and take preventive actions to mitigate
potential stability issues.
An accurate analysis of grid strength requires computationally heavy EMT simulations
and detailed converter models, which makes it infeasible for large grids. To identify weak
grid areas and take adequate mitigation actions, a reliable grid strength metric is essential.
Particularly, when working with large grid models, the assessment method has to be com-
putationally efficient and compatible with the available system data. This issue has been
addressed in the System Stability Report 2023, which was published by the four German
Transmission System Operators (TSOs), forming the basis of grid expansion plans and
laws [5].
System strength reflects the ability of a power grid to maintain stable operation. Low
system strength indicates a high probability for voltage instability and also increases the
risk for instability in other domains such as frequency, angle and harmonic stability.
System strength is traditionally quantified using the Short Circuit Ratio (SCR), which is
based on the fault current at a specific bus and the physical impedances of the network.
The SCR is most applicable in power systems dominated by synchronous generators
and may not accurately reflect stability in converter-dominated grids. Most converters are
limited to 1.1 - 1.2 p.u. fault current provision, leading to lower short-circuit levels when
conventional generation is replaced by IBRs [6, 7]. The SCR metric does not consider the
potential influence of electrically close IBRs and the resulting interactions between their
control loops, which can lead to inaccurate estimations of grid strength [8].
Several methods have been developed to tackle the issue of system strength assessment
in IBR-dominated grids; one of them is the equivalent short circuit ratio (ESCR). This

metric is still looking at a specific bus but aims to account for IBRs connected to other
buses and their contribution to system strength through an interaction factor. The ESCR
can therefore be understood as an extension of the traditional SCR, developed to address
its limitations by capturing the influence of nearby IBRs. It is a promising screening method
to evaluate system strength in large grids [9].
While the ESCR formula itself is clearly defined, the determination of the necessary pa-
rameters is still subject to ongoing research, and the required assumptions are not stan-
dardized. Likewise, the interpretation of ESCR results in terms of system strength and
stability boundaries needs further analysis.
This raises the following research questions:
• How can the evaluation and analysis of system strength be improved in upcoming
IBR-dominated power systems?
• Which methodology can be used to quantify system strength in large grids under
limited data availability and computational power?
• How can an ESCR value characterizing system strength be interpreted, and what
are the implications for grid planning, operation and stability?
1.2
Thesis objectives and organization
The goal of this thesis is to develop a reliable methodology to calculate the ESCR and
assess the meaning of this metric for system strength and stability. The work has been
divided as follows:
• Literature review on available metrics for the evaluation of system strength, with
main focus on how to calculate the ESCR
• Development and implementation of an automated ESCR calculation method for
different grid models using Python
• Comparison and review of different approaches for the ESCR calculation
• Plausibility check of the proposed methodology through EMT software.
This report is structured into five chapters. Chapter 2 introduces the reader to the relevant
technical background and the state of the art in system strength assessment. Chapter 3
describes the tools, and models used for the development of the ESCR calculation method
in this thesis. It also outlines the assumptions and limitations of the project. Chapter 4
presents the results of the analysis. In the first part of the chapter, different ESCR calcula-
tion methods developed within this project are compared, while the plausibility check with
EMT simulations is used in the second part of the chapter to assess the opportunities and
limitations of the ESCR as a metric for system strength. The last chapter gives a summary
of the findings as well as recommendations on future work and use of the ESCR.

2
Technical Background
In this section, system strength is described in the context of power system stability. The
relation between system strength, short-circuit contribution and system impedance is out-
lined in terms of the SCR. New approaches that extend the SCR to account for IBRs in
the system are introduced. Lastly, the relevant background on grid-forming (GFM) and
grid-following (GFL) converter control strategies is explained.
2.1
Power system stability
Power system stability can be defined as follows: ”Power system stability is the ability
of an electric power system, for a given initial operating condition, to regain a state of
operating equilibrium after being subjected to a physical disturbance, with most system
variables bounded so that practically the entire system remains intact” [10]
To reduce the complexity of power system stability analysis, the classification of stability
phenomena is a common practice. A widely used classification according to P. Kun-
dur distinguishes between rotor angle, frequency and voltage stability [11]. The increas-
ing penetration of IBRs in the power system led to the introduction of two new stability
classes, namely converter-driven stability and resonance stability. The classification of
stability in modern, IBR-dominated power systems remains a topic of ongoing discussion
in both industry and academia. Nonetheless, such classifications are simplifications, and
all stability classes have to be evaluated for a comprehensive stability study [12].
GFL converters rely on a strong grid to synchronize successfully [13]. Without sufficient
system strength, converter interactions can cause instabilities [10]. In the context of the
stability classes, system strength can be understood as a prerequisite for stable operation,
especially in converter-dominated grids. A weak grid correlates with instabilities in multiple
classes, but it is particularly relevant to voltage stability [14].
2.2
Definition of system strength
The definition of system strength varies across both industry and academia [4]. While it
used to be a synonym for short-circuit capacity, it is nowadays also understood as the in-
sensitivity of voltage to current variations [15]. Some define system strength more broadly,
considering it a more general measure of system stability [4].
In the context of this work, system strength can be defined as ”the ability of the power
system to maintain and control the voltage waveform at any given location in the power
system” [16]. In [17], system strength is also defined as the system’s distance to its voltage
stability limit.
A weak grid means the system is susceptible to voltage variations and there is a risk
for voltage stability, for instance, due to low available short circuit power [6]. In a strong
grid, the system voltage is not significantly influenced by changes in the network, such as
disturbances or active and reactive power injections (and absorptions) [15]. Voltage sen-
sitivity is directly linked to system impedance; higher impedance means the bus voltage
is more sensitive to changes [4].

2.5
Converter control strategies
To understand the influence of converters on system strength, it is necessary to distinguish
between GFL and GFM converters. Figure 2.1 shows a typical GFL control strategy on
the left and a GFM control strategy on the right according to [23].

Figure 2.1: GFL and GFM control strategy according to [23]
A GFL control scheme uses a phase-locked loop (PLL) to synchronize with grid voltage,
which is is why a strong grid is required for stable operation [24]. Based on this synchro-
nization, the converter injects a controlled current into the grid, meaning it behaves as
a current source. In contrast, GFM converters do not rely on the external grid voltage
for synchronization [25]. They act as voltage sources by directly regulating their termi-
nal voltage according to an internal voltage reference, which allows them to ”form” the
grid voltage. As there are numerous different control strategies, there is no single, com-
mon definition for GFM converters [26]. They can be characterized by forming the terminal
voltage directly based on a predefined reference voltage. Nowadays, the majority of IBRs
installed in the grid are equipped with a GFL control scheme.
The different types of control schemes and their implementation are not in focus of this
work, which is why they are not explained further. However, the control parameters of
the PLLs used for the GFL converters are modified later on, which is why the PLL and its
parameters are explained in the next section.
2.5.1
Phase-locked loop (PLL)
A PLL is a feedback control system that adjusts the phase of a locally generated signal
to match the phase of an input signal [27]. In GFL converters, the PLL is essential for
synchronizing with the grid, as it estimates the phase angle of the grid voltage, which is
then used by the converter’s inner control loops.
A widely used implementation in power electronics is the synchronous reference frame
PLL (SRF-PLL), illustrated in Figure 2.2. The input is the measured three-phase grid volt-
age ˜uabc, which is transformed into the rotating dq reference frame using a Park transfor-
mation. The PLL aims to minimize the q-axis voltage component ˜uq, which represents the
instantaneous phase error between the converter’s reference frame and the grid voltage
[28].
Figure 2.2: Synchronous reference frame PLL (SRF-PLL) used in GFL converters
To reduce this error, a Proportional-Integral (PI) controller is used as the loop filter. It con-
sists of a proportional gain kP,PLL and an integral gain kI,PLL, which determine the dynamic

behavior of the controller. The proportional term provides immediate correction based on
the current error, while the integral term accumulates past errors to eliminate steady-state
error. The controller output is the estimated grid frequency ˜f, which is integrated to com-
pute the estimated phase angle ˜φ. This angle is fed back into the Park transformation,
ensuring continuous synchronization with the grid.
The PI controller’s tuning directly affects PLL performance. Higher gain values lead to a
faster dynamic response but may cause oscillations or instability if not properly damped.
Conversely, lower gain values result in a slower response and may prevent the PLL from
effectively eliminating steady-state errors [29].

3
Modelling and Implementation
As stated in section 1.1 and 2.4, the ESCR presents a promising procedure to assess
system strength in IBR dominated grids under the given use case. Therefore, the ESCR
is the subject of the following implementation and analysis. The CIGRE definition of the
ESCR does not specify the detailed determination of the necessary parameters. Different
approaches can affect the results severely, which is why the calculation procedure for this
thesis is explained in detail in this chapter. The relevant values for calculating the ESCR
at a bus i are Si, Pi as well as the interaction factor IFij and Pj of all buses j acting as a
connection point to an IBR.
3.1
Short-circuit power of GFL and GFM converters
As outlined in section 2.3, the estimation of short-circuit power usually requires sufficient
information about the internal control structure or the black box EMT model. On the other
hand, the goal of this thesis is to estimate the ESCR without having access to EMT ven-
dors’ models and with access to marginal data of the IBRs in the system. To model IBRs
in the system, several assumptions have been made to account for the short-circuit be-
haviour of GFL and GFM converters.
The ESCR does not differentiate between converters based on GFL and GFM control.
If the user wants to address how differently these control philosophies affect system
strength, assumptions have to be made. Accounting for these differences requires an
analysis of the converters’ fault response characteristics.
Figure 3.1 shows the three-phase currents of a GFL and GFM converter as well as a
synchronous generator (SG) after a three-phase-to-ground short circuit at 0 ms from EMT
simulations. The results have been generated from a simple network model connecting
a grid equivalent, represented by a voltage source, and the element of interest to the
same terminal. The short circuit is then applied to this terminal with a fault impedance
(Zf = 2+j0.2 Ω) and cleared after 200 ms. The parameter values of the SG, GFL converter
and GFM converter can be found in Appendix A.1.
The SG shows a fast reaction and current increase reaching its maximum within 10 ms.
Both converters saturate at 1.1. p.u. because of the implemented current limitation. The
GFM converter provides a fast current reaction and supports the system, which is similar
to the SG’s initial response after the fault. In contrast, the GFL converter is not able to
provide this fast reaction.
Equal treatment of all converter types would mean counting the GFM converters in the de-
nominator of the formula. This results in GFM converters reducing the ESCR and showing
a negative impact on grid strength. Unlike GFL converters, they are able to support the
system. GFM converters are therefore disregarded in the denominator. They contribute
to the short circuit level with their nominal power, which means they are considered in the
nominator and increase the ESCR value (see Equation 2.5).

3.2
Methods for the calculation of the interaction factor
Since the literature presents principally empirical-based methods to calculate the interac-
tion factor, these methods do not have a strong theoretical foundation. Due to this, it is
not possible to assess, which of the proposed methods corresponds to the most accurate
one. The thesis covers several proposed methods, being listed and explained below.

3.2.1
Short circuit calculation (SHC)
The short circuit calculation is based on simulating a 3-phase short circuit at bus i and
measuring the voltage drop at bus i and at bus j. The ratio of these voltage drops cor-
responds to the IF. For the short-circuit analysis, the complete method in PowerFactory
is used. Based on superposition, all network voltage sources are shorted and a voltage
source is connected at the fault location. This additional voltage source injects a voltage
equal in magnitude but opposite in polarity compared to the pre-fault voltage at that loca-
tion.
3.2.2
Impedance based method
The impedance-based methods are used to create an impedance matrix of the system
and calculate the IF using the self and mutual impedance of the respective buses (see
(2.6)).
Frequency sweep (Imp) In frequency sweep, a current source is inserted at bus i and
the voltage reaction at the remaining buses is measured to determine the impedances.
Harmonic loadflow (HLF) Similiar to frequency sweep, a current source is inserted at
bus i and the voltage reaction at the remaining buses is measured to determine the
impedances in the HLF procedure.
Frequency sweep and harmonic load flow are expected to give the same impedance ma-
trix as an output. However, this could not be reproduced and the harmonic loadflow did
not show consistent results, which is most likely due to the used converter control models.
Therefore, the impedance method has been implemented based on a frequency sweep.
3.2.3
EMT simulation (EMT)
In the EMT simulation, a mainly inductive load consuming 1 MW and 10 MVAr is inserted
at bus i and activated during the simulation. The voltage reaction at each bus is calcu-
lated using the prefault values and the minimum values in the first 20 ms after the load
activation. The EMT method has been developed as a plausibility check to the stationary
methods. The initial definition of the ESCR does not suggest a dynamic simulation for the
determination of the IF.
3.3

loads L2 and L3 are modelled as constant impedance and consume between 0-200 MW

Many IFs are 1 or even above 1, which reflects the topology of the small and synthetic

elements in the system, which normally help reduce interactions between the converter

is used to create the voltage disturbance. Compared to a short circuit the chosen event,
the activation of a load consuming reactive power, gives lower IF values.

at other buses, as the GFM converter connected to bus 2 is acting as a voltage source.

4.1.3
Summary
The ESCR values across different methods (SHC, Impedance, EMT) show notable vari-
ations. The differences do not show proportional patterns, sometimes they converge for
bus 2 and 3 but not for all buses (see Figure 4.1). While the short-circuit power remains
identical for all methods its magnitude increases with system load due to the load’s influ-
ence on total fault impedance.
In contrast, the IF varies significantly across methods, particularly in how directional sen-
sitivity and voltage disturbance types are captured. The EMT method yields higher IF
values in some cases due to its dynamic modelling, while the impedance-based IFs show
strong directional differences due to varying self-impedances. The SHC method, although
simpler, provides less sensitivity to system changes. As stated earlier, the computational
complexity of using EMT simulations makes the developed EMT method inefficient for ap-
plications in large power grids. On top of this, it is highly dependent on the chosen event
causing the voltage disturbance. Considering both accuracy and consistency, especially
under different system conditions, the impedance method offers a balanced and event-
independent approach to ESCR calculation. For further analysis, this method is used as
ESCR reference.

4.2
Plausibility check of ESCR values using EMT simulation
As there is no reference for the ”correct” value, it is not possible to rate the methods
according to their accuracy directly. To interpret the ESCR values and check how well
they reflect the actual stability of the network, EMT simulations are used in this section.
Different sensitivities expected to affect system strength are applied to the system. The
objective is to assess how these changes are reflected in the ESCR value and how they
influence converter behaviour and system stability in the EMT domain. As a disturbance,
a three-phase fault is applied to L14 and cleared after 200 ms.
The following analysis includes:
• the impact of converter tripping mechanisms
• a comparison between synchronous generators (SG) and grid-forming converters
(GFM)
• sensitivities expected to affect system strength
– converter power ratings: Rated power is a key parameter of the ESCR for-
mula. It is assumed that a higher share of GFL converters decreases system
strength.
– line impedances: The interaction of converters as well as the available short-
circuit power depends on the system impedance, which is why this parameter
is chosen for modifications.
– PLL control settings: The control system has a significant influence on con-
verter behaviour and thus on system stability.

Summary
The impedance modifications are reflected in the ESCR values at all buses. As expected,
increasing line impedance leads to a reduction in short-circuit power and consequently
lower ESCR values. This behavior is consistently captured by the SHC method, which
aligns with theoretical expectations. However, the impedance-based ESCR metric shows
inconsistent behavior. In scenario 3B, an increase in line impedance unexpectedly re-
sults in higher ESCR values at certain buses, despite a clear reduction in short-circuit
power. This discrepancy is linked to the sensitivity captured in the IF matrices, where the
impedance method shows a much stronger reduction in IFs than the SHC method. Con-
sequently, the impedance-based ESCR may not reliably indicate a weakening system in
all cases.

Summary
As expected, the change of PLL parameters has no influence on the ESCR value. Even
with the voltage sensitivity captured through the EMT method, no difference could be
identified. Nonetheless, the PLL parameters or the converter tuning in general, has a
major impact on the converter’s fault behaviour and thus on system stability. With similar
ESCR values and different PLL dynamics, some scenarios lead to tripping of one, two
or even all three of the converters. The ESCR is not able to capture dynamic behaviour
driven by the converter’s control loop.

5
Conclusion and outlook
The goal of this thesis was an analysis of the ESCR and how it can be used to evaluate
system strength in converter-dominated power grids. The thesis showed advantages,
limitations as well as remaining research gaps of the ESCR methodology. In this section,
the findings corresponding to the research questions from Section 1.1 are presented,
followed by an outlook on potential future work.
Which methodology can be used to quantify system strength in large grids under limited
data availability and computing power?
The ESCR is able to capture the influence of short circuit capacity and grid topology affect-
ing system strength and voltage stability. However, the concept of short circuit capacity
still leaves open points when accounting for converters. The actual short-circuit behaviour
of a converter is highly dependent on its control settings. As generic assumptions have
been made for this thesis project, the uncertainty arising from the short-circuit power as
a parameter in the ESCR framework is still high.
The ESCR fails to account for the behaviour of dynamic control and protection mech-
anisms (e.g., converter tripping due to overvoltage). For instance, in the LL scenario,
tripping of GFL converters and subsequent system instability are not reflected in ESCR
values. On top of this, the ESCR as a stationary method cannot capture e.g. variations
in PLL parameters. Even including a dynamic component by calculating the IF in the
EMT domain could not help capture the impact of varying PLL parameters in the ESCR.
However, this modification showed a significant influence on fault behaviour and system
stability.
Unlike the SCR, which is based on well-established physical laws relating short-circuit
power and impedance for SGs, the determination of the short-circuit power and the IF
within the ESCR framework lacks a clear physical interpretation. This missing theoretical
foundation makes it difficult to fully understand and explain some of the ESCR results,
especially if they are not intuitive. Nonetheless, the ESCR approach is still an interesting
approach, as it offers a fast grid strength assessment based on limited data.
How can an absolute value characterizing system strength be interpreted, and what are
the implications for grid operation and stability?
It was not possible to identify universal threshold ESCR values for the given test system
that indicate a high probability of instability. ESCR thresholds depend a lot on the system
under analysis and the used methodology. As several factors influencing the stability be-
haviour have been identified which are not reflected in the ESCR, the uncertainty inherent
to the metric is too high for the definition of a threshold.
The ESCR could be used as a pre-screening method to reduce the complexity of grid
strength assessment and prioritize grid areas that need further investigation.
How can the evaluation and analysis of system strength be improved in upcoming converter-
dominated power systems?
Instead of assuming no short-circuit contribution for GFL and short-circuit power equal to
nominal power for GFM converters, EMT simulations could be used to determine a less

conservative short-circuit contribution. However, as stated before, this is highly depen-
dent on the individual control loops, which are usually not available for the grid operator.
Moreover, the methods for the assessment of voltage sensitivity need further analysis,
they differ a lot and some differences could not be tracked down to their root cause so far.
The ESCR enables the identification of system areas and nodes which are prone to show
(voltage) instabilities because they are relatively weaker than others due to grid topology.
Therefore, the ESCR might be used as a pre-screening tool to identify relatively weak grid
areas. The weak areas could then be prioritized for further investigations and a detailed
assessment, which increases the overall efficiency of the system strength assessment.
With the small test system, it was not possible to identify (significantly) weak(er) buses.
Usually, both GFL converters tripped almost simultaneously, and it was not possible to
generate an unstable scenario in which the GFL converters stayed connected. In future
work, the methods should be tested in larger grid models to enable a deeper understand-
ing of their differences. A generic use of the ESCR requires clear assumptions and a
common definition of how parameters are retrieved.
