Abstract 
Current production of organofluorine compounds for pharmaceutical, agricultural and polymer 
industries relies solely on traditional chemical synthesis, which is associated with high input of energy, 
solvents and strong acids. Therefore, this thesis aimed to expand a landscape of biologically-derived 
fluorinated building blocks through construction of a synthetic elongation pathway inspired by a fatty 
acid synthesis and comprising acetyl-CoA synthetase (ACS), thiolase, and thioesterase, for extension 
of fluoroacetate (FAc) with butyrate to yield an array of C4 and C6 fluorinated carboxylic acids. To 
select ACS with the most affinity towards the reaction substrates, 10 enzyme candidates were 
screened by the hydroxylamine assay, and acsP.aerA, acsB. sub, acsE.col and acsS.coe were selected for the 
pathway library construction due to their activity towards non-native substrates. Additionally, five 
thiolases and four thioesterases with reported activity towards a range of different substrates were 
chosen for the pathway library construction. A total of eight pathway variants were assembled by a 
Golden Gate approach. In parallel, three P. putida strains β1, β2, and β3 with reduced ability to 
catabolise short- and medium-chain fatty acids were constructed, as it was hypothesised to protect 
reaction substrates and products from degradation by the intrinsic metabolic machinery of the host. 
Functional knockout of seven P. putida genes involved in fatty acid degradation was achieved with aid 
of a CRISPR-Cas9n base editing tool by introduction of the STOP codons in the coding sequence of the 
genes. All strains showed reduced ability to assimilate butyrate, while strains β1 and β3 also 
demonstrated impaired growth on valerate and octanoate. Five out of eight synthetic elongation 
pathway variants were expressed in P. putida wild-type and β3 strains, and pathway activity was 
evaluated in a context of a cell-free extract system by measuring substrate consumption by HPLC. 
While some FAc/F-acetyl-CoA and CoA/ATP compound consumption was observed for all samples, 
there was no significant activity towards butyrate. Quantitative assessment of the pathway activity 
was hampered by multiple compound co-elution, thus, further analysis such as LC-MS and NMR 
spectroscopy might be required to evaluate development of the fluorinated reaction products.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Fluorine and its role in biomolecules 
1.1.1. Properties and applications of organofluorides 
Fluorine (F) is the 13th most abundant chemical element and the most abundant halogen in Earth’s 
crust. F is primarily found in its mineral form, as insoluble calcium fluoride (CaF2), and it is rarely 
incorporated in naturally-occurring biomolecules (X. J. Zhang et al., 2012). F is also the most 
electronegative element in the Periodic table, while displaying a size similar to a hydrogen atom. When 
attached to a carbon atom, F forms the strongest single bond known in organic chemistry, which 
exhibits high polarisation and stability (O’hagan, 2008; Shah & Westwell, 2007). These qualities of the 
C-F bond are actively exploited in a wide range of industrial applications, including synthesis of 
pharmaceuticals, non-reactive polymers, and agrochemicals. For instance, the substitution of a C-H to 
a C-F bond is a commonly employed strategy in medicinal chemistry for improving metabolic stability, 
altering physicochemical properties and impacting binding affinity of drugs (Shah & Westwell, 2007). 
This is illustrated by the fact that by 2019, 18% of all registered pharmaceuticals contained at least 
one F atom (Inoue et al., 2020). Moreover, fluorinated polymers have higher thermal stability, lower 
coefficient of friction and lower reactivity compared to non-fluorinated analogues (Cardoso et al., 
2018). Finally, in the agricultural sector, over 400 out of 1,200 listed agrochemicals contain at least 
one F atom, maintaining crop yield and granting enhanced protection against pests and undesired 
weeds (Ogawa et al., 2020).  
1.1.2. Synthesis of organofluorines 
As of today, industrial demand for organofluorine compounds is solely met by means of traditional 
chemical synthesis (Wang et al., 2021). Building blocks for complex fluorinated molecule assembly are 
most often produced by partial chlorination of methane and subsequent chlorine substitution via 
treatment with fluorination agents F2 or HF. It requires a vast amount of strong acids and energy 
consumed over the halogenation and pyrolysis stages and also leads to formation of corrosive and 
non-recyclable by-products that are usually incinerated (Mierdel et al., 2019). Building block 
conversion into molecules of increasing complexity can further proceed under mild conditions and 
with a high degree of control over the fluorination pattern. Another alternative to fluorination is 
treatment of the molecules directly with specific fluorination agents; however, control over selectivity 
of F incorporation is quite challenging in this approach (Shimizu & Hiyama, 2004). Chemical 
organofluorine synthesis is associated with numerous drawbacks, such as safety and environmental 
concerns, high cost of reagents, high energy input and often a lack of appropriate infrastructure to

carry out fluorination reactions (Caron, 2020). Therefore, to ensure sustainable and cost/effective 
organofluorine production, new methods must be developed. While new chemical synthesis 
approaches might be developed to meet the demand for “greener” selectively fluorinated molecules, 
Nature’s biosynthetic potential can be potentially harnessed by metabolic engineers to generate de 
novo fluorinated molecules and building blocks in the context of living self-reproducible systems.  
 
Figure 1: Canonical fluorination pathway. The reaction between S-adenosyl-L-methionine (SAM) and F- ion catalysed by the 
fluorinase leads to formation of 5′-fluoro-5′-deoxyadenosine (5’-FDA), which is further processed by phosphorylase, 
isomerase and aldolase enzymes to finally yield F-aldehyde (FAld). FAld can be transformed into F-acetate (FAc).  
Nature has not been particularly successful in evolving pathways for the incorporation of fluorine into 
organic compounds. As a matter of fact, amongst over 3,000 known naturally-occurring halogenated 
compounds, only around 100 contain F (Gribble, 2003). The main obstacle towards incorporation of F 
into biomolecules is its high heat hydration in an aqueous environment, which renders it a poor 
nucleophile (Wu et al., 2020).  The discovery of a fluorinase enzyme in Streptomyces cattleya opened 
doors towards engineering biosynthetic pathways for organofluorine production. The canonical 
fluorination pathway commences with the formation of a C-F bond between a fluoride ion (F–) and S-
adenosyl-L-methionine (SAM) by the fluorinase to generate 5′-fluoro-5′-deoxyadenosine (5′-FDA) and 
L-methionine (O’Hagan et al., 2002). 5’-FDA is further converted into 5-fluoro-5-deoxy-D-ribose-1-
phosphate (5-FDRP) by a purine nucleoside phosphorylase. An isomerase catalyses the conversion of 
5-FDRP into 5-fluoro-5-deoxy-D-ribulose-1-phosphate (5-FDRulP), which in turn is cleaved by an 
aldolase to yield fluoroacetaldehyde (FAld) and dihydroxyacetone phosphate. FAld is further 
converted into either fluoroacetate (FAc) or 4-fluoro-L-threonine via two separate pathways (Deng et 
al., 2008) (Figure 1). 
==PAGE:10==
3 
 
FAc is therefore an entry point towards the biosynthesis of other fluorinated building blocks and 
complex natural products, however, establishing a biosynthetic platform with this compound as a 
precursor is a challenging task due to its acute toxicity. Activated FAc and oxaloacetate are converted 
into (2R,3R)-fluorocitrate by citrate synthase. A potent competitive inhibitor (4-hydroxy-trans-
aconitate) is formed when (2R,3R)-fluorocitrate binds aconitase, the next enzyme in the citric acid 
cycle, which inhibits the citric acid cycle and halts metabolism (Deng et al., 2004).  
Engineering biosynthesis of organofluorine building blocks would require a host that can tolerate 
redox stress associated with de novo fluorination. One such microorganism that can thrive under harsh 
conditions includes the non-pathogenic soil bacterium Pseudomonas putida, whose attractive 
catabolic properties and flexible intrinsic metabolic machinery make it a great chassis for expression 
of ‘challenging’ pathways (e.g. those generating toxic or reactive metabolic intermediates and 
products). These characteristics are outlined in the next section. 
1.2. Pseudomonas putida as a prospective host for biofluorination 
1.2.1. Characteristics of P. putida 
Pseudomonas putida is a Gram-negative non-pathogenic soil bacterium that has become a 
microorganism of choice for metabolic engineers in the recent years. Such spike in its popularity can 
be attributed to numerous intrinsic properties that make this species a suitable cell factory for 
production of toxic and new-to-Nature products. P. putida is known for its fast growth, versatility, by 
accepting multiple carbon and nitrogen sources, and tolerance to solvents and oxidative stress (Nikel 
& de Lorenzo, 2018). These industrially attractive properties can be attributed to the peculiar 
metabolic architecture of this microorganism.  
The very first difference from canonical carbon metabolism can be observed in the routes for substrate 
uptake. 
Glucose 
uptake 
is 
not 
carried out 
via 
a 
phosphoenolpyruvate-carbohydrate 
phosphotransferase system (PTS) as in many other prokaryotes, since the only PTS system present in 
P. putida is responsible for import and phosphorylation of fructose. Instead, glucose is firstly imported 
through the porin OprB in a facilitated diffusion process (Poblete-Castro et al., 2012). Upon entry into 
the cell, glucose can be processed by three different pathways, either by oxidation to gluconate or 2-
ketogluconate, or phosphorylation by glucokinase. All three pathways converge at 6-
phosphogluconate prior to entering Entner–Doudoroff (ED) pathway, which in P. putida replaces the 
canonical Embden–Meyerhof–Parnas (EMP) pathway. While most of the genes for EMP enzymes are 
present in the genome of this bacterium, this pathway is not utilised for sugar catabolism as 
phosphofructokinase and some transcriptional regulators are missing. P. putida uses the combined

action of enzymes from ED, incomplete EMP, and pentose phosphate pathways to operate the so-
called EDEMP cycle, which enables this organism to regenerate NADPH through partial recycling of 
triose-phosphates. Therefore, P. putida is a promising chassis for expression of redox-challenging 
pathways, as the versatility and flexibility of its core carbon and redox metabolism can be harnessed 
and channelled towards the product of interest without significantly compromising viability of the cell 
(Nikel & de Lorenzo, 2018).  
1.2.2. Engineering P. putida towards bioproduction  
Owing to the industrially appealing properties of P. putida and vast accumulated knowledge on this 
microorganism, numerous synthetic biology tools have emerged over the past years. A genetic toolbox 
comprised of well-characterised regulatory elements and tools for genetic engineering, as well as 
transcriptional, post-transcriptional, and post-translational regulation enables efficient engineering of 
P. putida for a range of applications (Martin-Pascual et al., 2021). An array of tools and genetic circuits 
can be utilised to engineer P. putida towards production of a plethora of industrially-relevant 
compounds, both through modulating its endogenous metabolic pathways and by exploring 
possibilities of heterologous pathway expression (Nikel & de Lorenzo, 2018). 
P. putida hosts numerous biosynthetic pathways for production of industrially relevant molecules, and 
the product titers have been successfully improved by metabolic engineering efforts. P. putida is 
known for its native ability to produce medium-chain-length polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs) as means 
of carbon storage.  It was possible to improve PHA titer in P. putida KT2440 by screening for 
endogenous constitutive promoters and placing PHA synthase genes (phaC operon) and pyruvate 
dehydrogenase (acoA) under control of the strongest promoter candidate in glucose dehydrogenase 
(gcd)-deficient strain (Y. Zhang et al., 2021). Intrinsic capability of P. putida to catabolise aromatic 
compounds was also exploited in valorisation of lignin for PHA production, where metabolic 
engineering efforts allowed for increased PHA yield compared to the wild-type strain (Salvachúa et al., 
2020). Besides PHA, other compounds native to P. putida were produced by fine-tuning native 
metabolic pathways. One such example could be production of protocatechuic acid, which was 
achieved by the overexpression of the intrinsic pathway, heterologous replacement of one enzymatic 
step to relieve feedback inhibition, enhancement of precursor availability, and knockout of competing 
pathways (J. Li & Ye, 2021).  
P. putida was also extensively engineered to harbour heterologous pathways for bioproduction of 
various compounds which were until then primarily chemically synthesised. For instance, N-
methylglutamate was produced in P. putida KT2440 via the expression of heterologous genes from 
Methylobacterium extorquens. When grown on a minimal medium supplemented with glycerol and 
==PAGE:12==
5 
 
monomethylamine, the recombinant strain was capable of producing N-methylglutamate in a fed-
batch fermentation (Mindt et al., 2018). Some studies also focused on engineering isobutanol 
production in P. putida due to its native tolerance to solvents, however, the achieved product yield 
was lower than what was observed in recombinant E. coli and C. glutamicum strains (Nitschel et al., 
2020). Besides striving to achieve commodity chemical production, heterologous natural product 
synthesis was also successfully achieved in P. putida, which can be exemplified by integration of 
biosynthetic gene clusters for prodigiosin and glidobactin A into P. putida chromosome. After a few 
genetic manipulations aimed at improving natural product synthesis, competitive product titers were 
achieved (Cook et al., 2021).  
Finally, P. putida was also investigated as a potential host for biotransformation of halogenated 
compounds. De novo biofluorination for this microorganism is still at the onset of its hopefully long 
and prosperous journey, with the sole published report describing the expression of fluorinase and 
accessory genes regulated by a NaF-responsive riboswitch, allowing for the synthesis of 
fluoronucleotides and fluorosugars (Calero et al., 2020).  In parallel with de novo biofluorination, 
natural biosynthetic versatility of P. putida is harnessed for enrichment of biologically derived 
organofluorine compound landscape through exploiting its ability to process a variety of halogenated 
xenobiotics as precursors for essential biomass building blocks. Promiscuity of some catabolic 
enzymes enables utilisation of fluorinated substrates due to their proximity to natural analogues, 
allowing researchers to channel their catalytic activity into synthesis of fluorinated compounds (Cros 
et al., 2022). One successful application of such approach is the conversion of fluorinated benzoates 
into 2-fluoro-cis,cis-muconate achieved by harnessing the natural biosynthetic capability encoded 
within ben and cat gene clusters with subsequent adjustment of regulatory machinery to alleviate 
accumulation of toxic intermediates and improve product yield (Wirth & Nikel, 2021).  
While metabolic engineering of microorganisms towards in vivo fluorinated building block production 
from de novo synthesised FAc is yet to circumvent its toxicity, transformation of this compound can 
be achieved in vitro in a cell-free extract system by designing and expressing a synthetic pathway that 
captures the highly reactive FAld or its FAc oxidized form. One of possible routes of FAc incorporation 
into molecules of increased structural complexity could include its condensation with other carboxylic 
acids employing enzymes from fatty acid synthesis. Relevant enzyme classes that can be applied in the 
synthetic elongation pathway construction are further described in the sections below.  
1.3. Fatty acid synthesis pathway 
Fatty acid biosynthetic pathway is one of the most conserved pathways in bacteria and eukaryotes 
(Cronan & Thomas, 2009). Products of this pathway include precursors for phospholipids, 
==PAGE:13==
6 
 
sphingolipids, and sterols, which serve as structural components of the cellular membrane, secondary 
metabolites and signalling molecules. Additionally, fatty acids can be utilised as a means of carbon and 
energy storage, as their catabolism yields a large amount of ATP and reducing equivalents (Janßen & 
Steinbüchel, 2014).  In prokaryotes, fatty acid synthesis is carried out by a type II fatty synthesis 
pathway (Fab enzymes) primarily to supply building blocks for phospholipid production. Each reaction 
is carried out by a separate enzyme, as opposed to mammalian type I fatty acid synthase, which 
employs a multifunctional enzyme complex for this purpose (Parsons & Rock, 2011).  
De novo fatty acid synthesis commences with conversion of acetyl-CoA into malonyl-CoA by acetyl-
CoA carboxylase. Malonyl moiety is further transferred to the acyl carrier protein (ACP). Commitment 
to the fatty acid synthesis pathway is established by Claisen condensation reaction of malonyl-ACP 
and acetyl-CoA performed by a 3-ketoacyl-ACP synthase, also known as thiolase. Acetoacetyl-CoA, the 
product of this reaction, subsequently enters an elongation cycle, which includes further condensation 
of a growing acyl chain with malonyl-ACP by a thiolase and a series of reduction steps carried out by 
an NADHP-dependent 3-ketoacyl-ACP reductase, 3-hydroxyacyl-ACP dehydratase and enoyl-ACP 
reductase. Once the final reduction step of the cycle is performed, the acyl-ACP can either enter 
another elongation cycle or be transferred into complex lipids once it reaches the final length of 16-
18 carbons  (Cronan & Thomas, 2009) (Figure 2). 
 
Figure 2: Reactions of a fatty acid synthesis pathway. Acetyl-CoA is converted into malonyl-CoA by acetyl-CoA carboxylase, 
with malonyl moiety transferred to ACP in the next step. Acetyl-CoA and malonyl-ACP are used to form acetoacetyl-ACP in a 
Claisen condensation reaction performed by 3-ketoacyl-ACP synthase. Subsequently, acetoacetyl-ACP enters elongation 
cycle, which includes three β-reduction steps and carbon chain extension with a malonyl-ACP extender unit. 
Fatty acid synthesis pathway can be dissected for harnessing activity of each separate enzyme for 
production of molecules of industrial relevance, including fatty acids, alcohols, and lactones. For 
instance, free fatty acid synthesis was investigated in a context of biofuel production in E. coli. The

strategies for improving the product titer involved cytosolic expression of a periplasmic TesA 
thioesterase to cleave the acyl-ACP complex, improving organic solvent tolerance of bacterium by 
deleting marR and thus establishing a constitutive expression of an efflux system, deletion of β-
oxidation genes to limit product degradation and altering the regulation of the synthesis (Janßen & 
Steinbüchel, 2014). Synthesis of medium-chain (4-12 carbons) fatty acids, which are not canonical 
products of fatty acid synthesis, was achieved by expressing various thioesterases specific for medium-
chain fatty acids, engineering the FabF ketoacyl synthase to elongate acyl-ACP only up to 8 carbons in 
length, and tagging ketoacyl synthase FabB for inducible degradation (Torella et al., 2013). Finally, an 
orthogonal pathway derived from fatty acid and polyketide synthesis was engineered for converting 
a range of primer and extender units for the production of valuable small molecules, including 
carboxylic acids with numerous functional groups, lactones, and alcohols. This iterative platform is 
based on non-decarboxylative Claisen condensation, followed by β-reduction steps and various 
termination pathways depending on the desired product (Cheong et al., 2016). This platform served 
as an inspiration for constructing the elongation pathway in this project, which would accept FAc as 
the primer unit and butyrate as an extension unit for synthesis of a longer-chain fluorinated carboxylic 
acids. The pathway designed and deployed in this project encompasses primer and extender unit 
activation by acyl-CoA synthetase as the first step, Claisen condensation by thiolase as the second 
step, and finally, release of the product by thioesterase. β-reduction is currently beyond the scope of 
this project, as pathway viability with FAc as the primer unit for condensation must be demonstrated 
prior to introducing additional reduction steps to generate subsequent product variability. A brief 
summary on the relevant enzymes employed in this project is listed below. 
1.3.1. Ac(et)yl-CoA synthetases  
Acetyl-CoA synthetases belong to adenylate-forming enzyme superfamily, which includes acyl- and 
aryl-CoA forming synthetases, as well as adenylation domains of non-ribosomal peptide synthetases. 
The mentioned enzymes catalyse a two-step reaction, where the first step is the formation of an 
enzyme-bound acyl-adenylate intermediate, which is further converted into an acyl thioester in the 
second half-reaction (Figure 3). Acyl-CoA synthetases can be divided into four subfamilies based on 
the accepted acyl chain length. Long- and very long-chain acyl-CoA synthetases (EC 6.2.1.3) accept 
fatty acids with 14 carbons and above, medium-chain acyl-CoA synthetases activate 4-10 carbon long 
carboxylic acids, as well as some xenobiotics, while short-chain acetyl-CoA synthetases (EC 6.2.1.1) 
primarily conjugate acetate to CoA. The latter subfamily of enzymes is regarded as acetyl-CoA 
synthetases (ACS), and it is responsible for acetyl-CoA generation, which serves as a substrate for 
multiple anabolic and catabolic reactions (Mouterde & Stewart, 2019). ACS from different 
microorganisms are also known to accept a variety of other carboxylic acids besides their native 
==PAGE:15==
8 
 
substrate acetate, including propionate, acrylate, FAc, and 3-chloropropionate, among others (Patel 
& Walt, 1987). The versatility of downstream pathways utilising acetyl-CoA as a precursor for synthesis 
of functionalised small molecules has sparked interest in engineering ACS to efficiently accept 
substrates beyond acetate, as this strategy would open doors for the creation of a biosynthetic 
platform for diverse chemical production (Sofeo et al., 2019).

1.3.2. Thiolases 
Thiolase superfamily enzymes promote Claisen condensation of acyl thioesters and formation of C-C 
bond. Acyl moieties for this reaction are delivered in conjugation to either CoA or ACP, as thioesters 
are more reactive than oxygen esters. The condensation is accomplished in a two-step process, where 
the initial stage includes formation of acylated cysteine intermediate in the active site of the enzyme, 
which is followed by a condensation reaction and product release (Figure 4). Thiolase superfamily can 
be divided into three subfamilies, where the first one includes thiolases, while the other two comprise 
β-ketoacyl-acyl-carrier protein synthases (KAS) and fatty-acid synthases (FAS) of different types 
(Haapalainen et al., 2006). The thiolase subfamily can be classified into degradative type I (E.C. 
2.3.1.16) and biosynthetic type II (E.C. 2.3.1.9) thiolases, where the former play a role in β-oxidation 
of medium-chain fatty acids and can accept a broad substrate range, while the latter exclusively 
catalyse condensation of two acetyl-CoA molecules to yield acetoacetyl-CoA (Mann & Lütke-Eversloh, 
2013). Both thiolase types are capable to promote reversible reactions, however, biosynthetic 
thiolases demonstrate superior catalytic efficiency in comparison to their degradative relatives, with 
approximately 100-fold larger kcat values (H. Zhang et al., 2010).

1.3.3. Thioesterases 
Thioesterases (EC 3.1.2.1-27) is a diverse group of enzymes catalysing the hydrolysis of thioester 
bonds. They can be broadly divided into superfamilies I and II, which are further classified into 25 
families based on their tertiary and quaternary structure, substrate specificity, and active site 
configuration. The main distinction between the mentioned superfamilies besides the folding patterns 
includes affiliation of superfamily I enzymes with large multifunctional complexes such as polyketide 
and fatty acid synthases, whereas superfamily II commonly comprises independent enzymes with 
broad substrate specificity (Hickman et al., 2020). The thioesterase families of particular interest in 
this project include families 1-13, as the enzymes allocated to these families hydrolyse thioester bonds 
of aliphatic and aromatic acyl-CoA with varying specificity depending on the acyl moiety (Swarbrick et 
al., 2020). 
1.4. Fatty acid catabolism in Pseudomonas putida 
As outlined in section 1.2.2, P. putida has been used as metabolic chassis for production of a plethora 
of compounds of remarkable industrial importance. However, there is also evidence of interaction of 
native catabolic routes with the engineered pathways, which drains the precursor and target 
compound pool and impairs the titers. Therefore, understanding and subsequently reducing the 
native metabolism may present a viable strategy for enhancing the desired product output (Thompson 
et al., 2020). 
Fatty acid assimilation is initiated by their activation to respective acyl-CoA thioesters upon entry into 
the cell. Once activated, acyl-CoA thioesters enter a β-oxidation cycle, in which one acetyl-CoA 
molecule is liberated and further metabolised through a glyoxylate cycle and tricarboxylic acid cycle, 
==PAGE:17==
10 
 
while the remaining two-carbon shortened acyl-CoA thioester undergoes further oxidation in the 
subsequent cycle runs. The enzymes responsible for β-oxidation include acyl-CoA dehydrogenases, 
enoyl-CoA hydratases, hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydrogenases and thiolases as depicted in Figure 5 
(Jimenez-Diaz et al., 2019). 
 
Figure 5: Fatty acid catabolism of P. putida. Fatty acids are activated to their respective thioesters by acyl-CoA synthetase. 
The acyl-CoA further undergoes three steps of β-oxidation catalysed by acyl-CoA dehydrogenase, enoyl-CoA hydratase and 
hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydrogenase. Oxidised acyl-CoA is processed by a thiolase, which releases one acetyl-CoA molecule per 
oxidation round. Acetyl-CoA subsequently enters TCA cycle or glyoxylate shunt, while propionyl-CoA released as the final 
product of odd-chain fatty acid degradation is assimilated via a methylcitrate cycle. Acyl-CoA moiety reduced by two 
carbons re-enters β-oxidation cycle. 
In P. putida, multiple homologues of all the genes involved in fatty acid degradation have been 
identified, with six acyl-CoA synthetases, seven acyl-CoA dehydrogenases, seven enoyl-CoA 
hydratases, four hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydrogenases, and five thiolases contributing to the metabolism 
of fatty acids of varying length. The overall fatty acid degradation patterns in this microorganism seem 
to follow the same route for fatty acids with 7- to 14- carbon long chains, whereas degradation of 
shorter fatty acids can undergo more independent paths as suggested by a recent functional analysis 
employing random barcode transposon sequencing (Thompson et al., 2020). Reduction of P. putida 
ability to catabolise short- and medium-chain fatty acids via functional knockouts of the most critical 
genes involved in the pathway might lead to increased product yield and protect the substrates from 
degradation.

1.5. Objective 
On the background exposed above, the general aim of this project is to provide a bio-based alternative 
to organofluorine production. In particular, and inspired by the structure of fatty acid biosynthesis, 
the specific objective of the thesis is to assemble a synthetic elongation pathway consisting of ACS, 
thiolase, and thioesterase for the conversion of FAc and butyrate into extended fluorinated carboxylic 
acids. The pathway will be expressed in an engineered P. putida strain with reduced ability to 
assimilate short- and medium-chain carboxylic acids, as it was hypothesised that functional knockouts 
of the genes involved in β-oxidation could protect the reaction substrates and products from 
degradation by the intrinsic metabolic machinery of the host. When combined, these approaches 
could give access to a number of new-to-biology building blocks containing F (Figure 6).

2.2. Bacterial transformation 
2.2.1. Transformation of chemically competent E. coli 
50 mL of chemically competent E. coli cells were gently mixed with 5 µL of plasmid and incubated on 
ice for 30 min. Transformation was carried out by heat shock in a water bath at 42°C for 30 seconds, 
after which cells were transferred back to ice for 3 minutes. 950 mL of room temperature Super 
Optimal Broth with Catabolite repression (SOC) (Hanahan, 1983) medium was subsequently added to 
the cells, followed by 1-2 h of recovery at 37°C and 250 rpm. Cells were concentrated by centrifugation 
at 4000 rpm and resuspended in 100 µL of the medium.  Concentrated cell suspensions were plated 
on selective LB agar plates supplemented with the appropriate antibiotic for the overnight incubation 
at 37°C. 
2.2.2. Transformation of electrocompetent E. coli 
100 mL of chilled sterile MQ water were added to 50 mL of prepared electrocompetent cells. If 
relevant, dialysis of the plasmid solution was carried out for 15 minutes by applying 5 µL of solution 
==PAGE:20==
13 
 
to the top of the dialysis membrane (pore size 0.025 µm, MF-Millipore) floating on the surface of a 
Petri dish filled with deionised water. 50 µL of diluted cells and 3 µL of the plasmid solution were 
transferred to a prechilled electroporation cuvette (0.1 cm Gene Pulser, Bio-Rad) and mixed gently. 
Transformation was performed by applying a pulse of 1.8 kV (Bio-Rad gene pulser Xcell). Subsequent 
cell recovery, plating and incubation are identical to the procedure described in section 2.2.1. 
2.2.3. Transformation of P. putida 
Transformation of P. putida was performed similarly to a protocol introduced in Choi et. al (2006) 
(Choi et al., 2006). 6 mL of an ON culture was equally distributed between 4 Eppendorf tubes. Cells 
were centrifuged at 11,000 g and washed twice with 1 mL of 300 mM sucrose. After the final washing 
step, the supernatant was discarded, cells were resuspended in 100 µL of 300 mM sucrose and 
concentrated in a single tube to yield the final volume of 200-300 µL of highly electrocompetent cells. 
100 µL of the cell suspension and 500 ng of plasmid were added to a prechilled electroporation cuvette 
(0.2 cm Gene Pulser, Bio-Rad) and mixed gently. Transformation was conducted via applying a 2.5 kV 
pulse, followed by addition of 1 mL of LB medium to electroporated cells for further recovery for 1-2 
hours at 30°C and 250 rpm. Cells were concentrated to the final volume of 100 µL and plated on LB 
agar plates supplemented with an appropriate selection marker. Plates were subsequently incubated 
overnight at 30°C.

2.3.1. Production and purification of proteins 
5 mL of ON cultures of E. coli BL21 (DE3) harbouring ACS expression plasmids were used to inoculate 
500 mL of 2x YT medium with appropriate antibiotic in 2 L Erlenmeyer shake flasks. The cultures were 
grown at 37°C and 150 rpm until culture OD600 reached 0.5-0.6, after which the cultures were cooled 
down to room temperature and subsequently the protein expression was induced by addition of 0.1 
mM IPTG. The induced cultures were incubated overnight at 20°C and 150 rpm. 
Upon the end of cultivation, the cultures were transferred to the centrifugation tubes and centrifuged 
at 4000 rpm and 4°C for 20 minutes. Supernatant was discarded, and pellets were resuspended in 25 
mL chilled Buffer A (300 mM NaCl, 20 mM imidazole, 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.5) and transferred to 50 mL 
Falcon tubes. The cells were lysed using EmulsiFlex-C5 (Avestin) by passing the cell suspension twice 
and adding ~10 mL of MQ water to recover the remaining lysate out of the machine. 3 µL of Pierce™ 
Universal Nuclease for Cell Lysis (Thermofisher) were added to the lysate to degrade nucleic acids and  
incubated at room temperature for 30-60 minutes on a horizontal shaker. The lysate was subsequently 
centrifuged at 4500 rpm at 4°C for 10 minutes, and supernatant was filtered through a 0.45 m syringe 
filter (LabSolute). The His-tagged proteins were purified on HisPur Ni-NTA Resin (Thermo Scientific) by 
adding 1 mL of resin to the filter disk inserted into the disposable column, washing the resin with 5 mL 
Buffer A (same buffer as used for pellet resuspension), passing the protein sample through the resin, 
washing it twice with 10 mL Buffer A and eluting the His-tagged proteins twice with 2 mL Buffer B (300 
mM NaCl, 500 mM imidazole, 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.5). Protein purification was completed by buffer 
exchange in Amicon® Ultra-15 Centrifugal Filter Unit (Merck Millipore) with nominal molecular weight 
limit of 50 kDa to remove imidasol. The protein concentration was measured by NanoDrop2000 
(Thermo Scientific). 
2.3.2. SDS-PAGE electrophoresis for protein size confirmation 
10 µL of sample mixture was prepared by combining 2.5 µL 4X 2-mercaptoethanol, 2.5 µL 4X Laemli 
sample buffer, purified protein solution containing 1 µg of protein and MQ water. The sample mixture 
was boiled at 95°C for 5 minutes and loaded on a precast Mini-PROTEAN TGX Gel (Bio-Rad 
Laboratories). 3 µL PageRuler Plus Prestained protein ladder was loaded on the gel for protein size 
identification. Samples were run at 200 V for 30 minutes, after which the gel was washed twice with 
MQ water for 10 minutes, stained with 25 mL Bio-Safe Coomassie Stain (Bio-Rad Laboratories) under 
agitation for 1 hour and washed twice with MQ water for 30 minutes. The image of the gel was taken 
by a G:Box bioimager (Syngene, UK, Cambridge).

2.3.3. Hydroxylamine assay 
The assay is based on a colorimetric method for determination of acyl phosphates (Lipmann & Tuttle, 
1945). 300 µL of ACS reaction mixture was prepared in a 96-well non-skirted PCR plate to contain 70 
mM phosphate buffer (pH 7.4), 3 mM MgCl2, 0.5 mM CoA, 30 µg of purified ACS and 20 mM of 
substrate (acetate, FAc, or butyrate). A set of controls included enzyme background absorbance 
control (without substrate), controls for background absorbance of each substrate (without enzyme) 
and control of intrinsic absorbance of the stop solution (no substrate and enzyme). All samples and 
controls were prepared in three replicates. The PCR plate was incubated at 30°C without agitation, 
and the reaction was initiated by supplementing 2 mM ATP to the prewarmed reaction mixture. 40 µL 
of sample was taken out after t0 (prior to ATP addition), 5, 10, 20, 30 and 60 minutes and transferred 
to a 96-well plate containing 40 µL 0.1 M citrate buffer (pH 5.0) and 2 M hydroxylamine (pH 6.4). After 
incubation for 10 min, 40 µL 25% HCl (acidification), 40 µL 12% trichloroacetic acid (protein 
precipitation) and 40 µL 5% FeCl3 (colour development) were added to the plate. The absorbance of 
the produced ferric hydroxamate complex was measured with a BioTek Synergy H1 microplate reader 
(BioSPX) at 510 nm.

P. putida KT2440 genes PP_3553, PP_4549, PP_2136, PP_2217, PP_3754, PP_3755 and PP_3554 were

2.4.1. Protospacer selection and primer design 
Two protospacers per gene were selected by uploading the genome sequence of P. putida KT2440 to 
https://crispy.secondarymetabolites.org/#/input and searching for a gene of interest in CRISPR-BEST 
mode with C → T deamination and displaying only STOP codons. The criteria for selection included a 
balance between maximal proximity to the 5’ end of the coding sequence, location of the deamination 
cytosine target in position 3-9 of the protospacer and avoiding guanine as an upstream nucleotide to 
the target cytosine. To minimise possible off-target modifications, only protospacers with no 0 bp 
mismatches and the least possible number of 1 and 2 bp mismatches in the genome were considered 
to the extent that allowed fulfilment of the former three criteria. Forward primers for base editing 
plasmid construction were designed to contain (5’-3’): a random extension for digestion, BsaI (Eco31I) 
cut site, specific standardised overhang for Golden Gate assembly, the spacer sequence and gRNA 
scaffold binding site for amplification. Reverse standard primers were available in house and contain 
(5’-3’): random extension for digestion, BsaI (Eco31I) cut site, specific standardised overhang for

Golden Gate assembly and a Csy4 binding site for amplification. Sequencing primers were designed to 
flank the editing loci with a distance of approx. 250 bp. 
2.4.2. Construction of a base editing plasmid 
sgRNAs were prepared by PCR amplification of the gRNA-Csy4 construct from plasmid pEX128-
gRNAtemp with Phusion HS DNA polymerase (Thermofisher) using primers described in 2.4.1. Once

combining 2 µL sgRNA mix, 100 ng pBEC-UGI-Csy4, 1 µL 10X T4 ligase buffer, 0.5 µL of each T4 DNA 
ligase (Thermofisher) and Eco31I restriction enzyme (Thermofisher), and 5 µL MQ water. 5 µL of the 
reaction mixture was used to transform chemically competent E. coli DH5a. Non-fluorescent colonies 
were checked by colony PCR using primers PR1 and PR3, and PCR fragments displaying correct size on 
the 1% agarose gel electrophoresis were sent for sequencing to verify the assembly. Clones bearing 
correct constructs were inoculated in liquid medium for subsequent plasmid miniprep by NucleoSpin 
Plasmid Mini kit for plasmid DNA (Macherey-Nagel). 
2.4.3. Base editing in P. putida

µL 10X ligase buffer, 1 µL USER enzyme and MQ water. The reaction was performed in a thermocycler

2.5.5. Characterisation of a synthetic elongation pathway 
Resolved Level 2 plasmids were used to transform P. putida WT and strain β3. 10 mL LB precultures 
were supplemented with Sm (except for control stains carrying no plasmid) and Gm and grown for 36 
hours at 30°C and 250 rpm. 50 mL LB liquid cultures with Sm (where appropriate) and Gm were 
inoculated to OD 0.1 and grown in 250 mL shake flasks at 30°C and 200 rpm until OD reached 0.4-0.6, 
after which transcription of synthetic elongation pathway was induced by addition of 15 mM NaF. 
Following induction, the cultures were further incubated under the same conditions for 20 hours. At 
the end of the incubation period, cells were harvested by centrifugation at 4500 rpm and 4°C for 10 
minutes, washed twice with 10 mL of de Bont medium without carbon source and resuspended in 3 
mL of 50 mM phosphate buffer (pH 7.8). The concentrated cell suspension was aliquoted in three 2 
mL cryotubes with approximately 0.3 mg of glass beads (0.5 mm, acid washed, Sigma-Aldrich), and 
cells were broken down by bead beating twice at 6000 rpm for 20 seconds in a Precellys 24 tissue 
homogenizer (Bertin Instruments). Cell extract was collected by centrifuging the tubes at 13000 rpm 
and 4°C for 3 minutes and filtering the supernatant through 0.45 µm syringe filters to remove any 
remaining particulate matter. Total protein concentration was quantified by a Bradford assay by 
adding 250 µL of Quick Start Bradford 1x Dye Reagent (Bio-Rad) to 10 µL of cell extract, incubation for 
5 minutes and measuring the sample absorbance at 595 nm in a BioTek Synergy H1 microplate reader. 
The protein concentration was derived from a standard curve prepared with serial dilutions of 2 
mg/mL Bovine Serum Albumin; absorbance readings were obtained via the same procedure as 
described above. Reactions for characterisation of the synthetic elongation pathway along with a 
control containing no cell extract was set up in triplicates to contain 50 mM phosphate buffer (pH 7.8), 
2 mM ATP, 3 mM MgCl2, 0.5 mM CoA, 70 µg/mL of total protein in a cell extract, and 20 mM of each 
FAc and butyrate. The reactions were incubated at 30°C without shaking over 16 hours. Substrate 
consumption of samples taken at t0, after 2 hours and 16 hours was monitored via HPLC analysis by a 
Waters Associates chromatographic system (Millipore), comprising a gradient controller (Model 680), 
pump (Model 510), an automatic injector (Model 712), and two Aminex HPX-87H columns (300 x 7.8 
mm) connected in series. The samples were isocratically eluted with 5 mM sulfuric acid at a flow rate 
of 0.6 mL/min for 30 min at column temperature of 50°C. The eluted compounds were detected by a 
UV detector (Model 481) at 205 and 254 nm. The data was analysed by a Chromeleon Chromatography 
Data System (Thermo Fisher Scientific).

3. Results and discussion 
3.1. Selection of ACS for a synthetic elongation pathway 
3.1.1. Characteristics of ACS chosen for screening 
The first reaction of the synthetic elongation pathway designed in this project includes activation of 
FAc and butyrate to their respective thioesters by ACS enzymes. Ten ACS from six prokaryotic bacterial 
strains and one eukaryotic yeast strain were selected for further screening Figure 7. Each enzyme 
belongs to the EC:6.2.1.1 class, meaning that it ligates acetate and CoA at the expense of one ATP 
molecule, releasing AMP and diphosphate as a result. There is also evidence that this class of enzymes 
is natively capable of catalysing propionate-CoA ligation (Patel & Walt, 1987) . 
 
Figure 7: Phylogenetic tree of the ACS selected for this project, generated using Clustal Omega Multiple Sequence Alignment 
tool. 
The enzymes represented in three main clades, where the first one unites a rather taxonomically 
diverse group of Bacillus subtilis, Cupriavidus necator and Streptomyces coelicolor, the second one 
encompasses two enzymes from a yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae, while the third includes ACS from 
the relatively taxonomically close Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Pseudomonas putida and Escherichia coli. 
All ACS show the most variability at the N terminus, meanwhile the sites responsible for ATP, AMP, 
CoA, and cofactor binding located towards the C-terminal region are better conserved.   
All the pETDuet plasmids, with IPTG- inducible T7 promoter carrying ACS, were constructed in house 
prior to project initiation. The size and the purity of the purified proteins were evaluated by SDS-PAGE 
gel electrophoresis (Figure 8).

Figure 8: ACS size and purity confirmation by SDS-PAGE gel electrophoresis. The table enlists identifiers of ACS used further

Most of the proteins showed bands at their expected size with varying degree of purity depending on 
the enzyme. Two ACS, acsS coe and acsC.necI, demonstrated bands above expected sizes of 73 and 74 kDa, 
respectively, which can be attributed to post-translational modifications, incomplete protein

preparation phase. However, further assays confirmed that all these proteins were indeed ACS, as 
they were active towards a range of substrates demonstrating carboxylic acid conversion to respective 
acyl-CoA compounds (section 3.1.3).  
3.1.2. Hydroxylamine assay development and optimisation 
Most assays for ACS activity determination described in the literature involve colorimetric assessment 
of product formation, and one of these methods, the hydroxylamine assay, was selected to evaluate 
activity of the screened enzymes. 
The hydroxylamine method was initially developed for quantifying acyl phosphates in biological 
samples through a two-step reaction for detection of acid anhydrides. In the first reaction, acyl moiety 
of an acyl phosphate reacts with hydroxylamine leading to hydroxamic acid formation, while the 
second reaction involves the formation of a bright purple complex between hydroxamic acid and a 
ferric ion under acidic conditions. Experimental steps involve adding test sample to a slightly acidic 
concentrated solution of hydroxylamine, which after ten minutes of incubation at room temperature, 
fully converts acyl phosphates to hydroxamic acid. The reaction solution is further acidified by 
hydrochloric acid, proteins are precipitated by trichloroacetic acid, and colour is developed upon 
addition of a 5% ferric chloride solution. Absorbance of the brownish orange to brightly purple ferric-
hydroxamate complex formed in this reaction is measured at 480-540 nm, where ferric chloride does 
==PAGE:30==
23 
 
not absorb. Absorbance readings were reported to be comparable for acetyl, propionyl and butyryl 
moieties (Lipmann & Tuttle, 1945), thus it was decided to apply this method to acyl thioester 
quantification. In the context of this project, F-acetyl--CoA and butyryl-CoA thioesters formed as a 
result of ACS activity would react with hydroxylamine and subsequently be converted into F-acetyl- 
and butytylhydroxamic acids, with CoA released in its free form (Figure 9). Further acidification and 
addition of the ferric chloride would result in the formation of a ferric hydroxamate complex in the 
same fashion as in the original assay. 
 
Figure 9: Expected F-acetyl- and butyrylhydroxamic acid formation reaction from respective thioesters. 
The first and the optimised assay setups are depicted in Figure 10. Initially, 0.1 M acetate buffer at pH 
5.0 was used to keep the hydroxylamine solution slightly acidic to inhibit ACS activity during acyl 
hydroxamate formation, as it was previously reported that ACS show a greatly reduced activity at pH 
below 6.0 (Cao et al., 2016; R. Li et al., 2011). The absorbance of a ferric hydroxamate complex was 
measured at 490 nm. 
 
Figure 10: Hydroxylamine assay setup. Text in red indicates optimisation parameters. 
Despite assumed inactivity of ACS in acidic acetate buffer, it became apparent that the enzymes 
reacted with the buffer during the incubation period required for conversion of hydroxylamine to 
==PAGE:31==
24 
 
hydroxamate, since a high background absorbance was observed in enzyme controls containing no 
substrate. Consumption of acetate from the buffer was observed to be similar to ACS activity when 
acetate was used as a reaction substate. Additionally, high background absorbance for controls 
containing no enzyme served as an indication that selected detection wavelength was not optimal for 
the method (Figure 11). Therefore, the assay was revised and optimised to prevent buffer 
consumption by the enzymes during incubation time with hydroxylamine and to reduce background 
absorbance of the non-reacted ferric chloride. 
 
Figure 11: Example of a high background absorbance in acetate buffer for acsB.sub and its controls when absorbance was 
measured at 490 nm. 
In order to eliminate buffer interference with the assayed reaction, the acetate buffer was substituted 
with 0.1 M citrate buffer at pH 5.0, as there is no reported evidence that this organic acid is an ACS 
substrate. Moreover, absorbance wavelength for detection of ferric hydroxamate complex was 
changed to 510 nm following experimental determination of optimal absorbance in the range of 480-
540 nm. The absorbance wavelength selection was further supported by a similar study assaying ACS 
activity towards non-native substrates via the same method, where wavelength of 510 nm was 
identified as the most suitable for the purpose (Mouterde & Stewart, 2019). These optimisation 
attempts enabled a more precise identification of the thioester product formation and allowed to 
draw conclusions on the ACS candidates with both high substrate promiscuity and activity for further 
construction of the synthetic elongation pathway.

The optimized hydroxylamine assay allowed to track the conversion of acetate, FAc and butyrate by 
the selected ACS and shed some light on their activity. Absorbance of the samples at 510 nm was

All ACS demonstrated moderate activity towards acetate. The highest registered acetyl-CoA

enzymes did not show a particularly high affinity towards butyrate, with reaction rates much lower 
than for the other two tested substrates.  
Therefore, taking into consideration the ability of the screened ACS to convert non-native substrates, 
four enzymes were further selected for the synthetic elongation pathway library. acsP.aerA was included 
in the list based on its ability to efficiently convert both non-native substrates. acsB. sub and acsE.col were 
chosen due to great conversion of FAc, even though they did not display a particularly high butyrate 
transformation efficiency, while acsS.coe was selected due to its affinity towards butyrate.  
3.1.4. Possible improvements and alternative methods to measure activity of ACS 
The hydroxylamine assay is a chemical discontinuous method to measure the acyl-CoA product 
formation, and it was selected in this project owing to a quite straightforward procedure and clear 
prospects for optimisation. Further method optimisations that were observed in the literature could 
include reducing the hydroxylamine concentration to 600 mM and incorporating it in the reaction 
mixture rather than in the stop solution, along with skipping the protein precipitation step and 
proceeding only with equal volumes of hydrochloric acid and ferric chloride for acidification and colour 
development, respectively (Ingram-Smith et al., 2006; Mouterde & Stewart, 2019). The benefit of such 
setup includes less dilution of the ferric-hydroxamate complex (3-fold vs 6-fold in the original method) 
permitting more sensitivity at lower substrate conversion efficiencies, as well as a more accurate time 
==PAGE:34==
27 
 
point registration, as there was evidence that enzymes were still active during the ten minutes of 
incubation required for hydroxamic acid formation.  ACS activity can also be indirectly determined by 
coupling other reaction product formation to activity of accessory enzymes, allowing continuous 
measurements over extended periods of time and avoiding quite a laborious sampling procedure. One 
such method includes coupling of AMP release to NADH oxidation via myokinase, pyruvate kinase and 
lactate dehydrogenase enzymatic cascade, where NADH consumption is monitored by measuring 
decrease in absorbance at 340 nm. Myokinase converts two molecules of AMP into ADP, pyruvate 
kinase consumes ADP and phosphoenolpyruvate to yield ATP and pyruvate, while lactate 
dehydrogenase consumes pyruvate and NADH, releasing lactate and oxidised NAD+ (Sofeo et al., 
2019). It is possible that the employment of these methods could lead to a different outcome when 
screening selected ACS for activity towards the non-native substrates, however, the assay applied in 
this project still permitted to make quantitative evaluation of the enzyme activity. 
3.2. Engineering P. putida towards blocking fatty acid catabolism 
It was hypothesised that reduction of the innate β-oxidation pathway of the short- and medium-chain 
fatty acids could lead to increased product yield of the synthetic elongation pathway due to 
diminished substrate and product degradation, therefore, several P. putida genes involved in fatty 
acid catabolism were selected for functional knockout construction. A high throughput approach 
based on a CRISPR-Cas9 system was utilised, which allows multiplexed introduction of in-frame STOP 
codons into the desired genes. The base editing tool was developed in our research group for genetic 
modification of P. putida (Volke et al., manuscript in preparation). In this method, the Cas9n (D10A) 
enzyme, which has a nickase activity, is guided towards a desired modification site by sgRNA, where 
it cuts one of the DNA strands upon recognition of a PAM-protospacer sequence. Subsequently, 
cytidine deaminase fused to Cas9n catalyses conversion of a cytosine to uridine in the base editing 
window, leading to conversion of a G:C base pair into A:T. As the PAM sequence NGG recognised by 
Cas9n is very abundant in the genome, it is possible to introduce STOP codons in virtually any gene. 
Multiplexing is enabled by Csy4-based sgRNA processing machinery allowing modification of multiple 
targets at the same time (Tong et al., 2019).  
3.2.1. Selection of genes for construction of multiple functional knockout strains 
The choice of genes for functional knockout construction was based on a study conducted by 
Thompson et al. (2020), where genes of fatty acid catabolism were assigned a global fitness score 
through construction of the random barcoded transposon mutant library and its subsequent 
cultivation on various fatty acid substrates in minimal medium (Thompson et al., 2020). Genes 
displaying negative fitness scores when grown on C4-C8 fatty acid substrates were of particular

interest for this project, as their expression could potentially interfere with the activity of the synthetic 
elongation pathway.  
Seven genes were selected for functional knockout construction, including two acyl-CoA synthetases 
(PP_3553 and PP_4549), one acyl-CoA dehydrogenase (PP_3755), two enoyl-CoA hydratases (PP_2217 
and PP_2136, where the latter one was also assigned hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydrogenase activity), one 
hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydrogenase (PP_3755) and one thiolase (PP_3754) as enlisted in Table 5. From

C6-C20 fatty acids. Thiolase PP_3755 seems to enable butyrate degradation to yield two acetyl-CoA

3.2.2. Design and construction of base editing plasmids 
The genes for construction of multiple functional knockout strains were separated into two batches 
as designated in Table 5 to reduce possible toxic burden potentially brought upon the modified cells 
by inactivation of multiple potentially essential genes. It was also decided to aim at introduction of 
two STOP codons per gene to increase the probability of base modification events and to ensure 
functional knockout persistence in the event of reversion to the WT gene. Therefore, two plasmids 
further referred as B1 and B2 were designed and constructed to carry eight and six sgRNA, 
respectively.  
Plasmid construction was initiated by selecting two appropriate protospacers per gene with the aid of 
https://crispy.secondarymetabolites.org/#/input tool in a C → T deamination mode with STOP codon 
introduction (Blin et al., 2016). The selection criteria for a perfect protospacer included: a maximal 
proximity to the 5’ end of the coding sequence, the presence of cytosine in position 3-9 of the 
protospacer (where position 1 is the most distal from PAM), the absence of a guanine right upstream 
==PAGE:36==
29 
 
of edited cytosine as it decreases deamination efficiency (Tong et al., 2019), and a minimal number of 
possible off-target hits with 1 and 2 bp mismatches. Protospacers that repeat in the genome sequence 
without any mismatches were not considered. Despite those quite stringent selection criteria, it was 
possible to select two protospacers with all the desired properties for most of the genes. It appeared 
quite challenging to find two good protospacers for genes PP_3554 and PP_3754, therefore, one of 
the protospacers for each gene contained a guanine base upstream of the targeted cytosine. This 
limitation should not impair the functional knockout of the mentioned genes, as the second 
protospacer in the pair was matching all the required parameters.   
 
Figure 13: Workflow for base editing plasmid construction. 
The selected protospacers were incorporated in the design of forward primers which also contain a 
BsaI restriction site, specific overhang for Golden Gate assembly and gRNA binding region for 
amplification from the template plasmid. These forward primers together with standard reverse 
primers binding Csy4 were used to generate a sgRNA library that was further employed to construct 
two base editing plasmids by a Golden Gate assembly with eight (Plasmid B1) and six (Plasmid B2) 
sgRNA, respectively (Figure 13). 
3.2.3. Base editing in P. putida 
Verified base editing plasmids B1 and B2 were separately transformed in P. putida WT strain with the 
aim to obtain strains β1 and β2.  After base editing was completed, the plasmids were cured by 
growing cells on LB agar plates supplemented with 5% sucrose, inducing the expression of sacB on the 
base editing plasmids. This leads to the hydrolysis of sucrose and synthesis of high molecular weight 
fructose polymers known as levans, which exhibit a toxic effect on P. putida, and therefore, selecting 
the cells that cured their plasmid and survived (Pelicic et al., 1996).

Ten plasmid-free isolates were screened for STOP codon integration in the coding sequence of the 
selected genes. Sequencing confirmed 100% editing efficiency for sgRNA encoded in the plasmid B1 
and targeting genes PP_4549, PP_2136 and PP_2217, with two STOP codons per gene introduced in 
every screened isolate. Moreover, for most of the sgRNA, more cytosine bases in positions 3-9 were

efficient tool for construction of multiple functional knockout strains, however, it should be noted that 
the attained modifications might not persist over time. Careful protospacer design and thorough 
mutant isolation should ensure modification success for most non-essential genes.

3.3. Construction of a synthetic elongation pathway 
3.3.1. Enzyme selection for a synthetic elongation pathway 
It was hypothesised that the synthetic elongation pathway would allow expansion of fluorinated 
building block landscape through Claisen condensation of FAc with other short carboxylic acids, which 
are exemplified by butyrate in this project. In this pathway, FAc and butyrate would be first activated 
to their respective CoA thioesters by an ACS with a broad substrate range, which would be further 
condensed by a thiolase, and subsequently the products would be released by a thioesterase. The 
products of the elongation reaction may include C6 monofluorinated carboxylic acids with linear or 
branched chain depending on the substrate binding pattern of a thiolase, or C4 difluorinated 
compounds arising from the condensation of two F-acetyl-CoA molecules (Figure 17). As the substrate 
specificity must have been determined experimentally for all employed enzyme classes, several ACS, 
thiolases and thioesterases were selected for the construction of the synthetic elongation pathway 
library carrying combinations of the chosen genes. While in-house ACS were screened in this project 
==PAGE:42==
35 
 
prior to pathway construction, thiolases and thioesterases were selected based on their catalytic 
properties described in the literature. 
 
Figure 17: Reactions of the synthetic elongation pathway proposed in this project. FAc and butyrate are first converted into 
their respective thioesters by ACS, which are subsequently condensed by a thiolase. The reaction products might include C6 
monofluorinated compounds with linear or branched chain if F-acetyl-CoA and butyryl-CoA are condensed, as well as C4 
difluorinated molecules if two F-acetyl-CoA molecules accepted as substrates. Fluorinated carboxylic acids with extended 
chain would be released from their thioester forms by a thioesterase.  
Five thiolases from such microorganisms as Cupriavidus necator, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Thermus 
thermophilus and Clostridium acetobutylicum were selected for the library construction, as most of 
them were available in-house. Two thiolases from C. necator known as PhaA and BktB promote 
synthesis of PHA in this microorganism. PhaA is regarded as a biosynthetic thiolase that exclusively 
accepts acetyl-CoA as a substrate, while BktB was observed to possess a dual degradative and 
biosynthetic activity with a broader substrate range including propionyl-CoA and butyryl-CoA (Fage et 
al., 2015; Mifune et al., 2010). Biosynthetic thiolase Erg10 from S. cerevisiae natively promotes only 
two acetyl-CoA unit condensation. It has been reported that substrate binding pocket engineering 
efforts expanded its substrate pool to also include butyryl-CoA, thus enabling synthesis of branched 
compounds (Torres-Salas et al., 2018), however, the wild-type enzyme was used for pathway library 
construction in this project. The same study also described T. thermophilus degradative thiolase 
tt0182, which could accept both acetyl-CoA and butyryl-CoA as substrates. The last thiolase in the 
library includes biosynthetic thlA from C. acetobutylicum, which catalyses acetyl-CoA condensation 
(Stim-Herndon et al., 1995). 
Thiolases have been previously reported to exhibit a thermodynamical preference towards the 
reverse reaction under standard conditions, which could lead to product removal back to the substrate 
==PAGE:43==
36 
 
pool shortly after condensation. Therefore, it is of critical importance to select an appropriate 
thioesterase that would remove the thioester bonds from the newly condensed molecules to ensure 
that reaction proceeds only in the forward direction (Fage et al., 2015). A total of four thioesterases 
displaying specificity towards a broad range of acyl thioesters were chosen for the library construction 
in this project. Two TesB thioesterases from P. putida and E. coli were chosen based on their 
preference towards C5-C10 saturated and branched fatty acid thioesters as opposed to C2-C4 
compounds (McMahon & Prathera, 2014). Thioesterase YciA from E. coli was selected due to a report 
indicating increased 3-hydroxybutyrate yield upon overexpression of YciA in the host strain (Guevara-
Martínez et al., 2019). Selection of this enzyme might appear as an ambiguous choice in the context 
of this project, as its substrate pool includes acetyl-CoA, acetoacetyl-CoA, and butyryl-CoA (McMahon 
& Prathera, 2014), however, it was still included in the thioesterase library to observe whether its 
activity could positively affect production of C4 fluorinated carboxylic acids. The last selected 
thioesterase FcbC from Arthrobacter sp. Strain AU primarily hydrolyses a thioester bond of 4-
hydroxybenzoyl-CoA, while also showing some specificity towards short-chain fatty acid thioesters 
(Hickman et al., 2020).  
To conclude, a total of 13 genes with variable substrate specificities were included in the library for a 
synthetic elongation pathway construction. They were further combined to generate an ACS-thiolase-
thioesterase pathway library encoded in a single operon through Golden Gate assembly. 
3.3.2. Golden Gate assembly: design of a cloning strategy 
Golden Gate cloning strategy was selected for the construction of the synthetic elongation pathway 
as this method enables de novo modular plasmid assembly from basic parts stored as standardized 
plasmids, called Level 0. All the essential elements can be preselected for vector assembly, including 
promoters, RBS, coding sequences, terminators, ori and antibiotic resistance cassettes, thus allowing 
simple plasmid customisation in terms of transcription efficiency and copy number. Hierarchical 
assembly with increasing complexity proceeding to Level 2 and further is enabled by alternating type 
IIs restriction enzymes BsaI and BsmBI, whose activity generates 4-nucleotide long overhangs for 
specific fusion between parts in the correct order. Construction of operons and plasmids carrying 
multiple transcription units is possible by incorporating 5’ and 3’ connector parts, which can form 
overhangs after digestion, linking transcriptional units to the antibiotic resistance cassette, to each 
other, or to an ori part of the final vector. The assembly protocol encompasses cycling between 
digestion and ligation (T4 DNA ligase), and a long final digestion to dispose of the background noise 
created by antibiotic resistance vector in a single pot reaction, with no preliminary basic part 
treatment required (Stukenberg et al., 2021). The Marburg collection of parts for Vibrio natriegens 
(Stukenberg et al., 2021) was supplemented with genetic elements for P. putida by members of the 
==PAGE:44==
37 
 
Systems Environmental Microbiology group over the last year and applied here to generate a library 
of vectors harbouring the synthetic elongation pathway in this project. 
 
Figure 18: Golden Gate cloning strategy for assembly of the synthetic elongation pathway. Level 0 plasmids are created to 
store individual basic elements for assembly of more complex constructs. Level 0 parts digested by BsaI are used to form 
operon units for ACS, thiolase and thioesterase modules of the synthetic elongation pathway. All Level 1 operon units 
include RBS, respective gene, and specific connectors that would allow correct unit ligation in the subsequent cloning round. 
Additionally, ACS module carries T7 promoter, while the operon unit for thioesterase contains T7 terminator sequence. Level 
1 plasmids are digested by BsmBI to release the operon units that would be finally ligated into a complete Level 2 plasmid 
carrying the entire synthetic elongation pathway operon, RK2 ori, and SmR cassette. 
It was decided to express ACS, thiolase and thioesterase in a single operon under control of T7 
promoter with individual RBS prior to each gene for a high-level transcription and translation of the 
target proteins. Such promoter choice was motivated by a presence of a NaF-responsive riboswitch 
incorporated into P. putida genome, controlling the translation of a T7 RNA polymerase for orthogonal 
gene expression. Besides offering a tight inducible regulation over the expression (Calero et al., 2020), 
such a genetic circuit integrated in the host would theoretically allow merging de novo fluorination 
and elongation pathways in the future. Bicistronic design elements (BCD) of varying strength were 
selected as RBS for this project to alleviate possible translation issues associated with a high level of 
pathway transcription and formation of mRNA secondary structures (Claassens et al., 2019). Design of

the Golden Gate cloning strategy is presented in Figure 18. The final Level 2 plasmids would comprise 
of an operon carrying a T7 promoter, homologues of ACS, thiolase, and thioesterase, and a T7 
terminator. 
3.3.3. Golden Gate assembly: challenges and optimisation 
Assembly of Level 0 parts carrying coding sequences for operon unit construction proceeded according 
to the plan for most of genes. Three of the selected genes including acsE.col and thiolases BktB and 
tt0182 contained BsaI/BsmBI restriction sites that had to be removed via a Quick Change approach 
prior to assembly. A single nucleotide mutation for a cut site removal was successful for the ACS gene, 
while the amplification of the parental vectors for the thiolases caused some issues, as the 8-kb 
backbone fragments could not be amplified by PCR even upon supplementation of DMSO and using a 
gradient of annealing temperatures in the PCR cycling protocol. By chance, it was possible to assemble 
a Level 0 vector for BktB despite a BsmBI restriction site present in the gene, so this plasmid was 
further employed as a template for a site-directed mutagenesis. As Level 0 vectors are much smaller 
in size (2-4 kb) leading to a higher chance of successful PCR amplification, eventual cut site removal 
and subsequent Level 1 plasmid assembly was achieved for this thiolase, however, very late in the 
project, leading to BktB exclusion from the thiolase library. Removal of the two BsmBI cut sites from 
tt0182 was planned to be achieved in a similar fashion as for BktB, by cloning it directly into Level 1 
vector to bypass degradation of the gene, however, time constrains prevented execution of this 
cloning and mutagenesis strategy. As a consequence, this gene was not further considered for the 
synthetic elongation pathway construction. 
Level 1 plasmid assembly yielded all operon units for ACS and thioesterases, while cloning of thiolases 
was more challenging, with only BktB, PhaA and Erg10 modules obtained by the end of the project. 
An interesting yet completely wrong assembly pattern was repeatedly observed for thiolase ThlA from 
C. acetobutylicum. Whenever colony PCR was performed on non-fluorescent clones, it would identify 
a fragment of around 900 bp in size as opposed to expected 2190 bp. After sequencing of this 
fragment, it became apparent that KanR cassette got misligated to the ori part in the inverted 
conformation and once more from the wrong side at least once, which would justify the 900 bp 
amplification band on the agarose gel electrophoresis. That would mean that at least one pair of 
overhangs was mismatched with only 3 out of 4 complementary base pairs ligated as intended (Figure 
19). A high degree of promiscuity of T4 DNA ligase has been characterised before, with described 
tolerance towards pyrimidine-pyrimidine and wobble-stabilising mismatches such as T:G observed in 
this project (Lohman et al., 2016). Up to date it remains unclear why such unlikely mismatched 
assembly is preferred for a construct that cannot serve as a template for transcription due to absence 
of a promoter, and consequently cannot produce a protein with a toxic activity for the transformation 
==PAGE:46==
39 
 
host. In the end, no transformants carrying correct constructs were identified, which further reduced 
thiolase library to only three representatives, two of which were used to construct the entire 
elongation pathway.

Assembly of the final constructs in Level 2 plasmids proved to be the most challenging stage of the 
Golden Gate approach. By this point, four ACS and thioesterase units along with two thiolase modules 
were ready for the cloning, resulting in a total of 32 constructs that were attempted to be assembled 
in a combinatorial fashion. Only seven transformations with the products of the assembly reactions 
provided colourless clones, which upon investigation seemed to resemble constructs previously 
observed for ThlA Level 1 plasmids. This hypothesis was confirmed by the colony PCR, which only 
displayed bands on the agarose gel when two reverse primers binding in RK2 and SmR cassettes were 
used for the PCR reaction, while amplification with the standard primer pair did not yield any results. 
A few optimisation strategies involving extension of digestion and ligation time, increasing the number 
of cycles, stepwise assembly of parts, where SmR cassette vector was added at the end of the assembly 
reaction for 20 cycles to promote correct SmR incorporation, and decreasing the concentration of 
parts, did not yield any colourless clones. Competency of the chemically competent E. coli cells was 
also questioned, but it deemed non-critical as the transformation of the highly electrocompetent cells 
lead to the same observations.  
While trying to propagate some of the colourless clones in the liquid LB medium for plasmid 
extraction, no growth was observed in multiple cultures, which hinted that the standard Sm 
concentration of 100 µg/mL might be too restrictive for survival of the clones carrying such a low-copy 
plasmid as RK2. It was also hypothesised that the challenges with the assembly might be attributed to 
impaired T4 DNA ligase activity. Therefore, a reaction containing a pool of transcription units was run 
under a standard protocol as depicted in Table 4 with or without enzyme deactivation step, after 
which the reactions were held at 16°C overnight. Chemically competent E. coli cells were transformed

3.3.4. Characterisation of a synthetic elongation pathway by monitoring substrate 
consumption via HPLC 
Five out of eight constructs including acsE.col-Erg10-TesBP.put, acsS.coe-PhaA-FcbC, acsS.coe-PhaA-TesBE.col, 
acsP.aerA-Erg10-YciA and acsE.col-Erg10-FcbC were selected to evaluate their activity towards FAc and 
butyrate as the substrates. A peak of high intensity was recorded after 6 minutes at 205 nm for each 
sample that persisted when the detection wavelength was changed to 254 nm, where carboxylic acids 
were not observed. This peak was interpreted as a possible CoA or ATP elution, as these compounds 
contain an adenosine group whose UV absorbance is routinely measured at this wavelength (Tsuchiya 
et al., 2014). The identity of the peaks was confirmed by running CoA and ATP standards, however, it 
was also noted that these compounds elute at almost identical time, thus, they are further referred 
as CoA/ATP due to inability to distinguish them on chromatograms. Another peak of a relatively high 
intensity was detected after 13 minutes at 205 nm, which also displayed a signal when the wavelength 
was switched to 254 nm. The smaller shoulder fused to the mentioned peak at 12.5 minutes did not 
display UV absorbance at 254 nm. Since FAc standards eluted at 12.5 minutes, this large peak was 
identified as a possible mixture of FAc and F-acetyl-CoA, which is further regarded as FAc as these 
peaks cannot be separated for a quantitative data analysis. Butyrate peak was identified after 21.5 
minutes, however, it would demonstrate a reduced baseline separation and elution time shift in some 
samples. No additional peaks that could indicate fluorinated product formation were recorded over 
30 minutes of sample elution. 
==PAGE:49==
42 
 
 
Figure 22: Difference between the HPLC peak areas for FAc, butyrate and assumed CoA/ATP compounds in the beginning of 
reaction and after 16 hours of incubation. 
Cell extracts of WT and strain β3 controls carrying no constructs demonstrated some intrinsic activity 
towards all the substrates except butyrate, however, the peak areas for FAc were much lower 
compared to other samples (Figure 22). Consumption of assumed CoA/ATP compounds was also 
lower, which would align with gradual CoA recycling and a slow ATP hydrolysis in a reversible low-rate 
F-acetyl-CoA formation reaction. Reduction of the FAc peaks over time could be possibly attributed to 
degradation of F-acetyl-CoA if this intermediate product was not further consumed by other enzymes. 
A general trend across all the samples includes quite a high reduction of substrate concentration over 
16 hours. While acsE.col-Erg10-FcbC show a great decrease of FAc peak both in WT and strain β3, the 
overall substrate consumption pattern resembles the controls, which might indicate that the reaction 
does not proceed further than F-acetyl-CoA formation. A general tendency of some constructs such 
as acsS.coe-PhaA-TesBE.col and acsS.coe-PhaA-FcbC towards elevated CoA/ATP and FAc consumption could 
mean that thiolase manages to condense two F-acetyl-CoA units, however, it is hard to draw any 
definite conclusions when no unusual peaks are observed in the chromatograms recorded after 16 
hours. A fate of the butyrate consumed by a few constructs is also yet to be determined for the same 
reason, but it cannot be excluded that this compound is incorporated into extended carboxylic acids 
that elute outside of the detection time. It would be hard to record butyrate degradation by the β-
==PAGE:50==

oxidation through the emergence of the acetate peak, as it would most likely be rapidly consumed by 
the pathway enzymes and converted into acetoacetyl-CoA. 
Variability in t0 peaks for FAc as depicted in Figure 23 could be explained by a difference in activity of 
the ACS enzyme and specifics the sample preparation procedure, as the samples were frozen shortly 
after ATP addition. This would allow a narrow window for F-acetyl-CoA formation, which was observed 
to proceed within minutes from the beginning of the reaction earlier in this project. However, variable 
initial peak areas were recorded for the same ACS, and it remains unclear whether it was a 
consequence of the thiolase and thioesterase cascade activity consuming the intermediate products 
early in the reaction or variable pathway enzyme concentrations produced after NaF induction.

Therefore, no definite conclusion regarding activity of the synthetic elongation pathway can be drawn 
based on the presented data except that most of constructs consume the reaction substrates at a 
greater rate than the background strains alone. Longer elution time and supplementing HPLC analysis 
of substrate consumption with mass spectrometry or nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy 
studies of the product formation could shed more light on the pathway activity.

4. Conclusion 
This project focused on expansion of organofluorine building block landscape through construction of 
the synthetic elongation pathway inspired by a fatty acid biosynthesis. The pathway comprising of 
ACS, thiolase and thioesterase was designed for extension of FAc with other short carboxylic acids, 
exemplified by butyrate in this project, to yield a range of C4 and C6 fluorinated compounds. In the 
first stage of the thesis, the activity of ten ACS was assayed by the hydroxylamine method to determine 
enzyme candidates capable of converting both reaction substrates into their respective CoA 
thioesters. Four enzymes including acsP.aerA, acsB. sub, acsE.col and acsS.coe showed the highest affinity 
towards non-native substrates and were selected for further construction of the synthetic elongation 
pathway library. In addition to ACS, five thiolases and four thioesterases were chosen for the pathway 
construction. The plasmids carrying combinations of the selected genes were assembled by a Golden 
Gate approach, and a total of eight constructs were obtained after the last cloning round. Additionally, 
three P. putida strains β1, β2 and β3 with reduced ability to catabolise short- and medium-chain fatty 
acids were constructed, as it was hypothesised to protect reaction substrates and products from 
degradation by the intrinsic metabolic machinery of the host. Functional knockout of seven genes 
responsible for fatty acid activation, β-oxidation, and breakdown was achieved with the aid of a 
CRISPR-Cas9n base-editing tool for P. putida, which introduces in-frame STOP codons in the coding 
sequence by cytosine deamination. All constructed strains showed reduced ability to assimilate 
butyrate as the sole carbon source, while strains β1 and β3 also demonstrated impaired growth on 
valerate and octanoate when compared to the wild-type strain. The synthetic elongation pathway 
library was expressed in P. putida wild-type and strain β3, and the activity of the pathway was 
monitored by measuring substrate consumption in a cell-free extract system by HPLC. Removal of 
FAc/F-acetyl-CoA and CoA/ATP pools was recorded for all assayed constructs, however, no new peaks 
were observed after 30 minutes of elution. No significant activity towards butyrate was detected. Co-
elution of multiple reaction substrates posed a barrier to drawing quantitative conclusions on the 
activity of the expressed enzymes. 
4.1. Future perspectives 
This project was an early step towards the establishment of fluorinated carboxylic acid production in 
P. putida, and multiple optimisation steps can still be implemented to ensure viability of the pathway. 
Firstly, other ACS with activity towards FAc and short carboxylic acids can be screened for efficient 
substrate conversion into respective acyl-CoA thioesters, or alternatively, rational protein engineering 
can be employed to shift enzyme affinity from acetate towards non-conventional substrates (Sofeo et 
al., 2019). Degradative thiolases with a known activity towards acyl-CoA thioesters other than solely 
acetyl-CoA can be employed to ensure Claisen condensation of the reaction substrates, or protein 
==PAGE:52==
45 
 
engineering of more active biosynthetic thiolases can be used to expand the substrate binding pocket 
of these enzymes (Torres-Salas et al., 2018). Once efficient condensation is reported, the pathway can 
be supplemented with acyl-CoA dehydrogenase for subsequent fluorinated carboxylic acid 
incorporation into PHA as one of the downstream processing options. In order to observe the products 
of the synthetic elongation pathway, longer elution time can be employed during HPLC analysis, as it 
is likely that the hydrophobic reaction products elute outside of the 30 minutes of the run time. 
Additionally, reaction products can be analysed by mass spectrometry or nuclear magnetic resonance 
spectroscopy.
