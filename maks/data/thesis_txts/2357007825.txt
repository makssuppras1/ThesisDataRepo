ABSTRACT 
Additive manufacturing through polymer 3D printing technologies have recently been applied 
for insert fabrication in injection molding. There are several reasons for using this process. The 
machining time and cost are reduced compared to conventional metal machining, therefore the 
cycle of production is shortened. In addition, highly customized geometry can be achieved, 
which is important and wide used for industries such as hearing aids design and production. 
However, the life of the insert needs to be prolonged in order to withstand more injection 
molding cycles; the thermal and mechanical properties of the polymer inserts need to be 
strengthened so that it can produce polymer parts that need more demanding injection molding 
process conditions, such as high pressure and high mold temperature.  
There is a variety of methods to strengthen the inserts, for instance, choosing materials for the 
additive manufacturing (AM) process with high deflection temperature, or using fiber to 
reinforce the material.   
In this master project, the inserts will be improved by applying a ceramic coating to 
unreinforced, regular AM material, and the effect on the tool’s life will be investigated. For this, 
several different polymer materials will be chosen, being the coatable properties of them the 
main concern at this part of the project. 
Injection molding tests will be conducted using polymers with different thermal properties. The 
study will focus on the maximum number of cycles the coated inserts can withstand. Parallel 
tests will be conducted for uncoated inserts as a reference.  All inserts are fitted to an already 
existent steel mold available at DTU. 
An experiment will be carried out to investigate the durability of the coated inserts during the 
injection molding process. The influential parameters are the replication degree and variations 
on shape, depth and angle of the features, wear, among others. 
 
Additive manufacturing in injection molding 
Economical as well as functional molds can be produced by rapid prototyping technologies in a 
very short time. The tool is therefore a mold from which a final part can be obtained. 
Furthermore, if tooling costs are reduced, rapid tooling could allow low-volume production for 
traditional processes that are meant for a higher volume – as in injection molding (IM). This is 
especially interesting since IM is the main production technique for plastic parts. 
Many studies have focused on the creation of molds directly with an additive manufacturing 
(AM) process. It seems that this method is the most promising in terms of reducing time and 
cost of production in the low-volume range of parts. The short-run tooling can be referred to as 
prototype tooling or soft tooling, which aims to achieve a tool quickly, at low cost and few 
process stages – being this the only way to get a tooling solution in a very short time. [1] 
The newer generation of photopolymers, together with other improvements, opens up a world 
of possibilities for 3D printing technologies, leading to a search for new and alternative 
methodologies. The possibility of using AM technologies as well as new materials for inserts 
brings an improved way of production of molds for short runs of plastic components, which have 
a great industrial demand nowadays. This concept of making molds with complementary inserts 
– made out of other metallic materials or synthetic materials – is the main idea in the hybrid-

mold technology, which has opened up and developed since it became a successful 
manufacturing process in the mold making industry. [2] 
 
Ceramic coatings 
Materials for molds need to be able to withstand the temperatures and pressure changes in 
every cycle. Polymer based inserts have been the focus of several previous research studies, 
being proven non-competitive enough in terms of life span duration. The treatment of these 
polymer inserts is the goal of this project, where an improvement of the thermal properties is 
key. To get this done, some research in treatment of common molds is made. 
When working with micro features in IM, both the workpiece and the mold are susceptive to 
structural damage due to high thermal stress, adhesion and friction at the interface. This causes 
improper replication and inability of the mold to withstand a prolonged life, especially in 
sidewalls, and coatings can improve the surface properties as having a low friction coefficient, 
low adhesion and low wear rate – properties that improve the filling of mold cavities and reduce 
demolding stresses in injection molding. However, other parameters such as chemical inertness, 
thermal stability, toxicity freedom, good adhesion to base material and withstanding of high 
pressures are also desirable in these cases.  
Various ceramic coatings have showed superior stability at elevated temperatures, improved 
oxidation property, high hardness and wear resistance. They protect the mold from harsh 
environments and reduce the friction force and demolding energy. These parameters are helpful 
in the replication of sharp corners and are also of especial importance when complexity and 
aspect ratio increase and the size of the features decrease. Ceramic coatings, which seem like a 
good option to try on polymer inserts, are usually deposited by vapor phase deposition. 
Among ceramics, hard coatings play an especial role in the mold industry. Carbides and nitrides 
of transitional metals have been used to protect them from erosion and thermal cracking during 
injection molding. 
TiN is used to prevent the mold from the thermal fatigue, surface alloying, erosion and wear. It 
decreases the coefficient of friction and stickiness as well. It was also found that the lifetime of 
TiN coated molds could be increased by about 320% respectively through optimizing adhesion, 
surface topology, oxidation resistance and compressive stress of the coatings itself. 
CrN has been proven to have excellent wear resistance and thermal stability. CrN coatings have 
good adhesion to mold surfaces, highly improving the wear lifetime over an uncoated one. Also, 
these coatings drastically improved the wear resistance of the uncoated mold and no significant 
damage was observed on the coated mold surfaces after tens of thousands of injection cycles. 
[3] 
 
Conclusion 
AM is a very promising technology in the tooling industry. Although, polymer inserts cannot 
withstand a good amount of cycles in IM due to the high temperatures and pressures used, and 
ceramic coatings are to be employed to improve the behaviour of the inserts.

1. INTRODUCTION 
It is already possible to 3D print inserts for injection molding, but the replication degree of the 
produced parts, as well as the life of the polymer tool itself, are limited. Hence, the last goal of 
this research consists of improving them both. To do so, several methods can be used, being 
surface treatment one of them. Adding a coating layer on top of the insert surface is the choice 
used to try and improve the inserts. 
The content of this thesis paper will be explain through the different chapters it is divided in, 
and the order can be understood as follows: 
 
After the introduction, the first chapter is a preparation to the experiment. In it, the production 
of the inserts, depending on the materials and printing technologies used for them, is discussed. 
Design, information about the materials and technologies used are some of the topics to 
address. 
A preliminary test of the coating properties and performance is tackled in the second chapter, 
whereas the printed inserts are addressed in the third. They offer some directives on what to 
focus on during the final experiment, as well as some conclusions which could already be seen 
before the IM process. 
Then the final experiment is analyzed, followed by the conclusion and future work in the last 
chapter.

1.3 Background 
Background on AM 
AM is a manufacturing technique, initially oriented for prototyping and customized parts for 
low-production so that a piece can be tested before being mass-produced. This has to do with 
its poor condition for normal production – long-lead time, inexact repeatability, unpredictable 
shrinkage in most of the processes, etc. 
However, this technology has improved quickly. Nowadays, components can be fully 
manufactured by machines depositing polymer or metal powder. This is of particular interest for 
some geometries which cannot be produced in any other way, as in lattice or cell structures of 
interconnected struts. They are based on empty cells that offer a reduction of density as well as 
a unique overall stiffness and flexure to the structures, compared to the bulk properties. [4] 
 
Figure 1.1: classification of the AM processes [5] 
 
For this Project, the main AM technologies to be taken into consideration are Thermoplastic 
Extrusion and Vat photopolymerization. Both processes integrate CAD, computer numerical 
control and polymer science to produce solid parts in 3D directly from a CAD model file. It all 
starts with the modelling of the part using computer-aided design. Then the CAD file is sent to 
the printer, which will reproduce the exact insert as a solid layer by layer. Then the insert can be 
placed in the mold as seen in the next image. 
 
Figure 1.2: the insert-mold concept 
==PAGE:10==

Background on Thermoplastic Extrusion 
As shown in the previous diagram, Thermoplastic Extrusion is the AM process that creates 
structures by thermo-polymer extrusion from a material deposition unit. The plastic goes into a 
tractor-feed system, which melts the material and creates the pressure to extrude it through a 
nozzle as a thread to go on a computer-controlled positioning system. To create a new part, the 
nozzle moves on a 2-axis basis on the same plane, being the bed frame the one that moves 
vertically to create the different layers of the print. When the part is completed, it is then taken 
out of the build plate and supports are carefully detached. 
 
Figure 1.3: the thermoplastic extrusion system [6] 
Since this technique works by extrusion through a small nozzle, thin shelled structures are faster 
to produce when compared to any other AM process. The structures are built upwards, starting 
from the base layer. This way, collisions are avoided and the printer can be left unattended while 
working. Another one of the advantages is the small amount of space and machinery needed 
during the production: fume cabinets or any other external installations are not needed for 
thermoplastic extrusion unlike other AM production systems. Cleaning stations are only needed 
for commercial systems with soluble supports. 
The toxicity of the procedure is limited to the little fumes generated in the melting and extrusion 
of the polymer, present in most AM processes – which, unlike this one, tend to be very 
hazardous for the environment. Besides this, free-standing parts might need supports 
depending on the geometry, involving the use of extra material and time for production. The 
rest of the disadvantages of this printing process rely on the quality: visible ridges between 
deposited layers, residual material due to a discontinuous extrusion, possible defects and 
possible delamination and weaker bonds in the shear direction. Also, the importance of these 
effects tend to magnify after some cycles since the repeatability of this process is not good. 
Finally, some manual interaction is usually needed for an acceptable finish of the parts. 
Thermo-polymers are used for extrusion, where the most common materials are polylactic acid 
(PLA) and acrylonitrile butadiene styrene, both used in this project, together with 
polycarbonate. They come as filaments, what is cheap and easy to insert in the printer. These 
materials are primarily used because of their properties: their melting temperature is low 
enough for melt extrusion processes without using dedicate facilities, but high enough for the 
printed parts to remain their shape in normal work temperatures – and yet having decent 
mechanical properties. However, the used materials in this thesis work will be deeper discussed 
later. [8] [8]

Background on vat photopolymerization 
The first AM process created already worked with this principle, where the build-plate is located 
inside a vat of photo-initiated resin. A laser light is projected on it, tracing the contour of the 
part to produce, forcing the resin to cure with that shape to form a layer. Then the platform 
descends and the following layers can be printed – on top of the others – in the same way. Once 
the piece is completed, the plate is lifted out of the vat and the part can be manually removed, 
cleaned and cured. 
The bottom-up machine design differs with this one in the light source. The projection units of 
the bottom-up machine are strong and highly exceed the light emission of those used for the 
top-down design printers, creating very thin layers pixel by pixel. There is also a difference in the 
resin container, being for bottom-up a transparent tray that goes up after every layer in order 
to create the final part. This results in less waste and running cost than in the other design. 
 
Figure 1.4: bottom-up (left) against top-down (right) machine design [6] 
Although bottom-up printers are one of the most accurate 3D printing processes, with excellent 
surface finish, it has some disadvantages mainly related to photopolymers. The properties of the 
material, as well as its chemical composition, are not fully known and can change over time due 
to improper post curing, so that the material can become more brittle. This technology is also 
expensive, though it is more advanced and cost reduced than the top-down. Therefore, this 
process is used when high quality parts are sought.

 Materials 
Vat photopolymerization processes use resins as material, which are divided in acrylate and 
epoxy based resins. Their properties are similar to the polymers they resemble – they can be 
PLA-like, ABS-like… - but, in order to get stiff solid parts, resins need to be cured with light, 
usually UV. Composition and properties of resin products in industry tend to be unknown. 
Acrylates are the first being used and are still the material employed in this process, but they 
are more brittle and have worse properties than most of the thermo-polymers used in 
thermoplastic extrusion and other processes. To get final parts out of acrylates, they go through 
a radical-curing mechanism using a carbonyl component as a photo initiator that is normally a 
benzophenone. 
Photo initiators are chemicals that form energetic radical species when exposed to UV light and 
make polymerization possible. Benzophenone-initiator compounds form an excited state upon 
irradiation and then get an electron or atom from a donor molecule, which acts as the initiating 
species for polymerization. 
The process starts with the radical (I*) formation, done by the photo initiator (I) absorbing light. 
This then reacts with an acrylate functional material (R) in the initiation step. Propagation occurs 
as the growing polymer or reactive species (IR*) reacts with additional acrylate. The propagation 
step builds the molecular weight and crosslinking of the polymer – which creates viscosity. This 
process continues until the reactive species cannot locate more acrylate. If, at some point, the 
mobility of the growing polymer (IRR’*) is limited by that created viscosity, which limits the 
ability of finding more acrylate, the propagation will stop. This will happen as long as chain 
transfer or termination processes – which are used for this purpose of limiting the molecular 
weight – do not occur before. The first occurs when the growing polymer gets a hydrogen atom 
from a donor molecule, stopping this polymerization and starting the formation of a new 
polymer. The second occurs when two growing polymer chains react with each other. 
 
Figure 1.5: benzophenone initiator on top and UV free-radical polymerization process  
Epoxy based resins are more industrially oriented than acrylates. They use a cationically-curing 
mechanism with aryldiazonium salt as a photo initiator. The reactive chemical, which is obtained 
by the disassociation of a salt molecule, opens the epoxy ring structure causing polymerization. 
This is part of the acid formation process, and the rest of the steps are analogous to those in 
radical polymerization: initiation, chain reaction (propagation), chain transfer and termination. 
[9] 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
However, the behavior of commercially available photo-initiated resins has been improved by 
the use of additives but they are often not specified by the supplier. 
 
Background on PVD 
Physical vapor deposition (PVD) comprises several vacuum deposition methods that are used to 
produce coatings and thin films. In this process, a condensed material goes into a vapor phase, 
so it can be deposited into another material, before it is condensed again into a thin layer.  
PVD processes are commonly used in industry for deposition of materials such as TiN or CrN, 
among others, which are the used coatings for this experiment. Also a pure metal was tested to 
offer a comparison over hard coatings.  
The coating layers were deposited by reactive HiPIMS, in a commercial batch-coating system 
(CC800/9, CemeCon AG) on the different polymer inserts. The substrates were mounted on a 
substrate table carrying out a two-fold planetary rotation. The distance between the target and 
the substrate was approximately 8 cm.

Background on IM  
Injection molding is one of the most used processes in the plastic manufacturing industry. This 
technique allows the conversion of polymer parts into all sorts of products for several purposes. 
The IM machine does the work, after the material pellets have been dried (if needed), with two 
differentiated parts: the injection and the clamping unit.  
The first melts the plastic and moves it to the mold. To do so, the temperature of the plastic is 
increased by heating and the plastic pellets are grinded down to get a uniform viscosity. This 
process stops once the material is capable of flowing under the pressure a screw is creating 
inside of the barrel. This plastication of the material is followed by the access of it to the mold, 
which is done with flow channels that direct the molten, liquid polymer into the cavities of the 
mold – as shown in the next image. [10] 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
 
Figure 1.6: schematic view of an IM machine and one possible runner layout [10] 
The clamping unit keeps the mold in a closed position while injecting the material so it can 
withstand the pressures of the injection and the molding of the polymer into the final shape. 
Finally, after cooling, the unit opens to eject the part out of the mold. 
 
Figure 1.7: IM molding process in four steps [3] 
 
The following image represents the variation of parameters during time. In this case, injection 
speed is shown, where the first second (shown in the x axis) represents the injection step and 
the rest shows the packing one. The y axis indicates the value of the parameters represented, 
changing in values and scale when selecting another parameter: injection pressure or screw 
stroke. 
 
Figure 1.8: variation of injection parameters over time

In order to maintain consistency in product quality, the molding temperature, pressure and 
material flow rate must be accurately controlled to ensure proper filling of the workpiece. 
Replication wise talking, the main factors are different: 
Coefficient of friction, determines the replication quality and lifetime of the mold based on shear 
forces. Surface energy causes adhesion, hence it is desirable to keep it low. Roughness, has 
effect on the full filling of the cavity. Slanting angle, affects the demolding due the friction force. 
Chemical bonding, promotes deformation and adhesion being preferred to use an inert surface. 
Thermal expansion coefficient, generates thermal stresses in the subsequent injection cycles. 
Replication temperature, has to be high for a proper filling but not too much to avoid 
deformation of the mold or chemical bonding with the workpiece. Last, harsh environmental 
conditions need to be controlled. [10] [11]

2. CREATION OF THE INSERTS 
The inserts have been designed in a way they can fit the available mold but also that allows its 
production with several processes and materials. The idea behind this seeks for some real results 
on the possibility or not of working with inserts manufactured by both extrusion and vat 
photopolymerization printing processes, regardless the quality of the printed parts.  
In terms of mechanical and thermal properties, different materials are tested. For this, the 
available thermoplastic polymers haven been used, being ABS and PLA the ones, which 
properties can be found in the appendix 1. As already mentioned, a special photopolymer has 
to be used in the other printing process, and the differences will be later discussed. 
In terms of quality (resolution and accuracy) of the inserts, the distinct processes make the 
difference. Originally, ABS and PLA inserts were meant to be printed with the same extrusion 
printer (Ultimaker Original +) so the only differences between the parts are caused by material 
properties. However, the Stratasys machine had to be used for ABS parts just as a matter of time 
although both machines show differences as mentioned before. This is a factor to have into 
consideration since not only the quality differs; but also the coating adhesion might change in 
vertical/tilted walls because of the distance between printed layers – which is expected to work 
similarly to the roughness in horizontal walls. This is, that the coating will penetrate the space 
in between deposited layers and this will show some differences regarding the distance between 
them in the different printing techniques, which can be found in the description of the printers, 
in the appendix 2.  
The final result is predicted to show some variations in the parts produced both in different 
materials and with different processes, not only in geometry, quality and properties; but also in 
what the coating adhesion and performance concerns. Of course, different coatings and 
deposition properties will influence too, but this will be discussed in the chapter about coatings. 
 
 
2.1 Design 
The final goal of this project aims to find how the coating properties and its behavior are on 
inserts for injection molding processes. The dimensions of the insert are not a particular interest 
as such, therefore an available mold can be used and the insert design goes on from that. Even 
though another mold could be produced, the task of designing and creating a new mold is 
complex and time consuming hence not worth it for this purpose. In this situation, and contrary 
to the normal design line in injection molding – where the mold is adapted to the final product 
– the design of the insert will start with a study of the mold itself.  
The available information about the mold is all contained in the drawings shown in the appendix 
3. Since a previous insert was already designed, produced for and tested in this mold, its 
dimensions will be used for the design of the new inserts to be coated. The insert is 20x20 mm 
in size, a quadrangular prism which perfectly fits in the cavities of the mold – for more details, 
check the complete drawings. The idea behind the new design will start with modifications over 
this insert, while preserving its dimensions.

The surface shown in the top view of this picture is the only interesting one concerning the 
injection cycle. It is the one to be replicated after it has been coated. Although the coating can 
be tested over a flat surface, it is of a major interest to check the properties on a surface 
including different geometries and heights/depths. They can affect different parameters – such 
as coating thickness, adhesion and endurance, among others – and offer a better perspective in 
the end. 
Therefore, the final design of the inserts will contain several features of different shapes – 
circles, squares, triangles, ribs… – and heights, as extrusions and holes. These parameters (shape 
and height), together with the size of the features, have been chosen experimentally. The main 
idea consists on having several features at once in the same insert. In order to find the proper 
size and height for every feature with a particular shape, several samples of each shape with 
different dimensions and heights have been printed in a sheet of the same material.

difficult the option to obtain another print test in ABS. A much better quality is expected from 
the vat photopolymerization process, so the features can be smaller than those to be used in 
the other process. Again, the cost and time needed for printing in photopolymer forces us to go 
with just one printing test – the already shown PLA printing test.

2.2 Printed parts 
It is important to keep in mind that AM production has been used for all inserts in this project. 
In this manufacturing process, the structures are built in a 2½D method, meaning that 2D layers 
are deposited one on top of each other to get the final solid parts. This way, the workpieces 
accumulate residual stresses layer by layer, which elastically deform resulting in possible 
warping of the parts as wholes.

the problem could not be completely solved. Besides this, the layer resolution and the quality 
of the parts look good enough.

3. COATING TEST ON SAMPLES 
Once the design of the inserts has been discussed, the coating properties are to be addressed. 
For this purpose – and since printing all the inserts with the different AM techniques for this 
project takes some time – samples of different sizes and shapes, produced with the same 
materials as the inserts, have been tested to check the adhesion of the coating to the substrates 
before proceeding to the IM process with the actual printed inserts. For this purpose, CrN and 
TiN have been used as coating materials, since Cr was not available for the test. However, the 
procedures used to make the samples are not the same than the ones used for the inserts. Even 
though the printing techniques for PLA and photopolymer samples are the same than those in 
the inserts, the machines and parameters differ. In the case of PLA samples, there are two kinds 
of them, and have also been printed with different parameters, as well as having different 
geometries. Finally, the ABS parts have not been printed at all, since their production was made 
through an IM process. This will provide some predicting ideas over the final results on the 
coated inserts after IM – and how to study them – meanwhile the inserts are produced, which 
will be analyzed in the following chapters.

3.3 Hot press test 
This experiment was a hot embossing like process. It has been carried out using sheets of 
polystyrene (PS) on a hot press machine. The laminated material was heated up to 100º, 
temperature at which it becomes malleable enough for this purpose. Then the parts to be tested 
are placed on top of the PS, with the coated layer facing it. Finally, the metal plates have been 
pressed against each other receiving the sample a load of 100 kg.   
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
This process is meant to replicate the surface of the parts on the sheets of PS, on a similar way 
of that IM workpieces go through. Finally, to make it as similar as possible, only the bottom plate 
is being heated. 
There are many issues to point out in this test, starting with PLA. There is one CrN-coated sample 
and two samples of TiN-coated PLA, and all reacted in a different way in the end even though 
they showed some similarities in the test making. The surface in which the load was applied was 
different in each part, being this the reason of the differences after the test. Since the samples 
were curved in the coating process, one of the surfaces is concave and the other convex. 
When the load is applied on the convex surface, the stress distribution inside the part allowed 
some deformation so the inner tensions are released. Because of the load and temperature 
used, the sample plastically deformed to get a final flat shape. In the other case, the stress inside 
the sample is too high so the part cracked as shown – comparing it with the uncoated flat 
sample.  
 
Figure 3.5: uncoated (left) and TiN coated (right) PLA samples after the hot press test 
Due to the PLA thermal properties, already mentioned, the uncoated sample lost the definition 
in the shape after the process (blurred edges), what got improved by adding a TiN coating. 
However, this did not behave as great in the ring at one of the tips of the star. This makes sense 
since the ring is a stress concentration area, which could not resist the applied loads in this 
situation. Same as happened in the ring that cracked after the coating deposition, previously 
discussed. 
However, the quality of the coating in this part showed some defects after this test. It occurred 
partly on the flat surface of the sample, mostly the edges, but mainly on the vertical walls as 
shown in the next image, where some tracks of the coating were found in the sheet of PS.  
 
Figure 3.6: TiN-coated PLA and its replication on PS material after the test 
By definition, in this process the polymer flow is directed from the flat surface to the edges, and 
from there the walls are formed. This area is the most affected by friction when demolding. For 
this reason, the vertical walls are more prone to get the coating peeled off.

was lower (41º C), the sample did not look that bad after coating. However, the temperature 
used in this test – so that the polymer sheet could perform a replication (100º C) – together with 
the load, made this sample deform in the way the other PLA samples did after TiN coating. 
 
Figure 3.7: CrN-coated PLA and its surface replication on PS material after the test

3.6 Conclusion 
The polymer parts, which suffer from high residual stresses when they undergo the AM process, 
go through some warping deformation after the heat needed for the coating process. It has 
special impact when the temperature needed to do the deposition process goes beyond the 
glass temperature of the polymer. 
This is especially an issue with the TiN coating. When cooling, the substrate and the coating layer 
– which have different values for thermal expansion coefficient – contract by different amounts, 
creating thermal stresses on the coating. This might explain some of the issues originated after 
coating, like the warping showed before. [14] 
 
It is the case of PLA, which glass temperature is about 60º C. It already deformed after the TiN 
coating deposition (66º), but it heavily deformed after loads were applied, which can lead to 
cracks as it happened during the hot press test. The whole process was similar with the CrN 
coating, but not as critical since the deposition temperature is not that close to the glass 
transition (41º).  
Glass temperatures of both ABS (108º) and acrylate (120-150º) are way beyond that of the 
deposition process, and the only problem with them was found at high pressures, when the 
acrylate parts got stuck in the PS sheet after hot embossing, ending up breaking apart when 
excessive shear forces where applied for the demolding. The behavior of these materials will be 
further analyzed after IM, using the conclusions and some of the experiments from this study 
on coated samples. 
   
The case of Cr coated parts is yet to be investigated with the inserts once this material is used 
as a coating.

The real reasons for this phenomenon can be found in the 3d printing process itself. As already 
mentioned, there are high residual strain and stresses that might cause further problems after 
processing.  
In these AM processes, where the parts are produced by sequential deposition of material 
layers, residual stresses and strains are accumulated during the buildup. These stresses arise 
from non-uniform thermal gradients: the heating and rapid cooling cycles of the material, 
constrained by the platform or the other material layers, store stresses in the parts once they 
are cooled before removal. [14]  
Once the printing is finished, the part can be viewed as a laminate structure with anisotropic 
material properties, and thermally induced stresses are one of the main causes of failure – 
starting with part distortions and dimensional inaccuracy and ending with delamination and 
cracking. As for the strains, they can be released after heating above the glass temperature, 
which can trigger shrinkage or pattern transformation. [16]

Regarding the material, HDA is a photopolymer that needs to be cured in order to be stiff in its 
final shape. However, after these polymers have been printed, the built part is not completely 
cured. The curing percentage of the part can be between 65 and 90 depending on the material 
used. In order to fully cure the part, it undergoes a post-curing process that is normally done by 
UV light irradiation. This process has a greater effect in the areas that are closer to the surface, 
since the inner part is not exposed to as much of the UV light.   
Although UV irradiation is the most common post curing technique, there are alternative 
methods like thermal curing: by increasing the temperature of the part it continues curing. To 
test the effect of this process, previous experiments placed printed parts in ovens which would 
progressively reach a very high temperature, for example 250º C. This has shown that several 
parts started reacting at a temperature of 50º, which is lower than the TiN deposition 
temperature (66º) but higher than the others (≈40º). In the case that the polymer gets highly 
affected by the post-curing treatment, such as over curing the material, this process could lead 
to the formation of cracks. [17] 
In order to see the influence of the higher temperatures in the other inserts – Cr and CrN – a 
similar experiment to the one stated in the chapter 3.2 will be conducted. Both inserts will be 
heated in an oven to see if any changes can be experienced in the coating, mainly looking for 
similar cracks than the ones found in TiN. Since the coating temperature for this ceramic material 
was 66º C, the same one will be used for the oven experiment.

Focusing on the plating, some processes and parameters in the TiN deposition are different to 
those in the other coatings. These are the deposition temperature and the presence of an 
interlayer, besides a prior etching which does not seem to have any effect on the coating 
behavior.  
Regarding the inner layer, several studies showed that an interlayer is an effective way of 
accommodating the TiN internal stresses and improving the coating in terms of adhesion and 
toughness among others. Nevertheless, this applies to Ti interlayers. In this case, Cr was used 
for that layer, and it might be a problem itself. TiN, and the rest of hard coatings, normally 
contain a high degree of internal stresses, being the thermal mismatch effect one of the reasons 
why. In this case, there are three materials in contact, so the thermal mismatch has even more 
influence: HDA with Cr and Cr with TiN. And this is what leads to the next point: the differences 
between substrate and coating. [17] 
When two (or more) materials are put together into one object, big differences in some of their 
properties can cause trouble within this union. Thermal mismatch is always a problem in case 
there is a gradient in temperature. It happens during the coating process: when cooling, the 
substrate and the surface layer – which have different values for thermal expansion coefficient 
– contract by different amounts, creating thermal stresses on the coating. The accumulation of 
thermal stresses could initiate cracking even in the absence of applied mechanical loading. [18]

4.4 Conclusion 
As explained, the thermal properties of PLA are not good enough to withstand the coating 
parameters. Furthermore, the temperatures the inserts will be subjected to during the IM 
process are not far from those in the coating deposition. Finally, due the high deformation of 
the TiN-coated PLA inserts, both in the features but mainly in the size of the inserts itself since 
they cannot be mounted in the mold, this material is considered not appropriated. Same thing 
happened to the other PLA inserts when the temperature raised. As a result, the PLA inserts will 
not be tested in the IM machine. 
The other inserts – ABS and HDA – showed no variations in their original shape, but some of the 
features are slightly affected. All the changes have been recorded so that they can be compared 
again after the IM process. 
When looking at the inserts with the Alicona, micro cracks on the surface of the TiN-coated HDA 
inserts were found. However, it is not completely clear why they originated. According to the 
differences in the coefficient of thermal expansion between the polymer substrate and the 
coating materials, all HDA inserts should react. And all of them did when subjected to high 
temperatures (66º C) for some time, but not on the way they were meant to. The TiN coated 
insert was treated in a special way during deposition; but when the other two coated inserts 
were heated, the Cr coated one had more cracks on the surface than the CrN (larger mismatch). 
And the TiN coated insert, which has the smallest mismatch of them all, was the one that failed 
in first place – the reasons for an unexpected behavior to thermal contraction/expansion might 
be found in the treatment conditions or deposition parameters during the plating, since they 
differ for every coating material. Other factors might influence as well, and will be investigated 
after the IM experiment since there could be some new useful data for this analysis.

By looking at the cracks now it can be assumed that their formation can be explained by finding

6. CONCLUSION  
Firstly, and based on the coating deposition technique and the coated inserts behavior, some 
general ideas obtained through the experiments and research are given. It is possible to coat 
common thermoplastics and photopolymers, as long as the deposition parameters can be 
withstood by the polymer and there are no mismatches in the properties of base and coating 
materials. As an example of the former, the TiN was deposited at a temperature (66º C) above 
the PLA glass temperature (60º C), what led to important deformations that can end up cracking 
even without external applied forces. In the case of mismatches, it is important to keep both the 
substrate and the coating material balanced in terms of thermal expansion coefficients as 
explained later with the HDA inserts. 
In terms of adhesion, none of the coatings showed any problems in any of the substrate 
materials – even when high pressures and temperatures were applied up to the breaking of 
some features. Regarding the behavior of the features after coating, besides TiN-coated PLA 
inserts – which were heavily deformed – the rest were good overall. Some minor changes were 
found in some TiN-ABS features as well. The HDA inserts were more random, some changes that 
did not seem to follow any rule. 
Finally, and based on the behavior of the inserts after IM, the different substrates and their 
coatings are discussed. The problem of the HDA inserts was found at the corner of big holed 
triangles, which especially suffered when oriented next to the gates during IM. However, the 
uncoated inserts performed nicely whereas big cracks developed in the coated ones – after 
micro-cracks had been found throughout their surface. This does not mean that the coated 
inserts behave worse, but high inner tensions build up when two materials with big differences 
in their thermal expansion coefficient are put together in a single part; and it is the case. This, 
activated by external factors such as temperature or pressure, take the part to a failing point – 
cracking in this case. To solve this problems, the substrate and coating material should have 
similar coefficients in order to avoid this mismatch. Sad that this polymer – which seems nice 
for IM processes in itself – has such a high thermal expansion coefficient.  
In the case of the ABS inserts, both coated and uncoated inserts showed kind of the same 
behavior. There was wear in some areas (mainly by the gates) after 10-20 cycles, where no 
improvement was found when using coatings. The following graph shows the other main 
affected area, the extruded circles, which edges wear could be clearly perceived.  Besides wear 
in the gate surface and some of the edges, the features were fine.

7. FUTURE WORK 
Based on the results of this research, recommendations for further investigations will be divided 
in two: things to be changed and things to be tried out. The former focus on what could be 
changed in order to possibly get successful (or simply better) coated inserts for IM. 
First of all, the coating deposition temperatures should be as low as possible in any case. It has 
been proven, and could be anticipated, that high temperatures have no benefit and only 
deteriorate the inserts. In the same way, high temperatures are harmful during IM. This factor, 
together with pressure and injection speed, are determinant for the inserts stability during 
injection. The idea for future investigations consists on trying to lower them all as much as 
possible while still getting decent parts. Pressure and speed are primarily determinant in the 
area by the gate, where the main problems are found. 
Also, and trying to improve the behavior in the areas around the gates, variations on the 
positioning of the inserts can be tried to see the influence the different features have on that. It 
was checked that the corners on big triangles are very problematic due to stress concentrations 
so this feature can be removed in future experiments.  
Finally, simply as a test, different coating thicknesses could be tried to see its influence on the 
behavior of the inserts during IM. It could be interested to see how coatings react when their 
layer thickness is the only difference between the objects, or even when several coating 
materials with the same thickness are compared. Also, maybe a larger thickness could be 
employed as a protection in case of wear, as it happened with the ABS inserts. 
 
As a curiosity, the ABS samples – very simple geometry produced by IM – tested did not crack 
or show any visible problem, whereas the HDA where all cracked on the surface and ended up 
failing. It could be interesting to try with the exact ABS polymer, both 3D printed and injected, 
to see how the production process influences the coating behavior. Having into consideration 
to look for micro cracks on the surface of the injection molded one. 
 
Regarding the new proposals, the used coatings did not improve the behavior of the inserts in 
any case. But only three base materials and three coatings – all very similar – have been tested. 
There are many other coatings to be tried out, but they do not have to be ceramic or metallic 
either, and there is a whole world of available materials that can be tested. It is only a question 
of trying materials – base and coating – which have no mismatch in thermal expansion 
coefficient. In this sense, different polymer base materials can be tried.

Alicona InfiniteFocus 
The Alicona devices are based on the Focus-Variation technology. The main component of the 
system is a precision optics containing various lens systems that can be equipped with different 
objectives, allowing measurements with different resolution. This, combined with vertical 
scanning, provides topographical and color information from the variation of focus. 
With a beam splitting mirror, light emerging from a white light source is inserted into the optical 
path of the system and focused onto the specimen via the objective. Depending on the 
topography of the specimen, the light is reflected into several directions as soon as it hits the 
specimen via the objective. If the topography shows diffuse reflective properties, the light is 
reflected equally strong into each direction. In case of specular reflections, the light is scattered 
mainly into one direction. All rays emerging from the specimen and hitting the objective lens are 
bundled in the optics and gathered by a light sensitive sensor behind the beam splitting mirror. 
Due to the small depth of field of the optics only small regions of the object are sharply imaged. 
To perform a complete detection of the surface with full depth of field, the precision optics is 
moved vertically along the optical axis while continuously capturing data from the surface. This 
means that each region of the object is sharply focused. Algorithms convert the acquired sensor 
data into 3D information and a true color image with full depth of field. This is achieved by 
analyzing the variation of focus along the vertical axis. 
The other component of the InfiniteFocus consist of a XY stage where the specimens are placed 
to be measured. Typically, Focus-Variation delivers repeatable measurement results for surfaces 
with a local roughness of 0.009 µm at a cutting wavelength of 2 µm. Focus-Variation is used to 
perform high resolution 3D surface measurement in surface analysis and characterization (e.g.
