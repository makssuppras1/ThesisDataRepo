Abstract 
This thesis explores the role of leadership as a driver in the organizational transformation 
process, which leads towards regenerative economic implementation. This addresses the gaps 
in existing leadership frameworks which often include the sustainable aspect but not necessarily 
the regeneration as well. Environmental and social crises the world is struggling with nowadays 
show the real limitations of conventional economic models, and it highlights the need for 
regenerative approaches that not only minimize the harm but also go a step further and restore 
ecosystems and improve social well-being. Through analysis of existing literature, real-life case 
studies, and interviews, this project aims to develop a practical framework that helps leaders to 
implement regenerative principles within their organizational strategy and culture. The study’s 
findings will contribute to understanding of how leaders can catalyze regenerative practices and 
will offer insights for businesses committed to improving long-term socio-ecological health. The 
main research questions address leadership’s role as a catalyst in driving regenerative 
transformation and what frameworks could guide organizations through this complex shift.

1. Introduction 
The regenerative economy is becoming an important concept of a response to the constantly 
increasing environmental and social challenges which are dangerous for long-term economic, 
social and ecological well-being. While traditional economy and business models focus on 
prioritizing short-term financial gains it is significantly insufficient in addressing the complexities 
of sustainable development Fullerton (2015); Raworth (2017). Traditional sustainability models 
which we all are already aware of usually focus mainly on reducing negative impacts on the 
environment. while regenerative principles aim to create positive outcomes within the 
environments by actively regenerating ecosystems and fostering resilience within communities 
Hutchins and Storm (2019). The regeneration principles correspond directly with circularity, 
resilience, and systems thinking - an approach which focuses on recognition of the highly 
connected nature of social, ecological, and economic systems Meadows (2008). Organizations 
which decide to implement regenerative strategies and principles have a goal of not only 
minimizing their environmental footprint but to also implement regenerative principles within all 
their operational processes and frameworks which later on also helps to contribute to the long-
term well-being of people, ecosystems and the planet Raworth (2017). 
 
Within the last few decades business models have evolved from linear and exploitative 
approaches to incorporating sustainable, circular and even regenerative frameworks in order to 
address the increasing environmental degradation and escalating social inequities Fullerton 
(2015). In order to become a regenerative organization, its business models should  incorporate 
principles of ecological restoration, social equity, and general stakeholder well-being Elkington 
(1997). This shift in business thinking defines a transformation in how businesses define the 
fundamentals of their success. In this manner, they should be moving away from searching for 
profit maximization alone and move toward a more holistic value creation process. What is 
crucial, this transition creates a need for more than just operational adjustments, it creates a 
need for a holistic cultural and strategic shift, which puts leadership as a primary driver of this 
transformation Bui (2020). Leaders’ role is an essential one with transformations like the one of 
incorporating a regenerative vision. It is because they can foster the organizational culture 
incorporating values that support systemic change, and finding the right resources to implement 
regenerative principles with business models Hutchins and Storm (2019). 
 
Even though the recognition of regenerative leadership is constantly growing, some significant 
gaps still remain not being answered. This relates to the availability of frameworks which guide 
leaders in implementing and sustaining long-term perspective regenerative strategies within 
their organizations Hutchins and Storm (2019). What’s more, many of the existing leadership 
frameworks, such as transformational and adaptive leadership, even though they highlight the 
change management they do not integrate regenerative principles in a whole perspective into 
the corporate structures Bui (2020). The current lack of clear frameworks and methods to 
implement regenerative leadership makes it even more challenging for organizations to 
transition from sustainability-focused approaches to truly regenerative practices Raworth (2017).

This thesis aims to address and fill this gap by developing a structured leadership framework 
which is adjusted to the real business cases. What’s more, this framework will equip leaders 
with specific tools to understand and incorporate the regenerative principles into organizational 
strategy and decision-making. Thanks to that it will contribute to the challenging working 
process of regenerative transformation and will provide specific insights into leadership’s role in 
shaping a resilient and regenerative business. 
1.1. Problem Statement, research purpose and objectives 
Despite the rising interest in regenerative practices, there is still a notable gap in frameworks that 
effectively guide leaders in implementing regenerative transformation within organizations. 
Current literature and research projects offer foundation for insights into regenerative principles 
and leadership theories but there is still a lack and a need for a detailed, practical framework for 
leaders to start and sustain regenerative practices within their companies. The absence of such 
frameworks creates challenges for leaders who aim to transform their organizations from 
traditional models to those focused on regeneration. 
As regenerative business practices continue to gain interest within the industry, the need for a 
tested and clear guidance on how leaders can implement this transformation becomes more 
urgent than ever. This thesis project addresses this problem by exploring the role of leadership 
as a catalyst for regenerative transformation and developing a practical framework to support 
leaders in implementing regenerative principles. 
The purpose of this thesis is to explore the role of leadership in driving the transformation of 
organizations toward a regenerative economy and to develop a framework which together with 
suitable tools can help leaders to incorporate those principles within their teams and 
organizations. More specifically, the aim is to understand and address how leadership can 
influence an organizational culture that prioritizes ecological and social regeneration, while 
aligning its strategies, resources, and future goals with regenerative principles. 
To achieve this, the research aims for the following objectives: 
●      To understand & examine the practices and knowledge of leaders who effectively 
promote regenerative business models. 
●      To analyze existing theories and frameworks in leadership, identifying elements that 
can be adapted to support regenerative business transformation for other companies. 
●      To develop a practical framework for regenerative leadership that can guide companies 
through the complexities of adopting regenerative practices. 
This research is guided by two key questions that address both theoretical and practical 
challenges in regenerative leadership: 
==PAGE:9==
8 
●      Research Question 1: How does leadership serve as a catalyst for business 
transformation towards a regenerative economy? 
 
This question seeks to uncover the specific ways leadership drives the shift from 
traditional to regenerative business practices. It explores the role of leaders in 
establishing a regenerative vision, fostering a supportive culture, and mobilizing 
resources for change. 
●      Research Question 2: What framework is needed to guide leadership in driving 
business transformation towards a regenerative economy? 
  
This question aims to address the gap in practical tools available to leaders by 
developing a framework that provides actionable strategies for leading regenerative 
transformations. The framework will be designed to help leaders navigate the complexity 
of implementing regenerative practices, offering guidance on sustaining and scaling 
these efforts within their organizations. 
1.2. Scope, contribution and limitations 
This research focuses on implementation of regenerative practices in leadership within 
organizations at various sizes and stages of maturity and regenerative transformation. These 
potentially range from early adopters of regenerative practices to the ones that are fully 
prepared for being regenerative businesses. The study incorporates data and insights 
developed from a combination of interviews with experts from sustainability and leaders who 
use different frameworks in other industries and literature analysis. This combination offers a 
comprehensive perspective on how leadership influences the adoption and development of 
regenerative business models. 
 
Nevertheless, the thesis faces certain limitations and challenges. Given that regenerative 
leadership is still a fairly new and emerging topic, the data still remains limited and has some 
uncertainty, creating a need for support of qualitative research methodologies, such as expert 
perspectives and literature reviews. What’s more, while the aim of the proposed framework is to 
be applicable for a big range of industries and types of organizations, there are some sector-
specific factors like regulatory requirements, market dynamics, and industry-specific challenges 
which may cause a  requirement for adaptation in specific cases. Another thing is, In some 
cases the proposed framework might require customization based on organizational size, 
industry type, and cultural factors. 
 
This thesis contributes to both theory and practice within the regenerative economy and 
leadership topic. Theoretically, it moves further upon existing leadership literature as it puts 
together different approaches and frameworks and by integrating approaches like systems 
thinking, stakeholder collaboration, and biomimicry-inspired and different leadership strategies. 
It also contributes to the sustainability-to-regeneration discussion, elaborating on the shift from 
==PAGE:10==
9 
just reducing the harm strategies to net-positive impact frameworks. What’s more, it presents 
biomimicry as a powerful leadership tool, demonstrating how principles which already exist and 
work within natural ecosystems can inspire leaders to become resilient and adaptive within their 
practices. 
 
In practical angle this thesis presents a structured leadership framework developed with a 
purpose for real-world implementation. The thesis provides tailored strategies for implementing 
regenerative principles into organizational operations and gathers insights and important 
learnings from real-world case studies, based on interviews with experts who come from 
companies such as LEGO, Mars, Novo Nordisk, Matas, BCG or JYSK. These real life situations 
illustrate how regenerative leadership is being or could be successfully implemented across 
diverse organizational settings. What’s more it highlights both challenges and suggestions for 
improvements in the future. Additionally, the thesis introduces key performance indicators 
(KPIs) and measurement tools, which would allow leaders and organizations to measure, track 
and analyze the effectiveness of regenerative leadership initiatives. 
 
By combining theoretical insights with practical angle, this research proves that regenerative 
leadership is not just a concept possible to implement just in theory but a tangible, 
implementable strategy which organizations can adopt thanks to their leaders in order to drive 
meaningful transformation for the future. Given the growing regulatory pressures and constantly 
increasing demand for ethical, regenerative business practices among the customers and 
employees, this study is a provider of a crucial contribution to the evolving field of sustainable 
and regenerative leadership.

2.1. Foundational Leadership Theories 
This section focuses on three leadership theories which can help in understanding the current 
one, which is important as it provides context for regenerative leadership approaches. Starting 
from the beginning, in order to understand regenerative leadership there’s a need for having a 
foundation in understanding the traditional leadership theories that in previous decades have 
shaped organizational practices. This section describes three significant frameworks: 
transactional leadership, transformational leadership, and systems thinking/adaptive leadership. 
These three theories let us provide critical analysis of the changes in leadership and 
contextualize the shift toward regenerative approaches. 
2.1.1. Transactional Leadership 
The first type of leadership we will focus on in this study is transactional leadership. But first, 
let’s start with a description of what makes one a leader. Northouse (2022) defines leadership 
as a process of influencing individuals or groups to achieve a common goal and objectives. This 
broad yet applicable definition shows the collaborative nature of leadership. What’s more, it 
aligns well with its diverse meaning across different - transactional, transformational, and 
regenerative - approaches. Burns (1978) in his study Leadership  described transactional 
leadership as a model where the relationship between the “leader” and the “follower” is based 
on exchanges/transactions. What’s important, these transactions often reward based on 
performance and they ensure they align with established goals and tasks. Good leaders should 
provide clear and realistic expectations, and followers should be motivated through rewards or 
corrective actions dependent on the performance.  
Burns’ (1978) theory shows the importance of a management-oriented leadership style. It rather 
focuses on short-term objectives than starting a deep change or innovation. This approach is 
especially effective in stable environments where roles are clearly defined, and outcomes are 
predictable. On the other hand, transactional leadership has significant limitations while 
addressing more complex challenges that require adaptability and systemic thinking. 
Judge & Piccolo (2004), in their analysis on transformational and transactional leadership, 
elaborated Burns’ (1978) work by analysing the effectiveness of both leadership styles taking 
into consideration different contexts. They found out that transactional leadership is an effective 
way for leadership in maintaining organizational stability. It is done by structured frameworks 
and reward systems, which are especially advantageous in highly predictable environments. On 
the other hand they also highlighted its limitations. They noted that while transactional 
leadership is useful for achieving immediate goals, it doesn’t include aspects like bigger  vision 
and creativity which are needed for leaders to address dynamic challenges such as 
environmental and social crises. 
==PAGE:12==
11 
Podsakoff et al. (1984) also examined the factors that influence the effectiveness of 
transactional leadership. Their findings highlight the importance of contextual dynamics, which 
can either enhance or constrain the outcomes of transactional practices. What’s more, 
Antonakis and House (2014) went a step further and defined transactional leadership theory by 
introducing “instrumental leadership” which focuses on strategic use of rewards and 
punishments to align individual actions with broader organizational goals. 
The transactional model can be seen as a useful starting point for understanding leadership 
dynamics. It can be treated as a good base for exploring more innovative approaches like 
regenerative leadership. By analyzing its principles, people who decide to use it can appreciate 
even more the development in management stability to inspire systemic change. 
2.1.2. Transformational Leadership 
Transformational leadership proposed a different approach compared to the transactional 
approach. It is mainly done by focusing on inspiring and motivating the team to achieve great 
results. Bass and Avolio (1994) defined transformational leaders as those who increase 
innovation, build trust between the members of the team, and create a shared vision and goals. 
These leaders focus on personal development, encouraging the team to prioritize the overall 
goal instead of personal interests. There are four key components that characterize 
transformational leadership: 
● Idealized Influence - Leaders act as role models, it’s important to have respect and 
trust. 
● Inspirational Motivation - Leaders create a vision that engages the team. 
● Intellectual Stimulation - Leaders encourage creativity and problem-solving mindset. 
● Individualized Consideration - Leaders provide personalized support and mentorship. 
Northouse (2022) elaborates on the practical use of transformational leadership by highlighting 
its correlation with innovation and change management. Transformational leaders should be 
skilful at addressing challenges that the organization faces by improving the collaboration and 
adaptability. These make this model very relevant in today’s rapidly changing environments. 
Tracey & Hinkin (1994) also described the potential of the transformative leadership style, 
especially in industries struggling with significant disorganization. Their study shows how 
transformational leaders can act as change agents while catalyzing both organizational and 
cultural changes. This leadership style relates to regenerative leadership particularly in its focus 
on systemic change and empowerment. On the other hand, transformational leadership mainly 
focuses on human systems, while regenerative leadership also includes ecological and social 
systems.

2.1.3. Systems Thinking and Adaptive Leadership 
The third one, systems thinking and adaptive leadership perspective provides frameworks for 
complex and interdependent environments - core principles in regenerative leadership. Bui 
(2020) analyzed Senge’s (1990) concept of systems thinking. The concept has been described 
as a discipline which gives an understanding of organizations as internally highly connected 
systems. This perspective focuses especially on feedback loops within stakeholders, schemes 
and long-term consequences of decisions which leaders and teams make. This thinking 
encourages leaders to think holistically. 
Bui (2020) also highlights Senge’s reflections on organizations who learn and where continuous 
improvement and collaboration are a clear priority. This aligns with the regenerative approach, 
and seeks to integrate ecological and social systems into an organizational way of doing 
business. 
Heifetz et al. (2009) expand on this with their concept of adaptive leadership. Adaptive 
leadership focuses on navigating uncertainty and driving innovation. Leaders should 
experiment, come with conclusions from failure, and build resilience in their teams and 
organizations. This approach is relevant especially for complex challenges, such as reducing 
climate changes and inequalities within the system, it requires iterative and flexible solutions. 
Both systems thinking and adaptive leadership provide valuable tools for understanding and 
addressing the complex challenges fundamental in regenerative leadership. They underscore 
the need for flexibility, collaboration, and a holistic perspective in driving meaningful change. 
2.2. Introduction to Regenerative Principles in Business and 
Economy 
This section focuses on regenerative economy principles whose goal is to not only reduce the 
harm but also to achieve a positive impact on ecology and society, marking a shift from 
traditional sustainable models. Based on the fact that the world faces more and more ecological 
and social challenges, businesses and organizations need to face the shift from traditional 
harm-reduction models to approaches that also try to contribute positively to the systems they 
exist in. This change defines the regenerative principles, whose main goal is to have net-
positive outcomes. It’s done by combining and integrating ecological wealth, social equity, and 
long-term economic strength into the core of organizations. While sustainability often focuses 
only on reducing negative impacts, regeneration goes a step further and highlights the 
importance of actively restoring and revitalizing systems. 
2.2.1. Defining Regenerative Business Models 
Regenerative business models are based on a shift from linear or extractive frameworks to 
circular, restorative, and life-affirming approaches. Fullerton’s (2015) work can be seen as a 
foundation for understanding these models. Fullerton (2015) presents eight main principles of 
==PAGE:14==
13 
regenerative economy, such as good relationship, balance, and systemic health. These all 
together guide businesses to the state of a long-term ecological and social well-being. By 
incorporating these principles into their operations, organizations can go beyond only mitigating 
harm to creating conditions for systems to develop. 
Hutchins and Storm (2019) expand on this concept by emphasizing the integration of 
environmental operations with societal health. They claim that regenerative business models 
must go beyond profit-centric frameworks adopting a more holistic point of view that values 
collaboration, resilience, and the codependency of human and ecological systems. In the real 
world understanding these principles apply in companies adopting circular economy practices, 
regenerative agriculture, and community-focused initiatives. 
Konietzko et al. (2023) provided additional insights on the appliance of regenerative business 
models in their article Towards Regenerative Business Models: A Necessary Shift?. The study 
highlights the importance of innovation in design and production processes, as well as 
leadership’s role in driving cultural and organizational innovations and changes within the 
companies. By assessing these principles through various points of view, the authors offer 
specific strategies for introducing regeneration into business operations. The study also 
emphasizes why the transition from traditional sustainable frameworks towards regenerative 
practices is so important. It proposes integration of the circular economy together with adaptive 
leadership and agility.  
2.2.2. Biomimicry as integral part of regenerative principles and leadership 
Biomimicry offers a completely new perspective in developing regenerative economies by 
drawing inspiration from nature itself to address human and organizational challenges. As 
Benyus (1997) elaborates in her work: Biomimicry: Innovation Inspired by Nature, nature’s 
systems are inherently circular, self-sustaining, and optimized for long-term resilience. This 
approach can and should apply to all areas of regeneration. These principles can be applied to 
rethink business models, production systems creating opportunities for designing regenerative 
systems that align with ecological processes Benyus (1997). By studying how ecosystems 
maintain balance, businesses can implement the same strategies and thus not only minimize 
harm but actively regenerate resources de Pauw et al. (2010). 
In the context of regenerative leadership and principles, biomimicry emphasizes solutions that 
mimic nature’s processes, such as circularity, resilience, and adaptability. For instance, 
regenerative agricultural systems inspired by the diversity and self-replenishing capabilities of 
natural ecosystems demonstrate how businesses can integrate ecological insights into practice. 
These approaches prioritize interconnectedness, adaptability, and the balance of ecological, 
social, and economic systems.  
2.2.3. Biomimicry Design Spiral 
The Biomimicry Design Spiral is a structured, iterative approach that is a guide in applying 
biomimicry to the design process. It consists of 6 core stages: Identify, Translate, Discover, 
==PAGE:15==
14 
Emulate, Evaluate, and Iterate. These stages ensure a systematic integration of biological 
strategies into human designs while maintaining flexibility for  improvement de Pauw et al. 
(2010). 
● Identify: Focus on defining the design’s function and the desired outcomes rather than 
the solution itself. This stage involves asking, “What do we want the design to do?” 
rather than “What do we want to design?” 
● Translate: Reframe the design problem by exploring how nature performs similar 
functions, identifying biological principles and strategies relevant to the challenge. 
● Discover: Seek biological models and strategies through research, observation, and 
collaboration with biological experts to understand how organisms solve similar 
problems. 
● Emulate: Apply nature’s strategies to the design process by brainstorming solutions 
inspired by biological insights and translating them into practical applications. 
● Evaluate: Assess the design’s alignment with life’s principles, ensuring it is sustainable, 
adaptable, and conducive to long-term resilience. 
● Iterate: Refine the design by revisiting previous stages and incorporating feedback to 
enhance its functionality and alignment with regenerative goals. 
The approach promotes a mindset of constant learning and improvement, mirroring the iterative 
and adaptive nature of ecosystems. This approach fosters innovation while ensuring that 
solutions remain sustainable and aligned with nature’s principles. It is particularly effective when 
paired with the Challenge to Biology and Biology to Design pathways, offering a comprehensive 
framework for integrating biomimicry into leadership, innovation, and regenerative practices de 
Pauw et al. (2010). 
2.2.4. Biomimicry Thinking 
Biomimicry Thinking offers a comprehensive framework to integrate nature-inspired solutions 
into the design process across any discipline or scale. Rather than being a strict methodology, it 
provides a flexible structure that allows practitioners to apply biomimetic principles 
systematically. The framework is organized around four core phases - Scoping, Discovering, 
Creating, and Evaluating—which guide users through identifying challenges, exploring nature’s 
strategies, developing solutions, and assessing their effectiveness. 
Each phase ensures the incorporation of nature’s principles, encouraging designs that are

2.2.5. Challenges in Implementing Regenerative Practices 
While the benefits of regenerative practices are fascinating in many ways, their adaptation faces

organizations. She claims that facing and overcoming these barriers requires creation of a

transformation. 
What’s more, Bauwens et al. (2020) analysed that the concept of circular economy aligns with a

closing up the gaps between stakeholders and driving systemic change. 
Further elaboration on the topic was made by Caldera et al. (2022), who examined the 
challenges and opportunities for Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) in the adaptation

 On the other hand, the authors argue that SMEs, because of their agility and close community

2.3. Sustainable vs. Regenerative Leadership 
2.3.1. Sustainable vs. Regenerative Leadership 
This section differentiates sustainable leadership models focused on harm reduction from 
regenerative models that prioritize restoration and social equity. 
As organizations are constantly challenged with significant global challenges, leadership models 
are being developed to address not only the need for harm reduction but also the importance of 
fostering restoration and equity. While sustainable leadership focuses on minimizing negative 
impacts, regenerative leadership seeks to actively improve ecological and social systems. 
Regenerative leadership offers a different approach by fostering positive impacts on the socio-
ecological systems. Fullerton (2015) compared sustainable and regenerative models. It showed 
that sustainability often focuses on decreasing harm, and regeneration focuses on creating 
conditions that restore and improve the quality of systems. This approach aligns with principles 
of circular economy, where resources are not only maintained but also revitalized to sustain and 
develop within ecosystems. 
Hutchins and Storm (2019) further described these differences. They described regenerative 
leadership as proactive and future and innovation oriented. Furthermore, driven by a 
commitment to improve life-affirming systems. Unlike sustainable models that prioritize 
allegiance and efficiency, regenerative leadership prioritizes creativity, collaboration, and 
innovation, empowering organizations to become catalysts for positive systemic change. 
Bauwens et al. (2020) went a step further in this perspective by connecting regenerative 
principles to the circular economy. They argue that regenerative leadership requires a shift in 
mindset from linear and exploitative models to a holistic approach which focuses on renewing 
resources and maintaining the health of the ecosystem. This shift involves not only addressing 
the symptoms of  destruction but also the causes of systemic imperfections. Regenerative 
practices aim not just to restore but to improve the conditions of ecosystems, communities, or 
economies. 
2.3.2. Sustainability’s Limitations 
The concept of sustainability, while important in creating corporate responsibility, also has its 
limitations in the current ecological and societal challenges. Rockström et al. (2009) introduced 
the concept of planetary boundaries. It describes the limits within which humans have to adjust 
their actions to avoid catastrophic environmental degradation. The framework highlights the 
importance of mitigating harm, but it also highlights sustainability’s nature which often focuses 
on maintaining the status quo more than on driving systemic improvement and innovation. 
==PAGE:18==
17 
Elkington’s (1998) triple bottom line framework further analyzes and critiques the scope of 
sustainability. While the framework combines environmental, social, and economic aspects into 
the organizational decision-making process, its application often leads to progressive changes 
rather than transformational ones. The constant growth in complexity of ecological and social 
challenges shows the insufficiency of sustainability’s harm-reduction approach. It shows a need 
for moving toward models that prioritize systemic regeneration and flexibility. 
The concept of a regenerative economy is an innovative approach to sustainability, 
economic development, and social well-being. Its goal is to create systems that restore, 
renew, and revitalize their own sources of energy and materials. 
2.3.3. Toward a Regenerative Future 
The transition from sustainable to regenerative leadership creates a need for a new definition of 
organizational success. While sustainability aims for neutrality in operations within the planet's 
boundaries and without exhausting the resources, regeneration adds to this by making 
ecosystems and societies better than before. Morseletto (2020) underscored this distinction by 
showing that while restorative practices are used to repair the already made damage, the 
regenerative approach focuses on building strength and enhancing capacity and conditions for 
future generations. 
This proactive approach aligns with broader frameworks, such as those proposed for example 
by Casarejos (2020). He explored systems thinking (analyzed further on in this thesis) and 
resilience thinking as two important theories for implementation of regenerative perspectives in 
the long-term. His research also explores in depth the socio-ecological systems and their self-
organizing capacities. By doing this he emphasizes how in order to create regenerative and 
sustainable systematic change regenerative leadership should focus on being adaptive, goal-
seeking, and transformative. That’s why regenerative leadership is not only about achieving 
ecological balance but also about fostering equity, inclusion, and holistic well-being in human 
and non-human communities. 
By advancing the approach beyond sustainability’s limitations, regenerative leadership offers a 
model that addresses the real reasons for degradation but also fosters good conditions for the 
future for all stakeholders. This approach not only aligns with materializing expectations for 
corporate responsibility but also gives organizations the tools to navigate the complex 
challenges of the 21st century.

2.4.1. Core Traits of Regenerative Leadership 
One of the main characteristics of regenerative leadership is systems thinking, which 
emphasizes understanding the connection of social, ecological, and economic systems. 
Gharajedaghi (2011) presented the concept of systems thinking as dynamic, nonlinear, and 
interactive. It’s a concept that addresses complexity in organizational ecosystems. His work 
showed the decision-making processes are shaped by dynamic and adaptive processes in 
evolving environments. What is more, he also explained how leaders who adopt this mindset  
can prepare their teams and whole  organizations for different impacts in a long-term

Hutchins and Storm (2019) elaborate on this by highlighting the importance of empathy,

Wheatley (2006) showed the importance of leadership which prioritizes the well-being of 
systems. She highlighted the importance of values-driven frameworks, which motivate the

environments where both human and ecological systems can prosper. 
2.4.2. Ethical and Value-Based Leadership 
Ethical leadership is pivotal to the regenerative paradigm. It emphasizes commitment to shared

leaders extend this responsibility to ecosystems and future generations. This ensures that 
organizational practices contribute positively to all influenced systems. 
Freeman (2007) had a look at the stakeholder theory, which aligns closely with regenerative 
leadership by agreeing on the importance of a broader view of stakeholders. In the regenerative 
context, stakeholders include not only employees, customers, and shareholders but also 
ecosystems, communities, and future generations. This expanded perspective encourages 
leaders to adopt holistic strategies. This all balances competing priorities while focusing on 
mutual benefit. The ethical focus of regenerative leadership puts a need on leaders to act with

sustainability across all stakeholder groups. 
The mindset and values of regenerative leadership represent a significant departure from 
traditional models, emphasizing systemic thinking, empathy, and ethical responsibility. By

that regenerate ecosystems and promote social equity. While organizations face the complex 
challenges of the 21st century, regenerative leadership offers a potential framework for fostering

2.6. Systems Thinking 
Systems thinking is one of the main pillars of regenerative leadership, and can provide a 
framework for understanding the interconnectedness of social, ecological, and economic 
systems. Thanks to adopting a systems perspective it’s possible for leaders to navigate the 
complex challenges of modern organizations and design solutions which address the core of the 
problem causes and not just the symptoms. This holistic approach is very important for fostering 
resilience and adaptability in regenerative practices. 
2.6.1. Foundational Concepts in Systems Thinking 
Systems thinking discussed by Voulvoulis et al. (2022), is crucial for understanding and 
addressing complex challenges within sustainability. It involves recognizing the loops of 
interdependent factors and stakeholders, and how their decisions influence multiple factors in 
different angles. This approach shifts the focus only on isolated actions to the dynamic 
relationships within the systems. By adopting systems thinking, leaders can better prepare for 
complex challenges and design strategies that are more resilient and sustainable. 
Systems thinking gives a powerful way for understanding complex organizational and 
environmental challenges. It involves recognizing patterns, identifying feedback loops, and 
acknowledging how dynamic relationships work between different elements within a system 
Meadows (2008). Instead of only focusing on isolated components this approach helps leaders 
prepare for unexpected outcomes and develop sustainable strategies that enhance adaptability. 
By adopting a holistic perspective, leaders can create environments that encourage 
collaboration, continuous learning, and innovation while maintaining alignment with common 
social and ecological priorities Sterman (2000). 
Meadows (2008) expands on this by identifying leverage points where small changes can make 
significant impacts. For regenerative leaders this could mean identifying opportunities to support 
ecological and social well-being through targeted actions. For example, introducing circular 
processes within a supply chain can reduce waste while fostering partnerships that develop 
positive outcomes across industries. 
A holistic approach to leadership highlights the need for organizations to work in harmony with 
natural and social ecosystems. Leaders who recognize these interconnections can implement 
sustainable business principles within the core practices that integrate environmental 
responsibility, social well-being, and economic viability Meadows (2008). Aligning the practices 
and operations with regenerative principles ensures that businesses contribute positively to both 
their internal culture and the external communities they serve. 
2.6.2. Applications of Systems Thinking in Regenerative Leadership 
In practice, systems thinking gives regenerative leaders a way to address complex challenges 
such as climate change, biodiversity loss, and social inequality. For example, systems thinking

could help guide the integration of renewable energy solutions, ensuring that energy transitions 
consider not only technological and technical development but also community impacts and

What’s more, systems thinking promotes adaptive leadership. It is done by encouraging leaders 
to embrace uncertainty and experiment with innovative solutions. Furthermore, Uhl-Bien and 
Arena (2017) showed the importance of complexity leadership which can be used in enabling 
organizations to thrive in dynamic environments. Thanks to fostering collaboration and 
continuous learning and improvement regenerative leaders can build organizational cultures 
based on resilience to external changes and commitment to long-term regeneration. 
2.7. Head Heart Hand model 
The Head, Heart, and Hands model offers a holistic framework which can be used by the 
leaders for planning and practicing regenerative practices. This approach focuses cognitive, 
emotional, and practical aspects, which enables leaders to align vision, values, and actions in 
the process of systemic transformation. 
2.7.1. Head: Strategic Vision and Systems Thinking 
Islam et al. (2022) explained that the “Head” dimension in the model relates to using analytical 
tools and reflective practices to understand how complex systems work on a deeper level. 
Leaders can use methods such as scenario planning and data analysis to support and create a 
base for their decisions while ensuring they align them with sustainability principles at core. This 
dimension also highlights how important thinking is in the long-term and that leaders should be 
using diverse different perspectives to navigate uncertainty more effectively.  
According to Voulvoulis et al. (2022), systems thinking also supports the concept of "Head" 
dimension by motivating leaders to challenge the already existing reality and develop their 
vision beyond  current boundaries. This perspective could help leaders design projects that 
balance environmental, economic, and social goals, with a more holistic and sustainable 
approach to leadership in mind. 
The "Head" angle in leadership represents things like strategic vision, critical thinking, and 
holistic systems understanding. Leaders should develop a deep understanding of the 
ecosystems in which they operate and use the data-driven analysis and scenario planning to 
navigate uncertainty and drive innovation Sterman (2000). A focus on organizational learning 
fosters adaptive thinking and adaptive decision-making, allowing teams to respond effectively to 
change Uhl-Bien & Arena (2017). 
Taking into account a long-term perspective is crucial for introducing regenerative leadership, 
especially in fields such as renewable energy, circular economy, and social operations. By 
integrating systems thinking into decision-making processes leaders can create strategies that 
align economic growth with environmental and community well-being Meadows (2008). Such a 
==PAGE:24==
23 
mindset promotes within leaders a way to prioritize sustainability, embrace complexity, and 
foster a culture of continuous learning and adaptation Wheatley (2017). 
2.7.2. Heart: Emotional Intelligence and Empathy 
The "Heart" aspect focuses on the emotional and relational side of leadership. It shows that 
empathy and emotional intelligence are essential for regenerative leaders, as they enable them 
to build trust, improve collaboration, and address the diverse needs of all of the stakeholders. 
Wheatley (2017) highlights the importance of relational leadership, showing the advantage of 
leaders who prioritize connections and empathy in their interactions and actions. 
In a regenerative context, the "Heart" dimension ensures that leadership is inclusive, equal and 
supportive. By actively listening to all of the sides and voices and incorporating diverse 
perspectives leaders can create a culture of belonging and mutual support. This is all essential 
for driving a meaningful and constant systemic change. 
2.7.3. Hands: Action and Practical Implementation 
Finally, the "Hands" dimension focuses on the practical application of the leadership principles. 
This includes transforming vision and values into tangible outcomes. It can be done by effective 
planning, resource allocation, and execution of the planned actions. Leaders within their teams 
and organizations should implement strategies that align with organizational regenerative goals, 
such as adopting circular economy practices or investing in community development to ensure 
that it all becomes a reality. 
As an interesting analysis Laloux (2016) highlights the high potential of self-managing teams to 
incorporate the "Hands" dimension within the team’s work. By empowering teams with 
autonomy and focus on a purpose leaders can create an environment which ensures that 
regenerative practices are incorporated fundamentally across all levels of the organization. This  
approach fosters accountability and adaptability of the teams and their actions, enabling 
organizations to thrive within complex challenges. 
The true power of the Head, Heart, and Hands model lies in its integration. Regenerative 
leaders should focus on balancing strategic vision with emotional intelligence and practical 
actions. These all can ensure that their decisions are both structured and impactful. For 
example, a leader who implements a regenerative production initiative must combine a systemic 
understanding of production practices (Head), empathy for the needs of employees and local 
communities (Heart), and practical actions through resource allocation and training programs 
(Hands). 
This model can also serve as a guiding framework for leaders’ development. By incorporating 
the Head, Heart, and Hands approach into training programs, organizations can encourage 
collaboration with leaders who are prepared and motivated to drive systemic change and foster 
resilience within their teams and communities.

3. Methodology 
To explore the development and application of regenerative leadership practices, this thesis 
employs a qualitative research approach rooted in regenerative principles, systems thinking, 
and reflective inquiry. The methodology is designed to provide a holistic understanding of how 
leaders can integrate regenerative principles into their behaviors, decision-making processes, 
and organizational influence. 
 
This research follows a multi-phased, iterative design that combines a literature review and 
expert interviews. Each phase builds upon the previous one, ensuring a dynamic and 
interconnected approach that aligns with the evolving nature of regenerative leadership. Rather 
than reinventing the wheel, this study draws inspiration from existing leadership frameworks and 
sustainability methodologies, adapting and expanding them where necessary to fit the context of 
regenerative leadership. While some elements of our framework are entirely original and

The literature review establishes a theoretical foundation by synthesizing insights from 
regenerative principles, leadership, biomimicry, and systems thinking. Expert interviews provide

practices and relates the cognitive domain (head) to critical reflection, the affective domain 
(heart) to relational knowing and the psychomotor domain (hands) to engagement. The 
approach of this model is unique and can be applied in the context of regeneration as it not only 
focuses on action but also links intellect with emotions and looks at the leadership area in a 
more holistic way. 
 
Together, these elements create a comprehensive approach that equips leaders not only to 
perform but to catalyze transformation. Transformative processes are necessary to shift away 
from a human-centered view of the environment toward more sustainable and then regenerative  
values and behaviours.

Emphasizing Heart transforms the organization’s culture, leading to greater trust, inclusivity, and 
employee well-being. This creates a supportive environment where people are motivated to 
collaborate, innovate, and drive meaningful change. 
 
● Hands - Action-Oriented, Adaptive Leadership 
 
The Hands aspect of regenerative leadership focuses on taking decisive, courageous action 
and empowering teams to drive tangible change. Leaders must promote adaptability, encourage 
collaboration across teams, and lead by example. Key areas of focus include: 
 
➔ Empowering Teams: Equip teams with the tools, autonomy, and resources needed to 
work toward regenerative goals, encouraging decentralized decision-making. 
➔ Courageous Execution: Implement regenerative initiatives boldly, even when they 
challenge established norms, creating tangible results that reflect the organization’s 
purpose and values. 
➔ Adaptive Learning: Foster a culture of continuous learning, experimentation, and 
improvement to respond quickly to new insights or changes in the external environment. 
 
Leaders who focus on Hands can create agile, resilient organizations capable of making 
continuous, impactful strides toward regeneration. Empowered teams drive progress, supported 
by adaptive and innovative leadership.

4.3. System Thinking as an Integral Part of the Framework 
 
This framework places System Thinking at the heart of embedding regenerative practices, 
emphasizing the interconnected nature of all elements within an organization and its ecosystem.  
As Gharajedaghi, J. (2011) mentioned “understanding interdependency requires a way of 
thinking different from analysis. It requires systems thinking. And analytical thinking and 
systems thinking are quite distinct.” It shifts the focus from isolated actions to a holistic 
perspective, where all parts are viewed as interdependent components of a larger system.  
As Gharajedaghi, J. (2011) defined it “systems thinking uses a different process. It puts the 
system in the context of the larger environment of which it is a part and studies the role it plays 
in the larger whole.” 
By recognizing that changes in one area can move through the entire system, this approach 
enables leaders to design solutions that generate lasting value across multiple dimensions, from 
social well-being to environmental resilience and economic sustainability.

4.5. Addressing Similarities with Existing Models 
The Head, Heart, and Hands framework combined with the tools described in this thesis builds 
upon already existing models. The already existing frameworks often use similar terminologies 
but go in different directions considering the specific application of regenerative leadership. For 
example frameworks such as those presented and discussed by Allison (2018) and Her New 
Standard (2023) mainly focus on general leadership qualities and/or teaching techniques, 
however they don’t really focus on addressing the uniqueness of challenges within the 
regenerative transformation companies would face. The framework presented in this thesis 
goes further than this and beyond individual development or educational context. It is done 
thanks to: 
● Systems Thinking Integration: Unlike conventional theories and applications which 
treat "Head" angle as only cognitive reasoning, this model also incorporates systems 
thinking and long-term ecological and social consideration and strategies. What’s more, 
tools like doughnut economics and system mapping show in a practical way how leaders 
==PAGE:38==
37 
can align strategic vision of their activities within teams and organizations with planetary 
boundaries and social equity objectives. Thanks to this extensive focus it’s more likely 
that the decisions made by leaders consider different, often complex relationships on 
interconnected systems, from supply chains to local communities. 
● Emphasis on Regeneration: Many already existing frameworks focus primarily on 
personal or organizational improvement, and rather lack the straightforward goal of 
ecological and social restoration done thanks to the newly incorporated actions. This 
thesis focuses on putting the framework directly within the context of regenerative 
economy and leadership. It is done while emphasizing how leaders can restore already 
damaged ecosystems and make sure that they won’t get damaged any further in the 
future while fostering community well-being. From a practical point of view, leaders can 
get inspiration from biomimicry to design processes that mimic natural systems. This 
helps achieve both functional efficiency and environmental restoration. 
● Focus on Stakeholder Collaboration: While existing frameworks may also include 
collaboration as a value, the model proposed in this thesis emphasizes stakeholder 
inclusion as a foundation of the "Heart" dimension. Leaders are highly encouraged to 
engage in conversations and collaboration with diverse groups to notice the real 
problems and co-create solutions. This is a critical aspect of regenerative transformation. 
For example, involving communities and societies in sustainable projects ensures that 
the projects and its outcomes align with local needs and build mutual trust. 
● Holistic Adaptability: By combining tools and principles this framework provides a 
flexible but fully comprehensive approach that leaders can easily adapt to various 
industries, teams and organizational contexts. This makes it different from other 
frameworks with narrower applications. This framework is composed in a way that a 
manufacturing company and a marketing agency can both use and incorporate the 
framework by adjusting the tools and principles  to their specific challenges and goals. 
The Head, Heart, and Hands model represents a holistic approach for leaders which integrates 
cognitive, emotional, and practical angles. By putting it together with tools described above and 
system thinking and biomimicry the framework becomes a powerful instrument for leaders 
seeking to implement regenerative principles within their organizations. Together, they can 
provide a structured and planned route for leaders to challenge and navigate the complexities of 
implementing and developing ecological and social well-being both within and outside their 
organizations. 
The integration of the Head, Heart, and Hands model with practical tools creates a holistic 
approach to regenerative leadership. Each component complements the others and this all 
allows to form framework that support leaders in the process of: 
● Development of strategies based on the systems thinking (Head). 
● Encouragement of inclusive and empathetic organizational cultures (Heart).

In summary, by combining the Head, Heart, and Hands framework with different tools like

5.1. Findings and Insights from the Senior Manager in

The qualitative interview with a senior manager in sustainability at LEGO Group provided critical 
insights into the role of regenerative leadership in driving sustainable and impactful change 
within organizations. This discussion shed light on several important aspects of regenerative 
practices, other systems and frameworks that play an important role in regenerative practices, 
the challenges of implementation, and the broader implications of shifting towards a 
regenerative economy. 
 
The interview confirmed the theory that regenerative leadership emphasizes the creation of 
value that not only mitigates harm but actively generates positive impact. The interviewee 
explained that  
 
“Regenerative leadership focuses on fostering vibrant systems through high-quality 
relationships. For instance, a thriving ecosystem works because of the relationships between its 
parts, much like the human body’s organs functioning together through signals and feedback

This perspective goes beyond traditional sustainability by integrating social cohesion, 
innovation, and systems thinking. The interviewee put vast emphasizes on system thinking and 
the interconnectedness of it:  
 
“When you apply systems thinking practically, it’s not about analyzing individual elements—it’s 
about synthesizing the whole. For example, instead of building a strategy in isolation and 
presenting it, a systems approach involves collaboration to foster relationships and create 
shared ownership.”  
It was highlighted that regenerative approaches are transformative as they account for the 
interconnectivity of systems and strive to create enduring positive effects across economic, 
social, and environmental dimensions. 
 
In the interview social cohesion emerged as a foundational element of regenerative leadership. 
Strengthening bonds within communities was recognized as absolutely essential, whether 
internal to the organization or in the external ecosystem. The main insight is that stronger 
connections reduce stress, foster collaboration, and lead to long-term benefits such as reduced 
social inequalities and healthier environments. However, the interviewee noted that measuring 
social cohesion and its associated impacts remains a challenge for organizations.

5.1.1. Challenges in Implementation 
 
The complexity of integrating regenerative leadership within large organizations was another 
important topic that was widely discussed during the interview. Resistance to change and the 
fact that in most organizations traditional systems are still in place were identified as significant 
barriers. The process of steering a "large machine" like a multinational corporation toward 
regeneration requires a deliberate and phased approach. The interviewee shared examples of 
innovative companies successfully integrating regenerative practices, such as Interface, which 
adopted biomimicry to redesign its business model, demonstrating the feasibility and benefits of 
regeneration-focused strategies. 
 
It was also further noted that only a minority of organizations are genuinely committed to 
sustainability, let alone regenerative principles. While some pioneering, innovative companies 
are driving change, the interview highlighted that the majority remain hesitant, and fear moving 
away from traditional systems. Instead, they focus on superficial, short-term changes rather 
than deep systemic transformation. This gap highlights the urgent need for greater awareness 
of the global situation and the need for adoption of regenerative leadership across various 
industries.

Another critical point was the importance of frameworks to measure the impact of regenerative 
practices.  
 
“The biggest challenge I see is how we define value and how we measure it.“

investment companies and allows them to gain insights into the sustainability performance of 
their businesses and their partners, investment targets, or suppliers. 
 
Impactly and other such tools help organizations assess their value creation comprehensively 
and bridge the gap between conceptualizing and quantifying impact, enabling leaders to make 
informed decisions and align actions with regenerative goals. 
 
The conversation also underscored the importance of post-growth economic models. The 
models that were discussed challenge the current approach to economy and advocate more for 
a shift from linear growth metrics to holistic measures of well-being and sustainability. Adopting 
such models would mean a mindset change, reinforcing the role of leaders as catalysts for 
systemic innovation. Without realising that well-being should also be considered a value and a 
factor in the transformation towards regeneration a true, long-term change will not be possible 
within any organisation.

6. Evolving the Framework - After Empirical Insights 
 
This section presents improvements to the regenerative leadership framework developed in this 
thesis based on insights and feedback from conducted interviews. These improvements focus 
on improving general clarity, usability, and practicality in various organizational settings. The 
proposed improvements address areas like communication strategies, planned implementation 
steps, measurement and scalable evaluation and industry-specifications. This chapter aims to 
improve the framework to support leaders in driving transformative change. 
 
This chapter highlights only the elements that are completely new, have evolved or changed 
post-interviews, ensuring a clear focus on key developments. For instance, the Head, Heart, 
Hands model remains unchanged and is therefore not included in this section, although it

the framework to better support leaders in driving transformative change.

Regenerative Change 
 
The revised framework that highlights important steps of the leaders path to embed 
regenerative practices within the organisation reflects key modifications based on expert 
feedback, particularly in strengthening the planning stage. The process is still divided into three 
key stages: Planning, Execution, and Sustain and each stage focuses on distinct actions and 
principles that guide leaders in transforming organizational practices and mindsets.

6.3. Structuring Implementation pathways 
Based on our knowledge and feedback we’ve received during the interviews there’s a 
conclusion that transitioning regenerative leadership from theory to practice creates a need for a 
structured implementation process plan and it’s essential in order for leaders to succeed. That’s 
why the framework should introduce a phased approach which will allow leaders to integrate 
regenerative principles gradually within their teams and potentially whole organizations. 
6.3.1. Assessment & Visioning  
 
In this phase leaders can assess their current state using a regenerative leadership readiness 
tool and then define a vision aligned with long-term regeneration goals.One effective tool for this 
phase is System Mapping. System mapping is a rather easy and clear tool that helps to 
visualize the relationships between stakeholders, processes, and resources within an 
organization. This model allows leaders to identify leverage points, systemic inefficiencies, and 
opportunities for transformation Meadows (2008). 
The system mapping can be used for better understanding of the roles and influence of various 
stakeholders. For example, mapping could reveal the influence of local communities on supply 
chain operations. This on the other hand enables leaders to engage and address the problems 
within the community Meadows (2008). Another example of connection could be the fact that 
governments set regulations that guide processes like energy use or recycling and they can be 
influenced by concerns of local communities about specific matters. 
==PAGE:60==
59 
By analyzing connections between processes, leaders can identify areas where some even 
small changes could create significant benefits. For example, simply by improving energy 
efficiency in a production facility the company might reduce costs, emissions, and dependency 
on non-renewable resources. A better plan of recycling might not only reduce the harm to the 
environment but might also reduce costs, reduce the usage of materials in total and might 
create a need for local jobs in this field.  
The above examples show that system maps help leaders visualize feedback loops and 
potential trade-offs. It’s a perfect tool to notice patterns and schemes within the actions of the 
company not only inside it but also outside in the closest and further environment. It might show 
the connections and needs that weren't previously seen. 
6.3.2. Strategic Integration 
During the phase of strategic integration regenerative leadership principles are integrated into 
core business values and operations. What’s more the principles should also be added in 
leadership development programs, and stakeholder engagement strategies. To support and 
coordinate this process leaders can apply the Doughnut Economic Model as a guiding tool. 
Thanks to using this model, leaders can make sure that their operations and plans can be 
placed within both social foundations and ecological ceilings. This creates a structured vision 
that works well within planetary and societal boundaries. Thanks to integrating this tool 
organizations can structure decision-making processes that balance financial, social, and 
environmental outcomes. 
The Doughnut Economics model introduced by Raworth (2017) provides leaders with a powerful 
tool for redefining the vision of economic success within their teams and organizations. The 
model is presented as a doughnut-shaped diagram with two concentric circles: 
● The Social Foundation (Inner circle): Represents the basic needs and requirements 
for human well-being, such as access to water, food, education, health care, and social 
equity. 
● The Ecological Ceiling (Outer circle): Represents planetary boundaries that should 
not be exceeded, such as climate change, land conversion, and biodiversity loss. 
(Rockström et al., 2009). 
==PAGE:61==
60 
 
Figure 9: Figure 9: Doughnut Economic Model  
Source: Raworth (2017) 
Leaders can apply the Doughnut Economics model into their organizational strategy by 
preparing assessment whether operations fall within the "safe and just space for humanity" (the 
space between two circles of limitations) Raworth (2017) For instance, companies might be able 
to map their environmental impact compared to the planetary boundaries. This allows us to 
identify areas for improvement, such as water usage or carbon emissions. 
What’s more, the model is a great way for leaders to choose initiatives and projects that balance 
ecological sustainability with social equity, and thus should be prioritized in the organization’s 
actions. An example of such actions could be setting goals to eliminate single-use plastics by 
investing in community development projects introducing different alternatives for such 
products. Raworth (2017) 
Leaders together with their teams can establish key performance indicators (KPIs) aligned with 
the Doughnut’s dimensions. These could touch on aspects such as for example tracking metrics 
of employees living wages or biodiversity restoration in areas where the organization operates. 
To be more specific, an example of usage of the tool could be an international food company 
which could apply the Doughnut Economics model by ensuring a positive impact of its 
agricultural practices. These could be things such as restoring soil health, reducing water 
consumption and waste, and improving the well-being of smaller farmers. The companies could 
set KPIs for these goals and publicly report progress to stakeholders. Raworth (2017) 
6.3.3. Pilot and Scaling 
Initial regenerative projects as every other project serve often as prototypes. This allows leaders

6.4. Integrating Measurement and Evaluation Tools 
Based on the feedback received from experts and practitioners, there were identified key areas 
for improvement in the measurement and evaluation of the framework for regenerative 
leadership. While the initial framework provided a foundation for tracking the progress, the 
interviews showed four important areas for improvement: 
1. Leadership self-assessment; 
2. Holistic impact measurement;  
3. Third-party verification; 
4. Adaptive benchmarking models. 
These improvements focus both on quantitative and qualitative angles of regenerative 
leadership. 
6.4.1. Regenerative Leadership Self-Assessment Tool (RLSA) 
First addition to the framework in this section is the Regenerative Leadership Self-Assessment 
Tool (RLSA). It’s a tool which addresses the need for a structured self evaluation of leadership 
competencies in a regenerative context. Interviewees explained that in their opinions, while it’s 
already quite often that organizations measure sustainability performance in their operations 
there are no tools to assess how well leaders integrate regenerative principles into their 
decision-making and the way they influence their teams and organizations. The self-assessment 
==PAGE:64==
63 
tool would be designed to give leaders a structured feedback and tips on aspects across three 
key dimensions based on the head, Heart, Hands model: 
● Strategic Vision & Systems Thinking (Head) - evaluation of a leader's ability to 
incorporate regenerative principles into long-term strategy, apply systems thinking, and 
drive innovation. 
● Collaboration & Emotional Intelligence (Heart) - assesses how effectively a leader builds 
trust, engagement, and inclusive decision-making in the context of regenerative 
transformation within the teams. 
● Action & Implementation (Hands) - measures the leader’s ability to translate 
regenerative principles into tangible actions, introduce new initiatives, and perform a 
transformation. 
The self-assessment would be built on a combination of self-reflection questionnaires, 360-
degree feedback from peers and stakeholders, and scenario-planned decision-making 
exercises. This approach would ensure that the evaluation is not only based on self-perception 
but also incorporates external perspectives. What’s more, the new framework proposes AI-
driven analytics and coaching recommendations which would give leaders customized insights 
to support personal development in alignment with regenerative leadership goals. 
In addition to self-assessment tools there is a need for the regenerative leadership dashboard to 
integrate a bigger range of key indicators. It has been highlighted that while existing 
sustainability dashboards track quantitative environmental performance metrics, they often don’t 
capture the behavioral and cultural aspects of regenerative transformation. To address this, the 
dashboard now would include additional qualitative and stakeholder-based indicators: 
● Behavioral and cultural metrics - these will track changes in organizational mindset, 
leadership, and the integration of regenerative principles into structures. 
● Stakeholder trust indicators - these will assess employee engagement and external 
stakeholder perceptions of an organization’s regenerative efforts. 
● Feedback loops - these will allow organizations to monitor how internal and external 
stakeholders respond to regenerative strategies over time. 
These improvements will create a more holistic and practical measurement of regenerative 
leadership by combining traditional KPIs with qualitative leadership and culture assessments. 
The next key improvement to the framework is improving the  third-party verification processes 
to foster credibility and prevent greenwashing among stakeholders. Based on the feedback 
received during the interviews there was an identified risk that regenerative leadership, similarly 
to sustainable efforts and initiatives in general, could be used as a branding tool rather than a 
genuine transformation approach. That is why improving the third-party verification measures 
proves that organizations are accountable and that their progress is validated. To do so it is 
recommended to add to the framework following add-ons: 
==PAGE:65==
64 
● Regular third-party audits that evaluate association with regenerative practices, ensuring 
continuous improvement. 
● Stakeholder validation mechanisms such as public transparency reports and community 
feedback platforms to verify regenerative practices and their results from multiple 
perspectives. 
By adding these external validation processes into the framework leaders could make their 
accountability stronger, build trust with all of the stakeholders and make sure that their 
regenerative leadership commitments are actually  measurable. 
Lastly in this part, there’s an improvement in a comparative benchmarking model as a part of 
the framework to allow leaders to be more adaptive within the process of continuous change. 
Initial model focused on static industry comparisons, but based on the feedback it’s important  to 
sufficiently approach the differences in organizational maturity and industry-specific challenges. 
In order to do so the following are introduced: 
● Sector-specific comparison models which will ensure that regenerative impact is

● Collaborative benchmarking networks which encourage teams and whole organizations

This shift to a more flexible and adaptive model ensures that leaders can track both their 
internal progress but also their position within broader industries and ecosystems while 
maintaining focus on long-term regenerative goals. 
These improvements work on the scalability and credibility for the measurement and evaluation 
of the framework. The framework now provides a more comprehensive and actionable approach 
to tracking regenerative leadership and practices. These improvements align with the vision to 
create organizations that do not just sustain but actively contribute to the regeneration of 
ecological, social, and economic systems.

The intention is to encourage leaders from various sectors, who may not have prior knowledge

6.6.2. Overview of the Revised Framework 
This framework is still rooted in Biomimicry Thinking (Biomimicry.net) and the Biomimicry 
Design Spiral (de Pauw, I., Kandachar, P., Karana, E., Peck, D., & Wever, R., 2010), both of 
which guide problem-solving through nature-inspired strategies. However, adjustments have 
been made to ensure the model is clear  and adaptable to a variety of organizational contexts. 
The framework uses simple, universal language that follows a structured, iterative process that 
allows leaders to identify challenges, seek inspiration from natural systems, develop and test 
ideas, and continuously refine their approaches to align with regenerative principles. By 
simplifying the language and restructuring the flow, this model ensures that even those 
unfamiliar with biomimicry can effectively apply it. 
6.6.3. Steps of the Framework 
This biomimicry inspired framework follows an iterative process that consists of 5 steps that can 
be fitted into four border areas: scoping, discovering, creating and evaluating. These steps are: 
1. Identify Challenges (Understand Team & Environment) - The process begins with 
defining the key challenges within the team or organization. Leaders should assess their 
internal structures, cultural dynamics, and environmental impacts to determine areas 
==PAGE:71==
70 
that require improvement. This step ensures that the framework is applied in a context-
specific manner, addressing both operational and leadership-related concerns. 
2. Explore Nature’s Solutions (Find Patterns & Processes) - Once challenges are identified, 
the next step involves looking to nature for inspiration. In this stage leaders are 
encouraged to study how ecosystems and biological systems have solved their 
problems and seek inspiration in natural regenerative processes. Whether through 
resilience, adaptability, or efficiency, nature provides models that can be translated into 
leadership and organizational strategies. 
3. Brainstorm Ideas (Apply Nature’s Wisdom)- Leaders should try to get inspiration from 
the lessons from nature. In this stage leaders and their teams could begin brainstorming 
and designing solutions. This stage is about applying biomimetic principles to leadership 
practices, decision-making structures, and organizational workflows. The goal is to 
create strategies that are efficient, resilient, and regenerative — thus mirroring the 
success of nature’s long-evolved systems that not only survive but also thrive. 
4. Test & Adapt (Refine - Based on Feedback) - Even though well-defined vision, correct 
values and strong foundation is important it’s the practical implementation that is crucial. 
At this stage leaders should translate their ideas in real-world scenarios, gathering 
feedback and assessing effectiveness. At this stage of the spiral iteration is key. Testing 
allows organizations to refine their regenerative leadership strategies based on practical 
insights and real-time outcomes. 
5. Reflect & Improve (Ensure Impact and Assess Against Regenerative Goals) - The final 
step of the spiral involves evaluating the short term and long-term impact of the 
implemented changes. Leaders should continuously assess whether their approaches 
are fostering a regenerative and sustainable culture within their organizations. 
Adjustments should be made as necessary, ensuring that the organization evolves 
toward more effective and impactful regenerative practices over time. If the results of the 
implemented actions are not satisfactory the spiral can be applied again and the process 
should be repeated to try a different approach. 
By restructuring this framework into a clear and practical cycle with simplified language without 
technical jargon, it becomes more accessible to leaders that may not have prior experience with 
regenerative approaches. The simplified version encourages a broader audience to integrate 
biomimicry-inspired leadership practices into their teams, ultimately creating more sustainable 
and resilient organizations. This model not only aligns with biomimicry principles but also 
supports real-world application, ensuring that regenerative strategies are actually actionable.

Ditte agreed that a communication plan is critical and she also highlighted the importance of a 
common vision. 
“One key factor in driving change is clarity. Even if the process is continuously evolving, it’s 
important to define the "why" and "what" of the transformation. If people feel that the change 
has no clear goal, they may struggle to engage.” 
This validates our framework as it puts a vast emphasis on the planning stage and setting clear 
goals and communicating the vision to all engaged stakeholders. 
Ditte also highlighted that one of the most critical aspects of communication in any vast 
transformation is commitment and accountability at the level one (executive level). Ditte 
highlighted the necessity of embedding sustainability efforts within the company structure, 
ensuring that top-level management drives the change. 
"It’s more than just communication, it’s about leadership commitment. If lower-level employees 
are on board but top management isn’t vocal about it, the initiative loses momentum. 
Regeneration must be integrated into business decisions, KPIs, and daily operations, not 
treated as a side project." 
This observation aligns well with the Head, Heart, and Hands model included in the framework, 
reinforcing the importance of visionary leadership and strategic alignment. Furthermore it 
==PAGE:78==

validates the inclusion of KPIs and goal setting in the planning stage. However, Dittes insight 
adds additional value and suggest that KPIs set should be not only for the project but for all 
executive level employees engaged in the process. Ditte further noted that sustainability efforts 
should not be seen as standalone initiatives but should be fully embedded in the business 
model, influencing both decision-making and operational processes. 
7.3.2. Stakeholder Engagement and Implementation Challenges 
Another key takeaway from the interview was the importance of aligning stakeholders across 
different levels of the organization. Drawing from her own experience, Ditte discussed the 
impact of structured workstreams and clearly defined responsibilities in driving her successful 
transformations. 
"We identified eight workstreams with clear workstream responsibles who needed to ensure that 
different initiatives were solved around this transformation. For the ones where they really gave 
the mandate to the workstream responsible, outlined the plan, the KPIs for the process, and 
followed up on it week to week, we actually saw the positive benefit." 
This validates the importance of interconnectedness and ensuring alignment of all key 
stakeholders.  
Ditte also pointed out that when alignment was lacking or corners were cut, the projects were 
either abandoned or severely slowed down the pace. This insight highlights the framework's 
need to emphasize structured implementation approaches, ensuring that leadership’s initial 
vision translates into actionable and measurable outcomes. 
7.3.3. Addressing Greenwashing Risks 
During the interview the challenge of avoiding greenwashing was also discussed. Ditte was able 
to draw on her past job experience where a company faced legal repercussions due to 
sustainability claims. She stressed the importance of having clear, data-driven plans to support 
sustainability statements and ensure credibility: 
"So if you're in doubt about whether there could be a risk of greenwashing, you need to back it 
with a very clear plan around:  
Where are we today? Where do we want to go? How are we following up? What does the data 
actually show that will allow us to differentiate?  
And you follow that, monitor that, and have the background and roadmap in place to show why 
we are saying this. If it's not where we are today but a place we're aiming for, then how can we 
show what the plan is for getting there?" 
This aligns with the framework’s focus on measurability and accountability, and further 
reinforces the need for transparent reporting and robust sustainability roadmaps, which is 
included in the Sustain stage.

8. Conclusion 
This study explored the role of leadership as a catalyst in driving organizational transformation 
toward a regenerative economy. It identified and focused on filling the gaps in existing 
leadership models. It has been shown that due to the fact that environmental and social crises 
show the limitations of conventional economic models, leaders should aim to shift from reducing 
the harm to actively restoring ecosystems and improving social well-being. This research 
confirmed that leadership is a catalytic factor in this change process. The study found that 
leadership acts as a catalyst for regenerative transformation by: 
==PAGE:82==
81 
1. Setting a clear vision and strategic direction - they define purpose and align goals with 
ecological and social well-being, ensuring long-term impact. 
 
2. Creating cultural and systemic shifts -  the leadership integrates regenerative principles 
into organizational culture, and makes sure it is rooted at all levels. 
 
3. Engaging and inspiring stakeholders - regenerative leaders foster collaboration and 
engagement of employees, communities, and external partners in creating common 
solutions. 
 
4. Implementing change through structured frameworks - regenerative transformation 
requires a systematic approach.  
 
5. Ensuring measurement and accountability - leaders play a big role in defining 
regenerative KPIs, tracking progress, and ensuring transparency in impact reporting. 
 
6. Navigating resistance and challenges - leadership commitment is crucial in addressing 
internal resistance, regulatory challenges, and financial concerns. 
By combining insights from theory, real business practices, and validation through expert 
interviews, this research developed a comprehensive leadership framework which was 
designed to support organizations in a regenerative transformation. The Head, Heart, and 
Hands model, together with systems thinking and biomimicry created a foundation for structured 
three-stage approach (Planning, Execution, and Sustain) which allow to progressively 
implement regeneration. The study's very important findings is that regenerative leadership is 
not a one-size-fits-all approach and it requires flexibility for  different organizational contexts. 
The study developed a practical leadership framework to guide organizations through 
regenerative transformation. The key elements of this framework include: 
1. Head, Heart, and Hands Model - leaders should combine strategic thinking (Head), 
emotional intelligence and stakeholder engagement (Heart), and take action in 
implementation (Hands) to drive regenerative transformation effectively. 
 
2. Three-Stage Implementation Approach (Planning, Execution, Sustain) - the framework 
follows a structured, phased approach: 
 
a. Planning Stage: defining a regenerative vision, assessing current conditions, and 
aligning stakeholders to create a foundation for transformation. 
b. Execution Stage: initiation of regenerative projects, building teams, and 
integration of regenerative leadership principles into daily operations. 
c. Sustain Stage: creation of performance metrics, reporting, and continuous 
learning mechanisms to ensure the long-term success of regenerative leadership 
initiatives. 
 
==PAGE:83==
82 
3. Systems Thinking and Biomimicry as Foundational Tools - it helps leaders recognize the 
interconnectedness of all identified economic, social, and ecological systems. 
Additionally biomimicry provides a nature-inspired approach for designing regenerative 
business models. 
 
4. Communication Plan for Stakeholder Engagement - a crucial part of the framework, 
based on experts’ feedback. It’s a structured communication plan that ensures clarity, 
mutuality, and engagement from employees, investors, customers, and regulators. 
 
5. Measurement and Adaptability Mechanisms – they ensure responsibility and continuous 
improvement. The framework introduces tools such as self-assessment mechanisms, 
regenerative leadership dashboards, third-party verification, and adaptable 
benchmarking models. These allow organizations to track progress and prevent 
greenwashing. 
 
6. Economic Justification Models - organizations are more likely to adopt regenerative 
leadership if there is a clear business case. The framework includes financial modeling 
tools and cost-benefit analysis to demonstrate the long-term value of regenerative 
initiatives. 
The validation process confirmed that the framework is practical, actionable, and relevant. It 
proved that it can be used by organizations trying to move into regeneration. On the other hand, 
it also identified challenges that the process is facing and these are related mainly to 
expandability, cultural resistance, and economics. In particular, the research showed the need 
for organizations to find a balance between short-term financial pressures and long-term 
regenerative investments. 
In conclusion, this research contributes to the system of regenerative economy by filling the gap 
between theoretical frameworks and possible real-world application. It gives a practical 
approach for leaders to transition their organizations to truly regenerative ones, while ensuring 
that they are not only minimizing harm but actively contributing to ecological and social 
restoration. This study is a foundation for future research on regenerative leadership by offering 
a practical and structured framework that organizations can customize based on their specific 
needs, industry dynamics etc.

8.2. Research Limitations 
While this study provides valuable addition into regenerative leadership research it has some 
limitations which should be acknowledged. First of all, regenerative leadership still is a 
developing field of research and comes with limited prior academic findings. This study 
connects concepts from a broad combination of fields - sustainability, regeneration, systems 
thinking, biomimicry, change management, and adaptive leadership, however, as the theoretical 
foundation is so broad, that means the framework relies on multiple interdisciplinary sources. 
That is why there is a need for long-term practical testing to validate its effectiveness in 
organizational settings. 
What is more, somewhat a limitation, especially in a futuristic point of view is the mainly 
qualitative nature of the research methodology. This study used expert interviews and themed 
analysis which provided important insights but lacked statistical data analysis. Future research 
should incorporate a more quantitative approach, such as large-scale surveys or controlled 
experiments of real-life implementation, to analyse the impact of regenerative leadership in a 
measurable way. 
Finally, the process of implementation and organizational adoption is a limitation as well due to 
aspects such as resistance, financial uncertainty, and different strategic priorities. Many 
organizations are deeply cored in traditional leadership models which make the transition 
toward regenerative leadership a fundamental and highly complex shift in the core mindset. The 
risk of lack of immediate financial gains may often discourage leaders to use the framework, as 
regenerative leadership is often seen as a long-term investment rather than an immediate cost-
saving initiative. Even though this concern has been addressed in this study, future research 
should further explore best practices for overcoming these barriers. 
Despite the identified limitations, this study develops the theory and practice of regenerative 
leadership and  provides a strong foundation for further research and practical implementation 
within organizations. By addressing and developing the identified limitations, future studies can 
further improve the framework’s scalability, effectiveness, and its impact. 
8.3. Future Research Directions 
To further develop this study’s findings the future research should explore diverse angles to 
strengthen both the theoretical foundation and future practical application. First of all, it should 
focus on continuous studies which analyse a long-term adaptation of regenerative leadership 
within an organizational setting. It could assess changes in its culture, leadership mindset, 
financial performance, and finally environmental and social impact on a long run. Such research 
would provide real life proof on whether regenerative leadership leads to the desired 
transformation and how it develops across different ecosystems. 
Another important direction is a structured quantitative analysis mentioned in the research’s 
limitations section, which could measure the actual financial and operational impact of

develop data-driven models to evaluate indicators such as ROI, cost savings, leadership

Given the diversity of industries and how it’s been established that it influences the regenerative 
leadership implementation, the sector-specific adaptations of the framework should also be 
further explored. Even though this study explored different organizational settings and 
implemented customization within the proposed framework, there’s still area for improvement

Thresholds: Living systems operate within thresholds. Positive and negative feedback loops

Relationships: What’s crucial is focusing on the relationships between the parts rather than just

Interconnection: The interconnectedness of the system determines its functionality.

Interconnection over disconnection. 
Cycles over linear processes. 
Synthesis over analysis. 
Relationships over isolation.

thinking, it’s the relationships—not just the parts—that create change. 
 
Moving from degeneration to regeneration is about addressing the interconnected challenges 
we face—what some call the “polycrisis.” Regenerative leadership focuses on fostering vibrant 
systems through high-quality relationships. For instance, a thriving ecosystem works because of 
the relationships between its parts, much like the human body’s organs functioning together 
through signals and feedback loops.

For example, planting rows of pine trees for carbon credits ignores the broader ecosystem—like 
mycelium networks, foliage, and animal interdependence. A regenerative approach would 
consider how all these elements interact and contribute to a thriving ecosystem.

Interviewee 
Yes, greenwashing is a growing concern. The key to avoiding it is ensuring transparency—

The EU Green Claims Directive is highly relevant here, but there’s also the opposite issue, 
known as "green-hushing," where companies do good work but don’t talk about it for fear of 
being scrutinized. 
It’s important to educate organizations on what qualifies as greenwashing and how to 
communicate sustainability efforts effectively. Companies that do this well often turn their 
communication into an educational opportunity rather than just a marketing strategy.
