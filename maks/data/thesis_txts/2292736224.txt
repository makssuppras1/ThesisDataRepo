Abstract 
Orotate phosphoribosyltransferase (OPRTase) catalyzes the formation of oritidine 5’-
monophosphate (OMP) from orotate (OA) and phosphoribosyl pyrophosphate (PRPP). 
Various research groups have published crystal structures of OPRTase, and kinetic and 
mutant studies has been applied as well.  
In this thesis, the mechanism of the catalytic loop of the dimeric OPRTase is investigated. 
Wild type, and two mutant forms of OPRTase was used for X-ray crystallography 
experiments, and multiple crystal structures were solved. A new apo structure and an 
E.2CA complex is presented from wild type enzyme, along with a H111-5fW.OMP.SO4 
complex with a closed catalytic loop conformation, an asymmetric H111-5fW.OA.Pi 
complex, an R105.OMP complex, and an asymmetric DL.OMP.Pi complex. 
Molecular dynamics simulations were applied to 11 different model systems, designed 
from various crystal structures, using OPLS 2005 force field and TIP3P solvent model. 
These simulations provide insight into the movement of the residues that constitute the 
catalytic loop, and general fluctuations in the enzyme. A high correlation between B-
factors calculated from RMSF values of atoms in simulations and B-factors from crystal 
structures is shown, and appear to highly depend on ligands in the active sites. 
A general contraction of each subunit is seen in published crystal structures, and this 
movement is verified in simulations, where the distance between the catalytic loop and 
the hood region is measured and correlated with differences in ligation.  
Residues participating in ligand binding are analyzed in multiple published crystal 
structures as well as in crystal structures solved in this thesis. The importance of Arg105 
and Lys109 are discussed as their participation in ligand binding in simulations are also 
apparent.

1 Introduction 
Computational technology can give details on atomic level which is difficult with 
experiments, and it can also be much cheaper. Experimental work, on the other hand, may 
provide new bases for computational calculations. Combining computational chemistry 
with experimental work can be synergistic and take advantage of both and give an even 
deeper insight into the world of chemistry. 
The extensive work of Charles Grubmeyer, Temple University, and collaborators on the 
enzyme orotate phosphoribosyltransferase (OPRTase) has put the application of enzyme 
analytical tools to the test. What began as structure prediction [1], evolved into multiple 
X-ray crystal structures with different ligands and analogues [2]–[4], kinetic experiments 
[5], NMR analysis [6], mutant experiments [4], [7] etc. This work has given deep 
understanding of the enzyme’s structure, and the specific amino acids involved in catalytic 
reaction. The exact structural dynamics during catalysis is, however, still not fully 
understood. 
This thesis serves the purpose of describing the catalytic function of OPRTase in mutant 
and wild type (wt) forms and with various ligands bound by combining molecular 
dynamics and protein crystallography. The function of the catalytic loop and other 
structural effects are analyzed on the basis of crystal structures of OPRTase from S. 
cerevisiae and computational simulations of various structures, including those solved 
from X-ray crystallography. These simulations are discussed in terms of applicability to 
describe the movements of the catalytic loop. 
Chapter I will give a biological background on the OPRTase, characterizing the family of 
enzymes it belongs to etc. A review of the literature on the subject will be given. A brief 
description of the theory behind the employed methods will also be presented. 
In Chapter II, all experimental procedures are described, as well as the results of each 
experiment. This will provide the reader with an insight into the considerations behind 
each experiment, as one result leads to the next experiment. 
Chapter III includes a discussion, connecting the results from the different fields of study 
to the knowledge presented in the literature. The discussion will also provide the reader 
with perspectives on the subject and briefly summarize the thesis in a conclusion.

2 Biological Background of OPRTase 
Phosphoribosyltransferases (PRTases) are a group of enzymes that handle the cleavage 
of pyrophosphate from PRPP and anomeric inversion of C1 in the ribofuranose ring giving 
a β-N riboside monophosphate [8], see figure 1. 
 
Figure 1 – Schematic representation of the catalytic reaction of PRTases, involving an anomeric inversion of C1 in the 
ribofuranose ring. 
PRTases are generally multimeric consisting of two to six subunits with one active site 
per subunit, and sometimes occurs as a bifunctional complex including the subsequent 
enzyme of the respective biosynthetic pathway [8]. An overview of the PRTase reactions 
can be seen in figure 3. 
These enzymes have little sequence conservation, primarily located around the PRPP-
binding motif, which consists of two aspartates surrounded by hydrophobic residues [9]. 
Along

the 
publication

phosphoribosyltransferase, which exhibited a thus far unseen fold, this group of enzymes 
was divided into two families, type I and type II [10]. OPRTase belongs to the type I 
PRTase family which is characterized by a Rossman fold α/β core, a hood-region that 
covers the base binding pocket, as well as a so called catalytic or flexible loop, which 
provides important residues for catalysis [11]. Among enzymes in the type I family, the 
catalytic loop shows the highest level of sequence conservation of any contiguous region 
in the protein [11].  
OPRTase is unique among the type I PRTases, as the loop of one subunit reaches into the 
adjacent subunit of the dimer [4]. This enzyme is responsible for one of the last two steps 
of the de novo biosynthesis of UMP, a nucleotide used as a monomer in RNA [12]. In all 
multicellular eukaryotes, OPRTase joins the subsequent enzyme OMP decarboxylase into 
a bifunctional protein, UMP synthase, while they are separately functioning enzymes in 
all microorganisms [3]. Figure 2 shows how OPRTase catalyzes the formation of OMP 
from OA and PRPP, while OMP decarboxylase catalyzes the decarboxylation reaction into 
UMP.  
Various diseases have been associated with defects in UMP synthase in humans. 
Deficiency of both OPRTase and OMP decarboxylase is associated with excessive amounts 
of orotic acid excreted in the urine, a disease called Hereditary Orotic aciduria [13]. The 
insolubility of orotic acid may lead to precipitation in the kidneys and, if untreated, renal 
failure.

3 Crystal Structure of OPRTase 
OPRTase has been extensively studied by multiple research groups, using enzyme with 
origin in numerous different species.  
The first crystal structure of OPRTase was from the species S. typhimurium [2] (PDB ID 
1STO) published by Scapin et al. 1994. The enzyme was cocrystallized with OMP, which is 
also bound in the active site. Furthermore, the structure of the enzyme was described in 
details as consisting of three main regions:  
(1) An N-terminal region  
(2) An /-core structure 
(3) A C-terminal region 
The N-terminal region involves two antiparallel -helices that are connected by three 
antiparallel -strands that is described as forming a “hood” over the core region and the 
active site.  
The core region is divided into two halves. The first half consists of two parallel -strands 
and one -helix. The second half comprises three parallel -strands and two -helices. 
Connecting the two halves is the catalytic loop, which is solvent exposed and thus partly 
omitted from the structure due to low electron density in the map.  
Finally, the C-terminal region consists of two antiparallel -helices. 
The assignment and thereby numbering of secondary structure elements was changed in 
a later publication [11], so figures 4A and 4B show two models of the monomeric 
structure of the enzyme from S. typhimurium and S. cerevisiae respectively, with updated 
secondary structure as assigned by DSSP [14] which will be used onwards, and table 1 
provides a detailed overview of the residues involved.

Scapin et al. 1995 published another structure (PDB ID 1OPR) where they soaked the 
previous OMP crystals [2] with excess OMP and PPi, thus encouraging the reverse reaction 
giving PRPP and OA bound in the active site [3]. The structure is almost identical to the 
previous E.OMP, except from the catalytic loop which has been constructed in an open 
conformation, although the backbone was not well resolved [16]. Both loops have Lys106 
interacting with PRPP within the same subunit, an interaction which will be discussed in 
section 11. The authors later suggested that the open loop conformation was a result of 
the crystals being soaked with extra product instead of doing cocrystallization [11]. 
A crystal structure from E. coli was published by Henriksen et al. 1996 (PDB ID 1ORO) 
[17]. This crystal was grown against a reservoir containing Na2SO4, which resulted in SO42- 
binding in both subunits. This structure is asymmetric, having one catalytic loop closed, 
binding to SO42-, and the other loop unable to be build due to poor electron density. The 
closed loop interacts with the sulphate ion in the opposite active site through Arg105 and 
Lys109 and with sulphate in its own subunit through Lys106. The structure contains an 
unusual cis-peptide bond which is seen between residues A74 and Y75.

4 Mutant & Kinetic Studies 
A study on the kinetic mechanism of OPRTase was published by McClard et al. 2006 [6]. 
It concludes that OMP and PRPP binds to one site per dimer, and that the enzyme follows 
a Theorell-Chance variant of the ordered sequential mechanism, but with alternating sites 
wich they dub “double Theorell-Chance”. This mechanism requires that binding of PRPP 
in one subunit occurs simultaneously with release of OMP, and similarly with OA/PPi [6]. 
The mechanism was, however, rejected by Hansen et al. 2013 [4], see below. 
Alongside the publication of the S. typhimurium E.PRPP.OA structure [3], Scapin et al. 
published two mutation studies. The first study focuses on the lysines of OPRTase [23]. 
Five highly conserved lysine residues were mutated and studied, to inspect the effect on 
catalytic activity and binding of substrates. It found that kcat is affected least in the K22Q 
mutation, while being affected the most by K109A, K76A, and K29A mutations, indicating 
the importance of these residues in the catalytic reaction. 
The second study investigates the possibility of heterodimer formation for pairs of mutant 
enzymes [16]. K109A and D132N mutants were prepared and mixed in buffer solution. 
Neither of these mutants had activity on their own but about 2 h after mixture, 22 % of 
wild type activity was achieved. This led to the conclusion that subunit exchange had 
occurred, by which a heterodimer was formed. The heterodimer formation was confirmed 
by anion-exchange chromatography. Several other heterodimers containing pairs of 
mutations had enzyme activity measured, see table 2. The experiments show that while 
K109 and D132 have large impact on catalytic activity, K22, K76, and K106 have lesser 
impact [16].

Wang et al. 1999 published a proteolyse study, investigating the movements of the 
catalytic loop as a function of ligand binding [24]. A K26A mutant was treated with trypsin 
with and without addition of 2 mM PRPP or 1 mM OMP. Cleavage of this mutant was 
determined by mass spectrometry to be at Lys109, which is a residue on the catalytic loop. 
For cleavage to be possible, the loop needs to be accessible for trypsin. The study showed 
that presence of PRPP provided at least 30-fold protection against trypsin digestion, while 
OMP gave 6.7-fold protection. This suggests that the catalytic loop moves into a less 
exposed position when PRPP or OMP binds in the active site, with PRPP having the largest 
impact. The study also provides Chemical Exchange NMR Spectroscopy results, suggesting 
that the catalytic loop fluctuates with about 60° on the nanosecond scale, given that the 
loop movements are rigid [24]. 
Mutagenesis has also been performed on S. cerevisiae OPRTase [18]. In this study, three 
mutant enzymes D131A, D132A, and D131AD132A had kinetic constants measured. Km 
values 2-3 fold larger for D132A and D131A132A with respect to PRPP and a 100-1000 
times reduction in specific activity for the same mutants lead to the conclusion that the 
two aspartate residues have little influence on PRPP binding, but are of great importance 
in catalytic activity, perhaps stabilizing the transition state. 
Hansen et al. 2013 published a thorough study of the interactions in the catalytic site [4]. 
Kinetic studies on K106S showed that the residue has little influence on catalytic activity 
(2-fold decrease in kcat), consistent with previous studies [16]. Measurements on 
D131N+D132N (ribDD) and R105G+K109S+H111G (dead-loop, DL) mutant enzymes 
showed no catalytic activity (>100,000 times less than wt), in slight disagreement with 
previous studies that indicated an activity on a D131A+D132A mutant enzyme only 1000-
fold less than wt [18]. Previous results indicating half-of-sites binding of OMP and PRPP 
[6] was rejected, as the data was reanalyzed with a different, verified extinction 
coefficient, resulting in a 50 % increase in binding stoichiometry [4]. Mixtures of enzymes 
with mutations in PRPP binding motif and enzymes with mutations in the catalytic loop 
was measured, and the formation of heterodimers with one active site was confirmed as 
enzymatic activity rose to be approximately equal to that of one wt active site. The 
conclusion was that the two active site are completely independent with respect to 
catalytic activity [4]. 
Following this study, data from a ligand binding study was also published by Hansen et al. 
[25]. An unproductive OA analogue, citrazinic acid (CA), was introduced, and equilibrium 
binding of PRPP was measured. The Kd for PRPP binding in S. cerevisiae wt enzyme is 
about 6.3 µM, while two different Kd values, 19-fold lower and 3-fold higher, for PRPP was 
observed in the presence of CA [25]. This suggests that a highly asymmetric complex is 
formed upon CA binding. Unpublished data by Eric Barr, provided to me by Charles 
Grubmeyer, also indicates two different Kd values, 1.5 and 45 µM for CA in presence of 
excess amounts of PRPP.

5 Molecular Modelling 
Molecular modelling describes the use of computers to model molecular systems [26]. 
Two properties are often considered; structure and energy [27].  
The structural information can be broken down to atoms and their relative positions. The 
goal of molecular modelling is often to find the structure having the lowest possible 
energy, i.e. the global minimum. It is therefore also important to consider the potential 
energy surface (PES). The PES is a hypersurface defined by the potential energy of all 
possible structures that can be modelled from a system of atoms, with the 3N – 6 
coordinate dimensions, where N is the number of atoms ≥ 3. Because of this high 
dimensionality, it is difficult to visualize the PES. One can, however, fix all coordinates 
except two, and then plot the potential energy of the structure as a function of these 
coordinates [27]. 
There are different ways of calculating the energy of a system, depending on the nature 
and size of the system. The most accurate method is to solve the Schrödinger equation 
using the Hamiltonian operator, which includes contributions from the kinetic energy of 
electrons and nuclei, the attraction of electrons to nuclei and lastly the repulsion between 
electrons and between nuclei. More terms can be added in case of more complex systems. 
Calculating the energy using the Hamiltonian operator of the time-dependent Schrödinger 
equation is called quantum mechanics, QM [27].

The PES can then be calculated from the electronic energy of the system, which is a 
consequence of the Born Oppenheimer approximation. It states that nuclei are much 
larger and heavier than electrons, thus they move at much slower speeds. As a 
consequence, the electronic wave function only depends on the position of the nuclei, and 
not their momentum [28]. The term involving the kinetic energy of the nucleus can thus

5.1 Molecular Mechanics 
Molecular mechanics describes systems in terms of bonded atoms, which are in locked 
hybridization states. This simplification means that bonds cannot be formed or broken as 
a result of an MM calculation. For an MM calculation to succeed, one assumes high 
transferability of geometrical parameters between molecules. These parameters are 
collected in so-called force fields [26]. An atom is defined in a force field with an atom 
type. This atom type depends i.a. on the hybridization of the specific atom and its 
environments. The atom type is locked throughout the calculation [26].

5.1.1 Force Fields 
Molecular mechanics is the use of force fields. A force field is a collection of functions and 
parameters used to calculate the potential energy of a system using equation (3) [29]. 
Some force fields may have additional terms added to the equation. Parameters are fitted 
to empirically determined values included in the force field for a specific range of 
elements. Thus, different parameterizations can be utilized depending on the system 
which should be analyzed [26]. 
Force fields used to be determined experimentally, and are thus based on how molecules 
and atoms act inter- and intramolecular wise. However, recently it has become more 
convenient to use QM methods due to the development of faster computers [26].  
Force fields generally differ in three aspects; the functional form of each energy term in 
equation (3), the number of cross terms included, and information to which the 
parameters are fitted [28]. Force fields used for large molecular systems, such as proteins, 
will often have as simple functions as possible, for example by using the Lennard-Jones 
potential for Van der Waal interactions or by locking all bond lengths and angles, thus 
omitting the first two terms of stretch and bond energies, as it is seen in the ECEPP force 
field [28]. The number of atom types defined in the force field can also be different. While 
MM2 [30] and MM3 [31] force fields includes 71 and 153 different atom types 
respectively, AMBER [32] and the consequent OPLS [33] only has 41. This difference 
contributes to the fact that some force fields provide higher details for smaller systems, 
while others provide less for larger systems [28].  
Most force fields use a second order polynomial for bond stretch and angle bending 
energies and a Fourier series in the torsional angle for torsional energy. The second order 
polynomials include two parameters; a force constant and an equilibrium value. The 
Fourier series include three parameters, namely the Fourier coefficients. For the 
==PAGE:28==
18 
 
nonbonded interactions, OPLS use a combination of Coulomb’s Law and a Lennard-Jones 
potential.  
5.2 Molecular Dynamics 
It is at all times possible to calculate the state of a classical system by introducing the 
phase space, in which all atoms are defined by their three dimensional coordinates and 
momenta in each direction, which gives us 6 N dimensions [27]. The curve formed from 
these points in the phase space along a time axis is the trajectory. As the phase points 
contains information about all particles’ positions and momenta, it also determines the 
positions of the following as well as previous phase point, assuming no outside forces act 
on the system. This means that given an initial phase point, the whole trajectory is 
determined, i.e. the process is deterministic [34]. The movement of atoms is determined 
by the initial position and velocities of atoms, the thermodynamic environment, and the 
force field. To do a repetition of a simulation, the seed, i.e. the initial point in the phase 
space, is changed to another random number [35]. 
Multiple computer programs exists for carrying out molecular dynamics simulations. 
Desmond [36] is a suite of computer programs designed for this purpose. Desmond is 
given a model system, a total simulation time, a time step, and an ensemble class, and then 
begins a cycle of calculations and movement for each time step until the total simulation 
time is reached [35]. 
With MD, one is able to simulate molecular movements in proteins on the scale of ns/day. 
5.3 Trajectory Analysis 
When the trajectory has been calculated, one can analyze it in several ways. In this thesis 
VMD [37] will be utilized for intramolecular distance measurements, calculating root-
mean-square fluctuations (RMSF) and qualitative inspection.

6 Protein Crystallography 
Protein crystallography is the art of growing proteins into nearly perfect crystals with the 
purpose of analyzing the crystal structure via X-ray diffraction. The whole process can be 
divided into five parts; purification, crystallization, X-ray diffraction experiments, solving 
the structure, structure refinement, and validation [38]. In this thesis, only the last five 
parts will be covered, as purified protein was readily available at all times. 
6.1 Protein Crystallization 
As there is no comprehensive theory to crystallization, it is mostly an empirical method 
[39]. One way of achieving crystallization is vapor diffusion. The principle is to make a 
supersaturated solution of protein by mixing the protein solution with a solution of 
precipitation agents that encourage crystallization, and equilibrating against a pure 
precipitant solution. As the water diffuses out of the mixture, the concentrations of both 
precipitants and protein will increase and nucleation can occur, inducing the 
concentration of protein to decrease until the solubility curve is reached [38]. The process 
is sketched in figure 10. 
 
Figure 10 – Diagram showing different phases of a protein solution. The red line indicates the path of a mixture between 
protein and precipitant equilibrating against pure precipitant in a vapor diffusion experiment [38]. 
The chemical and physical environment can influence the ability of the crystals to form, 
including concentration of precipitants, ligands, and enzyme, as well as temperature and 
pH. The strategy of obtaining crystals is therefore often first to screen multiple conditions 
at once, for example by using a commercial crystallization screen. In this way, the 
conditions which may yield crystals can be optimized by in new experiments [38].  
When proteins crystallize, they pack in lattices. The smallest unit of the crystal lattice is 
called the asymmetric unit (ASU). A space group is the collection of symmetry operations 
which are applied to the ASU required to obtain the unit cell. Thus, the space group is an 
indicator of the level of symmetry in the crystal [40]. As mirror and inversion symmetry 
==PAGE:30==
20 
 
are forbidden in protein crystals because all amino acids are D-enantiomers, only 65 space 
groups exist of which some are much more common than others [41]. 
6.2 Crystal Structure Refinement 
When one has obtained merged diffraction patterns from an X-ray experiment, the 
problem of missing phases of each diffraction spot is faced. There are multiple ways of 
solving this issue known as the ‘phase problem’, but as the number of structures deposited 
to the PDB grow, the method of molecular replacement increases in popularity. The idea 
is to use an available structure that is expected to resemble the current, and see if it can 
fit into the ASU. Molecular replacement consists of two major steps, orientation and 
translation search, carried out by the Rotation and Translation function, respectively [42].  
When a good fit has been found, multiple two-step refinement cycles can begin. First, 
software is used to adjust the positions of the atoms with some physical restraints such 
as bond lengths and angles. Second, the structure is examined manually, adjusting major 
mismatches between observed and calculated data by adding ligands and waters or 
changing the orientation of backbone or side chains. These cycles continue until an 
acceptable fit between the calculated and observed data is achieved, which can depend on 
the quality of the data collection, i.e. the resolution [40]. 
6.3 B-factors 
To account for the disorder in crystal structures caused by thermal vibrations and crystal 
disorders, the B-factor is introduced. The B-factor smears the electron density by a 
Gaussian shape, which represents the thermal or crystal disorder. It results in a decrease 
in intensity of diffraction [40].  
A typical overall B value for a good crystal structure is 15-20 Å2. This value usually 
increases for more flexible structures [40]. 
6.4 Validation 
Although good X-ray diffraction data often will provide unambiguous results as to how a 
crystal structure should be modelled, some subjective decisions are unavoidable in the 
process of refinement, which may lead to errors if the decision has no basis in experience 
[43].  
Two classes of validation can be distinguished: the first class only considers coordinates 
and B-factors in the model while the other class takes diffraction data into consideration. 
The Ramachandran plot [44] is an example of a validation tool of the first class, and the 
Rfree value [45] is an example of the second.  
The Ramachandran plot, is a diagram showing the torsion angles (φ and ψ) of an amino 
acid. The diagram is divided into areas defining how favorable a particular combination 
of φ and ψ angles are. Some combinations will induce steric hindrance of the side chains, 
which is disallowed [44]. 
The Rfree value was introduced as the problem of bias was seen with the R value [45], 
which is defined as

successful experiments could rule out the first possibility, and setting up a crystallization

10 Distortion of the Catalytic Loop  
There is a clear tendency among published structures that E.PRPP.OA complexes have a 
fixed closed loop (with the exception of the E.PRPP.OA structure from S. typhimurium 
(PDB 1OPR) which was soaked in excess product), and E.PRPP and E.OMP complexes have 
a distorted catalytic loop, the exception being the in-house solved structure of 
H111−5fW.OMP.Pi.  
Figure 33 shows a plot of the calculated B-factors for the 2PS1_300K and 2PS1_310K 
simulations as well as the extracted B-factors from the PDB-file of 2PS1. What strikes one 
first is that the B-factors of the crystal structure are generally higher than those found 
from the simulation, but is much lower in certain regions of the chain. Especially the loop 
connecting β-7 and α-5 around residues 167-176 peak in the simulations while having 
modest B-factors in the crystal structure. The lack of spikes can be ascribed to the crystal 
packing, from which some regions may be stabilized, while the averagely higher B-factors 
may be a consequence of noise in the X-ray data. It is also interesting to note the relatively 
high B-factors of the catalytic loop, which was in a closed conformation. 
 
Figure 33 – B-factor values calculated from RMSF values of 2PS1_300K (grey) and 2PS1_310K (blue) simulations. Only 
backbone atoms were included, and only chain A. B-factor values extracted from the PDB-file of 2PS1 (yellow). 
The same procedure was carried out for the published apo structure from S. cerevisiae, 
the E.apo structure solved in this thesis, and the 2PS1_apo_300K simulation. Figure 34 
shows a plot of B-factors calculated from the RMSF of backbone atoms in the 
2PS1_apo_300K simulation, along with B-factors extracted from the PDB files of the apo 
structure from S. cerevisiae and the E.apo structure solved in this thesis. There is a clear 
tendency throughout the B-factors of all three systems as residues with high B-factors in 
the simulation also show high B-factors in the crystal structures.  
==PAGE:62==
 
52 
 
 
Figure 34 - B-factor values calculated from RMSF values of backbone atoms in the 2PS1_apo_300K simulation (grey), 
with B-factor values extracted from the PDB-files of 2PRY (dark blue) and the E.apo crystal structure solved in this thesis 
(light blue). 
A few areas are worth digging into. First, the hood region around residues 24-33 all show 
B-factors above 100 Å2 in the simulation, while the same residues are well defined in the 
electron density map, all with B-factors below 80 Å2. A different situation is seen at the 
catalytic loop, where the residues are unsolved in both crystal structures, as expected 
from the high B-factors of the simulation. In the areas where the two crystal structures 
differ as discussed in section 9.1.1 around residues 137 and 170, the simulation also 
shows peaks, with the higher around residue 170. In these areas, the simulation is more 
consistent with the crystal structure solved in this thesis, than with the published, but the 
tables are turned in the C-terminal region, where the simulation is more consistent with 
the published crystal structure.  
In the crystal structure of DL.OMP.Pi, one loop was resolved, indicating the flexibility of 
the loop to be less than that of the wt enzyme. As simulations were performed on the same 
system, it was possible to compare the B-factors of the mutated catalytic loop to those of 
the wt. Figure 35 shows the B-factors calculated from the RMSF averaged over the 
backbone atoms of all residues in the 2PS1_DL_310K simulation as well as in the 
2PS1_apo_300K simulation for comparison. For the crystal structure, the B-factors for 
backbone atoms were averaged for each residue. 
==PAGE:63==

Figure 35 – B-factors of residues, averaged over all backbone atoms, of chain A and B in 2PS1_DL_310K (light blue) and 
2PS1_apo_300K (grey) and from the DL.OMP.Pi crystal structure (blue). 
We see that the B-factors of the 2PS1_DL_310K simulation never gets above 10 Å2, while 
the B-factors of the crystal structure are much higher. The low values of the 
2PS1_DL_310K simulation must be ascribed to an error in the calculation, as visual 
inspection of the structure clearly gives a different picture. 
These results show that combining crystal structures solved from X-ray crystallography 
experiments with computational simulations may provide broader knowledge, especially 
when considering the flexibility of the molecule.

Another important feature to notice is the importance of the oxygen of Ala74, which is 
involved in a cis-peptide bond seen in many crystal structures from S. cerevisiae.  
As the residues that participates in ligand binding varies, so does the conformation of the 
catalytic loop. As mentioned in section 3, the E. coli E.SO4 structure is asymmetric, with 
one loop being closed with interactions to sulphate through Arg105, Lys106 and, Lys109 
[17]. The open loop has the same Arg105/Lys106 interaction but lacks residues 108-114, 
which leads to the impression that the Lys109 interaction should be enough to keep the 
loop in a closed conformation as there are no interactions with the hood region. This is in 
agreement with the mutations studies reviewed in section 4, and the fact that no crystal 
structure of the wild type enzyme has been published with a closed loop and missing 
interaction through Lys109. The importance of Arg105 is unclear, as the R105A.OMP 
crystal structure was a result of mutant enzyme cocrystallized with reactants OA and 
PRPP, still OMP was seen in the active site. Arg105 shows interactions with the phosphate 
group sitting in the binding pocket around Tyr75 and Lys76 in all structures where this 
interaction is possible (i.e. Arg105 is in the model, and either phosphate or PRPP is

bound). While the positioning of PRPP required for catalysis is handled by multiple other 
residues, it would be expected that the closure of the loop would be hampered. 
Only one molecular dynamics simulation succeeded in visualizing the closure of the 
catalytic loop. This simulation was analyzed for Van der Waals and electrostatic 
interactions, as well as salt bridges and hydrogen bond between the loop and the rest of 
the protein, without any apparent tendency. However, as was seen in figure 12, the 
Lys109 distance to O3A was stable around 5 Å, until the loop moved within range of the 
hood, but then went back down around 5 Å. 
It is also possible that closure of the loop is slower than the time scale of the simulations 
carried out in this thesis, although Wang et al. suggests fluctuations on scale of nano 
seconds [24], as pointed out in section 4.

12 Conclusion and Future Prospects 
The goal of this thesis was to investigate the mechanism of the catalytic loop of OPRTase, 
using crystallography and motional dynamics simulations. A profound review of the 
literature on OPRTase was presented. A total of 16 different systems were simulated, and 
in all trajectories the catalytic loop was analyzed for interactions, fluctuations and energy. 
Closure of the catalytic loop was observed in one simulation within the time scale. As the 
OPLS 2005 force field and TIP3P solvent model was used for all simulations, the 
possibility of other force fields and solvent models giving different results is present. A 
comparison study of different force fields could eliminate or highlight the importance of 
the choice of force fields in molecular dynamics simulations of OPRTase.  
Simulations up to a microsecond could be attempted to investigate if time is the constrain 
in loop movements. 
Three crystallization experiments were conducted and five crystal structures were 
solved. A wt apo structure of higher resolution than what was previously published is 
presented, and three mutant enzymes with various ligands has proven to be susceptible 
to crystallization, and the triple mutated DL enzyme variant had the catalytic loop 
constructed in an open conformation. 
An evaluation of residues participating in ligand binding was presented and the 
importance of various residues in binding of different ligands was discussed. 
An E.PRPP.CA complex was not achieved, and this could continue to be a target for further 
crystallization experiments, although the high concentrations of CA required for 
crystallization may complicate this. Attempting to set up crystallization experiments with 
even higher PRPP concentrations to overcome competition from CA is a possibility. An 
E.PRPP complex with a closed catalytic loop would also be interesting, and possible as 
PRPP is the basis of most interactions with the loop. 
By combining results from computational simulations and experimental X-ray 
crystallography, a clear correlation was found between B-factors calculated from RMSF of 
residues in simulations and B-factors from crystal structures. This correlation confirmed 
which areas of the enzyme are flexible, also depending on how the enzyme is ligated.
