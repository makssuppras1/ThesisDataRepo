Abstract
For years, the construction industry has been decreasing in productivity, while other en-
gineering industries have increased with the technological enhancements. As a response,
the researchers, developers and companies have resorted to identify the success factors of
the growing industries and adapt them to construction. Two of the concepts that emerged
was Integrated Project delivery (IPD) and Virtual Design & Construction (VDC). Two
concepts that interfaces and are highly connected. They are created to streamline and in-
tegrate the design. In the centre of these concepts is technology, in the form of BIM and
digital tools that can further the cause.
The digitisation and change in processes are measures to streamline the construction. The
changes have to be understood and implemented in a holistic way to work optimally and
integrated.
Cooperation with Bane NOR was established to measure the general level of development
in the Nordic industry. As a vast public client in the Norwegian transport and infrastruc-
ture industry, they had collaboration with most of the industry and their changes can affect
many. The focus of the investigation was their InterCity programme that consists of four
mega projects and the construction of 270 kilometres of double-tracked railway in and out
of Oslo, Norway.
The problem statement for the thesis is What applicable actions may Bane NOR take to
further their design process and facilitate its digitalisation goals? To answer this ques-
tion, it was chosen to have a qualitative research approach, including meetings, in-depth
interviews with a focus group, and a deep dive into literature and Bane NOR’s InterCity
programme documentation.
To determine a company’s development, one must achieve insight and overview of the
area of research. The key ﬁndings of the investigation were that VDC had gained a
foothold in the industry and Bane NOR, especially the concept of Integrated Concurrent
Engineering is becoming mainstream in the InterCity projects. The utilisation of BIM
is still in a phase of adaption and exploration, but it provides beneﬁts to collaboration
between disciplines, planning and management. Bane NOR are not heavily involved in
the technology themselves but works hard to accommodate for the stakeholders’ tests of
innovation.
Bane NOR’s design process is complicated and affected by numerous third-parties. From
the government to municipalities. They must follow a strict public plan to achieve con-
struction. It includes regulations, municipality approving, zoning and applications. Every
part of the plan has to have a certain degree of detail to be accepted.
To further the development of Bane NOR’s design process, it was concluded that In-
tegrated Project Delivery was a recommendation, though it might be too big of a step.
Therefore, the recommendations were divided into three parts, where the ﬁnal step is total
integrated project delivery. Co-location was found to be the most substantial step towards
the change.

Glossary
As-Built
A virtual equivalent 3D-model of the built/made physical product.
Also, refers to documentation of the installed products and con-
struction.
Consultants
Consultants describes externally hired specialists that design in a
design team or help facilitating the project in any way.
Design Teams
A team of engineers, architects of similar that design one or more
of the different disciplines included in the project. Could be both
internal employees or external consultants.
Digital Twin
A virtual equivalent 3D-model of the future physical product.
Level Of
Development
(LOD)
A scale that describes how precise and developed the model is.
From 100-500, where 100 is conceptual, 500 is As-Built and 500.
200, 300 and 400 is stages between.
Model Maturity
Index (MMI)
A scale that describe how mature the object is, both in terms of
geometry and information.
Management,
operations and
maintenance
(MOM)
Product documentation that include product speciﬁcations, attributes,
technical descriptions, operational information and manuals for main-
tenance and management.
Percent Plan
Complete (PPC)
A measure of completion of a plan where 100% is Task Ready Made
(TRM).
Virtual Reality
(VR)
A digital experience where you can see, hear and sometimes inter-
fere with the digital ”world” you are in. Its target is to replace as
many of your senses with digital impressions.

1. INTRODUCTION
1
INTRODUCTION
The goal of any projects is to serve a purpose, meet a demand, and proﬁt. Proﬁt is essen-
tial to maintain a part of the market. From a bigger perspective, it is also the goal of any
public construction project. Facilitating for demand or a purpose from a governmental
perspective is, or should be in a stable democracy, a step toward the greater good. The
greater good should include the inhabitants well being, rights, prosper or enhanced wel-
fare. All of these factors are, to some degree, the result of giving the government more
resources to facilitate for the country to develop or succeed ﬁnancially.
The continued improved effectiveness and efﬁciency of the construction industry is one
of the biggest focuses in the research, educational and governmental strategies in the
relevant institutions. This focus has resulted in several developments such as Building
Information Modelling (BIM) and a large number of tested processes and tools. Among
these, the concepts Integrated Project Delivery (IPD) and Virtual Design Constructions
(VDC). These are deﬁned and tested concepts. However, parts of the industry suffer
from misconceptions, a lack of understanding of the scope, and implementation. Due
to that, the responsibilities and involvements of the different stakeholders are sometimes
misunderstood or neglected.
In the Norwegian construction industry, the government accounts for 40% of the produced
products [13]. The industry has a revenue of over 500 billion yearly, which makes it a
substantial part of the Norwegian market [56].
1.1
State-of-the-art
The most recent research on the development of methods and concept to streamline the
construction industry and facilitate for digital technology belongs to Stanford University’s
Center for Integrated Facility Engineering (CIFE) through their book ”Integrating Project
Delivery” by Martin Fischer, Howard W. Ashcraft, Dean Reedm and Atul Khanzode and
their work on VDC and BIM. Several institutes around the world research the best prac-
tice of approach and adaptation to their countries regulations and systems. Substantial
parts of the research also regard the use of BIM, VDC and IPD in consideration of the
environmental issues the world is facing.

Situation & statistics
A focus on implementing BIM has strongly inﬂuenced the last 5-10 years. A misconcep-
tion about the nature of this development roam the market - This is not a case of just forc-
ing BIM into the industry, it is a case of streamlining the projects to be more productive
and efﬁcient. It provides more cost-efﬁcient solutions, better planning, safer construc-
tion and higher quality. The quicker the employees understand and grasp that fact, the
earlier we can start implementing changes. To achieve changes, one must achieve a high
motivation throughout the industry.
Today, the situation shows a growing trend in appreciation of digital tools in both plan-
ning and execution in the construction industry, as well as the operation and management
(O&M). There are still parts of the industry that faces obstacles and issues that must be
solved, but the increasing focus is present, and it is not going to decrease.
According to the National Statistical Institute of Norway, SSB, the labour productivity
in Norway has, from 2000-2016 dropped with 10%, 13% in Sweden and 3% in Finland.
Denmark has increased labour productivity with 10% in the same period [54]. In the US
there have also been a considerable drop [31].

attention. Lastly, to be a future-oriented community actor [43]. More, Bane NOR has a
superior strategy regarding technology that gives directions for choices of technology to
cope with the rise and realise the ambitions of the government. [44].

The InterCity Programme

1.4
Digitalisation
As presented, The Norwegian National Transport Plan has a strong strategy to facilitate
new technological development. A signiﬁcant part of that strategy is to cope with the
rapid developments in technology and to make the right decisions at the right time in the
larger projects [59]. This correlates with The Norwegian Directorate of Public Construc-
tion and Property (Norwegian: Statsbygg) and the industry’s biggest advocacy group, The
Federation of Norwegian Construction Industries (BNL). Both of which have published

4
THEORY
In this chapter, the most signiﬁcant theories revolving processes for BIM-implementation
and streamlining the design phase is presented. This includes the principals of Integrated
Design, Integrated Project Delivery and Virtual Design & Construction and exploration
of supporting engineering concepts. The deduction of these processes will help to un-
derstand where to focus and how the client must facilitate to achieve an integrated and
successful design process.
As easily imagined, the complexity of a design process in a construction project varies
enormously due to the span of construction types. For the sake of relevance, the presented
theories target medium to high complexity. For these projects, the number of stakeholders
can be extensive, and even the design team can consist of many different companies. That
indicates a need for high-level coordination and collaboration to make a functional and
proper foundation of design, and even more to make a digital twin and result in a As-Built
BIM which is one of the future goals of the Norwegian governmental [11].
The design phase can be described as separate segments which are linearly dependent,
from idea to production start. We know that this is not the case in practice. The design
phase is saturated with loops and revisions, changes and a need to make compromises.
Dependent on the contract form and the chosen delivery model, the design might be a
single phase or several. In the most common practice, there is a pre-design phase be-
fore tender and then the main design phase when a contractor is chosen and in charge of
the project. Another way is that the client hires the design teams and does the full design,
then the contractor who wins the tender executes the pre-designed solution. These are two
traditional delivery methods of a design-bid-build contract used in Norway. Other deliv-
ery methods include Design-Build, Construction Manager at Risk (CMAR), Integrated
Project Delivery (IPD) and Lean Project Delivery System (LPDS).

4.2
Building Information Modelling & Build-
ing Information Model
Building Information Modelling (BIM) is a conceptual term that relates to the technology
that allows us to design and visualise constructions and facilities in 3D, make social in-
terfaces, connect it to engineering information and execute simulations to make life cycle
analyses and the best possible design. The Building Information Model is the product of
that.
The concept of BIM has evolved dramatically since it ﬁrst appeared, not just the technical
uses but the term. The perception of the term depends on the country, industry, company,
and department one represents. Many have made new deﬁnitions and several claims that
the term has evolved to include processes and mindsets. In some way, they have a point,
because BIM is not just a model anymore, but for the sake of a deﬁnition and its func-
tionalities, it must be precise. Several other concepts have been presented to leave BIM
as the term it is and instead separate the technological aspect and the processes. For the
sake of the report, the discussion of where BIM has its boundaries will be left for another
day and the term presented above will be the grounds of discussion.
BIM was initially a technological development to replace the uses of paper drawings
where the information from the design teams happened through an exchange of paper
drawings. In the 80s and 90s technical drawers converted to Computer-aided Design
(CAD) and it was with tremendous success. The revelation that the production of draw-
ings and changes could be done and later changed had a considerable impact. In 1986 the
programs had been so developed that it was possible to use them in complex construction
projects. That was the year that Robert Aish introduced the world to Building Modelling.
He argued that the beneﬁts from CAD were not optimised for multi-disciplinary design
teams since it was isolated to the disciplines. An integrated system that could give the
model a holistic representation of the design with all of the disciplines would be more
beneﬁcial and “(...)encourage consistency and coordination of design information across
the whole multi-disciplinary teams” (Robert Aish, 1986 [1]). Aish was right. In the next
decades, BM evolved to include information directly linked to the objects and simulations
that can be connected to a centralised multi-disciplinary model. Including simulations of
sun path, energy consummation, cost estimations, airﬂow, trafﬁc and much more.

4.2.1
The Seven Ds
Part of the evolution of BIM is the development from 2D and 3D to 7D. The different
”dimensions” represents stages and uses of the model and the information. The reason
for including this part is to show possibilities and parts of what the digital development
offers the construction industry. These are the elements of BIM that we want to deliver
better products and better projects. It should be noted that the order of the dimensions is
not unanimously agreed upon throughout the industry.
2D BIM is self-explanatory and is the utilisation of 2D CAD drawings.
3D BIM is the use of design software to make a three-dimensional object-orientated
model composed of geometrical representations of the elements that are to be built. This
model includes the possibility to do changes in all views by changing an object even if
it is in a 2D-view. In comparison, 3D models are exceedingly more understandable than
2D drawings. In addition, product, assembly, management, operations and maintenance
(MOM or OAM) or other documentation and information can be linked to the different
objects [31].
4D BIM describes the phenomenon of a 3D BIM connected to the project schedule. 4D
includes a time-based animation that shows the whole building process step by step —
used to give stakeholders and affected parties an understanding and insight into the pro-
cess. It can also replace the Gantt charts and give the project management a visualisation
of the best possible plan for the production and design phase [15]. To do the right thing
at the right time is an essentiality to optimise project delivery. 4D BIM gives an insight
into the possible space-time interferences and predicts organisation backlog, coordination
points, time and cost risks [31]. 4D is generally established in the Norwegian industry,
though it might be utilised to a greater extent by using VDC.
5D BIM links the 4D BIM with costs of the different objects and production. Cost
estimation via a BIM-model is a quite established part of BIM utilisation, though it might
not be as established as one might wish. 5D BIM can also be used as a cost estimation
according to time/progress in the project [58][15].
6D BIM is the use of the model to simulate the energy and sustainability of the model
by implementing as many real-life parameters that you can and perform analyses. It is
also called Performance Analysis.

4.3.1
Lean Construction
Lean Construction is the basis of many of VDC theories and work methods and must,
therefore, be explored and explained to give background and basis.
As told, the industry has suffered from low productivity, which has lead to a set of new
ideas over the last decades. This involved looking towards other industries that had in-
creased productivity and efﬁciency. One of these was the Automobile industry and its
use of Lean Productions. Lean construction is the modiﬁed term and development of the
Toyota Production System (TPS) which lead to Toyota producing more efﬁciently, with
higher quality and more ﬂexibility [23].
Lean construction targets to eliminate waste and produce the customers desired product,
instantly. The idea is to manage the construction projects as mass production, as Toyota
did - based on Henry Ford’s assembly lines, by eliminating waste in the production pro-
cess and supply chain from the order to the handover, reduce the number of processes and
cycle times [33]. To execute this, the system was adapted to apply for unique projects
rather than repetitive tasks. The result was the Lean Project Delivery System (LPDS),
developed by the Lean Construction Institute (LCI). See ﬁgure 16 [30].
Figure 16 – Lean Project Delivery System. Taken From Ballard (2008) [3]
The LPDS differs from the traditional construction delivery by deﬁning the phases and
the connection between them and their participants. One of its main principles is ”early

4.3.2
Last Planner™System
Last Planner™System (LPS) is a big part of LPDS and is a framework and method for
schedule planning with a focus of improvement of the planning and enhance the precision
of the execution of construction projects. It is mostly used in the production phase, but
studies of Hamzeh et al. (2009) and Fosse et al. (2016) has shown that the process is
highly applicable in the design phase [26] [23]. LCI deﬁne it as a “system for project
production planning and control, aimed at creating a workﬂow that achieves reliable ex-
ecution, developed by Glenn Ballard and Greg Howell, with documentation by Ballard
in 2000. LPS is the collaborative, commitment-based planning system that integrates
should-can-will-did planning: pull planning, make-ready look-ahead planning with con-
straint analysis, weekly work planning based upon reliable promises, and learning based
upon analysis of PPC and Reasons for Variance.” (Lean Construction Institute [28]).
The LPS’ objective is to enhance planning reliability. It divides the planning of the tasks
into three: the process plan, the period plan and the week plan. The process plan is
the introducing planning of the whole schedule. The period plan is forward-looking and
focuses on removing obstacles of production. The weekly schedule is detailed planning.
[18]
The LPS includes some steps to provide success. The ﬁrst step is to identify the mile-
stones and the deliverables that complete it. All key stakeholders must attend to reveal
the common value expectations and requirements of the deliverables. The next step is
the ”pull scheduling” [26]. Pull is a lean construction element that describes a method of
only having the necessary information when needed and involving every responsible party
rather than a managerial group doing all the planning. Every planned step starts with the
last task required for fulﬁlment and working back from that [21]. Next up is the cluster
phase where you break the milestones and deliverables into manageable activities, assign
duration and prerequisites. This is written down and posted on the wall. After several
of these clusters, the team does a backwards scheduling where they start with the mile-

Integrated Project Delivery
Integrated Project Delivery (IPD) is a complete project concept in terms of it regarding the
owner’s values and goals, project organisational structure, transparency and information
ﬂow, work environment, collaboration processes, simulations and contracts to maximise
the project delivery [21]. Early involvement of the project members and the creation of a
unison project with common risk and opportunity possibilities is the key approach in IPD.
The Lean Construction Institute deﬁnes it as “A project delivery approach that integrates
people, systems, business structures and practices into a process that collaboratively har-
nesses the talents and insights of all participants to reduce waste and optimise efﬁciency
through all phases of the project, from early design through project handover. The three
contractual components of IPD include Organisation structure, Lean Operating Systems
and Commercial Terms” (Lean Construction Institute, undated, [28]).
IPD is a combination of principals and practices that creates synergy. It is a way to ”divide
and conquer” the design and execution of a construction project. As stated, constructions
are becoming more and more sophisticated in terms of technical solutions and regulatory
requirements, which demand a more complex project delivery and delivery methods. IPD
is a way of solving these tasks by including the collaboration techniques and methods to
the project management tools. It is a different way of handling construction project and
can seem complicated due to the extensive nature of it. It requires a change of attitude,
behaviour and skills. One of the main philosophies of IPD is to make the project stake-
holders become business partners rather than segregated parties that work for their own
beneﬁts and wealth. It is based on a philosophy of full unity, collaborations and trust. It
argues that the time of companies working in silos and sporadically exchanging informa-
tion must be over to fulﬁl this goal. In addition, it requires openness: admitting mistakes,
being critical and conﬁdence, and a culture that does not sanction mistakes. A project and
contract that does not punish mistakes, but rewards success. This means that every part
needs to be committed to each other and know that they can only succeed together or fail
separately - It is possible for all parts to win together [21].
A study sponsored by the Lean Construction Institute and conducted by Dogde Data &
Analytics, benchmarked the owner satisfaction and project performance of 162 projects.
It showed that IPD was by far the most successful projects delivery type that was tested.

The PESTEL analysis provides an overview of the macro-environmental conditions of
a company. It stands for Political, Economical, Socio-cultural, Technological, Envi-
ronmental and Legal and is often used in strategic management and marketing. It is
used to give an analysis of the macro environments that something affects. It presents
the instances that, and provides information to, enable a company to predict the situation
resulting from the change, a new product or a strategy. For a holistically minded thesis,
such as this, it suits the reﬂection on the impacts of the future recommendations [65].
Political conditions concerns what political measures that have been done and what po-
tential provisions that can affect the operations of the company in the future, such as
the national budget. Economical refers to the economic factors that can affect the mat-
ter. Socio-cultural conditions are the social aspects that inﬂuence the company’s actions.
For example, attitudes, educational levels, demography, etc. Technological refers to the
technological development in society. The Environmental aspect takes into account the
environmental impact the actions can have. Finally, the Legal refers to the existing or
future laws and regulations that can or will have an effect on the company [25].

5.1.2
VDC
For Bane NOR, VDC is a relatively new development. They have in the last years sent
employees on certiﬁcation courses at the Norwegian University of Science and Technol-
ogy where CIFE is teaching the attendees.
Bane NOR has used elements of VDC in many years, especially ICE-sessions and its
methodology. According to the interviewees, VDC is an answer to the client’s request.
The client is the ones that push for more efﬁcient solutions. Therefore, the rest of the
industry response with developing knowledge and processes to use it. When doing so,
they are making themselves more attractive to clients.
The success of utilising VDC is dependent on experience. Bane NOR states that it is not
something that one is good at, it takes time and one must repeat it over and over, with the
same team, in several projects. It also requires that the other stakeholders are experienced,
and a motivation to implement and execute it.
ICE is spreading and becoming more and more utilised in the projects of InterCity. From
the interviews and meetings, it was uncovered that three out of the ﬁve projects use it
actively. Also, one of the lacking projects is preparing to implement it. At the Ringeriks
line, which is still in the early stages, they have utilised it a lot. In 6 months they conducted
ICE-sessions for every section of the project once a week. There are ﬁve sections. Thus,
an ICE-session every day in half a year. They stated that the client team all had between 20
and 25 sessions each during this period. It was unaccustomed for many of the participants.
They described it as a method that ”goes a bit against the nature of the industry”. With
that, they meant that designers and decision-makers often have time to assess the different
alternatives with calculations and support accurately. In ICE-sessions, one must make
spontaneous decisions.
The described ICE as a time-consuming, but paradoxically, it saved much time in the
design phase. Bane NOR assessed that the result of the design was approximately as
good as it would have been in the traditional, more time-consuming, process. They beneﬁt
much from the preparation of the participants.
The issues, regarding ICE, was mostly due to individual experience. Some of the partici-

going from drawing on paper to real-time design in software and a new methodology.
Another point, they experienced that the consultancy often prepared themselves so much

Bane NOR presented a new view on ICE, compared with the literature - the difference

DISCUSSION
To fulﬁl their own, the governmental and industrial vision and strategy, Bane NOR have
to development, both in the technological aspect and streamlining. As they have stated
themselves, the methodology is a substantial part in terms of facilitation and renewal of
processes. The design phase is a substantial element of that. In this chapter, the research
questions and ﬁndings will be discussed in accordance with the relevant theory. A collec-
tion of recommendations will be presented to serve as support to their design process.
6.1
Research question 1
Why has the focus on streamlining-concepts had a considerable upturn in the
construction industry?
While the most innovative and productive industries have taken advantage of the digital
rise since it began, the construction industry did not. It took decades for the industry
to adapt. That resulted in an explosion of software and technology that could be utilised.
They did not adapt gradually, in the same degree as the other industries. Some argue that it
was because of the a lack of research & development and focus [63], others the increasing
complexity of the design and conservatism [31]. While some, will argue that there is
an unwillingness to risk proﬁt. It could also be a combination: The rising complexity,
conservatism withholding development and the pressure of proﬁt and schedule.
When the change ﬁnally arrived, the industry tried to “catch up”. As of result, the tech-
nology was implemented to a degree, yet the productivity did not reﬂect the beneﬁts. This
indicates a failure to meet the same standards in the conducted work, not the technology
itself. The focus of the industry turned to methodology and project delivery alternatives,
which has hit the “take-off” on the innovation S-curve and becoming mainstream.
One of the interviewees stated that the emerge of VDC is the industry’s answer to the
”client’s request”. The request of more productive projects and the use of digital tools.

6.2
Research question 2
Is Bane NOR’s InterCity programme beneﬁting from VDC?
To better facilitate technology and developments such as BIM, it is of importance to
change the way we do construction projects today. This could be done with new de-
livery concepts as a whole, implementation of different technology and methodology.
Bane NOR has to a degree seen the beneﬁts of VDC and tested it in several projects, in
different scales. At one hand, they have made an incentive to educate employees in the
VDC course and certiﬁcation. This implies that Bane NOR has a plan to utilise it as part
of the planning and design phases.
One must take into account the regulations, third parties, interfaces and political views
that they face in conducting planning, design and execution of projects, but small changes
they have tried in individual projects shows that changes can be beneﬁcial. ICE is almost
an implemented element in the InterCity programme. Still, the documentation does not
reﬂect that. It is mentioned a few times in Bane NOR’s design documentation, but no
instructions and no measured effect appear. It suggests a problem with the dedication to
the idea or plain ignorance to the aspect of standardisation.
By innovation theory (chapter 4.1), one would state that ICE is now, taking off, in the early
majority or just in the majority. ICE is still met with some degree of resistance, though
it is now being implemented at a much faster rate than before. The ”early adopters” have
observed the effect ICE produces, which is now so recognised in the industry that they are
starting to explore the Last Planner System. They understand that VDC has something
to offer. It takes time, but it is promising.
As discovered in the interviews, ICE provides as good, or better, as the results of a tradi-
tional design process, just in less time. They primarily used it when they had a shortage of
time and had to speed up the planning phase. Some also assess that the result was equal
to, or better, than the traditional methods. Individuals in Bane NOR have come so far,
utilising ICE, that they have developed two different versions, one for decisions and one
for design & planning.
POP is a vital part of VDC with its systematical approach to achieve control of the
projects. It is not explicitly stated in the documentation, but Bane NOR uses the general
idea. They divide their projects into manageable parts to make it possible to outsource the

contracts and design. One can also see that they have systematical approaches to manage

Research question 3
How far has Bane NOR gotten, not only utilising technological developments but

Bane NOR experienced that the BIM-term has developed independently by numerous in

The meetings uncovered that Bane Nor have tested a collaborative BIM-platform, but it
does not appear to manifest itself. Bane NOR are, on the other hand, highly invested in
promoting for the stakeholders to test innovations, winning two international BIM prices
in the last two years. The stakeholders have tested everything from VR, game technology
and drones.
The strategy and investments suggest a high level of facilitation of technology, while that
does not seem to include their own departments. On one hand, the commitment to the
strategy extends to their contracted stakeholders. As a client, heavily involved in the
projects as project managers, they might beneﬁt from utilising more technology to gain
insight and better performance into their internal processes.
One aspect that also needs to be included is the methodical facilitation of technology.
Several of the mentioned methods, processes and concepts accommodates for the util-
isation of technology, such as VDC’s inclusion of BIM, Big Room, and POP. In that
regard, Bane NOR also has increasing accommodation, as discussed above and presented
in chapter 5.3.

6.4.1
Co-location
From the interview, regarding Project X, locating at the consultancy’s ofﬁce shows that
co-location makes a huge difference in lowering the demand for people from Bane NOR’s
part. They did not need a lead designer for every discipline to do the coordination. It made
it possible to free up human resources and do several big projects simultaneously. This
might beneﬁt Bane NOR, particularly in the smaller projects. Every interviewee found
co-location beneﬁcial in terms of time, quality and demand of the clients time.
When the projects are pressured on schedule, the design will beneﬁt from being conducted
concurrently. In these cases, it could be argued that co-location with every key stakeholder
is very beneﬁcial. If a project possesses a culture where there is a low threshold for asking
quick questions across disciplines, it will provide an edge to control the interfaces between
the disciplines and areas while maintaining the schedule and quality. Co-location can lead
to an integrated team and
Co-location appears to be a popular idea in Bane NOR, and some of the projects have it
to smaller degrees, but it also depends on the size of the project. A smaller project, like
a depositing project, is dependent on people that only work with the project in a smaller
degree of their work portfolio. This is a challenge when a project uses both internal
and external design teams. There is no realistic way of demanding full-time dedication
to smaller projects. To adapt to that, one must ﬁnd other solutions. Project X had the
possibility to locate at the ofﬁce of the consultancy one or two days a week, which allowed
them to be a part of their design discussions and meetings and provided a better insight
to the project, as well as it being proactive. On the other days, Bane NOR was located in
their own ofﬁce to consult with the internal design teams. Moving back and forth might
not be the best solution altogether, but since it is too expensive for smaller projects to hire
consultants full-time and they have other projects as well it made for a great compromise.
It was expressed as a success in the interview, and the project who had not exploited,
Project Q, regretted it since they found it beneﬁcial in hindsight.
The main arguments of not doing co-location are, as presented, the expenses of facilitation
and consultancy fees, which in a co-located project would be high. On the other side, the
beneﬁts are so many that CIFE asks the question ”how can you afford not to?” [21]. The
added creativity, coordination and accuracy, lessened rework and especially the loss of
information surely will weigh up. To minimise the cost and still reap the beneﬁts of co-

6.5.1
Standardisation & Team Selection
One aspect the literature, e.g. Fischer & Kunz (2012), emphasises is the standardisation
of methods and implementations before using them to create a precise formulation of the
objectives, vocabulary, responsibilities and structure [31]. The interviews reﬂect that -
take time to gather experiences and learn ”best practice” before leaping into something
new. Standardise it and make routines. In standardising, one must ﬁrst agree on terminol-
ogy and formulations to create a common understanding. The same applies to the goals of
the concept or technology. After achieving common understanding, it can be customised
to provide Bane NOR with the best possible approach. This applies to VDC as well,
where Bane NOR has a golden opportunity. They already have VDC trained and certiﬁed
employees. Gather them and others with experience in a workshop where they can reﬂect
around the best practice for the company. No other could give better advises than the
VDC certiﬁed people that know the company from the inside.
Bane NOR are sending employees to VDC certiﬁcation courses while having require-
ments to do the design and planning and scheduling in a speciﬁc way. A paradox. One
could state that they are hindering themselves in utilising the innovative implementations
they are ordering. It implies that they want a more efﬁcient solution, but it does not extend
to the change of fundamental requirements. It is, of course, possible to combine some of
the solutions, but it might be more efﬁcient to have higher ﬂexibility in their routines.
Bane NOR’s problem with not standardising before implementing is a direct contradiction
to their superior technological strategy. As stated in one of the meetings, they tend to be a
bit hasty in the testing of new development, either it is technology or methods rather than
researching it and standardising the ”best practice”. This is supported by interviews and
other meetings as well. Even though it might function well, it does not correlate with the
overlying strategy. This can mean that there is a shortcoming between the strategic level
and the rest of the company. On the other hand, it might be the distance between projects
and the central power, and that the culture in the project does not reﬂect the central culture.

Long-Term
With the streamlining and facilitation that an Integrated Project Delivery provides, the
thesis recommends Bane NOR to explore the possibility of adapting it and utilise it in the

product, productivity, and it is a systematical approach to construction.

The last suggestion is to create a department of model maintenance. Documentation
is of utmost importance, especially for a governmental client. Today, the documentation
is preserved by controllers. The same is recommended for the models and the connection
information. Since the technology advances at high speed, one must be able to go back
and read, view and use the older versions. A new department of ”model controllers” is
necessary to maintain the As-built documentation of models for the future. This could
include the creation of a public BIM server that is maintained and updated on demand.

CONCLUSION
1. Why has the focus on streamlining-concepts had a considerable upturn in the construc-
tion industry? The focus on streamlining-concepts had an upturn when the productivity
of the industry started to decrease, and the adoption of digital tools did not give the re-
sults it had in other industries. It indicated a more signiﬁcant problem than not utilising
technology. It indicated that the industry is deﬁcient in the way we work, and especially
the way we collaborate. The two challenges the concepts address: adapting to the digital
age and changing the collaborative methods of design and planning.
2. Is Bane NOR’s InterCity programme beneﬁting from VDC? Bane NOR beneﬁts from
the elements of VDC they have implemented. They are not necessarily exploiting its
full potential of the concept, but it provides them with several of the beneﬁts - more
rapid design and planning, enhanced facilitation of technology, better communication
and decreasing latency. ICE is the primary source to the beﬁts along with the utilisation
of POP. However, Bane NOR is lacking the standardisation of the methods.
3. How far has Bane NOR gotten, not only utilising technological developments but
accommodating it? Bane NOR facilitates technology well in regard to both investment
and strategy. They promote for the stakeholders to test technological innovations in a
high degree. Internally, Bane NOR have adequate Big Rooms for their ICE-sessions with
visualisation support, but falls short on their development within BIM.
4. Could IPD be beneﬁcial as a new approach for Bane NOR and the rest of the design
teams? IPD and VDC are two concepts that interfaces and are highly connected. They
are created to streamline and integrate every discipline. In the centre of these concepts are
technology and collaboration. IPD addresses latency issues, communication, structure,
integrated design and information. All of which are relevant for Bane NOR and the design
teams and would, beneﬁt Bane NOR immensely.
What applicable actions may Bane NOR take to further their design process and fa-
cilitate its digitalisation goals? It is concluded that Integrated Project Delivery is the
primary action this thesis recommends for furthering the development of Bane NOR’s
design process and facilitation of their digitalisation goals. It might be too big of a step
in the ﬁrst phase. Therefore, the recommendation is divided into three terms with a per-

Co-location inspires collaboration, reduction of latency and integrated design to achieve
