Abstract
Capacitive micromachined ultrasonic transducers (CMUTs) have demonstrated
great promise for replacing the currently used piezoelectric transducers in medi-
cal ultrasound imaging. CMUTs are commonly fabricated using fusion bonding
or surface machining, but has been shown to also be producible with anodic
bonding.
Anodic bonding holds several advantages over other bonding tech-
niques such as: low bonding temperature allowing for combination with inte-
grated circuits, high tolerance for contamination and surface roughness and less
time consuming processing for fabricating CMUTs.
In this thesis CMUTs are fabricated with the use of anodic bonding. The
fabrication process is improved through two optimization steps, one for produc-
ing cavities in a borosilicate glass with the designed dimensions, and one for
producing a high quality anodic bond.
It is shown that cavities with the designed dimensions can, with the use of a
resist mask, be wet etched in a borosilicate glass. The optimized etching pro-
cess incorporates dehydration of the borosilicate glass and splits the etching into
three time slots with dehydration of the resist in between.
Anodic bonding voltage steps are optimized for diﬀerent combinations of ma-
terials and layout. An anodic bond for the CMUT fabrication is demonstrated
with the bond covering the entire surface and having bonding strength strong
enough for further processing.
Eight linear CMUT arrays are fabricated with an element yield of 15%.
Impedance measurements in air are conducted and the resonance frequency is
found to be 7 MHz with an impedance phase angle of 70◦at a bias voltage set
to 94% of the pull-in voltage. Asymmetrical polarity response is observed, by
charging being present in one bias polarity. The other bias polarity is stable
over a duration of one hour.
With the optimized fabrication process, a batch of anodic bonded CMUTs
has potential of being fabricated within ﬁve workdays, which is much faster
compared to fabricating fusion bonded CMUTs.
This thesis shows the great potential of anodic bonded CMUTs becoming the
preferred fabricated CMUTs of the future.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
The global health care sector is a growing market and the demand for smart and accessible
health care products is rising [1]. Innovations in the health care market are needed to keep
the costs down and improve medical diagnosing.
One of the innovations most likely to
transform the health care is point-of-care diagnostics. Point-of-care diagnostics allow the
patient to be diagnosed at a convenient location and time, giving faster and less expensive
patient care [2].
Ultrasound imaging is a point-of-care diagnostic method and has been used as a fast, mobile
and harmless way to scan for medical purposes since the middle of the 20th century [3]. It can
be used to obtain information of geometry and ﬂow inside parts that are non-transparent.
It is often associated with visualizing internal body tissue like scanning of a fetus inside a
pregnant woman, as seen in Figure 1.1.
This thesis focuses on producing capacitive micromachined ultrasonic transducers (CMUTs)
which is a relatively new way of producing ultrasound transducers. Even though the idea
CMUTs have existed for over 20 years, they have not entered the market yet [5]. One of the
main reasons is diﬃculties in fabricating them. Only in recent years has it become possible
to fabricate on the length scale and with the parameters required to become competitive to
piezoelectric transducers.
CMUTs have the possibility of taking over the ultrasound transducer market as they have
several advantages over the currently used piezoelectric transducers. CMUTs are more ﬂex-
ible in design and fabrication methods oﬀering more possibilities in design optimization for
diﬀerent frequencies, bandwidths, pressure, imaging methods like 3D imaging and integrated
circuits. CMUTs can also be designed to have larger bandwidths, leading to narrower pulses
in the time domain and thus, potential for higher resolution when used for imaging. With
the potential of these improved properties, the CMUTs can improve high-resolution imaging

and ﬂow detection, which is beneﬁcial in many research areas and medical diagnostics and
treatments.
Because the ultrasound scanner is transportable and an inexpensive investment, compared
to other medical imaging methods like magnetic resonance scanning (MR) and computer
tomography scanning (CT), the access to medical ultrasound imaging can be increased
worldwide.
In the future, CMUTs can be used for more than just ultrasound imaging. Optimized for
high-intensity focused ultrasound, it is be possible to use CMUTs for destroying unwanted
tissue deep inside the body [6]. Manipulating particles by the use of standing waves in a
ﬂuid channel has also been proposed as a future tool in medical diagnostics and research
[7]. The ﬂexible process of fabricating a CMUT has also been shown to produce bendable
CMUTs which can be implemented in a pill and in the future scan internal body parts [8].
1.1
Ultrasound imaging
Sound is propagating waves of pressure, transferring motion from molecule to molecule in
a medium. The sound wave’s velocity, attenuation and direction depends on the medium
properties. The human is able to hear sound waves in the frequency range from 20 Hz to
20 kHz. Sound waves with frequencies above this are called ultrasound. For ultrasound
medical imaging the typical range of frequencies are from 2 MHz to 50 MHz.
For medical imaging, a transducer which is optimized to transform electrical energy into
acoustic energy in the form of ultrasound waves is used. The transducer emits pulses of
ultrasound waves. If a spherical sound wave is emitted in a continuous medium it will travel
as a growing sphere from the emission point. The further away the wave is from the emission

a larger surface area and some of the energy being converted into heat in the medium.
If the sound waves meet a medium of diﬀerent acoustic impedance a part of the sound wave
will be reﬂected. The reﬂected sound wave’s amplitude depends on the diﬀerence in acoustic
impedance. The reﬂected wave is then detected by the same transducer as used to emit the
waves. Using the timespan from the emission to the detection of the ultrasound wave, the
distance from the transducer to the reﬂecting medium can be calculated. The amplitude of
the reﬂected wave will reveal the change in acoustic impedance. With several elements in
a transducer being able to emit and detect ultrasound waves a cross sectional image of for
example organs in a human body can be produced.
1.2
Transducers
The traditional piezoelectric transducers use piezoelectric crystals which mechanically de-
form when an electric ﬁeld is applied across it. In this way electrical energy is converted
into mechanical energy and back again. By controlling the AC voltage the volume change
can be optimized to emit and detect ultrasound waves as used in ultrasound imaging.
CMUTs are based on a diﬀerent idea where a plate is deﬂected and this deﬂection moves
the surrounding medium enabling an emission of ultrasound, like Figure 1.2 shows. A DC
potential attracts the plate so the plate is in a steady state bending towards the bottom sub-
strate. From this steady state an AC potential is applied and moves the plate in controlled
oscillations. The bottom substrate should be thick and the plate thin in order for the plate
to be the only one deﬂecting. Surrounding the gap, an insulating material is separating the
plate from the substrate and preventing short circuit.
In an ultrasound probe, multiple elements are placed side by side, forming an array as shown
in Figure 1.3. Here a linear, also called 1D, array consisting of 36 elements is shown. For
the CMUT design, every second element has the bottom contact on the same side of the
element, which gives 18 bottom contacts on each side of the array.
In a CMUT the elements consist of several CMUT cells and have a bottom contact pad.
3
==PAGE:14==
Figure 1.3: 1D CMUT array consisting of 36 elements with 18 bottom contact
pads on each side of the array. In a CMUT the elements consist of cells which
are able to emit and detect ultrasound.
In a piezoelectric probe each element consists of one piezoelectric crystal. The elements
are able to emit and detect ultrasound. By controlling the emission of ultrasound from the
elements electrically, it is possible to control the focus and produce a 2D image in front of
the ultrasound transducer.
The dimensions of a CMUT cell determine the resonance frequency being the frequency
in which the transducer is normally operated. The operating frequency is a trade-oﬀof
resolution and depth of penetration. A higher frequency will give a higher resolution but
will attenuate faster in the medium compared to a lower frequency.
Contrary, a lower
frequency will be able to produce images with larger penetration depths, but with lower
resolution.
1.2.1
CMUT fabrication methods
The CMUT is a microelectromechanical system (MEMS) fabricated in a clean room, where
the air is almost cleared from particles. This is important, as particles can cause problems
in fabrication and destroy the device due to the small dimensions of the CMUTs.
Starting from 1994 diﬀerent kinds of fabrication processes for CMUTs have been proposed
and carried out [5]. The processes can be grouped into surface machining and bulk machin-
ing. The most common surface machining fabrication method is to use sacriﬁcial release.
This was the ﬁrst method used for fabricating CMUTs. In this method, several layers are
deposited on top of a substrate. By etching one of the middle layers, it is possible to create
a cavity in between two electrodes, as seen in Figure 1.4. A potential across the electrodes
is used to deﬂect and oscillate the top plate to transmit and detect ultrasound waves. The
sacriﬁcial release fabrication method unfortunately induce some problems. One challenge is
the plate sticking to the bottom substrate after sacriﬁcial release and the CMUT thereby
being destroyed. The fabrication can also induce stress which aﬀects the CMUT operation
undesirably and the need for holes in the plate reduces the active plate area.
In bulk machining there are three main fabrication methods: adhesive bonding, fusion
bonding and anodic bonding.
4
==PAGE:15==

Figure 1.4: Four diﬀerent main methods of fabricating CMUTs. Sacriﬁcial
release uses surface machining to create the cavities, while the other three takes
advantage of three diﬀerent boding methods. The materials used in the device
depend on the fabrication method, as can be seen by the illustrations.
By using adhesive bonding it is possible to bond any two materials to each other.
An
intermediate layer between the two substrates is used to bind them together as shown in
Figure 1.4. The intermediate layer is hardened after assembling the stack, for example by
heat treatment or exposing it to light. In CMUTs the intermediate layer is often used as the
separation between top and bottom electrode. It should therefore be insulating to prevent
a short circuit. Depending on the adhesive, this can be a challenge to obtain, as recently
shown where the adhesive medium benzocyclobutene has a breakdown voltage too low to
be used as adhesive layer [9].
One of the other dominating fabrication processes for CMUTs uses fusion bonding to bond
a bottom substrate with a top plate. In this method two silicon wafers are bonded together
at a high temperature as the stack illustrated in Figure 1.4. The bottom wafer has previ-
ously been processed so it has cavities formed in silicon dioxide and the top wafer is often a
silicon on insulator wafer (SOI). The SOI wafer can be thinned down to a few micrometers,
thereby achieving a thin plate over the cavities.
A requirement for fusion bonding is that the bonding surfaces are very ﬂat with a root
mean square (RMS) surface roughness no more than 5 Å [10]. Thus it is very sensitive to
particles.
The typical process ﬂow for producing fusion bonded CMUTs involves several thermal pro-
cesses and local oxidation. The local oxidation requires additional process steps for masking
the oxidation. These process steps expand the process ﬂow and make the fusion bonded
CMUTs more time demanding to fabricate compared to the other bonding methods.
The high temperatures used in fusion bonding exclude the possibility of monolithic integra-
tion with circuits where metals are used and can be harmful to sensitive structures.
This thesis explores a relatively new CMUT fabrication method which uses anodic bonding.
It has potential of fabricating CMUTs faster and easier than with the use of fusion bonding
and overcome the problems experienced in adhesive bonding and in the sacriﬁcial release
method.
To fabricate CMUTs using anodic bonding the bottom substrate is replaced with an ion
containing glass substrate as illustrated in Figure 1.4. A potential is needed over the two
substrates to move the ions and produce a large electrostatic ﬁeld which will pull the bottom
and top substrate together and bond them. The temperature over the stack is also increased
so the ions become more mobile.
There are several advantages of producing CMUTs with the use of anodic bonding. Generally
5
==PAGE:16==
it is reported that anodic bonding can tolerate higher RMS surface roughness than fusion
bonding [11, 12]. It is reported that anodic bonding can tolerate 10 times higher RMS
surface roughness, but the empirical results supporting this cannot be found in the citations
[13].
The MEMS-Applied Sensors group has recently anodic bonded a glass wafer to a polysilicon
wafer with a RMS surface roughness of 360 Å. This is a much higher RMS surface roughness
than the 10 times reported. It supports the reports of anodic bonding being less sensitive
to the RMS surface roughness.
Though, a clean environment is still needed as particles can create devastating voids.
The process temperature during anodic bonding is relatively low compared to fusion bond-
ing, allowing the possibility of incorporating integrated circuits.
Integrated circuits are an advantage as the transducer is small and has a lot of connec-
tions. To make sure that the measured data is not attenuated from the transducer to the
electronics, it is beneﬁcial to have a receiver ampliﬁer as close to the contacts as possible.
Another advantage is that the cavities can be etched directly into the bottom substrate
and the bottom electrode can be deposited directly into these cavities. This reduces the
parasitic capacitance, which has been observed in fusion bonded CMUTs to lower the eﬀect
[14].
In industry, anodic bonding is a widely used technology for fabricating packaging appli-
cations in MEMS devices as pressure sensors and accelerometers because of its simplicity,
reliability and high tolerance of surface roughness [12].
Compared to the fabrication of fusion bonded CMUTs, the fabrication of anodic bonded
CMUTs is less time demanding, enabling a higher throughput, which is beneﬁcial for both
industry and research.
1.2.2
Fabrication of anodic bonded CMUTs
The goal of this project is to fabricate a CMUT with the use of anodic bonding. The chosen
bottom substrate is glass of the trademark Boroﬂoat and the top plate is silicon from a
device layer of an SOI wafer, as argued for in Section 2.2.1.
The fabrication is conducted in a clean environment in DTU Danchip clean room. The
clean room oﬀers an environment and equipment in class 10-100. This low particle level is
required for the CMUTs not to be destroyed during fabrication.
As showed in Figure 1.5, the process ﬂow can be split into these steps:
a) Etching cavities in Boroﬂoat
b) Depositing metal for bottom electrodes
c) Anodic bonding an SOI wafer with oxide to Boroﬂoat
d) Removing silicon handle layer and buried oxide from the SOI wafer
e) Depositing top electrode
f) Opening up to bottom electrode

CMUT design
An element in a CMUT array consists of cells as shown in Figure 1.3. The cells in the
elements are in groups of 4×4 cells. An element has 20 of these groups in a row, making
the element narrow and long. The elements are not connected to each other with cavities
or bottom electrodes.
There are produced two kinds of arrays: linear, 1D, arrays or row-column, 2D, arrays. A
1D array is able to produce 2D images and a 2D array is able to produce 3D images. A
part of a 2D array is illustrated in Figure 1.6. The 2D array has top electrodes with the
dimensions of the bottom elements. These top electrodes are rotated to be perpendicular
to the bottom electrodes and can be activated separately from the bottom elements. It is
thereby possible to activate a group of 4×4 cells at a time in a 2D array.
In a 1D array the elements have bottom contact pads which are connected to electrodes in
all the cells of one element. The top electrode is a layer which will cover all of the elements
at once and does not, for this project, need a contact pad as the connection can be anywhere
on the electrode.
The 2D arrays needs bottom and top contact pads, as all the electrodes are controlled
individually.
This project is aimed to produce eight 1D arrays and six 2D arrays, as shown on a wafer in
Figure 1.7. The remaining structure on the wafer is test structures and alignment patterns.
Of the 1D arrays, two are produced with 64 elements, four are produced with 48 elements
and two are produced with 36 elements. Of the 2D arrays, four are produced with 64 bottom
elements and two are produced with 72 bottom elements.

1.2.3
Publications with anodic bonded CMUTs
In 2009 the ﬁrst article of an anodic bonded CMUT was published [15]. Since then, anodic
bonded CMUTs have primarily been researched by Yamaner and Zhang, who are currently
in Oralkan’s group at North Carolina State University.
In the article from 2009 by Olcum, the CMUT cavities are created by etching both the top
and bottom substrate which are to be bonded. The cavities in the top substrate are created
in a silicon dioxide grown on a silicon wafer and the cavities in the bottom substrate are
dry etched in glass and then the bottom electrode deposited into. The thickness of the top
plate is 380 µm, which is over a 100 times thicker than the top plates commonly used today.
The silicon dioxide is used to isolate the top and bottom electrode in active mode. When
bonding the voltage is ramped from 200 V to 1000 V at 450◦C.
The next two articles regarding anodic bonded CMUTs were published in 2011 and 2012
with Yamaner as the lead author and Olcum as second author [16, 17]. The cavities are still
created in both the top and bottom substrate which are to be bonded. The cavities in the

CMUT
The CMUT is a transducer. A transducer is a device which converts energy from one domain
to another. In this case the CMUT is used for converting electric energy into acoustic energy
and back again, in this way emitting ultrasound waves and detecting them.
The CMUT is operated by applying a constant bias voltage, Vbias, and an alternating voltage
,VAC, leading to the total applied voltage

achieved.
The plate deﬂects towards the bottom substrate when the voltage is applied. The deﬂection
proﬁle is depended on the material properties, the design of the CMUT cells, the cells’ and

2.2
Anodic bonding
Anodic bonding, also known as ﬁeld-assisted thermal bonding or electrostatic bonding, is a
simple method for producing high-quality hermetic seals between, in its most simple case,
silicon and an ion containing glass like a borosilicate glass. [11]
Anodic bonding is conducted by applying an electrostatic ﬁeld of 100-1500 V across a stack
of substrates at a temperature of 300-500◦C [12]. The bonding method takes advantage
of the ions in the glass. The applied electric ﬁeld moves the ions and polarize the glass,
thereby producing an increased electrostatic ﬁeld in the gap between the substrates and
which forces the substrates together.
The concept of anodic bonding was ﬁrst introduced by Wallis and Pomerants in 1966 and
pantended in 1968 [11]. Today it is an important part in the industry when producing
MEMS devices like accelerometers, piezoelectric pressure sensors and actuators because of
its reliability, simplicity and advantages [12, 34]. Anodic bonding can produce long term
hermetic seals, which are important to maintain the function of MEMS devices as they
can be exposed to harsh environments, changes in temperature, vibrations and surrounding
ﬂuids.
As mentioned in Section 1.2.1, one of the main competitive advantages of anodic bonding
compared to fusion bonding is its tolerance for higher RMS surface roughness. Other com-
petitive advantages are how anodic bonding can produce strong bonds with tensile strength
in the order of 10-15 MPa, and in general, the substrate will break before the bond fails
[12]. The method opens for incorporating integrated circuits because of the low process
temperature, compared to fusion bonding. The transparency of the glass makes it easy to
visually check the quality of the bond just after bonding, as trapped air can be seen as voids
and electrode burnings might be visual by darker areas. Entire wafers and stacks of layers
can be bonded at one time [11].
2.2.1
Bonding materials
The material closest to the anode can be a metal, semiconductor or alloy. The material
should be able to prevent ion movement to and from the material closest to the cathode.
If it fails to do so, ions can be relocated into the material closest to the anode and prevent
the polarization needed for anodic bonding.
The two materials to be bonded should be able to produce a high quality bond at their in-
terface and if there is produced an intermediate layer between the materials during bonding,
this layer should adhere well to both materials.
The cathode and anode materials should have matching thermal expansion coeﬃcients, so
the build up of internal stress when bonding is as low as possible. A large stress can destroy
the bond and substrates. [12] For a glass to be suitable cathode for anodic bonding it must
contain alkali ions which can move at the bonding temperature.
All ion containing glasses can be used as cathode in anodic bonding. The most common
glasses used are sodium-containing and are for example Corning (Pyrex) 7740, Corning
7070, Boroﬂoat 33 by Schott(Boroﬂoat), SD1 and SD2.
These glasses are fabricated to be bonded together with silicon, as this is the most commonly
used anode material.

2.2.2
Bonding mechanism
Anodic bonding is obtained by stacking the substrates that are to be bonded, increasing the
temperature and applying a bias, where Boroﬂoat is at the cathode and silicon at the anode
as seen in Figure 2.4. The applied electric ﬁeld will make cations in the glass move towards
the cathode. Boroﬂoat consists of oxides and the main charge carrier in anodic bonding
is sodium oxide (Na2O). As there is no counterﬂow of anions, because they are bound in
the substrate, an alkali-depleted region is created in the Boroﬂoat in the vicinity of the
silicon as shown in Figure 2.4. The bias is mainly distributed between the alkali-depleted
region and the gap between the substrates. The alkali-depleted region’s width depends on
the applied bias and will for a ﬁxed bias increase in time until reaching a steady state.
As the alkali-depleted region’s width increases the electrostatic pressure between the two
substrates increases, causing them to increasingly attract one another which causes this
bonding method to be eﬀective. Being pushed together, the electrostatic pressure enforces
an electrochemical process that will bond the two substrates together. The electrostatic
pressure is highly inﬂuenced by the gap height between the substrates, the depletions region’s
width, charge density and applied voltage.
The two substrates are initially only in intimate contact at a few locations due to surface
roughness as illustrated in Figure 2.4. This means that the two substrates will be separated
with a gap almost across the entire surface. The electrostatic ﬁeld is largest where the gap
is most narrow, meaning that this is where the highest electrostatic pressure is and it is at
this position the substrates will be pulled together ﬁrst.
The bonding widens out from the initial contact points, as the surrounding gap is the
smallest and thereby the electrostatic pressure the largest.
Giving the bonding process
appropriate time and voltage, an anodic bond will be produced across the entire surface.
2.2.3
Bonding parameters
The main parameters to look out for when bonding are temperature, voltage and current.
The current will deﬁne the time for the bonding process.
When a voltage is applied across a stack of wafers a large current will be seen followed by
a gradual decrease which will reach a steady state after some time as seen in Figure 2.5.
The current is produced by the movement of ions in the glass. Two eﬀects contribute to
the shape of the current plot. As the ions move, the width of the alkali-depleted region
increases. This causes the electrostatic force to increase and in return the formation of
intimate contact and ions moving increases, which increases the current. Simultaneously
the voltage drop changes to being over the alkali-depleted region, as a larger resistance is
present. The capacitor charges up and the amount of ions moving decreases, which decreases
the current amplitude.
The current peak amplitude depends on the temperature, the voltage and glass substrate. A
larger current peak amplitude corresponds to a higher rate of formation of intimate contact
between the two substrates and a faster bond formation [11, 12].

A rapid rise in current followed by a fast decrease is believed to be an indication of a good
bond [34]. The magnitude of the total charge transported gives an indication of the strength
of the bond [11].
The bonding process is considered ﬁnished when the current has dropped to 10% of it’s
peak value, which takes approximately 10-30 min. [36]
Setting a limit on the maximum current gives better control of the process, improves the
reproducibility of devices and reduces the wafer bow caused by stress in the wafer [37]. A
high current can also produce localised heating at the interface between silicon and glass
where they are in intimate contact. As strong bonds are still required, i.e. high accumulated
charge, a method of three voltage increments leading to three current peaks is used. Figure
2.6 shows an anodic bonding process where three voltage increments have been used and the
ﬁrst current peak is limited. By increasing the voltage in each step, the intimate contact area
and the bond strength are gradually increased and the temperature increased uniformly over
the wafer. Limiting the current peak amplitude also decreases the chance of an electrical
breakdown, which could ruin the device. [37]
For this project, a current peak amplitude between 9 mA and 14 mA has been recommended.
The maximum current in the bonding machine is set to 14 mA, as can also be seen for the
limited peak Figure 2.6.
Bonding can be produced both in vacuum and at low pressures. A low pressure will help
transport heat and improve uniform heating compared to vacuum, but a vacuum can help
reduce the pressure inside the cavities, when they are enclosed in the bond. Gases can be

released during bonding from inner surfaces and be trapped in cavities that are buried when
bonding.
Many MEMS devices need a low pressure or vacuum in their cavities. The release of gas
during bonding can therefore be problematic. The pressure inside enclosed cavities bonded
in vacuum can be as low as 1 mbar, but for smaller cavities has seen to be up to 500 mbar.
[11]
The electrostatic force pulling the two substrates together is strongly depended on the
distance between the substrates. For the bond to be as successful as possible the distance
between the two substrates should initially be as small as possible and the amount of
particles between the substrates reduced to its minimum. A clean environment is therefore
preferred and pre cleaning of the surfaces that are to be bonded can be an advantage. [11]
Particles can create stress and voids and possible destroy a MEMS device. In anodic bonding
the temperature is increased and the glass is in a solid-state ﬂuid, meaning that it can
deform. This makes the glass able to ﬂow non-elastically around particles and roughness,
produce a hermetic bond and makes the bonding method more tolerant to surface roughness
and particles compared to other bonding methods like fusion bonding. [11, 34]
The cathode and anode enabling a potential over the wafer stack can either be plate or
point electrodes. The most commonly used anode is a plate electrode covering the entire
anode material while the cathode is varied between point and plate. [12]
Having a plate cathode electrode the electric ﬁeld will be distributed uniformly over the
bonding stack and so will the sodium cation current in the glass. This increases the uni-
formity of the bond quality. With a point cathode the electric ﬁeld is very concentrated at
one place in the glass and ﬁeld lines will spread out from this point. [34]
To avoid air voids, a point cathode can be preferable to let the intimate contact start from
this point and go towards the edges. This way air can be pushed out and voids reduced.
[38]
When using a plate cathode the eﬀect of point cathodes can occur if the surface roughness
is high and the contact points thereby few. This should be taken into account, if a uniform
current ﬂow is important.
The very concentrated electric ﬁeld lines can cause local electrical breakdown and produce
a non-uniform material composition of glass in vicinity of the cathode due to non-uniform
sodium cation current. The sodium cations in vicinity of the cathode can react with the air
and form sodium oxide which is white. A high concentration of this can be visible. [34]
2.2.4
Intermediate layers
During anodic bonding between silicon and glass a chemical reaction takes place at the
interface producing an intermediate layer of silicon dioxide. The chemical process on how
this silicon dioxide layer is formed is not agreed upon [11].

2.4
Current-time characteristics
For understanding the electrical properties and current response it is convenient to consider
the electric circuit and current response during bonding.
The circuit consists of a bonding machine in which there are electronics that produce a
potential over two electrodes, the cathode and anode. The focus in this study will be on the
electric circuit from the cathode to the anode, which is the electric circuit over the stack of
bonding materials.
Many electric circuit models have in literature been proposed and used to ﬁt the current
response of an anodic bonding process. Knowles and van Helvoort explains and discusses
seven diﬀerent models in two publications, of which one of the models is proposed by them
self [12] [40].
This section will describe three electric circuit models. The ﬁrst is a simple circuit with a
capacitor and resistor in series. The next two models take a leakage current into account by
a parallel connection with a resistor over the capacitor. The models will be used in Section
4.2 where they are ﬁtted to the data obtained from the anodic bonding processes.

2.5
Etching of cavities in Boroﬂoat
There are diﬀerent methods to which one can produce cavities. For this project anodic
bonding is used and therefore it is required that the bonding surfaces are Boroﬂoat and
silicon or silicon dioxide.
The cavities can be buried in one or the other or both. An illustration of the three cases
can be seen in Figure 2.10. It is required that there is electrical isolation between the top
and bottom electrodes, which are marked as metals in the illustration.
If the cavity is etched in both substrates and the bottom electrode deposited is in the
bottom, it requires two lithography steps and two etch steps.
Producing the cavity in the top substrate only requires one step for etching the cavity, but
still requires two lithography steps, as the metal also need to be patterned on the Boroﬂoat.
As there is pattern on the bottom and top substrate an alignment bonding is required.
The third case is where the cavities are etched in Boroﬂoat. This process only needs one
lithography step, as both the cavities and the metal can use the same mask. The metal
is also well isolated from the top electrode, as it only touches the glass. This method is
assumed to be the least time consuming way of producing the cavities and will therefore be
used in this project.
The cavities in Boroﬂoat can be etched either using dry or wet etching.
There has been several publications where Boroﬂoat was dry etched to create cavities. One

etchant can be pulled in between the masking material and Boroﬂoat by capillary forces
resulting in accelerated underetch.

steps for etching, depositing the bottom electrode and producing lift-oﬀ. The hard mask

and one to remove it again.
Chromium can be used as the hard mask material. Though in this case the adhesive for
the bottom electrode of gold has to be another material than chromium or it will be etched
when removing the hard mask. The adhesive could for example be titanium. Though it is
a disadvantage to use titanium, as the etch rate in the cleaning solution Piranha, which is
used before the anodic bonding process, is relatively high.

high an etch rate in Piranha as titanium and this allows for the cleaning process of Piranha

CHAPTER4
Anodic bonding optimization
In order to obtain an optimal anodic bond of a CMUT, this chapter will investigate diﬀerent
parameters when bonding. The goal is to understand the current response during anodic
bonding and ﬁnd the voltage steps needed for anodic bonding when fabricating CMUTs.
4.1
Bonding parameters
As described in Section 1.2.2, the CMUT is fabricated by anodic bonding an SOI wafer with
a Boroﬂoat wafer which has cavities with gold in. Between the SOI and Boroﬂoat wafer
there is an intermediate layer of silicon dioxide. This is a stack consisting of many layers
and factors which can inﬂuence the anodic bonding process.
To understand the anodic bonding process, the most simple combination of bonding ma-
terials, being a stack of materials, are started with. The complexity of the stack is then
increased gradually.
The diﬀerent stack combinations being anodic bonded are illustrated in Figure 4.1 and are,
corresponding to their complexity, in the following order:
a) Silicon to Boroﬂoat
b) Silicon with silicon dioxide to Boroﬂoat
c) Silicon with silicon dioxide to Boroﬂoat with cavities
d) Silicon with silicon dioxide to Boroﬂoat with cavities and gold
e) An SOI wafer with silicon dioxide to Boroﬂoat with cavities and gold
When substituting the silicon with an SOI wafer an additional oxide layer is added in the
stack, which introduces another capacitor in the electrical circuit.
49
==PAGE:60==
Figure 4.1: The diﬀerent stack combinations which are to be anodic bonded
in the following chapter.
4.1.1
Si to Boroﬂoat
The ﬁrst anodic bond conducted is between a Boroﬂoat wafer and a silicon wafer.
Inside the bonding chamber, the silicon wafer rests on a plate anode chuck and the Boroﬂoat
wafer rests on top of the silicon wafer. When bonding, a cathode electrode lowers down
on the Boroﬂoat wafer and presses with 1000 mbar. The electrode covers almost the entire
wafer.
All of the bonds in this project are conducted at a chamber pressure of 5×10−3 mbar.
The current and voltage graphs are seen in Figure 4.2. By producing the bond in three volt-
age steps with increasing voltage, it is possible to lower the current’s maximum amplitude
and keep a high accumulated charge, as described in Section 2.2.3. The voltage steps are
-200 V, -300 V and -450 V. The voltage input and output are negative, due to the electrodes’
conﬁguration in the bonding machine.
A rapid increase in current followed by a fast decrease indicates a good bond. When the
third voltage step cuts oﬀthe voltage and the third current peak is ﬁnished, the accumulated
charge is 900 mC. A higher accumulated charge indicates a stronger bond. See Appendix
A.2 for the accumulated charge curve.
Current peaks between 9 mA and 14 mA are preferred. A much higher current can poten-
tially destroy structures in the device. Therefore the anodic bonding machine is set to cut
oﬀthe current above 14 mA.
The temperature is at 350◦C throughout the ramp of voltages, as this increases the mobil-
ity of the sodium ions while still being below the temperature for eutectic bond between
gold and silicon at the bonding chamber pressure. Furthermore, at this temperature glass
and silicon have matching thermal expansion coeﬃcients which minimizes the stress when
bonding as described in Section 2.2.1.
The process with the chosen parameters produced a bond between silicon and Boroﬂoat.
Only few air voids at the edge are observed, indicating that the bond spans almost the
entire wafer. The current peaks in Figure 4.2 are well-deﬁned. The ﬁrst peak reaches the
limit of 14 mA, indicating that the current amplitude is too high. The second and third
current peak amplitudes are lower than 9 mA. They could be increased to increase the
charge accumulation and produce a stronger bond.

4.1.3
Si with SiO2 to Boroﬂoat with cavities
The next step is to ﬁnd the optimal bonding voltages when the Boroﬂoat wafer has cavities
as the CMUT will have.
The ﬁrst bond is conducted with the same voltage steps as used when bonding Boroﬂoat to
silicon with an intermediate layer at 350◦C. The current graph is shown in Figure 4.4. The
ﬁrst current peak has an amplitude of 8 mA, which is lower than the preferred minimum of
9 mA. The low peak means that there are still many free sodium ions which can be moved.
This is seen in the second voltage step, as an increase in voltage results in a high current
peak amplitude. The second peak is cut oﬀat 14 mA, but the wide plateau at the peak

For the CMUT to be operational as an ultrasound transducer all of the elements should be
able to transmit and receive ultrasound. The emitted ultrasound wave should be able to
penetrate the desired tissue without the pressure amplitude being attenuated to a degree
where the signal is undetectable by the transducer. For the purpose of this project, the
pressure amplitude should be as high as possible for the CMUTs. The coupling between the
electrical and acoustic domain is a measure of how eﬃcient the electrical energy is converted
into acoustic energy. This means that the higher the coupling between the electrical and
acoustic domain is, the higher the acoustic wave pressure will be.
With an increase in voltage, the capacitance should increase parabolic, as it depends on the
voltage squared. The capacitance is required to be stable in time, for the CMUT to be able
to scan for longer periods of time and reproduce images.
The resonance frequency is also of interest as this determines the frequency the CMUT will
be operated at and thereby the degree of resolution and penetration into tissue.
This chapter will characterize the CMUTs’ capacitance, pull-in voltage, electrical impedance
and stability over time.
Charging is a problem which has been seen in previous fabricated CMUTs. Therefore this
will be paid attention to during the characterization.
As several bottom contacts did not have the silicon dioxide removed, see Section 5, it will
be investigated if these elements are diﬀerent from the ones without silicon dioxide.
The electrical characterization was conducted on a Cascade Summit 12000 probe station
with a KEYSIGHT B1500A Semiconductor Device Analyzer. The probe station is a multi
parameter measuring tool. For this project it will be used to measure current, capacitance
and impedance.

Dynamic charging is an accumulation of charges inside the CMUT cells. The accumulation
of charges decrease the electric ﬁeld in the cells and make the top plate bend less. This will
decrease the spring softening of the top plate, the capacitance, the phase angle and thereby
the coupling between the electrical and mechanical domain.
Dynamic charging is also present when stepping the bias from 70 V to 0 V and from 70 V
to −70 V. A possible reason can be charges during the bias of 70 V situating themselves in
the cell, for example in the silicon dioxide layer, and needing time after the bias is changed
to be neutralized.

the silicon dioxide being responsible for the charging eﬀect as it is only deposited on one
side of the cell. The charges could potentially be ions and the polarity of the bias could
deﬁne which ions are created near the silicon dioxide. Diﬀerent ions have diﬀerent mobilities
in materials and if the ions move into the silicon dioxide and cause the dynamic charging,
this might explain why there is an asymmetrical polarity bias charging. It has previously
been seen in the group how CMUTs with silicon dioxide are more prone to have charging
problems. Therefore one has reasons to assume that the silicon dioxide is responsible for
the charging.

CHAPTER7
Conclusion and outlook
The aim of this project was to fabricate and characterize fully functional anodic bonded
CMUTs. This goal has been achieved. The process was optimized by two studies of etching
CMUT cavities in Boroﬂoat and optimizing voltage steps for anodic bonding respectively.
The fabricated CMUT elements yielded, by their impedance measurements in air, a coupling
coeﬃcient of 17% with an applied bias voltage set to 94% of the pull-in voltage.
It was found that the resist mask adhesion was poor when etching the CMUT cavities in
BHF. The poor adhesion caused an underetch during etching of more than 400 µm and
an underetch to vertical etch ratio of up to 755, resulting in cavities with too large lateral
dimensions. By dehydrating the Boroﬂoat and the resist before and in between etching it
was possible to reduce the underetch and the underetch to vertical etch ratio. This way
cavities were produced with side length of 58.3 µm and a depth of 434 nm, which is similar
to the designed dimensions of the squared CMUT cells. The optimal process was found to
be: 10 min 150◦C hard baking of Boroﬂoat prior to spin coating resist, splitting the etch of
cavities into three time slots of 5.5 min, 5.5 min and 6 min and 10 min 120◦C hard baking
of the Boroﬂoat between the etch time slots. Using this process on a batch of eight wafers
resulted in an underetch of underetch of 2.15 µm and an underetch to vertical etch ratio of
5.
An investigation of temperature and voltage steps during anodic bonding was carried out.
The current-time characteristics from the anodic bonding processes were used to estimate
the bond quality and optimize the bonding parameters. With a bonding temperature of
350◦C and absolute voltage steps of 450 V, 650 V and 850 V a Boroﬂoat wafer with cavities
and gold electrode was bonded to an SOI wafer with 150 nm silicon dioxide. The bond
covered the entire wafer surface and had no visible voids.
Eight linear arrays were fabricated, two of these arrays have 64 elements, four have 48
elements and two have 36 elements.

The yield of working CMUT elements is 15%. The remaining 85% of the elements had a

The CMUT elements were characterized electrically, and were found to have a minimum

Impedance measurements show a resonance frequency of approximately 7 MHz in air with

coeﬃcients for CMUTs fabricated with fusion bonding in the group.
In one bias polarity the CMUT element was stable over time and will be able to reproduce
images with a high coupling between the electrical and mechanical domain.
It is concluded that from the work of this thesis with optimization of two process steps and
determination of recipes and etch rates, it is in the future possible to fabricate a batch of
wafers with anodic bonded CMUTs in ﬁve workdays under the assumption of the equipment
being free to use.
Outlook
For future work it is recommended to investigate the stability of the CMUT elements with
an applied negative bias for a longer time period. A stable impedance over time has until
now led to a stable acoustic response. A stable acoustic response is important for the CMUT
to be commercialized.
The next step is to characterize the elements acoustically. This will determine the acoustic
bandwidth and ability to produce ultrasound images.
In the future it is recommended to design the CMUT arrays with bottom contact pads less
than or equal to 240×480 µm to minimize the risk of the top plate falling oﬀ. This suggestion
has already been implemented in an ongoing project in the group, and it is expected in the
near future to be able know if this will handle the problem.
Other ways to possibly avoid the top plate fallen oﬀare by increasing the anodic bond
strength, which could be done by increasing the bonding voltages, or changing the stress

the clean room rules. Another equipment capable of removing silicon which still allows
Boroﬂoat wafers to be exposed could also be used.
It is also recommended to investigate the demand in time and eﬀort for fabricating cavities
with a hard mask instead of a resist mask. As there is currently a master project in the
group investigating this, a comparison of the two methods will be possible in the near future.
For a higher yield of CMUT elements it is recommended to investigate the cause for the
short circuit. This could be possible by investigating the produced wafer from this project
and possibly producing scanning electron microscope images of the bottom contact areas or
of cross sections from the CMUT cells.
For the future, a cathode that covers the entire 4” wafer should be used when anodic bond-
ing a 4” wafer. This way the electric potential should be distributed evenly across the wafer.
As the cleaning solution Piranha before bonding etches the two most commonly used materi-
als for gold to adhere to Boroﬂoat, chromium and titanium, it is recommended to investigate
if there are possible other adhesion materials which can be used in stead.
A batch of anodic bonded CMUTs have potential of being fabricated in ﬁve workdays. This
is a huge advantage to the fusion bonded CMUTs and it is recommended to try out how
fast the fabrication can be done.

A.2
Charge accumulation
As the potential is applied over the wafers a current of cations is produced in the Boroﬂoat.
This shows as a current in the current-time characteristics. If the two wafers works as a
series connection composing of a capacitor or capacitors and resistors, the integrated current
corresponds to the build up of charge on the capacitor or capacitors.
For the optimal bonding voltage steps of the diﬀerent stack conﬁgurations, the accumulated
charges are shown in Figure A.1. The charge curves each show three steps corresponding to
the three current peaks.
For the most simple bonding conﬁguration, with silicon and Boroﬂoat, the accumulated
charge after third the current peak is ﬁnished is 900 mC. With an addition of a silicon
dioxide intermediate layer and an increase in voltage, the accumulated charge is 1.1 C.
The accumulated charge is decreased to 800 mC when cavities are etched in the Boroﬂoat
due to the less area being in intimate contact.
The two bonds with gold in the Boroﬂoat cavities had second and third current peaks
for much longer time than the previous bondings without gold. This indicated a leakage
current. It can also be seen how the accumulated charge is larger then the ones from the
others bonds. The accumulated charge for Si + SiO2 bonded to Boroﬂoat with cavities and
gold is 2.6 C and with an SOI wafer it is 2.9 C.
A.3
Anodic bonding at increased temperatures
Anodic bonding of silicon with silicon dioxide to Boroﬂoat. Figure A.2 shows the current and
voltage graphs when bonding at 400◦C and with voltage steps of -200 V, -400 V and -600 V.
The ﬁrst current peak is very low, indicating that the voltage was too low to create intimate
contact between the substrates and for the sodium ions to move towards the cathode. The
voltage is turned oﬀafter a time limit is reached. The second and third current peak are
well-deﬁned and at have a peak amplitude at 14 mA indicating a creating of a good bond.
