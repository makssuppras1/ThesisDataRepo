Abstract
Transcranial magnetic stimulation is a non-invasive brain stimulation technique in which a changing mag-
netic field is used to induce an electric current at a specific brain area through electromagnetic induction.
Recently, this technique has been combined with sensing to deliver a more precise therapy to patients of
certain diseases, such as depression, opening new research areas to improve treatments. However, the combi-
nation of these techniques presents challenges in its implementation. The interaction between Transcranial
Magnetic Stimulation and recording equipment results in artifacts, that is, signals that are not the signal of
interest. This MSc thesis presents a setup to analyze and reduce the most significant artifact, the TMS pulse
artifact. The setup can detect and filter this artifact both offline and online. This system uses conductive
gelatine, which acts as a medium for injecting an EEG signal. The signal is then captured using electrodes
while the TMS device stimulates the testing environment. To detect and determine the best output, a Mat-
lab algorithm was developed. This algorithm utilizes a Kalman filter and combines the measurements with
a mathematical model to reduce the TMS artifact. The setup demonstrates the capabilities of the Kalman
filter to accurately compute the EEG signal in noisy environments, such as the one created by the TMS
pulse artifact.

Introduction
1.1
What is Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation
Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) is a non-invasive brain stimulation technique in which a changing
magnetic field induces an electric current at a specific brain area through electromagnetic induction using a
stimulation coil. This procedure has been used since 1985, when Anthony T Barker and his colleagues invented
the first type of TMS at the Royal Hallamshire Hospital in Sheffield, UK. The experiments consisted of the
application of magnetic fields through the use of an inductor.
By modifying parameters such as intensity,
frequency, and pulse duration, Barker found that brain regions, such as the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, could
have its neural activity inhibited, modified, or even induced neuroplasticity, that is, the capacity for the brain’s
neural networks to adapt through expansion and reorganization. Therefore, Barker scientifically demonstrated
the effect of magnetic stimulation on the human brain [1] [2].
The most simple iteration of the circuitry of TMS, as shown in figure 1, consists of an LC circuit activated by
a switching device.
Figure 1: Basic topology of a TMS circuit, the capacitor (C) is connected to the inductor (L, stimulation coil)
through a switch (S) [3].
The circuit’s behavior follows: First, a high voltage is applied to the capacitor. When the switch is closed,
current flows through the switch from the charged capacitor to the stimulation coil. In the coil, the current
creates a fluctuating magnetic field that induces an electric field, activating the neurons underneath the coil.
After Barker’s experiment, multiple studies assessed the effectiveness of TMS in psychiatric diseases such as
depression. Canada first authorized TMS therapy for depression in 2003; in 2008, the FDA approved it. The
third edition of the Practice Guideline for the Therapy of Patients with Major Melancholy, published by the
American Psychiatric Association (APA) in 2010, listed TMS as a standard therapy for depression [4].
The most common approach to treat depression with TMS is to use repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation
(rTMS). This consists of a therapy session where first, stimulation is delivered at the motor cortex until the
necessary stimulus to make the contralateral thumb muscles twitch is found. Then, thousands of pulses are
delivered to the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC). Figure 2 depicts two common rTMS protocols applied
at 1 Hz and 10 Hz, respectively [5].

1.2
What is Electroencephalography
Electroencephalography (EEG) is a recording of brain activity used to analyze electrical activity in the brain
in real-time. It uses sensors (electrodes) attached to the scalp to assess brain function. By examining electrical
impulses, EEG can aid in the diagnosis and treatment of conditions such as Epilepsy, Strokes, Sleep Disorders,
and Brain Damage [7].
The brain’s electrical activity is classified into different frequency bands, representing other functions. These
bands include Delta, Theta, Alpha, Beta, and Gamma waves. Delta waves, ranging from 0.5 to 4 Hz, are
associated with deep sleep and waking.
Theta waves, ranging from 4 to 8 Hz, are linked to consciousness
and creativity. Alpha waves, between 8 and 12 Hz, are related to awareness and attention. Gamma waves,
encompassing frequencies >30 Hz, are markers for diseases such as Alzheimer’s or Parkinson’s disease [8] [9].
On EEG recordings, all these signals are simultaneously represented as a superposition of sine signals with
different frequencies and phases.
A reading from the most used configuration, the 10-20 system, recording a patient’s epileptic seizure is provided
in figure 3 .

EEG signals are initially recorded by electrodes and sent to a pre-amplifier to increase their voltage level for
better filtering. Various signal processing techniques and filters, such as low-pass filtering, high-pass filtering,
and DC offset removal, are applied to remove noise and artifacts. Next, the EEG signal is segmented into
different frequency bands, and various features are extracted from these bands to analyze brain activity. These

1.3
Combining Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation and Electroencephalography
Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation and Electroencephalography have been used throughout the years to treat
and asses psychiatric diseases, respectively. Separately they have their proven medical uses widely demonstrated,
as explained in the previous sections. Combined, these techniques allow for reading biological signals in real-time
from the brain while a patient is being stimulated.
Regarding the use of these techniques together, recent research conducted by Christoph Zrenner, University
of Toronto, shows promise in this application. [12] [13]. Suggesting that combining both could improve the
treatment of diseases such as depression.
Zrenner proved that when stimulated with an alpha-synchronized rTMS protocol, the negative and positive peaks
observed in the endogenous sensorimotor µ-rhythm indicate the corticospinal neuron’s high and low excitability
states. It is important to note that when repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation is applied using identical
parameters (200 triple-pulses at 100 Hz burst frequency and 1 Hz repetition rate) and consistently triggered
during either the high or low excitability state, it elicits effects on corticospinal excitability that resemble
long-term potentiation (LTP) in the former case while demonstrating no discernible impact in the latter [12].
Moreover, when stimulated at the DLPFC with alpha-synchronized rTMS, Zrenner found neuromodulatory
effects on patients with chronic depression (MDD). When analyzing resting state EEG, a reduction in left frontal
resting-state alpha power, figure 5, associated with symptom improvements in MDD patients, was found [13].
Figure 5: Reduction in resting state alpha power, 10 Hz, after Alpha-synchronized rTMS therapy [13].

1.4
Objective
The TMS artifacts have been filtered out of EEG in previous papers with either post-processing tools such as
Independent component analysis (ICA) [14] or with forward predictions [15].ICA is a method that can only be
applied offline, and the latter method works by using estimations based on the input signal by making a forward
prediction about the behavior of the EEG on the stimulation pulse, which results in the loss of continuity in
the signal.
The University of Oxford requires a module to add to the multiple TMS devices at the institution to filter these
artifacts too, to test for the research hypothesis provided by Christoph Zrenner that rTMS coordinated therapy
could lead to a betterment of MDD markers or reduce the motor evoked potential thresholds [12] [13]. However,
for this purpose, real-time filtering of the signal is required. Therefore the objective of this master thesis is to
comprehend, analyze and create a solution for filtering the artifacts of TMS-EEG co-registration present in the
integration of the two techniques.

2
Literature Review
2.1
TMS-EEG Co-registration Challenges
When integrating TMS and EEG, different artifacts appear that disturb the readings. These artifacts can be
classified into two categories: electronics and equipment artifacts and physiological response artifacts [16].
2.1.1
Electronics and Equipment Artifacts
The artifacts due to the interactions between the TMS device and the electrodes are:
• TMS Pulse: Applying a magnetic field produces a potent electric field that induces significant spikes in
EEG readings. The TMS pulse artifact is what is referred to as this, and it usually has a voltage level
in the range of hundreds of uV and lasts for five milliseconds, as shown in figure 6. Although the exact
cause of the artifact is unknown, it has been theorized that it is related to variables such as electrode
polarization, muscle activity, or the tools employed [16].
Figure 6: TMS pulse artifact, the zero second mark indicates the start of the TMS pulse artifact (red), orders
of magnitude larger than the previous, noiseless EEG signal (blue) [17].
• TMS Recharge: TMS units use capacitors to enable the discharge of energy in the coil. The capacitor’s
performance is influenced by pulse frequency and intensity. The TMS capacitor begins to recharge early
to prevent performance degradation; this energy restoration process leads to a recharge artifact, figure
7. The recharging artifact impacts the electrodes closest to the stimulation site, which an exponential
function can model [16].
6
==PAGE:16==
Figure 7: The capacitors at the TMS unit recharge after every pulse, introducing potential spikes (red) in the
EEG signal (blue) [17].
• Electrode Motion: Charge accumulation brought on by finger tapping, physical contact with the coil,
variations in air pressure, or magnetic coupling results in electrode motion. This may result in artifacts
and up to 10 mV of potential fluctuations. Additionally, parasite currents at the electrolyte-paste-sensor
interface during TMS might result in movement and heating, which can burn the skin [16].
• Electrode Polarization: There are two types of EEG sensors: polarizable and non-polarizable. In the
case of polarizable electrodes, polarizable sensors use Cl- conductive gel to maintain a constant flow of
ionic current between the skin and the transducer. During the TMS pulse, polarisation takes place at
the electrode-gel contact. Sweat glands and hair follicles can disrupt the stratum corneum, a layer of
dead skin cells that serves as a capacitor with a hydrophobic dielectric. At the sensor-skin interface, slow
capacitive discharging occurs, eventually resulting in capacitive discharging that behaves like a power law
function, meaning that it happens at a slower rate than expected [16].
• External Interference: TMS-EEG setups can produce EEG artifacts that aren’t always related to the
TMS or the tools used in the lab. These can be caused by several things, such as background noise, the
discomfort of the subject, electrical and electromagnetic interference, and lighting. While noise-removal
tools can create new artifacts, other factors, including standard cardiac and respiratory patterns, can also
cause artifacts. For instance, "ripple" artifacts in the analyzed time series can be produced by online
notch filters intended to reduce 50–60 Hz noise [16].

2.1.2
Physiological Response Artifacts
• Muscle Activation: TMS-EEG research generally focuses on the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex or the
central sulcus as stimulation locations. TMS can immediately depolarize facial and scalp muscles at these
locations when applied close to the neck or forehead, producing a muscle artifact, as shown in the figure
8, that manifests as high-frequency signals in EEG readings [16].
Figure 8: Muscle activation artifact, the trace indicates the artifact provoked by muscle activation in the
patient’s scalp after a TMS pulse is delivered (red) and disrupts the EEG signal(blue), found before the zero
time mark [17].
• Eye Movement and Blink: Cornea-retinal potential is larger than basal EEG activity.
During TMS
sessions, eye movements produce a high-voltage waveform that can obscure the response of the natural
TEPs [16].
• Coil Click and Somatic Sensation: The transcranial magnetic stimulation coil’s audible clicking noise,
which elicits auditory evoked potentials (AEPs) and somatosensory evoked potentials (SEPs), are trou-
blesome when used in event-related potential (ERP) research.
The SEPs are typically highest in the
first 80 ms, but the AEPs are notably linked with auditory stimuli and have many peaks with lengthy
components.
The AEPs are also asymmetric, with the contralateral hemisphere showing the highest
magnitude [16].

2.2
Kalman Filter
To filter the TMS artifact, different techniques have been used in the past, such as ICA [14] or the forward
prediction [15]. These methods require either offline signal processing or making predictions without recording
the actual signal at the TMS pulse. However, there is another method that has been used previously in EEG
analysis [18], the Kalman filter.
The Kalman filter is a mathematical technique invented by Rudolf E Kalman [19], that is frequently used to
estimate the state of a dynamic system in an environment of unpredictable, noisy observations.
The filter
employs a recursive process to estimate the system’s state from a series of measurements taken over time. The
algorithm is typically used when dealing with systems that behave linearly and when the measurements are
tainted by random noise.

3
Methods
The goal of this thesis was to build a real-time closed-loop setup to detect and remove the TMS artifacts from
EEG was the final goal of this thesis. To do so, two experiments and setups were developed along the thesis
work. The method was developed in 2 steps. First, a test bench with an offline TMS artifact removal using the
Kalman filter was implemented. After successfully implementing the method, the setup was adapted to work
in real-time.
3.1
Offline Artifact subtraction
The project’s first step was to demonstrate that the TMS pulse artifacts could be removed by using a Kalman
filter.Figure 9 depicts the data pipeline.

with the conventional filtering procedures (Notch, High Pass, Low Pass).
3.1.1
Gelatine Phantom
Before proceeding with any filtering procedures, it was essential to establish a testing environment that accu-
rately replicated the conductivity of the human brain. This environment would serve as the reference point
for all subsequent experiments, involving the stimulation of it using the TMS machine and the connection of
electrodes to record simulated electrophysiological signals.
In an ideal case, this environment would be the head of a test subject, where EEG signals are naturally present,
and the final solution achieved would be directly translatable to other subjects. However, in the case of this
master thesis, another testing environment was selected as suitable: A Gelatine Phantom. Gelatine phantoms
serve as the ideal testing setup for multiple purposes. It allows isolating the TMS artifact from physiological
artifacts such as the muscle activation artifact, providing a suitable environment for characterizing and mini-
mizing this effect and providing an accurate reference signal after characterizing the noise floor. Moreover, the
gelatine is easily replicable and provides stable characteristics.
Gelatine phantoms are entities made of a combination of mainly pork gelatine, NaCl, and deionized water; quan-

recorded physiological signals are sent and then recorded back again through the gel.

3.1.3
Electrodes
To measure the brain signals to be injected into the gelatine phantom, electrodes are needed to capture the po-
tential readings on its surface of it. Electrodes are electrical conductors used for measuring signals at nonmetallic
parts of a circuit.
Different ways exist to set up an electrode connection with the human skin, as shown in figure 12. Depending on
the application, usage, and type of signals that are to be picked up by the sensor. The most common iterations
are: Wet, dry, insulated, and non-contact.
• Wet: These electrodes use a conductive medium such as a saline solution or a conductive gel to reduce the
contact impedance with the skin. They are most commonly used in EEG or EMG (Electromyography).
• Dry: These electrodes are directly applied to the human skin since they typically have conductive material
built-in the device. The benefit of these electrodes is that there is no resistance, and a conductive gel or
saline solution introduces the capacitance. Most typically used in EEG or ECG (Electrocardiography).
• Insulated: These electrodes cover a part of the conductive surface of the material and restrict part of the
current flow through the device. Most typically used for DBS (deep brain stimulation)
• Non-Contact: These electrodes measure the target signals without direct contact with the skin and usually
have a material between it and the electrode (for example, cotton). They are used when direct contact
with the skin is not possible or comfortable.

3.2
Artifact subtraction in Real-time
As stated in the literature review section, synchronized repetitive transcranial stimulation applied at the negative
peak of the alpha waves may be more efficacious. To achieve this, the previously developed method must be
adapted for real-time recordings.
This approach presents the following challenges on top of the ones present in the post-processing process:
• Communication with the TMS device to update error covariance matrices.
• Computational speed.
To communicate in real-time, maintaining the current filtering method, the initial approach would be to directly
connect the TMS device with the computer where the filtering algorithm is being executed. However, there
ports in the TMS device ("insert name of port later") and the laptop (Bluetooth 2.0) are not compatible with
each other. Therefore an Arduino was used to interface the TMS device with the laptop.
3.2.1
TMS-PC Interface
Communication between the TMS device and the processing unit is essential to filter real-time data.
As
explained in previous sections, for this model to work, covariance matrices need to be quickly updated before
and after the tms pulse artifact occurs. An Arduino Elegoo Uno interface was set up
.
This Arduino has two functions: Sending and receiving trigger inputs and trigger outputs to and from the TMS
machine, as shown in figure18. This way, the system becomes fully autonomous and can be adaptive based on
the demands requested by design, like sending a TMS pulse when the lower peak of the alpha wave is detected.
Figure 18: Flow of data through the Arduino.
Whenever a TMS pulse is going to be sent, the trigger output terminal Produces a 20 Volt square wave logic
pulse for 100 µs. This voltage level is above the safety threshold for the Arduino Elegoo Uno. Therefore a
voltage divider to reduce the amplitude of the signal to one-fourth of its original value was used using resistors;
this does not induce delays in communication due to the linear nature of the components, which is essential for
real-time applications like this one.
23
==PAGE:33==
An interrupt at the microcontroller detects the trigger output and sends directly the information that a TMS
pulse has been detected to the PC Interface.
When the PC Interface deems that a TMS pulse has to be sent, for example, when a lower peak of the alpha
wave is detected, it sends a digital signal to the Arduino. When the Arduino detects this order, it creates a 100
µs 5 V CMOS Logic Level Pulse; this signal profile activates the TMS machine and sends a pulse.
3.2.2
Filtering in Real-time
Adding the Arduino Interface and real-time communication with TMSi modified the code. Nevertheless, the
software’s purpose remained the same, with the main additions being regarding the setup of the real-time
communication and plotting of the TMSi-Matlab interface. Another change on this iteration is that instead
of having one script running, two scripts were running simultaneously and synchronized.Figure 30 shows a
flowchart of the real-time filtering process.
The first script is the new Arduino code used to regulate communication, which is constantly checking for the
TMS condition to send the information to the Matlab script. This condition in a finalized system could be the
detection of a negative peak in the alpha waves.
After the TMS condition is met, the system sends two boolean signals, one to the trigger input at the TMS
machine, sending the information that a new pulse is incoming, and another to the Matlab script indicating
that the TMS trigger condition has been met. If there is a manual activation of the TMS device, the trigger
output is also connected to ensure that the TMS pulse is registered. Immediately after sending the boolean
signals, the system goes back to checking for the TMS condition
The other script, in Matlab, is a modification of the post-processing code implemented in the previous section.
The Kalman and DC offset remain the same. The additional setup needed is for the TMSi (setting up the
sampling frequency and the plotter) and the Arduino ( serial communication setup).
After all the setup and variables are initiated, the code starts a real-time plotter constantly being executed.
The order of actions in the code is as follows.
1. Check if any bites are available in the serial communication port, meaning that a TMS pulse is incoming.
2. Obtain a sample from TMSi.
3. DC offset gets removed from the sample
4. The Kalman algorithm predicts the expected value using as information the input data, the mathematical
model (Autoregressive), and the noise matrices that get updated depending on the pulse state
5. With the new Kalman prediction value, the system plots it on the real-time plotter and returns to the
first step.

Discussion
This project has taken the approach of using a modified Kalman filter for reducing the TMS pulse artifact.
This iteration of the Kalman filter allowed to rapidly detect TMS sections by modifying the covariance matrices
when needed, either with a Matlab function offline or with an Arduino Online. This approach helped maintain
the continuity of the recordings and effectively reduced artifacts.
Initially, an offline post-processing Kalman algorithm filtered an EEG signal coming from a physical setup
consisting of an EEG-induced gelatine phantom whose signal was recorded by a carefully selected TMSi amplifier.
This setup allowed for cleaning of the EEG recordings from the TMS artifact with 0.01244 seconds delays from
the actual input signal and with negligible artifacts present in the final filtered signal, which after doing Kalman
Filtering, removed all the high potential ( >10 µ V) artifacts.
In addition to the offline post-processing approach to filtering, a real-time Kalman filter implementation was also
developed. This implementation included adding an Arduino system that coordinated and sent communication
between the TMS machine and the Matlab Interface.
The online implementation successfully filtered the TMS pulse artifact in real-time, reducing its amplitude and
power while keeping the primary frequency component at the desired input frequency value.It must be noted
that the real-time application had a limiting factor in calculating the autoregressive model required for Kalman
filtering, which served as a blocking function. The autoregressive calculation reduced the effective sampling
frequency from 2048 Hz to 128 Hz and therefore caused the loss of samples in the real-time plot. A delay of
0.0081 seconds was introduced in this method of filtering.
An FFT analysis was conducted at both the offline and online applications to analyze the performance of
this Kalman filter in combination with TMSi electrodes. The post-processing algorithm delivers best in all
frequencies with a dB power of -115.67 dB at the input frequency, whereas the real-time filtering algorithm
delivers a similar power at the input, -114.84 dB.
The online method did not include bandpass or notch filters. Therefore, the attenuation at the values where
those are effective for the offline (1 to 30 Hz and 50 Hz) does not diminish those components. On the offline
algorithm, the FIR filters attenuate them. Nevertheless, if needed, these filters could still be applied to the
online algorithm.

5.1
Limitations and Future Works
The project had limitations that should be addressed in future works.
• Gelatine Impedance: Even though the gelatine provided the ideal environment for isolating the TMS arti-
fact from physiological signals other than the signal of interest, this did not come without disadvantages.
The main one is that the pork gelatine behaves as a capacitive load. Its impedance increases as the lower
input frequency is sent into the gelatine. Therefore even though the appropriate frequency for the studies
would be 10 Hz to emulate the frequency of the alpha waves. A 20 Hz input wave was selected as lower
frequencies did not produce satisfactory baseline signals.
• Empirical Test with patient EEG: To further examine the system, test benches with TMS-EEG datasets
from patients should be performed to assess the performance of the code.
• Low-pass offline filter order: Contrary to the High-Pass and low-pass filters used in the offline process,
which were designed as 5th-order filters, a 35th-order low-pass filter was deemed necessary to filter the
high-frequency noise. More fine-tuning or changing to IIR in the filter design could be options to improve
the system.
• Choice of programming language: When implementing any online filtering, the computation of these filters
would slow the processing to the point of losing samples, hence why the real-time plotting is undersampled.
As a programming language, Matlab offers only some optimal features for real-time computing compared
to other programming languages, such as C++ or Java. Matlab runs ten times slower than C++ and four
times slower than Java. [24]. Therefore further optimization in Matlab and a switch to a more efficient
programming language would be necessary for a more optimal implementation of this algorithm. For
instance, an RTOS (real-time operating system) would yield better performance. Embedding the system
in Arduino could provide a significant increase in efficiency.

Conclusion
The objective of this project was to analyze, comprehend, and reduce the artifacts induced by transcranial
magnetic stimulation (TMS) in electroencephalography (EEG) recordings. These artifacts pose challenges in
achieving precise online recording, which is crucial for providing accurate therapy to patients with depression.
The TMS pulse artifact was deemed the most significant when recording EEG data, and the project focused on
filtering it as much as possible.
A setup utilizing a gel phantom was devised to study and isolate the TMS artifact, allowing the recording and
subsequent filtering of the artifact using a Kalman algorithm, bandpass filter, and notch filter. This approach
demonstrated its efficacy in effectively reducing the TMS pulse artifact through offline filtering.
Building upon the success of the offline filtering technique, an online, real-time approach was implemented.
This involved integrating an Arduino into the setup to trigger the TMS device, enabling the delivery of TMS
pulses at controlled intervals. The online setup also demonstrated the capability to filter the artifact effectively.
