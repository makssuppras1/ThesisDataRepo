Abstract 
English 
Microbiological diagnosis of infectious pathogens including the identification of the pathogen in 
diarrhea is time-consuming, involves several laboratory tests, and does not always identify a pathogen 
and often clinical treatment is initiated without diagnosis. 
High-throughput sequencing cost keeps decreasing, and this gives the possibility of using sequencing to 
diagnose infections with metagenomic methods.  
This study utilizes canopy clustering and principal component analysis for diagnostic purposes in 
trying to identify different infections. 58 clinical fecal samples obtained from patient cases with 
diarrhea, along with 10 healthy controls were sequenced on the Illumina MiSeq system.  
When investigating the whole metagenome of samples both as co-abundance gene groups and as single 
gene abundances with PCA and Mann-Whitney U test, only separation of cases and controls were 
evident, with no markers for specific pathogens identified.  
When targeting the investigation by detection of known virulence factors (VFs) in the samples, the 
separation of C. difficile and C. jejuni diagnosed cases from non-infected were accomplished. 
Enterobacteriaceae pathogens (E. coli, S. enterica, Shigella spp., and Y. enterocolitica) had high 
sequence similarity in VFs, which resulted in cross detection, and relatively non-conclusion separation 
of infected from non-infected.

Introduction 
Identification of disease causing organisms  
Disease causing organisms include bacteria, viruses and parasites, which by different mechanisms 
invade and infect a human being. When a person gets symptoms, which can be due to an infection 
(like fever, chills, sweats, cough, shortness of breath or diarrhea) the medical doctor is often 
consulted.  
If the doctor thinks that an infection is possible, it is usual practice to take samples from the organ 
with a suspected infection and get this sample examined by microbiological procedures. These are 
often time consuming and in some cases treatment is initiated before a precise diagnosis is reached.  
There are several infectious diseases where a delay in the correct treatment or treatment with an 
antibiotic not efficient against the infectious organism is a problem. 
One of these diseases is diarrhea, which is one of the leading causes of death worldwide. WHO 
estimated that diarrhea caused 1.39 million deaths in 2015, with the primary burden in low income 
countries [1]. Furthermore diarrhea is the second greatest cause of mortality in children below the 
age of 5, with around 525,000 deaths per year out of 1.7 billion cases each year [2]. 
Due to the importance of diarrhea among the infectious diseases I have decided to investigate other 
diagnostic procedures for the discovery of disease causing organisms in diarrhea. 
Diarrheal diseases 
Diarrhea is usually a symptom of an infection of the gastrointestinal tract, caused by a variety of 
bacterial, viral and parasitic organisms. Most of these are spread through ingestion of contaminated 
food or water, usually due to a lack of sanitation. 
Diarrheagenic pathogens work by different mechanisms, either toxin production (Bacteria: 
Escherichia coli, Staphylococcus aureus, Clostridium difficile, Vibrio cholerae), enteroadherance 
(Parasites: Giardia and Cryptosporidium), and mucosal invasion (Viruses: adenovirus, norovirus, 
rotavirus, sapovirus. Bacteria: Salmonella spp., Shigella spp., E. coli, Parasites: Entamoeba 
histolytica)  
Diagnosis of infectious diarrhea involves several different tests for each microorganism including 
bacterial culturing, polymerase chain reaction (PCR), and microscopy [3]. Furthermore, using 
conventional procedures often only identify a minority of the microbial organisms causing diarrhea 
and up to 80 % of the patient cases are not resolved [4]. Another problem with conventional 
diagnosis is, that some bacterial pathogens are difficult to grow in the laboratories and some cannot 
be cultivated even though they are viable [5]. 
==PAGE:8==
2 
 
In order to get faster and more sensitive diagnostic tests different procedures using genetic material 
from the infectious organisms have been investigated. Among these are PCR based methods for 
the detection of enteropathogens from fecal samples, which are faster and more sensitive than the 
routine procedures [6]–[10]. However, using PCR it is usually only possible to detect the 
organisms that are specifically looked for, which makes this procedures less interesting.  
What is more interesting is the prospect of sequencing of clinical samples for detection and 
diagnosis of infection. Since sequencing detect DNA indiscriminately there is no need for selection 
of specific pathogens to detect.  
Metagenomics 
With decreasing cost of sequencing methods, sequencing of all DNA present in a sample has 
become more and more prevalent. It might therefore soon be more cost effective and faster to 
perform a single sequencing of a fecal sample compared to multiple different microbiological 
procedures.  
This way the total DNA content of the sample is available for further analysis, and make the 
identification of the etiological pathogen a possibility.  
An estimated 99% of all organisms cannot be cultured under lab conditions, which is one of the 
reasons why metagenomics have become popular, since it opens up the possibility to detect new 
unknown organisms and genetic diversity not yet identified [11]. 
Today two different procedures are available for the examination of the genes in sequenced 
samples: Alignment against a known genes/genome or de novo assembly of the sample. 
Alignment against databases of known genes can be used to identify known genes in a sample but 
excludes the chance of identifying new genes and organisms.  
de novo assembly of environmental samples pose a challenge in the sense that there are many 
different organisms in a single sample, some of which might have highly similar sequences. 
However, de novo assembly gives the possibility to detect unknown genes and give a broader view 
of the diversity present in samples. 
Canopy clustering 
New methods have been developed in the field of metagenomics in order to analyze the huge 
amount of DNA present in environmental samples.  
One of these methods is canopy clustering [11]. The procedure is used to bin together genes in 
canopies according to their abundance profiles, called co-abundance gene groups (CAGs). The 
aim of this is to identify groups of genes from the same organism. Through this the organisms 
present in each sample can be identified. 
==PAGE:9==
3 
 
Canopy clustering is an algorithm that works by grouping together genes with similar abundance 
into the same canopy. The algorithm first picks out a gene at random from the gene catalogue, 
called a seed. Genes with a gene abundance profile, which has a Pearson correlation coefficient > 
0.9 to the seed’s abundance profile, are grouped into the same canopy.  
The median abundance profile of all genes belonging to a canopy determines the canopy profile. 
To ensure that the canopy profile is stabilized the canopy is iteratively recaptured until a stable 
median abundance profile is found, and no extra genes are added by iteration. 
Canopy generation by selection of random seeds and clustering is iterated until all genes in the 
gene catalogue belong to a canopy. 
This way canopy clustering could be used in the discovery of disease causing elements in fecal 
samples. 
Another procedure to examine the huge amount of data in sequenced DNA is multivariate 
analysis methods such as principal component analysis (PCA). 
Principal component analysis 
Like canopy clustering PCA could be used in the 
discovery of disease causing elements in fecal 
samples. Principal component analysis (PCA) aim 
to reduce a complex dataset to a simpler one, which 
can be easier to interpret and understand. This is 
accomplished by reducing the dataset with many 
inter-correlated variables to a smaller set of non-
correlated variables called principal components 
(PCs). These PCs collectively explain the overall 
variance of the dataset. 
With a PCA of a dataset with n variables, a 
maximum of n PCs can be computed. The first 
identified principal component (PC1) explains the 
greatest variance in the dataset. PC1 is followed by 
PC2, which explains the next greatest amount of 
variance, and so on until a maximum of n PCs are 
computed.  
A PC is defined as the direction in the dataset, where the data are most spread out, thereby having 
the largest possible variance. The PC2 must be orthogonal to the vector of PC1, and be in the 
direction explaining the greatest variance orthogonal to PC1.

Known disease causing elements among common diarrheagenic 
bacterial pathogens 
In order to interpret the results of the genetic analyses some basic knowledge of the common 
bacterial disease causing elements are needed. 
Clostridium difficile 
C. difficile is prevalent in hospitals, being the leading cause of hospital-acquired diarrhea. The 
bacteria usually colonize the gastrointestinal tract without causing disease under normal conditions 
(pathobionts). 
When a patient colonized with C. difficile receive an antibiotic treatment the balance of the 
commensal bacteria is altered, which C. difficile exploit to cause antibiotic-associated diarrheal 
disease [13], [14]. 
The major virulence factors (VFs) of C. difficile are the two toxins TcdA and TcdB. Recent studies 
have found evidence that TcdB require the presence of TcdA to cause disease [14].

The genes tcdA and tcdB are encoded in a single pathogenicity locus approximately 19.6 kilo bases 
long, with a 1,350 bases between the two genes. Along with tcdA and tcdB this locus contains three 
regulatory open reading frames (ORFs): tcdC, tcdD, and tcdE [13]. 
Campylobacter jejuni  
C. jejuni is among the leading causes of food borne diarrheal disease worldwide [15]. 
The infectious dose of C. jejuni lies somewhere between 500 and 10 000 organism, with an 
incubation period of 1-7 days [16]. 
The exact mechanism by which C. jejuni cause disease is still unknown, but a wide variety of C. 
jejuni VFs involved in motility, chemotaxis, oxidative stress defense, adhesion, invasion, toxin 
production, iron acquisition and temperature stress response have been identified [16].  
The motility of C. jejuni is enabled by flagella, which structure and assembly apparatus is encoded 
by over 40 genes, including, but not limited to: flaA, flaB, flgS/flgR, fliA, rpoN, rpoD, flgP, and 
flgQ. 
To identify in which direction to move for optimal infection C. jejuni utilize chemotaxis, which 
has been found to be mediated by the genes in C. jejuni: cheA, cheW, cheV, cheY, cheR, and cheB. 
C. jejuni has several VFs identified to be involved in adhesion and invasion of mucosal surfaces. 
These include the periplasmic binding protein PEB1, along with the proteins CadF, CapA, CiaB, 
JlpA, and MOMP [15]. 
Cytolethal-distending toxin (CDT) is the only toxin produced by C. jejuni, encoded by cdtA, cdtB, 
and cdtC [15]. 
Escherichia coli 
E. coli is present both as a commensal and pathobiont organism in the microbiota. The pathogenic 
strains of E. coli have developed abilities to cause disease in the gastrointestinal tract, the urinary 
tract, and the nervous system. 
The diarrheagenic E. coli (DEC) strains have been divided into six major categories: diffusely 
adherent E. coli (DAEC), enteroaggregative E. coli (EAEC), enterohemorrhagic E. coli (EHEC), 
enteroinvasive E. coli (EIEC), enteropathogenic E. coli (EPEC), and enterotoxigenic E. coli 
(ETEC). 
Other pathogenic E. coli fall either into the uropathogenic E. coli (UPEC) or neonatal meningitis-
associated E. coli (NMEC) category, depending on whether they are associated with urinary tract 
infections (UTIs) or nervous system infections, respectively [17].  
DEC contain a wide variety of VFs, which can be used to identify the category of DEC, the major 
categories include adherence, toxin, iron uptake, and protease VFs [17], [18]. 
==PAGE:12==
6 
 
Both EHEC and EPEC have the locus of enterocyte effacement (LEE) pathogenicity island (PAI) 
present in their genomes. This PAI facilitate attachment to the epithelial cell membranes of the 
host, by creating a histopathological feature called A/E lesions, where the brush border microvilli 
are destroyed. The LEE genes generally encode the type III secretion system (T3SS) and include 
gene regulators (ler, grlA, grlR) chaperones (cesAB , cesD), secreted translocator proteins (espA, 
espB, espD), secreted effector proteins (espF, espG, espH, map, sepZ, tir), intimin (eae), and the 
structural proteins and machinery of T3SS (escC, escD, escF, escN, escR, escS, escT, sepL, sepQ). 
[19], [20]. 
Salmonella enterica 
S. enterica is, like C. jejuni, a major food borne pathogen. S. enterica, as a facultative intracellular 
pathogen, rely on invasion of host cells for efficient infection [21]. 
Salmonella pathogenicity is primarily encoded in five PAIs, responsible for colonization, 
inflammation, invasion, and macrophage survival. 
Salmonella PAI 1 (SPI-1) and SPI-2 both encode T3SSs. 
SPI-1 is involved in invasion of mucosal cell walls through its T3SS. The system induce epithelial 
cells to phagocytose S. enterica, thereby facilitating infection. SPI-1 also encodes an iron transport 
system and upon infection of epithelial cells, the PAI genes are in addition responsible for inducing 
the production of inflammatory cytokines and chemokines. 
The SPI-1 is a primary target for identifying Salmonella pathogens since this PAI distinguish 
Salmonella from other species groups in the family Enterobacteriaceae, such as Eschericia, 
Shigella, and Yersinia. 
SPI-2’s T3SS is involved in macrophage survival through alterations of macrophages, which have 
ingested S. enterica through phagocytosis. These alterations prevent phagosome-lysosome fusion 
of S. enterica, and the pathogen can thereby survive and hide inside the macrophages phagosomes. 
An additional three PAI are also present in pathogenic S. enterica serovars (SPI-3 to SPI-5). SPI-
3 like SPI-2 facilitate macrophage survival, SPI-4 is responsible for attachment to epithelial cells, 
and SPI-5 is involved in inflammation of the small intestines. 
Along with the SPIs there are a large diversity of genes encoding the outer membrane proteins: the 
O-antigen and flagella helping to mask Salmonella from the memory immune response of the host 
[22].  
Yersinia enterocolitica 
The virulence of all Yersinia species pathogenic in humans (Y. enterocolitica, Y. pestis, and Y. 
psedotuberculosis) is primarily conferred by the 70-kbp Yersinia virulence plasmid (pYV), which 
is non-specific to any of the species. This plasmid confer the ability to evade host immunity and 
==PAGE:13==
7 
 
furthermore enable replication and propagation extracellularly. The genes encoding the T3SS 
machinery of pYV require a millimolar concentration of calcium along with a temperature of 37 
degrees Celsius to become activated [23]. 
At 25 degrees Celsius Y. enterocolitica grows optimally, and have a coccidial, hydrophilic 
morphology with high motility. This is controlled by genes encoding proteins involved with 
flagella, invasin (Inv), and attachment-invasion locus factor (Ail). 
When inside the gastrointestinal tract, body temperature (37 degrees Celsius) enable Y. 
enterocolitica to change morphology to a pleomorphic, non-motile and hydrophobic organism. 
Calcium in the gastrointestinal tract will then activate pYV. This will promote intestinal 
proliferation and invasion of the epithelial cells by Y. enterocolitica. The pYV encode the proteins: 
adhesion factor (YadA), secreted effector proteins (Yops proteins), and V-antigen along with 
others [23], [24]. 
Aim of study 
The aim of this study is twofold. First, to evaluate the potential of canopy clustering and PCA 
based investigation for the identification of disease causing elements from metagenomic samples 
from patients with diarrhea. Second, to investigate if known virulence factors from known 
diarrheagenic pathogens can be linked to specific infection.

Data collection 
This study includes 58 fecal samples obtained from patients with diarrhea. Some of these has 
previously been included in analysis published by K.G. Joensen et al. 2017 [3]⁠.  
All fecal samples were collected for sequencing at two different locations. Samples from healthy 
controls (n = 10) were collected at Køge Hospital, June 2004. Samples from cases diagnosed with 
diarrhea (n = 58) were collected at Hvidovre University Hospital, September – November 2013. 
The samples were sequenzed as paired reads on the Illumina platform.  
Samples from Hvidovre were diagnosed using routine conventional diagnostics, the result of which

All samples From Hvidovre University hospital were investigated for the presence of pathogens

histolytica, Giardia intestinalis, Cryptosporidium spp. and Dientamoeba fragilis. The presence of

Methods  
Virulence factor analysis 
To investigate whether VF analysis can be used to diagnose a diarrheagenic pathogenic infection 
it has to be investigated whether identification and analysis of known VFs present in sequenced 
samples is sufficient to identify a pathogen. 
To identify VFs present in all samples, the sequenced reads are aligned against the Virulence 
Factor Database [25] giving an output of read depth of the identified VFs. See Figure 3 for a 
description of this procedure. 
Information about each VF from VFDB includes gene name, organism, and serovar.  
The VFs from selected specific pathogen groups associated with diarrhea were grouped into 
organism groups for separate analysis by PCA (see Figure 3).  
The resulting coverage data were log transformed using the 𝑙𝑜𝑔(1 +  𝑥) before analysis by PCA. 
The log transformed coverage data for each separate pathogen was examined by PCA using 
prcomp in R [26]. For each analysis the factor loading of PC1 and PC2 were used to plot the 
findings, both with only the samples and with the factor loadings, showing the contribution of each 
VF to the overall variance.

de novo assembly 
The blue diamonds in Figure 4 represent the de novo assembly part of the pipeline. 
First to ensure the quality of the de novo assembly AdapterRemoval [27] was used to remove 
adapters and bad quality bases, with a quality cutoff at quality 2, and the minimum length of reads 
accepted to 30 bases. 
Afterwards, to remove the reads of human origin, BWA-MEM  [28] was used to map reads against 
the human genome, and afterwards remove those identified. 
Each sample’s combined singleton and paired-end read files were then assembled using SPAdes 
[29]. 
Gene catalogue 
The gene catalogue creation in the canopy clustering pipeline are marked as yellow diamonds in 
Figure 4. 
A gene catalogue was made using the prokaryotic gene recognition program metaProdigal [30] for 
gene calling. Genes were renamed with unique gene names, and genes of a length below 300 bp 
were removed. 
Lastly to ensure a non-redundant gene catalogue of representative sequences, CD-HIT [31] was 
used to cluster together genes with ≥ 95% similarity. 
Count matrix 
The count matrix of genes, to be used as input for canopy clustering, was created by the steps 
shown as grey diamonds in Figure 4. 
First the gene catalogue was indexed using Samtools [32] as a preparation for mapping of reads. 
The mapping of the reads against the gene catalogue was then performed with BWA-MEM [28]. 
To count the number of read aligned to each gene, the alignment output was filtered, sorted, 
indexed and counted using Samtools [32].  
The filtering removed reads with an alignment quality below 30, as well as assuring that mapped 
paired end reads were not counted twice. 
Thereafter, to assure comparability across samples the count matrix was normalized in proportion 
to gene counts per sample.

Finally, the genes were clustered according to abundance by calculating Pearson correlation 
coefficient between abundances of genes, and clustering together genes with a Parson correlation 
coefficient > 0.9. 
Gene annotation 
Figure 5 show the overview of the annotation of genes as well as clusters. The initial gene 
annotation is shown as the column on the left. 
SwissProt sequence database [33]  was downloaded September 12th 2017, and afterwards prepared 
for Blast using makeblastdb in the command line. 
The gene catalogue from the canopy clustering pipeline is blasted against the SwissProt database 
using blastx for commandline. A maximum of five target sequences at an e-value ≤ 1e-2. 
For a hit to be accepted by filtering, the conditions of percentage identity ≥ 30% and a length ≥ 50 
amino acids should be met. Among the five possible hits for each query, the accepted hit was that 
with the highest value of length and percentage identity. The query gene was annotated as NA if 
none of the five hits met the conditions or no hits were found.

genes in a CAG should share a common taxonomy, at the lowest possible rank, of the following: 
strain, species, genus, family, order, class, phylum, or superkingdom.

of the CAGs were made, as seen in Figure 5 and Table 2.

In the workflow of annotation the percentage of genes annotated in each CAG were calculated.  
The CAGs with ≥ 50% genes annotated were gathered into one group, and those with < 50% genes 
annotated into another (see Figure 5 right column, upper level in the pyramid). 
Hereafter, the CAGs with < 50% genes annotated were sorted according to whether any genes in 
the CAG were annotated (Group C) or no genes in the CAG were annotated (Group Z). 
Taxonomic information about the genes were fetched by using UniProt’s Retrieve/ID mapping 
tool [34] on the basis of the list of accepted blast hits SwissProt entry names. 
The taxonomic information from UniProt (organism, taxonomic lineage (species), taxonomic 
lineage (genus), taxonomic lineage (family), taxonomic lineage (order), taxonomic lineage (class), 
taxonomic lineage (phylum), taxonomic lineage (superkingdom) being equal to: strain, species, 
genus, family, order, class, phylum, and superkingdom was used to annotate the CAGs with a 
taxonomic rank. 
For a CAG to be annotated with a taxonomic rank, at least 50% of the genes should at the lowest 
possible taxonomic rank have a consensus on the strain, species, genus etc. level. 
CAGs that had a consensus on taxonomy were accepted as annotated CAGs and placed in Group 
A, and annotated with their taxonomy consensus.

Statistical analysis of genes and clusters 
The genes and clusters identified through canopy clustering were investigated through a number 
of different tests (see Figure 6). 
 
 
Figure 6: Overview of workflow for analysis of genes and CAGs from Canopy clustering 
pipeline. 
 
CAG analysis 
The CAGs were first analyzed by calculating the median of normalized gene abundances in each 
CAG for each sample (Figure 6, column B top).  
In column A Figure 6 the CAG median values were log-transformed and used for plotting either 
all CAGs in a PCA, or CAGs of sizes between 1000 and 7000 genes.  
In column B the CAG median values were used to test controls against all cases as well as case 
diagnosis groups with at least five individuals (C. difficile, C. jejuni, E. coli, S. enterica, and 
norovirus) respectively, in a Mann Whitney U test in R [26]. 
Mann-Whitney U Test is a non-parametric statistic test, meaning it makes no assumptions 
regarding the probability distribution of the data, by testing based on ranks of the data instead [12]. 
The statistical tests were Bonferroni corrected, and all CAGs with a p-value ≤ 0.05 discarded.  
==PAGE:22==
16 
 
The resulting abundance matrix were then log transformed with 𝑙𝑜𝑔(1 +  𝑥) and a PCA was run 
on the dataset. 
Gene analysis 
In column C Figure 6, the normalized gene abundances for all genes were tested in the same 
manner as shown for the CAG median values in column B Figure 6. Controls were tested against 
all cases, as well as against diagnosis pathogen groups with at least five samples. The gene tests 
were Bonferroni corrected and sorted by significance of p-value ≤ 0.05. 
In column D the annotated genes were stratified according to the genus of each pathogen group 
identified according to the conventional diagnosis results (see Table 1). 
PCA for each gene genus group was then calculated on the basis of log-transformed normalized 
gene abundances.

Discussion 
Weaknesses in this study include that samples with low quality sequencing were not excluded 
from analysis, also the age and gender of the samples are not considered in the analysis. 
It should also be noted that even though the aim is to also analyze the presence of RNA viruses as 
well as other pathogens the sequencing method used is not optimized to look for these. This is 
because only the DNA of the samples are sequenced, the RNA viruses such as norovirus, 
sapovirus, and rotavirus should not be sequenced and therefore undetectable, while DNA viruses 
such as adenovirus could be sequenced and detected.  
Metagenomic data are complicated to analyze, and especially when looking at all genomic data 
available, as is done here with a de novo assembly. If a useful method to identify the important 
elements are found the potential of identifying new disease causing elements are there.  
Canopy clustering analysis 
In the canopy clustering pipeline (see Figure 6) some steps could potentially be improved or 
different methods investigated. For example metaProdigal [30] is optimized to predict prokaryotic 
and archaeal genes, and therefore the detection of viral and eukaryotic genes may be suboptimal.  
When annotating with SwissProt which only contain genes that curated, the amount of annotated 
genes is lower than what has been identified [33]. 
The impact of these choices in analysis of gene content along with lack of RNA sequenced data, 
can be seen in the annotated gene catalogue only containing 17 genes from the eukaryote G. 
intestinalis genome which is around 11.7 Mb long and contains approximately 5000 genes [40]. 
Also only a single gene from the four virus genera investigated can be found in the annotated gene 
catalogue (See Table 11). 
When creating the non-redundant gene catalogue using CD-HIT before doing canopy clustering 
you ensure that highly similar organisms are clustered together which result in a better gene 
catalogue when reads are realigned against this. However, with diagnosis of diarrhea where 
serotyping is sometimes needed [3] this can pose a problem. 
Canopy clustering is one of the methods developed to bin metagenomics assemblies into clusters, 
with the aim of identifying organisms present in samples without basing this on known reference 
databases. This is done due to the fact that these are biased for already known organisms, and 
thereby prevent detection of unknown organisms. An alternative to using canopy clustering could 
be MetaBAT [41] which bin on contig level, and thereafter create a gene catalogue. The main 
concern with these binning methods are contamination of clusters/bins, and binning on contig level 
could potentially lower the contamination compared to clustering on gene level. As seen in Figure 
26B around 25% of the CAGs with potential for annotation (Group A and B) do not reach a 
==PAGE:75==
69 
 
consensus on any taxonomic level, and in Figure 27 can be seen that the majority of those 
successfully annotated are annotated to superkingdom level (archea, viruses, eukaryotes, and 
prokaryotes). 
Generally seen for the canopy clustering data is that it is possible to separate out the controls from 
the cases, but when trying to detect anything that can be used to identify a pathogen infection both 
Mann-Whitney U test and PCA fails to identify anything. 
This is illustrated in the failure to find significant hits using Mann-Whitney U test both on gene 
and CAG level for anything other than tests of cases against controls, and a number of CAGs when 
testing C. difficile cases against controls (Figure 29, 42, 43). 
When looking at the genus stratified gene groups PCAs (Figure 30-39) there is generally not a 
separation of pathogen specific cases from other cases, although we see a separation of cases and 
controls. 
The exception is the Giardia PCA (Figure 38) where two of the three G. intestinalis cases lie 
slightly separate from the other cases, this might be due to the fact that G. intestinalis is not 
normally present in a healthy human gut microbiome, while other microorganisms from the same 
genus as the pathogens can be [42]. 
These findings coincide with finding from other metagenomics studies of diarrhea patients, where 
the metagenome is changed with a diarrhea infection, and the same general mechanism of change 
are seen regardless of infection, both in children and adults [43]–[45]. 
The general problem when analyzing the human gut microbiome in diarrhea is that there are 
general changes associated with diarrhea, and separating these markers from specific pathogen 
infections markers has not been possible since PCA and Mann-Whitney U test have been incapable 
of this. 
It could have been interesting to have paired samples from the patients after they recuperated from 
disease, thereby the normal gut flora could have been taken out of consideration, and thereby only 
changes analyzed. 
Virulence factor analysis 
When investigating specific known markers for pathogens the separation of specific pathogen 
cases from other cases as well as from controls become clearer. 
In the VF PCA plots (Figure 7-24), the expected result is that the healthy individuals along with 
the cases not infected with the specific organism should cluster together, while those with evidence 
of infection with a specific organism, due to the presence of virulence factors originating from that 
organism, should separate out from this point. 
==PAGE:76==
70 
 
As seen in Table 10 there is generally many cases not conventionally diagnosed with a specific 
pathogen which separate out from the non-infected cluster. What is also to be noted is that it is the 
same samples in the PCAs for the pathogens in the Enterobacteriaceae family (E. coli, S. enterica, 
Shigella spp., and Y. enterocolitica) (Figure 12, 14, 16, 18). This is probably due to high sequence 
similarities between these species VFs, which the output data from Assimpler fail to distinguish. 
Since a lot of VFs in these organisms are from PAIs (see Table 5-8), which are mobile genetic 
elements that can horizontally transfer between species[46], it makes sense that there are high 
sequence similarity between the different species when looking at VFs. A study have found 
evidence of the high-PAI in pathogenic Y. enterocolitica being present in different genera in the 
Enterobacteriaceae family, including Escherichia and Klebsiella species [47]. Generally the 
Enterobacteriaceae family has been found to have high genome plasticity, with horizontal gene 
transfer between species and genera [48]. So it is most likely that a lot of PAIs are shared across 
genera, thereby giving rise to this sequence similarity problem. 
Furthermore to investigate one of the most probable false negatives seen in Figure 24 showing V. 
cholerae VF ctxB which is responsible for separating out four E. coli diagnosed samples. When 
blasting ctxB (UniProt ID: P01556) against the E. coli non-redundant nucleotide database, the E. 
coli enterotoxin eltB (Accession M7874.1) shows a 81% identity, with an e-value 3e-71. This 
support the claim that Assimpler most likely has some false positive VF hits. 
Despite a lot of false positive VF hits the PCAs generally seem capable of identifying the infected 
samples. The PCAs based on C. difficile (Figure 8) and C. jejuni (Figure 9) VFs manage to separate 
out 11 of 16, and all five for C. difficile and C. jejuni respectively. 
In the case of the E. coli VFs PCA (Figure 11) where a lot of VFs that are not involved in 
gastrointestinal infection (Table 5) also impact the clustering of samples a separation of the E. coli 
infected from the non-infected is more or less successful with nine out of 15 samples clustering in 
a separate group. The remaining six E. coli samples could maybe also separate out from all the 
other non-E. coli cases if all UPEC and NMEC VFs were removed from the dataset. 
In regards to VFs factor loadings the tendency is for these to group together and point in a general 
direction. They all contribute a small part of the variance between infected and non-infected. 
Examples of this can be seen in the C. jejuni factor loadings plot (Figure 10) and the E. coli factor 
loadings group pointing downward (Group 1) (Figure 12, Table 5) which are primarily VFs 
responsible for DEC. 
But small groups of VFs can also contribute to effective separation of infected from non-infected 
as seem with C. difficile (Figure 8) where the four VFs contribute equally in different direction to 
separating the infected from the non-infected. 
==PAGE:77==
71 
 
Looking at the non-diagnosed pathogens (P. aeruginosa, S. aureus, and V. cholera) VFs PCAs 
(Figure 19-24) both P. aeruginosa and S. aureus has a majority of their VFs contributing to the 
variance in a single direction, which from the diagnosed PCAs (C. jejuni Figure 10, E. coli Figure 
12) seems to be a good indicator that samples separated in that direction are infected. Therefore it 
is probable that S124 is infected with P. aeruginosa, and S101 and S109 which both have a C. 
difficile diagnosis could also be infected with S. aureus. 
In this study the focus has been on investigation by PCA, other possible investigations could 
include alpha- and beta diversity.  
Conclusion 
On basis of the aim of this study it can be concluded that identifying disease causing elements in 
the microbiome by canopy cluster and analysis by PCA is only possible in determining whether 
the patient is infected or healthy, whether that be based on gene abundances or CAG abundances. 
When investigating if the known disease causing elements (VFs), it is generally possible to 
separate specific pathogen infected samples from the rest of a dataset. C. difficile and C. jejuni are 
especially promising, while VFs from Enterobacteriaceae (E. coli, S. enterica, Shigella spp. and 
Y. enterocolitica) pose the problem of high sequence similarity between VFs that make it difficult 
to distinguish between the specific pathogen infections. 
Future perspectives 
This study gives an overview of how VFs present in fecal samples can be used to identify which 
specific diarrheagenic pathogens are responsible for disease. But when investigating the whole 
microbiome things are less clear. 
To gain a better idea of pathogen specific disease causing elements which PCA along with Mann-
Whitney U test failed to identify, other possible analysis methods could be used. For example 
calculating Bray Curtis dissimilarity (beta-diversity) and thereafter clustering these with a 
principal coordinate analysis (PCoA). This method is used in ecology and could be useful in 
identifying the specific markers of the microbiome environment for different pathogen infections.
