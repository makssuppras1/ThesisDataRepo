Abstract
Sodium­ion batteries (SiBs) are attracting interest as a cost­effective alternative to Li+
ion technology, but proven end­of­life routes remain limited. This thesis develops and
evaluates practical recycling flowsheets for layered­oxide SiB cathodes recovered from
two commercial 12V packs. After safe pretreatment and disassembly at DTI, cathode
active material was delaminated and assessed via Scanning electron microscopy and
electrochemistry, and two indirect metal recovery pathways were implemented: (i) a pyro­
assisted route based on sulfation roasting with (NH4)2SO4 followed by water leaching and
pH­selective precipitation; and (ii) a hydrometallurgical route using H2SO4/H2O2 leaching
with sequential precipitation. One­sided cathodes were fabricated for CR­type half cells
to evaluate the residual electrochemical performance following battery aging.
The practicalities of pack architecture, adhesives, and BMS placement were documented,
as they significantly affected dismantling time and safety. Coin­cell tests indicated kinetic
limitations at high rate after prior cycling stress, and partial recovery at lower rates, high­
lighting the importance of material regeneration for second­life assessments. Recycling
process focused on controlling Iron (Fe) , Nickel (Ni) and Manganese (Mn) separation
through staged precipitation (Fe near pH ≈4; Ni and Mn near pH ≈8.5–9), managing
sodium­salt by­products, and minimizing co­precipitation. Though during hydrometallur­
gical method, a significant amount of sodium sulfate was identified as a byproduct. XRD
analysis of the roasted and precipitated products revealed the presence of sulfate and
carbonate phases, consistent with the selected processing routes, while SEM/EDX con­
firmed Fe­rich solids at low pH and mixed Ni/Mn carbonates at higher pH.
Element recoveries ranged from roughly 60–100%, with potential improvements through
optimized pH control, better carbonate formation management, and effective handling of
sodium by­products. With energy­storage demands increasing, this thesis demonstrates
a practical testbed for scaling sodium­ion battery recycling and integrating indirect re­
covery routes, enabling a faster transition from spent cells to cathode­grade sulfate and
carbonate precursors of transition metals.

Introduction
Energy is one of the most essential commodity in the world right now. There are various
sources from which energy is being generated; it can either be from combustion sources
of energy like coal, oil and natural gas which are non­renewable and have a huge impact
on environment or from renewable sources such as wind, solar, nuclear and hydropower.
These sources have no significant impact on environment. These resources are ulti­
mately used to generate electricity, which is then supplied to households and businesses.
To ensure sustainable advancement, however, alternative, environmentally friendly, and
renewable energy storage technologies are required due to the overuse of non­renewable
fossil fuels and the pollution they cause [1].
When it comes to energy consumption, electricity leads the market. The continuous de­
mand for power supply is increasing more quickly than the total amount of energy con­
sumed [2]. Global energy production by source is projected to reach ≈31,000 TWh in
2025. Fossil energy currently accounts for about 56% of total power generation (coal
33%, natural gas 20%, and oil 3%), with renewable energy sources accounting for 34%
and nuclear energy for 10% (Figure 1.1).

Given that the share of electricity generated from renewable sources is expected to reach

Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) [6] highlights the significance of ESS in tackling
the difficulties of sustainable development.

storage and production of energy from renewable sources. SDG 7 ensures access to

affordable, reliable, sustainable and modern energy for all. This emphasizes the need for
novel energy storage systems to help integrate renewable energy sources into the global
energy system. The need for a transition to more sustainable energy storage technology
is also emphasized by SDG 13, which underlines the need for immediate action to address

The evolution of batteries dates back to 19th century where it started with lead­acid bat­
teries which were commercially used and slowly shifted towards the use of lithium­ion
batteries (LiBs) in 1990’s. During charging and discharging, the lithium ions are the main
charge carriers in LiBs which move between the cathode and anode. LiBs have a high
energy density and a long cycle life because of the reversible flow of lithium ions through
the electrolyte, which facilitates effective energy storage and release. However, the use
of LiBs has increased due to a significant growth in global demand during the previous
few decades. However, the need for alternate battery chemistries has been highlighted by
the increased global demand for energy storage, which has raised worries about the sus­
tainability, affordability, and geopolitical concentration of Li resources [7]. Many studies
have been conducted on various battery technologies, including metal­air and solid­state
batteries, but sodium ion batteries (SiBs) are the most notable. SiBs have become a vi­
able substitute for LiBs, providing a cost effective and environmentally friendly option for
large­scale energy storage applications.
Sodium­ion batteries (SiBs) employ a non­aqueous electrolyte to insert and extract sodium
ions (Na+) between the cathode and anode, using the same electrochemical principles
as lithium­ion batteries. Sodium has a higher ionic radius (1.02 Å) than lithium (0.76 Å),
which affects its electrochemical performance greatly. This variation has an impact on
the intercalation mechanisms and structural stability of electrode materials, requiring the
use of modified or alternative host structures for efficient sodium storage. The cell volt­
age of SiBs is typically found to be lower than that of analogue LiBs. Lithium’s standard
electrode potential (−3.05V vs SHE) is 340mV more negative than sodium’s (−2.71V vs
SHE), which is the most common explanation. The fact that this argument is incorrect
and only tangentially related to the cell voltage of LiBs and SiBs, respectively, must be
recognized [8]. Despite having a lower electrode potential, SiBs have a higher potential
when they are employed with different cathodes and anodes [9]. Furthermore, compared
to lithium, sodium usually has lower specific capacities and ionic conductivity due to its
higher mass and weaker interaction with the host lattice. Together, these elements cause
SiBs to perform and have a lower energy density than LiBs [1].
Despite these limitations, sodium has advantages that make SiBs a viable option in some
applications. Sodium is significantly more abundant and uniformly distributed around the
world than lithium, which promotes resource sustainability and lowers raw material costs
[7]. Furthermore, sodium compounds are compatible with aluminum current collectors on
both the cathode and anode sides, resulting in lower production costs. SiBs are safer,
function at lower temperatures, have faster charging speeds, and integrate into systems
more effectively, even though they have a lower energy density [10] [11] (Figure 1.2).
However, SiB related challenges must be addressed, including volume expansion during
cycling (electrode swelling and mechanical stress), slower diffusion kinetics (sluggish Na+
ion transport within electrode materials), and mediocre cycle life (rapid capacity fading
over repeated charge–discharge cycles). These can be avoided by creating novel elec­
trode materials with open­framework structures, tweaking electrolytes to increase solid

2
Sodium ion Batteries
SiB is classified as either aqueous or non­aqueous. The two describe the type of elec­
trolyte: non­aqueous, in which the electrolyte is an organic solvent, and aqueous, in which
a salt dissolves in water [12]. Although aqueous batteries have some benefits, such being
inexpensive, non­flammable, and thermally stable, they also have serious adverse effects
and are corrosive, which eventually shortens their lifespan. Conversely, non­aqueous
SiBs have benefits such a wider voltage range, less adverse reactions, and compatibility
with most sodium battery chemistries; however, their flammability and greater cost must
be considered while manufacturing these batteries. The non­aqueous SiBs perform quite
similarly to the typical batteries. The current collectors used in this are aluminum (Al)
on both sides, which is different from lithium batteries, which act as an advantage during
manufacturing. The reversible intercalation and de­intercalation of Na+ between the cath­
ode and anode via the electrolyte during charge and discharge cycles is the fundamental
mechanism of SiBs (Figure 2.1). The mechanism of the battery is as follows,
• Charge Cycle: The Na+ ion moves through the electrolyte and intercalates into
the anode structure. This is done using an external power source. Here, the elec­
trons move through the outer circuit from cathode to anode, ensuring charge neu­
trality on both sides. This intercalation process involves the formation of a stable
solid­electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer on the anode surface, which is crucial for
stabilizing the system during subsequent cycles [7].
• Discharge Cycle: The Na+ ion de­intercalates from the anode and moves to the
cathode through the electrolyte. Here, the transition metal undergoes oxidation in
the cathode, which accepts the ion. The electron now moves back from the anode
to the cathode in the outer circuit to produce energy, which can be used for various
applications.

One of the main challenges in SiB charge and discharge cycles is achieving high cycle
stability. These SiBs often experience high structural degradation on the cathode and
anode as well as side reactions in electrode­electrolyte interfaces [7]. To address this
challenge, researchers are developing various cathode and anode materials that incor­
porate strategies such as doping, surface coating, and the use of multiple transition metals
or composite compounds designed to minimize volume expansion during Na+ ion inter­
calation. In addition to this, due to low electrical conductivity in a few of these cathode
materials, conductive carbon is added to enhance their performance, which has an impact
on the reduction of energy density [10].
2.1
Cathodes and Anodes
The key performance parameters which are influenced by the choice of good cathode ma­
terial include energy density, high cycling capability and overall performance. Moreover,
intercalation properties and Na+ ion storage capacity are important factors to take into
account. In order to minimize structural stress and capacity fade, volume expansion dur­
ing charge­discharge cycles should be minimized. The four primary cathode types under
investigation are layered oxides, Prussian blue analogues, polyanionic compounds, and
organic material [13].
Figure 2.2: Mind map of electrode and electrolyte types for sodium­ion batteries (SiBs),
reprinted with modifications from [13].
Each type offers distinct advantages, such as high capacity, stability, or eco­friendliness,
making them suitable for various applications. Layered oxides (NaXTMO2) offer high
theoretical capacity, structural flexibility, and electrochemical tunability. These materials
offer the advantage of reversible sodium­ion intercalation and de­intercalation within their
layered structures, enabling specific capacities ranging from 120­150 mAh/g [7]. The tran­
sitional metals used here offer performance tailoring, and doping these (e.g., with Mg or
Al) gives higher cycling efficiency. These are also cost­effective, as the abundance of
the transition metals (Mn, Fe) makes the most desirable cathodes. Some of these ma­
terials have certain disadvantages like high degradation at higher voltages, Jahn­Teller
distortions associated with Mn3+ ions can lead to lattice instability, affecting cycle life in
compounds like NaMnO2 [14].

Polyanionic compounds have also been an interesting cathode material as these also
offer electrochemical tunability, but have exceptional structural stability and high voltage
operation. The stability of this structure is mainly from phosphates, sulfates, and fluo­
rophosphates, which provide a stable lattice for Na+ ion intercalation. Due to the strong
covalent bonds, these materials have lower capacity fading during cycling. The most
widely studied compound is sodium iron phosphate, which exhibits high thermal stabil­
ity and delivers a theoretical capacity of ≈150 mAh/g, making it a good choice for the
SiB cathode. Despite their advantages, polyanionic compounds face challenges such as
low intrinsic electronic conductivity and complex synthesis processes. Addressing these
limitations requires innovative approaches, including nano­structuring, doping, and com­
posite formation with conductive materials. [7].
Prussian Blue Analogues (PBAs) have significant benefits for sodium­ion batteries, in­
cluding high theoretical capacity (≈170mAh/g), quick Na+ diffusion, low volume change
during cycle, and inexpensive cost. Their superior structural stability enables a long cycle
life and good coulombic efficiency. However, PBAs encounter obstacles such as wa­
ter incorporation, Na vacancies, and coordination defects, which could negatively im­
pact their performance [15]. While organic materials have high theoretical capacities
(≈250mAh/g), sustainability and have tunable molecular structures [7]. However, chal­
lenges remain, including dissolution in electrolytes, low electronic conductivity, and nar­
row voltage windows. Strategies such as polymerization, molecular functionalization, and
covalent frameworks help address these limitations, enhancing long­term cycling stability
and performance [7].
Anode materials, on the other hand, play a crucial role in battery’s energy density,
charge/discharge efficiency and cycling stability. Unlike cathodes, these materials have
to be stable when Na+ ion intercalates and de­intercalate during the cycles; these are
more prone to expansion, so they have to maintain structural integrity. There are mainly
5 different types of anodes: carbon­based compounds, alloy­based compounds, MXenes,
,metal oxides and organic anodes (Figure 2.2). The main problem for these compounds
is the volume expansion of these materials during cycling. To overcome these, advanced
material technology such as doping, nanostructuring or surface coatings mechanisms is
used, but this makes these anodes complex in nature and difficult to produce.
Carbon­based compounds don’t have major volume expansions. Their abundance, af­
fordability, and adjustable characteristics make carbon­based materials among the most
studied and used anodes for SiBs. Hard carbon’s structure, which includes both graphitic
layers and micropores, enables two methods for Na­ion storage: intercalation within graphitic
layers and adsorption within micropores. This combination allows hard carbon to provide
high specific capacities, in the range of 300­400 mAh/g, with great cycling stability [7].

2.2.1
Layered Metal Oxides
The transition metal oxide cathode materials have a very unique layered like structure
which makes these promising cathodes. These are simple to synthesis and have similar­
ities with LiTM02 cathode materials in Li­based batteries. To increase the performance of
these cathode materials, multiple layers/stacks are being examined to achieve high ionic
radius and producing octahedral or prismatic sites for ”Na+ ion” intercalation [16]. O3,
P2, and P3 are the most common configurations for transition metal oxides (TMO2) (Fig­
ure 2.3), where the numerals denote the number of TMO2 in the cyclic cell unit [13] [16]
[17]. Since the cathode is still a significant difficulty, the current review concentrates on
three distinct layered oxide cathode materials for SiBs. These were selected to illustrate
the benefits of a novel cathode material, its ease of production, and the importance of
TMO coordination.
Figure 2.3: Schematic illustration of the different coordination environments of transition
metal oxides (TMOs), reprinted from [16].
Sodium Manganese Oxide:
Sodium manganese oxide (NaMnO2) is a good cathode
material for sodium­ion batteries due to the abundance and low cost of manganese,
coupled with the high theoretical capacity for the layered oxides. The layered structure
offers superior electrochemical performance compared to the tunnel structure, primar­
ily because it allows efficient Na+ intercalation and deintercalation between well­defined
layers [7]. However, the material’s cycling stability is hindered by the Jahn–Teller dis­
tortion associated with Mn3+ ions, which causes structural degradation during repeated
charge/discharge cycles [7]. To mitigate this, recent studies have explored various sub­
stitution strategies. For instance, the substitution of Fe and Ni into Na0.67MnO2 to form
Na0.67Mn0.65Fe0.2Ni0.15O2 (NaMFN) has demonstrated enhanced structural integrity and
electrochemical stability, achieving a high reversible capacity of 208 mAh/g and a capacity
retention of 71% over 50 cycles. This performance is attributed to the suppression of Jahn
Teller distortion by increasing the average Mn valence and to the superior electrochemical
reversibility of Ni compared to Fe, which reduces polarization and structural strain [16].
Additionally, fluorine and lithium co­doping, as in Na0.67Li0.1Fe0.4Mn0.5O2, has further im­
proved rate performance and structural robustness by stabilising the crystal lattice and
mitigating the formation of detrimental phases [16]. Such modifications significantly en­
hance the practical viability of NaMnO2­based layered oxides for long­cycle and high­rate
SiB applications.

Structural Modification of Cathode:
The most common technique for improving the
characteristics of cathode material is to alter the structure of the layered oxide. This is
done to solve the challenges of energy density and low discharge voltage [18]. Coating
layered oxide cathode materials leads to increase in the material’s electronic and ionic
conductivity while shielding the active component from the effects of moisture and atmo­
spheric CO2, and preventing the growth of insulating materials like NaOH and Na2CO3
on the material’s surface [19] [20]. A particular coating of conductive sodium phosphate
coating is advantageous on Na(Mn0.3Fe0.4Ni0.3)O2 (NFM) material. Phosphate coatings
are attractive due to their high abundance and low cost, making them ideal for large­scale,
low cost grid storage applications. Furthermore, harmful residual sodium hydroxide and
carbonate species present on the material’s surface can theoretically react to form sodium
phosphate, enhancing surface stability. Finally, the application of lithium or sodium phos­
phate coatings onto various layered oxide materials is straightforward and scalable, fur­
ther supporting their practical use [16].
Multiphase Metal Oxide System:
Multiphase metal oxides have gained increasing at­
tention as advanced cathode materials due to their potential to overcome the limitations
of single­phase layered oxides, such as limited structural stability and poor cycling per­
formance. By doping multiple metal cations into the layered NaxTNO2 framework, it is
possible to induce the formation of mixed phases (e.g., O3, P3, P2) that interact syner­
gistically to enhance electrochemical performance. A particularly notable example is the
Ti­substituted compound O3 ­ NaNi1/4Co1/4Fe1/4Mn1/8Ti1/8O2, which showed enhanced
cycling stability with 91% capacity retention at 4C after 300 cycles, due to the complete
utilization of the Ni2+/Ni3+, Fe3+/Fe4+, and Co3+/Co4+ redox couples during charge/dis­
charge cycles [21]. It is found that intragranular cracks could be effectively suppressed
through the migration and segregation of dopant Mg in the gain bulk at a high cut­off
working voltage of 4.5 V during cycling [16].
2.2.2
Prussian Blue Analogues
Prussian Blue (PB) was originally developed as a blue pigment in the art industry and
exists in two soluble forms: K(Fe2+[Fe2+(CN)6]) and Fe3+[Fe2+(CN)6]. A unique prop­
erty of PB is its distinct color change when the oxidation state of iron shifts from Fe2+ to
Fe3+. Beyond its visual appeal, PB features distinctive structural characteristics, high Na+
storage capacity, and low cost synthesis [7]. Prussian Blue Analogues (PBAs), derived
from PB, form an important class of sodium­ion battery cathode materials. Their open
framework, large interstitial gaps, and strong covalent bonding between transition metals
and cyanide groups provide excellent Na+ diffusion pathways and outstanding structural
stability during cycling [7]. PBAs are also environmentally friendly and cost effective for
large scale energy storage. Among them, sodium iron hexacyanoferrate (NaFe[Fe(CN)6])
is widely studied for its high theoretical capacity (≈170 mAh/g), excellent cycling stability,
and low voltage hysteresis [15]. However, PBAs can suffer from structural degradation
due to water loss during cycling, Na deficiency, and coordination vacancies. Recent ad­
vances such as atomic­level doping and encapsulation within protective matrices have
significantly mitigated these issues [7].
2.2.3

Since the pioneering work of Stevens and Dahn in 2000, hard carbon has emerged as
the cornerstone anode material for sodium­ion batteries, offering a unique combination of
structural disorder and tunable porosity ideal for accommodating sodium ions [22]. Unlike
graphite in lithium cells, which can intercalate lithium, but it can’t intercalate Na+ in their
structure due to their large size. Hard carbon is the choice of anode material as it has a

carbon made from biomass precursors performed steadily throughout 1000 cycles and
had a reversible capacity of 320 mAh/g [23]. Soft carbon, or graphitizable carbon, has a
more structured structure than hard carbon, with layered graphene layers that allow for
Na+ ion intercalation. However, due to restricted intercalation sites, its specific capacity is
typically about 150­250 mAh/g [7]. Soft carbon is normally used alongside other materials
to enhance its performance.

Recent studies have compared various hard carbon (HC) precursors, mainly polymer­
based, biomass­based, and soft carbon­based, in terms of electrochemical performance
and cost. Polymer based HCs deliver the best performance due to their highly cross­
linked structure, which, after carbonization, results in wide interlayer spacing and abun­
dant closed pores, enhancing sodium storage, especially in the plateau region. However,
their high precursor cost limits their commercial appeal. Biomass­derived HCs offer a bal­
ance between performance and cost, thanks to their oxygen­rich structure and favorable
pore characteristics, though they suffer from low carbon yield and high ash content, affect­
ing consistency and scalability. In contrast, soft carbon­based HCs are the cheapest but
show the weakest performance, as they tend to graphitize during carbonization, reducing
pore volume and interlayer spacing. Improving cross­linking to avoid graphitization is cru­
cial for these materials. Commercial coal­derived HC is even cheaper but lacks capacity
and stability. The three types of HC have their advantages and disadvantages; a better
understanding of the three precursors can guide the preparation of different application
scenarios of sodium ion battery anode materials [23].

Recycling Techniques
To explore effective recycling strategies for sodium­ion batteries, this section begins by
examining established techniques used for lithium­ion systems, particularly lithium iron
phosphate and nickel manganese cobalt (NMC) chemistries. By analyzing both direct
and indirect recycling methods applied to these materials, key principles can be identified.
These insights will serve as a foundation for understanding how such approaches could
be adapted for sodium­ion batteries. In a later section (chapter 4), these findings will be
used to formulate a recycling hypothesis tailored to sodium­ion chemistries.
3.1
Pretreatment
Spent batteries must go through a number of pretreatment procedures to guarantee safety,
improve material recovery efficiency, and get the components ready for further process­
ing before using direct or indirect recycling techniques. Generally, pretreatment includes
three primary steps: discharge, disassembly, and material separation [35] (Figure 3.1).
In order to overcome issues like residual charge, thermal instability, and the intricate
multi­layered structure of battery cells, pretreatment is crucial. In addition to standard­
ized procedures, there are also alternative or supplementary methods in practice. One
such approach is discharging the SiBs electrically to 0 V using controlled circuitry. This
method ensures a uniform and complete depletion of residual charge, especially beneficial
for modules or packs that are still partially functional. Furthermore, in certain laboratory
and pilot­scale studies, manual disassembly of cells is conducted after safe discharge.
This involves physically opening the cell casing, allowing for the direct collection of inter­
nal components, including electrodes, separators, and electrolyte residues. While labor­
intensive, this method facilitates precise sampling and analysis and is particularly valuable
when investigating recycling techniques [36].

A key goal during materials separation is to recover active materials from the cathode and
anode. These active materials are typically coated on current collectors using binders like
PVDF, making their delamination and separation essential. Methods such as solvent­

fixed, several technical and environmental challenges remain. These include the genera­
tion of secondary pollutants such as toxic gases, waste liquids, and residues, which may
result in serious environmental burdens. Particularly, electrolyte treatment and recovery
needs more attention due to toxicity and resource value [36].
3.2
Recycling categories
Battery recycling methods can broadly be categorized into direct and indirect approaches,
depending on how the active materials are treated and recovered (Figure 3.2). Direct re­
cycling is a battery recycling method that involves collecting and sorting waste batteries,
physically processing them, recovering valuable materials, and disposing of any remain­
ing residues. This approach enables the extraction of valuable metals and other reusable
components without fully disassembling the battery, allowing direct access to the active
materials used within itself [38]. Although still in developmental stages, direct recycling
has gained attention for its lower environmental impact and energy consumption, as well
as its potential to preserve material value. The purification of active materials involves
repairing bulk defects by hydrothermal, solid­phase, electrochemical, and chemical re­
lithiation [39].

In contrast, indirect recycling involves decomposing battery materials into their elemen­
tal or ionic forms before reprocessing them into new battery­grade compounds or the sec­
ondary utilization or re­treatment of waste battery before valuable materials are derived
from them [38]. This category encompasses pyrometallurgical and hydrometallurgical

methods. Pyrometallurgy employs high­temperature smelting to recover valuable metals
such as nickel, manganese, cobalt, iron and copper. However, this method often results
in the loss of lithium and organic materials, along with high energy demands and signif­

leaching using acidic or alkaline solutions to dissolve metals, followed by solvent extrac­

achieves higher recovery efficiencies and metal purities but may generate wastewater and

3.3
Indirect Recycling
Indirect recycling describes well­established and frequently used ways of extracting valu­
able substances from used battery materials by chemically or thermally breaking them
down into simpler compounds. Unlike direct recycling, these methods require more rigor­
ous processing but provide greater compatibility with a variety of battery chemistries and
levels of degradation. Today’s industrial­scale battery recycling is based on two major
indirect strategies: pyrometallurgy and hydrometallurgy. Each approach has particular
advantages in terms of scalability and metal recovery efficiency, but there are significant
environmental and economic trade­offs. This section delves further into both approaches,
as well as developing techniques like bioleaching, which aim to solve sustainability con­
cerns. These indirect approaches also consist of the core techniques that will later be
used to define the recycling hypothesis proposed for sodium­ion batteries.
A number of companies focus on recycling batteries through the use of pyrometallurgy or
hydrometallurgy methods. In Europe, the main focus of Fortum (Finland), BASF (Ger­
many), Altilium (UK), Orano (France), Primobius (Germany/Australia), and SK tes (Sin­
gapore) is on hydrometallurgical processes that extract valuable metals such as lithium,
cobalt, nickel, and manganese through chemical leaching and purification.
However,
businesses like Aurubis (Belgium/Germany) and Umicore (Belgium) combine hydromet­
allurgical and pyrometallurgical processes, beginning with high­temperature smelting to
decompose battery materials and then using chemical treatments to purify and recover
high­quality metals. This combination addresses material purity and environmental con­
cerns in battery recycling while enabling effective metal recovery.
3.3.1
Pyrometallurgy
Pyrometallurgical processes work at high temperatures and efficiently handle compli­
cated, heterogeneous battery waste streams; they have been extensively studied for the
recycling of spent batteries [37][39][43]. Usually, these procedures use heat treatment to
eliminate organic materials, create slag­metal separations, and make it easier to extract
important metals like nickel, manganese and other transition metals (Figure 3.2). The pre­
cise coordination of phase changes and reactions to improve metal yields and purify target
phases for ensuing hydrometallurgical or direct regeneration processes is an important
issue [44] [45]. In order to separate metallic components, drive out organic binders, and
change cathode materials into more reducible phases, pyrometallurgical recycling usually
begins with pretreatment of black mass or whole cells that have been mechanically disas­
sembled. The principal techniques employed in this process include reduction smelting,

15
==PAGE:24==
reduction roasting, and salt­assisted roasting [41] [43].
Reduction Smelting:
Reduction smelting is a conventional pyrometallurgical process
that recovers transition metals from spent batteries by converting them into molten metal
alloys using fluxes (e.g., CaO, SiO2) and reducing agents (e.g., coke, Al) at temperatures
> 1000 °C [46]. It achieves excellent recovery rates for Ni, Co, and Cu (>99%) but suffers
from poor Li retention due to its high volatility at elevated temperatures [47]. Despite its
industrial scalability and simplicity, reduction smelting is inefficient for Li and Mn recovery
and leads to resource losses unless complemented by post­leaching [43] [47].
Reduction Roasting:
Reduction roasting is an advanced alternative to smelting that en­
ables lithium­first extraction at relatively low temperatures (≈600°C), minimizing Li losses
[43]. By using external reducing agents or graphite from anodes, it converts Li into water­
soluble Li2CO3, while transition metals remain as low­valent oxides or metallic forms [43].
This facilitates efficient separation through water leaching, achieving Li recovery rates up
to 85% [48]. The process is more energy efficient and economically favorable during high
lithium price periods, offering considerable industrial promise.
Salt­Assisted Roasting:
Salt­assisted roasting enhances transition metal recovery by
introducing exogenous salts (such as NaHSO4 and NH4Cl) that react with metal oxides
to form soluble or volatile metal salts at relatively low temperatures (200–600 °C) [43].
This process includes sulfation, chlorination, and nitrification roasting, each optimized for
specific cathode chemistries. Sulfation roasting with NaHSO4 has demonstrated high ef­
ficiency in converting Ni, Co, and Mn into water­soluble sulfates, enabling effective recov­
ery without requiring high­temperature treatments. Chlorination roasting using NH4Cl or
CaCl2 promotes the volatilization or solubilization of transition metals, making separation
more straightforward. Nitrification can also aid in selective oxidation and recovery of met­
als, especially in mixed­metal cathodes. Despite the advantages, managing emissions
such as SOx and NOx remains a challenge. Nevertheless, salt­assisted roasting offers
a promising low­energy pathway for selectively extracting valuable transition metals from
spent battery cathodes.
3.3.2
Hydrometallurgy
With its high recovery rates, low energy consumption, and suitability for room tempera­
ture and atmospheric pressure operations, hydrometallurgy is the most popular and es­
tablished technique for recovering valuable metals from spent batteries, particularly in
China, where it dominates industrial practices [43]. Acidic or alkaline solvents are used
to leach the pretreated black mass, and then methods such as solvent extraction, chemi­
cal precipitation, and selective adsorption are used for separation and purification [37]. It
is the most widely used recycling technique worldwide due to its economic viability and
industrial scalability. Nonetheless, there are still issues with waste management, com­
plicated processes, and possible environmental contamination from the use of acids and
alkalies. With different reaction routes and metal selectivity, hydrometallurgy is primarily
divided into two categories: ammonia leaching and acid leaching (Figure 3.2) [37] [43]
.
Ammonia Leaching:
Ammonia leaching is a selective method for recovering transi­
tion metals like Ni, Co from spent batteries based on their ability to form stable ammonia
complexes. This process minimizes the dissolution of impurity metals like Mn, Al, and
Recycling of Sodium ion Battery Materials
16
==PAGE:25==
Fe, simplifying downstream purification. Ammonia is typically used with ammonium bi­
carbonate as a buffer and hydrogen peroxide as a reducing agent [43]. Under optimal
conditions, Ni and Co can be leached at >96%, while Al and Mn remain largely undis­
solved [49]. Earlier systems using sulfites were effective but created sulfate­containing
wastewater, increasing treatment costs. The improved H2O2­based system avoids this is­
sue and improves selectivity [49]. Another advantage is that ammonia can be evaporated
and reused, lowering environmental impact. However, separating the leached metals
remains a key technical barrier to widespread use. Ammonia leaching is not typically
used as a standalone process but holds great potential in hybrid recycling flows due to its
targeted extraction capabilities. With further optimization, it may serve as a sustainable
alternative in LiB metal recovery.
Acid Leaching:
Acid leaching is a widely used hydrometallurgical process for recover­
ing metals from spent batteries. Inorganic acids like sulfuric acid (H2SO4) and hydrochlo­
ric acid (HCl) are most commonly used due to their high efficiency [43]. The H2SO4 +
H2O2 system can achieve leaching rates above 98.5% for Ni, Co, Mn, and Li in NCM
cathodes [50]. HCl performs even better in terms of leaching efficiency but generates
harmful chlorine gas, raising environmental concerns [50] . Nitric acid, while a strong oxi­
dizer, can volatilize easily and form toxic NOx gases, reducing its industrial feasibility [43].
Organic acids such as citric, oxalic, and malic acids are more environmentally friendly and
biodegradable [41] [43] . Citric acid achieves up to 99% leaching of Li and Co, and has
been shown to selectively remove Al from LFP cathodes [51]. However, organic acids are
costly, slower, and less suitable for impure battery waste streams, limiting their scalability
[37] [41] [43] .
3.3.3
Bioleaching
Bioleaching is a type of indirect recycling technique which uses microorganisms as a
leaching agent to extract metals (Figure 3.3). This follows three main steps: redoxolysis,
acidolysis, and complexolysis, during the metal dissolution process. The kinetics of the
bioleaching process relies on a) how the bacteria promote redox reaction, b) bacteria­
produced metabolites that complex with metals, c) binding ability to metal substrates [42]
[52]. Direct bioleaching is where these microorganisms directly combine with the metal
surface and oxidize it, while indirect bioleaching produces oxidants which chemically
attack the ore. Compared to conventional metal extraction techniques, bioleaching has
a number of benefits. In comparison to pyrometallurgy, it produces far fewer harmful
emissions, making it more environmentally benign. Low­grade ores and different types
of electronic waste can be treated with this energy­efficient method. Furthermore, a va­
riety of precious metals, such as copper (Cu), zinc (Zn), nickel (Ni), cobalt (Co), lithium
(Li), and manganese (Mn), can be recovered efficiently through bioleaching. The method
does have certain drawbacks, though. It typically takes longer than traditional extraction
techniques and is extremely sensitive to environmental factors like temperature, pH, oxy­
gen content, and the presence of harmful compounds. Furthermore, to maintain ideal
microbial activity, successful bioleaching frequently calls for either meticulously regulated
bioreactor systems or heap leaching configurations. The two main microorganisms used
in the industry are Chemolithotrophic Autotrophic bacteria and heterotrophic bacte­
ria and fungi. Chemolithotrophic autotrophic bacteria bioleach metals from spent LiBs by
producing ferric ions and sulfuric acid, enabling metal dissolution through direct contact
or indirect redox reactions. Heterotrophic bacteria and fungi aid bioleaching by secreting
organic acids, which dissolve metals via acidolysis and complexolysis. Fungi like A. niger
and P. simplicissimum are especially effective in forming metal­organic complexes.

3.4
Direct Recycling
The goal of direct recycling is to keep battery components, particularly the cathode, func­
tionally pristine without disassembling them into their component elements (Figure 3.2).
Through physical separation and chemical recovery, this method recovers, fixes, and re­
purposes active materials, providing major benefits in terms of lower energy use and envi­
ronmental impact. Direct recycling is especially well­suited for high purity recovery since it
preserves the content and structure of materials, in contrast to indirect approaches. While
still in the early stages of development, a number of methods, including electrochemi­
cal re­lithiation, hydrothermal treatment, and solid phase sintering, have demonstrated
promise in repairing damaged cathodes.
3.4.1
Hydrothermal Treatment
The hydrothermal method is a wet­chemical technique widely employed for the regenera­
tion of spent battery cathodes due to its ability to operate under relatively low temperatures
and controlled pressures. This method typically involves treating the cathode material in
a sealed autoclave with solutions containing lithium sources such as LiOH or Li2SO4, and
sometimes reductive agents like ascorbic acid or DMSO to aid in structural restoration
and Li replenishment [40]. Hydrothermal treatment allows the simultaneous removal of
surface impurities and relithiation, restoring both the crystal structure and electrochemi­
Recycling of Sodium ion Battery Materials
18
==PAGE:27==
cal performance of materials such as LFP and NCM. Studies show that hydro­thermally
treated cathodes can exhibit near­pristine capacity retention and improved cycling stability
[43]. Additionally, this technique can be tailored by adjusting pH, temperature, and addi­
tives to enhance specific phase recovery, as demonstrated with LiBr­assisted solvother­
mal routes for NCM materials [40]. Overall, it offers a scalable, cost­effective alternative
to high­temperature recycling methods.
3.4.2
Solid Phase Sintering
Solid­phase sintering is the most conventional method for the direct regeneration of spent
cathode materials from batteries. In this process, active materials are first separated from
the current collector, then mixed with a lithium source such as LiOH or Li2CO3, and finally
subjected to high­temperature calcination (typically 700–800°C) to restore lithium content
and repair the crystal structure [54]. This method is advantageous due to its low cost,
simple operation, minimal pollution, and avoidance of acid/alkali leaching [43]. However,
it requires accurate control of lithium stoichiometry and is less effective if the cathode
structure is heavily degraded [54]. Despite these challenges, solid­phase sintering is
widely applied for commercial cathodes such as LCO, NCM, and LFP [43].
3.4.3
Electrochemical and Chemical Re­Lithiation
Electrochemical and chemical lithiation are promising direct regeneration strategies for
lithium­ion battery cathodes due to their low energy consumption and operational simplic­
ity [55] [56]. Unlike thermal methods, electrochemical lithiation enables precise lithium
reinsertion by applying a potential to a cathode electrode in a mild aqueous electrolyte,
restoring performance efficiently without high­temperature treatment [54]. Chemical lithi­
ation, on the other hand, involves spontaneous lithium uptake using Li­containing agents
like LiI or redox mediators such as DTBQ or pyrene, offering fast regeneration without re­
moving binders or additives [57] [58]. Both methods have successfully regenerated LFP,
LCO, and LMO cathodes with restored capacities close to commercial levels [54]. In situ
regeneration is made possible by electrochemical configurations, such as functional sep­
arators or pre­lithiated graphite anodes, which do away with the need for material sep­
aration processes. These techniques, which are frequently needed for post­annealing,
are unable to reverse irreversible phase shifts in multilayer oxides. Large­scale industrial
adoption is now hampered by the toxicity of chemical agents and the limited throughput
of electrochemical devices. With all factors considered, these techniques may offer scal­
able, low­impact direct cathode recycling solutions with additional optimization [54] [55].

This section explains the complete experimental methodology used to examine SiB recy­

step in the investigation is choosing an appropriate SiB chemistry, which is determined
by the material’s availability, commercial importance and TRL. After that, a structured

objective of this section is to have a comprehensive understanding of battery behavior

4.4.1
Binder Identification using Raman Spectroscopy
”Raman spectroscopy is a technique where the sample is illuminated with a monochro­
matic laser beam which interacts with the molecules of sample and originates a scattered
light. The scattered light having a frequency different from that of incident light (inelastic
scattering) is used to construct a Raman spectrum” [67]. Raman spectroscopy is a power­
ful technique for analyzing battery electrode components due to its sensitivity to molecular
vibrations and local chemical structure. It is particularly well suited for the identification
of materials such as binders, conductive additives, and active electrode phases. For the
cathode analysis of the sodium­ion battery (SiB), a 532 nm green laser was used as the
excitation source. The wavenumber range was set from 100 to 2000 cm−1. This range
allows for the detection of layered oxide structures, which typically exhibit characteristic
peaks between 100 and 1100 cm−1, as well as the identification of carbon additives and
potential impurities, which may appear at higher wavenumbers up to 2000 cm−1 [68].
4.4.2
Delamination of Cathode electrode
Once the binder type in the cathode material is identified, the next step is the delamina­
tion of the cathode to extract the cathode active material (CAM). Commercially, two cat­
egories of binders are commonly used: they are water­soluble (Sodium Carboxymethyl
Cellulose, Sodium Alginate, Polyacrylic acid Sodium Salt) and water­insoluble (Polyvinyli­
dene fluoride (PVDF)). If the cathode contains a water­soluble binder, delamination can
be performed by simply immersing the cathode sheets in deionized (DI) water, allow­
ing the CAM to separate naturally from the current collector [69][70]. Surfactant­assisted
delamination is necessary for cathodes with water­insoluble binders, such as PVDF, to
release CAM without damaging the aluminum foil [71]. An alternate and more natural
way is to immerse the cathode in hot DI water, which promotes delamination via swelling
and interfacial weakness. Water penetrates the contact between the PVDF layer and the
aluminum substrate, weakening adhesion and facilitating CAM separation [72].

After completing the pretreatment steps and coin cell fabrication as a second­life assess­

looked into since these are commonly applied for lithium­ion batteries with chemistries
such as NMC and LFP. This section will outline the experimental setup and methodology

4.7
Characterization of Recovered Materials
Following the recycling experiments, a comprehensive characterization was performed to
determine the yield and composition of the components contained in the filter cakes at dif­
ferent stages. These investigations were critical for understanding the structure and mor­
phology of the materials following pyrometallurgical and hydrometallurgical treatments.
A combined approach of Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM), Energy Dispersive X­ray
Spectroscopy (EDX), and X­ray Diffraction (XRD) methods are used to understand the
outcome of recycling technologies as well as the quality of recovered materials.
4.7.1
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
SEM operates by focusing a focused beam of electrons onto the sample surface [75].
The interactions between incident electrons and atoms in the material produce a variety
of signals, including secondary electrons, which are employed to create high­resolution
pictures of the surface topography and surface morphology of the material [76]. A Zeiss
SEM instrument was utilized for imaging, and the samples were studied in powder form
without any conductive coating. To optimize image quality, SEM was run at both low and
high electron voltages (eV). Low­eV imaging reduced charging effects and increased sur­
face detail, but high­eV imaging improved penetration depth and material contrast. Low
magnification was first used to record the particles’ overall morphology and aggregation
behavior, while high magnification was used to study finer surface textures and internal mi­
crostructural features. A silicon wafer was used as a substrate to facilitate structural anal­
ysis and elemental distribution of individual particles of the compound. Back­scattered
electrons imaging at 20 kV was used to identify high­density particles. The SEM tests
were carried out at DTI.
4.7.2
Energy Dispersive X­ray Spectroscopy (EDX)
EDX operates on the fact that when a high­energy electron beam hits a sample, it can
eject inner­shell electrons from the atoms. As outer­shell electrons fall into these vacan­
cies, they emit X­rays with element­specific energies. X­rays are detected and processed
to determine the material’s elemental composition [77]. EDX analysis was used together
with SEM to determine the elemental composition of the recovered components. Low ac­
celerating voltages were used to enable quick scans at higher magnifications and facilitate
rapid detection of major elements. Additionally, higher accelerating voltages(20 kV) were
employed to achieve more precise and quantitative elemental composition data over the
sample area. These yielded average weight percentages (wt.%) of the elements, which
will be used to evaluate the recycling process’s elemental yield. Back­scattered electrons
were used at 20 kV, and an elemental EDX map was acquired to determine the elemental
composition at high­density particles. The EDX tests were carried out at DTI.

Results and Discussion
This section presents a detailed review of the experimental findings learned throughout the
project, which includes disassembly, material recovery, and cathode material characteri­
zation. The project focuses on electrochemical experiments on coin cells built from aged
cathodes and comparing their capacity to rated capacity and weight estimate capacity,
as well as the recovery of metal sulfates and carbonates from layered oxide cathodes via
pyrometallurgical and hydrometallurgical methods. These findings are grouped to show
the effectiveness and difficulties encountered during recycling at various stages. The ob­
jective is to quantify the metal recovery yield using Energy Dispersive X­ray Spectroscopy
(EDX). Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) was utilized to evaluate the material’s struc­

underscore the significance of further research into more effective recycling procedures.
5.1
Battery Pretreatment and Disassembly
5.1.1
Disassembly of Battery Pack
During disassembly, notable differences were observed between the two battery packs.
The GoKwh battery pack had no side padding and was housed within a plastic crash
structure case that holds the cells together, which provides a distinct boundary between
the terminals. Once completed, the cells were removed with no resistance.
In contrast, the LanPwr battery pack was encased in dense material (Figure A.6d) around
the circumference of the battery pack, which made the disassembly more challenging.
Multiple cuts were needed along different axes on the outer casing to detach the adhe­
sive layers, after which latex was encountered at the base, which was removed using a
knife (Figure A.6c). Subsequently, an additional layer of adhesive was found covering the
cell terminals (Figure A.6a), which was carefully scraped away using a ceramic scalpel to
avoid short­circuiting or damaging the cells (Figure A.6b). Following removal of the adhe­
sives, the cells were initially separated as a 1p4s grouping and then carefully detached at
the busbars to isolate individual cells without damage, and the BMS was isolated. The op­
eration of cutting open the battery was time­consuming, which would make pretreatment
difficult. A mass balance of all the components of both battery packs was conducted and
documented in Table 5.1.
The mass balance numbers from both battery packs reveal multiple challenges that
would arise when scaling up the recycling process for these battery packs. In addition
to BMS and sodium­ion cells, both packs contain complex materials such as latex, adhe­
sives, and protective plastics. These complex materials contribute ≥30% of the battery
weight. Such variability complicates automated material identification and separation.
The differences in design between these two battery packs make automated dismantling
challenging, requiring the adaptation of recycling lines with suitable training for manual la­
bor or robots. On a wider scale, tool degradation and the slow rate of dismantling compli­
cated structures may cause bottlenecks, while ensuring worker and environmental safety
owing to cell short­circuiting presents additional challenges. To address these issues and
enhance recyclability and automation for battery packs such as GoKwh and LanPwr, a
few design improvements are recommended:

• Reducing material diversity and complexity by limiting the variety of plastics and
adhesives, ideally using a single type of material where possible to minimize the
contamination of product streams.
• Employ detachable busbars (Figure A.7) instead of welding, which not only im­
proves the integrity of the cells but also allows the chances of re­purposing the
busbars for new battery packs.
• Implement clear disassembly guidelines inside the battery pack or as a form of

• Utilize recycle­friendly adhesives which can be removed using controlled methods

• Design for early isolation of sensitive materials such as sense wires, electronic

5.1.2
Cycling of Cells for Artificial Aging
Battery aging is a critical factor in studying the recycling of batteries and the overall perfor­
mance, especially when evaluated on different charge/discharge rates (C­rates) relevant
Recycling of Sodium ion Battery Materials
31
==PAGE:40==
to energy storage applications. In this study, the primary focus is on the recycling perspec­
tive, specifically examining cell behavior under various C­rates, despite lacking detailed
knowledge of their prior usage in real­world applications. The “C­rate is a measure of
how quickly a battery is discharged/charged and is numerically equal to a multiple of the
battery’s rated capacity” [81]. Artificial aging is a method that subjects batteries to oper­
ate beyond their standard range, which causes them to reach the end­of­life (EoL) more
quickly. In order to cause the cells to age, thermal, electrical, and mechanical stresses are
typically introduced by the battery’s real operating circumstances [82]. At high discharge
and charge rates, there is an increase in polarization, which leads to an increase in inter­
nal resistance, accelerated side reactions and enhanced growth of the solid­electrolyte
interface (SEI), which degrades the battery faster and reduces the cell’s rated capacity.
Conversely, the low C­rates ease the ion transport, making the cells last longer and de­
laying the degradation process.
The cells of both battery were cycled using the ChargeDischarge tester (Figure 4.2). To
determine the final voltage range of GoKwh cells, multiple trials were conducted at a
0.1C rate. The voltage range optimization revealed that these cells delivered increasing
capacity with broader voltage windows, unlike LFP cells. The narrow 2.5–3.6 V range
yielded only 800 mAh, while 2.0–4.0 V extended this to 1200 mAh. The full nominal ca­
pacity of 1300 mAh was achieved when the voltage range was tested from 2.0–4.2 V
window. The voltage range was extended at the upper limit from 4.0 V to 4.2 V, enabling
more complete oxidation of the cathode material. For LanPwr cells, BMS­derived limits
were 2.1–3.9 V per cell, which only provided 9800 mAh at 0.1C, which nearly matched the
rated capacity. Extending the range slightly to 2.0–4.0 V enabled the full utilization of the
10,000 mAh nominal capacity. Upper range accesses more redox states, particularly in
layered oxides, resulting in a complete capacity. The lower limit stayed at 2.0 V, as further
lowering resulted in minimal gain. These results confirmed the appropriate voltage range
for the layered oxide chemistry and guided the selection of limits for subsequent cycling
tests. In this project, the cells have been cycled across a range of 0.5C up to 2C to sys­
tematically study their aging behavior. This approach enabled a clearer understanding of
how cycling conditions influence degradation rates by looking at the discharge capacity
and columbic efficiency to validate the relationship between artificial aging protocols
and C­rate selection.
To evaluate the varying charge/discharge rates on cell performance, the discharge ca­
pacity was monitored as a function of cycles across different C­rates. As shown in the
Figure 5.1, GoKwh cells were tested at 0.5C, 1C, 1.5C and 2C rates throughout 300 cy­
cles. Cells operated at 0.5C show the best capacity retention, maintaining approximately
1100 mAh after 300 cycles. It also has a steady degradation in capacity. The cells at
1C and 1.5C showed similar behavior but experienced higher capacity fade compared
to the 0.5C cell. The performance of 1.5C is better than 1C; this could be due to the
natural variation among cells (amount of active material, wetting of electrolyte or the non­
linear degradation of Na+ ion systems), indicating that lower C­rates do not always ensure
improved performance. While the difference is minor, it emphasizes the significance of
cell­specific behavior in long­term cycling.

It is also important to consider current tolerance level of the capacity charger, which

5.3
Coin Cell Fabrication and Electrochemical Testing
5.3.1
Coin Cell Fabrication
During coin cell fabrication, several challenges were observed that adversely affected
the electrochemical performance of the cells. Despite taking precautions such as ensur­
ing good electrolyte wetting, using a sodium metal anode larger than the cathode, and
maintaining pressure during crimping, some cells failed to exhibit any open circuit volt­
age or failed immediately upon cycling. Notably, certain cells displayed voltage upon
connection to the coin cell tester, but failed as soon as cycling began, producing a flat
charge–discharge curve. At a 1C rate, the tester recorded completion of 100 cycles al­
most instantaneously, indicating negligible capacity retention. Even cycling at a slower
0.1C rate resulted in capacity loss within minutes. This issue is commonly attributed to
internal short­circuiting, as well as misalignment and poor contact during crimping.
For cells with higher degradation (2C_GoKwh), some capacity was observed at 0.1C cy­
cling. However, at 1C, these cells rapidly depleted capacity and prematurely ended the
test. This behavior suggests either loss of active material or accelerated cathode degra­
dation. At low C­rates, the slower charge/discharge process allows partially degraded
materials to participate in the reaction, whereas at higher C­rates, increased internal re­
sistance and loss of conductive pathways prevent effective energy delivery, causing the
cell to appear “dead”. To avoid such fabrication difficulties as short circuiting and early
failure, precise assembly techniques are required. These include precise electrode tab
trimming, accurate separator alignment, and maintaining complete cathode­anode con­
tact. Furthermore, starting cycling at extremely low C­rates (e.g., 0.05­0.1C) can assist in
gradually activating aging electrode materials and improve initial cell stability.
5.3.2
Coin cell charge discharge at C/10
The electrochemical testing data from coin cells fabricated with cathodes from two differ­
ent battery packs exhibit distinct performance characteristics. All coin cells were cycled at
0.1C for three cycles immediately after fabrication to establish a stable SEI. As displayed
in Figure 5.6, the Ref_GoKwh cell maintains a charge capacity of roughly 4 mAh during
the cycles, whereas the discharge capacity is between 1.8 and 2.0 mAh. This capacity
loss is partially due to the creation of the Solid Electrolyte Interphase (SEI) on the anode
surface. However, SEI growth alone does not fully explain the difference. The cell may
have internal parasitic conduction paths (self discharge) as well as dendrite formation,
both of which can lead to a reduced discharge capacity. Inadequate electrical contact
during assembly, together with certain design limitations, may also contribute to this is­
sue. Improving electrode contact with future coin cell assemblies may increase capacity
retention alongside data consistency. For the aged cell (0.5C_GoKwh), the charge ca­
pacity decreases from 4 mAh to 1.9 mAh, while the discharge capacity starts lower at
around 1 mAh but converges with the charge capacity by the third cycle. The initial ca­
pacity loss is predominantly caused by irreversible side reactions during the first cycle.
As cycling progresses, the system stabilizes and the discharge efficiency improves and
capacities converge, indicating more reversible and efficient sodium­ion insertion/ex­
traction. Similar stabilization trends are observed in other 1C_GoKwh, 1.5C_GoKwh and
2C_GoKwh cells. These improvements correspond to enhanced Coulombic efficiencies,
as seen in Figure A.12a. It is noted, however, that the Reference_GoKwh cell continues
to experience capacity fade over the cycles for reasons previously mentioned, which is
also seen in its coulombic efficiency trend.

Hydrometallurgical Treatment
Acid leaching and successive precipitation were used to hydrometallurgically process the
recovered CAM from both GoKwh and LanPwr cells. Active metal oxides were dissolved
into a clear sulfate solution by leaching with 2M H2SO4 and H2O2 at 70°C. Selective pre­
cipitation of distinct elements was made possible by gradually adjusting the pH with NaOH.
Iron rich blackish­brown precipitate emerged at pH4, followed by yellowish­green particles
containing manganese and nickel at pH9. The successful extraction of transition metals
from the leachate was made possible by these procedures. After drying and grinding, the
powders were analyzed by XRD, SEM, and EDX analyses to confirm phase formation and
elemental distribution. A considerable amount of white crystalline residue was seen in the
samples throughout the drying process. This was identified as sodium sulfate(Na2SO4)

==PAGE:61==
by EDX. It most likely developed as a byproduct of the interaction between the sodium in
the CAM and the reaction of H2SO4 and NaOH. As a result, after drying, additional mass
formed apart from the metal sulfates (iron sulfate at pH4 and nickel and manganese sul­
fates at pH9). This observation highlights the complexity of the system and the presence
of secondary reaction products, which should be considered during purification and anal­
ysis. As sodium sulfate is consistently present across all samples, it will be disregarded
in the subsequent discussion of transition metal sulfates. The generation of Na2SO4 as
a by­product poses a challenge for the industry, as it cannot be released into the en­
vironment in large quantities. Additionally, there is currently no commercial demand to
absorb the substantial amounts of Na2SO4 that would be produced from future recycling
processes. The XRD patterns presented here were processed and plotted using Origin.
The data were smoothed with a Savitzky–Golay filter (5–10 points per window) to reduce
noise. For each phase, 2–3 significant peaks were identified and marked with lines on the

Characterization of the Precipitates
GoKwh CAM:
In contrast to pyrometallurgical roasting techniques, the hydrometallurgi­
cal processing of Ref_GoKwh cathode materials using inorganic acid leaching followed by
NaOH pH adjustment yielded noticeably different morphologies and phase assemblages.
SEM analysis showed that while pH9 precipitation (Mag 250×) formed dense, blocky
aggregates with smooth, consolidated surfaces typical of controlled sulfate crystal­
lization under alkaline conditions (Figure A.21b), pH4 precipitation (Mag 250×) produced
highly textured, angular particles with extensive surface pitting and porosity, indi­
cating aggressive dissolution followed by rapid mixed­phase precipitation of iron sulfate
(Figure A.21a). Titration needs to be done carefully because of the diprotic nature of
sulfuric acid and its quick pH change, which starts at about 3 and quickly rises to 7. Al­
though the second dissociation does not present a distinct inflection point, the sudden
pH increase can easily result in overshooting, so NaOH should be added cautiously to
maintain precise pH control.
XRD analysis confirmed the high crystallinity of the pH4 hydrometallurgical precipitate,
revealing clear and intense peaks predominantly corresponding to sodium sulfate and
iron sulfate phases (Figure 5.18). In contrast to the pyrometallurgical approach, the leach
solution after inorganic acid treatment does not contain significant amounts of nickel and
manganese at pH4, which explains the absence of their sulfate peaks in the diffraction
pattern. The primary crystalline product in this fraction is sodium sulfate, but iron sulfate
is also present at smaller levels. The intensity of XRD peaks depends on the proportion
of each phase present in the sample. For instance, if 0.2g of sample is analyzed and
over 0.15g consists of one phase, the peaks for that phase will appear more intense
compared to those from phases present in smaller amounts. It is possible that trace
amounts of nickel and manganese are present as mixed sulfates or solid solutions, or
that they are dissolved in the solution phase, even though discrete sulfate peaks are
not detectable. This is probably because of their low concentration or incorporation in
amorphous or poorly crystalline forms. The material can be redissolved in acid and then
titrated with sodium carbonate or a more mild base to promote selective precipitation of
specific metal carbonates, improving selectivity in downstream processing and further
purifying or recovering individual metal phases.

6
Conclusion
This thesis demonstrated practical, low complexity recycling procedures for layered oxide
sodium­ion battery (SiB) cathodes sourced from commercial battery packs (GoKwh and
LanPwr). After safe pretreatment and delamination, two recovery routes were validated.
The sulfation­roast route, followed by water leaching and staged precipitation, consis­
tently separated the metal fractions: iron was concentrated as carbonates at low pH (≈4),
while nickel and manganese were recovered at higher pH (≈8.5­9). The hydrometallurgi­
cal route using H2SO4/H2O2 achieved a comparable Fe to Ni/Mn split at similar pHs with
lower thermal requirements but generated sodium salt byproducts that require dedicated
management. XRD and SEM/EDX analyses confirmed the expected phase evolution and
elemental partitioning, while process variables such as pH setpoints and residence times
were found to strongly influence product purity and particle morphology.
From a practical standpoint, pack architecture, adhesives, and wiring significantly affected
dismantling effort and safety, emphasizing the importance of design­for­recycling princi­
ples. Coin­cell tests on recovered one­sided cathodes provided a functional evaluation of
the recycled materials, revealing rate limitations caused by prior cycling stress. Gentler
formation protocols partly mitigated these limitations, enhancing electrochemical perfor­
mance and supporting direct regeneration to restore active material function. While the
current conclusions are drawn from a limited number of cell builds, fabricating multiple
cells is crucial for robust statistical validation. As performance metrics converge across
repeated trials, the findings will become increasingly definitive.
One major obstacle to experimental repeatability was found to be material input variability.
Different packs that were purchased showed variations in the chemistry and cell structure.
For instance, GoKwh cells were found to have a copper current collector, most likely as a
result of the inability to employ aluminum for the anode slurry coating and the consequent
need for copper. Although they belonged to the same model, the two LanPwr units (with
a time interval of six months) that were purchased also had different compositions. Due
to these revisions, manufacturers were continually evaluating and enhancing their prod­
ucts and online spec sheets, which makes recycling more difficult by introducing changing
standards. When additional battery pack was purchased, it did not match previous lots,
plans to repeat important measurements were hindered, illustrating the vital need of lot­
consistent sourcing and upfront compositional screening to guarantee fair comparison
and experimental repeatability.
When combined, the results show that it is possible to achieve strong Fe to Ni and Mn
separation with standard reagents. They also point to operational variables that could
increase purity and yield, like stricter pH control and efficient management of salt by­
products. These techniques can provide a realistic route to pilot­scale validation with
enhanced analytical capabilities (Mass spectroscopy, FTIR and quantitative XRD), auto­
mated pH control, moderate process intensification, and greater input standards. Such
recycling strategies offer a viable testbed for scaling SiB recycling and integrating both
direct regeneration strategies that restore active materials at competitive cost and envi­
ronmental footprint and indirect recovery routes that produce cathode­grade sulfate and
carbonate precursors of transition metals, as the demand for energy storage continues to
increase.

Future Work
Future research should mainly concentrate on establishing accurate and consistent pH
control at every stage of the process in order to further enhance the recovery and purity of
metal salts from cathode materials. This involves using sensors to continuously check pH
and adding base gradually and carefully to avoid overshooting or fluctuations around the
setpoint. To encourage the targeted precipitation of desired solid phases and prevent the
development of undesirable mixed­phase products, it is essential to maintain a steady pH.
Purification remains a critical challenge. To address this, a re­precipitation strategy is
recommended: dissolving the initial precipitate and repeating the precipitation step un­
der stricter pH control can yield a more refined and homogeneous product and there is
greater selectivity. Additionally, employing a two­step filtration process; an initial coarse
filtration followed by a “polishing” filtration will help remove fine, co­precipitated impuri­
ties. Thorough washing with high­purity water at both stages will further enhance product
purity. Reducing salt contamination, particularly sodium sulfate (Na2SO4), is also of key
importance.
Future studies should incorporate procedures for salt removal, such as salt crystallization
and separation from spent process solutions. Periodically refreshing or partially replacing
the process solution can further prevent salt accumulation. Further investigation utiliz­
ing comprehensive XRD analysis is required because the precipitates from these tests
displayed complex diffraction patterns. A detailed examination of XRD data can help
improve the recovery process further by revealing more information about phase compo­
sition, crystallinity, and the presence of mixed or amorphous phases.
Altogether, the integration of continuous pH regulation, re­precipitation, and sequential fil­
tration provides a practical approach to achieving higher product purity, minimizing mixed­
phase formation, and reducing salt carryover. These steps are critical for producing re­
liable, cathode­grade precursor materials suitable for use in advanced battery technolo­
gies.
