1 Introduction
On the surface of planet Earth, there is a limited amount of information we receive from
deep space. Depending on latitude, time and season, the bare eye will be able to observe
the most illuminated celestial objects; stars, comets and our most immediate neighbour-
ing planets. With the dawn of the space age, observations outside of the protective yet
limiting atmosphere became a reality and a range of new observations and discoveries
were made possible, opening up for a hitherto unexpected understanding of the universe.
One ﬁeld that was opened up and still keeps us occupied with new discoveries, is x-ray
astrophysics.
For x-rays with their wavelength of 0.1 Ã... to 100 Ã... and energy levels of 100eV
to 100keV, the atmosphere is close to completely opaque and observing them is ren-
dered impractical or close to impossible. As not even mountain-based observatories are
able to reach altitudes for where cosmic x-rays are present, observations of such are
performed by satellites.
Figure 1: Opaqueness vs Wavelength. The incoming radiation is blocked by different gaseous
constituents in our atmosphere. The atomic properties and distribution of these gases governs
the energy range of radiation which can penetrate and how deep. Only a fraction of the x-ray
spectral band experiences the atmosphere with any less than 100 % opaqueness(KJELDE).
The origins of the cosmic x-rays are large-scale stellar phenomena like mass accre-
tion onto stellar bodies, merger and collision of galaxies and bursts of energy from star
formation in galaxies, altogether making up some of the most energetic phenomena
in the universe. They have been studied by a series of missions hailing back to the
1960s(KJELDE), and amongst current observatories for cosmic x-rays ESAs XMM-
Newton and NASAs Chandra and NuSTAR missions.
Their missions will be handed over to the ESA mission ATHENA, with a planned
launch in 2028, as ESA has announced that an x-ray telescope will be their next great
science mission(KJELDE). Developments based on the successful designs of NuSTAR
and XMM-Newton will assure the success of the mission, and amongst these develop-
ments are new and improved focusing X-ray mirrors.

With the increase in energy from electromagnetic radiation, the interaction with mat-
ter also changes. Coupled with the demands of weight and space for a space-borne
observer, it creates demand for a drastically different optical focusing device.
Instead of having the optically focusing properties from a parabolic mirror, x-rays
only reﬂect when the angle between incoming x-ray and the reﬂecting mirror is very
small. The solution to this has been to place mirrors close to normal on the incident
direction of the x-ray photons. A series of these mirrors are placed in a circle around the
intended focal point of the x-ray photons. This circular structure is then expanded upon,
until there are hundred of layers of mirrors concentrically expanding from the center
axis.

As x-rays have a much higher penetrative power than normal light, an alternating
combination of two materials with different atomic weights are deposited onto a sur-
face to create a bilayer for maximum reﬂection. The material combination is central to
which energies can be reﬂected, as the difference in atomic weight inﬂuences the re-
ﬂecting properties and a heavy-material - light-material combination is used. A series
of bilayers is usually deposited on top of each other, composing a multilayer. Super-
polished silicon used as a substrate has been found to give good results for multilayer
coatings.
As cosmic phenomena and structures extrudes energy in a wide spectral range, the
goal for a X-ray astrophysics telescope is of course to be able to focus as much of it
as possible. To achieve this the bilayers are coated in increasing thickness following a
power-law distribution, very small but gradual increase in bilayer thicknesses so to build
up a depth-graded Multilayer.

2.5 X-ray focusing Optics
As shown earlier, focusing x-rays is a rather different task from that of focusing like
visible light. The wavelength is inversely proportional to the energy so as the shorter
the wavelength, the higher the energy the wave carries according to the relation E =
¯hw = hc/λ, where h is the Planck constant, c is the speed of light in vacuum and λ is
the wavelength. A shorter wavelength will thus carry a higher energy and have a dras-
tically different refractive index in the order of 10−5. Revisiting Snells law 3 makes it
abundantly clear that this would amount to practically no change in direction.
This in turn excludes normal optical methods developed for visible light with a tra-
ditional parabolic focusing geometry unless a very dense material. A material of such
density and atomic weight would nonetheless have a signiﬁcant if not scientiﬁcally de-
structive absorption rate.
A novel solution to this issue is to have the incoming photons reﬂect and not refract.
This poses challenges in itself, as shown in section 2.1, and is solved by operating on
the grazing incidence angle Θinc of values below the critical angle Θcrit as there is an
option of total reﬂectance at sufﬁciently low angles.

2.6 Sputtering Theory
To produce a multilayer of deposited layers of different materials at sufﬁciently low
thicknesses, a technique called sputtering is used to evaporate extremely thin layers of
down to 10 Å layer thickness. Sputtering is when atoms are ejected from a target ma-
terial by bombardment of energetic particles. The prerequisites for sputtering is that
the energy of the incoming particles must have kinetic energy much higher than normal
thermal energies, at a level where the kinetic energy is of the same order of magnitude
as the binding energies between the atoms or molecules of the target material.
The sputter rate is dependent on a range of factors, but foremost the atomic masses of
the ion and target atoms, and the surface binding energy of the atoms of the target ma-
terial. This gives a very material dependent sputter yield which also extends to that of
the inﬂow gases of the coating. The gases in the chamber needs to be highly regulated
so the chamber is pumped down to high vacuum of 10−7 Torr before coating. Once

At DTU Space the method of sputtering used is DC Sputtering, a method favored
for deposition of elemental metals(KJELDE 1). The magnetron itself is electrically
grounded box acting as an anode, set around the magnets where the sample is mounted.
When a coating is initiated a bias of -400 V is placed upon the cathode with respect to
the anode. The resulting electrical ﬁeld interacts with the inﬂow gas and ionizes it. Once
ionized they become attracted to the negative cathode and the target material mounted
on it. Accelerated by the electrical ﬁeld they collide into the target with sufﬁcient energy
to break free atoms of the target material. The now free target atoms are be ejected out
and onto the substrate located opposite to the target.
Once released, the free electrons are captured in an magnetic ﬁeld trapping them in
a magnetic mirror state, until they collide with an atom of the sputter gas and breaks
free another electron, hits the anode shield or recombines with a positive ion. The in-
teractions of particles in front of the cathode turns the area in front of the target into
a plasma. Once triggered by the bias voltage the free electrons will keep the plasma
present by adding more free electrons, and the gas ﬂow might even be lowered without
the plasma dying out.
The geometrical shape of the anode, cathode, or other physical elements surrounding
the magnetron also has an impact on the quality of the coating and of the electrical ﬁeld.
If there is connection between the cathode and anode other than from the sputtering
atoms the voltage will disappear and the plasma will die out due to arching. Arching

2.7.1 Collimation
The sputtering rate for a target is not uniform over its surface. It is largest at the center
of the target and diminishes towards the edges. This uneven distribution causes wide
angle sputtering which has been found to give samples a higher roughness. Collimation
ﬁlters are used to accommodate for this. The collimation ﬁlters can either be mounted
around, or on the plates together with the witness samples. Their objective is to alter the
path traveled by the sputtered particles. (KJELDE KJERNEARTIKKEL)

Seperator Plates
Seperator plates are two plates mounted perpendicular onto the

rate for the target material.

2.7.2 Long Term Stability
A polyatomic nonmetall component as carbon might have a suboptimal long term stabil-
ity issue, and diffuse into the material it was coated onto after some time. This will lead
to a higher degree of non-homogenous structure, increase diffuse scattering and reduce
reﬂectivity. As Carbon is apparent in both of the multilayer variations in this thesis, and
the metalloid Boron in one of them, the likelihood of a diffusion is quite high.
With the diffusion of different component into each others respective layers in a multi-
layer being a highly unwelcomed feature, the need for a long term stability investigation
is apparent, and is performed by re-measuring the samples using the X-ray Measurement
lab in the basement of DTU Space and comparing their attributes to those initially ob-
tained from the ﬁrst measurement. Diffusion is expected to ﬁrst become apparent after
150 days, and were such omitted from this thesis.(KJELDE)

coating the cathodes are usually set to have constant settings, and the parameter that can
alter the layer thickness is the ring velocity. The ring itself has a circumference divided
up into 668 000 steps, and ring-velocity will be given in steps/s. The layer thickness
will be inversely proportional to the ring velocity. See ﬁgure 16.
For this investigation the magnets in one of the magnetrons were replaced by a super-
strong magnet, to combat the inﬂuence on the magnetic ﬁelds from the ferromagnetic
material Cobalt. At each of the cathodes there is also a rotating shield that can be placed
in front of the cathode. These were initially used for the sample coatings, but due to
reliability issues the continued use of them for most of this project was terminated.
The chamber itself has an outer bell-jar shaped dome that is 1 m in diameter and 1.2
m tall. During mounting of samples or other work inside of the coating chamber the
bell-jar can be lifted completely off to give full access to the chamber. Also seen in ﬁg-
ure 16 are plates with samples mounted on the rotating outer ring. These are mounted
top and bottom to ensure that the distance between substrate and target is constant for
all coatings.
A coating at the DTU Space Multilayer lab starts with cleaning of the chamber and
mounting of plates holding the samples on the rotating ring. The bell-dome is then low-
ered unto the ﬂoor of the DC Sputtering Chamber. A rough-pump is turned on parallel
to a heating device encircling the chamber. The heating of the dome is to increase evap-
oration of organic materials and other impurities left inside the chamber.
When the pressure reaches 3.8*10−1 Torr, a Turbopump with a velocity of 38 000
rpm is turned on. In the current chamber conﬁguration it is capable of lowering the
pressure down to 10−7 Torr before an equilibrium state is reached. However at 10−6
the chamber has sufﬁciently low pressure to start coating. The predetermined gases are
infused into the chamber and power is supplied to the cathodes.

3.2 X-ray Measurements
To assess the quality of the samples produced, measurement of the reﬂectivity was per-
formed in the X-ray lab located at DTU Space. The X-rays are produced by a Rigaku
rotating copper anode at 8.047 kEV, corresponding to the Cu Kα1-edge.
Subsequent analysis of the measured data was performed by a the program IMD(KJELDE
IMD MANUAL). A model is constructed and manually adapted to ﬁt the measured data
as closely as possible. The parameters that were manually ﬁtted are the d-spacing, Γ,
the interface roughness σ and top roughness σ. The process is iterative and repeated
until satisfactory results are found. For larger structures more advanced models can be
made and ﬁtted to the data, with more parameters to be investigated. This was done for
the depth-graded multilayers.
As the beam leave the source, there are a number of alterations performed on it before
it interacts with the sample, as illustrated in ﬁgure 17.
A certain collimation is needed to shape the outgoing beam, and is initially performed
by two adjustable slits before it enters an evacuated chamber to avoid the beam losing
energy. It is then double-reﬂected from a pair of Ge-crystals acting as monochromators,
calibrated with an incident angle so that reﬂection only happens at the Ge(111) surface
for a certain wavelength, according to (??). The beam that passes the monochromators
has an enhanced energy resolution at 8.047 keV.
The beam is then subjected to an attenuator. The attenuator is a slab of metal with seg-
ments of different depth hollowed out of it. Depending on demand, the attenuator can
move and expose a variation of segment depths for the incoming beam to pass through.
The reason of this is as the detector has a saturation rate of about 2000 counts/s, at low
incident grazing angles the detector would be saturated if an unattenuated beam were to
be measured. During measurements the attenuator is set so that the count rate is around
1000 counts/s. After the attenuator another slit further focuses the beam before it hits
the sample mounted on the sample holder.

5 Analysis and Discussion
5.1 Material Combination
The material combinations studied in this project were Co/C and Co/B4C, where Co
had the role as the heavier material. Cobalt is lighter than other materials traditionally
used in this role, such as Gold and Platinum, and one of the motivations of using Cobalt
as the heavy material was to build up the amount of data on the "lighter" section of
heavy-layer bilayer constituents. Previous analysis of Cobalt as a unit in a bilayer has
been performed with satisfactory results (KJELDE COC-artikkel-WINDT), and an ex-
pansion on those seemed viable.
The light materials used in the bilayers are C and B4C. Both of these were subjected
to a series of coatings as shown in section 4, with each combination coated in two d-
spacings at different gaseous content, but all using reactive sputtering. A comparative
view of the attributes of the material combinations are then found by averaging all the
d-spacings of each target size of the respective material combinations and setting them
up against the roughness, as shown in the ﬁgure below.
Figure 34: Comparing Material Composition. The different materials both exhibit an increase
in roughness as the thickness of the bilayer increases, but it can be seen that the material
combination Co/C trends towards exhibiting a larger roughness than Co/B4C.
The samples were all coated using reactive sputtering with pressure levels ranging
from 2.6 mTorr to 3.0 mTorr, and at different partial pressure settings. Both of these
factors increase the uncertainty of the values, and explains the relatively large uncer-
tainties of the values. Power, collimation and coating velocities were kept constant at
the production of the samples compared above, so ﬁg 34 still stands as showing a trend.
The data used for ﬁg 34 is listed in 20 below.
