Abstract
Potato mill and winery industrial eﬄuents were utilized as a nutrient source
for Chlorella sorokiniana biomass production in batch reactors. Optimal ef-
ﬂuent dilution determined to be in 1-5 percent and 5-10 percent range, for
Potato Mill and winery eﬄuents, respectfully.
Volumetric productivity on
potato mill eﬄuent at 1 percent and 2 percent dilutions were 1.75 - 2.04 g
L−1d−1 and winery eﬄuent dilutes at 5 and 10 percent obtained 0.49-0.50 g
L−1d−1, respectively. Average nutrient reduction ranged between 81-85 per-
cent for potato mill dilutes 1, 2 and 5 percent, respectfully. Moreover, 57 and
88 percent nutrient reduction winery dilutes 10 and 5 percent, respectfully.
Life cycle assessment determined potato mill and winery both have good pos-
itive environmental mid and end-point indicators.
5 percent winery dilute
had the greatest environmental performance as quantiﬁed by LCA end-points
categories. Functional unit utilized is 1kg dw biomass LCA sensitivity analy-
sis performed on volumetric productivity indicate a direct impact on biomass
production an water consumption. Additionally, nutrient removal sensitivity
determined high beneﬁcial environmental performance of the product system.

Introduction
This century marks a monumental shift, from fossil fuel dependency towards sustain-
able energy technologies, as unsustainable anthropogenic products are incrementally
being phased out by European nations (Cottrell J. et al., 2015). The global com-
munity is still heavily reliant on non-renewable fossil fuels (i.e., petrol, natural gas,
and coal) for the production of the majority of goods and services[Pablo-Romero,
M. et al., 2017). Primary consumers of fossil fuels are still reluctant to make a
shift or unconvinced by climate change (McCright, A. et al., 2016). Moreover, the
global population is continuously increasing at an uncontrolled rate and consump-
tion along with it (Patarasuk, P. et al., 2016). This growth will increase the release
of atmospheric greenhouse gasses (GHG). Additionally, putting a strain on land
availability, energy, food and water security on an already overburden environment
(Cherp, A. et al., 2014). Therefore, there must be a signiﬁcant political, social and
scientiﬁc change from the current business as usual (BAU) model to a sustainable
one. Biotechnologies are in the unique position in which they can alleviate pressure
from all ﬁve burdens through sustainable algae biomass growth. Utilization of Al-
gae biomass production as an alternative tertiary wastewater treatment is possible
(Manheim, D. et al., 2013) provide liquid fuel, sustainable biogas, food, beneﬁcial
pharmaceutical properties and additionally it can be grown on brownﬁeld sites or
coastlines (Eksioglu, S. et al., 2009). Furthermore, CO2 released during combustion
of biomass is carbon neutral as it is assimilated during photosynthesis of organic
biomass [Gougoulias, C. et al., 2014]. The European Commission indicates that
renewable energy and environmental protection is a primary focus, as CO2 targets
have been set for both 2020, aiming for a >20 percent share renewable energy by

2020. The EU Commission also requires that surface waters reach a good ecological
standing by 2020 while procuring water measurement tools and standards by 2015
for groundwater. Moreover, the Nitrates Directive is set to prevent water pollu-

e.g. surface and groundwater areas that may be impacted by nitrate contamination.
”Vulnerable Zones are deﬁned as those waters which contain a nitrates concentration

(HVP), clean water and CO2 extraction (E4Water, 2013). It is vital to test a range
of industrial eﬄuents such as those from the potatoes milling and winery industry,
for their potential to sustain algae biomass growth. During these initial test phases,
governmental tariﬀs are a necessity to ensure economic feasibility in regards to algae
biomass until the technology is fully developed (Naghavi, A., 2007).

product systems via LCA.

Background information
In 1951, microalgae pioneers began research on the potential uses of algae as a sani-
tary process for biological stabilization. A conclusion that microalgae were a simple
and economically viable process, as it required little maintenance and labor for the
production (Ludwig, H. F. et al., 1951). This revealed environmental, social and
ﬁnancial related beneﬁts of algae biomass growth. Microalgae strains are from uni-
cellular species and thrive in freshwater and marine ecosystems (Abdel-Raouf, N., et
17
==PAGE:19==

al., 2012). There is a range of microalgae species; conservative estimation is 72,500
diﬀerent species (Guiry, M. D., 2012), each with their biochemical composition and
characteristics. Microalgae is an incredible versatile biomass and has more competi-
tive advantages compared with conventional land-based biomasses in regards to land
use, liquid fuel production, feed, and as a fertilizer (Ehimen, E. A., 2016). Microal-
gae is a third-generation biofuel that has the potential of increasing environmental
sustainability of liquid fuels and bioproducts, while other ﬁrst gen. biofuels are not
necessarily environmentally friendly. Microalgae have a signiﬁcantly higher growth
rate compared to competing for terrestrial plants in a 24-hour cycle (Martin, M. A.,
2010). Related advantages that favor algae amongst other proposed biomasses is
the ability to carbon sequester, absorb nutrients from wastewater; biomass harvest

Bioreﬁnery
The bioreﬁnery concept is split into two segments. The primary segment being full
utilization of outputs from the production processes e.g. zero waste systems. Sec-
ondly, to form an industrial symbiosis, ensuring another sector can utilize the output
of another. (1) Full utilization occurs regularly through identiﬁcation of organism
with HVP synthesis capabilities. Downstream after HVP extraction, residual waste
containing lipids, carbohydrates, and proteins are used for algal oils while the latter is
utilized for horticultural feed or further HVP extraction, respectfully. (2) Industrial
symbiosis focuses mainly on the inputs required for algae cultivation; CO2, and inor-
ganic compounds e.g. nitrates (N), phosphates(P) and organic carbons. Industrial
eﬄuent contains a high amount of N, P and COD inputs, while the CO2 fraction
is sourced from ﬂue gas produced by power plants. The principles of bioreﬁnery
support the EU commission’s waste framework directives aim of creating a closed
loop material ﬂow (EU Commission, directive 2008/98/EC); the key to sustainable
co-production of liquid fuels, feed, and HVPs.
Lipids
Algal oil is extracted from lipids; algal lipid content is observed to increase and
accumulate under certain environmental stresses (Perez-Garcia, O. 2011). Lipids

make up fats, oils and waxes, provide energy for cells, cell structure, and support in
chemical messaging (Samori, C. et al., 2013). The main types of lipids are phospho-
lipids and triacylglycerols (TAGs). TAGs are the most common type of lipid and are
great at energy storage, storing twice as much energy as carbohydrates. Phospho-
lipids are identiﬁed by a glycerol backbone with a hydrophilic phosphate head and
two hydrophobic fatty acid tails, while TAGs are identiﬁed by a glycerol backbone
attached to three fatty acid tails with an acyl group. The fatty acid structure con-
tains a hydrocarbon chain formed of a carboxyl group head. TAGs can be in form of
saturated or unsaturated fatty acids. Mono or polyunsaturated suggest a single or
multiple double bonds between the carbons respectively. The extra hydrogen space
in unsaturated fatty acid TAGs will form as a liquid at room temperature making
them prime as a liquid fuel. Unsaturated fatty acids such as Oleic and Linoleic acid
are commonly the most dominate fatty acids identiﬁed in Chlorella sorokiniana with
both saturated fatty acids, Palmitic and stearic acid being present to a lesser degree.
These four fatty acids are the most commonly identiﬁed in biodiesel (Chader, S. et.
Al, 2011). TAG is separated and converted into fatty acid methyl esters (FAME)
using methanol as a catalyst by the process of transesteriﬁcation. Methanol is com-
monly utilized as it is a cheap alcohol source. The option to upgrade TAG to FAME
is a method of reducing high viscosity of materials that leads to increasing operation
performance in diesel engines (Prabhakar, A. R. et al., 2011).

CO2 inputs modeled are sourced from ﬂue gas from a CHP plant and CO2 canisters
the remainder derived from ambient air. A 5 percent CO2 concentration is utilized
in the experimental phase. This is reﬂected by the CO2 input mix in the model-
ing. According to (Huang, G. et al., 2016) there is 10 - 25 percent of CO2 in ﬂue
gas, thereby representing adequate concentration for microalgae growth. Flue gas
represent 52 percent of global CO2 emissions and therefore essential sustainability
practice of biomass . According to (Slade, R., and Bauen, A., 2013) 1.83kg CO2
required to grow 1kg algae biomass DW. Therefore, a rough assumption of 0.77kg
CO2 removal from ﬂue gas with 0.73kg CO2 coming from CO2 canisters and the
remaining 0.33kg CO2 from ambient air.

Water Treatment
Wastewater treatment is modeled by the nutrient uptake. Two material ﬂows were
created; IN and OUT, were created within simapro for each individual dilution. The
“IN” material ﬂow is determined by nutrient content in the dilutes pre-treatment,
and “OUT” material ﬂow is determined by the nutrient content post-treatment. The
remaining nutrient content were categorized as emission, while the nutrients removed
during biomass growth were categorized as avoided emissions to the technosphere.
Water recycling
Water is modeled to recycled back into the product system. Achieved by adding
water as an input from the technosphere in the IN material ﬂow and was added as
an emission during the OUT material ﬂow. It is therefore assumed that the water
that is put out is the same that is put back into the system. Furthermore, 5 percent
water evaporation loss was assumed.

Conclusion

the experimental and LCA stages of this study that Chlorella sorokiniana biomass
production on V 5OUT has the best environmental performance. Moreover, V 5OUT
