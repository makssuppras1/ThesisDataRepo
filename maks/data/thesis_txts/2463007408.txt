Abstract
In response to the banking of large numbers of emission allowances in the EU ETS,
the European Commission decided upon the introduction of a market stability re-
serve (MSR) in the fourth phase of the trading scheme. The market stability reserve
controls the number of banked allowances by regulating the volume of allowances
auctioned.
The first part of this thesis investigates the impact of the MSR cost-optimal invest-
ment decisions in a small Northern European country.
This is done by solving a
stochastic two-stage mixed integer formulation of a generation expansion problem
that treats wind power and carbon prices as uncertain parameters, where 2020, 2030
and 2050 are investment periods. Generation technologies considered are supercritical
coal-fired plants, combined-cycle gas-fired units, woodchips-fired CHP plants, offshore
wind power and pumped hydro storage. Without consideration of carbon prices, the
model suggests the cost-optimal electricity supply to be fully based on coal-fired gen-
eration. Carbon prices as projected based on phase III policy lead to investments in
gas-fired units and wind power in 2050, while 2020 and 2030 electricity supply remain
the same as at zero carbon prices. Under the MSR, further low carbon investments
in 2020 become cost-optimal. Given the way emissions are calculated in this study,
the results suggest that the MSR causes economic inefficiency as later emission abate-
ment would lead to gains in temporal efficiency.
In the second part of this thesis, a bi-objective optimisation problem comprising the
simultaneous minimisation of system costs and carbon emissions is investigated. The
solution of such a problem is not a point solution, but a front of solutions that are
Pareto-optimal with respect to the two objectives. The problem is solved through
a combination of decomposition and an elitist non-dominated sorting genetic algo-
rithm (NSGA-II). This way, significant performance improvements are achieved com-
pared to solving the non-decomposed problem sequentially under decreasing emission
caps.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In connection with both international responsibilities arising from duties such as the
Paris Agreement (UN 2015) and Kyoto Protocol (UNFCC 1998) obligations as well as
unilateral climate targets, the nation-wide planning of energy supply is subject to not
only cost minimisation but also environmental considerations. In such a context, the
European Emission Trading Scheme (ETS) (European Parliament and Council 2003)
has been brought to life as a means of cost-effective emission reduction. However, it
failed to make the contribution that was expected of it due to low emission allowance
prices in the carbon market as a consequence of a stark surplus of allowances. As a
reaction, it was decided upon the introduction of a market stability reserve (MSR)
(European Commission 2014) to the scheme, with the goal of decreasing the allowance
surplus and stabilising quota prices.
Energy investment planning models solve the Generation Expansion Planning prob-
lem traditionally as deterministic linear or mixed-integer programs with the single
objective of minimising investment and operational cost of an energy system under
different constraints, one of which being compliance with emission targets, e.g. Bal-
morel (Wiese et al. 2018) or TIMES-DK (Balyk et al. 2019). Treating these emission
targets as set, and thus as hard constraints, to the model leads to a single cost-
minimising solution without the knowledge of surrounding solutions in the objective
space of emissions and costs.
This thesis is two-folded: In the first part, a two-stage stochastic energy system plan-
ning model for a small European country with investment periods 2020, 2030 and
2050, loosely based on data from Denmark is developed. Electricity demand scenar-
ios are obtained by Monte-Carlo simulation from a time series model and clustering
into one representative week for both winter and summer. Wind power scenarios orig-
inate from historical data which are clustered into three scenarios per season. EUA
price scenarios for both the status-quo policy scenario and ETS phase IV reforms are
based on data from the PhD thesis of Jörn Richstein (Richstein 2015). Optimal in-
vestment choices are then obtained under different policy scenarios for the European
ETS.
Secondly, these results are analysed in terms of their economic efficiency with regards
to emission reduction: Carbon prices, which penalise emissions, can, compared to
an upper bound on the system emissions, cause temporal inefficiency due to price
trajectories evolving over time. This raises the question of how to obtain solutions

to energy system investment problems which are optimal with respect to both costs
and emissions, i.e. Pareto-optimal in a bi-objective problem. Hence, a framework is
developed to efficiently solve these problems. In particular, the ϵ-constraint method
(Haimes, Yacob, Lasdon, and Wismer 1971) as a base line is compared to a combi-
nation of a Non-Dominated Elitist Genetic Search algorithm (NSGA-II) (Deb et al.
2000) and decomposition.
Through this analysis, the following research questions are aimed to be answered:
1. What is the effect of the introduction of a market stability reserve
on the cost-optimal investments for the power system of a small
Northern European country under EUA price and wind power uncer-
tainty? Does the market stability reserve impact economic efficiency
of emission abatement?
2. What is a computationally efficient method to derive the front of
solutions Pareto-optimal in terms of emissions and costs in such a
power system?
Scientific Contribution.
While the discussion of the MSR reforms is vivid (cf.
section 2.1.3), to the best of my knowledge, no previous publication has analysed the
economic efficiency of the new market stability reserve in a stochastic program under
consideration of both wind power and ETS price uncertainty.
Furthermore, even
though genetic algorithms have been applied in energy systems research (cf. section
7.4), no study has been found that constructs the front of Pareto optimal solutions
between the two objectives costs and emissions under uncertainty.
Structure of this thesis.
Following this introduction, chapter 2 gives an overview
over the functioning of the EU ETS as well as the market stability reserve and pro-
vides a brief overview on the recent discussion in the literature related to the MSR
in its latest form.
Subsequently, the methods used in the first part of this the-
sis, namely Stochastic Programming, linear Time Series Analysis and Partitioning
around Medoids, are introduced (ch. 3). In the following (ch. 4), the formulation
of the Stochastic Programming model which builds the basis of this study, is given.
Chapter 5 describes how the scenarios fed to that model have been obtained and pro-
vides data on the power technologies considered, while chapter 6 provides the results
regarding investment decisions as well as system costs and CO2 emissions. There,
the impacts of the introduction of the market stability reserve are discussed in the
context of their placement on the Pareto-optimal front between the two objectives
power system costs and emissions. Leading to the question of how such a front can
be obtained efficiently, an overview on multi-objective optimisation methods in the
context of energy system modelling is provided in chapter 7. Chapter 8 outlines the
implementation and performance of a single- and of a bi-objective genetic algorithm,

CHAPTER 2
The European ETS
The first part of this thesis (with the results in ch. 6) is devoted to an analysis of
the newly introduced market stability reserve of the EU ETS. In order to provide an
understanding of this policy, this chapter starts with a short theoretical background
on carbon pricing (sect. 2.1), followed by an overview on the EU ETS in phases I to
III (sect. 2.1.1) and IV (sect. 2.1.2), while a brief overview on the discussion of the
phase IV reforms in the literature is given in section 2.1.3.
2.1
EU ETS
Environmental economists traditionally see pollution as a ”consequence of an ab-
sence of prices for certain scarce environmental resources” (Cropper and Oates 1992).
Ibid. consider pollution as a public bad associated with the production of a good
X = X(L, E, Q) with labour L, waste discharge E and pollution Q = Q(E), where
utility U = U(X, Q) rises with higher production levels X and decreases with pollu-
tion Q, whilst emission reductions require additional labour input and emissions are
regarded as a production factor (ibid.). Hence, in a competitive market equilibrium,
firms pollute until their marginal return dX
dE = 0 (ibid.). As a consequence, in or-
der to internalise the full social cost in the production function, a price equal to the
marginal external cost of pollution, needs to be imposed (ibid.), i.e. a Pigouvian tax
(Pigou 1932). Quantity-based climate policies, in contrast to Pigouvian price-based
mechanisms, set an upper limit, a cap, on the total amount of emissions permitted
to be emitted and make those permits tradeable based on the idea that this leads to
cost-effective abatement (Coase 1960). Cropper and Oates (1992) mention a Pigou-
vian tax alongside unit subsidies for emission reduction and the trade of emission
permits as equivalent policy measures in this context, given the regulator enjoys per-
fect information. Then, a policy scheme based purely on a tax is equivalent to a
fully elastic allowance supply curve and a purely quantity-based policy corresponds
to fully inelastic supply (Fankhauser and Hepburn 2010). A theoretical discussion is
undertaken by Weitzman (1974).

2.1.1
Phase I to III
This section gives an overview on the first three phases of the EU ETS. If not in-
dicated differently, it follows European Commission (2015c), while some additional
references to legal documents are given.
Cap & trade.
The scheme was introduced in European Parliament and Council
(2003) following a ”Cap & Trade” approach. At the end of each year, participants
must surrender allowances according to their emissions. If they are short in allowances,
they can either purchase allowances in auctions or from other participants, or abate
emissions. If they fail to do so, a fine is imposed and allowances hold must be surren-
dered.
Phases.
The history of the ETS is split into four phases. The first phase, which ran
from 2005 to 2007, served the purpose of a pilot phase to secure a well-functioning
system. Since the start of the second phase, 2008 to 2012, emissions from the aviation
sector are covered by the scheme and international carbon credits obtained under the
rules of the Kyoto Protocol (UNFCC 1998) can be used to comply with EU ETS
obligations (European Parliament and Council 2004) (until 2020, as indicated in
European Commission (2013)). On contrary to the first two phases, the third phase,
which is running from 2013 to 2020, features auctioning of allowances, rather than
free allocation as the default allocation method. Phase IV will run from 2021 to 2030
(European Parliament and Council 2018).
Coverage.
From the start of phase III, 50% of the emissions in the ETS are covered
by the scheme. This comprises the power sector, GHG intensive industry and the
aviation sector, concerning flights within the EEA area only.
Small emitters are
eligible to opt-out from the scheme.
Emission Cap.
The cap on total emissions within the scheme was set to be fixed
during the first and second phase. In the third phase, it is set to a starting value and
reduced linearly each year by 1.74% of the total 2010 emissions (Linear Reduction
Factor [LRF]) (European Commission 2010) (Figure 2.1):

Allocation.
Participants can receive allowances either through free allocation or
through auctioning. In phase III, half of the total number of allowances issued are
auctioned. While in the power sector all allowances, with the exception of free al-
location concerning the modernisation of the power sector in some countries, are
auctioned, industry participants received 80% of allowances through free allocation
in 2013, with this number decreasing to 30% in 2020. Industry sectors ”which are
deemed to be exposed to a significant risk of carbon leakage” (European Comission
2014) receive all allowances through free allocation. In the aviation sector, 85% of
the allowances are allocated free of charge and 15% are auctioned.
Auctioning.
Responsibility for auctioning of their shares of allowances lies at the
member states [MSs]. The distribution of auctioning rights across MSs in phase III
takes place as follows: 88% of the allowances are distributed according the MSs’ GHG
emissions in the first phase; 10% are distributed among MSs with low per-capita in-
come; and 2% are given to MSs that already achieved their 20% emission reduction
target in 2005 (Commission of the European Communities 2008).
The auctions take place in a single-round, sealed bid and uniform price system.
Among the eligible bidders are ETS and aviation operators, investment firms and
credit institutions. The MS governments receive the auction revenues, of which at
least 50% need be utilised to combat climate change.
Free Allocation.
In phase III, no allowances for the power sector are freely issued
with the exception of MSs supported in the modernisation of their power sector up

Compliance is monitored and verified by accredited institutes who need to visit the

2.1.2
Phase IV
Phase 4 of the EU ETS will run from 2021 to 2030. It comes along with structural
reforms.
Market Stability Reserve.
The European Parliament and Council (2015) decided
upon the introduction of a Market Stability Reserve [MSR]. The decision suggested
the establishment of the reserve in 2018 with start of operation in 2019. In each
year, 12 % of surplus allowances shall be deducted from the auctioning volume and
added to the MSR, unless this number would be lower than a lower quantity trigger
of 100 million. If, on the other hand, the surplus is less than 400 million allowances,
the same number is to be released from the reserve and added to the auctioning
volume. Additionally, an emergency price trigger is introduced which releases 100
million allowances from the reserve in case ”the allowance price is more than three
times the average price of allowances during the two preceding years” (The European
Parliament and the Council of the European Union 2009; European Parliament and
Council 2015). Furthermore, the 900 million allowances backloaded during phase III
shall be added to the reserve instead of being added to the auctioning volume in 2019
and 2020 and any unallocated allowances shall be placed in the reserve as of 2020.
The surplus is calculated as the number of allowances issued since 2008 minus the
verified emissions ever since.
Amendments and Other Changes.

2.1.3.1
General Considerations
Fankhauser and Hepburn (2010) discuss different carbon market design features rel-
evant to temporal flexibility of the carbon market.
They argue for banking and
borrowing of allowances across periods due to increases in market flexibility and liq-
uidity. Allowance reserve triggered by minimum or maximum prices as well as price
ceilings and floors are discussed. The authors argue for long commitment periods in
==PAGE:21==
2.1 EU ETS

order to reduce regulatory uncertainty, for banking as a means of increasing system
flexibility and for allowance reserves due to price stabilisations as a consequence of
anticipative behaviour of market participants.
The discussion of banking behaviour of participants in the EU ETS is of severe im-
portance for the understanding of the scheme’s reforms. However, a discussion is out
of the scope of this study. An overview is given by Richstein, Chappin, and Vries
(2015), who define banking as ”holding of EUAs that exceed the amount needed for
compliance in the current year” and underline that unlimited banking and borrowing
is not optimal due to temporal inefficiencies: If investors have a discount rate higher
than the social discount rate, abatement is postponed past the socially optimal point
in time (ibid.). Schopp and Neuhoff (2013) argue that banking of allowances occurs
due to speculation on future prices and power companies hedging needs of future
electricity sales. From a series of interviews, they observe that the hedging demand
of power producers depends on their future contracts for power sales and their expec-
tation of future carbon prices.
Most Relevant Post-2018 Studies
A review of existing work studying the pre-2018 proposals and versions of the MSR
and other phase IV reforms is not given here, since it has been done extensively else-
where (c.f. for instance Richstein (2015)). Also Acworth (2014) gives an overview
over the discussion of the proposal by the European Commission (2014). He mentions
concerns in the debate regarding, among other things, the alignment of the trigger
level with the hedging demand of allowances, the time delay in the response and the
focus on quantity-based over price-based triggers.

serve” AND (”CO2”) AND ”Investment” and ”Prices” AND ”Reform” returns 49
publications since 2018. An overview on a selection of the most relevant studies, and
some additional ones, is given in the following.
The ”Waterbed” effect.
Several papers concern changes in the effects of uni-
lateral climate policies under the phase IV cancellation mechanism.
Carlén et al.
(2019) study the effects of unilateral emission reduction policies in the light of the
newly introduced ”cancellation mechanism”, which invalidates of allowances in the
reserve exceeding the auctioning volume in a specific year. They point out that this
mechanism gives a single MS government power over the effective emission cap if it
decides to reduce emissions in a given year in a way that the cancellation mechanism
is triggered. Such overlapping policy would not be cost-effective (Carlén et al. 2019).
Similarly, Perino (2018) comments on the puncturing of the ”waterbed” of the EU
ETS, i.e.
”overlapping climate policies affecting who emits but not how much is
emitted in total”. He argues that the cancellation mechanism in the newest reform

will cause this to disappear and raises criticism due to an associated complexifica-
tion of climate policy-making, which is why he recommends a linkage of allowance
supply to their price. The Swedish National Institute of Economic Research (2018)
concludes that the cancellation mechanism has an increasing effect on short-term
emissions (cited after Beck and Kruse-Andersen (2018), as the publication is written
in Swedish). Burtraw, Keyes, and Zetterberg (2018) study the effect of unilateral cli-
mate policies in light of the cancellation mechanism and its implications for Swedish

the MSR incentivises low-carbon investments, though not through the postponement
mechanism but as a result of the cancellation of allowances
Price floors.
Other studies investigate minimum prices or price floors in the EU
ETS in comparison to a purely quantity-based mechanism: For instance, Flachsland
et al. (2018) argue for an EU ETS price floor and discuss ”Five Myths” falsely existing
about it. Also Burtraw and Themann (2018) recommend the introduction of a price
floor as it would remedy the waterbed effect and incentivise abatement.
Future allowance prices.
Research has also been conducted on changes in invest-
ment behaviour and allowance prices under the phase IV reforms. Perino and Willner
(2017) analyse the potential effects of the amendment proposals to the initial deci-
sion upon the introduction of a MSR by the European Parliament and Council (2015),
specifically the proposals of the European Commission (2015b) (increased linear reduc-
tion factor from 1.74% to 2.2%), of theEuropean Parliament (2017) (increased linear
reduction factor, double intake rate until 2023 and cancellation of 800m allowances
in 2021) and of the Council of the European Union (2017) (increased linear reduction
factor, double intake rate until 2023 and introduction of a cancellation mechanism)
on allowance price paths. While their results suggest that all policy scenarios lead
to an upward price shift in relation to the initial MSR (European Parliament and
Council 2015), differences across the scenarios considered are observed to be minor:
Whilst the amendment by the Council of the European Union would cause prices to
increase by about 3.5 % compared to the parliamentary amendment proposal due
to the cancellation of 900 million additional allowances, the ”doubling of the intake
factor has virtually no effect on the price path” (Perino and Willner 2017). Beck
and Kruse-Andersen (2018) make different observations from the comparison of the
MSR before and after its last amendment: Their results suggest a significant effect on
banked allowances and the MSR levels at only minor differences in allowance prices,
stating that the cap on allowances in the reserve has a ”small effect on firm behaviour
in the short and medium run”. They conclude however significant influence of the
changes in the MSR design on unilateral policy making, which they state to become

more complex due to the cap and they suggest to remove it. Furthermore, they point
out that the cap has an influence on the effects of electrification, which would lead
to higher emissions due to a larger allowance demand, hence a lower surplus and less
cancellation.

Different assumptions on investor be-
haviour can also be found: Bruninx, Ovaere, and Delarue (2018) model an equi-

emission allowance markets, whereas power companies are considered as rational and
risk-neutral agents with perfect foresight deciding upon investment in and operation
of power plants. They compare the case of the MSR as proposed by the European
Parliament and Council (2018) with a system without a MSR, but with an increased
LRF of 2.2 %; their results suggest allowance price increases being 10.5 % higher in
the case of the MSR coming along with emission reductions (Bruninx, Ovaere, and
Delarue 2018). Willner (2018) studies the effect of different degrees of firms’ market
foresight by varying both the length of time of perfect foresight and of their planning
horizon under the MSR in a two-period equilibrium model finding imperfect foresight
to decrease allowance prices in the short-term while increasing them in the long run.
Quemin and Trotignon (2019) model the EU ETS phase 4 reforms under uncertainty
and bounded investor rationality considering different degrees of market foresight
finding that the MSR would increase allowance prices in any case.

CHAPTER 4
Model Formulation
This chapter outlines the formulation of the main energy system model which is used
to analyse the impacts of the market stability reserve on optimal investment decisions
for the power system considered. The model is formulated as a two-stage stochastic
MIP model. Time horizons of 168 hours in three periods, 2020, 2030 and 2050, over
two seasons, which are modelled as electricity demand scenarios of equal probability,
are used. Hence, investments made in 2020 and 2030 respectively determine the units
available in in 2030 and 2050. Wind power production and EUA prices are considered
as parameters uncertain at the time of investment planning and uncertainty on them
is revealed before that start of the dispatch of power generation technologies. Invest-
ments are assumed to be made just at the beginning of a period, such that they are
available for operation in this period. 2020 is assumed period 0 for cost discounting.
Investments are considered first-stage decisions and the power dispatch are second-
stage decisions. For the investments after 2020, this is a simplification, as they could
in practice be decided upon after the uncertainty has been realised in 2020. However,
a multi-stage stochastic program would further increase complexity and computa-
tional burden.
Hence, the model can be regarded as the planning of investments
in the respective year prior to the start of the year 2020.
Section 4.0.1 outlines
data transformations applied to generate the set of generation technologies fed to the
model, which is in detail described in section 4.1.
4.0.1
Prior Data Transformations
Generation of set of power units.
First, several units from each technology
were created at different power ratings. It is necessary to consider investments as bi-
nary instead of continuous variables in order to model unit commitment behaviour of
individual units. The method for that is the following: For coal-, gas- and woodchips-
fired units, for each period, one unit is created at a power rating equal to half the
maximum demand over the year, a second one at a fourth of the maximum demand,
etc. For instance, if the maximum hourly demand in a year is 1000 MW, then one
generation unit with a power rating of each 500 MW, 250 MW, 125 MW and two
units with a power rating of 62.5 MW are created. That causes the set of thermal
generation units to have a dimensionality of 45.

This chapter describes the data considered in this thesis as well as its processing. First,
electricity demand scenario generation by means of linear time series forecasting and
scenario reduction are outlined (sect. 5.1). Second, the generation of representative
wind power scenarios from historical data is described (sect. 5.2). In section 5.3, the
agent-based model used to simulate EUA prices under different ETS policy regimes
Richstein (2015) is briefly described and scenarios his data. Finally, section 5.4 pro-

The power system modelled in this thesis is one that is meant to be representative
for ”some” Northern European country. While the generation technologies as well
as wind power and electricity demand data are based on Danish values, no attempt
is made to model the Danish power system. Hence, the data processing described in
this chapter shall not be falsely perceived to have an effort in such direction.

5.3
EUA Price Scenario Generation
In this study, allowance prices as found in the last chapter of Richstein (2015) are
used as data input, which is why his work is outlined in more detail than other studies.
After that, the process of obtaining scenarios from the data is described.
5.3.1
EMLab-Generation Model
Richstein (2015) uses the Agent-Based Model EMLab-Generation that is based on
the work of Chappin (2011) to investigate the interaction of structural EU ETS re-
forms with the European power sector. The model is described in Vries, Chappin,
and Richstein (2015) and Richstein, Chappin, and Vries (2014).
A brief overview is given in the following: The model comprises two zones, the British
and the Central Western European power and carbon markets, which are interlinked
and modelled at a time resolution of one year, with demand being considered through
20 distinct load blocks a year. The main agents are power generation firms who are
active in power, carbon as well as fuel markets and invest in power plants. They
are considered bounded rational and make forecasts based on historical market data.
Power markets are modelled as spot markets for which fuel mixes and resulting bid-
ding prices are determined at the beginning of each year through a linear program.
This as well as exogenous emission caps serve as an input for clearing the carbon mar-
kets: Building on that, assumptions of the power producers and their hedging needs
for allowances, the carbon and power market are jointly cleared at common emission
caps and prices. Electricity producers make forecasts of different quality and invest
in random order to maintain heterogeneity in the electricity mix. Governments are
assumed to fulfil policy targets in terms of RES commitments and invest in RES in
case the targets are not fully covered by private investors.
==PAGE:48==

37
5.3.2
Study Results
Based on this model, Richstein, Chappin, and Vries (2014) analyse the influence
of CO2 price floors and price ceilings on power and carbon markets, which they
consider to introduce a hybrid between purely quantity-based and purely Pigouvian
mechanisms. They recommend moderate price floors and ceilings, as they are found
to be able to reduce price volatility and lower the costs of a low-carbon transition for
consumers due to lower abatement costs and lower uncertainty for power producers.
In Richstein, Chappin, and Vries (2015) the effect of the introduction of the MSR in
the zones of the EMLab-Generation Model are analysed. The study was conducted
based on the corresponding proposal by the European Commission (2014) before
the amendment proposal by the European Parliament (2015). Hence, it is not only
based on an intake factor of 12% and a lower trigger level of 100 million allowances
through the entire horizon of the MSR, which had been changed in the, at the time
of writing, final version of the policy (European Parliament and Council 2018), but
also assumes the backloaded allowances to be auctioned in 2019 and 2020 (European
Commission 2014), an operation start of the MSR in 2021 and a response time of two
years, both amended in the decision of the European Parliament and Council (2015).
Their simulation results suggest significant price peaks from the introduction of the
MSR in 2021 until 2031 as a consequence of the upper trigger level of the reserve,
meaning the level of banked allowances from which on allowances are put into the
reserve, being set below the hedging needs of power producers. Power companies
foresee high future quota prices and increase banking as a consequence. Concluding
that the MSR is sensible to hedging assumptions and that the effect of the emergency
price trigger is only marginal, they recommend an adjustment of the trigger levels
and the introduction of moderate price ceilings and floors as not purely quantity
based measures. In the last chapter of his PhD thesis, Richstein (2015), revisits the
analysis made by Richstein, Chappin, and Vries (2014) and Richstein, Chappin, and
Vries (2015). He compares effects of a MSR as proposed by the European Commission
(2014) with one as suggested by the amendment proposal of the European Parliament
(2015), i.e. backloaded allowances are inserted in the MSR, which starts operation in
2019 and has a one year response time. The study is not based on the latest version of
the MSR (European Parliament and Council 2018). He gives an overview on studies
that were deducted on the same topic as the analysis made by Richstein, Chappin,
and Vries (2015) and introduces risk-aversity of investors through the imposal of
Conditional Value at Risk on top of the changes in the functioning of the reserve.
He observes that the introduction of risk-aversity leads to decrease in investments
in capital-intensive technology such as nuclear and coal-fired plants and that the
amendments to the MSR lead to a lower price volatility and the prevention of price
peaks illustrated in Richstein, Chappin, and Vries (2015). Hence, the amendment
is concluded to have positive effects on the functioning of the reserve and frequent
updates of the trigger levels based on empirical data are recommended.
Existing studies (Beck and Kruse-Andersen 2018; Perino and Willner 2017, c.f. e.g.)
suggest only minor differences in the effect of the introduction of a MSR as suggested

NoETS
Without consideration of carbon prices, the electricity supply is mostly
based on coal, with small investments in gas-fired capacity in 2020. Pumped hydro
plants (960 MW in 2020 and additional 24 MW in 2050) serve as a storage in the

investment costs per MW are considerably lower than for coal, which makes them
useful for following demand peaks (fig. 6.3).

hydro power brings a technical lifetime of 50 years, such that only minor reinvestments

6.3
Analysis of Economic Efficiency
Economic efficiency of emission abatement under CO2 prices depends not only on the
level and costs of the abatement but also on its timing: Different price curvatures that
yield the same average prices come along with different investment choices, not least
due to varying discount rates. This raises the question if the total abatement through-
out the planning horizon caused by the policy regimes analysed can be achieved at
lower costs.
Hence, the model has been run two additional times with EUA prices equal to zero
and upper bounds on the total emissions equal to the emissions in the pureETS and
MSR policy scenarios.
We can observe that no significant cost reductions (0.07%) can be achieved in the
pureETS scenario, while investment decisions remain almost identical (fig. 6.7). In
the MSR scenario, however, low-carbon investments shift from 2020 to 2050 with
15.8 GW of wind power and 920 MW of woodchips-fired capacity in 2050, while no
investments in renewable generation capacity is made in 2020 (fig 6.8). That leads
to cost reductions of 5.7%. This efficiency gain has two reasons: First, discounting
incentivises late abatement when imposing a cap on the total emissions, while un-
der the MSR, early abatement is more cost-efficient as a consequence of high carbon
prices in 2020. Second, in the calculation of total system emissions, which a cap is
set on, 2050 emissions are weighted at a higher factor than 2020 or 2030 emissions
(sect. 5.3), which makes emission abatement in that period more impactful.

6.4
Reflection upon Existing Studies and Limitations
Among the policy cases analysed, significant differences in system costs, emissions
and investment decisions only exist between the noETS scenario and the two ETS
scenarios. Under the given EUA prices, their clustering and the approximation of the
curve in the investment periods considered, the introduction of the market stability
reserve leads to emission reductions of ca. 25% at ca. 14% higher costs without and
about 10% higher system costs with consideration of ETS compliance costs.
This is caused by 2020 EUA prices being higher under the MSR policy scenario which
causes early investments in low-carbon technologies. While the solution found in the
pureETS case is economically efficient with respect to the timing of abatement, the
early abatement under the MSR is temporarily inefficient as later abatement would
be cost-optimal with respect to discounting of expenses.
The solution found by the model for the MSR scenario suggest convincing metrics:
At the penalty used, the relative VSS is significantly above the relative EVPI. The
first-stage decisions show robustness when evaluated on wind power and EUA price
scenarios not considered for the optimisation of the first-stage decisions.
Comparison to other studies.
The direction in which the results found in this
study point are in line with those of Richstein (2015), who works with the very
same data, even though absolute numbers vary: Richstein’s results suggest emission
levels in the MSR levels to be ca. 10% below those in the pureETS case, while this

from this study.
While the results of Bruninx, Ovaere, and Delarue (2018) suggest milder differences

Limitations.
These results are subject to the price simulations made by Richstein
(2015), which do not take the most recent legislation into account. Furthermore, they
depend on the data processing used in this study, as EUA price curvatures are ap-
proximated for the three investment periods considered. Under a different approach,
results may vary. Furthermore, the conclusion drawn here that marks the MSR to
cause economic inefficiency only implies such a bottom line when total emissions from
2020 to 2050 are measured. If the efficiency of emission abatement was to be anal-
ysed at the basis of a shorter time frame, the analysis changes. Also the approach
of approximating the total emissions over 30 years by taking the weighted sum of
emissions across investment periods can be argued to distort the results: Due to the
fact that a higher weighting is given to 2050 emissions when imposing an emission
cap, abatement in 2050 becomes more cost-effective than abatement in 2020.
Moreover, the model used here suggests several simplifications: Firstly, no existing
portfolio of power plants is considered. That way, investments in 2030 are generally
not cost-efficient due to discounting. Secondly, not more than three investment peri-
ods are used. This is an unrealistic assumption since power system investments take
place continuously. Thirdly, only a very limited range of technologies is taken under
consideration. If more generation technologies would be given to the model, more
granular adjustments between the pureETS and MSR scenario, both in generation
capacity and technology, would become achievable.

Simplex Method.
The Simplex method was first introduced by Dantzig, George
(1949).
A thorough illustration of the method is given by Hillier and Lieberman
(2001), which is summarised in the following: First, the problem is formulated in a
Simplex tableau, meaning that the system consisting of inequalities and equalities is
converted into a pure equality system using slack variables - slack variables measure
the distance of a constraint to its bound. Also, the objective function is included in
the tableau with slack variables measuring its distance from zero. An initial feasible
solution to the problem is obtained by starting at some corner point of the feasible
region and it is called a Corner-Point-Feasible Solution (CPF). At each iteration of
the method, it chooses the adjacent CPF that gives the highest improvement in the
objective value. If no improvement can be obtained, an optimal solution was found.
Branch-and-Bound Algorithm.
The Simplex method cannot guarantee a feasi-
ble solution, if at least one of the decision variables is discrete. The advantage of
such a problem however is that only a countable number of feasible solutions for the
integer variables exists. The Branch-and-Bound algorithm (Dantzig, Fulkerson, and
Johnson 1954) provides a method to explore this solution space.
Hillier and Lieberman (2001) illustrate this method as well: It follows the Divide-and-
Conquer principle in the sense of dividing (branching) the problem into sub-problems,
which are subsequently fathomed by obtaining bounds on their objective values. The
problem can be represented in a tree with the initial problem in the root and arcs
leading to the sub-problems in sequential order.
Initially, an ”LP relaxation” of the problem is solved meaning that the constraints
that bound the variables to take only integer values are lifted (binary variables can
be constrained between zero and one). The objective value of the solution to the
LP relaxation gives an upper bound to the objective value of the initial problem. If
the solution is within the feasible region of the initial problem, it is optimal for that
problem, as well. If not, the problem is divided by fixing one of the variables, for
instance a binary variable to zero or one or an integer variable to the closest integer
value that is within the feasible region. Subsequently, the LP relaxation of the sub-
problem is solved and an upper bound to that branch of the search tree is obtained.
The problem can then be fathomed (dismissed from further consideration) in three
different ways:

as the new best solution (incubent solution) and it need not be further branched; if

to further consider the branch; if the LP relaxation is infeasible, the entire branch

Pohekar and Ramachandran (2004) review applications of multi-criteria decision mak-

publications reviewed in the survey are the Analytical Hierarchy Process and outrank-

mixed integer problems. Zhou, Ang, and Poh (2006) categorise multiple criteria de-

The authors classified 252 publications related to decision making methods in energy
research. Out of those studies, 32 fall into subject areas related to energy system
planning and are listed as multiple objective decision making methods.
Aki, Oyama, and Tsuji (2003) apply a bi-objective model to urban energy planning,
by sequentially imposing constraints on CO2 emissions to a cost-minimising linear
program. Borges and Antunes (2003) introduce an interactive method that uses a
fuzzy linear model of energy flow in the Portuguese economy. For fixed degrees of
membership for the coefficients of the objective function and of the right-hand side
of the problem, indifference regions consisting of those combinations of weightings of
the three objectives minimisation of energy imports, maximisation of self-production
of electricity and minimisation of CO2 emissions that lead to the same basic Simplex
solutions are illustrated. The decision maker can then vary the degrees of membership
in order to explore uncertainty in the model in terms of changes in the indifference
regions. Antunes, Martins, and Sofia (2004) use an interactive approach to optimise

the Utupia point obtained from the LP relaxation of the multiobjective mixed integer
problem. Then, the solution to the LP relaxation closest to this point by Tchebysheff
distance is presented to the decision maker. In case the decision maker is satisfied,
the solution to the multiobjective integer program closest to the reference point is
calculated and considered as a solution to the problem. If he is not satisfied, he is
asked to provide a new reference point or to update his preferences regarding the
objectives in order to compute a new reference point.

are invested in in each of the three investment periods for each of the three thermal
generation technologies. Then, for each investment period, two Genomes represent
the amount of wind power and PHS capacity invested in divided by 50. This way, the
length of the bit list is reduced, while allowing investments at a resolution of 50 MW.
For each Genome, the mutation probability decreases linearly from 20 % for the most
right Chromosome to 2 % for the most left Chromosome. That means that for the
strings indicating investment decisions for thermal plants, mutation probabilities are
higher for low-capacity investments and for the RES bit lists, bits representing part
of a binary number with lower value mutate at a higher probability, This way, overall
expected mutation is maintained per newly built technology per year. A Population
is then a list of Individuals on which the operators outlined in the following are per-
==PAGE:81==
8.1 Algorithm Description
70
formed as methods. The class GA generates a list of Populations evolving from one
generation to the next.
Initial solutions.
As initial solutions, half of the population in the first genera-
tion is initialised from solutions to decomposed problems: Prior to the start of the
algorithm, the problem is solved separately for each season and wind power scenario.
These problems are called sub-problems from now on. Among those, solutions cor-
responding to half of the population size are randomly drawn and mutated. That
allows to introduce diversity while having some solutions that might be close to the
optimum of the master problem (the full, non-decomposed problem). The other half
of the population is initialised randomly. The combination of these two parts of the
population is likely to point the algorithm in the right direction: The solutions to
the sub-problems are tailored towards specific scenarios and seasons and thus a lot
of them feature too little invested capacity. The random solutions on the other hand
have each bit set to 1 with a probability of 0.5. That causes them to over-invest in
many cases.
Fitness/solution evaluation.
The solution variables determined by the genetic
algorithm correspond to investment decisions, i.e.
first-stage variables.
The sum
of the costs corresponding to the cost-optimal dispatch given these investments, i.e.
second-stage decisions, and the investment costs are considered the objective value
of a given solution. The dispatch is determined by a MIP solver, while the problem
can be fully decomposed into one sub-problem for each wind power scenario and
season and then the expected value of the recourse function can be determined as the
expected value of the objective values over the evaluation of the first-stage decisisons
on the sub-problems. This follows the implementation of (Till et al. 2007).
Tournament selection.
In order to find a mating pool from which an offspring
population is generated, tournament selection is applied to the current population.
For that, the fitness of all individuals in the present population is determined and
the mating pool is initialised as an empty population. Then, the following procedure
is repeated two times: A copy of the population is created and the order of the
individuals is shuffled up; an empty population to contain the winners of the current
round is initialised; the first two individuals from the shuffled population are taken
and the one with the lower costs is added to population of winners. At a draw, each
individual wins with an equal probability. Then, the next two individuals compete
until all individuals have participated in a competition. The individuals who won
are then added to the mating pool. This way, each individual competes in exactly
two competitions and can create two, one or zero copies of itself in the mating pool
depending on its fitness.
Random crossover.
From this mating pool, crossover is done in a similar way to
the tournament selection: the population is shuffled up twice and two individuals are
==PAGE:82==
8.1 Algorithm Description
71
randomly selected at each iteration. Here, however no fitness is compared but the
two individuals are randomly crossed over: For each Genome present, the attribute
from each parent is chosen at equal probability.
Mutation.
The mutation operators flips the bit in a Chromosome if a randomly
drawn number between zero and one is lower than the Chromosome’s mutation prob-
ability.
Elite preservation.
In each iteration, the best individuals from the parent and
offspring population build the population for the next generation. Since the fitness
evaluation is the main factor impacting performance (c.f. sect. 8.2), and the offspring
population is evaluated in any case, this implementation does not impact performance
significantly compared to comparing considering only individuals from the offspring
population for the next generation. As elite preservation, the best 10% of the best
individuals from the combination of parent and offspring population receive two copies
in the next generation, while the worst 10% are deleted.
Stopping criterion.
The algorithm stops if no significant improvement to the ob-
jective value was made for five consecutive generations. The improvement is measured
as the relative absolute difference of the fitness of the best individual from the current
population to the one from the last population. An improvement is deemed significant
if it is greater or equal than three percent.
8.1.2
Bi-Objective Genetic Algorithm
Building on the cost-minimising genetic algorithm, a bi-objective non-dominated eli-
tist genetic sorting algorithm exploring the Pareto-optimal front of system costs and
total CO2 emissions has been developed. The implementation uses functions and
classes from its single-objective brother to a large extent. The specific operators and
implementations of the method (Deb et al. 2000) that are added to or are different
from those in the single-objective heuristic are the following:
Initial solutions.
As initial solutions, solutions to the decomposed cost-minimising
problem are also used here. First, the complete problem is solved, however for a
time horizon of 24 hours with wind power and demand data clustered into 24 hours
intervals accordingly. Then, each sub-problem is solved five times, imposing a cap
on total CO2 emissions each time: In the first run, no cap is imposed. Then, a cap
equal to 80 % of the total emissions found in the problem reduced in time decreasing
by 20 % per iteration to 0 in the last run is imposed. From this list, being five times
longer than the list of initial solutions for the single-objective GA, random entries are
drawn as initial solutions for half the population, while the other half is initialised
randomly.
==PAGE:83==
8.2 Performance of Genetic Algorithms: Results & Discussion
72
Crowded tournament selection.
As a selection operator, the crowded tourna-
ment selection proposed by Deb et al. (2000) is used.
First, solutions are sorted
into non-dominated fronts by determining and deleting non-dominated solutions from
the population and adding them to a new population sequentially until no solu-
tions remain in the population.
The solutions taken in the first run are given a
non-domination rank of one, the ones that are non-dominated after the first non-
dominated front has been removed are given a rank of two, and so on. Additionally,
for each individual in the population, its crowding distance is determined as the max-
imum of the Euclidean distances to the two closest individuals in the objective space,
i.e. costs and emissions. Sorting the population by crowding distance from largest
to smallest gives a crowding distance rank on top of the non-domination rank. The
overall rank of an individual is then determined by first sorting the population by
non-domination rank and then sorting each front by crowding distance. This way, the
tournament selection always selects the individual lying on a lower non-dominated
front and if the two non-domination ranks are equal, it selects the individual with the
higher crowding distance. If both values are equal, each individual enters the mating
pool with equal probability.
Stopping criterion.
As a stopping criterion, the mutual domination rate indicator
proposed by Martí et al. (2016) is used. It measures the difference in the number of
solutions from the Pareto-optimal front in the last generation P∗

An extensive runtime analysis is out of the scope of this
study. Deb et al. (2000) and Deb (2011) provide some runtime analysis of the NSGA-
II, so only a brief discussion is given here. Since the approach used is a hybrid between
an evolutionary algorithm and decomposition of a MIP, the main influence factor on
runtime is the solution time for each sub-problem required by the MIP solver. At
each iteration all solutions, if not evaluated before, require the solution of six MIPs.

Each of these sub-problems requires well less than one second to be solved on the
DTU HPC.
In the worst case, the runtime for this part of the algorithm with respect to the
population size is O(n); simulation results roughly confirm this conclusion (fig. 8.10).

8.2.3
Reflections & Limitations
Both the single- and bi-objective hybrid methods achieve significant runtime improve-
ments over their conventional pendants. Especially the results of the single-objective
genetic algorithm however may be subject to the data at hand and the simplicity
of the problem considered, since the solutions to the sub-problems obtained through
decomposing by scenario are partly very close to the global optimum.
If all model parameters were to be optimised towards the very problem solved here,
no further insight into their general applicability would be gained. On the other hand,
the methods require further tweaking and optimisation of their parameters. This is
briefly touched upon in this chapter, further analysis however needs to make use of
more complex and more renown problems in order to make the conclusions drawn
here more profound.
Especially the bi-objective method that was developed shows promising results. The
runtime enhanced compared to the status quo at little expense of solution quality.
However, the Pareto-optimal front produced through the ϵ-constraint method per-
forms better in generation an even spread of solutions.
An, until that point, unmentioned advantage of search heuristics is that not only the
final optimal decisions can be assessed, but also solutions around them, which are
either present in the last iteration, but are dominated by other solutions, or which
were found in earlier generations and disregarded. That allows for a wider knowledge
of technology compositions as well as associated costs and system emissions, which
might be of of great use for policy makers and social planners who wish to consider
different technology choices.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
Initially, two research questions have been formulated (ch. 1), which can now be
answered. The first research question of this study is:
1. What is the effect of the introduction of a market stability reserve
on the cost-optimal investments for the power system of a small
Northern European country under EUA price and wind power uncer-
tainty? Does the market stability reserve impact economic efficiency
of emission abatement?
In chapter 6, model results were presented that suggest that the MSR leads to total
power system emissions by 2050 ca. 20% below levels achieved by prices projected
based on ETS phase III legislation (pureETS policy scenario). This is achieved at ca.
14% higher system costs. While the pureETS case leads to temporarily efficient abate-
ment, higher 2020 EUA prices under the MSR incentivise early abatement. That is
the reason why the same total emission level over the time horizon considered can
be achieved at ca. 5% lower discounted system costs. However, it also needs to be
concluded that the way EUA prices are approximated and emissions are calculated
is likely to influence the results.
The second research question formulated is:
2. What is a computationally efficient method to derive the front of
solutions Pareto-optimal in terms of emissions and costs in such a
power system?
In chapter 8 an overview to the field of multi-objective optimisation techniques rele-
vant to energy system modelling was given and the implementation of a single- and
a multi-objective genetic algorithm developed for this thesis has been outlined. Both
algorithms use a hybrid method of decomposing the stochastic program by scenar-
ios into sub-problems for the second-stage decisions, which are solved by as MIPs,
and apply genetic search on the optimisation of the first-stage decisions. Both al-
gorithms achieve significant performance improvements compared to the base line
(non-decomposed solution through a MIP solver in the case of the single- and sequen-
tial computation in the ϵ-constraint method through a MIP solver in the bi-objective
case). While especially the performance of the single-objective heuristic might be
==PAGE:97==

specific to the simplified problem considered, the bi-objective method appears to be
highly promising.
Future research.
Future research on the economic case study of the MSR could
include:
• The consideration of a model with a larger variety of generation technologies,
more investment periods and an existing portfolio of power plants
• Different approximations and processing methods of EUA prices as well as a
more granular computation of system emissions than taking the weighted sum
of the emissions of all investment periods
For future research on the evolutionary methods developed, the following might be
of interest:
• A more complex search space to evaluate the single-objective method on
• Considerations of parallel processing and more efficient decomposition tech-
niques
• Further optimisation of the parameters of the bi-objective method, such as
population size, mutation probability, stopping criterion and crowding distance
