Abstract
Using a simple MIMO radar designed and optimized speciﬁcally to function as parking assistance and lane change
assistance for cars, one can implement various method to optimize its function in an airborne environment.
This is done by implementing super resolution spectral estimators to increase its DOA estimating power.
In this thesis 3 such methods have been implemented, in addition to a regular fft for comparison, these are the Capon,
the MUSIC, and the root MUSIC methods. Firstly the method’s resolving powers, precision, and interference suppres-
sion were tested in simple simulations. It was here found that the MUSIC and Root MUSIC functioned identically in
all relevant situations, and thus, of the 2 methods, only MUSIC was investigated further.
Some simple experiments were done with the AWR1642 sensor, with the intention of demonstrating the practical ca-
pabilities of the spectral estimators.
Once they were deemed functional it was time to demonstrate them on more complex situations, involving observing
ground targets from a moving drone, and also observing a moving drone from the ground.
Observing from the drone turned out to be problematic since high data rates were not available from the DCA1000
capture card, and thus these tests are not heavily relied on in the conclusions.
The ground based tests had the high data rates required, but as it turns out the drone was far too small a target to
reliably observe, and thus the results from these tests were also cut short.
The advanced spectral estimators were able to identify and resolve target much better than the fft, and thus they are
considered valid methods for object detection, though with a drawback in the sense that they lose the absolute am-
plitude of their targets. The Capon method retains targets relative amplitude, but the MUSIC loses all information
of amplitude. This will often result in targets seemingly being wrong sizes. It is therefore not suggested that these
methods are implemented on their own.
As ﬁnal conclusions it is further suggested that; More complex and more controlled tests are conducted, that object
detection algorithms are implemented on top of spectral estimators, and that the sensor is updated for angular resolu-
tion in more than one direction.

INTRODUCTION

1
Introduction
In this project I have investigated methods for spectral estimation, and have successfully implemented them on a
MIMO sensor with angular resolution. Before implementing the spectral estimators, they were extensively validated
through simple simulations. At the end of the project the spectral estimators were tested on a practical scenario
involving a ﬂying drone, with the intention of identifying this drones location.
The use of automotive sensors is essential and widespread in a variety of areas, such as the car-industry and the ﬂight-
industry. Thus using RADAR in both manned and unmanned aircraft is an area in fast development, and of great
importance.
Helicopters were ﬁrst successfully operated in 1936, and have been mass produced since 1942. The use of helicopters
is widespread, including the transportation of cargo and people, ﬁreﬁghting, search and rescue, news and media, aerial
observation etc. The advantage of using rotary wing aircraft in contrast to ﬁxed wing aircrafts lie in the ability to
perform vertical take-offs, hover, and ﬂy in all directions, forwards, backwards, laterally, and vertically.
Often when helicopters are ﬂying they will have good visibility and therefore are able to land and perform manoeuvres
without much difﬁculty. However, if a helicopter has to be ﬂown under conditions or in places that are not optimal,
the pilot must have other means to rely on to identify a safe landing spot or if other object are near. This is an issue
e.g. if the visual conditions for the pilot are impaired. This can be due to darkness, brownouts, whiteouts, or blind
angles inside the vehicle. It is under these circumstances the use of RADAR and LIDAR come into play as these are
examples of means to detect safe landing spots [1].
For this project a small unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) rotary-wing, referred to as a drone, has been selected as a
suitable vehicle. As a larger vehicle, such as a helicopter, would not be manageable due to the scope of the project,
time, cost and planning for such a large vehicle would simply not be probable.
This thesis will focus on four main areas;
• 1. Theoretical considerations of RADAR, and spectral estimators.
• 2. Selecting and characterising a suitable sensor for performing off-line tests.
• 3. Simulating the performance of spectral estimators for simple scenes.
• 4. Using the sensor mounted on, or observing a small UAV.
In order to cover these focus areas, the thesis has been divided into 10 sections.
Section 1-2 introduces the problems, and the motivation for our way of dealing with them. Section 3, deals with the
theoretical aspects needed. First with some theoretical considerations on the technical aspects of radars, speciﬁcally
MIMO radars, and angular resolution. Different spectral estimators, Capon, MUSIC and Root MUSIC are also ex-
plained. Section 4 explains how and why the speciﬁc sensor used was selected. Section 5 explains how the sensor
was actually implemented into the project, and what problems occurred during usage, and where possible solutions
are also presented. In section 6 simple simulations of the spectral estimators are shown. This is done to show how
well they perform in different situations, and what to watch out for in practical work. Section 7 deals with the different
experimental setups conducted during the project, how the experiments were performed, and what the expectation for
each of them were. In section 8 the results from the experiments are analyzed together with an evaluation of the sensor,
and spectral estimator performance. Finally, in section 9 and 10, the results will be discussed and conclusions will be
made.

Motivation

Theory
This section will include theory on basic mechanisms in radar, as well as some more specialized areas such as; FMCW
radars, MIMO (Multiple Input Multiple Output) radars, and also different types of spectral estimators.
3.1
RADAR
Radars are essentially all based on most of the same theoretical and technical principles. These principles involve
electromagnetic radiation, wherein a current with known Phase, amplitude, frequency, is sent through a transmitter,
and transmitted as an electromagnetic wave. Upon hitting a target this wave is reﬂected, and part of it returns to a
receiver. When the reﬂected wave is observed it is processed using spectral estimation techniques.
3.1.1
Transmitter
The block diagram of a simple pulse radar can be seen in ﬁgure 1, where a low powered version of the ﬁnal signal is
generated in the waveform generator. This signal is then passed into the power ampliﬁer, which ampliﬁes the power
of the signal, for transmission. The transmitted signal lies at some high carrier frequency, ωc = 2π · fc. For a pulse
radar, this power ampliﬁer can be turned on and off by the pulse modulator, sometimes referred to as a "pulser" [4].
Once a pulse of appropriate power and form has been created, it is transmitted through the antenna of the radar, and
into space. That simple signal can be described by equation 3.1.
s(t) = a(t)·cos(ωc ·t +Φ(t))
(3.1)
Where a(t) is the amplitude of the signal, changing with time, and Φ(t) is a phase modulation, or coding of the pulse,
note that the information of the signal is contained herein.
3.1.2

The receiver part of the radar is separated from the transmitter by a duplexer, though they often share the same antenna.
The receiver pictured in ﬁgure 1 is superheterodyne. The duplexer shields the sensitive receiver structure from the high
energy of the transmitter, and instead feeds the returned signal into a Low Noise Ampliﬁer(LNA).

a multistage circuit. The formula is seen in equation 3.2, where Fn is the noise ﬁgure of the n’th element in the circuit,

Frequency modulated pulses
In order to increase the range resolution, without signiﬁcantly degrading other radar parameters, a technique called
pulse compression is often used. In the pulse compression technique a much longer duration pulse is used to obtain
the necessary power to illuminate targets. This pulse is then Frequency Modulated (FM), or Phase Modulated (PM),
to increase its bandwidth [4]. The modulated long pulse, with a bandwidth B, will then be compressed to reduce the
width of its mainlobe to 1/B, this is often achieved through matched ﬁltering. The matched ﬁltering allows the radar
to achieve the energy of a long pulse, without having a high peak power.

the signal starts at f1 and over the duration of the pulse goes to f2, corresponding to a bandwidth B = |f1 −f2|. If
the frequency rises through the chirp ( f2 > f1) it is called an up-chirp, if the frequency falls ( f1 > f2) it is called a
down-chirp.

3.5
MIMO Radar
The MIMO radar is a class of phased array antennas utilizing the orthogonality of signals from multiple transmitters
and receivers. The MIMO radar functions as a normal FMCW radar with respect to the doppler and range character-
istics. However its multiple signals allow for Direction Of Arrival (DOA) estimations, and in order to do this a model
for the MIMO array, and received signal is needed.
Figure 4: A uniform linear mimo array
Seen in ﬁgure 4 is the general form of a 2 element array. In most cases there will be more elements for a better angular
resolution, which leads to a better DOA. In this report only the Uniform Linear Array (ULA) will be considered, as the
actual sensor used later is setup in this conﬁguration. This means that a number of Rx antennas are placed on a line,
with an inter-element distance, d, and any Tx antennas, will be placed with an inter-element distance Nr ·d, where Nr
is the number of Rx antennas. This conﬁguration ensures that the physical receiver antennas, receiving a signal from
Tx1, will be located in their physical location, but the virtual receivers, receiving a signal from Tx2, will be placed
Nr ·d besides their physical locations, and so forth for more transmitters.
With this condition it is ensured that there is no overlap between the channels, and this MIMO will function as an 8
channel virtual array with all 8 antennas spaced a distance d apart.

3.6
Spectral estimators for DOA
Spectral estimation is used extensively in signal processing to get information about received signals. In the simplest
terms spectral estimation is: "From a ﬁnite record of a stationary data sequence, estimate how the total power is dis-
tributed over frequency." [12]. With this in mind many methods have been developed based on different approaches. In
this section, four such methods will be reviewed. two are non-parametric methods; the Fourier transform and Capon,
and two parametric methods, MUltiple Signal Input Classiﬁcation (MUSIC), and root MUSIC.
The difference between parametric and non parametric methods lies in the assumptions that are made regarding a
signal.
In the parametric approach the spectrum is parameterized by means of a ﬁnite dimensional model, whereas the non-
parametric method, instead assumes that the spectrum is constant over some small bandwidth, β << 1, for a normal-
ized bandwidth (−π,π) [12]. This means that the parametric methods will require some knowledge about the statistics

The Capon method, will have some problems depending on the data set supplied. In some cases degradation of the
Signal-Interference-Ratio (SIR) will occur, and in other cases the algorithm might completely destroy the signal, im-
plementing a robust Capon algorithm can prevent some of this.
In source [14] an explanation is given on how a signal can be destroyed by the Capon algorithm. If the steering vector
is mismatched, the target at θ can be interpreted as an interference, and thus be suppressed. On the other hand, if the
sample size is small, degradation can also occur. The paper therefore suggests a way to implement a technique very
similar to the Capon, but in a way that is robust against these types of errors.
In the case that no noise is present in the signal, the REE is not a positive deﬁnite matrix, and the existence of ˆR−1 is
no longer guaranteed, thus preventing the algorithm from working at all. This can also lead to unstable answers due
to numerical instabilities in computer programming.
The Capon method has empirically been found to perform much better than the standard beamforming technique.
Their common advantage is that they do not require knowledge of the statistics of the data. They only require the data
themselves, which makes them quite easy to use, but also inhibits their usefulness. The latter especially occurs when
this information is available. In cases like this, one might opt to use a parametric method instead, which can obtain
much higher resolutions.

5.1.5
Utilizing the visualizer
It seems clear, that while this visualizer is capable of utilizing all the radar data it measures, it does not allow for the
user to get access to most of the data, which severely limits its usefulness. With that being said, it is still possible to
use this visualizer for some simple static tests, which was necessary due to limited access to the DCA1000EVM. It
should be noted, that many more parameters and other plots are available, than has been explained here, but they are
only used to change how the plots from the visualizer function, and have no affect on the actual data output from the
visualizer.
In order to use the data from the visualizer, it is necessary to access the .cfg ﬁle from a selected run, seen in ﬁg-
ure 12, as "Save conﬁg to pc". The conﬁg ﬁle contains all the necessary information to process the data, as speciﬁed
in [20].
The data ﬁle is in a binary format, and its structure is explained in a document available when downloading the TI
cloud agent, [19]. The data ﬁle is structured as follows; for each frame there is a header containing information about
the ﬁle, after the header there is a tag, which is the name of the plot about to be read, then comes information about the
length of the plot about to be read, then the actual data containing the radar data. This then repeats for all the outputs
selected in the setup of the visualizer, and for each frame.
The setup of the header is as follows; the ﬁrst 4 16bit values are initialization bits, and must always be 0x0102 0x0304
0x0506 0x0708, this is to ensure that the frame has started correctly. The next 7 32bits contains some relevant param-
eters for the speciﬁc run, in order: Version of the visualizer, total length of the frame, platform used, frame number,
time in cpu cycles, number of detected objects, and number of TLVs (Type-Length-Value), then comes the data.
All data is in 16bit format. The ﬁrst data packet, is a list of detected objects, ﬁrst the number of detected objects in the
frame, and then their position in xyz format, in range and doppler bins.
The next data packets, is the range proﬁle, which is 16bits in Q8 format, starting at range bin 0 until (R-1).
The noise proﬁle is given in the same way.
The static heat map data, is complex and given as imaginary ﬁrst, real second, with antenna 0 at range 0 ﬁrst, and then
up to antenna (N-1) at range 0, then increasing the range bin and ending at antenna (N-1) at range (R-1).
The range doppler heat map is given as the ﬁrst range bin 0 at doppler bin 0, then up until range bin 0 at doppler bin
(D-1), then increasing the range bin and ending at range bin (R-1) at doppler (D-1).
The last output is some stats about the processing time, and are not relevant to the data, it contains six 32bit words.
Finally the frame is padded, until its total length is a multiple of 32 bytes.

Limitations on the visualizer

Simulations of spectral estimators
In order to test how the different spectral estimators perform under optimal, or less-than-optimal but still controlled
conditions, simulations were performed in Matlab.
These simulations were made to test the Fourier transform, the Capon, the MUSIC and the Root MUSIC, and how they
react to changing different parameters, such as the number of targets, the spread of targets (resolution), how different
Signal-to-Interference-Ratios (SIR) affects them, and how many snapshots are required for them to work.
They are evaluated visually based on ﬁgures made from single iterations, but also based on 3 key parameters, SIR,
resolution, and precision from a number of iterations.
The SIR is deﬁned slightly differently depending on the method. For the 3 methods (Capon, MUSIC, and root MU-
SIC) it is deﬁned as the difference between the largest amplitude (target), and the average of values taken in a ﬂat part
of the spectrum, where no target is located.
For the FFT, it is deﬁned as the difference between mainlobe amplitude, and highest sidelobe amplitude. This is done
because the 3 other methods do not have sidelobes in the same sense as the fft.
Resolution is here deﬁned as the 3dB beamwidth, ie. taking the place of largest amplitude, and then going 3dB down
and to each side, and giving this width in degrees.
The precision is deﬁned as the point of the largest amplitude subtracted from the actual target location.
The comparison made here are course not fair for the fft, as it only uses a single snapshot, where the other methods
always have to use several, or their algorithms break down. This gives them a clear advantage in terms of amount of
available data, so the usual fft-based periodogram is expected to perform worse in nearly every circumstance, and will
therefore primarily be included for reference purposes.
The added noise component is here circularly-symmetric complex Gaussian, centered on zero, with a standard devia-
tion 1/10th of the targets amplitude, corresponding to a 10dB SNR.

6.0.1
Summary of key points
At optimal conditions the 3 methods are capable of suppressing about 30dB of interference more than the fft.
The functionality of the algorithms can be worse, or degrade in performance, if the targets are correlated. This is not
expected to be a problem, as targets are not expected to be completely stationary, and any movement in the order of
less than a millimeter will cause decorrelation.
When selecting the number of snapshots, be wary of the processing time required and the targets movement. If the
target moves too fast it would be preferable to either select a higher chirp rate or endure a lower number of snapshots,
and thus worse suppression, rather than the target moving out of the range/azimuth bins.

9
Conclusions and perspectives
In this Section conclusions and perspectives are given on three main focus areas; The performance of Capon and MU-
SIC, based on both simulations and practical cases, Some conclusions regarding the sensor chosen for this project, and
ﬁnally the tests conducted during the project
9.1
The performance of Capon and MUSIC
After comparing and analyzing many aspects of these preliminary tests a recap is necessary to focus on the important
parts. The spectral estimators have performed reasonably well when applied to simple tests and succeeded to some
extent in detecting targets corresponding to physical objects. However, some of these targets did not always stand
out clearly in the spectrum, and could therefore be difﬁcult to detect as actual objects. This was especially apparent
in the raw testing where realistic scenarios were applied, and due to the small radar cross section of the drone it was
simply not visible most of the time. In these speciﬁc cases the solution was to utilize the range doppler heatmap, and
identify the drone only when it was moving radially. Apart from this the spectral estimators also have problems if an
insufﬁcient number of snapshots were applied, or in the MUSIC case when a sup-optimal value for Ntar was chosen.
In order to solve parts of this, one would have to design a proper method for identifying Ntar, which is vital in the
MUSIC algorithm performance.
Another part of the problem has a simple solution, that is; the low number of snapshots, which was seen in the simula-
tion section to reduce the performance of the algorithms. Here it would be necessary to collect more data. This would
have improved the resolution capabilities of Capon and MUSIC. By collecting more data the covariance matrix would
be closer to its theoretical values, and thus the algorithms could provide better results. The algorithms would then be
susceptible to the movement of targets, ruining the covariance matrix, so the maximum number of snapshots would be
limited by this.
A further problem, which is more complex, is the intensity of the targets. As could be seen by comparing the different
methods, even when the fft had very little total power in its spectrum, MUSIC often had a target with intensity of
almost 0dB. In the cases with a corner reﬂector it is supposedly many times larger than the second largest target, and
yet secondary targets would show up in MUSIC with almost the same intensity.
While this is good for detecting the object, it means that one cannot trust the size of the object by focusing solely on
the MUSIC algorithm. Capon was much better in this regard. It would often place targets at very low intensity, while
still clearly showing a target being present, by reducing the interference further.
9.1.1
Suggestion for further work
It is suggested that the visualizer is discarded and instead the focus should be on the mmWave studio, which can utilize
the DCA1000 capture card, and therefore obtain raw data.
The user experience would heavily improve by having access to the raw data, and the increased ﬂexibility in the system
would also be very welcome.
Improvements should also be made to Capon and MUSIC. This could include a method to automate the number of
snapshots to be used, perhaps by utilizing the range doppler to ensure that things are stationary over the duration of all
snapshots.
It could also improve functionality by a large margin, if some object tracking/detection methods were implemented.

This would allow the user to see the objects much more efﬁciently, and could also make the selection of Ntar much
simpler.
9.2
Regarding the sensor
The AWR1642 used in this thesis, functioned to a certain degree in the limited testing scenarios conducted during the
project. However, it is expected to have major issues when applied to more complex scenarios.
The sensor is extremely short range, only capable of some 70 meters max range. This will not be enough in practical
scenarios involving multiple ﬂying objects, since from the time an object is detected an avoidance manoeuvre might
not be possible.
The sensor only has angular resolution in one direction. This combined with range resolution leads to a 3 dimensional
world being projected into a 2 dimensional radar map. While this functions well for a parking sensor that only has to
operate in 2D, it will not function well for airborne applications.
9.2.1
Suggestions for improvements
Two of the same sensors could be run in tandem, with each having angular resolution in a different direction. This
would lead to a complete 3D map. The difﬁculty here would be to correlate the outputs of the two sensors.
Another solution would be to design a sensor. This could still be a fairly simple sensor, but would need some opti-
mization for the speciﬁc application, ie. one could make a squared (NxM) array instead of line array (Nx1).
9.3
The execution of test plan
Though a test plan was made, the mode of preparations and the lack of experience to perform a set up of this kind,
resulted in difﬁculties when it came to interpreting the data. The practical setup and performance including details
of distances and angles, between both stationary and moving items, was not accurately noted, since the main focus
was on the operating procedures and the functionality of both the sensor and the drone. The lack of knowledge on the
importance of measuring distances etc. of the test area proved to be crucial when it came to interpreting and using data.
When this was realized the process was so far ahead that it was not possible to conduct new tests.
9.3.1
Suggestions for testing
A suggestion would be to perform the same test scenarios once more, but in more controlled environments. This
would include more set ways for record the physical scene, which would lead to much less confusion later. However
it could also be to ﬁnd a better test scene, with less interfering objects as a way to start. Then later the testing could be
expanded to scenes with more interference.
