IV 
 
Abstract: 
Photochemistry is a rapidly expanding field of chemistry used in various organic syntheses. One of 
the best-known photochemical reactions is the [2+2]-cycloaddition used to create cyclobutanes which 
is becoming an increasingly interesting motif for drug discovery. A very prominent scaffold seen in 
many approved drugs is pyrones and benzopyrones which makes them ideal as core scaffolds. In this 
project photochemical [2+2]-cycloadditions were developed and applied to various natural product-
derived pyrone-based scaffolds to add the cyclobutane motif. Screening different photocatalysts and 
light sources enabled the identification of optimal reaction conditions: isopropylthioxanthone (ITX) 
with 375 nm UV light. Especially for benzo fused pyrones good yields between 37-85% was obtained, 
but a general trend was observed with lower yields for crossed products compared to straight prod-
ucts. Seventeen new pyrone-based scaffolds containing cyclobutanes were successfully synthesized 
and characterized. It was discovered that certain reactions could yield different constitutional isomers 
based on which light source was used. Lastly multiple mechanistic studies were conducted which 
suggested that the [2+2]-cycloaddition reacts through a triplet excited mechanism with free radicals.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Cyclobutanes in natural products and drugs: 
Cyclobutanes are cyclic alkanes consisting of four carbons to form a strained cyclic compound. Even 
though cyclobutanes are very strained and hence can be hard to synthesize they are found in many 
natural products. (±)-grandisol, solanoeclepin A and repraesentin F are just a few of the known nat-
ural compounds which contain a central cyclobutane motif (Figure 1)1. 
 
Figure 1: Natural products (±)-grandisol, solanoeclepin A and rapraesentin F. 
(±)-grandisol was first isolated from boll weevils and the compound has been used as a pesticide to 
protect cotton1. Solanoeclepin A was first found in potato roots and has been linked as a natural hatch-
ing agent2. Finally repraesentin F has been found to exhibit growth regulation on plants3. 
Cyclobutanes are considered an important motif in drug discovery and drug improvements. Cyclobu-
tanes are sp3 rich compounds which can help increasing a drugs overall fraction of sp3 hybridized 
carbons (Fsp3). Increased Fsp3 has been shown to be an important feature for drug candidates clinical 
success rate since increased 3D structure allows for more optimal filling of a binding site and gener-
ally increases solubility. Cyclobutanes is a more rigid source of sp3 carbons which induces conforma-
tional restriction and also increases metabolic stability compared to its aliphatic counterpart4,5. 
All these qualities of cyclobutanes have led researchers to determine whether it is possible to add 
cyclobutanes into known biologically active compounds to improve features such as metabolic sta-
bility, while still retaining the same biological activity. This was investigated for a compound found 
as a potent inverse agonist against related orphan receptor 𝛾t which is related to various autoimmune 
diseases (Figure 2)6.  
==PAGE:12==
2 
 
 
Figure 2: Improvement of drug linker by introducing a cyclobutane6. 
In this case cyclobutane was used as an alternative to an aliphatic linker and thereby lowering the 
flexibility of the linker. It was proposed that the more rigid linker would lessen the entropy loss upon 
binding, but also increase the metabolic stability of the compound.  
Cyclobutanes are not very common in approved drugs (Figure 3) even though it is widely found in 
natural products. 
 
Figure 3: Cyclobutanes in approved drugs: mirogabalin and carboplatin. 
Mirogabalin is a pain reliver of diabetic peripheral neurophatic pain and carboplatin is a well-known 
chemotherapeutic from the group of alkylating agents7,8. These two compounds are some of the very 
few approved drugs containing a core cyclobutane.  
Cyclobutanes can be accessed synthetically through various starting materials and reaction condi-
tions. There is thermal [2+2]-cycloaddition which is formed upon heating of the substrates, photo-
chemical [2+2]-cycloaddition which is catalysed by light, through contraction of pyrrolidines using 
iodonitrene and more9. 
The lack of cyclobutanes in approved drugs is an intriguing field of research and the number of natural 
products containing cyclobutanes shows it could be important for certain biological activity. This 
theory is further reinforced as per January of 2021, there were 39 preclinical/clinical drug candidates 
containing cyclobutanes5. Synthetic ways of incorporating cyclobutanes into natural-product based 
scaffolds is therefore an important target for synthesising new biologically active compounds.

1.2 Pyrones: 
Pyrones are composed of an unsaturated six-membered ring containing an oxygen and a ketone and 
is a core scaffold in various natural products. There are two different types of pyrone isomers known 
as α-pyrone and γ-pyrone (Figure 4). 
 
Figure 4: Structures of 𝛼- and 𝛾-pyrone. 
For α-pyrone there are natural products such as coumarin and dibenzo-α-pyrone which are both in 
the class of benzopyrones (Figure 5). 
 
Figure 5: 𝛼-pyrone containing compounds. 
Both coumarin and dibenzo-𝛼-pyrone are made from fungi and plants and are important metabolites. 
Dibenzo-𝛼-pyrone has a broad spectrum of biological activities ranging from cytotoxicity to being 
an antioxidant. Dibenzo-𝛼-pyrone is also a key intermediate in synthesis of cannabinoids, progester-
one and androgens10. 
For 𝛾-pyrone there are natural products such maltol, kojic acid and flavone (Figure 6). 
 
Figure 6: 𝛾-pyrone containing compounds. 
Maltol is extracted from the bark of larch trees, is a known iron chelating agent and is approved as 
treatment of iron deficiency anaemia11,12. Kojic acid is produced by several species of fungi such as 
==PAGE:14==
4 
 
A. flavus-oryzae and A. tamarii and is widely used in food and cosmetic preservatives and just as with 
maltol it is also a known iron chelating agent13. Lastly flavone is from the flavonoid family and are 
synthesised in various plants. Flavones plays an important role in protecting the plant from UV-B 
(280-315 nm). Furthermore, flavones have been found to have anti-tumour activities and reducing of 
blood cholesterol levels14,15. 
Pyrones are a central part of numerous bioactive compounds as well as known drugs. Warfarin, Tri-
oxsalen and Flavoxate are just a few of already approved drugs which contains a central pyrone motif 
(Figure 7). 
 
Figure 7: Drugs containing pyrone. 
Warfarin is a known anticoagulation agent which contains a coumarin as its core. Trioxsalen also 
carries a central coumarin and is a potent drug against acute and chronic psoriasis and is furthermore 
used in phototherapy of hand eczema. Lastly Flavoxate contains a central flavone motif and is an 
approved drug used to treat overactive bladders. Many other natural products containing pyrone cores 
are under clinical investigation and is generally a very important motif in medicinal chemistry16. 
In addition to their biological relevance, pyrones has been studied in a broad range of reactions to 
create natural product-like scaffolds by groups such as Foley et al17 (Figure 8). 
 
Figure 8: [5+2]-cycloadditions of pyrone scaffold17. 
These compounds are formed through a silyl transfer-induced [5+2]-cycloaddition which leads to 
these bridged scaffolds. Then further reactions of the bridged scaffolds led to a broad range of natural 
product-like compounds.

1.3 Photochemistry 
Photochemistry is a branch of chemistry that works with reactions which are catalysed by the absorp-
tion of light. This type of chemistry is often coupled with a photocatalyst/photosensitizer which are 
excited by light and then undergoes energy transfer with a substrate. Photocatalysts and energy trans-
fer will be expanded on in the next chapter. Photochemistry has been used for many different reactions 
and research continuously keeps expanding the scope of this branch of chemistry. 
An example of a photochemical reaction is a rearrangement reaction of a γ-pyrone to an 𝛼-pyrone by 
Komogortsey et al18 (Figure 9). 
 
Figure 9: Photochemical rearrangement of 3-hydroxypyran-4-ones. Reproduced from Komogortsey et al18. 
They propose this reaction is possible because the formation of an anion lowers the energy gap be-
tween LUMO and HOMO which leads to change in photochemical behaviour. This also meant they 
could use light at 450 nm which lowers the amount of photodegradation compared to higher energy 
light. The proposed mechanism starts with photochemical excitation of the anionic species followed 
by an electron push from the anion that leads to various rearrangement steps which in the end forms 
the final α-pyron species18. 
1.4 [2+2]-cycloadditions 
The [2+2]-cycloaddition is a widely studied photochemical reaction used to form four-membered 
rings from the reaction of two unsaturated species. Normally a [2+2]-cycloaddition between two al-
kenes wouldn’t occur since there is the same amount of bonding vs antibonding interactions and 
therefore no driving force for the reaction. However, when one of the substrates in the [2+2]-cycload-
dition is excited, the reaction can be favourable (Figure 10). 
==PAGE:16==
6 
 
 
Figure 10: Normal vs excited orbital mixing. 
As seen on the left side orbital mixing leads to two electrons going down in energy while two electrons 
go up in energy hence no driving force for the reaction. However, when one double bond is excited 
one electron is sent to a higher energy orbital which now makes the [2+2]-cycloaddition favourable 
since only one electron will go up in energy while three electrons goes down in energy19. The example 
given in figure 10 is of an excited singlet state, but through intersystem crossing (ISC) it is possible 
to change into a triplet excited state this is best shown through a Jablonski diagram (Figure 11). 
 
Figure 11: Jablonski diagram. Reproduced from Edinburgh Instruments20. 
The molecules ground state is known as S0 which can be irradiated to its S1 (or to its S2) state which 
is the excited singlet state. The energy can then be released again through vibrational or internal con-
version to reach the lowest energy level of S1. Then through emission of a photon known as fluores-
cence the energy level can return to ground state S0. Instead of emission of a photon from S1 an ISC 
can occur where the electron spin is changed shown as the turquoise arrow and then reaches triplet 
state. This excited triplet state is longer lived than the excited singlet state since a change in spin is 
now required before returning to ground state. Return to ground state S0 can then occur through phos-
phorescence which is longer lived than fluorescence due to the forbidden energy transition. Depend-
ing on the [2+2]-cycloaddition goes through a singlet excited state, or a triplet excited state may lead 
==PAGE:17==
7 
 
to different outcomes of the reaction. The triplet state cannot form the final ground state product 
before another ISC has occurred which slows the final bond-forming steps21. An example of different 
results from singlet- vs triplet excited state was observed by Madhavan et al22. 
 
Figure 12: Photodimerization of acenaphthylene. Reproduced from Madhavan et al22. 
It was proposed that acenaphthylene (ACN) alone is excited to a singlet state which then reacts with 
a ground state molecule to form exclusively the cis product (Figure 12). With a photosensitizer it was 
possible to excite ACN in a triplet state that instead led to a mixture of the cis- and trans product22. 
Photosensitizers are a very useful tool to excite molecules into their triplet states because they have 
very fast ISC from their singlet excited state to their triplet excited state21. This triplet state must be 
relatively long lived since they need to interact with the substrate to transfer the triplet energy. This 
energy transfer is known as the Dexter energy transfer (Figure 13). 
 
Figure 13: Dexter energy transfer. Reproduced from Strieth-Kalthoff et al23. 
Firstly, the photocatalyst is excited into its singlet excited state where it rapidly undergoes ISC into 
its excited triplet state. The triplet energy state is then transferred to the substrate while the photo-
catalyst returns to its ground state where it can again repeat the photocatalytic cycle. For successful 
energy transfer it is required that the photocatalyst has a higher energy triplet state than the substrate 
in question. 
==PAGE:18==
8 
 
A broad range of photocatalysts including both organic photocatalyst and organometallic photocata-
lysts are known and used for different reaction conditions (Figure 14). 
 
Figure 14: Organo photocatalysts vs organometallic photocatalyst. 
Benzophenone and isopropylthioxanthone are both excited by UV light while the rest are excited by 
blue light in the visible spectrum. For organometallic photocatalysts there are iridium complexes but 
also other transition metals such as rhodium and ruthenium are widely used. The lifetime of the ex-
cited triplet state is important since it must interact with a substrate thus a longer-lived excited triplet 
state the more likely it is to interact with a substrate before returning to ground state.  
[2+2]-cycloadditions can proceed as intra- or intermolecular [2+2]-cycloadditions and both types are 
well documented in the literature. Intermolecular [2+2]-cycloaddition can proceed via a head-to-head 
interaction or a head-to-tail interaction (Figure 15). 
 
Figure 15: Head-to-head vs head-to-tail. Reproduced from Saner et al24. 
==PAGE:19==
9 
 
The favoured regioisomer of a given intermolecular [2+2]-cycloaddition can be hard to predict. 
Sometimes a mixture is obtained and sometimes exclusively one regioisomer (Figure 16). 
 
Figure 16: Example of head-to-head vs head-to-tail. Reproduced from Kirschning21. 
In the example above four products are made and through a diradical mechanism an almost 1:1 mix-
ture of head-to-head vs head-to-tail is observed. Other factors such as steric hinderance and dipole-
dipole repulsion also plays a role in the final outcome of the reaction21. For intramolecular [2+2]-
cycloaddition regioselectivity is well documented and determined by the length between the alkene 
and the excited species (Figure 17). 
 
Figure 17: Crossed vs linear product of intramolecular [2+2]-cycloadditions. Reproduced from Graßl et al25. 
An explanation for these outcomes can be described by the “rule of five” which states that the product 
will be formed via a 1,5-ring closure and a five-membered ring diradical will be formed26. This 1,5-
ring closure has also been proposed by Graßl and coworkers25 (Figure 18). 
==PAGE:20==
10 
 
 
Figure 18: Proposed mechanism of crossed – product. Reproduced from Graßl et al25. 
The crossed product here follows the five-membered ring diradical which suggest it indeed follows 
the “rule of five”. This can also be applied to the straight product (Figure 19). 
 
Figure 19: Straight product formation. 
The intramolecular [2+2]-cycloaddition usually yields a racemic mixture, but using enantioselective 
photocatalysts it can be possible to obtain enantiopure products. This has been done by Skubi and 
coworkers27 (Figure 20). 
 
Figure 20: Enantioselective intramolecular [2+2]-cycloaddition. Reproduced from Skubi et al27. 
Here the enantioselectivity is enforced by a complexation between the iridium-catalyst and the sub-
strate through a hydrogen bonding domain in the catalyst ligand27. This enantioselectivity is directly 
==PAGE:21==

based on interaction with the ligand, but there are other types of interactions that can lead to photo-
catalytic enantioselectivity. Another example on the enantioselective reaction of crossed intramolec-
ular [2+2]-cycloadditions was shown by Rigotti and co-workers28 (Figure 21). 
 
Figure 21: Enantioselective photocatalytic [2+2]-cycloaddition. Reproduced from Rigotti et al28. 
In this case a rhodium complex works both as the photocatalyst, but also a Lewis acid which plays a 
key role in the catalytic step which leads to the final enantiopure product. An important feature of the 
catalyst was also that it was excited at a long wavelength so the complex could be selectively excited. 
[2+2]-cycloadditions are not limited to only alkene-on-alkene but can also be used to synthesize ox-
etanes and azetidines. These species can be formed through a Paternó-Büchi reaction and azetidines 
has been synthesized this way by Becker and co-workers29 (Figure 22). 
==PAGE:22==

Figure 22: Azetidine synthesis via photochemical [2+2]-cycloaddition. Reproduced from Becker et al29. 
The scope of their project was to make functionalized azetidines via an excited triplet state of an 
alkene which then reacts in a [2+2]-cycloaddition with imines to make the azetidine. Imines are nor-
mally excited to an excited singlet state and can undergo radiation less decay upon bond rotation 
which usually makes the excitation of the imine unfavorable29.

2. Aims and objectives 
This project aims to synthesize various cyclobutanes through photocatalyzed [2+2] – cycloadditions 
from 𝛼- and 𝛾-pyrone-based scaffolds (Figure 25).  
 
Figure 25: Aims and objectives. 
Starting material will be synthesized from a variety of different pyrones through alkylation in the enol 
position. The alkylated species will undergo photocatalyzed [2+2] – cycloadditions to yield linear or 
crossed - product based on the alkene-linker. Different reaction conditions such as solvent, photocata-
lyst, and light source/wavelength will be evaluated to assess stereoselectivity, yield, degradation, ki-
netics and reaction mechanism. The influence of the different “core” pyrones will also be assessed 
regarding yield and kinetics.

3.1 Setup: 
Two different setups were used for the [2+2]-cycloaddition project and one of them was a UV light 
reactor with a wavelength of 375 nm. This included eight 10 W UV LEDs in a repurposed TLC 
chamber to shield the user from the UV light (Figure 26). 
 
Figure 26: UV reactor setup. 
20 mL glass vials were placed in front of the UV light and irradiated. The setup is equipped with a 
cooling fan and placed on a stirring plate. The UV reactor used is the one described by Bargum et 
al31.  
The second reactor was a blue light reactor with a wavelength of 465 nm. This included four 10 W 
LEDs together in a 3D printed PETG photoreactor (Figure 27). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:26==
16 
 
 
Figure 27: Blue light reactor. 
The light source was shielded in a part of a yellow clinical waste bucket partially wrapped in alumin-
ium foil. The core was 3D-printed and had a lid for 1 mL or 20 mL vials right above the light source. 
The cores were placed on a stirring plate and had an external cooling fan to reduce overheating within 
the shielding box.

3.5 Mechanistic studies 
The [2+2]-cycloaddition is proposed to go through a diradical mechanism as suggested by Graßl and 
co-workers25 (Figure 44).

form the [2+2]-cycloaddition product. A range of experiments were conducted to try and prove the 
existence of a diradical state which could support this mechanism.

4. Conclusion and future work: 
Seventeen new cycloaddition scaffolds were synthesised, isolated and characterised from various al-
kylated starting materials. Optimal conditions for [2+2]-cycloaddition were found through thorough 
screening and used for the [2+2]-cycloadditions. For the flavone-based reactions it was observed that 
introductions of methoxy groups in 6 and 4’ position lowered the yield of the crossed product but 
slightly increased the yield for the straight product. Addition of methoxy group in 3’ position seemed 
to yield similar results as 2a and 3a. More branched alkenes generally lowered the overall yields. 
Three unexpected products were found and isolated, being 14a, 15a and 16a, which all could be 
controlled with varying reaction conditions. 
For the rest of the [2+2]-cycloadditions a general trend was found where crossed product had slower 
reaction times which led to lower yields than for the much faster and higher yielding straight products. 
A photocatalytic degradation dependency was found for maltol, kojic acid and pyromeconic acid and 
proved that ITX significantly increased the degradation of the final [2+2]-cycloaddition product. For 
comanic acid only the homoallylated ester starting material would undergo [2+2]-cycloaddition. 
A broad mechanistic study was made to try and prove the reaction going through an excited triplet 
state with a diradical mechanism. No electron donor-acceptor complex was found, and no strain in-
duced [2+2]-cycloaddition was observed. The TEMPO experiment did not yield a definitive answer 
since no TEMPO-adducts were found, but since the reaction was completely blocked it seems to 
confirm a radical mechanism. Lastly the triplet energy blocking experiment with naphthalene sug-
gested that it could block the reaction of slower reactions, but not sufficiently enough for faster reac-
tions to yield a definitive answer. 
For future work a flow reactor could be used to increase yields of especially the low yielding crossed 
products of maltol, kojic acid and pyromeconic acid. A flow reactor would remove the products from 
the light source once they were made which could save them from degradation. Furthermore, the use 
of enantioselective photocatalysts could be employed to get the enantiopure [2+2]-cycloaddition 
products. 
Lastly all the compounds made in this project should be sent for a cell painting assay. Here the com-
pounds would be screened to see what organelles in the cell are affected. It is important to add both 
the alkylated starting materials and the final [2+2]-cycloaddition products, to see what the formation 
of the cyclobutane changes, regarding the effect of the compound.

General directions: 
All reactions were carried out under an inert atmosphere unless specified otherwise. Starting materials 
were made under nitrogen atmosphere and [2+2]-cycloadditions were made under argon atmosphere 
and at room temperature. The reactions were monitored by thin-layer chromatography (TLC) and/or 
reversed-phase ultra-performance liquid chromatography mass spectrometry (RP-UPLC-MS). Com-
mercially available reagents were purified according to standard procedures or were used as received 
from Sigma Aldrich, Alfa Aesar, Acros Organics, Combi-Blocks, Fisher Scientific, Strem, and Merck. 
All solvents used were of HPLC quality and dry solvents (acetonitrile, DCM and DMF) were obtained 
from a PureSolv system (Innovative Technology, Tronyx). Quantification of impurities and yield cor-
rection were based on quantitative 1H NMR spectroscopy.147 Analytical TLC was conducted on 
Merck aluminium sheets covered with silica (C60). The plates were either visualized under ultraviolet 
(UV)-light or stained by dipping in a developing agent followed by heating. KMnO4 [3 g in water 
(300 mL) along with K2CO3 (20 g) and 5% aqueous NaOH (5 mL)] and cerium molybdate 
[Ce(NH4)2(NO3)6 (0.5g), (NH4)6Mo7O24·4H2O (24.0 g), and H2SO4 (24.0 g)] were used as developing 
agents. Flash column chromatography was performed using Merck Geduran® Si 60 (40-63 μm) silica 
gel. All new compounds were characterized by NMR, LRMS (ESI), and HRMS (ESI) where appro-
priate. Structural assignments were made, when possible, for new compounds using COSY, HSQC, 
HMBC, H2BC, and NOESY spectra where appropriate. For the recording of 1H NMR and 13C NMR 
spectra, a Bruker Avance III 400 spectrometer with a Bruker Ascend 400 magnet and a Prodigy Cry-
oProbe (operating at 400 MHz for proton and 101 MHz for carbon) was used. The chemical shifts (δ) 
are reported in parts per million (ppm) and the coupling constants (J) in Hz. Spectra were referenced 
using the residual solvent peaks of the respective solvent; DMSOd6 (δ = 2.50 ppm for 1H NMR and 
δ = 39.52 ppm for 13C NMR) and CDCl3 (δ = 7.26 ppm for 1H NMR and δ  = 77.16 ppm for 13C 
NMR). The following abbreviations were used to report peak multiplicities: s = singlet, d = doublet, 
t = triplet, q = quartet, dd = doublet of doublets, dt = doublet of triblets, dtd = doublet of triblets of 
doublets, m = multiplet. Analytical RP-UPLC-LRMS (ESI) analysis was performed on a S2 Waters 
AQUITY RP-UPLC system equipped with a diode array 32 detector using an Thermo Accucore C18 
column (d = 2.6 μm, 2.1 x 50 mm; column temp. = 50 °C; flow = 1.0 mL/min). Eluents A (10 – 15 
mM NH4OAc in H2O) and B (10 – 15 mM NH4OAc in 9:1 MeCN/H2O) were used in a linear gradient
