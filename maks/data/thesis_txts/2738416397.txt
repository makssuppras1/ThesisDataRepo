Abstract
This thesis explores the role of allergen­like proteins (ALPs) in the gut microbiome and
their potential influence on allergy development. By employing sequence alignment using
DIAMOND BLASTP and allergenicity prediction with NetAllergen, a set of potential ALPs
were identified. The prevalence and abundance of these ALPs were analyzed in the gut
microbiome of subjects from the DIABIMMUNE study, which includes data from infants in
Estonia, Finland, and Russia. Key findings indicate a high prevalence of certain ALPs and
notable geographic differences in allergy prevalence and IgE levels. Despite a lack of cor­
relation between ALP abundance and allergenicity scores, the study suggests that ALPs
may play a role in allergy development, influenced by gut microbiome composition and
genetic background. Limitations such as the need for larger, more diverse cohorts and
experimental validation are acknowledged. This exploratory analysis lays the groundwork
for future research aimed at understanding the complex interplay between the gut micro­
biome, ALPs, and the immune system, with potential applications in allergy prevention
and management through probiotics.

Introduction
In the Western world, allergy is becoming an increasing issue. In recent decades, studies
have shown that IgE­mediated food allergy has increased from 1­2 % to 5­8 % in the
United States and similarly in Europe.[1] The reason for this increase is so far unknown.
Especially food allergies in children seem to be on the rise. The severity of this can be
seen in the increased number of hospital admissions for severe allergic reactions, which
has been reported to show up to a seven­fold increase. This also brings the risk of life­
threatening reactions that can result in death. [2] While allergy can come from many
sources, it is evident that some of the burdensome sources are peanuts, seafood, milk,
egg, wheat, and soy. On top of the large impacts on the quality of life of people with
allergies, the healthcare and personal costs can be quite severe. [2] This means that
labeling of goods and research into allergies and their development is more important
than ever before.
1.1
Allergy mechanism
Allergy is sensitization to otherwise harmless proteins, which leads to an overblown reac­
tion by the immune system producing antibodies, primarily immunoglobulin E (IgE), which
leads to inflammation when exposed to the allergen. This can lead to symptoms rang­
ing from mild irritation to fatal anaphylactic shock. Sensitization can occur in areas like
the skin, oral­nasal cavity, and the gastrointestinal tract. With food allergens, proteins in
food are broken down in the stomach and intestine. In the intestine, mucosal dendritic
cells sample the ingested allergens and further process them into an antigen. The den­
dritic cells will meet with naive T cells in the draining lymph nodes and present them with
the antigen on a Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) class II. Intracellular signaling
through costimulatory molecules is then what decides whether to downregulate T­cell ac­
tivation or induce it. This risks differentiation to Th2 cells, especially if the cytokine IL­4
is secreted in the area, as that is one of the signals that stimulate Th2 differentiation. If
Th2 differentiation happens, the Th2 cell will release cytokines to stimulate B cells and
start the process of creating plasma cells(antibody­producing cells) specific to the anti­
gen. The plasma cells will produce IgE antibodies that will bind to IgE receptors on the
surface of mast cells and basophils. While the sensitization does in itself not trigger an
allergic reaction, it primes the body to be ready for re­exposure. Upon re­exposure to
the same allergen, the IgE antibodies on the surface of the mast cells and basophils will
bind to the allergen and trigger the cells to release histamine, inflammatory cytokines,
and leukotrienes, which is what generates the allergic reaction. Allergens can come in
many forms, like food allergens (peanuts, seafood), inhalant allergens (pollen, dust mites),
venom/salivary (insect stings and bites), and contact allergens (chemicals, metals). [3,
4] Many factors can contribute to the development of allergy including genetic factors
and and environmental factors. Genetic factors such as HLA alleles will often determine
how predisposed you are to allergy and then exposure to environmental factors like air
pollutants and other attacks that can trigger the epithelial barrier to become more easily
sensitized. [5]
1.2
The gut microbiome
The gut microbiome plays a large role in many aspects of human health, including allergy.
Its diversity and composition have been shown to be linked to the development of allergies
and tolerance. [6] The gut microbiome is the name used to refer to the diverse community

of microorganisms like bacteria, viruses, and fungi living in the gastrointestinal tract. The
gut microbiome primarily consists of bacteria from the phyla Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes,
Actinobacteria, and Proteobacteria. The microbes in the gut microbiome often assist in
the breakdown of food and help produce beneficial compounds like short­chain fatty acids
and vitamins. They often also help in the regulation of a healthy immune system by out­
competing pathogens and promoting tolerance to harmless substances like proteins in
food, like allergens! The gut microbiome also affects many other systems in the body.
Being a living ecosystem, the gut microbiome will change throughout your life and will be
affected by things like lifestyle, medication, and diet. [7, 8]
One of the ways the gut microbiome helps regulate the immune system is by exposure to
the proteins that the bacteria are composed of. Since the microbes live in the intestines
the immune cells will constantly be in contact with the proteins and metabolites from the
gut microbiome. This can help induce regulatory T cells which helps maintain immune
tolerance. [9] However, what happens when the proteins in the gut microbiome happen
to look like allergens? How does this affect the development of allergies?
1.3
Metagenomics
One of the ways for us to study the gut microbiome is with metagenomics. Metage­
nomics is the study of genetic material from environmental or clinical samples, such as
stool samples. Metagenomics takes advantage of sequencing technologies such as next­
generation sequencing to get a comprehensive overview of the genetic material available
in the sample. This allows for the taxonomic profiling of microbes in the sample, allow­
ing us to determine which microbial species are present in a sample and how abundant
they are. Given enough genetic material in the sample it is possible to find and differen­
tiate many microbial species in a single sample without having to isolate each individual
species. This is especially easy if we already know what the genetic code of the microbe
is as that allows us to store it in a database and compare the genetic code in our sample
to it. Since we are working with DNA, we are able to see which genes are in each species
as well, so we are also able to functionally profile the samples. This means that we can
determine exactly which proteins the microbes produce or at least carry the DNA to pro­
duce.[10, 11] This is what we want to take advantage of in this thesis, as it allows us to
look for allergen­like protein.
The overall hypothesis for this thesis is that allergen­like proteins (ALPs) in the gut micro­
biome may influence the development of allergies. Specifically, these ALPs might either
facilitate the onset of allergies or promote tolerance and protect against allergies due to
their structural and functional similarities to common allergens. Depending on whether
an ALP is protective or sensitizing it could have the potential for probiotics that protect
against allergy or probiotics that compete with bacteria containing the sensitizing ALPs.

2
Material and methods
In this analysis, we are interested in finding peptides that look like allergens from the
perspective of the immune system. We expect that there could be some possible cross­
reactivity between the allergen­like proteins (ALPs) and the actual allergens. The hypoth­
esis is that the presence of these ALPs in people’s gut microbiomes could possibly have
a sensitizing or protective effect.
2.1
Searching for allergen­like peptides
The first step is to identify possible ALPs. A simple way to do this is to take a database
of verified allergens and align them against a database of proteins.
2.1.1
Peptide database selection
We chose the UniRef90 database (January 2019 version) as our protein database. UniRef90
is a comprehensive database of protein sequences clustered to a maximum of 90 % se­
quence identity, which reduces redundancy while maintaining sequence diversity. This
clustering facilitates more efficient and meaningful analyses of peptide data. UniRef90
integrates data from UniProtKB and is widely used in microbiome research for its exten­
sive coverage of protein sequences across various organisms. The January 2019 version
compatibility with HUMAnN3 allows for quantifying these peptides in the gut microbiome
later, making it an ideal choice for our study.
2.1.2
Allergen database selection
The AllergenOnline database from Goodman et al.[1] was chosen as the trusted database
of allergens. AllergenOnline contains a list of verified and peer­reviewed allergenic pep­
tides. The database was originally created to help evaluate peptides that might be created
in new foods through genetic engineering, novel food sources, or new processing meth­
ods. The 22nd version of this database, which was used for this project, was released
in May 2023 and contains 2290 verified allergens from 952 different taxonomic­protein
groups from 379 different species. The database includes food, inhalant, venom/sali­
vary, and contact allergens, and therefore, a good variety of allergens for this project.
The allergens in AllergenOnline are curated from NCBI and only contain allergens from
peer­reviewed papers that show at least in vitro protein­specific serum IgE binding. The
database has also sorted out proteins that are unlikely to cause allergies. The database
is also updated yearly, which makes this database reliable and updated.
2.1.3
Alignment method
The AllergenOnline database was BLASTed against the UniRef90 database using DIA­
MOND (v2.1.9.163) BLASTP search. Basic Local Alignment Search Tool for Proteins
(BLASTP) is a protein sequence alignment tool developed by Altschul et al.[12] that is
used for querying protein sequences against a protein sequence database. BLASTP
functions by performing a seeded local alignment, which is highly accurate, sensitive and
fast compared to other alignment methods when it came out. However, this approach
is still quite time­consuming when querying many sequences against a large database.
The more modern tool DIAMOND BLASTP is a better option for this use case. The tool
uses a double indexing approach, where both the queries and database are indexed, al­
lowing for an optimized search. DIAMOND BLASTP running in –very­sensitive mode is
able to achieve BLASTP like sensitivity and accuracy with an 80–360­fold computational
speedup.[13]

2.1.4
Search parameters
Since we are interested in finding close homologs of the allergens, DIAMOND BLASTP
is run in –very­sensitive mode and a minimum query coverage of 80 %. Since we do not
know which parts of the allergens are the most important for allergenicity, then the query
coverage restriction is important to increase the likelihood of the allergenic epitopes being
captured in the alignment. The BLASTP search was also restricted to a maximum of 500
significant alignments per query for the sake of ease of analysis. To define significant hits,
the default E­value threshold of 0.001 is used.
2.1.5
Initial findings and adjustment
Initially, when running the search, it was discovered that a vast majority of the UniRef90
clusters in the hits were not bacterial of bacterial origin (9343 bacterial hits out of 649368
hits). Among the bacterial hits, even fewer were actually found in the microbiome. Since
most of the allergens in the AllergenOnline database are of eukaryotic origin, it makes
sense that most of the closest homologs would also be of eukaryotic origin. To get around
this issue, the UniRef90 database was filtered to only contain clusters, where the repre­
sentative sequences are of bacterial origin, and then the DIAMOND BLASTP search was
rerun, yielding better results.
2.2
Evaluating allergenicity with NetAllergen
With a group of allergen­like proteins identified, it is important to determine if they can
elicit an allergenic immune response. This evaluation is essential for understanding the
sensitization potential of these proteins. While using BLASTP to find similar proteins
provides some information, it is unknown whether important epitopes from the original
allergens are conserved so they retain their allergenic properties. To determine this, we
need a specialized tool for evaluating allergenicity.
One such tool is NetAllergen(v1.0)[14], which utilizes a random forest model incorporat­
ing various features to evaluate the allergenic potential of proteins. These features in­
clude physiochemical properties, structural attributes, MHC class II presentation propen­
sity scores, amino acid compositions, and evolutionary information in the form of PSI­
BLAST Position Specific Scoring Matrices. NetAllergen calls on NetMHCIIpan(v4.3)[15]
to search for MHC class II binding motifs associated with common HLA alleles, ensuring
broad population coverage. This would give a good idea of which parts of the protein
would actually be presented by the MHC, if any. Additionally, it uses NetSurfP (v3.0)[16]
to predict residue­level secondary structure and surface accessibility. These structural
predictions are important as patterns here can have a big impact on the protein’s sur­
face topography and epitope accessibility, which are key factors for eliciting an allergic
reaction.
The outcome of this evaluation is an allergenicity prediction score, which can be used to
assess each allergen­like protein’s potential to trigger an immune response. This score is
very valuable for identifying a ”golden set” of allergen­like proteins in the gut microbiome
that could potentially play a role in the development of allergies.
2.3
Quantifying the abundance of allergen­like peptides in
the gut microbiome of healthy individuals
Understanding which proteins are present in the human gut microbiome and how abun­
dant they are is important for properly assessing the impact of ALPs.
To do this, we need a large collection of human metagenome samples. For this, a sub­
set of CuratedMetagenomicsData[17] was used. The subset contains 4035 gut samples

of healthy adults. The subject primarily contains subjects of Western origin, but some
smaller parts are of non­Western origin. The large sample size should allow us to get a
good understanding of the population abundances with some degree of certainty. The
metagenomics samples are in FASTQ format where the samples have varying quality,
in the sense that they come from different machines, the lengths of the reads vary from
100bp to 150bp, different adapters and some of the samples have had adapters removed

important to have a good pipeline setup for preprocessing the samples before running
them through HUMAnN3.
2.3.1
HUMAnN3 pipeline
The HUMAnN 3 pipeline, which is described in figure 2.1 is built using the Nextflow work­
flow manager[18]. Nextflow allows us to create a workflow with nf­core[19] that is scalable,
flexible, reproducible and can run on a large cluster. We need to be able to handle these
large amounts of data efficiently.
Preprocessing
The pipeline starts with preprocessing where we use fastp (0.23.4) to handle adapter
trimming and quality trimming and filtering. fastp is an ultra­fast FASTQ preprocessor that
provides state­of­the­art quality while working much faster than competing trimmers like
Trimmomatic and Cutadapt.[20] fastp is able to look for and remove a variety of adapters
when they are provided as a FASTA which is an advantage since we have data from
differences studies and do not know which adapters have been used for each sample.
FastQC (0.12.1) is used to check the quality of the reads before and after trimming to en­
sure that there are no samples with excessively poor quality reads or remaining adapters.
The FastQC results were collected into MultiQC (1.15) for an easier overview of the data.
After trimming, host DNA is removed by aligning the reads to the GRCh38.p14 human
reference genome using Bowtie2 (2.5.2)[21] and removing the reads that align. Doing
this helps prevent any human DNA from the host from contaminating the sample.
Taxonomic profiling
The preprocessed samples are then run through MetaPhlAn 4 (4.0.6). MetaPhlAn is a
tool for profiling the taxonomic composition of metagenomics samples down to a species
and even strain level. MetaPhlaAn specializes in profiling the gut microbiome by map­
ping the reads to clade­specific marker genes. This allows for unambiguous taxonomic
assignment and accurate estimation of the relative abundance of species. MetaPhlAn 4
uses Bowtie2 to map reads to the CHOCOPhlAnSGB database (vJan21_202103). This
database is built from ∼26000 species­level genome bins (SGBs) and contains ∼5.1 mil­
lion clade­specific marker genes. This yields a list of taxonomic abundances found in the
samples, which HUMAnN uses for functional profiling.
Functional profiling
HMP Unified Metabolic Analysis Network (HUMAnN) (v3.6.1) is a tool for functional profil­
ing of metagenomics samples. It uses the MetaPhlAn taxonomic abundance list to build
a community pangenome database that includes all the genes for the species found in
the SGBs found by MetaPhlAn. This approach allows for the database to be very detailed
without becoming too big by including genes that are not relevant to the samples. The pre­
processed reads are aligned against this pangenome database to yield organism­specific
gene hits, which in turn also map to UniRef90. The unmapped reads are then used for
a species­agnostic translated search using DIAMOND against UniRef90. This ensures
that the reads are utilized completely so everything present can be captured. Now that
the reads have been mapped to UniRef90 IDs, we can use these to quantify allergen­like
proteins in the gut microbiome.

2.4
Evaluating impact of allergen­like peptides on allergy
development
To understand the real­world impact of allergen­like peptides, we aim to find correlations
between their presence and the development of allergies, either individually or collectively.
This evidence will explain how these peptides influence allergy onset and progression.
2.4.1
DIABIMMUNE Study
The DIABIMMUNE study, originally conducted to investigate the development of Type 1
Diabetes (T1D) and the hygiene hypothesis, offers valuable data for our analysis. We

are particularly interested in their annotations for allergy, including total Immunoglobulin
E (IgE) levels and allergy sensitivity.
The study encompasses three cohorts of 74 infants (222 in total) from Estonia, Finland,
and Russia. The cohorts were selected based on matching gender and HLA risk class
distribution, where one of the recruitment criteria was an increased HLA­conferred sus­
ceptibility to autoimmunity. The data collected for the study were monthly stool samples
and questionnaires over a period of 3 years. It includes infant data with 812 metage­

• Age at sample collection
• Allergy diagnoses and total IgE levels
• Breastfeeding status and dietary information
• Development of Type 1 Diabetes

Given that the DIABIMMUNE study provides FASTQ samples, we can utilize our estab­
lished HUMAnN3 pipeline to quantify the presence of our ”gold standard” allergen­like
peptides in these metagenomic samples.
2.4.2
Data Processing and Correlation Analysis
The presence and abundance of ALPs will be processed, examined, and visualized using
a combination of bash scripts and R programming. Specifically, bash will handle initial data
processing and filtering, while R will be used for detailed statistical analysis, modeling, and
visualization of correlations.
To compare the DIABIMMUNE metagenomics samples, a Principal Coordinates Analysis
(PCoA) was constructed from a Bray­Curtis dissimilarity matrix calculated from the ALP
abundances. The Bray­Curtis dissimilarity is used to quantify compositional dissimilarity
between to samples of abundance data. This method is commonly used for metagenome
samples as it better captures the differences than an Euclidean distance matrix.
Using the detailed annotations provided by the DIABIMMUNE study, we can investigate
the correlations between the presence of our ”gold standard” allergen­like peptides and
various factors, such as allergy development and total IgE levels. This is done by fitting
a Generalized Linear Model (GLM) to predict total IgE levels and binary allergy status
from the abundances of the individual ALPs. GLMs are linear predictors that function as
a more flexible linear regression. GLMs also allow for model evaluation by calculating the
significance of each of the model variables. This allows us to determine if correlations are
significant.
Predicting Total IgE levels from ALP abundances does run into the issue that both these
features are not linear but rather exponential, so fitting a GLM on the raw data could lead
to inaccuracies. An option could be to log­transform the data to linearize the relationships
before fitting the model, but the ALPs are not present in all the samples, which means
trying to use the logarithm of zero, which is undefined. The workaround is to use a non­
parametric rank­based approach by fitting the GLM on the rank­transformed data.
When using GLMs to test correlations between many ALPs and allergies, we must correct
for multiple tests to ensure that the significant hits we get are actually statistically signif­
icant and not just due to chance. The method chosen to mitigate this is the Benjamini­

Hochberg method of controlling the False Discovery Rate(FDR). This GLM­based ap­
proach also allows us to check the direction the correlation goes. This means that we
can check for sensitizing ALPs and protective ALPs based on whether the correlation is
positive or negative.
We use the Pearson Correlation Coefficient (PCC), which measures how linear a rela­
tionship between two variables is. The PCC ranges from ­1 to 1 to define the perfect
negative linear relationship to the perfect positive linear relationship. A PCC of 0 indi­
cates no linear relationship. We also use Spearman’s rank Correlation Coefficient, which
is a non­parametric measure of the relationship between two ranked variables. Again,
­1 denotes perfect negative rank correlation, 1 denotes perfect positive rank correlations,
and 0 denotes no correlation.

3.5.5
Effect of individual ALPs on allergies
Total IgE levels indicate allergy and allergic disease, but the true test of the ALPs is how
they correlate with actual allergies. Therefore, binomial GLMs were fitted to the abun­
dance of each ALP to test if they predict specific allergies. Each ALP has a GLM fitted
to predict the presence or absence of each of the 8 allergies. As before, this leads to
multiple tests, necessitating the correction of p­values using the FDR method to ensure
that the significant correlations really are significant.
While the GLM fittings did find significant correlations, none of them survived the FDR
adjustment. This resulted in no significant correlations being found. One likely reason
for this is the small size of the allergy­tested cohort. In combination with the low rates of
most allergies, this leaves us with very small comparison groups. For example, only 9 out
of 195 subjects tested had a dust mite allergy. This means comparing ALP abundances
between groups of 9 and 186 subjects, where only large differences in abundance would
yield statistically significant results. Also, not all ALPs are present in all samples, further
reducing the amount of usable information for each correlation.
For instance, UniRef90_A6KXL2, shown in Figure 3.20a, is only found in 102 of the earli­
est samples from the first two years. In the case of dust mite allergy, we have abundance
data for only 97 non­allergic and 5 allergic subjects, illustrating the limited statistical power.
Although UniRef90_A6KXL2 was statistically significant for three allergies, after FDR ad­
justment, the lowest p­value was 0.799, far from actual significance.

4
Discussion
The overall hypothesis for this thesis was that allergen­like proteins (ALPs) in the gut
microbiome may influence the development of allergies. Specifically, these ALPs might
either facilitate the onset of allergies or promote tolerance and protect against allergies
due to their structural and functional similarities to common allergens.
This hypothesis was tested by finding a ”gold standard” set of ALPs known to be similar
to allergens from a trusted allergen database, known to be found in the gut microbiome,
and predicted to be allergenic. The abundances of these ALPs were then found in the
metagenomics samples from DIABIMMUNE, and their abundances were correlated with
the total IgE level and allergy prevalence of the subject to evaluate their relevance.
The search for ALPs yielded ∼160,000 hits with ∼48,000 potential ALPs. Of those, 1725
ALPs were found in the gut microbiome. When using DIAMOND BLASTP to search for
ALPs, the choice of database and search parameters are quite important, even after hav­
ing restricted our search database to only contain bacterial UniRef90 clusters that still
leave us with a large number of hits which had no chance of being relevant to us. Instead
of aligning against bacterial UniRef90, we could have restricted the search database only
to contain UniRef90 clusters that we know to have been found in the gut microbiome.
This would leave us aligning against ∼4M sequences instead of ∼60M while ensuring
that every hit could be relevant. This could lead to more candidate ALPs while saving
on computation. Furthermore, we could have chosen a stricter E­value threshold to get
hits more similar to the query allergens. However, given that we are conducting an ex­
ploratory analysis and will scrutinize the hits further, the less critical threshold was deemed
acceptable.
Using DIAMOND BLASTP also has shortcomings. This approach is purely sequence­
alignment­based without considering other protein structure information. Other methods
incorporating structural search or protein embeddings could help remedy this, but these
methods are much more computationally demanding compared to BLAST, making them
unfeasible. BLASTP can also not be aware of allergenic epitopes and conserve them
in the search for ALPs. The idea is again that the less strict E­values would allow us to
consider a broader range of ALPs and that NetAllergen could point out which ALPs are
relevant.
On NetAllergen, we know that AllergenOnline is an important part of its training data. This
opens us up to bias since our ALPs are specifically selected based on their similarity to
the AllergenOnline allergens. This bias risks falsely identifying some ALPs as allergenic.
It is hard to say how exposed we are to this bias without experimental validation. Still, it is
possibly an issue that could be addressed by raising the threshold for what we consider
allergenic. This was not something we experimented with.
The lack of correlations between the NetAllergen prediction score and the abundance of
the ALPs suggests that the immune system does not recognize them as a threat. If that
were the case, we could have observed that the abundance would decrease as the aller­
genicity increased. However, it is also very possible that the NetAllergen prediction score
does not indicate allergen severity. Furthermore, immune reactions are very dependent
on the subject’s HLA alleles. NetAllergen uses a general set of HLA alleles to predict
its epitopes, but this general set might not correspond to the HLA alleles in our subjects.
This means that the allergenicity predicted by NetAllergen would not be correct for all our
Identification of Allergen­Like Proteins in the Gut Microbiome
27
==PAGE:33==
subjects, which would create noise. Still, we would expect to see some correlation if this
was the case, and we see none.
As a whole, the ALPs seem quite prevalent on a population scale. ALPs are not com­
prised of only rare proteins, but some are quite common, and most subjects carry many
of them. This is a good indication that they can have a broader impact. The challenge is
determining which ALPs are the most important and who they can impact.
A good place to look for answers to this is in the allergy prevalences and total IgE levels.
Specifically, it is interesting to look at the geographical differences. The low Russian al­
lergy prevalence and total IgE levels are particularly interesting. They are so much lower
than their counterparts that something must be there to find. Based on the allergenic
load, we do not see any evidence that it correlates with total IgE levels, but looking at the
PCoA plots, we see that the Russian samples stick out. This indicates that it might not be
the total abundance of ALPs that makes the difference but rather the composition of the
gut microbiome. In the original DIABIMMUNE three country cohort paper[8], they found
that the Russian infants had a much larger proportion of Actinobacteria(Bifidobacterium)
in the early samples compared to the Finnish and Estonian infants who were more domi­
nated by Bacteroides. This also corresponds with our findings of the seemingly protective
correlation between UniRef90_E8ME30 and total IgE levels. This protein originates from
Bifidobacterium longum, which has been shown to have protective effects regarding food
allergy.[25] There are also other possible reasons why the Russian cohort might have a
lower prevalence of allergy, such as genetics. It is possible that the Russian population
carries HLA alleles that are less prone to the development of allergies. However, this
seems like an unlikely explanation, because the subjects in the DIABIMMUNE study are
selected to be in matching HLA risk groups.
In this thesis, we also attempted to find correlations between individual ALPs and specific
allergies. The hypothesis is that each of the individual ALPs has been found through their
similarity to an allergen. This similarity might lead to sensitization to exactly this allergen.
Unfortunately, selecting only one sample per subject was necessary to combat bias. The
DIABIMMUNE subjects were only tested for allergies and total IgE levels once during the
study, which limits our sample size. In order to try and capture the ALPs that affected the
immune system the most, the earliest samples from each subject were selected since this
is when the immune system is the most malleable. However, we might miss some ALPs
that could present themselves in the later samples. Missing ALPs in later samples might
not have the biggest impact on our correlations with allergenic load, but it is important
when it comes to correlations based on the abundance of the individual ALPs. As a
result, we did not have sufficient data points to find correlations between individual ALPs
and specific allergies with any statistical power after correcting for multiple tests.
We observed statistically significant correlations between allergenic load and total IgE
levels, which is an indicator of allergies. These correlations were even present when
using the earliest samples as described above. The correlations were not very strong
when looking at the correlation coefficients, but the GLMs fitted did show a statistically
significant correlation. It is also important to keep in mind that many factors affect total
IgE, including allergic diseases and autoimmune diseases like T1D, which can all add
noise to the total IgE level. This could mean that the correlation might be stronger than it
otherwise appears and should not necessarily be brushed off.
Furthermore, we tracked how this correlation behaved through the quarters after birth.
These correlations show that the first three quarters and the 5th quarter were especially
important and that the quarters after the 5th quarter did not show any correlations. Seeing

a correlation in the first three quarters makes a lot of sense since that is when the immune
system is the most malleable. It is not expected that the quarters after the testing for IgE
levels will correlate, so seeing no correlation in the 6th quarter and beyond is not sur­
prising. However, why the 4th quarter does not correlate, and the 5th correlates strongly
is a bit unexpected. A theory could be that the IgE tests were mostly taken in the 5th
quarter, so the immediate effects of the ALPs present in the gut microbiome might affect
the reaction of the immune system acutely, where the exposure in the first three quarters
contributed by the development of the immune system by setting it up to be more reactive.
Future research
We suggest that carrying out studies like the DIABIMMUNE study with a larger and more
diverse cohort would allow the finding of more significant correlations. While the DIABIM­
MUNE study does contain a lot of interesting data for a study like this, the lack of multi­
ple allergy tests and total IgE measurements per subject is severely limiting our ability to
track the development of allergy side by side with the development of the gut microbiome.
Having an expanded study like this would be a great benefit to our understanding of the
complex interplay between the gut microbiome and the development of allergies.
Further, due to the potential biases in using NetAllergen and ALPs found based on the Al­
lergenOnline database, experimental validation of the identified ALPs is very important. It
would be a great benefit to benchmark the performance and trustworthiness of the ”gold
standard” ALP pipeline. In the same vein, it would be great to be able to compare the
BLASTP­based approach with another more complex approach that integrates more in­
formation than just the sequence. It would also be interesting to examine the ALPs to see
if there are any patterns in the protein domains they contain.
In light of the finding of the possible protective Bifidobacterium longum ALP, it is crucial to
investigate whether it is the protein itself that is protective or something else in B. longum.
Regardless, B. longum seems like an interesting target for an anti­allergenic probiotic in
infants.

5
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to explore the role of allergen­like proteins (ALPs) in the gut microbiome
in influencing the development of allergies. Through a combination of sequence alignment
using DIAMOND BLASTP and allergenicity prediction with NetAllergen, we identified a set
of potential ALPs and analyzed their prevalence and abundance in the gut microbiome of
DIABIMMUNE study subjects.
Key findings include the high prevalence of certain ALPs, the lack of correlation between
ALP abundance and allergenicity scores, and the geographic differences in allergy preva­
lence and IgE levels. The low allergy prevalence in the Russian cohort is especially in­
teresting. These results hint that the impact of ALPs on allergy development is there and
dependent on the gut microbiome composition and genetic background.
The limitations, including the necessity of a single sample per subject and the potential
biases in allergenicity prediction, highlight the need for future research with larger, more
diverse cohorts and experimental validation of findings. Despite these limitations, the
observed correlations between allergenic load and total IgE levels support the hypothesis
that the ALPs in the gut microbiome play a role in allergy development.
In conclusion, this exploratory analysis lays the groundwork for understanding the rela­
tionships between the gut microbiome, allergen­like proteins, and the immune system.
Continued research in this area seems promising with the potential for allergy prevention
and management through probiotics.
