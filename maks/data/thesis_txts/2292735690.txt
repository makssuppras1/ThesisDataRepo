ABSTRACT 
This master thesis talks about work done in the program Nordic Maritime Engineering at Denmark 
Technical University (DTU), Denmark. This project is a proposal of a Norwegian company Kongsberg 
Maritime AS, located in Trondheim, Norway. Oil and gas have been major business in the offshore 
and maritime industries. Relevant to the carriage and transportation of liquids and gases, a radar 
level gauge is one of the relevant devices to mention. These liquids and gases are carried in tanks and 
it is very vital to know their levels and the volumes. The radar level gauge functions as a precision 
measurement device. It measures the ullage in tanks onboard ships and is positioned on the top of the 
tank, continues all the way down to the bottom (up to 50m) and its brackets are fixed to the 
instrumentation column to support the pipe. The presence of this support is to ensure that the pipe can 
withstand mechanical stresses due to sloshing and vibration from the propulsion system. The aim of 
this project is to reduce the number of brackets applied along the waveguide of the radar level gauge 
for Gas applications (LNG, LGP and NH3) [1]. The stiffness of the support brackets fastened on the 
pipe was estimated as well as the lowest natural frequency of the pipe. The analysis was done based 
on a prismatic Bernoulli-Euler beam with moment springs. Finally, forced vibrations and metal 
fatigue analyses were performed. In order to know the pipe resistance to the sloshing effect, the 
bending moment and the stresses were calculated. Employing polyethylene as the bracket's material, 
the total number of brackets along the pipe is reduced by 33.3%.  
 
Keywords: Radar level gauge, Tank ullage, Waveguide mechanics

1. INTRODUCTION 
 
1.1 Background 
 
A radar level gauge is a device used in most companies or industries that deal with any fluid. It used to 
measure liquid levels and distance measurements. It has a control unit which checks the accuracy of 
the measurements [1]. The most important aspect of a radar level gauge is its ability to give accurate 
results irrespective of changes in densities of the fluid. There are several types of radar gauges, which 
are employed in different domains.  Some of these include, air traffic control radar, shipping radar, 
weather radar, continuous wave (CW) radars, which are employed in automatic door detection and 
vehicle-speed measurements, and frequency modulated continuous wave radar (FMCW).  The FMCW 
type consists of a microwave transceiver and an antenna for its applications. It operates with a 
transmitted signal from the antenna to the liquid surface and the reflection to the antenna whilst it 
remains contactless with the liquid surface. The difference between these signals is used to calculate 
the distance to the surface of the liquid. [''To improve measurements, fixing/substitution in most cases 
involves emptying, heating up and gas freezing. Any competitive marine LNG gauging method (radar) 
will have to agree to similar installation necessities i.e. gauging inside the pipe tower (Moss-type 
tanks), or along the tripod structure (membrane type tanks). Using a steel pipe for such radar is 
incidentally very advantageous, since this means no disturbances from tank interiors''] [4].  
According to Kongsberg Maritime AS, a radar tank gauge is the core element of the liquid cargo 
monitoring and control system. Kongsberg Maritime AS uses the FMCW concept and some of the 
areas of applications areas are Oil and product tankers, Gas carriers-LNG/LPG, Chemical tankers, and 
pipe measurement. The distance (i.e. ullage) is computed from the time delay of the reflected signal. A 
frequency microwave signal is given off by the radar tank gauge through a steel pipe and by help of 
the antenna directed vertically down the tank. The reflected signal is received by the antenna and 
recorded by the radar gauge electronics. The steel pipe is assembled by sections of six meters lengths 
where the top and bottom section lengths are adjusted to match the total tank height. The vents on the 
pipe ensure the external and internal vapour pressures are stabilized, thus allowing the liquid to rise or 
fall unobstructed in the pipe. Each pipe section is supplied with flanges prepared with reference 
markers.  
The positions of the markers are recorded and stored in the system by careful calibration of the pipe 
section’s length before installation. A continuous auto calibration of the measurement is done by 
comparing the liquid echo with the signature plate echo. The electronic unit in the Radar Tank Gauge

1.2 Problem Statement 
Kongsberg Maritime would like to reduce the number of brackets applied along the waveguide of the 
radar level gauge. The current requirement is to apply a bracket every 1.5 m, and it can be seen in the 
figure 1.3 above. 
The radar level gauge for Gas applications (LNG, LPG and NH3) applies a Ø50mm pipe as a 
waveguide (pipe). The radar gauge is located on the top of the tank, and continues all the way down to 
the bottom of the tank (up to 50m). Brackets are fixed to the instrumentation column in 
the tank and support the pipe. The support needs to ensure that the pipe sustains moving cargo putting 
mechanical stress to the pipe (due to sloshing), vibration from the propulsion system and other forces. 
The subjects that need to be considered are: 
 
Could the current design apply fewer brackets as it is? 
 
Forces exerted on the pipe due to movement in the cargo. 
 
The pipe flanges and their design. Is it necessary to redesign the pipe, for example make it 
lighter? 
 
How much does the vibration from propulsion system influence the pipe and need for 
brackets? 
 
Consider the metal fatigue and the correlation with the forces exerted on the pipe. 
 
1.3 Problem formulation 
The radar level gauge is mounted on top of the tank. The pipe is connected all the way down to the 
bottom of the tank. It gives expected results through the trough of the pipe. The pipe has flanges in 
which a marker is placed and the radar gauge level links directly with each marker. The whole pipe is 
assembled in sections of 6m and the flanges are used to join them together to have the desired length. 
The radar level gauge itself has an antenna which sends a signal to the liquid surface and the signal is 
reflected back to the antenna through the flanges. The sent and received signals are not the same, so 
the difference between them is used to calculate the distance to the liquid surface. The volume of the 
liquid can also be calculated since the whole length/height of the pipe or the tank is known.

The brackets are fastened onto the pipe to sustain the stresses acting on the pipe. These stresses are 
caused by the sloshing load. A sloshing load occurs when the vessel voyages through severe irregular 
waves. The estimation of the sloshing load depends on the velocity of the fluid. In order for the pipe to 
resist this force, there is a need for the brackets.  
It is observed that in the present design, Kongsberg Maritime did not take the sloshing effect into 
account when designing the whole system of the radar level gauge. They have now realized that 
sloshing load has an effect on the pipe and its life time. At the same time they want to reduce the 
brackets along the pipe, since the brackets are very expensive to produce. The current pipe has a total 
number of 33 brackets (1.5m between brackets). Of course, the reduction of the brackets cannot be 
done without looking at the lowest natural frequency of the pipe, the stresses acting on the pipe and 
also its fatigue life time.

1.4 Kongsberg's expectations 
Kongsberg's expectations from the new design are to have: 
 
Fewer brackets 
 
An improved bracket design and lower weight for production and cost reasons 
 
Larger resistance to sloshing load 
 
Avoidance of resonance with the propeller induced vibrations (propeller is the main source of 
vibrations for their vessels). 
 
The material for the pipe should be steel and the brackets should be polyethylene (high 
density, since it is good for heat conductivity). 
 
 
1.5 Requirement for the new design 
Kongsberg maritime requirements in regard to the new design include the following: 
 
The natural frequency of the pipe in wet mode should be greater than the propeller induced 
vibrations (7.5Hz) times 2.5, i.e. >18.75Hz. 
 
The maximum sloshing velocity can be taken as 12m/s 
 
The maximum stresses on the pipe should be less than 80% of the yield stress. 
 
Life time of the design should be at least 30 years

2 METHODOLOGY 
 
2.1 System of interest 
This project focuses on the fixation of the pipe in the tank and how to estimate its lowest natural 
frequency. This calls for considering the configuration of the brackets on the pipe. Furthermore, the 
stresses acting on the pipe are well estimated. In regard to this, the sloshing load is the most critical 
load which must be taken into account. But since in this project work there is no available data 
concerning the sloshing effect, some simple assumptions have to be made.  
 
2.2 Direct calculations 
The analyses involved in this task are grouped into five: Firstly, statics and dynamics of standard beam 
theory with appropriate boundary conditions are employed. These analyses were performed to have a 
general view of the beam concept and also to compare Kongsberg and Kawasaki formulae. Secondly, a 
sloshing load analysis was performed since the pipe in the tank experiences sloshing when the vessel 
moves in the heavy seas. Thirdly, the pipe was considered as a prismatic beam with torsion springs. 
The torsion springs were taken as the brackets where their stiffness was derived from a Finite Element 
Method (FEM) analysis (ANSYS). In ANSYS software, the pipe is modeled as a rigid element while 
the bracket is modeled as the solid element. The whole idea behind this computation is to derive a 
more accurate lowest natural frequency coefficient (the lowest root frequency equation) of the pipe, 
since the pipe is neither fixed-fixed nor simple-simple supported. Fourthly, the lowest natural 
frequency of the pipe was estimated as function of the length i.e. the distance between the brackets. 
This analysis shows that the length can be increased to 2.6m as compared to the current design one 
which is 1.5m. Finally, a stress calculations and fatigue analyses were performed. In this analysis, the 
computation of the stresses was estimated based on the static solution, since the estimated lowest 
natural frequency of the pipe was much greater than the reference frequency that is the propeller 
frequency. The stress in the brackets was estimated by modeling one section of the pipe with ANSYS. 
The section pipe has two brackets. Here, the two brackets were modeled as the same mentioned above 
while the section of the pipe is modeled as a shell element. The life time cycles of the pipe were 
estimated employing one example of steel S-N curve but not for the exact type of the steel employed, 
since the precise S-N curve is not given.

5. CONCLUSION & DISCUSSION  
The aim of this thesis is to reduce the number of brackets along the pipe. Many brackets on the pipe 
cause financial loss to companies. The reduction of the brackets is an ordinary problem which is 
confronting some of the manufacturing companies. Though every company would like to minimize the 
cost, it is of importance to think how many years the pipe can last, resisting the applied forces on it.  
The main premise of the brackets' reduction has been that the pipe is considered as a prismatic beam 
with equal torsion springs in which the brackets’ stiffness was computed employing ANSYS. 
Furthermore, the first natural frequency of the pipe has been calculated. The results demonstrate that 
the brackets along the pipe can be reduced by 33.3%, that is the distance between the brackets can be 
2m, which fulfills the set goal. The calculation of the first mode or the lowest natural frequency of the 
pipe also satisfies the frequency requirements. Since the brackets are fastened on the pipe 
equidistantly, the frequency of one part of the pipe including two brackets is the same as the frequency 
of the whole pipe. The analysis involved in this project shows that the pipe is a flexible pipe with 
torsion springs.  
Furthermore, the stress analysis was carried out by computing the stresses in the pipe and in the 
brackets as well. Due to the stress requirement, the allowable stress in the pipe should be 80% of the 
yield stress, the new design can only fulfill this if the fluid velocity inside the tank fall within 1 to 
10m/s. Regarding the fatigue analysis, it is quite difficult to estimate the life time cycles of the pipe, 
since the S-N curve of the material is not given. Though some analysis was done based on a few 
assumptions concerning the S-N curve or the life time cycles of the pipe which can be seen in the 
appendix, it is not reliable. In general, the main task involved in this project work has been solved. 
The optimization of the new design shows that, choosing the distance between two brackets, l=2m as 
the maximum length is the best option, taking into account all the requirements.  
The sloshing loads are a vital aspect when designing a complete radar level gauge. The sloshing loads 
are considered to be uniformly distributed and its computation is done based on a series of fluid 
velocities, because the precise velocity of the fluid is unknown. Therefore, for future work, it would be 
good if sloshing data could be provided in order to know the exact sloshing load that sloshes the pipe, 
or the precise fluid velocity. It also important to provide the S-N curve of the material for the fatigue 
analysis.

7.1 Fatigue Analysis 
When designing a structure, it is important to think about its durability and the risk of fracture. Fatigue 
analysis describes how long the structure can last and how safe it is. Fatigue itself is a failure of any 
structure. Many definitions can be used to characterize fatigue, but for the purpose of this thesis, 
fatigue causes failure due to repeated sloshing load acting on the pipe until it reaches a critical level, 
when it cracks, bends or deforms. In general, there are many ways to perform fatigue analysis. The 
stress-life or S-N method, commonly called the total life method is considered in this analysis [15].
