Abstract 
Rapeseed ranks among the globe's most widely grown crops which contain valuable compounds that can 
be left in the meal after oil extraction. This study focuses on the investigation of two green methods -
supercritical CO2 (SC-CO2) extraction and ultrasound-assisted extraction (UAE)- on the extraction of lipids 
from rapeseed meal and seeds, using ethanol as a (co-) solvent. This research evaluates a range of various 
parameters affecting the yield and quality of the extracted oils, focusing on polar compounds like 
phospholipids and phenolic compounds. Between the evaluated parameters and values, in the SC-CO2 
extraction, optimal conditions were found to be 90 min and 60 oC, reaching 46.59% extraction yield. The 
addition of ethanol as a co-solvent in the process significantly increased the extraction yield, achieving 
high recovery (87.79%). It also enhanced the extraction of phospholipids by four times (0.039g/g ±0.001) 
compared to chemical solvent extraction. The UAE method, optimized at 15 min, 100% amplitude and 1:10 
sample/solvent ratio, reached 10.69% oil yield. Although the use of ethanol as an extraction solvent did 
not aid the extraction efficiency achieving only 20.14% recovery, it was highly effective in the extraction of 
polar compounds, reaching 0.056g/g ±0.004 phospholipids and 19.47 mg GAE/g phenolic compounds. This 
research demonstrated that between the studied methods, only the SC-CO2 extraction reached high 
extraction efficiency, but the use of ethanol in both SC-CO2 and UAE methods significantly improved the 
extraction of valuable polar compounds contributing effectively to the utilization of rapeseed seeds and 
meal.

1. Introduction 
Rapeseed (Brassica napus Linnaeus) is considered to be an important oil crop, cultivated worldwide (Chew, 
2020). Many rapeseed genotypes have been modified to contain high oil content (> 50%) thus making 
them valuable for the oil industry (Hu et al., 2013). Despite the extensive utilization of rapeseed, the by-
product of the oil production -meal- remains mostly unutilized. Rapeseed meal is mostly rich in proteins 
but there are also valuable compounds that remain in the meal such as residual oil and polar compounds 
like phospholipids and phenolic compounds, turning the meal into a valuable source of bioactive 
components (SzydłOwska-Czerniak, 2013). The traditional extraction methods fail to recover these 
compounds efficiently. Solvent extraction may be effective in extraction yield, but it produces an inferior 
product due to the leftover toxic solvents in the extracted oil. On the other hand, cold-press extraction 
results in high-quality oil, but it is not extraction efficient as up to 25% of residual oil can be found on the 
meal. Consequently, there is a necessity for more sustainable and efficient extraction methods to enhance 
the potential of rapeseed oil production from seeds and leftover meal (SzydłOwska-Czerniak, 2013; Chew, 
2020). 
The “green” extraction methods explored in this study are supercritical carbon dioxide (SC-CO2) extraction 
and ultrasound-assisted extraction (UAE). SC-CO2 extraction utilizes CO2 at its supercritical conditions 
where it exhibits characteristics of both a liquid and a gas, promoting the extraction of non-polar 
compounds (Ghosh & Pradhan, 2019). Incorporating a polar co-solvent like ethanol can be beneficial for 
the extraction of more polar compounds and thus, the overall yield (SzydłOwska-Czerniak, 2013). UAE is 
another promising technology that uses ultrasonic waves to create a cavitation phenomenon on the matrix 
thereby disrupting the cell walls and increasing mass transfer (Perrier et al. 2017). Depending on the 
solubility of the targeted compounds, the extraction solvent used in this process can change. For example, 
more polar compounds require polar solvents for better extraction efficiency and quality of the extract 
(Rutkowska et al., 2017). 
In this study, an evaluation of the effect of ethanol as a co-solvent in the SC-CO2 extraction, and as a solvent 
in the UAE process, on the extraction efficiency and extraction of polar compounds like phospholipids and 
phenolic compounds from the meal and the seeds was performed. For the SC-CO2 extraction, different 
extraction times and temperatures were assessed with a two-way ANOVA analysis to evaluate the yield 
from defatted meal. Also, the total fatty acid methylesters (FAME) method was utilized to determine the 
total amount of fatty acids in the raw material, the extracted oil and the defatted meal; and the lipid class 
method was used to determine the lipid composition (neutral lipids, free fatty acids and phospholipids) of 
the raw material and the extracted oil. For the UAE, the effect of extraction time, amplitude and 
sample/solvent ratio was assessed with a response surface methodology using the Box-Behnken design, 
in order to optimize the yield from rapeseed seeds. The total FAME and lipid class methods were also 
utilized to determine the lipid composition of the extracted oil. Furthermore, a total phenol content 
analysis was performed on the extracted oil and the defatted meal to quantify the phenolic compounds in 
the matrices.

2. Aim 
The objective of the present study was to examine the parameters affecting the two green extraction 
methods -supercritical CO2 extraction and ultrasound-assisted extraction- using ethanol as a (co-) solvent. 
The effect of the selected processing parameters in both extraction methods was assessed based on the 
yield efficiency. The extracted oil quality was also characterized, focusing on the polar compounds such as 
phospholipids and phenolic compounds to evaluate the effect of ethanol as an extraction solvent. 
3. Background 
3.1 Rapeseed 
Brassica napus Linnaeus, widely referred to as rapeseed or oilseed rape, is a crop used mainly for oil 
production (Izli et al., 2009). It is a hybrid between turnip rape (Brassica rapa) and cabbage (Brassica 
oleracege) that happened spontaneously over 7500 years ago. Rapeseed can be traced back to India and 
China since 4000 BC and 2000 BC, respectively (Chew, 2020). 
 
3.1.1 
World production  
Rapeseed is among the most popular crops cultivated globally, involving around 66 countries. Following 
soybean and palm oil, it ranks as the third most produced edible oil (Chew, 2020). Based on data from the 
United States Department of Agriculture (USDA, 2024), rapeseed world production exceeded 88 million 
metric tons (MMT) in 2023. European Union (EU) countries contributed 23% (20 MMT) of the world 
production, of which Germany, France and Poland are the leading producers with 21%, 21% and 18%, 
respectively. Outside the EU, countries that produced the most rapeseed in 2023 were Canada with 21% 
(18.8 MMT) of the world production, China 17% (15.4 MMT), India 14% (12.5 MMT), Australia 6% (5.5 
MMT), Ukraine 5% (4.4 MMT), Russia 5% (4.2 MMT), United States 2% (1.9 MMT) and United Kingdom 1% 
(1.2 MMT). 4% of the world's rapeseed production comes from countries that cultivated less than 1% (< 1 
MMT). A world map visual representation of the 2023 rapeseed production is depicted in Figure 1, and 
the percentage of exports and imports of rapeseed by country is presented in Table 1.

3.1.2 
Plant description 
3.1.2.1 Rapeseed varieties 
Rapeseed species are annually planted, and depending on their variety (winter, semi-winter, spring 
variety), they vary in cold tolerance and growing conditions, and hence, they thrive in different regions of 
the world. As seen in Figure 2, winter and semi-winter varieties are mostly cultivated in Europe and Asia, 
respectively, and spring varieties are mostly cultivated in Canada, Australia and Northern Europe 
(SzydłOwska-Czerniak, 2013).

3.1.2.2 Rapeseed genotypes 
A wide range of genotypes, derived from different origins and hybrids, have been created and are available 
commercially, for both winter and spring varieties, in the last years (Takashima et al., 2013). These different 
species have been created to optimize the cultivation needs and yield capacity, and thus the profitability 
of rapeseed production. Environmental factors differ in various cultivation locations and can affect plant 
development. For this reason, different species have been developed to withstand these diverse 
conditions. Yield stability and yield optimization also play a crucial role in genotype development as 
sustaining yearly production with optimal yield capacity is of high priority for farmers (Nowosad et al., 
2016).  
Furthermore, some hybridization of rapeseed species was developed to enhance the quality of the oil and 
the meal. The oil produced from Brassicas species contains substantial quantities (22-60%) of erucic acid 
(C:21, cis 13- docosenoic acid) and glucosinolates, which prohibits the use of this oil in food (Shahidi, 1990). 
The development of Canola oil, free from erucic acid and low glucosinolates opened different possibilities 
in the food and feed industry (Abbadi and Leckband, 2011). The description of this oil genotype will be 
further discussed in a later paragraph.  
Rapeseed breeders understand the effects of environment, yield and fatty acid composition that the 
different genotypes offer, and they produce them depending on the final purpose of usage (Nowosad et 
al., 2016). 
 
3.1.3 
Plant morphology 
Morphologically, the stem of rapeseed can be 120-150 cm high. The flower of the crop is yellow, and the 
seeds are spherical with 1.8-2.7 mm diameter and 3-7 mg weight (Figure 3). Depending on the variety, 
they usually require 110-150 days to fully mature, where they turn from green color seeds to a red-black 
color (Raboanatahiry et al., 2021).

the leaves, the stem and the root can also be used in various applications like oil production, animal feed, 
cosmetics and biodiesel (Orlovius, 2003; Chew, 2020; Raboanatahiry et al., 2021).

3.2.1 
Seed composition characterization 
Although different rapeseed varieties and species may be close in appearance, they can vary in their 
chemical composition (Table 2). A study by Eriksson et al. (1994), investigated the chemical composition 
of different rapeseed seeds from spring and winter varieties, where on average, the oil content was 
45.35%, the protein content was 21.4%, and the ash content was 3.9%.   
Another research, conducted by Nosenko et al. (2014), on the chemical properties of spring and winter 
varieties, showed that on average, the oil content was 43.65%, the protein content was 24.8%, and the 
moisture was 5.3%.    
There are, however, reported species that contain more than 50% oil in their seeds, which shows that 
rapeseed still holds potential for improvement in the oil production industry (Hu et al., 2013).

3.3 Rapeseed oil 
3.3.1 
Types of oil from different rapeseed species 
As mentioned above, the original wild-type rapeseed (B. napus) is not used for food and feed as it contains 
high levels of erucic acid and glucosinolates. Erucic acid is a toxic compound, and glucosinolates are 
compounds that contain sulfur and nitrogen that contribute to pungent and unpleasant taste. For this 
reason, breeding efforts were made in order to introduce rapeseed for consumption purposes (Chew, 
2020).  
In 1968, a new genotype of rapeseed species was produced in Canada that resulted in oil with erucic acid 
lower than 5%. These species were called “single-low”, or “single-zero”. By 1979, these species were 
altered to erucic acid lower than 2% and glucosinolates amount lower than 30 μmol/g. The newly formed 
species also contained high oil content (35-50 %) and high protein content (36-44 %). They were named 
“Canola” (Canadian Oilseed, Low Acid) oil or “double low”, “double zero” or “00-quality” and these names 
represented all species with these two baseline characteristics. From 1990 until now, Canola has been 
used for food and feed production exclusively, and it has been the primary type of rapeseed that is 
cultivated in the EU (Shahidi, 1990; Chew, 2020). 
Another example of oil from a new genotype was produced in 2008, and it is called HOLL rapeseed oil. 
This species yields oil rich in oleic acid and low in alpha-linolenic acid content that provides great oxidative 
stability thus, it is preferred for high-temperature processes, such as frying oil, but it is not very suitable 
for direct cold consumption (Jahreis and Schäfer, 2011).

3.3.2 
Oil composition characterization  
The growing demand for rapeseed is attributed to the high-quality oil produced for food, feed and non-
food uses. Different genotypes are constantly introduced in order to improve the nutritional characteristics 
of rapeseed oils and more specifically their fatty acid profiles (Abbadi and Leckband, 2011).  
As mentioned previously, different varieties and species can differ in chemical composition, but in general, 
rapeseed oils are defined by a low content of saturated fatty acids (5-10%), and a high amount of mono-
unsaturated fatty acids (44-75%) and poly-unsaturated fatty acids (22-35%). Also, the ratio of 
approximately 2:1 of omega-6 to omega-3 FAs is contained in the oil, which is proven to be favorable to 
human health by modulating inflammatory processes and supporting cardiovascular health (Jahreis and 
Schäfer, 2011).  
The main fatty acids reported in different studies consist of 59.07-64.79% oleic acid (C 18:1), 18.15-21.65% 
linoleic acid (C 18:2) and 3-9.22% alpha-linolenic acid (C 18:3), as seen in Table 3 (Szydłowska-Czerniak et 
al., 2010; Nosenko et al., 2014; Matthaus et al., 2016; Beyzi et al., 2019).

content varies from 80-1159 mg/kg, of which 65% is γ-tocopherol and 35% is α-tocopherol. Phenolic

3.3.3 
Traditional extraction methods 
The conventional extraction of oil from rapeseed is accomplished by heated seed pressing followed by 
solvent extraction of the meal. The stages of this process consist of seed cleaning, preheating, flaking, 
cooking, mechanical heated screw pressing, and at a later stage, hexane solvent extraction (Figure 4) 
(Gaber et al., 2018). The crude rapeseed oil from the mechanical heated screw-pressing has a dark color 
and distinctive aroma and taste. Due to market preference, this crude oil undergoes a refining process, to 
a final product with mild aroma and light (yellow) color. The process of refining involves degumming, 
neutralization, bleaching and deodorization. After this process, the compounds that provide beneficial 
effects on human health, such as antioxidants, tocopherols, phytosterols, phenolic compounds, and also, 
pigments and volatile components are removed (SzydłOwska-Czerniak, 2013).  
This traditional extraction method has proven efficient in terms of recovery that can reach 70-94%, 
combining both the heated seed pressing and the solvent extraction of the residual oil from the meal. It 
has a high smoke point (>200oC) and a long shelf life (Chew, 2020).  There are, however, some drawbacks 
of this method such as the emission of volatile compounds into the atmosphere, elevated operation cost, 
and inferior oil quality due to operation temperatures that can reach as high as 180oC (SzydłOwska-
Czerniak, 2013, Gaber et al., 2018). 
 
Another method of rapeseed oil production is cold-pressed extraction. Cold-pressing consists of seed 
fragmentation, seed conditioning and seed pressing. The cold-press stage is accomplished using a 
hydraulic or screw press, optionally adding a cooling system in order not to exceed 60oC in the process. 
Then the oil is cleaned with processes like filtration, sedimentation or centrifugation (Chew, 2020).  This 
method produces oil that retains its nutritional characteristics and can be consumed unrefined, thus it is 
considered a high-quality oil. The drawback of this method is the high amount of residual oil remaining in 
the meal after the press extraction, which can reach up to 25% (Zago et al, 2015; Chew, 2020). 
 
Alternative solutions are constantly investigated in order to produce a viable method suitable for the 
extraction of rapeseed oil that produces good yield capacity and good oil quality (Essien et al., 2020).

3.3.4 
Alternative uses 
Rapeseed oil is also used in various applications and with the correct seed genotypes it can fulfill many 
different desirable conditions. With these alterations, rapeseed oil can be used for cosmetics, 
pharmaceutical products, detergents, pesticides, inks, textiles, plastics biofuel, and many others 
(Raboanatahiry et al., 2021) 
Specifically, the energy crisis and the constant demand for biofuel are driving rapeseed production higher 
year by year. Rapeseed is the leading oil used for biodiesel production in the European market. The 
benefits of rapeseed for biodiesel include its high oil content and low iodine value relative to other oil 
crops, which can generate more energy, and less oxidation or undesirable deposit formations that can 
damage mechanical parts; its fluid properties at low temperatures; and its environmental benefits and 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
biodegradability (  ̴30 days) compared to fossil diesel. The drawbacks of rapeseed oil biofuel are the cost, 
the storage, and its degradability on some materials (Raboanatahiry et al., 2021). 
 
3.4 Rapeseed meal 
Rapeseed meal or rapeseed cake is the by-product remaining after the oil extraction. Approximately, for 
every kilogram of rapeseed oil, twice the amount of meal is produced (Lomascolo et al., 2012; Zago et al, 
2015). 
Rapeseed meal was mostly used as fertilizer (SzydłOwska-Czerniak, 2013). After the production of canola 
varieties, the meal produced from these varieties was able to be used also as animal feed. Further genetic 
improvement of varieties based on the meal quality for feed use includes the reduction of dietary fibers 
in the coat of the seeds and the reduction of sinapinoylcholine, commonly known as sinapine, an anti-
nutritive compound for livestock (Abbadi and Leckband, 2011). 
 
3.4.1 
Meal production 
In the conventional extraction method, a solvent is applied after the heated pressing to extract the 
remaining oil from the meal/cake, and the solvent-extracted oil is mixed with the crude oil before the 
refining process thus the extraction yield of the solvent extraction method can reach up to 94%. With this 
process though, residual solvent of around 100 ppm can remain in the final oil degrading its quality 
(SzydłOwska-Czerniak, 2013; Gaber et al., 2018).  
As mentioned before, in the cold press method the residual oil in the meal can reach up to 25% (Zago et 
al, 2015). For this reason, usually, a solvent treatment is used as well, in order to extract the high amounts 
of remaining oil (SzydłOwska-Czerniak, 2013). 
 
3.4.2

measured the chemical composition of rapeseed meal. The tested meal was produced by grinding and 
conditioning of the seeds, mechanical pressing followed by hexane extraction of the residual oil, and finally 
the process of toasting, drying and cooling of the meal. The results shown in Table 4 depict the composition 
of the different rapeseed meals. In the study of Bojanowska (2017), the main FAs of the tested meal were

3.5 Green extraction methods 
Extraction methods enable the isolation and transfer of compounds of interest into a more favorable phase 
that can be further processed. However, conventional extraction techniques that have been extensively 
used are nowadays proven unsuitable (Pena-Pereira and Tobiszewski, 2017). 
Sustainability is highly appraised and environmentally friendly practices and profitable techniques are in 
constant demand to substitute the conventional extraction methods that produce large organic waste 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
amounts or generate poor extraction capacity. Both supercritical fluid extraction (SFE) and ultrasound-
assisted extraction (UAE) are regarded as “green” extraction techniques (Essien et al., 2020).  
 
3.6 Ethanol as solvent 
Ethanol is commonly utilized for the extraction of bioactive components. It is considered a green solvent 
because it is biodegradable, renewable and generally recognized as safe (GRAS) in food applications. 
Furthermore, it is characterized by low toxicity, and high polarity which makes it effective in extracting 
polar compounds such as phenolic compounds and phospholipids. Studies have shown that, in some 
cases, the addition of ethanol can enhance the extraction yield and quality of the extracted oils, compared 
to conventional solvents (Stevanato and da Silva, 2019).  
 
3.7 Supercritical fluid extraction 
SFE uses the thermodynamic and kinetic properties of a fluid when it is above its critical point (Figure 6). 
This fluid reaches a supercritical state if both pressure and temperature exceed their critical point (Ghosh 
& Pradhan, 2019).  
 
 
Figure 6: Phase diagram of a substance in terms of pressure and temperature (Ghosh & Pradhan, 2019). 
 
With a combination of a high-pressure and low-temperature process, the natural characteristics of 
extracted compounds can be retained, without using high amounts of organic solvents, resulting in a final 
product with higher value (de Melo et al., 2017). 
At supercritical conditions, the fluid displays properties between a gas and a liquid, also known as “snow” 
phase (Ghosh & Pradhan, 2019). This phase creates low surface tension with liquid-like density and gas-
like viscosity, promoting extraction efficiency by producing high diffusivity and low viscosity, thus fast mass 
transfer in the extraction. Altering the temperature and pressure conditions of the fluid, can alter its 
solvent extraction properties such as its solubility efficiency, the dielectric constant and the partition 
coefficient (Vázquez et al., 2020). 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
SFE can be used by different industries like pharmaceutical or cosmetic, but mostly it is used in the food 
industry to extract compounds and deodorize or fractionate mixtures. It can also be used for the removal 
of toxic compounds, as a food preservation method or as a drying method (Wang et al., 2021). 
 
3.8 Supercritical CO2 extraction  
Different solvents can be used at their supercritical state in an SFE, such as CO2, N2O, NH3, C3H8, C2H4, Xe, 
Ar and H2O. Among them, carbon dioxide (CO2) is the most common due to its solvent properties and 
numerous benefits. CO2 has low viscosity, high diffusivity and low critical conditions with a temperature of 
31.1 oC and pressure of 74.8bar (Cunico and Turner, 2017; Vázquez et al., 2020).  
 
3.8.1 Advantages and disadvantages of SC-CO2 extraction 
Furthermore, CO2 is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) compound; it is chemically inert, non-flammable 
and non-corrosive; it is widely used and can be easily recycled, thus it is less expensive. Also, CO2 has a 
superior selectivity and better extraction rates compared to organic solvents and it can be easily separated 
from the extract by depressurization. Supercritical CO2 (SC-CO2) extraction is one of the most effective and 
environmentally friendly extraction methods. With SC-CO2 extraction, non-polar and some mid-polar 
compounds can be recovered; the low extraction temperatures do not damage thermos-sensitive 
compounds and help retain their sensory characteristics; and there is less oxidation reaction development 
(de Melo, 2017; Ghosh & Pradhan, 2019; Vázquez et al., 2020). 
Disadvantages of SC-CO2 include its inferior solvent power compared to organic solvents and its low 
solubility for polar compounds. In the latter, organic solvent can be added as a co-solvent in order to 
achieve the extraction of more polar compounds. Also, SC-CO2 and SFE in general, due to their equipment 
cost, require high initial investment and energy costs. This drawback, however, is surpassed by the low 
manufacturing costs compared to traditional extraction methods (Vázquez et al., 2020). 
 
3.8.2 
Parameters affecting the SC-CO2 extraction 
3.8.2.1 Pressure 
Pressure is among the crucial parameters that affect the SC-CO2 process. An increase in pressure raises the 
density of the solvent, which enhances solubility and results in higher extraction yield. By fluctuating the 
pressure, different targeted compounds can be extracted. However, very high pressure can result in a 
lower extraction yield, reduced recovery of targeted compounds and an increase in the extraction of 
unwanted compounds (Essien et al., 2020). Non-polar or slightly polar compounds can be extracted at a 
reduced pressure of 75-100 bar while moderate polar compounds require higher pressures. (Uwineza and 
Waśkiewicz, 2020). 
 
3.8.2.2 Temperature 
Temperature is another important parameter in SC-CO2 extraction. Increasing the temperature results, on 
one hand, an increase in solubility because of elevated vapor pressure, but on the other hand, it will 
decrease the dissolving power of the solvent due to a decrease in its density. These contradictory reactions 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
of temperature mean that a trade-off is required between these effects when optimizing the conditions 
for higher yield (Essien et al., 2020).  
 
3.8.2.3 Density 
Density is affected by temperature and pressure, and higher temperatures require proportionally higher 
pressure in order to achieve high density. A study from Cunico and Turner (2017) shows different densities 
achieved with different combinations of pressure and temperature ranging from 7.4-60 MPa and 31-100 
oC, respectively (Table 5). A density of 0.8 g/mL or higher is more optimal for the extraction of compounds 
such as lipids (Cunico and Turner, 2017).

3.8.2.4 Extraction time 
Time can also affect the extraction yield in an SC-CO2 extraction. Extended interaction between solvent 
and raw material can lead to higher yield and the extraction of different targeted compounds, as this effect 
has been demonstrated in several studies (Yang et al., 2013; Ekinci and Gürü, 2019; Essien et al., 2020). 
After a specific period though, the effect of time is no longer significant as the compounds have been 
extracted and the yield has reached its limit. After this point, prolonging the extraction time will mostly 
cause an extension in energy consumption and thus increase the production costs (Essien et al., 2020).  
 
3.8.2.5 Solvent flow-rate 
The flow rate of the solvent has an effect on the contact time with the raw material and thus the mass 
transfer coefficient. An increased CO2 flow rate leads to an increase in the mass transfer and thus an 
increase in the extraction efficiency. A very high CO2 flow rate, however, can reduce the extraction yield 
because of inadequate contact time between the solvent and the raw material (Essien et al., 2020). A study 
by Herzi et al. (2013) demonstrated the dependency of flow rate and extraction time, as the extraction 
was efficient with higher flow rate and less time, but insufficient with low flow rate and higher extraction 
time.  
 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
3.8.2.6 Particle size 
Small particle size of the raw material increases its specific interfacial area and therefore there is an 
increase in the extraction rate. However, using very fine particles can create clumping or clogging of the 
raw material and block the filters which results in a reduction of the extraction efficiency (Essien et al., 
2020). According to Uwineza and Waśkiewicz (2020), a mean particle size of 0.25-2.0mm is appropriate in 
SC-CO2 extraction. 
 
3.8.2.7 Moisture content 
Lastly, moisture of the raw material is another factor that can affect the extraction process. High water 
content in the sample can serve as a barrier to the diffusion of the CO2. Therefore, a dried sample or a raw 
material with a moisture content of around 4-14% is recommended (Li et al., 2010; Uwineza and 
Waśkiewicz, 2020). 
 
3.8.3 
Co-solvent in SC-CO2 extraction 
As mentioned before, CO2 is a solvent that can extract non-polar or low-polar compounds. Therefore, the 
introduction of a co-solvent in the extraction process can increase the polarity affinity of CO2 and thus the 
extraction yield with the extraction of more polar compounds. The co-solvent selection depends on the 
characteristics of the sample and the properties of the targeted compounds. Ethanol, methanol, acetone, 
water or their combinations in different concentrations can be used as co-solvents (SzydłOwska-Czerniak, 
2013). Amongst them, the most extensively used solvent is ethanol due to its low miscibility with the CO2, 
its low toxicity to human health and easy elimination compared to similar solvents. (de Melo et al., 2017; 
Essien et al., 2020). 
Numerous studies demonstrate that the addition of ethanol in SC-CO2 extraction increases the total yield 
and the extraction of high-value polar compounds such as carotenoids, phenolic compounds, tocopherols, 
phytosterols, etc. (Shi et al., 2010; Uquiche et al., 2019; Asl et al., 2020; Essien et al., 2020). 
As the co-solvent in the SFE process is collected with the extracted sample, it is essential to remove it at 
the end of the process to get the pure extract (SzydłOwska-Czerniak, 2013). 
 
3.8.4 
SC-CO2 extraction equipment and process 
The SC-CO2 extraction system consists of several equipment (Figure 7). Pressurized carbon dioxide in liquid 
state is stored in tanks. These tanks are connected to a chiller where the CO2 is cooled to around 4oC to 
increase its viscosity for easier pumping and then it is pushed with a high-pressure pump into the system. 
In this stage, if used, the co-solvent is introduced to the system as well. Then the CO2 is heated and 
pressurized to the selected supercritical conditions and pushed into the extraction vessel where it is mixed 
with the raw material. This process takes place in an oven where the supercritical conditions can be 
preserved.  In the extraction vessel, the solvent at a supercritical state, flows through the sample where 
the targeted compounds are dissolved and carried out with the CO2 and co-solvent. Finally, the mixture is 
depressurized by a back pressure regulator valve and the CO2 is evaporated or recycled back into the 
system. The extract is collected in the collection vessel with the co-solvent, which is essential to be 
removed at a later stage of the process to acquire the pure extract. The whole process is controlled through 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
a computer software where the selected conditions of temperature, pressure, time and CO2 and co-solvent 
flow rates can be determined (SzydłOwska-Czerniak, 2013; Cunico and Turner, 2017; de Melo et al., 2017; 
Shi et al., 2023).

3.8.5 
Supercritical CO2 extraction of oil 
Many studies have evaluated the different extraction methods of oil from a variety of seeds -including 
rapeseed- in terms of yield and oil quality. These studies assessed the SC-CO2 extraction without and with 
co-solvent compared to chemical solvent extraction. 
Castro-Vargas et al. (2010) reported that SC-CO2 with ethanol co-solvent was a better method for obtaining 
high yield and total phenols from guava seeds, compared to solvent extraction and SC-CO2 with ethyl 
acetate co-solvent. Ferrentino et al. (2019) on brewer’s spent grain, observed higher recovery (81.3%) and 
phenol content (26.2 mg GAE/g) using SC-CO2 with 8% ethanol co-solvent at 300 bar and 50 oC for 1 hour 
compared to solvent extraction, while the fatty acid composition remained the same in both methods.  
Boutin and Badens (2009) evaluated both sunflower seeds and rapeseed seeds without the addition of co-
solvent, using a wide range of extraction parameters. For both seeds they found that maximum recovery 
(89.8% for rapeseed and 78.5% for sunflower seeds) was achieved at 450bar, 75 oC for 120 min and 19 
kg/h solvent flow rate. In this study, it was also stated that no phospholipids were found in the extracted 
oil and for extracting such components a co-solvent should be introduced in the process (Boutin and 
Badens, 2009). 
Yu et al. (2012) found that the optimal extraction yield was 32.65% at 40 oC, 345 bar for 3 hours using SC-
CO2 without co-solvent for rapeseed oil. Buskol et al. (1997) observed lower phenol content in oil extracted 
with SC-CO2 compared to oil extracted with organic solvent. In the same study, it was also reported that 
the phospholipids present in the oil extracted with SC-CO2 (without co-solvent) were less than 0.5mg/g 
compared to about 6mg/g with organic solvent extraction. Therefore, the authors claimed that the slightly 
higher extraction yield with the solvent method was because of the small amount of polar compounds 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
(phenolic compounds and phospholipids) that the SC-CO2 method was not able to extract (Buskol et al., 
1997). 
Mohamad (2023), in his research using the same lab-scale equipment, evaluated the optimization of the 
SC-CO2 extraction of rapeseed without ethanol co-solvent and found that at 250 bar, 60 oC, 12 ml/min 
solvent flow rate and 90 min extraction time, the highest yield was achieved with 35.5%.  
Lastly, only few studies have evaluated the SC-CO2 extraction of oil from defatted rapeseed meal. Uquiche 
et al. (2012) examined the SC-CO2 extraction of oil from cold-pressed rapeseed cake. Pressure was found 
to influence the extraction yield the most, and optimal yield (13.69%) was achieved at 400 bar, 60 oC and 
120 min (Uquiche et al., 2012).  
Kim et al. (2013) studied rapeseed meal which was previously defatted with chemical solvents. The optimal 
yield using SC-CO2 extraction with ethanol co-solvent on the defatted material was 5.98% and was 
obtained at 350 bar, 65 oC, 6 L/min solvent flow rate and 228.55g co-solvent. The neutral lipids and 
phospholipids extracted from the meal under these conditions were 3% and 69.43%, respectively (Kim et 
al., 2013). 
 
3.9 Ultrasound-assisted extraction 
Ultrasound-assisted extraction (UAE) is another green extraction method. This technique utilizes ultrasonic 
waves in a frequency range of 20-100 kHz. These waves create compression and rarefaction cycles 
(negative pressure into the solvent that forms voids into the liquid). This phenomenon is called cavitation 
and creates small air bubbles in the solvent. The bubbles grow bigger and then collapse at their critical 
point generating intense local pressure (50-1000 atm) and heat (> 4000 oC). This effect disrupts the cell 
walls and increases the permeability and thus increases mass transfer by releasing the intracellular 
compounds into the solvent phase (Figure 8) (Jalili et al., 2017; Perrier et al. 2017; Chavan et al., 2022). 
 
 
Figure 8: Ultrasound-assisted extraction, cavitation phenomenon representation (Patra et al., 2021). 
 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
The benefits of UAE include high mass and energy transfer, shorter extraction time, smaller size equipment 
and fewer processing stages (Jalili et al., 2017; Perrier et al., 2017). On the other hand, high equipment 
costs and frequent equipment damage due to cavitation erosion can be some of the disadvantages of UAE 
(Chemat et al., 2017; Stella and Alcivar, 2019). 
 
3.9.1 
Parameters affecting the UAE 
3.9.1.1 Extraction Time 
The extraction time is the duration of the ultrasonic treatment and therefore the extent of cavitation and 
cell disruption. Studies have shown that extraction time mostly ranges between 10 and 60 min, and usually 
the longer the extraction duration the more optimal the extraction yield (Farahani and Azari, 2016). 
 
3.9.1.2 Amplitude and intensity 
Amplitude is correlated to the intensity of the ultrasonic wave. Intensity is the transmitted energy per 
emitting surface. An increase in the amplitude means an increase in the intensity of the process. By 
increasing the amplitude, there is an increase in the wavelengths and therefore bubble collapse is more 
violent. In samples with high viscosity, the amplitude should be increased in order to achieve better 
cavitation. However, very high amplitudes can result in liquid agitation rather than cavitation and 
inadequate wave transmission through the solvent (Chemat et al., 2017; Rutkowska et al., 2017). 
 
3.9.1.3 Ultrasound power 
Acoustic power (W) is the energy produced by the waves that is transformed into heat. The higher the 
power, the higher the achieved yield (Rutkowska et al., 2017). This parameter is frequently adjusted in 
order to use the least amount of power for the optimal extraction efficiency, and it is highly affected by 
other parameters like time and amplitude (Chemat et al., 2017). 
 
3.9.1.4 Frequency 
Ultrasound frequency is measured in Hertz (Hz), and ranges from 20 to 100 kHz are most common in the 
UAE method. The frequency determines the size of the bubbles and influences the resistance to the mass 
transfer. Higher frequency means lower cavitation, as at high frequencies the cycles of compression-
rarefaction are shorter and thus the size of the microbubbles is also lower. At lower frequencies, 
compression-rarefaction cycles are fewer but with higher diameter hence creating better cavitation and 
more violent eruption of the bubbles (Chemat et al., 2017). 
 
3.9.1.5 Solvent 
The solvent media used in the UAE is a critical parameter. Viscosity, surface tension, vapor pressure and 
solubility of target compounds are parameters that should be taken into consideration when choosing the 
extraction solvent as they are affecting the extraction. For example, high viscosity and surface tension 
solvents decrease the cavitation and therefore the amplitude should be increased to obtain the necessary 
waves to achieve good extraction. Also, more polar targeted compounds require polar solvents for better 
efficiency and quality of the extract (Rutkowska et al., 2017). 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
3.9.1.6 Temperature 
Temperature is a factor affecting the solvent’s properties. Higher temperatures decrease the viscosity and 
surface tension of the solvents, but they also raise the vapor pressure, causing vapor to fill the cavitation 
bubbles making them collapse less violently. In general, higher temperatures increase the extraction yield. 
However, the extraction temperature should be selected according to the nature of the target compounds.  
Lower temperatures should be used to avoid degradation when dealing with thermolabile compounds 
(Rutkowska et al., 2017). Some studies have also stated that extraction temperatures close to the solvent’s 
boiling point are less effective and low temperatures, below 30oC, are more beneficial for the UAE (Chemat 
et al., 2017). 
 
3.9.1.7 Sample 
The analyzed sample is another factor that can affect the UAE's efficiency. Some of its parameters consist 
of the solubility of the sample’s targeted compounds in the solvent, the sample/solvent ratio and the 
sample’s particle size. As mentioned earlier, more polar compounds in the sample require polar solvents 
for better extraction; a higher sample/solvent ratio increases the viscosity of the sample-solvent mixture 
and thus, decreases the cavitation phenomenon); and lastly, the finer the particle size, the better the 
extraction achieved due to better penetration of the solvent (Rutkowska et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2018). 
 
3.9.2 
UAE equipment and process 
The UAE process involves the application of ultrasonic waves to a mixture of solvent and sample, 
promoting the cavitation phenomenon and enhancing the mass transfer. The equipment used includes an 
ultrasonic generator, a transducer with a probe at its end, and an extraction vessel with a cooling jacket 
connected to a water-cooling bath, as seen in Figure 9. The generator produces electrical energy and 
converts it to sound waves which are transmitted by the transducer. The probe is immersed directly into 
the extraction vessel which contains the solvent-sample mixture. The heating of the mixture created by 
the cavitation is monitored with a thermometer and cooled using a water bath (Chemat et al., 2017; 
Rodrigues and Fernandes, 2017).

3.9.3 
Ultrasound-assisted extraction of oil 
The parameters, as mentioned earlier, play a significant role in the UAE of oil from seeds. Especially when 
focusing on extraction efficiency, the used solvent is very important. Several studies have evaluated 
different raw materials -including rapeseed-, different parameters and different solvents for oil extraction 
using ultrasound. 
Sicaire et al. (2016) observed an extraction recovery of 84% of the total oil found in the studied rapeseed 
meal using hexane as solvent, with ultrasonic intensity, temperature and sample/solvent ratio playing the 
most significant effect in the yield, and values at 7.7 W/cm2, 40oC and 1:15 ratio, respectively. 
Perrier et al. (2017) studied the addition of hexane, isopropanol and ethanol as a solvent in UAE of 
rapeseed flakes, with recovery reaching 67%, 65% and 41%, respectively, at 20min extraction time and 
1:30 sample/solvent ratio. Also, Moradi et al. (2017) found 11% extraction yield of oil from sunflower seeds 
at 20oC, 1:12 sample/solvent ratio and 2 hours extraction time. Overall, ethanol exhibited lower extraction 
yield in these studies compared to other solvents. 
Conversely, in the study conducted by de Menezes Rodrigues et al. (2017), ethanol seems to be more 
efficient than isopropanol for macauba pulp oil extraction. In this study, the achieved extraction oil 
recovery, with 60oC, 90min time and 1:10 pulp/solvent ratio, was 49.2% using ethanol and 38% using 
isopropanol (de Menezes Rodrigues et al., 2017). This could be attributed to the different lipid composition 
of the raw material, due to ethanol having a better affinity to polar lipids (such as phenolics and 
phospholipids), while triacylglycerol, the main component of rapeseed, is more soluble in non-polar 
solvents (Perrier et al., 2017). 
Nevertheless, all studies demonstrated that the fatty acid composition was not altered significantly when 
using different solvents, or when using UAE compared to traditional extraction methods, reinforcing the 
belief that the FA profile does not depend on extraction technology or solvent used (Sicaire et al., 2016; 
de Menezes Rodrigues et al., 2017; Moradi et al., 2017; Perrier et al., 2017). 
Regarding phenolic content, Wu et al. (2024) observed that UAE of oil from sea buckhorn using 75% 
ethanol solvent, extracted the highest total phenol content (TPC) compared to other solvents with the 
same concentration. Teh and Birch (2014) reported that the best parameter combination for extracting 
polyphenols from rapeseeds was 200W power, 70oC temperature, 1:10 sample/solvent ratio and 20 min 
extraction time, using a mixture of methanol/acetone/water (7:7:6 v/v/v). The total phenolics found in 
this study, using these parameters was 25.64mg GAE/g (Teh and Birch, 2014).

4.3 Laboratory supercritical CO2 extraction process  
The lab-scale supercritical CO2 extraction was performed using the MV-10 ASFE System (Waters, Milford, 
MA, USA). The experimental procedure consisted of two extraction cycles. 
The first cycle was a defatting process where it involved placing 7g of ground seeds into a 25mL extraction 
vessel, with cotton placed at each end of the vessel to avoid filter blockage. The vessel was connected to 
the SC-CO2 inlet and outlet lines and placed into the system oven to reach the desired temperature. The 
CO2, stored in a CO2 tank, was cooled with a cooler and was pumped into the system at the desired 
pressure, using a high-pressure pump. The process parameters of the first -defatting- cycle were based on 
the previous work by Mohamad (2023) and consisted of 90 min extraction time, 60oC extraction 
temperature, 250 bar extraction pressure and 12 mL/min CO2 flow rate. The extract (oil) was collected in 
a blue-cup bottle and the raw material inside the extraction vessel was identified as defatted meal after 
the end of the first extraction cycle.  
The second cycle was the introduction of co-solvent (ethanol) in the process. This cycle was performed 
directly after the first cycle to determine the leftover lipids in the defatted meal and it consisted of the 
addition of ethanol together with the CO2 in the vessel. For the second cycle, the pressure was constant 
at 250 bar, the CO2 flow rate was 10.5 mL/min, the co-solvent flow rate was 1.5 mL/min and the used 
ethanol concentration was 96% for all the extractions. Different values for time and temperature were 
evaluated with a screening design. 10 min before the end of the second cycle, the co-solvent flow rate was 
halted, but the CO2 flow was maintained in order to remove the residual ethanol from the vessel. A 
depressurization of the system followed the end of the extraction cycle, and the extract combined with

4.4 Pilot-plant supercritical CO2 extraction process 
The pilot-plant supercritical CO2 extractions were performed using the equipment SFX5L (Core 
Separations, Denton, USA). The experimental procedure consisted of two extraction cycles. 
The first cycle was a defatting process where it involved placing 300g of ground seeds into a 5L extraction 
vessel. Glass beads were placed in the extraction vessel as well, to fill the empty space. Cotton was placed 
at each end of the vessel and between the beads and the sample to avoid filter blockage. The extraction 
vessel was placed into the heating chamber to reach the desired temperature. The CO2, stored in two CO2 
tanks, was cooled with a cooler and was pumped into the system at the desired pressure, using a high-
pressure pump. The process parameters of the first -defatting- cycle consisted of 90 min extraction time, 
60 oC extraction temperature, 250 bar extraction pressure and 450 g/min CO2 flow rate. The extract (oil) 
was collected in a blue-cup bottle and the raw material inside the extraction vessel was identified as 
defatted meal after the end of the first extraction cycle. 
The second cycle was the introduction of co-solvent (ethanol) in the process. This cycle was performed 
directly after the first cycle to determine the leftover lipids in the defatted meal, and it consisted of the 
addition of ethanol together with the CO2 in the vessel. For the second cycle, the extraction time was 90 
min, the extraction temperature was 60 oC, the pressure was 250 bar, the CO2 flow rate was 435 g/min, 
the co-solvent flow rate was 15 mL/min and the used ethanol concentration was 96%. A depressurization 
of the system followed the end of the extraction cycle, and the extract mixed with the co-solvent was 
collected in a different blue-cup bottle. Figure 11 depicts the equipment configuration that was used for 
the pilot-plant extractions. The ethanol in the final mixture was evaporated under nitrogen in a fume hood 
and the final weight of the oil was recorded for the oil yield calculations. The extracted oil was kept in the 
blue cup bottle covered in aluminum foil and placed in the freezer until further analysis.

4.5 Ultrasound-assisted extraction process 
The UAE experiments were performed using the high-intensity ultrasound system SFX Sonifier Ultrasonic 
Processor with the SSE-1 Sonifier Sound Enclosure (Branson, Connecticut, USA). The experimental 
procedure started with wetting the raw material in the solvent. Wetting consisted of mixing the ground 
rapeseed seeds (weight depended on the trial) with 80mL of ethanol in a 100mL beaker, for 30 min at 
500rpm. The mixture was then placed in the extraction vessel equipped with a cooling jacket which was 
connected to a circulated water-cooling system. The vessel was then placed into the soundproof chamber 
where the transducer’s probe was immersed in the middle of the extraction vessel. A thermocouple data 
logger (TC-08 Data Logger, Pico Technology Limited, GB) was also placed in the extraction vessel to 
measure the temperature changes in the extraction process. The extraction parameters were inserted 
using the screen panel of the ultrasonic generator. Figure 12 depicts the equipment used for the UAE 
experiments. At the end of the UAE the mixture (sample-solvent-extract) was placed in 50 mL centrifuge 
tubes and was centrifuged at 5000 g for 10 min to separate the heavy phase from the ethanol-lipid extract 
(light phase). The light phase was then placed in a 100 mL blue-cup bottle, covered in aluminum foil. The 
ethanol was evaporated from the extract under nitrogen in a fume hood and the final weight of the oil 
was recorded for the oil yield calculations. The bottle containing the extracted oil was finally placed in the 
freezer until further analysis.

4.7 Oil Characterization  
4.7.1 
Total Fatty Acid Methylesters analysis 
The total amount of fatty acids in the oil samples was determined according to the DTU’s Bioactive group 
protocol “Fatty acid methylesters, Producing fatty acid methylesters (FAME) / FF-Analysis instruction no. 
108.07”. This method converts medium- and long- chain fatty acids into fatty acid methylesters. 
Methanolic sodium hydroxide trans-esterifies the glycerol-bond FAs into methyl esters. Also, the 
hydrolytically released FAs and free fatty acids (FFAs) are methylated by methanolic bortrifluoride. Then 
the methylesters are extracted using heptane and analyzed using gas chromatography (GC) to determine 
the fatty acid composition (LST AF0082, 1985; AOCS Ce 1b-89, 1998; AOCS Ce 2-66, 1998). 
 
4.7.2 
Lipid class 
The separation of the lipids in different classes was conducted according to the DTU’s Bioactive group 
protocol “Lipid classes, Separation of neutral lipids, free fatty acids and phospholipids on mini-columns 
(“Solid-phase extraction”) / Analysis Instruction nr. 702.07”. In this method, the lipids are separated into 
groups: neutral lipids (NL), free fatty acids (FFA) and phospholipids (PL), by a chromatographic separation 
in solid-phase columns consisting of aminopropyl-modified silica (Bond Elute column, Waters, Dublin, 
Ireland). Solvents were used by increasing polarity for the desired separation of the classes (chloroform/2-
propanol 2:1 v/v, ethyl ether/acetic acid 98:2 v/v, methanol). After the three classes were separated and 
collected, they were methylated (see Paragraph 4.7.1) and analyzed using gas chromatography (GC) 
(Kaluzny et al., 1985; Kim and Salem, 1990; Hansen, 1992). 
 
4.7.3 
Gas chromatography 
Samples from total FAME and lipid class were measured using a gas chromatography system (HP-7890 A, 
Agilent Technologies, CA, USA) according to the DTU’s Bioactive group protocol “GLC-FAME, Gas-Liquid 
Chromatography of Fatty Acid Methyl Esters / Analysis Instruction no. 105.03”. With this method, the long 
chain (C12 - C24) saturated, monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fatty acids are determined using a 
highly polar stationary phase regarding their chain length, degree of (un-)saturation, position of the double 
bonds and geometry. The methylated FAs are identified using a flame ionization detector and their 
retention time in the GC column. They are then compared to the FAME reference standards and reference 
oils (SRM© 1946, NIST, USA) and visualized as peak areas in a chromatogram. The results are measured in 
relative (area % of total FAs) and absolute (weight/weight of lipids) values, using Eq. 8 and 9, respectively 
(Shantha and Ackman, 1991; AOCS Ce 1b-89. 1998; AOCS Ce 1i-07, 1998).

4.7.4 
Total phenol content 
The TPC was assessed using the DTU’s Bioactive group protocol “Total phenol content (TPC), 
Spectrophotometric determination of total phenol content using Folin Cicalteu reagent / Analysis 
Instruction nr. 204.01”. With this method, the total content of phenolic compounds, including 
monophenols, phenolic acids, coumarins, flavonoids, stilbenes, hydrolyzable tannins, lignins and lignans, 
can be determined. The samples are measured colorimetrically in UV-VIS spectrophotometer at 725nm, 
after reacting with the Folin-Ciocalteu reagent to form a blue complex. The results are quantified using 
gallic acid and they are expressed in mg gallic acid equivalents (GAE) per g sample (Lowry et al., 1951; 
Gulcin et al., 2004; Pelozo et al., 2008; Honold et al., 2016; Bastola et al., 2017).

methodology with a Box-Behnken design. Also, Microsoft 365 Excel was used in the data evaluation of the 
results of the oil analysis. 
 
5. Results and Discussion 
5.1 Sample characterization 
Different analyses were performed on the raw material to determine its characteristics and composition, 
prior to the extractions. 
 
5.1.1 
Dry matter, ash, protein and oil content of raw material 
The raw material was stored in an airtight container, at room temperature in a dry place away from the 
sun. The average dry matter between three measured samples was 91.41% ±0.06 respectively, and the 
ash content was 3.18% ±0.03. The protein content was calculated using the DUMAS method, in three raw 
material samples and the average value was found to be 12.41±0.45%. The oil of rapeseed seeds was 
extracted using solvents with the B&D method, and thus the oil content of the raw material was calculated. 
Two seed samples were measured and the average oil content was 53.07% ±1.02. The results of dry matter, 
ash content, protein content and oil content of the analyzed rapeseed seeds can be seen in Table 11.

Box-Behnken design to optimize these extraction parameters.

extraction of the polar compounds in the process that can increase the yield (%).

in order to conclude if the achieved results in this research were due to a lab-scale equipment capability, 
as almost maximum values were used for the extraction parameters, or to other factors. The SC-CO2 
extraction performed slightly worse compared to the solvent extraction method which can reach up to 
94% recovery, but slightly better compared to the cold-pressed method, which reaches around 75% 
recovery (Zago et al, 2015; Chew, 2020).

to achieve better extraction (Chemat et al., 2017; Rutkowska et al., 2017).

phenomenon (Rutkowska et al., 2017). Similarly, studies found low sample/solvent ratios as optimal with 
values ranging from 1:10 to 0.3:10 (de Menezes Rodrigues et al., 2017; Perrier et al., 2017). Therefore, the 
combination of high extraction time and amplitude, and low sample/solvent ratio would be the optimal 
conditions for high oil yield.

6. Strengths and limitations 
This study showcased several strengths and limitations. One of the main strengths was the evaluation of 
two green extraction methods studied in the same research, expanding the comparison between them 
and traditional extraction methods. Furthermore, by exploring the extraction of lipids from defatted 
rapeseed meal, it addressed a relatively under-researched area, contributing to new knowledge on this 
valuable material. Lastly, the focus of the research on polar compounds (phospholipids and phenolic 
compounds) highlighted the potential of sustainable extraction methods in utilizing high-value 
components from both the seeds and the residual meal. 
 
==PAGE:56==
56 
 
However, there were notable limitations as well. Equipment malfunctions in both lab-scale and pilot plant-
scale SC-CO2 set-ups resulted in lost time and numerous failed trials, hindering a more comprehensive 
evaluation of some parameters. Moreover, the limited amount of oil extracted with lab-scale equipment 
restricted the ability to execute some oil analyses such as phenolic compounds that require higher oil 
quantities. Lastly, the restriction of defatting the meal using an initial SC-CO2 extraction (1st cycle) rather 
than using an industrially defatted meal may have impacted the accuracy of the results. Using industrially 
defatted meal would better simulate the industry conditions and provide results that depict a real 
scenario. An industrially defatted meal was not able to be obtained by the rapeseed provider at the time 
of this research. Despite the challenges, this study successfully demonstrated the ability of green 
extraction methods in oil efficiency and quality. 
 
7. Conclusions  
This research assessed the addition of ethanol as solvent in the extraction of lipids from rapeseed meal 
and rapeseed seeds by using two green extraction methods. Specifically, the effect of different processing 
parameters in the evaluation of the extraction yield and the efficiency of extraction of polar compounds 
using SC-CO2 with ethanol co-solvent and UAE using ethanol solvent was analyzed. 
 
For the SC-CO2 extraction experiments, using the JMP software and performing a two-way ANOVA, 
between different values of time and temperature, the optimal conditions at 90 min extraction time and 
60 oC extraction temperature achieved an extraction yield of 46.59% and oil recovery of 87.79%. The 
addition of ethanol co-solvent resulted in a significantly higher extraction yield compared to the one 
obtained in the previous research of Mohamad (2023) using the same parameters without the co-solvent, 
and the extraction efficiency achieved was almost as high as using the B&D extraction method. The 
extraction of polar compounds was four times higher (0.039g/g ±0.001) when using SC-CO2 extraction with 
ethanol co-solvent compared to oil extracted with solvents (B&D method) (0.010g/g ±0.0002). This result 
verified that the use of ethanol co-solvent assists in the extraction of polar compounds in SC-CO2 
extraction.  
 
For the assessment of UAE in terms of yield, a response surface methodology based on the Box-Behnken 
design was conducted using the JMP software. Between the evaluated parameters and values, 15 min 
extraction time, 100% amplitude and 1:10 sample/solvent ratio were chosen as optimal, yielding 10.69% 
oil and reaching 20.14% recovery. Although the use of ethanol as solvent was not ideal for achieving high 
oil yields, it was efficient in extracting polar compounds from the rapeseed matrix reaching 0.056g/g 
±0.004 phospholipid amounts and 19.47 mg GAE/g phenolic compounds content. 
 
To conclude, SC-CO2 extraction using ethanol co-solvent benefited both the extraction efficiency and the 
extraction of polar compounds from rapeseed meal. In the UAE the use of ethanol solvent was not optimal 
in reaching high oil yield amounts, however, the extraction of valuable polar compounds from rapeseed 
substantially benefited from the addition of ethanol in the processes.

8. Future perspectives 
Future research could focus on a more in-depth evaluation of ethanol co-solvent in the SC-CO2 extraction 
experiments. More specifically, examining the effects of co-solvent flow rate and ethanol concentration, 
which were not able to be evaluated in this research, could give a more holistic view of the extraction 
efficiency. Also, pilot-plant experiments with higher values of some key parameters such as pressure and 
flow rate, could be conducted to evaluate the efficiency and scalability of the process. For the UAE, 
expanding the range of parameter values, such as time and sample/solvent ratio, or including the effect of 
temperature in the process could further enhance the extracted oil quality. Furthermore, experimentation 
with other green solvents or different solvent combinations, in both extraction methods, might prove 
beneficial, improving the overall extraction. Lastly, a life cycle assessment could provide relevant 
information on the benefits of replacing the traditional extraction methods with more sustainable ones, 
supporting the promotion and acceptance of those methods to the industry.
