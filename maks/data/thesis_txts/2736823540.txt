Abstract
The global energy system has to undergo a profound transformation to achieve the targets in
the Paris Agreement. In this regard, Hydrogen produced from renewable electricity - achieved
through an electrolyser - could facilitate the integration of high levels of variable renewable
energy (VRE) into the energy system[1]. This green Hydrogen can be a substitute fuel in de-
carbonisation and transiting to clean energy sources. Hydrogen has a high gravimetric energy
density and can be stored in either liquid or gaseous form. It can be burnt to release heat or
used in fuel cells to produce electricity, thereby providing a broad spectrum of applications[2].
Grid issues and increased competition to win subsidies are pushing offshore wind developers
to search for new business models to stay active in the market. One of the options could be
offshore Power-to-Hydrogen, which consists in the production of hydrogen using the electric-
ity from an offshore wind farm. On the other hand, for hydrogen producers, the changing
economics of offshore wind power present a highly encouraging business opportunity to utilize
wind electricity (specially near-shore curtailed) as a feed-stock for electrolysis[3].
This research has been focused on identifying the key factors and methodology on selecting a
specific topology for a Hybrid Power Plant (HPP) consisting of offshore Wind turbines, Elec-
trolyzer, and Hydrogen Storage System (HSS). Centralized and decentralized configurations for
installing Hydrogen Production System (HPS) and offshore and onshore HSS has been devel-
oped to compare which configuration will provide highest NPV/CAPEX ratio.
This thesis demonstrates that hydrogen price, together with hydrogen plant output require-
ment and NPV/CAPEX, are three main factors to be considered at the same time for selecting
the most proper topology. As the result, under the light of these key factors, optimal plant
configuration can be chosen based on project specified objective.
In general, with hydrogen price of 4 e/kg topologies with grid connection provide higher
NPV/CAEX ratio, however, they have poor investment in electrolyser plant and subsequently
struggle to generate significant hydrogen output.

Introduction
The combustion of fossil fuels for energy production has had severe damage on the global cli-
mate, leading to significant environmental challenges affecting people worldwide. The extensive
utilization of coal, oil, and natural gas has been a primary driver of the current trends in global
warming and climate change. Simultaneously, the escalating demand for energy is depleting
finite fossil fuel reserves, rendering them an unsustainable energy solution in the long term.
In addressing this complex issue, it is crucial to consider the replacement of carbon-based power
systems with renewable energy technologies. Such a transition not only holds the potential to
harmonize the balance between energy supply and demand but also ensures energy security.
Furthermore, renewable energy technologies have a markedly lower environmental impact, par-
ticularly in terms of mitigating climate change. Importantly, these alternatives can also prove
to be economically viable solutions, assuring a sustainable future for the energy sector. [4].
Nevertheless, relying solely on green electricity is highly unlikely to meet the requirements of
the complete energy system. The production of green hydrogen has emerged as a pivotal so-
lution in addressing environmental issues and supporting energy sustainability. In addition,
the global gas and fuel crises have prompted governments to shift their attention towards sus-
tainable alternatives, particularly in the context of heavy transportation [5]. Notably, the steel
industry’s utilization of black hydrogen in the manufacturing of wind turbines exemplifies its
commitment to more sustainable practices. Moreover, green hydrogen offers practical advan-
tages, including simplified storage solutions and a reduced material footprint, distinguishing it
from electrochemical storage systems. Taken together, these aspects highlight its crucial role
in advancing a more sustainable and robust energy environment.
Renewable hydrogen energy can be produced from a range of resources, including wind, solar,
geothermal, and notably, hybrid sources. Hybrid power plants (HPP) have been increasingly
favored due to their ability to maximize the use of grid connection capacity, minimize fluctu-
ations in overall power generation, given the variability of weather-dependent energy sources,
and optimize land and electrical infrastructure utilization.
Integrating a hydrogen production plant with a wind power facility introduces an additional
source of revenue for the project. This hybridization also offers flexibility in meeting grid con-
nection prerequisites, including frequency and voltage regulation. Furthermore, since optimal
wind resources are frequently situated far from regions with high electricity demand, necessitat-
ing long-distance power transmission that incurs substantial energy losses, it becomes necessary
to explore the localized utilization of wind energy through electrolytic hydrogen production [6].
The danish government has declared ambitious goals to increase hydrogen production capabil-
ities for utilizing excess electricity production and entering the sector of green fuel production
2
==PAGE:10==
[9]. Also the European commission have stated an intention to build 17.5 GW of electrolyser
capacity by 2050.
Although not very old, the traditional approach to hydrogen production from offshore wind
energy involves transmitting electricity from offshore wind power plants to the mainland for
onshore hydrogen production. However, the potential cost disparities between high-voltage
direct current (HVDC) cables and gas pipelines, coupled with the probable existence of pre-
existing gas pipelines and storage systems from previous oil and gas ventures, open up various
design possibilities for the power plant’s topology. Consequently, there is a need for research
endeavors aimed at identifying the optimal topology, while taking into account the available
resources and project constraints.
In this project, a comprehensive study to assess the feasibility of three distinct topologies,
encompassing both centralized and decentralized configurations for electrolyser plants situated
onshore and offshore has been conducted. This study is coupled with an analysis of the optimal
sizing of different components within the power plant.
The conceptual design of these power plants, complete with optimal component sizing, plays
a crucial role in addressing the fundamental question of "How Much?". This inquiry gains
paramount significance in the initial phases of project development, particularly in the context
of renewable power plants, and becomes even more critical when hybridizing different power
production and conversion components.
The inherent dependence of electricity production
on weather conditions requires sizing the power plant while accounting for hourly variations
throughout its operational lifespan. Therefore, an optimization framework is required to pro-
vide the most sensible answer, that factors in all technical and financial considerations. In
this research, HyDesign has been used as the primary tool for for the optimal sizing of Hybrid
Power Plants (HPP).

Research question
There are two questions to be answered in this research:
• What are the potential different topology designs for hydrogen production from offshore
WPP?
• What are the boundary conditions in choosing a topology?

Assumptions and Limitations
The following assumptions has been considered for this study:
1.2.1
Wind power plant location
A thorough examination of seven distinct locations surrounding Jutland, Denmark, was un-
dertaken to assess their potential for offshore wind power generation. Among these locations,
the site exhibiting the most favorable wind profile was selected as the location of WPP of this
study. The geographical details of these seven locations are illustrated in Figure 1, with loca-
tion 6 emerging as the prime choice due to its superior wind characteristics, thus constituting
the primary site for this project.

1.2.2
Weather and price data
Using the location information on Latitude and Longitude, HyDesign extracts weather data
from DTU’s ERA5 database. This data is being corrected by mean wind speed from Global
Wind Atlas data to provide more precise data time series.
The balancing tool chain (BTC) is used to medel the spot price in 2030 electricity market,
which combines the Balmorel open source energy system model to emulate the operation of
electricity markets from day-ahead to real-time dynamics [7].

1.2.3
Hydrogen price
Hydrogen price produced from renewable sources can mainly vary from nearly 4 e/kg up to
8 e/kg. Price estimation for 2030 is 3.9 e to 5 e per kilogram [8]. For the baseline design the
price per kilogram of hydrogen has been fixed to 4 e/kg. However, the effect of hydrogen price
on design of plant has been studied in sensitivity analysis.
1.2.4
Electrolyser technology
Assuming that all three electrolyser technologies (Alkaline Electrolysis (AEL), Proton Ex-
change Membrane (PEM), and Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cell (SOEC)) to be matured by 2030,
with almost same CAPEX as AEL, PEM provides higher efficiency and high ramp-up time
(100%/second) which makes it the most proper to be coupled with Variable Renewable En-
ergies (VREs). Additionally, due to less heating requirement than SOEC and therefore less
equipment and maintenance, PEM electrolyser is the most promising option for offshore in-
stallation [15]. PEM electrolysers are a relatively new technology; main advantages include
faster cold start time and higher flexibility [3]. As a result, PEM electrolyser has been consid-
ered in this research. A peicewise model has been used to model the efficiency curve of PEM
electrolyser. This model is provided with more detail in 7.3.
1.2.5
Hourly hydrogen selling capacity
The capacity to sell the hydrogen per hour is considered to be 10,000 kg/h. This has been
decided based on designing the power plant for many different capacities of selling Hydrogen to
grid. With smaller capacities the wind power plant should be very small which considering an
offshore WPP is not of interest. On the other hand, larger capacity will require infrastructure
to make it possible to sell the amount. As there will be limits in real projects, therefore, this
number has been chosen to avoid getting too far from reality.
10 Ton/h of hydrogen production can provide the hydrogen consumption of 25 ammonia pro-
duction plants of 50 ton/day [17]. Cost estimation for this capacity was out of scope, therefore,
no cost has been added for this capacity. There is no penalty for not fulfilling this capacity,
however it is considered as a criteria for comparing topologies.
1.2.6
Distance
It has been assumed that the centralized offshore hydrogen production and storage system is
located in a distance of 20 km from shore [19] and 1 km of cable length or pipe is being added
per WT installation. The actual layout of WPP and electricity transmission to shore effects
this parameter. 20 km is also considered for offshore substation distance to shore, from where
the electricity will be transported by one HVDC cable to shore. This parameter can effect
5
==PAGE:13==
the cable/pipe cost in different topologies, however is not precisely measured by wind turbine
locations and overall layout of the plant. Therefore, the effect of changing it is studied in
sensitivity analysis.
1.2.7
Gas Transportation
Hydrogen transportation to consumption point is out of this project’s scope.
1.2.8
Electricity to gas strategy
Hydrogen production system is not acting as an electricity storage and hydrogen is not converted
back to electricity.
1.2.9
Cost differences
In decentralised offshore electrolyser plant, balance of plant costs has been removed presenting
the removed substation and DC/AC converter costs. However, for this solution there is require-
ment for equipment like water desalination at turbine level which has not been considered.
In addition both offshore platform cost and land use cost for offshore/onshore electrolyser
plant has not been considered. Finally, although less maintenance is required in turbine level
electrolyser installation due to elimination of power electronic devices, offshore electrolyser
maintenance might be more costly, which was also hard to estimate and out of scope of this
research.
In general the assumption is that these cost differences has negligible impact on final plant
design and specially on topology selection.
1.3
Contributions
Various approaches to hydrogen production from offshore wind power plants (WPP) have been
previously examined, primarily from a techno-economic perspective. These investigations typ-
ically assumed fixed component sizes, such as a 12 GW WPP and a 12 GW electrolyzer, while
exploring the impact of different topologies and operational modes on the Levelized Cost of
Hydrogen (LCOH) [11].
This research contributes in several ways:
• Identification of Key Design Variables: It identifies the most critical design variables
that influence the topology selection.
• Methodology for Topology Selection: It offers a methodology for discerning between
different topologies, aiding in the decision-making process.
6
==PAGE:14==
• Optimal plant design: It investigates how optimal component sizes vary across different
topologies, all with the overarching aim of maximizing the Net Present Value (NPV) to
Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) ratio.
The findings from the three above-mentioned points shed light on the ideal configuration and
scale for hybrid power plants.
In this study, the modeling and optimal sizing of the hybrid power plant were conducted using
HyDesign, a tool that incorporates both technical and financial considerations. HyDesign is
capable of determining optimal sizes for Hybrid Power Plants (HPP) comprising wind and
solar power facilities, along with a battery energy storage system. In this particular project,
modifications were made to HyDesign to integrate an electrolyzer model and account for various
revenue streams, moving beyond the conventional reliance on electricity sales to the grid as the
primary revenue source.

Literature Review
Different aspects of Power to hydrogen has been studied in the literature. Nan Nguyen et al.
[10] focused on dedicated hydrogen production from offshore wind farm. In this research a via-
bility assessment has been done for co-located offshore HPS and HSS, considering underground
offshore HSS and transporting hydrogen to shore periodically by marine tankers.
Alessandro et al. in [11] provides the techno-economic overview of alternative integration de-
signs for green hydrogen production (onshore, offshore or turbine level electrolysis) into offshore
wind power hub in north see. It considers two operation modes for electrolyser: whether it is
the main consumer or it uses the excess electricity generated. The first one ensures higher
utilization of electrolyser, therefore lower LCoH.
With an offshore WPP 60km away from shore in water depth of 35 meters, Hugo Groenemans
et al. has compared traditional hydrogen production configuration, which has wind turbines
offshore and transmits electricity to water electrolysers onshore to produce hydrogen, with co-
located offshore wind-water electrolyser, in which electrolysis system is directly coupled with
offshore wind turbine [12]. This study estimates that the cost of hydrogen from offshore wind-
electrolyser will be $2.09/kg versus $3.86/kg from traditional electrolysis using wind power.
In [3] a cost competitive hydrogen production from offshore wind farm has been studies. Here,
the configuration includes offshore WPP, onshore electrolyser, steel tank HSS and fuel cell.
Hydrogen market, spot market and ancillary services has been included. Due to the presence of
fuel cell hydrogen can also play the role of an energy storage specially from excess production
and when negative spot market price.
In [6] a method for assessment of wind–hydrogen (H2) energy systems is presented. The method
includes chronological simulations and economic calculations, enabling optimised component
sizing and calculation of H2 cost. System components include a wind turbine, electrolyser, com-
pressor, storage tank and power converter. A case study on a Norwegian island is presented.
The commuting ferry is modelled as a H2 ferry, representing the H2 demand. The evaluation
includes a grid-connected system and an isolated system with a backup power generator to
supply the minimum necessary Alkaline electrolyser power at long periods of zero wind power
generation. Simulation results show that much larger components are needed for the isolated
system. H2 cost amounted to 2.8 Euro/kg and 6.2 Euro/kg for the grid-connected and isolated
system, respectively.
In summery, power-to-hydrogen systems have been the subject of several researches that have
examined their feasibility, conducted techno-economic assessments, and explored various oper-
ational modes such as using hydrogen production to store surplus electricity or dedicating a
power plant specifically for hydrogen generation.

Topologies
A range of configurations can be developed for hybrid wind-hydrogen plant. These configura-
tions can involve various components, such as the electrical connection from wind turbines to
the grid, or the incorporation of Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) into offshore wind
power plants and electrolyser facilities. Additionally, the placement of electrolysis units and
hydrogen storage systems can vary, with options ranging from onshore to offshore locations,
including both centralized and turbine-level installations.
In the early stages of this research, various topologies were considered that incorporated BESS
at both turbine and plant levels, with the option of onshore or offshore installations. However,
several factors led to reconsider the inclusion of BESS in the optimal design for hydrogen pro-
duction.
Firstly, the high cost associated with BESS, coupled with the need for additional power elec-
tronic equipment, especially in turbine-level applications, made it less economically viable as
part of the optimal design.
Secondly, in offshore installations, the limited availability for maintenance further reduced the
feasibility of incorporating BESS.
Lastly, the fast response of PEM electrolyzers to changes in power input minimized the need for
BESS to regulate power fluctuations, thereby diminishing its utility in our hydrogen production
setup.
Another design consideration was the number of centralised offshore stations for the electrolyser
and gas storage system. Depending on the availability of pre-existing pipelines and reservoirs
from prior projects, it was feasible to have more than one centralized station. However, to
optimize construction costs and minimize expenses associated with piping and storage in case

Topology 1- Turbine Level Integration-Offshore
The first topology under discussion involves a decentralized offshore hydrogen production plant
where the electrolyzers are situated at the level of individual wind turbines. This setup offers
the advantage of direct coupling to the wind turbines, which reduces the need for additional
power electronic devices. As a result, this configuration leads to energy efficiency gains and cost
savings.[18]. There is no direct electrical connection to the grid. Instead, energy transportation
to the shore is facilitated through pipelines, conveying the produced hydrogen.
Additionally, there might be existing oil and gas infrastructure, sub-sea excavations, and reser-
voirs within the project layout. These resources can potentially serve as compressed air storage
facilities and/or refueling stations for ships. Leveraging these pre-existing assets could signif-
icantly reduce or eliminate the costs associated with pipeline construction and storage infras-
tructure.
The production of hydrogen from offshore wind energy necessitates the availability of large-
scale, cost-effective storage solutions. Underground storage offers the advantage of providing
extensive capacity for long-term or seasonal hydrogen storage. Moreover, it offers enhanced
safety levels when compared to above-ground storage methods, making it an appealing choice
for storing hydrogen produced offshore [10]. As a result, underground storage has been consid-
ered for offshore storage application.

Methodology
To address the research question and determine the most suitable power plant configuration,
HyDesign is utilized as the primary tool. HyDesign played a pivotal role in optimizing the
power plant’s size and structure, while also generating financial projections for the project.
HyDesign is an optimization tool specifically designed for achieving the optimal design and
operation of hybrid power plants.
It is integrated into the OpenMDAO environment and
is dedicated to the goal of maximizing the overall value generated by hybrid power plants.
In contrast to conventional designs that prioritize the minimization of the Levelized Cost of
Electricity (LCoE), HyDesign focuses on enhancing financial performance by maximizing key
metrics such as Net Present Value (NPV), NPV/CAPEX ratio, or Internal Rate of Return (IRR).
The core design variables considered within the optimization problem involve the rated capac-
ities of critical components within the hybrid power plant.
By concentrating on financial metrics and permitting flexibility in sizing, HyDesign enables
the discovery of a well-balanced and financially advantageous solution for hybrid power plant
configurations, customized to meet distinct financial requirements and limitations.
ERA5 is used to generate the wind speed ERA5 data is employed to create wind speed time
series data that spans the entire operational life of the power plant, typically assumed to be
25 years. To streamline the optimization process and ensure computational efficiency, the op-
timization loop operates by considering a specific number of weeks for each season within each
year.
The wind power plant module provides the wind power time series using a surrogate of the
wakes of sub-optimal wind turbine layouts. The energy management system then determines
the optimal operation of plant, in order to maximize revenues.
The WISDEM wind cost model developed by NREL is used to estimate the wind power plant
costs. The optimization algorithm employed in the sizing problem is surrogate based. It uses the
implementation from the Surrogate Modeling Toolbox (SMT). Using surrogate based optimiza-
tion instead of other optimization methods, such as gradient descent, enables the minimization
of the number of full-model evaluations [14]. A brief overview of component sizing of HyDesign
is presented as a diagram in figure 5.

5.3
Design variable boundaries
The boundaries for HPP component sizes including wind turbine, turbine and plant level elec-
trolyser, and gas storage system are as follows:
- Wind turbine: When considering an offshore wind power plant, the size of the wind
turbine can range up to 15 MW with current technology. As a result, the optimizer is
granted the flexibility to utilize turbine sizes ranging from zero to 15 MW.
- Number of wind turbines: A maximum of 400 wind turbines is allowed to be selected.
- Turbine level electrolyser: The same boundary as that of the wind turbine has been taken
into account for the decentralized electrolyser. This choice is based on the fact that the
input power of the electrolyser will not surpass the turbine’s rating.
- Plant level electrolyser: The size limit for the centralized hydrogen production plant, at
the plant level, is set at 1000 MW. This limit has been determined as the most reasonable
choice due to the presence of a grid connection and the competitive dynamics between
hydrogen and electricity prices.
- Hydrogen storage system: the upper limit for hydrogen storage capacity has been selected
at 50 Ton, which is five times the selling capacity. This decision has been made to ensure
an ample buffer of stored hydrogen is available to cover the production fluctuations and
accommodate the demand as much as possible.
The boundaries for HPP component sizes have been thoughtfully established to accommodate
the current technological capabilities and operational considerations. These boundaries, ranging
from wind turbine size to hydrogen storage capacity, provide the necessary framework for the
optimization and analysis of the hybrid power plant.

5.4.3
Additional discussion
Another question to be raised here might be:
What would be the difference between topology 1 and an onshore centralised electrolysis
with no grid connection?
Although the EMS model is different in this two concepts because of respectively decentralised
and centralised hydrogen production plant, major difference would be the cost, as one is with
no cable and the other with no pipeline. Modelling and running new configuration is out of
scope of this project, however, a rule of thumb can be used here:
If pipe lines are available for free, or in case of equal price for cables and pipe lines, topology
1 will end up cheaper, because it also has at least 20% less cost in balance of plant, for the
substation being replaced with pipeline connection[12].
It should be also mentioned that the cost of eliminated power electronic system in decentralised
hydrogen production concept is not available in detail in the cost breakdown. Although, there
can be a cost deduction in first topology for not using these equipment, water desalinization
and purification for each turbine level electrolyser, harsh offshore operation situation, availabil-
ity of maintenance days, gas pressure fall in pipe lines during transportation, etc. are other
challenges to be considered in offshore installation of electrolysers and may result additional
cost.
5.5
Key Findings and Implications
The research was centered on determining the optimal configuration and size of a hybridized
offshore wind power plant coupled with electrolyser hydrogen production. Based on the results
obtained from the analysis of various topologies in this chapter, it can be deduced that the
primary consideration of either producing hydrogen from WPP electricity or directly selling it
to the grid significantly influences the design.
In cases where the power plant is grid-connected, taking into account the costs and profits
associated with the hydrogen production plant at a hydrogen price of less than 5 euros per
kilogram, it is observed that the highest NPV/CAPEX ratio is achieved with almost no invest-
ment in the electrolyser plant. This outcome stems from the fact that, with higher electricity
price than hydrogen price, , the utilization factor of the hydrogen plant becomes excessively
low, that the revenue gained from selling hydrogen cannot cover its costs.
As a result, if the project goal is a dedicated power plant to hydrogen production, the electricity
37
==PAGE:45==
generated by WPP should first cover the nominal electrolyser capacity.
On the other hand, it’s noteworthy that all three distinct topologies exhibit feasibility with no-
tably high NPV. While topologies featuring grid connections yield higher NPV/CAPEX by 52%
in topology 2 and 94% in topology 3, topology 1 offers approximately 60% higher NPV despite
this difference.
Comparing the topologies with Offshore and onshore centralised electrolyser installation with
grid connection (second and third topologies), the difference is mainly the cost of pipeline. In
case of using available pipelines form older projects, there might be slight difference in CAPEX
and OPEX for offshore/onshore installation and maintenance.

Conclusion and future perspectives
6.1
Conclusion on topology selection
In chapter 5 the optimal design for different configurations of a hybrid power plant including
offshore wind power plant and electrolytic hydrogen plant was analysed. Within this chapter,
the goal is to provide clarity regarding the most favorable option for the development of the
hybrid power plant.
Considering the key design variables outlined in section 4.3, figure 12 serves as a visual map
facilitating the identification of the most suitable option based on project objectives. In this
figure, each distinct color represents a specific topology, while the size of the bubbles reflects
the maximum hourly hydrogen requirement met by each topology at different hydrogen price
points.
Consequently, this figure enables a simultaneous comparison of both the NPV/CAPEX and the
fulfillment of required hydrogen across various topologies for each hydrogen price level.
Figure 12: Topology selection map
When the hydrogen price is fixed at 4 e/kg, it becomes evident that both the second and third
topologies, while displaying higher NPV/CAPEX, ratios, significantly lag in terms of meeting the
required hydrogen production levels compared to the results achieved by the first topology.
Topology 1 also yields interesting results when hydrogen price is set to 5 e/kg. While it deliv-
39
==PAGE:47==
ers a superior NPV/CAPEX ratio compared to the second topology and comes remarkably close
to the third topology, it still notably outperforms both in terms of fulfilling hydrogen output
requirements.
For higher hydrogen prices, all three topologies progressively approach the goal of consistently
meeting the hydrogen production requirements. However, Topology 1 distinguishes itself as the
preferred choice due to its superior NPV/CAPEX ratio. The main reason for this preference is
primarily the elimination of balance of system costs and grid connection expenses. It’s impor-
tant to note that the topologies under comparison do not have identical designs in terms of
component sizes.
Therefore, when the objective is hydrogen production, Topology 1 demonstrates a more promis-
ing performance. It boasts the highest NPV among the topologies and a comparable NPV/CAPEX
ratio when taking hydrogen production outcomes into account.
In conclusion, to identify the most suitable and financially advantageous topology for a hybrid
power plant, the following steps are crucial:
- Define various potential topologies for the project.
- Optimize the sizing of each topology’s plant components, with a preference for maximizing
NPV/CAPEX, providing a robust basis for comparison.
- Evaluate the key operational and financial outcomes of these topologies.
- Select the best option in alignment with the project’s goals.
These steps collectively form a structured approach to ensure the optimal design and selection
of the hybrid power plant topology.
6.2
Future studies
In the realm of future research and exploration, several key areas and aspects beckon for deeper
investigation and analysis. The following bullet points highlight the avenues that merit atten-
tion and inquiry:
• Cost Analysis for Hydrogen Selling Capacity: In this research, the sizing of the
Hybrid Power Plant (HPP) is based on the assumption that the hydrogen selling capacity
is a constant 10 tons per hour. Importantly, there are no associated costs for the relevant
infrastructure in this analysis. It’s crucial to recognize that this parameter significantly
influences the sizing of the HPP for hydrogen production. In real-world projects, achieving
and maintaining such a selling capacity would require infrastructure investments and may
40
==PAGE:48==
entail associated costs. Therefore, there is a need for further research to determine the
cost of hydrogen selling capacity per kilogram or ton of hydrogen. This extended research
should also encompass the evaluation of different pipe capacities (whether for purchase or
available from previous projects) and the considerations related to onshore infrastructure
development.
• Introducing Penalties for Non-Compliance: Introducing a penalty for failing to
meet the hydrogen production requirement, combined with a well-defined estimation of
output capacity, can significantly alter the dynamics of plant sizing and, perhaps even
more crucially, influence the choice of topology. As previously highlighted, especially in
the context of grid-connected topologies, it’s imperative to consider the competitive pric-
ing dynamics between electricity and hydrogen. In this scenario, having a mechanism in
place, such as an incentive or penalty structure, becomes essential to create the economic
motivation necessary for investment in an electrolyser plant.
• Location optimization: If location optimization is of interest, a study could explore
the impact of cost reduction through the utilization of existing submarine pipelines and
the identification of the best wind profiles for design optimization.
• Variable hydrogen price As the hydrogen industry increasingly integrates renewable
energy sources, it’s likely that variable hydrogen prices will become more common in
the future.
To address this evolving landscape, providing hydrogen price time-series
data, similar to electricity spot market time-series data, can be highly beneficial in sizing
the HPP. By incorporating this dynamic pricing information into sizing optimization
calculations, we can adaptively optimize HPP configurations to align with fluctuating
hydrogen prices.
In conclusion, these future research directions aim to enhance our understanding of the optimal
plant configuration for hydrogen production and its related component design
