Abstract 
Biomass is worldwide known to be a sustainable source of energy. In the last decades anaerobic 
digestion has been affirmed as process to produce energy from biomass, in the form of biogas. 
Between biomass typologies, carbohydrate-rich terrestrial crops, such as corn, always played a key 
role in the selection of suitable feedstock for anaerobic digestion plants. Animal manures are also 
widely applied, but one of the main drawbacks of this choice is the low volumetric methane 
production. Co-digestion with an energy dense feedstock is therefore the optimal solution to boost 
the biogas production of manure-based Anaerobic Digestion plants.  
On this topic, marine biomass has been found to be suitable to be co-digested with diluted animal 
manures. Additionally, seaweeds occur naturally, and their growth does not exploit arable land. 
Moreover, the use of this feedstock does not create competition with food market and can be an 
optimal solution for mitigation of eutrophication effects. Furthermore, marine biomass does not 
contain lignin rich residues, which are not easily degradable in AD and can therefore decrease the 
overall energy output. 
In this research, macroalgal species Laminaria digitata was selected as carbohydrate rich substrate. 
However, co-digestion of marine macroalgae can be challenging due to the presence of salt; in 
fact, sodium is found to be present in high concentrations in marine biomass, and sodium is known 
to be an inhibitor of AD process. 
Thus, in this study, sodium inhibition was carefully analysed. The experiments explored the impact 
of increasing sodium concentrations on methane production. 
First, a batch Biochemical Methane Potential test was carried out to determine the optimum co-
digestion ratio between manure and L. digitata. The results confirmed that the methane yield with 
addition L. digitata is significantly boosted and revealed a linear increase in methane production 
with the increase of macroalgae concentration. The optimum co-digestion ratio was therefore 
found to be 80% VS from L. digitata and 20% VS from cattle manure. Neither synergy nor 
inhibition were observed coupling these feedstocks. 
Secondly, batch assays were peformed to explore the sodium concentration thresholds driving to 
inhibition of the process, and IC50 was calculated. Sea salt and sodium chloride were tested to 
discover the influence of sodium source and samples for COD and VFA analyses were taken to 
find which step of AD was the bottleneck of the process. 
The results showed a clear decrease in methane yield with increasing sodium concentration. The 
IC50 concentration was found to be 2.86 gNa/L in the case of marine salt and 9.00 gNa/L in the 
case of sodium chloride. Higher inhibition, therefore, resulted in the case of marine salt. Further 
==PAGE:6==
vi 
 
analyses showed an accumulation of acetate with increasing sodium concentration. This confirmed 
the hypothesis that the bottleneck of the process was most likely to be caused by acetoclastic 
methanogens. 
Lastly, co-digestion was run in two continuous stirred tank reactors. After an acclimatization 
period, the reactors underwent to increased sodium concentration up to IC50 of the marine salt set 
(2.86 gNa/L) and then up to two times this concentration. In the first reactor the aimed 
concentration was achieved with a pulse injection, while in the second this was done gradually. 
The results revealed a deeply different behaviour from the batch assays and demonstrated the key 
role of acclimatization. In fact, in continuous reactors, the inhibition thresholds were much higher. 
Hydrogen sulphide production was also tested in a last set of batch assays, revealing a linear 
increase of hydrogen sulphide content in the biogas with increasing salt concentration.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Anaerobic Digestion 
 
1.1.1 General overview 
Biomass is worldwide known as a resource for renewable energy production. The exploitation of 
renewable and sustainable energy sources can give the positive outcome to reduce the dependency 
upon fossil fuels and non-renewable energy sources, and to reduce the emissions of CO2. 
Moreover, this can contribute to create a local energy market, thus reducing the dependency on 
delocalized energy resources, lowering transportation costs and increasing safety of energy supply.  
The European Union set quite ambitious targets about renewable energies. A target of a 20% share 
of renewable energies in overall European Union gross final energy consumption is in fact set by 
2020, together with a minimum 10% share of biofuels in overall EU transport gasoline and diesel 
consumption. A target of 40% emissions reduction (compared to year 1990) and 27% share in 
renewable energy is also set for 2030. 
 
Figure 1: share of energy from renewable sources in the EU member states (in % of gross final energy consumption), source: 
Eurostat, 2018

Figure 1 shows the share of energy from renewable sources in the EU member states (in% of gross 
final energy consumption (Eurostat, 2018)). As it can be observed, in 2016 Europe reached 17% 
share of renewables in gross final energy consumption. Among the countries, Denmark and Italy 
already reached their goal of 30% and 17% respectively, but many other countries are far from 
their goal, so they should increase their effort to develop renewables. 
Regarding the primary production, the quantity of renewable energy produced within the EU-28 
increased by 66.6 % between 2006 and 2016, equivalent to an average increase of 5.3 % per year. 
This trend can be observed in figure 2.  
Energy production from biomass is going to play a key role in the set of renewables for the future 
and this can be deduced by the increasing exploitation of biogas, solid or liquid biofuels and 
renewable wastes as part of the renewables positive trend. Research is therefore needed to make 
processes more efficient and sustainable.  
 
Figure 2: trend of primary production of renewable energy, within the EU-28 between 1990 and 2016, source: Eurostat, 2018 
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is one of the processes which can be exploited to convert biomass into 
energy, in the form of biogas. AD occurs naturally in a variety of environments such as the 
intestinal tract of animals and in aquatic sediments, but it can be also engineered for anthropogenic 
purposes. 
==PAGE:15==
 
3 
 
The advantages that this process can give are several. In fact, compared to aerobic degradation 
processes, such as composting, it enables high degree of reduction of organic matter with a small 
increase in the bacterial biomass (Angelidaki et al.,2011 b), lower usage of land for the facilities, 
and often a gain in terms of energy, as biogas that can be utilized to generate heat and electricity. 
One of the main strengths of this process is also that it can combine the profit of energy recovery 
from organic matter and the possibility to use as feedstocks raw materials that are considered as 
residues, therefore manure, wastewater treatment sludge, putrescible organic fraction of municipal 
solid waste, residues from crops and seaweed. This is one of the reason why this process is 
considered not only renewable, but also sustainable. Additionally, organic matter used for biogas 
production has originally been produced by photosynthetic fixation of carbon dioxide from the 
atmosphere and thus biogas combustion does not add extra carbon dioxide to the atmosphere. This 
is one of the main advantages when compared to fossil fuel exploitation. 
AD has anyway always faced several challenges; first of all, about the competitivity of the process. 
Energy from fossil fuels is in fact often much cheaper than energy from biomass. Secondly, AD 
faces the risk of competition with food market when edible substrates are used as feedstock for the 
process. Lastly, it deals with the competition for land exploitation, when agricultural crops are 
used as feedstock. 
The partial solutions to these challenges are nowadays developing and are increasingly found in 
coupling the benefit of energy production with usage of residual feedstocks and algal biomass. 
The biofuels which are produced from algal biomass are generally named as third generation 
biofuels. This is to distinguish them from first and second-generation biofuels, made from 
terrestrial crops and from residues (of terrestrial crops, forestry, farming, or municipal and 
industrial organic wastes) respectively, in an escalation of sustainability. Exploring the potentials 
of seaweed based methane is part of this positive sustainability challenge. 
AD consists in the degradation of organic matter by the synergetic action of a wide spectrum of 
microorganisms in the absence of oxygen. This biochemical process involves a complex cascade 
of reactions, which convert a great variety of organic substrate materials to carbon dioxide and 
methane, molecules in which carbon appears at the most oxidized and the most reduced states. As 
a result of organic molecules degradation, also nitrogen, hydrogen, ammonia and hydrogen 
sulphide are generated: usually less than 1% of the total gas volume, while carbon dioxide is in 
the range of 30 to 50% and methane in the range of 50 to 70%. These products are together referred 
==PAGE:16==
 
4 
 
to with the name of biogas, which is precisely all the substances in gas phase which result from 
AD. 
1.1.2 Steps of anaerobic digestion process and microbiology  
AD is a complex biological process which can follow several metabolic pathways. For practical 
and didactic issues, it has been subdivided in four main consecutive steps: hydrolysis, 
acidogenesis, acetogenesis and methanogenesis. 
Figure 3 shows a scheme of this process, together with the inputs and the outputs of each step. 
 
Figure 3: Scheme of anaerobic digestion steps (From: Angelidaki et al., 2011) 
 
Hydrolysis 
In this first step occur depolymerisation and solubilisation of macromolecules such as 
carbohydrates, proteins and lipids into monosaccharides, amino acids, Long Chain Fatty Acids 
==PAGE:17==
 
5 
 
and glycerol, respectively. This is performed by hydrolytic and fermentative bacteria. These 
bacteria utilise extracellular enzymes to depolymerize complex organic matter and in particular: 
proteolytic bacteria catalyse the degradation of protein using proteases enzyme; cellulolytic and 
xylanolytic bacteria produce cellulases and xylanases to hydrolyse carbohydrates; while lipolytic 
bacteria use lipases to degrade lipids (Angelidaki et al., 2011). 
 
Acidogenesis 
In this second step, hydrolysis products are absorbed by the fermentative or obligatory oxidising 
bacteria. These bacteria metabolize hydrolysis products into acetate and other Volatile Fatty Acids 
(VFA), alcohols and hydrogen, which are smaller compounds compared to the input substrates of 
this step. The term acidogenesis is thus commonly referred to formation of these products that is 
usually driving the pH to acid. Fermentation refers instead to anaerobic conversion of organic 
materials in the absence of inorganic electron acceptors such as sulphate, nitrate, or oxygen 
(Angelidaki et al., 2011). 
In a good performance process, the main part of the input substrates will be transformed directly 
to methanogenic substrates (hydrogen, carbon dioxide and acetate), but a significant part (around 
30%) is nevertheless transformed into other fatty acids and alcohols. This part will be larger if the 
process is out of balance (Angelidaki et al.,2011 b), so for example if acetate is not consumed fast 
enough. 
 
Acetogenesis 
Acetogenesis is defined as the synthesis of acetate. This can occur by two mechanisms: the 
oxidation of fermentation products such as propionate, butyrate and ethanol to produce acetate and 
hydrogen, performed by obligatory hydrogen-producing acetogens or the utilisation of substrates 
such as hydrogen, formate and methanol to form acetate, performed by hydrogen-consuming 
acetogens (known as homoacetogens). The term obligatory in the first mechanism refers to the fact 
that acetogens, which oxidize organic acids, obligately use hydrogen ions and carbon dioxide as 
electron acceptor. Since an accumulation of hydrogen can inhibit these acetogenic bacteria, this 
results in a dependence of acetogenic bacteria on methanogenic archaea or other hydrogen 
scavengers for product removal. Hydrogen-producing and hydrogen-consuming need therefore to 
establish a syntrophic relationship (also known as interspecies hydrogen transfer (IHT)) to keep 
the hydrogen concentration below a certain threshold. 
 
==PAGE:18==
 
6 
 
Methanogenesis 
Methanogenesis is defined as methane generation and is the last step of AD process. All the known 
methane-producing microorganisms belong to the Archaea domain an can be classified as: 
acetoclastic, hydrogenotrophic and methylotrophic methanogens (Guo et al., 2015). Methanogens 
are strictly anaerobic microorganisms that are found in environments where external electron 
acceptors such as oxygen ammonia or sulphate are limited. Acetoclastic methanogens are so called 
because they produce methane from acetate; whilst hydrogenotrophic methanogens can produce 
methane trough reduction of hydrogen, carbon dioxide and formate; and in the end, methylotrophic 
methanogens use methyl compounds or methylated compounds for the same purpose. The 
decomposition of acetate to methane is often rate-limiting for the entire AD process, if the substrate 
is mainly consisting of easily degradable material (Angelidaki et al., 2011). 
 
1.1.3 Process parameters and monitoring  
Today, AD process is becoming more and more efficient. Several studies have been conducted on 
the aspects and parameters that control this process and the possible inhibition factors. 
The main physical and chemical parameters controlling the process are: temperature, pH, micro 
and macro nutrients, substrate inhibition and toxic compounds. 
 
Temperature 
AD can be performed in three different ranges of temperature: 
• less than 20°C (psychrophilic digestion) 
• 30-40 °C (mesophilic digestion) 
• 50-60 °C (thermophilic digestion) 
The most common temperature ranges utilised for AD are mesophilic and thermophilic because 
they enable higher growth rate for methanogens. Comparing these two ranges of temperature, the 
thermophilic digestion presents several advantages: it requires a shorter hydraulic retention time 
and ensures good disruption of pathogens, together with a better degradation of Long Chain Fatty 
Acids, a lower ratio of biomass formation with respect to products formation and an improved 
solubility of the substrates. However, thermophilic digestion is also characterized by higher 
instability and energetic demand. Moreover, the solubility of gases (as NH3, H2, H2S) also depends 
on the temperature. This can be an issue for material which have an inhibition effect on the process. 
==PAGE:19==
 
7 
 
This is the reason why in thermophilic reactors often the temperature is kept in not too high 
temperature range (between 52 and 56°C).  
The temperature must be constantly monitored during AD process and must be maintained stable 
for the optimal growth of the microbial population inside the reactor. A sudden drop of the 
temperature could drive to death of microorganisms and process failure. 
 
pH 
pH is a key parameter for AD process. The different microorganisms require a different pH level: 
for example, acidogens optimally operate at an acidic pH (6.0-6.5), whereas methanogens at a 
neutral to slight alkaline pH (7.0-8.5) (Khanal et al., 2016). Therefore, optimal pH for biogas 
reactors is between 6.5 and 8, in mesophilic conditions, whereas pH value in thermophilic biogas 
reactors is generally higher. The pH can be also an index of process instability, for example it can 
indicate an accumulation of volatile fatty acids. This imbalance can drive to process failure. For 
these reasons, pH must be constantly monitored during the AD process. 
 
Macro and micro nutrients 
Macro-nutrients as carbon and nitrogen are essential components of the organic biomass. Sulphur 
is also a necessary element, for the synthesis of amino acids, together with phosphorus, which is 
found in nucleic acids, phospholipids, ATP, NAD and FAD. Potassium, calcium, magnesium and 
iron are also required to achieve an efficient degradation during the AD process, as they work as 
cofactors for enzyme activity and components in metal complexes. Trace metals as cobalt, 
manganese and nickel are important for growth of anaerobic organisms. Macro nutrients 
concentration should be approximately 10-4 M, while the micronutrient concentration should be 
lower than 10-4 M to avoid process inhibition (Angelidaki et al.,2011 b). 
 
Inhibitory compounds 
Different compounds such as ammonia, VFA and trace elements can result toxic for the AD 
process.  
Ammonia, which is an intermediate of protein degradation, is commonly found in manure based 
anaerobic digesters together with AD processes treating slaughterhouse industrial effluents or 
dairy processing industry effluents. The anaerobic microorganisms that are more sensitive towards 
free ammonia inhibition are methanogens: when the methanogenic community is inhibited by 
ammonia, an increase in VFA concentration will occur due to insufficient degradation, causing a 
==PAGE:20==
 
8 
 
drop in pH. Ammonia inhibition can occur if the level of total ammonia is in the range of 1.2 to 4 
g/L (Angelidaki, Boe and Ellegaard, 2005).  
VFA are produced during acidogenesis and acetogenesis steps of AD process. Their accumulation 
reflects a kinetic unbalance between methanogens and acetogens, or between acetogens and 
acidogens, and therefore they are generally used as indicators. Accumulation of VFA may lead to 
a pH drop and subsequently can drive to toxic conditions for AD process (Ahring, 1995). A 
specific VFA toxic threshold has not been defined, as different substrates lead to different VFA 
levels and thus different conditions that may cause instability (Angelidaki, Ellegaard and Ahring, 
1993). However, some studies proposed propionate as instability indicator and found out that 
concentrations of propionate greater than 1 g/L may cause an inhibitory effect (Ahring, 1995). 
Furthermore, a propionate to acetate ratio was proposed for the same purpose, so a propionate to 
acetate ratio greater than 1.4 can indicate the failure of AD process (Hill, et al., 1987). Anyhow, 
in general, critical conditions in AD process can be identified by pH drop and sudden increase of 
total VFA concentration.  VFA concentration must therefore be frequently monitored. 
Trace elements such as aluminium, calcium, magnesium, potassium and sodium are required in 
moderate concentrations for microbial growth and so trace elements addition in anaerobic digesters 
can result in a stimulation of the process. However, in high concentration they can result inhibiting 
or even toxic for AD. For example, calcium is thought to be moderately inhibitory in 
concentrations between 2.5 and 4 g/L, whereas it is considered strongly inhibitory above 8 g/L 
concentration (Kugelman and Mccarty, 1965).  
Another important fact is that synergistic and antagonistic effects occur between the cations.  
These aspects will be better explained in chapter 1.2, with a focus on sodium. 
 
Substrate inhibition 
Lipids and protein concentration in the feed stream to biogas plants must be carefully monitored. 
A sudden addition of lipids to a digester can cause failure of the anaerobic process, since 
accumulation of long chain fatty acids produced by hydrolysis of lipids can inhibit microbial 
population. The toxicity of lipids is therefore much higher when the hydrolysis rate is greater 
compared to fermentation rate.  
The degradation of proteins instead results in formation of ammonia, which as discussed before, 
can be inhibitory for the AD process.

1.2 Literature review on sodium inhibition 
 
Sodium is an essential element for the metabolism of anaerobic microorganisms. This light metal 
ion plays an important role in the formation of adenosine triphosphate and in the oxidation of 
NADH (Dimroth and Thomer, 1989), therefore in small concentration it can enhance AD process.  
However, high salinity frequently causes inhibition and even failure in anaerobic digestion (Wang, 
Hou and Su, 2017). Under anaerobic conditions, high concentrations of sodium are proven to be 
detrimental for the activity of microbes and to negatively interfere with their metabolism. High 
concentrations of sodium are in fact causing cell plasmolysis and death due to the high external 
osmotic pressure, and the inhibition of reaction pathways in anaerobic digestion (Mottet, Habouzit 
and Steyer, 2014).  
Research on how high concentration of sodium affects AD process had begun to receive increasing 
attention since the’70s, as toxic levels of sodium were found in systems treating wastewater from 
food processing industries (Soto, Méndez and Lema, 1993). Since sodium is also a major 
component of sodium hydroxide, which is widely used for pH adjustment in AD systems, 
inhibitory sodium levels were also found in AD systems treating non-saline feedstocks 
(Hierholtzer and Akunna, 2012).  
During these years, many authors proposed different inhibition thresholds. These differences in 
inhibition levels are related to multiple factors, such as operational conditions, test methodology, 
differences in the sodium sensitivity between microorganisms, antagonistic and synergistic effects 
of other ions. 
For example, temperature of process has an influence on sodium inhibition. An early study about 
AD of saline wastewater from fish canning industry is reporting that mesophilic reactors achieve 
higher stability against inhibitory products (Soto, Méndez and Lema, 1991). Moreover, Soto, 
Méndez and Lema, (1991) reported that operation in thermophilic range of temperature generates 
5-15% higher degree of biodegradation, but lower VFA uptake in methanogenesis. This issue of 
possible higher VFA accumulation in thermophilic range of temperature should be considered in 
the design of AD reactors treating high saline feedstocks. 
Furthermore, different species of microorganisms have different resistance thresholds and 
adaptation capacity to high saline environments. 
==PAGE:22==
 
10 
 
Figure 4 presents the approximate upper salt concentration limits for the occurrence of some 
selected microbial processes (Oren, 1999). Although these values are based on laboratory studies 
of pure cultures (black bars) and on activity measurements of natural communities in hypersaline 
environments (white bars) it can be clearly seen how particularly acetoclastic methanogenesis is 
one of the weakest processes against salt concentration increase. 
Between the steps of AD process, regarding sodium inhibition, acetoclastic methanogenesis is also 
the most highly affected process and this is reported in literature by many authors (Rinzema, van 
Lier and Lettinga, 1988; Feijoo et al., 1995; Onodera et al., 2013; Mottet, Habouzit and Steyer, 
2014; Sun et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2017; De Vrieze et al., 2018). 
 
Figure 4: approximate upper salt concentration limits for the occurrence of some selected microbial processes (Oren, 1999) 
 
The influence of inoculum acclimation is also a key parameter to determine sodium sensitivity of 
microorganisms. In fact, it is reported that acclimation of methanogens to high concentrations of 
sodium can increase their tolerance to saline environments and accelerate the methanisation rate 
(de Baere et al., 1984; Feijoo et al., 1995; Omil, Méndez and Lema, 1995; Chen, Han and Sung, 
2003). Furthermore, methanogens tolerance increases with the increase of the sodium 
==PAGE:23==
 
11 
 
concentration and the exposure time they acclimated to (Chen, Cheng and Creamer, 2008). The 
sodium IC50 is in fact reported to achieve 37.4 g Na/L after acclimation (Chen, Cheng and Creamer, 
2008).  
Lastly, the relationships within concentration of different cations present in natural occurring 
marine salt and AD are really complex. Not only they can occur itself in both toxic and stimulatory 
ranges, but also antagonism and synergism must be considered in reporting data on toxicity and 
stimulation in anaerobic treatment systems (Kugelman and Chin, 1971). 
Therefore, the use of natural salt in research works allows a better reflection of this complexity of 
interactions. 
The use of complex substrates such as natural occurring macroalgae should also be encouraged 
when the objective is to reflect another type of complexity, i.e. that of the interferences between 
AD steps and its metabolic pathways.

according to different authors and to different parameters affecting this phenomenon.

As can be noticed from this table, only a few authors investigated the influence of high salinity on 
thermophilic AD process. Moreover, many of them used chemicals as feedstocks and non-natural 
sources of salt. Lastly, most of the authors performed their research on batch assays and not in 
continuous mode operation.  
However, it is well known that the results from batch experiments are prone to underestimate the 
tolerance of the microorganisms. This may be caused by the minimum time to adapt given to 
microbial population in batch testing. Moreover, in continuously fed reactors, microbes that cannot 
adapt to the applied environmental conditions are washed-out, and this can increase the overall 
productivity of the system. Lastly, in the cited literature, the sodium inhibition in continuous 
operation was performed with gradual increment of sodium salts, and therefore this procedure 
gives adequate time for the microbial adaptation (Feijoo et al., 1995). 
The comparison of data obtained from batch tests with experiments in continuous mode is hence 
of great significance.

2. OBJECTIVES 
2.1 Aims of the study 
 
The aims of the present study are multiple and interconnected. As first, this study aimed to obtain 
the most efficient mixing ratio (in terms of organic matter) of manure and macroalgae of the 
species Laminaria digitata exhibiting the highest methane yield in co-digestion strategy. This 
objective was addressed by conducting a Biochemical Methane Potential test (BMP) in batch 
reactors. 
An additional aim was to determine the sodium inhibition thresholds and to calculate the sodium 
concentration causing 50% inhibition in methane yield (IC50) for the AD system at chosen co-
digestion ratio. This aim was addressed by installing a toxicity test in batch assays, examining 
increased sodium concentration, starting from the one that it was supposed to be optimum, to the 
one that it was hypothesised that would have created severe inhibition, according to literature data. 
Moreover, it was aimed to explore how different sodium sources influence salt inhibition. This 
objective was addressed by testing two different sodium sources (i.e. synthetic sodium chloride 
and natural marine salt) in the toxicity test, at the same sodium concentration levels, to compare 
the results.  
Furthermore, it was aimed to potentially determine which step of anaerobic digestion is the 
bottleneck of the process during sodium inhibition, and therefore to have an initial indication of 
which group of microbes (i.e. involved in one of the four steps of AD process) is more sensitive 
to sodium inhibition. This objective was addressed by sampling one bottle per each concentration 
level in the toxicity test and measuring pH, soluble COD and VFA concentration.  
Lastly, it was aimed to test the response to sodium inhibition in two reactors running in continuous 
mode and performing co-digestion of cattle manure and L.digitata. This was performed by means 
of a pulse salt injection (i.e. shock load) or by gradual increment of the salt concentration inside 
the reactor. The reactors therefore underwent to different forcing conditions, that it was 
hypothesized would have driven to an acute and persistent or a gradual inhibition.

2.2 General objectives 
 
Exploitation of seaweeds and other algal biomass is considered one of the new frontiers in the 
biofuels production. These algae-based biofuels are called third generation biofuels. Algal biomass 
has the characteristic to be an energy dense material, suitable for many biofuels production 
processes, which avoids land exploitation for growth. Research on anaerobic digestion of seaweed 
is part of this challenge to find more and more sustainable feedstocks for biofuels production. 
Moreover, usage of seaweeds as feedstock for anaerobic digestion can enhance the mitigation of 
eutrophication effects, avoiding costs for landfill disposal. Exploitation of this biomass as resource 
however can be challenging due do the presence of salt, and in particular of sodium, which is 
known to be inhibitor of AD process. 
The main objective of the current study was to investigate the potential of marine biomass as 
feedstock for anaerobic digestion to enhance the sustainability of AD process. Moreover, by 
addressing the issue of salinity inhibition in seaweed-based anaerobic rectors and by knowing the 
salinity thresholds, it would be possible to design optimum co-digestion strategies and give 
confidence to the exploitation of this resource at full scale biogas plants. Lastly, knowing the 
salinity thresholds for AD process can also avoid the waste of fresh water that may be used in 
washing pre-treatment of saline feedstocks, and therefore this can again enhance the sustainability 
of the process.

3.2.2 Macroalgal biomass: Laminaria digitata 
Laminaria digitata is a large, leathery, dark brown seaweed which grows up to two or three metres 
(Dickinson, C.I. 1963). The species belongs to the family Laminariaceae. This algae occurs in the 
north west Atlantic, from southern Greenland to Massachusetts and in the north east Atlantic from 
northern Russia and to France.  
L digitata is mostly found on shores, on exposed sites, where it may form vast meadows. It has a 
high intrinsic growth rate (5.5% per day), and a high carrying capacity (40 kg wet weight per 
square meter) compared to other algal species (Lewis, J.R.,1964).

L. digitata used for this study was collected from Hamborg Strand (north of Hanstholm-7730,

3.5.4 Metal Analysis 
Metal analysis was performed with wet digestion of 260 mg of sample, using HNO3 and HCl in

3.5.5 Total phenolic compounds analysis 
The concentration of Total Phenolic Compounds produced was determined using UV-visible 
spectrophotometer (Helios Epsilon, Thermo Spectronic®) according to the Folin-Ciocalteu 
method. A characteristic peak at 725 nm reveals the TPC content after the reaction with the Folin-
Ciocalteu reagent and Sodium Carbonate. A calibration curve for each measurement was built 
using Gallic acid as standard and subsequently, a calibration plot was constructed to quantify the 
TPC content. 
 
3.5.6 Structural carbohydrates analysis 
Total carbohydrates, were detected and quantified using high performance liquid chromatography 
(HPLC) following the guidelines of the NREL protocol (A. Sluiter, B. Hames, R. Ruiz, Slui and 
ter, D. Templeton, 2008). 
 
3.5.7 Determination of Volatile Fatty Acids (VFA) 
Samples for VFA concentration analysis were prepared following the procedure described by 
(Kougias et al., 2015). In CSTRs samples of 30 mL were collected from each reactor, using a 100 
mL syringe. From toxicity test serum bottles samples were taken using a 10 mL syringe and placed 
in 10 mL vials. After the collection, 3 mL of each sample were transferred into 20 mL plastic 
container and mixed with 3 mL distilled water and 0.4 mL of ortho-phosphoric acid (H3PO4, 34%). 
Two replicates per each reactor were prepared filling 2 mL Eppendorf tubes with 2 mL of sample 
and then centrifuged for 12 minutes at 11000 rpm. Once centrifuged, from each Eppendorf tube 1 
mL of supernatant was taken and placed into a 2 mL glass vial together with 0.1 mL of 4-
Methylvaleric acid, used as internal standard. The prepared samples were stored at -18° before the 
analysis. Finally, the VFA concentration was determined by using a gas chromatograph (Shimadzu

GC-2010, Kyoto, Japan) equipped with flame ionization detector (FID), which detects acetate, 
propionate, iso-butyrate, butyrate, iso-valerate, valerate and hexanoic acid.  
 
3.5.8 Determination of pH 
The pH was determined according to the international standard ISO 10523:1994. A pH meter was 
calibrated with two different buffer solutions (pH= 4.0 and pH= 7.0) and used to measure the 
actual pH of the samples. In CSTRs samples were taken with a 100 mL syringe, placed into a glass 
Beker and measured at room temperature (24°C) directly after the sampling. 
From toxicity test serum bottles samples were taken using a 10 mL syringe and placed in 10 mL 
vials. pH was measured at room temperature from the 10 mL vials directly after the sampling. 
 
3.5.9 Determination of methane and carbon dioxide content 
Biogas composition was investigated once per week for the CSTRs and batch essays and twice per 
week for the BMP and toxicity test by using a Gas Chromatograph (Shimadzu GC, Kyoto, Japan) 
equipped with a glass column packed with Porapak Q 80/100 mesh (Supelco, Bellefonte, PA, 
USA) and with a flame ionization detector (FID). This GC detected methane, carbon dioxide and 
nitrogen concentration. The GC was calibrated using different gas mixtures as standards: first a 
gas mixture composed by 85% N2, 5% CO2, 5% CH4, and 5% H2, then 30% N2, 30% CO2 and 40% 
CH4 and in the end 0% N2, 40% CO2 and 60% CH4. To create a calibration curve, 0.2 mL of each 
gas mixture volume were injected using a 1 mL syringe. Subsequently, a volume of 0.2 mL of 
biogas was sampled and injected into GCs to detect methane and carbon dioxide concentration. 
Before each measurement batch bottles were shaken to homogenize their content and to release 
methane eventually trapped into the liquid phase. Afterwards, using a 1 mL syringe, 0.2 mL of gas 
were extracted from the headspace of each bottle and injected in the GC.  
In the continuous reactors the 0.2 mL of gas were extracted from the gas port of each reactor using 
1 mL syringe and subsequently they were injected in the GC. Methane and carbon dioxide contents 
(% volume) in the biogas of CSTRs were calculated by normalizing each gas percentage given by 
the GC with the sum of methane and carbon dioxide percentages in the biogas.

4. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP 
4.1 Experiments in batch reactors 
 
In this work, three batch assays were carried out. All these experiments were held at thermophilic 
operational temperature (55°C).  
Samples for BMP test were prepared following the protocol proposed by (Angelidaki et al., 2009). 
According to this protocol, triplicates of blank, control and substrate samples were prepared. 
Considering the chosen working volume, the amounts of inoculum, substrates, salt, avicel 
(cellulose, chosen for the control samples) and water were calculated. The other batch assays were 
also prepared following this protocol, with the difference that the ratio of substrates was fixed 
while the mass of salt varied between the samples.  
The procedure followed to prepare the bottles was the same for all the batch assays carried: firstly, 
the proper mass of substrates, avicel and water was added; lastly, inoculum was introduced. Glass 
bottles were closed thigh with a rubber stopper, sealed with aluminium crimp, flushed with 
nitrogen to guarantee anaerobic conditions and placed into a 55°C incubator. 
Subsequently, as previously said, the methane content (CH4%) of each sample was measured using 
the Gas Chromatograph (GC).  
In the following chapters a more detailed explanation about each type of batch assay can be found.

Continuous reactors operational periods 
The reactors operation can be divided into four different periods.  
During the first period (from day 0 to day 43), the reactors were fed with manure with an OLR of 
2.3 gVS/L*d.  
During the second period (from day 43 to day 70), co-digestion with L.digitata was performed in 
both the reactors and the OLR increased to 2.9 gVS/L*d. The VS ratio chosen to perform co-
digestion was 20:80 algae to manure. The reason of this change in VS ratio is merely operational: 
the opposite ratio would have increased the organic loading rate in a way that could have shocked 
the microbial community inside the reactor, therefore compromising the operational stability of 
the reactor and the further research on sodium inhibition. Furthermore, a bigger reactor and a more 
powerful stirring would have been necessary to ensure good mixing inside the reactor. 
In the third period (from day 70 to day 88), sodium inhibition was tested injecting marine salt in 
both the reactors, keeping the co-digestion strategy. The operational conditions of the two reactors 
in this period had been different: in reactor which from now on will be denoted as reactor one, 
sodium IC50 was injected directly inside the reactor and in the feeding, to achieve this 
concentration immediately in the reactor and keep it constant, while in reactor which from now on 
will be denoted as reactor two, IC50 was injected only in the feeding to achieve IC50 gradually 
inside the reactor. The two reactors were therefore subjected to different forcing conditions: in 
reactor one IC50 was injected like a “pulse-step” forcing, while in reactor two the forcing input was 
gradual and aimed to achieve IC50 inside the reactor in one HRT. 
Lastly, the third period was added a second step (period 4): keeping the same strategy and 
operational conditions, the sodium concentration was increased to two times the IC50. This 
concentration was achieved immediately in reactor one, while in reactor two it was achieved in 
one HRT. These conditions were maintained up to the end of the experiment.

5.2 Sodium toxicity test in batch assays 
 
This sodium toxicity test was performed to find the sodium inhibition thresholds and to calculate 
the sodium concentration causing fifty percent inhibition in methane yield (IC50) for the anaerobic 
digestion system at the chosen co-digestion ratio (20 to 80 manure to L.digitata). 
The sodium concentrations tested were: 0.00; 0.20; 1.00; 3.00; 6.00; 9.00 and 12.00 gNa/L. These 
concentrations were reached using both sodium chloride and marine salt.  
The background sodium concentration given from the substrates was also calculated and it resulted 
0.045 gNa/L, thus it was concluded that background sodium concentration is negligible compared 
to the set of additional concentration tested and therefore does not affect them. 
All the samples reached stable methane production within 19 days from the beginning of the 
experiment. The samples with 6.00, 9.00 and 12.00 g Na/L in the sea salt set and with 12.00 gNa/L 
in the NaCl set, at the end of the experiment had lower methane production than the blank, meaning 
the salt concentration was too high for the tolerance of the microbial population.  
Figure 12 presents the results of maximum methane yield according to the different concentrations 
tested. The trend of methane yield along the days for all the sodium concentrations analysed can 
instead be found in APPENDIX B. 
The highest methane yield (450±23 NmLCH4/gVS) was obtained in the samples with 1 gNa/L in 
the NaCl set (+15.8% respect to the untreated sample).

lower methane yield (389±1 NmLCH4/gVS) not only compared to the samples with 0.20, 1.00 and

reported 230 mg Na/L to be optimal for mesophilic acetoclastic methanogens, while Patel and 
Roth (1977) reported 350 mg Na/L for mesophilic hydrogenotrophic methanogens.

higher respect to the previous samples, reaching the maximum (899.59 mg/L) at the 12.00 g Na/L 
concentration level. Between the fatty acids, acetate was highly predominant, reaching 91% of the 
total VFA concentration in the 12.00 gNa/L from NaCl set.

a maximum of 714.18 mg/L. The acetate represented in this case 92% of the total VFA 
concentration, while in the untreated samples only reached the 64%, with a total VFA 
concentration of 176.1 mg/L. 
What can be concluded from this result is that by increasing the sodium concentration, the VFA

propionate) remained stable.

Interaction with magnesium 
As previously mentioned, the relationship with magnesium was considered relevant for the 
explanation of lower methane yield obtained from the set testing marine salt in the toxicity test.  
Literature about synergy or antagonism between sodium and magnesium is controversial. 
Kugelman and Chin (1971) reported that magnesium can create synergy with sodium under the 
following conditions; at sodium concentration equal to 0.3 M/L synergy starts at 0.01 M/L 
magnesium concentration, when sodium is 0.4 M/L, then synergy starts at 0.05 M/L. These 
findings are in agreement with the results of the present study.

more detrimental effect in hydrolytic bacteria then on methanogens and minor and major inhibition 
at 750 and 1000 mg Mg/L respectively. In the present study though, the inhibition observed 
interested mainly the acetoclastic methanogens, with lower methane production in the marine salt 
set with respect to NaCl set, at any concentration level, and higher VFA (and in particular acetate) 
concentration in all levels except the 9.00 and 12.00 gNa/L. 
What can be concluded then, considering the previous literature, is that with high probability, in 
the case of the present study, magnesium concentration below 691 mg/L (correspondent to the 
6.00 gNa/L level) created synergy with sodium, while magnesium concentration above 1037 mg/L 
(correspondent to the 9.00 gNa/L level) resulted in an antagonistic effect to sodium in the 
inhibition of acetoclastic methanogens. 
 
5.3 Continuous reactors 
 
Two continuously fed reactors were started up to perform co-digestion of cattle manure and 
L.digitata. Subsequently they were selected to test the response to sodium inhibition in continuous 
operation. 
The experiment was divided in four periods. In period one, both reactors were fed exclusively with 
cattle manure, until they achieved steady state conditions. During period two, the influent 
==PAGE:59==

feedstock was supplemented with L.digitata in co-digestion strategy. Once the reactors achieved 
steady state conditions, sodium inhibition was tested (period three and four). The concentration 
level chosen for the test was 2.86 gNa/L (sodium IC50 of marine salt set obtained in the toxicity 
assay) in period three, and then two times this concentration in period four. Sea salt was selected 
as sodium source.  
In period 3 and 4, the reactors underwent to different forcing conditions, that it was hypothesized 
would have driven to an acute and persistent or a gradual inhibition. In fact, in reactor one, the 
selected amount of salt was injected like a “pulse” and continued forcing, to achieve the aimed 
concentration immediately inside the reactor, while in reactor two, it was achieved gradually (in 
one HRT) through the feeding.

After the first injection of salt (period three) the results of the two reactors came to be very 
different. This because of the different strategies of salt injection: reactor one received a “pulse” 
injection, while reactor two faced a gradual increase in sodium concentration. As will be seen these 
different strategies played a key role in defining the results. 
In reactor one, the daily methane rate decreased up to 109 NmL/L*d. After a first decrease though, 
the reactor stabilized to 394 ±33 NmL/L*d on average within the last week of this period, while 
in reactor two the daily methane rate was stable on the levels of period two (491±11 NmL/L*d on 
average). In the very last days of period three can be observed that the methane rate was increasing 
even in reactor one, and it almost reached the levels of period two, probably meaning an adaptation 
of the microbial community inside the reactor to the higher salt concentration. This behaviour is 
also found in literature on a study in continuous reactors using non-acclimated inoculum, where 
every pulse increase of sodium concentration was resulting in a rapid decrease in methane rate, 
with a subsequent stabilisation period prior to the next addition (Hierholtzer and Akunna, 2012). 
The same study reported the hypothesis that every additional shock load to the AD system was 
likely to decrease the possible benefits of acclimatisation on the microbial response (Hierholtzer 
and Akunna, 2012). 
The methane yield had the same trend of methane rate: it decreased to 138 ±12 NmL/gVS in reactor 
one (but in the end of the period it increased again up to 155 NmL/gVS), while in reactor two it 
stayed in the range of previous period, with 171 ±2 NmL/gVS on average. The microbial 
community in reactor one therefore faced a “shock”, and recovered, while in reactor two the sea 
salt concentration increase was so gradual that did not really affect the process performance. This 
may be because microbial community had enough time to adapt to the new conditions and 
gradually utilized the ions present in the salt as micronutrients. This explanation is found also in 
literature on a study performed on Anaerobic Sequencing Batch Reactors observing non-alteration 
of methanogenic activity after the reactors underwent to gradual increase of sodium concentration  
(Chen, Han and Sung, 2003). 
The methane volumetric percentage in the biogas decreased in both reactors, resulting on average 
in 59.9±1.7% in reactor one and 63.4±3.3% in reactor two.  
Another important observation is that the behaviour of batch and continuous reactors were 
completely different. While in the toxicity test 2.86 gNa/L resulted in 50% inhibition in methane 
==PAGE:62==

yield, the same concentration did not affect (in case of reactor two) or decreased by only 21% (in 
case of reactor one) the methane yield. This demonstrates the key role of microorganisms’ 
acclimatization. In fact, as first, in the co-digestion period they adapted to a background sodium 
concentration (0.54 gNa/L), given by the substrates manure and L. digitata, then they had time to 
adapt to additional 2.86 gNa/L. Even in the case of “pulse” salt injection, from day 82 can be 
observed a recovery, and on day 88, a methane rate close to the condition before the salt injection.  
This is in accordance with other results obtained in literature. Feijoo et al.(1995) reported that after 
a sodium concentration shock in the continuous reactors the methane production decreased, but in 
the batch assays, at the same sodium concentrations, the methanogenesis inhibition was much 
higher. Even in another study it is reported that acclimated inoculum in continuous reactors could 
make AD well-performing even at sodium concentrations lethal in the batch tests (Chen, Han and 
Sung, 2003). 
As the results above described for period three did not drive to the expected inhibition behaviour, 
another level of sodium concentration was tested. This was the double of IC50 (5.72 gNa/L). Period 
three was thus interrupted after one HRT. 
In period four, after the injection of extra salt, the daily level of methane volume produced 
underwent a steep decrease in both the reactors, especially in reactor one. The behaviour of the 
reactors was again very different because of the different forcing conditions they underwent. 
Between days 109 and 115 the daily methane rate stabilized: in reactor one to 57±6 NmL/L*d 
while in reactor two it was 298±14 NmL/L*d. These data represent an 88.7% decrease and a 39.5% 
decrease respect to the level of co-digestion period (period two) for reactor one and two 
respectively, with reactor one facing a decrease of more than two times the reactor two.  
Also methane yield had the same trend and it stabilized around 20±1 NmL/gVS while in reactor 
two it was 104±3 NmL/gVS. This extremely low methane yield in reactor one created the 
supposition that process achieved failure, but this is not what happened. In fact, from day 116 
reactor one shows a recovery and achieved methane rate and methane yield of reactor two (504 
NmL and 98 NmL/gVS respectively) in day 120. Unfortunately for the limited time frame of this 
thesis work it was not possible to investigate the further trend of daily methane rate and yield but 
with high probability the recovery continued. This result is anyway of great significance because 
it testifies the recovery of microbial population after two consecutive sodium concentration 
“shocks”. This occurred 27 days after the last sodium concentration pulse. 
Making a literature analysis on this topic, what is generally found is the assumption that gradual 
and continuous exposure of methanogens leads to higher tolerance towards salinity compared to

microbial analysis was not performed, and further monitoring of daily methane rate would have

that further investigations are needed.

In the third period the different strategies adopted for the reactors reflected on pH and VFA levels: 
reactor one was characterized by a decrease in pH (7.54±0.06 on average) and a strong increase in

average of the period for reactor one was 1.25±0.54 g/L with average acetate concentration of 0.64 
g/L and 0.51 g/L of propionate. This lower acetate share compared to period two is explained by 
some operational problems (malfunction of stirring system), which affected the reactor 
performance in this period and that drove to the increase of propionate and other VFA. 
Reactor two was instead characterized by stability in pH (7.70±0.02 on average) despite an 
increase in VFA concentration up to 1.58 g/L. The average total VFA concentration in this period 
was 0.99±0.43 g/L with average acetate share of 41%. In this period propionate share was higher 
than acetate and reached an average 49% of total VFA concentration. This VFA concentration 
increase and high propionate share is explained by some operational issues (malfunction of the 
stirring system) that affected the reactor in this period. 
Globally the trend of VFA concentration in third period showed an increase, especially in reactor 
one. This is caused by the injection of sea salt and it confirms the findings of the toxicity test, 
where total VFA concentration, but in particular acetate concentration, increased with increasing 
sodium concentration. This is also in accordance with the decrease in daily methane rate and 
methane yield consequent to the salt concentration “shock” and it confirms the inhibition of 
acetoclastic methanogenesis, which was found to be the bottleneck of the process in the batch 
assays.

In both the reactors at the end of the period (close to day 88) we can observe a decrease of VFA 
concentration. This is in accordance with the increase of methane rate and methane yield and 
indicates an acclimatization of the microbial community to the applied conditions.  
In the fourth period the two reactors exhibit a huge difference in pH and VFA trend. While in 
reactor two (which underwent to gradual increase of sodium concentration) the pH was stable 
around an average value of 7.56±0.016, in the reactor one (which underwent to a “pulse” salt 
injection) pH decreased up to 6.86. This result was an index of the VFA concentration increase.  
In reactor one, the VFA concentration had a steep increase. As can be observed from figure 22 in 
day 115 the total VFA reached 10.26 g/L with an acetate share of 76% of the total VFA 
concentration. This, as already said, is a direct consequence of acetoclastic methanogens inhibition 
due to sodium concentration increase. Propionate concentration was quite stable at around 0.92±10 
g/L (10% on average of the total VFA concentration). This persistency of high concentration of 
propionate after process instability due to operational problems was verified in another study 
(Tsapekos et al., 2017). In fact, it is known that the concentration of propionic acid is a good 
indicator of process upset, as it tends to accumulate during AD instability (Angelidaki et al., 2006). 
In reactor two total VFA concentration also increased, but gradually, reaching 2.86 g/L in day 115. 
Acetate in this case reached.1.30 g/L (45% of total VFA concentration), while propionate reached 
1.08 g/L. The average acetate share of the period was 54%, for the above-mentioned issue of 
persistency of high propionate concentration.  
An interesting result can be observed in day 120. In fact, in both reactors the pH was increased 
with a concomitant decrease in total VFA and acetate concentration. This is in accordance with 
the higher methane rate and methane yield obtained in this day and is probably explained by the 
adaptation of the microbial community to high salt concentrations. This result demonstrates that 
methanogens can adapt in continuous operation even after two sodium concentration “shocks”, 
which could have driven process to failure. On this topic, Feijoo et al .(1995) found that the 
reversibility of sodium inhibition on methanogenesis depends on the intensity of the inhibitory 
effect. Moreover, this study stated that only partial recovery is possible when the activity reduction 
during exposure exceeds 90% (Feijoo et al., 1995). This drove to the hypothesis that after the 
second sodium concentration pulse, the methane rate and methane yield could not achieve the 
levels previous to the first salt injection. Further investigations would have been needed to prove 
this hypothesis.

Regarding acclimatisation phenomenon to saline environment for microorganisms involved in 
AD, it is believed to occur by two mechanisms: the adaptation (tolerance to high osmotic pressure) 
of the original population and/or the shift towards halophilic species (Hierholtzer and Akunna, 
2012). This issue needs further investigations about the microbial community structure before the 
salinity stress and after adaptation. 
In conclusion, the main evidence from this VFA concentration analysis is that acetate was 
accumulating even in continuous mode operation, especially after a sudden increase of sodium 
concentration. This is in accordance with the result obtained in toxicity test, where microbial 
community had not enough time to adapt to high salt concentration. It could be therefore 
hypothesised that acetoclastic methanogens are the most sensitive AD microorganisms regarding 
sodium inhibition. This result is in accordance with other continuous experiments in literature 
(Rinzema, van Lier and Lettinga, 1988; Feijoo et al., 1995; Chen, Han and Sung, 2003; Hierholtzer 
and Akunna, 2012).  
 
At the end of the experiment, in day 106, after one HRT from the last sodium injection, H2S 
concentration in the biogas was also measured. The H2S concentration in biogas of reactor one 
resulted to 20258 ppm, while in reactor two the corresponding value was 7023 ppm. The high H2S 
concentration can be explained by the high sulphur content in the sea salt (18.6 g/kg) and by the 
possible takeover of sulphate degrader microorganisms related with inhibition of acetoclastic 
methanogens. After the recovery of methane rate and methane yield (day 120), H2S concentration 
was measured again and resulted at 6312 ppm in reactor one, while in reactor two was 7764 ppm. 
This interesting result regarding reactor one, merged with the data about increase of methane yield 
and decrease of acetate concentration in the same operation days, drove the hypothesis that 
acetoclastic methanogens became again predominant in the microbial community inside the 
reactor. In fact, sulphate reducing bacteria are reported to have less affinity for acetate compared 
to acetoclastic methanogens (Colleran and Pender, 2002). Even for this reason, as future 
perspective, it would be interesting to analyse the microbial community before the salinity stress 
and after adaptation in order to obtain a deeper understanding of the reactors’ microbiome. 
As future perspective, it would also be relevant to investigate sulphides concentration in the liquid 
phase, especially in the period of maximum inhibition of methanogenesis. In fact, on this topic, a 
paper of Soto, Méndez and Lema (1991) found that concentration of sulphides in the liquid phase 
is high when biogas production is low, as most of them will not be removed passing to gas phase.

6. CONCLUSIONS 
This study revealed that co-digestion between manure and macroalgal species Laminaria digitata 
is successful both in batch and in continuous mode operation.  
The co-digestion of the two substrates was found to give higher methane yield with the increase 
of the L. digitata share, in terms of organic matter. This trend was approximated by linear 
relationship. The maximum methane yield for co-digestion in batch in this study was obtained by 
the 80:20 VS ratio L. digitata to manure resulting in 378±23 NmLCH4/gVS. The two substrates 
did not show synergy or antagonism in methane production. 
Furthermore, 0.20 gNa/L and 1.00 gNa/L sodium concentration levels were found to be beneficial 
to microbial community and they gave the highest methane yield, independently from the sodium 
sources tested. The sodium concentration causing 50% inhibition in methane yield (IC50) was 9.00 
gNa/L, when sodium chloride was used as sodium source, and 2.86 gNa/L in case marine salt was 
used. Generally, a much lower methane yield was obtained in the set testing marine salt compared 
to the samples testing the same sodium concentration with sodium chloride source. This study, 
therefore, demonstrated that sodium source plays a key role in determining sodium inhibition 
thresholds.  
Moreover, this study showed that acetoclastic methanogens are probably the most sensitive 
towards sodium concentration increase. This was indicated by acetate accumulation both in the 
batch and in the continuous mode operation. It was therefore concluded that the bottleneck of the 
process regarding sodium inhibition was methanogenesis step. 
Finally, this study demonstrated the key role of acclimatization of microbial community when 
facing sodium inhibition. The continuous reactor operation revealed much higher inhibition 
thresholds compared to the results obtained from batch assays. This study also revealed the 
importance of salt injection mode, showing lower inhibition effects in case of gradual increase of 
sodium concentration. Moreover, this study proved that the inhibition from salt is a reversible 
phenomenon, and thus the process can recover, as demonstrated after two consecutive salt 
concentration pulses in continuously fed biogas reactors.

7. FUTURE PERSPECTIVES 
In the present study, co-digestion of cattle manure and macroalgal species Laminaria digitata was 
performed successfully and sodium inhibition thresholds were defined. However, the process still 
has some challenges and needs to be optimized. Some suggestions for the future are presented 
below. 
Further research on different inocula should be performed in order to verify that the use of salt-
acclimated inoculum can lead to better methanation process, reduction of sodium inhibition effects 
and therefore process optimization. In this study, inoculum from lab scale reactors treating manure 
and organic fraction of municipal solid waste was applied.  
More investigations should be carried to find out the behaviour in continuous way in relation to 
the sodium concentration. In fact, in this study, optimum co-digestion ratio was not applied in 
continuous reactors. However, to maintain an optimum in biogas production, the reactor should 
also not undergo to sudden increase in organic loading rate. Therefore, the dosing of macroalgae 
in the reactor should be carefully monitored to avoid process overload. 
Microbial analysis should be performed, as it will provide essential information regarding the 
tolerance of the microorganisms to increased salt concentrations. The present study, in fact, did 
not analyse the microbial community shifts caused by high salinity stress.  
The relatively high hydrogen sulphide production from macroalgae should be considered for the 
choice of suitable hydrogen sulphide removal system, as sulphur is one of the main components 
in marine salt and it is also present in this feedstock.  
Furthermore, it would be relevant to analyse sulphide concentration in the liquid phase of 
continuous reactors, to investigate if inhibition caused by high sulphide concentration can occur. 
As regarding the appliance to full scale reactors, to have a sustainable production, they should be 
located in coastal areas, where this type of biomass is usually available/cultivated, and so the 
availability and transportation costs would not be an issue.  
Moreover, the usage of this type of biomass is meant to be applied on a local scale, and local 
macroalgae species should be exploited as feedstocks. 
Economic feasibility of this process in large scale production should also be analysed. As 
macroalgae are currently used in the food industry, and for extraction of algal hydrocolloids and 
==PAGE:71==
 
59 
 
pharmaceuticals, a good perspective in this direction could be to couple their use as bioenergy 
feedstocks with cultivation for other purposes, in a biorefinery concept. 
Lastly, other species of macroalgal biomass available on other local scales should be investigated 
to understand their potential as feedstock for anaerobic digestion. In fact, in this study it was 
investigated only the specie L. digitate, as it is abundant in Danish littorals.
