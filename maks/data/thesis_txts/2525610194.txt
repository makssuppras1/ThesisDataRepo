Summary
The present work was focused on the assessment of heterogeneous N-aromatic compounds
for flow battery application. As organic compounds change their physicochemical and
chemical characteristic with the addition of functional group, the effect of it was re-
searched. The desired properties can be achieved by adding specific functional groups,
which could maximize solubility, increase or decrease redox potential, and other; this pro-
cess is referred to as molecular engineering. In the present work, the functional groups
tested were carboxyl, methyl, sulfonate, hydroxyl and ammonium. Between pyrazine
and quinoxaline, the latter was chosen to be studied further as the redox potential of
pyrazine had been really close to the hydrogen evolution limit. Thus, the mechanism be-
hind the electrochemical response of quinoxaline was investigated by altering the pH, in
buffered and unbuffered solution and by similarly studying two derivatives of quinoxaline
that bear negative and positive charge. Pristine quinoxaline exhibits the best response
at high pH in both buffered and unbuffered solution. At low pH and unbuffered solution
two oxidation peaks are encountered, whereas at low pH and buffered solution only the
second peak appears. The first oxidation peak is due to the re-oxidation back to quinox-
aline, while the second has been attributed to an oxidative addition of a hydroxyl group.
The peaks of cyclic voltammograms of the other two quinoxaline derivatives couldn’t
be fully attributed to different processes. However, generally it was noticed that the
best response for the negatively charged sulfonated quinoxaline was at low pH, whereas
the positively charged ammonium quinoxaline demonstrated the best response at high
pH. Finally, quinoxaline, sulfonated quinoxaline, ferrocyanide and ferricyanide had their
kinetics parameters measured with rotating disk voltammetry (RDV), rotating ring disk
voltammetry (RRDV) and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) as to have a
quantitative comparison between them.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Energy storage
By the end of the 16th century and until the beginning of the 19th century, electricity
had been mainly in the sphere of research [20]. The shift from pure scientific research to
everyday applications happened as landmark inventions, in the likes of electromechanical
generators (for producing electrical energy from kinetic energy), transformers (allowing
transmission of electricity across great distances) and the light bulb by Thomas Edison,
started to emerge. Since then, use of electricity has been exponentially rising, as more
and more applications are being electrified. A trend which will continue in the future
as many of the new functions in modern society, such as IT sector, industry and health

Batteries
One of the most widely used energy storage system nowadays is the battery. Batteries
are manufactured in a plethora of different ways, with different chemistries incorporated
depending on the desired application. Their primary function is to generate electrical
energy from chemical energy, resulting from the net Gibbs energy of the reaction in the

1.2.1
Operational principle and characteristics
Albeit the differences between the batteries currently on the market and on a research
level, all of them share a common operational principle depicted in figure 1.4. The heart
of the battery consists of three main pieces, two electrodes and an electrolyte. The
electrodes can either be distinguished as positive/ negative or anode/ cathode. In a
battery system the former terminology does not change between charge or discharge,
while in the later anode is defined where the oxidation takes place (negative electrode
during discharge and positive during charge) and cathode where reduction takes place
(positive electrode during discharge and negative electrode during charge). As mentioned
before, electricity in a battery is being generated by the net Gibbs energy. The driving
force of the process is the difference in Gibbs free energy between the cathode and anode
material. Electrons are released from the high energy electrode, the anode (oxidation),
travel through the external circuit, and reach the cathode (reduction). Meanwhile, in
order to maintain charge neutrality, available charged species (ions) travel through the

Flow batteries
Traditionally batteries have been manufactured for applications in the first two categories
of energy storage, small and medium size applications. As renewable energy productions
penetrate the market, a growing demand on large application energy storage has emerged
and batteries present an interesting solution. Currently, the largest battery for storing
renewable energy is located in South Australia, installed by Tesla. Hornsdale Energy
Reserve, as it is called, utilize the Lithium ion technology and can provide power to over
30000 homes [53].
As promising as lithium ion batteries show to be, they lack on some important factors
when it comes to conserving large amount of energy for extended periods of time. In
the present configuration of lithium ion batteries, energy storage capacity and power
capacity are coupled together, alongside mass and volume. So, in order to double the
amount of output power, the mass and subsequently the volume of the battery has to
be doubled, which in some cases that would infeasible [5]. Additionally, lithium ion
batteries pose a fire hazard due to their inherently high potential combined with an
organic electrolytes; such a threat that becomes more apparent in large systems [46].

Electrochemical Techniques
One of the widest aspect of science been utilised in battery research is electrochemistry.
Since batteries are electrochemical systems, naturally the most applicable tools are those
used in electroanalysis. Techniques such as open-circuit potential monitor (OCP), cyclic
voltammetry (CV), rotating disk voltammetry (RDV), rotating ring disk voltammetry
(RRDV) electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), bulk electrolysis (BE), galvano-
static charge/ discharge and i-v curve have been proven extremely useful tools to elu-
cidate and obtain valuable results. Additionally, scanning electron microscopy (SEM),
transmission electron microscopy (TEM), x-ray diffraction (XRD), nuclear magnetic res-
onance (NMR) and spectroscopic techniques, like uv-vis and IR spectroscopy, has also
been proven to be powerful allies. Finally, in battery systems charge/ discharge cycles
is used to provide insight on the stability of the materials [25, 58].
Figure 1.10: Schematic of a three electrode setup.
Cyclic voltammetry, rotating disk voltammetry, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy
and bulk electrolysis all share a common three electrode setup consisting of a work-
ing electrode (WE), reference electrode (RE) and counter electrode (CE). This three
electrode-setup has become the standard practice over the last years as it is more advan-
tageous to a two-electrode system consisting of a WE and a RE. In the latter, the RE is
used both as a point from which the potential of the WE is measured and as a gateway
of electrons when there is a current passing through. The drawback of the the two-
electrode system is the additional transport and kinetic polarization introduced on the
RE side, resulting in overpotentials accumulated in the measuring potential. A solution
to this has been the use of a third electrode (CE) which serves only as the gateway of
electrons, practically vanishing the overpotentials related to the the two aforementioned
polarizations. Additionally, since the RE does not directly participates in the reactions
any longer, the size of it can decrease and be moved closer to the WE mitigating a third
type of polarization caused by the ohmic resistance of the electrolyte. Eventually the
only two types of polarization left (considering ohmic polarization is greatly reduced
by high conductivity of the solvent) are kinetic polarization at the surface of the WE
==PAGE:34==
16
1 Introduction
and transport polarization associated with transport to the WE. The instrument used
to control the three-electrode setup is called potentiostat and its main function is to
adjust the current between the WE and CE to whatever is needed to make the voltage
difference between WE and RE to the value indicated [38].
1.3.3.1
Cyclic voltammetry
Electroanalysis consists of a framework of different techniques which study the analyte
by the means of monitoring the potential and/or the current. Three main categories
are found, voltammetry in which a constant and/or varying potential is applied while
the current is being measured, coulometry in which either the potential or the current
held constant. Potentiostatic coulometry (also known as bulk electrolysis) gives insight
on the number of electrons involved by keeping the potential at a constant value and
measuring the amount of current pass through, while coulometric titration holds the
current constant [41]. Finally, potentiometry refers to the occasion where the poten-
tial is passively monitored by either applying or not constant current, an example of
potentiometry is the open circuit potential monitoring.
Cyclic voltammetry can be considered the most important type of voltammetry, as it
is often the case to be the first electroanalytical technique used in an unknown sys-
tem. It can readily provide important information about the system in question, such
as reversibility, reduction/oxidation potential, stability. Additionally, mathematical de-
scriptions have been developed sufficiently to allow determination of kinetic and other
parameters such as diffusion coefficient, charge transfer coefficient, number of electrons
involved and concentration of the analyte.

Cyclic voltammetry can be considered as an extension of the linear sweep voltammetry.
In the later the potential is varied linear with time, from an initial value, until a final
value under a specified scant rate (v), while the current is recorded. In the case that
the analyte is in its reduced form, the initial potential is set to a value less than the
oxidation potential and is ramped towards more positive values. When the potential

declined as the depletion effect sets in. This process, in terms of measured current, is

Organic redox molecules holds promising potential as candidates for electroactive ma-

Organic compounds can affect their electrochemical response regardless of their envi-
ronment. A notable example of that is the intramolecular hydrogen bonding, which is
shown to produce a positive shift in the quinone reduction. Moreover, in some cases of
quinones with high acidity (e.g. α-hydroxyquinone (QOH)), self-protonation is observed
in aprotic media. After the initial first reduction the radical anion is converted to neutral
anion by accepting a proton from an oxidised quinone. Hereafter, it will either pick up
another electron for a total ECE process, or in conjunction with a second anion radical
it will disproportionate in a DISP fashion.

1.4.3.1
Formal potential
Formal potential and reversibility are key factors for this type of application. The redox-
active core of the molecule defines the formal potential, which afterwards can be modified
by the addition of functional groups [32]. As an example, J. Swartz and F. Anson [50]
performed a holistic research on the staircase voltammateric and polarographic responses
of pyrazine and various substituted pyrazines.
It was proven that electron-donating
groups shifts the half-wave potential to more negative values, while electron-withdrawing
groups to more positive. This trend is shown if figure 1.31 which illustrates the formal
potential varied by the Hammett sigma-para values.

This merit was derived by Hammett after observing that for a specific reaction, the re-
action constant K0 would alter in a linear fashion depending on the type of substituent
located in either the meta- or para- position (the ortho- position is omitted as steric hin-
drance significantly contributes to the reaction rate). Based on the former observation,
equation 1.47 was developed and a list of σ values were calculated for both on ortho-

pressure, etc.) [23]. The reaction chosen for comparing the effect of the substituents is
the acidic dissociation of the benzoic acid. The rate of reaction of the latter is affected
by the degree of stabilization of the conjugated base, which increases when electron
withdrawing groups are present [54] (while the opposite will be for the electron-donating
groups). As such, the σ value will be positive and proportional to the degree of electron
withdrawal.

1.4.3.2
Reversibility
Reversibility of a reaction indicates the degree of the kinetic polarization compared with
other polarizations. For a fast reaction the kinetic polarization will be minimal and
as such is termed as reversible. The degree of it can be observed via various methods,
as cyclic voltammetry being a good start for investigating reversibility of an unknown
system, with criteria been developed to ”quantify” the range of reversibility (table 1.2).
Reversibility depends on many factors ranging from the medium to inherent properties of
compound in question. Examples of how it alters with various external parameters was
shown at the PCET part of thesis. Additionally, the type of electrolyte and counter ion
used affects the reversibility possibly through reaction of the unstable radicals formed
during redox process and the ions of the electrolyte [34, 40].
1.4.3.3
Solubility
As seen from equation 1.45, the concentration of the active substance must be as high
as possible to increase energy density. Which means that the solubility of the active
material is of utmost importance and needs to be maximized for a given solvent. Thank-
fully, this can be controlled with the use of functional groups. In contrast to the formal
potential, solubility can be altered by orders of magnitude with the choice of proper
groups; as a rule of thumb the solubility of a compound will be maximized when it is
dissolved in a similar in nature solvent. For example if water is used as solvent, which

is polar and protic, compounds that are polar and protic will be readily dissolved. As
such, attaching functional with the former characteristic will make the active material
to be highly soluble in water. Some examples of functional groups that can achieve that
are carboxylate, ammine, nitrate, sulfo.

Additionally, aqueous solubility of organic compounds is dynamic in relation to the

1.4.3.4
Stability
A major drawback of organic compounds, especially for energy applications, is their often
quite poor stability. Similar to the ease of altering properties of organic compounds,
degradation through various mechanisms can also occur. As such, a lot of research has
been focused on techniques and functional groups that increases the lifetime of organic
compounds [10, 19]. A major contribution to the degradation processes is the formation
of radicals that are highly reactive. This is apparent in organic electrochemical systems
as most of the electron transfer happens through an intermediate radical state which, if
given the time and correct conditions, can spiral out to a series of unwanted reactions
with either the atoms of the electrolyte and/ or with itself [11, 40]
In conclusion, all of the aforementioned properties need to be taken into consideration
when searching for the proper system of redox active organic compounds. The versatility
of functional group modification provides an extensive freedom of bending the properties
to fit the demands of the flow cell battery application.

All of the electrochemical experiments took place under the same setup using the same
electrodes. The standard electrolyte used for assessing the effect that functional groups
has on the electrochemical response was a mixture of 0.1 M KOH, 0.9 M KCl, which had
a pH value of around 13. The choice of electrolyte was based on Milshtein et al. [34],
who performed a thorough investigation on the electrochemical activity of quinoxaline
in different electrolytes and concluded that 0.1 M KOH, 0.9 M KCl resulted in the most
reversible voltammograms.
The next set of experiments was the investigation of the electrochemical behaviour as a
function of pH in buffered and unbuffered solution. In the unbuffered case, solutions of
1 M KCl were made and drop wise addition of HCl or KOH to the desired pH. As for the
buffered solution, Britton-Robinson universal buffer (pH range 2-12 [9]) was used which

to assist with the conductivity of the solution and have similar ionic strength with the

were 99% purity purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. The working electrodes were glassy
carbon of diameter 0.3 and 0.5 cm, as a stationary and rotating respectively. While the
rotating ring disk electrode was made of 0.55 cm diameter glassy carbon (disk) and gold
(ring). As for the RE and CE, Ag/AgCl (3M NaCl) standard electrode and strip of Pt
was used respectively.

Quinoxaline
Quinoxaline will inherently have a slightly more positive redox potential as the aryl
group attached to the pyrazinic ring behaves as electron withdrawing group. Moreover,
increased stability is expected stemming from the benzene ring and additional position
for substitution are provided further away from the pyrazine ring (positions 5,6,7,8). The
redox potential of the quinoxaline is suitable to be paired up with ferro/ ferri cyanide
as a conceptual flow battery operating at pH = 13. The resulting potential of such a
system would 1.25 V which is an ideal balance between having a high pH for increasing
the energy and power density, but low enough as not to cause water splitting.

Another consideration with quinoxaline is its solubility. It would be assumed that due to
the increased size and additional non-protic organic group it would lack on that aspect
when compared to pyrazine. However, J. Milshtein et al. [34] showed that in some
electrolytes the solubility limit reaches all the way up to 4 M, making it comparable to
the solubility of inorganic salts. The reason why quinoxaline preferably dissolves more in
some types of electrolytes is yet unclear; they reported that Cl ions could be the culprit
for the increased solubility through some sort of interaction between Cl- and quinoxaline.
The theoretical energy density of the battery will be a function of the concentration.
Between the ferrocyanide and quinoxaline, the limiting electrolyte in terms of solubility
would be the ferrocyanide with a concentration of 0.7 M at 25 oC [55], assuming the
maximum 4 M solubility of quinoxaline. By operating close to the solubility limit of
ferrocyanide, the energy density of such system will be 40 Wh/l, which is close to the
energy density of G3 vanadium redox flow batteries ranging between 35-55 Wh/l [52].

Investigation of the effect of pH
The first compound whose pH dependency was investigated was quinoxaline. As men-

bility of irreversible hydrolysis leading to electrochemically inactive ketone. There are
three ways to combat that, controlling the pH, controlling the potential and substituting
the 2,3 position. In the current step, controlling the potential was chosen to inhibit the
degradation mechanism which seemed to work, and no degradation was visible in the
time scale and potential range of the experiments, even at highly acidic pH (pH < 3).
Later it will be shown how the other two precaution measures performed.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
An assessment of the aromatic heterogeneous compounds for flow battery application
had been performed in the current work. Literature review on the available electro-
chemical and kinetic information for pyrazine, quinoxaline and derivatives of them was
initially performed. Additionally, an in-depth search on the nuanced effects of proton
coupled electron transfer deemed necessary for the proper evaluation of the results ob-
tain from the application of electroanalytical techniques. Pyrazine and quinoxaline were
assessed in terms of the electrochemical response by functional group modification, pH
dependency and kinetic analysis in different media.
A great amount of attention has been focused on electrochemical storage for storing
energy from intermittent renewable energy sources such as wind and solar [15, 56, 59].
Redox flow batteries are one of the most attractive alternatives for large scale energy
storage due to the decoupling of energy capacity and size [47]. Currently, out of the
many types of flow batteries being researched and proposed, only vanadium redox flow
batteries have seen some degree of commercialization [44]. The drawback of the former
however, is the material scarcity stemming from vanadium, thus discovery of alternative
more abundant redox active materials will help to extend the use of RFB. Addition-
ally, these novel materials must also be cheap, preferably non-toxic and stable; organic
molecules satisfy the cheap and abundant premises and offer extended tailorability to ful-
fil the rest of the requirements [14]. Thus, it is highly important to elucidate the effects
that functional group modification bear on the electrochemical response of electroactive
organic materials.
Currently, the organic group of molecules researched the most for RFB application has
been quinones [39]. In the present work, the focus had been on pyrazine and quinoxaline.
The electrochemical properties of both stem from the redox activity of the pyrazinic ring,
specifically alteration between pyrazine (oxidised) and 1,4-dihydropyrazine (reduced).
The eventual response is, however, greatly affected by both the functional groups at-
tached on the molecule and from the electrolyte where is dissolved. As a result, for the
holistic analysis of the electrochemical response of the pyrazinic ring in both pyrazine and
quinoxaline, the investigation process was divided in three steps. Firstly, the effect of
functional group modification was investigated with the use of pyrazine and derivatives
of it to quantify the effect of methyl and carboxylic group have on the redox potential.
Secondly, due to the importance of pH in the redox activity of many organic molecules,
the dependency of the potential (and stability) with pH in buffered and unbuffered medi-
==PAGE:94==
76

ums was investigated. Finally, kinetic analysis on the most promising compounds was
performed.
The effect induced by the addition of methyl and carboxylic group was studied on the
pyrazine ring. Methyl groups are considered electron donating group which effectively
lowers the potential by approximately 50 mv per methyl group. Carboxylic group on
the other hand, acts as electron withdrawing group raising the redox potential of the
pyrazine by approximately 70 mV per group. Additionally, carboxylic pyrazines were the
first group of compounds which started to showcase largely distorted voltammograms of
abnormal peak splitting. Sulfonated and mono-carboxylic quinoxaline derivatives also
demonstrated the same behaviour, but in less extend compared to the tetracarboxylic
pyrazine. Unfortunately, precise reasons as to why this took place have not been elu-
cidated; it’s speculated that the reasons might be due to interaction with the water
molecules, interactions with other ions in the solution, intramolecular hydrogen bond-
ing or subsequent chemical reaction after the initial reduction, which leads to oxidation
of a compound slightly different than the original. The last point is supported from
the fact that similar peak splitting was observed in quinoxaline at a highly buffered,
acidic (pH < 5.5) solution wherein the only oxidation peak visible is at a more positive
potential likely caused from oxidative addition of a hydroxyl group, instead of oxidising
back to quinoxaline.
Afterwards, the pH dependency in both buffered and unbuffered solutions was exam-
ined. Three compounds of different charge sign were chosen, these were quinoxaline,
sulfonated quinoxaline (DSDMQUI) and ammonium quinoxaline (TMDNQUI) repre-
senting a neutral, a negative and a positive compound. All three of them had significant
changes when dissolved in buffered or unbuffered solutions, with the most prominent be-
ing TMDNQUI which featured high degree of irreversibility in unbuffered solution. The
negatively charged compound had the highest reversibility at low pH, while the positively
charged seemed to have better kinetics at high pH. Similarly, quinoxaline performed best
at high pH as indicated in literature. It must be noted here that the electrolyte wherein
the active compound is dissolved in highly affects the eventual electrochemical response.
Finally, the kinetics and stability of quinoxaline and DSDMQUI were assessed via steady
state rotating disk voltammetry. The electrode used was a glassy carbon disk. Addition-
ally, ferro/ferri cyanide kinetics were also determined as a benchmark for comparison.
Quinoxaline resulted in exceptional high exchange current density, most probable due
to weak adsorption on the surface of the electrode, while DSDSMQUI and ferro/ferri
cyanide had similar i0. Rotating ring disk voltammetry with a glassy carbon disk, gold
ring electrode did not reveal any degradation in both quinoxaline and DSDMQUI.
