Abstract
Given a set of vehicles to load, the RORO ship stowage problem determines the plan to know where
each vehicle should be loaded on board the ship. This plan must be made by taking into account
various aspects such as safety on board, economic reasons, or the practicality of the plan.
With the globalization, the maritime transport has become the main transport mode for exchanges.
More than 80% of the goods are traded using this mode. Continuous improvement is so required
to meet the latest demands, always greater.
At the same time, these technological innovations
must also consider other issues of the world, such as global warming. While design and engine
innovations already focus on this problem, it is also possible to reduce the fuel consumption, and
so carbon dioxide emissions and the cost, by decreasing the weight or the speed of a ship.
The RORO ship is diﬀerent from LOLO ship, that loads the cargoes by using cranes. In RORO
shipping, the vehicles or wheeled cargoes are loaded and unloaded by means of built-in ramps. The
RORO ship loading problem has already been treated in diﬀerent aspects, but is not as popular
as the same problem for container ship.
Sometimes, authors decided to focus on the economic
aspect, sometimes, some new safety constraints regarding the ﬁre safety or some weight limits were
implemented. A brief survey of the literature is given in order to have an overview of the work that
has already been realised.
In this thesis, a new mathematical model is proposed to solve the stochastic cargo stowage planning
problem in RORO shipping under simple stability constraints. This work focus on the eﬀects of
adding the randomness in the model, and so some constraints that would not have been signiﬁcantly
impacted by the new stochastic variables have been ignored for more clarity. This thesis also deals
with a problem containing only journeys with two ports, which implicitly means that all the cargoes
and vehicles will have to be loaded at the ﬁrst port and unloaded at the arrival port. Because of the
numerous constraints and the diﬃculty to reach a good stability, this problem can be very diﬃcult
to solve by using a solver. Thereby, an adaptive large neighborhood search algorithm has been.
This heuristic algorithm takes advantage of the fact that it is possible to determine in advance the
most attractive slots on board to load the diﬀerent vehicles or cargoes. An exact repair method will
be used to rebuild the solutions.
The results show that the meta-heuristic algorithm can obtain some near-optimal solutions in a
reasonable time window for small instances of the problem, and can perform better than a math-
ematical solver for diﬃcult instances under short time limit. After evaluating the quality of the
diﬀerent methods of the ALNS, the ability of the solution to adapt to new scenarios is also tested.
Finally, a critical look at the method and the results is given, and several avenues for improvement
are given.
keyworkds: RORO ship, stowage planning, adaptive large neighborhood search, meta-heuristics

Introduction
3.1
Introduction to RoRo shipping
RORO stands for Roll-On Roll-Oﬀ, and it is used to describe the maritime shipping segment that
transports wheeled cargo such as cars, and trailers.
RORO ships are equipped with a mobile
access ramp allowing loading / unloading of the goods by towing between the ship and the dock.
This method has several advantages over conventional container ships. The loading and unloading
processes are faster and more ﬂexible because the vehicles can be directly driven into the ship. The
vehicles can also land few minutes after docking, and continue zith their routes, which allows to
have shorter delivery time. Finally, RORO shipping is much cheaper, and it also guarantees reliable
departure and arrival dates. The RORO ships carry a wide variety of wheeled cargo such as private
cars, buses, vans, semi-trailers, project cargo, as well as thousands of passengers on coastal ferry
voyages.
The RORO ships are traditionally opposed to the lift-on/lift-oﬀ(LOLO) ships, which needs cranes
to load and unload the goods on board. Instead of that, RORO ships have either built-in or shore-
based ramps that allow the cargo to be eﬃciently rolled on and oﬀthe vessel when in port. While
smaller ferries that travel across rivers and other short distances frequently use built-in ramps, the
term RORO is more commonly associated with large oceangoing vessels. The ramps and doors may
be located in stern, bow or sides, or any combination thereof.
Originally, the development of this type of ship has been motivated by the World War II, for the
loading of military equipment. Then, the use of this type of ship as merchant ships started in the
early 1950s’, and was used in short-sea routes [19]. The development of this new technique has
also been encouraged by the increase of road transports. With that system, cars and lorries could
directly drive on to a RORO ship at a port, and quickly drive oﬀthis ship at another port in few
minutes, which makes that methods very eﬃcient for this kind of transport.
3.2
Diﬀerent types of RoRo ship
It exists a whole variety of RoRo ships, such as:
• Ferries: merchant vessels, often used to carry vehicles and their passengers.
• Barges: more used to carry freight on canals and rivers.
• Pur car carrier (PCC): Pure Car Carriers are vessels, specially designed for the transportation
of passenger cars in large quantity.
• Pur car/truck carrier (PCTC): Like the PCC, its objective is to transport a massive number
of vehicles but only cars. PCTC transports also trucks and other variations of four-wheeled
vehicles:
• ROLO: a mix between a RORO and a LOLO vessel
• ROPAX: acronym for ROLO passengers, it used for freight vehicles transport, with passenger
accommodation. Often referred as cruiseferris.
And even more when adding the variations of these diﬀerent types of vessels, such as ConRo, LMSR,
etc...

• cars, or small size cargoes.
• High and heavy cargoes, such as trucks, tractors, construction machines, or heavy rolling stock

• Non-containerised cargo (NCC), which are goods that are carried without the use of cargo.
This types of loads includes goods in liquid or dry state, ant they are shipped in crates, bags,
boxes, drums, barrels and they are extremely large in sizes/dimensions.

World ﬂeet
In early 2020, the world ﬂeet totalled 98,140 ships of 100 gross tons and above, which corresponds to
2,061,944,484 deadweight tons of capacity (Deadweight tonnage is a measurement of total contents
of a ship including cargo, fuel, crew, passengers, food, water, etc...).
During 2019, the global
commercial shipping ﬂeet grew by 4.1 per cent, the highest growth since 2014, but still below levels
observed during the 2004–2012 period. According to UNCTA [21], over the past 20 years, vessel

since 1996. To conﬁrm that, the highest average vessel sizes are found within the youngest ﬂeet
segments (zero to four years). This perpetual increase aims at optimizing costs through economies
of scale.
Digitalization
The COVID-19 epidemic has exposed high levels of interdependence and sets in motion new trends
that go to maritime transport and commerce. The sector is at a pivotal moment as it grapples with
immediate concerns raised by the pandemic.
Like the other industries, the maritime transport is also transformed by the digitalisation, and the
optimal stowage problem has attracted more attention these past few years. Reducing the time
spending at the port with an eﬃcient stowage plan can have signiﬁcant impact in diﬀerent aspects.
The ﬁrst to come in mind for the carriers and the shippers is the economic impact: a vessel in the
port is not transporting goods, and so is not earning money. Moreover, the inventory-holding costs

The performance of seaports is a key factor in world trade. Ships are made to transports goods on
the sea, and the time spend in port is wasted time. In addition, the timing to deliver the goods is
pre-deﬁned and takes into account the time to load the vessels, so the longer vessels spend in port,
the less time they have at sea to carry cargo for international trade. Thus, they will have to move
faster which implies a greater consumption of fuel and so more carbon-dioxide emissions. This also
has an impact directly on the holding costs. Both carriers and shippers would like to avoid that.
Even the ports would beneﬁcial of faster turnaround because that will increase their throughput
capacity, with the same ﬁxed assets. The time spent in the port is a key indicator of the eﬃciency
of the port, that is why this time need to be minimized as much as possible. The time required to
generate a stowage plan and to apply this plan are two potential sources of improvement.
Ecology
Maritime transport is responsible of around 940 million tons of CO2 annually, which represents
approximately 2.5% of the world emissions [9]. If maritime transport were a country, it would be
the 6th country in the world in CO2 emissions ranking. These emissions could increase signiﬁcantly
in the future. Depending on future economic and energy development, these emissions could increase
between 50% and 250% by 2050 according to the 3rd IMO GHG study [13]. At the EU level, CO2
emissions from maritime transport have increased by 48% between 1990 and 2008, and are expected
to increase by 86% above 1990 levels by 2050. The projected increase in GHG emissions is due to
the expected growth of the world economy and the associated transport demand from world trade.

Today, more than 90% of EU external trade is seaborne. This clearly shows the need for enhanced
action on shipping emissions.
The amount of carbon dioxide emitted by a ship is inﬂuenced by wide panel of features, such as
the vessel type, the speed, size design or ballast. Thanks to economy of scale, a larger ship will
emit less carbon dioxide per ton-mile, despite the fact it will emit more carbon dioxide in total for
a journey. For instance, the smallest container ships of up to 999 TEUs emit about twice as much
carbon dioxide per container carried as the largest container ships. Speed is also an important
factor. Container ships tend to transit at higher speeds than dry bulk carriers, thus – all other
things being equal – emitting more carbon dioxide per ton-mile than the latter. Indirectly, the time
spent in the port is also a factor of CO2 emissions, as it will have an impact on the speed of the
ship. Travel time windows are deﬁned in advance, so if a ship spends too much time at the port,
it means it will have to be faster to reach its next destination, and so to increase its consumption
of fuel and CO2 emissions. Some further gains can reasonably be expected over the next decade.
New design solution more ecological could replace older, less eﬃcient.

3.4
Stowage plan
Stowage plan for container ships is the plan and method by which diﬀerent types of container
vessels are loaded with containers of speciﬁc standard sizes. For RORO shipping, it is exactly the
same but with cars, trucks, other vehiles or wheeled cargoes instead of containers. The plans are
used to maximize the economy of shipping. They are also very important for safety on board,
and it is recommended that personal working on ships are familiar with the plan. A completed
stowage diagram would show what materiel has been loaded and its stowage location in each hold,
between-deck compartment, or other space in a ship, including deck space. Each port of discharge
is indicated by colors or other appropriate means. Deck and between-deck cargo normally is shown
in perspective, while cargo stowed in the lower hold is shown in proﬁle, except that vehicles usually
are shown in perspective regardless of stowage [4].
Several information are essential in order to make an eﬃcient plan, such as the cargoes that need to
be loaded, the cargoes already present on board, or the route of the ship. In addition, some cargoes
could require special accommodation, such as dangerous cargoes, or cargoes too heavy.

4
Literature review
Stowage planning of RORO ships problem is not as popular as the same problem for container ships
in terms of research paper. The container ship stowage problem has attracted more attention and
so more articles have appeared on this topic, but the two problems have some similarities and some
ideas can be transferred between them. In this section, some literature about the stowage planning
problem in general is given.
4.1
Previous algorithms for stowage planning
In 2010, Hoﬀet al. [11] gives a literature survey of the ﬂeet composition and routing in maritime
and road-based transportation, and present the current status of the research. The literature of
extended problem or problem related to the RSSP is given.
In 2011, Bernt Olav Øvstebø, Lars Magnus Hvattum and Kjetil Fagerholt published ”Optimization
of stowage plans for RoRo ships” [16].
The problem is to decide which cargoes/vehicles must
be transported to the next port, and where to load them, in order to maximize the proﬁt, while
respecting some stability constraints. Some vehicles must be carried because of long-terms contract,
and special attention should be paid to the location of cargo ships because a road is made up of
several ports. Thus, certain freighters could be blocked, and it would be necessary to unload and
then reload certain vehicles in order to reach these freighters. The MIP formulation of this problem
gives solutions that cannot be easily applied in reality because it will be more annoying in a practical
perspective. In addition, the computational time of such a program is very long. In order to obtain
a stowage plan more desirable and faster, they have built an heuristic. The program ﬁrst takes all
the mandatory cargoes only and construct a stowage plan using squeaky wheel optimization and
local search optimization to improve the solution if the stability is violated. Then, each optional
cargo is tested to see if it can be added to stowage plan, by order of pseudo revenue. As it can
be seen, the priority in this problem is that, given a set of mandatory cargoes, how many optional
cargoes can be added, and which of them should be loaded on board in order to maximize the proﬁt.
In 1998, Mordecai Avriel et al. [5] focused on reducing the number of shifts for container ships. As
a route is composed by several ports, some containers will be displaced from location to another to
load and unload other cargoes which were previously blocked. This operation increases the amount
of time spent in the port, and the shifting cost, it is so interesting to try to minimize this amount
of shifts. Jone R. Hansen et al., 2016 [10] combined diﬀerent aspects of the previous problems,
considering in the objective functions both the proﬁt of the optional cargoes and the amount of
required shifts.
In 2018, Romanas Puisas [18] has improved the model, by adding some constraints regarding ﬁre
safety, but also by providing a new possible method to make the discretisation of stowage locations.

can be stowed, instead of having only one grid for every types of cargoes (cars, semi-trailers, etc...).
The ﬁnal improvement is to minimize the amount of shifts.

5
Problem description
According to the time span of the plan, operation plans can be divided into three main categories:
strategic, tactical and operational planning. Strategic planning involves a time frame of several
years, usually involving decisions such as determining ﬂeet size and combination. Tactical planning
usually has a time frame of several months. A typical problem might be to determine which ship
should serve which route. Operational planning involves short-term decisions usually related to a
given voyage, such as speed selection, weather routes or stowage. If a route is composed by more
than 2 harbours, it adds another constraint to the problem. For a route with 3 harbours, some
cargos that needs to be transported from harbour 1 to harbour 3 could be annoying during loading
and unloading at port 2. Another objective would be to minimize the number of shifts during the
route.
Because of the number of constraints, it can easily becomes impossible for a human to determine
the best way to allocate the cargoes in an optimal way. Currently a cargo is often allocated in the
vessels disregarding stability restrictions. But as cargo is loaded on the vessels, the balance of the
ship changes. The ship is then allowed to sail by loading ballast water and reaching a stable state.
Ballast water is, however, costly as it implies a bigger fuel consumption during sailing.
This project aims at implementing a model/algorithm to solve the stowage planning problem of
RORO vessels. Deterministic models have, however, some limits as the exact arrival time of the
cargo is not known. Stochastic optimization models are envision as a way to achieve better stowage
plans that reduce the time to load the vessel and minimize the use of ballast water.
In this problem, only a part of the goods to load are known in advance. It assumed that more
vehicles or cargoes will arrive, but their number and their weights is still unknown. However, an
approximation of the number of future cargoes is known, and it is also assumed that the weight of
the goods is following a triangular distribution whose extreme values and peak are known. All the
cargoes will have to be loaded on board, and of course, since it is a pure roll-on/roll-oﬀvessel, the
wheeled cargoes cannot be stacked, and so only 1 cargo can be loaded on each slot. The goal is to
obtain a stowage plan reducing the use of ballast water and the time to load the vessel as much as
possible.
The problem is considered as a two-stage stochastic linear problem. During the ﬁrst stage, a certain
number of cargoes are already present on the docks, and it is assumed that the future vehicles to be
loaded will all arrive at the same time (in reality, they will be stored in the docks until the last cargo
arrive). The weight of these futures vehicles or wheeled cargoes is not known, but the distribution
of the weights is known with a previous analysis.

The cost function with penalty
Sometimes it might be a good thing work with invalid solutions to progress.
However, a valid
solution should always be considered better than an invalid solution. So, it is required to ﬁnd a
way to evaluate an invalid solution, by measuring how the constraints are not respected. The goal
is to compare it with a valid solution, but also to compare invalid solutions between them to decide
which constraint is more valid than the other. A solution will be considered more invalid if the
constraints are more violated.
19

First, the only constraints that will be allowed to not be respected in this problem will be the
balance constraints, deﬁned by equations (3) and (4). In this work, the solutions can be compared
by looking at their costs: the cheapest solution (= the solution with the lowest loading time) is
considered as the best solution. But if it is allowed to break the constraints, solutions that should
be invalid with the full set of constraints will take the advantage, because they will be cheaper.
Indeed, it would be faster to load all the vehicles on the same side, close to the entrance of the ship,
but that will create instability on board, and it will require a big amount of ballast water to balance
the ship. So if a solution is non valid, this should appear in its cost by adding some penalties. This
should be done with the objective that when comparing two solutions, one valid and one invalid,
the valid solution will always appear as the best solution.
The new cost function can be found in equation (9). In this way, the cost of an invalid solution is
computed normally, like if it was a valid solution. But in addition, some penalty costs are added
in order to artiﬁcially increase the cost of this incorrect solution which doesn’t respect the balance
constraints. No penalty costs are added for the other constraints because they are always ﬁlled. An
important thing is that the penalty is a linear function of the gap between the minimal/maximal
limit value of x or y and the computed value of x and y, and so a linear function in the excess of x
or y, multiplied by a parameter M, the penalty unit cost. Thereby, the penalty is ”proportional to
the invalidity”: a solution which is close to respect the balance constraints will always considered
better than a solution where the values x and y are far from being in the interval allowed. This
allows to be able to compare the invalid solution. Otherwise, if the penalty cost was just a binary
value (+M if the constraint is not respected, +0 if it is), the invalid solutions will all be on a equal
foot and it will be harder to go from a random invalid solution to valid solution.

A clustering algorithm aims at partitioning a set of objects into clusters (groups) so that the objects

dissimilar as possible. The clusters are represented by so-called centroids, i.e, cluster centers. All
data points are assigned to one and only one centroid and, thus, belong to the respective cluster.

• k-means clustering: Clusters the data objects into k clusters. The centroid of a cluster is the
mean of all data objects in the cluster. This mean value is not required to be a point of the
data.
• k-medoid clustering / Partition around medoids (PAM): similar to k-means, but the centroids
are chosen from the data objects (called medoids then).
In this problem, the k-medoid clustering algorithm has been selected to perform the clustering.
Usually, the k-medoids problem is NP-hard to solve exactly, so it is too long. PAM uses a greedy
search which may not ﬁnd the optimum solution, but it is faster than exhaustive search.

The ALNS
7.1
Overview
Heuristics based on large neighborhood search have recently shown excellent results in solving dif-
ferent kind of problems, including transportation or scheduling problems. The large neighborhood
search method has been implemented the ﬁrst time by Paul Shaw in 1997 [20]. The LNS has been
applied to vehicle routine problems with very good results [20], [12]. Large neighborhood search
methods are very useful for problems with diﬃcult constraints. It allows to explore a large and
complex neighborhood in order to ﬁnd an improvement, which will not be present in a ”standard”
neighborhood. However, These large neighborhoods would require more computational time, be-
cause more solutions should be created, and then, evaluated. So a smart LNS algorithm explores
a complex neighborhood by use of heuristics, in order to reduce this computational time by only
exploring promising solutions path. For that, a destructor and a repair function are used, and the
solution is gradually improved by alternatively destroying and repairing this solution [17].
But in an Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search, the algorithm is not limited by only one destructor
and one repair method. Instead of that, the algorithm contains a collection of function and will
randomly uses a pair of (destructor,repair) methods in order to improve the solution. The prob-
ability of a function to be selected is equal for all the functions at the beginning of the process,
but then evolves depending on the results obtained. If a signiﬁcant improvement is observed, the
probability of the methods used will increase, and so their will be more likely to be selected later.
On the other hand, if the cost of the solution evolves negatively, or worst, if the new solution is not
valid, the probabilities will decrease.
This allows to use a large panel of destructor and repair methods, and to potentially have a good
pair in every circumstances. Indeed, it’s possible for a destructor to be very eﬃcient at a certain
moment (such as the beginning of the algorithm), and then to see its eﬃciency decreases along
time because it cannot destroy the function in a good way to then improve it at the end of the
algorithm (and vice-versa). ALNS have already shown good results with the RSSP [14], but also
with some stochastic problems, such as the vehicle routine problem with time window, with a
stochastic demand [15].
7.2
Why ALNS and not another algorithm ?
Several options have been considered before deciding the algorithm that will be used to solve this
problem. The ﬁrst choice was to decide if the program would be solved by using a decomposition
method or a meta-heuristic method. This section gives explanations concerning the choice of the
method of resolution.
Exact methods
Some decomposition methods have been considered. First the Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition, but
this option is not the best when trying to solve stochastic programs. Indeed, the Benders decom-
position for solving two-stage stochastic optimization problem appeared as a better option. This
method, also called L-shaped method, had the advantage to be able to provide an exact solution
of the problem. The purpose is to divide the problem in one master problem and several sub-
problems, easier to solve, in order to improve the computational time. However, the nature itself of
the problem seemed to be an obstacle for this method.
23
==PAGE:25==
Indeed, in the Benders decomposition (at least in the classic Benders algorithm), the integer vari-
ables can only belong to the master problem. In this program, only 2 variables are continuous, and
all the others are binary variables, so integer variables. Because of that, it would not be possible
to divide the problem in several sub-problems in an eﬃcient way, and the improvement, if it exists,
would not be signiﬁcant.
Heuristics methods
After these ﬁndings, the focus was quickly put on the meta-heuristic methods. The ﬁrst method
to be considered was the genetic algorithm.
This algorithm uses an analogy of the theory of
evolution to ﬁnd a suﬃciently good solution to the problem. The main idea is to create an initial
population of solutions, and then ”reproduce” them by crossing the solution in order to create a
new population, and then only keep the best elements with a selection procedure, and so improve
the population generation after generation. But the eﬃciency of this method heavily relies on the
cross-over function, and in that case, no good method could be found on time. Indeed, the nature of
the problem makes that it extremely diﬃcult to cross two solutions which respect all the constraints
of the problem, in order to create a new solution that will still be valid regarding these constraints.
So, if it’s already hard to obtain a valid solution, it’s even harder to ﬁnd an improvement in the
cost of the solution, and there is almost never an improvement when creating a new generation,
which makes this method non eﬃcient.
Thus it has ﬁnally been decided to continue with the Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS).
The idea is to start from an initial solution, to slightly destroy it and then to repair it by using a
repair method, which will improve the initial solution a little.
7.3
Algorithm structure
The process of the ALNS is illustrated in ﬁgure 5. First, an initial solution is created. This solution
can either satisfy the constraints or not, depending on how act the destructor and repair methods.
If these methods are able to work with a non-valid method, and if the cost of this solution can
be computed in a relevant way, then it is not a problem to start with a non-valid solution and to
improve it later. Regarding the initial cost, it doesn’t require to be good. Of course, it might be
better to start with a solution which already has a good cost, but the most important thing here is
to have a solution quickly, in order to quickly reach the next stage of the algorithm.
Then, the algorithm moves into the improvement stage, where the solution will be successively
destroyed and then repaired by a couple of methods (destructor, repair), randomly selected. The
new solution obtained will be assessed, and a score will be given to the destructor and repair
methods depending on the evolution of the solution. If the solution has improved, the score will be
positive and the probability of the methods to be selected later will increase, while if there is no
improvement or if the solution is worst than before, these probabilities will decrease.
This process is repeated until a stop criterion is reached. This criterion is often a time limit, but it
can also be a value to reach an objective value (for instance, if the optimal solution is known, when
the gap between the objective values of the current solution and the optimal solution is less than
1%). An eﬃcient improvement stage should be able to guide an initial solution with a poor cost to
a near-optimal solution, which is why it is not so important to start with a good initial solution.

for being usually empty, in order to reload them in a better place. The shape can be observed in

For this, 3 diﬀerent functions have been created:
• destructor random: this function randomly removes some cargoes from the solution. The
idea of this destructor is to disturb slightly the solution in order to avoid to be stuck in a
local minimum. For this, some cargoes are simply randomly selected and removed from the
solution, in order to not be focus only on one part of the grid.
• destructor more expansive cargoes: this function removes in priority the cargoes located
furthest from the entrance of the ship.
This has the advantage to speciﬁcally target the
cargoes that increase the most the cost function, in order remove them and so to improve the
result. In addition, other cargoes randomly located in the ship are also removed, in order to
27
==PAGE:29==
facilitate reconstruction while respecting the balancing constraints. With the same purpose,
the method doesn’t select the most expansive cargoes at all cost, but take a list larger than
required of the most expansive cargoes, and then select a certain amount of cargoes among
them. These small adaptations are necessary because all the cargoes that contribute the most
to the cost function are located in the opposite side of the entrance of the ship, removing only
these cargoes would create a problem in the balance that could be solved only by replacing
these cargoes in approximately the same location, and so the improvement would not be very
signiﬁcant.
• destructor groups: this function destroys some groups of cargoes. Some slots are deﬁned in
the solution, and all the cargoes located around these slots inside a certain ray are removed.
This has the advantage to give more ﬂexibility when reconstructing the solution. Indeed,
when trying to repair the solution, it appears sometimes that the the balance constraint could
be more easily satisﬁed if an empty slot was located 1 square further. With this method,
bigger spaces are created and it is easier to move the cargoes. These empty zones give more
ﬂexibility to the repair method rearrange the cargoes.
7.6
the repair method
The repair methods can be either an exact method or a heuristic method [17]. While exact methods
are more accurate, the advantage to obtain heuristic methods is to obtain a solution faster. When
repairing the function, 2 main diﬃculties where encountered:
• respecting the balancing constraint is extremely diﬃcult: the impact of a single cargo, even
located close to the center of the ship, can be very important. Because of that, reconstructing
the solution step by step while respecting at each step the balancing constraints is almost
impossible and doesn’t make any sense. Even if the constraints are satisﬁed when 50, 20, or
10 cargoes still need to be loaded, the impact of the last cargo is so signiﬁcant that it can
make all the solution invalid.
• the number of possible arrangement for a ship containing n slots, with k cargoes to load, is
n!
(n−k)! (the explanation is given below *). Even with a sub-problem, focusing only on a small
part of the ship, this number can quickly become too high to test all the possible combinations
in a decent time frame.
Because of these two points, it is complicated to build a greedy repair function that will be eﬃcient.
The two main obstacles of using a classic MIP solver were, ﬁrst than it would be too long, and then,
the stowage plan given by the MIP program could be not practical and easy to put in place. But
now, the problem doesn’t need to be solved entirely. It is just required to repair a partial solution.
Instead of using a single big MIP program, the idea will be to implement another MIP program,
easier, with variables, and thus, faster.

step 1: the repair method using gurobi
In the original problem, with Cp present cargoes and with max Cf future cargoes that will arrive in

binary variables was Cp × S + max Cf × S × W. Each of these variables could take 2 values, 0 or

several hundred of cargoes/vehicles to load. So the main idea here is to set some variables, in order
to reduce the degree of freedom of the program.
After destroying partially the solution, some cargoes are missing, but most of the cargoes are still
present in the solution. In this repair function, these cargoes will be blocked in their slots. Given
the cargo cp still loaded in slot s after destroying the solution, the constraint xcp,s = 1 will be added
to the problem. When doing this for all the cargoes which are still in the solution, the program
contains much more constraints, but less degree of freedom, and in consequences, the computational
time to solve this problem is way lower than the time required to solve the original problem.
step 2: Removing the set variables
This ﬁrst repair function already allowed to obtain some solutions that were close to the theo-
ritical optimal solutions, but it was still too long. The initialization phase of Gurobi, with the
implementation of all the cutting planes, could take too much time.
The problem with this program is that it contains a lot of variables which are set by a constraint,
and so which are variables only by the name, because their values cannot change. However, this
way do proceed add a lot of useless variables and constraints in the problem and increase the
computational time for no reason. In order to improve the speed of this repair method, it would be
better to simply delete them.
Thereby, the next idea of this repair function is to deﬁne a ”sub grid” of the original grid, containing
all the cargoes and possible slots to load them, and to work only with this smaller grid.
The ﬁrst step is to do the inventory of all the free slots, and all the present and future cargoes that
are missing in the solution partially destroyed. Then, a sub-grid with the available slots is created,
and will be ﬁlled only with the remaining cargoes.
Before solving this sub-problem, the current values of x and y are also computed, and will be
uptaded with the new sub-problem.
Finally, after solving this sub-problem, a function connect this sub-grid to the original solution, and
all the cargoes are loaded in their new slots.

8.2
Parameters tuning
In this problem, 2 parameters were considered in order to improve the eﬃciency of the algorithm,
one related to the destructors, and the other one to the repair method. These 2 parameters are:
• The proportion of cargoes removed in the solution: The more cargoes are removed,
the more complicated the solution is to rebuild. This can lead to longer iterations in terms
of computational time, or also to more iterations where no solution can be found within the
time limit. On the other hand, when more cargoes are removed, it oﬀers more freedom to
the repair function to build a new solution, and the improvement in the cost function is more
signiﬁcant. The goal is to ﬁnd a good balance between the number of iterations (bigger when
the number of removed cargoes is reduced), and the improvement in the cost function at each
31
==PAGE:33==
iteration (bigger when the number of removed cargoes is increased). Finally, there are good
reasons to think that this number of cargoes removed should be expressed as a percentage of
the total number of cargoes in the instance of the problem. Indeed, removing 50 cargoes in a
instance that contains 100 cargoes to load is much more signiﬁcant than removing 50 cargoes
in an instance that contains 1000 cargoes.
• The time limit of the repair function: depending on the number, the weights or the
position of the removed cargoes, the time required the repair a solution can vary a lot. Whereas
a longer time limit allows to ﬁnd some improvement in more complex situations, sometimes,
it’s not worth spending too much time on a single iteration, it’s better to give up and try
again with a new partially destroyed solution.
It exists many ways to perform parameter tuning, such as Bayesian optimization, gradient-based
optimization or evolutionary optimization, but the most traditional way or performing parameter
optimization is the grid search methods. After manually specifying for each parameter the possible
values that can be taken, the algorithm will simply test all the possible combinations in order to
ﬁnd the best set of values for these parameters. To evaluate each combination of values taken by
the parameters, the cost of the solution obtained after a certain time with these parameters will be
compared to a reference cost, and the relative gap between the cost of the new solution and the cost
of the reference solution will be saved, and compared to the other scores obtained with diﬀerent
parameters.

In this section, a qualitative analysis of the ALNS is done, and the results are compared with what
have been done in other papers.
As it can be seen in ﬁgure 8, the algorithm converges without problem. Here, it takes around 75

should always be lower or equal to 0. Indeed, because the repair function aims at ﬁnding the optimal

In this work, the solving time is increasing with the size of the ship. Actually, the 2 main factors
of the time are the number of slots, and the average number of future cargoes. The ﬁrst parameter
increases the number of combination to load the vehicles, while the second increases signiﬁcantly
the number of variable because it increases the complexity of the second-stage of the problem, which
is more complex to solve. The large majority of the instances have converged to a near optimal
solution within 1 hour. The main obstacle with this ALNS is that the repair function is an exact
method and not a heuristic method, so it can become hard to use it for bigger instances, even if it
solves an easier problem than the original problem.
In 2011, Bernt Olav Øvstebø, Lars Magnus Hvattum, and Kjetil Fagerholt [16] have tried to solve
with a time limit of 10%. Their algorithm was able to ﬁnd some optimal solution some instances,
with a gap in % oscillating most of the time between 20 % and for 40 %. The ship size varied
between 250 and 10,000 CEU. They have also noticed a positive eﬀect of the ship size, which means
increasing the size increases the solving time. However, it was possible to reach the optimal solution
for all the instances with a ship size of 7000 CEU. Obviously, they have also observed that adding
the constraints of stability increases signiﬁcantly the diﬃculty to ﬁnd feasible solutions for the
problem, and the majority of the instances remained unsolved by the heuristic. This is also due

better for practicality reasons, and in a real case, planners will try to respect this constraint most
of the time.
Romanas Puisa [18] has also noticed that the number of slots in the ship increases the solving time of
the instances. Indeed, the problem size is driven by the number of binary variables and constraints.
In his works, it means the complexity of the problem is driven by the number of cargo categories,
the number of decks, or the number of cells in grids. Romanas Puisa model could be solved in 20
minutes in 50% of the cases, and within 1 hours for 75% of the instances.
8.5
The behavior of the destructors
It is also in the interest of the program to evaluate the performances of the destructor methods.
Because of the randomness at diﬀerent step of the problem (the choice of the destructor method, the
choice of the cargoes to removed, the problem itself), and the diﬀerent factors such as the number of
slots or the weights of the cargoes which can vary a lot between 2 instances, this is something quite
challenging. The performances of a particular method are also depending on what have been done
previously, and not only on the destructor method itself. Therefore, evaluating the performances
of a destructor is not something that must be done in order to have an accurate measure of its
performances, but rather to have a relative comparison with the other methods.
Two methods are used to evaluate the performances of the destructor methods:
• Analysing the evolution of the scores of the diﬀerent methods through the iterations. However,
the program need to be run several times because of the randomness in order to have a relevant
conclusion. Moreover, this should also be done for diﬀerent size of instances, because the
results could vary between a small instance and a more complicated instances.
• The algorithm will also be executed with a reduced set of destructor methods. Each time,
one method will be deleted and the solution obtained will be compared with what has been
obtained with the full set of methods.
Regarding the evolution of the scores of the methods when they are all present, it doesn’t seem that
one method takes clearly the advantage on the other destructor methods. Indeed, as it can be seen
in ﬁgure 13, no clear conclusions can be made because the methods alternatively ﬁnish at the ﬁrst
position. Their evolution depends on the randomness in the problem and in the algorithm. Indeed,
they might be more eﬃcient with certain instances, but also, the order in which they are drawn will
also be important because at the beginning it is much easier to improve the solution than when it
gets closer to the optimal solution.
One trend that can be seen is that when a destructor method gains a strong advantage early on,
that advantage seems to diminish over time, and its score tends to approach the score of other the
methods. This is because, as previously said, it becomes harder to improve the solution. Because
this method has a higher score, its probablity to be drawn is higher, but the probability to improve
the solution is decreasing over time, and so this method is more likely to obtain a bad score and to
see its probability decreasing. This is particularly obvious with the ”most expansive” method and is
quite easily explained. Indeed, this method focus on priority on the cargoes located on the furthest
slot from the entrance of the ship. However, after some iterations, when some of the most expansive
36

slots have already been emptied, it reaches a point where these cargoes cannot be removed because
they are essential to maintain the stability of the ship.

Expected value of perfect information
The expected value of perfect information can be interpreted as the maximal amount the planner
would be willing to pay to get a priori information [8].
By deﬁnition, this value is always negative (it’s a problem of minimization). Indeed, the WS is the
mean of the optimal value in each scenario, while WS. It means that for each scenario, the solution
obtained when computing WS is at least as good that the solution obtained when computing RP.
A value close to 0 is better because it is closer to a wait-and-see approach, which uses perfect
information.
However, the metrics computed here to test the quality of the model will be slightly diﬀerent,
because of the constraints of stability. Indeed, the correct method is to compare the value of the
stochastic model with the mean of the objective values using the wait-and-see approach, but here
the problem is diﬀerent. When applying the wait-and-see approach: given a set of scenario and
an instance of the problem, each scenario is solved individually. This gives a list of deterministic
problem, and the mean of the objective value is computed. This mean is WS. It also means that
for each problem, the constraints of stability should be valid.
But when solving the stochastic problem, only one value for x and y are computed. It means that if
one scenario doesn’t respect the constraints of stability individually, it can be balanced by another
scenario. This approach is discussed in the conclusion. But this modelling choice makes that the
constraints of the stochastic problem are easier than the constraints of the wait-and-see approach,
and so, the 2 models cannot be compared properly.
To solve this problem, a little change is applied. The stochastic problem is solved and the ﬁrst stage
of the problem is blocked for the future. Then, started from this ﬁrst stage that cannot be edited
anymore, the same scenarios are solved individually. It also becomes a list of deterministic problem,
41
==PAGE:43==
but the ﬁrst stage has been obtained using the stochastic model. The mean of these objective values
is called RP. In this way, the two problems are the same in terms of constraints, while before, the
stochastic model was more ﬂexible.
Finally, the expected value of perfect information is computed with: EV PI = WS −RP.
EVPI should be computed with the optimal value, and so by using a mathematical solver and not
a metaheuristic algorithm. It is a measure to check the quality of the model. So EVPI has been
computed for some small instances only. The results can be found in table 12.

9
Conclusion
The last chapter of this thesis provides a summary of all the work, and give some ideas on future
research possibilites.
9.1
Summary
The RORO stowage planning problem is a crucial point in the supply chain of maritime transports.
Improving the positioning of the cargoes to reduce the amount of shifts, optimizing the space to
increase the stability or to be able to load more goods on-board, in order to reduce the weight
or increase the proﬁt, are some of the numerous aspects which will have their importance when
drawing up the economic and ecological balance sheet of a journey.
With global economic development, the number of goods transported through the world has in-
creased every year, and the digitalization of the processes become a major aspect if we want to
continue to meet the growing demand of the population. The VRP or the SSPP problem have at-
tracted more attention these last years. However, while the classic container carriers have attracted
almost all the attention, the RORO stowage planning problem is still a little behind in terms of
research content.
After describing what were the main issues of this problem, the mathematical model has been
given. It is a simpliﬁed version of the models which already exist because some constraints have
been removed, but these are the constraints that should not be important regarding the main change
in this model: the adding of a stochastic variable. Until now, this problem was only treated as fully
deterministic, but this is not the case here since it is assumed that the number and the weights of
the vehicles or cargoes that will be carried are not known in advance.
The model allows to highlight the complexity generated by the random variables. The objective
was originally to only ﬁnd a solution that could satisfy as much as possible the stability constraint,
by trying to put the center of gravity in the center of the ship. But ﬁnally, the notion of time
arrived, represented by the distance to move the vehicles from the decks to the slots on board, to
add another element in this model. By trying to reduce the time to load the ship, the objective is
to try to minimize the amount of time that the ship will spend in the port, which will have both
economical and ecological impact.
While several ways could be found to destroy the solution, the diﬃculty to satisfy the stability
constraints when repairing the solution makes extremely diﬃcult the creation of a heuristic repair
method. That is why it has been preferred to work with an exact repair method, which had the
advantage to be unique because compatible with all the destructor methods.
Regarding the destructor methods, the accent was put on how to destroy the solution in an eﬃcient
way that will create an improvement when repairing this solution. Each destructor had a speciﬁc
role. The method destroying in priority the cargoes located in expansive slots for the objective
function aims directly at reducing the cost of the solution, while the method destroying randomly
the solution is more to create some perturbations and avoid the local minimum. Finally, the method
destroying some groups of cargoes seemed to be the most eﬃcient method after a certain amount
of iterations, providing more ﬂexibility to the repair method to build a new solution.
The addition of the penalty cost in the objective function allowed to work with solutions that
didn’t respect the stability constraints and improved signiﬁcantly the algorithm. Before, the repair
methods required to start with a valid solution because of the use of a MIP solver. Indeed, it might
43
==PAGE:45==
be impossible to ﬁnd a valid solution starting from partially destroyed solutions which was not valid
before being destroyed. Because of that, the initial solution was created by a greedy process which
takes a long time, and as said previously, the initial solution is supposed to be made as fast as
possible. The other method was to start from a random solution and to improve it with a local
search algorithm. It was still too long, but after some improvements, it was possible to obtain an
initial valid solution quite quickly. However, this is still less advantageous than to add the penalty
in the cost.
The main improvement of the model is the addition of a stochastic variable to describe the amount
of cargoes, and their weights. The diﬀerent scenarios have been generated by using a Monte Carlo
process, and then, the set of scenario has been reduced with a k-medoid algorithm. Otherwise, the
full set of scenario would have made the problem too complex, even for a meta-heuristic solver.
The experiments show some encouraging results. The heuristic algorithm seems to perform in a good
way. As it was observed, for small instances, it doesn’t take a long time to obtain a solution near
optimal. For bigger instances, it has been possible to obtain some results whereas the MIP solver
could not give a solution, even after more than one hour of computation. The was the scenarios have
been generated corresponded to the expectations and doesn’t seem to create any bias. In-sample
test and the distribution of the objective values when testing the ALNS with diﬀerent scenarios
gives also good results.
9.2
Future research
In this last section, some ideas are given in order to improve the model.
Harder constraints of stability
The problem has been deﬁned in a way that the ”global” center of gravity should be located in the
center area. This center of gravity is computed as a sum of products between the weight of a loaded
vehicle and its distance from the center of the ship. Regarding the future vehicles: because their
weight is not known in advance, their impact on the center of gravity is also subject to randomness.
So the impact on the center of gravity of the future cargo cf in scenario ω is also multiplied by
the probability of this scenario. This approach makes that individually, the scenario don’t have to
respect the constraints of stability, because they can balance each other.
This approach has been chosen because of the nature of the stochastic variables.
As said, the
stochastic process is continuous, so the probability of one of the scenarios used for the calculations
really happening is 0. Because of that, it didn’t seem relevant to force each scenario to respect the
constraints of stability individually. However, evaluating the impact adding this constraint could
be interesting. The solving time might be longer, but it could be beneﬁcial for the robustness of
the model.
Test the eﬀect of unexpected scenarios
Another aspect that could not be studied in time is the ability of the solution to adapt to unepexcted
scenario after solving the ﬁrst stage.
The model has been evaluated on its ability to adapt to
new scenarios, but the new scenarios set has been created with the same method, using the same
distribution as the distribution used to generate the reduced set of scenario used to solve the
stochastic problem, and to obtain the ﬁrst stage.
44
==PAGE:46==
It could be interesting to obtain the ﬁrst stage by using the ALNS for an instance with 600 present
cargoes, and around 100 future cargoes for 1200 slots, and then, to analyse the objective functions
for scenarios generated with an average amount of 400 future cargoes. These objective values are
compared to the value obtained when the ﬁrst stage has been obtained by solving the ALNS directly
with the right amount of future cargoes: 400.
Several levels
Nowadays, the biggest RORO vessels contain more than 1 levels to carry all the goods. This allow of
course to carry more cargoes, but will also aﬀect the stability component. For the sake of simplicity,
the ship has been reduced to only 1 level, but it could be interesting to increase the complexity of
the problem and see an eventual impact on the solution.
A new grid
When working with a regular grid, the assumption that all the goods will have the same shape is
made. Of course, because of the nature itself of the goods carried by a RORO vessel (cars, buses,
trucks, etc...), this is not true. Thereby, either the slot deﬁned by a regular rectangular is too
small for some goods, and this will create some problems when loading the vessel with an infeasible
stowage plan because of the collisions. Or the slots are too big, and thus results in a lost of space
on board, and so less cargoes will be carried which leads in an economic loss for the freighter.
It could be interesting to create diﬀerent grids for each type of vehicle, and to deﬁne with some
constraints how a truck could eventually block a car and avoid it to be loaded in a speciﬁc slots.
Puisa Romanas already proposed some interesting ideas for the diﬀerent grids [18].
Optional cargoes
In the current problem, all the cargoes, present and future, needed to be carried to the next port.
In some situations, a freighter could have a stock of cargoes that need to be transported, and could
have to chose which cargoes will be carried, and which cargoes will stay in the inventory and wait
longer for another journey. This will have an economic impact, by increasing the holding cost and
potentially delaying the income due to the fact that the cargoes have not been transported yet.
But due to some long-term contracts, some cargoes could be mandatory to carry. In addition of
having 2 categories (present and future) for the cargoes, it could be possible to add new categories
such as ”mandatory” and ”optional”. Eventually, it also might be an option to consider the proﬁt
in the objective function, either by taking into account only the monetary aspect, or by adding the
proﬁt component to the objective function currently existing (with a negative sign if the problem
is still to minimize the objective, because increasing the proﬁt should be considered as a positive
thing).
Speciﬁc requirement
In addition of the categories ”optional” and ”mandatory” proposed in the previous section, it could
also be interesting to consider more categories, regarding some requirements for the cargoes. Indeed,
some of them could be refrigerated cargoes, or could contain dangerous substances. Thus result in
the fact that the cargoes could either require to be loaded in some speciﬁc slots (with an access to
electricity for instance), or on the other hand, these cargoes should avoid certain slots for safety
45
==PAGE:47==
reasons. This has not been taken into account for this thesis because the change should not have
a signiﬁcant impact on the most interesting aspect that is analysed here (the stochastic variable),
but could make the problem more realistic.
Obstacles
In this problem, the only constraints created to avoid collisions was the constraint to avoid that 2
cargoes could be loaded on the same slot in the same scenario. This model could be extended by
adding two other constraints:
• First, some obstacles inherent to the ship could change the aspect of the grid. In reality, the
grid would be a perfect rectangular, due the the shape of the vessel. But also, inside the grid,
some slots could be obstructed by some infrastructures, such as pillar in the lower decks, but
also ramps inside to access the diﬀerent decks. Finally, because of some safety reasons, it
might be dangerous to use certain slots.
• Then, the cargoes itself could be obstacle. In this problem, the journey was only composed
by 2 ports: one to start from, and one port of arrival. Thus, this constraint didn’t make sense
because all the cargoes were loaded and unloaded at both steps of the journey. But a journey
could be composed by 3 or more ports, and so some cargoes could stay on board. However,
they could be annoying, because they could block the access of some slots and so avoid some
cargoes to be loaded/unloaded. Some shifts would be required which results in a longer time
in the port, and so in an increase of the cost and CO2 emissions as previously said. This
number of shifts is also something that could be minimized, and again, could be added in the
objective function, or computed independently.
Other constraints
Finally, other constraints could be added in order to make the model more realistic. For instance,
for safety reasons, a maximum weight could be attributed for some area, like a full level or even a
smaller section, which will again add more complexity to make the stowage plan. Another aspect
that has not been taken into account is that the commands are composed by several vehicles or
cargoes, and it will be better to have all the elements of a command gathered in a same place.
Indeed, a customer often want to transport more than a single car from a port to another, and for
practical reasons, it is better to have all the vehicles or cargoes of a contract grouped on board.
This is not the case in this problem because all the cargoes are considered independent. This will
results in a less optimal solution from a mathematical point of view, but a better solution from a
practical point of view.
