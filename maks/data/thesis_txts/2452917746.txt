Summary
The sensitivity of an externally polarized condenser microphone depends on many pa-
rameters. One of them is the static deflection profile of the membrane due to the elec-
trostatic force of attraction from the backplate. Having some static deflection is good in
a way that the membrane is now closer to the backplate and thus helping to boost the
sensitivity. But this means a decrease in the equilibrium air gap and as a result viscous
damping at high frequencies increases. This not only reduces the sensitivity at high fre-
quencies but also effects the resonance frequency. At high Sound Pressure Levels (SPL),
the membrane gets much closer to the backplate because of an initial deflection. This
can distort the output signal due to the nonuniform deflection of the membrane. Hence
it is important to model the static membrane deflection before the dynamic deflection
is studied.
Analytical models are always reliable and provide a good understanding of the operation
principle. Before the numerical models of the membrane are presented, a study of the
nonlinear terms which are omitted to arrive at the lumped parameter model of the
microphone is made using State Space Models via MATLAB. Results from the State Space
Models are validated using the transfer function simulations and important conclusions
are made.
Very few papers presented a detailed study of the static deflection profile of the mem-
brane. In this thesis, the static deflection profile is studied in detail for a homogeneous
backplate and perforated backplate using Finite Element Method (FEM) via MATLAB.
Back volume is not studied as it does not have any effect on the static deflection profile.
Theory for the 1D axisymmetric analytical and FEM models is derived. Developed FEM
models are validated using analytical and COMSOL models. Static deflection profiles for
different patterns of perforations is studied and comments are made about the possible
effects on the dynamic vibration.
The frequency response for a simple case of a homogeneous backplate without a back
volume is studied. The FEM model is developed without modeling the viscous damping
while in COMSOL it is included. A comparison between FEM and COMSOL models
showed the importance of modeling the viscous damping due to the air gap. Important
conclusions are made possible future steps for the project are laid out.

Preface
This Master thesis was prepared at the Department of Acoustic Technology at the Techni-
cal University of Denmark, as part of the requirements for acquiring a Master of Science
degree in Engineering Acoustics. This thesis was conducted for 6 months starting from
the 28th of January 2019 and ended on the 28th of July 2019. This is equivalent to 35
ECTS.
The topic was originally proposed by Toni Torras Rosell from DPA Microphones. After
listening to the presentation from Toni Torras Rosell and the motivation from a course
on Numerical modeling at DTU, I have decided to actively pursue this topic for my
master thesis.
In the beginning, I have made a few literature studies to get an understanding of numer-
ical modeling. A 3-week course on the Nonlinear modeling using State Space Models and
some interesting discussions with Alexander Weider King, Vicente Cutanda Henriquez,
and Finn T. Agerkvist motivated my work on the State Space Models for Condenser
Microphones.
Upon looking for relevant literature regarding State Space Models for
Condenser Microphones, none are found. So this may be the first attempt made on the
same. Nevetheless, there could be other attempts before this.
Later moved on to numerical models to study the static membrane deflection. I would
like to thank Vicente Cutanda Henriquez for his support through weekly meetings and
the motivation for the rest of the thesis. I would also like to thank Toni Torras Rosell
for his support through Email and occasional meetings at DTU. I and Bibhas Nayak
discussed few results as the topics are similar to some extent and so I would also like to
thank him for the discussions.
Kongens Lyngby, July 28, 2019
Veera Venkata Vamsy Krishna Maddipati

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
For decades, condenser microphones served a great purpose in measurement systems
where a high degree of accuracy is needed.
We are now in an era where condenser
microphones have also become a major part of Audio microphones and are replacing
Dynamic microphones for various reasons. Some of them are better sensitivity, better
transient response; responsiveness to an instantaneous change in the sound, compact
sizes and being able to handle high SPLs. For Audio microphones, a case of High SPL
can be a person singing loud with the mic very close to the mouth or a mic placed very
close to a Bass drum.
A typical condenser microphone is constructed using two parallel plates forming the two
plates of a capacitor. The plate exposed to external sound pressure is known as the
membrane which vibrates upon an incident sound pressure while the other plate known
as backplate remains fixed.
When the membrane is displaced by an incident sound
pressure, the distance between the membrane and backplate changes thus changing the
capacitance. This change in capacitance creates a voltage drop across the condenser
which is the output voltage.
Condenser microphones are generally classified as Prepolarized condenser microphones
and Externally polarized condenser microphones. Prepolarized condenser microphones
do not require an external voltage source and work using a charged electret on the back-
plate. Externally polarized condenser microphones require an external source for their
operation and generally has better sensitivity compared to Prepolarized microphones.
In this thesis only Externally polarized condenser microphones are studied.
At low input SPLs, the output is distortion-free. But when the input sound pressure level
increases, distortion increases. The main source of distortion in a condenser microphone
arises from the nonuniform vibration of the membrane. This is because the electrostatic
attraction force from the backplate is higher when the membrane deflects inward than
when it deflects outwards. This leads to a nonlinear mapping in the output voltage
thus producing harmonic and intermodulation products. Before the dynamic deflection
is studied a precise estimation of static deflection is needed which happens when the
polarization source is switched ON. Since the membrane is clamped to the housing, the
static deflection is not uniform and is maximum at the center. This thesis aims to study
the static deflection of the membrane using 1D and 2D models via FEM for different
geometries.
First, the linear response of the microphone is studied and results are presented. Then
the nonlinearities due to charge leakage are modeled using LTSpice program and State

first. This impulse response is then convolved with the time domain input signal of a
specific frequency to get the output voltage. A FFT of the output voltage is taken which

is carried out using the built-in FFT function in MATLAB. The spectrum for LTSpice

for doing this is that LTSpice uses a nonuniform sampling rate in the time domain and
so calculating the FFT needed advancing FFT techniques.

2.5.2
Stability Study
Before proceeding to implement state space equation 2.24, firstly the sampling frequency
has to be determined to obtain a stable output from the model. At the sampling fre-
quency where the output is stable, the eigenvalues of the matrix I + TsF should be
numerically less than 1. Since the size of the matrix is 3 × 3, the absolute value of
all the three eigenvalues should be numerically less than 1. Upon testing, it has been
observed that the stability of the model is largely dependant on the chosen value of the
load resistance RL first and then the chosen sampling frequency.
Under Figure 2.15, the stability analysis is plotted for different values of load resistances.
The values are plotted in log10 scale and so the stable sampling frequencies start where
the value is less than zero. As can be seen, for a load resistance of 100 Ω, the output
is unstable even at the highest sampling frequency used for the analysis (10000000 Hz)
which is very high. Starting from a load resistance of 1MΩ, the stability pattern seems
to be constant. So, this value of load resistance (1MΩ) can be used as a starting value
for RL even though it is not a very realistic value for the input impedance of preamplifier
which is typical of the order of GΩ[2]. The sampling frequency at this load resistance
is 231400Hz, approximately 10 times the resonance frequency of the microphone which
is generally a rule of thumb. From the second subplot, it can be seen that as the load
resistance increases, the stability pattern does not change. This means that higher values
of load resistances do not affect the stability of the model.

the membrane. This nonuniform displacement is due to the stronger electrostatic force
from the backplate when the membrane displaces inwards for high input levels. This
leads to a nonlinear mapping in the output voltage generating harmonics. The lumped
parameter model assumes the membrane as a flat piston but in reality the membrane
deflection follows a parabolic shape. Since the gap width between membrane and back-
plate is very small, a precise estimation of the deflection and other fluid properties is
needed. This can be achieved via numerical models. Also the damping due to air gap
is modeled as a simple resistor but practically the damping depends of the fluid flow
behind the membrane for which properties such as viscosity and thermal conductivity
need to be considered due to the small gap. A precise calculation of these parameters is
possible via numerical models.

CHAPTER 3
Numerical Models
The microphone construction involves a thin movable membrane clamped at the edges
and a backplate that is fixed rigidly to the housing and is generally few µm away from
the membrane.
This wholes structure forms the two plates of a capacitor which is
charged using an external polarization source through a high resistance. Upon switch-
ing ON the polarization source, an electric field is created between the membrane and
backplate which induces charges of opposite polarity on the membrane. These unlike
charges attract each other thus deflecting the membrane towards the backplate. This is
known as the static deflection of the membrane when there is no external input pressure.
This static deflection largely depends on the polarization voltage level, static tension,
the radius of the membrane, backplate structure and also on the air gap between the
membrane and backplate.
The presence of static deflection reduces the distance between the plates of the capacitor
thus changing the capacitance. Since the backplate is charged through high polarization
resistance, the charge can be assumed constant. Now a constant charge and varying
capacitance cause the voltage drop to change. This means that the static voltage drop
across the capacitor needs to be determined precisely to determine the change in voltage
drop during the dynamic motion and so it is very important to estimate the static
deflection of the membrane first. The shape of the deflection can often be regarded as
parabolic but can deviate depending on the microphone parameters.
Very few papers are available with the main focus on studying the static deflection
profile of the membrane. The initial study dates back to 1972 by J.E Warren and team
under [13]. Nevertheless, there could be other studies conducted before this date. In this
paper, the author simulated the static deflection numerically and later validated the same
using an experimental setup. A good agreement between measurements and simulations
is found. This is however made for an electret microphone. The charge on the electret
remains at the same position where it is placed initially while in an externally polarized
condenser microphone, the charge is free to move and so the modeling of the electrostatic
force is not the same. Analytical solutions for the static membrane deflection profile are
given by Allen J.Zuckerwar under [14] for the cases of equal and unequal backplate
radius with respect to the backplate. Many authors conducted a numerical study on the
dynamic response. Allen J.Zuckerwar under [15] conducted a frequency response study
wherein the lumped parameters are calculated depending on the geometry and material
properties. Good agreement between measured and simulated responses is found. Under
[11], DH Robey conducted a pure numerical study.

deflection becomes stable and does not change anymore. This algorithm is described
under algorithm 1.

Parametric Variation of Membrane Deflection
In the previous sections, the membrane deflection with varying complexity of the geom-
etry has been studied. In this section, the membrane deflection is studied by changing
the polarization voltage E, air gap width d and membrane static tension T.
Under Figure 3.12, the deflection patterns for different polarization voltages, air gap
widths and Tensions are plotted. As the polarization voltage decreases, the electrostatic
force of attraction decreases and so the static deflection of membrane decreases. This
phenomenon can be observed in the first subplot. For the same E and T, as the air gap
width increases, the electrostatic force of attraction decreases and so the static deflection
of membrane decreases. This phenomenon can be observed in the second subplot. For
the same E and d, as the membrane tension decreases, the pulling force due to the electric
field is opposed less by the tension and so the deflection increases. This phenomenon
can be observed in the third subplot.
Decreasing polarization voltage also means decreasing sensitivity, so it cannot be changed
just to achieve a specific static deflection. Increasing the air gap also means decreasing
sensitivity, and decreasing the damping by the air gap to membrane movement under the
influence of an external input pressure. Reduction in the damping can lead to an under
damped response at the high frequencies. Decreasing tension means larger membrane
deflection and choosing a very low tension can lead to membrane collapsing on to the
backplate. Hence the value of tension needs to be carefully chosen. As discussed, one
cannot vary a single parameter to achieve a specific membrane deflection. It would have
adverse effects in the dynamic response.
Having a larger membrane deflection is beneficial in way that the sensitivity will be
better since the membrane is closer to the backplate. But this means also the membrane
deflection under the influence of high external pressures will be asymmetric since the
deflection is more towards inside than outside due to stronger electrostatic force. This
nonuniform membrane deflection can lead to a nonlinear mapping in the output voltage
thus creating distortion. One could expect all harmonics of the fundamental due to the
asymmetric distortion.

3.4.2.3
Patterns of Backplate Holes
In this section different patterns of holes on the backplate and the corresponding effect
on the membrane deflection will be studied. Generally speaking, the damping for the
membrane at high frequencies is provided by the air gap between the membrane and
backplate. This air gap not only determines damping but also the sensitivity of the
microphone in terms of length. With a larger air gap, the sensitivity is less and also
the damping. While on the other hand a smaller air gap provides better sensitivity and
damping. The purpose of holes on the backplate is to control this damping. Having
holes on the backplate reduces damping as the fluid can now easily escape through these
holes to the back cavity.
The radius and position of the holes play an important role in the amount of damping
known as damping factor. The smaller the radius of the holes the better the damping
due to air gap as it will be harder to move the air through a narrow vent compared
to a broad one. When acoustic wave moves through narrow slits, viscous and thermal

thermal losses can occur at the boundary layers[4].

3.4.3
Dynamic Deflection
Now that the static deflection of the membrane is studied in detail, importance of cal-
culating this static deflection is validated by doing a simple frequency response study.
For a dynamic study, in addition to the membrane vibration, other complex acoustic
phenomena described below also need to be simulated to obtain a precise response.
• As the air gap between the membrane and backplate provides viscous damping
and stiffness, it is important to study and simulate this effect.
• At low frequencies, the air behind the membrane has sufficient time to escape
laterally through the slit gap in to the back chamber.
Hence no compression
occurs and the fluid only offers damping. If the back chamber volume is less, the
low frequency response can be damped as the compliance decreases. Hence it is
also important to simulate the compliance of the back cavity.
==PAGE:80==
64
3 Numerical Models
• At high frequencies, the air behind the membrane does not have sufficient time to
escape laterally through the slit gap in to the back chamber and hence compresses
over the backplate. Hence it offers both damping and stiffness.
• In a real fluid, the acoustic wave motion is an adiabatic process meaning the energy
of the sound wave is only used in vibrating the membrane and no energy is lost
in the surroundings[6]. But when acoustic wave moves in small gaps, viscous and
thermal losses occur at the boundary layer. Hence they need to be considered.
• The damping effect is controlled by holes in the backplate which also need to be
simulated to model the damping precisely.
Including and studying all the effects via a numerical model can be complex in terms
of modeling mathematically and can take heavy computational resources. Hence it is
recommended to include each phenomena in a phase wise manner as the static deflection
analysis is carried out. Due to time limitation of this thesis, a simple 1D axisymmetric
model with homogeneous backplate is simulated. The modeling of the excess pressure
generated behind the membrane is not considered. This assumption is valid for low fre-
quencies where damping has very little effect as observed in the linear model simulations.
The frequency response calculated using the developed FEM 1D model is validated using
the response obtained from COMSOL.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
The results for transient analysis obtained from LTSpice proved to be not valid when
compared to the transfer function simulation. LTSpice adds inherent distortion and this
source of distortion is not known. Later the State Space Models developed proved to be
in good agreement with the transfer simulation without any distortion in the linear range.
State Space Models simulating the nonlinearties due to charge leakage showed that the
nonlinearities cannot be neglected for low frequencies. However, these nonlinearities can
be ignored if the preamplifier has very high input impedance.
1D and 2D FEM models are validated against the analytical and COMSOL models. Very
strong match between the models is found. Static deflection of the membranes for various
microphone parameters are studied and comments regarding their effect on the dynamic
behaviour has been made. A simple frequency response study of the microphone proved
the importance of a precise estimation of the membrane deflection to accurately model
the sensitivity. It was also noticed that the modeling of viscous damping due to the fluid
behind the membrane is very important when the air gap is small to obtain a precise
response.

CHAPTER 5
Possible Extensions
Since the static deflection of the membrane is studied in detail and the frequency response
study proved the need of modeling the damping behind the membrane, a possible list of
the project extensions is laid out below,
• The State Space Model can be modified to include the effect of electrostatic force
causing the nonuniform displacement of the membrane at high input levels. Once
this is modelled, the effect of stray capacitances due to the housing and holes can
be added to further simulate the nonlinearities[5].
• A measurement could be designed to validate the static deflection of the membrane.
Since the membrane deflections are very small, microphone with larger membrane
radius needs to be chosen. Also, the accuracy of the measurement can largely
depend on the choice of the measurement device.
• Modeling the viscous damping is a complex phenomena involving Navier Stokes
Equation which describes the motion of a fluid. But under certain assumptions
such as when the lateral dimensions of the membrane are larger compared to the
air gap, Reynolds Equation can be used which is a simplified form of Navier Stokes
Equation. One can refer to [9] and [10] for an initial motivation.
• Once the viscous damping is modeled, a frequency response study can be made
and the response can be validated using COMSOL or other industry standard
application.
• Now the holes on the backplate can be added. Since an axisymmetric cannot be
assumed anymore, one has to be careful with the mesh implementation as the
model can be computationally heavy. Theory and equations for this can also be
found under [10]. Again the frequency response can be validated against COMSOL
to check the model developed.
• Now lastly, a back cavity is added to complete the construction of a practical
microphone. This can be modelled using a real microphone such as B&K 4133 and
can be validated against the measured response to check the model validity.
• Since boundary layers are created at the walls due to the flow of fluid in a small
structure, viscous and thermal losses are also important. One can refer to [6] for
further motivation on this.
