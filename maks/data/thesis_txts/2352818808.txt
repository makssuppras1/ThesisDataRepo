Preface
Originating from a general interest in human conversational interaction, I carried out
a project on "Perception of Gaps and Overlaps in Conversational Turn-Taking" as my
ﬁnal project in my Bachelor of Engineering degree in 2014. In that project, I measured
categorical thresholds for gaps and overlaps in conversational turn-taking interactions
in quiet and noise for normal hearing and hearing impaired listeners.
The topic has
never left my interest since, and the master thesis was an obvious opportunity for me
to continue my investigation of the human conversational system, which is still not fully
understood by researchers. As opposed to the perceptual aspect of conversational turn-
takings, the following project aimed at investigating the production side of it. In particular

inﬂuence conversational dynamics. With assistance from my supervisors at the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU), Associate Professor Ewen MacDonald and Postdoctoral
Fellow Michal Fereczkowski, the project was ﬁnalized in its details. The work was carried

Abstract
Previous studies of conversational dynamics have shown that the latency from conceptual
preparation of a response to the articulatory output takes at least 600 ms, while transitions
between talkers (termed ﬂoor-transfer oﬀsets, FTO) are most often on the order of 200-
400 ms.
This suggests some overlap between comprehension and production in which
interlocutors are predicting the timing of the end of their partner’s turn and initiate
their response before this point. We hypothesised that speaking in a second language
(L2) and/or in background noise would introduce a delay in turn-taking due to increased
cognitive demands that would disrupt the participants’ ability to predict when to take a
turn.
A conversation corpus was made between 20 native-Danish friendly pairs of talkers in
Danish (ﬁrst language, L1) and English (second language, L2), in quiet and in noise (ICRA
7 noise presented at 70 dBA) using a spot-the-diﬀerence task called the "Diapix task".
FTO distributions were computed using a voice activity detection algorithm determining
boundaries in speech streams, and a ﬁnite state machine classifying these speech streams
into categories of silence, overlaps and inter-pausal units (i.e., utterances of continuous
speech).
The time it took the participants to complete the Diapix task increased signiﬁcantly in
both L2 and noise suggesting that both factors reduced communication eﬃciency. How-
ever, L2 had very little eﬀect beyond the completion time, likely because the participants
were very ﬂuent in English. In contrast to our predictions, in the presence of noise, the
median FTO decreased by approximately 30 ms in L1 (10 %) and 50 ms in L2 (16 %),
and the standard deviation in both languages decreased by 12 %. This was interpreted
as a change in the operating point towards more rapid conversation in noise. The rapid
conversation was attributed to the participants being stressed, and it was further sup-
ported by increased articulation rate, increased rate of overlaps per minute, and reduced
rate of pauses per minute. An increase in the average duration of inter-pausal units of
about 40 % in noise suggested participants held their turn longer, allowing more time for
themselves and their interlocutor to plan their response. This suggests that interlocutors
use strategies to maintain the overall turn-taking structure and that this is important for
maintaining socially acceptable behaviour. This is supported by the shape of the FTO
distributions found in this experiment being highly similar to FTO distributions found in
other studies that have been produced in a variety of diﬀerent conditions and languages.

Introduction
Conversation is an integral part of human social interaction. It requires the ability to
take turns; an ability learned throughout the early years (Clark and Lindsey, 2015) that
is a central part in developing social skills. However, the conversational system and its
dynamics are still not fully understood.
Traditionally, speech comprehension has been investigated through speech intelligibil-
ity tests that involve listening only. But real communication involves interaction between
people and cannot be investigated fully by only considering people’s ability to recognize
and recall spoken words or sentences. To get a full understanding of a person’s ability
to follow a conversation, one must investigate both the production and comprehension
involved in the interaction between two or more conversational partners, termed inter-
locutors. As a step towards building up models of the conversational system in order to
develop more advanced speech perception tests and/or processing schemes in hearing aids,
one must ﬁrst understand the system. This thesis is focused on the part of the system
that is concerned with timing in turn-taking and building up knowledge about the system
in normal-hearing (NH) listeners in adverse environments.
1.1
Classifying Talker Transitions in Conversation
In a landmark paper, Sacks et al. (1974) deﬁned a general, qualitative model of turn-taking.
They classiﬁed transitions between talkers into three categories: overlap, gap and neither-
gap-nor-overlap and presented 14 "grossly apparent facts" about turn-takings. Among
these they noted that "overwhelmingly, one party talks at a time", that "occurrences of
more than one speaker at a time are common, but brief", and lastly, that "transitions (from
one turn to a next) with no gap and no overlap are common. Together with transitions
characterized by slight gap or slight overlap, they make up the vast majority of transitions"
(Sacks et al., 1974, p. 700). Moreover and importantly, they noted that in order for the
system to operate at this speed, there must be a "projection" (i.e. a prediction) of turn-
ends by the listener.
In this thesis, the transition interval between talkers will be referred to as a ﬂoor-
transfer oﬀset (FTO). Transitions that overlap will be referred to as overlaps-between,
whereas those with a gap between them will simply be referred to as gaps. Overlaps-
between have negative FTOs, and gaps have positive FTOs. If the complete utterance
of a talker occurs within the utterance of his/her interlocutor, this is referred to as an
overlap-within.
A pause refers to a pause in one talker’s speech stream that was not
interrupted by the interlocutor. Lastly, an inter-pausal unit (IPU) refers to a sequence
of words surrounded by silence. Such IPUs, however, do not include sequences of words
produced within an interlocutor’s utterance, i.e. overlaps-within. The reason for this is
that they may not be terminated utterances (e.g. failed attempts to take turns), and
thus they are analyzed separately as overlaps-within. The ﬁve categories are illustrated
in Figure 1.
It has been shown in several studies that the latency from conceptual preparation of
a response to the articulatory output takes at least 600 ms, while transitions between
talkers are most often on the order of 200-400 ms (Levinson and Torreira, 2015; Heldner
and Edlund, 2010; Stivers et al., 2009). This suggests some overlap between comprehension

1.2
Previous Approaches to Studying Turn-Taking
There are several ways of investigating conversational dynamics. In a case study, Clark
and Lindsey (2015) investigated how children develop their ability to converse in their
early years. They studied the development of a single child beginning at age 1;4 (1 year,
4 months) to 3;5 through 26 1-1.5h video recordings of interactions with his mom. They
tracked both gestures and verbal responses to questions and found that overall, gestures
were produced faster than any verbal response. When giving both gestural and verbal re-
sponses, gestures were generally produced ahead of words, which indicates that the delay in
the verbal response is caused by the lagging word retrieval. Moreover, the child responded
faster when relying on one or more words repeated from the adult’s question than when he
retrieved and produced speciﬁc words on his own. Hence, they found that word retrieval
and articulation appears to be the bottlenecks in the timing of responses. Casillas et al.
(2015) did a similar investigation where they analyzed conversational question-answer
sequences between caregivers and their children from ages 1;8 to 3;5. They found that
children’s turn timing is aﬀected by the complexity of their responses, hence that plan-
ning of responses is limiting children’s ability to respond in time, although having already
acquired turn-taking skills.
De Ruiter et al. (2006) conducted an experiment in which people pressed a button
in anticipation of turn-ends in real conversations where only the lexico-syntax or into-
national contour was present. They made the strong conclusion that speech including
only lexico-syntactic content was suﬃcient to predict turn-ends, whereas speech with only
intonational contour was not suﬃcient for predicting turn-ends. However, in Levinson
and Torreira (2015) it was outlined that in a study by Casillas and Frank (submitted)
they investigated anticipation of turn-ends by using eye-tracking and found by ﬁltering
conversations between two adults such that they contained prosody only, lexico-syntax
only and both prosody and lexico-syntax, that children under 3 had an early advantage
for prosody in terms of predicting turn-ends, whereas adults used a combination of the
two.
In my bachelor project (unpublished) I determined categorical thresholds for gaps
and overlaps for NH and HI people in quiet and noise by having them listening to turn-
takings between two people in the range -500 to 500 ms and determining whether they
perceived it as a gap, overlap or no-gap-no-overlap. For the NH group, the thresholds

overlapping talk and minimize gaps between interlocutors, as suggested by Sacks et al.

1.3
Hypothesis
We hypothesised that speaking in a second language (L2) and/or in background noise
would introduce a delay in turn-taking due to increased cognitive load. Thus the distri-
butions seen in Figures 2a and 2b would shift to the right. In Section 1.2 it was outlined
that the bottlenecks in speech production for children was articulation and word retrieval.
While a second language may not be directly comparable to children’s language in all as-
pects, it is assumed that the same things will introduce cognitive demand on the speakers.
The articulation of words in L2 is diﬀerent from L1 and thus may be more demanding
both on the motor system and cognitively. Moreover, the word retrieval must be assumed
slower in L2 than L1. These two things would presumably introduce delays in the pro-
duction of speech in L2, and it would further be delayed by the cognitive resources used
for understanding their interlocutor. When speaking in the presence of background noise
it was furthermore assumed that the understanding of their interlocutor would be more
demanding and thereby also slowing down turn-taking, since cognitive resources are used
for ﬁltering out background noise and thus predicting missed words may be more diﬃcult.
In addition, we further hypothesised that the distributions would become broader in
L2 and noise as the cognitive load would disrupt individuals’ ability to predict turn-ends
of their partners and thus make them less precise in when to take their turn.
1.4
The Scope and Methodological Approach of This
Study
In this study it was investigated how communicating in a second language and in back-
ground noise aﬀected conversational dynamics in a manner similar to Levinson and Tor-
reira (2015) and Heldner and Edlund (2010). It was done by recording conversations be-
tween 20 pairs of native Danish talkers in Danish (ﬁrst language, L1) and English (second
language, L2) in quiet and noise. The pairs worked together to solve a spot-the-diﬀerence
task called "the Diapix task" (Van Engen et al., 2010; Baker and Hazan, 2011) in each of
the conditions. Basically, the task was to ﬁnd diﬀerences between similar pictures without
being able to see each other, such that they only had verbal communication as a tool to
==PAGE:13==
1.4. THE SCOPE AND METHODOLOGICAL APPROACH OF THIS STUDY
5
solve the task. In total, the pairs solved 12 pictures, three in each of the four conditions,
resulting in approximately ﬁve hours of recordings per condition.
Durations of gaps, pauses, overlaps-between, overlaps-within and inter-pausal units
were estimated in all conversations, along with the talkers’ articulation rates and average
RMS levels. There are several approaches to investigating timing in turn-taking such as
textual or auditive annotation of segments of speech, but in the work reported in this thesis
computational analysis of turn-taking was used. This means that boundaries in speech
streams and classiﬁcation of the conversations into the ﬁve categories presented above
were found computationally using voice activity detection and a ﬁnite state machine.
Annotating manually may be more accurate, however it is a labour intensive process,
which imposes practical limits on the corpus size. By using computational segmentations
and classiﬁcations, a much larger corpus can be analysed. As the results obtained from
a larger corpus that includes a larger number of talkers are more likely to generalize, we
felt this approach was justiﬁed.

2. DESIGN OF CONVERSATIONAL EXPERIMENT
2
Design of Conversational Experiment
The following chapter describes the conceptual considerations and design of the experiment
in which dialogue was produced in Danish (L1) and English (L2), both in quiet and noise
to investigate noise and second language’s eﬀects on conversational dynamics.
2.1
Task To Elicit Natural Dialogue
To investigate the eﬀects of noise and second language on conversational dynamics, dia-
logue had to be produced in the diﬀerent conditions. We wanted the dialogue to be as
natural as possible, and we wanted as much dialogue as possible to have enough data to
investigate the eﬀects of the conditions. For this to be obtained, the participants had
to be motivated to produce dialogue. It was therefore chosen to motivate the partici-
pants on two levels: (1) they were to solve a task together such that their communication
achieved an external goal in completing the task; and (2) they were recruited in friendly
pairs such that they were socially motivated to participate and communicate with their
partners (Brady (1968), De Ruiter et al. (2006) and other unpublished studies have also
used friendly pairs for generating natural dialogue). A further advantage of recruiting
them in friendly pairs is that their conversational dynamics were already well-established
before the experiment and thus it was assumed they would remain constant within the
conditions during the experiment. A key advantage of having the participants solving a
task is that their communication eﬃciency can be quantiﬁed by measuring the completion
time of the task. This allows us to quantify the impacts of noise and second language on
information transfer eﬃciency, which is one of the fundamental aspects of communication.
There are diﬀerent tasks that can be solved to elicit communication. Anderson et al.
(1991) designed "The Map Task" to produce spontaneous speech. In the task each of two
participants are given a schematic map, and they are to collaborate to reproduce the map
route on one of the maps that is already printed on the other. The drawback of using this
for creating dialogue is that the participant with the route printed on his/her map is the
instruction giver, and the other is the instruction follower, which creates imbalance in the
speech contribution between the pairs: the instruction givers were talking 68 % of the time
and the instruction followers were talking 32 % of the time (Anderson et al., 1991, p. 363).
Hence, in using this task there would not be produced a necessary amount of turn-takings.
Inspired by The Map Task, Van Engen et al. (2010) designed the "Diapix task", a more
collaborative task in which two conversational partners are each given one of two similar
pictures and have to work together to ﬁnd diﬀerences between them. With items missing
or altered between pictures they are designed such that both participants have to actively
take part in the conversation. Baker and Hazan (2011) made a new, updated version of
the Diapix task, called the DiapixUK, as they extended the set of picture materials and
found an equal diﬃculty across the 12 picture pairs, no learning eﬀect after completing
the task, and a balanced contribution from both speakers (51 % vs 49 %).
The latter task was used in this experiment, as it was found particularly relevant for
the purpose of eliciting natural dialogue. The 12 pictures consist of three diﬀerent scenes:
four in a beach scene, four in a farm scene and four in a street scene (see Appendix B.2.1,
Figure 23 for examples from each of the scenes).

2.2. SECOND LANGUAGE

2.2
Second Language
As the main second language spoken in Denmark is English, it was obvious to choose
English as the second language in this experiment. According to The Education First (EF)
English Proﬁciency Index (EPI) (http://www.ef.co.uk/epi/) Danes are ranked the second
best non-native English speakers in the world, so it was known that the participants could
be challenged more by a diﬀerent second language. However, recruiting enough talkers
of a diﬀerent language was judged not to be feasible. Having the participants taking a
English proﬁciency test was considered in order to ensure people were similar in their
English proﬁciency, but it was not possible to ﬁnd any tests that would be quick enough
to include in the experiment. Hence, a criteria for inclusion in the experiment was that
they had taken at least one university-level class where the course was taught in English,
and that they self-reported being comfortable in English.
2.3
Background Noise
In a small pilot test conducted by the author and one of the supervisors, it was found
that solving the pictures could take up to 10 minutes. Ideally a multi-talker babble would
have been used as background noise, but it was not available in the desired duration of at
least 10 minutes. Thus, ICRA 7, a mixed gender 6-talker speech shaped noise (Dreschler
et al., 2001) was used in the experiment. The strength of using this masker is that the
participants would not be able to fully exploit talking in the dips of the masker, but would
still be challenged by the ﬂuctuations in it. If they were able to talk in the dips of the
masker and gain higher SNRs from that it could aﬀect the dynamics, and it would be
more diﬃcult to go back and trace the eﬀects (Lecumberri et al., 2010).
2.4
Training
As the participants’ eﬃciency would be measured as the time it took them to ﬁnd 10
diﬀerences between the picture pairs in each conditions, training was done to familiarize
the participants both with the task and the background noise. There was a trade-oﬀin the
choice of training. As the experiment was estimated to take approximately 10 min/picture,
i.e. 120 minutes in total without breaks, the training session had to be limited in order
to not tire the participants. A further limit on the training was that there were only two
pictures available for training, i.e. two picture pairs from the original Diapix corpus (Van
Engen et al., 2010).
For the familiarization with the task it was essential that the participant’s understood
how to ﬁnd diﬀerences between pictures and mark them on their respective pictures.
Hence, the participant’s were asked prior to the experiment to ﬁnd ﬁve diﬀerences while the
instructor was observing them making sure the task was understood. If the participants
had any questions regarding the task they could ask them in this session.
The noise could be perceived as loud and annoying and it was assumed that the
participants would take some time getting adjusted to the noise the ﬁrst time they heard
it. Hence, to familiarize the subjects with the background noise and the experimental
procedure, they had a training session similar to a condition in Danish with background
noise prior to the actual experiment.

2. DESIGN OF CONVERSATIONAL EXPERIMENT
2.5
Communication System
It was chosen to have participants communicating in pairs of two situated in two diﬀerent
soundproof booths with a pair of headphones and a microphone for communicating. This
was done in order to have as clean recordings as possible, which was desired for two
reasons. First, the Hearing Systems Group at the Technical University of Denmark at
which the experiment was conducted needed clean recordings of natural speech for use in
other experiments. Second, by placing them in separate booths there was no crosstalk,
and thus the computational analysis of the recordings was made much easier.
2.6
Procedure Design
Each participant pair had to solve a Diapix task in each of the four conditions constituting
the basic design:
• Background: quiet, language: Danish (L1)
• Background: noise, language: Danish (L1)
• Background: quiet, language: English (L2)
• Background: noise, language: English (L2)
To increase the certainty of the observations for each pair, three replicates of the four
conditions was conducted for each of the pairs. However, there was a need for breaks
during the experiment because of its long duration, and it was found through recordings of
diﬀerent talkers using the system (see Section 4.1) that the placement of the microphone
on the participants inﬂuenced the level of the recorded signal (± 2 dBA). Hence, the
microphone position was considered a nuisance factor, that is a factor that has an eﬀect on
the response, in this case the recorded microphone levels, but is of no interest to measure.
By using a randomized complete block design (RCBD) the inﬂuence of a nuisance factor
can be decreased (Montgomery, 2013). The principle behind the RCBD is that a complete
replicate of the basic design is conducted within a block, and the conditions are randomized
within that block. Each block then represents a speciﬁc level of the nuisance factor, i.e.
a single microphone position, and the same oﬀset will be added to all four conditions in
each of the three replicates for each person.
In each of the three blocks four pictures were assigned to the conditions above with
the restriction that there should be at least one picture from each of the three categories
(beach, street, and farm). The pictures were balanced across all 20 pairs such that every
picture appeared ﬁve times in each condition.

3
Procedure of Conversational Experiment
This chapter describes the procedure of the conversational experiment and the test group
consisting of the conversational pairs.
3.1
Task Procedure
Two subjects participated at a time, constituting a conversation pair. Before starting the
experiment, they received instructions about the experimental procedure. The instructions
can be found in Danish and English in Appendix A. The ﬁrst training session was then
completed where a picture from a Diapix pair from the original Diapix corpus (Van Engen
et al., 2010) was given to each of the participants. They were asked to ﬁnd ﬁve diﬀerences
while the instructor was observing them in order to make sure the task was understood.
This session was conducted outside the booth.
The second training session took place in the booth. Each participant was placed in a
separate booth and equipped with a pair of headphones and a microphone through which
they communicated. They were then asked to solve another Diapix pair from the Van
Engen et al. (2010) corpus by ﬁnding 10 diﬀerences between them. This was done with
background noise played through the headphones. The Diapix used for training can be
found in Appendix B.2.1, Figure 22. Both training sessions were conducted in Danish.
They were then asked to start the experiment and were told to solve a Diapix task in
four diﬀerent conditions: both in Danish and English with and without background noise.
The participants were told that there would be three blocks of the four conditions, making
up a total of 12 picture pairs to solve. They were asked to ﬁnd 10 diﬀerences between
the pictures and were given no more than 15 minutes per picture. After each block the
participants were given the opportunity to take a break. Within a block, instructions
were given by the operator using a AKG DST 99 S microphone outside the booth. The
operator also monitored the conversations via headphones outside the booth to ensure
participants followed the instructions. The experiment took about 2.5 hours in total per
pair.
3.2
Test Group
40 normal-hearing, native-Danish talkers were recruited in pairs, making up 20 pairs in
total. 28 men and 12 women made up the 20 pairs, and the pairs were both of same
and mixed gender. Their mean age was 26 years (σ2 = 3.7 years) and they were mainly
undergraduate students from the Technical University of Denmark. All pairs had taken at
least one university-level class where the course was taught in English, and they all self-
reported being comfortable in English. Before the experiment, a hearing screening was
conducted on the participants to ensure they were normal hearing. A simple audiogram
was conducted, and all participants had hearing threshold levels within the normal-hearing
range of -10 to 20 dB HL between 125 Hz and 8 kHz.

4. SETUP OF CONVERSATIONAL EXPERIMENT
4
Setup of Conversational Experiment
The following chapter describes the choice of noise level, the calibration of the participants’
headphones, the calibration of the talker level corresponding to the level one meter away
from the talker, how the communication system was setup, and how recordings were made.
4.1
Noise Level
As a ﬁrst attempt to choose an appropriate noise level, the average speaking level of a
Danish talker in the booths used for the experiment was found. To the author’s knowledge
there are no standardized ways of doing this, and thus a procedure had to be invented.
To get a good estimate of the average speaking level, talkers had to produce speech for
a period of time long enough to be certain about their average speaking level. As pauses
in their speech streams would decrease their average speaking level, they had to produce
constant speech.
13 Danish talkers sat in the booth talking non-stop for two minutes about their day
with a Nor140 SLM situated 1 m in front of them. The average RMS of the talkers’
speaking level was 54.6 dBA (σ2 = 5.5 dBA). Based on this ﬁnding, a noise level of 70
dBA was chosen in order to have a level well above the average speaking level to make
sure the noise would have an impact on the speech production.
4.2
Calibration of Headphones
First, a B&K microphone type 4149 was calibrated to 94 dB SPL. This was done by
matching the level of a B&K 4230 sound level calibrator with the level recorded by a
B&K 4149 microphone preampliﬁed by a B&K 2619 (hereafter called the headphone cou-
pler) connected to a B&K 2636 sound level meter (SLM). Next, a white noise ﬁltered with
a spectrum based on the Long Term Average Speech Spectrum (LTASS) with an overall
level of 70 dB SPL (Byrne et al., 1994) was played through Sennheiser HD650 headphones
(ampliﬁed by a SPL Phonitor 2 headphone monitoring ampliﬁer) on the headphone cou-
pler. The noise level was read oﬀthe SLM to have a conversion between the electrical
output response of the computer and the SPL.
4.3
Calibration of Talker Level
In order for the setup to sound as natural as possible, the presentation level of speaker 1
in speaker 2’s headphones should be the same as the level measured in a normal speaking
distance (chosen to be 1 m) from speaker 1. To obtain this the calibrated headphones
were placed on the headphone coupler connected to the B&K 2636 SLM. A talker was
equipped with a Shure WH20 microphone, and a Nor140 sound level meter was placed 1
m away from the talker. Both SLMs had A-weighting on them to measure the relevant
frequency area for speech. While producing a prolonged vowel the SPL was read oﬀthe
Nor140 and B&K SLM. In RME Totalmix, the level of the Shure WH20 was adjusted
such that the readings on the B&K SLM matched the levels on the Nor140 SLM. This
was repeated several times for three diﬀerent talkers.

Communication System and Recordings
In each room a B&K 4192 (ampliﬁed by B&K 2692-C conditioning ampliﬁers) was hanging

5. PROCESSING OF RECORDINGS
5
Processing of Recordings
This chapter describes the algorithm implemented to classify the conversation recordings
into the ﬁve categories: pauses, gaps, overlaps-within, overlaps-between and inter-pausal
units (IPUs), along with an outline of how the RMS level of the recordings was converted
to dBA and how articulation rates were computed. The overall framework for the clas-
siﬁcation of the recordings is based on the one presented in Heldner and Edlund (2010)
consisting of three stages:
(1) Voice activity detection
(2) Communicative state classiﬁcation
(3) Silence and overlap classiﬁcation
In this implementation step 2 and 3 were combined into one and realized using a ﬁnite
state machine. All of the processing was done on the close mic recordings.
5.1
Voice Activity Detection
The purpose of the Voice Activity Detection (VAD) is to label the speech recordings in
a binary manner such that ones indicate speech units and zeros indicate silent units. To
do that the following steps were applied to each of the recordings in accordance with the
procedures found in Heldner and Edlund (2010), which are similar to Brady (1966).
First, the waveform was buﬀered into overlapping windows of 5 ms with 1 ms overlap
resulting in a temporal resolution of 4 ms. Second, the squared RMS value in each of the
windows was computed. Lastly, based on a threshold a binary decision was made in each
of the windows whether it was dominated by speech (1) or silence (0). The resulting array
will be referred to as Voice Activity Indication (VAI). Good results were obtained with
this simple procedure as the background noise in the booths was very low (about 25 dBA),
and there was no interfering noise in the background as the participants heard the ICRA
noise in headphones. Both the window size, the amount of overlap of consecutive analysis
windows and the threshold were found on a trial-and-error basis by comparing computed
boundaries and auditive inspection of boundaries in the recordings. Heldner and Edlund
(2010) used overlapping windows of 10 ms, and Brady (1966) used 5 ms analysis windows
without overlap, but a higher precision was desired for detecting boundaries in the speech
material in this experiment.
Following the procedure in Heldner and Edlund (2010), two manipulations on the
binarized speech recordings were made. In order to minimize the risk of mistaking stop
consonants from actual pauses, the ﬁrst manipulation was to bridge gaps smaller than 180
ms. This limit was based on an analysis of 13,000 voiceless stops that showed that 99.2 %
of the stop closures had a duration of less than 180 ms (e.g. Heldner and Megyesi, 2003).
This manipulation resulted in the smallest detectable pause being 180 ms. The second
manipulation was to discard spurts with a duration smaller than 90 ms to minimize the
risk of mistaking noise (like pops and clicks) for speech. The manipulations were made in
the mentioned order as it was found that removing bursts smaller than 90 ms ﬁrst removed
too many stop closures in the end of utterances. On the other hand there were almost
no artefacts like pops and clicks so the risk of bridging a series of those by bridging gaps

5.2
Silence and Overlap Classiﬁcation
The classiﬁcation of the speech portions into pauses, gaps, overlaps-within, overlaps-
between and inter-pausal units was realized using a ﬁnite state machine. The algorithm
worked on a frame-by-frame basis, where the current and previous frames were compared
to detect changes in the VAI array. If applied in parallel with the VAD it could be possible
to classify the conversations on-the-ﬂy. There would be a lag of 6 ms (5 ms window size
and 1 ms overlap) for detecting change in activity between two consecutive frames. There
would furthermore be a lag for bridging silent gaps and for removing noise like pops and
clicks, that would require at least 2 × 180 ms for considering bridging gaps both before
and after bursts of speech smaller than 90 ms, plus 90 ms to detect these potential noise
bursts. The total lag introduced by the VAD and the state machine would then amount
to about 400 ms. The advantage of doing this processing on-the-ﬂy is that it can be used
for ﬁeld recordings for classifying private conversations, where the interlocutors will not
be recorded and therefore can have more free conversation as it is only the dynamics that
are saved.

5.2.1
Inter-Pausal Units Before and After Turn-Takings
To investigate whether there is a connection between the duration of the IPUs before and
after turn-takings these IPUs are saved explicitly. Every time a gap is calculated, the last
terminated IPU is saved as IPUbeforeGap. Equally, an IPUbeforeOverlap is saved
as the last terminated IPU whenever an overlap-between is calculated.
Every time an IPU is calculated when leaving one of the activity states or the overlap
states when a turn-taking is made, a check is made whether the size of the gap array
matches the IPUafterGap array. If not, the last terminated IPU is appended to the array.
Similarly, this is done for the IPUafterOverlap when the size of IPUafterOverlap
does not match the overlap-between array. One thing to keep in mind when saving
IPUafterOverlaps is that if the IPU is terminated within an overlap-between the IPU
has to be saved before calculating the new overlap-between in order to match the sizes of
the arrays.

5.3
Calculation of RMS Level in dBA
The RMS levels of the close mic recordings were converted into dBA estimated at a position
1 meter away from the listener in the following way. The RMS level of the noise played
back in the headphones was known to be 70 dBA. Hence, by computing the arbitrary
RMS value of the noise in dB in Matlab there was found a conversion between actual
dBA level and the level of the recorded signal. To obtain this, a close mic recording of the
speakers in a condition with background noise was chosen, and segments with no speech
on either channel (i.e. only with noise) was found using the VAD algorithm (explained
in Section 5.1). The RMS value of these noise dominant segments was then computed on
the signal that was mixed with noise and sent to the headphones.
Next, the gain between the mixed signal and the close mic recordings was found. This
was done by using the VAD again to ﬁnd segments of speech in the close mic recording
on a recording of a condition without background noise. The RMS level of these portions
was then computed both on the close mic and mixed signal and the diﬀerence was found.
Finally, the conversion between the arbitrary RMS value of the close mic recordings
and the dBA was calculated. This was done by ﬁnding the diﬀerence between 70 dBA
and the RMS level of the noise in the mixed signal and then subtracting the absolute
diﬀerence between the close mic and mixed recordings. For the calculation in Matlab
see Appendix C.4.
5.4
Computing Articulation Rate
Each of the participants’ articulation rates were calculated in all conditions. The artic-
ulation rates were computed using the Praat script presented in De Jong and Wempe
(2009). It detects syllable nuclei (the peak within the syllable, typically the vowel) using
intensity and voicedness. First, it extracts the intensity, and only peaks above a threshold
corresponding to the median intensity over the whole sound ﬁle are considered. This is
done because the vowel within a syllable has higher energy than its surrounding sounds.
Of these peaks, only the peaks that have a preceding dip of at least 2 dB with respect to
the current peak are considered. Finally, the syllable nuclei are extracted by excluding un-
voiced peaks found by the pitch contour. This is done to exclude voiceless consonants with
high intensity. The Praat script outputs a speaking rate measured as the total number of
syllable nuclei divided by the total duration of the sound sample, and an articulation rate
measured as the total number of syllable nuclei divided by the portions of the recording
only containing speech. In this work it was chosen to use the articulation rate. The speak-
ing rate or speech rate is relevant in a monologue where pauses in the speech stream are a
part of the rate itself. But in a dialogue like the ones recorded in this work, there will be
long stretches of silence where the talker is not intending to speak. Hence, it cannot be
argued to take these pauses into account when computing the rate of the talker’s speech,
and therefore the articulation rate is used.
The automatically computed articulation rates presented in De Jong and Wempe
(2009) correlated well with human-measured articulation rates (R-values between 0.7 and
0.88 for diﬀerent corpora), but some of the mistakes that were made by the algorithm
was that there was a tendency that the algorithm underestimated the number of syllables
by missing mostly unstressed syllables. However, it was considered appropriate for this
experiment as the diﬀerences between conditions were of interest and not the absolute
numbers.

Results
The following chapter presents the results from quantitative measures of the conversational
experiment using the silence and overlap classiﬁcation algorithm presented in Sections 5.1
and 5.2, the RMS conversion as presented in Section 5.3, and the algorithm for extracting
the articulation rates presented in Section 5.4. First, an investigation of the eﬀects of the

measure of their eﬀectiveness, the average completion time in the diﬀerent conditions was
computed. Next, the inﬂuence of L2 and noise on conversational dynamics is presented

When analysing the data a repeated measures analysis of variance (ANOVA) with
three within-subjects factors (language, background and session) was run on observations
on all pairs in each condition in the statistical software SPSS. No between-subjects factors
were analysed. Both language and background had two levels: Danish and English, and
quiet and noise. The session factor had three levels corresponding to the three sessions
(blocks). A one-way ANOVA for the completion time for each of the twelve pictures was
also made. In a repeated measures ANOVA, sphericity is assumed. If the assumption is
true the variances of the diﬀerences between all combinations of within-subjects conditions
are equal. To test if this assumption was violated, a Mauchly’s Test of Sphericity was run in
SPSS. If the test is statistically signiﬁcant (p < .05), the null hypothesis that all variances
of the diﬀerences are equal is rejected. In that case the Greenhouse-Geisser correction
factor was used, which eﬀectively increases the p-value to compensate for the test being
too liberal when sphericity is violated.5

7. DISCUSSION AND OUTLOOK
7
Discussion and Outlook
This chapter will discuss the results presented in Chapter 6 and revisit the hypothesis
outlined in Section 1.3. It will furthermore discuss the validity of the stimuli used in the
experiment and give an outlook of what the recorded conversations can be used for moving
forward.
7.1
Speech Production and Conversational Dynamics
It was initially hypothesized that noise and L2 would impose a cognitive load on the par-
ticipants and thereby increase their median FTOs and broaden the FTO distributions.
However, the opposite was seen as presented in Section 6.3: the median FTO decreased
in noise both in the participant’s ﬁrst and second language, and the distributions were
narrower. This suggests that people move their operating point towards more rapid tran-
sitions and operate within a smaller range of ﬂoor-transfer oﬀsets when background noise
is present. However, the changes were small and the overall shape of the distributions
were similar across conditions. This suggests that this turn-taking behaviour is important
and that there may be a universal pattern in how we take turns that is more important
to maintain than other properties of the speech we produce.
One explanation of the small change in the duration of FTOs could be that the partici-
pants already planned their response but are awaiting a go-signal in the form of acoustical
absence of speech from their interlocutor, but ends of speech streams (termed IPUs) may
be masked by the noise and thus the turn relevant point may be perceived earlier by the
interlocutor. However, if it was the case that ends of speech were masked by the noise,
it is unlikely that the noise would have the same masking eﬀect on all types of phonemes
(Lecumberri et al., 2010), and thus it would result in a broadening of the distribution
rather than a narrowing as was seen.
An obvious question to ask is whether the participants were challenged enough by the
noise and L2, and the following paragraphs will discuss this question. It was presented in
Section 6.1 and 6.2 that L2 decreased the articulation rate and increased the completion
time, and noise increased the articulation rate, the RMS level and the completion time
signiﬁcantly.
The slower articulation rate in L2 may be a consequence of a slower word retrieval.
De Jong and Wempe (2009) argues that articulation rate is a good measure of ﬂuency,
and thus the slower articulation rate in English compared to Danish may suggest that
the participants were indeed challenged by their second language despite having a high
proﬁciency in English. However, it could simply be that English is, in general. a slower
language than Danish, but this is diﬃcult to quantify and an investigation of this is beyond
the scope of this project.
The increase in completion time in L2 suggests that the talkers were challenged. How-
ever, this could also be inﬂuenced by their decreased articulation rates. In general, there
was not much of an eﬀect of language on any of the quantitative measures presented in
Chapter 6. We attribute this to the participants being very ﬂuent in English, limiting L2
eﬀects.
==PAGE:39==
7.1. SPEECH PRODUCTION AND CONVERSATIONAL DYNAMICS
31
When listening to the recordings it was perceived as if the participants were stressed when
speaking in the presence of background noise. There was a trend that when speaking in
quiet they would slowly start solving the task, whereas in noise they would be more eager
to get on with the task. There was a signiﬁcant increase in the duration and amount of
the overlaps-within in noise (see Figures 18a and 18b). Inspecting the distribution of the
overlaps-within (Figure 18c) showed that there were more one-syllable overlaps in quiet,
whereas there were more two-three syllables in noise. One-syllable answers are presumably
backchannels or agreement present-indications, whereas longer overlaps may be attempts
to take the ﬂoor. However, it would require manual listening to the overlaps-within and
the IPUs they were produced within to validate this assumption. It was the subjective
impression of the operator that the participants were competing more for the ﬂoor in
noise, and the observations for the overlaps-within support this impression. Moreover,
the articulation rates increased and the median FTO decreased in noise. Hence, the com-
munication seemed more rapid in noise, but the speed did not help them increasing their
eﬃciency. In fact the eﬃciency, measured as completion time per picture (see Figure 12a),
decreased in background noise. Monitoring the recordings a clear trend was perceived,
that in noise people more often mis-predicted either when their interlocutor would ﬁnish
their turn or what they would say at the end of their turn. Hence, the last part of a
talker’s utterance would sometimes be masked by competing talk and background noise
(i.e. a low SNR) such that the interrupting talker would miss important information at
the end of the ﬁrst talker’s turn. This led to misunderstandings that took a long time to
correct, hence it could contribute to the longer completion times. However, a quantitative
analysis of these observations was not made in this thesis and is left for further analysis
by quantifying the types of overlaps in the diﬀerent conditions. These observations all
suggest that noise did challenge and even stressed the participants.
It was found that the participants increased their speech levels in noise such that they
on average spoke at an SNR of -2 dB both in L1 and L2, i.e. there was no diﬀerence
in the increase in speech level between the languages. An SNR of -2 dB would often be
challenging in SI tests involving listening only, but in this experiment people conversed
and thereby they had the ability to get error correction while speaking. It could be that
the participants are near ceiling performance, and thus are operating on nearly the same
high point on the psychometric curves which are too close to show an eﬀect. There could
be two reasons for this. First of all the SNR could be great enough for the interlocutors to
understand most of their interlocutor’s speech. Secondly, the speech is highly contextual
as the participants are having a conversation about similar pictures, and thus the oppo-
nent may be better at ﬁlling in the missing parts of their interlocutor’s speech stream.
Both of these reasons would push the operating point on their psychometric functions
towards ceiling.
Interestingly, both the articulation rates, pause durations and IPU durations increased in
background noise (see Figures 10, 17 and 19a). In Levinson and Torreira (2015), Section
6.3, it is outlined that comprehension is 2-3 times faster than production, and preartic-
ulation is 3-4 times faster. This means that while more words are spoken within each
utterance of the talkers such that there is more for the interlocutor to comprehend, the
increase in articulation rate is only about 36 % (see Figure 10), i.e. still much slower than
the comprehension.
Eﬀectively, this means that the longer IPUs give the interlocutor
more time to plan his or her response. The distribution of IPUs (see Figure 19b and 19c)

32

showed that the increase in IPU duration is not because there more outliers, but because
all IPUs are, in general, longer, as indicated by the shallower slope of the distribution
in noise conditions. The pause duration increased about 10-15 % in noise, and a simple
explanation for this is that the speakers have to take longer breaths than in quiet before
producing their next utterance as they have spoken faster, at a higher level and for a
longer time. But another, overall interpretation of the increased articulation rates, pause
durations and IPU durations is that people try to hold their turn for longer in noise by
increasing their articulation rate such that they do not leave open spaces for their inter-
locutor to take the turn. However when ﬁnally giving space for their interlocutor to take
their turn, they leave a longer space open to signal a turn-relevant point, and thereby their
pause durations increase. This assumption is also supported by the decreased number of
pauses per minute in noise, as this leaves fewer spaces blank for the interlocutor to take the
turn. However, the number of pauses per minute increased by about 15 % in L2 compared
to L1, which may have a few interpretations. One is that the talkers need longer time
for word retrieval but are still signalling that its their turn, and another reason may be
that they are leaving space for their interlocutor to ﬁll in their missing words. The latter
interpretation comes from a few observations of exactly that strategy used by some of the
conversational pairs: that they would rely on their interlocutor’s vocabulary when they
failed to ﬁnd the words themselves. As presented in the introduction, Clark and Lind-
sey (2015) found in their case study that the child responded faster when relying on one
or more words repeated from the adult’s question than when he retrieved and produced
speciﬁc words on his own. This could be an additional strategy for trying to maintain
normal turn-taking behaviour in L2. It would therefore be interesting to investigate if the
participants followed a similar strategy as children, where they rely on their interlocutor’s
vocabulary. This would require manual investigation by listening to IPUs before and after
pauses. All of the observations outlined above could be strategies to maintain the overall
turn-taking behaviour minimizing gaps and overlaps in turn transitions.
To investigate the assumption above that the participants beneﬁted from their interlocu-
tor holding their turn longer giving them more time to plan and respond "on-time", it was
investigated how the IPUs preceding and following a ﬂoor-transfer oﬀset were distributed.
These IPUs were explicitly saved as explained in Section 5.2.1. As a way of investigating
this the 25%, 50%, and 75% percentiles were found for these IPUs. The histogram of the
FTOs matching these IPUs (i.e. the FTOs following and preceding an IPU) were then
computed and the results for the condition in which people spoke in Danish in quiet can
be seen in Figures 21a and 21b, respectively. The pattern was the same in the other three
conditions and can be seen in Figures 24 through 26 in Appendix B.3, p. 47-48. There
is a hard limit on the FTOs belonging to the IPUs in the lower interval, as the FTOs
naturally cannot exceed a certain overlap length because of the length of the IPU itself.
Figure 21a indicates that with an increasing IPU preceding an FTO, the distribution
of the FTOs gets narrower and the median decreases (see medians in caption). This means
that the longer the IPU is before a turn-taking, the more "on-time" the next talker is in
giving his response likely because he/she has more time to plan his/her answer.
In Figure 21b it is indicated that the shorter IPUs are after an FTO, the more narrow
the distribution is, and the smaller the median is. This means that the shorter the response
is, the faster it is. This is likely because the respondent needs less time to plan (including
both motor-planning and conceptual planning) a short response. As the lower interval is
0-183 ms, the responses belonging to this interval are one-syllable responses, i.e. probably

7.2
Choice of Stimuli
The speech shaped noise used in this experiment was a mixed gender 6-talker babble-like
vocoded noise (ICRA 7, Dreschler et al. (2001)). As there was relatively many talkers
simulated in the noise, the overall envelope was relatively ﬂat.
Thus the participants
could not fully exploit talking in the dips of the masker, but were still challenged by the
ﬂuctuations in the masker.
This makes the timing analysis of the conversation easier
as the noise is assumed to inﬂuence the dynamics rather constantly. The noise is not
very realistic, but it forms a good baseline understanding of the inﬂuence of noise on
conversational dynamics. Moving forward, it would be interesting to use other types of
more realistic maskers such as competing talkers. It would be interesting to investigate if
the participants would alter timing towards dips in a highly modulated masker to obtain
higher SNRs rather than aiming for no-gap-no-overlap. However, from what is found in
this study, it is not assumed that this would happen as it seems conversational partners
change their behaviour to maintain a certain pattern considered as normal turn-taking
dynamics.
The conversations were elicited by the use of the Diapix task (Van Engen et al., 2010; Baker
and Hazan, 2011) to create natural dialogue. While it motivated people to communicate
with each other, a potential drawback of this kind of task is the goal in ﬁnishing the
task.
Speciﬁcally, it may be the case that people increased their articulation rates in
noise because they wanted more rapid conversation so they could ﬁnish the task quicker
and have the noise switched oﬀ. This may or may not reﬂect real-world scenarios. In
==PAGE:43==

this experiment the participants knew the noise would be switched oﬀwhen they ﬁnished
the task, but in a real-life scenario you may not be able to leave a noise-ﬁlled room
whenever a conversation is over. However, having a conversation in noise is presumably
more cognitively demanding than just being present in noise, and hence the same may be
seen in real-world scenarios, that people try to make their conversations short and rapid.
The increasing completion time in L2 is consistent with Baker and Hazan (2011)’s ﬁnd-
ings that pairs of native talkers were more eﬃcient than pairs of non-native talkers with
shared L1. However, there was found a training eﬀect between session 1 and 2, whereas
Baker and Hazan (2011) found no training eﬀect. Moreover, they found an equal contri-
bution from both talkers, while there was a slight tendency towards one talker dominating
more than the other (55% vs. 45 %). Regardless, the task fulﬁlled its purpose, namely
creating natural dialogue in a controlled manner that is easy to analyse computationally.
For the sake of avoiding crosstalk the participants were situated in separate booths.
Thereby they did not have visual cues, which could potentially have an impact on the
turn-taking pattern, as gestural and gaze cues are known to be used by interlocutors to
signal turn-ends. In Table 7 the summary statistics for the FTO distributions found in
this study in quiet and Stivers et al. (2009)’s study are seen. From this it is seen that the
median, mean and standard deviation of the FTO distribution found in this experiment
in the conversations produced in quiet in Danish were very similar to the ones found in
Stivers et al. (2009) from Danish conversations where people were facing each other. More-
over, an interesting observation is that the FTOs statistics of the conversations produced
in English in this experiment are much more similar to the ones in the Danish conver-
sations, both found in this study and Stivers et al. (2009), than they are to the English
conversations presented in Stivers et al. (2009). This implies that it is not the language
itself that drives the turn-taking behaviour, but the culture of the people producing it.

Anticipation of Turn-Ends
An interesting follow-up study that could be made on the recordings made in this study
would be to have people pressing a button in anticipation of turn-ends similar to De Ruiter
et al. (2006). However, instead of stopping the stimuli when the participants pressed the
button as they did in De Ruiter et al. (2006), the stimuli would be played until the end. De
Ruiter et al. (2006) argue that they stopped the stimuli whenever the button was pressed
to avoid creating a bias. However, it is assumed that this just creates another bias if they
do not get feedback. Hence, it would be interesting to investigate the diﬀerences in the
bias in stopping vs. not stopping the stimuli simultaneously with the button press.
The advantage of using the stimuli from this experiment is that there are both Danish
and English conversations, and thus it can be investigated how important context is

8
Summary and Outlook
This study investigated the eﬀect of noise and second language on conversational dynamics
between pairs of interlocutors. A conversation corpus was made between 20 native-Danish
friendly pairs of talkers in Danish (ﬁrst language, L1) and English (second language, L2),
in quiet and in noise, consisting of a mixed gender 6-talker babble-like vocoded noise. To
elicit conversations the talkers worked together to solve a spot-the-diﬀerence task, "the
Diapix task" (Van Engen et al., 2010; Baker and Hazan, 2011), in three replications of
each of the four conditions.
The recordings were categorised into gaps (gaps between talkers during turn-taking),
overlaps-between (overlap between talkers during turn-taking), pauses (pauses in one
person’s speech stream not interrupted by the interlocutor), overlaps-within (overlaps
within an interlocutor’s speech stream), and inter-pausal units (speech streams excluding
overlaps-within). Gaps and overlaps were combined into one ﬂoor-transfer oﬀset (FTO)
distribution where overlaps-between have negative FTOs and gaps have positive FTOs.
The categorisations were made computationally by the use of a voice activity detection
algorithm determining boundaries in speech streams, and a 7-stage ﬁnite state machine
classifying these speech streams into the categories above. Because of the simplicity of
the categorization algorithm it can be expanded to be implemented in hardware to make
annotations of private recordings in the ﬁeld.
We hypothesised that speaking in a second language (L2) and/or in background noise
would introduce a delay in turn-taking due to increased cognitive load that would disrupt
the participants’ ability to predict when to take a turn.
However, in contrast to our
predictions, in the presence of noise, the median of the FTO distribution decreased by
approximately 30 ms in L1 (10 %) and 50 ms in L2 (16 %), and the standard deviation
in both languages decreased by 12 %. This was interpreted as a change in the operating
point towards more rapid conversation in noise.
Overall, the participants took longer to solve the task in both L2 and noise, and they
increased their speaking level by 9 dB in the presence of noise. They furthermore increased
their articulation rates signiﬁcantly in noise (about 40 %), and decreased it in L2, likely
because of a slower word retrial. However, beyond completion time and articulation rate,
L2 did not have much of an eﬀect, likely because the participants were very ﬂuent in
English. The increased articulation rate in noise suggested that the participants were
stressed.
This was further indicated by the decrease in the median FTO and by the
rate of overlaps-within increasing by approximately 40 % in noise. However, the average
duration of IPUs increased about 40 % in noise, leaving more time for their interlocutor
to plan their response.
Regardless of the small change in the operating point in noise, the distributions of
FTOs in L1 and L2 both in quiet and noise were highly similar and suggest a univer-
sal pattern when comparing with FTO distributions found in other studies computed on
diﬀerent types of conversations in diﬀerent environments and diﬀerent languages. This
implies that turn-taking behaviour is important for maintaining socially acceptable be-
haviour, and that other strategies in the speech production are used to compensate for
cognitive demands to maintain the overall dynamics.
This work will be my ﬁrst step on the path of investigating turn-taking patterns
for hearing-impaired (HI) listeners and further investigate normal-hearing behaviour in
==PAGE:46==
38
8. SUMMARY AND OUTLOOK
noise.
It is generally known that HI people tend to withdraw from social situations
as their impairment gives them trouble communicating in noise. Traditionally this has
been attributed to their reduced speech intelligibility, but it is hypothesized that their
hearing loss will disrupt their ability to predict when a talker will end his or her turn by
reducing their ability to follow intonational or intensity cues, especially in noise, making it
diﬃcult for HI participants to maintain normal turn-taking behaviour in conversation. As
a foundation for investigating this hypothesis, knowledge about the normal turn-taking
behaviour in conversation must ﬁrst be obtained.
The work presented in this report
provides a solid baseline for understanding the importance of timing in turn-taking to
maintain socially acceptable dialogue behaviour.

Instructions 
Thank you for participating in this experiment. Today’s experiment will consist of a collaborative 
task, which is called the “Diapix” task. 
Instructions for Diapix session 
You will each be seated in your own booth wearing headphones and a microphone, and I’m going 
to give each of you a single picture. The pictures you get are almost identical. Your task is to 
communicate with each other to find differences between the pictures. You will be able to hear 
yourself and the other person in the headphones. 
The differences may involve a missing item from one picture or a change between the two pictures. 
When you find one of these differences, circle it and write down the missing/changed items directly 
on to the pictures. 
For example, if a woman is wearing a necklace in one picture, but not in the other, draw a circle and 
write “necklace” where the necklace should be.  
Don’t worry about making a perfect note, as long as the differences between your picture and your 
partner’s picture are clear.  
You should find 10 differences in all. Try to find them as quickly and accurately as possible. 
Remember, this isn’t a competition between you and your partner; rather you are to work together 
to find 10 differences as a team. You will have maximum 15 minutes to find the differences, before 
we move on to the next picture. 
You will go through three sessions in total. In each session you will have to do the task with four 
different pictures, making up a total of 12 solved Diapix. With each of the four pictures in each
