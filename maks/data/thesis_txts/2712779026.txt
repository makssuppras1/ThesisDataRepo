Abstract
As the scaling of traditional on-chip electrical networks to large multi-core processors is facing numerous challenges
such as high latency, high power consumption and limited bandwidths, the need for integrated photonics and on-chip
optical interconnects is higher than ever. Among others, energy-efficient and small-footprint light sources are key com-
ponents to enable this transition. Photonic crystal (PhC) based nanolasers show strong potential due to their ultra-low
threshold currents and ability to be integrated on the Si-platform.
Recent demonstrations show that lasing can be achieved at ultra-low threshold currents for heterogeneously integrated
PhC lasers on a 250nm InP/SiO2/Si platform. The laser consists of a membranised PhC slab with a line-defect cavity com-
prising buried hetereostructure InGaAsP/InGaAlAs quantum wells and employs a lateral injection scheme for electrical
pumping of the laser. The lasers, however, exhibit a drop in injection efficiency at higher currents. To improve the per-
formance of the lasers, a better understanding of the electrical properties is required.
In this work, important steps were made towards building a reliable numerical model of the PhC lasers. The model al-
lowed to qualitatively evaluate the impact of material properties and laser design parameters. In addition, lasers were
experimentally characterised and results were compared to the simulated results to evaluate the validity of the model.
Although the model was shown to capture important trends and phenomena, some results showed a discrepancy with
experimental results and remained unexplained.

As the scaling of traditional on-chip electrical networks to large multi-core processors is facing numerous challenges such as high
latency, high power consumption and limited bandwidths, the need for integrated photonics and on-chip optical interconnects is higher
than ever. Among others, energy-efﬁcient and small-footprint light sources are key components to enable this transition. Photonic
crystal (PhC) based nanolasers show strong potential due to their ultra-low threshold currents and ability to be integrated on the
Si-platform. Recent demonstrations show that lasing can be achieved at ultra-low threshold currents for heterogeneously integrated
PhC lasers on a 250nm InP/SiO2/Si platform. The lasers, however, exhibit a drop in injection efﬁciency at higher currents due to
the presence of leakage currents. In this work, numerical simulations were performed to investigate the electrical characteristics of
these lasers. The inﬂuence of several material parameters on the IV curve was discussed as well as an investigation into possible
leakage paths. At last, experimental characterisations of the lasers were performed to verify the validity of the developed model.
I. INTRODUCTION
During
the
last
few
decades,
information
and
communication technology (ICT) has played an increasingly
important role in our day-to-day lives. Enormous technological
advancements have been made in the reduction of component
size and the dense integration of electrical components on
chips, all in line with Moore’s Law [1]. This technological
advancement has resulted in the reduction of the energy
consumption for logical operations. However, this rapid
technological development is reaching its fundamental limits,
caused by the presence of quantum effects at very small scales
[2]. One way to further reduce the energy consumption of
chips is to use more energy-efﬁcient interconnects. Electrical
interconnects consume a considerable amount of energy
due to the charging and discharging of electrical wires at
high frequencies [3] and suffer from extra heating as more
components are crammed into a smaller area. With the
exponential increase of internet trafﬁc and the demand for
higher communication bandwidths, leading to large power
consumption, new efforts to increase energy efﬁciency of
chip-to-chip and on-chip interconnects are needed.
Optical interconnects show strong potential for very short-
distance
communication.
Vertical-cavity
surface-emitting
lasers (VCSELs) are already being used inside data centres
for short-distance optical communication [4] for link lengths
up to 300m. Due to their small active region, the required
operating energy of VCSELs is very low [5], [6]. However,
as even lower operating energies are needed for chip-to-chip
and on-chip interconnects, even smaller active regions are
required and thus VCSELs are not suitable. In the last two
decades, various potential alternatives for optical interconnect
light sources have emerged [7]. Photonic crystal lasers in
particular have attracted a lot of attention as they allow for the
design of extremely small mode volumes while maintaining
very high Q-factors, enabling lasing to be achieved at record
low threshold currents [8].
II. ELECTRICAL CIRCUIT OF A LASER DIODE
In order to understand the electrical characteristics of a
semiconductor laser, it is useful to investigate a simple electri-
cal equivalent circuit. Since semiconductor lasers consist of a
pn-junction, they can be modelled by an equivalent electrical
circuit, consisting of a diode in series with a resistor as shown
in Figure 1. The series resistor models the resistive paths

ideal terms of the current such as recombination or generation
currents. It is equal to 1 for the ideal diode while for lasers
it is close to 2. As shown in Equation 1, the diode has an
exponential IV characteristic. Below a certain turn-on voltage,
it does not conduct. Once the turn-on voltage is reached the
diode will conduct without any resistance. However, due to
the presence of the series resistance, the laser diode will have
a linear IV characteristic above threshold. This is reﬂected in
the IV curve shown in Figure 2, where ﬁrst an exponential part
is present while at higher voltages above the turn-on voltage
a linear characteristic can be observed. The series resistance
determines the slope of the IV curve which is equal to 1/Rs.
Therefore, as Rs decreases the slope of the IV curve will
steepen, meaning that a smaller voltage swing is required to

III. LASER DESIGN AND PERFORMANCE
A. Laser Design
The HERO laser device is a PhC laser integrated on a 250
nm InP/SiO2/Si platform. The cavity is created by omitting
N air holes in the PhC structure leading to a so-called LN
line-defect cavity [11]. The active region consists of a single
or multiple InGaAsP/InAlGaAs-based quantum wells (QWs)
embedded in the PhC line-defect using buried heterostructure
(BH) technology which provides carrier conﬁnement [12].
Using Zn diffusion and Si ion implantation to create n- and
p-doped regions, a lateral p-i-n junction is created to facilitate
charge carrier injection into the active region [13]. The PhC
area is membranised by wet etching. The laser design is
shown in Figure 3. The speciﬁc HERO device modelled and
characterised in this work is HERO 6. It consists of an L7
line-defect cavity and has a single InGaAsP/InAlGaAs-based
quantum well as the active region.

As camera emission signal (blue) superimposed to show the emission from
the QW. (c) Bird-eye view of the lateral injection p-i-n structure. Reprinted
from [14].
B. Performance
The performance of the laser devices has been characterised
in [14], [15] by researchers of NATEC Centre of Excellence.
Very low threshold currents down to 10.2 µA were achieved
at an emission wavelength of 1540nm [15]. The LI curve was
shown to exhibit a clear roll-off of the collected power at
higher currents. It was veriﬁed that this roll-off is not caused
by Joule heating in the device by thermal investigations. The
roll-off effect can be explained by the presence of leakage
currents which consist of electrons that ﬂow past the BH and
recombine at the p-i interface [15].
In Figures 4 (b) and (c), the recombination at the p-region
interface is shown using an Si camera that only captures
the shorter wavelength spontaneous emission of the InP. This
conﬁrms the presence of leakage paths.

IV. RESULTS DISCUSSION
In an effort to gain a better understanding of the electrical
properties of the HERO laser devices, a 3D model was created
in Lumerical CHARGE [16]. The developed model closely
resembles the physical laser design in terms of dimensions,
doping levels and material properties. The simulations were
performed using a 2D simulations region and it was not
==PAGE:9==
3
possible to introduce the PhC structure, meaning that not all
dynamics are captured by the model.
A. Inﬂuence of Material Parameters on the IV Curve
The PhC laser devices investigated in this work were
characterised by means of IV curve measurements. These
IV characteristics can be used to verify the validity of the
developed Lumerical CHARGE model. It proves therefore
useful to obtain an understanding of the inﬂuence of different
material parameters on the IV curve of the simulated laser
device. An investigation of the inﬂuence of the carrier
mobilities, the dielectric permittivity of InP and the bandgap
of InGaAsP was conducted.
First, the inﬂuence of the carrier mobility was studied.
The carrier mobilities µn and µp are inversely proportional
to the resistivity of the material [17] and thus an increase in
the mobility will lead to a reduction of the resistance. This
was conﬁrmed during simulations as an inverse proportional
relationship was observed. It was also shown that a decrease
in the carrier mobility resulted in a slight increase in the
turn-on voltage of the device. The cause for the trend
remained unexplained.
The change in dielectric permittivity can be used as a way to
partly model the introduction of a photonic crystal structure in
the software. Its inﬂuence, however, is limited in junctions in
forward bias as the carriers are able to rearrange themselves
to accommodate the same electric ﬁeld. The simulations
conﬁrmed this and the IV curve was hardly affected by a
change in dielectric permittivity.
At last, the bandgap of the active medium (InGaAsP) is
proportional to the turn-on voltage of the device as it
determines the forward bias required for a current to ﬂow.
This proportionality was conﬁrmed by the simulations.
However, the absolute value of the turn-on voltage was higher
than the bandgap of the active medium for all performed
simulations. Further investigations need to be conducted to
gain a better understanding of the underlying mechanisms
and could include investigating the inﬂuence of the bandgap
of InAlGaAs and InP.
B. Investigation of leakage paths
The presence of leakage paths has a detrimental effect on
the laser’s efﬁciency. Two leakage paths can be identiﬁed in
the device. One path constitutes of current ﬂowing through
the InP layers, while the other is caused by the high electron
mobility which enables electrons to escape the quantum well
on the p-side. The leakage of electrons into the p-region lead
to recombinations outside of the active region as was shown
experimentally [15] and therefore increases the threshold
current of the laser.
The presence of leakage currents is conﬁrmed by the
plot shown in Figure 5. It shows how the total electron and
hole current vary throughout the device. A decrease in the
electron and hole current is related to the recombination of
an electron and a hole. The ﬁgure shows that all the hole

current that reaches the active region also recombines there.
Their low mobility prevents them from escaping the well
before recombining. However, since the electrons have a
higher mobility, some manage to escape the active region
before recombining, and so part of the electron current ﬂows

is not able to capture this increased recombination rate at
the interface. The electron current decreases slowly as it

laser. At an applied bias of 1.6V, the leakage was shown to
increase to about 29%. A next step would be to quantify the
importance of each of the mentioned leakage paths.
C. Investigation of the injection efﬁciency
An important ﬁgure of merit to characterise the performance
of a laser is the injection efﬁciency ⌘inj, deﬁned as the ratio of
the total number of recombinations occurring in the QW over
the total number of recombinations in the complete device. It
thus signiﬁes how much of the current is captured by the QW.
A plot of the injection efﬁciency versus applied bias is given in
Figure 6. It shows that for voltages below 1.2V the injection
efﬁciency remains high, after which it decreases. As shown
before, the leakage of electrons increases at higher voltages
and causes more recombinations to occur outside of the QW,
explaining the observed drop in injection efﬁciency.
Although the injection efﬁciency is an important measure of
the ability of the QW to capture the current, it does not contain
any information about the type of recombinations occurring in

quantum efﬁciency (IQE), which is deﬁned as the portion of

Figure 7 shows how the IQE varies with the applied bias.
It should be noted that this plot is only valid below lasing
threshold. Once threshold is reached, the carrier density in
the QW will get clamped and so the recombination rates
will retain their threshold value. The ﬁgure shows that for
an applied bias around the designed turn-on voltage of 0.8V,
the device achieves an IQE of about 90%, which is very
high. However, with the turn-on voltage of the model being
around 1.1V, a quantum efﬁciency of only 47% would be
achieved. With increasing voltages, the Auger recombination
starts dominating as it is proportional to n3, with n the
carrier density. This was conﬁrmed by observing that the IQE
remained very high for high voltages in the case where the
Auger coefﬁcients were put to zero in the model. From the
slope of the IQE around 0.8V, it can be concluded that a small
deviation in threshold voltage will have a big impact on the
IQE.
The total efﬁciency can now be deﬁned as ⌘= ⌘inj · ⌘IQE
and is given in Figure 8. The laser efﬁciencies at 0.8V and
1.1V are 71% and 36%, respectively.
D. Inﬂuence of the p-doping offset
The offset of the p-doped region with respect to the BH
has a big impact on the performance of the laser. A trade-off
exists between the Q-factor of the cavity and the injection
efﬁciency when the p-region offset is varied. Electrons that
reach the p-doped region and recombine there, limit the
injection efﬁciency and this phenomenon could be reduced by
reducing the offset of the p-doped region. This would cause
more holes to reach the active region before recombining.
However, by reducing the offset, the Q-factor of the cavity
will decrease due to the high absorption coefﬁcient of the
p-InP which induces extra losses [18].

therefore imagine there to be a trade-off between these two
mechanisms which explains the trend observed in Figure 9.
The increase in hole mobility seems to dominate the decrease
in hole density until the offset is about 1.1µm after which
the decrease in carrier density seems to have a stronger effect
leading to an increase in resistivity.

sweep to the simulated results in Section IV-D, IV curves
were obtained from which the resistance was derived. The
IV curve is the only measure of the electrical properties of
the lasers that could be obtained experimentally and can be
used to verify the accuracy of the Lumerical CHARGE model.

VI. CONCLUSION
In this paper, the main results of an investigation into the
electrical characteristics of a PhC nanolaser through numerical
simulations were discussed. A Lumerical CHARGE model
was created to investigate the inﬂuence of several material
parameters on the electrical characteristics of the laser. Their
effect was evaluated by examining the IV curve. Next, the
presence of leakage paths was explored and conﬁrmed. The
dependence of the injection efﬁciency and internal quantum
efﬁciency on the applied bias was studied and showed a
relation that is in line with the relevant theory. At last,
the inﬂuence of the offset of the p-doped region on the
resistance of the laser was investigated. A sweep of the p-
region offset was performed both experimentally and in the
model and their results were compared. A ﬁrst step was made
towards developing an accurate model for the PhC nanolasers.
However, some results remained unexplained and call for a
further investigation into the model and the theory that governs
it.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
During the last few decades, information and communication technology (ICT) has played an increasingly important role
in our day-to-day lives. Enormous technological advancements have been made in the reduction of component size and
the dense integration of electrical components on chips, all in line with Moore’s Law [8]. This technological advancement
has resulted in the reduction of energy consumption for logical operations. However, this rapid technological develop-
ment is reaching its fundamental limits, caused by the presence of quantum effects at very small scales [3]. One way to
further reduce the energy consumption of chips is to use more energy-efficient interconnects. Electrical interconnects
consume a considerable amount of energy due to the charging and discharging of electrical wires at high frequencies [4]
and suffer from extra heating as more components are crammed into a smaller area. With the exponential increase of
internet traffic and the demand for higher communication bandwidths, leading to large power consumption, new efforts
to increase energy efficiency of chip-to-chip and on-chip interconnects are needed.
Optical interconnects show strong potential for very short-distance communication. Vertical-cavity surface-emitting
lasers (VCSELs) are already being used inside data centres for short-distance optical communication [5] for link lengths
up to 300m. Due to their small active region, the required operating energy of VCSELs is very low [6, 7]. However, as
even lower operating energies are needed for chip-to-chip and on-chip interconnects, even smaller active regions are
required and thus VCSELs are not suitable. In the last two decades, various potential alternatives for optical interconnect
light sources have emerged [9]. Photonic crystal lasers in particular have attracted a lot of attention as they allow for
the design of extremely small mode volumes while maintaining very high Q-factors, enabling lasing to be achieved at
record low threshold currents [31].

Scientific Background
2.1
Semiconductor lasers
A basic semiconductor laser diode consists of an n-doped region on one side, an intrinsic region containing the active
medium in the centre, and a p-doped region on the other side, constituting a pin-junction. Metal contacts are placed
on the p-side (anode) and n-side (cathode). To obtain population inversion, the active region needs to be electrically
pumped by the injection of holes and electrons from the p- and n-side, respectively. By applying a forward bias over
the metal contacts, the electrons and holes flow towards the active region. The active medium is chosen such that the
carriers get trapped inside it. This leads to high carrier densities inside the active region. Inside the active region, the
carriers will have a finite lifetime due to the presence of recombination mechanisms. To achieve lasing the active re-
gion is contained within an optical cavity to enable stimulated emission to overcome losses once population inversion
is achieved. A simple schematic is shown in Figure 2.1. Their low power consumption, compact size, low cost, and high
output power have gained these lasers their position as the main light source for data communication applications [18].
Figure 2.1: A simple schematic of a laser diode. Reprinted from [12].
Conventional laser diodes consist of a direct bandgap semiconductor layer as active medium, surrounded by cladding
layers on both sides. The active medium typically has a higher refractive index and lower bandgap than the cladding lay-
ers to ensure optical and carrier confinement, constituting a double heterostructure. Optical confinement is established
through total internal reflection due to the difference in refractive index between active and cladding layers. Carrier
confinement on the other hand is facilitated by the lower energy conduction and valence bands of the active medium
3
==PAGE:22==

compared to the cladding layers. The cavity mirrors can either be cleaved facets as in Fabry-Perot lasers, or grating struc-
tures as used in distributed Bragg reflector (DBR) lasers or distributed feedback (DFB) lasers [13, 14]. Contacts are placed
on the top and bottom of the structure creating a vertical current injection path. However, the double heterostructure
does not confine light in all directions and therefore leads to a larger active region with a higher threshold current as
a consequence. To improve optical and carrier confinement in the active region, a so-called buried heterostructure (BH)
was developed [20]. The active medium is embedded in a lower refractive index cladding material from all four sides,
achieving full confinement in the transverse directions. As discussed in Section 1.2, the reduction of the active region
leads to a lower threshold current.
2.2
Laser theory
Thebasicprincipleofanylasersystemistohaveanactivemediumenclosedwithinanopticalcavitythatcanbemodelled
as two parallel, highly reflecting mirrors one of which is partly transmitting. The active medium may be a solid, gas, or
liquid depending on the type of laser. In order to achieve amplification of light, the active medium is pumped either
optically or electrically to achieve population inversion, enabling the stimulated emission of photons to overcome the
total losses. The result is a monochromatic, highly directional, intense, and coherent beam. From a range of different
lasers discussed in [11], semiconductor laser diodes are the most suitable for communication applications.

2.3
Photonic crystal laser cavities
A photonic crystal (PhC) is a structure with a wavelength-scale periodic modulation of the refractive index [19]. Anal-
ogous to electrons in electronic crystals, one can achieve similar behaviour for photons in photonic crystals. The most
important result from this periodic structure is the existence of a photonic band structure (an ! vs. k representation,
where ! is the frequency and k is the wave vector). By manipulating the periodicity of the refractive index a photonic
bandgap (PBG), i.e. the absence of allowed propagating electromagnetic modes for a certain frequency range, can be
created. It allows one to confine and guide light in a more powerful way than can be achieved with total internal reflec-
tion. Furthermore, it opens up the possibility of creating small mode volume, high Q-factor cavities which are desired
for the integration and performance of on-chip optical interconnects.
Photonic crystals exist in 1D, 2D, and 3D where the periodicity of the refractive index is periodic in 1-, 2- and 3D re-
spectively, and the degree of confinement increases with the dimensionality of the periodicity. Although a 3D photonic
crystal would allow complete confinement of light in all directions, it is extremely difficult to produce defectless periodic
3D structures. Therefore, a more feasible PhC structure is the PhC 2D semiconductor slab which has a periodic refractive
index modulation in the in-plane directions and has a finite thickness in the vertical direction. The periodicity can be

total internal reflection (TIR) confines light in the vertical direction. Figure 2.3 shows a PhC slab, its corresponding Bril-

To obtain useful structures for confining and guiding light, the PhC lattice can be perturbed to introduce localized modes
within the bandgap. A point defect can be created by omitting a single air hole, while a line defect constitutes a number
of connected point defects. By carefully designing the perturbation, a cavity can be created that supports modes within
the bandgap that are not allowed to propagate in the unperturbed medium, effectively creating a cavity. The extension
of these line defects can be used to create waveguide structures [22] which could be used to couple light out of PhC
cavities. The influence of the cavity length, i.e. number of omitted holes, on the threshold gain has been investigated in
[26] and has shown to exhibit a minimum threshold gain for a certain length. This is in contrast to conventional lasers
where the threshold gain is decreasing monotonically with the cavity length and can be attributed to the presence of
disorder-induced losses. Furthermore, one can optimize the cavity Q-factor by shifting the position of the end holes of
the cavity mirrors [27]. A combination of these two techniques allows for optimization of the PhC cavity structure to
increase the Q-factor and reduce the threshold.
As discussed in Section 1.2, small mode-volume, high Q-factor cavities are essential to achieve low threshold lasers.
Since the confinement of light in photonic crystals is very strong, leading to high Q/V ratios, the Purcell effect will even
further enhance the spontaneous emission coupled to the lasing mode [24], effectively reducing the energy consump-
tion.
Impressive progress has been made by researchers of NTT Basic Research Labs in the last couple of decades since the
demonstration of the first proof-of-concept PhC laser in 1999 [25]. Their optically pumped PhC laser on the InP platform

2.4
Quantum wells as gain medium
Quantum wells (QW) are the most common gain media in today’s semiconductor lasers. The popularity of QW lasers can
be mainly attributed to their extremely low threshold currents, high-frequency modulation capabilities, and low degra-
dation rates [36]. Quantum wells are heterostructures in which a thin layer of active material (the well) is sandwiched
between two other semiconductor ’barrier’ layers. The materials are chosen such that charge carriers see a lower energy
in the well, leading to confinement of carriers in the well. As the carriers are confined in the well, much higher electron
and hole densities can be achieved. The enhancement of the carrier density is a crucial feature of QWs that leads to more
efficient lasers [33].
Due to the very small dimensions (thickness) of the active layer (typically 10nm), one can no longer neglect the wave
nature of the confined carriers. The allowed states correspond to standing waves in the direction perpendicular to the
layers, resulting in a quantized system [32]. The quantum carrier confinement, which results from the reduction in di-
mensions of the active layer, results in a step-like density of states (DOS) for a quantum well in contrast to the DOS for
bulk material which has a square root dependence on energy. By increasing the degree of confinement even further,
quantum structures such as quantum wires and quantum dots are obtained. The DOS versus energy for bulk material,
quantum wells, and quantum dots is given in Figure 2.4.
Figure 2.4: Density of states versus energy for bulk material, a quantum well, and a quantum dot. Adapted from [32].
Another advantage of quantum wells lies in the fact that their emission wavelength can be tuned by changing the
composition of the active material and by varying the width of the active layer [37]. Moreover, by inducing strain with
opposite sign in QW and quantum barrier (QB) layers, the DOS at the top of the valence band is decreased, allowing for
population inversion at a lower hole density, which reduces the threshold current [34].

2.5
Current injection schemes
To achieve robust and low-threshold PhC lasers that can be integrated on a chip, an efficient electrical pumping scheme
is needed. The requirements for good electrical, thermal, and optical properties of the laser are often conflicting, making
it a difficult task. Two competing current injection schemes have been developed over the last two decades: a vertical
current injection scheme [39] and a lateral current injection scheme [38].
In the vertical current injection scheme, the doped regions and contacts are placed above and below the active region.
The first vertically electrically pumped PhC laser was demonstrated by Imada et al. in 1999 [40]. However, as there was
no efficient optical and carrier confinement, the active region was large and this resulted in a high threshold current.
This injection scheme was later improved by a new design [39] which included the introduction of a post underneath
the cavity through which carriers could be injected more efficiently. On top of a reduction of the threshold current, the
post also improved the heat dissipation from the cavity [41]. However, the post limits the maximum achievable Q-factor
due to the optical losses induced by the doped layers. A trade-off in post size arises from the degrading Q-factor for
increasing post size while decreasing its size leads to thermal and electrical resistance issues [39].
In the lateral current injection scheme, on the other hand, the PhC membrane is doped on both sides of the cavity
constituting a lateral p-i-n junction while contacts are placed accordingly supporting a lateral injection of the carriers
into the active region. The first lateral injection PhC laser was demonstrated in [38]. Figure 2.5 shows a schematic of a
lateral current injection PhC laser.
Figure 2.5: A schematic of a lateral current injection scheme implemented in a PhC laser. Reprinted from [43].
The lateral injection scheme allows for more freedom in the laser design and as the optical losses can be limited due to
the selective doping of the PhC membrane, higher Q-factors can be achieved. Therefore, the lateral injection scheme has
proven to be superior in terms of threshold current and energy consumption over a vertical injection scheme [30, 31].
Furthermore, the selective doping of the membrane allows for the integration of other active and passive structures

small cross-sectional area of the p-i-n junction which offers potential for high-speed modulation.
ThefirstsuccessfulimplementationofthisschemewasdemonstratedbyEllisetal. [42], inwhichCWlasingwasachieved

4.1
Solver physics
Lumerical CHARGE is a physics-based electrical simulation tool well suited for modelling semiconductor devices. The
CHARGE solver self-consistently solves the system of equations that describe the electrostatic potential, through Pois-
son’s equation, and the density of free carriers using the drift-diffusion equations.
Even though there is no net current flow in a pn-junction in equilibrium (no applied bias), one can still identify two
current components: a diffusion current and a drift current. The diffusion current arises from the gradient in charge car-
rier concentration. The n-side of the junction contains a lot of mobile electrons while the p-side contains a lot of mobile
holes, due to this difference the diffusion of electrons (holes) from the n-side (p-side) to the p-side (n-side) takes place.
The diffusion currents are given by:

AsLumericalCHARGEdoesnotallowtoimplementaphotoniccrystalstructure, anattemptatmodellingthePhCstructure

in the InP membrane, a decrease in the effective dielectric constant is to be expected. However, it should be noted that
through the introduction of air holes, there would also be surface recombinations present at the air-InP interface which
would also influence the electrical characteristics. Implementing this extra recombination mechanism is not possible
in Lumerical CHARGE and its effect was therefore not investigated. The discussed results thus show only the effect of a
change in the effective dielectric permittivity.
The dielectric permittivity was swept over an arbitrary range around the default value provided by Lumerical to explore
any trends present. Figure 4.6 shows the result of the sweep on the IV curve.

The plot shows that the dielectric permittivity does not affect the IV curve in any notable way. This can be understood
by having a closer look at the definition of the dielectric permittivity. The dielectric permittivity is related to the ability
of a material to store energy in an electric field. It plays therefore an important role in pn-junctions in reverse bias
or equilibrium, where the charges are separated in space, constituting an electric field. However, when the device is
forward biased and current flows through the device, there are a lot of carriers distributed over the device. The carriers
will rearrange themselves to accommodate the same electric field, making the dielectric permittivity less relevant.

4.2.5
Investigation of leakage paths
As discussed in Section 3.2, the presence of leakage paths is a limiting factor in terms of performance. Two possible
leakage paths can be identified in this device. A first leakage path could be created by carriers flowing through the InP
layers above and below the active region. In the physical device, leakage paths next to the BH are also present but these
are not accounted for in the 2D simulations that were performed. A second path is formed by electrons that are captured
by the QW but manage to escape it at the p-side. The former is linked to the turn-on voltage of InP while the latter is
caused by the difference in carrier mobility between electrons and holes. Both contribute to the leakage of electrons
that end up recombining at the p-side, leading to an increase in the threshold current of the laser and a decrease in the
injection efficiency. However, for the electrons to leak through the InP layers, the applied voltage should be higher than
the turn-on voltage of InP which lies around 1.35V.

4.2.7
Investigation of the influence of the p-doping offset
The offset of the p-doped region with respect to the BH has a big impact on the performance of the laser. A trade-off
exists between the Q-factor of the cavity and the injection efficiency when the p-region offset is varied. As discussed
in Section 4.2.5, part of the electrons reach the p-doped region where they recombine with the holes. This limits the
injection efficiency and could be improved by reducing the offset of the p-doped region. This would cause more holes to
reach the active region before recombining. However, by reducing the offset, the Q-factor of the cavity will decrease due
to the high absorption coefficient of the p-InP [2]. The closer the p-doping to the BH, the more it will be felt by cavity
mode, leading to extra losses and reducing the Q-factor [50]. The absorption coefficient of n-doped InP is lower and
therefore its offset is less important.
During the characterisation of HERO lasers, performed by researchers at NATEC Centre of Excellence, it has been shown
repeatedly that pulling back the p-region too far inhibits the laser from lasing. This was also confirmed during the
experimental characterisation performed in the scope of this thesis and will be discussed in Section 5.2. The observed
effect is assumed to be the result of the low hole mobility, which prevents the holes from reaching the active region
before they recombine with leaked electrons.
A brief investigation of the influence of the p-region offset on the radiative recombination inside the QW was conducted.
The offset was increased over a set of values and the maximum radiative recombination rates in the QW were monitored.

Conclusion and Outlook
6.1
Summary and conclusions
The goal of this work was to gain a better understanding of the electrical characteristics of HERO laser devices and to
develop an accurate model using Lumerical CHARGE. A 3D model was created in close resemblance with the structure of
the physical laser devices and some important material parameters were set to fit values used in literature.
First, the influence of the dielectric permittivity and carrier mobility of InP and the bandgap of InGaAsP on the IV curve
was examined. Both the dielectric permittivity and carrier mobility followed trends that are in agreement with the rel-
evant theory. Although the trend observed in the influence of the bandgap of InGaAsP was in line with the theory, the
absolute value of the turn-on voltage was found to deviate from the values observed in experiments. A higher turn-on
voltage was observed and might be caused by the distribution of charge carriers in the well. However, a further investi-
gation is required to quantify the effect of this phenomenon.
An extensive study of the presence of leakage paths followed. Two possible leakage paths were identified. A first path is
caused by the high electron mobility that enables some electrons to escape the quantum well at the p-side of the well.
Electrons flowing through the InP layers constitute a second leakage path. Both lead to the recombination of electrons
at the p-side and limit the number of holes that reach the quantum well, effectively reducing the recombination occur-
ring in the quantum well. The presence of leakage was confirmed by examining how the electron and hole current vary
through the structure. A gradient in the electron current density towards the quantum well was observed and could be
explained by an excess of holes in the quantum well. The leakage of electrons into the p-region has been observed ex-
perimentally and leads to increased spontaneous emission at the p-region interface. The simulations, however, were not
able to capture this phenomenon and the recombination of electrons in the p-region was shown to take place gradually.
Next, an investigation of the injection efficiency was conducted. The injection efficiency was shown to remain around
80% for applied biases up to 1.1-1.2V. After this, it monotonically decreases as the applied bias increases and can be at-
tributed to an increased electron leakage which results in an increase in the recombinations occurring outside of the
quantum well. Although the injection efficiency is an important parameter, it does not give the full picture of how ef-
ficient the laser operates. Therefore, a discussion of the internal quantum efficiency was also included. It was found
that a maximum quantum efficiency of around 90% is achieved for an applied bias around 0.7V after which it severely
drops due to an increase of the Auger recombination in the quantum well. It was noted that these results are only valid

below threshold. Considering that the turn-on voltage of InGaAsP is designed to be at 0.8V, it was shown that a quantum
efficiency of 89% can be achieved. The overall efficiency was shown to reach a maximum value of 72% at an applied
bias around 0.72V and a value of 70% at 0.8V.
At last, the influence of the offset of the p-region with respect to the BH was studied. Experimentally, it has been shown
repeatedly that once the p-region offset becomes too large, the laser devices are unable to achieve lasing. A sweep of
the p-region offset was performed in the simulations to capture this phenomenon, however, the software failed to cap-
ture the same behaviour. A high radiative recombination in the QW was achieved for p-region offsets as high as 3µm.
A possible explanation was proposed which relates the high radiative recombination to the gradual recombination of
leaked electrons. A more extensive investigation is required to explain the observed behaviour. Next, the change of the
resistance of the laser device with an increasing p-offset was shown to contain a minimum value. The trend might be
explained by a trade-off present between the effect of an increased carrier mobility and the reduction of the carrier
density in the intrinsic InP region but should be verified by quantifying both phenomena.
To electrically characterise the PhC laser devices and to verify the validity of the developed model, the IV characteristics
of a set of lasers were measured. The chip contained a set of laser devices with the same p-region offset sweep as was
simulated. The obtained results deviate from the simulation results as the series resistance decreased monotonically
with increasing p-region offsets. The influence of an increase in the carrier mobility seems to dominate the decrease
in the carrier density but further investigation is required to draw any conclusions. Furthermore, the measurements
showed that none of the devices with a p-region offset larger than 1.2µm achieved lasing, which confirms what was
previously found by other researchers at the NATEC Centre of Excellence. At last, the IV curves of a physical device and the
model were compared. The model’s turn-on voltage is higher than the theoretical and experimental results, which both
have a turn-on voltage of 0.8V. Furthermore, the physical laser device showed a much lower resistance than the model
and this is believed to be caused by the limitation of the model as its simulation region is only 2D. It fails to capture the
dopingprofile and the leakage paths throughthe InP that are present next tothe BH, bothof whichreduce the resistance.
Overall Lumerical CHARGE was able to capture some important dynamics that agree with the relevant theory and exper-
imental observations. However, it also showed some discrepancies in its ability to capture some phenomena or trends
that remained unexplained and call for further investigations.
6.2
Outlook
Important groundwork was laid in the development of a 3D Lumerical model of the HERO laser structure and some
key dynamics were captured. However, some of the fundamental underlying mechanisms behind the influence of some
material parameters remained unexplained. Therefore, a further investigation into the influence of these parameters on
the IV characteristic must be conducted.
Inanextstage,itwouldbeveryusefultolinktheinfluenceofsomeoftheimportantmodelparameterstotheparameters
of the electrical equivalent circuit of the laser. This would allow one to model the electrical characteristics through a
much simpler model, eliminating the need for full 2D simulations.
Some valuable initial observations were made in terms of leakage currents and efficiencies of the laser. However, to
