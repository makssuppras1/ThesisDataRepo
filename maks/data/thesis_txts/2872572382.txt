Introduction
In recent years, PhysicsВӯInformed Machine Learning (PIML) has emerged as a powerВӯ
ful paradigm across various scientific and engineering domains. The core idea is to enВӯ
hance dataВӯdriven models using prior physical knowledge. The promise is to allow models
to generalize better, require less data, and remain consistent with known physical laws,
making them particularly valuable for complex dynamic systems. This thesis explores two
PIML approaches.
Sparse Regression assumes that the underlying system dynamics are dictated by only
a few active terms. By exploiting this sparsity, it becomes possible to efficiently identify
compact and interpretable models that capture the essential behavior of the system.
Model Augmentation involves an existing physical model, enhanced using machine
learning techniques, typically neural networks, to account for unmodeled dynamics or
discrepancies between the model and realВӯworld behaviors. This hybrid strategy aims to
build on approximate physical models and improve their accuracy.
The main motivation for this MSc Engineering Acoustics final project arises from the chalВӯ
lenge of modeling loudspeaker systems, complex electroacoustic devices with nonlinear
and frequencyВӯdependent behavior. Rather than approaching this problem from a purely
machine learning perspective, this thesis adopts the viewpoint of an acoustical engineer.
The focus is not on extensive optimization problems or fineВӯtuning of neural networks, but
to assess and apply existing PIML methods in a practical context, identifying those that
are promising for loudspeaker modeling and parameters recovery.
The structure of this report is organized as follows:
1. The Loudspeaker Problem вҖ” This section introduces general principles of loudВӯ
speaker modeling, defines the loudspeaker system identification problem, and reВӯ
views relevant literature in both acoustics and physicsВӯinformed machine learning.
2. Sparse Regression Вӯ SINDy вҖ” This chapter presents the SINDy methodology, deВӯ
tailing its theoretical foundation and evaluating its applicability to loudspeaker sysВӯ
tems through practical experiments.
3. Neural Augmentation вҖ” Here, a hybrid modeling approach called Aphynity is
described, that combines physical models with neural networkВӯbased corrections.
Preliminary studies are conducted to assess the feasibility and performance of this
framework for loudspeaker modeling.
4. Results вҖ” This section presents the main results obtained from applying the augВӯ
mented modeling approach to loudspeaker systems.
5. Discussion вҖ” The final chapter discusses the strengths and limitations of the methВӯ
ods explored, reflects on their practical implications, and outlines potential directions
for future research.

1
The Loudspeaker Problem
1.1
The Electrodynamic Loudspeaker
A loudspeaker system is an electroВӯacoustic transducer (see Fig 1.1), converting electriВӯ
cal signals into acoustic waves through mechanical motion. The most common design for
a loudspeaker driver, called electrodynamic, uses a voice coil (electrical wire wrapped on
an axis Вӯ C) suspended in a magnetic field, attached to a diaphragm (D). When an elecВӯ
trical voltage is applied (I) to the coil, the corresponding current interacts with the passive
magnetic field (from the magnet M and pole piece P) and generates an electromotive
force, making the voice coil move back and forth on its axis. The electrical energy from
the driving signal is converted into the mechanical domain as a motion of the coil. The
voice coil is rigidly connected to a cone shaped diaphragm, which in turn vibrates back
and forth in contact to free air, producing pressure waves, hence sound. The mechaniВӯ
cal motion is transduced into the acoustical domain (A). The diaphragm is suspended by
springs (S), ensuring onВӯaxis translation and a limited excursion.
Figure 1.1: Schematic of an electrodynamic loudspeaker, from [1]
The dynamic behavior of an electrodynamic loudspeaker is influenced by a combination
of electrical, magnetic, and mechanical phenomena. Modeling and understanding the
loudspeaker dynamics is a central task in electroacoustic research: models are growing
more complex as smaller and finer physical phenomenons are included.
At low amplitudes and frequencies, the loudspeaker can be approximated by a linear
model. The common linear description is a lumpedВӯelement model.
1.1.1
Linear Model Вӯ Lumped elements
The linear equivalent circuit of a loudspeaker (see Fig 1.2) is divided in two physical doВӯ
mains, electrical and mechanical. The electrical domain can be modeled with lumped
elements: an electrical resistance Re and an inductance Le associated with the voice
coil of length l. This coil interacts with a magnetic field of strength B, which couples the
electrical domain to the mechanical system. KirchoffвҖҷs laws in the electrical loop results
in Eq 1.1.

1.2
StateВӯspace Representation
Loudspeaker linear dynamics can be described by a set of three quantities, called state
variables, arranged in a state vector X = [i, d, v]: i is the voice coil electrical current,
d is the displacement of the diaphragm and v the diaphragm velocity. Those states are
deemed observable, as they can be measured without disturbing the system behavior.
The three states [i, d, v] characterize completely a linear loudspeaker system, but also
cover simple nonlinearities. To account for more complex phenomena, the state vector X
is modified to include other physical signals.
Electrodynamic loudspeakers are usually driven using a voltage input, described in this
work as the control input uc. The control input is used to excite the loudspeaker system,
creating and sustaining a diaphragm motion. In system identification, the Klippel meaВӯ
surement system ([3]) uses broadband pink noise, at different amplitudes. For nonlinear
identification, the loudspeaker system needs to be excited out of the linear behavior area:
high levels (meaning high diaphragm displacements) and low frequencies.
An alternative approach is using initial conditions: the most experimentally accessible
method involves applying a displacement initial condition, manually displacing the diВӯ
aphragm from its rest position and releasing it to observe its free response. While this
method can reveal some aspects of the systemвҖҷs dynamics, it has notable limitations.
8

First, the resulting motion typically consists of a few oscillations before rest equilibrium,
which may not provide sufficient data for robust system identification techniques. SecВӯ
ond, the excitation level achieved through initial conditions is generally low: this method
could fail to reveal the systemвҖҷs nonlinear behaviors, such as clipping or excursion limits,
typically active at high input levels. Finally, this method makes it impossible to recover
the inductance Le linear parameter: the control input uc is required to solve for G (see
Eq. 1.5).

As the coil moves, the surrounding magnetic field changes, altering inductance Le. When
the coil moves out (d > 0), more windings are exposed to free air, resulting in a lower
magnetic flux ОҰ. Negative displacement places the coil closer to iron components, inВӯ

in Fig. 1.3 Вӯ (a), that affects electrical impedance and current. To counter this effect, conВӯ
ductive materials (shorting rings) can be placed near the coil, generating a counteracting
magnetic flux that smoothen flux variation and therefore Le(d) variation.

Force factor Bв„“(d), the electromechanical coupling factor, depends on the coilвҖҷs position
within the magnetic gap. It peaks at the rest position d = 0, where the magnetic flux
density B is the highest. Bв„“(d) decreases symmetrically as the coil moves out, as coil
windings leave the air gap. Fig. 1.3 Вӯ (b) shows the bellВӯshaped curve around d = 0:
overВӯhanging consists of using a longer coil, effectively making the number of coil windВӯ
ings in the magnetic gap constant for a longer displacement range around d = 0. The
equalВӯlength configuration creates a peak around rest position, while an overhang design
creates a plateau.
Stiffness Km(d) defines a restoring force as a function of displacement, through HookeвҖҷs
law: F = Km(d) В· d. At low amplitudes, stiffness is nearly constant, but it increases
sharply at larger displacements due to suspension stretching. For higher excursion valВӯ
ues, the spring force increases a lot for small displacements, indicating an increased
stiffness value, hence the вҖқUВӯshapeвҖқ curve displayed in Fig. 1.3 Вӯ (c).
For this research work, I will use these nonlinearities to define a simplified nonlinear model
to benchmark methods capabilities for nonlinear system identification.
1.3.2
Complex nonlinearities
In addition to the Le, Bв„“, Km displacement dependent nonlinearities, other nonlinear beВӯ
haviors are documented, with various levels of influence on the overall performance.
Please refer to [5] for a survey of loudspeaker models, extensions of the linear model
presented in Sec. 1.2.
Eddy currents arise in conductive components near the voice coil when exposed to
changing magnetic fields, introducing frequencyВӯdependent losses and reducing the efВӯ
fective inductance Le. Modeling this effect requires adding an auxiliary electrical branch to
the system, which introduces a new internal state variable i2. However, this added state
makes the implementation challenging: the i2 current is not directly observable, therefore
not included in available measurement data. Another phenomenon is the viscoelastic
behavior of the loudspeaker suspension. These materials exhibit stiffness and damping
that vary with frequency, which can be modeled using the Standard Linear Solid (SLS)
model, from mechanical science. But this model also implies an additional hidden state,
an auxiliary displacement. Finally, some models extend the displacementВӯdependent paВӯ
rameters to also include current dependence, capturing effects like magnetic saturation
and reluctance force. Researcher Cunningham covers most of known magnetic effects
in [6].
These advanced models are significantly more complex and were not included in this
thesis work. The focus here is on evaluating methods and building a framework for modВӯ
eling nonlinear loudspeaker. This work represents an earlyВӯstage exploration of modeling
techniques rather than an inВӯdepth study of advanced loudspeaker behaviors.
1.4
System Identification and Parameter Recovery
Methods will be assessed on their capability to recover physical quantities that describe
the loudspeaker system, such as linear parameters [Re, Le, Bв„“, Mm, Rm, Km] and coeffiВӯ
cients of nonlinear polynomial expansions, from states i, d, v timeВӯseries data. This task
can be defined as system identification, parameter recovery or parameter estimation.
As a starting point, let us assume that the structure of the system is known, and focus on
identifying a set of parameter values that best reproduce the observed timeВӯseries data.
This requires building a model that is interpretable, meaning it should not only fit the data
well, but also allow direct extraction of the underlying loudspeaker parameters.
11
==PAGE:13==
In contrast to blackВӯbox models, which offer little insight into their internal mechanisms,
interpretable models are designed to model the system while providing meaningful paВӯ
rameter estimates. A naive machine learning approach would involve training a model
to match the timeВӯseries data as closely as possible. However, such a blackВӯbox model
would only replicate the systemвҖҷs behavior without providing insight into its internal strucВӯ
ture or physical properties. This makes it unsuitable for the task of parameter recovery.
In this report, I will sometimes refer to this specific task of recovering interpretable loudВӯ
speaker parameters from timeВӯseries data as the loudspeaker problem.
1.4.1
Available Data
Measurement Data
For this study, I had access to lowВӯnoise measurements of electroВӯ
dynamic loudspeakers in free air, provided by Franz Heuchel and the Acoustic Technology
Group at DTU. These measurements consist of timeВӯseries data for the primary system
states [i,d,v], with the applied control input uc. This dataset could be used as a benchВӯ

Loudspeaker System Identification
Klippel Analyzer [3]
The Klippel Analyzer system estimates the parameters of a loudВӯ
speakerвҖҷs lumped model, using laserВӯbased displacement d or velocity v measurements.
This technique enables estimation of most loudspeaker parameters. The original paВӯ

of the Klippel software suite, identifying the polynomial expansions. As a stateВӯofВӯtheВӯart
method in loudspeaker system identification, the Klippel analyzer sets a benchmark for
accuracy in parameter estimation and ease of use. Common guidelines for control input
uc selection are used for other identification techniques (sample rate, pink noise input...)
A quantitative comparison of linear and nonlinear loudspeaker models [5]
This reВӯ

1.5.2
Regression Methods
DataВӯdriven regression techniques offer powerful tools for identifying and approximating
the dynamics of complex systems. These methods rely on observed inputВӯoutput data to
infer underlying structures and behaviors.
13
==PAGE:15==
Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) [10]
Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), inВӯ
troduced by Schmid in 2010 ([10]), is a dataВӯdriven method that approximates complex
system dynamics using a linear model. Dynamic modes are extracted from timeВӯseries
data, allowing for a reducedВӯorder representation of the system. Originally developed for
fluid dynamics, DMD helps identify dominant patterns in other fields today. While DMD is
useful for understanding system behavior and can inspire modeling strategies, it is limited
by design to linear modeling.
Koopman Analysis [13]
Koopman analysis extends DMD by representing nonlinear
systems through a linear operator acting on a space of observable functions. This allows
nonlinear dynamics to be studied using linear tools. While powerful in theory, finding a
practical, finiteВӯdimensional approximation of the Koopman operator remains a major chalВӯ
lenge. A comprehensive overview of recent developments in Koopman operator theory
is provided by Brunton et al. in [13].
Recent works ([17]) suggests using sparse operators to make KoopmanВӯbased models
more tractable. This motivation is shared with SINDy, a related method that directly idenВӯ
tifies sparse nonlinear models from data.
SINDy [11]
Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy), introduced in 2015
by Brunton et al. [11] is motivated by the observation that many physical systems are
governed by only a few dominant terms, making their dynamics sparse. This method uses
sparsity promoting regression to identify the simplest nonlinear equations that describe
closely the systemвҖҷs behavior. Unlike DMD, SINDy can recover fully nonlinear models.
An extended version, SINDyc [12], incorporates external inputs, making it relevant for
loudspeaker system identification with a control input uc. This makes SINDy a strong
candidate for modeling nonlinear loudspeaker dynamics, where the goal is to identify
interpretable models from inputВӯoutput data, while making use of the sparse property.
1.5.3
Neural Networks for Dynamical Systems
PhysicsВӯInformed Neural Networks (PINNs) [14]
PhysicsВӯInformed Neural Networks
(PINNs) combine dataВӯdriven learning with physical laws by embedding equations directly
into a neural network training process. A penalty is applied when the predicted soluВӯ
tions violate known governing equations or constraints. PINNs were however not impleВӯ
mented in this work primarily due to their blackВӯbox nature, which limits interpretability.
Furthermore, the complexity of PINNs, combined with my limited experience in neural
network frameworks, posed practical challenges for implementation within this project
scope. PINNs remain a promising direction for future works: their ability to bakeВӯin physiВӯ
cal knowledge could make them a powerful tool for identifying loudspeakers parameters.
Residual Modeling Вӯ APHYNITY [15, 18]
APHYNITY [15] introduces a hybrid modВӯ
eling framework where a neural network is trained to predict the residuals derivatives,
i.e. the system dynamics not captured by a physicsВӯbased model. Combining physical
models with dataВӯdriven corrections improves accuracy while maintaining interpretabilВӯ
ity. The original research [15] demonstrates how to properly define the residual: this
decomposition leads to efficient and interpretable hybrid models, demonstrated on fluid
dynamics equations. In this thesis, APHYNITY is explored as a potential method for loudВӯ
speaker system identification. While promising, adapting the framework to this context
presents two key challenges: handling control input uc and modeling multiple states sysВӯ
tems. These aspects introduce additional complexity compared to original application.

inactive terms to be relatively small, but struggles to nullify them.

2.2
Applying SINDy
For this work, SINDy methods are applied using Python package pysindy, developed by
Steven BruntonвҖҷs research group. The package is available on GitHub at https://github.
com/dynamicslab/pysindy: see [23] and [24] for further details .
2.2.1
Simulating Data
In order to identify a sparse matrix Оһ, SINDy optimizers take as input the state time series
Xt, the time derivative time series ЛҷXt, and the relevant control input uc. If ЛҷXt is not availВӯ
able, SINDy uses finiteВӯdifference methods to compute the derivative of each time series
from the state time series Xt.
As explained in Sec. 1.2, the loudspeaker system is simulated numerically. This approach
makes it easier to explore different scenarios and investigate the behavior of the SINDy alВӯ
gorithm under various conditions. To generate the simulation data, I relied on the diffrax
18
==PAGE:20==
library [8, 7], which provides a flexible and efficient framework for solving differential equaВӯ
tions in Python. Diffrax allows for the direct output of the derivatives ЛҷXt while solving
the trajectory. Unless stated otherwise, derivatives ЛҷXt are inputted in SINDy algorithms,
rather than being estimated via finiteВӯdifference.
To incorporate the timeВӯvarying control input uc(t) into the system dynamics F(X) В· X +
G(X) В· uc, I interpolated the discrete control signal uc over the time. This allows the
function Fu(X) to be only X and timeВӯdependent, enabling compatibility with the ODE
solvers. The interpolation is performed using jnp.interp, from JAX library [25], which
linearly interpolates the control input at the current time step.
The Dopri5 solver, a fifthВӯorder RungeВӯKutta method with adaptive step size control, is
used. The integration step size is adjusted using a PIDController, configured with relaВӯ
tive and absolute tolerances of 10вҲ’3 and 10вҲ’6, respectively. This adaptive control ensures
accurate and stable integration, useful given the multiple time constants present in the
electrodynamic loudspeaker system. The solver is configured to sample the solution at
fixed time intervals, with sampling frequency of 8000 Hz. The initial step size is derived
from this sampling rate and the adaptive controller allows for finer resolution when necesВӯ
sary, to capture fast transients accurately. This simulation setup is designed to produce
a clean dataset, to evaluating SINDy capabilities for modeling loudspeaker dynamics.
2.2.2
Library Selection
In the SINDy framework, the choice of library functions used to construct the feature matrix
Оҳ(Xt) is central and motivated by prior physical knowledge. The pysindy package offers
several predefined libraries, including:
вҖў Identity Library: Raw state variables, used for linear systems.
вҖў Polynomial Library: Constructs polynomial features up to a specified degree.
вҖў Fourier Library: Includes sine and cosine terms, useful for periodic behavior.
вҖў Generalized Library: Combines multiple libraries for richer representations.
For this work, I selected the Polynomial Library with various degrees for nonlinear modВӯ
els. This choice is motivated by physical knowledge of the loudspeaker system, mimicking
polynomial expansions of nonlinearities (see Sec. 1.3.1). For purely linear models, when
validating the model for example, the Identity Library was used.

This error does not account for the different scaling between coefficients

from having a finer analysis, but the overall conclusions still hold). Interestingly,
even matrices with noticeable relative error can still yield high scores, indicating
good predictive performance on test data. However, low sampling rates and small
initial displacements negatively impact identification, leading to incorrect sparsity

This analysis provides guidelines for future SINDyВӯbased investigations. Notably, the
lower bound on the sampling rate required to achieve low relative error and high score
is relatively lowНҫ a rate of fs = 8000 Hz proves more than adequate. This suggests that

large to ensure that the system dynamics are adequately excited and observable. Larger
displacements seems to enhance the optimizerвҖҷs ability to distinguish meaningful signal
from integration noise. This effect would become even more pronounced when dealing
with noisy experimental data. Future work could incorporate simulated trajectories with
added gaussian noise, emulating experimental measurement noise.

LASSO
Despite its popularity for dataВӯdriven problems, LASSO (2.4) proves to be
poorly suited for this system.
Even after tuning of the regularization parameter Оұ,
it remains difficult to achieve a sparse solution without incorrectly eliminating active
terms. Please refer to Fig 2.6 for an example identification. When Оұ is set too high,
important coefficients are masked out, leading to structurally incorrect models. Using
very small values of Оұ results in a model that fits the simulation data well, but essentially
reduces to a dense leastВӯsquares estimate, defeating the purpose of sparse identification.
As such, LASSO fails to provide a meaningful balance between sparsity and accuВӯ
racy in this context, and is not further used in this study.

2.3.6
Nonlinear Model
Initially, I intended to apply SINDy to nonlinear loudspeaker models, to evaluate its ability
to recover nonlinear dynamics and parameters. However, this direction was not pursued
further for several reasons.
The previous examples show that SINDy struggles to consistently identify linear
parameters. Moreover, the discovery of the individual regularization possibilities offer by
SR3 came relatively late in the master thesis work. As a result, there was not enough
time to properly assess its potential on nonlinear systems.
Given that SINDy already shows limited reliability and interpretability for linear models in
this context, it does not seem worthwhile to invest further effort into nonlinear extensions
at this stage. While nonlinear modeling remains an interesting direction for future work,
it would require a more robust identification framework and better knowledge on SINDy
methods to be conclusive.

are recovered or not. Remember that coefficients from the outputted F, G matrices need
to be discriminated using a known value (as they are ratios of unknown quantities) to

The results summarized in Table 2.2 show the challenges and limitations of applying the
SINDy framework to loudspeaker models. While SINDy can recover correct dynamics
in simplified linear cases, its performance is highly sensitive to the choice of optimizer,
regularization strategy, and input conditions.
Obtaining meaningful results requires
a carefully tuned regularization policy, which in turn demands the use of advanced
optimizers like SR3 that support individual thresholding.
Unfortunately, this was only
discovered late in the study, limiting its use in earlier experiments.
The original SINDy literature [11, 12] focuses on relatively simple, lowВӯdimensional
systems. Extending the method to more complex systems, such as the electrodynamic
loudspeaker, introduces significant modeling complexity and requires substantial prior
knowledge of the physical system.
Although the initial goal was to apply SINDy to measured data, the results on simВӯ
ulated data were inconclusive. Given the added challenges of noise and variability in
experimental measurements, it is likely that applying SINDy would require even more
careful tuning and a rough estimation of the loudspeaker parameters.
Interestingly,
similar challenges have been reported in other fields. Recent research applying SINDy
to biological oscillators [26] concluded that SINDy struggles with lowВӯquality experimental
data and fails to handle timeВӯscale separation in oscillatory systems. To address these
issues, the authors proposed a stepВӯbyВӯstep guide for experimentalists, covering data
preprocessing, noise filtering, and sampling strategies. Future works on applying SINDy
to loudspeaker systems data could try to apply their recommendations.
In summary, while SINDy offers an elegant and interpretable framework for sparse
model discovery, its practical application to loudspeaker system identification remains
limited.
Even in linear cases, achieving accurate and interpretable results requires
significant tuning and domain expertise.
Therefore, SINDy may be best suited as a
research tool or for exploratory analysis, rather than as a plugВӯandВӯplay solution for
acoustical engineering applications.

3
Neural Augmentation
This chapter draws methodological inspiration from the framework proposed in AugВӯ
menting Physical Models with Deep Networks for Complex Dynamics Forecasting by Yin
et al. [15], which introduces a principled approach for augmenting known models with
neural networkВӯbased augmentations. Building upon this foundation, the present thesis
work extends the Aphynity framework to address the specific challenges of loudspeaker
modeling in a system identification setting.
The specific contribution of this thesis lies in the integration of controlled dynamics
into Aphynity. Specifically, the control voltage signal uc(t) is incorporated as an additional
input to the system. This modification requires a reformulation of the training procedure,
as the control input must be consistently integrated into each iteration of the optimization
loop. The neural network is tasked to learn the nonlinear behavior of the system, that is
not explained by the known model, while also taking into account how the control input
affects the system over time.
The control input uc is incorporated alongside the three primary state variables
[i, d, v] so that each item in the training dataset D represents a complete trajectory (as
defined in Equation 1.3). This trajectory includes both the states timeВӯseries Xt and the
corresponding control input uc used to drive the loudspeaker system, applicable to both
simulated and experimental data. For a detailed explanation of how uc is integrated,
please refer to Subsection 3.3.3.
3.1
Dynamics Decomposition
This section presents the framework used to augment known loudspeaker models with
neural networks. Starting from the general form of the system dynamics in Equation 1.4,
the function Fu(X, uc) is decomposed into two distinct components:
Fu(X, uc) = Fp(X, uc) + Fa(X, uc)
(3.1)
This way, Fp and Fa are introduced to decompose the derivative operator F. Fp repreВӯ
sents the known physical model and Fa the dataВӯdriven augmentation term, obtained
from a neural network trained to complete Fp.
In Aphynity original paper [15], the method was tasked to estimate both compoВӯ
nents from observed data. The main challenge was that this decomposition is not always
unique. A naive machine learning approach could lead to the dataВӯdriven part explaining
completely the model dynamics, reducing the usefulness of the physical model in the
overall model.
To ensure an interpretable decomposition, I decided for this work to fully define the
physical model Fp, including both its structure and parameters, without subjecting it to
training. The physical model Fp will therefore know the loudspeaker parameters and
model a linear electrodynamic loudspeaker (see 1.2). This approach is supported by the
availability of highly accurate system identification tools (such as the Klippel analyzer)
which allows to reliably extract linear physical parameters from experimental data.
33
==PAGE:35==
As a result, the learning process focuses solely on the dataВӯdriven component Fa,
tasked with modeling residual dynamics not captured by the inear model Fp.
This
is formalized through an optimization problem that prioritizes the physical model in
representing the systemвҖҷs behavior, ensuring that Fa plays a strictly complementary role.
Under certain mathematical conditions, the decomposition introduced in Equation 3.1 is
guaranteed to exist and may even be unique. A detailed mathematical treatment of these
conditions can be found in the original Aphynity paper [15]. However, since this work
applies the Aphynity approach to a different system (one that includes a control input and
three state variables), it is uncertain whether the original conditions for uniqueness and
existence still hold.
In this research work, I aim to extend the Aphynity decomposition to a new system
that incorporates a control input and three state variables. While the original conditions
for uniqueness and existence of the decomposition may not directly apply to this more
complex system, this extension is considered plausible due to the prior knowledge of the
physical model Fp. Knowing Fp simplifies the learning process by focusing exclusively
on the augmentation term Fa, thereby avoiding the more challenging task of jointly
identifying both Fp and Fa.

Avoiding Finite Differences
A natural approach to solve the optimization problem in

This strategy is reminiscent of the SINDy framework.
However, such methods reВӯ

impractical for loudspeaker measurements:
data is noisy because of measurement

behavior. Mathematical demonstration can be found in original Aphynity paper [15].

Relaxation
The original optimization problem defined in Equation 3.4 imposes an
equality constraint on the trajectory loss, requiring that the simulated trajectory exactly
matches the observed data for each weight vectors Оёa solution.
Such formulation is
often impractical due to its rigidity and computational complexity. In particular, given the
initializations of Fp and Fa, an exact solution for Fa may not exist, making the problem
infeasible under strict constraints.
Therefore, it is beneficial to relax the problem by approximating the strictly conВӯ
strained formulation with a more tractable alternative, which allows for small deviations
from the exact trajectory. Lagrangian relaxations introduce a penalty when the constraint
is violated, by adding a cost component linked to the trajectoryВӯbased error. The complete
cost function is built using Lagrangian multipliers, drawing complete inspiration from
Aphynity. This way, the optimization problem becomes more flexible and easier to solve.

3.3.3
Forecasters Вӯ Model Selection
The forecaster object is created as a custom class, taking as arguments the physical
model ODE and associated parameters for the physical term Fp and a PyTorch neural
network object for the augmentation term Fa. Multiple neural networks architectures are
possible, with different depths and size. But the purpose of the forecaster remains the
same, to model the ODE for the loudspeaker model.
When properly trained, the forecaster object takes as an input an initial state vecВӯ
tor X(t = 0), a control input uc and simulates the loudspeaker system by outputting the
solution state vector X to the forecaster derivative estimator, called deriv.est. in Eq. 3.12.
This derivative estimator uses the physical and the augmentation model to compute the
derivative: a derivative estimator is a function of one state vector sample X(kвҲҶt) and of
the control input value uc(t), that returns the instant state vector derivative dX

Fp Вӯ Physical Model
The physical component Fp computes the state derivatives based
on a predefined set of model equations. If not specified otherwise, this model is the
linear electrodynamic loudspeaker, as introduced in Section 1.2. In this case, the model
parameters are known. They match the parameters used to simulate the dataset, or are
known from the measured loudspeaker datasheet, or are found through classic linear
system identification techniques.
Alternatively, Fp can incorporate nonВӯlinear models, provided they depend only on
observable states available in the dataset. For instance, implementing Fp as an Eddy
current L2R2 model would be feasible if the hidden state i2 is included in the simulation
and dataset. However, since one of the primary objectives is to apply the augmented
model to experimental data, such hidden states are typically unavailable.
A possible extension could involve incorporating known nonВӯlinearities, such as a
fully identified force factor, directly into the physical model Fp through system identificaВӯ
tion. In such a case, the augmentation network Fa should not attempt to model these
nonВӯlinearities, as they are already captured by Fp, but look for finer nonlinear behavior.

5
Discussion
Reflecting on the work presented in this report, it is important to highlight not only the
final results but also the significant intermediate steps and challenges encountered.
These intermediate studies were particularly insightful, especially given the challenges
posed by the loudspeaker modeling problem: three statesВӯmodeling, control input uc, F
structure requiring specific regularization scheme, multiscale states.
A considerable amount of time was dedicated to implementing and adapting both
modeling methods to this specific context. One of the most demanding aspects was
understanding methods and tuning hyperparameters. This was an essential task perВӯ
sonally, especially coming as an acoustics engineer to the problem. This hidden work is
unfortunately hard to report about.
5.1
SINDy Methods
The primary learning gained from working with SINDy was the realization that, although
the method shows impressive results in the literature (particularly in the original SINDy
paper [11]), its application to the loudspeaker problem revealed to be far from straightforВӯ
ward.
The motivation for using SINDy stemmed from its success on various benchmark
problems. For instance, in [32], the authors demonstrate SINDy on the Lorenz system, a
wellВӯknown nonlinear ODE (see Eq. 5.1). The Lorenz system, described as uniВӯscale, is
relatively easy to sparsify and identify using SINDy. The paper then introduces a multiВӯ
scale system by coupling two Lorenz systems with different time constants П„fast and П„slow
(see Eq. 5.2), revealing the limitations of SINDy and proposing new sampling strategies
to address them.

In our case, the loudspeaker system exhibits similar multiscale behavior.

5.2
Model Augmentation
An early conclusion from the model augmentation method is that the implemented NRMSE
loss function outperformed the default MSE loss. This adjustment to the Aphynity frameВӯ
work significantly improved the training process and model performance, particularly in
handling state variables with differing magnitudes, for an controlВӯdriven system. Another
key contribution of this work is the integration of the control input uc into the forecaster
object, enabling the model to better account for inputВӯdriven dynamics during training.

5.2.2
About NRMSE Loss
Implementing NRMSE loss helped improve the augmented model fitting. The normalВӯ
ization of each state error by its maximum value helped the model handle the different
magnitudes of i, d, and v. A comparative study in Sec. 3.4.1 shows that, on this loudВӯ
speaker example, NRMSE loss outperforms the original MSE loss.
However, this homeВӯmade loss is not properly scaled for comparison with existing literaВӯ
ture. Ideally, the loss should be 1 (or 100%) when the predicted trajectory is zero and 0
when the prediction perfectly matches the ground truth. The current formulation, being a
sum of individual NRMS errors, does not satisfy this criterion.
5.2.3
Control input uc bandwidth
In most experiments, the control input uc used when training the augmentation model
had a limited frequency bandwidth [20, 200] Hz. This decision was initially inspired by
the SINDy experiments, where restricting the input bandwidth led to better identification
results. The results presented in Sec. 4.1, using simulated trajectories, are made using
this limited bandwidth.
This is a shortcoming of the current study.
By constraining the uc range during
training, it becomes difficult to evaluate the modelвҖҷs performance outside of this bandВӯ
width. It is likely that the model will struggle to generalize beyond the input conditions it
was trained on, but I do not have results to back this claim. This concern is supported
by classical loudspeaker identification knowledge, which shows that different frequency
bands (below, around, and above the resonance frequency) are more informative for
identifying specific parameters. This frequencyВӯdependent information was not explicitly
considered in this work.
Despite this limitation, the augmented model demonstrated promising results when
applied to measurement data, as shown in Sec. 4.2. For this dataset, the wider bandВӯ
width of uc allowed to compute the transfer function across the whole frequency range
[0, 4000] Hz.
In this example, the linear model provided a coarse approximation of
the systemвҖҷs transfer function, while the augmentation network refined the prediction,
attempting to close the gap between the model and the true experimental curves. This is
an encouraging step towards a method that would work on the entire frequency range of
interest.
5.2.4
SINDy Extension
While the method introduced in Sec. 3.5 appears promising, I was ultimately unable to
extract interpretable insights from trained augmentation network Fa.
For example, to explore the interpretability of Fa, I conducted an ablation study usВӯ
ing a modified linear model Fp in which the electrical resistance was set to Re = 0 в„Ұ. The
objective was to determine whether the augmentation network could learn to recover the
missing Re term. However, this modification significantly altered the systemвҖҷs dynamics,
making it difficult for Fa to compensate effectively.
Despite several attempts, this approach did not yield conclusive results and reВӯ
mains an open direction for future work. Overcoming the blackВӯbox nature of Fa and
gaining insight into the functions it learns could greatly enhance the Aphynity framework,
making it useful not only for improving linear models but also as a tool for nonlinear
system identification.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated two approaches for the modeling and system identification
of loudspeakers: Sparse Regression via SINDy and Neural Model Augmentation via
Aphynity .
Loudspeaker systems pose significant modeling challenges due to their complex
dynamics, inputВӯdriven behavior, and parameters spanning multiple orders of magnitude.
These systems involve multiple interacting states: current (i), displacement (d), and
velocity (v)Нҫ each operating on different magnitude scales.
Applying existing PIML
methods to this context was not straightforwardНҫ adaptations were necessary to account
for the control input uc and the multiscale nature of the system.
The SINDy framework demonstrated its strength in learning simplified models from
data, enabling simulations with a reasonable degree of accuracy. Additionally, combined
with custom regularization patterns, SINDy allowed to infer of governing equations and
recover loudspeaker lumped parameters.
However, when applied to the fullВӯsystem
identification of loudspeakers, the method proved insufficient. The complexity and scale
of the system exceeded SINDyвҖҷs capabilities, limiting its effectiveness in this context
In contrast, the Aphynity framework showed promise by reducing modeling error
through the augmentation of physical models with neural networks. Although the hybrid
model achieved lower prediction errors, the internal workings of the neural network
remained difficult to interpret. Attempts to extract meaningful dynamics from the learned
corrections were unsuccessful. Nonetheless, key contributions such as integration of the
control input and the development of a custom error metric significantly improved training
capabilities. Future work should focus on normalizing the error metric, refining training
hyperparameters, and incorporating more diverse measurements to improve the modelвҖҷs
robustness and modeling fidelity.
As a personal conclusion, this project has been challenging yet rewarding.
I gained
valuable insights into two advanced modeling techniques that were previously unfamiliar
to me.
While it was disappointing that SINDy did not perform as expected based
on existing literature, the findings and suggestions outlined in the discussion chapter
provide a solid foundation for future improvements. On the other hand, Aphynity appears
promising for further development and practical application in loudspeaker modeling.
In summary, I believe that this master thesis contributes to the growing intersection of
acoustics and machine learning by offering practical understandings for application of
PIML methods for loudspeaker modeling and parameter recovery.
