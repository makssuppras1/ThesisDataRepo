Abstract  
 
Hydroxycinnamic acids (HCAs) such as ferulic acid (FA) are dominant in plant cell walls 
(PCWs). Ferulic acid esterases (FAEs) catalyse the hydrolysis of ester bonds between HCAs 
and the hemicellulose structure. FAEs have recently received much attention due to their use 
in industrial biotransformations, such as converting plant biomass to biofuels. To allow a 
detailed biochemical characterization of FAEs, access to defined substrates is of key 
importance. In biomass, FA is often crosslinked (dehydrodimers) and certain FAEs may be 
specialized towards the hydrolysis of different dimeric substrates. 
This thesis investigates different methods for the chemical synthesis of ferulic acid dimers 
(diFAs). Specifically, five out of the nine naturally occurring diFAs were selected to be of special 
interest for this project.  
5,5’-diFA has previously been synthesized at Novozymes (NZ) via an inorganic oxidation 
approach, starting from vanillin. This substrate was oxidized with FeSO4/Na2S2O8, after which 
both phenols were protected before performing an aldol condensation to obtain the three-
carbon side chain of FA. In this project, the synthesis was optimized and 5,5’-diFA was 
synthesized in four steps with an overall yield of 28%. The enzymatic coupling of vanillin with 
horseradish peroxidase (HRP) and H2O2 was also successful and afforded 5,5’-divanillin with 
a yield of 89%.  
This synthesis of 5,5’-diFA was supplemented with the synthesis of 8,5’-diFA in both the linear 
and benzofuran (Bzf) forms. These two were prepared via an enzymatic oxidation approach, 
using once again HRP and H2O2 but starting from ethyl ferulate (EF). This two-step synthesis 
was followed by a separation of the two isomers by preparative HPLC. The linear form was 
obtained in a 4% yield while the Bzf form was made with an overall yield of 27%. 
Additionally, the synthesis of 8,8’-diFA was attempted starting from FA. Unfortunately, both 
inorganic (FeSO4/Na2S2O8) and enzymatic (HRP – H2O2) oxidations of FA were unsuccessful. 
In an alternative approach, it was tried to use a different substrate for the oxidative 
dimerization: EF with the 5-proton substituted by the bulky tert-butyl (t-Bu) substituent, blocking 
the coupling at this position. This was pursued successfully until the fourth intermediate, which 
is only three steps away from 8,8’-diFA.  
Moreover, synthesis of the last diFA in focus for this project, 8-O-4’-diFA, was tried with 
inorganic conditions (Ag2O) for the oxidative dimerization. However, it led selectively to 8,5’-
coupling instead.  
In a second phase of the project, electrochemical oxidation was investigated with the goal to 
form a shortcut to the 8-coupled dimers, including the two missing ones: 8,8’-diFA and 8-O-4’-
diFA. A range of conditions were screened and indeed different diFAs were formed using this 
approach, but unfortunately the low amounts obtained did not allow further characterization of 
the isomers prepared. The electrochemical reactions were performed in an undivided cell, with 
glassy carbon as the working electrode (WE) and platinum as the counter electrode (CE). 
Among the different conditions investigated, the best results were obtained by potentiostatic 
oxidation of EF 0.1 M in acetonitrile (MeCN), with lithium perchlorate as the electrolyte and a 
stoichiometric amount of base, at 1.4 V.   
In conclusion, out of the five diFAs in focus for this project, three were successfully synthesized 
and pathways for the other two have been investigated, enabling an overview of the possible 
directions to pursue.

Resume  
 
Hydroxycinnaminsyrer (HCA'er) såsom ferulsyre (FA) er dominerende i plantecellevægge. 
Ferulsyreesteraser (FAE) katalyserer hydrolysen af esterbindinger mellem HCA'er og 
hemicellulosestrukturen. FAE'er har for nylig modtaget meget opmærksomhed på grund af 
deres anvendelse i industrielle biotransformationer, såsom konvertering af plantebiomasse til 
biobrændstoffer. For at muliggørre en detaljeret biokemisk karakterisering af FAE'er er adgang 
til definerede substrater af afgørende betydning. I biomasse er FA ofte krydsbundet 
(dehydrodimerer), og visse FAE'er kan være specialiserede i hydrolyse af forskellige dimere 
substrater. 
I dette projekt undersøges forskellige metoder til kemiske syntese af ferulinsyredimerer 
(diFA'er). Specifikt blev fem ud af de ni naturligt forekommende diFA'er valgt til at være af 
særlig interesse for dette projekt. 
5,5'-diFA er tidligere syntetiseret ved Novozymes via en uorganisk oxidationsmetode, 
startende fra vanillin, som blev oxideret med jernsulfat og natriumpersulfat, hvorefter begge 
phenoler blev beskyttet inden en aldolkondensation gav 3-carbon-sidekæden af FA. I dette 
projekt blev syntesen optimeret, og 5,5'-diFA blev syntetiseret i fire trin med et samlet udbytte 
på 28%. Alternativt, kan vanillin dimeriseres med peroxidase og hydrogenperoxid hvilket gav 
5,5'-divanillin i et udbytte på 89%. 
Denne syntese af 5,5'-diFA blev suppleret med syntesen af 8,5'-diFA i både den lineære og 
benzofuran formen. Disse to blev fremstillet via en enzymatisk oxidationsmetode under 
anvendelse af peroxidase og hydrogenperoxid igen, men startende fra ethylferulat. Denne 
totrinssyntese blev efterfulgt af en adskillelse af de to isomerer ved præparativ HPLC. Den 
lineære form blev opnået i et 4% udbytte, medens benzofuran-formen blev fremstillet med et 
samlet udbytte på 27%.  
Syntese af 8,8'-diFA blev forsøgt med udgangspunkt i FA. Desværre var både uorganisk (jern 
sulfat og natriumpersulfat) og enzymatisk (peroxidase - hydrogenperoxid) oxidation af FA ikke 
succesfulde. I en alternativ fremgangsmåde blev det forsøgt at anvende et andet substrat til 
den oxidative dimerisering, ethylferulat med 5-protonen substitueret med en tert-
butylsubstituent, hvilket blokerer koblingen i denne position. Dette blev forfulgt med succes 
indtil det fjerde intermediat, som kun er tre trin fra 8,8'-diFA. 
Syntesen af den sidste diFA i fokus for dette projekt, 8-O-4'-diFA, blev forsøgt med uorganiske 
betingelser (med sølvoxid) til den oxidative dimerisering. Dette førte imidlertid selektivt til 8,5'-
koblingen. 
I en anden fase af projektet blev elektrokemisk oxidation undersøgt med det formål at 
syntetisere de 8-koblede dimere med en kortere rute, inklusiv de to manglende: 8,8'-diFA og 
8-O-4'-diFA. En række betingelser blev screenet, og forskellige diFA'er blev dannet ved hjælp 
af denne fremgangsmåde, men desværre tillader de lave mængder, der blev opnået, ikke 
yderligere karakterisering af de fremstillede isomerer. De elektrokemiske reaktioner blev udført 
i en udelt celle med ”glassy carbon” som arbejdselektroden og platin som modelektrode. Blandt 
de forskellige afprøvede betingelser blev de bedste resultater opnået med potentiostatisk 
oxidation af ethylferulat 0,1 M i acetonitril, med lithiumperchlorat som elektrolytten og en 
støkiometrisk mængde base, ved 1,4 V. 
Sammenfattende blev tre af de fem diFA'er, der er i fokus for dette projekt syntetiseret. 
Samtidig er veje for syntese af de to andre er undersøgt, hvilket afstikker retninger for fremtidigt 
arbejde.

1. Introduction  
 
1.1 
Background, biology and applications 
 
1.1.1 Ferulic acid and its dimers 
 
Hydroxycinnamic acids (HCAs) including ferulic acid (FA) 1 shown in Figure 1, are a class of 
aromatic acids containing a C6-C3 skeleton. The C6 moiety is a benzene ring while the C3 one 
is an unsaturated side chain [1]. In this project, the focus is on FA 1 - which has a tri-substituted 
phenol ring, with the important functionalities highlighted in red in Figure 1 – and more 
specifically its dimers. The numbering of the carbons that will be used throughout this work is 
also introduced on the structure. 
 
 
Figure 1-Ferulic acid (FA) 1 and the carbon numbering used throughout this project 
Ferulic acid dimers (diFAs) are products of the polymerization of FA 1. Several diFAs can be 
formed depending on which radicals are involved in the oxidative coupling. Indeed, oxidative 
dimerization occurs via the formation of a phenoxy radical 2. The single electron of 2 is 
delocalized and stabilized by resonance, as illustrated in Figure 2, leading to various coupling 
modes, which will be further detailed in section 2.1 [2]. Higher polymerization of FA 1 is not 
that likely because it would require a dimer to be close enough to another ferulate to further 
couple. This might however happen at the ends of the chains [3].  
 
 
Figure 2- Ferulic acid 1 oxidation leading to a radical 2 stabilized by resonance 
FA 1 is an extremely abundant and nearly ubiquitous phenolic cinnamic acid derivative. It 
occurs commonly in fruits, vegetables, grains, beans, leaves, seeds, nuts, grasses, flowers, 
and other types of vegetation. FA 1 is found in the PCW varying from 1.5 g/kg in the spice 
turmeric to 0.05 g/kg in refined wheat, for example [4]. However, in plants, FA 1 is rarely found 
in free form but is often crosslinked to various carbohydrates i.e. it is covalently bonded as side 
chains to molecules such as arabinoxylans (at the carbon-5 position of α-L-arabinofuranoside 
moieties), as illustrated with the example of 3 in Figure 3. The focus in this project is on the 
esters but the FA crosslinks can occur as amides as well. Dimerization of such ferulate esters 
provides a pathway for cross-linking polysaccharide chains [2][4].  
==PAGE:11==
 
2 
 
 
Figure 3- 5,5’-diFA linked to Me α-L-Araf, a FAE substrate found in nature 
NZ has expressed an interest in synthesizing diFAs, the purpose being to use them as 
substrates for assaying FAEs. FAE is a subclass of carboxylic acid esterases, which catalyse 
the hydrolysis of ester bonds between HCAs and xylans. In nature, FA 1 is released after 
enzymatic hydrolysis, i.e. when FAE breaks the ester bond with the lignin part.  
1.1.2 Applications of FA dimers  
 
The interest for FA 1 and its dimers has increased considerably in the last decade due to their 
bioactive properties such as antioxidant, anti-mutagenic, anti-viral and anti-inflammatory 
activities, which make them the subject of intensive research in the pharmaceutical industry 
[5]. These compounds are valuable precursors for the production of biochemicals used in the 
cosmetic and food industries as well [6].  
 
Furthermore, the cross-linking of FA 1 in biomass represents a biochemical mechanism for 
controlling the mechanical properties of cell walls [7]. FA 1 endows a structural rigidity and 
strengthens the cell wall architecture by cross-linking molecules such as arabinoxylans, 
making these components less susceptible to hydrolytic enzymes during germination [8]. The 
presence of FA cross-links reduces the cell wall digestibility of grass forages as it hinders its 
degradation by ruminant microbes or bacteria. Thus, diFAs are important structural 
components in the PCW [13][23][10]. Both the rate and extent of cell wall hydrolysis appear to 
decrease with an increased concentration of diferulate cross links [10]. Considerable evidence 
therefore suggests that FA 1 and diFAs are involved in regulating plants’ growth and control 
the PCW extensibility [4]. However, the FAEs enzymes can increase the PCW accessibility for 
other enzymes, by breaking the lignin cross-links (hydrolysing the ester linkages, and thus 
releasing FA 1 and diFAs) [11][12].  
 
Moreover, in recent years, there has been an increased interest in renewable bioresources as 
alternatives to fossil fuels for energy and material production. The lignocellulosic biomass 
sources such as grasses are a potential feedstock to produce biofuels and chemicals [8]. 
However, the complex array of phenolic crosslinks in the cell wall inhibits the grasses 
conversion to biofuels [13]. Thus, one of the many biotechnological applications of FAEs is to 
improve lignocellulosic biomass conversion to biofuel (mainly second-generation bioethanol). 
It occurs via enzymatic hydrolysis and therefore controlled reduction of FA level or FA cross-
linkage in plants of industrial interest. This way, FAEs help to achieve the goals set for cost 
effective production of alternative and renewable fuels and chemicals [25][26].

1.1.3 Different isomers 
 
Dehydrodimerization of the ferulate esters forms stable ether and carbon-carbon bonds [13]. 
From an organic synthesis perspective, the carbon-carbon bonds are more easily and 
frequently formed than the ether ones, as will be explained later, in section 2.1.  
 
There are currently nine known diFAs 4-12, illustrated in Figure 4, named after the position on 
each monomer that forms the bond between the two. Two of these dimers are however not 
topological isomers of diFAs but additional chemical forms. Indeed, 8,5'-diFA decarboxylated 
form (dc) 7 lost CO2 during its formation while 8,8'-diFA tetrahydrofuran form (THF) 10 gained 
H2O during the process. They are however both believed to possess a similar biological 
function as the topological isomers [2][13][3].  
 
Figure 4- The nine known ferulic acid dimers 4-12 
FA 1 can also form trimers and tetramers, known as triferulic and tetraferulic acids, 
respectively. This report will however only focus on the dimers found in biomass, and most 
specifically five of them, identified as specifically interesting for this project, namely:

1.1.4 Abundance of the dimers in nature 
 
Among the nine known dimers which structures are shown in Figure 4, 8-O-4'-diFA 11 tends 
to dominate in grasses while 5,5'-diFA 4 dominates in wheat bran and is one of the most 
abundant one in other types of cereals. However, the latter is not the only major one, unlike 
what was previously believed [2][16]. Indeed, 5,5'-diFA 4 was the first diFA discovered, and for 
a while this isomer was thought to be the only one existing [17]. Prior to the finding from Ralph 
et al. [2], the presence of only 5,5’-diFA 4 had been acknowledged. This is surprising since 
according to Ralph et al., the amounts of other diFAs obtained from saponified plant materials 
were higher than that of 5,5’-diFA 4 [2].  
 
In the literature, various proportions have been reported for the different dimers. For example, 
according to Mathew and co-workers, upon saponification of the cell walls, five diFAs were 
released, in the order of abundance: 8-O-4’-diFA 11, 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6, 5,5’-diFA 4, 8,5’-diFA 5 
and 8,8’-diFA aryltetralin form 9 (which is not in focus here) [10][18]. Larsen and co-workers 
then reported the same most common dimers, with in addition 8,8’-diFA 8 [19]. Neudorffer et 
al. reported these diFAs as well, but they also suggested that 5,5’-diFA 4 was less abundant 
[20]. However, the authors seems to agree upon the fact that 4-O-5’-diFA 12 is not common in 
grasses and cereal fibers since coupling at the 4-O position is not favoured among the three 
possibilities [9]. Finally, as a wrap-up, from nature extracts, 8-O-4’-diFA 11 appears as the 
most abundant, and 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6 the second most common diFA. Regarding the 8,5’-
diferulate, only the Bzf form 6 is found naturally in the wall while the linear one arises after 
hydrolysis [13]. More recently, Underlin et al. reported that, in nature, the most common diFAs 
esterified to PCW polysaccharides are 8,8’-diFA THF 10 and cyclic forms 9, 8,5’-diFA 5, 5,5’-
diFA 4 and 8-O-4’-diFA 11. In this recent study, the release of 5,5’-diFA 4 was the most 
prevalent and it was much higher than that of 8-O-4’-diFA 11, which was hardly released. This 
is somewhat different than the usual reported proportions, but it can vary depending on the 
plant considered, the enzyme used to release the dimers, etc. This finding however suggests 
that the structure of 8-O-4’-diFA 11 is less accessible for FAEs compared to 5,5’-diFA 4 – either 
due to the catalytic site structure or to the substrate inaccessibility [11].  
 
Furthermore, it is interesting to investigate what kind of control could explain the regioselectivity 
in the PCW. It was reported that guiding proteins could be involved in the lignin biosynthesis 
[21]. In vivo, different enzymes are indeed believed to be responsible for guiding the outcome 
of the bimolecular radical process [20].  
 
1.1.5 Dimerization in plants 
 
In vivo, peroxidases catalyse the oxidation of FA 1, resulting in the formation of diFAs. 
However, some literature reports also refer to a possible light induced oxidation in nature [10]. 
Peroxidases are found in multiple forms in cell walls, which makes them candidates for the 
catalysis of the dehydrogenative dimerization of FA 1 esterified to arabinoxylans. They can not 
only generate the reactive (free radical) intermediates of ester-linked FA but also the hydrogen 
peroxide needed to achieve this process from various hydrogen donors. Peroxidases catalyse 
==PAGE:14==
 
5 
 
the phenoxy radical’s formation by proton transfer and electron abstraction from the phenol. 
Then, radicals are coupled during the lignification process, and carbon-carbon or ether bonds 
are formed, resulting in dimers of the phenolic substrates [22][23]. The catalysis with 
peroxidases in nature has inspired its broad use for the biomimetic oxidative coupling to 
prepare the dimers by chemical synthesis from commercial compounds. In this case, the 
presence of hydrogen peroxide is required to induce radical polymerization, as will be 
described in section 2.2 [24].  
 
Different mechanisms of esterification of cell wall polysaccharides and phenolics have been 
proposed, both intracellularly and extracellularly. According to Adams et al., the intracellular 
feruloylation is limited to 8,5’-diFAs (5 and 6), while the other dimeric linkages might be 
incorporated extracellularly, via the action of peroxidases [25]. However, other reports claimed 
that only 5,5’-diFA 4 can form intramolecularly. This would explain the low amount of this dimer 
formed in vitro compared to its prevalence in some PCW where the ferulates’ positions can 
favour this coupling [3][9]. Thus, peroxidase-catalysed dimerization of wall ferulate upon 
hydrogen peroxide addition, yields mostly 8-coupled products [9].  
 
Most of the previously mentioned diFAs are not commercially available and must be 
synthesised in the laboratory. Several synthetic routes have been published in the literature, 
but these dimers have also been widely obtained after saponification of extracted plant 
materials. This method often appears to be more straightforward than the chemical synthesis 
from commercial FA 1 - or analogues such as vanillin 13 or EF 14, which structures are 
represented in Figure 5. Moreover, there is limited availability of authentic standards of the 
diFAs which makes their recognition and quantification non-routine. The extracts resulting from 
the treatment of PCW often are mixtures of FA moieties (including diFAs) that can be separated 
and identified by high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) with a ultraviolet (UV) 
detector or by liquid chromatography - mass spectrometry (LC-MS) [26]. However, HPLC has 
been reported to lack selectivity in this case as phenolic compounds may co-elute.  
 
1.2 
Synthesis from the literature 
 
As mentioned in section 1.1, among the nine known dimers shown in Figure 4, this work 
primarily focused on five: compounds 4-6, 8 and 11. The reason for this choice is that these 
five diFAs are the ones that will be useful for DTU Bioengineering in order to characterise the 
FAEs. Therefore, this section will mainly describe how the synthesis of these five dimers has 
been carried out previously, in the literature. 
 
In order to prepare the diFAs, different starting materials (SM) can be used and subjected to 
oxidative dimerization. Indeed, in the literature, some syntheses started directly from FA 1, 
while others used vanillin 13 [27] (mainly for the formation of 5,5’-diFA 4 or to make an 
analogue with a specific substitution pattern and block specific coupling paths, as will be 
described in section 1.2.3), EF 14 [28] or methyl ferulate (MF) 15 [29], shown in Figure 5. 
These reagents are commercially available, but the ester derivatives can also be synthesized 
in the laboratory from trans-FA 1. [28] Regarding vanillin 13, it is important to note that the 
reverse approach is also possible since it can be synthesized from FA extracted from 
agricultural by-products (or saponified PCW). This is one of the many applications of the HCA 
1, due to the low production cost [10][15].

John Ralph has been a main contributor to the field of diFAs and their synthesis, due to an 
interest in lignin biosynthesis and the improvement of its degradability. In 1994, together with 
his co-workers, he both synthesized and identified in saponified extracts of cell walls of various 
plants seven isomeric dehydrodimers of FA 1 [2]. These diFAs are the result of naturally 
occurring oxidative coupling of ferulate esters in cell walls. The dimerization can occur following 
two different mechanisms: either oxidative coupling or photochemically induced [2+2]-
cyclodimerization [2][10]. However, [2+2]-cyclodimers are not predominant when taking into 
consideration all isomers [2]. Prior to the findings from Ralph et al. [2], only 5,5’-diFA 4 had 
been identified in nature and prepared in the laboratory in a rather simple way. Thus, this paper 
was the first proposal of a synthesis of all seven isomers, and it was then widely used in the 
literature as inspiration for other syntheses. For example, Russell et al. worked on the 
syntheses of 5,5’-, 8,5’- and 8,8’-diFAs (4,  5 and 8) [30], using similar approaches and 
repeating some of the steps from Ralph et al. [2][28]. 
 
In the literature, diFAs have previously been synthesized via the oxidative coupling of different 
substrates (FA 1, vanillin 13, methyl 15 or ethyl 14 ferulate) following two approaches: the 
single-electron oxidation with oxidant systems such as FeSO4-Na2S2O8 or the oxidation of the 
same substrates using an enzymatic system such as HRP–H2O2 [2].  Some reports also 
describe electrochemical coupling strategies [11][29] and the photocycloaddition [34][35].  
 
Unlike the respective abundances of the different dimers in nature mentioned in section 1.1, 
from the in vitro synthesis, the main dimer obtained is 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6, both when using 
enzymatic conditions or single-electron oxidants [3][11][15][28]. For instance, Ralph et al. [2] 
reported that their enzymatic or metallic single-electron oxidations of ferulate esters were not 
successful to form 5,5’-diFA 4. The 8,5’-coupling was obtained instead, in a 55% yield. 
Oxidative dimerization thus leads predominantly to 8-coupled dimers especially the 8,5’-ones 
(5 and 6) but 8-O-4’-diFA 11 and 8,8’-diFA 8 had also been identified that way.  
 
Similar findings have been reported with related compounds. Indeed, according to Sarkanen 
et al. [36], the major coupling obtained with a similar phenol, (E)-isoeugenol 16, was the 8,5’- 
one in a 65% yield compared to 22% for the 8-O-4’- product and 13% for the 8,8’- one. The 
oxidative dimerization was therein performed both with the HRP–H2O2 enzymatic oxidant 
system and K3[Fe(CN)6] inorganic oxidant. The same major coupling resulted from oxidation 
of EF 14 with alkaline K3[Fe(CN)6] in a benzene-water two-phase system. Indeed, the products 
were diethyl 8,5’-diferulate (Bzf) 17 in 84% yield and 6% of diethyl 8,8’-diferulate 18. However, 
with the (Z) isomer of 16, the main dimer formed was the 8-O-4’- one, probably because with 
this stereochemistry of the double bond, the approach of the carbon-8 to the oxygen atom was 
less hindered than with the other isomer.  
 
Thus, the 8-coupling is usually described as the major one. It is not only the case with the 
carboxylic acids substrates but also with phenolic amides such as feruloyltyramine 19, for 
which the inorganic oxidation with FeCl3 or chiral Cu(II)-α(-)phenylethylamine complex and the 
enzymatic oxidation (HRP-H2O2) were reported to lead mainly to 8-coupled dimers as a mixture 
of 8,8’-cyclic form and 8,5’-Bzf coupled products [37][38].

However, according to Neudorffer and co-workers, from methods used in vitro, coupling at the 
8-position is predominant but the products’ distribution depends on both the oxidant and the 
solvent used [20]. With EF 14 and the enzymatic HRP-H2O2 system, 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6 was the 
main dimer isolated [14] [27] while when using a surfactant such as a quaternary ammonium 
salt in water, a mixture of 8,8’- (8), 8,5’- (5 and 6) and 5,5’- (4) dimers was formed [19]. 
Furthermore, when performing single-electron chemical oxidations with for instance Ag2O in 
acetone-benzene, 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6 was either reported to be the sole product [39] or obtained 
together with a minor amount of 8-O-4’-diFA 11 [29], as will be further detailed in section 1.2.5. 
 
In addition to the commonly used enzyme HRP [27][40], laccase has also been reported to

Lignin peroxidase (LIP) was also used for FA 1 polymerization. Kersten et al. showed that LIP 
is a stronger oxidant than HRP or laccase [41]. From the initial steps, the major products were 
dehydrodimers resulting from 8,5’- and 8,8’-coupling. However, these products are further 
oxidized by LIP and thus only represent intermediates in the polymerization of FA 1. Indeed, 
two trimers obtained upon addition of FA 1 to the decarboxylated forms of 8-O-4’- and 8,5’-
diFAs have also been identified [6].  
 
Previous reports claimed that different peroxidases catalyse the oxidation of FA 1 in different 
ways [4]. However, Ward et al. suggested that it might be a misconception due to an 
incomplete characterization of all reaction products. Indeed, their finding proved that the 
coupling step is non-enzymatic, which was also supported by other authors [6] and will be 
discussed further in section 2.2.

1.2.1  Synthesis of 5,5’-diFA 4 
 
This section will focus on different strategies reported in the literature for the synthesis of 5,5’-
diFA 4.  
5,5’-diFA 4 is, as mentioned in section 1.1.4, one of the most abundant diFAs isolated from 
biomass. It was commercially available from Rare Chemicals GmbH but is rather costly (1 g 
for 2990 Euro, per quotation from 2017-03-01).  
The first chemical synthesis reported for this first discovered diFA seems to be the one 
performed by Richitzenhain in 1949 in four steps [42]. This procedure relied mainly on the 
enzymatic oxidation of vanillin 13 to 5,5’-divanillin 30 in a peroxidase – H2O2 system and was 
also reported by Baumgartner et al. [43]. However, according to Yamamoto et al., this 
synthesis was often not reproducible and gave only low amounts of product. The unsuitability 
for a large-scale experiment was due to the poor water solubility of vanillin 13, the difficulty of 
controlling the unstable enzymatic reaction and the long reaction time (two weeks) of the 
elongation of two carbon units on the aldehyde by the Knoevenagel reaction [44]. Yamamoto 
et al. thus described a more convenient preparation of 5,5’-diFA 4 with the same SM, in two 
days which represents a significant improvement. The method involved the single-electron 
oxidation (inorganic approach) of vanillin 13 with a stoichiometric amount of FeCl3 (oxidation 
ortho to the phenol group) forming 5,5’-divanillin 30, followed by a Perkin reaction with acetic 
anhydride and sodium acetate. The two phenol groups of the substrate were not even 
protected prior to the Perkin reaction, unlike in other procedures [27].  
 
On the other hand, direct oxidation of FA 1 failed to give the desired 5,5’-coupled product 
whether it was with peroxidase – H2O2 [42] or FeCl3 [44]. This suggests that the carboxylic acid 
group needs to be esterified to a sugar moiety for the peroxidase mediated cross linkage to 
occur in the PCW.  
 
A few years later, the enzymatic coupling of vanillin 13 using the HRP–H2O2 system was again 
reported by Russell et al. [30]. The overall yield for the preparation of 5,5’-diFA 4 in this study 
was 51%, which shows an improvement compared to the 36% from Yamamoto et al. [44]. The 
synthesis of 5,5’-divanillin 30 from vanillin 13 was also described by Ruffin et al., using FeSO4-
K2S2O8 [45]. Additionally, the synthesis of 5,5’-diFA 4 from vanillin 13 was once again reported 
via an oxidative dimerization performed with FeSO4-Na2S2O8, which was also followed by the 
Perkin reaction to form the diacid [27], similarly as with Yamamoto et al. [44].

1.2.2 Synthesis of 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6 and 8,5’-diFA 5 
 
This section will focus on different strategies reported in the literature for the synthesis of 8,5’-
diFA in both the linear 5 and Bzf 6 forms.  
Ralph et al. first reported the synthesis of the 8,5’-coupled dimer with Ag2O as a single-electron 
oxidant, but the product separation was not trivial [2]. Therefore, a few years later, Ralph et al.  
found a more straightforward preparation of the 8,5’-coupled diferulate 17 switching to an 
enzymatic approach with the biomimetic HRP–H2O2 system [28]. However, in this paper, the 
synthesis was stopped at the diethyl ester form of 8,5’-diFA 17 – which possesses a Bzf ring 
– and did not go all the way to the diacids 5 and 6. This ester 17 can undergo a saponification 
reaction which would at least partially open the benzofuran ring at the same time as the esters’ 
hydrolysis [2]. Therefore, a mixture of 8,5’-diFA 5 and 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6 would result. Then, 
Russell et al. repeated the enzymatic 8,5’-diFAs (5 and 6) synthesis from EF 14 [30].  
 
Xia et al. also performed a biomimetic oxidative coupling of an ester of FA 1, but this time the 
methyl ester 15, with Ag2O in a mixture of dry acetone and toluene forming the dimethyl 8,5‘-
diferulate Bzf 31. This dimer was then treated with base to open the benzofuran ring and 
hydrolyse the esters to afford the 8,5’-diFAs (5 and 6) as well [39]. The method was similar to 
the one from Rakotondramanana et al. [29] except that Xia et al. did not report the minor 8-O-
4’-diFA’s 11 formation.  
 
Simple preparations of 8,8’- and 8-O-4’-diFAs (8 and 11) took longer to be discovered since 
the ones described by Ralph et al. [2] were rather long and complex.

5,5’- and 8,5’-couplings), such as the t-Bu group. This strategy forced the oxidative coupling 
to undergo an otherwise unfavorable pathway [48].  
 
Li et al. then repeated the synthesis of 8,8’-diFA 8 starting from 5-t-Bu EF 33 with a 53% overall 
yield. The key step of the reaction was the oxidative coupling using inorganic conditions: 
alkaline K3[Fe(CN)6] in a benzene-water two-phase system [47]. The desired t-Bu substituted 
diethyl 8,8’-diferulate 34 was obtained in excellent yield. The t-Bu protecting group was 
therefore once again effective in eliminating the undesired phenolic coupling modes. It can 
then be removed via the method reported by Tashiro et al. [49].  
 
Additionally, it is important to note that besides the lack of regioselectivity, there is also a risk 
of further cyclization with the linear 8,8’-coupled product. Li et al. realized that all the relevant 
reactions previously reported in the literature were carried out in acidic (FeCl3 and

can produce the linear 8 and cyclic 8,8’-diFAs 9 under alkaline and acidic conditions, 
respectively. They found that the linear one 8 is unstable under acidic conditions and therefore 
rearranges as the cyclic dimer 9, hence their choice of carrying out the coupling in alkaline 
conditions, with potassium ferricyanide as the oxidant [47]. The likely ring closure mechanism

1.2.5 Mixture of different dimers  
 
Multiple other literature reports describe syntheses that are not regioselective dimerizations 
and result in mixtures of diFAs in varying proportions depending on the conditions employed. 
Moreover, in this section, new approaches such as the electrochemical coupling and the use 
of surfactants to favour the formation of some dimers over others will be described.  
==PAGE:22==
 
13 
 
 
Chioccara et al. studied the oxidation of MF 15 using both inorganic and enzymatic conditions 
and found that it mostly led to dimethyl 8,5’-diferulate 31 together with a relatively small amount 
of an intermediate to dimethyl 8-O-4’-diferulate 44. The two catalytic systems used were: the 
enzymatic HRP-H2O2 and the synthetic tetraphenylporphyrinatomanganese(III) acetate or 
chloride (MnTPPX, X=OAc or Cl) – iodosylbenzene or H2O2 (used to mimic the enzymatic 
reaction). In the case of the enzymatic approach, the regioselectivity of the reactions was 
claimed not to be determined by the enzymatic radical formation but by the following non-
enzymatic coupling [22][6].  
 
Another interesting strategy for the regioselective dimerization of FA 1 has been introduced by 
Larsen et al. [19], in one step from FA 1. As described by Nakamura et al. [33][34], quaternary 
ammonium or amine N-oxide surfactants can control the photodimerization of cinnamic acids 
by forming large aggregates such as micelles in aqueous solutions. This study investigated 
whether the regioselective control with surfactants could also work with the oxidative coupling 
of FA 1. A micellar solution was used, and different dimers were obtained in varying proportions 
depending on the surfactant. With an aqueous solution of quaternary ammonium salts with a 
long aliphatic chain, micelles appear and organize themselves with the ionic part towards the 
outer sphere. Therefore, the regioselectivity of the oxidative coupling with the biomimetic HRP-
H2O2 system can be controlled via attachment of the carboxylic acids to these micelles – with 
the acidic part pointing inward. Depending on whether a surfactant was used or not and which 
one, mixtures of 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6, 8,8’-diFA 8 and 5,5’-diFA 4 were obtained in varying ratios.  
 
Furthermore, Rakotondramanana et al. described a similar procedure as the one from Xia et 
al., relying on the inorganic oxidative coupling of MF 15 with Ag2O as the oxidant, in a 
toluene/acetone mixture (2:1). The reactions conditions were different from those described by 
Xia et al. since therein the toluene/acetone ratio was 3:2 and the stirring under N2 atmosphere 
proceeded for 30 h at -20 °C compared to 8 h at r.t. here [39]. The oxidative dimerization gave 
two products: the dimethyl ester of the known 8,5’-diFA Bzf 31 and the dimethyl ester of 8-O-
4’-diFA 44. The mixture (50% yield) was then purified by flash chromatography after which the 
dimers were separated by preparative HPLC. The major 8,5’-coupled product 31 was obtained 
in a 45% yield compared to 5% for the minor 8-O-4’ dimer 44. Despite the low yield, this 
approach remains of interest since very few procedures have been reported for the preparation 
of 8-O-4’-diFA 11 [29]. If the desired products are the diacids, further saponification of the 
purified diferulates produced would be needed to hydrolyse both methyl esters to carboxylic 
acids – and probably partially open the benzofuran ring of the 8,5’-coupled product - and thus 
form the diFAs 11, 6 and 5 [2][22].   
 
Most of the syntheses based on the enzymatic method reported in the literature and mentioned 
in the previous subsections required the HRP catalyst, but other enzymes have also been 
used, such as laccase. Indeed, laccase catalysed dimerization of FA 1, using either a 
monophasic or biphasic system was reported by Adelakun et al. and gave a mixture of two 8-
coupled dimers: 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6 and 8,8’-dilactone 32, with respective proportions depending 
on the solvent system, as illustrated in Table 1. In ethyl acetate (EtOAc), the major product is 
the 8,5’-coupled dimer 6 while if one wants to obtain selectively the 8,8’-coupled dimer 32, the 
solvent should be ethanol. Therefore, by changing the nature of the organic solvent used in 
this enzymatic synthesis, one can favour the formation of one of the dimers over the other [5].

Another strategy for rapid syntheses of dehydrodiferulates via biomimetic radical coupling 
reaction of EF 14 was reported by Lu et al. [8]. The syntheses involved CuCl(OH)-
tetramethylethylenediamine (TMEDA) either as oxidant or catalyst in MeCN and the two

conjugated carbon-8) and carbon-5. When used as a catalyst (10%) with oxygen as an oxidant 
(to regenerate the catalyst in situ), the CuCl(OH)-TMEDA complex gave better yields of 
diferulates compared to when it was used directly as a stoichiometric oxidant. This might be 
due to the higher amounts of trimers and oligomers formed at higher concentrations of the 
copper complex. Six out of the nine diFAs can be synthesized by this Cu(II) catalysed aerobic 
oxidative coupling reaction and subsequent saponification. Some of the dehydrodiferulates 
were until then not readily available by synthesis, such as 8-O-4’-diFA 11. Additionally, this 
protocol also claims to be a simpler alternative to produce 5,5’-diFA 4 from vanillin 13, 
compared to the ones from Ralph et al. [2] and Yamamoto et al. [44]. The five dimers obtained 
were the diethyl esters forms of: 8,5’-diFA Bzf 17 (10% yield), 8-O-4’-diFA 28 (5%), 8,8’-diFA 
cyclic form 18c (11%), 8,8’-diFA 18a (6%) and 5,5’-diFA 27 (10%). Additionally, a sixth dimer 
(8,5’-diFA 5) was formed after TBAF treatment (saponification) of diethyl 8,5’-diferulate 17. 
Given the relatively low yields of the isolated dimers, it is not the most convenient preparation 
if a synthesis in a pure form is already available, such as the one for diethyl 8,5’-diferulate 17 
[28]. The dimers were not synthesized individually in pure forms directly, but the mixture was 
separated and purified by flash chromatography and crystallization. The 8,8’- (18) and 5,5’- 
(27) coupled diferulates were successfully isolated by chromatography but the 8,5’-one (17) 
coeluted with 8-O-4’-diferulate 28, hence the need for a separation by crystallization and 
rechromatography.

1.2.7 Dimerization from plant extracts  
 
As mentioned in section 1.1, diFAs can also be prepared upon saponification of PCW materials 
already oxidized in vivo as in Ralph et al. [2] or directly by oxidizing PCW materials in vitro. For 
example, Thibault et al. tested different oxidizing agents for the cross-linking of sugar-beet 
pectins (natural substrates), among which only H2O2–peroxidase and (NH4)2S2O8 were 
efficient. The efficiency was shown by an increase in the viscosity of the mixture (gel formation 
[52]). Both oxidants used are known to induce the generation of free radicals [53].  
Additionally, Figueroa-Espinoza et al. studied the oxidative cross-linking of other substrates 
from plants, pentosans, by two enzymes: laccase and HRP. They investigated the gel 
formation and the effect of adding free FA 1 and various analogues to the natural substrate. 
The water extractable pentosans form highly viscous solutions and can gel through FA 
covalent cross-linking with oxidizing systems generating free radicals [24]. The non-oxidized 
arabinoxylans used already contained FA 1 and its 8-O-4’- (11), 8,5’- (5) and 5,5’- (4) diFAs. 
After oxidation, 8,8’- (8) and 8,5’-diFA Bzf (6) appeared (becoming the major dimers together 
with the 8-O-4’-one (11) and the 8-O-4’- (11) and 8,5’- (5) contents increased while the 5,5’- 
(4) one stayed the same [52].  
 
1.2.8 Photocycloaddition  
 
Another type of dimers can be formed via photoinduced cycloaddition between the ethylene 
carbons of two phenolic acids. In this case cyclobutane type dimers such as truxillic and truxinic 
acids (45 and 46 respectively, which structures are shown in Figure 8), are formed instead of 
the classical diFAs prepared via oxidative coupling and studied in the previous sections. The 
cyclobutane type dimers are not as such the diFAs on which this project is focusing but if a 
subsequent reverse [2+2]-photocycloaddition takes place, opening of the cyclobutane ring 
should occur and thus diFAs would be formed. However, since this photocycloaddition involves 
the double bond in the side chain, only the 8,8’-coupling (8-10) can be obtained that way. For 
instance, Hanley et al. investigated the formation of these phenolic cyclobutane dicarboxylic 
acids in PCW materials via cyclodimerization initiated by light (UV irradiation) [34].  
 
 
Figure 8- Truxillic 45 and truxinic 46 acids 
Furthermore, as with oxidative dimerization, surfactants can be used in the case of 
photocycloaddition in order to obtain a regioselective dimerization, as reported by Nakamura 
with cinnamic acid 47 in a micellar solution [50]. The irradiation of cinnamic acid 47 and its 
methyl ester 48 in 1% aqueous (aq.) cetyltrimethylammonium bromide formed dimeric 
products as a mixture of truxillic and truxinic acids (45 and 46). The dimerization might be 
==PAGE:27==
 
18 
 
favored by the condensation effect of micelles on the substrates and the products obtained 
depend on the alignment of the micelles on the substrates. A few years later, Nakamura et al. 
then described the photodimerization of cinnamic acids controlled by molecular assemblies of 
surfactants alkyldimethylamine N-oxides leading to the synthesis of cyclodimers, via the 
formation of aggregates in water. Syn or anti cyclodimers are formed depending on the packing 
of the molecules (either tight or loose) [51].  
 
Then, Morrison et al. also reported the synthesis of truxillic acid 45 from FA 1, via [2+2]-
photocycloaddition, using high-intensity fluorescent light and compared the results with what 
occurs in grass cell walls. They found that depending on whether the dimerization takes place 
in a head-to-tail or head-to-head way, different truxillic acid stereoisomers can be formed. In 
order for the dimerization to occur with light catalysis, it is required that the double bonds in 
the side chain of the two monomers are less than 4 Å apart [34]. Therefore, within the cell 
walls, the amount of cyclodimers arising is limited by the number of monomers that are close 
enough to react together. If the two molecules are in the same relative orientation, then head-
to-head dimerization occurs, while if they are in opposite orientations, head-to-tail dimerization 
takes place [35].

1.3 
Aims and objectives  
 
In biomass, FA 1 is often crosslinked (dehydrodimers). FA 1 and its dimers (4-11) have gained 
considerable interest in the past few years due to their biological properties, which make them 
valuable precursors for the food, pharmaceutical and cosmetic industries. 
Furthermore, diFAs are important structural components in the PCW and influence its 
properties. Indeed, they reduce the cell wall digestibility of grass forages, enhance the PCW 
rigidity and strength and control its extensibility. FAEs are enzymes that can increase the PCW 
accessibility for other enzymes, by breaking the lignin cross-links (hydrolysing the ester 
linkages, and thus releasing FA 1 and diFAs 4-11). Therefore, FAEs are of great interest in 
order to improve lignocellulosic biomass conversion to biofuel – via enzymatic hydrolysis 
leading to controlled reduction of FA level or FA cross-linkage in plants of industrial interest. 
This is becoming more and more important with the increased interest in renewable 
bioresources as alternatives to fossil fuels for energy and material production.  
 
To allow a detailed biochemical characterization of FAEs, access to defined substrates is of 
key importance. The various existing FAEs may be specialized towards the hydrolysis of 
different dimeric substrates.  
 
In this project the aim is to synthesize as many of the diFAs as possible. The focus is on those 
that are most biologically relevant, namely 5,5’-diFA 4, 8,5’-diFA 5, 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6, 8,8’-diFA 
8 and 8-O-4’-diFA 11. These five dimers are of particular interest for Casper Wilkens’ group in 
order to characterise the FAEs and these enzymes’ activity on natural substrates. The dimers 
successfully synthesized in this project will be used during the Fall 2020 as standards, to 
compare with the material released upon treatment of natural PCW extracts. Moreover, in the 
future, they could be esterified to the arabinose moieties with the aim to be used as substrates 
to investigate whether the group’s collection of FAEs can degrade them. LC-MS will be used 
for the analysis to minimize the amount of substrate needed [54].

2. Results and discussion 
 
The first discovered dimer, 5,5’-diFA 4, has previously been synthesized in NZ, but the 
syntheses of several other diFAs have also been reported in the literature. Therefore, the initial 
scope of the project included the repetition of the synthesis of 5,5’-diFA 4, supplemented with 
the preparation of other dimers such as 8,5’-diFA 5. However, it was recognized from the 
beginning that it might not be feasible to succeed with all four other diFAs of interest for Casper 
Wilkens’ group (8,5’-diFA both linear 5 and Bzf 6 forms, 8,8’-diFA 8, 8-O-4’-diFA 11).  
The approach taken in this project was first to attempt the synthesis of each of the five diFAs 
in a pure form rather than synthesize various dimers in a mixture since it might lead to a difficult 
separation as some of the dimers have been reported to coelute by HPLC [8]. Moreover, in a 
first phase of the project, all the experimental work was conducted at NZ where there was no 
access to a preparative HPLC system. Thus, procedures such as the ones reported by Larsen 
and co-workers [19] for the preparation of a mixture of 5,5’-diFA 4 and 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6, or the 
one from Rakotondramanana et al. [29] for the preparation of a mixture of 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6 and 
8-O-4’-diFA 11, were not considered at that time. However, the latter would have been rather 
interesting for 8-O-4’-diFA 11 since alternative syntheses of this dimer other than the lengthy 
procedure of Ralph et al. [2] were not available. The approach was therefore still investigated 
when access to the preparative HPLC system at DTU was granted, later in the project.  
2.1  Oxidative dimerization  
 
As mentioned in section 1.2, one of the main strategies for the diFAs’ formation is the oxidative 
dimerization, which occurs in two main steps [6]. First, a phenoxy radical is formed, via a 
mechanism which depends on the oxidation system used. The phenoxy radical is stabilized by 
resonance, the single electron being delocalized. With FA 1 (or EF 14 or MF 15), five 
mesomeres exist - illustrated in Figure 3 - three of which are of interest for the coupling 
reactions. The different forms of the radical are called respectively MO, M5 and M8 (8 sometimes 
replaced by β in the literature) and are then coupled in a second step, via six possible coupling 
modes (O-O, 5-O, 8-O, 8-8, 8-5, 5-5) resulting in 8,8’-, 5,5’-, 8,5’-, 8-O-4’- and 4-O-5’-dimers, 
as illustrated in Scheme 1 [48][2][36]. The O-O coupling is not likely because of the instability 
of diaryl peroxides.  
 
The varying ratios of dimeric products obtained in the literature illustrate the lack of 
stereoselectivity of phenoxy radicals coupling during oxidative dimerization due to the several 
coupling modes competing [21][23]. The distribution of the coupled products is controlled by 
that of the unpaired electron and the relative energies of the coupling products [9]. The 8,5’- 
and 8,8’-coupling modes were thought to be favoured because of the stability of carbon-carbon 
bonds and the lower heat of formation of 8,5’- linkages [5]. Additionally, it was suggested that 
the coupling of phenoxy radicals is fastest at the positions with the highest free-electron 
densities (spin-density distribution on the aromatic ring [55]), except if these approaches are 
sterically hindered. On a phenol substrate, the highest densities are known to be at the para 
and ortho position [56]. In the case of FA 1, one of the ortho positions is blocked (C-3), the 
para position (C-1) has a side chain which is also conjugated (and leads to high density at C-
8) and the other ortho position (C-5) should be accessible.  Phenols with an unsubstituted para 
position usually form para-para coupling products as the major dimer [57]. If the para position 
is blocked, then ortho-ortho coupling becomes the main reaction [57], which would in this case 
lead to 5,5’-diFA 4. However, since the para position is blocked but the delocalization still 
==PAGE:30==
 
21 
 
enables a promising coupling at the C-8, the oxidation reaction probably give rise to mixtures 
of 8,8’- (8), 8,5’- (5 and 6) and 5,5’- (4) diFAs.  
 
Furthermore, from the literature review, it seems like coupling of vanillin 13 leads selectively 
to 5,5’-diFA 4, while coupling of EF 14 favours 8,5’-diFAs (5 and 6) and coupling of FA 1 
selectively forms 8,8’-diFA 8 (which however failed in this project). However, no direct 
explanation for this result was found in the literature.  
 
Figure 9- Structures of vanillic acid 49, cysteine 50 and fumaric acid 51 
Additionally, Figueroa-Espinoza and co-workers found that the initiating site for arabinoxylan 
cross-linking is the aromatic ring and more specifically the phenol function, and not the 
propenoic side chain of FA 1. This was demonstrated by adding different chemicals to a natural 
substrate, which showed that with extra FA 1, vanillic acid 49 and cysteine 50, the oxidative 
gelation of soluble arabinoxylans was inhibited with the H2O2–peroxidase oxidant system while 
the addition of fumaric acid 51 had no effect. The structures of these compounds are shown in 
Figure 9. The same results were obtained with laccase, suggesting that in the presence of both 
free FA 1 and ester arabinoxylan-linked FA, both enzymatic systems oxidized preferentially 
the more accessible free FA 1 rather than the ester linked one. However, due to the 
delocalization of the unpaired electron from the O-4 to C-5 or C-8 [52], the propenoic side chain 
double bond is still involved in the process, hence the 8-coupled dimers’ presence among the 
major ones.  
 
2.2  Enzymatic conditions  
 
Peroxidase catalysed oxidation is referred to as the most straightforward and highest yielding 
technique to synthesize diFAs [8]. However, as mentioned in section 1.2.5, this was not the 
only enzyme used, and for instance laccase was also the catalyst in some of the procedures 
[19]. The latter is a copper containing enzyme that catalyzes the oxidation of phenolic 
substrates in the presence of oxygen. HRP, on the other hand, is a metalloenzyme found in 
the root of the plant horseradish and it needs hydrogen peroxide, limiting factor, to be active. 
H2O2 acts as the acceptor and the (esterified) FA 1 as the donor of hydrogen atoms [52].  
 
Regarding the enzymatic conditions for the oxidative dimerization, it has been reported that 
only the phenoxy radical formation is enzymatic. The subsequent radicals coupling seems to 
be non-enzymatic and independent on whether it takes place in vitro or in vivo [6]. Instead, it 
relies on the chemistry of the phenoxy radicals. This finding explains why the same 8,8’- and 
8,5’-coupled products are obtained with different enzymes, such as HRP [6][28] and laccase 
[40] and the most abundant one – 8,5’-diFA Bzf 6 - is also the most abundant dimer in grass 
cell walls [2]. The 8,5’-coupling is therefore suggested to be favoured by the chemistry of FA 1 
(and EF 14), no matter which enzyme is used for the oxidation, and whether it takes places in 
vitro or in vivo. Indeed, the mechanism of phenol oxidation with laccase is different than with 
peroxidase but still leads to the same phenoxy radical. This phenoxy radical then probably 
vacates the active site of the enzyme after formation, making the subsequent coupling step 
non-enzymatic but rather dependent on the chemistry of the radical. The 8,8’-coupling might 
==PAGE:31==
 
22 
 
also be favoured, even though it was not successfully characterized with the methods 
experimented in this project. Furthermore, no 8-O-4’-diFA 11 was identified as such by Ward 
and co-workers, but as part of a trimer indicating that it was also formed during the oxidation. 
However, it preferentially underwent decarboxylation and further coupling with FA radical 2 to 
form the identified trimer [6].  
 
 
Figure 10- Heme structure in HRP [58]. 
The most common type of HRP, type C, has two metal centres: an iron heme group – which 
structure is shown in Figure 10 - and two calcium atoms. It is known for its catalytic role in the 
oxidation of a wide variety of phenols by H2O2. In its resting state, a histidine residue is vacant 
and thus, H2O2 can be attached through an oxygen atom, filling the sixth octahedral position 
of the iron atom during the oxidation reaction and activating the enzyme [58]. The enzyme has 
five oxidation states and the reaction starts when H2O2 binds to its active site. Three HRP 
intermediates are formed during the reduction-oxidation cycle. Aromatic hydrogen donors are 
also bound to HRP, and this occurs through hydrophobic forces (the aromatic ring is attached 
to a hydrophobic region of the protein) and hydrogen bonding (the phenol oxygen interacts 
with the pyrrolic nitrogen of the iron coordinated histidine) forming 1:1-complexes with the 
enzyme in its native state. The electron transfer from the donor to the heme iron therefore 
takes place via the π systems of the aromatic donor and the imidazole ring, and the hydrogen 
bond. Hence, the minimal distance within which the iron of the enzyme and the proton of the 
donor must be for this to occur. The addition of the substrate does not induce a significant 
change in the electronic spectrum of the HRP, which means that the binding site is quite distant 
from the ferric centre [59]. The reaction taking place is illustrated in Scheme 4 in a general 
example with a phenol donor.  
 
Scheme 4- HRP catalyzed phenol oxidation 
An aspect to consider when carrying out this kind of enzymatic reaction is the influence of the 
phosphate buffer on the thermal stability and H2O2 resistance of HRP [60]. Indeed, this enzyme 
is not stable in elevated temperatures and too high H2O2 exposure. Its industrial use is therefore 
limited and the need of carefully manipulating it when using it together with this oxidant arises. 
Studies have been done to find the optimal buffer depending on the effect on the half-life of 
the enzyme, which is prone to suicide inactivation by H2O2.

2.3.1 Step 1 – Inorganic oxidation 
 
This first step of the synthesis via the inorganic oxidation approach is the coupling of vanillin

produced by reaction between redox pairs. Trace quantities of certain transition metals, 
especially ferrous ion, are known to have an accelerating catalytic effect in many systems. 
Indeed Fe2+ transfers one electron to the persulfate, forming the sulfate radical which can then 
initiate the polymerization, as it is a powerful oxidant. Ferrous ion is also a scavenger of sulfate 
free radicals. Additionally, Fe2+ might be coordinating the phenolic compound, involving some 
chelation. This hypothesis may be supported by the fact that pure 5,5’-coupling is obtained, 
suggesting that the monomers were in the right position for the considered coupling to occur.

oxygen, the methoxy group being involved as well, as illustrated in Figure 11 [63]. This five-

2.3.3 Step 3 – Aldol condensation  
 
After protection of the hydroxyl groups, the synthesis continued with the aldol condensation of 
the aromatic aldehyde and malonic acid (a dicarboxylic acid) enabling a side chain extension, 
resulting in an α,β-unsaturated aromatic acid. When synthesizing 5,5’-diFA 4, Slavova-
Kazakova et al. [27] used malic acid, another dicarboxylic acid but Yamamoto et al. [44], on 
the other hand, performed the aldol condensation as a Perkin reaction with acetic anhydride 
instead. A plausible mechanism for the reaction performed here is via the Knoevenagel 
condensation with Doebner modification. The latter was already used for the side chain 
extension in the first synthesis of 5,5’-diFA 4, performed by Richitzenhain but at that time it 
took two weeks to complete [42].  
 
This reaction starts with the deprotonation of the acidic proton of the dicarboxylic acid by the 
pyridine catalyst (weak base), forming an enolate anion. Indeed, malonic acid has a pKa of 
2.85 at 25 °C [64] while the carboxylic acid of acetylated 5,5’-diFA 4 has a pKa around 3.3 [65]. 
Therefore, malonic acid is a stronger acid and is deprotonated first by pyridine (the pKa of the 
conjugated acid of pyridine being 5.2 [66]). The enolate anion formed then attacks one of the 
aldehydes of 53 (acetylated 5,5’-divanillin) in an aldol condensation (via a “soft-soft” 
interaction). The suggested mechanism is shown in Scheme 8. A protonation and water and 
carbon dioxide elimination are involved, to afford the conjugated side chain. Since the aldol 
condensation is based catalysed, the dehydration probably occurs via an E1cB mechanism, 
but it is unsure here whether the H2O and CO2 eliminations are occurring simultaneously or in 
two different steps.  
This reaction also takes place twice, on both aldehydes of 53. It could alternatively have been 
performed via a stereoselective Wittig reaction with a stabilized ylid, in the same way as for 
the preparation of 8,8’-diFA 8 described in section 2.5.4.

was hydrolysed in order to isolate the diacid instead of the diester, but also to open the 
benzofuran ring and form the linear dimer. Base treatment and then acidification were carried 
out, and this saponification was reported to lead to a mixture of the dimer in the benzofuran 
and linear forms which then require separation [2]. The LC-MS analysis of the product obtained 
after saponification and purification by flash chromatography shows three isomers with m/z 
corresponding to the dimer (1st one 0.89 min elution time, 58%; 2nd one 1.01 min elution time, 
22%; 3rd one 1.10 min elution time, 10%). The first eluting compound turned out to be the linear 
form 5, the third one the Bzf form 6, but the remaining one was not isolated nor identified. It 
might result from another coupling such as 8,8’- but remained uncharacterized. The fact that 
the linear form of the 8,5’-diFA 5 elutes first by reverse phase (RP) chromatography suggests 
that it is more polar than the benzofuran one. This might be due to the free OH group present 
in the linear form while in the Bzf one, the oxygen is part of the ring, making it less hydrophilic.  
The base used (KOH) was quite concentrated (40%) which is surprising since the reaction 
appeared quite fast and was left stirring for 2 h. Thus, it was also tried with KOH 20% which 
proved to be efficient as well.  
 
Furthermore, it was also attempted to hydrolyse the same material 17 with an enzymatic 
approach, in order to investigate whether selective hydrolysis conditions could be found for the 
two isomers 8 and 9. However, different immobilised enzymes available in NZ were tried but 
none of them resulted in hydrolysis of the esters nor opening of the ring. Therefore, it was 
concluded that there was no readily available enzyme that can hydrolyse this substrate. The 
approach could, of course, have been investigated with a broader range of reaction conditions, 
but it would have required a significant effort for an event with a low likelihood.  
 
Besides the enzymatic hydrolysis approach, different reaction conditions than the one from 
Ralph et al. [2] were also tested for the base treatment of diethyl 8,5’-diferulate 17. The purpose 
was still to investigate whether some conditions could favour the formation of one of the 
isomers rather than that of the other(s), to have a selective method. It was found that when 
using 1,8-diazabicyclo[5.4.0]undec-7-ene (DBU) in CHCl3 as a base the benzofuran ring is 
selectively opened without hydrolysing the esters because of the non-nucleophilic character of 
this strong base. Indeed, the NMR data was consistent with the diethyl 8,5’-diferulate in its 
linear form. The ethyl esters could then be hydrolysed in a subsequent reaction.

Regarding the hydrolysis of 17, under alkaline conditions, the mechanism suggested is shown 
in Scheme 12. It is such that the nucleophilic base (KOH) attacks the phenolic proton which 
initiates the ring opening, forming a quinone methide intermediate. The aromaticity is then 
regained by elimination of a proton. After acidification at r.t., the diethyl ester form of the linear 
8,5’-diFA is obtained while when further treated with base in water and then acidified,

suggested by Xia and co-workers [39]. On the other hand, the Bzf form of the diacid (6) is 
obtained by simply hydrolysing the ethyl esters without opening the ring, following the classical 
mechanism of ester hydrolysis with the hydroxide ion and is therefore not drawn here. 
However, the formation of the linear form might happen in the opposite way, starting with the 
esters’ hydrolysis and then ring opening. This is uncertain and has not been successfully 
determined experimentally. Furthermore, it is unsure why only a specific proportion of the 
benzofuran ring is opened upon basic treatment and the remaining amount of the substrate 
still possesses the ring. Since the nucleophilic base was added in large excess (5 mL), the ring 
opening is not limited by the stoichiometry. It can be speculated that the incomplete ring 
opening is related to kinetic factors. Indeed, it might be slow compared to the faster esters’ 
hydrolysis. Moreover, these two reactions might be competing in the sense that once the 
esters’ hydrolysis have occurred, it may be more difficult to open the benzofuran ring so this 
second reaction can only occur on the portion of the substrate that still has the ester groups 
and not the carboxylic acids yet.  
 
As a general comment for future work, since it was noted that the SM 17 conversion was not 
complete in these two reactions, it might be worth increasing the reaction time and temperature 
or for example in the case of the hydrolysis, adding more base could drive the reaction to 
completion.

2.5.1 Step 1– Aldehyde reduction 
 
The first step was the hydrogenation of SM 13 to reduce the aldehyde in a methyl group, using 
a palladium catalyst, giving compound 55. The hydrogenation was performed in acetic acid 
since this protic solvent can accelerate the reaction rate. The use of H2 is more spread as a 
reducing agent for alkenes rather than carbonyl compounds, due to its relatively low 
nucleophilicity and the stronger character of the carbon-oxygen double bond compared to the 
carbon-carbon one. However, in the case of aromatic aldehydes it has been shown that they 
can selectively be reduced to the corresponding alcohols under mild conditions (16 h, r.t.) using 
a selective heterogeneous catalyst Pd(0) with H2. Further hydrogenolysis to the corresponding 
hydrocarbon derivatives can occur, when raising the temperature and the amount of hydrogen 
[69]. Moreover, the palladium catalyst is a transition metal which reduces selectively the 
benzylic bond (faster reaction) in presence of an aromatic ring, unlike a platinum catalyst. The 
catalytic hydrogenation takes place on the surface of the metal. As illustrated in Scheme 14, 
the first step is the chemical absorption of H2 onto the metal surface, which leads to the 
breakage of the H-H bond, followed by the bonding of the substrate to the metal catalyst as 
well. Then, H2 is transferred from the metal surface to the substrate via a syn-addition of the 
two hydrogens leading to the alcohol. Finally, the C-O bond is broken via hydrogenolysis, i.e. 
the addition of another equivalent of H2 to the substrate, in the same way, with elimination of 
water [67].

2.5.2 Step 2 – t-Bu substitution   
 
The suggested mechanism for this t-Bu substitution step, illustrated in Scheme 15, starts with 
the protonation of the hydroxyl group of t-BuOH, by the phosphoric acid catalyst (protic acid). 
This could alternatively be done with t-BuCl and AlCl3 or the alkene isobutylene and a protic 
acid.  Water is then eliminated, leading to an alkene which gets protonated, forming a tertiary 
carbocation intermediate, that is the most stable one. One of the double bonds on the benzene 
of the substrate then attacks this carbocation, via a tert-butylation ortho to the phenol 
substituent. The OH group is known to be ortho, para-directing towards electrophiles such as 
the t-Bu cation. However, the para position is already blocked by the methyl group, and the 
other ortho position has a methoxy substituent. The latter can also donate electrons and thus 
activate and direct ortho, para. Since both of these positions are hindered by the methyl 
substituent and OH has a stronger directive power, the tert-butylation occurs ortho to the 
phenol [67]. The carbocation intermediate formed is stabilized by resonance and leads to the 
t-Bu substituted compound 56 upon deprotonation.  
With Friedel-Crafts alkylation reactions there often is a risk of multiple substitution which can 
be avoided with a large excess of the benzene substrate or by performing an acylation and 
subsequent carbonyl group reduction instead [67].

2.5.3 Step 3 – Re-oxidation  
 
The NMR data of intermediate 57 corresponded to the product after oxidation back to the 
aldehyde but also revealed the presence of impurities. It was however concluded that these 
impurities were not critical. Thus, it was still decided to continue further with the next step of 
the synthesis with the partially purified compound. 
 
The reaction continued with the methyl substituent oxidation back to the aldehyde using 
bromine and t-BuOH, as illustrated in Scheme 16. Unlike with electrophilic aromatic 
substitution, here the bromine does not react with the alkene to substitute a hydrogen atom 
ortho or para to the phenol as anyways, these positions are blocked. However, since the methyl 
group is also electron donating, the bromine could substitute a proton ortho to the methyl 
substituent via an electrophilic aromatic substitution reaction, but it is not the desired reaction. 
Indeed, in the reaction considered here, Br2 is suggested to react similarly as in the bromine 
oxidation of toluene, oxidizing the methyl substituent first to the alcohol and then the aldehyde. 
Thus, one can assume that the reaction starts with a bromination at the benzylic position via a 
radical mechanism. The benzyl radical is unusually stable since it can be delocalized into the 
aromatic ring. Therefore, selective free radical substitution at the benzylic position can take 
place. This benzylic bromination is usually catalysed by heat or light. There is however a risk 
of further benzylic bromination since more C-H bonds are present and can be substituted by 
Br2. Furthermore, H2O can be formed via the reaction of t-BuOH with HBr, as suggested on 
the reaction scheme. The reaction then continues with a second bromination which occurs via 
the same mechanism, to give the benzylic dibromide and another molecule of water (hence 
the excess of Br2 used). H2O then attacks the dibromide (nucleophilic substitution SN2) to form 
an unstable α-bromo alcohol which quickly collapses to the aldehyde via loss of HBr, giving

2.5.4 Step 4 – Wittig reaction 
 
This stereoselective Wittig reaction finally afforded 5-t-Bu EF 33, substituting the C=O bond by 
a conjugated C=C bond. The mechanism is shown in Scheme 17. The carbanion of the 
stabilized phosphonium ylid (Wittig reagent) attacks the carbonyl group of the aldehyde 57, 
after which the negatively charged oxygen attacks the positively charged phosphate 
(phosphorous has an exceptional affinity for oxygen) giving a 4-membered ring called 
oxaphosphetane. Then, a retro [2+2]-cycloaddition follows. This opens the unstable 4-
membered ring, forming the alkene (and therefore the three-carbon conjugated side chain) and 
triphenylphosphine oxide. The Wittig reagent can be made via deprotonation of a phosphonium 
salt, which is itself usually formed via reaction of triphenylphosphine with an alkyl halide and a 
strong base. Since the ylid is stabilized by an electron withdrawing group (CO2Et), the reaction 
leads mainly to the (E)-alkene. Similarly as for 5,5’- (4) and 8,5’-diFAs (5 and 6), the trans 
==PAGE:46==
 
37 
 
configuration of the two hydrogen atoms in the alkene on the side chain is confirmed by NMR 
data. Indeed, the coupling constant J between H7 and H8 is 15.6 Hz which is specific of a 
parallel alignment between the bonds. The Wittig reaction is irreversible and driven forward by 
the formation of the P=O bond which is one of the strongest in chemistry, with a bond energy 
of 575 kJ/mol [67].

2.7.3 Electrochemical oxidation reactions  
 
All reactions presented here were carried out in batch. However, for future work, it would be 
interesting to apply a flow set-up in order to optimize the 8-coupled dimers preparation by 
continuously flowing the substrate to be oxidized in the cell and out of the cell once oxidized, 
without the need for a too large set-up.  
 
The general procedure was inspired from Neudorffer et al. A solution of LiCl4 in MeCN was 
prepared, to which EF 14 (0.1 M) was added and treated with a stoichiometric amount of 
TMAOH to deprotonate the acidic phenol. These solutions were oxidized at r.t., under N2 (or 
Ar) atmosphere, at a GCE working electrode. The purpose of the inert atmosphere is to ensure 
that there is no oxygen or moisture present in the electrochemical cell, which could be oxidized 
or reduced and thus compete with the reaction of interest [71]. After exhaustive oxidation, 
meaning when a steady-state minimum value of the current was reached, the solutions were 
analysed by LC-MS and then poured into a citrate buffer. A standard work-up was performed 
after extraction with EtOAc. The various mixtures obtained from the different reactions 
performed were analysed by 1H NMR but only EF 14 could be identified, the rest of the signals 
being too small to allow full characterization of the mixtures. Separation by preparative HPLC 
was attempted, with different gradients but turned out unsuccessful as all dimers coeluted with 
the starting monomer. The initial experiments which served as a basis for the choice of 
electrochemical oxidation conditions but did not lead to the detection of any dimer by LC-MS 
are not described here. Only the experiments which conditions allowed conversion of monomer 
into dimers to some extent are illustrated in Table 3. The highest yield of dimers obtained, from 
the different conditions tried, was 42% according to the LC-MS UV chromatogram. It seemed 
to be a mixture of at least four dimers but without separation and isolation of each, full

The electrochemical oxidative coupling was also tried with FA 1 in a procedure inspired by 
Salas-Reyes et al. [31]. Hence, a solution of n-Bu4NPF6 in DMSO was prepared. The analyte

FA 1 and EF 14. In order to optimize the reaction and form as much dimers as possible, it was

so it was proceeded with this concentration, 50 times higher than the one from the paper. 
Following this optimisation strategy, the electrochemical experiments were first attempted at 
1.15 V – which according to the CV obtained seemed to be the oxidation potential - with an EF 
14 2 mM solution but it did not give much results. Therefore, in a stepwise approach, the 
reaction time was increased, and the potential was varied. Some lower potentials were also 
tried since they were reported as the oxidation potential for this system in the literature. Then, 
the concentration was increased to 20 mM, and with the same approach, better results were 
obtained, but they were still not satisfying enough. Thus, the concentration was increased to 
0.1 M and with the same optimisation strategy, different conditions were tried. Table 3 
illustrates the experiments which allowed detection of dimers by LC-MS analysis. Most of the 
successful electrochemical oxidation samples were identified as a mixture of SM, together with 
three or four dimers in varying proportions depending on the conditions used.  
 
On the PalmSens potentiostat, only potentiostatic electrochemical oxidation reactions were 
performed, with an oxidation potential based on the CV obtained. For EF 14 0.1 M, the potential 
leading to the highest ratio of dimer formed was found to be 1.3 V. On the other hand, with the 
Electrasyn potentiostat, it was first attempted to run similar constant voltage reactions but at 
some point, it led to a resistance problem within the cell. According to the IKA Electrasyn 
support team, the defect was probably linked to a too low voltage or current under which the 
electrical difference for the vial detection can be too low. This issue was then avoided by 
performing constant current reactions instead, with the assist mode of the potentiostat, defining 
the 5 mmol of EF 14 to be oxidized and the 0.7 F/mol charge desired, as described in 
Neudorffer et al. [20]. The assist mode then suggested to run the reaction at a current of 13.3 
mA corresponding to an initial potential of 4.24 V, higher than the 1.3 V with which the best 
results were obtained for the constant voltage reactions. The three different electrolytes 
solutions mentioned by Neudorffer et al., Salas-Reyes et al. and Hapiot et al. were tried 
[20][31][33]. They were usually based on a 2mM analyte (EF 14 or FA 1) with either 1 or 2 eq. 
of base to deprotonate the phenol and in the case of FA 1, also the carboxylic acid.  
 
At some point of the experiments, FA 1 was detected, among other compounds, in the LC-MS 
analysis of the samples after oxidation of EF 14. An investigation led to the following 
explanation: in the general procedure [20], a diluted TMAOH solution was used (not the 25% 
one which corresponds to 2.74 M but a 2 mM solution) but it was unclear when naming the

2.7.4 Mechanisms involved in the electrochemical oxidation 
 
The mechanism of the electrochemical oxidation involves, as with the other oxidative 
dimerization approaches - and as explained in section 2.1 - first the formation of phenoxy 
radicals by proton and electron transfers (the electron transfer here occurring between the 
substrate and the electrode) and then the chemical coupling of these radicals forming carbon-
carbon bonds [55]. Radicals can be generated at the anode in two ways: either by 
deprotonation of the phenol to an anion 58 that is oxidized or by oxidation of the neutral phenol 
to a radical cation 59 which has a high C-H acidity and is deprotonated fast by a weak base 
[57]. The concentration of radicals is usually high at the electrolyte layer at the anode surface. 
The slope of the CV has been reported to be consistent with a mechanism involving a fast 
electron transfer between the electrode and the phenolate and a following irreversible coupling 
of two phenoxy radicals according to a second-order rate law – favored by a slow diffusion of 
the radicals into the solution compared to their reaction rate [57]. The irreversibility and the 
diffusion-controlled one-electron process explain the non-symmetrical shape of the CV and the 
decrease of the current to a non-zero value. Initial electron and proton transfers are 
consecutive reactions, under pure kinetic control of the radical cation deprotonation as the 
conjugation with the double bond and the presence of electron donating groups on the ring 
stabilize the radical cation. The dimers can then be formed in a subsequent chemical reaction 
step. The absence of cathodic peak in the reverse sweep indicates that the dimerization occurs 
very fast after the electron transfer and formation of phenoxy radical 21 [20]. The anodic 
coupling reactions involve radical cation – radical cation coupling or radical cation – substrate 
coupling with the starting compound, as illustrated in Scheme 18 [57]. In the case of FA 1, 
Hapiot and co-workers reported that the decay rate constant of the radical does not depend 
on FA 1 concentration, showing that it is a radical-radical mechanism and not an addition of 
the radical to the parent molecule which would be too slow [33]. The mechanism shown in 
Scheme 18 also represents the principle of anodic umpolung, which is the coupling of 
substrates of equal polarity via a one-pot reaction by combination of an electron transfer with 
a chemical reaction in an electrochemical reaction [57]. 
 
 
Scheme 18- Mechanism of radical cation formation and coupling [57]. 
Since base was used herein, the phenolate anion 58 is oxidized to the phenoxy radical. The 
latter is stabilized by resonance (delocalized electron), with five mesomeres, shown in Figure 
3 in the case of FA 1. Only three of these structures have been proved to be involved in the 
coupling [36], when oxidizing a substrate with the three-carbon conjugated side chain such as 
FA 1 or EF 14. The propenoic side chain thus enables a stabilization of the radical.

The electron transfer occurs from the anode, here the GCE, to the analyte, for example EF 14. 
The reaction taking place at the WE is therefore the anodic oxidation of EF. In order to 
investigate the possible reactions happening at the CE (the cathode, here Pt), scanning of a 
broad range of potentials (including very negative ones) in the solvent window would be 
needed. This process would help in knowing whether the analyte is likely to be reduced at the 
CE, competing with the desired oxidation at the WE. This was not performed in the project 
since the focus was on the oxidation.  
 
Salas-Reyes et al. suggested that DMSO being a polar aprotic, hydrogen bonding acceptor 
solvent, it plays an important role in the anodic oxidation of phenolic substrates. The interaction 
between the phenol and DMSO through hydrogen bonding decreases the dissociation energy 
of the O-H bonds, making the proton transfer reaction more favourable in the second step of 
the oxidation mechanism, via a stabilization of reaction intermediates [31]. However, in this 
work, the experiments in DMSO did not give promising results.  
The oxidation reaction is limited by mass transport from the solution to the anode surface. 
Thus, adding a magnet to induce stirring can help getting a higher yield of oxidation products. 
However, given the set-up used, the magnet could only be added when running 
electrochemical reactions with the Electrasyn and not the PalmSens potentiostat as it would 
have been touching the electrodes and degrading them.  
2.7.5 General discussion and outlook 
 
The generated conjugated dimers have a lower oxidation potential than that of the monomer, 
meaning that further oxidation of the dimer can occur [55]. It is therefore likely that via the 
electrochemical oxidation reactions carried out, higher oligomers were made but because of 
the lack of solubility they went unnoticed. Indeed, the conjugated polymers formed are 
intrinsically conductive and they are obtained on the surface of the WE as films. The electron 
transfer and subsequent coupling of the monomers occur near the surface of the electrode and 
insoluble polymeric product is deposited on it [55]. Therefore, the low dimeric yields obtained 
might be due to the products building up on the electrode surface or being deposited at the 
bottom of the cell as an insoluble polymer powder, instead of completely dissolved in the 
solution analysed.  
 
Furthermore, the browning of the solution suggests that precipitation might have occurred to 
some extent. Additionally, the decrease of the current until a steady state noted in the 
potentiostatic reactions suggests that the analyte was consumed until all electroactive species 
were exhausted and oxidized. Thus, the low conversion into dimers could indicate that too 
much substrate was adsorbed on the electrode blocking the rest of the oxidation. However, 
the polymeric film formed is claimed not to be passive but still conductive to carry out a 
continuous electrochemical reaction on the anode [55]. Since the oxidative dimerization has 
been reported to take place in two steps, as described in section 2.1, it could alternatively be 
speculated that all analyte is oxidized in the first step, but the limiting factor is the chemical 
reaction between the two radicals formed. This hypothesis does not seem very likely since 
radicals are usually reactive intermediates and react fast. The exact reason why the reaction 
did not go until completion thus remains unclear.

of the reaction at the WE from the reaction at the CE so ideally, no competing reaction could 
lower the yield of the oxidative dimerization products. Additionally, performing the 
electrochemical reactions on a larger scale or in a flow set-up, in a second phase, would help 
promote the dimers formation.  
 
Another strategy would be to try different electrodes, solvents and electrolytes as the ones

3. Conclusion and outlook 
 
In this project, the synthesis of a range of ferulic acid dimers has been explored. These 
compounds were investigated with the end-purpose of being used as standards for assaying 
ferulic acid esterases’ activity and specificity. To conclude, three ferulic acid dimers (4, 5 and 
6) were successfully synthesized and the syntheses of other dimers (8 and 11) have been 
attempted by multiple methods.  
In the first phase of the project, both enzymatic and inorganic oxidation approaches were 
investigated in order to synthesize the desired dimers. It was shown that 5,5’-diFA 4 can be 
prepared by both enzymatic and inorganic strategies, in reasonable yields, following 
procedures previously reported in the literature, starting from low cost and readily available 
vanillin 13. Then, it was found that 8,5’-diFA can be obtained as a mixture of the linear and Bzf 
form, that need subsequent purification by RP chromatography to separate the two isomers 5 
and 6. However Bzf form 6 can also be isolated in acceptable yield (24%) by flash 
chromatography. Syntheses of the other two dimers of interest for this project (8 and 11), which 
were attempted by both inorganic and enzymatic oxidations, proved unsuccessful. This was 
due to lengthy procedures and lack of selectivity for the required 8,8’- and 8-O-4’-couplings. 
Regarding the 8,8’-coupling, it could be enhanced by adding a substituent such as the t-Bu 
group at the carbon-5 in order to block the major 8,5’-coupling. The substituted substrate was 
successfully synthesized in this project, but the strategy was unfortunately not completed due 
to insufficient amount of compound after several low yields’ steps.   
In the second phase of the project, an electrochemical approach was explored with the aim to 
form the 8-coupled dimers in a one-pot procedure, in a mixture requiring chromatographic 
separation, as described by Neudorffer et al. [20]. An optimization strategy was carried out in 
order to increase the yield of dimers formed by electrochemical oxidation resulting in a 
maximum of 42% conversion, as observed by LC-MS analysis. Purification by RP 
chromatography was investigated but did not result in a successful isolation of the dimers due 
to coelution with the starting monomer.  
3.1  Future work 
 
For potential work, the four steps of the procedure from Wang et al. [48] should be investigated 
further in order to optimize them and enhance the t-Bu protected EF 33 formation, to allow 
significant coupling to occur. Specifically, the t-Bu substitution reaction could potentially be 
replaced by alternative conditions such as the procedure from Saiz-Poseu et al. [72].  
 
Additionally, the general strategy of blocking one carbon with a protecting substituent in order 
to favour a specific coupling could be explored further, with other substrates and substituents, 
to force the coupling to follow pathways that are otherwise minors.  
Moreover, despite being very lengthy because of numerous protection and deprotection steps, 
the procedure from Ralph et al. for the synthesis of 8-O-4’-diFA 11 could be attempted [2]. This 
was initially planned as part of the project but was unfortunately not possible due to a delayed 
delivery of a reagent (coniferyl aldehyde 35). Then, the inorganic oxidation approach with silver 
oxide in a mixture of toluene and acetone could be investigated further. Considering the 
different results obtained by Rakotondramanana et al. [29] and Xia et al. [39], different ratios 
of the two solvents could be tried and reaction time and temperature could be varied in order 
to find the optimal conditions to enhance the 8-O-4’-diFA 11 formation. This could be combined 
with the blocking of the carbon-5 position with an extra bulky substituent.  
==PAGE:56==
 
47 
 
Then, other approaches not investigated in this work could be considered. For instance, the 
use of surfactants to promote a regioselective dimerization, or the use of different enzymes 
like laccase for the enzymatic method could be explored. Besides, since it was reported that 
the 8,8’-coupling catalysed by laccase is favoured in ethanol [5], it would be interesting to verify 
this finding and also determine if in a similar way, the 8,8’-coupled product 32 is favoured when 
performing the oxidative dimerization with peroxidase or a single-electron oxidant in ethanol. 
The regioselective dimerization strategy based on the copper complex from Lu et al. [8] could 
also be explored.  
 
Moreover, for future work based on information acquired in this project, further investigations 
into the most promising electrochemical oxidation conditions are desired. Indeed, LC-MS 
analysis of the experiments carried out was promising and indicated that synthesis of dimers 
in this way is feasible. It would therefore be of interest to further explore optimal conditions 
leading to the highest achievable conversion of the monomers into dimers (keeping in mind 
that full conversion may not be possible -  Neudorffer and co-workers reported 46% combined 
yield for the 8-coupled dimers [20]). Additionally, more work on purification of the resulting 
mixtures would hopefully allow complete characterization of the dimers formed by NMR 
spectroscopy studies and confirm that at least three dimers can be prepared via this one-pot 
procedure.  
Specifically, it would be worth investigating the electrochemical oxidation approach with a two-
compartment divided cell, in order to separate the reaction at the counter-electrode (sacrificial 
chamber) from the one taking place at the WE. This could also be performed on a bigger scale, 
with larger electrodes (especially the WE, to increase the surface area), a bigger volume of 
solution and higher concentration. A flow set-up might also be efficient to enhance and 
accelerate the formation of the different dimers in focus for this project.  
Furthermore, other conditions to be tested for the electrochemical oxidations include different 
electrodes, solvents and electrolytes. For examples, the different combinations suggested by 
the assist mode of the Electrasyn could be explored. 
Finally, as mentioned in the introduction, the diFAs are, in nature, linked to arabinose moieties 
such as methyl α-L-arabinofuranoside (Araf). These esters found in biomass are the substrates 
of the FAEs. Therefore, future work could further aim at synthesizing the arabinose esters and 
applying these in enzymatic assays to characterize the FAEs.

Experimental – Electrochemistry  
 
The electrochemical experiments were carried out with the following potentiostats: Electrasyn 
2.0, PalmSens4 and a custom-made battery potentiostat, used only for CV experiments. With 
the Electrasyn, the set-up was an undivided vial with two electrodes for the constant voltage 
reaction: a glassy carbon electrode (GCE) as the working electrode and platinum (Pt) as CE. 
The reference electrode used for the CV experiments was the SCE.  The reactions were 
performed at r.t., with stirring (400 rpm). In parallel, the reactions performed with the PalmSens 
potentiostat involved a three-electrode set-up in an undivided cell, at r.t. as well. The WE and 
CE were the same but the reference electrode was a silver wire coated with a silver salt 
(Ag+/Ag). This more stable reference electrode must frequently be calibrated with ferrocene. 
The WE was polished and washed with alumina slurry, then rinsed and sonicated. The CE on 
the other hand was burnt with a hydrogen flame in order to eliminate both organic and inorganic 
pollutants. Additionally, all glassware used for electrochemistry was boiled in nitric acid 65% 
(250 °C).  
The electrochemical oxidative coupling of EF 14 was investigated based on the procedure from 
Neudorffer et al. [20]. For each of the electrochemical oxidative coupling attempted, 15 mL 
solution were used with the PalmSens potentiostat while only 8 mL were used with the 
Electrasyn, due to the respective sizes of the cells. These solutions were oxidized at r.t., under 
nitrogen (or argon) atmosphere, after bubbling for 10 minutes in the solution, at a GCE working 
electrode. A solution of lithium perchlorate (20 mM, 106 mg) in MeCN (50 mL) was prepared, 
to which EF 14 (5 mmol, 1.11 g, 0.1 M) was added and treated with a stoichiometric amount 
of TMAOH (5 mmol, 1.8 mL). After exhaustive oxidation, meaning when a steady-state 
minimum value of the current was reached, the solutions were analysed by LC-MS and then 
poured into a citrate buffer (0.5 M, pH 6.0, 6 mL buffer/10 mL solution) and extracted with the 
same amount of EtOAc. The organic phase was dried over anhydrous MgSO4 and the solvent 
evaporated under reduced pressure at 50°C.  
The highest yield of dimers obtained, from the different conditions tried was 42%, according to 
HPLC UV quantification, at E = 1.4 V, for approximately 1.5 h, with EF 14 0.1 M. The UV 
chromatogram indicated a mixture of at least four dimers.  
The electrochemical oxidative coupling was also attempted with FA 1. A solution of 
tetrabutylammonium hexafluorophosphate (n-Bu4NPF6 0.2 M, 3.87 g) in DMSO (50 mL) was 
prepared. FA 1 (1.9418 g) was dissolved in the electrolyte solution, after which 2 eq. of TMAOH 
were added (10 mL).  
After exhaustive oxidation, meaning when a steady-state minimum value of the current was 
reached, the solutions were analyzed by LC-MS. Some of the conditions experimented (E = 
1.0, 1.2 and 1.35 V) resulted in positive identification of dimer formation, m/z 387 [M-H]- albeit 
in a very low conversion. Since it did not look as promising as with EF, the electrochemical 
oxidation of FA 1 was not investigated further.
