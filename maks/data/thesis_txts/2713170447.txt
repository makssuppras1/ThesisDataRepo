Abstract
High speed trains crossing long span bridges can result in substantial dynamic vehicle-bridge
interaction. This can effect both passenger comfort as well as safety and runability of the
train passage. This master’s thesis is the nth4 project in an ongoing collaboration between
the Technical University of Denmark, DTU, and Rambøll.
The main scope of the project
is to develop a numerical vehicle-bridge interaction, VBI, program to assess the dynamic
interaction, safety and runability of high speed train passage of long span bridges.
The goal of this master’s thesis is to continue the development of the VBI program. The project
was initiated by [1] before [2] and [3] continued the development. The general VBI program was
setup by [1] and [2] while [3] extended the program to include non-linear springs. The scope
of this master’s thesis is to further develop the VBI-program by extending it from a vehicle-
bridge interaction program to a vehicle-bridge-ship interaction, VBSI, program, capable of
conducting safety and runability analyses for fully coupled VBSI models. Initially, the theory
behind the previous version of the VBI program is presented.
This includes the vehicle-
bridge coupling theory as well as the implementation of non-linear time integration scheme
including non-linear springs. Afterwards the ship implementation theory is presented. Firstly
a fully geometric model, based on TMD theory is implemented.
A detaching mechanism
is included to make the ship "drift away" after impact, and dissipation of impact energy is
introduced. The implementations are validated with the use of dynamic extension of a static
3D beam Finite Element program provided by DTU [4], named MaxiFrameDyn. As the VBI
program is based on modal bridge coordinates, the theory for a ship-bridge coupling for a
modal bridge and geometric ship is developed and validated. A non-linear generalized-α time
integration scheme is presented and implemented, and the modal ship representation with the
generalized-α algorithm is shown to obtain the same results as the fully geometric model with
the Newmark algorithm. Furthermore, a inclusion of the non-linear springs worklaws based
on input worklaws is created and implemented for both non-linear foundation springs and the
ship. After presenting the implementation and validation of the VBI program, a case study with
a 9 span bridge, resembling a simplified Storstrøms Broen SSB viaduct, is setup in the extended
VBI program. The main analysis was to check if Rambølls approach of conducting the safety
and runability analysis for the SSB. Rambøll has a full bridge-ship interaction model of the
SSB in a commercial FE-software, where a ship impact analysis without the train is conducted
and the ship force time series is saved. The time series is then send to an external contractor to
conduct the safety and runability analysis. It was found, that for the case study of the simplified
SSB viaduct, for a train consisting of 8 trains with a speed of 200 km/t, there was a difference
of 0.2‰in the overturning criteria, hence the approach by Rambøll is more than sufficiently
accurate for this specific case.

1. Introduction
Technical University of Denmark
1
Introduction
As long span bridges are increasing in both length and slenderness, the dynamic effects will
have an increasing influence on the design of safe structures. As many bridges carry both
road and railway traffic, the dynamic interaction has to be investigated to ensure the safety
and runability of both the structure and train passage. A further challenge is present when
the bridge crosses shipping routes, as the bridge has to be designed for ship impacts, and the
passenger safety must be ensured. As both the road, train and ship interactions with long span
bridges can lead to severe dynamic effects, a need for a numerical tool to investigate these
effects is present. Commercial software for analysing high-speed train passage of long span
bridges does exist, but the underlying theory is not publicly available. This brings a need for
well documented numerical VBI tool, hence Rambøll Group A/S has chosen to develop an in-
house tool.
The work of this thesis is a continuation of three previous theses, where a vehicle bridge
interaction program, VBI, has been created in collaboration between Rambøll Group A/S and
the Technical University of Denmark. In the first two projects, the train passage was modelled
and implemented in a Matlab program where the structure is modal based and the vehicle
is in geometric coordinates [2] [1]. In the third project, non-linear springs was implemented
to take the non-linearity in the soil-structure interaction into account [3]. The VBI program
is an in-house numerical tool which operates on input from a commercial FE-software. The
commercial software should be capable of modelling the bridge with spatial linear-elastic
beam elements, and calculate the dynamic properties of the bridge. The input to the VBI
program consist of the bridge node coordinates, mode shapes and natural frequencies, hence
the bridge is in the modal domain. The vehicle model implemented in the VBI program is
based on a realistic train from the Design Basis of the trøms Bridge, SSB [5], and is a 3D 13 DOF
system which operates in Cartesian coordinates. The train and bridge is coupled by location
dependent system matrices, assembled by kinematic relations, where the bridge is in modal
coordinates and the train in Cartesian.
The scope of this project is to develop and implement a coupled bridge-ship model in the VBI
program and investigate the coupled dynamic effects for a fully coupled vehicle-bridge-ship
interaction, VBSI, model and its implications on the safety and runability of the train passage
during ship a collision. As of now, the ship impact analysis in Rambøll’s commercial software is
based on force-indentation curves from the SSB design basis [5], often referred as P-y curves,
or more general as a worklaw. When the safety and runability of the train passage is examined
in the VBI program, the impact is modelled as simple load time history. Hence there is a need
for a dynamic coupled model, to better capture the dynamics of a coupled VBS interaction
model. A new time-integration scheme, the non-linear generalized-α, is implemented in the
VBI program, in order to introduce numerical damping to the higher modes. As the most
critical case for safety and runability of the train passage is the head-on-bow collision, the
implementation in the VBI program is based hereoff. Furthermore, Rambølls approach of
conducting the safety and runability analysis for the SSB is tested. Rambøll has a full bridge-
ship interaction model of the SSB in a commercial FE-software. In the software, a ship impact
analysis without the train is conducted and the ship force time series is saved. The time series
is send to an external contractor for them to conduct the safety and runability analysis. The
consequences of the doing the safety and runability study for the fully coupled VBSI model is

1.1
Ship impact in standards and research
The Eurocode EN 1991-1-7 [7] covers the the general design codes for ship impacts for bridges
and other structures. In regard to ship impacts for sea waterways, the Eurocode proposes two
different types of ship impact analysis a simple and an advanced analysis, but both are based
on static equivalent forces. These methods will not capture the dynamic coupling effects of
ship impacts with train passage, hence there might be a need to go beyond what Eurocode
specifies, to capture the dynamic interaction. Looking towards the offshore industry and the
guidelines from DNV [8], the modelling of a ship impacts can be much more complicated
than just assuming an equivalent static load. In [8], the ship impact is modelled as a force-
indentation curve. Here both the ship force, and the tangential stiffness of the ship can be
obtained directly from the design curve for the specific situation. Looking into the reference
list of the DNV standard, all of the research papers are either authored or co-authored by Jørgen
Amdahl, hence it is natural to look into how his research group is modelling the ship impacts.
For ship-bridge impact analysis, Amdahl often splits the analysis into two steps [9]. First step
is a non-linear FE analysis where both the ship and bridge of interest is modelled. Here the
local deformations and damages can be found, and the force-indentation curve of the collision
is obtained. In step two, the force-indentation curve from the local collision is utilized as a
non-linear spring in the global model, representing the stiffness of the ship under impact.
To model the striking ship in the global model, a mass-spring system is used, where the P-y
curve describes the behaviour of the non-linear spring under the ship-bridge collision. As the
ship needs to detach from the bridge after collision, the P-y curve has to be zero in tension.
According to [9], this system consisting of a nodal mass and a nonlinear-spring models the
energy interaction between the ship and bridge accurately, hence this approach is chosen for
the implementation of the ship in the VBI-program. The same approach is also used in the
Rambøll’s global FE model of the SSB.

2.10.1
Rail irregularities
Rail irregularities are defined as a change in the geometry of the real rail, compared to
a theoretical straight rail.
rail irregularities can occur due to rail or support material
wear, installation or production errors, settlements or a combination of these. Horizontal
irregularities are often called gauge irregularities and vertical are called elevation. In the VBI
program both horizontal and vertical irregularities are implemented. The irregularities can
both take the form of random normal distributed irregularities, a random earthquake response
time series and sinusoidal irregularities. When introducing rail irregularities, the assumption
that the bridge deck displacement and wheel displacement are equal, is not longer true. The

2.11
Non-linear springs
With the update to the VBI program made by David Bilde Lang [3], a non-linear Newmark time
integration scheme was implemented based on [12], in order to include non-linear springs
in the vehicle-bridge-interaction analysis.
The non-linear springs, described by a tangent
hyperbolic function were implemented as foundation springs, but the implemented non-
linear time integration scheme can be used as a base to model any non-linear springs. In this
section, the general theory and main results will be presented to round up the state of the VBI
program, before the non-linear ship impact is implemented.
2.11.1
Non-linear modal Newmark time integration
As the non-linear springs are implemented, the linear EOM in (2.6) will no longer be valid,
hence the following EOM will be used
M¨x+g(x, ˙x) = f(t)
(2.71)
where g(x, ˙x) denotes the internal elastic and dissipative forces, which can both be linear and
non-linear. In this case, it is only the elastic forces that are non-linear, but the total internal
force term is kept for generality. In order to solve the non-linear EOM, a non-linear time
integration shceme has to be utilized, and in the current state of the VBI program, a non-linear
Newmark time integration scheme based on [12] is implemented.

2.12
Implementation of non-linear springs
As the time integration scheme should be able to handle several non-linear springs, such as
foundation springs, a way of implementing several non-linear springs to the pseudo code in
Tab. 2.1 should be chosen. The approx implemented in the VBI program by [3], is based
on [13]. The approach utilizes a spring placement matrix B with dimensions (n × p), where
n is the number of DOFs and nsp is the number of non-linear springs, hence each column
describes where a given non-linear spring is placed. The spring placement matrix consists of
-1,0 and 1, and these numbers describes the influence of the non-linear springs on the DOFs.
A value of 0, means that the spring does not interact with the DOF, while a value of -1 or 1
means that the non-linear spring is interaction with the corresponding DOF, in either positive
or negative direction, relative to the global axes. For a spring with a symmetrical P-y curve, the
sign does not matter, but for a ship impact where the ship detaches in tension, the sign is of
importance. In order to introduce the spring stiffness for each column in B, a diagonal matrix,
Γ with dimensions (p×p) is introduced. Γ holds the spring stiffnesses in the diagonal elements.

3
Ship representation - geometric
In the literature, a ship is often modelled as a nodal spring-mass connected to the bridge in the
FE-model at the point of impact [9] [14]. The spring is non-linear and describes the strength
of the ship. The spring force and stiffness is defined from P-y curves which is often found in
the design basis for bridge projects, which is also the case for the SSB design basis [5]. The
mass represents the mass of the ship, including the hydrodynamic mass. In order to test the
different implementations and scripts needed to construct a fully coupled vehicle-bridge-ship,
VBS, interaction model, a dynamic Matlab based 3D FE program is created. The basis of the
program consist of a static 3D beam FE program developed at DTU called MaxiFrame [4]. In [3]
a formulation of the mass matrix and an extension of the element property matrix was included
and verified to account for the mass matrix. From the extended version of MaxiFrame, the free
vibration characteristics of a given structure can be obtained, hence it is possible to examine
the dynamics of the bridge. The extended version will be named MaxiFrameDyn, and it will
be used as a testing platform for the ship representations, a new non-linear time integration
algorithm and other scripts needed to establish a fully coupled VBS model in the modal based
VBI-program.
For this part of the report, the ship is first implemented purely geometrically as a TMD in
accordance with [10]. The implementation is then validated by investigating the mechanical
energy of the ship impact, the tangent stiffness and spring forces. Afterward the script is further
expanded to include the dissipation of energy from the ship impact. In a later chapter, the
theory for a fully coupled ship-bridge system consisting of a modal bridge and a geometric ship
is presented, and it is shown that the results matches exactly those of the geometric model.
During the presentation of the theory, the Matlab scripts created will be presented, together
with relevant pseudo codes.

4.3
Generalized-α time integration scheme
Often times, discrete models used in structural dynamics analysis are only sufficiently accurate
for a number of modes below a certain frequency. Sometimes a rule of thumb stating: The first
half of the modes are sufficiently accurate to by used in the analysis. This also means that
an excitation of the higher modes can lead to unrealistic results, hence a dampening of these
modes can be favorable. Especially for impact analysis, like ship collisions which is expected to
excite the higher modes, it is expected that numerical damping of the higher modes will lead to
more precise results. In the general Newmark family, numerical damping can be introduced by
changing the γ parameter. The downside to the numerical damping in the general Newmark
algorithm is that its present already at low frequencies, which is not always desirable. However,
improved damping properties can be obtained by changing the assumption that the EOM is
satisfied at tn + 1. This modification was introduced by Hilber-Hughes-Taylor algorithm in
1977 [15], where all force terms are formed as a weighted mean between tn and tn + 1 while
retaining the acceleration at tn + 1. Wood [16] made an alternative, where the acceleration
term is formed as a weighted mean, while retaining the force terms corresponding to time
tn +1. A combination of these was developed by Chung and Hulbert [17] and is known today as
the generalized-α algorithm. Chung and Hulbert matched a weighted mean of the forces and
acceleration with different weights for the force and acceleration terms. These weights factors,
αm and αf , have given the name to the generalized α-procedure which has improved damping
properties in the high frequency regime. As the ship and foundation springs are modelled as
non-linear springs, a non-linear generalized-α procedure has to be developed. But in order to
give a better understanding of the non-linear generalized-α algorithm, the theory behind the
linear generalized-α is presented first.

5. VBI program update
Technical University of Denmark
5
VBI program update
This chapter will explain how the theory presented in chapter 3 and 4 is coupled and
implemented in the already existing VBI-program developed by Jacob Groes [1], Andreas
Indrebø [2] and David Bilde Lang [3]. It is mainly the changes to the VBI program that will
be presented in this chapter, and for a thorough description of the VBI program, see [2] and [3].
The main implementation includes extending the VBI-program from a coupled bridge-vehicle
interaction program to a coupled VBS interaction program. This includes changing the system
matrices, as the ship is formulated as a separate DOF as described in chapter 4. Previously the
non-linear foundation springs were described by a tangent hyperbolic function, but the VBI-
program is now able to use arbitrary worklaws, i.e. P-y curves, to describe the behaviour of
the non-linear foundation springs, and the non-linear ship impact. Furthermore a non-linear
generalized-α algorithm is implemented to include better numerical damping properties. The
implementation of the above mentioned additions yields a change of the existing functions,
and creation of several new, which will be presented in this chapter. A flowchart of the updated
VBI-program is presented in Fig. 5.1, where the changed and modified functions are marked
with red. An overview of the new parameters and scripts are presented in Tab. 5.2 and Tab. 5.1.

7.1.4
Deloading stiffness
As the stiffness of the ship during deloading is not well described in the literature and not
included in the SSB design basis [5], the influence is examined. The stiffness during deloading
is controlled by the factor Ke f ac, and a factor of 1 means that the deloading stiffness equals
the initial ship stiffness. It is already known that a larger deloading stiffness results in more
dissipated energy. In order to examine the influence of Ke f ac, the analysis presented at the
start of chapter 7 is conducted with changing values of the deloading stiffness, Ke f ac =[0.7,
1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0]. In Fig. 7.15, the force time series and P-y curves is presented. It clearly
shows that for lower deloading stiffness, more energy is transferred to the bridge. From the
time series it is seen that for a lower deloading stiffness, the impulse of the ship load will be
greater. From the P-y curve it can be seen that for a lower deloading stiffness, less energy is
dissipated. In Fig. 7.16 the transverse response for the node of impact and the deck node at
the pier is presented. For the node of impact, node 87, it can be seen that the greater deloading
stiffness results in the excitation of higher modes. This might be due to a more rapid change
of ship force, which can result in transient behaviour of the structure. From the transverse
response in node 29, it can be seen that the transient effect is not present in the bridge deck,
hence it will not influence the train response for this specific case. It should be noted that for
another structure it might influence the bridge deck response, hence the transient response
phenomenon should be taken into account when choosing a deloading stiffness. Furthermore,
the maximum transverse displacement for both node 29 and 87 occurs for the lower deloading
stiffness, due to less energy being dissipated. In order to check the influence of the deloading
stiffness on the safety and runability of the train passage, the maximum overturning is checked
for varying values of deloading stiffness. In Fig. 7.17, the overturning criteria as a function
of the deloading stiffness factor is presented. It can be seen that when the deloading stiffness
decreases the overturning criteria increase. The trend of the results is as expected, but the result
also clearly shows that the choice of decoupling stiffness can have an influence on the safety
and runability analysis. The changes might not seem big, but taking into account that the value

7.2
Model limitations
First of all, the model only contains 9 spans of the SSB viaduct, hence the main span, pylon
and cables and the influence on the dynamic behaviour can not be examined. Furthermore,
a ship impact on the main pylon, or while the train is on the mainspan is not examined.
In order to examine this, input created from Rambølls commercial software for the full SSB
is needed. Here the influence of the cables, pylons, bearings etc. will be included in the
mode shapes. Damping from the water on the pylons, or the hydrodynamic mass, is not
included in the current model. If damping from the water was included, it would decrease
the pylon response. The hydrodynamic mass moved during exitation of the pylons would
result in changed vibrations characteristics of the bridge, hence it will change the response.
The a hysteresis effect of the foundations springs is not implemented either. Including the
hysteresis effect for the non-linear foundation springs will dissipate energy, and hereby lower
the response. As the hysteresis effect dissipates energy during each load cycle, and the max
response was found to be during, or shortly after the ship impact, the hysteresis effect might
not have a significant influence in the safety and runability analysis of the train passage.
Even with these simplifications, the analysis can still be used to get a feeling of how the fully
coupled VBS model will behave in a simplified, but still somewhat realistic, setting. A more
important analysis is to examine the if the safety and runability analysis approach used by
Rambøll for the SSB is a good approximate. Their approach will be examined in the comming
section.
7.3
Time series vs fully coupled VBS model
In the current safety and runability study for the SSB, a coupled ship and bridge model, without
trains, is setup in the commercial FE-program of Rambøll. The ship is modelled as a nodal
spring-mass system, with the spring force- and stiffness obtained from the force-indentation
curves given in the SSB design basis [5]. The time series of the ship force is saved and send to
an external contractor, who runs the -bridge interaction analysis with the provided force time
series from the ship impact analysis. So the question is, what implications does it have on the
conclusions of the runability and safety analysis to not have a fully coupled VB model? Does it
change the ship force significantly to run the ship impact analysis without train passage on the
bridge?
To obtain the force time series, the same analysis as presented in section 7.1 is run without
the train, and the time series for the ship force is extracted. The time series is then added to
the DOF of ship impact in VBI, and the ship is excluded. The train is excluded by setting the
velocity to 0 and change all of the masses to numerical 0, and the ship is excluded by changing
the DOF of impact to a constrained DOF and setting the initial velocity to 0. One could also
change the ship mass to 0 in order to exclude the ship from the analysis. In Fig. 7.18, the
ship force is illustrated for the fully coupled VBS model and for the analysis where the train is
excluded. From the figure it seems that they are identical. The transverse displacement of the
DOF of impact is also presented in Fig. 7.18, and again the response for the two analyses seems
to match. In order to check the actual difference between the two methods, the numerical
results has to be checked. As the whole purpose of the VBI program is to examine the train
safety and runability, the most important aspect is to check the derailment and overturning

Non-linear generalized-α problems
During the implementation and validation of the generalized-α algorithm in the VBI-program,
some problems occurred with the non-linear generalized-α algorithm. It was discovered that
by lowering the timestep, the results from the generalized-α converged towards the Newmark
results, hence it must be the Newmark that obtains correct results. In order to locate the
mistake, the generalized-α algorithm was thoroughly tested. The program was stripped to the
bare minimum, and one setting after another was implemented to locate the mistake. Here
it was found that it was the implementation of the non-linear foundation springs that led to
deviation of the results. Starting with the full model from the case study, and setting the input
vectors, describing the worklaws of the foundation, to 0, both time integration algorithms
obtained the same results.
The non-linear foundation springs for both the generalized-
α and the Newmark algorithm runs on Py_numerical2 and Ks_numerical2, but for the
generalized-α algorithm, the implementation in the internal force led to incorrect results.
In Fig.
7.19, it can be observed that when decreasing the timestep, the results from the
generalized-α approaches the results from the Newmark algorithm. The non-linear foundation
springs are included as an internal force, hence it is part of g in the residual. This implies
that the mistake is in the ∆g term in (4.34). So when the timestep approaches 0, the term ∆g
approaches 0, as the internal force between timestep n and n+1 will almost be negligible. Even
though the location of the mistake was found, the exact reason was never located, as time was
running short close to deadline.

8. VBI program evaluation and future developments
Technical University of Denmark
8
VBI program evaluation and future developments
The modal representation of the ship presented in chapter 4 was implemented in the VBI
program, to extend it from a VBI model to a fully coupled VBSI model. From the validation of
the implementation of the non-linear ship impact in the VBI program in chapter 6, it has been
shown that the extension of the system matrices and the modal formulation is implemented
correctly. In this section, the limitations of the VBI program will be presented and discussed
and proposals for future development of the program will be presented.
8.1
Limitations
As this project is a continuation of three former master’s projects [1], [2] and [3], several
assumptions and limitations are present in the VBI program.
General limitations
The vehicle velocity is assumed to be equal and constant for all vehicles on the bridge during
the train passage. There is also no coupling between the individual trains, while in reality they
will pull/push the train in front and behind. For a more realistic train model, each individual
wagon should be coupled with the wagon in front and behind. This will most likely change the
train response, as the train response will be influenced by more than just a rotational DOF
in the coach of a single wagon. The VBI program is also limited to only having trains in a
single track at a time, hence it is not possible to study how trains running in different tracks
are influencing each other. As a lot of hours are put into the program by [1], [2], [3], Rambøll
and the work of this thesis, there is a need to couple all the different elements into a single VBSI
program capable of using all the functions. This will greatly improve the strength and usability
of the analysis tool, and will be of great value to Rambøll.
Non-linear contact model
An assumption for the VBI program is that the wheels will always be in contact with the bridge,
which is not necessarily the case. The assumption might be valid for many situations, it might
not always hold for high speed passage of trains. One can also easily imagine a situation where
a ship impact will results in lift of train wheels. As this is not taken into account in the current
VBI program, it could be of interest to implement a new contact model, which might be non-
linear. In relation to the contact model, the rails and tracks are assumed to be completely rigid,
hence the displacements at the interactions points are taken as the bridge displacements at the
interaction points. In reality the rail and track structure is flexible, and could be modelled as
springs through an updated contact model. From the SSB design basis, it is though stated that
the track can be assumed rigid and coupled to the deck structure.
Rail irregularities reintroduced
The rail irregularities were introduced by [2], and are implemented as a function in the current
VBI program. Somehow the functions were changed, so it is not possible to include the rail
irregularities in the analysis, which is why they haven’t been included in either this thesis or in
[3].

Hysteresis for arbitrary foundation worklaws
The hysteresis effect for the non-linear foundation springs were introduced by [3]. As the non-
linear foundation springs were based on a tangent hyperbolic function. As the foundation
spring worklaws are currently based on arbitrary input vectors, the hysteresis model has to
be updated so it can operate on any given worklaw.
Non-linear generalized-α non-linear springs
As presented in the report, the non-linear generalized-α algorithm has a bug in the
contribution to the internal force from the non-linear foundation springs.
When the
foundation springs are excluded, the results matches between the non-linear Newmark
algorithm and the non-linear generalized-α algorithm. Both algorithms runs on the same
scripts, and the mistake lies in the calculation of the internal force increment ∆g. A thorough
analysis of the simplest structure, or just a pair of fresh eyes, is needed in order to fix the bug
from the non-linear foundation springs in the non-linear generalized-α algorithm.

9. Conclusion
Technical University of Denmark
9
Conclusion
The main goal of this thesis was to develop and implement a ship into the system matrices of
the VBI program, in order to make a fully coupled vehicle-bridge-ship interaction model. The
extended VBSI program is able to model and analyse the dynamic interaction between vehicle,
bridge and ship, to analyse the safety and runability of the train passage during ship impact. A
main analysis goal was to analyse if Rambøll’s approach for conducting a safety and runability
analysis for the SSB is sufficiently accurate.
A big part of the thesis was to build a thorough understanding of the already existing VBI
program developed by [1], [2] and [3]. From the beginning, it was obvious that a non-linear
time integration algorithm, and an extension of the system matrices, was needed to develop
of fully coupled VBSI model. In chapter 2, the theory behind the VBI program is presented.
The theory covers modal analysis, the 3D vehicle model, the creation of the coupled system
matrices and load vector, the expansion of the theory to multiple wagons and trains and
the rail irregularities, developed by [1] and [2].
Furthermore the theory behind the non-
linear Newmark time integration scheme and the implementation of the non-linear springs
is presented before showcasing what the VBI program extended by [3] was capable of.
The next step was to develop the theory for including a ship into the system matrices. This
theory was first developed for a fully geometric model in chapter 3, where the implementation
takes inspiration from TMD theory.
As the ship has to detach after impact, a detaching
mechanism was included and the dissipation of energy during ship impact was included in the
algorithm. Previously, the P-y curves for the non-linear springs was based on a mathematical
expression, but the program was extended so it is able to take an arbitrary worklaw as input
for the non-linear worklaws.
It was validated that coupled ship-bridge model behaved as
expected. Afterwards, the ship theory was extended to a hybrid model where the bridge is
in modal coordinates, and the ship is in geometric coordinates, see chapter 4. The non-linear
generalized-α time integration scheme is presented, and the implementation of the new time
integration scheme and modal representation is verified against the Newmark algorithm and
the fully geometric model. It was proven that the ship representation with the modal bridge in
the generalized-α achieved the exact same results as the fully geometric model in the Newmark
algorithm.
Both the Newmark and the generalized-α algorithms with a hybrid modal bridge geometric
ship was implemented in the VBI program. In order to validate the implementation, the train
response was compared for the previous version of the VBI program, ModExp13dTrain_V2
/ MainProgram_v3, and the updated version with the inclusion of the ship in the system
matrices, ModExp13dTrain_V3 / MainProgram_v4. It was shown that the train response
is equal for the two versions. Furthermore, the bridge response from a ship impact in the
VBI program was verified against the fully geometric ship representation from MaxiFrameDyn,
and the ship was shown to behave and detach as expected. Lastly the scipts Py_numerical,
Py_numerical2, Ks_numerical and Ks_numerical2 were shown to capture the input data
correctly. The implementation was assumed to be successful, and the analysis of the fully
coupled VBSI model was the next step. Even though the program was validated, a bug in
the non-linear generalized-α algorithm was discovered. The calculation of the internal force
increment ∆g in (4.34) with contribution from the non-linear foundation springs resulted in

regarding the deloading stiffness of the ship was found, its influence was therefore analysed. It
was found, that for the specific analysis, changing the deloading stiffness to 0.7k0, increased the
overturning ratio by 3%. By increasing it from the initial stiffness to 2k0 resulted in a decrease of
the overturning ratio by 6%. For a case where the overturning criteria is not fulfilled, their might
be percentages to gain from changing the deloading stiffness, if the change is supported by the
literature and theory. Furthermore, the choice of deloading stiffness also has an influence on
the excited modes. It was found that a higher stiffness can result in transient behaviour of the
bridge during the ship impact, which was not present for a lower deloading stiffness. Due to
the influence of the deloading stiffness, it is recommended to make a literature study so an
educated choice of deloading stiffness can be chosen for future ship impact analyses.
The last analysis conducted was to check if Rambøll approach for conducting their safety and
runability analysis is a sufficiently accurate approximation. Rambøll has a full bridge-ship
interaction model of the SSB in a commercial FE-software. In the software, a ship impact
analysis without the train is conducted and the ship force time series is saved. The time series is
send to an external contractor for them to conduct the safety and runability analysis. The same
analysis was conducted in the VBI program for the above mentioned analysis. It was found that
by using the ship force time series from a ship-bridge model, and then including the time series
in a train-bridge model, a deviation of 0.2‰for the overturning criteria was found. Hence it can
be concluded that for the case of the SSB, the approach is more than sufficiently accurate.
