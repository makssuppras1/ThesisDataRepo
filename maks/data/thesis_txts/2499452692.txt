Abstract 
In the EU, 20% of the registered chemicals are categorized as UVCBs (Unknown or Variable 
composition, Complex reaction products or of Biological materials). The persistency, bioaccumu-
lation and toxicity assessment of this category of chemicals is difficult because they can be com-
posed of up to hundreds of constituents, they can be difficult to handle in standardized tests due 
to physical and chemical properties and their composition can vary. The aim of this study is to 
obtain primary biodegradation kinetics of main constituents during whole UVCB testing, and fur-
thermore to link the biodegradation kinetics to the fate-directed bioaccumulation potential and 
baseline toxicity potential. A 28 days biodegradation experiment was conducted using cedarwood 
oil as model UVCB, testing at environmentally relevant low concentrations. The experiment was 
conducted in 20 mL closed test systems with WWTP effluent as inoculum and incubated at 12°C. 
Primary biodegradation kinetics of the main constituents was determined based on substrate de-
pletion in triplicate biotic relative to abiotic test systems. The analytical method was automated 
solid phase microextraction coupled to gas chromatography with a triple quadrupole mass spec-
trometer as detector (SPME-GC-MS/MS) using a Multiple Reaction Monitoring (MRM) method. 
The analyses include 8 main constituents of the UVCB, α-Cedrene, Cedrol, Thujopsene, β-
Cedrene, β-Chamigrene, β-Himachalene, Cuparene and Widdrol, covering all fractions contrib-
uting more than 2% to the composition. All constituents were found to be primary biodegraded, 
with degradation rates in the range 0.09-0.24 d-1, resulting in half-lives of 2.9-7.3 days, but with 
uncertainty for the extent of degradation of Widdrol. From SPME-GC-MS/MS scan results it was 
found that also minor non-polar constituents were degraded and no metabolites occurred in the 
chromatogram. The metabolites are expected to be more polar and can therefore not be meas-
ured using SPME-GC. During biodegradation the bioaccumulation potential and baseline toxicity 
potential of the mixture significantly decreased. Assessing UVCBs based on whole-substance 
testing at environmentally relevant concentrations, including the possibility to assess single con-
stituents, appears to be more environmentally relevant and has a great potential for time and cost 
reductions related to producing data for these complex mixtures. Further development of this 
method requires better understanding of mixture effects, covering compositional variation of the 
UVCB and methods that can take metabolites and minor constituents into account.

1. Introduction 
The chemical sector is closely related to the global economy, therefore it has been rapidly evolv-
ing in the past decades. In 2017 the chemical industry contributed with $5.7 trillion to the global 
GDP, corresponding to 7%. That year the chemical industry was directly employing 15 million 
people and supporting 120 million jobs (Oxford Economics, 2019). The thousands of chemicals 
on the market today are indispensable in our societies, importantly influencing sectors like health, 
construction, transportation and agriculture. However, if chemicals are not managed or regulated 
correctly, it can have fatal effects on societies and ecosystems, e.g. by impacting safety of food 
or access to clean air and water (Krueger, 2015). According to the United Nations Environment 
Management Group they want to achieve, by 2020, “that chemicals are used and produced in 
ways that lead to the minimization of significant adverse effects on human health and the envi-
ronment, using transparent science-based risk assessment procedures and science-based risk 
management procedures, taking into account the precautionary approach” (Krueger, 2015).  
 
Chemicals that are persistent, bioaccumulating and toxic (PBT) or very persistent and very bio-
accumulating (vPvB) are especially of high concern. The degradation of these chemicals is slow 
or absent, causing a long retention time in the environment and they can therefore potentially be 
transported over long distances. Their bioaccumulative properties can cause biomagnification 
through food chains and since they are toxic they can cause severe effects to both humans and 
the environment (US EPA, 2018). One of the environmental risk assessment procedures within 
the EU is therefore the assessment of the PBT properties of a substance, evaluated with the 
sources and major emissions of that substance (European Commission, 2003). In EU a PBT 
assessment is required for all substances produced or imported in quantities >10 ton (EU 
Parliament and Council, 2006). Standardized test methods have been developed, separately as-
sessing the persistency, bioaccumulative and toxic properties of a substance. However, some 
compounds are out of the scope of the standardized test methods, due to their physical and 
chemical properties or because they by origin are complex mixtures and therefore are difficult to 
assess.   
 
Around 20% of the chemicals registered in EU are UCVBs (Unknown or Variable composition, 
Complex reaction products or of Biological materials) (ECHA, 2017c). Examples of UVCBs are 
biofuels, petroleum substances, vegetable oils and fragrances and flavoring agents (Clark et al., 
2013). The assessment of UVCBs poses a technical and scientific challenge because they are 
complex mixtures having a high number of constituents and have a varying composition. In addi-
tion, the constituents of UVCBs can be hydrophobic and volatile, which makes this category of 
substances hard to handle in standardized test methods (Sauer et al., 2020). At the moment, 
regulators, industry and academies are working to find a generic strategy for PBT risk assessment

1.1 Aim and objectives  
The first aim is to obtain biodegradation kinetics of main constituents during whole UVCB testing, 
using SPME-GC-MS/MS analysis and cedarwood oil as example model substance. The second 
aim is to link the change in composition of the UVCB during biodegradation to change in bioac-
cumulation potential and baseline toxicity potential, based on lipid and membrane partitioning. 
This will be addressed by the following objectives: 
1. Defining the main constituents in the tested batch of cedarwood oil. 
2. Conduct a biodegradation experiment on the whole substance, cedarwood oil, with 
wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) effluent as inoculum. 
3. Determine the biodegradation kinetics of the main constituents of the cedarwood oil using 
an SPME-GC-MS/MS method. 
4. Calculate the fate-directed concentration in the lipid at equilibrium (Clipid,equilibrium) and the 
concentration in the membrane at equilibrium (Cmembrane,equilibrium) for each of the main con-
stituents in cedarwood oil, using lipid-water (Klipid,water) partitioning coefficients, mem-
brane-water (Kmembrane,water) partitioning coefficients and the biodegradation kinetics ob-
tained from the biodegradation experiment. 
5. Calculate the fate-directed bioaccumulation potential of the whole cedarwood oil mixture 
by summing the equilibrium concentration of the main constituents in lipid as a function 
of time (ΣClipid,equilibrium (t)), and the baseline toxicity potential by summing the equilibrium 
concentration of the main constituents in membrane as a function of time (ΣCmembrane,equi-
librium (t)) . 
6. Track the change in total composition of cedarwood oil during degradation, in order to 
account for minor constituents and degradation products.

2.1 Regulation and PBT 
In 2007 the chemical legislation “Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and restriction of Chem-
icals” (REACH) entered into force in the EU, which is founded on the “no data, no marketing” 
principle. At the same time the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) was established to imple-
ment and control legislation of chemicals (EU Parliament and Council, 2006). From the REACH 
legislation, the registration of chemicals is based on evaluation of single substances, and is di-
vided into groups, depending on the manufactured and imported volumes. All chemicals manu-
factured or imported >1 ton per year need to be registered, and the higher the volume the more 
information is needed on the substance. Relevant for this study, is the requirement within this 
registration, that for all substances produced and imported >10 tons per year, the producer/im-
porter has to provide a chemical safety report, which includes an assessment of the persistence,

bioaccumulative (vPvB) assessment. The criteria for labelling a compound PBT or vPvB are given

2. Theory and background

Standardized tests are conducted to assess if a substance is PBT or vPvB (EU Parliament and 
Council, 2006). Some of these standardized tests are not suitable for substances that are hydro-
phobic and volatile or chemicals that by origin are mixtures. An example of such chemicals are 
essential oils, which are categorized as UVCBs (Unknown or Variable composition, Complex re-
action product or Biological material). 
2.1.1 Regulation of UVCBs 
According to the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA), substances are defined as UVCBs when 
they have a varying composition, a high number of constituents and are either obtained from a 
manufacturing process with many steps or obtained from a biological source, e.g. plant or animal 
material (ECHA, 2017b). Examples of UVCBs on the market are biofuels, petroleum substances, 
vegetable oils, fragrances and flavoring agents. It was estimated that around 16,000 UVCB sub-
stances were on the market in 2013 (Clark et al., 2013). 
 
There is at the moment not a generic strategy for PBT and vPvB risk assessment of UVCBs, since 
the current evaluation methods follow case-by-case and weight-of-evidence procedures. Espe-
cially standardized test methods, guiding how to test these compounds, are missing (Leonards, 
2017). There are several groups worldwide trying to find the best strategy to assess UVCBs, 
including the US EPA, ECHA and petroleum and personal care industries. In ECHA they have 
come a long way in this process, and their current UVCB assessment is based on three possible 
strategies: 1) known constituents, 2) fraction profiling and 3) whole substance approach (ECHA, 
2017a; Leonards, 2017). These three assessment strategies are briefly described below. In all 
three assessment procedures, constituents contributing more than 0.1% to the mixture are re-
garded as relevant in the PBT assessment (ECHA, 2017a). 
 
• 
Known constituents: This assessment procedure can be followed if it is known 
which constituents in the UVCB are expected to be the worst case in terms of PBT 
properties. Initial screening assessment is done for these constituents individually, 
and if needed, experimental testing on the individual constituents is conducted. The 
PBT assessment of the mixture will then be based on the PBT results of the infor-
mation and experiments conducted on the constituents expected to be the worst case 
in relation to PBT (ECHA, 2017a).  
• 
Fraction profiling: In the fraction profiling approach, the chemical is initially sepa-
rated into fractions of constituents which are expected to have similar PBT properties. 
PBT data is either collected for the fraction or for a structure chosen as representative 
for each fraction. The first step of collecting data is modelled data, such as Quantita-
tive Structure-Activity Relationship (QSAR) PBT models and predictions of physical-
chemical properties of the fraction or representative structure. Later steps can be

collection of experimental data. Based on the collected data the PBT properties of 
the UVCB can be estimated (ECHA, 2017a).  
• 
Whole substance: The present whole substance approach by ECHA, is focusing on 
PBT testing and assessment of the whole UVCB substance, considering it as one 
chemical. This assessment procedure requires that all of the constituents of the 
UVCB have very similar PBT properties. If one of the above-mentioned approaches 
is possible, ECHA recommends to follow this approach instead of the whole sub-
stance approach. In the description of this approach it is mentioned that there are 
many disadvantages and limitations related to testing on whole UVCB substance 
mixtures, especially related to that the constituents can have significantly different 
physical and chemical properties (ECHA, 2017a).  
A generic strategy for PBT risk assessment of UVCBs and guides on how to test these complex 
mixtures are needed, which takes both whole-substance and constituent evaluation into account. 
There are many challenges related to whole-substance testing, which needs to be accounted for. 
The constituents can have very different physical and chemical properties, meaning that it can be 
significantly different how they distribute and their fate (Salvito et al., to be submitted). Further-
more, when testing on mixtures, mixture effects can occur e.g. causing inhibition or enhancement 
of biodegradation (Baser and Buchbauer, 2010), which should also be considered in relation to 
whole-substance testing.  
2.1.2 Persistency  
The persistency properties of a chemical are important in relation to long-term exposure of the 
substance to the environment and the risk of the compound being transported to remote areas 
(European Commission, 2003). Persistency means the absence of degradation, and therefore 
the persistency of a chemical cannot experimentally be proven. In regulatory context, degradation 
data are used for assessing the persistency properties of a chemical. Degradation of a chemical 
can be abiotic and biotic. In many environments, the degradation of synthetic chemicals to inor-
ganic products is mainly, or in some environments entirely, governed by degradation by microor-
ganisms called biodegradation (Alexander, 1994). Therefore, data on biodegradation is of high 
importance in the regulatory assessment of the persistency of a chemical (EU Parliament and 
Council, 2006).  
 
Biodegradation is defined as “the biologically catalyzed reduction in complexity of chemical com-
pounds” (Alexander, 1994). The reduction in complexity means that the molecules are broken 
into fractions, and if the compound is completely degraded to for example H2O, CO2 and inorganic 
forms of N, P and S, the process is called mineralization (Alexander, 1994). The microorganisms 
are able to adapt to inhospitable environments, giving them an advantage in degrading organic

pollutants (Joutey et al., 2013). Important parameters that effect the rate and extent of biodegra-
dation both under natural conditions and in standardized tests are the temperature, oxygen avail-
ability, pH, chemical structure of the compound of interest, availability of  nutrients, the microbial 
population and whether the microorganisms have been exposed to the compound previously 
(Haritash and Kaushik, 2009). A general rule of thumb is that the metabolites of biodegradation 
processes will be less hydrophobic, less persistent and less toxic compared to the mother com-
pound, though there are exceptions (Smitha and Singh, 2017).  
 
For the purpose of a complete hazard identification, the biodegradation of the substance needs 
to be considered in different environmental compartments e.g. surface water, groundwater, sedi-
ment and soil, and both anaerobic and aerobic tests need to be conducted. The standardized test 
methods have reached the furthest for aerobic biodegradation in surface water, and for all new 
chemicals marketed in quantities > 1 ton in the EU, data is required on aerobic biodegradability  
obtained by standardized tests (EU Parliament and Council, 2006). There are three levels of bio-
degradation tests: Testing ready biodegradability, testing for inherent biodegradability and simu-
lation testing of biodegradation. The ready biodegradability tests and the tests for inherent bio-
degradability gives information at a screening level, whereas simulation test can be used for pre-
dicting degradation kinetics, half-lives and degradation products in various relevant environments 
(surface water, sediment, soil, marine water or marine sediment) (ECHA, 2017a). The ready bio-
degradability tests, e.g. OECD TG 301, are conducted at high concentration, 5-100 mg/l, and in 
an inoculum with low biomass (OECD, 1992). In inherent biodegradability tests, OECD TG 302, 
the circumstances for degradation are improved, by increasing the biomass to test substance 
ratio. In simulation tests the concentration of the substance should reflect environmental condi-
tions and are therefore low. The biomass can originate from recipient water, wastewater sludge 
or sediment (ECHA, 2017a). For ready and inherent biodegradability tests the test substance has 
to have a high solubility in water and it has to be non-volatile and non-adsorbing. Furthermore, 
these tests are based on CO2 generation or O2 consumption during the test period, from which 
persistent constituents in a complex mixture cannot be identified (ECHA, 2017a). These tests are 
therefore not suitable for UVCBs. ECHA (2017a) also states that a reason to deviate from the 
general approach for the simulation test can be necessary, if the substance is a UVCB, where 
different water solubilities of constituents affect the concentration at which the tests is conducted. 
The biodegradation experiment in this study, assess biodegradation testing of UVCBs by running 
an experiment on the whole mixture at environmentally relevant low concentrations. The experi-
ment is conducted in closed test systems, using Automated SPME-GC-MS/MS to determine pri-
mary biodegradation kinetics of the main constituents in a complex mixture based on substrate 
depletion.

2.1.3 Bioaccumulation 
Bioaccumulation means accumulation of chemicals in biota, where the concentration in the biota 
exceeds the concentration in the surroundings. There are two ways that chemicals can accumu-
late in biota, by uptake from surroundings, called bioconcentration or by uptake via the food chain 
where the concentration increases through several trophic levels, called biomagnification. The 
chemicals tend to accumulate in fatty organs and lipid (Baun et al., 2017). An indicator of bioac-
cumulation, is the bioconcentration factor (BCF), which is the ratio between the concentration in 
the biota and the concentration in the surroundings. According to the REACH legislation, a com-
pound is bioaccumulating if the BCF ≥ 500, and is potentially bioaccumulating if log Kow ≥ 4 (EU 
Parliment and Council, 2008). In PBT assessment a compound is considered bioaccumulating if 
BCF > 2000 in aquatic species (see Table 1) (ECHA, 2017a). 
 
In the bioaccumulation assessment of UVCBs developed by ECHA, the “known constituents” ap-
proach and the “Fraction profiling”, requires an initial screening of bioaccumulating properties of 
each of the constituents in the UVCB. The screening can for example be based on Kow or Quan-
titative Structure-Activity Relationship (QSAR) modelling. If the screening shows that some con-
stituents potentially can bioaccumulate, it is required to conduct experimental tests on representa-
tive structures (ECHA, 2017a; Leonards, 2017). In this study, the bioaccumulation potential is 
assessed for the whole UVCB substance based on the lipid-water partitioning of the main constit-
uents covering all that contributes >2% to the mixture.  
2.1.4 Toxicity 
Ecotoxicity is the toxic effects of substances on ecosystems and is assessed based on concen-
tration-response experiments. In the toxicity evaluation it is important to consider different trophic 
levels, in aquatic toxicity testing the test organisms are usually fish, crustacea and algae, and to 
consider both chronic and acute effects (ECHA, 2017a). Acute aquatic toxicity are short term 
tests, e.g. 96-hour fish test, determining the concentration at which 50% of the tested fish died 
(LC50) or a 48-hour test on crustacea, determining the concentration at which 50% of the tested 
crustacea are affected by the test substance (EC50). Chronic tests are long term tests, in the 
perspective of the length of a generation of the tested species. Examples on chronic tests are 
early life stage tests on fish, reproduction effects on daphnia or inhibition of algal growth (ECHA, 
2017a).  As seen in Table 1, a compound is considered toxic in the PBT assessment e.g. if EC10 
< 0.01 mg/L.  
 
Some chemicals can partition into the membranes of an organism. When reaching a critical 
threshold, the substance starts to affect the function of the membrane. For chemicals that do not 
have a specific mode of action, this is known as the baseline toxicity, which has been observed 
to start at a concentration between 40-160 mmolsubstance/kgmembrane. The chemicals which do not

act by a specific mode of action can cause narcosis effects and in worst case death. If the sub-
stance acts by a specific mode of action, effects might be seen at concentrations below the base-
line toxicity range, called excess toxicity (Van Wezel and Opperhuizen, 1995). In the study by 
Trac (2019) it was tested if cedarwood oil, the model substance in this thesis, caused excess 
toxicity, by testing on the whole substance. In the study, two different passive dosing approaches 
were developed, making the toxicity tests suitable for UVCBs. The dose-response curves in the 
results of the study showed that the toxicity was only observed in the baseline toxicity interval, 
meaning that cedarwood oil did not cause excess toxicity (Trac, 2019). In this thesis the baseline 
toxicity potential of cedarwood oil is assessed for the whole substance, based on the membrane-
water partitioning calculations of the main constituents. The results from Trac (2019) confirm that 
it is reasonable not to consider excess toxicity in the assessment of cedarwood oil. The toxicity 
of the constituents can be added together using the “concentration addition” method, since it has 
been proven that it is a conservative approach, even for mixtures with constituents that do not 
have the same mode of action. Thereby it is possible to assess the toxic effects of the whole 
substance based on the toxic effects of the individual constituents. The “concentration addition” 
method can be used for UVCBs when there is sufficient compositional information (Salvito et al., 
to be submitted; Cedergreen, 2014). 
2.2 Essential oils  
Essential oils are categorized as UVCBs, as they are obtained from biological material, typically 
have a high number of constituents and can have great variation in composition. A typical proce-
dure for producing essential oils are by distillation of raw plant materials (ISO, 2013). The output 
of the distillation process is the essential oil, which is a complex mixture of substances. Depending 
on the type of essential oil, the mixture can be composed of a few or more than 100 different 
substances (Turek and Stintzing, 2013). For each type of essential oil, the variations in the com-
position of end product depends on parameters such as plant health, the climate where the plant 
was produced, harvest time, processing and storage of the plant material upon distillation and 
handling and storage of the final oil product (Figueiredo et al., 2008; Baser and Buchbauer, 2010). 
Many essential oils are produced in high quantities (>1000 tons/year) or medium quantities (100-
1000 tons/year) (Baser and Buchbauer, 2010). They are commonly used in the food, cosmetics 
and pharmaceutical industries as perfume and flavor ingredients (Baser and Buchbauer, 2010; 
Turek and Stintzing, 2013).  
 
Physical and chemical properties that characterize essential oils are that they are highly volatile, 
since they are produced by distillation, they are lipophilic, have a molecular weight below 300 
g/mole and are fairly hydrophobic. The major proportion of essential oils is made up of mono- and 
sesquiterpene hydrocarbons and their oxygenated (hydroxyl and carbonyl) derivatives. Monoter-
penes consist of two isoprene units, and therefore have the molecular formula C10H16 if

unsubstituted, whereas sesquiterpenes consist of three isoprene units, and therefore have the 
molecular formula C15H24. Minor parts of the composition of essential oils are typically aliphatic 
aldehydes, alcohols, and esters (Baser and Buchbauer, 2010). Some constituents of essential 
oils have very similar chemical structure, and are known to easily convert into each other (Turek 
and Stintzing, 2013). 
2.2.1 Cedarwood oil 
The example model substance of this thesis is the essential oil extracted from the cedar tree 
called cedarwood oil. In 2008 the production volume of cedarwood oil was 1650 tons, with USA 
and China being the main production countries. The most common types of the cedarwood es-
sential oils commercially produced are: Chinese, Himalayan, Atlas, Texan and Virginian. Virgin-
ian, Texan and Chinese cedarwood oil are all produced in medium quantities, 100-1000 tons per 
year. The main application of cedarwood oil is perfumery, toiletries and household chemicals 
(Baser and Buchbauer, 2010). The type used in this study is Virginian cedarwood oil. Virginian 
cedar trees are grown in the United States. The Virginian cedarwood oil is characterized by its 
sweetly balsamic and non-smoky smell, and appears almost colorless. As a fragrance it is used 
in cosmetics, shampoos, aftershaves, furniture polish and for aromatherapy (Milchard et al., 
2019). 
 
The main constituents in cedarwood oil were identified based on a composition analysis (section 
4.1) and the resulting 8 constituents in focus in this study are: α-Cedrene, β-Cedrene, Thujop-
sene, β-Chamigrene, β-Himachalene, Cuparene, Widdrol and Cedrol. The chemical and physical 
properties for these constituents relevant for this study, are presented in Table 2. The water sol-
ubility, log(Kow) and the BCF is QSAR modelled data, and log(Kaw) is estimated from the UFZ 
LSER database. The chemical formula, C15H24, is the same for α-Cedrene, β-Cedrene, Thujop-
sene, β-Chamigrene, β-Himachalene. Cuparene has two less hydrogen atoms and Widdrol and 
Cedrol stands out by having an -OH group (chemical formula C15H26O1). This makes Widdrol and 
Cedrol more water soluble, less volatile (lower log(Kaw)) and less lipophilic (lower log(Kow) and 
BCF) than the other compounds. β-Chamigrene, β-Himachalene and Thujopsene are the least 
water soluble, and together with Cuparene they have the highest BCF. According to the REACH 
legislation, a compound is bioaccumulating if the BCF ≥ 500, and is potentially bioaccumulating 
if log(Kow) ≥ 4 (EU Parliment and Council, 2008). Based on the BCF criteria, all other constituents 
than Cedrol are considered bioaccumulating from the QSAR modelled BCFs and based on 
log(Kow) criteria, all of the constituents are potentially bioaccumulating.

3. Materials and methods  
Two experiments were conducted simultaneously, an experiment on the biodegradation of the 
main constituents in cedarwood oil (referred to as the main biodegradation experiment) and an 
experiment tracking the change in total composition of the cedarwood oil mixture during the deg-
radation (referred to as the scan experiment). An overview of the experiments is shown in Figure 
1. In both experiments, biotic and abiotic test systems were used for measuring relative substance 
depletion. In the biotic test systems, WWTP effluent was used as inoculum and the abiotic test 
systems were prepared using Milli-Q water. Both types of test systems were spiked with a stock 
solution of cedarwood oil and methanol and the test systems were incubated at 12°C. The exper-
iments were running for 28 days. For the main biodegradation experiment, there were seven 
measuring days and for the scan experiment there were four measuring days. On a measuring 
day, triplicate biotic and abiotic test systems were analyzed using Solid Phase Microextraction 
(SPME) coupled to Gas Chromatography (GC) and Mass Spectrometry/Mass Spectrometry 
(MS/MS). The Multiple Reaction Monitoring (MRM) method on the MS/MS was used for the main 
biodegradation experiment measuring the degradation of the 8 main constituents of cedarwood 
oil. In the scan experiment, the degradation was measured by a MS2 scan method on the MS/MS. 
Each step of the experiments is explained in detail in the sections below.

3.3 Experimental procedure 
3.3.1 Preparations 
The day before sampling, the blue caps flasks used for sampling water and the 20 mL vials for 
the test systems were heated to 120°C for 2 hours. The blue cap flasks were taken out and closed 
with lids and the 20 mL vials and the lids for the 20 mL vials were left in the oven overnight at 
60°C.  
3.3.2 Inoculum and water sampling procedure  
The biodegradation in this experiment is driven by microorganisms in a water sample from the 
effluent of a WWTP. The water was collected in the morning on the 5th of November 2019 at the 
WWTP Lynetten. Lynetten is the biggest WWTP in Denmark, treating wastewater from citizens 
and industry in Copenhagen municipality, Frederiksberg municipality and parts of the wastewater 
from surrounding municipalities. In total Lynetten WWTP is treating water for 750.000 person 
equivalent (Henze and Ventegodt, 2017). 
 
At Lynetten, the treated wastewater is stored in an outlet tower, before it is discharged to the 
ocean. The wastewater was let from the last treatment process to the outlet tower via large un-
derground pipes, which was connected to a small sampling container in a shed between the last 
treatment process and the outlet tower. The water was sampled from a small container in the 
shed, which Lynetten uses for automatic collection of effluent water samples for water quality 
analyses. The samples were collected and stored in 1 L blue cap flasks. All of the blue cap flasks 
were rinsed once with the effluent water before the sample was collected. It was ensured that all 
of the blue cap flasks had a proportion of air, to ensure aerobic conditions during transportation, 
except for the blue cap flasks for total suspended solids (TSS) and Non-Volatile Organic Carbon 
(NVOC) analyses, which were completely filled. The samples were stored in a cooling box during 
transportation. From the arrival at DTU and until the experiment was started, the flasks were 
stored at 15°C. The experiments were started within 8 hours from the collection time.   
3.3.3 Water quality measurements  
The following water quality parameters were measured in the WWTP effluent sample: Plate count, 
TSS, NVOC, temperature, pH, oxygen content, conductivity and DNA sequencing of the bacteria.

Simultaneously all blank, abiotic and biotic test systems were prepared for the main biodegrada-
tion experiment and the scan experiment. Firstly, the blank test systems were prepared by adding

to the vials. Then the abiotic test systems were prepared by adding 10 mL Milli-Q and spiking it

vials were cleaned with ethanol and rinsed several times with Milli-Q before the test systems were

All vials were then incubated at 12°C in a Binder incubator on tube roller mixers at ∼30 rounds 
per minute, until the vials were to be analyzed. In the incubator the vials were not exposed to

were incubated and analyzed together with the biotic and abiotic test system for reference re-

as “Old blank (incubated with the test vials)”.

3.4 Chemical analysis 
The chemical analyses were conducted by: a fully automated auto sampler (PAL RTC 120), a 
7μm polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) fiber (SUPELCO/RESTEK) used for the sample extraction and 
gas chromatography coupled to a triple quadrupole mass spectrometer (GC-MS/MS) (Agilent 
Technologies 7890B GC system / 7010B GC/TG) was used for separation and detection of the 
compounds. The GC column was from Agilent Technologies (122-5562UI) of 60 m length with a 
250μm inner diameter and 0.25μm film thickness using helium as carrier gas at 1.2 mL/min.  
 
The order of the chemical analysis conducted for one test system is illustrated in Figure 2. Firstly, 
the SPME fiber was conditioned for 5 minutes at 315°C. The first vial was then transported by the 
autosampler to the agitator port where the vial was heated to 35°C and agitated for 10 minutes, 
to ensure thermodynamic equilibrium of the test compounds between the air and water phase in 
the test system. Hereafter a needle penetrated the septum of the vial and the fiber was extracting 
the compounds during 40 minutes, where the vial was continuously agitated. Hereafter the SPME 
fiber was injected to the GC column (splitless mode), where the compounds were desorbed at 
300°C for 10 minutes. After separating the compounds in the GC column by a temperature ramp, 
they were detected by the MS/MS. After the sample had been injected to the GC column, the 
SPME fiber was conditioned for 5 minutes to minimize carryover, and the cycle started over with 
the next vial in the sequence. Each step of the chemical analysis is described in details in the 
following sections.

On the analysis days where the MS2 scan analysis was not conducted the order was the same, 
but without step 7) and 8). The test systems were analyzed alternating biotic and abiotic in order 
to minimize the time between the sets of replicates, that are compared in the data treatment. The 
reason for this is to have approximately the same degree of biodegradation in the compared vials, 
since hours of difference between analyses of the compared test systems could cause more deg-
radation in the last measured vial. Furthermore, it is to minimize the impact of analytical variation 
over time on the GC-MS/MS. On the first analysis day it was ensured that the biotic and abiotic 
test systems were no longer than 8 hours on the autosampler. Furthermore, in step 5) and 7) on 
the first analysis day, all biotic test systems were first analyzed and then all abiotic test systems 
were analyzed, because carryover was believed to be higher, than what was found after analysis 
of the sequence was done. 
3.4.2 Solid phase microextraction 
The amount of compounds left in the biotic and abiotic test systems was measured by extracting 
the compounds using the sample preparation process called Solid Phase Microextraction 
(SPME). In this technique a fiber is introduced to the sample matrix, which then extracts analytes. 
The extraction in this experiment was done with a 7 μm PDMS fiber, for 40 minutes. The PDMS 
fiber is 1 cm long, and with the penetration depth of 5 cm used in this experiment it corresponds 
to 0.7 cm of the fiber being in the water phase and 0.3 cm of the fiber being in the air phase, when 
the vial is not agitated. Usually the measurements are done in either the water phase or in the air 
phase, but in pre-test it was found that the best response was found when the measurement was 
done in the water phase. Choosing a lower penetration depth made the needle break several 
times, and the penetration depth of 5 cm was therefore chosen. After extraction the fiber was 
==PAGE:31==

19 
 
transferred to the GC injection port. The proportion of compounds extracted onto the fiber de-
pends on the partitioning coefficient between the fiber extraction phase and the matrix for each 
compound, meaning that the response between compounds are not comparable. In this study the 
results are based on relative response between abiotic and biotic test systems, which makes the 
degradation results between constituents comparable.  
3.4.3 Gas chromatography 
The analytical technique Gas Chromatography (GC) was used to separate the constituents in the 
cedarwood oil mixture. The sample was injected in the GC injection port, where the loaded SPME 
fiber was heated at 300°C for 10 minutes in order to desorb all of the compounds which were 
hereby transferred to the GC column. The GC cycle in the method used for this study was 41.2 
minutes, during which the GC column was heated with the following temperature program: 50°C 
constant for 10 minutes, then increased to 200°C during 15 minutes (30°C/min), increased to 
250°C during 35 minutes (2.5°C/min) and in the end increased to 310°C during 1.2 minute 
(50°C/min) which was held constant for 5 minutes.  
 
When the GC column is heated, the compounds volatilize and are mixed with a carrier gas (the 
mobile phase) that transports the compounds through the GC column. Depending on the molec-
ular mass, molecular structure and the volatility of the compound they are slowed down during 
their path through the column, as they interact with the surface of the column (the stationary 
phase). At the end of the column the compounds will appear at different retention times after the 
injection, the smallest and most volatile molecules will appear first and the largest and least vol-
atile will appear last (Agilent Technologies, 2015; Thet and Woo, 2019). The GC analysis method 
is well suited for analyzing essential oils due to the volatility of the oils and the separation capacity 
of the GC, making it possible to extract data for single constituents in the complex mixtures (Turek 
and Stintzing, 2013). At the end of the GC column, the compounds are transferred on to a MS/MS.  
3.4.4 Mass spectrometry/Mass spectrometry   
Two coupled mass spectrometers (MS/MS), also called a triple quadrupole mass spectrometer, 
were used after the GC column as detector. The MS/MS analysis method was in this study used 
for identification of constituents, to follow biodegradation of main constituents and to follow 
changes in the total composition of the mixture during degradation. In the MS/MS instrument the 
molecules coming from the GC column are blasted with electrons, forming ion fragments unique 
to each compound or ionizing the parent molecule. This step is called ionization. Then these ions 
travel through the first mass spectrometer, a collision cell, onto the second mass spectrometer 
before reaching the detector. The detector counts the signal from ions with the same mass to 
charge ratio (m/z) giving a spectrum as output (Stone et al., 2009). As the compounds appear at 
the MS/MS at different times, the signal from all compounds in a sample can be represented in a

MS1 scan and MS2 scan 
The MS1 and MS2 are known as scan methods. In the MS1 method the ions are separated based 
on their mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) in the first quadrupole cell, by searching for all mass-to-charge 
ratios one by one. The second and third quadrupole are turned off, so the ions are not affected 
by any processes moving through these. The MS2 method is similar to the MS1 method, but here 
the ions are separated based on their mass-to-charge ratio in the third quadrupole instead of the 
first, and the first quadrupole is turned off (Domon and Aebersold, 2006). The MS2 scan method 
was used for the scan experiment for tracking the change in composition of the total mixture. 
Product ion 
Figure 3 shows an overview of the processes in each of the quadrupoles for the product ion 
method. In this method, the first mass spectrometer is used for mass filtering, called Selected Ion 
Monitor (SIM), so only specific ions make it through to the collision cell. The selection of ions is 
based on what ion fragments the compounds of interest are known to form (Domon and 
Aebersold, 2006). If it is unknown what fragments the compounds form, this can be investigated 
by first running the MS1 scan method and analyzing the spectrum of the compounds of interest. 
In this study, ion fragments of the cedarwood oil constituents were selected based on both high 
response and high mass. In the second quadrupole, the collision cell, a fragmentation of the ions 
happens. The collision energy used for fragmentation of the ions were optimized, in order to make 
sure that the ions were not fragmented too much or too little. In the second mass spectrometer 
the ion fragments are then separated based on the m/z ratio, by searching for all mass-to-charge 
ratios one by one (Domon and Aebersold, 2006).

Multiple Reaction Monitoring 
The Multiple Reaction Monitoring (MRM) method was used for the main biodegradation experi-
ment, and an overview of the processes in each quadrupole for this method is shown in Figure 3. 
In this method, the processes in the first and second quadrupole are the same as in the product 
ion method and the optimized collision energy from the product ion method is continuously used. 
In the MRM method the ions selected to make it through the first quadrupole are called the pre-
cursor Ions. The third quadrupole is used for mass filtering, where again specific ions are selected 
to make it through, based on what fragments the precursor ions are known to form after the colli-
sion cell. If it is unknown what ion fragments the precursor ions are likely to form, this can be 
investigated from the output of a product ion analysis. The ions selected to make it through the 
third quadrupole are called product ions and the reaction from a precursor ion to a product ion is 
called an ion transition (LabCE, 2019). In this study three to four ion transitions were chosen for 
each of the main constituents in cedarwood oil, making up the MRM method used for the analysis 
in the main biodegradation experiment. As an example, Thujopsene gave a high response for the 
ions 189 and 204 in the MS1 method, which were chosen as precursor ions. In the product ion 
method, the ion 189 ion fragments gave a high response for 119 and 133, whereas the 204 ion

3.5.1 Detection limit 
The 3.3% calibration standard was detected, by a peak in the chromatogram, for all constituents 
throughout the experiment using the MRM method, except for Widdrol. Due to variations in the 
response on the GC-MS/MS over time it was decided to calculate a single detection limit ratio for 
each of these constituents from the relative response between the lowest observed response 
from abiotic test systems throughout the experiment and the highest observed response in the 
blank test system. The highest observed response in the blank included the pure effluent samples 
analyzed on day 0, the blanks which had been rolling with the test systems and the blanks freshly 
prepared. The detection limits were all rounded up to nearest whole number, meaning that the 
lowest possible detection limit ratio was set to 0.01, equal to 1%. During the experiment, a prob-
lem occurred concerning the response of Widdrol. On several days Widdrol could not be detected 
in the 3.3% calibration standards. On day 21 and 28 the 3.3% and 10% calibration standard were 
not detected as a peak, meaning that the lowest observed calibration standard was the 30%. On 
day 21 and 28 a peak was not found for Widdrol for 2 out of 3 biotic test systems. From the 
calibration standards it was known that the concentration in the vials where no peak was observed 
was below 30% of the initial concentration, but it was unknown how low these values were. The 
detection limit ratio for Widdrol was therefore assigned the value 0.1. The detection limit ratios for 
all constituents are presented in Table 5.

3.5.2 Non-linearity 
From the calibration series analyzed before and after the series of test systems each measuring 
day, it was observed that the relation between the area measured and the concentration in the 
calibration series was not a linear response for the majority of constituents measured by the MRM 
method. The influence of this on the biodegradation kinetics was investigated for α-Cedrene, Thu-
jopsene and Cedrol, since the content of these constituents in cedarwood oil is the highest and 
non-linearity was observed for two of these. The datapoints from the two calibration series from 
the same measuring day was plotted in the same figure and the best fit regression was fitted to 
the data in Excel, in both cases a 2nd order polynomial. The prescription of the 2nd order polynomial 
from one measuring day was used for translating the measured area response from the three 
biotic and three abiotic test systems from the same day into a concentration. Once all replicate 
measurements had been translated into concentration, the concentration ratios between biotic 
and abiotic test systems were calculated, and transferred to GraphPad Prism 8. The mean and 
the SEM of the relative concentration were plotted and a first order degradation model was fitted 
to the data, following the same procedure as the data treatment for the relative peak areas. The 
data points which were found to be under the detection limit were assigned the detection limit 
ratio found in section 3.5.1.

3.7.1 Partitioning coefficients 
The air-water partitioning coefficients (Kair/water), lipid-water partitioning coefficients (Klipid/water) and 
the membrane-water partitioning coefficients (Kmembrane/water) for each of the main constituents in 
cedarwood oil were provided by Goss (2019) obtained from the UFZ LSER Database (model 1) 
(Ulrich et al., 2017). The partitioning coefficients are shown in Table 6. The UFZ LSER Database 
is developed by Helmholtz Centre for Environmental Research and it uses SMILES as input to 
calculate descriptors.  
 
The lipid-water partitioning coefficients were used for estimating the concentration in storage lipid 
at equilibrium. Since the storage lipid phase is homogenous and is well defined, the root mean 
square error (RMSE) is expected to be between 0.4-0.5 log units for calculated descriptors from 
the UFZ LSER Database (Goss, 2019). The membrane-water partitioning coefficients are used 
for estimating the concentration in membrane lipids at equilibrium. The membrane phase is inho-
mogeneous and is therefore not well defined. This results in a higher RMSE, which for calculated 
descriptors from the UFZ LSER Database is estimated to be between 0.8-1 log units (Goss, 
2019).

4.2 Experimental quality assurance  
Several parameters were measured and analyzed to ensure the quality of the experiment. In 
this section these analyses are presented including a characterization of the inoculum, the tem-

effect of the non-linearity observed in the calibration standards.  
4.2.1 Water quality of inoculum 
The results of the measured water quality of the WWTP effluent sample, used as inoculum for the 
biotic test systems, are given in Table 8. At the inlet of a WWTP the temperature ranges between 
5-20°C, and the pH is in the range of 7-8 (Henze et al., 2010). The temperature and pH at the 
effluent was found to be within these ranges, which was expected, since the treatment processes 
at the WWTP should not cause great variations in pH and temperature. For the oxygen content, 
it was desired to run an aerobic experiment, and it was desired that the inoculum came from 
aerobic conditions, which the WWTP effluent fulfills with an oxygen concentration of 9 mg/L. For 
comparison, an oxygen concentration of 4-15 mg/L is needed for fish living in shallow water 
(Fondriest Environmental, 2013). Water samples typically appear clear if the TSS concentration 
is below 20 mg/L. The water sample from the WWTP effluent appeared clear, and was found to 
have a low TSS content of 4.2 mg/L. Bacterial density of the WWTP effluent were found to be in 
the same order of magnitude as the bacterial density found in a rural lake and an urban lake and 
the NVOC content was found to be comparable to five surface water samples from the study by 
Birch et al. (2017).

same time fit with the explanation that the volatile constituents might have escaped from the vial 
via the gas phase.

5. Discussion  
5.1 UVCB assessment  
In the paper by Salvito et. al. (to be submitted), the state of the science of UVCB assessment is 
summarized and critical research needs are identified. In the paper it is stated that there is “a 
need for a multi-pronged and transparent approach that integrates whole-substance and constit-
uent-based information” (Salvito et al., to be submitted). Some of the current UVCB assessment 
methods focus on either testing or assessing singe constituents or fractions expected to have 
similar fate and toxicity properties or on the whole-substance without considering the constituents, 
as described in section 2.1.1 (ECHA, 2017a). In this thesis biodegradation kinetics of main con-
stituents in a UVCB were successfully determined testing on the whole-substance. The results of 
the biodegradation of the individual constituents were used to assess the properties of the whole 
UVCB, in relation to the biodegradation, bioaccumulation potential and baseline toxicity potential. 
The method used in this study thereby takes both whole-substance and constituent-based infor-
mation into account. A potential of this method is to identify constituents in UVCBs that are not 
degraded. If any is observed, a screening of the change in bioaccumulation potential and baseline 
toxicity potential of the mixture can be used to assess, if the persistency of these constituents will 
have a major or minor influence on the bioaccumulation potential and baseline toxicity potential 
of the mixture.  
 
The advantages of the method used in this study are the possibility to test on a whole-substance 
UVCB at environmentally relevant low concentrations, where the biodegradation of the constitu-
ents can affect each other, which will also be the case in the environment. By using SPME-GC-
MS/MS it is possible to separate and identify constituents and determine biodegradation kinetics 
for the main constituents. A disadvantage of the method used in this thesis, using MRM to obtain 
biodegradation kinetics, is that it only gives results on primary biodegradation. It is therefore not 
possible to consider metabolites in the assessment. It was attempted to address this by the par-
allel experiment using the scan method, but no degradation products were observed in the chro-
matogram. The governing reason is that the used SPME-GC chemical analysis technique is fo-
cusing mainly on the non-polar, lipophilic and volatile compounds. Typically, metabolites are more 
polar making them more available to the microorganisms in water. Therefore, polar metabolites 
are often more easily degraded, but there are exceptions which will not be discovered using the 
SPME-GC technique used in this thesis. If it is desired to follow biodegradation of polar metabo-
lites, a parallel biodegradation experiment could be conducted with an analytical method more 
suitable for polar constituents.  
 
==PAGE:53==

41 
 
In some cases, non-polar UVCBs also contain a small fraction of polar constituents, which will 
also not be assessed with the SPME-GC chemical analysis technique used in this thesis. When 
assessing a UVCB it is relevant to firstly consider the production procedure of the UVCB. Since 
a typical production procedure of essential oils is distillation of plant material, common properties 
of the essential oil constituents are, that they are volatile, lipophilic and fairly hydrophobic. In this 
case analyses with SPME-GC, might be sufficient for covering the primary biodegradation of the 
mixture. If the UVCB includes a small fraction of polar constituents and it is desired to assess the 
biodegradation these constituents, a parallel experiment with an analytical technique suitable for 
polar compounds could again be a solution. 
 
Biodegradation kinetics were in this study obtained for all constituents contributing > 2% to the 
mixture, covering in total around 80% of the mixture. In the current legislation all constituents 
contributing > 0.1% need to be included in the assessment of a UVCB (ECHA, 2017a). From the 
scan results, all peaks in the chromatogram at the end of the experiment from the biotic test 
systems were on the same level as peaks in the chromatogram from the blank vials, indicating 
that also all minor lipophilic constituents were degraded. By further development of the method, 
it could be possible to obtain biodegradation kinetics of minor constituents as well. More research 
is needed, before it can be evaluated how much of a UVCB in general it is possible to cover with 
this method, especially taking into account that the MRM method development and data treatment 
becomes more time-consuming the more constituents that are included in the method.  
5.2 Is cedarwood oil PBT? 
In the safety data sheet from the producer of the cedarwood oil product it is stated that “this 
substance/mixture contains no components considered to be either persistent, bioaccumulative 
and toxic (PBT), or very persistent and very bioaccumulative (vPvB) at levels of 0.1% or higher” 
(Givaudan, 2005). The results found in this thesis of the biodegradation, bioaccumulation potential 
and baseline toxicity potential of cedarwood oil are in this section discussed with the purpose of 
how the results can be used for PBT assessment. Furthermore, the results will be compared to 
previous evaluations of cedarwood oil and cedarwood oil constituents. 
 
Persistency: All constituents in cedarwood oil in focus in this thesis were in QSAR-modelled data 
evaluated to be “not readily biodegradable” (see Appendix A). The biodegradation results ob-
tained in this thesis show that the main constituents in cedarwood oil are biodegradable in WWTP 
effluent, since all constituents were degraded within the 28 days (with uncertainty for Widdrol). 
The half-lives of the constituents were found to be in the range of 2.9-7.3 days. These are low 
when compared to the persistency label in PBT assessment is given if the half-life in marine water 
> 60 days or in fresh water > 40 days (Table 1), even though the half-life in WWTP effluent is 
expected to be lower than in marine and fresh water. From the results it cannot be concluded that 
cedarwood oil is “readily biodegradable”, since that requires other test circumstances. Therefore, 
==PAGE:54==
5. Discussion 
 
42 
 
the QSAR evaluation, saying that none of the constituents are readily biodegradable cannot be 
disproved or confirmed, based on the results from this thesis. 
Based on the use of cedarwood oil, it is expected that its main route of entry to the environment 
is through a WWTP. The constituents α-Cedrene, β-Cedrene, Thujopsene, β-Chamigrene, β-Hi-
machalene and Cuparene all had short lag-phases (<1 day), which could be an indication of that 
the microorganisms in the effluent, coming from the WWTP, are used to degrade these or similar 
compounds, and therefore an adaptation phase is not needed. Since cedarwood oil is presumed, 
from the results in this thesis, to be degraded in the WWTP effluent it is also expected to be 
degraded in the WWTP. Using products that contain cedarwood oil, which ends up at the WWTP, 
would according to this scenario not expose the environment to any non-degradable constituents. 
This evaluation does not take into account that the composition of cedarwood oil can vary and it 
is only based on primary degradation. Fate of metabolites and possible polar constituents should 
also be accounted for in further assessment of cedarwood oil. Furthermore, it would be highly 
relevant to assess degradation of cedarwood oil in sludge, since sludge adsorption is the most 
important removal process for all of the constituents according to QSAR modelled data (see Ap-
pendix A). 
 
Bioaccumulation: In the study by Sühring et al. (2019) it was found that α-Cedrene, β-Cedrene, 
Thujopsene and Cuparene had a median BCF>2000 and would therefore be categorized as ‘bi-
oaccumulating’ according to the PBT criteria. Cedrol was found to be ‘not bioaccumulating’ ac-
cording to the PBT criteria. The results were based on dietary exposure of the cedarwood oil 
mixture to fish. This experimental data is in alignment with the predictions from the QSAR data 
(Table 2), where based on modelled BCF values all constituents except Widdrol and Cedrol are 
characterized as bioaccumulative based on the PBT criteria (DTU food, 2019). Using the biodeg-
radation kinetics from this thesis to obtain a fate-directed bioaccumulation potential for cedarwood 
oil, a significant decrease was observed during biodegradation. The risk related to the bioaccu-
mulative constituents will therefore depend on the exposure scenario. In the scenario where all 
of the cedarwood oil is removed at the WWTP, there would be no risk for the aquatic biota at the 
discharge point of the WWTP. Another scenario could be that these compounds are continuously 
discharged to a surface water body, where steady state concentration could be reached depend-
ing on biodegradation and other removal processes. In this case the lipophilic constituents would 
bioaccumulate and potentially cause a risk for the aquatic environment, based on the results from 
Sühring et al. (2019). 
Regarding metabolites of the cedarwood oil mixture, these are from the scan results predicted to 
be less bioaccumulative than the parent compounds, since they could not be measured using 
SPME, which is a phase comparable to lipid.

Toxicity: In the study by Trac (2019), it was found that cedarwood oil only caused baseline toxicity 
and not excess toxicity. In this thesis the initial concentration of cedarwood oil in the test systems 
corresponds to a baseline toxicity potential of 45 mmol/kgmembrane. Subject to the uncertainty re-
lated to this value and that it is determined by the spiked concentration, this is in the lower end of 
the baseline toxicity interval (40-160 mmol/kgmembrane), meaning that this level could be toxic to 
aquatic organisms. At the end of the experiment the baseline toxicity potential had decreased to   
< 1 mmol/kgmembrane, which is well below the baseline toxicity interval. These results indicate that 
the fate-directed baseline toxicity potential of cedarwood oil significantly decreases during degra-
dation in WWTP effluent. From the results obtained in this report, the toxicity of the metabolites 
cannot be evaluated. As for the bioaccumulation potential, the risk related to the toxicity of cedar-
wood oil is depending on the exposure scenario. 
 
To sum up, a UVCB would only be considered PBT (or vPvB) if one of the constituents had been 
found to be P, B and T or vPvB according to the criteria in Table 1. Since the main constituents 
of cedarwood oil were found to be biodegradable in this study, that would point towards that ce-
darwood oil should not be categorized as PBT or vPvB, even though several constituents have 
been found to be bioaccumulating. Due to the limitations of the method used in this thesis, more 
data is needed for a complete persistency assessment, especially regarding minor constituents, 
metabolites, compositional variation and degradation in other environmental compartments.  
5.3 Biodegradation of cedarwood oil: Testing on mixtures vs. test-
ing single constituents  
An emerging research topic is whether or not to test chemicals as mixtures. The majority of current 
regulatory test methods are on single substances, whereas the chemicals will almost always be 
present in mixtures in the environment. Research has shown that testing on mixtures can cause 
interactions, resulting in enhanced or inhibited biodegradation. Enhanced biodegradation can for 
example be due to co-metabolism, defined as “the transformation of a non-growth substrate in 
the obligate presence of a growth substrate or another transformable compound” (Dalton and 
Stirling, 1982). Inhibition of biodegradation can for example be caused by the process called com-
petitive inhibition, where less degradation is seen for a substance due to the presence of another 
substance that it has to compete with e.g. for bonding to an enzyme catalyzing the degradation 
of the desired substance (NCGC, 2010). Co-metabolism and competitive inhibition have for ex-
ample been observed for hydrocarbons in Charng et al. (1993). Mixture effects have also been 
found to have no or only a limited effect on biodegradation half-lives, e.g. in a study testing com-
posed hydrocarbon mixtures with an increased number of constituents at environmentally rele-
vant low concentrations (Hammershøj et al., 2019). Mixture effects are still not fully understood, 
but taking into account that some UVCBs consist of hundreds of constituents, there could be 
many advantages related to testing on the whole-substance mixtures for this category of chemi-
cals. 
==PAGE:56==

Biodegradation tests on single substances have previously been conducted for four of the con-
stituents in cedarwood oil: α-Cedrene, Cedrol, Thujopsene and β-Himachalene (Jenner et al., 
2011). The tests followed the procedure of the aerobic ready biodegradability test, OECD 301F 
Manometric Respirometry. If the biological oxygen demand (BOD) exceeds 60% during 28 days 
of experiment, the compound is considered readily biodegradable. The inoculum used in the tests 
were fresh activated sludge from a biological WWTP and the compounds were tested separately 
at a concentration of 100 mg/L (Jenner et al., 2011). The results of the study were that α-Cedrene 
and Cedrol were found to be readily biodegradable. Thujopsene and β-Himachalene were not 
found to be readily biodegradable, but 36-38% was degraded after 28 days and 51-56% after 60 
days (Jenner et al., 2011).  
The results from the study by Jenner et al. (2011) cannot directly be compared to the results found 
in this thesis, because of different experimental procedures, but the conclusion of the study by 
Jenner et al. (2011) is more conservative than the conclusion in this thesis. In the study by Jenner 
et al. (2011) it is stated “given that the shape of the biodegradation curves indicates poor bioa-
vailability and ready biodegradability tests are very stringent, it is expected that all the sesquiter-
penes tested in the present study would be degraded under environmental conditions”. The 
method used in this thesis is expected to be more environmentally relevant, because the com-
pounds are tested at a low concentration and in the original composition of the mixture. As pre-
dicted in the study by Jenner et al. (2011) more biodegradation of Thujopsene and β-Himachalene 
was observed in this thesis, when comparing the biodegradation results after 28 days from the 
two studies. It could be mixture effects that caused enhanced degradation in this thesis. If that is 
the case, this might as well happen in the environment, since the test is on the original mixture 
and not a composed mixture. It can as well be caused by other differences in test parameters. 
Especially the great difference between the tested concentrations can have an important influ-
ence on the biodegradation (Hammershøj et al., 2019), where the concentration tested in this 
thesis is expected to be more environmentally relevant. Testing on the whole-substance can 
therefore be more environmentally relevant, but there are also disadvantages and complications 
related to whole-substance testing. It is not easy to handle many constituents with significantly 
different physical and chemical properties in laboratory settings and analytical methods, making 
sure that it is as environmentally relevant as possible.  
 
For UVCBs that by origin are mixtures, an assessment based on whole-substance testing, instead 
of testing on the constituents individually, appears to be more environmentally relevant and has 
a great potential for time and cost reductions related to producing data for these complex mix-
tures. Assessing based on whole-substance testing would require that the mixture mechanisms 
are understood to a degree where it can be expected that these tests are more environmentally 
relevant, especially if they are less conservative than testing on single constituents.

6. Conclusion 
In this study it was found that the major constituents in the tested cedarwood oil batch were: α-
Cedrene (23%), Cedrol (21%) and Thujopsene (19%). Furthermore, the following constituents 
were included in the study as main constituents, as they contributed to the mixture composition 
with a fraction of ≥ 1%: β-Cedrene (5%), β-Chamigrene (1%), β-Himachalene (2%), Cuparene 
(1%) and Widdrol (2%). Biodegradation kinetics and half-lives for these main constituents were 
successfully determined by running a biodegradation experiment testing on the whole substance 
at an environmentally relevant low concentration of 57 μg/L. The effluent from a WWTP was used 
as inoculum and the biodegradation was measured with an SPME-GC-MS/MS analytical method. 
From MRM results all main constituents were found to be degraded within the 28 days, but with 
uncertainty of the extent of degradation of Widdrol due to detection problems. The biodegradation 
rates in the test systems were for all constituents found to be within the same order of magnitude, 
0.09-0.24 d-1, resulting in half-lives of 2.9-7.3 days. Cuparene was the constituent that had the 
longest half-life of 7.3 days. From SPME-GC-MS/MS scan results the change in total composition 
of the mixture during biodegradation was tracked. From these results it was found that also minor 
constituents in the chromatogram, not covered by the MRM method, were degraded and no deg-
radation products occurred in the chromatogram. Overall the results show that the constituents in 
cedarwood oil are biodegradable, indicating that cedarwood oil should not be characterized as 
‘persistent’ in the PBT assessment. 
 
The biodegradation was linked to bioaccumulation potential and baseline toxicity potential by lipid-
water and membrane-water partitioning calculations. It was found that biodegradation significantly 
decreased the potential of baseline toxicity and bioaccumulation, however the risk of toxic and 
bioaccumulative effects in the environment will, among other parameters, depend on the expo-
sure scenario.  
 
The SPME-GC-MS/MS MRM method used in this thesis was found to be highly successful in 
determining primary biodegradation kinetics of main constituents during whole-substance testing 
of a UVCB. Hereby this method can be useful in the future of UVCB testing and assessment. In 
future studies the method can be developed to also assess the biodegradation kinetics of minor 
constituents, by including these in the MRM method. Another possibility of further developing this 
method is to work on assessing the biodegradation of metabolites. Since metabolites often are 
polar, running a parallel experiment using an analytical method suitable for polar constituents 
could be interesting to try out.
