Abstract 
The valorization of shrimp processing by-products offers a sustainable avenue for 
recovering high-value bioactive compounds, including astaxanthin and bioactive 
peptides. Nevertheless, the oxidative instability of these compounds restricts their direct 
use in food and nutraceutical applications. This study explores the utilization of β-
cyclodextrin (β-CD), 2-hydroxypropyl-β-cyclodextrin (HP-β-CD), and shrimp-derived 
tryptic peptides as encapsulating and stabilizing agents for astaxanthin-enriched shrimp 
oil within low-fat oil-in-water emulsions. 
Emulsions were formulated using cyclodextrins either alone or in conjunction with 
peptides, or peptides alone and were characterized for their physical stability, oxidative 
resistance, and encapsulation efficiency. The HP_OP formulation, which included both 
HP-β-CD and peptides, exhibited the most advantageous performance, characterized by 
reduced droplet size, minimal destabilization and favorable ζ-potential and TSI 
measurements. Oxidative stability was evaluated through peroxide value, astaxanthin 
retention measured by HPLC, and the formation of volatile compounds analyzed via 
GC–MS. The inclusion of peptides significantly enhanced antioxidant protection, while 
HP-β-CD enhanced emulsion homogeneity and decreased sedimentation. Gravimetric 
analysis validated high encapsulation efficiency in systems utilizing both agents. 
Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) revealed molecular interactions 
between oil, peptides, and cyclodextrins, thereby supporting the establishment of stable 
inclusion complexes.  
In summary, the study substantiates the dual function of tryptic peptides as natural 
emulsifiers and antioxidants, emphasizing their synergistic potential with HP-β-CD in 
safeguarding astaxanthin-rich marine oils. These results lay the groundwork for the 
creation of stable, bioactive delivery systems derived from seafood by-products, 
thereby contributing to food sustainability and the innovation of functional ingredients.

1. Introduction 
In recent years, the equation "World food security = sustainable production of food" 
appears to be an enigmatic and seemingly impossible challenge. According to the Food 
and Agriculture Organization (FAO), approximately one-third of global food 
production is either lost or wasted, which includes seafood. Following the conclusion 
of the Second World War, there has been a persistent rise in the demand for food that is 
affordable, convenient, and easy to prepare, leading to an increase in the consumption 
of highly processed foods, particularly in Western nations (Popkin, 2017). Nevertheless, 
dietary inadequacy remains a significant concern for the global population. This issue 
affects not only vulnerable demographics, such as infants, pregnant and lactating 
women, the elderly, and individuals who must avoid certain foods, but also those who 
are otherwise healthy (Minervini et al., 2022). Non-communicable diseases (N-NCDs) 
like diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular issues, and neurodegenerative disorders 
serve as a reminder to consumers about the critical connection between nutrition and 
health. N-NCDs, along with various other chronic illnesses, are often initiated by 
oxidative damage that leads to chronic inflammation. The increasing recognition of the 
strong correlation between nutrition and health encourages consumers to explore 
healthy eating habits and the positive impacts of nutraceutical and functional foods, 
particularly those with high antioxidant properties, in disease prevention (Jamioł-Milc 
et al., 2021).  
Food waste is a growing global issue, increasingly acknowledged as a significant 
sustainability challenge that has garnered heightened awareness in contemporary times. 
However, food by-products can serve as a valuable source of bioactive compounds that, 
when properly recovered and extracted, can be transformed into high nutritional value 
food ingredients, supplements, and/or nutraceutical formulations. These compounds 
can be utilized primarily in processed or packaged foods for various functions: to 
provide substances that reduce the risk of developing N-NCDs; to enhance nutritional 
content or texture; to extend shelf life; to inhibit spoilage microorganisms; or as 
alternatives to synthetic additives.  
The minimization of food by-products and waste aligns seamlessly with numerous 
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), influencing positively the environment, 
enhancing food security for the global population, and supporting the development of 
==PAGE:10==
4 
 
a circular economy. Specifically, SDG 12 aims to reduce per capita global food waste 
at both retail and consumer levels by 50% and to decrease food loss throughout 
production and supply chains by the year 2030. Furthermore, the "Zero Hunger goal" 
(SDG 2) seeks to eradicate hunger, ensure food security, and enhance nutrition while 
fostering sustainable agricultural practices. The reduction of by-products and waste also 
supports the fulfillment of SDG 6 (sustainable water management) and SDG 14 (marine 
resources), among other SDGs (Minervini et al., 2022). 
The aquaculture industry is among the fastest-growing sectors globally. As seafood is a 
vital component of a balanced diet, there is increasing consumer demand for 
aquaculture fish products that offer both high nutritional value and organoleptic 
qualities comparable to wild-caught fish. Although aquaculture is considered relatively 
sustainable, further research is needed to enhance its efficiency, particularly in the 
valorization of shrimp processing by-products. 
With the rising global demand for shrimp, industrial processing has intensified, 
resulting in significant quantities of shrimp waste. This waste, comprising 
cephalothoraxes, heads, shells, tails, pleopods, and other exoskeletal appendages, 
currently lacks value-added applications and is often discarded, contributing to 
environmental concerns. Annually, 6 to 8 million tons of crustacean waste, primarily 
from shrimp, crab, and lobster, are generated, with shrimp processing alone producing 
45–60% waste by volume. Improper disposal increases biochemical oxygen demand 
and causes strong odors during decomposition (Fotodimas, Ioannou and Kanlis, 2024). 
However, shrimp waste is rich in valuable bioactive compounds, including proteins, 
chitin, and carotenoids. The extraction of lipid extracts rich in polyunsaturated fatty 
acids (PUFAs), the carotenoid astaxanthin and bioactive peptides have been proposed 
as some of the possible alternatives to give value to this waste. Harnessing these 
compounds offers a sustainable solution for reducing environmental impact while 
promoting human health. In particular, shrimp oil enriched with astaxanthin shows 
potential as a functional food ingredient, especially for populations with limited access 
to fish (Montero et al., 2016). 
To protect shrimp oil from oxidation and enhance its stability, encapsulation techniques, 
such as ultrasonication to produce nanoemulsions, are employed. The effectiveness of 
encapsulation, however, depends on the choice of wall materials and methods 
==PAGE:11==
5 
 
(Rajasekaran et al., 2024a). This study investigates the potential cyclodextrin and 
tryptic peptides in improving the physical stability, oxidative stability, and 
encapsulation efficiency of astaxanthin-enriched shrimp oil for enhanced application in 
the food industries. The research questions guiding this investigation are:  
• How do cyclodextrins (β-CD and HP-β-CD) and peptides affect the physical 
stability of low-fat oil-in-water emulsions containing astaxanthin-enriched 
shrimp oil?  
• What is the impact of incorporating tryptic peptides on the stability and 
emulsifying properties of cyclodextrin-based emulsions?  
• How do different encapsulation systems (with or without peptides) influence the 
oxidative stability of astaxanthin-enriched shrimp oil, as measured by primary 
and secondary oxidation products? 
• What are the physicochemical interactions between shrimp oil, cyclodextrins, 
and peptides as characterized by spectroscopic and analytical methods? 
• To what extent do cyclodextrins and tryptic peptides improve the encapsulation 
efficiency of astaxanthin in shrimp oil emulsions? 
To address these questions, the study aims to: 
• Evaluate the physical stability of low-fat oil-in-water emulsions formulated with 
cyclodextrin, peptides and a combination of both. 
• Determine the storage stability of the emulsified systems under different 
encapsulation conditions. 
• Analyze the oxidative stability of astaxanthin-enriched shrimp oil by monitoring 
primary and secondary lipid oxidation. 
• Characterize the physicochemical properties of the encapsulated formulations 
using spectroscopic and analytical methods. 
• Assess the encapsulation efficiency of astaxanthin when stabilized with 
cyclodextrins, peptides or both agents.

2. Literature review 
2.1 Shrimp business 
Shrimps belong to the Crustacea family, which also includes commonly harvested 
species such as crabs, prawns, Antarctic krill, and crayfish. According to Food and 
Agriculture Organization (FAO), global shrimp catch is estimated at approximately 3.4 
million tons annually, with Asia being the leading region for shrimp fisheries (Jafarpour 
et al., 2024). Among the various identified species, the Northern shrimp (Pandalus 
borealis) is the most prevalent species located in the colder regions of the Atlantic and 
Pacific Oceans, accounting for 31,551 tons. In Denmark, the fishing of P. borealis has 
evolved into an international fishery, producing up to 12,500 tons each year. 
Additionally, Denmark serves as an intermediary for the final processing of catches 
from various countries. In 2009, over 79,000 tons of shrimp were imported into 
Denmark, primarily from Greenland and Canada (Razi Parjikolaei et al., 2015).  
 Shrimp constitutes 7% of aquaculture production, and it is considered a fishery product 
of high economic value. Over the past two decades, shrimp consumption has risen 
significantly, leading to the development of a wide range of shrimp-based food products 
(Ambigaipalan and Shahidi, 2017a). However, the growth of the shrimp processing 
industry has also resulted in an increasing volume of shell waste, which poses 
environmental concerns. Depending on the species, approximately 45–48% of the 
shrimp's raw weight is discarded during processing. This by-product, primarily 
composed of cephalothorax and cuticles from peeling, offers potential for valorization, 
as it is rich in functional components.  
Shrimp shell waste contains about 18–40% protein, 35% minerals, and 14–30% chitin. 
It is also a valuable source of bioactive compounds, including carotenoids such as 
astaxanthin, polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs), and carotene-protein complexes. 
These components have shown promise for use in the development of value-added 
products (Eggink, Gonçalves and Skov, 2024).  
Particularly, the health benefits of n-3 long-chain PUFAs, such as eicosapentaenoic acid 
(EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), are well established, with many dietary 
guidelines recommending increased intake. Additionally, astaxanthin has attracted 
scientific interest for its potential role in preventing and managing various diseases, 
==PAGE:13==
7 
 
including cancers, chronic inflammatory conditions, metabolic syndrome, diabetes, and 
cardiovascular disorders (Gomez-Estaca et al., 2016; Montero et al., 2016).  
 
2.2.1 Bioactive compounds 
Bioactive compounds encompass a diverse range of molecules, including proteins, 
peptides, various amino acids, and a significant number of phenolic-based compounds. 
Throughout the food processing stages, substantial quantities of both solid and liquid 
waste are produced. When these wastes contain bioactive compounds, it is essential to 
explore strategies for their recovery and utilization. The bioactive compounds derived 
from food are primarily peptides, which serve as a source of components that enhance 
health (Gringer, 2015).  
 A variety of bioactive compounds derived from marine by-products can be utilized to 
create consumables for human consumption. Apart from the high quality fat that marine 
side-streams contains such as EPA and DHA, known for their prevention from 
atherosclerosis, they also contain protein, minerals, vitamins and antioxidants that are 
also believed to impact human health. The greatest profitability is anticipated from 
bioactive compounds, including functional foods and nutraceuticals. 
Functional foods can be described as "those foods or ingredients that, when consumed 
regularly, produce a specific health benefit that goes beyond their fundamental 
nutritional value". Typically, these foods are composed of varying amounts and types 
of bioactive compounds (Nobili et al., 2009; Joana Gil‐Chávez et al., 2013). 
Nutraceuticals might be defined as "dietary supplements that provide a concentrated 
form of bioactive compounds derived from food, existing in a non-food matrix, and 
utilized with the intention of improving health in dosages that surpass those achievable 
through regular food consumption" (Shahidi, 2009). 
 
2.2.1.1 Bioactive peptides 
Bioactive peptides (BPs) are specific protein fragments composed of short amino acid 
sequences, typically between 2 and 20 residues, with molecular weights generally less 
than 10 kDa. These peptides are typically inactive within the sequence of the parent 
protein but become functionally active following hydrolysis, either through food 
==PAGE:14==
8 
 
processing or digestion. Once released, BPs can exert a variety of physiological effects, 
including antioxidant, anti-hypertensive, anti-inflammatory, hypoglycaemic, and 
antimicrobial activities. Notably, some peptides may exhibit multiple bioactivities, 
highlighting their potential for diverse biomedical and industrial applications 
(Bhandari et al., 2020).  
Marine by-products, such as those derived from fish, algae, crustaceans, and sponges, 
are increasingly recognized for their high protein content (10–20%) and suitability as a 
raw material for BP production. Among these, shrimp by-products have attracted 
significant attention due to their abundance, potential health benefits, and lack of 
toxicity. Recent studies have demonstrated that BPs derived from shrimp waste possess 
a wide array of biological functions, including angiotensin-converting enzyme 
inhibition, antioxidant, anti-hypertensive, anti-inflammatory, antiproliferative, and 
hypoglycaemic properties. These functional peptides may serve as dietary supplements 
or be incorporated into pharmaceutical and nutraceutical products (Rodríguez-Jiménez 
et al., 2024a). In addition to their primary use in the development of functional foods 
and/or nutraceuticals aimed at enhancing human health, BPs can also serve as food 
additives due to their techno-functional characteristics, which include solubility, 
emulsifying ability, foaming, water/oil holding capacity and gelling (Hajfathalian et al., 
2018). Protein emulsifiers possess the ability to reduce the tendency of the droplets in 
an emulsion system to coalesce and the tendency of the emulsion to break down (Zayas, 
1997). Moreover, owing to their antimicrobial and antioxidant properties, they are 
excellent candidates for use as natural food preservatives, thereby extending the shelf 
life of food products or being incorporated into packaging materials in the form of 
edible films and coatings to bolster food safety (Aguilar-Toalá et al., 2022). 
Given their emulsifying ability, BPs also be considered part of the broader group of 
emulsifiers, which includes proteins, phospholipids, and carbohydrates that enhance 
food texture, appearance, and flavor while aiding in the production of low-fat products. 
Although, the use of emulsifiers is regulated, the accurate measurement of their intake 
remains challenging. Emulsifiers and thickeners can affect the gut microbiota, mucosal 
barrier, and inflammation, with dietary emulsifiers increasingly linked to intestinal, 
cardiovascular, and metabolic diseases, including potential roles in Crohn’s disease 
(Halmos, Mack and Gibson, 2019). 
==PAGE:15==
9 
 
Elements that may influence the bio-functional properties of peptides and their 
commercial viability include maintenance of their structural integrity and their stability 
within food products. BPs may interact with components of the food matrix, including 
lipids, carbohydrates, phenolics, and metals, resulting in the creation of complexes or 
unwanted reaction products. These interactions can diminish the effectiveness of 
peptides and obstruct their bio-accessibility. Furthermore, their antioxidant activity is 
influenced by the presence and arrangement of amino acids within the peptide chain. 
Enzymatic hydrolysis alters properties such as hydrophobicity, primary structure, 
amino acid composition, and spatial conformation (Hajfathalian et al., 2018), but 
properties may also be improved by the use of specific enzymes and by choosing a 
defined set of hydrolysis conditions, such as time, pH, and temperature. Another 
significant drawback is their vulnerability to oxidation. Given their amino acid 
structure, particularly the presence of nucleophilic side chains, peptides are susceptible 
to oxidative degradation during both processing and storage. This oxidation undermines 
their structural integrity and bioactivity.  
To mitigate microbial contamination and degradation, peptides are frequently 
lyophilized into a powdered form; however, in this state, they demonstrate high 
hygroscopicity, which can further hasten oxidative and microbial spoilage, adversely 
affecting their shelf-life and efficacy (Bhandari et al., 2020). 
Encapsulation techniques are regarded as a promising approach to enhance stability, 
safeguard functionalities, and regulate the release of BPs for nutraceutical and food 
applications. Additional benefits of encapsulating peptides within nanocarriers 
encompass the enhancement of sensory attributes by masking their bitter flavor, 
increasing solubility, and reducing the hygroscopic nature of peptides to facilitate their 
preservation during storage (Aguilar-Toalá et al., 2022). Food-derived BPs have been 
effectively encapsulated within polysaccharide and oligosaccharide-based carriers, 
such as cyclodextrins (Sun, Okagu and Udenigwe, 2021). 
 
2.2.2 Astaxanthin 
Astaxanthin (C₄₀H₅₂O₄) is a lipid-soluble, dark reddish keto-carotenoid recognized for 
its remarkably potent antioxidant properties. It is naturally present in various marine 
==PAGE:16==
10 
 
organisms, especially crustaceans like crabs and shrimp, as well as in microalgae. 
Among natural antioxidants, astaxanthin is distinguished by its effectiveness, showing 
antioxidant activity nearly 100 times more potent than ⍺-tocopherol (vitamin E), 200 
times more robust than tea polyphenols, 17 times more effective than grape seed extract, 
and considerably more efficient than numerous other carotenoids. Astaxanthin is 
regarded as a promising natural substitute for synthetic antioxidants due to its high 
effectiveness and safety profile (Dang, Li and Yu, 2024; Debnath et al., 2024).  
Astaxanthin (3,3′-dihydroxy-β,β-carotene-4,4′-dione), a xanthophyll carotenoid, is a 
secondary metabolite structurally characterized by two terminal β-ionone rings linked 
by a polyene chain with conjugated double bonds. Astaxanthin contains oxygen 
molecules as hydroxyl (–OH) and carbonyl (C = O) groups within its β-ionone rings, 
classifying it as a xanthophyll. The presence of hydroxyl (–OH) and carbonyl (C=O) 
groups on its rings allows esterification with fatty acids, resulting in higher solubility 
within the cell and allowing it to bind with fatty acids, forming astaxanthin monoesters 
(E-Asta) and diesters, while also existing in unesterified (free) form (F-Asta) (Figure 
1). Esterified forms are generally more chemically stable, especially those with longer 
fatty acid chains or higher saturation, while bioavailability is higher with shorter and 
more unsaturated fatty acids. Among all derivatives, the diester form with 
docosahexaenoic acid (DHA, C22:6) has demonstrated both the highest bioavailability 
and stability (Martínez-Álvarez, Calvo and Gómez-Estaca, 2020).  
 
==PAGE:17==
11 
 
 
Figure 1. Structure of astaxanthin. A) Free astaxanthin, B) Astaxanthin-mono-ester and C) 
Astaxanthin-di-ester (Brotosudarmo et al., 2020). 
 
Astaxanthin’s physiological role is largely attributed to its potent antioxidant activity, 
which is vital in counteracting reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated during normal 
aerobic metabolism. Excessive ROS can damage proteins, lipids, and DNA, 
contributing to numerous chronic diseases. Astaxanthin effectively quenches singlet 
oxygen, scavenges peroxyl and hydroxyl radicals, inhibits the autoxidation chain 
reaction, and acts as a metal chelator, transforming prooxidant metal ions into non-
reactive forms. These mechanisms underline its broad therapeutic potential. 
(Brotosudarmo et al., 2020). 
Astaxanthin also exists in different isomeric forms, most notably the all-E and Z-
isomers (Figure 2), which vary in both stability and bioavailability. Z-isomers, 
particularly (9Z)-astaxanthin, exhibit higher bioavailability but are less stable than all-
E isomers due to steric hindrance and their tendency to revert to the E-form under heat 
or light. Suspension media like oils and antioxidants can reduce degradation but cannot 
prevent isomerization. Astaxanthin is highly sensitive to environmental factors like pH, 
temperature, and light, which can induce structural changes including isomerization, 
aggregation, and esterification. These modifications may alter its physicochemical 
properties and bio-efficacy. Therefore, preserving its functional integrity during 
processing and storage is crucial for effective utilization in food and pharmaceutical 
applications (Debnath et al., 2024).  
==PAGE:18==
12 
 
 
 
 
Figure 2. Different isomers of astaxanthin (Dang, Li and Yu, 2024). 
 
The primary natural source of astaxanthin is the microalga Haematococcus pluvialis, 
although it also accumulates in algae, plankton, and marine animals that consume these 
microorganisms. Crustaceans do not produce astaxanthin internally; rather, they obtain 
it through their diet, particularly from microalgae, which is responsible for their red 
coloration. Acknowledging its health-enhancing potential, the European Commission’s 
Committee on Nutrition, Novel Food and Food Allergens assessed the safety of 
astaxanthin in dietary supplements. The committee determined that its intake is safe for 
both adults and children within designated dosage limits (Dang, Li and Yu, 2024), with 
an acceptable daily intake (ADI) of 0.2 mg astaxanthin/kg body weight (bw) per day 
according to European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) (Bampidis et al., 2019). 
Astaxanthin displays a broad spectrum of bioactivities, including anti-inflammatory, 
antihypertensive, 
anti-obesity, 
anti-apoptotic, 
antimicrobial, 
gastroprotective, 
neuroprotective, and antitumoral effects. It also aids in stress tolerance, DNA repair, 
cellular regeneration, and skin protection.

13 
 
Driven by growing interest in natural health supplements and functional ingredients, 
the global astaxanthin market is experiencing rapid growth. Market predictions indicate 
a compound annual growth rate of 16.2%, with expectations for the market to reach 
around USD 2.57 billion by 2025 and USD 3.40 billion by 2027. The cost of astaxanthin 
varies significantly depending on its source (synthetic or natural) and quality, ranging 
from USD 2,000 to 7,500 per kilogram, with natural forms typically commanding a 
higher price than synthetic options (Debnath et al., 2024). 
 
2.3 Lipid oxidation  
Lipid oxidation is the most significant quality parameter in food products, and it can 
result in the degradation of nutritional value, flavor, odor, texture, and visual appeal of 
food. This process diminishes the quality of lipid-rich foods, reduces their shelf life and 
nutritive value by limiting the content of essential polyunsaturated fatty acids, and can 
lead to substantial economic losses (Wang et al., 2023). Lipid oxidation products have 
mutagenic, carcinogenic and cytotoxic properties and considered to be risk factor for 
human health. These metabolites cause severe health problems, such as cancer, 
coronary heart disease, atherosclerosis, arthritis, diabetes, and Alzheimer’s disease have 
been associated, at least in part, with oxidation or free radical reactions occurring within 
the body. Therefore, the prevention of lipid oxidation is a critical concern (Sarmadi and 
Ismail, 2010). 
Lipid oxidation is a complex process. Thermal oxidation and air oxidation are the main 
pathways of lipid oxidation. Heat, light, oxygen, enzymes, transition metals, 
metalloproteins and microorganisms can act as initiators or promoters for the process. 
When exposed to elevated temperatures, lipids undergo oxidation, decomposition, 
polymerization, and condensation, leading to the formation of various fatty acids, 
hydroperoxides, aldehydes, as well as dimers and trimers, which ultimately results in a 
deterioration of food quality. Unsaturated fatty acids are particularly prone to oxidation. 
The rate of oxidation is influenced by the degree of unsaturation and escalates with an 
increase in the number of double bonds in fatty acids (Belitz, Grosch and Schieberle, 
2009). The air oxidation of lipids encompasses a variety of reactions, with the most 
prevalent methods being autoxidation, photooxidation, and enzymatic oxidation. These 
==PAGE:20==
14 
 
oxidation processes that involve air significantly influence food quality during both 
storage and processing (Ahmed et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2023).  
 
2.3.1 Autoxidation 
Autoxidation reaction results in lipids breakdown and the generation of a diverse array 
of oxidation products. When lipid substrate (RH) is exposed to heat, light, or metal ions, 
the hydrogen atom from the double bond is removed, leading to the formation of free 
or alkyl radical (R*) (Equation 1). These free radicals then react with oxygen, resulting 
in the formation of peroxy radicals (ROO*) (Equation2) which extract a hydrogen atom 
from another unsaturated fatty acid. This process culminates in the creation of primary 
oxidation products known as hydroperoxides (ROOH) (Equation 3) through the 
mechanisms of initiation, propagation, and termination (Figure 3). The duration of the 
propagation cycle is directly related to the level of lipid unsaturation. These primary

2.3.2 Photooxidation 
Photooxidation results in the formation of both aliphatic and aromatic oxidized 
compounds, including ketones, aldehydes, carboxylic acids, fatty acids, esters, epoxies, 
sulfoxides, sulfones, phenols, anhydrides, quinones, and alcohols. When light is 
present, photosensitizers facilitate the conversion of triplet oxygen (3O2) into singlet 
oxygen (1O2), a highly reactive and non-radical molecule. This process requires less 
energy to initiate the reaction with food compared to triplet oxygen, meaning that 
temperature has a lesser impact on the oxidation driven by singlet oxygen than that 
caused by triplet oxygen. Sensitizer molecules emit fluorescent light, and to generate a 
triplet sensitizer, this triplet sensitizer reacts through two mechanisms. In the first 
mechanism, the sensitizer reacts with the lipid substrate to create a complex mixture of 
free radicals and singlet sensitizer (Equation 9). This mixture then further reacts with 
triplet oxygen, resulting in hydroperoxide (Equation 10), which has a structure similar 
to that formed by autoxidation. 
• 
3Sens + RH → R* + singlet sensitizer (SensH)  
 
(9) 
•

(10) 
In the second mechanism, the triplet sensitizer (3Sens) also interacts with triplet oxygen, 
resulting in the formation of singlet oxygen (Equation 11). This singlet oxygen 
subsequently reacts directly with the double bonds of unsaturated lipids, leading to the 
production of hydroperoxides (Equation 12). These hydroperoxides differ from the 
products of autoxidation, and in this scenario, radical scavengers do not interfere due 
to the absence of free radicals (Frankel, 2012).

2.3.3 Enzyme oxidation 
Enzymatic oxidation pertains to the oxidation reaction that involves enzymes, 
specifically two types of enzymes that play a role in lipid oxidation: lipoxygenase and 
==PAGE:23==
17 
 
hydroperoxidase. Lipoxygenase protein is capable of specifically catalyzing the 
oxidation of the cis-1,4-pentadiene structure found in polyunsaturated fatty acids, 
primarily linoleic acid and linolenic acid. This process results in the formation of 
hydroperoxides that possess conjugated C=Cs and exhibit optical activity through 
intramolecular oxygenation. The enzyme binds with unsaturated fatty acids and reacts 
with oxygen to produce hydroperoxide. It is important to note that lipoxygenase protein 
does not exert any oxidation effect on saturated fatty acids, mono-unsaturated fatty 
acids, or conjugated fatty acids. Lipoxygenases are present in fish tissues, and their 
presence should be avoided in properly refined marine oil (Kiokias et al., 2009; Wang 
et al., 2023).  
 
2.4 Prevention of lipid oxidation 
Ensuring food safety, quality, and shelf life is a major challenge in food production. The 
shelf life of foods is closely related to the oxidation stability of lipids. Consequently, 
enhancing the oxidation stability of lipids in food products is crucial for their 
preservation. Strengthening the tightness and light protection of food packaging, also 
using encapsulation techniques serves as an effective approach to enhance the oxidative 
stability of lipids. Furthermore, antioxidant methods have been effectively implemented 
in the food sector to extend the shelf life of various food items. At the same time, these 
methods have also found application in the pharmaceutical field to mitigate the risk of 
diseases associated with oxidative stress (Domínguez et al., 2019). 
 
2.4.1 Encapsulation 
Encapsulation is a technique to entrap bioactive agents within a protective wall or 
carrier material, serving as an essential process for improving the delivery and 
controlled release of bioactive molecules. Encapsulation, along with light protection 
techniques, has been employed to stabilize certain lipids, including ω-3 fatty acids 
found in pharmaceuticals, food products, and dietary supplements. The encapsulation 
of marine oil can create a protective shield against oxidation, effectively masking the 
unpleasant flavor and odor typically associated with oil (Wang et al., 2023).  
==PAGE:24==
18 
 
The choice of encapsulating agents, as well as the selected encapsulation method, are 
critical factors to consider when assessing the efficacy of the encapsulation process. 
Biodegradable polymers, which encompass both natural and synthetic types, are widely 
employed as microparticle carrier materials across various sectors, including 
pharmaceuticals, biomedicine, cosmetics, and plastics. In contrast, only natural 
polymers are sanctioned for use as carrier matrices within the food industry, 
necessitating that these materials be edible, biodegradable, biocompatible, nontoxic, 
and cost-effective. Furthermore, the carrier materials must maintain structural stability 
within food product matrices throughout processing and storage. Food-grade 
microparticle carrier materials are categorized into three main types: polysaccharides, 
proteins, and lipids. This master's thesis focuses on oligosaccharides as carrier 
materials, with a particular emphasis on cyclodextrins (Sun, Okagu and Udenigwe, 
2021).  
 
2.4.2 Antioxidants  
 Among the various methods available for controlling lipid oxidation in food products, 
the application of antioxidants stands out as the most effective, convenient, and cost-
efficient approach. They are commonly used to prevent oxidation, maintain sensory 
properties, and inhibit microbial growth. Food manufacturers globally utilize 
antioxidants to stabilize lipids in their products, thus preventing any deterioration in 
quality. Additionally, antioxidants find applications in health-related sectors due to their 
ability to protect the body from oxidative damage. In comparison to oxidizable 
substrates, antioxidants are substances of low concentration that significantly delay or 
prevent lipid oxidation. Examples of antioxidants that meet this criterion include radical 
scavengers, singlet oxygen quenchers, peroxides, metal ion chelators, and inhibitors of 
pro-oxidases (Gringer, 2015). Antioxidants can exert their protective effects through 
various mechanisms and activities. Based on their modes of action, they can be broadly 
categorized into primary antioxidants and secondary antioxidants. The former disrupts 
the oxidation chain reaction by scavenging radical intermediates, whereas the latter 
inhibits or delays oxidation by preventing the regeneration of the oxidation initiator, 
promoter, or primary antioxidant (Wang et al., 2023). 
==PAGE:25==
19 
 
Primary antioxidants consist of mono- or poly-hydroxy phenols that feature various 
ring substitutions, which include both synthetic compounds and natural substances. The 
effectiveness of these antioxidants is influenced by multiple factors, including their 
chemical reactivity with radicals, their concentration and mobility within the 
microenvironment, and their interactions with other antioxidants. Secondary 
antioxidants exhibit antioxidant properties when a minor component is also present in 
the sample. Many of these compounds are known as synergists because they enhance 
the effectiveness of primary antioxidants; examples include citric acid, ascorbic acid, 
etc. Additionally, the category of secondary antioxidants encompasses compounds that 
chelate metal ions (such as EDTA), reducing agents (for instance, ascorbic acid), and 
singlet oxygen quenchers (like carotenoids) (Kiokias et al., 2009). 
The primary antioxidant function of antioxidants, including the majority of phenolic 
compounds, involves neutralizing radicals by supplying a hydrogen atom. As illustrated 
in Equations 13 and 14, A-H represents antioxidant molecules that interact with 
radicals. During this process, the antioxidant seizes the radicals generated by oxidation 
and ultimately neutralizes them (Ribeiro et al., 2019).  
• R*/RO*/ROO* + A-H → A* + RH/ROH/ROOH  
 
(13) 
• R*/RO*/ROO* + A* → RA/ROA/ROOA  
 
(14) 
While synthetic antioxidants like tert-butylhydroquinone (TBHQ), butylated 
hydroxyanisole (BHA) and propyl gallate (PG) have been widely used, concerns over 
their long-term health risks, such as gastrointestinal issues and potential 
carcinogenicity, have led to increased demand for safer, natural alternatives. Indeed, the 
food industry has a strong preference for the use of natural antioxidants, some of which 
may exist inherently in foods or be added intentionally during their processing such as 
phenolic compounds (polyphenols, flavonoids), tocopherols, carotenoids (astaxanthin), 
and peptides. The following sections focus on the antioxidant potential of astaxanthin 
and peptides.  
Antioxidant peptides exhibit their activity primarily through metal chelation and 
hydrogen or electron donation. These mechanisms allow them to interact with free 
radicals, either by terminating radical chain reactions or by preventing their formation.  
==PAGE:26==
20 
 
The overall antioxidant capacity of bioactive peptides is highly dependent on their 
amino acid composition and sequence. Studies have shown that the presence of 
hydrophobic amino acids and specific residues such as histidine, proline, methionine, 
cysteine, tyrosine, tryptophan, and phenylalanine can enhance antioxidant activity. In 
particular, tyrosine and phenylalanine, both aromatic amino acids, are effective direct 
radical scavengers indicating chelating properties, likely due to their phenolic groups, 
which act as efficient hydrogen donors. Similarly, histidine, methionine, and cysteine 
are critical for radical scavenging, contributing through electron donation and 
stabilization mechanisms (Rodríguez-Jiménez et al., 2024). 
Astaxanthin is also a highly potent antioxidant, among various hydrophilic and 
lipophilic antioxidants, such as phenols, carotenoids, ascorbic acid, coenzyme Q10, and 
α-lipoic acid, it demonstrates the strongest singlet oxygen quenching activity under 
identical test conditions. Its overall antioxidant capacity is estimated to be about 10 
times greater than that of other carotenoids like zeaxanthin, lutein, and β-carotene 
(Santos-Sanchez et al., 2021). 
 
2.5 Cyclodextrins as encapsulation agents 
Molecular encapsulation is extensively utilized in the food and pharmaceutical sectors 
to enhance the physicochemical characteristics of bioactive compounds. Host–guest 
complexes are molecular aggregates stabilized by noncovalent interactions, such as van 
der Waals forces, hydrogen bonds, and hydrophobic interactions, but never by covalent 
bonds. Host molecules are characterized by having an internal cavity capable of 
incorporating another molecule, the guest (de Lima Paula et al., 2023). 
Among these, cyclodextrins (CDs) are some of the most widely used host molecules 
for forming inclusion complexes with organic compounds. CDs are cyclic 
oligosaccharides characterized by a macrocyclic structure composed of D(+)-
glucopyranose units linked by α-(1→4) glycosidic bonds. The three native types of this 
group of compounds, also called ’parent CDs’, are α-cyclodextrin, which contains six 
glucose units; β-cyclodextrin, comprising seven glucose units; and γ-cyclodextrin, 
consisting of eight glucose units (Loftsson and Brewster, 2012; Esteso and Romero, 
2024).  
==PAGE:27==
21 
 
CDs belong to the family of cage-type molecules and possess a non-symmetrical 
toroidal (truncated cone) structure, characterized by a hydrophilic exterior and a 
hydrophobic inner cavity. The exterior hydrophilicity arises from hydroxyl groups 
located at both the wider and narrower rims of the molecule, contributing to their 
solubility in water. In contrast, the interior cavity is hydrophobic, enabling the 
encapsulation of nonpolar guest molecules in aqueous environments. The formation of 
the truncated cone shape induces a specific orientation of the hydroxyl groups: the C2–
OH and C3–OH groups are directed toward the wider rim, while the C6–OH groups 
extend toward the narrower rim. This arrangement facilitates hydrogen bonding with 
water, further enhancing aqueous solubility. (Figure 4). This encapsulation capability 
allows CDs to form inclusion complexes that can significantly alter the physical, 
chemical, and biological properties of guest compounds. The stability of these 
complexes is primarily driven by van der Waals and hydrophobic interactions, 
depending on the fit and compatibility between the guest molecule and the CD cavity 
(Figure 5) (Poulson et al., 2022). 
 
 
Figure 4. Hydrophobic cavity of the cyclodextrin molecule (Poulson et al., 2022).

the empty CD cavity (orange). Once the guest molecule is inside the CD cavity, either partially or

Due to these properties, CDs are widely applied in fields such as pharmaceuticals, food, 
cosmetics, textiles, biotechnology (e.g., fermentation and bioconversion processes), 
agriculture, and environmental science. They are especially valuable in controlled-
release systems, enhancing the solubility, stability, and bioavailability of poorly water-
soluble substances in the presence of light, heat, and oxidizing agents, and also serve 
as catalysts in various chemical reactions. 
CDs are enzymatically produced using cyclodextrin glucosyltransferase from modified 
starch, a cost-effective and scalable process with an estimated annual production of 
around 150 tons. The final product is non-toxic, not absorbed in the upper 
gastrointestinal tract, and is completely metabolized by the colon microflora, making it 
highly suitable for food and pharmaceutical use. Physically, CD complexes are 
wettable, nearly odorless, and hygroscopic powders. Their mechanical properties, such 
as crystallinity and flow behavior, are influenced by the method of complex formation. 
Furthermore, moisture content and temperature are critical factors affecting the 
stability, deformation, and release behavior of CD–guest complexes (Pereira et al., 
2021). 
Several parameters must be considered when studying the mechanism of action of 
cyclodextrins, with cavity size being one of the most critical. The size of the internal 
cavity determines which type of CD is most suitable for complexing a particular guest 
molecule, as it affects the degree of molecular fit, a key factor in efficient encapsulation. 
The geometric shapes and varying sizes of the CDs create an advantageous setting for 
the incorporation of marine oils and their compounds, as can be seen in Figure 6.

Each CD type, α-, β-, and γ-CDs, has a distinct cavity diameter. α-CDs possess 
relatively small cavities and can only accommodate small molecules, limiting their 
applicability. In contrast, γ-CDs have larger cavities, often too spacious for many typical 
guest molecules, resulting in weaker interactions. Moreover, excessive cavity space can 
reduce the hydrophobic interaction strength, hindering the formation of stable inclusion 
complexes. β-CDs, with an intermediate cavity size, are generally considered the most 
versatile. Their dimensions are well-suited for encapsulating a wide range of 
biologically relevant molecules, such as vitamins, hormones, and other compounds 
commonly used in tissue and cell culture applications (Pereira et al., 2021; Ez-zoubi 
et al., 2024).  
One of the few disadvantages of natural cyclodextrins is their limited water solubility. 
For instance, the solubility of native β-CD is approximately 1.85 g/100 mL of water 
(Chatjigakis et al., 1992). Despite this suboptimal solubility, β-CD remains the most 
widely used native CD in commercial and pharmaceutical applications due to its 
effective inclusion properties and availability. To overcome solubility limitations, there 
is a growing trend to chemically modify native CDs, resulting in derivatives with 
improved aqueous solubility. In this project, the derivative 2-hydroxypropyl-β-

units, like β-CD, but features hydroxypropyl substitutions that greatly enhance its water 
solubility (Figure 7), reaching values greater than 60 g/100 mL (Zoeller, Dressman and 
Klein, 2012). This improved solubility makes HP-β-CD a versatile and effective 
alternative for applications requiring better dissolution and bioavailability of 
hydrophobic compounds.

2.5.1 Applications in the Food Industry 
CDs are primarily utilized in the food industry for encapsulating compounds of interest 
and enhancing water retention due to their hygroscopic nature. Their application can 
provide various technological benefits, including the creation of more uniform 
compositions that are simpler to standardize. A wide range of applications for CDs has 
been documented, such as enhancing organoleptic quality by eliminating or reducing 
undesirable flavors and odors, improving colors, extending the shelf life of food 
products, sequestering components to minimize unwanted substances like flavors, 
trans-fats, and allergens, forming pickering emulsions, inhibiting light-induced 
decomposition, mitigating heat-induced changes, modifying solubility and preventing 
oxidation. CDs can create complexes with various ingredients that are sensitive to 
oxygen or oxidizing agents, which typically results in enhanced stability of the 
encapsulated substrates. Complexation with these types of compounds nearly entirely 
==PAGE:31==
25 
 
inhibits the chemical modifications of these oxidizable substances, even when stored in 
an environment containing 100% oxygen (Pereira et al., 2021). 
This project focuses on natural β-CD and its derivative HP-β-CD. β-CD is generally 
recognized as safe (GRAS) by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and is 
approved as a food additive in Europe and Japan. It is also officially listed in the 
European Pharmacopoeia (Ph.Eur.), the United States Pharmacopeia/National 
Formulary (USP/NF), and the Japanese Pharmacopoeia (JP). However, while several 
CD derivatives are included in the FDA’s Inactive Ingredients Database, the majority 
do not possess GRAS status. HP-β-CD is the only cyclodextrin derivative listed in both 
the Ph.Eur. and USP/NF. Extensive toxicological evaluations in the literature indicate 
that HP-β-CD is well tolerated across multiple species, including humans, particularly 
when administered orally. It demonstrates low toxicity, with effects primarily 
depending on the dose and route of administration. The most commonly reported 
adverse effect in humans is diarrhea, while no evidence of renal toxicity has been 
documented (Zoeller, Dressman and Klein, 2012). 
 
2.5.2 High-Intensity Ultrasound (HIUS) treatment 
 Various methods can be utilized to prepare the inclusion complex, with each method 
influencing its physicochemical and biological properties. Recent advancements in 
technology aim to reduce both time and energy consumption, with High-Intensity 
Ultrasound (HIUS) treatment being a technique applied in this study. Ultrasonication 
involves the transmission of ultrasonic sound waves through a liquid medium, leading 
to the formation of a phenomenon known as acoustic cavitation. This process depends 
heavily on the frequency and intensity of the ultrasound applied (Leong, Martin and 
Ashokkumar, 2017). 
During ultrasonication, pressure cycles within the liquid generate microscopic voids or 
bubbles, which rapidly collapse during high-pressure phases. This implosion produces 
localized hotspots characterized by extreme turbulence, intense shear forces, high 
pressures, and elevated temperatures. These conditions contribute to the effectiveness 
of ultrasonication in enhancing various physical and chemical processes (Rajasekaran 
et al., 2024b).

3. Preliminary optimization of cyclodextrin to 
fish oil ratio 
Prior to finalizing the encapsulation parameters, a series of preliminary tests were 
carried out to identify the most appropriate CD to fish oil (FO) ratio for achieving 
optimal encapsulation efficiency and emulsion stability. These tests involved varying 
ratios (1:1, 1:2, 1:3, and 1:4 w/w), but also different concentrations of CD, including 
levels exceeding 0.6% w/w. Higher CD concentrations did not lead to acceptable 
outcomes; rather, they caused phase separation and incomplete emulsification. Based 
on these results, the concentration was set at 0.6%, and efforts were concentrated on 
determining the most effective CD:FO ratio within this limitation. 
During the initial tests, visual evaluations revealed that emulsions created with higher 
oil content (ratios 1:3 and 1:4) displayed significant phase separation and surface oiling. 
These samples contained a considerable number of unencapsulated oil droplets, 
indicating inadequate encapsulation performance. Conversely, the 1:1 ratio, while 
appearing more stable visually, exhibited lower efficiency because of an excess of wall 
material in relation to the oil, which was neither cost-effective nor optimal for 
emulsification. 
The 1:2 CD:FO ratio consistently showed the best results across all visual, physical, 
and stability evaluations. Emulsions at this ratio appeared more uniform, exhibited 
minimal surface oil, and maintained stability over time. This ratio also proved to be the 
most balanced regarding oil loading and complexation efficiency, providing sufficient 
CD to interact with hydrophobic oil molecules while preserving acceptable dispersion 
and flow characteristics. 
Based on these pretesting findings, the 1:2 ratio was chosen for all subsequent 
formulations involving β-CD and HP-β-CD. This choice was validated by 
measurements of the Turbiscan Stability Index (TSI), ζ potential, and droplet size, 
which confirmed improved emulsion stability and encapsulation performance at this 
ratio. Consequently, the 1:2 CD:FO ratio was established as the optimal standard for 
encapsulation in the experiments conducted throughout the main study.

4. Materials and methods  
4.1 Materials 
Astaxanthin-enriched shrimp oil was obtained from Pandalus borealis shrimp by-
products (heads and shells), originally supplied frozen by Launis A/S (Skagen, 
Denmark). The oil had been extracted using supercritical carbon dioxide extraction for 
the pressed side-streams. The extracted oil was stored at −40 °C until use in the current 
study (Badfar et al., 2025). β-CD and HP-β-CD were purchased from BOC Sciences 
(Shirley, USA). Peptides were produced via the enzymatic hydrolysis of defatted 
shrimp shells. Trypsin (Formea Prime) (Novozymes A/S, Bagsvaerd, Denmark) were 
used for the enzymatic hydrolysis. All the chemicals and solvents used were of 
analytical grade. Deionized water was used for the preparation of all the reagents and 
samples. 
 
4.2 Enzymatic hydrolysis 
Defatted shrimp shell powder was obtained from previous study (Badfar et al., 2025). 
Pressed shrimp shells were freeze-dried and pulverized, followed by astaxanthin 
extraction using supercritical fluid extraction (SFE) process (Jafarpour et al., 2024). In 
the present study, enzymatic hydrolysis was carried out using this defatted, lyophilized 
shrimp shell powder. Trypsin (TRYP) was employed to generate peptides from the 
substrate. The samples were mixed with distilled water at a ratio of 1:10 (w/v) and 
heated to 55 ◦C. The pH was adjusted to 8.5 using 1M HCL, and the hydrolysis reaction 
was conducted at an enzyme/substrate ratio (E/S) of 0.5% in a temperature-controlled 
shaking water bath. Hydrolysis proceeded for 2 hours, with continuous monitoring of 
the pH to ensure it remained within the active and optimal ranges for TRYP, thereby 
facilitating effective enzymatic hydrolysis. Subsequently, the sample was heated to 90 
◦C for 15 minutes to inactivate the enzyme. Then, samples were cooled down to room 
temperature and centrifuged at 4100 ×g for 10 min at 24 ◦ C to separate the soluble 
hydrolysates from the insoluble matter. The supernatant was freeze-dried, ground into 
a fine powder, and stored at -20 ◦C until further use.

4.3 Determination of protein content (Based on Total Nitrogen 
Content) 
The overall nitrogen content was assessed utilizing a Dumas instrument (Rapid MAX 
N exceed cube N/protein analyzer, Elemental Analyzer system GmbH, Frankfurt, 
Germany). A sample weight of 150 mg was used for the measurement. The total 
nitrogen content values (%) were multiplied by 6.25 to calculate the protein content. 
Nitrogen content measurements were conducted in duplicate, utilizing the milk powder 
conversion (Kp) factor.  
 
4.4 Emulsion production 
Oil-in-water (O/W) emulsions containing 1.2 w/w % shrimp oil were prepared in 300 
g batches using enzymatic protein hydrolysates and/or cyclodextrins (β-CD or HP-β-
CD) as emulsifying agents. Three different formulations were investigated: emulsions 
stabilized solely with peptides (PO), solely with cyclodextrins (CD_O, HP_O), and 
with a combination of both emulsifiers (CD_OP, HP_OP). Each sample was analyzed 
in duplicate.  
The aqueous phase for each formulation was prepared by dissolving cyclodextrins 
and/or peptides in distilled water. The hydrolysate powder was added in a final 
concentration of 0.2 w/w % in the emulsion, while cyclodextrins were added to achieve 
a concentration of 0.6 w/w %. The solutions were stirred at ambient temperature using 
magnetic stirrers at 500 rpm for 1 hour to ensure complete hydration of the 
cyclodextrins. When peptides were included in the formulation, added after the 
cyclodextrins had fully dissolved, the mixture was stirred for an additional hour at a 
reduced speed of 300 rpm to prevent foam formation associated with peptides 
incorporation. 
Pre-emulsification was carried out using a rotor-stator homogenizer (Ultra-Turrax, 
IKA, Germany) operated at 16,000 rpm for 3 minutes at 24 °C to improve emulsion 
uniformity. Final encapsulation was performed using high-intensity ultrasound (HIUS) 
treatment (Emerson, St. Louis, MO, USA), applying a frequency of 20 kHz in pulse 
mode (5 seconds ON, 3 seconds OFF) for 5 minutes at 90% amplitude. A cooling system 
maintained the temperature at 10 °C throughout the sonication process. The emulsions

were contained in amber glass bottles, after being flushed with nitrogen, with a distinct 
bottle allocated for each measurement day (day 0, 3, and 7). The samples were 
maintained in a dark environment at room temperature (24 °C) until the measurement 
day, and an additional set was prepared for visual inspection. Samples that were not 
analyzed on the scheduled days were preserved at −40 °C in a freezer until further 
analysis. 
 
4.5 Characterization of emulsions  
4.5.1 Physical Stability 
4.5.1.1 Visual appearance 
Aliquots of the emulsions were transferred into a 10 mL cylinder, and the stability of 
the emulsions was visually assessed for destabilization phenomena, including 
creaming, sedimentation, flocculation, and coalescence. Also, color changes were 
monitored over the storage period. The samples were evaluated on days 0, 1, 2, 3, 7 and 
30. 
 
4.5.1.2 Turbiscan analysis 
Following production, 20 mL aliquots of emulsions were placed in a Turbiscan tower 
(Formulaction, Toulouse, France) and analyzed over a period of 24 hours. The 
Turbiscan Stability Index (TSI) and variations in the backscattering data were 
monitored as indicators of physical stability. Samples with TSI values exceeding 3 were 
deemed unstable, while those with TSI values remaining below 1 were classified as 
more stable. Measurements were conducted on days 0,3, and 7. 
 
4.5.1.3 Droplet size 
The distribution of droplet sizes in the emulsions was evaluated using Mastersizer 3000 
(Malvern Instruments, Ltd., Worcestershire, UK), employing the laser diffraction 
method on days 0, 3, and 7 following the commencement of the storage experiment. 
The emulsions were diluted in recirculating water at a rate of 2500 rpm until an 
obscuration level of approximately 10% was reached. The refractive indices of cod liver

oil (1.481) and water (1.330) were utilized as particle and dispersant, respectively. The 
droplet size measurements were expressed as the surface-weighted mean diameter (d3,2) 
and the volume-weighted mean diameter (d4,3). The samples were measured in 
duplicates. 
 
4.5.1.4 ζ-potential 
The surface charge of the emulsion droplets was evaluated by measuring the ζ-potential 
of the emulsions on days 0, 3, and 7 using a Zetasizer Nano ZS (Malvern Instruments

solution, consisting of 10 μL of emulsion mixed with 1 mL of distilled water. The ζ-
potential was set to -100/+50 mV, and each sample underwent 100 analytical runs, two 
times. The samples were measured in duplicates. 
 
4.5.2 Oxidative stability  
The oxidative stability of the emulsions was assessed by measuring the peroxide value

samples taken on days 0, 3, and 7 of storage. On the designated sampling days, aliquots 
of the emulsions that were not analyzed were stored at −40 °C until analysis.  
 
4.5.2.1 Lipid extraction 
Lipid soluble compounds were isolated from the emulsion utilizing the method 
described by Bligh and Dyer, 1959 with modifications. Initially, 15 grams of the 
emulsion were combined with 30 mL of methanol, and 15 mL of chloroform, mixed at 
15000 rpm for 30 seconds using a T25 digital Ultra-Turrax (IKA-Werke GmbH & Co. 
KG, Staufen, Germany). Subsequently, an additional 15 mL of chloroform was 
incorporated, and the mixture was agitated again at 15000 rpm for another 30 seconds. 
Finally, 15 mL of deionized water was added, and the mixture was stirred at the same 
speed for 30 seconds. The samples were then centrifuged at 2800 rpm and 18 °C for 10 
minutes to separate the chloroform phase containing the lipids from the 
aqueous/methanol phase, which was discarded. The oil content of the extract was 
assessed by weighing approximately 8-9 grams of the extract in a pre-weighed dry

5. Results and discussion  
5.1 Protein content 
Τhe hydrolysates presented a protein content above 93%, with an average 94 ± 0.33%, 
demonstrating a highly efficient hydrolysis process. This value is substantially higher 
than that reported by Ambigaipalan and Shahidi, 2017b, who observed protein contents 
of approximately 42% under similar enzymatic conditions. The elevated protein 
concentration in the present study suggests that the specific substrate and hydrolysis 
conditions employed may have been more favorable for protein recovery and 
purification.  
Given the high nitrogen content, the peptides in this study are expected to exhibit strong 
emulsifying potential. Proteins, by nature, are amphiphilic and function as effective 
surface-active agents due to their ability to lower interfacial tension between 
hydrophobic and hydrophilic phases. The enhanced purity of the peptides obtained here 
may further improve their emulsifying efficiency, making them particularly suitable for 
food formulations requiring stable oil-in-water emulsions. In such systems, proteins 
play a critical role not only in forming emulsions but also in stabilizing them by 
adsorbing at the oil–water interface. They create a cohesive and viscoelastic film around 
fat droplets, serving as a physical barrier that inhibits coalescence and enhances long-
term stability (Zayas, 1997). 
 
5.2 Stability of shrimp oil emulsions during storage 
5.2.1 Physical stability of emulsions 
5.2.1.1 Visual characteristics of the samples 
The physical stability of the emulsions was evaluated through visual inspection at 
defined time intervals (days 0, 1, 2, 3, 7, and 30). Samples of visual inspection can be 
seen in Figure 8 and Figure 9. Destabilization phenomena including creaming, 
sedimentation, flocculation, coalescence, and color changes were monitored over the 
storage period. Two experimental sets, flushed with nitrogen and stored at room 
temperature in the absence of light, were assessed to determine the time-dependent 
stability of the emulsions. 
==PAGE:43==
37 
 
Sedimentation was particularly evident in emulsions stabilized with native β-CD, 
specifically the CD_O and CD_OP samples, where precipitation began to appear after 
just one day of storage. In contrast, emulsions stabilized with peptides (PO) and HP-β-
CD with or without peptides exhibited no visible sedimentation, indicating improved 
colloidal stability under the same conditions. 
Creaming stability was assessed by tracking the height and surface appearance of the 
emulsion layer over time. The CD_O samples demonstrated visible creaming after one 
week of static storage, indicating a lower ability to maintain droplet dispersion. 
Moreover, the upper creamed layer showed early signs of discoloration, likely due to 
oxidation of unencapsulated oil, which gradually turned white. Conversely, emulsions 
stabilized with HP-β-CD remained highly stable, showing no signs of destabilization 
even after one week. Notably, after 30 days the serum layer of one HP_OP replicate 
began to exhibit visible creaming, indicating the onset of slight physical instability over 
extended storage. 
Color change, as an indicator of lipid oxidation, was more pronounced in emulsions 
lacking peptides. After one month, CD_O exhibited the most significant discoloration, 
followed by PO and HP_O (i.e., CD_O > PO > HP_O), suggesting that emulsions 
containing peptides were better protected against oxidative degradation. Notably, after 
30 days, the CD_O samples exhibited a complete color shift from a vibrant orange to a 
pale white, indicating extensive pigment degradation. The HP_O samples faded to a 
lighter shade of orange, while the PO samples gradually developed a whitish 
appearance over time, suggesting progressive oxidative deterioration. Samples 
containing both peptides and CDs (HP_OP, CD_OP) maintained consistent coloration 
throughout the storage period, reflecting enhanced oxidative stability.

These findings underscore the significance of cyclodextrin structure in emulsion 
performance. HP-β-CD, due to its chemical modification, forms smaller and less-
defined microcrystals—or amorphous aggregates—compared to the crystalline 
structure of native β-CD (Mathapa and Paunov, 2013). The hydroxypropyl substitution 
(additional –OH groups) disrupts hydrogen bonding and crystalline packing, enhancing 
solubility and reducing sedimentation. As demonstrated, HP_OP stabilized emulsions 
were the most stable across all measured parameters, highlighting the synergistic effect 
of peptides and HP-β-CD in improving emulsion integrity and oxidative resistance 
during storage (Zhang et al., 2025). The contribution of peptides is closely linked to 
their high protein content and surface activity. The surface activity of proteins is 
expressed as their ability to migrate, adsorb, unfold, and form a layer at the oil–water 
interface as a result of molecular rearrangement. Proteins form cohesive membranes or 
films around fat droplets, effectively lowering the interfacial tension between water and 
oil phases. This interfacial film formation, driven by the diffusion and adsorption of 
protein molecules at the interface, serves as a physical barrier that retards the 
coalescence of fat droplets and contributes to enhanced emulsion stability. The 
amphiphilic nature of these peptides, combined with their ability to form protective 
==PAGE:46==
40 
 
films, further explains the superior performance of emulsions containing both HP-β-
CD and peptides in terms of physical and oxidative stability (Zayas, 1997). Besides 
their emulsifying characteristics, peptides also exhibit inherent antioxidant properties, 
which are vital for sustaining the color and oxidative stability of emulsions. Their 
inclusion aided in postponing pigment deterioration, thereby maintaining the visual 
appeal of the samples over time. 
Moreover, the results highlight that, when peptides were integrated into emulsions 
alongside CDs (CD_OP, HP_OP), they were effectively encapsulated, which improved 
their defense against oxidative stress. Conversely, samples that contained only peptides 
(PO) showed early indications of discoloration after prolonged storage, likely due to 
the lack of this encapsulation process and the heightened exposure of bioactive 
compounds to oxidative environments. 
 
5.2.1.2 Turbiscan analysis 
Figure 11 depicts the progression of the Turbiscan Stability Index (TSI) for the 
emulsion formulations over a 7-day storage duration. Samples were initially loaded into 
the Turbiscan on day 0 and subsequently reloaded to continue the analysis on days 3 
and 7 to monitor changes in physical stability. The TSI values indicate the degree of 
destabilization occurring within each sample, with elevated TSI values signifying 
greater physical instability due to processes such as creaming, sedimentation, or 
coalescence. According to the equipment manufacturer, systems with a TSI below 3 are 
deemed visually stable and in the initial phase of destabilization.  
The CD_O emulsion displayed the highest TSI values throughout the storage duration, 
reaching approximately 29 by day 7. This sharp increase implies considerable 
destabilization, likely resulting from sedimentation and creaming, which aligns with 
visual assessments in Section 5.2.1.1. The crystalline structure of native β-CD may have 
facilitated aggregation and phase separation, thereby limiting its emulsifying efficiency. 
In contrast, the CD_OP sample exhibited enhanced stability relative to CD_O, 
characterized by a more gradual rise in TSI and a final value around 16. The 
incorporation of peptides played a role in improving interfacial stabilization by 
decreasing interfacial tension and enhancing droplet dispersion. Nevertheless, the 
intrinsic crystallinity of native β-CD may still have constrained long-term stability.

Emulsions containing HP-β-CD demonstrated superior physical stability. The HP_O 
emulsion, with a final TSI of approximately 3.8, exhibited a significantly stable 
increase, indicating diminished destabilization maintaining the lowest TSI value. This 
can be attributed to the enhanced solubility and reduced crystallinity of HP-β-CD, 
which facilitates better dispersion and minimizes sedimentation (Zoeller, Dressman and

5.2.1.3 ζ-potential and droplet size  
The ζ-potential and droplet size, measured after 0, 3, and 7 days of storage, are 
presented in Figure 12 and Figure 13, respectively. Figure 12 shows the ζ-potential of 
oil-in-water emulsions as a function of time, with significant differences among the 
==PAGE:48==

different samples at the same storage time (p < 0.05) (Appendix B, Table 2). Throughout 
storage, the ζ-potential values of all emulsions remained below –30 mV. ζ-potential 
measurements exceeding ±30 mV are generally indicative of enhanced colloidal 
stability and a reduced tendency for particle aggregation. Such values are considered 
sufficient to provide electrostatic repulsion, thereby preventing droplet aggregation and 
coalescence. It is well established that a higher net surface charge slows down 
aggregation, while a lower net charge increases the likelihood of droplet aggregation 
(Marshall et al., 2011). Among the formulations, HP_O emulsions initially exhibited 
the lowest ζ-potential value (approximately –31 mV on day 0), but this value became 
progressively more negative, reaching around –70 mV by day 7. HP-β-CD formulations 
showed the most pronounced changes in ζ-potential during storage. The HP_OP 
samples followed a similar trend, with ζ-potential values shifting from about –33 mV 
at day 0 to nearly –52 mV at day 7. These findings are consistent with the TSI values 
and visual observations from previous sections, which help explain why the HP_OP 
samples exhibited less negative ζ-potential values compared to HP_O. This difference 
may be attributed to the earlier onset of creaming in the surface of HP_OP emulsions. 
These increasingly negative ζ-potential values in HP-β-CD systems suggest stronger 
electrostatic repulsion, which correlates with their enhanced physical stability observed 
in the Turbiscan measurements.  
Peptide-stabilized emulsions (PO) exhibited similar changes with HP_OP samples, with 
values increasing from around –33 mV at day 0 to approximately –53 mV at day 7. This 
indicates that peptides contribute to interfacial charge and stability, but the dynamic 
changes over time are less pronounced compared to systems containing HP- β -CD. The 
CD_O formulation displayed a substantial increase in negative ζ-potential from around 
–39 mV at day 0 to approximately –60 mV at day 3, followed by a slight decrease to 
about –58 mV by day 7. A similar pattern was observed for CD_OP, which showed a 
steady increase up to day 3, then a slight reduction by day 7. This behavior may reflect 
the desorption or phase separation of stabilizers. It is possible that β-CD or peptides 
initially adsorbed at the oil–water interface subsequently desorbed or became 
incorporated into sedimenting or creaming phases, thereby reducing the surface charge 
on the remaining dispersed droplets. This interpretation is consistent with the findings 
discussed in Section 5.2.1.1. Overall, the ζ-potential data demonstrate that emulsions 
stabilized with HP-β-CD develop stronger electrostatic stabilization over time,

correlating with their superior physical stability as evidenced by Turbiscan 
measurements. The increasing magnitude of negative ζ-potential in these formulations 
supports the notion of dynamic interfacial rearrangements that enhance droplet 
repulsion and help prevent coalescence and phase separation.

5.2.2.1 Primary oxidation products – peroxide value 
The formation of primary oxidation products was evaluated by measuring lipid

suggests that oxidation is already initiated during the emulsification process, likely due 
to high shear forces and the incorporation of air into the system (Takeungwongtrakul

comparatively absent metal-chelating and radical-scavenging abilities of CDs alone 
could explain the higher PV levels observed in those systems, contrary to emulsifying 
and antioxidant properties of peptides. Moreover, as mentioned in Section 5.2.1.3, 
emulsions stabilized with peptides exhibited better droplet size dispersion over the 
storage period, along with improved visual appearance as fas as the discoloration.

astaxanthin at the system. The synergistic effect of HP-β-CD and peptides could result

oxidation and promoting the slow release of astaxanthin (Ahmad and Nollet, 2021). The 
rapid loss of astaxanthin in most emulsions may be attributed to its susceptibility to 
oxidation, particularly in systems where interfacial protection is insufficient, emulsions

5.2.2.3 Volatile secondary oxidation products 
Secondary oxidation products were analyzed for the emulsions in order to evaluate the 
oxidative stability. In the presence of transition metal ions (such as Fe+2), unstable lipid 
hydroperoxides can decompose, leading to the generation of peroxyl and alkoxyl 
radicals (Frankel, 1980). A total of 9 volatiles were selected based on their formation 
during storage, origin and their abundance. These volatiles, including aldehydes, 
ketones, and alcohols are key indicators of sensory deterioration in emulsified systems.  
The volatiles measured are shown in Figure 17. The evolution of compounds can be 
seen in Appendix E with the significant difference (p < 0.05) between different samples 
in the same storage time.  
As the shrimp oil continues oxidize, the secondary oxidation products increase in 
concentration. This can produce off flavors and smells. Hexanal is presented in this 
study and it correspond to oxidation from omega-6 and omega-9 fatty acids, as 
==PAGE:58==
52 
 
described by Garcia-Moreno (García-Moreno et al., 2020). While both (E,E)-2,4-
heptadienal and 1-penten- 3-ol are derived from omega-3 fatty acids (Ballon et al., 
2025). Among the volatile products analyzed, 2,4-heptadienal, 1-penten-3-ol, and 
hexanal were identified in the highest concentrations, underscoring their importance as 
primary oxidation markers. 2,4-heptadienal, an aldehyde, exhibited a sharp increase by 
day 3 in the PO emulsion, followed by a decrease, indicating rapid formation and 
subsequent degradation. In contrast, the HP_OP and CD_OP emulsions demonstrated 
slower increases, indicative of more regulated oxidation. The levels of 1-penten-3-ol 
increased consistently across all emulsions, reaching their peak in the CD_OP and 
HP_OP samples, suggesting that even systems exhibiting low primary oxidation (lower 
PVs) can still accumulate certain volatiles over time. This accumulation may be 
attributed to ongoing, low-level hydroperoxide decomposition or inadequate 
scavenging of radicals after their formation. Hexanal showed a significant rise in HP_O 
emulsions but was notably suppressed in PO. This observation aligns with the 
antioxidant characteristics of peptides, which chelate metal ions and scavenge radicals, 
thereby inhibiting hexanal accumulation (Hennebelle et al., 2024).  
A similar pattern to hexanal was noted for 2-pentenal, pentanal, and 1-penten-3-one, all 
of them were found in lower concentrations in PO mixtures and higher in the other 
emulsions. 1-pentanol was more prominent in all emulsions. Notably, 4-methyl-1-
pentanol and heptanal displayed less consistent trends and smaller overall variations, 
indicating that they are less sensitive indicators of oxidation within this matrix. 
The volatile analysis indicates that peptides (PO) are more effective in reducing 
aldehyde formation, through mechanisms such as radical scavenging and metal-binding 
actions (see figures with pentanal, hexanal). CDs offer physical stabilization; however, 
they do not provide adequate antioxidant protection, leading to increased levels of 
volatiles. Emulsions that incorporate both agents exhibited synergistic antioxidant 
effects and more stable oxidation profiles over time for aldehyde formation.

5.4  Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy 
FTIR spectroscopy was used to analyze the raw materials (β-CD, HP-β-CD, and 
Peptides) (Figure 18A) as well as the emulsions  (Figure 18B) to evaluate the presence 
of functional groups and potential interactions between components. The FTIR spectra 
of β-CD  exhibited characteristic broad absorption bands near 3400 cm⁻¹, with peak at 
3320 cm⁻¹, corresponding to O–H stretching vibrations. An intense peak around 2900 
cm⁻¹ was observed in spectra, attributed to asymmetric and symmetric C–H stretching. 
A notable peak near 1650 cm⁻¹ was assigned to H–O–H deformation of water associated 
with β-CD. This peak reflects the presence of water molecules either trapped within the 
cyclodextrin structure or bound to the exterior hydroxyl groups through hydrogen 
==PAGE:62==
56 
 
bonding. The region between 1150 and 1020 cm⁻¹ displayed C–O–C stretching bands 
typical of the glycosidic linkages of the cyclodextrin structure. Also, peaks at 1153 cm⁻¹ 
and 1029 cm⁻¹ corresponded to C–H overtone and C–H/C–O stretching, respectively 
(Rachmawati, Edityaningrum and Mauludin, 2013). The FTIR spectrum of pure HP-β-
CD illustrated intense broad absorption bands at 3400 cm−1, with peak at 3345 cm⁻¹, 
which corresponded to the free stretching vibrations (O-H). The C-H3 and C-H 
vibrations were at 2920 cm−1. A peak near to 1650 cm⁻¹ with lower intensity of β-CD 
exhibited the H–O–H deformation of water. The large band in the region 1150 with1010 
cm−1 was ascribed to the C－O stretching vibration. The IR band between 1150 and 850 
cm⁻¹ was attributed to carbohydrate-related vibrations for both CDs (Kim, 2020). The 
peptide spectrum showed prominent bands at 3280 cm⁻¹ (N–H stretching, amide A), 
1640 cm⁻¹ (C=O stretching, amide I), and 1520 cm⁻¹ (N–H bending and C–N stretching, 
amide II), confirming the proteinaceous nature of the material. 
In all emulsions (Figure 18B) except for PO, a broad band around 3500~3000 cm⁻¹ was 
visible, attributed to intermolecular H-bonded NH and OH-stretching characteristic of 
hydrophilic materials, oligosaccharides, and residual water. In particular, the PO sample 
showed a flattened profile between 3500~3000 cm⁻¹, possibly due to the lower content 
of free –OH groups in formulations (Ballon et al., 2025). All emulsions exhibited bands 
around 2920 cm⁻¹, assigned to –CH₂ vibrations from lipid components. Characteristic 
protein bands were observed in CD_OP, HP_OP and PO samples at 1700~1600 cm⁻¹ 
(amide I; C=O stretching) and 1600~1500 cm⁻¹ (amide II; N–H bending and C–N 
stretching) and at 1300~1200 cm⁻¹ (amide III) bands. The IR band between 1150 and 
850 cm⁻¹ appeared prominently in CD-containing emulsions, confirming the 
carbohydrate contribution, while this region was less evident in the peptide-stabilized 
PO sample.  
Notable shifts and intensity differences were seen between the raw materials and the 
emulsions, suggesting interactions between the components. Additionally, the amide 
bands, which are sensitive indicators of protein secondary structure, may reflect 
conformational changes at the interface (Yang et al., 2015). 
FTIR spectroscopy revealed that the amide bands were among the most significant 
features in the emulsions, providing critical information about the role of peptides and 
their interactions at the oil–water interface. In the peptide-stabilized emulsion (PO), the 
==PAGE:63==
57 
 
amide bands, shown in Figure 18A, were diminished following their interaction with 
oil, indicating the formation of interactions between components. CD stabilized 
emulsions also demonstrated a reduction in the peaks associated with O–H stretching 
vibrations, suggesting that CDs are interacting with either oil or peptides. When 
comparing the PO sample in Figure 18B  to the others, distinct and pronounced bands 
of amide I and II were observed, with amide I located near 1700 cm⁻¹ and amide II 
around 1550 cm⁻¹, signifying that peptides were the primary constituents at the 
interface. Conversely, emulsions that contained CDs (CD_OP and HP_OP) still 
displayed the amide I and II bands, although with diminished intensity and slight shifts 
in peak positions, with amide I at 1700 and 1661 cm⁻¹, and amide II at 1553 and 1540 
cm⁻¹, respectively. These spectral changes suggest that peptides interacted with the CDs 
or the oil phase, leading to rearrangements at the interface and reduced availability of 
free peptide bonds for vibration. The presence of CDs likely facilitated the replacement 
of water at the interface, as peptides or CDs established new hydrogen bonding 
networks with the oil. In HP_OP stabilized emulsions, the interactions appears stronger, 
as evidenced by more noticeable shifts with higher intensity. In emulsions without 
added peptides, HP_O and CD_O, amide bands were absent, as expected, confirming 
that the interface was predominantly stabilized by CDs. Overall, the FTIR analysis 
confirms that peptides play a direct role in interfacial stabilization, interacting with both 
oil and CDs, and that shifts in these bands are a clear marker of these interactions and 
structural rearrangements at the droplet surface. All these data indicated the successful 
formation of CDs-Oil-Peptides inclusion complexes.

6. Conclusion and perspectives 
This study examined the encapsulation of shrimp oil enriched with astaxanthin through 
the use of cyclodextrins (β-CD and HP-β-CD) and enzymatically derived tryptic 
peptides, with a particular emphasis on how these substances affect the physical and 
oxidative stability of low-fat oil-in-water emulsions. The results indicate that both 
cyclodextrins and peptides play a crucial role in enhancing the stability and 
functionality of the emulsions, while also improving encapsulation efficiency and 
preserving the oil’s bioactive components from oxidative degradation. 
A significant finding of this research is the validation of the dual functional role of 
peptides, which served as both natural emulsifiers and antioxidants. Their amphiphilic 
nature facilitated effective stabilization of emulsion droplets, while their natural 
antioxidant properties helped mitigate lipid oxidation. Additionally, the interaction 
between cyclodextrins and peptides created a stable encapsulation environment, 
underscoring the advantages of integrating hydrophilic carriers with surface-active 
biomolecules sourced from food waste. 
The modified cyclodextrin HP-β-CD, owing to its enhanced solubility and lower 
crystallinity in comparison to native β-CD, improved the emulsifying system by 
fostering uniform dispersion and preventing sedimentation, which resulted in minimal 
physical destabilization as demonstrated by low TSI values and favorable ζ-potential 
measurements. Its compatibility with both shrimp oil and peptides rendered it the most 
effective encapsulation medium evaluated. 
The oxidative stability of astaxanthin in shrimp oil was significantly enhanced in 
systems that included both agents. Primary oxidation was measured using peroxide 
value assessments, while secondary oxidation was analyzed by monitoring the 
development of volatile compounds. Additionally, the characterization of Free 
astaxanthin content was conducted. In all evaluations, the HP_OP formulation 
consistently surpassed others, effectively reducing the oxidation process over a 7-day 
duration and maintaining the astaxanthin within the complex. These findings validate 
that the inclusion of antioxidant peptides, along with the encapsulating and protective 
properties of cyclodextrins, provides robust protection against lipid degradation.  
==PAGE:66==
60 
 
In addition, encapsulation efficiency was measured gravimetrically, revealing that it 
was among the highest in emulsions that contained both cyclodextrins and peptides. 
The surface activity of the peptides and the inclusion capacity of cyclodextrins worked 
synergistically to enhance oil retention within the emulsion matrix. Furthermore, FTIR 
spectroscopy demonstrated distinct structural interactions among the components, 
reinforcing the conclusion that complex formation took place, thereby contributing to 
the enhanced stability and protection of astaxanthin. 
In summary, this study comprehensively met its objectives: it assessed emulsion 
stability in systems with and without peptides, examined the oxidative degradation 
pathways of encapsulated shrimp oil, and effectively characterized the emulsions in 
both structural and functional terms. The combined application of HP-β-CD and tryptic 
peptides presents a promising encapsulation system for marine oils that are rich in 
sensitive bioactive compounds. 
From a research perspective, this project opens several avenues for further exploration. 
Future work may focus on examining the structural characteristics and amino acid 
sequences of the peptides that are primarily responsible for the observed emulsifying 
and antioxidant properties. Analyzing the release kinetics of astaxanthin from these 
systems, along with evaluating long-term stability under various storage conditions, 
will further substantiate the practical applicability of this encapsulation technique. 
Additionally, comprehending how the physical and chemical interactions between 
peptides and cyclodextrins affect the behavior of emulsions at the molecular level can 
guide the development of next-generation bioactive delivery systems. It is also 
important to investigate the encapsulation behavior with varying concentrations of 
peptides and to explore the limits of oil loading and release dynamics. Analytical 
methods such as differential scanning calorimetry (DSC), Raman spectroscopy, and 
scanning electron microscopy (SEM) could provide essential data regarding structural 
integrity and interactions within the emulsified systems. 
Moreover, selected samples from this research have already been submitted for 
cytotoxicity and antimicrobial testing to assess their potential anticancer properties. 
Considering the established bioactivity of astaxanthin and marine peptides, this further 
investigation will yield significant insights into their wider nutraceutical potential. 
==PAGE:67==

This research contributes to the progress of sustainable food systems by transforming 
shrimp by-products into high-value functional ingredients, while also paving the way 
for their application in health-related fields, including disease prevention and 
therapeutic support.
