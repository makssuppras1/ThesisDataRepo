Abstract
Accuracy in modelling of global mean sea level rates is important. Studies has shown that the global mean
sea level is rising. However the trends varies with a little less than a centimetre. The fault is pinpointed to
be either in the dierence in data processing or in the corrections applied. Most of these studies are based
on the NASA's series of satellite altimetry missions combined with tide gauges. In this report ESA's series
of satellite mission for measuring oceanography parameter is compared against NASA's altimetry missions.
Some studies have touched on this subject before but results are highly debated. Corrections are compared
between the two series of missions. This is to determine if the correction can explain the dierence in global
mean sea level obtain. An estimate of the global mean sea level for the 13 year period June 1995 - June
2008 is accomplished. The results showed that most of the dierence in the global mean sea level rates from
NASA missions and ESA missions can be explained by the applied correction. Only in the years 2002 - 2003
the dierence remain unsolved. This studie showed a global mean sea level rise of 2.7mm year in the period
June 1995 - June 2008 without GIA applied.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Change in Earth's climate is an global environmental concern with both direct consequences for all living
organisms as well as political and economical consequences for humans.
Climate changes is a complex
interaction between many geophysical parameters and there dicult to model.
A key parameter for understanding climate changes is global mean sea level [GMSL], there are various
reason for this. Firstly the mean sea level can be directly measured with equipment as tide gauges, buoys and
satellites. Secondly global mean sea level is an important indication of global warming (Ablain et al. 2009).
The water volumes expand, called thermal expansion, as a consequence of rising atmospheric temperatures.
Global mean sea level rise also has a direct impact on human lives as 40 procent of the worlds population
lives within 100 kilometres of the coast.
Sea level has been measured since the beginning of the 20th century by tide gauges. Tide gauges are
placed along coastlines aorund the world and therefore have z poor global spatial distribution. Most of them
are placed along the coasts of North America and Europe with a few on other coast lines. Hence Tide gauges
give bias results for global mean sea level rise.
With the introduction of satellite altimetry in the early 1990 global measurement of sea level became
possible with high evenly spatial resolution.
Most well known on the subject is the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change [IPCC]. The Fifth
assessment report concludes that is very likely that the global mean sea level on average has increased in the
period 1901 to 2010 with the rates shown in table 1.1. It is also virtually certain that sea level will continue
to increase the next 100 years if CO2 emission continues at present day rates (IPCC 2014).

Chapter 2
Background
The dierence between the observed sea height from satellite and the true sea height can be up to many
meters. Geophysical and instrumental errors disturbs the signal. This section will explain how the obtain
value from the satellite can be translated into an actually mean sea level value accurate enough to see global
mean sea level rates of few millimetre per years. Afterwards an overview of the dierent satellite missions
which provided data for this report is given. Third and last the corrections to remove both geophysical and
instrumental errors is explained.
2.1
Altimetry Theory
An altimeter measures the absolute distance between the satellite platform and the sea surface. A microwave
radar sends out a pulse from the satellite directed at Earth surface. This happens with a few seconds interval.
The pulse interacts with an area of Earth's surface and scatters. This area is called the satellites footprint.
Unprocessed the area of a altimetry footprint is around 15 km2. With new and more advanced equipment
and pulse processing this area can be reduced. When the pulse scatters a part of it will be reected back
to the satellite. This backscatter is measured by a receiver. As the pulse travel with the speed of light, the
distance to the surface can be calculated from the time it takes the pulse to travel forth and back by the
simple equation:
hobs = c
2t
(2.1)
Here d is the distance from the satellite to the surface. When determining the sea level a reference point
is needed.
The Earth can be mathematically approximated as an ellipsoid, called the reference ellipsoid.
The
distance from the satellite to the reference ellipsoid is determined with a on board GPS. A large number of
dierent parameters inuence the pulse and the equipment, causing errors in the measured distance. These
errors are corrected in dierent ways as described in section 2.3. The sea level is also aected by wind and
currents, which cause temporary disturbance. The Mean sea surface level is calculated by the following
formula.
hmean sea level = Hgps −hobs −hdynamic −herrors
(2.2)
A satellite altimeter is nadir looking. Meaning the pulse is hitting the surface at an angle of 90◦. This
way the strength of the backscatter is maximised if the sea in calm. Waves would cause more of the pulse
to scatter in other direction.

2.2
Satellites
Radar altimetry from space has been practised since early in 1970. The rst satellites mission was not precise
enough to estimate global mean sea level rates. In the early 1990 with the launch of Topex/Poseidon did
altimetry instruments become precise enough to see small scale variations of sea surface height.
Two dierent series of satellite missions has since then provided continuous observation of global sea level
since the beginning of the 1990.
Topex/Poseidon, Jason-1 and Jason-2 are joint satellite missions in co-operation of the National Aeronau-
tics and Space Administration [NASA] and Centre National d'études Spatiales [CNES]. which are respectively
the American and French space institutes. These satellites mission has provided continuous data since 1992
and is active. The primary function of these satellite missions is to measured sea surface height from space.
The data has been widely used in research (Cazenave & Nerem 2004, Ablain et al. 2009, Beckley et al. 2007)
and more.
Ers-1, Ers-2 and Envisat are satellite mission operated by ESA and missions provides data continuous
from 1991 until april 2012. Theese satellite missions were launch to measure multiple geophysical parameters
including sea surface height. Therefore they don't carry as specied instruments for altimetry measurement
as Topex/Poseidon, Jason-1 and Jason-2. How usefull ERS-1, ERS-2 and Envisat are at improving accuracy
of global mean sea level rates are debable (A. Ollivier 2012, Ablain et al. 2009).
Topex/Poseidon, Jason-1 and Jason-2 shared the same initial orbit in dierent time periods. ERS-1,
ERS-2 and Envisat also shared a lower altitude orbit. The initial orbit is listed in table 2.2. After the initial
missions the satellites were moved to other orbits to make way for their successors. Only the data from
initial orbit are processed in this study.
Data from four missions has been selected; Topex/Poseidon, Jason-1, ERS-2 and Envisat. The reason
for this is that Topex/Poseidon and ERS-2 gather data in the same time period from June 1995 - June 2002
. Jason-1 and Envisat extends this period to June 2008. This make a comparable trend of 13 years between
the two series of missions possible.
ESA missions are using a polar orbit and provides data for up to latitude 98 ◦.
This both has the
advantages of almost total global cover and the disadvantages of higher uncertainties due to sea ice at high
latitudes. NASA's missions span of latitude is - 66◦to 66◦.

2.3
Corrections
The error in the measured sea height can be divided into dierent small contribution groups which each have
dierent corrections.
Atmospheric corrections accounts for the delay in the pulse's travel time trough the atmosphere. The three
rst corrections described; ionosphere correction, wet troposphere correction, and dry troposphere correction
are atmospheric corrections. Errors from geophysical signals altering the sea surface height is corrected with
inverse barometer correction and tide correction. Instrumental errors is corrected with sea state bias. The
model mentioned in this chapter is the default option in the RADS database which will be metioned in
chapter 3.
herrors = hdry + hwet + hiono + hssb + hatm + htide
(2.3)
2.3.1
Ionosphere Correction
The ionosphere is the upper layer of the earth atmosphere ranging from 60 km to 1200 km from Earth's
surface. Due to solar radiation it contains free charged electrons and ions. The microwave pulse from the
satellite is slowed by the free particles in the ionosphere.
The error in the ionosphere path delay is in
the range of 0 −500 mm. The delay is proportional to the density of charged particles in the ionosphere.
The delay in the radar pulse is dispersive. Meaning the change in propagation speed is depended on the
pulse frequency. The ionosphere delay can therefore be corrected for by sending two pulses with dierent
frequencies instead of one.
Topex/Poseidon, Jason-1, Jason-2 and Envisat have dual frequency altimeters. The dierence measured
in the two frequencies are smoothed over 200 km of the satellite track, to minimize noise. In RADS ERS-2
uses the NIC08 model to correct for ionosphere delay (Scharroo 2008). NIC08 is based on global ionosphere
maps [GIM]. GIM's are made from other satellites with GPS's measuring with dual frequencies instrument
since 1998. The GIM model accuracy is 14 procent (Vignudelli et al. 2010).

2.3.2
Dry Troposphere Correction
Dry troposphere correction delays the pulse identically to ionosphere correction.
The delay is from the
dry gases in the atmosphere mainly oxygen and nitrogen. The correction has the highest adjustment of
around ±2.3 m (Vignudelli et al. 2010) . However the variance over time dry troposphere error is only a few
centimeters (Radar Altimetry Tutorial - 2014).
2.3.3
Wet Troposphere Correction
Wet troposphere delay is due to water vapour in the tropopsphere and liquid water drops in clouds. The path
delay is in the range of 0 −500 mm where water drops only contributes with around 0 −10 mm (Vignudelli
et al. 2010). Wet troposphere correction compared to dry troposphere correction has high spatial and time
variations. In air which is hot and has high humidity the correction is close to half a meter. The amount of
water vapour can be estimated with on board passive nadir looking mircrowave radiometer
Topex/Poseidon, Jason-1, Jason-2, ERS-2 and Envisat all carries onboard microwave radiometers used
for wet troposphere corrections (A. Ollivier 2012).
2.3.4
Sea State Bias Correction
Sea state bias [SSB] is known to be the most dicult correction to model.
Sea state bias corrects for
instrumental errors. Foremost there is a dierence between the true mean sea surface and the sea surface
measured inside the satellites footprint. In the footprint the dynamic topography varies due to waves. The
waves trough reects a higher percentage of the pulse than the waves crest. Hence the travel path of the
pulse increases and the mean sea level inside the footprint is measured to low. This eect is called the
electromagnetic [EM] bias. The rest of the error contributing to the sea state errors are small in comparison.
Sea state bias ranges from a few centimetres to a few decimetres.
The simplest way to model sea state bias is as a fraction of the signicant wave height [SWH]. The
signicant wave height is dened as the mean height of the highest third of the wave.

2.3.5
Inverse Barometer Correction
The inverse barometer correction, also sometimes called the dynamic atmosphere correction, removes the
contribution from the atmospheric pressure. The sea surface height depends on the pressure of the atmo-
sphere. At high pressure the surface is pressed downward and visa versa at low pressure. The size of the
correction varies within ± 150 mm and has higher values at high latitudes (Vignudelli et al. 2010).
The correction model used for all satellite missions is MOG2D model. MOG2D is a higly complicated,
barotropic and non-linear model. The resolution of the model is down to a grid of 100 km in deep ocean and
10 km in shallow and coastal waters (Vignudelli et al. 2010).
2.3.6
The Geoid
The geoid is an mathematical model approximating Earth's surface like the reference ellipsoid and its vari-
ation in time is negligible. The geoid is the shape the ocean surface would have if Earth's gravitation and
rotation was the only two forces aecting it. Hence it is a equipotential surface, meaning gravity everywhere
on it is constant.
The default model used in RADS for all satellites is the model CLS01 MSS (Scharroo 2008). MSS stands
for mean sea surface and is determined by satellite altimetry data. MSS is the mean of the geoid and dynamic
ocean topography [MDT] over a time period of decades. For increasing time periods MDT →0 and the MSS
coincide closely with the geoid. CLS01 benets from long altimetry trends and improved mapping technique
(Fabrice Hernandez n.d.).
When the MSS is subtracted from the measured height the remaining sea surface height is in meter scale.
Figure 2.3: Model of the geoid. Greatly exaggerated to illustration the variation. The variation is the range
±100 compared to the reference ellipsoid.
2.3.7
Tides
Tides gives an oset in the measured sea surface height. There are two eects contributing to the ocean
tides. The rst is the most well know gravitational attraction between water masses and the moon and sun.
The strength of the attraction varies due to the cycle of the moon and Earth relative to the sun creating ebb
and spring tides. The second eect is the load tide. This is the elastic response of the earth to the change
in ocean tides. The tidal correction becomes a sum of the two eects:
∆helastic ocean tide = ∆hocean tide + ∆hload tide
(2.6)
The load tide is small compared to the ocean tide and contributes to around 4-6 procent (Vignudelli et al.
2010). Tide gauges are xed on the earth and do not measure load tide in contrast to satellites.
The tides are corrected with the last version of the Finite Element Solution tide model called FES2004.
FES2004 is a global nite element model with a spatial resolution down to a 1/8◦x 1/8◦grid. The model
was made by Christian Le Provost in the early nineties and has been improved since (My AVISO+: Aviso+
n.d.). The FES2004 accounts for fteen tidal constituents. The bathymetry has been improved since the
early stage. The model is best on the deep ocean. In the coastal zones where the tidal range is larger, the
modelling of the tidal signal is more complex.

2.3.8
Global Isostatic Adjustment
In the recent ice age more of Earth was covered in ice. Ice presses the Earth's surface down. The Earth is
still slowly rebounding back. This causes the ocean basins to become slightly larger. The correction for this
is called glacial isostatic adjustment [GIA]. GIA is estimated by models to be −0.3 mm/yr (Peltier 2004).
Note that GIA is a correction applied on the Global mean sea level rates and not the individual sea surface
height measurements as the other correction described. Global mean sea level rates can be represented with
and without GIA. The rates from IPCC in table 1.1 has GIA included.

Chapter 4
Method
The data was downloaded from RADS database and stored to DTU servers for each satelite mission. RADS
stores each pass and cycle in individual les. With a simple costum made fortran program, SEL, each pass
is combined into one le. Reducing the amount of les to 253 for Topex/Poseidon and Jason-1 missions and
1002 for ERS-2 and Envisat.
Then the les were downloaded to a local computer. Here the les were processed in matlab with costum
scripts. The output is 1◦x 1◦interpolated grids of rates and timeserie trends for mean sea level and each of
the six corrections. The outputs are shown and described in chapter 5.
4.1
Time series
Trimester trends are made by sorting all data for each mission in time intervals. The time period is 10 days
for mean sea level and 30 days for each correction to decrease computer processing time. The global mean
sea level is found as the mean of all the sea level data in the 10 days period.
Topex/Poseidon and Jason-1 has a period of 10 days as seen in table 2.2. Hence the global mean sea level
found correspond to one full coverage of the globe. The same is not true for ERS-2 and Envisat which has
periods of 35 days, but the 10 day time interval is still chosen for better comparison between the missions.
Also only data between latitude −66◦and 66◦is processed for the same reason. Topex/Poseidon and Jason-1
has no coverages at high latitudes. The 30 day interval can give some periodic bias for some of the corrections
which will be taken into consideration when evaluating the data.
Trends are found by simple linear regression of the global mean sea level and each of the correction. The
linear regression is done in matlab with the function polyt. Polyt ts a rst degree polynomial to the
trends. The resulting coecient is the rate. The 95 procent condence interval is calculated by the simple
formula 4.1.

on each cell to get a local value of the sea level rise. Squares with less than 100 points is sorted out. After
this a land mask is added to remove bad trends over land. Finally empty cells a lled out by interpolation
in matlab by nearest neighbour method. This results is displayed in the next chapter. The dierence in
the correction in the grid is made by subtracting ESA's missions from NASA's mission. This way the grids
are positive where Topex/Poseidon and Jason-1 has highest trend and negative where Envisat and ERS-2
has highest trend. Each grid and global trend for corrections is investigated which can explain some of the
dierence in the obtained values for Global mean sea level rates.
Note that the corrections is in the RADS database positive but has to be subtracted.
