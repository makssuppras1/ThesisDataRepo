Abstract
This thesis explores advanced methodologies for the ecological analysis of shallow-water
benthic environments using Three Dimensional (3D) models generated by combining multi-
camera underwater imaging systems with auxiliary navigation sensors. The focus is on de-
veloping an integrated workflow that fuses visual data from synchronized camera arrays with
navigation inputs from Ultra-Short Baseline (USBL) systems and depth sensors mounted on
a Remotely Operated Vehicle (ROV). This approach enables the generation of georeferenced
3D reconstructions of the seafloor with high spatial resolution (typically below 1 mm per
pixel) and metric accuracy, meaning the models preserve real-world scales and geometries,
with minimal perspective distortion, suitable for precise spatial analysis and the extraction
of Essential Biodiversity Variable (EBV).
The proposed methodology comprises multi-camera calibration, photogrammetric prepro-
cessing, and 3D reconstruction using Structure-from-Motion (SfM) and Multi-View Stereo
(MvS), followed by post-processing steps such as orientation correction and geospatial align-
ment. A series of comparative experiments were conducted using different camera configu-
rations, ranging from single-camera setups to six-camera arrays, and varying reconstruction
parameters, in order to evaluate their impact on model quality, noise levels, computational
cost, and Ground Sampling Distance (GSD). Particular focus was placed on quantitatively
analyzing multiple 4-camera configurations to assess their influence on reconstruction accu-
racy and scene coverage, with the goal of identifying the optimal camera configuration for
underwater habitat mapping.
The standardized outputs, 3D tiles, orthophotos, and Digital Elevation Models (DEM); were
visualized and analyzed using geospatial platforms such as CesiumJS and QGIS for EBV
extraction.
In addition, the thesis reviews the state of the art in underwater 3D reconstruction, com-
paring leading photogrammetry software and geospatial visualization platforms in terms of
capabilities and support for marine data.
Furthermore, this study demonstrates the advantages of 3D approaches over traditional Two
Dimensional (2D) mapping techniques in the context of biodiversity monitoring, including
enhanced detection of benthic features, reduced misclassification rates caused by the limita-
tions of flat 2D views and more robust support for spatial EBV annotation. These findings
contribute to advancing scalable, automated workflows for marine habitat assessment and
long-term ecological monitoring.
Keywords: 3D reconstruction, underwater photogrammetry, sensor fusion, ROV, Structure-
from-Motion, Essential Biodiversity Variables, 3D tiles, orthophotos, marine benthic habi-
tats, CesiumJS, QGIS, ecological monitoring, navigation sensors, multi-camera systems, op-
timal camera configuration.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Benthic marine ecosystems, including coral reefs, seagrass meadows, and rocky seafloors,
are among the most biologically diverse and ecologically important regions on the planet
[1]. These ecosystems support a wide variety of species and provide essential services such
as carbon sequestration, coastal protection, and habitat formation [2]. However, they are
highly vulnerable to stressors like climate change, pollution, eutrophication, marine litter,
and unsustainable fishing practices [3].
To effectively manage and conserve benthic environments, reliable and scalable methods for
assessing biodiversity are essential. However, the complexity, inaccessibility, and spatial vari-
ability of these habitats pose significant challenges. Traditional sampling techniques—such
as acoustic methods, grab sampling and coring, beam trawling, and visual surveys—each
offer trade-offs. Acoustic systems provide broad coverage and depth penetration but often
fail to resolve fine biological detail or distinguish organisms from the seafloor. Grab sampling
and coring yield high-resolution data but are labor-intensive and spatially limited. Beam
trawling can estimate biomass but is invasive and ecologically disruptive. Visual methods,
including diver-based and camera-based observations, are non-destructive but constrained
by visibility, lighting, and deployment logistics underwater.
Within this context, optical imaging, particularly using multi-camera systems integrated
with navigation sensors, emerges as a compelling alternative. It enables high-resolution,
spatially continuous, and non-invasive seafloor monitoring. Despite challenges related to
turbidity and illumination, advances in underwater photogrammetry and sensor fusion have
significantly mitigated these limitations. By combining synchronized camera arrays with
navigation inputs from systems such as Ultra-Short Baseline (USBL), Inertial Measurement
Unit (IMU), and depth sensors onboard a Remotely Operated Vehicle (ROV), it is now
possible to generate textured, georeferenced Three Dimensional (3D) reconstructions of the
seafloor.
These models offer high spatial resolution (typically below 1 mm per pixel of Ground Sam-
pling Distance (GSD)) and metric accuracy, preserving real-world scales, distances, and
geometries with minimal perspective distortion. Such 3D reconstructions support detailed
structural and biological analysis and enable the extraction of key ecological indicators, in-
cluding Essential Biodiversity Variable (EBV), making them a powerful tool for benthic
biodiversity monitoring.
1.2
Problem Statement
While image-based 3D reconstruction has proven successful in terrestrial and aerial contexts
[4], its application in shallow marine environments faces challenges including variable light-
ing, water turbidity and low-contrast scenes [5]. Moreover, underwater photogrammetric
outputs are often poorly georeferenced and misaligned due to the lack of absolute reference
systems (e.g. the absence of Global Positioning System (GPS) signals underwater) [6].
Page 7 of 69
==PAGE:14==
Current underwater photogrammetry workflows typically rely on a single camera configura-
tions, which limits the geometric fidelity and texture resolution of the resulting 3D models
[7]. In addition, many 3D reconstruction tools do not inherently support the integration of
underwater navigation or positioning data, often requiring labor-intensive manual alignment
or post-processing steps (e.g. using ground control points or marker-based registration) to
achieve spatially coherent reconstructions [6].
This limits the use of photogrammetric outputs for scalable, reproducible, and automated
biodiversity monitoring. Therefore, there is a need to explore novel workflows that combine
multi-view camera systems with auxiliary navigation data to generate georeferenced 3D
models suitable for EBV extraction.
1.3
Project description
This thesis aims to investigate promising avenues of sensor fusion that combine multi-view
underwater camera imagery with underwater platform auxiliary navigation sensors (such as
USBL data and IMU) to increase the accuracy of reconstructed data. Finally, the project
will investigate ways in which such spatial data can be used to support acquisition of EBVs
in shallow water benthos.
The project will achieve the following objectives:
1. Describe relevant EBV for benthic mapping.
2. Identify the State-of-the-Art (SOTA) techniques in 3D reconstruction using synchronous
multi-view camera systems.
3. Synthesize and compare 3D textured models of benthic scenes using different recon-
struction methods to identify the most promising advancements in SOTA.
4. Analyze the complexities of obtaining 3D data from cameras and auxiliary navigation
sensors in underwater environments, and evaluate the limitations of SOTA techniques
in these conditions.
5. Explain methods to improve 3D reconstructions through sensor fusion of ROV navi-
gation sensors such as USBL, IMU, depth and altimeter readings, and imaging sonar.
6. Develop methods and software implementations to either improve upon SOTA in un-
derwater scenes or adapt existing SOTA approaches from other domains for underwater
applications.
7. Investigate and implement methods to extract meaningful spatial information from the
reconstructions to support benthos biodiversity analysis.
8. Writing of a technical report documenting the development process and the achieved
results.

1.4
Contributions
The main contributions of this thesis are:
• A reproducible methodology for underwater 3D model generation using multi-camera
photogrammetry combined with ROV navigation sensor data.
• A comparative evaluation of different multi-camera configurations and reconstruction
parameters to assess 3D model quality, noise levels, and computational cost. In par-
ticular, an in-depth analysis was performed on several 4-camera layouts to determine
the optimal camera configuration for underwater 3D reconstruction.
• The integration of georeferencing and orientation refinement techniques using naviga-
tion sensor data.
• Standardized outputs exported in formats compatible with CesiumJS and QGIS for
visualization and EBV extraction.
• A comparative review of state-of-the-art photogrammetry software and geospatial plat-
forms for underwater 3D reconstruction, including their capabilities, limitations, and
suitability for benthic ecological applications.
• A discussion of the advantages of 3D over Two Dimensional (2D) for biodiversity
applications and an outlook toward future automation and Machine Learning (ML)-
based workflows.

Background
2.1
Overview of EBVs
Monitoring and assessing biodiversity in benthic ecosystems is critical to understanding
their ecological function and resilience. However, the complexity and dynamic nature of
these habitats present challenges for consistent and accurate biodiversity assessments. This
is where EBV become particularly valuable.
EBV are a set of standardized measurements designed to capture key aspects of biodiversity
dynamics across ecosystems, over time. They provide a common framework (Figure 1) that
enables researchers and policymakers to monitor changes in biodiversity, assess the health
of ecosystems, and identify trends that may require conservation or management action [8].
EBVs are especially useful because they extract complex ecological data into metrics that are
both meaningful and comparable across regions and scales. They serve as a bridge between
raw biodiversity observations, intrinsically linked to primary observations, both in situ and
remote sensing; and high-level indicators used for policy and conservation planning [9].
Fig. 1. The EBV framework [10].
By integrating EBVs, researchers can gain a more comprehensive understanding of marine
ecosystem dynamics, as oceanographic conditions directly influence species distributions,
community compositions, and habitat structures [9].
In benthic environments, EBVs can be applied to various aspects such as species popula-
tions (e.g., abundance of starfish or anemones), community composition, organismal traits
(e.g., size, morphology, or spatial arrangement) and ecosystem structure—including habitat
complexity, substrate type, and spatial heterogeneity (see Table 2).

2.2
Emerging technologies for benthic observations
Emerging technologies for benthic observations are revolutionizing the way we monitor and
assess marine environments, particularly shallow-water benthic ecosystems [11]. This project
focuses on the integration of Multi-Camera System (MCS)s and navigation sensor data such
as USBL; mounted in a ROV to create high-resolution, three-dimensional reconstructions of
underwater benthic habitats. Despite challenges such as poor visibility, light attenuation,
and water-induced distortions, these technologies, combined with advanced preprocessing
methods like image enhancement and filtering, offer powerful tools for accurate spatial map-
ping. The goal is to use these refined datasets to extract EBV, ultimately improving the
monitoring and conservation efforts of benthic ecosystems [12].
Remotely Operated Vehicle (ROV)
Remotely Operated Vehicles (ROVs) have become an essential tool in underwater explo-
ration, particularly for benthic habitat mapping.
ROVs are tethered underwater robots
that can be equipped with a range of sensors, providing real-time data and high-resolution
imagery from the seafloor. Unlike Autonomous Underwater Vehicles (AUV), which are un-
tethered and navigate autonomously, ROVs rely on a tether that connects them to a surface
control system. This tether allows for continuous power supply and real-time data transmis-
sion, making ROVs ideal for environments with complex bathymetry where fine control is
required, such as mapping coral reefs or rocky substrates [13].
Fig. 2. BlueROV2 heavy configuration by Technical University of Denmark (DTU) Aqua.
The tethered nature of ROVs enables them to operate for extended periods without the
constraints faced by AUVs, which are limited by battery power. Additionally, the tether
Page 12 of 69
==PAGE:19==
allows for the attachment of various sensors, such as imaging sonar, video cameras, and
other sensors, that provide detailed and accurate data for benthic surveys.
However, ROVs require constant supervision and operation by trained personnel, which
increases operational costs. Their tethered design limits coverage compared to AUVs, and
additional sensors are often necessary for accurate underwater positioning. Additionally,
sediment disturbance caused by thrusters can be a concern during operations [14].
The visual data used in this project was externally captured using a customized BlueROV2
from DTU AQUA (see figure 2), equipped with a MCS, and was subsequently utilized for
the project. To enhance the precision of navigation and positioning, additional navigation
sensors were integrated, including a USBL (Ultra-Short Baseline) system for accurate real-
time positioning of the ROV and an IMU for providing data on the vehicle’s orientation and
movement. These combined sensors ensure more accurate spatial mapping and help maintain
precise control of the ROV, even in challenging underwater environments, facilitating high-
quality data collection for benthic habitat monitoring and biodiversity assessments.
Multi-Camera System (MCS)
In this project, a customized BlueROV2 heavy configuration from DTU AQUA was used,
equipped with a MCS (six cameras, see Figure 3) sourced from Blue Atlas Robotics [15],
powered by a 16.8V LiPo battery, housed in a separate container. The system is specifically
designed for high-precision underwater inspections and visual data collection. The cameras
are spaced 0.11 meters apart to establish a defined baseline, which is important for accurate
scaling of 3D reconstruction. The camera IDs, names, and positions are listed in Table 1.
Each camera is linked to a computer housed within a cylindrical enclosure, and the system
operates using Robot Operating System 2 (ROS2). ROS2 is responsible for managing cam-
era recordings and adjusting parameters such as exposure and focus through service calls.
The camera system operates independently from the ROV’s primary control system, com-
municating via Ethernet connections, ensuring that the camera network is separate from the
ROV’s core systems. This setup allows greater flexibility and control over the visual data
collection process without interfering with the vehicle’s navigation and core functions.
An advantage of this design is its offline, standalone operation: cameras start recording either
at system startup or via a pre-mission trigger. Data is stored locally on a 1TB SSD during
the dive and transferred post-mission, improving reliability by avoiding potential issues from
real-time dependencies.
In addition to the cameras, the lighting system is integrated into the same setup, with the
lights connected to the same computer within the cylindrical enclosure. The brightness of
the lights can also be adjusted via ROS2 service calls, ensuring synchronized control of both
the camera and lighting systems for optimal illumination during image capture.
The lights are numbered in the Figure 4, with lights 1 and 2 slightly angled to shine above the
area targeted by the cameras, while lights 3 and 4 are directed perpendicular to the camera’s
field of view. This configuration helps avoid direct illumination of the scene, encouraging
light scattering. This technique ensures more diffuse lighting, reducing harsh shadows and
enhancing the overall quality of the images captured, which is essential for accurate 3D
reconstruction.

3D Photogrametry
Photogrammetry from MCSs are essential tools for creating accurate 3D reconstructions
of benthic environments using software packages. These methods provide high-resolution
data that can be used to map and monitor various underwater habitats, including seafloor
fauna, seagrasses, macroalgae, and corals [16]. The process involves capturing overlapping
images of the habitat, which can then be used to generate 3D models of the seafloor. These
models are particularly valuable for extracting spatial information, such as habitat structure,
volume, and species distribution, which is crucial for biodiversity analysis and can be used
for non-destructive monitoring of benthic habitats [17].
By analyzing these reconstructions, it is possible to estimate the volume of benthic habitats
or the size of individual organisms, helping to assess ecosystem health and productivity. For
example, the volume and surface area of seafloor features, such as coral reefs or macroalgae
beds, can be calculated directly from the 3D models [18].
The use of navigation data, such as the positioning information provided by USBL systems
or Doppler Velocity Logs (DVL), enhances the accuracy of the 3D reconstructions by en-
suring the vehicle’s precise position and velocity during data collection. This integration of
high-resolution imagery with accurate positioning allows for the creation of detailed spatial
datasets that can be analyzed to extract meaningful information, such as the distribution of
Page 14 of 69
==PAGE:21==
species or changes in habitat structure, contributing to better-informed biodiversity conser-
vation strategies.
Despite its advantages, photogrammetry has limitations, including image quality dependence
on water conditions, geolocation challenges due to the lack of GPS underwater, and restricted
spatial coverage unless mounted on a mobile platform. Additionally, high equipment costs
and the need for trained operators can limit accessibility. Photogrammetry also lacks tax-
onomical accuracy, as species identification relies solely on visual data without biological
sampling [13].
The data presented in Tables 2 highlights the significant role of photogrammetry and drop-
video methods for monitoring benthic ecosystems, as they contribute to the assessment of
multiple EBVs [13]. By capturing high-resolution spatial and structural data, these technolo-
gies enable the assessment of key biodiversity and ocean variables, such as species abundance,
ecosystem structure, and habitat extent.

Image Acquisition
Capturing multiple images of the object or scene from different viewpoints ensures a complete
reconstruction. The choice of camera system significantly impacts reconstruction accuracy.
For this project, a multicamera system was chosen to obtain more views and improve recon-
struction quality. Compared to monocular and stereo camera setups, a multicamera system
captures a greater number of images from different angles in the same timestamp, increasing
the density of the 3D point cloud, reducing occlusions, and enhancing depth estimation,
resulting in a more detailed and robust 3D reconstruction [22].
Image Preprocessing
The quality of underwater images is often degraded due to the physical properties of wa-
ter, which affect both light propagation and image acquisition. Key factors such as light
attenuation, refraction effects, and scattering contribute to reduced image quality, leading
to challenges in feature extraction and accurate reconstruction [23].
The absorption and scattering of light due to particles cause low contrast, blurring, and color
distortions, making it difficult for both human observers and computer vision algorithms to
interpret underwater imagery [24].
In benthic shallow waters (depths <10 m), effects caused by light reflection and refraction on
the water surface introduce additional challenges. These distortions, which vary with illumi-
nation conditions, significantly affect image-matching algorithms, leading to inconsistencies
in texture reconstruction and inaccurate feature matching [25].
These challenges significantly impact visual-odometry-based methods, which rely on the
detection of reusable features for accurate homography estimation and transformation cal-
culations.
To mitigate these issues, image preprocessing plays a crucial role in improving feature ex-
traction, enhancing image clarity, and increasing the number of successful feature matches.
Common preprocessing techniques include contrast enhancement, color correction, and noise
reduction, all of which contribute to better frame alignment and overall accuracy in under-
water mapping and habitat monitoring [24] [26] [27].

Structure-from-Motion(SfM)
Structure-from-Motion (SfM) is a technique used to reconstruct a three-dimensional struc-
ture from a collection of two-dimensional images captured from different viewpoints. The
process takes as input a set of overlapping images of the same scene and estimates the 3D
geometry based on feature correspondences between them [28].
As shown in Figure 7, a 3D structure is captured from multiple viewpoints (Image 1, Image
2, Image 3). By tracking specific feature points (x1, x2, ...) across images, the pixel locations
(p1,1, p1,2, ...) are used to estimate both the camera motion (R1, t1; R2, t2; R3, t3) and the
3D reconstruction of the structure [29].
Fig. 7. Illustration of the SfM process. [29].

==PAGE:25==
The final outcome of SfM includes:
• A sparse 3D point cloud representing key feature points in the scene (see sparse A
point cloud in Figure 14).
• The computed camera poses, describing the position and orientation of each camera.
• The intrinsic and extrinsic parameters associated with each camera view.
Structure-from-Motion systems divide this process into four stages:
1. Undistortion and Rectification
Image undistortion is applied using camera calibration parameters, which include the
intrinsic matrix and distortion coefficients obtained from a checkerboard or another
known pattern. This step corrects radial and tangential distortions that cause straight
lines to appear curved, ensuring more accurate feature extraction and image alignment
[30].
Following undistortion, rectification is performed to align corresponding image pairs
onto the same epipolar plane. This transformation simplifies the stereo correspondence
search by ensuring that matching points lie along the same pixel row in both images.
By rectifying image pairs, the computational complexity of depth estimation is reduced,
and more robust feature matching is achieved.
2. Feature Detection and extraction
The next step is to detect the key features of each image.
A feature is a distinct
point in an image that carries unique information and can be identified from different
perspectives, such as corners, edges, or shapes (see x1, x2, ... in Figure 7). These points
must have a well-defined position, stability under illumination changes, and efficient
detectability.
Feature detection can be performed using different algorithms, each with its own ad-
vantages, particularly when working with underwater images. In underwater environ-
ments, factors such as low visibility, light scattering, and distortion due to water can
make the feature detection process challenging. However, several algorithms remain
effective [31].
Scale-Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT) is a robust method that detects key
points invariant to scale, rotation, and illumination changes. This makes it particularly
well-suited for applications requiring high accuracy, such as underwater robotic navi-
gation or marine life tracking, where the scale and orientation of objects may change
due to the environment [32].
Speeded-Up Robust Features (SURF) is an optimized version of SIFT that main-
tains robustness while improving computational efficiency. This makes it ideal for real-
time underwater applications, such as object recognition in underwater vehicles, where
fast processing is crucial for dynamic environments [31].

3. Feature Matching and Geometric Verification
For each feature in the first image, there can be many candidates pairs in the other
images along the epipolar line. Many algorithms can help to find the correct matching
such as Estimated Nearest Neighbor (ENNA). This algorithm can find corre-
spondences between features in different images of the same scene [35].
Moreover, algorithms such as Random Sample Consensus (RANSAC) can be
used for estimation in the presence of outliers by removing them. It is a good algorithm
to use when there is a large amount of noise in the image scene which is normally the
case of underwater pictures [36].

4. Sparse reconstruction
Reconstructing a 3D scene from multiple images involves estimating the camera poses
and recovering the structure of the scene. The process is greatly aided by epipolar

==PAGE:27==
geometry, which allows for the establishment of correspondences between images and
provides the necessary depth information [38].
Epipolar geometry defines the relationship between two cameras, a 3D point in the
world, and its projection onto the image plane, as illustrated in Figure 10. Consider
a point P observed from two different perspectives (Camera 1 and Camera 2). The
centers of both cameras, along with the 3D point P, define the epipolar plane. The
point P is projected onto each camera’s image plane as conjugate points x1 and x2,
which lie on their respective epipolar lines.
Fig. 10. Epipolar geometry model [39].
These projections are constrained by the epipolar geometry, as illustrated by the epipo-
lar lines. The epipolar points e1 and e2 correspond to the intersections of the line con-
necting the camera centers with the image planes. By connecting the projected point
x1 and the epipolar point e1, we define the epipolar line l1, which represents all possible
positions of x1 in Camera 1’s image frame. Similarly, the position of x2 is constrained
to the epipolar line l2 in Camera 2’s image frame. This constraint dramatically reduces
the search space for correspondences and aids in feature matching across views.

For more than one camera (stereo or multi-camera), the multi-view approach extends
SfM to sequences of images [42]. In this method, camera positions are estimated relative
to a reference camera, often chosen as Camera 1. By keeping all poses in a common
coordinate system, transformations between views are more consistent, simplifying
later steps like triangulation and dense reconstruction.
The goal of multi-view SfM is to estimate the camera trajectory as it moves through the
scene. However, errors accumulate over time due to image noise, lens distortions, and
environmental factors (e.g., floating particles in underwater imagery). This accumula-
tion of small errors leads to an effect known as drift, where the estimated trajectory
deviates from the true motion path (see Figure 13).
Fig. 13. Motion drift effect [43].
To mitigate drift, loop closure and global bundle adjustment are applied. Loop closure
detects when the camera revisits a previously seen part of the scene, aligning the
new observations with past ones to correct accumulated errors [44]. Global bundle
adjustment refines the full reconstruction by minimizing reprojection errors across all
views, reducing inconsistencies in the estimated structure and camera poses [45].
5. Dense reconstruction: Multi-View Stereo (MvS)
MvS refines the sparse point cloud obtained from SfM into a dense point cloud
by computing depth maps from multiple images [46]. A key factor influencing the
accuracy of MvS is the baseline, which represents the distance between camera centers,
and the disparity, which corresponds to the horizontal displacement between a pixel
in one image and its match in another.
For each pixel in an image, its depth is estimated by finding correspondences across
multiple images, following the depth-from-disparity relationship:

A larger baseline improves depth accuracy, particularly for distant objects, by in-
creasing the disparity; making it easier to detect correspondences and estimate depth
more precisely [47]. This is because a larger baseline causes a greater horizontal dis-
placement between corresponding pixels in different images, which allows for a more
accurate depth calculation, especially for objects further away from the camera. How-
ever, this also increases the risk of occlusions and potential errors in matching pixels,
as the disparity may become too large for certain objects or areas. For close objects,
a larger baseline can provide better precision, but for far objects, the baseline must be
carefully calibrated to avoid significant errors in disparity estimation.
Once individual depth maps are estimated for each image, they are merged into a
consistent 3D model by considering visibility constraints and filtering out inconsistent
points.
After depth map fusion, the resulting dense point cloud undergoes additional refine-
ment steps, including outlier removal and consistency checks across multiple views
[48]. This process ensures that erroneous or poorly matched points are filtered out,
improving the reliability of the reconstructed model.
Mesh Generation and Texturing
After obtaining a dense point cloud, the next step is to generate a continuous 3D surface
representation through meshing. This process involves techniques such as Poisson Surface
Reconstruction, which approximates a smooth and watertight surface by solving for an im-
plicit function that best fits the point cloud [49]. Another method is Delaunay Triangulation,
which constructs a mesh by connecting neighboring points in a way that maximizes the min-
imum angle of each triangle, thereby reducing irregularities and ensuring a more stable
structure [50].
Once the mesh is generated, texture mapping is performed to enhance visual realism. This
step involves projecting image data onto the mesh using the original input images.
The final textured mesh provides a detailed and visually accurate 3D model suitable for
visualization, analysis, and further applications in underwater mapping and photogrammetry
(see example Figure 14 D)).

Orthophotos and orthomaps
Orthophotos are geometrically corrected images derived from aerial or photogrammetric
data, in which distortions due to camera tilt and terrain relief have been removed. Unlike
raw photographs, orthophotos maintain a uniform scale, allowing for precise measurements
and spatial analysis [52]. When these images are georeferenced, they are often referred to as
orthomaps.
In the context of biodiversity monitoring, orthophotos and orthomaps are essential tools for
generating spatially explicit data. Their uniform scale and geospatial accuracy make them
particularly useful for the identification and analysis of EBV [53], such as:
• Species distribution: Mapping the presence and range of marine species or coral
patches across a surveyed area.
• Habitat structure: Characterizing the physical layout and composition of underwa-
ter ecosystems.
• Area estimation: Quantifying the extent of habitats or colonies with high spatial
accuracy.
In addition to orthophotos, photogrammetric workflows often produce elevation models that
provide complementary spatial information. These include the Digital Surface Model (DSM),
which represents the elevation of the terrain along with surface features such as corals,
rocks, or vegetation; the Digital Terrain Model (DTM), which represents only the bare-
earth topography; and the more general Digital Elevation Models (DEM), a broader term

3
State of the Art
3D reconstruction using synchronous multi-view camera systems has significantly improved
the accuracy and detail of underwater benthic mapping. These advancements allow for the
creation of high-resolution models of marine ecosystems, including the seafloor and benthic
organisms, which are crucial for monitoring habitat changes and biodiversity.
The fusion of multi-camera imagery with these navigation sensors aims to improve the robust-
ness and precision of underwater reconstructions, providing more reliable spatial datasets.
This enhanced data quality facilitates the extraction of a greater number of EBVs, enabling
researchers to conduct more detailed ecological analysis and improve conservation strategies
for benthic ecosystems.
3.1
Photogrammetry Software
During the last few years, several software solutions have been developed to perform pho-
togrammetry based 3D reconstruction, improving feature detection, matching, and bundle
adjustment algorithms to generate accurate models from multi-view imagery. Professional
softwares such as Metashape [57], 3DF Zephyr (Zephyr) [58], RealityCapture [59] or Pix4D
[60] are widely used in the field.
Other open-source alternatives like COLMAP [46], VisualSFM [61] or Meshroom (AliceVi-

Agisoft Metashape is one of the most widely used pipelines for underwater photogram-

3DF Zephyr is known for its user-friendly interface while being a robust automated work-

but lacks multi-camera support. The paid subscription includes additional features such
as multi-ICP registration, point cloud comparison tools, and the ability to control points,

RealityCapture is particularly recognized for its speed and efficiency, leveraging Graphics
Processing Units (GPU) acceleration to process large datasets rapidly. It supports a variety
of applications, including textured 3D meshes and geo-referenced maps, which can be auto-

for both beginners and experienced users. It offers powerful features like a one-click wizard
for creating geo-referenced maps, an AI-based tool to classify terrain, and a continuous draft
mode for on-site scanning [66].

3.2
Platforms Supporting 3D Model Integration
The integration of 3D reconstructions into biodiversity monitoring and marine habitat map-
ping has opened new possibilities for accurate, high-resolution environmental assessments.
Photogrammetry-based 3D models allow researchers to capture detailed representations of
underwater ecosystems, providing crucial insights into habitat complexity, species distri-
bution, and environmental changes over time. However, for these 3D reconstructions to be
meaningfully integrated into geospatial frameworks, it is essential to ensure Open Geospatial
Consortium (OGC) [80] compliance, enabling seamless interoperability with other geospatial
datasets and analysis tools.
Once georeferenced, the 3D models can be visualized on platforms like CesiumJS, where
they can be overlaid with bathymetric maps, satellite data, and additional oceanographic
layers. CesiumJS [81] allows users to interact with the 3D models, take measurements, and
assess biodiversity changes over time. The models can be uploaded as 3D Tiles (.glb) to
Cesium Ion, which optimizes large-scale datasets for smooth online visualization, supporting
OGC-compliant datasets and allowing seamless interoperability with marine spatial planning
tools.
Several studies have highlighted CesiumJS as a powerful tool for web-based marine data vi-
sualization. Kulawiak (2015) [82] proposed a web-based system for disseminating multibeam
sonar data in a geographic context, addressing the challenges of managing large underwater
datasets. His system uses 3D Tiles to serve point clouds and reconstructed shipwreck mod-
els through CesiumJS, demonstrating the feasibility of interactive, web-based marine data
visualization.
Similarly, Huadong Ma et al. (2022) [83] developed an integrated 3D visualization platform
for marine observation and environmental data in the South China Sea using CesiumJS and
WebGL. The system supports real-time monitoring, disaster warning, and scientific research
by making complex oceanographic data more accessible and interactive. The study high-
lights that one of the main challenges in marine research is not the collection of data, but
rather its limited practical use, often due to poor visualization tools and lack of integration.
By leveraging web-based 3D technologies, platforms like CesiumJS can enhance data uti-
lization by providing geospatially accurate, intuitive representations that help users better
understand and act on marine information.
Alternatively, these models can be integrated into EDITO (European Digital Twin of the
Ocean) [84], part of EMODnet and Copernicus, which provides a geospatial infrastructure
for marine data visualization and analysis. EDITO aims to create a virtual representation of
marine and coastal environments worldwide, enabling researchers to simulate and assess the
future impacts of climate change and human activities. By integrating 3D reconstructions
into EDITO, users can combine photogrammetric models with satellite observations, ocean
simulations, and biodiversity datasets, ensuring a comprehensive and interactive approach
to marine ecosystem monitoring. It is important to note that, while EMODnet does not
currently support direct uploads of 3D model files, EDITO does support Digital Twin appli-
Page 31 of 69
==PAGE:38==
cations. Additionally, it allows users to run Virtual Machine (VM) for processing, making
it a valuable tool for analyzing and visualizing complex marine datasets in a dynamic and
scalable way.
Also, these models can be integrated into Hydrophis [85], an innovative platform devel-
oped by Tritonia Scientific Ltd. Hydrophis transforms complex marine data into actionable
insights by integrating georeferenced 3D photogrammetry with real-time data. This combi-
nation enables detailed seabed visualization, tracking of environmental changes over time,
and the ability to overlay multiple data sources, including bathymetry, ecological surveys,
and structural assessments, within a single platform.
The platform features AI-powered tools for seabed feature identification, habitat change
monitoring, and automated species classification, streamlining decision-making processes for
researchers and marine professionals. Hydrophis supports the analysis of millimeter-precision
3D models and the integration of live survey data, ensuring accessibility, organization, and
ease of interpretation.
3.3
Platforms for 2D Mapping and Orthophoto Analysis
Georeferenced orthophotos, DSMs (Digital Surface Models), and DTMs (Digital Terrain
Models) serve as critical products for ecological and spatial analysis in marine environments.
These raster-based outputs enable precise habitat classification, temporal change detection,
and integration with other geospatial datasets such as bathymetry, benthic habitat maps,
and remote sensing products. For example, Janowski and Szulwic [53] employed orthophoto-
derived terrain models to support feasibility studies for a navigability channel, while Favaro
and Zargarpour [56] demonstrated the use of AUV-based orthophotos to study eelgrass
habitats in coastal ecosystems. These cases highlight the role of high-resolution orthophotos
in both planning and monitoring applications.
The most widely used platform for visualizing and analyzing these raster outputs is QGIS
[86], an open-source Geographic Information System.
QGIS allows users to overlay or-
thophotos with vector data, perform raster calculations, extract terrain metrics (e.g., slope,
roughness), and run custom geoprocessing workflows. For instance, Lamsters and Karuss
[87] produced high-resolution orthophotos and DSMs of Antarctic islands using AUV pho-
togrammetry and visualized and analyzed these datasets directly in QGIS to assess terrain
morphology and environmental conditions under extreme polar environments.
Additionally, platforms like Google Earth [88] support basic orthophoto visualization through
KML/KMZ overlays. Although more limited in analytical capability, Google Earth offers
an accessible interface for exploring spatial datasets in a global context, with measurement
tools for example.
For more advanced and scalable processing, orthophotos and DSMs can be uploaded to
platforms such as EDITO [84], which offers cloud-based analysis and interoperability with
Copernicus and EMODnet datasets.
These platforms allow combining orthophoto-based
data with oceanographic simulations and environmental models, enabling a more holistic
understanding of benthic ecosystems.

Data Acquisition
Multi-camera calibration
To ensure consistent spatial alignment across the six-camera system described previously, a
full calibration was performed using the Kalibr toolbox [89], a widely used tool for calibrating
multi-camera and visual-inertial systems with synchronized image streams.
The calibration dataset was recorded with the BlueROV2 in underwater conditions. The
vehicle was maneuvered in front of a 6 × 6 checkerboard calibration target with 88 mm
square size (Figure 17), ensuring that all cameras captured overlapping views from multiple
angles, distances, and positions within the frame.
Capturing the checkerboard from varied angles allows for robust estimation of the cameras’
relative orientations. Additionally, ensuring that the pattern is well distributed across the
entire image frame—covering different regions and positions—is essential for accurately es-
timating radial and tangential lens distortions. Changing the distance between the camera
Page 33 of 69
==PAGE:40==
and the checkerboard is particularly important for constraining the focal length during cal-
ibration. Placing the board in different image regions (e.g., near edges or corners) further
reveals lens distortion patterns, supporting more accurate intrinsic and extrinsic calibration
across the entire field of view.
The resulting image sequences were processed with Kalibr to estimate both the intrinsic
parameters of each camera and their relative poses. These calibrated models are reused in
the subsequent stages of point cloud and mesh generation.
Fig. 17. Checkerboard calibration target (6 × 6, 88 mm square size) used during the underwater multi-
camera calibration procedure.
To integrate the calibration into the photogrammetry software Zephyr, Kalibr’s output files
were converted from .txt to a compatible .xml format. This process included formatting
the intrinsic parameters, and for each camera, generating a dedicated .xml file containing
the intrinsic parameters extracted from Kalibr’s output. Each file follows a standardized
structure, listing the focal lengths (fx, fy), principal point coordinates (cx, cy), and dis-
tortion coefficients (k1, k2, p1, p2, k3), along with the camera name. This format ensured
compatibility with the photogrammetry pipeline and facilitated the seamless integration of
calibrated intrinsics for each individual camera.

4.4
Postprocessing
The postprocessing stage focuses on refining the initial 3D reconstruction results through
integration with additional navigation and sensor data, as well as enhancing the spatial
Page 40 of 69
==PAGE:47==
accuracy and usability of the outputs. In underwater surveying scenarios, this is particu-
larly important due to the challenging imaging conditions and the absence of GPS-based
positioning.
Photogrammetric reconstructions alone typically result in models that lack proper orienta-
tion and georeferencing, making them unsuitable for quantitative spatial analysis. Therefore,
this stage addresses these limitations by aligning the model to a global reference frame and
integrating external navigation data. In addition, the postprocessing includes all necessary
operations for generating orthophotos and terrain models directly within Zephyr.
This section presents the methods and tools used to improve the spatial consistency and
georeferencing of the reconstructed models by fusing navigation data from the ROV system,
including IMU, USBL positioning, and depth sensors with the outputs from the photogram-
metric reconstruction.
Model Orientation Refinement
Photogrammetric reconstruction of underwater scenes often results in models with incorrect
orientation relative to the global reference frame. This is especially common when cameras
are not pointing directly downward, but rather at an oblique angle as is the case in this
ROV-based survey. As a result, the reconstructed point cloud or mesh may appear tilted or
vertically stretched, misrepresenting the true layout of the seafloor.
Figure 25 left shows an example of this issue, where the reconstructed model appears to be
suspended vertically rather than aligned with the horizontal seafloor plane. This is likely due
to the camera setup, which captured the scene at approximately a 45-degree angle rather
than perpendicularly to the ground.
To correct this, the model was rotated approximately 40 degrees around the Y-axis within
Zephyr using the orientation tools, effectively "laying" the model flat on the XY plane to
match the expected seafloor orientation. Figure 25 illustrates the configuration interface and
the resulting correctly aligned model.

Georeferencing with Navigation Data
The sensor fusion phase plays a critical role in transforming raw positioning data into georef-
erenced visual outputs. The primary data source used for localization is the USBL system,
which provides spatiotemporal positioning of the ROV during underwater missions. This
data is initially recorded in a proprietary .log format, containing a mix of valid positioning
signals from the ROV and background signals from the ship’s onboard transceiver.
To extract usable information, a dedicated Python script processes the .log files and fil-
ters out invalid entries. Specifically, entries with a ROV flag value different from 4, which
indicates an active localization fix, are discarded. This ensures that only reliable latitude,
longitude, and depth values from the ROV are retained. The filtered data is then converted
into a .csv file containing timestamped and chronologically sorted positioning data.
Additionally, the script allows the user to extract subsets of the positioning data by specifying
custom time intervals. This is particularly useful to precisely align navigation data with
segments of video footage, enabling more accurate spatial referencing of the reconstructed
models.
Two alternative workflows were followed for integrating this geospatial information with the
3D reconstruction results:
1. Direct Georeferencing via Exchangeable Image File Format (EXIF) Meta-
data and Zephyr Constraints
To enable geospatial alignment of image-based reconstructions, this project employs
direct georeferencing using EXIF metadata. A Python-based pipeline is used to extract
timestamps from video frames and interpolate positional data (latitude, longitude,
depth) using synchronized USBL sensor logs. These coordinates are then embedded
into the image headers using the exiftool utility.
Once imported into Zephyr, the geotagged images can be used to constrain and orient
the reconstruction within a global coordinate system (e.g., WGS84). Only one camera
(typically the primary in a multi-camera rig) needs to be georeferenced, as the rigid
configuration is already calibrated.
A detailed walkthrough of this workflow is provided in Appendix D.
2. External Georeferencing via Transformation JSON for Cesium

Tiles-compatible tileset.json file that defines a global transformation matrix.
This matrix includes translation values that relocate the local model to its correct
geospatial location in Earth-Centered, Earth-Fixed (ECEF) coordinates. The structure

below:
"transform": [
1.0, 0.0, 0.0, 0.0,

Orthophoto Generation
Orthophotos and DEM were generated in Zephyr using the True Orthophoto from Mesh

The following subsections assess the quality and efficiency of the reconstructions generated
across different camera setups.
Visual inspection and correct scaling
Before delving into the quantitative evaluation, a preliminary visual inspection was per-
formed on all reconstructions to identify major anomalies (see Figure 38). This step re-
vealed that the reconstruction obtained using only a single camera view consistently failed
to produce a coherent model, highlighting the limitations of minimal viewpoint coverage.
Unlike the more structured and realistic meshes resulting from multi-camera configurations,
where there was little to no noticeable difference between the reconstructions, the one-camera
model lacked spatial depth and geometric consistency. Instead of a proper 3D shape, the
output often resembled a distorted or flattened surface, with irregular deformations and se-
vere noise artifacts. This is largely due to the absence of parallax and triangulation data,
which are essential for inferring spatial structure in photogrammetric reconstruction. Addi-
tionally, the single-camera setup proved to be highly susceptible to temporal inconsistencies:
if the camera paused, remained stationary over a region, or experienced vertical fluctuations
(e.g., slight up-and-down movements due to current or operator control), the reconstruction
exhibited repeated structures, blurred depth estimation, or disjointed patches. These effects
rendered the model unreliable and unsuitable for further analysis.

accuracy of the reconstruction.

6.1
EBV Extraction from Generated Outputs
Both 3D reconstructions and 2D orthophotos serve as valuable tools for extracting EBV, but
they offer different strengths depending on the application.
From 3D Models
3D models provide spatially rich representations of the benthic environment, preserving
depth, structure, and surface complexity. This allows for detailed quantification of organism
morphology (e.g., starfish diameter and height), surface area coverage, habitat volume, and
vertical profile. The ability to rotate, zoom, and view from arbitrary angles supports full
inspection of submerged structures and occluded areas, critical in complex habitats like
wrecks or rocky seafloors.
As summarized in Tables 5, the 3D reconstructions enabled the extraction of several key
EBVs, particularly those related to morphology, species distribution, and ecosystem struc-
ture. The volumetric and vertical data inherent to 3D models were essential for capturing
habitat complexity, which would be difficult or impossible to derive from 2D imagery alone.
From 2D Data
2D orthophotos provide a planar view of the seafloor, ideal for large-scale mapping, area-
based quantification, and habitat classification. Because they are lighter in file size and
more straightforward to process, orthophotos are well suited for tasks requiring coverage of
large areas or integration with satellite data and other remote sensing layers. Additionally,
computer vision algorithms can still be applied to detect and count visible features like
starfish or algae patches across wide spatial extents.
As shown in Tables 5, the EBVs extracted from orthophotos mostly pertain to horizontal
spatial distribution and abundance. While vertical structure and morphology could not be
captured, the 2D data provided useful insights into surface coverage, community distribution,
and macro-scale patterns, particularly in areas like Skovshoved A, where dense algal cover
was visible from above.
3D vs 2D
3D models offer distinct advantages over 2D orthophotos, particularly in tasks that require
structural disambiguation or volumetric analysis. The additional depth dimension allows for
better differentiation of features that may appear similar in texture or color from a top-down
perspective, for instance distinguishing mussels from rocks based on vertical profile, or iden-
tifying cylindrical worm casts in flat sandy terrain. Furthermore, 3D reconstructions enable
flexible visualization from arbitrary viewpoints, which is critical for examining features that
may be partially or fully occluded in a single overhead image. This includes organisms located
beneath overhangs, attached to vertical structures, or hidden behind relief features—elements
common in heterogeneous environments like shipwrecks or rocky outcrops, where geometry
Page 62 of 69
==PAGE:69==
and biodiversity are closely intertwined (see Figures 46 and 47). Such capabilities enhance
both manual interpretation and automated classification workflows, especially when inte-
grated with species-specific ML pipelines.

top-down view of the seafloor. While they lack structural depth, they are ideal for map-
ping large areas, estimating surface coverage, and running detection algorithms on plan-view

6.3
Limitations
Despite the successful generation of detailed and metrically accurate 3D reconstructions us-
ing multi-camera photogrammetry, several challenges and limitations persist, especially in
underwater environments where visibility and light behavior differ significantly from terres-
trial scenarios.
Blurriness in 3D models
One of the primary limitations in underwater 3D reconstruction arises from the optical
properties of the marine medium. Light scattering due to suspended particles, turbidity,
and the greenish tint of water significantly degrade image quality and contrast.
These
Page 64 of 69
==PAGE:71==
effects lead to reduced texture fidelity and detail loss in the periphery of the reconstructed
model, particularly at greater distances from the camera where backscatter becomes more
dominant.
Fig. 49. Blurriness in peripheral 3D model regions.
These effects also introduce difficulties in feature matching during SfM, which can lead to
lower point density and inaccurate geometry in affected areas.
Computational Load
Although reconstruction quality improves with increasing numbers of camera views, this
benefit comes at a computational cost.
As seen in Table 7, configurations with four or
more cameras can require over 1 hour for a single reconstruction when including dense point
cloud generation and meshing. For applications requiring near real-time feedback or rapid
processing of large datasets, this high computation time can be a limiting factor, particularly
when processing must be performed onboard or in resource-constrained environments.
Orientation and IMU Integration
One challenge encountered in the 3D modeling process is the integration of navigation or
orientation data, such as IMU outputs. While Zephyr supports georeferencing and manual
constraint import, there is currently no built-in mechanism to directly ingest IMU data to
automatically orient the point cloud based on recorded pose information. This limitation
may affect large-scale reconstructions where consistent alignment across scenes is critical.
Moreover, the onboard cameras on BlueROV2 are not typically oriented perpendicularly
(i.e., at 0° pitch) toward the seafloor; instead, they are tilted at a shallow oblique angle
(e.g., 30–45°). This orientation, while beneficial for capturing wide coverage, can introduce
ambiguity in identifying the horizontal plane of the seafloor during reconstruction. As a re-
sult, the reconstruction software may incorrectly interpret depth as height, leading to models
Page 65 of 69
==PAGE:72==
that appear elevated or angled incorrectly with respect to gravity. This misinterpretation
further complicates automated alignment and reinforces the need for additional orientation
correction steps during postprocessing, as illustrated in Figure 50.
Fig. 50. Incorrect initial orientation of the reconstructed 3D model due to camera angle.

Conclusion
This thesis has demonstrated the feasibility and advantages of using an integrated pho-
togrammetric workflow that combines multi-camera imaging systems with navigation sensors
to produce high-resolution, georeferenced 3D reconstructions of shallow-water benthic habi-
tats. The proposed approach leverages synchronized camera arrays mounted on a low-cost
ROV, along with USBL positioning and depth sensors, to achieve sub-millimetric spatial
resolution and metric accuracy in complex underwater environments.
Firstly, the thesis includes a critical review of SOTA photogrammetry software and geospa-
tial visualization tools, comparing their features, limitations, and suitability for marine eco-
logical applications. Through this, it contributes not only a reproducible and functional
methodology, but also practical guidance for future deployments in similar domains.
A key outcome of the study was the extensive evaluation of different camera configurations,
from one to six, which revealed that a four-camera setup (Configuration A) provides an
optimal balance between reconstruction completeness, noise suppression, and computational
efficiency. This finding is particularly valuable for researchers and practitioners seeking prac-
tical and scalable configurations for ecological surveys without overburdening data processing
workflows.
Beyond the technical aspects of reconstruction, the thesis also demonstrated how the gener-
ated 3D models support ecological applications through the extraction of EBVs. By export-
ing outputs in standardized formats, such as 3D Tiles for CesiumJS and orthophotos for GIS
environments, the data products could be directly integrated into spatial analysis workflows.
Biological metrics such as starfish diameter, anemone density, and habitat surface complexity
were successfully measured using tools like Hydrophis, underscoring the ecological relevance
of the reconstructions.
One of the central conclusions of this work is that 3D reconstructions offer substantial ad-
vantages over traditional 2D imagery for biodiversity monitoring: they improve classification
accuracy, reveal occluded features, and provide richer descriptors of spatial structure. This
was supported by the comparative analysis shown in Table 5, where 3D models outperformed
2D orthophotos in variables such as morphology, taxonomic diversity, and ecosystem vertical
profile, thanks to their ability to represent true depth and geometric complexity.
Beyond biodiversity monitoring, the 3D reconstructions proved useful for detecting and
classifying marine litter.
In environments such as Skovshoved Havn, anthropogenic de-
bris—including bottles, cans, and plastic waste—was clearly identifiable in the textured
models (Figure 51). Unlike 2D orthophotos, which may miss subtle or partially buried ob-
jects, 3D models preserve volumetric cues that aid in distinguishing debris from natural
benthic features. This capability opens up new avenues for applying the same reconstruc-
tion workflow to marine pollution monitoring. With appropriate annotation or training, ML
models could be used to automatically detect and track litter across large areas, providing
valuable data for coastal management and cleanup strategies.
Despite the successful generation of detailed and metrically accurate 3D reconstructions,
several limitations were encountered. Underwater imaging presents unique challenges due
to turbidity, light scattering, and reduced contrast, which result in blurriness and texture
Page 68 of 69
==PAGE:75==
degradation, particularly at the image periphery (see Figure 49). These optical effects reduce
the robustness of feature matching during SfM and lead to gaps or geometric artifacts in the
final model. Additionally, increasing the number of camera views, while improving recon-
struction quality, introduces higher computational load, with some configurations requiring
over one hour per model. Orientation integration also remains limited: IMU data cannot yet
be directly incorporated into the reconstruction process in Zephyr, and the oblique camera
angles used on the BlueROV2 platform can result in misaligned models that require manual
correction (Figure 50).
Importantly, the entire workflow was developed using low-cost, open-source or education-
ally licensed components—including BlueROV2, market cameras, and accessible educational
software, demonstrating that high-quality underwater 3D reconstructions can be achieved
without reliance on expensive commercial systems. This makes the proposed approach par-
ticularly well suited for small research groups, startups, and citizen science initiatives focused
on marine biodiversity monitoring and habitat mapping.
Future Work
• Machine Learning for EBV Detection: Can machine learning models trained on
visual features reliably extract EBVs from 3D data and improve 2D data in terms of
accuracy and reduced misclassification?
• Multimodal Sensor Fusion: How can optical and acoustic datasets be integrated
to compensate for visibility limitations and enhance coverage? Future studies should
explore hybrid reconstruction frameworks that combine photogrammetry and sonar to
extend applicability into turbid or low-light zones.
• Photogrammetric Optimization: Which combinations of advanced reconstruction
parameters yield the most accurate ecological reconstructions in underwater settings?
A systematic evaluation of tools like Zephyr can uncover parameter-tuning strategies
that balance resolution, noise, and computation time.
• IMU Integration: To what extent does integrating IMU data directly into the re-
construction process improve the geometric fidelity of 3D models? This invites inves-
tigation into more sensor fusion techniques that enhance pose estimation and reduce
reliance on post hoc alignment.
• Workflow Automation: Future efforts should focus on automating the entire pipeline
to support batch processing of large missions, from photogrammetric preprocessing to
final output generation, ultimately enabling a fully streamlined and repeatable work-
flow.

Appendix I: Codebase Structure
The implementation developed during this thesis is structured as a modular pipeline for un-
derwater 3D reconstruction using multi-camera systems. The complete codebase is publicly
available at the following GitLab repository:
https://gitlab.gbar.dtu.dk/jalfe/3d-reconstruction-multi-cam
The workflow is divided into the following main stages:
• Calibration: Converts intrinsic calibration data from Kalibr format into XML files
compatible with Zephyr for multi-camera setups.
• Preprocessing: Extracts images from videos based on a configurable frame skip.
Basic image filters are applied and frames are grouped by camera for Zephyr input.
• Postprocessing:
– EXIF metadata embedding: Synchronizes image frames with USBL positioning
data and embeds geolocation into image metadata.
– External georeferencing: Assigns geographic coordinates to the reconstructed 3D
model (.glb format) using known reference points for visualization in tools like
Cesium.
The repository follows this folder structure:
calibration/
calib_transform.py
preprocessing/
preprocessing.py
postprocessing/
exif_metadata/
extract_ptsag.py
geotag_images.py
external_georeferencing/
generate_tileset_json.py
This codebase was designed to support flexible integration with optical navigation systems
and is optimized for use with Zephyr as the reconstruction engine.

Appendix III: 3DF Zephyr 3D Reconstruction
The subsequent subsections describe in detail each stage of the reconstruction pipeline and
the input data needed. For further details on each processing phase and advanced options,
the official Zephyr documentation is available online at [96].
New Project Wizard
To create a new reconstruction project, the user must select Workflow > New Project, which
launches the Project Wizard interface (see Figure 52). This wizard guides the user through
the image import process and the configuration of the camera orientation phase. It also
allows the selection of additional reconstruction steps through the "Computation steps"
section, where the corresponding icons (e.g., model, dense cloud, mesh) can be activated
depending on the desired output.
For this project, the computation steps sparse, model, and texture were selected. The or-
thophoto generation was performed afterward, once the camera orientation was further re-
fined to ensure accurate georeferencing and visual alignment.
Fig. 52. Zephyr Project Wizard interface.
Additionally, the Set up multicamera groups option was used to handle image sets captured
simultaneously from different perspectives or devices, ensuring proper alignment and group-
ing of camera pairs. As part of the workflow for creating a new project, constraints can also
be set by importing known 3D positions from external control points or geographic coordi-
nates associated with images. To enable this, the Define constraints check-box should be
activated on the first page of the New Project Wizard.

that suggests image groupings (e.g., subfolders per camera or viewpoint), Zephyr automati-
cally detects this and prompts the user with the message: "It appears that images are grouped
in folders. Would you like to link them?"
Clicking YES allows Zephyr to automatically create multi-camera groups based on the sub-

The Multi-camera groups setup (see Figure 54) allows users to define the spatial relationship
between cameras that captured images simultaneously. This step is essential when using
synchronized rigs or stereo configurations, as it constrains the relative positioning of images
and ensures accurate model scaling.
For this project, the Translation + Rotation relation type was selected. Only the translation
(baseline) was provided, since the angular difference between cameras was negligible. This
baseline reflects the physical configuration of the rig and ensures the model is correctly
scaled, as detailed in Table 1.

The first phase of 3D reconstruction in Zephyr is the Structure from Motion (SfM), which

Dense Point Cloud
After the sparse point cloud is generated, Zephyr proceeds to densify the reconstruction (see
Figure 57). Like the previous step, the dense point cloud generation can be configured using
predefined categories and presets, or through manual adjustment in the advanced settings.
Categories help Zephyr adapt its processing pipeline to the nature of the dataset (e.g.,
general, aerial, urban, surface scan), while presets control the quality and computational
load (e.g., preview, default, high detail). For this project, the Surface Scan category with
the Default preset was used, offering a good trade-off between detail and performance.
Advanced settings also allow for additional control, such as enabling or disabling noise fil-
tering, adjusting stereo matching parameters, or tuning depth map generation to better suit
the dataset.

and texturing. Both phases can be configured using categories and presets, or by switching
to advanced mode for greater customization.
For mesh generation, categories help adapt the algorithm to different types of datasets
(e.g., General, Urban, Surface Scan, Vertical Structure, or Laser Scan). Presets range from

Appendix IV: 3DF Zephyr Direct Georeferencing via
EXIF Metadata
After preparing the CSV file, a second Python script is used to geotag the extracted im-
age frames from the video sequence. Images are named according to frame number (e.g.,
cam0_img_90.png), and their timestamps are computed using the known frame rate and the
acquisition time of the first frame. Using linear interpolation between USBL data points,
each frame is assigned latitude, longitude, and depth values.
These values are embedded into the image headers using the EXIF standard via the exiftool
utility. The result is a set of georeferenced images stored in a new directory.
Once imported into Zephyr, the geotagged images are processed using the Import Constraints
option available in the first page of the Project Wizard. By enabling the Define constraints
checkbox and selecting Load GPS positions from EXIF, Zephyr automatically extracts posi-
tional data—latitude, longitude, and altitude (depth)—and uses it to initialize the camera
positions within a global coordinate system. This process is illustrated in Figure 59.
(a) Project Wizard: enabling constraint definition.
(b) Loading GPS data from EXIF metadata.
Fig. 59. Importing GPS-based constraints from EXIF metadata in Zephyr.
In this case, only a single georeferenced image (typically from the main camera group, e.g.,
cam0) is used to provide the constraint, since the rigid baseline between the multicamera
setup is known and has already been calibrated.
This ensures that the relative spatial
arrangement of all cameras is preserved while allowing global positioning from just one
frame.
Within Zephyr, the selected geotagged point is marked as both a Constraint (CS) and a
Checkpoint (CK) (see Figure 59). In the control point table, each point can be toggled as:
• CS: Used actively in the bundle adjustment to influence model orientation and scaling.
These are points with high confidence.
• CK: Used to monitor reconstruction accuracy by computing residual errors, but not
used directly for optimization.
Page XVII
==PAGE:93==
Using both roles helps Zephyr refine the global alignment while allowing error metrics to be
tracked during reconstruction. This setup results in a sparse point cloud and complete 3D
reconstruction that is already aligned in real-world Cartesian coordinates (typically WGS84
or projected systems), facilitating direct export to formats compatible with Cesium, GIS
software, and orthophoto generation in GeoTIFF format.
As shown in Figure 60, the model is already correctly positioned on the map before the
reconstruction process is executed. In this case, the image corresponds to the Skovshoved
Havn Location A site, and the base map preview within Zephyr confirms the geographic
alignment of the input data.
Fig. 60. Skovshoved Havn Location A: 3D model positioned on the map before running reconstruction.

Appendix V: 3DF Zephyr Orthophoto Generation
Orthophotos and related geospatial products were generated using the True Orthophoto
from Mesh feature in Zephyr, after direct georeferencing via EXIF Metadata and Zephyr
Constraints. This tool allows for the creation and export of orthophotos, DSM, and DTM,
which can later be used in GIS platforms for ecological and spatial analysis, such as area
coverage or habitat fragmentation. The process involves three main steps:
1. Source and Orientation Setup
In the first wizard page, the orthophoto’s spatial extent and orientation are configured.
We selected the textured mesh as the reference object, applied a −90◦rotation around
the Z-axis to correct the model orientation, and adjusted the bounding box size to fit
the region of interest precisely.
Fig. 61. Bounding box and orientation setup in Zephyr.
2. Resolution Settings
In this step, the GSD and tile resolution are configured. The default resolution settings
were used, but various options are available to customize the output. Users can manu-
ally define the orthophoto width and height or set a desired GSD and allow Zephyr to
adjust the image size automatically. The tile grid settings can be modified to optimize
performance on lower-spec computers or for very large orthophotos. Additionally, im-
age blending methods such as Mean and Multiband can be selected to balance between
color consistency and sharpness.

==PAGE:95==
Fig. 62. Resolution and blending settings for orthophoto generation.
3. Generation and Export
After confirming the export settings in the final wizard step (e.g., enabling DSM or
DTM generation if required), the orthophoto generation is initiated.

output.
(b) DEM viewer showing elevation-based visualization.

ing surface with elevation-based color gradients (heatmap). From the DEM Viewer, the
elevation-colored image can be exported.
Finally, the Export Orthophoto menu was used to save the orthophoto in GeoTIFF export
option to enable integration with geographic viewers such as QGIS.
