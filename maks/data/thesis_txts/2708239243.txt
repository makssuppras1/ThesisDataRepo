Abstract
In cold climates, the increase of insulation and airtightness in residential buildings com­
bined with high solar gains, lead to overheating issues and occupant discomfort during
warm periods. The high indoor air temperatures have been proven to have a highly neg­
ative effect on occupants’ health and wellbeing.
This study investigates the risk of overheating on two different types of building construc­
tions; wooden row houses with decentralized mechanical ventilation with heat recovery
and concrete apartment blocks with centralized mechanical ventilation with heat recovery.
The buildings are single family dwellings located in Akia area in Sisimiut, Greenland. The
investigation includes the collection of original documentations, monitoring of indoor air
temperature and relative humidity, visual inspection, questionnaires and interviews with
the occupants. The indoor climate has been recorded for a period of 20 days, from 8th
to 28th of July 2021 and dynamic simulations have been carried out in IDA ICE software
for the same period. Different measures have been proposed to mitigate the overheating
hours and minimizing thermal discomfort. The measures include increased ventilation
rates, the use of bypass in the heat exchanger, as well as natural ventilation, changing
of the thermal mass and improved energy­efficient glazing and shading systems. Simu­
lation models were analyzed for each type of building construction and then a sensitivity
analysis was carried out.
Both measurements and simulations, showed a clear evidence of overheating. The sim­
ulation results indicate that when each measure is applied alone can not fully address
the overheating issues. A combination of various measures is needed to efficiently re­
duce overheating below the temperature limits of 24 ◦C for bedrooms and 25 ◦C for living
rooms in all tested cases. In wooden row houses, where decentralized mechanical venti­
lation with heat recovery is used, the overheating requirements can be achieved without
increasing the total ventilation rate, if both by­pass in the heat recovery system and pas­
sive measures are properly applied. On the other hand, in concrete apartment blocks,
where centralized mechanical ventilation with heat recovery is used, overheating can not
be avoided without combining both increased total ventilation rate and additional passive
solutions.
This study concluded that in order to address the overheating issues effectively in the
Arctic climate, various measures need to be explored in the design phase.

1
Introduction
In a changing climate, with temperatures increasing year by year, buildings are becoming
more and more exposed to experience indoor overheating during the warm periods [1].
Arctic is a part of the world where the average annual surface temperature is rising rapidly
at least twice compared to the rest of the world [2].
In regions with cold climate, indoor overheating has not been an issue in the past, or
has been simply ignored in the building regulations due to the cold outside air and the
high heat loss in older and leakier buildings [3]. However, the building industry in order
to address the requirements for energy efficiency paid more effort on how to minimise
the heat loss and subsequently the energy consumption for heating by increasing the
insulation and the air­tightness of the buildings. All these practices combined with high
heat solar gains during the summer have laid the foundation for the current overheating
problem. What is more, air­conditioning is a very uncommon solution in cold climate
regions. So far, very few things are known regarding the association of overheating risk
and low energy buildings in cold climates. Therefore, the risk of overheating and indoor
comfort becomes increasingly important as buildings are constructed or renovated to be
more energy efficient [4].
Overheating is the result of excessive heat accumulation in the buildings due to the heat
from outdoors combined with the generated internal heat gains. It has been proven that
elevated indoor temperature have a highly negative effect on occupants’ health, produc­
tivity and wellbeing [5]. Usually, the easiest way to address overheating is by installing
mechanical cooling in the buildings. However, this solution leads to additional running
costs, energy use, and subsequently increases in carbon emissions if the energy comes
from non­renewable energy sources, such as coal, petroleum, and natural gas.
The aim of this master thesis is first to address the factors that contribute to overheating
risk in Greenlandic buildings, then to determine how it could be monitored/assessed and
finally, to investigate and optimise the suitability of the selected mitigation measures on
reducing overheating risk. The investigated measures are implemented in two types of
building construction; wooden row houses with decentralized MVHR1 and concrete apart­
ment blocks with centralized MVHR.
This evaluation is based on real measurements on single­family dwellings located in Akia
neighbourhood at Sisimiut in Greenland. The measurements have been carried out for a
20 day period in July 2021 and simulations based on the same dwellings have also been
performed for the same tife frame to investigate the risk of overheating.

1.2
Buildings in Greenland
The building type in Greenland has changed through the years. First, buildings were build
as timber constructions while lately concrete constructions have entered the business.
According to a study [8], buildings in Greenland are made tight and with thick insulation
layers but most of the times, ventilation systems are inefficient and leads to poor indoor
climate, such as high air temperatures and mold growth.
Building materials
In a statement from Naalakkersuisut from 2019 [9], it is explained that almost all construc­
tion materials are imported by ship or plane, and production has been limited because
there has not been sufficient demand to keep it running. In addition, only a few Danish
companies are willing to ship to Greenland and therefore the Greenlandic market has low
availability of products [10]. Moreover, in many settlements it is challenging to introduce
and implement new products, such as window panes with special coating due to the fact
that the residents do not have the knowledge to replace them in case they are broken.
As has been described in ¨Building Material in Arctic Climate¨ by Jensen, O.M.(2005)

[7] each material under Arctic conditions has its own advantages and disadvantages.
According to that:
Concrete is considered the best material for larger buildings in Greenland since it is a
durable and fireproof product. Moreover, it is well suited in Arctic climate, due to the
fact that stone ,gravel and water can be extracted from the ground. However cement,
reinforcement and other additives must be imported.
On the other hand, wood is considered as the classical arctic building product. The main
advantage of wood compared to other building materials is that wooden constructions
can be built independent of the temperature. In addition, wood is very light compared
to its weight and provide a reasonable insulation. However, according to a study [8] on
current building strategies in Greenland , timber frame constructions must be substituted
to address the big problem of mold growth.
When it comes to brick and mortar, use in arctic climates is quite problematic. Both the
construction and the subsequent hardening phase requires environments with tempera­
tures above 5 ◦C and in addition, the brickwork on the facades does not contribute to the
insulation of the building.
Moisture
The amount of water vapour that can be held by air is highly depended on the tempera­
ture. In Greenland outdoor temperatures are very low (Figure 1.3), therefore even though
the relative humidity is high, the air is unable to hold much moisture (often less than 1
g/kgdry,air [11]), and as a result the climate is very dry. However, a study [12] has shown
that poor ventilation of many Greenlandic dwellings combining with indoor moisture gen­
erating activities, such as long time boiling of meat or drying of clothes indoors, can lead
to unacceptably high indoor humidity levels. Moreover, according to the same study, rela­
tive humidity higher than 80% in the air, on building surfaces, or withing building materials
and structure can be harmful to the indoor climate or can cause decay of materials.
CIBSE2 Guide [13] states that moisture does not affect a person’s feeling of warmth unless
their skin is damp with sweat. In terms of health, excessive moisture in the air may make
the indoor environment humid and uncomfortable, especially if it is hot. Conversely, if
there is very little moisture in the air, then the eyes, throat and skin may feel uncomfortably
dry and static electricity can build up.
The most important effect of moisture is related with risk of mold growth and increase of
concentration of house­dust mites (HDM). In details, mould growth is the most common
moisture related problem and normally requires a combination of relative humidity more
than 75­80% and temperature above 5 ◦C to grow. However, theses conditions are very
unlikely to present in ambient temperature or within buildings in Greenland [12].
In terms of HDM3, relative humidity 45% or more is required for house dust mites to thrive
[12]. In Sundell’s [14] study is stated that elevated concentrations of HDM, particularly
in bedrooms is correlated with the difference in absolute humidity between indoor and
outdoor air. This difference is also defined as additional moisture or indoor vapour con­
tribution . The same study found that homes with low­infestation of HDM had average
additional moisture of 1.9 g/m3 (1.6 g/kg) while homes with high­infestation of HDM had
average additional moisture of 2.2 g/m3 (1.8 g/kg). In addition, in Emenius [15] study is
stated that homes with additional moisture higher than 43 g/m3 have a 18% to 45% risk
of high humidity and HDM concentrations.

1.4
Thermal performance of buildings
Thermal performance of a building is determined by its efficiency to retain or prevent the
passage of heat and can be measured by a variation of metrics such as, conductivity, air
tightness , SHGC (solar heat gain coefficient) of windows, U values etc. However, none of
these performance indicators represent the overall thermal performance of the building.
In this study, thermal performance will be assessed with focus on overheating.
1.4.1
Thermal comfort
According to Fanger (1970) [26] ‘Thermal comfort’ has defined as ”the combined effects
of six fundamental factors: temperature, thermal radiation, relative humidity, air relative
velocity, clothing insulation and metabolic rate” . In DS/EN 17798­2:2019 [27] is stated
that thermal comfort criteria shall be the base for designing and dimensioning of room
condition systems.
Metabolic heat production
Metabolic heat production is depended on the activities of the occupants. For exam­
ple, the metabolic rate of a relaxed,seated person is set to 58W/m2 or 1.0 met. Typical
metabolic rates for various activities are given in DS/EN ISO 8996:2021 [28].
Clothing level
Clothing level refers to the amount of clothes people wear and depends on the season,
the weather and the thermal indoor environment. Clothing level is measured by clothing’s
thermal insulation value (clo). In winter people wear thicker clothes with more layers
and the clo value varies from 0.8 to 1.0 clo, while in summer people usually wear light­
weight clothes with clothing insulation values ranging from 0.35 to 0.6 clo. In DS/EN
ISO 8996:2021 [29] are given clothing insulation values (clo) for typical combinations of
clothing.
Temperature
Temperature is characterised as the primary factor affecting thermal comfort. Operative
temperature represents a simply way to measure the likely thermal comfort of the occu­
pants of a building. The value of operative temperature is derived from air temperature,
mean radiant temperature and air speed.
If the air speed is less than 0.1 m/s, (as is typical in buildings) then operative temperature
can be estimated as the mean value of air temperature and mead radiant temperature
[30].
1.5
Overheating
1.5.1
Definition of overheating
As described above, there are a variety of factors that affect thermal comfort , but over­
heating is specifically associated with high temperatures. In other words, overheating
occurs when the indoor temperature rises to a level that the individual finds unacceptably
uncomfortable [31].
According to NHBC Foundation [5] ¨Overheating is generally understood to be the ac­
cumulation of warmth within a building to an extent where it causes discomfort to the
occupants.¨
However, the sense of ideal temperature varies from person to person and therefore there
is no clear definition above what temperature the room is considered to be overheated.
In addition, occupied hours are difficult to be determined. Therefore, there is no robust,
universally accepted agreement on the specific amount of hours leading to overheating
[4],[1]. As a result, different criteria are used to determine whether overheating has oc­
curred.

Studies have shown that the temperatures where the occupants feel acceptably comfort­
able differs from one location to another [1]. However , many regulations have been set
to estimate a range where a great majority of people would feel comfortable to stay within.
Particularly in the cold climates the amount of research regarding to overheating is signif­
icantly less compared with other European countries, such as England. In Martin’s Kotol
PhD thesis [11] about indoor air quality and behaviour of occupants in existing dwellings
in Greenland is stated that 40% of the respondents reported frequent problems of over­
heating in summer.
1.5.2
Effects on human health
As defined by the constitution of the World Health Organisation (WHO) ¨health is a state
of complete physical, mental and social well­being, not merely the absence of disease
and infirmity¨ [32].
Throughout the comfort section of the CIBSE Environmental Design Guide A (CIBSE
2015) [13] is reported that excessive heat reduces the performance of mental tasks. How­
ever, it is also stated that the clothing that is worn affects more the thermal performance
rather than the thermal environment itself. Therefore, work performance is not only related
to the indoor temperatures.
Excessive warm temperatures for long period, or even for a few days in a row can lead
to serious health problems. According to studies, most common symptoms of overheat­
ing are irritability, fatigue, tiredness, headaches, reduced productivity and well­being. In
extreme cases, heat stress may lead to premature mortality, especially in the most vul­
nerable members of society [1]. Most people are not aware that the impact of heat on
mortality is so high. Most of the health burden from high temperatures occur because
they exacerbate existing heart or lung­related disease, rather than from conditions such
as heatstroke [33].
1.5.3
Selection of threshold values
In order to determine whether a building can be classed as overheating or not, different
approaches and methodologies have been studied. Comfort criteria for dwellings varies
depends on the period and the usage of the room. Moreover, CIBSE 4 Guide [34] states
that an overheating analysis should consider both frequency and length of time that high
temperatures occur in a building .
European Standards for indoor environmental parameters [35] specifies that each building
category has different acceptable temperature ranges. Recommended operative temper­
ature requirements vary for the intended use of the building. Four different categories are
defined (Figure 1.6). A normal residential building belongs to category II and according to
the table B.4 from chapter B.2.4 of DS/EN 16798 [35] it is recommended to remain below
the upper limits of 25◦C for living rooms and bedrooms. In addition, according to CISBE
[34] the indoor temperature for buildings without mechanical cooling should not exceed
25◦C for living rooms and 24◦C for bedrooms.

1.5.4
Why do buildings overheat?
Overheating can occur in any building, having mechanical cooling or not. It can either be
caused by a single predominant factor or by a number of issues that are compounded.
Excessive heat gains from both internal and external sources along with undersized or
ineffective ventilation strategies, as well as inadequate shading to dispel excessive solar
Thermal performance investigation and optimization of dwellings in Greenland with focus on overheating 9
==PAGE:26==
heating are usually the reasons. Moreover, both location and orientation of the building,
as well as building construction can contribute to heat accumulation.
External gains
Heat gains from the sun and high external temperatures are generally the external con­
ditions that contribute to overheating in the building. However, in Arctic the outside tem­
peratures are almost always cooler than inside and therefore heat gains from the sun are
considered as primary factor.
Solar radiation can be divided into three types, direct, diffuse and reflected radiation (Fig­
ure 1.8). Direct radiation, is the radiation that hits the given object direct from the Sun,
while diffuse radiation is radiation scattered by the atmosphere. When beam or diffuse
radiation hits the surroundings of the surface then part of the beam or diffuse radiation are
reflected on to the surface. This type of radiation is called reflected radiation and depends
on the surface. In practice, it varies from 0.2 for grass to 0.9 for snow [3].
Figure 1.8: Sketch of the three types of radiation that make up total radiation [3]
When the light from the sun (direct solar radiation) falling on external surfaces such as,
windows, walls, roofs etc. then solar gains passes through the surface into the building
and are absorbed as heat by surfaces and objects within the space. In case of windows,
the solar radiation enters immediately into the room and warm the indoor air quiet quickly.
On the other hand, when solar radiation falling on walls or roof, it takes longer time to
get into the room. In buildings with high insulated walls and energy efficient windows this
unwanted heat is blocked into the building.
Internal gains
Different internal sources can generate unwanted heat in the summer. Internal gains by
the activity of occupants as metabolic heat is the most common source. Moreover, ther­
mal emission of artificial lighting and utilization of appliances such as, fridge, dish­wash,
television, laptop or chargers emit heat withing the zone as well. Last, building services
such as hot water distribution and storage systems are usually generate unwanted heat.

Thermal Mass
Thermal mass refers to the ability of building materials to absorb and store heat from the
air within the space. That heat comes from internal or external gains as described before
1.5.4. The efficiency of thermal mass is highly depended on the climate. Particularly the
high thermal mass is more beneficial in locations with warm /hot climates where temper­
ature varies between day and night, on the other hand in locations with higher latitudes,

==PAGE:27==
where there is not much diurnal variation the benefit becomes relatively smaller.
Thermal mass is a common way for limiting summertime overheating, however, high ther­
mal mass without adequate ventilation it may lead to overheating risk [5]. A study [37] on
thermal mass in UK dwellings concludes that high thermal mass construction is shown to
have a large effect on summer peak temperatures and has a consistent beneficial influ­
ence compared to low thermal mass construction.
During warm periods thermal mass can be used to avoid overheating risk and keep the
home cool. As can be seen in the Figure 1.9, if the solar gains are blocked from reaching
the mass (for example, with overhang or other type of shading), then the mass will instead
absorb heat from inside the home.
Heavyweight building materials, such as concrete, brick or stone have a higher thermal
mass and absorb more heat than lighter timber­based materials. Therefore, they have
the ability to absorb and store heat during the day and release it when the temperature is
cooler, usually at night.
In cold climates, such as Greenland, thermal mass can maintain indoor air temperatures
in a comfortable range, but on the other hand, due to the highly insulated and airtight
buildings the accumulative heat cannot be removed and it can contribute to uncomfortably
high indoor air temperatures usually during the night.

partition walls.

Methodology
The following sections present the methodology that has been followed to investigate and
optimise the thermal performance of dwellings in cold climates with focus on overheating.
Two different types of building construction have been studied, both of them are located
in Akia , a neighborhood found 2 km North of the town of Sisimiut in Greenland. From
now on , the test dwellings will be referred according to their street address.
The first type of dwelling is wooden row houses (Figure 2.2a) located in Kimmik and
Uerneq street. While the second one is concrete apartment blocks (Figure 2.2b) in Na­
pariaq street. The figure below 2.1 highlights the buildings in which measurements have
been carried out, while a photograph of the analysed buildings are shown in the Figure 2.2.
Field measurements in dwellings have been carried out for a 20 days period in July 2021,
recording temperature, relative humidity and CO2 .
Figure 2.1: Map of Akia area with locations of buildings in which measurements have been carried out in the
period from 8th July 2021 to 28th July 2021
Overheating risk has been evaluated with both simulated and measured data. At first,
all the necessary data have been collected, analyzed and imported into the model sim­
ulations. Those data were collected though measurements, in person observations or
were provided from the construction companies. Despite that, some of the data were
also collected during the model construction when it was found to be necessary.
Then, a comparison analysis were performed between the outputs from the simulations
and the measured data to check the plausibility of the models. Once the models were
checked, different measures were investigated to mitigate overheating risk.
As has been already mentioned in the introduction, there is no robust, and universally
accepted thresholds to define overheating in dwellings. Therefore, for the evaluation of
overheating risk in this thesis have been selected threshold values of 24◦C for bedrooms
and 25◦C for living rooms.

Diffuse and direct solar radiation data is needed to conduct simulations in IDA ICE, how­

Indoor environment analysis have been performed by using IDA Indoor Climate and En­
ergy version 4.9 software, made by EQUA Simulation AB [48] and henceforth referred
to as IDA ICE. IDA ICE uses 3D dynamic multi­zone simulation where each zone has
different characteristics to calculate the energy use and the thermal indoor climate for
buildings.
The performance of the building is based on variables, parameters and equations. The
parameters are fixed values while the variables depends on the parameters. First of
all, geometrically creation of the model requires data such as, orientation of the building,
shading from surrounding objects and zone dimensions. Once the geometrical model has
been created the thermal properties of the construction material for the walls, roof, floor,
windows and doors should be set according to the tables describes in the section 2.5.2.
In the next step, set points for every zone such as, temperature, internal load, air­flows and
time schedules should be taken into account. Last but not least, weather files for outdoor
climate and geographical position (longitudinal and latitudinal direction) of the building are
required. The climate data consists of dry bulb temperature, Relative humidity of air, wind
direction, wind speed, direct solar radiation and diffuse radiation.
Even though most of the data were collected , some estimations and simplification have
also been performed due to software’s limitations and difficulties on data collection.

indoor environment by keeping the windows always closed. Considering this statement
the windows in the models are assumed to be always closed. In IDA ICE the openable
area of the windows is calculated as a percentage of the openable window total area and

2. Solutions which enhance heat rejection (removal)
3. Solutions based on occupant behaviour
4. Solutions which use cooling

By pass set point
One way to monitor the supply air temperature is through the by pass valve. If the tem­
perature of the extracted air is over the by pass set point temperature, then the valve
opens and fresh ambient air passes through the heat exchanger to the building. On the
other hand, whether the extracted air is under the point of the set point temperature the
valve is closed and the outside air is heated up through the heat recovery. In case the
minimum supply air cannot be reached through the heat recovery, then the electric heater
is switched on and operates until the set temperature is reached.
Simulations with different set points based on the data sheet of the existing air handling
unit have been carried out to investigate the optimal set points for Arctic conditions. It
is very important to mention that in Napariaq apartments with central air handling unit
controlling the temperature via by pass method is not possible solution since the return
air is extracted from all the apartments.

2.7.3
Measure B: Natural Ventilation
There are two common types of natural ventilation, infiltration and controlled natural ven­
tilation. Infiltration is uncontrolled movement of air through unintentional openings. This
movement occurs due to wind and pressure differences through the building envelope.
Controlled natural ventilation occurs when the air moves through particular openings such
as, windows and doors. This type of ventilation is usually controlled by the occupants.
Before we start modelling, it is very important to specify which parameters will be used
as constant and which ones as variables for the simulations. The airflow rate through
the window is a function of many parameters such as, wind incident angle, wind speed,
window opening angle, window geometry and opening time period. The scenario analysis
has been focused only on the window surface opening area and the duration of window
opening since those parameters are more reliable and easier to be controlled compared
to the others.
Window opening has an significant role to the indoor temperature of the building. First,
window opening increases the air change to the zone. The area of the opening determines
the amount of the airflow that can be gained into the zone through the opening. Different
window structures have different ratio of opening area. In both dwelling two types of
windows exist , ”casement” and ‘tilt and turn’ type (Figure 2.16). Moreover, each dwelling
has both open­able and fixed windows, the figure 2.15 indicates which windows are open­
able (blue triangle) and which windows are fixed (no symbol).

During the measurement period the occupants’ interactions with the windows (in the bed­

be assumed. Removing heat through opening the windows seems simple in theory, but
it is much harder to achieve in practice.
Opening of the window increases the airflow in the room and subsequently the air­change.
According to ASHRAE 2016[52] air changes depend on window area, orientation, and
maximum outdoor temperature. Rooms oriented to the south with large window areas
require about 9 air change per hour (ACH), while 6 ACH are required in rooms facing to
the north with small window areas. Very large window area and southern exposures may
require as many as 12 ACH.
However, in cold climates and subsequently in Greenlandic dwellings, even short period
of window opening has a significant impact on the indoor environment. Due to the cold
outside air opening of the window may decrease the indoor temperature rapidly, while the
strong winds can supply large amount of air in to the room and lead to undesirable draft
discomfort to the occupants.

2.7.4
Measure C: Reducing solar heat gains through shading
This Measure aims to minimize overheating hours by using different more efficient shading
systems. The Figure 2.17 shows the three most common types of shading in residential
buildings.
Figure 2.17: Three most common types of shading in residential buildings
External shading is the most effective way to reduce solar heat gains through windows, as
it prevents sunlight from reaching the window in the first place. Typical external shading
systems can be fixed overhangs or external balconies that control direct beam solar ra­
diation. Nevertheless, in the Arctic they will be less effective in preventing direct sunlight
from entering the house compared to other locations, such as countries in Mediterranean.
The reason for that is because of the the low angle of the sun during the summer period
(Figure 1.5b). As a result , overhang solution cannot be applied without reducing the ex­
ternal view and, to a lesser extent, daylight. Another solution for external shading is the
use of external blinds. However, Arctic harsh weather conditions such as strong winds
and snow loads make that solution impossible to be implemented. Therefore, none of the
external shading solutions has been further analyzed.
In contrast, internal shading is less effective in reducing solar gains since the solar en­
ergy has already passed through the window in the room but it can be easily retrofitted
and prevent the sun from reaching deep into the rooms. Most common types of internal
shading are Venetian blinds and drapes. The solar gains are absorbed by the shading
device and transmitted into the room.
Last, type of shading in which the blinds are integrated between the panes has also been
investigated. This type of shading restrict sunlight before it enters the house and provides
a better SHGC compared to other internal shading solutions.
Any type of shading provide additional insulation due to the air enclosed between the
window and the blind. The Table 2.11 shows the multipliers for SHGC and U­values used

Measure D: Glazing type
A window consists of glazing and frame. Both of them have a significant impact on the
performance of the window and subsequently on the indoor temperature of the zone.
Thermal transmittance (U­value) and solar heat gain coefficient (SHGC) are the main
parameters that determine this efficiency. Every climate has different needs for window
values.
In most cases, windows are selected to minimize the annually heating demand and sub­
sequently the energy consumption of the house by using well insulated windows with
low U­value and high SHGC. However, this selection will probably increase the risk of
overheating, especially in dwellings with limited use of natural ventilation like Greenland.
Therefore, an optimisation of the windows in terms of overheating risk should carefully
consider the amount of heat loss in winter as well.
In Kimmik dwellings the glazing has a low value for both thermal transmittance and SHGC.
On the other hand, in Napariaq apartments it has a higher U­value and SHGC 2.6. To
understand the effect of these values on indoor temperatures, four different simulation

frame fraction and light transmittance are fixed. The only variables are thermal transmit­
tance and SHGC. Scenario D.1 and D.2 have the standard glazing type of Kimmik row

Matrix analysis ­ Combination of improvement measures
In this section a combination of different scenarios has been performed. The previous

of occupant’s behaviour, such as mechanical ventilation and window type.

and is therefore avoided when possible.

windows for less than half an hour depending on the external conditions and usually they
prefer to open the windows from 25% to 50% of the total window area.

4
Conclusion
This study aimed to identify effective measures for mitigating overheating risk in dwellings
in cold regions such as Greenland and assessed their implication on the indoor air tem­
perature.
In­situ measurements of indoor air temperature and relative humidity were first carried out
for a 20 day period in July in Sisimiut, Greenland for two types of building construction;
wooden row houses with decentralized mechanical ventilation with heat recovery and
concrete apartment blocks with centralized mechanical ventilation with heat recovery.
Overheating can be affirmed by both measurements and discussions with the occupants.
Measurements give a clear evidence of overheating since indoor air temperature exceeds
the acceptable required range, 24 ◦C for bedroom and 25 ◦C for living rooms for more than
40% and 30% of the occupied hours. Moreover, according to the measurements, bed­
rooms with windows oriented to the north experience higher indoor air temperatures during
the occupied hours (21:00­07:00), since solar gains are observed late in the evening and
it is harder for the zone to remove this excess amount of heat before the occupants go to
sleep.
Elevated indoor air temperatures have been proven to have a highly negative effect on
occupants’ health and wellbeing. However, in comparison with other European countries,
the amount of research related to overheating in cold regions is significantly less and does
not receive the same attention. This thesis strives to fill this knowledge gap by introducing
several measures to mitigate overheating risk.
Based on dynamic simulations, it can be concluded that overheating requirements can be
achievable without mechanical cooling, if both passive and active measures are properly
applied.
However, a sensitivity analysis have shown that none of the investigated measures alone
can completely eliminate the risk of overheating and in all cases, a complementary mea­
sure is needed. According to the sensitivity analysis the single most effective solution to
address the risk of overheating in both dwelling types, is the efficient operation of ventila­
tion systems.
In apartments with central MVHR, when the required total airflow is doubled, the indoor
air temperatures are within the requirements for 45% of the occupied hours for bedrooms,
and less than 12% for living rooms. However, this solution will increase the fan energy
consumption as well.
On the other hand, dwellings with decentralized MHRV can regulate the indoor air tem­
perature through by­pass of the heat recovery unit. Simulations have shown that exhaust
air cooling set point of 22 ◦C can lead to 39% and 23% less overheating in bedrooms and
living rooms respectively compared to the higher set point of 25 ◦C.
In terms of passive solutions, external shading of windows is the most effective way to
reduce solar gains through windows. However, this solution was not analyzed further
because of the harsh Arctic climate conditions in winter due to the strong winds and snow
which can damage or freeze the external blinds and lead to non functional windows. In
contrast, integrated blinds between the panes have proven to be the most suitable way
to reduce overheating in Greenland, as they significantly reduce incoming solar radiation
Thermal performance investigation and optimization of dwellings in Greenland with focus on overheating 63
==PAGE:80==
before entering the room. In particular, they reduce the number of overheating hours by
min. 15% compared to the situation without solar shading and 10% compared to other
internal shading solutions such as, internal blinds and drapes.
Moreover, double glazed windows with relatively high thermal transmittance and low
SHGC (U­value = 1.00; SHGC = 0.35) reduce overheating risk by 9% for bedrooms and
16% for living rooms compared to double double glazed with higher SHGC (U­value =
1.00; SHGC = 0.50). On the other hand, window with low thermal transmittance and low
SHGC (U­value =0.50; SHGC = 0.35) resulted in significantly higher risk of overheating,
particularly in apartments.
In addition, simulations have shown that the proportion of overheating hours reduced
significantly by natural ventilation through the opening of windows two times per day for 30
minutes with 50% opening area. It can also be noticed that opening area has greater effect
on overheating hours compared to the opening time period. However, the effectiveness
of this measure is highly depended on occupants behaviour and can be used only when
outdoor conditions permit.
According to the simulations, the overheating requirements for both living rooms and
bedrooms can be achieved only if combination of previous improvement measures are
properly applied. Particularly the dwellings with decentralized MVHR can fulfill the over­
heating requirements by using by­pass set point temperature of 22 ◦C combined with
internal blinds between the panes and glazing type with low SHGC and relative high U­
value (U­value = 1.00; SHGC = 0.35) and 50% opening window area. On the other hand,
simulations in apartments with centralized MVHR have shown that proper design of venti­
lation rates before adapting passive solutions is critical. Sufficient ventilation rates, equal
to double hygiene standards combined with passive measures as before can fulfill the
overheating requirements as well.
Finally, the simulations showed that high thermal mass tends to produce slightly lower risk
of overheating in all types of dwellings. In addition, high thermal mass is more effective
at maintaining lower internal temperatures in a small temperature range, while low mass
experience large fluctuations.
The approach adopted in this study provides a basis to inform the design of appropriate
measures to mitigate overheating risk in current climates in Greenland. First of all, it is
clear that guidelines to address overheating needs a robust definition instead of abstract
theoretical models of buildings, in order to be used to evaluate actual occupied buildings.
In addition, the measurements showed that the design of the building is not the only pa­
rameter that affects the overheating but the occupancy behaviour has a great influence as
well, since buildings with exactly the same construction and orientation showed to have
significant differences on indoor air temperatures. Therefore, in locations like Greenland,
where there is little past experience with overheating, communicating a common clear ap­
proach to operate a building during overheating periods becomes a major challenge. This
challenge is exacerbated in such places where the number of daylight hours ranges from
0 during winter to 24 during summer and occupants constantly have to adapt to different
conditions and adopt different behavioural patterns. In addition, Greenlandic market with
limited availability of products is losing the advantage of low­cost sensors and the rapidly
developing technologies associated with the ‘connected home’.

4.1
Future work
An investigation on the stochastic behaviour of the window opening would provide useful
insights and more realistic simulation results. In addition, an implementation of different
window types available on the market will provide a more comprehensive overview of the
effects of window types on mitigating overheating.
Moreover, given that nowadays more and more people work from home, as well as the ris­
ing global air surface temperatures due to climate change, hypothetical scenarios should
be explored, considering adapted future weather files and contemporary occupancy be­
haviour patterns.
Lastly, the analysis in this thesis is based on the climate conditions of south­east Green­
land and the results may be suitable to similar climates. However, each arctic region
has different climatic conditions and the building characteristics differentiate. Therefore a
specific analysis may be required for further research on overheating risks in other arctic
regions or other parts of Greenland.

Appendix: Response from ASIAQ
climate station
Asiaq’s climate station in Sisimiut is placed at the airport where Mittarfeqarfiit (Greenland
Airport Authority) also operates a climate station. In the attached data, air temperature,
relative humidity and incoming solar radiation were collected by Asiaq. Raw data for wind
speed and wind direction were sourced from Mittarfeqarfiit and quality corrected by Asiaq.
All data points are one hour average values.
• Air temperature: measured by Asiaq and compared against readings from Mittarfe­
qarfiit.
• Humidity: the humidity readings never reach 100%, likely due to sensor calibration.
The error margin is 3 percentage points.
• Radiation: only global incoming solar radiation available. Some irregular spikes in
W/m2 are present. These can occur when the sensor is partially covered by dew
and frost, or when snow­covered mountains and certain cloud formations reflect
incoming light.
• Wind: the provided data comes with some missing entries, marked with “—”, where
the sensors were down. This is not to be mistaken with “0.0” which designates no
wind present. Some readings of “0.0” are possible incorrect, as wind sensors are
susceptible to malfunctions caused by icing plus wear and tear. Furthermore, the
position of the climate station near the foot of Præstefjeldet mountain will inevitably
have a local impact on both wind readings.
Disclaimer: Asiaq will not take responsibility of any interpretations and analyses
of the data. As mentioned in the description of the data above – there are data that
needs to be excluded in potential results and there are missing data.
