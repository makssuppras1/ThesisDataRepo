Abstract
The present Master Thesis constitutes a comprehensive study on nearВӯwellbore waВӯ
ter imbibition in the context of CO2 cyclic injection for storage purposes. The research,
conducted at INEOS Energy and DTU Chemistry, aims to provide a deeper understandВӯ
ing of the imbibition process and its implications for CO2 cyclic injection practices. It uses
two simulation cases, modeled in Eclipse (SLB), starting from a relatively simple Black Oil
model and inductively moving to a more complex compositional simulation. The models
are twoВӯphase, isothermal, homogeneous, and consist of a horizontal injection well and
a vertical crossВӯsection around it. Some of the most impactful parameters of the nearВӯ
wellbore water imbibition process are investigated in order to enhance knowledge about
the industrial approach of Project Greensand.
Initially, the concept of CO2 cyclic injection and its potential as a tool for reducing
greenhouse gas emissions are introduced. Then there are references to the key theoВӯ
retical concepts around CO2 injection and expected results based on previous studies.
Numerical simulations are employed to investigate the dynamics of water imbibition near
the wellbore during CO2 cyclic injection. The results of these numerical models are anВӯ
alyzed in detail, revealing key insights into the mechanisms of water imbibition and the
factors that affect its rate and extent. Continuing, the impact of various parameters, such
as injection rate, saturation function hysteresis, brine salinity, and well location, on nearВӯ
wellbore water imbibition is examined. The findings of this study, which are how those
simulation key parameters affect the water imbibtion, have significant implications for the
optimization of CO2 cyclic injection processes, contributing to enhanced operational effiВӯ
ciency and sustainability in the energy industry.
Overall, this work provides a valuable contribution to the field of engineering, offering
a deeper understanding of the complex dynamics of nearВӯwellbore water imbibition during
CO2 cyclic injection. The insights gained from this study have the potential to inform and
improve industry practices, leading to more efficient approaches to the application of a
crucial decarbonization technology.

1.1
Background study
Climate change has farВӯreaching effects for the world, having a profound impact
on different environmental, social, and economic elements. Rising global temperatures,
caused by humanВӯcaused greenhouse gas emissions, worsen extreme weather events
such as storms, droughts, and heatwaves. These occurrences alter ecosystems, agriculВӯ
tural production, and water supplies, threatening biodiversity and food security. FurtherВӯ
more, rising ocean temperatures are a major contributor to seaВӯlevel rise, endangering
coastal populations and lowВӯlying areas with greater vulnerability to storm surges. CliВӯ
mate change also affects precipitation patterns, resulting in both more intense rainfall and
longer periods of aridity, magnifying the frequency and intensity of floods and wildfires,
and having a considerable impact on agricultural productivity and economics. The distriВӯ
bution and abundance of species alter as the world heats, potentially having a cascade
effect on biological connections and ecosystems. [1] [2].
The impact of carbon dioxide (CO2) on climate change is a critical issue for the sciВӯ
entific community. CO2 is a greenhouse gas that, through the greenhouse effect, helps to
regulate the EarthвҖҷs temperature. Numerous studies have found a link between humanВӯ
caused CO2 emissions and the remarkable rise in global temperatures over the last cenВӯ
tury. Human activities, specifically the use of fossil fuels for energy generation and transВӯ
portation, have greatly boosted atmospheric CO2 levels above natural levels, resulting in
global temperature rise (Figures 1.1 and 1.2). To offset the negative impacts of CO2 on
the EarthвҖҷs climate system, immediate and comprehensive steps to cut emissions, conВӯ
vert to renewable energy sources, and implement sustainable practices are required [3]
[4].

1.2
Problem description
The present MSc thesis explores an aspect of the Greensand project, the nearВӯ
wellbore water imbibition. The main tool that is going to be used throughout this study
is numerical simulations. Greensand is an innovative offshore carbon storage project full
of potential and formidable challenges. Fundamental to the entire project is the cyclic inВӯ
jection scheme, which poses a serious risk to the injection wellвҖҷs structural integrity. This
vulnerability results from the spontaneous imbibition of water in every postВӯinjection peВӯ
riodвҖҷs early stage, which exposes the well to a corrosive mixture of water and CO2. The
integrity and longВӯterm functionality of the well are seriously threatened by this particular
environment.
The injection of CO2 on a cyclical basis starts a complicated interaction between the
reservoir components that make up the fluidвҖҷs composition and the well. These circumВӯ
stances frequently lead to a corrosive mixture that causes quick material deterioration,
which could jeopardize the security and effectiveness of the overall project. For the deВӯ
velopment of reliable solutions, it is essential to comprehend the underlying principles
and complexities related to the rate of water imbibition around the wellbore. It is crucial to
identify a potential dryВӯout zone around the well and, as accurately as possible, define itвҖҷs
boundaries. In order to study the geometry of the driedВӯout region versus time, it is necВӯ
essary to go through numerical models and simulations. At the end of this process, the
results that are going to be obtained will provide some criteria to determine if the proposed
injection schemes can be feasible [8].
In order to do that, it is very important to understand all the impactful aspects of this
complex problem. The approach is made through twoВӯphase isothermal modeling and
this study has as itвҖҷs starting point the simulation of a cyclic injection using a simple black

2.1
Multiphase flow in porous media
Fluid dynamics refers to the flow of materials with two or more phases (liquids and
gases) as multiphase flow. The study and modeling of multiphase flow in porous media is
crucial to methods for recovering hydrocarbons from reservoirs, storing CO2 in geological
formations, and remediating organic contaminants below ground. According to the type of
target storage formation, twoВӯphase flows typically occur in water/gas or oil/gas systems
throughout the carbon storage process ([9]).
In porous media, gravity, capillary, viscous, and advective forces frequently govern
the flow. A phase migration toward the gravitational field is influenced by gravitational
forces. A phase with a lower density tends to move upward and replace a phase with
a higher density. For instance, in carbon storage, injected CO2 (the gaseous phase)
migrates to the top of the formation and displaces heavier water or oil. This phenomenon
is frequently caused by the density differences between the two phases. Capillary forces
regulate the distribution of each phase (saturation) in a porous medium, which affects
the effective porosity and permeability. Viscous forces have an impact on relative phase
movement, with less viscous fluids moving more quickly due to their lower flow resistance.
The fingering of phase interfaces, which occurs when a fluid with higher density and lower
viscosity is displaced by a fluid with lower density, is a typical phenomenon associated
with viscous forces. Advection is the fluid movement brought on by pressure differences,
as opposed to gravity forces where buoyant flow is driven by density differences. When
CO2 moves from the injection well (higher pressure) to the formation (lower pressure), this
process is most noticeable. Advective flow, as opposed to flow caused by gravity, can be
characterized in all directions in this situation because pressure gradients may exist in all
directions ([10]).
Diffusion, dispersion, and heat conduction are additional fluid flow mechanisms that
may occur in a porous medium. The process of equating variations in molecule density
or velocity is known as diffusion as a result of Brownian motion. Diffusion, in contrast to

In investigations of subsurface flow, numerical simulations are frequently used and

Migration and trapping mechanisms for CO2 in the
subsurface
Several trapping mechanisms work during the CO2 storage process to stop the miВӯ
gration of CO2 back to the surface, which would otherwise cause an unfavorable leak, and
to assure secure and efficient longВӯterm storage. Depending on how long the procedure
NearВӯwellbore water imbibition study for cyclic injection of CO2 into the subsurface
11
==PAGE:24==
takes, the trapping methods can be split into two broad categories: geochemical trapping
and physical trapping [16].
2.4.1
Physical trapping
After being injected into a reservoir, CO2 maintains its physical character during the
physical trapping phase, which typically lasts little more than 100 years ([17]). Two trapВӯ
ping mechanisms are considered:
1. Structural trapping: The first process that engages in CO2 trapping in geological
storage is typically this one. The CO2 cannot migrate upwards above a storage
formation because of a very lowВӯpermeable rock layer (capВӯrock), fault, or other
discontinuity.
2. Residual (capillary) trapping: It is typically regarded as the most significant physВӯ
ical trapping mechanism throughout the postВӯinjection period. Surface tension beВӯ
tween CO2 and the formation water prevents CO2 from moving, leaving part of it beВӯ
hind as dispersed droplets in the pores (residual saturation). Because the droplets
can no longer migrate, any remaining fluid in the formation traps the CO2 and stores
it ([18]).
2.4.2
Geochemical trapping
In contrast to physical trapping, geochemical trapping occurs when CO2 undergoes
physical and chemical changes as a result of geochemical interactions with the formation
brine and the rock itself. It results in the CO2 dissipating as a single mobile, or immobile
phase. These mechanisms are prominent in the carbon storage process over a longer
time span. Those mechanisms are:
1. Solubility trapping: On longer time intervals, it is believed to be the most effective
trapping mechanism. As the CO2 moves through the subsoil, it dissolves in the
brine formation fluid that fills the pores. Due to the density disparities, it no longer
exists as a distinct phase, which stops it from rising. Brine that has been saturated
with CO2 has a tendency to sink to the formationвҖҷs bottom as it gets denser, offering
more safe storage.
2. Mineral trapping: As a result of the CO2 reaction with the formation rock, minerals
such as calcite (CaCO3), siderite (FeCO3), or magnesite (MgCO3) form, trapping
CO2 inside. Because the reaction of carbonate precipitation occurs at a rate that
is far slower than the dissolution of CO2 into the formation fluid stated earlier, this
process is seen as being less significant on shortВӯterm scales. On the other hand,
it is the method of storage that is the most longВӯterm ([19]).

The phenomenon known as вҖқspontaneous imbibitionвҖқ describes how a porous subВӯ

Conclusions and Future Work
5.1
Conclusions
The results and discussion section of this study have provided valuable insights into
the nearВӯwellbore water imbibition phenomenon and its impact on CO2 cyclic injection.
The following conclusions can be drawn from the research:
Initially, the Black Oil model provided some information about the overall behavior and
plume evolution of the gas inside the reservoir. In order to have a better understanding
of the phenomenon, a model that could simulate water vaporization and CO2 dissolution
was needed. Here is where CO2 STORE was introduced. At that point, the dryВӯout effect
was observed only at the upper boundary of the model where the gas was accumulatВӯ
ing. By comparing the two models the decision to do the sensitivity analysis of the key
parameters on the CO2 STORE model was made because of the significant influence of
CO2 dissolution and water vaporization. It should be noted that both of the models indiВӯ
cate that one of the most pronounced driving mechanisms regarding plume evolution is
gravitational forces.
Additionally, injection rate sensitivity analysis showed a small dryВӯout zone around
the wellbore for a very high rate injection scenario (two times the initial one). This dryingВӯ
out effect lasted only until a certain amount of gas reached the upper boundary of the
reservoir, and then the water started flowing back to the well again. This behavior has
been an indicator of a very pronounced relative permeability effect under which the CO2,
as long as it accumulates at the top of the model and itвҖҷs saturation increases, creates
preferential pathways and moves easier after this point, having as a result more water to
imbibe at the nearВӯwellbore area.
On the other hand, the sensitivity analysis of the phenomenon of hysteresis of the
saturation functions, while initially believed to be due to the continuous alteration between
drainage and imbibition in the nearВӯwellbore area, would have a big impact on the saturaВӯ
tions around the wellboreНҫ eventually, the simulation indicates that it doesnвҖҷt. Although the
effect is probably not visible in this particular simulation because of the coarseness of the
grid, a significantly more refined model could replicate hysteretic effects more adequately.
Moreover, brine salinity, even at a moderate level, seems to be impactful. The model
including salinity showed around 0.11 more water saturation around the wellbore than the
model with clear water. These results indicate that the salinity of the brine is a parameter
that should be taken very seriously into account in every CO2 cyclic injection project.
By analyzing the outputs of the simulation so far, it has been obvious that the distance
of the well from the upper boundaries of the reservoir will most likely affect the nearВӯ
wellbore dryВӯout region. By observing the actual location of the injection well for Project
Greensand (NAВӯ3B) which is in a pinchВӯout of the reservoir, a simulation replicating this
boundary effect was necessary. The well location sensitivity analysis for two different
injection rates indicates promising results in terms of spontaneous water imbibition near
the wellbore. The high rate case of 10000 tons of CO2 per day suggests a dryВӯout close
to the well after almost 3 and a half months, and the lower rate case of 3000 tons per day
suggests a dryВӯout after almost eight and a half months.
Closing, it should be noted that the favorable position of the NAВӯ3B well adds huge
potential to the Greensand project in terms of well integrity based on the models used
NearВӯwellbore water imbibition study for cyclic injection of CO2 into the subsurface

==PAGE:68==
in this study. Probably some kind of treatment with corrosion inhibitors until the nearВӯ
wellbore area drys out would be needed, but the final results are much better than those
initially expected. This novel study pointed out some of the key parameters around the
challenging topic of nearВӯwellbore spontaneous water imbibition using a relatively simple
twoВӯphase isothermal approach, but there is, for sure, a lot of room for more research in
this area using more advanced modeling options and possibly more specialized tools.
5.2
Future work
The findings of this study provide a solid foundation for future research on nearwellВӯ
bore water imbibition in the context of CO2 cyclic injection. One of the most important
areas of potential future work is the development of a model that can accurately simulate
the dryВӯout effect in a threeВӯphase system (water, gas, and oil). Currently, most models
used in the industry are for threeВӯphase systems, which do not account for the complex
dynamics of water and oil vaporization. This is crucial because the cumulative residual
saturation of oil and water is usually a very high percentage, and if the vaporization of
those two liquids cannot be modeled, the information that these simulations provide is
limited.
In addition, future work could explore the impact of heterogeneity on nearВӯwellbore
water imbibition. This studyвҖҷs models assume a homogeneous reservoir, which can give
a qualitatively decent approach to the phenomenon, but they are not one hundred percent
representative of the actual conditions inside the reservoir. A more realistic model that
accounts for heterogeneity could provide valuable insights into the impact of reservoir
properties on water imbibition and CO2 cyclic injection efficiency.
Another area of potential future work is the development of a model with a very fine
grid. The current study used a 2D, homogeneous model with a grid size of 2m x 3m.
However, a finer grid could provide a more detailed representation of the nearВӯwellbore
region and the dynamics of water imbibition and salt precipitation. In addition to that,
models that could replicate more chemical interactions, like pH changes, could provide
even more useful results.
In pursuit of advancing our understanding of nearВӯwellbore water imbibition during
cyclic injection, a nonВӯisothermal model would also be a significant improvement in the
study. Recognizing the impact of temperature on fluid behavior and phase interactions,
the integration of a nonВӯisothermal approach is imperative for a more comprehensive analВӯ
ysis of the nearВӯwellbore environment. By incorporating temperature variations, this study
would be able to elucidate the thermal effects on water imbibition and assess their implicaВӯ
tions for the nearВӯwelbore area. This modification will contribute to a more realistic repreВӯ
sentation of subsurface conditions, providing valuable insights into the intricate interplay
between temperature, fluid flow, and phase behavior within the nearВӯwellbore region.
Overall, future work in this area could lead to the development of more accurate and
efficient models for CO2 cyclic injection, contributing to enhanced operational efficiency
and sustainability in the energy industry.
