Abstract
The Earth’s core dynamics are linked to the magnetic field through a highly nonlinear cou-
pled system of Partial Differential Equations (PDEs). In the current age, high-resolution
data on the magnetic field is available through both ground stations and dense satellite
observations. Efficient numerical models are now needed in order to properly use ob-
servations together with knowledge of the governing equations to infer the state of the
core. This would open the way to a deeper understanding of how the magnetic field is
generated and allow better predictions of its future behaviour.
In this thesis, we revisit a previous numerical implementation of a Physics-informed Neural
Network (PINN) [1, 2], where governing equations of a prototype 1D-Magneto-Hydrodynamic
(MHD) system designed to mimic Earth’s core dynamics, along with known Initial Con-
dition (IC) and Boundary Condition (BC), was solved for the time evolution of the core
velocity- and magnetic field. We do so in an effort to more efficiently recreate the origi-
nal results, and ultimately try to infer knowledge of the velocity- and magnetic fields from
noisy and incomplete observations and without knowledge of the IC.
In tests carried out during the thesis, we propose a more efficient PINN setup, producing
slightly better results than seen in [2], according to the L2 metric, while significantly reduc-
ing training time. This is accomplished by implementing an adaptive resampling scheme
of the interior collocation points, using batches during training, modifications of the loss
function, and using a double optimizer setup of Adaptive Momentum Estimation (ADAM)
and Limited Memory BFGS (L-BFGS).
Additionally, we demonstrate that the PINN is able to accurately reproduce the true core
velocity field if dense satellite-type observations are present for a limited amount of time.
Furthermore, the PINN shows highly encouraging results in accurately retaining knowl-
edge of the future field and flow evolution, at least for a short time into the future. The
PINN is also able to reproduce the overall trend in the velocity field given knowledge of
the magnetic field alone. We also present a simple attempt to quantify uncertainty in PINN
inverse modelling and discuss perspectives as to how this could be improved in the future.

Introduction
Obtaining knowledge of the dynamical processes within Earth’s interior, more specifically
the core, is not easy. In fact, direct measurements are impossible and other means are
required to infer knowledge on this. One such way is through Earth’s magnetic field since
more than 94% of the contribution towards this stems from the core, where high-quality
measurements in the form of both magnetic ground stations, and, especially, dense satel-
lite coverage are available [3]. Currently, the hypothesis is, that the core field is gener-
ated from a self-exciting dynamo in the outer core since the temperatures here exceed
the Curie temperature, and as a consequence permanent magnetism is not possible [3].
In the outer core (conductive) liquid metal moves through a magnetic field, producing
electrical currents by motional induction, resulting in the main magnetic field [3]. A re-
quirement for the flow of the molten metal is energy, coming from (i) cooling of the planet,
(ii) freezing of the liquid outer core to the solid inner core, (iii) radioactive decay and (iv)
rotation from tidal forces and processional forces [3]. Since liquid metal has a relatively
low viscosity the aforementioned sources result in vigorous convection in the outer core.
The core flow and the magnetic field are coupled by a nonlinear set of PDEs: conservation
of momentum, magnetic induction, and heat transport, on the form [4]:

Automatic Differentiation (AD) and PyTorch
Error backpropagation can be seen as part of a wider array of automatic differentiation
(AD) algorithms; a field concerned with computing gradients algorithmically [10]. These
techniques are used to efficiently and accurately evaluate derivatives of numerical func-
tions in the form of computer programs [15]. AD can be separated into two different
modes; forward mode and reverse mode [15, 10], both efficient in different cases of com-
puting derivatives of functions mapping f : Rn →Rm. Forward for m ≫n and Reverse
for m ≪n. Both modes revolve around applying the chain rule, Equation 2.19, to com-
putational graphs, where reverse mode AD can be seen as a generalization of backprop-
agation.
Now why is this relevant for this thesis, as we have already covered the backpropaga-
tion algorithm? In this thesis in solving Equation 2.1, a method to evaluate the partial
derivatives is needed. AD provides a means to compute such derivatives in the con-
text of a NN. Fortunately, multiple modern programming libraries incorporate AD. In this
thesis, we follow previous work by Rasmus C. Jørgensen ([1, 2]) and use the PyTorch
framework. Pytorch incorporates AD within its Automatic Differentiation Package:
https://pytorch.org/docs/stable/autograd.html. For details see [16].

Batch training
For large datasets, the computation time of the previously introduced GD optimization
method, Equation 2.17 rapidly increases. This is avoidable by computing each update

==PAGE:28==
based on an expectation of the loss function, from a subset of the data [10]; this known as
batch training. The iterative descent algorithm is then a stochastic GD update, where the
update uses the gradient of the loss function wrt. some subset of the data ∇Ln, where
L = P
n Ln [11]. Henceforth by batch training we mean the update is computed from a
subset of the training data. Computing the update from the full dataset will be referred to
as Full batch training. Some authors, for instance in [10], use another terminology where,
batch means the full training dataset, and minibatch is a subset of the training dataset.
It is important that the selected batches are chosen randomly, such that subsequent gra-
dient estimates are independent of each other [10]. This is usually done by shuffling the
order of the dataset; meaning that consecutive datapoints should be independent in the
shuffled dataset. Failing to shuffle the data before selecting batches can heavily impact
the effectiveness [10]. The batch size is usually kept relatively small and a common strat-
egy is to keep the batch size as a power of 2, as this results in the best runtimes on
GraphicsProcessing Units (GPUs), so for example the batch size is often kept between
32 and 256 [10]. Compared to full batch training, utilizing smaller batches increases the
training time, as multiple weight updates are computed going through the dataset once.
However, optimization algorithms often converge faster if they rapidly use approximate
estimates of the gradient, rather than computing the exact gradient [10]. Thus one can
decrease the overall training time, and get a similar result, or keep a similar training time,
but increase the performance by using batches. In subsection 2.3.2 the choice of batch
size is discussed from recent studies using PINNs.
A number of different optimization algorithms are investigated in the thesis. Second-order
optimization methods typically require a larger batch size than those mentioned above e.g.
10000 [10]. This is a consequence of utilizing the Hessian when computing updates. A
small error in the estimate of the gradient, can, in turn, result in a large error in the update
step H−1g, where H is the Hessian and g is the gradient of the loss function.

2.3
Physics Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)
PINN, as the name suggests, is a type of neural network framework developed for solv-
ing forward and inverse problems wherein nonlinear PDEs are involved. This type of
network was first introduced in [17] utilizing equations governing the physical laws of
time-dependent dynamics of a system or domain. It involves implementing the governing
equations within the loss function while training the network; thus enforcing the correct-
ness of the PDEs describing the system. The effect of encoding such knowledge into the
learning algorithms of neural networks enables them to quickly steer towards both correct
and well-generalized solutions even when only sparse information is available[17]. This
is in high contrast to usual deep learning architectures/models, where large amounts of
data are usually needed to obtain accurate solutions.
The idea behind PINN is to utilize the capability of a NN as a universal function approxima-
tor, thus avoiding the need for time-stepping schemes as described in section 2.1. Using a
NN in this context is possible due to recent development within AD and its implementation
within deep learning, as described in subsubsection 2.2.3.
In Raissi et al (2019) [17] - and in this thesis - parameterized and nonlinear PDEs of the
following form are considered:
ut + N[u; λ] = 0, x ∈Ω, t ∈[0, T]
(2.31)
here u(t, x) is the latent solution to the PDEs with spatial domain, x, and temporal domain,
t. N[u; λ] is a nonlinear operator with control parameters λ and Ωis a subset of RD [17].

2.3.2
Additional methods to improve performance
The initial implementation of a PINN proposed by [17] is a good starting point but is not
necessarily the most effective way to solve all problems of interest. Depending on the
complexity of the system one might face difficulties, for example in [22] where a PINN is
not found to be capable of properly solving the Allen-Cahn and Cahn-Hilliard equations,
despite success in the original paper when considering the Burger’s equation [23]. The
problem, in this case, is due to the field of interest having sharp transition layers, and that
these layers evolve with time [22]. This is also the case for the 1D MHD problem consid-
ered in this thesis. In [22] several techniques are proposed with the aim of improving the
learning capability of the PINN.
Rebalancing of Loss function
First and foremost they propose to add weights to the different terms of the loss function.
Starting from the general formulation of the loss function from [17], Equation 2.32, MSEu
can be further split into losses of the BC and IC, MSEu = MSE0 + MSEb. Introducing
additional weighting parameters, the loss function becomes:
MSE = C0MSE0 + MSEb + MSEf
(2.34)
Reactive-diffusion equations, such as the Allen-Cahn equation in the paper and the mo-
mentum and magnetic induction equations considered here, can only be stably solved by
Physics Informed Neural Network for Modelling Earths Core Dynamics
15
==PAGE:34==
going forward in time [22]. Thus if the PINN fails to learn the solution at an initial time
step, t = t0, the accuracy of later time steps, t2 > t1 > t0, diminishes [22]. In [22] a value
of C0 = 100 is recommended.
Batch training
In [17] the PINN is trained on the full dataset for each training iteration. Use of smaller
batches during training has been investigated in both [22] and [24]. In [22] a batch size
of 32 - equivalent to 0.32% of the full training set - significantly improved the performance
of the PINN. A similar conclusion is reached in [24], where their investigation of imple-
menting batch training to solve the nonlinear Biot’s equation is more successful. Here it
is discussed to implement training with batches of sizes between 8 and 32, correspond-
ing to a compromise of improved accuracy without increasing training time too much, in
accordance with the discussion in subsection 2.2.4.
Adaptive Strategies in time
The main focus of [22] is the application of different adaptive strategies to improve PINN
performance. 3 different techniques are explored, Adaptive sampling of collocation points,
and two different Adaptive strategies in time. The idea behind Adaptive sampling of col-
location points, which will be denoted Spatial Resampling during the remainder of the
thesis, is rather simple. For a Spatio-temporal domain where sharp gradients of the field
are present, such as near the boundaries, in traditional solution methods one sometimes
adopts a finer mesh where needed to resolve the structures of interest. In the context of a
PINN it is therefore suggested to adopt a strategy of adding additional collocation points
where needed (where the error compared to the governing equations is largest) during
training. The PINN is at first trained on a set of randomly sampled points, then a predic-
tion of the entire Spatio-temporal domain of the governing equations is computed. Next
a specific number of collocation points in areas with the highest errors are added to the
existing training data. This procedure can be reiterated as needed. By adding the points
to the existing training data (i.e. points where the governing equations are enforced), the
technique should improve the solution in problematic areas of the domain, while still main-
taining the accuracy of the solution throughout the rest of the domain [22]. An illustration
of the strategy can be seen in Figure 2.5.

Forward modelling using PINN’s
In the original paper by Raissi et. al. [17], using a PINN to solve PDEs, given the gov-
erning equations, BCs, and ICs is called data-drive solutions of PDEs. Here we follow
the terminology more commonly used in the context of geophysical inverse problems and
refer to this as forward modelling using PINNs instead. This distinction stems from the
fact that we do not recognize sets of collocation points where one chooses to enforce
the governing equations as real data, hence we feel it is confusing to refer to this as
”data-drive”.
The objective of this chapter is to determine how well we are able to solve a well-posed
forward problem, here the 1D-MHD system described in section 2.1, with clearly defined
ICs and BCs, utilizing a PINN. In section 3.1 we set out the methodology used in the
various tests, while the results are collected in section 3.2.
3.1
Methodology
Before investigating the applicability of using a PINN in an inverse modelling setup for a
geophysical problem, we first need to determine an appropriate and efficient PINN setup
for the problem at hand. This is done by starting with the work accomplished by Rasmus

3.1.3
Recap of previous work by Jørgensen (2022)
Here we present an overview of the most important tests, findings, and encountered dif-
ficulties in the previous study [1, 2]. The reference PINN used in [1] was greatly inspired
by the data-driven solution to solve the Shrödinger equation from [17], with a similar per-
centage of the full domain used as collocation points for the governing equations. The
tests carried out can be categorized into two; changes made to the training data and their
influence on the solution, and changes made to the neural network architecture.
Training data changes included different amounts of collocation points used during training
of the PINN, and the implementation of batches during training. Changing the amount of
collocation points, where the governing equations are enforced, was tested to analyze the
impact of training time verses performance, when reducing or increasing it.
Both minor and major changes were made to the training architecture of the network. The
minor changes includes choice of activation function between TanH and ReLU, and con-
sequently their weight initialization scheme (Xavier and Kaiming respectively). A choice
of optimizer was also tested, between solely using the ADAM optimizer or a combination
of both ADAM and L-BFGS. This was done both in accordance with the implementation
in [17], but also to test if the solution was getting stuck at a local minimum.
Tests were also carried out, where knowledge of the U- and B-field at the collocation points
were incorporated into the loss function in the form of data misfit terms, to mimic a classic
inverse problem. We, however, are not in complete agreement with this method in [1],
and our interpretation of an inverse problem will be discussed in chapter 4. Lastly, an-
other modification was done to the loss function in the form of implementing an advanced
training technique called Causal training.
One of the key discoveries made after submission deadline was the impact of batch nor-
malization within the FNN of the PINN. Despite various modifications to the training data
and network architecture, the results were consistently poor. While some adjustments,
such as the implementation of batch training and causal training, yielded slight improve-
ments, it was ultimately the removal of batch normalization in the linear layers of the FNN
that led to a significant enhancement in the results of the causal training implementation,
as reported in [2]. However, the underlying reason for this remains unclear. Due to the
timing of this discovery, the tests were not re-run, except for the final implementation of
causal training.

3.1.4
A reference PINN case
A reference PINN is implemented to evaluate the performance of the forthcoming tests.
For this, we chose to ”strip down” the final complex implementation presented in [2], and
use this simple PINN version as our reference.
The reference PINN architecture is set to consists of a FNN containing 5 hidden layers,
each with 100 hidden units. Relevant studies concerning the use of PINNs have shown,
that comparable results are achieved using TanH and ReLU [24, 18], thus we chose to
implement TanH and Xavier weight initialization for all layers and units. Following the
important finding in [2] no Batch Normalization is performed at the layers. The number of
Training iterations are kept to 200000, where subsequent tests should strive to keep this
fixed, unless complications present themselves e.g. due to limited training time. Learning
will be performed with the ADAM Optimizer as described in subsection 2.2.5 Algorithm 1,
with a learning rate, η, set to 10−3. For all subsequent tests these choices will stay the
same, unless otherwise specified.
Secondly, the structure of the training data-set given to the PINN is important to note, and
is generated according to subsection 3.1.1. We chose to train the PINN on a subset of the
full data-set. Here we deviate from the sampling method performed in [1], where a strategy
was implemented to rearrange the generated data, such that (roughly) evenly amounts of
data was found at all spatial instances. This meant that despite having more knowledge
near the boundaries, and correspondingly near the sharpest gradient layer at the lower
boundary, this was not utilized. Instead the training data is simply randomly sampled from
the generated data set, thus allowing for more training points near both boundaries and
less in the middle of the spatial domain. We argue, that this could possibly help to better
resolve the gradients. This can be seen in subsection A.3.1, where histograms of the two
methods are presented. Regarding the amount of sampled data, we chose an identical
α = 0.4 measure as the one in [1], where α corresponds to the ratio between number of
collocation points in the generated data and the sampled collocation points given to the
PINN [1]. The total number of training points are then NIC = 1000, NBC = 198 · 2 and
Nf = 79000; the number of initial training points, boundary training points and collocation
points respectively.

Training structure
Figure 3.1 represents how the different parts of the network relate to each other, such
that training is possible. As depicted the PINN initially takes an input of (x, t) values and
passes it onto the FNN. The purpose of the FNN, as described in section 2.3, is to learn
the latent solution of the problem, the U and B field, thus outputting a prediction of the as-
sociated (u, b) to the input (x, t) points from the current weights and biases of the network.
This is then passed onto the PDE function, wherein the governing equations describing the
1D-MHD system are computed according to Equation 3.4. fu(x, t) and fb(x, t) along with
the predictions (ˆu,ˆb) (denoted (u, b) in the figure) are passed to the Loss function, Equa-
tion 3.3. Error back-propagation is then performed according to subsection 2.2.3 and the
gradients of the weight and biases are computed. Finally, these are passed to the ADAM
optimizer, where the weights and biases of the FNN are updated according to Algorithm
1. This is done iteratively for a specified number of iterations, until a final prediction of the
U and B field can be achieved.

governing equations, as we always know the true value at the boundaries. The least em-

Batch training
As mentioned in subsection 2.2.4 and 2.3.2 batch training is an adoptable method to fur-
ther improve the accuracy and decrease training time of a network, especially for large
data sets. In [17] this is not utilized, but was tested in [1]. Despite horrible results through-
out the thesis, batch implementation did seem encouraging though.
When implementing batch training, we argue that it is only necessary to batch data points
for the governing equations due to two reasons. 1) The governing equations are condi-
tionalized on the IC. Therefore, we find that each time a batch is passed to the network
ALL IC are as well. 2) The amount of data points on both the IC and BC are much smaller
compared to the governing equations. This means that either a batch of the IC and BC
would consists of almost all points anyway, or consists of only a select few points.
Utilizing batches during training an important parameter to specify is the batch size. Recall
that in [18] it was demonstrated that a batch size of 32 (equivalent to 0.32% of the training
data set) significantly improved training. This would amount to a batch size of 79000 ·
0.0032 = 252.6 for the interior collocation points. Since we train all networks on a GPU
we choose a batch size of 256, following the arguments presented in subsection 2.2.4. It
was furthermore specified, that selected batches should consist of random data, such that
the gradient descent estimates are independent of each other. Therefore it is important
to note, that we randomly shuffle the data within the training function before selecting
specific batches.
In order to compare training with batches to the reference PINN we propose two tests: The
first performing a similar amount of weight updates ( 200000) and the second training for a
similar amount of time. With a batch size of 256, one training iteration, denoted epoch for
batch training, consists of (79000/256) = 308.5 batches pr. epoch; or 309 weight updates
pr. epoch. In order to complete 200000 weight updates we need 200000/308.5 = 648
epochs, rounding up to 650. This means, that the network only sees the full data set 650
times, compared to the 200000 times without batches. However time limitations prevent
us from completing tests with 200000 epochs, therefore we instead opt to complete a test,
where the training time is similar to that of the reference PINN. To summarize, the tests
can be found in Table 3.3.

3.1.6
Spatial resampling
The conclusion reached in [1, 2] involving implementing the very time consuming causal
training that took 50 hours, while still not perfectly solving 1D-MHD problem. Here, we
revisit whether this implementation is necessary, or if a simpler approach can reach an
adequate performance. To do so, we wish to test a simpler adaptive strategy of sampling
interior collocation points during training, which has shown successful improvements to
the accuracy of PINNs in [22].
We implement the method following the description in subsubsection 2.3.2, where a PINN
is trained for a fixed number of iterations. A chosen number, Nnew, of the largest errors
on the governing equations from a randomly sampled subset of the remaining Spatio-
temporal domain, i.e. points that have not been used during training, is then added to
existing training data. We noticed, that there is a correlation between errors in the U-field
and errors in the governing equations. Therefore selecting new data from the governing
equations ensures that the method can be exploited, even in scenarios where the true
U-field is not known. Additionally the errors in the governing equations for the U-field, fu,
is chosen since this is the field with sharp gradients and is therefore more error prone.

3.3
Summary
Here, we present a summary to collect the most important results related to finding a more
simple and efficient PINN setup, than the one presented in [1, 2].
A much simpler reference PINN setup, described in detail in subsection 3.1.4, without
the complex causal training algorithm used by R. C. Jørgensen, is able to achieve similar
levels of performance in a much shorter time.
Small improvements to the performance can be made by:
• Better balancing the loss function
This is achieved by adding weight parameters to all terms. Most emphasis is put on
the IC, and with a higher priority given to the BC than points where the governing
equations are known, resulting in the following weight parameters: λic = 1000, λbc =
100, and λf = 1.
• Implementing a L-BFGS optimizer to finish training
The PINN setup now includes an additional L-BFGS optimizer to finish training for
relatively few iterations, compared to the ADAM optimizer. Doing so, the PINN is
able to converge to a lower value of the loss function.
• Using batches of the interior collocation points during training
During training with the ADAM optimizer of the PINN batches of size 256 for the inte-
rior collocation points, significantly decreases training time of the network compared
to the reference setup. Doing so, the PINN only trains on the interior collocation
points 650 times with the ADAM optimizer as to the 200000 for the reference PINN,
although performing a similar amount of weight updates.
• Implementing an adaptive strategy of sampling the interior collocation points
Finally, an adaptive strategy during training is implemented, where new interior col-
location points are added to the existing training points, where the PINN has a hard
time to reproduce the core velocity field (the sharp gradients), improving the accu-
racy in these areas.
With the addition of the described modifications above, the final PINN setup obtained an
error for the velocity field of L2(U) = 0.242, and the magnetic field L2(B) = 0.012 training
for
2 hours, compared to the implementation by Rasmus C. Jørgensen in [2], where
L2(U) = 0.738 and L2(B) = 0.016 was obtained after 50 hours.
Although an improvement to the performance is observed by utilizing these implementa-
tions with a final preferred PINN setup as summarized in Table 3.9, it has still not been
possible to fully reproduce the behaviour of the velocity field close the lower boundary
layer. However, away from this region the solutions are well retrieved, and the setup will
be used as the basis for experiments with the inverse problem in the next chapter.

4
Inverse modelling with PINNs
For a well posed dynamical system, such as the 1D-MHD problem described in sec-
tion 2.1, with known BC, IC and a set of PDE governing the system, we are able to
describe the exact solution of the system at all times, as seen in subsection 3.2.1 and
Figure 3.3. This is a unique solution, where no uncertainty is present.
In [17] Raissi et al. mention that their empirical evidence indicates, that only for well-
posed problems with unique solutions, PINNs are capable of achieving good predictive
accuracy, given sufficient amounts of collocation points and expressive architecture of
the NN. For typical applications within geophysics (geomagnetics), the problem is seldom
well posed, since only incomplete information is available.
In this chapter, we attempt to imitate an inverse problem within a geophysical application,
with incomplete (sparse and noisy) observations of the system, and aim to retrieve the
true state of the 1D-MHD system utilizing the new numerical method of PINNs. Previously
the IC, BC, and PDE were all available, however, in this chapter we assume only the BC
and PDE are available, along with some observations of the fields.
Inspiration for this chapter is drawn from Fournier et. al [5], where they tested how the
assimilation of observations could help improve the knowledge of the 1D-MHD system
exploring how spatial and temporal sampling of the observations influenced the output.
We choose to adopt similar experiments for spatial and temporal sampling as those used
by Fournier et al. In addition to the BC and interior collocation points governed by the
PDEs in Equation 2.1, (sparse) noisy information on the U- and B-field is therefore pro-
vided in the form of either virtual ground stations, virtual satellite data, or a combination
of both. We refer to these tests with incomplete data as Inverse modelling with PINNs. In
section 4.1 we set out the methodology used in the various experiments pertaining to the
inverse modelling, while results of these are collected and presented in section 4.2.
4.1
Methodology
The PINN setup proposed by Rasmus C. Jørgensen in [1, 2] was revisited in the previous
chapter, in an effort to find a simple, appropriate and efficient PINN setup for solving the
problem at hand. Here, implementing spatial resampling of the interior collocation along
with batching of the interior collocation points during training with an ADAM optimizer
subsequently followed by a L-BFGS optimizer was found to perform suitably, while greatly
reducing training time compared to R. Jørgensen’s setup. The same PINN setup and
training scheme was adopted for all experiments within this chapter, and is summarized
in Table 3.9 in the previous chapter.
Below we discuss some additional refinements of the PINN setup (data structure, addi-
tion of noise, loss function modification and batches) in order to accommodate inverse
modelling in subsection 4.1.1. Subsection 4.1.2 collects the diagnostics used throughout
this chapter. In subsection 4.1.3 through 4.1.6 we describe the motivation for each of the
inverse modelling experiments, going from No virtual observations, to a set of tests of
virtual ground stations, then virtual satellite data before a final set of experiments with a
combination of the two (ground stations and satellites) is introduced.
4.1.1
PINN setup and changes
Going from forward to inverse modelling a few important changes needs to be clarified
wrt. training of the PINN. This involves a new training data structure, modification to the
loss function, and addition of noise.

Training data:
Previously in chapter 3, the problem was well-posed, with in principle complete knowledge
in the form of IC, BC, and the PDEs governing the system. This is no longer the case,
as limited or no knowledge is present on the IC. Along with incorporation of (sparse)
knowledge on one or both fields (the core velocity -and magnetic field), we are required
to handle the training data differently. For this we chose to incorporate two different data-
sets; one consisting of points, where the governing equations are obeyed, and a new
data-set consisting of points, where knowledge on the field(s) are present in the form of
(possibly noisy) observations. These are denoted Datagov and Dataobs respectively.
Datagov is almost identical to that used for training the PINN in the previous chapter.
NBC = 2·198 data points on the boundaries and Nf = 79000 interior collocation points are
again used during training. The same random sampling strategy to chose training points,
described in subsection 3.1.4, is used for the interior collocation points, only now these
can also be present at T = 0, but described by the governing equations, instead of the
true field values. In chapter 3 points from three validation time-steps T = [0.1, 0.2, 0.3],
were not represented by the governing equations during training. However, since the
governing equations are known everywhere throughout the full Spatio-temporal domain,
we no longer exclude points from within these epochs to be sampled.
Dataobs consists of data points within the full domain, where knowledge of at least the
magnetic field and possibly the velocity field are present. Knowledge are available in the
form of two observations types to imitate real-life methods; virtual ground stations or virtual
satellite data. Virtual ground stations are specific spatial locations, where observations
of the field(s) are accumulated throughout all epochs. Virtual satellites instead consists
of observed field values within a specific epoch with high spatial resolution. Here obser-
vations are randomly sampled to be 95% of the available data points, during a restricted
time period.

4.1.3
No Initial knowledge, no observed data
We begin with an initial experiment, where knowledge of the IC is removed during training
of the PINN, and in addition we have no knowledge of either the core velocity field or mag-
netic field. This is done to assess the claim in [17] by Raissi et al., that only a well-posed
problem with a unique solution ensures convergence of the PINN. It is, in a sense, the
”ultimate” case, where no observations are available, thus simply utilizing the information
from the PDE at the collocation points along with known boundary conditions.
4.1.4
Virtual ground stations
The first set of experiments, in an attempt to emulate an inverse application in geomag-
netics, is inspired by that carried out by Fournier et al. [5]. Observations of both the
magnetic and core velocity fields are performed at ns = 10 virtual ground stations placed
equidistant throughout the spatial domain, with assumed measurements at all epochs.
As in the geomagnetic case, our ground stations provide long, high-resolution time series
at fixed locations, but an incomplete spatial sampling. We explore two different sampling
methods of the fields; regular and irregular spatial sampling.
Regular spatial sampling implies that ground stations are sampled evenly throughout the
full domain, whereas for irregular sampling ground stations are sampled evenly in only one
half of the domain xobs ∈[−1, 0] or xobs ∈[0, 1]. All observations contain noise according
to the previous description. An example of the observed data is visualized in Figure 4.1,
where the time series of each ground station is represented by the white dotted lines
plotted on top of the true core velocity field from the benchmark forward numerical run.

4.1.5
Virtual satellites
Since 1999 continuous measurements of Earth’s magnetic field are available from satel-
lites due to multiple successful missions (previous and ongoing), such as Ørsted, CHAMP
and Swarm [3]. Observations from satellites, contrary to ground station, are of high spa-
tial resolution but cover only a relatively short time period compared to the dynamical
timescales of interest [5]. This motivates us to perform another set of experiments, in-
troducing observations in the form of virtual satellites instead of ground stations. Despite
satellites only measuring the magnetic field, one can gain knowledge of the core velocity
field by inverting the magnetic induction equation, Equation 1.2, under the assumption of
good spatial coverage of the satellite data. Therefore, for virtual satellite observations, we
can utilize knowledge of the magnetic field and the core velocity field (with larger errors)
during training of the PINN.
We assume that satellite observations are made in a 10% interval of the full temporal
domain, where 95% (randomly sampled) of the data points constitute the observed data.
Similar to the virtual ground stations, noise is added.
Utilizing virtual satellites we propose two different experiments, with different satellite inte-
gration epochs. In the first experiment, satellites are present for epochs Tsat ∈[0.36, 0.4].
Here we investigate how well the PINN is able to describe the full Spatio-temporal domain,
with specific attention given to the epochs just before satellite integration time.
For the second experiment, we move the satellite integration time from Tsat ∈[0.36, 0.4] →
[0.26, 0.3]. The idea behind this is to assume that T = 0.3 is the current epoch (today),
with T = 0 being e.g 1900, then utilizing a PINN along with satellite observations and the
governing equations, how well are we able to explore the ability of the PINN to predict the
future evolution of the fields. Therefore, more attention will be given to epochs just after
the satellite integration time. We denote the experiments ”Satellite general” and ”Satellite
future prediction” respectively.

Combination of observations

Summary of experiments on the inverse problem
Here, we present a summary of the most important findings for the experiments performed
using PINNs for inverse modelling.
Training the PINN without any observations of either field, it is, as expected, not able to
62
Physics Informed Neural Network for Modelling Earths Core Dynamics
==PAGE:81==
reproduce the behaviour. Instead simple functions satisfying the governing equations with
known boundaries are obtained, as seen in subsection 4.2.1.
Having information available for both fields in the form of evenly spaced virtual ground
stations covering the entire time domain, the PINN is able to well reproduce the fields.
When ground stations are concentrated in one half of the spatial domain, the relative
L2 error increases drastically as soon as information is no longer available, as seen in
Figure 4.9. No experiments including ground stations are able to reproduce the sharp
gradient near the lower boundary, with the closest station being placed at x ≈−0.9.
For dense satellite observations with knowledge of both fields for a limited time period,
the PINNs are able to almost fully reproduce both fields within satellite integration time. In
addition, the knowledge gained during satellite integration propagates backwards in time,
as shown in Figure 4.12 and 4.13, where the L2 error steadily decreases toward the time
when the satellite data becomes available, with the largest errors found at or near the ICs.
For the problem at hand, it was also demonstrated that the PINN was able to retain knowl-
edge for future epochs, as solutions after satellite integration were close to similar levels
of error. Such predictions could have important applications. Further tests are needed
to determine whether this is an inherent characteristic of the PINN approach, or simply a
result of the core velocity field being less complex, with not nearly as sharp gradients due
to diffusion in the studied problem. Additional experiments should be performed with a
different field evolution to verify the predictive abilities of PINNs.
A slight increase in the quality of the solution for the core velocity field was seen, when
adding more temporal knowledge of only the magnetic field in the form of 10 magnetic
ground stations evenly spaced throughout the full spatial domain.
By only observing the magnetic field, for both satellites and ground stations, the PINN is
only able to reproduce the overall trends in the U-field, by fitting a curve, but does not
reproduce the sharp gradients in the velocity field and their propagation, as seen in Fig-
ure 4.15 and 4.17. Even at points, where the magnetic field has been observed, this does
not translate into a better description of the core velocity field at these locations. This con-
clusion may be a consequence of the control parameters used in the MHD system, where
the magnetic field evolution is dominated by diffusion, hence its very smooth structure.
Despite this, if only observations on the magnetic field are available, they still positively
effect the result on the velocity field.
Throughout all experiments, the overall L2 error is dominated by the sharp gradient near
the lower boundary layer.
From these experiments we conclude, that in order to accurately resolve the core velocity
field, knowledge in the form of satellite observations along with magnetic ground stations
is needed. Given such observations, it is possible to use the PINN to predict the field and
flow behaviour a short time into the future.

5
Discussion
In this chapter the results documented in the previous two chapters are discussed with a
focus on possible future extensions and improvements.
5.1
Forward modelling
In chapter 3 we arrived at a simple and efficient PINN setup that is able to successfully
solve the system of interest, in a much shorter time compared to the previous work by
Rasmus C. Jørgensen in [2]. The current implementation was found to perform well with
an error of L2(U) = 0.242, except for close to the lower boundary, where a very sharp
gradient in the velocity field is present, see subsection 3.2.1.
During the early stages of the thesis, some initial tests were done wrt. the depth of the
network. No significant improvements were found at this time, however no tests were
carried out on the width of the layers (i.e. the number of hidden unites per layer), thus it
is possible, that this might improve on its ability to possibly catch sharp gradients.
Throughout the thesis, we have kept a fixed number of iterations during training, both for
the optimizers as well as for the implemented adaptive strategy. However, a few studies
discuss the implementation of various possible stopping criteria for the L-BFGS optimizer
[18, 26], as well as stopping criteria for each adaptive resampling iteration in [22]. We do
not expect implementing stopping criteria to have a significant difference for our training,
as most tests have converged in the sense of the loss curves have levelled out. A small
reduction in training time might be possible though, as earlier stopping of the computa-
tionally expensive L-BFGS optimizer might be possible.This might also open up for the
possibility of implementing the L-BFGS optimizer through all resampling iterations, which
has not been the case so far, due to the computational time. It would be interesting to test
this, but we do not expect major improvements.
5.2
Inverse modelling
In chapter 4 we demonstrated that given incomplete and noisy observations our PINN
modelling setup presented in section 3.3, accommodated to account for these obser-
vations, is able to retrieve knowledge on the unobserved state of the system, including
predictions at times beyond where observations are available (see subsection 4.2.5 and
subsection 4.2.7)
So far all experiments have been carried out based on the same numerical forward model.
Therefore, we can not be certain how well the method developed here will generalize to
other conditions. Although we expect the developed approach to work well in other sce-
narios, and perhaps even better in cases when the magnetic field evolution is not so
smooth and gradients in the velocity field are not so sharp, however, it should be tested,
to ensure that our method is applicable across different settings of the problem. It will be
left as future work.
It would certainly be interesting to test a more realistic configuration of the magnetic field.
So far, we have worked with a very smooth magnetic field, which in fact is not entirely
realistic. In reality, the geomagnetic field contains more variations within the spatial do-
main, and this could possibly lead to more information on the entire state (U, B) from
observations of B alone.

5.3
Uncertainty Quantification
Following the encouraging inverse modelling results obtained in subsection 4.2.6 (with
knowledge on the core velocity field during satellite integration), the major challenge from
our perspective is now how to use this to provide probabilistic information on the likely state
of the system, given the observations, governing equations, and boundary conditions.
This comes down to quantifying the uncertainty in the inverse modelling results. How to
do this in the context of a neural networks is an active area of research.
Deep neural networks have become increasingly popular within a multitude of fields, giv-
ing rise to highly efficient and accurate predictions e.g. computer vision tasks, medical
diagnosis etc. However, rarely is the predictive nature of the applied models questioned,
and instead, high predictions are often presented as the be all and end all measure for
a model’s efficiency. Reliance on such overconfident predictions might be harmful, as
they are prone to both noisy measurements or model inference errors [27]. Uncertainty
quantification is by no means an easy task for NNs, and a plethora of different techniques
have been explored, highly dependent on both data, model, scoring rule etc.; an extensive
review of Uncertainty Quantification (UQ) in NN is presented in [27].
In this section we make a preliminary attempt to quantify (some part of) the uncertainty
inherent in the inverse modelling networks we have developed. Due to time constraints for
the thesis, we have chosen to implement only a very simple initial assessment towards
this, and is presented in subsection 5.3.1, followed by perspectives for how this could
be improved in the future in subsection 5.3.2. In addition a short ”review” of different
methods, which could possibly be applied to the current problem is given at the end in
section subsection 5.3.3.
5.3.1
A simple empirical uncertainty quantification by ensemble of neural
networks.
In an initial assessment towards UQ of the developed PINN of section 3.3 used (with
modifications) in subsection 4.1.6, we tested out a simple method of training an ensemble
of NNs and used the empirical mean and variance from the ensemble to measure an
approximate estimate of predictive uncertainty. Note, that this is in fact not a true Bayesian
nor probabilistic method, and we do realize its shortcomings of not properly representing
the true posterior. However, it does acknowledge the fact that there is uncertainty in the
inversion results and is a first step toward better approaches to characterize this.
We trained an ensemble of 40 identical PINNs, following the final implementation summa-
rized in section 3.3, where a different weight initialization was used for each network. All
PINNs were trained on the exact same observed data: Ns = 10 virtual magnetic ground
stations and observed magnetic- and core velocity fields from satellite data at epochs
T ∈[0.26, 0.3] with 95% coverage. All observations are noisy according to Equation 4.1
and Equation 4.2. The governing equations were implemented using a loss term eval-
uated at Nf = 79000 randomly sampled interior collocation points (although the same
randomly sampled collocation points for each networks), and the BCs at Nb = 2 · 198 on
the boundary.
For each of the 40 models produced, a forward pass is performed for the entire Spatio-
temporal domain to predict the fields. The empirical mean and variance of the models are
computed, in order to get an estimate of the predictive uncertainty. We present snapshots
at three different times to represent the uncertainty at the IC, T = 0, before satellite
integration, T = 0.2, and at the final observed satellite epoch, T = 0.3. The resulting
predictive uncertainties are shown in Figure 5.1, where the black line is the true field, the
red line indicates the mean of the ensemble, with a 2 standard deviation interval seen
66

as the red shaded area to represent a 95% confidence interval. On each subplot, the
magnetic ground stations, where data is available, have been plotted as black crosses.
In general we find the true solution of the core velocity field lies within, or close to, the
inverse model uncertainty envelope, except in the region of sharp gradients close to the
lower boundary layer.
The largest uncertainties for both fields are found at the IC, which matches the results from
the previous chapter. Larger confidence intervals are also found near sharp gradients for
the core velocity field throughout the full domain. It seems that the method is somewhat
able to capture all gradients, except for the sharp one near the lower boundary layer, and
at the IC. We also observe, that uncertainties decrease, when satellite data is available
of the core velocity field.
Even though ground stations observe magnetic data throughout the domain, this does not
lead to less uncertainty near ground station locations in the predicted magnetic field, as
seen Figure 5.1b. However, the uncertainty on the magnetic field is smaller at later times
(near or at satellite integration).

Additional methods
A number of other methods have been proposed for measuring uncertainty in NN, and
only a few that may be relevant for the current problem involving PINNs are summarized

Adversarial Uncertainty Quantification in PINN’s
An approach for measuring uncertainty in NNs is to use inference tools like Virtual Au-
toencoders (VAEs) [32], or Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [33]. Utilizing such
techniques in the setting of a PINN has been suggested in [34], where a probabilistic (vari-
ational inference) framework for scaling the uncertainty propagation in a physical system
is presented. Originally, this method was the preferred way for us to go in this thesis, due
to its successful applicability on a PINN. Unfortunately, due to time constraints, it was not
possible to implement it within the time frame of the thesis. We nevertheless present the
method and will leave it as future work.
The adversarial approach to UQ begins with a probabilistic formulation using a latent
variable model of the form [34]:

U or B. If this is to be implemented a probabilistic formulation for each field is required.
The idea is to match two joint distributions, one each from the observed data following a
distribution q(x, t, u) and newly generated data following distribution pθ(x, t, u), by mini-
mizing the reverse Kullback-Leibler divergence between the two, KL [pθ(x, t, u)∥q(x, t, u)]
during training of the network.
To do this a few extensions need to be introduced in the form of a Generator function,
Discriminator function, and an encoder function, all parameterized by deep neural net-
works [34]. The Generators job is to generate ”fake” samples (x, t, fθ(x, t, z)) obtained
from the generative model distribution pθ(x, t, u) by a deterministic function fθ using the
latent space variable, z.
The Discriminators job is to learn the ability to discriminate between these ”fake” gener-
ated samples and the true samples from the observed data distribution q(x, t, u), which is
done by constructing a binary classifier, Tψ(x, t, u).
Finally the encoder (an inference model), qθ(z | x, t, fθ(x, t, z)), is used as a variational
approximation to the true intractable posterior over the latent variables pθ(z | x, t, u) [34].

Monte Carlo Dropout as Bayesian approximation
Another method for quantifying uncertainty in a NN that has been discussed in the liter-
ature is a method called Monte Carlo Dropout as Bayesian approximation, that was first
proposed in [31]. Similar to the proposed improvement described in subsection 5.3.2, it
is also a popular method, due to its simplicity and low computational cost compared to
other approximations of Bayesian Neural Networks such as techniques within variational
inference [31].
Dropout was first used in [35] as a regularization method to avoid over-fitting, by using a
Bernoulli distribution, zi,j Bernoulli(pi) for i = 1, ...L, j = 1, ..., Ki−j, given some proba-
bility pi on all weights in each layer. Here L is the number of layers, and K the number
of hidden units in each respective layer. This effectively randomly drops the connections
between hidden units, forcing the network to learn the pattern of the input through the
active units at each given training iteration.
This same idea is utilized in the MC-Droput Bayesian approximation method, where it
has been shown, that for any NN, no matter depth or non-linearities, by applying dropout
at the weight layer (through training and inference) it is mathematically equivalent to an
approximation of a probabilistic Gaussian process [31]; simplified probabilistic models
that can be used to approximate the posterior distribution. It is shown in [31] that dropout,
in effect, minimizes the Kullback-Leibler divergence between an approximate distribution
and the posterior of a deep Gaussian process.
In practice, the statistical moments of the posterior distribution can then be estimated
through a collection, T, of stochastic forward passes (after training) through the network,
each with a different realisation from the Bernoulli distribution, zt

Conclusion
In this thesis, by revisiting an initial PINN implementation proposed by R. Jørgensen [1,
2] for studying a prototype geomagnetic problem, we found that a much simpler reference
PINN setup, without the need for causal training, can achieve similar performance metrics,
in a much shorter time span. We demonstrated that small improvements to this perfor-
mance can be made by: implementing batch training of the Interior Collocation points,
using an optimizer setup of an ADAM optimizer followed by an L-BFGS optimizer to fine-
tune the parameters, better balancing of the loss function focusing on prioritizing the IC,
then the BC and finally the governing equations, and finally by using an adaptive sampling
strategy to resampling the interior collocation points close to where the largest errors were
found. A final performance metric of L2 = 0.242 was obtained for the core velocity field
training for 2 hours, compared to L2 = 0.738 and 56 hours taken in [2] to reach a similar
level. Despite the improvement, it was still not possible to fully reproduce the behaviour
of the core velocity field near the lower boundary, where sharp gradients were present.
Utilizing this setup, we have further demonstrated that the PINN is able to accurately re-
solve the core velocity field if dense satellite observations of both the magnetic- and core
velocity fields are present for a limited period of time. Adding more temporal knowledge
via magnetic ground stations evenly spaced throughout the spatial domain improved the
solution slightly. In addition, it was shown that the PINN is able to retain knowledge from
epochs of dense satellite observations and make useful predictions of the field’s future
evolution. This observation is encouraging and can lead to possibly important applica-
tions. We expect this to be an inherent characteristic of the PINN approach, however
additional experiments with a different test field should be performed to verify this finding.
We also found that the PINN is not able to infer complete knowledge of the detailed dy-
namics of the core velocity field, solely from magnetic observations, at least for the forward
model setup here, where the magnetic field is smooth. However, it is able to reproduce
the overall trend, thus magnetic observations do positively affect the result on the velocity
field.
It is not inconceivable, that the results of the PINN can be improved by making the net-
work wider (adding more hidden units), hopefully making it able to better represent sharp
gradients and their evolution.
Additionally, prospects of implementing a Bayesian approach to measuring predictive un-
certainty in the posterior distribution in the form of a generative model seem encouraging,
since other studies have implemented such an approach for PINN applications, such as
[34], with positive results. At the very least we have shown that implementing a Bayesian
approximation to the posterior using Deep Ensembles of Gaussian mixtures is a simple
and feasible solution for UQ in PINNs.
