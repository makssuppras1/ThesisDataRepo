Abstract 
Baggrund: Hjerterytmevariabilitet (HRV) er defineret som forskellen i tiden mellem to på hinanden 
følgende hjerteslag (RR interval). HRV er samtidig et mål for samspillet mellem det sympatiske (SNS) – og 
parasympatiske nervesystem (pSNS). SNS og pSNS danner tilsammen det autonomiske nervesystem (ANS ) 
hvis primære opgave er at sørge for at kroppens indre funktioner altid fungerer mest optimalt og 
opretholde homeosate. Dette  inkluderer bland andet  regulering af blodtryk, puls, temperatur. ANS er 
automatisk reguleret, hvilket betyder at det er udenfor egen viljes. Der er indenfor nyere tid fundet beviser 
for at social interaktion kan føre til ændringer i ANS og det der kan ses en vis synkroni i denne ændring 
mellem de pågældende personer. I dette studie bliver det undersøgt hvorvidt HRV modificeres ved social 
interaktion samt om denne modifikation er anderledes ved en vellykket interaktion end ved en mindre 
vellykket interaction.  
Metode: Metoden der blev anvendt var  et socialt eksperiment, opstillet som et ’speed dating event’. Tre 
kvinder og tre mænd deltog i hver runde, hvor de mødte tre personer af det modsatte køn på et 
stævnemøde med varighed af fem minutter. Mellem hver stævnemøde blev der målt fem minutters 
’baseline’. Forsøget blev kørt tre gange hvoraf en kvinde udeblev; i alt deltog ni mænd og otte kvinder i 
alderen 20-29 år. Alle deltagere bar under forløbet en pulsmåler, som målte deres R-R interval, og data 
derfra blev klippet til segmenter af fem minutter indeholdende ’baselines’ og stævnemøder, som blev brugt 
i analysen. Deltagerne havde ydermere fået udleveret et vurderingsskema, der skulle udfyldes i 
overensstemmelse med det indtryk daten havde givet. Der blev lavet statistisk analyse på, om hvorvidt der 
var signfikant forskel mellem ’baseline’ og stævnemøde, mellem højt vurderet stævnemøde og lavt 
vurderet stævnemøde samt om der var signifikan forskel i interaktionen mellem disse to faktorer. Som 
tilføjelse blev det testet om hvorvidt der kunne findes signifikant forskel mellem første date og anden 
stævnemøde.  
Resultat: Der blev fundet en stigning i lav frekves/høj frekvens fordelingen  (LF/HF ratio ) under 
stævnemøderne sammenlinget med i hvile, uanset om det var højst rangede eller lavest rangerede 
stævnemøde. Ligeledes blev der fundet en signifant højere puls under stævnemøderne sammenlignet med 
i hvile. Som tilføjelse blev der fundet en forandring af påvirkningen på HRV over tid, repræsenteret ved 
niveauet af LF.  
Diskussion: Den lille stikprøvestørrelse gør det svært at konkludere et resultat på en valid baggrund. 
Endvidere, idet det ikke lykkedes at få de først antagede forudsætninger, som var at sammenligne en 
successfuld date med ikke ikke succesfuld date, er det ikke til at sige hvorvidt der ville være et resultat hvis 
disse forudsætninger i mindre eller større grad havde været opfyldt.  Signifikant stigning i LF/HF kan betyde 
stigning i det sympatiske nervesystem, som normalt er forbundet med situationer hvor hjernen eller

kroppen skal arbejde eller er stresset. Stigningen kan skyldes nervøsitet eller spændig. Envidere blev der

personerne snakkede og/eller grinede under stævnemødet. Den øgede LF komponent kan fortolkes som en

Konklussion: Det kan konkluderes at der var en forhøjet aktivitet i det sympatiske nervesystem under

som kunne danne baggrund for en udvidet undersøgelse.

1. Introduktion 
1.1 Motivation  
Physical activity causes the heart rate and the body temperature to increase, but sometimes the heart 
pumps slightly faster for other reason than physical activity. The reasons could be many, but there is a great 
chance that it is caused by bodily regulations from the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS). The ANS is the 
system that ensure the inner homeostasis and secures that the body works most efficiently given a current 
situation. The ANS has been found to not only constantly regulate inner functions in the body but 
investigations also found evidence proving that physiological mechanism in the ANS changes when people 
bond with others and also that it synchronizes somewhat (Palumbo, 2016). There still exist a lack in the 
knowledge around the exact mechanisms behind the synchrony and why it occurs (Palumbo, 2016). The 
synchrony are found by different methods, as the ANS regulates a lot of measureable functions, including 
heart rate, respiration, blood pressure, body temperature and Heart Rate Variability (HRV). Some measures 
are very individual why it is not possible to compare them directly in between people. By comparing in 
relation to the person’s baseline, it is possible to discover whether a synchrony appears in certain 
situations. The concept of Interpersonal Autonomic Physiology (IAP) indicates this by the term 
‘physiological synchrony’ (PS) (Palumbo, 2016). PS has been found among e.g. mothers and their infants 
(Puyvelde, 2015), between couples (Ferrer, 2013), and between teammates (Henning, 2001). Also between 
strangers a synchrony was found in a study where people in dyads met for the first time for a five minute 
period (Silver, 2004). It is very interesting to understand how social bonds are formed, for example, when 
people meet for the first time. This study will investigate a situation similar to, by measuring the HRV from 
a group of people meeting for the first time on speed dates. HRV is used as measurement as it is an easy 
and accessible method. Today’s heart rate monitors are developed into, with an algorithm, being able to 
convert the ECG signal directly into RR intervals where from the HRV can be calculated. 
1.1.1 Heart Rate Variability 
Heart Rate Variabilty (HRV) is defined as the difference in time between two adjacent heart beats and are 
calculated from R-R interval between two adjacent R peaks detected in an ECG. The many calculations of 
HRV can provide information about the individual parts of ANS, the Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS), the 
Parasympathetic Nervous System (pSNS) and the balance between the two (Task Force, 1996). This gives a 
picture of the interaction between the brain and the ANS as it is a part of the Periphery Nervous System 
(PNS) (Schaffer, 2014). The clinic use of HRV spans widely including as a predictor of different somatic 
illnesses like myocardial dysfunction and diabetes as they are is highly correlated with a low HRV (Task 
Force, 1996). The HRV is also measured when investigating in psychopathology, to discover how different 
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
conditions effects the ANS and vice versa. Psychiatric conditions like depression or anxiety often are 
correlated with changes in ANS, and especially decreased resting high frequency HRV (Kemp, 2014). HRV is 
no longer for clinical use only, today HRV has evolved to become a common used term in relation to 
training.  It has been approved as a tool to measure the stress level in the body (Altini, 2016). This has been 
an opportunity to developing several applications available for smartphones, tablets and smartwatches that 
cooperates with heart rate monitors to prevent people from overtraining their bodies, based on the SNS 
and PSNS balance (Altini, 2016).  
1.1.2 Speed-dating 
Speed dating is an innovative, very useful way to investigate romantic relation between people and how 
people choose a partner. Before speed-dating was invented most research in people’s choice of partner 
took place in a laboratory where the participant could fill out papers about their preferences, they did not 
meet the potential partner at any point (Finkel, 2007). With speed-dating, the research in romance and 
attraction has come to a whole new level.  The speed-dating studies gives the opportunity to investigate in 
natural surroundings in real time and compared to earlier study design where the participant reported the 
preferences of a potential partner it is now possible to investigate the social bonding and connection 
between the two involved in the date. Speed-dating also gives an opportunity to collect a lot of statistical 
data at once which gives a strong statistical basis to identify general statements in social interaction 
(Eastwick, 2008). Different studies have had interesting findings e.g. it was found that female’s selectivity 
increases with the group size and that the fear of being turned down can affect the way a person choose. A 
lot of studies had investigated which criteria males and females choose their partner from and additionally 
researches have examined whether preferences for a potential partner differed for long term relationships 
compared to the initial desire. Another study has been made where the participants had to specify their 
preferences prior to the speed-dating, have shown that often these were not consistent with their choice 
(Finkel, 2007). Speed-dating was invented in US in the late 1990s and was originally a help to get the Jewish 
people to meet (Finkel, 2007). Speed-dating is a modern way of meeting with a lot of new people within a 
short period of time. The main purpose of speed-dating is as alternative to regular dating, but the dates last 
much less time and there will be incurred several dates in one evening. Speed-dating is typically made as 
age grouped events, where the participants register and pay a certain price to participate. The organizers 
will makes sure that the ratio of male and female participants is as close to one as possible to avid 
participants having to skip a turn. A speed-dating event can contain a varying number of participants which 
determines the number of dates each person will take part in. Each date last for approximately 4-10 
minutes in which the participants have to decide whether they wish to see the person again. The answer is 
noted on a personal piece of paper, after each date. Between two dates the contestants rotate to a new

partner. Once all dates have been completed participants are informed of whether there are have been any 
matches. To have a match both daters must confirm an interest on the paper. Besides being used to find a 
new romantic partner, speed-dating is also used by companies and educational institutions as a way of 
network and finding project partners.  Speed-dating can be compared to meeting someone in a bar or at a 
party but in an organized speed-dating event people have the same reason to be there (Finkel, 2007). This 
study uses the speed-dating concept to discover whether a change in the ANS, measured by HRV, can be 
found within the interaction on a date and whether it matters if the date is successful or unsuccessful. To 
understand how HRV can reflect the ANS it is crucial to understand the underlying physiology. 
 
1.2 Physiology 
1.2.1 The heart 
As mentioned, the HRV is the variability in the frequency at which the heart beats. The heart is a muscle 
designed to pump the blood through the body to ensure all cells are provided with oxygen. It pumps 
approximately five liters of blood every minute via the cardiac cycle: The heart receives deoxygenated blood 
in its right atrium and pumps it from its right ventricle through the pulmonary circuit where the lungs 
oxygenates the blood. The oxygenated blood returns to the hearts left atrium, on to the left ventricle and 
then it is send into the systemic circuit through aorta. It is all done in one heartbeat. (Seeley, 2008) A 
heartbeat is initiated by electrical stimulation of the sinus node from the ANS. (Seeley, 2008)  ANS is a part 
of the peripheral nervous system and consists of the SNS and the pSNS also called vagus. The SNS is the 
promoting system while pSNS is the inhibiting system and together they ensure the maintenance of 
homeostasis, which amongst other involves regulation of blood pressure, blood oxygen, temperature and 
heart rate.  SNS is activated when the body is in some kind of stressed situations and the body needs to be 
prepared to ‘fight or flight’ and thus increases the blood pressure, heartrate and the supply of blood to the 
muscles. PSNS is responsible for ‘rest and digest’. It is most dominant when the body is at rest or asleep. It 
helps the body restore, by promoting the digests, increase the absorption of nutrients and decreasing the 
heart rate. A resting heart rate of a healthy adult is approximately 70-80 BPM (Beats Per Minute). (Seeley, 
2008)  The ANS is controlled unconsciously, which is the opposite from the somatic nervous system which is 
volitional. The somatic nervous system is responsible for registering external inputs and stimulations using 
sensory neurons and ensure the body respond to them using motor neurons (e.g. touching a hot stove makes 
you move your hand). The way ANS controlling the heart rate is by innervating the sinus node when it gets 
an efferent signal from the cardiac control center. The cardiac control center is placed in the medulla 
oblongata gets an afferent signal from the baroreceptors about the blood pressure or from the

chemoreceptors about the bloods oxygen content. The cardiac control center will send back an efferent signal 
depending on whether there is a need to inhibit or promote and the signal is either send to the pSNS or the 
SNS which then innervates the sinus node (Seeley, 2008).  
SNS stimulates the sinus node through the thoracic efferent nerves . Norepinephrine is released to the sinus 
node, which opens Na2+ - and K+ channels on the membrane. When the channels open, there is an influx of 
positive ions, which results in a shortening of the repolarization and an increase in the rate spontaneous 
depolarization, which increases the heart rate and the muscular contraction of. When pSNS nerves stimulates 
the sinus node acetylcholine is released by the vagus nerve, causing the K+ channels in the plasma membrane 
to open and K+ ions leave the cell, which means that the heart rate decreases as it draws out the 
depolarization and extend the duration of repolarization (Seeleys, 2008). The response time of the sinus node 
is very short when stimulated by pSNS. An impulse will cause an immediately response within the cardiac 
cycle in which it occurs and affects only one or two heartbeats. When the heart is stimulated by the SNS the 
response time is up to 5 seconds before the HR increases progressively and will reach a steady level in 20–

1.3 Heart Rate Variability (HRV) 
 HRV is defined as the variation in time between two adjacent R-R intervals. The R-R intervals are the 
distance in time between two R peaks in the QRS-complex in an ECG. R-R interval is also called the Normal 
to Normal interval (N-N interval) or the Interbeat Interval (IBI) (Task Force, 1996; Shaffer, 2014). HRV is a 
method to measure the body's ability to adapt to its surroundings (Berntson, 2007). It reflects the function 
of ANS, as the HRV is caused by fluctuations in dynamic balance between the SNS activity and the pSNS 
activity. It is important to notice when recording HRV, that it is highly correlated with Respiratory Sinus 
Arrhythmia (RSA) which is the variability in the heart rate caused by respiration. It can be influenced by 
moderate physically activity and thus experiments including physically activity in the record period, must 
calculated the respiration (Allen, 2006). The different calculations of HRV either uses the R-R intervals or 
the difference between the R-R intervals, and each provides information about the various functions of 
ANS. To ensure comparability between HRV measurements the recommended standard of HRV 
measurements are 24 hours as long term recordings, and five minutes as short term recording (Task Force, 
1996).  
The diverse calculations of HRV are differently appropriate for long term recordings, or for short term 
recordings or segments from long term recording, some calculations can be used in more contexts.  Some 
of the most common used calculations are time domain measures and frequency domain measures (Task 
Force, 1996).  
1.3.1 The time domain measures.   
The time domain measures are mostly used as a statistical overview of the time variance between R-R 
intervals. The SDNN is the standard deviation of the R-R intervals i.e. how much the R-R intervals varies from 
the mean value during the in whole measuring period (Task Force, 1996). The interpretation of SDNN does 
not give any directly information of the physiological mechanism of the ANS alone, but the value reflects all 
factors that influences the HRV and, according to Otzenberger, H. et al. (1998), SDNN is found to be strongly 
correlated to the low frequency component from the spectral analysis (Otzenberger, 1998). SDNN makes it 
easy to compare the different segments. The SDNN is increasing according to the length of the recording, 
which is one reason to the standards (Task Force).

1.3.2. The frequency domain measures 
The frequency domain or Power Spectral Density (PSD) measurements describes the oscillations in the heart 
rate divided into spectral frequencies. PSD provides information about the power distribution and includes 
both amplitude and frequency for a given rhythm in the HRV waveform.  There are parametric- and non-
parametric methods. The non-parametric methods are simple and rapid, while the parametric methods gives 
a smoother curve which makes it easier to identify the central frequency of each component. The parametric 
methods are more complex and requires a control of the suitability of the chosen model (Task Force, 1996; 
Shaffer, 2014).  
One non-parametric methods is the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) (figure 2). FFTis a PSD algorithm which 
presents the different components with peaks that reflect the power of the four frequency bands expressed 
as the square root of the areas under the power spectrum. The components are HF, LF, VLF and in long term 
recordings, ULF. The different frequency bands in the specter reflects the activity of the different parts of the 
ANS functions (Peltola, 2012).

The High Frequency (HF) band has the frequency 0.15-0.4Hz. It reflects the pSNS activity and are thus most 
dominant in resting and while sleeping. HF is also known as the respiratory band as it is highly correlated to 
the respiratory variance of the HRV under resting conditions; when inhaling, the heartrate increases and 
when exhaling the heartrate decreases. The respiratory variance is popularly called Respiratory Sinus 
Arrhythmia (RSA) which is defined as the respiratory variability of the heart rate and is used as the 
approximation of efferent activity of vagal node to the sinus node but can be affected by physical activity 
(Allen, 2006). Increased HF has also been found to correlate to autonomic flexibility (Shahrestani, 2015), 
socially competences and empathy (Eisenberg, 2008).  
Low Frequency (LF) has the frequency 0.04-0.15Hz. It has not been perfectly clear whether the LF reflects the 
SNS activity or both the SNS and the pSNS activity, but research has found that LF is related to both as 
investigations have found a decrease in LF during sympathetic arousal (Sztajzel, 2004; Task Force, 1996; 
Stauss, 2003). Another finding about the LF HRV is that it represents the baroreceptor’s activity (short term 
regulations of blood pressure, to maintain a mean arterial blood pressure). A Baroreceptor is a 
mechanoreceptor, sensitive to stretch in the arterial blood vessels. There a two types; atrial baroreceptors 
and low-pressure baroreceptors. The atrial baroreceptors are sensitive to high blood pressure, while the 
latter are sensitive to low blood pressure. When there is a change in the normal blood pressure the 
baroreceptor detects it and increases the firing of afferent signal to the cardiac regulation center in medulla 
oblongata to either increase or decrease the blood pressure. Low blood pressure causes the sympathetic 
activity to increase and inhibits the parasympathetic activity which raises the heart rate and contractility. A 
high blood pressure will decrease the sympathetic activity and increase the parasympathetic activity to 
decrease the heartrate and thus the blood pressure (Sealeys, 2008; Shaffer, 2014).

1.3.3 Pre-processing of HRV  
HRV recordings are mostly, imperfect and contains disturbance from technical equipment and abnormal 
heartbeats. These ectopic heartbeats are expressed on an ECG as abnormal waves in the QRS-complex. 
Cleaning or editing the data for these disturbances is important to avoid bias in the results. Leaving ectopic 
beats unedited, may bias the HRV power spectrum by increasing the power of HF or cause incorrectly 
higher values of the SDNN of the R–R intervals (Peltola, 2012). The method of editing the dataset, must 
depend on how much disturbance is found and what the data is used for. No standard is developed for the 
maximum of edited RR interval, but most investigators recommends not to use dataset at all, with more 
than 20 % artifact RR intervals (Peltola, 2012). The pre-processing of a dataset can include deletion or 
interpolation of the abnormal values. The deleting method is by default dissuaded in short term recordings 
as the effect to the HRV can be significant. There exist different interpolation algorithms, this study uses 
linear interpolation where the value is replaced by values calculated from the linear relationship between 
each RR interval and interpolation of degree zero which substitutes the abnormal R-R interval with the 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
mean of the two neighbor values (Peltola, 2012)..Other methods could be cubic spline –or - non-linear 
interpolation or by using different kinds of filters (Peltola, 2012). 
 
1.4 Social interaction 
Social interaction is a lot of things. Humans constantly coordinating their actions in relations to others is 
social interaction during childhood when they are building Lego towers together they exhibit the ability to 
interact with others in a joint task. That is also social interaction.  
When people collaborate on a joint task the way they interacts are essential for the result. A joint task is 
easier when it is allowed to talk and use body language as that is the most effective way to communicate. 
But even if people are not able see each other they still manage to achieve a result. Successful 
collaboration depends on the mutual ability to predict actions and consequences of these action from the 
others involved. Humans tend to have this ability to a great extent e.g. an investigation with two people 
who was placed seated side by side and asked to complete a task on a computer while not being able to see 
what each other did. The research showed that the participant very quickly predicted each other actions 
and included it in their own actions. There still exists a lack in knowledge of the cognitive and neural 
processes happening during joint actions (Sebanz, 2006).  This is interesting in relation to this study as the 
interaction between individuals depends on how the individuals respond to each other.  
Interpersonal Autonomic Physiology (IAP) is another way to investigate social interaction. IAP is according 
to Palumbo, R. et al. (2016), defined as "the relationship between people’s physiological dynamics, as 
indexed by continuous measures of the autonomic nervous system". (Palumbo, 2016) The concept of IAP 
indicates that functions related to the ANS, may be affected in social interactions and reveals consistency in 
physiological processes between two or more persons in different contexts or with different relationships. 
These associations are also called Physiological Synchrony (PS). Investigators are trying to connect the 
synchrony to specific physiological mechanism but while it has been possible to find positive results of the 
synchrony it is found to be much more difficult to find the mechanisms generating PS (Palumbo, 2016). The 
use of ANS in psychological research is important in the aspect of investigate how the human body reacts in 
social aspect cognitively and behavior. Various methods are used to measure the PS including heart rate, 
blood pressure, skin conductance and HRV. These measurements are all indicators of the functions in the 
ANS and proves consistency in the dynamics. When doing research in IAP it is crucial to know what exactly 
to be examined in terms of finding the right type of measurement as they each have their own 
characteristics i.e. heart reflects overall ANS, while e.g. RSA provides more specific information about pSNS 
(Palumbo, 2016). Further it is important to ensure that found effect is not caused by the surroundings or 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
other factors (e.g. A homogeny group of people playing the same video game can be effected in the same 
way due to the way the video game is aimed to influence people) (Palumbo, 2016). Studies made to detect 
PA between people includes several kinds of relationships between the contestants, e.g. Ferrer and Helm, 
found HR to synchronize between couples, Van Puyvelde et al. (2015), investigated the RSA between 
mother and child (Puyvelde, 2015), Armstead H. et al., (2009) and Henning, R., (2001) found PS in HRV in a 
larger teams (Armstead, 2009; Henning, 2001) and also Silver et al, (2004), found large significant PS in skin 
conductance in between strangers (Silver, 2004). This study also use strangers that is sat up on a speed 
date. The aim is to find if any effect of the social interaction is be found in the HRV among the participants.  
1.4 Hypothesis 
The hypothesis is that an interaction between two people can affect the ANS, expressed by a change in the 
HRV. More specifically: 
I. 
Attending on a date will have a main effect on the HRV compared to the baseline. 
II. 
A successful date will have a main effect on HRV compared to an unsuccessful date. 
It is expected to see an increasing sympathetic activity during a social interaction, as the participants have 
been expose to a stressed situation. And the parasympathetic activity will be decreased represented as a 
higher LF and LF/HF ratio. During baseline the parasympathetic will dominate which means a lower LF level 
and increased HF. The effect of successful date compared to unsuccessful date would be reflected as a higher 
sympathetic activity during the highest rated date compared to the lowest rated date. 
2. Method 
2.1 Study design 
This study was designed as a social experiment with 18 participants. The setup was a speed-dating event, 
where each participants would meet three of the opposite sex on a blind date lasting five minutes. Before 
and between the three dates the participants had to sit down as relaxed as possible for five minutes while 
having their baseline measured. All participant was wearing a heart rate monitor during the experiment 
period, which measured their RR intervals. The baseline was measured to see if the HRV changed compared 
to rest and thus detect whether an interaction had an effect. Further the baseline was used to be able to 
compare between individuals as the HRV measurement then was compared as the differences from the 
baseline. The data collected to the analysis included the R-R intervals from baselines and interactions and 
the answers from a rating sheet each participant had to complete between each date. In the rating sheet 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
they rated the person they just went on a date with and answered whether they would like to meet the 
person again.  
2.2 Planning 
Planning of the study involved recruitment of participant, finding equipment and location, and creating a 
plan for the process of the experimental days. The study required heart rate monitors, and there was 
equipment available for six people, which specified the number of participant attending at the same time. It 
was decided to do three sessions with six participant in each. To ensure a uniform group there was sat an 
age range between 20 and 29 years. The total number of participant was 17; eight females and nine males 
with the mean age 25.29 ± 2.47 years. 
Prior to experimental days all equipment was checked to make sure everything worked optimally. All the 
heartrate monitors and smartphones were checked to see if data was measured. The smartphones needed 
an application installed to synchronize with the heartrate monitors. The applied application “Elite HRV” is 
able to read RR interval. 
The setup for the experiment had some resemblance to a blind date, meaning the participants had not 
seen each other before entering the date, which is why it was important to find a location with several 
rooms and also make sure that the males and females did not arrive at the same time. The females were 
therefore asked to arrive 15 minutes before the males to try to avoid them meeting on their way in. To 
ensure most optimal conditions for the experiment all participant had separate room; partly to avoid the 
contestants talking about the dates and partly to create the best possible conditions for measuring the 
baseline as it is essential that the person is at their most relaxed. The location for the experiment was an 
office hall at DTU. The atmosphere in the offices was not optimal for a date but there was access to the 
required amount of rooms and the location was well known. In order to have access to the offices, the 
experiment was set to take place over a weekend. Also it was considered that more people would be 
available to participate at a weekend.  
Advertisement for the experiment was important in order to recruit as many participants as possible. It had 
to span wide to which a digital flyer was created (appendix A). It was easy to share through social media; 
Facebook and Instagram. The flyer had an appealing design and gave a clear message about what was being 
sought for and what the experiment was about.

2.3 Material 
2.3.1 Hardware 
A heart rate monitor functions through an electrode, which detects the electrical activity of the heart. The 
heart rate monitor converts the signals into a heart rate and RR interval which is sent though a wireless 
connection, to a receiver which is capable of converting that into readable data. The receiver is typically a 
watch, a smartphone or a tablet with an application installed to access the data (Giles, 2016). 
To perform the data analysis it was required to measure the RR-intervals. This is why all the contestants was 
wearing a heart rate monitor during the whole session. The model used was a “Polar H7” heart rate monitor 
with a chest strap which has a sampling frequency of 1000Hz (1000 samples per second (Giles, 2016) and 
thus detects the R-waves with a high accuracy. The heart rate monitor connects with the smartphones 
through Bluetooth. Six android phones with the “Elite HRV” application was used to access the data which 
was connected to one heart rate monitor each.  
2.3.2. Software 
“Elite HRV” is an application ordinarily used for training and uses the RR intervals to calculate variables 
reflecting the condition of the body in relation to training. It optimizes the training by indicating whether the 
body is in balance or it is over trained and needs to recover. 
Kubios is a user friendly software program to HRV analysis.  The first version was published in 2004. The latest 
version is 2.1 from 2014, developed using Matlab. This HRV analyzing tool are capable of detecting R-R peaks, 
calculating time domain measurements, frequency domain measurements and a lot more. (Tarvainen, 2014) 
Most analysis and calculation was done with Matlab version R2015b. Some statistics was done with the 
statistical program R version 3.3.0.

2.5 The experiment 
On the days of the experiment the three males and all the three females was gathered in two separate rooms,

the phone and heart rate monitor set. The ID tag was also used to fill out the rating sheets.  
After the instructions were given and all heart rate monitors was set to record the participants were placed 
in separate room, where they was told to sit as still and relaxed as possible for five minutes. Timestamps 
defining the five minute time periods was noted in a spreadsheet to make it possible to cut out the correct 
baseline- and date segments in the data processing. When the time was up, the males were asked to step 
out in the hall and were placed in front of the doors where the females were seated. At the same time, the 
three males entered the rooms and the first date had started. After five minutes the dates were interrupted 
and the males returned to their own rooms. In the next five minutes the participants completed the rating

2.6 Limitations 
The experiment had some limitations.  The rating sheets showed that only one match was found which 
changed the design of the study from comparing successful dates to unsuccessful dates to comparing highest 
rated dates with lowest rated dates. The instruction about not touching the phone during the experiment, 
wasn’t made clear enough which led to loss of data from three contestants. One participant did not show up 
which decreased the total number of dates which three. One participant had met one of the dates before. 
This was notated in the further analysis. Once phone stopped recording after first date so another set of data 
was lost. One male came more than 30 minutes early, and met two of the females from the session. This was 
noted in the further analysis.  
In general much importance had been put into the time periods of the baselines and the dates that they had 
to last exactly five minutes. This was not optimal according to ensure that the participants had reached to sit 
down and be relaxed. Only 10-40 seconds extra in each segment was included. Furthermore, it had not been 
taken into consideration that the participants were asked to fill out rating sheets during baselines. Also during 
baseline, the participants were interrupted when the phones was checked. This was notated in the further 
analysis. 
 
2.7 Within subject design 
This study used HRV as the indicator for ANS activity. In the analysis it is not possible to compare direct HRV 
measurements between subjects, as the HRV is highly individual. A within-subjects design is therefore a well 
fitted experimental design for this study. In a within-subjects experiment the same group of subjects serves 
in more than one treatment (Hall, 1998). It is beneficial in the sense that fewer participants may provide the 
necessary data, or in other words within-subject design have twice the amount of participants in the 
comparable groups which means a stronger statistical basis. When comparing the results in a within- subject 
designed experiment, the variance from individual differences is reduce, as the two groups compared 
contains the same persons. However, the disadvantage of within subject design is the possibility of 
performance bias by participate in more than one treatment (Hall, 1998). For example if a group of six 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
persons have to run 10 km and 5 km afterwards they may not performed as well in the latter due to 
exhaustion. This could be tested by a two way ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) as used in this study. Two by 
two ANOVA, also known as two factor ANOVA, compares the main effect between two or more groups in 
relation to two factors.  It tests, the variance inside the group and between the groups and gives the statistical 
probability that there is a significant difference between the groups (Lærd statistics, 2013). In this case, the 
ANOVA testing whether there is a significant difference between date and baseline (factor one), and if there 
is a significant difference between highest rated date and lowest rated date (factor two). The levels of factor 
one are thus date and baseline and the levels of factor two are highest rated date + baseline and lowest rated 
date + baseline. ANOVA also checks whether there is a significant effect of the interacting between the two 
factors. This is how the table for calculation looks like (Table 1). ‘14’ means the fourteen different values from 
the participants. This was repeated for SDNN, RMSSD, HF, LF, LF/HF and mean heart rate.

5. Discussion 
The study found that the LF/HF ratio increased during social interaction, when comparing to rest, 
irrespective of whether the date was high rated or low rated. LF/HF represents the dynamic between 
sympathetic activity and parasympathetic activity divided parasympathetic activity. As no main effect was 
found in the HF the interpretation of the result is most likely caused by an increase of sympathetic activity. 
There are different possibilities of why this results was found. An increased sympathetic activity is related 
to emotional stress reactions and physical activity that can cause variation in the heart rate (Shaffer, 2014). 
Earlier studies have found social interaction to affect the ANS (Palumbo, 2016) and the effect of the social 
interaction is related to the ability to emphasis with others (Robinson, 1982), and increased sympathetic 
activity has found to be related to stressful social event (Shahrestani, 2014) In this study the option to why 
the social interaction could affect the SNS could be that the participants felt stressed or nervous by the task 
they were giving. Silver et al. (2004) suggested, from a study in meetings between strangers, that a shared 
physiological reaction is correlated with the mutual perception of attractiveness (Silver, 2004). As described 
in section 1.4 the ANS reacts in several ways during social bonding (Henning,2001; Armstead, 2009; Silver, 
2004; Puyvelde,2015) but when the two involved are complete strangers and there is only the HRV to 
analyze, it is not easy to identify a the exact reason. Among the result also heart rate was found to increase 
during the interaction. This could be expected as increased sympathetic activity causes the heart rate to 
increase (BASIC). The heart rate could also be affected by increased respiration from talking or laughing 
compared to rest, but this is not necessarily the case as the respiration is not that easily affected (however 
depended on the persons physical condition) (Allen, 2006). The LF between first date and second date is 
also complex to interpret as the LF is reflecting both the SNS and the pSNS. In this case it is most likely that 
the increased LF means activity in the SNS as the pSNS is correlated which rest and sleep which is not the 
situations here. An aroused sympathetic activity in the first date could be due to the nervousness the 
participants most likely felt about trying something new which may have been inhibited over time. Another 
option is that even though the participant had had their baseline measured and rested for at least five 
minutes more of the participants had taken their bike several kilometers to DTU or walked quite a way 
from the bus. This could cause the heart rate to increase which is correlated with the LF component and 
due to the long lasting innervation from SNS (Shaffer, 2014), the heart rate could be affected for a long 
time after they arrived. The time between the arriving of the last participants to the first measuring started

There was not found any effect of interaction HF or RMSSD which is highly correlated (Otzenberger, 1998).

reflect that the participants did not feel relaxed during the dates, independent whether it was high- or low

Neither was a difference in the SDNN found in interaction or between high- and low rated date. SDNN is a

means that the result from the LF/HF must be influenced by both a small increased LF and a decreased HF. 
The study did not have many positive results. A lot of thing can influence this. The result could most likely 
have been affected by the missing matches. The small sample size is also a possible effect as the risk of 
accepting a null hypothesis by mistake are increased with a small sample size (Button, 2013).  Also is a small 
sample size more sensitive to errors in the data, which may risk being included in all studies. In this study 
one dataset was included with two participants who had met before (appendix E). This caused a large

5.3 
Future perspective 
Further investigations should include a bigger sample size which would give a more rigid statistical basis 
and the probability of rejecting a null hypothesis by mistake would decrease as well as mistakenly accepting 
a null hypothesis. A bigger sample size would further increase the possibility of finding more matches. 
Additionally with more time a study could include an extended background check, in an effort of finding 
even better matching pairs. In that context it could be investigated whether e.g. appearance is a more 
effective factor than chemistry when affecting HRV, by compare the difference in the HRV between the 
highest rated chemistry and the highest rated appearance, compared to a low rated chemistry and 
appearance. Then it could be investigated if the effect was more or less significant for one of the 
statements. 
The rating sheets indicated that most of the males had agreed on all the dates, while only one female had 
agreed to see the person again, in that context a difference in HRV of one way and both way attraction 
should be examined; Is the ANS affected differently if both parties had agreed on a date, compared to if 
one of them had said no. Another speed-dating study could include recording the audio, to see if the voice 
changes depending on whether the date is successful or not successful.  It is also interesting whether it is 
possible to identify a synchrony among the dyads of participants. This would require another type of 
analysis where the dates should be analyzed individual and the design would have to be changed as the 
participants should have the same starting point, meaning that the participant would have to be placed in 
the same room (possibly blindfolded) before starting the date. This would take a lot of time and planning. 
This way the HRV could be compared from same surroundings and by comparing the HRV during 
interaction, to their baselines, it would be possible to see if the HRV would modulate in the same direction.  
6. Conclusion 
It was found that the ANS was affected during a date compared to baseline and that the SNS is increased 
during interaction compared to baseline and thus means that social bonding with a stranger can affect our 
ANS. The pSNS is not affected by social interaction with strangers. Also the heat rate was found to increases 
when attending on a date, compared to resting which can be caused by the increased sympathetic activity. 
There was also found a difference in the sympathetic activity between first date and second date, which 
could be caused by nervousness or higher level of stress increasing over time.  The statistic basic for the study 
was not optimal as there was on a single matching date which could reduce an actual effect of social romantic 
interaction. The positive results forms a basis for at new expanded investigation.
