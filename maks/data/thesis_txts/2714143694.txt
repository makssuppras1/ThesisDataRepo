Abstract
This thesis investigates how to incorporate a periodic probe in a 2D PMoM solver to cal­
culate the current of a periodic patch­array based on work of Pozar. It is assumed that
the reader has basic knowledge in the areas of electromagnetics and harmonic analysis.
The commercial software TICRA tools uses the concept of a 2D PMoM solver to calculate
the reflections of a quasi­periodic reflect­array with the program QUPES, and this soft­
ware can potentially very easily be configured to model a phased­array by changing the
excitation to that of a probe.
Expressions for the MoM­matrix are derived for periodic and non­periodic MoM solvers,
and are applied on several geometric structures under different excitations, and the results
of these examples are presented and explained. An entire­domain and a piece­wise basis
function are presented and used to calculate the currents on two different planar periodic
patch­arrays with their strengths and weaknesses highlighted.
The work of Pozar explains how to apply the excitation of a probe in the PMoM solver.
This has been implemented, elaborated on and verified while using piece­wise and entire­
domain basis functions. The work of this thesis concludes that by expressing the electric
field due to the probe on the patch surface, and projecting this field onto the testing func­
tions, a viable excitation vector can be formed with similar results to what the work of Pozar
stipulated. The results using the entire­domain basis and testing functions employed by
Pozar were correlated with results using piece­wise basis and testing functions with good
agreement for the current distribution and the real part of the input impedance. The imag­
inary part of the input impedance showed inconsistencies between the basis functions.
Only a single model for expressing the electric field due to a probe was implemented, so
further work would involve implementing other approximations of fields due to the probe,
and cross­validate these with measurements and analytical results.

Introduction
One of the fundamental problems in antenna engineering is designing antennas with a
radiation pattern where the power is radiated in a desirable direction. For some situations,
when the relative position between transmitter and receiver is unknown, e.g. the antenna
in a phone, it is desirable to have a omnidirectional pattern with low directivity. For other
situations where the direction of communication is known, e.g. between a satellite and a
ground station on earth, the radiation pattern can be very narrow with a high directivity.
This then introduces a problem of maintaining communication when the target satellite
moves. Either the antennas are moved mechanically to face each other, or the main­lobe
(where most of the power is transmitted) can be electrically steered with phased array
beam steering [1] [2].
By changing the relative phase between each element of the antenna array, the direction
of the main­lobe shifts. But to actively move the main­lobe with digital user input, each
antenna in the array needs to be fed by an individually controlled probe. With the increase
in digital processing power, it has now become feasible to design these re­configurable
phased arrays where each antenna element is fed by a controllable probe, and each
probe is connected to an individual microwave network. Re­configurable phased arrays
are being studied and implemented for 5G networks [3] [4] [5] and space applications [6]
[7], because they increase the directivity and coverage of an antenna exclusively with
digital control, at the cost of increased complexity of feeding the antennas.
QUPES is a commercial software package in TICRA tools providing accurate modelling of
antenna radiation patterns, and is used specifically for periodic and quasi­periodic arrays.
But QUPES can currently only model the scattering of a structure due to the excitation
from a plane wave. However, the method used by QUPES should be able to model probe­
excitations as well. QUPES uses a mathematical method known as the periodic method
of moments (PMoM) to obtain an approximate solution to the current density distribution
on the patch, by looking at the relation between a known forcing function (the excitation)
and an unknown function (the current), to establish a set of linear equations with a vector
of unknown coefficients. PMoM is a derivative of the standard computational solution
Method of Moments, which is well studied for electromagnetic problems [1] [8] [9] [10].
Previous work has shown that a 2­dimensional PMoM solver can be used to calculate
the current on a periodic structure excited by a probe rather than a plane wave [11] [12].
Therefore, QUPES should be able to model the currents on a periodic patch array due to
a probe­excitation. The objective of this thesis is to implement a 2D PMoM solver, and
investigate how to formulate the excitation vector due to a probe compared to how it is
formulated due to a plane wave incidence.
The theoretical foundation of this work has been done by Pozar [11], where the field on a
unit cell in the (x, y)­plane, due to a z­directed current is projected onto entire­domain test­
ing functions. Appropriate piece­wise and entire­domain basis functions are employed, as
presented in the work of Mittra [13] concerning the 2D PMoM’s ability to analyse frequency
selective surfaces. The couplings between the probe current and the surface current have
been derived with the use of [11],[12] and [14], and are presented in this thesis. Several
other approaches to modelling the probe­excitation also exist [9] [15], and the process of
formulating the fields from these probe approximations will be presented. Based on the

work presented in this thesis, it should be possible to continue analysing different probes
using the 2D PMoM solver.
In chapter 2, mathematical derivations of the MoM and PMoM solvers are conducted. The
MoM equations are used to calculate the charge density distribution on a grounded wire
due to a static electric field, and to calculate the current density on a wire due to a time­
harmonic probe­excitation. The appendix contains more MoM examples with different
unit cells and excitations, but in the main part of the thesis, only the examples relevant
to the PMoM solver have been included. The PMoM solver is derived by representing
the fields and currents as Floquet series. The Floquet series of the periodic free space
Green’s function is presented and discussed, and is used to calculate the current of dif­

also presented here along with the expression used to calculate their Fourier transform.
Chapter 3 investigates several structures excited by a plane wave. In this chapter, the 2D

analytical expressions. Chapter 4 investigates the electric field due to a probe­excitation.
Three approaches are presented, and one from the work of Pozar [11] is implemented
and validated. An investigation into whether the probe excitation is valid for both piece­
wise and entire­domain basis functions is conducted, and a comparison of the results for
different configurations is shown.
In chapter 5, an example of how to build a PMoM solver is shown, and suggestions on
how to deal with code­acceleration and managing basis functions is discussed. Lastly,
chapter 6 concludes the work of this thesis, and outlines what the next steps in the work
could be.

The Method of Moments
The objective of this chapter is to introduce the Method of Moments (MoM) and Periodic
Method of Moments (PMoM), and derive the necessary equations to implement an algo­
rithm to solve electromagnetic problems.
In section 2.1, an introduction to the MoM­equations is presented, where a procedure to
calculate an unknown function due to a known forcing function is formulated. For this pur­
pose, basis functions and the inner product (moment) are introduced. This procedure is
then showcased to calculate the charge density distributions on a thin wire due to a static
electric field. A reconstruction of the known forcing function due to the calculated approx­
imate unknown function is calculated and shown, where the disadvantages of the MoM
are highlighted. Then the general equations for time­harmonic calculations are applied to
calculate the current density on a wire due to a probe­excitation.
In section 2.2, PMoM equations are established in order to calculate the current density
on a periodic surface by representing the current and fields as Floquet series. The electric
fields as a response to infinitesimal current on a unit cell, periodic in 1 and 2 dimensions,
are presented. This is used to model the current on different periodic array constructions
in chapter 3. Lastly, in section 2.3 the two types of basis functions used in chapters 3 and
4 to study PMoM excitations are introduced; the piece­wise rooftop basis functions and
the entire­domain sinusoidal basis function, and their Fourier transforms are analytically
derived for code­implementation.

functions however, is a free choice independent of the choice of basis functions. A com­

the same as the basis functions. This approach minimises the residual between the un­

2.4
Summary
In section 2.1 it was shown that an equation with an unknown function f(r) related to a
known forcing function h(r) through a known linear operator function L(f(r)) = h(r) can
be translated to a MoM matrix equation to solve for the approximation of the unknown
function. This was applied to a thin wire in free space illuminated by a static electric field
and a time­harmonic delta­gap excitation, where the unknown function are the charge
and current density distributions, the known function was the incident fields and the lin­
ear operator was the integral of the free space Green’s function. A pulse function was
used as a basis function and a Dirac­delta function was used as testing function. The
impedance matrix entries could then be evaluated analytically, and the solution for the
charge density was plotted. From this solution a reconstruction of the excitation based on
the approximated charge density was plotted showing a large discrepancy between the
true excitation and reconstructed at the edges of the wire.
For the time­harmonic case, the same basis and testing functions were used, where the
impedance matrix was calculated using an M­point quadrature. A plot of the current den­
sity showed a reasonable distribution coherent with the expected shape.
Section 2.2 developed the theory of how a periodic current could be calculated by rep­
resenting the current, electric field and Green’s function as Floquet series. Great care
was taken to derive how the periodicity of the current density could be moved from the
current to the Green’s function, such that the Green’s function was the periodic current
sampled at one point, rather than the response due to one point repeated periodically.
Thereafter, the periodic Green’s function was formulated and plotted to see the E­field
due to an infinitesimal current element. This was done both for a structure with periodicity
in 2 dimensions and 1 dimension. A rule for truncating the infinite sum of the Floquet
series was also created for a piece­wise basis function.
Section 2.3 presented the basis functions used in the further study and a derivation of
their Fourier transforms for further PMoM calculations was derived. The basis functions
presented were the piece­wise rooftop basis function, and the entire­domain sinusoidal
basis function.

3.5
Summary
This chapter presented one use of the PMoM to calculate the current density on a periodic
rectangular patch array due to a plane­wave incident field.
Section 3.1 presented the expression for the incident plane wave and the excitation vector
elements due to this incident field. The i’th element was shown to be expressed as the
product of the phasor of the incident field at the origin of the unit cell and the complex
conjugate Fourier transform of the i’th basis function.
Section 3.2 showed the comparison between the structure periodic in 1 dimension com­
pared with CST, TICRA Tools and the analytical current on a infinite PEC plane. The
analytical comparison showed complete agreement between the 1D PMoM implementa­
tion and the analytical expression. Here, the PMoM implementation was adjusted at the
edges such that current could flow out of the edge by including half of a basis function on
opposing sides. The comparison with TICRA Tools showed near perfect match of reflec­
tion coefficients sweept in θ and ϕ, but the comparison with CST showed a discrepancy in
the shape of the current. The PMoM currents goes to 0 at the edge for currents travelling
transverse to the edge, which is expected behaviour, but the currents from CST showed
much larger currents at the edge. Furthermore, better agreement between the analytical
result and the 1D PMoM was achieved than between CST and the analytical result.
Section 3.3 showed good agreement in amplitude at the centre of the square patch be­
tween CST and the 2D PMoM, and the shape of the current was also comparable. The re­
flection coefficients had again near perfect agreement between the 2D PMoM and TICRA
Tools.
Section 3.4 introduced the dyadic Green’s matrix from Pozars article [11]. A theoreti­
cal proof that the Green’s functions were interchangeable for configurations in the 2D
PMoM implementation was presented, and this was implemented with both piece­wise
and entire­domain basis functions. Great agreement between the types of basis functions
were achieved both for shape, direction, and amplitude, but the reflection coefficients were
much larger than anticipated, indicating a scaling issue for this Green’s function matrix.

4
Probe-Fed Excitation
The objective of this chapter is to show that it is possible to model the current on the unit
cell from a probe excitation by only changing the excitation of the PMoM implementation
defined in chapter 2 based on the approach from the work of Pozar [11].
In chapter 3 the unit cell was excited by a plane wave incident electric field. The incident
field due to a probe can also be calculated and projected onto the testing functions to form
the excitation vector. Several methods for modelling the incident field from the probe
exist, and in section 4.1, 3 different approaches to model the excitation from a probe
are presented. The processes of deriving the delta­gap excitation and the magnetic frill
excitations are briefly introduced, but the approach implemented in this thesis couples a
z­directed current over the probe to an (x, y)­directed incident field over the unit cell, and
a sampling of the field is shown.
Section 4.2 investigates the incident field after it is projected onto the entire­domain sinu­
soidal basis functions, and the solution of the PMoM­equations are compared with Pozars
work in section 4.3. Section 4.4 investigates the incident field from the probe projected
onto piece­wise rooftop basis functions, and in section 4.5 the results from the piece­wise
and entire­domain implementations are compared to verify that both approaches are valid
for probe­excitation.
4.1
Probe Excitation Approximation
The simplest expression of the (x, y)­directed E field due to a probe is employed in [12].
This is the delta­gap excitation, which was also used to excite the non­periodic structure
in section 2.1.2. With this approach, a voltage is impressed on the probe, and excites
only the testing function with domain centred at the probe location. The incident field
due to the delta­gap can be seen on Figure 4.1a. This approach is valid for a piece­wise
implementation, but fails for entire­domain basis functions, as all testing functions have
coverage of the probe.
A similar but more accurate approach is the magnetic frill excitation approximating the field
of the TEM­wave of a coaxial cable [8]. The field Eρ is a radially directed field between
the inner and outer conductor of the coaxial cable. If the patch is large enough, it can be
approximated as an infinite PEC, and by image theory Eρ can be modelled as a magnetic
frill current and can be seen on Figure 4.1b. The (x, y)­directed E field over the entire unit
cell can then be expressed due to this magnetic current [9] [15].

4.6
Summary
This chapter presented the method to construct the excitation vector due to periodic
probes on the patches. Several methods to calculate the incident field due to the probes
were briefly introduced. In section 4.1 an approach for modelling fields from electrically
short probes and the process of deriving it was presented. From this, the electric potential
on the unit cell could be plotted.
Section 4.2 explored the projection of the incident field of the probe onto entire­domain
basis functions, where plots of the projected field with different amount of modes were
plotted. From these plots it could be seen that including more modes led to fields more
closely approximated the original incident E­field.
Section 4.3 presented arguments to validate the results compared to the results in Pozars
article. Expressions for the reflection coefficient R and input impedance Zin were shown
and the derivations were discussed. With these, 3 plots from the article were recreated in
2 different ways. In the article it was stated that the imaginary part of the input impedance
was negligible, which is inconsistent with the findings of this report. Therefore, the recon­
structions were plotted with both a real and a complex broadside Zin, where the variation
with complex Zin showed great agreement near normal incidence, but some points with
reflections magnitudes larger than 1 which were not present in Pozars plots, and the vari­
ation with real broadside Zin showed good approximations at incidence angle close to
90°, but an offset near broadside.
Section 4.4 showed an implementation of the probe excitation for piece­wise rooftop basis
functions, where the plot of the incident field showed a clear radial pattern around the
probe. A weakness of the piece­wise functions was also shown with a symmetric sampling
around the probe showing an asymmetric field.
Section 4.5 investigated whether agreement was achieved between the validated imple­
mentation using entire­domain functions and the unvalidated implementation using piece­
wise basis functions. The shape, phase and amplitudes of the currents showed great
agreement, and the real parts of the input impedances were also close, and the imagi­
nary parts of the input impedances were very large for both cases, and approximately a
factor 2 larger for the piece­wise compared to the entire­domain implementations.

Conclusion and Outlook
This thesis sought to determine whether the current density on a probe­fed periodic patch
array could be calculated by modifying the PMoM­equation used to calculate the current
on a periodic reflect­array. For this purpose, a 2D PMoM implementation has been de­
veloped, and a published algorithm for a probe­fed periodic patch array has been tested
and verified using several types of basis functions and correlated with other software and
theoretical expressions.
The theory of the PMoM and MoM algorithms have been derived and presented in chap­
ter 2. The MoM equations were established while introducing simple basis and testing
functions using the pulse and Dirac­delta respectively. This was applied to calculate the
charge density and current density distributions on a thin wire in free space excited by
a static electric field and a time­harmonic delta­gap excitation respectively. A case was
shown where the impedance matrix entries where calculated analytically, and the other
case showed a computational calculation using an M­point quadrature integration. The
implication of representing the densities as sums of weighted basis functions was pre­
sented, and the conclusion is that the approximation of the unknown function could ac­
curately reproduce the forcing function, except at the edges of the wire, where large de­
viations occur due to the singularity of the charge density distribution at the edges of the
wire. Appropriate solutions to best circumvent this issue was discussed here as well.
After this, the PMoM equations were derived by representing the periodic fields as Floquet
series. The periodicity of the current was moved to the linear operator such that the calcu­
lated current could be limited to the unit cell only. An expression of the impedance matrix
entry was developed along with a presentation of the periodic dyadic Green’s function
matrix used for a 1D and 2D planar periodic array in free space. The basis functions used
in the following studies were presented, and are the piece­wise rooftop basis function and
the entire­domain sinusoidal basis function, and a truncation rule for the piece­wise basis
functions was developed.
Chapter 3 used the 1D and 2D PMoM implementation to calculate the current densities
on planar arrays in free space. The vector elements of the excitation vector was derived,
and the currents were compared to CST and an analytical expression of reflection from
an infinite PEC. The reflection coefficient of the structure was compared to TICRA Tools,
and great agreement was shown between the PMoM implementation and QUPES and
the analytical expression. The results from CST showed deviations from the expected
results. Lastly, the 2D PMoM implementation was compared to Pozar’s article where
it was concluded that to calculate the currents on periodic arrays of different geometric
shapes, the Green’s function should be changed. Great agreement between the entire­
domain and piece­wise implementations were achieved, but a scaling issue is likely to
be the cause of a reflection coefficient to be much larger than expected compared with
QUPES.
Chapter 4 showed the results of exciting the unit cells with a probe. The method of deriva­
tion for the incidence field due to a probe was presented as derived in Pozars article, and
the fields were shown before being projected onto the basis functions. An excitation was
shown as the incidence field projected onto the entire­domain and piece­wise basis func­
tions. A method to verify the implementation with Pozars article was shown and good
agreement was found for the real part of the input impedance. The imaginary part was
Analysis of Infinite Arrays of Microstrip Patches
63
==PAGE:71==
reported to be negligible, but this was inconsistent with the findings of this thesis, an issue
persisted for calculations of other configurations as well.
This thesis concludes that calculation of currents on a periodic structure due to a periodic
probe can be calculated by changing the excitation vector of the PMoM equation. By
evaluating the field on the unit cell due to the probe on the unit cell and projecting it onto
the testing functions, the excitation vector can be formulated.
While a reasonable excitation vector was formulated and good agreement of the input
resistance was achieved, the large input reactance was unresolved. This issue could be
due to incorrect calculation of the input impedance or due to the implementation of the
probe coupling. However, only a single approach to calculating the probe excitation was
implemented, and more solutions exist. Implementing other probe­excitations could be
used to validate the fields shown in this thesis.
This would also serve the purpose of comparing the accuracy of different probe approx­
imations. The delta­gap and magnetic frill approaches were presented in chapter 4, and
would be good places to start. The magnetic frill approach calculates an electric field on
the entire plane, and this field should be projected onto the basis functions to form the
excitation vector in the same way as it was shown in this thesis.
Another unresolved issue was the reflection coefficient for the plane wave incidence on the
printed patch array. Whether the Green’s function of Pozars article takes into account the
reflections from the backplate has not been thoroughly investigated, and requires further
studies to resolve.

mode = (αm, βn)

response from the TE and TM mode of a normally incident plane wave.
The post­processing result panel allows the user to define which fields to calculate. The
surface current can be calculated with the H­field monitor. With this applied, each of the
modes chosen in the boundary settings are calculated due to the excitation modes. No
matter how many modes are included in the excitation, the mode 1 surface current due
to mode 1 will be calculated. The expected behaviour is that this current will not change
if the user includes more modes in the excitation, and that including more modes than
the default 18 would not lead to significant differences. However, this is not the observed
behaviour.
