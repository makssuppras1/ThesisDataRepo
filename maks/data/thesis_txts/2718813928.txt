Abstract 
Background: Today, companies are encountering challenges to build a responsive business in a 
cost-effective manner. For a manufacturing company, one approach to tackle this challenge is the 
transition to a hybrid lean-agile, or leagile, production system.  
Purpose: This thesis aims to determine the approach to apply lean and agile principles to a 
production system to build leagile competency and promote continuous improvement. In addition, 
this thesis aims to present the approach for leagile production in form of a methodological 
framework to guide the company towards leagile improvement. 
Methodology: This thesis follows a three-phase guideline on developing a methodological 
framework. The methodology started with identifying evidence to inform the framework through 
analysis of theoretical data and interviews with the partner company. Then, the framework is 
developed from the analyzed data, as well as, refined by additional data from incurring interviews. 
Finally, the developed framework is evaluated and tested by the partner company, who is a 
representative of the manufacturing industry, in form of a case study at the company’s production 
site. 
Result: This thesis has developed the “lean-agile framework” consisting of three parts: Current 
State Assessment, Project Selection, and Evaluation and Establishment. The first part provides the 
company an understanding of the production characteristics and the production KPIs based on the 
lean-agile perspective, by which, set a direction for implementing lean and agile practices. The 
second part provides suggestions on lean and agile practices which are suitable for the company’s 
production system, and a structured approach to prioritize the implementation of the suggested 
practices. The last part provides methodological steps of establishing a system to facilitate the 
adopted practices and sustaining it towards the future. The framework was well received by the 
partner company where its proposition was tested. 
Conclusion: This thesis recognizes that lean and agile principles can be implemented to a 
production system in a stepwise manner. In this way, leagile production system can be developed 
as the leagile concept is based on exercising the two principles simultaneously. Moreover, this 
thesis recognizes that lean and agile principles are rooted in the concept of continuous 
improvement. The adoption of the two principles promotes continuous improvement naturally. In 
addition, this study recognizes that continuous improvement can be achieved in a stepwise 
development of the production system side by side with the stepwise implementation of lean and 
agile practices.

Introduction 
1.1  Theoretical background 
The production as an organizational function is conventionally known for its traditional ways of 
performing activities (Goldsby & García-Dastugue, 2003). Companies that perform this function 
as their main business face challenges of securing their competitiveness in the market due to the 
advancement of technologies and the ever-changing business trend (Goldsby et al., 2006). Thus, 
the increasing turbulence in the market demands organizations to find new ways to adapt to the 
recurring changes. Manufacturing companies especially is faced with the challenge of producing 
products in varieties and quantities that are in sync with the marketplace (Goldsby et al., 2003). 
This refers to the changing of role of manufacturing where in the past, manufacturing companies 
normally produce a small group of similar products (Yusuf & Adeleye, 2002). Thus, 
manufacturing companies are in pursuit of new initiatives at improving operations. Additionally, 
given the more intense competitiveness in today’s market, manufacturing companies are pushed 
into expanding their improvement effort into the supporting functions in the organization rather 
than merely the manufacturing function (Virmani et al., 2018). 
Generally, an organization is an operational entity comprises of a group of people which perform 
various activities to achieve a common goal (Jones, 2013). The theory underlining the functioning 
of any organizations is simply the use of organization’s core competences to transform resources 
input into outputs in forms of deliverables bound for the customers, and in turn, building its ability 
to source new input (Jones, 2013). This process is so-called ‘value-creation process’ which is the 
root of an organization’s existence. A simplified illustration of the value-creation process as shown 
in Figure 1.  
 
 
FIGURE 1 ORGANIZATION'S VALUE-CREATION PROCESS (JONES, 2013) 
 
In the context of this thesis, value-creation process for a manufacturing company is simply the use 
of raw materials, machines, and human workers to create products through manufacturing 
knowledge and technologies. Given that manufacturing processes normally require more than one 
factor and one step to complete the value-creation process, a structured way of performing 
operations is needed to ensure that the production system, comprising of products, processes, and 
==PAGE:8==
 
 
 
Page 2 of 101 
human, is properly integrated. In the other words, a production system requires management in 
order to run operations smoothly.  
Two of the well-accepted approaches used for managing a production system are the lean and agile 
approach, particularly, lean manufacturing and agile manufacturing. Lean manufacturing is a 
mixed socio-technical system whose main objective is to eliminate waste and reduce variability in 
the processes (Virmani et al., 2018). Additionally, agile manufacturing is also advocated by many 
industrial experts and researchers. The concept has its roots from the Agile Manifesto in 2001 
which was first developed for software development sector (Beck et al., 2001). The 12 principles 
from the manifesto became the underlying principles for the concept of agile organization, and 
later, agile manufacturing. However, the agile principles for manufacturing were not as concrete 
as lean. This is due to the adoption of agile principles that was more widespread compared to the 
adoption of lean, which was significantly recognized by Toyota Motor Company (Krafcik, 1988). 
However, an investigation in the UK reported that majority of the surveyed Small and Medium 
Enterprises (SMEs) unconsciously employ agile manufacturing techniques within their system 
(Moradlou & Asadi, 2015). According to research, agile manufacturing targeted the improvement 
of the overall system around the value-creation process of an organization (Yusuf et al., 1999).  
Apart from these two approaches for production management, a hybrid approach of lean and agile 
is also in the topic of interest of the industry. The term ‘leagility’ was coined to represent the 
approach to build a production system that constitutes both lean and agile features (Naylor et al., 
1999). The leagile approach was adopted as a supply chain strategy in many research and case 
studies (Goldsby et al., 2006). The benefit of this approach is essentially assisting companies in 
exploiting market opportunities in a cost-effective manner (Virmani et al., 2018).

1.4  Thesis scope and objective 
This thesis was conducted to provide the company informational support to the company’s ‘Lean 
and Agile’ strategic initiative. The main approach for the framework development was through 
collaboration with the Global Manufacturing department at the company headquarter in country 
D. Supplementary data collection was also carried out with other global production sites. Thus, 
the framework was developed with inputs of the Global Manufacturing department and 
generalized using the inputs from the manufacturing department at other global production sites. 
Ultimately, the developed framework is expected to be adopted by the central department to 
enhance the ‘Lean and Agile’ strategic initiatives and employ action plans to all production sites. 
The objective of this thesis is to develop a lean-agile framework which uses lean and agile 
principles to guide the company in building a hybrid production system. The outcome from this 
thesis is expected in the form of a methodological framework that guides the company in pursuing 
a leagile production system. The framework is expected to be based on the theory of lean and agile 
manufacturing given that lean manufacturing is perceived to secure quality and the agile 
manufacturing is perceived to secure competitiveness of the company, and in turn, achieve the 
goal for the Global Manufacturing Program.  
The scope of the framework covers the lean-agile improvement projects from planning to post-
implementation phase. This includes current state assessment, initiation of new lean-agile projects, 
and concluding the improvement project. However, the framework is designed to take the 
==PAGE:12==

management perspective which helps managers in strategic planning level. The framework does 
not provide a guideline to operational employees on the approach to execute the project. 
Apart from creating a support system for Company X, this thesis aims to fill in the research gap

hybrid approach in supply chain management area. Thus, the lean-agile framework of this thesis 
aims to explore the applicability of principles of lean, agile, leagile on the operations management 
area, especially for manufacturing company.  
 
1.5  Research question 
It is a fact that Company X in encountering a challenge to improve their production system to be 
more responsive to the ever-changing demand from the business environment while maintaining 
a cost-effective process. As already foreseen by the company, the adoption of a hybrid lean-agile

doing so, the research question which outlines this thesis is as formulated below: 
 
“How can lean and agile principles be applied to a production system to build a 
leagile production system and promote continuous improvement?”

Literature Review 
A literature review method was used in this project to present the existing backgrounds regarding 
the concept of lean, agile, leagile, and continuous improvement in manufacturing. The literature 
review sourced information from online scientific databases, namely, Scopus, DTUFindit, and 
Google Scholar. The research was conducted by querying the relevant key words including “lean”, 
“agile”, “manufacturing”, “leagile”, and “continuous improvement”.  
The results of these searches were articles explaining the concepts, and the implementation of each 
concept in real-life case studies. The articles explained the characteristics of a lean manufacturing, 
its tools and the implementation, the benefits, and limitations. The same result applied for agile 
manufacturing. Some other articles presented the core concepts of lean in relation to that of agile 
and suggested the methods to integrate them together. When considering the articles about 
integrated lean-agile approach, most of the relevant articles emphasized the leagile concept on the 
supply chain level. 
These articles presented the needs from the industry for the integrated lean-agile approach as well 
as the systematic method for developing it. There were some articles which presented case studies 
about leagile supply chain and gives valuable insights to the applicability of the integrated 
approach in supply chain management. Moreover, some articles mentioned the connections 
between lean and agile practices to a continuous improvement concept and thus, indicate that both 
practices can contribute to continuous improvement in an organization. However, there are not 
many articles suggesting explicitly the activities used for promoting continuous improvement in 
the lean-agile environment. 
After multiple articles are reviewed, they are analyzed for their correlations about ideas or concepts 
that they agree or disagree on. This builds an overview about the specific aspects of lean and agile 
that have been used previously in theoretical research and case studies. This analysis, therefore, 
provides a direction to the development of lean-agile framework. 
 
2.1  The Concept of Lean 
Lean as a concept is the approach for performing a task and completing it in the most efficient 
manner which is using the least amount of input and still receiving the expected level of output.  
Lean can be viewed as three operations principles (Slack et al., 2016): 
1) Lean as a philosophy of performing operations: It represents a smooth flow of operations. 
The key issues in lean philosophy include staff involvement, continuous improvement 
program, and waste elimination. 
2) Lean as a method of planning and controlling operations: The methods of lean aim to 
achieve a synchronized flow with reduced amount of waste. The key issues include Kanban 
system, level scheduling and delivery, and mixed modelling. 
3) Lean as a set of tools that improve operations performance: The lean tools in this context 
are in forms of techniques and human practices that can be used to eliminate waste.  
==PAGE:14==
 
 
 
Page 8 of 101 
The term ‘waste’ in this context is defined as all activities in a process that are wasteful because 
they do not add value to the operation or the customer (Slack et al., 2016). More specifically in 
lean manufacturing, waste can be categorized into seven types: transport, defects, overproduction, 
overprocessing, inventory, waiting, and motion as shown in Figure 2. 
 
 
FIGURE 2 SEVEN TYPES OF WASTE IN LEAN 
 
As lean concept emerged during the early 1900s, its main application was to maximize operations 
performance especially in the industrial settings which were experiencing the emergence of mass 
commercials and globalization (Krafcik, 1988). Especially lean in manufacturing, an automotive 
company, Toyota Motor Company, has successfully implemented in its production of automobiles 
in the 1950s (Krafcik, 1988). The developed lean manufacturing method by Toyota focused on 
maintaining a continuous flow of the products in a production process, or as known as just-in-time 
(JIT) production, by focusing on the ultimate reduction of all forms of waste. The method also 
included high involvement level of the production employees and mistake-proofing system which 
contribute to higher production quality. This method comprised of multiple techniques and tools 
applicable directly in the production itself, for example, the use of Kanban card to pull raw 
materials to a workstation and start an operation. The focus of lean during these early years was 
the reduction of waste and cost as can be seen that the methodology aimed to find the best approach 
for performing operations. Since then, many businesses have adopted lean in their production of 
their product and services. The terminology of this practice was coined as “lean production” or 
“lean manufacturing” by Womack et al (2007). 
During the early years of lean, academics around the world showed a great interest in continuing 
research about the benefits of lean in the automotive industry. One of which was done back in 
1988, by which, the study showed that production plants that were managed with a lean production 
policy can achieve high performance level with high quality compared to other non-lean plants, 
based on a quantitative study (Krafcik, 1988). The lean method at this point is merely related to 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
Page 9 of 101 
the shop floor level in terms of cost reduction. After lean was adopted by different manufacturers 
in the western world, it received criticisms regards the inability to deal with varying demands and 
exclusion of human aspect. A new design of lean was presented by Womack and Jones (1997) who 
brought the lean concept to a broader, more diverse sectors. Womack and Jones advocated this 
extension of lean and coined the term “lean thinking” for it. 
Lean thinking covered the use of lean in more aspects of a business, namely, customer relations, 
product design, production planning, and supply chain management, etc. The key focus of lean 
shifted from reducing cost to managing process flow to customers. Lean thinking proposed as a 
guide for businesses to eliminate waste throughout their whole organization in five steps process 
as shown in Figure 3. 
 
 
FIGURE 3 LEAN THINKING PRINCIPLES (WOMACK & JONES, 1997) 
 
Taking lean thinking in practice, a lean implementation principle was proposed by Bicheno and 
Holweg (2016) as a hierarchical transformation framework, as shown in Figure 4. Their framework 
suggested sequential actions to establish a long-term lean implementation. Its main theme 
encompassed lean thinking principles incorporated with redesigning of the organizational culture, 
strategy, and communication and building lean culture. This follows Womack and Jones’s “lean 
leap process” which proposed the need to identify a change agent to create a new lean organization 
(Womack & Jones, 1997). The change agent is supposedly the first person equipped with lean 
knowledge and be able to share it with the organization before performing value stream mapping. 
The lean principle can be seen as a continuous process for finding the important value and creating 
improvements based on it. Values, or the expected benefits, of a product should be based on the 
customer’s perspective. With this, both the firm and the customer can aim to satisfy the customer’s 
needs with less focus on the needs of the individuals or the company. Given the specific values 
required from the customer, the sequential activities of producing the specific product are mapped 
out accordingly, from product design, production planning, to delivery. Lean techniques are 
applied to keep all the activities which are either adding value or not adding any value but is 
necessary for the process, while eliminating the activities that does not add any value to the product 
(Mrugalska & Wyrwicka, 2017). This indicates that non-production activities, such as quality 
inspection, would not be cut out from the process since they add values to customers.  
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
Page 10 of 101 
 
 
FIGURE 4 LEAN IMPLEMENTATION PRINCIPLES (BICHENO & HOLWEG, 2016) 
 
As a result, the company recognizes the essential activities that can fulfill the customer’s needs 
while doing nothing more or nothing less, so-called ‘value stream’ (Pattanaik & Sharma, 2009). 
Using value stream as the main outline the material flow and information flow in the production 
are designed in the way that values are created in a continuous stream of activities. This step aims 
to ensure that the product is continuously moving, or developing, towards the customers without 
disruption nor corrective activities to address mistakes (Bicheno & Holweg, 2016). The continuous 
stream of values then enables pull production. The production should be pulled from the customer 
so that the required resources are provided at the right time, and the right place. With the resources 
used when it is pulled, the tendency of building inventory in the production system is reduced. 
This step strongly resonates the concept of just in time which has been the core idea of lean since 
it is discovered (Bhuiyan & Baghel, 2005). Finally, the production should be evaluated continually 
to seek improvement that can fasten the flow of product pulled by the customer with precision, 
and ultimately achieve perfection in that value stream. 
From the lean implementation principles, lean thinking shifts the focus of lean, from merely cost 
in lean production, to quality, cost, and delivery (Hines et al., 2004). The concept of lean then 
continues to be developed to cover the whole value system with focus on improving the capability 
at system level. Thus, lean is shown to be implemented at the strategic level of different types of 
manufacturing sector as well as service sector.  
Overall, lean thinking is as a philosophy underlying lean manufacturing in the recent production 
system. Lean thinking provides the background understanding and the desired mindset in 
achieving lean operation and direct waste-eliminating operations. As the core objective of lean is 
to provide values to customers, the main activity of lean is managing the production system which 
is the operation of value-adding itself. However, other supplementary activities which contribute 
to more values to customer can well be included as a part of lean activity. A framework for lean

The use of many management practices in lean had led to a wide collection of lean manufacturing 
characteristics as well as lean definition. However, Pattanaik and Sharma (2009) proposed a lean 
manufacturing model which involved the production of one unit at a time, elimination of non-
value-added activities, production in only the work content time, relocation of required resources 
to the point of usage, and balancing of all processes at the same takt time. Takt time is the rate at 
which work progresses through the shop flow. As all processes are perform simultaneously at the 
same take time, the products will be continuously moving from one process to another until it 
becomes the final product. This indicates that, ideally, there will not be any product waiting to be 
processed at any processes at any time, and therefore, reduce waste in more types than one.  In 
summary, an ideal lean manufacturing system is the system that produces product in only the sum 
of its value-added work content time (Pattanaik & Sharma, 2009).

2.2 The Concept of Agile 
Agile practice was introduced to the industry in the 1980s where many were prompted by the 
advancement in technology and the volatility of the market environment (Moran, 2015). It is 
commonly accepted that agile is hardly accredited to a specific group of individuals due to the 
emergence of agile from experienced practitioners and being adopted in more of a widespread 
manner. Advocates of agile promote the agenda for businesses to embrace uncertainties in the 
market and aim to creating working solutions in a flexible manner with the organization’s 
stakeholders. Thus, agile calls for operational reformation in matured organizations which are 
likely to follow traditional style of management (Moran, 2015). The emergence of computer 
science during the early 2000s brought about the debut of ‘Manifesto for Agile Software 
Development’. The manifesto entailed the notions of iterative development and incremental 
delivery based on twelve principles that commend individual interactions, working solutions, 
customer collaboration and responsiveness to change (Beck et al., 2001). The twelve principles 
are as listed below: 
 
“Our highest priority is to satisfy the customer through early and continuous 
delivery of valuable software. 
Welcome changing requirements, even late in development. Agile processes 
harness change for the customer's competitive advantage. 
Deliver working software frequently, from a couple of weeks to a couple of 
months, with a preference to the shorter timescale. 
==PAGE:20==

Business people and developers must work together daily throughout the project. 
Build projects around motivated individuals. Give them the environment and 
support they need, and trust them to get the job done. 
The most efficient and effective method of conveying information to and within a 
development team is face-to-face conversation. 
Working software is the primary measure of progress. 
Agile processes promote sustainable development. The sponsors, developers, and

The principles of the agile manifesto were analyzed with regards to the perspectives of product, 
process and people in a book about agile principles by Moran (2015). One of the analyses in the 
book showed that the manifesto was relevant to process, product, and people domain (as shown in 
Appendix B). Thus, the manifesto as an overall represented a significant mark in the studies of 
agile manufacturing, supporting the notion that agile manufacturing, derived from agile principles, 
is the competitive strategy for businesses in the 21st century (Kidd, 1995; Gunasekaran, 2001). 
Agile, or agility, in a general business context is defined as the ability of a business to response 
quickly to rapid and unexpected changes in a competitive market driven by customer-based values 
of product and services (Kidd, 1995; Yusuf et al., 1999). Thus, a collective of research identified 
changes as the drivers for building an agile organization and shares common theme for 
categorizing changes. A study by Sharifi and Zhang (1999) categorized changes factors into five 
general areas: Market, Competition, Customer Requirements, Technology, and Social Factors. 
Since this categorization was highly generic, it was difficult to recognizes the impact of the change 
factors on the organization. Another study by Yusuf et al (1999) was used in this section to present 
the main issues incurred from the five groups of change factors, referred to as drivers of agility.

value-creation process and its survival.

2.3 The Concept of Lean, Agile, and Leagile 
Naylor et al (2007) coined the term “leagile” in 1997 referring to the hybrids of the lean and agile 
approaches. Leagile emerged as an innovative management strategy which has been discussed 
widely among researchers and practitioners in the last two decades (Krishnamurthy & Yauch, 
2007; Yusuf & Adeleye, 2002). As discussed in the previous sections, lean approach centers 
around waste elimination of a process whereas agile approach on value-based management. Agile 
practice contrasts with lean practice as lean aims to create a stable environment to eliminate waste 
while agile aims to create a strong internal value-creation competences in a turbulent environment. 
Thus, leagile approach gains the benefits of cost reduction from lean and organizational 
capabilities development from agile.  
Originally, organizations competed on the basis of profitability. This gave birth to the lean 
manufacturing which aims to lower the cost and increase quality (Krafcik, 1988). Lean 
manufacturing was proven to be capable of promoting high productivity level, quality, and 
minimum complexity simultaneously. However, many advocates argued that lean manufacturing 
was under threat. A comparative study between lean and agile by Yusuf and Adeleye (2002) 
presented that manufacturing competitiveness becomes the proficiency of organizational ability to 
adapt its process according to the changes as well as the contextual factors. Thus, agile 
manufacturing was brought forward as a solution to tend to the changes. A collective of research 
supported this notion and some reported increased pressure on lean manufacturing (Yusuf & 
Adeleye, 2002). Firstly, the economic crisis was less severe than in the period after World War II. 
So, there is no obligation to push the production as much as possible. Secondly, lean was based on 
a strong discipline of a Japanese culture which to an extent, caused workers to become stressed for 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
 
Page 20 of 101 
pursuing continuous improvement. Thirdly, the large output from lean production was likely 
followed by aggressive sales, imposition on customers, and master-servant relationship with 
suppliers. Forth, the emergence of cutting-edge technologies brought about a knowledge-based 
production where the value was added to the product through innovative and effective process 
behind it. Lastly, cost and quality were not sufficient to retain customers any longer. With this, the 
most challenging objective became the flexibility in production volume. Thus, the ability to 
decouple cost and lot size became critical for retaining competitiveness (Goldsby et al., 2006). 
From all the threats mentioned, agile manufacturing was perceived as a good counter for market 
instability and shrinking product life cycle in the future.  
Based on contingency theory, the effective risk mitigation strategies are conditional upon internal 
and external environment of an organization (Jones, 2013). So, there is no ultimate answer to which 
extent lean or agile manufacturing should be used to build a hybrid lean-agile model that can 
mitigate the risks in a market environment (Ahmed & Huma, 2021). However, a good start can be 
the exploration of the characteristics lean in comparison with agile as well as the present use of 
leagile in different sectors.  
In the larger view, both lean and agile shares similar principles and objectives. Both require a high 
level of product quality, minimization of total lead time, defined as time taken for receiving 
customer orders to delivering finished products to the customers (Christopher & Towill, 2001). As 
speed is one of the key competences of agile organization, it implies that the lead time needs to be 
small to be able to compete in the turbulent business environment. Flexibility of agile 
manufacturing derives from having an easy-to-change manufacturing setup which can be built 
using the lean tool, Single Minute Exchange of Die (SMED). Moreover, continuous improvement 
which has its roots from lean plays an important part of structuring an agile organization, in the 
sense of improvement of both process and workforce. This can be seen in Table 4 where 
continuous improvement manifested as a key attribute of an agile organization in change and 
education. Based on these concurrencies, a statement about agile manufacturing is given (Sarkis, 
2001): 
 
“Agile manufacturing = Flexible manufacturing system + Lean manufacturing”  
 
Despite the similarities between lean and agile manufacturing, there are a few essential differences 
between the two. A more explicit definition of lean and agile paradigms was given by Naylor et al 
(1999), emphasizing the distinguishing features of each approach from the supply chain 
management perspective: 
 
• Leanness: Development of value stream to eliminate all waste, including time, and ensure 
a level schedule. 
• Agility: Utilization market knowledge and a virtual corporation to exploit profitable 
opportunities in a volatile marketplace.

A successful leagile implementation can result in improved quality of products, reduced 
manufacturing lead time, better customer satisfaction, better sales of product and hence increased 
profitability of firm (Virmani et al., 2018). Examples of these benefits can be seen from case

reduced production cycle time, less downtime, more ability to pull production.  
As mentioned, de-coupling point concept was adopted in many leagile studies in supply chain 
management (Virmani et al., 2018; Krishnamurthy & Yauch, 2007; Mason-Jones et al., 2000). De-

implementation of lean manufacturing whereas downstream supply chain, with a fluctuating 
demand, is most fit for agile manufacturing (Naylor et al., 1999). This also refers to the 
postponement concept as it refers to delaying operations in the system until the customer is 
received, or the demand becomes known (Hoek, 2000). The two production systems, separated by 
the de-coupling point, in a supply chain have their own objectives following either lean or agile 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
Page 25 of 101 
principles (see Appendix F). Thus, the supply chain can realize the benefits aggregated from the 
two parts simultaneously.  
 
2.4 Continuous Improvement 
Continuous Improvement (CI) is defined in general term as a culture of sustained improvement 
targeting the reduction of waste throughout all processes and operations of an organization 
(Bhuiyan & Baghel, 2005). This implies that improvement is made in form of process streamlining 
which refers to the minimizing the non-value adding activities that emerge through time. This can 
be related to any kind of work apart from production-related activities. Since generally a process 
is likely to include people from more than one department, continuous improvement is made 
possible with involvement of people. The improvements can be done in relatively slow pulses of 
incremental changes or a large-scale revolutionary action (Kreye, 2020). Thus, it refers to 
continuous improvements being occurred in an ‘evolutionary’ fashion rather than ‘revolutionary’. 
This is due to the simplicity of changing a small scope of work which is more distinctive and does 
not include a complex relationship of environmental factors. The incremental changes then 
aggregate and result in a large movement towards the organizational improvement (Kreye, 2020). 
Which area is in need of improvement is dependent on the organization’s change strategies and 
the objective of the changes, for instance, some organization favor small-scale incremental 
changes when the requirement is to create localized change (Kreye, 2020).  
It is important for organizations to be active in adapting its business according to the ever-changing 
environment (Jones, 2013). Such adaptations, if successfully done, strengthen the value-creation 
ability of the organization to fit the new environmental setting. Any rational organizations would 
also do the same to compete in the market as well. This whole conception can be perceived as 
continuous improvement and thus, is crucial to any type of business. According to a study, the Red 
Queen effect states that the struggle for survival never gets easier and the implications for 
operations improvement are clear especially when competitors are actively improving their 
operations (Slack et al., 2016). Thus, the industries were forced by the industrial revolution to 
pursue continuous improvement in all aspects of an organization as much as possible to secure 
competitiveness and ensure survival of the business (Virmani et al., 2018).  
Continuous improvement concept was put in simple words by Deming, a lean management expert, 
as follow: 
 
“Improvement initiatives that increase success and reduce failures.” 
 
With this notion, there are various approach to achieve success and prevent failure depending on 
the context of the problem and the overall organization (Juergensen, 2000). For example, the 
approach can be technical-based that utilizes lean principles to refine the production and achieve 
competitiveness in a cost-effective manner, or on the other hand, the agile approach can be used 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
 
Page 26 of 101 
to enhance the internal competences to achieve competitiveness in terms of flexibility and 
responsiveness. With many possibilities for implementation of continuous improvement, it 
becomes a difficult concept to succeed at in numerous occasions despite the low investment it 
requires (Juergensen, 2000). Thus, many research work are exploring new approaches which are 
suitable for supporting continuous improvement, by which, leagile manufacturing is one of the 
approaches of interest (Virmani et al., 2018).  
It is worth mentioning that some study suggested explicitly that some of the lean and agile practices 
can promote continuous improvement. For example, PDCA approach was suggested by Owens 
(2000) that it has versatility in driving incremental changes for continuous improvement of the 
systems, processes, and operational activities in an enterprise. Moreover, some studies reported 
that modern continuous improvement program is usually associated with change initiatives 
underlining total quality management (TQM) programs from lean manufacturing (Bhuiyan & 
Baghel, 2005; Slack et al., 2016).  
Another relevant CI principle is prioritization of improvement projects. This principle manifested 
as the Kaizen priority matrix (as shown in Figure 11) which is used to measure the scale of a 
problem and prioritize the opportunities for continuous improvement. The matrix is constructed 
by two dimension measures: level of impact and effort. four quadrants. These measures prioritize 
the improvement projects into four groups: do now, do next, do last, and don’t do. Today, the 
matrix is widely adopted in the standardization and decision-making process in business and social 
contexts, especially relating to quality and safety issues (Simmons, 2018). In some instances, the 
matrix is known as a generic prioritization matrix.

3.1 Problem statements 
This study on lean-agile framework development begins with defining the problem statements of 
this project based on the research question. The research question of this study touches upon 
different subject matters in manufacturing namely, lean, agile, leagile, and continuous 
improvement. On the forefront, it would inquire a study which related to all the subject matters 
simultaneously. However, this would obstruct the degree of freedom to explore each subject more 
thoroughly. Thus, this study chose to research on each subject matter individually.  
The research question is broken down into multiple set of more specific problem statements, each 
with a focus on one subject matter. This makes the research question more manageable and creates 
structure to the framework development. The problem statements are formulated as listed below: 
1) What is the approach for implementing lean and agile principles in a manufacturing 
setting?  
2) What are the principles of leagile and the approach to adopt them into a production system? 
3) What is the approach to incorporate continuous improvement as its own distinguished 
concept to the development of a leagile production system?

Given that the project is regarding the production and operations management, with the objectives

methodology used in this thesis follows the guidance on methodological framework development 
(see original version in Appendix G), proposed in a published study from the medical research

It was based on multiple published literatures selected through scoping review. The literatures 
were investigated on their approaches for developing methodological frameworks for their 
represented industries: ecology, education, manufacturing, and healthcare. The approaches used 
in these literatures were agglomerated and synthesized to be the guidance for future work on any

The guidance for methodological framework development has 3 phases: 
Phase 1 Identifying evidence to inform the methodological framework

4 Framework Development 
4.1 Structuring the framework 
The findings have shown that the concepts of lean and agile encompass a broad knowledge 
landscape covering from the conceptual notions to operational tools and practices. Considering the 
research question, there are usually more than one approach to apply the two principles, lean and 
agile, together to a production system to build leagile competency and enhance continuous 
improvement. However, this study chose to the approach of applying lean and agile practices 
directly to the production development as these practices are seen as the representation of the lean 
and agile principles in the forms of operational activities. Thus, this approach centers around 
creating a change to the production system through the actions of the employees. By using 
supplementary theories to internalize these actions into habits, the lean and agile principles can be 
exercised subconsciously and bring about continuous improvements. 
When considering a production system, the activities are altogether carried out on an on-going 
process. The production activities in this context include all kinds of activities which contribute to 
the production in both meaningful and non-meaningful ways. The meaningful activities are value-
adding activities whereas non-meaningful activities are all the other activities apart from the value-
adding activities. Ideally, these activities are constantly being performed. An example is the 
installing of water-sensitive lightings on the liferaft makes the liferaft more functional and that the 
inventory of the lightings reduces. Another example on the contrary is the walking of operators to 
retrieve the tools from the main toolbox which does not add any values to the product, but during 
this action the product’s material may deteriorate slightly. Such instances indicate that the state of 
a production system changes continuously through the recurring actions. To create some 
interventions to such system, it may produce positive or negative effects on the effectiveness and 
efficiency of the actions. Therefore, an appropriate design of the intervention actions is important 
to induce changes in the system towards the desired outcome. For this reason, change management 
theory is used as an underlining concept for structuring the lean-agile framework. The theory in 
the overall supports the notion that implementation of new things would affect the organization 
with the intention of changing the way things are done (Kreye, 2020). However, to avoid 
confusion, ‘change’ discussed in this section differs from those in section 2.3 which are changes 
in the environment of an agile organization. 
When considering the development of organization leagility, or any competency at all, it can be 
referred to as a business endeavor to obtain another state of the organization in terms of having a 
stronger leagile competency. Thus, one can refer this development endeavor as a ‘project’ which 
is essentially a temporary endeavor to achieve one or more defined objectives (ISO, 2021). 
Therefore, a project management theory is used as an auxiliary concept for structuring the lean-
agile framework alongside change management theory.  
In particular, this study adopted the Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) cycle or known as “Deming 
cycle” into the framework development. It is a project management model for carrying out change 
(Kanbanize, 2022a). Although PDCA cycle has become an integral part of modern lean 
==PAGE:41==
 
 
 
Page 35 of 101 
management, this study utilizes this model from project management perspective. In this way, an 
implementation project of a practice, either lean or agile, can be more structured and effective 
according to the PDCA cycle principles. The PDCA cycle has four stages as shown in Figure 15. 
 
 
FIGURE 15 PLAN-DO-CHECK-ACTION (PDCA) CYCLE (KANBANIZE, 2022A) 
 
When starting a change initiative, or project, the model calls for a plan for the project. This ‘Plan’ 
phase includes recognizing the opportunity for improvement and addressing some of the basic 
requirements to achieve those improvements such as outlining the core problems, available 
resources, timeframe, etc. The project then moves to the ‘Do’ phase which is carrying out actions 
to create changes according to the plan. This phase is usually exercised in forms of pilot, or scall-
scale studies. After the actions are made, the outcomes are checked and analyzed with regards to 
the objectives of the project in the ‘Check’ phase. Then lastly, if the project was done successfully, 
the full-scale implementation is carried out in the rest of the organization in the ‘Act’ phase. 
Lessons learnt are documented and further utilized to plan the new improvement projects, 
repeating the cycle again. 
 
4.2 Findings 
This section presents the findings derived from analyzing the literature review data and interviews 
with Company X. The findings represent the key information which are used to inform the 
initiation of the framework development. In this study, six findings were derived. Each finding has 
its own specific context covering from the correlations between in the core concepts to the 
structural design of the framework.  
 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
 
Page 36 of 101 
1) Lean and agile principles are shown to have similarities regarding the objectives and 
the expected benefits towards production development.  
Lean manufacturing is built upon the ‘lean thinking’ principles which are essentially about ‘doing 
just enough to meet the expectations. Lean’s main objective is eliminating the operational waste, 
or the things that are unnecessary to produce the deliverables. This can be referred to as 
inefficiencies in the production. The inefficiencies are systematically determined one by one by 
problem-searching lean principles and are eliminated by problem-solving lean practices. Reducing 
these inefficiencies lead to lower risk of ‘things going wrong’ that, in turn, improve the quality 
level of the production output and lower the cost incurred by inefficiencies. Thus, lean principle 
in manufacturing has its focal improvement areas on cost, quality, and delivery on the foundation 
of operation efficiency. 
On the other hand, agile manufacturing is more of a business practice rather than a tool or a 
methodology to be employed to a single area of operation. The target area of this agile approach 
is not as clear as lean, in explicitly of ‘where to implement’, but its overall concept puts emphasis 
on strengthening the organizational internal competencies through facilitating the integration of 
operations. Integrated operation denotes a system comprising of well-coordinated operations that 
has high responsiveness at the connections between the one operation to another. This is made 
possible through the concept of becoming a virtual enterprise. Any changes in terms of the state 
of a production system are acknowledged faster through digital information system, as well as 
operations are made transparent through digitalized control system. Any discrepancies in the 
system can then be detected faster, or whether, a change in system setup can be distributed 
expediently through the virtual connections. So, integration of operations, in turn, creates the 
ability for a production system to be more responsive and flexible towards the changing 
requirements from the customer side. Therefore, especially in turbulent business environment, 
agile production system is able to respond to environmental changes in a cost- and time-efficient 
manner while maintaining the quality of deliverables. Overall, this study recognizes that agile 
principle focuses on the production improvement in speed, cost, and quality. 
From all above, the approach to implement lean and agile principles are different but 
complementing one another. As lean is to eliminate wastes, this denotes that it reduces 
inefficiencies in the operations, and in turn, enhance the internal competences in the overall 
production system according to the agile principle. Likewise, exercising agile practices to facilitate 
operations integration enriches management proficiency and promotes ease in collaboration. As a 
production system normally involves stakeholders from different functions to run it, the coherence 
of all operations, achieved through agile principles, can contribute to building an efficient 
operational system, or ‘lean operation’. For example, through IT-infused management system and 
reconfigurable production system, the production plan can be discussed using virtual meetings and 
routings of production can be done instantly on the ERP system. Such example indicates savings 
in time and cost in hosting physical meetings, travelling to and from the shop floor, printing routing 
and material-release documents, etc. All in all, lean and agile principles have a shared objective of 
improving production efficiency. If both are successfully used together, the production can achieve 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
 
Page 37 of 101 
improvements in terms of cost, quality, and responsiveness, according to the lean and agile 
principles. This finding can be drawn as a statement below: 
 
“Lean and agile approaches complement each other in creating a more efficient operational 
system, which can lead to reduction in cost, improvement in quality and responsiveness.” 
 
This statement conforms to conclusions from the previous work on leagile concepts. Studies 
showed that leagile supply chain achieved better customer relations and satisfaction as well as 
financial savings in different areas. However, such cases reported the improvements realized for 
the supply chain as a whole. The distinct improvements at each unit in the supply chain remained 
unfold. This finding then, contribute to addressing this statement by providing a theoretical support 
to the compatibility of lean and agile principles in bringing certain benefits for a manufacturing 
organization.  
 
2) Leagile principle is built upon the lean and agile principles through a simple coupling 
of core concepts in reducing operation wastes and improving organization 
competencies. 
From the literature review, leagile concept does not seem to have its own distinctive definition nor 
underlying principles that differentiate the concept significantly from lean and agile concept. The 
leagile principle so far was established as the adoption of the fundamental principles of lean and 
agile, accompanied by the use of de-coupling point and postponement. Hence, the development of 
leagile was widely undertaken in supply chain management field. Studies on leagile proposed that 
the principle was realized in the design of a supply chain where lean principle was used as the 
management strategy for the upstream units and agile in the downstream units of the supply chain. 
The two sections are separated by a de-coupling point and thus, are managed by different principles 
but are governed by the same business strategies, such that the company realizes benefits from the 
production without compromising the business values. Based on such case studies, it indicated that 
the leagile principle only entails the separation of the supply chain entities into lean-governed and 
agile-governed section. 
As can be seen prior in Table 6, leagile practices are a mix of lean and agile tools, for instance, 
flexible setups comprised of minimization of setup time and skilled worker to perform the setups. 
Some of the leagile practices were the exact same practices claimed by lean or agile, such as ERP, 
lead time reduction, quality assurance, etc. This indicates that a good understanding of lean and 
agile practices, is required to adopt leagile practices.  
In addition, leagile practices were shown to be very broad in terms of the included aspects to 
consider. For example, the practice of creating innovation can cover from technological invention 
to management solutions. Some managers from the interviews pointed out that leagile principle is 
sometimes naturally adopted by companies without being acknowledged. For example, 
digitalization and virtual collaborations from agile principle are deemed critical as a 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
 
Page 38 of 101 
complementary part for a successful adoption of lean practice regardless of whether the production 
has lean- or agile-dominant features (See Appendix H.6 and Appendix H.7). 
 
3) For a manufacturing organization, lean manufacturing practices target the 
production process, whereas agile manufacturing practices target the whole 
operational system encompassing the production process. 
As mentioned briefly, although lean and agile manufacturing principles share similar objectives 
towards operational improvements, the two differ in the area of improvement. The core idea of 
lean manufacturing is to focus on eliminating the activities, of both human and machine, in the 
value stream that are considered waste, or not adding any value to the deliverable. When an 
inefficient process in the value stream is discovered, lean practices are applied to redesign and 
recreate this process so that it only contains a set of activities which are necessary to produce the 
desire output. Based on this, an improved value stream can be achieved by targeting improvements 
on one process at a time. Given that the improvements create changes directly at the production 
process, it impacts the production characteristics and performance directly, in terms of cycle time, 
production lead time, level of work-in-progress, and more. Such measures are to an extent, 
quantitative, providing a clear result of the improvement.  
On the other hand, agile manufacturing principles give more emphasis to the surrounding system 
of a production as much as the production process itself. According to the twelve principles of 
agile manifesto, the overall conception of the agile way of working is about the repetition of 
producing a working solution. The agile principle breaks down the deliverables into multiple low-
complexity sub-deliverables which are assessed against the customer’s requirement. A sub-
deliverable is used to drive the changes in the subsequent process to ensure highest effectiveness 
of the next sub-deliverable, and so on, so forth. In the manufacturing context, agile principle is 
essentially assessing of the output at each production phase, or process, and modifying the 
subsequent process to ensure the effectiveness of the production to the changing environment. In 
order to carry out such changes, it requires good collaboration within the organization as well as a 
supporting technological infrastructure (Gunasekaran, 1999). This can refer to the integration of 
operation where departments as individual silos are connected by digital communication system. 
This promotes productivity and quality through flexibility of access and utilization of resources 
and information, rather than through stepwise improvements in certain process. In overall, agile 
manufacturing is recognized as a management strategy focusing on integrating operations at intra- 
and inter-organization levels. Based on the theories, this strategy involves facilitating rapid 
partnership, building a virtual enterprise, and creating a production system which has 
reconfigurability and mass customization. Thus, this study perceives agile manufacturing as an 
improvement strategy that targets the organization surrounding the production system with the aim 
to accommodate the needed changes in the production process. This is supported by Hamel and 
Prahald (1996) who claimed that agile manufacturing is a boundary-focused organization whereas 
lean manufacturing is a process-focused organization. Moreover, it is supported by Ramesh and 
==PAGE:45==
 
 
 
Page 39 of 101 
Devadasan (2007) who proposed that agile manufacturing calls for radical change in the 
organizational system, management style, and culture. 
 
4) Lean and agile practices have different level of complexity and thus, have different 
level of requirements in terms of resources, time, involvement, and digital 
infrastructure.  
From the previous findings, this study recognizes the difference in the area of deployment for lean 
and agile manufacturing principles. This brings about another analysis on the requirements for 
implement the principles in the actual settings. As lean targets improvement on a specific process, 
it indicates that lean projects tend to have a small scope of intervention. Examples include changing 
of methodology in one workstation to reduce cycle time, redesigning layout of workstations, 
installing mechanisms into workstations to perform quality control, etc. Such examples signify 
that the intended changes are expected to occur at the process to create an impact on the process 
output but not the other aspects in the production system.  
On the contrary, agile targets the intervention in many functions which are directly and indirectly 
related to the production process. Examples include using Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) 
system to control a proper material feed to the production lines, training worker to gain more 
knowledge horizontally and vertically to enable flexibility in job assignment, collaborating 
digitally using online meeting tools and central data archives to facilitate faster information sharing 
throughout the organization, etc. The intended changes of agile principles are expected to occur to 
different areas of the operational system which related to the production process. Considering the 
definition of a value-creation process by ISO, agile principle induces change in the process input 
and the value-creation process through the changes in the environment surrounding the process. 
Accordingly, the process output is influenced. This indicates that agile principle has a wider range 
of affected areas. Moreover, many agile practices are related use of digital software or data system. 
This indicates that agile manufacturing demands some level of digitalization as well as the 
worker’s skill to work with digitalized system. 
Generally, one of the sources of complexity of a system incurred from interconnections between 
the entities within the system. Introducing changes to these entities, if not managed properly, can 
lead to unwanted negative outcomes. So, the more areas being affected by a change intervention, 
the more difficulty in managing the change towards success. Therefore, this study sees agile 
manufacturing as a more complex principle to be adopted given that agile practices usually touch 
upon multiple areas in an organization. Implementation of agile practice calls for reorientation of 
a larger group of people relative to lean practice. Hence, agile principles demand higher level of 
investment in terms of resources, time, cost, and digital infrastructure but potentially create a more 
far-reaching impact on the organization than lean.

Page 40 of 101 
5) Lean and agile principles are rooted in continuous improvement principle. By 
implementing lean and agile manufacturing in practice, continuous improvement is 
exercised.  
From the literature review, the core principle of lean manufacturing is lean thinking which is a 
cyclic process of defining new improvements for a process based on a given set of values. Thus, 
lean by the concept itself is already based on creating improvement continuously. Given that the 
business environment changes, today’s efficient processes may become inefficient in the future as 
they may not effectively serves the expectations that has been changed (see Appendix H.7). 
Therefore, the company should always be open for ideas for improvement and discussed them 
through (see Appendix H.5). 
In agile, continuous improvement can reflected strongly from the original work of Agile 
Manifesto. The manifesto essentially encourages an organization to embrace changes and 
adaptability of the organizations to these changes. For example, agile principle is about producing 
smaller work progress in iterations so that each project can capture adequate level of certainty from 
the present time. In other word, agile projects are simplified to have shorter completion time to 
avoid new uncertainties in the future. By this idea, milestones within a project provide the 
opportunities for organizations to reassess the work and incorporate needed improvements to the 
work. Apart from Agile Manifesto, modern agile principles are shown to be embedded with 
continuous improvement mindset as that they are accepted in today’s industry to provide an 
effective approach for business in a dynamic environment. As can be seen in Table 4, one of the 
main attributes of an agile organization is having a culture of change. Additionally, the modern 
agile manufacturing practices altogether aim towards integrating organization’s operations to 
enable fast and efficient information sharing and communication. They facilitate continuous 
improvement in the way that they allow faster decision-making process, especially, for adapting 
to changes, so that the organization improves their work to fit the new business environment (see 
Appendix H.6).  
Overall, by using different tools and practices from lean and agile tools and practices, the 
organization is already creating continuous improvement (see Appendix H.5, and Appendix H.7). 
However, it is reported from managers from Company X that maintaining the implemented lean 
and agile practices in the long-term is one of the critical issues which deteriorates continuous 
improvement (see Appendix H.4, Appendix H.5, Appendix H.6, and Appendix H.7). Therefore, to 
effectively create continuous improvement, it is important to build improvement-oriented 
mindsets, and ultimately a continuous improvement culture (see Appendix H.4, Appendix H.5, 
Appendix H.6, and Appendix H.7). In addition to this, a manager from Company X stated that 
knowledge sharing is crucial for building this continuous improvement culture (see Appendix 
H.4), especially for large companies. The preservation of knowledge and expertise of current 
employees can build a foundation for promoting a continuous improvement culture among future 
generations of employees.

Page 41 of 101 
6) The characteristics of the production system should be assessed in the initial phase to 
determine the appropriate extent of lean and agile implementation. 
As implementation of new principles refers to making changes to the existing system, it is 
important to understand the existing system to create an appropriate intervention that ensure 
success. In this study, it can be referred to as current state analysis which includes studying the 
production process, product characteristics, business objectives and values, organizational culture, 
and other factors that influence the approach to manage the production. From the interviews, some 
managers from Company X reported that it is not practical for an improvement project to changes 
the production processes, but it should rather support them (see Appendix H.4, Appendix H.7, and 
Appendix H.9). So, with the understanding of the characteristics of the existing system, the 
company can perform an analysis to search for rooms for improvement as well as setting a realistic 
boundary for the changes. Moreover, in change management theory, managers can use the 
information about the production to communicate the cause and effects of the changes more 
effectively to stakeholders (Kreye, 2020). In this way, managers can build a common ground of 
understanding the current state and addresses issue where both sides may have their own 
perceptions of the future state when implementing improvement project. This can reduce the force 
of resistance to change, or negative attitude of stakeholders. From the interviews, all managers 
from Company X claimed that the most prominent challenge in any change projects is the human 
factor (see Appendix H). People generally have different level of expertise and perception of thing, 
especially, experienced managers who would likely to see things from the practical perspective, 
and guide the projects based on their experiences. By introducing a theoretical framework as a 
guideline for adopting lean and agile practices, the biases from the one own’s experience can be 
diminished, and all stakeholders’ perception and expectation can be easily aligned. This makes it 
easier to set a starting point and define a practical direction for the change (see Appendix H.7).  
From all above, it is useful to incorporate current state analysis into the framework to help 
managers set a realistic lean-agile implementation project with respect to the characteristics of the 
production process. This can be done by analyzing the production characteristics and KPIs for 
production (see Appendix H.3). The analysis should provide information on the type of production 
of the company to determine if the production is lean-dominant or agile-dominant, which builds a 
foundation for setting the directions of change and discovers limitations to the adoption of lean 
and agile practices. Moreover, given that businesses measure their performance on KPIs, it is 
important that the change projects towards developing a leagile production system are bounded to 
improve these KPIs as well (see Appendix H.4, and Appendix H.10). Therefore, the framework 
should also incorporate KPIs analysis that can provide suggestions for the appropriate lean and 
agile practices to be implemented to the production system. 
 
4.3 Development of the Lean-Agile Framework 
With regards to the research question, this study aims to build the framework as a guideline for the 
company to achieve leagile in the long-term. This gives the notion that a sustainable change is 
needed to be ensured from using this framework. Therefore, this study adopts Lewin’s field theory 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
 
Page 42 of 101 
and three-step process in change management theory to explain the science behind building a 
sustainable change (Kreye, 2020). Lewin stated that any level of behavior is maintained in a 
condition of quasi-stationary equilibrium by a force-field comprising a balance of forces pushing 
for and resisting change (see Figure 16). This level of behavior can be changed by either adding 
forces for change in the desired direction or by diminishing the opposing or resisting forces.  
 
 
FIGURE 16 A FORCE FIELD OF THE STATE OF AN ORGANIZATION AT A CERTAIN POINT IN TIME (KREYE, 2020) 
 
Based on the field theory, Lewin’s three-step process entails three steps of a successful intervention 
to this equilibrium: unfreezing the existing level of behavior, moving to a new level, and refreezing 
behavior at this new level (Kreye, 2020). The first step is creating a vision of a more desirable 
future state, by which, if communicated properly to organizational members, it can create a sense 
of urgency and motivation among the group to let go of the current working behaviors. In the other 
words, the state of current behaviors unfreezes. This paves ways to the second phase, movement, 
which including adjusting attitudes and mindsets of the people and modifying the processes, 
systems, and structures to the one that shape the desired behaviors. Finally, the new behaviors are 
set out to be maintained in the third phase, refreezing. By reinforcing the new behaviors, the new 
level of performance can be retained, and regression can be avoided. This phase involves feedback 
loops, reward systems, performance monitoring system, and more.  
As the framework is to be used to create continuous improvement, it refers to the iterative and 
cyclical process of change projects. Thus, this study refers to the ‘change wheel’ that captures the 
essence of Lewin’s three-step process but with more generalized terms. The change wheel is as 
shown in Figure 17.

Adopting the mentioned principles from change management, the lean-agile framework is 
constructed by three subsequent parts: current state assessment, project selection, and evaluation 
and establishment. Overall, this structure encapsulates Lewin’s three-step process towards a 
sustainable change and support user from the ideation of the lean-agile implementation projects to 
the post-execution evaluation. The structure of the lean-agile framework is established as shown 
in Figure 18.  
 
 
FIGURE 18 STRUCTURE OF THE LEAN-AGILE FRAMEWORK 
 
The first part of the framework is the assessment system of the current production. As KPIs are 
usually designed to be tailored to the company’s business strategies, this part allows the company 
to assess their own KPIs from the lean-agile perspective and determine the target dimension for 
improvement project. The second part is the project selection evolving around the lean-agile 
practice selection model which gives an overview of the common tools and practices from lean 
and agile. A Kaizen priority matrix is also included in part 2 to give prioritization to the selected 
practices. The last part is the evaluation and establishment that provides basic guidelines to finalize 
the pilot test of the new practice and create a system to facilitate continuity of that practice. 
 
Part 1 Current State Assessment 
This part comprises of two analyses: KPI translation and defining the objective of the lean-agile 
project. This part resonates to the ‘Plan’ phase of the PDCA cycle which essentially establishes 
the definition of the project according to the organization’s goal. From the interviews, the 
production managers stated clearly that the implementation of any lean or agile tool must respect 
the nature of the production system as it is the critical operational system for a manufacturing 
company (see Appendix H.10). The changes to be implemented must not disturb or modify the 
production process but rather support the system which encloses it, and in turn, improve the 
production from the influence created by its surroundings. Therefore, to study the production 
characteristics, one possible solution is to look into the KPIs for the production as they demonstrate 
the effectiveness of a company in achieving its key business objectives. So, this study incorporates 
this notion into the first analysis based on the lean-agile outlook, called KPIs translation.

Part 1.1 KPI translation  
The KPI translation is the process that convert the production’s KPIs into the extent of lean and 
agile characteristic of the production system. It is carried out in a number of steps as shown in the 
schematic flow in Figure 19.  
 
 
FIGURE 19 STEPS FOR KPI TRANSLATION 
 
The process starts with determining the production areas to be the target for improvement. Based 
on these areas, one can determine the production-related KPIs of the company, the dimension of 
the production used to measure each of them, and the range of possible value for the measure. It 
is important that the KPI measures are quantitative as their values are used to calculate lean-agile 
characteristics in the later steps. It is also worth mentioning that the unit of measurement is also 
important to indicate the magnitude, in some instances, with regards to time, of the measured 
values. It can help user to avoid confusion as well. Moreover, the range of possible value must be 
included as it helps the user comprehend the magnitude each KPI value. This can be obtained from 
analyzing the historical data and other statistical studies.  
Given that all relevant KPIs are listed, they are categorized into two groups: lean-focused KPIs 
and agile-focused KPIs. This can be done by referring to the core concepts of lean and agile. KPIs 
that are related closer to the practice of minimizing waste can be grouped as lean-focused KPIs, 
whereas KPIs that are closely related to increasing responsiveness of the production can be 
grouped as agile-focused KPIs.   
After the grouping, the number of KPIs belonging each group becomes more apparent. As in some 
instances, KPIs are perceived to have different significance due to the company strategy, current 
business situations, or else. Therefore, the KPIs in each group are given a weight on a scale of 0 
to 1, where the total sum of weight of all KPIs in one group is equal to 1.  
Furthermore, the value range of each KPI measure is segmented into three smaller ranges including 
low, medium, high which indicates the extremity of being lean or agile. The three ranges are 
encoded with arbitrary numbers: 1, 4, and 7 accordingly. This set of encoding values imitates the 
7-point Likert scale which is commonly used in research to collect qualitative data which requires 
a neutral point. Using this approach can accommodate KPIs that are transformed from a qualitative 
==PAGE:51==
 
 
 
Page 45 of 101 
data such as customer’s satisfaction or employee’s perceived support. Moreover, the mentioned 
numbers are used to magnify the differences of between one range to another when compared to 
another set of numbers such as 1, 2, 3 or 1, 3, 5. For example, if a ‘fast delivery’ KPI measure can 
range from 0 to 30 days, then this range can be divided into the range of 0 to 10 days, 11 to 20 
days, and 21 to 30 days. Given that shorter order-to-delivery (OTD) period indicates 
responsiveness of the organization, the range from 0 to 10 days is considered as high level of agile 
and is encoded with number 7, with other ranges in the decreasing manner.

weights) are defined, the KPI table is as established in Figure 20.

Part 1.2 Definition of improvement objective 
The second analysis is about determining the direction of the lean-agile implementation project. 
From the findings, it is established that lean and agile practices can create improvements in three 
dimensions: cost, quality, and responsiveness. However, it does not indicate that a one-time 
implementation of lean and agile practice can cover all three dimensions simultaneously. This 
brings about the common issue of selecting which dimension the company wants to target the 
improvements. The term ‘delivery’ is used to represent responsiveness as it covers a broader 
perspective of creating a value proposition to the customer as well as being responsive to change. 
This analysis aims to tackle this issue by providing a decision-support guideline for companies to 
determine the critical dimension of improvement based on their KPIs and the business context. 
The analysis uses the KPI scoring table as shown in Figure 23.

assessed and scored by production managers according to their affinity to cost, quality, and 
delivery on a scale of 1 to 7 based on the Likert scale. The scoring is based on the manager’s 
perception and experiences on the impacts of the KPIs on the three dimensions of the production 
or the level of governance enhanced from using the KPIs. Although the KPIs are given weight of 
importance, but in this analysis, an addition weight factor of 0.5 is used to calculate the total score.

KPIs in both groups. This means that, on the overview, the lean- and agile- focused KPI groups

Part 2 Project Selection 
From the literature review, there are a large number of tools and practices in lean and agile 
manufacturing. However, it is generally difficult to implement large number of those practices at 
the same time as it creates a strong pressure of change on stakeholders, and potentially induce 
them to go into a state of extreme shock and denial (Kreye, 2020). Therefore, this study proposes 
a lean-agile practice selection model which presents a selection of lean and agile practices already 
known by the company and provides suggestions for choosing the practices based on their 
complexity and dominant area of improvement as discussed followingly.  
Part 2.1 Lean-agile practice selection model 
As there is a large number of lean and agile practices, it can be difficult for one to comprehend all 
of them or compare one to another. To ease this issue, the first draft of the framework was 
developed to present a small set of lean and agile practices which are founded commonly in the 
industries. The draft was created in a graphical representation as shown in Figure 25.

Some studies proposed that agile manufacturing principles comprises of lean principles with extra 
elements such as advanced manufacturing technologies, or flexible production setups (Sarkis, 
2001). Such findings supported the notion that lean manufacturing principles is the basis for agile 
manufacturing principles, and the implementation of lean practices can positively impact the later 
implementation of agile practices. Therefore, the draft of the project selection model is constructed 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
 
Page 50 of 101 
by presenting lean practices in the lower level and agile in the upper level. Moreover, continuous 
improvement is expressed in forms of a using this framework iteratively.  
This part of the framework presents a categorization of the lean and agile practices which were 
mentioned among the interviewees of this study (see Figure 26). Interestingly, most of the 
mentioned practices were presented as popular practices in the literature review (see section 2.1 
and 2.2). Thus, the interviews were utilized as a filter to select the practices to be included into the 
framework. The result included 12 lean practices and 11 agile practices.

As mentioned in the interview, the decision to adopt a practice should not be done arbitrary. Many 
criteria for choosing a practice were discussed in the interviews. An interview with managers of 
Company X mentioned that choosing to perform low-complexity quick-wins projects can increase 
the success rate of future advanced projects that require high investment and involvement. Such 
fruitful projects can drive the momentum for pursuing changes and continuous improvement 
towards leagility (see Appendix H.5, and Appendix H.6). Apart from its complexity of the practice, 
an interview with a manager of country B proposed that other factors, such as demand quantity 
and variation, are the main criteria when considering a new practice (see Appendix H.4). However, 
all interviews showed a common agreement that there are more than these two criteria that should 
be used, especially human-related criteria. Therefore, this study utilized the 5Ms principle in lean 
management theory to form the criteria for selecting which and when practice should be adopted. 
The principle includes manpower, machine (equipment, technology), materials, methods, 
measurements (Allred, 2019). These five aspects manifested into four criteria: level of engagement 
of stakeholders in the implementation project, level of demanded investment (in terms of capital, 
human resources, and time), level of skills and expertise in a specific topic, and maturity level of 
organizational digital infrastructure. This set of criteria was used to sort the selected lean and agile 
practices in the framework.

of improvement and steps towards leagile improvement. 
 
 
FIGURE 27 LEAN-AGILE PRACTICE SELECTION MODEL 
 
Given that companies have different levels of ‘readiness for change’ or capacity to implement new 
changes, the derivation of results from this model can varied from one company to another. The 
framework does not strictly suggest any direction to adopt one practice before another. The model 
merely presents the perception on complexity from a group of lean and agile practitioners, 
supported by theoretical definitions of these practices.  
As can be seen in Figure 27, leagile practices were not included in the lean-agile practice selection 
model. This is due to the findings from the literature review that leagile principle is built from the 
principles of lean and agile and that many leagile practices are similar to that of lean and agile, or 
a mix of both. So, it will be repetitive information within the framework. This study recognizes 
that, by following both lean and agile principles, the organization is essentially pursuing leagility 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
 
Page 53 of 101 
in the way that fits their business context. If leagile practices from theory were included in the 
framework, they may restrict the company to implement a set of practices to achieve only one 
leagile practice and potentially incur unnecessary costs in doing so.  
However, a question remains regarding whether how many practices should be adopted to enhance 
leagility of the production system. The number of practices to be adopted in a company should fit 
the nature of the production in that way that complements the production characteristics (see 
Appendix H.9). This issue can be addressed by the results of the KPIs translation in the first phase 
of the framework. Specifically, the framework showed the extent of leanness and agility of the 
production system. This information can be used to choose the improvement projects in lean and 
agile accordingly. For example, if a production is a mass production, part 1 of the framework 
would likely suggest high level of leanness and low level of agility. Thus, the company can choose 
to implement three practices in the lean group from low to medium complexity and one agile 
practice with low complexity.  
Part 2.2 Kaizen priority matrix 
With this selection model, the framework can help managers in planning a roadmap towards 
leagility of the production system but the decisions of which practices or how many of them to be 
adopted are dependent on the manager’s judgement. This is because the framework does not cover 
the examination on other organizational factors, such as culture or standard operating procedures. 
So, this part of the framework requires the managers to finalize the selection by taking in account 
their expertise in their business (see Appendix H.11). 
This brings about the second analysis of part 2 of the framework which focuses on prioritizing the 
selected tools and practices. This analysis centers on the use of the Kaizen priority matrix as 
founded through the literature review (see Figure 11). This matrix allows the company to consider 
the organizational context surrounding the implementation of the lean-agile practices as projects. 
The impact dimension in the matrix can become a point of discussion regarding numbers of 
departments in the company, the relationships between them, or the extent that the improvement 
project can exercise the company’s strategy towards the goals, etc. The effort dimension, on the 
other hand, can be related to the number of employees required to initiate the project in relation to 
the total number of employees in the company. The effort level would suggest the sense of 
sacrificing the resources to carry out the improvement project. For example, if a company has 10 
office employees and 30 shop floor employees, then the practice of building knowledgeable 
worker (as an agile quality-focused practice) would refer to a training program for 75% of the 
employees, thus, requiring high effort in doing so. Therefore, this matrix is used to derive a 
structured approach towards the improvement projects of the selected practices. 
The part of the framework should not be used as a representative of ‘perfection’, but rather, as a 
structured view on the practices which are suggested to be used for developing a leagile production 
(see Appendix H.6, and Appendix H.7). For example, after the first lean improvement project is 
done, the company should continue to part 3 of this framework to evaluate the outcome of the 
project and establish the project officially. Then, the company can use part 1 of the framework 
again to assess the new state of the production system and verify the existing roadmap for future 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
 
Page 54 of 101 
lean-agile projects. In this sense, the framework would not restrict the company to follow the 
roadmap completely but give suggestions for future plans based on any given production state. 
In the overall, part 2 of the framework calls for the use of PDCA principles to ensure an effective 
change implementation project. This part of the framework helps the company plan for the lean 
and agile implementation projects, according to ‘Plan’ phase. The next phase the ‘Do’ phase which 
the company tests the proposed change in a pilot project in a certain area in the organization. By 
this approach, negative response from stakeholders can be minimized since a smaller-sized 
intervention creates smaller effects on the system’s entities. The company then evaluates the 
outcome of the first implementation project using the next part of the framework which represents 
the ‘check’ phase of the PDCA cycle, before continuing to the next suggested practice.  
The conclusion from this part needs to be acknowledged with caution as it represents a series of 
improvement projects which takes considerable amount of time to complete. Given the further 
away the point in time is, the more uncertainty there is. So, the company needs to revise the 
suggested programs after the completion of each project. The decision of what kind of conclusion 
should be taken from this part is dependent on the manager to make in order to fit the improvements 
to the business strategy. 
 
Part 3 Evaluation and Establishment 
Part 3.1 Project checklist 
At this point, the framework assumed that the company have implemented the lean and agile 
practice in a certain area of the organization according to the suggestions given by the framework. 
This part of the framework, called the evaluation and establishment, incorporates the ‘Check’ and 
‘Act’ phase of the PDCA cycle into proposing guideline for the company to assess the 
implemented project and sustain the new working practices. 
The framework suggests using the same set of KPIs used in the first part of the framework to 
compare pre- and post-implementation states. Using this method, the company can quantify the 
results in terms of KPI measures which are naturally deemed important to the business directly. 
Additional evaluation method can be employed by the company to ensure qualitative 
improvements, such as employee and customer satisfaction.  
Given that the framework suggests implementing one new practice at a time and only for a certain 
area of the organization, it is possible that the KPI measures may not have been affected. Therefore, 
a simple checklist is proposed to measure the completion of the pilot project as shown in Figure 
28. The three points for the checklist are established following the criteria suggested in change 
management theory (see Appendix I).

Part 3.2 Sustaining the adopted practice 
As lean and agile principles are adopted in forms of working practice, it is important that they are 
continuously exercised to the future. Thus, apart from marking the project done, building a system 
to facilitate the new practice in the organization is important for realizing the full benefit. This 
system essentially comprises of stakeholders, or employees who are involved in the pilot project, 
and the reporting system to be used in audits of the new practice. 
The former plays the main role in carrying out the new practice, and the latter verifies the existence 
and performance of that process. With the establishment of the two factors, the company can 
proceed to standardize the new practice and handover the project to other areas of the organization. 
The framework suggests three steps for the process as illustrated in Figure 29.  
 
 
FIGURE 29 STEPS FOR SUSTAINING THE NEW PRACTICE 
 
This stepwise process adopted the theory in sustaining change to ensure a smooth transition 
between one improvement project to another (see Appendix J). The process starts with a project 
wrap-up which evolves around finalizing the results and standardizing the approach to implement 
the practice. It includes documenting the lesson learnt, performing risk analysis for undesired 
scenarios, and creating standard operating procedures (SOPs) if the implemented practice includes 
a use of any device or software. To magnify the effects of the implemented practices, the 
framework recommends the standardizing the new working practice. From this step, the 
information for the new practice is gathered and used for project briefing with all the key 
stakeholders such as future project managers and top managers. The project brief enables the 
knowledge of the new practice to spread across the organization and increase awareness for the 
upcoming change, in turn, inducing driving forces for change. The brief can be carried out in 
==PAGE:62==
 
 
 
Page 56 of 101 
meetings with each group of stakeholders to discuss the details of the new practices, the standards 
and the audits for the new practice. The necessary key documents, or procedures for the project 
handover can be listed in a checklist to ensure completeness (see Appendix H.10). The documents 
can include SOPs, reporting document format, digital files for documenting data etc. They are 
shared to the key stakeholder, or responsible person, in the project handover step to be used for 
the implementation of the new practice in other areas. However, there is an exception that if 
company has multiple production systems which have large differences in their characteristics, the 
project can only be handed over to the managers of the operational area which the pilot project 
was done. Thus, the new practice is managed by the operational manager to ensure its continuity. 
This process represents the ‘Act’ phase in the PDCA cycle which refers to the actions of creating 
change on the larger scale.  
This part of the framework connects the gap between testing the suggested practice in a small 
operational area and establishing a system for integration of the new practice into the norm as well 
as future adoption in other area. Based on the ‘moment of critical mass’ concept in change 
management theory, when the groundwork for the change adoption has been done, there comes a 
point in time after the implementation when the change has a momentum of its own without the 
needs for attention to make it stick (Kreye, 2020). Therefore, the study recognizes the importance 
for building a system specifically to facilitate the new practice until it reaches the moment of 
critical mass. This marks the end of the framework. 
After part 3 of framework, the company can choose to start from part 1 again to assess the new 
state of the production system, or factory, Then, they can use part 2 to verify the suggestions given 
by the framework in the first time. However, the use of the framework is flexible to the 
organizational context of the company, especially part 2. Finally, after a pilot project, part 3 can 
be used to evaluate the results and employ the new practice in the larger area.

Framework implementation 
Given the lean-agile framework in Figure 30, this study carries out a pilot test of the framework at 
Company X. As mentioned, part 2 of the framework suggested a small-scale implementation

5.1 Production site at Company X 
The partner company of this study, Company X, is one of the largest global manufacturers of safety 
equipment. Currently, the company has eight production sites in six countries around the world, 
producing different variety of products. In this study, the framework was tested at the production 
site in Country D. 
Company X’s production site in country D produces mainly three products: liferafts, maritime 
evacuation system (MES), and personnel protection equipment (PPE) suits. The evaluation system 
consists of a metal frame for connection with the maritime vessel, evacuation chutes as the escape 
tunnel for humans, and folded liferafts which blow up when the system is deployed. The three 
products are, by global standard, very different in their characteristics and design. Moreover, given 
Company X’s specialty and expertise in the industry, the three products are further developed with 
a wide range of variations. For example, the model of maritime evacuation system (MES) offered 
by Company X are distinguished by two factors: embarkation height and embarkation mean. As 
one can simply cannot evacuate from a 20-meter-high cruise by simply climbing down a ladder 
along with hundreds of fellow passengers in time of safety, variations of the MES is developed to 
fit different heights level of the passenger vessel. Thus, this product has four model groups for four 
different evacuation height: 0 to 1.5 meters, 1.5 to 4.2 meters, 5 to 16 meters, and 8.3 to 20.2 
meters. Depending on the height level, the MES can be categorized further into a chute-based 
system and a slide-based system where chute or slide is installed as the means to evacuate from 
the maritime vessel to the liferaft. Such examples can be reflected in the portfolio of other products 
of Company X. Therefore, the production systems for Company X’s products are highly different, 
both in terms of production facilities and management. Given that the production site in country 
D is producing three products, the company has three distinguish departments to manage each of 
the production system. 
This study chose to employ the lean-agile framework on the production system of liferafts as this 
product is most significant in values compared to the other two products, MES and PPE (see 
Appendix H.2). The production system of liferaft in this production site is mainly comprised of 
unautomated assembly process. The main body of the liferaft is produced in country T and is sent 
to country D for final assembly, packing, and delivering. The main body of the liferaft is called 
‘raw raft’ which becomes an inflatable pre-made product to be fed into the assembly lines in 
country D. The assembly process in country D is responsible for installing the mechanical system 
and accessories such as automatic light, towing lines, connection lines, emergency packs, etc. 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
 
Page 58 of 101 
Moreover, the assembly covers the packaging of the finished liferafts into standard containers and 
delivering them to customers. 
From the observation and interviews, the current production site at Company X has adopted some 
lean and agile practices prior to this study (see Appendix H.1). One example is the Kaizen program 
which was implemented as a documentation system that employees can use to propose 
improvement ideas. However, from conversations with the shop floor employees, it was observed 
that they acknowledged the Kaizen program but there was not a lot of improvement ideas being 
submitted. This is possibly due to the production area which is very limited in space and the 
production standards which does not allow many changes as they may impact the product quality. 
Another adopted practice is Kanban. The company uses the Kanban system to feed the materials 
into the production line as well as to initiate production process. Lastly, the company has an on-
going 5S implementation program which is bound to be used in all global production sites. The 5S 
project at country D was scheduled around the same time as this study. Thus, the framework was 
implemented to validate the company’s decision towards 5S and to guide the company further in 
sustaining the 5S practice, limited to liferaft production system.

Discussion 
This study evolves around the research question: how can lean and agile principles be applied 
simultaneously to a production system to promote continuous improvement? To answer this 
research question, three problem statements were derived to tackle each subject matter:  
• What is the approach for implementing lean and agile principles in a manufacturing 
setting?  
• What are the principles of leagile and the approach to adopt them into a production system?  
• What is the approach to incorporate continuous improvement as its own distinguished 
concept to the development of a leagile production system? 
The problem statements were addressed by the six findings of this study (see section 4.2 for full 
context) as listed briefly below: 
1) Lean and agile principles are shown to have similarities regarding the objectives and the 
expected benefits towards production development.  
2) Leagile principle is built upon the lean and agile principles through a simple coupling of 
core concepts in reducing operation wastes and improving organization competencies. 
3) For a manufacturing organization, lean manufacturing practices target the production 
process, whereas agile manufacturing practices target the whole operational system 
encompassing the production process. 
4) Lean and agile practices have different level of complexity and thus, have different level 
of requirements in terms of resources, time, involvement, and digital infrastructure.  
5) Lean and agile principles are rooted in continuous improvement principle. By 
implementing lean and agile manufacturing in practice, continuous improvement is 
exercised.  
6) The characteristics of the production system should be assessed in the initial phase to 
determine the appropriate extent of lean and agile implementation. 
The first problem statement was addressed by theoretical data and information from interviews, as 
they were agglomerated into findings number 1, 3, 4, and 6. The second problem statement 
regarding leagile was also addressed in the similar manner. Finding number 2 was derived 
particularly to answer this problem statement. Lastly, the third problem statement was addressed 
by finding number 5 which discussed continuous improvement concept as the foundation of lean 
and agile improvements. For further elaboration, this chapter presents the discussion on the lean-
agile framework and the approach that it manifested towards production improvement. 
 
Connecting the findings to the framework 
In this study, the lean-agile framework was developed based on two sources of information: 
literature review and interviews (including meetings). The first findings obtained from following 
the methodology showed that the lean and agile principles shared similarities regarding the 
objectives and the expected benefits when applied to a production setting. This gives a validation 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
 
Page 65 of 101 
point for including both lean and agile practices together into one framework with the expectation 
for the company to assess them simultaneously. This conclusion manifested in two forms: the 
design of part 1.2 Definition of improvement objective and part 2.1 Lean-Agile project selection 
model. The former utilized this finding into the process of structuring the KPI scoring table where 
both lean and agile practices are scored according to the same set of improvement dimensions. The 
latter used the finding as a structural factor of the lean-focused and agile-focused practices, and as 
one of the two sorting criteria of the practices.  
In addition to the first finding, the framework design was also informed by the third and the fourth 
findings which suggested that the approach to implement lean and agile practices are different in 
terms of the scope of employment and complexity level. These two findings showed that a 
structured assessment is needed to address the question of “what” and “when” for the improvement 
project. Thus, they were utilized in the design of part 2 Project selection in sorting and prioritizing 
the practices. In the design process, a scoring method was used to sort the practices according to 
four complexity criteria, inspired by the MCDM principle. The criteria were designed in the way 
that consolidate the two findings into a quantitative analysis by reflecting on the different aspects 
in the production system. This provided a structured approach for the comparison of one practice 
to another. Furthermore, the two findings also brought about the Kaizen priority matrix in part 2.2 
as it can be used to address the practicality in progressing with the selected practices. 
Lastly, part 2.1 of the framework was influenced by the second findings in this study. Although 
the framework as a whole targets the development of a leagile production system, but this finding 
directly led to the exclusion of the leagile practices in the design of the project selection model. 
This may limit the company’s perception on the leagile principle with regards to this framework. 
The framework only provides the information on the relative complexity of the practices between 
the lean and agile group, in particularly, for the included set of practices. Given this setup, the 
framework can be perceived as a methodological guideline on implementing lean and agile 
practices in succession, under the same improvement program, rather than in a simultaneous 
manner. In this way, continuous improvement is exercised over a series of lean and agile projects, 
in turn, developing leagile character to the production system. Moreover, this setup indicates that 
the framework design is tailored to the partner company to a great extent. Especially that the 
inclusion of practices in the model were from the company’s representatives. This was perceived 
as the generalization process of this framework which based on the different cultural settings and 
managers’ expertise. This approach was proven beneficial in terms of the practicality of the 
framework as this design would give the company a better sense in comprehending the practices 
presented by the framework. Moreover, if the managers from one production site are not familiar 
with some practices in the framework, they can always learn from another manger using the 
company’s communication infrastructure. This is way, the framework encourages organizational 
learning and continuous improvements, particularly the managers’ knowledge and skills.  
In relation to the fifth finding of this study, it informed the relationship between lean and agile 
practices to the concept of continuous improvement. When considering the concept as its own 
theory, it is essentially about creating any kinds of initiatives that increase success and reduce 
failures of a process. This resonates to the lean and agile principles by their core concepts. The 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
 
Page 66 of 101 
reduction of waste, as the core concept of lean, can refer to reducing cost and increasing operational 
efficiency which ultimately promote organization’s success in terms of its ability to obtain 
resources from its environment. Exercising the core concept of lean through lean tools and 
practices is, in one way, can create continuous improvement in the process as well as the culture. 
The similar explanation can be made for the agile principle. Improving the organization’s core 
competences through operational integration refers to the stronger ability of a group of people to 
create the desired output through the sharing of knowledge and collaboration. This in a way, can 
reduce the chance of failures of the processes. Therefore, the overall discussion showed that 
continuous improvement is embedded in all kinds of improvement initiatives, inclusive of lean 
and agile implementation. Furthermore, the framework provides more structuring of the approach 
in carrying out these initiatives through the Kaizen priority matrix in part 2.2. As part 2 is 
essentially designed to support the company’s decision-making process, this matrix was included 
to enhance the completeness in the process. A basic notion supporting the use of this matrix is that 
all organizations have a limited capacity (both tangible and intangible forms) in performing 
activities. This simply becomes the foundation of optimization in operations management science. 
Therefore, this study incorporated this notion in designing the framework which can propose an 
optimal approach to develop a leagile production system to the company. 
Apart from part 2 of the framework, the fifth finding (regarding continuous improvement) was 
used to inform part 3 Evaluation and establishment as well. Based on change management theory, 
it is important that the implementation of new elements to an existing system is sustained to the 
point of ‘moment of critical mass’. In the context of this study, it refers to building a new state of 
a production system where the newly implemented practice is exercised subconsciously. The key 
success factor governing this transition is the internalization of the new practice by the employees 
which can be seen in forms of a changing mindset or habit. This indicates that to truly implement 
a new practice in an operation, it needs to be sensible for the people who will adopt that new way 
of working. Therefore, part 3 of the framework was designed to help the company build a system 
that facilitate the internalization process of the employees. The new improved state of an 
operational system can then become the new basis for future improvements, creating continuous 
improvement in a stepwise manner as recommended by a manager at Company X (see Appendix 
H.3). 
Finally, the sixth finding of this study is about fitting the framework to the organizational context 
through part 1 Current State Assessment. The design of part 1.1 KPI translation stemmed from the 
notion that KPIs are generally constructed to monitor the factors which are deemed critical to the 
company in achieving its goals. This study recognizes the relationship between the KPI design and 
the production system, by which, the KPIs would likely be designed to emphasize the features of 
the production system which are crucial towards success. Thus, lean and agile principles were 
applied here to set the perspective in analyzing these crucial features to inform the improvement 
project. In particular, the analysis in part 1.1 was designed to reflect the KPIs with the 
characteristics of the lean and agile organization established in the theory. In turn, the company 
can gain a sense of a starting point for the improvement project and a preliminary overview on the 
direction of implementing lean and agile to its production system. Similar reasoning can be used 
==PAGE:73==

to discuss part 1.2 which was designed to reflect the KPIs upon the three dimensions of operational 
performance. Although this part requires subjective inputs, the analysis gives a structured approach 
for the company to determine the critical dimension to be targeted in the improvement project 
which is impactful to the KPIs, and the critical factors for the company. This contributes to building 
the optimality of the leagile improvement approach.  
 
Reflection on the framework implementation at Company X 
The framework was tested in a case study at Company X. The framework was implemented as a 
supplementary material for the current 5S implementation project. The implementation of the 
framework went well. This could be due to the involvement of the global manufacturing manager 
in the development of the lean-agile framework which prompt the company to obtain the required 
input data for the framework.  
Part 1 of the framework was done proficiently given the data prepared by the company. The results 
of part 1.1 KPI translation were well received by the company as the framework can provide the 
manager a brief overview of the production systems from the lean and agile perspective and 
effectively showed the difference between one production site to another. However, one concern 
was raised regarding part 1.2 implementation results where the scores of quality and delivery 
dimensions were eminently equal. This contrast to the company’s business strategy which put 
highest emphasis on quality. Thus, the implementation of this part in the case study may not be 
the most effective. This is potentially due to unconscious bias during the KPI scoring as this study 
was well aware that the company’s 5S project had already initiated. In this sense, the scoring may 
be biased towards a high score in the quality dimension so that the framework would reassure that 
the company’s 5S practice was reasonable. 
Apart from the bias, the score for the cost dimension was relatively low compared to the quality 
and delivery. This observation can be reflected upon the iron triangle principle in project 
management, by which, a prioritization of two dimensions (from quality, cost, and time) would 
lead to the other dimension being compromised. Therefore, the result suggested that the company 
prioritizes ensuring product quality and being responsive to the customers’ requirements and the 
incurring cost in doing so is acceptably unavoidable. By employing projects to improve the six 
KPIs, the KPI scoring table informed the company that the cost dimension may not be taken into 
consideration in a great extent, and the company should be aware of potential issues related to 
monetary investment.  
However, the overall result from part 2 implementation was acceptable for the company. The KPI 
analyses showed that the company’s value proposition and strategies were manifested in the 
production management, particularly the six selected KPIs. 
As part 1 suggested that the company has an agile-dominant production system and the main target 
area of quality, the project selection model in part 2.1 of the framework suggested the company to 
adopt quality-focused practices, including 5S (lean), FMEA (lean), work standardization (lean), 
six sigma (lean), building knowledge worker (agile), using engineering software (CAD/CAE, 
==PAGE:74==
 
 
 
Page 68 of 101 
CAM, CAPP), and real-time control (agile). The framework then suggested that these practices 
should be prioritized using the Kaizen priority matrix in part 2.2. However, the case study did not 
outright follow this procedure. Two of the suggested practices, six sigma and real-time control, 
were excluded from the consideration by the manager before using the priority matrix. Although 
the leanness-agility measure from part 1 played a part in this exclusion but a more thorough 
analysis should be used to determine the relations between the suggested practices and the 
production processes in detail. The remaining five practices were put into the Kaizen priority 
matrix without difficulty. The matrix was found useful for the company as the manager was 
reported to gain a sense of sequence towards future improvement projects. One of the results from 
this part suggested 5S practice which correspond to the company’s decision. This gives the 
framework a validation on its practicality. However, given that the framework does not cover a 
guideline on how to carry out the suggested practices, the effectiveness of the leagile improvement 
of the production is dependent on the company. The framework would only be able to support the 
company in setting the directions of the improvement projects but not in executing the plans. 
Therefore, the company should be aware that the framework requires their project management 
competency in order to successfully create leagile improvements to the production system.  
Finally, the framework on part 3 was perceived by the company as a guiding principle rather than 
a set of procedures to be followed. This may be due to the lack of analytical elements to the 
framework such that this part became highly generic. Overall, part 3 did not help the company 
recognize, or measure, the improvement in a comprehensible way but rather it gave assurance in 
terms of defining the actions that should be made to sustain the practices. Thus, part 3 of the 
framework requires more refinement in the future. This, for example, can be the inclusion of 
instruction on building a scoring method for the audit system, so that the company can be able to 
have a standard in creating a reporting mechanism to monitor the new adopted practice. However, 
as the objective of this part is to support the company in their plans towards sustaining the adopted 
practice, part 3 was not perceived as a failure. 
The overall implementation of the framework was well-accepted by the company, especially in 
the way that the framework provided a basis for communication between stakeholders when 
carrying out the improvement projects. Thus, the objective of including change management 
theory in structuring the framework was achieved. The framework was founded useful for the 
company in planning the change, as an improvement project, to setting the change in motion. Based 
on this, the thesis objective was achieved as well. 
 
Practical implication 
As mentioned throughout this study, the framework provides support for production mangers to 
plan their strategy in developing a leagile production system. The framework enables a discussion 
regarding the natural characteristics of the production system and the KPIs that govern the system. 
With this, the framework provides theoretical suggestions to the company, particularly on the lean 
and agile practices that are suitable for the production system. Finally, the adopted practice can be 
ensured on its continuity by a step-by-step guideline from the framework.

Limitation 
One significant limitation in this study is that this thesis was expected to be adopted by the partner 
company. Thus, the design of the framework was influenced by the company’s input to a 
considerate amount. An obvious example of this is the design of part 2.1 which only included a 
selection of lean and agile practices based on the company’s input. The company in this process 
was seen as a representative from the manufacturing industry which was useful for enhancing the

validity. 
Additionally, the framework design can provide a guidance to the company only of the available 
set of practices included in part 2.1. For other companies, the practices apart from this selection 
may suit the company better, especially due to the change in the cultural and organizational 
contexts.

7 Conclusion 
This study was done to build a methodological lean-agile framework to develop a leagile 
production system. This study was carried out under the collaboration with Company X, a global 
manufacturer of safety products. The aim of this study was to build a framework that provides 
support to the partner company in their “Lean & Agile” strategic initiative which focuses on 
building a responsive production system in a cost-effective manner. This brought about the 
research question for this study:  
 
“How can lean and agile principles be applied to a production system to build a 
leagile production system and promote continuous improvement?” 
 
The research question was broken down into three problem statements which outlined this study. 
In the overall, this study addressed the problem statements as discussed followingly. 
 
“What is the approach for implementing lean and agile principles in a 
manufacturing setting?” 
 
The implementation of the two principles differs in the approach for creating improvements. Lean 
emphasizes the improvement of the process itself with the main objective of reducing cost, whereas 
agile targets the enhancement of organizational competences in carrying out activities in the way 
that operations are integrated. Thus, implementation of lean and agile differ in the level of 
complexity, incurred from the organizational entities that are affected in the process. On the 
contrary, this study recognized that lean and agile principles shared the same improvement 
objectives namely, reducing cost, improving quality and delivery, although the two principles may 
not give equal emphasis on these dimensions. Therefore, these notions were incorporated in the 
lean-agile framework, by which, the project selection model presents a sorting of lean and agile 
practices according to their target improvement dimensions and complexity. 
Apart from this, this study also showed that in practical settings, lean and agile principles may not 
be the one-for-all solutions to all production systems due to the characteristics of the production 
system and the organizational context that governs it. Thus, the approach to implement lean and 
agile principles to a production must be conforming to these two factors. The framework is then 
designed to address this by providing an assessment system to set the direction of the 
implementation of lean and agile practices. 
 
“What are the principles of leagile and the approach to adopt them into a 
production system?” 
==PAGE:77==
 
 
 
Page 71 of 101 
This study acknowledged that leagile principle are merely a joint of lean and agile principles which 
forms a leagile production system. Previous studies showed that a leagile production system 
comprises of two separate subsystems, which one is managed by lean principles and another, agile 
principles. Many instances, the production system that adopts leagile principle is accommodated 
by de-coupling point and postponement. However, the leagile principle was not recognized as a 
new breakthrough concept in this study as it does not change the fundamental principles of lean 
and agile, but rather, use them directly. Therefore, this study proposed that by adopting and 
exercising the lean and agile principles in a continuous effort can also lead to the development of 
leagile production system as well. This finding manifested in the form of a framework design 
which provide suggestions to a limited selection of lean and agile practices. 
 
“What is the approach to incorporate continuous improvement as its own 
distinguished concept to the development of a leagile production system?” 
 
Continue from previously, one approach to develop a leagile production system is to implement 
lean and agile principles and exercise them in a persistent manner. By exploring the core principles 
of lean and agile, this study recognized that both principles are rooted in the concept of continuous 
improvement. Lean principle has its core concept from lean thinking which refer to a loop of 
identifying value for the customers, creating a pull-production flow, and seeking perfection of the 
flow. Agile principle was built from the twelve principles in the Agile Manifesto which focus on 
iterative process of defining values and delivering it to the customers. These two notions resonate 
strongly to the concept of creating improvement as a cyclic process, by which, becomes the basis 
for the development of lean and agile practices. Thus, this study proposed that by implementing 
lean and agile practices to the production system and internalizing them as standard practices, the 
organization is exercising the concept of continuous improvement. 
The developed lean-agile framework addresses the research question as it provides a structured 
approach to develop a leagile production system through sustainable implementations of lean and 
agile principles in production management. The framework provides a combination of analyses 
and guidelines that instruct the company from initiating the leagile development to establishing a 
new state of the leagile production system.  
The framework development was informed by change management theory, by which, strengthen 
the foundation to build continuous improvement on top of it. Moreover, the PDCA principles were 
also used in the framework development process to enhance the integration of continuous 
improvement concept. The final lean-agile framework consists of three parts. 
Part 1 Current State Assessment  
This part contains two analyses based on the company’s production KPIs. The first analysis is KPI 
translation which is an analysis that assess the leanness and agility of the production system given 
the KPI measures. The second analysis is the Determination of improvement objective which is an 
analysis for identifying the main target dimension for improvement among the three dimensions: 
==PAGE:78==
 
 
 
Page 72 of 101 
cost, quality, and delivery. These two analyses provide insightful information for setting the 
direction for an effective leagile improvement project. 
Part 2 Project Selection  
This part contains two models aiming to support the decision-making process of choosing lean and 
agile practices for the improvement project. The first model is the lean-agile practice selection 
model which presents a selective of well-known lean and agile practices, sorted according to their 
main improvement target and complexity level. The model would suggest a group of practices 
which deemed suitable for the production system, based on the results from part 1. This suggestion 
is taken further to fit the Kaizen priority matrix which formulates the order of implementation 
projects which respect the constraints from the business context of the company. 
Part 3 Evaluation and Establishment 
The framework expects that the company takes the suggestion into deploying a pilot project to 
implement the lean or agile practice by themselves. This project can be done in a small-scale study 
in a certain area of the production system, thus, introducing the new way of working to the 
organizational system. The first model in this part proposes a pilot project checklist to ensure 
completeness of the introduction of the new practice, and the creation of a system for facilitating 
it. The second model is sustaining the adopted practice which proposes a series of activities that 
should be performed to ensure a smooth transition of the adopted practice to other areas in the 
production and establish a new state of the production system. 
The framework was tested at Company X’s production site in country D. Overall, the framework 
was well-received among the managers and stakeholders. Thus, the objective of this thesis is 
achieved as the framework is developed and founded useful for the company in building a hybrid 
production system.
