Abstract
Prototyping in games is an essential part of game creation. When it comes to
virtual prototyping it has both pros and cons. This project explores virtual
prototyping speciﬁcally for board games. A virtual prototyping tool has been
developed to explore the advantages and disadvantages of virtual prototypes.
The tool is a multiplatform application that is targeting board game designers
with no programming skills. Since the feature list of such a tool can be inﬁnite,
the project focuses on problems dealing with creation of virtual environments,
rapid play-testing, deﬁning rules and with tracing of player’s turns, economy
and movement.

Summary
Prototyping in games, being physical or digital, is an essential part of the game
design and creation process. Many software game prototyping tools have been
developed to help game designers with simple tasks, visualization and play-testing
but none of them speciﬁcally for board game development. Furthermore they do
not incorporate behaviours (AI or rule enforcement), but rather they are used
as sandboxes where the designer has to know the rules and register the changes
by himself or he/she has to possess programming skills to automate the process.
The objective of this thesis is to explore the applications and potential of virtual
prototypes with augmented reality in the context of iterative prototyping in
board games. In order to do so a virtual prototyping tool has been implemented
during the thesis. It is speciﬁcally designed for board game designers with no
programming skills. The diﬀerence from the existing tools is that it incorporates
behaviours and it focuses on problems dealing with rapid play-testing and with
tracing of player’s turns, economy and movement. It also incorporates augmented
reality so that the connection between the material and virtual world is not
completely lost since it is essential for board game designers.
In order to investigate the potential of the tool, public tests and private interviews
with people from the game industry have been conducted and the results have
been analyzed. The ﬁndings presented in the thesis suggest, that, although
people are used to work with paper and create paper prototypes, a mobile
software tool for prototyping tabletop games like the one implemented in this
project, has potential and can be useful for certain games and situations by
game designers and enthusiasts. According to some of the subjects that tested
the tool an AR feature that can render the prototype over table’s surface can be
useful for visualization of the actual scale and the look of the board game early

Chapter 1
Introduction
Prototyping in games is an important part of the game design process [Fuller-
ton(2004),Fullerton(2006),Fullerton(2008),Glinert(2010),Salen(2004),Schell(2008),
Manker(2011)]. There are diﬀerent game prototyping methods. The most com-
monly used are paper and digital. Paper or physical prototyping is widely known
and used method in the game design process but when it comes to digital games,
it is often considered outdated [Glinert(2010),Manker(2011)]. Digital prototyp-
ing gives the ability to virtually explore a speciﬁc behaviour and mechanic and
quickly play-test it. It is very useful for testing complex behaviours but it also
requires more time and skills to be implemented.
Game designers who design digital games, are often part of teams consisting of
many diﬀerent roles [Bethke(2003)]. Even in the concept phase, when prototypes
are made, they often have access to more resources like programmers or in-house
developed software tools. On the other hand, board game teams usually do not
require programmers, thus, game designers who design tabletop games often stick
to paper or physical prototypes or they have to ﬁnd their own ways and methods
to prototype complex behaviours. I.e. creating a game with economy requires
constant resource tracking and even with the use of excel sheets people experience
diﬃculties. Software tools like Zun Tzu and TableTopia help game designers
with simple tasks, visualization and play-testing but they do not incorporate
behaviours, rather, they are used as sandboxes where the designer has to know
the rules and register the changes himself.
==PAGE:16==

Introduction
Augmented Reality (AR) has various applications but currently its role within
prototyping for games has not yet been explored. The advancement of new
technology has now provided us with widely accessible software and hardware
tools that can build, process and render AR experiences on our every day used
devices such as smart phones, tablets and PCs, thus, bringing the AR experience
closer to a much broader audience [Vuforia(2016),Unity3D(2016)].
In order to investigate and combine the existing technology with the game design
process, we decided to design and develop a digital, multiple platform, AR
featured tool for creating prototypes which can simulate certain mechanics for
games. We have focused on resource tracking and turn based systems as main
features since they are hugely used in board games and since designers have
diﬃculties prototyping tracking behaviours on paper. The AR part of the tool
should illustrate the physical representation of the prototype in a real world
environment. This project aims to explore how a possible implementation of
such a tool can work and can help game designers in their eﬀort of creating
virtual prototypes and rapidly iterating and play-testing over them.
1.1
Project goal and motivation
The project goal can be stated in one sentence:
To explore the application of virtual prototypes with augmented reality in the
context of board games by creating a virtual prototyping tool.
There are certain advantages in using a virtual prototype over paper prototypes.
For instance in a virtual prototype it is way easier for the game designer to keep
track of the diﬀerent components in a scenario and change them in real time so
he/she can instantly see the applications of that change. Another advantage is
that virtual prototypes are more accessible and have the ability to be quickly
play-tested. Knowing this and having interviewed several game designers we
noticed that game designers have a huge problem in doing virtual prototypes,
mainly because they do not have any programming experience. While computer
game designers might have some basic programming knowledge or maybe work
with a programmer to create virtual prototypes, game designers designing board
games do not tend to develop virtual prototypes, mainly because their medium
is the physical world, but this does not exclude all the beneﬁts that might come
from a virtual prototype. In this regard, we decided to focus on the group of
board game designers and develop a tool that can create prototypes which does
not require the designer to have any programming background. Since it is so
easy to break the immersion of the game being only a virtual prototype, we

This is a prototype development based on a proof of concept mentality. The
application of augmented reality in the project is explored only within the smart

1.3
Approach
The ﬁrst step of the thesis was to study prototyping in games both paper and
digital and also research augmented reality applications in prototyping. By
doing this, a general knowledge was achieved which functions as a foundation
through the entire project. The next step was to create a questionnaire and ﬁnd
the diﬀerent approaches to prototyping, the process of work and the tools that
game designers are using for iterating over their game ideas. The purpose of
the questionnaire was to get insights of the current methodologies and also to
ﬁnd what diﬃculties the game designers experience in using them. The next
step was to analyze the results, ﬁnd common patterns of data and sort them in
use cases. And thereafter to design and create a tool based on the use cases.
First a level editor was implemented where the user can place objects. Then
a movement mechanic followed with "timeline" feature and resource tracking
behavior were added. At that point the virtual environment was ready to be
tested with game designers. At last, valuable feedback regarding usability and
potential was gathered from tests with game designers and non game designers.

Chapter 2
Background theory
2.1
General deﬁnition of a prototype
There are diﬀerent uses of a prototype and prototyping. Prototypes and proto-
typing are used in almost any design process. Having a big and complex design a
designer cannot predict all the eﬀects that solution has on the outside world. So
a prototype is used as an exploring and revising tool: “In design and development
processes, prototypes are used not for proving solutions but for discovering prob-
lems or for exploring new solution directions.” [Lim(2008)]. However this use of a
prototype does not cover the full scope of what prototyping can do. Prototypes
have a signiﬁcant trait - they are way simpler than the full solutions and they
tend to be focused on just one or several aspects of the solution. In other words
they serve as ﬁlters. So “Knowing that prototypes ﬁlter certain aspects of a de-
sign, we can become more aware of the complexity and responsibility of a design,
and hence be more thoughtful about our design decision making.” [Lim(2008)]
Another aspect of the prototype is that it may serve as a manifestation: “We
argue that the purpose of designing a prototype is to ﬁnd the manifestation that,
in its simplest form, will ﬁlter the qualities in which the designer is interested
without distorting the understanding of the whole...Prototypes are representative
and manifested forms of design ideas.” [Lim(2008)]. To summarize a prototype
is supposed to be as simple as possible (to the point that the idea of the whole is
not distorted), focused on some of the aspects of a solution and serve as a ﬁlter

so that any design problems can be discovered and as manifestation so that new
directions of a solution can be explored.
2.2
Deﬁnition of game prototype
Knowing what a prototype can provide for a design, it is needed to ﬁnd out
what a prototype means for designing speciﬁcally a game and how exactly it is
used in the game design process. “The word “prototyping” means to create a
working version of the formal system that, while playable, includes only a rough
approximation of the artwork, sound, and features ... Prototypes are used as
crude models whose purpose is to allow the designer to wrap his brain around the
game mechanics and see how they function ... To make a prototype is to break
out a speciﬁc problem in the design and work with it” [Fullerton(2004)]. So it
seems that a game prototype also has a ﬁltering aspect where all artwork, sound
and features are approximated so that the prototype can be focused on a certain
game mechanic. But creating a game is a cooperative process and prototypes
can be used not only for exploration and ﬁnding issues in the solution on your
own but also from other people. A prototype becomes the most eﬃcient way of
communicating an idea to others and later play testing the prototype is another
technique of exploring people’s incentives about the solution. “A fundamental
characteristic of a prototype is that it is a manifestation or externalization of an
idea. As such it represents something that the designer, or the design team, can
reﬂect upon.(Fallman 2003)” [Lim(2008)]
2.3
Rapid Iterative Prototyping in Games
The game industry uses widely methods that are iterative and incremental.
Software development on its own has methods like Agile Uniﬁed Process and
Scrum which are quite similar to the Rapid Iterative Prototyping method used
in the game industry. The prototypes used can diﬀer from paper sketched
to software simulations. “A prototype is an early design that is tested and
improved upon. It is “played, evaluated, adjusted and played again, allowing
the designer or design team to base decisions on the successive iterations or
versions of the game.” [Salen(2004)]. The idea is to have a working prototype
that can be reviewed and then reﬁned or said in other words “Iterative design is a
cyclic process that alternates between prototyping, play-testing, evaluation, and
reﬁnement.” [Salen(2004)].The three main stages of rapid iterative prototyping
are:

• Prototype - Create a prototype speciﬁc on what you want to evaluate.
• Review - Evaluate the prototype by sharing and playing with other people.
• Reﬁne and Iterate - Incorporate a given feedback and repeat the process.

Rapid prototyping provides feedback early in the game production and improves

So the better and more speciﬁc the prototype used, the better tool the game
designers have to determine the game design they are trying to create.
2.4
Types of prototyping
There are two environments for a designer to prototype a game - physical and
digital.
2.4.1
Physical environment
In a physical environment a designer can create physical prototypes by the use
of paper, cardboard, pieces from analog games, Lego bricks, along with craft and
oﬃce supplies. A physical prototype can also be created through acting, playing
or pretending.
2.4.2
Digital environment
In a digital environment a designer can use software to build virtual prototypes.
Software tools like Zun Tzu and TableTopia are used as sandboxes where designers
can drag and drop pieces and props on a virtual table and share them with
friends and public. Also there are software tools that can help game designers lay
out the design. These include Visio, for making ﬂowcharts, Excel, for creating
spreadsheets, Access and Filemaker, for structuring the data. They can save
designers an enormous amount of time when preparing their game concept and
presenting it to others.

Background theory
2.4.3
Pros and cons of diﬀerent prototyping methods
Which prototyping method should be used for prototyping a game is often
considered by two factors. Time that in most cases is translated to money
and the amount of ﬂexibility provided. Time wise the software method takes
the most amount of time and has the least amount of ﬂexibility. The physical
prototyping oﬀers the greatest amount of ﬂexibility and costs the least amount
of time. [design prototyping(2010)]
Prototyping is used to make current design ideas explicit so that they can be
evaluated and easily iterated upon, which can be more eﬀective if the scope is
deﬁned before hand. The broader the scope of the prototype the longer it will
take to create. The complexity of the game can also make balancing the diﬀerent
mechanics and systems that it consists of a diﬃcult task. Concepts like core,
progression, individual game mechanics and play mechanics, are possible scopes
for a prototype.
Physical and paper prototypes can be tailored to any scope. The paper method
tends to be better at formalizing the design. When it comes to time, it works
great when the concept is relatively simple, but it gets really time consuming
when the complexity increases. Also they are not very accessible in terms of
mobility, rapid iterations and playtesting.
Limiting the scope decreases the amount of ﬂexibility needed in a prototype.
Software prototypes are limited in ﬂexibility so they tend to be best when dealing
with limited scopes (e.g. core game mechanics). Pitfalls of software prototyping
is that the scope is actually concerned with technology issues and not the game’s
design. Their strong side is that they can deal with complexity and are great for
playtesting and tuning and tweaking mechanics, systems and variables.
All diﬀerent prototype types are great for certain scenarios and it is common
in diﬀerent stages of a production to be used more than one type. This the-
sis explores the possibilities of increasing the virtual prototypes usability and
ﬂexibility.

Thesis contribution
The tools that are presented in the “State of the art” chapter are of two categories.
The ﬁrst ones are oriented speciﬁcally to board game players and are mainly
for playing board games online and not deﬁning any rules. The second ones
are tools for building games without any programming skills required. However
they are not speciﬁc for board game development and most of them are missing
features like having turns and rules.
This thesis is going to take a slightly diﬀerent approach. It is going to try and
create a tool that has all the means of creating rules and turns without coding
and is going to investigate the eﬀectiveness of virtual prototypes with augmented
reality and their application in game design prototyping. The tool is to be used

part of Unity since this proves to be a big obstacle for game designers. The
tool is going to be available as a mobile app so that it is easily accessible. Also
augmented reality is used so that the connection between the material and virtual
world is not completely lost since that is important for board game designers.

4.1
Augmented Reality
Augmented Reality (AR) is a variation of Virtual Environments (VE). In AR
environment the user is able to recognize a virtual layer of reality on top of his
normal visual sensation. To achieve this result, each frame captured by a camera
is constantly analyzed for predetermined image targets. Once an object or an
image is recognized, the result is rendered on a digital screen or head-mounted
displays with a world position and orientation. AR combines real and virtual
and it is interactive in real time [Azuma(1997)].
AR has various diﬀerent applications. For the purpose of this project, we are
going to explore its application in relation with board game design prototyping.

4.2
Vuforia
Qualcomm’s Vuforia is one of the most advanced SDKs for augmented reality in
the market. It uses Computer Vision technology to recognize and track objects
in real-time. This enables us to position and orient virtual objects, such as 3d
objects, in relation to real world images when viewed from the camera. Thus, it
appears that the virtual objects are part of the real world scene. It is relatively
simple to setup and use. Having a ready to use solution for augmented reality
(free for noncommercial usage) gives us the advantage of getting to the core of
the problem faster and spending more time on designing, developing and testing
the prototyping tool. Furthermore, it supports multiple platforms, which gives
us the freedom to test on various devices.
4.3
Unity 3D game engine
In the recent years Unity have been drastically improved and became one of the
most popular game engines. It is free, very reliable, extensible and it has very
wide developer base. That attracted many non-game companies as well, who
have developed diﬀerent tools and plug-ins that are helping developers, including
us, get closer to their goals. Since, the engine supports building on multiple
platforms and since we are already familiar with it, we decided to use it for our
project. One of the reasons we chose Unity is also the support of Qualcomm’s
Vuforia augmented reality SDK. Other game engines like Unreal Engine and
CryEngine were missing Vuforia support at the time this project took place or
were missing mobile platforms (Android, iOS) support (CryEngine), and since
we needed reliable AR solution and mobile platforms support we picked Unity.

Initial research
Half of the interviews were conducted in person and the other half using an
online form. The survey’s goals are to ﬁnd if and how game designers use paper
prototypes in their work and what is their opinion on virtual prototypes and
augmented reality prototypes. This will help in identifying the problems with
usage of paper prototypes and also ﬁnding possible solutions to these problems
using virtual prototyping. For the diﬀerent questions asked check Appendix A
and for the raw answer data check Appendix B.
5.1
Questions
The questions asked were in this exact order. There are 3 types of questions.
General questions that ask for the interviewee’s background, as this may aﬀect
his answers and knowledge of the topic discussed. The second type are connected
with prototyping and what advantages and disadvantages have the interviewees
encountered in the diﬀerent types of prototypes they have used. And the third
type of questions are designed to ﬁnd how much is known about augmented
reality and what is the general opinion in such technology used in a prototyping
tool.

In order to investigate if virtual prototypes with augmented reality can be used
by game designers and to what extent compared to paper prototypes we decided
to develop a software tool. Before we start proving anything, that tool needs
to be good to use when designing a game prototype in general. The following
chapter is going to describe the requirements, the technologies used and the
implementation of the tool.

In this chapter we are going to describe the minimal requirements for a proto-

cases. While user stories are user oriented and closer to the actual statements of
the people interviewed in the survey the use cases are more system oriented and
can serve better purpose in planning the development of the tool.

6.1.2.1
Level design use cases
The level design use cases are those performed by the level designer to design a
level or a particular setup. However in board games the game designer is also a
level designer. The purpose of naming the use cases as level design is to keep
tasks separated.
• Create a project - There can be diﬀerent scenarios that a game designer
might want to test for his game. On top of that the tool should be able
to store them in diﬀerent projects so that these scenarios are available for
playtesting.
• Create a grid - The ﬁrst and most important feature is to create a grid.
Since the system is developed mainly for board games, there is a strong
need of having a grid layout. Deﬁning the grid’s size deﬁnes the world’s
size in the current prototype ( this includes grid sizes in the three physical
dimensions).
• Place assets in the world - This feature enables the level designer to actually
build a level so it is one of the essential parts of the system.
6.1.2.2
Game design use cases
• Create a resource - a resource is needed so that a resource system can be
prototyped for a game.
• Create a player - a player is needed so that multiple players can be simulated

• Create and customize assets - an asset can be a prop, environment or a
character. Creating and customizing assets is an essential part in building
prototypes and balancing prototypes.
• Customize game mechanics - tweak game mechanics to the right values is
one of the main responsibilities of a game designer and has direct impact
over the game’s "feel" and "ﬂow". Being able to completely customize a
game mechanic and tune it speciﬁcally for a case is an important use case.
• Assign game mechanics - have predeﬁned game mechanics that are ready
to be assigned to any asset in the tool without requiring any programming

6.2
Database
In order to keep track of everything that the user does in the tool and also to save
his current progress, we needed to design and implement a local database solution.
Furthermore, a well designed database provides a foundation for extensibility
which was one of our main concerns during the concept phase. We wanted to
design the database in such a way that it will be reusable for most of the cases
and features that were planned to be implemented in the tool. The database
consists of 26 tables. The design was made according to the diﬀerent use cases
that were described in the previous chapter. A brief overview of the database
will provide better understandings and insights of tool’s architecture and the
design decisions taken.
We assume that the user will be working on more than one prototype or project
so the main table that relates to everything else is the "Project" table. From
there, we link project dependant entities to it. For instance the Player table
stores the data for the players in the project; the resources table stores the
diﬀerent resources chosen by the player in the project. Same goes to cards,
custom actions, custom presets, grid cells, grid objects and turns.
Since we decided to have square and hex based grid for prototyping environment
in the system, we created the "Cell" table to store each grid cell in a project.
Cell is the single most important building block used in the entire system. It
represents a unique tile in the grid on which an object can be placed or moved.
Since the grid is based in a 3d environment, each cell has its own Vector3 location
and also a level attribute which corresponds to its location on the stack. By
having such a table the system can always save, load and generate the exact
same grid between sessions.
The "Grid_object" table is then used to link a 3d object such as character or
prop with a cell on the grid. This gives us the ability to move and place objects
only on predetermined areas on the grid. The table also stores the player-object
ownership information of each object. The appearance and functionality of the
grid object is derived from its preset.
We call a preset, a 3d object that the user selects to use for prototyping for the
current project. Since the user is prototyping a certain scenario, he does not
need all the available 3d meshes that are in the system. For instance he selects
one 3d object of a farm and he makes three diﬀerent farms out of it. The user
can name each of those farms as he wants so it make sense for him. The "Preset"
table is used to store this information. Once a preset is made, actions can be
linked to it. It is used as a template for the appearance and functionality of a
grid object. A detailed description of the tables in the database can be found in

When creating a project (ﬁg. 6.2), a name of the project and the dimensions of a

The grid can be square or hexagonal. Currently the system supports only the

The logic used in generating a grid is written in GridManager.cs. Before we start
generating a grid we need several parameters. Number of tiles for both width and
height, width and height of a tile from the grid and a position that we want the
grid to be generated on. Let us mark that position with the letter A in ﬁg. 6.3.
First we need to ﬁnd position B which is the center of the ﬁrst instantiated tile.
To calculate the coordinates of B from point A. Point A is exactly in the center
of the grid so half of the width of the grid must be subtracted from x and half
of the height from y. Because B is in the center of the ﬁrst tile we add to B’s
coordinates half the width of a tile and half the height of a tile.
Bx = Ax −tileWidth ∗gridWidth/2 + tileWidth/2
By = Ay −tileHeight ∗gridHeight/2 + tileHeight/2
Having point B coordinates we can calculate the position of every cell and
instantiate it in a grid. To do that we just need the indexes of the tile in the
grid matrix. For example point C’s indexes are (3,1). We are going to name
them Cxindex and Cyindex. To calculate C’s coordinates we need to add the
tileWidth multiplied to the x index to Bx and the tileHeight multiplied with the
y index to By.
Cx = Bx + tileWidth ∗Cxindex
Cy = By + tileHeight ∗Cyindex
In this way all points’ positions are calculated and tiles are instantiated.

6.3.4
Customize actions
The actions in the tool are essentially the game mechanics available in the tool.
There is a list of predeﬁned diﬀerent actions that can be assigned to an object.
And while the user cannot create his own unique mechanic he/she can customize
the existing ones via a set of parameters that can be attached to an action. For
example the action movement (ﬁg. 6.6) can have several parameters like range,
speed, execute (a special parameter that invokes the action). These parameters
have diﬀerent types and depending on them a default value can be entered. Most
of the types are the standard data types like string, integer, ﬂoat except the
additional Vector2, Vector3 and command which are directly connected to the
action speciﬁcs. For example the action "move" has the command parameters
execute and preview. Execute makes an object move. The preview, duplicates the
object, moves the duplicate to a speciﬁed path and then destroys the duplicate,
thus previewing how the object would move. The range parameter when added
provides a way for adjusting the movement range. The range is measured in
grid cell radius, and since it has to be a whole number its data type is integer.
Another example is the produce action. As it is shown in ﬁg. 6.7 it has two
resources attached as parameters. Whenever the resource option is selected in
the parameter drop-down, resources that have been already added to the project
can be selected and added to the action. Those actions with their parameters
are used as templates which can be assigned to objects. That is done in the
"Preset actions" menu.

All the logic related with going back and forth in turns, players and rules is
in the ProgressManager.cs script. Let us ﬁrst see how things change when a
diﬀerent turn is selected. Depending if the turn is before or after the previous
turn a direction is established. We are going to refer to the actions that are
executed according to these rules and not the rules themselves. In this way we
are going to avoid confusion with the rules that are existing but not ﬁnished so
do not have any actions to execute or revert. If the direction is back all actions
between the previous turn and the current turn are reversed. If the direction is
forward all actions between the previous turn and the current turn are executed
(including the current turn actions). In this way every time a new turn is selected
the actions are executed till the end of that turn.
Another case of going back or forth from a rule to another rule is within a turn
of a player. This is again calculated by ﬁrst ﬁnding the direction. The direction
==PAGE:58==
44
Tool
can be found by comparing the rules’ execution orders. Next is ﬁnding the rules
in between the previous rule and the current one and depending on the direction
execute them or revert them. However sometimes there might not be a previous
rule. For example the ﬁrst selected rule. In this case the system reverts only the
selected action to this rule and if the rule is deselected the corresponding action
is executed.
The third speciﬁc case for going back and forth is by selecting diﬀerent players
in a certain rule. In this case the direction is found by comparing the current
player id with the previous player id. Where the smallest id is the ﬁrst player
and the biggest is the last one. If the current player is after the previous player
all actions in between are executed and if a current player is before the previous
player all actions in between are reverted.
6.3.10
Keeping track of the resources of the players in the
prototype
Currently the resources are strictly dependant on the turns. When a farm is
created in a turn it starts producing the speciﬁed amount from that turn on. So
the resources of a player depend on the amount of the farms he/she has for this
resource and the number of turns he has had them in. For example if a gold
mine produces one gold per turn and is created on the ﬁrst turn then the player
is going to have 3 gold on the third turn. If the player creates another gold mine
in the 4th turn, at the 5th turn the amount of gold he/she is going to have is 5
(from the old farm) plus 2 (from the farm created in the 4th turn).
So going back and forth in the "timeline", shows how much resources every
player has. The result of adding or removing more farms is displayed instantly.
Balancing the economy for a game becomes an easier task by being able to track
the economy state on each turn. The tool has the option of having multiple
players so it is also suitable for balancing the game for diﬀerent number of
players.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis was to explore the application and the potential of
virtual prototypes with augmented reality in the context of iterative prototyping
in board games. The approach taken to fulﬁl the goal was by implementation
of a software tool that provides features for creation of virtual prototypes for
board games. In order to investigate what kind of features are important for
board game designers and should be included in the tool, existing prototyping
methods and tools were studied and analyzed in chapter 2 and 3 and a relevant
survey was performed and analyzed in chapter 5. From the research in these
chapters it was concluded that designers need ﬂexible and customizable virtual
environment with the ability to assign simple behaviours to objects and simulate
play testing. Furthermore, it was concluded that a feature that allows observation
of game progress including resource and player tracking is needed. Based on
these requirements, a tool was developed in chapter 6 and it was tested and
analyzed in chapter 7.
The experimental results obtained suggest, that, although people are used to
work with paper and create paper prototypes, a mobile software tool which
requires no programming skills for creation of virtual prototypes for board games
has potential and has useful applications:
• The results for the "timeline" feature suggest that it can be quite beneﬁcial
for detecting existing mistakes within the design as well as for rapid
==PAGE:70==
56
Conclusion
iterations and play-testing of the prototype. Especially when combined
with the "resource tracking" feature it ensures that every important game
stats is kept track of.
• According to some of the subjects that tested the tool an AR feature that
can render the prototype over table’s surface can be useful for visualization
of the actual scale and the look of the board game early on the prototyping
process - something that otherwise have to be produced on paper - thus
saving time and money by not having to print out and physically assemble
prototypes.
• An interesting ﬁnding regarding future implementation and extension of the
tool draws attention to a feature that can generate scenarios and simulate
entirely the play-testing with players easing the exploration of potential
mistakes in the design.
Moreover, the fact that the tool runs on mobile devices has proven to be one of its
main advantages since it provides portability and accessibility to the prototype.
All of these features address diﬀerent scenarios in which virtual prototypes can
surpass the eﬃciency and accessibility of other ways of prototyping and while
there is plenty of room for improvements, the results are promising to conclude
that virtual prototypes with and without augmented reality can ﬁnd utility in
the context of iterative prototyping in board games.

Appendix E
Google Cardboard tests
Since augmented reality was part of the project we wanted to explore the diﬀerent
technologies that are linked to it and if they are practical and useful within our
case. So we decided to try Google Cardboard (GC) and check its utility.
The implementation of GC in Unity was straightforward and setting it up with
the project did not take much. Surprisingly, connecting GC with Vuforia in
Unity was also very easy and short after that it was ready for testing.
E.1
Problems found
Firstly wearing GC as a head mount with a smartphone attached is not the
best experience. Since the image from the camera rendered on the screen of
the smartphone is a bit zoomed in by default, the user can immediately sense
that everything is zoomed in, which is not natural for the human eye. However
after few minutes the brain can either adjust to the zoomed image or the user
might begin feeling dizzy. In this case the impact over the immersion and the
experience is rather negative.
Another problem arises when it comes to controlling virtual objects. Since the
==PAGE:102==
88
Google Cardboard tests
device is attached to the GC, the user can not use its touch interface. The
solution that GC uses is by sending a ray straightforward from the orientation of
the device to a point in the virtual world. Keeping the ray for a certain amount
of time within the boundaries of the target can trigger an event. Since we are
doing a prototyping tool we need quick interactions with the system to boost
the whole process and this kind of control scheme was insuﬃcient.
In order to ﬁnd a better control mechanism we also looked into Leap Motion
device. However during that time it did not have the needed support on Android
devices and there was no purpose in investing more time in it. Also the number
of devices used in the project was growing thus its complexity.
E.2
Conclusion
Without a natural way of interaction with the virtual objects seen through the
GC and with issues related with the incorrect perception of the environment it
was clear that this technology could not be used for the purpose of the project.

Additional found notes
• I’ve used Zun Tzu quite a bit for solo playtesting, and it works quite well
for that purpose, and can do most of the things you’d like to be able to do
– roll dice, shuﬄe cards, ﬂip cards over, rotate cards, etc. The one thing it
can’t do is resource tracking. Whenever I playtest a game with an economy,
I always have to use a pencil and paper or an Excel spreadsheet; it’s just
too tedious to use Zun Tzu for that kind of thing (you can make "money"
counters and move them around but it’s horribly slow). If your program
can do resource tracking and transactions, that’s certainly appealing. The
other limitation in Zun Tzu is that you can’t group objects by shift-clicking
them. You can form a stack by dragging them on top of one another, but
in a game with a lot of multiple-piece movement, this is slow and tedious.
