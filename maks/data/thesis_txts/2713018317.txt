Abstract
Unlike traditional reinforcement learning, in multi-agent reinforcement learning several agents
coexist in an environment and perform actions simultaneously. This introduces an additional
complexity layer, since agents need to learn how to interact with each other to accomplish a
particular task. Such interactions lead to the emergence of cooperative or competitive behavior
patterns between the agents, which can be implicitly modelled through the reward structure. It
is especially challenging to learn such behaviors in the offline setting, where agents are trained
from a fixed dataset collected by other policies. Such policies might be designed to solve different
tasks and therefore the behaviors defined by that policy do not necessarily align with an agent’s
goal.
In this paper, we explore an alternative formulation of offline multi-agent reinforcement learn-
ing that addresses this challenge. We define multi-agent reinforcement learning as a sequence
modelling problem in which we treat trajectories as sequences of state observations, actions
and rewards, and leverage the transformer network to model behavior conditioned on a target
episode return. Such an approach promises to disentangle the agents’ goals, which can be mod-
elled through reward conditioning, from the learning dynamics, which transformers can easily
deal with.
In this experiment we focus on modelling the two most well-defined behaviors, namely collabo-
ration and competition. For such, we define an appropriate reward structure and two variations
of the Decision Transformer architecture; one modelling the joint action and another individual
actions, which follow a fully centralized and a fully decentralized paradigm, respectively. We
evaluate their performance on two different tasks to determine how effective sequence modelling
is to solve multi-agent reinforcement learning problems, and whether agents can develop com-
petitive and cooperative behavior from a cooperative-only training dataset using the proposed
framework.
The results proved that the present approach can effectively select actions that lead to high
returns, showing an overall good performance, albeit with some limitations. Moreover, such
models showed their versatility to induce the desired behaviors in the agents from pure cooper-
ative data just through reward modelling.

Introduction
In the last few years, the field of deep multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) has become
increasingly popular [40] due to its relevance in many real-world applications where several agents
interact with each other in the same environment, such as traﬀic control [31], network packet
routing [17], energy distribution, robotics [13], economic modeling [18], or the analysis of social
dilemmas [15]. The automation of such tasks has for a long time been object of desire, but their
complexity makes it diﬀicult to solve with pre-programmed agent behaviors. The impressive
results that reinforcement learning (RL) has achieved in single-agent tasks on a diverse range of
complexity settings, has prompted the current high research activity in reinforcement learning
for the multi-agent domain, with the purpose of achieving the super-human performance that
the famous Atari [20], AlphaStar [2] and AlphaGo [32] models have shown.
However, a series of new challenges arise in multi-agent reinforcement learning that were not
present in the single-agent setting, such as non-stationarity [24], given that the environment is
no longer stationary from the agent’s perspective, and scalability [21], since the action space
grows exponentially with the number of agents. One big challenge is that agents need to be
aware of the other decision-makers in the environment and learn how to interact with each other
in a way that it will help them achieve their goal.
Such a behavior is broadly classified as
cooperative, where agents cooperate towards a common goal, competitive, where agents have
competing goals, or a mix of both [40]. These behaviors are implicitly modelled through the
reward structure. For instance, if agents are rewarded for others’ successes, then agents will
learn to collaborate to optimize a common goal. On the contrary, an agent can be penalized for
other agents’ progress so that agents learn to compete against each other, looking to maximize
their individual return.
But in MARL the boundaries between competition and cooperation can often be blurry. Each
agent may adopt a different behavior depending on their task, and agents can even change their
behavior during an episode to adapt to the surrounding circumstances. For example, taking
the case of self-driving cars (figure 2.1), the cars are designed to cooperate and coordinate to
ensure a fast, smooth and safe experience. However, in the case of an eventuality, the cars might
switch to a competitive behavior to protect their own passengers. Moreover, modelling such
behavior dynamics is particularly challenging in the offline setting, because agents learn from a
fixed dataset collected by other policies, which might be designed to solve different tasks and
therefore the behaviors defined by such policies are not necessarily aligned with the goals of the
agents.

To deal with these issues, this project will explore a recently proposed alternative formulation to
traditional offline single-agent reinforcement learning [12, 7] for the multi-agent setting, which
considers trajectories as a long sequence of states, actions and rewards, and selects actions
conditioning on a high target return. This allows us to leverage the Transformer network [38],
which has become the front-runner in many sequence modelling problems ever since it was first
defined and, which has in particular revolutionized the field of Natural Language Processing
[9]. By treating MARL as a sequence modelling problem, the effectiveness of these large and
generic models can be transferred to the RL domain to bring to MARL the desired stability
and scalability that this method enjoys [5]. But the main appeal of such approach relies on
its capacity to develop different policies just by conditioning on different target returns. As
such, we theorize that it is possible to influence the emergence of cooperative and competitive
behavior amongst the agents by using the right reward structure to condition on.
The first two models to explore solving conventional offline RL problems by generative trajectory
modelling while conditioning on a high return are Decision Transformer [7] and Trajectory
Transformer [12]. These models are trained on a fixed dataset to auto-regressively select an
action conditioned not only on the current state, but also on a target return, formally expressed

a high future return. On the other hand, the Trajectory Transformer models distributions over
the input trajectories and then uses beam search as a planning algorithm to select a sequence of
actions that yields the highest return. Both models have matched or exceeded the performance
of state-of-the-art offline RL baselines on a diverse set of single agent environments.
This project will extend the sequence modelling for single-agent reinforcement learning frame-
work to multi-agent reinforcement learning, parting from the Decision Transformer architecture
and investigate two aspects. Firstly, how effectively can this approach be used to solve multi-
agent reinforcement learning problems, which focuses on the learning dynamics, and secondly,
how well can agents learn to develop competitive and cooperative behavior from a fixed training
dataset, which concerns behavior modelling. These considerations are phrased in the following
two research questions.
1. Can we model offline Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning as a sequence modelling prob-
lem effectively?
2. Can agents develop a cooperative and competitive behavior from off-policy cooperative
data in such a framework?
More specifically, we approach these research questions as described below. A more thorough
elaboration of our methods can be found in section 4.
1. Can we model offline Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning as a sequence mod-
elling problem effectively?
Firstly, the MARL problem is defined as a conditional-sequence problem, by formulating tra-
jectories as sequences and where the actions are auto-repressively selected conditioned on the
current state and a desired return. Then the Decision Architecture transformer architecture is
extended to a multi-agent version, which we will refer to as Multi-agent Decision Transformer
(MADT), to solve MARL as a sequence problem.
This report explores two different learning-execution strategies; a fully centralized and a fully
decentralized version, which constrains the trajectory sequence formulation as well as the archi-
tectural design of the transformer model. For the fully centralized setting we define a joint-action
modelling approach whereas for the decentralized we propose modelling actions individually. A
behavior cloning (BC) variant of the joint-action transformer is also explored, which simply mim-
ics the input data, ignoring returns. These different variations of sequence modelling MARL
are then tested on two environments of different complexity and goals to compare the episode
returns the achieve to other baselines and determine its effectiveness in terms of performance.
2. Can agents develop a cooperative and competitive behavior from off-policy co-
operative data?
We propose a reward transformation that modifies the return values in the trajectory sequences
depending on whether we want an agent to behave in a cooperative or competitive manner. We
then train the Multi-Agent Decision Transformer on a dataset of trajectories collected by online
algorithms following exclusively a cooperative paradigm, replacing the returns in the sequence
with the transformed ones.
This method is tested in different environments and conditioning on different behaviors for
both joint and disjoint action MADTs to investigate whether this approach actually induces the
specified behavior on the agents as seen from their episode returns, even if it has not trained on
such a behavior.

For these experiments we consider the multi-agent environments Level-based Foraging (LBF),
a simple environment, and Multi-robot warehouse (RWARE) [23], a more diﬀicult environment
with partial-observability and sparse-rewards, to examine how our approach performs in different
levels of complexity. The dynamics of these environments are described in depth in section 3.
The results obtained from the experiments indicate that not only is formulating offline MARL as
a sequence modelling possible, but it also achieves an overall good performance. The joint action
MADT achieved episode returns close to the expert online dataset used for training, whereas
its decentralized counterpart slightly lagged on performance in the LBF environment and was
unable learn in the RWARE environment and produce significant results. Moreover, we show
that this approach is capable of successfully modelling different behaviors from cooperative-only
data through reward modelling as seen from their episode returns, where competitive agents were
able to outperform the cooperative ones. These experiment results are shown and analyzed in
section 8.
The results suggest that treating offline multi-agent reinforcement learning as a sequence mod-
elling problem is a promising alternative to traditional methods. Although it is important to
bear in mind that the main goal of this project was to develop a proof-of-concept for formulating
MARL as a sequence modelling problem, rather than developing a sophisticated state-of-the-art
algorithm, with the purpose of exploring the potential of this approach and set the basis for
future research. For such, the experiments were limited to specific and limited use cases, behav-
iors and learning strategies, thus more in depth-research is needed to examine more scenarios
and determine the real extent of stability-scalability trade-offs and other limitations that this
approach conveys before considering real-world applications.

Environments
To conduct our research, we will focus on two environments, Level-Based Foraging (LBF) and
Multi-Robot Warehouse (RWARE). Each environment has a specific goal and they vary in
complexity level; LBF is a relatively simple environment whereas RWARE is more challenging
with spare rewards and partial observability. The nature and dynamics of such environments
will shape the design of our methods, therefore they are introduced before commencing the
research.
3.1
Choosing the environment
The first and most important consideration for choosing a suitable environment for this project,
naturally was that it should be a multi-agent environment where it is possible for agents to both
cooperate and compete with each other. Moreover, we aimed for a relatively simple environment
where it would be easy to interpret the results, such that its complexity would not be a distraction
for one of the main goals of this project, which concerns studying the behaviour of the agents.
Since this project considers offline learning, a collection of fixed trajectories from an environment
of the above characteristics is required, which our algorithms will use to learn a policy. However,
the only open-sourced multi-agent offline dataset consists of replays obtained from the StarCraft
environment [39], which has a big action and state space and is only defined for cooperative
settings. This would require to modify the environment itself to allow for competition, and,
given its complexity, was deemed unfeasible. Consequently, the only alternative way of obtaining
the offline dataset was to manually collect the replays of an online algorithm interacting in a
multi-agent environment during training, which entails an additional side task.
Fortunately, although also scarce, there are suﬀicient multi-agent environments available to the
general public [27]. One of these is MazeBase [33], which was the original consideration to use
as an environment for this project, since it could be customized and would allow us to design an
environment ideal for competitive and cooperative tasks. Nonetheless, such an environment was
implemented in the lua language [16], and existing implementations of online MARL algorithms
were written in Python.
Hence, using this environment would mean that both frameworks
would have to be integrated, which would be too time consuming and was out of the scope of
this project, so this option was discarded.
After some further exploration, we found a framework that included a series of new suitable
multi-agent environments, Level-Based Foraging (LBF) and Multi-Robot Warehouse (RMWARE)
[23], and an integrated pipeline for training many online algorithms of diverse characteristics on
such environments, which could easily be used to obtain our offline dataset. Both these envi-
ronments are primarily defined for cooperative purposes, so they are not ideal for the purpose
of this project where different kinds of behavior are explored, but we believe that their nature
could still be exploited.

3.2
Environment description
In the following section we describe in detail the characteristics of each of the two environments,
Level-Based Foraging (LBF) and Multi-Robot Warehouse (RMWARE), including their setup
and goals, as well as their dynamics, such as action space, reward structure or state transitions.
3.2.1
Level-Based Foraging
Level-Based Foraging (LBF) is a mixed cooperative-competitive game, where agents navigate a
grid world and coordinate with each other to collect food items scattered across the grid. This
environment is hosted in an open-source codebase on GitHub [29].
In this environment, agents and food items are randomly placed in the grid world, and each
agent and food item is assigned a level. The agents’ goal is to collect all of the food items, or at
least, maximize the sum of the levels of the food items collected. An agent or a group of agents
can only collect a food item if the sum of their levels is equal to or higher than the level of the
food item. That is, collecting a food item may require the cooperation of several agents, which
need to pick up the food in the same timestep.
For this experiment, a specific configuration of such environment is used with an 8x8 grid with 2
agents and 3 food items, as shown in Figure 3.1. Agents, food and levels are scattered randomly

limited by the following factors:
• The environments are relatively simple, since they are only instantiated on two interacting

4.2
Training and execution paradigms
During the training process agents optimize their policy from experience with respect to the
received reward signals. There are two types of learning in MARL depending on the informa-
tion shared between the agents, namely independent or decentralized learners and joint-action
learners, which follow a centralized approach. In a similar manner, there is centralized and de-
centralized execution, which concerns action selection. Based on this, one can distinguish three
training-execution paradigms:
• Fully centralized
During training all information is shared between the agents through a central controller
or communication network, which is used to update a common policies. Then a centralized
unit computes the joint actions for all agents. This approach is a naive solution to the
non-stationarity limitation of MARL, which reduces the problem to a MDP. However, this
is computationally ineffective, since the action space dimension grows exponentially. This
ultimately makes it unfeasible for large-scale applications [11].
12

• Fully decentralized
During training, has an individual policy which updates with its own experience, without
taking into account the other agents´ and use these same policies to determine their actions
at execution time.
While decentralized learning is scalable, since the algorithmic time complexity only grows
linearly with the number of agents, it is susceptible to the non-stationarity problem [24],
since the environment is no longer stationary from the single agent’s point of view.
• Centralized critics and decentralized actors
This approach was introduced to take advantage of the availability of the full observ-
able state or central controller during the training phase to correctly estimate the value-
functions or the policy gradients for then executing independent policies to maintain scal-
ability [21].
In partially observable environments, algorithms sometimes support the use of communi-
cation between agents through communication protocols or neural communication model.
This helps collaborating agents inferring the underlying state of the environment to deal
with the non-stationary problem.

Background
In this section we introduce the theoretical background on which this research and methodology
is cemented.
We briefly introduce the most important notions behind this project, which a
seasoned reader might prefer to skip, as well as give an overview of the work that has been done
until the moment in the field of sequence modelling in reinforcement learning, which sets the
grounds for our work.
5.1
Reinforcement Learning
Reinforcement learning is a subfield of machine learning that uses a trial-and-error procedure
to make agents learn to perform a task, such they are rewarded when they perform actions that
contribute to achieving the goal [34].
In the single-agent RL, the environment is usually mathematically modeled as a Markov Decision
Process (MDP), formally given by a tuple (S, A, P, R, γ) [4]. Where:
• S is the set of possible states
• A is the action space
• P are the transition probabilities
• R is the reward function.
• γ ∈[0, 1) is the discount factor.
Such notation is the foundation for many reinforcement learning algorithms. these algorithms
function in an iterative process where, at each timestep, the agent perceives the current state of
the environment and chooses an action to perform. When the agent performs that action, the
state transitions into a new one and the agent receives a reward. This process is repeated until
the end of the episode, which is reached either when the task has been completed or because
it has reached the timestep limit. By doing so, the agents learn a policy to determine the best
action to select, which is a mapping from all the possible states of the environment to the action
that can be selected.
5.1.1
Offline Reinforcement Learning
An important distinction in reinforcement learning arises depending on the source of the data
the algorithm uses experience to learn; it can be collected during training or it could have been
previously collected. Based on this, RL algorithms are classified into two types; online and
offline. While most of reinforcement learning algorithms are online [14], for this project we will
focus on offline reinforcement learning.
Online reinforcement learning iteratively collects experience directly by interacting with the
environment and then uses that experience to update the policy at each timestep [14]. That is,
the model can learn from new information in real-time and adapt to changes in the environment
dynamics. These algorithms can be on-policy, which use experience to directly improve the
policy used to select actions, such as Policy Iteration or PPO [22], or off-policy, which improve
a policy other than the one used to select actions, such as Q-learning or DQN [22].
16

In contrast, offline reinforcement learning uses a fixed set of experience collected over a period
of time for learning, such that it does not interact with the environment in real-time during
training [14]. Offline reinforcement learning is preferred in settings where online interaction is
impractical, either because data collection is expensive (such as in robotics) or dangerous (such
as in healthcare). Although offline RL algorithms are less computationally expensive, one of it’s
biggest drawbacks is the trade-off between good generalization and avoiding undesired behaviors
outside of the training data distribution [14].

5.2
Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning
As briefly introduced in previous sections, Multi-agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) is a
sub-field of Reinforcement Learning that studies how multiple agents interact in a shared en-
vironment.
It is becoming increasingly popular due to the many real-world applications of
multi-agent systems, such as traﬀic control [31], network packet routing [17], energy distribu-
tion, robotics [13], economic modeling [18], or the analysis of social dilemmas.
To model multi-agent environments and their dynamics, the Markov Game is usually used as a
framework. The Markov Game extends Markov Decision Processes (MDP) to multiple agents
that simultaneously interact within a common environment and with each other. Formally, a
Markov Game is expressed as a (N, X, U, P, R, γ), where:
• N = {1, . . . , N} is the set of agents present in the common environment.
• X = {x1, . . . , xN} is the set of the state observations for each of the agents.
• A = {A1 × · · · × A2} is the action space for each agent.
• P is the state transition probability.
• R = {R1 . . . RN} is the reward function for each of the agents.
• γ ∈[0, 1) is the discount factor.
5.2.1
Current challenges and limitations
There are many underlying challenges in the multi-agent settings, in addition to the ones inher-
ited from single-agent RL, ones intrinsic to MARL framework itself and others related to the
lack of existing foundations and open-source material available to progress with the research in
this field. It is important to review these since they also apply to our work and we will address
them as reflected from our results.

1. Algorithmic challenges
While well-understood and provably convergent algorithms are available for solving single-agent
reinforcement learning algorithms, experiments have shown that such algorithms fail to converge
to optimal solutions when applied to multi-agent scenarios [19]. The transition from single-
agent to multi-agent settings introduces new challenges that require a different algorithm design
approach.
The main limitations of current MARL and the different algorithms designed to
overcome each one of these are described below.
• Non-stationarity
The environment in a multi-agent setting is being altered by the actions of all agents
present in such an environment, thus the environment becomes non-stationary from the
single-agent perspective [24]. This has a major implication, and it is that the Markov
property does not longer hold in non-stationary environments, which is the foundation
of most single-agent reinforcement learning algorithms. Consequently, traditional single-
agent RL independent learners often lead to inconsistent results when applied to multi-
agent environments, that is, they are unstable.
• Scalability
The curse of dimensionality present in regular RL is magnified in the multi-agent settings
due to increase in the number of agents and their different behaviors. This is especially
significant in centralized learning with discrete action spaces, where the joint action space
grows exponentially with the number of agents. This makes training and execution com-
putationally expensive and not unfeasible for large-scale environments.
• Partial observability
Most of algorithms are based on the assumption of the full observability of the state by
all agents. However, this is rarely the case in most real-world applications, where agents
have different observations of the state.
Although obervability can be a constraint in any environment (not just multi-agent), it is
often limited by the scalability of MARL algorithms in environments with a large number
of agents, where the agents’ observability might be restricted to just its vicinity.

5.3
Behavior cloning
Behavior cloning is a method within the field of imitation learning. Imitation learning simply
learns a policy given a dataset of trajectory demonstrations with the objective of mimicking
them, in contrast to reinforcement learning which aims to find an optimal policy solving the
MDP that models the environment.
The simplest form of imitation learning is behavior cloning (BC), which learns from expert
replays thorough supervised learning. For example, dataset is collected from a human steering a
car from which BC learns a policy which can be used for self-driving cars. This policy is simply
a function that maps a set of states to a distribution over actions.

Although this approach is stands out for its simplicity and eﬀiciency, it is highly dependant on
the quality of the demonstrations it is trained on and when it encounters observations that it
has not seen before, this can lead to poor results.
5.4
Reinforcement Learning as a sequence modelling problem
Concurrent research work [7, 12] has recently proposed a new formulation for reinforcement
learning by treating it as a sequence modeling problem, where the states, actions and rewards in
the Markov-decision process are modelled as a long sequence. Then sequence-to-sequence algo-
rithms, namely transformers, replace the conventional RL algorithms by using auto-regression
to select optimal actions. Such papers show that this approach has matched or exceeded the
performance of state-of-the-art offline RL baselines on a diverse set of single agent environments.
A way of viewing this approach is as a supervised learning problem conditioned on a return;
algorithms learn the distribution of actions conditioned on both the state and the return of the
trajectory and define a policy by conditioning on achieving high return. Such an approach but
in an online setting has been explored in Upside Down RL [28].
Advantages
A big advantage of using a transformer to solve RL problems is that it bypasses two important
issues of traditional RL: the need for bootstrapping for long term credit assignment, which
can now be done directly via self-attention, and discounting future rewards, which can lead
to a myopic behavior, that is, that it only considers immediate rewards.
These issues are
addressed because transformers can capture long-range dependencies [12]. Moreover, formulating
reinforcement learning as a sequence modeling problem reduces it to a single problem, compared
to other algorithms which decompose it into many sub-problems, such as actor-critic algorithms,
which require separate actors and critics, model-based algorithms, which require performing
predictions for planning, and offline RL methods, which often require estimation of the behavior
policy. Also, transformers are high-capacity architectures which can capture dependencies on
long and complex sequences through its attention mechanism which makes it highly versatile
and scalable to many real-life applications.
We will now briefly present the work that has been done so far within the sequence modelling
formulation of reinforcement learning.
5.4.1
Decision Transformer
The Decision Transformer [7] is, together with the Trajectory Transformer, the first architecture
developed to solve a reinforcement learning problem in the form of sequence modelling. Given
an input sequence of returns, states and actions, it uses a causal mask to auto-regressively select
the optimal action conditioned on a desired return. An important observation to note is that
the desired return must be within the distribution of the input training data, which must also
be known in advance.
Our work will be based on the Decision Transformer architecture, which we will extend to
support multi-agent environments.

Decoder
The decoder consists of a stack of 12 identical layers, each composed of four sublayers. The
first sublayer receives the input {x2, x3, . . . , xn+1}, and performs multi-head self-attention over
it. This layer also masks future tokens, such that it attends only to the preceding observations,
since the prediction of an element can only depend on the known outputs for the elements that
come before it in the sequence.
So when it is processing the token at position “t”, it only
considers the sequence up to the previous position: {x2, x3, . . . , xt−1}. A normalization layer
follows. The next layer implements a fully connected feed-forward network, also followed by a
normalization layer.
Softmax/Linear layer
A softmax layer is used for discrete outputs, such as for classification tasks and a linear layer is
used for continous outputs, such as text prediction.
5.5.2
Multi-head Attention
The attention mechanism enables the Transformer to have extremely long term memory. The
attention mechanism ”attends” on all elements in a sequence, such that it learns a matrix where,
for each element in the sequence, there is a row that holds the values that quantify how relevant
the other elements are to it. From this, it outputs a new embedding for each token that takes
into account this information. It does so as follows:
1. For each token in the sequence, there are three vectors: a query, a key and a value, stored
as matrices. The model learns these three weight matrices during training.
• Query: a vector representation of the current token.
• Key: the vector representations for all the other tokens.
• Value: the actual token representation.
2. The model calculates the self-attention score, which is the measure of relevance between
the current token and any other token in the sequence. It is calculated by computing the
dot product between the query vector of the current token with the key vector of the token
being scored. The scores are then passed through a softmax function.
3. It outputs a new representation for the current token. For the current token, the value
vector is multiplied by the softmax score computed in the previous step, so that the overall
vector is scaled according to its relative importance for the current token. Finally, all of
the scaled values are summed together to produce the encoding of the current token.

Multi-Agent Decision Transformer
Previously, in section 4 we defined the different formulations of offline multi-agent reinforce-
ment learning as a sequence in a fully centralized and a fully decentralized training-execution
paradigms. We also explained how we condition on the desired returns in these paradigms to
select optimal actions and how we define a return transformation for such conditioning to model
competitive and cooperative behavior in the agents. We also defined a behavior cloning variant
which is not conditioned on returns, and is merely used for comparison.
In this section we present the transformer architecture that we use to solve the MARL prob-
lems we described above within the sequence modelling framework, which we refer to as the
Multi-Agent Decision Transformer (MADT). We implement two different variations for a fully
centralized and fully decentralized paradigm, the joint-action MADT and the disjoint-action
MADT, respectively.
This transformer architecure is an extension of the Decision Transformer, thoroughly explained
in section 5.4.1. The reason behind this choice instead of choosing the Trajectory Transformer
is that, even though the latter one performs slightly better, since it is a model-based algorithm,
it requires far more computational resources that the decision transformer due to the extensive
planning, specially with complex environments as multi-agent are. On the other hand, Decision
Transformer is model-free, and the implementation is cleaner, simpler to extend, and easier to
understand.

Experiment settings
Level-Based Foraging (LBF) and Multi-robot warehouse (RWARE) environments are run on the
configurations Foraging-8x8-2p-3f-v2 and rware-tiny-2ag-v1 respectively.
All experiments are run on 10 seeds (DT experiments are only run on 3), and from each run, 100
episodes are collected for evaluation. The data is logged evaluation episodes run every 3000 steps
of training. The episode timestep limit on LBF is set to 50 and to 100 for RWARE, the same
we used for training the respective online algorithms. All the parameter settings are available
in more detail in C.
For the training phase, teacher-forcing training is used, which uses the ground truth from a prior
time step as input for training the model, instead of the model’s previous predicted value. That
is, the model learns to fit the sequence xt+1, xt+2, ..., xt+N+1 given xt, xt+1, ..., xt+N. The loss
function is cross-entropy, since actions are discrete in both environments.
The models were implemented in Python, using the Gym and Pytorch library frameworks. The
code was an extension of Decision Transformer, whose reproducibility was also challenging, given
the multiple setbacks such as broken dependencies and installation problems. The experiments
were run on DTU’s HCP cluster, on a GPU with 32GB of memory.

Conclusion
In this project a framework that models offline MARL as a sequence modelling problem was de-
veloped, alongside a transformer architecture that learns from this formulation to select optimal
actions and model different behaviors exploring two learning strategies. The action selection was
conditioned on the episode return, which was defined differently depending if we wanted agents
to be cooperative or competitive. Experiments were run on two environments, and based on
these results we can now answer our research questions as well as have a better understanding
of the limitations of this approach.
9.1
Research questions
1. Can we model offline Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning as a sequence mod-
elling problem effectively?
The experiments proved that modelling offline Multi-agent Reinforcement Learning as a sequence
modelling problem and solving it as such not only is possible, but this approach also yielded a
good overall performance across the joint and disjoint action MADT variants.
One can observe from the experiments results that, as expected, the joint-action MADT, which
follows a centralized approach obtains significantly higher episode returns than its decentralized
counterpart, the disjoint-action MADT. In the case of LBF, the joint-action MADT can almost
always solve the environment, even outperforming the offline dataset collected by an expert
online IQL algorithm.
The disjoint action MADT obtains a good total return for LBF, well above the random baseline
but on par with the BC baseline. However, in the RWARE case it was simply unable to learn
and its results are indistinguishable from a random action selection, which indicates that it does
not perform well on complex environments.
As for the behavior cloning MADT, even though it was defined just as a baseline, such model
was capable of learning to output actions that led to high episode returns, which, in the LBF
environment outperforms the average of the training dataset and was not far behind in RWARE.
2. Can agents develop a cooperative and competitive behavior from off-policy co-
operative data?
The above experiment results are a clear indicator that agents successfully developed compet-
itive and cooperative behavior from cooperative data. We could see that the agents that were
conditioned on a cooperative return formulation performed similarly whereas when one agent
was conditioned to be cooperative and the other competitive, the competitive agent achieved
higher returns than the cooperative ”naive” agent, which indicates that the competitive one
found some way to exploit the other agent’s collaborative attitude to obtain higher returns.
When conditioning both agents on a cooperative behavior, episode returns in both agents were
very similar in both environments, as shown from the graphs and supported by the computed
p-values 8.1. This is the case for both the joint-action MADT and the disjoint action MADT.
Moreover, when conditioning one agent on a competitive behavior and another on the compet-
itive, one could easily discern both behaviors from the episode returns, where the competitive
one obtained significant higher returns than the other. This could be seen from the graphs and
Cooperative and competitive Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning with Transformers

==PAGE:56==
the p-values in both the joint-action MADT and the disjoint action, which however took longer
to induce such behavior in agents.
9.2
Limitations and future work
Based on the experiments, a few limitations were encountered that the MADT is subject to
and which are important to understand the real capabilities of sequence modelling for MARL
problems.
As reflected in experiment 8.3.2, the quality of the performance of the MADT was bounded by
the quality of the input dataset itself. As such, we saw that even if it was possible to obtain higher
returns in the environment within the same number of timesteps, if trajectories that achieved
such returns were not present in the training dataset, the MADT was not capable achieving them
either. Hence the MADT obtains completely different episode returns when different datasets
are used. Therefore, this method proved to be highly dependent on the training dataset and
can only approximate to the target return it is conditioned on when it is drawn from the return
distribution of the dataset. This also means that such upper bound must be known in advance.
This contrasts with other methods like DT, which are more flexible. This limitation was also
observed in the Decision Transformer paper and further supported by a more rigorous study [5]
released two weeks before the hand-in of this project.
Moreover, the disjoint action MADT failed to work on a more complex environment, which
posses a risk towards the adoption of this method for large-scale real-world applications, since
the joint-action is not scalable and cannot be used for competitive settings, and the disjoint
action MADT’s performance is not good enough.
A possible variation of the MADT could
leverage the offline setting to follow a centralized learning-decentralized execution by training
on the full information during training and masking the other agent’s information at evaluation
time, approach that has obtained good results in other MARL algorithms.
Furthermore, [5] also suggests than regular Decision Transformers do not work on stochastic
environments, which were not explored in this project, and which likely also applies to MADT.
This project also used relatively simple environments with only two agents, so future research
could replicate these results on competitive and stochastic environments with several agents.
This would help to have a better understanding of the limitations of this approach, such as
performance and scalability.
Nonetheless, the simplicity of such approach and its versatility to solve different tasks and
model competitive and cooperative behaviors in agents makes the sequence modelling framework
for MARL an attractive alternative to traditional MARL approaches, specially when applied
to a subset of environments where it has demonstrated it can achieve a good performance.
This project sets the basis for formulating MARL as a sequence modelling problem, which will
hopefully mature into a well developed framework.
