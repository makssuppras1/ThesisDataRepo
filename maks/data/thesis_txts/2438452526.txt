Abstract  
The last years Lactococcus lactis and Bacillus subtilis have proven to be highly potent on 
secreting heterologous and homologous proteins. Strains with the ability of secreting high 
amounts of proteins are of major interest for the industry for various reasons. Key characteristics 
that make these microorganisms ideal for the food industry are their status as “GRAS”, they have 
annotated genomes, they can be cultivated easily and various tools for their genetic manipulation 
are available.  
This study investigates the possibility of improving protein secretion in Lactococcus lactis and 
Bacillus subtilis using adaptive evolution and random mutagenesis. The first step for improving 
the secretion capacity in Lactococcus lactis was to create recombinant strains with the ability of 
secreting the heterologous enzymes β-galactosidase and β-glucosidase. The enzyme β-
galactosidase is the product of the gene lacZ which is a part of the lac operon in Escherichia coli. 
In its natural form the enzyme is found in the cytoplasm so in order to render Lactococcus lactis 
able to secrete the enzyme a sequence coding for a signal peptide should be added upstream of 
the enzyme’s sequence. The chosen signal leader sequence was of USP45, the main Sec-
depended protein in Lactococcus lactis. Both of the sequences would be cloned downstream of 
the gapB promoter which naturally controls the expression of the enzyme Glyceraldehyde-3-
phosphate dehydrogenase, a very important enzyme involved in glycolysis. The enzyme β-
glucosidase originated from Saccharophagus degradans and it would be cloned in an expression 
vector which offered a signal peptide. After successfully acquiring recombinant L.lactis strains 
the secretion capacity would be tested using droplet-based microfluidics screening. The sorting 
of droplets in which β-galactosidase would be found extracellularly was based on Fluorescence 
Resonance Energy Transfer (FRET). The development of a protocol for the detection of β-
galactosidase in the droplets using FRET was also one of the goals in this project.  
Bacillus subtilis naturally secretes the enzyme α-amylase thus there was no necessity on inserting 
any heterologous protein in the bacterium. The microorganism was subjected to treatment with 3 
different concentrations of the mutagenesis agent Ethyl Methanesulfate (EMS) for inducing 
mutations that potentially could be proven beneficial. Furthermore, the secretion capacity was 
put under test by exploiting the power of evolution. The microorganism was transferred daily for 
a period of 7 weeks in new media with the ultimate purpose of creating strains with accumulated 
beneficial mutations. The strains acquired with both of the approaches were examined using 
turbidimetric monitoring of the bacterial growth. The growth rates were supplemented with the 
rates of starch degradation as well for testing how cell growth and starch degradation are 
correlated. In both cases no improvement was noticed either in the growth rates or the rates of 
starch degradation.

Resume 
Igennem årene har Lactococcus lactis og Bacillus subtilis bevidst at de er meget kompetente til 
at udskille både homologe og heterogene proteiner. Stammer der er i stand til at udskille store 
mængder af proteiner er af stor industriel interesse. Nogle af de vigtigste karakteristika der gør 
disse mikroorganismer ideelle for fødevareindustrien er deres status som ”GRAS” organismer, at 
de har annoterede genomer, de kan let kultiveres og der er mange værktøjer til at kunne lave 
genetiske modifikationer.  
Dette studie undersøger mulighederne for at forbedre udskillelsen af proteiner i Lactococcus 
lactis og Bacillus subtilis ved hjælp af adaptiv laboratorie evolution og tilfældig mutagenese. Det 
første skridt i at forbedre udskillelsesevnen af Lactococcus lactis var ved at lave en rekombinant 
stamme der kunne udskille de heterologe enzymer β-galactosidase og β-glucosidase. β-
galactosidase er et produkt af genet lacZ som er en del af lac operonet i Escherichia coli. Dette 
enzym findes naturligt i cytoplasmaet, så for at få det udskilt af Lactococcus lactis skal det 
tilføjes en sekvens kodende for et signal peptid opstrøms af enzymsekvensen. Signal leader 
sekvensen fra Usp45 blev valgt til dette formål som er den vigtigste Sec afhængige protein i 
Lactococcus lactis. Begge disse sekvenser vil blive indsat nedstrøms af gapB promoteren, der 
naturligt kontrollerer ekspressionen af Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase, som er et 
meget vigtigt enzym i glykolysen. Genet for β-glucosidase, som oprindeligt stammer fra 
Saccharophagus degradans, blev indsat i en ekspressionsvektor der indeholder et signal peptid. 
Efter at have fundet rekombinante L. lactis stammer ville deres evne til at udskille enzymet være 
blevet testet i et dråbebaseret mikrofluid screeningssystem. Sorteringen af dråber der indeholder 
ekstracellulær β-glucosidase blev baseret på Fluorescence Resonance Energy Transfer (FRET). 
Udviklingen af en protokol for detektionen af β-galactosidase ved hjælp af FRET var et af 
målene for projektet.  
α-amylase bliver naturligt udskilt af Bacillus subtilis hvorfor der ikke var behov for at skulle 
indsætte et heterogent protein i denne bakterie. Mutagenese blev lavet for B. subtilis ved hjælp af 
det mutagene stof Ethyl Methanesulfate (EMS), i tre forskellige koncentrationer, for at inducere 
mutationer der kunne vise sig at være favorable. Derudover blev evolution også anvendt til at 
forbedre udskillelsesevnen. Mikroorganismen blev dagligt overført til frisk medie i syv uger, for 
at få stammer der har akkumuleret mutationer der giver forbedret vækst karakteristika. De 
fundne stammer fra begge fremgangsmåder blev undersøgt via turbiditetsmålinger af bakteriel 
vækst. Væksthastighederne blev suppleret af nedbrydningshastigheder af stivelse og 
sammenholdt for korrelation mellem disse. I begge tilfælde var der ingen forbedringer af 
væksthastigheder eller nedbrydelsen af stivelse.

1. Aim of the study 
Heterologous and homologous protein secretion constitutes a field of great concern for the food 
industry. A little less than half of all the enzymes sold in a world-wide scale correspond to food 
and feed enzymes (Fernandes, 2010). Regardless of how potent an enzyme is, its utilization in 
the industry is directly related to possessing a host organism from which the protein can be 
isolated successfully in large quantities. Thus, the search for microorganisms with the ability of 
secreting considerable amounts of proteins is a field of intense research. 
Two highly potent bacteria that are under examination for their ability to secrete homologous and 
heterologous proteins are Bacillus subtilis and Lactococcus lactis (García-Fruitós, 2012). Protein 
secretion is a multifactorial process that needs further detailed elucidation in order to exploit the 
foul potential of these microorganisms. The variables that constitute the equation of protein 
secretion are the type of promoter, the translation efficiency of the mRNA, the number and type 
of intracellular and extracellular proteases, the type of signal peptide, the existence of a pro-
peptide, the type of secretion system, the correct folding of the protein, the various chaperones 
and the possibility of a stress response due to protein over-expression [(Nijland & Kuipers, 
2008),(Morello et al., 2007)]. 
The purpose of the current study was to improve protein secretion in Lactococcus lactis and 
Bacillus subtilis by taking advantage of the beneficial effects of adaptive evolution and random 
mutagenesis. The goals set during the project were the following: 
• Create recombinant strains of Lactococcus lactis with the ability to secrete the 
heterologous enzymes β-galactosidase and β-glucosidase. 
• Design a protocol for the detection of β-galactosidase in droplets based on Fluorescence 
Resonance Energy Transfer. 
• Examine the secretion efficiency of the recombinant bacterial strains using droplet-based 
microfluidics screening. 
• Apply repetitive rounds of adaptive laboratory evolution and droplet-based selection for 
further enhancing heterologous protein secretion. 
• Improve α-amylase secretion in Bacillus subtilis using the mutagenesis agent Ethyl 
Methanesulfate (EMS). 
• Improve α-amylase secretion in Bacillus subtilis by applying adaptive laboratory 
evolution.

2. Introduction 
This section can be divided in three basic part. The first part provides a succinct general 
overview on the impact of enzymes through history and their importance in the food industry. 
The second part addresses the importance of L. lactis in the food industry, an overview of the 
cell’s secretion system and how adaptive evolution and droplet-based microfluidics screening 
can be used for the optimization of heterologous protein secretion. The third and final part 
concerns the role of B. subtilis in science and how random mutagenesis can be used in order to 
improve protein secretion. Insights in these different areas of knowledge can be proven 
beneficial in order to fully grasp the connection within the field of optimizing protein secretion 
and this study. 
2.1 Enzymes through history 
The first records of beer, wine and bread are dated thousands of years ago in scripts originating 
from Sumerians, Greeks and Romans. In the case of the two last communities the existence of 
biocatalysts was unknown so a successful fermentation was a gift from the gods Dionysus and 
Bacchus (Quitmann, Fan, & Czermak, 2013). Antonie van Leeuwenhoek, a pioneer and founding 
father of microbiology, was the first who directly observed microorganisms using a homemade 
microscope in 1675 (Quitmann et al., 2013). The first approach to establish a nomenclature for 
describing these micromachines was in 1876 with the introduction of the term enzyme 
(Quitmann et al., 2013). The same period, a Danish pharmacist under the name Christian Ditlev 
Ammentorp Hansen introduced in the market rennet (enzyme preparation) for the production of 
cheese. Major breakthroughs that followed were the Michaelis-Menten equations for the 
description of enzyme kinetics, the crystallization of enzymes and the elucidation of their 
structure (Quitmann et al., 2013). Even though not directly related to the food industry it would 
be unfair not to mention the discovery of restriction and thermostable enzymes. Their catalytic 
properties were a cornerstone for molecular biology with the advantages of their utilization being 
evident in the pharma, biotech and food industry (Quitmann et al., 2013). 
2.2 Enzymes in the food industry 
A more systematic utilization of enzymes in the food industry began in the twentieth century 
with the advent of large scale fermentations and downstream purification processes which 
allowed the production of enzymes on a large scale (Gurung et al., 2013). Further advances in 
genetic engineering, the optimization of fermentation processes, of downstream process and 
identification of new bacterial strains were factors which facilitated the expansion of the 
enzyme-market (Gurung et al., 2013). Some of the most important enzymes used in the food 
industry are listed in table 1.

2.3 Lactococcus lactis  
Lactococcus lactis is a Gram-positive bacterium with a prominent position in the production of 
fermented dairy products such as cheese and yogurt (van Hylckama Vlieg et al., 2006).This 
microorganism possesses various desirable features that make it ideal for further elucidation of 
its potential as a microbial cell factory. Its status is “generally regarded as safe”, the low GC 
content, its genome is sequenced (strains IL1403, MG1363, NZ9000), it has a simple metabolism 
and various tools are available for its genetic manipulation [(García-Fruitós, 2012)], (van 
Hylckama Vlieg et al., 2006), (Linares, Kok, & Poolman, 2010)].  
2.4 Overview of the Sec-translocon in Lactococcus lactis 
Bacteria would never be able to survive and adapt in various ecological niches, defend 
themselves or develop mutualistic relationships with other organisms without the means to 
secrete proteins. The complex of proteins used in gram-positive bacteria for the translocation of 
proteins across the hydrophobic lipid bilayer is known as Sec-translocon (Loir, 2006). In 
Lactococcus lactis the basic components of the Sec-translocon are the proteins SecY, SecE, 
SecG and SecA (Loir, 2006). As mentioned earlier one of the advantages of using Lactococcus 
lactis as a model organism is that its genome is fully sequenced. In comparative studies between 
the proteome of L. lactis and the proteomes of B. subtilis it was identified that the Sec translocon 
of L.lactis misses the proteins SecDF (Gold, Duong, & Collinson, 2007). This discovery is an 
example of the advantages when working with L.lactis as it shed light on a factor that could be 
examined during optimization of heterologous protein secretion (Loir, 2006).  
The channel that spans the cell’s membrane is comprised by the hetero-trimer SecYEG (Gold et 
al., 2007). The protein SecY is the largest of the three subunits and forms the pore of the channel 
(Gold et al., 2007). The subunits SecE and SecG reside in the back and in the side of SecY and 
have a more ancillary role (Gold et al., 2007). SecA is an ATPase which converts the chemical 
energy from the hydrolysis of ATP in mechanical energy for the translocation of the polypeptide 
chain through the pore (Kuipers et al., 2006).  
Bacteria don’t have endoplasmic reticulum so the process of translation takes place in the 
cytoplasm. The nascent polypeptide chain holds a signal sequence in the N-terminal which 
facilitates the targeting and recognition of the newly arising protein by the components of the 
translocation system (Kuipers et al., 2006). One further advantage in the secretion features of 
Lactococcus lactis is that it has only one Sec-mediated secreted protein (USP45) with a well 
characterized N-terminal signal sequence (Morello et al., 2007). The signal peptide of USP45 
and of most of the lactococcal signal peptides have three basic regions on the span of their 
structure (Ng & Sarkar, 2013). The n-region found in the N-terminus which includes positively

2.5 Heterologous protein secretion in Lactococcus lactis  
As mentioned earlier heterologous protein secretion is a multistep process, figure 1 depicts an 
overview of the process in Lactococcus lactis. 
 
Figure 1. Different stages during heterologous protein secretion in Lactococcus lactis

The first step in obtaining high amounts of proteins is to establish increased levels of gene 
expression. Bacterial promoters can be divided into two basic categories. The first category 
consists of the constitutive promoters with the availability of the RNA polymerase being the only 
factor affecting their activity. The second category consists of the regulated promoters, with the 
inducible promoters of this category being mainly utilized in heterologous protein expression 
(Nijland & Kuipers, 2008).  
==PAGE:18==
6 
 
A strong constitutive promoter used for heterologous protein expression in Lactococcus lactis is 
the P59, originally isolated from L. lactis subsp. cremoris (van der Vossen, van der Lelie, & 
Venema, 1987). In western blot experiments where the promoter was fused with the nuc reporter 
gene the results showed that protein expression was increased 10-fold (5mg/ml from 0.5 mg/ml) 
compared to the expression of nuc with its native promoter (Langella & Le Loir, 1999). The 
Western blot experiments were supplemented with Northern analysis which confirmed that 
improved protein secretion was the result of increased transcription (Langella & Le Loir, 1999). 
Nonetheless the secretion efficiency, namely the amount of secreted protein compared to the 
intracellular, was almost 60% in both cases (Langella & Le Loir, 1999). The levels of USP45 
secretion were used as control during cell growth, in cells with and without the P59nuc cassette. 
No major differences were noticed in growth and Usp45 secretion, proving that heterologous 
protein secretion does not stress the cell (Langella & Le Loir, 1999).  
In the same study the secretion efficiency of the nuc protein was tested by altering the 
composition of aminoacids in the propepetide sequence. The propeptide sequence is found 
downstream of the signal peptide’s sequence and can influence the secretion capacity by 
affecting the efficiency of the signal peptide’s cleavage (Langella & Le Loir, 1999). Exclusion of 
17 out of 21 aminoacids from the sequence of the propeptide, near the cleavage site, resulted in a 
secretion efficiency near 30 % (Langella & Le Loir, 1999).  On the flip side of the coin, insertion 
of the 9-residue LEISSTCDA synthetic propeptide between the nuc signal peptide and the 
mature protein ended in a 2-5 fold (10-25 mg/ml to 5 mg/ml) increase in expression when 
compared to nuc with an un-altered propeptide (Langella & Le Loir, 1999).  
One well known expression system with an inducible promoter is the Nisin Controlled 
Expression System (NICE) (Morello et al., 2007). Nisin is an antimicrobial peptide produced by 
L.lactis and it is used in the food industry as a natural preservative (Mierau, Olieman, Mond, & 
Smid, 2005). The biosynthetic gene cluster of the molecule is comprised of the promoters nisA, 
nisF and nisR (Kleerebezem, Beerthuyzen, Vaughan, De Vos, & Kuipers, 1997). The activity of 
the promoters nisA and nisF is mediated by nisin-autoregulation while the promoter nisR is 
constitutively activated (Kleerebezem et al., 1997). The promoter nisR controls the expression of 
the genes nisR and nisK (Kleerebezem et al., 1997). The nisK gene codes the histidine protein 
kinase nisK and the and the nisR gene codes the response regulator nisR (Kleerebezem et al., 
1997). Major advantages of this expression system are: (i) nisin is a molecule already used in 
food industry, (ii) there is a linear relationship between nisin concentration and protein 
production, (iii) the induction of the desired gene is controlled tightly, (iv) the protein production 
can reach the 60% of the total amount of intracellular proteins (Kleerebezem et al., 1997). Figure 
2 presents an overview of the NICE system.

2.6 Adaptive Laboratory Evolution  
In the effort of scientists to harness the power natural selection the method of Adaptive 
Laboratory Evolution was devised. In this method an organism is cultivated for long periods of 
time under defined and controlled conditions. Through the passage of time beneficial mutations 
that increase the fitness of the microorganism will rise randomly inside the population. Due to 
their increased fitness some strains will dominate over other strains that luck similar features so 
after several generations multiple beneficial mutations will accumulate in some strains resulting 
in higher fitness when compared to the ancestral strain (Dragosits & Mattanovich, 2013). 
Bacteria are ideal microorganisms for ALE as they can be cultivated easily, they have simple 
nutritional requirements and they have short generation times (Dragosits & Mattanovich, 2013). 
==PAGE:21==
9 
 
The advent of new technologies like Whole Genome Sequencing supplemented ideally ALE as it 
facilitated the correlation between genotype and phenotype (Dragosits & Mattanovich, 2013). 
Lactococcus lactis is known for its ability to grow in dairy environments, nonetheless it has been 
proposed that the ancestral strains of this species originally grew in plants (Bachmann, 
Starrenburg, Molenaar, Kleerebezem, & Vlieg, 2012). The introduction of agriculture and food 
fermentations were important factors that facilitated the transition of the species from one 
ecological niche to another (Bachmann et al., 2012). Adaptive Laboratory Evolution was the 
perfect tool in order to test this proposal.  
The strain L.lactis KF-147 was isolated from mung-bean sprouts and showed a poor growth in 
milk, when compared to dairy L. lactis dairy strains (Bachmann et al., 2012). The bacterium was 
propagated for 1000 generations [3 different serial propagations(SP1,SP2,SP3)] in milk with the 
ultimate purpose of acquiring similar traits as the dairy stains. One neuralgic factor for improved 
growth in milk was the introduction of the plasmid pNZ521 which encodes the extracellular 
protease PrtP (Bachmann et al., 2012). The bacterium was transformed with this plasmid for 
three reasons, i) Extracellular proteases are important for fast growth in milk for dairy strains, ii) 
to test if the coevolution with the unstable plasmid bearing the protease, will result in the 
stabilization of it, iii) to guarantee sufficient growth of the strain in milk (Bachmann et al., 2012). 
Lactate is the main by-product of carbohydrate catabolism in L.lactis thus growth can be 
monitored by measuring acidification rates (Bachmann et al., 2012).   
The acidification rates were fast during the initial propagations of the experiment but decreased 
after 300 generations. It was also noticed that the protease-positive strains decreased as well after 
300 generation. Figure 5 presents the general correlation pattern between number of mutations, 
fitness and generations of propagation. It was assumed that extended periods of plasmid – host 
coevolution would result in plasmid stabilization so protease – positive strains (~100 colonies 
from each culture) were isolated and propagated in milk. Further propagation for 300 generations 
resulted in similar results as in the first 300 generations of propagation. After 1000 generations 
the protease-positive strains were undetectable in all three parallel cultures. Regardless of the 
proteolytic trait loss the cultures displayed acidification rates must higher when compared to the 
starting strain. Isolation of one colony from each culture and further cultivation (strains NZ5521-
SP1, NZ5522-SP2, NZ5523-SP3) showed acidification rates similar to the cultures they were 
isolated from (Bachmann et al., 2012). The results are presented in figure 6.

2.7 Fluorescence Resonance Energy Transfer 
FRET is a physical phenomenon in which energy is being transferred between two 
chromophores in a non-radiative way. Initially both chromophores reside in a ground electronic 
state where there is no emission of light. Absorption of energy by the donor causes a transition 
from the ground electronic state to an excited electronic state. If the acceptor is in a certain 
distance (2-10 nm) from the donor and has the appropriate orientation in relation to the donor 
then the energy from the donor can be transferred to the acceptor causing its transition from the 
ground electronic state to an excited electronic state. After the energy is being transferred the 
donor returns in the initial ground electronic state and the acceptor emits light. One very 
important parameter for FRET is that the emission spectrum of the donor must overlap with the 
absorption spectrum of the acceptor (Hussain, 2009). 
2.8 Droplet based microfluidics screening  
The process of cell line improvement for the optimization of heterologous protein secretion 
requires robust and sensitive screening tools. A typical High Throughput Screening assay takes 
place with disposable microtiter plates and automated robot systems. A library of compounds is 
distributed in plates, which hold a biological target (bacterial cells, enzymes, etc.). After the 
designated period of incubation is over the plates are monitored for signs of biological activity 
and the best candidates are chosen (Caraus, Alsuwailem, Nadon, & Makarenkov, 2015). Inherent 
limitations of this approach are the costs of the consumables, capillary forces, the operating rate 
of the robotic system, prior wet-lab preparation, possible robotic failures, pipetting errors, 
differences in compound concentration, lighting or airflow anomalies, and evaporation of the 
samples [(Caraus et al., 2015), (Agresti et al., 2010)]. One tremendously promising alternative 
that could revolutionize screening is Droplet Based Microfluids. In this method single cells are 
compartmentalized in droplets which are then screened in rates that could reach thousand per 
second (Agresti et al., 2010). Major advantages offered by this method are the considerable 
reduction in costs due to the miniscule sample volumes and the high speed of screening (Agresti 
et al., 2010).  
Droplet based microfluidic screening have been used successfully used for the identification of 
enzymes with improved catalytic properties. One such example is the case of horseradish 
peroxidase. A research group managed to promote a 10-fold improvement in the activity of the 
enzyme using directed evolution and droplet-based selection (Agresti et al., 2010).

Bacillus subtilis is one of the best studied Gram-positive bacteria, the study of which facilitated

2010). The species have been considered strict anaerobe but new evidence suggest that it

2.10 Overview of protein secretion in Bacillus subtilis 
Similarly, with L. lactis the Sec-translocon mediates the transport for the majority of proteins 
destined to be secreted in B. subtilis. In the first steps of protein secretion the gene is subjected to 
transcription (Nijland & Kuipers, 2008). The ribosome then recognizes and binds the Ribosome 
Binding Sequence (RBS) of the mRNA and the process of translation is initiated (Nijland & 
Kuipers, 2008). The complex formed by the ribosome, the mRNA and the newly synthesized 
protein is called  “Ribosome- Nascent Chain complex (RNC)” (Nijland & Kuipers, 2008). The 
RNC is then recognized by the Signal Recognition Particle (SRP), a co-translation chaperone, 
which facilitates the transport of the complex in the membrane (Nijland & Kuipers, 2008). The 
RNC/SPR complex is recognized by the membrane bound protein FtsY which targets the nascent 
polypeptide chain in the Sec-translocon (Nijland & Kuipers, 2008). SecA interacts with the 
unfolded protein and by hydrolysing ATP pushes it through the channel formed by SecYEG 
(Nijland & Kuipers, 2008). The N-terminal end of the proteins emerging from the translocon is 
cleaved and the proteins are released to the periplasm rapidly acquiring their mature form 
(Nijland & Kuipers, 2008). A very important facilitator of the folding process is the membrane 
bound chaperone PrsA (Nijland & Kuipers, 2008). Overexpression of proteins can cause a 
scarcity in the available PrsA chaperones causing an increase in misfolded proteins (Nijland & 
Kuipers, 2008). An overview of the process can be seen in figure 7.

The increase in unfolded proteins in the periplasmatic space is detected by the cell using the two-
component system CssR-CssS (Nijland & Kuipers, 2008). The CssS component is a 
transmembrane sensor, for unfolded proteins, with two transmembrane domains (Harwood & 
Cranenburgh, 2008). Detection of unfolded proteins in the periplasm causes phosphorylation of 
the CssR component in the cytoplasm [(Nijland & Kuipers, 2008), (Harwood & Cranenburgh, 
2008)]. Phosphorylated CssR functions as a transcription initiator by binding to the promoter 
region of the stress-related genes CssR-CssS, htrA and htrB (Harwood & Cranenburgh, 2008; 
Nijland & Kuipers, 2008). There is a duality in the function of htrA which can be a chaperone for 
helping misfolded proteins regain their correct configuration and also target unsuccessfully 
==PAGE:26==

folded proteins for degradation (Harwood & Cranenburgh, 2008). The htrB protein functions as a 
protease for unfolded proteins (Nijland & Kuipers, 2008). Finally, after passing the 
peptidoglycan layer the proteins are directly released in the external environment where 
misfolded proteins are rapidly degraded by a plethora of proteases residing in the extracellular 
space (Nijland & Kuipers, 2008).  
2.11 Strain improvement using random mutagenesis 
Mutations are the cornerstone of evolution and the underline cause for the ability of 
microorganisms to adapt in constantly changing environments. In addition to adaptive evolution 
another powerful laboratory approach for strain improvement is random mutagenesis. In this 
method, cells are exposed to mutagenic agents which cause lesions in the DNA sequence 
(Ribeiro et al., 2013). Among the various mutations that can occur some can be proven beneficial 
and result in strains with desired traits. 
One agent which is extensively used in random mutagenesis is the inorganic agent ethyl methane 
sulfonate (EMS) (Dahmann, n.d.). The chemical agent interacts with guanine bases and can 
induce the formation of O6 – methylguanine adducts which can form hydrogen bonds with 
thymine instead of cytosine (Dahmann, n.d.). Inability of the replication system to correct the 
changes in the DNA sequence causes GC to AT transitions to occur (Dahmann, n.d.). Common  
mutations caused by EMS treatment are missense and nonsense mutations (Dahmann, n.d.). 
EMS treatment for enhancing protein secretion has been applied in the case of Bacillus 
licheniformis (Ikram-ul-Haq, Ali, Saleem, & Javed, 2009). The strain was subjected to treatment 
with EMS and 28 strains were chosen based on the size of clearing halos in starch-agar plates 
(Ikram-ul-Haq et al., 2009). The activity of 16 strains was lower than the activity of the wild type 
(wild type activity: 73.98±1.76 U/ml/min). The activity of 6 strains was improved by 6% and the 
activity of 5 of them by 17% (Ikram-ul-Haq et al., 2009). The activity of the best secreting strain 
was improved by 28% and with further optimization of the growth conditions (media 
composition, pH, temperature, lactose concentration, volume of the fermentation, size of the 
inoculum, nitrogen sources) it reached a total of 35% (Ikram-ul-Haq et al., 2009).

3.2 Polymerase Chain Reaction 
The method of PCR was used to amplify the promoter sequence gapB, the signal peptide of 
USP45, the plasmid pAMJ399-araH2 and the enzymes β-galactosidase and β-glucosidase. The 
enzyme β-galactosidase is the product of the gene lacZ found in the Lac operon of E. coli. The 
enzyme β-glucosidase originated from Saccharomyces degradans and was cloned in a plasmid 
transformed in a Corynebacterium glutamicum strain. The promoter was the native, for the strain 
L. lactis MG1363, gapB promoter. The signal peptide of USP45, which is the main Sec-
dependent protein in Lactococcus lactis, was also found in the strain L.lactis MG1363. The 
plasmid pAMJ399-araH2 was purified from L.lactis MG1363. 
Prior to the PCR reaction for amplifying β-galactosidase one colony was picked from an agar 
plate using a pipetting tip and was added in an 1,5 ml eppendorf tube containing 30 μL of water. 
The tube was placed in 90 oC for 5 minutes and 1 μL was used as DNA template. The same 
process took place for amplifying the enzyme β-glucosidase, the promoter and the signal peptide.  
The primers arrived in a lyophilized form so before adding them in the designated PCR reaction 
they should be dissolved in water. The registered concentration (in nmoles) of each tube was 
multiplied by 10 and the computed number was the volume of water needed for the creation of a 
100 μM stock solution. After the addition of the appropriate amount of distilled water the tubes 
were left to sit in room temperature for 10 minutes, followed by a 1-minute shaking. A volume 
of the working solution was then diluted 10 times yielding a 10 μM working solution.

3.3 Touchdown PCR 
Instead of performing multiple PCR reactions at different annealing temperatures for finding the 
optimal Tm, modern PCR machines provide a more time saving approach. The starting annealing 
temperature can be set much higher of the estimated Tm of the primers and then gradually 
decrease until it reaches a Tm much lower from the initially estimated one. At some point during 
this gradual decrease the Tm will be optimal allowing the primers to bind and the polymerase to 
function. After the cycles where the temperature is dropping steadily are over the program is set 
to perform a number of cycles, usually at a Tm of 50 oC. The PCR product obtained during the 
cycles where the temperature decreased steadily will serve as template in the rounds with the 
fixed Tm at 50 oC.

3.4 Agarose gel electrophoresis 
Depending on the size of the nucleic acid under investigation the percent of agarose in the gel 
can be adjusted accordingly for better visualization. Agarose gel electrophoresis was utilized for 
the verification of the amplification of the desired DNA sequences from paragraph 3.2 and 3.3. 
The concentration of agarose in the gels was kept steady at 1% w/v, 125 ml of SYBR safe DNA 
gel stain and 1.25 grams of agarose were added in an Erlenmeyer flask which was then 
microwaved in 30 second intervals until the agarose was completely dissolved. The mix was left 
to cool down and it was poured into a plastic mold with the appropriate comb being added. The 
gel was left to sit for 15 minutes and was placed in the electrophoresis chamber. The running 
buffer was TBE 1X which was poured until it covered the surface of the gel. For the size 
verification of the PCR products, a DNA ladder was added in every gel. Prior to loading the 
samples 1 μL of the ladder was mixed with 4 μL water and 1 μL 6X- loading dye  and was added 
to the gel. After the addition of the ladder, 15 μL of every PCR reaction was mixed with 3 μL of 
loading dye and the mix was added in the gel’s wells. The gel was electrophorized at 80 Volts 
for 45 minutes, depending on the migration distance of the samples longer times applied when 
necessary. The gel was then visualized in a gel image station.

The ability of a microorganism to secrete enzymes that are able to degrade starch can be tested 
using the starch agar plate method. In this method the organism under investigation is grown in 
agar plates that contain starch. The organism is left in the optimal temperature to grow overnight 
and then iodine reagent is added in the plate. The iodine reacts with the starch creating a strong 
blue colour while in the absence of starch the colour remains unchanged. After the plate has been 
treated with the reagent the areas with no blue colour are an indication of extracellular enzymes 
that degrade starch. Figure 9 shows how a starch agar plate, from the project, looks after iodine is 
added, the white spots in the plate indicate secretion enzymes that can degrade starch.

Even though iodine is an easy, fast and effective way for screening for starch degrading enzymes 
it has the disadvantage of being destructive for the spatial arrangement of the cells and the cells 
themselves thus the plate must be replicated before the addition of the reagent.  
Replica plating is a technique that enables the creation of secondary agar plates with the same 
colony positioning as the original plate. During replica plating a sterilized paper filter is placed 
firmly on a plastic block either by a rubber band or a plastic holder. After the paper filter is 
stabilized on the plastic block the original agar plate is placed on top of it. A miniscule amount 
of pressure is applied to the plate which is then removed, and a new agar plate is placed on the 
plastic bloc. It is important to mark the orientation of the plate (black vertical line in top of the 
plate, figure 9) so the correct colony will be picked from the original plate. Figure 10 shows the 
plastic bloc used in the project (left), and the same block with a filter paper (Schleicher &

4.1 Improving Heterologous Protein secretion in L.lactis 
In the initial stages of the project the aim was to create strains of L.lactis with the ability to 
secrete the heterologous enzymes β-galactosidase and β-glucosidase which could be subjected to 
screening for improvements. From there, repetitive rounds of adaptive evolution and random 
mutagenesis would take place to further enhance the secretion efficiency of the cells. This would 
be achieved by encapsulation of the cells in water droplets in an oil stream, known as droplet-
based microfluidics. The droplet-based selection would accommodate the identification of the 
best secretors from the general population of mutants. For the detection of β-galactosidase in the 
droplets we would have employed a custom FRET-assay while for the β-glucosidase enzyme, a 
commercial fluorogenic surrogate substrate would have been used.   
To obtain recombinant L.lactis capable of secreting either β-galactosidase and β-glucosidase, a 
source of the genes was needed. The β-galactosidase found in the lacZ gene of E.coli and the β-
glucosidase from S. degradans were chosen as appropriate sources for gene amplification be 
PCR. The next steps involved the digestion of the fragments and the desired DNA vector with 
the appropriate restriction enzymes, ligation of the fragments in the DNA vector and finally 
transformation of the desired host-organism with the recombinant vector. Parts 4.1.1 and 4.1.2 
describe the approaches and efforts for completing the first step of cloning in order to acquire 
strains of L.lactis which secrete the enzymes β-galactosidase and β-glucosidase. 
4.1.1 Constructs for the secretion of β-galactosidase 
In order for the DNA sequence of the enzyme β-galactosidase to be functional inside L.lactis the 
gene would have to be associated with a signal peptide and a promoter, inside a vector. A 
sequence coding for a signal peptide would allow the enzyme to enter the secretory pathway, 
thus the incorporation of one was of paramount importance. The translation of an mRNA is 
universal and is done in a 5’-3’ way with the N-terminal end being the first to arise in a newly 
synthesized peptide chain. The majority of the naturally occurring signal sequences reside in the 
N terminal-end rendering it the first part to arise. In bacteria the transcription and translation take 
place at the same time so the signal peptide is the first to be translated and the protein is set to 
enter the secretory pathway immediately. This is the reason why the sequence coding for the 
signal peptide of USP45 was decided  to be found upstream of the sequence coding for β-
galactosidase. 
The levels of protein secretion also depend on the level of gene expression so one more key 
element for high secretion capacity was to include a robust bacterial promoter. Promoters are 
DNA sequences occurring upstream of coding and non-coding sequences and function by 
controlling the transcription levels of a gene. The chosen promoter gapB naturally controls the 
expression of glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase, which is involved in glycolysis, 
providing intrinsically high levels of expression. This strong house-keeping promoter would 
similarly facilitate high levels of expression for the enzyme β-galactosidase. 
==PAGE:39==

The final sequence envisioned to be inserted in L.lactis comprised therefore of the gapB 
promoter, the signal leader sequence of USP45 and the enzyme β-galactosidase. 
4.1.1.1 1st approach 
The first approach for the creation of the desired sequence was based on overlap PCR. This 
version of PCR allows the fusion of two DNA fragments using two sets of special primers. In 
more detail, the 5’end of the reverse primer of the upstream DNA sequence is complementary to 
the 5΄ end, of the forward primer of the downstream sequence. Initially two separate PCRs take 
place, each for the amplification of the individual DNA fragments resulting in fragments that are 
complementary in their 5’ ends. The amplicons are then added in a new PCR reaction with 
primers that anneal only in the two far ends of the DNA fragments. In this way the overlapping 
parts will create a spliced DNA fragment and the primers in the ends will amplify the whole 
sequence. 
In the current project, the upstream DNA sequence was the promoter and the downstream DNA 
sequence was the gene coding for β-galactosidase. The reverse primer of the promoter was 
designed to have the first 60 bases of the signal peptide and the forward primer of the enzyme-
sequence to have the last 60 bases of the signal peptide. The overlap was found in 39 bases 
between the 5’ end of the primers, in table 2 the overlapping part is shown as bold. The secretion 
efficiency is directly related to the primary sequence of the signal peptide so the sequence 
USP45TM8 was the signal peptide of choice as it was an improved version of the signal peptide 
of Usp45 (Ng & Sarkar, 2013).

4.2 Fluorescence Resonance Energy Transfer 
For most projects, multiple efforts are ongoing simultaneously in a nonlinear time fashion, why 
even though the β-galactosidase cloning was not successful, as described above, its later 
envisioned assay was sought to be developed. The idea for the detection of β-galactosidase in 
droplets was applicable via FRET and we tried to simulate the conditions of a droplet in a 
macroscopic scale. In our approach we tried to use a system of three antibodies, one primary 
antibody and two secondary antibodies as well as a purified version of β-galactosidase, all 
commercially available. The primary antibody is a polyclonal IgY antibody, isolated from 
chicken, and specific against β-galactosidase. The secondary antibodies are polyclonal IgG 
antibodies, isolated from goat, both targeting the primary antibody. The fluorophore AlexaFluor 
488 would serve as the donor and the fluorophore AlexaFluor 594 as the acceptor.   
The main idea behind this set up was that the primary antibodies will bind in various spots on the 
surface of β-galactosidase. The primary antibody is a polyclonal antibody thus recognizing 
multiple epitopes on the surface of the enzyme β-galactosidase. The two secondary antibodies 
would bind the primary antibodies coming spatially close. When the donor would be excited, the 
acceptor would have a chance to use that energy, due to the close proximity (FRET) which 
would only occur if β-galactosidase is present. Consequently, in an experiment where cells are 
tested for their ability to secrete the enzyme β-galactosidase, the cells which secrete the most will 
also yield the highest levels of fluorescence. 
Due to the fact that there wasn’t any previous knowledge on the optimal concentration of the 
components, the ratio between the enzyme and the primary was set at 1:180 (0.8 μL for each 
from the stock solutions), the ratio of the primary to the donor at 1:2 (0.2 μL from stock solution  
of Donor), the ratio of the primary to the acceptor at 1:2 (0.2 μL from stock of Acceptor) and the 
ratio between the donor and the acceptor at 1:1. With this set up all the primary antibodies would 
be occupied by an equal amount of the two secondary antibodies. The assay included eight 
controls in order to study the spectral behaviour of individual components of the system and 
different combinations of them. Based on the results of the run the behaviour of the system was 
studied and further adjustments for the optimization of the assay took place. In the same assay 1 
μL from each sample was diluted 20 times in order to see how the fluorescence would differ 
between the diluted and undiluted samples. The results of the run are depicted in figure 23. 
Figure 24 presents the results of the diluted samples.

4.3 Improving  α-amylase secretion in Bacillus subtilis 
The second part of the project aimed on improving the secretion efficiency of α-amylase in        
B. subtilis. The two approaches chosen for the creation of strains, that could potentially secrete 
higher amounts of the enzyme, were adaptive evolution and random mutagenesis. The 
performance of the strains was validated using their growth rate and the rate of starch 
degradation. 
4.3.1 Mutagenesis

4.3.1.1 Biolector 
The biolector platform was the first choice for studying the growth patterns of the strains of B. 
subtilis treated with the mutagen EMS. The first run could be characterized more as a pilot run 
because two important factors affecting the monitoring of the growth were noticed. The run 
consisted of duplicates for all the strains, a control with cells untreated for EMS and a control 
with only the medium (M17 with 1% starch). In figure 40 it can be seen that the control with 
only media has a relatively high starting Scattered Light Intensity meaning that some 
components in the media could scatter light at some degree. The bacterial growth in the control, 
and almost all the other samples, presented an abnormal behaviour. The intensity of scattered 
light is steady for a period of time, at 7 hours it starts to drop and then at 12 hours it starts to 
increase until it reaches a plateau. From a turbidimetric point of view this decrease in the 
intensity of scattered light means that less light is scattered by the constituents of the sample. 
From a microbiological point of view though and assuming that the cells are the only reason for 
altering the starting detected intensity of light that means that the cells are dying and are being 
lysed. Nonetheless at 12 hours the intensity of scattered light starts to increase meaning that the 
cells are starting to grow. The explanation given to the effect was that the starch couldn’t be 
dissolved in its entirety in water, creating small aggregates that interfered with the light 
illuminating the samples. In appendix vi a bottle with starch aggregates and a biolector plate are 
presented for a more thorough understanding. After a period of time in which the cells adapted in 
the medium they started secreting the exo-enzyme α-amylase. The starting concentration of the 
enzyme was too small compared to the volume of the well thus the number of enzymes breaking 
down the starch was too small. With the passage of time the number of cells increased and so did 
the units of enzyme activity. Increased enzyme secretion meant higher rates of starch degradation 
thus elimination of starch aggregates all translated in less scattering of light. At the lowest point 
of the curve the measured light intensity stems from an equilibrium between the starch that is left 
in the wells, the number of cells and the shaking of the plate. 
The second observation was that two wells which were replicates of the same strain presented an 
incredibly high growth. Figure 40 presents the growth of the cells, namely replicates 1 and 2 
from the fourth round of mutagenesis with 3% EMS. When the plate was examined by eye it was 
observed that a white substance covered the bottom of the wells. The scattered light intensity of 
the two wells presented relatively the same general pattern with all the other strains but from a 
point and on the intensity kept rising until it reached a maximal. One possible explanation for the 
effect can be that the cells formed a biofilm that covered the bottom of the well causing 
increased levels of scattered light. The fact that the same behaviour was noticed in both wells 
could be an indication of mutations that promote biofilm formation giving rise to the substance. 
Turbidimetry can be used as a valuable tool for detecting deviating behaviours in the growth 
patterns of the general population. This observation can also prove how laborious screening 
strains obtained with random mutagenesis can be. Nonetheless regardless of how tempting an

5. Discussion 
5.1 Improving heterologous protein secretion in Lactococcus lactis 
The inability to complete the first step of cloning was a pivotal barrier for the later stages of the 
project.  
5.2 FRET 
The purpose of this sets of experiments was to examine if a protocol based on FRET could be 
implemented in order to detect β-galactosidase in droplets. A major problem encountered in the 
first steps of setting up the process was unwanted detection of the donor in the emission 
wavelength of the acceptor. The problem was solved by transferring the assay in a new 
instrument in which the energy of excitation could be controlled. One more advantage of the new 
instrument was that it provided the option of emission scans. This allowed the study of the 
excitation and emission properties of the chromophores in the same run.  
Due to the fact that a droplet has can have a size of 50 pL the components of the system were 
mainly kept in low concentrations (Chen et al., 2017). One inherent limitation of the set up was 
that the secondary antibodies should be at a certain concentration in order to produce the lowest 
detectable signal ( 2.57*1012 molecules/ 20 μL for the donor and 6.42*1013 for the acceptor). 
These facts slowed down the acquisition of some positive results because all the components 
were used in a semi-optimal concentration. After multiple rounds of varying the components of 
the assay a positive hit was established. When a volume of 9 μL (molecule-ratio of enzyme to 
primary 1:2, donor-acceptor 1:1), from the enzyme-stock solution, was added in the sample a 
signal related to FRET was acquired. Apparently, the concentration of the components in the 
assays should be extremely higher compared to the initially applied, in order for a detectable 
signal to be produced. The signal of the whole complex was slightly higher that the signal 
produced by the combination P-D-A indicating that the ratio of primary to secondaries needs 
exhaustive adjustments in order to increase the signal to noise ratio. Both primary and secondary 
antibodies are polyclonal thus various types of interactions can form affecting the results of the 
assay. Appendix ix presents possible combinations of the components used in the assays.  
Surprisingly, when the concentration of the duplex enzyme-primary was doubled the signal 
wasn’t proportional to the increase. On the contrary, the signal from the triplex primary-donor-
acceptor was almost at the same levels as the whole complex. Unavailability to conduct further 
experiments in order to standardize a protocol with the optimal concentrations of the components 
hinders any insights on how the combination primary - donor - acceptor affects the signal. Based 
on the current results a preliminary protocol was synthesized and it is presented in table 9.   
5.3 Improving  α-amylase secretion in Bacillus subtilis 
The turbidity of a bacterial suspension depends on the number of cells in the suspension but is 
not explicit about the physiological state of the cells as metabolically active, inactive and dead 
cells can scatter light. On the flip side of the coin, cell improvement with the chosen approach 
can be compared to searching for a needle in the haystack as the number of mutations that could 
occur and have a detectable effect on the phenotype of the cells are immense. Consequently, the 
==PAGE:70==
58 
 
screening approaches that could be applied in order to investigate all the possible phenotypic and 
genotypic alterations are also vast. Turbidimetric measurement of the cell growth can at some 
degree be troublesome. For example, differences in the starting inoculum of every culture or 
contaminations can be reasons for statistical incoherence or repetition of the experiment. 
Nonetheless, when practiced with the necessary attention turbidimetry can definitely be proven a 
valuable tool for screening the growth of cells allowing the acquisition of the general growth 
patterns in a fast and reproducible way. Detectable and systematic abnormalities in the growth 
patterns that could potentially be appointed to desired phenotypes can further be screened with 
the appropriate tools and level of detail.  
In the current study turbidimetric monitoring of growth was used in order to estimate the growth 
rates of the strains acquired through adaptive evolution and random mutagenesis. Beneficial 
mutations could be the underline cause for higher growth rates. Each bacterial growth rate was 
supplemented with the corresponding rate of starch degradation in an attempt to study how cell 
growth and α-amylase secretion are related.  
The growth rates of the strains acquired through random mutagenesis didn’t present any 
fundamental differences compared to the untreated strain. By looking at the growth rates of table 
10 and taking into consideration the standard deviations as well, none of the rates deviate 
considerably from the growth rate of the untreated strain. This observation is backed up by the P 
values which are higher than 0.05, meaning that there is no statistical significance among the 
values. The differences in the growth rates are mostly caused by technical rather to robust 
phenotypic adaptations. A similar conclusion can be extracted for the rates of starch degradation 
as in their majority the numbers are in close proximity. This observation is not 100% valid 
though as more repetitions of the procedure should take place in order for an adequate statistical 
treatment of the data. 
The strains acquired through adaptive evolution also didn’t present any divergent growth rate. By 
looking at table 11, it is evident that no growth rate is significantly higher or lower compared to 
the first day. The estimated P values didn’t show any difference in the statistical significance of 
the measurements meaning that the differences mostly exist due to technical errors. The rates of 
starch degradation also didn’t present any gains or losses meaning that enzyme secretion 
remained unaffected. 
Even though the rates of starch degradation didn’t present any information whether beneficial 
mutations could have occurred, the pattern of starch degradation holds some valuable 
information. For all the strains (Appendix viii) during the span of 8 hours it can be noticed that 
the levels of starch start to decrease near the end of the exponential phase. This observation 
could indicate a possible correlation between the phase of growth and the secretion of the 
enzyme. 
A similar observation on the relationship between growth and a-amylase secretion was made in a 
study from (Božić, Ruiz, López-Santín, & Vujčić, 2011). The research group tried to find the 
optimal conditions of growth for Bacillus subtilis IP5832, in order to achieve the highest 
amounts of α-amylase secretion, using shake flasks (Božić et al., 2011). They noticed that 
==PAGE:71==
59 
 
growth and enzyme secretion were correlated with the maximum enzyme activity being at the 
beginning of the stationary phase. Figure 41 presents the growth of the strain and the α-amylase 
activity during growth.  
 
Figure 41: Pattern of growth and α-amylase activity of Bacillus subtilis IP5832.(BM medium, 2% peptone, 0.5% 
starch, 37oC) [Reference: (Božić et al., 2011).] 
One possible explanation behind this correlation of growth and α-amylase secretion could hide in 
the role of SecA, the ATP-depended motor protein required for protein translocation. In a study 
from (Halbedel, Kawai, Breitling, & Hamoen, 2014) it was shown that SecA is required for the 
targeting of the protein DivIVA which functions as a scaffold for other proteins required for cell 
division and sporulation. Thus, SecA indirectly holds an important role in cell division. Taking 
into consideration this fact it can be assumed that during exponential phase the majority of SecA 
is devoted to cell growth resulting in small amounts of secreted α-amylase due to small amount 
of SecA being available. During stationary phase of growth, the cell division starts to decline 
resulting in higher amounts of available SecA thus maximum the detected enzyme activity. The 
role of SecA in protein secretion can be visualized in figure 7. 
6. Conclusion 
6.1 Improving heterologous protein secretion in Lactococcus lactis 
Unfortunately, no valid conclusion can be extracted about the potentiality of L. lactis as a vessel 
for heterologous protein secretion. A total of three different approaches were followed in order to 
create DNA vectors that could express the enzymes β-galactosidase and β-glucosidase with none 
of them being fruitful. Within the context of time and the available tools it was a considerable 
number of potentially attainable approaches. Multiple factors that could hinder PCR 
amplification were tested in an effort to optimize the process but with trace amounts of success 
and without any hints on where the underline problem is. Repetition of the process from scratch 
would be the best approach in order to test the hypothesis. 
6.2 Fluorescence Resonance Energy Transfer 
The fact that the primary and secondary antibodies are polyclonal complicate the measurements 
immensely. More assays and optimization should be performed in order to minimize unwanted 
interactions and achieve a high signal to noise ratio.  
==PAGE:72==
60 
 
6.3 Improving protein secretion in Bacillus subtilis using adaptive evolution and random 
mutagenesis 
The chosen approaches for creating strains with improved protein secretion didn’t yield any 
results in the current project even though they are widely applied for this purpose. In all the cases 
the growth rates and the rates of starch degradation didn’t present any differences that could 
indicate any type of mutations. The secretion of α-amylase is a multifactorial process in which 
various steps needs elucidation rendering the process of strain improvement for this trait 
extremely demanding. The fact that SecA maintains a role in both cell division and protein 
secretion complicates the process of strain improvement even further. Elucidation on the exact 
role of SecA in cell division could be a basis on which protein secretion could be further 
enhanced. 
7. Future perspectives 
7.1 FRET 
Future considerations about the optimization of the protocol and other potential applications of it 
are listed below: 
• Reduction of the assay’s volume for maximizing potential interactions between the 
components of the assay. 
• Conjugation of the two chromophores in the primary antibody. In this way the donor and 
the acceptor will bind the enzyme directly and the need for primary antibody will be 
bypassed. 
• (Prior to the assay) Incubation of each of the two secondaries separately with the primary 
antibody in order to saturate the primary antibodies with secondary antibodies. 
• Include assays in which cells secreting β-galactosidase are grown in the wells of the plate 
and test the fluorescence levels over time using the system. 
• Create a fusion between a desired protein and the part of the primary antibody recognized 
by the secondary antibodies. In this way the system can be used as is in droplets without 
the need of primary antibodies and with smaller chances of false positive fluorescence 
signatures. 
7.2 Improving α-amylase secretion in Bacillus subtilis 
Due to the fact that protein secretion goes through multiple steps it would be optimal to use a 
combination of selective pressure and droplet-based microfluidics in order to improve protein 
secretion. The microorganism could be maintained in exponential phase in a continuous 
fermentation with low amounts of starch. This way cell division will go side by side with the 
need to secrete the enzyme in order to achieve efficient nutrient assimilation. Samples could be 
tested in regular intervals using the advantages droplet-based microfluidics offer.

Standard curve for OD600 - % starch 
In order to correlate correctly the OD600 to the starch concentration of the samples a standard 
curve was constructed. For the standard curve 50 μL of solutions with M17 media and fixed 
concentrations of starch (0, 0.2%, 0.6%, 0.8%, 1%) were mixed with 50 μL of iodine and the 
solution was then diluted 10 times by the addition of 900 μL 5X TBE. The concentration of TBE 
5X was ideal as the solution with the highest amount of starch (1%) had a maximum OD600 value 
of 2,85 and the solution with the lowest amount of starch (0.2 %) an OD600 value of 0.5. This 
range of measurements could be trusted as readings above OD600 =3 are not trustworthy. As 
blanks we used the stock solution of M17 with 0% starch, 50 μL were mixed with 50 μl of iodine 
and then diluted with 900 μL of TBE 5X. The process for the creation of the standard curve was 
repeated 10 times to test the reproducibility of the measurements and estimate the dispersion.
