Abstract
Yellow mealworm larvae Tenebrio Molitor were exposed to diﬀerent levels of Salmonella
Typhimurium and analysed over a 14 days period, to examine if the detected levels were
depended on exposure dose and time. Surface decontaminated larvae, non-surface decon-
taminated larvae and substrate were analysed by qualitative (present/absent testin) and
quantitative detection (enumeration). The levels detected in non-surface decontaminated
larvae and substrate were depended on exposure dose and time. It was observed that meal-
worm larvae may contribute to the removal of Salmonella at a low initial exposure dose.
However, at higher exposure doses, contribute to a stable Salmonella level due to the colo-
nization in the gut and the continuous recontamination of the substrate.
Salmonella were qualitatively detected after heat treatments at at 55, 60 or 75 °C for 1,
2.5 or 5 min, and therefore not suitable for removal of the bacteria.

Introduction
The continually growing world population is estimated to increase to 9 billion in 2050 [Nadeau
et al. (2015)]. This leads to a demand for alternative food solutions, as today’s most wide
spread protein sources such as meat, are unsustainable and resource-intensive.
Livestock production accounts for the largest anthropogenic land use by covering 26 % of
the planet’s ice-free terrestrial surface and accounting for 80 % of emission related to the agri-
cultural food sector. 75-90 % of the energy and resources associated to livestock production
are assumed to go to body maintenance, waste (manure) and byproducts (skin and bones)
[Steinfeld et al. (2006)]. The general consumer have become more conscious, and animal
welfare, health and environmental impact are major factors in the choice of food. [Westhoek
et al. (2014)] estimates that if the current consumption of animal food products in the EU
were reduced by 50 %, the emission of green house gasses would be reduced with up to 40 %
and import of soy beans would decrease with 75 %.
Human entomophagy - the habit of eating insects as food - have for centuries been com-
mon in countries outside Europe, including South America, Mexico, Africa and Asia. The
preferred type of insects around the world is strongly depended on culture, social status,
sensory quality and availability, but have the economic, nutritional and ecological beneﬁts in
common [Grau et al. (2017); Gahukar (2011)].
Mealworm larvae have the advantages of being low in maintenance, as larvae have low
space requirements, a short life cycle compared to other livestock and a high feed (or sub-
strate) conversion eﬃciency [Gahukar (2011); Rumpold and Schlüter (2013)]. Yellow meal-
worm larvae production line is well established commercially, as it is commonly used as feed
for pets as reptiles, ﬁsh and birds. The water footprint per unit of nutritional value for meal-
worm larvae is 23 L/g protein, which is 5 times lower than for beef [Miglietta et al. (2015)].
The environmental impact of producing mealworm larvae is 18 m2/kg edible protein, which is
up to 14 times lower compared to beef Nadeau et al. (2015). The production of substrate for
mealworms accounts for most of the land use; if low value organic side streams or biowaste
were utilized as substrate, the land use is down to 0.04 m2/kg edible protein [Nadeau et al.

(2015)]. Food safety is highly important, and the usage of biowaste as substrate for edible
insects as mealworm larvae may lead to a food safety risk; that human pathogens enter the

The aim of this thesis is to examine if an initial exposure dose of bacteria, time of harvest

is one of the most common cause of food-borne illness. Both qualitative and quantitative
detection methods are used in this thesis, and results are reviewed according to food safety
criteria.
1.1
Yellow mealworm larvae Tenebrio Molitor
Yellow mealworm larvae are the larvae stage of the yellow mealworm beetles (Tenebrio Molitor
Linnaeus). Larvae are the second out of four life stages in the mealworm; egg, larvae, puppae
and adult beetle. White small larvae (3 mm) hatch from eggs and few days later, develop
a yellow chitinous exoskeleton. A grown mealworm larvae is approximately 25-35 mm and
weigh between 15-20 mg after 7-12 weeks [Andersen et al. (2017); Mariod et al. (2017)].

1.1.1
Microbiological proﬁle
The microbiological gut ﬂora in mealworm larvae includes Enterobacteriaceae, Lactic acid
bacteria (LAB) and endospores, where the total viable aerobic count (TAC) ranges between
7.5-8.5 log CFU/g, Enterobacteriaceae = 6.6-7.6 log CFU/g and LAB = 6.7-8.2 log CFU/g
and spores up to 3.5 log CFU/g [Klunder et al. (2012); Stoops et al. (2016); Mancini et al.
(2019); Osimani et al. (2018)].
Other studies have established that boiling or cooking in a water bath at various temper-
ature and time combinations reduced TAC or Enterobacteriaceae load in larvae signiﬁcantly
[Rumpold et al. (2014); Caparros Megido et al. (2018); Megido et al. (2017)].
However,
the previous mentioned studies are focusing on the natural gut ﬂora of mealworm larvae
and not on Salmonella. Mancini et al. (2019), Osimani et al. (2018) and Vandeweyer et al.
(2017) concluded that Salmonella were absent in 25 g larvae in qualitatively detection by
present/absent testing (ISO 6579-1) on fresh larvae.

In the reproduction, the mother culture of adult beetles are laying eggs. The optimal
adult beetle density is 0.84 beetles per cm2 in a box with dimensions 40 × 60 cm. Each adult
beetle lay 300 eggs in their life span (1-3 months) [Andersen et al. (2017)]. The eggs are
separated from the mother culture in the reproduction step and into the production step,
where the eggs are replaced to a 20 × 30 cm box for hatching and growth, and where the age
of the larvae can be estimated. As the larvae grow in size, it may be necessary to change
into a 40 × 60cm box.
The larvae are full grown after 50-70 days, depending on the temperature [Andersen et al.
(2017)]. It is time for the separation step when the ﬁrst pupae start to appear. Larvae, pu-
pae and frass are separated by sieving with a combination of diﬀerent pore sizes. A sieve
with pore size of 4 mm is suitable for separation of wet substrate residues and larvae, and
a sieve with pore size 1.5-2 mm is suitable for removal of frass from the larvae [Andersen
et al. (2017)]. A portion of larvae are relocated to the reproduction step where they grow
to become the mother culture. The frass can be used as fertilizer or for biogas production.
The larvae are euthanized by starving at cold temperatures (15 °C) for 24 h, before being
transferred to -18 °C for at least 48 h [Andersen et al. (2017)].
Density, temperature, relative humidity (RH) and substrate have an impact in the pro-
duction of larvae, especially in the early stages as eggs and smaller larvae. During the ﬁrst
4 weeks, mealworm larvae prefers a high larvae density of 10 larvae/cm2, but as they grow
the optimal density is 4.2 larvae/cm2 [Andersen et al. (2017)]. Mealworm are poikilothermic
and their metabolism change with the temperature. The optimum production temperature
is 25-30 °[Andersen et al. (2017)]. A proper humidity (50-70 %) is crucial for the survival
of especially eggs and younger larvae, due to limited body water, where as older larvae has
a developed exoskeleton, which works as a protective layer [Andersen et al. (2017); Ribeiro
et al. (2018)], so although the RH may eﬀect the growth rate for older larvae, it is not critical
if the optimum temperature is maintained [Ribeiro et al. (2018)]. Larvae have a high feed
conversion eﬃciency (FCE) and consume substrate in a ratio 2:1 (substrate:larvae) in the
developing phase [Andersen et al. (2017)]. The mealworm larvae combustion and FCE de-
pend on protein, fat and carbohydrates in diet [Oonincx et al. (2015); Ribeiro et al. (2018)].

1.1.3
Legislation associated with insects for human consumption
Whole insects and their parts are regarded as novel food since January 2018 according to
Regulation (EU) 2015/2283. Producers should make an application consisting of a detailed
description of the novel food, including its composition, production line, labelling require-
ments and a risk assessment which secures that the ﬁnal product is safe to consume.
The primary production includes the rearing and selling of living larvae and must be
registered as a primary farmer by the competent national authorities of operation [Føde-
varestyrelsen (2017); Internationale Platform of Insects for Food and Feed (IPIFF) (2019)].
However, if a producer wants to do post-rearing processing activities until distribution, such
as euthanizing and heat treatment where the natural state of the primary product change,
the producer must be registered and approved speciﬁcally by the authorities (described as a
"non-primary production" in this section).
Both a primary and non-primary production of mealworm larvae should follow the gen-
eral hygiene requirements for a food production mentioned in Reg. (EC) no. 852/2004 on
food hygiene and Reg. (EC) no. 853/2004 laying down speciﬁc hygiene rules for food of
animal origin [Fødevarestyrelsen (2017)]. A primary production is not required to implement
a Hazard Analysis Critical Control Point (HACCP) for their activities, however it is highly
advised to do so. A non-primary production must comply with Reg. (EC) 853/2004, annex
II (e.g. implement a HACCP) due to it’s registered status as a "food business operator"
[Internationale Platform of Insects for Food and Feed (IPIFF) (2019)].

As mealworm larvae are considered to be livestock, the feed or substrate should follow
Directive (EC) 2002/32 on undesirable substances in animal feed and Reg. (EC) No 767/2009
on the placing on the market and use of feed. Manure have been used as substrate for larvae
[EFSA (2015)], but is prohibited in the EU. Only feed which is ensured to be safe for the
animal, the ﬁnal consumer and the environment must be used as feed or substrate.
Mealworm larvae are considered livestock according to Reg. (EC) No 1069/2009 and by
the Danish Veterinary and Food Administration and is protected by the Animal Protection
Act [Fødevarestyrelsen (2017)]. A non-primary production is therefore additionally required
to have separate slaughter facilities for production and the euthanizing so living and dead
mealworms do not cross paths, as stated in Regulation no. 852/2004 Annex III on speciﬁc
requirements for meat processing. Boiling, freezing and quickly homogenization by crushing
are the approved methods by Danish Veterinary and Food Administration for euthanizing.
The production is also required to have post-harvest production steps such as heat treatment,
packaging and storage facilities, which eliminate the change of re-contamination.
Reg. (EU) no. 1169/2011 on the provision of food information to consumers, that a
product labelling must not mislead the consumer in terms of characteristic and claims. A
placebo study by Broekman et al. (2016) indicated that individuals with crustacean allergy
most likely also will have a mealworm larvae allergy. An allergy warning is therefore highly
advised.
1.2
Salmonella Typhimurium
Salmonella are an enteric gram-negative facultative anaerobic non-spore forming bacteria and
belongs to the Enterobacteriaceae family [Crump and Wain (2016); Percival and Williams
(2013)]. It has peritrichous ﬂagella, which makes it motile [Crump and Wain (2016)]. The
genus covers two main species; Salmonella enterica and Salmonella bongori, where Salmonella
enterica includes bacteria sub-species such as S. Typhi, S. Paratyphi A, B and C, S. Choler-
aesuis, S. Enteritidis and S. Typhimurium.
6
==PAGE:14==

The normenclature of Salmonella sub-species, also called serovars or serotype, are based
on their antigene reaction with speciﬁc antisera and each serovar is classiﬁed by an speciﬁc
antigenic formulae. The nomenclature originates from Kauﬀmann’s serological identiﬁcation
based on primarily thermostable lipopolysaccharide (LPS) in the cell wall (O-antigen), ther-
molabile proteins in the ﬂagella (H-antigen) and in few cases, the Vi antigen [Percival and
Williams (2013); Crump and Wain (2016)]. The structure and combustion of the O-speciﬁc
LPS side chains are unique, which make it possibly to biologically distinguish serovars from
each other [Crump and Wain (2016); Bell and Kyriakides (2009)]. The antigens are system-
atic subdivided with antisera into O-serogroups also called O-antigen pools, where the most
common serogroups are OMA, OMB, OMC, OMD and OME [Crump and Wain (2016)].
The reactions between antigen and antisera are systematically assessed according to the
Kauﬀmann-White scheme [Grimont and Weill (2007)]
Salmonella serovars are classiﬁed into three main groups; Host-restricted serovars such as
S. Typhi, S. Paratyphi A, B, and C, Host-adapted serovars such as S. Choleraesuis and
S. Typhisuis and nontyphoidal serovars (NTS) such as S. Typhimurium and S. Enteritidis
[Crump and Wain (2016)]. S. Typhimurium have positive reaction in the O-serogroup OMA,
following by positive reaction with O-antigens 1,4,[5],12. S. Typhimurium are monophasic,
meaning it only has one H-antigen i in phase 1 [Grimont and Weill (2007)].
The optimal temperatures for Salmonella growth is 35-37 °C, but where most of the
serovars are able to grow at 5-47 °C, and prefers high water activities (0.94-0.99) [Crump and
Wain (2016)]. Most Salmonella serovars (except for S. Paratyphi A and S. Typhi) form lysine
and ornithine decarboxylases and are catalase positive and oxidase negative. Salmonella
ferment glucose, mannitol and sorbotol and are producing gas. They are able to ferment
sucrose, but rarely adonitol and not indole. Most Salmonella (exept for S. arizonae) do not
ferment lactose. They form H2S on triple sugar iron agar and are able to use citrate as carbon
source [Crump and Wain (2016); Bell and Kyriakides (2009)]. Salmonella Typhimurium is a
non-sucrose/lactose fermenting and H2S forming bacteria.

1.2.1
Pathogenicity and epidemiology
Salmonella infections in humans are a signiﬁcant problem, especially in low-income countries
such as Africa, Southeast Asia, and Eastern Europe due to unhygienic conditions and poor
water treatment [Crump and Wain (2016); Ao et al. (2015)], Host-restricted serovars such
as S. Typhi and S. Paratyphi is responsible for over 21 million cases and more than 200,000
deaths related to enteric fever, which is characterized by sustained fever, abnormal pain and
fatal damage on essential organs and nervous system. [Percival and Williams (2013); Roth
(2013); Crump and Wain (2016)]. Host-restricted serovars are not common in nature, and
originates from host-speciﬁc faecal pollution.
NTS, such as S. Typhimurium, is associated with immunosuppression, malaria, or malnu-
trition with 93.5 million cases and 155,000 in mortalities globally per year, where the mortal-
ity range is almost exclusively restricted to developing countries [Crump and Wain (2016)].
Salmonella enters into its host through ingestion of food, milk or drinking water which is
infected with faeces from a infected host. NTS is common in nature and is of zoonotic origin
[Percival and Williams (2013)]. Outbreaks of Salmonella infections are commonly sources
back to meat products, where feacal contamination may occur during the slaughtering and
mincing of meat [Percival and Williams (2013)]. The severity of salmonellosis is depended on
the host (age, medical history) and the exposure dose [Percival and Williams (2013); Crump
and Wain (2016)].
In Denmark and other developed countries, gastroenteritis caused by
Salmonella Typhimurium is the most common type of salmonellosis and the symptoms are
acute diarrhoa, fever and cramps [Percival and Williams (2013); Crump and Wain (2016);
Bell and Kyriakides (2009)]. It is rarely mortal and is treated with ﬂuid and electrolyte
replacement, but do have an impact on import economically and public health [Crump and
Wain (2016)]. The amount of registered Salmonella outbreaks by Statens Serum Institut for
the last decade (including until mid 2019) are shown in Figure 3.

1.2.2
Food safety legislation
Microbiological limits have been established for meat and meat-based products. Regulation
(EC) No 2073/2005 on microbiological criteria for foodstuﬀs includes diﬀerent types of minced
meat, poultry and crustaceans, but does not include safety criteria for insect-based food
products speciﬁcally.
Salmonella is required to be absent in 25 g for minced meat and
meat preparations intended to be eaten raw, absent in 25 g for minced meat and meat
preparations made from poultry meat intended to be eaten cooked, and absent in 25 g for
cooked crustaceans and molluscan shellﬁsh. Salmonella should also be absent in 10 g for
minced meat and meat product intended to be eaten cooked.
ISO 6579 describes methods for detection of Salmonella spp. and is used on food, animal
feed, animal feaces and envorimental samples. It includes four stages; pre-enrichment in a
non-selective liquid medium, selective enrichment, plating on selective agar and veriﬁcation.
For the selective enrichment, meadium Modiﬁed Semi Rappaport-Vassiliadis (MSRV) agar
are intended for detection of motile salmonellae. Salmonella migrate from the inoculation
points and through the semi-solid media, where competing motile gram-positive microorgan-

Lactose/sucrose-fermenting Salmonella strains appear as yellow colonies and agar.(CM0469-
Product details,http://www.oxoid.com/, accessed 2019-06-24). Non-sucrose/lactose ferment-
ing Salmonella such as S. Typhimurium are on Brilliant Green Agar (BGA) plates greyish red
and the surrounding media will be colored phenolred, while sucrose or lactose fermenting re-
sults in yellow to green colored colonies and agar (CM0263-Product details, http://www.oxoid.com/,
accessed 2019-06-24).
Salmonella isolates are serotyped by biochemical or serotypical testing, were antigens
are identiﬁed.
Serotyping by slide agglunation are further described in Section 2.5 and
characteristic of Salmonella Typhimurium is described in Section 1.2.
1.2.3
Antibiotic Resistance of Salmonella Typhimuriumrif
When a microorganism is treated with rifampicin, the rifampicin binds to the active site
on RNA polymerase (RNAP) and inhibit transcription of transcript which is up to three
nucleotides in length. This causes an inhibitory or terminating eﬀect on the microorgan-
ism [Brandis et al. (2015); Crump and Wain (2016)]. When Salmonella Typhimurium are
mutated on the gene rpoB (encodes β-subunit of RNAP), it causes resistance to the an-
tibiotic rifampicin [Brandis et al. (2015, 2012)]. By using a rifampicin resistant Salmonella
Typhimurium strain ("S. Typhrif" for abbrivation) as the strain for spiking, it can be re-
covered from agar plates containing rifampicin where non-rifampicin resistance bacteria are
repressed.

Mealworm larvae

SmartStore clear storage box (50 × 39 × 26 cm).
Rod mortars used for homogenization of larvae were either sterile by autoclave or disinfected
in Virkon S for 10 min and rinsed with autoclaved MilliQ water (Substrate department, DTU

Slide agglunation serotyping were done by placing one droplet of antisera O:4 (REF 23839, SSI

suspect colony from a Narif plate were mixed into the droplet and the reaction between
antisera and antigenes were read according to the Kauﬀmann-White scheme [Grimont and
Weill (2007)]. If the mixed suspension of antisera and colony material remained homogen
and transparent, the reaction were negative (i.e. the examined colony was not veriﬁed to be
the inoculum strain). Visual agglutination in the mixture between antisera O:4 and O:5 and
antigens after 5 sec indicated positive reaction (i.e. the inoculum strain was veriﬁed)

2.7.3
Statistical Analysis
A two way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with a 5 % signiﬁcant level were used to evaluate
if the detection of S. Typhrif were depended on spike level and sampling day, and if there
is variation between the detection across spike level and time. The following null hypothesis
were set:
• H01: There is no diﬀerence in mean of triplicate samples for surface decontaminated
larvae, non-surface decontaminated larvae or substrate across spike levels.
• H02: There is no diﬀerence in mean of triplicate samples for surface decontaminated
larvae, non-surface decontaminated larvae or substrate over time.
• H03: There is no interaction between spike levels and the day, the samples were taken.
An one way ANOVA were used for comparison of detection of S. Typhrif in surface
decontaminated larvae and non surface decontaminated larvae. Same statistical tool were
used in the comparison between substrate from the boxes which were spiked with medium
levels of S. Typhrif (i.e. box 2 with larvae and box 5 without larvae). The null hypothesis
H04 were that there is no diﬀerence in mean between surface decontaminated larvae and
non-surface decontaminated larvae, or between substrate with and without larvae.
The statistical analysis were done with plugin data analysis ANOVA Two-factor with
replication in Microsoft Excel version 16.26.

Discussion
4.1
Detection limit for S. Typhrif
S. Typhrif was quantitatively detectable in 1 g larvae when exposure dose was ≥2.10 ± 0.14
log CFU/g in the ﬁrst replication and ≥1.70 log CFU/g in the second replication. The
exposure doses and their appurtenant recoveries share similar values, indicating that the
individual exposure doses were almost fully recovered.
However, at an exposure dose of 2.01 log CFU/g in the ﬁrst replication, the recovery was
higher for the larvae sample (2.10 ± 0.14 log CFU/g). A higher recovery is also observed
in the second replication where the exposure dose was 1.21 log CFU/g and the appurtenant
recovery was 1.70 log CFU/g.
Each of the S. Typhrif O/N dilutions were each plated as 100 µL, (i.e. detection limit
<2.00 log CFU/g) on TSASB plates, where the larvae samples were plated as both 100 µL
and 1 mL (i.e. detection limit <2.00 log CFU/g and <1.00 log CFU/g respectively). A re-
covery of 1.70 log CFU/g were detected when the detection limit were <1.00 log CFU/g, but
were not detectable (ND), when detection limit was <2.00 log CFU/g. The higher recoveries
could be an indication of a higher detection was possible when the detection limit was lower.
In the qualitative detection by present/absent testing it was observed that S. Typhrif was
present in 10 g larvae when the exposure dose was ≥2.01 log CFU/g in the ﬁrst replication
and ≥1.21 log CFU/g in the second replication, which was expected. In the second replica-
tion, a positive control of both S. Typhrif and S. Typh, and a negative control of only larvae,
were included. In the ﬁrst replication, only a positive control of S. Typhrif was included. The
two positive controls in the second replication were expected to have an evidently positive
reaction on the MSRV plates, which was not the case. The subculturing and later veriﬁcation
of the inoculum strain emphasize the importance of subculturing (whenever possible) onto
indicative media.
It was assumed that if a colony from a NArif plate was veriﬁed as Salmonella Ty-
phimurium according to the Kauﬀmann-White scheme (positive reaction with antigene O:4
36
==PAGE:44==
Technical University of Denmark
Master’s Thesis
and O:5), the analysed strain was assumed to be the S. Typhrif. The positive control of S.
Typh in the second replication was detected on the NA plate but not on the NArif plate,
which was as expected. The veriﬁcation of S. Typh was done on a colony from the NA plate.
The MRSV plate for larvae exposed to 1.01 log CFU/g indicated gemmation around the
inoculations points. The following XLD plate was yellow with opaque colonies, while the
BGA plate was green with green colonies (MRSV, XLD and BGA plates pictured in Figure
10). Based on morphology, the growth could potentially have been E.coli or another lactose-
or sucrose-fermenting microorganism (as mentioned in Section 1.2.2 on Salmonella detection
methods).
In present/absent testing, either both XLD and BGA were used or only XLD plates was
used. Based on the results, BGA seemed to be the most reliable of the two plates, as the
growth reminded the most of Salmonella suspect colonies.
Mooijman et al. (2019) did a validation on ISO 6579-1 where it was concluded that the
method was able to detect a low concentration of Salmonella (1-10 CFU/g) in a sample
with a high concentration of background ﬂora. However, in this study, Salmonella were not
detected when the exposure dose was 1.01 log CFU/g, but were detected when the exposure
dose was 1.21 log CFU/g (second replication). It would have been ideal to include more
replicates and also include an exposure dose of 1 CFU (i.e. 0 log CFU/g) of S. Typhrif for
more comparable results to the Mooijman et al. (2019) study.
4.2
Detection of Salmonella during the exposure experiments
The temperature in the incubator was for both replications in the optimal temperature range
of 25-30 °C. However there was diﬃculties with keeping the humidity in the second replication
(39.59 ± 4.36 %) at the recommended value (50-70 %) [Andersen et al. (2017)]. As stated
in Section 1.1.2 on production conditions, the diﬀerent life stages of yellow mealworm are
depended on the combination of temperature and humidity. It was observed in the studies
Punzo (1980) and Punzo and Mutchmor (1978) that yellow mealworm larvae, pupae and
adult beetles did not experience any stress at 25 °C at low (12 %) or high (98 %) humidity,
where the same conditions resulted in a mortality rate of 2-5 % for younger larvae. The
mortality did however increase for egg, larvae, puppae and adults when the temperatures
37
==PAGE:45==
Technical University of Denmark
Master’s Thesis
were extreme (12 or 35 °C). No noticeable amount of larvae deaths were remarked in the
boxes during the exposure experiment.
It was therefore assumed that the results from the exposure experiment were valid and was
not inﬂuenced by the low humidity, since the temperature was stable in the optimum range
and the larvae used were full grown larvae (52-55 days old). However, a similar observed
humidity was preferred in both replication.
No S. Typhrif was detected in the negative control (box 4) in present/absent testing,
meaning that no cross contamination took place between spiked and non-spiked boxes, which
were placed in the same incubator. No growth was detected on NArif plates for the rinsing
water for the rod mortars, which indicates successful disinfection of equipment and no cross
contamination between larvae samples.
The wet spent grain and the dry ﬂour clumped together, which could be an indication of an
excessive amount of liquid were present. It was considered if the ﬂour absorbed the liquid from
the contaminated spent grain to such an extent that it potentially became inaccessible for
the larvae to ingest the contaminated spent grain. The amount was for the second replication
adjusted down to 1 mL of inoculum dilution, which decreased the clumping slightly.
It was chosen to increase the exposure dose in the second replication for box 1-3 and 5
since the level of S. Typhrif in the ﬁrst replication in box 1 was low or non-detectable. The
increase of exposure dose resulted in that more quantitative data were obtained in the second
replication.
4.2.1
Detection of S. Typhrif in surface decontaminated and non-surface decon-
taminated larvae
By analyzing both surface decontaminated and non-surface decontaminated larvae in parallel,
it was examined if Salmonella were mainly detectable on the larvae surface and/or ingested
by the larvae.
As seen in Table 5, box 1 with a S. Typhrif exposure dose of 1.73 log CFU/g was under
the quantitative detection limit (i.e.
<1.00 log CFU/g) from day 1 onwards in surface

There was a similar pattern between surface
decontaminated and non-surface decontaminated larvae in the ﬁrst replication, as both has a
decreasing level of Salmonella over time, as seen in Table 5. However, one out of three samples
for the non-surface contaminated larvae had a high Salmonella detection when exposure dose
was 5.58 log CFU/g, which could be an indication of a high surface contamination (illustrated
in Figure 11b).
An one way ANOVA was done only on box 3 (i.e. exposure dose 7.42 log CFU/g) data
from the second replication, as more quantitative data were available. The one way ANOVA
results could neither conﬁrmed or denied if there was a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between sur-
face decontaminated and non-surface decontaminated larvae (P = 0.052). The mean level
in non-surface decontaminated larvae was higher (2.74 log CFU/g), compared to detected in
surface decontaminated larvae (1.76 log CFU/g). This could indicate that the decontamina-
tion had removed external bacteria from the larvae. High variance (3.59 log CFU/g) for the
surface decontamination larvae did however indicates a noticeable diversity in the detected
internal bacteria load. This is illustrated in Figure 12a, where a great deviation between
triplicate samples for box 3 are observed on day 1,2 and 7 (last-mentioned was serotyphical
verﬁed). A signiﬁcant diﬀerence between detection in surface decontaminated larvae across
exposure dose and over time (P<0.05) were observed as well. The slightly lower variance for
non-surface decontaminated larvae indicate a higher, but more stable detection of Salmonella.
Crippen and Sheﬃeld (2006) tested various disinfection methods on lesser mealworm
beetle (Alphitobius diaperinus) to dissociate the internal and the external load of bacteria
from each other.
The individual variation between tested beetles was high, even-though
the beetles came from the same environment, which is similar to the results obtained in
this study. Crippen and Sheﬃeld (2006) discuss the possibility that the insuﬃcient surface
decontamination was due to the complex surface oﬀthe beetle, where some areas may have
been inaccessible for the Ethanol (EtOH) to fully disinfect. Wynants et al. (2019) did a 7
days exposure study, where the possibility of transfer of Salmonella from a contaminated
substrate to mealworm larvae were investigated at three diﬀerent exposure doses (2,4 and 7

At an exposure dose of 7 log CFU/g, Wynants et al. (2019) observed a stable level of

the Salmonella detection in surface decontaminated larvae are depended on exposure dose,
which is supported by the ANOVA results (P = 8.41 × 10−5).
The sampling on day 0 was done right after the contaminated spent grain were scattered

4.2.2
Detection of S. Typhrif in substrate with and without larvae
In boxes with larvae where the exposure dose was ≤3.63log CFU/g, the detection over time
of Salmonella in substrate was similar to what was observed for both surface decontaminated
larvae and non-surface decontaminated larvae, which were decreased in level and eventually
decreased to be under the detection limit. The standard deviations between the triplicate
substrate samples was high (1.15-2.17 log CFU/g) compared to the mean of their representa-
tive mean value (0.80-2.49 log CFU/g), which indicate that there is great variation between
the triplicate samples and there may be a correlation between where the samples were taken
in the box and appurtenant detected value. Salmonella were not quantitatively detected in
boxes with larvae from day 1 onwards when exposure dose was ≤3.63 log CFU/g. Presence
of Salmonella were conﬁrmed qualitatively in boxes with exposure dose 3.42-3.63 log CFU/g.
In boxes where the exposure dose was ≥5.42 log CFU/g, the level of Salmonella in sub-
strate with larvae was quantitatively detectable during the 14 days (when not taken day 2 in
the second replication into account), which was opposite when the exposure dose was ≤3.63
log CFU/g. This indicates that there is a diﬀerence in detection across exposure dose. This
was conﬁrmed by the two way ANOVA on the ﬁrst replication (P = 0.81) and the behaviour
observed in Figure 12.c. The detection values were decreasing from day 0 to day 1, but on day
1 onwards the detection were ﬂuctuating between high and low values (illustrated in Figure
11.c and 12.c). One or two out of three samples on day 0, 7 and 14 in box 3 in the second
replication (7.42 log CFU/g) were TNTC, meaning that mean values would potentially have
been signiﬁcantly higher, indicating the same ﬂuctuating behaviour as seen with exposure
dose 5.58-5.42 log CFU/g. The values had been more indicative, if the samples the following
day were 10-fold diluted to reach a plate count in the range of 0-150 colonies.

There was observed a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the detection of Salmonella in sub-

In the boxes without larvae, no new spent grain was added as the extra liquid could
potentially make the environment more favorable for growth of fungi and other unwanted
microorganism. The substrate in boxes with larvae were added new spent grain every sam-
pling day to keep the larvae hydrated. The lack of liquid in the boxes without larvae could
also potentially be the reason for the diﬀerence in Salmonella detection (and potentially
survival) between boxes with and without larvae. The continuation of supplementing new
moist spent grain and/or the excretion of frass could potentially have increased the available
moisture in the box, causing a more suitable environment for the Salmonella to survive in
[Wynants et al. (2019, 2017)].
However the exposure study done by Wynants et al. (2019) did detect a high viable count
of Salmonella in the wheat bran substrate without larvae even after 7 days, which is the
opposite of what was observed in this exposure experiment. This could potentially be due to
the diﬀerent combustion and water activity of substrate used.
The mealworm larvae may be able to digest and break down Salmonella bacteria due
to it’s competitive bacterial community in the guts, which results in decreased detecting of
Salmonella over time in the substrate [Wynants et al. (2017)]. However, if the exposure dose
of Salmonella is high, the presence of larvae may have the opposite eﬀect, where the growth
conditions are favorable as a combination of repeated supplementation of moisture from spent
grain and the colonization in the larvae intestine, resulting in Salmonella contaminated frass.

Heat treatment
The present/absent testing was done on larvae, which were non-surface decontaminated. In
the heat treatment experiment, it was not to be concluded if the microbial load was due to
contamination on the surface or in the internal larvae gut.
As seen in Table 11, S. Typhrif were qualitatively detectable in all samples heat treated
at 55 or 60 °C for 1, 2.5 or 5 min in all four replicates in each of the time-temperature
combination. At 75 °C, no eﬀect was observed after 1 min, while 75 °C for 2.5 and 5 min
show an reducing eﬀect on S. Typhrif in one replicate for each of the two time-temperature
combinations, as no S. Typhrif were detectable in present/absent testing. The two samples
which were tested negative for Salmonella came from the same replicate box in the second
replication. The spike dose in the second replication (7.51 log CFU/g) were 0.54 log CFU/g
lower compared to the spike dose in the ﬁrst replication (8.06 log CFU/g). It can not be
concluded that a concentration diﬀerence of 0.54 log CFU/g between the ﬁrst and second
replication were crucial, as only one of the two replicates for combination 75 °C, 2.5 min and
75 °C, 5 min were Salmonella negative.
In the ﬁrst replication of the heat treatment experiment, both batch samples were heat
treated at the same time, while in the second replication, the heat treatment was done on one
sample at the time. Additionally, in the ﬁrst replication the heat treated larvae samples were
poured into the 50 mL centriﬁgation tube from the top of the stomacher bag. In the second
replication, the heat treated larvae samples were poured from a cut open in the bottom of
the bag. A re-contamination of heat treated larvae in the ﬁrst replication could have resulted
in a Salmonella positive result.
The heating time required to reach a core temperature for one larvae of either 45 or 90
°C was described by Rumpold et al. (2014) to be 30 and 45 s respectively. It was therefore
assumed that the core temperatures (55, 60 or 75 °C) were reached after 1 min of heat
treatment. The Rumpold et al. (2014) study also indicated that a 90 °C water bath resulted
in a approximately 3 log CFU/g reduction (from a microbial load of 7-8 log CFU/g) after
2.5 min, while a heat treatment at 45 °C for up to 15 min had minimal reduction on overall
microbial count. Other studies have shown that a combination of blanching of non-starved

mealworm larvae at 50 °C for 2.5 and 5 min had minimal eﬀect, while 60 °C for 2.5 min
resulted in a signiﬁcant reduction of total aerobic count (TAC), including Enterobacteriaceae
[Mancini et al. (2019)]. After 60 °at 5 min, the Enterobacteriaceae loads were under the
detection limit of 1.0 log CFU/g. This could indicate that the bacterial load after 60 °C for
5 min removed a high amount of bacteria (i.e <1.0 log CFU/g after the heat treatment), but
the heat treatment were insuﬃcient to properly remove all bacteria and a low amount were
still present (1-10 CFU/g).
Although the time duration was elongated to insure the reach of core temperature in the
larvae, a thermal gradient within the 10 g larvae cannot be denied. The temperature may
have not reached the assigned core temperature as the larvae were slightly bulked together
in the stomacher bags (see picture in Appendix D, Figure 16). Rumpold et al. (2014) did
include core heating time of the larvae (30-45 s), before the heat treatment. In the exposure
experiment, the heating of the larvae core were included in the heating times of 1, 2.5 and 5
min. After the heat treatment the larvae were soft and discolored, indicating the larvae were
heat treated to some extent.
Caparros Megido et al. (2018) compared boiling and vacuum cooking with other household
cooking methods on microbial load and nutritional quality. 30 g of frozen mealworm larvae
was either vacuum packed in bags and treated at 74.0±0.5 °C for 60 min, or boiled (100±0.5
°C) for 1 min. TAC were for vacuum cooking 3.9 ± 0.7 log CFU/g and 1.6 ± 0.8 log CFU/g.
However, there is a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in TAC between the two cooking methods, which
could indicate an issue with larvae bulking in bags, leading to a possible thermal gradient in
larvae, where the boiled larvae were not in bags.
According to the Danish Veterinary and Food Administration’s guideline on heat treat-
ment of food, a food should reach a core temperature of at least 75 °C (Hygiejnebekendt-
gørelsen §25, Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet (2017)). However, with the exception that the
food is treated with a proper combination of time and temperature, which the food producer
has proven to reduce the microbial hazards. As a safety food criteria, Salmonella bacteria
are not allowed in minced meat and poultry attended to be eaten raw or cooked (Regulation
(EC) No. 2073/2005).

Conclusion
Human ethomophagy have been a common practice for centuries outside of Europe and could
potentially be the solution to the demand for more sustainable protein source in the future,
due to the increasing world population. Mealworm larvae have a high protein content and
has a already well established production line due to its popularity as feed for pets as reptiles,
ﬁsh and birds. The environmental impart of a mealworm larvae is already 14 times lower
than the impact of a beef production, and could be even lower if organic waste streams are
utilized as substrate.
It was examined if Salmonella Typhimurium, which is one of the most common food
pathogens, were detectable after a one-time exposure dose and if the detected level was
eﬀected by the initial exposure dose and time of harvest. Samples of surface decontaminated
larvae, non-surface decontaminated larvae and substrate/frass were examined for Salmonella
levels by quantitatively detection by plate count (<2.00 log CFU/g or <1.00 log CFU/g)
or qualitatively detection by present/absent testing (ISO 6579-1). Inoculum strain were a
rifampicin resistant Salmonella Typhimurium, where Nutrient agar plates with 50 mg/mL
rifampicin was used for the enumeration.
Salmonella were still present at discontinuation after 14 days in most cases. However, at
exposure dose ≤3.63 log CFU/g, a decrease in detection of Salmonella were observed over
time in surface decontaminated larvae, non-surface decontaminated larvae and substrate
and eventually no Salmonella were detected in most cases. This could potentially be to
the presence of larvae, which breaks down the Salmonella due to the competitive bacterial
community in the guts.
However, At an exposure dose of 5.42 log CFU/g, Salmonella were quantitatively de-
tected in high numbers in the substrate when larvae were present and were still present in
larvae, where the opposite were observed in the substrate without larvae. The combination of
continuous addition of moist spent grain, recontamination due to colonization in the larvae,
could potentially have made the growth conditions favorable for the Salmonella bacteria.
Surface decontaminated larvae were observed to have a lower level (1.76 log CFU/g) of
Salmonella compared to non-surface decontaminated larvae (2.74 log CFU/g), but a high
46

variance between samples (3.59 log CFU/g) were observed compared to non-surface decon-
taminated larvae, which could indicate that the surface decontamination were ineﬀective at
high concentrations.
Heat treatment at 55, 60 or 75 °C for 1 or 2.5 min had no eﬀect in the removal of
Salmonella in 10 g of larvae. A heat treatment at 75 °for 2.5-5 min were eﬀective in one out
of two replicates, since no Salmonella were detected in the qualitative detection. However,
since no of the tested heat treatments were able to remove all Salmonella in all replicates,
the heat treatment are concluded to be insuﬃcient to secure proper food safety.

Future perspectives
Due to the great individual variation between larvae samples, it should be considered to
analyze with a larger sample size, but also do a validation of the decontamination method.
To test if the surface decontamination were suﬃcient it could be considered to do similar to
as described by Crippen et al. (2018) and Crippen and Sheﬃeld (2006), where an external
and internal value for the same beetle were obtained. Beetles were disinfected in 95 % EtOH,
followed by rinsing with sterile destilled water and transferred to 1-1.5 mL of either BPW or
TSA, where the disinfection of the beetle were validated by doing enumeration before and
after the homogenization of the beetle.
Implementation of water activity measurement on substrate and frass before and during
the exposure experiment could give an idea of growth conditions in the substrate/frass in
boxes with and without larvae. Having a positive control with substrate without larvae at
an higher exposure dose than 5.42 log CFU/g could give an indication for if presence of
larvae had an increasing eﬀect on Salmonella detection in substrate/frass as observed in the
exposure experiment, or Salmonella were capable to survive in the substrate without larvae
as observed by Wynants et al. (2019).
A level of Salmonella were detectable in mealworm larvae, especially when the exposure
dose was high. It should be considered if a contamination with Salmonella could potentially
be carried on to future oﬀspring, which was the case in Osimani et al. (2018) who observed
a passing on of pathogens from mother culture to oﬀspring.
More work is required to fully establish a heat treatment which removed the spiked S.
Typhrif, potentially at a temperature of 75 °C. It could be ideal to establish a heat treatment
combination which take the mealworm larvae composition into account and do not lower the
sensory quality and nutritional values of the larvae in that correlation. Sensory aspect in food
product is highly important for consumers. Consumers prefer crispy insects, while cooking
in water or a sealed environment makes the larvae soft and discolored.
