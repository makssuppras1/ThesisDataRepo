DTU Physics
Master of Science
Exploring the eﬀect of nanoparticle size of Cu/Ru catalysts for
selective ammonia oxidation
by Nicolai Hagen
This Master thesis presents the work done in synthesising Cu/Ru cata-
lysts for the selective catalytic oxidation of ammonia to dinitrogen. Ammonia
is a promising candidate for production of clean hydrogen for application in
energy production in fuel cells. To ensure the clean ammonia decomposi-
tion, exhaust ammonia must be eliminated, hence the SCO of ammonia to
dinitrogen. The Ru core, Cu overlayer catalyst has shown promise for this
reaction and is investigated in this work. A polyol method for synthesis is ap-
plied to provide a size-controlled, narrow size-distribution synthesis method.
However, the reducing eﬀect in the polyol method must be improved for
successful, and consistent synthesis. Additionally, the Cu/Ru catalysts are
prepared by incipient wetness impregnation for higher loading catalysts. The
catalysts are characterised by TEM for size determination and by ex-situ and
in-situ XRD for determining constituents as well as crystalline phases of Ru
and Cu. Catalyst performance was tested in a packed bed reactor with a gas
mixture in reaction stoichiometry of the SCO reaction at appropriate tem-
peratures on the parameters of ammonia conversion and nitrogen selectivity.
Further work must be done to improve the polyol synthesis method to be a
size-controlled method for synthesising supported nanoparticulate Ru.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Ammonia (NH3) was ﬁrst synthesised in 1774 by Joseph Priestly, a British
chemist most known for his discovery of oxygen. Ammonia is primarily used
as a fertiliser, or as a compound for the production of other fertilisers (urea,
ammonium nitrate). Industrially, ammonia is most commonly produced by
the Haber-Bosch process which, under high temperatures and pressures, pro-
duces ammonia from atmospheric nitrogen by a reaction with hydrogen as
shown in Equation 1.1.
N2 + 3H2 →2NH3
(1.1)
It is one of the world’s most produced chemicals with a global, yearly
production of 145 million tonnes as of 2014(Apodaca, 2014). Recently, am-
monia has seen interest as a resource for clean production of hydrogen for use
in fuel cells(Choudhary, Sivadinarayana, and Goodman, 2001). As an alter-
native to hydrogen production from steam reforming, which gives unwanted
by-products like COx that poisons the PEM fuel cells, ammonia decomposi-
tion produces COx-free hydrogen with the only co-product being dinitrogen,
which is benign to the fuel cells, as shown in Equation 1.2
2NH3 →N2 + 3H2.
(1.2)
The decomposition is usually done using a metal catalyst and it has been
shown that Ru is one of the best metals for this reaction(Bell and Torrente-
Murciano, 2016). Hydrogen production from ammonia does, however, intro-
duce an issue with the undecomposed ammonia. Ammonia poisons the PEM
fuel cells, albeit at a much slower rate than CO while having a slower recov-
ery rate as well(Gu et al., 2004). Additionally, ammonia is hazardous and
may pose a threat to the environment, especially aquatic life. Therefore it is
necessary to eliminate the undecomposed ammonia from the decomposition.
1.1
Selective Catalytic Oxidation of Ammonia to
Dinitrogen
The most promising technology for elimination of ammonia from exhaust
gases, for example from the ammonia cracking (decomposition) process as
discussed above, is the SCO of ammonia to dinitrogen.
The reaction of
selective oxidation of ammonia to dinitrogen is shown in Equation 1.3.
4NH3 + 3O2 →2N2 + 6H2O.
(1.3)

The unwanted side-products of ammonia oxidation are NO (Equation
1.4), N2O (Equation 1.5) and NO2 (Equation 1.6).
4NH3 + 5O2 →4NO + 6H2O,
(1.4)
2NH3 + 2O2 →N2O + 3H2O,
(1.5)
2NH3 + 2O2 →NO2 + 3H2O.
(1.6)
NO and NO2 are highly polluting and a major cause in formation of
smog, acid rain and tropospheric ozone.
In addition, these NOx species
are hazardous to humans.
N2O, also known as laughing gas or nitrous,
although not hazardous to humans, has recently been shown to be the major
factor in ozone-depleting emissions(Ravishankara, Daniel, and Portmann,
2009). Furthermore, the NOx-gases are impeding performance of the PEM
fuel cells(Faber et al., 2012). Therefore, eliminating NOx-gases is important
and SCO of ammonia is necessary. The temperature dependence of Gibbs
free energy and the equilibrium constant for the reactions in Equations 1.3,
1.4, and 1.5 is shown in Figures 1.1(a) and 1.1(b), respectively.

It is seen that the NOx species are not thermodynamically favoured for
any temperature, meaning that the appearance of these will be because of
reaction kinetics introduced by a catalyst. Certain reaction intermediates
may form on the catalyst surface from dissociative adsorption of reactants,
which are not possible otherwise.
It is apparent that the NOx species can form from the side-reactions, thus
supporting the need for a high selectivity catalyst in addition to the high
conversion.
The catalysts used for ammonia oxidation have been studied
extensively as is seen in the review article(Chmielarz and Jabloska, 2015).
The important attributes for the catalyst is the temperature range in which

The temperature should be low of course since it is expensive and impractical

Chapter 2
Experimental Procedure
This chapter will cover the procedures used for synthesis, characterisation,
and activity measurements of the catalysts.
2.1
Catalyst Synthesis
Two diﬀerent methods are applied for catalyst synthesis. The ﬁrst method
is known as the polyol method, utilising the reducing eﬀect of a polyol and
the catalyst support as the stabilising agent (Chen, Liew, and Li, 2008).
The second method is the wetness impregnation method, impregnating the
support with a metal salt solution followed by a reduction after drying, a
tried and true approach for the synthesis of a range of catalysts here shown
by Postdoc Hugo J. L. Silva, DTU Physics.
2.1.1
Polyol Method
The goal of the polyol method is to obtain a controlled narrow size distribu-
tion of supported nanoparticles. The approach borrows from that of (Chen,
Liew, and Li, 2008; Zawadzki and Okal, 2008). Equipment used:
• 100 mL round-bottom ﬂask.
• heating plate with magnetic stirring.
• reﬂux tube with tubing.
• silicone oil bath.
• magnetic stirring bars.
• ice bath
A sketch of the set-up is shown in Figure 2.1.
Chemicals used in the polyol method:
• Ethylene glycol
• RuCl3 (anhydrate)
• γ-Al2O3
• 0.5/1 M NaNO3

Figure 2.1:
Set-up used for polyol method, consisting of
heater, silicone oil bath, round-bottom ﬂask with magnet stir-
ring bar, and reﬂux tube with tubing to water.
Prior to the synthesis the alumina is prepared by grinding and sieving it
to 105 µm −210 µm. With the appropriately ground alumina at hand the
synthesis starts with adding 50 mL ethylene glycol to the round-bottom ﬂask
and pre-heating the oil bath to the appropriate temperature, in the range
of 160 ◦C to 180 ◦C. A higher temperature during reduction yields smaller
particles with a narrower size distribution (Chen, Liew, and Li, 2008). Next
step is weighing oﬀ1000 mg of alumina and the ruthenium chloride needed
for the given loading. The ruthenium chloride is added to the EG and the
ﬂask and is stirred. Dissolution is incomplete but larger chunks of ruthenium
chloride should be crushed/stirred to a ﬁne powder-like state. The incom-
plete dissolution is possibly due to the precursor being anhydrous.
Then
the alumina is added and immediately the ﬂask is put in the oil bath. The
alumina and the precursor in the EG now has a greyish color, which darkens
in minutes in the oil bath. After approximately 10 minutes the solution is
almost black indicating the reduction of the ruthenium chloride onto the alu-
mina. The solution is kept in the oil bath for another 20 minutes to ensure
complete reduction of the precursor. Following the reduction the solution is
immediately put in an ice bath for swift cooling in minutes. The solution is
then diluted with 1 M NaNO3 to remove the organic solvent(Zawadzki and
Okal, 2008) and is ﬁltered to collect the sample . During ﬁltration the sample
is rinsed with additional NaNO3, and millipore water to wash oﬀremaining
Na and Cl residuals. The sample is then left to dry in ambient conditions
over night, and is now ready for electroless copper plating. A table of the
syntheses characterised and tested is given in Table 2.1. The naming scheme
is S for synthesis and the numeral is sequential for the synthesis. The remain-
ing 6 syntheses are unsuccessful and are not listed, as they are not further
investigated.

2.1.2
Incipient Wetness Impregnation
The incipient wetness impregnation is a tried and true method for synthe-
sising supported metal based catalysts. The impregnation is driven by the
capillary forces of the micro-porous support, ensuring a homogeneous dis-
tribution of precursor. The speciﬁcs of the impregnation of alumina with
ruthenium chloride (hydrate) is shown by, at the time Postdoc, Hugo J. L.
Silva, DTU Physics. The procedure requires only the precursor, support,
Petri dish, small glass beaker, and millipore water. The volume of water
needed for incipiently wetting the support is the pore volume which for γ-
Al2O3 is 0.9 mL/g. More precisely, the volume can be found by incrementally
adding a volume of water to a weighed amount of alumina observing when
the alumina is completely wetted, and is determined to be 1.9 mL/g, twice
the listed volume. The determined volume of water is then used for dissolv-
ing the precursor in a beaker, and is then impregnated in the support in
a Petri dish of appropriate size. Depending on the loading additional wa-
ter may be needed to completely dissolve the precursor resulting in slight
over-wetting of the alumina. Furthermore, a small volume of water is needed
to wash of the remaining precursor solution from the beaker causing a bit
more over-wetting. Over-wetting the support introduces diﬀusion driven im-
pregnation which may promote a more inhomogeneous distribution of the
precursor. The impregnated support is left to dry in ambient conditions over
night. The dried, impregnated support is then reduced in a furnace. This is
done in a small ceramic boat, placed in a quartz tube connected to a gas line
with hydrogen. The quartz tube is placed in a furnace, and the support is
reduced in a pure hydrogen ﬂow of 100 mL/min at 500 ◦C for 2 hours. When
reduction is complete the sample is ready for electroless copper plating. A
table of the samples prepared by IWI is shown in Table 2.2. The naming
scheme is HW for wetness impregnation performed by Hugo J. L. Silva, W1
and W2 for the ﬁrst and second wetness impregnation done.

2.1.3
Electroless Copper Plating
The samples prepared by either the polyol method or by incipient wetness
impregnation are plated with copper by the same procedure, namely electro-
less plating. Electroless plating is employed as it does not require a plating
bath with external power source, and this technique is able to plate diﬀerent
shapes, compared to the more common electroplating. A drawback of the
electroless deposition is that the process is slower and cannot plate in thick
layers. Luckily, only thin overlayer is needed for these catalysts. Not much
literature is available on the electroless deposition of Cu on nanoparticulate
metals. In short strokes, the deposition happens in a solution of reducing
agent for the Cu precursor, an inhibitor to prevent nucleation of Cu on Cu,
and control of the pH value of the solution to ensure that the reduced Cu
ions are attracted to the Ru surface only due to the point of zero charge of
the two colloidal parties (Cu and Ru).
The speciﬁcs of this procedure are those from1, which will be described
in the following. The chemicals used in the deposition are determined for
a 20 wt% Ru catalyst and the solution in which the deposition is done is
as such identical across every deposition. The only thing diﬀerent for the
depositions is the amount of CuSO4, the Cu precursor, which is determined
from the amount of Ru in the catalyst in a linear dependence on Ru mass.

2.2
Catalyst Characterisation
Several techniques are used for characterisation of the prepared catalysts.
For the purpose of obtaining a size distribution images are produced from
Transmission Electron Microscopy. Catalyst constituents are characterised
by X-ray Powder Diﬀraction.
2.2.1
Transmission Electron Microscopy
All imaging is performed on the FEI Tecnai T20 G2 at DTU Cen, un-
der supervision of Associate Professor Christian Damsgaard or PhD Student
Monia Runge Nielsen, both DTU Cen. Identical grids are used for all TEM
imaging, 400 mesh carbon coated Au grids. The electron source is thermionic
LaB6/CeB6. The point resolution is 0.24 nm and a line resolution of 0.14 nm.
The TEM is operated at an acceleration voltage of 200 kV. Pictures are ob-
tained in bright ﬁeld mode. The grids are prepared by dipping them in the
powdered catalyst with a careful, thorough stir. If large grains are visible on
the grid these are knocked oﬀ. This is the easiest way to get some sample on
the grid without requiring to disperse the catalyst in some liquid and placing
droplets to dry on the grid, while still having plenty of sample on the grid.
The grid is then placed in the sample holder (either a Cu single tilt holder or
Be double tilt holder), and inserted in to the microscope. After pumping to
vacuum the sample is ready for imaging. There may be signiﬁcant thermal
drift in the ﬁrst 15-20 minutes after insertion, making it diﬃcult to obtain
any images. Luckily, some alignment procedures are performed in the mean-
time to ensure the best possible resolution. These include beam shift/tilt,
gun tilt/shift and rotation center. When alignments are done, the TEM is
ready for imaging.
2.2.2
X-Ray Powder Diﬀraction
The x-ray patterns are obtained on the PANalytical Empyrean multi-
purpose x-ray diﬀractometer. The diﬀractometer has a Cu x-ray tube with a
Kα1 wavelength of 0.154 059 80 nm. The diﬀractometer is operated in reﬂec-
tion mode under the Bragg-Brentano θ −θ conﬁguration. Attached is a long
diﬀracted beam monochromator. Further speciﬁcations of the diﬀractometer
can be found in Appendix A. The patterns are acquired in ambient condi-
tions (ex-situ) in a cylindrical sample holder of volume 281 mm3 (⊘= 16 mm

2.3
Activity Measurements
The catalyst performance is tested by measuring the activity in the SCO
reaction. This is done in a PBR with output to an FTIR spectrometer. The
FTIR has previously been set-up to measure ammonia and the NOx-species.
The bed is packed with a 250 mg mixture of catalyst and alumina, the ratio
depending on the Ru loading of the catalyst. The catalyst is mixed with
alumina to increase the length of the bed in the reactor, such that it is kept
identical across the samples, as well as to ensure a homogeneous dispersion of
catalyst particles. Dilution of the catalyst also ensures that the conversion is
not too high. This is important to make certain that all of the catalyst in the
bed is used in the reaction and to avoid temperature gradients. Additionally,
mass transport becomes a problem at high conversion as the catalyst particles
are on a micro-porous support where diﬀusion cannot carry the reacted gas
away fast enough.
Besides dilution, moderate temperatures also promote
lower conversion.
The set-up consists of a quartz tube as the reactor with the catalyst bed
contained by a glass wool plug in each end. The reactor is loaded by insertion
of some glass wool, then the support/catalyst mix, then another glass wool
plug. Teﬂon tape is used to ensure tight connections between the reactor and
the tubing. Connections are checked with soap bubbles in an Ar ﬂow, if tight
then with a H2 gas detector in H2 ﬂow. The reactor is placed in a furnace
and connected to the gas tubing. The furnace is plugged with some glass
wool to have the reactor temperature as close to the furnace temperature as
possible. The entire tubing system is heated to 120 ◦C to eliminate adsorption
of water vapour and ammonia to the tubing, which will interfere with the
measurements. The FTIR chamber is heated to 165 ◦C for the same reason,
only at a higher temperature because it can handle it, while the Swagelok
connections in the gas tubing cannot.
The gas lines and the furnace are
controlled by a LabView program, that reads from a script designed for the
reaction parameters in Table 3.1. The FTIR measures continuously during
the entire reaction and is controlled separately.

3.1
Transmission Electron Microscopy
The TEM pictures in this study are all obtained on the FEI Tecnai T20
G2 available at DTU Cen. The pictures are obtained under supervision of ei-
ther Associate Professor Christian Damsgaard or PhD Student Monia Runge
Nielsen, both at DTU Cen. The data will be presented for each sample with
a typical picture of a support particle, concurrently with a qualitative size
distribution of the Cu/Ru nanoparticles. The particle sizes are determined
using ImageJ and its manual measurement tool. The size distribution is
labelled qualitative as the number of particles for each distribution is in the
range of 10 to 70 particles, and as such they are not true distributions, only
a measure for a typical particle size.
For a true distribution the particle
count would need to be over 1 000, which is not that practical working with
TEM, where particles are measured individually and manually. The typical
particle size, in other words the center of the population, for each catalyst
is determined by one of two methods. For the catalysts which have a mea-
sured particle count of 25 or above the sample median, M, is applied. The
reason for this choice is that the median is not as skewed towards outliers as
the arithmetic mean is, and as such it gives the best estimate of the center
of the population. However, for catalysts which have a measured particle
count below 25 the geometric mean, µg, is applied, as it turns out that the
geometric mean of a sample is actually a better estimate of the population
median for such small sample sizes (Sauro and Lewis, 2010). The geometric
mean is determined as

parison of the median to the mean of a population is illustrated in Figure

insensitive to structure changes. The catalyst will be more dependent on size
for active edge sites as the number of edge sites does not increase drastically
with particle size, compared to facet sites. As the number of corner sites is
almost independent of particle size, the catalyst with active corner sites is
largely dependent on the structure, and thus the particle size.

Chapter 4
Conclusion & Outlook
In this work the goal has been to investigate the size eﬀect of nanoparticle
size in Cu/Ru catalysts on selective catalytic oxidation of ammonia. The
SCO is a promising technology in removing exhaust ammonia from ammonia
decomposition, which is a suitable candidate for production of clean hydro-
gen as a clean energy source in PEM fuel cells. The Cu/Ru catalyst has
shown promise as a catalyst for the SCO of ammonia, why it is chosen. The
size eﬀect investigation was unsuccessful partly due to problems with the re-
actor set-up that could not be solved within the time constraints, and partly
due to the issues that the polyol synthesis method proved to have. However,
the catalysts successfully tested showed moderately high nitrogen selectivity
with fair conversion at low temperatures. The only side-product introduced
is N2O and investigation into the speciﬁcs of the reaction kinetics could be
fruitful, for instance active site determination by chemisorption. The cata-
lysts additionally showed high conversion with complete nitrogen selectivity
at moderate temperatures.
The polyol synthesis produced particles in a narrow size distribution, al-
though the particle size is larger than expected. The synthesis showed signs
of loss of Ru in the XRD and TEM characterisation, as well as by requiring
a higher reaction temperature for the SCO than the IWI. The problem may
be incomplete reduction by the EG, and insuﬃcient stabilisation by alumina.
Introduction of an extra reducing agent and/or stabilising agent may improve
the results in the polyol synthesis. Additionally, the method may be evalu-
ated by ICP-MS, as the XRD technique is inadequate for particles of that
size, and where the alumina support is problematic in the TEM imaging.
Incipient wetness impregnation was applied as a high loading synthesis
method as the higher loading attempts in the polyol method proved unsuc-
cessful. However, the particle sizes determined in the IWI catalysts were
smaller than those from the polyol method, which was the opposite of the
expectation. Nevertheless, the IWI catalysts were consistent across the 3
impregnations performed in terms of catalyst performance.
In-situ XRD experiments yielded insight in to the eﬀect of reduction of
the prepared catalysts, as the Ru was determined to easily reduce from an
oxide phase to a metallic phase. The Ru was thereby proven to be oxidised in
ambient conditions. Further in-situ experiments were unfortunately hindered
by a malfunctioning X-ray diﬀractometer. Exploration of the eﬀect of the
reaction conditions on the catalysts by in-situ XRD is a candidate for further
work.
The after-the-fact calculations on Cu overlayer thickness in Section 3.4
shows that the method used is not perfectly reﬁned with respect to acquiring
the desired thickness, as the estimated thickness is greater than the intended,

Cu content is linearly scaled with the Ru loading requires that the particle
size is constant for all Ru loadings, which is shown to not always be the

order to calculate the amount of Cu actually needed in the deposition is an
option. This procedure can be evaluated for instance with ICP-MS, which
additionally can be used to evaluate the true Ru loading in a given sample
