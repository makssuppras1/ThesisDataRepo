Summary
There is a growing number of protocols for the Internet of Things(IoT), and a variety of models
for different formal verification tools. In the past, several researchers have compiled libraries of various
protocols in concise Alice-and-Bob-style descriptions, e.g. the Clark-Jacob and the Avispa libraries [5].
The aim of this thesis is to initiate a formal library focused on IoT protocols. This is helpful for
several reasons. First and foremost, we aim to obtain an overview for many of the concepts that are
widely used in IoT. It also gives a concise description that is not biased by a particular tool. On top of
that it gives a testbench to compare tools in terms of expressiveness and eﬀiciency.
Moreover, it can highlight to the designers of verification tools which features are most needed to
better support the area. As for the researchers, the library provides verified results that can help the
comparison and decision making between IoT protocols.
As a starting point for initiating such library, we carry out study on three existing IoT protocols.
First, we study the 5G-AKA protocol, used for mutual authentication between a subscriber and the
home network. The second protocol we examine is the method variants of the EDHOC protocol for
lightweight IoT devices. The third protocol we are studying is called rTLS or ratchet-TLS, an extension
on TLS 1.3 that is also focused on constrained IoT devices.
For all three of those protocols we provide formal models and formal specifications and then verify
various security properties using the automated verification tool OFMC.
Keywords
IoT protocols, protocol library, 5G-AKA, EDHOC, rTLS, OFMC, formal protocol verification

Resumé
Der er et stigende antal af protokoller for Internet of Things(IoT), og et udvalg af modeller af forskel-
lige faglige verificeringsværktøjer. Adskillige forskere har tidligere udarbejdet biblioteker af forskellige
protokoller gennem kortfattede Alice-and-Bob-lignende beskrivelser, eksempelvis Clark-Jacob og Avispa
bibliotekerne [5].
Formålet med denne afhandling er at påbegynde en formel bibliotek med fokus på IOT protokoller.
Dette er nyttigt for et stort antal af årsager. Først og fremmest bestræber vi os efter at opnå et overblik
over flere af de koncepter der bruges hyppigt gennem IOT. Studiet giver ydermere en kortfattet objektiv
beskrivelse og specifikke værktøjer. Derudover giver det en mulighed for at afprøve og sammenligne
værktøjer, og deres grader af hurtighed og effektivitet.
Dette kan ydermere oplyse designerne af verificeringsværktøjer hvilke egenskaber der er bedst egnede
til at støtte det faglige område. Med hensyn til forskerne, yder bibliotekerne understøttede resultater
der kan være behjælpelig med at drage konklusioner og beslutninger imellem IOT protokoller.
Som en start på at opføre et sådant bibliotek, studerer we the eksisteremde IOT protokoller.
Først studerer vi 5G-AKA protokollen, som bruges til fælles autifikation mellem en subscriber og et
hjemmenetværk.
Den anden protocol vi undersøger er metodevarianter af EDHOC protokollen for
lette IOT enheder. Den tredje protokol ve undersøger hedder rTLS eller ratchet-TLS, hvilket er en
forlængelse af TLS 1.3, som også fokuserer på begrænsede IOT enheder.
For alle tre protokoller, oplyser vi formelle modeller og specifikationer, og derefter en bekræftelse
på flere sikkerhedsområder gennem det automatiske verificeringsværktøj OFMC.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Internet of Things or IoT is an infrastructure of interconnected «things» using advanced services based
on existing and newly developed communication technologies [1]. IoT devices embedded, for instance,
with software or sensors exchange data with each other creating networks with huge scalability. Usually,
low power consumption clients provide information to central servers for further data processing, i.e
mobile phones, smart devices, monitoring sensors etc. The current number of the connected devices in
IoT is huge and it is increasing exponentially every year. Despite the COVID-19 pandemic the market
continues to grow and is estimated to reach 21.5 billion active connections by the year 2025 [26].
For IoT entities to be able to connect and start communication among others within the infrastruc-
ture, they are required to comply with specific communication protocols. A protocol is described as a
set of clearly defined rules, principals and synchronization guidelines for each of the participants in a
system. Many and various protocols have been developed over the years based on the situation and
parameters for different communication systems and scenarios. Since the information transmitted in
any system can be of any type and contain sensitive information it should be obvious that the agreement
on a communication protocol must require that this protocol is strongly secure. Various standards orga-
nizations namely IEEE, ITU-T and 3GPP are just a few examples with responsibility in development,
examination, revision, rework and publication of technical standards, protocols.
As mentioned in the beginning, the rapidly increasing number of active connections between IoT
devices alongside the transmission of very sensitive and confidential information has as a consequence
the attraction of malicious attackers in the field. Such attackers will try to find security flaws and vul-
nerabilities in the protocols in order to use them in their favor to breach into the actual communication
session for private data extraction. In many cases, even if a protocol is just a short specification it can
take years for attackers to find vulnerabilities but then, when this protocol is widely in use if a security
goal fails it is enough to cause a catastrophe.
Security goals, for communication protocols, aim to protect specific data on a communication chan-
nel. A protocol decides on security goals and specifies which parts of the message must be encrypted
and what to include to ensure that the transmission between two or more participants is attack proof.
Of course, not all protocols require the same security level. Various protocols choose on less strict
security goals based on the purpose of use. Security goals such as Confidentiality, Authentication and
Integrity (CIA) are very common goals and essential for data transmission. In the following chapters,
we define and formally prove such goals. With Confidentiality, the protocol assures that the encrypted
messages can be decrypted only by the authorized participants. The Authentication goal ensures that
the communicating participants authenticate each other, meaning that a message is coming from the
person that claims to have sent it. With Integrity, an attacker has no means of modifying information
and replaying a message in order to alter the communication outcome.
Therefore, the need for secure and strongly verified protocols when applied to IoT has become to
prime importance. Even in less sensitive areas, basic authentication and secrecy goals should hold. As
the technology continues to develop, it is critical that the current standards are also getting revised and
even replaced with more advanced and improved ones. Nowadays, verifying totally a newly specified
protocol can be very diﬀicult since the few available verification tools have still limited capabilities in

1.1
Purpose of this thesis
The primary goal for this thesis is to make an effort and provide a library for available IoT protocols.
As mentioned above, there is an increasing number of protocols in the IoT field and it is important and
useful to have a compilation of them in a library that would provide the model and compare related
work and different approaches with formal specifications and last but not least verify the protocols
specifications with a tool. In that way there will be a better and more clear view if an IoT protocol is
secure or has vulnerabilities and whether it is the best suited one for a given system or scenario.
A rich and expanding library for IoT protocols would be very useful not only as a starting point
for researchers but also for people trying to look for an overview or references on the rapidly increasing
number of IoT protocols. It would be useful not only to those who are considering applying a properly
verified protocol to their systems but to people that wish to start formally verifying their own ideas or
existing untested models. The library would be a valuable source of demonstrations and suggestions with
comparisons between different verification tools. Having such a centralized source of IoT protocols will
also give the opportunity for the developers of verification tools to enhance and adapt new techniques in
their tools. Such a library would be the vision, but of course in an MSc thesis only a first step towards
this can be made.
Hence, the question arised was if it’s feasible to provide such a library by studying some available
IoT protocols, compare with related work and then formally verify with a tool defining security goals
and provide our results for validation. Like other various teams did in the past compiling other types of
protocols, the library for IoT would be accessible for the public not only to study the already available
work but enhance with new specifications and provide their own research and proofs.
More specifically, as a starting point in assembling such a library we consider 3 IoT protocols.
Initially, we choose on analysing the 5G Authentication and Key (5G-AKA) protocol for mutual au-
thentication between a home network, a service network and a user-subscriber in 5G communication.
Another IoT protocol we present is the EDHOC protocol for simple low consumption sensors which
only relay environment measurements to a server using different implementations PSK-based, SIG-
based and STAT-based. As for our third protocol, we agreed to examine a new protocol developed
at DTU called rTLS, that enhances TLS 1.3 with a resumption mechanism using the Double-Ratchet
algorithm, targeting mostly limited-power sensor systems.
It is worth mentioning that with a first glance all those 3 protocols have some similarities or common
parameters and those are the client-server architecture, where usually big number of client devices
connect and transmit data to one or more servers on a communication channel-link. This is a common
characteristic for IoT systems meaning that differently designed protocols can be used or combined with
others to various IoT environments.
We wanted the specifications in the library to be technologically independent meaning that they
should not be bound to a specific language or tool. This is indeed a really hard objective to achieve
based on the constrains applied to today’s available tools. For the scope of this master’s thesis we
chose to focus on a high-level formal language AnB, that is OFMC’s main input language. OFMC,
natively, works on a more lower-level language called IF. However, AnB being easier to understand and
develop with but at it’s current state, it cannot provide us with all the necessary means to achieve our
goals and for this case we have to turn towards the lower-level intermediate language IF. On the other

1.2
Contribution
In the following section, we present the contribution and essential steps performed for this thesis.
• Research and study of realated work based on the three IoT protocols in the follwing order. First
the 5G-AKA protocol, second the EDHOC and it’s variants and last but not least the rTLS or
resumption TLS 1.3. The main goal is to have a clear understanding of how the protocols work
and their properties. That helps us make decisions on which language and tool fit our purpose
best.
• For each of the mentioned protocols, we created and then encoded a specification into a formal
language with syntax and semantics. For the protocols 5G-AKA and EDHOC we encoded in AnB
language and for rTLS in IF.
• We specified various goals for each of our specifications in order to achieve as attack proof protocols
as possible. Those goals refer to secrecy and authentication.
• A formal analysis using a tool, namely OFMC. We manage to successfully verify our specifications
with no attacks on our specified goals.
• Discussion between different approaches and versions of our protocol trying to solve issues.
• Presentation and discussion of our results.
• Various challenges had to be tackled. For rTLS we had find ways to formalize loops, states and
extensive search trees. Using the intermediate language IF helped significantly to achieve those
objectives. For 5G-AKA and EDHOC usage of XOR was required and we handled it with AnB’s
partial support.
• For rTLS, we carried out research and testing purely on the Double-Ratchet algorithm and then
applied to the TLS 1.3 model. Abstracted many propertied from the Double-Ratchet and kept
what was essential for rTLS.
• Review, discussion and comparison with other related tool results for the above protocols, from
other researchers.

1.3
Thesis layout
This master’s thesis layout has as follows:
Chapter 2 is dedicated to a general discussion and rough presentation of a very short protocol
example. The main purpose of this chapter is to provide mainly some background of what a specification
in AnB looks like and and provide a quick introduction to the reader and explain the syntax. We also
present the Dolev-Yao model introducing the intruder and lastly verify our AnB protocol with the
OFMC tool.
In Chapter 3 we present our first IoT protocol namely 5G-AKA. First we give the protocol’s intro-
duction and talk about why it is important and what it is meant for. Then, we analyze the protocol’s
execution and based on that we create a formal specification in AnB that we verify using the OFMC
tool.
In Chapter 4, for our second IoT protocol, we study the EDHOC protocol and more specifically we
examine the methods of the protocol that are divided into three major categories: PSK-based, SIG-
based and STAT-based. Next, we present our formal specifications in AnB for the three sub-methods
PSK-PSK, SIG-STAT, STAT-SIG and later we verify them with OFMC.
In Chapter 5, we describe and evaluate the rTLS protocol. Since rTLS is recently introduced we will
be modelling it and formally specify it for the first time. We will be using the intermediate language
IF for our purpose and we verify our security goals using OFMC.
In Chapter 6, we conclude this master’s thesis with a short discussion and we also provide suggestions
about what could have been done as future work.

CHAPTER 2
Preliminaries
In this chapter, we review briefly the most important concepts that are necessary to follow the rest
of the work. Those are a brief explanation of the Dolev-Yao Intruder, a small example of a protocol
specification in AnB language for how to model it and by applying a verification tool to it, namely
OFMC, we are able to detect attacks on this protocol.
OFMC [22] is an automated protocol verification tool that accepts simple languages as input, such as
the formal language AnB [14][17] based on the widely used Alice and Bob notation. The AnB language
has syntax and formal semantics. In the background, AnB is translated to a lower level language called
IF from ”Intermediate Format”.
This intermediate language can be really extensive and very hard
to read caused by the long and complicated syntax for more complicated protocols. In addition we
translate AnB into strands to illustrate the execution of the protocol between the participants together
with an intruder.
For the first half part of this thesis we will focus on the higher level AnB language but later on
we are forced to move to IF in order to use some extra features that at the current state AnB cannot
cover, i.e states and loops.
2.1
The Dolev-Yao Intruder model
The Dolev-Yao model [10], one of the most widely referred models in bibliography for cryptographic
protocols, proposed an intruder in the sense of a perfect attacker in the network.
As attacker or
intruder is referred to a malicious person in the network as attacker or intruder. Some of the main
points suggested in the Dolov-Yao model are:
• Abstraction of cryptographic primitives, meaning that the protocol using those primitives is not
secure.
• The protocols run in a network as in worst case scenario.
• The attacker can freely interrupt the exchange of messages, eavesdrop, inject, replay messages
and switch the order they occur. In other words, we have the worst case scenario that he controls
the network.
• The attacker can be an insider or outsider, passive or active.
• All of the participants have a pair of public-private keys and they know each other’s public keys.
• The attacker can be a genuine participant of the protocol and as such is given the respective part
of the key-infrastructure, i.e., knowing his own public/private key pair. In AnB this is handled
by the knowledge section.
In a real world scenario, a perfect attacker is hard to be achieved. But it is important when it comes
down to formal verification of protocols that we should be aware and try to cover as many attack cases
as possible. The Dolev-Yao model is quite realistic as it covers most reasonable attacks. Even though it
is agnostic to many quantitative aspects of IoT systems, this does not seem so relevant in the examples
we considered.

Modeling a protocol in AnB language
In this section we briefly present, step by step, a short and simple protocol to show and explain
the main points and syntax of AnB. This is a public-key based protocol with objective the mutual
authentication between two communicating participants in the same network. Initially, it was presented
way back, in 1978 and it proved to be susceptible to Man-In-the-Middle attack years later. To verify
this, first, we formally model it in AnB and then we check for attacks with the tool OFMC. This
protocol is known as Needham-Schroeder Public Key protocol NPSK [15].

Please notice how the Agent names and numbers start with uppercase letters. This is the way to define
variables. With lower-case letters we would define constants. Constant and variables of type Agent
are also referred as Roles. We define two Numbers as variables and the Function pk for public-key.
This is basically a one-way function and declared also in lowercase. Having the Roles as variables
denotes that not everybody, including an intruder i, is honest. As we will see in later chapter, we can

essential for the protocol. As our next step is to specify the initial knowledge for each of the type Agent
we declared above. For a valid AnB syntax note that only those of type Agent can have knowledge.
2.2.2
Knowledge
The Role A has in her Knowledge, first of all, her own name and also B’s name, the one she wants
to ”talk” with. On the other hand B only knows his name but not A’s name, yet. Of course, A knows
her public-key described by pk(A) and private-key described by inv(pk(A)). Same applies to B with
pk(B) and inv(pk(B)) respectively. We should make clear at this point that messages encrypted with
someone’s public-key pk() can only be decrypted by the owner of the private-key inv(pk()). Since A
knows B’s name in addition to the one-way function pk(), we can then conclude that she adds pk(B)
to her knowledge, something that B cannot do for pk(A) because he doesn’t know name A initially.
In reality, those public-keys are published by the previously mentioned trusted server based on the
participants’ names but we abstract that for this case. Everything that is not in the knowledge of an
Agent, i.e numbers NA,NB, is newly generated for every instance of the protocol.
2.2.3
Actions
With Actions we explicitly define the messages’ formats. The various messages are transmitted in
a channel of a network applying the Dolev-Yao model, meaning that an intruder might be listening,
replaying or disrupting the exchange of messages. The following syntax A →B means that A sends a
message to B on an non-secure channel. Each Action runs in sequential order. The syntax {m}pk(A)
denotes that message data m are asymmetrically encrypted with A’s public-key and can only be de-
crypted by A the owner of inv(pk(A)), as we already mentioned in the previous paragraph. In our
example protocol we can see three Actions.
Role A initiates the communication after generating a fresh nonce NA. Then, she sends a message
asymmetrically encrypted with B’s public-key pk(B) containing the former’s name A and her nonce NA.
Usually a fresh nonce plays the role of making the message fresh for replay attacks and also adding a
randomness to the encryption making it computationally hard to revert.
When role B receives the message {NA,A}pk(B), he is able to decrypt it for being the owner of
inv(pk(B)) and now he adds to his knowledge A’s name and NA. If everything went smoothly, by the
time B received the message the NA should now be a secret between A and himself. By knowing name
A now he understands with whom he is talking to. Next, he creates his own fresh nonce NB, providing
freshness to the new message, and encrypting both NA and NB asymmetrically with public-key of A,
he then proceeds by sending them to her via the channel. A should receive B’s message and since it
was encypted with her public-key she can also decrypt it adding to her knowledge B’s secret nonce NB.
In the last step of the protocol, A sends back to B the term {NB}pk(B) as an acknowledgment
that she indeed received his sensitive information NB and that means that everything executed as the
protocol specifies.

Autentication goals as B authenticates A on NA means that Bob received nonce NA from Alice

comes in two variants:
Weak authentication means that A started a session and have agreed with B on the nonce NA. As

then B finished exactly those sessions. An attack on strong authentication would be when there are

CHAPTER 3
The 5G-AKA protocol
3.1
An introduction to the 5G-AKA protocol
Today, in 2021, it is estimated that there are approximately 5.1 billion mobile users worldwide[25,
11]. This means that around 65% of the world’s population are owners of a mobile device subscribed
and connected to a mobile network allowing them to make calls, send SMSs, browse the internet on the
go and many more options. In order to make this possible, the network providers offer communications
services to their users, referred to as subscribers, creating a huge network of interconnected devices
that exchange data with each other. The service providers using different types of protocols for the
communication between them and their subscribers can achieve better security and overall higher service
quality with faster speeds for transfer.
The 3GPP organization releases secure specifications and telecommunication standards, protocols
for the evolving mobile systems. With the term ”secure” we mean that, their protocols provide such
mechanisms that aim to uninterrupted operation and guarantee that the subscribers’ data and infor-
mation remain confidential throughout the duration of the communications in the network with their
carriers. Their latest 5G standard, successor to the previous technologies 3G/4G, provides fixes to
many issues from previous standards and achieves significantly faster speeds in communication. In the
first stage of 5G standard, in Release 15, the initial specifications have been presented for commercial
use, followed by a second stage, Release 16, describing more requirements and currently in works the
Release 17 will provide even more enhancements to the standard [2].
Despite the huge contribution and work done for an upgraded standard like 5G-AKA, it is essential
that it has gone through not only proper formalization but also an extensive verification process in
order to answer various security concerns. Even if the available verification tools, today, are limited,
especially for newer and more complicated protocols using recent methods and techniques, it is still
important to try and formally model them and put them through one or more verification tools.
In this chapter, we will go extensively through the above-mentioned process. First we understand
the purpose of the protocol and what it is trying to achieve. Then we describe in detail the architecture
and distinguish all the properties that we need. Once we have a good understanding of our system
and decide on our assumptions and goals, we then implement a formal specification and then apply an
automatic verification tool, namely OFMC. This tool provides support for XOR that as we will see later
it is needed for some encryption parts of the protocol specification. Lastly we will talk about potential
attacks and how we tackle them.
3.1.1
Purpose of 5G-AKA
The protocol 5G-AKA is designed for Authentication and Key Agreement based on an improved
EPS-AKA method used in 4G/LTE. As we describe in the following sections, we are introducing three

participants in the network: the subscriber, the home network or carrier and the intermediate serving
network providing antennas, stations etc.
Whereat, the purpose of 5G-AKA is to achieve mutual
authentication between the subscribers and the home network while the subscriber and the serving

3.1.2
Related work
The 5G-AKA and in general the AKA protocols have already been formally analyzed in the past [7,

formal methods to AKA protocols while specifying sometimes weaker goals for their system and models.
This, understandably, makes many of the published results a bit ambiguous and uncertain since they

models very close to the actual protocols.
As mentioned before, the available tools are not that many today that even if they are regularly
updated and improved they are still found lacking a support of some features that of course would
help with the above-mentioned phenomenon of protocol simplification. Some popular verification tools
among others are OFMC[22], TAMARIN prover[27], ProVerif[4] etc.
This chapter was inspired by the article ”A Formal Analysis of 5G Authentication” [7] that is referred
as the first faithful formalization of 5G-AKA protocol. The article indeed provides an extensive and
notable research on the 5G-AKA with rich analysis of the properties with clear security goals that are
verified with the TAMARIN prover. Following this research, we stay, mainly, in the same protocol
architecture but we create our own formal specification based on our simplifications and use a different
verification tool, OFMC, to evaluate the security goals based on the mentioned article.
Since we, primarily, aim at developing an IoT library, one of the main points is to examine already
available research and compare with our approach in order to create a constructive discussion between
the differently approached results. In our case, we chose to model our specification with AnB language
(based on Alice-and-Bob notation) that is easily accessible. We are using OFMC as our verifier so it
is easier for the reader to study, and additionally, we wanted to test on how our approach performs on
the same protocol compared to other tools i.e the TAMARIN prover.
3.2
The protocol’s architecture
In this section we describe the architecture, all the elements and properties of the protocol. Those
include the participants and their characteristics, the communication channels etc.
There are many opinions regarding the number of participants in 5G-AKA as seen in other related
published papers, i.e in [9] refers to 5G-AKA as a four-party protocol dividing the Home Network the
credential repository in different entities. Another related work [3], although based on 3G networks,
proposed only two participants merging the Serving Network and the Home Network in one single
entity called ”Network”. This over simplification takes away many of the main properties and raises
extra security concerns. In our opinion, a model with three participants: the Subscriber, the Serving
Network and Home Network should be suﬀicient to cover the protocol in reality. In Figure 3.1 we
illustrate all of the participants that are connected in a network.

3.2.1
The participants
More specifically, the first of the entities is the User or Subscriber, since it is a requirement that
they are subscribed to a carrier company in order to have rights and be part of a communication
network. Of course, the Subscriber is a real person that cannot directly encode/decode or transmit
signal information.
To do so, each Subscriber is equipped with a physical device i.e smart phone that supports 5G
technology. Since the Subscriber has an active subscription with a carrier, the latter provides them
with a USIM card. For simplicity reasons, we consider the Subscriber, the smart phone and the SIM as
a single entity called User Equipment (UE). Including the Universal Subscriber’s Identity Module or
USIM is highly important, since this tiny module stores essential information required by the protocol.
First and foremost, it stores an encrypted subscriber’s unique and permanent identifier, referred
to SUPI. Every UE is uniquely identified by their SUPI. In addition to that, and since the USIMs
are provided by the carriers, they contain the respected Home Network’s (HN) public-key pk(HN) and
also an Identifier used for asymmetric encryption. A shared secret key SK between UE and HN is
also included in the module. Lastly, a sequence number(SQN) is shared between UE and HN for
synchronization purposes.
The second participant is the Home Network or HN. The Home Network, just like the UE consists
of many smaller entities such as servers, databases, subscription systems etc. As mentioned above,
the protocol 5G-AKA aims for mutual authentication between HN and UE. The HN stores in their
systems, just like the UE, it’s own public-key pk(HN), the shared secret key SK and the permanent
identifiers SUPIs for each of the subscribers, and also the sequence numbers SQN respectively.
Since we are talking about mobile users, meaning they can constantly change physical locations the
two aforementioned parties need a way to keep a constant and reliable connection with each other. This

is achieved via the third participant in the protocol, the Serving Network or SN. This is the intermediate
that provides antennas and helps creating a communication link between UE and HN. The fact that
the other two aforementioned participants are not directly communicating, makes the authentication
part of the protocol even harder and complex. Looking back in Figure 3.1 the UE directly transmits
to SN and the SN forwards the data to HN and vice-versa.
3.2.2
Channels
Now that we defined who the main actors are, in this small section we will talk about the channels.
If we look closely at the illustration in 3.1 we observe two different communication channels.
The left channel between UE and SN is wireless meaning that the link between them is insecure.
With the term ”insecure” we refer to a channel that an attacker can also exist with possibility to eaves-
drop transmitting messages, inject, replay them and generally attack during the protocol’s execution.
In Chapter 2, section 2.1 we analyzed the Dolev-Yao model and so we will be implying a perfect intruder
during the development of our formal specification in order to achieve strong results.
On the other hand, the right side channel, linking SN with HN is considered to be secure and the
transmission is carried out via cable. In this case, the term ”secure” means that it provides the CIA
triad: confidentiality, integrity and availability.
In Figure 3.1 it is the arrows that an intruder may control, but not the home network or the serving
network itself. Although the latter may be dishonest.
3.2.3
Assumptions
Below, we present the assumptions we considered while studying the architecture of the protocol.
• An intruder can be a legitimate subscriber. In this case, he owns a USIM, have a pre-shared key
with the HN and in general be a normal participant under his real name.
• The intruder can be active (transmit messages) or just passive (eavesdropping).
• The base stations (antennas) can be compromised or an attacker decides to set up his own antennas
(malicious network). That means that an attacker can play the role of SN (malicious) under his
name that other honest participants(UEs, HNs) have zero knowledge about.
• As we mentioned above, we will model the channel between UE and SN as insecure while the
channel between SN and HN as secure. The intruder can move freely in the insecure channel but
cannot break into and act on the secure link.
• Throughout this chapter, HN will be considered as a completely honest actor and we will be
referring to it in the specification with lower-case letters. That means that any of the UEs and
SNs can be assured that an intruder cannot impersonate a Home Network.

OK message is basically a confirmation to the UE that the authentication was verified by hn and the
DATA messages can be essentially SMS, voice, internet data or any kind of information the subscriber
chooses. Both OK and DATA type of messages are encrypted with KSEAF to authenticate the senders.

3.4.1
Types and Knowledge
As we described in the example section of Chapter 2, it is very important to define as type Agent
all of the protocol’s involved participants. So the roles UE, the SN and hn are declared as type Agent.
For the roles UE, and SN we define them as variables with upper-case letters, while the home network
is defined as constant with lower-case letters. We want the home network to behave always as a trusted
participant otherwise we cannot achieve the goals if this party could be dishonest. On the other hand, for
the upper-case variables means that anyone can participate under their real name so does an attacker.
We do not specify an attacker explicitly. An attacker can be either an external intruder in the
communication or a malicious Agent. He can have a legitimate USIM and he plays under the role of
UE. For a serving network with bad intentions (malicious), the attacker plays under the role SN. The
OFMC tool takes care of covering those cases above.
As type Number we define five variables that will be used throughout the protocol.
From the
previous section 3.3 the main variables that are mentioned are the sequence number SQNHN that
is shared between the user and the home network and a random NONCE RAND generated by the
home network. The variable RS is generated by UE and is part of the SUCI that constructs for the
initialization phase. The other two dummy variables OK and SMS aren’t used until the protocol is
about to finish, in order to check for secrecy and authentication goals.
The fact that we didn’t give to anyone’s knowledge, all the variables proposed here are freshly
generated with really high randomness that an intruder cannot have prior knowledge of their values.
The last type we define for this specification is the functions, where basically we specify all of the
function symbols we will be using. Those are all one-way cryptographic functions that in order to
decrypt them, one requires knowledge of the initial input.
Now that we have defined the three roles, we need to specify for each one of them their initial
knowledge. The UE initially knows it’s name, the name hn of the home network that is subscribed
to and it’s public-key pk(hn), it’s own SUPI specified as supi(UE) and the pre-shared key with hn,
sk(UE,hn).To be able to encrypt and compose messages, some functions are also part of the knowledge:
suci, mac, kseaf, ak. It is very important to remember that in initial knowledge only variables of type
Agent are allowed, hence a shared-key declared as a function sk(UE,hn) is acceptable for it includes
only Agents.
For the initial knowledge of SN we have it’s name, UE’s name, hn’s name, the public-key of home
network pk(hn), and the all the related function symbols: xres, res, sha256.
Finally, the hn’s knowledge consists of it’s own name, of course the name of it’s subscriber UE, the
name of serving network SN, the subscribed user’s SUPI and the long-term secret shared key sk(UE,hn),
it’s public-private key pair pk(hn) and inv(pk(hn)) respectively and all the required function symbols:
suci, mac, xres, sha256, kseaf, ak.
In AnB, we can also add restriction rules for the roles in order to avoid one Agent playing the
roles of others well. If not done, it can have impact when verifying with OFMC, resulting to attacks
and we can do this with the keyword ”where”. Hence, in our case we have defined the constrain: where
UE!=SN,SN!=hn,UE!=hn. This prevents all instantiations of the roles where UE, SN and hn are played
by the same agent and is necessary since OFMC detects an attack on authentication without specifying
it.

3.4.2
Actions
Let us continue with the Actions in A.1, a set of rules that describe the messages and how their
exchange is carried out. We distinguish our protocol specification in two phases and we discuss the
steps for each phase. The Actions is a formalization the flow described in 3.3.
On the first message (line 17), the SN initialized the protocol by announcing it’s name to the UE.
The symbol →indicates that the medium is insecure. Also SN →UE means that UE is waiting to
receive the message before proceeding to the next step.
The UE then replies to SN with asymmetrically encrypted message SUCI that includes the SUPI
modelled as supi(UE).
For the next two steps (lines 23-28), note that the channel between SN and hn is defines by the
symbol • →•. This symbol indicates that the channel is secure providing confidentiality and integrity.
An attacker in this channel can replay messages but cannot decrypt them or compose messages. In
other words, the channel behaves as an encryption with a shared key between the two endpoints.
The forth step (line 28) describes the message RAND, AUTN, HXRES∗, KSEAF . In line 29, we
make use of Exclusive-OR that creates encrypted output based on SQN, AK and RAND. The term
AK is specified as a function ak(sk(UE,hn),RAND) that produces a hash output. Same logic applies
for MAC =⇒mac(), XRES =⇒res() , KSEAF =⇒kseaf(). Since HXRES∗is the hash of XRES
that the hn sends to SN, we model it as HXRES∗
=⇒sha256(). The term AUTN is defined as a
concatenation of the XOR with MAC outputs.
Looking at the fifth step (line34), we can see that the SN forwards the message (RAND,AUTN),
received from hn, to UE. Since SN has no knowledge of the long-term key sk(UE,hn) and sequence
number SQNHN, it cannot change or retrieve the input content. On the other hand RAND is displayed
in plain text that anyone can read. That means that in case of a malicious SN it won’t be able to
retrieve the secrets.
In the sixth and seventh steps, the UE simply sends the message with the generated RES∗to SN
and then the SN forwards RES and SUCI back to the hn. RES and SUCI are still secret from SN since
they are encrypted with confidential information sk(UE,hn) and supi(UE) respectively. Again we define
a secure medium for this message transmission.
Once the hn receives the UE’s RES∗and checked that it is indeed on par with his generated
HXRES∗then announces to the SN the subscriber’s unique identifier SUPI. This concludes the step
number eight (line 46).
Now that both UE and SN have established a key, we test it with SN sending a confirmation
message ”OK” to the UE. We do this since we need a sequencial order of messages since there are still
two important steps left we need to keep the protocol execution going.
The last two steps, as we describe in later section, are important because they serve as late au-
thentication fixing an attack. The UE now should be able to send securely data/SMS etc. Hence we
put this in test sending some dummy data called ”SMS” encrypted with the new established secret key
KSEAF including all the participants names. This message goes initially through an insecure channel.
If everything went correctly so far, then the SMS data should be a secret between UE, SN and hn and
an attacker should not be able to inject or retrieve anything from this encrypted message.
This and many other rules, we explicitly specify in the Goals section next.

Strong Authentication goals

The home network must authenticate the serving network on key KSEAF

3.4.3.2
Secrecy goals
Lastly, as one of the most simple and straightforward the secrecy goals clearly indicate what property
should be secret throughout the protocol’s execution and who are the roles that are allowed to know

3.4.4
Challenges across development
Our protocol specification development involved many iterations and improvements. In each itera-
tion, various challenges had to be tackled and fixing identified attacks. Some of the biggest challenges
was finding a way to model SUPI since the role UE is identified by it’s name but SUPI is also a unique
identifier. We ended up using a function supi(UE) that we keep secret initially to the subscriber and
the home netwrok and later announced to the serving network.
Another challenge that we tackled was the requirement for Exclusive-OR for which we used the
AnB language’s support on the algebraic properties of xor.
Last but not least, deciding on most of the security goals provided with significant challenge since
most of the times we had to change many parts of the specification in order to resolve attacks to end
up with what we have now.

3.6
Comparing the results
Concluding our study on the 5G-AKA, we would like to compare our approach and results with
other approaches and similar research on the same protocol. Since our study was initially inspired by
the article ”A Formal Analysis of 5G Authentication” [7] we consider analysing our findings and see
how far or close the two approaches are.
Starting with the two protocol specifications, directly, one might observe that the message flows
and structures are, indeed, very close to each other. The idea for this master thesis is to provide an IoT
library that will provide protocol descriptions that are in the same scope but approached with different
techniques.
Comparing the security goals of the two specifications, we see many similarities since the security
goals in both studies are derived from the 5G documentation [23]. In our case we added some extra
secrecy goals since our solution requires extra steps to provide late authentication. Both studies check on
weak agreement on KSEAF and SUPI and both cover secrecy goals on sequence number SQN, KSEAF ,
SUPI and shared-key SK(UE,hn).
Since it was hard in our approach to implement the sequence numbers mechanisms in AnB, we had to
make the assumption that the anti-replay mechanism just works and only check for weak (non-injective)
authentication. Over all our specification goals stay faithful to the proposals in the 5G documentation.
In both approaches the channels are modelled in the similar way. Both assume that that the channel
between serving network and the subscriber is insecure, where an attacker can act freely doing as they
please. For the channel between serving network and home network, we modelled it as • →• meaning
that the channel acts as an actual encryption between the two roles and an attacker cannot decrypt
but can replay. In the TAMARIN approach the channel is described as completely protected by an
attacker, providing the CIA-triad and replay-protection.
Unlike the other article’s approach developed with the TAMARIN prover[27], our specification was
developed purely in AnB language and was continuously tested through the automatic verification tool
OFMC[22].
Developing in AnB proved to be a straightforward process, since the language itself is very easy
to understand and read. After all, AnB is based on the Alice-and-Bob notation. In our opinion is a
good practice to have protocol specifications in a fairly comprehensible form especially in the case of
compiling a library of protocols.
Other than that, the TAMARIN model seems equivalent to our model, and we agree with their
formalization, as part of our contribution and for constructive discussion, we wanted to check on their
verification result with a different method.

4.1
Introduction
In the recent years, the Internet of Things (IoT) has disrupted and are continuously affecting our
everyday lives for good.
We can find ourselves using IoT in our homes, at our workplace, in the
healthcare and almost everywhere around us. In other words, our lives have become directly dependant
on some sort of IoT system that further impacts our well-being. Thus, there is an emerging need for
strong security solutions for IoT, in order to ensure system stability and protection of our privacy.
Today, there are already many proven solutions focusing on security between interconnected entities
over a computer network. Such is the widely used TLS protocol that has been proven very effective, and
for many applications it provides with CIA-triad (Confidentiality-Integrity-Availability). Such protocols
were designed initially with a web infrastructure in mind. There are various categories of IoT devices
that can be connected in a network at any given time, from computers to smartphones and cameras
all the way to small sensor devices.
In the case of lightweight sensors, that are usually developed
to announce state change measurements to a server wirelessly, it can be ineﬀicient to apply heavy
protocols like TLS. Some of the characteristics of those lightweight devices are their low computational
capabilities, they are very power-constrained with restricted bandwidth.
4.1.1
The EDHOC protocol
In the last few years, a lot of research has been carried out on new protocols and extensions for
establishing secure communication within constrained IoT systems. The EDHOC [18] protocol is a new
approach that was designed purely with IoT in mind, aims to replace TLS for systems of constrained
IoT devices. This protocol is proposed to go along side with the standard protocol OSCORE[24], for
constrained devices. The EDHOC itself is simpler and lighter than TLS but also aims to satisfy all
the necessary security properties such as forward secrecy, authentication with aliveness and consistency,
even for really large scale sensor infrastructures.
More specifically, the EDHOC is a three-way protocol(see Figure 4.1) for authenticated key estab-
lishment between two parties.

consists of the Initiator’s ephemeral Diﬀie-Hellman[20] public key G_X1, a list of cipher suites, a
connection identifier C_I, opaque auxiliary data AD_1 and in a METHOD field with the Initiator’s
proposed method for authentication.

4.1.2
Related work
The EDHOC protocol was firstly introduced in 2016 as an internet draft and it’s first formal anal-
ysis [8] was carried out in 2018 using ProVerif. Since then, the protocol has seen various extensions
and improvements, such as the three new methods as described above. Later, a new formal analysis,
specifically on those methods, was carried out using the TAMARIN prover and was presented in the
article ”Formal Analysis of EDHOC Key Establishment for Constrained IoT Devices”[16]. Our work
was inspired by the latter article, and so we wanted to study it further and present our formal specifica-
tion and verify it using OFMC. In that way, our IoT library is expanded by making available another
verification approach of EDHOC protocol.
4.1.3
Contribution
As part of our library of IoT protocols, we study the various methods of the EDHOC protocol and
present specifications in the formal language AnB, and then verify the security properties using the
OFMC tool. We will be focusing on three method variants: PSK-PSK, SIG-STAT, STAT-SIG. At the
end we discuss our results compared to the ones of the above-mentioned article.
4.2
An analysis of the EDHOC methods
Before we begin to analyze the protocol’s methods, we explain a bit further some of the properties
and formats that are used throughout the analysis. All of the methods are using the three-way schema
between two participant that referred as Initiator(I) and Responder(R), with The Initiator being
always the one that starts the protocol.
The connection identifiers C_I & C_R, the SUITES and METHOD mentioned before, are not
adding extra value since they don’t serve a cryptographic purpose. The auxiliary data is an optional
field, since they serve the purpose of reducing the number of transmitted messages and round trips. As
seen in Figure 4.1, the occurring auxiliary data AD_1 are sent unprotected, but AD_2 and AD_3
are sent protected.
The CRED_I and CRED_R are the credential identifiers for the Initiator (I) and Responder
(R) respectively.
Additionally, EDHOC makes use of the HKDF-based[12] functions HKDF-Extract and HKDF-
Expand in order to derive keys based on some input with salt. The AEAD(K; . ) denotes authen-
ticated encryption using the key K (derived by the shared key). Lastly Sig(I; . ) and Sig(R; . )
denote signatures using the private keys of I and R respectively.
There are a lot more structures that denotes various output hashes from one-way hash functions,
but we describe them at later point when they become relevant. With all that said, we continue with
the methods analysis.

4.3
Specifying formally the methods in AnB
In this section, we present a formal specification for each of the EDHOC methods from the above
analysis. For out approach, we formalize the models using the high-level language AnB that is based
on the Alice-and-Bob notation and will be verified with the tool OFMC[22]. As we mentioned above,
there are some parameters such as C_I/C_R, the list of supported SUITS, and the METHOD field
that does not really serve a cryptographic purpose. In those cases, we choose not to directly include
them in the specifications, in order to make them as simple as possible, but without making them less
secure.
==PAGE:47==
4.3 Specifying formally the methods in AnB
39
4.3.1
Channels
We model our specifications assuming the Dolev-Yao model [10], where a symbolic attacker can
control the entire network. Hence, the attacker can eavesdrop, inject and create messages, but the
attacker cannot break cryptographic primitives (one-way hash functions and encryption functions). All
channels between the Initiator I and the Responder R are considered insecure and we model them as
I →R.
4.3.2

but this has been done in order to make it easily readable and minimize execution time, when parsing the
protocol to our verification tool. An explanation on that decision is that nested one-way hash functions
don’t change the security properties, when constructed properly. In our models above we have HKDF
inside other HKDF and so on. Technically, in OFMC we could just have one main ”wrapping” hash
function that covers all the parameters of the nested functions.

5.1
rTLS for IoT
Today, one of the most widely used Internet protocols is the TLS protocol.
TLS is short form
of Transport Layer Security and it is designed to provide security during communication between the
participating entities connected in a network. The TLS protocol, successor to the now deprecated SSL,
has seen significant development for the last 22 years with TLS version 1.2 being the most used by most
of the internet services. TLS v1.3 [21] being the newest (2018) and improved version of the protocol is
gradually replacing v1.2. It provides, overall, with better security and performance than the previous
variants of the protocol.
In the recent years TLS 1.3 has been gaining popularity in the field of Internet of Things (IoT)
too. It is known that many IoT systems consists of very small and limited power devices i.e sensor
network infrastructures. Since this protocol was mainly designed for computer networks, in order to
be eﬀicient on lightweight devices there is a need for TLS expansions that are more focused towards
IoT. The questions arises whether it is feasible to optimize the protocol for IoT, keeping all the security
properties of TLS 1.3.
A very interesting approach that models such an extension has been proposed by K. Tange et al.
[28], called ”rTLS” that stands for Ratchet TLS. It is a very clever idea that combines the standard
TLS v1.3 protocol with the Double Ratchet algorithm [19]. The protocol describes a new resumption
process between a low-consumption Client device (i.e sensor) and a Server. To make the concept more
clear to the reader we first give a rough idea of the algorithm and the TLS protocol and then explain
how rTLS manages to combine those two implementations.
5.1.1
TLS 1.3
Arguably, one of the biggest innovations that the TLS 1.3 protocol introduced in the communication
between a Client and a Server is the 0-Round-Trip-Time session resumption or 0-RTT. Assuming that
the pair Client-Server have already established a session in the past, with the 0-RTT technique a Client
can immediately send encrypted early data (0-RTT data) while sending request to the Server for the
a session resumption.
One of the main security properties of TLS 1.3 is forward secrecy that was mentioned above. This
”forward secrecy” property assures that if an attacker retrieves knowledge of some session secret key
then he is unable to decrypt anything from previous sessions, making any hacked key completely useless
for other sessions. Forward secrecy and replay protection would be achieved if the Server kept a state for
each of the Client’s session keys and after a session being terminated the Server deleted the session key
==PAGE:52==
5.1 rTLS for IoT
44
entries. That would require an enormous cache for the Server side and the TLS 1.3 protocol assumes
that the Server keeps no such states.
What really happens is that TLS 1.3 utilizes a Session Ticket approach. In a session, both Client
and Server share a PSK(pre-shared key). Instead of storing many keys, the Server keeps one Symmetric
Key secret only to himself but sends a NewSessionTicket to the Client. This NewSessionTicket
has encrypted the PSK with the Server’s Symmetric Key. Then the Server deletes the PSK entry, saving
memory space. The PSK is technically relocated via the ticket on the Client side. See the Figure 5.1.1
below.
When the Client wants to resume later, he sends the previously received NewSessionTicket and
early data encrypted with PSK. Since the Server didn’t store the previous shared key, he first has to
decrypt with his Server Symmetric Key the NewSessionTicket and retrieve the PSK from that. Then
he is able to decrypt the early data from the Client. The actual structure of NewSessionTicket can be
found in the Anppendix B.1.
This approach solves the problem with huge memory on Server side but it doesn’t provide forward
secrecy and replay protection anymore, i.e the Symmetric Key of the Server gets hacked the attacker
can decrypt all other messages, or an attacker can replay the early data message without the Server
being able to know if he already has received it before.

5.1.2
The Double Ratchet algorithm
The Double Ratchet algorithm[19], was initially used in messaging apps (Signal, WhatsApp) but
lately it has attracted attention in the cryptography field. In the simple case where two participants
exchange messages back and forth, the idea to keep one established key to encrypt all the messages does
not guarantee security since with the acquisition of this key by an attacker then the whole sequence
of messaging can be decrypted. The Double Ratchet algorithm provides a solution where, for each
message there is a new established key, making the acquisition of one key pretty useless for the rest of
the messaging sequence.
More specifically, the algorithm utilizes the concept of spinning ratchets (Figure 5.1). We imagine
a ratchet as a one-way hash function, where we parse the input through a function and we end up with
a hash output. The key derivation function used, is called symmetric KDF-ratchet. In the very first
step, the KDF-ratchet takes an input and the Initial Key or the so called Root Key and provides with
a key output. Then, the output key becomes input key to the KDF for the next message, and this
process continues, described as ”spinning the ratchet”. Hence, a chain of KDF keys is being created,
see Figure 5.2.
Figure 5.1: A ratchet repre-
sentation.
Figure 5.2: A KDF chain representation in [19].
The above ratchet implementation ensures forward secrecy since by breaking a key from the chain,
the ratchet doesn’t allow forging any of the previous keys. However, it doesn’t guarantee future secrecy,
since an attacker can use the compromised key to create all the keys forward. To fix this issue, the
==PAGE:54==
5.1 rTLS for IoT
46
algorithm introduces another spinning ratchet but this time by using the Diﬀie-Hellman public-private
keys[20]. This is referred as ”outer”, DH-ratchet.
In every spin of the DH-ratchet, new DH-keys are generated for the inner, KDF-ratchets. The plural
here refers to the two inner ratchets that each participant has, one for sending chain and for receiving
chain. In practice, between a Client and a Server the sending chain of one matches the receiving chain
of the other and vice-versa. When the outer DH-rathcet spins the inner ratchets are being reset. This
technique adds an extra layer of protection to model. Now if an attacker breaks into a key then he can
only manage to decrypt just a few messages since the public-private keys change regularly spinning the
DH-rathet and resetting the inners.
We choose not to go too deep into the analysis of the Double Ratchet algorithm but we wanted to
give a small background, preparing the reader for the next main part. For en extensive study of the
algorithm, we encourage the reader to refer to [19]. We conclude this section providing an illustration
(see Figure 5.3) with ”outer” and ”inner” ratchets.
Figure 5.3: DH & KDF ratchets as seen in [19].

5.1.3
Purpose of the rTLS protocol
Now that we have given a very high level overview of the two main implementations that inspired
the rTLS protocol, we continue by looking at what the new protocol/extension proposes in the field of
constrained IoT devices.
As mentioned before, the TLS protocol was designed with a web infrastructure in mind, hence is not
optimized for transmission overhead dependant and fixed systems. In an IoT system with sensors where
Clients have a fairly static distribution, the Server can have good knowledge of the connected nodes.
As it is described in the publication ”rTLS: Lightweight TLS Session Resumption for Constrained IoT
Devices”[28], The rTLS protocol was designed with stateful Server and Clients in mind. This in fact,
can help reducing the resumption overhead significantly. Also from the article, it is considered that a
typical handshake is in the range of 1-4 kilobytes, that can be very costly in IoT environments.
Introduced with TLS 1.3, the 0-RTT allows transmission of early data on the first fly of resumption
but without replay protection and forward secrecy, it makes the TLS not a very strong protocol solution
for IoT systems.
There are two main goals that the rTLS extension aims to achieve.
• Reduce the resumption overhead dramatically, combining the above-mentioned 0-RTT session
resumption from TLS 1.3 with the ratchet mechanism proposed in Double Ratchet algorithm.
• Ensure strong replay protection and forward secrecy.
This can be highly beneficial, especially, for low-power IoT devices transmitting sensitive and
confidential data to a Server. Next we provide an analysis on the actual rTLS protocol.
5.2
rTLS protocol analysis
The rTLS extension utilizes two ratchets. An outer DH-ratchet, generating Diﬀie-Hellman keys and
one inner symmetric, KDF-ratchet for the session keys. The two types of ratchets work similarly to the
ones described in section 5.1.2, the inner ratchet spins for each message and the outer spins periodically
for new DH keys resetting the inner ratchet with each outer spin. Since TLS 1.3 already provides
methods that allows to integrate the ratchet concept easily, it doesn’t require tremendous modifications
on the protocol itself.
More specifically, if we look at the TLS 0-RTT in depth, it already provides the DH key encryption
method. Then, if we make the Clients and Server stateful we can easily adapt an extra inner KDF-
ratchet. A new PSK Mode is added to the psk_key_exchange_modes of the 0-RTT handshake,
denoting to the Server that the Client requests rTLS resumption mode. A more detailed visualization
of TLS 1.3 initial handshake is presented in Figure 5.4 below.
5.2.1
Initial handshake
After the initial handshake between a Client and Server has been completed, the latter sends a
NewSessionTicket to the former.
The rTLS proposes that in this session ticket a new entry for
Connection ID is added. This is done so that the Server knows which session to resume. Next, both
Client and the Server perform a ratchet initialization and continue. The keys generated during this
initialization match each other and will be used as master secret for resumption later. Note that we
won’t be modelling the initial handshake in our specification since we assume that the two parties have
already established a session in the past and already have a master secret key. Our protocol starts with
the resumption handshake.

Before starting with the actual development of the formal specification from scratch, we first had

5.3.1
Participants and Channels
Typically a sensor system consists of many nodes (clients) connected to a central server. In our
model, for simplicity reasons, we consider that the system includes two participants a Client and a
Server (See Figure 5.7). As described many times before the Clients are small, low-power devices that
measure state changes and report them to the Server for further processing. To preserve power, a Client
might connect with the Server every so often when its state has changed. Each participant keeps an
outer DH-ratchet and and inner symmetric KDF-ratchet accordingly.

Both participants communicate via a wireless medium. As in a real world scenario this channel can
be insecure and susceptible to attacks, meaning that an intruder can listen on the traﬀic, inject and
replay messages and interrupt their transmission. Since we want to prove strong security, we implement
our model with a perfect intruder in mind.

Now that we have defined the landscape, next, we try to model in a detailed descriptive way what

resumption handshake. An important assumption we make here is that the Client and the Server have
already established a session in the past. With that said, both of them have their key chains initialized
and have already established a resumption master secret. The base protocol of rTLS is the standard
TLS 1.3, hence out model is also based on TLS 1.3 modified where needed.

help with the resumption process. The Figures 5.8 & 5.9 below give a representation of the parameters

Whenever the Client sends a message, his inner ratchet spins and a new key is being added to the
chain. The Client ”sends” the first resumption message with the Chain Key, therefore ClientCKs keeps
this sending chain key. Likewise, the Server ”receives” the resumption message, spins the ratchet and
the chain key is added to ServerCKr. It is very important that those two fields are in sync, meaning
that the sending key of the former should match with the receiving chain key of the latter, after they
has spun their ratchets. Then and only then can the messages be decrypt.
To be able to check on which chain number the other participant is, they both keep a counter
increment it every time they spin the inner ratchets. ClientNs and ServerNr store those counter
values respectively. Initially both counters start from 0. For each message in the resumption handshake
both parties announce their counter within their messages. Then, when one side receives a message
they can verify if the other’s key has changed or not.

In the case that the Server receives a key_share he knows that he should prepare to spin his DH-
ratchet. Mind that the message he just received are still encrypted in a way that in order to be able
decrypt them, first, he has to recreate the same encryption keys. This is because the Client used his

So far, the Server has in his knowledge his current private-public DH-key pair Y and gY. In the

Described above, this early_secret is the hash output of HKDF_Extract(ClientCKs). All it’s left to

private DH key Y with the Client’s DH public key gXX.
exp(Y, gXX) = gXX·Y

don’t really affect the decryption process and can be abstracted.

5.4
IF specification
Now that we have the rTLS model, our next step is to translate it into a formal specification that
we can parse into a tool for formal verification. In the rest of this master’s thesis we presented our
other formal specifications developed in AnB language. Initially, we tried to figure out if it was still
possible to go again with the same language, since we already have a nice operational verification tool
like OFMC that accepts protocols written in AnB. Unfortunately, this high level language is lacking
of a very important functionality for handling states, that it is crucial in order to develop a faithful
version of our rTLS model we have just described.
On the other hand, internally OFMC uses an intermediate language IF (AVISPA Intermediate-
Format) and this is a much more general language that we can directly use for specifying, but at the
price of a much harder to read specification, comparable to other rewrite-based formalizations like
Tamarin. So we chose to proceed by directly formally specify each of our model’s notations into IF
syntax. This process is relatively tough to do since we couldn’t use any syntax highlighters to help with
development and in this format everything has to be declared as clearly and it can easily get extensive
and overwhelmingly hard to debug and read. The final rTLS specification in IF is approximately 663
lines of code. For convenience, the reader can find the full specification in the appendix B.2. Since we
want to keep this chapter comprehensive we choose to only point out key rules and syntax that we find
more interesting and describe better our model 5.3.2. The reader is highly encouraged to refer to the
well written article ”A symbolic model checker for security protocols” [6] first, for a better understanding
on the IF language. Without further delay we continue with the specification’s analysis.

We can easily see that the first state denotes an attack on secrecy.
More specifically, the fact

The rule AnB_A/=i specifies that the intruder is not in the role of AnB_A.
We have specified many secrecy states throughout the specification so that we are ensured that
no messages are vulnerable and their encryption is suﬀicient. We applies the goal on each MOUT
and MIN respectively. For instance, in line 175, the Client has sent the initial resumption handshake
message to the server with some early application data that we need to ensure they won’t and up on
the hands of an attacker. The fact secret(MOUT,S) indicates that we want the message MOUT to
remain secret only between the Client (C) and Server (S).
We also considered about forward-secrecy as a security property. However this is a diﬀicult task
to do since it requires that one could ”retract” secrecy of particular terms. This can be expressed in
IF, but it is something that would probably require more work. That leave space for future work and
improvement on our specification.
The other security property we are want to verify on is strong agreement (5.4 line 648). This is
implemented using the events witness & request. The witness event is added after the creation of a
message like so:
=[exists MOUT]=>
witness(C, S, pCSMOUT, MOUT).
The above translates to the following. The participants on roles C and S agree on MOUT when a
session has opened between them. In order to achieve strong authentication, there should be a request
event on the Server’s side that receives the message MOUT believing that it originates by the Client in
this session. In the specification’s line 300, there exists such a request event:
request(S, C, pCSMOUT, MOUT, SID).
We put witness-request events for every outgoing message to ensure that no attacks on authentication
occur. With this we finalize our specification analysis.

test it with a formal verification tool. The tool OFMC proved to be very effective. We talked about how
we intentionally chose to implement the specification with 2 sessions and also mentioned implementing
a looping counter referring to how many times we want the resumption protocol to run. We tried to
increase the depth of the looping, but it turned out that the OFMC’s execution time increased dramat-
ically even for just one extra loop. Of course the tool might execute slightly faster in other computer
machines and environments in opposition to the ones we tested in on while developing. Nevertheless, by
parsing our IF specification to the OFMC tool with the command ofmc rTLS.if --untyped we managed
to get an Attack-proof(based on our goals) specification. For convenience, the tools output is illustrated

5.6
rTLS Challenges and Conclusion
Modelling and developing the formal specification for rTLS was both interesting and very challenging
task to work on as part of this master’s thesis. There were many iteration on the specification and
the model in order to reach it’s current state. For a better understanding on the subject initially, a
lot of study was carried out on the standard TLS 1.3, the Double Ratchet Algorithm and the rTLS
description.
One of the biggest challenges was, definitely the development of the formal IF specification based
on the model 5.3.2. The main reason is that there was knowledge on AnB language but very little on
it’s lower level Intermediate Format. Of course, the specification is open for discussion and constructive
criticism.
Another challenge was the fact that there is not an way to help the development in IF
language, no fancy syntax highlighters or editor extensions. So, we had to do it the slow and hard way
of plain text paying extra attention on every little detail.
Of course, the idea of a small compiler that translates notation to IF came up later to help us with
this but since we were time constrained, this implementation wasn’t able to be happen. However that
would be a really nice addition in the project as future work and it would give extra value using such
a tool when developing protocols in IF.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In this master thesis we tried to initiate a formal library of IoT protocols. Our vision is to have an
extensive and continuously developing library of protocols that can serve as a valuable tool, not only for
researchers and experts in the IoT area, but also become a starting point for people that just started
exploring the field. The library would be a compilation of protocol overviews, models, specifications in
different formal languages, and analyze results from various verification tools. In that way the available
information will be technologically independent and it will be easily accessible by anyone. Of course
compiling such an extensive library requires a lot of work since the field of IoT is rapidly expanding,
and there are already many protocol solutions to start working on.
In the context of this thesis, we initiated the process of creating a formal library for IoT by including
three IoT protocols namely 5G-AKA, EDHOC and rTLS. Not only did we present an overview on those
protocols but we provided detailed models. Furthermore for each one of them we analyzed our own
specifications and explained our approach.
For the 5G-AKA, we analyzed the protocol’s execution flow between a subscriber, the intermediate
serving network and home network and then we provided a specification in AnB language. Based on
our proposed security properties we verified it successfully for two sessions using the verification tool
OFMC.
Next, we broke down the EDHOC protocol to smaller protocols based on the key authentication
methods they use. As of today the EDHOC protocol supports five method combinations. For three of the
methods PSK-PSK, SIG-STAT and STAT-SIG we provided and formally verified separate specifications
in AnB with no detected attacks.
As for our third protocol addition to our formal library, we studied the newly developed protocol
rTLS and gave the first formal model describing the message structures and exchange flow. Before
started developing the formal specification we identified that we would require to use states and loops
so we chose to work using the IF language that proved very effective achieving the requirements. By
completing the specification we used OFMC to verify secrecy and authentication goals and the protocol
proved to be attack proof based on the tool’s output.
In conclusion, during this master thesis we learned how to carry out study and research not only on
already verified protocols but we had the opportunity to create the very first model and provide with
a formal specification for rTLS. Additionally, by working in such big project it gave us the opportunity
to expand our knowledge in AnB and learned how to write a specification completely in IF. By making
OFMC our main verification tool for our specifications, proved a good way to better understand how
to use such tools and how to use them eﬀiciently. At the end, we consider this first step towards, a rich
collection of IoT protocols, a success.
6.1
Future work
In this small section we give some suggestions and directions for what can be done as further work.
Of course the goal of this library is to become a big collection of IoT protocol specifications. There is a
==PAGE:79==
6.1 Future work
71
rapidly increasing number of protocols focused in IoT, and that make them a perfect addition to this
collection. Thus one could always formally specify, and automatically analyze any of the available or
new protocols.
About our 5G-AKA specification, as mentioned in chapter 3 the protocol is implemented on a
sequence number mechanism in order to achieve synchronization and add freshness for the messages.
Since we didn’t implement directly such a mechanism but we just assumed that the replay protection
is in effect, a proposal for future work would be to try expanding our specification using states in IF,
and check in what way that approach would perform better in comparison to our current one in AnB.
Regarding the EDHOC protocol, during this master thesis we were able to model and verify for
three of the five methods supported by the protocol. The methods that are missing from our analysis
(SIG-SIG, STAT-STAT) does not greatly differ from the ones we already specified, but in order to have
a complete study of the EDHOC protocol in AnB, it would be beneficial to have those two formally
analyzed by OFMC.
In the third case, since we had to start from scratch modelling the rTLS protocol and then based on
that we had to implement a formal specification, we only managed to verify on authentication and some
secrecy goals on encrypted data. For future work, it would be valuable to verify for forward-secrecy as
well, something that we couldn’t complete due to time constrains.
Last but not least, for all of our specifications more security goals can be added in order to enhance
the security.
