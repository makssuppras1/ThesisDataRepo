Abstract
B&W Vølund is one of the main suppliers of incinerator plants providing both a nal waste to
energy solution and the following maintenance of the incinerator plant. In the year 2001 they
acquired a new grate technology- The Dynagrate. This new technology provides a more robust
solution for the incinerator plants with the purpose of reducing maintenance that cost expensive
down time, however after a short period aws in the grates around the more exposed areas of
the combustion chamber began to unveil, which lead to cooling water from inside the grates to
leak through the aws. To analyse these aws two grates has been received that both has failed
during operation. Both the location and the size of these aws are at key points documented.
To quantify the stresses applied on the grate as a result of the increased operational temperature
the thermal stresses are analysed through a nite element model in Abaqus for dierent operating
conditions. To investigate what has lead to the failure of the grate the aws documented are
compared with the dierent thermal FEM models, where an equality between the location of
the fractures and the highest stress intensities is seen. To determine if the quantity of these
stresses are sucient to form these fractures, an assessment of the fractures are made through
fracture mechanics, including fatigue failure.
The result of this analysis concluded the fractures documented from the submitted grates in all
but one case were deemed unacceptable for the structural integrity. The applied stresses for all
but one case could have caused the formation of the fractures which has lead to the failure of
the grate.

Introduction
In Denmark Vølund is one of the main suppliers of incinerator plants providing both a nal waste
to energy solution through a combustion process of waste products and the following mainte-
nance of the plants. Vølund A/S was founded in 1898 and then in the year 2000 integrated
with Babcock & Wilcox. In the year 2001 they acquired the Dynagrate technology from Bruun
& Sørensen. One of the main purposes of this new technology was to achieve a more robust
solution that required less down time maintenance for the incinerator plant.
During operation the combustion process occurs on top of the grates which thereby applies sup-
port and movement of the waste material through the incinerator plant. According to B&W
Vølund's own statement "The DynaGrate R⃝provides excellent performance with low mainte-
nance costs due to a design technology that causes little wear and tear"1.
This is in general true for the grate, however, a small amount of the grate in the more exposed
areas of the incinerator plant, where the load from heat and ue gas is highest began to reveal
problems after a short period of time. E.g. a network of micro cracks emerged at the surface
of the grate which eventually reached a sucient length so that a small amount of the cooling
water inside the grate could leak through the crack.
To better understand what has lead to these failures a thermomechanical analysis of the grate
both during operation and shut down will be conducted for dierent conditions. I have received
two grates that both have failed during operation where the failure of these grates will be
analysed. The thermal model will then be compared with the failures through fracture mechanics
to investigate if the applied stresses from the model could have caused the failures.

Corrosion process
In general a corrosion process is in large extent a natural electrochemical reaction where a pole
either donated or received an electrode. The reactions occuring are represented by reduction
process at the cathode and an oxidation process at the anode. It is then the anode which is
aected by the corrosion process. Equation the anodic/cathodic reaction is shown for iron:
Anodic reaction : 4Fe2+ →4Fe3+ + 4e−
(3)
Cathodic : reaction : 2H2O + O2 + 4e−→4OH−
(4)
Combined gives:
4Fe2+ + O2 + 2H2O →4Fe3+ + 4OH−
(5)
This means that for the corrosion process to occur, oxygen and water must be present and the
electrons will always because of its negative charge travel against the current meaning it is the
least noble metal that will become the anode. In general stainless steel mainly consists of the
elements as nickel, chromium and iron. Of these elements the main element which characterises
stainless steel is the chromium. Looking at the reactivity series it is seen that chromiums nobility
is almost comparable to zinc which means that it can be presumed that chromium corrodes very
easily when it is in contact with water and oxygen [12]. However when Chromium starts to
corrode it under normal conditions starts to form a thin passivation layer of an oxide lm on the
surface. This oxide lm layer works very ecient as an oxygen/water barrier limiting the access
of water and oxygen to the surface of the stainless steel which passivates the corrosion process
quite ecient. The passivation reaction can be expressed, either involving water or oxygen, as

5.2
Carbides precipitation
It is well known that operating austenitic steel at elevated temperatures causes a precipitation
of intermetallic phases which can have a huge inuence on the behaviour of the material. At
the operational temperature Vølund has supplied, it is very likely a precipitation of intermetallic
phases occurs. The most likely intermetallic phase to be precipitated in the alloy is chromium
carbides. The chromium carbides are precipitated if the temperature exceeds around 460C0 (see
gure 19) and the higher the temperature is, the faster the precipitation occurs until the tem-
perature reaches a sucient temperature where the carbides are dissolved. Since the incinerator
plant is operational most of the year it has a lot of hours at the operational temperature. The
combination of high amount of hours at an elevated temperature and medium-high content of
17
==PAGE:19==
both chromium and carbon gives the ideal environment for a precipitation of chromium carbides.
Figure 19:
Precipitation of chromium carbides as a function of time and temperature for
austenitic stainless steel type AISI 3043
The precipitation of chromium carbides exposes the material to intergranular corrosion because
the chromium is depleted in a small zone in the grain boundaries, which under normal condi-
tions provides the passivation layer. Both this and the fact that chromium carbides is a brittle
phase, which is precipitated in the grain boundaries, reduces the creep resistance and fracture
toughness in the grain boundaries which could lead to intergranular fracture.
The purpose of sample no. 6 was to investigate if chromium carbides had been precipitated in
the part of the grate with the highest temperature. Unfortunately, it was not possible to make
this analysis within the time frame of this master thesis.

6.1
Implicit and explicit integration method
Over time several dierent direct integration methods have been developed to solve problems in
the eld of nite elements. In general they can be divided into two dierent groups, implicit and
explicit integration method. I have in my master thesis used an implicit integration method in
Abaqus/CAE 6.14-2.
In short term the advantage of the implicit integration method is its unconditionally stability
even for large time steps, because it is only dependent on the current and previous time step
where the explicit integration method is only conditionally stable because of its demand for
suciently small time steps to maintain stability.
The disadvantage of the implicit integration method is that it is more ops and storage demand-
ing than the explicit and if the time step becomes to large it risks both loosing its accuracy and
that a non physical oscillation will arise in the solution [11]. In general the choice between
the implicit and explicit integration method is related to stability and economics. If there is a
demand for a high precision in the solution then it is likely preferable to use many time steps to
achieve this, where the explicit integration method is more economic. However, if a suciently
small inaccuracy in the solution can be neglected, the implicit integration method can maintain
its stability for a higher time step and thereby nd the solution with much less iterations.

Conclusion
A thorough investigation of the thermomechanical stresses applied to the grates in an incinerator
plant has been analysed in this report. Of the submitted grates the aws have been measured,
documented and analysed according to the applied stresses found in the nite element model
conducted in Abaqus. There were also found a similarities between the location of the cracks
in the submitted grates and the highest stress intensities simulated in the nite element model.
Through fracture mechanical analysis it was concluded that the stresses applied to the cracks
by the nite element model in most of the cases were sucient to provoke a propagation of the
fractures.
