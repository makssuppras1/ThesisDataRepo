ABSTRACT 
The maritime industry for transportation of passengers and goods is continuously growing, both 
internationally and locally. As a consequence, its impact on the environment is becoming more severe 
because of the increasing consumption of fuel. It has therefore been of growing interest to use lightweight, 
composite materials in cruise liners in the recent two decades, to decrease the consumption of fuel, 
resulting in more sustainable and environmentally friendly constructions. The materials are called Fiber 
Reinforced Plastic (FRP) and consist of fibers and plastic components. There are only few requirements in 
relation to reaction-to-fire properties, when using these relatively new materials. Therefore some of the 
materials could possibly be more dangerous than expected, if a fire should accidentally occur on a 
lightweight ship. The new lightweight materials can possibly affect the Available Safe Egress Time (ASET), 
which is the time available to reach safety, in the event of an emergency. The ASET can possibly be 
shortened if combustion of the material results in reduction of the visibility, increased radiation or if the 
toxic gasses from FRP combustion influences humans’ reaction time or leads to unconsciousness. 
This thesis will focus on an investigation of the toxicity of FRP materials through a series of experiments, 
where samples are burned under controlled conditions with varying incident heat fluxes. The material of 
the samples consists of laminates of polyester with glass fibers, enclosing a core of PVC – Glass Fiber 
Reinforced Plastic (GFRP). The emissions of gasses are quantitatively measured and the time of ignition is 
determined by observations in order to determine thermal properties of the material. The heat release rate 
is determined with Janssens’ method [1]. The material parameters are compared to table values for similar 
composite materials and to other relevant construction materials. This is done both to ensure that the 
results are valid, and to investigate how the fire properties varies compared to other building materials. 
The experiments showed that the Heat Release Rate (HRR) of the GFRP follows an expected development 
with a peak just after ignition. The average peak HRR was found to be 372 kW/m2. This is significantly 
higher compared to other composite materials. In the steady state period the soot yield was found to be 
0.17 g/g in average. The soot yields for similar materials were both found to be higher and lower than this 
value in the literature. This is due to the available oxygen concentration during combustion, which the soot 
yield is highly dependent on. 
Combustion of the FRP material will result in high soot formations which will both increase the toxicity and 
decrease the visibility in a room. An investigation of the decomposition of the composite showed that 
several toxic gases were a consequence of the pyrolysis, and the combustion of polyester and PVC. This 
includes among others toxic hydrocarbons (including carcinogenic PAHs) and HCl. The glass fibers are non-
combustible and will not contribute to the toxicity. 
Some material parameters derived from the experiments are used as inputs in the CFD program Fire 
Dynamic Simulator (FDS) to evaluate the available safe egress time in a fire enclosure on a ship. The ship is 
a fictional ship called the Eco-Island ferry, constructed of the specific glass fiber composite. A worst-case 
scenario is defined and simulated in FDS, where the design fire is the ignition of a chair in the 
accommodation space. The ASET is found by assessing the tenability criteria in the output files from the FDS 
simulations. The most critical parameter was found to be the visibility, and the ASET was 63 seconds for the 
most critical side of the space, and 103 seconds for the other side. This was compared to the Required Safe 
Egress Time (RSET) to be 163 seconds on the critical side, and 238 seconds on the other side. An estimate of 
the amount of people exposed to critical conditions from the critical side was 33 out of 55 passengers, 
which is unacceptable fire safety conditions. Examples of means for increasing the fire safety are suggested 
to conclude the project. Among others, this includes the use of fire retardants, use of a mix of other 
building materials with FRP and appropriate training of the crew on board.

1. INTRODUCTION 
The maritime industry is expanding remarkably these days – up to 7% per year. With this follows an 
increased impact on the environment; the maritime industry has today an estimated emission of 870 
million tons of CO2 annually, corresponding to around 2.7% of global CO2 emissions [2][3]. Maritime 
transportation is the lowest emitting transportation type in the world in terms of grams of emitted CO2 
per tons of cargo transported 1 kilometer [4]. The pollution of the environment is still essential, 
because of the high number of maritime vessels. Therefore the industry seeks to decrease the 
environmental footprint, where the primary focus is on fuel efficiency. This includes the use of different 
types of fuels and fuel reduction. Fiber Reinforced Plastic (FRP) is a composite material used as 
construction material on ships in the last two decades, and has a high strength to weight ratio 
compared to steel or aluminum [5]. The construction material of cruise liners has recently partly or fully 
changed to this material to lower the weight and thereby decrease the amount of fuel, making them 
more environmental friendly and cheaper in operation and maintenance.  
The fact that FRP cannot corrode is another benefit of the material and there is a larger freedom in the 
design phase, as it is possible to produce more advanced geometries at an acceptable cost [6]. The 
change of material has raised the question about some of the material properties – including the 
reaction and smoke development if a fire accidently occurs on a ship. The purpose of this thesis is to try 
to answer this question by investigating the FRP material in relation to fire. This is done by conducting 
laboratory experiments, igniting the material and simulating a specific fire enclosure in the Fire 
Dynamics Simulator (FDS) program. The available safe egress time is evaluated from the simulations 
and compared to required safe egress time, determined with engineering calculation methods. 
1.1 METHOD 
This thesis is a practical as well as a theoretical project. The first step in the process was a wide-range 
literature study focusing on scientific articles, relevant regulations and standards, and other projects 
discussing the implementation of a new material in the maritime industry with potentially unpredicted 
fire hazards. The international SOLAS Convention (International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea) 
has been used in the study, which has to be complied with in the design of world-wide maritime 
vessels. A specific composite FRP material is evaluated through fire tests, and the fire performance is 
determined from the results of the tests, including the heat development and smoke production. The 
cone calorimeter method is the approach used in the set-up of the experiment and the test procedure. 
The characteristics of a fire evaluated throughout this study can be seen on Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1 – Fire characteristics 
==PAGE:16==

A fire enclosure results in a temperature release, increased heat flux, decreased visibility and 
potentially toxicity to occupants exposed to the fire. The heat (release rate) and smoke production is 
evaluated through experiments. Results from the fire tests are used as input parameters in FDS, which 
is used to simulate a fire compartment in a specific ferry to evaluate the time available to evacuate 
(ASET). The evacuation time is determined from specific tenability limits, which includes temperature 
limits, limited heat flux, limited visibility and limited gas concentrations. The toxicity of the material is 
also evaluated by studying the chemical decomposition of the FRP composite. 
While the ASET is found by the FDS program, the required safe egress time (RSET) is found by 
engineering calculations. Two methods are used for the purpose: The International Maritime 
Organizations’ requirement for maximum evacuation time and the approach which is typically used in 
commercial buildings where ASET is compared to RSET. Both approaches include an estimation of the 
time to escape (travel time + queue time) which is determined with the hydraulic model. 
1.2  LIMITATIONS 
This project does not include how the FRP material influences the environment, but is limited to the 
investigation of life safety on board a passenger ship. As the smoke production will be larger in case of 
fire compared to a ship made of steel, the environmental impact will probably be higher as well. 
Another issue about FRP is the reduction of strength as a load-bearing material when exposed to fire, 
which will not be a topic in this project; it is limited to smoke production, development and heat 
release. However, this is another important issue of using FRP, as the strength decreases a lot faster 
with increased temperature compared to steel [7, p. 282]. 
There are possibly long-term, carcinogenic effects when humans are being exposed to soot from FRP 
materials. Even though this is a negative outcome of using FRP instead of steel, the effects are not 
evaluated in this project. Only effects occurring immediately during a fire (short-term effects) are taken 
into account when evaluating the fire safety. 
This project focuses on the analysis of one type of maritime vessel, which is a smaller passenger ships. It 
is possible that the results would give the same tendencies for any other maritime transportation type, 
but the project is limited to this one ship due to project time limitations.

2. BACKGROUND 
Over sea transport is one of the cheapest ways to transport people and goods and has been used for 
thousands of years. In the Industrial Revolution at the end of the 18th century there was an expansion in 
the need for oversea transportation. The construction material changed from bark and wood to iron 
and steel, materials that have remained the basis of a ships’ structure throughout the 19th century and 
into today. As an effect of the broad use of iron and steel, almost all oversea transport regulations are 
based on these materials. This is one of the reasons why it is difficult to implement other materials as 
construction materials. Recently there has been a change in the construction material for the hull, 
which for larger vessels often is aluminum; a strong material that is lighter than steel. Smaller boats and 
ferries are to some extent built of light weight composites such as FRP [8], which not only has the 
benefit of having a low weight compared to its strength, but also other advantages like no corrosion, 
design flexibility and a low life cycle cost; in general decreasing the environmental impact of the 
vessels. The material has been integrated in the maritime industry for several applications; larger 
components as structures, deck house and cabin modules, and smaller components as ventilators, 
pressure bottles and propellers [9]. It has also been used in other sectors e.g. in aircrafts, naval ships, 
military tanks and public transportation vehicles [7]. 
The use of FRP composites in the maritime industry has been questioned in relation to fire safety 
[10][5][11][9]. The concern about the material is not only about toxicity and smoke production. Another 
issue raised using FRP instead of steel is the heat transfer. This is lower in FRP, which can both be seen 
as an advantage and disadvantage. The advantage is that a fire might be contained more in an FRP 
construction, and it might be possible to evacuate through a deck above the fire as long as the deck 
does not collapse. The bad thing about the low heat transfer is that it is less efficient to cool when 
trying to stop the fire [9]. 
Fire on board a ferry is historically and statistically a major risk in maritime safety [12]. An example of 
an incident was in December 2015 where a lightweight FRP ship called Umoe Ventus caught fire, see 
Figure 2. The fire started because the cooling system of an engines’ exhaust system did not work, and 
the hot exhaust started a fire on the silencer of the same engine. The flames spread quickly, and only 15 
minutes after the fire was visually observed, the whole ship was surrounded by flames and the smoke 
was thick and increasing rapidly. Because of this accident, a new project has started at Danish Institute 
of Fire and Security Technology (DBI) concerning fire and security at FRP ships. Both the design phase 
and the training of the crew are included in this project. The crew on Umoe Ventus did not act optimally 
in the case of fire on the FRP ship [13]. 
 
Figure 2 – Umoe Ventus: An FRP ship which caught fire [13] 
==PAGE:18==

Another incident happened in 2014, where the High Speed Craft ship “SEA GALE” caught fire in the 
machine room. The building material of the ship was FRP and the fire started in a carbon composite 
panel and insulation above the main engine exhaust pipe. The 12 crew members decided quickly to 
evacuate the ship because of rapid flame spread and dense smoke, and only 15 minutes after the fire 
was visually confirmed, the vessel was totally surrounded by flames [14]. According to the following 
report, the major problem was that the crew did not know to what extent the smoke from the FRP 
materials were toxic, and they did not know how fast the fire would spread and affect the structural 
strength of the ship. These factors had an effect on the reaction of the crew members who did not 
follow the procedure of evacuation and firefighting during the incident. The crew was trained to react 
at accidents on steel ships, and did not know how to react at an FRP ship where the visual of the 
incident is much more severe [14]. If the crew did not react like they did, they could potentially have 
been exposed to toxic effluents from the FRP material and have lost consciousness. Those cases 
underline the importance of a fire risk analysis before use of an FRP ships and underlines the concerns 
of the FRP materials as construction material. 
2.1  FIRE SAFETY REQUIREMENTS AND REGULATIONS 
The International Maritime Organization (IMO) is the global standard-setting authority for the safety, 
security and environmental performance of international shipping. The requirements to secure proper 
safety in relation to fire include structural and material requirements as well as fire protection systems 
(alarms, sprinklers etc.), fire safety plans and an adequate available safe egress time. IMO has worked 
over decades to improve the fire safety on ferries by improving structural, functional and operational 
barriers to prevent or mitigate fire and using non-combustible construction materials (steel) and limited 
use of combustible materials. As mentioned, all maritime transportation guidelines and regulations are 
based on steel as the implementation of composites on ships is relatively new, but recently an addition 
to the guidelines has opened up for the use of the materials. It is much more demanding to obtain 
approval and the design requires much more engineering rigor when designing a ship of FRP. The 
material has been allowed for many years for High-Speed Craft (HSC) passenger vessels, up to 24 
passengers and small commercial vessels shorter than 24 m, up to 12 passengers [12]. 
When designing a marine vessel, it has to comply with a certain regulation. If a ship has the purpose of 
sailing on international waters, it has to comply with the SOLAS Convention (International Convention 
for the Safety of Life at Sea), a guideline for safety on ships. This is the most internationally 
acknowledged set of regulations for maritime safety, described by IMO. The first version was adopted 
in 1914, just after the Titanic disaster. After this, it was revised in 1929, 1948, 1960 and the one used 
today is from 1974, which is updated occasionally. It applies for all ships with more than 12 passengers 
[15]. Chapter II-2 in the regulation is specific about fire protection, fire detection and fire extinction, 
and includes detailed fire safety provisions for all ships and specific measures for passenger ships, cargo 
ships and tankers. The objectives of the chapter are the following [6]: 
1. Prevent the occurrence of fire and explosion 
2. Reduce the risk to life caused by fire 
3. Reduce the risk of damage caused by fire to the ship, its cargo and the environment 
4. Contain, control and suppress fires and explosion in the compartment of origin 
5. Provide adequate and readily accessible means of escape for passengers and crew 
==PAGE:19==

To fulfil these objectives, the following functional requirements should be complied with [6]: 
1. Division of the ship into vertical and horizontal zones by thermal and structural boundaries 
2. Separation of accommodation spaces from the remainder of the ship by thermal and structural 
boundaries 
3. Restricted use of combustible materials 
4. Detection of any fire in the zone of origin 
5. Containment and extinction of any fire in the space of origin 
6. Protection of means of escape and access for fire fighting 
7. Ready availability of fire-extinguishing appliances 
8. Minimization of possibility of ignition of flammable cargo vapor 
The main barrier for using FRP composites is the SOLAS convention which is meant for conventional 
ships of steel. One barrier is the statement that the use of combustible materials shall be restricted, 
where a combustible material is defined in SOLAS Chapter II-2 by “a material which neither burns nor 
gives off flammable vapors in sufficient quantity for self-ignition when heated to approximately 750°C, 
this being determined in accordance with the Fire Test Procedures Code (FTP code). Any other material is 
a combustible material.” The materials have to comply with a standard fire test, where the material is 
exposed to temperatures corresponding to a standard time-temperature curve in accordance with the 
test method specified in the FTP Code [16]. As FRP is a combustible material [9], this requirement is not 
complied with. 
Another barrier in the convention is the requirement for divisions between bulkheads and decks to be 
constructed of steel or other equivalent materials. This is defined as “any material which, by itself or 
due to insulation provided, has structural and integrity properties equivalent to steel at the end of the 
applicable fire exposure to the standard fire test (e.g. aluminum allow with appropriate insulation).” 
In 2002 there was developed an additional regulation to SOLAS: Regulation 17. It was added to the 
SOLAS regulation to make it possible to deviate from the traditional ship design – and especially with 
the aim of using FRP as a construction material [17]. This regulation has to be followed in order to use 
lightweight combustible FRP panels within a ships’ structure. It requires a risk-based design approach in 
order to deviate from the prescriptive requirements in SOLAS. Regulation 17 is furtherly elaborated in 
the guideline IMO’s Circular 1002 for ‘alternative designs’ [18] which is a more detailed, but still very 
general approach to Regulation 17. The fire safety objectives and functional requirements pointed 
above still have to be fulfilled, but should be complied with by following the procedure in this guideline. 
When approving products for installation in ships in accordance with the fire safety performance of 
SOLAS, it has to be tested in accordance to the FTP Code. This could be for example new materials for 
furnishing or structural elements. Certain elements have to comply with certain requirements to be 
able to classify them as e.g. A-60 or B-15. The classification defines the structural strength during a fire 
and the heat development in the element. The FTP Code uses approaches in ISO standards 
(Internationals Organization for Standardization) and does refer to the standards in the test procedures.

If a ship is not sailing on national waters and only sailing within EU countries it has to comply with the

than steel without regulative restrictions. A HSC ship has to fulfill the requirement of a “simple 
evacuation”, which most often is interpreted as having only one deck so there will be no stairways to go 
through which prolongs the evacuation time1. The HSC Code applies for both national and international 
ships, but the layout and use of such a ship has many limitations.

2.1.1 REGULATIONS AFFECTED BY THE CHANGE OF MATERIAL 
It has been investigated which regulations in SOLAS are affected by the change of material from steel to 
FRP composites by M. P. Amen and F. Evegren from SP Technical Research Institute of Sweden [6] (now 
known as RISE). As the SOLAS convention is better structured and more internationally applicable than 
the EU legislation, it has been assessed that it is appropriate to base the analysis on SOLAS. The 
following regulations are affected [6]: 
- 
Regulation 5: Fire growth potential 
- 
Regulation 6: Smoke generation potential and toxicity 
- 
Regulation 9: Containment of fire 
- 
Regulation 10: Fire fighting 
- 
Regulation 11: Structural integrity 
Ad. Regulation 5: This regulation states that the use of combustible materials should be restricted. 
When changing from steel to plastic composites the amount of combustible material is increased. 
Ad. Regulation 6: This regulation states that the hazard should be reduced from smoke and toxic 
products generated during a fire. The quantity of smoke and toxic products released from combustible 
materials shall be limited. As the amount of combustible materials is increased with FRP, the amount of 
released gases will increase as well as plastic materials emit toxic smoke during combustion. 
Ad. Regulation 9: The regulation defines requirements for division of thermal and structural zones, 
which has to be done by materials classified as “A-60”. A-class divisions have to be made of steel or 
equivalent material which the FRP composites cannot be defined as. Fire insulation can be added to the 
inside of the FRP layer which can perform “equivalent” to steel, but when the insulation is consumed, 
the FRP materials will release heat which may accelerate the fire development. 
Ad. Regulation 10: This regulation states that fixed fire-extinguishing systems shall be installed, having 
due regard to the fire growth potential of the spaces. The fire growth could happen faster in an FRP 
ship than with steel and this have to be taken into account when designing firefighting systems. The 
flames will spread faster upwards and especially where there are openings to the outside, where there 
might be an additional need for sprinklers. 
Ad. Regulation 11: The regulation about structural integrity will also be affected as the FRP panels lose 
their structural strength at much lower temperatures than steel. This can be seen on Figure 4 showing 
the variation of strength with temperature for various materials.

2.1.2 FIRE SAFETY DESIGN 
In order to maintain the fire safety as high as possible, several requirements must be complied with. It 
includes division of the ship into vertical zones by thermal and structural boundaries; separation of 
accommodation spaces from the remainder of the ship by thermal and structural boundaries; restricted 
use of combustible materials; detection of any fire in the zone of origin; containment and extinction of 
any fire in the space of origin; protection of the means of escape or of access for fire-fighting purposes; 
ready availability of fire-extinguishing appliances and minimization of the possibility of ignition of 
flammable cargo vapor [21]. 
SOLAS Chapter II-2 consists of 20 regulations defining several fire protection requirements, where the 
ones relevant to this project are specified below for passenger ships carrying more than 36 passengers 
and for a passenger ship of FRP material [22]. 
- 
At least two widely separated and ready means of escape shall be provided from all spaces 
(or group of spaces). 
- 
Stairways and corridors used as means of escape shall be not less than 700 mm in clear 
width. 
- 
Maximum allowable total passenger ship evacuation time should be 60 minutes if the ship 
has no more than three main vertical (fire) zones, and 80 minutes if the ship has more than 
three main vertical (fire) zones. 
- 
Minimum four evacuation scenarios needs to be considered [23]: 
• 
Primary case: full capacity of passengers on the ship evacuating at the night and 
during the day 
• 
Secondary case: modelling just one of the main vertical fire zones but assuming that 
only 50% of the stairways are available or that an additional 50% of the passengers 
from a neighboring zone are forced to move into the zone to proceed to the 
assembly point. These are also to be modelled at night and during the day. 
If a fire occurs on a ship the passengers should have sufficient time to escape the ferry before critical 
conditions occur. The endpoint of ASET (Available Safe Egress Time) is when conditions in an enclosure 
are considered untenable. Untenable conditions are when people have a low possibility of saving 
themselves from an enclosure because of effects from smoke (limited visibility), heat and/or toxic 
effluent. The definition of critical conditions depends on the local regulations [7, p. 2309]. It is difficult 
to quantify the effects on humans exposed to conditions followed by fire, and there is limited data 
available from real-fire incidents [24]. Professor D. A. Purser has researched the subject, and most 
tenability limits in guidelines is a result of his work; this includes limits for gas concentrations, visibility, 
convection and heat radiation [25]. 
The effects of a fire can be psychological, physiological or physical, and those parameters can all affect 
each other. For example, when a person is panicking (psychological) because of the incident of fire, it 
will have an influence on the escape time (physical). Also the individual differences makes it difficult to

Exposure to heat can lead to incapacitations in different ways: through hyperthermia, skin pain, burns 
or respiratory burns. During and after second WW the knowledge of thermal tolerance of human was 
more important than ever before. The effects of high radiation and convective heat resulted in 
disabilities and death during the war, and research on this topic should provide protection and medical 
care, but also help to develop thermally effective weapons [27]. Different combinations of time and

on post-war victims. The experiments on humans were said to be conducted on volunteers, and the aim 
of these was primarily to determine what types of experiments on pigs were applicable to humans [28]. 
Studies from that time are still used today in design criteria.

A major concern about the FRP material is the smoke generation and toxicity during combustion, and 
according to the SFPE Handbook and other literature [7, p. 2311] [30], the major cause of death due to 
fires is contamination of smoke. It is difficult to define the point where irritant gases and particulates 
are life-threating. The severity of the effect increases continuously with the concentration; starting with 
eye irritation and going towards more severe eye and respiratory pain. The individual is an important 
factor in defining critical limits for this [24].

hydrogen bromide HBr and hydrogen fluoride HF) are defined as irritant gases [25]. 
The other type of life-threatening gas in a fire is asphyxiant gases which are carbon monoxide (CO), 
carbon dioxide (CO2), hydrogen cyanide (HCN) and reduced oxygen (O2). They result in confusion 
followed by loss of consciousness, and ultimately death. Experiments have been carried out with 
different gas conditions, and for ethical reasons, those have not been carried out on humans. 
Therefore, primates have been used for the purpose. It has been tested when low O2 concentrations, 
high CO concentrations and high HCN levels have the effect of functional impairment. CO2 is toxic 
because it increases the rate of consumption of the other asphyxiant gases, and it does not make sense 
to make experiments with an increase of this gas alone [25]. 
CO is one of the main reasons for intoxication and loss of consciousness in fires [25]. CO is toxic because 
it binds strongly to hemoglobin in blood and forms carboxyhemoglobin (COHb), which interferes with 
oxygen transportation in the body. The fatal limit of COHb in the body is 50-60% [31]. 
HCN is a very asphyxiating gas and has been used as war gas at concentration camps during second 
WW. It is released in any fire involving fuels containing nitrogen in the molecular structure [25]. O2 is 
necessary for the human body which is transported to all parts of the body by red blood cells. Therefore 
low oxygen concentrations are dangerous.

Preliminary analysis 
A preliminary analysis is the first step in fire safety modelling and defines the scope of the project. The 
ships, ship system(s), component(s), space(s) and/or equipment subject to the analysis are thoroughly 
defined. This information will be used further in the FDS analysis of the project. This may include ship 
plans, drawings, equipment information and fire test data [18]. 
Different fire scenarios should be defined in this section. The fire hazards should be identified and 
specification of design fire scenarios should be pointed out. The identification of fire hazards can be 
done by several available procedures, and should include a qualitative description of the design fire 
(e.g. ignition source, fuel first ignited, location etc.), description of the vessel, compartment of origin, 
fire protection systems installed, number of occupants, physical and mental status of occupants and 
available means of escape [18]. 
Quantitative analysis 
The next step is a quantitative analysis. The heat release rate and location of the design fire(s) is 
defined, and calculation of the effects of fire detection, alarm and suppression methods, generating 
time lines from initiation of the fire until control or evacuation,  fire growth rate, heat fluxes, heat 
release rates, flame heights, smoke and toxic gas generation, etc. [18]. Some of this information is 
found with experiments. 
A time line is developed for each fire scenario. It should include some of the following: ignition, 
established burning, fire detection, fire alarm, fire suppression/control system activation, personnel 
response, fire control, escape times, manual fire response, untenable conditions, etc. The specific 
location(s) of ignition is pointed out [18]. 
Risk analysis 
The risk analysis has mainly the purpose of assessing if the relevant scenarios and data are included in 
the analysis. It is impossible to analyze all possible fire scenarios as there will be varying ignition 
sources, ventilation conditions, climatic conditions and reaction of people leading to several varying 
cases. A risk analysis should be made to be able to determine if the most critical scenarios are 
evaluated with a representative worst case scenario. It is assessed if the performance criteria are 
complied with in the worst case scenario.  
The performance of the prescriptive vs. alternative design is evaluated as well. The prescriptive design 
is a design which complies with SOLAS, and the alternative design could be an FRP design which follows 
regulation 17. The final documentation shall demonstrate that the alternative design and arrangements 
provide the equivalent level of safety to the prescriptive requirements of SOLAS chapter II-2. In this 
project, the fire safety analysis is conducted by evaluating the evacuation time with a quantitative 
method. 
If the results of the risk analysis shows that the performance criteria are not complied with or there is a 
lack of investigated scenarios (i.e. the most critical scenario is not evaluated or other conditions have 
been found to be applicable than were assumed earlier), the design should be modified and the process 
should be followed once again starting with the preliminary analysis. These scenarios are marked with 
arrows on Figure 5 above.

5.1  FIBER REINFORCED PLASTIC 
Fiber Reinforced Plastic (FRP) is a composite material consisting of two elements; fibers working as 
reinforcement and a polymer matrix, for example epoxy, vinyl ester, phenolic or polyester resin. The 
most commonly used FRPs are glass fiber-reinforced plastic (GFRP), carbon fiber-reinforced plastic 
(CFRP) and aramid fiber-reinforced plastic (AFRP) composites, with glass used as fibers 90% of the time 
[38]. FRP was developed after second WW, and the use of FRP has been growing the last two decades 
in civil engineering applications – and not only in the maritime industry. It has been used as internal 
reinforcement in roads, bridges, slopes, tunnels and marine environment. Also ducts and bridges have 
been constructed of FRP for many years. The first composite bridge was built in Denmark in 1997 in 
Kolding [39]. It is widely used because of the materials’ high strength compared to weight and the 
limited risk of corrosion. When using FRP as a building material it is constructed as a sandwich panel 
consisting of a lightweight plastic core and two FRP laminates as seen on Figure 10. The way it is 
designed gives it structural properties as an “I-beam” which makes the panel very stiff [40]. 
 
Figure 10 – Structure of FRP sandwich panels [10] 
 
The performance of the sandwich panel when exposed to fire varies with several properties; the 
thickness of the laminates, the cores density, the type of plastic and the type of fibers are the most 
influential properties [40][7]. FRP exists in many different combinations, and it is therefore difficult to 
say something general about the fire performance, and information about fire properties and smoke 
==PAGE:32==
 
18 
 
production is limited [7, pp. 309–314]. Typically the core material has a density in the range of 60-200 
kg/m3 and a thickness of 25-100 mm. The laminates are at least 1 mm thick and have a density around 
2000 kg/m3 [10]. 
Building materials can be divided into two groups; combustible or non-combustible, depending on their 
thermal properties. Combustible building materials may contribute to faster development during a fire 
as they react as fuel and add to the severity of fire when igniting. Building materials can further be 
divided into five sub-groups depending on the reaction-to-fire [7, p. 280]. FRP are classified as an L/I/F 
type of material, which stands for load-bearing/insulating/fuel. The material is classified as load-bearing 
as it can resist moderate stresses. During fire, it can provide thermal protection to load-bearing 
materials (therefore insulating). The ‘fuel’ part indicates that the material might become fuel in fires (as 
a negative property) [7, pp. 280, 309–314]. 
As mentioned, FRP can be produced with a large variety of reinforcement, resin systems and also 
processing methods. The following principles should be considered when choosing the type of FRP [38]: 
- 
Amount and direction of fibers determine the mechanical strength 
- 
Formulation of the resin determine the chemical, electrical and thermal performance 
- 
The size, shape and complexity of parts determine the molding process

5.2  THERMAL DECOMPOSITION OF THE FRP 
High molecular weight organic compounds may release high concentrations of soot, and especially 
under ventilation controlled fires. Polymers consist of a high number of repeat units, forming very long 
chain molecules. The FRP material consists of organic polymers, and for polyester the chain consists of a 
number of (C10H8O4)n units, and PVC consists of (C2H3Cl)n units. Both materials are in the group called 
thermoplastics, which means that they can be repeatedly deformed by heating [7, p. 170]. Polyester 
can also be in the group called thermosets, but the most commonly used polyester is PET which is a 
thermoplastic [7, p. 216]. 
Pyrolysis of the plastics is the first step in a combustion process which involves breaking the polymer 
chain and starts when the polymer is heated above a certain temperature, which is individual for each 
polymer. The process turns chains of 10,000-500,000 carbon atoms into species small enough to be 
volatilized. During combustion, molecules are released from the decomposing polymer. The process of 
decomposition of polymers depends of the type of polymer. The mechanism of decomposition of PVC is 
chain stripping, which is a process in which groups attached to the backbone as side chains are 
released. The most typical products of decomposition of PVC is HCl, 
aromatic hydrocarbons and char [7, pp. 197–198]. Dioxins are another toxic 
product of PVC combustion as TCDD (tetrachlorodibenzodioxin) is toxic, 
which chemical composition can be seen in Figure 12. Dioxins are an 
environmental pollutant and are toxic for humans as they can be endocrine 
disrupting and carcinogenic. 
Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) are a possible combustion product as well, which are formed 
during incomplete combustion. It is a group of over 100 different organic compounds containing only 
carbon and hydrogen. Soot can contain several PAH particles [43] which can have a carcinogenic effect. 
For PET, the combustion mechanism is random chain scission, where the chain breaks at random points 
along its length. Typical products of combustion is alkanes (hydrocarbons consisting of single bonds), 
alkenes (hydrocarbons consisting of single and double bonds) and very little monomer [7, pp. 197–198].

A fire development is commonly described as a t-squared fire with the expression [45]:

kW/s2, and an ultra-fast fire has a rate of 0.19 kW/s2 [45] resulting in a heat release rate in kW. 
The development of a fire depends on several factors, e.g. the type of fuel, amount of fuel, geometry of 
enclosure, geometry of fuel and ventilation conditions [46]. The typical phases in a fire are seen on 
Figure 14. The filled line shows a fully developed fire reaching the flashover point. The dotted lines

The ignition time defines the time at which the fire can continue without an external heat source. The 
surface properties of the material are of very importance in this stage. After ignition, the fire growth 
phase starts, and if there is enough oxygen and fuel, the fire will continue to develop until flashover. 
When a fire is developed to flashover, it is at the critical point for humans residing in the enclosure. If 
there is a lack of either oxygen or fuel, the fire will not develop into flashover, which is indicated by the 
dotted lines. The temperature in the enclosure will increase due to energy release during combustion. 
The energy release is defined as heat of combustion, ΔHc. The energy release will result in heat transfer 
through radiation and convection from the fire, radiation from the smoke layer and from surrounding 
surfaces [46]. 
A fire can either be fuel or ventilation controlled. As long as the fire is small in a relatively large 
compartment it will be fuel controlled. At flashover, the temperature of the hot layer is high enough to 
ignite all combustible materials in the enclosure, and at this point, all surfaces will contribute to 
combustion. Incomplete combustion will take place as there will not be sufficient oxidizer to a complete 
combustion. The production of toxic fire effluent will increase as furtherly described in section 6.3.

If breaking of glass windows or opening of doors happens at flashover, the flames will spread out of the 
openings where there is more oxygen available. The thermal properties of the surrounding elements in 
the room (walls, ceiling, furnishing etc.) have a large influence on the time to flashover. The fire will 
reach the decay phase when there is a lack of oxygen or fuel [46]. 
6.2  HEAT RELEASE RATE 
The heat release rate (HRR or Q_) is the most critical property during a fire because it is the driving force 
for fire spread [47]. It is calculated with results from the experiments with oxygen consumption 
calorimetry, in accordance to Janssens’ method [1]. The oxygen consumption calorimetry and carbon 
dioxide generation calorimetry are usual methods to determine the heat release rate in bench-scale 
and large-scale fire tests. This is a well-defined method and is very applicable for the testing method at 
the fire laboratory at the Technical University of Denmark. 
The heat release rate is determined by the principle of the theory that an average amount of heat is 
released per unit mass of oxygen consumed for complete combustion. This value is 13.1 MJ/kg O2 and 
applies for all organic liquids and gases and organic solids, and is within ±5% or better accuracy [48], 
which is used for the calculations. For PET (polyethylene) the value is 12.65 MJ/kg O2 and for PVC 
(polyvinyl chloride) 12.84 MJ/kg O2, [48] which makes an error of 3.6% and 2.0% respectively when 
using the value of 13.1 MJ/kg O2 instead. Using the value of 13.1 MJ/kg O2 is a conservative assumption. 
The following assumptions apply for using this method [1]: 
- 
All combustion products are collected and removed through a duct 
- 
The amount of energy released by complete combustion per unit mass of oxygen consumed 
is a constant of E = 13.1 MJ/kg [48] 
- 
All gases behave like ideal gases 
- 
Incoming air consists of O2, CO2, H2O and N2. Other gasses, not taking part of the combustions 
are ignored 
- 
O2, CO2 and CO are measured on a dry basis i.e. water is removed before the gas analysis

6.4  THERMAL PROPERTIES

experiments. The material properties controlling the heat flow through the constructions are the 
conductivity (k), density (ρ) and specific heat capacity (c). The product of these properties is called

where it never ignite.

6.5  METHOD 
The cone calorimeter method was developed by Babrauskas [7, p. 952] to measure the heat release 
rate and other combustion properties. The test method is in compliance with the procedure in DS/ISO 
5660 [44]. Before starting the experiments, the gas analyzer was calibrated in order to get correct test 
results. 
6.5.1 CALIBRATION AND PREPARATION 
Calibration of O2, CO and CO2 is conducted before the experiments are started. Two bottles of known 
concentrations of gases (O2, CO2, CO, N2) are used for the calibration. The computer is turned on, and 
started for data logging. First, the bottle with Nitrogen is connected to the analyzer in the “Nitrogen” 
opening with a flow of approx. 3 l/min to establish zero values. There are two knobs on the analyzer, 
which both are turned towards “Nitrogen”. The gas analyzer is then calibrated by choosing one gas at 
the time (of O2, CO2, CO), and choosing the lower value to 0.0000% as target value as there is neither of 
these gases in the in-flow from the bottle. It takes about 1 minute to complete the calibration of each 
gas. 
The flow from the gas bottle is turned off and the connection is dismounted, hereafter connecting the 
other gas bottle to the opening labelled with “CO2/CO”, and the right knob is now turned towards 
“CO2/CO”. The “high-value” is now chosen on the analyzer, which should correspond to the molar mass 
percentage in the gas bottle of CO2 (0.05%) and CO (0.005%). The gas bottle is dismounted and the 
ventilation is turned on to correspond to the flow used throughout experiment.

The left knob is turned to “Air” and the right one towards “Sample gas”. When the air flow rate has 
reached max, oxygen is finally calibrated with its maximum value in the air of 21.950% as the target 
value. 
All measurement outputs are in volts (V), and so have to be correlated according to the values in the 
calibration file as described in section 6.2. The constants of the correlation between mass (g) and volt 
(V) are set as input to the computer program, so that the resulting outputs of the mass are in grams:

It is necessary to absorb humidity in the sample gas to ensure correct measurements. Therefore, three 
cylindrical containers with silica gel are placed in front of the cabinet. Those containers are emptied if 
the color changes from orange to green (when the absorption capacity is used), and new fresh silica gel 
is filled in. This is done when necessary in between experiments. 
A cold trap is placed in the gas analyzer to condition the gases before measurements. It ensures that all 
vapors condense to liquid or solid phase. The exhaust air also passes a particle filter to prevent the 
entry of soot in the analyzer. It needs to be monitored every 10th experiment, and has to be replaced 
with a new one if there are black particles in the inner side of the filter. 
The height from the bottom of the cone heater to the top of the sample is adjusted to be 25±1 mm, 
corresponding to a height of 23 mm to the top of the holder to the cone, as the thickness of the overlap 
to the specimen is 2 mm. The shutters are in the closed position. The cone heater is turned on and the 
exhaust hood is ensured to be on. 
6.5.2 EXPERIMENT 
When starting the experiment, the mass and dimensions of the sample are measured and noted to be 
able to determine the density. The sample is placed in a holder with a thermocouple placed between 
the holder and the sample to measure the surface temperature. The temperature of the cone is 
adjusted appropriately in accordance with the wanted heat flux. The set-up of the computer program is 
adjusted to measure the wanted output parameters, which are measured every second. When the 
temperature of the cone is stabilized, the data logging on the computer is started. 
The sample is placed underneath the radiative conical heater, the shutters are removed, the spark 
igniter is moved on the top of the sample and the stopwatch is started. The set-up of the experiment 
can be seen on Figure 18 showing a photo from the executed experiment. 
When ignition occurs, the time is noted down and the spark is removed. This defines the time to 
ignition. The smoke production and flames are filmed as well as the weight measurements to ensure 
that the output weight is correct.

6.6.5 THERMAL PROPERTIES 
The average density of the sandwich panel was found to be 220 kg/m3 with a standard deviation of 2.6 
kg/m3. With the core having a density of 80 kg/m3 and a thickness of 50 mm, it results in the laminates 
having an average density of 2992 kg/m3. The laminates are 1.3 mm thick, so the average density of the 
sandwich panels is still very low because of the lightweight core material. The density of steel varies 
slightly, but is around 7850 kg/m3. The difference in densities indicates a significant reduction of the 
weight of a ship when changing the material from steel to composites. 
The density of the laminates seems high compared to other FRP materials. The value is usually around 
2000 kg/m3 [10] for FRP laminates and a specific example shows almost a doubling of the density from 
[38] for a PET composite with 30% glass (0.056 lb./in3 ≈ 1550 kg/m3). As the fiber content is higher in 
the tested FRP, this could be the reason why the density is higher than the table value, but does not 
explain the significant difference. A possible source of error is the glue between the layers which 
probably has a higher density than the core which is not included, but this would neither give a 
significant difference. The tested FRP material has therefore a relatively high density compared to other 
FRP materials.

When looking at the expression for determining the properties, it seems that the reasons why the 
numbers for heat capacity are too high could be either too high of a time to ignition, too low of a critical 
heat flux or too low of an ignition temperature. The variation in the time to ignition could indicate that 
the samples are inhomogeneous. The amount of glass fibers could vary and it could be argued that the 
resin has not been properly homogenized during the molding process. The laminates and the PVC 
material are separately made and subsequently glued together. It is unknown what the glue consists of; 
this could possibly stop the heat flow through the material, which would result in a lower critical heat 
flux as the material cannot absorb the heat. The amount of glue and air bubbles could also vary over 
the surface. 
According to SFPE Handbook [7] the ignition temperature for polyester is around 380°C and for glass 
reinforced polyester 390-400°C. This indicates that the ignition temperature has been estimated as too 
low (300°C). In other literature [60] there is information about polyester glass fiber laminate, which 
claims an ignition temperature between 346-399°C, also indicating that the measured temperature is 
too low. As the temperature increases rapidly after the ignition point, it is difficult to estimate the exact 
temperature at ignition as there was only one measurement per second. The rapid increase in

the SFPE Handbook [7] which states a CHF of 10 kW/m2 for PET and 8-18 kW/m2 for polyester.

6.6.6 DISCUSSION OF RESULTS 
In physical experiments, several uncertainties can influence the results which can be more or less 
managed. Possible uncertainties throughout the experiments include: 
- 
The material could be non-homogeneous as the results have a significant variation, even 
though all samples were cut from the same plate. 
- 
The comparison of results is a comparison to FRP materials which does not contain a PVC 
core and which does not consist of different layers. It would be more comparable if the 
different materials of the composite were tested individually. 
- 
Delay in response time. The gas analyzer measures the gas concentrations with a delay of 10-
17 seconds. This influences the comparison of time to ignition to the gas measurements. 
- 
The filter in the analyzer was perhaps not replaced as often as it should have. This could 
possibly result in dirty air in the measurements, resulting in inaccurate measurements of the 
gas concentrations or a higher pressure drop through the duct. This is not assessed as being a 
remarkable error. 
- 
The accuracy of the HRR could have been improved if the water vapor in the gas was 
measured as well, but the implementation of a water vapor analyzer is cumbersome as it 
requires that sampling lines, filters etc. be heated to avoid condensation. 
- 
The heat release rates calculated with Janssens’ method are only approximate. For accurate 
heat release rate calculations, the chemical composition of the material and net heat of 
combustion are required. Also the soot yield would have been more accurate with these 
information [49]. The results are only estimates, and will also always depend on ventilation 
conditions as earlier mentioned. 
- 
The results were sampled from an open hood, which could possibly be imprecise as some of 
the combustion gases might flow outside the hood. The hood was closed with walls three out 
of four directions. This was done because it was desirable to watch the experiments from one 
side, and to be able to reach the sample physically if anything should go wrong. 
- 
A possible error in determination of the soot yield is that a constant mass loss rate is 
assumed through the experiments to simplify the calculation. This was not the case, which 
was seen on the figure with the mass loss rate varying over time. The mass loss rate 
decreases over time, which would lead to an increased soot yield over time.

7. FDS MODELLING 
The Building and Fire Research Laboratory at the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) 
has free available programs to simulate fire enclosures. The latest version of Fire Dynamic Simulator, 
FDS6, and Smokeview are the programs used in this project; later herein referred to as FDS and SMV. 
Smokeview is a tool for visualizing FDS data. FDS is a Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) program, 
simulating fluid flows and heat transfer. FDS is not widely used in the design phase of marine vessels3, 
but is most often used in research projects. It is used after incidents of fire in buildings to investigate 
why the fire originated and how it developed. The main purpose of the simulations is the research of 
how to ensure proper fire safety design considerations in future buildings and constructions. 
The design of the Eco-Island ferry is modelled in FDS model to evaluate the fire safety design of an FRP 
vessel. According to [37] there is only a 10-15% chance that fire-fighting will be successful in case of a 
fire. If this does not happen, the fire will not be under control and the fire development as simulated in 
FDS will consequently happen. 
7.1  SIMULATION SPACE AND OCCUPANTS 
The information stated in the preliminary and quantitative analysis, as well as the results from the 
experiments are used as input parameters. The base model for the FDS input file is from the Eco-Island 
ferry project [61], which is modified where relevant. The Eco-Island project included two simulations, 
both simulating the accommodation space. One simulation includes one side of the accommodation 
space, assuming the doors between the two sides are closed. The second simulation includes the whole 
accommodation space with open doors between the two enclosures. In an emergency situation, there 
is a risk that the electrical systems will fail; this includes the automatic door system. In that case, the 
second simulation model applies. 
The amount of people in the ferry depends on the season. In the winter season the number of 
occupants is limited to the number of seats inside, which are 101. There will be additionally 3 crew 
members (in total 104 people). In the summer season, when people can be outside as well, the 
maximum number of occupants is 200. The crew number in the summer season is 4 (in total 204). 
The modification of the input file includes the properties of the wall material, the ignition source and 
the output parameters. Also, the geometry has been modified significantly as there were many 
simplifications in the original Eco-Island FDS file.

7.5 DISCUSSION OF THE MODEL 
Modelling in FDS involves certain simplifications, which can influence the results. Some of the 
assumptions and simplifications include: 
• 
Lack of fuel: The accommodation space is modelled without furniture. There will be 101 
seats located in the accommodation space which would further supply the room with 
fuel and increased smoke production, which could potentially decrease the evacuation 
time even more. 
• 
Initial fuel: the assumption of FRP being the initial fuel includes the assumption of an 
initial fuel size. When fire spreads in an enclosure it will ignite materials starting at one 
==PAGE:70==
 
56 
 
point and then developing further through the material. The chosen size of the initial 
fuel being 1 m2 is an assumption and it might take some time for the fire to spread 
across the entirety of that area. Though, an analysis of the fuel size being 0.25 m2 
instead of 1 m2 showed similar results.  
• 
Ventilation: there is not any ventilation or window openings/breakings modelled in the 
FDS file. If this was included, it would increase the oxygen level which would possibly 
react with the fuel and increase the severe of the fire development. It would though 
keep the enclosure fire well-ventilated for a longer time, which would decrease the 
toxicity in the room as well as the visibility. 
• 
Smoke- and heat detectors: these are not included in the model as it is assumed these 
are not functional when the door-closing device system does not function either. A 
sprinkler system would most likely start when the temperature reaches 57°C at a heat 
detector, which would decrease the heat release and extend the available egress time. 
• 
Material properties: the conductivity, specific heat capacity and density were defined as 
constants for the materials. These parameters will vary with temperature, which makes 
the inputs parameters less accurate. 
An improved model could be suggested for further studies which include interior, ventilation conditions 
and different initial fuels. It should not necessarily include smoke- and heat detectors, as a conservative 
and critical situation is when these are not functional, as well as the doors between the accommodation 
spaces are not functional. The results from the FDS simulations are assumed to be representative for an 
enclosure fire on a smaller passenger ship. 
The results of the simulation could be validated with engineering calculations of smoke layer height and 
gas temperature [45]. This is not done due to project time limitations.

8. EVACUATION 
When a fire occurs on a ship, the accident may develop to critical conditions so that the passengers 
need to evacuate. The design of the ship needs to be evaluated in order to secure a sufficient available 
safe egress time. IMO has specific requirements for the required safe egress time, which the ship needs 
to comply with. Two different approaches are used to assess the fire safety on board: 
1) IMO’s requirement for maximum evacuation time, where ‘MSC.1/Circ.1238, Guideline for 
evacuation analysis for new and existing passenger ships’ [23] is used as guideline. 
2) The Available Safe Egress Time (ASET) is compared to the Required Safe Egress Time (RSET) 
which is based on a “design fire” specified in the previous section as the most critical fire 
scenario. This approach is typically used for commercial buildings.

Previously, human behavior was seen as immeasurable. Recently more research has been executed on 
the topic, leading to more realistic approaches to include the factor in the calculations. The behavior of 
humans during evacuation relies on a number of factors, which can interact in different ways. Among 
others it relies on the incident scenario, local conditions and information available. There is no available 
method including all these factors, which is an important note when applying a specific method. There 
are four commonly used models to determine the evacuation time [7]: 
1) The application of prescriptive codes. This method focuses on the physical surroundings of the 
fire exit path. This model accounts partly for the performance of the evacuating population. 
Human behavior is excluded. 
2) The performance of an egress trial. A full-scale experiment is executed, where a representative 
population is gathered. 
3) The application of a (computer-based) simulation model. This method focuses on including the 
human behavior in a more realistic way, and different scenarios are simulated to find the egress 
performance. 
4) The application of engineering calculations. Empirical equations are used to determine the 
evacuation movement, also known as the hydraulic method. Amount of people and structural 
surroundings are influential on the results. Individual human behavior of the population is 
excluded in this approach. 
None of the above models include all factors influencing the evacuation time. Method #1 can be too 
restrictive, and project-based data cannot be included. Method #2 is rarely used as the constructions 
are rarely built at the moment where the fire engineering analysis is conducted. Aside from this, the 
method is expensive and it is difficult to achieve sufficient realism in such an event. A computer-based 
model (#3) requires much detailed information to simulate a complex model, which might not be 
available. The hydraulic model (#4) is applied in this project. Limitations to this model should also be 
noticed, including [7]: 
- 
Behaviors as a result of egress are not entirely included. 
- 
The amount of people is an important factor. The peoples’ identity is ignored and they are 
seen as a homogeneous population. 
- 
Movement between different components are considered (e.g., from room to room), rather 
than within the components. 
- 
The results are deterministic and will not be changed unless the assumptions or scenario are 
changed.

Despite these limitations, the hydraulic model can be successfully applied for most evacuation 
scenarios, and is known as the most sophisticated engineering model for this purpose. Two versions of 
the hydraulic model exist: first order and second order. The first order is a simplified approach which 
requires several assumptions. The controlling factor (the most severe constraint on the flow of people) 
is used to determine the movement time. The total time between reaching the controlling component 
until the last person has reached safety from the controlling component is determined, which 
represents *. 
The second-order hydraulic model sums up the movement time in between all structural components 
in the escape path, and is therefore more precise. This requires fewer assumptions and more 
calculations, and is the method applied for the scenario on the Eco-Island ferry. Travel- and queue time 
are determined with the approach in the Fire Engineering Design Guide [24, p. 223], which can be seen 
in Appendix G. 
The pre-evacuation time for an enclosure is given by ‰‡* = ‰‡*ƒ + ‰‡*££ according to Purser [67], 
which is the sum of the time to the first people to respond and the 99th percentile to respond. It is not 
appropriate to use the average pre-evacuation time, as the 99th percentile is often much longer. In the 
case of fire in the accommodation space, it will be visual just after ignition, and it is therefore assumed 
that all people will respond at the same time.

10. 
DISCUSSION 
This chapter includes a summary of the most important factors influencing the results and conclusions 
in this thesis. The discussion is divided into the three main topics: Experiment, FDS modelling and 
Evacuation, and further discussion of how the fire safety can possibly be increased by different means.  
10.1 
EXPERIMENT 
Several reaction-to-fire properties were determined with the cone calorimeter experiment performed 
on a specific FRP material. This method is a widely acknowledged approach to determine the heat 
release rate and other fire properties, but several factors and assumptions influences the results. The 
heat release rate was found with Janssens’ method (the oxygen consumption method), which is only 
approximate. For accurate results, the chemical composition is required and the net heat of 
combustion, which can be determined with a bomb calorimeter. An important factor on several fire 
parameters is the ventilation conditions, which in the experiment was well-ventilated. This was due to 
the air flow rate of the exhaust air which was relatively high, to avoid being exposing to hazardous air 
conditions during the experiment. The ventilation conditions will change continuously in a fire 
enclosure, and the properties will therefore vary as well. This influences the input parameters used in 
FDS, as those are modelled as fixed parameters. 
Some of the results varied a lot from sample to sample which indicates that the material has not been 
completely homogenous. More samples would have been necessary to test to avoid this being a 
significant source of error. In some cases it was difficult to compare the results from the experiments to 
values in the literature as the FRP materials vary a lot in composition and fiber content. The comparison 
of the results have in some cases only been to one layer of FRP (the outer layer), whereas the tested 
material consisted of three layers with PVC as core material. 
10.2 
FDS MODELLING 
The interior and a design fire of an accommodation space were modelled in a fictional passenger ship to 
determine the time available to evacuate. Some material properties found with the cone calorimeter 
experiment was used as input parameters. FDS modelling is in general a demanding tool as many input 
parameters can influence the results more than would be expected. Therefore, it is essential to perform 
a verification and sensitivity analysis. 
For design purposes, two different set-ups of the model was simulated; the first one with the only 
purpose of finding the time to ignition of the FRP walls. The second set-up had the purpose of 
determining the ASET including ignition time of the FRP walls, found with the first model. This is a quite 
rough assumption, and many parameters must be assessed thoroughly to ensure sufficient output 
results. The design was tested with varying input parameters which showed that these had a great 
influence on the results but also showed that the first assumed set-up was a conservative model. 
The model could be further improved and become more detailed by modelling the furniture inside the 
accommodation space. This will act like additional fuel and furtherly decrease the ASET. The model 
could also have been tested with different ventilation conditions. As people will be evacuated through 
the door to the Ro-Ro deck, those will most likely be open and add oxygen to the enclosure which 
==PAGE:80==
 
66 
 
would results in a more severe heat development. This would also result in a decreased amount of 
smoke and, therefore, increased visibility, which was seen as being the determining parameter for 
evacuation. The results could be even furtherly validated with engineering calculations of the smoke 
layer height and gas temperature to ensure sufficient results. 
10.3 
EVACUATION 
The evacuation was evaluated by the method defined by IMO and the method used for commercial 
buildings. The IMO approach is a method where the time for total evacuation is determined, which 
should be lower than 60 minutes. The travel time in the calculations only included the passengers 
travelling to the Ro-Ro deck. If the method should have been used feasibly it should also include the 
travel time to the MES systems, to ensure that the people are completely evacuated from the ship. It is 
still evaluated that the time would not exceed the 60 minutes limit as it is a relatively small ship. This 
approach does not seem entirely suitable for the purpose of evaluating the safety on board the FRP 
ship as the conditions will be critical much faster than one hour. Therefore, the evacuation analysis also 
included the comparison of ASET and RSET. The travel time of the RSET was found with the hydraulic 
model which includes several assumptions and limitations. The approach does not include individual 
factors as travel speed and time to respond to an evacuation situation which will vary a lot with e.g. 
age. This would, in real life, be a vital factor and can be included with computer simulations. The model 
assumes that the sum of movement between components (doors/stairs/hallways) results in the 
evacuation time, which would overlap in real life. This could have been accounted for with multiplying 
factors (as in the IMO approach), but is a conservative method of determining the travel time. 
If the evacuation time is assessed to be critical (for example be in an FRP ship), there will in some cases 
be conducted a full-scale evacuation simulation before the ship is allowed to sail on its intended route. 
A representative group of people is gathered on the ship, and a full-scale evacuation simulation is 
performed to evaluate if the time match the expected. This could be an advantage on the Eco-Island 
ferry, where the fire safety is a very essential issue. 
The full process in this thesis can advantageously have use in other scenarios, including the three main 
elements; 1) the cone calorimeter experiment to determine fire properties of material(s), 2) using those 
properties as input parameters in FDS and determine the ASET, and 3) determine the RSET with 
engineering calculations or by computer simulations. As a final conclusion, compare ASET and RSET and 
determine the evacuation safety level. This full investigation process can possibly be used for other 
constructions, especially when implementing new materials, e.g. in airplanes or commercial buildings. 
10.4 
PROPOSALS FOR INCREASING THE FIRE SAFETY 
The fire safety was not found to be adequate on a small passenger ship, with the layout and materials 
tested in this project. Several initiatives can be taken in order to increase the fire safety on an FRP ship, 
and still maintain the light weight as the most important benefit of the material. 
- 
Addition of fire retardants to the material, which can possibly reduce flammability and delay 
the combustion process. This can be done by different means, and the type of retardant 
determines in which stage of combustion it interferes [7, pp. 229–231]: 
==PAGE:81==
 
67 
 
• 
Chemical agents can be used to interfere in the gas phase or the solid phase during 
the combustion process. In the gas phase the exothermic process is interrupted by 
the retardant which prevents flaming. Retardants which react in the solid phase can 
accelerate the breakdown of the polymer, which result in dripping or additional 
formation of char on the surface. The latter was seen in the experiment as 
suppressing the heat release rate and soot formation. 
• 
Physical action is another way to add fire retardants. This can be done with an 
endothermic process, which cools the substrate so that the combustion process 
cannot proceed. It can also be done by adding a protective solid layer on the material 
which acts like a shield and prevents the gaseous phase to take place. The surface is 
also protected from radiation and oxidization. The last physical action is dilution, 
where an inert substance or additive is added, to prevent that the lower ignition 
temperature of the gas mixture is not exceeded. Additives are often used in 
thermoplastics, and could with advantageously be used in the FRP material. 
Unfortunately fire retardants are expensive and are only added in order to meet regulatory 
requirements [7, pp. 229–231]. 
- 
A more comprehensive guideline for ships consisting of FRP. The SOLAS Regulation 17 is a 
very general approach, and there has not yet been developed a detailed standard with the 
purpose of designing the fire safety on an FRP ship. The legislation should be more suitable to 
which materials are used. 
- 
More focus on training the crew in the fire-fighting technique and evacuation management. 
The crew on board the small ships mentioned early in the thesis was trained to fire-fight on a 
steel ship. Therefore, the training needs to be more appropriate to an FRP ship. 
- 
Fire safety systems with back-up systems to decrease the risk of them not functioning. 
- 
The risk could also be decreased by using lower amounts of the materials. For example use 
FRP as bulkheads and steel as the deck divisions. This would also possibly delay fire spread 
from a lower level machine room to a higher level accommodation space. The approximation 
of the amount of material burned away within the ASET showed that very small amounts of 
unprotected FRP can lead to very inexpedient fire safety conditions. A mix of fire retardants 
and a mix of materials could be advantageously. 
A common practice of using FRP materials in the maritime industry does not yet exist. Weather 
conditions, regulations and economy are some of the speculations of using FRP material. Knowledge 
about handling the legislation and the economic potential should be spread if the wish is to increase 
the use of FRP material in the maritime industry. Also the high durability compared to steel should lead 
to a more positive opinion of the material [6]. But before spreading the use of FRP, it would be an 
advantage to update the current legislation. Also a comprehensive risk analysis of the FRP ships should 
be conducted to decrease an unnecessary risk to human lives on maritime vessels. 
It should be evaluated if the additional time spent to design an FRP ship, and the investment in 
increasing the fire safety is worth the change of the material. The cost depends on a variety of factors 
such as composite type, fabrication process and the use of additional fire retardants and fire insulation. 
This should be taken into account when deciding if the ship material should be steel or FRP. The 
reduction of maintenance and fuel consumption should also be taken into consideration when deciding.

CONCLUSION 
New light-weight materials used in the maritime sector have been investigated in this thesis in relation 
to the risk posed to human lives, if a fire accidently occurs on a ship. The lightweight materials are 
sandwich composites consisting of Fiber Reinforced Plastic (FRP) and a thermosetting polymer 
surrounding a lightweight plastic core. The materials are used as low-impact materials to make the 
maritime vessels more sustainable. The requirements of reaction-to-fire properties are few for these 
materials, as most regulations are based on steel. Only limited research has been carried out on the 
topic, and lack of knowledge leads to inexpedient solutions, even though the material has been used 
several years for smaller vessels and HSC ships. 
The main issue about using FRP instead of steel is the fact that FRP is combustible. A risk-based analysis 
has to be conducted, which includes a preliminary analysis, a quantitative analysis and a risk analysis. 
The preliminary analysis defines the ship and the equipment of the ship, and defines possible fire 
scenarios. The quantitative analysis defines in details, the fire scenario(s) and specifies design fire(s). 
Also the detection- and alarm systems are defined, as well as the location of ignition(s). A risk analysis 
has to be made in order to evaluate if the performance criteria are complied with, and if the evaluated 
fire scenario represents the most severe situations. If this is not the case, the design of the ship should 
be modified, and the full risk-based analysis should be followed once again. 
FRP composite materials can vary a lot, as different resin can be used; varying amount and directions of 
fibers, as well as varying core materials and molding process. All these factors define the properties of 
the sandwich panel. A specific FRP material has been investigated to evaluate if the use of this material 
results in a higher risk to human life, if a fire accidently occurs on a ship. The tested FRP was a sandwich 
panel consisting of 1.3 mm polyester/glass fiber laminates and a 50 mm core of PVC which was 
delivered to DTU from RISE in Sweden. The first part of the investigation was an evaluation of the toxic 
fire emissions, which was based on the chemical composition and decomposition of the individual 
materials. Polyester and PVC emits several toxic gasses as combustion products including CO, CO2, HCl 
and different types of PAH particles, which is carcinogenic gas particles. The amount of gasses and 
toxicity depends a lot on the ventilation conditions, i.e. the amount of available oxygen. 
The FRP sandwich panel was tested in accordance to the cone calorimeter method in DS/ISO 5660-
1:2015 [44], where it was exposed to different heat flux. The experiment was carried out to determine 
several properties of the material. As expected, the concentration of O2 decreased, and CO and CO2 
increased in the combustion air, as a result of the combustion chemistry. The heat release rate was 
found with the oxygen consumption method. The theory behind this approach is that an average 
amount of heat is released per unit mass of oxygen consumed for complete combustion. The average 
heat release rate was found to 372 kW/m2. This seemed high when comparing to similar materials. The 
reason of this could be due to the fact that the sandwich panel consisted of three layers, and was 
compared to FRP only consisting of one layer. 
Thermal properties was tried to be determined with the theory that the properties is dependent on the 
time to ignition, incident and critical heat flux, ambient temperature and surface temperature at 
ignition. The results did not give reliable values and could not be used further in the study. One reason 
of this was probably the mix of the two materials, which does not have the same thermal properties. 
==PAGE:83==
 
69 
 
The mass loss rate was found to increase with increased incident heat flux, which was expected. The 
mass loss rate varied throughout the experiment and was highest immediately after ignition and then, 
decreased over time. During the combustion, there was formed a layer of char on the surface of the 
sample. As a result of this layer, the mass loss rate, soot yield and heat release rate decreased. 
The toxicity was determined in the unit grams of soot produced per unit mass of fuel consumed (g/g). 
The average soot yield was assumed to be in the steady state burning period, which varied for the 
different samples. The average of all the samples’ steady state period was found to be 0.17 g/g. The 
soot yield of similar materials was found to both higher and lower values in the literature, than the 
value found in the experiment. It is difficult to compare the soot yield to table values as it depends a lot 
on the ratio between the oxidizer and fuel. The conditions in the experiment were found to be well-
ventilated with an estimation of the equivalence ratio (-) below 1. 
It was found that combustion behavior of materials depends on many factors, such as the ventilation 
conditions, chemical composition of the fuel, size of the fuel and the exposed heat flux. As the results 
varied significantly from sample to sample, it indicates that the surface laminates and/or the layer of 
glue have been inhomogeneous. 
Reaction-to-fire properties found in the experiment have been used as inputs in the Fire Dynamic 
Simulator program as well as table values from the literature. The simulations have been used to 
determine the available time until critical conditions occurs, also known as Available Safe Egress Time 
(ASET). A specific passenger ship was used for the purpose: the Eco-Island ferry. This is a fictional FRP 
ship, used in earlier research of FRP materials on ships. The accommodation space of the ship was 
found to be the most critical area, as the passengers will reside here. A chair was assumed to be a 
realistic design fire, and the location of ignition was set closest to the FRP walls, which were assessed as 
the most critical scenario. 
It was assumed that two base models set-ups were adequate to determine the ASET. One set-up to 
determine the time to ignition of the FRP walls, the other to determine the time till untenable 
conditions occur. The first simulation showed that the walls would ignite after approximately 30 
seconds after ignition of the chair. The second set-up showed that the most critical parameter was the 
visibility. The ASET was found to be 63 seconds on the critical side of the accommodation space where 
the ignition source was located, and 103 seconds on the other side. An investigation of model 
parameters was conducted as a sensitivity analysis, which showed that the first model set-up was a 
conservative set-up. 
The IMO approach to evaluate the evacuation time was used to determine the level of fire safety on the 
Eco-Island ferry. With this approach, the requirement was met on the ferry. This approach was not 
evaluated to be adequate to a small passenger ship such as the Eco-Island ferry, as many assumptions 
was taken and the only requirement is a 60 minutes limit, which is not appropriate. Therefore, an 
approach used for commercial buildings was conducted as well. 
The Required Safe Egress Time (RSET) was determined with a widely acknowledged approach used for 
commercial buildings to be able to compare this to the ASET. The detection, alarm and decision time 
was conservatively assumed to be 50 seconds. The travel- and queue time were determined with the 
hydraulic model; a widely used and well-defined method for the purpose. The RSET was found to be 
163 seconds to get people from the critical side to the other side, and 238 seconds in total, to get 
==PAGE:84==
 
70 
 
people from the critical side to the Ro-Ro deck. Only 33 of 55 passengers will reach the second side of 
the accommodation space before critical conditions occur. It should be noted that this is not the same 
as to conclude that the people on the critical side will die. The Evacuation Safety Level (ESL) was found 
to <1, which does not comply with the requirements. 
It can be concluded that the safety on board the Eco-Island ferry is not adequate. The risk to human life 
is too high in the event of a fire when using FRP materials as main construction material, with the 
assumptions taken in this thesis. If the ship was going to be built in reality, modifications of the design 
should be implemented to improve the fire safety, and the full risk analysis as described in section 3 
should be followed once again. It can also be concluded that the requirements for fire safety on FRP 
ships is not strict enough, as the regulations are very vague and inadequate. 
11.1 
FURTHER STUDIES 
This project has emphasized the importance of analysing the effects on fire safety when introducing 
FRP as construction material on ships. However, the topic can be furtherly investigated, as 
simplifications and assumptions have been made in this study. Further studies could include: 
- 
Improvement of regulations for FRP ships. SOLAS regulation 17 is too general compared to 
the requirements for steel ships. The statement that the level of safety on an FRP ship should 
be equivalent to a steel ship is very unspecific, and the analysis requires many assumptions. 
- 
Different types of ships could be investigated, i.e. larger passenger ships, High speed crafts 
and cargo ships. The interior of the space could be simulated differently and with varying 
amount of passengers and geometry. 
- 
Other materials could be investigated with the cone calorimeter method. Different types of 
FRP and also separate materials instead of sandwich panels. 
- 
Another type of lightweight material that could be used for maritime vessels is aluminum 
honeycomb which can be seen on Figure 46. This is also a lightweight material with high 
strength and has good fire-resistance. The disadvantage of this material is the price. 
 
Figure 46 – Alu honeycomb [69] 
- 
The evacuation analysis could be verified by a full-scale test. This is done by gathering a 
representative group of people on the ship before it sails on its intended route. 
- 
Full scale tests of the fire development of an enclosure with FRP as wall materials, and with 
different types of fire retardants and fire insulation. 
- 
This study does not include research on the structural strength with temperature of the 
composites. This is possibly an important factor when determining the available time to 
egress as the FRP will lose its structural strength quicker than steel in a fire scenario.
