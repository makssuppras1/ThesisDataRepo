Abstract
This report investigates the prediction of torsional performance related to wind turbine
blades utilizing Finite Element Modeling. Four studies investigate increasingly more
complex features of modeling torsion. The performance of the model methods is evaluated
against analytical, numerical, and experimental data. This report finds solid and shell
elements capable of predicting torsional response in simple geometry. However, the studies
reveal limitations in the shell element’s ability to accurately predict torsion when sharp
intersections in the geometry are present. The results indicate, among other things, a
relation between the erroneous behavior and the modeling with shell in non-zero offset
configurations. A comparison to a full-scale turbine blade reveals inaccurate predictions
of torsion using shell elements, while solid elements perform well. Modeling techniques
are proposed to improve torsional prediction for shell models based on the findings in
the studies. This report primarily utilizes ABAQUS 2022 in its endeavor to explore the
torsional performance of wind turbine blades.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The demand for renewable energy sources, such as wind turbines, has never been greater.
In 2021 electricity generation by wind increased by 17%, making it the highest-growing
renewable energy source according to the International Energy Agency [1]. At the same
time, the wind turbine blades are reaching record breaking lengths of up to 123 meters [2]
as the quest for greater energy output pr. wind turbine continues.
As the blades become longer and more slender, the mode shapes producing torsion become
a more important aspect of structural analysis[3]. The natural frequency associated with
the torsional mode shapes is getting closer to the lowest natural frequency of the entire
blade, meaning that this mode can become a design constraint. Furthermore, inaccurate
prediction of twist, which is related to the angle of attack (AOA), can lead to sub-optimal
energy production as it influences the turbine blade’s overall efficiency [3]. Thus, accurate
prediction of the torsional behavior of wind turbine blades is, therefore, more important
than ever.
However, many common modeling techniques for structural analysis utilizing the Finite
Element Method (FEM) are seen to yield high deviation in the prediction of the torsional
behavior of the blades[4][5][6]. These modeling techniques, which primarily utilizes shell
elements, are seen to be more compliant in torsion than benchmark results yielding devia-
tion in angle-of-twist up to 45.5%[4] for a full scale turbine blade in torsion.
This study seeks to investigate the modern practice of representing the three-dimensional
structure of wind turbine blades in Finite Element (FE) models using shell and solid
elements. Four individual numerical studies performed in this report aim to explore the
current accuracy of these element types in predicting torsion and propose solutions to
mitigate problems. The work is performed with commercial use in mind, which will be
reflected in the proposed solutions.

1.2
Literature Review
Current Modelling of Wind Turbine Blades in Torsion
A wind turbine blade is comprised of an airfoil, which shape is highly dictated by
aerodynamic calculations[4], and an inner structure called a shear web, which adds rigidity
to the blade. The blade is built from composite materials[4] consisting of layers of fiber
materials with different material properties. In Figure 1.1 a cross-section of a wind turbine
blade is illustrated.
Figure 1.1: Illustration of a cross-section of a wind turbine blade with marking of trailing
edge, shear web, and airfoil. An illustration of a composite layup is also given in the
top-right corner.
In FEM, this cross-section can be represented using either solid or shell elements as
illustrated in Figure 1.2. As shell elements are less computationally expensive they are
preferable for the modeling of wind turbine blades and commonly seen in literature in
papers like Tarfaoui et al. [7], Banner et al.[5], Peeters et al.[8] and Noever-Castelos et al.
[4].

(b)
Figure 1.2: (a) Illustration of a FE mesh with solid element. (b) Illustration of a FE mesh
with shell elements.
The thickness and layup of airfoils vary across the span of the blade, as seen in Figure 1.1,

due to structural requirements. Modeling these changes is important because they greatly
affect the stiffness of the turbine blade.
Shell elements are based on shell theory, as described in Section 2.1.2. Each element
is defined by a 2D reference plane located at the center of the thickness. When using
shell elements, it is important to align these reference planes or discontinuities may occur,
making the modeling more complex[9]. This is shown in Figure 1.3b, where the airfoil has
a reference plane in the middle, and a thickness change abrupts the alignment.
To address this issue, some FE analysis software enable the reference plane to be offset
to the top of the thickness[10][11], ensuring they are all aligned as illustrated in Figure 1.3a.
The reference plane configurations will be referred to as zero-offset (ZO) when the reference
plane is placed in the middle and top-offset (TO) on the top of the element thickness.
(a)
(b)
Figure 1.3: Illustration adapted from [9]. Black dots illustrate the kinematic reference
plane/nodes and the shell thickness visualized with gray. (a) Top-offset (TO) configuration.
(b) Zero-offset (ZO) configuration.
As the geometry of the airfoil from aerodynamic optimization is often given as the outer
circumference shape[4], it is convenient to use shell element with TO configuration, as the
reference plane stays the same even with changes in thickness as seen in Figure 1.3. The
ZO configuration can also be utilized but is more cumbersome when the thickness of the
airfoil change, as seen in Figure 1.3b. These issues are not a problem if solid elements are
utilized, as they discretize the entire 3D structure with the ability to change thickness
from one end to the other in the element.

All of the above authors report errors between 30-45.5% in AOT when shells with TO are

Studies into Torsional Response of Shell Elements
In the literature, a few papers are found that investigates the problem of shell elements’
torsional response when modeling turbine blade.
Paper by Laird et al. from 2005
In 2005, Laird et al.[9] published a paper where they compare the performance of shell
elements in different configurations when modeling a simple isotropic hollow cylinder to
analytical solutions. Here they reported noticeable differences in the response of shell
elements with TO and ZO when utilizing the FE analysis software ANSYS. In their
convergence study for torsional loading, they found that the TO shell elements were seen
to diverge and produce relative errors of up to 70% in shear stress. The ZO shell element
was seen to converge and to be in good alignment with the analytical results. Laird et al.
also performed an aspect ratio study, where the TO shells were seen to reach errors of to
80% in shear stress. Laird et al. conclude that using the TO shell elements for torsional
loads is inaccurate.
Paper by Tavares et al. from 2022
The 2022 paper by Tavares et al.[6] investigates the behavior of shell elements for modeling
wind turbine blades when subjected to torsional loads in ABAQUS 2021. Analogously to
Laird et al., they performed a study on a hollow isotropic cylinder subjected to torsion,
where they found no difference in angle-of-twist between TO shell elements and analytical
results. By replacing the isotropic material properties with a composite layup, they do,

however, find a difference in angle-of-twist for linear TO shell elements. They state that
"artificial deformation modes" in the shell elements are the reason for this deviation.
Tavares et al. also try to mitigate the error by changing the drill stiffness of the shell

blade noticeably for TO shell elements. They show that they can reduce the error in TO
models from 35% to 5% by increasing the ABAQUS 2021 default drilling stiffness by 500
times. They conclude that the ABAQUS default drill stiffness is to low for the TO shell
elements.
1.3
Problem Statement
This project aims to investigate and improve the torsional performance of Finite Element
simulations when modeling wind turbine blades. The project seeks to answer the following
questions:
1. What is the current practice when modeling wind turbine blades with respect to
torsional performance?
2. What is the current state of performance when simulating torsion with Finite Element
Modeling utilizing ABAQUS 2022?
3. What aspect(s) in torsional simulation can decrease modeling performance?
4. How can the current modeling technique(s) be improved to enhance the accuracy of
the torsional performance?
5. How can the enhancements be implemented for commercial use of wind turbine blade
modeling?
1.4
Thesis Outline
This thesis will approach the problem by using a ’simple-to-complex’ practice. Thus,
starting with an evaluation of simpler models, from which complexity is added gradually,
is deemed to make the origin of errors easier to identify.
The first study, the Element Study, will evaluate the performance of different element
types on a simple hollow cylinder. This study aims to explore how capable the elements
can model simple structures, as this is reported to be a problem by Laird et. al[9] for some
Page 5 of 132

shell elements. The study will therefore map the performance of relevant elements that
could be used to model full-scale wind turbine blades and thus contribute to answering
question 2. in the problem statement
Next, the Geometry Study will add a layer of complexity by evaluating the elements’
behavior on a square hollow beam. This is meant to resemble the closed geometry between
the shear webs as depicted in Figure 4.1a. Here in-depth analysis of the modeled stress
state will be performed, along with the influence of various settings of the element, for the
elements found to be erroneous. Thus, the study will contribute to answering questions 2.
and 3. in the problem statement.
In the Experimental Validation Study the performance of the elements in simulation on
a full complex model of wind turbine blade will be evaluated, along with validation of
the results against experimental data. The study will therefore uncover how the elements
perform compared to recorded data and if the magnitude of the erroneous behavior found
in the Element Study and Geometry Study are present for a full complex model. This
study will contribute to answering questions 1. and 2. in the problem statement.
In the Correction Study, methods will be proposed that attempt to mitigate or overcome
the errors found so far in terms of torsional behavior. This is conducted by modeling
a prismatic cross-section of the full turbine blade model utilized in the Experimental
Validation. The behavior of the model with the proposed methods is compared to a

2.1
The Finite Element Method and Element Types
The Finite Element Method (FEM) is used to determine the structural response of a
structure. For instance, the stresses and strains of an object subjected to an external load
can be approximated using the method for highly complex structures, such as a turbine
wing blade, where no analytical solutions exist. Mathematically, the method solves a
partial differential equation (principle of virtual work) by discretizing a domain into smaller
sections using different element types [12]. In its simplest static form, Finite Element
Analysis (FE analysis) relates external forces to internal displacements of a domain through
the use of a stiffness matrix:
{F} = [K]{d}
(2.1)
Thus, given, for instance, an external load {F} the displacement at certain locations {d}
in the elements called nodes can be calculated, strains and stresses are found at integration
points via constitutive relations, e.g., Hooke’s law. From Equation 2.1, it is evident that
the stiffness matrix [K] is of great importance, as it dictates the relation between force and
displacements, much like a spring coefficient. The geometry dictates the shape and content
of the stiffness matrix (shape of the object) and the element type formulation, which makes
the choice of element an essential part of any FE analysis. Furthermore, the solver used
to find the solution to, e.g., Equation 2.1 also has a great effect on, e.g., computational
time and may vary depending on whether a linear/non-linear, static or dynamic situation
is to be simulated. In this report, only static linear analyses are considered.
Finite Element Types
Elements in FEM are like building blocks on a construction site; they come in various
shapes and forms, have different properties, and have a general or specific application. In
the terminology of structural mechanics, for instance, some elements are good at modeling
curves shape, some are well suited for representing bending loads, and some are specifically
designed to, e.g., the representation of thin plates. When performing FE analysis, all these
characteristics must be considered to select the optimal element.

==PAGE:18==

January 28, 2023
Figure 2.1: Illustration of the different aspects of an element.
The following aspects often categorize element types in FE analysis [12]; 1) how they rep-
resent the structure (2D/3D/structural), 2) which degrees-of-freedom (d.o.f.) they possess
(displacement, rotational), 3) if they are linear or quadratic elements (shape function),
4) how the element is formulated and 5) whether or not full- or reduced integration is
utilized[10]. These all dictate the behavior and performance of a given element.
1) Representation of Structure
In FE analysis, two- or three-dimensional structures are the most common geometries to
be considered. However, to conduct the analysis, a geometry, e.g., a CAD model, has to
be converted into a FE model consisting of nodes and element definitions. Some elements
represent a structure in 2D, some in full 3D, and others represent a full 3D beam structure
in terms of a 1D line element. An important aspect of FE analysis is how the element can
represent a certain geometry, which dictates the analysis’s input format.
2) Degrees-of-freedom
FE analysis results are presented in a displacement field expressed in the degree-of-
freedoms d.o.f. of an element. Some elements only have translations d.o.f., and some are a
combination of translations and rotational d.o.f., which are defined in certain directions.
Choosing an element with the correct d.o.f. is important in interpreting the output of an
analysis.
3) Linear/Quadratic Formulation
The sides of an element, the connection between nodes, can either be a straight or curved
line. More mathematically, the sides/element is formulated by shape functions, which can
be either linear or quadratic in their expression. Quadratic elements (with curved sides) are
often better at representing curved structures and converge faster but are computationally
more expensive than linear elements - however, linear elements have ’design limits’ that
may cause incorrect stress representation in some load cases. Computational time (CPU
time) is important, so this property must be selected carefully [12].

Master Thesis
January 28, 2023
4) Element Formulation
The mathematical element formulation dictates how the element behave relative to defor-
mation. Thus, choosing an element with the correct mathematical formulation is important
to its behavior.
5) Integration Method
To obtain stresses and strain in an element, element integration is performed in defined
integration points, also known as Gauss Points in the element. E.g., in a solid linear element,
four Gauss points are normally defined. Some elements allow for reduced integration,
which means that the number of integration points is scaled down. This decreases CPU
time but can lead to other problems, such as hourglassing, etc., discussed in the later
sections.

2.1.2
Conventional Shell Elements
Conventional shell elements (shell elements) have been developed to model structures
where one side is far smaller than the other. Suppose 3D continuum solids are to be
modeled thin in only one direction. In that case, they will have problems of ill conditions
and shear locking, solved by using many compact 3D elements, which cause a FE structure
of far too many elements. The use of shell elements avoids the problem of too many
elements.
General shell theory
The following section introduces the mathematical model used to describe the behavior of
shell elements with the theoretical basics from the textbook by Cook R.D. [12].
Many different shell theories exist, but the primary software used in this report is ABAQUS

based on a Mindlin type of first-order deformation theory (FSDT). Therefore, the FSDT
is the only presented shell theory.
A shell is considered a plate with a curvature that supports both in-plane and lateral loads.
In the FSDT theory, the shell is considered a two-dimensional continuum, defined by the
reference plane placed in the middle of the thickness and a shell normal.
The displacement field is represented with a total of five d.o.f. at each nodal point of
the element as illustrated in Figure 2.5a for a linear shell element. This includes three

When meshing linear shell elements together, ABAQUS uses two methods to determine

as:
• Average Shell Normal
• Separate Shell Normal
In the ’Average Shell Normal’ method, the adjoining elements’ shell normals are projected

default in ABAQUS if the adjoining shell normals are within 20o of each other [10]. This
situation is illustrated in Figure 2.10a.

than 20o out of alignment. Then no averaging occurs, and the adjoined elements will
have their own separated shell normal. This is illustrated in Figure 2.10b. In most cases,
a refined mesh can reduce the difference between the shell normals to below 20o. If it
cannot, it means that a single node is shared by multiple elements with different shell
normals, indicating the presence of a fold line between the elements [10]. According to the
ABAQUS manual, this can lead to a conflict in the node as the shell’s normal direction is
needed for the nodal stress output.

Coupling Constraints in ABAQUS
A general coupling constraint involves a reference node that controls the motion of a group
of coupling nodes.
Surface-based Coupling Constraint
ABAQUS 2022 offers a surface-based coupling constraint called Continuum coupling that
controls the coupling nodes on a surface [10]. This method distributes the forces and/or
moments from the reference node to the coupling nodes by means of forces. The coupling
allows for relative motion between the reference node and the coupling nodes, as well as
between the coupling nodes themselves, allowing for warping at the boundary applied. As
demonstrated in the ABAQUS manual [10], continuum coupling can prescribe a twist at a
reference node to allow the coupling nodes to warp, as shown in the figure in Figure 2.14.
Another surface-based coupling constraint in ABAQUS 2022 is a structural coupling, but
as mentioned in ABAQUS manual [10], these two are identical when enforced with respect
to rotation along the twist direction.
Figure 2.14: Figure from ABAQUS manual [10], show a continuum coupling example
to prescribe a displacement and rotation condition on a boundary. The structure has a
prescribed moment of twist in a reference node, and this moment is transmitted to the
coupling nodes where motion between the nodes is permitted.

When discretizing a structure into FE elements, some areas may need to be modeled

Mesh Tie Coupling
Tie coupling is used to connect two or more parts of a structure together [10]. It creates a
set of equations that tie the displacement of the nodes on one surface to the displacement
of the nodes on the other. This can be used to model situations where two parts of a
structure are in contact and must move together, such as a beam resting on a foundation.
The tie constraint is also a surface-based coupling, where the displacement of the coupling
nodes is constrained to be equal to the displacement of the reference nodes. Tie constraints
can be used with solid and shell elements and can be defined in various ways.

Element Study
This chapter investigates the performance of a selected group of element types to evaluate
how the elements perform when utilized to model a simple hollow cylinder subjected to
torsion and bending loads.
3.1
Introduction
The Element Study aims to answer the 2. question in the problem statement by determin-
ing the current state of performance for an array of different element types, primarily in
ABAQUS 2022 and selected cases in ANSYS 2020 APDL Mechanical.
As the ’simple-to-complex’ practice is used in this report, the first study will evaluate
the individual element’s performance on a simple hollow cylinder. The cylinder have
isotropic material properties for which closed-formed analytical solutions exist as described
in Section 2.3 and 2.4. The element types are tested in three different load scenarios; 1)
torsion, 2) bending-by-point load, and 3) bending-by-moment (pure bending).
The element types’ performance is evaluated in three individual sub-studies:
1. Number of Elements
2. Aspect Ratio
3. Computational Time
The first two studies look at the element types’ responses in stress and displacement at
certain locations with the number of elements used in the FE model and the element aspect
ratio as dependent parameters. Then the computational time (CPU time) is evaluated for
each FE model to provide insight into the cost of each element type.
As part of the study, the effects of boundary conditions are investigated to ensure the
validity of the study’s results.
This study is highly motivated by the work performed by Laird et al. [9] from 2005, which
shows that TO shell elements in torsion yield high deviations from analytical solutions

Validity of Study

Model Generation Technique
In the results for the Number of Element study (Section 3.3.2) in torsion, the model with

That is, if the longest side of the element is in the circumferential direction, as is the case
for the two models.
Nevertheless, this is not deemed to invalidate this study’s results as the aspect ratio
definition in Equation 2.2 is upheld. Furthermore, the above is unlikely to have affected
the back-to-back comparison of element types as all elements, solid or shell, used the same
type of mesh.
3.4.2
Element Performance
Torsional Performance
For solid elements, errors in shear stress are present for the C3D8R and SC8R elements;
the C3D8R is prone to have hourglassing (spurious mode) which is likely the case in
this instance. As illustrated in Figure 3.10, the element has no shear stress variation
through its thickness. The relative deviation seen for the SC8R element is at 2.6%, which
is in the same range as for some of the shell elements. As the continuum shell SC8R is
formulated on conventional shell element theory, it seems plausible that this error could
be related to the general error seen for shells in Table 3.3. Thus, it may have been more

Performance in Bending
The systematic negative deviation for the shell element types with TO configurations in
bending loads suggests that it is generally too stiff in bending. A non-physical overlap of
the thicknesses between each element exists for at least linear shape formulations. This
indicates, at least geometrically, that double stiffness could exist for TO reference plane
configuration, which may explain the too-stiff behavior observed. This is also suggested
by Vales et al. [21]. This should be less of an issue for quadratic elements as the curved
shape functions allow the shell normal to be parallel to each other faster at its boundaries.
With the reasoning presented above of overlapping stiffness, the behavior of the ABAQUS
element type cannot be logically explained.
For solid elements, C3D8R results indicate it suffers from hourglassing by being too
compliant and not having sufficient normal stress accumulated in the structure. The cause
of this is the single Gauss point in the element, creating the numerical problem as depicted
in Figure 2.4. The underperformance of element type C3D8 in normal stress can be a
symptom of shear locking a problem with all fully integrated, first-order, solid elements,
depicted in Figure 2.3b.
3.4.3
ABAQUS vs. ANSYS
In the screening of selected cases in ANSYS Mechanical 2021, it is seen that some differences
exist compared to the ABAQUS 2022 software. While the element types are assumed
to be built on the same concept (as given in Cook et al.[12]), it is likely that ABAQUS
and ANSYS-specific element formulations are different. While overall properties of the
behavior of the element types are described in user manuals, the direct implementation
cannot be found, as these are presumed trade secrets. Thus, it would be very difficult to
pinpoint why some elements perform better than others across platforms beyond what is
shown in this study.
3.4.4
CPU Solver Time
Although the linear solid elements C3D8R and C3D8 from the performance study seem
like good candidates due to their low computational expense, they are limited by their
need to have a sufficiently low aspect ratio in all three dimensions. This means, that

In this chapter, a numerical study of a square hollow beam is performed. It is investigated

4.1
Introduction
This study continues to investigate the 2. question in the problem statement to determine
the current state of performance of simulation by studying the impact of different modeling
techniques. Moreover, the study will seek to answer the 3. questions in the problem state-
ment by identifying what aspects of torsional simulation can decrease modeling performance.
In the Element Study, the element types tested predict the torsional shear stress to within
2.6% error. This indicates that for a simple circular geometry, the element itself is not the

geometrical dependencies. This is done by modeling a square hollow beam, as depicted in

4.4
Discussion
This section discusses the validity of the reference model and important aspects of the four
sub-studies.
4.4.1
Validity of Reference Model
In torsion, the relative error between the reference model’s maximum shear stress value
and the approximate theories is seen to be between 20% and 5% depending on which
theory is utilized. The solution by Saravanos et al. is in best agreement with the C3D20
model at 5.0% relative error, while the thin-wall approximation by Bredt’s theory yield
19.9%. While 5.0% can be deemed a significant difference, the solutions, to which the FE
model is compared, are all approximate and thus not closed-formed. With this in mind,
5.0% seems reasonable. Thus, the model can be argued to be valid as a reference based on
the rationalization performed.
In bending the model can be deemed to be in good alignment with analytical results as a
deviation of just -0.3% exists to the Bernoulli-Euler theory utilized.
Page 86 of 132
==PAGE:96==
Master Thesis
January 28, 2023
4.4.2
Change of Wall Thickness
A linear progression in error as thickness increases for the FE shell models is discovered.
It can be argued that this error either comes from the theory the elements are based on or
the implementation of non-zero offset configuration by the ABAQUS 2022.
It is believed the answer can be found by looking at the relatively low increase of error in
the FE shell model with ZO compared to models with TO. The models with ZO follow
the theory convention and has no offset added to disrupt the results. Therefore, it can be
argued that the large increase in error as thickness increases is linked to the implementation
of the offset option in the ABAQUS 2022.
4.4.3
Square-to-Circular Cross-Section
By modeling a beam with a cross-section fully square, the adjoining shells at corners
are forced to have two different shell normals at the corner node. This type of geometry
is in this study seen to be associated with a significant deviation in the prediction of
angle-of-twist for shell elements with TO according.
Moreover, the results of linear shell element with TO indicate that the accuracy of
prediction torsion increases as the filet size increases.
The increase in corner fillet size also leads to a decrease in the angle between the adjoining
elements’ shell normals. Hence it can be claimed that the torsional accuracy increase as
this angle decrease. The quadratic shell element S8R with TO further supports this claim
by a drastic fall in angle-of-twist deviation as soon as a corner fillet is used. As the S8R
element has a parabolic-shaped shell edge, it can bend; thus, issues with adjoining shell
normals at the corner will be less.
This behavior can contradictory not be seen in the shell models with ZO configuration.
4.4.4
Shell Offset Configuration
This study shows a significant change in stress pattern for shell elements when offsets
away from zero-offset configuration are applied. Thus based on the results presented, it
can be argued that calculating all the element’s kinematic quantities with a non-zero offset
configuration is an erroneous modeling technique.
Furthermore, in the ABAQUS user manual 2022 [10], it is emphasized to use the offset
option with care as "Large offset values for shells may also lead to a surface integration
error, affecting the stiffness, mass, and rotary inertia for the shell section".

4.5
Sub-conclusion
The Geometry Study seeks to answer the 2. and 3. questions in the problem statement by
investigating simulation errors arising from geometrical dependencies and element settings.
This is conducted through four sub-studies that investigate different aspects of a square
hollow beam subjected to torsion and bending loads.
It is found that:
• In the benchmark performance test of elements (Benchmark of Element Types), the
element types C3D8I, CSS8 and SC8R predicts both torsion and bending behavior
to with ≈|2%| error of the reference model. The linear shell elements with top-offset
configuration are seen to yield up to 28% error in angle-of-twist, with the quadratic
elements performing slightly better at ≈20% error in angle-of-twist. Shell elements
with zero-offset configurations are seen to yield errors of up to 4.5% in angle-of-twist.
A discrepancy is seen between the angle-of-twist simulated and the shear stress,
especially for top-offset shells.
In bending, an error of ≈−14% is seen in deflection and normal stress for the
top-offset elements. Zero-offset shell elements are seen to predict the bending to
within ≈1%. It is furthermore found, that if the results for shell elements are
compared to beam theory representing the stiffness at the intersection as performed
by ABAQUS, the FE results are in much better alignment. This indicates that in
bending a problem of overlapping area stiffness exists at shell intersections.
• In the dependency of wall thickness (Change of Wall Thickness), a linear trend
between error in angle-of-twist and shear stress with increasing wall thickness-to-
width (t/w) ratio is observed in torsion for the shells with TO and BO. In bending,
a similar linear trend is observed with relative errors of up to ≈35% in deflection
error for the shell TO elements with a t/w-ratio of 0.15. Here shells BO are seen to
yield an opposite negative error trend than the TO shell elements. Shell element
with ZO configuration is found to only be slightly influenced by the change in wall
thickness.
• In the torsional performance of models with sharp or blunt corners (Square-to-
Circular Cross-Section), it is found that the error in angle-of-twist is reduced when
the corners become blunter. For linear top-offset elements, this is seen to gradually
reduce the error. On the contrary for the quadratic top-offset shell elements, good
alignment with the reference model is seen as soon as the edge becomes blunt. It is
discussed if a relation between the reduced error and the reduced angle of adjoining
Page 88 of 132
==PAGE:98==
Master Thesis
January 28, 2023
shell normals exists. It is furthermore found that the highest error in shear stress is
present near the corners of the model.
• In the dependency of shell offset configuration (Shell Offset Configuration), it is
shown that the error in angle-of-twist increases if a shell offset value away from ZO
(0.0) is utilized. For the square beam tested, the error is shown to be locally the
lowest at an offset value of -0.8. Further, a stress singularity is observed for top
offset shell elements near the corners. It is discussed whether the use of non-zero
offset configuration, in general, is erroneous with references to the ABAQUS 2022
user manual.
From the Geometry Study it can therefore be concluded, that sharp edge intersections
modeled by shells reduce the performance of torsional simulations. This is in regard to the
prediction of angle-of-twist and shear stress in ABAQUS 2022 for top-offset shell elements.
The use of shell offset configuration is furthermore seen to increase erroneous behavior in
torsion. It can also be concluded that the solid elements tested performed well in modeling
torsion on the test geometry selected.

Experimental Validation
In this chapter, a comparison between FE models and experimental data from static tests
on 12.6m wind turbine blade is performed. This chapter will therefore reveal how well FE
models compare to experimental data for a present modeling technique.
5.1
Introduction
The Experimental Validation study continues to investigate the 1. and 2. question in the
problem statement by comparing FE models in ABAQUS 2022 to experimental data.
In the Experimental Validation study, solid and shell FE models generated by DTU Wind
is compared to experimental data for static tests performed on a 12.6m wind turbine blade.
Here elements previously found to capture torsion and bending behavior well are utilized.
The FE model’s natural frequencies are also generated.
A preliminary study will examine ABAQUS 2022’s ability to model composite laminates
for a test case of a simple plate with layers of uni-direction (UNIAX) and bi-axial (BIAX)
materials. This will highlight if any errors arise in ABAQUS when modeling composite
materials compared to CLT theory. A description of DTU Wind’s process for generating
FE models of the wind turbine blade is also presented in this chapter.
It should be emphasized that both the FE models and experimental data are provided by
DTU Wind, as these activities could not be conducted within the scope of this project.
The methods for retrieving experimental data are provided to give a full overview of the
validation process for the reader. However, the main objective in this study is to determine
the elements performance when simulating a turbine blade in bending and torsion loads.
5.1.1
The Experimental Setup
In this section the experimental setup for the static tests is presented. Senior Development
Engineer at DTU Wind Peter Berring has provided the information.
The static test experiments on a 12.6m turbine blade were performed at the Large Scale
Facility at DTU Risø Campus. Here the wind turbine blade was mounted into a vertical

Experimental Procedure
The following list is a holistic representation of the procedure conducted for each experi-
ment/load case in the static test:
1. The marking paths along the length of the blade in undeformed state are recorded
via the ARAMIS system. Five records of each mark are taken.
2. Positioning of free-hanging weights on the wooden clamps to achieve the desired
load case.
3. Recording of the marking paths in the deformed state along the length of the blade
with five recordings of each mark.
4. Repeat step 1, 2, and 3 until records of four undeformed states and three deformed
are performed.

In Figure 5.4 the load cases tested in the experiment is illustrated. A total of three load
cases were conducted; 1) suction-towards-pressure side loading (STP loading), 2) pure
torsional loading (PT loading), and 3) combined loading (STP-tor loading).

STP loading is defined as a loading condition where the blade is loaded on its pres-
sure/’bottom’ side of the blade (see Figure 5.4a). This can be interpreted as some kind of
direct bending of the blade. In STP-tor loading the load force is offset from the turbine
blade coordinate system’s longitudinal axis, in order to induce both bending and torsion
loads. In PT loading a pure torsion load is applied to the blade utilizing a truss system
as depicted in Figure 5.5. Here a string is run through two pulleys on either side of the
wooden clamp. By fixing one end to a solid fixture, in this case the roof, and suspending
weights from the other end a torsional moment is induced on the blade. This is illustrated
in the top-right corner of Figure 5.5. Note that load values given in Figure 5.4 is recorded
at the load cell for all load cases.

Model Generation
The solid and shell models provided in ABAQUS are generated using DTU Wind’s own
model generation tool. In an Excel spreadsheet, the geometry of the outer airfoil at
different cross sections along the length of the blades are defined. Thus, the blade is ’sliced’
into a certain number of cross-section geometries. These cross-section geometries are
inputted to ABAQUS 2022 through a Python scripting routine. The routine connects each
sketch via the ’Loft’-featuring method to generate the outer geometry of the turbine blade.
The ’Loft’ method takes two-dimensional cross-section areas with different geometries and
fuses them together to form a three-dimensional structure.
Further scripts then generate shear webs, assign the material layup, define section thickness,
etc. Thus, the model is generated automatically from section geometries and material
properties/layups defined in an Excel spreadsheet. Boundary conditions and load are
applied manually. The process is illustrated in a flowchart in Figure 5.7 below.

5.4
Discussion
In this section a discussion of the experimental data quality, ABAQUS’ ability to model
composite, and the performance of the elements is conducted.
5.4.1
ABAQUS Composite Modelling
The relative difference of 0.0% between shells type elements with orthotropic material
properties and CLT theory suggests that the composite modeling in ABAQUS aligns
with CLT in this simple case. Thus the assumption of e.g. neglecting transverse shear
stress in CLT is present for this study. However, the test example "Composite shells in
cylindrical bending" in ABAQUS user manual [10] suggests that the composite modeling
also incorporates FSDT. This suggests that the composite modeling for the shell would
not only align with CLT as seen in this instance but also with theories that take transverse
shear stresses into account.
The CSS8 and C3D8I elements results suggest they utilize another formulation than
CLT as a discrepancy in reaction force and lateral contraction is seen. Furthermore, as
all elements yield perfect results for an isotropic material utilizing the Composite Man-
ager, it can be argued, that the composite manager itself is not the cause of this discrepancy.
While the C3D8I and CSS8 elements yield noticeable deviations, it should be noted that
it is compared to CLT theory which e.g. builds on the assumption of plane stress. Thus
while the results indicate one should be aware of the C3D8I and CSS8 elements’ modeling
performance, this may only be the case for situations where the assumptions for the CLT
theory similarly apply. Thus, it might be possible that for cases where the assumptions
for the CLT theory falls-through the C3D8I and CSS8 elements may still be accurate.
5.4.2
Experimental Data Quality
A great resemblance between load sets is seen for STP, and STP-tor load cases, which
indicates that the experiment is reproducible. For the PT load case load set 1 is seen to
deviate from load sets 2 and 3 in deflections and x-rotations, while the y- and z-rotations

are seen to be repeatable. While it can be argued that this experiment cannot be replicated
due to the deviations mentioned above, load case 1 can also be seen as an outlier in the
experiment as load set 2 and 3 are in good alignment. Furthermore, if one argues that the
only interesting parameter in the PT load case is the z-rotation, the experiment would be
deemed repeatable as all load sets are in good alignment.
While it has not been quantified whether the material properties of the blade have changed
due to the creeping behavior observed, the reproducibility of the experiments indicates,
that this has not influenced the data presented in this report.

The trends seen in this study for performance of FE models in torsion and bending aligns

blades involves a series of automated processes to define a FE model.

This chapter proposes modeling techniques that eliminate shell corner intersections in

6.1

This study seeks to investigate the 4. question in the problem statement by proposing and
testing new modeling techniques for wind turbine blades.
This study explores if the current modeling techniques, as presented in Section 5.1.2, can
be improved in regards to torsional response. This is performed by proposing new model-
ing techniques, which is tested on a prismatic model of a cross-section from a turbine blade.
The proposed modelling techniques aims to improve the erroneous behavior near sharp
corners found in the Geometry Study. This is conducted by using solid element at the

Discussion
This section discuss the coupling methods used in this study and the performance of the
hybrid models.
6.4.1
Preliminary Study into Couplings
It is seen that the tie constraint performs noticeably better than the solid-shell coupling
model in both predictions of angle-of-twist and in the trend of the shear stress. This
indicates that the tie constraint is better at transferring the displacements between the
solid and shell regions in the model.
6.4.2
Performance of Hybrid Models
The hybrid models perform worse than their equivalent shell models in both torsion and
bending-by-point loads. It seems counterintuitive that a combination of solid and shell
models should yield results worse than the pure shell models. This can be argued, as the
preliminary study showed that using either of the two coupling methods improves torsional
prediction on a simpler isotropic model. It is, therefore, possible that the coupling does
not perform well at coupling layered composite elements in torsion or bending.
The geometrical representation of the airfoil cross-section shows discrepancies between the
use of element types. The hybrid and shell models do not accurately represent the physical
cross-section, whereas the solid model succeeds. This is seen in the transition in thick-
nesses where the non-solid models are missing material. While this would make the solid
model stiffer than the shell and hybrid models, it is not deemed to affect the relative com-
parison between shell and hybrid models, as their geometrical representation is very similar.

Discussion
The main discussion of this report, comment on question 5 in the problem statement. This is
done by discussing how enhancements to torsional prediction using Finite Element Method
can be implemented for commercial use of wind turbine blade modeling. Furthermore, the
use of solid elements is discussed.
7.1
Implementation of Enhancements for Commercial
Use
If the proposed modeling methods presented in the Correction Study had been found to
improve the prediction of torsion, the question of commercial implementation arises. The
FE modeling process explained in the Experimental Validation involves a series of custom
scripting routines to generate the FE models. A similar custom method for modeling is
found in the literature by Noever-Castelos et al. [4].
For a new modeling technique to be commercially applicable, the method would likely
have to be implementable in such a routine. For the proposed method in the Correction
Study, this could be performed by adding subroutines in the modeling flow, automatically
recognizing critical areas such as sharp corners, and replacing those areas with solid
elements. The routines should then also be able to generate couplings between solid and
shell elements automatically, as is performed manually in the Correction Study.
While such an implementation could be tedious and require expert insight into program-
ming and commercial FE solvers, the potential of using modified shell models could be
computationally beneficial compared to, e.g., pure solid models.
7.2
Use of Solid Elements for Modelling
Throughout this report continuum, solid elements are generally seen to perform well in the
prediction of both torsional and bending loads. However, as seen in the computational time
study in the Element Study, the computational effort of solid elements is noticeably higher
than for shell elements. While the continuum solid shell element SC8R is seen to perform
slightly worse in torsion compared to the other solid elements’ tested, the computational
129
==PAGE:139==
Master Thesis
January 28, 2023
time study shows that this element is less computationally expensive. If no improvements
for the existing shell elements are found, continuum solid/solid shell elements might become
a more attractive alternative as the importance of predicting torsional behavior accurately
increases. This trade-off is similarly highlighted by Noever-Castelos et al. [4].

Conclusion
This project aims to investigate and improve the prediction of torsional behavior utilizing
the Finite Element Method for modelling of wind turbine blades. This is investigated in
four individual studies, which seek to answer the questions raised in the problem statement.
It can be concluded that:
1. current FE modeling process involves a series of automated processes to generate
a 3D FE model. This automation is required to create high-fidelity models from
extensive input data, which can simulate torsional behavior. These observations are
primarily based on DTU Wind’s current modeling process.
2. the shell and solid ABAQUS 2022 elements tested perform well in the prediction of
torsion and bending on simple geometry. Furthermore, a high discrepancy for shell
models in primarily top-offset configuration is seen for a hollow square geometry in
torsion.
In modeling a 12.6m wind turbine blade, the solid elements are seen to predict
torsion well, while shell elements in bottom-offset configuration yield high deviation
in the prediction of angle-of-twist.
3. shell elements with non-zero reference plane offset configurations yield erroneous
behavior in torsion near sharp corner intersections. It is discussed whether the angle
between adjoining shell normal’s are correlated to this behavior.
4. a proposed modeling technique that replaces shell corner intersection with solid
elements is not shown to be successful in improving the torsional prediction.
5. if the proposed technique in 4) had been successful, it has been discussed how
the implementation into the existing FE modeling flow could be done by adding
new subroutines. The feasibility of utilizing solid elements for accurate torsional
prediction is furthermore discussed as an alternative to shell elements.
Overall, the scope of this work is deemed successful and can be used as inspiration for
further analysis into the FE modeling of the torsional behavior of turbine blades.

Future Work
In the list below, suggestions for future work into improving torsional prediction utilizing
FE modeling is given:
• Further analysis into the reasons behind why the hybrid model in the Correction
Study yielded results worse than for corresponding shell models.
• Further analysis into the effect of corner intersections for shell models on non-
prismatic sections of a wind turbine blade model.
• Further in-depth investigation into the behavior of conventional shell elements in
torsion with a focus on shell offset configurations and shell normals.
• Further investigation of shell element models prediction for very long wind turbine
blades.

E.1
Routine for Convergence
In order for the FE models to converge the change in stress and displacement as the
models is refined must be sufficiently small. In this report a relative convergence rate of
1% or less is utilized. For displacements this rate is calculated as:
Rel. Change = dispn −dispn−1
dispn−1
(E.1)
where n is the displacement of the current model iteration. The stress convergence rate is
calculated analogously to the displacements.
As the Python script function in ABAQUS 2022 is utilized a scripting routine was developed,
which refines the mesh of a model until the convergence rate limit is reached. This process
is illustrated in Figure E.1 below.
Figure E.1: Computational process logic for the Python script utilized. Note that ’conv.’
is short for ’convergence’.
The process is as follows:
1. Base Model: The model is created using ABAUQS 2022 GUI where the part, load,
boundary conditions, solver type and initial mesh is defined. The mesh is setup so
that mesh refinement either by global seeding, or a custom edge seeding process, can
be performed by changing a set of parameters.
2. Mesh Refinement: The selected meshing procedure is implemented into the Python
scripting routine, and a range of values is assigned to the parameters, which are
deemed to be fit-for-purpose for the specific model. A new model is then generated
based on a specific seeding, ranging from low to high refinement
3. Solving of Refined Model: The model generated in Mesh Refinement is then solved
using the predefined job definition.
LIII
==PAGE:195==
Master Thesis
January 28, 2023
4. Retrieval of stress and displacement: Stress and displacement, e.g. Uy and σ33, are
retrieved from specific sets defined in the base model. The location of the data
retrieved stays the same with changing mesh. The user must define these location
appropriately in the model, as only these values are used for evaluation.
5. Evaluation of Convergence: The convergence rate is then calculated for both stress
and displacement values as defined by Equation E.1. Its evaluated whether the set
convergence criteria is fulfilled. If not, the process loops back to the mesh refinement
step, where seeding values with a higher distritatizion is chosen and the process is
repeated.
6. Converged Model: If the convergence criteria is fulfilled the model and results file
(.cae & .odb files) are stored, and a resume file of the convergence process is generated.
This files contains data about the recorded stress, displacement and convergence
rate at each step in the process. The user then assess if the model is converged
satisfactory and is in alignment with other criteria there might be applicable for the
model (e.g. aspect ratio).
