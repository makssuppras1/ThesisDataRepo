Abstract
The objective of the present thesis is the investigation of the two separate methods present in EN
1993-1-1:2007 used for the design of beam-columns. This type of structural members, subjected
to combined compression and bending, exhibit a highly complex behavior. Therefore, the two
methods have to account for phenomena such as pronounced second order e’¼Ćects and plastic
interactions between bending moments and axial force. Both methods account for each loading
type separately and then their interaction is accounted for through the so called "interaction
factors", which in turn, are based on the so called "equivalent moment factors".
Firstly, a thorough analytical investigation into both methods is performed in order for the rules
governing the behavior of beam-columns to be fully understood and shown from a mechanical
perspective.
Subsequently, Geometrically and Materially Non-linear Imperfection Analyses (GMNIA) are
performed in order to obtain limit loads for beam-columns as part of an extensive parametric
study for various common load cases. The results obtained from the numerical simulations
are then validated through comparisons with previous simulations. Aftwerwards, the obtained
N-M diagrams are used for determining the accuracy of the two methods.
The results are ’¼ünally used for the recalculation of the equivalent moment factors and interaction
factors, which are then compared to the analytical analogues, in order to assess the extent to
which these factors correctly account for various phenomena. Both methods are found to be
on the safe side and to generally account for plasticity e’¼Ćects quite well.
Keywords:
Beam-column, GMNIA, stability, plasticity, buckling

Introduction
When designing steel beam-columns subjected to combined compression and bending, a lot of
aspects must be taken into account compared to normal columns subjected mainly to axial
forces. The combined e’¼Ćects of axial force - bending moment interaction, plasticity, initial
imperfection shape and magnitude, along with notable second order phenomena lead to a
highly complex behavior of the structural members.
Research and development towards a
method covering all aspects included in the design of beam-columns has been ongoing and
improving for over forty years. The latest version of Eurocode 3 has incorporated two separate
methods which have been developed during a nine year period of extensive research into this
subject, namely Method 1 and Method 2.
The two methods present in the standards have been developed independently by two research
groups. All details have not been disseminated in published ISI papers however and veri’¼ücation
is necessary.
1.1
Purpose and problem statement
Columns loaded in bending are treated in the Eurocode by two di’¼Ćerent methods, Method 1
and Method 2. Both methods present "black box" formulas, which are di’¼ācult to interpret from
a mechanical perspective. They are both based on the use of an equivalent bending moment.
While Method 1 uses a sinusoidal equivalent moment and is based mostly on theoretical aspects,
Method 2 uses a uniform constant equivalent moment and is based on parameters calibrated
from numerical simulations and real test results.
It is the aim of the thesis to investigate, interpret and ’¼ünally verify the two methods, along with
their expressions for the interaction factors, such that the underlying concept and approach for
the design of beam-columns become understandable for engineering students.
The investigation will start with a literature study followed by theoretical investigation and
interpretation. Di’¼Ćerent pro’¼üles are then analyzed through Geometrically and Materially Non-
linear Imperfect Analysis (GMNIA) in the Finite Element (FEM) software Abaqus, in a wide
range parametric study.
The results obtained from the numerical simulations will then be
compared to the Eurocode, in order to validate the two methods.
Throughout the project Tudor-Cristian Stan is mainly responsible for investigations into Method
1, while Oskar Hausgaard Larsen is mainly responsible for investigations into Method 2.

are elaborated upon and investigated from a theoretical point of view.

1.3
Historical overview
The current rules for the veri’¼ücation of members subjected to combined bending and com-
pression, present in the latest version of Eurocode 3, EN 1993-1-1:2007 [19] are the result of a
tremendous amount of work performed by two teams of researchers, with the backing of ECCS
Technical Committee 8 "Structural Stability" [4]. The team of researchers referred to as the
"French-Belgian" team, consisting of M. Villete, J. P. Jaspart, N. Boissonnad and J. P. Muzeau
has developed Method 1, while the team referred to as the "German-Austrian" team, consisting
of R. Greiner, R. Ofner, G. Salzgeber, P. Kaim, J. Lindner, A. Rusch, S. Heyde abd J. Wang
Kunming, has developed Method 2. The main reasons behind the need of improvement in the
area of beam-columns stemmed mainly from the fact that the ENV-1993-1-1:1992 [17] rules
tended to be overly conservative when treating structural members subjected to bending and

2.2
General stability concept
Due to the increasing slenderness of steel structural members used nowadays, the ultimate
bearing capacity of such members is usually limited by the loss of stability. Buckling represents
a form of instability for which a structural member undergoes large and sudden sideways defor-
mations, without the possibility of sustaining the load it is subjected to. Buckling is one of the
most common and dangerous failure modes in constructions. While several types of buckling
exist, the cases investigated in the this thesis only deals with bifurcation buckling, which is
the result of a bifurcation in the solutions de’¼üning the equilibrium path. At a certain point, a
column loaded with an increasing axial force may switch to a post buckling equilibrium path,
path which includes large lateral displacements in the column.
The structural members investigated in this document are referred to as "beam-columns". The
name simply arises from the fact that they represent structural members loaded in compression
(most columns are subjected to a dominant axial load) and bending (most beams are subjected
to dominant loads which result in bending moments).

2.3
Second order elastic theory
In this section, a general overview of what second order theory implies is presented. In most
cases, ’¼ürst order theory is used in order to perform capacity and stability checks for members
and structures. This basically means that the way the structure or member deforms under
the action of loads is not taken into account. All calculations in this case are performed on
the initial geometry of the structure. The Eurocode provides detailed explanations on when
this type of analysis is su’¼ācient and when it is not. Second order theory is based on the fact
that the deformed geometry of the structure or member has a strong impact of how it behaves,
on the evolution of stresses in members, on the way the section forces and bending moments
change and subsequently on the capacity of the member or structure, as the geometry changes.
Here, a distinction can be made between global second order e’¼Ćects (also re’¼Ćered to as P ŌłÆŌłå
e’¼Ćects) and local second order e’¼Ćects (also re’¼Ćered to as P ŌłÆ╬┤ e’¼Ćects).
P ŌłÆŌłåe’¼Ćects are related to structures as a whole, such as in the case of frames. An extremely
common example would be multi-story frames subjected to horizontal forces, as in the case of
wind or seismic loading. As the frame experiences lateral displacements, the vertical forces (i.e.
dead or live loads) would now have an eccentricity, which would produce bending moments
in structural members. This global phenomenon is not investigated in this report. The local,
P ŌłÆ╬┤ e’¼Ćects are of interest in the thesis. The same principle is used, but in this case, a single
structural element is assumed to be subjected to second order e’¼Ćects. In reality, no beam or
column is perfectly straight. This leads into the fact that the initial imperfection is ampli’¼üed
under increasing loading, which in turn corresponds to a change in section forces and member
behaviour. Additionally, even if a member could be assumed to be perfect, the de’¼éection caused
by a certain type of loading would lead to second order e’¼Ćects. Here, the case of a perfectly
straight beam-column can be considered. If the beam-column is subjected to transverse and
axial loading, the transverse forces will cause a de’¼éection of the member, which would in turn
cause a bending moment due to the axial loading and the de’¼éected shape. This subject is
elaborated upon in Section 2.5 and in Section 5.7.
One ’¼ünal point to note here is that, throughout this thesis, global behaviour (buckling, im-
perfection, etc.) will refer to the structural member as a whole. Local behaviour will refer to
speci’¼üc parts of the structural member (web, ’¼éanges). This is done due to the fact that only
structural members (beam-columns) are considered in this document and not structures.

Method 1
This section will provide insight into Method 1, as it is outlined in the Eurocode. The design
formulae will be broken down and analyzed step by step. Wherever possible, a theoretical back-
ground will be provided for every parameter and coe’¼ācient used. Due to the complex behavior

coe’¼ācients may not have the best format when it comes to pure understanding of buckling

Method 1 was thought out in such a way that it would be easy to understand, due to the fact
that every actual, physical phenomenon which appears in a real beam-column is represented

the connection between di’¼Ćerent concepts and physical quantities and how one certain e’¼Ćect
in’¼éuences another e’¼Ćect. The fact that this method relies heavily on theoretical aspects further
enhances its transparency and consistency.
There are instances however, where coe’¼ācients
which are tied to real phenomena are curve ’¼ütted and not deduced analytically, as in the case
of lateral torsional buckling and plasticity.

3.5
Conclusion
Based on the analytical investigations, it can be concluded that Method 1 does indeed heavily
rely on theoretical concepts. The fact that the equivalent sinusoidal moment factor represents
the basis of the method represents quite an innovative approach for investigating beam-columns.
By breaking down all parameters on which the method relies, a clear picture of the assumptions
made becomes evident. Furthermore, the general outline is represented by the fact that every
physical aspect and phenomenon is represented, in one form or another, by a parameter or
coe’¼ācient, through a "nested" approach.
The fact that plasticity e’¼Ćects are accounted for
by the calibrated plasticity factors however, goes against the general ideology of the method.
Moreover, the way the plasticity coe’¼ācients are described throughout the literature as denoting
the "amount" of plastic bending resistance developed by the cross-section is slightly misleading.
The fact that the term (wŌłÆ1) "represents the maximum possible increase in bending resistance
from pure elasticity to full plasticity" [4], [12], [11], [3] is again, only partly true, as in some
cases it seems the plasticity factors account for e’¼Ćects which are not developed theoretically.

Introduction and General description
This chapter will provide insight into how the beam-column is analyzed in Abaqus. A general
methodology will be presented and each step will be expanded upon in subsequent sections.
After a careful reading of this chapter, the results obtained in the present document should
be easy to replicate. An important thing to note is that, due to the large number of varying
parameters (see the end of this section) and di’¼Ćerent types of complex analyses, the current
Abaqus models are created using Python scripts.
Thus, all parameters investigated in the
present document can be easily changed. A thorough guide of the Python scripts can be found
Annex A, along with some helpfull hints, and an additional guide. The scripts are broken
down into separate sections, each including a multitude of comments and explanations for all
parameter used . Therefore, when combining the "user-friendly" way in which the scripts are
written with the guide and the present section of the document, a large number of analyses can
be performed.
First of all, the beam-column is modeled by using shell elements (3D model through section
sketch and path sketch). The whole element is created as one part. This eliminates the need
of connecting the web and the ’¼éanges later on, in the assembly step (through a tie or coupling
connection). Two types of sections are used: one homogenous shell section for the ’¼éanges and
another homogenous shell section for the web. The sections are assigned through the middle
surface. In order for the model to be as close as possible to real pro’¼üles and in order for the
analytical calculations to be compared with the FEM results, a small adjustment is done here:
the initial height of the pro’¼üle (de’¼üned as the height of the web) is adjusted, as shown in Figure
5.1, where tf represents the thickness of the ’¼éanges and h represents the total height of the
pro’¼üle. The mesh elements used are S4 shell elements (see Section 5.2), the material assigned
to the sections varies (see Section 5.4) and the boundary conditions are explained in Section
5.3.

Furthermore, due to the fact that the aim of the project is to investigate how a beam-column

The theoretical aspects of these phenomena are explained broadly in Section 2.2. In reality, all
structural elements are imperfect. This imperfection causes the member to fail due to loss of
either local or global stability. Certainly, in some cases, the member fails due to pure cross-

main step used in Abaqus is the Static, Riks step. Basically, the LBA is used to extract the
eigenshape i.e. the de’¼éected shape of the member and the eigenvalue corresponding to the

5.2
Mesh
This section deals with the type and density of the mesh used to perform the FE investigations
of the beam-column in Abaqus. Note that a mesh sensitivity analysis is not a main objective in
this report. It is of great importance however, that the results obtained from the simulations are
not inaccurate due to the meshing process. Furthermore, as presented in the previous section,
two models are created in Abaqus for every type of beam investigated: a "buckling model"
on which a Linear Buckling Analysis is performed and a "static model" on which a GMNIA
is performed. Due to the fact that the displacements at each node are imported in the static
model from the buckling model, the two models use the exact same mesh. A di’¼Ćerent approach
would consist of performing a mesh-to-mesh solution mapping between the two models. This
was deemed overly complicated to the alternative of having the same mesh. Therefore, the
mesh element type and the mesh density are both imposed by the static model. This is due
to the fact that the buckling model only needs a su’¼ācient number of elements for capturing
relevant buckling modes. Moreover, local buckling is not investigated in the present thesis and
only global buckling modes are of interest. DNV-RP-C208 [7] recommends a minimum of 3 to
6 number of elements per expected half wave, regarding capturing relevant buckling modes and
suggests avoiding 3 noded shell elements, as they should only be used as transition elements.
Thus, the only requirement for the buckling model is capturing the desired buckling modes,
which was ful’¼ülled for all types of meshes investigated in this section.
A mesh density study was performed in order to determine the appropriate number of elements
to be used. Due to the fact that I-type pro’¼üles are investigated which are double-symmetric and

ŌĆó Number of mesh elements along the length of the beam (X direction)
ŌĆó Number of mesh elements along the height of the web (Y direction)
ŌĆó Number of mesh elements along the width of the ’¼éanges (Z direction)

Boundary conditions and load application
This section will provide insight into how the boundary conditions for the beam-column are set

5.3.1
Boundary conditions
The boundary conditions for the Finite Element Model are set so as to replicate as much as
possible the boundary conditions assumed throughout the whole document: simply supported
beam-column with fork end conditions. Fork end conditions simply imply that the rotation of
the ends of the member around its longitudinal axis is restrained. Additionally, these conditions
are set to be respected by using a minimum number of retraints and are shown in Figure 5.7.
Note that the torsional restraint is not shown in the ’¼ügure. The following boundary conditions
are used:
ŌĆó The mid-end points of the web (points A and B) are restrained in the Y direction
ŌĆó The whole web ends (edges DE and FG) are restrained in the Z direction
ŌĆó The middle point of the web (point C) is restrained in the X direction
ŌĆó The whole beam is restrained against rotation around the X axis
Figure 5.7: Boundary conditions for the FE model
The middle point of the web (point C) is restrained in the X direction, due to symmetry reasons.
Another alternative to this type of restraint would have been to restrain the X direction in the
middle of the web at the end of the model. The two solutions are equivalent. This becomes
49
==PAGE:63==
clear when the methodology for axial loading is explained in subsection 5.3.2. Furthermore,
the vertical (U2 direction) restraint positioned in the shear center of the cross-section, coupled
with the torsional restraint (UR1) and the lateral restraints (U3) all add up in order to not
allow rigid body movements. Regarding the torsional restraint, it must be stated that this also
refrains the beam-column from experiencing lateral torsional buckling e’¼Ćects.
The boundary conditions mentioned above are the main ones used throughout the FE inves-
tigations. The only boundary condition used for the application of residual stresses through
temperature di’¼Ćerence loading is the torsional restraint. The reasons behind this choice and
the methodology of applying residual stresses are explained in Section 5.6.

Linear Buckling Analysis (LBA)
This section describes why and how a Linear Buckling Analysis (LBA) is performed in Abaqus.
As mentioned in Section 5.1, the role of a LBA is twofold:
ŌĆó Computation of eigenshapes
ŌĆó Computation of eigenvalues

First of all, it is important to note that the information extracted from the LBA is used in
slightly di’¼Ćerent ways throughout the document. Nevertheless, the methodology used is the
same.
The material used in the LBA is elastic. The iteration method used is the Subspace method,
since it requires fewer computational resources than the Lanczos method and due to the fact
that in most simulations, global buckling modes of the structural member are investigated.
This implies that the number of eigenvalues and eigenshapes required is small. The Lanczos
method is used in the cases when a speci’¼üc buckling mode needs to be investigated, as in the
case of local buckling.
A linear perturbation procedure with a buckling step is used in order to set up the eigenvalue
problem. The equivalent load used in this case is 1N and the way this axial force is applied is
explained in Section 5.3. Hence, the ’¼ürst eigenvalue obtained will represent the critical buckling
load for the ’¼ürst buckling mode. Due to the fact that I-shaped members are investigated, the
beam-column will buckle under the axial load in the weak plane. In instances where buckling
about the strong axis needs to be investigated, the extracted eigenshape (and eigenvalue) is
based on observations. A full table of which buckling mode represents is the ’¼ürst one, depending
on which pro’¼üle and the type of buckling (strong plane or weak) is investigated can be found in
Annex C. An equivalent solution would have been to "force" the structural member to buckle
in the ’¼ürst mode for the desired plane by adding additional restraints (for example, restraining
the beam from weak axis translation in order to obtain as the ’¼ürst critical mode global buckling
in the strong plane).
Once the LBA is performed, the results are used as an input for the Static, Riks Analysis (see
Section 5.7). First of all, as mentioned in the beginning of this section, the buckling shape of
interest needs to be set as output. This is done through an edit of the Keyword ’¼üle for the
model as follows:

This section presents the methodology used for connecting the LBA (and the buckling model)

5.7.1
Imperfection seeding
In order to simulate the real behavior of the structural member in a Static analysis, the global
and / or local imperfections must be accounted for. This generally can be achieved in one of
59
==PAGE:73==
two ways:
ŌĆó Through the application of loads in a static step which cause the required de’¼éection of
the member; the Static, Riks analysis then uses this de’¼éected shape as a starting point
ŌĆó By direct imperfection seeding
The ’¼ürst method has some disadvantages: in some cases, it becomes di’¼ācult to predict the
way in which loads have to be applied in order to obtain the desired de’¼éected shape of the
member; secondly, it sometimes becomes di’¼ācult to predict the values of the loads applied in
order to obtain the desired values for nodal de’¼éections along the member. Hence, the second
method is more straightforward and is used in the present document. All nodal displacements
corresponding to the desired buckling shape are imported from the results of the LBA through
the editing of the Keyword ’¼üle for this model in the following way [6]:
IMPERFECTION, FILE =< LBA jobname >, STEP =< LBA step number >
buckling mode, imperfection scale
For more details regarding initial conditions in the form of imperfections, please consult Section
11.3.1 of the Abaqus Analysis UserŌĆÖs Guide. The section also describes the way in which the
imperfection may be de’¼üned as a superposition of weighted mode shapes.
5.7.2
Static Riks step
A detailed description of Riks method and why it is used in this case can be found by consulting
Section 6.2.4 of the Abaqus Analysis UserŌĆÖs Guide. One main point is that this method is
extremely ’¼éexible and ’¼üts extremely well with the stability problems investigated in this project.
Furthermore, due to the fact that all non-linearities are taken into account in the simulations,
the Riks step is con’¼ügured to account for geometric non-linearity during the step. For more
information about this, please see Section 14.3.2 of the Abaqus Analysis UserŌĆÖs Guide.
The general loading procedure in for the static analysis is the following (the direct way in which
the loads are applied on the model is described in 5.3):
ŌĆó For the case when the structural member is subjected only to axial load, the magnitude
of this load applied in the Riks step will be Ncr. This value is taken from the LBA
ŌĆó For the cases when the structural member is subjected to axial load and additional types
of loading, the axial load is inputed in a Static, General step before the Riks step and
the additional types of loads which cause bending in the structural member are applied
in the Riks step
After the analysis is complete, the Load Proportionality Factor (LPF) is extracted. Due to the
large number of simulations performed, this is done through a post-processing Python script.
Each value of the LPF describes a state on the equilibrium path. Therefore, the maximum
LPF will correspond to the maximum load being able to be sustained by the member. Thus, a
failure criterion is reached.
ŌĆó In the case when the structural member is subjected only to axial load, the failure load
for the member is: LPFmax ┬Ę N; N denotes the applied load in the Riks step, i.e. the
critical load; note that for this load case, no Static, General step is used
ŌĆó For the cases when the structural member is subjected to axial load and additional types

==PAGE:74==
of loads, the same methodology for failure is considered, except that in these cases it
represents a combination of all the applied loads. As stated above, here, the axial load is
applied in a Static, General step before the Riks step. Hence, the failure of the structural
member when loaded in this manner in compression and bending, is represented by a point
on the M-N interaction diagram corresponding to N ŌłÆLPFmax ┬Ę M, where N represents
the applied axial load in the Static, General step and M represents the applied bending

The failure criterion mentioned above was chosen due to the fact that it ’¼üts extremely well
with the type of simulations performed. Other types of failure criteria can be found throughout
the literature, such as the total (or plastic) strain exceeding a certain value, the moment one
part of the cross-section of the member (either a ’¼éange, or the full cross-section) becomes fully
plastic, or the moment when the normal stress becomes equal to the yield stress in one ’¼über
of the member [20]. In the present document, the failure criterion is chosen in such a way that
the cross-section stresses are not speci’¼ücally investigated, but the member capacity as a whole,
due to the fact that material plasticity is taken into account (see Section 5.4).
A stopping point for the analysis was chosen through a parameter in the Riks step. A control
point at the end of the structural member was chosen and the stopping point for the analysis
can be controlled by inputting a maximum displacement in this point. The limit displacement
mainly used was 0.02 m in the longitudinal direction. However, in many cases, a limit load was
not reached and this displacement was increased. Post-buckling capacity is not investigated in
the present document, hence, a balance usually needs to be found i.e. buckling needs to be

of the initial bow imperfection (see Section 2.5). Basically, in this case, the e’¼Ćects of residual
stresses are included in the initial de’¼éected shape of the beam-column.
Subsequently, the
residual stresses are "physically" included, resulting in a initial stress ’¼üeld (see Section 5.6). In
doing so, the magnitude of the initial bow imperfection is now reduced, in order to not take into
account the e’¼Ćect of residual stresses twice and only account for the real, purely geometrical
imperfections. Furthermore, the four di’¼Ćerent material models are studied. The goals for these
simulations are:
ŌĆó Observation of the in’¼éuence of the material models used with e’¼Ćects such as strain hard-
ening
ŌĆó Observation of the in’¼éuence of di’¼Ćerent imperfection magnitudes
ŌĆó Observation of the di’¼Ćerence in behavior for the IPE500 and HEB500 pro’¼üles
ŌĆó Observation of the di’¼Ćerence in using the in’¼éuence of residual stresses through an equiv-
alent geometrical imperfection and through a stress ’¼üeld
ŌĆó Investigation on how all results compare to the appropriate buckling curves, since the
di’¼Ćerence between the simulations and analytical solutions translate further on, when the
structural member is subjected to additional types of loads
Secondly, the beam column is loaded separately in the weak and strong plane with the load

ŌĆó Application of load cases which include end moments; the methodology of load application
is explained in Section 5.3. Due to the fact that the bending moments are applied using

One possible solution which was tried out is presented in Figure 6.2 (left), for bending
moments in the strong plane of the beam. Through an extension of the beam beyond
its supports and by applying loads at the new end of the member, the bending moments
along the beam may be controlled through the magnitude of applied loads, length of the
extensions, ey and by ensuring that the material for these extra parts (AC and BD) is
considerably more sti’¼Ćthan the actual beam (AB). This method was not used however,

This causes additional bending and torsional moments,
which cannot be controlled. A second option is shown in Figure 6.2 (right). In this case,
the axial load is not applied centrally on the beam-column, but with an eccentricity. In
this manner, the bending moment distribution at the ends and along the beam may be
controlled. Neither this method was used however, due to the fact that it would imply also
modeling very sti’¼Ćend plates and the ends of the beam. Furthermore, if beam elements
are used in Abaqus, and not shell elements, this method becomes extremely feasible,
as many simulations which can be found in the literature have adopted this method
( [20], [11]). One ’¼ünal and probable solution which could have been used in conjunction
with the type of models created for the present document involves the application of a
plastic distribution of stresses at the ends of the beam. This would have been done only
for the cases in which the axial force applied is relatively low, as in these cases, the beam
could develop its partial / full plastic moment capacity. This would also eliminate faulty
limit cases on the N-M interaction diagrams i.e. cases in which no axial force is applied.
This concept is highly recommended if the same type of simulations are to be performed
in the future.

6.7.3
Discussion
The material used and the way of taking into account plastic e’¼Ćects such as strain hardening,
either through plastic strain magnitudes or through the tangent modulus, as well as ultimate
stress values, is shown to have a small in’¼éuence on buckling behavior.
For example, the
di’¼Ćerences between the DNV material model without the proportionality limit and the material
model with linear strain hardening proposed by the Eurocode are very small, although the
former is based on true stress - true strain curves and the latter is based on engineering stress-
strain relations. The in’¼éuence of strain hardening can be seen in the di’¼Ćerences between the
material model with a nominal yield plateau and the other material models only in the very
small slenderness range. What does matter however, is the yield stress of the material model
75
==PAGE:89==
used, as shown by the di’¼Ćerences between the DNV material with the proportionality limit and
the other materials. As a general outline, the proportionality limit represents the point on the
stress-strain curve after which there is no longer a linear relation between stress and strain.
Between the proportionality limit and the elastic limit, although the stress-strain relation is
no longer linear, the material should still behave elastically i.e. no permanent deformations
remain if the loading is removed. This type of material model however is impossible to model
in Abaqus and that is why the proportionality limit was considered as the elastic limit.
By including residual stresses through temperature ’¼üelds, the di’¼Ćerences can be seen for buck-
ling in the weak plane can be observed by comparing Figure 6.6 with Figure 6.7(b) and 6.8(b).
The buckling curves obtained when using all material models move downwards, indicating the
fact that the residual stress distribution and magnitude has a great in’¼éuence on the behavior
of the structural element. This in’¼éuence was studied in [20], but only for rectangular hollow
sections. This phenomenon is still a subject of ongoing research. Furthermore, regarding the
results obtained in the present simulations, the fact that the buckling curves obtained for the
weak plane of the cross-section for the HEB500 pro’¼üle translate below the European buckling
curves may be the result of two e’¼Ćects, one being the fact that the residual stress magnitude
of 0.3fy for the IPE500 pro’¼üle and 0.5fy for the HEB500 pro’¼üle may be overly conservative.
Secondly, the appropriate buckling curve selection from Table 6.2 in the Eurocode is made
based on the cross-section dimensions: the thickness of the ’¼éanges and the height / width
ratio. The height to width ratio transition value from one curve to another is 1.2. Therefore,
the buckling curve selection is "quanti’¼üed". For example, when comparing the IPE500 pro’¼üle
with the HEB500 pro’¼üle for buckling about the z-z axis, both ’¼üt into curve "b" and are treated
in the same manner, even though the IPE pro’¼üle has a much higher height to width ratio.
Consequently, if the IPE pro’¼üle should ’¼üt buckling curve "b" perfectly, the HEB pro’¼üle should
’¼üt somewhere between buckling curve "b" and "c". The results back up this concept, as shown
in Figure6.8(b).
Regarding the initial imperfection magnitude, Figure 6.10 shows an almost perfect ’¼üt of the
results with buckling curve "a", when using the imperfections extracted from the same buckling
curve, as expected. The values proposed by DS/EN 1993-1-1:2007 in Table 5.1 dramatically
reduce the capacity of the structural member, for both pro’¼üles. This was expected, since the
initial imperfection governs the limit load analysis for members subjected to an axial load.
Moreover, the old standard, DD/ENV 1993-1-1:1992 prescribtion for the imperfection magni-
tude in the case of strong axis buckling and elasto-plastic analysis, as stated in 2.5, simply
represents the value extraction from the buckling curve, coupled with the consideration of a
plastic capacity of the cross-section. This concept stands valid. When investigating weak axis
buckling, the di’¼Ćerences are even larger, as seen in Figure 6.11. Again, the old standard imper-
fection places the buckling curve obtained from the simulations closer to the European buckling
curve, but through the ’¼üxed expression of the imperfection magnitude, it is still placed lower.
Finally, it is important to note that while the obtained buckling curves are plotted for the
case of using a plastic capacity across the whole range of slenderness, this deviates from the
real behavior of the structural element, as in a higher slenderness range, the behavior becomes
increasingly elastic.

6.8.6
Discussion
When applying axial load and strong plane bending, particularly for load cases B, C and D,
the N-My diagrams obtained from Abaqus for both pro’¼üles lie slightly above the Eurocode as
expected. This means that both methods are conservative compared to the Abaqus models. It
is also noticeable that Method 1 is the more conservative choice of the two methods. For some
cases the capacity of Method 2 and Abaqus models goes above the plastic moment capacity
for low axial load ratio. For all cases however, a cross-section check should always be made, as
both methods rely on equivalent moment concepts.
For load cases E and F two facts are important. First of all, the comparisons between the
results from the present simulations with the results obtained by Ofner, presented in Section
6.10 show that for data points on the N-M interaction diagrams corresponding to high bending
moment values, the present results may underestimate the true capacity of the beam-column.
82
==PAGE:96==
Secondly, as brie’¼éy stated in the previous section, for load cases which include end moments,
the axial force applied needs to exceed a certain limit for the maximum second order bending
moment to occur inside the beam and not remain at one end. For the case of strong plane
bending, a slenderness of 0.5 and a value of Žł = 0 or Žł = ŌłÆ1 this minimum value for the axial
force exceeds Npl, for both pro’¼üles (calculated using Equation (3.26)). Therefore, all load cases
which include end moments in the strong plane become irrelevant for beam-columns with a
slenderness of ╬╗y = 0.5 from a stability check perspective.
The results for load case F may also be attributed to the methodology of end moment applica-
tion, i.e. through an elastic distribution of stresses. Other ways to add end moments in Abaqus
may turn out to be more accurate, see Section 5.3.2 and Section 6.6 for a description of how
the loads where applied in Abaqus for this thesis and other possible modes of end moment
application.

In this section, the N-M diagrams obtained from the Abaqus simulations will be compared to

residual stress state and imperfection magnitudes used by Ofner when investigating ’¼éexural
buckling is available. In a very wide range of simulations performed in this ’¼üeld, the material
model used was elastic-perfect plastic with a nominal yield plateau. The initial imperfection
87
==PAGE:101==
magnitude used was L/1000. It is assumed here that Ofner used the same parameters. More-
over, it is not known if the analysis was previously adjusted in order for the buckling curves in
the Eurocode to be reproduced, when only an axial load is acting on the beam-column. In the
simulations performed in the present document, the only adjustment of this kind was made for
the case of the HEB500 pro’¼üle, when buckling in the weak plane is investigated. Discrepancies
may arise from the aforementioned reasons, coupled with the concepts explained in Section 6.6,
such as the application of end moments by using an elastic distribution of stresses. Addition-
ally, the present simulations were performed with three slenderness values (╬╗y = 0.5; 1.0; 1.5)

7.1.3
Discussion
The expectations before obtaining the results from the simulations and before performing the
comparisons, were that the Eurocode formulations would prove to be conservative, i.e. the
analytical expressions present in the Eurocode for the formulation of the equivalent moment
factors would result in higher magnitudes for these factors. This is due to the fact that the
expresions in the Eurocode are based on elastic second order theory, at least for Method 1. This
was proven to be true when considering strong plane as well as weak plane buckling, for load
cases B and C. Furthermore, in some cases (such as Figure 7.6(b) and 7.7(b)), the recalculated
factors approach the analytical factors when the axial force is high. This directly implies that
in the structural member check equation, the moment term becomes smaller, as the axial term
increases. Therefore, the in’¼éuence of the moment term and consequently, the in’¼éuence of the
equivalent moment factor included in this term is small. Moreover, the di’¼Ćerence between the
two studied pro’¼üles is seen, as the in’¼éuence of plastic e’¼Ćects is higher, for the case of the HEB
97
==PAGE:111==
pro’¼üle than for the IPE pro’¼üle. Therefore, there is a smaller di’¼Ćerence between the theoretical,
elastic moment factors and the recalculated, plastic moment factors when plasticity e’¼Ćects are
reduced. An expected result, due to the aforementioned plasticity e’¼Ćects, would have been the
observation of di’¼Ćerences between the case of ’¼éexural buckling in the strong plane and ’¼éexural
buckling in the weak plane. This should occur, due to the fact that the plastic capacity - elastic
capacity ratio is higher in the weak plane than in the strong plane (Mplz/Melz > Mply/Mely)
for both pro’¼üles. The results however, are extremely similar. While the Eurocode proves to
be on the safe side every time for load cases which include concentrated and uniform loads,
for load case E (end moments with Žł = 0), this does not seem to be true. One must keep in
mind however, that load cases which include end moments are more complicated as in many
cases, the maximum bending moment remains at the end of the member, where a pure cross-
section check becomes necessary, as it was previously explained. By observing Figures D.3,
D.4 and D.5, it can be seen that the recalculated moment factors lie above the theoretical
ones only for a certain lower range of the axial force. The speci’¼üc magnitudes of the axial
force for which the maximum bending moment remains at one end are not calculated in the
present document for this case, however one can conclude that the theoretical derivation of the
equivalent moment factors in the Eurode mainly provide safe and accurate approximations, for
all load cases investigated.

7.2.3
Discussion
First of all, it is important to mention again that the interaction factors for Method 1 account
for plasticity e’¼Ćects through the inclusion of the plasticity coe’¼ācients (see Section 3.4). The
interaction factors for Method 2 account for plastic e’¼Ćects through their inherent nature, as
they are based on numerical simulations.
Secondly, it was seen that for load cases B,C and D, the recalculated interaction factors are
mostly lower than their analytical analogues. This holds true for both buckling planes and
both pro’¼üles. The di’¼Ćerence between the interaction factors generally becomes smaller as the
slenderness increases, fact which indicates that both methods are able to safely account for the
fact that the buckling phenomenon occurs in the elastic-plastic range. When considering the
di’¼Ćerent behavior of the two pro’¼üles, it was observed that the HEB500 recalculated coe’¼ācients
are closer to their analytical analogues, while for the IPE500 pro’¼üle, the recalculated factors
105
==PAGE:119==
have smaller values, relative to the theoretically derived ones.
Moreover, Figures 7.16(a),
7.18(a), 7.20(a) and E.29(a) for the IPE500 pro’¼üle and ’¼ügures E.23(a), E.25(a), E.27(a) for
the HEB500 pro’¼üle show the di’¼Ćerence in the interaction factors when weak plane buckling is
investigated, for a low slenderness of the beams and when load cases B, C, D (and E for the
IPE pro’¼üle) are applied. The fact that ╬╗z = 0.5 in these cases directly means that the length
of the beam-column is small. For these cases, the bene’¼ücial plasticity e’¼Ćects arising from the
interaction between monoaxial bending and axial force become increasingly important. The fact
that the buckling plane is the weak plane also means that, due to the geometry of the pro’¼üles,
the plastic shape factor is higher than for the case when strong-plane buckling is investigated.
Considering all the facts mentioned above, it can be concluded that for these cases, Method
1 correctly accounts for this positive interaction, as the decrease in the analytical Method 1
interaction factors follow the increase of the axial force applied and ’¼üt well with the results
obtained from the simulations. Method 2 seems to provide interaction factor values which show
an increasing trend, as the axial load acting on the beam increases. This results in interaction
factor values which are well above the recalculated ones.
For load case F, the recalculated interaction factors are generally higher than their analytical
analogues, for both methods. As state before, load cases which include di’¼Ćerent end moments
(load case E and F) are more complicated as in addition to the classical buckling check, pure
cross-section checks are necessary. Therefore, for certain values of the axial force applied, the
buckling checks become irrelevant, as the maximum second order bending moment remains
at one end of the structural member. Additionally, as shown in Section 6.10, the comparison
with the simulation results obtained by Ofner for this load case show that the N-M diagrams
obtained in the present document may underestimate the capacity of the member. This may
be the reason the recalculated interaction factors are lower than the analytically derived ones.

Further Research
Quite a lot of work and e’¼Ćort has been put into the present document in order to cover as
many di’¼Ćerent cases and phenomena possible. During the working process many issues were
encountered, mainly regarding the modeling process, due to the fact that the automation of
numerical simulations was always an important goal. The sheer number of simulations required
in order to obtain N-M interaction diagrams for as many load cases as possible is very large and
as such, Python scripts were developed. Nevertheless, only a part of all the di’¼Ćerent cases was
investigated, due to the lack of time available compared to the time required for investigation,
GMNIA modelling in Abaqus and interpretation. Notable cases which were not investigated
include out-of-plane buckling, when bending moments act in the strong plane of the cross-
section, spatial behavior under biaxial bending moments and compression and lateral torsional
buckling.
8.1
Class 3 and Class 4 cross-sections
As the pro’¼üles investigated were IPE500 and HEB500, class 3 and class 4 cross-sections were
not dealt with. While proposals for the incorporation of an elastic-plastic modulus for class
3 cross-sections have been made [3], they have yet to be introduced in the Eurocode. As the
sudden transition from class 2 to class 3 cross-section is unrealistic, numerical simulations could
be performed order to check the accuracy of the proposals. Additionally, the ability of class 4
cross-section to develop local plastic capacities should be of interest.
8.2
Lateral torsional buckling
Lateral torsional buckling and biaxial bending were left out during this thesis, since these
investigations would imply additional time, not only for performing the numerical simulations,
but also for the interpretation of results. When looking into lateral torsional buckling, most
factors included in both Method 1 and Method 2 change rather signi’¼ücantly. A tremendous
e’¼Ćort has been put in by Peter Kaim, Richard Greiner and Jean-Pierre Jaspart into investigating
LTB [20]. Nevertheless, independent validation of the results obtained should always be needed.
Based on the numerical models created as part of the present thesis, LTB should be easily
investigated by simply removing the torsional boundary condition from the models.
8.3
Imperfection study
As outlined in the present document, imperfections, along with residual stresses dominate
the behavior of beam-columns. While in this thesis, only imperfections in the loading plane
were included, a thorough imperfection study should be performed. Cases in which the initial
imperfection in the beam column represents a superposition of local and global buckling modes
may lead to reduced load bearing capacities. The loading case of end moments with Žł = ŌłÆ1
and an initial imperfection representing the second buckling mode (one full sine wave) may be

Conclusion
The analytical investigations into the values of initial imperfection magnitudes, which would be
further on used in the numerical simulations revealed that the values prescribed by Table 5.1 in
EN 1993-1-1:2007 are highly over-conservative. When using these values, the European buckling
curves could not be reproduced by numerical simulations. When extracting the imperfection
values from the buckling curves, they were accurately reproduced. Furthermore, the old version
of the standard, namely ENV 1993-1-1:1992 has a di’¼Ćerent approach, as in this case, all safety
coe’¼ācients which are included in the expressions for the magnitude of initial bow imperfections
are shown. Using the values provided by this standard, the results obtained were signi’¼ücantly
closer to the buckling curves. Furthermore, no signi’¼ücant in’¼éuence of the material models used
to represent plastic behaviour was observed.
The theoretical investigations into Method 1 reveal that for the most part, it indeed attains its
goal of being transparent, and heavily reliant on theoretical aspects. This being said, the factors
used by Method 1 to account for inelastic e’¼Ćects are calibrated from numerical simulations.
This concept is not aligned with the general philosophy of Method 1 and the e’¼Ćects of these
factors on N-M interaction curves are signi’¼ücant. The fact that Method 1 strives in linking
every coe’¼ācient and parameter used to a physical e’¼Ćect or phenomenon holds true, however the
fact that these plasticity factors lead in some cases to the development of a bending capacity
higher than the plastic bending capacity suggests it is still di’¼ācult to develop a theoretical basis
associated to every physical e’¼Ćect, due to the highly complex behaviour of beam-columns.
The objectives of Method 2 is to give the user a simple and user friendly design formula where
su’¼ācient design capacity with a good economical aspect remains. For high slenderness the
interaction factors remain constant which leads to decreased capacity compared to Method 1
and the theoretical interaction factors, hence a more conservative design. For the obtained
simulations Method 2 showed up to lie between the simulations and Method 1 for strong plane
mostly, however, for weak plane it lies below Method 1. Method 2 could then be chosen as a
valid design due to its aim for simplicity, hence less required work for the structural engineer.
The limit loads obtained from the analytical expressions of the stability checks present EN
1993-1-1:2007 were found in almost all cases studied to be lower than the ones obtained through
numerical simulations. This further translates into the same conclusion for the magnitudes of
the equivalent moment factors and interaction factors, leading to the fact that both methods
lie on the safe side when predicting loss of stability in beam-columns. In some cases, when the
structural members have a very low slenderness, the methods may be slightly over-conservative,
especially for weak plane buckling, where the plastic behavior of I type cross-section is more
pronounced.
