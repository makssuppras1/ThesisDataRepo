Abstract
Every year, millions of tonnes of plastic waste enter the ocean. The main transport of plastic
waste from land-based sources to the ocean is through the coastal zone. Larger pieces of plastic
waste in the environment eventually degrade into smaller plastic fragments. This master thesis
focuses on plastic particles with sizes smaller than 5 mm deﬁned as microplastic. Little is known
about the transport mechanisms of microplastic in the coastal zone. However, the transport
of microplastic particles in nearshore regions is important to investigate as it can help predict
the areas where the plastic particles accumulate and thereby aid eﬀective waste management
strategies.
This thesis investigates the wave-induced cross-shore transport of buoyant and
non-buoyant microplastic particles by numerical modelling. The computational ﬂuid dynamics
are simulated in the OpenFOAM software and are coupled with a force balance based particle
model. The coupled model is able to loop over the computational ﬂuid dynamics simulation
and thereby allow for long simulation time of the particle transport. The model is validated
by comparing with experimental results by Alsina et al. (2020) for transport of microplastic
in regular non-breaking waves. For transport of microplastic in irregular breaking waves, the
model results are compared with the experimental ﬁndings of Larsen et al. (2023) and Guler
et al. (2022) for buoyant and non-buoyant particles respectively. For buoyant particles, it was
found that the model overestimates the horizontal particle velocities prior to breaking.
In
breaking waves, the cross-shore particle velocities correspond well to the experimental ﬁndings
of Larsen et al. (2023) that shows that particles with lower Dean numbers (high rise velocities)
are transported faster than particles with higher Dean number. However, the model slightly
underestimates the particle velocities under breaking waves, which could be due to a low write
interval of the model. For non-buoyant microplastic particles, it was found that the model is
able to simulate the transport of particles on both a ﬂat, sloping, and barred bed. The model
was able to predict the same hotspots as found by Guler et al. (2022) for most of the simulated
particles. However, the model reached the hotspots within a short simulation time compared
to the experimental ﬁndings. This is possibly due to the fact that the model does not account
for bed ripples and a lower possibility of large wave events due to a shorter repeating phase of
the wave spectrum. Overall, the model shows potential as an initial framework for simulating
the nearshore transport of buoyant and non-buoyant particles.

Introduction
1.1
Microplastic Pollution
Plastic pollution has become an increasing environmental concern in recent years due to the
widespread usage and low degradability of plastic (Beaman et al., 2016). Unfortunately, the
improper disposal and management of plastic waste have led to leakage of plastic into the
environment, particularly the ocean. If the patterns of today’s plastic usage and management
continues, it is estimated that the amount of plastic waste ending up in the ocean yearly
will increase from 11 million tonnes in 2016 to over 29 million tonnes by 2040 (Lewis, 2023).
Once released into the environment, plastic can remain for hundreds of years before eventually
decomposing (Lassen et al., 2015). Plastic can be transported very long distances after it has
entered the ocean and plastic pollution has even been found in remote locations, such as the
deep sea and polar regions (Barnes et al., 2009; Lassen et al., 2015).
Plastic waste can be classiﬁed by size, distinguishing between macroplastic (larger than 5 mm)
and microplastic (smaller than 5 mm) (Guler et al., 2022). In the environment, plastic waste
undergoes degradation, resulting in the breakdown of macroplastic into microplastic (Lassen
et al., 2015). Microplastic takes many diﬀerent shapes and can have diﬀerent densities that
determine the buoyancy of the particles (Larsen et al., 2023). As microplastic particles are very
small, they are easily consumed and microplastic has been found in organisms at every level of
the marine food chain (Lassen et al., 2015). Due to the long degradation time and accumulation
in the marine food web, microplastic is considered a pervasive threat to the marine environment
(Law and Thompson, 2014). However, the severity of the damage is still not fully known.
It is generally assumed that the majority of plastic waste in the oceans comes from land-based
sources. This means that the primary pathway for plastic waste to reach the ocean from land is
through the coastal zone (Alsina et al., 2020). Several studies have investigated the abundance
and spatial distribution of plastic waste in oﬀshore waters (Aytan et al., 2016; So et al., 2018;
Zhao et al., 2015). However, there have been relatively few studies focusing on the transport
and life-cycle of plastic waste in the coastal zone, especially in nearshore waters (Zhang, 2017).
Understanding the nearshore transport of microplastic is of great importance as it can provide
valuable knowledge about the distribution of microplastic in coastal environments (Ho and Not,
2019). Such insights can help to identify the speciﬁc areas within the nearshore region where
microplastic accumulates, and thereby facilitate eﬀective removal and management strategies
(Guler et al., 2022).
Gaining knowledge through experimental investigation and numerical
modelling of the transport mechanisms can lead the way for more informed decision-making
and sustainable practices in combatting plastic pollution in coastal environments.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.2
Nearshore Particle Transport
Alsina et al. (2020) experimentally investigated microplastic transport of buoyant and non-
buoyant microplastic particles beneath regular non-breaking waves at intermediate water depth.
Alsina et al. (2020) observed that non-buoyant particles moved onshore with velocities depend-
ing on the wave steepness and that buoyant particles were transported onshore in accordance
with Stokes drift.
Similar results was found by Larsen et al. (2023) who experimentally investigated the transport
of nearshore buoyant microplastic particles under irregular breaking waves. Prior to breaking,
Larsen et al. (2023) also observed particle velocities close to Stokes drift regardless of particle
characteristics. Additionally, Larsen et al. (2023) observed that buoyant particles were trans-
ported with velocities close to the wave celerity in the surf zone, depending on the dimensionless
settling velocity (Dean number).
In a similar experimental setup as Larsen et al. (2023), Guler et al. (2022) studied the nearshore
transport of non-buoyant microplastic particles under irregular waves both prior to and after
breaking. The ﬁndings by Guler et al. (2022) indicated that microplastic accumulates at dif-
ferent hotspots in the nearshore region depending on the particle Dean number and the wave
breaker type. The microplastic particle distribution was additionally investigated for diﬀerent
conﬁgurations of the bed morphology.
Regarding numerical investigations, Deike et al. (2017) utilised computational ﬂuid dynamics
(CFD) to study the Lagrangian drift by tracking tracer particles in the water before, during, and
after wave breaking. Deike et al. (2017) observed that particle velocities could be described by
the Stokes drift velocity for non-breaking waves and observed ten times larger particle velocities
relative to Stokes drift velocity for breaking waves. However, the study by Deike et al. (2017)
considered passive tracer particles whereas the transport mechanisms of inertial particles are
more complex.
For this purpose, a coupled computational ﬂuid dynamics and discrete element method (CFD-
DEM) can be used. A widely used software is CFDEM®coupling. This software couples CFD
simulated in the OpenFOAM® software with DEM simulated in the LIGGGHTS® software,
thereby coupling the particle movement with the ﬂow dynamics. The DEM part of the CFDEM
software tracks the particles by solving their trajectories based on a force balance. The force
balance includes forces exerted by the surrounding ﬂuid, contact forces between particles, and
body forces, considering both translational and rotational motion (Kloss et al., 2012).
A
limitation of CFDEM is that the simulation of particle transport is done in run time. This
means that running a simulation in CFDEM can be computationally demanding and therefore
limit the simulation time (Kloss et al., 2012).

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.3
Motivation
A new approach that can reduce the limitation of simulation time will be investigated in this
master thesis. The approach runs the CFD model separately and then couples the solution
with the particle model. The approach consists of the following two steps:
- Step 1 is to simulate a periodic CFD solution. For regular waves, this means simulating
one wave period. For an irregular wave spectrum, this is obtained by simulating one
repeating phase of the spectrum.
- Step 2 is to couple the CFD solution with a particle model. The coupled model numer-
ically solves a simple force balance of particle motion by looping over the periodic CFD
solution.
The new approach allows for more control of what is being implemented in the particle model
and allows for a longer simulation time of the particle transport.
This thesis aims to develop a coupled model that can simulate the cross-shore transport of
buoyant and non-buoyant microplastic particles.
To ensure the accuracy and reliability of
the model, validation is sought through a comparative analysis with experimental ﬁndings.
Speciﬁcally, the model performance will be evaluated against the regular wave experiments
conducted by Alsina et al. (2020) for both buoyant and non-buoyant particles, as well as the
irregular wave experiments conducted by Larsen et al. (2023) for buoyant particles and by Guler
et al. (2022) for non-buoyant particles.
1.4
Outline
The structure of the remainder of this thesis is as follows: The CFD model is described in
Chapter 2 followed by a description of the particle model in Chapter 3. The coupled model is
then described in Chapter 4. The results are presented and discussed for buoyant particles in
Section 5.3 and for non-buoyant particles in Section 5.4. Finally, Chapter 6 provides concluding
remarks for the thesis.

Model Description - CFD Model
To simulate waves, the open source computational ﬂuid dynamic (CFD) software OpenFOAM®
is used. Two main model setups are used. The ﬁrst simulates regular waves in a cyclic domain
and the second simulates irregular waves in a wave ﬂume domain where waves propagate towards
a beach.
2.1
OpenFOAM
OpenFOAM is a software developed to solve ﬂuid dynamics numerically. The version of the
software used for this thesis is OpenFOAM v1912. The waves2Foam toolbox is utilised to
generate free surface waves in a multi-phased ﬂow of water and air and is further characterised
by the use of relaxation zones in which waves are generated as well as absorbed (Jacobsen,
2017).

Turbulence Model
In this thesis, the turbulence is modelled using the stabilised k−ω turbulence model, kOmegaW-
ilcox2006Stab. As the name suggests, the turbulence model models the kinetic turbulent
energy, k, and the speciﬁc dissipation rate of turbulence, ω. The model is based on the original
k−ω turbulence model by Wilcox (2006) with the addition of a stabilisation term derived by
Larsen and Fuhrman (2018) and implemented in OpenFOAM by Li et al. (2022). The closure
coeﬃcients applied in the stabilised model are set in accordance with the values utilised by Li
et al. (2022).

kinematics are important as this thesis aims to model particle transport beneath waves.
The spatial discretisation is deﬁned by the cell size in all directions of the domain. As this
thesis only considers the cross-shore transport mechanisms, the spatial discretisation is two-
dimensional in the (x, y)-plane. According to Larsen et al. (2019), vertical discretising in space
with 12.5 cells per wave height, ∆y = H/12.5, allows for suﬃcient accuracy while still ensuring
low computational cost. In this thesis, a spatial discretisation of ∆y = H/15 is chosen where
H is the wave height.

Boundary Conditions
The boundary conditions for the wave ﬂume domain resembles those of the cyclic domain
described in Section 2.2.3. However, a no-slip boundary condition is applied for velocity at the
bed. The wave conditions described in Section 2.3.1 are applied at the inlet boundary of the
domain.
At the bed, wall functions are applied as boundary conditions for the four turbulent quantities:
k, omega, nut, and R. These represents the turbulent kinetic energy, the speciﬁc dissipation
rate of turbulence, the dynamic eddy viscosity, and the Reynolds stress tensor, respectively.
The wall functions allow to reduce the number of cells needed to correctly resolve the near-bed
ﬂow within the wave boundary layer. Instead of resolving the entire wave boundary layer,
requiring a cell size below y+ < 5 to resolve the viscous sublayer, the wall functions assume a
logarithmic velocity proﬁle near the bed that is valid for 30 ≤y+ ≤300.
For this reason, the cell size near the bed in the model was set to y+ = 30, although it would
have been preferable to set the ﬁrst cell centre to y+ = 30 as recommended by Sumer and
Fuhrman (2020). However, the choice of a ﬁrst cell size of y+ = 30 is justiﬁed as the wave
boundary layer thickness is calculated to be δ = 1.5 mm, see Appendix A.6 for detailed calcu-
lations. The particle diameter for all simulated particles exceeds δ, see Section 5.1. Therefore,
a completely resolved wave boundary layer is not necessary. Utilising the wall functions as
boundary conditions reduces the number of cells in the mesh, thereby lowering the computa-
tional time. Additionally, larger cells allow for larger time steps, see Equation 2.6, emphasising
the computational advantage of applying wall functions.
The remaining boundary conditions for the turbulent quantities are set in accordance with the
setup by Li et al. (2022).

Another type of particle model is the so-called random walk model. In Sections 5.3.1 and 5.4.1,

Model Description - Coupled Model
Having validated the CFD model and the particle model individually, the two models are now
coupled to make a computationally cheap and easily applicable coupled model. This is obtained
by temporally looping over the CFD model results. In the case of the cyclic domain, this means
looping over the single regular wave period, while for the wave ﬂume domain, it means looping
over the repeating phase of the irregular spectrum. This temporal looping is advantageous for
the coupled model as it signiﬁcantly decreases the run time compared with standard CFD-DEM
models.
The coupling is implemented in a MATLAB code originally developed by our supervisors for the
random walk model.
4.1
Post-Processing in OpenFOAM
Extracting results from the CFD model is done in two ways. Firstly, by sampling the sur-
face elevation through wave gauges as explained in Section 2.3.5. Secondly, by sampling two-
dimensional ﬂow kinematics in the (x, y)-plane located in the centre line of the single-celled
z-direction.
To couple the CFD model with the particle model, the sampled ﬂow kinematics are utilised as
input in the particle model. The quantities sampled from the ﬂow are: the velocity ﬁeld, u, the
indicator function, α, and the Lagrangian derivative of the ﬂow velocity, Du/Dt. Additionally,
to conduct a model comparison in Section 5.3.1 and 5.4.1, the results generated with the coupled
model are compared with the results of the random walk model. To run the random walk model,
the gradient of the velocity ﬁeld, ∇u, is additionally sampled.
4.2
Model Setup in MATLAB
The coupled model is set up and run in MATLAB. Prior to running the model, the sampled data
of the CFD model is pre-processed.
4.2.1
Pre-Processing
The sampled data is pre-processed in the preProcess.m script before it is ready as input for the
particle model, see Appendix A.13. The sampled ﬂow kinematics are interpolated on a uniform
grid using the function griddedInterpolant. The interpolation is done in time and space
in accordance with the mesh resolution and the write interval chosen for the output. Since
the grid is in fact non-uniform, to correctly apply the force balance, an additional variable is
introduced within the coupled model, namely y∗
p, elaborated in the following section.

4.2.2
Running the Model
The particle model is coupled with the interpolated ﬂow data in the mainScript.m script, see
Appendix A.14. The ﬂuid ﬂow is modelled by the mean ﬂow. This method is valid for non-
breaking waves where turbulence is absent.
However, the method is a simpliﬁcation when
simulating breaking waves, where the instantaneous ﬂow is the sum of the mean and the
ﬂuctuating velocity.
The model runs for a set time with a time step according to the output write interval of
OpenFOAM. At each time step, the equations of motion (Equation 3.17) are solved numerically
to estimate the particle position and velocity.

to the uniform grid, that is, for (xp, y∗

Boundary Conditions
A number of boundary conditions are applied within the coupled model. The surface boundary
condition is kept in accordance with the script of the random walk model, stating that the
particle cannot leave the water surface. The position of the water surface can be deﬁned by
αthreshold. Setting αthreshold = 0.5 deﬁnes the surface where there is an equal fraction of water
and air, see Section 2.1.2.
The bottom boundary condition is implemented in accordance with the description in Sections
3.1.5 and 3.1.6.
In the case of a cyclic domain, conditions at the cyclic boundaries are kept as implemented
in the script of the random walk model. This states that if the particle position exceeds the
length of the domain, the particle keeps its position but extracts the interpolated data at the
x∗

length for the cyclic setup.
Beaching
According to Larsen et al. (2023), a particle is considered beached when it is onshore of the
water level and the wave has fully withdrawn, excluding potential resuspension of the particle
oﬀshore. However, due to the simpliﬁed implementation of bed friction and the impermeable
rigid bed, such beaching was not observed during model trial runs. Instead, a simpliﬁed solution
was kept, as introduced in the script of the random walk model, deﬁning particles as beached
when they reached a certain x-position. This x-position is determined as the x-coordinate for
the still water level (SWL). For the plane bed, this is xbeached = 21.2223 m and for the barred
bed xbeached = 21.8933 m.

Results and Discussion
In this chapter, the results of the coupled model for both buoyant and non-buoyant particles

rectangle with dimension 3 mm x 2 mm x 1 mm, but in the model, it is treated as a sphere
with a nominal diameter of dn = 3.1 mm. For this reason, the modelled particle is more likely
to be exposed to the return current due to its height. In general, most of the particles with
high Dean numbers in Guler et al. (2022) had shapes with short heights, e.g. the disk shape.
This could indicate that these particles were less exposed to the return current due to their
heights, therefore having onshore movement. Final conclusions cannot be made on this matter
as the coupled model does not account for eﬀects due to shape.

Key Takeaways
In summary, for simulations on both barred bed and plane bed, it was argued that the main
limitations of the coupled model is the use of a rigid bed, the absence of bed forms, the shortage
of components in the wave spectrum, and the lack of randomness. These four factors contribute
to the particle distributions generated by the coupled model deviating from those observed in
the experimental results by Guler et al. (2022). In addition, this explains the rather narrow
particle distributions for the coupled model results compared to the experimental results, which
generally show more widespread distributions.
The run time of the particle model was in the order of hours (ranging 0.5 hr-10 hr) for simulating
30 min transport for a total of 30 non-buoyant particles. For a repeating phase of 30 s, the run
time for the CFD model was approximately 12 hr (excluding warm-up time). This example
serves to emphasise the potential of the coupled model, allowing high simulation time for
numerous particles at low computational cost.
On a ﬁnal note, it is worth mentioning that while the results of the coupled model were not able
to fully validate the experimental results, the modelled results are likely to be representative
for the microplastic particle distribution in an actual coastal environment. This is arguable
due to the eﬀect of scaling: In a wave ﬂume setup, the Shields parameter, θ, will be an order
of magnitude smaller compared to θ in a real-scale coastal zone. Therefore, in a coastal envi-
ronment, bedforms will be washed out and the presence of vortex ripples are therefore unlikely.
Consequently, the microplastic particles, particularly those on the plateau, may accumulate
diﬀerently than observed experimentally. It can therefore be argued that the distribution of
the coupled model would resemble that of an actual coastal environment.

This section covers further developments of the CFD model as well as suggestions for optimi-
sations related to the particle model.
Improving the near-surface values of the Du/Dt is an important matter to prioritise in the
further development of the coupled model. This was identiﬁed as the primary driver for over-

estimating buoyant particle velocities in non-breaking waves. Alternative ways of outputting
Du/Dt when running waves2Foam should be investigated.
The results in Section 5.3.1 indicated that the random walk model was good for capturing the
buoyant particle behaviour in non-breaking waves. For this reason, it would be interesting to
use the random walk model in comparison with the experimental results by Larsen et al. (2023)
in future investigations. However, due to memory limitations in MATLAB, it was not possible
to obtain the interpolated data for the random walk model in one single ﬁle. This increased the
run time of the random walk signiﬁcantly to an extend where comparative analysis of the wave
ﬂume setup was not possible within this thesis. Diﬀerent measures can be taken to reduce the
size of the interpolated data ﬁle. This includes reducing the spatial and temporal resolution,
however this could be at the cost of model accuracy.
The convergence analysis showed that the write interval could be decreased for simulation of
particles in non-breaking waves as well as for non-buoyant particles.
It could therefore be
advantageous to use diﬀerent strategies when choosing temporal resolution depending on the
modelling case.
Regarding the particle model, it is proposed that the force balance presented in Equation 3.17
can be expanded to account for additional forces, as implemented in comparable CFD-DEM
models.
The implementation of bed friction could be improved additionally by expanding
the current expression of Equation 3.16 to account for static friction during ﬂow reversal.
Furthermore, the rotation of particles could be implemented as this proved to be important for
non-buoyant particles in Guler et al. (2022).
The simpliﬁcation of all particles as spherical in the current version of the coupled model
could be overcome by including a more elaborate drag model to account for diﬀerent shapes.
Experimental results by Goral et al. (2023) showed that sharp-edged particles had a constant
drag coeﬃcient, which could easily be accounted for. Similarly, a shape-dependent relationship
for the added mass coeﬃcient should be included.
It is our hope that in the future the coupled model can be utilised to accurately estimate mi-
croplastic particle transport in coastal environments. This can help identify accumulation sites
and provide insights into particle movement, thereby allowing for eﬃcient waste management
strategies. The coupled model demonstrates a cost-eﬀective utilisation of CFD to model particle
transport, thereby showcasing its potential application beyond microplastics to other particle
types. This includes the possibility of employing a similar coupled model as a computationally
eﬃcient scour model.

Conclusion
From the ﬁndings of this master thesis, it can be concluded that a cheap and valid coupled
model can be developed for simulating the motion of microplastic particles under non-breaking
as well as breaking waves. This was done by coupling the ﬂow simulated in the CFD software
OpenFOAM with a particle model set up in MATLAB. The model’s short run time makes it
computationally cheap as it was validated that only one CFD solution for a single wave period
(for regular waves) or a single repeating phase (for irregular waves) was needed.
Based on the results of the coupled model, it is concluded that a simple force balance that
only considers translational particle motion and assumes spherical shape is able to capture the
main physics of motion and to some accuracy predict the behaviour and distribution for both
buoyant and non-buoyant particles.
It is concluded that the coupled model overestimates the horizontal particle velocities for buoy-
ant particles in regular waves by a factor 2 when applying αthreshold = 0.9. This was seen when
comparing with experimental results by Alsina et al. (2020) as well as quantitatively by com-
paring with the Lagrangian drift. The overestimation was identiﬁed to be related to spiking
interface values of Du/Dt. It is concluded that this issue should be resolved to increase the
performance of the coupled model for buoyant particles. Until then, the random walk model
may be a better choice for modelling buoyant particles in non-breaking waves.
The coupled model showed an adequate match with the experimental results by Larsen et al.
(2023) for buoyant particle in the surf zone. The coupled model was able to capture the Dean
number dependency of the particle drift, where lower Dean regime particles travelled faster
than particles in the high Dean regime. Results revealed that the model was able to capture
the physical mechanism of low Dean particles surﬁng on the breaking waves.
It is concluded that the coupled model was able to capture the particle motion of non-buoyant
microplastic particles beneath regular waves when assuming zero Eulerian drift. The robustness
of the result was emphasised by comparing with model results obtained by the random walk
model and CFDEM, showing an equally good match with experimental results by Alsina et al.
(2020). Based on this, it is concluded that the coupled model is advantageous to apply for
non-buoyant particles beneath non-breaking waves as it is computationally cheaper and allows
for user control of the force balance.
From simulations of non-buoyant particles beneath breaking waves on a barred and a plane bed,
it is concluded that the coupled model is to some degree able to capture the particle distribution
when comparing with the experimental results by Guler et al. (2022). The deviations observed

could be explained by the model’s absence of randomness, lack of bed formations, and the use
of a rigid bed. Additionally, the assumption of spherical shape and the short repeating phase

capture Dean number dependencies to some extend, showing oﬀshore movement for low Dean
number particles and onshore for high Dean numbers.
In conclusion, the coupled model provides a promising framework for modelling the transport
of both buoyant and non-buoyant microplastic particles in the nearshore region.

Updated Project Description
All the goals presented in the initial project description was reached within the scope of this
master thesis. The thesis investigate the wave-induced transport of buoyant and non-buoyant
microplastic particles by computational ﬂuid dynamics simulation in the software OpenFOAM.
The aim is to develop a coupled CFD and particle model to describe the nearshore transport
