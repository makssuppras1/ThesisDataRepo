Abstract
Renewable energies are entering more and more in our lives, changing our landscapes, our
environment and, in a more silent way, our distribution systems. Finding a way to facilitate
their adoption is a challenge that still has to be overcome.
The subject in this project is a rSOC1 micro-plan with a hydrogen storage able to absorb
the energy provided by solar panels or the grid and use it when it is needed. The aim is to
ﬁrst reduce the price of energy and second, provide an ally to high variable load grids.
Firstly, a new simulation program for PV2 panels have been modelled. The model is able to
provide reliable data according to the environmental condition starting from the technical
information from the manufacturer.
Secondly, the work has been focused on modelling
the micro rSOC power plant, designed for single households with annual need of around
3500kWh without electric heating. The ﬁnal system was able to provide a 64% round-trip
eﬃciency producing also 80 liters of warm water in an average day using 4kW of input power
in SOEC3 mode and providing 2kW in SOFC4 mode. The ﬁnished system has been tested
in two diﬀerent environments; North Europe (Denmark-Copenhagen) and South Europe
(Portugal-Lisbon), ﬁnding big diﬀerences in the system setup and the solar panels output.
While in the north it is diﬃcult to provide enough energy during the winter, in Portugal a
stand-alone micro-plan can be installed using half of the solar panels area than its danish
counterpart.
Finally, an economical analysis is performed in the two scenarios for the 2020 case and 2030
projection. The results showed that the system is already economically viable in Portugal
in 2 out of 3 setups (IRR5 of 17.4% and 9%, PBT6 7 and 13 years) while Denmark struggles
with just one setup (IRR of 10.4%, PBT 11 years). The 2030 projection shows a bright
future due to the natural decrease of the main components’ price. In both countries two
setups are economically possible, while Portugal (IRR of 26.2% and 16.6%, PBT 5 and 7
years) is still a better place to invest in the project than Denmark (IRR of 14.8% and 6.5%,
PBT 8 and 15 years).
Ultimately, the simulation proved the impossibility to pay back the investment in 20 years
just by using and storing self produced electricity without selling the overproduced energy
back to the grid.

Introduction
Climate change is one of the biggest threats the world as to face in order to prevent a dra-
matic and uncertain future. Nowadays, almost, everybody recognize the problem and is
committed to contribute to ensure a better environment for the future generations.
The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) deﬁnes it as a
change of climate that is attributed directly or indirectly to human activity, altering the
composition of the global atmosphere.
Human activity includes the pollution that arises from industrial processes and other sources
that produce, as a waste, greenhouse gases. These gases, such as carbon dioxide, have the
ability to absorb the spectrum of infrared light and contribute to the warming of our atmo-
sphere creating a sort of ”greenhouse”. Once produced, these gases can remain trapped in
the atmosphere for tens or hundreds of years.[1]
The Swedish Nobel prize laureate Svante A. Arrhenius (1896) was among the ﬁrst to theorize
about the impact of CO27 on the earth’s climate. However, these ideas were initially met
with criticism and fell into obscurity until around the 1950s. Various future pathways for
society, its energy system and the associated release of greenhouse gases are a cornerstone
in the estimation of future climate change.[2]
The largest source of greenhouse gas emissions from human activities is produced from fossil
fuels combustion for electricity, heat, and transportation. In the USA the transportation
sector generates the largest share of greenhouse gas emissions with 28.5% of the total closely
followed by Electricity production (28.5%), Industry (22%) and, lastly, Commercial and Res-
idential (11%).[3]
Today, around 81.4% of the primary energy supply rely on fossil fuels with oil account-
ing for 31.7%, coal for 28.1% and natural gas for 21.6%. Combustible biomass and waste
(9.7%), nuclear power (4.9%) and hydroelectric dams (2.5%) are the largest contributors to
the global energy system after fossil energy, but they account for only a minor share of the
global primary energy supply [4]. A mere 1.5% of the world’s primary energy is derived from
geothermal, wind, solar or other alternative renewable energy sources.[5] In details, wind
provided more than half of renewable growth, while solar energy contributed almost a third
despite accounting for only 18% of the total.[6]
However, this trend is changing, especially after the Paris climate agreement of the 22nd of
April 2016 where the United Nations come together to tackle the problem. In particular, they
agreed on the need of increase ”the ability to adapt to the adverse impacts of climate change
and foster climate resilience and low greenhouse gas emissions development, in a manner that
does not threaten food production; and making ﬁnance ﬂows consistent with a pathway to-
wards low greenhouse gas emissions and climate-resilient development.” (Article 2.b and c)[7]
In the last few years the renewable sources of energy meet 40% of the increase in primary
demand and their explosive growth in the power sector marked the end of the boom years
for coal. Renewables capture two-thirds of global investment in power plants as they be-
come, for many countries, the least-cost source of new generation. Rapid deployment of

solar photovoltaics (PV), led by China and India, helps solar become the largest source of
low-carbon capacity by 2040, by which time the share of all renewables in total power gener-
ation is expected to reach 40%. In the European Union, renewables account for 80% of new
capacity and wind power will become the leading source of electricity soon after 2030, due to
strong growth both onshore and oﬀshore. Policies continue to support renewable electricity
worldwide, increasingly through competitive auctions rather than feed-in tariﬀs, and the
transformation of the power sector is ampliﬁed by millions of households, communities and
businesses investing directly in distributed solar PV. Growth in renewables is not conﬁned
to the power sector; the direct use of renewables to provide heat and mobility worldwide
also doubles, although from a low base.
Electricity is the rising force among worldwide end-uses of energy, making up 40% of the
rise in ﬁnal consumption to 2040.[8]
The incredible growth of Wind and Solar power generation opens the market to a broad of
diﬀerent technologies studied to support their development helping to overcome the typical
drawbacks proper of the renewable energies. In particular there is the obvious impossibility
to produce power from solar during the night and, for both Solar and Wind, the stochastic
presence of the energy source leads to a random power production with many picks and
valleys. That production curve is diﬃcult to optimize in order to adapt to the demand;
therefore devices capable to absorb energy during the picks and give energy during the pe-
riod of low production in order to level the loads are needed.
Electrical energy storage systems are, therefore, expected to be a critical component for
the future of energy, performing load-levelling operations to enable increased penetration of
renewables and provide distributed generation. Reversible Solid Oxide Cells (rSOC) tech-
nology is expected to play a key role in this scenario since it can operate in both power
producing (Solid Oxide Fuel Cell, SOFC) and fuel producing (Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cell,
SOEC) mode. As result, synthetic fuel can be produced from any electricity source.
A stand-alone energy storage system can be realized by coupling the two modes of operation
with intermediate storage of gaseous ”fuel” and ”exhaust” species. In this project a Dual-
mode Solid Oxide Cell system for small scale and individual use will be modelled and deeply
analyzed taking into account the stack thermal management, system optimization, dynamic
system operation and economic analysis of these systems. The ﬁnal goal is to provide the
knowledge to apply this new technology to single households and, thanks to its scalable
design, to hotels, shopping centers or even hospitals. Since the prices of technologies are
falling, the cost of the system could be soon aﬀordable for an individual use. Therefore, an
overview on the predicted prices over the time will be provided to give idea and an input for
a real future application.
The system is designed to absorb renewable energy when the production exceed the demand

2.4
Load levelling
Renewable energies are well known to not supply a constant power output, their production
is heavily inﬂuenced by the natural phenomena such as: time of day, moon phase, season,
and weather condition. The generation capacity can variate in a very short time period and
therefore a grid fed mostly with renewable sources can be subjected to sharp and diﬃcult to
foresee variations.
Moreover, the irregular production has to meet the consumption curve that is itself very
variable. This problem is addressed as load levelling, load matching, or daily peak demand
reserve and it refers to the use of various techniques to store excess electrical power during
low demand periods to release them as the demand rises.
The goal is, for the power supply system, to reach a Load Factor15 of 1.[13] The idea is to
store electrical energy from intermittent power plants when it exceeds the consumption and
provide the power back when the production is lower than consumption, whereas it would
otherwise have to be transmitted for sale elsewhere.

2.6
Power Storage
One of the new century’s biggest challenges and the main obstacle for the renewable energies’
development is the storage. The increasing energy demand and the need to be free from
greenhouse gasses requires a compatible storage technology that can collect enough power
to compete with the fossil fuels’ high energy density.
To address the vastness of the problem and the crucial role that the storage will have in
the near future it has been foreseen, from the French consulting ﬁrm Yole D´eveloppement,
that the “stationary storage” market could be a $13.5 billion opportunity by 2023, compared
with less than $1 billion in 2015.[14]
Till now, none of the technologies available in the market have proven to be better than
another, and still, even though the technology has been improved massively, the capacity is
not close to the non renewable competitors.
The choice to use hydrogen instead normal lithium-ion batteries is due to three main reasons:
they are economically comparable in price,[15] H2 can be kept indeﬁnitely in tanks while
batteries loose power after few weeks [16] and because compressed hydrogen has a sensibly
higher energy density allowing to save space in big storages. It’s also believed that hydrogen
will have a main role in the transition to the renewable energies with a possible hydrogen
grid throughout the major cities,[16] therefore having a H2 storage can be a great asset and
investment for the future.
There are two ways to store the hydrogen produced by electrolysis. The ﬁrst, is using a
pressurized tank; this method is well known and already vastly used in every hydrogen
application. The second is the solid state storage using metal hydrides; this method is still
in development and in an early stage but in a, not too far, future it has the potential to
make the diﬀerence projecting the whole system in a better competitive level.[17]

2.6.2
Solid State
A solid-state storage system based on metal hydrides, compared with the common pressurized
physical storage systems, is safer and it is capable to store more hydrogen per unit volume.[21]
According to the actual research state, the solid storage can be collected following two
diﬀerent routes. The ﬁrst one is based on the chemical absorption of atomic hydrogen in
simple or complex light metallic hydrides, whereby the second one relies on the adsorption
of molecular hydrogen on high surface area materials.[17]
Regarding the ﬁrst method, at a given temperature the hydrogen in the storage splits into
atoms at the surface of the metal and then enters the metallic lattice in the atomic form,
the hydrogen begins to dissolve in the host metal forming a solid solution, jumping between
interstitial sites; and ﬁnally forms a hydride phase with a more or less ordered hydrogen
sub-lattice.[22]
The working pressure and temperature should be in the ranges from 1 to 10bar and from
-20 to 100◦respectively, allowing therefore to be used at low pressures and temperatures,
enhancing accordingly the safety.
None of the two diﬀerent storage solid state methods mentioned are developed enough for a
large scale application. However, many innovative systems are promising enough to justify
active research in the next years, in order to oﬀer the market an eﬃcient, safe and possibly
cheap hydrogen reservoirs.[17]

4.1.1
PV Cell Theory
At the base of the PV technology there is the photovoltaic phenomenon that allows to con-
vert sunlight directly into electricity, i.e. generate a potential diﬀerence at the junction of
two materials in response to electromagnetic radiation.[28]
The eﬀect was ﬁrst observed in 1839 by Edmund Becquerel who noted that a voltage ap-
peared when one of two identical electrodes in weak conducting solution was illuminated.[29]
The light is composed by particles of energy called photons whose contain diﬀerent amounts
of energy correspondingly with the diﬀerent wavelengths of the spectrum.
When a photovoltaic cell is hit by a photon it can be absorbed or reﬂected back; in the ﬁrst
case it produces electricity while in the latter it is wasted.
When a photon is absorbed its energy is transferred to an electron in the cell. From the
atom this electron can escape from its normal energy level position and becomes part of the
current ﬂow in an electrical circuit.[9]
To make it work the cells are made from a selected material which is always a semiconductor
and in the great majority silicon.
Page 17 of 100
==PAGE:22==
DTU
The production of electricity using the photovoltaic eﬀect can be divided into three points
(Figure[4.1]):
• Generation of charge carriers:
In the semiconductor the energy can be adsorbed only if it is in a certain band-gap
Eg20, if it is below this energy gap the photon will just pass through.
If an electron is excited from Ei to Ef 21, an hole is created in Ei. That behaves like a
particle with a positive elementary charge which leads to the creation of an electron-
hole pair. Meaning that the radiative energy of the proton is converted to the chemical
energy of the electron-hole pair.
• Separation of the photo-generated charge carriers in the junction:
Normally the electron-hole pair would recombine, however, a semipermeable membrane
is present on both sides of the absorber to separate electrons and holes.
In most the solar cells in the market these membranes are formed by n and p-type
materials22.
• Collection of the charge:
The charge carriers are extracted with electrical contacts so they are able to do work
in an outer circuit.[28]
Last but not least, there is one important loss mechanism in a solar cell: the impossibility to
convert in electricity the photons that exceed or are below the band-gab level of energy. Only
this mechanism account for almost half of the loss of incident solar energy in the conversion
process.[30]

then grouping the strings in series in order to increase the amperage.

4.2
Reversible Solid Oxide Cells
A rSOC is a device that can eﬃciently operate in both fuel-cell and electrolysis modes. Thus,
in the electricity production mode, the systems functions as a SOFC, generating electric-
ity by electrochemical combination of a fuel (hydrogen is used in this study as fuel, other
hydrocarbons or alcohols can be used as well) with the oxygen contained in the air. In
the electrolysis mode, the system runs as an electrolyzer (Section[2.2]) (SOEC), producing
hydrogen from water vapour (other liquids can be used such as syngas) when coupled with
a power source.[33]
The technology has been known for many years, especially the Solid Oxide Fuel Cell has
been used successfully to produce energy from hydrogen since 1960 where a hydrogen-based
SOFC was used to power on-board electrical systems on the Apollo journey to the moon.[34]
However, the technology that allowed to use the same cell in both ways, as Solid Oxide
Electrolyzer Cell, is more recent and it opens a new range of utilization where the fuel can
be ﬁrst produced and the used in the same system.
The RSOC has some attractive features for witch it has been taken in account as a interesting
system to work in the side of renewable energies:
• Environmental compatibility due to the reduced CO2 emissions in power generation
mode.
• High fuel ﬂexibility and possibility to be integrated with any type of electricity source.
• Adaptability with a variety of applications from micro-plants to transportation.
• Competitive price tag.
• Low degradation rate.
The combination of these features leads to a number of distinctive and attractive attributes
for the SOFC including cell and stack design ﬂexibility, multiple fabrication options, multi-
fuel capability, and operating temperature choices. Moreover, SOCs can be conﬁgured to
be self supporting (electrolyte-supported, anode-supported, cathode-supported) or external
supporting (interconnect-supported, sub-layer-supported).
Stack designs being developed for the SOC28 include the tubular design, the segmented
cells-in-series design, the monolithic design, and the planar design, with the planar design
currently being the most common (Figure[4.8]). These design options permit ﬂexibility to
shape the system having the desired electrical performance along with required thermal man-
agement, mechanical integrity, and dimensional constraint to meet operating requirements of
speciﬁed power generation applications.[35] All those features allow to easily scale the power
plant studied from a micro production for single households to big buildings; theoretically
its capability can be indeﬁnitely augmented.
Therefore, the rSOC possesses all the desired characteristics to serve as a technology base
for green, ﬂexible, and eﬃcient energy systems in the near future.

5.6.2
Storage design
Since the battery used in the system is a tank and not chemical accumulator it is possible
to have higher energy storage capacity than usual. The stored amount of hydrogen and
consequently energy is also easily increased just using higher pressures in the SOEC mode
adding a compressor before the tank. As presented in Section[2.6.1] bigger is the volume
lower will be the leaking losses, leading to almost negligible losses and the possibility to
perform seasonal energy storage from summer to winter.
The simulation is done over one year with a temporal step of 1h. It aims to maximize self-
suﬃciency for the household, minimize the amount of electricity bought from the grid and
maximize the utilization of electricity produced from the PV. When maximizing this system,
the sizes of the PV system and the battery need to be predeﬁned and limited, otherwise the
maximization of self-suﬃciency would always result in a PV-battery system with the largest
allowed installation size. Therefore, assuming an average consumption of 10kWh per day
(Table[.1]), taking in account the eﬃciency of the SOFC, an autonomy of two full days (two
days more the night of the last day of production), the storage dimension and pressure; the
result is 30 to 33kWh. Nonetheless, even if the tank is not subjected to degradation due
to discharge below a certain percentage it is not recommended to empty the tank in order
to avoid to switch oﬀthe system. Starting the rSOC cell from zero can take up to an hour
because of the temperatures needed to run the process.

7.1.1
System pressure
The working pressure is really important parameter since it inﬂuences directly the two modes
and the tank design. Looking at Figure[5.12] or the SOFC is clear that a working pressure
from 10bar or more it is optimal since the eﬃciency grows and the hydrogen consumption
diminishes. Regarding the SOEF mode the sensitivity analysis didn’t ﬁnd any variation in
the parameters aﬀected by the pressure. However, the pressure aﬀect directly the cost of the
compressor and the tank design. If for the compressor it is not a problem to go up with the
pressures, the tank is designed to work at low pressures to contain the costs (Section[2.6.1]).
Finally, considering all the aspects, a pressure of 8bar will be used to run the system.
7.1.2
Plant size
The studied system is meant to work for diﬀerent input and outputs using the same design
thanks to the modular nature of both the rSOC stacks and PV modules. The project is
focused to the smallest plant economically viable, it is thought to work for single households
with a power demand of around 10kWh per day.
The physical dimensions are really small with the plastic 1.5 cubic meters (8bar) tank taking
most of the space. The other components due to the limited mass-ﬂows have a modest size.
The whole plant has the dimension of a big fridge.

ηOverall = (ηSOFC + ηDH) · ηSOEC = 0.64

Overview and Discussion
The study started with the modelling of a DNA component able to give realistic predictions
of diﬀerent PV modules output. It has been used to calculate the electrical production and
design the PV modules in the two scenarios studied. Since the solar panels are the only
part of the system to be placed outside, the environmental conditions play an important role
and they have to be carefully calculated. The results of the model testing (Table[4.2 and
Figure[4.5]) shows a great approximation especially in the ”knee point” (where there is the
maximum power production and the module is working) and for the irradiance grater than
700G.
The rSOC micro-plan as been modelled using DNA. The two modes have been coded in
diﬀerent ﬁles since they work only when the other one is oﬀand vice versa. Several designs
have been investigated, choosing as ﬁnal one the simplest in therms of components and with
better overall performances (Figure[5.1]). The sensitivity analysis showed good ﬂexibility
and scalability of the system within the investigated parameters, they are found to stay
mostly constant (Table[5.1] for SOEC and Figure[5.12] for SOFC) or proportional to the
changed variable (Important is the behaviour varying the power Figure[5.7]). This is an
asset for a further development of the system to supply higher power.
The two working modes showed a good integration as result of a design that led to adopt
a 4kW SOEC and a 2kW SOFC and a working pressure of 8.2bars (Figure[5.12]). On one
hand, in Figure[7.1] is calculated that the Hydrogen produced in SOEC mode is enough to
feed the SOFC phase during periods of low sun irradiation. On the other hand, in Figure[7.2]
is shown that the hot water production could not be suﬃcient to feed the SOEC, therefore
it is needed to be supplied also from another source. Moreover, the plant is able to provide
an average of 78 liters of warm water (60◦) per day to the house, helping to consume less for
heating (Table[7.1]).
The overall round-trip eﬃciency for the system with the design values (Table[7.2]) and the
district heating is calculated to be 64% while the only electrical round-trip eﬃciency is 45%.
Denmark and Portugal have been chosen to be the countries where the system is simulated
because of their high electricity price (Figure[5.14]), considerable share of renewables in the
system and diﬀerent environmental conditions (Figure[5.15] DK, Figure[5.17] PT).
Portugal has a sensibly more favourable environment to host the PV-based power plant
(Figure[7.5]) compared to Denmark (Figure[7.3]).
That is reﬂected in the battery test
(Figure[7.6], Figure[7.4]) where the battery is used the whole year in PT and just in the
summer months for DK.
The economical analysis is done in a 20 years period with a contingency of 15% taking in ac-
count the countries’ individual prices and economical scenario (Table[7.3],Table[.4]). Turns
out a clear trend of the project towards the future, the 2030 scenario with the cheaper solar
panels and rSOC modules (but lower prices of selling) favourites the return of the invest-
ment and make it safer (Table[8.1]). However, already today it is possible to safely invest in
Portugal and in general all the southern countries with a consistent sun availability.
Finally, the system modelled and the reversible fuel cell technology in general has a big
potential, the study done highlighted their feasibility and therefore they are worth of further

Conclusions
The study showed the feasibility of the project for certain scenarios with a better chance to
have a payback installing the rSOC micro-plan in a 10 years future. The success of the system
rely in part on the future political and energetic development of the single countries. The
simulations have shown that selling the surplus energy during the overproduction months
plays a fundamental role, more important than the peak shaving service that the system can
provide.
Since the future is pushing toward renewable energies, an eﬀective method to store energy
is required, therefore betting on the Hydrogen is most likely the safest option. The storage
technologies are mature while the fuel cells system have been recently stating to widespread
and in few years the eﬃciency and life-time will be further improved allowing to provide a
cheap, safe and green option in service of renewable energies.
The Portuguese scenario provided better results than the Danish one, that is obviously due
to the more favorable conditions, especially in therms of solar irradiation. In Lisbon the
investment is safer; when it is feasible in both the countries, the pay back time is sensibly
lower and the initial investment is one third less than its counterpart.
The conclusion is that the system designed in this project have its better conditions in
southern countries, the optimal would be regions with above average electricity prices, a big
share of renewable energies and with a modern grid able to withstand peak-shaving devices.
For Denmark and Nordic countries in general, the system with solar panels is more a bet on
the sun availability and on the grid electricity prices. For the 2030 scenario the investment
is more secure but the revenues rely on an unsure and politically unclear compensation
(such as peak-shaving, energy selling and variable prices contracts). Therefore, it would be
interesting to simulate a diﬀerent system working for a bigger community and using wind
instead of sun to generate power. That would use the scale factor in its favour and could
optimize the battery sharing the energy among more households.
In the end, the future is bright for Hydrogen and renewable energy storage, this study showed
a solution easily scalable, doable for single households, allowing privates to contribute to a
greener and renewable world.

9.1
Recommendations for a future work
The RSOC micro-plan presented is in the early stages of the development, the thesis aim
was to investigate the feasibility of the system both from the thermodynamic and economic
point of view.
What need to be done is a more detailed simulation where the day to day charge and dis-
charge of the storage, the expected hydrogen production and the dependence from the grid
can be tested with real data. Real time data from many year has to be taken in account and
ﬁnally calibrate the system to the optimum. Especially for Denmark, and all the countries
with a major share of wind power, the price of electricity should also be taken in account
with real data since it is not unusual that during overproduction the price fall signiﬁcantly
and design the storage size and the PV modules accordingly to that.
The system can be easily scaled to any kind of power, therefore a study for production and
storage up to Megawatts and even Gigawatts can be interesting considering the urgent need
for cost-eﬀective energy storage capability in Europe.[50] For Nordic regions can also be
possible to have a bigger storage and do season to season electricity conservation. That is
possible to the very low losses of hydrogen from the tank (Section[2.6.1]). Finally, another
interesting and eﬃcient application could be a shared rSOC for community-ownership, al-
lowing to use the scale factor to reduce the price and the many users to have a smooth and
more predictable utilization of the system.
