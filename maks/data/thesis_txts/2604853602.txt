Abstract
Great potential lies in unlocking the capabilities of robots through simulation in terms
of cost, productivity, and flexibility. The concept of a digital twin has emerged in recent
years aimed at improving the impact of simulations. A digital twin constitutes a virtual
replica of a physical system and has been one of the most significant advances in Industry
4.0. Twins can be used to develop and test systems in a simulated environment before
the actual system is deployed. In essence, the twin takes real-world data from a system
as input and produces a response identical to the original system. The central idea is
that the physics of the physical system is mimicked or copied by using data to develop
a model that simulates the real-world system in a virtual environment.
Such systems can help in testing autonomous systems that operate in uncertain
environments and have to make crucial decisions that become increasingly demanding
with an increase in the complexity of the system and the environment. These digital
models can also provide valuable insights into system behaviour as we move towards
developing and testing complex systems (robots, autonomous vehicles) in the real world.
These simulation-based digital twins can also speed up the development process by
allowing developers to solve design issues faster. They can also help in predicting the
behaviour of the systems in novel environments with a high degree of accuracy. The
results obtained from a simulation can also be used for real-time operations.
In this work, we create a software-based digital twin for a small mobile robot (REG-
BOT) with multiple control loops and laws. The robot executes certain control loops,
which have been transformed into their digital equivalents and are assessed on their
ability to replicate and predict the motion of the hardware equivalent. We also analyze
the circumstances under which the behaviour of the twin starts to deviate from the real-
world system. This analysis is performed using MATLAB and Webots, an open-source
robot simulator used for designing virtual simulation environments and exploring multi-
ple simulation possibilities. Finally, we test our twin on a virtual replica of the RoboCup
arena.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The development process for autonomous systems requires the design of critical subsys-
tems for planning and control. While these subsystems can be individually designed
and tested, their integration within the larger autonomous system can be complex and
challenging. Combining subsystems designed and tested in isolation can result in unde-
sirable interactions between the constituent subsystems and lead to unexpected behavior.
Furthermore, it may be difficult to separate fundamental design problems from imple-
mentation problems like software errors. In this work, we present a Digital Twin for a
robot and its simulation environment which consists of virtual components designed to
be equivalent to their real-world counterparts. For this work, we built a World Model
that acts as a virtual replacement for the real-world environment and provides the
ground truth for the sensors in the simulation environment. This allows us to perform
safe and reliable testing of the planning and control subsystems of a robot; activities
that would otherwise have to be performed on the real-world robot and would require
actual hardware.
Testing of robot systems in a simulation environment has significant advantages over
implementing it in the real world. Testing in simulated environments is cheaper, safer
for the user and can be designed to help the user observe and validate the state(s) of the
virtual world, and interact with it.The performance of individual sub-systems within the
robot can also be visualized.
Simulation-based digital twins constitute the virtual replicas of physical systems and
have been one of the most significant advances in Industry 4.0. These digital simulations
provide us more insight into complex systems as we move towards developing and testing
complex systems (robots, autonomous vehicles) in the real world.
Digital twins aim to improve the impact and fidelity of simulation capabilities to a
point where testing in digital environments can be considered functionally equivalent to
real-world testing. Since digital twins are a virtual representation of physical device/as-
set within a larger system, they can be considered a part of the larger Cyber-Physical
Systems(CPS) paradigm; systems that are capable of mirroring their static & dynamic
characteristics. In essence, digital twins can be seen as an integration of the digital
and physical forms of a system which allow the realization and testing of configurations
(and re-configurations) of a system in a functionally-equivalent simulated environment
instead of the real-world.

1 Introduction
During this thesis, we investigate the process of reconstructing the required charac-
teristics for a REGBOT used at DTU within a digital RoboCup simulation environment.
This involves the study and development of necessary capabilities to be able to simulate
the motion & other behavior of the robots like line following. Finally, we simulate the
behavior of the robot for motion commands provided by an interface. This interface has
been designed to be functionally equivalent to the actual interface that interacts with
the REGBOT. This setup serves as a realization for the core concept of digital twins.
The developed interface (GUI) to communicate with the REGBOT is used to evaluate
the developed simulated environment in terms of how well can it replicate the motion
commands and also the cycle time. The functionality is particularly assessed on its
ability to replicate and predict the motion of the hardware equivalent.
To summarize, this thesis revolves around the creation of the digital twin for REG-
BOT which is used for educational purposes at DTU. The robot consists of certain
control loops, which have to be transformed into a digital equivalent.We assess & inves-
tigate how well the loops could perform compared with the real-time conditions of the
robot. Our end objective is to introduce the real-world obstacles into the virtual world
and see how well our proposed system works in terms of the robot’s ability to see the
obstacles properly, to map the environment well, and to follow the desired trajectory.
This would help us in establishing a link between the real and virtual world and help us
in identifying and visualizing system(robot) errors before they occur.
Within DTU, REGBOT is also used as the framework for the course Linear Control
Design-1 course for various experiments.

1.1
Objectives
In this work, our goal is to create the required capabilities and characteristics that
enable a robot, specifically REGBOT, to work within the digital twin framework of
the RoboCup simulated environment. To build a digital equivalent, the following core
functionalities need to be realized:
• Virtual (Digital) Representation of Robot: A digital representation of the
real robot (REGBOT in our case) would share the same functionality as its real-
world counterpart. It will be presented with tasks & obstacles that are a part
of the RoboCup event like slope trail, motion commands, and line following. A
robot uses multiple control loops to manage motion parameters like velocity, turn,
heading, tilt, and control. In the simulation, we use a software method to specify
these parameters for the digital equivalent. In this work, we monitor how the robot
moves and reacts to motion commands issued to complete the aforementioned tasks.
We also present our observations on differences in differences between sensor data
generated by performing test runs for the physical robot and its digital equivalent.
• Real-World Robot & Physical Interface: Simulations can help us in under-
standing what is happening (how well the design behaves in the real-world) and in
predicting model behavior in different environments. This involves updating the
joint values (the kinematics of the robot) based on the dynamics of the real robot.
This involves updating the state of the robot, the I/O values, and ensuring that
sensor data from the robot is available and can be used as and when required. This
also provides a possibility to optimize certain characteristics of the digital form of
the robot with states from the real robot.
The state of the robot, its joint position, and values, data from various sensors, and
the I/O data is provided to the virtual representation of the robot. A network connection
is used to establish a link between the real robot and its digital equivalent. In this work,
we control the robot using an external open-source software Webots1. The choice of
Webots as the platform for simulating the robot provides us with the necessary flexibility
in adapting the robot for the task at hand.
To summarize, our end objective is to deploy a REGBOT within the RoboCup
simulated environment. In the course of fulfilling this objective, we evaluate our work
along with the following criterion:
1. The accuracy and fidelity of our simulation of the digital environment. This is
based on the movement and cycle time of the robot in response to motion com-
mands provided by us.

1.2
Practicality of Simulations
Simulations are typically used in the design and, in our case, for offline optimization.
They not only help us in understanding how a robot behaves in the real world but also
in predicting its behavior in novel environments. As systems become more complex and
generate more data, it becomes increasingly difficult to test them over multiple scenarios.
In our setup, we consider a situation where real-world data obtained from sensors is fused
to provide a simulated equivalent for the real system. This offers users extensive testing
abilities in a 3D simulation environment. Within the scope of our project, this ability
helps us in evaluating how well our simulation environment replicates real-world behavior.
A simulated environment for REGBOT also provides the user with real-time insights
on robot behavior and can help prevent unfavorable circumstances when it interacts
with the physical world. In this way, Digital Twins help us evaluate what-if scenarios
for our robot. Such functionality helps us in assessing situations where the robot can
deviate from expected behavior. The evolution of the technology behind these twins has
included complex processes and behaviors and has been mentioned in the Gartner Hype
Cycle as seen in Figure 2.1 [Hav+19].

1.3 Scope and Limitations
5
The simulations are model-driven in nature and controlled by the sensor data. In
our case, we aim at building a simulation environment and a twin of the REGBOT that
would exhibit characteristics similar to the physical robot.
Our Simulation is characterized by the following:
• Driven by Sensor-based Data: The sensor data is captured for the robot by onboard
sensors on the robot within the RoboCup environment.
• Digital Framework: With the data provided by the robot, a digital model is simu-
lated in a 3D simulation environment. This model aims at simulating the behavior
of the real world REGBOT.
• Predictive Insights: This digital model helps us to mimic the performance of the
existing physical variant of REGBOT. When simulating different environments/s-
cenarios, it provides us with rich insights on how well our robot performs.
• Offline Simulations: These twins are then simulated on Webots in real-time. Al-
though simulations tend to be computationally intensive, Webots provides a plat-
form to run these simulations with simultaneous updates for sensor-data. Webots
also allows running multiple missions together on the REGBOT.
1.3
Scope and Limitations
Certain limitations apply to the work presented in this thesis:
• The work during the thesis focusses more on creating the required capabilities
to use the REGBOT within a digital twin RoboCup environment.
Overlaying
parameters such as intelligence, optimization, and re-configuring systems are not
considered.
• Only High-level motion commands are considered for simulation.
• Only Static off-line motion is considered for this work.
• Simulations take place in internal(compliance) mode and unexpected events are
not considered.
• Control aspects are handled, but only the accuracy of the internal controller is
taken into consideration for the robot.
• The real robot has a sample time of the order of 1ms, which is faster than what
can be reasonably processed by the simulation physics engine. We do not address
this issue as it would require major changes to the way Webots internally handles
input sensor data.

1.4
State-of-the-Art
The connection between the physical form of a system and its virtual counterpart is cen-
tral to the concept of digital twins. This can be achieved with an appropriate integration
between the cyber and physical environment [Tao+18]. As the real-world system under
study becomes increasingly complex, it is critical to obtain a process model that has a
very high quality in terms of representing the real-robot while at the same moment is ca-
pable of processing multiple numerical based simulations. For complex and challenging
problems in the domain of robotics, it is very important to produce good results when
the system behavior (robot) is available. Digital Twins provide a means to solve a dy-
namic model trajectory with respect to time, given that other model-specific attributes
have been defined. A model, in general, can be considered as an approximation or an
abstraction of a well-defined part of the real-world truth. Simulations provide a means
to observe the model’s behavior w.r.t time.
A central concept within the digital twin framework is the idea of (co)-simulations,
software-based simulations that co-exist with their real-world counterparts. Within a
(co)-simulation, we monitor the ability of the virtual/software copy of the system to
mirror its real-world counterpart. The (co)-simulations allow validation of multi-domain
and cyber-physical systems and offer a flexible solution that allows us to consider mul-
tiple domains within the same time frame. Digital Twins are also provided with sensor
data from the physical robot to allow the software to assess the deviations in predicted
behavior of the robot over time. To facilitate this communication, a software glue/mid-
dleware layer is introduced that provides the network/link between the physical and
virtual environments.
The choice of software tools to build the aforementioned simulation and networking
capabilities is a critical aspect in the design of digital twins for a system. Software
packages such as MATLAB/Simulink and Webots provide a solid base to build these
capabilities. While these software solutions are certainly not perfect, they are preferable
over other solutions as they offer a comparatively higher degree of flexibility during
development. In this work, we use high-level programming frameworks to design and
execute experiments with the REGBOT.
While Digital Twins are key enabling technologies for intelligent systems (autonomous
devices), they are also useful in the context of product life-cycle management. Open
source 3D robot simulators have seen an increase in demand as it supports the building,
execution, and evaluation of the digital twin [Qi+19]. A 2018 study on the Hype Cycle
shows the Digital Twin reaching the top of the peak of expectations, depicting the rise
of Digital Twins as an emerging technology in the near future[EF18]
The objective of the process aiming at converging the physical and virtual states
of a system. This would also enable a physical process with the required mathematical
model that can describe the process to carry out the analyses and then apply the relevant
strategies. These twins have established themselves and have created a competitive edge
in the industry. [20d]

1.4 State-of-the-Art
7
As we move towards the virtual-physical integration, twins provide us with an en-
hanced level of safety in terms of human-machine interaction. This integration will result
in digital models that are more flexible and interactive in nature [Ma+19]. The litera-
ture, however, generally focusses on certain specific use-cases within the manufacturing
industries where autonomous devices are in use.
In this work, we consider a general architecture of the twin which requires the fol-
lowing:
• Calibration: Since the state of a robot is determined thorough sensor data, real-
world measurements are important for us. Consequently, the 3D model needs to
be aligned precisely with the physical world.
• Control: Control logic is an important component as it is the principal means for
the user to interact with the virtual world.
• Process Augmentation: The twins and the simulation environment must provide
the user with an intuitive view of the available information e.g. the data from the
sensors.
There are still certain misconceptions about what a Digital Twin is. A system is
a “Digital Twin” if there is a communication and exchange of information between the
physical and the digital/virtual counterpart of a system. Furthermore, both components
should be able to operate autonomously. A schematic for such a digital-twin is provided
in Figure 1.2). We will formalize the notion of a digital twin in the next chapter.

1.5
Challenges Associated with the Digital
Twin
We now draw our attention to challenges that are associated with digital twins. As
digital twins are researched more, certain challenges around the development of digital
twins have become more clear. This section outlines some major challenges and offers
some practical advice on how to address them.
Infrastructure
Digital twins are a part of the Cyber-Physical System paradigm and
use existing approaches within the internet of Things (IoT) framework to capture, trans-
mit, and process sensor data. Consequently, a successful digital twin requires a good
software and hardware platform for running the physical and virtual representations of
the system and a strong networking layer to synchronize sensor data between the two
environments.
Processing of Data
To obtain a high-quality representation of the real world, the
digital environment requires good quality data, ideally noise-free values. An additional
requirement is that sensor- data reception should be largely uninterrupted. If incoming
sensor-data is extremely noisy or suffers from transmission loss, it can result in an under-
performing system and under extreme circumstances, a complete failure of the digital
twin. The key is to have the right data that can be made available and used efficiently.
Trust and Validation
When implemented at a large scale or for a critical process,
it is important to talk about the initial foundation of the twin, as it is important for
the user or the organization to understand how the twin is beneficial for them.
A
thorough validation of the twin for the real system under study is the key to achieve
trust. Validation can verify that the twins are performing exactly as expected by the
user.
Domain Modelling
The modeling of twins is an additional challenge associated with
the design of digital twins. As previously discussed, there is no standard approach for
the development of a digital twin. The design process involves careful modeling of the
physics of the system.

2.1
Background
Digital twins are the digital equivalent of a physical object, an autonomous robot in our
case.A digital twin mirrors the state of a real-world robot(Physical Counterpart) inside a
simulated environment.As such, a digital twin offers multiple useful applications. A key
application of twins is enabling simulations which could replace the real robot and help in
testing the control software setup for the robot under restricted operating environments.
Consequently, Digital Twins have gained a lot of traction in recent years.
Digital twins are now considered an important aspect of business and rank high
among the top 10 technology trends (Figure 2.1). The initial idea was introduced in
2003 by Micheal Grieves and was released by NASA in 2012 [GS12]. With a Digital
Twin, we aim at integrating all the possible information for the process from start to
end. They help us bridging the virtual and real-world with the end goal of understanding
and predicting the behaviour of the robot and optimizing/improving the corresponding
real-world counterpart.
To facilitate the development of a Digital Twin, complex functionality must be in-
terfaced with data obtained from sensors capturing feedback from numerous complex
functionalities of the twin. This thesis focuses on developing a digital twin for an au-
tonomous robot, which is supplied by data collected from sensors present in its physical
twin. This work specifically focuses on the following objectives:
• Creating a Virtual Representation of the robot setup with all core functionality
from the real-world robot. For users, we introduce the REGBOT Graphical User
Interface (GUI) for managing control aspects, sensor management and data man-
agement aspects to control the remote transmission of data.
• Setting up of the simulation environment which, in our case, is the RoboCup
environment.
We design and test every possible obstacle within the RoboCup
environment in our simulation environment.

Given the plurality of use-cases for digital twins, multiple definitions exist for what
constitutes a digital twin. Despite the relative diversity of use-cases, a common theme
is that digital twins are used for managing development and testing of products and
this involves processing and storing the data throughout the life-cycle of the product as

the physical counterpart during its life cycle.
Rosen et al. suggest that digital twins will be the “the next wave in modelling, simu-

the system and to run forward simulations. These forward simulations are then used to
support action planning for the autonomous system.
A digital twin is essentially a complete digital copy of something physical. It can be
an organism or a system, a process or a device. A digital twin is so accurate that it acts,
reacts, ages and fails in the virtual world in the same way as the physical twin in the
physical world.
The purpose of the digital twin is, via modern sensor technology and artificial intelli-
gence, to simulate its physical counterpart to such an extent that it creates entirely new
insight into the counterpart. This can be extremely valuable for Industry 4.0 companies
as they can improve the performance of a given product and rapidly advance it to the
next generation.
To summarize the vision of the digital twins provided in [Sch+17], digital twins are
considered as: “a bi-directional relation between the physical system and a set of its
virtual models”. These distinct simulations models are flexible enough to evolve from
simple to complex models during the operating life-cycle of the product or system. DT
helps us in assessing the consequences of the design decisions and improve the efficiency
of the systems while minimizing testing costs and time.

digital twin concept is provided by Grieves et al. [Gri14]. It states that a digital twin
consists of only three primary components:
• A physical system in real space.
• A virtual system in virtual space.
• Data and information connections that acts as a converging interface between the
physical and virtual systems.

3.1
Introduction
In this section, we introduce central concepts and terminology used in robot control
software and the physical control loops present within the robot. We also discuss certain
aspects of simulation relevant to our work.
3.2
Overview
Control takes place on different levels within autonomous mobile robots.
High-level
control tasks are used to determine the robot’s next set of actions, e.g. they could be
used to plan the robot’s path through the environment( virtual or real), using inputs
from sensors(IR, Distance Sensor) whereas low-level control tasks are responsible for
executing the action/mission instructions on the robot.
The robot perceives its environment from data in the form of sensor inputs following
which several different approaches can be taken by the robot depending on the nature
of those inputs. In general, a robot uses sensor data and its current internal state of the
world (world-map) to estimate its position and pose. It then combines this information
with high-level instructions from mission scripts provided by the user to move within
its environment. These missions are highly dependant on how accurate or well-defined
the world model is and in some cases, robots cannot react to drastic changes in its
environment quickly. However, Reactive Systems [Bro86] like our mobile robot do not
rely on a world-model but react directly to immediate input from different sensors, thus
allowing a prompt reaction to changes in a dynamic environment. As these techniques do
not use a world-model, their use is limited to applications which do not require long term
planning or learning. Hybrid systems combine the advantages of deliberative systems
(e.g. strategic planning) with those of reactive systems (e.g. fast response to changes in
the environment).
Even though these techniques vary in the way they solve the user-issued mission
task, the general architecture of the robot boils down to three main aspects of the robot
which are referred to as the Robot-Control Procedures. These procedures are the three

critical capabilities that every autonomous robot must possess to operate effectively and
efficiently.
The following are the three critical capabilities:
• SENSE: This gives the robot the ability to sense the properties of the environment.
This can be the presence of obstacles but also what information does the robot have
about its immediate surroundings and how would it gather the relevant information.
In short, it uses the robot’s sensors to provide relevant information on the state of
the robot and it’s the operating environment.
• PLAN: The robot needs to consider the data its receives from the sensors and
decide on how to respond in accordance to the instructions issued to the robot,

the robot. This also involves a determination of whether the system should rely
on issued mission statements or the data it receives from the sensors. In a nutshell,
the robot uses relevant information from the SENSE stage and decides the next set
of actions.
• ACT: Once the robot has finalized a plan, this stage executes the motion com-
mands/actions that the mission calls for. This stage also determines whether a
robot can execute the plan (if a specified motion is possible?). In a nutshell, this
framework is responsible for executing the actions
The Sense-Plan-Act (SPA) framework acts on different levels within a control frame-
work. For example, at the servo-motor level where it handles information concerning
the motor’s position and velocity whereas on the planning level it handles information
about the motion of the robot.

• Proportional: The P-action is one of the most relevant control for us as it controls

large steady-state errors.

factor. We cannot independently tune the three gains, Kp, Ki, Kd. They offer

Control Loops
• Open Loop: This control framework does not account for the output of the
system. The output of the system is hence not correlated with the inputs of the
system. The disadvantages are that the controlled variable is very sensitive to
changes that unsettle the inputs. This concept is illustrated in Figure 3.4.
If our robot was associated with an open-loop system, the output of the system

simply follow the instructions from the user input irrespective of the outcome. In a
nutshell, within an open-loop system, the output does not affect the initial actions
of the robot controller.

At present, Webots simulations can be run in two different modes:
1. Real-time: The simulations in this mode are executed in ‘real-time’, which implies
that simulations are executed in the same amount of time as would be required to
run them in Real-Life. Thus within the implementation, the physics step of the
simulation is synchronized with the controller inputs. For example, a controller

2. Fast Mode: In this mode, Webots runs without considering the controller exe-
cution time. The physics engine and the controller run at the maximum possible
speed without any graphical output. This is useful when the user wants an overview
of the simulation for long run-times.

is being run compared to real-time execution. For a system running at real-time perfor-
mance, the expected value for this figure is 1. Similarly, for the fast mode, the value of
this figure is greater than 1. This figure provides an interpretable measure for the speed
of the underlying physics engine and by proxy, the speed of the simulation.

most of the experiments, and their limitations are set only by ODE’s capabilities.

4.4
Need for Simulations?
When working with robots, the use of simulation is of significant importance. It enables
the evaluation of different alternatives during the design phase of robot systems and
may therefore lead to better decisions and cost savings. It also supports the process
of software development by providing a replacement for robots that are currently not
on-hand (e. g. broken or used by another person) or not able to endure long-running
experiments (e. g. learning tasks). Furthermore, the execution of robot programs inside
a simulator offers the possibility of directly debugging and testing them.
Although the final objectives are to get the real robots in operation, simulations
often are very useful before deploying real robots in the real world. They are easier to
set up, less expensive and faster in terms of computation. A simulated robot is less
expensive than the real robot and the accompanying real-world setup, giving the user
the flexibility for better design exploration. These simulations also make it easier to use
complex algorithms for the robots which can later be transferred onto the real robots.

can appropriately handle the computational demands of the multiple parallel processes.
Offline physics engines are based on frame-by-frame processing which is slower compared
to real-time simulations.

where the red(level) gives us the level of the red colour of the object when the sensor

A PBRAppearance node is a physically-based visual appearance of a node. ”PBR”
refers to ”Physically-Based Rendering”, which is used to designate a class of shading
models based on the physical properties of an object. For this node, a combination of
the PBR appearance and transparency value of the objects is considered. The roughness
value in case of PBRAppearance is evaluated else is considered as 0. Same is the case
for occlusion, if not stated it is considered as 0. In the end, the value received by the
simulator is divided with the reflection factor (f) value calculated and finally, the lookup
table is used to compute the actual value. We need to know that the distance sensors
will only work if we ensure that the solid nodes are located between the sensors and the
solid bounding area of the solid.
The line following robot can follow a visible path with a white line present on a
black surface. Detection of the white line requires the fine-tuning of the robot to stay
on course, and whenever it deviates from the white line, it corrects itself using sensor
feedback. This line following behaviour is used in multiple applications within industrial
automation and guidance [Gos+12].

6.1
Analysis of the Odometry and Sensors
Odometry is a very important aspect associated with robots. The use of data from
motion sensors is used to estimate the change in position over time. It helps in estimating
the relative position of the robot w.r.t to its origin. This helps the robot(s) determine its
pose when it does not follow the line during the simulation. The offsets can be adjusted
by performing missions to drive the robot in a straight line and compensate for the
errors if any. In our case, we could have done this by correcting the offsets (if they are
found to be large) by changing the size of drive wheels. Given our robot dynamics and
the simulation software, we did not change the sizes as they would spoil the geometry
of the robot. Also, this calibration needs to be iterative so that it does not impact
the other dynamics of the robot. These errors could also be measured quantitatively,
by the UMBmark test. The odometry errors in our case could be Systematic Errors
caused by the imperfections in the kinematics of the robot ( unequal wheel-diameters)
or Non-Systematic errors which are caused by slippage or irregularities. In our case the
small errors in the distance travelled could be due to the properties of the floor and the
complex simulation environment. Thus, it is important to ensure that the robot is aware
of the actual odometric accuracy of the platform, to space the absolute position of the
robot. These non-systematic errors [BF95] in the simulation would possibly occur due
to:
• Over-Acceleration of the robot.
• External Forces while interacting with other bodies or solids.
• Internal Forces due to Castor wheels.
• Non-point wheel contact with the floor.
These odometric calibrations can be corrected in future by considering Non-Systematic
Errors. These errors can be fixed by calibrating the motor encoder data with the Gyro
present in the robot. This could provide us with optimal values.

6.2
Line Sensors
The Line-Sensing task was a very crucial part of this work as it was the most well-built
form of navigation for the robot to move around the map. Eight distance sensors were
mounted below the front bounding object of the robot. The major challenge during this
stage was to obtain good values of reflection to allow the robot to follow the line. The
reflection values varied throughout the simulation and required good calibration. The
light sensors use the Average method to compute the average of the distances measured
by all the rays. This gives us raw readings from the eight distance sensors present on
the REGBOT. We need to identify what surface is below the robot, and if it is an edge,
on what edge is the robot present and what position does the robot have. The nominal
values to the sensor are then obtained by moving the robot to a completely black surface,
where all the distance sensors point towards a black surface and the same is repeated for
the white surface. The values for the individual sensors are then used to map to the raw
value so the black and white correspond to nominal values of 0 & 1. This is formalized
in the following equation:
SensorOutput = s −b
w −b
(6.1)
where ‘s’ is the sensor value, ‘b’ is the value of calibration for black colour, ‘w’ is the
value for the white colour calibration and the Normalized value of the sensors is given
by the Sensor Output. The reflection values from the AD converter are roughly mapped
as around 3000 for the ‘white line‘ and around 950 for the ‘black line‘ or surface.
These values can then be used for analyzing the white line for crossings or valid edges.
If a sensor has value above a (settable) value for fixed crossing, the crossing flag is set.
For a valid line edge, the value should be more than 1/3 of the entire spectrum to qualify
as a valid edge on the white line. The edge can be found by scanning from one side of
the sensors to the other, looking for a value that corresponds to the average value of the
maximum and minimum values. If the sensors are however not placed directly on the
edge, this can be found by interpolation of values on either side of the average. This
results in an offset w.r.t to the middle of the line sensors, and if it is positive it moves
towards the right edge of the line.
In practical cases, this may not always be the case. A small margin between the
sensors would not be an optimal solution to offer. Instead the wide sensor can be used
(range = 10 cm) instead of the old sensors (range = 6cm). This solution only works well
when the robot is perfectly aligned, but in real life, the robots are not always aligned
with the line where the margin is less than 2cm.

Robustness
In terms of robustness, multiple simulations were performed on the same mission to
validate the results and see how well the robot performed. From the analysis above all
the solutions were robust except in the case of the Staircase, Ørsted Challenge, Golf
balls and the 3-gate obstacle. This is because of the limited functionalities of the robot
and simulation software-WEBOTS.
6.15
Summary
From the analysis above we are successfully able to create a good virtual replica of the
real-world environment. Although all the obstacles could not be added to the simulations,
the major navigation obstacles in RoboCup were successfully simulated.
The robot,
given its limited functionality, is not able to perform all the challenges but works well
with most challenges and obstacles in the environment. Given the complexity of obstacles
and the limited simulating capabilities of the robot, this simulation could be considered
a success.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
The primary objective of this work was to develop a virtual/digital simulation for the
REGBOT robot along with its relevant control architecture within the RoboCup envi-
ronment REGBOT robot. In this work, we leverage the Webots robot simulator as our
platform of choice for this task.
In Chapter 1, we introduce the task at hand and define the objectives and scope
of this work. Since our task is centred around the development of a digital twin for an
existing robot, we introduce the software simulator used in this work - Webots with a
brief introduction to the Robot and the REGBOT GUI. An introduction to the Robot
and GUI is provided. In Chapter 2, we survey relevant literature and work in the field
of Digital Twins. Results from the survey suggest that while there is a wide variety of
formal definitions for the Digital twin framework, the central ideas common to Digital
Twins and Cyber-Physical Systems emphasize distinct digital and physical components
of a system that communicate with each other. Importantly, Digital Twins emphasize
that the digital and physical components, while identical, are entirely autonomous. The
chapter also elaborates on why and how can these simulations be useful to our project.
In Chapter 3, we some concepts from Control and rigid body simulations and provide
a brief overview of how the object and the environment physics are transformed into
geometric primitives in simulators.
Chapter 4 discusses the modelling approach of
the REGBOT with an introduction to the core structure of simulations in WEBOTS.
This chapter also sheds light on the existing robot simulators and their advantages. We
chose WEBOTS as our simulator given its high quality and ability to render geometries
very well. It also provides a good quality ODE engine to simulate the physics of the
obstacles in the environment. Chapter 5 introduces the obstacles present in the physical
RoboCup arena. These real-world obstacles are modelled in the virtual world (Webots)
using CAD as high-quality solid structures. Chapter 6 performs an analysis of the
objectives of the project and discusses what went well and what did not. The ability of
our simulated REGBOT to tackle most obstacles in the RoboCup arena demonstrates the
versatility of the robot simulation methodology developed in this work. We also present
some additional evidence that demonstrates that our virtual robot exhibits behaviour
similar to its real-world counterpart. All simulations presented in this thesis were created
using Webots.
Although this work has not addressed all the issues associated with closing the reality
gap, I believe that our results show that using simulation to integrate the simulated
world and real robotic experience is an appealing choice for performing experiments and

7.1
Future Work
We presented an experimental paradigm which would enable us to test complex robots
and simulation environments with speed and accuracy in a real-virtual world. This will
enable further development on the different aspects such as planning, perception and
control of our Autonomous Systems. These simulations can provide us with a high degree
of reliability. In the future, we hope to work on modelling objects that could not be
simulated due to technology constraints. In this direction, we would like to investigate
other ways of modelling complex environments. Optimization techniques could also be
used for better sensor calibrations. An aim to capture the dynamics of the system in a
better way could enhance the accuracy of simulations.
