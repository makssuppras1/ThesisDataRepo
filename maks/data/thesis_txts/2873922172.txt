Abstract 
Biomolecular condensates formed by liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) of intrinsically 
disordered proteins (IDPs) are central to dynamic cellular compartmentalization and have been 
implicated in cellular physiology and neurodegenerative disease. Despite their importance, the 
sequence grammar that governs biomolecular condensate formation and its modulation by 
environmental stimuli such as temperature remains poorly understood, particularly due to the 
limited availability of experimental data. This Master thesis applied the recently developed 
method Condensate-partitioning by mRNA-display (CPmD) [1] to study sequence 
determinants and temperature-dependency of LLPS using the N-terminal domain of the DEAD-
box helicase Ddx4 (Ddx4N1) as a model system. A molecular biology pipeline to produce 
mRNA-displayed peptides from the intrinsically disordered protein (DisProt) library was 
established in the Biophysics group at DTU, which revealed the puromycin-ligation as critical 
bottleneck. Implementing splint ligation using a biotinylated oligo as DNA splint followed by 
biotin-streptavidin purification achieved >90% purity of ligated mRNA and sufficient 
concentration (~30 nM) of mRNA-display. Subsequent CPmD experiments enabled 
quantification of partition free energies of DisProt library peptides in Ddx4N1 condensates 
across different temperatures. The results revealed contribution of positively charged motifs, 
driven by arginine and aromatic residues, to the phase separation of Ddx4N1. In response to 
temperature decrease between 20 °C and 4 °C, the 14 amino acid peptide tiles showed a 
positive offset and no detectable sequence level changes in partition free, while the full-length 
N-terminal domain of Ddx4N1 displayed enhanced phase separation. Further characterization 
of the temperature-dependency would require correcting for overestimation of the dense phase 
volume fraction when calculating the partition free energy. These findings validated CPmD as 
a powerful high-throughput method to dissect the molecular grammar of biomolecular 
condensate formation and demonstrated its reproducibility and applicability to other IDPs 
beyond Ddx4.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Biomolecular condensates 
Reactions in biological systems rely on specific interactions between colocalized molecules of 
high concentration, which is achieved through cellular compartmentalization. In addition to 
membrane-bound organelles such as the nucleus, vesicles and mitochondria [2], this is 
enabled by membrane-less organelles (MLOs) such as nucleoli, ribonucleoprotein granules or 
stress granules [3]. 
MLOs or biomolecular condensates are condensed droplets with liquid-like properties [4] that 
contain high concentrations of proteins and nucleic acids [5]. Instead of being surrounded by 
a lipid membrane, they are formed through liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) [5]. They 
provide a dynamic, tunable form of compartmentalization [4], [6] to regulate biochemical 
activity and respond to environmental changes and enable rapid exchange of components with 
the surrounding environment [3], [7]. MLOs are involved in many essential cellular processes, 
such as regulation of gene expression, RNA metabolism or signal transduction [8], [9]. 
Moreover, they have been linked to a variety of neurodegenerative diseases such as 
Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS), Frontotemporal Dementia (FTS) or Alzheimer’s disease 
[10], [11]. Due to the crucial role of biomolecular condensates in cell biology, it is of particular 
relevance to achieve a mechanistic and predictive understanding of cellular LLPS. 
1.2 Role of disorder in phase separation 
The proteome of MLOs is enriched in intrinsically disordered proteins, which represent a large 
fraction (~30%) of the human proteome [12], [13]. Those proteins show conformational 
heterogeneity and exhibit functionality without a stable three-dimensional structure, thus being 
referred to as intrinsically disordered proteins (IDPs) and regions (IDRs) [14]. They differ from 
structured globular proteins in terms of amino acid composition, sequence complexity, 
hydrophobicity, charge and flexibility [15].  
IDPs often possess low-complexity regions that exhibit multivalent weak interactions involved 
in LLPS [16]. These low-complexity sequences are characterized by low overall diversity of 
amino acids [17]. On the one hand, those sequences are depleted of order-promoting residues, 
such as hydrophobic (Ile, Leu, Val) and Trp which form the hydrophobic core of folded globular 
proteins. They also display low contents of Cys, which contributes to conformational stability 
of folded proteins via disulfide bond formation [14]. On the other hand, they are enriched in 
disorder-promoting amino acids [14], including polar (Gly, Gln, Asn, Ser), positively charged 
(Arg, Lys) and negatively charged (Asp, Glu) amino acids and aromatic side amino acids (Phe, 
Tyr) [17]. In addition, they contain hydrophobic but structure breaking Pro and Ala [14]. Low-
complexity regions are rich in short, repetitive motifs and contain blocks of alternating charge 
[16], [17]. Popular examples for IDPs involved in MLO assembly include Ddx4 [18], FUS [19], 
TDP-43 [20], and LAF-1 [21]. 
1.3 Ddx4 as model system for intrinsically disordered proteins 
The human DEAD-box helicase 4 (Ddx4) is a member of the RNA helicase superfamily 2 (SF2) 
[22] and primary constituent of MLOs like nuage and germ granules [18]. While the function of 
Ddx4 is not fully elucidated, it is a driver of granule assembly and involved in helicase-mediated 
RNA metabolism by ATP-dependent formation of RNA-binding complex or local unwinding of 
==PAGE:12==
2 
RNA duplexes [23]. Further functions include post-transcriptional control in the germline [23] 
and piRNA biogenesis essential for silencing transposable elements [24]. In this context, it has 
been shown that Ddx4 forms condensates that comprise ~75 % water and provide a unique 
solvent-like environment that could enhance electrostatic interactions [25]. Moreover, they act 
as a molecular filter by excluding double-stranded DNA while concentrating single-stranded 
DNA [18]. 
Ddx4 has a helicase core composed of two bacterial recombinase A (RecA)-like domains 
separated by a short semi-flexible linker that contains the characteristic Asp-Glu-Ala-Asp 
(DEAD) motif. The core is flanked by intrinsically disordered C- (50 residues) and N-terminal 
(250 residues) domains [23]. The intrinsically disordered N-terminal domain (Ddx4N1) has 
been shown to undergo reversible phase separation, while the exact mechanism has not been 
finally resolved. It contains clusters of opposing net charge of 8-10 residues in length, 
containing 3-8 similarly charged residues, that are suspected to be involved in phase 
separation through electrostatic interactions. Moreover, Phe-Gly and Arg-Gly pairs are 
arranged in close proximity, indicating that Phe and Arg residues could drive phase separation 
through cation-pi interactions [18] or hydrophobicity [1]. Those interactions are sensitive to 
temperature, ionic strength, arginine methylation and alternative splicing [18]. This tunability in 
combination with its ability to undergo phase separation in vitro make Ddx4N1 a suitable model 
system to study phase separation in biological systems. 
1.4 Studying phase separation through Condensate partitioning 
by mRNA-display 
The structure-function paradigm of protein engineering implies that the amino acid sequence 
encodes the three-dimensional structure of the polypeptide chain, which in turn determines the 
protein function [26] It was derived from concepts explaining enzyme specificity and structures 
of folded proteins determined by X-ray crystallography [13]. In contrast to globular, structured 
proteins, IDPs or IDRs do not possess a defined three-dimensional structure. Their 
functionality thus cannot be derived from the structure-function paradigm [13], [14]. In fact, the 
role of sequence for IDP function is not well understood. This is due to the lack of structural 
information from experiments, which highlights the necessity of alternative measurements that 
leverage novel descriptors to elucidate sequence-determinants, such as phase separation 
propensity [27]. 
In general, sequence-perturbative approaches can be used to study how specific sequence 
features determine a protein property. This approach systematically introduces controlled 
modifications to the amino acid sequence, characterizes the biophysical properties of the 
protein variants and maps the sequence function landscape. 
Recently, Norrild et al. 2025 published a novel method to study the relationship between amino 
acid sequence and LLPS of IDPs, termed Condensate-partitioning by mRNA-display (CPmD) 
[1]. They studied the partitioning mRNA-displayed peptides from the intrinsically disordered 
protein (DisProt) library into condensates of the N-terminal domain of Ddx4 (Ddx4N1). Based 
on the assumption that the peptides experience similar interactions in the condensates as they 
would in longer IDPs, the partitioning of the library members was used to derive energetics of 
protein phase separation. Thus, the combination of mRNA-display and massive parallel 
sequencing enabled identification of sequence determinants. 
==PAGE:13==
3 
mRNA-display is a molecular biological technique that establishes phenotype-genotype 
connection by covalently linking members of a protein library to its own encoding mRNA 
through a puromycin-linker [28], [29]. It is specifically suited for such an analysis, since it 
provides a minimally invasive linker and its building blocks, mRNA and peptides, are natural 
components of biomolecular condensates. However, it has primarily been used to investigate 
protein folding and binding energies, which involves the screening of properties of individual 
molecules. As protein liquid-liquid phase separation is a collective property, this approach 
requires high concentrations of the scaffold undergoing phase transition [1]. 
Ddx4N1 was chosen as a model system before expanding the method to more complex IDPs. 
The peptide has a sufficiently well studied phase separation behavior and can undergo LLPS 
in vitro while being devoid of liquid-to-solid transitions. Lastly, the role of Ddx4 in RNA 
metabolism provided evidence for compatibility of the condensates with nucleic acids from the 
mRNA-display [1]. 
Temperature is one of the most crucial stimuli for liquid-liquid phase separation of proteins, and 
the phase transition behavior in response to temperature changes is encoded by the amino 
acid sequence. Thus, the work of Norrild et al. 2025, which forms the basis of this thesis, should 
be expanded to study the temperature-dependency of biomolecular condensate formation. 
1.5 Aim and scope of this thesis 
Building upon the work of Norrild et al. 2025, this thesis is dedicated to fulfilling the two key 
research objectives: 
1. The first objective is to establish a molecular biology pipeline as described in Norrild et 
al. 2025 to produce mRNA-displayed peptides from the intrinsically disordered protein 
(DisProt) library in the Protein Biophysics group at DTU 
2. The second objective is to identify sequence-determinants and study the temperature-
dependency of Ddx4N1 liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) using Condensate 
partitioning by mRNA-display (CPmD). 
The first part of this thesis describes general thermodynamic principles and sequence-level 
driving forces underlying LLPS in biological systems. Different methods to study the sequence-
space of biomolecular interactions are compared. Lastly, a detailed review of the current state-
of-the-art in mRNA-display is provided. 
The second part of this thesis presents and discusses the experimental results with regards to 
the formulated research objectives. The first research objective requires setting up and 
identifying bottlenecks in the mRNA-display pipeline described by Norrild et al. 2025, followed 
by optimizing for yield and purity of the mRNA-displayed peptides. The second research 
objective involves replicating the CPmD measurements conducted by Norrild et al. 2025 to 
compare sequence-determinants for Ddx4N1 LLPS and then extending those studies by 
examining the influence of temperature-changes on the model system.

The corresponding temperature-composition phase diagram (see Figure 1 b) can be derived. 
The binodal is composed of the Gibbs free energy minima at different temperatures and the 
spinodal is composed of the inflection points at different temperatures. This means that the 
region between the binodal and the spinodal is the metastable region, while the region below 
the spinodal is characterized by spinodal decomposition [30]. 
The critical solution temperature describes the specific temperature threshold beyond which 
the system undergoes liquid-liquid separation. In the temperature-composition phase diagram 
it is marked as the point where binodal and spinodal touch [30]. An upper critical solution 
temperature (UCST) describes the highest temperature achievable within the two-phase state, 
which means that a temperature decrease below the UCST induces phase separation (as 
illustrated in Figure 1 c). A lower critical solution temperature (LCST) describes the lowest 
temperature achievable within the two-phase state, which means that a temperature increase 
above the LCST induces phase separation. In a system beyond the critical solution 
temperature (below the UCST or above the LCST) and within the in the metastable region, 
droplets enriched in dense phase form by nucleation in the continuous dilute phase. Once the 
droplets reach their equilibrium composition they grow by coarsening to minimize the free 
interfacial energy by reducing the interfacial area [31].

Φ is the volume fraction of solute A (the polymer), (1 – Φ) describes the volume fraction of

The Flory-Huggins model is derived from the regular solution model for a binary solution, where 
the solutes A and B occupy one lattice site, respectively. Polymers, however, can occupy more 
than one site in the lattice. Thus, it is assumed that monomers and solvent molecules occupy 
single lattice sites. The number of lattice sites occupied by the polymer is then given by the 
chain length N. Thus, for a constant volume fraction Φ, Fmix is inversely proportional to the 
chain length [34]. 
The first two terms of the equation together describe the entropy of mixing. The last term 
contains the interaction parameter χ, which describes the enthalpic and entropic contributions 
from chain-solvent, chain-chain, and solvent-solvent interactions [34]. χ > 0 indicates that self-
interactions (ωAA and ωBB) favor demixing of the system, while χ < 0 means mixing of the 
system driven through chain-solvent interactions (ωAB) [31]. 
The Flory-Huggins theory can be used to describe UCST phase behavior, where entropy favors 
mixing, while enthalpy favors demixing/LLPS. The interaction parameter χ is inversely

proportional to the temperature. Thus, at temperatures above the UCST, χ becomes < 0, while 
for temperatures below UCST, χ becomes > 0. The application of Flory-Huggins to describe 
LCST transitions requires adaptations to the model that will not be discussed here [31]. 
The Flory-Huggins theory, which only considers contact-like interactions, was extended by 
Overbeek-Voorn to include longer-range electrostatic interactions to describe complex 
coacervation of polyelectrolytes [34]. Additionally, the random phase approximation for 
polymers by Edwards and de Gennes considers the influence of chain connectivity in polymers 
instead of just looking at monomeric interactions [34]. Thus, it explains different phase 
separation behavior for varying sequences with the same composition [2]. 
2.1.2 Sequence-level driving forces of protein LLPS 
Multivalent, non-covalent molecular interactions on the sequence-level drive LLPS in proteins 
by collectively contributing to a favorable (more positive) free energy of mixing of the system. 
Amino acids can interact through different forces, including electrostatic interactions, 
hydrophobic effects, van-der-Waals interactions and hydrogen bonding. The most important 
ones for promoting protein LLPS will be discussed in this chapter (see Figure 2). 
 
Figure 2: Sequence-level driving forces of protein liquid-liquid phase separation. 
Graphic taken from Dignon et al. 2021 [27]. 
Charge-charge interactions can take place within single sequences containing both cationic 
(Arg, Lys, His) and anionic (Asp, Glu) amino acids, such as in Ddx4, or by complex 
coacervation between two oppositely charged molecules. Both cases lead to clustering of the 
molecules to achieve charge neutralization [35]. Electron-rich pi systems in aromatic side 
chains of Phe, Tyr, Trp can interact by pi-pi stacking with other aromatic side chains or sp2-
hybridized atoms in e.g., Arg [36]. Moreover, cation-pi interactions can take place between 
aromatic and positively charged amino side chains of Lys, Arg or His [37]. The hydrophobic 
effect originates from different interactions and also comprises entropic effects [38], [39]. Polar 
water molecules form structured hydration shells around hydrophobic side chains (e.g., Leu, 
Ile, Val, Phe, Met, Ala) that would otherwise disrupt the hydrogen bonding network [40]. The 
polarity of water molecules causes non-polar surfaces to cluster, which releases water 
molecules into disordered state, thus increasing the entropy of the system. 
==PAGE:17==

It has been demonstrated that the sequence and the composition of amino acids could be 
crucial to protein LLPS [18], [41]. This includes the patterning of charge as well as the 
arrangement of hydrophobic and aromatic amino acids. Repeat motifs, charge blocks and 
overall chain length have been shown to influence Ddx4 phase separation [18], [25]. 
2.1.3 Stimuli for protein LLPS 
System changes can influence the phase separation of proteins. Depending on the amino acid 
sequence, temperature, salt concentration or pH can either promote or inhibit phase separation 
[27]. 
2.1.3.1 Temperature 
The amino acid composition of a sequence determines if phase transitions follow an UCST or 
LCST and thus how the system reacts to temperature changes (see Figure S 1 in the appendix) 
[19]. Sequences containing many polar and aromatic amino acids are generally expected to 
follow a UCST phase transition, where LLPS is favored at temperatures below the UCST [27], 
[42]. UCST phase transition is driven by enthalpy from electrostatic interactions, cation-pi 
interactions, and pi-pi stacking that overcome the entropic costs [43]. The amino acid arginine 
often plays a crucial role in those systems due to its multivalent interactions. Those include 
electrostatic interactions, forming salt-bridges to negatively charged residues (Asp, Glu) and 
cation-pi interactions, forming non-covalent interactions with aromatic residues (Tyr, Phe, Trp). 
These features are typical of resilin-like sequences and RNA-binding low-complexity domains 
(e.g., FUS, Ddx4) [42]. 
In contrast, sequences containing more hydrophobic, often aliphatic amino acids are generally 
expected to follow a LCST phase transition, where LLPS is favored at temperatures above the 
LCST [27], [42]. LCST phase transition is driven by the gain in solvent entropy through release 
of structured water following the hydrophobic collapse of the chain [31]. It has been shown that 
the amino acid proline in P-Xn-G motifs (with X being an amino acid and n = 0-4) plays a central 
role in those systems. The N-substitution means proline lacks a hydrogen bond donor, which 
promotes hydration of the backbone carbonyl. The release of this structured water around the 
backbone leads to an increased entropic gain. Moreover, proline’s cyclisation can increase cis 
content within the chain and chain stiffness, promoting chain collapse and dense phase 
formation. These features are typical of topoelastin-like sequences [42]. 
2.1.3.2 Salt 
The phase separation behavior of proteins has been shown to be influenced by both the salt 
concentration and the chemical identity of the salt ions, depending on the amino acid 
sequence. Addition of salt can induce (salting-out) or prevent (salting-in) phase separation. 
The salt dependency of electrostatic amino acid interactions increases in order from pi-pi 
stacking over dipole-dipole and cation-pi to charge-charge interactions [17]. Ionic screening of 
electrostatic interactions by salt ions can inhibit phase separation. This was shown for Ddx4N1, 
where increasing the salt concentrations lowered the UCST until the critical salt concentration 
was reached [25]. For protein systems where phase separation is suspected no to rely on 
electrostatic interactions, such as FUS, increasing salt concentrations can promote phase 
separation [44]. 
The Hofmeister series characterizes the impact of salts on protein solubility. In general, 
kosmotropic salts from the Hofmeister series can stabilize hydrophobic interactions by 
dehydration of amino acids (salting-out), while chaotropic salts disrupt those interactions 
(salting-in). On the one hand, it was demonstrated that kosmotropic anions increase phase 
==PAGE:18==
8 
separation of FUS, while chaotropic anions decrease phase separation. In contrast, Hofmeister 
cations did not affect FUS phase separation, most likely due to their similar interactions with 
water [45]. On the other hand, it was described that while the phase transition of elastin-like 
polymers generally follows the Hofmeister series, high concentrations of chaotropic anions 
increase phase separation through surface tension effects [46]. Salt concentration is an 
important parameter to control phase separation, as it is more physiological than large 
temperature changes [27]. 
2.1.3.3 pH 
Changes in pH impact the charge state of amino acids like aspartic acid (Asp), glutamic acid 
(Glu), lysine (Lys), arginine (Arg) or histidine (His) and thus influence the charge patterning of 
the protein sequence [27]. Proteins in LLPS are often polyelectrolytes enriched in charged 
amino acids that undergo pH-dependent changes in phase transition behavior (see Figure S 2 
in the appendix). If the pH approaches the pKa of anionic or cationic groups, the neutralization 
of charges could weaken the chain-solvent interactions and drive collapse through chain-chain 
interactions. For polyanions possessing negatively charged acid groups (Asp, Glu) the 
protonation at low pH could lead to phase separation below an upper critical solution pH. For 
polycations, the charge loss of positively charged groups (Lys, Arg) at high pH could induce 
phase separation above a lower critical solution pH. In this context, histidine has a special role 
among the amino acids due to its physiological pKa, which creates a cation at mildly acidic 
conditions [43]. Most proteins in LLPS are polyampholytes and possess positive and negative 
charges. They display different collapse transitions depending on mixing and segregation of 
oppositely charged residues [47]. 
2.2 Methods to study the sequence-space of biomolecular 
interactions 
As described in the previous chapter, the phase separation of proteins is a result of multivalent, 
non-covalent biomolecular interactions. The intrinsic phase separation behavior of a protein is 
encoded in the amino acid sequence, particularly through composition and patterning. Thus, 
the phenotype-genotype relationship can be elucidated by leveraging molecular biology tools 
that were initially developed for directed evolution. Directed evolution is applied to optimize 
proteins for a desired function (e.g., binding affinity, enzyme activity). It is practiced in the 
laboratory by introducing mutations to create new protein variants and selecting for the desired 
function, discovering the optimal sequence through multiple rounds of evolution. While the 
selection acts on the phenotype, the evolutionary information is encoded in the genotype. 
Thus, it is necessary to link genotype and phenotype, i.e. to connect proteins to their encoding 
DNA (or mRNA). To be truly effective, directed evolution furthermore requires screening or 
selection of very large libraries under the desired conditions.  
This chapter discusses the mechanisms, advantages and drawbacks of selected approaches 
that can be used to study the sequence-space of biomolecular interactions (see Figure 3). The 
goal is to validate the choice of mRNA-display to establish the genotype-phenotype linkage in 
CPmD.

2.2.1 Mass spectrometry-based approaches 
Mass spectrometry-based approaches allow the direct identification of the protein variants. 
Various different methods are available to study the protein-protein and map the interactome, 
including affinity purification-MS (AP-MS), cross-linking MS (XL-MS) or proximity labeling-MS 
(PL-MS) [49] and native MS [50]. 
In the selection platform published by Ye et al. 2022 [51], a synthesized peptide library is 
enriched by affinity selection against the target protein. Peptide-target complexes are 
separated from unbound library members, e.g., by high-performance size exclusion 
chromatography. Bound peptide binders were dissociated from the protein and sequenced by 
liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS). This approach is directly 
decoding the phenotype without sequencing the genotype. 
Another approach that leverages MS to study proteins interacting under physiological 
conditions is by combination with co-immunoprecipitation [52]. The target protein (bait) is 
equipped with an affinity tag. Protein complexes (prey) containing the bait protein are 
immunoprecipitated from cell extract using antibodies against the affinity tagged target protein 
[53]. After affinity enrichment, proteins interacting with the tagged bait can be eluted from the 
target protein/antibody complex by e.g., competitive displacement. Discrete members of the 
protein complex are then identified using LC-MS/MS [52]. 
MS-based approaches allow for direct identification of polypeptides and are not limited to static 
protein interactions, as they can capture dynamic protein complexes [48]. However, the 
method lacks sensitivity, making it difficult identify weak interacting partners, particularly when 
the abundance is low [48], and to distinguish them from unspecific binders. MS has bias 
against, e.g., hydrophobic peptides and those that do not ionize well [1] and limitations in 
throughput.

2.2.2 In vitro compartmentalization 
First published in Tawfik and Griffiths, 1998 [54], the in vitro compartmentalization (IVC) 
method uses surfactant-stabilized water-in-oil emulsions to compartmentalize a DNA library 
along with an in vitro transcription and translation system. Emulsions can be formed by stirring, 
vortexing, extrusion, or microfluidics [55]. In the compartments, the library is diluted so that the 
majority of the aqueous microdroplets contain only a single DNA molecule, thus linking the 
expressed protein to its encoding gene. In compartments with proteins that possess the 
desired catalytic activity, a substrate that is linked to the gene is converted to product. The 
emulsion is broken, and the aqueous compartments are combined. Genes linked to the product 
are selectively enriched, PCR amplified and prepared for further rounds of selection [56]. 
IVC allows high-throughput library screening by flow cytometry. In microbead display, the DNA 
and proteins (as well as substrate or product) are attached to microbeads, forming complexes 
large enough to be detected by flow cytometry [57]. In double emulsion, water-in-oil emulsions 
can be re-emulsified into a second aqueous phase. The droplets of this water-in-oil-in-water 
emulsion can be sorted like cells [58]. 
Most biological displays were developed to screen for binding of a displayed protein to an 
immobilized ligand. However, to screen for enzyme variants, selection should be simultaneous 
and direct for multiple enzymatic properties, including substrate recognition, product formation, 
and reaction rate. In this context, microdroplets function as miniaturized reaction environments. 
By compartmentalizing gene, protein, and substrate/product, each droplet creates a genotype-
phenotype-catalytic activity link [48], [57]. 
Water-in-oil emulsions can achieve ~1010 droplets per 50 µL, making it possible to screen 
libraries of 108 to 1011 sequences, even though the number of compartments and thus the 
library size is limited by the capacity of the microfluidics system [59]. IVC methods can produce 
droplets of variable sizes. Large droplets can contain multiple genes, disrupting the one gene-
one compartment rule. To avoid this, the DNA-to-droplet ratio must be lowered, which in turn 
reduces the library size [48]. Establishing the genotype-phenotype link through 
compartmentalization alone makes it vulnerable, especially in the context of high-throughput 
screening. Lastly, water-in-oil emulsion is not suitable for a variety of protein classes, such as 
membrane proteins. Due to their hydrophobic regions, expression in aqueous compartments 
can cause misfolding and aggregation [60]. 
2.2.3 In vivo displays 
2.2.3.1 Phage display 
In phage display, developed by Smith 1984 [61], library proteins are fused to coat proteins 
(typically p3 or p8) of filamentous bacteriophages, such as M13. The phage genome containing 
the fusion protein is transfected into Escherichia coli. The bacterium produces phage particles 
that display the fusion protein on their surface and the corresponding DNA packaged inside, 
thus non-covalently coupling genotype and phenotype. Phage libraries can be screened by 
panning against immobilized targets. Phages are amplified by re-infecting E. coli for the next 
round of selection or sequenced and analyzed [61]. 
Phage display has been improved to increase the number of displayed copies of the fusion 
protein per phage by controlling the expression levels of the fusion protein or by changing the 
fusion partner to other phage surface proteins (e.g., p3, p6, p7, p8, or p9) [62]. Moreover, the 
==PAGE:21==
11 
types of proteins that can be displayed can be expanded by controlling the folding location 
(cytoplasm or periplasm of the bacterial host) and the translocation mechanism [63]. 
On the one hand, the high infectivity and propagation of the phage, phage display allows library 
sizes of up to 1011 variants, limited by transformation efficiency in E. coli [64]. On the other 
hand, the protein expression in bacterial hosts leads to a lack of post-translational 
modifications and limited folding of complex proteins [63]. The most significant drawback is 
that panning of phages does not allow for precise control over the binding affinity and cannot 
easily differentiate between proteins with minor affinity differences due to the high background 
of unspecific binding [48], [62]. 
2.2.3.2 Cell surface displays 
Bacterial and yeast surface display platforms can present library proteins on the surface of 
microbial cells, especially E. coli [65], [66] and Saccharomyces cerevisiae [67] due to the wide 
range of available molecular biology tools and high transformation efficiency. The gene library, 
inserted into a microbial expression vector, is transformed into the host organism. Similar to 
phage display, library proteins (passenger) are expressed as C-terminal, N-terminal, or 
sandwich fusion with an anchoring motif (carrier). These are typically cell-surface proteins that 
act as an anchor to display the library proteins on the surface of bacteria or yeast cells [68]. 
Cell-surface displayed targets can be coupled with fluorescent probes that enable selection by 
fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS). After selection, genes are extracted and 
transformed into new host cells for the further rounds of selection. 
Increased particle size and multivalent presentation of cell surface displays allows monitoring 
of the binding strength via the fluorescence intensity during FACS for better affinity maturation. 
This method has a lower nonspecific background compared with that of biopanning, while the 
sequence space that can be screened is limited due to the much lower throughput of the cell-
sorting. FACS enables high-resolution library screening and discrimination between proteins 
with only minor difference in binding affinity [48], [69]. 
In general, the library size of cell surface displays is limited by transformation efficiency in 
microbial host. Moreover, the proteins to be displayed must be compatible with the transport 
machinery of bacteria or yeast to be displayed on the cell surface correctly [62]. In bacterial 
surface displays, the high transformation efficiency allows libraries between 109-1011 [70]. 
Displaying proteins on the surface of gram-negative bacteria like E. coli is challenging due to 
the outer membrane barrier. Large passenger proteins either display poorly or can cause outer 
membrane fragility, which reduces host viability and limits the library size [62]. This variant of 
surface displays is incapable of expressing complex eukaryotic proteins that require 
posttranslational modifications (glycosylation or disulfide isomerization) [68]. Yeast surface 
displays mitigate the disadvantage of the lacking endoplasmic reticulum-specific post-
translational modifications of the prokaryotic system [70]. However, the library size is limited to 
up to 109 variants and the yeast growth cycle is longer than the bacterial growth cycle, which 
increases the time requirement for selection rounds. Moreover, the higher degree of 
glycosylation can negatively affect folding and function of the displayed proteins [68]. 
2.2.4 In vitro displays 
2.2.4.1 Ribosome display 
The ribosome display method, first developed by Hanes and Pückthun 1997 [71] non-
covalently links a protein to its own mRNA through the ribosomal complex. Ribosome leverage 
in vitro transcription and translation of DNA libraries containing templates without a stop codon. 
==PAGE:22==
12 
During the in vitro translation, the missing stop-codon causes the ribosome to stall at the 3’-
end of the mRNA, leading to formation of a non-covalent complex composed of the mRNA, 
ribosome, and protein. This complex can be stabilized by cooling the reaction to 4 °C and 
addition of Mg2+. Ribosome-displayed libraries can be screened by panning against 
immobilized targets. Bound library members are eluted by addition of EDTA, which chelates 
magnesium ions and thus destabilizes the ribosomal complex. The enriched sequences are 
recovered by reverse transcription and amplified by PCR to be used in further rounds of 
selection [72]. 
Ribosome display overcomes many limitations of in vivo systems [73]. The in vitro translation 
enables production of cytotoxic or proteolytically sensitive proteins and removes cellular 
expression bias. Ribosome display routinely generates libraries of over 1012 independent 
molecules because no transformation step is required [74]. This moreover facilitates recursive 
mutagenesis after each selection cycle by starting directly from the amplification of enriched 
sequences [62]. RNA appears to improve the solubility of the attached protein, enabling 
functional selection of sequences that can aggregate or are only partially soluble when 
expressed by themselves [73]. 
The major limitation of ribosome display is the integrity of the fragile ribosome/mRNA/protein 
complex [74] during selection. Moreover, it is vulnerable to RNase contamination. The intrinsic 
affinity of mRNA and ribosomes toward target molecules might affect selection, while the size 
of ribosomal complex might inhibit presentation and folding of large proteins [75], [76]. 
2.2.4.2 mRNA display 
In contrast to ribosome display, mRNA-display, which was first published by Nemoto et al. 1997 
[29] and Roberts and Szostak 1997 [28] establishes a covalent link between a protein and its 
own mRNA through the small molecule puromycin. The original design used libraries of DNA 
templates that did not contain stop codons. Following the in vitro transcription, a puromycin-
containing DNA linker is attached to the 3’-end of the mRNA transcript by enzymatic ligation 
[28], [77] or photo-crosslinking [78]. During in vitro translation, the ribosome stalls at the 
DNA/RNA junction, which allows the puromycin to enter the A-site of the ribosome, where it is 
incorporated in the nascent peptide, forming a covalent mRNA-puromycin-protein fusion and 
inducing premature termination of the translation reaction. The fusion formation can be 
promoted by addition of Mg2+ or K+ [79]. The mRNA-displayed peptides are released from the 
ribosome by EDTA addition, followed by reverse transcription. Binding selection is performed 
by panning the cDNA/mRNA-protein fusion against immobilized targets. Enriched library 
members are amplified by PCR and exposed to further rounds of selection. 
mRNA-display provides the same advantages over in vivo displays as described for ribosome 
display and can generate libraries of up to 1014 variants [80]. The major difference to the 
ribosome display is the genotype-phenotype coupling through the puromycin linker. The 
covalent nature of the link ensures more control over binding conditions and stringency of 
selection [48]. As the least invasive link among all display variants, it minimizes interference 
with protein-target interactions [79]. mRNA-display also provides a higher stability by reverse 
transcription to cDNA/mRNA-hybrid [81]. Incorporation of non-canonical amino acids by using 
cell-free reconstituted translation systems allows expansion of library diversity [48]. 
The selection efficiency of sequences is strongly dependent on the efficiency of in vitro 
expression and fusion formation. In general, longer peptides are displayed with lower efficiency 
[82]. Despite the increased stability of the cDNA/mRNA-hybrid, mRNA-display is still 
vulnerable to RNase contamination. The high content of negatively charged mRNA in the 
==PAGE:23==
13 
fusion makes selection against positively charged targets challenging promotes unspecific 
binding [76]. 
2.3 mRNA-display – State of the art 
Out of the methods described above, mRNA-display provides the most minimal, covalent link 
of genotype and phenotype, combined with the capability to screen libraries of unmatched size 
and complexity. Since the initial description of the mRNA-display system [28], [29], various 
developments have been made to the protocol. Some selected ones will be described here. 
2.3.1 Principle 
2.3.1.1 Puromycin mechanism 
Puromycin is an aminonucleoside antibiotic produced by Streptomyces alboniger. The 
structure of puromycin resembles the 3’-end of a tyrosyl-tRNA, with a modified adenosine base 
covalently linked to an O-methylated tyrosine amino acid. The key difference to an amino-
acylated tRNA (aa-tRNA) is the missing ester bond between the amino acid and the ribose, 
which is replaced by a more stable peptide bond (see Figure 17). Due to its structural homology 
to aa-tRNA, puromycin can enter the A-site of the ribosome during translation, where it accepts 
the nascent polypeptide chain from the peptidyl-tRNA in the P-site. Facilitated by the peptidyl-
transferase center of the ribosome, this peptide bond is formed by nucleophilic attack of the 
free amino group of puromycin on the carbonyl carbon of the ester bond in the peptidyl-tRNA. 
Puromycin is incorporated into the C-terminus of the growing polypeptide-chain, but the 
peptide bond between its two units cannot be cleaved by incoming aa-tRNA. This prevents 
further extension, resulting in premature termination of translation and release of the 
puromycylated polypeptides (see Figure S 3 in the appendix) [83]. 
2.3.1.2 mRNA-protein fusion 
In the context of mRNA-display, where the puromycin is covalently linked to the 3’-end of 
mRNA, the C-terminal incorporation leads to fusion of the polypeptide to its own mRNA [28], 
[29]. Despite its structural resemblance to tyrosyl-tRNA, the incorporation of puromycin is not 
amino acid specific [84]. At low concentrations used in the mRNA-display, puromycin cannot 
effectively compete with aa-tRNA and only becomes incorporated when the ribosome stalls, 
e.g., by reaching the RNA/DNA junction or by encountering a stop-codon (the latter requires 
the absence of decoding release factors, i.e. RF1 and RF2). Fusion takes place in cis between 
the nascent peptide and the bound mRNA on the same ribosome. Trans products, a peptide 
fused to another than its own mRNA, would prevent directed evolution, and it has been shown 
that they are not formed. According to the threading model, the 3’-end of the ligated mRNA 
threads through the ribosome rather than wrapping around it, where the puromycin enters the 
A-site and fuses to the nascent peptide [79]. 
2.3.2 Design considerations 
2.3.2.1 Construction of the DNA library 
The double-stranded DNA template should have a 5’ T7 promoter that allows large-scale 
synthesis of mRNA using T7 RNA polymerase. The transcript should begin with three guanine 
nucleotides to aid transcription initiation [79]. The 5’-untranslated region (UTR) containing the 
ribosome binding site (RBS) must be designed based on the used in vitro translation system. 
E. coli-based systems require a Shine-Dalgarno sequence with appropriate spacing to the 
start-Codon, while eukaryotic systems depend on the Kozak sequence. For the commonly 
utilized rabbit reticulocyte lysate, a deletion mutant of the tobacco mosaic virus 5'-UTR (∆TMV) 
==PAGE:24==
14 
was incorporated [79]. Traditionally, the open reading frame (OFR) encoding the peptide or 
protein library and the following 3’ constant region must not contain any stop-codons, as they 
lead to termination of translation before the puromycin attack [79]. However, this design 
constraint was overcome by using defined in vitro translation systems that are deficient for 
release factors (e.g., the PURE system), instead of crude cell lysates. The amino-acid 
sequence in the 3’-end can be used to encode an epitope tag (e.g., His tag, FLAG tag, 
hemagglutinin (HA) tag) for a suitable purification strategy. His tag purification, for example, is 
not possible with rabbit reticulocyte lysate due to the presence of metal-binding proteins. 
Moreover, the 3’-end of the resulting mRNA transcript should not have secondary structures 
that interfere with the cross-linking [79]. 
2.3.2.2 Puromycin-linker design 
Efficiency of the mRNA-protein fusion formation in the translation reaction is dependent on the 
length, flexibility and sequence of the puromycin-linker. As specified by the threading model, 
the linker must directly span the distance of ~75 Å between the decoding site and peptidyl-
transferase site of the ribosome [79]. The original linker developed by Roberts and Szostak, 
1997 [28] was a poly-deoxyadenosine spacer (5’-dA27dCdC[puromycin]-3’). The original in vitro 
DNA virus (IVV) design by Nemoto et al. 1997 [29] relied on a significantly longer DNA/RNA

2.3.2.3 Cross-linking approaches 
Cross-linking the puromycin-linker and the mRNA is a crucial step in all mRNA-display variants, 
as it establishes the link between phenotype and genotype. Different approaches have been 
developed. 
Splint ligation. The original mRNA-display by Roberts and Szostak 1997 [28] was constructed 
using splint ligation. The 3’-puromycin DNA linker was phosphorylated at the 5’-terminus. In 
the presence of a DNA splint with complementary sequence to both the mRNAs 3’-hydroxyl 
and the DNA-linkers 5’-phosphoryl termini the ligation was performed using T4 DNA ligase. Liu 
et al. 2000 [79] increased the efficiency of the splint ligation by using a PEG-containing 
puromycin-linker (as described in Chapter 2.3.2.2) and optimizing the DNA splint. In addition 
to requiring complete removal of the DNA splint which otherwise would inhibit fusion formation 
[78], 3’-terminal heterogeneity caused by T7 run-off transcription could be problematic for splint 
ligation of the puromycin-linker to the 3’-terminus [79]. 
Psoralen-induced photo-crosslinking. Kurz et al. 2000 [78] developed an alternative 
method for light-induced crosslinking. The approach relies on a 3’-puromycin linker carrying a 
==PAGE:25==
15 
psoralen at the 5’-terminus. Its 5’-complementary sequence to the 3’-sequence of the mRNA 
was synthesized from 2’-O-methyl RNA phosphoramidites for enhanced pairing stability. After 
hybridization of the two strands, irradiation with UV light (> 300 nm) was used to form a 
covalent cross-link between the linker and the mRNA’s 3’-terminus [78]. The method was 
improved by equipping the puromycin linker with a photo-cleavable biotin group that enables 
integration of transcription and cross-linking step [85]. This method does not require purification 
of a DNA splint and is less salt-sensitive than ligase-dependent reactions (only the cross-
linking, not the fusion reaction) [79]. 
Y ligation. Tabuchi et al, 2002 [77], [86] published a novel method to attach the puromycin-
linker using Y-ligation with T4 RNA ligase. They designed a 3’-puromycin DNA linker with 5’-
complementary sequence to the 3’-sequence of the mRNA. Upon hybridization of the two 
strands, a mismatch between the 3’-hydroxyl terminus of the mRNA and the 5’-phosphoryl 
terminus of the DNA linker led to formation of two branches after the stem, resulting in the 
characteristic Y-shape. The ligation by T4 RNA ligase resulted in a stem-loop. In addition to 
providing a yield exceeding that of splint ligation and photo-crosslinking, this approach had the 
advantage of not requiring any after treatments, like removal of the splint DNA. 
Non-covalent fusion. In their initial IVV design, Nemoto et al. 1997 [29] constructed a 5’-
phosphorylated DNA/RNA chimera with five ribonucleotides before the 3-terminal puromycin 
that was ligated to mRNA by T4 RNA ligase. Tabuchi et al. 2001 [87] published a second 
generation of the IVV, where a DNA primer with a 3’-puromycin linker was non-covalently 
bound to the mRNA by hybridization. The linker contained 2’-O-methyl RNA oligonucleotides 
that improved the thermostability of RNA binding and reduced mRNA degradation by RNase. 
Virion formation was completed by reverse transcription, resulting in the first cDNA/mRNA 
display [87]. 
2.3.2.4 Further display variants 
The basic cross-linking techniques described above have been used to develop several 
modified variants of mRNA-display. 
cDNA display. Kurz et al. 2001 [81] described a primer-branched 3’-puromycin linker with a 
5’-psoralen photo-crosslink site. The branched 3’-hybridization site functions as reverse 
transcription primer. After translation, reverse transcription of the mRNA is used to synthesize 
an mRNA/DNA hybrid. RNase digestion followed by another round of reverse transcription 
results in double-stranded cDNA fusion. Naimuddin et al. 2016 [88] published a similar design 
that included a 5’-terminal Y ligation site. The main advantage of cDNA or cDNA/mRNA display 
is the increased stability of the fusion compared to mRNA-display. 
Solid-phase translation. Biyani et al. 2006 [89] and Yamaguchi et al. 2009 [90] described a 
design for solid-phase translation of mRNA-display. They used a primer-branched 3’-
puromycin linker with a hexaethylene glycol linker (Spacer 18). The 5’-sequence of the DNA 
was folded into a hairpin structure with 5’-terminal Y-ligation site. The DNA loop accommodated 
a biotinylated oligo for immobilization, while the formed DNA duplex provided an endonuclease 
restriction site for release of mRNA/DNA fusion. The puromycin-ligated mRNA was 
immobilized to the surface of streptavidin-coated beads. On-bead translation and reverse 
transcription followed by RNase digestion of the mRNA resulted in cDNA display. This design 
was refined by Mochizuki et al, 2011. The key improvement to the linker was the utilization of 
a different restriction site (RNase T1 instead of PvuII), which did not require dsDNA for 
recognition. This allowed the design of a shorter linker, which increased ligation efficiency from 
90 % to 95 %. Based on the assumption that this improvement made purification of the ligated 
==PAGE:26==
16 
mRNA dispensable, they proposed a one-pot reaction coupling ligation and translation, 
reducing time from 120 to 30 min [91]. This approach provides several advantages over liquid-
phase translation (in addition to stability of cDNA-display). It is moreover an important step 
towards high-throughput screening and automation strategies. The biotin-streptavidin 
immobilization offers a novel, direct and efficient purification strategy (other than oligo-dT 
cellulose and His tag). The immobilization of mRNA protects the 3’-end against contaminating 
nucleases, increasing the half-life. Lastly, the stationary mode of solid phase translation could 
help control cell-free protein synthesis by avoiding collision of growing polypeptide chains, 
which would lead to misfolding and aggregation and directing folding of specific proteins (like 
a ribosome bound to endoplasmic reticulum) [89]. This also allowed for the introduction of 
complex post-translation modification, such as disulfide bonds [90]. 
Coupling of transcription and translation. Ishizawa et al. 2013 [92] developed a RF1 
deficient, cell-free transcription and translation system, called TRAP display (transcription-
translation coupled with association of puromycin linker). After transcription, the mRNA forms 
a stable, non-covalent hybrid with 3’-puromycin linker through a GC-rich complementary 
sequence in the 3’-UTR of the mRNA. During translation, the ribosome stalls at a UAG stop-
codon placed immediately before the mRNA/DNA duplex. This enhances the puromycin attack 
and prevents dissociation of the heteroduplex by helicase activity of the translating ribosome. 
This Integration of transcription and translation in a ligation-free approach, without the need 
for purification in between the steps significantly reduces the time for polypeptide selection to 
3 h per round (compared to otherwise 8 hours with other methods). 
2.3.2.5 In vitro translation systems 
In general, two different types of cell-free translation systems exist: those derived from cell 
lysates or those composed of purified recombinant proteins. Historically, mRNA-display has 
relied on in vitro translation systems based on different crude cell extracts derived from E. coli, 
rabbit reticulocytes, or wheat germ [79]. Prepared as S30 fraction (supernatant from 30,000 g 
centrifugation), those systems have a largely undefined composition, which significantly 
reduces the controllability. Most of the components do not contribute to the desired translation 
reaction. This causes rapid energy depletion independent of peptide bond formation and can 
even lead to degradation of DNA, mRNA and proteins products by nucleases and proteases, 
which would inhibit the production of mRNA-display. Moreover, those systems are limited to 
proteins and peptides comprising the 20 proteinogenic amino acids and cannot be easily 
reconfigured due to their complexity [26]. 
To overcome these challenges, Shimizu et al. 2001 [93] developed the PURE (protein 
synthesis using recombinant elements) system, which has since been integrated into various 
mRNA-displays. The reconstituted E. coli translation machinery is constructed from individually 
purified components, including ribosomes, initiation factors (IF1, IF2 and IF3), elongation 
factors (EF-G, EF-Tu, EF-T), release factors (RF1 and RF3), and ribosomal recycling factor 
(RRF). Additionally, it includes the machinery for transcription (T7 RNA polymerase, NTPs), 
amino-acylation (amino acids, tRNA, aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases, methionyl-tRNA 
transformylase, 10-formyl-5,6,7,8-tetrahydrofolic acid) and regeneration of energy sources 
(creatine phosphate, creatine kinase, myokinase, nucleoside-diphosphate kinase and 
pyrophosphatase), as well as defined buffer system [93], [94]. In addition to using an efficient 
in vitro translation system, the yield of mRNA-protein fusion can be improved by post-
translational addition of Mg2+ and K+ and low-temperature incubation [79]. 
==PAGE:27==
17 
Even though the PURE system achieves coupling of transcription and translation, it is only 
used for in vitro translation in the context of mRNA-display. This is because the mRNA-protein 
fusion requires a covalent linkage of mRNA transcript and puromycin-linker, that can only be 
achieved in a distinct reaction. However, there are few exceptions that rely on non-covalent 
hybridization, as described above [92]. In recent years, efforts have been made towards low-
cost production [95] of the PURE system and switching the energy source [96]. 
The advantage of the PURE system is that it uses His-tagged translation factors that allow 
reverse purification of native protein products. Moreover, as an entirely defined system, it is 
not only contamination-free but also easily reprogrammable. The latter allows for the design of 
PURE systems that are deficient for release factors. The class I release factors RF1 and RF2 
decode the stop codons UAG, UAA, UGA and catalyze peptide release from the ribosome. 
The class II release factor RF3 is a GTPase that promotes the release of class I RF from the 
post-termination ribosome complex [97]. Release factor-deficient PURE systems make it 
possible to incorporate the corresponding stop-codons at the 3’-terminus, that, without the 
presence of decoding release factors, stall the ribosome in place. As the puromycin attack has 
been shown to depend on the pausing of the ribosome, this increases the fusion efficiency. 
Moreover, reprogramming the PURE system allows for the incorporation of non-canonical 
amino acids. This was first demonstrated by suppression of the amber stop codon (UAG) in 
an RF1-deficient PURE system supplied with a chemically synthesized, orthogonal UAG-
specific suppressor tRNA, combined with termination reprograming to RF2 (UGA) [26]. 
In addition to utilization of nonsense codons, purified in vitro translation systems provide the 
possibility for unambiguous reassignment of sense codons to non-canonical amino acids [98]. 
Genetic code reprogramming through codon-reassignment was demonstrated using the FIT 
(flexible in vitro translation) system, a custom, E. coli reconstituted cell-free translation system 
that is deficient for arbitrarily chosen amino-acids and the respective aminoacyl-tRNA 
synthetases. The vacant codons were reassigned by supplying mischarged acyl-tRNAs, 
loaded with non-canonical amino acids, that possess the corresponding anticodon [99]. 
Instead of chemical acylation or engineering aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases, the mischarged 
acyl-tRNAs were prepared using flexible tRNA-aminoacylating ribozymes (Flexizymes) that 
catalyze the loading of virtually any natural or unnatural amino acid onto tRNA. Substrate 
recognition only depends on the activating ester group [100]. Libraries of proteins containing 
non-canonical can be fused to their encoding mRNA, as described by the RaPID (random 
nonstandard peptides integrated discovery) system, that allows the selection of non-
proteinogenic and even macrocyclic peptides from mRNA-displayed libraries [101]. 
2.3.3 Applications 
The chemical diversity of mRNA-display was initially limited to the 20 proteinogenic L-amino 
acids. Thanks to the combination of reconstituted in vitro translation systems and genetic code 
reprogramming, mRNA-display has been expanded to use of D-amino acids, β- and γ-amino 
acids, and N-methylamino [102]. mRNA-display has been used to design binders to specific 
ligands from libraries of proteins [73], [103], peptides [104], [105], circularized peptides [106], 
[107], and peptides built from unnatural amino acids [108], [109]. Moreover, it has been used 
from selection of catalysts [110] from protein libraries. Recent efforts combined mRNA display 
with Deep Mutational Scanning [111], powered by high-throughput next generation 
sequencing.

3 Material and Methods 
The protocol to produce mRNA-displayed peptides as well as the partitioning experiments are 
based on the approach described in Norrild et al. 2025 [1]. 
3.1 mRNA-display 
3.1.1 Amplification of the DNA template 
DisProt library template. The used DisProt library DNA template contains aT7 RNA 
polymerase (RNAP) promotor and a ribosome binding site (RBS) at the 5’-end and two GS-
linkers, followed by the HA-tag to the 3’-end (see appendix). 
PCR. The 5’-end of the DNA template is extended by three GS-linkers, followed by a Stop-
Codon and the puromycin-linker sequence in a PCR reaction. For 100 µL reaction, DNA 
template from the first PCR was diluted 1000-fold in 1X Phusion HF buffer (NEB), 0.2 mM 
dNTPs, 1 µM Flank_fw, 1 µM Threep_flank_HA and 0.02 U/µL Phusion HF DNA polymerase 
(NEB). The reaction was split into 2x50 µL. The PCR program included an initial denaturation 
at 98 °C for 30 sec, 12 cycles of: denaturation at 98 °C for 10 sec, annealing at 56 °C for 30 
sec, extension at 72 °C for 40 sec, followed by a final extension at 72 °C for 60 sec. To check 
the length of the obtained second PCR products, samples were run on a 2 % agarose gel with 
1X GelGreen DNA dye at 100 V for 70 min.  
Phenol-chloroform extraction. The second PCR products were purified with phenol-
chloroform extraction. To enable the later EtOH precipitation, 0.1x sample volume of 3 M NaCl 
were added to the sample. For 100 µL second PCR reaction, 100 µL PCI (phenol-chloroform-
isoamyl alcohol) at 25:24:1 ratio, pH 8.0 were added. After vortexing, the samples were 
centrifuged at 13,000 rpm and 25 °C for 5 min. The top, aqueous phase was transferred to a 
new tube. 100 µL CI (chloroform-isoamyl alcohol) at 24:1 ratio were added, vortexed and 
centrifuged at 13,000 rpm and 25 °C for 5 min. The top, aqueous phase was transferred to a 
new tube and the volume was estimated. 
EtOH precipitation. The aqueous phase was mixed with 2x volume of ice-cold, 200-proof 
EtOH, vortexed and centrifuged at 13,000 rpm and 25 °C for 15 min. The supernatant was 
removed and 25 µL ice-cold 70 % EtOH were added. The sample was centrifuged at 13,000 
rpm and 25 °C for 5 min. The supernatant was removed, and the pellet was dried in the open 
tube for 5 min. The pellet was resuspended in 10 µL RNase-free ddH2O for 20-fold 
concentration up to a theoretical maximum 5 µM (assuming no losses in extraction). The 
concentration of the purified DNA was determined by measuring absorbance at 260 nm using 
NanoDrop 
3.1.2 Transcription 
In vitro transcription. The in vitro transcription reaction was assembled at room temperature 
to prevent precipitation of buffer components. For 100 µL reaction, 2.5 µL dsDNA template 
were mixed with a custom transcription buffer (0.4 M HEPES, pH 7.6, 20 µM Spermidine, 0.1 
M NaCl, 0.1 % (v/v) Triton X-100), 15 mM rNTPs, 20 mM DTT (Diethiothreitol), 22.5 mM MgCl2, 
0.1 U/µL TIPP (NEB), 1 U/µL T7 RNA-polymerase (Thermo Scientific) and 1 % (v/v) RNasin. 
2x50 µL transcription reaction were incubated at 37 °C for 3 h or overnight. The template 
dsDNA was degraded by adding 5.7 µL 10X DNase buffer (0.4 M Tris, pH 8.0, 0.1 M MgSO4, 
0.01 M CaCl2) and DNase 1 (Thermo Scientific) to each 50 µL reaction and incubating at 37 
==PAGE:29==
19 
°C for 30 min. The transcription reaction was stopped by 5.6 µL 0.5 M EDTA, pH 8.0 to each 
50 µL reaction. 
Low pH phenol-chloroform extraction. To enable later EtOH precipitation, 7 µL 3 M NaAc, 
pH 5.2 were added to each 50 µL reaction. The RNA was purified using low-pH PCI/CI 
extraction as described above, using PCI at pH 5.2. After treatment with PCI and CI, the 
aqueous phase was transferred to a NAP-5 column (Cytiva) that was equilibrated with 10 mL 
RNase-free ddH2O. A total of 500 µL RNase-free ddH2O minus the loaded sample volume 
was added to the column. The RNA was eluted in 500 µL RNase-free ddH2O. The 
concentration of the purified RNA was determined by measuring absorbance at 260 nm using 
NanoDrop. 
EtOH precipitation. For concentrations < 5 µM, the RNA was concentrated by EtOH 
precipitation as described above. For 500 µL eluate, 50 µL NaAc, pH 5.2 were added. The 
pellet was washed 2x with 70 % EtOH, to avoid transfer of salts which might inhibit the 
subsequent ligation reaction and resuspended in 125 µL RNase-free ddH2O for 2-fold 
concentration. For each purified sample, 0.3 µL RNasin was added. The RNA was diluted to a 
working concentration of 5 µM and samples were stored at -80 °C. 
3.1.3 Ligation 
T4 ssRNA ligase reaction. For 50 µL ssRNA ligase reaction, 1 µM mRNA stock was mixed 
with 1.5 µM puromycin-linker and annealed at 95 °C for 3 min. The mix was cooled to room 
temperature and mixed with 1 mM ATP, 1X T4 RNA ligase reaction buffer, 1 U/µL T4 ssRNA 
ligase (NEB) and 0.3 µL RNasin. The reaction was incubated at 37 °C for 3 h. To stop the 
reaction, 0.5 µL 0.5 M EDTA, pH 8.0 was added to 50 µL reaction. 
T4 DNA ligase and SplintR ligase reaction. For comparison, T4 DNA ligase and SplintR 
ligase reactions were set-up as described above. For 50 µL reaction, the annealing reaction, 
supplemented with 1.5 µM biotin-oligo, was mixed with 1X T4 DNA ligase or SplintR ligase 
buffer (both already containing 1 mM ATP) and 1 U/µL T4 DNA ligase (NEB) or SplintR ligase 
(NEB), respectively. 
Phenol-chloroform extraction. The puromycin-ligated mRNA from T4 ssRNA and T4 DNA 
ligation was purified using PCI/CI extraction and concentrated up to a maximum of 5 µM using 
EtOH precipitation as described above.  
SplintR ligase reaction. For 50 µL SplintR ligation reaction, 1.5 µM mRNA stock were mixed 
with 1.5 µM puromycin-linker and 1.5 µM biotin-oligo and annealed at 95 °C for 3 min. The mix 
was cooled to 20 °C and mixed with 1X SplintR ligase buffer (NEB), 1.25 U/µL SplintR ligase 
(NEB) and 0.3 µL RNasin. The reaction was incubated at 20 °C for 80 min. To stop the reaction, 
0.5 µL 0.5 M EDTA, pH 8.0 was added to 50 µL reaction. 
Biotin-streptavidin purification. At room temperature, 48 µL SplintR ligase reaction were 
mixed with 48 µL 2X blocking buffer (20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 2 M NaCl, 0.1 % (v/v) Tween20,

(Thermo Scientific) were washed 3 times with 1X washing buffer (10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 1

were pelleted using a magnet and the clear supernatant was removed. The beads were 
resuspended in fresh washing buffer and transferred to a new tube. For binding of the biotin to 
the streptavidin, the pelleted beads were resuspended in 96 µL blocked sample at 35 °C. The 
suspension was incubated at 35 °C for 15 min and resuspended multiple times to avoid

4.1 Part I: Production of mRNA-displayed peptides 
The first research objective was to establish a molecular biology pipeline to produce mRNA-
displayed peptides from the DisProt library. Both mRNA-displayed peptides and puromycin-
ligated mRNA are constructs required for CPmD experiments and further biophysical 
screenings. 
4.1.1 Identifying bottlenecks in the mRNA-display protocol 
The first step was to establish the mRNA-display protocol as described in Norrild et al. 2025 
[1] (see Figure 4) and identify bottlenecks that limit the yield and purity of the product. While 
the protocol was initially designed to produce mRNA-displayed peptides from the DisProt 
library, the required DNA template is constructed by introducing up- and downstream 
sequences through PCR amplification, including T7 RNAP promotor, RBS, HA-tag, and 
puromycin-linker sequence. This makes it possible to easily pivot to other inserts, such as 
peptide libraries or full-length proteins, e.g., alpha-synuclein (as demonstrated in Figure S 4 in 
the appendix) or TDP-16. 
 
Figure 4: Protocol for production of mRNA-display peptides. 
The protocol is based on Norrild et al. 2025 [1]. The DisProt library DNA template is PCR amplified and purified by

template is transcribed by T7 RNA polymerase. The mRNA is purified by low pH PCI/CI extraction followed by EtOH 
precipitation and quantified by measuring absorbance at 260 nM using NanoDrop. 1 µM of the DisProt library mRNA 
is annealed to 1.5 µM puromycin-linker and ligated using T4 ssRNA ligase. The ligated mRNA is purified by PCI/CI

based on fluorescein fluorescence on a super-denaturing PAGE gel. The ligated mRNA is translated using PURE 
system and purified via immunoprecipitation using Pierce Anti-HA beads. The purified mRNA-display is quantified 
by or qPCR or fluorescence assay using VantaStar. 
While mRNA-displayed peptides form the DisProt library were successfully produced following 
the protocol illustrated in Figure 4, the yield of the mRNA-display was significantly lower than 
expected. In order to identify bottlenecks in the molecular biology pipeline, the obtained yields 
from crucial steps in the protocol (transcription, ligation and translation) were analyzed and 
compared to reference values from Norrild et al. 2025 [1] (see Figure 5).

The low concentration of purified mRNA-display of 1 nM indicated a problem with the 
translation reaction. The PURExpress Δ RF123 Kit used for in vitro translation is derived from 
the original PURE system by Shimizu et al, 2001 [93] and does not contain the release factors 
RF1, RF2 and RF3. Release factors are excluded to stall the ribosome when it reaches a stop-
codon and allow puromycin-attack. Even though no clear consensus on the fate of the 
ribosome could be found in literature, the premature translation termination without release 
factors most likely interferes with the mechanism of ribosome release and recycling. Thus, the 
ribosome might release the mRNA-displayed peptide only after addition of EDTA. Following 
this argumentation, every ribosome could only be used once. The translation would thus be 
limited by the concentration of ligated mRNA template, which is supplied to the reaction, and 
the ribosome concentration in the reaction. As the PURE system has a ribosome concentration 
of 1.2 µM [94], this could be excluded as limiting factor for a target yield of 10 nM. 
The ligation only yielded 20 nM of ligated mRNA, which means a maximum final concentration 
of 6 nM in the translation reaction, which could limit the yield of the mRNA-display. Since the 
concentration of mRNA after transcription (8 nM) exceeds the target value (5 nM), the crucial 
bottleneck seemed to be the ligation reaction. The low yield of ligated mRNA could be caused 
by different factors, including low efficiency of the T4 ssRNA ligase reaction or poor recovery 
by PCI/CI extraction. This highlighted the necessity to validate those procedures and 
potentially look for alternative approaches. Furthermore, even though the concentration of 
mRNA seemed to be sufficient, the integrity of the mRNA transcript could impact the 
puromycin-ligation. Thus, the quality of the mRNA transcript had to be assured. 
In order to achieve sufficiently high mRNA-display concentrations for the CPmD experiments, 
the goal was to remove this bottleneck in the protocol. 
4.1.2 Increasing the yield of the mRNA-display 
The previous analysis showed that the main bottleneck limiting the yield of mRNA-display was 
the low concentration of ligated mRNA after the ligation reaction. The goal was to increase the 
ligated mRNA concentration to >1 µM, while minimizing the amount of remaining unligated 
mRNA.

4.1.2.1 Improving mRNA quality 
To assure the quality of the mRNA transcript, the transcription was repeated in a 3 h and an 
overnight reaction. The mRNA was purified by low pH PCI/CI extraction followed by EtOH 
precipitation and quantified by measuring absorbance at 260 nM using NanoDrop (Figure 6). 
 
Figure 6: Yield of mRNA after 3 h and overnight transcription 
Transcription at 37 °C using T7 RNA polymerase. Only the 3 h mRNA transcript was concentrated by EtOH 
precipitation. 
Running the transcription for 3 h and overnight could affect the quality and yield of the mRNA. 
On the one hand, overnight transcription could increase the RNA yield, since longer incubation 
allows for more complete use of NTPs. Thus, overnight transcription is particularly useful to 
synthesize large amounts of RNA for mRNA-display libraries and to transcribe long full-length 
templates. Lastly, difficult templates with secondary structures or low GC content may also 
benefit from extended transcription time. The latter is however not the case for the DisProt 
library mRNA (GC = 50.98 %). On the other hand, extended transcription could lead to enzyme 
degradation and NTP depletion. The T7 RNA polymerase can produce side-products, including 
truncated transcripts [113] or 3’-end additions of random nucleotides through run-off 
transcription [79], which reduces mRNA quality.  
The overnight reaction did not yield a significantly higher mRNA concentration. The integrity of 
the mRNA is crucial, not only for puromycin-ligation, but also for the quality of the displayed 
peptides and the purification via the HA-epitope. Thus, the 3 h transcribed mRNA was further 
concentrated to ~10 µM and used for the following ligation reactions. 
4.1.2.2 Testing different ligases 
After improving the quantity and quality of the mRNA, the remaining causes of the bottleneck 
in the ligation were investigated. Therefore, the performance of the T4 ssRNA ligase was 
validated by comparison to T4 DNA ligase, and SplintR ligase regarding the yield of ligated 
mRNA. 
The T4 RNA ligase reaction was set-up by annealing 1.5 µM puromycin-linker and 1 µM mRNA. 
T4 DNA ligase and SplintR ligase reaction were modified by adding 1.5 µM biotin oligo to the 
annealing reaction, serving as DNA splint. In addition to the DisProt library mRNA template, 
the ligations were also performed with full-length TDP-16 mRNA. All six ligations were 
incubated at the same conditions (37 °C for 3 h). The unpurified ligation reactions were 
compared by super-denaturing PAGE analysis (see Figure 7).

All three types of ligations were successful also for full-length mRNA of TDP16. The fluorescein 
detection reveals the expected band shift for the ligated mRNA. For the T4 DNA ligase and 
SplintR ligase reactions of the DisProt library mRNA, the PAGE analysis reveals a slight upshift

by the supplementation of the biotin oligo, which anneals to the ligated mRNA. 
Even though all three reactions were set up with the same template concentration of DisProt 
library mRNA, the PAGE quantification reveals varying total mRNA concentrations significantly 
below the expected 1 µM. The ligations were therefore compared regarding ligation efficiency 
(see Figure 7), which describes the proportion of the initial mRNA template that was ligated to 
puromycin-linker. The ligation efficiency for T4 ssRNA ligation is approx. 2-fold higher than for 
the other two reactions, resulting in the highest yield of ligated mRNA. Moreover, the fact that 
there was almost no unligated mRNA left means that ligation might be limited by the 
concentration of the mRNA template. However, it must be considered that the reaction 
conditions were not optimized for the T4 DNA ligase or SplintR ligase. 
In the PAGE analysis, ligated mRNA was quantified by SYBR Green staining and fluorescein 
fluorescence. The quantification by fluorescein fluorescence yielded a significantly lower 
concentration of ligated mRNA than the SYBR Green staining (see Figure 7, bottom right). A 
comparison with the qPCR quantification indicated that the SYBR Green staining provides the 
more accurate estimate of the ligated mRNA concentration. Lietard et al. 2022 [114] show that 
fluorescein fluorescence is influenced by the presence of nucleic acids in the environment. The 
authors demonstrate that the quenching of fluorescein fluorescence is strongly sequence-
dependent and can lead to an intensity drop between ~30-60% (depending on 5’- or 3’-labeling 
and single- or double-stranded systems). The quenching is mostly driven by photoinduced 
electron transfer (PET) between fluorescein and guanosine as the most oxidizable nucleotide. 
Even though it was shown that only the identity of the nucleobases in direct proximity of the 
==PAGE:38==
28 
fluorescein significantly impacts the fluorescence intensity, it was observed that guanosine-
rich sequences exhibit increased fluorescence quenching [114]. The ligated mRNA has a total 
length of 169 bp, with a 48 bp insert that varies for the different members of the DisProt library. 
The peptide encoding sequence is located 59 bp upstream of the puromycin-linker and should 
therefore not substantially influence fluorescence quenching. In addition to sequence variation, 
Lietard et al. 2022 observed that a change from single-stranded to double-stranded DNA can 
reduce the fluorescence intensity by ~30 %. This is because the PET is facilitated in base-
paired systems, since the oxidation potential of a guanosine-cytosine base pair is lower than 
that of unpaired guanosine. The same accounts for the oxidation potential of an adenosine-
thymine base pairing [114]. The puromycin-linker comprises a 16 bp ssDNA sequence (see 
Figure S 5 in the appendix). The fluorescein is attached to the thymine close to the 3’-end of 
this DNA oligo (5’-…CGTCC-3’), flanked by a guanosine. After ligation of the 5’-end of the 
puromycin-linker to the 3’-end of the DisProt library mRNA, the 3’-end of the ligated mRNA 
folds into a hairpin, forming a 13 bp DNA-RNA heteroduplex. For labelling of terminal 
nucleotides, fluorescein mostly interacts with the closest covalently bound nucleotide and does 
not reach over to the complementary base or intercalate between base pairs. The labelled 
nucleotide in this case is a thymine, which has a significantly lower oxidation potential 
compared to guanosine, cytosine or adenine [114]. It is, however, not located at the 3’-
terminus, which might affect the PET to neighboring nucleotides. Thus, the flanking guanosine 
and the base-pairing in of the secondary structure could increase the quenching of the 
fluorescein fluorescence in the ligated mRNA compared to the free puromycin-linker. As a 
result, the concentration of the ligated mRNA would be underestimated. Therefore, the 
quantification of ligated mRNA in the following experiments was done by SYBR Green staining 
only. The fluorescein fluorescence was used to identify puromycin-containing in the PAGE 
analysis. 
The T4 ssRNA ligase was confirmed to be the most efficient among the three tested ligases, 
reaching 0.3 µM ligated mRNA in the unpurified reaction. For further optimization, the 
concentration of the mRNA template should be increased to rule out a limitation.

The previous experiments indicated that the T4 ssRNA ligase reaction for the DisProt library 
mRNA could be limited by the mRNA template concentration. Thus, different mRNA 
concentrations of 1 µM and 2 µM in the annealing reaction with 1 µM puromycin-linker were 
tested. A reaction without T4 ssRNA ligase was used as a negative control. The unpurified 
ligation reaction and the purified, ligated mRNA were compared by super-denaturing PAGE 
analysis (see Figure 8).

the quantification of the purified mRNA by NanoDrop and the quantification of the band 
intensity following SYBR Green staining. It could also indicate degradation of mRNA by RNase 
contamination. However, as the 2 µM ligation reactions contained >2-fold higher concentration 
of mRNA template, the data could still provide information on the impact of higher template 
concentration. 
Doubling the amount of initial mRNA-template in the 2 µM ligation led to a 2-fold increase in 
ligated mRNA in the unpurified reaction as well as the purified sample, reaching 1.3 µM ligated 
mRNA after 5-fold concentration. Unfortunately, this high yield of ligated mRNA was 
accompanied by a significant contamination of unligated mRNA in the purified sample (> 65%), 
that was even higher than in the 1 µM template ligation (45%). In the traditional PCI/CI 
extraction, the highly polar DNA and RNA molecules remain in the aqueous phase, while 
proteins and other impurities (e.g., lipids) are removed in the interphase or organic phase. 
Thus, the PCI/CI extraction effectively purifies nucleic acids from proteins and other impurities 
but is not suitable to distinguish between unligated and ligated mRNA and puromycin-linker. 
The remaining mRNA in the purified sample is therefore a direct result of the increased 
template concentration. The amount of mRNA after purification can only be reduced by 
changing the purification strategy or selecting a different approach for the ligation, which 
utilizes the mRNA template more efficiently. 
The bottleneck in the ligation reaction was removed, exceeding the target concentration of 
1 µM after 5-fold concentration by EtOH precipitation. This should allow for production of 
mRNA-display at >10 nM concentration in the translation.

The concentration of the purified mRNA-display was quantified by qPCR and fluorescence 
assay using Vantastar. The quantification by qPCR gave a slightly higher signal than the 
fluorescence assay (see Figure 9). The fact that these two very distinct methods delivered 
closely matching results supports the reliability reproducibility of the measurements. The 
deviation could be explained by the fact that both quantifications relied on a standard curve. 
The qPCR used a standard curve of reverse transcribed mRNA, whose concentration was 
determined by NanoDrop measurement. Thus, the accuracy of the standard curve is 
dependent on the accuracy of the NanoDrop measurement. The fluorescence assay used a 
standard curve of puromycin-linker aligned to an excess of mRNA. The accuracy of the 
standard curve is influenced by the accuracy of the NanoDrop measurement of the mRNA 
concentration. Additionally, it is dependent on the concentration of puromycin-linker annealed 
to mRNA, since the base-pairing will lead to quenching of the fluorescein fluorescence 
compared to the free puromycin-linker (as described in Chapter 4.1.2.2). 
Overall, the yield of mRNA-display was successfully increased to ~20 nM, exceeding the target 
of 10 nM. The results indicate that impurities from mRNA, ligated mRNA and puromycin-linker 
could not be fully removed by immunoprecipitation. These contaminations are most likely 
artifacts from the ligation reactions, that were not removed by PCI/CI extraction and EtOH 
precipitation. This highlighted the necessity of improving the purity of the ligated mRNA. 
4.1.3 Improving the purity of the mRNA-display 
The results obtained in the previously described experiments highlighted the necessity to 
reduce the amount of unligated mRNA and free puromycin-linker after the ligation. While those 
impurities could interfere with the quantification of the mRNA-display by fluorescence assay or 
qPCR, a low purity of ligated mRNA could be even more problematic when used as reference 
in the CPmD experiments. As described in Chapter 4.1.2.3, removing those contaminations 
can only be achieved by changing the purification strategy. 
One approach could be to perform the PCI/CI extraction at low pH. By supplementing sodium 
acetate and using low pH PCI (pH 4.5), the phosphate groups of the DNA backbone are 
protonated, which reduces the negative charge and thus the polarity of the DNA molecules. 
RNA on the other hand has hydroxy groups that can form intramolecular hydrogen bonds with 
the phosphate backbone, making it more resistant to protonation. At low pH, RNA molecules 
remain negatively charged and thus stay soluble in the aqueous phase, while DNA molecules 
partition to the organic phase. Norrild et al, 2025 introduced low pH PCI/CI extraction to remove 
free puromycin-linker and other DNA impurities from the ligated mRNA. However, reproducing 
this approach proved to be difficult and led to a low yield of ligated mRNA after the extraction. 
This indicates that, at low pH, the ligated mRNA-DNA hybrid, which also contains a long PEG 
linker (see Figure S 5 in the appendix), does not behave as expected for pure RNA or DNA 
species. 
4.1.3.1 SplintR ligation and biotin-streptavidin purification 
A better approach to remove both DNA impurities, such as free puromycin-linker, and unligated 
mRNA could be high-affinity and specificity purification using a biotin-streptavidin system. A 5’-
biotinylated DNA oligo was designed so that its complementary sequence spans the 3’-end of 
the mRNA as well as the puromycin-linker (see Figure 10).

In the first approach, the biotin-oligo was added after T4 ssRNA ligase reaction, which resulted 
in low recovery of the ligated mRNA. An explanation could be that the secondary structures 
formed by the ligated mRNA (see Figure 10) could not be outcompeted by the biotin oligo, 
even after heating to 95 °C for 3 min. Thus, the second approach was to supply the biotin oligo 
in the initial annealing reaction. The resulting complex of mRNA, biotin oligo and puromycin-
linker (see Figure 10) could be annealed using SplintR ligase. 
The NUPACK (Nucleic Acid Package) [115] software suite developed at Caltech was used to 
analyze the formation of complexes at different temperatures (see Figure S 6 in the appendix). 
The simulation showed that the proportion of the complex comprising mRNA, biotin oligo and 
puromycin-linker increases with lower temperatures. Thus, the ligation was performed at 20 °C 
to meet the temperature optimum of the SplintR ligase at 16-37 °C. The temperature of 35 °C 
for biotin-streptavidin purification was selected based on two criteria: minimizing the amount 
of complexes with unligated mRNA and biotin oligo to reduce mRNA impurities, while 
maximizing the amount of ligated mRNA bound to the biotin oligo. It is to be noted that the 
equilibrium complex concentrations and secondary structures generated using NUPACK only 
provide an approximation, since the simulation does not support DNA/RNA hybrids. 
After annealing equimolar amounts of mRNA, biotin-oligo and puromycin linker the ligation was 
performed using SplintR ligase. The ligated mRNA was purified by biotin-streptavidin 
purification. The results were analyzed by super-denaturing PAGE and compared to the 
benchmark protocol (see Figure 11), starting with annealing of mRNA and puromycin-linker, 
followed by T4 ssRNA ligase reaction and PCI/CI extraction with EtOH precipitation.

This experiment shows that, by combining a similar yield of ligated mRNA with potentially 
higher recovery and a significant improvement in purity of the ligated mRNA, the SplintR ligase 
reaction with subsequent biotin-streptavidin purification represents a major improvement to the 
protocol.

4.1.4 Summary of Part I 
The molecular biology pipeline to produce mRNA-displayed peptides from the DisProt library 
as described by Norrild et al. 2024 was successfully established. The puromycin-ligation was 
identified as crucial step limiting the yield of mRNA-displayed peptides. To remove this 
bottleneck, three experiments were conducted. First, mRNA transcript was produced by 3 h 
transcription to ensure sequence integrity. Next, a comparison of different ligases confirmed 
that the T4 ssRNA ligase reaction provided the highest ligation efficiency. Finally, the yield of 
purified ligated mRNA from this reaction was improved to 1.3 µM by increasing the 
concentration of mRNA template. The subsequent translation resulted in >20 nM purified 
mRNA-display, which confirmed the removal of the bottleneck. 
However, analysis of the purified translation reactions and negative controls revealed that 
unligated mRNA and puromycin-linker were not sufficiently removed from the ligated mRNA 
without an effective purification strategy. On the one hand, those impurities, if transferred to 
the translation reaction, could reduce the efficiency of the translation reaction, even though the 
purity of the mRNA-display is guaranteed by immunoprecipitation. On the other hand, 
contamination by unligated mRNA could distort the quantification of the dilute phase 
concentration in the following CPmD experiments, where the ligated mRNA is used as a 
reference. To prevent this problem, the mRNA and puromycin-linker were annealed to a 
biotinylated oligo and ligated using SplintR ligase. The ligated mRNA was recovered via biotin-
streptavidin purification, which improved the purity of the ligated mRNA to >90 %. 
The established and optimized molecular biology protocol (see appendix) can be used for full-
length protein sequences, as different steps of the pipeline were tested for e.g., TDP-16 
(results not discussed here).

4.2 Part II: Condensate partitioning by mRNA-display 
The second research objective was to identify sequence determinants and study temperature-
dependency of Ddx4N1 phase separation using Condensate partitioning by mRNA-display 
(CPmD) with the produced mRNA-displayed DisProt library peptides. The CPmD experiments 
were carried out as described by Norrild et al. 2025, with the additions of measuring the dilute 
phase concentration of Ddx4N1 (and not just the dilute phase concentrations of ligated mRNA 
and mRNA-display) and conducting the experiments at multiple temperatures: 20°C, 15 °C, 10 
°C and 4 °C (see Figure 14). 
 
Figure 14: Experimental design to study Ddx4 LLPS and condensate partitioning of the DisProt library at 
different temperatures.

600 µM Ddx4 in high-salt (HS) buffer to induce phase separation. After incubation at room temperature for 1 min, 
the samples are centrifuged at 16.9 rcf for 10 min at 20 °C, 15 °C, 10 °C, or 4 °C, respectively. 5 µL supernatant

ligated mRNA and mRNA display is quantified. To study the LLPS of Ddx4, this procedure is repeated with LS buffer 
without ligated mRNA or mRNA-display. After sampling 5 µL supernatant directly from the centrifuge into HS buffer,

separate positive controls where phase separation is inhibited.

4.2.2 Identifying sequence determinants 
The first step was to confirm the sequence-determinants of Ddx4N1 phase separation 
identified by Norrild et al. 2025. Thus, the results from Norrild et al. 2025 were compared to 
the corresponding CPmD experiment at 20 °C from this thesis. The DisProt library contains 
60,000 peptides of IDRs from the entire DisProt database [116]. Among those are peptides of 
16 amino acid length that are tiled through the sequence of Ddx4N1. Calculating the running 
mean of partition free energy at each position in the Ddx4N1 amino acid sequence from 
overlapping tiles results in a profile of partition free energy (see Figure 16). 
==PAGE:49==
39 
 
Figure 16: Profile of partition free energies along the sequence of Ddx4N1. 
Running mean of partition free energy calculated from overlapping peptides at each position of Ddx4N1. NCPR 
indicates the net charge per residue (red = positive charge, blue = negative charge). Arg/Aro indicate the positions 
of arginine and aromatic residues. 
The profile of partition free energy at 20 °C (Figure 16, 20 °C, lig mRNA) reveals a pattern of 
blocks of ~40 residues length with alternating partition free energy. Those blocks with more 
negative partition free energy contribute stronger to phase separation. Positions ~140 to ~160 
demonstrate particularly strong partitioning. Overall, a stronger partitioning of positively 
charged regions can be observed, particularly in the presence of Arg. Stronger partitioning also 
appears to be related to the presence of aromatic amino acids. 
The partition free energy profile showed that phase separation of Ddx4N1 could be driven by 
specific regions with mainly positive charge, which would partially align with the findings from 
Nott et al. 2015 [18]. They reported that Ddx4N1 phase separation is driven by blocks of 
opposing net negative or net positive charge, based on the observation that a variant with 
scrambled charge blocks at the same overall composition was unable of phase separation. In 
contrast, Brady et al. 2017 reported that substitution of Arg with Lys reduced the phase 
separation propensity of Ddx4, even though the overall protein charge was preserved [25], 
[89], [90], [91]. Thus, positively charged blocks alone are not sufficient to describe Ddx4N1 
phase separation. The profile of partition free energy shows that the regions driving partitioning 
contain both aromatic residues and arginine, which could indicate cation-pi interactions, a 
theory often discussed in literature. This could be complemented by the findings of Nott et al. 
2015 [18], where replacement of Arg by Ala prevented phase separation. Separate 
replacement of Phe by 3-fluorophenylalanine destabilized Ddx4N1 condensates. However, 
neither of those findings provide evidence that those interactions actually take place. Instead, 
the relevance of those amino acids in phase separation could also be explained by the 
hydrophobicity of aromatic residues and the unique combination of positive charge and 
hydrophobicity of Arg, as described by Norrild et al. 2025 [1]. 
The profile of the partition free energy at 20 °C was compared to the results obtained by Norrild 
et al. 2025 under the same conditions (Figure 16, Norrild, lig mRNA). Overall, the profile from 
Norrild et al. 2025 seems to be shifted upwards. There is a significant ~2-fold differences in 
the amplitude of the partition free energy between positions 110 and 160 compared to Norrild 
et al. 2025. Moreover, the partition free energy is significantly lower than Norrild et al. 2025 at 
position 20 to 60. 
==PAGE:50==
40 
The partition free energy of the peptide is typically calculated as the difference in the partition 
free energies between the mRNA-display and a reference sample of ligated mRNA (as done 
for Figure 16, 20 °C, lig mRNA and Norrild, lig mRNA). However, impurities of unligated mRNA 
in the reference distort the partition free energy of the ligated mRNA. To illustrate this impact, 
an additional profile was plotted for Norrild et al. 2025, where the partition free energy of the 
peptide was calculated based on a reference sample that only contained unligated mRNA 
(Figure 16, Norrild, mRNA). Corresponding data was not available for the experiments carried 
out in this thesis. Interestingly, the profiles based on ligated and unligated mRNA references 
from Norrild et al. 2025 seem to be comparable in the two intervals (positions ~20-60 and ~110-
160) where the results from Norrild et al. 2025 and this thesis show the largest deviation. This 
could indicate that the reference sample of unligated mRNA from Norrild et al. 2025 contains 
more unligated mRNA impurities. This would lead to the conclusion that higher purity resulting 
from splint ligation and biotin-streptavidin purification is beneficial for the CPmD experiments. 
4.2.3 Studying temperature dependency 
After the results from the CPmD experiments at 20 °C confirmed the main sequence features 
that had previously been reported by Norrild et al. 2025, the second step was to evaluate the 
influence of temperature reduction to 15 °C, 10 °C and 4 °C on the phase separation behavior 
of Ddx4N1. 
4.2.3.1 Dilute phase concentrations 
During the CPmD experiments, the dilute phase concentrations of mRNA-display, ligated 
mRNA and Ddx4N1 were measured (see Figure 17) and provided insights on the phase 
separation of Ddx4N1 and the partitioning of nucleic acids and peptides into the condensates. 
 
Figure 17: Comparison of dilute phase concentrations of ligated mRNA, mRNA-display and Ddx4N1 
Left: mRNA-display and ligated mRNA determined by qPCR. Right: Ddx4N1 determined by measuring absorbance 
at 280 nm on NanoDrop. The initial concentrations were measured from positive controls: ligated mRNA: 1.37 nM, 
mRNA-display: 0.36 nM, Ddx4N1: 85.18 µM. 
The dilute phase concentration of Ddx4N1 decreased with lower temperatures. This indicated 
increased phase separation, resulting in an increase in dense phase volume fraction and 
dense phase concentration at lower temperatures. The fact that the phase separation of 
Ddx4N1 seems to be inversely correlated to temperature, which generally confirms that 
Ddx4N1 follows UCST phase transition behavior.  
It should be noted that the initial concentration of Ddx4N1 measured in the assay (85.18 µM) 
was lower than expected based on the stock concentration of the protein (528.2 µM, which 
should lead to 105.64 µM in the assay) and that the quality control of the used protein batch 
revealed unexpected behavior during cloud point measurement. While this questions the

reliability of the data, the same trend in Ddx4N1 phase separation was observed in a separate 
replicate of the CPmD experiment with a different batch of Ddx4N1 (see Figure S 8 in the

Both the ligated mRNA and the mRNA-display showed an increase in dilute phase 
concentration with lower temperature. The higher dilute phase concentration of ligated mRNA 
could be explained by different initial concentrations of ligated mRNA (1.37 nM) and mRNA-
display (0.36 nM) in the assay. Generally, the partitioning of ligated mRNA and mRNA-display 
into the dense phase seemed to decrease at lower temperatures, even though the dense 
phase volume fraction increased. This observation will be further discussed in Chapter 4.2.3.3. 
The dilute phase concentration of ligated mRNA and 10 °C deviates from the otherwise linear 
trend. The separate replicate of the experiment, which was not sequenced, showed an overall 
similar trend of decreasing partitioning at lower temperatures, but with a deviation at 15 °C for 
the dilute phase concentration of both ligated mRNA and mRNA-display (see figure Figure S 
8 in the appendix). This indicated that the resolution from sampling four temperatures is not 
sufficient to study the partitioning and that future CPmD experiments are required to solve this 
issue. 
4.2.3.2 Partition free energy of the peptide 
The free energy of partitioning for the mRNA-display and the ligated mRNA reference were 
calculated and used to derive the free energy of partitioning for the Ddx4 peptides as described 
by Norrild et al. 2025 (see Figure 18). 
 
 
Figure 18: Partition free energies of ligated mRNA, mRNA-display and peptide at different temperatures. 
Free energy of partitioning at 20 °C, 15 °C, 10 °C and 4 °C for ligated mRNA and mRNA-display (left) as well as 
the peptides (right). 
The calculated free energies of partitioning for mRNA and ligated mRNA reveal an inverse 
correlation with the temperature, as expected based on the observations for the dilute phase 
concentrations from Figure 17. The partition free energy for Ddx4N1 peptides, which was 
calculated from the partition free energies of ligated mRNA and mRNA display, also displayed 
a decrease at lower temperatures. This was a surprising finding, as the phase separation of 
Ddx4N1 increased at lower temperatures (see Figure 17). Based on the assumption that 
partitioning of short peptides into the condensates informs about the interactions taking place 
in full length Ddx4N1 during phase separation, it was expected that both follow the same trend. 
On the one hand, this could lead to the conclusion that temperature has a different impact on 
the partitioning of peptides into condensates than on the phase separation of the full-length 
domain. On the other hand, the peptides only showed a small energetic contribution to the 
partition free energy of the mRNA-display, which makes it challenging to detect even smaller 
==PAGE:52==
42 
temperature-dependent changes in the partition free energy of the peptide. Additionally, the 
calculation of the partition free energy for mRNA-display and ligated mRNA is strongly 
influenced by the inaccuracy of the qPCR measurement of the dilute phase concentration and, 
even more important, the modeling of the dense phase volume fraction based on the phase 
diagram from Brady et al 2017 [25]. Thus, it is likely that the contribution of the peptide to the 
partitioning of the mRNA-display was masked by the impact of nucleic acids. 
4.2.3.3 Impact of varying protein concentration 
The previous two chapters revealed that two inverse effects could be observed by lowering the 
temperature: One the one hand, the increase in dense phase volume fraction at lower 
temperatures (see Figure 17) should drive depletion from the dilute phase. On the other hand, 
the partition free energy becomes more positive at lower temperatures (see Figure 18), which 
inhibits depletion from the dilute phase. The result of those two effects was an increased 
depletion from the dilute phase at lower temperatures (see Figure 17), which indicates that the 
impact of the partition free energy is stronger than the volume effect. 
However, the outcome of those counteracting effects could be dependent on the protein 
concentration. Inducing phase separation at high protein concentration will lead to a large 
dense phase volume fraction at 20 °C. Thus, the relative increase in dense phase volume 
fraction by lowering the temperature is limited and could be overpowered by the more positive 
partition free energy, leading to less depletion from the dilute phase at lower temperatures. If 
phase separation would be induced at low initial protein concentration, the relative increase in 
dense phase volume fraction by lowering the temperature from 20 °C would be more 
significant, potentially outcompeting the more positive partition free energy. This scenario 
would lead to increased depletion from the dilute phase at lower temperatures. The impact of 
the protein concentration was modelled in Figure S 10 in the appendix. 
4.2.3.4 Sequence-level impact of temperature changes 
The sequence-level impact of temperature changes was evaluated by calculation of the 
running mean of partition free energy across the sequence of Ddx4N1 for 20 °C, 15 °C, 10 °C 
and 4 °C (see Figure 19). 
 
Figure 19: Temperature-dependent profiles of partition free energies along the sequence of Ddx4N1. 
Running mean of partition free energy calculated from overlapping peptides at each position of Ddx4N1 for CPmD 
experiments carried out at 20 °C, 15 °C, 10 °C and 4 °C and compared to the results from Norrild et al. 2025 [1]. 
Overall, the partition free energy displays similar profiles across the Ddx4N1 sequence for the 
different temperatures with an upward shift at lower temperatures. Notably, the profiles for 15 
==PAGE:53==

°C and 10 °C are almost overlapping, while the profiles of 4 °C and 20 °C show a larger offset, 
even though the temperature was changed in equal intervals of ~5 °C. The partition free energy 
profiles at the different temperatures are similar in positions ~20-60 and ~110-160 and 
therefore collectively deviate from the profile of Norrild et al. 2025. This supports the 
explanation that this difference might be caused by the higher purity of the ligated mRNA 
reference. 
There seemed to be an equal change of partition free energy in response to the temperature 
across the while Ddx4N1 sequence rather than more pronounced changes in individual amino 
acid positions. This speaks against sequence-level effects of the temperature and indicates a 
more global effect, e.g., changes in system entropy or volume. However, a more likely 
explanation would be that the offset in the profiles is a result of the model used to calculate the 
partition free energy. The dense phase volume fraction is estimated based on the phase 
diagram from Brady et al. 2017 [25]. This serves as approximation, since it would be 
challenging to measure the dense phase volume fraction directly. The protein used by Brady 
et al. 2017 shows a slightly different phase separation propensity, which could lead to 
overestimation of the dense phase volume fraction (see Figure S 10 in the appendix). Due to 
the inverse relationship between partition free energy and the dense phase volume fraction 
this would result in a positive offset (as observed in Figure 16). This systematic offset would 
align with the observations made in Chapter 4.2.1. Temperature-dependent changes in the 
partition free energy at single amino acid position could be present but too small to detect. 
Their detection might only be possible after correcting for the offset in the partition free energy, 
which in return would require modifications in the modelling of the dense phase volume 
fraction. As a result, sequence-level impact of temperature changes could not be clearly 
determined. 
4.2.4 Summary of Part II 
The results from Norrild et al. 2025 were reproduced in a CPmD experiments at 20 °C with a 
Pearson correlation coefficient of 0.77. Direct comparison of the partition free energy profile 
across the Ddx4N1 sequence showed that differences result from the higher purity of the 
ligated mRNA reference after splint ligation and biotin-streptavidin purification. The analysis 
further showed a periodicity of positively charged regions contributing to Ddx4N1 phase 
separation, particularly amino acid positions ~140-160, driven by Arg and aromatic residues. 
CPmD experiments at 20 °C, 15 °C, 10°C and 4 °C showed that Ddx4N1 displayed increased 
phase separation at lower temperatures. In contrast, the partition free energy of ligated mRNA, 
mRNA-display, and peptides decreased at lower temperatures. It was derived that the interplay 
of increasing dense phase volume fraction and more positive free energy of partitioning at 
lower temperatures resulted in higher depletion of mRNA and mRNA-display from the dilute 
phase. This could be dependent on the initial protein concentration and the temperature range. 
However, the different behavior of peptides and full-length Ddx4N1 challenges the underlying 
assumption of CPmD that partitioning of short peptides into condensates informs about the 
interactions in full-length IDPs during phase separation. 
The comparison of the partition free energy profile across the Ddx4N1 sequence at different 
temperatures revealed a positive offset at lower temperatures. The offset could be caused by 
an overestimation of the dense phase volume fraction based on the phase diagram from Brady 
et al. 2015 [25]. As a result, small sequence-level changes in partition free energy could not 
be detected. Thus, studying the temperature-dependency of Ddx4N1 phase separation 
requires modification of the model underlying the calculation of the partition free energy.

5 Conclusion 
In the first part of this Master thesis project, a molecular biology pipeline to produce mRNA-
displayed peptides from the DisProt library was established in the Protein Biophysics group at 
DTU. A key improvement to the design described by Norrild et al. 2025 was the splint ligation 
of the puromycin-linker using a biotinylated oligo as DNA splint followed by biotin-streptavidin 
purification. This not only removed the initial bottleneck in the ligation reaction but also 
increased the purity of the ligated mRNA to >90 %. While this enabled the production of mRNA-
display at 29 nM, this improvement is even more crucial in the context of CPmD, since the 
calculation of the partition free energy of the peptide is directly influenced by the purity of the 
ligated mRNA reference. It was demonstrated that the increased purity altered the partition 
free energy observed in short sections of the Ddx4N1 sequence. 
Further developments could be made to the mRNA-display protocol in the future, inspired by 
designs presented in literature. The current time required from transcription to quantification of 
the mRNA-display by qPCR is approx. 24-32 hours. This could be reduced by solid-phase 
translation through biotin-streptavidin immobilization [89], [90], [91] using the biotin-oligo or a 
redesigned puromycin-linker, which could eliminate time-consuming washing steps. Moreover, 
the combination of mRNA-display with IVC could extend its application to mapping catalytic 
activity and potentially even allow sequential screening of multiple peptide properties [54], [56], 
[57]. 
In the second part of this Master Thesis project, CPmD was used to study molecular grammar 
and temperature dependency of Ddx4N1 phase separation. The concluded importance of Arg 
and aromatic residues aligns with the findings from Norrild et al. 2025 and thus supports the 
hypothesis that Ddx4N1 phase separation could be driven by the hydrophobicity of those 
residues rather than by positively charged patches and cation-pi interactions, as often reported 
in literature [18], [25]. While Ddx4N1 displayed increased phase separation at lower 
temperatures, the underlying sequence-level effects could not be clearly identified. Instead, 
the analysis revealed a positive offset in the partition free energy of the peptide at lower 
temperatures that could result from an overestimation of the dense phase volume fraction. 
Further investigation of the temperature dependency thus requires an adjustment of the 
underlying model, which goes beyond the scope of this thesis.  
By replicating the results reported by Norrild et al. 2025, this thesis demonstrated that CPmD 
can be used to study the sequence space of biomolecular condensate formation in a 
reproducible manner. After establishing and validating the method for the model system 
Ddx4N1, its application can be expanded to other IDPs in the future, such as TDP16. In doing 
so, CPmD could contribute to understanding the mechanisms underlying IDP-related 
neurodegenerative diseases and crucial cellular processes. 
Lastly, this Master Thesis will contribute to the “Sologics” project driving the commercialization 
of mRNA-display as a platform technology to optimize developability profiles of biologics. Many 
promising biotherapeutic candidates fail due to developability challenges, such as solubility or 
stability issues. Multidimensional biophysical screening of mRNA-displayed peptide libraries in 
experimental set-ups similar to CPmD provide a powerful approach to rescuing failed drug 
candidates.

Figure S 3: Structure and mechanism of puromycin 
Figure taken from Aviner, 2020 [83]. Top/A: Structure of puromycin compared to tyrosyl-tRNA. Puromycin 
resembles the 3’-end of the amino-acylated tRNA (aa-tRNA), with a modified adenosine base and an O-methylated 
tyrosine amino acid, connected by a peptide bond instead of an ester bond. Bottom/B: Mechanism of translation 
inhibition by puromycin. During translation, puromycin enters the A-site of the ribosome, where it accepts the 
nascent polypeptide from the aa-tRNA in the P-site by peptide bond formation. This causes premature termination 
of translation and release of the peptidyl-puromycin.
