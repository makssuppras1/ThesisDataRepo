Abstract 
This study sets out to optimise protein production in Aspergillus nidulans by examining the effect proteases have 
on the protein production as previous reports have indicated that proteases pose as problem for heterologous 
protein production. By searching the literature and by performing BLASTp searches, various proteases were 
identified as troublesome for heterologous protein production. Eight proteases were chosen as targets based on 
the results from the bioinformatical study. For the chosen protease genes, protospacers were designed up- and 
downstream of the genes. Based on these protospacers, four multiplexing CRISPR/Cas9 vectors were constructed. 
Through transformations of A. nidulans strains with the constructed multiplexing CRISPR/Cas9 vectors, three 
different strains were obtained (One strain was AN1426Δ, AN10198Δ, the other was prtAΔ and the last was 
AN10198Δ, AN1426Δ and pepEΔ).  
Besides from employing the CRISPR/Cas9 system, sexual crossing of two of the constructed strains were also 
performed to obtain a strain with protease gene deletions from both parent strains. Multiple crosses were 
obtained with the deletions from both parent strains as well as crosses with only one gene deletion from each 
parent strain. In order to assess the effect of gene deletions on protein production, overexpression vectors were 
constructed. The overexpression vectors expressed either the protein BxlA, AN5463 or mRFP. The affinity tag His-
tag was also fused to some of the proteins. The linearized overexpression vectors were transformed into a strain 
constructed in this study as well as a reference strain. As no overexpression transformants were finally verified, 
the protease deficient strains were characterized through phenotypical studies and by physiological 
characterisation in the BioLector® I micro-fermentation system alongside appropriate reference strains for 
comparison. The micro-fermentations showed that the specific growth rates of the protease deficient strains 
were not necessarily improved compared to a reference strain but nothing could be concluded regarding the 
protein production.  
Resumé 
Dette studie havde til formål at optimere heterolog protein produktion i A. nidulans. Dette blev udført ved at 
undersøge hvilken effekt proteaser havde på protein produktion, da det tidligere er blevet bevist, at de kan 
udgøre et problem for heterolog protein produktion. Ved at undersøge litteraturen og ved at foretage BLASTp 
søgninger blev forskellige proteaser identificeret som mulige kandidater til deletion. Baseret på det 
bioinformatiske studie blev otte proteaser blev valgt til deletion. For de udvalgte proteaser blev protospacers 
designet up- og downstream af generne. Ud fra disse protospacers blev fire multipleksing CRISPR/Cas9 vektorer 
konstrueret. Gennem transformation af A. nidulans stammer med de konstruerede CRISPR/Cas9 vektorer blev tre 
stammer fremstillet (En stamme var AN1426Δ, AN10198Δ, en anden var prtAΔ og den sidste var AN10198Δ, 
AN1426Δ og pepEΔ).  
Ud over brugen af CRISPR/Cas9 systemet, så blev det også udnyttet at A. nidulans har en seksuel cyklus. To af de 
konstruerede stammer blev seksuelt krydset og forskellige kryds kolonier blev isoleret. Nogle af disse kryds havde 
flere gendeletioner fra begge udgangsstammer mens andre kun havde en fra hver. For at vurdere hvilken effekt 
gendeletionerne havde haft, blev forskellige overekspressionsvektorer konstrueret. Overekspressionsvektorerne 
udtrykte enten proteinet BxlA, AN5463 eller mRFP. Affinitetstagget His-tag blev også fusioneret til nogle af 
proteinerne. De lineariserede overekspressionsvektorer blev transformeret ind i en stamme konstrueret i dette 
studie såvel som en reference stamme. Da ingen af overekspressionstransformanterne blev endeligt verificeret, 
blev stammerne nogle af de konstruerede stammer karakteriseret ved hjælp af et fænotypestudie og ved 
fysiologisk karakterisering i mikrofermenteringssystemet BioLector® I sammen med reference stammer. 
Mikrofermenteringerne viste, at den specifikke væksthastighed ikke nødvendigvis blev højere på baggrund af 
deletion af protease gener. Imidlertid kunne intet endeligt blive konkluderet angående protein produktion.

1. Introduction 
1.1 The fungal kingdom 
The fungal kingdom is represented by a diverse group of eukaryotic organisms. The kingdom is traditionally 
divided into six phyla. The different phyla are differentiated between by modes of sexual reproduction, 
ultrastructures and biochemical characters. Two of these traditional phyla are no longer considered a part of 
the fungal kingdom. These are Myxomycetes, which is now considered protists and Oomycetes, which is now 
in the Kingdom Straminipila. The true fungi are classified in the Chytrids, Zygomycetes, Ascomycetes and 
Basidiomycetes (Samson et al. 2010). The majority of fungi are described in Ascomycota, including yeast and 
filamentous fungi, along with Basidiomycota, which include mushrooms (Blackwell 2011).  
Fungi have historical applications, most of which are related to food production. The applications include 
edible mushrooms and the usage of different filamentous fungi and yeast in fermentation processes. The 
most commonly known yeast is Saccharomyces cerevisiae which is used in the production of alcohol and 
bread. For filamentous fungi, different species are known for various processes, such as the production of 
soy sauce by Aspergillus sojae and fermentation of meat by different species in Aspergilli and Penicillia 
(Campbell‐Platt and Cook 1989). These applications are still presently in use, but biotechnology have 
broadened the applications of yeast and filamentous fungi, which now can serve as production hosts for e.g. 
enzymes, colour pigments and biopharmaceuticals (Adrio and Demain 2003).  
Among the different genera of filamentous fungi, one has been employed in several industrial processes – 
The genus Aspergillus. 
 
1.2 Aspergillus species as hosts for protein production 
Members of the genus Aspergillus have been known in the biotechnological industry for their ability to 
produce secondary metabolites for many years. They furthermore facilitate a comprehensive secretory 
system, which is an advantage for the downstream processing in the production of heterologous 
compounds. Many processes involving members of Aspergillus are Generally Regarded as Safe (GRAS). 
Therefore, new processes, which involves Aspergilli, are more readily accepted as GRAS than non-approved 
production hosts. As genetic engineering has advanced, the production of heterologous proteins in 
Aspergillus have been achieved in industrially relevant amounts (Fleißner and Dersch 2010). 
A well-established cell factory of the genus Aspergillus is Aspergillus niger. The most widely used application 
is the production of citric acid. The fungus is also used as host for production of gluconic acid and various 
enzymes, such as cellulases (Pariza and Cook 2010; Frisvad et al. 2011). Another well-established cell factory 
is Aspergillus oryzae which is widely used in the production of hydrolytic enzymes, such as lipases and 
amylases (Yu et al. 2017). 
An organism taxonomically related to the cell factories A. niger and A. oryzae is A. nidulans (Nekiunaite et al. 
2016). A. nidulans has been used as genetic model organism for over 50 years due to its ability to grow on a 
very simple defined medium, its ability to utilize various carbon and nitrogen sources, it has uninucleate 
conidia and for its ability to reproduce sexually. The uninucleate conidia are an advantage when performing 
genetic engineering as to ensure that the induced mutations is retained in the colony. A. nidulans produces 
green colonies with a dark reverse when grown on solid media (Pontecorvo et al. 1953; Samson et al. 2010). 
Due to the ability of A. nidulans to reproduce sexually, the filamentous fungus can undergo three different 
reproductive cycles; asexual (anamorph), sexual (teleomorph) and parasexual (Rogerson and Webster 1981; 
Casselton and Zolan 2002). An overview of the different cycles is present in Figure 1.

The anamorph state is characterised by the germination of a uninucleate, often haploid, asexual spore, 
which swells and starts polarized growth by apical extension. The fungal pigments are produced during the 
germination as they are required for sporulation structures. The extension, called hyphae, is tubular and 
common for filamentous fungi. Subapically, the hypha branches in order to produce new hyphal tips. 
Thereby, the number of extending tips increases and a continuous tubular network is built; this is called 
mycelium. The hyphae are compartmentalized by septa, which is a sub-apical formation of cellular cross-
walls. When grown on solid media, the hyphae can sense the air, leading to formation of aerial structure. 
These are termed conidiophores and grow vertically.

The conidiophores swell before generation of a rounded top, called a terminal vesicle. From the terminal 
vesicle, up to 60 buds appears simultaneously. This structure is called metulae. The metulae extends and 
bud, forming a second row of cells. This second row is called phialides. The metulae and phialides are 
referred to as sterigmata. The phialides bud mitotically resulting in a chain of consecutive spherical 
conidiospores. The conidiospores mature after formation and when the chain consists of 4-5 conidiospores, 
the oldest spore has fully matured (Pontecorvo et al. 1953; Calvo et al. 2002). A visualization of the 
conidiophore can be seen in Figure 2.

It is not clear exactly what initiates sexual reproduction in A. nidulans. It is thought that when the asexual 
reproduction has slowed down in the centre of the colony, two mycelia, which grows in close proximity, may 
anamostose. This allows for exchange of nuclei. In the teleomorph state of A. nidulans, any two haploid 
nuclei can fuse and form a zygote as the filamentous fungus is homothallic. The anamostosis creates a 
mycelial compartment that undergoes synchronous divisions, which results in a network of ascogenous 
dikaryotic hyphae. The ascogenous hyphae forms an ascocarp called cleistothecium. Asci, a sac, are formed 
within the cleistothecium. Ascospores are formed by meiosis followed by mitosis within the ascus. Eight 
ascospores are produced after the first round of meiosis and mitosis for A. nidulans. An additional mitotic

division occurs in the mature ascospores, yielding binucleate ascospores. The ascospores are released from 
the cleistothecium when it bursts open. Both heterokaryon, which contains with multiple genetically 
different nuclei, and homokaryon, which contains multiple identical nuclei, can enter the sexual cycle 
(Champe, Rao, and Chang 1987; Yager 1992; Casselton and Zolan 2002; K.-H. Han 2009). A diagram of the 
cleistothecium, which contains the asci with ascospores, covered in Hülle cells, can be seen in Figure 3.

The third cycle, called the parasexual state, is initiated when haploid nuclei fuse in heterokaryon and 
continue to divide mitotically. Cross-over might occur between homologous chromosomes. Random 
chromosome loss, termed haplodization, restores the haploid chromosome number of eight for A. nidulans 
(Casselton and Zolan 2002).  
The development of the filamentous fungus through the different cycles is strictly regulated. Even though it 
is not known exactly how the sexual reproduction is initiated, various regulators have been identified. An 
important positive regulator of sexual development in A. nidulans is encoded by the gene veA. The 
polypeptide VeA consists of 573 amino acids and contains a bipartite nuclear localization signal (NLS) located 
in the N terminus. The polypeptide VeA interacts with the protein VelB in the cytoplasm. The protein VelB, 
encoded by the gene velB,  is 369 amino acids long and a member of the velvet protein family (K. H. Han et 
al. 2010; Ahmed et al. 2013). The VeA-VelB complex migrates to the nucleus when the cell is not illuminated. 
The complex is transported by the α-importin KapA. In the nucleus, the VelB/VeA complex promotes 
cleistothecium formation. The velvet protein VelB can also bind with another member of the velvet protein 
family, VosA, which promotes spore viability and repress asexual development. The proteins VelB and VeA 
can also take part in the velvet complex VelB/VeA/LaeA, which activates sexual development and secondary 
metabolism in A. nidulans (Ö. Bayram et al. 2010; Ö. S. Bayram et al. 2010). An overview of the velvet 
complex and its actions can be seen in Figure 4.

The mutant veA1 contains a point mutation from G to T at the first codon, which results in the use of the 37th 
codon as the translation initiation codon. This leads to a truncation of 36 codons at the N terminus and the 
NLS is reduced by half (K. H. Han et al. 2010; Regulin and Kempken 2018). The veA1 mutation causes a 
delayed and reduced production of cleistothecia which results in development of asexual spores, which are 
preferred in genetic studies (K.-H. Han 2009). The delay is due to weakened interaction between the proteins 
VeA1 and VelB and between the protein VeA1 and the α-importin KapA and thereby reduced transport to 
the nucleus (Ö. Bayram et al. 2010; M. Calvo and Dhingra 2011). Other factors affect the sexual development 
of A. nidulans such as nutrients, high osmolarity and oxygen supply. A high salt concentration in the media 
inhibits sexual development, while restriction of oxygen promotes sexual development (K.-H. Han 2009). 
In order for the filamentous fungus to proliferate, the organism secretes different enzymes which can 
degrade the polymers present in the extracellular matrix. Once degraded to monomers, the fungal cells are 
capable of taking up the monomers which can then participate in the metabolism of the fungi. The protein 
production is therefore central for the development of the organism. 
1.3 Production of proteins 
Proteins are produced through the protein synthesis. The protein synthesis can be divided into transcription 
and translation. For eukaryotes, the transcription takes part in the nucleus, while the translation takes part 
in the cytoplasm. The RNA polymerase transcribes the gene into pre-mRNA. The introns of the mRNA are 
spliced out and the mature mRNA is transported to the cytoplasm. Here, the mature mRNA is translated to 
protein by the ribozyme (Madigan, Martinko, and Parker 2003).  
Some proteins contain a signal peptide and is transport into the rough endoplasmic reticulum (ER) – This 
happens co-translationally. The signal sequence, once translated, is recognized by a signal recognition 
particle (SRP). The SRP binds to the signal sequence and binds to an SRP receptor of a translocon, which is 
located across the membrane of ER. The mRNA is translated by the ribozyme and fed directly into the ER 
lumen. In the ER, the proteins are integrated into transport vesicles. The transport vesicles move in an 
anterograde direction from the ER to the Golgi.  
 
==PAGE:18==
Page 18 of 150 
 
From the Golgi, they can move to endosomes or lysosomes. From here, they can be secreted constitutively 
or by regulation (Alberts et al. 2002). 
Besides from the production of native proteins, microorganisms can also be genetically manipulated to 
produce heterologous proteins. This can be done by introducing linearized DNA to the cell. Linearized DNA 
mimics DNA which contains double-stranded breaks (DSBs). Linear DNA recruits proteins from non-
homologous end joining (NHEJ) or homologous recombination (HR). Repair through NHEJ is error prone and 
directly leads to mutations as it repairs. In NHEJ, a complex of the proteins Ku and DNA-dependant protein 
kinase (DNA-PK) binds to the ends of the DSB. The protein Ku is a heterodimer of Ku70 and Ku80. After 
formation of a synapse, the ends are further processed by nucleases in order to remove single-stranded tails. 
The ends are then ligated together by ligase. The DSB can also be repaired by HR. This pathway repairs the 
DSBs by the usage of homology to a template. HR can result in the insertion of a new gene or the deletion of 
one. In HR, the ends are digested by 5’-exonucleases which leads to single-stranded 3’ ends. In eukaryotes, 
Rad51 catalyses hybridization of one of the single-stranded 3’ ends and a complementary sequence in a 
homologous double-stranded molecule. This process is called strand invasion. The 3’ end of the invading 
strand is extended by DNA polymerase which displaces the parent strand as a growing single-stranded loop. 
When the loop extends to a sequence which is complementary to the other end of DSB, the complimentary 
sequences basepair. This 3’ end is then extended by DNA polymerase using the single-stranded loop as 
template. The new 3’ ends are ligated to the exonuclease-digested 5’ ends, creating two Holliday junctions. 
Phosphodiester bonds that cross over from one parent strand to another is cleaved and the ends are ligated. 
Genes can be integrated into the genome of the host by flanking the genes, which has to be inserted, with 
homologous sequences. For A. nidulans, the homologous regions have to be long, >1,500 bp, compared to 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae, which only needs 20-50 bp in order to ensure integration at the desired locus 
(Hansen et al. 2011). Integration of DNA by HR is advantageous as random integration can cause mutations 
or alter the expression of the nearby genes. This can lead to unknown pleiotropic effects. Site-specific 
integration ensures that the gene is integrated into a site with high expression levels. The site-specific 
integration also allows for comparative studies in which a wild type and a mutant strain can be compared on 
equal terms to assess the effect of mutations (Nødvig et al. 2015; Lodish et al. 2016). The integration of a 
general expression cassette by HR can be seen in Figure 5.

After integration of the DNA molecule, the organism can transcribe and translate the integrated gene and 
thereby produce a heterologous protein. 
To ensure a high yield of the heterologous protein, the transcription and translation of the gene must be 
high, the secretion system must be utilized correctly (if the protein is to be secreted), correctly folded, 
limited induction of stress secretion, the desired post-translational modifications must be performed 
correctly and limited or no degradation of the product. Different strategies can be used in order to enhance 
the expression of heterologous proteins. 
To enhance the transcription of the gene, a constitutively strong promoter can be employed in the 
expression system. This ensures high availability of recombinant mRNA. Another strategy is to use a strong 
inducible promoter in order to employ a biphasic process. 
If the recombinant protein is to be secreted, gene fusion can be used to enhance the secretion. One way is 
to add a secretion leader signal. For different commonly used hosts, efficient signal peptides are known from 
native proteins, which are secreted. These known signal peptides can be fused to the gene of interest. 
Sometimes, the usage of signal peptides is not successful. In those cases, the gene of interest is fused with 
the sequence of a carrier protein.  
The stability of the recombinant mRNA is also important. For eukaryotes, the mRNA contains introns as 
mentioned earlier. To get a functional gene product, the introns have to be removed due to differences in 
splicing machinery between different species. Codon optimization can also be employed to enhance the 
stability.  
The translocation to the ER has to be improved for secreted proteins as the production of heterologous, 
secreted proteins puts a pressure on the secretion system. The entry into the secretion system can be 
improved. This can be done by overexpressing signal peptidases, which cleave the signal peptide from the 
mRNA, as well as proteolytic cleavage enzymes.  
Vesicle trafficking between ER and golgi can be a rate-limiting step. Overexpression of SNAREs and their 
regulators can stimulate vesicular trafficking and thereby increase the secretion of the heterologous protein. 
Overexpression of proteins can increase protein secretion stress, which decreases the productivity and 
fitness of the cell. One way to accommodate this is by overexpression of chaperones. As this can be tricky, 
regulation of global activators of the ER or the unfolded protein response has also been proven successful. 
Correct folding of the heterologous protein can be a bottleneck in the production. Formation of disulphide 
bridges is a general engineering target.  
If the heterologous protein is to be glycosylated, it can be of importance to engineer the glycosylation 
system of the host. This is mostly the case when using prokaryotes as host. 
By-product removal can be used to increase the flux through the desired pathway.  
Proteases poses as a problem as they can degrade the heterologous protein. The deletion of extracellular 
proteases has proven to give a higher yield of the heterologous protein. Regulators of proteases has also 
been deleted and shown to increase the yield (Davy, Kildegaard, and Andersen 2017).

1.4 Proteases 
Proteases, also known as peptidases, are hydrolases, which act on a peptide bond. The proteases can be 
divided into two groups; endopeptidases, which act on internal peptide bonds, or exopeptidases, which act 
on a free N- or C-terminus. Endopeptidases are also called proteinases. A visualization of the division of the 
terms can be seen in Figure 6 (Barrett and McDonald 1986; Rawlings 2013).  
 
Figure 6: The definition of a protease 
Peptidases can be divided into different categories based on their catakytic type. The different classes are 
serine, threonine, cysteine, aspartic, glutamic and metallo-.  
Serine peptidases are, often, characterized by a catalytic triad with a serine, a histidine and an aspartate. 
Threonine peptidases are all presumed to have an N-terminal threonine which carries both the nucleophile 
and the general base. Cysteine peptidases are characterized by a catalytic dyad consisting of cysteine and 
histidine. A third and even a fourth residue can also be involved. Aspartic peptidases are all endopeptidases. 
The active site is composed of two aspartates binding water. Glutamic peptidases have two families. The 
members of the first family, G1, have a catalytic dyad consisting of a glutamate and glutamine. Members of 
the other family, G2, has a catalytic dyad consisting of an aspartate and a glutamate. Metallopeptidases have 
a metal ion located in the catalytic site (Rawlings 2013). 
Proteases have two different modes of action. Aspartic, glutamic and metalloproteases perform a one-step 
catalysis. They activate a water molecule which performs a nucleophilic attack on the peptide bond 
(Mandujano-González et al. 2016). Cysteine, serine and threonine proteases perform a two-step catalysis. 
These types of proteases use a nucleophilic residue, usually in a catalytic triad, to perform a nucleophilic 
attack to bind covalently to the substrate. In this step, the first product is released. The covalent acyl-enzyme 
intermediate is then hydrolysed by activated water in order to achieve complete hydrolysis. This result in 
release of the second product and the enzyme is free again (Dodson and Wlodawer 1998).  
As the interest in production of heterologous proteins is rising, the issue with proteases, which degrades 
heterologous proteins, should be handled. This can be done by lowering the proteolytic activity of the host 
through deletion of protease genes. To increase the production of human lysozyme in A. oryzae, two 
protease genes were disrupted. The two genes were tppA and pepE and the production was increased with 
63%. The protease genes pepA, pepE, alpA, tppA and palB was first individually disrupted in A. oryzae to 
assess the proteolytic activity by each protease. The genes pepA and alpA encode acid and alkaline

pepAa, pepAb and pepAd. The effect of these gene disruptions was measured by the heterologous 
production of laccase. The production was increased with 21%, 42% and 30% for disruption of the genes 
pepAa, pepAb and pepAd, respectively (Wang et al. 2008).  
A regulatory factor, called prtT, has been identified in various Aspergillus species. The regulator prtT is 
involved in the regulation of protease expression. When disrupted in A. oryzae, lower levels of the alkaline 
serine protease AlpA and neutral metalloprotease NpI was detected. For A. fumigatus, the secreted levels of 
six proteases were significantly lowered. No ortholog has been identified in A. nidulans (Punt et al. 2008; 
Sharon, Hagag, and Osherov 2009). 
In the production host Trichoderma reesei, which is an efficient producer of extracellular lignocellulolytic 
enzymes, 13 secreted proteases where identified to troublesome the production of therapeutic antibodies. 
Seven of these, the genes pep1, pep3, pep4, gap1, gap2, tsp1, slp1, were disrupted in the strain. The genes 
pep1, pep3 and pep4 encode aspartic proteases. The genes gap1 and gap2 encode glutamic proteases and 
the genes tsp1 and slp1 encodes trypsin-like and subtilisin proteases, respectively. The resulting protease 
deficient strain of T. reesei showed a 96% reduction in proteolytic activity. This is higher than the reduction 
in the ten-gene disrupted strain of A. oryzae, which, in total, showed a decrease of 45% in proteolytic 
activity. The genome of Aspergilli shows an increase of putative protease, typically over 300 genes per 
specie. A. oryzae have 336 putative genes, of which 57 is secreted, while T. reesei have up to 39 secreted 
proteases. For A. nidulans, the number of secreted proteases is around 80. The number of secreted 
proteases may be higher in Aspergilli than T. reesei. Therefore, more protease genes may be needed to be 
knocked-out to ensure a low protease activity. The production of the heterologous protein of interest in A. 
oryzae was increased even though the protease activity was not lowered as much  (Landowski et al. 2015).  
As evident from past experience of disruption of protease genes, heterologous protein expression can 
benefit from deletion of protease genes. Furthermore, it is also to be considered which protease genes to 
knock-out. The impact of each knock-out on the proteolytic activity and the heterologous protein expression 
is different depending on the gene, which is disrupted. The disruption of multiple protease genes is also a 
possibility and has proven to give a better result. However, the fitness of the organism is also to be kept in 
mind – Hence, not all proteases can be deleted (Price, Reed, and Palsson 2004).

In order to optimise the heterologous protein production, the microorganism has to be genetically 
manipulated. Different genetic tools exist to do so. The effect of these genetic alterations also has to be 
assessed to evaluate if the genetic alterations have had the desired effect. 
1.5 Genetic editing in fungi and reporter systems 
Different gene editing tools can be used to disrupt or delete unwanted genes in microorganisms. One of 
these tools is the gene editing technology termed CRISPR/Cas9. CRISPR is the abbreviation for Clustered 
Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic repeats. The system consists of two components; the Cas9 nuclease 
and chimeric guide RNA (sgRNA). The sgRNA can be engineered to target specific sites in the genome. The 
part of the sgRNA which can be “programmed” is termed the protospacer and has a size of 20 bp. The 
protospacer binds to the specific site in the DNA through base pairing. The sgRNA can also bind to the Cas9 
nuclease and thereby guide it to the specific site. The binding of the sgRNA to the DNA and the Cas9 
nuclease can be seen in Figure 7. 
 
Figure 7: An illustration of the binding of an sgRNA to a DNA strand and a Cas9 nuclease. The sgRNA (green) binds to the DNA 
molecule (blue) at the target site (upstream from the PAM (red)) through base pairing. The Cas9 nuclease (yellow) binds to the sgRNA 
and induces a DBS. Adapted from (Nødvig et al. 2015). 
The Cas9 nuclease can induce DSBs as indicated by scissors in Figure 7. The microorganism can repair this 
break by NHEJ or HR as mentioned earlier. NHEJ dominates HR in filamentous fungi (Nødvig et al. 2015). One 
way to direct filamentous fungi towards HR is by disruption of the gene Ku70. The protein Ku70 forms a 
complex with the protein Ku80 and is involved in initiation of NHEJ. If the gene Ku70 is disrupted, the  HR 
frequency is enhanced (Gandía et al. 2016).  
The Cas9 nuclease requires the presence of a short Protospacer Adjacent Motif (PAM) next to the binding 
site of the protospacer. As different Cas9 nucleases exists, varying among species, the different nuclease 
requires different PAM sequences. The PAM sequence for the commonly used Streptococcus pyogenes Cas9 
nuclease is NGG. NAG can also be used but with decreased efficiency (Nødvig et al. 2015).  
The CRISPR/Cas9 system can be ribozyme-mediated or tRNA-mediated. In a ribozyme-mediated CRISPR/Cas9 
system, the sgRNA is flanked by the hammerhead (HH) and hepatitis delta virus (HDV) ribozymes. The 
cassette is expressed by RNA polymerase II. The sgRNA can therefore be released from the mRNA by the 
ribozymes and initiate the genetic engineering. In the tRNA-mediated CRISPR/Cas9 system, the protospacer 
is flanked tRNA sequences. Pre-tRNAs contain internal splicing sites and can thereby release the sgRNA. The 
system utilizes RNA polymerase III. The tRNA-based CRISPR/Cas9 system allows for simple delivery of

Multiplexing using the ribozyme system is troublesome. An expression cassette has to be produced for each 
sgRNA and a multiplexing ribozyme-based CRISPR/Cas9 vector will therefore have to contain multiple 
expression cassettes. This complicates the construction of the vector as the promoter and the terminator is 
fairly large (2 and 0.7 kb respectively). The vector will also be unstable as the same sequences will be 
repeated in the plasmid. Multiplexing using tRNA as spacers solves these problems. Only one expression 
cassette is needed and each sgRNA is spaced by a tRNA. The construct will therefore by smaller and 
repetition of the promoter and terminator is not necessary.  
The different fragments of a vector can be assembled through USER™ fusion. USER™ (Uracil specific excision 
reagent) fusion is based on the excision of single uracil bases in a DNA fragment. The DNA fragments, which 
is to be fused together, is amplified by Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) and primers which contains an 
uracil base linked to a 5’ extension. By digestion with the USER enzyme, the uracil base is excised. This 
generates 3’ single stranded overhangs on both sides of the PCR fragment. By complementary design of the 
primer 5’ extensions, the overhangs line up and makes seamless insertion of the fragments possible. 
Overhangs for the backbone is created by linearization, i.e. by dsDNA cleavage with an endonuclease 
followed by ssDNA nicking with a nicking enzyme which results in specific overhangs ready for fusion with 
the other fragments. Multiple fragments can be fused together by this method and allows for the 
construction of among other the multiple splicing cassette constructed by Nødvig et al. (2018). Here, the 
overhangs of the different PCR fragments generate the protospacers designed. The assembled vector is 
transformed into Escherichia coli. A schematically overview can be seen in Figure 9 (Nødvig et al. 2018).

The CRISPR/Cas9 vector can, after being assembled and purified, be transformed into the fungal cells. The 
transformation can be achieved in multiple ways. The most common is by transformation of fungal 
protoplasts. Protoplasts are fungal cells with enzymatically digested cell walls. This improves the DNA uptake 
of the fungal cell. The cell wall is degraded by mixing fungal mycelia with an enzymatic mix in a buffered 
solution in order to keep the protoplasts osmotic stable. For the transformation, the protoplasts and the 
DNA construct are added to a transformation buffer, which improves the DNA uptake and protect the 
protoplasts (Ruiz-Díez 2002). Using the strategy from Nødvig et al. (2018), oligo’s are added to this mix as 
well; these serve as gene-targeting substrate (GTS) (Nødvig et al. 2018). 
In order to assess the effect of genetic modifications, such as deletion of protease genes, a reporter gene can 
be expressed in the system. A reporter gene encodes a product which is easily monitored. The expression of 
the reporter gene can be measured by the amount of reporter mRNA, reporter protein or by enzymatic 
activity of the reporter protein. Reporter genes should not be natively produced in order to have reduced 
background activity. The signal produced should also be rapid, highly sensitive, reproducible and easily 
detected (Jiang, Xing, and Rao 2008). One commonly used reporter gene is Red Fluorescent Protein (RFP). 
RFP is a fluorophore which emit red-orange light when excited.  
==PAGE:25==
Page 25 of 150 
 
Different variations of RFP exist but all have a weight of app. 25.9 kDa. The excitation minimum is 558 nm 
while the maximum is 583 nm. Fluorescent proteins have a conserved tertiary structure which consists of a 
chromophore protected by a β-barrel. The RFPs have different maturation times which makes each useful 
for different experimental setups (James Remington 2002; Piatkevich and Verkhusha 2011).  
The enzymatic activity of a reporter enzyme can be determined by the usage of colorimetric substrate. When 
then substrate is cleaved by the reporter enzyme, a chromophore is released and can be measured at a 
specified wavelength. The optical density (OD) measured by a spectrophotometer will increase with 
increasing concentration of the chromophore and thereby enzyme activity (Jiang, Xing, and Rao 2008).  
The reporter protein can also be purified using an affinity tag. An affinity tag used for purification is defined 
as exogenous amino acids sequences that bind with a high affinity to a chemical ligand linked to a solid base 
or to an immobilized antibody. The affinity tag is fused to the N or C terminus of the protein. One of the 
most used affinity tags is the His-tag. It contains six or more consecutive histidine residues. As the tag is 
small (0.84 kDa for a hexahistidine tag) and is uncharged at physiological pH, the His-tag does not usually 
affect the folding and function of the protein. The tagged protein is purified by using immobilized metal-ion 
affinity chromatography (IMAC). Metal ions are immobilized in the chromatographic matrices by a chelating 
agent. The imidazole ring of histidine has a high affinity for the chelated metal ions. The tagged proteins can 
be eluted from the matrix by either adjusting the pH of the column buffer or by adding free imidazole (Unal 
et al. 2008; Bornhorst and Falke 2010; Spriestersbach et al. 2015). 
One group of enzymes which can be used as reporter proteins is lytic polysaccharide monooxygenases 
(LPMOs). This group of enzymes is rather unelucidated but can play an important part in the biofuel 
production. 
1.6 Lytic polysaccharide monooxygenases  
LPMOs are monocopper redox enzymes which hydroxylate the C1 or C4 carbons of scissile glycosidic bonds. 
The enzymes are capable of converting recalcitrant polysaccharides. LPMOs have been thought to use 
molecular oxygen and a reductant that delivers two electrons per catalytic cycle when hydroxylating a 
polysaccharide. Bissaro et al. (2017) proved that H2O2 was the preferred co-substrate of LPMOs, changing 
how the enzymes are thought to function. This insight into LMPOs mode of action, which previously has 
been inconclusive, has brought new perspective to the usage of the enzymes regarding conversion of 
recalcitrant polysaccharides. The usage of LMPOs can reduce some of the issues from the petrochemical 
industry as they can convert recalcitrant polysaccharides into substrates for other enzymes involved in the 
biofuel production (Bissaro et al. 2017).  
LPMOs are divided into different auxiliary activity (AA) families in the CAZy database; currently AA9, AA10, 
AA11 and AA13. Members of the AA9 family is characterized by the ability to oxidize glycosidic bonds in 
cellulose. As the members of AA9 shows a broad sequence diversity, is has been suggested that they might 
be able to oxidize other substrates. Members of the AA10 family are capable of degrading chitin, while 
others degrade cellulose. Only few members of the AA11 family has been annotated and only the function of 
one enzyme is known. This enzyme is able to cleave chitin. The knowledge about the mode of action for 
members of AA13 is also limited. One enzyme has been proven to cleave starch (Vu et al. 2014; Hemsworth 
et al. 2014; Busk and Lange 2015; Voshol, Vijgenboom, and Punt 2017). 
The gene AN5463 encodes an LPMO which belongs to the AA13 family and is predicted to have starch 
binding sites (Arnaud et al. 2012; Lombard et al. 2014).

1.7 Cultivation of microorganisms 
For high-throughput characterization of constructed strains, micro-fermentations can be used for cultivation. 
The company m2p-labs has developed a bench top micro-fermentation system which can carry out high-
throughput fermentations while monitoring most common fermentations parameters, such as cell density, 
pH, oxygen saturation (DO) and fluorescent molecules, online. The system is called BioLector® I® and has an 
incubation chamber with an orbital shaker which can hold a single microtiter plate. The temperature and 
humidity can be controlled during the fermentation. The principle behind the online monitoring of the 
BioLector® I® can be seen in Figure 10.

The cell density is measured by scattered light intensity and filters can detect pH and DO values by optode 
technology. Concentration of fluorescence proteins is measured by fluorescence intensity. The optodes are 
located in the wells of the microtiter plate. A visualization of the FlowerPlate® can be seen in Figure 11. The 
design of the FlowerPlate® provides aeration that is scalable to lab and pilot scale fermentors. Microtiter 
plates with round wells are also an option.

When setting up the experiment, the gain of the different parameters can be chosen. The gain of a

are recommended by m2p-labs. The gain can be optimized by running experiments with different gains and 
assessing which gain gives the best resolution (m2p-labs 2015). 
1.8 Aim 
The aim of this project is to construct a new protein production host using A. nidulans. This will be achieved 
by multiple gene deletions by using the multiplexing tRNA-based CRISPR/Cas9 system. The targeted genes 
for deletions are extracellular proteases some of which have proven to affect the expression of heterologous 
proteins in other species. The orthologous of the proteases will be identified in A. nidulans, if identified in 
other organisms. The effect of the deletions will be assessed by integration and production of a model 
protein and, after proof of concept, for other proteins of interest. In this project, RFP, BxlA and AN5463 are 
chosen as model proteins/enzymes. 
1.8.1 Experimental strategy 
The experimental strategy of this project has been visualized in Figure 12. A literature study will be carried 
out to identify proteases which can be of interest to knock-out. By performing BLASTp searches, homologs to 
the identified candidates will be identified in A. nidulans. Based on the BLASTp results, the proteases to be 
targeted are chosen and based on the gene sequences, protospacers will be designed. Fragments with 
appropriate overhangs will be amplified by PCR and linked together and inserted into a vector backbone by 
USER™ fusion. The constructed tRNA-based CRISPR/Cas9 vectors are transformed into protoplasters of A. 
nidulans. Mutants will be confirmed through tissue PCR. Primers will therefore also be designed to identify if 
the desired mutation has been employed. The transformation will be performed in two different strains of A. 
nidulans. These two strains will be crossed to yield a final strain with the desired genotype. The effect of the 
deleted proteases will be assessed by production of model proteins and thereby other proteins through 
==PAGE:28==
Page 28 of 150 
 
proof of concept. Some of the model proteins will be fused to a His-tag to enable purification. The model 
proteins will also, in some cases, be fused to a secretion signal peptide. The overexpression fragments will be 
integrated into IS4 of A. nidulans (Unpublished data). The marker gene in the overexpression cassette is 
flanked by direct repeats for possible loop-out. The strain will be cultivated using high-throughput micro-
fermentations.

2.2 Identification of proteases to knock-out 
The literature was searched to identify candidates for gene deletion, both proteases, which are secreted and 
proteases, which previously have been disrupted. 32 secreted proteases have been identified in A. niger (Pel 
et al. 2007). Different secreted proteases have previously been disrupted in A. oryzae as described in the 
introduction. An overview of the genes encoding these proteases and their description can be seen in 
Appendix 3. 
The amino acid sequences of the proteases listed in Appendix 3 were used as input for BLASTp searches 
against the genome of A. nidulans FGSC A4 using Aspergillus Genome Database (AspGD) Multi-Genome 
Search. AspGD uses NCBI BLASTp v.2.2.25+. The systemic and standard name is related to AspGD database.

The BLASTp searches were performed using default settings. A minimum of 50 % in identities is set as 
criterium for the hits in order to be considered as a candidate for gene deletion. 
Landowski et. al (2015) identified proteases in T. reesei which could optimize the strain for production of 
biopharmaceuticals if they were knocked-out. The proteases are listed in Appendix 3. 
The proteases from T. reesei were used as input for BLASTp searches using NCBI Standard Protein BLAST with 
default settings and blastp as algorithm and Non-redundant protein sequences (nr) as database. The 
searches were performed against the genome of A. niger as the genome of A. niger is better curated than 
the genome of A. nidulans. The best BLASTp hits from the searches against the genome of A. niger was then 
used as input for BLASTp searches against the genome of A. nidulans. The BLASTp searches were performed 
in the same manner.

2.5 Protoplastation of strains 
Protoplaster was made for the strains NID108, NID109, LJ1 and LJ2. 100 mL of MM was inoculated using MM 
plates inoculated with the given strain. Some of the 100 mL MM was added to the plate and the mycelium 
was loosened with a Drigalski spatula. The mycelium containing MM was added to a 500 mL baffled shaking 
flask and incubated for 24-48 h at 37 °C (150 rpm). The culture was filtered through sterile Mira Cloth and 
washed with sterile Milli-Q water. The mycelium was transferred to a 50 mL falcon tube using a sterile 
spoon. Glucanex was dissolved in Aspergillus protoplastation buffer (APB) (40 mg/mL) and sterile filtered. 10 
mL of the Glucanex solution was added to each tube. The tubes were incubated at 30 °C (150 rpm) for 3 h. 
APB was added to the tubes; Up to 40 mL. An overlay of 1:1 Aspergillus Transformation buffer (ATB) in Milli-
Q water was made; 5 mL was added. The tubes were centrifuged at 3,000 g for 13 min with slow 
deceleration. A halo was observed between the two layers; This was captured and transferred to a new 
falcon tube. ATB was added up to 40 mL and the tubes were centrifuged at 3,000 g for 13 min. The 
supernatant was discarded by decantation. The pellet was resuspended in 1 mL ATB. The mix was 
transferred to cryo stocks and stored at -80 °C. 
Media and buffer compositions can be seen in Appendix 2.

4. Discussion 
4.1 Choice of proteases 
The literature was searched to investigate, which proteases have previously been disrupted to optimise 
protein production. The findings were used as input for BLASTp searches to identify orthologs in the genome 
of A. nidulans. Extracellular proteases from the filamentous fungi A. niger was also included in the BLASTp 
searches. Based on the bioinformatical study, eight different proteases were chosen for gene deletion. The 
proteases were chosen based on past experience and because they were representative to the various 
categories of proteases. The ortholog to prtB in A. oryzae has proven to decrease proteolytic activity when it 
was knocked-out while the ortholog to pepE has previously been knocked out in both A. niger and A. oryzae. 
In A. niger, the knock-out resulted in an increase in production of heterologous protein and in A. oryzae, the 
knock out resulted in decreased proteolytic activity and increased heterologous protein production.  The 
disruption of the ortholog to prtA in A. oryzae resulted in a decrease in proteolytic activity. These proteases, 
which have previously been disrupted, are either aspartic (prtB and pepE) or serine (prtA). From the BLASTp 
hits, other proteases were chosen in order to cover the different types of proteases. These were 
metalloproteases (AN0747 and pepI) and serine proteases (AN7159, AN10198 and AN1426). Both modes of 
action of the active site are represented in the chosen proteases – However, not all types of proteases were 
present in the candidates found in this thesis. The categories, which were not represented, are threonine 
and glutamic proteases.  
The strains, which were constructed by usage of the CRISPR/Cas9 system, either contained a deletion of one 
serine protease, two serine proteases or two serine proteases and an aspartic protease. Strains constructed 
through sexual cycle of strains LJ1 and LJ2 contained either no deletions, deletion of two serine proteases or 
deletion of three serine proteases.   
4.2 Multiplexing CRISPR/Cas9 vectors 
To utilise a multisplicing cassette, the release of all sgRNAs have to be successful. By using tRNA as spacers in 
the transcript, the sgRNAs are released by RNase Z and P which are highly conserved. Previously, up to three 
genetic alterations have been achieved in A. nidulans using one CRISPR/Cas9 vector. In this study, four 
genetic alterations have been accomplished in a single round of transformation using a single tRNA-based 
CRISPR/Cas9 vector and oligos as GTS. More genetic alterations using one vector can be exploited. This was 
attempted by amplifying the expression cassette of the constructed CRISPR/Cas9 vectors. However, it was 
not possible to achieve a high enough concentration of the cassette to purify it. As the sgRNAs are spaced by 
the same tRNA sequence, unspecific binding posed as a problem. One way to bypass this hurdle could be to 
space the second last sgRNA by a different tRNA sequence and thereby assemble the multiplexing cassette 
of multiple rounds. First, constructing the CRISPR/Cas9 vectors should be constructed as done in this study 
but the second last tRNA sequence switched to a different tRNA. Then the expression cassette should be 
amplified with appropriate overhangs to a second round of USER fusion. A second CRISPR/Cas9 vector is 
constructed in the same manner but with the second tRNA changed to reduce unspecific binding. A third 
PCR product is also to be constructed linking the two expression cassettes. This linker can also contain the 
first protospacer of the second expression cassette, as this is lost otherwise. The two expression cassettes 
can then be fused together by USER fusion. If the constructs are to problematic to fuse, the expression 
cassette amplified can be fused with PCR fragments with overhangs for gene deletion of another gene can 
be fused together. The vector can thereby be extended continuously. 
Changing the different tRNA as spacers can be time-consuming but could in the end be rewarding compared 
to multiple rounds of transformation and protoplastation. 
==PAGE:72==
Page 72 of 150 
 
If the CRISPR/Cas9 vector is constructed to induce more genetic alterations, the splicing cassette might be 
unstable due to direct-repeat recombination. To decrease this problem, other tRNAs could be used as 
spacers.  
4.3 USER fusion for assembling of vector 
Different CRISPR/Cas9 vectors, which were to delete protease genes, were constructed in this study. One 
plasmid was constructed to delete the genes prtA and prtB, while another was constructed to target the 
genes AN1426 and AN10198. A third plasmid contained sgRNA targeting the genes AN7159 and AN0747. For 
the construction of the CRISPR/Cas9 vector targeting the protease gene pepE and pepI, a correct assembled 
vector was never obtained. Various experimental strategies were employed, adjusting the PCR reactions and 
program.  When going over the primers for the amplification of the fragments for USER fusion, an error was 
discovered in primer B947. The designed primer was ACTATCAUATTGCATCATCCGTGAATCGAAC, which is 
identical to B942. The correct primer should have been GGAAATCCUATTGCATCATCCGTGAATCGAAC. A 
CRISPR/Cas9 vector targeting the gene pepE was therefore constructed instead.  
Besides from the construction of four CRISPR/Cas9 vectors, four overexpression vectors were constructed in 
this study through USER fusion of PCR fragments. 
4.4 Construction of protease deficient strains 
Both the CRISPR/Cas9 technology and sexual crossing of strains was used in this study to obtain strains 
which had different protease genes deleted. 
For transformations inducing gene deletions, the transformations were performed with and without the 
addition of oligos as GTS. When transforming with the CRISPR/Cas9 vectors pAC1313, pAC1350 and 
pAC1376, no transformants were observed when GTS was not added. For CRISPR/Cas9 vectors pAC1313 and 
pAC1376, transformants which had the desired gene deletions were obtained. For transformation using 
CRISPR/Cas9 vector pAC1250, transformants were observed even in the absence of GTS; as the strains, 
which was used for the transformations, are nkuAΔ, transformants, which had an induced DSB, but with no 
presence of oligo, should not be vital. This is because the oligo functions as template for the repair of the 
DSB which have to be repaired through HR. The NHEJ pathway is impaired by the nkuA deletion. As five 
transformants were observed with no addition of GTS compared to six transformants when GTS was added, 
there is an indication that the DSBs were not induced as desired. For the given transformation, a deletion of 
prtA was obtained but not one of prtB. The protospacers designed for deletion of prtB is not effective 
enough to induce the required DSB. This can be due to not effective enough formation of the Cas9 complex 
consisting of the Cas9 nuclease and the sgRNA. It can also be the translocation to the specific locus which is 
the issue or that the Cas9 nuclease does not induce the break. 
The oligos, which were used as GTS, had a length of 30 bp except for the oligo for the deletion of the gene 
prtB. This oligo had a length of 40 bp. From the transformations in this study, it is evident that an oligo of 30 
bp is long enough to be efficient as template for HR. 
For the sexual crossing of the strains LJ1 and LJ2, cross colonies from ascospores with various genotypes

also been restored through HR. This is also the case for cross 12, which produced green colonies but needed

uracil. The gene yA therefore has to have been restored through HR and the cross has obtained chromosome 
II from strain LJ2. Cross 18 has the genes prtA and AN1426 deleted but not AN10198. As the genes AN1426 
and AN10198 are located on the same chromosome, the gene AN10198 has been reintroduced though HR.

4.5 Model proteins for overexpression vectors 
For the assessment of the effect of the deletions of protease, various model proteins were chosen for 
reporter assays. The proteins which were chosen was BxlA, a β-xylosidase, AN5463, an LPMO with starch 
binding sites and mRFP. His-tags were also chosen to be fused to the proteins. Both BxlA and AN5463 
contains a signal peptide. The signal peptide from AN5463 was chosen to be fused to mRFP as well to locate 
the mRFP to the extracellular matrix. In this study, the protein BxlA, with and without His-tag, AN5463 and 
mRFP, with and without His-tag, were successfully amplified with appropriate overhangs for USER fusion 
through PCR. Overexpression vectors, which expresses these model proteins, were successfully constructed 
and could be linearized and delivered to the fungal cells. However, it was not possible to amplify AN5463 
with His-tag, mRFP with signal peptide and mRFP with His-tag and signal peptide. The PCR, program which 
was used, included a gradient temperature step in order to test various annealing temperatures for 
amplification of AN5463 with His-tag and mRFP with signal peptide. These were also run with varying primer 
concentration. However, the desired PCR fragments were not obtained. When the His-tag or signal peptide 
is added to the primers, the length of the primer is increased but only a fraction of the primer anneals to the 
template. The affinity of the primer is therefore lowered. One way to increase the affinity could be by 
increasing the part of the primer which anneals to the template to increase to affinity of the primer. This can 
create another issue with the length of the primer. 
For the constructed overexpression vectors, the plasmids were linearized and delivered to the fungal cells. 
Tissue PCR was used for verification of the transformants. The primers, which were used, annealed in the 
two target sites. The tissue PCR only revealed wild type PCR products and not a larger PCR product, as would 
be present if the overexpression cassette was integrated. However, the strains used in this study are nkuAΔ,  
and random integration of the overexpression cassette through NHEJ is not possible. The strains, which were 
included in the overexpression study, were pyrG89 and therefore required the overexpression cassette to 
survive as it included the pyrG gene. The reason for the presence of wild type PCR product is that the fungal 
cells can be multinucleate and there therefore are genomic DNA present which may not have the 
overexpression cassette integrated. However, as the transformant survive without the supplement of uridine 
and uracil, the overexpression cassette must be integrated. The reason for the absence of a PCR product 
which is larger, is the size of the PCR product. The resulting PCR product if the overexpression cassette is 
integrated is large (>6 kb) and is therefore hard to obtain. The transformants should therefore be further re-
streaked on solid MM plates to favour the cells with the overexpression cassette integrated. A different 
primer set should also be employed. The primers should be designed so one primer anneals outside the 
integration site and one primer anneals inside the overexpression cassette. The primers should be designed 
==PAGE:74==
Page 74 of 150 
 
so that the resulting PCR product is not too large. If the overexpression cassette is integrated correctly, a PCR 
product of the expected length would be visible on the gel when running a gel electrophoresis. If the 
overexpression cassette is not integrated correctly, no PCR product would be obtained. 
4.6 Characterization of constructed strains 
Some of the strains, which were constructed in this study, were characterized through phenotypical studies 
and through micro-fermentations in order to obtain the specific growth rate of the strains.  
The specific growth rates were determined though the usage of the BioLector® I micro-fermentation system. 
Pre-cultures were inoculated by adding fungal tissue from a colony grown on solid MM plate. To uniform the 
inoculum, spore suspensions could be prepared and used for inoculation of the microtiter plate used in the 
BioLector® I system. The data obtained was also transformed in order to reduce noise and remove outliers as 
the data otherwise would be inconclusive. Optimisation of the BioLector® I system for cultivation of A. 
nidulans strains should be explored. The average and standard deviation is also based on few replicates, 
more experiments should be run to lower the standard deviation and widen the pool by which it is 
calculated. 
When comparing the results of the phenotype study and the specific growth rates, the strain LJ1 generated a 
colony larger than the reference strain NID108. However, the cultivation of the strains revealed that the 
strain LJ1 had a lower specific growth rate than its reference strain NID108. This can be due to a difference in 
the number of spores used for inoculation of the solid MM plates. If more spores are present, the branching 
of the fungal colony will happen faster. The confidence interval for the two averages also revealed that it 
could not be rejected that the average specific growth rate of strain LJ1 is higher than for reference strain 
NID108. 
For the strain LJ3, a specific growth rate which was lower than the reference strain was obtained. When 
cultivated together, the two strains developed in the same manner regarding the colony size. However, 
other colonies were present on the solid plate, restricting the growth of the colony by strain NID108. 
Comparing the strains LJ1 and LJ3, the specific growth rate of strain LJ1 was higher than that of the strain 
LJ3. When the phenotypical traits were examined, it was observed that the colony generated by the strain 
LJ1 was larger. It should be noted that the growth of the colonies can be restricted due to the presence of 
other colonies, which were observed on day 5.  
The specific growth rate of cross 12 was higher than that of the reference strain NID108 when the cut-off for 
the R2-value was set to be equal to or higher than 0.9. However, only one replicate was then included. 
Lowering the cut-off resulted in the inclusion of another replicate, which had a lower specific growth rate. 
However, the confidence interval for cross 12 was also so wide that negative values would be included.  
For the strains, which required a supplement of arginine, only two replicates were included when the cut-off 
for the R2-value was set to 0.9. All strains were included when the cut-off was lowered. By comparing the 
confidence intervals from Table 26, it cannot be rejected that cross 15 and 20 have a higher specific growth 
rate than the reference strain NID109. When comparing the size of the colonies when inoculated together, 
the strain NID109 generated a larger colony than cross 15 and cross 20 and one of the same size compared 
to strain LJ2.  
For the strains, which did not have growth requirements, the average specific growth rate of cross 18 was 
higher than the reference strain cross 19 even when the confidence intervals are taken into consideration. 
All replicates were included for cross 18 and two replicates were included for cross 19 when the criterium for 
the R2-value was set to at least 0.9. When inoculated together, the colonies produced were of the same size. 
Another colony was present on the solid plate, which could restrict the growth of cross 18.

5. Conclusion 
The aim of this study was to create a new protein production host using A. nidulans. Proteases poses as a 
problem for the production of heterologous proteins as they can degrade the final protein product. 
In this study, various extracellular proteases were identified in A. nidulans through bioinformatical research 
of proteases, which are known to be problematic for heterologous protein production in other strains or 
proteases, which are known to be secreted. The proteases were used as input for BLASTp searches and 
based on these, eight different protease genes were chosen for gene deletion. 
By employing the CRISPR/Cas9 system and by sexual crossing of two constructed A. nidulans strains, 
different protease deficient strains were obtained. The generated strains were characterized through a 
phenotypical study and cultivation using BioLector® I micro-fermentation system to assess the specific 
growth rate.  
To further assess the effect of the deletion of the protease genes, overexpression vectors were also 
constructed. The overexpression vectors expressed various reporter proteins; either a β-xylosidase, an LPMO 
or mRFP. The affinity tag His-tag was also fused to some of the proteins. The overexpression contained 
target sequences for IS4 in A. nidulans (Unpublished data). The transformant obtained were not finally 
verified. 
The work represented in this study proves that gene deletions can be shared by two A. nidulans strains and 
is the first step towards the production of an improved protein production host. However, further work is 
needed for establishment of an improved protein production host using A. nidulans.

6. Further perspectives  
This study resulted in the generation of different protease deficient strains. But various work has to be 
completed in the future in order to achieve the final A. nidulans strain optimised for protein production. The 
next steps of the study will be represented in this section. 
Even though different CRISPR/Cas9 vectors targeting protease genes were constructed in this study, one 
plasmid was not obtained; one targeting both protease genes pepE and pepI. After reviewing the primers, an 
error in one primer was discovered. The next step would be to order a new primer and perform the PCR 
again to obtain the desired fragments. A new CRISPR/Cas9 vector can then be constructed, containing the 
four protospacers for the deletion of the genes pepE and pepI. The strain LJ1 should be transformed with the 
constructed CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid to obtain a quadruple protease deletion strain. A primer set verification of 
deletion of the gene pepI should also be designed as performed previously for the other genes. 
For the transformation of strain NID109 with CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid pAC1250, transformants were observed 
in the absence of GTS. The desired DSBs are therefore not induced. As the transformants, which were 
supplemented with GTS, had the deletion of prtA, the protospacers of prtB must therefore be the issue. The 
protospacers for this gene should therefore be changed. To elucidate if both protospacers have to be 
altered, two new CRISPR/Cas9 vectors should be constructed, each carrying one protospacer. New oligos 
should also be designed for GTS. The new vectors are transformed into strain NID108 with and without 
supplement of GTS. If the transformants can survive without the supplement of GTS, the protospacer should 
be altered.  
Transformation of strain LJ2 was also performed with CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid pAC1350. Only one transformant 
was obtained and did not have the required gene deletions. The transformation should therefore be 
performed again. However, the strain to be used should be the strain generated after altering the 
protospacers targeting prtB. A quadruple protease deletion strain can thereby be obtained. 
When two A. nidulans strains have been obtained, each carrying four protease gene deletion, one with 
prtAΔ, prtBΔ, AN7159Δ and AN0747Δ and one with AN10198Δ, AN1426Δ, pepEΔ and pepIΔ, the two strains 
should be sexually crossed to generate cleistothecia. The ascospores from the cleistothecia should then be 
plated out to attain cross colonies with all eight proteases deleted. The cross colony which should be chosen 
for further work should require uridine and uracil for growth. This is to allow for selection of transformants 
with integrated overexpression cassettes after transformation. New primers should be designed for the 
amplification of AN5463 with His-tag, mRFP with signal peptide and mRFP with signal peptide and His-tag so 
the last overexpression vectors can be constructed and transformed into the reference strain NID108. The 
cross colony, which have all eight protease genes deleted, can then be sexually crossed with the reference 
strain NID108 which have the different overexpression cassettes integrated to obtain new cross colonies 
which both are protease deficient and have the overexpression cassette integrated. The overexpression 
strains should then be cultivated using the BioLector® I micro-fermentation system.  
The micro-fermentations should be optimised for cultivation of A. nidulans. This can be done by trying 
different working volumes in the wells of the microtiter plate, different agitation speeds, inoculating using 
spore suspensions and trying different gains for the light scattering filter and the RFP filter. The filamentous 
nature of the fungus can create noise in the light scattering data. Dispersed morphology of the filamentous 
fungus when submerged cultivated is preferred when using light scattering to monitor the cell density. The 
dispersed morphology can be achieved by increasing inoculum size, changing the process pH and using 
additives such as anionic polymeric additives. Anionic polymeric additives have been proven effective in 
reducing agglomeration for spores and hyphae. This resulted in reduction of pellet formation (Knudsen et al. 
2015). Besides from optimisation of the micro-fermentation system, reference strains of the same colour as 
==PAGE:78==
Page 78 of 150 
 
the strains should be constructed. 
After cultivation, the overexpression strains expressing the β-xylosidase BxlA should be analysed though an 
enzyme assay. Dilokpimol et al. (2011) performed an assay be applying the following steps: BxlA (20-150 nM; 
50 mM sodium acetate pH5.2) hydrolysed pNPX in a 50 µL reaction mixture. The hydrolysation was stopped 
by addition of 1 M Na2CO3. The amount of pNP was quantified spectrophotometrically at 410 nm. 0.05-0.5 
mM pNP should be used as standard. For the purification of BxlA with His-tag, the fermentation culture has 
to be centrifuged to pellet the cells. The supernatant has to be concentrated with buffer exchange to 10 mM 
MES pH 6.0 (Pellicon tangential flow filtration systems, nominal molecular weight limit (NMWL) 10 kDa; 
Millipore, Billerica, MA) and applied to a 6 mL Resource Q column (GE healthcare, Uppsala, Sweden) which 
has to be equilibrated in the same buffer. The column should then be washed with 37.5 mM NaCl in same 
buffer and the proteins can then be eluted by a linear 37.5-325 mM NaCl gradient with a flowrate of 2 
mL/min (Dilokpimol et al. 2011). A one-step purification method has been developed for purification of His-
tagged RFP. Yang et al. (2015) designed Ni2+-functionalized FeO4@Polydopamine magnetic particles and 
synthesized the particles by in situ coating of magnetic nanoparticles with polydopamine followed by 
conjugation of Ni2+-ions to polydopamine film. The nanoparticles should be ultrasonically dispersed in 
phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) buffer (pH 7.4, 20 mM) before cell lysate containing RFP with His-tag is be 
added to the mix. The mix should be incubated for 5 min at room temperature and the solution should be 
separated by a magnet. 1 mL of 10 mM imidazole in PBS buffer should be added to remove non-specifically 
bound proteins. The RFP with His-tag can then be eluted by addition of 1 mL of 300 mM imidazole in PBS 
buffer (Yang et al. 2015). The activity of RFP can be measured through the filters of the BioLector® I or by 
spectrophotometrically measurements of purified fermentation cultures.  
Depending on the stability of the LPMO AN5463, the protein can either be purified by IMAC, anion exchange 
chromatography (as for BxlA) or by usage of magnetic particles (as for RFP). An assay has to be developed in 
order to measure the activity of the LPMO. As the protein has predicted starch binding sites, one possible 
assay could be a β-amylase assay. Leggio et al. (2015) reported that starch-active LPMO degrade starch into 
aldonic acid-terminated malto-oligosaccharides. These can serve as substrates for β-amylase for degradation 
into maltose and thereby be employed in a β-amylase assay, which are commercially available (Lo Leggio et 
al. 2015; Megazyme 2018).   
The expression of these reporter proteins can then be compared between the reference strain NID108 and 
the constructed strain, which have the eight chosen proteases deleted. After initial characterization of the 
constructed strains in microbiorectors, the fermentations can be scaled up to first shake flasks followed by 
larger bioreactors. The scale up should be of the strains, which shows an increase in protein production, 
based on the different assays.

8.10 R code for transformation of light scattering data from BioLector® I microbioreactor 
The light scattering data obtained from cultivation using the BioLector® I micro-fermentation system was 
transformed to reduce noise and remove outliers. The data was transformed using an R code constructed by 
Associate Professor Christopher Workman. The R code uses a package, which has been developed by 
Christopher Workman. 
 
#Script for transforming BioLector® I output data 
#Required packages are loaded into the current session.  
library(htmf) 
library(RColorBrewer) 
library(MASS) 
 
#Set working directory to location of raw data files  
setwd("~/Location/Folder/Sub-location/") 
 
#Show files in working directory 
dir() 
#csvFiles is the output file from the BioLector® I in csv format 
csvFiles = "BioLector® I_data_output.csv" 
#desfiles is a table with information on each well.  
desFiles = "Design_table.txt" 
#Outfiles are the new files created by the script, that contains the processed data.  
#One file is created for each filter in the BioLector® I; e.g. Biomass and RFP 
outfile1 = "Outfile_Biomass.txt" 
 
#Defining values for baseline and outlier correction  
baselineThr <- 3 
outliersThr <- 5 
 
pr = read_BioLector® I2(file=csvFiles, designfile = desFiles) 
pInt <- plate_intervals(pr) 
print(pInt) 
 
#The following determines the time window for the output data 
==PAGE:116==
Page 116 of 150 
 
#Defining the end time-point (in hours) 
tMax = 72 
#Trim data to this time window (in hours) 
prs <- sliceByTimeIntervals(pr, intervals = list(c(2, tMax))) 
 
#Correct baseline and remove outliers  
prsn = plate_normalize(x=prs, filtersets="Biomass", spar=0.6, 
                            valign=TRUE, targetTime=2, targetAmp=0.5, 
                            baseline=FALSE, baselineThr=baselineThr, 
                            outliers=TRUE, outliersThr=outliersThr)
