Abstract
Concerns about the negative environmental impact and the rise in waste generation have been
gaining importance in the current society strongly marked by an active and intense industrial and
technological development. Therefore, the management and optimization of the different resources
have caused increased interest in governments and industries which are hardly working to reduce the
exploitation of the different resources and the implementation of more sustainable and eco-friendly
production systems.
One of the major approach of this sustainable awareness is focus on the wastewater efﬂuents
produced by the big factories and industries which discharge diary huge amounts of pollutants and
contaminants into the environment. A green alternative based on the use of microalgae to treat
industrial water coming from the brewery industry and reduce the polluted charge of it is developed
during this work.
The selection of a brewery efﬂuent to treat is a part of the hypothesis, as its high content on
organic pollutants, P and N mainly resulted from the brewing process turns the residual brewery
efﬂuent a suitable media for microalgae growth. Assuming this capacity of microalgae to absorb the
pollutants from the nutrient-rich wastewater at the same time that grow becoming useful and versatil
biomass, this project discuss the potential of the use of microalgae as wastewater treatment method.
3 different microalgae species Chlorella vulgaris, Chlamydomonas reinhardtii and Scenedesmus
obliquus were ﬁrstly investigated in terms on nutrient removal and biomass production in growth
media to furtherly continue with experiments in brewery wastewater with Chlorella vulgaris as
main target microorganism, in which different growth conditions (variation in light wavelength,
light intervals, inoculum growth phase of the cells, pH and N/P ratio) were evaluated.
Eminent nutrient removal proﬁles were reported after 48h of cultivation for the majority of the
conditions tested, achieving the highest values for ortophosphate removal of 89,3% for the mi-
croalgae cells inoculated in log phase in WW with a N/P ratio of 5:1. IN removal was remarkable
high in cells cultivated under a combination of red and white (R+W)LED light with light cycles
of 12h light which report assimilation values of 83%. COD reduction was also outsanding for
this condition (81,18% of removal), even the best COD assimilation was carried out by Chlorella
vulgaris cultivated under heterotrophic conditions in dark environment. Algae was able also to
successfully growth under the different conditions tested achieving a maximum productivity of
1,20 x 107 cell/mL.d growing in waste water with an N/P ratio of 8:1. The optimal µmax value
( was obtained by cells inoculated in decline phase in 3:1 N/P ratio and under 12h white light

Introduction
The rapid industrial and technological development of the current society, as well as the
growing of human population has generated an important environmental impact, produced
by the exploitation of the different resources and the increasing on waste generation.
This situation starts gaining in prominence as resource scarcity and climate change place
increasing pressure on all life on earth. Among all these global concerns water management
and consumption is one of the biggest matters affecting the continuous stress on the Earth’s
resources [4]. Water, as an essential natural resource is needed for the development of life
and almost all the human activities. It is at ﬁrst place crucial as a nutrient to develop all
the physiological functions of the organism making up 60% of the human body. Apart
from that, water is massively used as raw material to cover all the daily mankind’s needs
including agricultural, industrial, household, recreational and environmental activities [4].
In fact, according to the data reported by the United Nations Environment Program (UNEP)
in 2008, the industrial sector is the second largest user of freshwater after agriculture in the
world [5]. Therefore, the scarcity of water, sped up during recent years due to the rapid
and modern development of the global industry and society, is becoming one of the biggest
concerns of today´s society. Aside from the increase in the water demand also water
pollution, specially inherited by human activities appears as main factor playing a part in
this existent water crisis. In the ﬁrst instance, all the polluted effects of the wastewater
coming from different sources (agriculture, domestic uses, industries,...) are generating
environmental damages when are directly discharged to natural habitats as eutrophication
of freshwater bodies, changes in aquatic habitats or the death of marine species and life
[6].
Is therefore a clear need to reduce the polluted charge of the wastewater streams before
their release in the environment. Indeed, a right decision and design of a tertiary water
treatment applied to the polluted efﬂuents can lead to achieve an appropriate quality
of the untreated water, reducing the environmental impact and allowing the use of the
contaminated streams as an alternative water resource [7]. Also, as a sustainable approach
is promoted to recover apart from the water itself, other materials and functions from the
WWT (Waste Water Treatments) as energy (methane from anaerobic digestion), materials

2.1
Motivation for study
In the theme of the topic presented above, this project is designed to treat residual water
and remove high-value products from a speciﬁc kind of wastewater: brewery wastewater
(BWW). This speciﬁc kind of water as result from the fermentation step in the brewery
production process is fully charged of environmental pollutants including yeast surplus
or spent grains and high content of other residues as phosphorous or nitrogen. It also
contains a high amount of oxidisable pollutants and other contaminants coming from the
CIP (clenaing in place) operations.
The characteristics of this wastewater motivate the development of a biological cleaning
technology based on microalgae treatment. The composition of the resulting brewery
wastewater includes in general high chemical oxygen demand (COD), nitrogen and phos-
phorus turning the residual liquid from the beer production a suitable media for microalgae
growth. The potential of these microorganisms, which have been exponentially spreading

Microalgae are microscopic photosynthetic algae typically found in a huge range of aquatic
habitats, including lakes, pounds, rivers, oceans, and even wastewater.They are unicellular
species which can range from a few micrometers to a few hundred. They can grow in
extreme conditions as they can tolerate a wide range of temperatures, salinity and pH

In general microalgae are photosynthetic and autotrophes organisms, outstanding by

Autotrophic metabolism
Autotrophic algae, as mentioned before uses photosynthesis as their key survival mode.
The photosynthesis process transforms the light energy into chemical energy, converting
CO2 and water into carbohydrates and oxygen. The process take place in the chloroplasts
through the photosynthetic pigments which are responsible of the light absorption (light-
phase) and the following excitation of the electrons which will trigger a cascade of chemical
reactions ending up with the production of ATP and re-dox power. This energy produced in
the light-dependent stage is used in the subsequent sequence of light independent reactions
to incorporate CO2 into carbohydrates via Calvin cycle pathway (dark phase).
Heterotrophic metabolism
Heterotrophic algae are non-photosynthetic and therefore, require organic matter to replace
light as the substrate and energy source for their growth [26]. The chemical energy is
obtained in this case through respiration from glucose and other carbonated compounds.
Glycolysis, tricarboxylic acid cycle (TCA), glyoxylate cycle and the pentose phosphate
pathway (PPP) are the main pathways involved in the metabolism route, taking place in
this case in the cytosol and mitochondria [27]. The glucose is ﬁrstly oxidized to a 3C
compound (pyruvate) which is the initial molecule of the TCA cycle, the central important
chemical reaction for carbohydrate, lipid and protein metabolism. The energy needed

of ATP and re-dox precursors (NADH). An alternative route to break-down the glucose,

for nucleotides, nucleics acids and amino acid synthesis. The energy is accumulated in

3.3.1.3
P assimilation
The phosphorus needed for microalgae metabolism is uptaken from the dissolved organic
phosphorus presented in aquatic environments and subsequently transformed to use as
building block in nucleic acids, lipid membranes and energy through complex metabolic
and biodynamic routes on the cells. Phosphate is widely accepted to be the preferred
form of phosphorus for growth. Phosphate uptake is controlled by phosphate transporters,
which drive the afﬁnity and the rate of phosphate assimilation. The kinetics of phosphate
uptake behave with Michaelis-Menten kinetics which varies depending on several factors
as (1) the energy steps needed for the transport, (2) the nutritional history of the cell, (3)
the P bioavailavility per cell, (4) the cell quota and (5) the experimental substrate. After
uptake, the majority of transported phosphate is stored as polyphosphate (PolyP) in form
of osmotically inert granules, inorganic chains or in nucleotides molecules (ATP). 2 basic
processes have been describing in microalgae to explain the phosphorus dynamics.
• In environments where the phosphate is in excess relative to other resource as
nitrogen, the model follow by algae is known as luxury uptake. Algae are able to
store this excess of phosphate in form of polyphosphate for future utilization.
• The cells can also follow an over-plus response in environments where are periods
with lack of phosphate. In the moment when the phosphate becomes available algae
can store enough amounts of polyphosphate to survive in the periods where they are
deprived of this nutrient.

Successful microalgae cultivation requires speciﬁc environmental conditions which will

3.3.2.1
Light
Light is the basic energy source for phototrophic algae. They need a speciﬁc duration of
light/dark periods for performing the algal photosynthesis reactions with the formation of
ATP and NADPH molecules, which drive the dark phases of the photosyntesis that produce
carbon skeletons [21].
Therefore, the availability and intensity of light is one of the key parameters affecting the
successful growth of microalgae, having a direct relationship until reach a saturation point,
where the growth rate is the maximum attainable [21].Beyond this point, which varies
from specie to specie, any rise in the light intensity will have any constructive effect on
microorganism growth. This threshold point is about 20% of solar light intensity and a
big excess over this point can lead to cell damages by deteriorating the light receptors
and thereby, decreasing the photosynthetic rate and productivity (photo-inhibition) [23].
Depending on the source, the light intensity received on the cells can changes. Therefore,
the natural light provides radiation from which only 40% of the total received is accessible
for the chloroplasts and photosyntesis mechanisms. This photosintetically active radiation
(PAR), corresponds with a wavelength between 400-700 nm. If the light is artiﬁcial, the
best is the light from LED tubes, which emit 98% of the light with wavelength around

Temperature
Temperature is one of the factors affecting the most for the microalgae growth. The optimal
temperature for microalgae is normally around 20 to 25°C, although some species can
growth in extreme temperature conditions [21], [31]. In general higher temperatures help
positively to the growth curve increasing the growth rate until an optimal level after which
growth declines. Normally temperatures above this optimal value may kill the algal cells
and temperatures below the optimal range may not, except for freezing conditions [23].
In this way some reviews ﬁx the highest temperature value to allow cell growing around
35°C, while a range of temperature above 16°C, usually implies a decline in the growth
factor [31]. According to some reviews result’s, temperature and light conditions should
be coordinated as higher values in loss biomass were registered at high temperature during
dark periods.Therefore an optimal culture which minimizes the biomass losses will be
obtained from high temperatures in lightly periods with a reduction in the temperature
when decreasing the light [23].

The CO2 needed for the algal biomass is generally blended with air in aerated tanks or

3.3.4.1
Acuiculture and animal nutrition
Even the cultivation of microalgae as food source started only a few decades ago, many
species of green algae have been utilized as food from ancient time. In particular nowadays
microalgae constitute the basis of the aquaculture nutrition, as they are considered the ﬁrst
link of the natural food chain, being the main feed source for many species of mollusks,
larvae, crustaceans and ﬁsh. In aquaculture they help in addition keeping the quality of the
water, being also the food source for zooplankton production. In farming and domestics
animals, microalgae are frequently added in the main meal as a supplement which improve
the characteristics and physiology of the animals, giving positive effects in their health
and constitution as stronger and healthier skin, higher disease resistance, antiviral and
antibacterial action, improvements in reproductive functions or better food assimilation as
well as an easier control of the weight [38].
3.3.4.2
Biofuels
The production of biofuels is a recent emerging topic in research which takes more and
more territory in the current industry due to the global environmental concerns and the
social and legislative pressure to make more sustainable the industries and factories.
However, the majority of commercially available biofuels are derived from food crops and
other food sources as starch or sugar cane, which make them not suitable from a sustainable
point of view because they can be used to fulﬁll the feeding requirements demanded by
the recent boom in world population [36]. Microalgae appears as an alternative of second-
generation biofuel system which apart of be more sustainable, present more advantages as
their higher growth or the requirements of land areas for the production. In fact, microalgae
are reported to produce 15-300 times more oil for biodisel production than traditional
crops on area basis and the cultivation can be carried out on marginal land, reducing the
competition for land and openining up new economic opportunities. Besides, the culture
of microalgae results in less waste and pollution than higher plants [23].
In spite of all these positive and promising aspects of the use of microalgaes as biofuels
production system, nowadays there is no any method industrially applied which use these
microorganisms for biofuel generation. Researches conclude that the economics of algae

3.3.4.3
Human nutrition
The high protein content and essential amino acids make microalgae a good supplement or
fortiﬁcation for malnourished children and adults. They are used as single cell proteins

and the predicted insufﬁcient protein supply [21]. Although the ancient consumption of

to undergo of toxicological test to prove they are harmlessness for human consumption.
Withal, some of them are already commercialised mainly as healthy food, food supplement
or as food additive in the Asiatic countries. They are commonly sold in form of tablets,
capsules, powder or liquid and beneﬁt effects of their ingestion were already demonstrated
with a daily intake below 20 g of algae [38].

Microalgae represent a very large, relatively unexploited reservoir of novel compounds,

novel and biochemically active natural products are isolated, the study of these secondary

are not implemented yet in pharmaceutical market due to the relatively low yield of these

3.4
Microalgae for Wastewater Treatment
The culture of microalgae in industrial polluted efﬂuents as wastewater treatment system
represent a low-cost, practical and environment-friendly alternative when compared to
the conventional processes presented in section 3.2. Algae´s nutritional and chemical
requirements offer promising opportunities for advanced bioremediation, being considered
as prospective candidate for tertiary wastewater method, as it has been demonstrated
than microalgae systems can reduce the pollutants at concentrations encountered after the
secondary treatment. Indeed, the potential of these microorganism by removing inorganic
nitrogen and phosphorus from wastewater is well documented and considered a sustainable
approach to nutrient polishing.
The attraction of microalgae as wastewater treatment alternative includes an extra proﬁt
due to the added beneﬁts they offer when they grow as biomass, as they are considered
a source of a wide range of added value products as we show in previous sessions. The
combination of the desired nutrient features being able to meet low nutrient discharges
and the generation of added value components give an extra interest compared to the other
emergent and novel wastewater alternatives.
Specially, due to its nutritional characteristics and composition brewing wastewater seems
to be a suitable growth media for some microalgae species. Therefore, nitrogen, phosphorus
and other nutrients presented in this wastewater are adequately absorbed by microalgae
for their growth. They also play an interesting role by establishing beneﬁcial relationships
with bacteria present in the efﬂuent.
Until recently, despite of the successful results of this biological alternative as tertiary
treatment, the application of microalgae in wastewater treatment had only been restricted
to the laboratory. The different methods and the wide range of species generate different
challenges to be resolved when applying the culture of microalgae as advanced wastewater
treatment. However, there are already many authors demonstrating the evidence of success
of these microorganisms in the nutrient removal. Like so, Lutzu et al. [42] report in
their publication a removal of more than 99% of both nitrogen (N) and phosphorous
(P) from brewery wastewater within one week by using the microalgae Scenedesmus
dimorphus. Also, Ferreira et al. [43] exhibit in their report the ability of Scenedesmus

wastewaster. Similarly, Subramaniyam et al. [44] cultivated Chlorella sp. in brewery

completely with substantial growth of the microalgae. With the same microalgae specie
Travieso et al. [45] have treated varied distillery efﬂuents, showing a reduction of more

Other microalgae species were also successfully tested in brewery wastewater for different

3.5.0.1
Chlorella vulgaris
It is a green unicellular microalgae with eukaryotic structure. It is belong to the phylum
Chlorophyta. It is spherical in shape without ﬂagella and diameters between 2 and 10 µm.
It is characteristic by their green colour given by the photosynthetic pigments produced in
their chloroplast, mainly chlorophyll-a and chlorofyll-b. It multiplies rapidly through pho-
tosynthesis with an optimal temperature of 20°C and reporting the maximum growth rate
and biomass productivities at pH 9–10. This algae can follow an autotrophic, heterotrophic
or mixotrophic growth [47]. Chlorella vulgaris is famous nowadays by its potential as
source of food and energy because the photosynthetic efﬁciency of this algae can reach
higher results compared with other highly efﬁcient food crops as sugar cane. It is also a
promising source of bioenergy, in agrochemical applications and as wastewater treatment.

Scenedesmus obliquus
It is an unicellular and eukariotic green algae which grow in freshwater with a high degree

studied as model organism due to its easy cultivation and ability to manipulate its genetics.
They can follow a photoautotrophic metabolism in the presence of light or they can growth
heterotrophically with an external supply of organic carbon. Commercially, this algae is
interesting in the pharmaceutical industry as well as a biofuel producer, specially famous
by their success producing hydrogen [50].

Total Suspended Solid (TSS) method was used to set up a similar initial cell concentration
to inoculate the experiments when working with more than one specie at the time. As the
cell size differs from one specie to the other, the cell density is not an accurate methodology
to make the initial values comparable.
Therefore, the algae were measured in terms of dry-weight and the mass was the parameter
equalized for the initial inoculum. To make it more reasonable, an assumption was made,
supposing that all the inorganic matter is negligible, and therefore the microalgae mass
will theoretically be equal to TSS.
The procedure starts by heating microﬁber ﬁlters in an oven during two hours at 105°C,
to remove the possible moisture generated by the air contact. Afterwards, the ﬁlters were
cooling down for 30 to 60 min. into a vacuum desiccator, which offers a suitable dry
environment to avoid the remoisturing of the ﬁlters. When dried, the ﬁlters were weight
again using a 4 decimals precision balance. Subsequently, a sample of 1 to 3 mL of the
cells resulted from the rinsed up step after growing (see Section 4.2.1) were added into the
TSS ﬁlters using a vacuum pump. Right after, they were put again into the oven for another
2 hours at 105°C followed the same step than before: desiccator and weight, obtaining now

Conclusion
So far, all data collected during the whole research work evidence the potential of
the 3 microalgae species evaluated (Chlorella vulgaris,Chlamydomonas reinhardtii and
Scenedesmus obliquus) on nutrient removal (N, P and COD) when cultivating them in their
respective growth media.
The further evaluation made in brewery waste water (from Jacobsen´s Brewery), carried
out exclusively with Chlorella vulgaris also shows satisfactory results to further implement
microalgae as tertiary waste water treatment.
From the compilation of all the data and discussion made in Section 5, the main conclusions
withdrawn are mentioned below:
• Optimal inoculum growth phase Reactors inoculated with cells in log phase reach
the highest and faster IN and COD removal attaining NRE values of 84 and 59%
respectively just 48hours after the inoculation. Decline phase inoculum is concluded
to be the optimal removing 74% of PO3−
4
from the efﬂuent on day 2 after inoculation.
• Optimal light conditions All the light exposure conditions chosen in this project
resulted to be signiﬁcantly similar in terms of IN removal, which makes no possible
to select an optimal light condition. In terms of ortophosphate assimilation both
light combinations tested (R+W and R+W+B) found to be the most quickly and
with higher assimilation capacity with an insigniﬁcantly preference of R+W which
removes 77% of PO3−
4
after 2 days. Both achieve the NRE maximum value of
87% at the end of the experiment. Heterotrophic conditions associated to darkness
cultivation, were have been reported as the worst option for PO3−
4
assimilation while
the best operation mode for COD removal.
• Optimal N/P ratio 5:1 was reported to be the best N/P ratio removing ortophosphate,
as well as the optimal ratio for COD removal. In this sense already in day 2 the
microalgae cells growing in this ratio can remove 83,5% of PO3−
4
and 78,5% of
COD. In COD removal 8:1 shows insigniﬁcant lower NRE values, matching the
results reported in literature. In contrast the third ratio tested (3:1) have been reported
as the optimal for IN removal, assimilating 89% of it after 4 days.

• No effect of pH variations The pH alterations caused by the biomass growth does
not affect to either nutrient removal or biomass production.
• Optimal biomass productivity and growth rate Within all the conditions tested
in terms of maximum growth rate, the best is obtained by the cells inoculated in
decline phase (µmax = 0,77d−1). From the test run in log phase, the ratio 8:1 has the
following highest µmax value of 0,68d−1, which is also the one giving the highest
productivity of 1,20x107 cell/mL.d. In terms of cell concentration the maximum

All in all, the results obtained in this work point out the use of microalgae as a promising
alternative to be further investigated as WWT. Indeed, some of the ﬁnal nutrient concen-

and regulations. Therefore, even some challenges and future development on the topic are
needed, the outcome of the project make the use of microalgae in BWW as an attractive
substitute to the current waste water treatments, which have been shown to achieve pollu-
tants removal values in BWW similar to the existent methods. Besides, the sustainable
and environmental-friendly approach shown by the use of these microorganisms open an
area of considerable opportunities which could be the starting point for a future socially
equitable and green cleaning model to implement at further industrial levels combating the
current and huge problem of water pollution and its mankind’s negative impacts.

Future work and perspectives
The results obtained in this work open a wide range of future research topics that can
be investigated, with the aim to extend and improve the current project. Some of the
alternatives suggested as future work are presented and discussed below:
• Shorter sample taking and analysis. As explained in Section 5.3. the most of
the nutrient removal, specially for IN and ortophosphate is carried out within the
ﬁrst 48 hours of cultivation. Therefore, an evaluation of the nutrient assimilation by
microalgae during the ﬁrst 2 days of cultivation should be carried out to better select
the optimal growth conditions to get the fastest and most efﬁcient nutrient removal.
• Study of the nutrient removal of the other 2 species mentioned in the work. Due
to the short time of the project, only the microalgae Chlorella vulgaris was examined
in terms of biomass production and nutrient removal into BWW. Nevertheless,
according to the results presented in Section 5.2, the other 2 species evaluated have
also a great potential as waste water tertiary treatment. Similar experiments than the
ones carried out in this work could be done for the other 2 microalgae.
• Further study of the variables modiﬁed and other essential parameters. The
project was design according to the research time and the material available. Nev-
ertheless further studies on the environmental factors evaluated can be done. A
bigger analysis on light conditions, trying continuos red light or exclusively blue
light as well as green or yellow LED light which have been also reported to be
favorable for microalgae growth and nutrient removal could be an idea. Also, a
deeper evaluation of the N/P ratios could be done, by trying higher ratios or by
modifying the P concentration instead of N. Also other external parameters as the
temperature effect or the light intensity could be tested.
• Microalgae applicability. A completion of the lipids, proteins and pigments anal-
ysis could help to obtain more speciﬁc properties of the microalgae cultures and
their possible applications, which could add economical advantages to the process
by revalorization of the microorganism in further products or industries.

Glosary
• Ammonia sttriping. Physicochemical process based on the principle of mass
transfer, by which wastewater in contact with air release ammonia gas, which comes
from the chemical transformation from ammonium dissolved ion as an effect of a
pH increase or temperature variation.
• Cleaning in Place. It is a method of industrial cleaning used to clean and disinfect
the interior surfaces of pipes, vessels, process equipment, ﬁlters and associated
ﬁttings, without disassembly of the equipment. It is often used in industries that
need frequent internal cleaning of their processes as: beverage; brewing; processed
foods; pharmaceutical; and cosmetics, assuring a high levels of hygiene through a
faster and less labor-intensive cleaning system.
• Eutrophication. Natural phenomena occuring in aquatic environment when a water
body becomes overly enriched with minerals and nutrients which induce excessive
growth of algae. This process may result in oxygen depletion of the water body.
• Tertiary waste water treatment. Final step of the cleaning process aplied to resid-
ual waste water to improve the efﬂuent quality before reused, recycled or discharged
to the environment. The treatment removes remaining inorganic pollutants, and
substances, such as the nitrogen and phosphorus.
