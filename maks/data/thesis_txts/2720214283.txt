This thesis is presented as the final project to obtain a MSc. In Aquatic Science & Technology. 
It has been developed over six months of work in collaboration with the Department of

together with the advice of the NGO Køge Bugt Stenrev (KBS). An optimal design for concrete

Abstract 
 
In Denmark, stone fishing was widely practiced, using the removed stones from the seabed 
for coastal protection projects and harbor constructions. This activity became a problem in 
many coastal areas, resulting in the destruction of natural stone reefs, which were very 
valuable to the local environment.  
 
Stone reefs can be re-built by putting back the stones or building artificial reef units with man-
made materials. Several materials have been tested and used over the years, concrete being 
one of them, which has shown great performances and different positive effects. 
 
In this thesis, state-of-the-art research of concrete usage in marine habitats, analyzing 
different mixture’s durability performance, ecological aspects, biological recruitment, and 
other benefits, was conducted. Overall shape complexity and surface textures on artificial 
reefs have been found to be of great relevance for the biological recruitment of the structure, 
thus, different designs were developed and compared. It was fundamental to understand the 
local environment, the reef purposes and to define the species that will interact with the reef, 
in order to select the most beneficial design and parameters. 
 
The final design will be considered for being used in KBS’s project. This NGO project aims to 
produce an approximate of 1000 m3 of concrete reef units and to locate them along the bay 
of Køge, which implies complying with certain guidelines and ecological requirements, both 
from local and regional authorities as from The Velux Foundations, the funding foundation of 
the project (The Velux Foundations, 2022). Therefore, an environmental impact assessment 
and a lifecycle analysis of the units should be conducted when implementing, and design 
optimizations shall be made according to the obtained results. 
 
Different production processes were screened according to practical and economical 
limitations of the project. Final design selection and modifications were made according to 
the production process selected. Sample units were casted in a smaller scale to analyze both 
the feasibility of casting the concrete in the desired shape and to run deployment tests. 
 
As the reef units are artificial, they should present more benefits than re-locating natural 
stones. According to previous similar projects, there is one main uncontrolled factor that 
could affect the efficiency of the reef, the final shape obtained, which varies due to 
interactions throughout the deployment process. The most likely-to-be-used deployment 
method is dumping the units from a ship, which will make impossible to manually locate the 
units in the desired position, thus, approximations of the shape and its efficiency were 
conducted.  
 
Several methods to analyze the final reef shapes from these deployment tests and to 
represent the efficiency of the reefs via their void’s space generated were proposed, which 
will give a perspective on how more valuable artificial reefs can or cannot be in comparison 
to natural boulder reefs.

Introduction 
 
A very important part of our world is endangered, the ocean. Covering more than 70% of our 
world’s surface and containing more than 90% of the water (National Geographic Society, 
2022), this massively large body of saltwater has an important role on our world. 
 
Producing over half of earth’s oxygen, absorbing more carbon dioxide than our atmosphere, 
transporting heat over the whole globe and regulating the climate, providing transportation 
for commercial trading globally, facilitating touristic and recreative activities, and being a very 
big source of food and ingredients for food or medicine production, are some of the most 
important benefits of having this huge body of water in our planet. (NOAA, 2021) 
 
There are several factors affecting the oceans from recent years back until today, with the 
majority being related to climate change and human activities. Some of the most relevant are 
the rising ocean heat content and temperatures, rising sea levels, ocean acidification, 
overfishing, plastic and hazardous leaking and habitat destruction, among others. (EPA, 2022) 
 
The ocean has been constantly changing and being affected by climatic changes through 
millennia, but these changes are increasing faster since industrialization and other 
anthropogenic disturbances. These changes are making it more difficult to many organisms 
to survive and could alter the biodiversity and productivity of the oceans.  
Habitat destruction and biodiversity loss is one of the biggest problems in the marine 
environment, with an approximate of 50% extent of coral reef loss since 1870 and 40% extent 
of mangrove loss since 1970, healthy marine habitats are decreasing faster than ever. (Our 
shared seas, 2022) 
 
There are many ways that habitats are being destroyed in the marine environment, for 
example, rising temperatures causing coral reefs destruction, by coral bleaching, but also 
direct human activities, such as pollution, mangrove forest clearing, deep-sea trawling and 
stone fishing, which are destroying the local and regional habitats. (Serkissian, 2019) 
 
According to The World Economic Forum (2021), scale of biodiversity loss damage is massive, 
and the consequences could have a big impact on the world as we know it. Currently, it is the 
most predominant anthropogenic stressor to marine species threatened with extinction (Fig. 
A.1.1). Therefore, biodiversity loss has been categorized as one global risk with very high 
likelihood and impact importance. (Fig. A.1.2)  
 
Several technologies, management projects, and products are being developed with the 
purpose of reverting the effects of habitat destruction and to protect the remaining healthy 
habitats. Artificial reefs, re-building natural reefs, mangrove restoration and designation of 
protected areas are some examples of these global ongoing projects. 
 
In Denmark, habitat destruction can be observed in the coastal areas of Sjaelland. Big reefs 
were once composed from natural stones formed as a consequence of natural transportation 
in the ice age. Today, many of the coasts in Danish waters lack of these boulder reefs as a 
consequence of decades of stone fishing. These activities took place specially on the first half 
of the 20th century, when stones were removed in order to be used as coastal protection and

harbor constructions. Today, stone fishing is prohibited, but other harmful activities such as 
seabed’s resources extraction by sand dredging are still permitted. Habitat reconstruction 
should be done to protect the local environment, and projects such as KBS are being 
conducted. Stones can be placed back in the seabed, re-building the natural reefs, but also, 
artificial materials can be used to simulate and achieve similar or greater positive effects on 
the environment. (Køge Bugt Stenrev, 2020) 
 
Artificial reefs have been widely used and produced in different shapes and materials, but 
concrete has shown very good physical and ecological performances and has seemed to be 
one of the most preferred artificial materials by marine species in several studies. Its great 
mechanical properties, easy production, and capability of creating different mixtures 
depending on the purpose, concrete has been widely used in marine applications all around 
the world. 
 
Using concrete units to build an artificial reef rather than re-locating a natural boulder reef 
should have greater benefits to the environment, which can derive from the capability to 
design the reef specifically to address certain problems or requirements of the local species.  
 
This project looks to design concrete reef units according to defined parameters, and to 
introduce a method able to compare the possible benefits of its design, particularly focusing 
on the reef shape and the voids generated, in order to analyze the overall efficiency, over 
natural boulder reefs, for the designated environment. It should also give an insight on the 
state-of-the-art of concrete usages in marine habitats and give a step forward on innovative 
methods to analyze the shape of the reefs.

has been affected severely by our day-to-day activities. The underlying motivation of this

Background Theory 
 
3.1 Artificial reefs 
 
Reefs are complex structures formed naturally, which create valuable habitats for the local 
marine species (Schwartzbach et al., 2020), such as protecting coastlines, create shelter and 
protection for several species, support commercial fishing and recreational activities, etc. 
There are several types of reefs, varying mainly on their composition and material, commonly 
categorized by those formed by rocks or by corals. 
 
Artificial reef is the name given to the manmade structures situated in marine habitats, which 
purpose is to mimic some or all the characteristics and tasks of a natural reef. 
 
They have been created and used historically and for many years by people for different 
purposes. In the past, they were mainly used for military and strategic purposes, such as 
trapping ships or harbor constructions. Nowadays they have very different usages, but still, 
very important both for the environment and for the population. They are primarily produced 
for projects focused on restoration and conservation of the marine environment and its 
biodiversity, which normally are aimed at mitigating effects of resource exploitation and 
destructive human practices, such as trawling, stone fishing, etc. Nevertheless, they can have 
other main and secondary purposes, such as tourism enhancement, commercial fisheries, 
recreational diving, etc. (Ly et al., 2021) 
 
Artificial reefs have been released all over the world by now. In Europe, most of them are 
located in the Mediterranean Sea, while few artificial reefs are located in the Atlantic Ocean 
areas, due to the strong and very frequent storms and currents, which make its benthic zone 
much more unstable and difficult to study and to work with. (Ly et al., 2021) 
 
Having artificial reefs can be very beneficious for the local environment, but usually they are 
not able to perform as good as natural reefs. Natural formed habitats and reefs often provide 
shelter and protection for the local species, mainly because of their rough surfaces, holes, and 
shape complexity; While in artificial reef structures is difficult to find this degree of physical 
complexity. (Natanzi et al., 2021) 
 
Design complexity and texture implementation in artificial reefs has shown to affect the rate 
and time of marine growth and colonization of small organisms. Tests have been conducted 
(Perkol-Finkel, 2014), where concrete blocks with different surface textures have shown 
significant and immediate differences in biological recruitment, concluding in marine species 
preferring a rough and texturized surface rather than a flat surface. In a bigger scale, holes 
and crevices have shown to increase fish production by creating shelter and minimizing 
predation risk (Schwartzbach et al., 2020). Therefore, by having artificial reefs packed with 
shape and texture complexity, great reef’s biological performances can be achieved.

3.2 Materials on artificial reefs 
 
Over the years, different materials have been used to create artificial reefs, or to simulate a 
reef, such as wood and waste materials that were considered acceptable for ocean disposal, 
as using clean concrete rubble (Vose & Nelsont, 1998) or as dumping vehicle tires. With recent 
technology developments and availability of new materials, several options to build a reef are 
available, and the selection of material can become a difficult decision to make before 
building a reef.  
 
As artificial marine habitat construction has been on the rise in recent years and more 
ecological regulations are being applied, many materials have been tested, analyzed, and 
used. 
 
In recent studies, concrete has been compared against polymers, metals, and other biogenic 
materials and has proved to be one, if not the most preferred material by marine species and 
organisms, outperforming the other artificial components by providing higher biological 
abundance (density and coverage) and faster colonization rates, even performing similar to 
natural materials (Dodds et al., 2022). This attractiveness to many species can be explained 
as it benefits their adhesion and survival stages due to the roughness and porosity features 
that characterizes it over other artificial materials; Additionally, concrete releases calcium 
hydroxide by leaching, and therefore it attracts calcifying organisms, organisms that use 
calcium and carbonate to create shells and skeletons, which often facilitate the recruitment 
of bigger species.  
 
Natural materials as rocks and other biogenic materials generally seem to perform similar to 
concrete, with some exceptions, such as wood, which has shown to attract and support less 
abundance (Dodds et al., 2022). Polymers, like Nylon and Teflon, are often hydrophobic and 
seem to create some of the worst environments, presenting considerably less local-
community abundance, and greater abundance of Non-Indigenous Species (NIS), which are 
often considered a threat due to their competition with local species for space and food. And 
finally, metals, which provide the lowest levels of both abundance and richness of species in 
marine structures, tentatively due to its toxicity for many species and overall, to the 
environment, being the worst assessed option. 
 
When deploying an artificial structure, or more specifically, an artificial reef, project managers 
should be aware of the main goal or purpose of it. This will help to decide which type of 
material and structure complexity will be suitable in order to get the maximum possible 
performance. These decisions can be made based on several studies that analyze different 
materials and their specific benefits. In (Lemoine et al., 2019), artificial reefs made out of 
concrete and of metal ships were deployed in order to identify their optimal purpose, showing 
concrete reefs to perform similar to boulder reefs in all tasks and processes, and metal ship 
reefs to support the biggest richness and biomass of big fishes, even though it was proved to 
be toxic and to have poor performances in small organisms abundance and richness (Dodds 
et al., 2022). Therefore, as proposed, depending on the purpose of the reef, material should 
be chosen; If the goal is to have a bigger number of fishes, metal has shown to be a good 
material, but to replicate physical, chemical, and environmental tasks of boulder reefs, 
concrete performs best.

3.3 Concrete 
 
Concrete can be understood generally as any product or composite material made by the use 
of a cementing paste. A cementing paste is the product of reaction between hydraulic cement 
and water (Neville & Brooks, 1987). Today, concrete is generally understood as the composite 
material made with cement, aggregates, admixtures, and water (Ramachandran & Feldman, 
1996). 
 
Cement is the active constituent of the concrete, being responsible of bonding all other 
components together, and from which main properties of the concrete depend on. The most 
“basic” cement, named Portland cement, consists mainly of lime, silica, alumina, and iron 
oxide (Neville & Brooks, 1987). Nowadays, more cements are being tested, developed, and 
produced, providing several mixtures to be available on the market. 
 
Fine and coarse aggregate are bigger particle components added to the mixture, and they are 
an important factor for the concrete workability and durability. They are generally composed 
of natural and geological materials, but as with the cement, new materials are being tested 
and used, in order to offer better performant concrete and more ecological, even by recycling 
waste materials and using them as aggregates. They are categorized into fine and coarse 
aggregates, depending on the sorting size of the particles. Some of the most common used 
aggregates are sand, fine crushed stones, crushed shells (fine), and gravel, crushed stones, 
and recycled concrete (coarse). 
 
Concrete is one of the most used materials in the world, being the largest volume material 
used, due to the great availability of its constituents and its simple and cheap way to produce 
it. The production itself does not involve that much pollution and CO2 emissions, but due to 
the volume used and produced, the CO2 footprint needs to be accounted for and alternatives 
or improvements should be found. In recent years, research and testing has been conducted 
to find these alternatives, focused specially on changing or modifying the cement composition 
and the aggregate’s materials, without compromising the performance of the material. (Flatt 
et al., 2012) 
 
These modifications and alternatives will affect the concrete’s performance and should be 
implemented depending on the conditions and purposes of the concrete. Different physical 
and chemical stressors will affect the concrete’s properties and lifespan, and as concrete is 
used in many different industries, variations are not always useful for every application. 
 
In marine conditions, there are several stressors to take into consideration when selecting the 
concrete mixture to be used, such as chloride ingress, sulfate attack, physical stressors, 
erosion, etc.  
 
Chloride ingress is a very common problem in submerged and partially sub-merged concrete 
structures. Concrete is commonly reinforced as it is often used to build bridges or big 
structures that demand high durability due to the big physical stresses applied, and its lifespan 
is usually limited by the chloride ingress rates. Chlorides from the sea water start to penetrate 
the first layers of the concrete and when reaching the reinforcement structures, they start 
creating corrosion. For concrete itself, chlorides are harmless, but by corroding the 
==PAGE:16==
Design of concrete reef units for Danish marine habitats 
8 
reinforcement core, they start generating cracks and decreasing the performance of the 
structure. Nevertheless, many more ions are present in the sea water, some of them being 
harmful to the concrete, and normally associated with chloride ingress, as they affect the 
porosity of the material and facilitate the ingress. Such is the case of sulfur, being one of the 
six most abundant ions at sea water, penetrates into the concrete and start forming gypsum 
or ettringite by reacting with the calcium hydroxide or the aluminate phases respectively, 
which normally leads to physical deteriorations such as expansion, cracking, loss of strength, 
etc. (Jakobsen et al., 2016). This problem has seemed to be controlled by replacing portions 
of the cement with SCMs (Hosseinzadeh et al., 2022), using high quality concrete together 
with lower water-cement ratios, porosity, and permeability.  
 
On the other hand, damage, and implications from using concrete in certain environments 
should be analyzed. In marine applications, concrete can pollute or affect the water quality 
and local environment in several aspects, and one of them is by changing the pH of the water 
by releasing hydroxyl ions contained. When submerged, especially as a freshly casted 
concrete, a rapid increase on water’s pH levels can be identified (Law et al., 2013), which is 
temporal and usually decreases until reaching the initial pH of the water, but with a peak that 
can be toxic for several local species. Several factors affecting this pH peak values, its duration, 
and its appearing time can be found, but the main factors appear to be the flow rate of the 
water and the ratio between the concrete’s surface area and the water volume, which means 
that concrete can cause significant problems for small, enclosed water bodies with low flow 
rates. 
 
3.4 Concrete Marine Usages: State-of-the-art  
 
As a consequence of unique interactions and stressors that concrete faces underwater, 
different materials and alternatives have been used all over the world. As the number of 
possible materials and mixtures is considerably big, an assessment of usages and their 
respective performances and benefits, was conducted.  
 
Different research and projects were found by prioritizing marine application concretes, being 
tested, or used in current projects by the industry. Concrete marine applications ranged from 
harbors to sea walls and artificial reefs. A global search of all marine applications was first 
conducted, followed by a reef specific research in order to include all type of alternatives and 
options available. Furthermore, special focus on bio-enhanced concretes rather than 
structural concretes was used.  
 
Modifications on binder and aggregate composition can result in modifying its mechanical 
properties and performance, but differences on colonization rates can also vary due to these 
changes, and thus, become very relevant for marine applications. Ground Granulated Blast-
furnace Slag (GGBS) is one of the most used variants for binder composition and appears to 
be a good alternative for marine concrete, also being an ecological binder, as the lowest 
values for carbon footprint in concrete typically contain GGBS (Urban, 2018). GGBS has 
proved to outperform Fly Ash, another combustion residual, in having faster and higher 
colonization rates (Ly et al., 2021), and presenting higher biomass in the longer term. 
Nevertheless, pulverized fly ash concrete has shown to be a potential substratum for artificial 
reefs if the purpose is to recruit corals, providing good colonization rates (Lam, 2003). 
==PAGE:17==

9 
Furthermore, GGBS has shown greater biodiversity and species richness than with Portland 
cement and lower ratios of invasive-native species in the environment (Perkol-Finkel et al., 
2018), which translates in helping the local biota on surviving; Also, it has shown to generate 
a notable bigger live coverage as early as 3 months (Perkol-Finkel, 2014), in comparison to 
Portland cement.  
 
As of big fish, species richness and biodiversity will depend partly on the capability of the 
concrete to be colonized by engineering species and other small organisms, which will then 
attract bigger species by providing food source and a valuable environment. Nevertheless, 
Coal and Oil ash residues as binder elements were compared in fish richness and occurrence 
(Vose & Nelsont, 1998), without having significant differences, suggesting that bigger 
differences on fish abundance should occur when changing the reef size and design features. 
By applying high complexity surfaces and design features, such as holes and cavities, which 
normally appear in natural reefs, the biological preference for the structure is seen to be 
promoted (Perkol-Finkel et al., 2018). 
 
Structures in marine environments are often affected by physical stressors and external 
forces, maintenance is more difficult that on onshore applications, and the constant removal 
and deployment is not the best option as it normally disturbs the environment. Therefore, to 
have a concrete mixture with good mechanical properties and durability is key. Changes in 
binder composition will affect the “bonding” performance of the concrete and replacing 
Portland cement with GGBS as in (Ly et al., 2021) has presented better mechanical behavior 
and to be a stronger mixture, as well as showing a higher chloride penetration resistance 
(Perkol-Finkel, 2014). Moreover, in regard to compressive strength, even though GGBS 
presents bigger values than most binders, it shows lower values than Silica Fume (Seleem et 
al., 2010), but when looking the strength deterioration ratio (efficiency to maintain strength 
values in a marine environment over time), GGBS outperforms Silica Fume, concluding GGBS 
as the best option for structural marine projects that need low maintenance and long-life 
spans. 
 
Natural materials can also be used in the binder composition and replacing a fraction of the 
cement. Nonetheless, this replacement should be controlled, as mechanical and chemical 
properties could deteriorate. As it is the case of replacing cement with waste oyster shells, 
that proved to be beneficial for biological attachments by lowering the pH of the concrete, 
but with a threshold of 20% replacement of the cement (Kong et al., 2022) because oyster 
shells increase the porosity of the structure due to their great water absorption, and therefore 
leads to strength deteriorations. 
 
Different “eco-friendly” concretes have been developed and compared against standard 
concrete, as in the case of Dodds et al., 2022, where the ecological concrete based on natural 
materials, showed records of increased density and diversity of colonization, but not 
significant differences in the abundance of settling communities. Furthermore, the natural 
additives concentrations added to the concrete in order to reduce pH variations, resulted 
having bigger influences on biological recruitment, rather than in reducing the pH of the 
concrete.

Portland cement’s poor colonization may be due to its high alkalinity, which at high levels 
tends to present difficulties for biological attachments (Kong et al., 2022), which 
colonization’s are normally predominated by alkotolorant species. Alternatively, GGBS 
presents lower alkalinity, which attracts more species and in a bigger abundance (Natanzi et 
al., 2021). 
 
The aggregates composition can also play an important role when optimizing the colonization 
rates of the reef or structure. Natural elements or recycled materials can be used as 
aggregates or partial replacements of the aggregates, such as using oyster shells, which has 
been seen to have positive effects in overall richness and to attract a considerable number of 
mollusks (Hanlon et al., 2018). In some cases, specific species, such as worms and barnacles 
(Collins et al., 2022), have been recruited in considerable amounts. Nevertheless, replacing 
the aggregates with oyster shells will decrease the overall strength of the concrete (Georges 
et al., 2021), similar to when replacing binder with them. Thresholds for this replacement to 
comply with minimum strength performances will vary depending on the aggregate category 
to be replaced, as a fine aggregate replacement, it should be 20%, and as a coarse aggregate 
replacement, it should be 50% (Eziefula et al., 2018).  
 
Waste recycled materials can be an option as binder element or replacement. By using 
recycled materials, besides the obvious availability of the material, a reduction of waste and 
likely pollution, leads to be categorized as a more ecological concrete. Some materials are 
already widely recycled and used, as Fly Ash, but other by-products from burning fuel for 
electricity generation, are not recycled and end up as soil contaminants, such as Coal Bottom 
Ash (BAA). BAA has been recently tested as concrete’s aggregate substitute, showing similar 
mechanical performance but with increased porosity (Lee et al., 2016). By having this added 
porosity, the concrete will enhance biological adhesion, but the likelihood of sulfate attack 
and chloride ingress will increase. Therefore, a threshold of substituting only 30% of the 
granite with BAA as aggregate, has been proposed. 
 
There are many other waste materials that can be used, as is the case of natural fibers, such 
as hemp. When fibers are used in the concrete mixture, they act as a “bridge” between the 
mix, and help bonding the material together, and therefore, they show an increase on flexural 
strength and prevent from spalling and disintegration to happen. Furthermore, hemp seems 
to have the best mechanical performance, the most biological recruitment and the less CO2 
emissions accounted for at the production processes (Dennis et al., 2018). The strength 
increase achieved by using hemp is about 18 to 28%, but as many other materials, using 
excessive amounts will lead to a decrease in strength (Chandeng et al., 2022), and a threshold 
of around 1.5% is proposed. 
 
One of the most common used recycled aggregates consists of old concrete, natural stones 
from construction, and clay bricks. Using these materials as aggregates increases porosity in 
a much smaller ratio, thus replacement of 100% can be conducted without compromising the 
strength of the concrete. Nonetheless, the alkalinity of the concrete increases with these 
materials (Kong et al., 2022), therefore, the rate of replacement should be depending on the 
requirements of the reef and local conditions.

Lastly, the component that is the same for all concretes, water, can be modified in terms of 
water content and water-cement ratios. Water content in concrete is crucial for the 
performance of it. Excessive amounts of water have resulted in lower density concretes, due 
to the formation of gaps which were occupied by the water at casting, and eventually 
evaporate forming capillary pores (Chandeng et al., 2022). As seen, pores in the concrete will 
enhance biological recruitment, but will cause strength losses and increase the likelihood of 
sulfate attack or chloride ingress.

3.5 Biological Interactions 
 
Understanding which species benefits the most from the artificial structures, and which 
species are in charge of starting the colonization of the reef is key for selecting the optimum 
material. 
 
When deploying an artificial structure, which often are not covered by organisms and 
biological material, a natural process of colonization occurs. The first species to interact with 
the reefs are microorganisms, such as bacteria, and other bigger species that start adhering 
to the surfaces and creating changes in the material, the so-called habitat-forming species, 
and they usually are corals, oysters, bivalves, and barnacles, among others. These species are 
therefore the most sensitive to the material type of the structures (Dodds et al., 2022). 
 
The first biofilm produced in artificial reefs and structures is formed very rapidly and consists 
of diatoms, bacteria, and other microorganisms (Natanzi et al., 2021), which will be the main 
food source for bigger invertebrates that will adhere afterwards, such as the above-
mentioned habitat-forming invertebrates.  
 
==PAGE:22==
Design of concrete reef units for Danish marine habitats 
14 
Habitat-forming species contribute massively on transforming the structures into a valuable 
environment by changing directly or indirectly the physical and chemical state of the 
structure, such as by depositing calcium carbonate (CaCO3) skeletons (Jones et al., 1994) 
which is an essential process in providing habitats for the local species and maintaining high 
biodiversity levels on the ecosystem (Andersson & Gledhill, 2013), and that also provides the 
structures with bio protection, by reinforcing material, reducing its sensitivity to corrosion, 
prolonging its lifespan, etc. (Perkol-Finkel et al., 2018) 
 
Even though surface complexity and concrete composition are key to colonization success, 
location (Collins et al., 2022) and the period of immersion or settlement orientation (Lam, 
2003) have proved to be more relevant in community development and colonization than the 
concrete mix or surface complexity. Thus, local, and regional testing should be conducted in 
order to know the best design parameters for the artificial reef or structure. 
 
Bigger species, as fishes, also benefit from reefs and thus, artificial reefs. Artificial reefs create 
shelter and protection for these fishes and other species against predators, which often 
attracts more of them and increases fish abundance; But they also play an important role 
increasing fish production by enhancing their growth rate (Schwartzbach et al., 2020). The 
structure complexity of the reefs allows fishes to save more energy than when in open 
habitats by minimizing their predation risks which often involves bigger swimming activity, 
and when having lower energy demands, fishes often present higher reproductive outputs 
(Schwartzbach et al., 2020; Skjæraasen et al., 2006). 
 
Therefore, an optimum material of the reef will lead to an increased colonization and to 
enhance the richness and abundance of small and bigger species, such as fish, and together 
with the reef shape complexity, it will provide them with a valuable environment with food 
and protection.

4.1 The problem 
 
In Denmark, stone fishing was practiced for decades, mainly in the 20th century, and it has 
resulted in the destruction of natural boulder reefs in many large areas of the coast. Stone 
fishing was banned from 2010, but it is estimated that around 40 km2 of the approximate of 
1200 km2 known boulder reefs in Denmark, were removed from 1950-2000 (Dahl et al., 2003). 
 
Køge Bay is a shallow Danish Bay, in the southern part of Øresund, enclosed by Greater 
Copenhagen area in the North, Køge Municipality in the Southeast and Stevns Municipality in 
the South (Figure 3). 
==PAGE:23==
Design of concrete reef units for Danish marine habitats 
15 
 
Figure 3. Map of Denmark. Køge Bay marked in red. Figure taken from: (Wallentin, 2017) 
 
In this area, stone fishing was and has been an issue as all around Denmark. Stones were used 
for many different things, such as coastal protection, construction of piers and harbor 
constructions (Køge Bugt Stenrev, 2020). These boulder reefs have many benefits both for 
the local biota and for the population; They usually work as shelter for many species against 
predators, act as nursery and feeding areas for some marine organisms, provide coastal 
protection from storm surges, and even support fishing and recreative activities such as scuba 
diving (Coral restoration foundation, 2019). Unfortunately, they are unable to restore 
themselves, which makes restoration projects to be key for maintaining these environments.  
 
Hard bottom habitats, thus, boulder reefs, are utilized by several species at one or several life 
stages, such as herring (Clupea harengus), Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) and European Eel 
(Anguilla anguilla), and at Køge Bay, boulder reefs have seemed to work as shelter for small 
Atlantic cod. 
 
Atlantic cod used to be one of the largest cod stocks (Akimova et al., 2019), but nowadays is 
being depleted due to overfishing and low recruitment levels (Cook et al., 1997). These low 
recruitments levels could be mainly due to temperature-induced starvation (Cushing, 1984) 
and predation (Kempf et al., 2010). 
 
Overexploitation and temperature-induced starvations are problems that are not likely to be 
solved with artificial reefs, but predation rates can be lowered, and growth rates can be 
enhanced, by providing valuable and complex structures to the local cod. 
 
Juvenile cod being predated at Køge are known to be around 6 to 8 cm long and commonly 
preyed on by birds and other predatory fish, including cod themselves. Boulder reefs usually 
generate fish abundance and increase their production; therefore, they are expected to be of 
great importance and help for cod in the area.

filter-clean the eutrophic water (Enderlein & Wahl, 2004), and provide food and habitat for

is an invasive species in Køge and in Denmark, and which is outcompeting native fish species 
and damaging fishermen’s catch. (Food Nation Denmark, n.d.)

4.4 Criteria and constraints  
 
As the output of this project will be assessed in order to be used in the NGO project “Køge 
Bugt Stenrev”, there are certain production and environmental criteria and constraints to be 
considered. 
 
The whole project will cover an extensive part of the Bay (1000 m3); thus, the complete 
coverage of the reef will be most likely very long. A considerable amount of concrete will be 
used, therefore a concrete mixture with a low CO2 footprint and with optimal performance 
to minimize maintenance and enhance durability, should be used. According to lifecycle 
==PAGE:26==
Design of concrete reef units for Danish marine habitats 
18 
analyses made of concrete reefs, the cement production is the main contributor to 
environmental impacts (Boll et al., 2022), therefore, an ecological cement in terms of CO2 
emission should be used. Moreover, by increasing the volume occupied by the reefs, creating 
more voids, and by reducing the density of the concrete units, the efficiency of covered area 
and environment created with less material (concrete) will be achieved, and these 
environmental impacts will be reduced. 
 
The production process should be as simple as possible, allowing to conduct bulk production 
with minimal production-line errors. As an NGO with external fundings, the budget is reduced 
compared to similar projects. Therefore, elaborated production processes, or expensive 
production methods should be avoided. 
 
On site, the reef should create shelter and protection for key species such as juvenile cod and 
blue mussels, by providing surface and shape complexity, and to have high biocompatibility 
in order to attract more species to colonize its surfaces and improve the biomass of the area.

4.5 Production method alternatives  
 
One of the main advantages of concrete is the ease of molding and shape it to the desired 
form in the production processes. The most common process of producing concrete units, is 
by casting fresh concrete into molds, consolidating it, and removing them when dried. This 
allows to produce big amounts of concrete units, even with the exact same shape, just by 
using the same molds. 
 
A new way of producing concrete units and structures has been developed in recent years, 
and it is 3D printing. Concrete printing consists of large 3D robotic printers which extrude the 
mixture in layers from a nozzle, following the coordinates of a digitalized 3D model. This 
allows to produce complex structures and brings to life more creative designs.  
 
==PAGE:28==
Design of concrete reef units for Danish marine habitats 
20 
In artificial reefs, the designs have been very simple over the past decades, using the 
traditional method of casting the fresh concrete into the molds. Reefs usually were basic 
geometric shapes as cubes and pipes, often called “Bonna”, but more complicated shapes 
called “Typi” and “Babel” can be found. (Ly et al., 2021) 
 
With 3D printing, artificial reefs have evolved over the last few years, bringing innovative 
designs and in some cases, more useful for certain aspects, such as printing reefs with a 
complex inside structure, which is more complicated with a mold, as it is not possible or is 
very difficult to demold, unless the mold material is soluble or has specific features.  
 
 
4.6 Deployment methods 
 
Different deployments methods have been used and can be used in order to place an artificial 
reef. The most common are by dropping the units from a ship, using a big mechanical crane 
which takes the units from the ship, and deploys them into the water. This process can vary 
depending on the ship and the crane used, and it can end up deploying the units from above, 
at, or below the sea surface. The deployment height will directly influence in the position of 
the units, making the final shape of the reef to be random if the units are deployed from or 
above the surface, or to have a controlled shape, if the crane can release the units very close 
to the seabed.  
 
The other deployment method is by manually locating the reef, with help of professional 
divers. This method has been used with modular reefs to build it on-site (Reef Design Lab, 
2022), and to deposit bigger complete reefs with the help of a crane, where the diver helps 
by moving it under water to reach the desire position.

1. Concrete preparation 
 
As the molds were relatively small and for the practicality of the prototype production, 
a mortar was used, instead of a concrete mixture. The difference between mortar and 
concrete is that the mortar is composed only from cement, sand, and water, and the 
concrete also includes gravel, or another coarse aggregate.  
The mortar should be workable enough to cover all desired areas. As the mixture was 
made depending on the availability of materials at the workshop and without relation 
to the state-of-the-art research, it was first produced and tested changing the w/c 
ratio to find the best proportions. 
 
The components used were future CEM (CEM II / BM Q-LL class 52.5N) as the cement 
binder, sand in the fraction [0-4 mm] as the aggregate, and water. 
 
The process to produce the mortar both for the slump test and for casting the samples 
was following the steps shown on Table A.5.1. 
 
Three mortars were produced and all of them underwent a slump test (Figure 10), 
which results can be found in Table 4. A slump test (Figure A.5.1) is a very common 
practice in the concrete industry. It is a test used to measure the consistency of the 
fresh concrete, which is normally made to assess the workability and uniformity of the 
mixture. It is made by pouring the fresh concrete into a conical mold, called “slump 
cone”, which is opened at both ends. The cone is filled in three stages or layers, each 
of it being tamped before pouring the next layer. When filling the slump cone 
completely, the mortar is struck off the flush at the top of the mold, which is 
afterwards removed for the mortar to spread on the table. 
Usually, the slump, which is the difference between the height of the mold and that 
of the mortar, together with the type of slump (Figure A.5.2) and will determine the 
consistency properties of the mix. For this specific test, by introducing a shaft that 
strikes the surface to allow the mortar to spread on the table, that was done 15 times 
for each mixture, it was possible to measure the diameter of the spread, which give 
us an overview of which mortar is the best. The desired spread considering the size 
and shape of the molds was about 17 to 18 cm.

The basin of water used was approximately  200 cm and 90 cm height. In order to be able 
to deploy the samples as in the expected scenario, a release mechanism was needed, which 
would minimize user errors and disturbances in regards of deployment location and/or 
strength of the release. 
 
The design of the mechanism was discussed, developed, and then produced with the help of 
DTU’s Sustain workshop, and it consisted of a base of wood to be located on top of the basin 
borders and which had a metal guide with height-level adjustments, able to modify the 
release position to the desired one. To grab the units and release them, a quick-release 
pressure clamp was welded to the height-leveler (Fig. A.6.1). 
 
The test consisted of dropping the units from the clamp and analyzing their sinking behavior, 
tendencies, final positioning, and the size and voids generated as a reef. Tests were conducted 
in two different water depths (30 cm & 60 cm), to assess the influence of water depth in the 
results. There were 5 categories, which consisted of the natural stones, the standard units, 
the randomized units, a mix of the standard and the randomized height units, and one last for 
the variants. Each category contained 10 runs at each depth, giving a total of 20 runs, and 
each run consisted of dropping 8 units of the respective category, except from the variants, 
which only consisted of 4 units on each run. Units in runs 1 to 10 (1st depth) for each category 
were sorted in a different deployment order to cover the most possible combinations, and in 
runs 11 to 20 (2nd depth), the first 10 runs were replicated, being able to compare just the 
depth influence.  
 
A total of 720 single unit releases were conducted, resulting in 100 reef simulations, from 
where analyses were made. 
 
Several analyses were made in every category; Some of them analyzed the behavior of the 
units themselves, and others were focused on the final reef obtained. The different analyses 
were as follows:

• Reef voids and shape – 3D reef projection 
 
 
The voids produced by the reef can be mostly visualized from the outside, but interior voids 
can and will most likely be generated and were not visible from the photos obtained. These 
voids were estimated with Equation 3, but this calculation is not accurate, and a more precise 
method was presented. 
 
A 3D projection of the two “faces” of one reef was conducted in one of the reefs to test the 
method. The projection consisted of analyzing the values of the pixels from each perspective, 
as in the 2D void detection, and projecting these values along a 3rd plane. 
 
For this to be possible, the void images of the tested reef (RND #1) were cropped to 800 x 800 
pixels resolution, and a 3D imaginary matrix based on that resolution (800 x 800 x 800) was 
simulated, to which the pixel values (RGB values) of the void images were assigned in their 
respective planes, as the first layer (Figure 25). 
 
==PAGE:46==

Afterwards, the first layer values were projected along their respective perpendicular axis, 
generating a projection of one side of the reef in 3D (Figure 26.A). When projecting both 
planes, each pixel of the matrix would have two theoretical values (one from each projection), 
and if the values matched (Figure 26.B), that pixel’s coordinate would be filled in another final 
generated matrix (Figure 26.C). There were two final matrices, one for the voids and one for 
the reef itself. 
 
 
Figure 26. 3D pixel projection method. A. Imaginary matrix 1st projection. B. Imaginary matrix

The two matrices were then generated and should contain the 3D shape of the reef and the 
voids respectively, being able to visualize them.

• Sink & Positioning 
 
From the different sinking data obtained from each unit, three different results were 
obtained. 
 
• Sinking behavior 
 
As of the unit’s sinking behavior, there was a clear difference between when deployed 
sidewards and when deployed upwards or downwards. When the units are being deployed in 
their side position, they have a high tendency to present a rotational sinking, most likely due 
to their gravity mass being located in the base, making the units to rotate towards it. One 
thing to remark, is that the variant units were only deployed in the upwards position, 
therefore, the sidewards and downwards occurrence should not be considered. When the 
units are deployed upwards, there is no rotational behavior in the standard units nor 
==PAGE:49==
Design of concrete reef units for Danish marine habitats

randomized units, but in the variants, there is, and it can be explained due to the design 
changes of some of the units, which most likely displaces the gravity mass to somewhere else 
in the unit. 
Furthermore, when deployed downwards, standard, and randomized units present almost all 
the time a straight sinking behavior.

height units. C. Variant units 
When having a straight sinking behavior, the final location and orientation of the units can be 
more predictable, as the units will make impact at the same location and position as deployed, 
which allow to “build” the reef, unit by unit, while when the units are rotating, the impact 
position will be uncontrolled and the ending position for the unit will be random. 
 
• Complete turns 
 
The second one was the total amount of complete turns and the average turns per unit of all 
the sinkings on each depth and from each category analyzed in this variable (Figure A.8.4). 
The result obtained on every unit was 0 complete turns, as the rotational behavior observed 
was only tilting and a maximum of 90° rotations. Therefore, we can expect to have none or a 
minor number of units completing turns at deployment.

7 
Discussion 
 
As the output of this thesis will be assessed to be used for KBS, in particularly to be part of 
the “Circle Reef” project, consisting of comparing reefs made of stone and concrete, concrete 
was pre-selected as the material for these reef units. 
 
State-of-the-art research to analyze different benefits and usages of several concrete’s 
mixtures gives an overview of the available options to be used and options to be further 
developed or modified. 
 
Concrete selection 
 
No testing or conclusions were proved in this project, but as theoretically found from the 
state-of-the-art research, many different variables from the concrete mixture elements and 
the design features of the reefs, will be responsible for the performance of the structure and 
should be previously assessed specifically for the location and purpose of it.  
 
As of today, there is no such perfect concrete mixture for marine applications. Replacement 
and different materials can be used as binder elements or aggregates, but all materials will 
give the reef different characteristics and will perform differently. Selection of these materials 
==PAGE:60==
Design of concrete reef units for Danish marine habitats 
52 
should be according to the usage of the reef or structure, and to the surrounding 
environment. 
 
By-products of furnaces and fuels, such as GGBS, Fly Ash, Silica Fume, and BAA, among others, 
have shown to provide the concrete with similar or even better mechanical properties as the 
standard concrete, and the availability of the materials due to big production waste, allows 
these products to be some of the most attractive as the replacement of Portland cement for 
structural constructions. Furthermore, they have shown good performances at recruiting 
species and building valuable environments, making them optimal elements for marine 
concretes. Consequences of using these materials vary from one to another, but overall, the 
downsides of these elements are minimal, and can be controlled if needed, such as varying 
replacement rates for porosity influence and for pH variations. 
 
Organic waste material in overall has shown very good colonization rates and biological 
compatibility with the local species, but differences in mechanical properties between them 
are notorious. While fibers, because of their physical features, presents a strength 
enhancement for the concrete, oyster shells, for example, decreases this value. Consequently, 
different organic materials can be seen as a positive or negative usage depending on the 
specific structure purpose. For structural usages or when big forces are applied to the reef or 
structure, fibers can be the best organic option, as it will reduce risks of disintegration, and 
for non-structural purposes, oyster shells or similar materials, should be considered due to 
their biological enhancement, lower pH, and reduced contamination. 
 
For the last component of the concrete, water, the proportion is the only thing that could be 
changed. As more water leads to porosity, big water amounts can be used in non-structural 
components and will help to the biological recruitment. Otherwise, lower amounts should be 
considered. 
 
Design 
 
Many designs can be achieved for a reef, but there are many factors that will affect their 
performance, and therefore, should be accounted for in the design. The design of a product, 
and in this case, the reef, will not always be finished with one prototype, and further 
variations and changes should be made. 
 
For these reef units, different design alternatives from the main design were developed in 
order to visualize and identify the sensitivity from these changes. From these differences, an 
optimal design was selected depending on the desired output and production processes. 
 
As the units are most likely to be manually casted and produced in considerably big quantities, 
the simpler the design is, the cheaper and easy the production will be. The design selected 
allows to use simple and replicable molds, and to cast them in an easy, fast, and cheap way. 
 
The design shows good structure complexity, which has shown to enhance both colonization 
and fish production, which in this project, are the main objectives. Also, it has a good surface 
coverage able to implement with texture features, increasing its bio-performance.

53 
As prototype production was conducted in a smaller size than the probable final size of it, the 
concrete used for the prototype was not based on the state-of-the-art research, but in 
workshop’s material availability. Furthermore, for the tests conducted, variations due to 
concrete’s composition are not relevant, as overall volume, shape, and density of the units 
would not change. Therefore, when having an optimum concrete mixture, only on-site testing 
should be conducted. 
 
Physical status 
 
Physical status overall showed a pattern of the units to be intact after deployment. Some of 
the units were damaged, as in the case of one unit of the randomized category, and two of 
the variants, specifically one from the variant of randomized units with different diameters 
and one from the standard units with different diameters. 
As seen from Figure A.2.1, the damaged units appeared always in the second depth runs, 
which most likely translates as damages due to repetitive impacts (as first depth runs were 
conducted first), more than depth influence, as impact velocity seemed to be almost the same 
in both depths.  
 
Moreover, as variant units damaged were both from the one’s varying the cylinder diameters, 
design features could be interpretated as not optimal, which also visually some cylinders 
seemed fragile when decreasing their diameter. 
 
Thus, designs in general, presented good durability and sufficient mechanical properties to 
be deployed without being damaged or destroyed. As the purpose of the reef is to be 
deployed and sit at the bottom as a non-structural feature, designs comply with the 
requirements, and can be produced with almost all concrete alternatives reviewed and 
without reinforcement needed, in terms of mechanical limitations. 
 
Sinking: behavior and positioning 
 
After analyzing all sinkings, the behavior presented a pattern where rotational behavior was 
clearly present in the units deployed sidewards, while it showed a straight sinking in almost 
all of the units deployed upwards or downwards. Furthermore, not a single turn was detected 
in any of the runs, meaning that the units will sink rotating slightly, but overall, the final 
positioning of the units can be somewhat controlled and not be random. 
 
By analyzing the final position of the units after contact, an overview on the likelihood for the 
pieces to finish at certain position at the bottom was presented. No big dominance of an 
orientation was obtained, but the units deployed sidewards or upwards, showed an 
occurrence of more than 50% in being positioned as deployed. 
 
Consequently, units, even though the positioning will be random, and several factors will 
affect the sinking at the time of deployment, can be partially controlled and their positioning 
can be estimated. By deploying the units in an upwards position there is a high likelihood that 
they will finish being upwards or sidewards, due to its straight sinking. Alternatively, by 
deploying the units sidewards, the units will rotate, but without reaching a complete turn, 
and as observed, there is high likelihood for them to be positioned sidewards again. 
==PAGE:62==
Design of concrete reef units for Danish marine habitats 
54 
 
Overall, deploying the units in a sidewards position, appears to be statistically the position 
where most controlled final positioning will be achieved. 
 
Nevertheless, all sinkings were conducted in a still water basing, which will not be the case in 
the real deployment. There, current, waves, and more physical disturbances will appear. 
Therefore, testing with these factors should be conducted, as these results could change. 
 
Horizontal displacement and spread 
 
Knowing the horizontal displacement of the units, and the average spread of the reefs by their 
category, will allow us to know if the reef is more likely to be spread all over the seabed or to 
be stacked, building the reef more vertically than horizontally. 
 
By looking at the individual unit’s displacement per category, stones are clearly the units that 
tend to spread over the surface, while the concrete units tend to stack onto each other and 
build a more vertical reef. Among the concrete units, the horizontal displacement is similar, 
and depth only influences the variant units, this most likely due to the lower density of some 
of the units, which at increasing the depth, the units were observed to drift around and reach 
bigger distances from the deployment point. Individual displacement can affect the patterns 
as some of the units were displaced a lot due to contact or by other factors, which these 
extreme results are minimized, and a clear trend of displacement is seen with the reef spread 
analysis. Having less displacement means that the units are more likely to end up in the same 
coordinate as deployed, controlling the final reef shape. 
 
Consequently, stone reefs should be considered as the best option if the purpose of the reef 
is to cover greater surface areas, but to build a reef with a considerable height, creating a 
great value environment and in a more controlled space, a big number of stones would be 
needed. Alternatively, using the concrete units, an efficient number of units would be used in 
order to build a reef with a considerable height, and with a great control of the surface area 
covered. 
 
2D voids 
 
When analyzing the void ratios obtained from image processing, no clear trend is observed 
between the concrete units, nor depth influence. Stones do present a lower ratio of voids 
generated than all concrete units. When mixing standard units and randomized units (mix 
category), an stable pattern of void ratio is observed between depths, therefore, we can 
assume, together with the inverse patterns of both standard and randomized as single 
categories in different depths, that standard units generate more voids when deployed in low 
depths and randomized units when deployed in higher depths, this potentially due to the 
bigger possible gaps from the cylinders when being sidewards, which is the most occurred 
final positioning in bigger depths for the randomized units. 
 
When selecting a reef unit, considering that the reef will be focused on creating shelter, a 
bigger void ratio should be the best option, therefore, concrete units would be the best 
performing units. Among the concrete units, as relevant differences were not found, design 
==PAGE:63==

55 
should be selected according to other parameters, or by selecting the unit with most, even 
slightest, void ratio, which are the standard units. 
 
Great number of voids seem to happen in the concrete units, which will allow, from what 
seemed in the concrete blocks extraction and the literature reviewed, for the blue mussels to 
locate themselves and grow in the internal areas of the reef and hidden surfaces, and for the 
juvenile cod to have shelter from predators, and to enhance their production. 
 
3D voids 
 
When projecting the voids in a three-dimensional model, a clear visualization of the reef 
structure is possible. As the 3D projection was made from only 2 planes, the projection lacks 
precision, but works as the basis for further development of the method. When looking the 
projection, a clear projection of the 2 plane silhouettes is observed, but by looking from a 
third perspective, a flat projection along the plane appears, which most likely is the reason 
for more voids to appear in the inside layers of the model. 
 
Furthermore, slight variations in size between perspectives due to camera positioning, was 
observed. These variations do not affect the void ratios analysis, but when projecting the reef 
in 3D, size irregularities were identified. For further development of the model and 
replications, an establishment of camera distance and fixation should be used. 
 
To the author’s knowledge at the time of publication, no similar method has been developed 
in order to analyze the voids of a reef or an artificial reefs in 3D, therefore, this will serve for 
continuing research and optimization to have a method to analyze the void richness of the 
reefs, which will allow for new projects to come, to assess and to design the most optimal 
reef specifically for the location and its purposes.  
 
Automatic image void detection 
 
By obtaining the number of pixels on the reef’s image ranging in certain values, in our case, 
darker pixels, allowed the user to identify and approximate the void coverage area. From the 
obtained histogram, noise in the void readings was detected, but a good approximation of 
which elements were voids by pixel detection was achieved. 
 
Controlled environment in the sense of colors and background opacities, will give more 
precise lectures of the voids and of different features of the reef. On-site reef images will be 
more difficult to read, and filters should be applied. 
 
When having a more accurate detection of elements, a calculation of voids can be made and 
compared to the manual 2D void calculations, in order to see how precise the method is.

Conclusion 
 
Artificial reefs are a constantly evolving engineering field, where many innovations are being 
applied every day, and from where many positive ecological impacts can be achieved. There 
are many materials and design alternatives that can be used to build an artificial reef, and 
depending on the purpose of it, a material and design should be chosen. 
 
As the specific project seeks to use concrete for the reefs and several components with 
different outcomes are being used for marine-application concrete, an environmental 
assessment of possible concrete’s mixtures should be conducted, in order to select the 
optimal elements.  
 
When having the optimal mixture of the concrete, the reef needs to be designed according to 
local species, requirements and guidelines applied to it. Relevant information for designing a 
reef can be contained mainly in two questions, and which answers should elaborate further 
criteria from different guidelines and considerations: 
 
What is the specific purpose of the reef, and which are the current problems present in the 
area? 
Which are the chemical, physical, and biological properties of the area for the reef to be 
located? 
 
When answering these questions and having all criteria and constraints for the design, several 
options should be developed and analyzed, finally selecting the most beneficial one. 
 
In this project, the main objective was to make a design to be used in Danish marine habitats 
and to develop a method able to analyze the voids generated from the artificial reef units. 
The method was developed, and it was able to compare the reefs, from which the designed 
units provide bigger void spaces than natural stones. Therefore, designed units create a more 
valuable environment than boulder reefs, with a great amount of shelter spaces and providing 
protection for target species. 
 
Furthermore, an overview on the sinking behavior and final shapes of the reef was presented. 
Due to all uncontrolled factors while deploying the units, final reef shapes and exact position 
of the reef units will be random, but patterns on the shape and overall size were found. By 
using the concrete units rather than stones, a ‘stacked’ reef will be generated, rather than 
spread over the seabed, facilitating the construction of a reef in the 3 dimensions desired, 
and by controlling the seabed covered by the reef. Likewise, the efficiency of material will be 
greater, by covering the desired area with a lower number of units (material), rather than 
dumping many stones to achieve the desired shape. 
 
Overall, concrete units outperform stones in almost every aspect if an optimal concrete 
mixture to create bio-enhancement is used, the only benefit of using stones against the units 
would be covering a greater surface area with less material, which in our case, is not the goal.  
Therefore, the design seems to be optimal for creating a valuable environment in Danish 
marine waters, specifically for mussels and juvenile cod, and for which materials can be used 
in low amounts and produced ecologically.

Further steps 
 
 
As no similar methods and analyses were found previous to the tests conducted, errors and 
room to improved appeared along the process. 
 
As of testing, the test was well controlled in many ways, but unexpected changes appeared. 
When deploying the units, some of them were displacing from the deployment point in bigger 
distances than those covered by the sizer located on the surface (chess board), resulting in 
noise and thus, approximation of the displacement was needed. In further steps and future 
replications of testing, a bigger surface able to cover all possible reef spreads should be used. 
 
Masking and void detection was conducted manually from the images obtained. This allows 
to reduce misreading’s and to interpretate the void space’s more accurately, nonetheless, 
automatic image processing and void detection was proposed but no develop further due to 
time constraints. Program development and method refining for future replications or similar 
applications is advised, due to the big and specific complexity of using manual masking void 
detection. 
 
Finally, the last area to improve is 3D void projection. Both for reefs already placed in the 
seabed and for new reef’s designs, void detection and shape projection can be a valuable tool, 
and 3D projection helps to have these simulations without being physically with the reef. 
3D projection from the images seemed to work and to project accurately both side views 
used, but a third perspective image was clearly missing to make the reading more accurate 
and project the reef and voids more precisely. Side view photograph should be included in 
the analysis, and model refinements should be done. 
 
In terms of the unit’s usage, concrete’s elements should be selected according to an 
environmental impact assessment and project requirements. Re-defining the design is also 
advised, by adding surface complexity and texture features that were not applied in the 
prototype for simplicity and lack of relevance for selected testing. Surface complexity and 
textures, seems to enhance colonization, and together with the overall shape of the units and 
the voids generated, the environment created promises to be of great value for the local 
biota. 
 
Further steps for this project can be summarized as: 
• Reef units’ production: Refine the design to enhance bio-colonization and select an 
optimum material based on a LCA and local biological and chemical analysis. 
• Sinking testing: Include water disturbances and test deployment on site for more 
accurate readings. Create a more controlled testing set-up and fixate all readings. 
• Void detection methods: Add a third view image and refine the 3D projection, 
analyzing the volume generated with the real volume. Furthermore, refine the 
automatic void detection method by altering image values.
