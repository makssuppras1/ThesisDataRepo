II 
Abstract 
Cities are exercising growing pressure on biodiversity, due to increasing urbanization, and participate 
in the 6th mass extinction. Biodiversity dynamics in cities are complex, and require understanding 
biologic dynamics, anthropogenic pressures on ecosystems but also socio-political criteria, i.e. how 
stakeholders interact with biodiversity in cities. There is an urgent need for policy makers to act for 
biodiversity preservation but so far, no study has provided a complete framework ensuring the 
measurement of biodiversity state to implement an action plan for its preservation and restoration. 
Here we proposed a 3-steps iterative framework, ensuring urban system definition, urban biodiversity 
indicators measurement and implementation of an action plan. The measurement of urban 
biodiversity indicators allows the identification of areas of concern for the city. Qualitative links 
showing the expected impact of actions on indicators were built, ensuring selection of actions by 
practitioners, based on identified areas of concern. The choice of a multidisciplinary framework was 
proven necessary to ensure global understanding of urban biodiversity. An iterative framework is also 
relevant to engage municipalities in the long-term, to implement perennial actions and to measure the 
impact of those actions on urban biodiversity. Future literature search could focus on strengthening 
the link between actions and indicators. This framework could be completed by the consideration of 
life-cycle perspective to gauge the city’s impact on biodiversity outside its borders.

1. Introduction  
By 2050, it is projected that 68% of the world population will live in cities (United Nations, 2018). This 
growing urbanization will increase already existing pressures of urban areas on the environment. 
Urbanization is responsible for the destruction, fragmentation and pollution of natural habitats and is 
recognized as a major cause of biodiversity loss (Luck and Wu, 2002; Melles, 2005; Salinitro et al., 2019) 
and thus an important contributor to the 6th mass extinction. Cities impact biodiversity within its 
borders but also outside, due to importation of raw materials, food, water, or energy necessary for city 
consumption. If the latest category has the highest impact (McDonald et al., 2020), this study focuses 
on biodiversity state within city borders, later called urban biodiversity. This severe diminution of 
biodiversity decreases the ecosystem services (ES) indispensable for human population well-being. ES 
are the benefit humans obtained from ecosystems (Gómez-Baggethun and Barton, 2013; Zhang et al., 
2020) but also the benefit obtained for nature itself. In cities, ecosystems ensure the reduction of the 
heat island effect (higher temperature in cities compared to outlying areas due to the high 
concentration in built infrastructures), improve air quality or bring aesthetic values, to name but a few. 
Besides, biodiversity preservation is often missing from urban planning (Puppim de Oliveira et al., 
2011) or is less considered than other issues (Sun et al., 2019). Therefore, there is a general need to 
measure and understand the state of biodiversity at urban level to improve it. 
International targets and indicators on biodiversity were first instituted by the Convention on 
Biological Diversity (CBD) in 1993, recognizing the need to include cities in biodiversity preservation 
action plans (Puppim de Oliveira et al., 2011). In 2009, the City Biodiversity Index (CBI) was developed 
specifically for urban area. Its revised version (2020) is composed of 28 indicators grouped into three 
categories: Native Biodiversity in the City, Ecosystems Services provided by Biodiversity and 
Governance and Management of Biodiversity (Chan et al., 2020). Uchiyama et al. (2015) identified that 
the main drawbacks of the CBI are the complex data collection of Native Biodiversity indicators and 
the establishment of the spatial territory. Pierce et al. (2020) also shows a lack of alignment between 
indicators developed in the CBI and actions implemented by policy makers, preventing a possible 
feedback loop to attest the efficiency of these actions. Besides, the measurement of indicators of  
existing methods is often complex and time consuming which is not suitable for policy makers and the 
urgency to act (Spangenberg, 2007). Among these other methods, most focused on one type of 
indicator such as habitat connectivity (Deslauriers et al., 2018; Vasiljević et al., 2018) or ES (Ahern et 
al., 2014; Andersson-Sköld et al., 2018; Basnou et al., 2020). Some methods provide a framework for 
biodiversity as a whole, but they differ in how biodiversity is defined and how actions are integrated 
into urban planning (Pierce et al., 2020). Recommendations regarding temporal frequency of 
measurement are also rarely given by methodological frameworks. Given the biodiversity crisis, there 
is an urgent need for cities to act but so far, no study has provided a scientific framework allowing the 
comprehension of urban biodiversity as a whole and the development of adapted actions. 
This study aims to address those gaps and develop a methodology for policy makers to (i) gauge the 
biodiversity state of an urban system, including the potential of involvement of concerned 
stakeholders, (ii) identify biodiversity hotspots and areas of improvement within the city, (iii) 
recommend policy actions and other urban initiatives and monitor their effects on biodiversity state. 
The developed framework and its associated methodology are presented in Section 2, the 
recommended set of indicators and actions followed by results from a case study are presented in 
Section 3.

2. Methods and materials 
2.1. Definition of urban biodiversity  
Literature search has shown a lack of a clear definition of urban biodiversity. Studies rarely mentioned 
its definition and when they do, it lacks details and remains limited to unspecific characteristics such 
as “ biodiversity located within the spatially defined city” (Uchida et al., 2020).  
There is no generally agreed definition of an urban system and its perception varies over countries and 
actors (Mori et al., 2015). Criteria used to determine the urban borders are administrative definition, 
i.e. the geographical area under the jurisdiction of corresponding authority (Altherr, 2007; Larondelle 
and Haase, 2013), population density (Altherr, 2007; Bryant, 2006), functionality (Uchida et al., 2020) 
or morphological attributes such as extent of built infrastructures or area of dominated human 
landscape (Uchida et al., 2020). Albertí et al. (2017) showed that most used criteria are population 
density and administrative area of influence. As the proposed framework is dedicated to policy makers, 
the administrative border of the urban area is relevant as being their area of influence. However, to 
differentiate rural cities form urban cities, a criterion of density of population is needed. The FAO 
defines an urban cluster as a “contiguous grid cells of 1 km² with a density of at least 300 inhabitants 
per km² and has a population of at least 5,000 in the cluster” (Statistical Commission, 2020). In the 
current study, this definition is therefore used as a condition to define an urban system, which must 
either fulfil it or be part of another urban system meeting its requirements. 
The term biodiversity, contraction of biological and diversity, was first presented in 1992 at the Earth 
Summit of Rio as “the variety and variability among living organisms and the ecological complexes in 
which they occur” (U.S. Congress and Assessment Office of Technology, 1987). Noss (1990) added that 
this diversity is addressed at four levels (genes, species, ecosystems, and landscape), characterised by 
three attributes (composition, structure, and function).  The composition diversity deals with variety 
and identity of genes, populations, ecosystems, and landscape, the structural attribute refers to the 
organisational pattern from genetic to landscape scale, and the functional diversity assesses the 
function of the components. For example, two species having the same feeding mechanisms answer 
to the same functionality (Reed , 1990).  
All previously mentioned levels of biodiversity are not equally relevant when considering urban 
systems. First, measuring biodiversity at the genetic level is time consuming and can be indirectly 
assessed by fragmentation and connectivity of the territory (Deslauriers et al., 2018), thus falling under 
landscape level. Regarding species level, biodiversity in urban area may differ from the native 
biodiversity -  “species or lower taxon living within its natural range (past or present) including the area 
which it can reach and occupy using its natural dispersal systems” (Convention on Biological Diversity 
(CBD), n.d.) - due to the introduction of exotic species, for ornamental plantation for example. 
Emphasis must be made on native biodiversity as it is important for the resilience of the territory 
(Werner, 2011). If considering exotic species is necessary to understand biodiversity state, actions for 
preservation and restoration will aim at favouring native species. Then, these levels are not exhaustive 
for urban biodiversity assessment and the need of a multidisciplinary approach is recognized (Haines-
Young, 2009). Biodiversity in urban system cannot be assessed without including anthropogenic factors 
as biotic and abiotic components of  cities are continuously modified by anthropogenic activities 
(Uchida et al., 2020). Finally, many stakeholders interact in cities and their behaviour can impact 
positively or negatively urban biodiversity. Understanding these interactions is important to identify 
areas of concern  and induce behavioural change (Yli-Pelkonen and Niemelä, 2005). For example, 
private land can be relevant areas of conservation where inhabitants and companies involvement is 
needed (Black et al., 2017; Shwartz et al., 2012).

In this way, urban biodiversity can be defined as the native biodiversity, from species to landscape 
level, located within the previously defined urban system. The assessment of urban biodiversity must 
be done by considering anthropogenic pressures as well as political, social, and economic 
considerations. This categorisation of urban biodiversity will be used as a basis for indicator selection. 
 
2.2. Assessment framework 
The proposed methodology to measure and improve urban biodiversity is a multi-step iterative one as 
illustrated in Figure 1. In Step 1, practitioners define the geographical border of the area of study and 
provide the following generic information: geographical location, and density of population. 
Geographical location has a strong effect on species diversity (Chan et al., 2014) and is important for 
data treatment while density of population provides a first information on the type of city. In Step 2, 
data are collected and treated for indicators calculation. This step is the most time consuming and 
requires the involvement of other stakeholders. Indicators are then normalised based on target values 
to identify areas of concern. Then Step 3 consists of selecting relevant actions for biodiversity 
preservation and improvement from previously identified areas of concern. This method is iterative, 
and Steps 2 and 3 are to be reapplied 3 years after the first iteration. Thus, data used for the calculation 
of the indicators must be evaluated over the three-year period preceding the corresponding iteration. 
Iterating the framework allows the measurement of impact of the implemented actions but also the 
identification of new prioritisation area and the definition of a new action plan for the city.

2.3. Critical review of indicators 
2.3.1.  Identification of studies 
Only scientific reports were considered with a few exceptions of public institution reports regarding 
national or international biodiversity indicators and targets guidelines. Studies were selected using 
both Web of Science search engine (https://login.webofknowledge.com) and Google Scholar search 
engine (https://scholar.google.fr/). Google scholar was only used to retrieve studies from 2019 and 
2021 that may not have been indexed in Web of Science yet at the time of the review. The following 
procedures was applied to retrieve studies.  First, studies on biodiversity at urban scale were selected. 
Studies on biodiversity at a bigger or smaller scale than urban systems (e.g.: parks, regions, countries)

2.4.3.  Correlation analysis 
To investigate possible correlations between indicators, a cross-correlation analysis was done using 
Microsoft Office Excel. Relationships were investigated using regression analysis in R software. Species 
inventories were found to be largely incomplete for some cities of Lyon metropolis, hence subsets of 
data were generated, with two groups: (i) the 30 top French cities, offering complete data set (i.e. 
designated as Group A), and (ii) the Lyon’s metropolis cities (i.e. Group B). Species indicators 
relationship were only investigated for Group A. 
2.5. Critical review of actions 
2.5.1. Identification of studies 
The studies retrieved for the critical review of indicators that were including actions for urban 
biodiversity preservation (see Section 2.3.1) were completed by a second literature review to identify 
actions applicable by policy makers to preserve urban biodiversity. Once again, both Web of Science 
(https://login.webofknowledge.com) and Google Scholar search engines (https://scholar.google.fr/) 
were used with Google scholar restrained to studies from 2019 and 2021 that may not have been 
indexed in Web of Science yet at the time of the review. Only scientific studies in English were retained, 
which resulted in 484 studies corresponding to the key words “urban” AND “biodiversity” AND 
“actions” and 233 studies answering to “city” AND “biodiversity” AND “actions”. The full list of 
retrieved actions is presented in Table S18. 
2.5.2. Shortlisting of actions 
Three levels of actions for policy makers were identified. Actions can first be in the form of awareness 
raising campaigns or educative programs, to increase knowledge of targeted stakeholders (Shwartz et 
al., 2012). Then, some actions can directly modify or interact with the urban fabric (e.g.: modification 
of built infrastructures), called field actions. Finally, policy makers can use regulations to induce 
changes and increase number of stakeholders involved.  Actions were thus classified according to their 
level of actions and were also categorised according to their area of implementation in the city (e.g.: 
built infrastructures, transportation, inhabitants…).   
Actions not in the scope of policy makers or too wide actions, not detailed enough by authors were 
disregarded from the review. Very specific actions (e.g.: specific to a city in particular) were not 
retained but could lead to the creation of generic actions which can be implemented in all cities. Finally, 
this obtained list of actions was completed, when necessary, by identifying missing elements following 
the classification of action’s level of implementation and categories of area of implementation.

2.6. Case study  
2.6.1. Presentation of Albigny-sur-Saône municipality 
The methodological framework was tested on Albigny-sur-Saône municipality, located in France, in 
Lyon metropolis. Albigny-sur-Saône has a density of population of 1,121 inhabitants/km² for a total 
surface of 2.61 km². If its population is below 5,000 inhabitants, Albigny-sur-Saône is part of Lyon urban 
area which fulfils the FAO definition of an urban system (see Section 2.1). 
2.6.2. Retained indicators and data sources 
Retained indicators belong to categories Nature, Pressure, Governance, Economic actors and 
partnerships and Social. A summary of the main data sources is presented in Table 4. Data for Nature 
and Pressure indicators were collected from public databases and literature searches.  
Data for both Governance and Economic actors and partnerships indicators were assessed during a 
face-to-face interview with a representative of the municipality's environmental department. A 
document was prepared beforehand and distributed to the representant of the municipality to guide 
the interview. More information can be found under Supporting Information (Section S.1.2). 
Social information was collected using a face-to-face survey followed by an online survey. In both 
surveys, respondents were asked to provide personal information such as age, professional activity, or 
type of housing. Then, social indicators were converted into different areas of investigation. Likert-
type scale questions (R Likert, 1932) were preferably used for the online-survey as to evaluate 
respondent preferences on a fixed scale. For example, respondents were asked to give their opinion 
on the implementation of municipal actions on a scale from 1 to 4 where 1 is “completely against” and 
4 is “completely in favour”. The face-to-face questionnaire was mainly composed of open-ended 
questions as to encourage the dialogue with the respondent and to avoid guiding the respondent’s 
answer. The face-to-face interviews were conducted on the city’s market. Following those interviews, 
the online survey was distributed to inhabitants by the municipality using social media and the 
municipality’s newsletter. Complete surveys are presented in Supporting Information (Section S.1.3).

3.1. Application to French cities 
3.1.1. Variability of indicators 
Figure 3 illustrates the distribution of indicators Share of natural areas, Habitat diversity, 
Fragmentation of natural areas (degree of coherence), and Share of sealed surfaces for the 87 studied 
cities (see Section 2.4.1 for presentation of cities). Complete results are presented in Supporting 
Information (Table S20). Studied cities have in average 20% of natural areas, which is equal to the 
recommended value for cities given by the CBI (Chan et al., 2020). However, natural areas are highly 
fragmented in those cities, with a degree of coherence equal to 1.4%. Indeed, taking the average 
proportion of natural areas, if all natural areas were connected, the degree of coherence would be 
equal to 4%. Then, the average share of sealed surface in the tested cities is equal to 34% which is 
below the recommended value (40%) given by the CBI (Chan et al., 2020). This difference of variation 
between the proportion of natural areas and the share of sealed surface is explained by the presence 
of green areas and private gardens, no being part of natural areas but counted as permeable surfaces. 
Finally, average Habitat diversity is equal to 1.4, which is nearly two times less than the maximum 
possible habitat diversity (see Section S.1.4).  
The variability of both proportion of natural areas and share of sealed surface is quite important with 
values within the respective range of 0-52% and 4-70%. Those variations can be explained by different 
level of urbanisation inside cities and different historical urbanisation dynamics. For instance, cities 
such as Paris, heart of a metropolis, tend to have higher level of urbanisation than peri-urban cities, 
such as Albigny-sur-Saône (see Section 2.6.1). Besides, the Share of sealed surface is found to be 
correlated with density of population (see Section 3.1.2 below). Finally, similar observation can be 
made for habitat diversity. The range of variation is quite important (0.65-1.90) and can be explained 
by different level of urbanisation but also by cities geographical location.

3.1.2. Relationship between indicators 
From the cross-correlation analysis (see Section 2.4.3), a positive link was found between the Share of 
natural areas and the Fragmentation of natural areas (correlation factor = 0.78). Fragmentation of 
natural areas is a function of natural areas surfaces, which explains this dependency. The two 
indicators are to be kept due to their complementarity in understanding the distribution of natural 
areas in cities.  Then, a positive correlation was also found between PM10 and NO2 (correlation factor 
= 0.90) as NO2 is a secondary precursor of PM10. However, as will be presented below they have 
different impacts on biodiversity and keeping track of these two pollutants is relevant. A positive 
correlation was identified between Habitat diversity and the Number of native bird species (correlation 
factor = 0.86). The two indicators are kept as measuring habitat diversity provides an understanding 
of the type of habitat and bird diversity is an indicator of ecosystem health (European Environment 
Agency (EEA), 2007). A dependency was identified between Light pollution and the Share of sealed 
surfaces (correlation factor = 0.89). The Share of sealed surfaces is mainly impacted by buildings and 
roads infrastructures which are the main sources of light pollutions (Kyba et al., 2015). These two 
indicators must be kept as their impacts on biodiversity are very different. All other indicators were 
found to display no correlation. 
Correlation between indicators and city surface, average income, and population density were also 
investigated. Population density was found to be correlated with both Light pollution (correlation 
factor = 0.71) and the Share of sealed surfaces (correlation factor = 0.73), possibly due to increasing 
buildings and human activity. This parameter is to be given in Step 1 to give a first understanding of 
the city.  No correlation was identified for average income or city surface. Complete results of the 
correlation analysis are presented in Supporting Information (see Table S22).  
Then, relevant results from the regression analysis are presented below (see Table S14 in Supporting 
Information for complete results). 
Native species. Native bird species diversity was found to be significantly correlated with both Habitat 
diversity (p<0.01) and Native plant species diversity (p<0.01) while Native lepidoptera species was 
found to be correlated with Native plant species diversity (p<0.01). These observations are consistent 
with results found by Sandström et al. (2006) and Threlfall et al. (2016) who identified a strong 
correlation between vegetation diversity and bird species diversity. Similarly, Wang et al. (2020a) 
studied a link between vegetation diversity and lepidoptera species diversity.   
Natural areas. It is often observed that species diversity increases with natural areas size in cities  
(Honnay et al., 1999; Jooss et al., 2009; Sandström et al., 2006; Soga et al., 2014) and decreases with 
fragmentation of natural areas (Di Giulio et al., 2009; Taylor et al., 2010). These two indicators are built 
upon the island biogeography theory which shows the importance of both patch size and patch 
connectivity to preserve diversity and prevent local extinction (Jooss et al., 2009). If no correlation was 
identified between species diversity and natural areas, correlation was identified between the Share 
of threatened species and the Share of natural areas (p<0.01). Linear regressions were also observed 
between the logarithm of Native bird diversity and the logarithm of Fragmentation of natural areas 
(p<0.01) on one side and the logarithm of Native lepidoptera diversity and the logarithm of 
Fragmentation of natural areas (p<0.01) on the other side. If those observations are consistent with 
previously mentioned studies, no evidence of such strong relationship was found in literature. 
For other pressure indicators, no relation was identified but punctual studies justify the importance of 
considering these metrics for cities.

Light pollution. Light pollution disturbs seasonal and biological cycles of species and prey-predator 
relationships (Dominoni et al., 2013). Laforge et al. (2019) studied the positive effect of reducing light 
pollution on bats population and recommended measuring these metrics for urban biodiversity 
preservation.  
Air pollution. O3 is responsible for necrosis and chlorosis of plants through stomata uptake – absorption 
by plant leaves - (Ågren and Andersson, 2011). The impact of NO2 on ecosystems is more complex. It 
is a matter of concern as a precursor of O3, participating in soil acidification but also reducing plant’s 
growth through stomatal uptake (Ågren and Andersson, 2011). Critical values for ecosystems were 
thus defined for O3 and NOx (European Environment Agency (EEA), n.d.; WHO Regional Office for 
Europe, 2001).  As for PM10, Sterzyńska et al. (2018) found out microarthropods populations decrease 
with particulate matter pollution in urban areas.  
Water regulation: share of sealed surface. The effect of soil sealing, not considering habitat 
destruction, on species diversity is more difficult to assess but is recognized as disturbing water cycle 
in cities, preventing natural filtration in the soil (Chan et al., 2020). Eftene et al. (2016) studied the 
impact of soil sealing on the deterioration of soil properties, affecting vegetation.  
Average income and city surface. Regressions were also investigated between indicators and average 
income or city surface, but no relevant relations were identified. However, Acar et al. (2007) studied 
that ornamental vegetation structure is correlated with standard of living in residential areas. 
Complementary, Kinzig et al. (2005) and Melles (2005) found that native bird species abundance 
increases with neighbourhood wealth. Considering average income at city level does not allow the 
consideration of neighbourhood disparity within cities, explaining why such observation could not be 
seen. 
3.1.3. Limitations of the statistical analysis 
The lack of completeness of some species data is the main limitation of this statistical analysis and 
could explain that some correlations could not be identified. For some cities, databases were quite 
complete between 2000 and 2010 but showed an important lack of data between 2010 and 2020. 
Thus, correlations including species indicators were only investigated for cities of group A (see Section 
2.4.3), reducing the size of the dataset. This analysis could also be completed by a dynamic study, 
measuring indicators at several time steps in each city to observe more correlations. This could not 
have been done in this study due to data limitation. Finally, Governance, Economic actors and 
Partnerships and Social indicators could not be included in this statistical analysis due to data 
unavailability, but such analysis could be relevant to complete the understanding on the relationship 
between indicators.

which may be driven by other motives than the state of biodiversity. However, such information could

not be found in the literature review for the following indicators: Actors invested in biodiversity in the 
city, Laws in favour of biodiversity and Education in school. They could be edited based on results from 
application of the framework in different cities. As for native species indicators (5-7), no target values 
are provided as the number of native species depends on geographical localisation of the city (Chan et 
al., 2014). It is recommended to practitioners to use average number of species in the region. Finally, 
no scientifically based targets could be identified for the Share of natural areas but practitioners can 
use national, regional or municipal targets for this indicator. 
It was decided to consider equal weighting of each indicator to identify areas of concern, due to lack 
of scientifical knowledge on their relative influence on urban biodiversity.

3.2.3.  Linking indicators and actions to identify priority actions 
Each action can have a direct, or an indirect impact on several indicators. Conclusions from literature 
review were used to assess impacts of actions. Creating several scenarios was necessary to quantify 
the impact of some actions. For instance, the impact of the creation of a shared garden is different if 
it is built on a build-up area or on a green area. Qualitative links were defined based on the strength 
of the impacts (“NA” when the action does not result in non-negligible changes of an indicator, and 
then gradually “low”, “moderate” and “large”) and represented in a generic matrix (see Table 7 below). 
The full matrix and detailed explanations on each link are respectively presented in Table S24 and Table 
S25. 
Normalisation of indicators allows the identification of areas of concern for urban biodiversity. From 
this, practitioners can identify in the generic matrix actions to reach targeted values of indicators. 
Priority actions are thus selected and a municipal action plan for urban biodiversity can be defined. 
Practitioners may then need to include other parameters in the definition of the action plans than 
indicators targeted values. Information collected from both social and governance surveys can help 
policy makers choose between equivalent actions, or in some cases eliminate some actions already 
implemented by the municipalities or by other actors. For instance, policy makers must consider 
willingness of inhabitants to see developed some actions in the municipality. Besides, some actions 
are complementary, and such complementarity is indicated in Table S23. For example, lowering 
intensity of management of some green areas leading to unmown lawn can be complemented by the 
use of educative panels to ensure public acceptation (Ramer and Nelson, 2020).  This example shows 
how awareness raising actions can be used to induce behavioural change to implement some field 
actions (Le Roux et al., 2014). Then, economic, political, and physical constraints (e.g.: presence of built 
infrastructures in the soil preventing revegetation of an area) can be limiting factors for the 
implementation of actions that policy makers may also have to consider. 
Actions’ impact on indicators could not be assessed quantitatively for two reasons. First, some actions 
lack scientific analysis monitoring and studying their effectiveness on urban biodiversity, some of 
which may be associated with time lag before effects can occur, e.g. restoration projects. Then, 
quantifying with precision such impact would require investigating several scenarios for each action as 
the number of parameters responsible for impact tends to be important. For instance, vegetation 
removal of air pollution depends on the canopy cover, on the type of vegetation, but also on the 
distance of vegetation to road traffic. Such a detailed level of connections was considered outside the 
screening scope of this study but should be considered in future work. Finally, future work could also 
improve precision of the generic matrix by using future literature results or by using future iterations 
of the framework on cities. By implementing this framework, practitioners will be able to measure the 
impact of tested actions on the indicators, thus precising the relationships between indicators and 
actions.

3.3. Case studies results 
3.3.1. Indicators results and identification of areas of concern 
Normalised scores (designated as NSq for quantitative indicators and NSsq for semi-quantitative 
indicators) are presented in Table 8, following the methodology presented in Section 3.2.1. More 
information, including indicators value and specificity on target values are presented in Supporting 
Information (see Section S.2.2). 
It can be said that Albigny-sur-Saône has an important proportion of natural areas (NSq <1), that are 
connected (NSq <1) and protected (NSq <1). Besides, ecological networks are recognised by the 
municipality and preserved (NSsq=1). However, Albigny-sur-Saône could favour biodiversity (see NSq 
>3.5 for Native bird species) by increasing habitat diversity (NSq >2) and by focusing on the 
management of green areas (NSsq >1.5) to increase their quality for wildlife. This point was highlighted 
by one inhabitant, saying that Albigny-sur-Saône offers many natural spaces and green areas, but that 
priority should be put on increasing their quality and on preserving them from future construction (see 
Section S.2.3.1). Specific actions could also be carried out to protect threatened species (NSq >5). The 
restoration of degraded areas (NSq >10) is also a lever of actions to favour biodiversity and light 
pollution seems to be the main pressure affecting biodiversity (NSq >4).  
Then, it seems that the municipality lacks knowledge on which past actions were carried out, or which 
regulations relate to biodiversity and how the municipal budget is used for such actions. Indeed, data 
could not be collected for those indicators. Difficulty to collect information for governance indicators 
is also a sign that urban biodiversity consideration is new for the municipality and that this framework 
can help policy makers take the first step. Regarding Economic actors and partnership indicators, it 
seems that the municipality could increase in action efficiency by creating partnerships with invested 
actors in the territory (NSq >10) but also with non-invested actors (NSq>6), thus increasing the number 
of mobilised actors (NSq=5). The municipality could also consider creating more areas for urban 
agriculture (NSq >5). 
Inhabitants are quite sensitive to biodiversity (NSsq <1.25) but lack knowledge on biodiversity 
challenges (NSsq >1.67). They are eager for more actions both in private and public areas for 
biodiversity preservation (NSsq <1.25). However, a low number of respondents (less than 1% of 
inhabitants) answered the online survey following face-to-face interviews to collect social information 
and a statistical analysis of the answers could not be conducted. It seems that the choice of the 
communication channel (social media and municipality newsletter) was not appropriate for such issue 
or that most inhabitants do not feel concerned by such survey. If conclusion from the pool of 
respondents cannot be generalised to the entire city, it shows that some inhabitants are very 
implicated for biodiversity in the city and could be used by the municipality to create a dynamic to 
reach more inhabitants. 
Data could not be collected for all indicators, either due to time limitation or to data unavailability. 
This concerns the following indicators: Air Pollution: SO2, Number of native vascular plant species, 
Share of invasive alien species, Share of municipal budget allocated to biodiversity, Number of 
projects/strategies/actions on biodiversity implemented by the municipality annually, Laws in favour 
of biodiversity, and Share of urban agriculture area under sustainable practices (See Section S.2.3.2 in 
Supporting Information). If the indicator Share of invasive alien species could not be computed due to 
inventories incompleteness, exchange with the municipality on green areas management showed the 
presence of Japanese knotweed, an invasive plant alien species. Thus, the municipality should 
complete inventories on local species to cover this knowledge gap.

3.3.2. Recommended actions 
Recommended action plan for Albigny-sur-Saône municipality is presented in Figure 4 below. First, it 
is recommended for the municipality to collect more information on municipal budget, actions and 
laws dedicated to biodiversity to evaluate missing indicators at the time of this study, which could lead 
to some adjustment of the proposed action plan. 
==PAGE:28==
Master thesis: Indicator for gauging and managing the biodiversity state in cities  
DTU 
21 
 
It is recommended to create partnerships with existing actors for biodiversity and relevant ones. For 
instance, the city is divided along a north-south axis by a railway. Partnering with the SNCF (French 
national companies in charge of railway networks) should be relevant to favour biodiversity on the 
slopes along the railway.  
Another priority for the city is to increase habitat diversity and ensure quality of existing natural and 
semi-natural areas. This could be done by implementing differentiate management plan in green areas 
or by revegetating specific areas. An important lever of actions would be to vegetate the geriatric 
centre who covers 63 ha and has an important share of lawns, with very rare vegetation. This ties in 
with the importance of setting up partnerships with private actors to promote biodiversity. Regulation 
on permit approvals could also be reviewed to limit urbanisation near natural areas. The municipality 
could also invest in parcels and create shared gardens, fulfilling the need to increase habitat diversity 
and areas of urban agriculture but also to involve citizens. 
Then, to reduce light pollution, the municipality should consider creating an ecological network for 
nocturnal species. This action could be preceded by a reduction of public lighting and regulation on 
advertising panels and shops lighting as such measures are often in conflict with safety in the city. If 
this action seems well received by interviewed inhabitants, the results cannot be generalised to the 
entire city (see Section 3.3.1). 
Strengthening species inventories is another area of concerns for Albigny-sur-Saône to ensure species 
protection, prioritising threatened species. A reduction of birds, bats and squirrels population in the 
forest surrounding the city was observed by some inhabitants reinforcing the need for specific actions 
for species protection. The municipality could consider partnering with specific association such as the 
LPO (French association for fauna protection) to quickly increase knowledge on local species. 
Increasing habitat diversity (see points above) and installing nesting areas for some species could help 
solved this issue. The latter could be done to prevent destruction of bats habitat in the 
decommissioned cement factory where work is planned. This degraded area could be restored as 
natural habitat, but it has already been decided to build new houses there. 
Finally, involving inhabitants remains an area of concern for the municipality. Due to lack of answers 
to the social survey, the municipality should strengthen its communication on biodiversity. Besides, 
part of natural areas belongs to inhabitants that may not always be aware of biodiversity issue. In the 
long-term, a relevant action could be to create a biodiversity charter with inhabitants to ensure 
preservation of private areas. This need to be preceded by awareness raising actions on urban 
biodiversity issues, focusing on the management of private gardens for urban biodiversity. The 
municipality could consider creating regular communication on biodiversity and offering workshops as 
they seem to be more attractive to inhabitants (see Section S.2.2.1) Participative inventories could also 
be carried out to involve inhabitants, also allowing collecting more information on species (see above). 
The municipality could also involve children through programs at school or through the CME (Children 
Municipal Council), who was identified as a dynamic actor for urban biodiversity, reaching many 
inhabitants. Indeed, it was already volunteer for the restoration of the fitness trail by including 
elements on biodiversity. 
Due to time constraint of the study, only one partial iteration of the framework could be done. To be 
complete, the case study should have included the implementation of this action plan in the city to 
measure the feedback loop of these actions on the indicators.

3.4. Biodiversity preservation, as a way to increase well-being in cities  
In addition to contributing to ecosystems quality, biodiversity contributes to city’s resilience by 
ensuring ecosystem services (see Section 1) indispensable for good quality of life in cities. First, 
biodiversity provides provisioning services such as food or water supply. For instance, cities may rely 
on local groundwater tables for their drinking water supply which are directly dependant on 
ecosystem’s health (Gómez-Baggethun et al., 2013). Cities depend mainly on surrounding countryside 
for their food supply and the development of peri-urban farms, and urban farms (e.g.: on roofs, shared 
gardens) contribute to ensuring food security (Gómez-Baggethun et al., 2013; Martellozzo et al., 2014) 
while favouring habitat for wildlife. Biodiversity in cities also provides regulating services and ensures 
urban temperature and noise regulation or increase air quality. It also contributes to the moderation 
of climate extremes. Besides, nature in cities provides cultural services by offering recreational areas 
or increasing cities’ aesthetic values (Gómez-Baggethun et al., 2013). 
Finally, all those services contribute to public health. A feature of the 21st century is that for the first 
time, accidents and contagious diseases are secondary causes of mortality. Non-communicable 
diseases such as cancer, obesity or lung diseases are responsible for most deaths. Many studies agree 
that urban environment and urban way of life contribute to the development of these diseases but 
that favouring nature in cities can be a major tool for public health (Voisin-Bormuth, 2020). A simple 
example is that inhabitants having access to natural areas during coronavirus shutdown benefited from 
better living condition. Nature in city reduces stress and anxiety, reduces factors of depression, 
restores attention, or even promotes social cohesion (Meyer-Grandbastien et al., 2021). Considering 
the current corona crisis, it is relevant to add that biodiversity preservation is also indispensable to 
limit the spread of viruses, the latest being facilitated by genetic diversity reduction and species 
extinction (Azat, 2021).  
 
3.5. Relevance and operability of the framework 
3.5.1.  Relevance of the framework for policy makers 
This framework is comprehensive for practitioners and efficient to address the measure and the 
management of urban biodiversity. Step 1 is important to launch the study with practitioners and 
policy makers.  
Step 2, through data collection, provides a first communication on the method and is in a way, a first 
awareness raising action. The representant of Albigny-sur-Saône in charge of the project said learning 
a lot from this collection phase as it made him aware of some aspects, he had little knowledge on. 
Similarly, the questionnaire that was distributed to the municipality to collect information for 
governance indicators (see Section 2.6.2), was a source of reconsideration for many actors inside the 
municipality especially regarding management of green areas. It seems that for many years, the same 
intensive management was applied and never questioned. Some municipal actors were also surprised 
to see that biodiversity issues could be raised beyond management of green areas. Besides, calculation 
methods for indicators were chosen first on their scientific robustness but also on their accessibility 
for practitioners ensuring comprehension of policy makers. 
As for Step 3, it answers the need to move from indicators to actions (Pierce et al., 2020). The generic 
matrix provides an overview of different actions that can be implemented by a municipality and is 
comprehensive to select priority actions. 
==PAGE:31==
Master thesis: Indicator for gauging and managing the biodiversity state in cities  
DTU 
24 
 
Another strength of the framework is its use of a multidisciplinary approach which is indispensable for 
the comprehension of urban biodiversity (Haines-Young, 2009). The implementation of the framework 
at Albigny-sur-Saône has shown the importance of citizen’s consultation. For instance, many 
interesting observations on species depletion on specific areas were given by citizens during the face-
to-face interview, allowing further guidance for actions selection. This is consistent with observations 
from Black et al. (2017) who studied the importance of citizen’s consultation prior to municipal actions, 
mainly to engage more effectively inhabitants. 
3.5.2.  Usefulness of an iterative framework 
As presented in Section 2.2, indicators should be measured every 3 years for impact assessment of the 
actions and redefinition of the action plan. This iterative methodology has several advantages.   
First, it is useful to engage municipalities to biodiversity preservation for several years. In the case of 
Albigny-sur-Saône, policy makers were eager to commit the city to a multi-year biodiversity 
conservation policy and this framework was thus well-received.  
Then, iterating the framework ensure a regular follow-up and allows building perennial actions. Several 
iterations of the framework are necessary to reach all possible levels of urban biodiversity actions in 
the municipality. For instance, an iterative framework is useful to follow up with inhabitants. Social 
actions have a short-term impact on citizens, and offering long-lasting programs is important to ensure 
real behavioural change of inhabitants (Shwartz et al., 2012). 
Finally, not all indicators are expected to follow similar temporal variation after action implementation. 
If a variation of sociological, governance and economic factors is expected within this time scale, 
change in some nature indicators is more uncertain. Several iterations of the framework are necessary 
to observe the evolution of these indicators and thus to obtain impact assessment. As discussed in 
Section 3.2.3 the latter can also be used to strengthen the link between actions and indicators. 
3.5.3.  Adaptability of the framework to different geographical scales 
A limitation of this study is the hypothesis that the developed framework is relevant independently of 
city size (for city responding to the criteria defined in Section 2.1). As studied by Uchida et al. (2021), 
uncertainties remain in “how biodiversity scales with city size, and how ecological, evolutionary, and 
socioeconomic drivers of biodiversity scale with city size”. However, it can be expected that several 
implementations of the proposed framework, through the measure of feedback from the action plan 
could help increase the knowledge on the subject and strengthen the method.  
Besides, the framework provides a broad approach for cities to preserve local biodiversity, that could 
lead to more studies depending on conclusions and local needs. To engage further research and 
actions, some municipalities may find the need to study more precisely specific areas. To cover this 
issue, indicators could easily be adapted to smaller zones. For instance, Habitat diversity can cover a 
smaller area by considering other categories of habitats, other species can be monitored, or a precise 
map of light or air pollution can be done. Examples of indicators down-scaling are presented in 
Supporting Information (Table S15).  
3.5.4.  Other considerations for further research 
The proposed methodology aims at measuring and improving biodiversity within urban system 
boundaries. However, cities mainly impact biodiversity outside its boundaries through the 
consumption of raw materials for buildings, the importation of foods and other products to name but 
a few reasons. Cities occupy 2% of Earth surface and consume 75% of human resources (Oliveira 2011).  
==PAGE:32==
Master thesis: Indicator for gauging and managing the biodiversity state in cities  
DTU 
25 
 
This aspect is not measured by chosen indicators but is introduced by some actions, mainly through 
awareness raising actions, as a first approach on the subject (see Section 3.2.2). To complete this 
framework, methodology such as Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) could be applied on the whole urban 
system to have a complete cover of the city’s impacts on biodiversity. LCA is a methodology aiming at 
quantifying all environmental impact of a system, during its whole life cycle, i.e. from cradle-to-grave 
(Hauschild et al., 2018).   
Finally, climate change is one of the main cause of biodiversity loss (Balvanera and Pfaff, 2019) and 
species response to climate change (adaptation and inadaptation) remains uncertain, limiting some 
recommendations for policy makers. For example, it is recommended to policy makers to prioritize 
revegetation of urban areas using native plant species. However, some native species may not adapt 
to changing climate, and selections could be done within native species to select the ones the most 
susceptible to resist such change. Further research is necessary for specific recommendation regarding 
urban biodiversity preservation and adaptation to climate change.  
 
4. Conclusions and recommendations 
The aim of this study was to define a framework to gauge and manage urban biodiversity. A 3-steps 
iterative framework was thus developed ensuring the definition of the urban system (Step 1), the 
measurement of urban biodiversity indicators to identify areas of concern in the city (Step 2), and the 
selection of relevant actions based on the identified areas of concern (Step 3). Qualitative links were 
built between defined indicators and actions to visualise the expected impact of each action on each 
indicator to ensure the selection of actions. It was chosen to build an iterative framework to ensure 
the measurement of implemented actions’ impact but also to continuously adapt the action plan. 
The implementation of this framework at the municipality of Albigny-sur-Saône showed the relevance 
of using a multi-disciplinary framework to ensure a global understanding of urban biodiversity. Besides, 
it also showed the utility of this framework for policy makers to define an action plan at municipal 
level. Data collection phase is time-consuming and must be carefully done to guarantee completeness 
of the results. It is recommended to policy makers to choose the most adapted way of communication 
to collect data from inhabitants. Communication on the purpose of the framework could be given as 
to show the importance of their answers to citizens. 
Finally, this study highlighted some aspects where further research is needed. First, the link between 
actions and indicators could be strengthen by studying more precisely several scenarios for each action 
and by using feedback from future iteration of this framework. Then, future research could extend this 
framework by adding life cycle perspective to also attest cities imported impact on biodiversity. Future 
research could also provide specific recommendations to policy makers regarding urban biodiversity 
preservation to climate change.
