Abstract
Carbon capture sequestration and utilisation has been identiﬁed to play a key role in
dealing with carbon dioxide’s impact on global climate change. One of the main chal-
lenges faced by state-of-the-art solvent technology is the large energy consumption
and high costs incurred due to thermal degradation and corrosion in the regenera-
tion unit.
One of the principal applications of ultrasound is degassing of liquids, through cavi-
tation. Therefore, the current work aims at intensifying the desorption section by use
of ultrasound to enhance the liquid to gas mass transfer of CO2. Monoethanolamine
(MEA) was selected as the base solvent for carrying out the experiments. The inﬂu-
ence of operating parameters like the intensity of ultrasound, operating temperature,
concentration of MEA, and vapour reduction additives such as ethylene glycol and
urea were tested. These results were further compared to desorption by heat alone
and magnetic stirring.
The intensity of ultrasound was varied from 86W-147W. Two diﬀerent tempera-
ture, 60 and 80oC were tested, and three diﬀerent stirring speeds: 500, 700, and
1000 rpm were used. 30 and 10 wt% MEA and 30 wt% MEA + 15 wt% MEG/Urea
were the solvents used.
Ultrasound showed an improvement in the rate and amount of CO2 desorbed when
compared to heating alone. The desorption rate of ultrasound at 80oC and 86W
was higher than just heat by 12% as opposed to 5.7% by 147W. The desorption
at higher intensities is understood to be due to various counteracting forces and
transient cavitation. Magnetic stirring at 1000 rpm also exhibited an increase in
desorption rate by 13%.
At lower concentration of MEA, ultrasound exhibited desorption rates lower than
just heating at 86 W and 147 W. However, at 114 W, it improved the desorption by
5%. Stirring at 1000 rpm improved the rate by 7.5%
In the presence of MEG and Urea as additives, stirring improved the desorption
rate by 24.2% and 18% respectively, which is signiﬁcant. As opposed to 30 wt%
MEA, 147 W of ultrasound gave the best performance, improving the desorption
rate by 10.6% for MEG. For urea, 114 W increased the rate by 5.3%
Results of this study reveal that ultrasound can enhance the desorption rate of
CO2.

Executive Summary
This report consists of six chapters, which are drafted as follows:
Chapter 1 overviews the urgency of need of large scale deployment of carbon cap-
ture technology followed by a summary of the diﬀerent kinds of capture technologies
available. The state-of-the-art solvent technology is explained in detail along with
the need for the development of new technologies to counter the disadvantages of
high energy and costs associated with it.
Chapter 2 details the physics involved in the operation of ultrasound. The ap-
plication of ultrasound for degasiﬁcation of liquids and the theory behind cavita-
tion, bubble behaviour in acoustic ﬁelds are further detailed. The relevance of the
application of ultrasound for the regeneration unit in carbon capture technology is
explained.
Chapter 3 overviews the experiments carried out and reviews the materials and
equipment used.
The diﬀerent stages involved in constructing the experimental
setup and troubleshooting at each stage is detailed. Furthermore, the experimental
procedures involving the ultrasound bath and magnetic stirrer are presented.
Chapter 4 presents the theory and equations behind the determination of mass
transfer coeﬃcients and development of the Sherwood correlation based on the ex-
perimental results.
Chapter 5 documents the results of the experimental work and interprets them.
The preliminary results obtained by ultrasound-assisted desorption are presented
ﬁrst, followed by an analysis of the inﬂuence of intensity, temperature, the concen-
tration of MEA, and presence of Vapor Reduction Additives (VRAs). The same
is done for the magnetic stirring of the solvent, where the ﬁrst parameter changes
to stirring speed. This is followed by a concise comparison of all the experimen-
tal results. Furthermore, the overall liquid mass transfer coeﬃcients are presented,
followed by the depiction of the Sherwood correlation parameters. Besides, a com-
parison of the speciﬁc energy consumed by all the diﬀerent cases is demonstrated,
followed by a brief comparison of the current work’s results to literature. Finally, a
sensitivity analysis is provided on the mass transfer coeﬃcient as a function of the
density coeﬃcient.
Chapter 6 concludes the current work and gives a synopsis of the future work
that could be done.

Objective of the thesis
The objective of the thesis is to examine the use of mass transfer enhancing tech-
nique such as ultrasound in addition to conventional heating used for regeneration
of the amine solvents. Conventional strippers work at high operating conditions of
temperature and pressure to desorb the chemically absorbed CO2. This makes the
regeneration of the solvents very energy-intensive and also causes thermal degrada-
tion. One of the ways to reduce the high operating conditions is to optimise the
mass transfer kinetics of the process and ultrasound is a viable option because of
its ability to cause cavitation in liquids. Even though ultrasound cannot inﬂuence
the equilibrium of the process, it is expected to improve the rate of desorption. The
work done in this thesis is an attempt to validate this hypothesis.
The main solvent used in the experimentation is the established 30 wt% MEA.
Additionally, a few other solvents are also tested. Parameters like the intensity of ul-
trasound, temperature, magnetic stirring speed are varied to analyse their inﬂuence
on the process. The experimental analysis is followed by determining parameters
like mass transfer coeﬃcients.
The following are the learning objectives and diﬀerent tasks carried out:
• Collect and overview literature about carbon capture, existing methods for
solvent regeneration and their drawbacks.
• Discuss the theory behind the functioning of ultrasound and its industrial
applications.
• Design of the experimental setup.
• Plan and execute the experimental work.
• Compare the results of the diﬀerent methods adopted.
• Compare of the results with literature.
• Determine and analyse the mass transfer coeﬃcients and develop correlations
where applicable.
• Evaluate the practical applicability of the process at an industrial scale, in
terms of energy consumed and eﬃciency.
• Report and present the work.

Methodology
This section gives an overview of the methods adopted to achieve the objective stated
Literature Review
The literature review was done throughout the thesis, with most of it being done in
the ﬁrst month. Search engines like "Google Scholar", "Researchgate", and "DTU-
Findit" were used by employing keywords such as carbon capture, solvent regenera-
tion, ultrasound for CO2 desorption, reaction mechanism, kinetics, gas-liquid mass
transfer, among various others. Work previously done by authors such as Ying,
Eimer, Tanaka, and Muthupandian was speciﬁcally referred to, and a further search
was done based on the references in these works. The literature search also is done
to obtain values and equations to determine properties such as viscosity, density,
Henry’s coeﬃcients required for the determination of mass transfer coeﬃcients. Ad-
ditionally, the theory required to analyse the mass transfer kinetics was obtained
through a mixture of published papers and books related to process engineering and
mass transfer operations.
Experimental Work
The experimental work started with the purchase of the ultrasound bath. The bath
was chosen based on the literature survey. Following this, several experiments were
carried out to reach the exact conﬁguration that worked. Four diﬀerent solvents (30
wt% MEA, 10 wt% MEA, 30 wt% + 15 wt% MEG, 30 wt% MEA + 15 wt% Urea)
were tested at diﬀerent operating parameters. The loadings of the solvents were
determined by collecting samples during the experiments at regular time intervals
and measuring their densities.
Discussion
The interpretation of the results was made based on the theoretical knowledge ob-
tained through a literature survey. The multiphysics of ultrasound is very compli-
cated, which is out of the current work’s scope. Therefore, relevant factors have
been considered in the analysis of the results. The determination of mass transfer
coeﬃcient, linear and parametric ﬁttings were done by Excel.
The report was written in Overleaf, and an electronic version of the report is sub-
mitted on July 26, 2019.

Introduction
1.1
Global warming and CO2 emissions
Global warming is deﬁned as an increase in a combined air and sea surface temper-
atures averaged over the globe and 30 years [1]. Global warming is one of the conse-
quences of increasing greenhouse gas emissions caused as a result of anthropogenic
activities like burning of fossil fuels, deforestation, industrialization, etc. In its Fifth
Assessment Report, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) con-
cluded that there’s a more than 95 per cent probability that human activities over
the past 50 years have warmed our planet [2]. The greenhouse gases contributing to
global warming include water vapour, CO2, CH4, Chloroﬂuorocarbons (CFCs).The
main contributors to these greenhouse gases are presented in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: The primary sources and their respective contribution to production of
greenhouse gas emissions [5].
Figure 1.1 depicts that energy production of all forms accounts for 72% of the green-
house gas emissions. The primary sources of energy production predicted for the

1.1. Global warming and CO2 emissions
1.1.1
Impact of global warming
The rising temperatures due to global warming are resulting in the melting of the
glaciers and sea ice, changing precipitation patterns, and setting animals on the
move. In addition to the rising temperatures, there have been complex shifts in
our planets’ weather and climate systems, commonly known as climate change [7].
Extreme weather conditions, rising seas, endangerment of wildlife, increase in pre-
cipitation levels, higher frequency of droughts, deﬁcit of drinking water, and spread
of diseases are some of the signiﬁcant consequences of climate change. Discharge of
more greenhouse gases into the atmosphere only worsens the situation.
However, as depicted by Figure 1.2, even though the use of renewable sources of
energy is expected to increase, most of the world’s energy is still forecasted to be
from the use of fossil fuels, which would result in the addition of CO2 to the atmo-
sphere.
1.1.2
Mitigation of CO2 emissions

1.2
Carbon Capture Technologies
Various technologies for separating CO2 from a mixture of gases are commercially
available and widely used for puriﬁcation in industrial processes.
Since most of
the anthropogenic CO2 emissions are a byproduct of the combustion of fossil fu-
els, capture technologies are commonly classiﬁed as either pre-combustion or post-
combustion systems, depending on whether CO2 is removed before or after the fuel
is burned. Another approach, known as the oxyfuel or oxy-combustion, does not
require a CO2 capture system. This technology is still under development and is
not yet commercial in power plant operations even though some industries like glass
and metal do employ it [1].
The CO2 captured by these methods is sold as a commodity or as a raw material to
nearby industries or permanently sequestered, typically in a geological formation.
Globally, however, only a small amount of CO2 is utilized to manufacture industrial
products [1].
1.2.1
Post-combustion Carbon Capture
Post-combustion processes capture CO2 from the ﬂue gases produced after fossil
fuels, or other carbonaceous materials are burned.
In a coal-ﬁred power plant,
pulverized coal is mixed with air and ignited in a furnace or boiler and the heat
liberated is used to produce steam which drives a turbine-generator. The hot ex-
haust gases are a mixture of nitrogen, water vapour, and carbon dioxide. Additional
products like sulfur dioxide, nitrogen oxides, and ﬂy ash are also formed depending
on the impurities in coal. These regulated pollutants must be removed to meet the
appropriate emission standards and to provide a suﬃciently clean gas stream for
subsequent CO2 removal [1]. In particular, sulfur levels have to be low (less than
10 ppm and possibly lower) [13]. A schematic of a coal-ﬁred power plant with post-
combustion CO2 capture using an amine scrubber system is presented as Figure 1.5.

using an amine scrubber system [15].
The post-combustion technology used in a pulverized coal plant is also adopted in
a natural gas-ﬁred boiler or combined cycle (NGCC) power plant. High removal

1.3
Chemical absorption-desorption process
Among the diﬀerent types of post-combustion capture processes, chemical absorp-

holds compared to other methods, the most important ones are; there is no need for
radical changes for this process technology to be retroﬁtted with the existing com-
bustors [17], the liquid absorbents used in this process provide high CO2 absorption
capacities provided by the solvents even under low CO2 partial pressures [18] and
are less expensive compared to other capture methods [33]. The alternative meth-
ods of electrical power generation like the integrated coal gasiﬁcation combined cycle
(IGCC) produce higher pressure and higher concentration stream of CO2 [34,37,38],
which allows the use of precombustion CO2 capture by physical absorption. How-
ever, the majority of the existing power plants produce ﬂue gas at 1 atm with a
CO2 concentration of <15% [39]. Chemical absorption plays an important role in
achieving a signiﬁcant reduction in CO2 emissions [33].
The current technology involves the capture of CO2 from the ﬂue gas of a pul-
verised coal plant by chemical absorption with an organic solvent such as MEA.
The ﬂue gas ﬁrst gets scrubbed with the chosen solvent in a column known as the
absorber, which operates within a temperature range of 40 to 45 oC at the top and
50 to 60oC at the bottom [137]. Typically, 85-90% of the CO2 gets absorbed in case
of alkanolamine solvents [1]. The CO2 rich solvent is then pumped to a second col-
umn known as the regenerator or the stripper, in which heat is applied in the form

1.3.1
Amine solvents
Amine-based capture technology is regarded as one of the most reliable and advanced
post-combustion capture technologies [19]. The ﬁrst paper on the solubility of CO2
in alkanolamines was published in 1931 by Bottoms (1931). Some of the advantages
of using amine solutions for post-combustion CO2 capture include [18]:
• High CO2 absorption capacity even under low CO2 partial pressure
• High reaction rate
• Reversible reactions of CO2 with amines makes it possible to regenerate and
reuse the solvents after absorption
Aqueous MEA is a standard solvent for gas streams with low CO2 concentration. It
is primarily used for low-pressure operations. Aqueous solutions of diethanolamine
(DEA) have also been used for the removal of H2S and CO2 from reﬁnery gases [19].
A disadvantage of DEA is that it exhibits slow kinetics. Nevertheless, its high ab-
sorption capacity for treatment of high-pressure natural gas makes it an attractive
option for CO2 capture.

1.3.3
Drawbacks of chemical absorption-desorption process
1.3.3.1
Solvent degradation
One of the major problems associated with chemical absorption using MEA is the
degradation of the solvent by irreversible side reactions with CO2 and other com-
ponents present in the ﬂue gas [40–42], leading to numerous problems within the
process. MEA degrades in the presence of CO2 at the stripper operating conditions.
The degradation of MEA results in a solvent loss, requiring the replacement of ∼2.2
kg of MEA per tonne of CO2 captured [43]. The degradation also results in foaming
[44], fouling [45, 46], and increase in viscosity [40] of the amine solvent. The rate
of degradation is temperature-dependent, where the equivalent activation energy of
29 kcal/mol corresponds to a quadrupling of thermal degradation rate with a 17oC
increase in temperature or a doubling of the stripper pressure [51]. The rate of
degradation is also dependent on the loading of the solvent, which has a ﬁrst-order
eﬀect and amine concentration, which has a slightly more than a ﬁrst-order eﬀect
[51]. In existing CO2 capture facilities that use MEA, the degradation products are
separated in an evaporative reclaimer and disposed of as hazardous chemical waste,
resulting in increased disposal cost [47].
Due to the parasitic load on the plant and the need for higher eﬃciencies, control
of solvent degradation is quite an essential issue to sequestration [33].
1.3.3.2
Corrosion
In the case of carbon sequestration, increased corrosion is a signiﬁcant problem asso-
ciated with MEA degradation [40,48]. The iron content in the carbon steel columns
is very susceptible to corrosion by oxidation. Furthermore, the corrosion rates are
accelerated by higher solvent concentration, higher acid gas concentration, higher
operating temperatures, and a higher pH [35]. The free acid gas released from the
amines is more responsible for this acceleration than its concentration itself. The
concentration of the MEA solution should be kept low (typically under 20% for
coil boilers and ∼30% for natural gas-derived ﬂue gas if corrosion inhibitors are
employed) to maintain the machinery corrosion at an acceptable level [33]. How-
ever, low MEA concentrations reduce the eﬀectiveness of the solvent, requiring large
equipment sizes and faster circulation rates. Additionally, more energy is required in
the stripping column to raise the temperature and regenerate the amine solvent [49].
This increased "parasitic load" is particularly concerning for carbon sequestration
as it is not only an additional cost but also leads to increased CO2 emissions, thus
decreasing the overall beneﬁts of sequestration [50].

Chapter 2
Theory of ultrasonication
This chapter gives the theory behind the operation of ultrasound. A detailed discus-
sion is provided for the mechanism of cavitation along with its physical and chemical
eﬀects. Applications of ultrasound and the stages involved in degasiﬁcation of liquids
by ultrasound are explained, followed by its relevance in carbon capture systems.
2.1
Sonoprocess Technology
An acoustic wave is the propagation of pressure oscillation in a medium such as
air or liquid with the velocity of sound [55]. Ultrasound is inaudible sound with
a frequency of pressure oscillations above 20 kHz (20,000 oscillations per second)
[56]. It has been a useful tool in a variety of commercial applications in the ﬁelds
of chemistry, physics, biology, medicine, engineering, and waste treatment [57]. The
basis of most of the applications of ultrasound is acoustic cavitation, which is the
formation and collapse of microbubbles in an aqueous solution [58] as a result of the
pressure ﬂuctuations that occur in the applied sound ﬁeld. The collapse of the bub-
bles generates high local turbulence and release of heat. This consequently leads to
a signiﬁcant increase in temperature and pressure of up to several thousand degrees
Kelvin and several hundred Bar. These physical phenomena mimic those observed
as hydrodynamic cavitation causing damage to mechanical parts of pumps and pro-
pellers [59].
The elevated temperatures in the neighbourhood of the collapsing bubbles occur
in the form of "hot spots" [60] leading to the formation of free radicals which can
enhance the reaction rates of chemical processes. High turbulence and pressure con-
ditions aid in mechanical eﬀects such as mixing and shearing which, can accelerate
dissolution or aid the renewal at the surface of a solid reactant [61].
2.1.1
Cavitation
Cavitation was ﬁrst reported in 1895 by Thornycroft and Barnaby [59] when they
observed that the propeller of their submarine became pitted and eroded over a rel-
atively short operation period. Their observation, as previously mentioned, was the
consequence of collapsing bubbles that generated intense pressure and temperature
gradients in the local vicinity [61]. In 1917, Lord Rayleigh [66] published the ﬁrst
mathematical model for a cavitation event in an incompressible ﬂuid, and it was

not until 1927 that Loomis [67] reported the ﬁrst chemical and biological eﬀects of
ultrasound. It was then recognised that cavitation could be a useful tool in chemical
processes. One of the ﬁrst applications reported was the use of ultrasound-induced
cavitation for degradation of a biopolymer [68]. Ever since, the popularity of ultra-

2.2
Theory of cavitation
Cavitation is generally deﬁned as the phenomena of the formation, growth, and
subsequent collapse of microbubbles or cavities in minimal intervals of time (mil-
liseconds) releasing high magnitudes of energy [69–71]. Cavitation ususally occurs at
millions of locations simultaneously in a reactor generating conditions of localised
high temperatures and pressures, with the overall conditions remaining ambient
[71]. It also results in the generation of local turbulence and liquid micro-circulation
(acoustic streaming) in the reactor [74]. There are four diﬀerent types of cavitation
based on the mechanism and eﬃciency of producing cavities. These are summarised
as follows [74]:
• Acoustic Cavitation: Pressure variations are caused by sound waves, usually
by ultrasound (16 kHz-100 MHz). The chemical changes caused by acoustic
cavitation is termed as sonochemistry. Acoustic cavitation also accompanies
the production of light, and the phenomena are known as sonoluminescence.
• Hydrodynamic Cavitation: Pressure variations are caused by gradients in ve-
locity due to the geometry of the system. Exchange in pressure and kinetic
energies resulting in hydrodynamic cavitation is observed in ﬂow systems like
an oriﬁce or venturi.
• Optic Cavitation: It is produced by high-intensity light (lasers) rupturing a
liquid continuum.
• Particle Cavitation: A beam of elementary particles produces it. A proton
rupturing a liquid, as in the case of a bubble chamber is an example.
Of the four types, only acoustic and hydrodynamic cavitations can generate the
desired conditions required for chemical and physical processes. Mode of generation
is one of the ways of classifying cavitation. Cavitation can also be classiﬁed based
on the maximum radius reached, the lifetime of the cavity in the liquid bulk, and
the pattern of cavity collapse. Based on these parameters, cavitation is classiﬁed as
[71]:

signiﬁcantly in ﬁne emulsions, thus increasing the rates of reactions.

2.3
Use of ultrasound for degasiﬁcation of liquids
Ultrasound’s application to degas has been studied since 1920s [80] and has been
used in both industrial and pilot-scale applications. One of the ﬁrst successful appli-
cations was ultrasound degassing of optical glass. Extraction of gases from hydro-
carbons, oils, and resins accelerated by the aid of ultrasound has also been reported
[81]. Successful trials for the use of ultrasound degasiﬁcation have been carried out
in the food industry, particularly for beer, juices, chocolate, and vegetable oils. In
the food industry, removal of oxygen improves the quality and preservation, and the
removal of carbon dioxide [85] accelerates fermentation.
Based on these applications, the use of ultrasound in assisting the desorption of
CO2 from loaded MEA solvent is being ventured into. This section will describe the
mass transfer kinetics involved which causes ultrasound in degasiﬁcation of liquids
and other materials.
Ultrasound produces multiple eﬀects on the surface of the liquid, some of which
are summarised as follows [101].
• Cavitation: Refer Section 2.2.
• Micro streaming: Micro streaming occurs when a micro-bubble, surrounded
by a liquid environment, undergoes direct oscillatory action when exposed to
ultrasound [101]. Microstreaming refers explicitly to the streaming ﬂow of ﬂuid
around an oscillating object such as a gas bubble. The ﬂuid ﬂow is generated
from the vorticity caused by the oscillation of the boundary layer surrounding
an object such as an oscillating cavitation bubble [102]. Therefore, it is, in a
way, dependent on cavitating bubbles.
• Shock waves: A collapsing bubble in the presence of a high-intensity ultra-
sound produces a spherically diverging shock wave in the liquid surrounding
it [103].
• Acoustic streaming: The propagation of sound waves of medium and high
intensity in a liquid is accompanied by a unidirectional motion of the liquid,
commonly called as acoustic streaming [90]. Acoustic streaming can inﬂuence
the translational velocity of the bubbles [81].
17
==PAGE:37==
2.3. Use of ultrasound for degasiﬁcation of liquids
• Acoustic radiation pressure: The time-average pressure acting on an object
in a sound ﬁeld is interpreted as acoustic radiation pressure [82].
• Bjerknes forces: Two spheres present in an incompressible ﬂuid, separated
by a distance and pulsating at the same frequency are shown to have an
interaction force between them [91]. This force is called as Bjerknes force.
The Bjerknes force attracts the spheres if they vibrate in phase and repulses
them if they vibrate in antiphase [81].
Based on these physical eﬀects produced by ultrasound, the degassing process is
broken down into three stages [81]:
• Stage 1: Small air bubbles existing in the liquid grow due to the diﬀusion of
dissolved gas into them. This diﬀusion, which is extremely slow under ordinary
conditions, can be accelerated in the presence of acoustic oscillations.
• Stage 2: Swarms of groups of small bubbles coalesce, resulting in larger
bubbles. This is caused as a result of acoustic streaming, acoustic radiation
pressure, Bjerknes and Bernoulli forces.
• Stage 3: The growing bubbles, which have greater buoyancy force, rapidly
reach the surface, thus escaping the liquid.
Stages 1 and 2 are explained detailed in the sections to follow:
2.3.1
Stage 1-Bubble formation
Ultrasound during the compression cycle exerts positive pressure on the liquid push-
ing the molecules together, and negative pressure during rarefaction, pulling the
molecules apart. At suﬃcient intensities, cavities can be formed during the expan-
sion cycle. For a cavity to be formed, a largely negative force in the expansion cycle
is required to overcome the tensile strength of a liquid. For pure water, more than
1000 atmospheres of negative pressure is required, which is beyond the capacity (50
atm) of any powerful ultrasound generator [84]. Most liquids are however suﬃciently
contaminated with dissolved gases, which reduces this pressure requirement to only
a few atmospheres.
2.3.2
Stage 2-Bubble growth
A bubble present in a liquid is inherently unstable. A large bubble can rise to the
surface and burst, and a small one redissolves into the liquid. However, under the
inﬂuence of ultrasound, a bubble absorbs energy from the alternating compression
and expansion cycles and grows in size through rectiﬁed diﬀusion.
2.3.2.1
Single bubble
Rectiﬁed diﬀusion is essentially a mass transfer phenomenon with a moving bound-
ary layer. The growth of the bubble occurs due to an unequal transfer of mass across
the air/liquid interface during the compression and rarefaction phases of the sound
wave cycle. Diﬀusion of gas occurs across the surface of the bubble in a way that
more gas diﬀuses into the bubble during the expansion than that leaves out during
18
==PAGE:38==
2.3. Use of ultrasound for degasiﬁcation of liquids
compression [79]. Rectiﬁed diﬀusion is posited to occur by two eﬀects, known as the
"area" eﬀect and the "shell" eﬀect [104].
Area eﬀect
According to this, the surface area of the bubble during expansion is much higher
than that during compression. As the rate of diﬀusion across an interface is propor-
tional to the surface area available for mass transfer [61], the amount of gas that
enters the bubble during its expansion phase is much higher than the amount of
gas that leaves the bubble during the compression phase. As a result, the amount
of gas that accumulates in the bubble is considerable over many acoustic cycles [104].
Shell eﬀect
According to this, the gas diﬀusion is controlled by the thickness of a diﬀusion layer
or a shell formed in the liquid surrounding the bubble (assuming that a constant
mass of liquid surrounds the bubble) [104]. During bubble expansion, the shell be-
comes thinner, and the concentration gradient increases, causing the ﬂow rate of gas
to the bubble to increase. During bubble compression, the thickness of the liquid
layer shell increases, causing the concentration gradient to decrease, leading to a
decrease in the ﬂow rate of the gas to the bubble. Consequently, more gas accumu-
lates in the bubble than that leaves, causing its growth.

Bubble coalescence
Smaller bubbles may coalesce and produce a larger bubble. Growth of a bubble
by this form makes it reach the resonance size much faster when compared to the
rectiﬁed diﬀusion pathway [92]. Some of the coalesced bubbles are larger than the
resonance size, due to which, accumulate at the nodes and become "inactive". In
that, they do not cavitate anymore. This coalescence is a result of the attractive
Bjerknes force acting between the bubbles. These bubbles eventually ﬂoat to the
surface of the liquid due to buoyancy [96].
Other interactions
Under certain conditions, big bubbles which oscillate in the same phase, instead of
coalescing through Bjerknes forces, form stable clusters called as "bubble grapes",
and small ones group themselves in strong ﬁelds in branched ﬁlamentary structures
called "acoustic streamers". This has been reported as anomalous bubble behaviour
[97]. There are many forces that act against bubbles from coalescing, one of them
being the viscosity of the surrounding liquid. At higher viscosities, the interaction
forces between the bubbles can be repulsive [97].

3.6.1
Analysis of CO2 content in Liquid Phase (by BaCl2
method)
The liquid analysis of CO2 loading of MEA is based on the titrimetric method for the
determination of acid gases in alkali hydroxides and amines developed by Ralph and
Trass [105]. The method described by Weiland and Trass is for ethylenediamine as
the amine solvent, but the same procedure can be extended to monoethanolamine as
the reaction mechanism is fairly similar. The amine carbamate present in the form
of a zwitterion is thermally unstable and goes back to its original constituents at
higher temperatures. When heated in the presence of BaCl2, the CO2 present gets
precipitated as BaCO3. The actual method employed is a slightly altered version of
this, as described by Sun et al. [106].

From Figure 5.5, it can be inspected that the loading of the solvent goes down from
an initial value of 0.6 to 0.463 at the end of two hours of ultrasonication. Even
though the general trend of the loading is decreasing with time, there are still a few
ﬂuctuations in the loading at certain times, similar to the ones observed in Figure

• It is possible that these ﬂuctuations are observed because of experimental
errors. However, these disparities were observed in multiple trials, indicating
that there must be a reason.
• As previously mentioned in Section 2.3, the process of cavitation involves the
formation, growth, and collapse of the bubbles. The growth of the bubbles
occurs by diﬀerent pathways. Once the bubbles grow, they reach a critical
59
==PAGE:79==
5.3. Experimental Results
size known as the resonance size [92]. At this point, the bubbles can either
reach the surface and escape the liquid, or collapse and get dissolved into the
liquid, causing an intermittent increase in the loading of the solvent. Due to
this collapse of the bubbles ﬁlled with gas, both desorption and absorption
co-occur, causing a net mass transfer [94]. Another possible explanation could
be that the bubbles ﬁlled with CO2 which have risen to the surface, instead of
escaping the liquid, got absorbed back into it.
• The bubble dynamics in acoustic cavitation is very complicated. Therefore, the
explanations provided are based on a beginner’s understanding of the process.
It is possible that there is a better explanation for this behaviour. But it will
require a deeper theoretical understanding and experimental analysis of the
bubble and ultrasound’s multiphysics behaviour.
Another observation that can be made from Figures 5.4 and 5.5 is that the rate
of desorption in the presence of ultrasound is approximately linear in the ﬁrst two
hours. However, the rate becomes almost zero after a point. Since the primary
mode of mass transfer is through cavitation, the liquid needs to be saturated with a
minimum concentration of dissolved gas. This minimum is called as the cavitation
threshold concentration [87]. A concentration below this causes no cavitation, thus
leading to no further degasiﬁcation. As ultrasound can only cause physical desorp-
tion, it needs the presence of aqueous CO2 which it can release as bubbles. CO2
which is present as carbamate in MEA, can only be in its aqueous form through
heat. Therefore, it is possible that the amount of aqueous CO2 produced is limited
at 80 oC, which has been completely removed until then, thus causing no more des-
orption. Additionally, the temperature of the solvent decreases as CO2 desorption is
an endothermic process and as ultrasound desorbs free CO2 almost instantaneously,
the temperature falls further due to which the desorption of CO2 from the solvent
also decreases [125], as the solvent needs some time to heat up again.
The rate at which the loading reduces is higher in the case of ultrasound when
compared to heating alone. This reaﬃrms the enhancement of mass transfer by
ultrasound through cavitation and other bubble interactions.

10 wt% MEA is not as pronounced as in the case of 30 wt% MEA. It was expected
that ultrasound would produce better desorption for 10 wt% MEA, as the viscosity
of the solvent decreases at lower MEA content [108], and viscosity plays a vital role
in the cavitation of the dissolved gas [126]. However, this is not seen, which implies
that stronger opposing factors counter the low viscosity of the solvent. Some of the
plausible explanations are listed as follows:
• The heat of absorption of CO2 increases with a decrease in the concentration
of MEA in the solvent [95]. As previously explained in Section 1.3.3.3, during
the regeneration of the solvent, heat equivalent to the heat of absorption is to
be supplied to the solvent to reverse the reaction and release aqueous CO2.
A higher heat of absorption implies that more heat is required to produce
CO2.
This increase is due to higher water content increases.
The speciﬁc
heat capacity of water [4.18 J/*(g· K)] is higher than MEA [2.72 J/(g· K)]
[124] due to which the amount of energy required to desorb a mole of CO2 per
mole of solvent increases. Therefore, the heat supplied to the solvent through
the water bath at 80oC goes into heating water instead of contributing to the
heat of reaction. Without the presence of aqueous CO2, use of ultrasound is
redundant.
• At lower concentrations of MEA, the absorption capacity of the solvent is also
lower. The starting loading of 10 wt% MEA is 0.47 mol CO2/mol MEA as
compared to 0.6 mol CO2/mol MEA in the case of 30 wt% MEA. A lower
loading also reduces the amount of CO2 that can be desorbed [126].

Instead of improving the process, ultrasound at intensity levels of 6 and 9, gives
higher loadings at the end of 150 minutes in comparison to just heating the solvent.
As mentioned earlier, desorption of CO2 is endothermic. It can be observed that
the initial rate of decrease in the loading by intensity levels 6 and 9 is higher than
plain heat. Due to the high initial rate of desorption, the temperature of the solvent

• At high-intensity levels, as mentioned previously, it is possible that the ul-

For the case of 30 wt% MEA, the behaviour was conﬁrmed by carrying out
multiple experiments at the same operating conditions. The same could not
be done for 10 wt% MEA. Therefore, one possible explanation for this is errors
in experimentation.
The cause in the jumps in the loadings in the presence of ultrasound has already been

energy required.

5.7
Comparison with literature
Use of ultrasound to assist in desorption of CO2 was ﬁrst identiﬁed by Gantert
[122]. Inﬂuence of various parameters like the reactor geometry, solvent variation,
desorption temperature, ultrasound frequency and power were analysed.
It was
observed that CO2 could be degassed in the presence of ultrasonic radiation even
at temperatures of 60 oC and 80 oC, as seen even in the present work. The power
range of ultrasound utilised in [122] was 10-50 W, as opposed to the higher intensity
(87-147W) in the present work. It was also mentioned that use of a high contin-
uous ultrasonic ﬁeld (>80W) yields a subproportional degassing eﬀect due to the
increase in the disturbing Bjerknes forces at higher intensities. This is also reﬂected
in the present works’ results where the desorption obtained at higher intensities
was lower than that at a lower intensity. It was also concluded that the frequency
of ultrasound has no signiﬁcant eﬀect on desorption. The results from this work
could not be quantitatively compared to the present works’ results as the solvent
used was a mixture of 30 wt% MEA-MDEA. Hence a qualitative comparison is given.
Following the work done by Gantert [122], ceratin other researchers have done no-
table work in this ﬁeld.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Based on the experimental results, it can be concluded that ultrasound has the
potential to increase the rate of CO2 desorption of 30 wt% MEA even at lower
temperatures of 80oC. Ultrasound, however, cannot inﬂuence the thermodynam-
ics of the process. It can only intensify the process when there is enough aqueous
CO2 available for cavitation. At lower temperatures, the ultrasonic energy would be
utilised for heating the solvent rather than degassing. Therefore, the main purpose
of using ultrasound is to quicken the rate of CO2 desorption, before it gets reab-
sorbed into the solvent.
Various parameters inﬂuence the functioning of ultrasound, which is summarised
as follows:
• Intensity: At higher intensities, transient cavitation occurs, which causes a
violent collapse of the bubbles over a very few acoustic cycles. The bubble-
bubble coalescence is hindered. At a lower intensity, the bubble can grow in
size over many acoustic cycles and escape the liquid due to buoyancy before
collapsing.
• Viscosity of the solvent: High viscosities inhibit mass transfer, which hin-
ders the formation and cavitation of bubbles. The gas diﬀusivity in the liquid
reduces. Additionally, higher viscosities hinder bubble-bubble coalescence due
to repulsive Bjerknes forces.
• Density of the solvent: At higher densities of the solvent, the mass of the
liquids is higher. The bubbles have to overcome a greater amount of inertia
to be able to cavitate, thus hindering degasiﬁcation.
For 10 wt% MEA, even though low density and viscosity work in favour of ultra-
sound, the high regeneration energy required makes its application redundant. Only
at temperatures above 80oC can ultrasound intensify the process for this concentra-
tion of MEA.
In the presence of additives like MEG and Urea, the density and viscosity of the
solvent increases, which hinders cavitation. The desorption obtained in these cases
is not signiﬁcantly better than plain heating at 80oC.
Magnetic stirring gives comparable results to that of ultrasound for 30 wt% MEA
105
==PAGE:125==
and gives the best desorption results in rest of the cases. The main mode of transfer
is mixing, which is not as aﬀected by density and viscosity.
The speciﬁc energy consumed by ultrasound is not signiﬁcant for 30 wt% MEA.
Due to hindrances to desorption in the other solvents, higher speciﬁc energy is re-
quired. Magnetic stirring requires the least speciﬁc energy as the power consumed
by the stirrer is 100 times lower than that by the ultrasound. This is due to the high
volume capacity of the ultrasound bath, which implies that the energy eﬃciency of
the bath is low.
Sensitivity analysis for density shows that the mass transfer coeﬃcients are aﬀected
by its variation, more so in the case of MEG than Urea.
Even though ultrasound has the potential to improve the desorption rates, it is
not practical to install a bath in the reboiler section of the regeneration unit. How-
ever, transducers available as probes can be ﬁtted into the reboiler section. The
diﬀerences in the operational diﬀerences need further investigation.

Chapter 7
Future work
The experimental setup can be reﬁtted to determine the ﬂow of CO2 in the gaseous
phase. This would provide a clearer picture of the period where ultrasound performs
its best.
The current work used supersaturated solvents. Loading plays an important role in
the desorption rates. Therefore, future work could involve the inﬂuence of loading
on ultrasound’s performance.
In the current work, all the experiments were carried out at atmospheric pressure.
Whereas, at industrial reboiler conditions, the pressures are higher than 1 atm. De-
termination of the behaviour of ultrasound at higher pressures is another prospective
future work. Furthermore, an ultrasonic bath was utilised for the experimental work,
which cannot be retroﬁtted at a pilot or industrial scale to a reboiler. Transducer
probes can be used at much larger scales. However, there can be many operational
diﬀerences between these two types of transducers, which needs further investigation.
The multiphysics behind ultrasound is complicated, very interesting nonetheless.
It would be beneﬁcial to gain a deeper understanding of the dynamics and bubble
interactions involved and develop a mathematical model to simulate its behaviour
for its application in CO2 desorption. The mass transfer coeﬃcients were determined
based on the assumption that quiescent desorption takes place, when, in fact, it is
through bubbling. Understanding the physics of the process can help in obtaining
a more accurate representation of the values.
Due to limited data, only overall liquid mass transfer coeﬃcients were calculated.
Therefore, improvements can be made to the experimental setup to obtain data for
calculating the individual mass transfer coeﬃcients and the enhancement factor.
Lastly, the density, viscosity, and solubility data available for solvents with the
VRAs is very limited. It would be valuable to experimentally determine these prop-
erties for better estimation of mass transfer coeﬃcients. The sensitivity analysis
could also be done only concerning density. Investigation of the eﬀect of viscosity
and henry’s coeﬃcient would also be worthwhile.

Description of chemicals/products (Relevant issues from SDS, section D) 
• 
Urea is colorless or white crystalline powder. It is soluble in ethanol, methanol, acetic acid, etc. Contact with 
strong oxidizing agents should be avoided. May react explosively with sodium hypochlorite. 
• 
CO2 is a colorless gas. It is miscible with water, hydrocarbons, and most organic solvents. Materials to avoid: 
Magnesium-, zirconium-, titanium-, and some magnesium-aluminium dust compounds ignite and explode in 
CO2 atmosphere. Dust of aluminium, chromium and manganese ignite and explode at heatening in CO2. 
• 
MEA is a colorless, hydroscopic, viscous liquid. It can absorb CO2, which is why it is used as an absorbent in 
the current experiment. Oxidizing agents, acids, acid anhydride, acid chlorides, etc are to be avoided.
