Abstract
In this study three different machine learning methods, Elastic Net, Gaussian Process
Regression, and a neural network containing a Long-Short-Term-Memory (LSTM) mod-
ule, in combination with two different approaches for peptide encoding have been used to
create a model to score virtual peptides for their binding affinities towards three receptors.
Virtual peptides have then been generated with two different routines and scored for their
affinity. For each combination of machine learning method, peptide encoding, receptor,
and virtual peptide generation routine 8-10 candidates have been selected. Those 320
peptides were synthesised in array format, and their binding potential towards the three
receptors was measured.
In the last part of this study the results were compared with the predictions and the per-
formance of the different models and encoding discussed.
I denne undersøgelse er der anvendt tre forskellige maskinlæringsmetoder, Elastic Net,
Gaussian Process Regression og et neuralt netværk, der indeholder et Long-Short-Term-
Memory (LSTM) modul. Dette er i kombination med to forskellige tilgange til peptidkodning
anvendt til at skabe en model til at score virtuelle peptider for deres bindingsaffiniteter til
tre receptorer.
Virtuelle peptider er derefter blevet genereret med to forskellige rutiner og scoret for
deres affinitet. For hver kombination af maskinlæringsmetode, peptidkodning, receptor
og virtuel peptidgenerationsrutine er der valgt 8-10 kandidater. Disse 320 peptider blev
syntetiseret i array-format, og deres bindingspotentiale til de tre receptorer blev målt.
I den sidste del af denne undersøgelse blev resultaterne sammenlignet med forudsigelser
og ydeevne for de forskellige modeller og kodning, der blev diskuteret.

Introduction
The road to a new drug on the market is usually a long and expensive one (see for example
[1]). In very rough terms there are three stages: target identification, drug discovery,
and drug development. The cost and length of the latter is determined by the type of
drug and local and international laws. For example the number of patients in a phase III
clinical study depends on the size of a statistical significant effect one needs to show to get
approval. The length of the drug discovery phase depends on the target. In the best case,
one can take a natural occurring compound and enter the development phase without
much further work. An example of a compound that went almost direct from discovery
into humans to the market is insulin (see for example [2]). But those cases are rare and
the normal discovery process requires longer time to e.g. improve the properties of the
lead compound like in vivo half-life, drug stability in formulation, or in vitro potency and
thus hopefully in vivo potency.
The typical design cycle in drug discovery contains usually four step:
• Design new compounds
• Synthesis of compounds
• Test of compounds
• Analyse results
The most time consuming and also expensive steps in this cycle are the synthesis of
the compounds and the testing of those. These steps are then repeated until there is a
compound fulfilling all or most requirements to move on in the process. The number of
cycles depend on the complexity of the problem, numbers of compounds tested in each
cycle and the performance of the analysis and design step, which is still based on human
understanding and imagination (figure 1.1). The usual approach to speed up the process
was and still is to increase the number of compounds synthesised and tested, although
there is a limit due to cost and time restrictions.
Figure 1.1: Design cycle
With the rise of powerful computers and large data sets this focus has changed towards

1.1
Outline of the thesis goal
The target of interest is a peptide hormone with 31 amino acids. This peptide can bind
and activate several receptors in vitro, herein they are called a, b1, and b2. There are a
few independent ideas and goals for this thesis related to this peptide.
• The biological target for this peptide is still under evaluation at Novo Nordisk. The
affinity to at least one of the receptors needs to be improved before the project can
move on.
• There exists a data set of 300 peptides randomly derived from a reference peptide
with binding data on all three receptor. Can machine learning methods be used as
tool to guide the design of new compounds?
• There are many methods available to do this. Will they perform similar or is there
a significant difference? How does one represent a peptide, so it can be fed into a
computer?
• Can one envision a recipe for the design cycle which can run without human inter-
vention?
The last point requires some explanation. Chemists have been optimizing targets in the
pharmaceutical industry for more than hundred years now. They have been doing this
successfully for most of the time without the help of computers. Why should that change?
One reason could be to speed up the design cycle and save some time on the way from
project initiation to first introduction to the market. Another, maybe more interesting, point
is that an algorithm might be able to extract interaction patterns a normal human mind
can’t see and therefor might help create compounds with surprising properties. For the last
point to work it would require the algorithm to process data without any biased information
from the human operator.
The steps of this thesis/experiment are then:
• Process the existing data and encode the peptide structures so that a computer can
read them.
• Train and cross validate three different machine learning methods (models) on this
data set.
• Create virtual peptides and use the above obtained models to score the peptides.
• Synthesize a new array with the best peptides from the step above and measure
their biological activity.
• Evaluate the performance of the models.

2.1.1
Peptides and peptide synthesis
Peptides are polymers built from amino acids (figure 2.1). Most protein and peptides are
build up by a set of 20 amino acids, although there are found many more in nature. Usually
peptides have up to 50 amino acid blocks. Larger polymers are called proteins. Another
way to differentiate between a peptide and protein is the structure. Peptides are more
flexible, while proteins have a well defined structure.
Figure 2.1: Example of a tetrapeptide (Met-Ala-Arg-Tyr) build up from the four amino acids
methionine (Met), alanine (Ala), arginine (Arg), and tyrosine (Tyr).
Amino acids consist of a backbone and a side chain. The backbone forms the polymer
and the different side chains determine the function and structure of a protein (see e.g. [3]
for an introduction to peptide and proteins and [4] for an introduction to peptide synthesis).
Peptide synthesis in the laboratory is usually done on solid support, coupling the first1
amino acid via the C-terminal to a cleavable linker on an insoluble polymer. The synthesis
can be described as a loop (algorithm 1) starting with the C-terminal amino acid. Each
cycle elongates the peptide chain with one amino acid. In contrast to lab work nature
synthesises peptides and proteins from the N- to C-terminal.
1The amino acids in a peptide are numbered from the N- to C-terminal. During standard peptide synthesis
the first amino acid to be attached is the amino acid with the highest number.

2.3
Peptide description
Peptides are build up of amino acids; in this case the peptides have a length of 31 units.
The most natural approach is to split the sequence into the amino acids and then encode
each amino acid separately. Considering the amino acid label being a category, a common
approach to describe an amino acid is one-hot encoding (see for example the description
in [5]). There are many different descriptors in use attaching measured or calculated
properties to the encoding [6]. The so called z-scales by Sandberg et al. [7] are the result
of a principle component analysis of experimental and calculated properties of amino
acids. The publication lists the projections of the first five components of 87 commonly
used amino acids. Herein all five projections will be used (zscale5), although it is also
common to use only the first three projections (zscale3). This thesis will compare one-hot
encoding and zscale5 in different machine learning models, see also figure 2.5.
The main advantage of the one-hot encoding is the representation of each amino acid by
a bit. There is no additional requirement for structural, physical or chemical information.
If we keep to the 20 amino acids encoded by the DNA, each amino acid is encoded by a
vector of length 20. But this is also one of the disadvantages of the one-hot encoding. If
one wants to include other amino acids, this vector becomes longer. Another disadvan-
tage is the lack of any structural, physical or chemical information. Similar amino acids
like glutamate (Glu) and aspartate (Asp) have the same distance in encoding space as
for example dissimilar amino acids like glutamate and leucine (Leu). Any model trained
on a smaller encoding vector needs to be retrained if we want to explore peptides with

2.4.1
Cross-Validation
Cross-validation is a tool in the field of machine learning to either determine an estimate
of the prediction error or it can be used to select parameter for a model by estimating
the prediction error for different parameter combination and then selecting the model/pa-
rameter combination with the lowest prediction error. The problem is then, that the data
used for model selection has also been used for estimation of the prediction error. One
solution to this would be to run a two layer nested cross-validation (Algorithm 2), in which
the inner cross-validation loop is used for model selection and the outer loop estimates
the prediction error. For this the data set needs to be split into a train, development or
validation, and test set. Here the data set is split 9:1 in the outer layer so that 10% of the
data is used for testing and 90% for training and development. This 90% data set is then
split again in the inner layer 9:1, meaning 81% of the data set is used for training and 9%
is used for development (figure 2.6).
After running the cross validation, finding the hyper parameter for the model, and deter-
mining the estimate of the prediction error, the model is retrained on the whole available
data set to give the final model.

rameter estimation for linear regression (see e.g. [11]). Applying Bayes theorem to the
linear regression problem gives equation 2.9.

independent of β and therefor a constant in this problem.

An LSTM is a more advanced version of a neural network and has been first described
by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber in 1997 [12]. It is a refined form of a recurrent neural
network (RNN). The basic idea is that the RNN reads a sequence in a sequential way and
the sequence information is stored in a cell. For example it has been used in language
processing where words are read in from a sentence and then translated into a different
language (e.g. Spanish to robot commands [13]). It has also been used to process se-
quence information in proteins (e.g. [14]). The output of an RNN or LSTM layer is the
prediction of the next element in a sequence/sentence, which is not used here.
The LSTM layer used in the study processes the peptide sequence in a bi-directional
manner into a 2 layered hidden cell of size 50 so that it contains 2×2×50 nodes. The
input is required to be in matrix form. For example, each column in the matrices in figure
2.5 stand for one amino acid, and the LSTM will read the columns for left to right and right
to left (bi-directional). Therefor the input vectors of size 19∗31 or 5∗31 were transformed
into a 19×31 or 5×31 matrix, respectively for the LSTM.
The content of the cell is than fed into a feed forward layer, which handles the information
and passes it on to the output layer (figure 2.9). The hyper-parameter optimized during
cross validation are the size of the FFNN layer, an L2 regularization and the learning
rate. The python code defining the network is given in the appendix A.1.1. There isn’t an
activation function added to the network. In the exploratory phase of this project, several
builds with and without activation function have been tested, but no added benefit could
be seen for any activation function.

2.5.1
Random Peptide Design
10 ∗106 have been randomly generated with at most three mutations relative to the ref-

tations was chosen to keep the new peptides close in chemical space to the reference
peptide. The 10 best peptides for each model and each receptor have been saved into
a list together with a tag, indicating which model and receptor combination has put this
peptide on the list. This will allow later to track back and evaluate the models, encoding
methods, and peptide generation routines. The list of peptides for the new array con-
tains so far 10 peptides for each combination of receptor (3), model (3), and encoding
(2), which will be max. 3×3×2×10=180 peptides. Only unique peptides were added to
the list, meaning if the models predicted the same 10 peptides for each model/encod-
ing/receptor combination, the list would contain only 10 peptides. In reality 171 unique
peptides were found, which is already an indication that the predictions of the models
differ substantially (figure 2.10).

Array Synthesis
The new designed peptide array has been synthesised on an Intavis MultiPep RSi using
standard Fmoc-amino acid coupling conditions. After cleavage from the solid support with
trifluoroacetic acid and precipitation with diethyl ether, the peptides were redissolved in
80% dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO)/water. Peptide concentrations were determined using a
Charged Aerosol Detector (CAD), ThermoFisher. All 320 + 32 peptides were synthesised
in decent quality and processed further.

3.4
Comparing Prediction and Reality
Out of the new 312 peptides (320 −8 control peptides), 158 were surprisingly inactive
(rel. log > 2) on receptor a. Using the same filter, no peptides were inactive on the
other two receptors. One of the possible reasons for this might be, the peptide generation
routines (random and search) introduced mutations in positions which were beneficial for
the receptors b1 and b2 but detrimental for receptor a, since the process didn’t counter
check for activity on the other two receptors. Receptors b1 and b2 are closer related in
structure and their structure activity relationship (SAR) should follow to a higher degree,
therefor a beneficial mutation on receptor b1 is more likely to be beneficial on receptor b2
and vice versa.

models. The one exception is the LSTM predictions for receptor a.
• The z-scale models using elastic net or GPR on the receptors b1 and b2 are com-
pletely useless as indicated by the almost zero PCC.
• Although the LSTM was superior with respect to the estimated prediction error from

Conclusion
4.1
Current Work
In this study it seems possible with the right model and encoding combination to pre-
dict and score virtual peptides, so that the measured binding affinities are improved as
compared to the reference peptide.
One-hot encoding seems to be superior to z-scale encoding for most models in this study.
This observation might be linked to the advantages/disadvantages of the encoding dis-
cussed in section 2.3, page 6. If amino acid XXX in position Y is not included in the train
set using one-hot encoding, the model can not include any contributions from XXX in this
position in the prediction of the affinity of new peptides. On the other hand, the models
based on z-scales will not have this limitation. They will use the coefficients trained on
the z-scales of other amino acids to make a prediction of the contribution of XXX to the
affinity. In other words, a model trained on z-scales will extrapolate unknown amino acids
while a one-hot encoded model will ignore this amino acid. This fact combined with the
strategy for how peptides were selected will result in a more challenged task for the mod-
els based on z-scales. This also means, the uncertainty is much larger for z-scale based
models on peptides with unknown amino acids in position Y. The LSTM seems to handle
the z-scales a bit better, which might reflect the fact that this model is a bit more complex.
The z-scale based models should perform better, when the training data set becomes
larger.
The predictive power of the models might be affected by the difference in the chemical
space of the first and second data set, as indicated by the analysis of the substitution
pattern (figures 2.4 and 3.10), number of mutations (table 3.8, and the retraining of the
LSTM (figures 3.8 and 3.9).
4.2
Future Work
What is the goal of a peptide array? In this study, the simple goal was to create pep-
tides with a higher affinity towards the receptors. This means most likely that the newly
designed array lays (partly) outside of the chemical space covered by the initial data set.
As discussed above, one-hot encoding based models will lead to more conservative esti-
mates, while z-scale based models will try to extrapolate and may have therefor a larger
error on the estimates.
But more often, improved affinity is only a secondary goal. With the peptide and receptor
profile used in here, a realistic goal could have been to create a selective peptide or a
dual or triple ligand. Instead of selecting the most potent binders on a certain receptor,
the algorithm would look for peptides which are as good as the reference peptide on for
example receptor a, but have no affinity on the other two receptors. Another opportunity
could be to select compounds as good as the reference peptide but include a calculated
physicochemical property, e.g. isoelectric point, as second selection criteria (see for ex-
ample figure 4.1).
This study seems to indicate that a z-scale based model will most likely benefit from a
larger data set. To secure a better coverage of all positions so called amino acid scans with
’extreme’ amino acids should be the base for the initial data set. A scan is a set of peptides
in which each position is replaced by a certain amino acid (figure 4.2). The ’extreme’ amino
