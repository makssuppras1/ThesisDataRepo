Abstract
The increase of fuel prices in the maritime industry, CO2 emissions and the search of greener
measures, along with the importance of maritime transport in the international supply chain
and trade is leading to the market towards more efficient practices in the operation of vessels.
Ship weather routing optimization has been recognised as one of the efficient tools leading
these reductions. Different companies can be found designing softwares within this field and
the development of such programmes require a considerable effort.
This thesis reviews the theory behind ship weather routing optimization and the factors
involved in the design of such systems. A market analysis presenting the different companies
that currently offer weather routing optimization services is presented. Possible savings
achieved by this type of programmes, in terms of reduced fuel consumption and emissions,
is assessed.
A commercial weather routing software (Sea Planner provided by Force Technology) is
used in order to assess the performance of such system in various case studies. The tool
offers substantial savings compare to traditional practices. The performance and savings are
highly linked to the resolution of the weather forecast, nevertheless savings of between 1 and
5% could be achieved in the examined scenarios.
Key Words: Ship Weather Routing Optimization, Routing Algorithms, Ocean Currents,
Fuel Savings.

Chapter 1
Introduction and outline
Maritime transport has always played a significant role in the prosperity of the countries
and their economies. To illustrate this and looking back in the history, we just have to
look at the discovery of America in the fifteenth century. This had a remarkable impact
in the trade, especially in Europe. The transport of new raw materials, precious metals,
spices or food caused great benefits to the economies of the old continent. Later, during the
industrial revolution an increase on the trade with the colonies was achieved thanks to the
improvement of conventional maritime technology (Rodrigue et al. (2013, chapter 3, p. 78)).
However, it was during the second half of the twentieth century that the development of the
container ships brought a great impulse to the world trade. Today, new technologies are being
developed to achieve safer and more efficient voyages.
It is stated that seaborne trade represents, nowadays, nearly the 90 % of the world trade by
volume (Chang et al. (2016)), still and despite all the advances, in values of 2014, excluding
catastrophic losses, more than 700 containers1 were lost overboard every year and in the
decade of the 2000 serious partial losses in containers were gone up to 270 % (Chen (2007)).
Furthermore, about 55% of the liner shipping companies are arriving +/- 24 hours from the
advertised ETA 2. This represents a serious problem affecting the whole supply chain of the
goods transported by sea. On the other hand, bunker costs can represent up to the 50% of
carriers’ costs 3. This together with the higher concern about the emissions and environment
is taking on a higher importance in Maritime Shipping. This is why, day after day, more
advance routing methods and algorithms are trying to be designed to reduce such problems
or handle them in a smarter way.

Introduction and outline
Maritime weather routing consist in determining the best route to follow between an
origin and destination points or ports. This best route is defined according to a specified
objective function that may be time, cost or fuel consumption. Usually, it also involves
speed optimization. Weather conditions (winds, waves and currents) are a very important
factor when it comes to obtain an efficient route and will not only affect the cargo of the
ships en route, but also the performance of the vessel (e.g. fuel consumption, emissions,
engine overload,etc...). This, at the same time, will influence the arrival time of the vessel.
It is therefore important, when designing an optimum maritime route, to take into account
such factors as to achieve reliable routes, not only in the terms of safety but also in terms of
Estimated Time of Arrival (ETA) and performance (fuel consumption).
Fig. 1.1 Container ship under rough weather conditions Source: gCaptain
Not just weather conditions are taking into account but the consequences of these towards
the ships. Hull and engines are considered in the design. The selection of the appropriate
method or algorithm it is very crucial as it will have an influence, in the quality of the results
and in the running times to obtain a feasible solution. Three Dimensional Modified Isochrone
(3DMI), Dynamic Programming or A* algorithm are some of the most utilized methods .
Calculate the optimum route, avoiding possible dangerous areas, achieving savings in the
fuel consumption and with good estimation on the arrival times is what optimization weather
routing services aim to achieve. Currently, there exist few companies working in weather
routing optimization. The design of such complex systems require a great effort as per the
numerous amount of factors involved.
The purpose of this Thesis is to research on the methods used, analyse the role that
environmental factors play when optimizing ship routes and assess the possible savings
achieved by a commercial ship weather routing service. Special attention will be given to the

Chapter 2
Background
First of all it is important to state what Maritime weather routing is, what the different authors
consider when designing an optimum ship route or under which criteria they do it. We
have found common basis that differ in the form but have the same content. Interesting as
a base, is the definition which gives Christiansen et al. (2007) , who define environmental
weather routing as "the choice of a shortest route or path in terms of e.g. costs, fuel or
time between two consecutive harbours or any coordinates and considering environmental
influences for this shortest route". Roh (2013) proposition lies on finding an economical
route combining two items: Sea state and fuel consumption that is influenced under the sea
conditions. Additionally, Sen and Padhy (2010) consider the optimal track based on weather
forecasts, ship’s motions and sea conditions. Gershanik (2011) highlight the importance
of considering sea state, especially its influence on the vessel regarding ship motions and
performance to reduce fuel consumption and emissions. Very relevant is the approach of
Chen (2007), who defines the factors affecting weather routing optimization, among which,
he emphasizes the need of taking into consideration ship motions and the loads due to
changing weather.This input is used to optimize the fuel consumption in combination with a
range of arrival times, under safe operational limits. Fang and Lin (2015) in their paper aim
to optimize the fuel consumption and ETA considering ship configurations,environmental
forces and operational conditions. Finally Lee et al. (2002) using the definition of Hanssen
and James (1960) define it as " the selection of an optimum track for a transoceanic crossing
by the application of long-range prediction of wind, waves and currents, to the knowledge of
how the routed vessel reacts to these variables" . We can see there is a common agreement
among the authors on which factors to consider when optimizing maritime routes.
In this chapter, a research on the works of maritime weather routing has been carried
out. Here, we will expose the different works done until today in the area of marine weather
routing. We have decided to, first, present in a chronological order the different works done

2.1
Related work
Marine weather routing has become a hotter topic throughout the years. The increase of
speed in computers, which helped to achieve more accurate and faster results, has played
a decisive role in the development of this field. Not only this, but the higher concern in
reducing emissions and, of course, economic reasons have put forward the research and
development on methods and tools in this field.
It was James (1957) developing the isochrone method as the one we consider the first
work on weather routing. Going deeper and making an advance search by the words ship,
weather and routing gives as results relevant works in the field in the decade of the 60’s and
the 70’s. For example Bleick and Faulkner (1964) expose the Theory of minimal-time ship
routing through time-dependent ocean wave height and direction fields is put to numerical
test with information provided by the U S Navy Fleet Numerical Weather Facility. In 1968
Dupin (1968) proposed the need for new data sources to reduce the exposure to unfavourable
weather conditions; the same year Haltiner et al. (1968) described two advances in the
calculus of variations method for minimal time ship routing (ocean wave field forecasts
and improved technique for varying time extreme ship tracks towards admissibility). In
the 70’s few more works start to appear and the first approach to solve the problem with
dynamic programming is found (Zopoli (1972)).Stochastic conditions are proposed in the
problem and expressed as a discrete decision process. It was in 1976 when Chen (1976)
published a paper in which he proposed a method to derive the optimum trajectory with the
use of dynamic programming, considering sea environment and ship responses . In 1977
Burger (1977) proposed the use of computer methods to consider weather conditions and
more frequent updates to be more accurate and to achieve better routes in terms of safety and
economy. Significant was the contribution of Chen (1978) with his PhD Thesis. In it we find
the first programme in Optimization of Maritime Weather Routing. This programme with
an environmental data input, was able to perform route optimization, through a stochastic
dynamic programming algorithm.
==PAGE:23==
2.1 Related work
7
In the decade of the 1980’s more works start to be written within this field and companies
as Ocean Inc. (Art, 1983) start to appear offering services on Maritime Weather Routing
in the market. New papers can be found discussing these new systems Hoffman (1980a),
Hoffman (1980b) and Petrie and Hoffman (1980). Petrie et al. (1984) demonstrated that there
was an improvement and a reduction in costs using the commercial weather routing services
and by the Optimal Ship Routing Algorithm between the ships sailing with and without
routine advisory. Motte et al. (1988) analysed the current methods on weather routing and
the considerations and constraints in ship-based weather routing procedures. We cannot
leave out the works of Perakis and Papadakis (1989a)Perakis and Papadakis (1989b) in
which they developed methodologies for the minimal time routing problem of a vessel in
a stationary and time-dependent environments and they discussed the implementation of
the methodologies developed, respectively.The methods used in this case had been optimal
control technologies which is an extension of the calculus of variations. Hagiwara (1989)
modified the isochrone method proposing an isochrone method with area partitioning, still
this method when computerized can cause problems with the so-called isochrone loops Tsou
and Cheng (2013) .

In this section we will introduced the most common used methods applied when optimizing
maritime weather routing. We will introduce the Modified Isochrone Method by Hagiwara,
continuing with Dynamic Programming, the Calculus of Variations and finally we will
present two other methods that we have considered relevant as the Dijkstra’s algorithm and
the A* algorithm.
2.2.1
Modified Isochrone Method
The Isochrone Method was originally created by James (1957) and later modified by Hagiwara
(1989) leading to its current name.
An Isochrone is a line represented in a map or a grid. These lines shape different
trajectories of a ship and have the commonality that the travel time for all of them is the same.
However, as Hagiwara introduced, the length of these lines will vary when environmental
influences are taking into account, hence according to the heading of the ship and the wave
direction, different lengths can be reached within the same times. New isochrones are
constructed at the end of each isochrone until the destination point is reached by any of the
isochrones. On the figure 2.1 the construction of the isocrhones can be observed.
The modification exposed by Hagiwara was done for its application to computers and
achieved to avoid the so-called Isochrone-loops. The Isochrone-loop is an irregularity in the
shape of an isochrone caused by the non-convexity of speed characteristics for given weather
data Szlapczynska and Smierzchalski (2007).
Hagiwara presented the method to minimise either time, fuel or cost. The function to be
minimised was the following one:

2.2.2
Dynamic Programming
Dynamic programming breaks a problem into stages, at which decisions take place and find
a recurrence. Dynamic programming is based on the principle of Optimality, also known as
Bellman Optimality Conditions, which we show in the lines below.
Principle of Optimality:
Supposing that the solution of a problem is the result of n decisions D1,D2,...Dn; if a given
sequence is optimal ,then the first k decisions must be optimal but also the last n - k decisions
must be optimal as well.
This principle adapted to maritime weather routing has to find the mininimal fuel path or
shortest path among the different points between origin and destination. This path should
go within a predefined grid where the different cells would represent the stages on the
problem. These cells contain information which, normally, define the sea conditions and the
geographical position.This is in the case of two-dimension. The grid might be defined in
different ways, as an example we are showing here the proposal from Shao et al. (2011) on
figure 2.2, where the grid is defined around the great circle route. The different set of points
represent the stages (from 1 to 16). ∆Y is the distance perpendicularly from the great circle
route and the distance between neighbouring stages is always ∆X .

Dijkstra’s algorithm
Dijkstra’s algorithm is used to solved the shortest path problem.
The shortest path problem consist of a directed network G = (V,E,w) (Figure 2.3) . This
network is composed by V vertices and E directed edges or arcs. w represents the costs
of traversing traversing the arcs . An origin vertex r is given . The objective of the problem
is to find for each v ∈V a shortest (with respect to w) directed path to from r to v if such
one exists. This algorithm is guaranteed to find a shortest path from the starting point to the
destination on condition that no negative edges can be found in the graph.
In the generic case, w represents the distance, but when applied to ship routing, the
distance can be substituted by any other "weight". This weight can be the objective function

When sailing through the sea, the performance of a ship is determined by the surrounding
conditions. Within these surrounding conditions we consider Winds, Waves and Currents;
we refer to these as Environmental Factors. These factors, equally, will affect the fuel
consumption. If a certain sailing speed and course wants to be maintained and the weather
conditions are adverse, a higher power output would be necessary and this would lead to
an increase in the fuel consumption. This increase in the bunker consumption follows the
so-called cubic law, that relates it proportionally to the sailing speed to a power that varies
around 3 depending on different factors. These environmental factors play a very important
role in routing, this is why in the following chapter we will introduce how the aforementioned
factors affect the dynamics of a ship and we will relate them to the fuel consumption, then a
section of how fuel consumption is calculated will be presented and finally, a section on how
to assess and quantify emissions will close this chapter.
3.1
Environmental factors
As already mentioned the factors affecting the performance of a ship will be winds,waves
and currents. These will have different influence in the routing and the consumption of the
ship. The first two have a relation as waves are due to wind blowing. These two have higher
impact in the routing procedures, though it is important not to underestimate currents as they
can also play a relevant role and depending on which areas, considerable savings can be
achieved.

Ocean currents and altimetry
It is clear that ocean currents have an effect on maritime routing (Chang et al. (2016)), still
we do not find them in the literature enough considered. Lately, the compatibility of using
satellite altimetry as routing information is gaining importance and the possibility of consider
it in the different softwares it can be seen.
Even though there are many papers or studies dealing with weather routing and environ-
mental influences, not many have addressed currents, or they do not give much relevance
to this factor. However, it has been reported by different authors the advantages of taking
them into account in routing. Lo et al. (1991) reported savings in commercial fleets up
to $ 70 millions in annual fuel costs (in figures of 1991). Looking more into details the
average savings percentage was different between different speeds 0.5 % in 25 knots ships,
1.2 % for 16-knots ships and 1.4% in 15-knots ships, these values also differ slightly if we
differentiate between North-Pacific and North Atlantic trades, being slightly higher in the
former . Later also Lo and McCord (1995) used ocean currents dynamics in their formulation
for the optimal fuel routing problem for the Gulf Stream Region, resulting in fuel savings
of 7.4 % and 4.5 % for east and westbound, respectively. Not only that, but McCord (1999)
also investigated the potential of strategic ship routing through dynamic currents derived
from satellite altimetry simulating current patterns. Four different scenarios were proposed,
in which some bias can be deleted achieving good results with respect to fuel consumption.
Furthermore, the potential of the ability to estimate dynamic currents is demonstrated.
More recently, Chang et al. (2016) proposed a very interesting approach. In this one,
taking advantage of West Boundary Currents, he presented a map for energy savings while
sailing. From geostrophic current data obtained of 21 years satellite-derived information,
they are able to provide a 1/3º x 1/3º global map of ocean surface currents.
All this, draws the idea that currents should not be overlooked. In the following sections
we will introduce the different factors affecting the currents, we will differentiate the currents

4.1
Types and causes of currents
We can differentiate between two origination types of currents, these might be either wind-
driven or thermohaline which means by differences due to salinity or temperature(Bowditch
(2002)). A different criteria might be also the depth (surface, intermediate, deep or bottom).
Another feature that characterises the currents is if they are Seasonal or Periodical . The
latter depends if the speed or direction changes cyclically , this is the case of tidal currents;
the former its characterized by the change due to seasonal winds.
The main cause of the currents is due to winds and the differences in the salinity and
temperature of the waters. The latter factor also depends on the depth and the topography of
the waters.
4.1.1
Wind driven
The winds cause a movement of the top surface of the water. This movement is not transmitted
to the bottom of the ocean but it is balanced due to the Coriolis force from 10 to 200 m depth.
This range comprises the Ekman Layer and within this layer the Ekman transport occurs .
Due to the deflection by the Coriolis force the Ekman transport does not occur in the same
direction as the wind. In the case of the Northern hemisphere is 90º towards the right and in
the Southern hemisphere 90º towards the left. d
This movements lead to a large scale convergence or divergence which derives in the
general oceanic circulation. This convergence of the Ekman transport result on what is called
Ekman Pumping. The effects of this Ekman Pumpking give raise to anticyclonic subtropical
gyres in the ocean basin in mid latitudes. In the subpolar region the suction provokes cyclonic
gyres .
Differences are found in the shape of the wind driven gyres. In the western boundaries,
currents are narrower, stronger and deeper; these gyres often are called stream. On the other
hand, if we look at the mid ocean eastern boundary, the gyres are broad, shallow and slow
moving; often called Drift currents. (Bowditch (2002))

Ekman Spiral and it is shown in 4.1. In the surface the velocity of the current is composed
by the sum of the Ekman, geostrophic, tidal and other currents. We can find that the difference
between the wind and the current direction on the surface can vary from 15 to 45º depending

There exist also currents due to density differences between locations. At a specific depth,

Coriolis force these movements toward the left in the Southern Hemisphere and towards the
right in the Northern Hemisphere. The sum of these forces (Coriolis and Subsurface Pressure

the geostrophic current speed is directly proportional.

4.1.3
Dynamic elements
Another element which is going to play a very important role in routing are the so-called
dynamic elements. These are elements that due to the changes in velocity and location of the
core current flow and of the rings and eddies shed from the core; make it difficult to forecast
and the changes between the strategic routes prescribed and the actual situation can differ
considerably (McCord (1999)).
Ocean eddies and rings
Ocean eddies are circular currents of water, the diameter can vary from 50-150 km. The
patterns are either cold or warm core and the currents flow around this core. If it is a cold core
the currents flow cyclonically, on the other hand if the case is a warm core, the current flow
anticyclonically. When these currents are very intense they are called rings. The maximum
speed associated with these are 2 knots.
We can find strongest variability in the region of Western Boundary Currents and in the
Circumpolar Current. The strength reduction is considerable at a distance of 200-400 km
from the boundary currents, as the mean currents are weaker in these regions. The strength
of the eddies can be sufficient to reverse the direction of the surface currents.
4.2
Oceanic circulation
We can find different areas with distinct currents around the world. These currents are
flowing constantly, with small changes throughout the year. As mentioned above, due to the
wind, there is a direct relation between the ocean currents and the general circulation of the
atmosphere. In some areas there are changes due to the season. This is the case in the Indian
Ocean,where currents are highly influenced by the monsoons.
We should differentiate between Western Boundary Currents (WBC) and Eastern Bound-
ary Currents (EBC), where the former have a higher influence in the routing practices. It
is important to highlight that strong currents( ∼2 knots ) are typical Western Boundary
Currents in the Pacific, Atlantic and Indian Ocean .The dynamics of WBC’s and EBC’s are
determined by the presence of a coast line (Chang et al. (2016). On the map below we can see

4.3
Altimetry
As mentioned above, the dynamic elements are the ones playing a more influential role in
operational routing and where potential routing benefits can be found (Lo et al. (1991)). Thus,
a higher attention should be aim to detect and track such phenomena.
There are few ways that currents and dynamic sea activity can be measured. One
is infrared imagery, still, this faces difficulties to measure the activity immediate below
the ocean surface (McCord (1999)). This immediate below activity will affect the ship’s
performance. Furthermore weather environment factors such as clouds, wind or waves can
contaminate sometimes these images (Joseph (2014)).
Satellite altimetry and technological developments in such field, however, can avoid the
aforementioned problems and provide a good information about the sea state which would
lead to a better operational planning. Though, this is a complex procedure and highly sophis-
ticated algorithms and ancillary information is needed in order to achieve good measurements
free of error (Joseph (2014)).

Ocean currents and altimetry
In the following section we will explain how satellite altimetry operates and the elements
involved in it .
Currently DTU Space is able to provide real-time data from operating altimetric missions.
This means data retrieved from satellites (CryoSat-2, SARAL and Jason-2). This data is
stored in the Radar Altimeter Database System (RADS) and then it can be delivered in the
following form:
• Sea Level Anomalies (SLA): defined by National Weather Service and NOAA (2013),
occurs when the 5 month running average of the interannual variation is at least 0.1
meters greater than or less than the long-term trend.
• Sea Weight Height (SWH): based on National Weather Service and NOAA (2009)
definition, is the average of the highest one-third (33%) of waves (measured from
trough to crest) that occur in a given period.
• Wind speed: the speed of the wind at the surface of the sea.
This information can be supplied every 12 hours or every 24. The latter can show
the observations of the last 3 days and the former shows the information every 12 hours.
Historical data can be also retrieved from the archives. This information can be retrieved by
region. The packages include the time, latitude, longitude, SLA, SWH and wind speed.
4.3.1
Altimetry measurements
To explain how satellite altimetry we have to define the different elements that are involved
in these calculations.
First, it is important to mention that the shape of the Earth is not exactly round and it is
more similar to an ellipsoid. This is the first element which is involved in the measurements
and this ellipsoid is defined as the Reference Ellipsoid. The second element involved in
the calculations is the Marine Geoid. The Marine Geoid represents the sea surface in the
case that the water mass would be bounded to the earth as an equipotential surface with no
variations nor movements due to the gravity or currents. Because of the different shape in the
previous two elements, in some areas we can find that they lie far from each other. However
due to gravity variations, the shape of the geoid is oftern irregular and in some cases departs
from the reference ellipsoid (Joseph (2014).

5.2

In the following subsections we will introduce which elements define the voyage and how
they are built, as well as under which objectives and constraints is the optimization carried

5.2.1
Problem description and definition of a voyage
We want to analyse the performance of a ship in a trans-atlantic/trans-oceanic voyage and the
different alternatives that the vessel has to reach to the destination. These alternatives will be
considered under the previous concept of weather routing optimization.
As we have mentioned previously, we need at least one origin and a destination to define
the trip. Furthermore waypoints can be added to the route, so now we can define the voyage
as a series of ordered waypoints from the origin to the destination. These points are connected
by legs. If we compare it with a network, these legs are like the arcs connecting the different
vertices. Finally, we have different options to route the vessel between two points, these
different types of legs are listed below:
1. Great Circle (GC): the connection between two points can follow the great circle path
which would define the shortest path in terms of distance between both points.
2. Rhumb Line (RL): it is also possible to join two waypoints or an origin and destination
with a RL. The RL connect two points with a line at a constant heading, traversing
each meridian at the same angle.
3. Optimal track: it is also possible to join two waypoints as the optimal track leg,
this means according to one of the optimization criteria (minimum crossing time or
minimum fuel consumption taking into consideration the weather conditions) that we
will present in the following section 5.2.2.
4. Navigation: finally there is also the option to set the navigation manually, indicating
the duration and the fuel consumption in metric tonnes (mt).

5.2.2
Optimization criteria
The voyage optimization for Sea Planner considers a ship travelling from A to B in a safe
and economic manner. Safe means that the dynamic limitations of the ship are considered
for the crew and cargo. Economic means that the voyage is performed achieving the smallest
amount of fuel consumed. The voyage can be optimized according to one of the following
objectives:
• minimum crossing time
• minimum fuel consumption
These objectives are all the time subject to a specific ETA that is defined prior to the
optimization. The first objective will show the fastest crossing. The second will aim to reduce
as much as possible the fuel consumption, but as we have said, always subjected to a specific
ETA.
Under the second criteria it has also to be defined one of the following power configurations:
• Constant power: the power of the propeller shaft will be maintained constant along
the voyage. This will result on a variable speed of the ship achieved depending on the
sea conditions. Under adverse sea conditions a reduction on the speed will occur and
under favourable environmental conditions a better speed will be achieved This option
==PAGE:73==
5.3 Case studies
57
will lead to a minimization of the fuel consumption as there will not be modifications
of the power and hence a constant FC output will be achieved.
• Optimized speed: the speed is adjusted in a way such as the time-derivative of the fuel
consumption is constant. This means that the we will have variable speed maintaining
constant fuel consumption.
• Constant RPM: propulsion mode will be set to constant RPM on the propeller shaft,
this implies that depending on the environmental conditions a higher or lower speed
will be achieved.
• Fixed calm water speed: in this case the propulsion is set to constant to the propeller
shaft which will be equivalent to the calm water speed. In this case the ETA is not
applied.

Weather data influence
As anticipated, one of the aims of this Thesis is to assess the importance of the weather
forecast and optimization to design an efficient maritime route, achieving savings in fuel
consumption and a safer route. Therefore, in the following pages we will assess how
important the role the weather forecast is really playing in this optimization, as well as
the role of currents.
The instance chosen is WB.1.11 Le Havre-Quebec Westbound, with the Fixed ETA
and Opt. Speed configuration, Optimal Great Circle legs and the arrival time to achieve
an average speed of 20 knots, options which we consider, as in the previous analysis, a
reasonable configuration.
To analyse in detail which is the influence of the weather forecasts and the currents,
we have created different scenarios. The scenarios compare the actual forecast and
modified versions of the actual one. As previously mentioned, the weather forecast is a
XML file composed of different parameters. The modifications to these parameters have
been done editing the actual forecasts so we have the following alternatives to choose
among:
• Weather: original forecast with all the elements included in it.
• No weather: the forecast is eliminated from the optimization such as the only
resistance encountered by the vessel is the calm water resistance.
• No currents: The parameters regarding currents are eliminated from the actual
forecast, hence its effects are removed.
• Currents only: All the parameters from the weather forecast are eliminated but the
currents. Thus we can assess the effect of these ones on the routing.
• Currents updated: A new version of the currents-only. In this one, certain parame-
ters of the current speed are changed in such a way that the effect is more severe.
This wants to represent the uncertainty that weather forecasts show in real life,
therefore a more severe scenario can foresee the possible inaccuracies.
• Scenario 1: Weather vs. No weather.
==PAGE:85==
5.3 Case studies
69
The purpose of this scenario is to analyse how much the weather conditions account
for the total voyage . For this we will compare the fuel consumption with the
actual forecast with a scenario without weather forecast, which will calculate the
fuel consumption just taking into account the calm water resistance.
• Scenario 2: Weather vs. No currents
To assess how much the currents account in the overall optimization. Doing this
and comparing this results with the actual ones we will be able to assess the weight
of the currents.
• Scenario 3: No weather vs. Currents only
The previous analysis would not be complete if we do not account the currents by
itself and compare them with the calm water resistance performance, therefore the
currents only file is used to assess the voyage.
• Scenario 4: Weather vs. Currents only
In this case we want to assess the weight that the currents have by itself in a
scenario just accounting for these and compare with the actual situation.
• Scenario 5: Currents only vs. Updated Currents
The purpose of this scenario is to analyse how much can affect the uncertainty in
the weather forecasts regarding currents and how a more severe current forecast
would affect the results.

This variable shows the fuel consumption difference between two alternatives for the
specific scenario, or in other words how much the factors included in the specific forecast
accounts for the total fuel consumption, f.eg. in the first scenario we can see that the
weather forecast accounts for the 15% of the total fuel consumption if we compare it to
the calm water resistance (no weather).
The results from this analysis are considerably remarkable, we can see that the effect
of currents in this case is almost insignificant, its influence does not reach to the 1% of
the total fuel consumption. As we have already mentioned, also the effect of the weather
forecast is accounting for not so high percentage (15%). Finally, we can see that a more
severe current definition can help to a better planning for the voyage. Considering a more

Chapter 6
Conclusions and further research
During the elaboration of this Thesis, the topic of Ship Weather Routing has been in-
vestigated and analysed under various scenarios. In this chapter we present the general
conclusions, the drawbacks and limitations faced by the method and finally what we
have consider to be interesting in the future of maritime weather routing and suggestions
for further research in the area.
6.1
General conclusions
In the literature review we presented the methods that are mostly utilised in the opti-
mization of maritime weather routing. Among them, we furher investigated the most
popular ones, namely the Modified Isochrone method, Dynamic Programming, Calculus
of Variations, Dijkstra’s and A* algorithms. It has also been seen that new algorithms
started to be incorporated into the procedures of optimizing ship weather routing. Genetic
Algorithms or Ant Colony Optimization belong to this last group.
An analysis of the market regarding this kind of products has been carried out. During
it, the different companies currently working on the field and their specific programmes
have been shown. The different features of the programmes have been also explained and
the issue of incorporating ECDIS to such type of routing software have been discussed,
commenting especially on the suitability for such incorporation.
In chapter 3 we analysed the different environmental factors involved in weather rout-
ing optimization procedures. The effect of Waves, Winds, Currents and other factors (i.e.
displacement) in the total resistance has been presented. All these factors have an effect
on the total resistance faced by a vessel when sailing, hence in the fuel consumption.
The latter has been related to the sailing speed introducing different models exposed by
a variety of authors. This has been, as well, linked both to the Effective Power and to
the Resistance of the Ship. At the end of this chapter, a methodology to calculate CO2
==PAGE:100==
84
Conclusions and further research
emissions has been introduced. This is based on the type of fuel used by the vessel and
the fuel consumption.
The importance that currents may have in the routing procedures have been presented
and the potential of them in achieving fuel savings have been reported. It has been seen
that dynamic elements are the ones that will potentially play a more decisive role in the
future. With the aid of satellite altimetry better and more accurate current forecasts will
be able to be predicted.
One of the aims of this Thesis was to assess the potential of a commercial routing
service to achieve savings, in comparison to traditional methods. To do this, different
case studies have been designed and the commercial software Sea Planner by Force
Technology has been used. The methodology to assess the different savings have been
broken down in different stages. Three different cases have been designed, two trans-
oceanic voyages (5.3.2 and 5.3.3) and one Short Sea Shipping Case (5.3.4). In addition,
two different forecasts were used. The first one was used for the first two cases and a
last one, with a greater accuracy was used for the third case. Then, different types of
analyses have been defined.
Firstly, the savings comparing traditional routing (i.e. Rhumb Line and Great Circle)
with optimized alternatives have been analysed. Showing, in fact, savings between the
traditional and optimized alternatives. Although the comparisons have shown small
savings, it can be stated that they align with the savings reported in the IMO GHG study
2009 between 1 % and 4 % (IMO (2009)). The first two cases show smaller differences,
aligned with the values already mentioned, while the third case showed slightly higher
differences for the Southbound (around 2%) and considerably higher for the Northbound
( between 7% and 9%). Though, these higher differences are attributed to a specific
episode of currents at a specific time period. A rolling horizon approach, analyzing the
following days has shown that the values stabilise around 2% , similarly to the ones
encountered in previous cases.
Secondly, a draught and trim analysis have been carried out for case 1. The results
of it have shown the advantages of negative trims by itself and the savings towards
proportional even keel configurations (between 5% and 7%). On the other hand, as it
was expected, savings have been also proven the lower the draughts.
Thirdly, the influence of the weather forecasts have been assessed as the backbone of
this type of programmes. This analysis have been broken down into different scenarios
and the different elements of the forecast have been assessed separately. The first two
cases share the same forecast, hence comparisons between these two can be made. These
results have shown that the influence of currents within these cases are almost non
significant. Quantitatively, the currents effects on the forecast do not reach the 1% over

the total fuel consumption shares.
Another interesting finding is the accounting of the weather forecast in the overall fuel
consumption. From the different cases and scenarios, it can be concluded that the weather
forecasts accounts for around a 15% of the total fuel consumption. This means that the
rest of the fuel consumed is due to the calm water resistance. Finally, it can be seen that
a more severe currents forecast, which has been interpreted as a more accurate currents
prediction, will lead to higher shares on the fuel consumption over the total. Some of
these values are backed by the third case, showing, particularly, that the influence of the
weather accounts for 18% in the total fuel consumption. However, the higher level of
resolution has shown higher differences with respect to currents.
Regarding the third case, where a different forecast has been used, it can be stated that
the effect of currents play a more significant role. Savings achieved due to the currents
account for an 8% of the overall fuel consumption in the Southbound. In the Northbound
scenario, however, the contribution to the overall fuel consumption is reduced, but there
are still some savings around 1%. Undoubtedly, these figures can be attributed to the
higher resolution of the weather forecast used.
As a final remark, optimised Great Circle legs have shown the highest savings in
most of the routes, this is logical due to the greater flexibility that these type of legs
show compared to the ones using Rhumb Lines. Equally, optimized speeds power
configuration shows better results due to the greater flexibility that they can offer during
a voyage modifying the speed, while using Constant RPM or Constant power limits these
possibilities. On the other hand, the latter configuration are more suitable for the engines.
6.2
Drawbacks and limitations
The savings achieved by Sea Planner have been reported. It can be seen that these
are aligned to what is stated in IMO (2009) and some of the providers of this type of
programmes (i.e. Jeppesen). However, we should highlight the drawbacks and limitations
of the method.
The resolution of the forecast had a considerable effect in the routes showing notable
differences depending on the forecast used. This, naturally, is an aspect to take into
consideration, especially with respect to currents.
Except for some specific instances, the best savings are achieved by using the optimal
speed configurations. Analysing the planning speed for the voyages, it can be acknowl-
edged that the speed changes are numerous. The planning speeds have shown speed
changes every 40 minutes between waypoints. This practice, though, does not seem
very practical neither for the engine of the vessel, nor for the captain as speed cannot be
changed in so short time intervals.

Conclusions and further research
It is worth commenting on the the min Enroute time speed configuration. This might
not seem suitable for liner or industrial shipping, but for situations where the owner and
the charter of the vessel are the same person (i.e. RoRo, oil companies transporting crude
oil) can offer advantages.
On the basis of the case studies, long haul cases have shown longer running times to
optimize the routes. This must be applied specifically for optimized Great Circle and
optimized Rhumb Lines, which optimizations have taken longer than 6 minutes. This can
lead to drawbacks in running the program offshore in the vessel computer. Furthermore,
complications can be faced with the graphical card, the programme requires a significant
effort from the computational point of view. Besides, as it has been mentioned, the
optimised solutions present a higher number of middle waypoints, which affects the
running time. At this point, considerations of optimising the route onshore and being
sent to the vessel afterwards might be considered.
6.3
Further research and future considerations
Different components involved in the optimization of ship weather routing have been
analysed. Among others, we have focused on the potential of currents and satellite
altimetry. Taking advantage of them to achieve more accurate predictions is something
that we have to focus on in the near future. Furthermore, the adaptation of existing
algorithms or the use of new ones should be beneficial. Finally, the possible combination
of them seems to be an appealing alternative.
The trends nowadays show that the collection of data, the so called Big Data, is
taking on in many fields of our society and that its application can be very beneficial in
this area. This collection of data might be a very interesting approach in the future of
ship weather routing. The collection of voyage data through sensors and the subsequent
analysis, compared with the results obtained by this kind of programmes, could help the
fine tuning of the algorithms and determine the elements that can be improved.
Regarding the weather forecasts, inaccuracies and high uncertainty have been en-
countered. Therefore, an alternative to consider in the future is the possible combination
of weather forecasts from open sources or different providers. At the same time, an
interesting consideration would be the customization of the forecast by the user, speci-
fying the level of detail, or the parameters of the forecast that wants to be retrieved by
a specific Meteorological Institute or administration (f.eg. retrieving winds and waves
from a meteorological institute and currents from an hydrological institute, to combine
them afterwards). This option would need, though, an homogenization of the different
forecast files and weather and software providers.
