Abstract
For many cochlear implant (CI) users the understanding of speech is very good
in quiet conditions, however, drops substantially in realistic conditions with
background noise. The goal of this thesis was to objectively evaluate the ef-
fectiveness of CI stimulation strategies in challenging conditions.
Therefore,
a vowel identiﬁcation framework was developed based on a Gaussian Mixture
Model (GMM) classiﬁer. The framework was trained with diﬀerent vowels in
quiet and the confusion patterns were compared to the response of CI users.
This framework was then used to evaluate the ability of three diﬀerent channel
selection strategies to improve the vowel identiﬁcation in the presence of back-
ground noise: (1) the frequently-used advanced combinational encoder (ACE)
which selects channels based on energy; (2) a speech presence probability (SPP)
based channel selection strategy and (3) an ideal channel selection based on a
priori knowledge of speech and noise. Finally, the thesis includes implemen-
tations of diﬀerent parameters and processing methods to improve the perfor-
mance of the SPP-based stimulation strategy.
The SPP-based channel strategy showed promising results in the presence of
stationary noise. With a vowel identiﬁcation accuracy of around 70% at 5 and
10 dB signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), it showed similar results as the ideal channel
selection.
At 0 dB SNR performance dropped to 56%.
In comparison, the
ACE stimulation strategy achieved an accuracy of 32%, 40% and 55% at 0, 5
and 10 dB SNR respectively. However, no substantial beneﬁt, using the SPP
strategy, was achieved in conditions where the interfering noise was speech-
modulated, where performance followed that of the ACE. The ideal, but not
realizable, channel selection strategy achieved an accuracy of around 70% in all
noise conditions. This indicates that improvement is still achievable for the SPP-
based channel selection strategy, especially in speech-modulated background
noise.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
For millions of people, the sense of hearing is completely nonexistent - an ability
most of us take for granted [1]. Until the 1960s, where scientist began experi-
menting with electrically stimulating the auditory nerves on deaf people, these
profoundly deaf people had to use lip reading and sign language for communica-
tion with others. In the ﬁrst cochlear implants (CI) only a single electrode were
used for stimulation, however, in the 1980s multichannel systems began to arise
with electrode arrays stimulating multiple sites in the cochlear [2]. To date, over
300,000 patients worldwide have received CI implants [3]. Even though many
CI patients have been successful in understanding speech in quiet environments,
speech intelligibility degrades quickly in the presence of background noise. This
is a critical issue since most realistic conditions with speech communication will
include some kind of background noise.
Today, one of the most frequently-used CI processing strategies is based on
advanced combinational encoding (ACE), which uses the energy in the electrode
array as the deciding factor to select a subset of 22 channels [4]. It usually
selects 8 out of 22 channels, since studies found that 8 channels is a good trade-
oﬀbetween recognition and the spectral resolution in each channel [5]. Many CI
users with the ACE strategy implemented usually have better speech recognition
score in quiet compared to alternatives such as the SPEAK and CIS strategies
[6]. However, whenever background noise is present the ACE may select channels
with no speech information because it only selects the most energetic channels.
==PAGE:12==
2

The ACE cannot distinguish between speech and noise information since energy
is the only selection factor. If the noise contains more energy and has a diﬀerent
frequency spectrum than the target speech, the ACE may simply select channels
with no speech information. Consequently, the CI user might miss important
speech cues that would make him/her understand the spoken words.
If the
channel selection factor was based on the presence of speech instead of energy,
the background noise would not matter as much (unless the background noise
is interfering speech).
This thesis investigates alternative channel selection strategies that either ex-
ploit prior knowledge of speech or detects speech via a speech presence prob-
ability algorithm. A strategy like that selects the channels with the highest
likelihood of containing speech information and thereby may increase speech
intelligibility even in environments with high level of noise.
A proper evaluation of CI stimulation strategies is quite challenging. Listening
studies are usually time consuming and ﬁnding the test persons can be a struggle.
Furthermore, CI users are accustomed to their own device strategy and may
score worse with a new CI strategy that might have performed better over time.
Therefore, an objective evaluation based on a vowel classiﬁcation framework has
been developed in MATLAB.
The ACE strategy, an ideal channel selection strategy and the speech detection
strategy are evaluated using the classiﬁcation framework. The processing in the
framework incorporates the function of a CI by using the Nucleus MATLAB
Toolbox developed by Cochlear [7]. The framework is trained with diﬀerent
vowels in quiet conditions and the confusion pattern is compared to the responses
of CI users.
1.2
Thesis structure
The structure of this thesis is organized as follows:
• Chapter 1 and 2 are introductory. Chapter 2 provides the reader with
the background of audiology and fundamentals of CI and its operation,
including a description of well known CI stimulation strategies.
• Chapter 3 introduces two alternative CI stimulation strategies designed to
improve speech intelligibility in noise.

2.1
Physiology of Hearing
The auditory system can be split up in three sections: the outer, middle and
inner ear. In a functioning ear, sound waves travel from the outer ear (pinna and
auditory canal) and are transformed into mechanical displacement in the mid-
dle ear. The eardrum also called the tympanic membrane is a thin cone-shaped
membrane at the end of the auditory canal that separates the outer from the
middle ear. The middle ear consist of three small bones called malleus, incus and
stapes together known as the ossicles. The stapes is connected to the cochlea, a
bone-structured spiral-shaped cavity, via the oval window. The function of the
middle ear is to transmit the vibrations of the tympanic membrane to the ﬂuid
inside the cochlea [8].
The cochlea makes 2.5 turns in its spiral-shaped structure and the total length
is approximately 30 mm. The basilar membrane divides two champers in the
cochlea called Scala Tympani from Scala Media. On top of the basilar mem-
brane an important structure called the organ of Corti in Scala Media is found.
The organ of Corti consists of one row of inner hair cells and three rows of outer
hair cells. Pressure variations from the stapes at the oval window cause displace-
ments of the basilar membrane equivalent to the frequencies of the sound waves
oscillating the tympanic membrane [8]. Thus, the basilar membrane functions
as a frequency analyzer. The displacements of the basilar membrane deforms
the inner and outer hair cells, resulting in a chemical process that initiates ac-

tion potentials in the associated auditory nerve ﬁbers. The perception of the
original signal is achieved by these neural signals, interpreted in the auditory
cortex of the brain.
2.2
Cochlear Implants
For centuries it has been impossible to restore hearing to the deaf.
It was
not until over 50 years ago scientists began to restore hearing to deaf people
by adding electrical impulses to the auditory nerve [9].
Today the cochlear
implant, a prosthetic device, can be implanted in the inner ear. The result is
partial hearing for profoundly deaf individuals.
Many cochlear implants have been developed over the last 30-40 years and
according to [10] modern devices have the following features in common: 1)
a microphone that picks up the surrounding sounds, 2) a signal-processor that
converts it into an electrical signal based on a stimulation strategy and processes
the signal, 3) a transmission system that transmits the electrical signals to the
electrodes (single or multichannel), which are implanted into the cochlea, see
Figure 2.1. In multichannel cochlear implants electrode arrays are inserted in the
cochlea to stimulate diﬀerent auditory nerve ﬁbers at diﬀerent places. Electrodes
near the base of the cochlea are stimulated with high frequency signals and low
frequency signals near the apex. It is the signal processors main function to
decompose the input signal into its frequency components as a healthy cochlea
would do it. The frequencies are split into diﬀerent frequency bands or channels
and transmitted to the associated electrodes [10]. The implant is dependent on
suﬃcient surviving auditory nerve ﬁbers being present at the electrode positions
in the cochlea [10]. Once stimulated, the nerve ﬁbers ﬁre and propagate neural
impulses to the brain, which perceives the impulses as sound. The perceived
loudness of the sound is dependent on the number of nerve ﬁbers activated
and their ﬁring rate. The number of active nerve ﬁbers are a function of the
amplitude of the stimulus current.
Thus, the loudness can be controlled by
varying the amplitude of the stimulus current. The pitch of the sound, on the
other hand, is related to the placement of the stimulus in the cochlea [10].

2.3.1
Continuous Interleaved Sampling (CIS)
The CIS strategy and its variations are widely used CI processing strategies.
The CIS method delivers a train of biphasic pulses to the electrodes which do
not overlap [12]. Thus, only a single electrode is stimulated at a time, which
avoids a problem from previous strategies using simultaneous stimulation. The
problem were caused by a summation of electrical ﬁelds between individual
electrodes [12]. A diagram of the CIS strategy is shown on Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: CIS block diagram [13].
The sound is captured by a microphone, the signal pre-emphasized and passed
through a band-pass ﬁlter bank. The envelope of the ﬁltered signals are ex-
tracted by full-wave rectiﬁcation and low-pass ﬁltering. The envelope is then
mapped to ﬁt the patient’s speciﬁc dynamic range by using a non-linear com-

logarithmic).
Finally, the signal is modulated with
biphasic pulses, which are transmitted to the associated electrodes in an inter-
leaved fashion. The amplitude of the pulses is proportional to the amplitude of
the envelopes [12].
2.3.2
Advanced Combinational Encoder (ACE)
The ACE strategy is used in most of the CI systems manufactured by Cochlear
Corporate and is based on SPEAK, a previously used channel selection strat-
egy. The SPEAK and ACE strategy uses spectral peaks as the channel selection
factor and selects therefore only the most energetic channels. The ACE typi-
cally selects a subset of 8 channels out of the 22 for stimulation. This is with
a stimulation rate of 900 pulses per second (pps) for each electrode, whereas
the SPEAK uses a stimulation rate of 250 pps per electrode [14]. The ACE
stimulation rate, however, can be varied to optimize performance for certain CI
users [15]. The block diagram of the ACE strategy is illustrated in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: ACE block diagram [13].
The SPEAK and ACE are based on a N-of-M principle, which means that a
number of channels, N, is selected out of a total of M channels (N < M). The
processor selects the N’th envelope output with most energy and, thus, only N

channels are selected [13]. This is in contrast to the CIS strategy, that uses all of
the integrated electrodes and does not selects a subset of those for stimulation.
As mentioned frequency bands containing low energy are not represented in
the stimulation pattern. Consequently, CI users can perceive certain spectral
features enhanced by the ACE. This could be a reason that users of the ACE
strategy usually performs better compared with users of the CIS strategy [15].
As an example a paper [6] concluded that CI listeners in two separate studies
scored 6-9 percentage points better using ACE compared to CIS.
2.4
Nucleus MATLAB Toolbox
The Nucleus MATLAB Toolbox (NMT) is a collection of MATLAB software
implementing algorithms used in Cochlear’s Nucleus CI products [7]. It is used
by digital signal processing (DSP) engineers to easily implement and test new
algorithms for Nucleus CIs only by using MATLAB.
The signal ﬂow of the SPEAK, ACE and CIS strategies through the NMT is
shown in Figure 2.4. Though, the following is only explaining the ACE despite
many similarities. The microphone input in MATLAB is a sampled audio ﬁle
with a sampling frequency of 16 kHz. The microphone frequency response is
applied in the Front End part together with pre-emphasis and input scaling.
The signal is then passed though a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) ﬁlterbank
that converts the audio into a Frequency Time Matrix (FTM) very similar to
a spectrogram. The ”Sampling & Selection” step extracts the envelope of the
FTM channels and discards the ones with lowest energy for each time frame until
the number of selected channels are left. The ﬂow diagram of the selection is
illustrated in Figure 3.1. Moreover, a random patients electrical dynamic range
is mapped, compressed by a Loudness Growth Function (LGF) and collated
into sequences. Finally, the signal is translated into pulses that are send to the
”electrodes”.

Chapter 3
CI Processing in Noise
In the previous chapter CI processing was described for two diﬀerent stimulation
strategies, the CIS and the ACE. Both strategies (as many others) are designed
to work best in quiet conditions. In this chapter two alternative stimulation
strategies are introduced, which both select channels based on speech presence.
The underling theory of how the algorithms work are also explained. Finally,
the two new strategies are compared to the CIS and ACE in noise.
3.1
Ideal Channel Selection Strategy
Instead of selecting the most energetic channels as the ACE stimulation strategy
does, the ideal channel selection strategy uses a priori knowledge about the SNR
in each subband to select the channels containing the most speech information.
The Ideal Binary Mask (IBM) [16] is a binary matrix that classiﬁes the time-
frequency (T-F) representation of some noisy speech into target-dominated (the
speech) and masker-dominated (the noise) T-F units. Thus, the speech is rep-
resented by a 1 and noise by 0 in the T-F domain. To create the IBM a priory
information about both speech and noise are a necessity. This means that the
IBM will never work in real-life applications, since prior knowledge of the present
noise are usually not possible. The IBM represents the ceiling performance that
would be possible if there was no error in estimating the speech-dominated
channels, which makes it a good "goal" for other strategies.

In this chapter the developed classiﬁcation framework is explained. The frame-
work is created to objectively evaluate diﬀerent channel selection strategies. The
goal is to emulate the behavior of CI users, so the evaluations of diﬀerent stim-
ulation strategies are comparable with data from CI users. If relative similar
vowel confusions are achieved, the framework would seem to be a good approx-
imation of a CI user and can be used for further processing using the diﬀerent
strategies.
The perception data, used to evaluate how well the classiﬁer mimics CI users,
are examined. Finally, a comparison between the classiﬁer and the perceptual
data is conducted and discussed.
The classiﬁcation framework is written in MATLAB in four diﬀerent scripts to
avoid one long confusing script, to simplify the process and most of all to reduce
time. The four parts consists of:
1. Creating the feature spaces
2. Training stage
3. Test and evaluation
4. Validation and evaluation

It is important that the vowel sound ﬁles used for training, testing and validation
are not used twice. This is to avoid that the classiﬁer gets a 100% match in the
evaluation, which in reality would be very unlikely and would not give a general
view on the identiﬁcation performance. Throughout this thesis 720 vowels sound
ﬁles is used for both training and tests and 228 ﬁles for validation. This will use
the total amount of 1668 vowel ﬁles of the database (see section 4.1).

4.2
Creating the Feature Space
This stage extracts and processes the feature space of the vowel sound ﬁles for
training and validation.
In the algorithm 720 ﬁles for training and 228 ﬁles for validation are randomly
selected in a way that each vowel gets an equally amount of ﬁles. Thus, for
training each vowel would have 60 ﬁles each. Since the training stage only uses
ﬁles with no added noise the SNR is set to inﬁnity in this case. The feature
space is found by processing the sound ﬁles trough the NMT, using the ACE
map settings [7], except the process is stopped right before the channel selection
(before Sampling & Selection stage in Figure 2.4). This way, the process from
microphone to just before channel selection is simulated. The feature space is
therefore a FTM with 22 channels frequency bands, corresponding to the 22
electrodes in the CI and the number of time frames correspondent to the ﬁle
length.
All input sound processed in the identiﬁcation framework are performed before
the LGF. This is because the data will be compressed by the LGF depending
on the speciﬁc mapping of the CI user and sound processing should therefore
be executed before.
The validation feature spaces are looped over diﬀerent SNR and noise types.
The SNRs are 0, 5 and inﬁnite dB (no noise). The noise added to the sound
ﬁles are for the validation of type ICRA1 and ICRA7 (see section 5.1.1). For the
validation feature space there is an option to choose either IBM, EBM or ACE
with no channel selection. An example of a feature space from four diﬀerent
vowels are illustrated in Figure 4.2.
Finally, the output data is saved all together as a MATLAB-struct containing
the data from the training and the validation feature spaces. The training data
are concatenated to one feature space for each vowel. The validation feature
spaces are saved separately for each vowel and the diﬀerent combinations of
SNR and noise types. Furthermore, extra data info such as the used parameters
and whether or not the IBM/EBM is used, are saved as well.

4.3
Training the Classiﬁer
The training stage is where the classiﬁcation takes place. The feature spaces for
the training ﬁles saved in the ﬁrst stage are used in this stage to train a GMM
classiﬁer that contains all the unique features for each of the vowels.

In this thesis the Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) is used as a classiﬁer, how-
ever, only a short explanation of the method is described here, for more in-detail
information and the mathematics behind see [22, 21, 23].
In statistics, a mixture model is a way of representing the presence of subpopula-
tions in an overall population. The mixture model corresponds to a mixture or
superposition of distributions, that represents the overall population [23], in our

4.3.1.1
Netlab - a MATLAB toolbox
Throughout this thesis a MATLAB toolbox called Netlab, generally used for
pattern recognition, is used for implementing the GMMs. The code is written
by Ian Nabney and Christopher Bishop [24] and is based on techniques from
Bishop’s book ”Neural Networks for Pattern Recognition” including recent de-
velopments in the ﬁeld [24].
4.3.2
The Training Script
The GMM parameters are set as the ﬁrst thing in the script. For more informa-
tion about these and their meaning see section 5.1.2.1. Afterwards, the feature
spaces for the 12 vowels are concatenated into one long data set, however, each
vowel data point is labeled with a number from 1-12. This way of training the
GMM classiﬁer is called supervised learning. It is supervised since the classiﬁer
already knows the diﬀerent vowels apart because of the labeling. Therefore, it
only needs to learn the frame-based features, instead of trying to separate all
the vowel features without guidance.
The feature space has 22 channels for each time frame, giving the classiﬁer 22
dimensions. Each frame is an observation for the GMM model, which calcu-
lates a mean and covariance of that observation, compared to the others. The
more vowels used for training, the greater mean estimation and thereby better
classiﬁer representation of the vowel.
Finally, after ﬁnishing training the GMM model data are saved in a MATLAB-
struct including all used parameters.

4.4.1
Missing Data Techniques
In missing data recognition, diﬀerent approaches exists for utilizing the knowl-
edge of reliable (target dominated) and unreliable (noise dominated) T-F units.
The two approaches are [25]:
1. full marginalization (FM) and
2. bounded marginalization (BM)
The FM ignores the unreliable T-F units and determines the likelihood of the
reliable T-F units being speech. The bounded marginalization also exploits the
unreliable T-F units by computing counter-evidence based on the concept of
energetic masking [25].
The advantage of using missing data techniques is that no prior knowledge of
the acoustic environment or the interfering noise is required. Therefore, the
model is trained only with clean speech [25].
A critical factor that limit the use of missing data techniques is the accuracy of
the binary mask. The IBM in this regard is usually quite accurate when using
the BM with very high speech recognition scores [26]. However, when using the
EBM misclassiﬁcation of the T-F units in the binary mask will increase and
the achieved speech recognition will decrease. This is specially true in highly
ﬂuctuating non-stationary background noise [26].
As shown in Figure 5.2, BM improve performance compared to FM and baseline
GMM classiﬁer. This is simply because it exploits the unrealiable T-F units.
Even though the T-F unit is speciﬁed as unrealiable, important speech cues
could still be present and, thereby, give the classiﬁer a better model of the
vowel. Though, in theory the EBM channel selection performance would be
better using BM, the FM is more in line with how the CIs and the channel
selection works. In reality the unrealiable T-F units will be discarded and won’t
be available for processing. Therefore, the FM will be the primary classiﬁcation
method used throughout this thesis.
4.4.2
Testing and Evaluation Script
The testing stage is build up almost as in the feature space stage. 720 vowel ﬁles
are processed either with no channels selected, ACE, IBM or EBM using baseline

GMM method or FM. When a test with no channels selected is performed
the vowel sound ﬁle is initially processed with a speciﬁc noise type and SNR.
Afterwards it is processed with the NMT choosing 22 channels.
After the processing and feature extraction, the feature spaces are evaluated
with the GMM classiﬁer. The probabilities found by the classiﬁers are integrated
across frames and the model, that matches one of the trained vowels best, is
chosen. Identiﬁed vowels are compared with the correct ones, which were labeled
during the feature extraction and setup in a confusion matrix. The classiﬁcation
accuracy is then the % correct vowel identiﬁcations across the diagonal.
4.5
Validation and Evaluation
The purpose of the validation script is to check vowel identiﬁcation performance
using new processing techniques or diﬀerent parameters. It works basically as
the testing and evaluation step but with fewer sentences and noise types. In this
way time can be saved while evaluating new and possibly many combinations of
diﬀerent parameters to achieve best identiﬁcation performance using the IBM
or EBM channel selection strategy.

5.2.4
Improving Spectral Resolution for the EBM
Choosing a window function and its length in the STFT domain based on the
DFT is a trade-oﬀbetween high spectral resolution using long windows, or high
temporal resolution using short windows. The windows used in FFT ﬁlterbanks
are commonly chosen to be in the range of 20-30 ms. However, in hearing aid
applications such as a CI, there is a strong focus on real-time processing, thus
the window size is typically shorter.
The CI settings in this project gives a
windows size of 8 ms, resulting in a low spectral but high temporal resolution.
In an attempt to increase the spectral resolution and increase classiﬁcation per-
formance of the EBM strategy, but still use the same block size of 8 ms, a
method from a study [32] is used and implemented. The block size is not di-
rectly altered, however, a buﬀer is created to save the previous blocks up to 64
ms. This means the ﬁrst cycle has a windows size of 8 ms, the next 16 ms and so
on. The signal is zero padded to accommodate the longer window. In order to
give more weight to the most recent block samples, the window is asymmetric
and skewed to the right.
The results is shown in Figure 5.12 for windows of the following sizes: 8 ms with
standard Hann window, 16 ms, 32 ms and 64 ms. The test were performed for
both ICRA1 and ICRA7 noise types. Results indicate no beneﬁt in performance
for 5, 10 and inﬁnite dB SNR but a small increase for 0 dB when using 32
ms or 64 ms asymmetric windows. The method did not beneﬁt performance
at all when ICRA7 background noise were present. Thus, the extra spectral
information does not help performance for the EBM strategy except a little at
0 dB SNR.

Chapter 6
Discussion
To discuss the overall results of this thesis, the following section will address the
performance of the classiﬁcation framework and how well it simulate real life
perception data. Furthermore, the performance of the three channel selection
strategies are discussed. Finally, the classiﬁcation in real life environments are
addressed, and suggestions for further research are given.
6.1
The Classiﬁcation Framework and Evalua-
tion
One goal of this project was to create a classiﬁcation framework to evaluate
channel selection strategies. It was trained with diﬀerent vowels in quiet and
processing using the ACE strategy. The resulting confusion pattern were com-
pared to the response of CI users. A good classiﬁer would be one that correctly
imitated CI users general vowel confusions and not necessarily only acquire the
same identiﬁcation score. The result in Figure 4.5 indicated some of the same
confusions as for the good listeners, though CI users generally have more confu-
sions than the classiﬁer. Especially poorer listeners have a much broader range
of confusions (see Figure 4.4). The classiﬁer acts more like an excellent CI user,
but is still an acceptable approximation of CI users confusion pattern.
Evaluation results of the three channel selection strategies for stationary back-
ground noise are illustrated in Figure 5.6.
The EBM strategy showed good
==PAGE:58==
48
Discussion
performance with an identiﬁcation accuracy of 70% at 5 dB SNR and a drop to
56% at 0 dB SNR. In comparison, the ACE accuracy were 40 and 32% at 5 and
0 dB SNR respectively. It should be emphasized that these results are based
on a single accuracy score per test. The framework structure makes it diﬃcult
to take more averages and is designed to make reproducible results. The scores
shown could therefore diﬀer, though the general perception is that the results
does not vary signiﬁcantly.
The results in speech modulated noise with 6 overlapping male and female speak-
ers are shown in Figure 5.7. The ACE has a similar performance as in stationary
noise, however, the accuracy of the EBM drops signiﬁcantly to the same level as
the ACE. The interfering noisy speech contains similar properties as the target
speech, which makes the EBM select wrong channels.
The two diﬀerent noise types are extreme cases in both ends of the scale. The
ACE have similar performance in the two cases, whereas the EBM performs
similarly in cases with interfering noisy speech but improves a lot in more sta-
tionary conditions. Therefore, the EBM stimulation strategy could be promising
for CI users to increase speech intelligibility in noisy conditions.
6.2
Channel Selection Strategies and Performance
From the previously discussed results, the channels selected for each vowels have
been analyzed in quiet and with ICRA1 noise at 0 dB SNR. The results for the
three channel selection strategies are illustrated in Figure 5.8 and 5.9.
The
channels selected by the EBM strategy are quite similar to the ideal in quiet
conditions, which is also indicated by the similar performances in Figure 5.6.
Though the selected channels of the ACE strategy somewhat follows the pattern
of the ideal selection, it is more ﬂat and the selections are spread over more
channels. A broad selection of channels could be a reason that identiﬁcation
accuracy drops. As vowels have most of their energy in lower frequencies and
for some also within short frequency ranges, a broad selection of channels lead
to poor identiﬁcation performance. Vowels with energies within a small range
of frequencies are apparent for vowels /oa, oo, uw/ in the ﬁgures if comparing
with the ideal selection.
Investigations of averaged number of selected channels for all vowels were per-
formed in ICRA1 and ICRA7 noise. The results in Figure 5.10 indicated that
a lower average number of channels are selected in lower SNRs, except for the
ACE which always selects 8 channels. Furthermore, the IBM channel selection
uses fewest channels. This is due to the high local criterium where the T-F units
==PAGE:59==

needs to satisfy a local SNR of 9 dB, which for most channels are not possible
in loud noise. Moreover, the ideal strategy conﬁrms that with a speciﬁc choice
of channels, even few, a good vowel identiﬁcation accuracy can be achieved. It
should be noted that results are based on an average, and consequently sev-
eral times frames did not select any or few channels, whereas other important
time frames might have selected channels above average. The ideal and EBM
strategies both selects more channels in ICRA7 noise because the noise contains
several identical properties as speech.
Another interesting aspect of lower selection of channels by the IBM and EBM,
is that fewer channels needs to be stimulated. This could potentially lead to
lower current drainage, as the the CI processor can limit the amount of total
used clock cycles. In an environment with interfering speakers, even if the EBM
strategy performs the same as the ACE, fewer selected channels of the EBM
still increases battery time.
6.3
Realistic Conditions
Besides evaluating the three channel selection strategies using the ICRA1 and
ICRA7 noise types, a comparison with environmental noise were conducted be-
tween the ACE and EBM strategies. The results in Figure 5.11 indicates that
the EBM performs slightly better in the crowded cafeteria and the public bus.
However, the EBM performance increases in the traﬃc intersection, since no in-
terfering speech is present. Despite many cars, trucks and busses radiating loud
low frequency and other surrounding noise, there is a substantial performance
beneﬁt when using the EBM strategy.
The ICRA1 and ICRA7 are chosen as two extreme noise cases but since it is
the realistic environments the CI user will most likely encounter, these are more
interesting to study. Despite a tough listening environment like the cafeteria
would trouble most CI users, there are the public bus with better identiﬁcation
accuracy as expected. This is despite diﬀerent noise types such as small talk,
engine noise, bus doors opening etc..

6.5
Further Research
There are many possibilities for further investigation. The primary task would
be to test the ideal and EBM channel selection strategy, ideally on CI users by
performing hearing tests. Moreover, a comparison with frequently-used strate-
gies such as ACE, CIS, or what the CI user is currently using could be performed.
It can often be diﬃcult to acquire enough test subjects using CIs, therefore a
test setup playing vocoded stimuli for normal hearing subjects could also be
an option. Another issue is that CI users are often used to their own stimula-
tion strategy and would need long training sessions to gain the beneﬁts of new
alternatives.
To test the classiﬁcation framework even further, tests should be performed us-
ing consonants as stimuli. Though, the consonant database should contain more
ﬁles than found in this thesis (see section 4.6). Additional ﬁles are needed since
more consonants are present than vowels for the classiﬁer to learn. Further-
more, the speech stimuli might be extended to words or even whole sentences
for example by using the TIMIT database [33].
It could also be interesting to look into using supervised learning for the noise
as well. With prior knowledge of the noise during the training stage, the clas-
siﬁer would already have an estimation of the noisy speech features. But as a
consequence, a mismatch between the acoustic conditions during training and
testing is challenging for the supervised learning approaches [17, 34].

Chapter 7
Conclusion
Many profoundly deaf people are dependent on their CI to communicate with
other people, living their lives as any other normal hearing individuals. The ACE
stimulation strategy is frequently used by CI users and works by selecting the
most energetic channels. This is, however, problematic in noisy environments,
since it cannot distinguish between target speech and the noisy background.
Consequently, there is quite some room for improving the channel selection in
noisy conditions.
The aim of this project was to evaluate and implement two alternative stimula-
tion strategies that selects channels based on the presence of speech instead of
energy. The ﬁrst was an ideal channel selection strategy based on the IBM, the
other attempting to estimate the ideal selection using a speech presence proba-
bility algorithm. The stimulation strategies were evaluated using a classiﬁcation
framework based on a GMM classiﬁer, applying a missing data technique. It
was tested with vowel data and compared with vowel confusion patterns from
CI users.
The evaluations showed promising results for the EBM strategy. Which, were
comparable to the ideal channel selection from quiet conditions to a SNR of 5
dB with an accuracy of around 70%. At 0 dB SNR the accuracy dropped to 56%
in stationary noise. In comparison, the ACE showed identiﬁcation accuracies
of 40% and 32% for 5 and 0 dB SNR respectively. No signiﬁcant performance
improvement were achieved in speech modulated noise compared to the ACE
strategy. However, it was found that the EBM generally selected much less chan-
nels on average than the ACE, which potentially could lead to longer battery
life for CI implants.
==PAGE:62==
52
Conclusion
There are many possibilities for further investigation on this topic. Increasing
performance of the EBM strategy, in environments with speech interference, is
especially important. Finally, testing on other ﬁles than vowels is recommended
to thoroughly evaluate the EBM strategy in other types of speech.
