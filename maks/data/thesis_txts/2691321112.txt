II 
 
Abstract 
The purpose of this project was to study the indoor environmental conditions in 
Greenlandic offices and collect data that enables comparison between Denmark and 
Greenland for benchmarking assessment purposes. Thus, enabling implementation of 
tools for diagnosing indoor climate issues and assessment of the gains by upgrading the 
indoor climate. 
22 Greenlandic (5 in Sisimiut and 17 in Nuuk) and 5 Danish office buildings were used 
to investigate the general indoor climate in each country during March and April 2021. 
The indoor environment was assessed based on CO2-, relative humidity-, air temperature- 
and illuminance measurements along with an in-depth questionnaire provided to all 
employees in each investigated building. Measurements were conducted using HOBO-
loggers and Vaisala CO2-loggers, and then compared to the Danish Work Environmental 
authority’s (DWEA) guidelines. 
Out of an estimated 1005 employees in the 22 Greenlandic buildings 284 responded to 
the questionnaire. 144 respondents were registered in the Danish survey out of an 
estimated population of 512 employees. 
No significant differences, between the Greenlandic and Danish measurements were 
notable in either of the measured indoor climate parameters, except illuminance. 
However, significant differences were determined between demographics and building 
ventilation strategy. Buildings with mechanical ventilation had significantly reduced 
numbers of reported/experienced headaches and lethargy. The Greenlandic response 
group was also reporting significantly more building-related symptoms (in 2/10 
parameters) than their Danish counterpart. 
Measurements and questionnaire responses indicated a slight discrepancy between the 
Greenlandic and Danish demographics. However, the discrepancy may have been slightly 
skewed by the state of buildings which upon inspection suggested existing indoor climate 
problems. Differences may also have been subject to inequality between building types. 
Only one building, having 12 employees, in Denmark relied on natural ventilation, while 
the distribution was roughly even in Greenland. 
The DWEA guidelines were seemingly valid in case of CO2 and illuminance, however, 
temperature was often much higher than the recommended temperature interval in both 
Greenland and Denmark. Very low relative humidity levels of 8-15% were measured in 
Sisimiut where ambient temperature was around -20° for most of the measurement period.  
It was deemed sufficient to evaluate Danish and Greenlandic office buildings based on 
the same parametric benchmarking tool. However, the benchmarking tool should use 
updated temperature and RH guideline values.

1.Introduction 
On the subject of indoor environmental quality (IEQ), numerous studies have determined 
that the performance of buildings affects the productivity and health of the occupants. 
Poor IEQ is associated with negative building related symptoms, while a high IEQ is 
identified in increased employee productivity and health benefits [1] [2].  
Traditionally, office buildings have been operated with little regard to the optimal well-
being of the occupants, and the primary focus from owner and policy makers have been 
fixated on the energy savings and cost minimization [3]. 
A significant economic gain and health benefit could be obtained by improving the IEQ 
as the cost of the investment will be considerably lower than the potential gains of 
improved employee performance and well-being [4] [5]. However, due to the intricate 
and complicated nature of a well-balanced and optimal IEQ, it is often difficult to 
determine or visualize the benefits directly [6]. 
One of the major obstacles in IEQ performance evaluation is to find accurate, 
inexpensive, and easy-to-use measurement equipment and develop consistent 
performance measurement protocols for commercial buildings, both measured and 
subjective standardized methods [7] [8]. 
The primary focus of this study aims to collect data and analyze the indoor environmental 
conditions in Greenlandic offices. The results are then compared to data from similar 
Danish offices and findings from earlier studies to determine whether Danish and 
Greenlandic conditions are comparable in benchmark purposes.  
The study is conducted in collaboration with a broader underlying PhD study [9], which 
is projected to characterize the indoor environment in Danish and Greenlandic 
commercial buildings in order to develop consistent and standardized benchmarking 
tools. 
In this study the parameters of temperature, relative humidity (RH), CO2 and 
illuminance were measured in 70 offices across 22 buildings in the towns of Sisimiut and 
Nuuk, Greenland, during March and April of 2021. Furthermore, extensive building 
registration was conducted on-site and questionnaire surveys regarding the perceived 
indoor climate was distributed to all employees of the participating buildings. Finally, the 
results are compared to similar measurements from Danish office buildings.

2.Background 
Greenland, or Kalaallit Nunaat, is the largest island in the world, with a landmass covering 
approximately 2,166,000 𝑘𝑚2 of which 81% is completely covered by ice [10]. The 
island is part of the kingdom of Denmark and since 2009 has been self-governed [11]. 
Approximately 56,000 people are currently residing in Greenland, spread across 17 cities 
and 53 hamlets1 [12]. The majority of the Greenlandic population is concentrated around 
the lower west coast in the largest cities. The capital of Nuuk, located on the west coast 
roughly 240 km south of the arctic circle, compounds approximately 18,000 inhabitants 
or 1/3 of the total population [10]. Sisimiut is the seconds largest city in Greenland with 
approximately 5,600 inhabitants [10]. Sisimiut is located 320 km north of Nuuk and close 
to 50 km north of the arctic circle being the northernmost Greenlandic city with an ice-
free harbor.  
All cities and hamlets are connected only by sea- and airways, hence, all inhabited areas 
resemble island economies2. This, along with the low population, implies great 
difficulties in maintaining technologically advanced systems such as building 
management systems (BMS) and variable air volume (VAV) systems in Greenland, as 
the technical expertise is scarce and scattered [13].  
2.1. Climatic conditions 
The weather is predominately coastal with both extreme wind conditions and sub-arctic 
to arctic low temperatures as the hamlets and cities are generally located along the ice-
free coasts. Temperatures can reach as low as −50 ℃ and averages of −20 ℃ in some 
cities during the winter months are common [14].  
The ambient air’s ability to contain moisture at low temperatures is limited. High relative 
humidity (RH) of +70% during winter months, under arctic temperature conditions is not 
equal to a high moisture content. In Sisimiut, Greenland, the average absolute ambient air 
moisture content ranges between 0.9-5.2 
g

[15].        
The high latitudinal placement of most of Greenland implicates that the sun is positioned 
lower on the sky compared to Denmark. This along with the albedo effect of snow results 
in higher solar irradiance on south-facing facades during more hours of the day [16].

2.2. Building stock and building regulations 
Traditionally, buildings and housing were erected by the owners to the best of their 
ability. To change this, in the 1950s, the Danish government decided to develop several 
standardized house models of varying sizes and layouts.  
The “type”-houses could then be prefabricated in Denmark and constructed by 
professional craftsmen, achieving some quality assurance. The prefabrication was 
conducted in Denmark as Greenland largely is without any natural building materials and 
historically, no industry has been producing building materials until recent years, (rocks, 
concrete, and concrete prefabrication) [15] [13].   
The smaller single-family dwellings were usually constructed as wood frames with sloped 
wooden roofs and wooden cladding. The larger apartments and office complexes were 
usually constructed with a concrete load bearing structure with a modularized wooden 
envelope [15] [17]. 
Greenland has applied a simplified version of the Danish building code since 1982. The 
current building code corresponds to the Danish code from 2002 (BR02) with only a few 
alterations and was implemented in 2006 [18]. A new and updated building code, with 
improved demands for ventilation and energy is expected to be implemented by 
December 2021 [19].

In terms of indoor environment, the current building regulation does not specify any

values for RH, lighting nor temperature. However, the building regulations in Greenland 
refers to Danish Standard and specifies a minimum ventilation rate of 0.5 h-1. 
The Greenlandic Working Environment Authority has not established equally descriptive, 
detailed, and comprehensive guidelines for indoor environment for workers in offices 
compared to the Danish Working Environment Authority. Thus, the Danish guideline 
values are applied as comparison (Guideline values are shown in later sections) 
2.4. Corporate culture 
The typical Danish working week have since 1990 been 37 hours (including lunch), while 
still being adjustable in each working sector [20]. In Greenland however, the typical 
working week is 40 hours (including lunch) [21] [22]. Although this only represents a 
small difference, a Greenlandic worker has an obligation to stay at the office for three 
more hours per week than a typical Danish office worker. 
There are typically small differences between working hours during the week in private 
companies and municipal/governmental jobs in both Greenland and Denmark. However, 
this difference is usually just omission of paid lunchbreaks, although this still results in a 
typical working week being three hours longer in Greenland. 
2.5. Building register 
Greenland does not have a building register like the Danish BBR, although a commission 
is working on developing and gathering the needed data to establish a Greenlandic version 
[23]. However, a more archaic B-numeration is used. The B-number is given to each 
individual building upon erection, meaning that the oldest buildings will have the lowest 
B-number. This number is given a prefix to define the city in which it lies, thus a B-
number is individual in each city. 
2.6. Pollution sources 
Greenland has experienced a great increase to sales and registrations of motorized 
vehicles since 2007, even though no direct land connections between cities are currently 
present. In 2007 there were 770 and 2645 registered motorized vehicles in Sisimiut and 
Nuuk, respectively. In 2021 the number of registered motorized vehicles had increased to 
2100 and 5356; an increase of 173% and 102% in Sisimiut and Nuuk, respectively [24].

2.7. Greenland specific studies  
Few studies have been conducted in investigating the indoor climate in Greenlandic office 
buildings. The available studies are primarily focused on indoor climate in dwellings or 
low-energy houses. 
Previous studies have confirmed that dwellings in Greenland face problems of 
insufficient ventilation. The central issue is a growing focus on energy saving and 
improved building techniques allows for a tighter building envelope, however, the 
ventilation strategy is usually neglected [25]. 
Studies in Sisimiut found that a lack of onsite expertise, cost of implementation of 
complex ventilation units and a lack of requirements to ventilation in the Greenlandic 
building regulations typically causes commissioners to exclude mechanical ventilations 
units or replace these with a bare minimum solution [15] [13].  
Implementation of mechanical means of ventilation is entirely possible, feasible and will 
ensure a healthier indoor environment, at least in dwellings in the Arctic. [13] [26] [27] 
[28].  
Furthermore, studies of buildings in Greenland in general found that low relative 
humidity and high CO2-concentrations are both present and causes discomfort for 
occupants [29] [30] [31]. 
One study of an office building within Greenland, where occupants developed skin and/or 
airway symptoms after moving into a newly renovated building indicated that the 
symptoms were related to the recent renovation. The actual trigger was not determined, 
further illustrating that determining the source of a symptom is a complicated and difficult 
task [32].     
A survey of occupant behavior, energy use and the indoor air quality in Greenlandic 
dwellings indicates that renting occupants living in apartments will potentially complain 
more than people living in houses. However, the overall indoor climate in dwellings is 
perceived by the occupants to be generally good [33].

3.Indoor climate 
Humans in the western world spend most of their time indoors [34] [35], making it 
essential for buildings to maintain a healthy, pleasant, and effective working environment.  
The perception of the indoor environment is dependent on a complex interaction between 
many different factors. The indoor environment can be described by four main parameters 
which each influence the overall perception of the indoor environment quality (IEQ) [36].   
• Atmospheric 
• Thermal  
• Visual 
• Acoustic 
A good indoor climate must ensure the well-being and health of the users of the building. 
If the indoor climate in the building is insufficient in relation to the conducted work, the 
resultant performance and well-being of the employees will be negatively affected [2] 
[37]. The following sections shortly describe the indoor climate parameters, measured in 
this study – and how they affect the human occupants.  
3.1. Atmospheric - Air quality 
The CO2 concentration is often used as an indicator of the air quality in areas where 
occupants constitute the primary source of pollutant, such as offices.  
The presence of CO2 is not dangerous unless it is present in extremely high 
concentrations. This parameter is useful as it is comparably easy to measure and gives an 
indication of human presence. High CO2 concentrations are often associated with 
symptoms of headaches and fatigue. It is also an indication of an inadequate ventilation 
strategy. It is indicated that an increased air supply can increase the average performance 
of office workers by 1-10% [38] [39] [40].   
 
 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
7 
 
3.2. Thermal indoor climate 
The indoor air temperature should be adapted to the dominant activity and clothing of 
occupants in the zone. The temperature is governed by multiple factors. Both indoor and 
outdoor climate influences the temperature. The presence of electronic equipment, 
humans, lighting, and sunlight through windows may all affect the indoor temperature 
and the energy consumption.  
The temperature in particular influences the comfort and performance ability. Even minor 
variations from optimal temperature levels can lead to discomfort and cause an increased 
predisposition towards fatigue, headache, discomfort, and lessened concentration ability 
[41]. Raised temperatures in intervals 22°C to 30°C have been shown to have negative 
effects on office work performance with the greatest negative effect at 30°C [42]. 
Furthermore, people can adapt to the environment. Individuals used to work- and living 
in warm climates can readily accept and maintain a higher performance under warmer 
conditions than those used to colder climates [43] [44].   
The relative humidity causes discomfort when the relative humidity is either too low or 
too high. A low relative humidity is sensed as dry air and causes a nuisance such as 
irritated dry airways, eyes, and dry skin. A high relative humidity (more than 60% at 
20°C) can cause favorable growth conditions for bacteria and mold, impacting the 
building and the IEQ negatively [45]. 
3.3. Visual  
A proper level of lighting is important to create an ambiently pleasing working 
environment. In new buildings, the lighting conditions should be designed to minimize 
the risks to the occupant’s health, safety, or comfort nuisances. The design must ensure 
that sufficient daylight, visibility, and electrical lighting in relation to the function, is 
achieved [46].          
A significant part of the year in Greenland receives reduced daylight from the sun. The 
importance of the proper framework to ensure sufficient natural daylight and artificial 
lighting is not neglectable. Inadequate lighting can cause fatigue, irritated eyes, 
headaches, and muscle tension [47]. 
3.4. Acoustics 
While no acoustics were measured during this study, the effects of poor acoustics have 
been gathered through the questionnaire as acoustics remain a very influential part of 
IEQ.  
Acoustics in open-plan offices at 55 dB have been shown to increase fatigue and decrease 
concentration levels while also negatively affecting arithmetic and proof-reading [42].

3.5. Working Environment Agency guidelines 
The Greenlandic Working Environment Agency does not specify any guidelines for IEQ 
in offices. Instead, the Danish guidelines are used. 
The Working Environment Agency in Denmark (DWEA) have made guides to quantify 
the main parameters affecting the IEQ in office buildings. These guides are published 
with different criteria and values for the IEQ parameters in AT-Guidelines [48] [49] [50]. 
In this study, as no acoustics were measured, only three guidelines were used. 
Temperature 
• The temperature in offices should never get below 18°C. 
• The temperature should under normal conditions be kept between 20-22°C. 
• The temperature may never exceed 25° (exempt at heatwave). 
CO2-concentration 
• CO2-concentrationen should be kept below 0.1% (1000 ppm). 
• CO2-concentration above 0.2% (2000 ppm) for longer periods of time (20-30 
minutes) indicates inadequate ventilation. 
Relative Humidity 
• Relative humidity should be kept between 25 and 60%. 
Artificial lighting 
• Artificial lighting at office desks should be minimum 500 lux. 
Another comparative tool to determine the IEQ would be DS/EN 16798 [51]. It used 
categorization of the different IEQ parameters, making it a solid choice when determining 
how good the overall IEQ is. However, this standard is commonly used for the design of 
buildings and was not applicable back when the measured buildings were erected. 
However, this standard along with the Danish building regulation (2018) [46] helps to 
interpret the 0.1% and 0.2% as 1000 ppm and 2000 ppm, respectively. 
The relative humidity guideline values are not adamantly enforced. They are however just 
guideline values in between which people would not normally feel discomfort. This 25%-
60% RH would also be difficult to maintain in cold climates.  
The artificial lighting minimum is taken from the AT-guideline referring to DS/EN 
12464-1. This standard has a specific minimum of 500 lux at office work / constant 
reading. However, the 500-lux minimum is assuming that the worker has normal vision. 
Older people and visually impaired people may need higher levels of illuminance.

3.6. Occupant perception of indoor climate 
Building-Related Symptoms 
Building-Related Symptoms (BRS) (sometimes called Sick Building Syndrome) are 
conditions in which people suffer from symptoms or illnesses, which may be caused by 
the buildings which people occupy.  
BRS constitute many different possible symptoms such as: dryness, headaches, soreness, 
lethargy, coughing, sneezing, concentration difficulties and wheeziness. All these 
symptoms may affect occupants’ workability and their perception of the building and the 
indoor climate [52]. 
BRS may adversely affect women to a larger degree than men. A 1990 Swedish study of 
over 5000 office workers in northern Sweden found that women tended to report more of 
the building-related symptoms than men [52]. 
A German study of more than 8000 persons found that Building-Related symptoms were 
reported more often by occupants in air-conditioned buildings than in non-air-conditioned 
[52]. A Danish study investigating four naturally- and five mechanically ventilated 
buildings also found a lower, although often statistically insignificant, prevalence of 
reported symptoms among the occupants in naturally ventilated buildings [53].

4.Method 
As mentioned, this study is part of a larger Ph.D. project which aims to investigate the 
general indoor climate in different buildings in both Greenland and Denmark. Preliminary 
studies and research were conducted to initiate the Ph.D. project and to define the 
methodology needed to keep results unbiased and methods consequent. The Ph.D. study 
aims to produce a benchmarking tool for building owners/managers with which they 
would be able to compare possible measurements with other buildings of that type. 
The previous studies investigated building typologies4, best measurement equipment and 
developed examples of feedback reports based on building measurements and their 
respective placement compared to the benchmarking tool. The method in this study is 
applied with the knowledge from the previous studies.  
4.1. Building selection 
The previous studies investigated and sampled different buildings from the Danish 
Building and Housing Register (BBR), using Stratified Random Sampling to randomize 
the sample population of buildings.  
However, with a population of only 56,000 people the number of eligible office buildings5 
in Greenland were too few to properly randomize. Additionally, the building typology is 
difficult to determine in Greenland, as they do not have a building register like BBR. 
Instead, the building selection in this project was performed through contacting known 
companies, municipal- and Greenlandic government officials. Information sent to each 
potential participant regarding the study is shown in Appendix B. 
Geographical placement for measurements 
As mentioned, all settlements resemble island economies. Due to this fact it was necessary 
to focus on only two areas for the investigation; one in the northern region and one in the 
southern region in order to get data from buildings both above the Arctic circle and below, 
while also adhering to getting data from buildings comparable to Danish buildings 
(minimum 10 employees in each building). In the town of Sisimiut five buildings were 
chosen for measurements, shown in Figure 1.

4.3. Building-specific questionnaire 
A questionnaire was developed to compare measured data with the occupancy well-being 
and satisfaction. The questionnaire was based on an earlier study which concluded that a 
questionnaire is a reliable way of gathering information about perceived building related 
symptoms and provides solid and reproduceable results [54]. The questionnaire, adjusted 
for Greenlandic circumstances, would ask questions regarding factors of indoor climate 
and perceived IEQ. 
The questionnaire was available as an online-survey8 accessed by a building-specific link 
sent to each manager for further distribution to all employees. Furthermore, information 
regarding access to the study and the general data protection regulation (GDPR) was sent 
to the building managers (Appendix D). 
Greenlandic is the official language in Greenland [55]. Danish and English are only 
secondary languages in Greenland. The questionnaire, originally in both Danish and 
English, was translated to Greenlandic to incentivize participation in the questionnaire 
for all employees.

4.4. Measurements 
Three to four offices were chosen as the most representative office types for each 
building. The most prevalent office types were chosen by the investigators based on 
occupancy (during the entire measurement period), similarity of other offices and general 
direction of windows. No equipment was placed with intent to find the zones with the 
worst conditions or in zones with previously established issues.  
The equipment was placed on the selected workstations, usually the desk, in the vicinity 
of the occupant breathing zone, whilst still adhering to general best practice placements 
whenever possible: 
• Minimum one meter from corners, doors, and windows 
• Minimum one meter from the occupant 
• Minimum two meters away from local contaminant sources 
• Avoiding direct sunlight and possibility of this. 
• Not immediately adjacent to air supply units, ventilators, fan heaters or similar 
equipment.  
The placement was however often determined based on the electrical power- and space 
availability for the relevant office.    
Equipment 
Although different equipment was available for this study, the Hobo-dataloggers and 
Vaisala CO2-loggers were deemed best suited for the task. The reason behind this 
equipment selection was due to its independence of internet connection, which at times 
can be very unstable in Greenland. Furthermore, previous studies have shown the HOBO-
logger with attached Vaisala CO2-loggers performs with the lowest numerical 
measurement deviation out of several tested dataloggers and sensors [56]. Measurement

Calibration of Vaisala CO2-sensors

Feedback to participants 
A short feedback report was written and sent to each building manager following the 
investigation. The report contained analyses of measurements and questionnaire data, a 
short description of the measured indoor climate, how it compared to the DWEA 
guidelines and how the indoor climate could affect the employees. A feedback report 
example is shown in Appendix F. 
The data for the feedback reports were handled in MATLAB. This was done due to the 
large number of similar reports and to achieve general uniformity in the reports. The 
primary function of the script was to exclude data outside of defined working hours of 
weekdays between 08:00 – 17:00 and display the relevant data in refined figures. The 
script is shown in Appendix G.

Heating and ventilation  
The heating strategy varied from central heating coil, district heating and electric heating. 
All investigated building offices were heated through radiators under the windowsill. The 
investigated buildings had an even distribution between relying on mechanical- and 
natural ventilation. The mechanically ventilated buildings varied between having 
balanced constant air volume (CAV) and exhaust from offices. 
Office employment and presence   
The smallest office building had six employees currently working, while the largest office 
building hosted approximately 200 employees. The investigated offices had a median of 
26 employees per office and a daily presence of 79% during regular activity. None of the 
Greenlandic offices had any COVID-1913 restrictions during the measuring period.  
In the tested office buildings, 95% answered that the employees primarily work during 
office hours between 08:00 – 16:00, Monday to Friday, with an estimated average of six 
hours of building usage during the weekends. 
Ownership and primary function of the office 
Of the surveyed buildings 73% were publicly owned, by either the municipality or the 
government. The other 27% were privately owned-/leased.  
60% of the surveyed buildings had an administrative function another 32% held a 
consulting function while the last 8% were in the field of IT and tourism.  
Indoor pollution sources 
Out of the investigated buildings 88% had dedicated exhaust to the printshops.  
36% had a kitchen in the building and all of them had exhaust hoods/dedicated exhaust 
of the kitchen zone. All buildings had at least one kitchenette. 
Of the surveyed buildings 76% had a dedicated cleaning storage, 75% of these had 
exhaust ventilation.  
All buildings had ventilated restrooms. The exhaust system from restrooms in some 
buildings were not obviously active. None of the buildings had a parking garage.    
Outdoor pollution and historical damage 
The surrounding area of each building was categorized as urban, with 45% being situated 
in a commercial area, 41% in a residential area and the last 14% in an industrial area.

Lighting 
The measured illuminance in each building was much lower than the recommended 500 
lux from the DWEA guidelines in several of the buildings. However, the measured 
lighting should not be directly interpreted as a lack of possible artificial lighting as they 
were done over a week without possibility to check occupation status of offices. Spot 
measurements with lights turned on, should have been done to properly compare to 
DWEA. Instead, the measured illuminance can indicate the workers need and wish for 
more lighting.

6. Discussion 
The purpose of this study was to establish a fundamental basis of measurements for the 
indoor environmental quality in Greenlandic offices. With the amount of data and 
numbers of screened offices in this study, this has been substantiated. Naturally, inclusion 
of more buildings, parameters and a longer monitoring period would more accurately 
depict the long-term variations.  
Despite the obvious climatic differences, potential cultural differences, and disparity of 
building methods, the differences between Danish and Greenlandic occupants’ perceived 
indoor environmental quality seems surprisingly modest. Benchmark tools should be 
applicable in both countries without major consequential adaptation. 
The applied method in this study is a simple yet effective approach to survey the perceived 
indoor environment and sensed symptoms of the occupants, while monitoring the indoor 
environment in several zones simultaneously. Although the method is easy to replicate, 
it is appended with several vulnerabilities. A general issue regarding the results from both 
measured data and the qualitative answers is the selection of buildings in Greenland. 9 of 
the 22 buildings came from two managers, how they selected the buildings is unknown, 
but it is unlikely that they selected the buildings completely randomly. Hence, a slight 
predisposition towards buildings having poor indoor climate conditions should be 
expected in the results. Furthermore, the selection of representative offices is highly 
subjective. The offices were chosen with intent to optimally represent the reality; 
however, inherent subconscious preferences are not possible to exclude. 
284 responses were registered in the questionnaire out of the total estimated 1005 people 
working in the buildings (in Greenland). The low participation rate was in some cases due 
to broken lines of communication - and presumably, not employee unwillingness - 
resulting in employees not receiving and participating in the questionnaire. Compared to 
earlier studies [53] [54], the participation rate in this study was significantly lower. 15 of 
the 22 participating buildings, in this study, had questionnaire participation rates below 
50% (average for all buildings 34%), this could generally negatively misconstrue the 
results, compared to reality. Another weakness of the survey is the possibility of multiple 
answers stemming from the same individual. The survey relies on personal integrity, 
ulterior motives, i.e., preexisting poor indoor environment or otherwise discontent, could 
alter the results, especially in surveys with low participation rates. 
In relation to the physical measurements, the naturally ventilated buildings had lower RH 
and higher CO2-concentrations, the expected outcome of this would be a higher 
proclaimed intensity of perceived symptoms. This was however only significantly 
reported for headaches and Stuffy or runny nose. Statistical analysis showed prevalence 
and direct influence between building-related symptoms and indoor climate related 
discomfort among people. The response group in mechanically ventilated buildings had 
a much lower report rate on building related symptoms and indoor climate related 
==PAGE:62==
 
 
53 
 
discomfort compared to naturally ventilated buildings’ response group which is contrary 
to previous findings suggesting the opposite [52] [53].  
In the offices, the RH was predominately lower than what earlier studies have found in 
the dwellings of the arctic region [25] [15] [30], which measured high RH of +90% during 
short periods in some zones. In this study, the opposite is generally measured, suggesting 
that condensation on indoor surfaces is not common in Greenlandic offices. However, 
this does not exclude the possibility of mold within the construction, many of the 
inspected buildings had water damage and no measurements interiorly in the wall 
construction was included in this study. 
The Greenlandic Work Environmental Authority lacked IC-guidelines for indoor work, 
resulting in the usage of the Danish counterpart to compare measurements. The use of a 
Danish standard proved deficient on some IC parameters. CO2- and illuminance levels 
never showed any worrisome results compared to DWEA guidelines, while temperature 
and relative humidity commonly exceeded the guideline values. The temperature range 
of 20-22°C was surpassed in all investigated buildings for more than 80% of the working 
hours while the upper limit of 26°C was exceeded more than 10% of the working hours. 
The relative humidity was lower than the 30% minimum value more than 50% of the time 
in Greenland because of low outdoor temperature and ultimately low moisture content of 
the air – a state which applies to most of the year in the arctic. A good comparison of IC 
based on the DWEA becomes hard, when relative humidity levels do not consider the 
ambient nor indoor conditions in the guideline values. Temperature sensation is also more 
than just dry bulb air temperature. Radiation, draught, clothing, and radiant temperature 
asymmetry may all affect the thermal sensation of the occupants. However, these factors 
are not contained in the DWEA guidelines and may explain the higher temperature levels 
found in this study. 
The initial assumption of 40 hours workweek between 08:00-17:00 proved to be 
erroneous as most building managers reported working hours to be 08:00-16:00. The error 
will likely have the most evident impact in the illuminance, while still present in all 
measured and presented parameters.   
Sex was influential when it came to reporting building-related symptoms. In this study, 
the data displays a tendency of females reporting significantly more issues with the indoor 
environmental quality. This correlates well with earlier studies [52].  
It is regrettable that both noise and volatile organic compounds measurements were 
omitted in this study. It could have brought valuable information to also include these in 
the measured parameter spectrum.

7. Conclusion  
No significant differences, between the Greenlandic and Danish measurements were 
notable in either of the measured indoor climate parameters, except illuminance. 
However, significant differences were determined between demographics and building 
ventilation strategy. Buildings with mechanical ventilation had significantly reduced 
numbers of reported/experienced headaches and lethargy. The Greenlandic response 
group also reported significantly more building-related symptoms (in 2/10 parameters) 
than their Danish counterpart. 
Measurements and questionnaire responses indicated a slight discrepancy between the 
Greenlandic and Danish demographics. However, the discrepancy may have been slightly 
skewed by the state of buildings which upon inspection suggested existing indoor climate 
problems. Differences may also have been subject to inequality between building types. 
Only one building, having 12 employees, in Denmark relied on natural ventilation, while 
the distribution was roughly even in Greenland. 
The DWEA guidelines were seemingly valid in case of CO2 and illuminance, however, 
temperature was often much higher than the recommended temperature interval in both 
Greenland and Denmark. Very low relative humidity levels of 8-15% were measured in 
Sisimiut where ambient temperature was around -20° for most of the measurement period.  
It was deemed sufficient to evaluate Danish and Greenlandic office buildings based on 
the same parametric benchmarking tool. However, the benchmarking tool should use 
updated temperature and RH guideline values.

8.Future endeavors  
Further Greenlandic studies are encouraged to randomize building samples, which may 
become possible when the ongoing BBR-project in Greenland is concluded.  
Measurements and survey should be conducted during different seasons to deepen the 
knowledge and understanding of Greenlandic indoor climate perception and building 
regulatory management throughout the varying climatic seasons. 
Update the DWEA guidelines to Greenlandic conditions through usage of measured 
seasonal data to improve the boundary conditions for the final benchmark tool adaptation.

Age groups 
Age was found to have great effect on both IC perception and experience of building-
related symptoms. The questionnaire divided respondents into five different age groups; 
20’s, 30’s, 40’s, 50’s and 60+ years, however, this investigation will divide them into 3 
groups of 20-year intervals (easier to visualize): 20-39, 40-60 and >60 as seen in Figure 
37. The age groups consisted of 122, 112 and 26 people, respectively. 
 
Figure 37: Building-related symptoms between age groups: 20-39 (left), 40-60 (middle) and 
>60 years. 
Figure 37 shows elderly people (>60) people reporting most building-related symptoms 
in almost all listed categories. However, there are a tendency for the younger people of 
20-39 years old having the same problems with dry eyes, tiredness and fatigue and 
concentration difficulty. The younger age group (20-39) was also more frequently 
reporting problems with headaches than the other age groups. 
The middle age group (40-60) had a consistent 9-17 % report rate in all listed symptoms, 
indicating that this group was the least susceptible to building-related influence on 
symptoms and health perception.
