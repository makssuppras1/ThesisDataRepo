Abstract
Denmark has already demonstrated that a modern economy can lean heavily on renewable
energy, and the country remains confident of achieving full carbon neutrality within the
coming decades. Its next step is especially ambitious: in 2024 the government opened an
offshore-wind tender that will free up sea-sites for 9 GW of new capacity in the Baltic and
in the North Sea. While this expansion secures more green electricity, it also raises urgent
questions about how the national grid will cope with the sharp rise in weather-driven
generation. Variable output must be channelled through transmission corridors that were
never designed for such extremes; without careful planning, bottlenecks, curtailment and
price spikes could offset the climate benefits.
The tender is not just about power: it is also intended to underpin Denmark’s emerging
hydrogen economy. By coupling the new wind farms to a multi-gigawatt fleet of electroly-
sers, the country aims to supply Germany and other neighbours via the planned European
Hydrogen Backbone, creating a new export revenue stream and, at the same time, intro-
ducing a large controllable load that can absorb renewable peaks. Accordingly, this thesis
pursues two main objectives: to quantify the grid reinforcements necessary to accommo-
date the 9 GW addition, and to assess how effectively hydrogen production can act as
a flexibility provider, thereby reducing the need for extensive interconnector expansion.
Moreover, the aim is also to highlight what happens if key milestones like new flexibility
assets fail to materialise.
A customised implementation of the open-source model PyPSA-Eur is used to simulate
four contrasting scenarios: a grid-only baseline, a battery option, a hydrogen-enabled case
and a full-flexibility scenario that combines both flexibility types.
The simulations show that substantial reinforcement is unavoidable in all cases, raising
questions about capital needs and public acceptance. Without additional flexibility, the
network must be expanded on a scale rarely seen in a single decade, challenging both
Energinet’s construction capacity and the political willingness to approve new corridors.
Introducing a multi-gigawatt electrolyser fleet, however, cuts the required interconnector
capacity by roughly one-third, confirming hydrogen’s value as a dual-purpose technol-
ogy, an export commodity and a controllable load. Batteries do not appear under the
current cost and revenue assumptions, suggesting that their economic case will rely on
ancillary-service or capacity markets not yet modelled. Together, these findings provide
a quantitative basis for government and TSO planning as Denmark moves from climate
ambition to concrete infrastructure.

Introduction
Denmark has earned a global reputation as an early mover on renewable‑energy integra-
tion, deriving the largest share of its electricity from wind of any country worldwide—close
to 60 % in 2023 and more than 56 % in 2024 [1, 2]. Building on this legacy, the Danish
Parliament adopted the Climate Act in 2020, committing the nation to cut greenhouse‑gas
emissions by 70 % compared to 1990 levels by 2030 and to reach climate neutrality by
2050 [3]. Meeting such ambitious targets will require not only accelerated deployment
of low‑carbon generation but also a integral transformation of the energy system and,
crucially, its supporting infrastructure.
A cornerstone of Denmark’s next decarbonisation phase is the historic 9 GW offshore‑wind
tender agreed on 30 May 2023. The framework foresees 6 GW of offshore wind projects
plus 3 GW centred on the Bornholm Energy Island concept, all to be commissioned by
2030 [4]. Once operational, these wind farms will more than double Denmark’s currently
installed offshore capacity and, at times, generate electrical output comparable to peak
national demand. While the tender cements Denmark’s leadership in wind technology,
it simultaneously exposes new systemic bottlenecks: existing high‑voltage corridors were
never dimensioned for gigawatt‑scale injections from remote sea‑based hubs, nor for the
bidirectional trade envisioned with surrounding Nordic and Continental markets.
Energinet’s strategic studies already highlight emerging congestion on the 400 kV back-
bone and identify multi‑billion‑euro reinforcement needs in the 2025–2035 horizon if to-
day’s planning philosophy remains unchanged [5].
Grid expansion, however, competes
with other public‑interest objectives—cost containment, landscape protection, and social
acceptance—and therefore cannot be the only lever. Flexibility assets such as grid‑scale
batteries, demand‑side response, and green‑hydrogen electrolysers offer alternative or com-
plementary means to relieve transmission stress by shifting supply or demand in time and
space. Hydrogen in particular serves a dual purpose: it supplies decarbonisation pathways
for hard‑to‑electrify industries while acting as a long‑duration storage medium that can
absorb surplus offshore wind and re‑inject energy during lulls.
Quantifying the trade‑offs between conventional grid reinforcement and portfolio‑based
flexibility requires modelling tools that combine techno‑economic rigour, high spatial gran-
ularity, and transparent data practices. This thesis employs PyPSA (Python for Power
System Analysis) and its pan‑European dataset PyPSA‑Eur. PyPSA is an open‑source
framework capable of co‑optimising generation, storage, and network expansion under
hourly resolution, with proven applications in long‑term planning and sector coupling [6].
Using PyPSA‑Eur’s validated representation of the Nordic and Continental grids as a foun-
dation [7], this thesis develops a Danish sub‑model that explicitly includes offshore‑wind
connection points, hydrogen production clusters, battery siting options, and cross‑border
interconnectors.
In light of these, this thesis aims to adress two interlinked research questions:
• Can the Danish transmission grid reliably integrate an additional 9 GW of offshore
wind capacity without significant expansion of cable infrastructure?
• Which combination of flexibility assets—specifically hydrogen and batteries—minimizes
the required interconnector expansion and associated investment costs?

To answer these questions, four scenarios structure the analysis:
1. Baseline Transmission – optimal reinforcements under no additional flexibility.
2. Battery-driven – deployment of battery storage (0.5–2 GW / 2–8 GWh) near
major load centres to mitigate peaks and enhance local resilience.
3. Hydrogen-driven – large‑scale electrolysis (2–5 GW) co –located with offshore
wind hubs and industrial offtakers, enabling sectoral integration.
4. Full-flexibility – combined batteries and hydrogen, optimised jointly with targeted,
least –cost grid upgrades.
Each scenario is optimised for total system cost under 2030 demand, fuel price, and emis-
sions cap assumptions consistent with the national climate pathway. Key performance
metrics include the volume and location of new transmission circuits, annualised invest-
ment and operating expenditures, optimal installed capacity of electrolyzers, hydrogen
storage, battery storage and security-of-supply indicators such as p95th percentile loading
of the corridors.
Answering these questions will provide quantitative guidance for policymakers, network
operators, and investors navigating Denmark’s transition to a high‑renewables, sector–
coupled future. Moreover, the open‑source nature of PyPSA-EUR ensures that all as-
sumptions, data, and code are fully transparent and reproducible, facilitating future work.

Background and Literature Review
Denmark’s energy transition starts with the Climate Act of 26 June 2020, whose very first
article says “Denmark is about to reduce greenhouse-gas emissions in 2030 by 70 % relative
to 1990 and to become climate-neutral by 2050”. This national law, adopted by eight of the
ten parties in the Parliament, puts Denmark on a very steep path to cut carbon emissions
and guides every later decision for the power system, including new grid lines. At EU
level, the Danish targets sit inside the “Fit for 55” legislative package, which makes a 55
% carbon reduction by 2030 and establishes measures such as an expanded EU Emissions
Trading System. The update of the Trans-European Networks for Energy (TEN-E) Reg-
ulation, in force since 2022, widens Project of Common Interest (PCI) eligibility to cover
offshore hybrid grids and dedicated hydrogen pipelines, explicitly highlighting the need
for meshed North-Sea infrastructure that can handle very large Danish wind surpluses.
The shift from fossil to renewable electricity is already advanced. Statistics show that in
2022 renewables accounted for 82.1 % of Danish electricity production, with wind alone
contributing 54.2 % [8]. Data provided by the Danish Energy Agency’s open API indicate
that in 2024 wind generation rose further to 59.3 % of total output [9]. These shares are
the highest among OECD countries and demonstrate that variable sources can already
dominate the mix without compromising security of supply.
To meet the Climate Act trajectory and create exportable surpluses for Power-to-X projects,
the government turned to a massive new offshore round.
On 22 April 2024 Denmark
launched its largest ever tender, offering six sites and up to 10 GW of capacity on a
subsidy-free, pay-the-state model [10]. When surging capital costs and fixed auction rules
produced zero qualified bids in December 2024, the process was paused. In January 2025
the Energy Minister announced that all ongoing tenders would be halted and replaced with
a redesigned scheme expected to award 2–3 GW with optional state support [11]. The
latest ministry timetable (May 2025) foresees three auctions totalling ≈3 GW, opening in
autumn 2025, closing in 2026-27 and aiming at reaching commercial operation in 2032-33,
backed by a subsidy cap of DKK 55.2 bn over 20 years [12]. Although the nominal volume
has been reduced from 10 GW to 9 GW (accounting for already-contracted Thor 1 GW
and Energy-Island contingencies), the build-out still more than triples existing Danish
offshore capacity and exceeds projected domestic peak demand.
Implications for the transmission grid
The integration of large-scale renewable energy, particularly wind power, will place sig-
nificant stress on the existing transmission grid due to sharp production peaks. While
curtailment is a strategy to manage this excess, it must remain within limits that preserve
the economic viability of new wind energy projects. Substantial investment is therefore
required to upgrade or enhance the current power infrastructure, or to deploy complemen-
tary technologies that maximize the utilization of wind resources. As previously noted,
hydrogen plays a key role in Denmark’s strategic energy plans. It is viewed as a flexible
solution to absorb surplus electricity, generate additional revenue, and meet the growing
demand from Germany, its primary export market.
Large injections will land mainly at HVDC sea-cable terminals on the west coast of Jut-
land. Current 400 kV backbones (e.g. Kassø-Tjele) and the 600 MW Great Belt link are
insuﬀicient to carry continuous multi-gigawatt north-to-south flows [13], making grid re-
inforcement or alternative flexibility (batteries, electrolysers, curtailment) necessary. The
revised TEN-E framework [14] and Denmark’s climate law [15] complement each other:

2.1
Grid Expansion Planning
Grid expansion planning is central to ensuring reliable and cost-effective integration of
large-scale renewable energy. With increasing shares of variable renewable energy sources
(VRES), especially wind and solar, transmission networks face challenges related to con-
gestion, curtailment, and grid stability. Traditional planning approaches, based on deter-
ministic methods and peak-load heuristics, are giving way to more advanced optimization-
based models that incorporate uncertainty, sector coupling, and flexibility options [16].
In Europe, the Ten-Year Network Development Plan (TYNDP) by ENTSO-E provides a
cross-border perspective on grid expansion, but national-level studies remain critical to
address regional constraints. Denmark, with its high penetration of VRES, is a particularly
relevant case. Grid expansion must be not only technically but also economically feasible,
given the costs of new lines and public opposition [17].
2.2
Danish power-system topology and current bottlenecks
This section highlights some of the key constraints currently affecting the Danish power
system, providing an initial indication of which transmission corridors may require further
expansion under the 9 GW framework analyzed in this thesis.
2.2.1
Synchronous areas and the Great Belt interface
The Danish power system is split into two electrically separate synchronous zones: DK1
(Jutland + Funen) is synchronised with the Continental Europe (CEC) system, whereas
DK2 (Zealand + Bornholm) runs with the Nordic system. The only tie between them
is the Great Belt HVDC link, a 250 kV, 600 MW monopole commissioned in 2010 and
operated as a merchant interconnector [18].
Because every cross-zonal power exchange – and every remedial action market coupling
chooses – must pass this single 600 MW corridor, the link frequently saturates during high-
wind periods in DK1. Any additional north-south flow triggered by the 9 GW offshore
programme therefore exacerbates pressure on the Great Belt converters and the already,
at times, insuﬀicient transfer capacity.
2.2.2
400 kV backbone in Jutland–Funen
The spine of the DK1 network is the 400 kV Kassø – Tjele double-circuit line (174 km).
Upgraded in 2014, it now carries 2 × 1.8 GW (3.6 GW) and is explicitly described by
Energinet as “the backbone of the Jutland–Funen electricity transmission network”[19].
Energinet’s long-term strategy already foresaw (Strategy Plan 2008) that the new twin-
circuit conductor would replace the 1960s steel-tower corridor to “keep the 400 kV grid’s
vital backbone down through Jutland” while the obsolete tower row is dismantled [20].
Yet the backbone is still stressed further south: Energinet’s 2022 Needs-Analysis (En-
erginet’s long-term development plan 2022) shows that the Kassø – Revsing section will
be energy-overloaded after 2025 in every scenario, even before the Energy Island in the
North Sea is connected [21]. The overload reflects a structural imbalance: large export
surpluses accumulate on the windy west coast, whereas demand, PtX electrolysers and
cross-border sinks lie east and south.

2.3
The 9 GW offshore-wind tender
In 2023, Denmark announced its largest-ever offshore wind tender, aiming to deploy 9 GW
of new capacity by 2030. This marks a significant step forward from its current capacity
and aligns with the country’s ambition to become an exporter of renewable energy [26].
The tender includes development areas in the North Sea and Baltic Sea and is structured
to allow for overplanting and hybrid interconnections, meaning wind farms can potentially
connect to both domestic and international grids. Yet tender design challenges remain:
in December 2024 a zero-bid round revealed that a “no-subsidy + 20 % state equity”
model is diﬀicult under today’s high interest rates and supply-chain inflation, prompting
the government to revisit auction parameters [27].
This growing share of offshore wind presents significant challenges and opportunities for the
Danish power system. On one hand, it enables low-carbon electricity exports and domestic
decarbonization; on the other, it creates temporal and spatial mismatches between supply
and demand, necessitating either substantial grid reinforcement or alternative flexibility
solutions [28]. Assessing how the existing transmission infrastructure can handle this influx
is essential, especially under different flexibility deployment scenarios.
2.3.1
Capacity and Location
In 2022, Denmark’s Parliament committed to tendering a minimum of 9 GW of offshore
wind capacity by 2030, as part of the “Climate Agreement on Green Power and Heat”
[29]. This capacity is distributed across several sites:
• North Sea I (A1–A3): Minimum 3 GW
• Kattegat II: Minimum 1 GW
• Kriegers Flak II: Minimum 1 GW
• Hesselø: 0.8–1.2 GW
• Bornholm Energy Island: Minimum 3 GW
The tender design allow for “overplanting,” enabling developers to install a windfarm ca-
pacity greater than the rated transmission capacity or grid connection. Due to intermittent
wind conditions, turbines rarely operate at full capacity simultaneously. Overplanting en-
sures the wind farm can produce maximum possible energy within available transmission

2.3.2
Grid Connection Type and Cost
The connection of large-scale offshore wind to the mainland grid is one of the most critical
and costly technical challenges in the Danish offshore wind expansion plan. Denmark’s
9 GW tender framework shifts considerable responsibility for grid connection to developers,
a policy departure from traditional TSO-managed grid access seen in many EU countries.
Under the current tender conditions, developers will be responsible for covering the costs
of the inter-array system, the offshore export system—including the HVDC substation
and export cable—and the onshore point of connection (PoC). Energinet will only be
responsible for the necessary onshore grid reinforcements.
North Sea I and Bornholm Energy Island will be connected to the mainland via HVDC
architecture, mainly due to the large capacity and distance from shore. The remaining
windfarms will be connected via HVAC.
When deciding between HVAC and HVDC export systems, distance from shore is the
most important cost driver. Reactive-power compensation and ohmic losses in an AC
cable grow approximately with the square of the length, so the specific cost (million € per
MW delivered) of an HVAC link increases sharply once the route extends beyond a few
tens of kilometres. Figure 2.6, taken from a 2020 Chen et al. study [31], shows that the
levelised transmission cost curves for AC and DC cross at 45 km for the turbine and cable
ratings considered in that study. Applying this rule of thumb to Denmark’s 9 GW tender:
• North Sea I lies > 50 km offshore, so an HVDC export system is favored.
• Bornholm Energy Island will likely use HVDC, not only because of distance (7̃0

2.4.1
Batteries
Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) are increasingly recognized as key enablers of grid
flexibility. Their ability to respond quickly to fluctuations, provide frequency regulation,
and store excess generation makes them well-suited to complement VRES integration [34].
In the context of grid expansion, BESS can mitigate the need for transmission upgrades
by managing local congestion. A 2023 NY-BEST white paper shows cases in Germany
and the US Midwest where a battery costing $8 million avoided a $11 million line rebuild
while cutting congestion fees [35].
Several studies using techno-economic optimization models like PyPSA have shown that
BESS can be a cost-effective alternative to traditional grid expansion, particularly in areas
with high renewable penetration [36]. However, the cost of batteries, degradation over
time, and competition with other flexibility assets (like hydrogen or demand response)
remain key variables in planning studies.
For Denmark, where offshore wind will be
dominant, batteries could play a significant role in balancing the system locally without
excessive transmission reinforcements.
Batteries offer a variety of grid services that go beyond simple energy storage:
• Peak-shaving. Most wind-curtailment or overload events last only a few hours. By
charging during those surpluses and discharging later, a battery lets the existing
lines move the same amount of energy over a longer time period, reducing thermal
limits without the need for expanding the relevant line.
• Virtual Power Line (VPL). It consists of placing batteries at each end of a con-
gested corridor. Controlling the operation of the two batteries allows the bottleneck
to be eliminated. Operators gain additional transfer capacity at a lower cost and
permitting effort of enhancing or building a new powerline.
• Dynamic congestion management. When real-time loading on a line approaches its
limit, a strategically sited battery can absorb excess power, then release it once
the constraint clears. Because the response is sub-second, this approach prevents
overloads without curtailment in the line.
• Ancillary Services. Batteries ramp from zero to full output in under 200 ms [37],
delivering synthetic inertia, primary frequency response, and reactive power. These
qualities help stabilize a system whose traditional rotating mass is steadily being
displaced by inverter-based renewables.
These capabilities make batteries an attractive alternative: they postpone or even avoid
expensive line upgrades, can be installed in a short time, fit inside existing sub-station
limits, and face higher public-acceptance than new high voltage corridors [38].
2.4.2
Demand-side flexibility (DSF): EVs and heat pumps
Denmark’s flexibility resources are growing fast. The Danish vehicle register lists around
402 000 battery-electric cars at the end of 2024, up from 132 000 in 2022 and 30 000
in 2019 [39]. Energinet’s vehicle-grid-integration roadmap projects 760 000–1.5 million
EVs on the road by 2030, depending on policy ambition [40].
This represents a huge
opportunity for avoiding build-out of new transmission lines. Smart EV charging allow

Electric heat pumps also represent a valuable mean for DSF. Space or water-heating set-

2.4.3
Sector coupling: power-to-X
Hydrogen, produced via electrolysis using excess renewable electricity, represents a long-
duration and sector-coupling flexibility solution. It can absorb large amounts of curtailed
VRES output and serve users in transport, heating, and industry, decoupling electricity
production from consumption [44]. Unlike BESS, hydrogen can store energy seasonally
and across sectors, making it attractive for systems with high renewable curtailment.
Studies show that including hydrogen in power system models significantly reduces the
need for grid expansion and lowers total system costs when VRES penetration exceeds
certain thresholds [6]. In Denmark, hydrogen is a strategic component of national energy
plans, with a focus on Power-to-X projects. Evaluating hydrogen’s role in integrating the
9GW offshore wind capacity is essential to assess whether grid expansion can be limited
through sector coupling and demand shifting.
Denmark’s Power-to-X (PtX) strategy sets a binding objective of at least 4–6 GW of
electrolysis by 2030 in order to turn surplus wind into hydrogen-based fuels for domestic
industry and export [45]. Unlike transmission lines, an electrolyser behaves as a control-
lable load: modern PEM stacks move from 10 % to 100 % of rated power in 1-3 s [46].
When an electrolyser is located close to offshore-wind site, it can absorb generation peaks
that would otherwise push the 400 kV corridors beyond their thermal N-1 limits, acting
like a peak shaving battery. The first 40 GW commercial electrolyser is already on the
Danish grid. HySynergy in Fredericia feeds a refinery and heavy-transport hub; Phase II
lifts the site to 300 MW by 2025 with step-wise expansion toward 1 GW by 2030. Situ-
ated inside a nodal congestion zone at the confluence of three 150 kV lines, the plant can
provide flexibility services to the grid.
Another large project is in place in Denmark. HØST PtX Esbjerg has secured permits for
1 GW of electrolysis and an ammonia reactor; half of the hydrogen produced is meant to
be exported via pipeline to Germany, reducing electrical load through the Kassø–Revsing
400 kV line [47].
2.5
Methodological landscape for grid-expansion studies
In the last decade there has been an expansion of methodological approaches leaning to-
wards capacity expansion planning. These approaches can span from deterministic engi-
neering studies rooted in power-flow security criteria to economy-wide optimisation models
that co-optimise electricity, hydrogen, heat and even CO2 pipelines. Understanding these
is essential for positioning this PyPSA-EUR analysis of Denmark’s 9 GW offshore wind
tender.
2.5.1
Security-oriented deterministic planning used by TSOs
Most TSOs still begin with scenario analysis (e.g. 2030, 2040, 2050) and then test rein-
forcements with AC or DC load-flow plus N-1 security checks. The ENTSO-E Ten-Year

cons is that flexibility is an after-thought. Batteries, demand response or hydrogen are
usually bolted on after the grid is sized, so they seldom get credit for avoiding lines.
2.5.2
Least-cost optimisation models
Academic and consultancy studies increasingly utilize capacity-expansion models that co-
optimise generation, storage and transmission over hundreds of hourly time-steps. PyPSA-
Eur lies in this category.
It spans 6001 AC and DC lines and 3657 buses, providing
a meshed representation of the entire ENTSO-E grid suitable for both operational and
expansion studies. Similar open platforms include PowerSimulations.jl (Julia), TEMOA
(Python) and PLEXOS (commercial). A recent systematic review comparing 30 modeling
tools concluded that open-source frameworks now rival commercial ones in temporal and
spatial resolution while offering auditability, an increasingly important requirement for
policy processes relying on public trust [49].
Their main limitation is computational
tractability: with perfect foresight they ignore risk, and integer formulations for line build
decisions can still demand cluster-level aggregation.
2.5.3
Stochastic and robust optimisation under uncertainty
The variability of wind and load growth has driven a shift toward explicit uncertainty mod-
elling. A 2025 review of 150 papers by Shengming Cao and Waqquas Bukhsh, shows de-
terministic models dominate publications but highlights rapid uptake of robust, stochastic
and chance-constrained formulations, often solved with Benders or column-and-constraint
generation [16]. These methods are still research-heavy but are trickling into practice.
2.5.4
PyPSA-Eur for Capacity-Expansion Planning
PyPSA-EUR is a powerful open-source energy model of the European power system that
supports detailed capacity expansion planning. It integrates electricity transmission, gen-
eration, and storage technologies across hourly resolution and spatial granularity, making
it ideal for scenario-based studies like the one undertaken in this thesis [50]. Developed by
a community of researchers led by Tom Brown and colleagues, PyPSA-EUR builds on the
broader PyPSA framework and is maintained through a collaborative GitHub repository.
Its transparency, modularity, and strong documentation have made it a widely adopted
tool in academic, policy, and consulting environments across Europe.
PyPSA-EUR’s flexibility allows researchers to model cross-border flows, assess the trade-
offs between local flexibility and grid expansion, and evaluate policy-relevant questions
under cost minimization frameworks.
Its adoption in recent studies across Germany,
France, and the Nordics underscores its robustness and transparency [51]. Using PyPSA-
EUR for Denmark, this thesis contributes to the growing literature that uses open energy
modeling to make informed decisions under high-renewable scenarios.

PyPSA-EUR Model Overview
PyPSA‑EUR is an open‑source data set and modelling workflow that builds a detailed
representation of the European energy system at transmission level and then passes it to
the PyPSA power‑system analysis toolbox for optimisation and simulation. Covering the
full ENTSO‑E footprint, the model integrates demand and supply for all major energy sec-
tors, not just electricity. Since version v0.8.0 the former electricity‑only (PyPSA‑Eur) and
sector‑coupled (PyPSA‑Eur‑Sec) branches have been merged, so a single repository now
supports both classic power‑system studies and broader energy‑system decarbonisation
scenarios.
The workflow is governed by Snakemake. Each script is exposed as a rule whose inputs and
outputs are chained automatically; when any upstream data or code changes, Snakemake
rebuilds only what is necessary. Scenario parameters are propagated through the file names
and keywords to be set in a configuration file. This fully automated pipeline ensures that
the model can be reproduced from scratch from open data sources, an essential requirement
for transparent academic work such as this thesis.

main source [52].

PyPSA‑EUR can be run in capacity‑expansion (CE) mode, where the optimiser decides not
only how to dispatch existing assets but also how much new generation, storage and grid

capacity to build. All CE features are activated automatically whenever any component

The investment decisions are the following:
• Generation and storage: Each extendable asset gets a variable power P_nom and,

Methodology
In this chapter, the modelling behaviour of PyPSA-EUR will be presented. Since the
model is large and present wide variety of constraints, only the most relevant to this thesis
are included.
Moreover, the essential model settings and changes are explained. Understanding these
is crucial for comprehending how the analysis is conducted and recognizing the underly-
ing assumptions that influence the final results. The PyPSA-EUR repository code has
largely remained unchanged; however, the modifications necessary for this investigation
are detailed in this section.
4.1
System Optimization
The following problems can be optimized with PyPSA:
• Economic Dispatch (ED) market model with unit commitment and storage operation
with perfect foresight or rolling horizon,
• Linear Optimal Power Flow (LOPF) with network constraints for Kirchhoff’s Voltage
Law (KVL) and Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL),
• Security-Constrained Linear Optimal Power Flow (SCLOPF) for network contin-
gency analysis,
• Capacity Expansion Planning (CEP) with single or multiple investment periods and
system-wide constraints, and
• Modelling-to-Generate-Alternatives (MGA) for near-optimal space exploration.
These build on each other, capacity expansion planning models include economic dispatch
and linear optimal power flow constraints. The objective is to minimize the total system
costs for the snapshots selected, where each snapshot is given a weighting ωt to represent,
for example, multiple hours.
The choice of formulation—linear program (LP), quadratic program (QP), or mixed-
integer linear program (MILP)—depends on the parameters in play, such as whether
components are allowed to be extendable or committable.

System Settings

4.2.1
Foresight, planning horizon
Choosing an appropriate foresight setting is the first critical step in representing the power
system accurately in PyPSA-Eur. This thesis models Denmark’s current power grid and
then incorporates the 9 GW of offshore wind awarded in the latest tender. Perfect foresight
supports a greenfield study, building every network element from scratch, whereas myopic
foresight enables a brownfield analysis that retains existing infrastructure. With myopic
foresight, all assets already online—or scheduled to be online—by a specified year are
included with their technical characteristics, commissioning dates, expected lifetimes and
de-commissioning times.
Accordingly, this thesis adopts the myopic foresight setting,
enabled in the corresponding configuration file.
Foresight choice also determines the planning horizon—the years analyzed in the study.
Here, 2025 and 2030 are selected: 2030 captures the full deployment of the 9 GW offshore-
wind tender, while 2025 provides a reference year that allows the myopic algorithm to
create a baseline network containing all capacity currently operating in Denmark.

Myopic foresight workflow
The myopic code solves the network for the time steps included in planning_horizons in
a recursive loop, so that:
• For baseyear=2025, capacities installed before 2025 are added. In addition, the net-
work comprises additional generator, storage, and link capacities with p_nom_extendable=True.
The non-solved network is saved in the folder ”resources”.
• The 2025 network is optimized. The solved network is saved in the folder ”results”
• For the next planning horizon, 2030, the capacities from the previous time step are
added if they are still in operation (if they fulfil planning horizon <= commissioned
year + lifetime). In addition, the network comprises additional generator, storage,
and link capacities with p_nom_extendable=True. The non-solved network is then
saved.
Steps 2 and 3 are solved recursively for all the planning_horizons included in the config-
uration file.

Indeed, this thesis also wants to answer the question: How much do cross-country trans-

ration file under ”countries”, the United Kingdom, Germany, Norway, Sweden, and The
Netherlands will be considered. This choice lies in the fact that these are the countries
connected with Denmark as of today by HVDC cables. Any countries without a direct
tie to Denmark (e.g. FR, BE, PL) are therefore kept outside the optimisation, preserving
computational tractability while maintaining the realism needed to judge if — and under
which flexibility assumptions — Denmark’s transmission grid can accommodate the full 9
GW of new offshore wind.
Clustering
PyPSA-EUR builds the full 220 kV+ European transmission grid (≈6 000 buses) and then
offers several ways to aggregate it so that optimisation remains tractable.
This thesis rely on the administrative clustering mode. In this mode each original bus is
mapped to the centroid of the NUTS region it lies in; generators, loads and lines inside
the region are merged and electrical attributes are preserved (impedances are combined,
capacities summed, etc...). Denmark is clustered at NUTS3 level, resulting in a 10 bus
power system, while the other countries at NUTS0, resulting in a single node system. The
final network then contains 15 total nodes.
The motivation is the focus on the 9 GW offshore tender. Keeping NUTS3 detail lets the
model capture the distinct on-shore landing points (Jutland, Funen, Zealand and Born-
holm) and the internal 400 kV corridors that may need reinforcement, without inflating
the problem size in neighbouring countries. In figure 4.1 Denmark’s clustering can be
seen.

4.2.4
Transmission limit expansion
Since this thesis also wants to asses how much new capacity is needed in the transmission
network, setting properly a transmission expansion goal is essential. In PyPSA-EUR the
decision to reinforce the grid is handled by the transmission_limit switch in the config-
uration file. Here, the chosen method is VOPT, which stands for optimal volume. This
adds the MW-km of every extendable line or link as a cost term in the objective function
to let the solver economically weight new grid against batteries, electrolyzers, curtailment
and so on.
The VOPT setting fits this study because it gives an answer to whether 9 GW of new
Danish offshore wind can be integrated more cheaply by reinforcing transmission or by
deploying local flexibility (batteries, hydrogen).

4.2.5
Sector considered for sector coupling
PyPSA-EUR can simulate a fully integrated energy system, but every additional sector
increases problem size as well as result-space complexity. For this thesis the sector matrix is
deliberately restricted to heating, biomass and industry (incl. hydrogen), while transport,
shipping, aviation and agriculture are switched off.
Why these three sectors matter for Denmark
• Heating – Almost half of Danish space-heat demand is met by district-heating net-
works, and 70 % of the heat fed into those systems already comes from biomass
CHP. Electrification of boilers and large heat pumps therefore represents the single
biggest new electrical load in winter, and, because hot-water tanks provide multi-
hour storage, a prime source of demand-side flexibility.
• Biomass - Denmark operates six large biomass-fired CHP plants. These plants are
dispatchable generators, therefore essential for maintaining grid stability in a renew-
ables dominated system towards 2030. In addition, Denmark has a relatively large
amount of biogas plants, in the European perspective, and questions of conflicting
use of biomass could arise.
• Industry - Industrial hydrogen is projected to be the dominant power-to-X sink in
Denmark, with 4 GW of electrolysers targeted for 2030 and up to 9 GW by 2035.
Electrolysers act as highly elastic demand: they ramp up in windy hours, absorbing
offshore surpluses that would otherwise flow onto the grid or be curtailed, and they
ramp down during scarcity, relieving network congestion. Giving the large capacity
projected by 2030, including them is therefore essential for an assessment of grid
expansion.
Focusing on these sectors therefore balances model tractability with the need to capture
the dominant sources of electrically coupled flexibility that determine whether Denmark
can integrate the 9 GW of offshore wind without excessive transmission build-out.
4.2.6
Time resolution used in the simulations
Simulating a full year is essential to capture the seasonal variability of variable renewable
energy (VRE) generation and hydrogen storage behavior. While a one-hour resolution
would offer greater detail in representing weather fluctuations, it proves computationally
intensive for this case, often exceeding available memory on a standard laptop. To address
this, the analysis relies on time segmentation as a practical compromise.
For the simulations, a resolution of 1095 segments is chosen. Segmentation reduces the
full 8 760 hourly snapshots to 1 095 contiguous segments, each of which groups consecutive
hours with similar net‐load or patterns. Each segment carries a weighting equal to the
number of original hours it represents, ensuring that energy balances and cost calculations
remain accurate despite the reduced set of snapshots. Chronology is preserved by ordering
segments according to their original sequence, so ramping constraints and storage dynamics
are still meaningful over the course of the year. By choosing 1 095 segments, it is possible
to achieve an average resolution of about eight hours per segment, but with variable
segment lengths tailor-made to capture key operational regimes like dawn ramps or peak
evening loads. The primary advantage is a roughly eight-fold reduction in the size of the
optimization problem, solving across 1 095 weighted segments instead of 8 760 individual
hours, which dramatically speeds up model runs and reduces memory requirements. On
the downside, some temporal granularity is inevitably lost: short, extreme events that last
less than a segment’s duration may be smoothed out and under-represented.

4.2.7
Limitations of the setup
Every simplification that makes the optimisation easier to solve also moves the model
further from physical reality.
The key limitations to remember when interpreting the
results are summarised below.
Administrative clustering of the Danish grid, 10 buses
Denmark’s 400 kV network contains many substations, yet it is represented here by ten
aggregated nodes. Before clustering, several distinct circuits and corridors may connect
two Danish substations. After aggregation they are replaced by one equivalent branch
whose rating equals the sum of the originals lacking the reality that flows can share or
bypass different paths with different thermal ratings. Similarly, the impedances are lumped
into a single averaged value, therefore the model may misrepresent how flows split between
alternative routes. This and the lack of a real but an approximate N-1 contingency may
result in an underestimation of bottlenecks and over estimate the ability of batteries to
relieve them.
Neighbour perimeter limited to directly connected countries
Only GB, DE, NO, SE and NL are modelled together with DK. Power flows therefore stop
at the first foreign node: Dutch surpluses cannot reach France, German deficits cannot
count on Poland, and Nordic flexibility cannot be exported to continental Europe but
through Denmark. This truncation can exaggerate congestion on Danish interconnectors
and trick the solver toward local flexibility. If the model included more European countries,
it would be possible to see whether their extra demand or supply changes the amount of
grid expansion in Denmark.
Single meteorological year (2013)
Wind, solar and heat-demand patterns from 2013 are the only ones considered. Extreme
lull periods (e.g. the January 2021 Dunkelflaute) or exceptionally windy autumns (2017)
are missing, so the model may underestimate storage, peaking capacity and transmission
reinforcements required for multi-year robustness. A rolling set of weather years would
give more confidence around expansion needs.
Time resolution (1095 segments)
Segmentation tends to flatten short, sharp spikes in demand or generation, so brief high-
demand hours or sudden drops in wind or solar output may not show up clearly.
It
also removes any changes that happen within the hour, things like quick battery charge-
discharge cycles or the start-up time of a power plant aren’t captured. Because those
peaks get averaged out, the model might end up investing less in fast-acting assets (like
batteries or demand response) that are most valuable during brief extremes, or might favor
slower, cheaper options that look better once everything is smoothed.
These limitations do not invalidate the results, especially the trade-off between flexible
demand and grid reinforcement, but they mean that the results should be considered as
lower-bounds estimates undergoing accurate sensitivity analysis in subsequent work.

Results and Discussion
Denmark’s 2024 offshore-wind tender will more than double today’s installed wind capac-
ity, adding 9 GW of variable renewable generation within a single decade. Whether the
existing transmission grid can absorb this step-change hinges on how quickly complemen-
tary flexibility options—short-duration batteries and power-to-hydrogen infrastructure—
are deployed.
This chapter presents the quantitative outcomes of four scenarios that
represent the plausible range of future Danish power-system designs. The results are sup-
ported by graphs, tables and geo-referenced maps of Denmark that highlight how asset
location shapes the outcomes.

6.6
Curtailment
Curtailment is fundamental metric for judging system performance under high penetration
of variable renewable energy. A certain amount of curtailment can be healthy: it allows
operators to avoid oversized grids or storage that would be used only a few hours each
year, and it provides head-room for inertia and reserve services. But when curtailed energy
reaches certain thresholds, investors see under-utilised turbines and falling revenues. From
a network perspective curtailment could be a symptom of congestion: if a wind farm
regularly lowers its power output because the nearby corridor sits at its thermal limit, the
episode represents that either more transmission or more local flexibility is needed. The
following section therefore tracks how many terawatt-hours of wind and solar are spilled
in each scenario and what that implies for grid planning.
Figure 6.10: Total curtailment of renewable energy in Denmark by carrier and scenario in
2030
The stacked bars in Figure 6.10 highlight how the introduction of electrolysers changes
renewable curtailment. With no hydrogen demand (Base and Battery-driven) total cur-
tailment reaches 187 GWh in the weather year analysed. Offshore wind landed via AC
connections accounts for the highest share at 105 GWh, followed by on-shore wind (30
GWh), DC-connected offshore wind (23 GWh) and solar PV (20 GWh). Nearly half of
that lost energy is concentrated at a single node, DK022 on southern Zealand: the bus
hosts 3 GW of Bornholm offshore capacity, 1 GW from Kriegers Flak, 570 MW of solar
and 2 GW of on-shore wind, so even the 3.5-GW export cable to Germany cannot drain
every peak.
Switching on the 3.2 GW electrolyser fleet (Hydrogen-driven and Full-flexibility) reduces
annual curtailment to 133 GWh, a drop of almost 30 %. The electrolyzers load absorb

rather than accepting frequent spills. When no electrolysers are present it opts to enlarge
the Great-Belt AC link, the German HVDC export cable and the north–south Jutland
backbone, thereby exporting most surplus instead of dumping it. The result is a network
that exports most peaks, imports when winds calm, and keeps curtailment low.
6.7
Electricity prices
Electricity prices provide reality check on any high-renewables energy system. However
well a network meets technical targets, it will not gain political agreement if the resulting
tariffs force households to pay more or to lower the competitiveness of local industry.
In the Danish context, already a leader in wind penetration, keeping prices moderate is
essential for public acceptance. The analysis that follows therefore compares the electricity
price profiles generated by each of the four scenarios, showing how different mixes of grid
investment and flexibility assets translate into the kroner-per-megawatt-hour ultimately
seen by consumers and businesses.

Figures 6.11 and 6.12 plot the hourly electricity prices for both pairs of identical scenarios

to about 64 €/MWh, June to 31 €/MWh—even though total curtailment is reduced (see

bottlenecks in the lines (as the map 6.9 suggests) creating higher price difference between
the buses. Another explanation for this is the constant load of electrolyzers. This load is
formally inflexible (outcome of the optimization) as can be seen in figure 6.3 therefore, the
electrical load curve shifts upwards pushing the marginal units into a higher cost segment
raising the average market price. Prices are given by the dual variable of the balance
equation (4.9) where the flow in the corridors is considered. Taken together, these two
mechanisms explain why Hydrogen-driven and Full-flexibility scenarios show higher aver-

In summary, bringing 9 GW of new offshore wind onto the Danish grid does push electricity
prices down overall, but the weather-driven swings of wind and solar can still create short
but very costly price spikes. Adding large electrolysers helps in two ways: they soak up
surplus power that would otherwise crash the market, and they allow Denmark to get
by with less new transmission lines.
The downside is that because these electrolysers
draw electricity almost all the time, and the grid is now a little less interconnected, the
average price ends up higher. Decision-makers therefore have to balance two aims: keeping
everyday prices low by building more grid capacity, or accepting a small rise in the average
price in exchange for smoother price swings and a smaller network, thanks to hydrogen
production.

6.9
Key takeaways
In this section, the key takeaways are reported to summarize the results obtained so far.
Energy storage:
Batteries are not installed because, at the considered price point and given the electricity
prices produced by the optimisation, they are not profitable. The absence of a remuner-
ation mechanism beyond price arbitrage limits further investigation; therefore, the only
conclusion that can be drawn from this thesis regarding batteries is that price arbitrage
alone is not effective in a system with such high renewable penetration.
Hydrogen storage is almost absent in the simulation results for reasons similar to those
Strategic Investment Planning for Grid Expansion Using PyPSA
43
==PAGE:52==
affecting battery storage. With the installed capacity obtained, it cannot operate as sea-
sonal storage in any scenario; consequently, the hydrogen must be used soon after it is
produced, aside from a small buffer.
These storage outcomes significantly influence the other results, yet they also highlight
the challenges that must be addressed to unlock the full potential of battery and hydrogen
storage.
Electrolyzers:
Because hydrogen storage is not large enough, hydrogen must be produced and delivered
to demand immediately. Consequently, the electrolysers in the simulation operate at full
load throughout the year to meet the 17 TWh target projected by Energinet.
The full-load operation has a consequence: to keep the electrolysers running during low-
wind and low-solar periods, the model imports electricity from neighbouring countries.
Thus, in every scenario the electrolysers behave almost like inflexible loads rather than
flexible ones. Because electrolysers are costly and hydrogen demand is high, the model
prefers importing electricity to expanding electrolyser capacity. A larger installed capacity
would still meet the 17 TWh target, yet it could also allow the units to shut down when
necessary, giving them true flexibility.
New lines and Links:
In all scenarios the added AC and DC capacity is considerable, highlighting the substan-
tial investments required if the 9 GW tender is implemented at its full potential. The
results from the Base and Battery-driven scenarios (which are practically identical) high-
light that flexibility options such as hydrogen and battery storage must be high priorities
for the Danish Energy Ministry and Energinet; without these flexible assets, the pathway
would be diﬀicult to pursue.
Moreover, in all scenarios, interconnections with neighboring countries are essential in
order to keep balance in a system with almost 20 GW of VREs being denmark a net
exporter under these circumstances.
A final takeaway on transmission is that bottlenecks must be addressed by Energinet un-
der these demanding conditions. Although the Base and Battery-driven scenarios show
slightly lower line utilisation than the Hydrogen-driven and Full-flexibility cases, all simu-
lations depict a grid that would be challenging to manage in practice. Persistent congestion
can elevate electricity prices, and lines or links that frequently reach their thermal limits
are likely to degrade more quickly.
Curtailment:
The model is not inclined to curtail renewable energy, as shown by all the results. This
tendency—combined with the large installed capacity of interconnectors—drives many of
the outcomes. Allowing modest levels of curtailment could have reduced the need for some
of the new corridors.
6.10
Future work
There are several aspects of this analysis that could be refined to improve the accuracy and
realism of the results. The following points outline potential areas for future work, high-
lighting adjustments and enhancements that could strengthen the model’s performance
and insights.
The first improvement would be to increase the number of clusters within Denmark,
44
Strategic Investment Planning for Grid Expansion Using PyPSA
==PAGE:53==
allowing for a more detailed representation of the interconnectors, particularly the internal
AC lines. A higher spatial resolution reduces the need for simplifications when modeling
the network, leading to more realistic results. This is especially important when analyz-
ing grid bottlenecks and assessing new capacity installations. In the current setup, new
capacity is evaluated as an aggregated value between zones; however, the more accurately
the transmission paths are represented, the more precise the resulting capacity expansion
decisions will be.
A second key area for improvement is the representation of interconnections with
neighboring countries. Expanding the model to include all of Europe would reduce
the over-reliance on cross-border HVDC links through Denmark. In the current setup,
Denmark functions as a transit corridor, channeling the surplus variable renewable energy
(VRE) from the neighboring countries considered. This central role can distort results,
as it places Denmark at the core of regional power flows. A more comprehensive and
interconnected European model would distribute these surpluses more realistically across
the continent. Therefore, future work could focus on adapting the code to support more
accurate single-country analyses—ones that preserve realistic electrical interconnections.
A third improvement would be to conduct a sensitivity analysis on weather years.
Currently, the model relies on a single weather year (2013), but incorporating multiple
years would provide a more robust assessment of system performance. Given that Den-
mark’s 2030 power grid is expected to be heavily dominated by variable renewable energy
(VRE), interconnection needs and capacity expansions are likely to vary significantly with
different weather patterns. Including a broader range of weather years would enhance the
reliability of the results.
A fourth avenue for future work would be to enable batteries to access additional
revenue streams, particularly from balancing markets. Relying solely on price arbitrage
may underestimate the true potential and value of battery storage. Capturing their role
in providing system flexibility and ancillary services would offer a more comprehensive
assessment. However, implementing this would require significant modifications to the
underlying model code.

Conclusion
The integration of large-scale offshore wind into a national power system already rich in
renewables is not just a technical challenge, it is a defining test of strategic planning.
Denmark’s ambition to deploy 9 GW of offshore wind by 2030 pushes the boundaries of
what current transmission infrastructure can accommodate. This thesis was motivated by
the need to assess whether grid reinforcements alone can deliver that vision, or whether
alternative pathways involving flexibility assets offer a more eﬀicient route. By focusing on
a cost-optimised comparison of grid expansion versus flexibility expansion, the study con-
tributes actionable insights to policymakers and transmission planners tasked with turning
climate ambition into real-world systems. The findings also underscore the importance of
open-source tools like PyPSA-Eur in navigating this complex transition.
Outcomes:
Denmark’s plan to commission 9 GW of new offshore wind by 2030 is technically feasible,
but the pathway chosen will determine both the scale of grid investment and the wider
economic footprint of the transition. The scenario analysis carried out with a tailored
version of PyPSA-Eur shows two clear routes forward.
First, if no additional flexibility is deployed, the power system can still keep the lights on,
yet only by building almost 13 GW of new high-voltage corridors. Curtailment remains
low, but the investment bill reaches 290 m€/year and 95-th-percentile loadings on several
export cables lies at the thermal limit. In other words, a “grid-only” strategy keeps prices
modest but locks the Danish TSO into a decade of heavy construction and low public
agreement.
Second, coupling the tender to a 3.2 GW electrolyser fleet changes the picture. Electrol-
ysers absorb roughly one-third of the wind peaks. Total transmission CAPEX falls by 73
m€/year and the bottleneck map cools, though at the cost of a small rise in the electric-
ity price floor. Peak imports from Norway increase by about 1 GW, but the net export
balance remains firmly positive, making Denmark a net exporter for Northern Europe.
Taken together, the results point to a “Wind-to-Hydrogen plus targeted grid” pathway as
the most balanced compromise: it minimises expensive HVDC build-out, maintains low
curtailment, and leaves room for later batteries or demand-side schemes to shave residual
peaks.
PyPSA-EUR:
PyPSA-Eur proved to be a powerful tool for exploring these options.
The study also
demonstrates the strengths and current limits of PyPSA-Eur as a planning tool.
On
the positive side, its open-data pipeline, hourly resolution, spatial resolution and co-
optimisation of generation, storage and network assets make it transparent for policy
work. Yet this thesis highlighted two gaps that should be addressed before PyPSA-Eur is
used for investment decisions. First of all the limited storage remuneration logic. Batteries
never built because only day-ahead arbitrage was valued. Adding ancillary-service markets
would give a fairer view of their future role and might shift the electrolyser/battery trade-
off. Second, the simplification of cross-border interactions in single-country analyses: to
keep the model computationally manageable on a standard machine, the interconnections
between neighboring countries and their own neighbors are often reduced. This simplifi-
cation significantly distorts the results by effectively isolating the country under analysis
46
Strategic Investment Planning for Grid Expansion Using PyPSA
==PAGE:55==
from the broader European system. Future work could be tailoring the model to perform
this kind of situations unleashing its potential use for single country policymakers and
and governments.
Looking ahead:
If policymakers accept the thesis findings, the preferred implementation sequence is clear:
• Lock in at least 3 GW of electrolyser capacity,
• Invest in battery projects coupled with ancillary-service markets,
• Prioritise the Skagerrak and Great-Belt reinforcements.
With this roadmap Denmark can deliver its 9 GW tender on time, contain public-funded
expenses, and preserve its leadership in energy planning, while PyPSA-Eur evolves to
remain the analytical backbone of that journey.
