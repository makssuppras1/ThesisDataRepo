Abstract
Hearing loss is one of the most common disabilities globally, and hearing loss in chil-
dren leads to long-term developmental delays. However, existing techniques for passive
assessment of hearing are poorly compatible with corrective devices. Functional near-
infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) is a novel technique that employs spectroscopy to assess
functional activation of neural tissue.
This study investigates the impact of analysis parameters on the detectability of fNIRS
responses to auditory stimulation in the primary auditory cortex. A preprocessing pipeline
was developed, and the effects of conservative versus aggressive band-pass filtering
was compared, as were averaging-based and generalized linear model (GLM) analysis
approaches. Aggressive filtering reduced drift and unwanted oscillations and improved
GLM analysis. However, this analysis yielded mixed results for averaging-based anal-
ysis. Contrary to previous studies, we did not observe consistently stronger responses
to speech compared to noise stimuli. Further, while previous studies indicated that a
GLM-based approach was superior, a waveform averaging-based approach had superior
performance in this study.
These findings highlight the importance of empirical validation of analysis methods and the
need for standardization in fNIRS research. While fNIRS shows promise for non-invasive
and passive assessment of auditory processing, further refinement of methodologies is
necessary before reliable clinical application. This study contributes to the ongoing devel-
opment of fNIRS techniques and underscores the need for continued research to optimize
data processing and analysis approaches in auditory fNIRS applications.

Introduction
Hearing loss is a major cause of disability. In 2021, it was estimate to be the fourth-leading
cause of years lived with disability, with a total of 44.4 million years lived with disability
[1]. Hearing loss in children is especially problematic, as it has been shown to lead to
delays in acquisition of language, but also reduced acquisition of mathematical and social
skills, even in children with only partial hearing loss [2]. Hearing loss in children has been
estimated to be present in up to 1 in 7 children (13.1%), while only 1 in 80 children (1.2%)
self-report hearing loss [3]. High-quality interventions such as hearing aids and cochlear
implants are freely available to children with hearing loss. Still, the wide gap between
prevalence and diagnosis limits the effective use of these interventions.
Because the importance of early detection of hearing loss, screenings of newborn children
using tests based on otoacoustic emissions and EEG-based auditory brainstem tests are
now routinely performed in developed countries. These objective measures tests do not
require the patient to follow directions or perform tasks, making them suitable for use in
very young children. However, otoacoustic emissions have high rates of false positives
and cannot detect all forms of hearing loss, while EEG-based methods are perturbed by
the presence of electrical devices such as hearing aids or cochlear implants. These limi-
tations make it difficult to ascertain the effectiveness of corrective interventions in young
children with hearing loss.
functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) is a method for detecting brain activity that
relies on spectroscopic measurements of hemoglobin oxygenation.
Thus, fNIRS can
measure brain activity associated with the processing of auditory or other stimuli with-
out relying on electrical measurements. These factors make fNIRS a promising method
for assessing hearing in patients with suspected hearing loss, and for assessing the ef-
ficiency of corrective treatments in patients with documented hearing loss. Further, the
possibility of using fNIRS in an objective measure paradigm makes it possible to use
fNIRS in patients who are not able to follow directions, such as young children or patients
with cognitive impairments or limited language.
However, several limitations to the clinical use of fNIRS remain. In particular, it is un-
clear what approaches to signal processing that will give optimal clinically relevant inter-
pretations of fNIRS measurements. Further, it is unclear whether stimulation with noise
or speech is most suitable for detecting stimuli, although previous studies have shown
stronger responses to speech [4, 5].
This study will develop a pipeline for analyzing fNIRS data and compare the impact of
selected signal processing choices. the data will undergo pre-processeing using a previ-
ously established method that involves temporal derivative distribution repair, short chan-
nel regression, and negative correlation enhancement. These steps aim to enhance the
quality and reliability of data before further analysis A previously published approach to
pre-processing based on temporal derivative distribution repair, short channel regression,
and negative correlation enhancement will be applied to all data.Further, low-quality mea-
surements will be removed based on scalp-coupling index.
After preprocessing, the impact of using either a conservative or aggressive band-pass
filter to remove drift and heart-rate artifacts will be assessed. The impact will be assessed
by both waveform averaging and using a generalized linear model. Further, the morphol-
ogy of the average response waveforms for noise and speech will be assessed.

Theory
This section will first cover the physiological responses to auditory stimulation that we
attempt to measure through fNIRS. This is followed by an overview of the principles un-
derlying fNIRS measurements. A final section will cover the central sources of errors and
artifacts in fNIRS data. Descriptions of the methods used to minimize this noise will follow
in the methods section.
2.1
The auditory pathway
In humans, sounds waves received through the outer ear reach the tympanic membrane.
Vibrations in the tympanic membrane are transmitted through the middle ear by the malleus,
incus and stapes bones, the latter of which moves the oval window on the cochlea, lead-
ing to the sound vibrations being transmitted to the endolymph filling the cochlea. In the
cochlea, vibration of hair cells on the basilar membrane on the organ of corti leads to
release of the neurotransmitter glutamate, which affects the activity of the neurons of the
auditory nerve. This nerve transmits the encoded signals to the brainstem, where au-
ditory signals are initially processed. The transmission through the auditory nerve and
through the brainstem pathways forms the basis of auditory brainstem responses, which
are recorded electrically as an objective measure of hearing. The auditory neuronal sig-
nals then pass through the midbrain to the thalamus, from which they are transmitted to
the auditory cortex, which is located superficially in the superior temporal gyrus [6]. The
specific location of the superior temporal gyrus on the outside of the brain makes it an opti-
mal target for measurement using fNIRS, which works best when the depth of penetration
is small [7].
Bordering the primary auditory cortex is Wernicke’s area, which is important for language
comprehension. This is located in the left hemisphere of most humans, and contains
part of the superior temporal gyrus, supramarginal gyrus, middle temporal gyrus, angular
gyrus, and inferior temporal gyrus [8]. These gyri are all superficially located, and changes
in their activity could thus be recorded using fNIRS. The presence of Wernicke’s area is
a likely explanation for why speech is generally found to induce a stronger response than
other auditory stimuli, and why some studies find different responses in left versus right
hemispheres [4, 5].
2.2
The hemodynamic response
The presence of the auditory cortex explains why neuronal activity in the superior tem-
poral gyrus changes in response to auditory stimulus. However, fNIRS does not directly
measure neuronal activity, nor the electrical fields that neuronal activity creates. Rather,
fNIRS measures the concentration of oxygen, specifically oxygenated and deoxygenated
hemoglobin. One might expect increased neuronal activity to deplete oxygen. However,
there exists a close coupling between neuronal activity and blood flow [9]. Thus, an in-
crease in neuronal activity increases blood flow, and thus leads to a measurable increase
in the concentration of oxygenated hemoglobin. While the presence of a coupling be-
tween neural activity and blood flow has been suspected for more than a hundred years,
the exact mechanisms of hemodynamic coupling to neural activity remain incompletely
understood. It is however clear that this response involves both neurons and astrocytes,
acting via a wide range of molecular signals [9]. The consequence of this response is that
stimulation of the auditory cortex by exposure to sound will result in a transient increase
in the local concentration of oxygenated hemoglobin, which can be measured by fNIRS.

2.3
Fundamentals of functional Near Infrared Spectroscopy
Measurement of oxygenated and deoxygenated hemoglobin concentrations using fNIRS
is made possible by the absorption features of human tissue. Above wavelengths of
1300nm, water rapidly absorbs any photons, whereas wavelengths below 700nm are
susceptible to scattering and absorption by hemoglobin. This leaves a gap in the near-
infrared spectrum between 700-1300nm where light can penetrate relatively deeply into
human tissue [10]. In practice, absorption by water limits the high end of the spectrum to
950nm. Within this space, the absorption spectra of hemoglobin and deoxyhemoglobin
differ significantly, with oxyhemoglobin having higher absorption above around 800nm
and deoxyhemoglobin having higher absorption below 800nm [11]. Further, while scat-
tering is lower within this spectral range, it is still significant. This scattering allows for
the detection of light traveling through non-linear paths. Thus, placement of optodes and
detectors on the surface of the skull can measure optical density in a banana-shaped
volume between them, which includes a significant volume of cortex close to the skull
[12]. Together, these properties allow for non-invasive optical detection of changes in
hemoglobin concentrations within the regions of the brain closest to the cortex in the near-
infrared spectrum. Following the terminology initially used for detection of functional brain
activity using magnetic resonance imaging, this technique has been termed functional
near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS).
2.4
Sources of errors and artifacts in fNIRS data
Like all measurements, recorded fNIRS signals include a significant noise component.
Due to the high level of scattering and absorption within the scalp and skull, which the
light has to pass twice, the signal-to-noise ratio for fNIRS signals is significantly lower
than for fMRI [7]. This lower signal-to-noise ratio necessitates better signal processing
pipelines, which is a key motivation for this project.
Noise in fNIRS data can be broken down into instrumental noise, experimental noise
and physiological noise [12]. Instrumental noise refers to interference with measurement
equipment function, which can be due to both hardware limitations or degradation and en-
vironmental perturbation of measurement due to external light or electrical interference.
Experimental noise mainly refers to motion artifacts, where movement of the head during
measurement can lead to abrupt changes in signal intensity. while fNIRS offers greater
resistance to motion artifacts compared to other methods, addressing these artifacts re-
mains a significant concern. Finally, physiological noise is often considered the most
important component. Due to the scalp and skull being closer to the optode and detector
than the region of interest, physiological parameters such as respiration, heart rate, and
changes in blood pressure greatly influence the recorded fNIRS signal.
Instrument noise is typically associated with high frequencies (>1 Hz), whereas human
heart rates typically fall around 50 bpm (equivalent to 0.83 Hz). To mitigate these sources
of noise, low-pass filtering can be applied. However, addressing other components of
physiological noise and motion artifacts can be more challenging. Fortunately, a wide
range of methods has been developed to minimize their impact. In this project, time-
derivative distribution repair will be used to correct for motion artifacts, while short channel
regression and band-pass filtering will be used to correct for physiological noise. A de-
scription of these methods and how they are applied is contained in the following section.

3
Methods
This section will explain the data collection, preprocessing, and analysis performed for
the project. A brief overview of the theoretical background for each applied technique will
be provided.
3.1
Data collection
Data acquisition was performed prior to the beginning of this project. Briefly, fNIRS data
was collected using a NIRScoutX cap (NIRX). At each visit, the subject was exposed to
20 seconds of stimulation with either noise, speech, or silence as a control. The spacing
between stimuli and ordering of stimuli was randomized. Longer breaks between trials
where the subject could move around or drink water were also included in the measure-
ments.
12 participants volunteered for the study. Participant ages were between 24-37, and none
had a history of hearing loss. Subject hairstyle, hair color, and skin color were recorded.
Data was recorded twice for each participant at separate visits. No consideration was
given to the repeated measurements, and thus 24 datasets were initially pooled for fur-
ther analysis. However, after analyzing scalp coupling indices, 4 subject visits showed
poor coupling in more than a third of the channels, indicating poor data quality. The scalp
coupling indices for these visits can be seen in appendix A. Two subjects had one trial
excluded, while one subject had poor coupling on both trials. All three subjects with ex-
cluded trials had thick, dark hair. Concerningly, the subject with both trials excluded had
the darkest skin color of any participant, with a Fitzpatrick scale rating of 4 or ”medium
brown”. These subjects were excluded from further analysis, resulting in 20 trials for final
analysis.
3.2
Data pre-processing
Raw recordings consisting of raw intensity measurements were loaded into python, and
were then converted to optical density. Epochs with severe artifacts were manually noted
and marked as ’BAD’ in the data, as were the longer break periods. The Scalp cou-
pling index (SCI) was then calculated and any channels below an 80% threshold were
excluded. Temporal derivative distribution repair was used to correct for motion artifacts.
Short channel regression was performed and subtracted from the remaining channels
to account for physiological background noise. Using the modified Lambert-Beers, data
was then transformed to concentration measurements for oxygenated and deoxygenated
hemoglobin. Next, negative correlation enhancement was used for signal enhancement.
Finally, one of two bandpass filters was applied to exclude the signal components which
were not predicted to include the relevant physiological response. A box diagram of the
preprocessing procedure can be seen in figure 3.1

both the high- and low-wavelength measurements to assess the coupling of electrodes to

3.2.2
Signal enhancement
After removing poor-quality channels using the approach described above, signal en-
hancement was performed following a similar approach to a recently published paper[4].
First, temporal derivative distribution repair was applied to minimize motion artifacts [14].
This approach capitalizes on the fact that motion artifacts typically exhibts significantly
higher magnitude than any other signal components. They will thus differ from the mean
signal value by several standard deviations, and this effect can be utilized to remove
the artifacts. This is done by calculating a temporal derivative function. A weighting
function is then iteratively estimated using Tukey’s biweight function, which results in a
reduced weighting of residuals with high standard deviation. These weights are then
applied to the temporal derivative, which is then integrated to yield the corrected signal

[14]. In practice, temporal derivative distribution repair was performed using the tempo-
ral_derivative_distribution_repair-function in the preprocessing.nirs-package in the MNE
library.
Second, short-channel regression based on the nearest short channel was carried out
to subtract as much physiological background signal as possible. Short channels only
superficially penetrate the skull, and thus contain no information about the brain region
of interest.
However, they do contain information about physiological effects on oxy-
hemoglobin and deoxyhemoglobin concentrations, such as those resulting from global
changes in blood oxygenation or pulse. By subtracting a scaled version of the closest
short channel from each informative long channel, these variations can be reduced [15].
Practically this was achieved by the use of the short_channel_regression-function in the
signal_enhancement-package in the MNE-NIRS library.
Third, correlation-based signal improvement as described in [16] was applied. This method
makes use of the fact that while the concentrations of oxy-hemoglobin and deoxy-hemoglobin
are negatively correlated, parts of the noise component will have equal effects on the oxy-
and deoxyhemoglobin measurements. Assuming that the noise amplitude ratio is equal to
the true amplitude ratio between oxy- and deoxyhemoglobin, a simple formula is derived
that can enhance the contrast to noise ratio of fNIRS measurements:
[HbO]Actual = 1/2 ∗([HbO]Measured −α ∗[HbR]Measured
[HbR]Actual = −1/α ∗([HbO]Actual
Where α is the ratio of the standard deviation of [HbO]Measured and [HbO]Measured. In
practice, we applied this method using the enhance_negative_correlation-function in the
signal_enhancement-package in the MNE-NIRS.

3.2.3
Modified Lambert-Beers Law
The relationship between optical density and concentration is described by Lambert-Beers
law, which includes a term for the path length and a wavelength-specific extinction coef-
ficient. However, since fNIRS relies on photon scattering, the path length traveled by de-
tected photons will differ from the linear separation between detectors. Further, the path
length traveled will depend on the scattering properties of the media [11]. As a result, a
modified version of Lambert-Beers law is used to convert changes in optical density to es-
timated changes in concentrations of oxy- and deoxyhemoglobin. This modified version
takes the form [17, 11]:
∆OD = ϵ(λ) ∗∆c ∗d ∗B(λ)
Where ∆OD is the change in optical density, ϵ(λ) is the wavelength-specific extinction
coefficient, ∆c is the change in concentration, d is the linear distance between the optode
and the detector and B, which must account both for the longer path length traveled by
photons due to diffusion and that the change in hemoglobin is only happening in part of
the imaged volume. The first component is called the differential pathlength factor (DPF),
while the latter is commonly called the partial pathlength factor (PPF). The specific values
for ϵ(λ)HbO and ϵ(λ)HbR are known and constant, while DPF and PPF will differ between
subjects. Unfortunately, estimating DPF for each experimental subject is usually imprac-
tical, and thus pre-tabulated values are usually used [11]. In this study, the transformation
was performed using the nirs.beer_lambert_law-function in the preprocessing-package in
the MNE-NIRS, with a PPF value of 0.1 for both wavelengths.

3.2.4
Bandpass filtering
Established protocols within the field of auditory fNIRS analysis use band-pass filtering to
remove unwanted high- and low-frequency signal components including the contributions
of drift and pulse [4]. To correctly parameterize their filter, Luke et al. plot a model of
the expected hemodynamic response against their measured data, which exhibits a clear
pulse-rate component. A similar plot for the data collected for this project can be seen in
3.6. As can be seen in the figure, the bandpass parameters used by Luke et al. (filter A)
are very conservative and include frequency bands between 0.01 hz and 0.7 hz, which the
model of the hemodynamic response does not predict to be informative. An additional,
narrower bandpass filter with the cutoffs at 0.025 hz and 0.35 hz (filter B) will also be
assessed.
3.3
Averaging analysis
For the waveform averaging analysis, the remaining channels were divided into four re-
gions of interest. These regions were the frontal lobe, occipital lobe, and the right and left
superior temporal gyri. The average intensity of HbO 4-6 seconds after onset of each of
the stimuli was then calculated for each subject. The averages for each condition across
each subject was compared using a linear mixed model. The Python code for this analysis
can be found in appendix E

3.4
GLM analysis
For the GLM analysis, the pre-processing steps included conversion to optical density,
removal of bad epochs, breaks, and channels with low scalp coupling index, conversion
to HbO concentrations, and filtering using one of the two filters. Temporal-derivative distri-
bution repair, short channel regression and negative correlation enhancement steps were
thus skipped for this analysis. A design matrix was then constructed with a cosine drift
model, 5 second stimulus duration, short channels and an SPM hemodynamic reponse
function, which is a canonical model consisting of a linear combination of two gamma
functions [4]. A generalized linear model was then fit to this design matrix. Channels
were then grouped into the same regions of interest as for the averaging analysis (frontal
lobe, occipital lobe, left and right superior temporal gyri). A mixed linear model was then
used to compare the regression coefficients θ for each of the conditions, chromophores,
and regions of interest. The Python code for this analysis can be found in appendix D

Waveform averaging

5
Discussion
This section will discuss the collected results and their implications. First, the main com-
parisons of the project between filtering and analysis approaches will be discussed. Then,
the findings of this study will be compared to the findings of a similar recently published
fNIRS study. The final section contains a discussion of the findings of this study for future
fNIRS applications.
5.1
Comparison of aggressive and conservative filtering
One aim of this study was to assess the impact of a narrower band-pass filtering, filter B
on the analysis of fNIRS data in the auditory domain. We theorized that the conserva-
tive filter, filter A, employed by a previous study [4] would be insufficient to fully remove
the confounding heart rate component of the measured data. We further expected that
increasing the high-pass cutoff would remove more drift with limited effect on the measure-
ment of the hemodynamic response of interest. While we observed narrower confidence
intervals in both averaging and GLM-based approaches and a reduction in both drift and
unwanted oscillations, the impact on p values in our statistical analysis was ambiguous.
In the averaging-based analysis, the results of noise versus control and speech versus
control showed much lower p-values with filter A, despite the narrower confidence inter-
vals. However, the pooled analysis of both stimuli versus control exhibited the opposite
pattern. For the GLM, the p-values were lower for filter B across all of the three men-
tioned comparisons, despite the ability of the GLM regression to account for the filtered
noise. Thus, the more aggressive filtering seems to be preferable overall, but with the
potential to reduce the power of the averaging-based analysis under some conditions. A
more in-depth analysis, preferably including independently collected data, is necessary
to definitively determine the optimal filtering cutoffs for this application of fNIRS.
5.2
Comparison of averaging and GLM
GLM-based analysis of fNIRS data has several theoretical advantages compared to averaging-
based approaches, including the ability to account for more complex models of signal
noise [4]. This contrasts with averaging, which assumes that whatever shape the noise
component assumes, it will have a mean of zero. This assumption may not hold for
fNIRS data [18]. However, in our case, the averaging-based approach displays much
more power to detect differences in responses to stimuli.
While this may result from our choice of GLM model parameters, this highlights another
issue with the use of GLM models in offline analysis. If the researcher is free to choose
any parameters that perform well in their collected data, it is less likely that their results will
replicate in new data sets. An averaging-based approach with fewer degrees of freedom
for the researcher offers significant advantages on these terms.
5.3
Relationship of results to published studies
The approach to data collection and analysis in this study is fairly close to that performed
by Luke et al. in a recent publication [4]. This similarity allows for a comparison of data
and conclusions.
Despite the similarity in data collection and analysis, both the waveforms and GLM results
display clear differences. The most relevant data for both analyses is that recorded in the
The impact of analysis parameters on the detectability of fNIRS responses to auditory stimulation
15
==PAGE:22==
right superior temporal gyrus. This data is the most similar, with the exception that this
study estimates a higher response to noise while Luke et al. report a higher response to
speech. However, these differences are non-significant in both cases, and thus likely re-
sult from random differences in measurement. The largest difference is in the response of
the left superior temporal gyrus. Our GLM analysis reports no significant oxyhemoglobin
response in this region, while Luke and colleagues report a similar response to the right
superior temporal gyrus. Further, our GLM analysis displays a significant decreases in
the oxyhemoglobin concentrations in the frontal and occipital regions of interest, which is
unexpected, doesn’t seem present in the average waveform, and isn’t reported by Luke
et al. It is unclear why we observe these differences.
When our data is filtered with the same filter that is used by Luke et al., we observe
period oscillations in the waveform average. These oscillations are not present in Luke
et al.’s waveforms [4]. We speculate that these differences may result from differences in
participant characteristics, as small differences in physiological parameters such as heart
rate could bring their frequency below the conservative cutoff chosen by Luke et al..
A main conclusion of Luke et al.’s study is that GLM and averaging-based approaches
lead to similar experimental interpretations [4]. However, this study reports quite differ-
ent interpretations for the averaging and GLM-based method. In particular, the power of
the averaging-based analysis displays superior performance in detecting differences in
oxyhemoglobin concentration after stimulation.
Another conclusion from Luke et al.’s study is that the response to speech is higher than
that to noise. Such a pattern has also been reported in another independent study [5].
However, our data displays the opposite pattern in the right superior temporal gyrus, al-
though the magnitude of the difference is small and non-significant. This difference in
observations could be due to random fluctuations, which happened to result in higher
speech responses in the two previous studies, which were in both cases not statistically
significant when directly compared. If this is the case, it would highlight the importance of
being rigorous when evaluating data that agrees with theoretical predictions.
5.4
Implications for future fNIRS applications
While GLMs offer several theoretical advantages, this study highlights the advantages of
the relatively simpler waveform averaging. If GLM-based approaches are chosen, care
should be taken in evaluating whether the results are generally applicable, or whether
they result from the particular fit between model parameters and the specific data.
The discrepancies observed between our results and those of previous studies under-
score the importance of replication studies in fNIRS research. Future studies should aim
to validate findings across multiple datasets and research groups to establish more reli-
able and generalizable results. This is particularly crucial as fNIRS moves towards po-
tential clinical applications in assessing auditory function.
A final application is the choice of study population. Both our study and that of Luke et al.
assessed responses in healthy, adult volunteers with no histories of hearing impairment.
Our study encountered issues with some participants, with thick, dark brown hair likely
resulting in poor scalp coupling of the optode-detector pairs. Concerningly, the participant
with the darkest skin color also had the worst measurement quality at both visits. Further
studies are required to assess the efficacy of fNIRS measurement in other populations,
and especially to assess whether processing protocols that are effective in a population
of healthy, light-skinned adults apply equally well to other populations.
==PAGE:23==
6
Conclusion
This study aimed to develop a pipeline for analyzing fNIRS data and compare the impact
of selected signal processing choices in the context of auditory stimulation. The effects of
using either a conservative or aggressive band-pass filter to remove drift and heart-rate
artifacts were assessed, as were averaging- and GLM-based analysis approaches.
The results demonstrate that more aggressive filtering could reduce both drift and un-
wanted oscillations in the fNIRS signal, resulting in narrower confidence intervals. How-
ever, the impact on statistical significance was not uniform across all comparisons. Con-
trary to expectations, the GLM-based analysis generally benefited from more aggressive
filtering, while the averaging-based approach showed mixed results.
Contrary to some previous studies, this study did not display a consistently stronger re-
sponse to speech compared to noise stimuli. This highlights the importance of rigorous
evaluation of data, even when it agrees with theoretical predictions and underscores the
need for replication in fNIRS research.
A key finding of this study is that the averaging-based approach displayed superior per-
formance in detecting differences in oxyhemoglobin concentration after stimulation com-
pared to the GLM-based method. This contrasts with the theoretical advantages of GLMs
and emphasizes the importance of empirical validation of analysis methods.
While fNIRS shows promise as a non-invasive method for assessing auditory processing,
our results suggest that current methods may not yet be sufficiently reliable for clinical ap-
plications. Further refinement of data collection, preprocessing, and analysis techniques
is needed to improve the consistency and interpretability of fNIRS results.
