I. 
Abstract  
Generating building requirements based on user activities is often a manually performed process 
that is long-winded, error prone, and most often based on the personal experience of architects. 
Literature and interviews suggest that architects don’t always know who the users of buildings are 
going to be, and that a database of user activities might be useful. In this thesis the work of Kim et al. 
is used together with Activity Theory and Workplace Planning to develop a process of automatically 
generating spatial requirements based on empirical activity data. The solution is developed using the 
design science research method (DSRM) to iterate over the solution multiple times to test and 
improve it. The developed solution consist of three parts: A method of collecting activity data in a 
formal way, an ontology to arrange and store the collected data, and a process of using the data to 
generate requirements. A non-conducted step is described at the end of the iterations to prepare for 
future work.

1 Introduction  
Advances in digitalization and new technologies within the building industry prompted a need for 
new ways of designing space that focus more and more on space efficiency. Space efficiency is 
important because not only does it affect the wellbeing and productivity of the inhabitants, but also 
influences the energy performance, and its economic impact. 
Traditionally, space design is done based on architects’ and designers’ accumulated knowledge and 
experience, whose decisions are often difficult to argue and explain. Studies to formalize and 
automate decision making in space design have been done repeatedly to increase performance, 
facilitate integration with technologies such as building information modelling or artificial 
intelligence, and enhance the overall process (Kim, Rajagopal, Fischer, & Kam, 2013) (Anderson, 
Bailey, Heumann, & Davis, 2018).  
The main driver of good design could be defined, in a sense, by how people choose to use it. In the 
case of space design, it’s how people choose to use spaces and what activities are performed within 
them. Dr Tae Wan Kim has conducted a series of research studies that focuses on human activity 
within an office and university setting, developing a method to optimize space-use.  
To address the issue of manually generating space requirements, this research focuses on gathering 
empirical data centred around the activities and activity needs of the observed participants as 
opposed to any spatial requirements derived from rules and regulations. This is because the 
research could be considered as an extension of Kim et al.’s papers as they points out the need of a 
database that contains a description of user activities and spaces, and can potentially be further 
explored to create a formalized activity-space pair generator  (Kim, Rajagopal, Fischer, & Kam, 2013). 
The gathered data is then processed through a method similar to Kim et al.’s method of automated 
space-use analysis, which then generates an accurate representation of activity based spatial 
requirements (Kim, Kim, Cha, & Fischer, 2015).  
In order to validate the results, the generated spatial requirements must prove accurate applicability 
in similar settings during the early stages of design. They must show a valuable addition to 
traditional regulatory design choices, without interfering in the exploration of multiple design 
solutions. 
The study is done following an optimization process that is used in design science, called design 
science research methodology (DSRM). DSRM is an iterative method of research that develops and 
optimizes design artefacts based on scientific research and applicable in real life.

 Literature review 
To help identify the problem, literature on the subject of early stage design requirements and 
requirements generation was reviewed. This literature review led to gaps in the research that would 
be interesting to further research. 
 
1.1.1 A database of user-activities for Space-use Analysis in early design phases 
In the paper “A knowledge-based framework for automated space-use analysis” (Kim, Rajagopal, 
Fischer, & Kam, 2013) Kim et al. propose a method of doing automated space-use analysis of 
buildings based on user activity data. In the discussion of the paper Kim et al. mention the possibility 
of using the proposed method in iterative design phases where space-use analysis is used to validate 
design iterations. In the same context they mention the usefulness of having a knowledge database 
on user activities that is searchable by project type and region. This kind of database could assist 
architects in early design phases, where information about users, user activities and spaces are often 
insufficient (Kim, Rajagopal, Fischer, & Kam, 2013).  
 
1.1.2 Requirements model for use in rapid prototyping 
In the book “Requirements management interface to building product models” (Kiviniemi, 2005), 
chapter 5 “Rapid Prototyping” Kiviniemi describes a method to link a requirements model with 
design models in a design process. This allows building designers to immediately check if current 
design solutions fulfil the requirements set for the project, both if the design changes or if the 
requirements model changes. 
==PAGE:13==
Page 3 of 83 
 
 
 
Figure 1 - Integrated Project Information Model: Model Hierarchy and connections (Kiviniemi, 2005) 
In Figure 1 one can see how a single requirements model is connected to multiple design models, 
production models and finally the maintenance model. This allows building designers to verify the 
design solutions in every step of the building design process and detect potential issues should the 
requirements change (Kiviniemi, 2005).  
What could be interesting to research further is how such a requirements model might be 
automatically updated if user information is updated. In the method that Kiviniemi describes the 
requirements would still need to be manually updated when user information changes. 
 
1.1.3 Automated updating of space design requirements connecting user activities and space 
types 
In the paper “Automated updating of space design requirements connecting user activities and 
space types” (Kim, Kim, Cha, & Fischer, 2015) Kim et al. describe a problem where architects have to 
manually update building requirements as user data is changed. The paper describes this practice as 
error-prone and time consuming for two reasons: 
1. “Architects do not systematically document the rationale behind requirements engineering 
with regard to user information. Often the rationale exists only in the architects' minds and is 
therefore difficult to track over time.” (Kim, Kim, Cha, & Fischer, 2015) 
 
2. “Architects need to deal with the complexity that arises from many types of requirements, 
many types of space in a building, and many types of users and their activities.” (Kim, Kim, 
Cha, & Fischer, 2015) 
==PAGE:14==
Page 4 of 83 
 
Although the paper describes a solution to this problem the solution is never tested on real life 
activity data. The authors validate the solution by testing it against hypothetical activity data based 
on observations. First the method is tested against changes in the data, although still hypothetical, 
to see if the method can take changes into account. It can.  
Secondly the hypothetical data is presented to experienced architects who are asked to generate 
requirements manually and their results are then compared to the ones from the proposed method 
described in the paper. Some of the architects are then asked about the discrepancies between the 
manually obtained requirements and the automatically produced ones, and the architects agreed 
more with the automatically generated ones (Kim, Kim, Cha, & Fischer, 2015).  
To expand this work by Kim et al. it would be interesting to look into how the automated generation 
of requirements would respond to activity data collected in real life. Secondly it would be useful to 
research a formalized method of collecting such data, as it forms the foundation of the generated 
requirements. 
 
 Interviews with DTU Campus Service 
In interviews with two managers from DTU Campus Service (A. Møller & B. Carlsen, Personal 
interview, June 21st, 2019), two problems related to requirements generation were identified. The 
first one was about how they would try to predict the need for new buildings before the need 
actually arose. The rationale behind this is that you can then have the building done much sooner 
than if you only start building it when the need is present. This does however lead to a situation 
where they are “designing for no one”, meaning that they don’t know exactly who are going to 
occupy the buildings and requirements therefore might be difficult to define. They would know 
roughly the type of users who would occupy the new buildings though. 
The other problem, they described, was that when designing new buildings for known future users, 
the requirements would sometimes be defined by the users themselves. This would sometimes lead 
to requirements based on want the users think they want and not what they actually need. 
Both of these problems could potentially be solved by having a more formal way of generating 
requirements based on data about user’s activities, thus supporting doing more research in this 
area. 
 
==PAGE:15==
Page 5 of 83 
 
 Problem statement 
Generating building requirements based on user activities is often a manually performed process 
that is long-winded, error prone, and most often based on the personal experience of architects. 
Automated methods have been developed to support this generation process and to allow rapid 
changes in requirements when user activity information changes. These methods have, however not 
been properly tested with real life data and are dependent on sufficient data on building user 
information for each specific design project. Furthermore, the collection of this user activity data is 
currently not well defined nor systematic, making inaccurate and biased activity data more likely. 
 
 Research questions 
To solve the stated problem the following questions should be explored. These questions are 
assumed to lead to further questions, but they provide a place to start the research: 
• 
Is it possible to use empirical data of building users’ activities to generate and support 
early stage requirements for future building design projects?  
• 
If it is possible is it then an advantageous way to do it, that could improve the design 
process in the future?  
• 
How should one go about collecting the user activity data in a formalized and efficient 
way?  
• 
Do already proved ways of collecting and analysing this kind of data already exist?  
• 
If so, what are they and can they be applied in this context of generating requirements?

2 Background 
Currently there is a multitude of space generating methods that are implemented in design 
conceptualization and development, but most of them rely heavily on experience, vast amount of 
knowledge, and laws and regulations. This leaves very little room for expressing what people need 
from a space they inhabit or the way they may use it. Even less so, a formalized method that could 
automatically generate information on needed spaces based on a series of inputs. 
With advancements in technology within the architecture, engineering and construction industry 
such as BIM and Virtual Design & Construction (VDC), digitalization is an important but often a slow 
and tedious process. Kim et al. had made significant contributions by developing space-use analysis 
methods to study the effects of user activity on the quality of office space design. But throughout 
their research, Kim et al. observed a lack of formal methods that create relationships between 
different perspectives that can influence the space-use of a facility. This led to the development of a 
framework that would intuitively formalize relationships between spaces, users and activities. Kim et 
al. also mention the need of a general database that will be used in the early stages of designing in 
order to cover some of the information gaps (Kim, Rajagopal, Fischer, & Kam, 2013). 
In later papers, Kim et al. underline the need of digitalization by mentioning that it is difficult to 
record reasoning behind design choices and to record all the various elements that are used in the 
design (Kim & Fischer, 2014a) (Kim, Kim, Cha, & Fischer, 2015). And the existing frameworks, such as 
virtual design and construction (VDC), could facilitate data collection and analysis, but are still in 
their early stages of development and rely heavily on assumptions and simplifications. The VDC 
method directly involves users in the design process, which enables the designers to take more 
accurate decisions throughout the entire design process. The users in such are virtual 
representations of real-life users without the latter’s involvement (Kim, Cha, & Kim, 2016). 
But a virtual representation of users must be created based on real life information that, in this case, 
could be very difficult to gather. There are methods, such as activity theory, that are used 
extensively in studies related to human behavior in the context of information systems and human-
computer interaction. Using activity theory, it facilitates constructing a framework around complex 
notions such as activities (Kaptelinin, n.d.) (Crawford & Hasan, 2006). 
In the context of working environments, activities are analyzed through the use of workplace 
planning method developed by Pennanen. This method looks how the utilization of each space 
supports the core activities of the organization. This shows the importance of how spaces are used 
to the business aspect of an organization (Pennanen, 2004). 
==PAGE:17==
Page 7 of 83 
 
Given the presented papers, it shows a clear need for a more standardized method that analyzes 
activities as a measuring unit for generating spatial requirements. This is important because it 
provides a valuable view on the necessity of people when designing new spaces. It also provides an 
additional support in design programming, alongside rules and regulations.  
The following parts will go into an in-depth analysis, in order to propose a possible solution through 
a synthesis of the theory presented in the literature. 
 
 Activity theory 
Activity theory is a theory that contextualizes activities based on multiple interconnected systems. 
Engeström explains it as a triangular diagram, or better said, a group of triads that ultimately 
influences the desired outcome (Engeström, Miettinen, & Punamäki, 1999). 
The first and probably the most important triad contains the subject, the object, and the instrument 
or tool (see   
Figure 2). Here lies the direct relationship between the subject and the object, the activity itself. It is 
defined as the work a subject does on an object in order to obtain a desired outcome. An example 
could be an architect (the subject) who is drawing a floorplan (the object) to create a design 
proposal for a client (the outcome). Moreover, the architect is using pen and paper as tools (the 
instrument). 
  
Figure 2 - Subject - Instrument - Object triad 
In the same sense, a subject uses tools that can be either external (e.g., a computer, a hammer) or 
internal (e.g., a plan or schedule). They can impose limitations or enable the activity (Kuutti, 1995). 
For example, having a meeting is enabled by the existence of a space that has the minimum 
requirements for a meeting, such as a table and chairs, but having a private meeting can also be 
limited to a specific meeting room and cannot be performed elsewhere. This is important because 
through the use of instruments, the object is adjusted in order to reach its goal. It also shows 
whether or not there is a designated instrument that should be used to perform the activity. 
 
==PAGE:18==
Page 8 of 83 
 
Engeström goes further and explains that there are two other triads that influence the process of an 
activity (Figure 3 and Figure 4). The rules mediate between the subject and its community and the 
division of labour mediates between the community and the object.  
The community can be described as the social context of said activity or all the users involved in the 
activity system. In an office environment this can be associated to a team or a department. On a 
larger scale it can also take the form of an organization.  
The rules cover all the explicit and implicit norms and conventions within the community, such as 
following certain drawing conventions when designing a new building. 
 
Figure 3 - Subject - Rules - Community triad 
The division of labour is the explicit and implicit organization of the community in relation to the 
activity, such as, in the process of designing a new building, the end-users will only be involved in the 
very end stages of the design.  
 
Figure 4 - Object - Division of labour - Community triad 
In a larger perspective, the three triads are part of a system that describes an activity according to 
activity theory. Bakhurst calls the system the second-generation activity theory (Figure 5). 
  
==PAGE:19==
Page 9 of 83 
 
  
Figure 5 – Activity theory according to Bakhurst (Bakhurst, 2009) 
Bakhurst makes a clear distinction between action and activity using Leontiev’s hierarchy, thus 
determining how detailed is the activity theory analysis. 
“Leontiev distinguishes between “action” and “activity”. An action is conducted by an 
individual or group to fulfil some “goal”. An activity, in contrast, is undertaken by a 
community and it has an “object” and a “motive” […] He invites us to consider a 
member of a hunter-gatherer society who is a “beater”, startling animals so that 
others can catch them (see Leontiev 1981, 210). According to Leontiev, his action is 
beating a hedge to startle the game; his activity is hunting. The action is individual, 
the activity collective.”  - (Bakhurst, 2009) 
In an activity theory perspective, an activity is represented by the outcome and must have a motive. 
Given Leontiev’s example, the activity, which is hunting, has the motive of having food and eating. 
The action, beating the bush, would then be the object in the triple mentioned above and has the 
goal to make noise so the animals are startled out of their hiding place. The operation itself is how 
the action is further broken down and shows how it’s performed based on certain conditions. In 
Leontiev’s example the conditions are to use branches based on how much noises they can make in 
order to scare the animals. 
 
Figure 6 - Hierarchical levels of activity (Hashim & Jones, 2007)

2.1.1 Application of Activity Theory. 
Crawford & Hasan have developed a method in their paper “Demonstrations of the Activity Theory 
Framework for Research in Information Systems” (Crawford & Hasan, 2006), in which they use 
activity theory to study the social technical systems in both professional and day to day situations. 
They describe a series of five research projects in which the activity theory framework helps 
establish clear explanations of the studied activities and appropriate methods of data analysis. The 
reason why they used activity theory is because the subjects of their analysis are described as 
complex systems that are characterised by the interaction between human and technical elements. 
The framework is inspired from the developmental work research (DWR), a framework that expands 
cultural-historical activity theory into more practical cases. Aside from the five vignettes, another 
example of DWR is also analysed by Amstel et al in “Expanding the representation of user activities” 
where activity theory is applied in a case study on the design development of a medical imaging 
centre (Amstel, Zerjav, Hartmann, Voort, & Dewulf, 2015). 
Crawford & Hasan expand on the framework by breaking it down into seven parts that are ultimately 
applied onto the five vignettes mentioned above: 
1. The object of the activity. 
2. The subject of the activity. 
3. The purpose of the activity. 
4. The tools needed, which are split into 3 categories: 
a. Primary – tangible, external or physical. 
==PAGE:21==
Page 11 of 83 
 
b. Secondary – internal, semiotic, mental. 
c. Tertiary - schematic, where mind and culture act together: environment or 
ecosystems. 
5. The culture and context. 
6. Activity tools. 
7. Research interventions and its evolution. 
This seven-point framework helps explain the complex and dynamic processes of people within 
certain social-technical contexts. It helps analyse the relationships between each element of the 
system and how much they influence each other. 
“As already stated, an activity is defined by the tool-mediated, dialectic relationship 
between subject and object i.e. between the doer and their purpose. The mediation is 
a mutual development of both the activity and the tools which including primary 
(physical) tools, secondary tools (ideas, models etc) and tertiary tools, such as the 
community within which the activity takes place. The capability and availability of 
tools, mediates what can be done in the community at all three levels.” - (Crawford & 
Hasan, 2006) 
 
 Workplace planning 
Workplace planning is an iterative process that predicts space-use based on user activities to 
support core business operations by using production theories. From the business perspective, 
workplace planning has three main drivers: access to qualified employees, efficiency and 
productivity in the workplace, and cost reduction (Pennanen, 2004). 
Workplace planning is defined as a complex mathematical model that uses production theories to 
conceptualize an effective workplace based on the flow of activity and strategic decisions. Pennanen 
defines that the size of a space is determined by activities that are taking place there and the 
number of spaces is linked to their utilization. In contrast to that, spaces that are not needed in the 
organization’s strategy are considered as a waste. This model then outputs the different space types 
needed and their respective areas as an intricate function of the activity timing, duration and 
performance.  
The information that is typically needed in the workplace planning procedure is acquired through 
various documents and sources such as: the total volume of the functional sector, the activity bill

 Ontology for representing building users’ activities in space-use analysis 
When collecting data on user activities it’s useful to have a formalized way to store and handle it. 
This ensures that data is always comparable and easy to work with, and it eases the process of 
digitalizing and analysing the data. In information science such a representation is called an 
ontology. Kim et al. (Kim & Fischer, 2014b) have already developed an ontology to store user activity 
data for use in Space-use Analysis. The ontology is made to enable architects to gather, store and 
reuse user activity information, that is possible to feed into a computer for analysis. The ontology is 
designed to be formal and comprehensive. It is formal when all activities can be described using the 
ontology, and it is comprehensive when the activities can all be distinguished from each other (Kim 
& Fischer, 2014b). 
 
2.3.1 Characteristics of User Activities 
The ontology developed by Kim et al. is based on five characteristics of User Activities, which were 
identified by Kim et al. by using literature review, case observations, and interviews with architects. 
The characteristics are as follows (Kim & Fischer, 2014b): 
C1: Some users have more demanding spatial requirements than the minimum requirements 
might provide. This can also be tied to the nature of the organizational structure of a 
company, where a hierarchical company sees a bigger difference in user requirements than 
a company with a flat structure. 
C2: Some activities require a designated space e.g. a personal office. A designated space cannot 
be used by other users even when vacant. 
C3: Some activities occupy an entire room, whereas others only need part of a room. 
C4: Some activities need a certain type of space e.g. a lab, or a certain type of feature inside the 
space e.g. ventilation or light. Other activities can be conducted in any vacant space. 
C5: Some activities are atypical and are not conducted on a regular basis. These are not included 
in Space-Use Analysis, but as they are still predictable, they should be accommodated by the 
design. 
 
==PAGE:26==
Page 16 of 83 
 
2.3.2 Space-Use Type Differentiators 
The five characteristics described above are then used to define six Space-Use Type Differentiators 
(SUTD). These SUTDs should be seen as a set of choices that help explain a user activity.  The first 
three SUTDs should be applied to all activities: 
SUTD1: Is the activity typical or atypical? An atypical activity is not included in the space-use 
analysis but should still be accounted for in the design. 
SUTD2: Is the user important or regular? An important user should have their preferred 
requirements (preference) fulfilled whereas a regular user can be given the minimum 
requirements (constraints). 
SUTD3: Are the preferred and minimum requirements the same or different? If they are the 
same there is no reason to differentiate between important and regular users.  
The following three SUTDs are applied to both the preference and the constraints. If they are the 
same, as per SUTD3, the following three SUTDs are only applied once: 
SUTD4: Does the activity need a designated space or not? 
SUTD5: Does the activity occupy an entire room or just part of a room? A part of a room is 
referred to as ‘equipment’. 
SUTD6: Does the activity require a specifically named space or just a set of certain features?

•

•

•

The spatial requirement-object represents the physical requirements of the activity. All spatial 
requirements need the following traits: 
• 
Name: The name of the requirement. 
• 
Space type: The type of space that the activity occupies. This represents activities being 
conducted in specifically named spaces. E.g. a classroom or food lab. 
• 
Space super type: This represent the super type of a space. E.g. lab or meeting room.

•

• 
Space number: The number of rooms the activity needs to occupy. 
•

•

• 
Equipment other feature: The features of the required equipment. E.g. specification of a 
computer. 
 
 Automated updating of requirements 
In the paper “Automated updating of space design requirements connecting user activities and 
space types” (Kim, Kim, Cha, & Fischer, 2015) Kim et al. presents a method of automatically updating 
design requirements when user information changes, activities and their requirements changes, or 
when target utilization of space types changes.  
 
2.4.1 Workplace planning 
Kim et al. describes the traditional approach to determine space requirements and number of space 
types as using standardized parameters based on personal experience or surveys. This method can 
adapt requirements based on the number of users but does not take changes to activities into 
account. Also, if a client wants to have higher space utilization in some areas than others this is 
==PAGE:30==
Page 20 of 83 
 
difficult to dynamically consider when changing requirements. Using workplace planning would 
allow architects to set the target utilization of space types and determine the required number of 
each type based on user activity information such as frequency and duration (Kim, Kim, Cha, & 
Fischer, 2015). If utilization is below 50% then activities can be performed without waiting and 
below 75%, they can be performed with appropriate scheduling (Pennanen, 2004). 
 
2.4.2 Method of updating requirements 
Kim et al. (Kim, Kim, Cha, & Fischer, 2015) describes a method to formalize and automate the 
practice of updating requirements based on workplace planning. The first part of this process is to 
represent requirements in a computer-interpretable way and formalize the connection between 
user information and requirements.  
Kim et al. classifies space requirements into two categories: 
1. Activity-based requirements which are directly tied to the activities being performed. These 
requirements cover examples such as “computer work” needing a table, computer, and a 
chair. 
2. Space-based requirements which are requirements tied to the space itself regardless of the 
activities being performed. These requirements are often defined by law or company 
regulations. An example could be ventilation which is often defined by regulation. 
 
Figure 11 - Relationship between users, spaces, and design requirements (Kim, Kim, Cha, & Fischer, 2015) 
In Figure 11 the relationship between the user model and requirements model is shown. A user 
activity is comprised of a user group and an action. That user activity can then be tied to an activity-
==PAGE:31==
Page 21 of 83 
 
based requirement and must be tied to at least one space type. That space type then has a set of 
space-based requirements defined, and a super type with its own set of space-based requirements. 
In this way all requirements are directly or indirectly linked to a user activity and compared to the 
ontology presented in section 2.3 (Kim & Fischer, 2014b)  it embraces a broader set of activity-based 
requirements (Kim, Kim, Cha, & Fischer, 2015). 
The second part of the process is to actually update the requirements based on the activities of the 
users. The process of updating requirements is divided into the following phases: 
1. User information is updated by an architect. 
2. The requirements are updated for each room. 
3. The required number of each room is updated. 
Where phase 1 is of course manually performed while phase 2 and 3 are done automatically by the 
system.  
In phase 1 Kim et al.’s method is able to consider the following changes: 
• 
Add or remove a user group. 
• 
Change the number of people in a user group. 
• 
Add or remove an activity from a user group. 
• 
Change requirements of an activity. 
• 
Change the space type needed for an activity. 
In phase 2 the requirements for each room is found based on the given information. Each room has 
the possibility to have three different requirements defined from three sources: the super-type, the 
type, and the activities. If a space has two or more similar requirements from different sources the 
system chooses the more stringent one. A lab might have a super-type requirement of 10 l/s of 
ventilation but also have an activity-based requirement of 20 l/s because of fumes produced by the 
activity. In that case 20 l/s would be set as the requirement for the lab, as it is more stringent. This 
comparison is made for all space-based requirements of the space and all the requirements of the 
activities within the space, resulting in a set of requirements that should fulfil all needs of the space. 
Phase 3 consists of calculating the utilization of each space, finding the space type that is furthest 
from its target utilization, change the number of that space type, and then repeating until a 
satisfactory result is achieved. The way utilization is calculated is by finding the total number of 
activity events happening, find their total duration, dividing that duration out onto the possible 
spaces that can accommodate that type of activity, and then calculate the utilization of the space by 
dividing the total duration of activities in the space by the business hours of the space.  
==PAGE:32==
Page 22 of 83 
 
For example, take a school where you have 3 classes happening for 3 hours each. The school has 2 
classrooms that are available for 8 hours. The total duration of the classes is 9 hours (=3x3) and 
thereby the average load of each classroom is 4.5 hours (=9/2). This would lead to a utilization of the 
classrooms of 56% (=4.5/8). If we say the target utilization for classrooms is 50%, the number of 
classrooms should be increased, and the utilization recalculated. 
 
2.4.3 Validation of the process 
To validate the process Kim et al. used a hypothetical case based on observations of university 
buildings. First the process was tested by making changes to the user information in the test case 
and see if the method could adapt to all the changes that were made.  
Secondly, seven experienced architects were asked to perform the same generation of requirements 
without using the developed tool to see the amount of time they used and the amount of errors that 
might occur. The average time spent by the architects to update requirements when changes 
occurred was 27 minutes, which is a considerable amount of time, given the relatively small example 
case. The error rate of the architects were found by comparing their results to the result made by 
the automated system. There was an average of 36% errors. The architects were shown the 
recorded errors and agreed with the results of the automated system. 
 
 Measuring time use 
In the chapter “Changing Times: Home Life and Domestic Habit” (Hamill, 2011) of the book “The 
Connected Home: The Future of Domestic Life” Hamill discusses the difficulties of measuring 
people’s activities. Measuring people’s time can be done in multiple ways, says Hamill, either by 
interviewing people in the end of the day or by having them fill out diary-like forms. Hamill refers to 
a report done in London in 2005 measuring people’s activities. This report (HMSO, 2006) uses pre-
coded diaries, which lets people choose from 30 predefined activities. This pre-coded diary has been 
developed over many years prior and improved upon over time. The pre-coded diary uses 30 
predefined activities which are a reduction from around 250 activities previously recorded (Hamill, 
2011). The collected activity data is then averaged over all the people being studied to produce an 
average day for an average person. No one person is expected to experience this average day, but 
the average is still useful to assess how people spend their time (Hamill, 2011).

 Linked data 
Linked Data is about storing data in a way that is machine-readable and linked together with 
external datasets. This allows databases to be interconnected and thereby easily expandable by 
multiple users. The data should be stored in triples consisting of subject, predicate, and object (Bizer, 
Heath, & Berners-Lee, 2009).  
 
Figure 12 - Subject - Instrument - Object triad 
An example can be the Subject-Instrument-Object triad shown in Figure 12, where one say the 
subject is a cook, the instrument is a knife, and the object is cutting carrots. A linked data triple could

knife”. The strength of linked data then comes when the cook is part of something bigger, “The 
kitchen – employs – the cook”. In this way complex networks of data can be created, which are 
easily understood by a computer. It also enables a network to be connected to other external 
networks allowing easy expansion between multiple users.

4 Problem definition 
As mentioned in the problem statement (section 1.3), there is a clear gap in the generation of spatial 
requirements during the early stages of planning because of the limited methods of defining key 
information related to user spatial needs. 
Kim et al. first proposed a method to automatically analyze the space-use based on activities 
through their 2013 paper “A knowledge-based framework for automated space-use analysis”, but 
found that a database of user activities could prove useful in design analysis where information 
about users, user activities and spaces are often insufficient (Kim, Rajagopal, Fischer, & Kam, 2013). 
Throughout their research, Kim et al. also observe a lack of a formalised rationale behind 
requirements generation. They noted that there isn’t a way to systematically document why 
architects and designers choose certain solutions, most of their decisions being based on 
accumulated knowledge and experience.  
Kim et al. however do propose a method to automatically generate and update space use 
requirements based on user activity, but he does so by using hypothetical data and validating it 
through comparing its outcome to the requirements generated by architects of various degree of 
experience (Kim, Kim, Cha, & Fischer, 2015). 
Moreover, in the paper “A framework for evaluating user involvement methods in architectural, 
engineering, and construction projects”, Kim compares different methods of user involvement in the 
design process. He states that in order to facilitate data collection and analysis, virtual user models 
could prove the most efficient, but are still in their early stages of development and rely heavily on 
assumptions and simplifications (Kim, Cha, & Kim, 2016). 
But to create an accurate virtual user model in practice can prove difficult, as it must be created 
using real life data. When observing human behaviour, it is hard to define what exactly it is needed 
and how to then use the gathered data in a smart and practical way. For this, activity theory is a 
good example of a method that can potentially bridge the gap between the need of order in human 
centred data and the creation of virtual user models. Activity theory has been used extensively in 
studies relating human – computer interaction or information systems by creating a framework 
around complex notions such as activities (Kaptelinin, n.d.) (Crawford & Hasan, 2006).  
The analysis of the activities can be done using workplace planning concepts. Workplace planning 
looks at the utilization of space as a support to the core activities of organizations. It states that the 
size of a space is determined by the activities that partake there and their utilization, thus putting 
emphasis on the space’s effectiveness (Pennanen, 2004)

Looking at the analysed literature, a need for a standardized method to analyse activities as a 
measuring unit for generating spatial requirements can be clearly formed. This could be possible by 
synthesizing elements of the literature background.

5 Solution objectives

planning by attempting to interpolate the found data that could be applied to other similar settings 
by creating an empirical virtual user database defined by user activities. Based on the problem that

 Data collection

 Requirements generation  
•

• 
The process of generating requirements should be intuitive to the architect. 
 Results 
• 
The generated results should be realistic and applicable to real life design projects. 
• 
The generated results should be easy to understand and follow.

•

6.1.1.1 
Application of Activity Theory  
Activity theory is important in the context of this thesis because it can help define the concept of 
activities in an office. We developed Kim et al.’s space-user analysis based on the first triad (Figure 
19) described in section 2.1, in which the users take the role of the subject, the activity is the object 
and the space is the instrument.  
The instrument, or more precisely the space, in its definition in section 2.1 can be the enabler of an 
activity such that the activity cannot be performed if the is no designated space for it. For example, 
an employee has an office, so they can perform office work.  
           
 
Figure 19 – Activity theory as User - Space - Activity triad 
==PAGE:45==
Page 35 of 83 
 
This also helps put the activity in the perspective of the organization or department, through the 
other two triads (subject – rules – community and object – division of labour – community) 
explained in section 2.1. The process allows the owner of the future design to choose utilization of 
said design, the number of users and the opening hours, which from an activity theory point of view, 
could fall into the rules category as they impose how much and when the users will perform 
activities. The process looks at office environments, which in this context could be considered as the 
community, and the division of labour should explain which users perform certain activities, as users 
can have different positions throughout the community. 
Going further with Activity theory, Bakhurst explains the level of detail observed and defines the 
activity as the highest level in the hierarchy of activities. For us, this is not what we are looking for 
when observing and registering activities in the virtual user model, so we go a level below and look 
at what Bakhurst says is the action level. This is because the two bottom levels, the operation and 
action levels, can partly define the spatial requirements needed, for example a desk or table for 
taking notes or a specific space to have a meeting (Bakhurst, 2009). 
In an office context we can say that the three levels of hierarchy can take the following form: 
- 
The activity level: Successfully realizing a project 
- 
The action level: Having a meeting 
- 
The operation level: Taking notes 
On the other hand, Hashim & Jones say that the operation level is too superficial and does not 
explain why an activity is done and that the observations should be done at a higher level. 
“While observation and interviewing may reveal the explicit aspects of the 
participants’ actions, they will not assist in the understanding of implicit 
motivation of actions and operations. While it is not always possible for people to 
articulate what they do: “it is certainly very difficult to say how you type, or how 
you see the winning pattern on the  chessboard, or  how you know when  you have  
written a  sentence that communicates well” […], it is possible to gain some 
understanding of actions and objectives when they are executed at a higher level” 
(Hashim & Jones, 2007)

6.1.1.2 
Application of Workplace Planning 
Workplace planning is a process that calculates space use by applying production theories to the 
workplace. Pennanen theorizes that user activities determine the size of spaces, and the utilization 
of such spaces will determine how many there should be.  
The concept of functional sector is defined by a sum of activities that have the same independent 
goal, e.g. in a day care facility, the functional sector would be “day care for 1-2-year olds”. In our 
case, the functional sector that we are observing activities in, is office environment. In workplace 
planning, the sector function defines the bill of activities and sets activity drivers. Which in the 
process would be the function of office environments defines activities that take place in offices by 
people/users with different functions. 
Even though they are different methods of analysing space, the process takes the notions of target 
utilization from Pennanen’s work on Workplace Planning (Pennanen, 2004) and applies it to Kim et 
al. work on “Automated updating of space design requirements connecting user activities and space 
types” (Kim, Kim, Cha, & Fischer, 2015). 
How workplace planning differs from Kim et al.’s and our proposal is that workplace planning is 
highly dependent on stakeholders’ involvement and commitment to the process. The first two are 
intended to automate such processes, while our process is taking the idea even further to use this 
system in the early stages of design.  
Similar to activity theory, an activity in workplace planning is a set task that occurs, and it can be 
defined by the condition of achieving something on a higher level. Sub activities are tasks that form 
the activity. What sub activity means in workplace planning is an action in activity theory as it is 
meant to achieve something (e.g., cooking is a sub activity of catering).  
In workplace planning, sub activities hold all temporal and non-temporal information such as load – 
how many people are needed to do the sub activity, how long is the sub activity done, and how 
much space is needed to perform that sub activity. This information then ties in with the utilization 
factor to output the total space needed to perform that sub activity.  
The utilization is, in fact, the most interesting as it shows how much the space is used throughout 
the determined timeframe. Pennanen gives the example of an interviewing space from a police 
station that has a utilization of 50% which can be used 4 out of an 8-hour working day.

6.1.1.3 
Adapted ontology 1 
In this project the ontology from section 2.3 has been adapted to better fit with the way it’s being 
used in this thesis. When adapting the ontology, the five characteristics of user activities have been 
kept in mind to make sure that the ontology is still formal and comprehensive. To summarize they 
are as follows (Kim & Fischer, 2014b): 
C1: Some users have more stringent spatial requirements than the minimum requirements 
support. 
C2: Some activities require a designated space. E.g. a personal office. 
C3: Some activities require occupying an entire room. E.g. a meeting room. 
C4: Some activities require a specifically named space. E.g. a food lab. 
C5: Some activities are atypical activities, which are not conducted on a regular basis. 
Please note that in the original ontology by Kim et al. (Kim & Fischer, 2014b), the five characteristics 
were used to define six space-use type differentiators (SUTD) which helped define the ontology. 
These six SUTDs are not used here as we saw them as biased interpretations of the defined 
characteristics, since there is little explanation of them in the original paper. By following the SUTDs, 
the development of this adopted ontology would have been heavily limited. The five characteristics, 
however, were well supported and should be considered in this work. 
In Figure 20 one can see a UML-diagram of the adapted ontology in the same style as the original 
ontology to better keep them comparable. The adapted ontology is described in detail below, and 
the changes from Kim et al.’s ontology (section 2.3) are laid out after this.

Pilot 
The pilot-object defines the user-type in an activity which is the main driver of the activity or said in

teacher is the pilot even though students also partake in the activity. The number of teachers

•

•

• 
Activities (List of objects): A list of activities that this type of user is the driver of. 
==PAGE:49==

Activity 
An activity-object describes the activity being performed by a pilot. Additionally, an activity needs to 
have some kind of spatial or equipment requirement to be taken into consideration.  
• 
Name (String): The unique name of the activity. 
• 
GroupSize (Integer): The group size of pilots needed to perform the activity. 
• 
Duration (Float): The duration of the activity. 
• 
Instrument (Object): The instrument required to perform the activity. 
• 
Appointed (Boolean): Whether or not the activity requires a dedicated named instrument 
e.g. a personal office. If true, the instrument requirement generated for the activity is 
blocked for other activities. 
Activities can be either Typical or Atypical. An atypical activity is an activity that is not performed on 
a daily basis. For this reason, it is not considered when generating requirements but should still be 
considered when designing. 
A typical activity is an activity that is performed on a daily basis. It requires two additional fields: 
• 
Ratio (Float 0-1): The ratio of users who perform the activity on any given day. 
• 
Frequency (Float): The number of times the activity is performed by each user in a day. 
Instrument 
The instrument-object describes all the physical conditions that are needed to perform an activity. 
This includes everything from “a 4m by 4m silent and dark room” to “a desk with a computer” and 
whatever could be needed. The term “instrument” is borrowed from activity theory (Amstel, Zerjav, 
Hartmann, Voort, & Dewulf, 2015) 
• 
Name (String): The unique name of the instrument. 
• 
CCS Type (String): The CCS specified type (Molio, n.d.) that best describes the instrument. 
This can be left blank when it doesn’t make sense to have it. Defining the CCS-type enables 
one to define requirements on a higher level connected to the CCS-type; so-called space-
based requirements (See section 2.4). 
• 
MinArea (Float): The minimum required area of the instrument. 
• 
TimeOpen (Float): The duration each day that the instrument is available for use. 
• 
TargetUtilization (Float 0-1): The utilization factor which is wished for the instrument. 
Utilization is defined as the factor between how long the instrument is available and how 
long it is in use. 
==PAGE:50==
Page 40 of 83 
 
• 
Features (List of objects): A list of features that the instrument possesses. The features are 
really the defining traits of an instrument, as it is here the more specific demands would be 
defined. 
The word “instrument” is suitable since it doesn’t limit its use to only spaces but also equipment or 
parts of spaces. One could imagine instead of defining offices as individual spaces, one could define 
them as “office units” referring to the smallest unit an office can be broken into. This provides the 
benefit of flexibility when passing the requirements to an architect as the architect is not locked 
onto a certain design but just a requirement for the design. A number of units could be arranged in 
single offices or an open office. It’s left to the architect to decide. 
Feature 
The feature-object describes any feature that an instrument might have. This can be anything that 
helps to further define what the instrument is, e.g. “a chair” in a room, “fifteen chairs” in a room or 
more abstract concepts such as “privacy” for a meeting room etc. A feature is only defined by its 
name and is therefore very flexible in use but can also be hard to grasp. When defining the features 
of an instrument it is important to not overdo it, and only define features that are absolutely 
necessary for the instrument to support the activity. 
6.1.1.4 
Changes from the original ontology 
There are some key differences between this ontology and the ontology described in section 2.3, but 
since they are based on the same groundwork, they are functionally very similar. All changes to the 
adapted ontology has been designed to still fulfil all the five characteristics of user activities. The 
UML-diagrams of the both ontologies can be seen in Figure 21 below. Below that is a description of 
the major changes made.
 
Figure 21 - Left: The ontology made by Kim et al. (2014b)   Right: The adapted ontology 
==PAGE:51==
Page 41 of 83 
 
The action-object has been fused into the activity-object since an action would never exist without 
being tied to an activity anyway. This reduces complexity and avoids possible confusion over the 
difference between an action and an activity. The spaceCriteria-field of an action has also been 
removed since this is assumed to be defined in the instrument-object. 
The user-object has been changed to a pilot-object. This is mostly a name change, but it is done to 
avoid confusion. The name “pilot” indicates the main user who defines how many possible activities 
can go on at the same time.  
The important/regular trait has also been removed from the pilot-object. The first of the five 
characteristics clearly calls for a way to differentiate between users, but this is already done by the 
name of the user and the important/regular trait does therefore not add any new information to the 
object. 
Lastly the spatial requirement-object has been changed to an instrument-object. This is again a 
simplification where an instrument-object covers all the needs that an activity might have. In this 
way this ontology does not differentiate between whole room and equipment as the original one 
does. This change is possible since the adapted ontology is going to be used to generate 
requirements from activities whereas the original one was used to analyse existing spaces. 
Also, since the users are no longer split up into important or regular users the activity only needs 
one set of spatial requirements (instrument) defined instead of two as in the original ontology. 
6.1.1.5 
Generating requirements from activities 
When enough activity data has been collected, interpolated and stored in the ontology it can be 
used to generate requirements for future projects. The premise is that if one tracks the activities of 
many users (pilots) of similar types, one can interpolate that data to create an average of that type; 
a virtual user model. This virtual user model can then be used to predict what future, maybe 
unknown, users would need of a building. The process is heavily inspired by the one described by 
Kim et al. (Kim, Kim, Cha, & Fischer, 2015) although changed to fit to the adapted ontology and with 
a focus solely on activity-based requirements. This adapted process of generating requirements from 
virtual user models will be described here.  
==PAGE:52==
Page 42 of 83 
 
 
Figure 22 - Flow diagram of requirements generation 
In Figure 22 is shown the general outline of what the process looks like. In general, the process is 
based on seeing the instruments as containers for activities. To start with an oversized container is 
produced and then shrunk until the desired utilization is achieved.  
Input: Other than the virtual user model, the process needs some input from the architect before 
requirements can be generated. This includes: The number and types of pilots that is being designed 
for, the number of open hours of the building in general or each individual space type, and the 
target utilization of the instruments in general or each individual instrument type. The target 
utilization is seen as a maximum value that is not to be exceeded. 
Atypical activities: Since atypical activities are not considered in calculating space utilization they are 
directly sent to the output, so that the architect can take them into account in the design. 
Number of activities calculated: The number of typical activities in a day is calculated by using the 
number of pilots, the group size, ratio, and frequency. For each pilot type and for each activity of 
that pilot type, the number of pilots is multiplied by the frequency and ratio of the activity and 
divided by the group size of the activity. Written as an equation it looks like this:  
#𝑝𝑖𝑙𝑜𝑡𝑠 ∙ 𝑓 ⋅ 𝑟
𝑔𝑟𝑜𝑢𝑝𝑆𝑖𝑧𝑒= #𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑖𝑡𝑖𝑒𝑠  
This gives the number of times each activity occurs in an average day based on the virtual user 
model. 
Generate one instrument per activity: In this step, one instrument is generated for each activity. 
This is done to generate a list of instruments that is guaranteed to be able to accommodate all the 
activities, which is the starting point of the iterative process. 
Appointed instruments: Instruments generated from an activity, that demands its instrument to be 
appointed, are sent directly to the output, as they cannot be reduced further without breaking the 
appointment.  
==PAGE:53==
Page 43 of 83 
 
Reducing the number of instruments: Lastly the process enters an iterative process where 
instruments are removed one by one as long as the target utilization is not surpassed. This process is 
done for each instrument type. The utilization is calculated by taking the total duration for all 
activities using the same instrument type and dividing it with the total number of hours that 
instrument type is available. Written as an equation it looks like this: 
∑(#𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑖𝑡𝑖𝑒𝑠 ∙𝑑𝑢𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛)
#𝑖𝑛𝑠𝑡𝑟𝑢𝑚𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑠 ∙𝑜𝑝𝑒𝑛 ℎ𝑜𝑢𝑟𝑠= 𝑢𝑡𝑖𝑙𝑖𝑧𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛𝐹𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑜𝑟 
If removing an instrument doesn’t result in the utilization factor surpassing the target utilization the 
instrument is removed.  
This method guarantees one of two outcomes:  
1. Either the utilization factor is at least half of the target utilization, or  
2. There is only one instrument of that type left.  
This is evident if one looks at the last iteration, an instrument can take: going from two to one 
instrument. This would double the calculated utilization factor, since the number of instruments is 
halved. If doubling the utilization factor is not possible without surpassing the target, then the 
utilization factor must be higher than half of the target. Since going from two to one instrument is 
the biggest relative step it should be evident that this is true for all other cases.  
Of course, it’s not possible to remove the last instance of an instrument since that would make the 
utilization factor go towards infinity.  
Merging underutilized instruments: When the number of instruments has been reduced as much as 
possible similar instruments will attempted to be merged if they are still underutilized. Finding 
instruments that are able to be merged is done by comparing their features and sizes. If a larger 
instrument has the same features as a smaller one it will inherit the activities of the smaller 
instrument. An example of this could be an underutilized small meeting room that is then merged 
into a medium sized meeting rooms which can easily accommodate the activities of the small one.  
When all instruments are either close enough to their target utilization or no more merges are 
possible, the instruments are sent to the output together with their calculated utilization factor. 
Output: The output is divided into three parts:  
1. Atypical activities and their required instruments. 
2. Appointed instruments, that cannot be reduced. 
3. Optimized list of instruments and their utilization factors.

6.1.3 Evaluation 1 – findings from pilot test 
6.1.3.1 
Data collection 
The first very apparent issue with our data collection was that each subject seemed to interpret the 
form in their own way, giving us slightly different types of data from each. Luckily most of it was 
understandable and could be rewritten to fit the correct format. When collecting more data, the 
form should be made much clearer to reduce difference in interpretation. Additionally, it might be 
better to give the subjects a list of common activities to choose from to reduce variation. 
To reduce the different number of similar Activities such as “Working in Revit”, “Sending emails”, 
and “Writing” they would be grouped together and renamed to be simply “Computer work” since 
they all had the same requirements – a computer – and are in nature very similar. Meetings were 
also hard to compare and would greatly benefit from being simplified into a few predefined 
categories. Here Neufert Architects’ Data (Neufert, 1991) and Planning Office Spaces (Van Meel, 
2010) were used to come up with three different types of meetings based on typical meeting room 
sizes: Small for 2-4 people, medium for 5-8, and large for 8-12. In this way all meetings could easily 
be reduced to only three types. Meetings larger than 12 people would be handled in a similar way 
when they should arise. 
6.1.3.2 
Adapted ontology and Generation of requirements 
The ontology and requirements generation is evaluated together here as they go hand in hand. 
Changes to one lead to changes in the other. Some major and minor flaws were found which should 
be addressed in future iterations. 
==PAGE:57==
Page 47 of 83 
 
The first issue that became evident when using the ontology and requirements generation was that 
the use case of the these were not well defined. This was mostly a symptom of being developed 
from theory, and it showed that what you think you need, might not be the same as what you need. 
The pilot test forced us to think about who would use this, why would they use it, and how would 
they use it. In future iterations the use case should be much clearer.  
When defining pilot types and doing calculations on their needs it quickly proved to be more 
complex than anticipated. The way one interprets the collected data would add a significant bias to 
how the virtual user model would end up like. The transformation of data from the form to the 
ontology should be clearer and less prone to interpretation. Additionally, the grouping and 
separation of different pilots made the generation of requirements much more troublesome than 
expected. What happens when two different types of pilots attend the same activity? Either you end 
up with a list of wrong requirements or the calculation becomes too complex to use in general cases.  
When taking the data collected with the forms and trying to put it into the ontology some values 
turned out to be very difficult to extract. Namely the “ratio” that is defined for a typical activity was 
very difficult to extract from the data. This test gave valuable insight in what to actually expect from 
the data that can be collected. 
The definition and grouping of activities were also not clear enough. The generation of requirements 
is split into three sections for, typical, atypical, and appointed activities, but the ontology does not 
reflect that. There should be a stronger connection between how the ontology is structured and how 
it is used. 
The last step of the requirements generation is merging instruments that are similar and 
underutilized. This however proved to be much more complex than anticipated. In the example 
when doing it manually it appeared easy but trying to generalize it for automation was another 
story. To a human it’s easy to see that a small and medium meeting room might be merged when 
both are underutilized but to a computer it isn’t as obvious. Whether or not merging instruments is 
possible within the scope of this work should be considered. In Kim et al.’s (2015) process of 
generating requirements this problem was solved by manually defining alternative spaces 
(instruments in our case) to each activity, so that the automated system knew which activities could 
be moved to other spaces and which spaces to move to.

6.2.1 Design and Development 2 
6.2.1.1 
Changes to ontology  
Based on the pilot test a lot of changes were made to the ontology. 
 
Figure 24 - Left: Ontology before testing. Right: Ontology after testing. 
In Figure 24 one can see the difference between how the ontology looked before and after the pilot 
test. The biggest changes are: 
• 
Removed “pilot number”, “target util.” and “time open”. They are not needed in the 
ontology but in the calculation. These numbers are expected to change whenever the 
process is used to generate requirements. Therefore, it doesn’t make sense for them to 
be in the ontology. 
• 
“Ratio” and “frequency” are combined to one value. It is very difficult to separate them 
when analysing collected data, and only the product of them is needed in the 
calculation. Before “ratio” referred to the number of users who performed this activity 
==PAGE:59==
Page 49 of 83 
 
and “frequency” referred to the number of times those users would perform the activity 
per day. In the new ontology “frequency” refers to the number of times the activity is 
performed per day per user on average. 
• 
“Appointed” is changed to a subclass of “typical” activities requiring a “ratio” of the 
users who do this activity. Additionally, “not appointed” is also a subclass of “typical” 
moving “frequency” and “duration” to it. This change makes the calculation much more 
straightforward as the process can separated for atypical, not appointed and appointed 
activities.  
• 
“Features” changed to “description” since instrument-merging is not done. “Features” 
were meant to be computer-readable, so that instruments that were underutilized could 
be merged with similar instruments based on their features. E.g. a small meeting room 
being absorbed by a medium meeting room if the utilization allows it. This merging-step 
was scrapped as it proofed to be very difficult to formalize. A human might find the task 
easy, but for a computer it quickly becomes very complex, and it was deemed out of 
scope of this project. 
• 
“Pilot” changed to “user group” as it is no longer seen as the driver of activities but 
merely potential participants. In some cases, they can still be seen as drivers e.g. 
teachers driving classes. This change was made as it was difficult to separate users into 
different categories and made data interpolation much more complex. This does lead to 
loss of some information, but it was assumed that an architect using the process would 
not have access to detailed information about user-composition anyway. Additionally, 
the calculation would become weird when two different pilots where doing an activity 
together e.g. a meeting between architects and engineers.

The first choice is whether the activity is Typical or Atypical. An atypical activity is an activity that 
doesn’t occur on a daily basis and therefore doesn’t affect space utilization. It’s still important to 
include as it should still be considered when designing. An example could be a monthly conference.  
A typical activity is an activity that do occur on a daily basis. The Typical class is also abstract and 
should be defined as one of its sub-classes: Appointed or Not Appointed. An appointed activity is an 
activity that demands a dedicated space, that can’t be used by other activities. E.g. office work using 
a personal office. An appointed activity needs the following additional trait: 
• 
Ratio (Float 0-1): The ratio of users in the user group that is doing this activity and thereby 
needs an appointed instrument. 
A Not Appointed activity is an activity that doesn’t need an appointed space and thereby can share 
an instrument with other activities. E.g. a meeting using a common meeting room. A Not Appointed 
activity needs the following two additional traits: 
• 
Duration (Positive Float): The duration of the activity on average. This may be defined in any 
time unit as long as it is consistent for the whole model. 
• 
Frequency (Positive Float): The average number of times this activity occurs in a day. 
To summarize: An activity is either Typical or Atypical, and if it’s Typical it is either Appointed or Not 
Appointed.  
Instrument 
The instrument-object describes all the physical conditions that is needed to perform an activity. 
This includes everything from “a 4x4m silent and dark room” to “a desk with a computer” and 
whatever else could be needed. The term “instrument” is borrowed from activity theory (Amstel, 
Zerjav, Hartmann, Voort, & Dewulf, 2015). 
• 
Name (String): The unique name of the instrument. 
• 
CCS Type (String): The CCS specified type (Molio, n.d.) that best describes the instrument. 
This can be left blank when it doesn’t make sense to have it. Defining the CCS-type enables 
one to define requirements on a higher level connected to the CCS-type; so-called space-
based requirements (See section 2.3). 
• 
MinArea (Positive Float): The minimum required area of the instrument. 
• 
Description (String): A detailed description of the instrument that can be passed on to an 
architect using the model.

6.2.1.3 
Changes to requirements generation 
With the changes to the ontology the generation of requirements could be simplified. The new 
method is similar to the old one, but with one minor difference: Instead of being iterative, it could 
be changed to be algebraic – being directly calculable. In short, the process would make an 
overestimated guess and then improve the guess over a number of iterations, but now the 
calculation is done via a simple equation. This makes it possible to implement the calculation in a 
simple worksheet instead of needing custom coding. 
With the changes made to the ontology and method of calculations a result was found for the 
hypothetical case we had made. The resulting requirements seemed very reasonable but could not 
at this time be verified by any other means than our experience in the field. It did however proof 
that the calculation is possible, and that the outputted results are somewhat reasonable. 
Since the virtual user model is also based on only seven days’ worth of data the resulting 
requirements would not be expected to be of much value, but the test of the method was. 
As mentioned in the previous section merging of similar underutilized instruments was removed 
from the generation of requirements. It simply proved to be too complex to formalize in a good way. 
To a human it sounds simple but when you want to automate it, it becomes very difficult. Instead 
the resulting instruments are outputted together with their utilization factor, and the architect can 
than decide to merge rooms if possible. Kim et al.’s (2015) didn’t have this merging-step since they 
manually defined alternative spaces for each activity, e.g. a small meeting being in a medium 
meeting room. Since their way of doing it is also manual, we chose to let the decision fall on the 
architect to merge underutilized spaces, since Kim et al.’s way is more labour intensive and might 
introduce unwanted bias. 
6.2.1.4 
Generating requirements from activities 
When enough activity data has been collected, interpolated and stored in the ontology (See section 
6.2.1.2) can be used to generate requirements for future projects. The premise is that if one tracks 
the activities of many users of similar types, one can interpolate that data to create an average of 
that type: a virtual user model. This virtual user model can then be used to predict what future, 
maybe unknown, users would need of a building. This process of generating requirements from 
virtual user models will be described here.

6.2.1.5

Simulated data is in principle a substitute for real life data created using a stochastic process 
described by specific algorithms. Such data is used to analyse systems in which real life data is very 
difficult to obtain in large quantities.  
As mentioned in the section above, the pilot test has been performed with a limited dataset, which 
indeed helped improved the development of the method, as well as identify issues regarding the 
type of data that is required.  
The concern here is whether the limited dataset would be enough to support the initial phase of 
method development and whether a larger set of information would still prove the solidness of the 
process.

assumptions are based on observation of the activities and the frequency of their occurrence. 
It is very important to take in account that the simulated dataset is created based on assumptions as 
it may not give a clear reflexion of real life, but it does help estimate results of larger information 
sets.

(Workplace Denmark, 2019).

and because it should be able to round up the remaining time while no other activity is occurring. 
To be able to carry out the spatial requirements calculation for each space, a specific weight is 
attributed to the activities based on assumptions of their occurrence. Such as lunch is assumed that 
it takes place every workday, once a day, its weight is set to 1. Any other activity is assumed that it 
does not require to happen every day, which can show for example a day filled with meetings and no 
office work.  
Office work is the highest occurring activity as it is, as said above, the core activity of an office 
building. Following are the meeting that occur depending on the size of the meeting. Overall it is 
difficult to estimate the likelihood of meetings since it varies based on factors such as position, 
available space or technology, etc., but it has been observed that meetings take about a third of the 
time (Doodle, 2019) and that their frequency increases yearly (Mørch, 2017). 
6.2.1.6 
Data simulation algorithm 
A group is defined as a list of activities where the sum of the durations of the activities totals to 480 
minutes, which is equivalent to a full working day. An error margin corresponding to 5% of a full 
working day is accepted for any given group. 
An activity is defined as an object with the following properties: 
- 
Group ID 
- 
Activity Name 
- 
Group Size 
- 
Duration 
- 
Appointed 
==PAGE:67==
Page 57 of 83 
 
- 
Room Type 
Group ID is a randomly generated unique identifier that is shared among all activities in a group. 
A stochastic function 𝑓(𝑡) is used to generate values for all remaining properties, where 𝑡  is a 
discrete stochastic space. 
There exists a set 𝑆 of activity types, where each activity type holds predefined stochastic spaces for 
any property that uses the stochastic function 𝑓(𝑡). 
Each activity type also holds a weight 𝑤 where 0 ≤𝑤< 1 (see Table 3). 
The procedure of data generation follows this set of rules: 
- 
A new empty group 𝑔 is created. 
- 
A random number 𝑛 is generated, where 0 ≤𝑛< 1. 
- 
A subset 𝑠 of 𝑆 is created, where 𝑠= {𝑎𝑠: ∀ 𝑤𝑒𝑖𝑔ℎ𝑡 𝑤 𝑜𝑓 𝑎𝑠→𝑤> 𝑛} . 
- 
The group 𝑔 is populated by picking random elements of 𝑠 to create activities using the 
stochastic function 𝑓(𝑡) to generate values for the activities. 
- 
The procedure is repeated until the definition of a group is satisfied. 
That is, a group contains activities where the sum of the durations of the activities totals to 
480 minutes, ± 5%. 
The weight of an activity type determines the probability of that type being present in 𝑠, and as a 
consequence, being present in a group. 
For example, the activity type “Conference” holds a weight of 0.1, and is therefore less likely to be 
present in 𝑠. 
On the contrary, the activity type “Lunch” holds a weight of 1, and is therefore guaranteed to be 
present in 𝑠. 
Additionally, in the specific case of an activity type “Lunch”, the element is effectively removed from 
𝑠 after being used for creation, hence it will be present in any group exactly one time.

Activity theory in data collection 
In a more practical sense, Crawford & Hasan’s work on “Demonstrations of the Activity Theory

An optimal work environment can enhance the productivity and wellbeing of the users. Job

The tools that are of most interest are the spaces themselves. They can be categorized as 
primary tools. The spaces themselves are chosen based on certain (proximity?) rules (e.g., a

6.3.1.2 
Measuring time use 
In the first iteration two issues were identified with the collection of users’ activity data. The first 
was that users would fill out the diary-form in different ways, complicating the analysis of the data. 
The second issue was that grouping users into meaningful groups to define pilot-objects around 
them proved difficult. To address both of these problems, inspiration was taken from studies in 
London measuring people’s time use (Hamill, 2011). In these studies people weren’t grouped in any 
way, but where instead all averaged together to give one unified model of how people spend their 
time. In this iteration a similar approach is tested, to see if a simplification of user groups can lead to 
a less bias-prone and more usable method. Groups can of course still be defined based on separate 
datasets but preferably not within a single dataset. Regarding collection of data the studies 
addressed the issue of having too many different activities by using pre-coded diaries that provided 
30 pre-defined activities to choose from. Even though 30 different activities might still be a lot, it’s 
less than the 250 that the studies originally dealt with (Hamill, 2011).

7 Discussion 
This thesis has worked with the problem of automatically generating spatial requirements based on 
empirical user activity data, to make the early building design stages easier. This problem and the 
objectives to solve it, was developed using literature on Activity Theory, Workplace Planning, and 
the work of Kim et al., whom has worked on similar problems.  
To solve this problem a process of generating spatial requirements was developed, which consist of 
three parts: Collecting user activity data, analysing the collected data, and using the data to generate 
spatial requirements. This solution was heavily based on the work by Kim et al., which formed a 
starting point for this work. In their work Kim et al. proposes a process of automatically updating 
spatial requirements based on changes in user activity data, but this process is never tested on real 
life cases. The proposed process in this thesis is a continuation of Kim et al.’s process but literature 
on Activity Theory and Workplace Planning has been used to make the process more scientifically 
tight removing unwanted bias from Kim et al.’s work.  
Through use of an iterative Design Science Research Method (DSRM) the adapted process has been 
tested to reveal shortcomings in the solution, which has then been updated to provide an even 
better solution. The use of DSRM meant that the problem definition and objectives were very clear 
through the background literature studied and this allowed demonstrations and evaluations to 
greatly improve the solution.  
When using an iterative design method such as DSRM one should expect to run into issues, as these 
issues are what allows the solution to be improved. In this project the first major problem that was 
encountered was that the format of the collected data in iteration 1 (section 6.1) did not match the 
theoretical format of the hypothetical data that had been used to do preliminary tests of the analysis 
and generation process. This problem was addressed, and by use of information from literature, the 
process was updated to take the found issues into account. Another not as crucial issue was that 
collecting data was more difficult than anticipated, and the data collection form needed to be much 
clearer to collect usable data. As said before, these issues were only welcome as they provided the 
possibility to improve and were handled in the evaluation part of the iterations.  
Some of the limitations of this project was the lack of data collection that took place, and the lack of 
confirmation of the automatically generated requirements. The relatively small amount of data 
collected in iteration 1 was sufficient to do a pilot test of the proposed solution, but it lacked the 
scale to prove the solution usable in a real case study. This problem should be solved in iteration 3 
which has yet to be conducted. The same goes for the lack of confirmation of the generated 
==PAGE:74==
Page 64 of 83 
 
requirements as there were no real case to compare to. This would also be done in iteration 3 
(section 6.3). 
Some features of the solution unfortunately had to be scrapped due to their unforeseen complexity. 
An example is the merging of underutilized spaces generated by the process. This proved to be a lot 
harder to formalize and automated than anticipated, as the task of merging seems easy when you 
think about it but is very difficult when carried out. 
Despite limitations the work done in this thesis is a good step towards improving the early stages of 
the design process by providing a process to scientifically generate spatial requirements, thus 
reducing the dependency on architects’ personal experience, rules of thumb, and unrealistic wishes 
from clients. Applied in a design process, this solution could help produce more accurate design 
proposals earlier, easier, and with less hassle. Furthermore, if connected to a requirements model 
which in turn is connected to building information models, changes in user information can more 
easily be accounted for resulting in less discrepancies between what the building user needs and 
what they get.

8 Conclusion 
Activity based spatial requirements are often difficult to develop as they highly depend on 
architects’ personal experience and accumulated knowledge. Even more so, creating such 
requirements usually means collecting a considerable amount of data, which then must be 
processed into usable information. This can prove difficult since activity data is often collected 
through surveys and interviews and are prone to be inaccurate and biased.  
Methods have also been developed to automatically generate and adapt to new requirements but 
are still in their infancy and have not been tested with real life data.  
Some requirement generation methods have been studied by Kim et al. but lack a generalized 
dataset that overviews typical user activities. This paper takes Kim et al.’s research and expands on 
the idea of generating activity-based requirements and adds notions from activity theory and 
workplace planning. This has been done through an iterative process that incorporates both data 
collection and requirements calculation using information from both real-life subjects and 
simulations. A next step is then prepared to the process will be creating a formalized data gathering 
method and using real life data from a larger pool of information. 
Even though further research needs to be done before it can be concluded that this is a valid process 
in itself, the thesis shows that, when enough information is gathered, a convincing list of 
requirements can be generated. This process is, however, just a proof of concept and it requires 
further work to validate its use in real life situations.  
 The results then can be used in the early stages of design to give a more compelling list of 
requirements alongside rules and regulations. This is important since they help create a better 
product without hindering the creative process of the architect.

9 Further work 
With the advancement in technology, it is clear that the present research has the potential to 
achieve more reliable results and a better workflow. The following sections explore some of the 
possibilities in which this research could head towards. 
 
 Linked Data 
The kind of data that would be represented in the ontology is ideal to arrange using Linked Data.  
In our case “User – is doing – Activity” or “Activity – has requirement – Instrument”. A more specific 
example could be “Kitchen staff – is doing – Cleaning”. But when looking further than just the 
ontology one could imagine user groups being linked to “departments” which is then linked further, 
building a web of data that can describe very complex hierarchies of information.  
This also ties in well with the decision to specify the CCS type for instruments, as it makes it easier to 
connect them with space-based requirements that are independent of the activities being 
performed. Additionally, it should make it easier to connect the instruments to a BIM-model that 
uses such terms. 
In the scope of this thesis Linked Data doesn’t add much as the datasets are quite small, but it allows 
the Virtual User Model to be expanded more easily and to connect to other building information in a 
more intelligent manner.  
 
 Future data collection 
Data collection process can be optimized through various methods including smart sensors, Wi-Fi 
information tracking, Bluetooth beacons data, and innovative scheduling assistants. 
The interview with DTU Campus Service has revealed the department’s intentions of creating 
cognitive buildings, where user data can be readily available with low to no user participation in the 
data gathering process (A. Møller & B. Carlsen, Personal interview, June 21st, 2019). This project is 
tested with smart sensors and Wi-Fi information tracking. The sensors are at the moment 
implemented in meeting rooms and are capable to track the number of people in the room and 
whether they are sitting or standing. Such sensors are tested out in larger spaces but are not able to 
generate reliable data due to their low range.  
==PAGE:77==
Page 67 of 83 
 
Wi-Fi information tracking has been used during the DSE fair to record student traffic through the 
DTU premises. They can track the number of unique individuals, whether they have been there 
before, their route through the fair, and the duration of their stay. This type of information, 
however, is available only if the users are connected to the Wi-Fi network through their mobile 
devices (Savnik, 2016). 
Other such sensors are implemented in the DTU library that generate heat maps to better explain 
student behaviour and use of the library’s facilities (DTU Library, n.d.). 
 
Figure 27 - DTU Library activity heat map (DTU Library, n.d.) 
DTU Campus Service designs such spaces based on the concept of Living Laboratories, in which all 
data gathered is used to inspire the creation of new, innovative products and services (DTU 
Smartcampus, n.d.). 
Other technologies such as Bluetooth beacons are proposed by Kim et al. for activity detection and 
recognition. They base their research on human activity recognition studies that use physical sensing 
technology, such as smart phones and wearable sensors, to detect both individual and group 
activities (Chen, Cha, & Kim, 2019). 
Bluetooth beacons are real – time locating systems that transmit a signal that is then received by 
nearby smart sensors. The signal is then processed, and a rough location is estimated through 
received signal strength indicator (RSSI) that reads the power value received at the sensor. Beacons 
are able to be placed in larger areas given the floorplan (Proximi.io, n.d.) 
==PAGE:78==
Page 68 of 83 
 
Through the interview with DTU Campus Service, we learnt that data can also be collected through 
scheduling assistants connected directly to users’ Outlook calendars (A. Møller & B. Carlsen, 
Personal interview, June 21st, 2019). This is relevant for tracking and managing meetings, as well as 
displaying information about meeting room use, how many people attend, the meeting duration, 
etc. 
Using technology to register activities is also supported by Kim et al.’s work as a more reliable means 
of gathering objective data. They say that self-reported behaviour may be biased and adjusted due 
to knowing that they are observed. As a future improvement of the process, technologies such as 
the ones mentioned above should be considered to better reflect human behaviour and activities 
(Chen, Cha, & Kim, 2019). 
It is interesting to see how such data gathering methods will affect the work presented in this thesis 
and how they can influence future design. 
 
 Merging spaces 
The development of the current process still needs to be improved since, at the moment, the results 
only show all possible used spaces even if their utilization degree is suboptimal. This is because, 
when registering information, there could be instances in which two similar activities could be 
performed in different rooms, making the program output more space than required. 
In the first iteration, we found that for a test calculation for 20 people, there should be one medium 
meeting room and one large meeting room. But because our test data showed that there were few 
activities occurring in those rooms, their utilization was calculated at 11% and 3%, which were far 
from the target of 50% that was defined. 
A future step in the process could be automating the process of merging similar underutilized spaces 
in the resulting requirements. E.g. a medium sized meeting room being merged with a large meeting 
room, resulting in a large meeting room with a higher utilization factor. 
 
 Connecting to Building Information Models 
In the future it would be ideal to configure the output of the developed process to fit into other 
building information models. Such a model could be the one described by Kiviniemi (2005), where a 
requirements model is connected to the other models of the design process. This would take the 
==PAGE:79==
Page 69 of 83 
 
developed process of this thesis to a more practical level where it can more easily be applied in real 
life design processes.  
 Regulation-based requirements 
This thesis focused mostly on activity-based requirements, looking at only the requirements of 
spaces that where directly connected to the activities being conducted. A future feature could be 
including requirements that are defined by other external sources such as law and regulations. Such 
a feature would allow to create more accurate requirements that takes more building actors into 
account, thereby leading to better design solutions in the early stages of designing.
