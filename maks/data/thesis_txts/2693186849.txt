Abstract
Microplastic (MP) are small plastic pieces (1-1000 µm) and they are ubiquitous throughout
the environment. MP is a growing environmental concern due to their persistence and poten-
tial harmful eects. They accumulate in the sediments in the marine environment, and here
organisms are exposed to the increasing MP concentration. This thesis explores the potential
of accumulation of MP in benthic organisms focusing on deposit-feeding organisms. From the
literature review presented, it is shown that there is a lack of knowledge on MP accumulation
in deposit-feeding organisms. 28 studies were found investigating the potential accumulation
of MP in benthic organisms and 21 of these showed low or no potential for bioaccumulation
of MP particles. Yet only 3 of these were on deposit-feeding organisms, ans none were on
deposit-feeding bivalves.
To study this, an exposure experiment was performed with the deposit-feeding bivalve Nu-
cula spp. exposed to MP of varying polymer type, size and shape. To analyse the bivalve
tissue for MP particles, a protocol of pre-treatment with enzymatic digestion and analysis of
MP with the Laser Direct Infrared (LDIR) spectroscopy were developed. The negative and
positive controls of this protocol showed a large amount of non-plastic particles still present
in the sample and identied by the LDIR. Furthermore the LDIR analysis showed, clusters of
particles as one particle, and there were a large amount of larger sediment particles like sand
and quartz as well.
It was therefore decided to optimise the pre-treatment and analytical methods further and
hence was the analysis of the bivalves from the exposure study not carried out as part of this
thesis. The pre-treatments presented in the thesis are enzymatic digestion with 2.5 % Alcalase,
chemical digestion with the oxidation agents 7 % or 10 % sodium hypochlorite (NaClO) and 3
% potassium hydroxide (KOH) and density separation with a 1.69 g/cm3 zinc chloride (ZnCl2)
solution. The LDIR was the main instrument to determine MP quantication and identica-
tion. For comparison, the samples were also analysed on optical and uorescent microscopy
and µ-Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy. The results of these analyses showed
a further need for improvement of the pre-treatments as the total particle numbers analysed
by the LDIR in the samples of bivalve tissue still were too high to conduct accurate analysis
on either of the analytical instruments. The results show varying recovery rates of the MP
types from 0 % to 55,800 %. The results of the positive and negatives controls are presented
in the thesis mainly to show that all of the MP types used in the exposure experiment are
possible to recover from the bivalve tissue in the LDIR analysis.
Several steps to improve the methods are presented in this thesis. For the digestion the com-
bination of enzymatic and chemical digestion is suggested and for the density separation the
adding of oil as well as a longer settling time of minimum 5 hours is recommended. Finally,
it is concluded that the LDIR showed potential for MP analysis of deposit-feeding bivalve
tissue, but an improved pre-treatment as well as more experience operating the instrument is
needed.

Sammenfatning
Dansk titel: Optag af mikroplastik i sediment-levende muslinger
Mikroplastik (MP) er små plastik partikler (1-1000 µm), som er til stede i miljøet over hele
verden. Der er en stigende bekymring for hvordan og hvad MPs spredning i miljøet har af
betydning for dyr og mennesker. Flere studier har påvist, at MP akkumulerer sig i havsedi-
menter og her er dyr og organismer derfor i stigende grad eksponeret for MP. Dette speciale
afdækker den mulige bioakkumulering af MP i bundlevende dyr og organismer i et litter-
aturstudie. Litteraturstudiet viser, at der er mangel på viden om bioakkumulering af MP i
sediment-levende dyr og organismer. 28 studier blev gennemgået og af dem viser 21 studier
lav eller intet potentiale for bioakkumulering af MP i bundlevende dyr og organismer. Der er
dog kun tre af 28 studier, der er udført med sediment-levende dyr og organismer og ingen af
dem var på sediment-levende muslinger.
For at øge videnen om MP i sediment-levende muslinger, blev der i specialet gennemført et
eksponeringsforsøg, hvor sediment-levende nøddemuslinger, Nucula spp., blev udsat for MP
af forskellig polymertype, størrelse og form. For at analysere MP partiklerne blev en enzyma-
tisk oplukning af muslingevævet udført. Da det oplukkede væv blev analyseret for MP med
Laser Direct Infrared (LDIR) spektroskopi viste resultaterne for mange ikke-MP partikler til
at kunne bruges til analyse af muslingerne fra eksponeringsforsøget. Der blev både fundet
grupperinger af partikler, der blev identiceret som én partikel af LDIR'en, og der var sam-
tidig mange store sandpartikler tilbage i prøverne.
Det blev derfor besluttet at arbejde på at optimere disse analysemetoder, og derfor er muslingerne
fra eksponeringsforsøget ikke analyseret i dette speciale. I stedet bliver her præsenteret resul-
tater fra yderligere optimering af oplukningsmetode og MP analyse metoden. De to oplukn-
ingsmetoder undersøgt er henholdsvis med enzymer (2.5 % Alcalase) og oxidanter (7 % eller
10 % natrium hypoklorit (NaClO) og 3 % kaliumhydroxid (KOH)). Til sidstnævnte oplukn-
ingsmetode blev en densitetsseparation med en zinkklorid-saltopløsning (ZnCl2) på 1.69 g/cm3
tilført. LDIR'en var det primære analyse instrument til identicering og kvanticering af MP,
men for at sammenligne resultaterne fra LDIR analyserne blev prøverne også forsøgt analy-
seret med optisk mikroskop, uorescerende mikroskop og µ-Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR)
spektroskopi. Resultaterne af analyserne viser, at der er brug for yderligere udvikling af især
opluknings- og densitetsseparationsmetoden, da antallet af ikke-MP partikler i prøverne fort-
sat var alt for højt. REsultaterne viste gennding af MP partikler fra 0-55.800 % grundet de
upræcise analyser. Resultaterne er alligevel medtaget i dette speciale, primært for at vise, at
det var muligt at gennde alle de tilføjede MP typer i resultaterne fra LDIR'en.
I specialet foreslås en række forbedringer til metoderne, deriblandt en undersøgelse af en
opluknings-kombination af enzymer og oxidanter. For densitetsseparationen foreslås der blandt
andet at tilsætte olie, for at minimere partikeltab, og at øge tiden til minimum 5 timer, hvor i
prøven og saltopløsningen sætter sig efter blanding. Endeligt kan det konkluderes, at LDIR'en
viser potentiale for MP analyse fra sediment-levende muslinge-væv, men at det kræver mere
kendskab til maskinen og prøver med få ikke-MP partikler i for at udnytte analyserne opti-
malt. Derfor viser specialet, at forbedringer af opluknings- og densitetsseparationsmetoderne
nødvendige.

Background and aims
Plastic has been used in various industries since the beginning of the 20th century (Boucher
and Friot, 2017). As a result, plastic production has increased exponentially since the 1950's;
in 1955, the global plastic production was 16 million tonnes, and in 2015 this number had
risen to 7.82 billion tonnes (Jambeck et al., 2015). The amount of plastic reaching the marine
environment is dicult to measure, yet Jambeck et al. (2015) estimated that 1.7  4.7 % of
the plastic produced in the worlds coastal countries reached the marine environment in 2010.
Some of the most valued qualities of plastics, like persistence, bio-inert coating, and exibility,
makes plastic waste in nature a growing concern due to negative eects on organisms and
potentially humans (Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2015). Furthermore, plastic can remain in the
environment for hundreds of years, slowly fragmenting and decomposing (Lassen et al., 2015).
Microplastic (MP) can be dened as plastic pieces from 1 µm to 1 mm in size, or in other
denitions up to 5 mm(Hartmann et al., 2019). In 2015, 5 % of the plastic produced was
MP and even more MP is formed from fragmentation and weathering of plastic waste (Geyer
et al., 2017). From agriculture elds in North America (Zubris and Richards, 2005) to deep-sea
sediments in Antarctica (Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2013), MP have been found in almost all
ecosystems (GESAMP, 2015). Evidence suggests that the majority of MP entering the oceans
accumulate in the marine sediments (Harris, 2020), and concentrations have been found vary-
ing from <1 particles kg−1 dry weight in deep-sea sediments (Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2013)
to 106,745 particles kg−1 dry weight sediment reported in Oslo fjord, Norway (Singdahl-Larsen,
2019). In inner Danish waters, concentrations varying from 202 to 3511 particles kg−1 dry
weight sediment have been reported (Strand et al., 2019). MPs are in the same size range as
plankton, making them ingestible for several suspension- and deposit-feeding aquatic organ-
isms, e.g., amphipods, copepods and bivalves (Lassen et al., 2015). Uptake has been shown
to be the main route for MP to enter the benthic biota (Pinheiro et al., 2020). Laboratory
studies have shown various negative eects from MP ingestion, though it has been challenging
to identify eects from environmentally realistic concentrations (Lenz et al., 2016). Likewise,
the accumulation and trophic transfer of MP is dicult to determine, and there are few studies
on bioaccumulation of MP (Carbery et al., 2018).
The main objective of this thesis is to assess the possible uptake and accumulation of MP in
deposit-feeding bivalves. This is achieved by the following sub-objectives:
 A literature study was conducted to get an overview of the bioaccumulation studies of
MP in benthic organisms since only a few studies have been made on deposit-feeding
organisms.
 In addition to the literature study, an experiment was developed and completed, which
exposed the deposit-feeding bivalve Nucula spp.
to dierent types and sizes of MP
particles to investigate the ingestion and excretion of these.
 This experiment led to the development and optimisation of a method for the preparation
of deposit-feeding bivalves to conduct MP analysis on Laser Direct Infrared chemical
imaging spectroscopy (LDIR).
 In view of the above exposure experiment and method development, the thesis nally
addresses, which MP analysing methods are the most promising for quantifying and
identifying MP uptake and accumulation in deposit-feeding bivalves.

Introduction to microplastics in the

This chapter is an introduction to microplastic (MP) and its fate in marine environments
focusing on the benthic biota and sediment.
First an introduction to plastic and MP in
general is given, and afterwards the pathway into marine sediments are elaborated. In the
nal section a literature review of the potential of MP bioaccumulation is presented.
1.1
The plastic in microplastic
Plastic can be dened as solid materials formed by polymers of a mainly petrochemical origin
(Council, 1994). Plastic reaching the environment is commonly dened in size fractions, and
Hartmann et al. (2019) suggested the following categorisation:
 Nano-plastic: 1 to 1000 nm
 Microplastic: 1 to 1000 µm
 Meso-plastic: 1 to 10 mm
 Macro-plastic: > 1 cm
Microplastic (MP) production, usage and waste disposal have increased like plastic in general
(Geyer et al., 2017).
1.1.1
Plastic production and waste
Plastic production has increased exponentially since the 1950's. It started to replace many
traditional materials like wood, glass and metal; and this trend is predicted to continue reach-
ing an annual plastic production of 1,124 million tonnes in 2050 (World Economic Forum
et al., 2016). There are however an uncertainty in the estimation of future plastic production
regarding mainly bio-plastic, waste management and change in consumer and industry trends
(Geyer et al., 2017). As the plastic production and use has increased so has the amount of
plastic waste and the need for waste management. In Europe plastic waste is handled in one
of three ways: incineration (42.60 %), recycling (32.50 %) or landlling (24.90 %) (European
Court of Auditors, 2020). Globally the shares of incineration or recycling are lower due to
dierent regional or national regulations and traditions on waste management (D'Ambrières,
2019). Correspondingly the amount of land-lling and poor plastic waste management, dened
as scattered or inadequately disposed plastic, goes up (Jambeck et al., 2015).
1.1.2
Polymer chemistry
Plastic is the common name for macromolecules formed by large chains of repeating molecules.
Typically alkene molecules, known as monomers, create bonds to form a polymer and for plastic
the lengths of a polymer chain can vary from 10,000 to 100,000,000 monomers (Crawford and
Quinn, 2016). The creation of the chains is called polymerisation and there are primarily
two types of polymers: chain-growth polymers and step-growth polymers. The chain-growth
polymer is created by polymerisation of alkene monomers added to a long chain in sequence
called addition polymerisation (Figure 1.1). Polyethylene is initiated by heating and adding

in the PP chain and nally, atactic is when there is no regularity between the placement of

Polymer toxicity
Toxicity in plastic can be attributed to either residual monomers from manufacturing or in-
termediates released in the degradation of plastics (Andrady, 2011).
Lithner et al. (2011)
developed a hazard ranking model for polymers based on the monomer hazard classications
in the EU classication and labelling regulation.
The hazard classication data of all the
chemical substances used in the production of a plastic polymer were assessed and given a
hazard score based on ve rankings (I-V). The most hazardous substances being in level V
(e.g. carcinogenicity or reproductive toxicity) and the least hazardous in level I (e.g. self-
reactive substance or ammables). Many of the substances t in several of the hazard levels
and therefore each level has a hazard grade (I = 1, II = 10, III = 100, IV = 1,000 and V
= 10,000) making it possible to calculate a total hazard score for each substance. For each
polymer the sum of the hazard scores from all of the chemical substances are used to calculate
the total hazard score of the polymer. Lithner et al. (2011) assessed 55 polymers and ranked
them based on their total hazard score (Table 1.3). Furthermore each polymer was given the
highest hazardous level that was in the substances used to produce the polymer. As described
previously the same polymer can be produced dierently depending on the use, and Lithner
et al. (2011) therefore assessed dierent specications of the same polymers.

1.1.3
Categorisation of microplastic
MP is divided into two groups depending on the origin: primary or secondary. There are two
dierent denitions of primary and secondary MP used in literature. The rst one is (Boucher
and Friot, 2017):

Secondary MP is small pieces of plastic originating from fragmentation or weathering of meso-

1.2
Microplastic entering the environment
MP enters the environment as primary MP through wastewater, surface run-o to rivers or
ocean, atmospheric deposition or directly from wastewater or weathering of paint from ships or
other activities on the sea (Figure 1.4). Secondary MP can enter from disposal and degradation
of plastic on land, or directly from activities on sea like disposal from ships or lost shing gear.
The largest source of secondary MP is poor waste management both on land and on sea, and
much of the secondary MP found in the marine environment is degraded from meso- or macro-
plastic in the marine environment (Jambeck et al., 2015). MP is present in water-bodies and
sediments around the world (Wu et al., 2019). The concentrations in the marine environment
vary from a few particles per m3 with the highest concentrations found in coastal areas, where
it has been found to reach 102,000 particles per m3 (Norén and Naustvoll, 2011).

and biological by microbial communities. Throughout the water column degradation of MP

1.2.2
Microplastic in the sediment
Several studies have shown the sediment as a sink for MP. In 2014, Woodall et al. (2014)
found that MP bres were more abundant in deep sea sediments compared to surface-water in
the north and north-east Atlantic, Mediterranean Sea and the south-west Indian Ocean. The
largest dierence showed up to four times more MP in the deep sea sediments compared to
the surface water. In a review by Harris (2020) of 94 studies on MP in sediments, showed that
MP concentrations are between 4 and 140 times larger in sediment from estuaries and fjords
than from shelf or deep sea sediments.
Most polymers have densities around the ones of seawater and the organic matter typically
found in marine sediments (Figure 1.6). This is called hydraulic equivalence and it means that
a plastic particle of the same size, density and shape will be transported the same way as a
similar sediment particle (Harris, 2020). Furthermore Enders et al. (2019) have shown that
the abundance of larger (> 500 µm) high-density plastic is related to the sediment grain size.
In the study they concluded that the relation was based on the fact that the plastic particles
would be found with the grain sizes needing the same force for transportation in the marine
environment, and the relation was of one to two orders of magnitude from sediment grain to
MP particle. The most likely hydraulic equivalence based on density alone is between MP and
organic matter, such as wood or organisms debris (Figure 1.6). One study of Arctic sediment
by Bergmann et al. (2017) found a correlation between high quantities of MP, Chlorophyll-A
and particulate organic matter (POM). The authors account this to the seasonally phyto-
plankton bloom, where small MP particles can combine with ocs of sinking detritus from the
phytoplankton. In a study from Strand et al. (2019), the sediment in the Danish inner waters
was examined for MP. It showed a strong correlation between total organic carbon (TOC) and
MP in the Danish Inner waters. The authors suggest that this relationship is due the fact that
the MP found was in sedimentary areas and not erosion zones, where sediment consists of less
TOC and more dense particles like sand and silt.
It is however important to keep in mind when comparing the transport of organic matter
and MP that organic matter usually is transformed during transportation in the marine en-
vironment, for example by ingestion of organisms or by oxidation under changing chemical
conditions. MP is not likely to change either by consumption or oxidation (Harris, 2020).
After reaching the seabed most MP particles are transported as suspended-load, meaning that
it is uplifted by the ow of the seawater just above the sediment (Harris, 2020). In addition
to this, organisms play a major role in MP transport in the sediment (de Smit et al., 2021).

Microplastic abundance in sediments
The amount of MP reaching the sediment is debated, Project Kaisei studied several plastic
sources and fates mainly in the Atlantic Ocean from 2007 to 2017 and it showed that 70 %
of the plastic reaching the marine environment was transported to the seabed. According to
Harris (2020) around 90 % of the plastic reaches the sediments and the remainders are either
oating in the sea-surface or deposited on beaches. This corresponds to the Eunomia Research
& Consulting Ltd report "Plastics in the Marine Environment" from 2016 showing that up to
94 % of the plastic that enters the marine environment ends up on the seabed. They further
estimated that these 94 % would correspond to 70 kg of plastic per square kilometre of seabed
(Sherrington et al., 2016). As the production of plastic has increased, so has the MP abun-
dance in sediments. Brandon et al. (2019) studied MP abundance in coastal sediments for
historical changes. They found that from 1945-2009 MP abundance increased exponentially
with a doubling time of 15 years.
MP concentrations found in sediments around the worlds oceans vary a lot (Table 1.5).
Hotspots are found mostly in populated areas where studies mainly on beaches or in har-

concentrations are found in remote areas of the deep sea like the Arctic (Harris, 2020). How-
ever Browne et al. (2011) found a concentration of 4000 particles per kg dry sediment in the
sediment of the Artic deep sea. This is higher than the so far reported concentrations in the
inner Danish waters varying from 202-3511 particle per kg dry sediment (Strand et al., 2019).

Bio-accumulation of microplastic in benthic organisms
1.3.1
What is bioaccumulation?
Bioaccumulation is a part of the risk assessment of environmental hazards. In general the
denition of bioaccumulation is: "The uptake of substances from both food and water", as
it is dened in the Handbook of Environmental Chemistry by Geyer et al. (2000). In most
research bioaccumulation is examined and discussed based on laboratory experiments. Bioac-
cumulation can also be dened based on experiments, like this denition from the OECD
guideline for testing of chemicals: "Bioaccumulation is the increase in concentration of the
test substance in or on an organism relative to the concentration of test substance in the sur-
rounding medium" (OECD, 2008). Bioaccumulation in an organism can be the result of two
things: bioconcentration or biomagnication, which can occur separately or simultaneously.
The denitions by Geyer et al. (2000) are:
 Bioconcentration: The increase in concentration of the substance/chemical in or on an
organism, resulting exclusively from uptake via the body surface (direct uptake), relative
to the concentration of the substance/chemical in the surrounding medium.
 Biomagnication: The increase in concentration of the substance/chemical in or on an
organism, resulting from dietary uptake (trophic transfer), relative to the concentration
of the substance/chemical in the food/prey digested.
The bioconcentration of a specic substance, like MP, is aected by several factors divided in
these main categories (Geyer et al., 2000):

 Physico-chemical properties of the particles
 Toxicity towards the given organism
 Concentration of MP in the surrounding medium (water or sediment)
 Bioavailability
The physico-chemical properties like water solubility or lipophilicity of the MP can depend on
many dierent factors, like polymertype, size and shape, but also the additives (section 1.1).
In relation to bioaccumulation the toxicity is especially important when designing bioaccumu-
lation experiments. If the toxicity of the MP is too high, the results of the bioaccumulation
experiment will be misleading since bioconcentration has been found to decrease if toxic eects
occur (Geyer et al., 1995). OECD has set 20 % mortality as maximum at the end of a valid
bioaccumulation test.
Increasing concentration of MP in the surrounding medium usually
increases the uptake of MP (Kögel et al., 2020). In a study by Pedersen et al. (2020) uptake
of MP by the freshwater lter-feeding mussel, Dreissena bugensis, was investigated and it was
found that the uptake and the retention time increased along increasing concentrations of MP
in the water column.
Bioavailability is the part of MP present in the environment available for uptake by the organ-
ism (Botterell et al., 2019). For MPs this part is aected by many dierent biotic and abiotic
factors. Most important categories are these three:
 MP and the organisms simultaneously present
 Selectivity/feeding strategy of the organism
 MP characteristics
For most benthic and sediment living organisms the geographic range of the habitat is not very
large, so the co-occurrence between MP and organisms is determined by the MP concentration
in the surrounding sediment. The feeding type and structure of ingestion system determines
the degree of how an organism is able to select food. Sediment-dwelling organisms are either
suspension-feeders or deposit-feeders. Many deposit-feeders show low selectivity when it comes
to feeding (Graham and Thompson, 2009; Compton et al., 2007). Setälä et al. (2016) compared
MP ingestion by benthic organisms with dierent feeding strategies, and found that suspension-
and deposit-feeders ingested the highest amounts of MP.
Translocation
Bioaccumulation is more likely to occur if the particles are able to translocate from the gas-
trointestinal tract inside the organism. Translocation in an organism means the transfer of
the particles from gills, guts, digestive glands (in bivalves) to organs, muscles or other parts
of the organism. It can be forced by chemical, biological or physical processes. It has been
showed that MP particles of 2 - 4 µm of size can translocate from the digestive gland in the
suspension-feeding mussel, Mytilus edulis, (?). That the upper limit for translocation of MP
particles is around 10-20 µm was also shown by Devriese et al. (2015), where it was found
that MPs larger than 20 µm are unlikely to translocate in the marine brown shrimp, Crangon
crangon. However in 2017 Ribeiro et al. (2017) showed that 20 µm PS particles are able to
translocate into the digestive gland of the bivalve, Scrobicularia plana. The main obstacle for
translocation is the size of the particle, therefore nano-plastics are seen as a bigger threat than
MP to bioaccumulation.

Accumulation of nano-plastic
The potential accumulation of nano-plastics is an issue that is not addressed thoroughly in
this literature review, but it is necessary to emphasize, that the potential of nano-plastic
bioaccumulation is expected to be much higher than for MP (Peng et al., 2020).
Studies
on nano-plastic in the marine ecosystem is still scarce due to challenges in sampling and

on analysis of nano-plastic in the environment, and identied ve studies able to determine
nano-plastic in samples from the environment, one of them being in seawater.
There are
however studies identifying negative eects from laboratory nano-plastic exposure experiments
on marine organisms. Haegerbaeumer et al. (2019) reviewed studies showing eects from MP or
nano-plastics on benthic marine organisms. Nine studies were found showing dierent negative
eects from nano-plastics on a various range of benthic marine organisms. For example, Jeong
et al. (2017) identied eects on development and reproduction of the copepod, Paracyclopina
nana, from exposure to 50 nm PS spheres with a lowest observed eect concentration on 10

µm HD-PE on the oyster, Ostrea edulis, and found signicant increased feeding rates at a
concentration of 25 µg/L, but no eect on mortality. Petersen et al. (2019) presents strategies
for experimental approaches to quantify bioaccumulation of engineered nanomaterials and
concluded that there was a critical need for more accurate analytical methods to identify
and quantify enginereed nanomaterials in both single-celled and multi-celled organisms. For
marine multi-celled species they concluded that one of the main challenges is to dierentiate
between the engineered nanomaterial being adsorped on the surface and the nanomaterial
being accumulated inside the organism.
Trophic transfer of microplastic
Trophic transfer of MP particles is important to address as well as the bioaccumulation, as
the combination of these two processes can mean increasing MP bioavailability for animals
in higher trophic levels. Sfriso et al. (2020) showed the potential of trophic transfer between
dierent feeding groups in benthic communities, and Gutow et al. (2019) has shown trophic
transfer of MP particles from sea snails (Littorina littorea and Littorina obtusata) to other
gastropods via the pedal mucus produced by the sea snails. Furthermore there is evidence of
MP particles present in higher trophic levels as Romeo et al. (2015) showed microplastic in
the stomach content of bluen tuna, and Eriksson and Burton (2003) showed accumulation
of MP particles in fur seals. The rst study combining bioaccumulation and trophic transfer
was conducted by Farrell and Nelson (2013). It showed a low transfer of PS spheres from
the mussel, M. edulis to the coastal crab, Carcinus maenas. The mussels were exposed to
the spheres and thereafter fed to the crabs. It was found that the crabs retained 0.04 % of
the MP initially fed to the mussels. A study by Hasegawa and Nakaoka (2021) showed that
the ingestion of MP particles through trophic transfer of MP particles from mysids (Neomysis
spp.) to benthic sh (Myaxocephalus randti) were 3-11 times higher than what the sh ingested
directly from the water column. In this study it was further more investigated that the smallest
of the MP particles (<32.40 µm) were able to translocate from the digestive system into the
tissues.

2.
Deposit-feeding bivalves
This chapters introduces deposit-feeding bivalves especially the genus Nucula spp., since they
are the test organisms of the exposure experiment and the rst method development experi-
ments conducted in this thesis. Finally a short description of the suspension-feeding bivalve,
Mya arenaria is given, as it is used in the pre-treatments for MP analysis methods later on.
Deposit-feeding animals live in or on sediments and feed of soft ne sediments (Lopez and
Levinton, 2011). This is in contrary to suspension-feeders ltering seawater for food particles
to digest. Deposit-feeding bivalves have large palps and small gills compared to suspension-
feeding bivalves enabling them to rapidly sort sediment (Figure 2.1).
Figure 2.1: Feeding organs from typical suspension- and deposit-feeding bivalves living in the surcial
sediment (Compton et al., 2007).
Deposit-feeding bivalves use the gills to pump in the sediment and the palps to sort after-
wards (Younge, 1939). Some organisms living on the surface of the sediment have the ability
to switch between deposit and suspension-feeding. This can depend on the sediment-type
the animals feed in, for example some bivalves are deposit-feeders in muddy sediments and
suspension-feeders in sandy sediments (Taghon and Jumars, 1984). The nutritional value of
the food for deposit-feeders comes from the non-living and living organic matter they come
across in the sediment. Mostly, the digestible organic matter in sediments is less than 1% of
the sediments mass (Shull, 2019). Therefore many deposit-feeders have high ingestion rates,
and it is typical that deposit-feeding invertebrates ingest and deposits between 1 and 3 body
weights per day (Lopez and Levinton, 2011; Shull, 2019). Ingestion rates have been found to
be correlated with organism size for deposit-feeders. Cammen (1979) examined 19 dierent
deposit-feeders and detrivors including three bivalves, and found that for all of them the in-
gestion rate could be described as:
C = 0.381 ∗W 0.742
Where C is the organic matter ingested per day in mg and W is dry body weight in mg.
Another way to adapt to low nutritional value in food is by ecient ingestion selectivity. Many
deposit-feeders use a combination of rapid ingestion and selectivity (Lopez and Levinton, 2011).

Bivalves have the ability to reject particles pre-ingestively. These particles are then egested
as pseudofaeces. For most deposit-feeders the size of the mouth apparatus is the upper limit
for food particle size. Furthermore most deposit-feeders have specialised in selecting ne grain
sizes with low surface areas, since that is the part of the sediment with the highest organic
matter content. There are however other ways of sorting sediments for example by particle
shape, surface texture or specic gravity (Lopez and Cheng, 1982). Some deposit-feeders like
sea-snails have adapted to feed on the surface of the sediment, where microalgae and recent
deposit organic detritus tends to accumulate. Particulate organic matter (POM) is decreasing
with depth in the sediment, and the densest populations of deposit-feeders are found in places
with high POM values (Lopez and Levinton, 2011).
Deposit-feeders aect the sediment structure by the rapid processing of sediment through their
gastrointestinal tract. It has been found that deposit-feeders accelerate microbial productivity
(Lopez et al., 1977). This happens when deposit-feeders feed equally on all types of bacteria
creating a favourable environment for fast-growing strains and species of bacteria. This eect
can reach a large area of the seabed since some deposit-feeders burrow in galleries several me-
ters wide (Lopez and Levinton, 2011). The burrowing mechanism of deposit-feeding bivalves
is a ud-lled foot and their activity in the surcial sediments results in bioturbation as e.g.
increase in water content or oxygen levels (Lopez et al., 1977). The bioturbation further more
increases the interaction between the surcial layer and bottom water currents which enhances
the suspension of particles from the water column (Rhoads and Young, 1970).
Deposit-feeder populations are typically stable in size over time due to the fact that the POM in
sediments are stored faster that the deposit-feeding animals are able to digest (Levinton, 1972).
However this might be dierent for populations living in shallow waters than in deep-water
sediments. In shallow waters, POM and detritus are mainly driven by episodic inputs such
as detritus from seasonal plankton blooms or macroseaweed growth (Elmgren, 1984; Mann,
1988). Eects from contaminants can also have an impact on deposit-feeder communities. For
example has been deposit-feeders been found to be have a higher anity for bioaccumulation
of organic pollutants compared to suspension-feeders (Kaag et al., 1997).
2.1
Nucula spp.
Nucula spp. is a genum of deposit-feeding bivavles (Table 2.1). Nucula spp. live and feed
burrowed in marine sediments around 1-10 cm from sediment surface on depths varying from 5
to 2,000 meters in marine and brackish water (Seiderer, 2008). The typical lifespan of Nucula
spp. are 5-10 years growing to a shell size between 1 and 2 cm and a height of 0.5 cm (Bondesen,
1984). The population size of Nucula spp. varies a lot, but if the physio-chemical conditions
(POM, grain sizes, etc.) are good for the specic species and there are few competitors several
hundred individuals can be found within one square meter (Seiderer, 2008).
The dierent species of Nucula spp. have a large spatial distribution around the world and
in the Danish inner waters four species of Nucula spp. is commonly found: Nucula tenuis (in
Danish: Glat nøddemusling), Nucula nitida (formerly: Nucula turgida, in Danish: Almindelig
Nøddemusling), Nucula nucleus (in Danish: Grøn Nøddemusling) and Nucula sulcata (in
Danish: Ribbet Nøddemusling) (Bondesen, 1984). These four species live on depths from 5 to
100 meters and it is dicult to distinguish between them (Bondesen, 1984) (Figure 2.2).

2.1.1
Feeding strategy
In the subclass Protobranchia the palp is the food collecting organ, in contrast to other deposit-
feeding bivalves using the gills (Hirasaka, 1927). The shells are completely shut when the
bivalve is not eating (Hirasaka, 1927). The selectivity of Nucula spp. has been studied by
Lopez and Cheng (1982) examining the selectivity of Nucula annulata. However this study
showed inconclusive results on whether the species were selective or non-selective in regards to
organic fractions of sediments. Another species of Nucula spp., Nucula turgida has been found
to be highly selective (Davis and Wilson, 1985). Nucula spp. are able to move slowly around
the sediment for food with their foot muscle, which also enables them to burrow (Hirasaka,
1927).

2.2
Mya arenaria
Mya arenaria is a commmon soft-shell suspension-feeder in shallow waters in the northern
hemisphere (Snelgrove et al., 1999) (Figure 2.3). It mainly populates muddy sand areas and
the population densities can vary a lot, making the distribution patchy. It lives in the sediment
from 15-30 cm in shallow waters up to 60 m depth (Steen, 2015). Their lifespan is up 10-12
years and they can grow to a shell size of 14 cm and a height of 0.6 cm (Gofas). They are
selective feeders able to select specic plankton sizes through their gills. They are able to
move around the sediment when they are small, but full-grown individuals spent the rest of
their life in the same burrow (Günther, 1992). The shells are always a bit open, and therefore
sediment particles are commonly found inside the shells (Günther, 1992). Mya arenaria has
been found to have a high tolerance to low salinities and also pollutants (Gofas).

3.
Examining microplastic: extraction,
identication and quantication
This chapter describes MP analysis in bivalve tissue. The pre-treatments and analytical meth-
ods described are those that will be used in the experimental part of this thesis or the meth-
ods that were used in the studies presented in the literature review in section 1.3.
Some
of these have been adapted to deposit-feeding organisms, but only very few have been tried
with deposit-feeding bivalves. The process of analysing MP in deposit-feeding bivalves can be
divided into three main steps: sampling, pre-treatment and analysis.
3.1
Sampling
Sampling can be in-situ sampling in sediment with organisms from a specic geographical area.
This is done for most studies independently on whether the aim is to identify natural ingestion
of MP or to use the organisms for laboratory experiments like exposure experiments or toxicity
tests. Sampling of deposit-feeding organisms in-situ is much like sampling sediments and work
is mainly done by using grabs (Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2015). For sampling individuals for
laboratory experiments in-situ, the challenges are the uncertainty of what species or how many
individuals are collected. Therefore in-situ sampling acquire species determination, counting
and looking through potentially massive amounts of sediments to get the required individuals.
Alternatively it is possible to cultivate some species of deposit-feeders in laboratory conditions,
this is not usually done with bivalves, but it is possible for e.g. a wide range of oligochaetes
(OECD, 2008).
3.2
Pre-treatment
To extract the MP particles from the organisms a pre-treatment of the bivalve tissue is re-
quired. It is necessary to remove as much of the organic matter (tissue) as possible. To digest
the organic matter from marine organisms dierent media have been used. The most common
ways to achieve digestion are by enzymes or various chemicals as acids, alkaline chemicals and
oxidation agents (Rist et al., 2019; Claessens et al., 2013). A successful digestion leaves no
visible pieces of organic matter, and at the same time does not alter MP particles. Especially
the chemical digestion must always be done with care and with mind on what MP polymer
types there might be in the sample. Some acidic treatments have been shown to damage the
polymers making identication inaccurate, instead oxidation agents like sodium hypochlorite
(NaClO) and potassium hydroxide (KOH) have been recommended (Enders et al., 2017). The
digestion method is often optimised by varying the time period or temperature. Chemical di-
gestion with oxidation agents can be successful during two hours at room temperature (Strand
et al., 2019), while enzymatic digestion needs to work around 12 hours and 37◦C (Rist et al.,
2019). However enzymes are often a cheaper choice than some of the chemicals and it is also
the digestion method with the least impact on the MP particles (Catarino et al., 2017).
It is common to use density separation as the next step in extracting MP from sediment-living
organisms. This step allows denser particles than MP, like quartz (2.63 g/cm3), to be sepa-
rated from the liquid digested tissue. The density separator is often a saturated salty solution,
and the salt used depends on the densities of the MP known or expected to be in the sample.
The most common salt is sodium chloride (NaCl) resulting in a density of 1.2 g/cm3. This is a

cheap solution, but limited to lower density polymers (see densities of polymers in section 1.1).
Using less common salts like zinc chloride (ZnCl2) or sodium iodide (NaI) makes it possible to
reach a density of 1.6-1.7 g/cm3 (Quinn et al., 2017). Density separation is conducted in three
steps: 1) mixing digested sample and salt solution of specic density by stirring or shaking
a dened time period, 2) settling of the mix and 3) removal of the dense part from the mix
(Hanvey et al., 2017). The stirring is usually between 2 and 10 minutes, but the settling time
is varying a lot between studies using from 5 minutes to 12 hours (Hanvey et al., 2017). A
separatory funnel is used for the separation, because it is possible to remove the dense part
from the lighter part by a valve in the bottom of the funnnel. During the process it is dicult
to avoid particles losses, since the salty solutions are sticky. In order to minimise the particle
loss and make the process easier, Imhof et al. (2012) developed a Munich Plastic Sediment
Separator, which is a machine that provides the mixing, settling and removal all in one. An-
other way of minimising the sticking has been found to be by using oil drops for example olive
oil, which should bind to the MP particles and thereby keeping the MP particles in the salt
solution (Karlsson et al., 2017).
With or without a density separation step, the nal step of pre-treatment is ltration. The
most common ways of ltration is by using a vacuum-pump or a sieve on to a lter (Hanvey
et al., 2017). It is important to make sure that the lter material is not a source of MP poten-
tially contaminating the sample, and that the pore size is smaller than the detection limit of
the MP analysis method (Hanvey et al., 2017). Usually glass bre lters have been used, but
also metal lters or plankton or zooplankton meshes can be used (Hanvey et al., 2017) (Rist
et al., 2019).
3.3
Microplastic analysis
There are several analytical techniques for identifying and quantifying MP in deposit-feeding
organism tissue (Table 3.3). In some studies a combination of two techniques is used, for
example one for quantication and another for identication. The most common combination
in the studies reviewed is counting of MP particles on optical or uorescent microscope and then
use of Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy to analyse a selection of the particles
to identify polymer types present in the samples (Hanvey et al., 2017). The LDIR is a new
spectroscopy, and so far only two studies have been published using it as MP quantication
and identication method (Scircle et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021a).
Since most of the analysis options in Table 3.3 are expensive it is worth to mention that there
in recent years have been attempts to nd a cheaper and still viable solution. This include a
study from Gebhardt and Forster (2018), where they dried the extracted MP and weight them
in order to get a quantity of MP particles. A study by Ehlers et al. (2020) provides a method
for gaining uorescent MP fragments and bres to use in ingestion and egestion laboratory
studies. They further show that the used uorescent MP can be identied with UV-ashlights.

4.1.1
Fieldwork
The animal collection was conducted on a cruise around the Danish Inner waters on the
research vessel DANA (owned by DTU) in the end of October 2020. The bivalves of Nucula

To compare the eciency of the digestion methods: enzymatic with Alcalase and chemical
with oxidation agents NaClO and KOH on tissue from Nucula spp., a protocol for digestion
comparison (Appendix C) was developed with the goal of comparing the dried tissue remaining
after digestion.
Bivalve preparation
The protocol developed tted seven Nucula spp. individuals (approximately 2g of tissue), so
seven bivalves were carefully opened with tweezers to avoid any shells breaking (Figure 4.3).

The tissue from each individual was cut into small pieces with tweezers and a scalpel. After-

were added, and the total weight was noted (see supplementary data).
Digestion
The enzyme digestion were done with 2.5 % Alcalase, which Rist et al. (2019) found as the most
ecient concentration and enzyme for digestion of blue mussel tissue. The enzyme solution

for 1 minute to make sure that all tissue was in the 20 mL solution. After this the vials were
placed at a shaking table inside the oven at 37◦C for 12 hours under continuous shaking.

sity, adapted to the test organism and instruments available in this thesis. For the chemical
digestion the oxidation agent solution were prepared with 5% NaClO and 3% KOH. The so-
lution were made with milliQ, KOH pellets (Merck) and NaClO 12% active chlorine (Merck).
200 mL of the solution were mixed in a fume hood.

6.
Discussion
The discussion starts with a look at the results of from the pre-treatments and MP analytical
methods performed in this thesis. The focus is especially on discussing the possible improve-
ments of these. Afterwards the exposure experiment and MP analysis will be put in perspective
with a discussion of the literature review of potential MP bioaccumulation in deposit-feeding
bivalves presented in section 1.3.
6.1
Pre-treatment eciency
6.1.1
Comparison of digestion methods
In the digestion comparison, there were no signicant dierences in the wet or dry weight
percentages, but there was a clear visual dierence. The chemical digestion seemed to leave
larger pieces of tissue, though the dry weight % showed similar eciencies. Furthermore did
the results from the MP analysis show a need for improvement of the digestion. Despite the
dilution factor in chemical digestion + density separation pre-treatment samples, it is seen
that the number of particles identied from each sample is around the same as for the enzy-
matic pre-treatment. This shows that the total number of particles in the samples from the
chemical digestion + digestion separation is a lot higher than from the enzymatic digestion,
which ts very well with the visual observations of the lters (Figure 5.4).
A combination of an enzymatic digestion and chemical digestion has previously been shown
successful for both sh and bivalve tissue (Karlsson et al., 2017). Karlsson et al. (2017) used
the enzyme proteinase K and the oxidation agent hydrogen peroxide (H2O2). The mussels in
this study were blue mussels, and prior to digestion, they were freeze-dried and grounded. The
enzymatic digestion with Alcalase has already been optimised by Rist et al. (2019), but this
has not been tried in combination with a chemical digestion. In a study by Xu et al. (2020), a
combination of enzymatic digestion with proteinase K and chemical digestion with both acid
and oxidation agents has shown better results than KOH and NaClO digesting alone. This
was shown by comparing lters after digestion of gastropod tissue spiked with MP particles.
In addition to the results from Karlsson et al. (2017) this indicates that a combination of
chemical and enzymatic digestion at least with the enzyme proteinase K is possible and more
ecient than chemical digestion alone.
On one hand, the chemical digestion is a lot faster than the enzymatic digestion, which in ad-
dition requires temperatures of 37◦C. On the other hand the enzymatic digestion is more easy
to handle, as there are no need for it to be handled with security equipment as the chemicals
used in this thesis. It might be a possibility to increase the eciency of the chemical digestion
by increasing the temperature to 40-50 ◦C, which was seen having a positive eect on the
digestion in a study by Pfeier and Fischer (2020). This study did not show an improvement
of a longer time period, but a study by (Cole et al., 2014) showed that a digestion period of
24h can increase the eect of chemical digestions with the alkaline sodium hydroxide (NaOH).
In choosing the digestion method it is important to remember the eect it might have on the
MP particles used. Both acidic and alkaline digestion methods have been seen to change the
spectra of MP particles (Enders et al., 2017), while enzymes have not (Rist et al., 2019). The
chemicals used in this thesis, NaClO and KOH, have however been shown to have an eect

6.1.2
Challenges with density separation
When extracting MP particles from sediment samples, a density separation is common (Van
Cauwenberghe et al., 2015). In this thesis, it was therefore decided to add a density separa-
tion step to the pre-treatment when several large sediment particles where identied in the
samples analysed with enzymatic digestion pre-treatment. There is a risk of increasing the
MP particle loss during a pre-treatment when adding another step in the protocol, and it had
previously been shown that there was a large loss in the sample preparation for the LDIR at
DTU Environment described further in the section below. Despite of the density separation,
the results of the chemical digestion + density separation still showed large sediment particles
in the samples. The density achieved (1.69 g/cm3) has previously been shown to be ecient
at separating sediment particles (Quinn et al., 2017) and therefore the low separation achieved
could indicate that the time of settling during density separation was not long enough. As
described in section 3 the time period used for settling varies a lot in published studies, and
there are studies conrming that as long as 12h could be necessary (Hanvey et al., 2017). The
long settling times have been shown ecient for river and beach sediments (Klein et al., 2015;
Stolte et al., 2015), but marine sediments have also been separated with zinc bromide (ZnBr2),
that have similar density as ZnCl2, settling only for 10 minutes (Quinn et al., 2017). Karlsson
et al. (2017) used approximately 5h for bivalve tissue using a saturated NaCl solution, and
the results in this thesis could indicate that a settling time longer than 2h is necessary, but
whether 5h is enough is unknown for now.
During the experiment the sample became very sticky after the adding of the dense salty solu-
tion. This is of course due to the salts, but it is possible that this added to the loss of particles
and might also add to the uncertainty of the particle identications by the LDIR, since the
particles were seen to cluster more on the sides of the separatory funnel. It would be recom-
mended to try and dilute the salty sample with milliQ before ltration, this could perhaps
avoid the clustering of the particles, and help the later identication and quantication. In
other studies this issue has been addressed by adding of olive oil to the salt solution. This has
been shown to gather the MP particles in the oil and thereby avoiding that they stick to the
walls of funnels or vials (Karlsson et al., 2017). However extra rinsing was also necessary in
the study by Karlsson et al. (2017), where dishwasher soap was used to rinse the sides of the
funnel to avoid the oil from sticking to the sides. Another study by Imhof et al. (2012) showed
recovery rates as low as 39 % for a similar density separation set-up as used in this thesis.
They improved the recovery rates to 95.5 % for MP <1mm by developing a Munich Plastic
Sediment Separator that for example included aeration of the sample and salt solution for up
to 12h prior to settling. With the experiences from the experiments in this thesis and the
studies presented, there are four applications that fairly easy can be implemented to improve
the density separation: 1) oil added to the sample as described previously, 2) aeration for up
to 12h of the sample and salt solution, 3) increasing settling time to at least 5 hours, 4) rinsing
of the funnels and vials with dish washing soap.

6.1.3
Loss of MP particles during pre-treatment
The loss of MP particles in the steps of the pre-treatment after ltration had been reported
as a big problem in the un-published bachelor theses measuring MP ingestion in blue mussels
with the LDIR. The protocol of the steps after ltration was similar to the method described
as pre-treatment for analyses on the LDIR in the study by Scircle et al. (2020) where Agilent
(LDIR manufacturer) is among the authors. Scircle et al. (2020) reported however no control
of recovery of the MP particles, so the author of this thesis had a correspondence with James C.
Cizdziel, the corresponding author of Scircle et al. (2020). He reported that they experienced
no signs of loss of particles in their controls of the pre-treatment. It was however dicult to
examine MP particle losses in their study, as it examines the abundance of MP particles in the
environment and not recovery of MP particles added in the laboratory. In the bachelor theses,
the nal steps included a complete evaporation of the 50 % EtOH solution and a re-suspension
of the sample. In Scircle et al. (2020) as well as in this thesis, the protocol was dierent in
order to avoid the complete evaporation. In Scircle et al. (2020), no evaporation took place
and the lter was just removed from the 50 % EtOH solution after ultra-sonication. In this
thesis, the vials were set to evaporate 1 of the 2 mL of the 50 % EtOH solution (controlled
by weighing) following the ultra-sonication. The eect of this optimisation has not been
investigated further in this thesis, and the total amount of particles in the samples makes it
dicult to estimate the loss of MP particles. There are some of the recovery rates showing
big losses, like no identied PS or PE particles by the LDIR in the pos2 sample from the
chemical digestion and density separation pre-treatment. However the recovery rates from the
microscopy analysis of the same samples shows high recovery rates of both PE and PS, so
the lack of PE and PS particles identied by the LDIR can be explained by the clustering of
particles in the analysis or covering by other particles.
In addition to losses in the nal steps, there might also be losses just from handling the samples
as described above with the density separation, or as seen in the microscope analysis of the
FLM2 from the enzymatic digestion pre-treatment. There it was seen that PEgreen particles
stuck on the walls of the glass vials. This may be the case for the other MP types as well, but
without a thoroughly rinsing of the vial and check of particles in the water or EtOH from this
rinsing, it is dicult say with certainty. To minimise this it was tried to handle the samples
with care e.g. avoid unnecessary shaking of the vials with the nal samples. In general in the
controls of these pre-treatments it was tried to minimise the steps, because more steps would
most likely lead to more particles lost. From the controls conducted in this thesis, there are
however too many particles still present in the samples, so it is evaluated that more steps like
two digestion methods during pre-treatment could decrease non-MP particle numbers. The
gain in accuracy of analysis is most likely worth the risk of loss of particles.
6.1.4
Background contamination
Throughout the pre-treatments, there were risks of background contamination, since MP is
present almost everywhere. The pre-treatments were done in a clean fume-hood, but plastic
gloves were used. It was tried to avoid the use of plastic equipment and instead use metal or
glass. However in the case of the density separation, a potential source of unidentied plastic
contamination is from the container storing the ZnCl2. The salt was purchased minimum 4
years ago and it was hardened into one big block. In order to get it out of the container, a
steel spatula and a lot of hard work had to be applied. This could have gotten plastic particles
loose from the container into the salt and thereby into the sample. In these samples rubber
particles were very abundant in both positve and negative controls, and this might be the
source. Besides the gloves, there were a minimum of plastic tools used in the laboratory work,

6.3
The potential of MP accumulation in deposit-feeding bi-
valves
Deposit-feeding bivalves ingest MP particles while feeding, and looking at the ndings pre-
sented in section 1.2, the bioavailability of MP for the deposit-feeding bivalves is expected to
increase as MP particles sinks and settles in the sediments. From the literature review on the
potential of MP bioaccumulation in benthic environments 21 out of 28 studies show no or low
potential for bioaccumulation of MP particles in benthic organisms. This indicates that there
might not be a large accumulation of MP in the benthic environment, but it has also been
seen in some of the studies that the results depend on concentration of MP particles (Besseling
et al., 2012). Therefore it might not be an issue with the current concentrations found in the
sediments, but there is a risk in the future if MP concentrations continues to increase in the
sediment.
The exposure experiment in this thesis were set-up to understand the uptake and potential of
accumulation of the added MP particles. The set-up is not similar to any of the experiments
found in the literature review, and the protocol in this thesis does not follow the guidelines
of the 28 days time period from the OECD (2008). Instead, the results of this exposure ex-
periment will make it possible to compare the MP concentrations in the bivalves from two
dierent time periods and thereby a potential accumulation. An exposure experiment like this

is similar to many of the laboratory experiments investigating negative eects of MP particles,
but most of these do not include analysis of MP in the organisms. However the exposure
study in this thesis has a similar approach to the potential of accumulation, as was seen in the
study by Welden and Cowie (2016), who studied the MP concentrations in lobsters found in
the wild, but then analysing the lobsters either before or after moulting.
The diculties of studying bioaccumulation will increase when looking at nano-plastis instead
(Petersen et al., 2019). It is necessary to continue to improve methods for nano-plastic analysis,
as many of the potentials for accumulation seen with MP will increase looking at nano-plastic.
Translocation is much more likely to occur with nano-plastics, and this will increase trophic
transfer as well (Peng et al., 2020).
In the literature review of potential MP bioaccumulation, three of the studies have deposit-
feeding organisms as their test organism. They all showed low or no potential of bioaccumula-

et al. (2019) analyses translocation of MP particles from the digestive system to the coelomic

accumulation comparing the concentrations found in the digestive system and the coelomic
uid. Besseling et al. (2012) conducted a bioaccumulation study in the laboratory showing
low potential for MP particles accumulating in the lugworms, Arenicola marina, investigated.
Gebhardt and Forster (2018) did a long exposure study to examine the transportation of MP
particles by the same lugworms. The objective was not to study accumulation within the
lugworm, but the results from the particle distribution in the sediments suggests that accumu-
lation of MPs in the lugworms are unlikely. The study by Gebhardt and Forster (2018) stands
out from the other studies by running over by far longest period of the time between 106 and
124 days. In general, it seems like the required time period of 28 days could be limiting on the
amount of bioaccumulation studies of MP. In the three studies on deposit-feeding organisms
the organisms were analysed for MP particles with microscopy. This is a time consuming and
operator-dependent method, but it does require a minimum of pre-treatment compared to the
LDIR and µ-FTIR. The pre-treatments for LDIR and µ-FTIR analysis performed in this thesis
has been challenging, and needs to be improved as discussed previously, so it will be easier to
analyse MP particles in deposit-feeding organisms.
6.3.1
Limitations of MP bioaccumulation studies
In the literature review on bioaccumulation of MP particles in benthic organisms, only one
study was found, that showed results based on experiments following the guidelines on bioac-
cumulation in sediment-dwelling benthic Oligochaetes from OECD (2008). Besseling et al.
(2012) conducted this experiment, and the main goal was not to assess bioaccumulation of
MP, but to evaluate negative eects from MP particles on benthic marine organisms and po-
tential increase of bioaccumulation of persistent organic pollutants from MP ingestion. In
bioaccumulation studies like this involving MP there are several studies analysing the accu-
mulation of chemical using the MP particles as vectors resulting in ingestion and uptake of
the chemical in the organism (Avio et al., 2015; Browne et al., 2013; Gomiero et al., 2018;
Chua et al., 2014; Haegerbaeumer et al., 2019). Many of these studies follow a similar ap-
proach starting with exposure of the chemical to the MP particles, and afterwards conducting
an exposure experiment with test organisms and the chemical-exposed MP particles. The
results often do not include MP analysis of the organisms, but focus on the accumulation of
the chemical. In a few cases however, the MP analysis is conducted as well. For example by
Avio et al. (2015) where MP occurrence in the haemolymph, gills and digestive system after
exposure where analysed and Besseling et al. (2012) where the lugworms were analysed for MP

Conclusion and outlook
Deposit-feeding bivalves are at risk of accumulating the MP particles that they ingest. The
literature review in this thesis shows that there were a majority of studies showing no to low
potential of bioaccumulation of MP in organisms in the benthic environment. However only
three of the total 28 studies are on deposit-feeding organisms. This highlights the scarceness of
information about potential bioaccumulation of MP in deposit-feeding organisms and bivalves
in particular. In this study an exposure experiment was performed, and it will be interesting
to add the results, to the knowledge about potential MP accumulation when the samples are
analysed in the future.
The methods of pre-treatment investigated to prepare the bivalve samples for MP analysis
show needs of further improvements. There are seen potential for good performance by both
the pre-treatments and the analysis on the LDIR, but there are too many particles to be
certain about the accuracy of the results presented. Therefore it is needed to improve the
pre-treatment of the samples as well as gaining further experience using the LDIR.
The use of both enzymatic and chemical digestion on the same sample is worth trying since
this has been seen to increase digestion eciency before on similar samples. There is also a
need of improvement in the density separation where especially four improvements should be
implemented: 1) increase mixing by aeration, 2) increase of settling times to at least 5 hours,
3) addition of oil to avoid particles sticking to the walls of funnels and vials, and 4) rinsing
funnels and vials with dish washing soap to decrease loss of particles.
In this study the samples were prepared for analysis on the LDIR a relative new spectroscopy
and there are no previous data published on the LDIR analysing MP in deposit-feeding organ-
isms. More often MP particles in sediments or deposit-feeding organisms have been analysed
with µ-FTIR or microscopy. In this study the analysing methods (LDIR, µ-FTIR, uorescent
microscopy, optical microscopy) are compared with the analyses of similar samples, but it
has been dicult to examine the results because of the large amounts of particles still in the
samples after pre-treatment. The results of the microscopy analyses are limited as only parts
of the MP types added in the positive controls were identiable on microscope. It was how-
ever possible to recover all the dierent MP types in either optical microscope or uorescent
microscope. The recovery rates for the optical microscope were very high (>100 %) indicating
challenges with the sub-samples analysed being representative. The LDIR results are uncer-
tain due to the large number of particles in the samples, and the samples pre-treated with
chemical digestion and density separation also show a large contamination of rubber particles.
Nonetheless show the results from the LDIR that it is possible to identify all of the MP types
added in the positive controls though only in one of the two positive samples. A few good
recovery rates (50 % - 150 %) for the samples from both enzymatic digestion pre-treatment
and chemical digestion and density separation pre-treatment are found with LDIR as well.
Finally it is concluded that the LDIR shows potential for analysing MP in tissue from deposit-
feeding bivalves, but for further research it is necessary to optimise the pre-treatment. Then it
will be possible to evaluate the MP analysis by the LDIR and accurately compare its eciency
to the performance of the µ-FTIR.

Chemical digestion:  
- 
Add 15 mL of a solution of 7 % NaOCl and 3 % KOH 
- 
Let agitate for 24h on a shaking table 
- 
End the digestion with 2 times 5 minutes ultra-sonification 
Density separation: 
- 
Fill a separatory funnel with 53 mL ZnCl2 solution 
- 
Add 0.5 mL acetic acid to the digested sample to neutralize it – check pH before continuing, 
should be around 6 
- 
Pour the digested sample into the funnel and stir vigorously with a stirring rod for 2 minutes 
- 
Allow to settle for 1h 
- 
Open the stopcock to let the heavy separate pour out and gather it in a beaker, close the 
stopcock again. Close the beaker with tin foil and save it for microscopic analysis 
Filtration: 
- 
Weigh a filter + 10 mL glass vial to be prepared for vacuum-filtering  
- 
Place the filter in the Büchner funnel, close the clamp and check that pump is ready and 
attached properly to the Büchner flask 
- 
Open the stopcock placed over the funnel at the top of a vacuum-filter system to let the light 
separate pour out directly onto the filter. Rinse the separate funnel thoroughly with milliQ 
- 
Vacuum-filter the digested tissue onto the filter and rinse the funnel thoroughly with milliQ 
poured directly onto the filter

-

analysis is conducted on LDIR
