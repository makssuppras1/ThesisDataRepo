Abstract 
The 60,000 m3 pit thermal energy storage (PTES) installed in Dronninglund solar district heating 
plant has been investigated in this project. PTES is a promising heat storage technology, using 
renewable energy. As PTES can be integrated well with solar district heating plant, and can achieve 
a better system performance compared to conventional district heating plant. This new technology 
has experienced a fast spread in Denmark.  
This project aims to assess thermal stratification situations, heat losses, charged/discharged energy 
contents, soil region temperature distribution as well as storage efficiency for the whole 2015 period. 
The experimental measurement data has been investigated and seven different charging/discharging 
categories was therefore classified. Based on the features of Dronninglund PTES region, some 
geometric and statistic coordination has been made, the C++ code was re-wrote and a new model 
named вАЬUGSTSxвАЭ was created.  
The experimental measurement data was used as input data to validate the new designed model. 
Based on the simulation results from TRNSYS, the total yearly heat losses from the PTES is 
1030MWh in 2015, with 65% heat loss from the top surface, 31% from the side surfaces and 4% 
from the bottom surface. Resulting to an overall differential rate of 19% compared to the measured 
total heat loss (1275 MWh). The calculated energy contents based on both simulated and measured 
results also show good matching: The annual charged/discharged heat error rate between simulated 
and measured data are only 3.4% and 2.4%, respectively. In addition, after comparing the changing 
curves of temperature distribution at different height levels inside PTES region, as well as four 
temperature sensorsвАЩ outputs in the soil region. Suitability for all sets of data was determined, and 
the thermal stratification conditions in both PTES and soil regions were therefore validated. Finally, 
two key system performance indicators: PTES storage efficiency and number of storage cycles were 
calculated and compared. 
Based on all the statements above, the new model вАЬUGSTSxвАЭ was validated. And this TRNSYS 
model can be used to implement further data analysis work for the Dronninglund PTES, in order to 
optimize its performance 
Keywords:  PTES, Thermal stratification, Heat loss, Seasonal energy storage, TRNSYS.

Chapter 1  Introduction 
It is an emerging question nowadays, that buildings consume a large amount of conventional energy 
sources all around the world, and contribute significantly to the fossil fuels emission. In order to 
minimize the negative influence resulted from greenhouse gases emission, several measures have 
been implemented. Such as governments introduced several policies to encourage utilization of 
sustainable energy, aims to minimize the conventional energy sources consumption. In this context, 
solar energy utilization is currently the most popular one. Because of the characteristic of solar 
energy, it is usually applied on heating system, especially in European countries. Solar district 
heating is a promising technologies, that can reduce the consumption of fossils and can lead to fewer 
carbon dioxide emissions. The advantages of solar systems are highlighted to reduce the 
management costs during mid-season periods and summer when an almost zero marginal cost 
thermal energy can replace the application of boiler . However, due to the main drawback of solar 
energy, it fluctuates on daily and seasonal basis and has the highest heat availability during summer, 
while the highest heat demand during winter. 
1.1 Solar district heating plant in Denmark 
It is a common sense that the building sector consumes 40% of total society energy consumption in 
European countries[1]. Among this sector, space heating and domestic hot water has covered more 
than 80% of the total energy consumption. 84% of the energy demand for space heating and 
domestic hot water is still provided by fossil energy system, while the remaining 16% comes from 
renewable energy. Based on the economic and energy optimization, solar collector fields were 
suggested to be integrated in the district energy system. 
As an application of solar energy, large scale solar collector fields are very popular in district heating 
system in Denmark[2]. Even though the solar radiation source is not favorable at the high latitudes 
compared to many other regions(the global radiation on the horizontal surface is between 1,000 and 
1,150 kWh/m2/year, and the winter in Denmark is quite cold, with an average temperatures about 
13-17 вДГ). As is stated in TianвАЩs research [2], nowadays, more than 64% household heating demand

1.2 Seasonal heat storage 
In order to solve the main drawback of solar energy as mentioned before, seasonal thermal energy 
storage (STES) was proposed to compensate the mismatch between heat production and energy 
demand. The following figure shows the role and cycle of a seasonal thermal energy storage in a 
district heating system with exemplary load during curves.  
 
Fig 1.2  The role and cycle of a seasonal thermal energy storage in a district heating system[5] 
STES has a long history to trace back for utilization of underground heat sources. Since the late 
1970s, the use of underground spaces as an energy storage medium вАУ Underground Thermal Energy 
Storage (UTES) вАУ has been investigated and closely observed in experimental plants in many 
countries[6]. During 1960s, seasonal storage of solar thermal energy was proposed in the USA. And 
the first demonstration plants were built up in Sweden in 1978-1979 based on the results from a 
national research program. 
Seasonal heat storage can be classified in two main categories, short-term heat storage and long-

term heat storage, depending on the storage timing requirements. Short-term heat storage system 
has a storage capacity from a few hours to a maximum of one week; long-term storage refers to a 
system has a storage requirement up to three or four months.  
Seasonal storage requires great volume and can involve a large amount of energy to be stored. The 
working principle for seasonal heat storage system, is in general, storing the solar heat collected 
during summer period, and release energy for space heating demand during winter period. In winter, 
the heat pump extracts heat from the water, and in summer, it extracts the heat from the building to 
store it in the water. Because of the large required volume of water for seasonal storage purposes, 
heat stores are in most of the cases, installed in the ground or placed close to the surface.  
Systems using the natural underground sites for storing thermal energy are called Underground 
Thermal Energy Storage (UTES) systems, and they are mostly applied for seasonal heat/cold storage.

A mixture of water and rocks is sometimes utilized as storage medium in some existing pits, which 
called gravel-water pit[7]. Heat is charged into and discharged out of the storage medium either by 
direct water exchange or by plastic piping installed in different layers inside the pit[8]. 
Compared to water pit storage, water-gravel pit technology can reduce the construction cost since 
no structural frame is necessary. Water tank technology does not need excavation operations or such 
a large surface, but results more expensive because of the tank construction and the structure is quite 
visible. And the upper part of a water-gravel pit can be used as a part of a residential area. [6] However, 
the gravel-water mixture has a lower specific heat than water. As a result, it needs more volume to 
store the same energy than a water tank design, which is approximately 50% larger than an 
equivalent water pit heat storage system to obtain the same heat storage capacity.[6]

1.3 Pit thermal energy storage  
As water pit heat is an essential part of district heating system, it can easily integrate several kinds 
of renewable energy.[8] 
Water pit thermal energy storage (PTES) concept has the most significant advantage, it is possible 
for quick charging and discharging, and has relatively short storage period. Furthermore, because 
of the higher thermal capacity, water is an ideal storage medium to be used compared to water-
gravel mixture. Based on all the above mentioned advantages, water pit heat storage systems are 
currently the most widely-used and reliable systems for seasonal heat storage application. [9]  
1.3.1 Geometry of PTES  
Water PTES is often constructed using a geometry with a slope, for example pyramid stump with a 
rectangular cross-section and upside-down placement, or a truncated cone with a circular cross 
section. 
As Yakai Bai[10] stated in his research, the optimum geometry should be a sphere or cylinder due to 
their smaller external surface areas and substantially larger storage volume. Because of the 
decreasing surface-to-volume (A/V) ratio, a large volume of a buried storage tends to minimize 
positively the external heat losses through the envelope. According to this, it is a necessity to design 
and built the storage system with a maximum volume and a minimum surface area. However, in the 
reality, for the convenience of construction, in most of the cases water pit were built with the 
geometry of either a reversed pyramid stump with a rectangular cross-section or a reversed truncated 
cone with a circular cross-section. The following figures shows the shapes of typical water pit

1.3.2 Envelope of buried PTES 
In water pit thermal energy systems, external heat losses frequently take place, because of the heat 
transfer between the storage body and the ground due to the large interface area. As a result, it is 
important to design the envelope, with suitable insulations with the aim of minimizing the heat 
losses in accordance with economic aspects. PTES is usually enclosed by thick insulation layers 
forming a composite wall. And, in the composite wall, the insulation layers can be introduced in the 
envelope either inside or outside the main construction material. 
Since the thermal losses happened in large-scale heat storage system is mostly moisture transfer, 
liners are therefore introduced to the internal side of the tank, in order to prevent water from leakage. 
Besides, liners can also help to reduce heat loss by providing tightness against vapor transfer through 
walls. Otherwise, the vapor will gather in the PTESвАЩs insulation layers and cause damage, resulting 
a even higher heat loss.[7] Liners are commonly made of stainless steel, high- and low-density 
polyethylene (HDPE, LDPE) or polypropylene (PP). 
1.4 Simulation and modelling 
It is especially costly to build up a water pit storage system. Therefore, it is necessary to simulate 
and model the thermal performance and energy consumption issues of a storage body before 
construction, in order to minimize the possibility of risk.  
Computational fluid dynamic (CFD) and TRNSYS are two main methods to carry out simulations 
==PAGE:17==
 
Chapter 1  Introduction 
 
 
8 
for water pit heat storage system. 
1.4.1 CFD modelling 
CFD modelling is frequently carried out when doing simulation for water pit heat storage system. 
Researchers have did a lot of work in this aspect. 
J.Fan et.al [11] investigated the thermal behavior of a 75,000 m3 water pit heat storage in Marstal 
solar heating plant. A CFD simulation model was created for the water pit storage to investigate the 
development of temperatures in and around the storage body. The investigation result shows the 
thermal stratification situation of the PTES was obtained. In addition, it is shown that return 
temperature back to the water pit is much higher than the temperature at the bottom of the pit. As a 
result, strong mixing will be caused and will destroy the stratification in the lower part of the pit. 
Another research group, C.Chang et.al [12] studied the heat loss of a pyramid shaped water pit 
heat storage system, with the aim of investigating the mechanism for thermal stratification driven 
by buoyancy. Therefore, a two-dimensional axisymmetric CFD model was built up and simulated 
in order to analyze 100,000 m3 water pit with various geometries based. Their study research shows 
that deeper water pits and steeper side wall can result in a more significant thermal stratification and 
a better thermal efficiency.    
However, it is a common sense that CFD simulation is always an extremely time and effort 
consuming work. In addition, all of the CFD simulations mentioned above were either based on 
short period analyses or just considered in the standby mode. Therefore, researchers thought that 
using CFD calculation as a method to simulate seasonal heat storage plant may not be a practical 
way. 
1.4.2 TRNSYS modelling 
Instead of the conventional CFD method, recently, more and more water pit heat storage system 
simulations were carried out based on TRNSYS.  
Transient Simulation (TRNSYS) is a compiler for a high level computer designed specifically to 
==PAGE:18==
 
Chapter 1  Introduction 
 
 
9 
connect component models of transient systems and differential equations describing the system. 
TRNSYS is particularly applicable for solar energy system simulations, because a library of 
component subroutines modeling the components common in these systems has been established. 
This program, is generally in the sense that it can be used to simulate any transient system for which 
the system component models are expressible by FORTRAN statements. [13] 
It is an attractive way to implement a simplified method that can simulate the water pit region with 
a one-dimensional model and the surrounding soil region with a two-dimensional axisymmetric 
model, considering about the difficulty to use CFD method to simulate a large-scale water pit.[10] 
Now Type XST and Type ICEPIT model used to simulate large-scale water pit storage system have 
been already available in TRNSYS component library. It means that more and more one dimensional 
models for seasonal heat storage systems have been developed and are commercially available now. 
1.5 Dronninglund Solar District Heating Plant 
1.5.1 Development History  
Dronninglund solar thermal plant is located at Lunderbjerg, in the west of Dronninglund. It is a 
consumer-owned cooperative. It became the first Danish district heating company to install natural 
gas-driven engines for combined heat and power production in 1989. [14] 
By 2005, more and more Danish district heating companies had started to install solar thermal plants, 
which covered almost 20% of the yearly heat production. Dronninglund Fjermvarme also realized 
that it is a necessity to use renewable energy to replace natural gas to generate heat. Based on this 
idea, Dronninglund Fjermvarme aspired to cover up to 50% of the yearly production with solar 
heating. The new project was subsidized by EUDP пЉИEnergy Technology Development and 
Demonstration Progarmme) and financed by the Danish state. [14] 
In 2006, it was a must for Dronninglund Fjernvarme to find a suitable replacement for two oil boilers. 
The incentives for this update can be concluded to four parts[14]:  
вЪЂ 
A higher tax on natural gas was implemented (same level as the gas price itself);

No tax on solar energy;  
вЪЂ

As a result, solar energy was introduced to this solar heating plant, after May 2014, solar heating 
had covered 40% of the total energy production. The last approval for the project upgrade was given 
in October 2012, with the objective to optimize, design and implement a full scale demonstration 
plant with 35,000 m2 thermal collectors, 60,000 m3 pit heat storage and a heat pump.[14] And the 
plant were finally opened to the public in May 2014. 
1.5.2 Executive Summary for Dronninglund Fjernvarme 
Water pit heat storage is a relatively cheap and efficient method to store energy, as the heat produced 
in summer can be stored for use in winter. Traditional solar heating plant cover heat demand by only 
15-20%. But with a heat storage pit, solar heating can cover 50-60% of demand. [15]  
A heat storage pit resembles an enormous buried thermos that utilizes heat from various sources 
such as the sun and wind power, combined heat and power (CHP) and residual heat from industrial 
plants. As for this project, the main components in Dronninglund Fjernvarme solar district heating 
plant are a large solar thermal plant and a pit heat water storage. The plant, supported by the national 
Danish EUDP-program, is currently able to deliver heating to the consumers from 70% renewable 
energy sources where approximately 40% is solar heat. [16]

2.2  Introduction of Original Model 
The TRNSYS model used in this project was developed by Yakai Bai. Figure 2.8 shows a sketch of 
the reversed truncated cone water pit that Bai studied.  
==PAGE:32==
 
Chapter 2  Basic Information for Model 
 
 
23
 
Fig 2.8  A sketch of the reversed truncated cone water pit 
As stated in [10], the model was built up based on several assumption: 
a. There is no rust on the heat transfer surface of the water pit that will reduce the heat transfer 
rate; 
b. The soil is homogeneous and the influences of humidity and other factors on heat transfer is 
ignored; 
c. Mixing resulted from the incoming water flow in the pit region is not considered; 
d. Water in water pit at the same height has the same temperature; 
e. There is no temperature gradient in the radial direction in the water region (caused by thermal 
stratification in the water pit region). 
 
Fig 2.9  Geometry of the symmetrical model (half part) 
With the mentioned assumptions, the water pit storage can be simplified to a one-dimensional model 
for the water region, and a two-dimensional model for the soil and insulations . Since the PTES 
==PAGE:33==
 
Chapter 2  Basic Information for Model 
 
 
24
model is designed in a symmetrical way, half part of the geometry structure of BaiвАЩs model is shown 
in Figure 2.9. The insulation at the edge of the water pit region is marked in yellow. The model was 
divided into five different parts, where parts I to IV are soil and insulation regions, part V is water 
region.  
2.3  Coordination for Transformation Method 
In order to find a way that can easily represent the boundaries and conditions, a coordinate 
transformation method was introduced. Bai[10] implemented the coordinate transformation method 
in four main procedures: 
a. Found a suitable coordinate transformation function to transfer the original complicated 
boundary conditions to a more simple one; 
b. Generated suitable mesh for the new geometry after coordination; 
c. Rebuild the control equations and boundary conditions; 
d. Implement infinite difference method. 
Figure 2.10 shows the new geometry of model after coordination, and Figure 2.11 shows the mesh 
scheme for the water pit after coordination. As can be seen in Figure 2.11, the water body is divided 
equally into nw nodes, and was numbered from 1 to nw from the top to the bottom of the water pit. 
 
Fig 2.10  Geometry of BaiвАЩs model after coordination 
==PAGE:34==
 
Chapter 2  Basic Information for Model 
 
 
25
 
Fig 2.11  Mesh scheme for the water pit region (in new coordination) [10] 
The thickness of the concrete wall and the air layer above the water level is very small 
compared to the diameter of the water pit body, therefore they were neglected in the soil and 
insulation region and were treated as thermal resistance.[10] Insulation thickness at the top of 
the water pit region is still considered in this model although it is actually very small. The size 
of nodes created along the thickness of the insulations near the top, the bottom and the side 
wall were ќФрЭЬВ0, ќФрЭЬВ0 and ќФрЭЬЙ0 respectively. For the soil region, the size of the node in the radial 
and the axial direction will increase with a factor рЭЫљрЭЬЙ and рЭЫљрЭЬВ respectively. As can be seen from 
Figure 2.11, there is a denser grid applied in the adjacent area of the water pit where a large 
temperature gradient is expected. 
It is also very essential to take the heat loss from the water pit region to the soil into account. 
In order to simulate the semi-infinite boundary in the soil, a soil region is created in the model 
with a radius five times of the radius of water pit (Radius =5R2). Since the bury depth of the 
water pit is d, the domain extent of the soil is five times the height of the water pit plus bury 
depth (Depth = 5H + d). [20] 
Detailed heat transfer equations and control equations for the soil and insulation regions will 
not be mentioned in this paper. However, detailed information about this part can be found in 
BaiвАЩs research [10].

3.1 Solar district heating plant at Dronninglund 
Operating data of a 60,000 m2 water pit installed at Dronninglund, Denmark built in 2014 was 
employed to validate BaiвАЩs model. The measured charging and discharging parameters, as well as 
some initial conditions were taken as input data for TRNSYS simulation. The measurement time 
step was set as 10min.  
As is shown in Figure 3.1, this PTES was just buried underneath the ground level with a insulation 
thickness of 0.24 m installed on the PTES top. Air layer thickness and concrete wall thickness were 
neglected in this PTES model. 
Three opening were installed inside the water region for charging and discharging usage. The upper 
opening was placed at 16m high from the bottom; the middle opening was placed 11.5m high from 
the bottom while the lower opening was placed 0.5m high from the bottom. In order to determine 
the initial condition and to validate the model, temperature and flow rate sensors were installed in 
both PTES and soil region. Detailed information for measurement sensors were listed in Chapter

Chapter 4  Conclusion 
This project explores the operation performance of Dronninglund solar heating plantвАЩs pit thermal 
energy storage system. A simple simulation model for reversed pyramid water pit was built up in 
TRNSYS. The newly developed model simulates the water region with a one-dimensional model 
and the surrounding soil region with a two-dimensional axisymmetric model. The experimental data 
from a 60,000 m3 storage volume PTES in Dronninglund solar heating plant was used to validate 
the model. 
Pit thermal energy storage system is a very promising technology, using the heat storage capacity 
of water, is the most fast-developed seasonal heat storage technology. Denmark has witnessed the 
fast development of solar district heating, several solar district heating plant with PTES system have 
been built up with the sponsor from the government. It is necessary to simulate the system 
performance in advance, in order to found out the potential problems as well as proposing the 
decision scheme. Therefore, this project investigated the heat loss distribution, tried to determine 
the thermal stratification condition inside PTES, determined the charged and discharged energy as 
well as show how the PTES influence the surrounding soil temperature. 
The experiment measured data for the PTES yearly heat loss in 2015 is 1275 MWh. The total heat 
loss can be separated into three categories: heat loss from the top, heat loss from the side and heat 
loss from the bottom. After carried out simulations based on the new model structured in TRNSYS, 
the total heat loss for 2015 is calculated to be 1030 MWh. With the top heat loss ends up to be 667 
MWh (65% of total), side heat loss ends up to be 321 MWh (31% of total) and bottom heat loss to 
be 41 MWh (4% of total). Heat loss from the PTES top covered the most of the total heat loss, 
because of the relatively larger surface area and better heat conduction capacity. To sum up, the 
differential rate between experimental and simulated heat loss amount is calculated to be 19.2%. 
In addition to heat loss, thermal stratification inside PTES region is also an important indicator to 
show the system performance as well as validating the model. PTES inner temperature at 24 
different levels were simulated in TRNSYS, the results were compared to measured ones. Inner 
temperature at the top/ middle/ bottom openingsвАЩ positions were investigated, simulated and 
==PAGE:78==
 
Chapter 4  Conclusion 
 
 
69
measured values matched very well in this case. Besides, in order to improve the validation of 
thermal stratification as well as the temperature distribution inside the PTES. A comparison between 
the calculated average PTES temperature based on both simulated and measured data was carried 
out, the result also shows a good matching. Therefore, based on the two-dimensional comparison, 
the thermal stratification cases for the TRNSYS model was validated. 
The energy content of the TRNSYS model was also investigated, and compared with the 
experimental data to validate. The yearly total charged energy for the simulation PTES is 13718 
MWh, for the experimental data is 13266 MWh, with a differential rate of 3.4%. The annual total 
discharged energy for the simulated and measurement results are 12540 MWh and 12242 MWh, 
respectively. The error rate for discharged energy is then calculated to be 2.4%. In addition, monthly 
comparison for discharged/charged energy based on simulation and measurement results was carried 
out. It shows that charged energy has the lowest differential rate in April 2015 with only 0.1%, while 
the highest value in November 2015 with 23.1%. While for the discharged energy case, the lowest 
value occurred at April as well (0.1%), and the highest one happened in August, with 28.7%. Besides, 
charged/discharged comparison was also proposed based on 12 different charging/discharging 
situations. After calculations and comparisons, category 12 enjoys the lowest error rate of 4%, while 
category 4 has the highest value of 27%. Furthermore, the internal energy inside PTES region 
throughout 2015 was also compared. Based on the simulated data, this value ends up to be -394 
MWh, while for the measurement case, the internal energy change ends up to be -497. The error 
rate is 21%, which is a bit large. This can be explained by a problem when initializing the original 
thermal stratification condition inside PTES in the TRNSYS model. 
The temperature distribution situation of the PTES surrounding soil region is also an essential part 
to validate the model, as the soil region is an important part in the TRNSYS model. Therefore, 
temperatures of four temperature sensors placed at different buried height were investigated and 
compared with the measurement data. The results show that instead of the soil temperature at the 
buried depth of 10m, other temperature can match the experimental data very well. Furthermore, 2-
D expressions of the temperature distribution at the start of each month in 2015 were also shown. 
Based on these two aspects, the soil region in the TRNSYS model is therefore validated. 
==PAGE:79==
 
Chapter 4  Conclusion 
 
 
70
Finally, some key PTES system performance indicators were investigated (PTES storage efficiency, 
number of storage cycles, maximum and minimum temperature etc.). Calculated value for storage 
efficiency based on experimental and simulated data are 90% and 88.5%, respectively. For the 
number of storage cycles, the results for measured and modeled data are 2.2 and 2.4.  
According to all the mentioned comparisons between measured and experimental data for several 
different parameters as stated above. The new coordinated TRNSYS model designed for 
Dronninglund solar district heating systemвАЩ s PTES region was therefore validated. The next step 
for the further optimization can be some data analysis work.: Change the geometry of PTES region, 
change the material and thickness of the insulation layer, as well as changing the buried depth and 
the air layer thickness etc. can all be available options. In this project, no further investigation will 
be carried out with all the potential parameter analysis.
