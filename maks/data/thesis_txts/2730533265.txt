Abstract
Image analysis and classiﬁcation play a crucial role in the ﬁeld of automated in-
spection of photovoltaic cells. This thesis explores the use of deep learning and
computer vision techniques for defect classiﬁcation in electroluminescent images of
photovoltaic cells. Furthermore, the study focuses on evaluating the model’s per-
formance in detecting four di↵erent defect classes. A modular CNN model based
on the ResNet family serves as foundation for an extensive hyperparameter study.
The performance of the model is assessed using several performance metrics, thus
enabling a thorough evaluation of the prediction outcome. The developed model
performs well in detecting defects which cover large cell areas, while it has diﬃ-
culties to detect defects with narrow shapes. The thesis highlights the importance
of class-speciﬁc data augmentations methods and loss function weights to increase
the model’s performance and to prevent overﬁtting. Furthermore, the comparison
of binary and multiclass classiﬁcation shows that cells containing multiple defect
types a↵ect the model’s ability to correctly classify defects. In conclusion, this the-
sis demonstrates the potential of deep learning in the context of photovoltaic cell
inspection, paving the way for future improvements in automated defect detection
through advanced machine learning algorithms.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
The increasing global emphasis on renewable energy sources, particularly solar en-
ergy, is supported by the target of achieving global net-zero carbon emissions by
2050, formulated by the International Energy Agency [3]. Wind, hydroelectric, and
solar power are the foremost contributors to the renewable energy landscape. These
technologies’ expansion and further development form the basis for achieving the
net-zero carbon emission goal. As shown in Figure 1.1, prices for producing and

1.1.1
Structure of solar panels
In solar power plants, sunlight is absorbed and converted into electricity with the
help of solar arrays. A solar array consists of multiple solar panels, also known
as solar modules. These panels, again, are a matrix arrangement of multiple solar
cells, which are of size 15x15 cm2.
A solar cell is made up of di↵erent layers, as visualised in Figure 1.3(b). The lowest
layer is made of a conductive metal plate, which serves as the positive electrode
to collect all electrons. A semiconductor forms the second layer, absorbing sun-
light and generating solar power. A metal grid is placed upon the semiconductor,
which collects and transports all electrons from the cell to the electric grid. The
thicker metal bars covering the whole solar cell are called bus bars, while the small
metal bars that leave perpendicularly from the bus bars are called ﬁngers. The
semiconductor is covered by an anti-reﬂective coating to maximise the solar input.
A glass covers the solar cell to protect the silicon from any external hazards and
degradation.

ple crystals forming a heterogeneous structure. The boundaries of each crystal are

A silicon atom has exactly four valence electrons. Each atom forms a total of 4
bonds with its neighbouring atoms. To generate free-ﬂoating electrons, which are
required to generate power, a part of the silicon is doped with an element containing
ﬁve valence electrons, e.g., Phosphorus. This half is called the N-junction. On the
other half of the crystal, the silicon is doped with an element containing only three
valence electrons, e.g., Boron. This half is called the P-junction. The depletion
region describes the boundary of both these junctions, where electrons from the
N-junction start to ﬁll the holes in the electron shell of the atoms in the P-junction.
This creates an electric ﬁeld due to the positively charged donor atoms on one side
and the negatively charged receiver atoms on the other. Figure 1.4(a) visualises the
P-N junction.

1.1.2
Power Output
The amount of solar power harnessed is directly proportional to the area which
captures sunlight and, therefore, to the number of solar panels installed and the
eﬃciency of converting sunlight to power for each cell. Simpliﬁed, the capacity of a
power plant can be calculated as follows:
Capacity = Area ⇥Solar Irradiance ⇥Eﬃciency ⇥Performance Ratio
A power plant’s solar capacity [W] describes the maximum amount of power that
could be generated, assuming that the panels are always perpendicular to the in-

cident sun rays and operate under maximum solar irradiance of 1000 W m−2. The
area [m2] describes the cumulative surface of solar cells capturing the sunlight. The
eﬃciency is how much incoming sunlight can be converted to power, ranging from
0.13 to 0.25. The performance ratio accounts for any additional power losses in the
system, such as converting direct to alternate current or heat losses. A typical value
for the performance ratio is in the range of 0.75 to 0.85.
1.1.3
Maintenance
To generate the highest possible power output, both the eﬃciency of converting solar
energy into electric power and the performance ratio of the whole power plant must
be maximised. Defects in solar cells or the grid can severely a↵ect the generated
electricity output of a solar power plant. In a study of 2019, the company DNV GL
found a median performance gap of 3% between expected and actual power outputs
of solar power plant [5], [6]. To minimise these power losses, solar power plants need
regular inspection and maintenance. With the inspection, two signiﬁcant challenges
arose: Solar power plants grew primarily in size with the rise and industrialisation
of solar power. For example, the 2021 commissioned solar power plant near Holste-
bro, DK, extends over 2.22 km2, generating a peak power of 207 MW [7]. This area
is the equivalent of around 300 soccer ﬁelds. Manually inspecting every solar cell
is a very tedious and time-consuming task. Additionally, detecting defects in the
semiconductor material of solar cells is not straightforward as they are not clearly
visible to the naked eye. Special equipment such as near- or thermal-infrared cam-
eras are needed, and even then, inspectors need to be speciﬁcally trained to detect
those defects.
1.1.4
Emerging of automated inspection technologies
The industrialisation of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) and advancements in
Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI) present an opportunity to fully automate the inspection
and damage reporting procedure. The use of these technologies promises to signif-
icantly reduce manual labour time and maintain or even improve the accuracy of
the inspection results.
Computer vision, a subﬁeld of AI, plays a central role in this automation. This
technology is dedicated to enabling machines to interpret and understand visual
information from the world, like the human visual system. Various tasks are un-
dertaken with computer vision, including image processing, object detection and
image classiﬁcation among others.
One crucial component of computer vision are Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNNs).
CNNs are a speciﬁc class of deep neural networks designed to handle
visual data e↵ectively. They draw inspiration from the visual processing that oc-
curs in the human brain.
CNNs consist of several building blocks, each serving the purpose of extracting
meaningful information and features.
• Convolutional layers automatically learn hierarchical features from input im-
ages. Learnable ﬁlters or kernels slide over the input image, capturing local
patterns such as edges, textures, and simple shapes.
• Pooling layers reduce the spatial dimensions of feature maps while preserving
essential information. This process aids in making the network more invariant
to small variations in input and reduces computational complexity.
• After several convolutional and pooling layers, CNNs typically have one or
more fully connected layers that perform high-level feature aggregation and

classiﬁcation. These layers produce class predictions or feature vectors.
• Non-linear activation functions like ReLU (Rectiﬁed Linear Unit) introduce
non-linearity into the model, enabling it to learn complex data relationships.
• In the context of supervised learning, CNNs are trained using labelled datasets
through backpropagation. This optimises their internal parameters, weights
and biases, to minimise the di↵erence between predicted and actual labels.
Integrating computer vision into UAVs o↵ers a comprehensive solution for solar cell
inspection. Drones equipped with high-resolution cameras can eﬃciently capture
images of solar panels. Computer vision algorithms can then process these images
on board or in a cloud-based system. The result is a detailed report highlighting
any defects found, including their type, location, and severity.
This automated approach saves time and resources and ensures a consistent and
thorough inspection of solar power plants. It can signiﬁcantly reduce the perfor-
mance gap observed in solar power plants and can be used for predictive mainte-
nance. As solar energy production continues to expand globally, the role of computer
vision and UAVs in defect classiﬁcation becomes increasingly vital for optimising
energy output and minimising maintenance costs.
In this context, this thesis explores the application of automated defect classiﬁcation
of solar cells, aiming to contribute to the eﬃciency and sustainability of solar energy
production.
1.2
Thesis Objectives and Limitations
The goal of this thesis is to create an algorithm which correctly and accurately clas-
siﬁes defects in electroluminescent images of photovoltaic cells. The algorithm shall
use deep learning techniques and computer vision to achieve this goal. Furthermore,
the thesis aims to answer the following questions:
• Can defects be accurately detected and correctly classiﬁed?
• Are certain defects better detectable than others?
• Do multiple defects in a single cell inﬂuence the prediction results?
• Do deeper networks yield better prediction performance?
• Does an additional classiﬁer that further processes the output vector increase
the model performance?
• How do di↵erent hyperparameters inﬂuence the classiﬁcation performance of
the model?
The thesis focuses on the task of classifying defects within the speciﬁc dataset
provided for this task.
Its performance is not tested on other datasets, which
limits the assessment of the generalisability of the model. Additionally, this thesis
solely focuses on the task of single-label defect classiﬁcation.
The use of image
segmentation as well as multi-label classiﬁcation is not part of this work.

2.1
Related Work
With the task of automatically detecting and classifying defects in electrolumines-
cent images, several challenges arise that require careful and critical considerations.
Firstly, acquiring a suitable dataset is not straight forward as in terms of accessi-
bility, only little data on electroluminescent images of photovoltaic cells is publicly
available.
Furthermore, when creating the dataset, a complex and standardised
setup is required to guarantee the quality of the images. Additionally, extensive
manual labour is required to correctly label defects in image data. Moreover, cor-
rectly recognising and classifying defects needs to be done by trained experts. Sec-
ondly, an algorithm has to be developed which is capable of recognising di↵erent
defect shapes and patterns and clearly distinguish them from other elements in a
cell. The algorithm’s performance highly depends on the number of defects in the
image data. Meanwhile, most available datasets show a high imbalance between
the No Defect and Defect class, complicating the learning procedure of generalis-
able defect patterns.
To address these challenges, extensive research has already been conducted in the
ﬁeld of defect detection and classiﬁcation in electroluminescent images of photo-
voltaic cells, providing the foundation for the analysis at hand.
The following papers were crucial for the deﬁnition of the model architecture and
the experimental setup and model evaluation:
Bartler et al. [8] demonstrated the eﬃcacy of deep CNN adapted from the VGG16
architecture. Their study tackled the issue of imbalanced datasets through minority
oversampling and data augmentation, signiﬁcantly reducing the false negative rate
and underscoring the crucial inﬂuence data imbalance, which forms a challenge for
the dataset at hand.
Tella et al. [? ] focused on testing various deep learning networks, including ResNet
variants, on a dataset of EL images with high class imbalances. Their approach for
class imbalance handling included rotatianal and brightness augmentation methods,
which are used in thesis. Furthermore, their expolaritve analysis of di↵erent CNNs
revealed that the ResNet family, especially the ResNet34, perform well for this task.
Deitsch et al. [9] explored binary defect classiﬁcation for both poly- and monocrys-
talline photovoltaic cells.
They implemented two approaches, one using feature
description together with Support Vector Machines (SVM) and deep CNN with an
additional classiﬁer. The CNN was conﬁgured with an additional classiﬁer, which
further processes the output vector of the CNN. Their results show that while the
SVM approach is very time eﬃcient, the use of deep CNNs yields better accuracy.
Furthermore, the additional classiﬁer enhances model performance, thus inspiring

Tang et al. [10] developed a novel data augmentation strategy for EL images, com-

Their CNN-based

Pratt et al. [11] performed image segmentation on both multi- and polycrystalline
cells, analyzing di↵erent segmentation models and the inﬂuence of loss function class
weights. Their work showed that the choice of inverse or customised weights highly
a↵ects the model performance and can improve the output accuracy. Furthermore,
their test results highlight the challenge of detecting narrow defect shapes, such
as cracks and inactive regions, which are similar to certain defects in the present
dataset.
Chen et al. [12] introduced an automated data pipeline to classify cell defects from
solar modules. Their approach uses deep learning with di↵erent residual networks
and the YOLO network, which both yielded similar performance. Their dataset
used for training showed a high imbalance between defect and intact cells, a↵ecting
the model performance for strongly underrepresented classes.
Zhang et al. [13] introduced a novel neural network for detecting defects in EL
images, emphasizing global pairwise similarity and concatenated saliency. Their
method enhances feature reﬁnement in conventional CNNs, e↵ectively representing
long, thin defects and di↵erentiating between defects and noises. Comparing these
enhancements to baseline models like VGG16 and ResNet50 showed a performance
increase in identifying defects in both monocrystalline and polycrystalline solar cells.
Chen et al. [14] integrated a convolutional block attention module (CBAM) into
a Faster R-CNN framework, enhancing the feature extraction process to more ef-
fectively distinguish defect features in complex backgrounds like those of polycrys-
talline cells. Their approach showed an improvement in average accuracy for surface
defect detection, highlighting the need for background noise ﬁltering.
Cao et al. [15] introduced an improved photovoltaic surface defect detection method,
enhancing both accuracy and inference speed for building photovoltaic inspections.
This method, an advancement of the YOLO-v5 algorithm, incorporates attention
mechanisms to reﬁne defect detection while reducing model complexity. It outper-
formed the original YOLO-v5 in both accuracy and speed, demonstrating a balance
between these crucial factors in defect detection. The study underscores the ongo-
ing need for eﬃcient and accurate photovoltaic maintenance solutions, which aligns
with the overarching thesis goal of implementing the algorithm on an onboard drone
computer for automated inspection.
A subset of the dataset at hand was used by Mantel et al. [16] for defect detection
and classiﬁcation in EL images. They implemented the Hough and percentile region
extraction algorithms, and further extracted 25 features for each region detected.
The features are classiﬁed using Support Vector Machine and Random Forest al-
gorithms, coupled with undersampling and SMOTE oversampling for imbalanced
dataset handling.
Thomsen and Mantel [17] introduced an innovative approach to deep learning-based
defect classiﬁcation in solar cells by incorporating conformal prediction into an
active learning framework. This method improved the model’s performance and
managed the uncertainty in predictions. The model calculated conﬁdence levels for
its predictions, sending low-conﬁdence ones for expert evaluation, thereby eﬃciently
integrating human expertise in the loop. This approach demonstrated e↵ectiveness
in handling the imbalanced dataset at hand and provided a framework for ongoing
model improvement with real-world data.

Table 3.1 shows the high imbalance between the number of cells with no defects and

In total, nine di↵erent augmentation

The following list explains
the applied augmentation methods, while Figure3.3 visualises the e↵ect of each
augmentation compared to the original image.
• Vertical ﬂip: The image is mirrored at its x-axis.
• Horizontal ﬂip: The image is mirrored at its y-axis.
• 180-degree rotation: The image is rotated 180 degrees around the center.
• Color jitter: The brightness and contrast of the image are randomly modi-
ﬁed. Brightness a↵ects the overall lightness and darkness of the image. It is
adjusted by randomly multiplying every pixel by a brightness factor between
0.8 and 1.2. Additionally, the contrast of the image is changed. Contrast
describes the spread of the intensities of pixel values. Lowering the contrast
brings all pixel intensities closer together, while increasing it spreads the pixel
values apart. It is adjusted by a factor between 0.8 and 1.2.
• Noise addition: Every pixel value is randomly increased by up to 0.1 times
its initial pixel value.

A neural network is built using a deep learning approach combined with transfer
learning to perform the classiﬁcation experiments. The model used for this thesis
combines an existing CNN and a self-developed multi-layer classiﬁer, which further
processes the output vector of the CNN. This modiﬁed model architecture is inspired
by Deitsch et al. [9]. For the CNN, the residual network (ResNet) family is used,

trained on the ImageNet are pre-loaded to utilise the beneﬁts of transfer learning
[18]. Figure 3.4 visualises the di↵erent building blocks of the proposed model.

deep layer neural networks by introducing skip-connections, which bypass one or
multiple layers. He et al. [19] showed in their work that ResNets perform better in
image classiﬁcation tasks than plain deep neural networks with the same amount
of layers.

In the ﬁrst step, the data is split into three sets: training, validation and testing
set. 70% of the data is used to train the model, and each 15% of the data is used

3.3.2
Model Conﬁguration
The ’pre-trained model’-parameter deﬁnes which network is used for the ﬁrst part
of the CNN described in Section 3.2. The ”depth classiﬁer”-parameter represents
the number of additional fully connected layers in the second part of the designed
model. Each layer is activated by the deﬁned activation function. Dropout is ap-
plied, which randomly sets a fraction of neurons to zero to prevent overﬁtting.
The error is minimised using a loss function, which minimises a deﬁned norm be-
tween the true labels and their predictions. The importance of certain labels can
be weighted higher than others, which can be adjusted by the ’weight adjustment’
parameter. Di↵erent loss functions use di↵erent adjusting parameters, which are
explained in more detail in Chapter 4. To converge towards a solution, di↵erent
optimisers may be used. During training, the learning rate decays step-wise for
convergence.
3.3.3
Model Training and Validation
The model runs through two training cycles, inspired by the enhanced performance
of this approach stated by Deitsch et al. [9]. In the ﬁrst cycle, only the neurons of
the classiﬁer are trained, while the neurons of the pre-trained model are kept frozen.
Based on the insights of Can et al. [15], the model stops automatically if the AUC
of the validation set has not increased over a certain amount of epochs, which is
deﬁned by the patience parameter. In the second cycle, all layers are unfrozen,
and the model is again trained with the same automatic stop mechanism as in
the ﬁrst layer. Applying the test data on the newly trained model yields the class
probabilities for every image in the test set, which are saved for further processing.

Receiver operating Characteristic Curve and Area-under-Curve
The neural network outputs a vector with a length equal to the number of classes.
The ith-entry is equal to the class probability of how conﬁdent the neural network
is in its prediction. As default, the threshold for classifying defects is 50%. All
defects that have a probability above this threshold are TP, and all others are FN.
Similarly, all images with no defect and a probability below the threshold are TN,
otherwise they are FP. Lowering this threshold increases the number of TP and FP.
Increasing the threshold correctly classiﬁes more images without defects but also
decreases the model’s ability to detect images with defects.

3.4. Performance Metrics
A perfect model has an AUC of 1 and predicts all classes correctly at any given
threshold.
Precision-Recall Curve and Average-Precision
The F1-Score calculated with equation 3.3 is a speciﬁc combination of Precision
and Recall at a certain threshold. The Precision-Recall (PR) curve shown in Figure
3.8 gives a deeper understanding of the relation between Precision and Recall at
di↵erent thresholds. Additionally, the isolines for di↵erent F5-Scores are plotted as
dashed grey lines. For a classiﬁcation task where overseeing a defect is worse than
misclassifying a cell as defective, choosing a di↵erent classiﬁcation threshold with a
higher Recall and an acceptable low Precision might be preferable. Therefore, the
F5-Score is a better-suited performance metric than the F1-Score for this task.
Similar to the AUC, the area under the PR curve can be calculated. This results
in the Average-Precision (AP), another metric to assess the model’s performance.
The AP is set to [0,1], where 1 means the model perfectly identiﬁes defects with no
FP classiﬁcations and 0 that no defect is correctly classiﬁed.
Mutliclass Classiﬁcation
In the context of a multiclass classiﬁcation problem, where a one-vs-rest (OvR)
strategy is employed, the performance metrics are adapted to handle multiple classes
by treating each class as a separate binary classiﬁcation task. In this scenario, each
class is considered as positive class (class 1), while all other classes are combined
into a single negative class (class 0). This approach calculates the performance met-
rics for each class individually, which are then averaged to obtain a comprehensive
evaluation of the model’s overall performance. The averaging methods – macro,
micro, and weighted – play a crucial role in this process:
Macro Averaging in the multiclass setting computes the metric for each class inde-
pendently and then takes the average, treating all classes equally. This approach is
particularly useful for understanding the model’s performance in each class without
giving undue weight to the more prevalent classes.
Micro Averaging aggregates the outcomes of all classes to compute the overall met-
ric. It sums up the individual true positives, false positives, and false negatives
for all classes and then calculates the metrics. This method reﬂects of the model’s
performance on the majority class.
Weighted averaging takes a di↵erent approach by computing the metric for each
class independently but then averaging them weighted by the number of true in-
stances in each class.

4.5.3
Label smoothing Loss Function
As described in Section 3.1.1, each image in the dataset was carefully reviewed
by experts in the ﬁeld, who double-checked images labelled as defects. As images
labelled as No defect were only inspected once, a small uncertainty remains that a
defect was overseen. Furthermore, the classiﬁcation of Cracks is partly qualitative,
as there is no quantitative threshold for classifying a Crack in each category. This
uncertainty can be taken into account during the training process by applying label
smoothing to each class.
Label smoothing operates on the principle of diluting the absolute certainty tradi-
tionally associated with one-hot encoded labels. It redistributes a small fraction of
the conﬁdence from the target class to other classes. This redistribution is quantiﬁed
by a class-speciﬁc smoothing parameter ✏, which is chosen to balance maintaining
the true label’s integrity and incorporating a degree of uncertainty. Mathematically,
==PAGE:62==
Chapter 4. Results
44
for a classiﬁcation problem with C classes, the modiﬁed label y0
i for the true class i
is expressed as 1 −✏i + ✏i/K, while the labels for all other classes j 6= i are adjusted
to ✏i/K. This approach not only addresses the issue of potential label inaccuracies
but also serves as a form of regularisation, discouraging the model from ﬁtting too
closely to the training data.
Introducing label smoothing does not result in a performance increase. The results
of this experiment can be found in Appendix A.3.2.

Chapter 5
Discussion
5.0.1
Interpretation of Results
This thesis set out to tackle the task of defect classiﬁcation in electroluminescent
images of photovoltaic cells using state-of-the-art deep learning and computer vi-
sion techniques. The primary objective was to investigate the most e↵ective neural
network architecture and hyperparameters for accurate and eﬃcient classiﬁcation
of various defect types. Speciﬁcally, the thesis aimed to understand the impact of
di↵erent defect types on classiﬁcation accuracy and explored how the presence of
multiple defects in a single cell inﬂuences prediction results.
A robust methodology was employed, centring around the use of ResNets. The
choice of ResNet as the foundational architecture was informed by its proven eﬃ-
cacy in previous research in the ﬁeld [12]. The conducted experiments were compre-
hensive, involving variations in model architecture as well as di↵erent combinations
of hyperparameters.
The use of diverse performance metrics such as Precision,
Recall, F5-Score and AUC provided a deeper understanding of the model’s perfor-
mance, ensuring a thorough evaluation beyond mere accuracy. The results of the
experiments yielded several insights.
Model Architecture
Contrary to initial expectations, deeper ResNets do not signiﬁcantly improve the
classiﬁcation performance.
This ﬁnding suggests that for the task of classifying
defects in the provided dataset of electroluminescent images, a moderate network
depth, such as that o↵ered by ResNet34, suﬃces.
This is a crucial ﬁnding, as
it shows that deeper networks do not always result in a higher classiﬁcation per-
formance.
This allows for more computational eﬃciency in ﬁnding the optimal
hyperparameters of the model.
Additionally, the results show that further processing the output vector of the
ResNet results in higher model performance compared to just making the pre-
dictions based on this output vector. The model achieves the highest prediction
performance with a classiﬁer depth of ﬁve layers. On the other hand, the results of
the experiments with di↵erent dropout rates and activation functions suggest that
the classiﬁcation task’s outcome is more robust to variations in these hyperparam-
eters.
Classiﬁcation Results
The binary and class-speciﬁc binary classiﬁcation results support the assumption
that the pixel intensities of the defect area play a crucial role in detecting defects.
Defects such as Crack B, Crack C and ﬁnger failures, showing lower pixel intensities
49
==PAGE:68==
Chapter 5. Discussion
50
in the defect area than to its surrounding, are well detected. Additionally, the shape
of the defects also inﬂuences the prediction results. For defects covering a wider
area of the cell, such as Crack B and Crack C, the model also succeeds in correctly
classifying the images containing no defects. For defects with a narrow shape, such
as the ones of Crack A defects and ﬁnger failures, the FPR is notably higher.
Furthermore, it is observed that the number of defects in the training dataset
strongly a↵ects the prediction performance, as a certain number of defects is needed
to learn the general underlying defect patterns. While data augmentation methods
are a reliable to reduce this imbalance, the risk of overﬁtting the model to spe-
ciﬁc defect patterns is increased. The experiments show that the optimal number
of augmentations applied vary between the individual classes. Carefully selecting
these parameters based on defect type and their proportion in the dataset allows
to maximise the Recall with an acceptable Precision.
The results further reveal that the presence of multiple defects in one cell of di↵erent
defect types strongly inﬂuences the prediction outcome, especially when analysing
classes individually.
This behaviour needs further investigation, for example by
expanding the current model to multilabel classiﬁcation where the classes are not
mutually exclusive anymore or by extending the current approach to a segmentation
task, where the defect location within the cell is predicted as well.
When predicting each class with the multiclass classiﬁcation approach, a lower TPR
for each class is achieved compared to the binary classiﬁcation task. This result is
expected, as the model devotes additional resources to di↵erentiate between the
individual defect classes.
Although less severe than in the class-speciﬁc binary
classiﬁcation, cells with di↵erent defect types inﬂuence the prediction performance.
Additionally, the FPR for no defects is notably higher than in the binary classiﬁ-
cation, due to the lower loss function weight for the No defect class. Condensing
the multiclass classiﬁcation to a binary representation shows that a higher Recall is
achieved, resulting in a higher F5-Score compared to the binary classiﬁcation with
default threshold.
This insight emphasises the assumption that cells containing
multiple defect types inﬂuence the model prediction. Additionally, this outcome
underlines the importance of well chosen loss function weights.
The multiclass classiﬁcation with custom loss functions weights conﬁrms that a
careful adjustment of these can inﬂuence the prediction performance of individual
classes. The experiment reveals that the TPR of defects can be enhanced by in-
creasing the loss function class weight, but at the cost of a lower TPR for the classes
whose weights remain unchanged or are lowered. The optimal choice of loss func-
tion weights needs further investigation, as the model performance with the selected
values is not increased.
Contrary to initial expectations, the introduction of label smoothing does not lead
to any increase in performance. The signiﬁcant di↵erence to the experiment with
default parameters does not support the assumption that the result can be posi-
tively inﬂuenced with this technique.
The analysis of the multiclass classiﬁcation with di↵erent numbers of augmenta-
tions further strengthens the conclusion that the selection is of crucial importance
for maximising the model performance. The highest binary F5-Score over all experi-
ments is achieved in the multiclass classiﬁcation with the numbers of augmentations
adjusted to six for Crack A and three for ﬁnger failures, while keeping the number
of augmentations at six for both Crack B and C.
Excluding Crack A as defect aims to solely detect cells which are assumed to sig-
niﬁcantly impact the cell eﬃciency. Compared to the multiclass classiﬁcation, this
results in a higher TPR for Crack B and C while it reduces the number of False pos-
itives of the No defect class. Due to the model performance, this approach presents
a suitable solution when only focusing on maintenance instead of predictive main-
tenance.

5.1. Limitations
This thesis demonstrates the signiﬁcant potential of automated inspection methods
in identifying and classifying defects in photovoltaic cells. It highlights the critical
role of tailored deep learning approaches to enhance the accuracy and eﬃciency
of defect detection. The gained insights emphasise the importance of continued
research and development in this ﬁeld, paving the way for more sustainable and
eﬃcient solar energy production through advanced technological solutions.
5.1
Limitations
While the model demonstrated high performance in detecting defects such as Crack
B, Crack C, and ﬁnger failures, it notably struggled with accurately identifying
Crack A. This limitation suggests a need for further model reﬁnement or alternative
approaches to improve the detection of less prominent defects.
The current approach focuses on single class classiﬁcation, which inherently does
not take into account the complexity of cells containing multiple defect types. This
limitation restricts the model’s applicability in real-world scenarios where multiple
defects may coexist, potentially impacting the overall accuracy and utility of the
model.
The study does not employ image segmentation techniques to localise defects within
a cell.
This limitation means that while the model can classify the presence of
defects, it does not provide information on their location within the cell. Conse-
quently, additional manual inspection e↵orts to pinpoint the exact areas of concern
are needed.
The research is based solely on a speciﬁc dataset, which may not adequately repre-
sent the diversity of defects in other types of photovoltaic cells. This dataset-centric
approach limits the generalisability of the ﬁndings to other contexts or defect types.
The dataset exclusively contains polycrystalline cells, thereby excluding other cell
types such as monocrystalline or thin-ﬁlm cells. This focus narrows the study’s
applicability across the broader spectrum of photovoltaic cell technologies.
The study does not incorporate power output data, which could provide insights
into how speciﬁc defects impact cell eﬃciency. The lack of this correlation restricts
the ability to prioritise defect detection based on their potential impact on cell
performance.
Addressing these limitations in future research could signiﬁcantly enhance the model’s
applicability and e↵ectiveness, providing more comprehensive solutions for defect
detection and analysis in photovoltaic cells.
5.2
Potential for Future Research
Building on the ﬁndings and limitations of this study, several opportunities for
further research emerge, each with the potential to advance the ﬁeld of defect clas-
siﬁcation in photovoltaic cells.
Future studies should delve deeper into determining the optimal number of augmen-
tations for each defect type. This involves experimenting with di↵erent augmenta-
tion techniques and frequencies to enhance model performance without overﬁtting,
particularly for less represented defect types like Crack A.
An in-depth investigation into the loss function weight parameters could yield valu-
able insights. Fine-tuning these parameters might help balance the model’s sensi-
tivity towards di↵erent defect types, especially in multiclass classiﬁcation settings.
Exploring network structures beyond the ResNet family, such as InceptionV3, could
provide new perspectives on model eﬃcacy. Comparing the performance of di↵erent

architectures may reveal strengths and weaknesses speciﬁc to defect classiﬁcation
in electroluminescent images.
Transitioning to multilabel classiﬁcation or integrating image segmentation tech-
niques would address the current limitations in handling cells with multiple defects
and in localising defects. These approaches could enhance the model’s practical
utility by providing more detailed and actionable insights.
Given the challenges in detecting Crack A defects, applying classic image analysis
methods, particularly those adept at recognising narrow shapes, could be a promis-
ing direction. This approach might complement deep learning models, especially
for subtle defect types.
A novel and potentially impactful area of research involves combining electrolumi-
nescent image data with thermal infrared imagery. This approach would explore
whether defects identiﬁable in electroluminescent images are also detectable and
classiﬁable in thermal infrared images. Since thermal imaging is more feasible for
drone inspections, this research could signiﬁcantly streamline the inspection process.
Each of these research directions has the potential to address current gaps in the
ﬁeld, leading to more robust, eﬃcient, and practical solutions for defect classiﬁcation
in photovoltaic cells.

Conclusion
6.1
Summary
This thesis has made contributions to the ﬁeld of defect classiﬁcation in electro-
luminescent images of photovoltaic cells, employing state-of-the-art deep learning
techniques with a focus on ResNet architectures. The research made advancements
in handling imbalanced datasets, highlighting the beneﬁt of carefully choosing the
number of augmentations applied to defect classes. Furthermore, the results un-
derscore the importance of the choice of loss function weights and reveal that the
detection of speciﬁc defect classes can be prioritised with adjusting these weights.
The developed model successfully identiﬁed and classiﬁed various types of defects,
particularly excelling in detecting Crack B and C defects. However, it faced diﬃcul-
ties in accurately classifying Crack A defects. These results show that the detection
of narrow shapes still poses a challenge for deep learning models and further re-
search is needed. Furthermore, the ﬁndings of the study reveal that multiple defect
types in one cell a↵ect the prediction output, challenging this study’s reliance on
single class classiﬁcation. The implementation of multilabel classiﬁcation and image
segmentation techniques are areas where the model could be improved.
6.2
Outlook
Looking ahead, there are several promising directions for future research. Optimis-
ing data augmentation strategies for each defect type and ﬁne-tuning loss function
weights could enhance model performance. Experimenting with diverse network ar-
chitectures and implementing multilabel classiﬁcation or image segmentation tech-
niques could address current limitations and broaden the model’s utility. Employing
classic image analysis methods, particularly for narrow-shaped defects like Crack
A, could complement deep learning approaches.
Additionally, an promising area of research involves combining electroluminescent
image data with thermal infrared imagery, especially for drone-based inspections.
This approach could improve the inspection process, making it more eﬃcient and
less labor-intensive. Such advancements could positively impact the ﬁeld of pho-
tovoltaic cell maintenance and lead to more sustainable and eﬃcient solar energy
production.
In conclusion, while this thesis has laid a strong foundation and made advance-
ments in defect classiﬁcation using deep learning, the study also revealed several
opportunities for further exploration and innovation. The potential to enhance the
accuracy and eﬃciency of photovoltaic cell inspection and maintenance through
