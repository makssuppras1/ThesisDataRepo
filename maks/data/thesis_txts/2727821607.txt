Abstract
This MSc thesis introduces Honeyscanner, an innovative vulnerability analyzer tailored
for honeypots. Honeypots serve as decoy systems that emulate vulnerable environments,
enticing and trapping cyber­attackers while safeguarding legitimate networks. Honeyscan­
ner is engineered to autonomously execute a diverse range of attacks against speci­
fied honeypots, assessing the probability of an attacker exploiting the honeypot to gain
unauthorized access to the actual corporate network. The analyzer’s attack methods en­
compass a wide array of techniques, including the exploitation of susceptible software
libraries, DoS attacks, and fuzzing. Upon completion, Honeyscanner generates a com­
prehensive evaluation report for the honeypot administrator, offering targeted recommen­
dations to bolster the honeypot’s security measures. The analyzer’s effectiveness is rig­
orously appraised through an extensive series of experiments conducted on locally de­
ployed honeypot instances, demonstrating its potential as a valuable tool in strengthening
cybersecurity defenses.

Introduction
The rapid increase of internet­connected devices and the growing dependency on digital
infrastructure have led to a surge in cyber threats worldwide. As cyber­attacks continue
to evolve in complexity and scale, it is crucial for organizations to proactively protect their
networks and sensitive data. One of the most effective and widely adopted cybersecurity
strategies is the use of honeypots. According to a survey from Neustar International Se­
curity Council [1], 7 in 10 cybersecurity professionals are using or considering Honeypots
in order to protect critical infrastructure. A honeypot is a decoy system designed to ap­
pear as a legitimate and vulnerable target, luring attackers away from the actual network
and gathering valuable intelligence on their tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs).
This information can be used to improve an organization’s overall security posture and
enhance its ability to detect and mitigate threats.
1.1
Motivation
The motivation behind the use of honeypots lies in their proactive and reactive nature,
which provides organizations with an exceptional capacity to understand, anticipate, and
respond to cybersecurity threats. By strategically deploying honeypots across the net­
work, organizations can create a more robust cybersecurity strategy.
The honeypots
serve as tripwires or early warning systems, providing alerts whenever they are interacted
with, as this is typically indicative of malicious activities. This early warning can provide
critical lead time to respond to threats before they affect the primary infrastructure.
Moreover, honeypots allow organizations to engage with attackers in a controlled envi­
ronment, thereby gaining valuable intelligence about the nature and origin of the threat,
the attacker’s intentions, the tools, and the malware they employ. This intelligence is
invaluable in predicting future attack trends, refining threat models, and enhancing the or­
ganization’s threat response mechanisms. Specialized cyber security professionals can
use the malware samples caught in honeypots, in order to reverse engineer them and
thus prepare for similar attacks that may occur in the actual corporate network.
Honeypots also offer a unique advantage in their ability to divert attackers’ attention and
resources away from actual assets. By presenting seemingly vulnerable targets, they can
keep attackers occupied, thereby limiting their opportunities and resources to discover and
exploit real weaknesses in the network. This distraction tactic can be particularly effective
in defending against automated attacks, as bots can be easily fooled and trapped into
honeypots, reducing the load on actual systems and networks.
1.2
Challenges
Despite their usefulness, honeypots are not without their challenges. The effectiveness
of honeypots is directly tied to their credibility and maintenance. A poorly configured
honeypot may fail to attract attackers or, worse, could be identified and exploited as a
weak point within the network. When deployed in a production environment, they must
be carefully configured and monitored in order to ensure attackers cannot pivot from the
honeypot to the real network or other critical systems.
Furthermore, the effectiveness of a honeypot relies on its ability to maintain the illusion
of a vulnerable system while preventing unauthorized access to sensitive information. To
this end, it is essential to regularly assess and improve the security of these systems. This

includes systematic patching, active maintenance of dependencies, active monitoring of
access logs, and regular security audits by experienced penetration testers.
This MSc thesis presents Honeyscanner, a vulnerability analyzer specifically designed
for honeypots. The primary goal of this project is to develop a tool that can automatically
evaluate the security of honeypots, identify potential weaknesses, and provide actionable
recommendations for enhancing their defenses.
1.3
Research Objectives
The key objectives of this research are as follows:
1. To systematically review and analyze existing honeypot technologies, including the
way they work, common techniques, and popular software libraries used.
2. To study in­depth common honeypot misconfigurations and weaknesses.
3. To study the common attack methods employed by adversaries.
4. To design and develop the Honeyscanner vulnerability analyzer, which can auto­
matically assess the security of popular honeypots such as Cowrie and Kippo.
5. To evaluate the effectiveness of Honeyscanner by conducting a series of experi­
ments on locally deployed honeypot instances and analyzing its ability to identify
vulnerabilities and potential attack paths.
6. To provide recommendations for improving the security of honeypots based on the
findings of the Honeyscanner analysis.

2
Literature Review
This chapter presents a comprehensive literature review on honeypot technologies, their
classifications, and the common methods used by attackers to exploit these systems.
It also discusses the techniques used for honeypot detection and evasion employed by
adversaries, as well as the challenges with deploying and managing honeypots. Finally,
this section reviews existing vulnerability analysis tools and their applicability in the context
of honeypot security. This in­depth review will provide the foundation for the design and
development of the Honeyscanner vulnerability analyzer.
2.1
Honeypot Technologies
Honeypot technologies are generally classified into two primary categories: low­interaction
and high­interaction. This section provides a detailed analysis of the characteristics, key
differences, benefits, and implications of these two types of systems. Furthermore, it
discusses their effectiveness as decoy systems.
2.1.1
Low­Interaction Honeypots
Low­Interaction honeypots are described as the subset of honeypots designed to emulate
only specific services or protocols. They provide limited functionality to an attacker and a
restricted set of commands, typically emulating application services such as HTTP, SSH,
or FTP [6]. This restrained interaction implies that attackers cannot gain full control over
the system, hence minimizing the potential risk of a severe security breach. The inherent
simplicity and resource efficiency make low­interaction honeypots highly appealing for a
wide array of cybersecurity scenarios, making them a tool for quickly gathering informa­
tion about potential threats and breaches [7]. However, the simplicity that serves as their
strength can also be a double­edged sword. Sophisticated attackers with advanced tech­
niques can exploit their limited interaction capabilities, detecting these honeypots and
subsequently avoiding them, rendering them useless. Recent research has shed light
on these detection and avoidance tactics, especially, Vetterl et al.’s work ”Bitter Harvest:
Systematically Fingerprinting Low­ and Medium­interaction Honeypots at Internet Scale”
[8]. This innovative study demonstrated the possibility of systematically fingerprinting and
detecting low­ and medium­interaction honeypots, revealing their potential weaknesses
and the need for continuous innovation in their design and implementation. A few ex­
amples of low­interaction honeypots in operation include Honeyd, Conpot, and Kippo.
Honeyd, a flexible tool that allows the user to set up and configure virtual hosts on a net­
work, mimics various operating systems and services [9]. On the other hand, Conpot is an
industrial control system honeypot that emulates a complex industrial environment, par­
ticularly appealing for detecting and analyzing threats in the industrial sector [4]. Kippo,
a low­interaction SSH honeypot, specializes in detecting brute force attacks [5]. Each of
these honeypots plays a critical role in identifying, analyzing, and preventing various types
of cyberattacks, but their effectiveness heavily relies on their ability to evade detection and
trick sophisticated attackers, making constant updates and improvements crucial.
2.1.2
High­Interaction Honeypots
High­Interaction is the other subset of honeypots, boasting an increased level of sophisti­
cation and realism to lure and trap potential attackers. Unlike their low­interaction counter­
parts, high­interaction honeypots provide a full­fledged environment for attackers, often
using real operating systems, applications, and services [10]. Their capability to facil­
itate the full exploitation of vulnerabilities allows for the capture of a broader range of
Honeyscanner
5
==PAGE:14==
attacks, making them extremely valuable in gathering comprehensive insights into an at­
tacker’s methodologies and techniques. Alata et al.’s study titled ”Lessons Learned from
the Deployment of a High­interaction Honeypot” [11], presents this, demonstrating the
vast potential of high­interaction honeypots in inspecting and understanding highly com­
plicated attack patterns. However, this increased complexity and realism come with their
fair share of challenges. High­interaction honeypots are resource­intensive, requiring sig­
nificant computational power and storage capacity, especially when they are deployed at
scale [12]. Moreover, their management and maintenance require a higher degree of
technical knowledge and effort compared to low­interaction honeypots. Modern research
has started to explore novel solutions to these challenges, such as the deployment of high­
interaction honeypots in cloud environments. Kelly et al. conducted a comparative anal­
ysis of high­interaction honeypot performance across various cloud platforms [6]. This
emerging research direction promises potential advancements in honeypot technology,
leveraging cloud environments’ scalability and resource efficiency. Despite their chal­
lenges, high­interaction honeypots have shown considerable promise in detecting com­
plex and advanced attacks that low­interaction honeypots might miss. Notable examples
of high­interaction honeypots include Cowrie, Dionaea, and Amun. Cowrie, a successor
to the Kippo SSH honeypot, simulates a more extensive and realistic SSH and Telnet
environment [3]. Dionaea, on the other hand, is designed to trap malware exploiting vul­
nerabilities exposed by services offered to the network [2]. Amun, is also another older
honeypot, which acts similarly to Dionaea, in the sense that it exposes specific ports and
services that appear to be running vulnerable versions of services [13], in order to trap
attacker techniques. These honeypots allow researchers to gain a deeper understanding
of cyber threats, demonstrating the ongoing importance and relevance of high­interaction
honeypots in the cybersecurity landscape.
2.2
Attack Vectors
Attackers use a variety of techniques to compromise honeypots. This section outlines
some of the most common attack vectors, including exploiting vulnerable software, launch­
ing Denial of Service (DoS) attacks, and fuzzing, and discusses their implications for hon­
eypot security.
2.2.1
Exploiting Vulnerable Software
Attackers frequently exploit known vulnerabilities present in software libraries, applica­
tions, and services. These vulnerabilities are typically assigned a Common Vulnerabili­
ties and Exposures (CVE) identifier, which facilitates easy referencing and tracking [14].
By capitalizing on these vulnerabilities, attackers can gain unauthorized system access
or execute arbitrary code. In the context of honeypots, such exploitations pose a signifi­
cant risk. If a honeypot’s software library is compromised, it could potentially provide an
attacker with a gateway into the organization’s broader system network. This could in­
advertently transform the honeypot from a protective measure into an entry point to the
organization, thereby increasing the organization’s exposure to potential threats. How­
ever, it is important to note that, as of the writing of this MSc thesis, there are no publicly
documented instances of attacks on honeypots initiated through vulnerabilities in their
associated software libraries.
2.2.2
Denial of Service (DoS)
Denial of Service (DoS) attacks are designed to incapacitate a system or service, mak­
ing it inaccessible to its legitimate users. This is typically achieved either by flooding the
network with an overwhelming amount of traffic or by depleting the system’s resources.
In the context of honeypots, attackers may employ tactics such as initiating multiple TCP
6
Honeyscanner
==PAGE:15==
or UDP socket connections concurrently. This strategy floods the network with traffic,
thereby rendering the honeypot unavailable to other attackers who would otherwise con­
nect to it. Another way that an attacker can perform Denial of Service in a honeypot is the
deployment of a zip bomb attack. A zip bomb is a maliciously crafted archive file designed
to crash the system it is extracted on, effectively rendering it inoperative [15]. The primary
objective of this method is to consume all available disk space on the server where the
honeypot is deployed. The attacker, upon establishing a connection to the honeypot,
downloads a zip bomb from the web. While the archived zip bomb file is small in size,
it expands into a large file filled with NULL bytes when unzipped, thereby occupying the
entirety of the disk space. The overarching goal of an attacker executing DoS attacks on
honeypots is to disrupt the decoy system’s operation. This prevents the honeypot from
fulfilling its primary functions of monitoring and data collection, thereby undermining its
effectiveness as a security measure.
2.2.3
Fuzzing
Fuzzing is a robust technique employed to uncover vulnerabilities within software sys­
tems. It operates by supplying the target system with malformed or unexpected and long
inputs, thereby testing its ability to handle such anomalies. Attackers can leverage fuzzing
to pinpoint weaknesses in honeypot systems, subsequently exploiting these vulnerabili­
ties to gain unauthorized access or induce system instability. This technique can prove
particularly potent when applied to high­interaction honeypots. These honeypots, due to
their complex environment, may present a broader surface area for potential vulnerabili­
ties. For instance, in the context of SSH honeypots, attackers could attempt to fuzz the
SSH banners during the SSH handshake process. This could potentially expose vulner­
abilities within the handshake mechanism itself. Moreover, attackers could also employ
fuzzing within the honeypot environment using an active SSH session. By fuzzing various
terminal commands, they may uncover additional vulnerabilities that could be exploited
to compromise the honeypot system.
2.3
Honeypot Detection and Evasion
Attackers are constantly developing new techniques to detect and evade honeypots, ren­
dering them less effective as decoy systems. Research has shown that this includes
methods to systematically fingerprint low­ and medium­interaction honeypots at an Inter­
net scale, making the issue of honeypot detection a significant concern.
2.3.1
Honeypot Fingerprinting
Honeypot fingerprinting is a technique used by attackers to identify honeypots in a net­
work. There are two main types of honeypot fingerprinting techniques: active and passive.
Active fingerprinting involves creating specific probes and querying the target system to
collect as much data as possible. Passive fingerprinting, on the other hand, makes use of
available data about the target system for further analysis to determine information about
the target [16].
Active Fingerprinting
Active fingerprinting involves direct interaction with the system. Attackers create queries
to derive fingerprinting information. These methods focus on leveraging the data from
responses and classifying the target machine based on fingerprinting identifiers. The in­
formation may include system­specific unique identifiers. For example, tools like Nmap
[17], XProbe2 [18], Metasploit [19], and Hydra [20] utilize probe­based methods to de­
termine the Operating System (OS) and the protocol versions of the target systems [16].
Moreover, recent research has highlighted how active fingerprinting can be used at an
Internet scale to identify low­ and medium­interaction honeypots. [8]

Passive Fingerprinting
Passive fingerprinting, also known as metascan­based fingerprinting, leverages known
information about the target system without direct interaction. The technique uses the IP
address and performs a search on Internet mass­scan engines to obtain attributes like
the hosting provider and the Internet Service Provider (ISP). The data obtained through
metascan can be leveraged for fingerprinting purposes. For example, if the target system
has the TCP port 502 (i.e., the Modbus protocol default port) open and the IP address
is attached to a network assigned to a university or research facility, this might act as an
indication that the target system is a research honeypot [16].
2.3.2
Honeypot Evasion
Honeypot evasion is another technique used by attackers to avoid detection. One com­
mon evasion technique used in malware is to perform honeypot fingerprinting and add a
blocklist of the identified IP addresses into the code, in order to avoid being installed and
executed in honeypot systems and reduce the risk of detection. Modern malware already
includes hard­coded IP addresses that are blocklisted from all communications [16]. In
addition to these techniques, attackers may also employ more sophisticated methods to
evade honeypots. For instance, they might use advanced malware that can detect the
presence of a honeypot based on certain characteristics of the system, such as the lack
of user activity or the presence of default configurations, and passwords.
2.4
Countermeasures Against Honeypot Fingerprinting
Countermeasures against honeypot fingerprinting techniques are crucial to maintain the
effectiveness of a honeypot. One effective countermeasure includes maintaining an up­to­
date honeypot system. Given the fast­paced evolution of cyber threats, regular updates to
the honeypot systems are essential to ensure that they can convincingly mimic the latest
real systems and counter new fingerprinting techniques [8]. Another strategy involves the
use of complex honeypot configurations. A more complicated honeypot setup can vastly
increase the difficulty for attackers to distinguish between a decoy and a real system,
thereby enhancing its deception capabilities. A diverse selection of honeypots can also be
employed to confuse potential attackers. By deploying a range of honeypots with varying
interaction levels and capabilities, it becomes more challenging for attackers to fingerprint
and evade all of them. Employing deception techniques, to make the honeypot appear
as a legitimate system is another crucial countermeasure. Techniques such as mimicking
realistic network traffic, simulating user activity, and customizing system configurations
can significantly increase the honeypot’s believability [10]. These techniques can confuse
the attackers into misidentifying the honeypot as a legitimate target, making the decoy
more likely to capture the attack. Lastly, continuous monitoring and analysis of attacker
behavior can provide valuable insights into new and evolving fingerprinting techniques,
enabling the proactive development of new countermeasures to improve the honeypot’s
effectiveness.
2.5
Challenges in Deploying and Managing Honeypots
Deploying and managing honeypots present numerous challenges, including sustaining
the facade of a vulnerable system, averting pivoting to the actual network, and addressing
the legal and ethical concerns associated with monitoring the activities of attackers.
2.5.1
Maintaining the Illusion of a Vulnerable System
Maintaining the illusion of a vulnerable system is crucial for the success of a honeypot.
If attackers recognize that they are interacting with a decoy system, they may abandon
8
Honeyscanner
==PAGE:17==
their efforts, or worse, attempt to use it to their advantage. To maintain this illusion, hon­
eypot administrators must carefully configure the system to mimic a genuine target while
avoiding obvious signs that may reveal its true nature, such as unrealistic network con­
figurations, inconsistent services, or outdated software versions. At the same time, it is
crucial to prevent any information leakage from the real corporate network.
2.5.2
Preventing Pivoting to the Real Network
One of the primary concerns in deploying honeypots is ensuring that attackers cannot
pivot from the honeypot to the real corporate network or other critical systems. This can
be achieved by isolating the honeypot from the main network using firewalls, network seg­
mentation, and strict access controls. Additionally, continuous monitoring of the honeypot
and its network traffic is crucial for detecting and mitigating any potential threats. For ex­
ample, if an attacker attempts to scan the network from the honeypot, this could indicate
that they have recognized the honeypot and are attempting to pivot to the real network.
In such cases, the honeypot administrators should take immediate action to block the
attacker and investigate the incident.
2.5.3
Legal and Ethical Considerations
Honeypots involve monitoring attacker activities, which raises legal and ethical concerns
regarding privacy and the collection of personal information. To comply with relevant reg­
ulations and ethical guidelines, honeypot administrators must ensure that the data col­
lected is limited to the minimum necessary for their research objectives, stored securely,
and used only for the intended purpose.
2.6
Existing Vulnerability Analysis Tools
A variety of vulnerability analysis tools have emerged in recent years, assisting organi­
zations in pinpointing and addressing security vulnerabilities within their systems. These
tools employ various techniques, such as static code analysis, dynamic code analysis,
and penetration testing, to uncover vulnerabilities in software and hardware components.
Some well­known vulnerability analysis tools include Nessus [21], OpenVAS [22], and
Metasploit [19]. These tools are valuable for assessing the security of static code, com­
piled binaries, or web applications. However, they are not specifically tailored for analyz­
ing honeypots.
2.6.1
Nessus
For instance, Nessus is a widely used vulnerability scanner that can identify vulnerabilities
in various types of systems, including operating systems, databases, and web applica­
tions. It uses a database of known vulnerabilities and checks the target systems against
this database. However, it does not provide specific features for analyzing honeypots.
2.6.2
OpenVAS
Similarly, OpenVAS is an open­source vulnerability scanner that can identify vulnerabilities
in various types of systems. It uses a database of known vulnerabilities and checks the
target systems against this database. However, it also does not provide specific features
for analyzing honeypots.
2.6.3
Metasploit
Metasploit, on the other hand, is a penetration testing tool that can be used to exploit
known vulnerabilities in systems. It provides a framework for developing and executing
exploit code against a remote target machine. While it can be used to test the security of
honeypots, it is not specifically designed for this purpose.

Summary
This literature review has provided a comprehensive and in­depth overview of honeypot
technologies, their classifications, common attack vectors, and the challenges associated
with deploying and managing these systems. It has also critically reviewed past academic
work on honeypots, existing vulnerability analysis tools, and their applicability to honey­
pots. This information along with the manual penetration testing presented in the next
chapter, will serve as the foundation for the design and development of Honeyscanner,
a vulnerability analyzer specifically tailored for honeypots. The next chapter will describe
the selection of attack techniques used during the manual penetration testing phase. As
the use of honeypots becomes more prevalent, it is crucial to develop more sophisticated
and resilient honeypots that can effectively serve as decoy systems, while also providing
valuable insights into the techniques and strategies used by attackers.

Manual Testing of Honeypots
Before the development of the Honeyscanner tool, a series of comprehensive manual
security tests were conducted on four well­known honeypots, Dionaea [2], Cowrie [3],
Conpot [4], and Kippo [5]. These four honeypots were selected because they represent
the most well­maintained, actively used honeypots, so it is a good starting point to under­
stand how honeypots work and behave to different inputs, which libraries are commonly
used, and what honeypot code looks like. The primary objective of this manual testing
phase was to assess their vulnerabilities, gain a deeper understanding of their security
posture, and identify potential security weaknesses that can be found across multiple of
these honeypots. These findings would later inform the design and implementation of
Honeyscanner. This chapter presents the manual testing process, the tools utilized, and
the key results of these tests. The manual testing process entailed analyzing the source
code and configurations of the four honeypots using open­source and proprietary static
analysis tools and vulnerability scanners. The testing process also involved forking the
honeypots’ code and employing cloud­based vulnerability analyzers to detect potential
bugs and security holes in the software. Finally, manual attacks were executed against a
locally deployed Cowrie honeypot instance.

3.2
Experiences with Manual Attacks on Honeypots
This section describes the experiences and insights gained from manual vulnerability test­
ing of the honeypots, focusing on potential attack vectors and techniques that an attacker
might employ.
3.2.1
Attacking a Cowrie Honeypot
A Cowrie honeypot was deployed on a local machine using the latest official Docker image
[32]. Various attack techniques, including denial of service (DoS) attacks and fuzzing,
were attempted on the honeypot to assess its resilience.
DoS Attacks
For DoS attacks, multiple TCP connections were established in parallel, in order to ex­
haust the honeypot’s resources and potentially cause it to crash or become unresponsive.
The results of these attacks were that the honeypot indeed crashed and became unavail­
able, even after the attacker halted the attack.
Fuzzing Attacks
Fuzzing attacks were conducted on the Cowrie honeypot using a combination of SSH
banner fuzzing and terminal command fuzzing. The fuzzing process involved generating
random input data using the Radamsa tool [33], which was then sent to the honeypot in
an attempt to trigger unexpected behavior or crashes. The fuzzing attacks aimed to iden­
tify potential vulnerabilities in the honeypot’s implementation, such as buffer overflows or
insecure user input handling. Despite the bombardment of malformed inputs, the hon­
eypot handled each one correctly, successfully preventing any buffer overflows, unusual
behaviors, or system crashes.
Tar Bomb Attack
To verify the tarbomb vulnerability, which was first identified using SonarCloud, a tarbomb
was manually created and used to attack the locally deployed Cowrie honeypot. To create
a tarbomb the following commands were used.
1. dd if=/dev/zero of=sparse_file bs=1 count=0 seek=10T
2. tar -cf not_a_tar_bomb.tar sparse_file
These commands, create a sparse file, which is a file that does not contain any real data,
just NULL bytes, but its virtual size is very large. In this example, a virtually 10 Terabytes
file is created. Then this file is turned into a tarball. If any software attempts to extract this
archive, it runs the risk of crashing either itself or the entire system it is running on.
However, when the attack was executed against the locally deployed Cowrie instance,
it was unsuccessful, as the honeypot did not crash. From the honeypot logs though,
the honeypot seems like it encountered a segmentation fault or some other unhandled
exception. Refer to figures: 3.16, 3.17

3.3
Key Results from Manual Testing
The manual testing phase provided valuable insights into the security posture of the Dion­
aea, Cowrie, and Conpot honeypots. Some of the key findings include:
• Initially, for all four honeypots, vulnerable libraries were identified manually by an­
alyzing their dependencies and searching for known CVEs online. However, this
approach did not yield any significant findings.
• Many ”FIXME” and ”TODO” comments were found throughout the honeypots’ source
code, revealing unfinished or potentially vulnerable functions that could be exploited
by attackers.
• A tarbomb vulnerability was identified in Cowrie version 2.5.0 through its automated
SonarCloud analysis. However, a manual attack using a tar bomb did not succeed
in compromising the honeypot.
• The Dockerfile for Cowrie did not follow best practices and installed unnecessary
packages, potentially exposing the honeypot to exploitation.
• Forking the public Github code repositories of Conpot, Kippo, and Cowrie v1.5.3
enabled the free use of the SonarCloud vulnerability scanner, which provided in­
valuable insights into their security posture.
• Dionaea required significant configuration and could not be analyzed using Sonar­
Cloud. However, the Python part of the code was successfully scanned using the
CodeQL security analysis tool available on GitHub. [34]
• Safety Check revealed multiple vulnerabilities in the Twisted library 22.10.0 used by
Cowrie version 2.5.0. These vulnerabilities have been assigned Vulnerability IDs
(CVE­2022­39348) [30] and (CVE­2022­24801) [31].

Summary
The manual testing of the four honeypots played a crucial role in the development of Hon­

architecture and potential weaknesses, informing the design and implementation of the
vulnerability analyzer. The insights gained from the manual testing phase also highlighted
the need for regular security assessments and vulnerability management for honeypots,
motivating the development of Honeyscanner as an automated solution for honeypot vul­
nerability analysis.

Honeyscanner Design and
Implementation
The focus of this chapter is to explain the structural design and implementation of Hon­
eyscanner ­ a tool explicitly engineered to automate the analysis of vulnerabilities within
selected open­source honeypots. The essential function of Honeyscanner lies in its abil­
ity to facilitate an integrated security evaluation of these honeypots by leveraging both
passive and active attack strategies. The programming language which was chosen for
the development of Honeyscanner is Python version 3.9.12. Python’s extensive library
support and ease of use make it an ideal choice for the rapid development and prototyping
of Honeyscanner’s core functionalities.

4.1.1
Passive Attacks
The passive attack engine is the principal component responsible for acquiring extensive
information concerning the target honeypot. It investigates potential misconfigurations,
bugs, and security vulnerabilities. Central to this process is the mechanism of white­box
testing of the honeypot’s source code [35].
White­box testing, also known as clear­box or glass­box testing, is an approach where
the internal workings of the system under test are fully known to the tester of the sys­
tem. In contrast to black­box testing, which treats the system under examination as an
opaque ’black box’, white­box testing provides extensive visibility into the internal logic
and structure of the code.[36]
Honeyscanner
23
==PAGE:32==
This visibility allows for more thorough, granular testing of the individual functions, loops,
conditional branches, and statements in the code. Consequently, the chances of iden­
tifying and correcting potential issues and security vulnerabilities are significantly higher
[37].
The implementation of white­box testing in Honeyscanner’s context occurs through vari­
ous technical approaches. These include container security scanning, static code analy­
sis, and vulnerable libraries analysis. Each approach aims to uncover different types of
potential vulnerabilities and ensure that the honeypot has a good overall security posture.

ning module uncovers potential vulnerabilities by utilizing the trivy scanner [38]. Trivy, an
open­source security scanner, excels in identifying Infrastructure as Code (IaC) miscon­
figurations, Cloud scanning, and Kubernetes security risks, among other anomalies.

Active Attacks
The active attack engine bears the responsibility of executing an array of attack tech­
niques against the targeted honeypot. Its modular design enables the easy integration of
newly developed or discovered attack techniques through the ”AttackOrchestrator” class
instance. This class oversees the coordination and sequential execution of attacks and
compiles a unified results report upon the completion of all attacks. The active attacks
are:
Denial of Service (DoS) Attack
The active attack engine performs a DoS attack by spawning a number of parallel threads
in the host machine and initiating simultaneous socket connections toward the target hon­

eypot. The attacking machine keeps sending socket connection requests to the honeypot
until the honeypot has so many open connections that can not open more and respond
to new socket connection requests. At this point, the attack is considered successful, as

the honeypot unavailable. DDoS attacks have been increasingly popular and present in
the headlines in the last decade. This is because of the huge financial cost they bare
to the targeted company. Some well­known targets of the most powerful attacks have
been Google, AWS, and GitHub [41]. In the context of Honeyscanner, a DDoS attack is
not needed. As proved here, a honeypot instance can be easily taken down by a single
computer system, just by using Honeyscanner’s DoS component.

Software Libraries Exploitation
The exploitation of vulnerable libraries’ attack module is designed to utilize the list of Com­
mon Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVEs) generated during the phase of passive attacks.
Despite the ability of passive attacks to identify potential weaknesses in the targeted hon­
eypot, they do not carry out the exploitation. This is the point where the exploitation of
vulnerable libraries’ attack module gains significance.
Initially, Honeyscanner sought to exploit potential software libraries’ vulnerabilities by us­
ing the list of CVEs to perform web scraping techniques, identify GitHub repositories that
mention the CVE number in the URL, the repository title, or description, download the
relevant Proof of Concept (PoC) code, and attempt to automatically run the exploit code
against the targeted honeypot [44] [45].
Unfortunately, this approach exhibited flaws and posed significant challenges for Hon­
eyscanner. First and foremost, such a technique could be dangerous to the host ma­
chine operating Honeyscanner, as malicious actors might intentionally incorporate the
CVE number into a repository’s URL, but include malware that infects the host machine
rather than the actual PoC code for exploiting the specific vulnerability. Additionally, even
if Honeyscanner managed to download legitimate PoC code from GitHub, executing it
against the honeypot would be complex. This is because the code could be written in
different languages such as Python, C, or Go, making it challenging to establish a unified
method for compiling and executing any PoC both automatically and safely against the
targeted honeypot.
In order to address the aforementioned issues and ensure the necessary functionality for
Honeyscanner, Metasploit was employed. The exploitation of vulnerable libraries’ attack
module operates by receiving the list of CVEs as input to locate Metasploit modules asso­
ciated with the security weaknesses of the targeted honeypot and subsequently launches
automated attacks. Honeyscanner currently utilizes Metasploit version 6.3.20 [19]. In con­
trast to the previous approach, Metasploit offers a user­friendly Application Programming
Interface (API) to search for modules related to specific CVEs, as well as a straightfor­
ward API to execute a Metasploit module against a targeted IP address and port [46].
This provides the safety that each module is truly an exploit for the specific CVE and a
unified way to run any of the modules against the targeted honeypot.

Tarbomb attack
The tarbomb attack was originally inspired by the manual testing of the Cowrie honeypot’s
source code. As described above, a tarbomb attack is possible when software extracts
an archived file without controlling the space it occupies when extracted. Tarbombs are
special types of files that when archived take up only a few kilobytes of space in the disk,
but when expanded, they occupy many Gigabytes or Terabytes of space, filling up the tar­
geted system’s disk with useless NULL bytes. The tarbomb attack module of the active
attack engine initiates multiple SSH sessions with the targeted honeypot and simultane­
ously downloads and extracts three tarbombs in each session. One small, one medium,
and one large. The smallest one, when expanded occupies 5GB of space, while the
largest one occupies 10TB. These tar bombs were created by archiving with ’tar’ sparse
files [47].

Evaluation and Experiments
This chapter outlines the evaluation of Honeyscanner’s effectiveness in analyzing the se­
curity of honeypots. In the scope of this MSc thesis project and at the time of writing,
Honeyscanner supports the evaluation of Cowrie versions 2.5.0, 2.4.0, 2.1.0, 1.5.3, 1.5.1
[3] and Kippo’s only available version, version 0.9 [5]. A series of experiments were con­
ducted to assess the vulnerability analyzer’s ability to identify potential weaknesses and
provide actionable recommendations to enhance the honeypot’s security. In this section,
the results for Kippo v0.9 and Cowrie v2.5.0 will be presented. This chapter will discuss
the experimental setup, the evaluation metrics, as well as the results of the experiments
that were conducted as part of this MSc thesis project.
5.1
Experimental Setup
5.1.1
Honeypot setup using Docker
For the Cowrie honeypot, the latest official Docker image was used [32], in order to run a
Cowrie instance on the local machine, where Honeyscanner was running. For the Kippo
honeypot, an official Docker image was not provided by the honeypot developer. The
challenges in running Kippo, such as installing outdated Python 2 packages and mitigat­
ing compatibility issues between them, inspired the decision to craft a custom Dockerfile.
This Dockerfile would allow anyone to smoothly run Kippo in any Docker­compatible envi­
ronment, thereby removing all the above­mentioned challenges. A Docker image, derived
from this Dockerfile, was subsequently hosted on Dockerhub [50] and used in running the
Kippo honeypot for Honeyscanner experiments.
5.1.2
Honeypot Configuration
The experimental setup aimed to mirror real­world scenarios in assessing the perfor­
mance of Honeyscanner. Research reveals that honeypots are often deployed with de­
fault settings and credentials in real­world scenarios, due to the failure of administrators in
proper configuration [16]. To simulate these circumstances and to evaluate Honeyscan­
ner’s effectiveness in such scenarios, the honeypots were deployed using their default
configuration.
5.2

execution of active attacks causing the honeypot to crash, the successful exploitation of

The results of the evaluation experiments demonstrate that Honeyscanner is effective in

Cowrie v2.5.0 honeypot

were 132 potential CVEs discovered. A lot of them may be false positives, as the ”Stat­
icAnalyzer” passive attack scrapes CWE’s website [51] using the identified CWEs and
matches all the potential CVEs that it finds on the website. This gives a higher chance to

Successful Active Attacks: According to figures A.2 & A.3 only the ”DoS” active attack

Bomb” attack showed similar results as with the manual tests (figure 3.16). The result is
partially successful because it indeed caused the honeypot to face a segmentation fault­
/unhandled exception as intended, but did not succeed in crashing it or allow the attacker
to have arbitrary write access to other parts of the host machine. The ”SoftwareExploit”
attack was also executed successfully, but it did not manage to crash the honeypot or

5.3.3
Misuse attack of deployed Cowrie honeypots
Inspired by research papers on honeypot fingerprinting [16], honeypot instances mis­
usage [52] and the functionality validated on the ”TarBomb” active attack component,
that the Cowrie honeypot allows the execution of the ’wget’ command, a new idea was
generated. What if a malicious actor instead of wanting to render useless an identified
honeypot, misuses it for their own malicious purposes? For example, to use it as part of
a powerful distributed botnet, that can be used for a DDoS attack.
According to the research study ”Gotta catch ’em all: a Multistage Framework for honeypot
fingerprinting”, by Srinivasa et al. [16], there are more than 3000 instances of deployed
outdated (version 1.5.1) Cowrie honeypots reachable on the Web. An attacker, that has
identified all these instances, being able to use them as a botnet for free, is a powerful
Honeyscanner
33
==PAGE:42==
resource. Proof of Concept code was created as part of this experiment, which can be
found in Appendix A.1.
This Go code creates an SSH client for each given honeypot, specified in a text file,
it then connects to each honeypot simultaneously and repeatedly instructs them to run a
’curl’ command against the given target every 5 seconds. When deciding if ”wget” or ”curl”
should be used, ”curl” was preferred as it is less resource intensive for the honeypot itself.
Using the ”wget” command downloads the webpage on the honeypot’s local storage, so in
order to avoid bloating and filling up the disk space of the honeypot with non­useful data,
”curl” was decided as the best option moving forwards. This code is written in Go instead
of Python, because after experimenting with both programming languages, Go was better
at handling multiple concurrent processes. Go is built for concurrency and it is inherently
better for such tasks than Python [53]. So this Proof of Concept, proves that the misuse
of honeypots is probably the most interesting way an attacker might want to take in order
to take advantage of fingerprinted honeypots. In general, simple ”curl” GET requests to a
homepage of a website, generate a few KB of incoming traffic to it. If the attacker wants to
create more incoming traffic for the targeted website, a POST request with a large payload
or a GET request of a large file such as a .PDF file, stored in the target’s webserver can
be used. For this experiment, even the simple case of a GET request with a few KB
of network traffic, multiplied by more than 3000 available honeypot instances, is enough
to showcase the significant effect it can have on the target. In general, for a successful
DDoS attack, the network bandwidth can range from a few gigabits per second (Gbps) to
over a terabit per second (Tbps) in the most extreme cases. It all depends on the targeted
website and the protections it employs against DDoS attacks.
5.4
Summary
The evaluation experiments presented in this chapter demonstrate that Honeyscanner is
a powerful vulnerability analyzer for honeypots. Even though it is still in the early stages
of its development, it can successfully perform static code analysis, analyze and discover
vulnerable libraries used in open­source honeypots, and scan docker images. It uses
all this information to actively and automatically exploit software libraries, and perform
DoS attacks and Fuzzing attacks against deployed honeypot instances. For now, it can
only analyze 2 honeypot types and specific versions, but in the future, it will be able to
analyze more honeypots, running different services, in different ports and using various
programming languages. The Evaluation Report now provides a basic overview of the
conducted tests and attacks against the honeypot, but in the future, it could provide more
actionable recommendations or even automatic patching of vulnerable libraries.
Even though Honeyscanner is powerful and can crash honeypot instances, the misuse
attack experiment showcases another angle of how an attacker could use the information
that behind a specific IP address, a honeypot is running. Instead of trying to crash it,
the attacker can see it as a valuable resource and use it to create powerful botnets, that
can bring down important targets by employing them in a DDoS attack. This experiment
sparks the curiosity for further exploration and it can definitely lead to more work in the
future.

source honeypots, inspired by the experience gained on this research project. Proposed
future work and enhancements to Honeyscanner will also be discussed.

6
Conclusion and Future Work
This MSc thesis project has presented the design, implementation, and evaluation of
Honeyscanner, a first­of­its­kind vulnerability analyzer explicitly tailored for honeypots.
By identifying potential weaknesses, Honeyscanner can act as a defensive tool that en­
hances the security of a honeypot, revealing its weaknesses to the honeypot administra­
tors first, before malicious actors get their chance to uncover and exploit them.
The research started with a thorough exploration of the landscape of honeypot technolo­
gies, from their different architectures to their applications in the cybersecurity space. A
deep dive into the academic literature, coupled with an extensive study of open­source
honeypot codebases, provided a comprehensive understanding of the state­of­the­art
in honeypot security, honeypot fingerprinting, and attacking techniques. The following
phase involved manual penetration testing, utilizing both white­box and black­box test­
ing techniques, uncovering the hidden weaknesses that existed within some of the most
widely used honeypots. The insights gained from this process became the foundation for
the design and development of Honeyscanner.
Throughout the implementation process, Honeyscanner was continuously refined and
evaluated using local instances of deployed honeypots, which proved fundamental in
shaping the tool’s development. The evaluation reports generated by the Honeyscanner
proved the tool’s efficacy, providing actionable insights and invaluable recommendations
to enhance the security posture of the honeypots.
6.1
Remediation Advice for Open­Source Honeypots
The vulnerabilities detected by Honeyscanner in open­source honeypots often appeared
due to misconfigurations or outdated components. Remediation advice for these honey­
pots mainly falls under four categories:
1. Regular Updates: Open­source honeypot code should be updated regularly to in­
corporate the latest security patches and feature improvements for their direct and
transitive dependencies.
2. Secure Configurations: System administrators should adhere to best practices for
secure configurations to minimize potential attack surfaces. They should also change
the publicly available default configurations, especially the root’s password, to avoid
getting attacked by bots.
3. Active Monitoring: Continuous monitoring and incident response strategies should
be implemented to quickly identify and respond to any suspicious activity or breaches.
4. Setup free open­source security tools: SonarCloud, CodeQL, Trivy, and Bandit are
just a few of the most popular, powerful, and free to use for public open­source code
repositories, security solutions. Setting up automatic, continuous security scans for
a honeypot is the single best way to keep informed about the security posture of the
code the honeypot is running on.
It is recommended that these remediation measures are included in the maintenance
routine of open­source honeypots to ensure their robustness against cyber threats.

6.2
Future Work
Although Honeyscanner has demonstrated its efficacy in the present study, there is po­
tential for future enhancements and extensions. These may include, but are not limited
to:
1. Support for additional honeypots: An obvious area of expansion is the inclusion of
more honeypots, particularly those developed in languages other than Python and
those that do not have SSH ports open but for example FTP ports or HTTP. This will
broaden Honeyscanner’s applicability across a wider range of honeypots.
2. Advanced Attack Techniques: The incorporation of more sophisticated attack strate­
gies will increase Honeyscanner’s ability to uncover and exploit vulnerabilities, of­
fering a more thorough assessment of honeypot security.
3. Automated Remediation: By automating the application of patches and configu­
ration updates based on Honeyscanner’s vulnerability findings, this feature could
significantly reduce the time­to­fix, improving the overall security posture more effi­
ciently.
4. Improved User Interface: By providing a web interface for easy testing of a honey­
pot’s security posture, more non­technical users would be able to test the security
of their honeypots, thus improving the cybersecurity space as a whole.
5. Docker Image: A docker image should be released for Honeyscanner, making it
easy for everyone to run the tool in any environment just by having Docker installed.
6. Integration with Cybersecurity Tools: Compatibility with other security platforms would
provide Honeyscanner greater visibility into an organization’s overall security pos­
ture, contributing to a holistic and integrated security approach.
7. Misuse attack: The misuse attack described during the previous chapter has a lot
of potential and definitely sparks ideas of how attackers can use honeypots as part
of a larger botnet that they control and employ for their attacks. The Proof of Con­
cept code provided in Appendix A.1, showcases that it is possible to command and
control multiple honeypots at once, ordering them to ”curl” target websites.
8. User study regarding Honeyscanner’s Report: A survey type of research could be
conducted in the future, trying to answer if the Report is useful the way it is, and
how it could become more accurate, clear, concise, easy to understand, and useful
to the users of Honeyscanner.
9. By continually improving on these points, Honeyscanner has the potential to remain
a dynamic and indispensable tool in the ever­changing landscape of cybersecu­
rity, arming organizations with invaluable insights to defend their networks against
evolving threats.
In the initial state of this research that led to the development of Honeyscanner and during
the manual testing of honeypots, an interesting trend was observed in the domain of
static application security testing (SAST) tools. Several previously free and open­source
SAST tools, such as ThunderScan [54], have transitioned to paid models. This shift in
the market underscores the increasing value and demand for robust security testing tools
but also highlights the need for maintaining accessible, open­source options for academic
research and small­scale organizations.
