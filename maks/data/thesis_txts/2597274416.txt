Abstract
This thesis contains the analysis and development of a proposed system for active yaw
control of a HARDI trailed sprayer, in order to reduce relative boom tip velocity caused
by motion of the trailer. A mathematical model of the multibody system is developed
and the existing passive yaw system is mounted onto. Simulated behaviour of the system
is consistent with that reported by HARDI and pressures are found to be well within the
allowable range. The active yaw system is proposed with the main components being a
proportional directional control valve, controlled by a P-controller, and a oriﬁce valve.
Baseline simulations shows that boom tip velocities can be brought down substantially,
when compared to the existing passive yaw system. A parameter study of the new active
yaw system is performed, where the main ﬁndings are that the diameter of the oriﬁce
plays a signiﬁcant part in lowering boom top velocities, and the P-controller’s inﬂuence
on how fast response to trailer motion can be.

Introduction
The Danish company HARDI INTERNATIONAL A/S1 has a focus on the ”application
of crop protection products” (quote from website [1]). One of the means to do so is by
using a so-called trailed sprayer. Such a machine has long booms to either side, on which
spray nozzles are mounted. As the trailed sprayer is pulled by a tractor, bumps on the
ﬁeld, and minor driving corrections made by the driver, will cause small rocking motions.
These rocking motions propagate out into the boom, causing it to swing back and forth.
The eﬀect of this is, that the tip velocity of the boom is no longer the same velocity
of the tractor itself. As the boom swings forward the boom tip velocity is higher than
the tractor velocity, and when swinging backwards the boom tip velocity will be lower
than the tractor velocity. This in turn leads to areas of under-sprayed and over-sprayed,
respectively.
Currently HARDI has a passive yaw system consisting of hydraulic cylinders, with ac-
cumulators attached, which can somewhat mitigate the motions, compared to a system
with no yaw system. However, the company is interested in investigating the possibility
of a active yaw system, which will further mitigate the motions, in order to minimize
the possibility of under- and over-spray even further.

Project statement
In order to even better control the motion of a sprayed trailer, HARDI wishes to develop
an active yaw system, as an improvement to their current passive yaw system. In order
to do this a mathematical multibody dynamical model of the actual trailer itself will
be made. This will be the underlying system onto which the mathematical hydraulic
yaw systems are mounted. Firstly the passive yaw system is modelled to ensure realistic
behaviour and understanding of the system itself. Secondly an active yaw system is
proposed and tested using baseline values for the critical parameters, to verify that the
proposed system is a viable solution. Thirdly, and lastly, the inﬂuence of the critical
parameters are to be investigated in a parameter study. This is done to expose any
underlying caveats of the system.

When looking at moving systems, one can generally divide the issue into two diﬀerent
categories, which are kinematics and dynamics (the latter also being known as kinetics).
Kinematics is purely concerned with the motion of the mechanism, meaning that one is
investigating positions, velocities and accelerations. This is done purely without regards
to the forces and/or moments, which either set these motions into play or, the other way
around, are produced by these motions. Due to this, kinematics is also called referred
to as the ’geometry of motion’ by Meriam and Kraige[3].
Dynamics, on the other
hand looks into relationship between motions and forces. If a system is purely motion

Parameter study
With the baseline system and simulation showing that controlling the boom position is
a feasible task, a new point of interest is in the variation of parameters. The parameter
study will cover the variation of three diﬀerent variables. Two which are within the
control of the designer, being the diameter dAB of the oriﬁce connecting the two sides of
the folding cylinder, and the proportionality constant Kp of the P-controller. Finally a
study of the air entrapment in the system is completed, as this is of interest, since this is
somewhat less controllable by the operator/designer (proper oil ﬁltration and on-board
pump station not accounted for). Once again the model for free air regarding the oil
bulk modulus will be used.
Note that for the sake of brevity, the parameter study will focus on the boom tip position
and velocity.
6.1
Oriﬁce diameter dAB
From Figure 6.1 and Figure 6.2 it can be seen that by varying the diameter of the oriﬁce,
one is able to increase or reduce the response. Increasing the diameter from the baseline
dAB = 1.50 mm might give less ”jitter”, with regards to the position, but it is at the cost
of the boom wandering oﬀfrom the desired position. This will most likely be caught by
the P-controller at a time later than t = 120 s, but is considered insuﬃcient. Decreasing
the diameter of the oriﬁce will, on the other hand, bring the boom to a position closer
to what could be described as the steady state error, almost immediately. It does so at
a much higher positional amplitude, which means that the velocity is undesirably high
at the boom tip for a small oriﬁce diameter.
6.2
Controller proportional value Kp
As the Kp-value is the main parameter in the controller, a study of its inﬂuence is
essential.
From Figure 6.1 it can be seen that even the lowest value of Kp = 20 is
able to stop the boom from wandering within the ﬁrst 120 seconds. As the Kp value
is increased the controller is able to ”catch” the boom sooner. Regarding the velocity
shown in Figure 6.2, the diﬀerence is only signiﬁcant in the interval from 40 seconds to
90 seconds.
Note that the simulation for Kp = 100 stops at t ≈43 s. This is because the controller
over-reacts at t ≈23 s which likely forces the the cylinder to react in unison with the
motion of the trailer and thereby amplifying the error. Which then in turn forces the

Conclusion
By analysis of the sprayed trailer it was possible to set up a working mathematical
model for the multibody system, onto which the existing hydraulic passive yaw system
was imposed. By subjecting the main body in the multibody system to certain mo-
tions, it was possible to induce the motions of the boom as reported by HARDI[20],
both for hydraulic oil with dissolved air and with free air. As HARDI[19] claims issues
with aeration of the oil, the free air model was used henceforth. System pressures and
accumulator behaviour were well within the acceptable range of operation.
A simple active yaw system governed by a P-controller was proposed. This would control
the position of a proportional directional control valve, which in turn would move the
folding cylinder, attempting to move the boom such that is always at 0◦. Between the
to cavities of the cylinder, a oriﬁce valve was installed in order to eliminate any harsh
motions. A baseline simulation with speciﬁc values of the oriﬁce valve diameter dAB, the
controller constant Kp, and the amount of air in the system Fa was chosen, and showed
promising results regarding the relative boom tip velocity. System pressures reached
quite low values, but not as low as the vapour pressure of the hydraulic oil.
In order to investigate the potential of the simple active yaw system, a study was done on
the inﬂuence of the three main parameters dAB, Kp and Fa, by varying one at the time
and comparing to the baseline simulation. Only boom tip position and velocity were
investigated in the parameter study. From the variation of dAB it was found that larger
values would result in less jittery motion of the boom, but slower control of the system,
and lower values of dAB resulted in faster control but with more jitter. With dAB = 0 (no
oriﬁce valve) the system would be so stiﬀ, that the boom tip velocity would exceed those
of the passive yaw system. Intuitively lower values of Kp would yield slower responses
and larger values yould yield faster responses with respect to the boom position. For the
boom tip velocity, some variation could be seen, but seemed to end up at similar values.
A value of Kp = 100 did however result in the controller over-reacting, such that the
simulation terminated, as the system extreme positions and velocities. Event though a
fairly broad range of Fa-values were tested, it was seen that the inﬂuence on both boom
tip position and velocity were not as signiﬁcant as expected.

Future work / possible improvements
As the modelling of both multibody dynamics and the hydraulic system assume some
simpliﬁcations, the accuracy of simulations and performance of the active yaw system
could be improved by some additional work.
8.1
Application of motion on trailer
In order to ensure a more realistic motion of the, a physical measurement of the trailer
movement could be found via experiments. HARDI has a test-center, where it would be
possible to setup such experiments[20].
The motion of the trailer is currently governed by the function fϕ, as shown to the left
in Figure 8.1. This is under the assumption that the motion of the trailer is the same
whether there is no yaw control, passive yaw control or active yaw control. This might
not be the case, so another way to apply motion to the trailer is proposed as shown to
the right in Figure 8.1. The forced motion will be transmitted via a spring/damper to
point T’ and then onto the trailer hitch at point T. This way the trailer’s rotational
movement is also dependent on the dynamics of the rest of the system.

Hydraulics
As the hydraulic cylinders assume no friction losses and no leakage ﬂow, these would
be obvious points of improvements. Regarding the passive yaw system, the assumption
is also that there is perfekt elastic impact when the cylinders ”bottom out”. This is

not true, and this should also be included in the formulation of the cylinders forces.
Coeﬃcients for this behaviour would have to be determined by experiments.
8.3
Controller
As this thesis has only tested the possibility for at simple P-controller, the next step
would be to expand the controllers abilities. As the P-controller only looks at the angle, a
too high Kp value would make the system over-react, it could be beneﬁcial to implement
a PD-controller, such that there is also control of the derivative ( ˙ϕ3). This would most
likely prevent the controller from trying to rapidly force the boom into desired position
and overshooting as a consequence.
Implementation of model-based controls could also prove beneﬁcial as this could give
insight to the stability of the coupled multibody and hydraulic system.
