Abstract
Because of the high auto-ignition temperature of methanol, it is often ignited together
with a pilot fuel, usually diesel. Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) models to optimize
marine engines needs sufficiently accurate chemical mechanism describing the dual-fuel
system while balancing the computational cost.
To keep the simplicity, n-heptane is
usually used as a diesel surrogate in industrial scale engine CFD. This study investigates
the auto-ignition of the dual-fuel system of methanol/n-heptane. The detailed model of
Zhang et al. (2016) was validated against shock tube (ST) and rapid compression machine
(RCM) experimental data for ignition delay time (IDT) of n-heptane, methanol and
methanol/n-heptane fuel mixtures and found agreement within a factor of 2. The model
was also compared to diesel RCM experiments in the negative temperature coefficient
(NTC) region; here the agreement was found to be inadequate, questioning the use of
n-heptane as a diesel surrogate. n-Heptane was continued used in this study as it is
the industrial standard. The chemical coupling of methanol and n-heptane was found to
occur through the competition for the small radicals, with an introduction of an error
in the IDT of a factor of 1.01 when removing the direct coupling C7H15 + CH3OH ⇀
↽
C7H16+CH2OH/CH3O, and an introduction of an error in the IDT of a factor of 10 when
removing the reaction of n-heptane with OH-radicals, or when removing the reaction of
methanol with OH-radicals. The skeletal model of Liu et al. (2023) was compared to the
model of Zhang and found agreement in the IDT within a factor 2.5. Directed relation
graph with error propagation (DRGEP) was performed on the Zhang and Liu models,
but the resulting reduction was inadequate. Semi-global mechanisms consisting of the
global mechanisms of Müller et al. (1992), Zheng et al. (2004), or Gowdagiri et al. (2014)
combined with the model of Li et al. (2022) resulted in predictions of the IDT within a
factor of 10 at 90/10% methanol/n-heptane fuel mixtures for all semi-global models. All
semi-global models failed to predict the NTC region accurately, as the models lagged the
radical competition. The predictions of semi-global models, in the NTC region and at
low temperature, are found to strongly depend on the reaction C7H16 + 2 O2 ⇀
↽I. Two
models using the mechanism of Müller and Li expanded with an inhibiting reaction on the
n-heptane ignition are proposed. The first model resulted in predictions within a factor
of 1.8 at worst, however, IDT predictions at different equivalence ratios were consistently
I
==PAGE:3==
wrong. The second model resulted in predictions within a factor of 10 at worst, however,
the model was found to be very dependent on the optimization conditions, resulting in
the model being better than the first model at high concentrations of methanol, but
considerably worse for pure heptane. The presented results indicate the accuracy of a
semi-global model approach is highly dependent on the optimization conditions, as they
fail to capture the chemical coupling of the co-combustion.

1
Introduction
The movement of goods by ocean is a crucial transportation path for the international
shipping industry. On a global scale the maritime shipping industry accounted for 2.9%
of the total greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions in 2018 and was projected to increase its
emissions by up to 130% by 2050 [1].
The total GHG emissions from the maritime
shipping industry have been increasing over the past years after its decrease during the
covid-19 pandemic and are today higher than the pre-pandemic emissions [2]. Further
it has been stated as a goal for the International Maritime Organization (IMO) that
international shipping should be net-zero GHG emissions by 2050 and a large uptake of
the use of alternative zero or near-zero GHG fuels by 2030 should be achieved [3].
The reduction of GHG emissions by the adaptation of alternative fuels from the transport
sector will be able to help on multiple of the UN sustainability development goals. By
reducing the GHG emissions and changing to other fuels it is possible to create a more
resilient shipping industry as the reliance on fossil-fuel becomes lessened. It is therefore
not only attractive for companies from an environmental and sustainable perspective, but
also from a supply and economical perspective [4].
It is therefore important to find solutions to the GHG emissions from the maritime
shipping industry. One of the promising solutions to the reduction of emissions is the
use of dual-fuel systems. Where an alternative fuel source is combined with diesel to
make the alternative fuel easier to ignite. Companies like Maersk have been introducing
cargo ships into their fleet able to use methanol as a fuel.
The first of these ships
where taken into use in 2023 and further 180 new ships are being made or retrofitted [5].
Other alternative dual-fuels using liquefied natural gas (LNG) or ammonia have also been
suggested, and are currently in production. With the use of ammonia leading to a non-
carbon fuel as green hydrogen becomes cheaper and more available. The use of methanol
can already be realized to have near net-zero emissions from the engines. With the use
of dual-fuel systems it is also possible to convert a large selection of already installed
engines in ships saving the cost and environmental footprint of making new engines to
be installed [6]. As the use of methanol and diesel as a dual-fuel is already in use today,
there is a growing interest in the ability to optimize the engines with computational fluid
1
==PAGE:9==
dynamics (CFD). The use of CFD for optimization of engines is very practical as it is
relatively cheap, compared to experimentation on test engines. One of the CFD methods
of particular interest is the use of 3-D CFD, as it is a promising tool to provide extensive
insight, into the dynamics of the internal combustion of the engine. Because of this,
3-D CFD has the potential to give a good understanding of the emissions of GHG, SOx,
NOx, and soot, and therefore, how the engine can be optimized to reduce the emissions.
However, 3-D CFD is very computationally costly, meaning it will be unfeasible to use
larger detailed chemical mechanisms in the simulations. It is therefore important to find
reduced chemical mechanisms for fuels, that can describe experimental data relatively
well [7, 8]. Currently however, not many chemical mechanisms exist describing the co-
combustion of methanol and diesel simple enough to use in 3-D CFD.

2
Background
In the following the background for the study will be presented. This includes the un-
derstanding of the fuels, diesel, n-heptane and, methanol, followed by the important
properties of the fuels, to determine the quality. Experimental methods and results from
literature relevant for the study is further presented. Algorithms for reducing mecha-
nisms are explored, followed by the presentation of different mechanisms for n-heptane
and methanol found in literature.
2.1
Compression ignition engines
Compression ignition (CI) engines work by the compression of air taken in during the
intake. At the top of the engine stroke, fuel is injected into the cylinder with a high-
pressure fuel injector. When the fuel and air is mixed, it auto-ignites, and the piston is
forced down as gas is released and heated from the reaction, leading to the expansion
of the gas. The time it takes from the injection to the ignition is known as the ignition
delay time. CI engines usually have high efficiencies peaking around 45%, making them
more ideal to use for long distance transport as compared to spark ignition (SI) engines
[9]. Many parameters influence the efficiency and emissions of CI engines; some of these
include the time of injection of the fuel [10], Laminar flame speed [11], as well as, the
ignition delay time of the fuel [12]. Laminar flame speed and ignition delay time will
be explained in section 2.4, as these are important parameters in characterizing the fuel
behavior from a chemical kinetics perspective. In figure 1 is an illustration of different
stages in a two-stroke engine cycle, often used in marine engines. In (a) air is pulled in
to the combustion chamber as a fuel charge is injected and ignited at the top. In (b)
the piston is forced down by the ignition, pushing the air up into the upper part of the
combustion chamber, while the exhaust gas from the ignition is forced out. In (c) the air
enters the combustion chamber preparing a new air charge, while the last exhaust gas is
removed together with some of the air. In (d) the exhaust port is closed, and the piston
moves up to ignite the new charge [13].

2.2
Diesel
The most common fuel used in CI engines is diesel. Diesel is a fossil fuel produced from
crude oil, usually by distillation. The properties of the produced diesel depend, to a
large degree, on the crude oil used for the production, as components like impurities and
the size of the carbon containing molecules can vary with the oilfield from which the
crude oil is sourced. Diesel consists of many different components such as alkanes, cyclo-
alkanes, aromats, and some alkenes, as well as some impurities such as sulfur, nitrogen,
and oxygen. In general, diesel consists of carbon based molecules with carbon numbers
between 9 (C9) and 23 (C23), with 16 (C16) being the most prevalent. A carbon number
distribution of a typical diesel fuel can be seen in figure 2. The combination of components
has a large influence on how the diesel behaves, as the components influence the cetane
number, volatility and viscosity. Cetane number, volatility and viscosity therefore also
have a large influence on the properties of the fuel when used in a CI engine. The cetane
number is important as it determines the ease of auto-ignition in the CI engine and is,
therefore, the primary factor determining if a fuel can be used in CI engines. In Europe,
the cetane number for commercial diesel fuel needs to be minimum 51 [14].

2.2.1
n-Heptane as diesel surrogate
As diesel is very complicated and consists of many components a surrogate chemical is
usually used to describe diesel in modeling. In many cases n-heptane is used as it has
a cetane number (53.8) [15] close to that of commercial diesel. Doing this drastically
simplifies the modeling work even if some of the nuance is lost when burning n-heptane
compared to diesel. Studies have been made looking into if additives to n-heptane would
make it act closer to diesel, without drastically increasing the complexity of the mixture.
One such study is that of Luo et al. [16] that added varying amounts of toluene and
1-hexene to the n-heptane, to closer represent the mixture of alkanes, aromats, and
alkenes found in diesel fuel. The mixtures were compared to the ignition and emissions of
commercial diesel. The study found that a mixture of 76%, 19%, and 5% of, respectively,
n-heptane, toluene, and 1-hexene, gave ignitions delay times and emissions closest to that
of diesel. n-heptane was found to be in good agreement on some parameters, however, it
did show some larger differences on others such as the in-cylinder pressure profile and the
heat release rate. Machrafi et al. [17] also found that a mixture of n-heptane and toluene
would result in ignition delay times closer to that of diesel however here a 70% n-heptane,
30% toluene mixture was found to be the best. They also found that n-heptane slightly
5
==PAGE:13==
underpredicted the ignition delay time compared to diesel. Other studies have also been
made looking into surrogate diesel fuels [18, 19], however, no studies have been found
comparing pure n-heptane to diesel. It was, however, found that mixtures of n-heptane
and iso-octane at different ratios is used as primary reference fuels and, therefore, also as
surrogates for fuels in simulations [20]. The use of n-heptane as a base for most purposed
diesel surrogates, indicates the possibility of using n-heptane as the surrogate for diesel
in this study. The validity of this is further explored in section 4.
2.3
Methanol as alternative fuel
Methanol is a promising green fuel alternative for use in the transport industry. However,
methanol has a very low cetane number (∼3) [15] compared to that of diesel, meaning
it is hard to ignite by compression. This leads to the introduction of dual-fuel, where
a smaller amount of a fuel with a higher cetane number, such as diesel, is used. In the
dual-fuel system, the methanol is either introduced into the engine in the intake, where
it is mixed with air before entering the combustion chamber. In this configuration the
methanol/air mixture is compressed, and diesel is injected when the piston reaches the
top, igniting the diesel/methanol/air mixture. In the other configuration, air is added
as the only intake and methanol is injected into the combustion cylinder much like the
diesel. However, the methanol and diesel are not necessarily injected at the same time
[21, 22].
2.3.1
Methanol production
Because of the growing interest in methanol as a green alternative fuel, it is important
that the methanol is produced sustainably. Today the production of methanol world wide
is around 98 Mt annually where almost all the methanol is produced from fossil fuels,
usually natural gas or coal. Only around 0.2 Mt is produced renewable, where most is
produced as bio-methanol. Further, methanol consumption on a global scale is expected
to grow to 120 Mt by 2025 and 500 Mt by 2050, with methanol applications in transport,
heating, and methanol-to-olefins expecting to grow [23].
6
==PAGE:14==
Gray and brown
Most of the worlds methanol is produced from natural gas (gray).
This is done by
first producing syngas (H2, CO, and CO2) by either steam reforming or autothermal
reforming. Some of the syngas can also be produced by partial oxidation of coal (brown).
The syngas is then converted into methanol at a pressure of 50-100 bar and a temperature
of 200-300 ◦C by catalytic hydrogenation of CO using Cu/ZnO/Al2O3 catalysts [24].
Blue
An alternative to gray methanol is what is known as blue methanol. Blue methanol
is produced in much the same way as gray methanol, however, the reforming step to
produce the syngas is changed. This can be done in multiple ways, such as, electrical
heating from renewable sources in the reforming step, as well as reusing any CO2 that is
produced during the reforming, and storing any excess using carbon capture and storage
(CCS). Blue methanol can also be produced from the catalytic hydrogenation of CO2.
This is done by using hydrogen produced from electrolysis using renewable energy (green
hydrogen), and adding a source of CO2 captured from other non-renewable industry, such
as cement or electricity [23, 25].
Green
Finally, methanol can be produced sustainably as green methanol. Green methanol is
usually produced in one of two ways known as E-methanol and bio-methanol. E-methanol
is the production method of using green hydrogen together with a source of green CO2,
meaning CO2 produced from the burning of biomass, by the use of catalytic hydrogena-
tion of CO2. Bio-methanol is produced from biomass, often this will be by-products form
other industries such as agriculture, but it can also be directly produced for the purpose.
Bio-methanol is produced by first gasifing or reforming the biomass, depending on the
source, to produce renewable syngas. The syngas is then used as it would be in the
production of gray and brown methanol. In some cases the syngas from the biomass will
be added extra hydrogen from green hydrogen to get a better stoichiometric composition
of the syngas, resulting in a better yield of bio-methanol [23–25].

2.4
Performance parameters
As discussed in section 2.1, some parameters of the fuel are useful in determining how
well a certain fuel will work in a CI engine. Most CI engines are kinetically controlled,
meaning it is the reaction of the fuel that is the primary influence on the ignition of
the fuel and, as such, how the engine should be designed. It is therefore important to
understand these parameters and how they are measured experimentally.
2.4.1
Laminar flame speed
Laminar flame speed (LFS) is an important property of a fuel. LFS is defined as the speed
an adiabatic, unstretched, premixed planar flame propagates through the unburned fuel.
At high pressure the LFS is usually found using propagation spherical flame method,
where a fuel is ignited in the center of a sphere and the pressure and volume curves are
measured. A schematic of a representation of the experimental setup can be seen in fig-
ure 3. The LFS can be found in two ways known as constant-pressure method (CPM) or
constant-volume method (CVM). In CPM the increase in radii of the flame is measured
using high speed imaging to determine the LFS. In the CVM the LFS can be determined
from the change in pressure as the flame propagates in the vessel. This method is usually
used for high pressure high temperature processes as it does not require optical access,
and can therefore, be used at engine-like conditions. LFS indicates many of the physic-
ochemical parameters of the fuel hereunder diffusivity, reactivity, and exothermicity. As
such, LFS is an important property as it helps indicate the amount of fuel needed and
the efficiency of the fuel [26]. LFS is, therefore, often used to validate chemical reaction
mechanisms of fuel combustion [27–29]. For the validation of mechanisms in this study,
LFS experiments will not be considered, as it is complicated to simulate and, therefore,
out of time scope of the study.

2.4.2
Ignition delay time
Ignition delay time (IDT) is the time it takes for a fuel/air mixture to auto-ignite at a
specific condition. The IDT is to a very large extent dependent on the system conditions,
such as the temperature, pressure, dilution, and stoichiometry. This comes from the IDT
being largely controlled by the reaction path and kinetics of the fuel [30]. The IDT,
therefore, gives a very good indication of the quality of the fuel and the ability to use
the fuel in a CI engine [31]. IDT will, in this study, be used as the primary validation
parameter of the developed models. Multiple methods exist to determine the IDT of a
fuel, these are discussed below. A list of experiments of IDTs from literature can be seen
in tables 1 to 3 for shock tubes, rapid compression machines, and single-cylinder engines,
respectively.

Shock tubes
Shock tubes (ST) work by having one end of the tube filled with the fuel/air mixture of
interest at low pressure, and another end filled with an inert gas at high pressure, with
the two sides being separated by a diaphragm. The diaphragm is then burst and the inert
gas shock wave begins to move towards the fuel side. For temperatures and pressures at
engine conditions reflection STs are usually used. These work by measuring the pressure
close to the end wall of the ST. Here the pressure increase from the shock wave can be
measured, when the wave moves towards the end wall and, when the wave is reflected
back. The IDT can then be determined from the time between the pressure change from
the reflected shock wave, to the further pressure change caused by the ignition of the fuel.
The use of reflected STs also means, that it is possible to measure different components in
the ignition, commonly OH-radicals are measured as these are often closely correlated to
the ignition of the fuel. Some problems can however occur when the IDTs become long.
This is caused by the reflected shock wave hitting the other end of the ST being reflected
back towards the fuel mixture, if the ST is not long enough. This leads to ST mostly
being used for lower IDTs up to 100 ms, with the possible time being dependent on the
length of the ST [61, 62]. A conceptual schematic of the workings of an ST can be seen
in figure 4, and an experimental pressure curve can be seen in figure 5 with indication of
IDT from Wu et al. [48]. Measurements of IDT using ST have been made by Burke et al.
[32], Fieweger et al. [33], Cooke et al. [34], Bowman [35], Natarajan et al. [36], Pinzón
et al. [37], Noorani et al. [38] for methanol, Ciezki et al. [39], Vermeer et al. [40], Shen
et al. [41], Gauthier et al. [42], Heufer et al. [43], Gowdagiri et al. [44], Alturaifi et al.
[45], Wang et al. [46], Li et al. [47] for n-heptane or diesel and Wu et al. [48], Li et al.
[49], Zhu et al. [50] for methanol/n-heptane or methanol/diesel mixtures.

Rapid compression machine
Another method for determining IDT involves rapid compression machines (RCM). The
RCM works by compressing a premixed fuel charge very quickly. This is done by moving
a piston, pneumatic, hydraulic or a combination, that moves a piston inside a combustion
chamber. The use of RCM is interesting, as it much more closely represent the combustion
inside an engine cylinder, compared to ST, as the RCM resembles a one cylinder engine
only performing the compression. RCMs can usually be preheated as well to more closely
mimic an engine. The IDT can then be found from the change in pressure, as IDT is
defined as the time from the initial pressure increase from the compression of the gas
to the pressure increase from the ignition.
Some problems can arise however, if the
IDT becomes to low the ignition of the fuel can happen during the compression, leading
to inaccurate results.
RCMs are therefore only used at high IDTs.
The mechanical
nature of RCMs will lead to larger uncertainties in the measured IDTs, compared to the
STs, as keeping the volume constant after the compression is difficult, and often require
elaborate setups.
Further the long IDTs observed in RCM will, in many cases, lead
to a decrease of temperature and pressure as energy is lost to the environment. The
compression temperature and pressure, used as the ignition temperature and pressure
when comparing experiments, is therefore not constant after the compression and will,

can be seen in figure 6 and an experimental pressure curve can be seen in figure 7 with

Single-cylinder engine
A final method of determining IDT is the use of a single-cylinder engine. This is practical
as the experiments will naturally run at engine like conditions. However, as this method
uses an actual engine, the IDT will can become dependent on the parameters of the
engine, such as, the size of the combustion chamber, the load on the piston, and the
injection strategy [64]. When an engine is used, the IDT is often measured in one of two
ways. Either from the injection time to the time when certain amount of heat energy
has been released, this can either be the point of maximum heat release rate, or after an
amount of the total heat energy of the fuel has been released. Pressure can also be used
from the time of injection to the sharp pressure increase caused by the combustion. When
engines are used for the experimental results, the IDT can sometimes be given in crank
angle degrees (CAD), a unit indicating the position of the crank in the rotation around
the connecting rod, and is a time unit given the rotational speed of the engine is known
[16, 17]. An illustration of an experimental setup can be seen in figure 8. Measurements
of IDT using single-cylinder engines have not been found for methanol, most likely caused
by the inability to ignite methanol in a CI engine by itself. However, Machrafi et al. [17]
and Luo et al. [16] used it for n-heptane and Yin et al. [22] used it for methanol/diesel
mixtures.

2.5
Chemical kinetics modeling
To use computational fluid dynamics (CFD), for simulations of engines, a reaction mech-
anism must be made to describe the combustion of the fuel. This can be done in multiple
ways depending on the complexity of interest in the model. As such, a model can contain
many thousands of reactions and species or very few.
2.5.1
Detailed
Detailed models are made to be as comprehensive as possible.
They only consist of
elementary reactions, and contain as many side chains needed, to describe the system
of interest. They therefore, include many species and reactions. One example is the
AramcoMech 3.0 model for C1-C4, consisting of 581 species and 3037 reactions [65].
Many of the more simplified mechanisms are based on the larger more complex models
from where a new model is produced [66]. Earlier versions of the AramcoMech mechanism
also exist, called 2.0 and 1.3, and is also extensively used in literature [27].
2.5.2
Skeletal
Skeletal mechanisms are developed from the more comprehensive mechanisms, and in-
clude only the reaction paths and components relevant to the given initial reactants, final
products, and reaction conditions. This may drastically simplify the mechanism by re-
moving most side chains, and reactions of molecules irrelevant to the reaction of interest.
The skeletal mechanism, as compared to simpler mechanisms, is still only comprised of
elementary reactions, meaning the mechanism still may include, a relatively large num-
ber of species and reactions [28]. Skeletal mechanisms are often generated by the use
of different algorithms such as directed relation graph with error propagation (DRGEP)
and computational singular perturbation (CSP) [27, 28, 67], the DRGEP algorithm will
be discussed in section 2.6.
Skeletal mechanisms are then often further reduced and
optimized.
16
==PAGE:24==
2.5.3
Semi-global and global
Semi-global reaction mechanisms are based on the concept of lumping multiple elemen-
tary reactions into a global reaction. However, compared to the full global mechanisms,
where all elementary reaction is combined into one or more reactions, in the semi-global
mechanisms, only some of the elementary reactions are lumped into global reactions.
This is most common in the case of combinations of models, where a model of one com-
ponent is combined with the model of another component. Thorsen et al. [68] used the
global four-step reaction mechanism for n-heptane from Müller et al. [69] and a skeletal
mechanism of methane to describe the ignition of methane/n-heptane mixtures. The
generation of a semi-global or global model, from a more detialed model, is often done
using quasi steady-state approximations (QSSA) [70, 71], as this can drastically reduce
the mechanism while still keeping most of the physical perspective.
2.5.4
Optimized models
Optimized models are the use of an often reduced model, either skeletal, semi-global
or global, from where the reaction constants are fitted to data for the predictions to
better coincide with measurements or another more detailed model [72]. It is, however,
important to stress that some of the physical perspective is lost as the reaction kinetics
(reaction rate constants) no longer reflect the real reaction rates. This can result in the
model giving good predictions within the optimized area, but give extremely inaccurate
predictions outside. These models are therefore only made for exact conditions, meaning
extrapolation is impossible. It is, therefore, not always recommended, even if the resulting
model will fit measurements better.

n-Heptane

Methanol/n-heptane mixtures
Today there is a growing interest in alternative fuels for use in transport.
One such
alternative is using methanol and diesel (heptane as surrogate fuel), where the diesel
is used to ignite the methanol.

methanol and n-heptane, making the system considerably more complicated.

follows that the detailed n-heptane mechanisms includes a detailed methanol mechanism.
This can be seen from the detailed models for n-heptane such as that of Curran [80]
or Zhang [81]. Detailed methanol/n-heptane mechanisms are therefore not developed.
However, varying degrees of complexity in skeletal models do exist. For validation of
methanol/n-heptane mechanisms, detailed mechanisms made for combustion of larger
alkanes are therefore often used.

2.8
Goal of study
The goal of this study is to develop a semi-global model to describe the ignition of a dual-
fuel mixture of n-heptane and methanol. A detailed mechanism will be found in literature
and validated against experimental data of ignition delay times (IDTs) from the literature.
This mechanism will be used for validation of future mechanisms. Experimental IDTs
from literature of n-heptane and diesel will be compared to confirm the validity of using
n-heptane as a diesel surrogate. The influential reactions and chemical coupling in the
n-heptane and methanol co-ignition system will be explored to find important reactions
to keep in a semi-global model. The possibility of reducing models from literature using
DRGEP will be explored. A semi-global mechanism will be developed by combining a
global mechanism from literature with a detailed methanol mechanism from literature,
the semi-global mechanism will be compared to the validation mechanism. The semi-
global mechanism will expanded and optimized to better fit the methanol/n-heptane
co-combustion system, and compared to the predictions of the validation mechanism.
The simulations in this study will be conducted with ANSYS CHEMKIN commercial
software as provided by the technical university of Denmark (DTU).

Validation of mechanism
For the development of future models, it is practical to be able to compare to a model
validated experimentally. This is done by comparing a mechanism to experimental data
from literature. The primary reason to do this is the ability to compare models outside
experimental data making it easier to validate other mechanisms at the conditions of
interest. To do this model predictions of IDTs are calculated in CHEMKIN, using the O-D
homogeneous reactor at constant volume, for ST experiments, and for RCM experiments
a volume profile is added, calculated from pressure profiles of experimental data, while
the volume is assumed constant at each time step.

n-heptane models contain the methanol sub mechanism, as such, it should be possible

5
Chemical coupling between n-heptane and methanol
The chemical coupling of the n-heptane and methanol is important to get a good predic-
tion of the IDT. As such, it is interesting to look into the different potential couplings.
In the following the reactions and thermodynamic data is taken form the Zhang model.
5.1
Direct coupling
One of the potential pathways for H-abstraction from n-heptane and methanol is the
reaction between the radicals of both, resulting in a direct coupling between the two
species. This could be possible in different ways depending on the hydrogen abstracted
from the n-heptane or the methanol. In general two reactions could happen:
C7H15 + CH3OH ⇀
↽C7H16 + CH3O
(7)
C7H15 + CH3OH ⇀
↽C7H16 + CH2OH
(8)
Here C7H15 can take four forms in both reactions, depending on which H has been
abstracted from n-heptane, with H-abstraction on the second C being the most thermo-
dynamically favorable. It is important to state that, in the Zhang model it was found that
only CH3O of the two methanol-radicals, the other being CH2OH, was included to react
with n-heptane. To explore the influence of these reactions on the IDT of the system,
the CH3O reactions are removed from the model and the predictions are compared to
the model with the reactions. This can be seen in figure 20 (a). Further, the reactions
of n-heptane with CH2OH-radical is added, with the same reaction parameters as for
the reaction with CH3O-radical, to see if the addition of this reaction will result in a
difference in the IDT, this can be seen in figure 20 (b).
39
==PAGE:47==
Figure 20: Model predictions of IDT of Zhang model with reactions of
n-heptane and CH3O-radical removed (a) or n-heptane and CH2OH-
radical added (b), with the relative difference at each temperature of the
two models.
As can be seen from figure 20 (a) and (b) the direct coupling has a no influence on the
system. The predictions of the IDT are within a factor 1.01 of the Zhang model at low
temperature, resulting in a maximum 1% difference in the IDT predictions. The same
conclusion of the unimportance of the direct coupling of methanol and n-heptane in the
fuel blend was also drawn by Xu et al. [87].
5.2
Competition of OH-radical
An important aspect of the ignition of the methanol/n-heptane fuel mixture is the first
H-abstraction of the n-heptane. A strong contributor to this, is reaction of n-heptane
with the OH-radical.
C7H16 + OH ⇀
↽C7H15 + H2O
(9)
However, methanol can also react with the OH-radical to from either CH2OH or CH3O.
CH3OH + OH ⇀
↽CH2OH + H2O
(10)
CH3OH + OH ⇀
↽CH3O + H2O
(11)
here the reaction of CH3OH to form CH2OH-radicals are more thermodynamically favor-
able, at low temperatures, leading to more production of CH2OH-radicals compared to
CH3O-radicals. However, CH2OH-radicals are less reactive than CH3O-radicals, slow-
ing the chain propagation of radicals to some extent. Further, the CH3O-radicals will
40
==PAGE:48==
undergo thermal decomposition to form CH2OH-radicals and H-radicals. This leads to
a large concentration of the less reactive CH2OH-radicals. CH2OH-radicals can again
favorably react with another OH-radical to from CH2O (formaldehyde), a stable species,
or with O2 to form CH2O and HO2-radicals, a less reactive radical. The formation of
CH2O effectively ends the chain propagation of radicals, resulting in a slowing effect of
the system. Further, the oxidation of n-heptane and its radicals lead to the generation of
significantly more radicals, and therefore, a better propagation of radicals in the system
compared to the methanol oxidation. Overall, this leads to a significant reduction in the
availability of OH-radicals resulting in a considerable increase in the IDT at low temper-
atures. To further explorer this, the influence of the reaction C7H16 with OH-radical
to form C7H15 is removed from the model, this can be seen on figure 21 (a) and the
reactions of CH3OH with OH-radical to form CH2OH or CH3O are removed from the
model in (b).
Figure 21: Model predictions of IDT of Zhang model with reactions of
n-heptane (a) or methanol (b) and OH-radical removed compared to the
base model, with the relative difference at each temperature of the two
models.
As can be seen from figure 21 in both (a) and (b) the removal of the reactions between the
fuels and OH-radicals results in a drastically different prediction of the IDT. The removal
of the n-heptane reactions can be seen to increase the IDT at low temperature, with
predictions of the IDT falling within a factor 10 of the Zhang model, resulting in a 750%
difference compared to the base model, this is expected as n-heptane in the system is
added to ignite the methanol. When comparing to pure methanol as fuel for the system,
it can be seen that some of the n-heptane is ignited, as the IDT is still faster than for
pure methanol. Removing the reactions between methanol and OH-radicals results in a
41
==PAGE:49==
much lower prediction of the IDT at low temperatures, with predictions of the IDT falling
within a factor 10 of the Zhang model, resulting in a 95% difference compared to the base
model. Comparing to the pure n-heptane IDT predictions the resulting predictions are
relatively close. This is especially the case at low temperatures where the predictions
become almost identical. This could indicate that at low temperatures the removal of the
methanol OH-radical reactions results in no ignition of methanol. This indicates, that
the competition of the radicals in the system is the driving force in the NTC region and
at low temperature for predicting the IDT of the mixture, and the increase in the IDT
with addition of methanol.
Similar conclusions was drawn by Zhu et al. [50] for methanol/diesel blends using sim-
ulations with a diesel surrogate fuel blend consisting of 41.3, 36.8, and 21.9 molar% of
n-hexadecane, isocetane, and 1-methylnaphthalene, respectively. Here it was found that,
the methanol generates very little OH-radicals compared to the diesel at low tempera-
tures. Further, they found that the OH-radical consumption of the diesel decreased as
the concentration of methanol was increased in the fuel. This was said to indicate a com-
petition between the diesel and methanol for the OH-radicals, leading to a promotion of
the consumption of methanol and a decrease in the consumption of diesel. It was found
that the reaction of CH3OH with OH-radicals to form CH2OH-radicals was the most
likely, causing the reaction of diesel and HO2-radicals to be promoted, a significantly less
favorable reaction than with the OH-radicals. Overall it was found to lead to an increase
in the IDT at low temperatures as the methanol concentration in the fuel was increased.
Further, it was found by Wu et al. [48], that as the methanol concentration was increased,
a promotion of the formation of HO2-radicals were observed. At low temperatures the
reaction of two HO2-radicals produces H2O2 (hydrogen peroxide). At low temperatures
H2O2 is relatively stable, leading to a slowing effect on the ignition. At high tempera-
tures, HO2-radicals tend to produce H2O2 by H-abstraction with the fuel instead, at the
same time, H2O2 decomposes much easier at high temperatures to two OH-radicals. This
leads to an increase in IDT at low temperatures for higher methanol concentrations, while
the IDT at high temperatures is almost the same for every concentration of methanol.
The same conclusion was drawn by Xu et al. [87].
42
==PAGE:50==
It can be concluded that chemical coupling of n-heptane and methanol is primarily hap-
pening in the competition for small radicals, as this seems to be the driving force in the
predictions of the IDT at low temperature, with the direct interaction between methanol
and n-heptane having little to no influence on the predictions. For skeletal models this
should not be a problem, as the reactions included in skeletal models are still elementary
and would therefore still include the radical chemistry found to be important for the
system. However, this could lead to problems for semi-global models, as the chemistry
described does not include all the radical chemistry and could, therefore, significantly
impact the predictions of the models.

Detailed methanol model with global n-heptane model
The global mechanisms are of particular interest, as these include only few species and
reactions and, therefore, require much less computational power. It would, therefore, be
very favorable if it is possible to use global mechanisms to describe the system. However,
as methanol and n-heptane are highly chemically coupled, as discussed in section 5, this
could make it difficult to make global models work for this system. The use of a global
model for n-heptane to ignite another fuel has been done in literature to some success.
Thorsen et al. [68] used the four-step global n-heptane mechanism from Müller et al. [69]
for methane/n-heptane mixtures. It is therefore interesting, if this simple method could
also be used for the methanol/n-heptane mixtures. The n-heptane mechanism of Müller
et al. will henceforth be refereed to as the MPL mechanism.
8.1
MPL mechanism
The MPL mechanism developed by Müller et al. [69] is a four-step global mechanism
for the combustion of n-heptane to H2O and CO2. The mechanism consists of three
irreversible and one reversible reaction containing in total five species. The reactions and
rate expressions of the mechanism are as follows:
C7H16 →X
k1 [C7H16]
X + 11 O2 →7 CO2 + 8 H2O
k2 [X] [O2]

reaction is the addition of O2 to the n-heptane molecule to form the oxygenated radical
OOQOOH represented by I1. This reaction is reversible to represent the inhibition of
the reactions at low temperatures. The fourth reaction describes the reaction of I1 to
form chain-propagating radicals Y. The fifth reaction describes the reaction of a chain-

sixth reaction describes the possibility of the oxygenated radical I1 to form a branching
radical I2. The seventh reaction describes the reaction of I2 to from chain-propagating
radicals Y. Reactions 6 and 7 controls the high temperature NTC behavior of n-heptane.

pre-exponential factors are very high in both models in the region of 1020 for the forward
reaction and 1030 for the backward reaction, when given in cal, K, mol, cm3, s. Changing
these exponential factors slightly, would result in very different predictions of the IDTs,
and in many cases numerical problems would be encountered for the models, making it
numerically impossible to calculate.
The model of Voglsam and Winters (VW) [86], consisting of 11 global reactions expanding
the ZMC model further, was also considered. Here the model took a very long time to
calculate, giving a result where the time steps were very small at periods. This seems to
indicate a numerical problem in the model. It was attempted to increase the tolerance of
the system, however, this did not improve the solutions. The reaction R0+2 O2 ⇀
↽RO0-1,
equivalent to the reaction C7H16 + 2 O2 ⇀
↽I1/I in the ZMC, GO and MPL models, was
observed to result in the biggest problem, where again the pre-exponential factors are
very large. The sensitivity and numerical stability of the global models, therefore, seem
to be largely dependent on the pre-exponential factors of the reaction to produce the
oxygenated radical. However, the MPL model did show less sensitivity to this reaction,
as the change in the pre-exponential factor did not result in numerical problems.

Optimization of MPL/Li model
As the initial semi-global models described in section 8 did not result in sufficient accuracy
in the IDT predictions. It was decided that the MPL model [69] was interesting, as this
was the most stable of the global models. However, the MPL model predicts the IDT to
be much lower than the predictions of the Zhang model, as high as a factor 20, as the
concentration of methanol in the fuel was increased. This short coming of the semi-global
model could be solved in multiple ways. One method would be the expansion of the model
by the addition of reactions of the fuel with the small radicals in the system as this was
found in section 5 to be important for controlling the IDT. However, addition of reactions
with radicals in the system would increase the number of reactions and species in the
global model considerably increasing the computation time. The method chosen to try
was instead to expand the MPL model with an inhibiting reaction that was dependent
on the concentration of methanol, as this was the simplest approach to slow the reaction
of the n-heptane. By doing this the model was only expanded with one more species
and reaction.
The inhibiting effect was achieved by effectively removing some of the
n-heptane from the system. By holding the n-heptane in a "non-reactive" form, and
letting it slowly react back to the "reactive" form of n-heptane. This was done with the
reversible reaction:

Conclusion
In this study the mechanism of Zhang et al. [81] was validated against experimental
ignition delay time (IDT) data of both shock tube (ST) and rapid compression machine
(RCM) experiments from literature. The mechanism of Zhang found agreement with IDT
experimental data for n-heptane, methanol and methanol/n-heptane within a factor 2.
Experiential data of n-heptane was compared to experimental data of diesel and found
large discrepancies for RCM experiments in the negative temperature coefficient (NTC)
region. The diesel experiments where attempted recreated using the Zhang model, but
found a constant decrease in the IDT at the experiential conditions, this was not the
case for the diesel experiments. The comparison of n-heptane and diesel ignition warrant
further study.
The chemical coupling of methanol and n-heptane was explored. It was found that the
coupling primarily happens through the competition for the small radicals, with resulting
IDT predictions being within a factor 10 at low temperature, for the removal of the n-
heptane reaction with the OH-radical, or the methanol reaction with the OH-radical. The
direct coupling between methanol and n-heptane was found to happen in the reaction be-
tween the n-heptane-radicals and methanol to produce n-heptane and methanol-radicals
and the inverse reaction. The direct coupling was found to have no influence on the sys-
tem, with the removal of the reaction of n-heptane-radical with methanol and the inverse
reaction resulting in IDT predictions within a factor 1.01.
The skeletal mechanism of Liu et al. [29] for methanol/n-heptane co-combustion was
compared to the model of Zhang and found close agreement with IDT predictions in the
NTC region being within a factor 2.5 for pure n-heptane and IDT predictions at high
concentrations of methanol within a factor 2. The Liu model resulted in a decrease in
computation time of 63% compared to the Zhang model. Directed relation graph with
error propagation (DRGEP) was preformed on the model of Zhang and Liu for further
reduction. The Zhang model was reduce from 1268 species and 5336 reactions to 231
species and 1317 reactions with an induced error of 10%, resulting in IDT predictions
within a factor 1.1, and the Liu model was reduced form 52 species and 182 reactions to
46 species and 179 reactions with an induced error of 0.22%, resulting in IDT predictions
69
==PAGE:77==
being close to a factor 1.
The four-step model of Müller et al. [69], and seven-step models of Zheng et al. [84] and
Gowdagiri et al. [85] were combined with the detailed methanol mechanism of Li et al.
[78]. The semi-global models of Müller and Gowdagiri found good agreement with the
Zhang model for pure n-heptane at low and high temperature, within a factor 2.5 and
1.1, respectively. This was also true in the NTC region where the models of Müller and
Gowdagiri were within a factor 2 and 4, respectively. The Zheng model performed worse,
within a factor 20, in the NTC region and low temperature. For 90/10% methanol/n-
heptane fuel mixtures all the semi-global models preformed comparatively within a factor
10 at low temperature. All semi-global models failed to predict the NCT region well at
all conditions, this is attributed to the lag of radical competition in the models. The
accuracy of the IDT predictions of the semi-global models where found to be highly
dependent on the reaction C7H16+2 O2 ⇀
↽I, with activation energy and pre-exponential
factor controlling the IDT prediction in the NTC region. It was found that small changes
in the pre-exponential factor could lead to numerical problems in the models, with the
model of Müller being most resistant.
Two semi-global mechanisms for the methanol/n-heptane co-combustion based on the
four-step global n-heptane mechanism of Müller et al. [69] combined with the detailed
methanol mechanism of Li et al.
[78] was proposed.
The four-step global n-heptane
mechanism was expanded by a reversible inhibiting reaction slowing the ignition of n-
heptane in the system. In the first proposed mechanism this was done by the addition of
a methanol dependent reaction with n-heptane to produce a "non-reactive" n-heptane,
with the inverse reaction only dependent on the "non-reactive" n-heptane, this reaction
was then optimized. In the second proposed mechanism the inverse reaction was added
an oxygen dependence to control the equivalence ratio, the full global model was then
optimized.
The optimizations were performed in the NTC region of n-heptane.
At
high concentrations of methanol low concentration of n-heptane in the fuel mixture the
proposed mechanisms showed significant improvement to the model of Müller with the two
models resulting in IDT predictions within a factor 1.6 and 1.8, respectively, compared to
a factor 10 of the model of Müller. At high concentrations of n-heptane low concentration
of methanol, the first model resulted in IDT predictions within a factor of 1.8 of the Zhang

model, with the second model resulting in worse IDT predictions within a factor of 2.9.
The model of Müller resulted in IDT predictions within a factor of 2.5. The models
were also compared for pure n-heptane here the second model was observed to perform
worse, within a factor of 4 of the Zhang model, than the first model and the model of
Müller, which predicted the same IDT within a factor of 1.5 of the Zhang model. The
first model was found to inaccurately predict the equivalence ratios at most conditions,
while the second model showed significant reduction in the accuracy when used outside

optimization conditions resulting in inaccurate IDT predictions outside the optimization
conditions. The primary factor for the inaccurate IDT predictions is expected to be the
lag of radical competition in the semi-global models.

Future work
An important part of any further work would be to take a deeper look at the comparison
between n-heptane and diesel. As it was found here that n-heptane does not necessarily
function well as a surrogate for diesel in simulation. Further, the comparison between
n-heptane and diesel is very limited and highly lagging in literature. In the literature,
the comparison does not go much further than the cetane number of n-heptane and diesel
is close, and therefore n-heptane can be used as a surrogate. However, this ignores the
very large chemical difference between n-heptane and diesel. The use of n-heptane as a
diesel surrogate seems to be the "standard" that most literature uses. This might the
coming from the use of n-heptane/iso-octane mixtures used as laboratory standard fuel
mixture for petrol, as the mixture of n-heptane and iso-octane is how the octane rating
of petrol is defined. This makes sens for petrol, as it consists of smaller carbon chains
making n-heptane and iso-octanes carbon content, within the usual distribution of petrol.
However, diesel consists of much larger carbon chains, and is, therefore, chemically very
different form both n-heptane and iso-octane. Looking at cetane, iso-cetane, or a mixture
thereof, as a surrogate could be interesting. However, cetane and iso-cetane have much
bigger carbon numbers potentially complicating the model considerably, especially if a
semi-global mechanism approach is not feasible.
because of the time constraints, it was not possible to optimize the models for the time
needed to guarantee an optimum. Further, using CHEMKIN it was very hard to follow
the optimization procedure, as it is not described outside the program, and is not very
transparent within the program. It would therefore have been interesting if it was possible
to either, use another program, or making the optimization algorithm myself. This would
have made it easier to follow the optimization as it progressed to see the improvement
over time. This is especially the case for the genetic algorithm. Here it was not possible
to find information on how each generation was calculated, and in turn when a new
generation was started. The only thing stated by the program was the "Trial" number,
and judging from the calculation time, this could not have been a generation, as one trial
took approximately the time of one simulation of the model. If, however, this could be
solved. It would have been interesting optimize the expanded MPL/Li O2 model by using
72
==PAGE:80==
different methanol/heptane fuel mixtures as well, to see if this could give an improved
result.
As the problem with the global models was the competition for small radicals. It could
have been interesting to try and change the MPL model to include the initial reaction of
n-heptane with some of the small radicals. As the Li model was a detailed model, and
therefore already included the radicals, the inclusion of the radicals in the MPL model
would not lead to more species when combined with the Li model. This could potentially
solve the problem of the slowing effect of the methanol, as the radical competition would
exist in this model. If this could work, it would be interesting to also look at the simpli-
fication of the Li model. As the Li model is detailed, the full MPL/Li model is still very
large, and to be useful, the methanol part would also need to the reduced considerably.
This would be needed for any improvement using a semi-global approach, compared to
using a reduced skeletal mechanism like the Liu mechanism instead.
To reduce the Li model it would be interesting to explore more mechanism reduction
methods such as principle component analysis (PCA) or computational singular pertur-
bation (CSP) in combination with DRG and DRGEP. This would be interesting for the
Li model, as it contains the radical chemistry, found to be the controlling coupling of
methanol and n-heptane, and would therefore be favorable to keep as a skeletal mecha-
nism. By then combining a reduced Li model with an MPL model containing some of
the radicals reaction with n-heptane, could potentially result in a good semi-global model
describing the methanol/n-heptane co-combustion chemistry.

Problem description
Project title: Development of a dual-fuel mechanism for methanol/diesel co-combustion
ECTS points: MSc 30
Student: Emil Blicher Hansen (s194173)
Supervisors: Peter Glarborg, Hamid Hashemi, Anna Tveen Trap
Place: DTU Chemical Engineering.
Period: 2/9 2024 – 2/2 2025
Background:
Alternative fuels such as methanol are attractive to use in large maritime diesel engines
to lower the environmental impact. However, methanol is a poor fuel for compression-
ignition engines due to comparatively long ignition delay times. This shortcoming can
be overcome by adding a small amount of diesel as pilot fuel to the engine.
As en-
gines are expensive to run, it is important to develop reliable computational models for
methanol/diesel ignition for use in engine design and optimization. Currently many of the
available detailed reaction mechanisms for heptane (as a diesel surrogate) and methanol
may involve more than 1000 species and are too computationally expensive for engine
modeling. A need therefore arises for simplified chemical schemes that provide reliable
predictions of ignition while being sufficiently small for use in CFD.
Project Objectives:
The objective of this project is to develop and validate a simplified model for ignition
of methanol/heptane dual-fuel mixtures, that have sufficient accuracy while being com-
putationally inexpensive. The reference calculations will be conducted with a detailed
reaction mechanism and validated against experimental results from literature. Global,
semi-global and skeletal schemes for heptane from the literature will be combined with a
DTU mechanism for ignition of methanol to evaluate the potential of different approaches.
The modeling will mainly be conducted using the ANSYS Chemkin software.

chemical coupling between the two fuels.

literature. Combination with methanol mechanism and validation against reference
calculations with a detailed mechanism.
