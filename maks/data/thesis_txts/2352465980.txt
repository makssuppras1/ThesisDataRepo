Abstract (dansk)
Informationer om traﬁkstrømme er en væsentlig planlægningsparameter i den kollektive transport.
I denne sammenæng er den s˚akaldte OD matrix (origin destination matrix) et vigtigt og interessant
redskab. Matricen indeholder informationer om rejsestrømme mellem zoner i netværk. Det er
dog ofte problematisk, for ikke at sige umuligt, at indsamle alle individers rejsemønstre for at
udarbejde det komplette overblik. Derfor tyer man ofte til alternative metoder, som eksempelvis
matrix estimation methods, hvor man ved hjælp af delvise input kan estimere en fuld OD matrix.
I denne afhandling behandles denne problemstilling ved at forsøge at estimere en OD matrix for
bustraﬁkken p˚a Sjælland. En af de mest anvendte matrix estimation methods er kendt som IPF,
Iterative Proportional Fitting. Denne anvendes bredt til at estimere elementer i mindre tabular
designs og er nem at implementere og har mange fordelagtige matematiske egenskaber. Det gør
den oplagt til at estimere OD matricer.
IPF’en best˚ar af to komponenter: totaler og en startløsning. Totalerne sikrer at den estimerede
løsning ligger indenfor grænserne, det vil sige at der kan ikke ankomme ﬂere passagerer til et bus
stop end der totalt stiger af. I opgaven anvendes tællinger fra Movia som totaler. Startløsningen
sørger for strukturen af løsningen, alts˚a hvor meget traﬁk der skal estimeres mellem forskellige
busstop.
Et omdrejningspunkt i afhandlingen er startløsningen til IPF’en, hvor datakilden er Rejsekort.
Denne datakilde er kun anvendt i begrænset omfang og en del af arbejdet har derfor best˚aet
i at analyse og organisere denne datakilde, som best˚ar af mange millioner observation for maj
2016. Problemet er imidlertid, at eftersom strukturen af løsningen kontrolleres af Rejsekort, og at
fordelingen af rejser med Rejsekortdata muligvis ikke er retvisende, s˚a vil den endelige OD matrix
være forkert. En hypotese kunne være, at der foretages ﬂere fritidsrejser med Rejsekort og for f˚a
pendlerture.
Hvis man antager at Rejsekortdata er fordelt korrekt og derefter estimerer en matrix, f˚as et plau-
sibelt resultat. Eksempelvis med relationen Nørreport Station til Nørrebro Station/Rigshospitalet
som en stor relation med mange rejser. Ligeledes ses et stort behov for kapacitet fra Lyngby Station
til DTU og fra Hillerød Station til uddannelsesinstitutionerne ved Hillerød Vest.
Et nærmere kig p˚a fordelingen af rejserne foretaget med Rejsekort viser, at der er sm˚a tendenser,
som peger p˚a at data er skævt, bl.a. i forhold til tid og form˚al. Samtidig er estimationen generelt
svær at sammenligne med andre datakilder, dels fordi den er foretaget med en række begrænsninger
af data til r˚adighed og fordi der ikke eksisterer en sammenlignelig opgørelse.
I et forsøg p˚a at forbedre estimatet justeres startløsningen - og dermed ændrer strukturen af
løsningen sig. Denne justering foretages ved hjælp af data fra Transportvaneundersøgelsen (TU),
s˚aledes antallet af ture med bus i Rejsekortdata ﬂugter med antallet af ture i TU, inddelt i
grupper efter timeb˚and og turlængde. Ligeledes er to andre estimationer foretaget, hvor enten
startløsningen eller totalerne justeres.
Denne justering giver anledning til en mindre ændring i resultatet. Resultaterne viser, at IPF
algoritmen er særdeles anvendelig til at estimere OD matricer, men at der bør arbejdes med
at optimere og forbedre den anvendte algoritme i MATLAB. Resultatet af analyserne kan kun
bruges delvist, da begrænsninger i forhold til adgang af data og mulig regnekraft har sat nogle
uhensigtsmæssige rammer i forhold til at konkludere p˚a de estimerede matricer.
Samlet set er IPF’en en fornuftig og eﬀektiv m˚ade at løse matrix estimations problemer p˚a.
Den kræver dog væsentlig forberedelser af input data og tilstrækkelig mængde regnekraft til
r˚adighed.

Abstract (English)
Traﬃc patterns are a key parameter to perform and analyse public transport networks and the
so-called OD matrix (origin destination matrix) is an important tool in this context. It contains
information of traﬃc ﬂows between zones and may be available on diﬀerent level of details. Es-
timation of the OD matrix is, however, a complicated task as it is impossible to obtain trip data
from all individuals. The project is limited to public transport data from buses in Zealand and
alternative data sources are used to make the matrix estimation possible in terms of computational
power available in the project.
A well-known and commonly used way of estimating the matrices is the Iterative Proportional
Fitting (IPF) algorithm. The procedure has appealing mathematical properties and is easy to
implement in any programming language. The IPF has two inputs: totals and an initial matrix.
The totals ensures that the estimate is ﬁtted within the boundaries, in this case how many trips
origin and destinate at each zone.
Other totals may be added.
In this project the totals are
obtained by passenger count data from Traﬁkselskabet Movia. The initial matrix is obtained from
Rejsekort data; a national smart card for public transport in Denmark. A main problem of the
thesis is the distribution of the initial matrix, which will be reﬂected in the ﬁnal estimate. If this
distribution of trips does not represents the true trip pattern, the estimated OD matrix will be
systematically skewed.
Estimating the IPF with the base initial matrix indicates plausible results and from the estimated
ﬂows between zones, it is not possible to conclude any skewness in the initial matrix.
When
investigating the initial matrix further it seems to be somewhat biased in terms of time and
purposes, although it is hard to determine ﬁrmly. Also, when comparing with alternative sources
a slightly diﬀerent distribution of trips is seen.
As a trip indicator the average length of a trip is used. This makes it possible to compare with
diﬀerent sources of data and as a consequence another estimate of the OD matrix is performed.
In this case, the initial matrix is adjusted to follow the trip length pattern of survey data from
Transportvaneundersøgelsen (TU). Two additional estimates are performed, adjusting for variance
in targets and small ﬂow relations. Results from all three estimates changes the estimated ﬂow
between zones slightly.
This preliminary study shows potential of using Rejsekort data as initial matrix to estimate OD
matrix. The limited access to data and computational power, however, puts a restriction on the
possible conclusions which may be drawn from the project.
The iterative proportional ﬁtting
algorithm shows good results, but in order to conclude ﬁrmly on estimated ﬂows a better data is
needed.

Introduction
In the transportation sector a frequently used measure of traﬃc ﬂows is the origin destination
matrix, OD matrix (Peterson, 2007). The OD matrix may be conceived as a system, represented
by three diﬀerent states (Ort´uzar and Willumsen, 2003):
• Micro state. A disaggregated state where the travel behaviour of each individual is de-
scribed.
• Meso state. A semi-aggregated state where the OD matrix is described by frequencies, eg.
how many passengers travel from A to B.
• Macro state. An aggregated state where the total amount of passengers going to or from
a A or B is described.
The OD matrix is widely used and applied, however the estimation is challenging. Matrix estima-
tion methods are commonly used to estimate a OD matrix and is a generally applied mathematical
and statistical method, which may be applicable in various ﬁelds, for instance biostatistics, eco-
nomics and computer science.
Figure 1.1. Distinction between ”classic” OD matrix (dashed pattern) and applied OD (solid pattern)
Figure 1.1 shows a tiny network of bus stops. In transport research the term OD matrix usually
describes the movement of an individual from origin (A) to destination (C) (”classic” OD). In this
thesis the origin and destination will be at a public transport facility, as the actual origin and
destination is unknown. Furthermore, a journey may be conducted from A to C, which will be
considered as two trips (A-B and B-C). As a consequence one may perceive the OD matrix slightly
diﬀerently than normally.
The OD matrix is an important tool to understand the traﬃc ﬂows in the public transport sector;
in this thesis exampliﬁed by considering buses in Zealand, Denmark. Issues of patterns, however,
may be expanded to include private cars, transport of goods, shipping etc.
To accommodate
mobility demand of the future more research and more optimization of transport networks has
to be conducted. Since the transportation section accounts for at least 4.6% of the GDP in the
European Union (Eurostat, 2015), optimizations and improvements are important. Trip pattern
analysis through OD matrices may be important keys to the puzzle.

Knowledge of trip patterns is especially useful in bus traﬃc planning as it helps to understand
the operating network better. Such information will greatly increase the traﬃc planners’ ability
to:
• Quantify passenger routes/lines and evaluate whether a demand is met.
• Assess the need for adjusting frequencies at speciﬁc times.
• Optimize and dispatch buses properly.
• Evaluate costs and beneﬁts of route alternatives to improve decision-making in municipalities.
• Compare the demand for buses at diﬀerent locations with similar socio-economic standards.
Advantages of having trip pattern knowledge will increase when looking at transportation in a
broader perspective. Changing patterns and route assignments are two equally important parts
of transport modelling, and both may beneﬁt greatly from having a detailed pattern of trips. As
the generation and attraction of trips between zones are necessary to compute ﬂows on routes,
additional knowledge of trip patterns will greatly help to quantify demand in the public transport
sector, namely bus transportation. This allows planners to improve and optimize existing routes
when having near-exact traﬃc volumes on stretches, stops and buses.
Using the actual demand it is furthermore possible to quantify, assess and compare the bus opera-
tion situation with alternative scenarios. When knowledge of trip patterns is available, it is possible
to determine whether a line should follow one route or another. This optimization problem may
be conducted with respect to minimizing travel time, change time, which will have a beneﬁcial side
eﬀect with increasing costumer loyalty (Foote et al., 2001). Additionally, the estimation of OD
matrices by using diﬀerent approaches may be put into a larger context of using matrix estimation
methodologies in other ﬁelds of science or in other parts of transport research.
It will also be possible to use trip pattern data to assessments of routes when changes happen in
the network. OD information can also be applied in the ﬁeld of cost-beneﬁt analysis in order to
evaluate diﬀerent bus line scenarios. It is outside the scope of this thesis to consider all derived
advantages of estimating a full matrix of trip patterns.
Concluding, an OD matrix is an important piece of information in itself, especially when analysing
large and strategic trip patterns in a network.
1.1
Matrix estimation methods
Matrix estimation methods are methodologies to estimate cell-entries in a matrix. Imagine a level
of spatial representation, described as traﬃc model zones, train stations, bus stops or addresses.
From each spatial representation a trip may occur to or from. The goal is to derive (denoted as
estimate or ﬁt the matrix) the full travel pattern between all relations.
Matrix estimation methods generally require two inputs: targets and an initial matrix. One of
the key advantages of the methods is the connection between inputs. While the targets ensure
the ﬁt to stay within the boundaries, the initial matrix holds the structure (distribution) of the
solution.
Targets are also denoted margins, marginal totals or totals, illustrated in ﬁgure 1.2. These are
found on a macro state and each margin represents a space in which the estimated matrix must
be within the boundaries of. Dimensions of the margins may vary, but when estimating an OD
matrix they usually consist of i and j, eg. origin and destination. Time, purpose or mode may
be added as additional dimension. It is, however, relevant to consider the dependencies between
margins.
2
==PAGE:17==
Figure 1.2. Margins of a two dimensional matrix (i and j) and a three dimensional hyper cube (i, j, k). This is
also known as a cube which reﬀers to a multi-dimensional array of data. Source: (Rich, 2016)
In this thesis bus stops are used as a preliminary spatial representation and the Automatic Passen-
ger Count (APC) system of Traﬁkselskabet Movia provides the marginal targets. The APC system
collects number of boarding and alighting passengers at each stop on selected trips. Afterwards
the trips are scaled to full level, matching the actual departures of buses. APC data ensures that
the ﬁtted OD Matrix stays within the boundaries of targets. Without any information to support
the APC system an accurate estimation would be problematic.
The initial matrix (also referred to as starting solution or structure) may be perceived as a survey,
because population data are normally impossible to obtain. In this thesis Rejsekort data (a Danish
smart card used as ticket in public transport) are applied as the initial matrix and thereby hold
the structure/correlation of the ﬁt. Note, for bus transport in Zealand, Rejsekort data are found
on micro level as each movement in the network is traceable. The initial matrix, however, will be
aggregated to meso state, eg. ﬂow from one zone to another.
Estimating a full OD matrix with conditions described above is mainly complicated because both
sources (APC and Rejsekort data) contain large amounts of data. Secondly, Rejsekort data has
only been used sporadically for planning and estimation purposes. Following, the structure of the
initial matrix is uncertain and as a consequence has to be analysed. Alternatively, the ﬁnal estimate
will be a re-scaled solution following a ”false” trip pattern obtained from Rejsekort data.
To analyse, evaluate and improve the matrix estimation method additional data will be applied.
This is mainly Transportvaneundersøgelsen (TU) and Landstraﬁkmodellen (LTM). Both are key
sources for further knowledge of trip patterns and are mainly applied to test the estimation and
evaluate structure of the initial matrix.

1.3
Notation
Let the trip be deﬁned as a movement from an origin to a destination (Ort´uzar and Willumsen,
1994). Furthermore, let the sum of all trips between i and j represent the ﬂow and a set of i
and j be denoted as a relation. Additionally, a journey may consists of more trips (also known as
legs).
Estimates are presented with the hat operator.
The true ﬂow between the spatial relations (bus stops, stations or zones) i and j in time band t
is then deﬁned as Pijt. A time band t is deﬁned as the time passing between two following hours,
eg. t = 8 represents the ﬂow from 08:00:00 (hh:mm:ss) to 08:59:59.
The total ﬂow generated from origin zone i is denoted Oi - the total ﬂow attracted destination
zone j is denoted Dj. Kt deﬁnes the total amount of trips in time band t. All three are acquired
from the APC system of Traﬁkselskabet Movia and may be grouped by t. Let the initial matrix
be deﬁned as Gijt.
Using Oi, Dj, Kt and Gijt generates an estimate, deﬁned as ˆPijt, which is assumed to be approxi-
mately equal to the true and unknown ﬂow Pijt. Throughout the thesis data are pronounced plural
(Oﬃce for National Statistics, 2016).

2.1
Rejsekort data
To perform matrix estimation an initial matrix is needed. In this thesis data for the initial matrix
will be obtained from the Danish smart card system, Rejsekort. The system has been operating
since 2013 and its purpose is to ease public transport in Denmark, independent of geographic
location and mode. The company behind Rejsekort is the state-owned Rejsekort A/S.
In September 1997 a new touch-and-go ticket system was launched in Hong Kong (Research and
Library Services Division, 2007). Six years later London introduced the Oyster Card. Both systems
gave inspiration to the development of the Rejsekort, the main diﬀerence being area of coverage.
The equipment is placed in buses and train/metro stations. Whenever a person wants to conduct
a trip, the person puts the Rejsekort (similar to a payment card) in front of Rejsekort equipment.
This registration is called a check-in. Whenever a new vehicle is entered the passenger has to
perform another check-in. When a journey ends, the person put the Rejsekort near equipment
to end the journey, denoted check-out. It is possible, but not mandatory, to perform a check-out
when ending part of a journey (trip).
Data represents trips performed with Rejsekort in May 2016 on Zealand - and only trips within
Zealand. Due to restricted access, not all data are available. Only journeys where at least one

leg/trip has taken place by bus are available. This restriction mainly aﬀects the interpretation of
the estimated OD matrix as it does not contain the actual ﬂow of the public transport.

with number of records, columns and storage3 in table 2.2. Additionally, table 2.2 illustrates how

In order to extract meaning full information an overview and data mining process is required (Ma

Miscellaneous
Equipment used in the Rejsekort system is highly dependent on GPS-locations.
The quality of these leads to errors, in data marked as Delocalized. This means either the origin
or destination cannot be determined. These observations are removed.
Since data are only available for bus stops there is no point in including trips between train and
metro stations. Recall that these are only part of the data because at least one leg of the journey is
performed by bus. Targets are only available on an average week day level, which means weekends
and holidays must be removed from Rejsekort data. The remaining trips are averaged over the
week days of May 2016. Furthermore, the OD matrix should represent an ordinary week day (and
not the sum).
To sum up, Rejsekort data initially consisted of 9.3×106 records. As a result of the data mining in
the trip table approximately 2.5 × 106 records are translated into trips used in the initial matrix.
This corresponds to an average of 125,000 trips per week day of May 2016.

2.4
Transportvaneundersøgelsen (TU)
Transportvaneundersøgelsen (TU) is an interview-based survey. It aims to describe travel habits
of the Danish population and has been conducted since 2006. Data obtained from TU are used for
test and evaluation of the matrix estimation method. Furthermore the initial matrix is compared
to TU data.
Let an adjusted version of TU data be similar to a synthetic dataset. In many circumstances it is
useful to apply a test data set as it eases the procedure of testing and evaluation. Additionally, the
properties of Rejsekort data make it diﬃcult to test of full scale level due amount of data.
There is no ﬁrm deﬁnition of synthetic data, but most sources states it to be data obtained in
a technical manner. It may be completely synthetic, for instance drawn from an empirical dis-
tribution, or computed partly by measured data (Rubin, 1993). Beneﬁts of using synthetic data
depends on the situation, but Bersano et al. (1997) emphasizes four beneﬁts. Two of these are
applicable:
1. In testing the performance of a given database, the average time required by a given query,
etc., synthetic data can allow for testing such performance when actual data are unavailable
(e.g., a database application is being designed for the ﬁrst time), scarce or possibly biased.
This also allows the database implementers to ask ”what if” questions and see what eﬀect
changing certain database parameters would have on the performance of the speciﬁc database
application
2. In choosing a representation for the data, it is again possible to ask other ”what if” questions
on what the best format for the data are (with respect to a chosen database set-up) even
before the database is actually ever used (and thus when it is still cheap to make such changes)
Algorithm run times are a crucial consideration. Applying the synthetic data set will highlight
possible weaknesses and pitfalls of using diﬀerent matrix estimation methods.

• Rejsekort data represent the largest and most advanced data source in the thesis and have

3
Iterative Proportional Fitting (IPF)
The Iterative Proportional Fitting (IPF) is the matrix estimation method of choice in this thesis.
The aim of this section is to introduce the reader to IPF and its application:
1. Description of the general problem of matrix estimation and the literature review of the IPF
algorithm.
2. Mathematical deﬁnition and explanation of the algorithm implemented in MATLAB.
3. Selection of spatial representation (zone level) and evaluation of algorithm performance by
setting up a minor simulation scheme.

The iterative proportional ﬁtting algorithm is a well-known and widely applied matrix estimation
method. It combines two datasets to estimate value of cell-entries and has been applied in a large
variety of scientiﬁc ﬁelds over the past 70 years. It was proposed by Deming and Stephan (1940),
although Kruithof (1937) explored the issue of estimating cell-entries three years earlier.
3.1
Entropy maximization
The IPF is a method to solve a more general problem: the maximum entropy problem Bishop
et al. (1975). Fundamentally, the maximization of entropies is based on thermodynanic concepts,
where the purpose is to ﬁnd a conﬁguration to elements that match a constrained situation. It
was formulated as the least biased estimate on given information by Jaynes (1957) who deﬁnes the
maximum entropy estimate as a procedure of setting up probability distributions on the basis of
partial knowledge.
Entropy maximization methodologies are capable of providing solutions to constrained optimization
problems in many parts of transportation research (Bar-gera, 2008). Solutions may, to some extent,
be applied analytically. Estimation of OD matrices by using Gravity Models, equation 1, was one
of the earliest considerations of the maximum entropy problem (Wilson, 1967). It is advantageous
to use the log function to solve the expression, where Tij is the trips between i and j, while tij is

3.2
Literature review
IPF is also known as RAS algorithm, bi-proportional ﬁtting algorithm or matrix scaling. Estimating
Nonnegative Matrices from Marginal Data by Bacharach (1965) has same implications and methods
as Deming and Stephan (1940).
Even though Bacharach (1965) was a major mathematical improvement of the problem, the ac-
tual mathematical deﬁnition of the IPF algorithm was performed by Fienberg (1970). As the ﬁrst
researcher Fienberg (1970) explored the mathematical properties of the algorithm. The mathemat-
ical description of the problem gave inspiration to development the Furness algorithm four years
later (Evans and Kirby, 1974). This algorithm reproduces cell-entries of IPF, but is by deﬁnition
more similar to a Gravity Model.
Since the 1970s research of IPF has mostly been focusing on:
• From being a mathematical statistical methodology the IPF is now applied in a many scien-
tiﬁc ﬁelds, ﬁtting diﬀerent kinds of matrices. This includes: medicine (Agostino et al., 2016),
water demand and consumption (Deng et al., 2014), thermal power dispatch (Senthil and
Manikandan, 2010) and generation of synthetic populations (Mckay, 1996).
• One of the reasons why IPF is applied widely is the simplicity of its use. The simplicity of
computation was also addressed by Navick and Furth (1994), who found it to be the main
and most important property of the algorithm. The properties of the IPF are described as
appealing, according to Liang et al. (2006).
• Orcutt (1985) applied the IPF using simulation models to social and ﬁscal policy, but was
limited by the computational power at the time. Combining microsimulation models and
IPF has also resulted in a small area statistics approach (Ballas et al., 1999).

Initially the marginal probabilities are calculated. If inputs from the initial matrix are equal for

3.5
Selection of spatial representation
To describe correlated ﬂow patterns using the IPF a spatial representation (zones) is necessary. In
the introduction this was proposed to be on stop-to-stop level. Choosing a spatial representation,
however, is not a simple task, as it is a trade oﬀbetween detailed results and computational power
available. It is furthermore complicated by availability of data, which is represented at diﬀerent
zone levels. Rejsekort and APC data are available on bus stop level, LTM data are available on a
unique zone level and TU data are represented on a municipality level.
If a large part of Oi, Dj, Kt or Gijt contains no ﬂows, the IPF will not converge. Therefore, a
proper spatial representation level has to be chosen.
It is possible to switch between zone levels by the use of a Geographical Information System (GIS)
software package.

Simulation has no clear deﬁnition, but methodologies developed as computational power increased.
It is methods to iterate results from a model that enable tests otherwise impossible. One of the most
famous simulation experiments is known as Buﬀon’s Needle Experiment, in which the estimation
of π is possible (Buﬀon, 1777). It is a methodology to draw many pseudo-random 4 numbers to

A well known class of simulation methods is the Monte Carlo Methods; computational algo-
rithms that rely on repeated pseudo-random sampling to obtain numerical results. (Sawilowsky
and Fahoome, 2003) distinguishes between Simulation, Monte Carlo Methods and Monte Carlo
Simulation. Imagine one wants the probability of getting a head on a fair coin:
• Simulation: Draw a random number between 0 and 1, more than 0.5 it equals head
• Monte Carlo Method: Take a box of coins and pour it on the table and count the number
of heads and tails
• Monte Carlo Simulation: Draw a large amount of numbers between 0 and 1, and count
numbers above and below 0.5
In this case the Monte Carlo simulation may be used to draw targets and initial matrix data to
evaluate the performance of the algorithm.

allowing for intra zone activity within larger zones. This, however, does not come without a cost:
research shows that especially bus traﬃc suﬀers heavily from aggregating zone level (Walker, 2014).
The aggregation may be implemented in several ways:
• Removing targets with a small amount of activity. Applying a boundary on which
stops to include, for instance by removing all stops with less than 50 passengers per day.
This would decrease the number of stops to 2,772, but include 90% of the total passengers in
the network. Most trips of the initial matrix will most likely be going between the remaining
stops. This does not solve the problem of the matrix being too large to be initialized, however.
• Using LTM zones (L2 or L3). Using LTM zones will decrease the spatial representation
dramatic, reducing the number of zones to 376 or 1436. This decreases the level of detail,
but will make the IPF run more easily. Furthermore, it may be seen as a preliminary test of
computing improved public transport OD matrices to the LTM when all data are available.

Bishop et al. (1975) explores the convergence of the IPF and deﬁnes convergence at the point
where the diﬀerence between iteration k and iteration k + 1 is smaller or equal to a pre-deﬁned
desired accuracy. Ben-Akiva (1987) deﬁnes no ﬂows as structural zeros and suggests that the IPF
will yield a unique solution.
Additionally, Bishop et al. (1975) proposes three necessary criteria (denoted as treatments) of data
in order for convergence to happen:
(A) No zero cells in marginal targets
(B) No negative numbers in targets or initial matrix
(C) No balance in targets
Bishop states that the initial matrix cannot have too many zeros, but suggests that more than 30%
zeros may cause problems in terms of convergence (assuming these 30% to be evenly and equally
distributed). In the initial matrix of the synthetic data, Gij, approximately 50% of the relations
encounter a zero. By the use of Bishop’s criteria, the algorithm may not converge. The three

5
Distribution of Rejsekort data
The purpose of this section is to assess the hypothesis regarding trip distribution of the initial
matrix (Rejsekort data). This validation is problematic due to the fact that the true ﬂow between
zones, Pijt, is unknown. Recall that the IPF estimates produce an OD matrix, ˆPijt, which is
based on the correlation structure of Oi, Dj, Kt and Gijt. The estimated matrix will be within
the boundaries of the constraints, but not necessarily represent the actual passenger ﬂow between
zones.
The problem of using Rejsekort data as an initial solution is representation of data (distribution
of trips in space), especially in terms of commuting trip relations. Worst case scenario is a zone
relation with no trips in the initial matrix, but many commuting trips in the true scenario. Such
a case cannot be estimated using the IPF. However, relations with few Rejsekort trips, and many
commuting trips, may likewise induce problems.
5.1
Literature review: smart card data
Estimating public transport ﬂows from smart card data has only been covered vaguely, hence it is
not possible to adopt a methodology to solve the problem of representatively directly. Rejsekort is
used far less than smart card systems in other cities, such as Seoul (Pelletier et al., 2011), London
and Montreal, where the use of smart card systems are approximately 70-80% (Seaborn et al.,
2009). Smart card was introduced in the public transport sector in the late 90s, however, the
technology was patented in 1968 (TechRepublic, 2016).
A larger sample makes it easier to analyse commute travel patterns, which are essential to get a
complete overview of traﬃc ﬂows (Deane et al., 2008). Additionally, some research ignores whether
data obtained from smart cards represent the true trip pattern:
”It should be noted that the datasets might be representative of groups holding the smart
cards in the study areas, but not necessarily of the population” (Bagchi and White, 2005)
Bagchi and White (2005) concludes that not collecting the trip purpose is a major issue. Generally
the problem of estimating an OD matrix is well researched. The estimation, however, does not
imply the use of smart card data, because many smart card systems only involves the registration
of boarders. As a consequence the destination has to be estimated (Barry et al., 2007) by applying
alternative approaches. Furthermore, the invention of smart card system is the ﬁrst reliable source
for information regarding passengers in developing countries Munizaga et al. (2012). This is why
there has been little focus on having a check-out possibility, as for instance systems in Denmark
and the Netherlands.
Even though variants of the classic 4-step model by Manheim (1979) and Florian et al. (1988)
approaches appear, for instance the limited destination model by Veenstra et al. (2010) (which was
used to predict distribution of shopping mall trips from actual locations), there is no methodology
to solve the problem of selecting a proper deterrence function.
Other demand methodologies involve the use of additional data sources, using car ownership,
income level etc., to predict the number of public transport trips possible Aljouﬁe (2014).

The targets are, equally aﬀected by a large amount of uncertainty. It is therefor interesting to

the consequences of variation in targets an additional ﬁt is performed. In an optimal situation with

based on the variation of targets (monthly passengers) from 2014 to 2016, is applied.
Figure 6.8. Index of total number of passengers/month (index)) on all buses of Movia
Figure 6.8 shows the development of total passengers pr. month (index 100 = January 2014). As
many bus passengers presumably use the bus in winther months, due to the weather, few months
will have more passengers and thereby a larger index. Note, May 2016 corresponds to x-axis value
29 where the index of monthly passengers is at 95, eg. slightly lower than January 2014. The
relevant information to extract from ﬁgure 6.8 is the variation between months throughout the
period.
Before using this information however, each month of each year must be similar, otherwise the
distribution applied to the targets is a product of a tendency in passenger between years. The
actual (and ”natural”) variance of passengers is the aim. One methodology is to let the index be a
stochastic process dependent on discrete time, t, and compute the autocorrelation function (ACF).
It describes the correlation between data at diﬀerent lags; a high value of autocorrelation at lag
1 means, that a value is highly correlated with the previous value (Madsen, 2006). If months of
each year are alike, then the autocorrelation must be signiﬁcant at lag 13 - this point measures the
correlation with the current month and the number of passengers one year ahead. This conclusion
looks to be conﬁrmed, as lag 13 is the only signiﬁcant correlation (95% conﬁdent level), ﬁgure 6.9.
Note, the correlation at point 1 is caused by the points correlation with itself.
The standard deviation of the index is estimated to 9.7, eg. the monthly numbers of passengers
vary approximately 10% from month to month, and may be applied to targets to test the robustness

7.1
Discussion
7.1.1
Data availability and structure
A majority of the work presented relies heavily on the access to data and management of it. The
limitation of the thesis is inﬂuenced by the amount of Rejsekort data available, especially as no
train or metro data are available. Obviously, this puts a restriction on the conclusions to be drawn
from the work and the estimated ﬂows cannot be put directly in use of planning and optimization
of buses in Zealand.
Accessing Rejsekort data for all public transport modes requires access to targets of metro and
train stations, and it is currently unknown whether these data are available in the same quality as
data of bus stops at Movia. Even though this process is expected to be trivial, merging marginal
data from multiple traﬃc operators is time consuming. A signiﬁcant amount of time in this thesis
was used understanding, organizing and structuring data in the book-keeping matrix to be able to
perform the matrix ﬁt.
Another issue in terms of data is how each traﬃc operator organizes Rejsekort data. Due to the
organisational structure of data each traﬃc operator handles data diﬀerently and combining these
57
==PAGE:72==
data sources may involve complications. All processes of data should aim towards having one data
set, combining Rejsekort data and targets. As table 3.2 shows the entire network consists of more
than 37,000 unique stops, resulting in a incredible large amount of unique travel combinations.
Whether such a book-keeping matrix, M, can be computed, is unknown.
7.1.2
Content of data
Besides the issue of accessing data the contents of Rejsekort data are a main driver of uncertainty
in the thesis. Comparison with alternative data sources shows that Rejsekort data at some points
looks to be distributed diﬀerently.
Predeﬁned knowledge and assumptions is still the primary
reason to believe that the actual trip pattern is slightly diﬀerent in reality.
Some analyses indicate that Rejsekort data lacks the ability to catch peak hour estimates. It seems
possible to compare the ﬂow at speciﬁc bus stops and conclude that Rejsekort does not match the
actual pattern.
On the other hand, stops exists where Rejsekort data is a completely perfect
subset of the total amount of passengers. Initially Rejsekort data was expected to be distributed
completely diﬀerent from the actual trip pattern, eg. primarily leisure trips outside peak hours.
This is not the case as a large amount of passengers use Rejsekort data in the peak-hours and
to what is expected to be commute destinations.
Whether this conclusion is applicable when
considering all public transport modes is unknown. An interesting improvement to the ﬁt would
be to obtain more data on commuting trip patterns from Ungdomskortet, Pendlerkort etc. With
these sources it will be possible to completely conﬁrm the trip pattern. Alternatively the trip
pattern of Landstraﬁkmodellen may be applied, even though this contains a number of additional
uncertainties.
Another point worth discussing is the time period from which Rejsekort data are extracted. Firstly,
it is not certain whether an average week day of May 2016 is representative for an average week
day. Also, it is assumed that all trips conducted with bus in Zealand are conducted at some point
during May 2016, because the IPF cannot ﬁt cell-entries with a zero in the initial matrix. Adding
the period of Rejsekort data will without a doubt result in more unique trip; whether these new
trips have a large eﬀect on the estimation is highly unlikely. As an example, 99% of the unique trip
relations of May 2016 are covered by more than half of the single days during the month, hence
adding additional days generate relatively little new information. Adding data for an additional
period may change the ratio between trips from relation i to j.
Finally, it is worth acknowledging the uncertainties regarding APC counts from Movia. These
targets plays a signiﬁcant role in estimating the OD matrix. The variance of APC data, and the
consequences when estimating the OD matrix, is only brieﬂy covered.
7.1.3
Algorithm
A second point of discussion is the algorithm - a subject also covered in the literature:
”What are the opportunities for further improvements in computational eﬃciency of
the code?” (Lovelace et al., 2015)
In this study the issue of run times, algorithm optimization and matrix size is analysed. The
largest matrix considered consists of 9504 combinations (sex, modes, distances etc.) and thereby
illustrates that the size of matrices considered in this thesis are signiﬁcantly larger. Hence, applying
the Iterative Proportional Fitting algorithm to very large data sets is currently not well covered
in terms of literature. Algorithm optimization, matrix evaluation and programming language are
three obvious challenges in this regard. Additionally, this troubles validation of matrices.
58
==PAGE:73==
To deal with matrices with millions of observations algorithm optimization in the form of computer
science needs to be a key factor to take into account. In this study, however, it has only played
a minor role and little time has been used improving algorithms. The algorithms implemented
in MATLAB have only been changed slightly, for instance by considering single values instead of
doubles, to reduce memory space required. Increasing the complexity or sizes of the matrices should
involve considerations of more computational power to enhance and quicken the estimation.
The longest run time to achieve convergence is seen when using LTM L3 zones, as it is where the
book-keeping matrix M is largest. Furthermore, the study from 2015 ﬁnds very low run times using
the programming language C and R. As the matrices estimated in the study are small, compared
to the fully spanned M of this thesis, the algorithm run times of MATLAB is in line with existing
literature. When estimation IPF with only small amount of variables or groups the actual language
chosen may be irrelevant. MATLAB, however, might be a good choice as it is ﬂexible and easy to
perform data management.
Computing the fully-spanned M using LTM L3 is problematic when using SQL Express and more
advanced tools may be necessary to perform this task. Next, the size of the workspace in MATLAB
does not allow matrices of all sized. A platform switch to Linux should enable the possibility to
enlarge workspace capacity (Mathworks, 2017).
7.1.4
Estimated ﬂows
A main objective of the thesis is to be able to enhance and increase the knowledge of traﬃc patterns
(buses) by the use of the IPF algorithm. It is clear that the obtained ﬂow between zones cannot
be put directly into use, especially because it is problematic to verify the estimated ﬂow ˆPijt.
Constraints trouble the ﬁrmness of conclusions to be drawn, however all the estimates present
plausible ﬂows.
The large amount of data also troubles the possibilities to get a grip on the structure of the solution.
As input matrices (targets and initial matrix) contains millions of cell-entries it is problematic to
validate, whether the estimated OD matrix is plausible. When adjusting the initial matrix, and
applying variance to targets, the estimated ﬂows are reasonable.
Results show small changes
in estimated ﬂows when adjusting the initial matrix. The largest estimated ﬂows are between
Nørreport Station and Rigshospitalet/Nørrebrogade, independent on the LTM zone level applied.
This is in line with the expectation, as Nørrebrogade and Nørre Campus are two main corridors of
bus transport in Zealand. Unfortunately, it is not possible to ﬁt a matrix on stop zone level and
this limits the possibilities of concluding further upon the estimated ﬂow. A way of conﬁrming the
estimated ﬂow is to compare it with a simple up-scaled Rejsekort estimate (for instance by using
17% as proxy).
For multiple reasons structural zeros of the initial matrix is of importance. A main assumption of
the thesis is that all relevant trips are observed at some point during May 2016. However, when
expanding the time period this may change. A comparison of diﬀerent Rejsekort data periods may
reveal inconsistency in trip patterns.
Applying the IPF to estimate an OD matrix also illustrates the robustness of the algorithm. First
indication is the peak hour adjustment, where Rejsekort trips observed once during May 2016 are
removed. This adjustment does not aﬀect the estimated ﬂow signiﬁcantly and illustrates robustness
as one of the advantages of applying the IPF algorithm. Another indication of robustness is the
ease of computation when adjusting the initial matrix and generally the IPF add a good amount
of ﬂexibility to the estimation. Adding the easiness of understanding and computation is seen to
be a promising methodology to apply to OD matrix estimation in general.

7.2
Further work
Based on the discussion a set of recommendations to the further work of OD matrix estimation
using iterative proportional ﬁtting algorithm are suggested:
• Obtain targets and initial matrix data from all public transport agencies and combine these
data at a desired level to estimate the public transport matrix. This will require signiﬁcantly
more data management and cooperation between transit agencies. As the limitation of data
availability is one of the key issues in the thesis, it is the obivous path to continue on.
• Furthermore, the estimate will beneﬁt from including commute data sources (Ungdomskort,
Pendlerkort etc.). This data may be applied to conﬁrm or reject some of the distribution
hypotheses regarding Rejsekort data.
• Verify and analyse variance of the two inputs to the IPF. By doing so, it is possible to gain
further knowledge of the initial matrix (Rejsekort data) and the targets (APC data).
• Explore whether alternative programming languages may be appropriate to use and optimize
the algorithm in order to increase the level of detail, which may describe the trip pattern
in details (zone level, time band). Consider using more computational power to handle and
estimate matrices.
• Obtain or develop methodologies which may be applied to validate and conﬁrm the estimate
ﬂows. This may be done by conducting and following the literature of OD matrix estimation.

7.3
Conclusion
The iterative proportional ﬁtting (IPF) algorithm shows great potential to estimate OD matrices.
The methodology is generally found applicable and may be expanded to other planning discipline
in the transport sector. The limited data accessibility, however, puts a constraint on the validity
of the estimated matrices. Only having access to an initial matrix (Rejsekort data) and targets
(APC counts) from buses limits the interpretation possibilities signiﬁcantly.
Rejsekort data holds unique information regarding trip pattern in the public transport.
It is
possible to perform a large variety of analysis using the data and together with additional data
sources (targets) it is a solid foundation to estimate OD matrices upon.
Using Rejsekort and
APC (boarding and alighting passengers from Movia) data as input to the algorithm require
data management and computational power, which was not present in suﬃcient amounts. As a
consequence, the ambitions of the estimate had to be considered. The data mining process of
Rejsekort data is a substantial and complicated process and should be taken into account when
doing similar estimations.
Using the IPF to estimate OD matrices is advantageous for multiple reasons: Appealing mathe-
matical properties, easy implementation in programming language of desire (MATLAB) and well
described in the literature. The thesis conﬁrm all these properties. The size of the data set, how-
ever, inﬂicts issues, as most IPF research is performed by using substantially smaller data sets.
Even when considering zones from Landstraﬁkmodellen (LTM), instead of unique bus stops of
Zealand, the estimation problem grows and in some dimensions of targets has to be omitted with
the current computational power available.
From the initial estimates it is also important to distinguish between reasonable and true. It may
be concluded that the initial estimated ﬂows between LTM zones, when using the IPF, seems
reasonable. Whether estimates corresponds to the true ﬂows is impossible to conclude. Most trips
happen between Nørreport Station and Nørrebro/Rigshospitalet. Other relations, such as Lyngby
Station to DTU, likewise has many passengers.
It may be concluded that the distribution of Rejsekort data is not as problematic as expected. Even
though trip purpose data is impossible to achieve, most trips with Rejsekort data is performed
during peak-hours. Still, by the use of external data sources, it is possible to highlight problematic
locations, where the match between APC counts and Rejsekort data is lackluster.
The busy
commuting relations of Rigshospitalet and DTU has a diﬀerent Rejsekort pattern compared to
APC targets. The estimate will beneﬁt with the addition of travel purpose data; either to use in
the IPF or to analysis the commuting trip pattern.
Using trip length and time band data from Transportvaneundersøgelsen (TU) to adjust Rejsekort
data illustrates minor diﬀerences. Applying the TU distribution to Rejsekort data only changes
the overall OD ﬁt slightly. The two additional estimates yield only limited new information and
requires more work to perform solid conclusion.
Summarizing, the IPF algorithm indicates promising results and appears to be a suitable algorithm
to solve matrix estimation problems. In order to conclude ﬁrmly on estimated ﬂows in the thesis,
however, a more coherent (across dimensions) package of data needs to be available.

1. For each item in a sample of n items, compute a diﬀerence score |Di|, between the two paired

nonzero absolute diﬀerence scores, where n′ ≤n. Zero diﬀerence observations are removed
3. Assign ranks from 1 to n’ to each of the |Di| such that the smallest absolute diﬀerence score
gets rank 1 and the largest gets rank n’. If two or more are equal, assign each of them
the mean of the ranks they would have been assigned individually had ties in the data not
occurred.
4. Reassign the symbol or to each of the ranks, depending on whether was originally positive
