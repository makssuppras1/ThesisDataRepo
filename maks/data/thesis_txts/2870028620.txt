Abstract 
This thesis examines gender bias in the context of an entrepreneurial pitching 
competition at a leading technical Northern European university. By analyzing three 
consecutive semesters’ worth of recordings of the competition, it seeks to uncover 
gender-based bias patterns in investor questioning. The study uses Regulatory Focus 
Theory (RFT), a framework that differentiates between promotion and prevention-
focuses. By using theories that talk about RFT in the context of gender studies and 
entrepreneurship, it aims to discover whether the type of questions asked from 
entrepreneurs has a gender-based bias, and whether there is correlation between the 
type of questions asked and the gender of the investor. Furthermore, it uses a large 
language model as a tool for a potentially unbiased lens to examine the pitches and 
compares those questions to the investors’. The study found no statistically significant 
gender-based bias in either the team genders, or the investor genders correlations. It 
did however find a large difference between the investor questions and the large 
language model questions, which points to a male-bias in the context of 
entrepreneurship in both the LLM’s case and general discourse’s case. The findings of 
this thesis contribute to a broader understanding of gendered dynamics in 
entrepreneurship, especially in educational entrepreneurial environments in Northern 
European examples.

1. Introduction 
To our current days, gender bias in entrepreneurship remains a persistent challenge, 
having an influence on the way entrepreneurs are evaluated, funded, and perceived in 
business environments. Even though we can observe a progress in gender equality, 
academic research continues to highlight the different treatment of female and male 
entrepreneurs, generally pointing to a disadvantage for women. This thesis aims to 
explore the present gender bias in the case of a pitching competition at a leading 
technical Northern European university. The study applies Regulatory Focus Theory, 
more specifically, it groups the investors’ questions asked from the teams by 
differentiating between promotion-focused, prevention-focused and neutral questions. 
By analyzing recorded pitch sessions, spanning over the course of three consecutive 
semesters, this paper seeks to uncover any present gender-based bias. Kanze et al. 
(2018) say that men are more likely to be asked promotive questions, while women are 
more likely to be asked preventive ones, and this thesis aims to discover whether that is 
true in the example of this case. Furthermore, Gutermuth and Hamstra (2023) say that 
women are more likely to have a preventive focus, while men are more likely to have a 
promotive focus, and this study will also look at and examine that factor. Furthermore, it 
introduces a large language model based analysis, to see whether through the lens of 
the tool there would be a difference in the questions asked by the teams. 
 
2. Case introduction 
This study is based on a pitching competition in a leading technical Northern 
European university. It serves as a final exam for a semester-long course, which focuses 
on establishing skills to build a viable technology startup. Upon starting the course, the 
students are handed a project, which includes an idea for a technology that they could 
turn into a potential startup. Throughout the course, students are given the formerly 
mentioned skills to make the idea into a viable business, which they often do through 
pivoting and changing aspects of the idea they were handed in order to succeed. 
Students from all backgrounds are accepted into the course and join teams of five to 
==PAGE:6==
 
5
seven, to build their startup together. For some, the startup journey ends after the final 
exam, but for others, these projects become viable ventures and provide careers for the 
founders.  
The pitching competition takes place in a similar setting to the American 
business reality tv show, which was described by Jetter and Stockley as the following. 
 
"A typical Shark Tank episode includes four pitches that are presented and fully concluded 
subsequently. Before the show, the five independent investors, known as Sharks, do not know 
which products or even which type of products would be presented. … A typical pitch proceeds 
as follows: 1. An entrepreneur or a team of entrepreneurs enters the ‘tank’ to face the Sharks 
and pitch their product, hoping to receive an investment. The entrepreneurs introduce 
themselves and their product, stating the amount of cash they are requesting and the 
percentage stake in their company they are offering in return. 2. After the pitch is concluded, the 
Sharks are invited to ask questions to inform their investment decision. There is no pre-set 
sequence or speaking order, so any Shark can speak at any point." (Jetter and Stockley, 2022, p. 
1953-1954) 
 
The subjected competition follows some elements of the reality show Shark Tank. There 
are five “sharks” in the room, including the course-responsible teachers from the 
university. The so-called “sharks” are not necessarily actual investors, they could also 
be industry professionals or just simply working in the startup-scene – and the panel 
changes every semester. The sharks do not meet the teams prior to the pitching 
sessions and do not know anything about the startups beforehand. They sit in individual 
chairs facing a projector, where the pitcher presents, while the rest of the team sits on 
the side. The room is colorful and has a laid-back setting, with laughter and small talk. 
Each pitching competition day has about 15 to 20 startups pitching, and the 
competition goes consistently with minimal breaks for the sharks for about 5 to 6 hours. 
The voting for which startup should advance into the accelerator program is done after 
the last pitch, following a small break. 
Before entering the pitching room, students sign a sheet whether they wish to 
continue working on their startup after the course ends – as a few of those who sign yes 
will be chosen by the sharks to continue to an accelerator program. When the teams go 
in the pitching room one-by-one, they first pitch their startup and their progress in six 
==PAGE:7==
 
6
minutes in the form of a presentation that must include the following elements: 
problem statement, solution, traction, business model, market size, competition, 
budget, timeline, team and technology. Another six-minute question and answer round 
follows the six-minute presentation, equaling twelve minutes per startup spent in the 
room. Based on the presentations and the questions, the sharks choose which teams 
should go onto the accelerator program after the course.  
 
3. Research questions 
Within the above-described case, this thesis is interested in the interaction between 
the investors and the teams. More specifically, whether there is any gender-based bias 
present in that interaction. Therefore, it asks: 
Is there a relation between the questions asked by the investors and the gender of the 
investor and/or the team? 
Furthermore, this thesis uses a large language model to act as an investor in such a 
setting and ask questions from the teams as an unbiased lens. This will provide an 
interesting ground for comparison between the two: the investors’ questions and the 
large language model’s questions. Thus, it asks:  
What can we understand by the similarity or difference between the type of questions 
asked by the investors and the type of questions asked by the large language model? 
 
Now let us explore contemporary research that talks about gender bias in 
entrepreneurship to better understand the field and the basis of this research. 
4. Literature review 
4.1. What happens in the Q&A phase? Investor behavior and decision-
making patterns 
 
This paper’s data is based on an interaction between entrepreneurs and 
investors and has a large focus on the questions asked after the pitch, in the question-
and-answer round – which is what the next theories talk about. Lastly, a new approach 
==PAGE:8==
 
7
compared to traditional sexism is considered, to understand the playing field more 
efficiently. 
 
Crowe and Higgins, in their 1997 paper write about regulatory focus theory – a 
psychological tool that explains how people regulate their behavior to achieve their 
goals (Crowe & Higgins, 1997). There are two motivational factors they take into account 
to do this: a promotion-focus, and a prevention-focus. A promotion focus is associated 
with elements of aspirations, growth, and positive outcomes (1997). People with this 
focus are motivated by advancement and are more likely to take risks. In contrast, a 
prevention-focus is associated with security, avoiding negative outcomes and focusing 
on responsibility (1997). People with a preventive focus tend to avoid risk-taking and 
prioritize safety and caution (1997). Crowe and Higgins proposed that these focuses 
influence individuals’ strategic tendencies in decision-making (1997). They state that 
when faced with a task, a promotion-focused person is more likely to perform better, 
while a prevention-focused person is more prone to quitting. Crowe and Higgins also 
state that a regulatory focus can also be situationally induced – for example in feedback 
mechanisms, which is something along the lines of this thesis’ focus (Roney, Higgins, & 
Shah, 1995; cited in Crowe and Higgins, 1997). Furthermore, they conclude that 
regulatory focus theory shows us that in certain situations, people tend to shape their 
behavior in a way that allows them to reach the desired end-state of that interaction or 
activity (Crowe and Higgins, 1997).  
 
Brockner, Higgins and Low (2004) studied the application of regulatory focus 
theory on entrepreneurship. The authors argue that both promotion and prevention 
focuses are essential, but at different stages of entrepreneurship.  Promotion focus is 
said to be more effective for idea generation, while a prevention focus is essential in 
helping in risk assessment in the due diligence phase. Across different stages of 
business development, it is useful for entrepreneurs to adopt either a promotion or a 
prevention-focus and to switch between the two (Brockner et al., 2004). In the idea 
generation phase, it is useful to take a promotive focus for the entrepreneur, as it 
encourages creative thinking; but as the process develops to critical reflection and the 
due diligence phase, it becomes useful to adapt a preventive focus (2004). This paper 
from Brockner et al. will be useful for this thesis, as the startups that are the subject of 
==PAGE:9==
 
8
this paper’s dataset are all in the early stages of their business development, but 
through the idea generation phase – they are all in the due diligence stage.  
 
Another theory that discusses regulatory focus theory and the presence of 
promotion and prevention focus differences is the one written by Gutermuth and 
Hamstra (2023). They apply the theory on gender studies to see if there is a difference in 
self-regulatory focus in promotive and preventive approaches (Gutermuth and Hamstra, 
2023). An important takeaway from the paper, which will be useful for this thesis, is that 
generally, because of socialization processes, there is a difference in the regulatory 
focus approach that women and men take. Generally, women tend to take a preventive 
focus – as they aim to maintain security and safety, protecting their status quo, while 
men tend to take a promotive focus – they are ambitious to potentially develop their 
current status quo (2023). These findings are useful for this thesis as we can examine 
whether there is a difference in what type of regulatory focus women and men investors 
take, and whether we can observe the above-mentioned difference in our subjected 
setting. 
This thesis studies interactions between entrepreneurs and evaluators in a 
similar setting to Shark Tank, so a review on the show by Jetter and Stockley (2023) on 
gender bias in Shark Tank episodes will be useful for our research. They discovered that 
a female entrepreneur is more likely to receive funding from a female investor – 
especially in industries such as “beauty, health, children or fashion” (p. 1950), while 
male investors did not show as significant of a preference towards either gender (2023). 
Jetter and Stockley’s therefore posed that it may be a solution-oriented factor towards 
gender inequality in funding decisions if there were more women investors on board. 
Furthermore, Jetter and Stockley draw on Kanze et al.’s study on behavioral dynamics 
between entrepreneurs and evaluators during a question-and-answer setting after a 
pitch (2018). Their theory builds on the previously mentioned theory by Crowe and 
Higgins (1997) about regulatory focus theory, moreover the different decision-making 
orientations: prevention and promotion focus. Kanze et al. (2018) connect this theory to 
gender studies, as they found that investors or evaluators are more likely to ask 
prevention-focused questions to female entrepreneurs, while male entrepreneurs are 
more likely to be asked promotion-focused questions (2018). The distinction comes 
from Higgings’ regulatory focus theory (1997, 1998, cited in Kanze et al. 2018), who says 
==PAGE:10==
 
9
that promotion focused questions emphasize gains and aspirations, while a preventive 
focus is more so about risk-aversion and safety (2018). Kanze et al. argue that the type 
of questions investors ask influences the outcome of the pitch and how the ventures are 
perceived and presented, which is important as they found that promotion focused 
questions lead the discussion in a way that is more favorable for the entrepreneur 
compared to those asked more prevention focused questions (2018). However, the 
authors also found that “entrepreneurs can significantly increase funding for their 
startups when responding to prevention-focused questions with promotion-focused 
answers" (Kanze et al., 2018, p. 586). Promotion focused questions think about growth, 
opportunities and how the business can succeed, while prevention-focused questions 
talk more about what could go wrong, the risks and challenges of the startup. 
Malmström et al. (2020) builds on Kanze et al’s research (2018) by adding 
additional layers to the theory. In simple terms, they add that the problem is not only in 
the type of questions being asked of entrepreneurs (promotion or prevention focused 
questions), but rather that investors think and react differently to women than men, 
which affects the former in a negative way. In their study, Malmström et al. apply role 
congruity theory, which focuses on how traditional gender stereotypes influence 
perceptions of behavior – and show that even when women act masculine, expressing 
more entrepreneurial traits of ambition and confidence, they are more likely to be asked 
prevention-focused questions during the Q&A session, and the stronger the shown 
entrepreneurial traits are by women the more resistance they face, even though men’s 
masculine behavior triggers a positive response in investors. Malmström et al. explain 
this by pointing out that when women act as the traditional feminine stereotype, 
investors tend to think they lack the “toughness” that is needed in the entrepreneurial 
world, therefore labeling them as unsuitable for the role – or in the opposite case, when 
women act masculine they are seen as not trustworthy or credible, as they do not 
match the expectations posed upon them (2020). Either way, women lose.  
To understand why these bias may arise, it is useful for us to look at a study by 
Nguyen et al. (2024), who bring another perspective to gender bias in entrepreneurship 
– benevolent sexism. Unlike traditional sexism portraying women in entrepreneurship as 
incompetent, benevolent sexism is characterized by a seemingly positive attitude 
towards women and is therefore more socially acceptable in modern society – or at 
==PAGE:11==
 
10
least does not seem as discriminatory (2024). However, the authors say it reinforces 
traditional gender roles and portrays women as being dependent on men, while those 
that are evaluating entrepreneurs through the lens of benevolent sexism prefer to see 
women in roles of nurturing and support but would “perceive them as unsuitable for 
roles that require assertiveness, competitiveness, or independence, such as 
entrepreneurship” (p. 510). Nguyen et al. hypothesized that benevolent sexism would 
negatively affect the evaluations of women-led startups, however, the findings show 
that it does not have a direct influence on women-led startups but does in fact have a 
positive impact on men-led startups (2024). This can be explained by evaluators 
unconsciously perceiving men as a “better fit” compared to women, who are not 
necessarily seen as incompetent, but rather men are seen more competent – which has 
to do with the stereotypical masculine traits linked to entrepreneurship (2024). This is 
important for this paper as the subjected pitching competition’s investors or judges are 
aware of the male dominance of the field and are warned against traditional sexism – 
but the benevolent sexism aspect may still be relevant, as it is more socially acceptable 
in modern society, as mentioned before. 
4.2. Alternatives to Venture Capital Funding: Crowdfunding and GVCFs 
to eliminate biases 
 
According to Cornell (2017), startups that develop an innovative technology 
often need external funding, which is the case for this paper’s research subject.  
Venture capital is probably the most commonly known form of securing funding, as 
seen on many reality TV shows like Shark Tank. However, as research has shown over 
the years, there could be many bias present from the investors’ side (Johnson et al., 
2018; Cowden et al., 2021; Johansson et al., 2021; Balachandra et al., 2017; 
Balachandra et al., 2021). Could crowdfunding or government venture capitalist 
financiers be a less discriminatory alternative? Let us look into what contemporary 
research has to say about that. 
 
Firstly, Johnson et al.’s paper, “A woman’s place is in the… startup!” (2018), talks 
about how implicit gender bias affect crowdfunding decisions. There has been 
significant research on gender inequalities in venture capital funding, typically unveiling 
a disadvantage for women entrepreneurs. According to Johnson et al., "Female-led 
==PAGE:12==
 
11
firms only receive 1.3% of venture capital financing" (2018, p. 813), which already 
speaks on its own, but additionally, when female entrepreneurs do secure funding, they 
usually face less favorable terms, such as having to surrender larger ownership stakes 
than men (Johnson et al., 2018). However, compared to venture capital funding, 
crowdfunding has offered a new paradigm. Crowdfunding is an emerging, more 
democratic take on funding practices where unlike venture capitalists, who are typically 
highly experienced and formally educated in investment, there are crowdfunders, 
amateurs with moderate personal wealth and limited financial expertise (Johnson et al., 
2018). In their paper, the authors discuss this new paradigm. The results of the analysis 
reveal that "women were significantly more likely to be funded than men" (Johnson et 
al., 2018, p. 821). To explain, Johnson et al. (2018) draw upon the Stereotype Content 
Model, which states that people make judgements about others based on two 
elements: competence and warmth – where trustworthiness closely relates to warmth. 
They found that in a crowdfunding environment women are viewed as more nurturing 
and trustworthy but less competent than men – but despite the latter, "the positive 
trustworthiness effect was sufficient to create a net funding advantage for women" 
(Johnson et al., 2018, p. 823). Overall, they found that individual differences in implicit 
gender bias are what decide the relationship between gender and funding outcomes – 
but in a crowdfunding environment, women are more likely to be funded. 
Secondly, Cowden et al. (2021) in their study “Gender Role Congruity and 
Crowdfunding Success” also talk about the crowdfunding setting and how it affects 
funding outcomes, but its findings conflict with the previous study by Johnson et al. 
(2018). It is said that traditional funding environments are often biased against female 
entrepreneurs, limiting their access to venture capital (Cowden et al., 2021). However, 
crowdfunding is regarded as a more democratic alternative that allows public 
participation in funding decisions (Cowden et al., 2021). According to Cowden et al. 
(2021), "funders tend to back entrepreneurs that look like them or those that they can 
relate to" (p. S135). The authors hypothesize that female entrepreneurs are more likely 
to succeed when exhibiting feminine traits and that male entrepreneurs succeed when 
behaving masculine, however assertiveness is thought to be a beneficial behavioral trait 
for an entrepreneur regardless of gender. Through their study all hypotheses proved to 
be true. Female entrepreneurs benefit from being perceived as humble and agreeable, 
==PAGE:13==
 
12
while balancing the trait of assertiveness correctly to have better results, whereas male 
entrepreneurs succeed when being seen as assertive and emotionally stable (Cowden 
et al., 2021). While crowdfunding platforms aim to eliminate biases, gender role 
expectations still shape funding decisions (Cowden et al., 2021). These findings 
emphasize the notion that societal norms, no matter the setting, still persist to have an 
influence over entrepreneurial success, even in the more democratized settings. The 
study says that entrepreneurs may benefit from balancing their gender signaling to align 
with the expectations of the investor (Cowden et al., 2021). 
Johansson et al. (2021) talk about governmental venture capitalist financiers or 
GVCFs for short, and their cognitive biases and decision-making patterns in a Swedish 
example. GVCFs are legally mandated institutions, which operate to ensure non-
discrimination in their funding process – however, Johansson et al. show that even in 
such settings there are cognitive biases in place which influence their evaluations 
(2021). Firstly, they identified that when assessing women-led ventures, they allocate 
more cognitive effort to analyzing their ventures which leads to a more complex 
evaluation, meaning that an increased reliance on subjective perspectives of the 
evaluator. As a result of this they focus more on the trustworthiness and passion of the 
female entrepreneur, rather than the venture’s business potential alone. Secondly, 
Johansson et al. find that men’s ventures are mainly assessed based on financial 
viability and growth potential, whereas women’s ventures are more so evaluated with 
emphasis on the entrepreneur's personal characteristics (2021). This misalignment 
results in systematic bias, where female entrepreneurs face additional barriers in 
securing funding. Another factor Johansson et al. highlight is that GVCFs often question 
female entrepreneurs’ ability to handle risk and challenges, even though they present 
similar business plans to men. They explain this by role congruity theory (Eagly & Karau, 
2002; cited in Johansson et al. 2021), positing that women in leadership positions are 
often evaluated more critically. Overall, these findings contribute to a better 
understanding of implicit gender bias in financial decision-making. Although the 
subjected pitching competition of this paper cannot be viewed as a governmental 
venture capitalist financier, the study is important as the course responsibles and the 
subjected leading Northern European technical university all strive for diversity, equity 
and inclusion – trying to remain non-discriminatory in the evaluation of the startups 
==PAGE:14==
 
13
during their final exam, which could potentially play a part in making the competition 
itself less susceptible to biases. 
 
4.3. Behavior, rather than gender: How does the entrepreneur’s 
language or behavior affect investor perceptions? 
 
The next studies talk about how the entrepreneur’s behavior and gendered 
communication affect the investors’ perspectives, and they were all researched in 
venture capitalist environments. 
Balachandra et al. (2017), in their paper “Don’t pitch like a girl!” challenge the 
thought that investors’ preferences favor men only due to sex-based bias. Their findings 
suggest that such biases may be more accurately explained by investors' negative 
perceptions of feminine-stereotyped behaviors rather than the entrepreneurs' sex 
(2017). Gender role theory explains the way that societal expectations create 
stereotypical behaviors for both men and women. While men are generally expected to 
display traits such as assertiveness and dominance, women are expected to exhibit 
traits such as warmth and expressiveness (Eagly & Wood, 2011; Heilman, 1983, cited in 
Balachandra et al., 2017). Entrepreneurship is traditionally viewed as a masculine 
sphere, and favors the display of masculine-stereotyped behaviors, which are 
associated with business competence and leadership (Gupta et al. 2009, cited in 
Balachandra et al., 2017). The findings of the study suggest that entrepreneurs that 
displayed feminine-stereotyped behaviors, regardless of sex, faced significant bias, 
while masculine-stereotyped behaviors positively striked investor interest. Balachandra 
et al. also added to gender congruity theory – which states that people who do not fit to 
gendered expectations of their role (in this case the masculine-stereotyped 
entrepreneurship) experience negative evaluations and is mostly talked about with 
regards to women displaying feminine-stereotyped behavior. However, Balachandra et 
al. add that men, who display traits of feminine-stereotyped behaviors also face 
setbacks in these settings. These findings show that the main determining factor of the 
investor’s bias is not the sex of the entrepreneur, but whether their behavior aligns with 
gendered expectations of the role they are trying to fulfill, in this case, masculine-
stereotyped entrepreneurship. The findings of Balachandra et al.’s study would be 
==PAGE:15==
 
14
important to analyzing the behavior of entrepreneurs and investor engagement in our 
study, as similarly to its elevator pitch style data collection, our study’s pitches are short 
and concise in a pressured environment.  
Balachandra et al. analyzed pitches to understand the relationship between 
gendered language and funding outcomes from evaluators in their paper “Do (women’s) 
words matter? The influence of gendered language in entrepreneurial pitching” (2021). 
They characterized the feminine communication style as rapport-building talk, and 
relational talk – more specifically “indirect language that minimizes their agency and 
focuses on establishing connections” (Mulac et al. 2001; Tannen, 2001, cited in 
Balachandra et al. 2021 p. 2) with their conversation partner. They characterize the 
masculine communication style as “forceful, self-promoting, direct language” (Mulac et 
al. 2001; Tannen, 2001, cited in Balachandra et al. 2021 p. 2), and using more complex 
words. The study found that there is no direct preference from the evaluators to either 
feminine or masculine communication styles, but perhaps rather a balance between 
the two. First, using gender congruity theory, the study examined whether women were 
more likely to use feminine communication styles and men were more likely to use 
masculine, but that did not prove to be true – rather, it seemed that women were more 
likely to adapt to masculine communication styles during the pitches (2021). Rapport 
talks and ambivalence from feminine communication styles negatively impacted the 
outcome of a pitch, while inspirational language from the masculine style positively 
influenced the outcome of the pitch. However, excessive focus on work, or personal 
accomplishments and the use of more complex words negatively impacted the 
outcome of the pitches. Thus, Balachandra et al.’s study reveals that it is not necessarily 
gender that influences the success rate of an entrepreneur pitching, but rather their 
linguistic style (2021). 
 
4.4. Hypotheses 
After carefully reviewing the relevant literature in gender studies in entrepreneurship 
and relating research, some hypotheses will be developed. 
According to current research, the field of entrepreneurship not only remains a male-
dominated space, but the preference for masculinity – including sex and gendered 
==PAGE:16==
 
15
behavior – are largely preferred by evaluators, such as venture capitalists (Johnson et 
al., 2018; Cowden et al., 2021; Johansson et al., 2021; Balachandra et al., 2017; 
Balachandra et al., 2021). According to the regulatory focus theory’s application to the 
entrepreneurial process combined with the latter statement, the following hypothesis is 
derived:  
 
 
H1: During the question-and-answer rounds, female-dominated teams will be 
asked more prevention-focused questions than the male-dominated teams. 
 
  
H2: During the question-and-answer rounds, male-dominated teams will be 
asked more promotion-focused questions than the female-dominated teams. 
 
Furthermore, Gutermuth and Hamstra (2023) brought an interesting layer of 
research onto the regulatory focus theory, stating that there is an observed difference in 
what type of focus different genders tend to take. This thesis would like to examine 
whether their findings can also be applied in the subjected setting of this research, 
whether women investors or “sharks” will have a more preventive focus, and whether 
men investors or “sharks” will have a more promotive focus when asking questions of 
the entrepreneurs. We have three semesters’ worth of data, and since the panel of 
investors does not stay the same each semester, we have a handful of cases to 
examine. Thus, another hypothesis is stated: 
 
H3: Female investors will ask more preventive questions than male investors. 
 
 
 
H4: Male investors will ask more promotive questions than female investors. 
 
Additionally, as the “method” section of this paper will further elaborate, a large 
language model was used to generate questions for the pitchers. Without further 
information about the outcome of this test, it is expected that the questions generated 
by the tool will follow the same pattern as the sharks’ questions in terms of preventive-
promotive-neutral interrelation. Based on this, a hypothesis is developed, which is as 
following:  
==PAGE:17==
 
16
 
H5: The Large Language Model generated questions will have the same or similar ratio 
of preventive-promotive-neutral questions as the investors’ questions. 
5. Method 
5.1. Data collection 
As mentioned above, this paper is based on a pitching competition at a leading 
technical Northern European university, more specifically, pitching competitions 
through three consecutive semesters. A microphone was set up in the pitching room to 
record the pitches and the Q&A sessions that follow them, which all were later 
transcribed. In the transcriptions we noted whether the pitcher was male or female, and 
whether the shark asking the question was male or female. A data collection of the 
teams’ gender distribution also exists, which will be used for the analysis of the findings.  
5.2. Analyzing the dataset 
 
Firstly, to analyze the dataset, the questions from the sharks in the Q&A sessions 
were separated team-by-team and categorized based on Crowe and Higgins (1997); 
Kanze et al. (2018) and Malmström et al.’s (2020) work on prevention and promotion 
focused questions, to understand whether there is such a bias in this particular setting 
of entrepreneurial pitching. The questions were each examined and evaluated whether 
they are prevention-focused, promotion focused, or neutral – and the examination was 
done by two human raters. As mentioned before, prevention-focused questions are 
those which talk about risks and challenges of the startup, what could go wrong – its 
keywords are safety and security. Promotion-focused questions, on the other hand, talk 
about how the business can succeed, growth, advancement, and opportunities. 
However, we found it to be useful to have a third category – neutral – questions that we 
think are neither preventive nor promotive. Neutral questions are clarifying questions 
about the product, business, or market in general.  
The two raters’ evaluations were then compared to each other, using the Cohen 
coefficient by Jacob Cohen, which is a statistical tool used to quantify the agreement of 
the raters in datasets such as ours, and it also accounts for the possibility of agreement 
==PAGE:18==
 
17
occurring by chance (Cohen, 1960). The observed agreement between the raters is 
0.83; whereas the agreement expected by chance is 0.32. Cohen’s Kappa is calculated 
using these two metrics, where Kappa could equal 1, meaning that there is a perfect 
agreement, or 0, which suggests an agreement that is no better than chance (1960). 
Landis and Koch (1977) made a scale for assessing the Cohen Kappa’s results between 
0 and 1, where our result of Kappa that equals to 0.83 accounts for an “almost perfect 
agreement” ( 1977, p. 165). Based on this result, we can say that the agreement 
between raters has minimal disagreement far beyond what would be expected by 
chance alone, and the results are highly reliable and consistent. 
5.3. Using a Large Language Model 
The transcribed text files of the latest pitching competition (particularly only the 
presentation or pitch part) were each given to a large language model, ChatGPT-4. The 
idea behind was that the model does not see or know the gender and the gendered 
behavior of the pitcher, but only the pitch alone, acting as an unbiased, unfiltered lens 
to look at each pitch and generate questions by itself. To do this, a prompt was given to 
the LLM: "As an experienced startup investor, read this pitch and generate 5 specific, 
concise questions to evaluate if the startup qualifies for our accelerator program. Focus 
on crucial aspects like market potential, business model, team capabilities, 
competitive advantage, technology and traction.”. The questions that the LLM came up 
with were then also evaluated based on the same criteria as the sharks’ questions – 
they were divided into the three categories of prevention, promotion or neutral 
questions. As the agreement was almost perfect based on the Cohen kappa coefficient 
based on the previous results, only one of the raters went through these questions 
generated by ChatGPT-4. Observing whether there will be a difference between the 
sharks’ questions and the LLM’s questions would in turn provide interesting data to 
analyze. 
Later, as the results of the first LLM prompt proved to be drastic, it was evaluated 
whether the fact that the prompt did not include the fact that the pitchers were 
students. Therefore, another round of questions was generated, now with another 
prompt, which included that the pitchers were students.

On average, the amount of preventive questions accounts just slightly below the 
number of neutral questions, which is an interesting phenomenon to look at. This could 
be interpreted in several ways. Firstly, to understand the case of neutral questions, we 
have to consider the setting of the pitching competition. The sharks meet the startups 
on the spot when they present, which means that they cannot prepare prior to the pitch 
with questions. The pitches are also done back-to-back, with minimal breaks, and since 
all the startups are in a highly-technical field in diverse industries, it can be hard for the 
human mind to follow such complex topics for a long time – therefore the sharks may 
ask these clarifying questions that serve for the sole purpose of understanding. 
However, approaching the interpretation of the difference between preventive and 
promotive questions, it is useful to bring Brockner et al.’s theory into play (2004). As 
mentioned before, they note that in the early-stages of a startup, such as the phase of 
due-diligence, it is beneficial to adapt a preventive-focus, as it focuses on risk 
assessment, which is vital in this phase. Thus, it could be said that the same 
phenomenon is true for the type of questions that evaluators tend to ask the 
entrepreneurs, and it could be an answer for why there are far more prevention-focused 
questions than promotive. As Brockner et al. say: 
 
“Screening the idea entails assessing the feasibility of the venture. Put differently, it requires 
asking the hard questions, which typically call attention to ways in which the venture might fail… 
This seems to be the essence of a prevention‐focused orientation.” (2004, p. 210) 
 
Asking the hard questions is essential to assess whether a startup should go forward to 
the accelerator program, and in the end finding that out is the job of the investors on this 
panel. In this setting, asking prevention-focused questions may be a way to help the 
entrepreneurs, rather than it being a “bad” thing. Calling attention to the ways in which 
the venture could fail is essential to ensure they are not overlooking anything. However, 
assuming that the setting is an entrepreneur-investor space, in a similar setting to Shark 
Tank, one may assume that it is supposed to be a professional field, where the fact that 
these are students presenting work done during a university course would be 
overlooked, and they would be treated as real-life entrepreneurs as if they were 
==PAGE:22==
 
21
presenting to investors. However, it is useful to consider this “artificial setting”. I say 
artificial, as the competition is treated as a real-life investor-entrepreneur interaction, 
while in reality it is just a final exam for a university course. Considering that the 
students are aware of this paradox – and considering that some students decide prior 
going into the pitch session that they do not wish to continue working on their startup, 
but still must present as if they were, in hope of receiving a good grade – exaggeration 
may be present during the presentation and blowing certain things up to look better 
than they actually are. Could this be the reason behind the number of preventive 
questions? As Brockner et al. say:  
 
“When screening an idea it is important to be careful and accurate. This is not a time to be 
impulsive or overeager. It is a time to be concerned with ‘quality’ and not just the generation of 
many possibilities (i.e., ‘quantity’).” (2004, p. 210) 
 
The investors are in fact screening the idea for the first time, and as they have the 
deciding power on who goes forward or not, it is essential that they are critical when 
asking questions – and find out what is the exaggeration behind the painted picture, if 
there is any. This could be the reason for why they are asking more preventive questions. 
However, this thesis is dedicated to assessing whether there are gender bias present in 
the subjected pitching competition and aims to discover that by looking at the question-
and-answer rounds. According to  Nguyen et al.: 
 
“… judging the potential of early-stage startups is an inherently uncertain process, as objective 
information about startup quality is incomplete (Cao et al., 2021), making it susceptible to the 
influence of biases based on ascriptive characteristics such as entrepreneurs’ gender (Kanze et 
al., 2020)” (Nguyen et al., 2024, p. 508) 
 
Similarly as in Nguyen et al.’s example, the pitching competition that this thesis 
discusses involves judging early-stage startups, which is an uncertain process – the 
startups only pitch for and interact with the judges for a brief amount of time, thus bias 
may arise – thus let us look into whether we can observe bias based on the 
==PAGE:23==

research. 
 
6.2. Bias based on entrepreneur’s gender 
 
The initial results of this research suggest that there are more prevention-
focused questions asked from the entrepreneurs than promotion-focused ones, which 
is interesting, as in general, through all three years, 64% of the startup teams are male-
dominated (in cases when there is 51% or more men in the team). This initially, 
according to Kanze et al.’s (2018) theory about prevention and promotion focused 
questions would suggest that due to a male majority, the majority of the questions 
would also be promotive – but that is clearly not the case. Therefore, let us continue this 
analysis by looking at the dataset in different ways to identify whether we can observe 
any form of gender bias in the question-and-answer rounds.  
Firstly, to analyze the data statistically, the Shapiro-Wilks test showed that most 
variables were deviated from normality, thus other tests were done. The Kruskal-Wallis 
test on the distribution of prevention and promotion questions showed no significant 
difference; and Spearman’s rank test showed that  the more women there is in a team, 
the more preventive questions are being asked of them, while the more men are present 
in a team, the less preventive questions are being asked – however, the correlation did 
not reach statistical significance. 
 
Firstly, let us consider whether the gender of the teams play a part in the 
distribution of prevention or promotion questions. It is important to note here, that the 
teams in general are very diverse, therefore it is difficult to judge – unlike on Shark Tank, 
where generally one or two people represent the startup, the teams here are 
represented by all of the members. However, we can look at average results and see if 
there are any findings we can derive. As mentioned before, 64% of the teams are male-
dominated, and only 25% of them are female-dominated throughout all the years (the 
rest are mixed). Based on a comparison of the type of questions asked from the 
entrepreneurs eliminating neutral questions and only looking at preventive and 
promotive questions(Appendix 2), we can say that in the case of female dominated

promotive and equal preventive. There was not one time, that a female-dominated 
group got asked more promotion-focused questions, however, there was 4 times that 
male-led groups got asked more promotional questions than anything else. These 
findings could be interpreted by Kanze et al.’s theory, which says that evaluators are 
more likely to ask prevention-focused questions to female entrepreneurs, and that male 
entrepreneurs are more likely to be asked promotion-focused questions (2018). On the

6.5. Subject seen as crowdfunding 
 
As Johnson et al. say, the practice of crowdfunding is an emerging, more democratic 
take on funding practices where unlike venture capitalists, who are typically highly 
experienced and formally educated in investment, there are crowdfunders, amateurs 
with moderate personal wealth and limited financial expertise (Johnson et al., 2018). 
Furthermore, Johansson et al. (2021) found that governmental venture capitalist 
financiers create funding conditions that are less susceptible to gender-based 
discrimination. 
It could be argued that the subjected pitching competition counts more as a 
crowdfunding practice than a venture capitalist environment, similar to the widely 
known reality TV show, Shark Tank, which is what it is advertised to the students as. This 
could be said as the panel of the investors, who sit in the subjected pitching 
competition, are changing every year, and are not necessarily investors, let alone 
venture capitalists – but simply industry professionals or people from the startup field 
with expertise on some aspects of technological startups. The panel of investors also 
have no financial interest in the startups as they do not gain or lose money out of this 
process. This altogether could make the competition’s setting lean more towards to a 
crowdfunding platform than a venture capitalist funding setting and could begin to 
provide an explanation for why we are unable to observe any major gender bias based 
on the chosen theories. Furthermore, as Johansson et al. (2021) say, governmental 
venture capitalist financiers are less susceptible to gender-based discrimination, due to 
the fact that they allocate resources to educating their organization about bias. The 
==PAGE:34==
 
33
same can be said about the leading technical Northern European university that this 
thesis is situated in. Diversity, equity and inclusion are strong parts of their vision and 
mission, which could translate into the studied university course that the pitching 
competition is the final exam of. Perhaps this is why it is not so easy to find gender-
based discrimination in it and why the results of the research do not show strong 
correlations to theory. 
Furthermore, Johansson et al. (2021) find that men’s ventures are mainly assessed 
based on financial viability and growth potential, whereas women’s ventures are more 
so evaluated with emphasis on the entrepreneur's personal characteristics (2021).  
This misalignment results in systematic bias, where female entrepreneurs face 
additional barriers in securing funding. Johnson et al. (2018) and Cowden et al. (2021) in 
their research also have shown that even though the gender bias in crowdfunding 
environments differs to venture capitalist settings, they are not non-existent. Thus, even 
though the environment differs, bias will arise based on gender, both in a GVCF and 
crowdfunding environment. Thus, a following study conducted on the same dataset 
would be beneficial to discovering what bias, if any, can be observed in this setting by 
looking deeper into the complexity of the interaction between the parties – not only 
gender but race, language barriers, psychological relations and team dynamics of both 
the entrepreneurs and the investors. That study extends the capacities of this thesis, 
but in light of it – and this thesis together – we could summarize a fuller picture of what 
happens exactly in this setting and how the overall process could be improved. 
 
7. Discussion 
This section of the paper will be firstly used to revisit the hypotheses and connect 
them to the findings of the research; and secondly, to answer the research questions. 
The hypotheses, and their connections to the findings are as follows:  
H1: During the question-and-answer rounds, female-dominated teams will be asked 
more prevention-focused questions than the male-dominated teams. 
Technically, this hypothesis proved to be true, as in a prevention-promotion comparison 
only, 69,2%% of the questions asked of female-dominated teams were prevention-
==PAGE:35==
 
34
focused questions, while only 58,8% of the questions asked from male-dominated 
teams were prevention-focused ones. Female-led teams were asked more preventive 
questions; however, the results could be said to be statistically insignificant as the 
results are too close to each other.  
 H2: During the question-and-answer rounds, male-dominated teams will be asked 
more promotion-focused questions than the female-dominated teams. 
Yet again, this hypothesis could be said to be true, as in the case of male-dominated 
teams 23,5% of the questions were promotional, while in the case of female-dominated 
teams 23,1% of the questions were (when excluding neutral questions). However, due 
to the small difference in percentage we cannot say that it is a relevant finding. 
H3: Female investors will ask more preventive questions than male investors. 
Female investors did ask more preventive questions than male investors, however, this 
is only true because female investors asked more questions in general. As can be seen 
on Figure 7, the preventive questions outweigh the promotive ones in the cases of both 
genders. 
H4: Male investors will ask more promotive questions than female investors. 
This is not true. Female investors asked more promotive questions than male ones, but 
only because they asked more questions in total. Male investors did not ask more 
promotive questions than female investors, and in fact, promotive was the least type of 
question asked by male investors. 
H5: The Large Language Model generated questions will have the same or similar ratio 
of preventive-promotive-neutral questions as the investors’ questions. 
This is not true. As the ratio between the two can be seen on Figure 9, there is a big 
difference between the questions asked by the large language model and the investors. 
There are differences in all categories asked by the investors and the large language 
model, the major difference being that the majority of investor questions are 
prevention-focused, and the majority of the LLM’s questions are promotion-focused. 
H6: With the second prompt that includes “students” in the task, the LLM will ask more 
prevention-focused questions than promotion-focused ones. 
As can be seen on Figure 10, the second prompt did indeed ask more preventive 
questions, but the overall ratio stayed the same. It only asked 6 more preventive 
questions than the prompt without having mentioned students. The reason why this 
==PAGE:36==
 
35
hypothesis was created is that there was a major observed difference in the sharks’ 
questions and the LLM’s questions – and it aimed to explore whether this difference 
would be purely because of the educational setting, but that did not prove to be the 
case. However, all of the names and potential pointers to the presenter’s and the team 
members’ genders were taken out of the transcribed pitch given to the LLM, so the small 
difference between the two prompts given to the LLM was interpreted in another way 
– but the hypothesis is technically true, however, the difference cannot be statistically 
interpreted as significant.  
 
Furthermore, the research questions of this paper were:  
Is there a relation between the questions asked by the investors and the gender of the 
investor and/or the team? 
What can we understand by the similarity or difference between the type of questions 
asked by the investors and the type of questions asked by the large language model? 
 
And can be answered as follows. No statistically significant relation was found in the 
relation between the questions asked by the investors and the gender of the team or the 
gender of the investors. To unveil potential gender bias present in the interaction, 
additional research is needed examining the intricacies and psychological relations, 
perhaps the additional language barrier. Furthermore, the type of questions asked by 
the large language model and the investors had a significant difference, the major 
difference being that the LLM asked far more promotive questions with both prompts, 
while the investors asked far more preventive questions. The analysis interpreted the 
difference in a way that the LLM, instead of being an unbiased lens, is actually biased, 
as it is trained on general data from the internet, and general data largely shows a male 
bias present in entrepreneurship – thus based on the difference between the type of 
questions asked by the investors and the large language model we can understand that 
the general public has a large male bias but investors in the subjected pitching 
competition did not show the same bias. 
 
The findings from this study would suggest that investors ask more promotional 
questions from the startup teams, to provide a fairer landscape for startups in an 
educational setting.

8. Limitations and Ethical Considerations 
This section of the paper is dedicated to delving into the limitations and ethical 
considerations of this paper and the research. First and foremost, it is important to 
address the interchangeably used terms for “female” and “woman”; as well as “male” 
and “man”. While female and male refer to the person’s biological sex, woman and man 
refer to the person’s gender – which “relates to traits of masculinity or femininity, 
including such characteristics as sex‐typed interests and occupations, appearance, 
mannerisms, and nonverbal behavior (Lippa 2005).” (McDermott and Hatemi, 2011, p. 
89). The author acknowledges that the terms should not be used interchangeably, it is 
solely done for the ease of writing; and it is also acknowledged that it is not only the two 
genders of women and men that exist, but for the ease of research other genders have 
been not included in this research. 
Furthermore, it is important to yet again mention the artificial setting of the 
subjected pitching competition. It is situated in an educational setting, which is 
portrayed to the students as a setting similar to Shark Tank. It is at the end of the day an 
exam, but due to the nature of the setting, the artificiality of it has to be considered. 
They all pitch for a grade, and as part of a university course, and the full picture cannot 
be considered. A more cohesive study is needed considering all aspects of this setting, 
including the psychology behind it. 
Furthermore, the pitches and the questions that were asked by both the sharks and the 
large language model were not shared in protection of sensitive data about the startups 
and identities of investors and students. 
9.  Suggestions for further research 
As mentioned before, based on the theories this paper used for analyzing its 
findings, we could not observe significant gender-based bias in the subjected setting. 
This could be due to the educational element of it, or the crowdfunding aspect – but 
either way, it has been proven by prior academic research, that bias may still be present 
in such situations. Therefore, it is useful that suggestions are made for future research 
that could uncover such bias. 
==PAGE:38==
 
37
For example, it would be beneficial to look into the language barrier between the team 
and the investors. The university course, as well as the pitching competition are done in 
English, while that is not the native language of the country that it is situated in. This 
could add some additional barriers with accent, potential lack of understanding, and 
would be beneficial to look deeper into. Elements such as age, visual representation 
such as clothing (professional or not), visible ingroup relations of team members, the 
number of team members that speak, the industry the startup is in – would all be 
interesting layers to look into. 
As mentioned before, the large majority of preventive questions could be 
explained by the fact that the competition is in an educational setting. In these terms, it 
would be interesting to look into the presentation style – what approach do the 
entrepreneurs have? Perhaps they represent themselves as entrepreneurs building a 
venture and sound excited about it, but perhaps they represent themselves as students 
completing a university course. Looking into whether this has a role in determining the 
success of the startups would be an interesting layer added to the research. As 
Brockner et al. say: 
 
"Sophomoric mistakes in presentation style may well turn investors off. So, getting financial 
(and other sources of) support from would-be providers may require more than a lofty vision; 
those seeking support need to show would-be providers that they ‘have their act together,’ that 
they can be trusted to do things competently and with good intentions." (Brockner et al., 2004, 
p. 211) 
 
Theory shows that presentation style matters and looking into how that affects the 
questions and answer rounds in all the individual cases in this case study would add 
good quality data. Furthermore, as Brockner et al. say: 
 
“The more that the rhetoric of authorities focuses on ideals (e.g., continuously reminding 
employees of the exciting vision that the organization is trying to enact), the more likely will 
organization members develop a promotion focus. The more that the rhetoric focuses on 
responsibilities (e.g., such as the mutual ones that employers and employees have to each 
other), the more likely will organization members adopt a prevention focus.”  (2004, p. 216) 
 
==PAGE:39==
 
38
The above statement from Brockner et al.’s paper would provide interesting ground for 
research in this case study as well. Looking into such interplay between the investors as 
a group, and each startup team as well would provide an interesting ground for research 
in team and in-group dynamics.  
Furthermore, a common question that arose during almost all pitches was: “What is 
your biggest challenge?”, which is a question that sheds light onto the reason why the 
business might fail. However, during the pitching session we were notified that in each 
investor panel, there is an “external sensor”, who is responsible for making sure that the 
pitchers are all asked certain questions that help the course responsibles’ evaluate the 
startup, to grade them in the end fairly. We did not know what these questions 
particularly were but noticed that the external sensor kept asking this question. 
Interestingly enough, while some teams were asked “What is your biggest challenge?”, 
others were asked “What is your biggest learning so far?”. It would be interesting to look 
into the cases where this has been said and what we can derive as findings from that, 
but this thesis did not have the capacity to examine that. 
Lastly, a large part of this research that this paper did not have the length and 
capacity to examine was the gender performance of both the investors and the 
entrepreneurs. As many theories point to the influence of gender-based performance 
and behavior, and the effect of masculine and feminine behavior, it would be beneficial 
to look at video recordings of both the team and the investor and examine what can be 
observed, as that would paint a fuller picture. 
 
10. 
Conclusion 
This paper has conducted a case study done on a pitching competition at a leading 
technical Northern European university. The pitching competition serves as the final 
exam to a university course about entrepreneurship, where students get to pitch their 
semester’s project in a setting similar to the American reality television show, Shark 
Tank. The question-and-answer rounds, which took place after each startup pitched 
between the team members and the panel of sharks or investors was analyzed in this 
thesis. Using regulatory focus theory, or more specifically, the prevalence of promotion-
==PAGE:40==
 
39
focused and prevention-focused questions were used to see if any gender-based bias 
could be observed in the dataset. Firstly, the paper looked into whether there were any 
connection found between the gender dispersion of the team and the type of questions 
and found that there was no statistically significant differences. Later, it looked into 
whether it can find any correlation between the gender of the investors and the type of 
questions asked based on gender, but it did not find differences between the two 
genders. The paper then employed the help of a large language model to see if there 
would be a difference in the type of questions asked by the tool and the investors 
themselves and found that there is a significant difference between the two and 
connected it to male gender-based bias. The thesis conducted on the case study thus 
found that in an educational-entrepreneurial setting, investors are more likely to ask 
preventive questions, while taking the role of mentors or advisors instead of investors 
and that, large language models have a male-bias when it comes to entrepreneurship.
