Abstract
The growing share of wind energy in the power grid presents the challenge of having simulation
models to represent the electrical system and controls with high fidelity. Offline and real-time
simulation (RTS) tools are essential for simulating the transient and steady-state characteristics
of an electrical system through Electromagnetic Transient (EMT) analysis. Offline models have
been extensively analyzed and implemented in the wind industry, while RTS simulation tools
have advanced their share only in the past few years. Achieving accurate and faster simulation
results without compromising model fidelity is challenging.
The thesis addresses this challenge by conducting a comparative analysis of offline (EMT)
and real-time (RTS) simulation models. Similar events are simulated in the respective tools,
namely PSCAD (offline) and RSCAD (real-time), with the latter employing the Software-in-the-
Loop (SiL) tool. SiL testing enables high-fidelity real-time simulations, facilitating faster model
verification.
The study develops models simulating the grid-side converter of a Type 4 Wind Turbine Gener-
ator (WTG) integrated with a Power Park Controller (PPC), focusing on the grid-side dynamics.
These models are subjected to events involving setpoint changes to active power, voltage, and
reactive power. A bottom-up modeling approach helps capture the effects of different compo-
nents in each tool from the initial stages, ensuring a thorough comparison.
The SiL tool in RSCAD allows the Intellectual Property (IP) protected integration and testing of
control codes with other models. This thesis details the processes of creating and loading appro-
priate files into the SiL-associated hardware. Python automation scripts facilitate the interaction
and simulation of various events in both tools, enabling efficient testing.
The key differences in modeling stages are highlighted, emphasizing the importance of model-
ing components with similar functionalities in both RTS and offline tools. The thesis identifies
the components exhibiting differences and suggests possible solutions. Various scenarios are
simulated to compare the dynamics.
Differences are observed in the transient and steady-state quantities, such as active power, re-
active power, voltage, and phase angles. Variations in time-step are tried to increase the match
between tools. Additionally, comparing online models with and without the SiL methodology
revealed the differences introduced by implementing the SiL tool.
To conclude, Real-time (RTS) simulations increasingly complement offline EMT tools. This the-
sis provides insights into the advantages and challenges observed in modeling and simulating
the tools using the offline (EMT) and real-time (RTS) methodologies and implementing SiL for
controller testing.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Wind Energy: Status and Future Challenges
The wind industry has experienced rapid growth over the past years, with the Global Wind En-
ergy Council (GWEC) characterizing 2023 as a record year for new capacity installation that
reached 117 GW [1]. The growth is evident since it represents a 50% increase compared to
2022, with countries contributing to new onshore and offshore installations led by China, the
US, Brazil, Germany, and India. The evolution of the wind sector reflects the transition from
conventional fossil-based power generation to renewable energy resources to cover society’s
increasing energy needs while concurrently protecting the environment. The importance of up-
scaling the wind industry is reflected through the need for the already record-breaking annual
growth to triple by 2030 to meet the United Nations’ 28th Conference of the Parties (COP28)
requirements in 2023 [1].
From the estimates shown in Figure 1.1, wind energy will dominate the energy mix and replace
conventional base-load producers by deploying large-scale offshore wind farms whose capac-
ities will substantially exceed 1GW per project [2]. Therefore, Transmission System Operators
(TSOs) are challenged to harmoniously integrate this new variable production in the grid un-
der steady-state and transient conditions. In steady state conditions, TSOs face the challenge
of having a variable energy source as a base producer and heavily relying their scheduling on
weather forecasts. Additionally, in transient conditions such as faults, Wind Power Parks (WPP)
are expected to contribute with ancillary services, such as inertia support, frequency and volt-
age regulation, and black-start, traditionally provided by conventional generation. Therefore, a
synergy is forming where manufacturers have to develop grid-forming capabilities for their wind
turbines. At the same time, TSOs have to adapt the grid codes to incorporate large-scale wind
power production.
Figure 1.1: World installed wind capacity by region [3]
The increasing penetration of Inverter Based Resources (IBR) can also potentially introduce
==PAGE:18==
interoperability issues - control algorithms, grid compliance, and integrating with existing sys-
tems - among multi-vendor converters, developed with their proprietary technology. IBRs are
expected to become prevalent in the grid infrastructure as they are vital components in Type IV
wind turbines and High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) systems. For the former, Type IV wind
turbines utilize a full-scale back-to-back converter that achieves the decoupling of the grid and
generator sides. Moreover, Europe envisions offshore wind farms as nodes in the grid that can
interconnect various countries, offshore wind farms, and energy islands with full control over
the power flow [4]. The longer transmission distances and the need for full controllability of the
power flow are feasible only through Multi-terminal HVDC systems, thus further increasing the
need for converters in the system to ensure controllability, security, and resilience [4]. Therefore,
interoperability must be achieved among all multi-vendor converters in the system.
1.2
Challenges in Wind Turbine Model Validation
Until now, conventional synchronous generators, the stator of which is directly connected to
the grid, constituted the main power generation equipment. As discussed in the above section,
generators are decoupled from the grid using inverter-based technologies. Conventional units
exhibit predictable behavior due to their comparatively larger inertia and synchronous opera-
tion. Model validation for conventional units focuses on stability, voltage regulation, and fault
response. On the other hand, WPPs using inverter-based technologies have low inertia and
are operating asynchronously due to which their behavior is dependent on control algorithms
and grid connections. Model validation has to be considered for grid support, and verification
has to be done for control algorithms [5].
Therefore, it is more timely than ever to address the harmonious integration of large-scale wind
farms to the grid and to ensure interoperability among the multi-vendor converters. These chal-
lenges have also driven the TSO demands for rigorous model testing and validation from de-
velopers and, subsequently, manufacturers throughout the life cycle of the project [6].
In the meantime, the market is developing rapidly, with developers pressuring manufacturers
for faster delivery of validated and high-fidelity models to perform the necessary grid studies
dictated by the TSOs [6]. Consequently, the model validation process needs to be sped up.

1.3
New Model Testing Methods
In the scope of subsystem and component testing, the Hardware-in-the-Loop testing method
may be implemented along with its subcategories, such as Control HiL (cHiL) and Software-
in-the-Loop (SiL). HiL is performed in real-time simulation environments using a combination
of high fidelity Electromagnetic Transient (EMT) models and hardware such as wind turbine
controllers, power park controllers, and other components, allowing for faster model verification
times [9] and increased reliability in component design [10]. Based on [7], verification is the
process of determining that a model implementation accurately represents the developer’s con-
ceptual description of the model and the solution to the model. In other words, verification seeks
to identify, quantify, and reduce errors that may cause the computational model to deviate from
the behavior designed and expected by the developer without addressing the model’s fidelity in
the representation of the real-world system under test [7].
Until now, most stakeholders in the wind industry have been performing simulations using of-
fline models in EMT simulation software such as PSCAD and RMS software such as PowerFac-
tory, and PSSE. Despite their high fidelity, solving these models is a computationally intensive
and time-demanding process, especially when conducting system-level studies, that leads to
adopting simplifications and assumptions to accelerate simulation times. RMS tools simplify the
process by using root mean square (RMS) models to observe load flow. Simplifications adopted
in EMT tools could include the implementation of generic models issued by organizations such
as the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) and Western Electricity Coordinating
Council (WECC) instead of the proprietary models and the omission of modeling communica-
tion subsystems and their respective delays. Therefore, achieving faster simulation times by
compromising on fidelity [6].
HiL and other in-the-loop testing methods, usually referring to control hardware, such as the
power park or wind turbine controller, do not need to simplify the models. They represent it
more realistically with minimal modeling by incorporating the Device Under Test (DUT) as either
the actual hardware or by implementing its software version on specialized testing equipment.
Testing the control code of controllers constitutes the Software-in-the-Loop testing method that
is the main focus of this thesis and is highly applicable for testing controllers since their function-
alities can be easily compiled in a code format. Additionally, the SiL method allows for effortless
parameterization and easier upgrades throughout the life cycle of the project [6].

Motivation
Offline models have been extensively analyzed and implemented in the wind industry, while
online models in real-time simulators have advanced their share only in the past few years
[11]. Additionally, although comparing real-time and offline models may have already been thor-

of the wind industry. The novelty of the method raises concerns about comparing similar models

conducted. The paper considers a Doubly-Fed Induction Generator consisting of the electri-

2.1
Grid Strength – Short Circuit Ratio
The Short Circuit Ratio (SCR) is a representative indicator of the grid strength. By definition,
SCR is the ratio of the short-circuit apparent power in case of a three-phase to ground fault at a
location in the grid where a generator is connected over the power rating of the generator itself
[15], as shown in Equation (2.1) [16].
SCR = SCMV A
GMW
(2.1)
In Equation (2.1), the term SCMV A denotes the short-circuit apparent power at the PCC in the
existing network before the new generator is connected, and GMW denotes the rated megawatt
value of the new connected source. The SCR is used in quantifying the grid strength, and a
high SCR value denotes that the particular generator represents only a small power of the
entire grid. Hence, the problem at the location of this generator is incapable of affecting the grid
as a whole. Synchronous generators are designed to provide fault currents at 2 −4 pu [17],
which is not the case with the converters, which have strict over-current limits (less than 1.2 pu)
[18]. Consequently, the SCR is bound to decrease with more renewable energy sources using
converter technology added to the grid. A grid with a high SCR has more resistance to voltage
events and less chance of voltage instability. Hence, a grid with high SCR is seen as having
a stiffer voltage source. An SCR (>3) is said to represent a strong grid as it would be able to
provide the needed fault current during the case of a short circuit and will also have a larger
capability of active power transfer.
In present times, due to the increased presence of converter-based sources that are replacing
the synchronous generators, different modifications of the SCR are being used, which include
weighted SCR, effective SCR, and composite SCR to prevent the overestimation of grid strength
[19]. Also, newer methods could be used to calculate the grid strength in the presence of more
inverter-based resources (IBRs), such as the Grid Strength impedance metric that considers
the contribution of the converters to the voltage instability [19].

According to the CIGRE 671 document, an SCR of 5 is considered a strong grid at the point of
connection of the wind farm. This has been used for the models of this thesis, where the SCR
rating is assumed to be 5 for all the models to provide stable operation to the wind farm. Also,
since the aim is to compare similar models using two tools by running various steady-state and
dynamic operational cases, it is beneficial to have a strong grid at the point of connection.
2.2
Wind Turbine Generators
Wind power plants (WPPs) comprise numerous wind turbine generators (WTG), a collector sys-
tem, and substations connected to the grid through export cables. These components help in
capturing the wind energy and injecting this converted energy into the grid. WTGs have evolved
from simple machines that are directly connected to the grid to complex systems that demon-
strate high controllability and decoupling from grid events. There have been different types of
wind turbines, but the predominantly implemented one in modern WPPs is the Type 4 WTG
with its full-rated back-to-back converters that help achieve independent control over active
and reactive power injection [20].
The principle of WTGs is based on converting the kinetic energy of the wind to the mechan-
ical energy of the moving blades. The moving blades are coupled with the generator’s rotor,
thus converting mechanical energy into electrical energy. During variable wind conditions, the
wind turbine blades may need to adjust their position around the blade axis to optimize the cap-
ture of wind energy or to protect the WTG components from mechanical damage during windy
conditions. This feature is called pitch regulation. Depending upon the wind speed conditions,
the blades can be turned into the wind at lower speeds for optimal power generation or out
of the wind to reduce aerodynamic efficiency and prevent mechanical damage to the WTG.
This method achieves optimal power control and maintains the power output close to the rated
power of the generator. In the earlier WTGs, pitch regulation was not available, and therefore,
stall regulation used to be the counterpart method [20]. Stall regulation is achieved by shaping
the turbine blades accordingly to generate less aerodynamic force at high wind speeds, leading
to stalling and, hence, helping reduce the WTG’s torque [20].
As shown in Figure 2.1, the turbines are classified into four types. The Type 1 WTG, the simplest
and least expensive of the four, has the rotor connected through a gearbox to a Squirrel Cage
Induction Generator (SCIG) [20]. The stator of the SCIG is connected to a step-up transformer
that is then connected to the grid. This type of turbine is characterized as a fixed-speed turbine,
whose speed is fixed to the grid’s frequency. It generates power when the turbine rotates faster
than the grid’s frequency. The main drawback is the need for reactive power provision, which,
in turn, raises the need for reactive power compensation devices such as capacitor banks.
The Type 2 WTG is similar to Type 1 with the presence of an additional variable resistance. The
rotor resistance control is used to reduce power fluctuations and provide a level of controllability,
as the turbine needs to spin faster with the added resistance to produce the same power output.
Therefore, the rotor resistance control allows for speed control of the WTG. The reactive power
requirements of Type 2 WTGs are similar to those of Type 1.

Implementing a back-to-back converter allows for independent control of the required active
and reactive power, provided the demand lies within the PQ capabilities of the turbine. The con-
verters must be appropriately sized to handle the full active and reactive power requirements.
This means that special attention needs to be provided to the current rating of the converter, as
power electronics in these converters are susceptible to excessive currents. The Type 4 WTG
is the variation with the highest cost due to implementing a full-rated power converter.
In this thesis, a representation of the Type 4 WTG is modeled. The back-to-back converter
is distinguished between the grid-side converter and the machine-side converter. The names
of these converters indicate the side that each converter is connected to and controlling. The
machine-side converter is an AC-to-DC converter that converts the AC power produced by the
generator to DC. The produced power is then directed across the DC link to the DC-to-AC
grid-side converter that injects the required active power and reactive power into the grid. The
grid-side converter is tuned to regulate the DC bus voltage and AC voltage at the WTG terminal.
This independent control is achieved through per unitization and transformation of the current
and voltage measurements to another reference frame, namely the dq0-frame, that allows for
improved controllability. Through this conversion, the three rotating phasors that represent the
three phases can be represented by the stationary direct (D-axis) and quadrature (Q-axis) cur-
rent references. This allows for improved controllability since Proportional Integral controllers
can follow constant reference setpoints instead of oscillating ones at the grid frequency. Ad-
ditionally, the independent control of the active and reactive power is achieved by decoupling
the two axes. Finally, the priority of current control has to be set accordingly to support the grid
during transient conditions [22]. The topics of independent control and current priority are imple-
mented during modeling for this thesis, and the current controls are discussed in greater detail
in the relevant Section 3.1.4.
The machine-side controller is tuned to control the machine’s active power and AC voltage to
obtain the required ratings at the WTG terminals. During a fault condition, protection is required
to avoid overvoltages on the DC link capacitor. This protection is provided by the DC link chopper
resistance activated to dissipate overvoltages.

Average Value Models
From the previous analysis on Average-value models of GSC, it is evident that power electronics
are core components of modern electrical systems and thus need to be included in the models
for grid studies [24]. Detailed Switching Models can simulate the behavior of these components
with high fidelity, capturing high-frequency transients of power converters. However, switch-
ing models suffer from high computational time since they require fairly low time steps [25] to
capture the rapid switching operation of the switches that commonly comprise Insulated Gate
Bipolar Transistors (IGBT). Additionally, switching models are discontinuous due to the state of
the switches being either open or closed. The computational burden is greatly escalated when
performing system-level studies, and thus, compromises must be made between the system
scale and computational resources [24].
Average Value Models (AVM) are developed as a counterpart to switching models to overcome
the latter’s challenges without compromising fidelity. The primary objective of AVMs is to elim-
inate the discontinuities of switching components [26] and instead use algebraic equations to
represent the terminal characteristics of these components, namely current and voltage. These
equations average the output of the fast switching actions over the switching period, achiev-
ing faster simulation times since time steps can exceed 10µs [26]. The following Equation (2.3)
demonstrates the average-value calculation:

Proportional Integral (PI) Controllers
The PI controller is a variation of a wider family of controllers called Proportional-Integral-
Derivative (PID) controllers. The name of this family of controllers reflects the elements that
may be included within each implementation of the controller, depending on the needs of each
application. According to [30] and as shown in Figure 2.7, PI control is the method of feedback
control that uses PI controllers to manipulate a variable called process variable (y) to achieve
setpoint (r) tracking. For this purpose, the controller controls a variable called manipulated vari-
able (u), which is then fed as an input to the process.
Figure 2.7: Conventional feedback control system
An example from this thesis is that the setpoint r is an active power reference setpoint, the
process variable y is the active power output of the WPP, and the manipulated variable u is
the phase angle input to the voltage source for the initial simple models. In this example, the
process block is the WT model, and the applied controller only contains the Proportional (P)
and Integral (I) elements, as shown in Figure 2.8.

Each element serves an individual purpose. The combination of these elements leads to a vari-
ant of the controller. For this analysis, the Proportional element introduces an output that is
proportional to the error of the same time step, which can be termed as present error, while
the Integral element introduces an output proportional to the integral of the error up to the cur-
rent time step, thus the accumulated error [30]. The Integral element of the PI controller is an
integrator. Therefore, a selection has to be made among the integration methods. In this analy-
sis, the Trapezoidal integration method is considered the optimal choice based on the fact that

2.6
Power Park Controller (PPC)
A vital component within the WPP that utilizes PI controllers as a core structural component
is the Power Park Controller (PPC). Based on [32], Power Plant Controllers are centralized
control devices that allow Variable Renewable Energy Power Plants (VREPP) to satisfy grid
code requirements related to frequency and voltage control, both under dynamic and steady-
state conditions. According to [33], VREPP are power plants whose output is limited by natural
conditions and is associated with strong variability and uncertainty. Such power plants are wind
and solar parks, and conventional hydropower plants.
The PPC operates in a closed-loop control scheme where the wind farm outputs, as measured
at the PCC, are provided as inputs to the controller. Such measurements typically include active
and reactive power flow and voltage levels. As mentioned above, the PPC operates under both
steady-state and dynamic conditions and receives relative inputs for both cases. For steady-
state, the PPC receives reference setpoints dictated by the Transmission System Operator
(TSO) or by the Maximum Power Point Tracker (MPPT) of the park, and for dynamic operations,
it monitors the local grid frequency and voltage levels. According to [34], MPPT aims to maintain
the maximum possible power output while the wind is lower than the rated one, thus optimizing
efficiency. This is achieved by altering the rotating speed of the WT generator by responding to
CHAPTER 2. BACKGROUND
15
==PAGE:32==
wind speed changes to achieve an optimal Tip Speed Ratio (TSR). The tip speed ratio is given
by Equation (2.2)
The PPC usually consists of two branches; the first is for controlling the active power output, and
the second is for controlling the voltage output of the wind farm. In steady-state operations, the
first branch receives the required active power setpoint by the TSO or MPPT and the measured
active power output of the wind farm and tries to match the wind farm dispatch to the TSO
requirement. This is achieved by calculating the error between the aforementioned values and
trying to eradicate it by feeding the error to a Proportional Integral (PI) controller. For the second
branch, the operation is more complex since there are various control modes available that
require different inputs. The usual control modes are fixed voltage, fixed reactive power, and
fixed power factor [32]. The branch may operate under any one of these control modes in steady-
state. Therefore, depending upon the control mode, the input to the second branch may be a
voltage, power factor, or reactive power setpoint. The input at hand is then compared to the
respective measurement, and the error is fed to another PI controller. For the case of power
factor control, the power factor is first translated into a reactive power value and then compared
to the reactive power flow. The output of this branch is always a voltage setpoint.
Under dynamic conditions, the inputs to the two branches may vary, but the outputs remain ac-
tive power and voltage. Dynamic conditions mainly refer to grid events, such as short circuits,
during which the wind farm may be required by the grid code to remain connected and provide
ancillary services such as frequency and voltage support. According to [35], the change in fre-
quency and voltage output due to changes in active power and reactive power, respectively,
can be interpreted as the direct coupling of active power with frequency and reactive power
with voltage in large power systems. Therefore, it is evident that frequency is monitored and
fed into the first branch. The second branch is naturally configured for voltage support under
dynamic conditions. There are limits set by each TSO within which no controlling action takes
place for either frequency or voltage fluctuations. This deadband is enforced to avoid constant
unnecessary reactions that may stress excessively the equipment.
The response of the PPC for frequency support is achieved through the use of droop control.
Based on [36], droop is a curve trajectory that a control function must follow. In the context of
frequency response, droop is the change in active power as a function of the grid frequency
and is expressed as a percentage of the nominal output of the wind farm, as shown in Equation
(2.8). For voltage level support at the PCC, a droop scheme may be implemented, and due
to the aforementioned coupling, the change of reactive power is a function of voltage levels.
However, due to the existing voltage monitoring functionalities of the second branch and the
provision for a deadband, it is not mandatory to implement a droop scheme. Additionally, [37]
mentions that in Denmark, utility-scale renewable generation is operated under a reactive power
control scheme.

2.7.1
Software-in-the-Loop (SiL)
Based on [38], the black-box model is a method to test software when a system’s internal struc-
ture, design, and processes are not disclosed to the tester. Only the inputs and outputs are
known, and the system boundaries are well-defined. The method provides high accuracy and
low computational cost and is widely implemented to protect Intellectual Property (IP). The latter
is especially crucial in the scope of electrical component modeling and testing since vendors
need to protect their IP while providing models to their clients. Black-box models replicate the
behavior of the component at hand without disclosing any sensitive proprietary information.
The SiL testing method aims to incorporate the vendor control code in the real-time simulations
to test and tune the control algorithm without compromising the vendor’s proprietary informa-
tion [39]. In this thesis, the virtual model comprises the electrical model in escalating levels of
complexity. The control code represents the Power Park Controller (PPC), receiving reference
setpoints and measurements from the PCC as inputs, executing its internal control algorithm,
and finally sending control signals to the electrical model running in RSCAD. This control loop
topology is characterized as a closed loop [39].
Numerous advantages are introduced by the SiL testing method as it enables faster verification
processes, earlier error detection, and mitigation, reduced hardware costs compared with HiL
since only the controller code is tested, and easier programming. The latter is due to the easier
controller code generation that can be performed either through software such as Simulink or
through the direct application of the vendor-generated code in the Firmware generation process.
Finally, since SiL heavily relies on software, scalability is enabled, allowing the end-user to scale
the virtual model from a simple wind turbine to a full-scale wind farm [39].
Along with advantages, the SiL testing method introduces some new challenges and limita-

==PAGE:34==
tions. Since the nature of SiL is to test the software interpretation of controllers, real-world as-
pects such as physical connections, component failure, and hardware capabilities cannot be
accurately considered. Additionally, high model fidelity can be difficult to achieve since it may
compromise computational efficiency by drastically increasing the simulation time. Therefore,
reducing fidelity for efficient simulations may lead to models that do not fully represent the actual
component. Then, the real-time environment imposes the challenge of synchronization between
the virtual model and the control software tested with the SiL logic. The difficulty lies within the
validation of proper control logic in the same time step as the virtual model [39].
Adding to the challenges suggested by the literature, there are some further challenges and
limitations derived from this thesis. First, there is a lack of organized documentation compared to

Another aspect to consider is that, although many processes can be accelerated, the creation

2.7.2
Real-Time Digital Simulator (RTDS)
The Real-Time Digital Simulator (RTDS) is the real-time platform for the SiL testing method.
Each unit of RTDS hardware is referred to as a Novacor 2.0 chassis and is designed to per-
form real-time EMT simulations under a real-time clock. This indicates that the time duration
of a simulated event on RTDS is equal to that of the potentially real event. On the other hand,
non-real-time (offline) simulators simulate the same event faster or slower, depending on the
provided time step and the complexity of the model. In RTDS, all calculations are performed
and completed under a discrete and constant time step.
The solution process, which is completed within each time step, comprises transforming the
electrical model into an equivalent network of branches that consists of only current sources
and resistors. The solver formulates the conductance matrix and computes the current values
for each branch for the specific time step using the historical data or initial conditions. Then, it
proceeds to derive the voltage values for each node for the same time-step [40]. EMT simulation
tools widely use this method to solve the electric system equations for each time step faster than
solving differential equations. It is based on the Dommel algorithm, named after Hermann W
Dommel, who suggested that an equivalent resistor and current source can represent any circuit
element. The case of an inductor is considered to provide an example of Dommel’s theorem
[41].

Simulink
Simulink is a graphical block diagram modeling tool developed by The MathWorks Inc. that

translate a system developed in Simulink into an equivalent C-Code. The C-Code can then
be implemented in RSCAD either through the CBuilder, a tool provided in RSCAD to import

black-box model method that entails compiling the model into a firmware file and installing it on
an external hardware component called Giga-Transceiver System-on-a-Chip (GTSOC) [43].
2.7.5
Giga-Transceiver System-on-a-Chip (GTSOC)
GTSOC is a rack-mountable device that combines an FPGA board and a Multi-Processor System-
on-Chip. It can run in parallel to the RTDS simulation hardware, receive signals from the latter,
and transmit the output of the black-box model back to the RTDS platform through fiber optic ca-
bles. The main application of GTSOC is the integration of black-box controllers into a real-time
simulation environment while protecting the manufacturer’s intellectual property. [43].
To fulfill this purpose, GTSOC features a quad-core 1.2GHz ARM processor, each core capa-
ble of running up to five instances of the same or different black-box models. Each instance is
represented by a static library file (.a), while for each core, a single firmware file (.mcs) is gen-
erated, as thoroughly described later in Section 3.1.8. Therefore, one GTSOC unit can house
up to 20 different instances of black-box models. Each instance may have up to 255 inputs and
255 outputs, but overall, each core can handle up to 320 inputs and an equal number of out-
puts throughout the instances. Finally, GTSOC supports up to 1024 configurable parameters
per instance. [44]

3.1.2
Tuning of PI Controllers
The characteristics of PI controllers directly affect the dynamic response of the controlled mod-
els. Therefore, ensuring proper tuning of PI controllers to achieve the required response profiles
is essential. Tuning the PI controller implies setting the necessary proportional and integral gain
values to acquire the behavior needed in terms of overshooting, settling time, and aggressive-
ness of the corrective action. There are numerous tuning methods, and the selected one for
this analysis is the Ziegler-Nichols method for its simplicity since the aforementioned gains are
derived through only the critical frequency and the corresponding gain that causes the output
to reach the critical frequency [47].
The Ziegler-Nichols method is applied by initially setting both gains to 0 and then gradually
increasing the Proportional gain until the output of the PI controller oscillates consistently with
a steady frequency and amplitude[48]. At the first value of P that the output starts oscillating,
the frequency of the sustained oscillations is the critical frequency wc, the period is the critical
period Tc, and the Proportional gain value is called critical gain Kc. After that, the Proportional
Kp and Integral Ki gains are calculated through the formulas below:
Kp = 0.45 · Kc
Ki = 0.83 · Tc
(3.1)
In many cases, the Ziegler-Nichols tuning method may not produce the optimal outcome, leading
to manual fine-tuning to achieve the desired response [48]. The technique is known for its harsh
reaction that usually leads to overshoots. However, this profile is not optimal for WPP since
these overshoots and oscillations may be translated into mechanical stress on the equipment,
such as the WT generators. Therefore, in many cases of this analysis, fine-tuning is required
to acquire a smoother response, limit the overshoot, and achieve a new steady state in an
acceptable time. This tuning uses the Ziegler-Nichols suggested gains as a basis to perform
repetitive simulations and readjust the gains accordingly.

3.1.3
Anti-windup Mechanisms
Based on [49], physical systems may be subject to actuator saturation due to the physical limits
of the actuator. The latter is the system that receives the control signal from the PI controller
and changes its output to implement the desired control. In this case, the actuators are the WTG
that comprise the WPP. Each WTG receives the control signal from the PPC in terms of active
power and voltage and changes its dispatch to satisfy the reference setpoint. However, WTG
is subject to physical limitations in both active power and voltage operational ranges. In this
analysis, the active power limits are 0 and 1.2 p.u. to consider the possibility of modern turbines
for short-term exceeding of the nominal capacity and 0.9-1.1 pu of voltage, conforming with grid
codes.
Therefore, the possibility exists that the actuator limits are reached during controlling activity, a
phenomenon called actuator saturation. When this phenomenon occurs, the actuator remains
at its limits in the effort to correct the error. This situation leads to excessive error accumulation
in the integral part of the PI controller, a phenomenon called windup. The results of integrator
windup are slower response times and overshoots. To unwind naturally, the error needs to have
a negative sign for a long time, meaning that the reference setpoint r has to be less than the
process variable y in Figure 2.7.
To overcome the windup phenomenon, the back-calculation anti-windup method may be imple-
mented. This method utilizes an extra feedback path where the difference between the manip-
ulated variable u and the process variable y is calculated and multiplied with a gain equal to 1
Tt .
Tt is the time constant that dictates the integrator unwind. Then, the error is passed to the inte-
gral part of the PI controller. When the actuator is not saturated, this error equals zero, and the
anti-windup path is inactive [49]. Extending Figure 2.8, the components of the back-calculation
anti-windup mechanism are distinguished in Figure 3.1.

Fault Synchronization Approach
In the scope of comparing offline to real-time simulation tools that also implement the SiL testing
technology, a significant observable difference is the arbitrary start of the instantaneous signals
in the latter. For every iteration of a PSCAD simulation, the user can configure the initial values
of the instantaneous currents and voltages and be assured that the model adheres to these
values. However, in RSCAD, due to the real-time nature of the simulations, the initial points of
these values are unknown to the user, and hence, a signal synchronization logic needs to be
developed. This logic is essential in analyses that require comparing the instantaneous signals
instead of RMS ones, such as fault analyses.
RSCAD Fault Synchronization
Beginning with RSCAD, since it introduces the issue, a signal needs to be identified upon which
the synchronization logic is developed. This thesis selects Phase A of the three-phase volt-
age measurement at the PCC, which is the HV terminals of the transformer, before the grid
impedance. Initially, this waveform is compared to zero in a comparator component that raises
the flag (boolean 1) the moment the waveform performs a zero-crossing to the positive side. An
Edge Detector detects this positive transition of the boolean variable and is configured to also
a TRUE output. This output is then directed to an AND Gate and comprises one of the latter’s
two inputs. The other input is generated through a Push Button component that, when pushed
in the RSCAD RUNTIME, gives a TRUE output. Its signal is then led to a Pulse Generator that
generates a TRUE pulse for one cycle (i.e., 0.02sec) that is directed to the AND Gate. The
Pulse Generator is configured to be triggered when the input is rising. The one-cycle output is
selected to ensure that TRUE values can coincide for both inputs to the logic gate, as shown in
Figure 3.13a. Otherwise, the user may press the button whenever the waveform is in the neg-
ative region. This case, without the application of the one-cycle signal, is presented in Figure
3.13b where at no point are both inputs to the AND gate TRUE.

PSCAD Fault Synchronization
In PSCAD, the fault synchronization logic is identical to that of RSCAD, with the application of
configurable blocks that perform individual actions sequentially. Once again, the fault is applied
by multiplying the voltage amplitude of the grid-side voltage sources by zero during the fault
and through a signal selector. The sequence begins by removing the fault to avoid residual
configurations from previous simulations. Then, a master switch is placed that enables or dis-
ables the entire fault sequence. If enabled, the sequence waits for a user-defined time Tstart
that represents the point during the simulation duration at which the event occurs. After waiting
for that time, the sequence expects the Phase A waveform of the three-phase voltage at the
PCC to perform a zero-crossing to the positive side and then waits for the waveform to reach
the user-defined POW. The POW is called FDelay in PSCAD and is calculated identically to
RSCAD. Upon reaching the designated POW, the fault is applied by switching the signal se-
lector to the zero-multiplied for a user-defined duration TPROT . It is then cleared by resetting
the signal selector. Due to the default naming of the sequential block components, the signal
selector is considered as a breaker (”BRK1”) with the open state reflecting the multiplication
with 1 and the closed state reflecting the multiplication with 0.

the PLL. The RSCAD equivalent configuration is that the Q-axis is lagging the D-axis, and the
transformation angle is equal to only Theta.
With the PLL ABC-to-DQO component configured as such, the external to the PLL ABC-to-DQO
blocks in PSCAD are configured so that the Q axis is lagging, and the transformation angle is
equal to the Theta output of the PLL. Then, the RSCAD equivalent configuration has to be that
Q is leading and the Theta remains the same.
Finally, another observation while using the conversion tool is that in the generated ’RTFX’ file,
discontinuities tend to appear in the circuits even though they do not exist in the PSCAD file.
Overall, this conversion tool makes it easier for the user to replicate an equivalent PSCAD model
in RSCAD. More drawbacks than the ones mentioned above may exist, but depending upon the
complexity of the converted model, it provides a good starting point for RSCAD to build a similar
model to PSCAD.
3.1.7
Power Park Controller (PPC)
The following section presents the development of an initial simplified PPC model and then the
adaptation of the generic WECC PPC model to the needs of this thesis.
Simplified PPC Model
Following the same approach as the electrical model, the PPC is first modeled as a simplified
system with reduced functionalities, basic internal components, and control logic. The purpose
of simplifying the PPC model lies in understanding the fundamentals of the PPC, which are mea-
suring the error between the reference and measured values and applying corrective actions
to eliminate the error. Through developing the simple model, several subjects are addressed,
such as tuning the PI controllers using the Ziegler-Nichols method and then fine-tuning to ac-
quire the necessary behavior, implementing anti-windup techniques to avoid error accumulation
at the integrator of the PI controller, and applying saturation limits. The latter applies to most
PI controllers throughout this thesis and confines the output of the PI controller according to
the voltage and active power capabilities of the controlled component, which is the WPP. For
the simple models, only the steady-state operation is tested; thus, no droop characteristics and
frequency monitoring are introduced.
The simple model retains the structure of two branches described in Section 2.6. The first branch
consists of one PI controller receiving the error between the reference and measured active
power as input, as shown in Figure 3.22. The output of the PI controller is limited from 0 to
1.2pu to represent the capability of exceeding the nominal active power output of the WPP for
a limited time. For this PI controller, the analysis indicates that there is no need for anti-windup
mechanisms since the controller could alter its setpoint from 0 to 1.2pu successfully without
major overshoots or delays.

responsible for controlling the voltage output of the WPP and thus, depending on the control

suggestion in the Simulink Help. The comparison with PSCAD was successful, concluding that
the PSCAD PI controller component has some embedded anti-windup features.
WECC Power Park Controller Model
The Power Park Controller model developed by the Western Electricity Coordinating Council
(WECC) is based on the same concept as the simplified PPC one. It comprises two branches
for independent active and reactive power flow at the Point of Common Coupling (PCC), which
issues commands to the generating unit of the WPP. The council has issued various versions
over the years that demonstrate elevated capabilities. For the purposes of this thesis, the basic
version REPC_A is implemented since later versions incorporate controls that are more suitable
for hybrid power plants (REPC_B) and reactive power compensation devices (REPC_C) [53].
Based on [21], REPC_A allows for active power and asymmetrical droop-based primary fre-
quency response and either reactive power control or voltage control, all applicable at the PCC.
44
CHAPTER 3. METHODOLOGY
==PAGE:61==
For the voltage control option, there is a series of options whose deployment depends on some
parameter configurations, and these options are: with/without dead-band and with/without droop
or current compensation. When enabled, the dead-band option allows for a range of error values
within which the PI controller does not react to avoid constant controlling actions. This analysis
is deemed outside the scope of the thesis, and therefore, this feature is disabled. Then, the cur-
rent compensation approach for voltage control refers to countering voltage deviations caused
by current flow instabilities. This method would require system impedance inputs that may in-
crease the analysis complexity and thus are omitted. Finally, as for the droop compensation,
the measured reactive power flow at the PCC is multiplied with the reactive current compensa-
tion gain (Kc) and then summed with the measured voltage at the PCC before being compared
with the reference voltage value. Based on [54], the droop feature enhances reactive power
stability without compromising the voltage control. However, determining the Kc value requires
a thorough analysis of the model dynamics, which steers away from the scope of the thesis.
Moreover, later versions of the WECC PPC, namely REPC_C, altered the reactive power mea-
surement for the droop feature, deeming it inapplicable in such generic models applied in this
thesis [21]. To conclude, the selected options in this thesis are without deadband and without
either droop or current compensation.
Delving into a more structural analysis of the active power branch, a summation node is formu-
lated where the user-determined active power reference setpoint (Plant_ref) and the active
power setpoint determined by the frequency droop (Pref_fq) are summed. Then, the active
power measurement at the PCC (Pbranch) is subtracted to generate the error input to the PI
controller. The Plant_ref is provided in pu. The Pbranch measurement is in MW and passes
through a resettable filter with a time constant Tp. This filter represents the equipment lag in
delivering the power measurement. After the filter, the measured Pbranch is transformed into pu
before reaching the calculation node. As for the frequency droop, the measured frequency Freq
is first per-unitized before being compared to the nominal frequency and then passed through a
deadband that eliminates unnecessary reactions to small deviations. The deadband limits are
given by fdbd1 and fdbd2. Then, a droop logic is created where the frequency error is multi-
plied either with the downside droop Ddn for an over-frequency event or with the upside droop
Dup for an under-frequency event. The result of the droop logic is a positive value in case of
an under-frequency that is then added to the summation node and increases the active power
dispatch or a negative value in case of an over-frequency that ultimately decreases the active
power dispatch.
Figure 3.24: WECC PPC Active Power Branch [21]
After the summation block, the generated error is directed to a saturation block that limits the
error between femax and femin before reaching the PI controller. In the WECC PPC version in

this thesis, the PI controller is represented by its core components, as shown in Figure 2.8, to
achieve improved controllability of the resettable integrator. The output of the PI controller is then
saturated between Pmax and Pmin, which are the upper and lower available power references,
respectively. The exact limits are applied to the PI controller integrator. Finally, a resettable filter
with the time constant Tg is placed before the PPC active power branch output to replicate the
communication delay between the PPC and WTG [21].
Figure 3.25: WECC PPC V/Q Branch [21]
As for the implemented voltage branch demonstrated in Figure 3.25, for the voltage control
option, the measured RMS voltage in pu is directed to a resettable filter with the time constant
Tfltr that replicates dynamics of the measuring equipment. Then, the measured voltage is led to
a summation block where it is subtracted from the user-defined reference voltage in pu. For the
reactive power control option, the reactive power measurement at the PCC in MVAr is passed
through a resettable filter with the same time constant Tfltr as in the voltage control option. Then,
the measured value is subtracted from the user-defined reactive power reference Qref again in
MVAr. The error value is then per-unitized. At this point, a signal selector component chooses
between the voltage or reactive power error values, depending on the variable RefFlag. If
RefFlag = 0, then the reactive power error is directed to the PI, while if RefFlag = 1, then the
voltage error is passed.
The selected error value is directly led to a saturation block with limits between emin and emax
since the deadband block is disabled as previously mentioned. Then, the detailed model of the
PI controller is placed, and its output is saturated. The saturation block limits are equal to the
PI controller integrator ones and given by the variables Qmin and Qmax. Then, a resettable lag
pole replicates the communication delay between the PPC and WTG [21].
The WECC PPC model introduces two further capabilities compared to the simplified PPC
models: the freeze and initialization features. The freeze logic is enabled either during a Fault-
Ride-Through (FRT) event, which is detected when the measured voltage is lower than the
user-defined parameter Vfrz, or during the model initialization period, but with different output
values [21]. In case of an FRT event, the freeze feature remains enabled for a user-defined
time through the PostFRTV oltageFreezeTime and PostFRTPowerFreezeTime variables.
The boolean outputs of the FRT logic, namely FrzV and FrzP, are applied to the reset in-
put of the respective PI controller integrators.
As for the values that the two integrators reset to, namely V _PI_reset_value and P_PI_reset_value,
they are equal to the user-defined initial voltage and power reference values of the V ref_WT_init
and Pref_WT_init variables respectively. In this thesis, the selected approach dictates that the
initial values equal the initial V ref and Plant_ref values, respectively. The analysis converged

to this selection because it was observed that excessive initialization times were required when
the V ref_WT_init and Pref_WT_init received other lower values. This behavior was caused
by the PI controllers struggling to eliminate large initial errors.
Upon initialization completion after the user-defined duration in hppp_time, the V _PI_reset_value
is calculated as the difference between the last sample value of the PI controller output of the

3.1.8
Firmware Generation
To conclude with the PPC model analysis, it is necessary to address the model migration from
PSCAD to RSCAD. Initially developed as an open model in PSCAD, the PPC model is applied
in RSCAD as a black-box model, installed on GTSOC. This configuration enables the Software-
in-the-Loop testing method as described in Section 2.7.1, which is the core of the comparative
analysis of this thesis. As a black-box model, the PPC receives measurement signals and pro-
vides reference setpoints to the wind farm to complete the control loop. Additionally, a set of
parameters is selected as configurable during simulations to replicate the real world, where the
vendor allows some configurability for their customers.
A firmware file needs to be generated before installing the PPC model on GTSOC. The format
of the final file is Memory Configuration File (.mcs), and to generate that, a series of interim files
must be generated beforehand. The model of the PPC is first developed in MATLAB Simulink
and placed inside a Subsystem block that specifies the inputs and outputs of the system. Once
transferred, the Simulink model is tested in an open loop configuration to verify the identical
behavior to the PSCAD model. Upon initial verification, the Embedded Coder of Simulink is uti-
lized to generate the C-Code that describes the system’s internal processes. A key requirement
is that the chosen solver must be discrete and function with a fixed time-step to align with the
Novacor specifications [42].
While black-box models are genuinely applied to withhold information from third parties, some
parameters may need to be configurable to tune and scale the model according to the client’s
requirements. A typical example is to scale the WTG model to the WPP scale for system-level
studies. In Simulink, these parameters are declared as Simulink parameter arguments and their
Storage Class as MultiInstance to achieve tunability. By applying this configuration, the param-
eters are not explicitly represented by their values in the generated C-Code but are defined as
variables whose value is read through external text files in RSCAD.
Upon generating the C-Code, the generated C-Source files are compiled in the RTDS Blackbox
Builder tool to generate a static library file of .a format. RTDS Blackbox Builder is a front-end GUI
for the Xilinx Vitis software development kit (SDK) that helps compile the firmware generation
file. Finally, the static library file, the C-Header files generated by the Simulink Embedded Coder,
and a wrapper code are utilized to create the final firmware file installed on GTSOC. Upon
generating this firmware file, it can be installed through the designated interface provided by
RSCAD, as described in 2.7.3.
The wrapper code maps the system’s inputs, outputs, and tunable parameters. It serves as
an interface between the data from the RTDS simulation and the controller code executed on
GTSOC. For the purposes of this thesis, the wrapper code template provided by RTDS for
Simulink is modified to incorporate the mapping of tunable parameters in the controller code
to the user-defined values in the text file in RSCAD. Pointers need to be developed using the

3.2.1
Combination of RSCAD and GTSOC
The cooperation of RSCAD-Novacor and GTSOC is essential to perform successful SiL simu-
lations. The operation of these two tools is dictated by individual time steps that are configured
in the RSCAD Case Settings for Novacor and in the DOTA block under the dotadt variable for
GTSOC. Since GTSOC contains the controller code generated in Simulink, its time step needs
to be the same as the time step pre-defined in Simulink. Depending upon the selection of these
time steps, the system may operate in synchronous or asynchronous mode. This thesis utilizes
the synchronous mode, meaning that the GTSOC time step is an integer multiple of the Novacor
time step.
Additionally, the GTSOC time step selection needs to consider the execution time required for
the controller code in GTSOC. The DOTA block in RSCAD can calculate this execution time
by configuring the StepExeTiming setting within the block. In this thesis, the execution time is
calculated at 1.27µs, and thus, the two time steps are equally configured at 10µs.
The DOTA Block in RSCAD handles the data exchange. The synchronous operation mode of
GTSOC and Novacor indicates that the GTSOC time step is triggered as soon as data arrive
from Novacor, and upon completion of the GTSOC operations within its time step, the data
are sent to Novacor and stored until the next Novacor time step begins. At the beginning of
each Novacor time step, the DOTA Block in RSCAD delivers whatever values are stored in the
memory from GTSOC at that time. As previously mentioned, the controller code execution time
(i.e., 1.27µs) is less than the GTSOC time step (i.e., 10µs). Therefore, the data that the DOTA
Block provides are always updated since the GTSOC has fully executed its controller code. This
procedure is demonstrated in Figure 3.29, and evidently, a natural one-step delay is introduced
as Novacor receives the result from GTSOC in the next time-step.

3.2.2
Automation Scripts
The development, execution, and analysis of simulations involve repetitive tasks, especially
when a model has to be examined under multiple scenarios. This requires configuring the sim-
ulation tool, manually adjusting model settings, component parameters, and network configu-
rations between scenarios, and capturing and analyzing the results [45]. Figure 3.30 demon-
strates a flowchart of the iterative configuration process. The manual labor involved significantly
increases simulation times, as extensive checklists must be completed, and mistakes are dif-
ficult to identify. As a result, the process becomes time-consuming and may distract from the
main objective of analyzing the results.
Figure 3.30: Scenario-based simulation configuration flowchart [45]
Automation scripts are developed to improve the efficiency of simulations. These scripts can
perform tasks such as loading models in the simulation tools, configuring model settings and pa-
rameters, changing signals during the simulation to capture the dynamic behavior of the model,
and exporting the results to Comma-Separated Values (CSV) files for further analysis in exter-
nal data visualization software. Consequently, replacing the manual processes with automation
scripts achieves efficient simulations by reducing the preparation time and ensuring proper set-
tings and configurations for each scenario.
PSCAD
The PSCAD automation scripts implement the package named ’mhi’ which is used for interac-
tion with the PSCAD tool and has to be installed locally on the computer executing the automa-

3.3
Result Extraction
After successfully simulating using the offline and real-time tools, the results are stored and
compared using a MATLAB script. This section focuses on the processes involved in configuring
the plots in RSCAD before extracting the data in external files and the type of analysis performed
to compare the results of the two tools.
3.3.1
Plot Capturing
Since RSCAD is a real-time simulation tool, the challenge of capturing the plots at the proper
time is introduced. That is because the duration of the plot is not identical to the duration of the
simulation, as is in offline environments, since the simulation is constantly running under a real
clock. However, RSCAD provides some settings that enable the creation of a time window to
capture an event at the desired time.
This option is called ”Plot Pre-trigger” and is located in the RSCAD Case Settings menu. This
setting receives a percentage value as input that indicates the percentage within the plot window
at which the event should be illustrated. For example, suppose the RSCAD plotted window has
a duration of 10s and a ”Plot Pre-trigger” value equal to 50%. The event (indicated by the vertical
orange line) will be depicted at the 5th second of the plot. This approach is also illustrated in
Figure 3.32.
Figure 3.32: Plot capturing example
A plot update is triggered by modifying a slider’s value or pressing a button or switch in the
RSCAD Runtime. Additionally, it can be triggered at the user’s discretion by pressing the Plot
update button in RSCAD. As mentioned in Section 3.2.3, the plot duration is set at 10s. This
indicates only that the plots depict data of that duration. However, the simulations in both PSCAD
and RSCAD may be performed longer to allow for the model initialization and stabilization before
the event occurrence. The initialization time required for both tools is different. This can be
understood from the Section 2.7.1. This is mainly implemented in the final combination of the
WTG AVM and the WECC PPC since the initialization procedure takes some seconds to reach
an initial steady state.
In PSCAD, the settings are more straightforward. The simulation duration is always equal to
the plot duration. However, this leads to acquiring a heavy data load, including the initialization
phase. For example, to comply with the RSCAD plot window of 10s upon initialization, it is
possible that the PSCAD simulation duration is set to 20s. The extracted CSV files are then
cleared of the initial data up to the point when synchronization with the RSCAD plots is achieved,
and the time stamps are reassigned so that the first sample is at t = 0.

3.3.2
Error Analysis
Steady-state analysis is performed after obtaining the plots from the tools with equal plot times.
All the events are simulated for 10 seconds to achieve a new steady state towards the end of
the simulation and after the transients introduced by the simulated event have settled. A new
steady state is easily achieved for the simplified models since the PI controllers are tuned for
faster response and settling time. In the case of the AVM model, achieving steady-state values
towards the end of the simulation time of 10s was challenging due to the slower and more
realistic response of the WECC PPC model. Also, acquiring plots with a larger duration than
10s in RSCAD was challenging due to the automation script facing time-out errors.
Nevertheless, it can be observed in the plots and confirmed in the simulation tools that the sig-
nals are converging toward a new steady state. Hence, to perform the steady-state comparison
of the results from the two tools, the values at the 9th second of each graph are compared for ev-
ery simulation. The absolute error method is deployed as given in Equation (3.19) to determine
the deviations between the tools. For the transient state, the analysis is performed by visual
observation and commenting on the dynamics of the tools when an event occurs.

Results and Discussions
This chapter thoroughly discusses the results acquired through the simulations of the scenarios
described in Table 3.7 of Section 3.2.3 with the ultimate goal of comparing the offline and real-
time-with-SiL testing methods. The chapter is split into five sections that distinguish the focus
areas of the comparative analysis. It begins by analyzing the acquired results from the simplified
models and then proceeds with investigating the effects introduced by the SiL testing method.
Then, the chapter continues with reevaluating the initially selected time step to scrutinize the
impact of the time step selection. Upon selecting a new time step, the simulations and compara-
tive analyses continue with the deployment of the WTG AVM and the WECC PPC models. The
chapter concludes by analyzing a three-phase to-ground fault applied in both tools and clarifies
why the simplified models are implemented in this scenario instead.
The discussion of each scenario comprises an initial statement of the purpose and configuration
of the scenario, followed by analyses and observations of the electrical behavior and a focus on
comparing the plots between PSCAD and RSCAD. Then, a presentation of the crucial absolute
error values in the new steady-states is developed with references to analytical tables in the
Appendix, which include further values. Each scenario analysis concludes with a dedicated Key
Takeaways paragraph that allows the reader to conceive the main outcome of each scenario.
These key findings of some similar sections may be aggregated into one. Finally, the findings
of significant sections may be grouped into a larger conclusive paragraph to convey the results
of a specific type of analysis.
For improved clarity over the plot analysis, it should be noted that the sending-end measure-
ments follow the generator convention, and the receiving-end measurements follow the load
convention. The generator convention dictates that a positive active power value implies pro-
duction, while a negative one means active power consumption. A positive reactive power value
indicates the production of reactive power, thus an overexcited generator, while a negative
value indicates the consumption of reactive power, thus an under-excited generator. On the
other hand, the load convention dictates that a positive active power indicates consumption,
while a negative value suggests active power production. Finally, a positive reactive power
value indicates consumption and thus an under-excited grid, while a negative value indicates
the production of reactive power and thus an overexcited grid.

4.2.4
Conclusions on Open vs. Closed Model Comparisons
It is understood that the GTSOC can run in parallel to the RTDS simulation. The communica-
tion latency between NovaCor and GTSOC is given as 20ns per signal plus approximately 1µs
overhead. The simulation time-step, until this scenario, is 50µs for both GTSOC and NovaCor,
and the required time by GTSOC to solve the control code per signal is approximately 1.3µs. At
every 50µs, the NovaCor communicates with GTSOC, which carries the PPC controller code
in this case. The NovaCor sends its signals as per the design, and GTSOC solves its internal
control code and sends the results back to NovaCor before the start of the next cycle. This
ensures that the code on GTSOC can be executed and meet real-time requirements since the
total execution time of GTSOC, including the communication latency, is less than the time-step
of 50µs.

4.5
Fault Analysis with Simplified Models
Initially, a fault analysis was attempted using the WTG average-value model integrated with the
WECC PPC to understand the behavior of the different signals during fault conditions. It was
observed that there was a mismatch between the PSCAD and RSCAD results due to the imple-
mented reactive current injection and priority logic. Therefore, this attempted scenario is shown
in the Appendix 6.2. The issue was mainly attributed to the inability of the simple logic for reac-
tive current injection to provide a realistic response during faulty conditions. Additionally, in the
developed logic for current priority, some components could not be transferred identically from
PSCAD to RSCAD, namely a look-up table with embedded interpolation features in PSCAD.
Therefore, due to time constraints, it is preferred to study the behavior under fault conditions
using three single-phase voltage sources integrated with the simplified PPC. This selection in-
dicates no complexities were involved in the current control, and this scenario could be used
to observe the differences in a simplified case. Despite returning to simplified model implemen-
tation, Scenario Sc_19 generates a particularly interesting finding regarding the RMS voltage
calculation methods.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion and Future work
The increasing share of wind energy in the power grid requires high-fidelity simulation models
to accurately represent electrical systems and controls. Achieving this requires faster simula-
tions without compromising model fidelity. This thesis addresses this challenge by conducting a
comparative analysis of offline (PSCAD) and real-time (RSCAD) simulation tools, with the latter
employing the Software-in-the-Loop (SiL) tool.
SiL offers advantages such as faster verification processes, earlier error detection, scalability,
and the ability to develop IP-protected models. This tool can aid vendor interoperability, as dis-
cussed in Section 2.7.1. The thesis deals with a comparative analysis of two tools by modeling
similar Wind Turbine Generator (WTG) models integrated with a Power Park Controller (PPC).
For the latter, a comprehensive framework has been developed in this thesis to facilitate the
integration of SiL with the RTS tool.
Different models of WTG and PPC have been developed for both tools to thoroughly analyze
various components in the tools at hand - PSCAD and RSCAD. These models have been simu-
lated for different events, as formulated in Section 3.2.3, to observe possible differences during
the transient and steady states.
The thesis has found that both tools employ the classic Dommel algorithm [41] for solving the
electrical circuits. However, certain variations in PSCAD algorithms support interpolation func-
tions, along with the ’Chatter Removal Algorithm’ (which is enabled by default). These differ-
ences result in a smoother output with fewer oscillations. This information is detailed in Section
2.7.6.
During the modeling phase, differences were observed in equivalent components of PSCAD
and RSCAD. The provided PLL components were initially implemented in PSCAD and RSCAD,
but soon, their behavior was understood to differ. This led to the development of an open PLL
where it was found that the ABC-to-DQO transformation blocks were not giving similar results.
Upon investigation, it was found that the transformation equations in PSCAD and RSCAD for
these simple blocks are different, thus leading to different outputs for the same inputs. The
transformation equations have been mentioned in Section 2.4. Further, challenges were faced
to achieve smooth signals from the PLL. Eventually, this was achieved by modeling the sawtooth
generator appropriately in the two tools, as described in Section 3.1.4.
The simulations in the two tools revealed that the PI controllers in both tools had similar re-
sponses for identical Proportional and Integral gain values. They also revealed that the RMS
values taken from the multimeter component in PSCAD have smoothened output. Better match-
ing was achieved when PSCAD’s RMS values were calculated from instantaneous voltage val-
ues using MATLAB.
There are some numerical differences between the two tools, as some steady-state errors are
observed for quantities not actively controlled by the PPC. This error was decreased by changing
the time-step from 50µs to 10µs. The change in time-step led to a more significant change
in the RSCAD values than in the PSCAD values, which can be understood by reading the
characteristics of PSCAD in Section 2.7.6.
==PAGE:106==
Further, RSCAD models were compared to check for any effects introduced by modeling using
the SiL methodology. A significant observation in cases of active power step changes, where
the PI controller had a faster response, was a one-time-step delay with the model integrated
with SiL lagging. This is expected and can be understood by reading Section 3.2.1 about the
interaction between GTSOC and Novacor.
Simulation with the current controls also provided successful matching results between the two
tools. It was found that similar results are obtained upon the proper configuration of the ABC-
to-DQO transformation blocks.
Overall, the results matched in the two tools — PSCAD and RSCAD — with minor deviations
in the transient state and steady state for quantities that were not controlled. Therefore, as a
general conclusion, the real-time Software-in-the-Loop testing method demonstrates promising
results and can be on track to become a well-established testing and verification approach
within the wind industry, complementing the established EMT tool (PSCAD) and accelerating
the verification processes. To achieve this milestone, a coordinated effort of academia and
the industry is necessary to combine the scattered documentation into structured guidelines,
generate test cases, and create a common foundation upon which the SiL testing method can
be established.
The scope of this thesis can be expanded in the future to incorporate more component features
and models of higher fidelity. Some of the future works are listed below:
1. This study can be expanded in the future to look into the transformer and enabling mag-
netizing currents in the transformers. Studies could be performed with varying parameters
in the transformer model to understand the effect of transformers and the corresponding
component similarities in the two tools.
2. Switching model studies could be incorporated to analyze the switching harmonics in-
troduced. This can lead to the development of filters to counter the harmonics. Hence,
the performance of these complex models will provide more intriguing and higher-fidelity
studies.
3. Current controls can be improved to incorporate the scenarios for fault where the priority
has to be changed to reactive current injection while respecting the converter’s current
limits. This can help in performing studies on fault scenarios for complex models and
observing the similarities/deviations of currents.

6.1
Large and Small Time-step Environments in RSCAD
In RSCAD, the simulator has two different environments, referred to as the large-time step and
the small-time step.
In the large time step environment, the minimum time step allowed is 10µs. It is suitable for a
simulating electrical network with a lesser requirement for time resolution. It allows for a faster
simulation but might also result in lower accuracy [40].
Small time step environment has a minimum time-step of 1.4µs. This environment is used for
modeling fast transients, for example, while modeling switching circuits. It can provide a detailed
component behavior and is used when high accuracy and fine-grained analysis is required [40].
6.2
Scenario Sc_18
This scenario has a three-phase to ground fault occurring at the PCC at the 3s. There is an
inductor of 0.0001H and a fault resistance of0.0005Ω. During this scenario, the PPC controls the
active power and voltage at the PCC. The PPC controls the active power and voltage at 1pu.
At the 3rd second, the fault is applied, which provides a path of very low impedance for the
current to flow. The voltage at the WTG terminal and the PCC drops to 0pu, after which it sta-
bilizes at a value just above 0pu at the WTG terminal and remains at 0pu at the PCC. The fault
is applied for 0.05s, after which it is cleared.
In Figure 6.1, it can be observed that at the moment when a fault is applied, the active power at
the PCC and WTG fall to 0. This fall in active power is reflected instantly in RSCAD at the WTG
and PCC, whereas, for PSCAD, this sudden fall in active power to 0 is only observed at PCC. At
the WTG terminal, the active power falls to 0 with a smooth transient. Once the fault has cleared,
it is observed that the transient of the active power signals is different for PSCAD and RSCAD
for the measurements at both places. RSCAD seems to have more oscillation when going back
to the previous setpoint of 1pu active power, whereas PSCAD transitions in a smoother way.
As for reactive power, the RSCAD signal at the PCC shows similar characteristics when the fault
happens and after the fault is cleared. At the WTG terminal, the transients behave differently in
the two tools where RSCAD shows more oscillations as the reactive power drops and is then
increased to support the low voltage at PCC. The voltage signals at the WT terminal voltage
go to 0 in RSCAD when the fault occurs and then stabilize at a value above 0. In contrast, the
PSCAD voltage signal transitions smoothly to this stabilized voltage value. Also, once the fault
is cleared, the voltage increases in both tools. In RSCAD, some oscillations are present during
this transition to 1pu voltage that is not found for PSCAD. Hence, in general, PSCAD voltage is
smoother than RSCAD. These observations for the two tools are similar for the measurements
at the PCC, with the difference in that the voltage drops completely to 0 as the fault occurs at
the PCC.
