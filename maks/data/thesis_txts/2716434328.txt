2. Introduction 
 
Significant investments in offshore wind turbines and energy infrastructure are required to transform 
Bornholm into an energy island. While power could be transmitted to consumers in the surrounding 
area of Bornholm, questions about the most efficient energy transfer originate. Because wind power 
fluctuates, it is known that this power will have to be used partially by power to X in order to obtain 
higher value final products like liquid fuels. At the same time, Bornholm is central to Baltic Sea traffic, 
which will necessitate significant fuel consumption in the future.  
This thesis focuses on the production of green methanol. It is a versatile fuel that can be used to power 
internal combustion engines, hybrid and fuel cell vehicles, and vessels. It is easy to store, transport, and 
distribute because it is a liquid at room temperature and pressure [1]. In the next subsegments the 
introduction will be further analyzed. 
 
2.1 World targets 
 
Alternatives to current fossil fuel consumption must be introduced in all sectors of the energy system 
to keep global temperature increases below 2 oC, and even 1.5 oC, as agreed upon by the Paris 
Agreement's represented nations [5]. Over the next several decades, policy advances and stakeholder 
engagement will encourage shipowners to explore, evaluate, and implement technologies, fuels, and 
solutions that will help decarbonize ships, reduce energy use, and comply with other environmental 
regulations [1]. 
 
2.2 Green transition in Denmark and the Baltics 
 
Recently, the Danish Parliament authorized a plan to boost offshore power in Denmark. One of the two 
islands is the natural land mass of Bornholm in the Baltic Sea, which has been selected to connect 2 
GW. The other one will be an artificial structure in the North Sea with the capacity for 3 GW and at 
least 10 GW in the long term. This agreement aims to reduce 3.4 million tons of CO2 equivalents by 
2030, while promoting economic recovery through the green transformation of industry. Eventually, 
Power-to-X (P2X) devices that can store or convert electricity into renewable fuels will utilize the 
island's energy. The agreement includes a solicitation to fund the building of 100 MW P2X reactors on 
a significant scale. The new agreement also contributes to a greener transportation sector, with funding 
earmarked for charging stations, heavy freight, ferries, etc. [1]. 
 
 
Bornholm has emerged as a P2X development hotspot. It is less expensive to generate hydrogen on 
Bornholm using electricity from nearby offshore wind power parks than it is to convert the electricity 
in Copenhagen [1]. With 60000 ships passing through the island each year, the Bornholm Bunker Hub 
consortium (Ørsted, Molslinjen, Haldor Topsoe, Bunker Holding Group, Wärtsilä, Ramboll, Bureau 
Veritas, and Port of Ronne) is looking into making Bornholm the Baltic's "gas station." hydrogen can 
be converted into several different chemicals/fuels, including methanol, which can be used in maritime 
transportation.

2.3 Green fuels for ships 
 
This fuel could be green methanol, which is currently used by many shipowners. However, it is widely 
acknowledged that the biogenic carbon on Bornholm will not be sufficient to meet this demand [1]. 
Renewable methanol can be made, using both renewable energy and renewable feedstocks:  
Green e-methanol is made by capturing CO2 from renewable sources (bioenergy with carbon capture 
and storage [BECCS] and direct air capture [DAC]) and converting it into green hydrogen [8]. The main 
challenge is obtaining CO2 through production, transfer, and/or storage so that it can be combined with 
hydrogen to produce e-methanol. Based on its origin, CO2 feedstock for e-methanol production is 
divided into two types. 
The second category includes CO2 captured directly, which is the focus of this thesis. Direct air capture 
(DAC) technologies remove CO2 from the atmosphere directly. The CO2 can be permanently stored in 
deep geological formations (achieving negative emissions or carbon removal) or used, for instance in 
food processing or combined with hydrogen to create synthetic fuels.  
Today, liquid and solid DAC technologies are used to capture CO2 from the atmosphere. Liquid systems 
remove CO2 by passing air through chemical solutions (such as a hydroxide solution). By applying 
high-temperature heat, the system reintegrates the chemicals back into the process while returning the 
remaining air to the environment. Solid DAC technology employs solid sorbent filters that bind CO2 
chemically. When the filters are heated and placed in a vacuum, the concentrated CO2 is released and 
then captured for storage or use [4]. Another demarcation of the way of capturing CO2 is if the process 
takes place cyclic or non-cyclic. If it is cyclic, the CO2 extracted from the atmosphere returns to the air. 
If CO2 is captured from fossil resources or non-renewables, it needs to be isolated (CCS), whereas point 
source CO2 from biomass can be collected and utilized to produce green methanol (CCU). Alternately, 
direct air capture can be used for CCU.

2.3.2 Methanol (MeOH) 
 
Methanol, also known as CH3OH and MeOH, is found naturally in fruits, vegetables, fermented foods 
and beverages, as well as in the environment and even in space [5]. It is one of the 4 important basic 
chemicals (together with ethylene, propylene, and ammonia) and is necessary to produce all other 
chemical products such as formaldehyde, acetic acid, and polymers. MeOH is utilized in the mixing of 
gasoline, as well as in the manufacturing of biodiesel and DME (dimethyl ether) [5]. 
Methanol is a colorless, water-soluble liquid with a moderate alcoholic odor that has the highest 
hydrogen-to-carbon ratio of any liquid fuel at normal ambient temperatures [5]. As a result, it can serve 
as a critical energy carrier and be employed as an alternative shipping fuel. It is a low-viscosity fuel 
(like ammonia), which means that: 
a) MeOH has a low calorific value (about 40% of diesel), which means that more fuel is required 
to achieve the same power output, and  
b) MeOH has a low ignition temperature, which means that diesel pilot fuel is required for stable 
engine operation [5]. 
Renewable methanol can be produced using renewable energy and renewable feedstocks via two routes:  
1. Bio-methanol is produced from biomass. Key potential sustainable biomass feedstocks include:  
forestry and agricultural waste and by-products, biogas from landfill, sewage, municipal solid 
waste (MSW) and black liquor from the pulp and paper industry [8]. 
2. Green e-methanol is obtained by using CO2 captured from renewable sources (bioenergy with 
carbon capture and storage [BECCS] and direct air capture [DAC]) and green hydrogen, i.e., 
hydrogen produced with renewable electricity [8]. 
3. The conventional method for gasifying biomass to produce methane or methanol employs pure 
oxygen or steam-oxygen mixtures to generate synthesis gas. The synthesis gas is then purified 
and processed to produce methane or methanol. Significant quantities of tars and water-soluble 
organic materials are by-products of gasification, particularly with counterflow, fixed bed 
operation. By gasifying biomass with only steam and the appropriate catalysts in the gasifier, 
less cleaning and processing equipment will be required to prepare the gas for the methanator 
or methanol converter. The primary by-product of this gasification scheme is char. The 
production of tar and water-soluble organic compounds is reduced [6]. 
4. Thermal depolymerization and decomposition of biomass, including cellulose, hemicelluloses, 
and lignin, produced liquid and gaseous byproducts in addition to a solid charcoal residue. The 
pyrolytic conversion of biomass under a variety of experimental conditions has produced 
charcoal, tarry material, an aqueous fraction, and gaseous products, making it a promising 
method for biomass processing. About fifty percent of the pyroligneous acid consists of 
methanol, acetone, phenols, and water. Biomass can be pyrolyzed to produce methanol. The 
methoxyl groups of uronic acid and the breakdown of methyl esters and/or ethers from the 
decomposition of pectin-like plant materials are the primary sources of methanol. Acetic acid 
originates primarily from the acetyl groups of hemicelluloses [7].

2.4 Need of sustainable fuels in Shipping industry 
 
CO2 emissions from maritime shipping are projected to account for 2.2 % of worldwide CO2 emissions. 
Since 2007, this proportion has decreased, primarily as a result of reducing traffic and different steps 
implemented by ships owners themselves [1]. However, this proportion might potentially surpass 10% 
by 2040 if global transport demand grows [1]. Future CO2 emissions from maritime shipping are 
difficult to anticipate since they are greatly dependent on worldwide shipping demand [2]. Other 
variables affect the magnitude of CO2 reductions in shipping, such as the availability of technical 
solutions and the development and economics of alternative fuels [2]. A substantial increase in maritime 
commerce would lead to a proportional increase in CO2 emissions and a significantly larger proportion 
of global emissions [2]. According to a major IMO study, CO2 emissions from shipping will be 50-250 
% greater in 2050 than in 2012, assuming extremely sharp increases in transport demand. Given this 
fast increase and the lack of action, maritime shipping's proportion of global CO2 emissions will climb 
dramatically [8]. A possible approach could involve a trade-off between fewer shipping and green 
shipping. 
CO2 emissions from shipping are predicted to increase, as depicted by the IMO diagrams below (figure 
7) [9]. The worst-case scenario is predicated on the supposition that traffic volumes will increase rapidly 
and that the fuel mix will not reduce CO2 emissions [9]. In the best-case scenario, traffic growth will 
moderate, and the fuel mix will contain a greater proportion of alternative fuels with lower CO2 
emissions [9].

3.Outline of the thesis  
 
This thesis research is organized as the following. After the introduction section, a thorough literature 
review will be presented first to include relevant studies on P2X, value chains of hydrogen and 
methanol, strategic for the decarbonization of maritime transport and analysis of electricity prices. A 
theoretical framework will be introduced to conclude the literature review. Secondly, a methodology 
section covers the case background and selection, research, strategy, approach, method, data collection, 
and limitations. Thirdly, an analysis chapter demonstrates case data collected in line with the theoretical 
framework to analyze the findings and results. Fourthly, an analytical framework will be presented that 
integrates the findings, implications, and possible solutions. Some critical interpretations and answers 
to the research question are also included, followed by suggestion. Lastly, a brief conclusion restates 
the research's purpose, methodology, findings, solutions, limitations, and proposes further studies. 
 
3.1 Thesis’ Aims and objectives 
 
The purpose of this master's thesis is to evaluate the cost and efficiency of green methanol production 
with energy from an offshore wind park distant from the shore. A 2 GW offshore wind park will be 
built 20 km outside Bornholm where H2 can be retrieved and, in combination with CO2, will produce 
green methanol. In this MSc project, four different supply chains are analyzed in order to find the 
optimal solutions from an environmental perspective and the most cost-efficient way for companies and 
investors. The electrolyser is considered alkaline, and the location of electrolysis is on Bornholm Island. 
Furthermore, different options for the transportation of CO2 and H2 from or to Bornholm were analyzed 
and the most cost-efficient solution found. Figure 8 briefly illustrates the way of how from renewable 
H2 and CO2 can produce green fuels for transportation and heating.

4.1 Steps to produce e-methanol 
 
Methanol synthesis is a well-studied process, and its result, methanol, is extensively applied in industry 
as a fuel, solvent, energy storage medium, and feedstock. Methanol will likely play a key role in the 
future, particularly in energy-related conversion. To fulfil the demands of the future, it is necessary to 
design processes that produce methanol in a sustainable and energy-efficient manner [14] 
Renewable methanol can be produced in the following ways, using both renewable energy and 
renewable feedstocks:  
1. There are three primary biomethanol production methods: gasification, reformer-based (from 
biogas), and the pulping cycle in pulp mills. The production of methanol from fossil feedstocks 
is well-established, and much of this knowledge can be applied to the production of methanol 
from renewable feedstocks. Similarities exist between the gasification and reformer-based 
production of biomethanol and the production of nonrenewable methanol. For these two routes, 
the production of methanol can be broken down into the following steps:  
 
o synthesis of synthesis gas,  
o synthesis of methanol, and  
o product purification.  
The synthesis gas is a mixture of hydrogen (H2), carbon monoxide (CO), and carbon dioxide (CO2) 
derived from numerous sources [15]. 
Another method is the pyrolysis of biomass to make methanol. The methoxyl groups of uronic acid and 
the breakdown of methyl esters and/or ethers from the disintegration of pectin-like plant components 
are the primary sources of methanol. Acetic acid originates mostly from the acetyl groups of 
hemicelluloses [16]. 
2. There are several electrochemical processes for producing e-methanol. Hydrogen is produced 
through the electrolysis of water using renewable electricity, followed by a catalytic reaction 
with carbon dioxide to form e-methanol. Another strategy is to produce both components of 
syngas, CO and H2, through electrolysis, and then convert the syngas to e-methanol in the same 
manner as conventional methanol manufacturing. Although this route could achieve a higher 
conversion efficiency than water electrolysis, it is less developed (conventional water 
electrolysis is in the megawatt range, whereas this co-electrolysis route is on a kilowatt scale in 
the laboratory). Direct electrochemical conversion of CO2 and water to methanol is also being 
researched, but laboratory-scale efficiency and yield have not yet been [27]. 
 
The main challenge is obtaining CO2 through production, transfer, and/or storage so that it can be 
combined with hydrogen to produce e-methanol. Based on its origin, CO2 feedstock for e-methanol 
production is divided into two types. CO2 emissions from various industrial sources, such as power 
plants (e.g., gas plants) and the steel and cement industries, are included in the first category. More 
specific, point source capture (PSC) from industrial sources (e.g., chemical production [ammonia, 
hydrogen, petrochemical], mineral production [cement and lime], natural gas processing, and iron and 
steel production plants) removes carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions from the flue gas or other exhaust 
stream that would have otherwise been released into the atmosphere [17]. The second category includes 
CO2 captured directly, which is the focus of this thesis.

4.5 CO2 captured 
 
Direct air capture (DAC) systems remove CO2 from the atmosphere directly. The CO2 can be stored in 
deep geological formations (achieving negative emissions or carbon removal) or used, for instance in 
food processing or mixed with hydrogen to create synthetic fuels[4] 
Today, liquid and solid DAC technologies are used to capture CO2 from the atmosphere. Liquid systems 
remove CO2 by passing air through chemical solutions (such as a hydroxide solution). By applying 
high-temperature heat, the system reintegrates the chemicals back into the process while returning the 
remaining air to the environment [4]. 
Solid DAC technology use solid sorbent filters that bind CO2 chemically. When the filters are heated 
and placed in a vacuum, the concentrated CO2 is released and then caught for storage or use. 
The vast majority of large-scale chances to employ captured CO2 would result in its release back into 
the atmosphere, such as when synthetic fuel is burned. This would not result in negative emissions, but 
it may still produce climatic advantages, such as if synthetic fuels replaced conventional fossil fuels [4]

4.6 CO2 transportation 
 
Transporting captured CO2 to a storage site or methanol plant in a safe and durable manner is a crucial 
step in the synthesis of methanol [19]. CO2 is transported daily in many parts of the world, but 
substantial infrastructure investments are required. In this master's thesis, cost of transport CO2 was 
calculated for two modes of transportation. The first version is with pipelines, where gaseous CO2 is 
transported, and the second version is the transportation of CO2 with ships after the liquification process. 
 
4.6.1 CO2 pipelines pros & cons 
 
The primary benefits of pipelines are that they enable the transportation of large volumes at low 
operating costs, with low energy consumption (and CO2 emissions), without requiring the use of 
existing infrastructure (roads, harbors, etc.), and with continuous operation regardless of weather 
conditions or other external disruptions. 
On the other side, the disadvantages of pipeline transportation include high investment costs, lengthy 
planning and construction times, extensive approval procedures (constructing within city limits is 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
P a g e  29 | 57 
 
difficult), land acquisition issues, public perception, and limited flexibility (end-use value) if the CO2 
source is eliminated or relocated. 
 
4.6.2 CO2 liquification 
 
Today, pipelines operate as a mature commercial technology and are the most prevalent means of 
transferring carbon dioxide. Typically, gaseous CO2 is compressed to a pressure greater than 8 MPa to 
eliminate two-phase flow regimes and enhance the CO2's density, so making it easier and less expensive 
to transport [3]. CO2 can also be transported as liquid in ships, road, and rail tankers that transport it in 
insulated tanks at temperatures much below the ambient temperature and at much lower pressures [3]. 
To efficiently transport large quantities of CO2, the gas must be transformed into a form with a greater 
density. CO2 is transportable in liquid, solid, and supercritical phases [23]. In this thesis project it is 
assumed that the CO2 is transported in liquid form. According to ‘Evaluation of CO2 liquefaction 
processes for ship-based carbon capture and utilization (CCU) in terms of life cycle cost (LCC) 
considering availability’ there are 4 ways to liquify CO2[23] 
CCS is the process of capturing, transferring, and storing carbon dioxide underground [19]. At big 
sources of emissions, such as fossil-fueled power plants and steel mills, CO2 is absorbed. The waste is 
then stored in onshore or offshore facilities [19]. When an offshore storage site is chosen, CO2 can be 
transferred by offshore pipelines or ships. For cheaper shipping, CO2 is liquefied for transit. The volume 
of CO2 in liquid form is approximately one-fifth of that of CO2 in gaseous form. The economic 
component will be discussed in the following chapter [19]. 
Between the triple point and the critical point, CO2 can be liquefied under various conditions (i.e., 
pressure and temperature) [19]. Situations close to the triple point are characterized by low pressures 
and low temperatures, whereas conditions close to the critical point are characterized by high pressures 
and high temperatures. Due to the low temperatures, liquefying CO2 near the triple point demands a 
large refrigeration load, whereas a vast compression system is required for conditions near the critical 
point [24]. 
 
4.7 Electrolysis Process 
 
When voltage is provided to an electrolysis unit, two reactions occur at two electrodes — the negatively 
charged cathode and the positively charged anode. At the anode, the Oxygen Evolution Reaction (OER) 
produces oxygen, whereas the hydrogen Evolution Reaction (HER) creates hydrogen. An electrolyte is 
employed to balance the charge of the gases produced, while a membrane separates them, avoiding 
mixing and contamination [25] 
At the moment, three electrolysis technologies are considered commercially feasible. There are three 
types of electrolysis: alkaline, solid oxide electrolysis cells (SOEC), and polymer electrolyte membrane 
(PEM). Each technique has its own set of advantages and disadvantages, but they all contribute 
significantly to the scaling up of green hydrogen generation. In this project AEC method was considered 
[26]. 
A mature technology, alkaline electrolysis (figure 12) is capable of producing hydrogen on an industrial 
scale. A typical alkaline electrolyser splits water using an electrical current, a porous diaphragm, and 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
P a g e  30 | 57 
 
an alkaline liquid electrolyte. This method's primary benefit is that it employs a liquid electrocatalyst, 
eliminating the need for costly metal components. Alkaline electrolysis cells can be stacked indefinitely 
and are renowned for their stability and longevity over the long term [23]. 
 
Figure 12 Electrolysis process [13]. 
In all scenarios involving onshore electrolysis, tap water supply was examined. Water consumption is 
relatively high, but nevertheless doable given Denmark's supply of approximately 100 million m3 in 
2018 [27] 
In an electrolyser, hydrogen and oxygen are separated from water using water as a main feedstock and

4.8 Methanol infrastructure from power 
 
Methanol from power is a scenario that combines electrolysis-produced hydrogen and carbon dioxide 
to produce methanol. Iceland has one facility in operation (Carbon Recycling International, CRI). The 
factory produces 5 million liters of methanol each year. The facility uses grid electricity to produce 
hydrogen, which is then catalytically transformed with carbon dioxide into methanol (CO2). 
There are numerous potential sources of CO2 (biomass plants, biogas plants, ethanol plants), and 
versions of the concept where renewable electricity sources substitute grid power have been proposed. 
As an alternative to methanation, CO2 from biogas plants could be utilized in smaller-scale systems. 
Currently, methanol is manufactured commercially by reforming natural gas with steam. In this process, 
methane is converted into hydrogen, carbon monoxide, and carbon dioxide, which are then recombined 
to make methanol. As indicated in the following diagram, different sources of hydrogen and carbon 
dioxide are combined to make methanol in the power-to-methanol system. 
 
Figure 13 Methanol process [30]. 
 
Prior to the production of methanol, electricity is utilized to generate hydrogen and compress the gases. 
If low-carbon power is used, the produced methanol has a small carbon footprint, which is an attractive 
feature of the process.

5.3 Cost of H2 Transportation with pipelines 
 
In the first scenario, the possibility of H2 pipeline integration was assumed. It is essential to determine 
the appropriate method of transporting H2 (table 17) (offshore or onshore) in order to produce methanol 
at a lower cost (€/ton).  
Transporting H2 through pipes necessitates extra measures with regard to both the material and 
operational conditions of the pipelines. The fact that the H2 can diffuse into steel presents a significant 
issue. At the surface of composites, H2 molecules may split into two H atoms, which can subsequently 
move deeply into the material. H atoms can recombine in micro voids within steel after diffusion to 
create molecular H2 gas. Therefore, accumulates in these voids, reducing the flexibility and tensile 
strength of the steel to the point where it might break. This process, known as H2 embrittlement, makes 
steels with a high manganese and carbon content sensitive to breaking. Using thick, low-strength steel 
and maintaining a gas's relative humidity below 60 % could help prevent cracks.

6.3 Research and overview of various supply chains  
 
6.3.1 CO2 transportation and sources 
 
CO2 transportation through ship and pipeline both have their advantages and disadvantages. Generally, 
CO2 transmission through ship is a more flexible mode of transport than pipeline. For ships, the 
transportation route may be simply changed if another CO2 source or storage location becomes 
available; similarly, the transportation chain's capacity can be gradually increased by adding additional 
ships as demand increases. Additionally, ships (assuming a standard carrier type is used) can be 
repurposed for the transportation of other commodities, such as LPG, NH3, and so on, if the CO2 source 
ceases production. CO2 transportation by ship, on the other hand, is more expensive than pipeline 
transport for short to medium distances, and it necessitates the construction of expensive CO2 terminals 
with intermediate storage facilities [33]. 
 
6.3.2 Game changing technologies 
 
If the so-called "direct air capture" technology matures and becomes commercially viable, this will have 
a substantial impact on the current findings. Therefore, the location of the CO2 source becomes 
irrelevant. Here, it would be crucial to evaluate what impact this will have on the PtX manufacturing 
value chain as a whole. For instance, the source of CO2 would no longer be constrained. [39] 
The availability of green methanol is limited. If it is possible to construct optimal capacity for each 
demand, this could be an important parameter. In order to divide the amount of methanol produced by 
each country, one method is to separate the ports from which methanol fuel is exported into many 
locations. 
In addition, there is no flexibility between the beginning and endpoints of CO2 pipeline transit. 
Alternatively, if the cost of transporting CO2 by ship can be reduced, this will be the most efficient 
method, and methanol could be used as a fuel in order to avoid CO2 emissions.

6.3.3 Environmental Impact Assessment 
 
All energy sources have some impact on our ecosystem. Coal, oil, and natural gas do significantly more 
harm than renewable energy sources by most measures, including air and water pollution, damage to 
human health, loss of species and habitat, water use, land use, and emissions that contribute to global 
warming. However, renewable energy sources such as wind, solar, geothermal, biomass, and 
hydropower have substantial environmental implications. 
The exact nature and magnitude of environmental impacts vary depending on the used technology, 
geographic location, and other variables. By knowing the existing and potential environmental 
difficulties associated with each renewable energy source, we can successfully prevent or mitigate these 
impacts as they constitute a larger proportion of the nation's electricity supply. 
The main environmental impacts are associated with the transportation of CO2 due to:  
1. During the construction phase of the transportation system, all aspects that are typically 
associated with construction must be considered, such as terrestrial habitat alteration, loss of 
valuable eco systems, land consumption, negative impacts on biodiversity, particle and NOx 
emissions, noise, and hazardous materials [1]. 
2. Leakages should be kept to minimum during the operation period. Underground networks have 
the potential to disrupt groundwater systems and local ecosystems [1].
