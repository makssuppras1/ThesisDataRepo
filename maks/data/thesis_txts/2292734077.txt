Abstract
This thesis deals with modeling and numerical implementation of tilting-pad
journal bearings, actively lubricated by means of radial oil injection through the
pads.
It is an expansion of an existing model implemented in Matlab.
The
model is extended with radial and transverse pad degrees of freedom. The pads
rocker pivots supporting the pads are modeled using Hertz contact theory, and
it is shown how a low pivot compliance can have severe eﬀects on the dynamic
coeﬃcients of the bearing, reducing both stiﬀness and damping approximately 25%
for pivot compliance equivalent to that of the oil ﬁlm. In this thesis a fast, reliable
and versatile bearing program is developed, and connected with a rotor-bearing
program with the purpose of automating bearing design. One important factor in
making this possible was the substituting of the popular Newton-Raphson method
for determining equilibrium, with a trust-region method that has outstanding
convergence.

Chapter 1
Project Introduction
The purpose of this introductory section is to explain the nature of this project.
First, the initial project description is presented. Then the progress of the project
is explained in broad terms, which elucidates why the project turned out the way
it did. Lastly, the purpose of the project is described.
1.1
Initial project description and motivation be-
hind it
The subject of this thesis is dynamic modeling of tilting-pad journal bearings
(TPJBs).
The initial objectives for the project were as follows:
• Starting with the TPJB program from the thesis of Bo Kristiansen [5]:
• Add ξ and η to the model
• Determine equilibrium
• Determine dynamic coeﬃcients
• Perform experiments to validate the addition of ξ and η
==PAGE:22==
1.2 Project progress
2
• Implement adiabatic energy equation (optional)
For TPJBs there is a trend in discrepancy between theory and experiment in
regards to damping coeﬃcient, in which the theory will overestimate the damping.
This trend is seen for the test-rig TPJBs at DTU. It has been speculated that the
discrepancy can be attributed to not modeling the ﬂexibility of the pad pivot. This
is further explained in Section 3.6.
This motivated the attempt to model the pad pivot and check with experiments if
the speculation could be validated. This was the initial scope of the project. The
provided program was to be modiﬁed to accept ξ and η as the radial and tangential
translations of the pad pivot. Along with an appropriate stiﬀness model, they may
be used to model the ﬂexibility of the pad pivot. The addition of ξ and η requires
a rework of the program subroutines which determine equilibrium and dynamic
coeﬃcients. Incidentally these subroutines make up more or less the entirety of the
program. The initially provided program did not have an energy-equation routine.
It was decided that if time allowed it, the energy-equation was to be implemented
as well. The experiments were to be performed on the 1-DOF TPJB test rig
at DTU, since it is speciﬁcally designed for extraction of dynamic coeﬃcients.
Additionally it was decided to produce additional pad pivots of varying stiﬀness for
the test-rig to get more distinct data for the validation and to test of the speculation.
1.2
Project progress
ξ and η were added to the program. Most routines in the program had to be
modiﬁed to quite a large extend. The modiﬁcations were based on many diﬀerent
concepts in mathematics and physics which had to be fully understood ﬁrst. Many
of these concepts were completely new material to the author, who initially had very
limited experience with lubrication theory. The original program was developed
by Professor Ilmar Santos for his article [9] on tilting-pad journal bearings with
active radial lubrication. It was later modiﬁed by Bo Kristiansen as part of his
masters thesis [5]. The program was working well, and had the core features in
place. However, to some extend the implementation still bore the distinct marks of
a proof-of-concept stage with ad hoc data handling, inconsistent variable naming
between subroutines and excessive use of global variables. On top of that, a large
part of the variables and comment strings were in Portuguese since this was the
language used in the original program. Together, these factors made for a very
==PAGE:23==
1.2 Project progress
3
time consuming up-hill battle to modify the program, especially for implementing
additional degrees of freedom.
Whilst working on the program, preparations for the experimental part of the
project were carried out by creating drawings of the new pivots for the 1-DOF
test-rig for the workshop to produce. This took an unusually long time, compared
to their otherwise high eﬃciency. This was the ﬁrst in a series of bottlenecks related
to the experimental work. When the new pivots were done at last, other students
had commenced experiments on the test-rig. As they were about to ﬁnish, the high-
pressure pump broke, and had to be replaced. This also added to the bottleneck.
As the hand-in date for the project approached, it became clear that extra time
was needed, if experimental data was to be obtained in due time to also document
everything. Not willing to compromise the last and most important project of his
academic endeavors, the author ﬁled for, and was granted, a two month extension.
The experimental work commenced, where nearly three weeks passed working on
conﬁguring, calibrating, testing and developing a post-processing method for the
data. The ﬁrst real experiment was commenced, but halfway through, the axle
holding the journal broke in half. The painfully unfortunate event is described in
detail in Section 4.2.2, but the bottom line was that experimental data would not
be obtained for this project.
Although ξ and η had long since been implemented, the numerical work had in
no way stagnated. Previous experimental endeavors had taught the author to
anticipate events such as this, and keep a backup plan. Therefore, a secondary
objective was adopted early on. The secondary objective was to use the bearing
program to optimize stability for a speciﬁc rotor-bearing system which had stability
issues below its optimal operating speed. An in-house written rotor-bearing code
was obtained, courtesy of Ph.D. Jorge Andrés González Salazar, and the bearing
program was coupled to it. Initial simulations of the system conﬁrmed the stability
issue. However, it quickly became clear that with the vast amount of design
variables, it would be virtually impossible to manually navigate to, or even identify,
any optimal conﬁguration in the high-dimensional space spanned by the design
variables. Instead, work focused on using optimization algorithms to navigate this
high-dimensional solution space. However, for that to be feasible, the bearing
program must be fast, robust and and the design variables must be accessible
without altering the program. This leads up to what this project really amounts to.

1.3
The purpose of the project
The purpose of this project is to create the foundation for a tool that is capable of
autonomously optimize an arbitrary rotor-bearing system. The tool will be given
a speciﬁc rotor-bearing system, a number of objective functions such as damping
ratios, and a list of design variables. The tool will then use global optimization al-
gorithms to explore the solution space of the objective functions by using heuristics
to navigate the high-dimensional space spanned by the design variables, in an eﬀort
to ﬁnd an optimal solution. At the core of this tool is a bearing program which is
fast and has guaranteed convergence within reasonable use. Furthermore, for this
to be possible the design variables of the bearing program must be conﬁgurable
without making changes to the program or its data ﬁle. The bearing program
should have all the important bearing design variables available, and they may not
interfere with each other to cause program failure. This requires well structured
data management throughout the program. Furthermore, the program should
be easy to understand and utilize, and it must be structured to facilitate future
program expansion.
These criteria have been kept in mind and followed during the development of this
program. The present written documentation of the project takes in to account that
the next academic working to expand the program, may be in the same position as
the author was at the initial phase of the project, with little knowledge of lubrication
theory. Therefore emphasis has been put on providing an intuitive introduction to
bearing types and lubrication theory in Chapter 2, as well as a theory chapter in
Chapter 3 which provides all the mathematics and physics necessary to understand
the theoretical model behind the program. In the same sense, in Section 4.1 the
utilization and capabilities of the program along with a fairly exhaustive program
ﬂowchart is provided.

Chapter 2
Theory Preconditioner
The main purpose of this chapter is to establish terminology and nomenclature used
throughout the report. To begin with, the main categories of bearings are intro-
duced along with a short description of their respective advantages and drawbacks.
Following, the mechanism behind hydrodynamic lubrication is explained with
emphasis on tools of imagery and reasoning rather than mathematical derivations.
Following, a description of the diﬀerent types of journal bearings is given. Later,
the speciﬁc bearing modeled in this work is introduced. Lastly, is an overview of

2.1
Deﬁnitions and classiﬁcations of bearings
A bearing is a structural component within the class of machine elements. Its
purpose is to control the freedom of relative motion between parts in a machine,
to ﬁt their desired motion of the design. Usually the parts are rigid within the
bearing, and one part remains stationary. The desired motion will be either linear,
rotational or a combination thereof. The typical machine designer will adhere to
the notion that bearings are necessary evils, i.e. an unavoidable element which does
not add to the function of the machine, but merely subtracts from its power train
by way of friction, whilst adding to it another failure mechanism. The bearing
==PAGE:26==
2.1 Deﬁnitions and classiﬁcations of bearings
6
modeled in this report does not exclusively lent itself to that notion, in that it
may be used for control and stabilization of inherent adverse eﬀects of the shaft
it supports. It is a unique type of tilting pad journal bearing (TPJB) which uses
hydrodynamic lubrication as well as passive or active radially injected hydrostatic
pressure through oriﬁces in the pad surface. To set such a speciﬁc bearing into
perspective, it is categorized alongside more basic bearing types. The bearing types
are limited by directing focus on bearings for supporting shafts.
Shafts are rotating structural machine elements designed to transmit power as
torque also known as moment. Shafts distinguish themselves from other common
machine elements in that they are not highly standardized. Shafts serve as an
assembly mount to ensure the correct relative positions of other machine elements,
which together with the shaft make the rotor assembly, or just rotor for short.
Depending on the application, these could be elements such as gears, pulleys,
bearings, spline couplings, turbine blades, cams and cranks, to name a few. As
such, the geometry of the shaft, usually the diameter, tends to change along its
axis of rotation, making it unique to the machine for which it is designed. Since the
shaft is required to accurately position the other machine elements, its radial and
axial alignment is paramount for the operation. For horizontal shafts, this is usually
accomplished by two trust bearings for axial alignment and two radial bearings at
either end of the shaft for radial alignment. In the case of long horizontal shafts
where lateral deﬂection is a cause for concern, additional radial bearings may be
required. In the case of a very short shaft, a single radial bearing may suﬃce. For
vertical shafts where the axial load is always in the same direction and lateral
deﬂection is negligible, it may be possible to adequately ﬁx the shaft with one
axial and one radial bearing. Classiﬁcation of such bearings is covered in this section.
The bearings for shafts are used to facilitate axial rotation, and constraining axial
and radial movement by supporting its load. Upon choosing a bearing a number
of design variables are taken into account. These may include size, maintenance
requirements, price, service life, ability to withstand shock, maximum load, shaft
rotational speed, transport of heat and damping capabilities. There is a wide variety
of bearings for shafts. However, they can be classiﬁed by two main characteristics
listed in Table 2.1.

the main lubrication system, as plain bearings are designed to avoid surface contact.

2.3
Types of hydrodynamic journal bearings
These bearings oﬀer high load carrying capacity, low friction at high journal speeds
as well as damping capabilities, which arise when temporal eﬀects in the ﬂuid
are considered. The constant lubrication also allows for bearing cooling, as well
as ﬁltering potentially destructive debris from the surface interface. The bearing
demonstrated was the closed cylindrical journal bearing under hydrodynamic
lubrication, which is arguably the most basic of its kind. A number of diﬀerent
geometric modiﬁcations are used for these bearings. Perhaps the simplest signiﬁcant
variation is the addition of axial oil grooves, which are added to combat oil
instabilities, which are touched upon later in this section. The geometry of the
converging gap plays an essential role in bearing behavior.
By changing the
geometry of the bearing surface, the initial wedge inclination can be varied, which
changes the load-carrying capacity and damping characteristics of the system. A
more fancy geometry variation is the three-lobe bearing, as illustrated in Fig. 2.4.
Figure 2.4: Three-lobe journal bearing
The surface of the bearing mimics that of a much larger bearing bore, which results
in a steeper incline of the wedge. This results in a larger load carrying capacity.
Although this seems like a worthwhile pursuit, it also causes an increased ﬂuid ﬁlm
stiﬀness. This reduces the movement in the bearing, which means the damping

capabilities are not used to their fullest extend. Other geometrical proﬁles include
bearings with more lobes, diﬀerently conﬁgured lobes and the the lemon bore
which resembles a lemon on its side. On top of that there are variations to each
of the above mentioned types, in which the bearing shell is cut in to a number of
rigid, potentially skewed sections called pads. Each bearing has diﬀerent dynamic
behaviors, and much like with the rolling element bearings, the choice of bearing
depends on the application.
There is one major drawback to this type of bearing. As the journal is sped up,
it will travel both upwards and sideways, due to the curvature of the bearing
surface. During operation, a perturbation in one direction leads to a response
in both directions. This cross-coupling of the system introduces the risk of a
self-excited instability mechanism in rotor-bearing systems. In between the ﬁrst
two critical speeds of the rotor, the journal will orbit in the bearing. This is known
as oil whirl, and is easy to recognize due to the distinct property that the orbital
frequency of approximately half the rotational speed. As the rotor passes its second
critical speed, a phenomena known as oil whip may occur. This is a destructive
instability of high amplitude that can not be passed like a critical speed. Instead
the rotor must be slowed below the second critical speed before the instability
vanishes. Although oil whips sets in at the second critical speed, its frequency
and mode is that of the ﬁrst critical speed. Rotors with light loads and thin
shafts are more prone to these instabilities. Multi-lobe bearing such as the one il-
lustrated in Fig. 2.4 are better at handling these instabilities than the plain bearings.
This leads to the TPJB. This type of bearing is named after its characteristic
feature, i.e. the tilting pads. Similarly to the ﬁxed-arc journal bearings mentioned
above, its surface is cut into segments known as pads. The pads are provided with
a pivot which allows them rotate. Incidentally, this also means that they are unable
to sustain a moment, which results in the pads aligning to solely take up radial
loads in the direction of the pivot point. For all intents and purposes, this serves to
completely uncouple the forces, rendering them virtually immune to the oil induced
instabilities. As such, TPJBs are widely used for high-speed turbomachinery, and
are commonly regarded as superior to other bearings. TPJBs are classiﬁed by
the orientation of the external load relative to the pad position. The bearing will
either be Load On Pad (LOP) in which the external load load points directly on
to a pad, or Load Between Pads (LBP) in which the external load points directly
between two pads. For LOP the pads conﬁgurations are designed to be symmetrical

cross-coupled stiﬀness and damping terms.
2.4
The modeled bearing
The bearing modeled in this report is an actively lubricated TPJB. It uses con-
ventional hydrodynamic lubrication combined with radially injected oil through
oriﬁces in the pad surfaces. Santos [10] pioneered the concept by proposing two
TPJB designs that used hydraulic actuation to manipulate the ﬂuid ﬁlm pressure
and change the bearing characteristics. The idea behind this was to transform the
already superior TPJB into a mechatronic machine element capable of controlling
high speed rotor systems. The ﬁrst design introduced a cavity within the pads,
in which a hydraulic system could change the pad shape using oil pressure. The
second one applied radial oil injection through oriﬁces in the pad surface to order
to augment the pressure distribution in the ﬂuid ﬁlm interface thus changing the
dynamic properties of the bearing. Santos [9] developed a theoretical and numerical
model for the radial injection TPJB, coined the actively lubricated bearing (ALB),
by introducing the oil injection in Reynolds’ Equation, using the pad assembly
method developed by Lund [6], and a coupling to servo-valve control. The model
showed that it was in fact possible to control the bearing dynamics. Better yet,
this happened without compromising the low cross-coupling, which is the main
selling point of the TPJB. Problems with numerical stability and processor time
were encountered, which are still a major issue to this day. Santos [8], expanded
his model to include hydrothermal eﬀects. Here focus was on investigations of the
inﬂuence of oriﬁce conﬁguration with constant pressures on the journal position
and bearing cooling. It was concluded that the conﬁguration of oriﬁces mainly
inﬂuence the bearing cooling, in which optimal cooling was observed with oriﬁces
close to the pad edges. It was also reported that the injection pressure played
an integral role in journal positioning. Since then, a lot of work has gone into
developing the ALB, especially at DTU. Two test rigs reside at DTU, made for
model validation and proof of concept. One of them is a 2-pad LOP ALB with a
rigid journal constrained in the horizontal direction. It is purposefully designed to
have only one degree of freedom, which greatly facilitates bearing model validation,
since theoretically it can not be cross-coupled. This 1-DOF test-rig plays a role in
the present project, and is discussed in Section 4.2.2. The second test-rig is consists
of a ﬂexible shaft supported a 4-pad LOP ALB. The setup is designed to resemble
a scaled down rotor-bearing system, which can be used to validate rotor-bearing

codes, and develop control algorithms for using the ALB eﬀect to stabilize the
rotor. Of these two test-rigs, this project devotes focus only to the 1-DOF model.

3.2
Film Thickness Function
The ﬁlm thickness is the distance between the mating surfaces of the bearing along
the ¯x-direction, which is always perpendicular to the pad inner surface. The ﬁlm
thickness varies in time and the ¯y direction. The ﬁlm thickness function (FTF)
maps a ﬁlm thickness over the pad inner surface. The FTF h(β, t) along with its
two ﬁrst order partial derivatives are used in the MRE. In this section a method
for deriving an exact solution for h is presented. Following, the 2-pad DTU model
along with computed static equilibrium position is used as a case study to compare
the exact solution to the approximated solution by Springer [13].
3.2.1
Geometry
The ﬁlm thickness function h is dependent on the relationship between the general-
ized coordinates of the journal and the pad in question. As such, there will be a h
associated with each pad. In this section h will be derived for a single journal-pad
subsystem. The nomenclature and geometry are deﬁned in Section 2.5.
The subsystem is examined in two conﬁgurations. In the ﬁrst conﬁguration all
the generalized coordinates are zero. This serves as a reference to the second
conﬁguration in which they all take on arbitrary symbolic values. The system is
illustrated in Fig. 3.2.

The automatic discretization is programmed to have an increased node density
close to an oriﬁce. At the oriﬁces, a high pressure gradient is likely to occur, which
conﬂicts with the truncation of higher order terms from Eq. (3.35). The increased
grid solution near high gradients is used to counteract the loss of accuracy. ∆¯yn
and ∆¯zm are distributed such that a the nodes coincide with the a circle slightly
smaller (2%) than the oriﬁce. This is highlighted in Fig. 3.5. In case of multiple
oriﬁces, the grid resolution is increased at each oriﬁce. As the total grid resolution
is predetermined by pl and pc, the addition of extra oriﬁces reduce the remaining
grid resolutions, and calls for a new grid convergence study.
This ﬁnite diﬀerence scheme is a central diﬀerence scheme, expanded to two
dimension. Thus the standard 3-point stencil is now a 5-point stencil with 1 central
point, and one point on either side, both in ¯y and in ¯z. The coeﬃcient matrix A is
a 5-banded sparse matrix. Due to the indexing of pressure notes, the band has the

pressure falls below ambient pressure, a phenomenon known as gaseous cavitation
occurs, in which the gases dissolved in the lubricant are released. At pressures
below the oil vapor pressure, a phenomenon known as vapor cavitation occurs,
in which the oil vaporizes. Such phenomena are devastating to the bearing for a
variety of reasons, including the immediate drop in load carrying capacity. However,

way, the negative pressures must be removed, to avoid a non-physical solution.

3.5 Dynamical Model

3.5
Dynamical Model
As mentioned, the main goal of modeling TPJBs is to obtain dynamical stiﬀness
and damping coeﬃcients for the system, which can be coupled to rotor-bearing
ﬁnite element models for investigations of critical speeds and stability. There are
two main methods of modeling the system dynamics to obtain dynamical coeﬃcients.
The ﬁrst method, is to consider all DOFs in the system. This leads to coeﬃcient
matrices of size 2+PDOFnS ×2+PDOFnS, where nS is the number of pads, and PDOF
is the DOFs considered for each pad. In this study, PDOF = 3, if both rotation and
translations of the pads are considered. For a 4 pad TPJB, this leads to coeﬃcient
matrices of size 14 × 14. When coupling these to the rotor-bearing model, the
rotor-bearing model must have the additional pad DOFs allocated, which increases
its size and thus the computational eﬀort associated with analyzing the model.
This leads to the second and more widespread method for modeling the system.
Originally proposed by Lund [6] this method assumes that the pad motion is syn-
chronous with the journal motion. This allows for reducing the coeﬃcient matrix
sizes to 2 × 2, with two main and two coupled journal coeﬃcients. This method
restricts the system to the frequency domain, which means it can not be used for
non-synchronous forced response, or determination of damped eigenvalues [1].
In this section the method for determining the full dynamic coeﬃcients of the
TPJB is shown. Afterwards, the method for synchronously reducing these is shown.

3.6
Support Stiﬀness and Hertz Contact Theory
This section covers the modeling and implementation of the radial stiﬀness of the
pad pivots.
3.6.1
Reasoning behind including radial pivot stiﬀness
Typically when comparing dynamic coeﬃcients of theoretical TPJB models with
those of the real systems, the theoretical model will consistently and signiﬁcantly
overestimate the damping. It is suspected that this is due to an inappropriate
assumption of a rigid support structure.
The pads for the present model are supported by cylindrical rockers. At the backside
of the pads, a cylindrical section with radius RP is attached, which conforms with
a cylindrical socket of radius RH, which is part of the housing. This allows for
tilting of the pad, to enable the main mechanism of the TPJB. With zero load
between the cylidrical sections, the contact between them is in the shape of a line.
As the contact is loaded, the cylindrical sections deﬂect, and the contact becomes a
rectangular, the area of which increases with the load. As such, the radial support
is no longer rigid, but rather a non-linear spring. The dynamic bearing stiﬀness is
therefore two springs in series, where the ﬁrst spring is determined by the ﬂuid ﬁlm,
and the other is determined by the pivot spring. The equivalent spring stiﬀness of
two springs in series is given by Eq. (3.80).

The dynamic coeﬃcients were determined using the method speciﬁed in Springer
[13]. This method applies synchronous reduction immediately, which means the
full bearing coeﬃcients can not be obtained. Furthermore, it an unnecessarily
obscure method of determining dynamic coeﬃcients which makes it diﬃcult to

remove unwanted DOFs. It is likely slightly more computationally eﬃcient than
the method based on Allaire [1], however, the computational eﬀort associated with
ﬁnding dynamic coeﬃcients is minuscule and utterly dwarfed in comparison with
the computational eﬀort of determining equilibrium. Thus, Allaire’s method was

4.1.3.2
Object oriented design
One issue with the previous iterations of the program was the handling of data
throughout the code. They were based on global and single-class variables. Global
variables can be accessed and changed in all parts of a the program, which creates
coupling throughout the program that can pose diﬃculties with unintended over-
writing, parameter testing and debugging. The single-class variables were passed
between functions individually, resulting in copious amounts of function in- and
output which is hard to keep track of.
In order to circumvent such issues, an object oriented design is employed, by
handling variables using a data struct. A struct is an easily accessible library of
ﬁelds, which can contain a variety of classes such as strings, arrays, cells and even
nested structs. In general, functions accept the data struct as an input, deﬁne and
manipulate single-class variables based on the input, and return designated output
back to the data struct. Besides reducing the issues mentioned above and making
the program more easy to maintain and expand, this design oﬀers an important
advantage with regards to application of the program. It allows for the input struct
to control all program conﬁguration by overruling the data ﬁle. Therefore bearing
optimization can be performed without having to modify the program, with the
added beneﬁt of keeping every program conﬁguration as a potential design variable.
4.1.3.3
Equilibrium convergence issues
Issues with convergence success rate of the equilibrium solver prevented automating
design optimization and posed severe limitations on the range of important design
variables. As a result, a large amount of work has been invested into resolving this
problem.
==PAGE:84==
4.1 The TPJB program
64
In the previous iteration of the program, the equilibrium solver was a multidi-
mensional Newton-Raphson method using forward diﬀerences for approximating
derivatives for the Jacobian matrix. In case of failure to ﬁnd a solution, a routine
called NRHC (Newton-Raphson Help Converge) would be invoked, whose purpose
it was to ﬁnd an initial guess closer to the actual equilibrium position. NRHC
would reduce either the external load or the injection pressure by a set factor,
determine equilibrium for that system, and use the equilibrium as an initial guess
for the actual system.
Despite the assistance from the NRHC routine, convergence to a solution would
only occur in a narrow range of operating conditions, and only if the initial guess
was suﬃciently close the solution. This is the result of incongruity between the
model and convergence limitations of the Newton-Raphson method. The global
convergence of the Newton-Raphson method is inherently poor, and therefore
heavily reliant on the accuracy of the initial guess. Moreover the local convergence
is poor if the function behavior close to the solution is non-smooth or highly
non-linear. In that case, it tends to induce iteration steps which overshoot the
solution resulting in either divergence or perpetually stalled oscillation around the
solution. The following three attributes of the model inﬂuence the local convergence:
1. The pressure distribution is non-smooth due to the hole-function which
imposes a numerically inﬁnite gradient in the boundary between domains
with and without oriﬁces.
2. Oil cavitation invokes the Gümbel boundary condition which suspends the
resulting negative pressures, thereby causing a numerically inﬁnite pressure
gradient and consequently a non-smooth function.
3. The ﬂuid ﬁlm forces determined by integrating the pressure distribution
are highly non-linear functions of the bearing DOFs, especially for a highly
loaded bearing.
As a result, the region for successful local convergence diminishes or even vanishes
by increasing injection pressures, external load, preload factor and any other pa-
==PAGE:85==
4.1 The TPJB program
65
rameter inﬂuencing bearing loading. Furthermore, the equilibrium position and
consequently the necessary initial guess is highly dependent on journal velocity.
Lastly, the Newton-Raphson method is computationally expensive. Each function
evaluation means ﬁnding resultants for each DOF and therefore requires solving
the MRE for each pad. Since the Jacobian matrix must be built using forward
diﬀerences, the number of function evaluations required is one more than the
number of DOFs in the system. For pads with 3 DOFs each, this corresponds to
solving the MRE 3n2
s + 2ns times per iteration, where ns is the number of pads.
Finding equilibrium can easily take 20 iterations, which for a 5-pad bearing means
solving the MRE 1700 times.
The purpose of the program is to be integrated in a rotor-bearing program, thereby
providing a solid foundation for studying and optimizing stability. Therefore it is
critical for the program to have reliable and fast convergence regardless of bearing
design and operating conditions. For the latest iteration of the program, several
initiatives were taken to achieve this:
1. A second hole-function was implemented, which was based on a 2D circular
Gaussian distribution, to obtain a more smooth pressure gradient. This
function would make the otherwise very sparse system matrix full, and
therefore cut-oﬀwas added to the function to minimize the compromise
in solver time. The two hole-functions are visualized in Fig. 4.1 using the
plotting capabilities of the program. The image is a close-up on the oriﬁces
and the mesh is set at a very high resolution to illustrate the diﬀerence in
gradients, which is very easily visible.
2. A new version of the NRHC was developed. The idea behind it was to
reduce the system to have it approach a system in which a good initial
guess was known, and then solve in a sweep from the reduced system to
the actual system, whilst using each solution as the next initial guess. The
routine worked as follows:
Reduce discretization for faster solving
Set external load and injection pressures to zero, forcing the journal

3. An algorithm within the Newton-Raphson code for improving convergence.
It was implemented to address the problem with overshooting as a result
of high bearing loads, which the NRHC routine could not handle. The
strategy of handling overshooting was to only take a fraction of the step
proposed by the Newton-Raphson method. This decreases the rate of
convergence, but also the risk of divergence. The algorithm would keep
track of guesses and corresponding function values for each iteration, to
continuously monitor convergence. If sustained divergence was detected, it
would step back to the best solution on record, decrease the step fraction,
and continue the solver. If sustained convergence was detected it would
increase the step fraction (up to a maximum of a full step), and continue
the solver. The algorithm was optimized by adjusting increase and decrease
factors for the step fraction, as well as how many iterations were required
for detection of sustained divergence and convergence respectively. The
solver would terminate if the step fraction became too small, or if the
number of iterations exceeded a preset number.

These initiatives all improved on the convergence issues. However, the robustness
was still lacking, and was not deemed good enough for automation. It was decided
that the Newton-Raphson method was not good enough. Various methods for
solving nonlinear systems of equations were investigated. One with particular
success was a speciﬁc trust-region method in one of Matlabs native solvers. The
solver is called fsolve, and the method was the trust-region dogleg method. This
method is less costly than Newton-Raphson, since it iteratively updates the Jacobian
rather than computing it at each iteration. It is also able to handle the situation
in which the Jacobian is singular. Most importantly, it has much better chance of
convergence when starting far from the root. The algorithm may also be run in
parallel, which is important for the scalability of the program. Additionally, since
the Jacobian no longer has to be built manually, it is very easy to add DOFs. This
has allowed a simple method for neglecting η or both ξ and η. It has also simpliﬁed
constraining DOFs, which is used to add an option for constraining the journal
horizontally, so that it resembles the 2-pad test-rig at DTU. It is the intention to
add a full feature, which allows for constraining any DOF to any value through
the dataﬁle. The method may also simplify the addition of the energy equation to
account for oil temperature change, which has a major impact as it controls the oil
viscosity.

4.1.3.4
Solving the modiﬁed Reynolds’ equation
In previous iterations of the program, the built-in Matlab function mldivide was
used for solving the MRE for ﬁnding pad pressure distributions. It is an advanced
function, which chooses its solver based on the coeﬃcient matrix A. From on the
documentation, it is concluded that it uses a banded solver, since A is banded with
ﬁve diagonal entries.
While ﬁnding equilibrium, the pad pressure distributions are determined for each of
the numerous function calls made by fsolve. Between function calls the pressure
distribution for a pad only changes slightly, as the system DOFs are changed slowly.
Therefore, for a given function call after the initial one, the solution to the MRE is
well approximated by the last function call. However, mldivide can not use this
information since it is a direct method.
Stationary iterative method can use such information for increasing solver speed.
Therefore the Successive Over-relaxation (SOR) method was implemented. Unfor-
tunately, the speed of determining pressure distributions using SOR compared to
mldivide ranged from being signiﬁcantly lower to marginally better. Continually
overwriting stored pressure distributions is expensive. Furthermore, the convergence
rate of the SOR method depends on the value of the over-relaxation parameter ω,
whose optimal value varies with the properties of the constantly changing A.
In the end mldivide proved to be the superior choice. Interestingly, careful study
of computation time showed that building A took twice as long as actually solving
the system. A was originally initialized using the sparse(m,n) command, which
allocates to memory an m × n all-zero sparse matrix. This is computationally
ineﬃcient because every time a non-zero element is added, the structure of A has
to be redeﬁned. In this version A is initialized using spalloc(m,n,nz), which
allocates to memory an m × n sparse matrix with nz non-zero elements. This
change in allocation single-handedly cut the program execution time in half.

4.3
Rotor-bearing code implementation
The TPJB program is designed for easy implementation in a rotor-bearing code.
Such a code was obtained, courtesy of its author Ph.D. student Jorge Andrés
González Salazar. The mathematical model behind the code will not be derived, as
the author has had no part in its creation. Only a short description of the model
characteristics is given. As mentioned in Section 1.2 a speciﬁc rotor-bearing system
with observed stability issues is given. An illustration of said rotor is shown in
Fig. 4.10.
Figure 4.10: Rotor FEM discretization
The rotor consists of a 2050mm ﬂexible shaft, which has mirror symmetry about
the XY plane intersecting Z through its center. The shaft is modeled using the
ﬁnite element method (FEM), in which 19 nodes (the red dots) which pair-wise
encompass 18 ﬁnite elements (FE) called the shaft elements. The shaft elements
are modeled with Bernouilli-Euler beam elements which approximate the shaft
geometry between nodes using a two-dimensional (XY ) cubic spline shape functions.
The shape functions of two adjacent shaft element are constrained to having the
same value of shape function and shape function derivative evaluated at the
node connecting the two. This ensures a continuous and diﬀerentiable geometric
representation of the entire shaft. The shape function value represents the two
orthogonal translations in X and Y , and the derivative value represents the two
angles Z makes with X and Y . These four values deﬁne the state of the generalized
==PAGE:101==
4.3 Rotor-bearing code implementation
81
coordinate representing each node. This also serves to create a coupling between
adjacent shaft elements at their shared node.
These nodes are used to represent the physics of the system and to couple shaft
elements with generalized coordinates of other machine elements, in this case rigid
discs and TPJBs. For each added machine element, the position of the node must
coincide with that of the machine element. Furthermore, shaft element diameter
must be continuous, so whenever the shaft changes diameter, a node must be placed
to that the change does not happen within a shaft element but rather in between
them.
Starting from the left, the ﬁrst node represent the end of the shaft with a diameter
of 110mm. The second node represents the position of the TPJB. The third node
represents a large rigid disk. The following two nodes represent two of the smaller
rigid disks. The following node represents a change in shaft diameter to 120mm.
The next three nodes represent three of the smaller rigid disks. The next node
represents the center of the shaft from which the following nodes are mirror images

4.4 Current investigations
85
4.4
Current investigations
In the end, the hope for this program package is, that it becomes a useful and
easily applicable tool for optimizing rotor-bearing systems, where instead of brute
forcing design variables, the rich ﬁeld of optimization is used to automatize if not
all then at least parts of the design process. This ﬁnalized combination of TPJB
and rotor-bearing program is now a function that can be called with full control
of all TPJB design variables. The plan is to have a global optimization algorithm
wander in the space of design variables and use the objective function as a compass
to ﬁnd a combination of design variable values that satisﬁes the requirements for
the rotor-bearing system. The above rotor-bearing system will be used as a proof
of concept, where the low damping ratio is to be maximized. Matlab has several
interesting optimization algorithms that are being tried out. The ﬁnal optimization
tool will likely consist of a multi-start routine that gives a number of randomized
initial guesses, and then have either pattern-search or the genetic algorithm begin
at these guesses and mapping the solution space to ﬁnd a suitable local maximum
of the damping ratio. Pattern-search is faster and more robust, but the genetic
algorithm has the distinct advantage that it may work with discrete and bounded
design variables. This means it could test various pad numbers or control the ﬂow
proﬁle variable allowing it to explore the possibility of having a combination of
conventional pads, and pads with radial oil injection. Importantly, both algorithms
are designed for parallel computing, which means they can be sent to a CPU cluster
for much faster solutions.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The majority of the work put into this project has been associated with the theo-
retical and numerical modeling an actively lubricated tilting-pad journal bearing.
The modeling has been an extension of existing models, originating back to the
groundwork laid out in the mid 1990’s [10, 11, 9, 8] by the adviser of this project,
Professor Ilmar Santos.
The ﬁrst main objective was reached after the successful implementation of radial
and transverse degrees of freedom the pads. For this to be possible, the routines for
determining static equilibrium and dynamic coeﬃcients were completely rewritten.
Additionally, the compliance related to the radial pad degrees of freedom was
modeled based on two models of Hertzian contact theory, in accordance with the
cylinder-rocker pivots implemented in the two tilting pad journal bearing test-rigs
at the DTU MEK solid mechanics laboratory. An initial objective was to show,
theoretically as well as experimentally, that pivot compliance could bridge the gap
between theory and experiment in which theory will consistently overestimate the
damping capabilities of the bearing.
This was partially achieved, in that it was shown very clearly in Section 4.2.4, that
a low pivot stiﬀness can have a devastating impact on the dynamic characteristics
of a tilting pad journal bearing. New pivots of lower stiﬀness were also produced,
for impending experimental validation of the predictions.
Eﬀort was put in to investigating the validity of the common approximations to
the ﬁlm thickness function. The exact ﬁlm thickness function was independently
derived to make sure that the approximations reasonable. This also led to the
development of a program based on the derivations, which simultaneously graphi-
==PAGE:107==
87
cally illustrates and mathematically determines the geometrical relationships in a
journal-pad subsystem including the exact ﬁlm thickness function.
Several major changes have been made to the program. Focus has been on making
it diverse with various model conﬁgurations and design variables which can be
chosen in any combination whilst still having the program work. The pivot oﬀset
was added to the model, which is a common and inﬂuential design variable. With
basis in the perturbation method in [1], the routine for determining dynamic coeﬃ-
cients was rewritten to allow for extraction of both full and synchronously reduced
dynamic coeﬃcients. A major game changer for the program was substituting the
ever problematic Newton-Raphson method for determining equilibrium, with a
trust-region method that has outstanding convergence properties and is faster both
on a method basis, and for the fact that it supports parallel computing. Besides
not having to worry about convergence and the costly assembly of the Jacobian,
this new approach allowed constraining generalized coordinates by substituting
their equilibrium position for a reaction force which ensures the constraint. This
made for a more appropriate modeling of the horizontally ﬁxed 1-DOF test rig, and
allows for modeling a variety of specialized constraints. It also facilitated toggling
the new pad degrees of freedom on or oﬀ, allowing for backwards compatibility with
results from the earlier version, and removal of the transverse pad translation which
currently serves no other purpose than to de-scale the model and add computational
eﬀort.
Whilst expanding the program, careful attention was paid to ﬁx the work ﬂow
and data handling in the code which resulted in a program that is consistent
throughout its subroutines. Furthermore, it resulted in a conﬁnement of all case
speciﬁc data to a data ﬁle, which is automatically trumped with the single program
input struct. This made for a core program that is completely case de-coupled,
and which allows for controlling it outside its function call. This in turn greatly
facilitated its implementation in a rotor-bearing code for complete control over
model, design variables and output. The major vision for this, is to create an
automatized bearing optimization tool, which ﬁnds the best bearing for a given
rotor under user-speciﬁed constraints, design variables and objective function. A
concept the author intends to pursue, be it an academical or a recreational activity.
The verbosity of this written work is extensive, especially for a technical thesis.
The author recognizes that academics is a continuous group eﬀort. The program
and its underlying model are sought to be well documented, such that this report
may serve to excel the work ﬂow of the next student who gets the pleasure to work
on this particular subject.
==PAGE:108==
Chapter 6
Future work
Over the course of this project, many ideas for program capabilities have been
generated. Some have been implemented and some have been rejected. A few have
been recognized as good ideas, but were not implemented due to time constraints
of this project. Here the latter ideas are presented.
Pivot support
The implantation of the ξ and η DOFs in the code has cleared the way for a great
many interesting ﬁelds of study. First, rigorous experimental data, preferably
for various TPJB test-rigs, is needed to validate and update the support model.
With experimental data, the support models can be chosen to most accurately
predict the behavior of the bearing being designed or identify issues with existing
bearings. Hertzian contact models are fairly easy to implement, and multiple
models exist, which vary due to diﬀering initial assumptions made during their
derivation. Alternatively, FEM models could be used. These would need to be
reduced in size, to not hinder the very useful design aspect of having a fast TPJB
program. A reduction could either be in regression ﬁt to a closed form model. It
could also be a FEM model reduction, a ﬁeld of research which is currently very
active. It would also be interesting to include a viscous model to account for the
internal friction caused by pivot deformation. Lastly, torsional and direct spring
==PAGE:109==
89
models for α and η respectively along with damping characteristics associated with
those DOFs could be investigated.
Pad design variables
The program should be expanded to allow more freedom in choice of pad design
variables. This project has made progress in that regard. Prior to the project,
the only pad speciﬁc variable, were the position φ and the injection pressure
Pinj for each pad. The pad speciﬁc variables introduced in this project are pivot
stiﬀness (and all the material variables associated with it), pivot oﬀset, and the
hole-function. However, hole-distribution (with individual hole-diameters, nozzle
lengths and injection pressures), arc length β0, inner surface radius RS, FD mesh,
pad thickness ∆, pad mass mS and pad mass moment of inertia IP are all variable
which could be made pad speciﬁc. As design variable, some may be more useful
than others, but there is no reason the option should not be available.
Numerical enhancement
Making some or all parts of the program dimensionless, could reduce round-oﬀ
errors and numerical instabilities which arise from solving ill-conditioned matrix
systems. If not possible for all aspects of the program, at least it should be done
for solving the modiﬁed Reynold’s Equation.
Better assembly of the coeﬃcient matrix. Although the process time was halved
in this project by proper storage allocation to the sparse coeﬃcient matrix, the
coeﬃcients are still distributed in a loop. A much faster way of doing this, is using
the concept of triplets. Triplets are 3-column arrays with the same number of rows
as there are non-zero elements in the coeﬃcient matrix. Each triplet (one row,
three columns) contain an element value and two indices pointing to where in the
coeﬃcient matrix the element is to be distributed. With a simple Matlab command
these triplets may be stored in the coeﬃcient matrix. Since Matlab has all the
triplets at once, it can distribute them in a much more eﬃcient way than when
having to loop over the coeﬃcient matrix and update its deﬁnition each time an
element is added. It is made even more eﬃcient because the indices do not change
over a program call. The elements are always placed at the same point in the
coeﬃcient matrix. Therefore it would only be necessary to build the index vector
==PAGE:110==
90
once, and from there on only the elements would have to be computed. There is
no doubt that this would make the computation much faster.
Energy equation
The model assumes isoviscous (IV) conditions with a predeﬁned viscosity. This
is the simplest, fastest and most incorrect method of handling the problem of oil
viscosity. For a validating against an physical bearing, it can be representative,
assuming that the outlet oil temperature can be measured accurately and used
to predict oil viscosity. For designing a bearing, this is not possible, as physical
measurements on a theoretical bearing is a self-contradicting concept.
There are several ways of accounting for temperature in a TPJB model. A simple
way is to include the energy equation, with heat generated by the oil friction at
the pad being distributed through the oil ﬁlm. One can take into account that the
heated oil which escapes from the outlet will be carried to the next pad, thereby
thermally coupling the pads. Under the assumption of IV conditions, the modiﬁed
Reynolds’ Equation can be solved, then the energy equation may be evaluated
and used to update the oil temperature and thereby the viscosity. The modiﬁed
Reynolds’ Equation is then solved again with the new viscosity, and the process
is repeated until the viscosity converges. This method is widely used, and should
be implemented in the program in a fashion such that it can be toggled on or oﬀ.
Naturally, this additional step increases the solution time, so if the program is
run over a number of variables that do not impact bearing temperature much, the
designer could make due with a single viscosity evaluation, and then toggle it oﬀ
for the remainder of the computation.
It is also possible to not assume IV conditions, and solve the system with a viscosity
distribution. Such models are classiﬁed as thermo-hydrodynamic (THD) models.
This could also be implemented but it is a large endeavor. The typical cutting edge
research models are the thermo-elasto-hydrodynamic (TEHD) models, which also
use ﬁnite element modeling to account for thermal expansion and ﬂexibility of the
pad. Turning the program into such a model is likely out of scope for any thesis in
the near future.
==PAGE:111==
91
Reynolds boundary condition
Currently, the handling of cavitation is done with the simple Gumbel boundary
condition, which identiﬁes negative pressures and sets them to zero, as they are
non-physical and usually only occur numerically during equilibrium solving, but
do not appear in the actual solution. Actual physical cavitation is usually limited
to closed journal bearings such as the lobe-bearings, lemon-bore and especially
the full cylindrical journal bearing. However, for a very heavily loaded TPJB,
cavitation can occur in the region of the oil ﬁlm immediately after the maximum
pressure. In this case, the Gumbel condition is not only non-physical, but also a
major computational issue since it forces a pressure discontinuity at the point of
cavitation which causes an inﬁnite pressure gradient at the same point. Computers
have a very diﬃcult time dealing with inﬁnity, whether it is inﬁnitely large or
inﬁnitely small. It can cause convergence failure when solving for equilibrium, and
worse yet, it may predict strange equilibrium positions and dynamic behavior of
the pad.
For bearings with cavitation issues, the Reynolds’ boundary condition is typically
used. The Reynolds boundary condition requires the program to solve the modiﬁed
Reynold’s Equation a few more times per iteration for the cavitated pad, but in
return it ensures that the pressure gradient at the point of cavitation is zero. In
other words, it performs condition based iterations which smooths the pressure
distribution such that it falls asymptotically towards zero at the cavitation point.
Implementing this should be done as a conﬁgurable feature, which can be turned
oﬀavoid the extra computations when there is no risk of cavitation. The beneﬁts
would include faster and more accurate equilibrium predictions for TPJBs in risk of
cavitation. Furthermore, it might allow for a wider range of TPJBs to be modeled
that would otherwise cause unavoidable convergence failure. In a physical sense,
Reynolds’ boundary condition is still far from a correct prediction, but it does do
better than the Gumbel condition in cavitated cases.
