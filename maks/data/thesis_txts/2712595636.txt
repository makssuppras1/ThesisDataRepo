Preface
This thesis was written at the Department of Health Technology at the Technical University of Denmark to fulﬁl
the requirements for acquiring the M.Sc. in Medicine and Technology. This project investigates the use of a mobile
EEG device in brain computer interfaces for neurorehabilitation of stroke patients and deals with the related signal
processing. The work took place during the period from January 2022 - June 2022.

==PAGE:4==
Abstract
Brain computer interfaces can be used in neurorehabilitation of stroke patients in which the aim is to induce
neuroplasticity by providing the brain with a feedback during MI exercises. Here it is important that the BCI can
diﬀerentiate between motor intentions and provide the patient with a real time response. This thesis investigates
if it is feasible to use the MENTALAB Explore (which is a mobile EEG device) in MI based BCI systems. This
is done by evaluating the user experience and whether or not motor intentions can be identiﬁed and classiﬁed
correctly. The feature extraction was based on FBCSP and AM and the classiﬁer was an SVM. The results of the
classiﬁcation scheme were inconclusive since the model was overﬁtting. The user experience evaluation found some
issues that needs to be addressed if the device should be used for MI-based BCI system for neurorehabilitation of
stroke patients.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Stroke is one of the leading causes of death and disability worldwide [1][2]. It is caused by cerebrovascular disease
and can be divided into ischaemic and hemorrhagic stroke of which the prior is the most common accounting for
about 80% of the stroke incidents and the latter, while being the most fatal [3], only accounts for about 20% of the
stoke incidents [1].
Post stroke the area of irreversible damage will result in a loss of function e.g. motor deﬁcits, dysarthria and
aphasia (deﬁcits of language) and cognitive deﬁcits. The degree of loss of function varies with both the size of the
damaged area as well as the location. Post stroke management aims at preventing future incidents as well as early
rehabilitation [3].
Motor deﬁcits are the most common and rehabilitation of motor function includes physical activities, such as passive
range-of-motion exercises [3][4] and motor-skill exercises, as well as technology-assisted physical activities such as
functional electrical stimulation (FES) [4]. These activities aims at improving strength, coordination and range of
motion as well as recovering motor function.
Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) have been suggested as another approach to the rehabilitation of stroke patients
and it has been showed that these outperform the conventional rehabilitation methods [2].
A BCI obtains a
signal from the brain and extract features for classiﬁcation in order to provide the patient with feedback during
rehabilitative exercise. The aim is to induce neuroplasticity by providing repetition to reinforce neuronal circuits
as well as positive feedback when a task has been performed correctly and negative feedback when it has not; The
feedback provided can be e.g. visual or sensory.1 A sensory feedback is an attempt to close the sensory-motor loop
which is lost if a patient is not able to execute a certain motor function and thus, does not receive the sensory
feedback following motion; Here the patient uses motor imagery (MI) in order to activate the sensory feedback.2
This feedback mechanism is based on the Hebbian learning principle stating that ’Neurons that ﬁre together wire
together’[2] i.e. if a neuron A successfully leads to activation of neuron B this activation will become more eﬃcient.
Regardless of the chosen feedback mechanism it is important, that the BCI can diﬀerentiate between motor intentions
and provide the patient with a real time response.
In this thesis it is investigated whether a new mobile EEG, the MENTALAB Explore, can be used in MI based BCI
systems. To this end feature extraction and classiﬁcation algorithms within the area of MI BCI based systems are
investigated and a scheme is chosen to evaluate the the MENTALAB device.
The next two chapters covers the theoretical background for this work. Chapter 4 reviews the methods and results
from other studies and presents the method used in this work. Chapter 5 covers the signal processing background
for the chosen scheme and chapter 6 present the results. The ﬁnal chapter is dedicated to an evaluation of the work
done and suggestions on future work.
1A visual feedback could be an image on a screen or a light. A sensory feedback could be FES resulting in muscle activation, a
robotic arm moving a limb or a vibrating device.
2MI is the imagnation of movement and sensation of movement i.e. the subject is simulating movement without physical movement
output.

Chapter 2
Clinical Background
In this chapter the basic anatomy of the nervous system will be described. This will include a brief introduction
to the peripheral nervous system (PNS) but will focus more on describing the central nervous system (CNS) and
organisation of motor function. Brain functions will be mentioned and the anatomy and function of the neuron will
also be described as well as the eﬀects of stroke on brain function. Current treatment and rehabilitation regimes
are also described in this chapter. The ﬁnal section of this chapter will describe how brain signals are measured
focusing on the EEG.
2.1
Anatomy of the nervous system
The nervous system can be anatomically divided into the CNS and the PNS. The CNS consists of the brain and
the spinal cord and is responsible for interpreting sensory input and producing responses to sensory input or motor
outputs i.e. movements. The PNS is responsible for carrying sensory output from to the CNS and motor output
from the CNS to the target e.g. skeletal muscle, cardiac muscle or smooth muscle; Smooth muscle is found in e.g.
glands. The PNS thus consists of the nerves outside the spinal cord and their ganglia1 [5].
2.1.1
The CNS
The CNS is composed of neurons and neuroglial cells. The neuron is the functional unit of both the CNS and PNS
and allows for communication i.e. signal transmission such as motor output to muscles or signal reception such as
sensory feedback. The neuroglial cells are supporting cells and there are four types of neuroglial cells in the CNS.
These are the astrocytes, ependymal cells, microglial cells and oligodendrocytes. The astrocytes are responsible
for repair and scar tissue formation in the brain after damage, they maintain the tight junction of the capillaries
which creates the blood-brain barrier protecting the brain from infections, and take up neurotransmitters after
they have been released by neurons to help regulate the synaptic activity. In addition, they can communicate with
the neurons modulating or supporting their activity. The ependymal cells from the lining of the neural cavities in
which the cerebrospinal ﬂuid (CSF) ﬂows and combine with vascular networks to form the choroid plexus. CSF is
produced in the choroid plexus and provides a protective cushion for the CNS [6] [7]. The microglial cells clear oﬀ
debris from infections, tissue damage or cell death. The oligodendrocytes are responsible for creating myelin sheets
around the axon on multiple neurons as opposed to the Schwann cell found in the PNS which can only create a
single myelin sheet. Myelin allows for saltatory propagation of a signal down the axon thereby resulting in faster
signal transmission [6]. The tissues of the CNS is categorised into grey matter and white matter. Grey matter
is composed of neuron soma ad dendrites which are not covered in myelin and form the brain cortex and clusters
within the brain called nuclei. The white matter is the myelin covered axons forming the conducting pathways
namely the nerve tracts of the CNS [5]. Anatomically the CNS is divided into the brain and the spinal cord.
1A ganglion is a cluster of neuron somas found outside the CNS and makes up the grey matter of the PNS while the nerves (clusters
of parallel axons) correspond to the white matter of the PNS.

2.1.2
Anatomy of the brain
This section describes the anatomy of the brain as illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1 shows the basic anatomy of the brain.
Figure 2.1: Medial view of the brain showing the anatomy of the brain. Image taken from [8].
The brainstem
The brainstem is a structure that connects the spinal cord and the brain. The spinal cord is the link between the
CNS and PNS and will be covered in a later section. The brain stem can be divided into the midbrain, pons and
medulla oblongata [8].
The medulla oblongata contains ascending and descending nerve tracts to allow for signal transmission to and from
the brain via the nerve tracts found in the spinal cord and the nerves of the PNS. In addition to this, the medulla
oblongata contains nuclei responsible for the regulation of heart rate, breathing, blood vessel diameter, balance,
coordination and other reﬂex functions such as coughing, sneezing, vomiting and swallowing [8].
The pons contains ascending and descending nerve tracts and some parts of the medulla oblongata extend into the
pons giving it similar functions. In addition to this, the pons serves as a bridge between the cerebellum and the
cerebrum relaying information between the two structures [8].

Within the midbrain are found structures named colliculi. These are found as inferior and superior pairs. The

involved in cyclic motor functions (walking, breathing etc.), state of consciousness and regulating the sleep-wake

The cerebellum
The cerebellum functions as a comparator that compares motor intention from the motor cortex of the cerebrum
with the proprioceptive information from joints, tendons and muscles within the body providing information of the
position of relevant body parts. If the information from these sources is diﬀerent the cerebellum will detect this and
correct this by sending potentials to motor neurons in the motor cortex and the spinal cord. The cerebellum is thus
responsible for coordinating ﬁne movements and for keeping movements smooth. It is also involved in maintaining
balance and muscle tone and in learning motor skills [9].
Diencephalon
The diencephalon is located superior to the brain stem and is composed of several structures. The main structures
of the diencephalon are the thalamus, epithalamus and hypothalamus. The thalamus sends sensory input from the
brainstem to the cerebral cortex and is involved in pain perception and mood. The epithalamus contains the pineal
gland which is thought to inﬂuence the onset of puberty and control long-term cycles inﬂuenced by light level i.e.
light-dark cycles. In addition to this, the epithalamus contains nuclei involved in the visceral and emotional response
to odours. The hypothalamus plays a major role in endocrine function i.e. the regulation of hormone secretion
from the pituitary gland and control of the autonomic nervous system (ANS) which is the part of the nervous
system related to ”ﬁght-or-ﬂight” (sympathetic part) and ”rest-and-digest” (parasympathetic part) functions as
well as sensations related to basic needs; It is responsible for maintaining homeostasis i.e. body temperature, blood
gas concentration (respiratory rate), water balance, food consumption etc. It is also involved in some emotional
response [10] [11].

2.3
Stroke
Stoke is a term for acute focal neurological deﬁcit caused by ischaemia or hemorrhage in the brain. Ischaemic
stroke is the result of a blood cloth and most common type; It accounts for about 85 % of all cases and is less
severe than hemorrhagic stroke which is the result of either direct bleeding into the brain tissue (10 % of all stroke
cases) or a subarachnoid hemorrhage (5 % of all stroke cases). Stroke leads to loss of brain function where 50 % of
the patients demonstrate some degree of disability while 15 % are severely disabled; The post stroke the mortality
rate is about 20 % during the ﬁrst month. Treatment of stroke can be divided in two phases: 1) The acute phase
which aims at diagnosis and quick treatment to limit the damage to the brain tissue reducing the resulting loss of
function (disability) and 2) the post stroke management phase which focus on rehabilitation of function loss as well
as prevention of new incidents by addressing risk factors (see table 2.3 in section 2.3.4) [21] [22].
2.3.1
Ischaemic stroke
Ischaemic stroke is caused by cerebrovascular obstruction i.e. obstruction of blood vessels in the brain by thrombosis
or emboli; Thrombi and emboli most frequently form as a result of atherosclerosis. Atherosclerosis leads to the
formation of plaque in the blood vessel and damage of the vessel wall which triggers mechanisms leading to the
formation of thrombi. An embolus is a thrombus (or part of a thrombus) which has formed in another part of the
circulation and was carried by the blood ﬂow to a new site where it obstruct blood ﬂow; Emboli in brain arteries
usually arise from the carotid arteries or the heart. Thrombi can form in areas of the heart with low blood ﬂow due
to e.g. arterial ﬁbrillation or valve disease. Figure 2.12 illustrates the most common sites from which cerebrovascular
obstruction can arise [21] [22].
When an artery in the brain is obstructed an ischaemic focus will develop. In this area the blood supply is insuﬃcient
for the neurons and glial cells to retain normal function and metabolism and a cascade of processes are initialised
resulting in cell death. These includes the formation of oxygen radicals, release of glutamate in toxic levels and
an increase in the intracellular calcium concentration. Surrounding the ischaemic focus is the ischaemic penumbra
which is an area of marginal blood supply; Normal cell metabolism is impaired and the neurons cannot retain their
function. The damage to this area is reversible depending on how quickly blood ﬂow can be reestablished, the
volume of toxic product released by dying cells, cerebral edema and alterations in local blood ﬂow. If blood supply
is not restored cell death will occur in the penumbra. Post stroke the infarct will be removed by macrophages
leaving behind a cavity [21] [22].
2.3.2
Hemorrhagic stroke
Hemorrhagic stroke is caused by the rupture of a blood vessel in the brain. This results in a focal hematoma,
edema, spasm of adjacent blood vessels and compression of the brain contents. The rupture can occur suddenly
when a person is active and is accompanied by headache and vomiting at the onset. Causes of hemorrhage are
hypertension, advanced age, trauma, erosion of vessels by tumours, blood coagulation disorders, vasculitis, drugs,
aneurysms and arteriovenous malformations. If the hemorrhage is severe enough the intracranial pressure will cause
the cerebellum and the brain stem to be pushed through foramen magnum3 leading to ischaemia in the brain stem;
The brain stem controls breathing, circulation and wakefulness and thus, the clinical course of hemorrhagic stroke
will progress rapidly to coma and frequently death [21] [22].

2.3.3
Diagnosis and treatment
Acute stroke management aims at determining the cause of the stroke and stabilise the patient in order to limit
brain tissue damage. The diagnosis is based on symptoms, patient history and neurological examination. Symptoms
occur suddenly and vary depending on the aﬀected brain area; If the visual cortex is involved vision will be aﬀected,
an infarct located at a. cerebri medias in the left hemisphere will manifest as speech impediment and right sided
hemiparesis and if the infarct is located in the right hemisphere neglect often occurs. A CT or MR scan of the brain
is done to determine whether the stroke is caused by ischaemia or a hemorrhage. Treatment of ischaemic stroke
include re-perfusion techniques such as thrombolytic therapy (at the latest 4.5 hours after symptoms occurred) or
invasive catheter based methods. In some occurrences of hemorrhagic stroke it is possible to drain the blood [21]
[22].
2.3.4

phase aims at preventing new occurrences by administering anti-platelet agents or anticoagulants or by treating
other health related risk factors such as diabetes or hypertension. Preventative measures also address risk factors
related to lifestyle such as smoking, obesity or alcohol use (see table 2.3) [21] [22].

2.4
Clinical measurements
Clinical measurements of the brain include both anatomical and functional measurements. CT and MRI are used
to examine the anatomy of the brain e.g. with the aim of locating bleeding or infarcts in stroke patients [21].
Functional measurements are used to examine brain activity and can be used for signal acquisition for BCIs.
Examples of functional measurements are fMRI or EEG of which EEG is the most commonly used modality.
2.4.1
The EEG
The changes in electrical potentials over the membrane of the neurons described in section 2.2 result in an electrical
ﬁeld which can be measured via electrodes placed on the scalp as is done in EEG measurements [23].
The signal obtained with a single electrode (or channel) from the EEG is a sum of changes in postsynaptic potentials
for millions of neurons. Each electrode is placed in accordance with the 10-20 electrode placement system4 and each
channel captures the brain activity in the area of the corresponding electrode. On the EEG recording the change
in brain activity over time presents as brain waves or rhythms as seen in ﬁgure 2.13b.
The brain waves illustrated in ﬁgure 2.13b are dependent on the attention level, consciousness and age of a subject.
Beta waves are observed during wakefulness and the frequency range of these is 13-30 Hz.5 Beta waves occur during
active thinking and attention e.g. during problem solving and central beta rhythms can be blocked by motor or
sensory activity. Alpha rhythms occur within the frequency range of 8-13 Hz in a wakeful state. The alpha activity
becomes more prominent during a wakeful state where eyes are closed and less prominent when performing mental
tasks i.e. during concentration and attention. The theta waves occur during drowsiness and deep meditation within
the frequency range of 4-8 Hz and is more prominent in infants and children. Delta waves are found during deep
sleep and has a frequency range of 0.5-4 Hz[25].6

In addition to the brain rhythms described above the raw EEG contains noise or artefacts originating from other
sources. These sources can be physiological or non-physiological; Examples of physiological artefact sources are
the EOG (electrooculography) aﬀecting the EEG during eye blinking or eye rolling, the EMG (electromyography)

interference from other electrical components, cable defects or the electrodes e.g. if the impedances are ﬂuctuating

spike with a frequency of the heart rate which is about 1 Hz and an amplitude of 1-10 mV and EOG artefacts lie
within a 0.5-3 Hz frequency band and have amplitudes of 100 mV; The frequency band of these artefats overlap
with the EEG and the amplitudes are higher as well. The power line noise is observed as 50 or 60 Hz low amplitude
oscillations in the EEG while interference from electrical devices can present with very high amplitudes. Artefacts
can also present with similar morphology to signals of interest[28] thus, hiding the signal and requiring the use of
noise reduction methods to increase the SNR (signal-to-noise ratio).

The sensorimotor rhythms (SMRs) (i.e. the central beta and the mu rhythms) relate to the planning, preparation
and execution of movements and to sensory function. Change these rhythms are observed during tactile stimulation,
actual movement and MI. The change in activity can be observed as a suppression or blocking of the mu and central
beta rhythms when the sensorimotor cortex is processing sensory input or performing motor related tasks; This is
termed as event-related desynchronisation (ERD). When movement seizes and during relaxation the activity in the
mu and beta bands increases; This is termed event-related synchronisation (ERS)[30][31].

in the motor area are shown for the left hemisphere and the cortical locations of sensory input sources is shown for
the right hemisphere[32].

3.2.2
MI based BCIs for neurorehabilitation
Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) have been suggested as an approach to the rehabilitation of stroke patients and it
has been showed that these outperform the conventional rehabilitation methods[2]. In this case the BCI provide the
brain with feedback during rehabilitative exercises which aim to induce neuroplasticity. Neuroplasticity depends on
both repetition and feedback mechanisms to reinforce neuronal circuits. For motor functions a sensory feedback is
given to the brain when a movement is performed. A positive feedback will strengthen connections between neurons
while a negative feedback weakens the connections. For stroke patient this feedback loop can be broken due to
loss of function and the aim of BCI implementation is thus to close the feedback loop by providing an external
application for sensory feedback in response to MI. The sensory feedback can be provided by e.g. FES resulting in
muscle activation, a robotic arm moving a limb or a vibrating device. In this case it is important that the BCI can
diﬀerentiate between motor intentions and provide the patient with a real time response[2].

4.1
State of the art
Initially a search on 1)”multi-class” OR ”multiclass” OR ”multi class” AND ”MI” OR ”motor imagery” AND
”real-time” OR ”real time”, 2)”multi-class” OR ”multiclass” OR ”multi class” AND ”MI” OR ”motor imagery”
and 3)”multi-class” OR ”multiclass” OR ”multi class” AND ”MI” OR ”motor imagery” AND ”BCI” OR ”brain-
computer interface” OR ”brain computer interface” was carried out to ﬁnd studies relevant to this thesis. A total of
46 articles were found and subsequently sorted by applied classiﬁcation method. In a majority of the articles CNN
or SVM was applied for classiﬁcation of MI signals. In addition to this LDA was also a common classiﬁers. Other
methods such as Na¨ıve Bayes, KNN (k-nearest neighbours), SAE (stacked auto-encoders) or other deep learning
networks were less common and only applied in very few of the studies. In order to limit the content of this section
articles covering less commonly used classiﬁcation schemes as well as articles from 2016 or earlier has been omitted.
DBNs were also found among the less commonly used classiﬁers in this search however an article by Craik et al [35]
reviewed deep learning methods for EEG classiﬁcation tasks recommending that CNN and DBN based methods
should be applied for MI classiﬁcation tasks. For this reason the articles on DBNs were included in this section as
well. A summary of all the articles can be found in table 4.1.
4.1.1
CNN and DBN based methods
A total of 10 studies used a CNN based classiﬁcation schemes and 3 were based on DBNs. Input features for the
DBNs were obtained using a FT based algorithm to obtain power spectral densities which were either normalised or
used directly as input features for the DBN. The highest accuracy obtained using DBN schemes was 84 %(Lu et al
[36]). This study used dataset 2a from BCI competition IV which contains 4-class MI for 9 subjects recorded across
22 channels. The frequency spectrum was calculated using FFT and subsequently normalised and used as training
features for the DBN. The other two studies built a classiﬁcation tree from DBN based binary classiﬁers (Hassanpour
et al [37]) or applied a scheme similar to Lu et al (Chu et al [38]). The latter study simulated incomplete data by
removing data points or chunks in the pre processing step and achieved accuracies of 70.72 % and 68.86 %.
In the studies concerning CNN based classiﬁers features included, Raw EEG, images, PSD, CSP features or other
calculated features such as signal envelopes. Image features were obtained by investigating the frequency content
of the signal(Fadel et al[39] and Wang et al[40]) or from a CSP approach were FFTEM was used to create energy
maps from CSP features (Abbas et al[41]). Wang et al[40] used STFT to generate images for classiﬁcation. These
images were resized prior to classiﬁcation using the CNN and performance was evaluated for diﬀerent activation
functions. This study obtained the highest mean accuracy (of 92.73 %) with SELU as activation function. Of the
studies using raw EEG signals Lun et al[42] achieved the highest accuracy of 97.28 % for 20 subjects. In this study
a CNN was trained on 9 electrode pairs of raw EEG signals using data from 10, 20, 60 and 100 subjects. Other
studes achieving high accuracies using CNNs were Ma et al[43] (with a mean accuracy of 96.21 %) and Tang et
al[44] (with a mean accuracy of 91.9 %). Ma et al separated the data into 5 subbands and used DWT to calculate

4.2
Proposed method
The aim of this thesis is to evaluate the feasibility of using MENTALABs EEG for MI based BCI systems. To this
end data have been collected using the MENTALAB EEG.
4.2.1
MENTALAB data
This data set was collected using the MENTALAB Explore which is a mobile dry electrode EEG[54]. 19 subjects
participated in the data collection all of which signed a written consent form. All subjects were healthy and MI
na¨ıve. For the data collection 8 electrodes were placed at C3, Cz, C4, FC3, FCz, FC4, CP1 and CP2 This placement
is not optimal for the MI tasks performed during this data collection (left and right hand MI) however, it was initially
the plan to compare this data to another data set no longer included in this work. The electrode placement for the
MENTALAB device was chosen so all 8 channels were comparable between the two data sets.
The data was collected over one session for each subject consisting of 5 runs each lasting 6 min with a 2 min.
break in between. Before the data collection started subjects were required to perform a 3 min. practice run to
familiarise themselves with the procedure. They were required to perform MI of the left and right hand and were
guided through each trial by an interface providing visual and auditory cues (see ﬁgure 4.2).
Figure 4.2: The interface used for guiding the subject through the trials.
A single trial takes 10 seconds and the following cues are given:
• A beep marks the start of a trial and a yellow dot appears (2 seconds)
• A visual cue for right or left hand MI is provided (1 second)
• The visual cue (left or right hand) disappears and MI is performed for 5 seconds
• The yellow dot disappears and the subject can rest his/her eyes for 2 seconds

Attractor metagene
The attractor metagene algorithm calculates metafeatures from a weighted sum based on mutual information. The
metagenes are calculated as follows [50][57]:
Mi = wi · G
(5.3.1)

the number of samples and p is the number of genes (or in this work CSP features).

wj,i+1 = J(Mi, Gj)

greater than zero. Here I(Mi, Gj) is the mutual information between two genes in the range [0; 1] and α is any
non-negative number.
The algorithm runs until the change in the weights, wi, becomes less than a deﬁned tolerance or the maximum
number of iterations has been reached.

The MENTALAB system comes as an electrode cap, 8 pin-electrodes and the mobile EEG device (MENTALAB
Explore). The electrodes have wires with diﬀerent length and some of these are very short and can potentially limit

had to be reattached when ﬁtting the cap to a new subject or even in between runs with the same subject if they
had moved during the break. With some of the taller subject the connection between the computer and the device
was lost during data collection and the connection was not restored. Due to this some subjects had to be moved
closer to the computer or sit diﬀerently on the chair e.g. with a less straight back or bad posture. The device also
seemed very noise sensitive; If the person overseeing the data acquisition moved a bit it showed up as noise on the
measurements.
After data acquisition the subjects answered a few questions related to the cap and MI experience. Some subjects
found that the cap and electrodes were itchy or hurt distracting them from the MI task; the latter of these is
attributed to a cap that is too small since this was done using a medium sized cap and some subjects needed a
large. Most subjects were not bothered by the cap.

7.1
Discussion
The mean accuracies presented in table 6.1 indicate overﬁtting of the model. During training the model attempt to
ﬁnd an optimal time interval, number of features and SVM classiﬁer. When introducing more parameters to tune
when ﬁtting a model to a set of training data the chance of overﬁtting is increased. In the optimisation of the SVM
K-fold cross validation was used to minimise overﬁtting where K = 5 since this provided the best results for the
algorithm. One could tune the value of K based on the leave-one-out cross (LOV) validation which corresponds to
the case were K equal the number of samples K = N; In this approach an optimal K is chosen based on the mean
classiﬁcation accuracies of the LOV and K-fold cross-validations that are closest. Another optimisation approach
for the SVM could be the bat algorithm used by Selim et al [50] achieving a classiﬁcation accuracy of 85.01 %
for a 2-class 18-channel MI data set. It could also be argued that trying to ﬁnd an optimal time interval could
induce some amount of overﬁtting in the model; If diﬀerent trials for the same subject have diﬀerent optimal time
intervals it is possible that an optimal time interval for the test set is from 1.5 to 4.5 s while it is 0.5 to 3.5 s for the
training set. This could reduce the performance on the test data. In the proposed model the optimal time interval
is chosen as the time interval for which the model obtained the highest training accuracy. If the model achieves a
training accuracy at 100 % in the ﬁrst interval for a given subject (as observed in table 6.1) and again in the fourth
interval for the same subject then the ﬁrst interval is chosen as the optimal interval. It is possible that the number
of features in interval four is 10 while it is 16 in the ﬁrst interval; This makes the the fourth interval the optimal
one if the aim is to use as few features as possible.
In an attempt to evaluate the use of the MENTALAB data trial and error approaches for selecting the number
of features were used; Here the number of features were manually selected and features were still sorted by AM
weights. Other approaches limited the feature search to features above speciﬁc AM weights. While these approaches
resulted higher classiﬁcation accuracies for the test set the trial and error approach also revealed that the relation
between the number of features and the accuracies for both test and training set did not follow the expectation.
It is expected that the accuracies increases as more features are added to the model. This reaches a peak when
all important features are selected. The accuracies are then expected to decrease as more features are introduced
since these additional features add noise when model parameters are obtained. If the relation between accuracies
and number of selected features does not follow this expectation it could be that the AM does not ﬁnd the most
important features in this data set. When the SVM was restricted to the Gaussian Kernel, pairs were not chosen
and restrictions on feature selections were removed some test and train accuracy pairs presented with the same
overﬁtting patterns of 100 % training accuracy to chance level or below chance level test accuracies.
Based on the observations done during this work it seems feasible that the MENTALAB device can be used for
BCI implementations (some higher accuracies were obtained) however, the algorithm used in this work needs to be
altered or changed completely. Suggestions for alterations would be to use the bat algorithm for optimisation and
follow the procedure by Selim et al [50] completely since this method has yielded high accuracies for three MI data
sets. Another approach could be a CNN based method such as the one by Wang et al [40] were STFT was used to

Regarding the use of the device some problem were encountered as described in section 6.2. The loss of connection

7.2
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to investigate the feasibility of using the MENTALAB Explore for a MI-EEG based BCI
system for rehabilitation of stroke patients. Based on the work by Selim et al [50] an algorithm for extracting MI
features and classifying these was proposed. This algorithm was applied to a benchmark data set and a data set
obtained from 19 subjects using the MENTALAB device. The model overﬁtted and the results of this part were
inconclusive with respect to whether the MENTALAB device can be used in a MI-Based BCI system. During the
data acquisition some problems were experienced with the device. While easy to ﬁt electrodes would fall out of the
cap or the device lost its connection to the computer. These issues needs to be addressed before the device could
be used in a system.
7.3
Suggestions for future work
Since this work did not yield any useful conclusions on the extraction and classiﬁcations of the algorithm a suggestion
for future work could be 1) altering the algorithm as mentioned in the discussion or 2) ﬁnd an alternative method
such as CNN-based methods for the classiﬁcation. If a successful method is found and the issues with the user
experience are ﬁxed applying the method on patient data could be interesting.
