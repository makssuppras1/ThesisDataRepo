Abstract
Light field imaging has become anticipated due to a high angular resolution. However,
there is a trade-off between spatial and angular resolution. Light field images may
offer a high angular resolution, while it may also sacrifice spatial resolution and occupy
a large storage space. Aiming at reducing the redundancy in light field views and
simultaneously acquiring high angular-spatial resolution, we propose a learning-based
and occlusion-aware method for light field novel view synthesis. The proposed method
simply uses four corner views of a 4D light field to synthesize any in-between views.
it consists of a pretrained model for optical flow estimation and dual convolutional
neural networks, namely a selection network for view selection, and an inpainting
network for occlusion refinement. Quantitative and qualitative results show that the
proposed method synthesizes high-quality light field novel views, which are superior
to other state-of-the-art approaches on a challenging light field dataset with high
baselines. Furthermore, the proposed method is time-eﬀicient in synthesizing novel
light field views.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and Problem Statement
Light field(LF) imaging is a technology which captures the whole light information
including spatial and angular information. A LF image can be considered as a series
of conventional 2D images collection from different viewpoints arranged on a regular
grid.
However, one major challenge in LF imaging is that it occupies a large storage
space, causing diﬀiculties in storage and streaming. A LF image with 624×432 spatial
resolution and 15 × 15 angular resolution captured by commercial level camera Lytro
II requires an average 178MB of storage space after decoding[DPW13]. Whereas, each
view of one LF image captures the same scene from various viewpoints, which results
in highly redundant spatial information. Another challenge is the trade-off between
spatial and angular resolution. LF images may offer a high angular resolution, but
it may also sacrifice spatial resolution. For example, LF image captured by Lytro II
only offers 624 × 432 pixel resolution, which is much less than 2D image taken by
normal camera. Therefore, an eﬀicient LF reconstruction maintaining a high visual
quality is a well-known and popular topic in the research field.
To minimize the LF image storage and simultaneously preserve the image quality,
some research works consider lossless compression schemes, specifically, exploiting
the inherent correlations and similarities of the neighbour-view imagery to reduce
redundant data via lossless compression algorithms. A 4-D LF image is composed of
a matrix of rectified and decoded 2-D lenslet images captured by a lenslet based LF
camera. Therefore, two main perspectives can be considered in LF lossless compres-
sion schemes, namely compressing the raw captured lenslet images and compressing
the rectified LF images.
Perra et al.
proposed a lossless compression method to
compress the raw LF lenslet images by exploiting the lenslet structure and spatial
redundancy of the plenoptic data[Per15]. Other methods compressed the raw lenslet
images by exploiting the correlation in inter or intra microlens[TH17][Miy+18]. How-
ever, the compression ratio considered from this perspective is limited, because the
raw lenslet images are still unrectified. Helin et al. proposed a lossless compression
method for rectified LF data. They first encoded the central view by JPEG2000 loss-
less mode, then used a sparse predictor to obtain partitions for side LF rectified view
into regions(termed as Image Segmentation) and encoded it with color information
as side information[Hel+16][Hel+17]. Umair et al. proposed a method which extends
==PAGE:18==

a Context-based adaptive, lossless& near-lossless image coding(CALIC) for rectified
LF images by utilizing the linear structure of Epipolar Plane Images(EPI) in a slope
based prediction scheme[MF20b][MF20a].
In addition to the lossless compression, some other research works mitigate the
LF storage by synthesizing LF sub-views simply based on few sampled views instead
of coding all the LF images. Wanner et al. warped the sampled view to novel posi-
tion and synthesized the novel views using estimated disparity map[WG13]. Marwah
et al. used a sparse coding method to reconstruct the novel view image signal to
synthesize the novel view[Mar+13].
Such traditional methods mainly used a geo-
metric model or sparse coding method to obtain an intermediate representation of
synthesized views. Recently, with the rapid development of deep learning, researchers
proposed learning-based methods to synthesize the novel LF views. Wu et al.[Wu+17]
and Wang et al.[Wan+18] synthesized the novel LF views using Convolutional Neu-
ral Network(CNN) based on EPI. Kalantari et al.
proposed a method using four
corner views to generate all the rest in-between views using two sequential CNNs
to respectively estimate the disparity and predict color values[KWR16]. Navarro et
al. then added another selection CNN based on Kalantari et al’s network, which is
used to generate four weighted selection masks for the novel view synthesis[NS21].
jin et al. proposed a super resolution method, which also synthesized the LF sub-
view via disparity map. Afterwards, they refined the geometry structure of the EPIs
of synthesized view through a LF blending module[Jin+20]. Similarly, Shahzeb et
al. proposed an approach to synthesize novel view by disparity but further utilized
Convolution Block Attention Module(CBAM) to improve the performance[Gul+20].
The existing state-of-art methods have produced high baselines, while they mainly
reconstructed the novel views by the geometrical information such as the disparity
maps or EPI([Wu+17].[KWR16],[Wan+18],[Jin+20].
These cause an issue in oc-
cluded regions since the occluded pixels do not have a reference(ground truth) to
predict depth or other geometrical representations. Therefore, the novel views syn-
thesized by these methods may have some artifacts in the occlusion regions. Although
Navarro et al.[NS21] have considered this issue and given a solution by discarding the
occlusion pixels and using a weighted masks to select similar pixels in other input
views to fill the holes, their synthesized results still have some distortions in occlusion
regions especially in some challenging LF scenes, where contain a large number of
occlusions.
In view of the occlusion issue, a learning-based occlusion-aware view synthesis
method is proposed. In our proposed method, the LF view synthesis problem can be
divided into four parts to be solved, respectively estimating the disparity, warping the
input views, generating weighted masks by a selection CNN and refining the occlusion
regions by an inpainting CNN. Specifically, it collects four corner views of light field
image as inputs to estimate 12 pairwise optical flows based on a pretrained model
named Recurrent All-Pairs Field Transforms method (RAFT)[TD20] as disparities.
Moreover, it backward warps the input four corner views towards the target novel
view position based on 12 flows, thus generates 12 warped images. Afterwards, it con-
catenates the warp images, the optical flows and the angular position information as

the input, then forwards to two CNNs. The selection CNN will generate 12 weighted
masks and the inpainting CNN will use the attention mechanism to focus on which
objects have the occlusions and where the occlusion regions are. 12 weighted masks
will be refined by the inpainting CNN and finally 12 refined masks will average the
corresponding 12 warped images and synthesize the novel target views. The exper-
imental result shows that our proposed method outperforms three state-of-the-art
methods and we demonstrate several challenging LF scenes to compare against other
methods, which prove that the proposed method performs better especially in the
occlusion regions.
The proposed learning-based method simply uses four corner views to synthesize
other in-between sub-views with high quality both objectively and subjectively. Mean-
while, our method is time-eﬀicient in synthesizing novel views. Such advantages make
the proposed method be potential to integrate with existing coding scheme that can
improve the performance for light field image compression.
1.2
Goals and Schedule of the Thesis
This project continues the work on the author’s two courses projects Light Field
View Interpolation Based on Optical Flow[Luo21a] and Light Field View Synthesis
based on Convolutional Neural Network[Luo21b]. The precious works have proved
the feasibility of using single CNN to synthesize the novel views, while the quality of
reconstructed views is rather poor, which we will demonstrate in Section 4.32. There-
fore, the goal of this project is to synthesize the LF novel views with a high quality
and simultaneously time eﬀiciency in execution. We propose a dual-CNN model to
reconstruct the LF novel views given four corner views as inputs. The dual-CNN
model contains a selection CNN to learn the weighted masks for view synthesis, and
an inpainting CNN for the refinement of occlusion regions. We evaluate the quality of
novel views objectively and subjectively by calculating quality metrics such as PSNR,
SSIM and demonstrating the novel views to assess the reconstruction quality visually.
In addition, we make a benchmark and compare the reconstruction performance of
our proposed method against other state-of-the-art methods. Moreover, in order to
achieve the goal with a better result, we visualize the intermediate results such as
feature maps and conduct an ablation study, which removes or replaces certain parts
of the network for gaining a better understanding of the network’s behaviour. Mean-
while, we measure the execution time in synthesizing 30 LF scenes and calculate the
running time synthesizing an arbitrary novel view and a whole LF scene.

2.1
Background
2.1.1
Light Field Imaging Technology
The light field(LF) is a vector function that describes light flowing from all directions
through every viewpoint in space[Wik20]. LF first was defined as a 5D representa-
tion, consisting of three spatial dimensions and two angular dimensions. M. Levoy
et al. simplified the LF vector function from 5D plenoptic function to 4D LF by
constraining attention to rays passing through free-space, which means the length
and intensity of light traveling along a ray is constant, making one spatial dimension
to be ignored[LH96]. They further converted 4D LF representation to a set of 2D
image slices. A 4D LF model is represented in Figure 2.1. In Subfigure 1.1(a), the
uv plane is regarded as multi-camera array plane and the st plane is regarded as the
focal plane. From spatial plane perspective, the cameras on the uv plane capture the
rays arriving from all points on the focal plane as 2D LF sub-views. From angular
projection perspective, the rays left from one point on the focal plane and scattered
to all points on the uv plane are similar to reflection maps[LH96]. Two visualization
of LF are shown as Subfigure 1.1(b).

2 Background
After simplifying the LF representation to 4D, one 4D LF can be permuted and
reconstructed to a 2D image matrix and vice versa. This conversion makes the LF
applications such as LF camera feasible. Stanford university built a multi-camera
array, which is composed of 100 CMOS-based cameras, to capture 2D LF images
with different angles and converted to 4D LFs. While using a multi-camera array
to capture 4D LF image is inconvenient and Ren Ng et al. proposed a technique to
reduce the equipment size by replacing the multi-cameras to micro-lens arrays[Ng+05].
It enables the LF imaging technology applied into commercial camera products. Ren
Ng then established a company called Lytro to produce commercial LF cameras. In
the project, we use an open-source LF image dataset captured by Lytro LF handy
camera, which contains 100 4D LF images as training set and 30 4D LF images as
test set[KWR16].

2.1.2
Novel View Synthesis& Frame Interpolation
Novel view synthesis has been studied by the computer vision and computer graphics
research community for a long time. It is widely applied in imaging technologies, such
as LF imaging, Augmented Reality/Virtual Reality(AR/VR), video game rendering,
etc. In LF view synthesis, most methods generate a proxy geometric representation
of sampled LF scene view and the novel view can be reconstructed based on the esti-
mated geometry representation. In this subsection, we first introduce the knowledge
of two common geometric representations, respectively the disparity and the epipolar
plane images(EPI). Afterwards, We elaborate the basic concepts about image warp-
ing, a necessary step in view synthesis approaches, which warps the LF sampled
view to synthesize the novel view. Moreover, we briefly introduce the video frame
interpolation, which is a similar task to the novel view synthesis and the use of a
disparity-like representation named optical flow.
Geometric Representations
==PAGE:25==
2.1 Background
9
One of the most commonly used geometric representations is disparity/depth map.
Disparity in stereo vision refers to the displacement difference in coordinates of points
within two stereo images. A typical example of binocular disparity is human eyes,
which is the difference in the location of a scene seen by the left and right eyes,
resulting from the parallax. It is easy to experience the disparity: when we open
one of our eyes and then rapidly close it while opening the other eye, the foreground
objects appear to swift a significant distance while background objects move little.
Figure 2.3 shows an example of disparity, when human focus on the green point with
the near disparity, the displacement is larger than the red point with far disparity.
Disparity can reflect the depth information correctly, therefore, it is one of the most
common geometric models to reconstruct the novel view.
Figure 2.3: Binocular disparity[Che+13].
Similarly, in computer stereo vision, human eyes are replaced by two stereo cam-
eras, which are able to capture two images and generate the corresponding disparity
map. Intuitively, there is an inverse relationship between disparity and depth map
and indeed, they are inversely proportional with constant coeﬀicients.

Apart from the disparity map, another common geometric representation is EPI.
In the EPI representation, each pixel in the image is projected onto an epipolar line,
whose slope reflects the depth information of the image. The EPI representation of
a LF is denoted as L(s, t, u, v), which is obtained by fixing an angular or a spatial

Deep learning is a subset of machine learning algorithms, which allows computational

In recent years, along with the exponential growth of computing power by using

deeper, and meanwhile, they are allowed to train on much larger datasets. Machine
learning algorithms such as Neural Network then develops a new concept termed Deep
Learning. By scaling up the model size, Deep Learning methods have dramatically
reached the state-of-the-art performance not only in the computer vision fields such
as view synthesis, image classification, object recognition and detection etc, but also
in other fields for instance, the Natural Language Processing(NLP), Speech Recogni-

Convolutional Neural Network
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), first proposed by Lecun et al. is a specific
type of NN that has been tremendously successful in different research fields and
practical applications, especially in computer vision tasks. CNN is similar to tradi-
tional NN, which is made up of neurons that is able to learn the optimal weights
and biases. CNN is designed to learn the data in the form of multi-arrays, for ex-
ample RGB images composed of three 2D arrays containing pixel intensities in the
RGB channels[LeC+89][LB+95][LeC+98]. CNN is able to extract spatial hierarchies
of features through Back Propagation by using multiple layers, such as convolution
layers, pooling layers, and fully connected layers. A basic CNN architecture is shown
in Figure 2.12.
==PAGE:34==

The convolution layer(Conv) is the core component in CNN. It performs convolu-
tion operations with a set of learnable filters, which is small in spatial (along width
and height) but extending through full depth of input volume. Specifically, the con-
volution operation computes dot products between the filter and the input at any
depth position. During the forward propagation, we convolve each filter across the
spatial plane of input volumes and produce a 2-dimensional feature map at every
spatial position as the resulting output. In convolution layer, there are two kind of
hyperparameters respectively the filter size F and stride S. The filter size refers to
the receptive field of the neuron in the Conv and the stride refers to the step size we
slide the filter.
The pooling layer (POOL) can be regarded as a down-sampling operation and
is typically inserted in-between successive Conv layers in a CNN architecture.
In
particular, there are two kind of POOL operation namely max pooling and average
pooling, where the maximum and average value is calculated. Its use is also similar
to the down-sampling in traditional method, that is to reduce the spatial size of the
representation to decrease the amount of parameters and computation in the network
as well as controlling overfitting.
The fully connected layer has full connections to all activated neurons in the
previous layer and computes the optimal weights, which is the same as the Neural
Networks introduced in previous part. Figure 2.13 shows a visualized example of

Deep Convolutional Neural Network
People find that the performance is better when the CNN is deeper, since the CNN
is able to integrate low/mid/high level features extracted by various layers[ZF14].
However, when the CNN layer goes very deep, the performance starts decreasing
and the training process becomes much more diﬀicult. One reason is the problem of
vanishing/exploding gradients. When training a deep NN, we use the chain rule to
backward propagate and learn the gradient. In a NN of n hidden units(layers), n
derivatives will be multiplied together. When the derivatives are large, the gradient
will increase exponentially, and this problem is called exploding gradient. Alterna-
tively, if the derivatives are very small that even close to zero, the gradient will
decrease exponentially until it vanishes, and this problem is called vanishing gradi-
ent[GB10].
One solution given by Schmidhuber et al.
is using a Recurrent Neural Net-
work(RNN) structure including Long Short-term Memory(LSTM) units which allow
gradients to flow unchanged[HS97]. Experiments show that although it is well-suited
to work in classifying, processing and making predictions based on time series data,
it still remains some issues in gradient exploding and meanwhile, LSTM sometimes
is very hard to converge because of large nonlinearity. Another common solution is
Batch Normalization proposed by Ioffe et al. that implements a normalization to fix
the means and variances of input batches at each layer. Batch normalization avoids
the changes in the distributions(termed as Internal Covariate Shift) of internal nodes
in a deep NN model. Batch Normalization allows deep NN to use much higher learn-
ing rates, which accelerates the training and releases the gradient vanishing issue.
Although batch normalization mitigate the gradient problems and accelerates the
training, there is still a decline in performance when the network becomes very deep.
Aiming at solving this problem, Kaiming He et al. proposed one of the most commonly
used and brilliant deep NN architecture – Deep Residual Network(ResNet)[He+16].
The most important problem they solved is the degradation due to a very deep net-
==PAGE:36==
20
2 Background
work. Concretely, as the network depth increasing, the training and testing accuracy
get saturated and then degrade rapidly. They first inferred that such degradation is
not caused by overfitting, because overfitting will improve the training performance
while perform poorer in testing, instead of the decline in both training and testing.
In order to validate the hypothesis,they did a series experiments by adding identity
mapping(without any learning process), and kept the other layers the same as the
previous shallower model. A deeper model should perform no higher training error
than its shallower model, beacause everything stayed the same, except adding the
identity mapping, while the experiments proved that it inevitably lost some informa-
tion during the mapping, due to the existence of nonlinear activation functions such
as Relu etc. Furthermore, they addressed the degradation problem by introducing
a deep residual learning framework. Figure 2.14 shows one basic block of residual

2.2.1
Non-learning Approaches
In Section 1.1 and Section 2.1 we took a glance at some traditional LF view synthesis
approaches and introduced related geometrical representations. In this part we will
take an overview of two typical kind of non-learning approaches. The first kind of
non-learning approach proposed by Wanner et al is synthesizing the LF novel view
based on the depth information. They formulated the view synthesis as a continuous
inverse problem then estimated the depth information locally using epipolar plane
image(EPI) analysis at the input LF views and optimized the obtained depth maps
using convex relaxation techniques. Afterwards they warped the input view based
on the depth information and combined the warped views by weighting each of them.
Moreover, they incorporated a binary mask to indicate the occlusion regions then
used a smoothness term to mitigate the occlusion effects[WG13]. However, although
they blurred the occlusion region to reduce the error, the artifact is obvious. Tao et
al. proposed a method to find angular coherence in the angular pixels, namely photo
content consistency, depth consistency, and shading consistency. They further esti-
mated the lighting in natural LF scenes and shading effect and used the optimization
to optimize the depth and shading effect in synthesized views. Nevertheless, neither
Wanner nor Tao produced a good performance in occluded areas until Wang et al.
improved depth estimation in occlusion region by modifying the photo-consistency
condition on angular pixels at the occlusion region explicitly[WER15].
The second non-learning approach is proposed by Marwah et al, which is a sparse
coding method including three components: a LF atom as a sparse representation
of LF scene, an optical design that recorded the captured 2D light field projections,
and a robust sparse reconstruction method to reconstruct the synthesized view. Con-
cretely, they built a dictionary to record the similarities between neighbour views
and reconstructed the novel LF view and coded LF projection and reconstructed the
synthesized view based on the dictionary[Mar+13].

Learning-based Methods
With the explosive development of deep learning these years, learning-based methods
have made a great success in different computer vision tasks. Recently, there are
a lot of research works focusing on LF view synthesis using deep learning methods.
Learning-based methods have proved that the performance of reconstructed views is
much more better than the non-learning methods. In this section we will introduce

As we introduced before, one of the most successful deep learning model in image

siderable learning-based model. It was first applied by Yoon et al. who proposed a
super resolution method to enhance the spatial resolution via a single CNN[Yoo+17].

novel view to the second CNN. This CNN is to predict the colors so-called color

reconstructs the 2D novel view image as output. Moreover, they built a open-source
LF dataset including both indoor and outdoor scenes for training the network and
testing. The experimental results showed that the proposed method outperformed

3.1.1
Network Architecture
We convert the view synthesis problem into three parts: disparity estimation and
image warping, warped view selection and the occlusion inpainting. First a pretrained

Section 3.1.2) are averaged as the estimated disparities S{F(p,q)} of target view I(p,q).

3.1.3
Selection Network
The goal of the network is to generate 12 selection masks, which can average weight
the corresponding 12 warped images as the novel view. The selection masks can be
further improved by another inpainting network, which generates the refined masks
to optimize the weights in occlusion region.
Figure 3.5 shows the internal structure of the selection network. Comparing to
Navarro’s network, we remove the feature extraction network and disparity estimation
network. Instead, we directly use the pretrained model RAFT[TD20] to generate the
optical flow as the disparity and design a single selection network with much deeper
and wider layers. It consists of 12 convolutional layers and a maximal 256 channels
in the intermediate layers, all of which are with a 3 × 3 kernel(filter) size. In contrast,
Navarro’s selection network only has 6 layers with a maximal 128 channels in inter-
==PAGE:47==
3.1 Proposed Model

mediate layers. In addition, all layers except the last layer use an ELU activation
function and batch normalization. In the last layer, we remove the batch normal-
ization and activation function and simply remain a convolutional layer, since here
we will forward the output through a softmax function to project the values to [0, 1]
as weight masks. Adding batch normalization and activation function will generate
extra nonlinearity and cause errors. Besides, skip connections are implemented in
network, which are the arrows concatenated convolutional layers in Figure 3.5. Skip
connections improve the network performance and the convergence by preserving the
gradient in previous layes. Our proposed structure is able to converge much faster
comparing to other SOTA methods, but reaches a comparable results even without
optimizing by inpainting network.

3.1.4
Inpainting Network
The inpainting network aims to refine the 12 selection masks by inpainting the oc-
clusion region. We use the same network architecture as Gul et al. proposed, which
consists of a head layer for feature extraction, 5 ResBlocks for occlusion detection and
a tail layer for output masks generation. Each ResBlock is composed of 3 CBAMs
with a skip connection.
In the CBAM, there are two core components: Channel
Attention (CA) and Spatial Attention (SA) module, respectively. CA enables the
network focusing on ‘what’ is meaningful to be learnt given an input and SA focuses
on ‘where’ is the informative area, which is complementary to CA. In the inpainting
network, we use CBAM to learn what is occlusion and where is the occlusion region
by imposing constraint in loss function. Therefore, the inpainting network will pay
more attention on the occlusion region instead of the global scene. The architecture
of inpainting network is shown in Figure 3.6.
Figure 3.6: The architecture of inpainting network and the internal blocks.

3.2.2
Training Settings
The proposed model is implemented using Pytorch and trained on Lytro LFs diverse
dataset[KWR16], which have a spatial resolution of 541 × 376 and an angular one of
14 × 14. Since there are some lens distortion in Lytro LFs, we select central 7 × 7
array of views as the input, which is the same setting with Navarro et al., Jin et
al. and Gul et al. (Kalantari et al. used 8 × 8). In order to compare with other
three SOTA baselines[NS21],[KWR16][Jin+20], we retrain their works and test on
the Diverse dataset, including 100 LFs for training and 30 LFs for testing.
In each training epoch, we randomly select in-between view I(p,q) as the target,
where p, q ∈N∩∈[0, 6], excluding 4 input corner views. In order to synthesize the
target view ˆI(p,q), we input 4 corner views from each LF and randomly crop each view
to 192×192 patch size. The reason we randomly crop the input view to a smaller size
is not only to accelerate the training, but also increase the amount of data to avoid
the network overfitting and such process is called data argumentation. The proposed
model uses a L1-Charbonnier based loss function ℓpresented in Section 3.2.1 and uses
ADAM optimizer[KB14], with hyperparameters β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, ϵ = 1e −08.
β2 = 0.999. The learning rate was set to 1e−4 initially and decreased to 1e−5 for
fine-tuning after first 200 epochs. The proposed model is trained around 1 day and
6 hours on one NVIDIA Ampere A100 GPU and converges approximately at 300
epochs. The model is trained with an initial learning rate 1e−4 in first 200 epochs
and with a decreasing learning rate to 1e−5 in next 100 epochs for the fine tuning.
The training batch size is set to 20. Figure 3.8 shows the training and test results in
200(left)+100(right) training process. We measure the PSNR performance between
the synthesized novel views and the ground truth, and the model reaches an around
39.25dB in training set and 39.68dB in test set at convergence.

The result shows that the Charbonnier loss performed better than L1 and L2,
while L2 performed worst in the comparison. Intuitively, we might think the L2 will
perform a better result since it has an explicit relationship with the PSNR metric.
However, it is not suitable to be the loss function in the image restoration tasks
such as the view synthesis, image super resolution etc.
The reason is the Mean
Square Error(L2) will penalize larger errors, but be more tolerant to small errors,
regardless of the structure in the image. This will smoothen some region in the image
and cause blurs, splotchy artifacts and grating lobe artifacts. Whereas the L1 and
Charbonnier loss(still based on L1) can avoid over-penalizing larger errors, therefore
the performance are both superior to L2. Moreover, L2 gets stuck more easily in
a local minimum when training the network and is tend to converge to a worse
result comparing to L1 and Charbonnier loss. In Zhao et al.’s paper[Zha+16b], they
proposed the hypothesis and did very detailed experiments, proving L1 loss function
performs better than L2 in image restoration tasks, which also supports our result in
Table 4.2.

4.4.3
Limitations
Although we reach a state-of-art performance in the experiment, there is a major
limitation in the proposed method, which is the optical flow estimation. So far we
directly estimate the optical flow pairs from corner views and average the flows as
the estimated disparity at the target view. Nevertheless, the average flows might not
reflect the accurate depth, especially in the large displacement case. For example, in
the Rock scene, it can be noticed that the leaf tip still are not able to reconstruct
perfectly in the center view. The issues also happens in some other scenes, that the
reconstruction result of center view is poorer because of a large displacement from
four corner views. This limitation can be improved by a more accurate optical flow
estimation.
In Bao et al.’s paper, they have discussed this limitation caused by averaging
flows, as shown in Figure 4.12[Bao+19]. In this case multiple flow vectors could be
projected to the same position, causing the average flow vector(green arrow) may
not point to the correct pixels. Therefore, they proposed a depth-aware projection
method to add another network to learn the depth map separately and use the depth
map of estimated position as a constraint term to refine the average flow vectors(red
arrow). Similarly, we can find a reference as a constraint of the average flows and
improve the estimation of the disparity.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
5.1
Summary
In the thesis, a learning-based occlusion-aware LF view synthesis method has been
described, which uses four corner views to synthesize all the in-between LF views
with different angular positions. In the proposed method, first a pretrained model
RAFT is applied to estimate 12 pairwise optical flows as the predicted disparity maps.
Four corner views are warped towards the target position view using the 12 optical
flows. 12 warped images will be concatenated with 12 optical flows and the position
encoding p, q and together forwarded to the proposed dual-CNNs, respectively the
selection CNN and the inpainting CNN. The selection CNN is to learn the detailed
contents in the view and generate 12 masks. Instead of learning the detailed contents,
the inpainting CNN pays more attention to which objects to be focused and where the
occluded regions are. Afterwards, 12 masks will be refined by the output of inpainting
network as refined masks. 12 refined masks are used to calculate a weighted average
of corresponding 12 warped images as the novel synthesized view.
We test the proposed method on the Diverse test set and the experiment shows
that the novel view reaches 39.43dB in PSNR and 0.980 in SSIM, which has out-
performed other three state-of-the-art methods. At the time evaluation, our method
takes around 0.45 seconds to synthesis an arbitrary view in the LF and around 20
seconds to synthesis all the in-between views in a 7 × 7 LF scene. Furthermore, we
conduct an ablation study to analyze the effectiveness of different loss function and
different network architecture. We have proved that the proposed loss function and
network architecture can improve the performance of LF novel view synthesis.
5.2
Future Work
The work introduced in this thesis can be further improved and extended.
The
following issues can be addressed:
• Generalization. Although we have trained and tested the proposed method
in a open dataset with wide baselines, it still can be retrained or tested in other
datasets such as HCI[Hon+16], EPFL datasets[WMG13], etc. to validate the
generalization ability of the model. Moreover, we can contain more results of
SOTA methods such as [Gul+20],[Wan+20] into the benchmark.
==PAGE:68==
52
5 Conclusion
• Method Optimization. We have mentioned the major limitation in Section
4.33, that we can improve the accuracy of optical estimation in large displace-
ment case in future work. Another improvement for future is to decrease the
running time. We have tested the execution time to synthesize a novel view can
be even optimized to three times faster in both training and test by paralleling
warping images and separating the warping part from dataloader. While we
found that the test results decreased after changing the code structure. One fu-
ture work can be optimizing the code structure to accelerate the view synthesis
time.
• Eﬀicient Coding scheme Since the proposed method only requires 4 corner
views per LF scene to synthesize all the in-between views and simultaneously
preserves a high quality as well as executing with an acceptable time, it can be
further used to reduce the storage size of the LF image. For instance, it can be
integrated into the existing lossless coding scheme to replace the compression
process of all the in-between views by synthesizing the views on the fly. However,
one possible limitation is that deep learning-based methods require a relatively
high performance computing hardware such as a GPU with large memory. It
can be solved by providing a pretrained model but still a potential drawback.
Our future work can find an appropriate way to balance the computational
requirement and the compression rate/eﬀiciency.
