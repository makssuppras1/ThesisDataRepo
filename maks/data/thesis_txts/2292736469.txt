Abstract 
This report documents the work carried out in relation to the masters’ project “Redesigning the ecocar 
DTU Dynamo” at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The project was carried out by master 
student at DTU Design and Innovation Christian Klarskov Gørtz (S091105) during the spring semester of 
2015 (F15) and describes an engineering design process utilizing methods from industrial design. The 
aim of the project was to generate a new design for the DTU ecocar “DTU Dynamo” which would 
resemble a contemporary production car but retain the exceptional aerodynamic properties of the 
current ecocar. The project was supervised by head of bachelor studies at DTU Design and Innovation 
Per Boelskifte and course manager from the DTU ecocar course Jesper Schramm, both from the DTU 
department of Mechanical Engineering (DTU Mek). 
The project was carried out in two main phases: the first and second design phases and involved 
analyzing competitor cars from the event Shell Eco-Marathon Europe, as well as current concept and 
production vehicles from the automotive industry. The first design phase involved a light assessment of 
different car design shapes. The main focus was to find the most aerodynamically optimal base shape 
for the new ecocar design. 5 form models were generated using industrial design methods and tested 
using CAD modeling and CFD testing. This resulted in a base shape which was used to conduct a second 
design phase, where a new design would be generated based on the selected form model. 3 different 
concept models were selected for CFD and wind tunnel testing. Following these tests, the concept 
models were presented to the DTU Roadrunners team in order to get their opinion on the new designs 
and in order to investigate which of the concept models would be recognizable as an actual car. Based 
on the test results and following reactions, a final design was chosen and further detailed into a concept 
solution, featuring an exterior design and suggestions for the structure of the new vehicle design.   
Readers guide 
The structure of this report will take the reader through the individual project phases of this masters’ 
project, from initial analysis work through design and testing and onwards to final design. The report 
starts with an introduction to the DTU Roadrunners team and the Shell Eco-Marathon event and 
concludes with the final design for a possible new ecocar conceived during the course of this project. A 
list of appendixes is found at the beginning of the report and a list of literature at the end. All internet 
sources used throughout the report has been marked as foot notes and indicated with their respective 
links. The report serves as the main documentations and supported by a separate set of appendixes. It is 
therefore advised to read the main report and refer to the relevant appendixes when needed. 
It is to be noted that the latest verion of the DTU ecocar (DTU Dynamo 11.0) was renamed to DTU 
Phoenix during the Shell Eco-Marathon 2015. For this reason, the same car may be described using 
either name throughout the report and/or in the appendixes.

Introduction 
This master thesis has been written during the spring semester of 2015 at the Technical University of 
Denmark (DTU) department of Mechanical engineering with the purpose of redesigning the award 
winning ecocar “DTU Dynamo”. DTU Dynamo competes in the annual competition Shell Eco-Marathon 
and has won its class at the competition for the last seven years in a row1.  
The thesis describes an engineering design process including the methods of industrial design2. The end 
result will be a physical shape design of the new ecocar, which will be tested using prototypes. The 
proposed design will include structural engineering considerations regarding the cars construction, 
weight, aerodynamics and production costs. 
The thesis is authored by master student Christian Klarskov Gørtz (S091105) at the Design and 
Innovation study line at DTU. The supervisors for this thesis are head of bachelor studies for design and 
innovation Per Boelskifte and course manager for the DTU ecocar project Jesper Schramm, both from 
the DTU department of Mechanical engineering.

Project scope 
This master thesis will be conducted over a 20 week period from January 26th 2015 to June 26th 2015. 
This time frame does not allow for a complete design and testing process with a subsequent production 
of a 1:1 scale model of the new car design. The project has therefore been scoped only to include the 
design and testing phases using smaller scale models of the new car design for wind tunnel and 
computational fluid dynamics tests (CFD) as a mean of proving the concept of the new design and 
confirm its aerodynamic properties and its possibility of realization.

About the Shell Eco-Marathon 
 
Figure 6: Family photo of the competing teams from the Shell Eco-Marathon Europe 20159. 
Shell Eco-Marathon is an international competition divided into three regional competitions in America, 
Asia and Europe respectively. The competition is organized by the oil company Shell and challenges 
student teams from technical universities and high schools from the three regions to compete on who 
can build the most fuel efficient and environmentally friendly car design and driving pattern. The 
competing teams are divided into two competition classes, the Prototype and Urban Concept classes 
respectively, and are divided into categories based on their choice of fuel type. In 2015, the competition, 
which is the closest one can get to an actual world championship in eco-friendly car construction and 
driving, celebrates its 30th anniversary. The purpose of the competition is to promote energy saving 
technologies as well as Shell’s products in order to encourage the consumers to think green and make 
better use of energy efficient technologies. At the same time, the competition serves as a show case for 
the technological development taking place in the industry and at universities in relation to internal 
combustion engines, battery electric systems, fuel cells and other energy efficient technologies. 
The European edition of the Shell Eco-Marathon has been taking place at the Ahoy! Street Circuit in 
Rotterdam, the Netherlands since 2012. The competition is known for teams beating world records in 
fuel consumption each year. Different fuel types are available for the competing teams and are free for 
choice of the teams. These fuel types include:

 
Bio-ethanol (2nd generation) 
 
Diesel 
 
GTL (synthetic diesel) 
 
Hydrogen

It is up to the teams to decide which kind of fuel they wish to use and modify their vehicles accordingly, 
so that they comply with the rules and regulations set by the organizers in relation to the individual fuel 
types. The fuel consumption of each competing vehicle is calculated based of the actual amount of fuel 
used as well as the energy density of the given fuel. For the European Shell Eco-Marathon, each 
competing car needs to complete 10 laps on the 1.6 km Ahoy! Street circuit within a time frame of 39 
minutes. Ideally, the teams should strive to get as close to the time limit as possible in order to drive 
most fuel efficiently. When doing so, the competing cars should cover the app. 16.117 km race distance 
at an average speed of app. 25 km/h. Once completed, Shell officials measure the actual amount of fuel 
used to complete the race, and convert the results into either km/l gasoline equivalents or km/kWh 
equivalents, depending on the fuel type of the vehicle in question. The conversion is necessary in order 
to directly compare the performance of each car. Prototype vehicles are allowed to run continuously 
throughout the entire race duration, whereas Urban Concept vehicles have one mandatory full stop per 
lap in order to simulate urban traffic running conditions. Before being allowed on to the track, each 
vehicle needs to pass through a technical and safety inspection, performed by Shell officials, in order to 
ensure that the vehicle is safe and complies with the Shell Eco-Marathon rule set. Should the vehicle be 
involved in an accident or any other occurrence which may affect the structural integrity or in any other 
way the safety of the vehicle, regardless of whether the incident takes place on the track or in the 
paddocks, the officials may require the vehicle to be subjected to a new inspection before being allowed 
back on to the track. The majority of the competing teams utilize a driving strategy known as coast and 
burn. This involves using the vehicle power plant in order to achieve a certain speed after which the 
drive line will be shut down and the vehicle will free wheel (the coast phase) until it reaches a certain 
minimum speed at which point the drive line is reengaged in order to power the vehicle back up to a 
certain speed (the burn phase). Depending on their driving style, rolling resistance and driving strategy, 
each team use different numbers of coast and burn phases in order to complete their competition runs 
during the competition in order to maximize fuel economy.

Methodology 
The engineering design principle used to conduct this project builds on the methodology presented in 
the book “Engineering design methods” by Nigel Cross11, which describes the design process as an 
iterative process, in which a need is identified, analyzed and a solution is designed. The process used for 
this project draws inspiration from French’s model of design, although the full process will not covered 
by this project, as the end design will kept at a conceptual level.

The most critical element of the design process for the new car will be the design of the exterior body 
shell. Different shapes act differently when subjected to a fluid flow of a given type and velocity. The 
different properties of different designs ultimately come down to friction between the flowing particles 
and the surface of the object in terms of surface quality and angle of attack. If the fluid encounters a 
surface normal to the flow direction, the impact forces and resulting deceleration between the fluid and 
the surface will be great. If the fluid encounters a more streamlined surface, the fluid will meet less 
resistance and will pass easier over the surface of the object with less deceleration. Streamlining an 
object also reduce the amount of drag forces acting on the object, which are caused by the fluid leaving 
the surface of the object. This is because the fluid will meet les resistance from the shape of the object 
and therefore will follow the shape of the object for a longer period of time. Drag turbulence will only 
occur once the decelerated fluid leaves the surface of the object and once again encounters the fast 
moving fluid flow around the object causing interferences. 
 
Figure 10: Wind tunnel testing of the 2012 BMW 6-series convertible, illustrating the air flow over the vehicle body13. 
The theory of streamlining can also be applied to automobiles as a more streamlined car body will help 
the vehicle cut easier through the air. Research has shown that the most aerodynamically optimal shape 
for a car design would be one resembling half a droplet. A correct design will reduce the initial impact 
forces between the air and the car and also minimize drag forces occurring at the back of the vehicle.

The drag coefficient Cd is the most common way of describing the aerodynamic properties of a vehicle. 
The Cd-value of a vehicle design can be determined in multiple ways, either by calculations, wind tunnel 
testing or CFD simulations. In order to manually calculate the Cd-value of a vehicle, the following formula

In this formula, Fd describes the resistance forces acting on the car as a consequence of the fluid 
encountering the vehicle body. ρ describes the density of the attacking fluid, V describes the velocity of 
the fluid flow and A is the area of the vehicle in relation to the fluids angle of attack on the vehicle. Once 
calculated, Cd will provide a unit less number describing the fluid dynamic properties of the vehicle. The 
higher the Cd-value, the more resistance is exerted on the car and the more power it will require to 
overcome the resistance forces from the fluids encountering the vehicle. For comparative reasons, the 
Cd-value of an object is often multiplied by its frontal area. This gives a more realistic impression of the 
actual aerodynamic performance of the object. Two identically shaped objects, say a sphere, of different 
sizes may achieve the same Cd-value from calculations or CFD testing, but if the one object is 
significantly larger than the other, it will naturally experience greater resistance from the incoming fluid. 
The Cd-value is therefore multiplied by the area in order to achieve the most realistic impression of the 
actual resistance, and in such event, the larger object will naturally achieve a greater CdA-value than the 
smaller object due to its larger frontal area.

Design specification 
In order to be able to come up with the most desirable design for the new ecocar, from a Roadrunner’s 
point of view, a number of technical and esthetical requirements and criteria must be thought into the 
new design. These requirements and criteria serve the purpose of ensuring that the new car can be 
approved for competition in the Shell Eco-Marathon and making sure that the new car will be 
recognizable as an actual car in the eyes of the team’s followers and visitors rather than a science fiction 
project.  
Technically, the new design needs to obtain as a low wind resistance coefficient as possible, which 
means that the car optimally should have as small a frontal area as possible and that the surfaces of the 
bodywork should not be disturbed by protruding design elements obstructing the airflow over the 
vehicle body.  
Aesthetically, the car should resemble a modern production car, or at least a car which could be put into 
production within a reasonable time frame. This means that the car should be easily recognizable as an 
actual car, which requires it to feature a luggage compartment, a passenger cabin, front and rear lights, 
windscreen wiper etc. The new design also needs to be recognizable as the DTU ecocar “DTU Dynamo”, 
which means that the new design ideally should contain at least some references or design 
characteristics known from earlier versions of the ecocar. 
Most importantly, the new car needs to comply with the rules and regulations governing the Shell Eco-
Marathon competition, as the new car will be useless if it can’t be approved for participation in the 
competition.

Initial design work 
In order to get the design work off to the best possible start, four mood boards were generated at the 
very start of the design process19. The purpose of these mood boards was to provide inspiration for the 
design of the new car in relation to its body shape, as well as clarifying the desired signal values of the 
new car. The four mood boards were each given an individual headline: styling, usage, heritage and 
competitor and lifestyle respectively. The mood board “Styling” would form the basis of the visual 
appearance of the new design. “Usage” would ensure that all through the design phase, it should be 
kept in mind that the car should be a competition car, and as such the team should be able to easily 
work on the car before, during and after the competition itself. “Heritage and competitors” would 
provide inspiration from earlier versions of the ecocar “DTU Dynamo” as well providing pictures of the 
main competitors. This would ensure that the new design would be recognizable as the DTU ecocar and 
that the new design wouldn’t resemble an existing competitor from another university. “Lifestyle” 
would provide inspiration for the life the new car and its future teams would go through, in terms of 
long working nights, exhibitions, official presentations, the Eco-marathon itself, etc. The four mood 
boards can be seen in Appendix RR-01: Combined mood boards. 
Apart from the mood boards, the earlier formulated design brief and requirement specification were 
also used in relation to the design work with the new car. A successful design depends on how well the 
new design lives up to the formulated design brief and requirement specification. A design which 
doesn’t comply with either the design brief or requirement specification must be considered to be 
undesirable, as this would mean that the new car either wouldn’t be able to be approved for 
competition in the Shell Eco-Marathon or wouldn’t pay homage to DTU’s ecocar traditions. 
The design phase was initiated with an idea generation phase where a number of different design 
languages for the new car was explored using side view drawings. This initial idea generation would later 
form the basis of the further design work and ultimately result in 3-5 concepts for initial CFD-testing 
(Computational Fluid Dynamics). The initial sketches were made in side view in order to provide the best 
possible impression of the new design and to minimize time consumption from drawing each new 
design idea in perspective drawings. A total of 4 A3 pages featuring 26 side view sketches were 
produced. From these sketches, 5 conceptual ideas were selected for initial CFD-testing. The resulting 
concept sketches can be seen below.

CAD modeling and CFD testing of initial design concepts 
In order to carry out CFD simulations of the 5 selected form models, 5 different CAD-models were 
created in the CAD program PTC Creo Parametric. The models were created on the basis of the 
maximum allowed full scale dimensions from the Shell Eco-Marathon chapter 1 rule set, and were 
modeled as surface features. The purpose of this initial test procedure was to investigate which design 
elements could affect the aerodynamics of the car in a positive way. The generated designs each 
featured different front, rear, cabin and under body designs, in order to roughly investigate the effects 
of these designs on the cars aerodynamics. The models were tested using a rough CFD simulation 
model, much similar to the one used to test the aerodynamics of the upcoming new version of the 
prototype car DTU Innovator24. The main objective was to obtain an air resistance coefficient (Cd) and a 
CdA- value for each of the 5 concepts as well as a rough simulation of how the air moved across the 
model. The results of the CFD tests would be used to select a form model for further design work with 
the new Urban Concept car, as the model which performed best in the CFD test would be used as 
reference in the second design phase.  
Following the CAD modeling of each of the 5 form models, each model was exported to an x_t-format in 
order to be able to import them into the CFD software Star CCM+. The 5 form models were then tested 
with the help of fellow DTU Roadrunner and master student Neus Mesalles Teixidó. Pictures of the 
tested CAD-models and respective modeling descriptions can be seen in Appendix RR-03: CAD-modeling 
and CFD testing. The CFD simulations revealed that the Shorthatch concept surprisingly appeared to be 
the best performing of the 5 selected concepts given the simulation conditions25. The Shorthatch 
concept proved to have the lowest Cd-value of the tested form models, achieving a Cd-value of app. 0.20 
according to the test conditions and before multiplying with the frontal area of the vehicle. The 
Shorthatch concept was followed by the Dolph concept with a Cd-value of 0.21, again without 
multiplying with the frontal area, closely followed by the Dolphin concept with a Cd-value of 0.22. The 
CFD-simulations therefore indicated that the Shorthatch concept would have the lowest Cd-value of the 
five form modles, and as such should form the basis of the futher design work. The aerodynamic 
performance of the Shorthatch concept was however not all satisfying, as the current version of DTU

Design work stage 2 
Based on the results from the CFD tests of the form models, 3 new concepts were to be generated using 
the best performing shape concept, the Shorthatch, as a basis. The initial goal for the design of the new 
ecocar was to generate a design which would resemble an actual production car more than the current 
version of DTU Dynamo (version 11.0). In order to do so, it was necessary first to obtain a better 
understanding of what was currently being done in terms of design in the automotive world. A design 
study was therefore carried out on contemporary car design in order to identify current design trends 
and shaping techniques. This design analysis would then be used in the design process for the new car, 
in order to make it look more contemporary and realistic while still maintaining the required 
aerodynamic efficiency.

the major manufactures and on high profiled concept cars. The analysis included 10 different cars and 
resulted in a small collection of desirable and interesting design elements which should be considered 
for the new design of the ecocar. The complete analysis can be seen in Appendix RR-08: Contemporary

Second competitor analysis 
During the Shell Eco-Marathon 2015, a unique possibility of in-depth analysis of different competitor

design. However, some competitors also marked themselves by featuring very interesting designs from 
a pure design point of view, and a very limited number of these competitors had also managed to get 
their cars approved for public road use.  
A total of 10 competitor cars were analyzed for this phase, which provided a broad and nuanced insight 
of the many possibilities in terms of ecocar design. The knowledge gained from this competitor was 
used as inspiration during the second design phase in order to make sure that the new design would 
differentiate itself from the other European competitors and in order to generate an efficient design

Final concept generation 
Based on the initial CFD simulations of the 5 form prototypes, a final concept generation was carried out 
in order to establish the final design of the new DTU Dynamo ecocar. The final concepts would be largely 
based on the Shorthatch form concept, which proved to be the best performing of the form concepts 
during the initial CFD simulations, achieving a Cd value of 0.13 compared to the 0.14 of the current DTU 
Dynamo when not considering the frontal area, and 0.14 vs. 0.12 respectively when multiplying with the 
frontal area of each design34. Based on this form concept, a number of new possible design concepts 
were then produced in order to generate an actual design for the new ecocar. As earlier mentioned, the 
ultimate objective for the project would be to create a new design which greatly resembles a 
contemporary car design while still preserving the exceptional aerodynamic performance of the current 
design. Many of the newly generated concepts had similar or closely resembling form languages and a 
small number of the generated concepts could therefore be eliminated right away. Based on differences 
in form design, 3 concept models derived from the Shorthatch concept was selected for further work 
due to their representations of different design concepts. Before finally selecting the concepts though, 
the 3 selected concepts were evaluated against the design specification in order to verify their usability 
in relation to engineering desires. This was done as a loose evaluation, as the most important feature of 
the new design would be its aerodynamic performance. The differences in design layout of the selected

current ecocar has been designed to comply with the maximum allowed measurements for width and 
length, but to only comply with the minimum height set forth in the Shell Eco-Marathon chapter 1 rule 
set. This means that the current ecocar app. has the following dimensions: 
3500 x 1300 x 1000 mm (L x W x H)

Design selection 
In order to make a final design selection, two key methods has been used: design specification 
comparison and user inclusion. A desirable design should live up to as many of the requirements and 
criteria stated in the design specification as possible. If a given design falls short of one or more of the 
stated requirements, the design can be deemed undesirable and as such shouldn’t be selected. The 
different concept can be expected to comply differently with the criteria stated in the design 
specification and as such, the concept which best fulfills the criteria should be the most desirable 
concept. The design specification cross check is an engineering method to check generated designs. The 
method doesn’t however sufficiently cover the issue of checking that the selected design will actually be 
recognizable as a road car. For this reason, real people and preferably users would need to be included 
in the design selection process. A demonstration was arranged with the DTU Roadrunners team in order 
to get their opinions on the generated designs and in order to identify which concept would be most 
desirable to them. The final design would then be selected based on the results from the design 
specification cross check and the user presentation.

Design selection 
Based on the results from the wind tunnel and CFD tests, the design specification cross checks and the 
reactions gained from the DTU Roadrunners presentation, one of the three concepts would have to be 
selected for further detailing.  
Concept 2 had turned out to be the most promising in both the wind tunnel, CFD testing and in the 
design specification cross check, but fared poorly in the DTU Roadrunners presentation. It was therefore 
highly suspected that this concept was very likely to suffer from the same problem as the current DTU 
Dynamo ecocar: the design would be very efficient and aerodynamic, but people from outside the 
project would not be able to recognize the design as a road car design.  
Concept 1 fared poorly in the wind tunnel tests and the design specification cross check and received 
poor comments during the Roadrunners presentation. The only plus for this concept was that it tied 
with concept 2 in the CdA category of the CFD tests. 
Concept 3 performed beyond expectations in the wind tunnel tests and received quite promising 
remarks during the DTU Roadrunners presentation. The concept also scored one additional green 
marking over the other two concepts in the requirements cross check and tied with concept 1 in the 
criteria cross check. The Achilles heel of concept 3 was proven to be its large frontal area, affecting its 
aerodynamic performance, and giving it the poorest results in the CFD wind tunnel tests.

fully desirable for this project. For this reason, concept 3 was concluded to be the concept of choice for 
further development, as it fared well on almost all other parameters than the CFD tests. Concep 3 would

When looking at the pros. and cons. of the generated solution principles, the green and purple 
principles receive one additional pros. each in comparison to the red and blue principles. Looking at 
cons., the blue, red and purple principles each receive 4 cons., whereas the green principle receives only 
three. The result indicates that the most ideal solution principle for the construction of the new ecocar 
design should be formed on the basis of either the green or the purple solution principles. Looking 
strictly at the individual pros. and cons. for these two solution principles, one particular con. stands out 
for the purple concept: potentially high weight. When constructing and ecocar, weight is of outmost 
importance. As the weight of a vehicle increases, so does the amount of energy required in order to 
move the vehicle and to maintain speed. More energy equals greater fuel consumption, so it is vital to 
try and save as much weight in the construction of an ecocar as possible. Looking at the green and 
purple solution principles, the most desirable concept should be that which would give the new ecocar 
design the lowest possible weight.  Ideally, a combination of the two solution principles could be used, 
utilizing a tubular carbon fiber space frame design with aluminum joints. If designed in the right way, 
this type of supporting structure would provide the car with the strength and stiffness of a carbon fiber 
monocoque with the minimalistic and versatile design of a space frame structure. The carbon space 
frame would need some additional aluminum support for the roll bar and floor of the vehicle, but it is 
assumed that this would be the ideal structure for the new ecocar. The structure would also allow 
individual carbon fiber tubes to be change if they were to be damaged, whereas a with a monocoque 
structure, the entire monocoque would need to be changed if its structural integrity were to be affected 
by a crash, a fire or other types of damages.       
 
The steering, suspension and braking systems of the new vehicle could be made as carryover systems 
from the current car. DTU Dynamo 11.0 features a rack and pinion steering mechanism which has 
proved to give the car exceptionally good handling. The rack and pinion steering can be very precisely 
adjusted and gives the car good maneuverability. Depending on the dimensioning of the new car, the 
rack and pinion steering could either be a direct carryover, or would require redesigned connecting rods 
between the steering column and uprights in order for the steering mechanism to work. The braking 
system is a conventional twin circuit hydraulic system, using brake pistons and calibers on all four 
wheels. The brake line would of cause need to be fitted to the new design, but the calibers and pistons 
themselves could be reused on the new car. DTU Dynamo is not equipped with suspension at the rear, 
and it is expected that the new design won’t need to be either, unless a second passenger is to be fitted.

For the rear body shell, an additional shell division should be introduced in order to allow for access to 
or inspection of the engine bay without removing the entire rear body shell. This should be done with a 
removable engine cover, which could be fastened to the top of the rear body shell using hinges.

Possibilities of realization 
Based on the suggested design of the new ecocar, and the two main suggested structural solution 
principles, the possibilities of realization for the new design could be estimated. This estimated would 
be based on the available knowledge of DTU Roadrunners economy and facilities.  
The exterior of the car would be produced from carbon fiber at DTU campus Risø, who facilitates the 
carbon fiber part production for DTU Roadrunners. The exterior body would be produced using negative 
molds, onto which mats of viewed carbon fiber will be laid and bound together using an epoxy matrix 
and vacuum process. This form of carbon fiber production process has very specific requirements to the 
design of the required body shell parts in terms of wall thickness, slip angles etc. If the new design were 
to be produced using a monocoque structure, this monocoque could be produced in the way as the 
body shells. This production was also used for the production of the monocoque and body shells of the 
current ecocar and could be carried out by students, as long as they have followed a safety course in the 
use of epoxy glue.

If a carbon fiber monocoque construction is chosen in combination with a space frame, the space frame 
part of the construction could be formed by either carbon fiber or aluminum tubing. The aluminum 
tubing will increase the weight of the supporting structure but will be easier to shape, as the aluminum 
tubing can be easily manipulated using heat or bending forces. Carbon fiber tubing can’t be manipulated 
in the same way, and the carbon fiber tubes would have to be molded into the desired shape right from 
the start. The new design could also be build on the basis of a tubular aluminum space frame, as 
suggested in the green solution principle. This would allow for easy construction of the car, but might 
also result in a potentially high weight compared to an equivalent carbon fiber monocoque construction. 
The drive line for the new concept would either be a carry-over from the current ecocar, as the current 
drive train has already proven its worth and efficiency, or a completely new assembly based on a 
production engine and transmission. If a new drive line is selected, a production engine will require a 
number of modifications in order to be able to run on bio-ethanol and in order to work with the team 
CompactRio ECU controller. Such work has already been carried out on the current engine by students 
following the course, and it is most likely that a new team of students would easily be able to carry out 
the required conversions to a new engine. In order to save time and money however, the most optimal 
choice would be the carry-over solution, utilizing the current engine and gearbox from the current 
ecocar. 
As DTU Roadrunners is well equipment had has both a professional fine mechanics work shop and a 
carbon fiber production facility at their disposal, it can rightfully be assumed that the new design would 
in fact be fairly easy to realize and produce for the team. The internal structure would however have to 
finalized and optimized as well as the body shell shapes, in order to ensure the most appropriate design 
of the individual parts and thereby molds. As carbon fiber parts of this size are expensive, additional 
funds might be needed for the project in order to produce this new design, but it is assumed that this 
would be possible due to the many connections DTU Roadrunners has to the industry.

Further work 
At the end of this project, some further work still needed to be carried out on the design and 
construction of the generated concept in order to optimize it and actually make it realizable.  
First of all, the wind tunnel test on the three concept models would have to be revised in order to obtain 
reliable results. The models would need to be scaled to a more suitable scale and subjected to a more 
appropriate wind speed. A revisit to the wind tunnel calculations at the end of this project revealed that 
the results gained from the 60m/s wind tunnel tests might actually be accurate for a full scale car 
moving at app. 10km/t53. One factor was however not taken into account during the wind tunnel 
testing, which may have a significant impact on the obtained results. The surface roughness of the 
models could not be measured, as the DTU technician controlling the necessary equipment was on 
vacation during the final two weeks of this project where the wind tunnel testing took place. The surface 
roughness of the models would therefore have to be checked against the physical car in order to make 
sure that the test models represent the actually surface quality of the DTU Dynamo carbon fiber body 
shell. Furthermore, the CAD models used for the CFD testing also showed some problems which may 
have affected the test results. The most significant of these problems regards the design of the venturi 
tunnels, which on two of the models turned out to have a too steep incline, and therefore which 
allowed the fluid flow to slip from the surface prematurely underneath the vehicle. The models could be 
improved in order to obtain better results.  
Regarding the design of the new ecocar, a form of dashboard could be fitted inside the cabin in order to 
enhance the production car appearance of the new car. Previous versions of DTU Dynamo have featured 
dash boards but the current version doesn’t. Another design element which still needs to be decided is 
whether or not the new car should feature open front wheel wells, like the vast majority of the other 
European Urban Concept teams, or should it feature enclosed wheel wells like the current DTU 
Dynamo?

Evaluation 
By following an engineering design process and using methods from product development and industrial 
design, it has been possible to generate a new exterior design for a DTU Urban Concept ecocar, which 
could replace the current DTU Phoenix ecocar. The process has been time consuming and a number of 
problems were encountered along the way. The new design has received good critic from the current 
DTU Roadrunners team, and has obtained a Cd- and CdA-values well below the average for current 
production cars. The main objective of the project was to see if it would be possible to generate a new 
design for the DTU ecocar which would be recognizable. By using knowledge and inspiration from the 
automotive inspiration and valuable first hand experiences with ecocar design during the Shell Eco-
Marathon 2015, this has been possible, as a number of students from outside the ecocar team also 
acknowledged that the new design could be mistaken for a production car, rather than an Urban 
Concept car.

Conclusion 
By drawing inspiration from the automotive industry, analyzing competing vehicles and conducting CFD 
and wind tunnel testing, it has been possible to generate a new design for the DTU Urban Concept car. 
The new design may not be as aerodynamically efficient as the previous design due to its dimensioning, 
but is more recognizable as an actual car due to its based on user reactions. The lacking aerodynamic 
performance can be achieved by re-dimensioning the project, or ignored by fitting a passenger space 
inside the vehicle. Using a number of carry-over components from the previous version of the ecocar 
and a new space frame design based on carbon fiber tubing, the production costs of the new design can 
be assumed to be relatively low, and the design can therefore be assumed to be relatively easily 
realizable.
