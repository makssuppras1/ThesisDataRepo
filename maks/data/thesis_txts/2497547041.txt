Summary
The higher penetration of photovoltaic energy in the power grid accentuates the need
for more accurate solar energy forecasts. To this purpose, studies suggest that the use
of spatio-temporal information, improves the performance of the available methods for
making solar predictions and that a type of model called “convolutional neural network”
(CNN) performs better than other state of the art techniques in handling data structured
in a regular grid. Hence, the present thesis compared the forecast performance for the
2, 4, 6 and 8 hour horizon of two CNN’s with respect to naive time series models and
to two state of the art machine learning methods, namely: a lasso regularized vector
autoregression (lasso-VAR) and a gradient boosted regression tree (GBRT). The CNN’s
and the machine learning models were trained and optimized using four years of satellite
data (2015-2018) and the performance of all the models was then compared on one
year of observations (2019). The results showed that the spatio-temporal models are
able to make a general better assessment of the state of the sky, but their advantage
with respect to the naive models decreases as the lead time increases.
The results
from a Diebold-Mariano hypothesis test indicate that the CNN’s perform consistently
better than the other models up until 6 hours ahead, but at greater forecast horizons,
the observed differences in forecast scores in terms of RMSE can only be considered
significant if the CNN model is complex enough. With respect to the training time and
the amount of hyperparameters to tune, once the relevant hyperparameters are identified,
the computational effort of optimizing the CNN’s is comparable to the training of the
GBRT at the expense of achieving a better model.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Context and motivation
As photovoltaic (PV) power becomes more competitive, the amount of installations
worldwide have increased [61]. In fact, PV installed capacity experienced the an aver-
age yearly growth of 37% between 1990-2017, which is the highest among the different
renewable energy sources [65], and studies predict similar trends for the upcoming years
[69].
However, cloud movement can lead up to 60% ramping in the nominal output power
of a PV plant, which is why at solar penetration rates above 15% the intermittent nature
of the PV output may affect grid operation and threaten the security of supply [57]. In
response to this variability, the operation of the electric system must be backed up by
ancillary conventional generators [33] at higher operational costs than normal, as they
have to operate at sub-optimal eﬀiciency levels, turn on and off more often and also have
to change their output levels more frequently (which increases the wear-and-tear of the
units). Furthermore, if the solar power production is higher than expected and the grid
penetration rate is high enough, the grid operators might be in the need of curtailing
the renewable power production [55]. Thus, over- and under-producing solar electricity
often results in increased capital and operational costs [33].
Consequently, the ability to forecast oscillations of solar production becomes increas-
ingly important to the integration of this technology in the electric system as solar
penetration grows [52][59].
Actually, renewable energy forecasts allow the electricity
market participants for estimating the reserves, scheduling of the power system, man-
aging system congestion, optimizing the management of a storage systems, developing
strategies for trading the produced power in the electricity market, and for increasing
the profitability of renewable power production [80].
In PV power prediction systems, forecasting of global horizontal irradiance (GHI)
is the first and most essential step [12]. However, this task is of great complexity due
to the non-linear and chaotic effect of cloud motion on solar irradiance at the ground
level [33][5]. Therefore, for the short-term forecasting, models that use spatio-temporal
information as input often provide more reliable forecasts compared to the traditional
time series methods [37].
Among the different models available for making forecasts, machine learning tech-
niques have been widely used. Particularly, artificial neural networks (ANN’s) are the
mostly researched methods in global radiation forecasting [80]. In recent years, a partic-
ular type of ANN, called convolutional neural network (CNN), has shown better perfor-

mance than other ANN’s in tasks such as image recognition and classification because
of its particular ability to handle data structured in a regular grid, since it consider local
neighboring features on different scales and because it has high statistical eﬀiciency by
parameter sharing [24]. Consequently, various studies show that these type of models
have good performance when processing spatio-temporal information for solar power [35]
and solar irradiance prediction [22][46][81].
In the case of Germany, ranked among the top ten countries in electricity consump-
tion [23], solar power has increased 3.5 times in the last decade [19], reaching over 1.6
million PV plants in operation [1], for a total installed capacity of 45.9 GW by 2018.
This is enough to supply 45% of the momentary electricity demand on sunny weekdays,
or even 60% on weekends and holidays [83]. Consequently, different German entities are
dedicating efforts for improving the quality of solar forecasts. In particular, the Fraun-
hofer Institute for Energy Economics and Energy System Technology (IEE), in Kassel,
is working together with the German Weather Service and grid operators to provide new
methods for the improved estimation of the future PV power supply within the next few
hours to days [25].
1.2
Objectives
Based on the previous framework, Fraunhofer IEE is interested in investigating better
ways of processing satellite images with solar irradiance and ultimately being able to
improve their forecasts for solar power production. Particularly, they want to assess
the suitability of convolutional neural networks with respect to other machine learning
models.
The main objective of this project is, then, to compare the forecasting performance
of convolutional neural networks with respect to other machine learning techniques at
using satellite based measurements of solar irradiation as the main input. A first step
towards this goal, is to develop a customized CNN model for forecasting short-term
solar irradiance at ground level. As ANN’s are able to represent non-linear relationships,
a linear and a non-linear machine learning model are to be optimized and used as
benchmark.
1.3
Research Question
Based on the previous sections, the task to be addressed can be framed in the following
research question:
• How do convolutional neural networks perform with respect to other machine
learning models in the short-term (2-8 hours) forecasting of solar irradiance based
on spatio-temporal date from satellite?

1.4 Delimitations

1.4
Delimitations
The following considerations are done to specify the scope of the study.
First, state of the art convolutional neural networks (CNN’s) are combinations of
different types of artificial neural networks. However, the interest of this project is on
CNN’s, so hybrid architectures are not implemented. Also, the focus is on forecasting of
solar irradiance, since it is the most important part of solar power oscillation, as it was
cited in the introduction. Further details of the application of such forecast are provided
in the Appendix A.
For comparing the forecast performance of the CNN model, two benchmark models
are chosen before hand based on previous literature review. The linear model to be used
is a lasso-regularized vector autoregression (Lasso-VAR), while the non-linear one is a
gradient boosted regression tree (GBRT). The training of all the models is done using
irradiance measurements from satellites. Hence, no numerical weather predictions are
used as inputs.
Also, for compatibility reasons with the existing platform at the research institute,
all the data handling and the models were trained and tested in Matlab, version 2018b
[48], which will be simply referred to as Matlab in the rest of this document.
1.5
Outline
The body of this thesis is structured in the following way:
• Chapter 2: State of the art A general overview of state of the art methods for
renewable energy forecasting based on spatio-temporal information are presented
and discussed.
• Chapter 3: Methodology The mathematical theory of the models used is ex-
plained in a technical level, with focus on features available in Matlab, when it is
considered necessary.
• Chapter 4: Experimental setup A description of the techniques used for han-
dling the data, engineering the features, optimizing the models and evaluate their
performance is provided.
• Chapter 5: Results and discussion The obtained results from the study are
presented and analyzed.
• Chapter 6: Conclusions and further work The main conclusions are drawn
and actions for further work in the processing of satellite images with CNN’s are
suggested.

CHAPTER 2
State of the art
In this chapter, first an overview of the models that have been used to make forecasts
in the field of solar energy is provided. This is mostly based on the works by Inmann
et al. [33], Voyant et al. [80] and Antonanzas et al. [3], who made extensive reviews of
methods used in solar irradiance and solar power forecasting. Then, examples of studies
that use spatio-temporal data are presented.
2.1
Basics of forecasting
In forecasting, the horizon is the amount of time between present time t and the effective
time of the predictions, the forecast resolution describes the frequency at which the
forecasts are issued, and the forecast interval denotes the time range of predictions [3].
The different authors provide various classifications of the type of forecasts and models
used. However, an adaptation of the classification made by Lago et al. [41] is found more
general. This criteria classifies the type of models used for forecasting solar irradiance
based on the input data:
1. Time series models based on sky images, measurements on the ground level
or satellite images. Within this field, the literature can be further divided into:
(a) classical statistical models (like ARMA and ARIMA), (b) artificial intelligence
models such as neural networks models, support vector machines, or decision trees-
based models, and (c) cloud-moving vector models that use satellite images.
2. Numerical weather prediction (NWP) models that simulate weather con-
ditions using physical models.
These methods are suitable for longer forecast
horizons (ca. 5 hours onward), where they outperform the statistical models. A
detailed explanation of these models can be found elsewhere [9].
2.1.1
Sources of spatio-temporal data
Sky images obtained with an sky camera located at the forecast location are the best
input data for predicting several minutes ahead in the future, whereas sensor networks
and satellite images are both suitable for short term up to 4-6 hours[5], in which case
the forecast horizon and its precision increases with the spatial resolution and the size
of the region that is represented in the data [5][84].
==PAGE:18==
6
2 State of the art
Ground measurement of solar radiation are commonly done using two types of solar
radiometers: a pyrheliometer and a pyranometer. Their measurements may be consid-
ered valid in a 30 km radius. However, their investments and maintenance costs limit
the spatial coverage of the sensors. Therefore, stations are usually too far to deliver
accurate and satisfactory information to forecasters [58].
On the other hand, satellite observations from meteorological geostationary satellites,
orbiting at about 36,000 km, can offer images with frequency of up to 15 minutes and a
spatial resolution of up to 1 km. Processing of these images has allowed the identification
and forecasting of the clouds evolution, the fraction of the sky coverage, the type of cloud
[58][36], its size, thickness and altitude [5], leading to the prediction of spatial variability
of solar radiation at the ground level. Satellite measurements are then considered a
suitable solution for forecasting solar irradiance at locations that are further away than
25 km from a ground station [58].
Nonetheless, according to Antonanzas et al.
[3]
publications on deterministic forecasts based on satellite information are scarce with
respect to the other types.
When comparing satellite and ground data, one of the main diﬀiculties that satellite
data are instantaneous “snapshots” while ground data have been historically integrated
over time (typically an hour) [33].
2.1.2
Models that use satellite images
Inmann et al. [33] elaborate on the different models for processing satellite images, which
are further classified in physical and statistical models. The former ones were mostly
developed in the 1980’s and require a series of physical atmospheric parameters such
as diffuse radiation, atmospheric transmissivity and reflection coeﬀicient, absorption
coeﬀicient for slant water vapor and atmospheric albedo which vary interdependently in
time. The statistical models on the other hand, rely on simpler variables to obtain from
the satellite data such as the mean cloud brightness, the normalized clear brightness,
the clear sky irradiance, the clear sky index [33] and the Linke turbidity (TL), which
represents the number of normal clean and dry atmospheres that would be required to
produce the observed irradiance at ground level. In general it has been observed that
the accuracy of the forecast improves with hybrid models which consider both, solar
irradiance and other variables as exogenous variables (exogenous forecasting) [80].
Models to calculate the clear sky irradiance (i.e the absence of clouds) require solar
geometry, as well as local meteorological inputs such as ozone content, water vapor
content and/or the Linke turbidity. With respect to clear sky model selection, they refer
to the work of Ineichen et al. [32], who concluded that the accuracy of the forecast is
not highly dependent on the clear sky model itself and therefore, its selection criteria
can be tailored to the application and availability of data. However, Inmann et al. [33]
recommend the use of the Ineichen model or the one from the European Solar Radiation
Atlas (ESRA) as they rely on only one experimental parameter.

2.2 Linear models
7
2.2
Linear models
Autoregressive (AR) models are used as reference models in forecasting papers due to
their simple implementation, fast computation, robust results, and clear interpretation
[11]. In this direction Dambreville et al. [11] used a vector autoregression (VAR) model
to study the influence area of cloud coverage from satellite irradiance data and ground
based information for 15 to 60 min in the future. They used the weights of their model
to identify the relevant area for each forecast horizon, which was later used to increase
the resolution of the images and the performance of the model for the selected test
location. Xu et al. [84] showed how the forecast performance increased when a VAR
was used instead of a simple AR model, as it is able to model the underlying correlation
among locations, i.e. by giving a higher weight to the lagged value of the most relevant
neighbors, without explicit interpretation of wind field or cloud movement. In their
work, they also show how the model performance improves when the model is both
regularized and recursively allowed to adapt to the different seasonalities. Similar results
with respect to forecast performance were obtained in the field of wind power forecasting
by Messer and Pinson [50], when comparing a lasso regularized adaptive VAR algorithm
with respect to non-adaptive lasso VAR and univariate autoregression models.
2.3
Non-linear machine learning models
With respect to other machine learning techniques, a support vector regression has
be used by Amaro et al.
[2] to provide a forecast based on time series of different
photovoltaic plants. In their study, they conclude the SVR to have lower computational
time than an autoregression with exogenous variables, however the forecast skills tend
to overlap.
Dong et al. [14] used solar irradiance time series, satellite image analysis and a hybrid
exponential smoothing state space (ESSS) to forecast a cloud cover index, which was
fed into a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) to derive hourly solar irradiance in Singapore.
This hybrid model was found to outperform autoregressive integrated moving average
(ARIMA), linear exponential smoothing (LES), simple exponential smoothing (SES) and
random walk (RW) models.
Torres et al. [76] studied the application of Random Forest Regression (RFR), Gra-
dient Boosted Regression Trees (GBRT) and extreme Gradient Boosted Trees (XGBT)
to global and local wind energy and solar radiation forecasting. They showed that these
ensemble methods can improve on SVR in the forecast of solar irradiance. They also
considered an MLP model in their study, which in all cases fell behind with respect to
the other models. In a similar direction, Persson et al. [60] made multi-site solar power
forecasts using GBRT’s. They used power generation and relevant meteorological vari-
ables related to 42 individual PV rooftop installations to train different models. When
compared to single-site linear adaptive AR and variations of GBRT models the multi-site
model showed competitive results in terms of root mean squared error on all forecast

3.2.1
Training
Training a machine learning models is an optimization process, which consists on min-
imizing the error on the data used for training the model (i.e. training set). However,
during the training process machine learning models are often able to reduce the train-
ing error by fitting the random noise in the training data. This is called overfitting and
reduces the performance of the resulting models when tested on new unseen information,
thus increasing the gap between the training error and test error. On the contrary, if
the model is too simple, it will under-fit the data [80]. When choosing a training and
testing set, it is important that examples in both sets are independent from each other
and that the distribution of the test data represents later application of the model [24].
Most machine learning algorithms have hyperparameters, settings that can be used to
control the algorithms’ behavior. The values of the hyperparameters are not optimized
by the learning algorithm itself during training, otherwise such the maximum model
capacity would always be chosen, resulting in overfitting. Consequently, a validation
set of examples that the training algorithm does not observe during training, must be
taken from the training set and used to find the optimal values of the hyperparameters.
However, if the model design is iterated on a limited size data set or for tuning several
hyperparameters, then their selected values might be adapted to the validation data but
not for real world tasks [6]. A common procedure t reduce this risk, known as k-fold
cross-validation, consists on partitioning the data set into “k” non-overlapping subsets.
The test error may then be estimated by taking the average test error across k trials. On
trial i, the i-th subset of the data is used as the test set, and the rest of the data is used as
the training set. Any modification that is made to a learning algorithm that is intended
to reduce its generalization error but not its training error is called regularization [24].

Training a neural network
Neural networks are often trained using variations of the stochastic gradient descent
algorithm (SGD). This method first needs the weights to be initialized. Then it iter-
atively moves the weights of the neural network in the direction of steepest descent,

which usually leads to a local minimum with good operational performance. The main
characteristics of weight initialization are now described, followed by an explanation of
the SGD algorithm and its main variations.
3.5.1.1
Weight initialization
Weight initialization can determine how quickly the learning process converges and
whether it reaches to a point with high or low error error. There are different meth-
ods for assigning the initial values, but unfortunately, the understanding of how the
initial point affects generalization is still primitive. However, all methods make sure
that the parameters “break symmetry” between the different units. Also, it is known
that larger initial weights will yield a stronger symmetry-breaking effect, helping to
avoid redundant units and dead neurons. On the other hand, too big values might make
the network to diverge during training [24]. In the utilized version of Matlab [48], the
initialization is done by independently sampling from a normal distribution with zero
mean and standard deviation 0.01 [10].
3.5.1.2
Stochastic gradient descent
This technique requires the computation of the negative gradient of the loss with respect
to the weights. Since the computation of the exact gradient over the whole training batch
can be very time consuming or even impossible (depending on the size of the data set),
SGD approximates the gradient by using only a sub-sample (i.e. minibatch) of size n
in every iteration. Doing so, increases the standard error of the estimated gradient by
a factor of √n, while decreasing the computation time by a factor of n, which results
in faster convergence. However, it is crucial that the mini-batches be selected randomly
so that two subsequent minibatches are independent from each other (i.e the data must
be shuffled). Typical minibatch sizes are in the range of 32 to 256, but this depends
on the size of the model, the size of the data and the available memory [24]. At a given
iteration t, the weights w are updated using the following iterative expression:
wt+1 = wt −v · gt
(3.20)
where gt = ∇wL(wt, MBn) and the learning rate 0 <v controls the size of the steps

gradient of the loss ∇wL(·) with respect to the weights for a given minibatch MBn =
{x(i:i+n); y(i:i+n)} and weight vector wt, is calculated using ”backpropagation”.
This
method consists on two steps: forward and backward pass. During the forward pass,
the activations of all layers and the loss function for the samples in the minibatch are
calculated. Then, during the backward pass, the chain rule is applied for calculating an
expectation of the gradient [24][68]. One epoch is when an entire dataset is passed both
forward and backward through the neural network only once.

3.5.2
Regularization of neural networks
Given the complexity and flexibility of ANN’s, they are prone to overfitting if no regular-
ization is applied. Several techniques such as L1 and L2 regularization can be utilized,
however it requires setting the right amount of regularization. In this section two popular
regularization methods are presented: early stopping and dropout.
3.5.2.1
Early stopping
This technique is probably the most commonly used form of regularization in deep learn-
ing due to its effectiveness, its simplicity and the fact that it reduces the computational
cost of the raining procedure. Early stopping consists on computing the validation error
with an specific frequency. However, every time the error on the validation set improves,
a copy of the model parameters is stored. Then, if the error on the validation set has not
beaten the best value after some pre-specified number of iterations (i.e. validation pa-
tience), the training algorithm terminates. However, the training algorithm will return
the parameters that performed best that provided the best validation error, rather than
the latest parameters. In this case, the number of epochs is just another hyperparameter
[24].
3.5.2.2
Dropout
Dropout can be thought of as a method of training many neural networks at the same
in one single model time in a practical way [24]. The key idea is to randomly set neuron
activations to zero with a given probability p during training of one network.
This
prevents the weights from co-adapting too much. The hyperparameter p controls the
intensity of dropout.
In Matlab, a higher p number results in more elements being
dropped during training. A suitable value of p can be chosen using a validation set or
==PAGE:32==
20
3 Methodology
can simply be set at 0.5, which seems to be close to optimal for a wide range of networks
and tasks [73].
During training, dropout introduces a significant amount of noise in the gradients. In
order to make up for this, a dropout net should typically use 10-100 times the learning
rate that was optimal for a standard neural net. It could also use higher momentum in
the range of 0.95 to 0.99. These modifications help to speed up the learning [73].
3.6
Convolutional neural networks
In a normal neural network, every output interacts with every input unit. However,
convolutional networks use convolution in place of general matrix multiplication in at
least one of their layers. This makes them more eﬀicient in computation because the
convolution operation allows them to have sparse connectivity. They are also easier to
store because all the activations in a feature map share a limited number of parameters.
A typical convolutional layer consists of a stack of three sub-layers. The first of these
sub-layers performs several convolutions in parallel to produce a set of linear activations.
In the second sub-layer (detector stage), each linear activation is run through a non-
linear activation function. In the third stage, a pooling function is used to summarize
the output of the layer [24].
Different configurations of these operations have been
applied. For example, several sets of convolution-activation operations can be used in
series before pooling. Also, after Ioffe et al. [34] it has become a common practice to
apply batch normalization (see Section 3.6.3) between the convolution and the activation
operations. A typical CNN architecture for image recognition and regression consists of
the following layers:
• imageInputLayer
• Body=[(convolution, batchNormalization, activation) x N , maxPooling] x M
• Close=[(convolution, batchNormalization, activation) x O , averagePooling]
• fullyConnectedLayer x P
• regressionLayer
It is a common practice to double the number of filters after every pooling operation.
In case all the connections are made in series the structure is called a feedforward network
[24].

The default setting applies normalization to the data by calculating one single average
image in representation of all the channels, which is then substracted to the input data.
This feature is called “zerocenter” and can be turned on and off by the user.

3.6.4
Activation layers
An activation layer applies a non-linear operation to each feature of the input feature
map. In the field of neural networks, many activation functions have been developed [56],
however the soca called rectified linear unit (ReLU) activation function has been the most
widely used for deep learning applications with state-of-the-art results to date. It can be
computed as ReLU(x) = max(0, x). It also offers better performance and generalization
in deep learning compared to others such as the hyperbolic and sigmoid activation
functions, and introduces sparsity in the hidden units as it squishes the values between
zero to maximum. As it is nearly linear and does not compute neither exponentials
nor divisions,it is also faster to compute than tanh. However, ReLU has a tendency to
overfit the data if no regularization is used and can cause some of the gradients (and
neurons) to die [56].
On the other hand, a modified function called “leaky ReLU” introduces some small
slope (α ∼0.01) to the ReLU for values smaller than zero, to sustain and keep the
weight updates alive during the entire propagation process, thus it avoids dead neurons.
It can be defined as leakyReLU(x) = max(α · x, x), with 0 < α < 1 [56].
3.6.5
Pooling
A pooling layer replaces the output of the previous layer with a summary statistic of the
nearby outputs, such as the maximum (max pooling) or the average (average pooling)
within a rectangular neighborhood defined by the pooling size. This helps to make the
representation approximately invariant to small translations of the input, which means
if the input is translated by a small amount, then the values of most of the pooled
outputs do not change. Invariance to local translation can be useful if we care more
about whether some feature is present than exactly where it is [24].
Using a stride s > 1 improves the computational eﬀiciency of the network because
the next layer has roughly s2 times fewer inputs to process [24]. The pooling size and
the stride are commonly employed the same, however, if the pooling size is greater than
the stride (overlapping pooling) the model might be less likely to overfit [39]. A usual
value is s = 2 which reduces the number of features to one fourth, followed by a doubling
of the amount of filters in the next convolutional layer.
State of the art convolutional networks usually use max pooling for hidden units and
average pooling in the end [75]. However, variations of this are also used in different
architectures. For example, Springenberg et al. [72] showed that max-pooling can simply
be replaced by a convolutional layer with increased stride without loss in accuracy on
several image recognition benchmarks. Thus, the summary statistic obtained by the
pooling operation is learned.

3.6.6
Modern architectures
Various modifications of the basic feedforward convolutional layer architecture that was
explained at the introduction of Section 3.6 have yielded better performance in famous
competitions. Two interesting cases are the inception and the residual networks.
The inception network, by Szegedy et al. [74], was responsible for setting the new
state of the art for classification and detection in the ImageNet Large-Scale Visual Recog-
nition Challenge 2014 (ILSVRC 2014), with 12 times less parameters than the previous
benchmark. Its main architectural improvements are:
• In the classic architecture, the choice of the filter size is a hyperparameter to tune.
Bigger filters are able to extract features of greater size in a image, but at a squared
computational cost. The inception architecture gives flexibility in this decision by
stacking various convolutional layers of various sizes in parallel. In concrete, the
layer consists of a parallel arrange of 1x1, 3x3, 5x5 convolutions and fourth path
with a pooling operation.
• In order to compensate the additional computational effort, 1x1 filters are used for
reducing the number of channels that enter each of the 3x3 and 5x5 convolutional
filter. The structure also includes a non-linear function between each convolution
in order improve the capacity of the network.
• The 5x5 filters can be changed for a sequence of two 3x3 filters, which has the
same view factor, with a reduced number of parameters [75].

4.2.1
Image size reduction
The original size of the images is reduced, so that several years of data can be fit in
the computer memory while training the models.
The image size reduction is done
by averaging the values over grid distributed blocks of 6x10 pixels, which shrinks the
original images to a size of 25x25 pixels. This block size is observed to best match
the original images, so that the borders—which provide information relevant for longer
time horizons—are preserved. Thus, each pixel of the shrunk images is spaced around
37,6-39,8 km in the North-South direction and between 31,6-32,8 km in the East-West
direction, which corresponds to an area of about 1190 - 1305 km2 per pixel. Hence, the
new aspect ratio is more similar to that of a square. As a reference, Paulesco et al. [58]
mention that ground irradiance measurements can be considered valid at a 30 km radius
from the meausuring station. In the present case, even though the spacing between the
central point of each pixel is higher than this threshold, each pixel is the average over
a whole region instead of a point measurement, and can be therefore considered more
representative than a ground measurement.

Hastie [27], states that for most applications, choosing a size of the weak learners J

different values of J in this region. Then, for a given tree size, effective regulatization
can be done by tuning the number of boosting iterations M and the learning rate v,

The Deep Learning Toolbox of Matlab is used for training the CNN’s. This software
package offers several options for tuning the different layers and the training process, as

4.6.1
Initial architecture
The initial architecture used in the tests is a modified version of the default feedforward
CNN presented by Matlab for regression tasks [77].
This base model has then the
following characteristics:
• Input Layer: The default zero-center feature is kept the same as in theory it
favors the speed of the learning process, and avoids scenarios where changes in the
filter have little to no effect in the non-linearity response.
• Activation: the default Matlab network uses ReLU. However, this still has the
risk of leading to dead neurons. Thus, the leaky ReLU is used instead with a slope
in the negative side of α = 0.01.
• Convolution: the filter size of 3x3 with a stride s = 1 and zero-padding of
p = 1 is not changed. State of the art CNN’s used for image recognition have a
width decades or hundreds of filters. However, the number of filters increases the
computational time and the risk of overfitting. The default Matlab network has
8 filters and during previous tests, showed somewhat better performance than the
naive models. Hence the number of filters in the first layer is raised to 16. The
depth is kept the same, which means the network has 4 layers of convolution.
• Pooling: The model has three pooling layers that make an average pooling oper-
ation with a stride of s = 2 and a 2x2 filter.

4.6.3.1
Pooling
During the preliminary tests, it is noticed that the default setting of the pooling layer
(filter size of 2x2 with a stride of s = 2) fits a 24x24 image instead of a 25x25 image,
thus leading to uneven representations of the feature map borders after the first pooling
layer. Three options are considered to study this:
• reduce the initial image size to 24x24,
• applying a convolutional 2x2 filter with zero padding at the first layer instead of
the 3x3 filter, and
• using an overlapping pooling with stride 2 and 3x3 filter also only in this first layer.
At a second stage, the possibility of using an overlapping pooling operation through-
out the network is also explored, and the different possible pooling operations are tested,
namely: max pooling, average pooling or using a convolutional filter of size 2x2, no
padding and stride s = 1.

4.8
Test for equality of model performance
on the test set
Given various sources of forecasts of the same quantity, it often happens that one set of
forecasts will appear more successful than another by a small amount. In such a case,
the question naturally arises as to how likely it is that this outcome is due to chance
[26]. For clarifying such situations, Diebold and Mariano [13] developed a hypothesis
test of equal accuracy in forecast performance, which compares the value of the so called
Diebold-Mariano statistic (DM) with respect to threshold value (i.e p-value) obtained
from the normal distribution for a given confidence level. This test is performed in the
final comparison of the models with a significance of α = 5%. As this is a two tailed
comparison, the null hypothesis of equality in forecast performance can be rejected when
the test statistic is |DM| > 1.96. The mathematical details of the method are provided
in the Appendix G.

been reached.
Also, as the forecast horizon increases, the effect of augmenting the model complexity
has an even lower impact on the skill and most of the improvement is already given by
training only one parameter with the climatology model.

than 90% of the variables so that the model considers only between 100-182 inputs

Number of hyperparameters
A practical aspect to take into account when comparing the the spatio-temporal models

the highest impact on the validation RSME’s, meaning that the effective tuning of this
model could be done with only two hyperparameters.
Finally, the CNN’s had a lot of aspects to consider, which added complexity to their
optimization. However, after experimenting with several architectures and ending up

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions and further
work
The goal of this thesis was to evaluate the performance of CNN’s with respect to other
machine learning models at making short term predictions of solar irradiance, using
spatio-temporal data from satellites. To this purpose, a base convolutional neural net-
work (“CNN1”) was structured following general guidelines that have been developed in
the field of image classification and recognition. This initial model was later optimized
in a systematic stage-wise process for the forecast of solar irradiance until a second
model had been obtained and customized for this task (“CNN2”). Then, both CNN1
and CNN2 were trained on four years of data and their performance was tested on an
out-of-sample data set. A lasso-regularized vector autoregression (“lasso-VAR”) and a
gradient boosted regression tree (GBRT) model were also optimized and trained for this
task using the same spatio-temporal data as the CNN models so that a fair comparison
could be made. Three naive time series models were also kept into consideration, in the
analysis, namely: persistence, climatology and a linear regression of them.
A visual inspection of the time series forecasted by the different methods and the
performance metrics showed that the spatio-temporal models are able to make a general
better assessment of the state of the sky for providing the forecasts. This means, they
are not able to predict the detailed ups and downs of the solar irradiance, but they
rather provide a smoothed trend line of the expectations.
The advantage of the spatio-temporal techniques with respect to the naive models is
better shown by the RMSE for shorter horizons, while this difference becomes narrower
for lead times of 6-8 hours. However, at around 4 hours in the future, the changes in
the atmosphere are better described by non-linear models. Also, the CNN’s perform
consistently better up until 6 hours ahead, and after that, only CNN1 presents an
advantage from the rest. However, the margin between the CNN’s and the next best
model is between 3-5 W/m2 for the 2-6 hour horizon and then —depending on the
CNN—this difference is not even observable.
In order to clarify whether these small differences are due to chance, a Diebold-
Mariano hypothesis test was performed for evaluating the superiority in forecast skill.
Based on this, it was possible to find out with a 95% confidence that CNN1 performs
better than CNN2 in the 8 hour horizon and that CNN1 also performs significantly
better than the GBRT, the lasso-VAR and the naive models in the 2, 4, 6 and 8 hour
horizon. This hypothesis test also allowed to conclude that that CNN2 performs better
than the benchmark models in all the tested horizons, except for the case of the 8 hour

forecast with respect to the lasso-VAR.
In regards to the mean bias error, the CNN’s showed among the lowest errors for the
2-4 hour horizon. However, as the lead time increased, their bias became increasingly
negative, which was not the case of the naive models. Also, the maximum absolute error
of the non-linear models is overall greater than the naive and the lasso-VAR. However,
the magnitude of this error varies with the model.
When it comes to practical matters, the results showed that the number of coeﬀicients
and how the network is trained are more important than fine tuning the connections
inside the network—once the model contains the basic state of the art elements of a
CNN. Hence, the number of important hyperparameters of CNN’s can be significantly
reduced, so that the implementation of such a model becomes comparable to other
machine learning models. However, care must be taken in selecting the right size of the
network when it comes to optimizing the training time as this one increases with the
number of inputs, parameters to train, a well as with the number of layers inside the
network.
Consequently, CNN’s should be considered in the application of solar forecast, since
in this scenario the results indicate an advantage over models which do not consider the
spatial strucuture of satellite data.
6.1
Further work
Given the time constraints of this project, and also based on these conclusions, the
following recommendations are made for the future:
Due to the stochastic nature of training a neural network, the output of the training
process can be viced with noise that obscures the relative improvement during the hyper-
parameter optimization. This was handled in several ways like increasing the amount
of data in the training set, which also extended the computation time and constrained
the complexity of studied models. To address this issue, a better control of the random
number generation could be implemented by “seeding” the generation of random num-
bers, so that the sources of noise can be blocked but still advantage can also be taken
of them.
After fine tuning many different hyperparameters of the CNN’s and the GBRT, a
general assessment was made in retrospective about the significant hyperparameters to
tune. However, in order to have more confidence in the decisions, and make a more
through analysis, future studies could implement the Diebold-Mariano test as part of
the hyperparameter optimization.
A natural next step for improving the model performance is to optimize the spatial
resolution of the input information to the forecast horizon. To this purpose, the coeﬀi-
cients of the lasso-VAR model could be used so that only relevant inputs are fed into
the model. However, as time horizon increases, the area of influence becomes greater
and regression trees or other non-linear methods could be used to research the size and
averaging of the grid (image) in dependence of the forecast horizon.

APPENDIX A
Relationship between solar
irradiance and solar power
The utilization of solar energy depends on the availability of sunlight. Outside Earth’s
atmosphere, the solar irradiance is a relatively constant value of 1367 W/m2; which
oscillates ±3.3% due to the eccentricity of the Earth’s orbit [17] and about ±1.1%
during the solar cycles [58]. When the solar radiation flux passes through the Earth’s
atmosphere, its spectral distribution is modified by [49]:
• Reflection of light, mainly from clouds .
• Absorption of specific wavelengths from oxygen, ozone, water and carbon dioxide,
among others.
• Rayleigh scattering from particles that are smaller than the incident wavelength.
Shorter wavelengths (blue side of the spectrum) are scattered particularly strongly
by atmospheric gases.
• Mie scattering due to aerosols and dust particles that are large compared to the
wavelength of light. Their concentration is greater in industrial and densely pop-
ulated areas.
In both Rayleigh and Mie scattering ligth’s direction is altered without loss of energy.
For any given set of atmospheric conditions, these light-atmosphere interactions reduce
the fraction of the incident extraterrestrial beam irradiance that reaches Earth surface.
The resulting irradiance is also divided into a direct and a diffuse component, which are
commonly measured on a horizontal plane and thus, referred to as Direct Normal Irradi-
ance (DNI) and Diffuse Horizontal Irradiance (DHI). The Global Horizontal Irradiance
(GHI) is then defined as [49]:
GHI = DHI + DNI ∗cos(θ)
(A.1)
Here, θ is the solar zenith angle [33], which depends in a deterministic way on the
location (latitude, longitude) and time of the year [49].
In order to better visualize the relation between solar power output and different
environmental factors, the results from a simulation made in PVsyst [64] for a 1 kWp
power plant located at 51° N and 10° E are presented in Figure A.1. Here it can be

APPENDIX H

H.1

H.1.1
Why solar irradiance forecasting?
As solar photovoltaic (PV) becomes more competitive, the amount of installations world-
wide have increased with a learning curve of 24% [61], that is, for every doubling in
installed capacity, the costs of solar energy have decreased by 24%. In fact, PV installed
capacity experienced an annual average growth of 37% between 1990-2017, which is the
highest among the different renewable energy sources [65] and studies predict similar
trends for the upcoming years [69].
However, cloud movement can lead up to 60% ramping in the output power of PV,
which is why at solar penetration rates above 15% the intermittent nature of the PV
output may affect grid operation and threaten security of supply [57]. Therefore, the
ability to forecast these variations is essential to the integration of solar power generation
in the electric system [52]. In fact, renewable energy forecasts are used for estimating
the reserves, scheduling of the power system, congestion management, optimal manage-
ment of the storage with stochastic production, for trading the produced power in the
electricity market and to achieve a reduction of the costs of electricity production [80].
In PV power prediction systems, forecasting of global horizontal irradiance (GHI) is
the first and most essential step [12]. However, this task is of great complexity due to
the non-linear and chaotic effect of cloud motion on solar irradiance at the ground level
[33][5]. Therefore, models that use spatio-temporal information as input often provide
more reliable forecasts compared to the traditional time series methods [37].
In the case of Germany, ranked among the top ten countries in electricity consump-
tion [23], solar power has increased 3.5 times in the last decade [19], reaching over 1.6
million PV plants in operation [1], for a total installed capacity of 45.9 GW by 2018.
This is enough to supply 45% of the momentary electricity demand on sunny weekdays,
or even 60% on weekends and holidays [83]. Consequently, different German entities are
dedicating efforts for improving the quality of solar forecasts. In particular, the Fraun-
hofer Institute for Energy Economics and Energy System Technology (IEE), in Kassel,
is working together with the German Weather Service and grid operators to provide new
methods for the improved estimation of the future PV power supply within the next few
hours to days [25].

H.1.2
Overview of solar forecasting methods
The accurate prediction of the irradiance varies with the input data, the location, the
type of climate [21] and the lead time [58]. Based on the lead time, the forecasts can be
split into ’now-casting’ (0-3 h), ’short-term forecasting’ (3-6 h) and ’forecasting’ (>6 h).
For now-casting and short-term forecasting, extrapolations of real-time measurements
(field and/or satellite data) tend to give the best results, while for several hours in the
future, Numerical Weather Prediction models (NWP’s) are usually more accurate [80]
[58].
Spatio-temporal prediction models lead to more reliable forecasts compared to the
temporal models [37]. Indeed, the precision increases with the size of the region that is
taken into account [5]. To this purpose, satellite data with a resolution of minutes-hours
used to provide short term forecasts [5] may consider the fraction of the sky coverage,
the type of cloud [58] [36], its size, thickness and altitude [5]. However, performance
of the satellite-based models are limited by the uncertainties of the satellite-based esti-
mations themselves, even if the use of short-term overlapping of in situ pyranometric
measurements is possible [7].
H.1.3
Linear models
Autoregressive (AR) models are used as reference models in forecasting papers due to
their simple implementation, fast computation, robust results, and clear interpretation
[11]. Studies show that these models improve their performance when they use spa-
tio temporal information and when the coeﬀicients are allowed to recursively adapt to
changing atmospheric regimes [84][50].
H.2
Non-linear models
Among the different non-linear machine learning methods used in solar forecasting, SVR,
regression trees, boosting, bagging and random forest are some of the most eﬀicient tech-
niques, and ”ensembles” of different predictors tend to give even better results. However,
artificial neural networks (ANN’s) are the mostly researched methods in global radiation
forecasting and in making ensembles [80].
A support vector regression (SVR) has been shown to have lower computation time
that autoregressive models, but yeilding similar forecast performance [2]. However, a
study by Torres et al. showed that tree ensemble methods can improve on SVR and multi-
layer perceptrons (MLP) in the forecast of solar irradiance. Also, a study by Persson
et al. [60] for multi-site solar power forecasts using gradient boosted regression trees
(GBRT’s) concluded that predictive performance and the simplicity of the model make
the boosted tree model a simple and attractive compliment to conventional forecasting
techniques. Also, another publication by Muckhoty et al. [53] compared the forecast
performance of a MLP, recurrent neural network (RNN) and long-short term memory

network (LSTM) and GBRT models, which use spatio-temporal information for solar
energy forecasting. They conclude that the GBRT tends to have greater bias than the
other models, but it performs better in terms of the mean squared error in the 0-24 hour
horizon. Part of their motivation for including the GBRT in their study was due to the
fact that this type of model won the Global Forecast Competition in 2014 [30].
H.2.1
Convolutional neural networks
In recent years, a particular type of ANN, called convolutional neural network (CNN),
has shown better performance than other ANN’s in tasks such as image recognition and
classification because of its particular ability to handle data structured in a regular grid,
since it consider local neighboring features on different scales and because it has high
statistical eﬀiciency by parameter sharing [24]. Hence, various studies suggest that these
models have good performance when processing spatio-temporal information for solar
power and solar irradiance prediction. For example, Ghimire et al. [22] showed that a
hybrid CNN-LSTM network was able to make better solar irradiance predictions than
the individual models and a recurrent neural networks (RNN), a deep neural network
(DNN), a MLP a GRU and a decision tree. Also, Wang et al. [81] proposed a deep
learning model based on wavelet decomposition, CNN and long-short term memory
(LSTM) for day-ahead solar irradiance forecasting, which considers the previous three
days of data and uses 64 convolutional filters. Again the forecast error was overall lower
than the individual equivalent deep learning and linear time series models, however the
observed performance depends on the location, type of weather and model architecture.
A different approach by Liu et al. [46] used 2 years of weather data and solar angles in
the form of 20x20 images as input. Their model, an ensemble of variational Bayesian,
3D-convolutional and a gate recurrent unit network (GRU), proved to be better than the
individual ANN’s, but using 50 times less parameters, only three layers in its architecture,
and limited amount of data with respect to the previous examples.

for such task. Consequently, the objectives of this project are:
• Develop a novel customized model for forecasting solar irradiance based on spatio-
temporal satellite information by using a convolutional network.
• Optimize the customized model for making solar power predictions for a given

H.5
Methodology
In order to achieve the proposed objectives, the first step is to define the models to be
used. Apart from the CNN model, the spatio-temporal models that will be implemented
for comparing the performance are a linear and a non-linear model. The selected linear
model is a regularized vector autoregression, given that it has been used in various studies
for analyzing spatio-temporal data and that it is relatively simple to apply. On the other
hand, the non-linear model to be considered is a gradient boosted regression tree. This
decision is based on the performance of this model in the Global Forecast Competition in
2014 and also on the suggestions found that this non-linear model can outperform other
types of neural networks and linear models when processing spatio-temporal information
for making forecasts.
With respect to the convolutional neural network, still a deeper research must be
done in order to establish a way of customizing the network for the given task. To this
purpose, a review of state of the art practices in the design of these models will be done
as neural networks in general have many aspects that at the moment of making this plan
are still are not entirely understood. However, a systematic approach will me adopted

in order to fine tune and optimize as much aspects of the model as possible to better
understand its behavior.
For the optimization of the models, standard machine learning procedures will be
utilized such as splitting the data in various sets for training, validating and testing the
models. Also various types of feature standarization and engineering will be evaluated
and selected for each type of model. The so called clear sky index standardization is
a first candidate to take into account [33]. During the optimization phase, the model
performance will be evaluated based on the root mean squared error (RMSE), which is
a common practice in this field [3]
Other models to be implemented are the so called “naive” models which are used
as benchmark in most forecast studies [33]. These estimators will not take any spatio-
temporal infomation into consideration, so that they can be used as reference for com-
paring the relative improvement of the spatio-temporal models with respect to them.
The data available corresponds to satellite images and, since the focus of the project
is on better understanding how the models process this type of information, no additional
sources of data—such as numerical weather predictions—will be taken into consideration.
For training the models, various years of information will be used, given that machine
learning models improve their performance based on the amount of data they are trained
on. However the selection of the size of the data sets will depend on a compromise
between the ability of the models for fitting the training data and the computational
time required for making the tests. Also, based on a first inspection of the satellite
images, some work has to be done in reducing the size of the images.
Filtering of
outliers, missing inputs and errors in the time series are part of the tasks.
The analysis of the results and proposal of actions to be done in continuing projects
will be presented in a written report and in an oral defense by the end of the project.
