Abstract
Over the past decade, underwater noise from pile driving during offshore wind tur-
bine installations has raised significant concerns, as its harmful impact on wildlife was
investigated in several studies. Since then, extensive research has been conducted to
predict this noise based on the understanding that the transient impact load generates
a stress wave in the steel pile. This wave reflects between the pile ends and radiates
into the water column as high-pressure (Mach) waves, resulting from the radial expan-
sion of the pile. Numerical simulations, mainly using finite element method (FEM),
have been employed to model this phenomenon, and the results have been validated
against measurement data.
In this project, an alternative approach was developed to address the high compu-
tational costs associated with conventional FEM models, featuring an equivalent line
array of sources along the pile. The alternative model utilized near-field results from
FEM as input for an underwater sound propagation model, which then simulates
noise in the far field by superimposing the contributions of individual sound sources.
The results from this alternative model were validated by comparing them with those
from the conventional FEM model. Additionally, parametric studies were performed
to assess the impact of various model parameters on the accuracy and eﬀiciency of
the alternative model. A recommended guideline was established for selecting these
parameters and understanding their physical implications on the system model for
future studies.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction
Over the past decade, the offshore wind power industry has grown significantly. In
2022, offshore wind accounted for approximately 24% of Denmark’s total domestic
electricity supply, with these figures increasing as capacity expands [1].
However,
along with the development of the industry, the levels of underwater noise during
impact-driven wind turbine pile installations (pile driving) have raised significant
concerns, as its harmful impact on wildlife has been investigated in several studies.
For marine animals, hearing is the primary source of communication, used to detect
and capture prey and navigate the sea. The noise affects the animals on different
levels based on their exposure time and range from the source. Figure 1.1 shows that
the effect on marine life is significant, even at large ranges, causing stress response
and masking, interfering with their functional requirements. While at short ranges,
it has been shown to cause injury or hearing loss, disabling the animal to fend for
itself [6, 10].
Figure 1.1. The different effects of underwater noise on marine life (Reproduced from [6])
Several countries have now introduced standards and regulations on the noise
produced by pile driving, limiting it in terms of different factors. Germany has well-
==PAGE:13==
1.2 Literature review
2
defined regulations for managing underwater noise during pile driving. The Federal
Maritime and Hydrographic Agency mandates that sound exposure levels 1 must not
exceed 160dB and peak sound pressure levels must stay below 190dB at 750m from
the source, 2m above the seabed for the frequency range 10Hz to 20kHz. These reg-
ulations protect marine mammals, especially harbour porpoises, from harmful noise
impacts [11]. Denmark follows general European Union directives for environmental
impact assessments that include considerations for underwater noise. Specific Danish
guidelines address the impacts of offshore wind construction, including noise from pile
driving, and require detailed marine impact studies as part of the permitting process.
However, no definitive noise threshold standards are outlined, like in Germany. Local
environmental agencies may set project-specific noise limits[5]. Hence, over the years,
extensive research has been conducted to simulate and mitigate this noise. Some com-
monly used techniques for sound simulation are the finite difference method (FDM),
the finite element method (FEM), the equivalent source method and others.
1.2
Literature review
Zampolli et al. [15] validated the noise simulated by FEM with measurement data
within feasible distances (limitations of FEM) and frequency ranges.
They used
a linear, axisymmetric, structural acoustic frequency domain finite element model,
which included the pile and the surrounding underwater environment. The use of
Wave Equation Analysis for Piling (WEAP) along with FEM was demonstrated by
Lützen et al. [4, 20], validated by comparison with measurements to confirm a good
match.
WEAP is a 1D FDM that accurately models the hammer force and the
interaction of the pile with the foundation. The force and energy calculated are fed
into the FEM model (2D axisymmetric) to calculate sound pressure at a distance from
the pile. These studies were for low-range sound propagations, focussed on accurately
modelling the hammer force.
Lippert et al. [22] is a compilation of several models developed on a benchmark pile
driving case. Most methods have a separate near-field and far-field model because the
sound fields are significantly different and are affected by different parameters. Several
different approaches are presented and compared with each other. It showcases that
most methods, varying in their model of the soil, chosen sound propagation models,
and near-field models, can predict sound levels with reasonable accuracy.
1Sound exposure level is widely used, denoting the total energy in a sound event over its entire
duration, expressed in decibels (dB).
==PAGE:14==
1.3 Objective of the project
3
Duncan et al. [3] used measurement data and different sound propagation methods
to model the pile as an equivalent source. The comparison with measurement data
was encouraging, though not exemplary. However, a few years later, Dahl et al. [19]
discovered that piling noise cannot be accurately modelled using a single point source
due to the inherent physics of how sound is radiated into the water. They proved
that the field could instead be accurately modelled as a line array of point sources
(equivalent source method) with a time delay between them based on the speed of
sound in the pile. This work led to more studies exploring the possibility of using
a line array coupled with a sound propagation model to accurately simulate sound
in the far field with lower computation load [18, 21]. Further, Wilkes et al. [24]
simplified this method by using the near-field FEM simulation and short-range sound
propagation model to calculate the source spectrum of the line array and feed that
into the long-range sound propagation model to simulate sound in the far-field. The
previous methods used time domain results or time delay, but solving everything
in the frequency domain removed the need to modify the sound field since it would
include the phase delay inherently. The method’s accuracy was proved by comparison
with measurement data.
1.3
Objective of the project
This project focuses on the method described in Wilkes et al. [24], referred to as
the ’alternative method/model’ throughout this report. COWI, a company that has
been involved in offshore wind projects for over twenty years, proposed the problem
statement. The company was interested in setting up the alternative method and
understanding the importance of parameters of the equivalent source method (ESM)
and their physical implications on the simulated sound field, which has not been stud-
ied thoroughly in previous implementations. It was asked to generate a recommended
list of to-dos and to-not-dos for future implementations of this method.
Hence, the alternative method was developed, utilizing the near-field results from the
FEM model and an underwater sound propagation model as input to an equivalent
line array of sources along the pile. The sound field is regenerated by superimposing
the contributions of individual sound sources. The regenerated sound field from this
alternative method is validated by comparison with the FEM simulations.

1.4
Research questions
The following research questions were defined at the beginning of the project: -
1. How do the predictions from the line array of sources model compare to tradi-
tional FEM models? How do they compare in computational eﬀiciency?
2. How do the chosen model parameters affect the simulation results and compu-
tation? Some important ones are listed here:
a) Meshing in FEM models
b) Boundary conditions and their implementation
c) Distance of reference plane from the pile
d) Number of sampling points
e) Number of sources in line array
3. What are the physical implications of the discrepancies observed between the
line array of sources model and the FEM model?
4. How can some parameters (as listed in Question 2) be modified to resolve these
discrepancies, and what is the physical meaning of those modifications?
5. What are the limitations of this approach? Is there anything that could not be
studied due to these limitations?
1.5
Report summary
The second chapter, Theory, discusses the physics of the problem statement and cov-
ers all the theoretical aspects of the models used for this project. The third chapter,
Methods describes the details of setting up the models on different software and the
design used for the alternative method. Then, the results of two preliminary models
are discussed, followed by discussions and key takeaways to improve the actual model.
Next, the results of the actual model and the parametric studies are presented, fol-
lowed by discussions on the model’s accuracy, reliability and tendencies. Finally, the
report concludes with a recommended list of to-dos and to-not-dos. A self-reflection
section is also included, highlighting some areas that could not be explored but might
be interesting for future work.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
2.1
Pile driving noise
An offshore wind turbine pile is a hollow cylinder used as a foundation structure to
support offshore wind turbines. These piles are driven into the seabed to provide
a stable base for the wind turbines. The pile installation process typically involves
using a large hydraulic hammer to drive the pile into the seabed.
The radiated
underwater noise from pile driving is due to the elastic wave from the impact of
the hammer travelling down the pile. This wave causes vertical compression, which
causes an outward radial deformation in a local pile element. The deformation is
referred to as the radial bulge, and it causes displacement of the surrounding water,
which gets transferred to the local element below and so on, as the energy from the
impact travels down to the bottom as shown in Figure 2.1. However, since the radial

2.2
Sound propagation models
Sound propagation in the sea or ocean can be described mathematically with sound
propagation models derived from different forms of the wave equation in cylindrical
coordinates. Different models are more suitable for different applications, ocean con-
ditions, frequency spans, and near-field or far-field ranges. These models allow for
variation of the environment with depth, and some also allow for variation with range,
referred to as range-dependent models. Some commonly used models are Ray tracing,
Wavenumber Integration, Normal Modes and Parabolic equation.
2.2.1
Wavenumber Integration Model (WNI)
The model is initiated with the depth-separated Helmholtz equation, followed by the
application of integral transforms to reduce the dimension of the wave equation. The
equation is then solved for different depth values for a horizontal wavenumber range
using the boundary conditions of the waveguide.
Next, an inverse integral trans-
form is applied to the solution, leading to the complex pressure varying with range
and depth. The WNI model is very accurate for acoustic propagation calculations
==PAGE:18==
2.2 Sound propagation models
7
at ranges close enough to the source, although it cannot account for environment
variations with range and incurs a relatively large computational load, making it un-
feasible for far-field simulations. The model allows the waveguide to be separated
into several homogeneous layers with different material properties and requires the
presence of an infinite bottom half-space at the bottom that absorbs all of the waves
and reflects nothing, forming the basis of a necessary boundary condition. This is
why the WNI model was chosen to solve the equivalent source problem in this project.
A detailed explanation of this model is provided in [12], which was also referred to
for this project.

2.2.2
Normal Mode (NM)
NM involves solving a depth-dependent equation (without enforcing integral trans-
forms) using the separation of variables. This model is very closely related to the
Wavenumber Integration. The solution of the wave equations finds a set of modes
of vibration of water in the waveguide, similar to the modes in a vibrating string,
called the ”normal modes”. The modal frequencies give the horizontal wavenumbers
associated with the wave propagation in the waveguide. The complete acoustic field
is then constructed by summing up the contributions of each mode [12]. For any
waveguide thickness, the angles of the modal wavefronts are more for higher frequen-
cies. At low frequencies, the modes, known as ”leaky modes”, they cannot travel in
the waveguide due to steep angles, and most of the energy flows into the soil. For
this reason, these modes cannot propagate into the far field. Since NM only considers
propagating modes, it cannot capture the leaky modes, thus making it inaccurate for
near-field calculations. The cut-off frequency at which the horizontally propagating
modes start to show up can be calculated for a waveguide of depth D, speed of sound
in water cw and speed of sound in seabed/soil cb by,

2.3.2
Parameters of ESM
A simple point source is defined as one whose size or spherical radius a is much smaller
than the wavelength of the sound it emits. However, as the wavelength reduces and
becomes similar in size, the source behaves more like a pulsating sphere rather than
a point source, the boundary effects coming into the solution to the sound field; thus,
the field very close to the sources is expected to be erroneous. At higher frequencies,
there are chances of coherence between the sources; thus, the configuration of the
sources is an important aspect in ESM [7]. The configurations relate to the parame-
ters selected to set up the ESM for underwater sound propagation, such as the size of
the inversion matrix, distance of sound field sampling and others. This has not been
studied extensively for underwater sound applications, limiting its practical applica-
bility [7].
Some of these parameters are listed here (Figure 2.3):
• Number of sources (n): The number of point sources distributed vertically along
the depth D.
• Number of receivers (m): The number of sources distributed vertically along
the depth D. These can be related to the ”information” or ”sampling” input
into the alternative method for calculating the source coeﬀicients.
• Distance of the reference plane from the equivalent sources (rref): This is the
horizontal distance to the vertical plane at which the field is sampled, i.e. at
which the receiver points are distributed.

2.3.3.1
Ill-conditioning
The ESM is inherently ill-conditioned even by considering the big difference in dis-
tance between the source and receiver furthest and closest from each other. Adding
on to that, the nature of the sound field radiated into the water with the conical
Mach waves and their reflections along with the the complex evanescent spectrum;
the pressure field is very irregularly distributed along the depth of the water column,
adding to the significant differences between the elements of the Green’s function
matrix that is to be inverted. A good way to quantify the ill-conditioning in a ma-
trix is the condition number, defined as the ratio of the sensitivity of the function to
changes in the argument. A big condition number indicates high ill-conditioning. For
matrices, it is defined as the ratio of the largest and smallest singular value,
κ(A) = ∥A∥∥A−1∥
(2.13)
As a rule of thumb, the digits of the accuracy of the solution lost due to ill-conditioning
can be calculated as 10 log10(κ). It then depends on the digits of accuracy stored in
the solver, and loss relative to that can be big or small [8].

2.3.3.2
Rank deficiency
Wilkes et al. [24] mention issues of rank deficiency at lower frequencies. In the case
of large wavelengths of the sound field, the pressure field does not change much along
the water column, causing coherence in the columns of the matrix and leading to rank
deficiency. This can lead to ambiguity in the least squares solution and numerical
instability during inversion.

3.3.1.3
Perfectly Matched Layers
Spherical PML had concerns about waves incident at angles other than usual inside
the waveguide. Meanwhile, the cylindrical PML allowed for more flexibility. The
PML implementation in COMSOL does not allow it to support two different mediums
on the same PML domain. Hence, the PML was broken down into two parts. Thus,
cylindrical PMLs were used for the waveguide layers (water and soil), and the spherical
PML was used for the soil bottom half-space. A mesh convergence for the PML was
also conducted and ensured that the distribution specified was suﬀicient to handle
the absorption of all wavelengths and field variations. However, there was a concern
about defining two PMLs with different material properties connected at the interface
but the pressure field in the interface between the two PMLs had a good enough
continuity, as shown in Figure 3.6, indicating that this issue was not a big concern.
The PML definition in COMSOL has some parameters that ensure all frequencies can
get absorbed with minimal reflections back into the domain, some of them are listed
here:
• Domain Width: The domain width ensures that the field has reached an equi-
librium and most of the evanescent spectrum has decayed before reaching the
PML, allowing the layer to absorb the remaining energy properly and reduc-
ing reflections. This reduces the possibility of creating standing waves in the
domain at certain frequencies and ruining the originally simulated sound field.
• PML width: The PML implementation is scaled with their geometrical width
based on the wavelength by default, thus making the defined width irrelevant to
==PAGE:37==
3.3 Final Model
26
the absorption. The critical parameter is the mesh that controls the effectiveness
of the PML, which must resolve the scaled layer.
• Scaling factor: The scaling factor makes it possible to stretch the PML geomet-
rically to increase the effective width.
• Curvature factor: The curvature factor allows the mesh to be modified based
on the stretching and a non-linear distribution of elements, allowing the PML
to absorb quickly near the boundary.

Scaling of the inversion matrix
Further, certain scaling processes were explored to address the ill-conditioning in the
system (details in section C.2). It was observed that for 1Hz, scaling, in general,
significantly reduces the error cost values, especially the addition of Gaussian noise.
However, for frequencies above that, the reduction is not consistent and is not as big.
The condition number was also not affected by scaling with range or with SVD. This
led to a question about whether ill-conditioning in this system was even a significant
issue to work on.
The loss of digits of accuracy is calculated for the maximum
condition number, which comes out to be 8. It was found that MATLAB uses 16
digits of accuracy by default [16]. Thus, the solution is accurate up to 8 significant
digits, corresponding to an error of 0.1% dB SPL re.10−6. Thus, the effect of ill-
conditioning can be said to not be big apart from very low frequencies. But for this
application where the sound pressure field in the far field is more important, lower
frequencies, usually <100Hz are not that important.
At this stage, the system was also tested with the use of lsqminnorm to check if
it solves with lower errors.
However, it was found that results did not show any
noticeable change, indicating that the backslash operator was the quickest and most
eﬀicient tool for this system since the computation time with lsqminnorm was twice
that of the former.

The computation time for the alternative method for the case where only 23 fre-

However, this method is proposed for
far-field calculations with a minimum range of 1km as per requirements from the reg-

made for the computation time using extrapolations from the computation time for
the current FEM model. AcTUP prints the time taken to solve each run definition
file, using which the time taken for the alternative method for the far field was also
calculated, assuming usage optimal parameters, details are given in section D.6. The
computation time was found to be 50hours for a big FEM model and 10hours for the
alternative model if they were run independently on the same computer system. The
memory size of the FEM solution is expected to be as big as 50 GB, also requiring a
high-end computer with a big enough RAM to handle the model during solving stage.
However, the output files from AcTUP are also expected to be big, perhaps double
the size requirements for FEM. However, with AcTUP, it is possible to control the
amount of data solved, thus solving in smaller chunks or overwriting certain data that
is not required.

Conclusion
The FEM model and the alternative model were set up for a pile-driving problem
with a hammer force. The near-field sound propagation model, WNI and its imple-
mentation in the Acoustics Toolbox package were ensured to have minimal errors by
studying an example case. Similarly, the FEM model was set up on COMSOL Multi-
physics software and ensured to also have minimal errors. Next, the ESM was set up,
parametric studies were conducted to understand the significance of the parameters
and optimization runs were done to find the recommended values of the parameters.
In response to section 1.4 Q1: The two models were compared and the alternative
model was found to be a very close match (<2dB) with the significant benefits of
lower computation time (less than 5 times reduction) and memory.
In response to section 1.4 Q2.1: The implications of the mesh weren’t specifically
studied in detail. However, the mesh proved to be accurate since the two results from
the two models were a good match.
In response to section 1.4 Q2.2: The implications of the boundary conditions
were not explored in regards to the bathymetry of the soil or accounting for the soil
elasticity in the models.
In response to section 1.4 Q2.3 & Q3: The distance of the reference plane used
for the ESM depends on the frequency of the sound field, the extent of the evanescent
spectrum and the ill-conditioning in the system. The lower the presence of evanescent
waves in the sampled field, the lower the errors in the regenerated field. But the higher
the distance between the sources and receivers, the higher the ill-conditioning in the
system.
In response to section 1.4 Q2.4 & Q3: The number of receivers is a very important
parameter that relates to the information of the field that needs to be input into the
ESM for accurate regeneration. In general, the higher the information the better,
however at lower frequencies or with lower complications in the sound field, more
information leads to ill-conditioning.
In response to section 1.4 Q2.5 & Q3: The number of sources required to accu-

In response to section 1.4 Q4:
• The initial number of sources in the line array can be assumed to correspond
to a spacing of λmin/2 along the submerged pile.
• Further, taking the maximum number of sources into account for the highest
frequency, the number of sources for the rest of the frequency span can be
decided based on the number of propagating modes in that frequency.
• It is very important to ensure that there are enough receivers to sample the field,

• Ill-conditioning and rank-deficiency issues at low frequencies, as encountered by

receivers for the ESM. However, the numerical errors still need to be monitored
and checked as changes are made in the ESM system. The scaling processes
suggested here can be explored further for mitigation in case it becomes a big
concern.

D.6
Computation time calculations
The computation time for the final model was calculated based on the time it took
for the models to solve individually on the computer used, which had 32 GB RAM.
• Calculations for FEM are made by extrapolating the simulation time based
relative to the number of degrees of freedom.
• WNI solved for 200m, resolution of 0.1m, based on 4sec per frequency per source.
Solving only for the optimal number of sources and one-third octave frequencies,
a single source at a time.
• NM solved for 1km, resolution of 1m, for one-third octave, multiple sources at
a time.
Note: For a lower range, FEM is much faster than the alternative method. How-
ever, the alternative method can be easily extended to higher ranges (>5km) by
compromising the range resolution (around 2.5m) at the same computation cost.
