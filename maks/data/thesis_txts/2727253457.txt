Abstract
This study attempts to create a better assumption for occupant window behavior. Cur┬Ł
rent deterministic assumptions are not adequate and contribute to the performance gap.
Therefore this study aims to create multiple stochastic models for describing occupant
window use. As the world attempts to transform into more energy┬Łefficient buildings, the
need for meaningful simulations becomes bigger. The stochastic models are based upon
ten different data sets═Š one measured during the study, one provided by Andersen, R. K.,
and eight provided by the ASHRAE OB database. Each model is created based on their
respective data set using R┬Łstudio and the package glmmTMB. The models will consist
of a Markov chain containing two states. Each state will consist of a Generalised Lin┬Ł
ear Mixed Model, which calculates the probability of an action occurring, and a random
number generator for a comparison with the calculated probability. The models are im┬Ł
plemented in the building simulation tool IDA ICE. The results from this are compared to
the data measured during the study and the results from a deterministic approach. The
study found that the proposed approach is capable of creating stochastic models, which
can meaningfully be used when performing energy simulations. These models tend to
be more conservative than the tested deterministic approach, thus helping to close the
performance gap. The models should ideally be created on large data sets measured on
multiple different dwellings situated in similar conditions to where the models are to be
applied.

Introduction
A vast amount of resources are spent on buildings and the maintenance of facilities. This
is understandable as 90% of peopleŌĆÖs time is spent indoors [1]. This means that potential
improvements in a buildingŌĆÖs energy efficiency can have significant monetary and environ┬Ł
mental impacts. Therefore, accurately judging the effect of any given solution is essential.
To do this, one must create a realistic simulation of a building and its use.
This study explores an alternative approach for simulating when humans open or close
windows. A better assumption for this is needed when performing energy calculations.
To explain why this is necessary, a typical assumption and the problems associated with
it are presented, followed by an alternative approach based on Generalized Linear Mixed
Models (GLMM) nested within a Markov chain.
How these models work, and the method for creating them is explained. The data which
is used for creating the models are presented. When the models have been created,
they are implemented in a typical simulation tool. The results are then compared with
measurements conducted throughout the study and a typical approach. Finally, the study
wraps up with a discussion of the results and a conclusion on whether or not the alternative
approach is better for simulating human window behavior than the typical deterministic
approach.

To improve the energy efficiency of the Danish building mass, the Danish government
has implemented a maximum on how much energy a building is allowed to use to main┬Ł
tain an indoor environment of a certain quality (see table 1.1). [2]. As it is necessary to
conduct these calculations uniformly across the whole construction sector, a somewhat
standardized approach has been developed [2].

1.2
Previous behavioral models
Previous studies, Nicol [5], Rijal [6], and Haldi and Robinson [7], have calculated the
probability of finding the window in a given state based on the contemporary indoor mea┬Ł
surements, in particular, the indoor temperature. While this approach is intuitive, it comes
with a problem. Namely that an open window heavily impacts the indoor parameters of
the room. This means that many data points marked as having an open window will have
their indoor parameters affected by the open window. To illustrate this problem, imagine
a model which uses the indoor temperature to predict whether or not a window is open.
Following the approach mentioned above when creating a model. Results in a model cre┬Ł
ated on a few data points with high indoor temperature and an open window. However,
most data points with an open window will have a relatively low indoor temperature. This
is because the temperature has been lowered by having the window open for a while.
Therefore, the model might determine that a low indoor temperature leads to open win┬Ł
dows. This is a problem when this model is used to simulate human window interaction,
as it might never open a window during the heat of summer, but as the temperature falls
during winter, the model might open the window and therefore reinforce the lowering of the
temperature and get stuck in a negative feedback loop. To avoid this problem, the model
should predict whether or not the action of opening or closing the window will happen in
the next time step. Not simply the probability of the window being open in the current time
step.
Models predicting whether a window will open or close can consist of a Markov chain.
This strategy has been implemented with success before by Haldi and Robinson [8], An┬Ł
dersen et al. [9], and Rijal [6]. A Markov chain can switch between different equations,
in this case, an equation that provides the probability of opening a closed window and an
equation for the probability of closing an open window.
Generalized Linear Models (GLM) have previously been used in Markov chains to provide
these probabilities by, among others, Haldi and Robinson, [8], Andersen et al. [9], and
Rijal [6]. These models provide a stochastic result, though they tend to have a relatively
narrow distribution range, as shown by Schweiker et al. [10]. A different approach is
the use of Generalizes Linear Mixed Models (GLMM) in the Markov chain. A GLMM can
represent the diversity of behaviors between different occupants, as stated by Haldi [11].
A similar approach is used by Haldi et al. [12] and to some extent by Fabi et al. [13] as
in this study, many models, one for each room, are created instead of one single model.
This is the essence of a GLMM, as it can be seen as a model containing multiple different
GLMs.
1.3
Aim of this study
Considering this, this study aims to create multiple stochastic models describing when
inhabitants open or close a window. This will be achieved stochastically through a Markov
chain containing two generalized linear mixed models, one calculating the probability of
opening the window and the other calculating the probability of closing the window. The
models will be implemented in a simulation tool. The results from this will be compared
to the results from a deterministic method. This is done to investigate how the stochastic
approach stacks up against a deterministic approach. Real┬Łworld data will be measured
with the aim of both creating a stochastic model and providing measurements on which
the behavior of interesting models can be verified.

2.2
Markov chain
A Markov chain can contain multiple different states. Haldi and Robinson [8] uses six
states depending on not only the state of the window but also whether a person has just
arrived, is about to leave, or is going to stay in the office. The present study will utilize a
Markov chain with two states which only depend on the state of the window. This approach

If the window is closed, the Markov chain will use the equation which gives the probability
of opening a closed window. This equation will use the indoor and outdoor parameters
and calculate a probability between 0 and 1 of opening the window. This probability is
then compared to a randomly generated number between 0 and 1. Suppose the equa┬Ł
tion calculates a probability of popen = 0.78 given the input parameters, and the random
number is rolled at n = 0.34. In that case, the window opens as 0.78 > 0.34. The Markov
chain now changes state to the open window and starts calculating the probability of clos┬Ł
ing an open window. This time the result might be a probability of closing the window of
pclose = 0.18, the random number is found to be n = 0.64, as 0.18 < 0.64, the window
remains open, and the Markov chain stays in the current state. How often the Markov
chain is running depends on the program and the size of the time steps used.
2.3
Generalized Linear Mixed Models
This still leaves the issue, what is the probability that the window opens or closes given
the current conditions? The present study aims to answer that question by approximating
the probability through binomial Generalized Linear Mixed Models (GLMM). The idea is
to find the probability of the window changing position P(Y = y) for the current values of
the input parameters (x1, x2, ..., xn). This can generally be expressed as a function:
py(x1, x2, ..., xn) = P(Y = y|X1 = x1, X2 = x2, ..., Xn = xn)
(2.1)
One approach for finding this probability is to fit models to real┬Łworld data sets. These
models will then try to predict the dependent value (Y ) based on one or multiple explana┬Ł
tory values (x1, x2, ..., xn). This is done by letting a computer try different values for the
intercept and the coefficients for each explanatory value in the models. The computer then
tests and optimizes these values and thus tries to create an optimal model. Different func┬Ł
tions exist for this operation, mainly differing in the optimizing process. This study uses
R┬ŁStudio [14] and a package called ŌĆØglmmTMBŌĆØ [15] to create the models. This specific
method was chosen because Faber C. M. showed in his thesis, ŌĆØMixed effect modeling of
occupantsŌĆÖ window opening behaviourŌĆØ [16], that this package was the fastest for creating
GLMM models in R on this kind of data sets.

2.3.1
Random intercept or slope
Two types of GLMMs exist, Random Intercept Models and Random Slope Models. A
GLMM containing only the red and the green line in figure 2.3 would be a random intercept,
as only the intercept value (a) changes between each sub┬Łmodel. In contrast, a model
which contains all five lines in figure 2.3 would be a random slope model, as both the
coefficients (bi) and the intercept (a) changes value between each sub┬Łmodel. Thus, a
random slope model is more capable than a random intercept model for describing very
different behaviors.
2.4
Selection criteria
Having the ability to create a model is only half the journey. It is equally important to
discern which out of all possible models is the optimal one. This necessitates a way
to judge the performance of each model and a procedure for minimizing the number of
models which need to be created. This study utilizes up to 5 different parameters: The
Temperature INDoor and OUTdoor (T_ind and T_out), the Relative Humidity INDoor and
OUTdoor (RH_ind and RH_out), and the CO2 concentration (CO2). Having just these five

different explanatory variables creates 31 possible combinations1, but adding the possi┬Ł
bility of mixed effect on each of the parameters makes this 242 possibilities2, which makes
checking every possible combination of explanatory variables impractical. Therefore, two
problems exist:
ŌĆó How to judge the effectiveness of different models?
ŌĆó Which models should be created for evaluation?
As the models potentially involve more than one explanatory value, the usual approach
of R2 becomes less useful, as it would always prefer the most complicated model, e.g.,
it is always possible to draw a line between two points and a plane between three points
and so forth. Thus, having more dimensions in the model will produce a better R2 value.
Therefore this study relies on Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) [18]. AIC allows for a
meaningful selection method for models with multiple dimensions. This method balances
the goodness of fit of a given model with its simplicity. AIC is calculated as:
AIC = 2k ŌłÆ2ln(╦åL)
(2.5)
Where k is the number of explanatory parameters and ╦åL is the goodness of fit calculated
by the Likelihood function. This means AIC creates a number for each model tested, and
the model with the smallest number is chosen as the best model. These numbers are
unique to a given data set. Thus, comparing models created on different data sets with
this approach is impossible.
A step┬Łwise regression was implemented to minimize the number of models created, thus
lowering the computing time. A step┬Łwise regression could either be a Forward selection
or a Backward elimination. Forward selection starts with the simplest possible models
and moves toward more complicated ones. Backward elimination starts with the most
complicated model and then simplifies the models as it goes along. As complex models
take more computing time, Forward selection was selected.
Forward selection is exemplified in table 2.2. In step 1, the model with T_ind is selected
as it has the lowest AIC score. In step 2, new models are created by adding a second
new parameter. In step 2, the model using T_ind and RH_ind has the lowest AIC. The
best model in step 2 is then compared with the best model from step 1, and as step 2 has
the lowest score, the procedure moves on to step 3. This time the best model in step 3
has a higher AIC than the best model in step 2, there for the procedure does not move on
to step 4 but chooses the best model from step 2 as the final most optimal model. This

2.5
Statistical assumptions
When creating predictive models from data, a few assumptions about the data generally
must be met for the models to be reliable [17]. These are shortly presented here:
ŌĆó Linearity
ŌĆó Absence of collinearity
ŌĆó Homoskedasticity
ŌĆó Normality of residuals
ŌĆó Absence of influential data points
ŌĆó Independence
It must be stressed that these assumption tests are commonly used for deterministic mod┬Ł
els and not for stochastic models. Thus, the results of these tests are not expected to be
well┬Łbehaved. More information on statistical assumptions can be found in Winter [17].
Linearity
This assumption states that the residuals between the data points and the model should be
distributed in a linear pattern, e.g., not a curved or non┬Łlinear pattern. The data is binary,
as each window is either open ŌĆØ1ŌĆØ or closed ŌĆØ0ŌĆØ. An effect of the stochastic approaches
is that the model calculates a probability between 0 and 1. Thus if the model calculates a
probability of opening the window of 0.05, there is a 95% probability of the model saying
the value is closed ŌĆØ0ŌĆØ, therefore a residual of 0ŌłÆ0.05 = ŌłÆ0.05, but also a 5% probability of
a value of 1 ŌĆØopenŌĆØ and a resulting residual of 1 ŌłÆ0.05 = 0.95. This creates two lines, not
one. There will likely be a slight slope to one side. This is the result of the model predicting
an increase or decrease in the probability of an open window as the explanatory value
changes. Thus, the residual of a closed window might become more negative, while the
residual of an open window becomes less positive.
Absence of collinearity
This is an assumption on whether any of the explanatory variables influence or explain
Stochastic simulation of occupant window behavior
9
==PAGE:17==
each other. This will be tracked through the Pearson correlations coefficient, which mea┬Ł
sures the correlation between two parameters, ranging between +1 and ┬Ł1. A 0 means no
correlation between the two parameters, and +1 means a complete positive correlation,
meaning that an increase in one parameter equals a proportionate increase in the other.
An example could be one parameter measuring peopleŌĆÖs height in meters and another
parameter measuring their height in centimeters. A doubling in one will see a doubling in
the other, thus a Pearson correlation of +1. A Pearson correlation of ┬Ł1 is the complete
reverse, where an increase in one leads to a proportionate decrease in the other.
Homoskedasticity
This is the assumption that every residual is equally distributed. As with the Linearity
assumption, the binary nature of the input data and the fact that the models provide a
probability create two separate clusters which are expected to slope to one side.
Normality of residuals
This assumption is that the residuals are normally distributed. Bodo Winter [17] states this
is the least important assumption. It can be assessed from a QQ┬Łplot of the residuals.
Absence of influential data points
This assumption stipulates that no single data point has an unreasonably large effect on
the model. This can be tested by creating many models, all of them containing all but
one data point. A comparison of these models then shows the effect of each removed
data point. This approach would work for the data sets used in this study, but as some
data sets have millions of data points, this approach becomes impractical, as the comput┬Ł
ing time would be enormous. The approach used in this study to verify this assumption
consists of three parts: The first part is the size of the data sets, as they contain many
thousands or even millions of data points. One data point, whether weirdly large or small,
will thus vanish in the sheer size of the data sets. The second part is simply looking at the
descriptive statistics and looking at the mean, 1st┬Łquartile, 3rd┬Łquartile, minimum value,
and maximum value of each explanatory variable. The final part is to look at the impact
of each coefficient on its respective parameter and compare this to the distribution of the
values. The impacts are calculated through the following:
I = (MAX(xn) ŌłÆMIN(xn)) ┬Ę bxn
(2.6)
I is the impact, (MAX(xn) ŌłÆMIN(xn)) gives the range a given parameter attains in the
data set, and bxn is the coefficient used for parameter xn. The larger the impact, the more
important the parameter is for the modelŌĆÖs outcome. When calculating the impact, the
range must reflect the actual range the model receives. Thus it is necessary to replicate
any transformations applied to the parameter.
Independence
This assumption, while critical, applies in a particular manner when using GLMM. The
assumption states that each data point is independent of each other. If one is to model
childrenŌĆÖs height as a function of their age, measuring the same person ten times is not
a reasonable approach as it breaks the assumption of independence. When applying
this assumption to the data sets, questions start arising. Firstly, an issue known as auto┬Ł
correlation, meaning that the explanatory values are not independent of each within the
same parameter, e.g., T_ind at a given time step is heavily dependent on T_ind at the
previous time step, this problem has not been addressed in this study, but the models
solely uses these parameters as explanatory variables. A second objection is that each
data set contains multiple measurements from each room. This is comparable to the

sub┬Łgroupings of data points are connected. This way, the model knows that data are not

every 5 minutes.

allowing instant access to the data without the need to visit the places physically. Ensuring
that making backups and checking if everything runs smoothly is incredibly easy. The

a level of redundancy that allowed for data to be collected even if one sensor temporarily
goes offline. As the ŌĆØWireless tagŌĆØ was physically placed on the door frame, it measured

Transformation of data sets
Data that are normally distributed provide more stable models. The data will, therefore, in
this study be transformed to better fit a normal distribution. Transformations are applied
to the data if judged necessary by a visual judgment. Data transformation is done as
follows: If the data is skewed to the left, it can be transformed by taking the square root
or log10 of every data point. If the data is skewed to the right, then every data point is
subtracted from a constant before taking the square root or log10 of every point. This
constant must be larger than the largest data point as the square root or log10 produces
imaginary numbers when confronted with negative numbers.

4.1.3
Stability in numbers
Running the simulation many times is an integral part of the process when simulating
stochastic models. Running the simulation just once provides very little information. It
essentially only shows one possible outcome but with no information on the expected
range of results. For example, imagine having a program that simulates a dice throw.
The outcome might become a ŌĆØ2ŌĆØ when running this once. Claiming that your program
has proven that you have a 100% probability of getting a value lower than a ŌĆØ3ŌĆØ when
throwing a die is obviously wrong. A better solution would be to run the dice┬Łthrowing pro┬Ł
gram 100 times and then look at the distribution of outcomes. This should roughly provide
you with a probability of 1
3 for attaining something lower than a ŌĆØ3ŌĆØ. This approximation
should become more precise each time you run your program. Plotting the distribution

Stability of models
Looking at the evolution of the coefficients of variations from each model (Appendix A.6).
It becomes apparent that models consisting of only a few or a single sub┬Łmodel reaches
stability much faster than more complex models. Models 5, 8, 21, 23, 24, 25, and M attain

are therefore deemed trustworthy.
Besides showing the relatively large difference between each sub┬Łmodel, Figure 5.6 un┬Ł
derscores something very important, if only a few simulations are performed using a
GLMM model, the results might be heavily skewed if the model randomly chooses three
or four quite similar sub┬Łmodels, e.g., 2, 5, or 13. the results will be very uniform. Thus,
the coefficient of variation will look stable, and the ┬Ą and Žā might be accepted as stable.
But adding the result from a new sub┬Łmodel might change these values drastically. Thus
the more sub┬Łmodels a given model contains, the more simulations should be performed
before judging the coefficient of variation.

6
Discussion
In this section, it is discussed whether the proposed approach creates meaningful models
of how occupants interact with their windows and if it makes sense to apply the described
procedures in the real world. Finally, the quality of the 10 models will be evaluated, and
potential sources of errors from the development and implementation of the models are
dissected.
6.1
Meaningful stochastic models
It was shown in section 3.2 that it is possible to create GLMM models from data sets com┬Ł
prising continuously measured data. Section 5 showed that it was possible to implement
stochastic GLMM models through a Markov chain in a commercially available simulation
tool, as well as analyze the outcomes produced by the simulations. That this was possible
does, however, not mean that it is a useful approach when considering different possible
designs. For the approach to be meaningful, it should both be able to create models which
describe actual human behavior and be practical to implement.
6.1.1
Human like behaviour
This study showed that it was possible to create stochastic models which had a reasonable
probability of providing results that were within the range of observed behavior, as shown
in section 5.5. While it was possible, it was by no means guaranteed that all produced
models would deliver realistic results.
While model A had a reasonable Ōēł20% probability of predicting a result within the range
of the measured values (table 5.5), model 8 only had a decent probability for providing
results within the range in a single month (table 5.6). The study provided a possible
reason for this in section 5.4, where it was shown that the mentioned models had a very
different use pattern. This could be due to the difference in the data sets each model was
created on. Thus, whether a model became realistic or not likely depended on the data
the model was created on, as well as the data and assumptions used when performing
the simulation.
This study used weather files and assumptions representing Denmark. Model A likely
performed better as it was created on data collected in Denmark, while model 8, which
was created on data that differed drastically from data set A (table 5.2), had a worse
performance. Thus, stochastic models created as described in section 2 likely need data
obtained under conditions similar to the situation in which the model will be applied.
Not only did the data need to be relevant for the desired simulation, the data set on which
the models were created needed to have a certain quality and size. An example of this is
how model A was better than model M at describing the measured behavior. As model
M never used the windows, it had a 0% probability of attaining a measured result. Both
models were created on data measured in Denmark. But data set A contained measure┬Ł
ments from every part of the year and contained 256130 data points while only having
3743 missing values. Data set M, on the other hand, only had 78312 data points with
a total of 36491 missing values. Additionally, data were only collected from February to
June. The effect of this is visible in the fact that model A, in contrast to model M, is capa┬Ł
ble of producing realistic results. This means that the stochastic approach presented in
this study is capable of creating stochastic models which provide realistic results. But it
requires large relevant data sets of high quality.

6.1.2
Feasibility of stochastic models
As described in section 4.1.3, simulating the same situation multiple times is essential
when working with stochastic models. This can serve as a bottleneck if computing time is
a limiting factor. While this has been a problem in the past, faster computation and more
computing time are available now. This makes it possible to easily perform the many
simulations required. A second limiting factor is a need for large high┬Łquality data sets on
which stochastic models can be created. As measurement equipment becomes cheaper
and more readily available, this issue becomes less of a hurdle. It is presumably a hurdle
that only needs to be overcome a few times, as models created on high┬Łquality data sets
could, in principle, be publicized for other actors to use. This would greatly lessen the
barrier of entry for the adoption of the stochastic approach. The ASHRAE OB database
[21] is a recent and very good start on this process, as it was the source of data for 8 out
of 10 models created in this study.
The implementation of the models in IDA ICE, which were described in section 4.1, was
rather involved and complex, making this process difficult and time┬Łconsuming. This could
presumably be diminished greatly if dedicated programs were developed. Ideally, these
should contain premade stochastic models while still allowing for user┬Łcreated models.
The programs should allow for multiple runs of the same simulation without the need for
a human to initiate each one. The resulting average and standard deviation for a range
of relevant parameters should be presented after the simulations were performed. These
programs do not currently exist, and the data sets necessary still need to be collected.
But the biggest limiting factor for the proposed approach is the fact that the regulatory
requirements stipulate a deterministic result [2].
If a stochastic approach was accepted, potentially as a requirement that a proposed de┬Ł
sign would have a 95% probability of having an energy usage lower than a given limit.
There would still persist a problem, as the stochastic models seem to be less optimistic
than the deterministic approach, as seen in table 5.8. This leaves consultants with a
negative economic incentive to utilize this approach, as a given design would perform
worse if calculated stochastically. The legal requirements need to not only be changed
to allow a stochastic result but crucially have goals that would not place this approach
at a disadvantage. This would not be a decrease in the energy performing standards as
the change would hopefully help lessen the performance gap. Thus improving the future
building mass [4], and be a signal that a proposed design or improvement would not be
guaranteed to lessen the environmental or economic impact but simply have a probability
of doing so.
6.1.3
The effect of assumptions made
Figure 5.1, figure 5.3, and table 5.1 showed that the stochastic approach was capable of
producing a wide range of results, but the figures and table highlighted something else
as well. When performing stochastic simulations, the design choices and deterministic
assumptions determine the range of the stochastic results. In figure 5.3, model M had
0 window openings, while model 25 had the most openings by far. This is reflected in
their respective energy usages. Model M predicted an energy usage of 82.16kWh
m2 while
model 25 predicted 145.5kWh
m2 . Opening the window while the outdoor temperature is
lower than the heating set┬Łpoint will increase the required energy consumption. An upper
bound is thus a model which opens the window in every situation where this is the case.
If a model never opens the window, heat is still lost through the building envelope. The
energy required to counteract this will act as the lower bound.
Besides providing an upper and lower bound, the design choices also determine the con┬Ł

sequences of a modelŌĆÖs actions. An example of this is that even though model 10 and

the other hand, only had, on average, 275.2 hours, where it would have used more energy
if it could. This means that had a larger ideal heater been assumed, model 23 might have
calculated a larger energy requirement than model 10.

6.3
Sources of error
The potential sources of error, which have occurred throughout this study, include:
Parameters not measured: It is quite possible that the reasons behind occupant window
usage depend partly or fully on parameters that were not modeled in this study. Thus
leading to erroneous predictions. These parameters might be FangerŌĆÖs comfort index,
outdoor sound level, wind speed, or something else entirely.
Sudo┬ŁRandom number: The random numbers used for the simulation are only sudo┬Ł
random. Figure 6.2 shows the cumulative distribution of random numbers used in a typical
simulation. Ideally, the cumulative distribution should attain a straight line, but it is evident
that the list has too many small numbers and too few large ones. This means that the
simulations are a bit too pessimistic. This is because a model predicts an action will occur
if the random number is smaller than the predicted probability. This means that more small
numbers will, all other things being equal, lead to more actions occurring.

Use of models outside its range: As described previously, the models should be used in
situations that are similar to the data they were created on. In the same fashion, the mod┬Ł
els ideally should not be applied outside the ranges of their data set. If a data set contains
values for the CO2 concentration between 400 ppm and 2500 ppm, then the respective
model should not be applied outside this range. Implementing that a model should not be
used outside its range would, in practice, require assumptions for the occupantŌĆÖs behavior
when out of bounds. Larger data sets would hopefully contain more extreme measure┬Ł
ments, thus increasing the range of the models and lowering the number of calculations
performed when out of bounds.
Simple Markov chain: The Markov chain, which has been implemented, was a simple
one. It assumes that occupants have a constant behavioral pattern independent of time
of day, year, or length of stay. As mentioned in section 2.2, more advanced Markov chains
have been utilized by others. They assumed that occupants change their behavior if they
are about to leave or just arrived [8].
6.3.1
Measurement errors
Garden doors: Garden doors, not windows, were measured for data set M. While a
garden door can, of course, be used like a window to let outside air into the room, they
serve other functions as well, namely to grant access to the garden. This will likely cause
the measured results to differ from the ones predicted by the models, as they were created
on data stemming from actual windows.
Only a single room measured: As only windows in the living room were measured, it is
plausible that occupants used windows in other rooms and then let internal and thus not
tracked doors remain open to mix the indoor air.
Sample size Data set M were only measured on a small and relatively uniform subset of
the general population and might not be representable in all cases.
Mechanical ventilation The data from Fensmark were measured on a house that had
mechanical ventilation. This means that the indoor environment was always very good.
Thus the garden door was presumably never used for ventilation purposes, as it was not
needed. No sub┬Łmodel in model M relies on this data, but in the comparison with the data,
this is important to keep in mind.

7
Conclusion
This study succeeded in creating multiple stochastic models, at least one of which was
shown to be reasonably capable of describing when occupants open or close a window.
This was achieved, by implementing a Markov chain containing two generalized linear
mixed models in IDA ICE, one calculating the probability of opening the window and the
other calculating the probability of closing the window. The models were compared to re┬Ł
sults created by a built┬Łin deterministic assumption for window opening and closing. Most
stochastic models were more conservative than the deterministic model when predicting
the required energy consumption. Two models were compared to data measured from
February to June in 2023. One of the models was capable of recreating the behavior
measured in the real world.
Throughout this study, it was seen that for stochastic models to be a useful tool when
performing energy calculations, they must be created on large and relevant data sets
of high quality. The creation and implementation of such models are complex and time┬Ł
consuming, but a stochastic approach to energy calculations may be necessary to explain
the performance gap.
