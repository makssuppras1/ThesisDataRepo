Abstract
Seagrass meadows are important ecosystems providing many crucial services, such as car­
bon and nutrient cycling. Seagrass meadows have experienced global losses during the 20th
century, most recently due to eutrophication from nutrient over­enrichment. Almost all coastal
waters have seen the typical signs of eutrophication, such as coastal hypoxia, phytoplankton
blooms and reduced water clarity. This has led to several European directives, such as the
Water Framework Directive (WFD), and national action plans. In the 21st century, despite suc­
cessful reductions of nutrients in the Danish estuary of Limfjorden, the signs of recovery of
the seagrass Zostera Marina (eelgrass) have been minimal. The knowledge of the ecosystem
response to cumulative pressures and the continuous climate change is still limited. Climate
change is believed to prolong or even negate the recovery of eelgrass. Increasing water tem­
peratures have shown to increase the respiration of eelgrass, causing its light requirements to
increase as well, and extreme temperatures is thought to directly increase mortality. In general,
the lack of knowledge about the impact of cumulative pressures in a time of changing climate
is limiting our understanding and proper management of coastal ecosystems. In this study, a
coupled hydrodynamic and ecological deterministic model of Limfjorden is used to explore the
effects of increasing temperatures and changes to wind speeds on eelgrass recovery. More­
over, the effect of further nutrient reductions of 30% in nitrogen (N), phosphorous (P) and carbon
(C) is also examined.
The results indicate that the effects of increased water temperatures coupled with other present
pressures is hindering the recovery of eelgrass. The combined effect of increased water tem­
peratures and changes to wind speeds resulted in a loss of 5.4% of eelgrass coverage when
compared to the 2010 baseline scenario. The results show that a nutrient reduction in N, P and
C in today’s climate could increase the coverage of eelgrass with 1.2 to 1.3% depending on the
nutrient reduction scenario. Although this increase was negated when combined with the effect
of climate change, where the findings showed a reduction in eelgrass coverage of 5.2% when
compared to the 2010 baseline scenario. The increase in water temperatures forced the eel­
grass to grow at shallower depths, exposing it to temperatures above its optimum temperature.
The exposure to high temperatures caused reduced growth and the loss of coverage were most
profound in less developed eelgrass meadows. This study showcases that future management
needs consider cumulative pressures and especially the effects of climate change, in order to
achieve successful recovery of eelgrass.
Keywords: Eelgrass (Zostera Marina), cumulative stressors, climate change, ecological mod­
elling, nutrient reduction, temperature response.

1
Introduction
Seagrass meadows are among some of the most productive and diverse coastal ecosystems.
They provide many important ecosystem services, especially for their role in carbon and nutrient
cycling in coastal ecosystems (Orth et al. 2006). In Denmark, Zostera Marina (Z. Marina), which
is commonly known as eelgrass, is the most prevalent seagrass (Borum et al. 2004). Eelgrass
meadows have a high primary production, providing oxygen to benthic communities (Hemminga
and C. M. Duarte 2000). The meadows also restrict water flow and wave energy, causing them
to trap suspended organic particles and decrease the sediment re­suspension, leading to an
overall improvement in water clarity and stabilization of sediments (Hemminga and C. M. Duarte
2000). The entrapment of suspended organic particles leads to accumulation of organic matter,
making it an important carbon sink (C. M. Duarte, Middelburg, and Caraco 2005; Moksnes et al.
2021). The meadows also function as foraging areas or as nursery grounds for juvenile stages
of different fish species depending on the region and local climate (Orth et al. 2006; Hemminga
and C. M. Duarte 2000).
Unfortunately, the global seagrass coverage has declined in past decades, as seagrasses are
impacted by several natural stressors and human activities (Orth et al. 2006; Hauxwell, Ce­
brian, and Valiela 2003). One of the main recent stressors on seagrasses has been the in­
creased loading of nutrients from anthropogenic activities in the adjacent watersheds (Hauxwell,
Cebrian, and Valiela 2003; Valdemarsen, Canal­Vergés, et al. 2010). The increased concen­
trations of nutrients, such as nitrogen (N) and phosphorous (P), stems from nearby urban or
agricultural land development. The increase in nutrients stimulates the growth of the otherwise
nutrient­limited opportunistic phytoplankton, ephiphytic micro­ or macroalgae and unattached
macroalgae, thereby increasing the amounts of autochthonous organic matter in the body of
water. Due to the increased microbial decomposition of the organic matter, eutrophication with
reduced water clarity and coastal hypoxia occurs (Hauxwell, Cebrian, and Valiela 2003; Pinck­
ney et al. 2001). The reduced water clarity is especially proven to have a severe impact on
seagrasses, as it increases the light attenuation and therefore limits the growth of seagrasses
(Hauxwell, Cebrian, Furlong, et al. 2001; Hauxwell, Cebrian, and Valiela 2003; Valdemarsen,
Canal­Vergés, et al. 2010). In recent years, there have been increasing focus on reducing the
anthropogenic nutrient loading, leading to the European Water Framework Directive (WFD) and
its requirements for River Basin Management Plans (RBMP). The WFD stipulates that the mem­
ber states should aim to achieve Good Ecological Status (GES) in surface waters (WFD 2000).
With the WFD, the depth limit of eelgrass was introduced as a bio­indicator of the ecological
state of coastal waters. The depth limit is defined as the deepest water depth where eelgrass
grows, which is generally considered to be a good bio­indicator as it correlates with water clarity
(Krause­Jensen, T. Greve, and Nielsen 2005).
Following the management actions, the nutrient concentrations in Danish estuaries and coastal
waters have generally been decreasing since the 1990s (Hansen and Høgslund 2021). De­
spite the nutrient reductions, the eelgrass meadows have not yet recovered as expected and
studies show that eelgrass recovery depends on the cumulative pressures from a range of
stressors (Canal­Vergés et al. 2014; Valdemarsen, Canal­Vergés, et al. 2010; Valdemarsen,
Wendelboe, et al. 2011; Kuusemäe et al. 2016). The idea that eutrophication can be reversed
by managing nutrient inputs alone have already been challenged, and studies from Limfjorden
shows that managing nutrient input over a short time scale does not result in recovered eel­
grass (C. M. Duarte, Conley, et al. 2009; Carstensen et al. 2013). It is argued that as of today,
the challenges for eelgrass recovery have shifted, and that climate change has emerged as an

additional potential stressor (Krause­Jensen, C. Duarte, et al. 2020).
Yet the effect of climate change on the recovery and coverage of eelgrass is still relatively un­
known. Studies show an optimum temperature for eelgrass is approximately 20°C and has a
threshold temperature of approximately 25°C (Nejrup and Pedersen 2008). Warming above
this optimum and threshold temperature causes reduced growth or increased mortality and in­
creased light demands (Hammer et al. 2018; Nejrup and Pedersen 2008; Marba and C. M.
Duarte 2010; Staehr and Borum 2011). However, it is also argued that increasing tempera­
tures might stimulate growth in waters with temperatures below the threshold (Beca­Carretero
et al. 2018). The aim of this study was to investigate the effects of possible future scenarios
on eelgrass in Limfjorden, Denmark. The future scenarios consists of a combination between
further external nutrient reductions, and predicted changes in air temperature and wind speeds
according to the IPCC’s RCP8.5 scenario (IPCC 2014; Thejll et al. 2020).

Methods and model description
2.1
Study site
The study site comprises of the largest Danish estuary Limfjorden, which is located in northern
Jutland. In general, Limfjorden is a shallow estuary bordering the North Sea in the west at Thy­
borøn and Kattegat Sea in the east at Hals, see fig. 2.1. The Limfjorden estuary has a surface
area of approximately 1,500 km2 and a runoff area of around 7,600 km2 with a mean depth of
4.6 meters (Naturstyrelsen 2011). The sediment in the area of Limfjorden is characterized by
glacial till from the Weichselian glaciation and consists mainly of sandy and loam soil types. The
waterbodies within Limfjorden consists partially of large and calm wide areas, shallow bay areas
and narrow but deep sounds with rapid currents. The sounds typically have depths of 18 to 22
meters, with Oddesund being the deepest part at 28 meters. The large and calm wide areas
are typically 5 to 8 meters deep, with Thisted Bredning being an exception with a depth of 11
meters (Naturstyrelsen 2011). Due to the western winds and tides in the North Sea, Limfjorden
primarily receives saline ocean water (32 to 34‰) from its western boundary at Thyborøn, and
the eastward flow through Limfjorden has a magnitude of 6.8 km3 per year. The salinity of the
water at Kattegat is slightly less saline at 19 to 25‰(Naturstyrelsen 2011). According to the
national RBMPs, Limfjorden have been divided into different waterbodies based on the hydro­
morphological, chemical or biological properties (A. Erichsen et al. 2019). Waterbody number
158, Hjarbæk Fjord, is uniquely a gated bay which closes at high water levels to protect from
flooding. In comparison, waterbody 157 is characterized by high salinity and is stratified more
than half of the year (Naturstyrelsen 2011). The remaining waterbodies are all characterized by
high salinity, but the seasonal variations in stratification varies between them (A. Erichsen et al.
2019). The location of the different waterbodies utilized in the most recent RBMP is shown in
fig. 2.1.

The land use in Limfjorden’s watershed consists of approximately 70% agricultural land, 5%
urban land use and 9% forests, with the remaining 16% being natural areas such as meadows
and bogs. The anthropogenic nutrient loading from the agricultural land has been historically
high for Limfjorden, which has resulted in annual anoxic conditions at the bottom in some parts
of Limfjorden (Naturstyrelsen 2011). Limfjorden is a very large and dynamic system, and as a
result, the anoxic bottom conditions appear and disappear quickly. Anoxic conditions generally
occur around the month of June and can last until September (Naturstyrelsen 2011). The an­
nual anoxic events have a profound impact on the local ecosystems, and the events have been
coupled with a historic decline in landings of demersal fish species in Limfjorden (Riisgard, An­
dersen, and Hoffmann 2012). Although annual anoxic events still appear, the nutrient loading
to Limfjorden has been successfully reduced, with linear regression analysis showing negative
trends from 1985 to 2008 for both total nitrogen (TN), total phosphorous (TP), dissolved inor­
ganic nitrogen (DIN) and dissolved inorganic phosphorous (DIP) (Krause­Jensen, Markager,
and Dalsgaard 2012).
The eelgrass depth limit that is used as a bio­indicator for GES is in reference to historical
data of the main coverage from before year 1930 (Timmermann, Christensen, and A. Erichsen
2020). The reference depth limit of eelgrass and today’s depth limit to achieve GES is shown
in table 2.1. Eelgrass has been continuously monitored since 1989, and this data shows that
the depth limit has seen a significant reduction of 17%, with no signs of improvement despite
reductions in nutrient loading in recent years. In 2019, the reported depth limit of eelgrass was
reported to be 1.8 to 2.6 meters in Limfjorden (Hansen and Høgslund 2021).

2.2
Model setup
The present study utilizes a dynamic mechanistic and deterministic model setup using model
software from DHI. The MIKE 3 HD Flexible Mesh (FM) was applied for hydrodynamic mod­
elling, the MIKE 21 Spectral Waves was applied to model wind­ and current­induced waves,
and the numerical MIKE solver ECOLAB was used for biogeochemical modelling. First, the
hydrodynamic model and the wave model were run for a single year, followed by running the
biogeochemical model as a decoupled model, which was forced by the results from the hydro­
dynamic and wave model.
The models utilized in this study were initially developed to support the implementation of the
4

European WFD in Danish coastal waters, and therefore also the implementation of the Danish
RBMPs. The mechanistic models were used together with statistical models to provide tools
and methods for calculating Maximum Allowable Input (MAI) of nutrients in order to maintain or
achieve GES as required by the WFD (A. C. Erichsen et al. 2017).
The hydrodynamic MIKE 3 HD Flexible Mesh model was set up to represent the hydrodynamic
conditions of Limfjorden. The hydrodynamic model solves the three­dimensional Reynolds­
averaged Navier­Stokes equations which describes the flow of incompressible fluids. The hy­
drodynamic model thus consists of continuity, momentum, temperature, salinity and density
equations and is closed by a turbulent closure scheme (DHI 2021b). The MIKE 3 FM utilizes
an unstructured flexible mesh and a finite volume solution technique that allows variation of the
horizontal resolution of the model grid mesh within the model area to obtain a finer resolution
of selected sub­areas, see appendix chapter L (DHI 2021b; A. C. Erichsen et al. 2017).
The MIKE 21 Spectral Waves models the wind­generated swells and waves (DHI 2021a). The
significant wave height, wave period and mean wave direction is outputted and used in the
ECOLAB solver to calculate a combined wave­ and current­induced bed shear stress using a
root­mean­square method (Soulsby and Clarke 2005; Kuusemäe et al. 2016).
The numerical solver, MIKE ECOLAB, is used for the ecological modelling of aquatic ecosys­
tems. The model is coupled to the advection­dispersion modules of the MIKE 3 HD FM flow
model, so that transport mechanisms based on advection­dispersion is integrated in the MIKE
ECOLAB simulation. The description of the ecosystem state variables is formulated as a set
of ordinary coupled differential equations describing the rate of change for each state variable
(DHI 2021c). The biogeochemical model describes the relationship and interactions between
nutrients and primary producers in both the pelagic and benthic compartments, see fig. 2.2.
In the pelagic compartment, the ecosystem is represented by one zooplankton group and one
phytoplankton group. Their growth rates and light dependencies changes between spring and
summer/autumn to represent the shifts in the ecosystem being dominated by green algae or
diatoms (A. C. Erichsen et al. 2017). In the benthic compartment, the model describes four
different primary producers: perennial brown macroalgae, annual opportunistic macroalgae,
benthic microalgae and rooted vegetation which represents eelgrass, see fig. 2.2. Together,
this provides a system consisting of 98 state variables, 308 constants, 29 forcing functions, 275
auxiliary processes and 475 processes. A graphical overview of the main state variables and
processes can be seen in fig. 2.2.
Lastly, the model results were further analyzed using a Geographical Information System tool
(QGIS) for spatial distribution and depth calculations. The model results were extracted as a
GIS shapefile containing the models monthly averaged results for every grid cell in the model
mesh as seen in appendix chapter L. In GIS, the area of each polygon was calculated based
on the projection used in the model, and the spatial distribution was calculated using this area
coverage. Similarly, the bathymetry used in the model was extracted as a GIS shapefile and
joined with the results shapefile, and statistics on eelgrass depth was calculated on these joined
shapefiles. All statistics on spatial distribution and depth of eelgrass were calculated on grid
cells with eelgrass densities greater than 5 g C m­2. This limit was considered efficient to leave
out polygons with very small densities of eelgrass from the statistics. Kuusemäe et al. (2016)
similarly uses 5 g C m­2 as a limit in their baseline scenario.

The loss of eelgrass in the present paper is calculated the same way for both number of shoots,
NNEC, and for the shoot biomass, EC, and it consists of seven different loss processes, see
eqs. (2.9) and (2.10).

The seven different loss processes represents biological processes that are to some degree
uncertain and difficult to quantify into mathematical equations. The processes and their de­
velopment are further explained in Kuusemäe et al. (2016), where they examined the effect
of the different stressors on eelgrass recovery in two other Danish estuaries, utilizing simi­
lar model setups as used in the present paper. For example, ploss2 represents the loss of
seedlings due to burial by lugworms, which is reworking sandy sediments and thereby burying
eelgrass seedlings. The reworking of sediments by lugworms and its pressure on eelgrass is
described further in Kuusemäe et al. (2016), Valdemarsen, Canal­Vergés, et al. (2010), and
Valdemarsen, Wendelboe, et al. (2011). Ploss4 represents the loss of seedlings to drifting
macroalgae, which is attached to stones and is calculated as seen in appendix section F.4.
When the current­ and wave­induced bed shear stress, τ, is high enough, the macroalgae that
is attached to stones start to drift, causing increased turbidity and physical stress on eelgrass
seedlings (Canal­Vergés et al. 2014; Valdemarsen, Canal­Vergés, et al. 2010; Kuusemäe et al.
2016). The loss process is dominant in eelgrass beds with biomass below 0.01 g C shoot­1,
which leaves the well­established eelgrass fields untouched by this loss process (Kuusemäe
et al. 2016).
Apart from the seven loss processes, the model in this paper also includes a dynamic sediment
stability. The critical shear stress value, in m s­1, is corrected for the sediments organic matter
content (Loss of Ignition, LOI), for the production of extracellular polymeric substances (EPS)
by microbenthos and for the presence of opportunistic macroalgae (Kuusemäe et al. 2016).
The dynamic sediment stability does not have a direct loss effect on eelgrass in the model, but
low stability has the ability to limit eelgrass growth through increasing the turbidity, which again
limits the available light for growth.

Model scenarios
The present paper presents a total of 16 different scenarios, divided into four main categories
specifying the different climates, see table 2.4. The main categories consists of: 1) a category
based on 2010 climate, 2) a category with increased temperatures based on the IPCC RCP8.5
scenario, 3) a category with increased wind speeds based on the IPCC RCP8.5 scenario and
4) a combined increase in temperature and wind speeds based on the IPCC RCP8.5 scenario.
Each of the four main categories consists of four model simulations based on the different nu­
trient loadings: 1) the original nutrient loading from 2010, 2) a scenario of reducing N, P and
C by 30%, 3) a scenario of reducing only N and C by 30% and 4) a scenario of reducing P by
30%. The scenario with 2010 climate and the original 2010 loadings is therefore referred to as
the baseline scenario in this paper.
When running the model scenarios, the hydrodynamic module was run first followed by the
spectral wave module since it uses the calculated flow fields from the hydrodynamic module.
Lastly, the ECOLAB modules were run as a decoupled module using the results from the hy­
drodynamic and spectral wave modules as forcings, see table 2.4. The scenarios based on the
nutrient loadings from 2010 (Baseline, T_2010, W_2010 and TW_2010) were simulated for nine
consecutive years to ensure steady state were reached. For the nutrient reduction scenarios,
the combined N, P and C reduction scenarios (2010_NPC, T_NPC, W_NPC and TW_NPC)
were also simulated for nine consecutive years to ensure steady state. The two other nutrient
reduction scenarios (..._NC and ..._P) were hot­started with initial values from the last time­step
from the 8th simulation of the combined reduction scenario (..._NPC). This was chosen as the
most correct method in order to ensure the sediment pools were at a reduced state, while also
saving simulation time by not having to simulate both the ”..._NC” and ”..._P” scenarios nine
times each.

2.4.2
Nutrient abatement scenarios
The present paper utilizes four different nutrient loading scenarios, one scenario consisting
of the original loading from 2010 (Baseline and ”..._2010”) and three scenarios consisting of
reducing the 2010 loading by 30%. One reduction­scenario examines the effects of a 30%
reduction in the external loading of both N and C (”..._NC”) from the 41 streams that supplies
Limfjorden with freshwater. Both N and C is reduced by 30% in order to avoid altering the
local C:N ratio. Another scenario is examining the effects of a 30% reduction in the external
loading of P (”..._P”). The third scenario is a combination of the first two scenarios, where both
the external loading of N, C and P is reduced by 30% (”..._NPC”). The reduction of 30% was
chosen across the three reduction scenarios to avoid unnecessary complexity. According to the
Danish RBMP for 2015 to 2021, they had a reduction goal of 31.8% for N based on a total annual
loading calculated as an average from 2010 to 2014 (Miljø­ og fødevareministeriet 2016). The
N loading in the present paper is therefore close to the average loading used in the previous
RBMP, and the reduction of 30% is close to that of the RBMP from 2015 to 2021. In regards to
P, it is thought that it would be difficult to realize a 30% reduction in P loading. However, a 30%
reduction was chosen to keep it similar to the N reductions.

4
Discussion
To support the knowledge of potential issues with climate change and the recovery of eelgrass
meadows, a dynamic model was used to investigate the effects of changing temperatures and
wind speeds in Limfjorden. All scenarios of future and present climate settings included scenar­
ios with reductions in nutrient loading to accommodate potential future management initiatives.
The findings showed that increasing temperatures pose a threat to eelgrass in Limfjorden and
resulted in a loss of more than a third of the existing eelgrass coverage. The decrease of wind
speeds in the winter and spring, and increase in wind speeds in summer and autumn had a mi­
nor effect on eelgrass coverage. The reduction in external nutrient loadings showed potential
to increase the coverage of eelgrass, but the potential was negated by the increase in temper­
ature. Similarly, the reduction in external nutrient loading allowed eelgrass to grow at greater
depths, while the increasing temperatures led to eelgrass growing at shallower depths. The
potential causes and interplay between these scenarios will be discussed further.
4.1
Model validation
In general, the model was able to sufficiently simulate locations and eelgrass biomasses when
compared to observational data, see fig. 3.3 and fig. 3.4. The model performance with mod­
elling chemical parameters were examined visually and Spearman’s Rank Correlation coeffi­
cients were calculated at three different measurement stations, see fig. 3.1 and table 3.1. The
modelled values generally looked sufficient in the summer for CH, DIN, DIP, TN and TP. For
the nutrients DIN, DIP, TN and TP, the model catches periods with limited nutrients effectively,
while the peaks in the spring are simulated by the model to the same extent as the observed
data. The model did not simulate the lack of DO at the bottom to a satisfactory degree, while the
surface DO levels were simulated with a better fit. The model simulates the water temperatures
very well, as shown in fig. 3.2. For station VIB3702, the bottom temperature varies greatly in
the months of December and January, which is thought to be due to warmer waters flowing into
the system at the Thyborøn boundary along the bottom as VIB3702 is located relatively close
to the boundary.
The model performance measured as Spearman’s Rank Correlation coefficient did not return
adequate values for most of the chemical parameters as seen in table 3.1. It could be due
to only modelling a single year, and therefore only validating with observational data from a
single year. The model has previously been validated for the period of 2002 to 2016, when used
commercially by DHI (DHI 2020b). They evaluated the model performance both qualitatively and
quantitatively across three performance measures: Percent Bias, Spearman’s Rank Correlation
and Cost Function across 12 measurement stations. According to their Percent Bias, the model
achieves what they categorize as ’excellent’, ’very good’ or ’good’ in 84% of all measurements.
According to the Spearman’s Rank Correlation, this was 81% and for the Cost Function, this
was 100% (DHI 2020b).
The validation over a longer time period by DHI (2020b) shows that the underlying mechanisms
and processes are well described by the model, despite the inadequate validation results shown
in this paper. DHI’s validation over a period of 14 years is based on a larger quantity of observed
data and it is thereby considered to be better than the validation presented in this paper. It
is possible that the year of 2010 was a year with unusual weather and as a result provided
abnormal observed values compared to other years. According to Cappelen (2012), the winter
of 2010 was colder than usual, and the ice cover of Limfjorden could have been greater that
year, which could explain cases of observed abnormally lower DO levels at the water bottom

4.2
Scenario setup
For all the reduction scenarios, the initial conditions were the simulated results of the combined
scenario ’..._NPC’ when simulated for eight consecutive years. This was done to ensure the
sediment pools of both N, P and C had reached a reduced level, so that the effect of reducing
external loadings were not negated by internal loadings. This means that the ’..._NC’ scenarios
have also been subjected to reduced internal P loading and conversely, the ’..._P’ scenarios
have been subjected to reduced internal N and C loading. This should be taken into consid­
eration when interpreting the results, since the results across the reduction scenarios are very
similar. A more satisfactory method would be to run each of the reduction scenario for eight
consecutive years, but due to time constraints this was not possible.
The paper has utilized climate scenarios thought to represent the expected climate change
within the study area. The climate scenarios were constructed as changes to temperature and
wind speeds, yet the reality is that climate change will change other climate variables than these.
For example, the model in the presented paper does not include changes in mean water depths,
and this is expected to increase by 50 centimeters in the RCP8.5 scenario (DMI 2020). This will
likely affect the eelgrass meadows in Limfjorden since it will potentially increase or decrease the
area with potential for eelgrass growth. Other climate variables expected to change includes
precipitation, evaporation and solar irradiance. Daily mean precipitation is expected to increase
by 14% and evaporation is expected to increase 4% in Limfjorden, while the solar irradiance is
predicted to decrease by 3.4% (DMI 2020). These predicted changes to climate variables were
not included in this paper, and therefore requires further examination.
4.3
Spatial distribution of eelgrass
Regarding the spatial distribution of eelgrass meadows as predicted by the model, there ap­
pears to be a good alignment with observed eelgrass when comparing fig. 3.3 and fig. 3.4. It
is documented that eelgrass has high light requirements, and therefore the growth of eelgrass
depends on the water depth (Lee, Park, and Kim 2007). This is also apparent in the predicted
eelgrass meadows by the model, where the eelgrass densities decrease with increasing depths
and the highest densities are seen closer to the shore. Multiple studies also show that light is
the main governing factor for eelgrass growth (Carstensen et al. 2013; T. M. Greve and Krause­
Jensen 2005; J. Carr et al. 2010). The accumulated dose of irradiance at the bottom is plotted
in appendix chapter K, where it is apparent that the accumulated irradiance dose are largest at
shallow waters. It is apparent that the larger broads in Limfjorden are too deep, or otherwise
does not receive enough light for eelgrass to successfully grow in these areas.
The loss of eelgrass coverage in the scenarios with increased temperatures, T_2010 and
TW_2010, seems to be most visible in areas with already low densities of eelgrass. This can
especially be seen in the area of Nibe Bredning that has large areas with densities of 5 to 50
g C m­2 in the baseline, which disappeared in the TW_NPC scenario, see fig. 3.7. This is also
apparent in table 3.2, where densities of 5 to 25 g C m­2 decreased from 6% in the baseline to
4.2% in T_2010 and TW_2010. This highlights that the loss of eelgrass is most pronounced in
less developed eelgrass meadows. It is also visible that the areas with high densities in Dråby
Vig in the baseline does not have the same high densities in the TW_2010 scenario.
The distribution of eelgrass is also impacted by the bed shear stress, and 0.08 N m­2 is the
threshold in the model for re­suspension from the upper sediment layer and the underlying layer
as explained in Kuusemäe et al. (2016). The accumulated amount of days where the current­

4.4
Eelgrass coverage’s across scenarios
The modelled eelgrass coverage was highest in the scenarios with nutrient reductions coupled
with either 2010 climate or future wind scenario. The eelgrass coverage is calculated as the
sum of the area of grid cells with eelgrass densities above 5 g C m­2. The average cell size in
the model was 0.075 km2. In reality, the eelgrass might not cover the entirety of the grid cell,
but only part of it. This should be taken into consideration since it would possibly decrease the
actual coverage of eelgrass.
4.4.1
Temperature effects on coverage
The total areal coverage of eelgrass decreased by 5.4%, from 13.9% in the baseline to 8.5%
in T_2010 and TW_2010 scenarios with the biggest losses in less developed meadows with
densities of 5 to 25 g C m­2, see table 3.2 and fig. 3.5. Studies of Chesapeake Bay showed
that early summer heat events with mean water temperatures of 30°C for two weeks resulted
in a loss of 97% of eelgrass coverage (Moore, Shields, and Parrish 2014). The loss of eelgrass
coverage in the temperature scenarios T_2010 and TW_2010 is not thought to be due to same
extreme temperature events, since the year of 2010 did not have record high temperatures in
Denmark (Cappelen 2012). The loss of coverage is the result of simulating nine consecutive
years with increased temperatures, and it is therefore thought to be a gradual decrease in eel­
grass coverage. It can stand to reason that similar events of high temperatures, as described
by Moore, Shields, and Parrish (2014), will occur for Limfjorden in the future.
Eelgrass has an optimum temperature of around 20°C, and a threshold of 25°C (Nejrup and
Pedersen 2008; Moore, Shields, Parrish, and Orth 2012). Therefore, it is thought that the in­
creased temperatures will exceed this threshold in shallow waters, and thereby have a detri­
mental effect on eelgrass meadows (Krause­Jensen, C. Duarte, et al. 2020). In a temperate
setting such as Limfjorden, where water temperatures are generally below the optimum tem­
perature during most of the year (fig. 3.2), it is considered that an increase in temperature might
stimulate eelgrass growth and result in increased coverage. However, that is not the case in
the results presented in this paper, where the increased temperature reduced the eelgrass cov­
erage by more than a third compared to the baseline, see fig. 3.5. Beca­Carretero et al. 2018
also argued that the eelgrass in temperate waters such as Limfjorden already lives close to its
optimum temperature, which is why increasing temperature is expected to negatively impact
the eelgrass meadows. The temperatures in the baseline scenario generally reached the opti­
mum temperature of 20°C in the end of July and in August for the three different measurement
stations as seen in fig. 3.2. Although for VIB3702 and VIB3708, they are located at deeper
waters in broads, whereas VIB3727 is located further inwards of Limfjorden in Skive Fjord at
shallower waters and the temperatures at the bottom reaches 24°C. This highlights that the wa­
ter temperature in shallow waters might exceed the optimum temperature for extended periods
and cause reduced growth. This is corroborated by the water temperature time series of the

One of the potential causes in the loss of eelgrass coverage is the increase in the light com­
pensation point, ice, as described in appendix section B.3. The increase in temperature affects
the light required for eelgrass to have a net carbon uptake due to increasing respiration rates
(Staehr and Borum 2011; Krause­Jensen, C. Duarte, et al. 2020; Hammer et al. 2018). This
increase in required light is modelled to grow exponentially with temperature. For example,
temperatures of 10°C yields a compensation point of 24 µE m­2 s­1 compared to 54 µE m­2 s­1
at 30°C, see fig. B.6. When the available light for eelgrass production, ieec in appendix chap­
ter B, is lower than the compensation point, the growth factor flige_EC and subsequent µiec in
eq. (B.1) and eq. (B.3) becomes negative. When the available light is below the compensa­
tion point, the behavior of the growth factor flige_EC can be seen in the right side of appendix
fig. B.4. It is likely that in shallow waters with high temperatures, the high light compensation
point is the reason behind the loss of eelgrass coverage in the temperature scenarios.
The results in fig. 3.8 show that 75% of eelgrass in baseline is found at depths shallower than
3.4 meters with a median depth of 2.3 meters. This is well aligned with the latest officially
reported numbers, where the main distribution of eelgrass in Limfjorden is found at depths of
1.8 to 2.6 meters in 2019 (Hansen and Høgslund 2021). In scenario T_2010 and TW_2010,
75% of the eelgrass is found at depths shallower than 2.8 meters with a median depth of 1.8
meters. The decrease in the upper quartile from 3.4 meters in baseline to 2.8 meters in T_2010
and TW_2010 is thought to be because of the increased temperatures. It is possible that the
increased temperatures causes the light compensation point, ice, to increase as previously
discussed. The increase in required light causes the eelgrass to grow at shallower depths that
are typically also warmer. This leads to a possible feedback cycle where eelgrass moves to
shallower and warmer waters, causing the net growth of eelgrass to decrease.
The dispersion of eelgrass across depths changes as well, and the majority of the eelgrass
seems to move towards shallower waters. In the baseline, 50% of the eelgrass is found at
a range of 2.2 meters, which is between depths of 1.2 to 3.4 meters, whereas for T_2010 and
TW_2010, it is found at a range of 1.7 meters, which is between depths of 1.1 to 2.8 meters. This
highlights that the range of depths suitable for eelgrass growth decreases. This was unexpected
to some extent, as an increase in temperature was thought to be largest at shallower waters,
and thereby causing decreased growth in shallower waters. Other model studies examined the
effect of temperature on eelgrass systems, and they found that the system was bistable with
stable states of no eelgrass (bare sediments) or a vegetated state. They found that increasing
the temperature by either 3 or 5°C will shift the bistable range towards shallower depths, and
eventually push eelgrass meadows past a critical bifurcation point and beyond recovery (J. Carr
et al. 2010; J. A. Carr et al. 2012). It is possible that if increased temperatures is accompanied
by increasing water clarity and better light conditions, it might be possible to increase growth at
greater depths, but this study shows that this is at least not the case in Limfjorden.
Increasing temperatures not only affect the growth of eelgrass, but also other important bio­
logical processes that could also have an affect on eelgrass recovery. It is possible that the
eelgrass is still limited to the shallower waters due to its light requirements. The increasing
temperature also stimulates growth of phytoplankton and zooplankton, which in return would
lead to worse light conditions for eelgrass at greater depths. Similarly, the amount of sediment
rework performed by lugworms is dependant of temperature. Increased temperatures in turn
increases the loss of eelgrass seedlings due to burial by lugworms, as described in eq. (F.3),
which is explained further in Kuusemäe et al. (2016). Therefore, it is likely that the potential for

The depth distribution shown in fig. 3.8 also makes it apparent that it is unlikely that the depth

the depth distribution of eelgrass in 2010 did not achieve GES, and the results show that the
depth distribution will move towards shallower waters, it is considered unlikely that Limfjorden
will achieve GES in a future with increased temperatures.
4.4.2
Nutrient reduction scenarios
The nutrient reduction scenarios showcase a potential to increase the coverage of eelgrass.
Limfjorden has seen significant reductions in external nutrient loadings since the 1990s (Hansen
and Høgslund 2021). Yet the recovery of eelgrass meadows have been lacking, and the results
in this paper highlights that a further reduction in nutrient loadings could aid the recovery of
eelgrass in Limfjorden. The difference in coverage between the three nutrient reduction sce­
narios is minor as seen in fig. 3.5 and table 3.2 with the ’..._P’ scenario yielding the highest
coverage. Studies from parts of Limfjorden have shown that despite a previous reduction in
nutrients, especially N, the light conditions have not improved as expected (Carstensen et al.
2013). Carstensen et al. (2013) argues that perhaps a further reduction in nutrients could make
the primary producers nutrient limited and thereby improve the light conditions for eelgrass in
Limfjorden and support deeper meadows. The further reduction in nutrients were modelled in
this paper, and the climate in 2010 would allow eelgrass meadows to grow at greater depths
as seen in fig. 3.8. This could indicate that the argument presented in Carstensen et al. (2013)
is likely to be true, and that reducing nutrients further could improve the light conditions in Lim­
fjorden enough to allow eelgrass to grow at greater depths. Although when the further nutrient
reductions were coupled with changes in temperature, it negated the effects of nutrient reduc­
tions, see fig. 3.8. This indicates that the increasing temperature will be the main challenge
for eelgrass recovery in Limfjorden. It is not known whether the nutrient reduction scenarios
are sufficient for creating better light conditions for eelgrass in the present model, as argued by
Carstensen et al. (2013). However, it highlights that the light conditions for eelgrass, especially
with increasing temperatures, need to be improved to allow eelgrass growth at greater depths.

Conclusion
In conclusion, the model was successful in modelling the spatial distribution of eelgrass in Lim­
fjorden in the baseline scenario. The model setup has previously been successfully validated
for commercial use by DHI over a period of 14 years (DHI 2020b). However, when validating
the model for a single year it highlighted some discrepancies between the model’s results and
observed data. Since the only change in this model setup from the one validated by DHI is the
optimum temperature for eelgrass, the model results can be considered to be representative of
Limfjorden.
The findings highlighted that a further reduction in external nutrient loadings in today’s climate
would increase the eelgrass coverage in Limfjorden by 1.2 to 1.3%, depending on the nutrient
scenario. The predicted changes in wind speeds allow for a slightly higher eelgrass cover­
age, except when combined with temperature changes, which then negates the positive effects
of changes in wind speeds. The increasing temperatures caused eelgrass coverage in Lim­
fjorden to decrease by more than a third, even when accompanied with a further reduction in
external nutrient loading. Other studies have shown that increased temperatures at shallow wa­
ters poses great threats for eelgrass meadows (Krause­Jensen, C. Duarte, et al. 2020; Moore,
Shields, Parrish, and Orth 2012; J. Carr et al. 2010; J. A. Carr et al. 2012). The increasing
temperatures are thought to increase the required light for eelgrass to grow, causing the eel­
grass to move towards shallower waters. The reduction in nutrients allowed the eelgrass to
grow at greater depths for all climate scenarios, although the effect were minimal in scenarios
with increased temperatures. The results of eelgrass growing at shallower depths accompanied
by a large decrease in coverage highlights the need for future management of improving light
conditions in Limfjorden. It is thought that the reduction in nutrients is not sufficient to enhance
the light conditions in Limfjorden. The light conditions do not enable eelgrass to grow at depths
where temperatures are at or below the optimum temperature, which causes decreased growth.
5.1
Future perspectives
This study highlights the need to consider multiple pressures, including climate change, in order
to achieve successful recovery of eelgrass, as also argued in Krause­Jensen, C. Duarte, et al.
(2020). With impending climate change, it is likely that eelgrass recovery will be even harder to
achieve. It is therefore suggested that future studies continue to examine the effects of climate
change on eelgrass populations to provide better insight into the recovery trajectory. It would be
relevant to use a similar model setup as the one used for this study, but with definite changes in
water clarity to examine the necessary light conditions needed to enhance eelgrass coverage
with a changing climate. Furthermore, it would be relevant to model the impact of other climate
variables, such as rising water levels and decreases in solar irradiance, on eelgrass recovery
