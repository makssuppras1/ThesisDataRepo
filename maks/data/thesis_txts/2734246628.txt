Abstract
Shading and shadowing of linelights remains a challenge in real-time computer graphics. Mod-
ern stochastic methods can leverage hardware ray tracing to provide excellent results in well-lit
regions, but shadows are still noisy. Analytic shading methods are well developed, but analytic
shadowing is costly. In this thesis I perform analytic computation of the visibility of the linelight
on the GPU to facilitate real-time shading and shadowing of linelights. I show a method for com-
puting the visibility of the linelight for a given point in the scene, then show how to implement this
method as a fragment shader on the GPU. A modified triangle-triangle intersection algorithm is
developed for the purpose of computing occlusion of the linelight. A primitive ray traced shader is
implemented to verify correctness of the analytic solution, and compare performance to a similarly
implemented stochastic solution. GPU-friendly acceleration structures are used to bring perfor-
mance of both barely within the range of practical usability, but performance remains too slow
and variable for most real-world applications. Finally, I explore the effects of scene complexity
and placement of the linelight on performance.

1
Introduction
Realistic lighting of scenes is a persistent challenge in real-time computer graphics. In a real-time
context such as games, the time permitted for rendering a frame is strictly budgeted, limiting the
kinds of light that can easily be rendered. The point light is commonly used, many lights are well-
approximated as a point source such as light bulbs or candles, and good methods exist for computing
the light and shadow cast from these in real-time. In the real world fluorescent lights and neon tubes are
common, but these are poorly represented by the zero-dimensional point source. The one-dimensional
’linelight’ (usually ’linear light’ in literature) is a good model for these light sources, but presents a
challenge in real time rendering. It is defined in 3D space as a line of finite length and zero width
connecting endpoints l0 and l1,
L : t ·⃗l + l0,
⃗l = (l1 −l0),
t ∈[0, 1].
(1)
While the above definition fixes the location in space, it does not characterize the light emitted. Several
models are used for the linelight, in particular modeling the light as an infinite series of collinear point
lights, or as an emissive cylinder with a radius taken to zero in the limit. In this work I choose to use
the former for its mathematical simplicity, as well as restricting the shading of surfaces to the diffuse
component of the light for simplicity. The methods I present are concerned with shadowing of the
linelight, and so are not exclusive to this model of the linelight for the purposes of shading. Any model
for which the emission profile is uniform along the length of the light will work for shading.
The primary challenge of illuminating a scene with a linelight is the computation of the shadows cast
by the light. Many approaches have been taken towards doing so efficiently, and it is the primary
focus of this project. The basic principle involves constructing a triangle spanning the endpoints of
the linelight and the shaded point, which I define the ”light-triangle”,
Light-triangle: The triangle spanning the endpoints l0, l1 of the linelight and the shaded point P.
If an object intersects this triangle, it casts a shadow on the point.
In this work I apply simple
methods for directly computing the visibility of the linelight in a GPU fragment shader, in an attempt
to produce real-time analytic linelight shading and shadowing. Source code for the renderer can be
found at https://github.com/WorstWizard/linelights.
1.1
Prior work
Both the challenge of accurate shading as well as fast evaluation of the visibility of the linelight has
been tackled many times in the past. Nishita et al. [NON85] computes analytic solutions for diffuse
shading for linelights parallel and perpendicular to a surface, resolves specular shading by sampling
the light as point sources. For efficient shadowing, they suggest the use of shadow volumes to compute
penumbrae, restricting the amount of computation needed. Poulin et al. [PA91] develops an analytic
model of the diffuse reflection, and approximates the specular reflection using Chebyshev polynomials.
For shadowing, they use a 3D grid to voxelize the light-triangle and a linelight-oriented segmented
buffer, partitioning the space wrt. to the linelight itself. This method was later expanded upon by
Tanaka et al. [TT95]. Picott [Pic92] computes an exact expression for the diffuse component, and uses
a most-representative-point approach to approximate specular the reflection, an approximation that’s
still popular in real-time graphics [Ri˜n19]. Bao et al. [BP93] similarly develop analytic expressions for
diffuse and specular reflection, and use a BSP tree for acceleration of shadowing.
More recently, Billen et al. [BD16] use linelights as a tool to sample area lights for global illumination.
They use a BVH accelerate shadowing.
Heitz et al.
[HH17] builds on their own prior work on
linearly transformed cosines (LTC) for area-light rendering to produce cheap specular shading for
linelights.
Most recently, Christoph Peters [Pet21] develops techniques for solid-angle importance
sampling of linelights. The result, Monte Carlo integrated shading with ray traced shadows, almost
entirely converges in two samples per pixel, with almost no noise in well-lit areas, although noise

Theory
Consider the problem of computing the shadows cast by a point light: A straightforward solution and
the working principle of ray tracing, is to draw a ray from the light to each point in the scene that
is to be shaded, testing whether the ray hits any geometry between the light and the shaded point.
An analogue exists for the linelight: Substitute the point light for a linelight and the ray becomes
a light-triangle. As in ray tracing, we can check whether the linelight is visible at a given point by
testing if the light-triangle intersects any geometry.

2.1
Fundamental challenge of visibility computation
The problem of efficiently computing the intersection of the light-triangle with the scene is inherited
from ray tracing. The addition of an extra dimension to the intersection makes the problem much
more however. Suppose we wish to compute shadows of a point light using ray tracing. The brute force
approach of testing for intersection against each triangle in the scene requires O(N) time, ⃗n being the
number of triangles. Since only a single intersection is enough to rule out visibility, if we had perfect
knowledge of which triangles were in the path of the ray, we would only need to visit the closest of
these triangles to guarantee the outcome. Using an acceleration structure such as a kd-tree or BVH,
one can reduce the number of operations from O(N) to O(log N).
Now we try doing the same for a linelight, computing the intersection of the light-triangle with the
scene: The brute force approach works much the same as for ray tracing, requiring a test with each
triangle, yielding O(N) tests. Let’s once again assume perfect knowledge of which triangles of the
scene can intersect the light-triangle: Since an intersection with the light-triangle does not necessarily
entirely occlude the light, we actually need to visit every single candidate triangle to ensure that we
compute the correct visibility. This is still on the order of O(N) time in the worst case. A realistic
example is to compute visibility of a point occluded by a triangle-strip, where the resulting light-
triangle intersects the entire strip. Each triangle requires a separate intersection test to ensure the
visibility is correctly evaluated.

Once the visibility of the light has been computed for the shaded point P, we are left with zero or more
pieces of the linelight, each of which can be treated as separate unobstructed linelights for the purpose
of shading, so long as the shading method assumes uniform brightness across the length of the light.
Any such method for computing the shading of a linelight work at this stage. In particular, [HH17]
uses linearly transformed cosines to efficiently approximate the specular component of the lighting for
linelights, a method I would have attempted to implement given more time. It is worth noting that
they use a cylinder model of the linelight with optional end caps. If the cylinder end caps are enabled
on each effective linelight, additional ”virtual” emissive surfaces are created on the insides of the light,
increasing the effective radiance of the light.
3
Methodology
Analytic shadowing is achieved through direct per-pixel evaluation of the visibility of the light. If we
can compute the intersection between the light-triangle and another object, then we can compute the
5
==PAGE:6==
Figure 3: Caption
occlusion of the light by that object. I here limit myself to triangles as a geometric primitive, but
the method is at least theoretically applicable to any convex object for which an intersection with a
triangle can be computed.
The visibility of the linelight L, defined by its endpoints l0 and l1 at a given point P with surface
normal ⃗n is computed as follows:
1. Clip the linelight to the part visible above the horizon wrt. ⃗n.
2. Construct the light-triangle TL between points l0, l1, P.
3. For each occluder in the scene, compute the intersection between the occluder and the light-
triangle TL. If an intersection exists, project it onto the light L through point P, the overlapped
part of the linelight is then occluded.
4. Any part of the light that was not occluded is visible at point P.
This general method for occluding the light, sometimes called ’light clipping’ is the foundation of
analytic linelight shadowing, and used universally [NON85; PA91; BP93; TT95; BD16]. The following
sections describe a method for tracking the visible parts of the light, and an algorithm for computing
the intersection and subsequent projection of occluding triangle.
3.1
Visible intervals
Recall the parametrization of the linelight,
L : t ·⃗l + l0,
⃗l = (l1 −l0),
t ∈[0, 1].
Given this parametrization, the part of the light visible at a point P is conveniently represented as a
set of subintervals on the unit interval [0, 1]. I denote this set of ”visible intervals” VL , which initially
equals {[0, 1]}, entirely visible.
After computing the intersection between an occluder and the light-triangle, the intersection is pro-
jected onto the light. The overlap of the projection on the light can be described by an ”occluding
interval” of t-values (t0, t1).
Once the occluding interval is obtained, it is used to ”cut away” the visible parts of the light, summa-

Interval occlusion procedure
Given an occluding interval: For each visible interval remaining in VL , one of four cases is true:
1. The occluding and visible interval are disjoint: No change is made.
2. One edge of the visible interval is occluded: The occluding part is cut by shrinking the interval.
3. The visible interval is contained in the occluding interval: The visible interval is removed from
VL .
4. The occluding interval is contained in the visible interval: The occluding interval is ”cut” from
the visible interval, resulting in two visible intervals, to the left and right of the occluding interval.
Due to case 4 where a visible interval is split into two new intervals, in the worst case ⃗n occluders
results in N + 1 visible intervals. As such, this procedure requires memory on the order O(N), per
pixel. In practice, the highest number of visible intervals will be much less than ⃗n, unless the scene
contains ”fence-like” geometry, that splits the light into many visible pieces. But without a guarantee
on the order in which occluding triangles are visited even a contiguous piece of geometry may still
result in many visible intervals temporarily, as illustrated by figure 4.
3.2
Occlusion interval of a triangle
In this section a triangle-triangle intersection algorithm for computing the occlusion interval of tri-
angles is described. The algorithm is derived from the work of [Ake97], modified for the purpose of
more directly producing the occlusion interval. See also sec. 6 for a discussion on the merits of this
modification.
It is not only of interest whether or not the light-triangle and a potential occluding triangle intersect;
if they do intersect, the intersection (a line segment) is projected onto the light to determine how
much of the light is occluded by the triangle, as described in the previous section. Rather than finding

returns an occluding interval as a pair of t-values. If the triangles do not intersect, an arbitrary pair
of t-values that do not overlap the interval [0, 1] is returned, e.g. (∞, ∞).

The remainder of the problem is handled in the 2D plane of the triangle. For the sake of brevity I
reuse notation, such that P may be understood as the 2D point resulting from projection of the 3D

As shown on figure 5, denote the edges connecting the sampled point P to l0 and l1 the ”left” and

point P and III: the region ”below” point P.
Intersection cases
There are six possible different cases depending on the location of the intersection C relative to the
light and point P, see figure 6 for reference. Other configurations are rejected by the previous test. A
seventh case is possible, but isn’t handled by the method as described here, see sec. 3.2.1 below.
1. C is entirely in region II: The intersection is projected onto the light, and the corresponding
t-values (t0, t1) are returned.
2. C is entirely in region I: There is no intersection with the light-triangle.
3. C is entirely in region III: There is no intersection with the light-triangle.
4. One endpoint of C is in region II, another in region I or III, and C does not intersect the left or
right edge: There is no intersection with the light-triangle.
5. One endpoint of C is in region II, another in region I or III, and C intersects both the left and
right edges: The light is fully occluded, t-values (−∞, ∞) are returned.

3.2.1
Limitations
The presented algorithm for the computation of the occluding interval of a triangle assumes that the
linelight does not itself intersect any triangles in the scene. If this is not true, then one endpoint of C
will fall in region I, the other in either regions II or III (figure 5). This causes errors in the shadowing
computation, and thereby graphical artifacts. This can be handled with relative easy generally, but
if the triangle to be shaded is the same triangle that is intersected by the linelight, then one of the
endpoints of C will exactly coincide with the shaded point P, and it is not obvious which part of the
linelight should be occluded. A solution is to use the normal of the triangle to resolve which part
should be occluded.

3.4
Acceleration
The described method for computing shadows suffer a familiar problem in common with ray tracing:
The majority of the computation is spent calculating the intersections between the light-triangle and
the scene. While there may be many thousands of triangles in a scene, usually only a very small
subset of these are hit by the light-triangle. I test two simple acceleration structures for improving
performance, a uniform 3D grid, and a similar double-layered 3D grid. More sophisticated acceleration
structures such as bounding volume hierarchies (BVH) and binary space partitioning (BSP) trees are
commonly used in ray tracing; a BSP tree was already used by Bao et al. [BP93] for linelight shadowing.
The acceleration structures used for this project were chosen for their ease of implementation, especially
in regards to a GPU-based implementation.
3.4.1
Uniform grid
The entire scene is partitioned into a uniform grid of axis-aligned bounding boxes (AABB). Each voxel
contains zero or more triangles. Intersection tests are performed on the bounding boxes first, and if an
intersection is found, every triangle contained within the box. For simplicity, triangles on the borders
of boxes are said to be contained in both. The physical extent or size of the grid Sgrid, is made just
large enough to contain the scene. The resolution of the grid is the number of boxes along each axis,
(Gx, Gy, Gz). For simplicity, I let the grid be cubic, that is G = Gx = Gy = Gz, and define G as the
grid resolution. Then the total number of bounding boxes is G3.
Suppose testing for intersection with a box has zero cost: Then ideally the grid should be so dense that
each box contains only one triangle, so that only those triangles that can intersect the light-triangle are
ever tested. Since checking intersection with a box does itself have a computational cost, at a certain
density, the cost of box intersections will outweigh the cost of triangle intersections. An experiment
was conducted to see measure this tradeoff, see figure 13. The more triangles in the scene, the more
benefit there is to a denser grid, so the optimal density is ultimately dependent on the scene, also
shown by figure 13 by the Sponza scene benefitting from higher density than the dragon scenes.

3.4.2
Top/bottom grid
The scene is first partitioned into a uniform grid of AABB’s, with a grid resolution GT . Let this be the
”top-level” grid. Each bounding box of the top-level grid is then further subdivided into subgrids, with
grid resolution GB, see figure 7. I call this the bottom-level grid. All these subgrids together cover
the entire scene in a dense grid of resolution G = GT · GB, colloquially referred to as the bottom-level
grid as well, but which I here call the dense grid for clarification.
Intersection tests are performed on the top-level grid first. If the light-triangle intersects a box in the
top level, then intersection tests are performed against every box in the contained bottom-level grid.
As with the uniform grid, if there is an intersection with one of these boxes, then tests are run on each
triangle contained within. Using two levels in this manner allows a denser bottom-level grid, effectively
increasing the grid resolution while keeping the number of boxes to check low. It is convenient to use
the same number of subdivisions for the top and bottom grids GT B = GT = GB. Then the total
number of boxes in the dense grid is G3 = G6
T B.
3.4.3
Triangle-AABB intersection
The light-triangle is voxelized into the grid to quicker find candidate triangles for intersection. To do
so I use the triangle-AABB intersection algorithm proposed by Schwartz et al. [SS10], which takes
advantage of each box being identical by reusing a lot of the intermediary work that depends only on
the triangle and the size of the box. A brief summary of the algorithm follows:
First it is checked whether or not the triangle plane intersects the bounding box. This is done by
identifying the two diagonally opposed points of the box most closely aligned with the normal of the
plane, and checking whether these points are on opposite sides of the plane using equation 4. If the
plane cuts through the box, then for each coordinate plane, it is checked whether the 2D projection of
the box and the triangle into that plane overlap. This check is performed using edge functions[Pin88]
to check whether the projected vertices of the box are contained within the projected triangle.
3.5

As a point of comparison and used to verify the correctness of the analytical method, a stochastic

simple: A point along the linelight is picked uniformly at random, and the point is treated as a point

on the offset, with no intermediate values.
4
Implementation
The renderer is implemented in the Rust programming language for reasons of preference and perfor-
mance, using the Vulkan graphics API. Vulkan allows very direct access to the GPU, making it a good
choice for experimental graphics programming, although its verbosity and low-level control likewise
makes it difficult to work with. Shaders were written in GLSL for reasons of familiarity, but I would
strongly consider HLSL if I were to do another similar project: The memory layout specifications of
GLSL are not easily understood and it caused some problems that were very hard to track dowm.
The lack of a 16-bit primitive also proved frustraing when trying to pass 16 bit vertex indices to the
fragment shader, for which the solution was of course to switch to 32-bit indices, which turned out to
be necessary regardless when I switched to the larger scenes.
In the Rust language, third-party libraries/modules are called ’crates’. The renderer is built using many
crates, of which the most important dependencies are: The ’ash’ crate for Rust language bindings for
Vulkan, the ’winit’ crate for cross-platform window, event and input handling, the ’glam’ crate for
vector and matrix types and operations, the ’shaderc’ crate for Rust bindings to the C library of the
same name, for runtime compilation of shaders and the ’gltf’ crate for loading model files.
The entirety of the shadowing and shading method described in the previous sections is implemented
as a single fragment shader on the GPU, as a proof-of-concept.

4.2
Visibility computation
Visibility of the linelight is computed in the fragment shader by the method descibed in sec. 3. The
position of the linelight is given through a uniform buffer, the position and surface normal of the
point P as outputs from the vertex shader/mesh. The visibility is tracked using an ’IntervalArray’
struct, the implementation of which is described in sec. 4.4, initially containing just the unit interval
[0, 1]. Without assumption of how the candidate triangles are obtained (as it depends on the choice of
acceleration structure), the visibility is calculated by testing for intersection between the light-triangle
and each candidate triangle, and if an intersection occurs, using the resulting occluding interval to
obscure part of the light, see listing 1. For a full example of how triangles are acquired from the
acceleration structures, the top/bottom grid traversal code for the analytic shader can be found in
appendix B.

distance from the sampled point P and the endpoints of C to the linelight is computed using equation

determined if one of cases 1, 2 or 3 is true. If none of these are true, I compute whether C crosses the
left or right edge of the light-triangle using eq. 7, and depending on the answer decide between cases

4.3
2D projection artifacts
When calculating the occluding interval, I project the intersections and light-triangle into the xz-
plane by dropping the y-coordinate. Doing the proper projection into the plane of the light-triangle
would require a full coordinate transformation of each point the problem into the new point of view.
Dropping one coordinate costs nothing, and is almost always good enough: The method only fails if
the light-triangle is parallel to the y-axis. Per visual inspection, these errors are hardly noticeable. It
wasn’t possible to find an example at all in the dragon scene when using the small linelight. Using the
scene with the larger light, an example was found along the spine of the dragon, see figure 8.

bool tri_tri_intersect(
vec3 l0, vec3 l1, vec3 pos,
vec3 v0, vec3 v1, vec3 v2,
out vec2 interval
) {
// Plane equation for occluding triangle: dot(n, x) + d = 0
vec3 e0 = v1 - v0;
vec3 e1 = v2 - v0;
vec3 n = cross(e0, e1);
float d = -dot(n, v0);
// Put light triangle into plane equation
float d_l0 = dot(n, l0) + d;
float d_l1 = dot(n, l1) + d;
float d_pos = dot(n, pos) + d;
// Same sign on all means they're on same side of plane
if (d_l0*d_l1 > 0.0 && d_l0*d_pos > 0.0) return false;
// Plane equation for light triangle: dot(n, x) + d = 0
vec3 L = l1 - l0;
e1 = pos - l0;
n = cross(L, e1);
d = -dot(n, l0);
// Put triangle 1 into plane equation 2
float dv0 = dot(n, v0) + d;
float dv1 = dot(n, v1) + d;
float dv2 = dot(n, v2) + d;
float ddv1 = dv0*dv1;
float ddv2 = dv0*dv2;
if (ddv1 > 0.0 && ddv2 > 0.0) return false;
interval = compute_interval(l0, l1, pos, v0, v1, v2, n, ddv1, ddv2);
if (interval[0] > 1.0 || interval[1] < 0.0) {
return false;
}
return true;
}

vec2 compute_interval(
vec3 l0, vec3 l1, vec3 pos,
vec3 v0, vec3 v1, vec3 v2,
vec3 n, // Normal vector for plane defined by l0,l1,pos
float dd1, // Product of signed distances of v0 and v1 to triangle l0,l1,pos
float dd2 // Product of signed distances of v0 and v2 to triangle l0,l1,pos
) {
const float INF = 1e10;
// Compute intersection points between triangle and light-triangle plane
vec3 isect0, isect1;
if (dd1 < 0.0) { // Line v1-v0 crosses plane
line_plane_intersect(n, pos, v1 - v0, v0, isect0);
if (dd2 < 0.0) { // Line v2-v0 crosses plane
line_plane_intersect(n, pos, v2 - v0, v0, isect1);
} else {
line_plane_intersect(n, pos, v2 - v1, v1, isect1);
}
} else { // Lines v1-v0 does not cross plane, the others do
line_plane_intersect(n, pos, v2 - v0, v0, isect0);
line_plane_intersect(n, pos, v2 - v1, v1, isect1);
}
// Checking cases 2 and 3
float dp = dist_to_line_2d_unnormalized(l0.xz, l1.xz, pos.xz);
float sign_of_dp = sign(dp);
dp = abs(dp);
float di0 = sign_of_dp * dist_to_line_2d_unnormalized(l0.xz, l1.xz, isect0.xz);
float di1 = sign_of_dp * dist_to_line_2d_unnormalized(l0.xz, l1.xz, isect1.xz);
if ((di0 < 0.0 && di1 < 0.0) || (di0 > dp && di1 > dp)) return vec2(INF, INF);
// Project intersections onto line t*(l1-l0) + l0 by computation of t-values
float t0, t1;
t0 = line_line_intersect_2d(l0.xz,l1.xz,isect0.xz,pos.xz);
t1 = line_line_intersect_2d(l0.xz,l1.xz,isect1.xz,pos.xz);
// Case 1
if (di0 < dp && di1 < dp) {
sort(t0, t1);
return vec2(t0, t1);
}
// Cases 4, 5, 6
if (di1 >= dp) t1 = t0;
bool intersects_left = linesegments_intersect(l0.xz,pos.xz,isect0.xz,isect1.xz);
bool intersects_right = linesegments_intersect(l1.xz,pos.xz,isect0.xz,isect1.xz);
if (intersects_left) {
if (intersects_right) { // Both
return vec2(-INF, INF);
} else { // Only left
return vec2(-INF, t1);
}
} else if (intersects_right) { // Only right
return vec2(t1, INF);
} else {
return vec2(INF, INF);
}
}

Here I define an ”IntervalArray” struct. It holds an array of intervals with a fixed maximum capacity
to comply with the static size requirements, and a size variable to track the current number of intervals
in the array.
A few methods are defined for manipulating the struct:
• add interval: Adds the specified interval to the end of the array, incrementing the size.
• remove interval: Removes the i’th interval by swapping it with the last interval in the array,
then decrementing the size.
• occlude intervals: Using the provided occluding interval, iterates over each visible interval in
the array and modifies the array, as detailed in sec. 3.1.
The struct is initalized with a size of 1, and the first element of the array the unit interval [0, 1].
The simple loop over the intervals in occlude intevals means that occlusion takes time on the order
of the amount of intervals in the array.

void remove_interval(inout IntervalArray int_arr, int i) {
vec2 last_interval = int_arr.data[int_arr.size - 1];
int_arr.data[i] = last_interval;
int_arr.size--;
}
void add_interval(inout IntervalArray int_arr, vec2 new_interval) {
if (int_arr.size < ARR_MAX) { // Avoid overflow
int_arr.data[int_arr.size] = new_interval;
int_arr.size++;
}
}
void occlude_intervals(inout IntervalArray int_arr, vec2 occ_int) {
for (int i=0; i < int_arr.size; i++) {
vec2 interval = int_arr.data[i];
if (occ_int.x <= interval.x && occ_int.y >= interval.y) {
// Interval is fully occluded, remove it but do not increment `i`
// as the swapped-in element needs to be checked too
remove_interval(int_arr, i);
i--;
} else if (occ_int.x > interval.x && occ_int.y < interval.y) {
// Middle is occluded, shrink existing to the left and add new interval to the right
add_interval(int_arr, vec2(occ_int.y, interval.y));
int_arr.data[i].y = occ_int.x;
} else if (occ_int.x > interval.x && occ_int.x < interval.y) {
// Right side is occluded, shrink to fit
int_arr.data[i].y = occ_int.x;
} else if (occ_int.y > interval.x && occ_int.y < interval.y) {
// Left side is occluded, shrink to fit
int_arr.data[i].x = occ_int.y;
}
}
}

4.5
Acceleration structures
After loading the scene, before rendering, an acceleration structure is built to speed up rendering.
Both of the acceleration structures I implement work in fundamentally the same way, so a lot of
the infrastructure is identical: The fragment shader is given the acceleration structure as a struct
through in a uniform buffer, which contains information on the spatial partitioning of the mesh. The
geometry itself is accessed directly from the vertex buffer, but the vertices are indexed from a separate
”acceleration index” buffer. Like the regular index buffer, the acceleration index buffer contains indices
into the vertex buffer for constructing triangles. The difference is in the ordering, having been built
specifically for reference by the acceleration structure, and permitting duplicated triangles.

4.5.1
Uniform grid
Note: The uniform grid is reused as the bottom-level grid of the top/bottom grid structure, sec. 4.5.2.
Therefore, code listings in this section refer to the structure as BLAS, for ”bottom level acceleration

The acceleration structure is implemented simply as an array of BBOX COUNT= G3 BufferViews, each
pointing to a slice of the acceleration index buffer representing the triangles contained in one of the
bounding boxes of the grid.
Construction
1. Initialize a dynamic array to hold the vertex indices; the acceleration index buffer.
2. For each bounding box, create a new BufferView. The offset is set to the current size of the
acceleration index buffer.
3. For each triangle in the mesh, test for intersection with the bounding box. I reuse the triangle-
aabb intersection algorithm detailed in sec. 3.4.3. If the triangle intersects, the vertex buffer
index associated with each vertex is appended to the end of the acceleration index buffer.
4. The size of the BufferView is set to the amount of indices added.
The construction process can be optimized: The triangle-aabb intersection algorithm works fast when
making several checks against the same triangle. Additionally, each triangle is read from memory once
per bounding box. Swapping the iteration order solves both problems but introduces a new one: The
indices would need to be written to a separate list for each bounding box, which then merge at the
end. Since preprocessing only has to run once at the beginning, the simpler method described above
was used.

Scene complexity
To further explore the effect of scene complexity on performance, an experiment is made to measure
the impact of ”fence-like” geometry. The experimental setup is akin to the illustration of figure 18; a
grating is placed between the linelight and a light catching plane. By varying the length of the light,
we can choose how many disjoint segments of the linelight are visible to points on the light catcher.
Changing the length of the light is permitted in all variants of the rendering method, as only the mesh
must remain static. The camera is pointed at the light catching plane during rendering.
The results of the experiment are shown on figure 19. While performance degradation was expected,
the effect on the performance is a lot larger than one might perhaps expect, going from sub-10ms
to around 200ms: Over a millisecond slower per segment. Do keep in mind that for the test scenes,
the total number of visible intervals rarely reaches higher than 15 (see figure 9) and the final count
is usually much lower still, whereas the test goes to 64 intervals.

Comparison to stochastic shader
I here compare a stochastic shader to the analytic shader. While better solutions exist for stochastic
sampling of linelights, the aim here is to compare the two techniques on as even a footing as I could

its implementation. To facilitate comparison, the dragon scene with a small light was used to measure
performance of both shaders. The number of samples taken of the linelight is set as high as possible,

6
Discussion
The performance of the renderer is good for small amounts of geometry and when using short lights.
Unfortunately, even with a low triangle count, if the light is in an inconvenient position, performance
is heavily affected. The ironic consequence is that performance is best when the linelight shrinks to a
point light. As discussed in sec. 2.1, this dependence is fundamental to the problem of analytic linelight
shadowing. This makes its application in games especially dangerous, as the consistency of framerate is

The chosen acceleration structures do not permit dynamic scenes, although the light source is able to

force method on objects that can move around in the scene, after checking for intersection with an

Another acceleration structure is a good option if the light is static, and given more time I would have
liked to have tested it: A linelight-oriented segmented buffer, as used by [PA91] and further developed
by [TT95]. The space is partitioned into wedge-shaped sections, such that any possible occluding
triangle is contained in the same section as the shaded point. This makes lookup very straightforward
compared to the voxelization required for the grid structures. The downside is a decreased resolution
of the segmentation far away from the light, whereas the grid remains consistent throughout space.
Triangle-triangle intersection
Throughout development of the project, the triangle-triangle intersection algorithm was changed from
the standard algorithm of [Ake97] to the current version as presented in sec. 3.2. The changes mere
motivated by the idea of directly obtaining the occlusion interval without unnecessary intermediate
computations, and the initial modifications seemed a definite improvement for the special purpose of
computing the occlusion interval. At this stage many of the cases described in sec. 3.2 were not handled
correctly however, due to these not occuring in the test scene used at the time. As each case has now
been handled, the algorithm has changed to a state where I suspect using the regular algorithm to find
the intersection (circumventing the need for handling each case separately) and projecting it in 2D
would be equally as efficient or better. As of writing, I unfortunately do not have time to reimplement
the standard algorithm and test the difference.
Additional optimizations
There are a few potential optimizations to the methods and algorithms which have not been included
in the final version of the renderer:
Shrinking the light: During shadowing, if at any point the set of visible intervals VL contains
just one interval (t0, t1), then we can shrink the effective light to just the visible part for all future
computations.
As discussed in sec.
2.1 and verified in sec.
5, a smaller light-triangle results in
fewer possible intersections, and thereby better performance. This optimization was implemented,
but a cursory test showed performance was worse than just using the full light-triangle throughout.
Implementing the optimization requires conditionally shrinking the linelight, as well as recomputing
the intermediate values for the triangle-AABB intersection algorithm, as these depends on the shape
of the triangle. In addition, time is only saved if the shrunken portion of the light would have been
occluded by another triangle in the scene.
Backface culling of occluders: If an occluding triangle faces away from the linelight, it cannot cast
any shadow and can be skipped. Given the surface normal ⃗n of the triangle, the shaded point P and
the linelight endpoints l0 and l1, this is easily tested,

Future work
Many opportunities remain for improvements, optimizations and changes to methodology.
A few
options were discussed above for small optimizations that I have attempted myself in limited capacity.
The following are suggestions for more radical changes:
First, it seems likely that some amount of the visibility computation could be precomputed in a compute
pass, prior to shading. One possibility is to precompute the voxelization of every light-triangle, saving
the result as a 2D array of 64-bit bitmask (assuming a grid resolution of 4), passed to the fragment
shader. Doing these identical calculations at once should reduce branch divergence in the fragment
stage, hopefully producing a speedup. Another avenue for speedup is in the interval array (sec. 4.4):
Figure 19 shows that more visible intervals results in significant performance degradation, and while
a large portion of that time loss is due to repeated calls to the shading function (eq. 3), a lot of
time is spent manipulating the interval array. A possible method for optimization, credit of Mikkel
Gjøl, is to simply approximate the visibility of the linelight with bitmasks marking whether or not a
given part of the light is visible. The principle advantage of this approach is the ability to occlude the
visible intervals by doing a few bitwise AND operations with an occluding interval, also represented by
bitmasks. The downside is the loss of accuracy, swapping the continuous intervals for discrete bitmasks
is roughly equivalent to swapping the linelight for a finite number of point lights, one per bit.
For a final idea: It seems as though it should be possible to gather patches of a mesh, and treat these
as single occluders. As a simple example: A perfectly flat piece of geometry, if subdivided, will cause
a performance loss due to the increased number of triangles. If one could detect and assemble the
subdivided geometry into a single polygon, then performance would improve. The patches do not need
to be flat either: Imagine putting a light above a cone. The shadow cast by the cone would be a circle,
and importantly, even if the cone is rotated to an extent, the shadow would remain elliptical. Looking
only at the shadow then, the occluder that cast it could just as well have been a flat disk. One could
imagine that a patch of a complex surface could be ”reduced” in this same way to a simpler shape for
the purpose of occlusion. Mesh shaders may be useful for computation.
8
Conclusion
In this project I have explored the viability of directly computing the visibility of linelights on the
GPU, on a per-pixel level, for the purposes of real-time shading by linelights. The methods presented
produce accurate analytic lighting at steady framerates for non-complex scenes of low triangle count.
As the performance is tied proportionally to the amount of geometry in the scene, performance steadily
degrades as the scene gets larger. Additionally, using the methods presented in this work, performance
is adversely affected by geometry that splits the visible light, i.e. ”fence-like” geometry. I argue that
this is an inherent challenge in computing shadowing of linelights, but this performance loss can be
mitigated using approximations for both visibility and shading.
The methods as presented do permit real-time usage, but in restrictive circumstances as outlined
above. For the scene of a dragon with a small linelight containing about 30k triangles, frame times
of less than 10ms are achieved.
It seems for my hardware, around
10-30k triangles is the limit
for real-time performance with this method. More optimizations are certainly possible as well. A
simple stochastic shader was built for comparison purposes, but this was limited to the same primitive
acceleration schemes as the analytic linelight shader, inheriting the same performance issues, and so
is easily beaten by the analytic approach in every scene. As of yet, the most practical solution is a
hardware accelerated stochastic approach, such as the method provided by C. Peters [Pet21]. Their
method still suffers noise in penumbra, but this could be mitigated using TAA or denoising.
