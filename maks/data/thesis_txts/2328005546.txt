Abstract 
  
 
Oil exploration in the North Sea is and will continue for the foreseeable future to pose a real and 
substantial risk to marine life in the event of accidental oil spillage. In conjuncture with the 
predicted sea temperature rise associated with climate change, little is known in regards to the 
combined effect of both of these stressors on copepods. 
Although some knowledge has been gained in regards to the effect of these stressors 
individually, many studies have used laboratory reared cultures which have lost much of the 
biological and behavioural traits found in the wild. Due to this, our study focused on comparing 
lethal and sub-lethal factors known to be a strong indicator for survival between laboratory 
reared and wild stock Calanus finmarchicus; the most dominant copepod species in the region.   
Our findings indicate both populations are adversely influenced by pyrene and 
temperature, however a significant difference in their responses could be seen. An increased 
toxic effect to lower pyrene concentrations was apparent in laboratory reared when compared to 
wild stock copepods, with a reduction in faecal pellet production and fecundity observed. 
Combined with increasing temperature these effects where further compounded with wild stock 
appearing more susceptible. This susceptibly was also seen at high pyrene concentrations, with 
significantly greater mortality observed compared to laboratory reared experiments. These 
factors are believed to be largely attributed to differences in biological traits such as thermal 
adaptation and laboratory reared copepods showing greater wax ester content, a factor known to 
offset toxicity of pyrene exposure.

1. Introduction 
 
Calanus finmarchicus is the most prolific boreal Atlantic copepod species and is found primarily 
in the Barents Sea and North Atlantic (Conover 1988). It is the essential component in the 
transfer of energy from primary producer to higher trophic levels with its abundance believed to 
directly linked to the considerable fish stocks, marine mammals and benthic organisms present in 
these areas; many being of strong economic importance (Beaugrand et al. 2003; Heide-Jørgensen 
et al. 2013; Watling and Les 2007; Dodds et al. 2009).  
These regions are being placed under increasing stress due to changes in seasonal 
weather patterns and increasing sea surface temperatures (SST) associated with anthropogenic 
climate change (Hoegh-Guldberg and Bruno 2010; Lockwood et al. 2007). Over the past 25 
years these changes are proceeding at an increasingly accelerated rate, with SST rising five to six 
times faster than the global average (European Environment Agency 2008).  
It has been previously shown that increases in water temperature can influence the 
physiological and metabolism of C. finmarchicus (Pasternak et al. 2013; Kjellerup et al. 2012; 
Halcrow and K. 1963), subsequently altering their distribution, abundance and phenology in the 
wild (Kvile et al. 2014) as well as the composition of their dominance in the zooplankton 
community (Kjellerup et al. 2012). 
As of January 2015, the North Sea is the world's most active offshore drilling region with 
173 rigs drilling and despite recent global declines in production, associated oil activities are 
likely to continue well into the coming decade (Fould et al., 2015; Nakhle 2016). In addition, due 
to rapidly retreating polar ice, previously impassable routes will become a lucrative option for 
commercial shipping (Ho and Joshua 2010). Oil tanker traffic has already increased dramatically 
in the Barents Sea with this trend likely to continue in the coming decades (Huntington 2009). 
As human activities increase and encompass increasing areas, there is a greater inherent 
risk from shipping and platforms accidentally releasing polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons 
(PAHs) into the marine environment (Huntington 2009). 
The adverse effects of PAHs in copepods have been well characterised and range across 
both lethal and nonlethal consequences of exposure. These include multiple gene modifications 
involved in shell replacement, metabolism of amino acids, and the conversion and storage of 
protein and fat in addition to defence mechanisms against oxidative stress and toxicity (Hansen 
et al. 2008). Non-polar narcosis caused by interruption of membrane fluidity has also been 
implicated in the reduced motility and ultimate death (Barata et al. 2005).  
==PAGE:7==
 
 
   
 
7  
Within PAHs, pyrene is among the most abundant found in the marine environment 
following oil activities (Barata et al. 2005). Specifically, pyrene exposure has been attributed to 
signiﬁcantly reduce both the egg reproduction and ingestion of female as well lengthening time 
dependant development of female C. ﬁnmarchicus ( Jensen et al. 2008). Due to its environmental 
dominance, bioavailability and toxic profile, pyrene has been approved as a suitable candidate by 
to model the effects of PAHs (Flowers et al. 2002). 
Despite some fundamental knowledge gained about the combined effects of these 
stressors (temperature and pyrene) relatively little is known, though it is likely that all lifecycle 
stages of C. finmarchicus will be subject to the toxic effects of PAHs (Grenvaldet al. 2013).   
In our experiments field-caught and laboratory-reared (Olsen et al. 2013) C. finmarchicus 
were used. However, despite both being of the same species, a number of key differences must 
be taken into consideration. 
As the laboratory culture has been isolated for more than 50 generations, selection forces 
may have played a role in favouring distinct behavioural and phenotypic differences between 
them and wild stock.  
Due to the relatively small culture size, the bottleneck effect can limit genetic diversity 
within a given isolated population (Nei et al. 1975) as well as increasing its susceptibility of 
genetic drift (Lande and Russell 1976). Furthermore, the two populations will face a variety of 
different selection pressures, ultimately favouring survival traits best suited to their given 
environment. It has already been established that if given sufficient time, behavioural strategies 
such as loss predator avoidance and selection for the rapid growth, which are strong in laboratory 
cultures will likely emerge (Fenchel 1982). Importantly, like in other laboratory-reared copepods 
loss of diel feeding and overwintering behaviour had also been previously observed in the 
SINTEF cultures (Tiselius 1995).  
In laboratory cultures, key interactions between other organisms may also be 
significantly altered. These include differences in potential exposure to pathogens, 
commensalism or symbiosis processes present in the wild. A study by Sochard et al. (1979) 
found evidence for a unique intestinal flora in laboratory reared Acartia tonsa, although did not 
elaborate on its significance. Is it thus conceivable that maintaining C. finmarchicus in laboratory 
culture imposes special restrictions on the expression of the normal flora, potentially influencing 
biological processes. Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that after exposure to PAHs there is 
a reduction in grazing pressure on particles of smaller diameter (Berman and Heinle 1980). As 
laboratory experiments reply on in-house running cultures, the limited species available will not 
give a “real world” representation of their diet and subsequently may not give an accurate picture 
of what is observed in the wild. Our knowledge with regards to the combined effects of 
==PAGE:8==

temperature and pyrene on ecological and physiological responses of C. finmarchicus is limited. 
Due to its regional importance and the issues highlighted previously, it is thus paramount to 
understand if findings obtained via experiments with laboratory-reared copepods are 
representative for the field populations as well. Inconsistencies could not only skew our 
understanding from a scientific standpoint but also impact human activity in a region through 
influencing legislation and environmental risk assessments (Bakkeet al., 2013).    
The aim of this thesis will hence present a comparative study between laboratory-reared 
and field collected C. finmarchicus to investigate the combined physiological effects of pyrene 
and temperature to evaluate if the plasticity of the laboratory reared culture is the same as that of 
the field populations it originates from. As mortality, faecal pellet and egg production are 
accepted indicators for ingestion and secondary population dynamics, this study will use these 
parameters to compare responses (Hirche et al. 1997; Head et al. 2003; Besiktepe and Dam 
2002). 
2. Materials and methods 
2.1) Experimental design 
To test whether the effects of pyrene exposure depend on temperatures and these effects differ 
between laboratory-reared and wild collected populations, a full factorial experiment was 
conducted with 2 populations (laboratory reared and wild collected) × 3 temperatures (10, 14 and 
18 °C) × 6 exposure solutions (sea water control, acetone control, 1, 10, 100 and 100+ nM 
pyrene) (a total of 36 treatments). Each treatment had three replicate, resulting a total of 108 
experimental units. The comparison between laboratory-reared and wild collected populations 
may reveal the different sensitivity of copepods to pyrene exposure as laboratory reared 
populations have been cultured in a stable environment for more than 50 generations, thereby 
losing some important traits such as hibernating during the winter. The temperature  
10 oC was used for this study based on the temperature in the copepods culture. By 2100 the 
IPCC estimates that global warming will cause sea temperature rise by 4 oC, hence 14 and 18 oC  
was chosen to account for the predicted and extremes in summer weather (scenario RCP8.5, 
IPCC 2013).  
2.2) Laboratory reared (LR) 
Calanus finmarchicus from a continuous laboratory culture at the Norwegian University of 
Science and Technology/SINTEF SeaLab was used for the experiments. The culture is 
continuously fed a mixture of 3 microalgae (Dunaliella tertiolecta Bucher, Isochrysis galbana 
Parke and Rhodomonas baltica Karsten) and consistently kept at 10 °C in a 8:16 hour day night 
cycle (Olsen et al. 2013)

2.3) Sampling 
Females were collected for experiments using a glass bowl and individually sorted under an 
Olympus SZ40 stereoscopic microscope (×20 magnifications). Females were selected on the 
basis developed sexual maturity, sufficient lipid content and minimal damage to antenna.   
Females were transferred to white 50-L polyethlene tanks. The seawater (34.5‰) used 
for this experiment was collected from Trondheimsfjorden (Norway) at 70 meters depth and was 
sand filtered, temperature adjusted to ambient room temperature of cultured copepods 
(10 ± 0.2 °C). Acclimatization up to the following water temperatures 10, 14 and 18°C was 
carried out in stages, allowing a 24 hr resting period per 4 °C increase, respectively.  
2.4) Wild stock (WS) 
Water and copepods for the experiments were collected from Trondheim Fjord, ambient water 
temperature was registered at 7 °C. Copepods were collected in the upper 70 m using a WP2 
plankton net (200 µm mesh size) with 1-L non filtering cod end and were transferred to white 
50-L polyethlene tanks. Maximum depth, position and temperature were recorded using a CTD 
attached to the plankton net. The tanks were moved to a 10 °C temperature controlled room to 
allow for acclimatization of copepods. The same sorting and acclimatization procedure as was 
done with laboratory reared was then carried out.  
2.5) Preparation of pyrene solutions 
Four stock solutions of pyrene (purity ≥ 99 %, Sigma-Aldrich Chemie, Schnelldorf, Germany) 
with acetone (purity ≥ 99.8 %, VWR International, Poole, England) as a solvent was prepared 
in the concentrations 3 mM (stock 1), 1 mM (stock 2), 0.1 mM (stock 3) and 0.001 mM (stock 4) 
in 200 ml brown glass bottles, respectively. Each bottle was stored at room temperature and 
wrapped in aluminium foil, degradation of pyrene will occur rapidly under UV light conditions 
(Pelletier et al. 1997). The experimental concentrations: 1 nM, 10 nM, 100 nM and 100+ nM 
were obtained as shown in table 1.

2.6) Preparation of algae feed  
Our experimental copepods were fed a solution of lab cultured Rhodomonas baltica. 
Concentrations were verified daily by a particle counter to ensure that food saturation was 
present in each bottle throughout the experiment.    
2.7) Egg laying chamber  
The egg-laying chambers (n = 54, one for each treatment/replication) for the single female 
reproduction test (SFR test) were designed for semistatic/static operation. Each chamber was 
made from a 75-mm length of a 110-mm diameter polyethylene pipe, fitted with a 300 µm nylon 
square mesh stretched over the underside opening. Chambers were placed in a polypropylene 1-L 
food grade container, which served as an egg collector and water reservoir. The chambers were 
filled with approximately 600 mL of filtered seawater at the corresponding temperatures, 
allowing sufficient space for the copepod above the mesh. 
Due to the mesh size, eggs were expected to fall through and come to rest on the bottom 
of the container. The shallow water column would minimize time eggs are accessible for 
maternal predation.  A distance of 30 mm between the mesh and bottom of the container was 
allowed in order for reflux of eggs to the chamber to be minimized. Each chamber was labelled 
using the same system as their corresponding bottle to avoid cross contamination. 
Experiments were carried out over a period of period of seven days with the observations 
on laboratory reared C. finmarchicus commencing on the 4/03/16 and wild on the 15/03/16.  
==PAGE:11==
 
 
   
 
11  
One day prior to the addition of copepods, bottles were treated with their corresponding 
pyrene concentrations to avoid pyrene loss due to adhesion to the glass bottles.  
To analyse the effect of pyrene and temperature on C. finmarchicus, ten copepods were 
transferred to eighteen 1.12-L red caped glass bottles in each of the temperature controlled rooms 
(10 °C, 14 °C and 18 °C). Rhodomonas solution was pipetted into each bottle at appropriate 
saturation levels. Copepods were then exposed to six different treatments: a solvent control (no 
pyrene and acetone present) a negative control (no pyrene nor acetone) and dilution series of four 
pyrene concentrations: 1 nM, 10 nM, 100 nM, and 100+ nM. Three replicates for each treatment 
were used. 
Prior to placing on the plankton wheel, bottles were topped up with water ensuring no air 
bubbles present to minimise potential physical damage to the organisms. Furthermore, studies on 
other species of copepod suggest that small scale-turbulence can alter ingestion rates (Saiz et al. 
2003). 
Experiments were then left in darkness overnight to avoid phytotoxicity; UV-light has the 
ability to alter the structure of pyrene generating free radicals that cause disruption in the 
membrane of living cells (Duesterloh et al. 2002) 
Water, feed and pyrene were changed daily for duration of experiment to minimize the 
fluctuations of pyrene and food concentrations. All temperature controlled rooms were kept as 
dark as possible throughout the duration of the study due to the prior stated reasoning.  
2.8) Faecal pellet analysis 
After 48 hr of pyrene exposure (d3), faecal pellets were collected by pouring/rinsing the entire 
content of individual bottles into sieve with mesh size 100 µm while semi submerged in a glass 
bowl. To avoid physical damage to copepods, care was taken when pouring to minimize the 
force exerted onto C. finmarchicus when hitting the mesh net. After counting, one copepod was 
moved to a hatching chamber with the remainder transferred back to their corresponding 
bottles.   
The underside of the sieve was then rinsed several times to concentrate the pellets into a 
100 mm × 15 mm petri dish and counted using a counter and an Olympus SZ40 microscope (×20 
magnifications). Pellets were only counted if they had a minimum length of three times its width.

2.14) Statistical analysis 
To test for the effects of population origin, temperature and pyrene exposure and their interactions 
on survival, wax ester content, we ran general linear models with a normal error structure. In these 
models, population, temperature and pyrene exposure were included as fixed effects. For faecal 
pellet and egg productions, we ran repeated measures ANOVAs as both response variables were 
measured on multiple days of the exposure period. Population, temperature and pyrene exposure 
were also included as fixed factor and exposure duration was within-subject factor (repeated 
factor). The Dunnett's post hoc tests were employed for specific comparisons when needed (see 
the results). For all tests, the level of significance was set at P < 0.05  
 
 
 
3. Results 
3.1) The effect of pyrene and temperature on survival 
Survival percentage varied in laboratory reared (LR) and wild stock (WS) populations from 19-
100 and 0-100 % respectively (Fig. 1). Temperature alone showed no significant effect on 
mortality whilst pyrene was seen to negatively influence both populations (P > 0.05), however a 
difference in response was observed (P < 0.05). 
At low pyrene concentrations (1-10 nM), survival did not statistically differ to controls of 
laboratory reared copepods at all temperatures (P > 0.05), nor could and effect of pyrene be 
observed at 10 oC (P > 0.05). At 14 oC concentrations of 100+ nM was only shown to have a 
negatively influence with a reduction of 48% in survival (P < 0.05). The effect of pyrene was 
also seen at 18 oC with a reduction 80 and 70% at 100-100 +nM respectively (P < 0.05).  
In wild stock, the effect of pyrene could be seen at every temperature (P < 0.05). In 10 
and 14o C experiments this could be seen at pyrene concentration 100 +nM with a reduction in 
survival of 48 and 100% respectively (P < 0.05). At 18 oC this effect could be seen at lower 
concentrations (10-100+ nM), with a reduction in survival of 50, 63 and 100% at each 
concentration increment respectively (P < 0.05).

3.2) The effect of pyrene and temperature on wax ester weight 
Wax ester weight varied in laboratory reared and wild stock populations from 0.0002 - 0.09 and 
0.0001 - 0.056 mg respectively (Fig. 2). Increasing temperature was shown to have no influence 
on wild stock populations (P > 0.05) but was seen to increase in laboratory reared copepods at 
14°C (P < 0.05). Pyrene was demonstrated to have no effect on either population (Pyrene × 
Temperature) (P > 0.05). Wild stock did however possess significantly lower wax ester weights 
than laboratory reared populations (average: 0.009 and 0.03 mg, respectively) with this pattern 
not dependant on the combination of either stressor (Temperature × Pyrene × Population 
interaction) (P < 0.05).

4. Discussion 
4.1) Temperature effects  
In general, mortality was shown to not statistically increase with temperature in both populations 
with a survival percentage range of 80-100% (Fig. 1). This is consistent with previous 
investigations examining the influence of temperature on C. finmarchicus, which observed as little 
as of 10% mortality in controls over the whole experiment (Hirche et al. 1997). Although in our 
study increasing temperature alone was seen to have no significant effect on mortality, a 
correlation between higher temperatures and mortality rate in C. finmarchicus naupliar have been 
documented in other studies and was suggested to be a result of lower tolerance to warmer 
temperatures (Grenvald et al. 2013). 
Faecal pellet production was also shown to be negatively correlated with increasing 
temperature (Fig. 3), which is supported by previous studies in other species of copepods (Garrido 
et al. 2013; Carlotti et al. 1997; Lee et al. 2003). Conversely, other experiments have shown food 
uptake increased significantly when temperatures were raised from 0.5 to 8 °C and thus implies 
certain “ideal” ranges for each population (Hjorth et al. 2011).  
The variability in tolerance effects could be explained by the inherent intraspecific regional 
plasticity within C. finmarchicus, where populations are adapted to specific regional temperatures. 
As a consequence, different populations have evolved metabolic processes especially suited for 
specific environments (Peck et al. 2014). This is evident in the fact that this species is able to thrive 
across a broad range of temperature (-2°C to 22°C) and therefore, different populations may 
conceivably be optimised for any given environmental parameter within the species range  
(Garrido et al. 2013; Marshall et al. 1935).  
Enzyme kinetics are also greatly influenced by changes in temperature (Portner et al. 2007); 
shifting thermal optimum and/or critical minimum and maximum limits for thermal performance 
curves vary depending geographic location (Peck et al. 2014). This was also suggested by 
(Halcrow et al. 1963) who found that increasing water temperature from 10 to 20 °C showed a 
clear impairment of respiration in C. finmarchicus. As our experiments were carried out under 
food saturation, we can assume ingestion rates were dependent not by their capacity to gather food, 
but their ability to process the food consumed (Hassett and Landry 1988). On a metabolic level 
digestive enzymes are an essential link between ingestion and assimilation, factors inhibiting their 
functionality will thus reduce grazing (Freese et al. 2012). This variability was clearly 
demonstrated by Dam and Peterson (1988) who showed gut clearance rates in Calanus pacificus 
to be temperature dependence. The reduction in faecal pellet production with increasing 
temperature observed in our study (Fig. 5) is thus likely to be due to a disruption of metabolic 
process within the experimental organisms.  
==PAGE:26==
 
 
   
 
26  
Furthermore, egg production in our experiments was found to decrease with higher 
temperatures, which is in accordance with other studies (Carlotti et al. 1997; Lee et al. 2003). 
Contrary to this, field observations in North Sea have failed to show a statistically significant 
relationship between egg production of C. finmarchicus and temperature, however this may be 
explained by the relatively small temperature increments used (Head et al. 2003). As egg 
production in C. finmarchicus is also believed to be closely related to feeding activity (Swalethorp 
et al. 2011; Niehoff and Barbara 2004), we can thus assume that factors that cause a reduction in 
the assimilation efficiency of food will also influence egg laying which may account for our 
observed reduction. In addition, it has been shown that temperature can influence fecundity by 
affecting the spawning interval rather than the clutch size and could also account for the negative 
correlation of egg laying and temperature seen in both populations, especially if a difference in 
temperature optima is apparent (Hirche et al. 1997; Hirche 1990).  
 
4.2) Pyrene effects  
Pyrene was shown to have a strong influence on mortality particularly at higher 
concentrations (Fig. 1). These observations are in accordance with other pyrene exposure 
experiments on copepods (Hjorth et al. 2008; Spooner and Corkett 1979; Almeda et al. 2013; Olsen 
et al. 2013; Hansen et al. 2011; Bellas and Thor 2007). In addition, in line with our results (Fig. 
3.) a reduction in grazing as a consequence of exposure to pyrene has been documented in C. 
finmarchicus as well as other copepod species (L. K. Jensen and Carroll 2010) (Hjorth et all 2011;  
Jensen et al. 2008; Almeda et al. 2014; Hjorth et al. 2008; Spooner and Corkett 1979; Olsen et al. 
2013; Bellas and Thor 2007; Avila et al. 2010). Active uptake through feeding compared to passive 
diffusion has been implied as the most important route for the accumulation of pyrene in C. 
finmarchicus (Jensen et al. 2008; Nørregaard et al. 2014).  Thus for this study we can assume 
bioaccumulation of pyrene was caused primarily though feeding. The reduction in feeding and 
subsequent mortality seen in our experiments is likely to have been a direct effect of pyrene caused 
by non-polar narcotisation in organisms and has been suggested as the mode of action in other 
studies (Van Wezel and Opperhuizen 1995; Jensen et al. 2008; Jensen and Carroll 2010; Hjorth et 
al. 2011; Almeda et al. 2014; Hjorth et al. 2008; Spooner and Corkett 1979). Another contributing 
factor for the apparent depression of grazing could also be caused by an inhibition of the copepod 
to perceive food, possibly due to blockage of chemoreceptors by PAHs although could not be 
confirmed in our study (Blumer 1973; Atema et al. 1982).  
In regards to the observed reduction fecundity (Fig. 5), the most plausible explanation 
might be found in the link between feeding and reproduction. As pyrene was shown to drive the 
toxic mediated reduction in feeding, it is likely that a similar reduction will be mirrored in 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
   
 
27  
fecundity. Reduced fecundity as a direct consequence of lowered grazing was also suggested by 
Jensen et al. (2008) who observed no detrimental effect on egg production, when comparing the 
influence of pyrene under fed and starved conditions.   
 
4.3) Difference between lab-reared vs wild populations 
4.3.1) General difference of lab vs wild populations: survival, faecal pellet production, egg 
production, wax ester. 
In general, mortality in wild stock was greater than that of the laboratory reared population (Fig. 
2). In addition, both wax ester content, pellet production and fecundity were all lower in the former 
group.  
The clear difference in wax ester volume was likely to be a result of the loss of seasonal 
variability and continual optimal feeding conditions within laboratory reared copepods. During 
overwintering lipids are used to for metabolism and to fuel mass spawning events that occur in 
advance of the spring blooms, hence accounts for the diminished lipid content of our wild 
stock  (Mayor et al. 2009). Other factors apart from temperature and pyrene must also be taken 
into consideration as they can potentially influence or at least contribute positively or negatively 
to the variability seen.  
To date, no work has been done using the same parameters as our experiment, although in 
another comparative study, wild stock showed twice the feeding activity of laboratory reared 
copepods and yet produced only half the number of eggs (Tiselius 1995). This was partly believed 
to be attributed to a reduced reproductive potential associated with older females which were likely 
to be more abundant in the wild stock culture (Carlotti et al. 1997; Rodríguez-Graña et al. 2010) 
This factor could feasibly contribute to the differences in egg production obtained in our results in 
addition to the increased natural mortality.  
One potential explanation for the general reduction seen in wild stock could be fact that in 
their natural habitat, fecundity, ingestion rates, wax ester content and mortality vary cyclically due 
to overwintering and other diel mechanisms (Swalethorp et al. 2011; Ohman et al. 2004; Yusuf et 
al. 2008; Jónasdóttir 1999). Conceivably if our wild sample were collected at different time periods 
in the spring bloom, baseline functions of these biological process may vary and could be attributed 
to the lower observed in controls compared to laboratory reared.  
Despite being fed identical diets; it has been shown that in Calanus helgolandicus there is 
a delayed acclimation behaviour of the enzyme systems in response to changes in diet (Harris et 
al. 1986). Significantly for our experiment, Harris et al. (1986) found these optimal levels were 
only reached after 8 days, meaning the lower ingestion rates seen in wild stock could be attributed 
to a reduced ability to assimilate food. If this metabolic time lag is a factor it could explain the 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
   
 
28  
increase in grazing in wild stock seen between days 2 and 6 the experiment. Furthermore, the 
conversion of food into viable eggs has been demonstrated to be also subject to a time lag of up to 
several days in Calanus species (Kiørboe et al. 1985). If this was apparent in our experiment then 
we would expect to see changes in pellet production reflected in fecundity. 
Despite a positive gradient seen when SEP was plotted against SPP in both populations, 
laboratory reared was shown to have a stepper correlation than wild stock (Fig. 7), implying a 
stronger dependency on food to drive reproduction. This acclimatization would not be a factor in 
laboratory reared copepods as they have been continually fed a constant diet at food saturation. 
However, due to the relatively short time span of our experiment, fecundity rates may reflect 
historic feeding conditions of wild stock in their prior habitat. This could be the reason we observed 
an increase in fecundity between days 3 and 7 only in wild stock, although it may be also linked 
directly to the increased grazing mentioned prior.  
4. 3.2) The difference between laboratory and wild population in response to temperature. 
Distinct difference between laboratory reared and wild stock copepods were seen their responses 
to temperature. With regard to faecal pellet production, after two days of exposure laboratory 
reared experiments only showed a decrease at 18 °C whilst wild stock appeared more susceptible 
as a reduction was apparent at 14 °C. When pellet production was counted again 4 days later, a 
decrease was seen laboratory reared controls excluding 18 °C. Wild stock by comparison was seen 
to increase in production rates at 14 and 18 °C (Fig. 3). 
Discrepancies in their response to temperature were also apparent when comparing egg 
production rates (Fig. 5).  At 10 °C no significant difference could be seen between controls of 
either population on day 3, however at 14 and 18 °C fecundity was dramatically lowered in wild 
stock. On day 7, fecundity in wild stock controls increased to comparable levels of laboratory 
reared population.  
Significant gene-environment interactions for traits important to fitness at environmental 
temperatures have been demonstrated in marine zooplankton (Bradley and Ketzner 1982). It could 
thus be conceived that as laboratory reared copepods are consistently exposed to higher 
temperatures than wild stock, the temperature optima for this population is greater and has an 
increased resilience to higher temperatures. 
In addition, this feeding fecundity time lag discussed previously is also believed to be 
dependent on temperature; with warmer increments having extended periods (Tester and Turner 
1990). This could further explain the observation of lowered faecal pellet and egg production on 
day 2 and 3 at higher temperatures in wild stock, as it may increase the likelihood of observations 
to reflect historic periods of restricted food availability.

4.3.3) The difference between laboratory and wild population in response to pyrene exposure 
In this experiment, a difference in response to pyrene was apparent when comparing laboratory 
reared to wild stock copepods. Whilst the negative effect of pyrene on faecal pellet production and 
fecundity can be seen at lower concentrations in laboratory reared experiments, wild stock is seen 
to be particularly vulnerable at high pyrene concentrations showing increased mortality (Fig. 1).  
To our knowledge there has been no comparative studies similar ours that focused around this 
aspect of pyrene, however other experiments comparing responses to different calanoid species 
may give us insight into our observations. 
Once pyrene is introduced into copepods it is either metabolized or accumulated in lipid 
stores (Carls et al. 2006). It has been suggested that small lipid volumes promote elevated 
bioavailability of accumulated oil compounds (Hansen et al. 2009).  This removal, is believed to 
offset the main toxic effects until lipid supplies are depleted and has been suggested as the reason 
for copepod species with larger lipid content showing higher tolerance to pyrene (Lotufo 
1998; Swalethorp et al. 2011;  Jensen et al. 2008; Hjorth et al. 2011; Hansen et al. 2011). In 
addition, A. tonsa which does not accumulate and build lipid storage like the Calanus species was 
shown to be more sensitive to a range of oil dispersant chemicals when compared to either C. 
finmarchicus or C. glacialis (Hansen et al. 2014). Thus signifying that the time scale for toxic 
effect will be positively correlated to the lipid content of the organism.  
Our results show wax ester weight was significantly greater in laboratory reared copepods 
than wild stock (Fig. 2). We can thus theorise a decrease in response time to pyrene in wild stock, 
which could account the greater mortality observed. The delayed effects in laboratory reared 
copepods may however become apparent when the lipid stores are mobilized. The implications of 
this may be far greater in the wild as narcotised C. finmarchicus will likely have reduced clearance 
rates as well as an increased vulnerability to predation (Fields and Jeanette 1997).  
These observations were corroborated in a similar study by (Hansen et al. 2011)  which 
compared the same SINTEF cultured C. finmarchicus used in our study with field caught C. 
glacialis and their response to artificially weathered crude oil. Significantly, lipid content and the 
trend in their response to increasing pyrene concentrations was comparable between the two 
populations, further implying the relationship between lipid volume and offsetting PAH toxicity. 
Moreover, they also demonstrated a direct link between lipid content and mortality with lipid rich 
copepods surviving longer than lipid poor copepods exposed to identical pyrene concentrations.  
In contradiction to this, wild stock samples do however appear to be more resilient to lower 
concentrations of pyrene. As active uptake is the primary source of PAH exposure, the 
significantly higher grazing in laboratory reared copepods when compared to their wild stock 
counterparts could conceivably increase pyrene accumulation and accounts for the initial 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
   
 
30  
heightened toxic response seen. This implies that as grazing is reduced as consequence of the 
narcotic effect of pyrene, so too is the concentration of pyrene entering the organism. The 
significantly higher wax ester content in laboratory reared copepods combined with the reduced 
grazing caused by pyrene at higher concentrations could thus explain the lower lethal effects 
observed.   
Another possible explanation although only speculative, as Trondheim harbour receives 
relatively high traffic (ferries, cruise ships and military vessels) it is conceivable that previous 
generations in the wild have been exposed to some level of PAH’s. Rapid evolution in term of 
toxic tolerance has been well documented in the case of interactions between zooplankton and 
toxic algae (Colin and Dam 2005). Furthermore, a rapid loss of toxicity resistance due to 
evolutionary mechanisms has also been implied in zooplankton in response to heavy metals (Turko 
et al. 2016). If indeed prolonged historic exposure of oil is present in wild but absent in the 
laboratory setting, genetic divergence in terms of their tolerance will likely have occurred and may 
explain our observations.  
Work by Calabrese (2005) has documented a positive response to low concentrations of 
toxins in a range of organisms; causing an activation of repair systems. If a genetic disparity 
between the two populations is apparent, then we can hypothesise that mechanisms that underpin 
this are more prevalent in the wild due the high environmental variability they encounter. 
Calabrese (2005) also suggested that this compensatory mechanism is dose-time dependant, which 
could account for the increases in pellet production seen between day 2 and 6. 
4.3.4) Combined effects of pyrene and temperature. 
In our study the negative effects of pyrene seemed to be amplified when combined with increasing 
temperatures, with wild stock in particular being more sensitive to higher concentrations. 
Relatively little is known about the combined effects of pyrene and increasing temperature in 
zooplankton although in accordance with our results, a similar response in C. finmarchicus was 
observed by (Hjorth et al. 2011).  
The markedly higher toxicity of pyrene may be due by a relative increased metabolism at 
elevated temperatures (Hansen et al. 2011). Alternatively, Kennedy and Walsh (1991) 
observed that in Toadfish metabolism of benzo[a]pyrene (BaP) was shown to be temperature 
insensitive, however high temperatures produced a significantly greater accumulation of BaP triols 
and tetrols (breakdown products of highly carcinogenic BaP diol epoxides). In addition, increases 
in temperature have been implied in enhancing the formation of covalently bound adducts with 
macromolecules, potentially increasing their cytotoxicity and carcinogenic risk (Egaas et al. 1982). 
If either, or a combination of these temperature induced factors is apparent in our study, enhanced 
toxicity from pyrene and may account increase the associated narcotic responses observed.  
==PAGE:31==
 
 
   
 
31  
Furthermore, the lower wax ester content of wild stock, could mean that toxic effects of 
pyrene are induced quicker. A similar explanation was also given by (Hjorth et al. 2011) in regards 
to the apparent lack of effect of combined temperature and pyrene on the more lipid abundant 
copepod species C. glacialis. When lipid volume was comparable between species as was seen in 
Hansen et al. (2011) experiment, C. glacialis appeared to respond slower and with a lower intensity 
for the acute toxicity data. However, this was explained by reduced uptake rates of oil compounds 
at lower temperatures. 
 
4.4) Conclusion 
From this study it is clear that there is a distinct difference in the response of laboratory reared 
copepods and wild stock populations when exposed to increasing temperatures and pyrene 
concentrations. 
As biological rates and responses in terms of feeding, fecundity wax ester content and 
mortality vary seasonally (Swalethorp et al. 2011), baseline levels and their vulnerability to 
PAH’s and temperature may also vary depending on time of year. As our laboratory reared 
copepods have lost most of these rhythmic characteristics in may be inappropriate to use a “one 
size fits all” approach when extrapolating results to the wild.  
If laboratory reared C. finmarchicus are used as experimental organisms to represent 
responses in the wild, their increased feeding behaviour may cause an overestimation of the sub 
lethal consequences of exposure to pyrene. However due to their significantly higher wax ester 
content, lethal effects may be greatly underestimated particularly at higher pyrene 
concentrations. The toxic effect is further compounded via increasing temperature although 
laboratory reared copepods appear more resilient, possibly due to adaptation to higher 
temperatures.   
 This miscalculation could conceivably have devastating consequence for the next 
generation and subsequent higher trophic levels whose survival relies upon them if that data is 
used as an estimated response to oil spills and increasing temperature in impact assessments or 
legislation. It is thus paramount that future laboratory experiments in these regards, take into 
consideration the district plastic biological differences when evaluating the true environmental 
consequences of stressors.
