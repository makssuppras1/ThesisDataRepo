Abstract
Access to healthcare is considered a fundamental human right. Hospital layout design
can have signiﬁcant eﬀects on the eﬃciency, the workload on staﬀ, and patient well-
being. This thesis considers the Hospital Layout Problem (HLP) for the in-patient
area in the hospital in Esbjerg, Denmark. Subjective considerations guided the deci-
sions on the current layout of a new in-patient building where staﬀpreferences played
a signiﬁcant role. The objective is to suggest layout alternatives, which involves lo-
cating 17 specialisms in 12 wards and evaluate them based on performance indicators.
A two-phase solution framework was suggested which integrates mathematical mod-
elling and simulation, and the emphasis is put on managing the real-world aspects of
the hospital environment. Facility layout problems are generally formulated as the
Quadratic Assignment Problem (QAP) since it considers inter-departmental relation-
ships. A mathematical model based on the QAP was formulated with a stochastic
variant since the number of patients is not deterministic. An iterative improvement
heuristic, ﬁx-and-optimise, was suggested to solve the problem since it has proven
to be NP-hard. The multiple objective optimisation problem with diﬀerent weights
resulted in nine layout alternatives. Discrete-event simulation (DES) models have
successfully been applied as a tool for hospital layout design.
A DES model was
designed to analyse the system behaviour for each alternative in a real-world set-
ting. Multiple random runs were performed, and the performance of each layout was
analysed in regards to performance indicators. The results showed that the hospital
is experiencing a bed shortage, and no suggestion is robust to a potential patient
increase. Peak periods result in undesirable patient transfers between wards, and
no layout suggestion mitigates this problem entirely. Assumptions had to be made,
which limits the viability of the results for Esbjerg hospital. However, the results
showed that the performance metrics that deﬁne the eﬃciency and eﬀectiveness of
proposed layouts could be improved by using the suggested approach. The framework
considers multiple complex aspects of the HLP and could be improved and extended
further.

Learning Objectives
• Identify and reﬂect on academic literature related to QAP for facility layout
planning. Situate the problem under consideration within the available litera-
ture.
• Reﬂect and discuss how the level of applicability in the practice of the present
work on QAPs for facility layout design.
• Explain how simulation can be used for performance tuning within an optimi-
sation framework and comment on the experience from the master thesis
• Analyse the available data set and generate sets of scenarios to be used in the
optimisation.
• Formulate a mathematical model of the problem and solve a stochastic variant
in which patient arrivals are not deterministic.
• Propose techniques to reduce running times.
These could be, for example,
aggregation techniques, heuristic methods or both.
• Build a simulation tool to model the arrival of patients by specialism at the
hospital.
• Assess the performance of the proposed approach through statistical testing.
Emphasis should be given to the scalability of the approach.
• Critically evaluate the results. Where possible, compare the results obtained
with the hospital’s current solution.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The enjoyment of the highest
attainable standard of health is
one of the fundamental rights of
every human being without
distinction of race, religion,
political belief, economic or social
condition.
Constitution of the World Health
Organization [1]
Health is a fundamental human right [1].
Hospitals come with high initial costs
and running expenses, but they are investments that pay oﬀand have a positive
impact on economic performance [2]. The underlying principle in Denmark’s Health
Law is universal access to health care [3]. The Danish health care sector is ﬁnanced
by local and state taxes and is dominated by the public sector. At the same time,
the hospitals are generally owned and operated by the ﬁve regions [3]. The total
expenditure on health as a percentage of gross domestic product (GDP) in Denmark
has been increasing since 2000 and was 10,5% in 2018 while other OECD countries
had the average of 8,8% [4].
Hospital layout design can have signiﬁcant eﬀects on the eﬃciency, workload on em-
ployees, and patient well-being. It is a complex task where qualitative factors and
competing objectives can limit the ability to obtain optimal solutions [5]. Health-
care providers might be interested in eﬃcient, low-cost decision-making processes,
but there are pitfalls to a blind reliance on data-driven decisions [6]. Unsuccessful
hospital layout design can have long-term consequences where the lives of patients
are at stake [7].
One of the chronic problems in many of the industrialised world’s health care systems
is bed shortages. While high bed occupancy rates are an indicator of high productivity
in hospitals, it also correlates to reduced patient comfort and privacy. Hospitals with
occupancy rates over 85 percent are generally considered to experience bed shortage.
High occupancy rates are associated with 9 percent higher mortality of in-patients,

compared to hospitals with low bed occupancy. A solution that mitigates this problem
is considered valuable for society [8]. Patient transfers due to overﬂow in hospitals
have also been proven to be a safety threat and a cause of patient dissatisfaction [9].
Unnecessary overﬂow and patient transfers can be due to poor facility layout planning
and can have serious consequences.
This thesis focuses on the Hospital Layout Problem for the in-patient area at the
Hospital in Esbjerg, Denmark. A new building was taken into use in 2019 to mitigate
bed shortages. The current arrangement of specialisms was determined based on the
hospital staﬀ’s preferences without the help of optimisation tools [10]. It is known to
lead to problems in the future, according to Hamrock, Paige, Parks, et al. [11]:
Frequently, healthcare decision makers use subjective information from
frontline staﬀ, providers, and other stakeholders to make strategic im-
provement decisions. Change attempts–whether to structures (e.g. change
in ﬂoor plan or layout) or to processes-may prove costly in terms of time
and capital.
Solely subjective considerations in layout design can lead to re-organisations later
when preferences and criteria change. Making decisions that are supported by opti-
misation tools where improvement strategies are developed, assumptions are tested,
and potential outcomes of decisions are observed before implementation provides a
low-risk, lower-cost methods [11].
Esbjerg Hospital is interested in revised layout
suggestions where the aim is to improve the current layout based on diﬀerent perfor-
mance metrics. These include reducing patient transfers between wards, dispersing
the workload, while preferably suﬃcing preferences of the hospital employees. This
is a multi-objective problem where the objectives can be conﬂicting.
The scope of this thesis is to propose layout alternatives for the in-patient area at
Esbjerg Hospital and evaluate them based on the performance indicators. The facility
layout problem deals with assigning 17 specialisms in 12 wards. These wards are
mainly in the new building, and in the old in-patient building where two wards are
still utilised. The aim is to develop a solution framework where both quantitative and
qualitative factors are collected, analysed and integrated. Due to conﬁdential reasons,
the current layout is not known and can unfortunately not be used as a benchmark
for the proposed solutions.
Tools used to formulate and solve the problem are Operation Research (OR) tech-
niques, including mathematical modelling and simulation. Both methods have been
applied in the literature of Hospital Layout Problems. A two-phase framework is
suggested where both techniques are integrated.
It is considered an eﬃcient ap-
proach where the emphasis is on the real-world aspects of the hospital environment.
The mathematical formulation of the problem is based on the Quadratic Assignment

Problem (QAP) where inter-departmental relationships between specialisms, patient
transfers between wards, and overﬂow are considered. Since the number of patients
is not deterministic, a stochastic variant is introduced to obtain solutions that are ex-
pected to be good on average. A heuristic procedure is suggested to generate multiple
solutions representing diﬀerent layout alternatives by putting weights on conﬂicting
objectives. A discrete-event simulation model is built that enables to experiment the
performance of diﬀerent suggestions before implementation. Output analysis of the
simulation model provides insights on how diﬀerent layout suggestions perform in
a real-world setting and hopefully improve the current layout. The results of this
project should give Esbjerg Hospital in-depth insights on the performance and eﬃ-
ciency for diﬀerent layout suggestions. Furthermore, the thesis provides a scalable
solution approach that could systematically support similar Hospital Layout Planning
processes.
The thesis follows a topological order. In Chapter 2, the problem is situated within
the available literature where the emphasis is on the solution methods and the appli-
cability for Facility Layout Planning in healthcare. Chapter 3 discusses the solution
methods chosen based on the literature study, and the process implemented from data

Literature Review
A Facility Layout Problem (FLP) is where the physical structure of a system is deﬁned.
It is a general class of well-studied optimisation problem that deals with determining
a layout with the most eﬃcient arrangement of facilities in available locations [12].
The most eﬃcient arrangement is when the total expected cost of inter-departmental
ﬂows inside a facility is minimised [13]. Adequate facility planning has proven to
increase eﬃciency, enhance productivity and reduce material handling cost up to
30% annually [14].
Rosenblatt [15] discussed two approaches in the literature of computerised layout
problems. In a quantitative approach, the ﬂow cost between departments is minimised.
This problem assumes that the historical pattern of ﬂow is ﬁxed and not dependant
on the layout. It may not be true since the workﬂow may change when distances
change. In the qualitative approach, the ﬁnal layout depends on a subjective closeness
rating. The major problem with this approach is that the closeness rating does not
incorporate the workﬂow cost [15]. The FLP where the objective is to optimise only
one factor, which is quantitative, is called single-objective FLP. In the real scenario
where layouts have both qualitative and quantitative objectives, they are classiﬁed as
multi-objective FLP’s [14].
A Hospital Layout Problem (HLP) is often referred to as a multi-objective problem
with conﬂicting objectives [6]. Benitez, Silveira, and Fogliatto [5] identiﬁed the most
common factors involved in HLP’s. While the typical layout planning problem in
industry considers the ﬂow of material, healthcare facilities prioritise the ﬂow of
people, that is, patients, families and staﬀ. These studies usually involve professional
users in the data collection and the layout alternative selection phase. Inﬂuential
factors in complex healthcare environments such as ﬂow and safety of patients and
staﬀ, among others, can limit the ability to obtain optimal solutions [5].
It is diﬃcult to quantify the cost of people’s lives, health and feeling, which puts
extra emphasis on the qualitative factor compared to manufacturing organisations.
In a patient safety and quality handbook for nurses [9], hospital design elements were
identiﬁed as critical in ensuring patient safety and quality care. It was noted that
when it comes to eﬃciency, room layouts and the location of supplies and medical
equipment should be standardised. Also, to minimise potential safety threats and to
==PAGE:22==
6

improve patient satisfaction, patient transfers should be minimised [9].
Research on HLP and the impact of facility design on the quality and safety of patients
has grown in recent years. Various tools and techniques have been used to solve layout
problems in healthcare, see [5] and [6] for a comprehensive overview. Two techniques
that are frequently seen in the literature on HLP’s are mathematical programming
and simulation. The applicability of these methods is discussed in Section 2.1.

FLP has generally been formulated as a Quadratic Assignment Problem (QAP), in-
troduced by Koopmans and Beckmann [16] in 1957. The problem generally considers
n! possible permutations having a set of n possible sites and n facilities where one
facility is located at each site. The optimal solution is the best permutation when
minimising the interaction cost. Let di,j be the distance between sites i and j, and
ci,j be the interaction weight between facilities i and j. This original formulation is
based on deﬁning n2 binary variables xi,j = 1 if facility i is located at site j and
xi,j = 0 otherwise [17]. QAP is a restricted version of FLP where the dimensions of
departments are all equal and ﬁxed, and the set of possible location is ﬁxed [13]. The
problem is generally formulated as follows:

The QAP is complicated from a mathematical
standpoint since it is both integer and non-linear. It has been proven to be NP-hard
[18], meaning that there is no known algorithm for solving it in polynomial time and
even small instances may require long computation time.
There have been substantial improvements in the development of exact algorithms for
the QAP where all possible solutions are generated and evaluated. However, it has
only been applicable for small instances. Burkard, Çela, Pardalos, et al. [19] discussed

that no exact algorithms could solve traditional QAP problems of size n > 20 in
a reasonable computational time.
Therefore, much research has been devoted to
the development of heuristic approaches that provide good solutions in a reasonable
time. Heuristic algorithms for solving the QAP can be classiﬁed as construction- or
improvement-type algorithms [12]. Improvement methods belong to the class of local
search algorithms. A local search procedure starts with an initial feasible solution,
and iteratively tries to improve the current solution. Construction type algorithms
are iterative approaches, usually starting with an empty permutation, and iteratively
complete a partial permutation into a solution by assigning facility which has not
yet been assigned to some free location [19]. The quality of solutions produced by
the construction method has not proven to be satisfactory.
Meta-heuristics such
as Simulated Annealing, Genetic Algorithms and ant colony are frequently used to
approximate the solution of large problems [12].
The QAP is applied in the context of location theory in several papers; for example,
Dickey and Hopkins [20] generated arrangement patterns for campus planning. They
developed a computationally fast algorithm that proved eﬃcient and gave solutions
close to optimal. In the context of hospital layout planning, Elshafei [21] conducted
a study aiming to improve the hospital outpatient department layout leading to a
reduction in the total distance travelled by patients. The problem was to assign 19
facilities to locations, and an improvement heuristic algorithm was designed, which
found a good initial solution and iteratively improved it until some stopping conditions
were met.
Some extensions of the QAP have been introduced which broaden the range of appli-
cability of the formulation. A Generalised Quadratic Assignment Problem (GQAP)
was introduced by Lee and Ma [22] in 2005. This generalisation is applicable when
multiple facilities are to be assigned to a single site as long as resources permit. They
showed that the GQAP is at least as complex as the QAP when solving an instance
grouping m facilities to n sites and allowing for empty sites. Matousek, Popela, and
Kudela [23] presented a QAP involving stochastic parameters (StoQAP) where at
least one of distance or ﬂow matrices is random. A so-called here-and-now approach
was used where decision-makers make decisions before the realisation of uncertainty
is known. Reformulations for expected value and expected objective are considered,
but the choice of expected value was motivated to obtain a solution that is good
on average. It is known that deterministic models can make it challenging to model
variable combinations of values to see the eﬀects of diﬀerent scenarios [24]. However,
there is a considerable lack of research focused on StoQAP, which is likely because it
adds even more complexity to this already NP-hard problem.
Jamali, Leung, and Verderber [6] discussed that despite the fact that QAP is generally
used as a FLP modelling technique, it could be debated when applied to HLP in terms
of ethical ramiﬁcations. As a result, this subject has not received as much attention
as traditional manufacturing facilities.
While industrial engineers involved in OR

continue to develop modelling techniques, their studies may often be distant from
human stakeholders. After all, the most important stakeholder is the patient [6].
Ahmad, Basir, Hassanein, et al. [25] also highlighted that there are signiﬁcant limita-
tions of existing mathematical formulations of layout design problems, which makes
them incompatible with most real-world applications. Gass and Harris [26] discussed
these drawbacks. Firstly, it does not allow for diﬀerent shapes of modules in the

Butler, Karwan, Sweigart, et al. [27] showed a multi-objective formulation where
units could be split and located on diﬀerent ﬂoors of the hospital. Helber, Böhme,
Oucherif, et al. [28] showed a hierarchical approach where spatial requirements and
other real-world aspects were considered in HLP for a large maximum-care hospital.
They developed an iterative improvement heuristic, ﬁx-and-optimise, to solve the

instances [29] showing that most problems gave solutions less than 10% worse than
optimal or best-known solutions, even for large instances of size n > 100 [28].
Jamali, Leung, and Verderber [6] recently gave an overview of HLP studies based on
inputs, outputs and solution methods. The most common inputs are traﬃc frequency,
travel length, overall area/boundary and facility areas. The most frequent objectives
are minimising area, maximising closeness rating and minimising travel cost. When
it comes to solution methods, the heuristics that seem to be popular in this ﬁeld are
improvement methods [6].
Based on the literature study, there has not much attention been paid to mathemat-
ical formulations of HLP, and research is very limited opposed to traditional FLP.
Most formulations found in the literature are based on deterministic data, while the
eﬀect of stochastic inputs such as patient arrivals and recovery times on the solutions
is an under-researched topic. Benitez, Silveira, and Fogliatto [5] found that HLP is
an emerging topic, which showed signiﬁcant development in the last ten years. They
highlighted the most common themes and approaches in the literature and discovered
research gaps. One suggestion is that patient safety could be improved by incorpo-
rating the risk of transportation of patients in the FLP methods [5].

2.1.2
Simulation
The literature on simulation modelling in the context of FLP is usually more general
than when mathematical formulations are presented. Speciﬁc models are usually not
presented, and the processing logic that the simulation follows are seldom published.
Normally, the purpose of the simulation experiment is stated, what program is used,
the method utilised and what is measured. This section will, therefore, ﬁnd relevant
papers where simulation has been applied for HLP’s and discuss the increase in pop-
ularity of simulation as a solution method. Also, papers are cited that aﬃrm the
advantages simulation provides when dealing with problems that involve uncertain
inputs.
Simulation provides the ability to experiment with new designs and enable low-cost
experimentation before implementation [30]. It is a way to solve real-world problems
before executing them.
It can model an entire system and its complex interrela-
tionship with the ﬂexibility to adjust the model and capture the processes required.
Questions are answered about the future behaviour of a system by executing diﬀerent
modelling scenarios, observing the consequences of the decisions and optimising based
on performance metrics [31].
Simulation has proven to be the most popular tool when analysing unit capacity and
many other hospital management concerns, since it can model the stochastic nature
in a hospital setting [27]. There are several simulation methods frequently applied
in the healthcare industry; discrete-event simulation (DES), system dynamics (SD)
and agent-based modelling (AB). DES is used when there is a ﬂow of entities that
enter the system, move through it and exit [32]. DES is primarily an operational
tool, designed for optimisation of system performance at a highly detailed level, and
modelling queuing systems where stochastic variability is an important feature [11],
[33]. SD models are suitable for analysing healthcare policy decisions, and AB models
are useful for a large variety of decisions, but particularly those requiring individual
population-level analysis [34].
DES popularity has been increasing steadily since the introduction of the method.
The ability to support various decisions and to optimise processes in a system are the
main reasons for this increase in popularity [11]. Mustafee, Katsaliaki, and Taylor
[35] published a paper in 2010, stating that there had been a spike in papers related to
DES in healthcare. They found 40 papers using DES in healthcare published between
1970 and 2007. These papers had a variety of focal points; some focused on economic
models where the cost of providing healthcare was evaluated and others on policy
and strategy evaluation. Researches more related to this thesis focused on planning,
re-organising and management of healthcare service models to assess the performance
of the system. The popularity has increased signiﬁcantly in the last two decades due
to increasing computational power and more variety of solutions [35]. Another study
by Thorwarth and Arisha [36] from 2009 supports this statement. They noted that

bed management practices and are capable of capturing increases in admissions and
suggesting changes to the structures, ﬂoor plan or layout [34].
DES modelling is
especially useful when there is a need to incorporate randomness in demand as there
is a relationship between ﬂuctuating demand and available bed capacity in hospitals
[11].

The concept of modelling a DES model involves inputs, resources and outputs. Stan-
dard inputs to the models are entities, resources, locations, arrival rates, service times,
and processing logic. There are numerous examples of entities in hospitals, but pa-
tients are the most common entities in healthcare applications.
Labels are often
assigned to entities to diﬀerentiate their characteristics and may change, based on
logic, as the entities ﬂow through the system [11]. Individual heterogeneity is where
each entity can be stochastically assigned with unique label properties at the begin-
ning of a simulation. This advantage provides the opportunity to solve the problem
and account for variability in arrivals and LOS of patients [38]. Resources, which
can be rooms, equipment or people, process entities through the system in a hospital
environment. Locations are the placements of areas where the entities are processed.
These locations are where the actions take place, and processing logic are the rules for
how entities ﬂow through the system and how resources and entities interact. Arrival
rates deﬁne at what rate entities arrive into the system, for example, the arrivals of
patients to a hospital. The service time is the time needed for patients to be processed
at a set location [11].
Simulation models are only a representation of the real world. If a simulation model
runs smoothly, it is a good indicator that it will run smoothly in the real world.
However, one should be aware that it is in a controlled environment where nothing
improbable happens.
In addition to the literature research, a notable limitation is a potential for publication
bias. Studies showing signiﬁcant, positive, results are more likely to be published than
studies showing non-signiﬁcant or "negative" results. As a result, it is harder to ﬁnd
papers that suggest negative eﬀects than positive eﬀects.

3.2
Mathematical Modelling
The conceptual starting point of modelling the problem was the QAP. The tradi-
tional formulation is not compatible with the various real-world aspects of the HLP,
as discussed in Section 2.1.1. The problem was formulated based on the QAP, or as
a Mixed Integer Quadratic Program (MIQP), as it considers interdepartmental rela-
tionships, but the constraints are linear. The assignment process aims to minimise
the distances between functions that require proximity. Emphasis was on real-world
aspects of the hospital environment, and multiple variables and constraints were in-
troduced to consider the fundamental requirements of the problem. The formulation
suggested can allocate specialisms more ﬂexibly than the general QAP, allowing large
specialisms to be divided over more than one location. It can handle diﬀerent area
requirements, represented by the number of beds required for each specialism. This
parameter is not deterministic since the number of beds required can vary signiﬁcantly.
Therefore, the problem was modelled as a stochastic generalisation of the QAP. The
formulation has a stochastic variant outside the quadratic term, diﬀerent from the
StoQAP discussed in literature where either the distance or ﬂow matrix is stochastic.
It is diﬀerent from the approaches found in the literature study, where formulations
of HLP’s were based on deterministic inputs. The number of wards is less than the
number of specialisms which makes this a GQAP where empty sites are not allowed,
and capacity is measured in the number of beds available in each ward. The multiple
objective optimisation problem considers the traditional qualitative QAP objective,
patient transfers and overﬂow. The optimisation is based on a here-and-now approach
where the solution should be good on average when considering the various realisation
of the number of patients.
The mathematical formulation of the problem is even more complex than the NP-hard
QAP. The aim was to design a solution algorithm with the ability to produce good
solutions with reasonable computational requirements.
An iterative improvement
algorithm, ﬁx-and-optimise, was developed to solve the problem. It is a combination
of mixed-integer programming and heuristics that takes advantage of the signiﬁcant
improvements on MIP solvers that have happened over the last decade. It iteratively
decomposes the problem into smaller sub-problems exploring a part of the solution
space that can be solved in polynomial time. This matheuristic has been successfully
applied to a HLP, as discussed in Section 2.1.1. The heuristic uses problem-speciﬁc
knowledge, and the framework is easily extendable due to the ﬂexibility of modelling
with MIP’s. It is a valuable feature since new constraints and objectives can be added
without the need to alter the algorithm itself. The matheuristic will, at the same time,
beneﬁt from future improvements of MIP solvers [40].
Julia was used as a programming language in the mathematical modelling part of the
project, and Gurobi was used as a solver to solve the sub-problems using a branch-
and-cut approach. The algorithm was tested on established test instances of the QAP
as a benchmark. Several layout alternatives were suggested after implementing the

ber of scenarios grows, making scenario reduction operations necessary [46]. Scenario
reduction techniques aggregate scenarios that have similarity based on distance met-
rics. Much research has been dedicated to scenario reduction techniques where the
goal is to reduce the number of scenarios while retaining the analytical value [45].
Dvorkin, Wang, Pandzic, et al. [47] compared diﬀerent scenario reduction techniques
for stochastic formulation based on Monte Carlo simulations. They show that some
methods are not as adaptable to worst-case realisations of uncertainty than others.
Clustering is a process of partitioning data into useful clusters which have analytical
value. K-mean and K-medoid are two popular and widely used partitioning algo-
rithms that can be used to divide a set of scenarios into a given number of clusters.
The fundamental diﬀerence between them is that K-means attempts to minimise the
total squared error, while K-medoids minimises the sum of dissimilarities between the
points within a cluster. It makes K-medoids more robust for noises and outliers in
the data. K-means uses an artiﬁcial scenario as a centroid, while K-medoids chooses
data points as centers. The original scenario with the lowest probability distance
from the centroid is used to represent the K-mean clusters [47], [48].
The performance of both algorithms depends on the predeﬁned value of K. Several
approaches have been proposed to determine the optimal number of clusters, K. The
oldest and most practical one is the Elbow Method. It is a visual heuristic method
based on the idea of choosing a certain number of clusters, so adding another cluster
does not give much better modelling of the data [49]. The idea is to identify for which
K the error does not decrease signiﬁcantly between values. Figure 4.7 shows the
error when clustering the 1.000 scenarios generated. It can be seen that if choosing
K > 5 then the clustering does not improve signiﬁcantly. It is also considered to
be a reasonable size to include in the mathematical model without aﬀecting the
performance signiﬁcantly regarding computational hardness.

the time between the arrival of a patient to the next one. The ﬁrst studies on in-

punctual and the arrival process was, therefore, deterministic [50].

and a notable diﬀerence is between medical and surgical specialisms, as seen in Fig-

signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the mean arrival rate. Also, naturally, there are more arrivals
during the day than during the night. It supports a hypothesis stated by Hamrock,
Paige, Parks, et al. [11] that the interarrival rates diﬀer over time.

4.7
Length of Stay
Length of Stay (LOS) in hospitals plays an important part in the eﬃciency of hospital
management [51]. It refers to the duration of a single episode of hospitalisation for
a patient. LOS is challenging to estimate since it is both aﬀected by the hospital’s
service and other complex factors related to individual patients [52]. LOS involves
signiﬁcant uncertainty, though patients are undergoing the same type of surgical
interventions [51]. The LOS determines the service time of patients in simulation
models.
It is a signiﬁcant factor when measuring the utilisation of beds and the
ability of the hospital to meet the demands of society. The longer patients stay, it
increases the hospital utilisation and decreases turnover rate [11].
According to Ickowicz and Sparks [53], LOS is known to be positively skewed, multi-
modal, contain outliers and vary between specialisms. Due to the frequent outliers, it
is diﬃcult to ﬁt a certain distribution to the LOS. This heterogeneity of LOS poses a
problem for statistical analysis since it limits the use of inference techniques based on
normality assumptions. Each specialism needs to be analysed speciﬁcally to account
for skewness [53].
Multiple distributions have been associated with LOS of patients in hospitals. Fit-
ting distribution to LOS in hospitals is complex, and several assumptions have been
made. In 1998 the adequacy of Lognormal, Weibull and Gamma models were studied
concerning LOS. Piece-wise exponential models were considered in 2002 and 2007.
In 2014, Coxian phase-type models, mixtures of Exponential distributions, and other
heavy-tailed distributions such as Weibull, Pareto, generalised Pareto and Lognormal
were analysed. In recent years, practitioners have used several software platforms to
analyse LOS data sets and ﬁt them to theoretical distributions. Multiple ﬁts have
been tested such as Pareto, Lognormal, Weibull, Erlang, Burr, Exponential, Gamma
and Lomax distributions but not one distribution returns the best ﬁt. Recent studies
have, however, shown that transforming distributions such as Gamma and Cauchy
with Pareto and Beta distributions have been performing relatively well [11], [52].
Earlier paragraphs support the hypothesis that it is challenging to ﬁt LOS to dis-
tributions. The 17 specialisms considered here have diﬀerent interrelated LOS with
varying parameters and distributions. It is conﬁrmed by Figure 4.9, which shows
the average LOS in days for all specialisms. It can be seen that specialism 13 has
signiﬁcantly higher expected LOS than specialism 1 with a spread of approximately
seven days.

Performance Indicators
The traditional QAP objective function minimises the distance between specialisms
that are preferably located together.
There are, however, other criteria that the
hospital considers critical in the layout decision. The focus should be on performance
measures that are the most important to the system decision-makers and tied directly
to the study objectives [31].
Bed shortages have been a concern at Esbjerg Hospital where the emphasis is on
patient comfort and privacy. Since the current location of specialisms is unknown,
it is not easy to propose a layout without having a numerical comparison with the
==PAGE:49==

33
current practice. Figure 4.1 shows that only one specialism is located in more than
one ward. Figure 4.2 shows that it is likely that more specialisms need to be divided
to prevent patient transfers. In a proposed layout, specialisms should be assigned in
such a way that patients are rarely transferred between wards. If patients are not
able to secure a bed in a proper ward, they are transferred to another ward where
there is an available bed, preferably as close to the assigned ward as possible. There
can, however, be a trade-oﬀbetween meeting the preferences listed in Section 4.4 and
avoiding transferring patients from their assigned location due to overﬂow in wards.
A layout proposal where the preferences are a priority could induce multiple patient
transfers. The hospital is experiencing an increase in patients which makes robustness
also a critical performance measure. Another signiﬁcant factor is to evenly spread

Formulation
The mathematical formulation of the HLP for Esbjerg hospital is presented in this
chapter, and a solution algorithm is suggested.
The mathematical formulation is
based on a stochastic generalisation of the QAP, as mentioned in Section 3.2.
It
was also stated that StoQAP generally has stochastic distance or ﬂow matrix. The
formulation proposed here has a stochastic parameter which describes the bed ca-
pacity required by each specialism. Diﬀerent from the general QAP, the formulation
incorporates ﬂexible capacity considerations where the feasibility of all scenarios in
ensured by using slack variables. The ﬁrst-stage decisions determine in which ward or
wards each specialism is located, and the fraction of patients allocated to each ward.
The model allocates specialisms to wards without determining the exact location of
specialisms in each ward. This means that if two or more specialisms are located in
a ward, they are not assigned to a ﬁxed location within the ward. The second-stage
variables determine patient transfers and overﬂow induced by the layout deﬁned by
the ﬁrst-stage variables.
The model is based on the qualitative and quantitative requirements of the project
described in Chapter 4. It is formulated with scalability in mind to make it adjustable
for other instances of HLP. The notation, constraints and objective functions are
presented in Sections 5.1-5.3.
The complete mathematical model can be seen in
Appendix E. Moreover, in Section 5.4, a heuristic algorithm will be suggested to ﬁnd
a near-optimal solution to this computationally challenging problem in a reasonable
time.

5.4
Fix-and-Optimise Heuristic
The QAP has proven to be NP-hard, even for small instances, as discussed in Sec-
tion 2.1.1. The formulation stated earlier increases the complexity even further by
introducing additional variables and constraints necessary to model the problem re-
quirements. Moreover, multi-objective problems are known to be harder to solve than
single-objective problems.
Various heuristic approaches have been suggested for the typical QAP. Here, an it-
erative improvement heuristic, ﬁx-and-optimise, is proposed to ﬁnd a near-optimal
solution, see pseudo-code in Algorithm 1. This algorithm is similar to restrict-and-
relax and relax-and-ﬁx heuristics.
Restrict-and-relax is a branch-and-bound algo-
rithm which explores the solution space by ﬁxing variables and also freeing previ-
ously ﬁxed variables. It requires the solver to be capable of starting from a restricted
problem and to ﬁx and unﬁx variables at selected nodes in the search tree. This
functionality is not available in Gurobi or CPLEX [57]. Fix-and-relax is where the
variables are separated into subsets assuming that one subset is more important than
the other, relaxing the less important subset. It then ﬁxes the important subset to
their incumbent value and solves again forcing all variables to be integer values [58].
Both these heuristics are not suitable here since restrict-and-relax is not available
in Gurobi and ﬁx-and-relax assumes that some specialisms are more important than
others. That would contradict the ethical code of healthcare that some patient lives
are more important than others. Therefore, ﬁx-and-optimise was chosen because of
its neutrality and simplicity.
Fix-and-optimise is a problem-oriented method where problem aspects are carried
into the solution procedure [59]. It is a matheuristic, which combines mathematical
programming and heuristic methods [60]. Similar to ﬁx-and-relax, it deﬁnes a subset
of variables. In each iteration, the MIP solver is used to explore a large neighbourhood
deﬁned by the subset.
It results in a new MIP, and the solver is then used as a
black-box to search for improved solutions [40].
One of the essential ingredients
of improvement methods is the order in which the neighbourhood of the solution
is scanned, which can be ﬁxed previously or chosen at random. Also, a rule for the
update of the incumbent solution should be chosen [19]. Here, the neighbourhoods will
be explored through basic variable neighbourhood descent (VND) approach, which is
a descent, ﬁrst improvement method with randomisation [61].

Designing a heuristic algorithm for the QAP is not an easy task.
Much research
has been devoted to the development of these solution approaches, as mentioned in
Section 2.1.1. The aim here is to design a good heuristic for the HLP based on the
computationally complex QAP. Despite the simplicity, it seems to provide acceptable
performance from a practical perspective for various sized problems. Reaching the
global minimum in the stochastic formulation of this problem does not necessarily
mean that it is the best performing layout when all problem objectives discussed

Before the model building phase, it is essential to list all assumption and document

consultation with the hospital representative. The following assumptions are applied
to simplify the model building and to make the DES model more realistic.
• It takes the model one month to reach a steady state.
– The hospital should be in a normal state before measurements start. It makes
the results in the output analysis more realistic.
• Patients with LOS longer than two months are removed.
– Patients staying more than two months are outliers, according to Range [10]. It
is a possibility due to the long-tailed distributions, but not viable. The outliers
that reserve beds for a long time would impact the performance.
• The walking speed of entities is set to one meter per second.
– This is estimated to be a natural walking pace. The walking pace impacts the
throughput time of the system.
• All patients in the new building use an elevator. The process of using an eleva-
tor follows a triangular distribution, TRIA(4, 6, 8) minutes. The capacity of the
elevators is 3.
– Elevator times and capacity can induce queues before the elevators and reduce
throughput time.
• No patients have priority if all beds are full, and the patients exit the queue ac-
cording to the ﬁrst-in-ﬁrst-out (FIFO) principle.
– This principle ensures that no patients are prioritised to receive a bed.
• The room preparation process follows triangular distribution, TRIA(5, 7, 9) min-
utes.
– This is an estimate from Range [10]. The room preparation is dependant on the
nurse capacity and aﬀects the throughput time.
• Nurses only serve one ﬂoor where they are based.
– The nurse capacity on each ﬂoor can aﬀect the throughput time [10].
• If there are no available beds in the hospital, the patient waits in a queue.

6.2.3
Patient Admittance
The ﬁrst step in the patient admittance is where a nurse guides the patient through a
Seize block to the in-patient building from surgery or consultation. Next, a decision is
made whether the patient goes to the new building or the old building. That decision
is made in the SelectOutput block where the label from the Source block determines
the result. Based on the SelectOutput results, the patient either moves towards the
elevator to enter the new building or the old building through the basement using a
MoveTo block. For simplicity reasons, the next block is a Release where the nurse
is dropped.
Then the patient enters the SelectOutputIn block, which assigns the
patient to a ﬂoor based on the label. The ﬂoor assignment is dependant on the layout
simulated and the specialism where the patient is being treated. A SelectOutputOut
block represents the selected ﬂoor.
The SelectOutputOut assigns the patient to a
speciﬁc ward on the ﬂoor based on the label. The next step depends on the building
assigned, where patients in the new building go to the elevator while others go directly
to the old building. There is a queue to the elevator represented by a Queue block.
The queue follows the ﬁrst-in-ﬁrst-out principle, where the ﬁrst patient that enters
the queue is the ﬁrst one out. When the patient leaves the queue, he enters a Delay
block which simulates the time in the elevator.
6.2.4
Patient Stay
Next steps in the process are where the patient is guided to a bed. For simplicity, the
patients from the two buildings are merged in a queue before entering a SelectOutput
block. It determines whether the patient is arriving or leaving the system. This block
is after the elevator because patients that are leaving the system share elevators with
arriving patients. If the patient is arriving, the PlainTransfer block is entered. In
this block, all wards are analysed to see if they are full or not. If the proper ward
is full, another ward needs to be found. As discussed in Section 4.8, the aim is to
minimise patient transfers and the corresponding travel distance. These principles
are considered here when the wards are analysed to ﬁnd a free bed for the patient.
The simulation model follows the same logic as the distance matrix presented in
Section 4.3. The least preferable patient transfer is to the old building if the patient

6.2.6
Verification and Validation
One of the most important steps in the simulation model building process is valida-
tion and veriﬁcation. The veriﬁcation involves assessing if the model is performing
as intended. That includes model debugging and locating any errors that appear.
The model is stressed to see how it performs under extreme circumstances to ensure
compliance with expected behaviour [31].
Modelling a big hospital with limited resources due to student licence restriction in
AnyLogic requires simplicity. Building a model with a limited number of blocks re-
quires more coding, which results in less transparency, making the debugging process
more complicated. A method for verifying the model is visually tracing the patients
through the processes by using 2D visualisation.
Validation is performed to determine whether the simulation model adequately rep-
resents the real system. This step is often performed with personnel associated with
the system. Their input concerning the realism of the model is critical in establishing
the validity of the simulation [31].
The professional would statistically compare the output of the simulation to the out-
put from the real system and analyse the diﬀerence between the two. Unfortunately,
it is not possible to get the hospital representative to validate the simulation model.
Instead, this phase involves comparing the historical data provided with multiple runs
of the model and continuously improving when necessary, for example, by adjusting
statistical distributions or assumptions. The model is also benchmarked with similar
healthcare models where the outputs are analysed to ensure that the model behaves
as intended.

The selection of the algorithm time limit of two hours proved to be suﬃcient for
problems of similar sizes where the algorithm rarely terminated on the time limit.
Also, the time limit of 60 seconds for sub-problems is a reasonable choice when the
decomposition size is kept relatively small for problems of this size. The limit of 100
rounds without improvement was suﬃcient to generate good solutions for problems
of similar sizes. These parameters will, therefore, be the same when the algorithm is
applied here.
A good performance can be expected with a decomposition size of around 50% of the
problem size, n, if possible. Since the size of this problem is I = 17 and J = 12 it
is plausible to set decomposition sizes as SD = 8 and WD = 6. It is a manageable
size of sub-problems where each problem can be solved to optimality within the time
limit of 60 seconds, assuring that multiple sub-problems can be considered within the
algorithm time limit of 2 hours.
The objective of many studies of the QAP is to dominate other state-of-the-art algo-
rithms using the QAPlib as a benchmark. It means that algorithm parameters are
often tuned on the same set of common benchmarks on which they are evaluated. It
can, however, cause over-tuning since the algorithm is tuned to work well on a speciﬁc
set of instances. One way to mitigate this problem is to include a suﬃcient number
of diverse problems instances in the set of benchmarks [65].
The parameter values picked here gave good results in general for the benchmark
instances.
It is hard to say whether the QAPlib is large and diverse enough to
mitigate the problem of over-tuning. Also, the problem formulation here is diﬀerent
from the general QAP formulation, which creates a risk that the parameters are not
ideal.

Penalty
Putting weights on diﬀerent objective functions is frequently seen in multiple objective
optimisation problems as a way to prioritise them. Pareto optimality is deﬁned as a
state of allocation of resources, in which it is impossible to make one individual better
oﬀwithout making at least one individual worse oﬀ[66]. In this context, a Pareto op-
timal solution is found if no other solution obtained dominates all objective functions.
There can be many Pareto optimal solutions based on the weights on the objective
functions, and there is a trade-oﬀbetween conﬂicting objectives which is controlled
by the weight, or penalty value in this case. The disadvantage of this approach is
that the problem needs to be resolved multiple times with diﬀerent weights.
The conﬂicting objectives in this problem are the quadratic cost (z1) and patient trans-
fer cost (z2). Overﬂow is always less preferable than patient transfers, as discussed
in Section 5.3. As a result, the overﬂow takes a ﬁxed value in all results obtained,
which is the expected overﬂow of 10,4 found in Section 4.5. The set of near-optimal

solutions found for diﬀerent penalty values (ϕ) gives an indicator of a Pareto frontier.
The aim is to propose layout alternatives without involving the decision-maker in the
weight setting. Therefore, multiple diﬀerent weights were implemented to establish

The ﬁx-and-optimise algorithm for the stochastic optimisation model was applied for

8.1
Interpretation and Implication
The main objective of the project was to propose layout alternatives for the in-patient
area in Esbjerg Hospital and to evaluate them based on performance indicators. The
initial goal was to give results where improvements could be made on the current
layout. The mathematical model with diﬀerent penalty values provided nine diﬀerent
layout suggestions for the in-patient area.
The output analysis of the simulation
gave insights into the performance of each alternative and how conﬂicting objectives
increase complexity and impact the eﬃciency of the hospital. The following are the
main ﬁndings for the hospital:
• Regardless of the layout, there is not much buﬀer capacity for peak periods.
• The new in-patient building does not solve the overﬂow of patients.
• The hospital can not manage much increase in patient arrivals.
• HLP can increase the eﬃciency and eﬀectiveness of the system.
In the preliminary phase where historical data was analysed, it was discovered that
the hospital experiences bed shortage on average. The results show that though a
new building for in-patients was introduced, the hospital still experiences overﬂow
regardless of the layout. The bed occupancy rate is higher than 85%, which results
in patient transfers and more discomfort. One specialism was left out of the study,
and the patient volume left out is unknown. An increase of 5% in patient arrivals has
eﬀects on the system, resulting in at least a 5% increase in patient transfers. Analysing
==PAGE:98==
82

Appendix I it can be seen that the average queue size is reasonable without the 5%
increase. However, in peak periods, long queues start building up. With a 5% increase
in patient rate, this becomes even more chronic and peak periods can lead to a ripple
eﬀect of long waiting lists and more patient transfers. The results revealed that the
performance metrics that deﬁne the eﬃciency and eﬀectiveness of proposed layouts
could be improved by using the two-phase approach and are dependent on the layout.
It was discovered that when focusing on minimising patient transfers, it could be
reduced by 21,6%. Also, the utilisation spread in wards was evened by 31,1%.
As often highlighted in the literature on HLP, it can be challenging to address the inﬂu-
ential factors in complex healthcare environments. The solution approach suggested
here addresses some vital factors of hospital layouts. Diﬀerent weights prioritise the
objectives in the multi-objective optimisation problem to present alternative layouts.
According to Gass and Harris [26], this ability is one of the desirable features which
makes the QAP compatible with real-world applications. They also noted that a de-
sirable feature is the ability to produce good solutions with reasonable computational
requirements. The ﬁx-and-optimise heuristic produced acceptable solutions for most
established QAP instances of size less than 70 within two hours of CPU time. Some of
the benchmark instances proved to be simply harder in nature, and the randomness
in the heuristic also has an impact. In the results, it revealed that on average, around
50 minutes of CPU time were required until stopping criteria were met. Nothing can
be concluded in regards to CPU times of smaller or larger instances, and time limits
and other parameters should always be critically evaluated. The disadvantage of the
multi-objective approach is that it needs to be resolved with diﬀerent weights which
is not eﬃcient when multiple penalty values are compared. However, these are all
independent problems and could, therefore, be solved in parallel. Also, in practice,
the decision-maker would be involved in the weight setting, which would increase the
eﬃciency of this phase in regards to CPU time. The quality of the heuristic solutions
was evaluated by deriving lower bounds. An integrality gap of less than 10% was
found for ﬁve out of nine instances. Less than 20% gap was obtained for all instances,
except for the ﬁrst two where the penalty was low, and a large gap was generated
within the time limit of two hours. The time constraints for this study restricted the
amount of time that could be allowed for the solver to tighten the gap.
It was unexpected that the objective values of the mathematical model gave a biased
solution compared to the simulation. Since a large number of scenarios were clustered
into only ﬁve scenarios, the model gave skewed positives. The impact of randomness
was not captured fully in the mathematical model, and increasing the number of
scenarios increases the computational complexity. The simulation considers a signif-
icantly higher number of scenarios and is, therefore, more robust. The simulation
and the real-world are dependants on the randomness in arrivals and LOS of patients.
It has proven to be a valuable tool to analyse any feasible solutions. According to
Anylogic [41] it does not have a built-in mathematical optimisation. Instead, it uses
an optimisation engine called OptQuest where the problems are solved in a black box,
==PAGE:99==
8.1 Interpretation and Implication
83
considering only input and outputs. OptQuest can tackle competitive objectives, but
multiple simulation runs are needed. As a general rule, they state that when solving
multi-objective problems with more than 100 decisions variables, 25.000 simulation
trials are needed [41]. Since the number of variables in the mathematical model sug-
gested here is much larger, it is more eﬀective to use more powerful solvers. This
paragraph supports the decision of combining mathematical modelling and simula-
tion, as suggested in Section 2.1.2 where it is stated that combining simulation with
another tool had led to great success.
In Chapter 2, a work published by Butler, Karwan, Sweigart, et al. [27] was found
with similar solution approach and objectives, where mathematical modelling and
simulation was integrated. Furthermore, the scale of this problem was very similar
to this project, since it deals with allocating 14 services in diﬀerent wards on seven
ﬂoors, having 17.800 yearly patient admissions. They suggested a multiple objective
optimisation where the quadratic term with distances, the number of split units, and
the penalty for overﬂow and underﬂow were minimised. The spatial requirements are
also deﬁned in the number of beds, and some constraints are similar. The fundamen-
tal diﬀerence in the formulations of this thesis to theirs is that minimising patient
transfers is considered in the objective but not only in the simulation model. Also,
a stochastic variant is added, which proved to provide more robust solutions in Ta-
ble 7.3. Lastly, hospital preferences were included, which is vital for the viability of
the ﬁnal suggestions. They proposed a layout suggestion with 27,5% patient trans-
fers and 47,0% utilisation spread after implementing a two-phase approach. After
discussion with administrators, an alternative solution was generated with diﬀerent
bed levels and priority structures. It improved the patient transfers to 18,2%, and the
utilisation spread to 29,0%. It was mentioned in Chapter 2 that usually, professional
users are involved in the layout alternative selection. This thesis shows that there
are signiﬁcant limitations in obtaining good solutions using the two-phase approach
without involving decision-makers. As a consequence, it was emphasised to suggest
an easily extendable framework, where new constraints and objectives can be added
without the need to alter the algorithm itself, as discussed in Section 3.2.
In Chapter 2, it was identiﬁed that formulations of HLPs are usually deterministic
and the stochastic nature of these problems are an under-researched topic.
The
estimated V SS indicated that involving stochastic inputs improves the robustness of
the solution, and it is beneﬁcial to involve stochasticity in HLP models instead of
deterministic data.
The results from this project did not meet the expectation set in the beginning since
there is no indication whether the layout suggestions are viable for the hospital. The
validation phase was skipped due to circumstances, and no conﬁrmation took place.
Because of no comparison with the current layout, the authors suggested AHP anal-
ysis to evaluate the layouts internally.

8 Discussion
8.2
Limitations
The largest limitation of this study was the lack of subjective data from the hospital
due to the unexpected circumstances caused by COVID-19. The closeness ratings
impact the results signiﬁcantly. The matrix had to be assumed by classifying spe-
cialisms based on the arrival trend. It is hard to estimate how well this represents the
hospital’s preferences, and the suggestions may violate some preferences. Also, some
assumptions had to be made for other subjective factors which inﬂuenced the results.
These are, for example, the minimum proportion of each specialism located in each
ward and having no constraints on the maximum distance between specialisms that
are split. Due to the lack of subjective data, the results cannot conﬁrm that the
layout suggestions are a viable option for the hospital to consider.
There is also a limitation on the reliability of stochastic optimisation models that
are resolved using historical data. However, the simulation allowed to ask "what-if"
questions and address the potential increase in the number of patients. The simulation
model has some limitations that should be acknowledged. The AnyLogic academic
license only allows for 200 blocks, which limits the complexity of the model, making it
harder to simulate all processes within the hospital. That increased the importance of
the decision of what to simulate and what to leave out. The limitation on blocks also
prolonged run time of the model, since there are many conditional sentences triggered
in every event in the model. It made 100 replications for nine layout suggestions time-
consuming.
8.3
Recommendations
It is apparent from the simulation that the bottleneck of the system is the bed avail-
ability. When there is an increase in patient arrivals, the waiting list for bed increases
and peak periods become hard to manage. The hospital should implement surgical
planning tools where bed availability is considered when patients are admitted. That
can ensure that patients are admitted in respective wards based on the specialism.
The patient transfers logic suggested here could support decisions to even out work-
load between wards and reduce walking distances.
To mitigate the possible trend of increasing patient rate, more radical actions are
highly recommended. More capacity needs to be utilised from the old building if the
hospital wants to emphasise on patient comfort and well-being.
Before implementing the two-phase framework proposed, assumptions need to be
reviewed and adjusted by relevant stakeholders. The problem should be extended
by adding the specialism that had to be left out in this project due to conﬁdential
reasons.
The ﬂexibility of the modelling part of the project makes it reasonably
easy to add new constraints and objectives without altering the algorithm. When
==PAGE:101==

implementing the algorithm, the penalty setting phase should be less time consuming
when decision-makers can address their preferences. It is recommended to start with
one penalty value and modify until content. It should also be considered to add more
complexity to the simulation to represent the processes better. Before implementing,
they should validate and verify the model based on the results.
8.4
Further Research
The authors believe that despite the limitations, the two-phase framework for HLP
has the necessary features to produce good solutions while providing ﬂexibility and
scalability. It would be worthwhile to make future improvements to the framework.
If time allowed, it would have been interesting to solve a similar HLP and implement
the solution approach suggested here. For example, it could be convenient to have
these tools when unexpected occurrences force hospitals to adapt quickly. A valid
example of a situation where this would be applicable is the COVID-19 pandemic,
where the framework could help hospitals to adapt quickly by rearranging the spatial
assignments.
There are aspects of this problem that would be interesting to analyse further. The
mathematical model could have more restrictions, for example, a maximum distance
between wards when a specialism is split. Also, if all qualitative data would have been
available, there would probably be restrictions on how patients can be transferred
within the hospital. Patient transfers are likely more inconvenient in some specialisms
than others. It should be noted that more constraints could negatively impact the
performance of the heuristic algorithm.
The extent to which this performance is
aﬀected could, however, be interesting to investigate.
When it comes to the heuristic approach, there are certainly better algorithms that
have been applied to QAP’s and would be interesting to implement. Future work
could involve studying these heuristics further and comparing diﬀerent methods. It
would be essential to explore more eﬃcient heuristic algorithms, for example, meta-
heuristics, to make it applicable for very large instances. More eﬃcient algorithms
could also allow for a higher number of scenarios. However, the results show that
better solutions do not always correlate with improved simulation results. Therefore,
it might not be worth the eﬀort to ﬁnd the optimal solutions to this problem if good
solutions can already be generated.
Furthermore, the suggested algorithm could be improved when it comes to CPU
time since much time is spent on storing numbers and writing to the console while
running the code. It could also be tested in another programming language than
Julia to see if it improves the performance.
All these suggestions would lead to
possible improvements when it comes to the eﬃciency of solving the problem.
It
is also suggested to implement lower bounding techniques other than LP-relaxation

Some processes are left out of the simulation model due to the lack of data. The

The results from this thesis support the statement put forth in Chapter 1.
Bed

to solve this problem, it is still apparent. Strategic HLP has proved to mitigate this
problem as the results indicate. Diﬀerent layouts result in fewer patient transfers and
more even utilisation across wards. The two-phase framework utilised in this thesis
project proved to be a good approach. Solving the problem using the mathematical
model to obtain layout suggestions and stressing them in a simulated environment
provided the reassurance needed. Also, the ability to create an animation of the ﬂow
where bottlenecks can be visually identiﬁed is a valuable feature.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The main objective of this project was to propose layout alternatives for the in-patient
area at Esbjerg Hospital and evaluate them based on performance indicators. The
results should guide and support improvement decisions for the hospital’s decision-
makers. The focus was on reducing patient transfers between wards and overﬂow,
dispersing the workload, while preferably meeting preferences set by the hospital. It
emphasised qualitative factors in the layout design to ensure patient safety and a
healthy working environment.
A two-phase approach was proposed as a solution method.
A stochastic multi-
objective optimisation problem was formulated based on the QAP, and a heuristic
procedure was suggested to generate alternative layouts. To analyse the patient ﬂow
at the hospital, a DES model was designed. Due to limitations, it was not possible to
propose layout suggestions and use the current layout as a benchmark, and subjective
data had to be assumed. Nine layout suggestions were generated and analysed. The
results indicate that Esbjerg hospital is experiencing an overﬂow of patients, even
with the introduction of the new in-patient building. The historical data showed that
the bed occupancy rate was 89% on average. A bed occupancy rate of over 85% can
be a safety threat, leading to a higher mortality rate and decreases patient comfort
signiﬁcantly. The results show that there is not much buﬀer capacity for peak periods
and an increase in the patient arrival rate results in queues and a higher number of
patient transfers. In peak periods where the maximum capacity of some wards is
reached, it impacts the patient transfers since the aim is to assign patients to the
nearest available bed. In chronic cases, this leads to a ripple eﬀect, eventually result-
ing in long queues. The hospital should seek ways to expand even more to reduce the
bed occupancy rate.
The mathematical model and the simulation model provide the upper management
at the hospital in Esbjerg with a tool that could improve their current layout. The
assumed data impacts the results signiﬁcantly, which would likely be adjusted with
real data before viable suggestions could be considered.
The two-phase approach
can be used to generate multiple diﬀerent layout suggestions and compare them vi-
sually using simulation. The results showed that performance indicators linked with
the hospital’s eﬃciency is dependent on the layout and can be improved. Also, the
preferences of the decision-makers can be incorporated and reﬂected in the layout

suggestions. The two-phase approach diﬀers from the methodology in previous work
on similar problems. The authors believe that this approach is a step closer to more
robust hospital layout planning and future works and improvements could make it
even more applicable. The project can be taken a step further by implementing con-
ﬁdential information and adding more complexity to mimic the real world processes
as close as possible. This project is an excellent basis, and we look forward to seeing
what the hospitals next steps are.
