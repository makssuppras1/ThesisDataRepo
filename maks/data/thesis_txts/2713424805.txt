Abstract
In this thesis, molecular design using artificial intelligence is explored with the goal of comparing
how the techniques perform to a more traditional approach using mixed integer non-linear program-
ming (MINLP). In existing publications, the topic of AI in molecular design is mostly applied in the
field of drug discovery, where it is used to explore a bigger part of the molecular space. The area
of drug discovery have huge public data-sets available, making it easily accessible for researchers.
Meanwhile, in this thesis, deep generative models are applied for thermodynamic properties in data
scarce application. In drug discovery, deep generative models such as graph neural networks (GNN),
variational autoencoders (VAE), and generative adversarial networks (GAN) have been used to gen-
erate molecules with desired properties.
To further improve the chances of AI models working, public websites such as NIST and Pub-
Chem were scraped. These websites contain data of interest for the chemical engineering field, and
increased the amount of data available up to five-fold for some properties. The new collected data
is then applied to a case study to screen for an entrainer for a distillation problem. The design
problem is originally formulated as a Computer-Aided Molecular Design (CAMD) procedure using
MINLP to find the best entrainer with regard to a set of process performance metrics. The extractive
distillation process is used to separate a non-ideal mixture of acetone-methanol. The explored AI
solutions are not able to fully solve the original case study, and instead can help to generate more
candidates for screening. The proposed solution in this thesis is to use a generate and test approach
to screen a larger part of the molecular space. The molecular candidates were screened based on
constraints such as melting point, boiling point, and enthalpy of vaporization. To determine if a
generated molecule’s properties were within the constraints, graph neural networks were trained to
predict the properties. The models performed not as well as state-of-the-art, for boiling point the
coefficient of determination was R2 ≥0.82 which was lower compared to published results with a
R2 = 0.959. A similar result was observed for melting point and enthalpy of vaporization where the
coefficient of determination was R2 ≥0.91 and for the published results the value was R2 ≥0.99.
The NIST and PubChem was tested further by comparing the performance to models trained on
a DIPPR data-set alone. The performance were similar when predicting properties of molecules in
DIPPR, thus indicating further that the collected data is of high quality.
The generation of molecules was solved using different techniques such as baseline generative
models, conditional models with/without transfer learning, and Bayesian optimization of VAEs
latent spaces. Before picking a specific architecture for implementation, two publicly available ar-
chitectures GraphINVENT and CVAE were tested. GraphINVENT uses a GNN architecture and
one of the biggest issues is related to the required preprocessing of data-sets before training. On a
benchmarking data-set MOSES used for testing generative molecular models, the preprocessing took
< 24 hours, which was reduced from the initial 5 days of preprocessing in the original publication.
The CVAE is a conditional VAE where the number of conditional parameters were set to zero to
turn it into a simple VAE. The VAE performed similarly to the GNN approach while requiring less
preprocessing and training was faster. Based on this, a new β-VAE was implemented using three
different encoder architectures and one decoder architecture. All the different architectures were
trained on different data-sets.
The first data-set eMolecules contained 26 millions commercially
available compounds, the second data-set GDB-9 contained small molecules with up to 9 atoms not
including hydrogen, and the third data-set was a custom-made data-set based on molecules from
the collected property data. For each model, 30,000 molecules were generated and for the valid
and unique molecules the property prediction networks were used to discard molecules not following
both the structural and the pure compound property constraints. The models trained on the custom
data-set produced around 2,000 molecules related to the case study. For models trained on GDB-9
produced around 4,000 molecules, and models trained on eMolecules produced almost no molecules
related to the case study. The models trained on GDB-9 performing the best were a clear indicator
that smaller molecules were more likely to follow the property constraints.

To further test generative models, both structural and property data were incorporated into con-
ditional models. These models were trained on different combinations of the property data-sets based
on the overlapping molecules, allowing the incorporation of property data as conditional parameters.
Additional structural data of the molecule’s molecular weight, formal charge, and if certain atom
types were in the molecules were added as additional conditional parameters to the conditional VAE.
The hypothesis was that the addition of the structural information would allow the generation of
molecules with the desired structural requirements of the entrainer design case study. The condi-
tional models were tested similar to the baseline models, and they performed similar or better than
the baseline models trained on GDB-9. For the best model, around 5,000 out of the 30,000 molecules
followed all the constraints, showcasing that even with a small training-set the generative AI models
can work. An additional model was trained on eMolecules with structural conditional parameters.
Adding the structural information resulted in a model trained on eMolecules producing up to 3,500
out of the 30,000 molecules following all the required constraints. This confirmed the hypothesis, as
the previous baseline models produced almost no molecules following all the constraints. To further
improve upon the conditional models, transfer learning was applied using a conditional model with
extra unused conditional parameters trained on the eMolecules data-set. Afterwards the transfer
learning was applied by training the model on the different property data-set allowing the model
to further learn to incorporate property data into the latent space. The outcome of the transfer
learning was models that produced a higher percentage of valid and unique molecules. The model
training on fewest data-points went from generating 51% valid molecules to around 90% using trans-
fer learning. Additionally, the models performed slightly better than the conditional models without
transfer learning on the case study, with an improvement in the range of 5−25% for the best model
for each new property data-set.
Models using baseline architectures and conditional architecture with and without transfer learn-
ing techniques were able to outperform the original proposed solution solving the generating molecules
following the constraints using MINLP. As a last step to further explore molecular design models, the
latent space produced from the trained models were explored using Bayesian optimization. Bayesian
optimization was not promising for this case study, as it was struggling to optimize for the many
dimensions of the latent space.
Overall, the use of generative models showed great promise in
molecular design and can be applied in a low data domains.

Introduction
Chemical product design is the science of identifying chemicals intended for a specific application.
This field of research has produced a variety of products that have benefited society and humanity
on various levels. Some of these products are pharmaceutical products for treating diseases, coating
to protect structures and ships from rust, additives to mimic sensory experiences as well as increase
shelf life, and stability of food products and medicine. Traditionally, chemical product design was
performed using heuristic trials based on experience as well as trial and error experiments[5]. This re-
quired highly specialized people with deep chemical knowledge to set up the correct experiments and
identify the potential candidates. Additionally, it is very time-consuming; designing, experimenting,
and screening. In the end, it might not result in the optimal solution, as the focus is on a small class
of compounds or a set of compounds with similar structure to already known compounds[5]. One
issue with molecular design is the sheer size of the design space. The theoretically possible chemical
space for unique molecules for small drug-like structures is estimated by Bohacek et al.[7] in 1996
to be around 1060. In more recent years, it is estimated to be closer to 1063[23] which is more than
the estimated number of stars in the universe (1024).
In the field of molecular design, two very common approaches to experiments are in-vitro and in-
silico. In-vitro covers experiments conducted in test tubes, which is very close to the traditional
method of designing molecular design. In-silico on the other hand covers experiments performed on a
computer or using computer simulations[11]. With the increase of computational resources, in-silico
experiments have increased in popularity. This area of research relies heavily on what is referred
to as Computer-Aided Molecular Design (CAMD)[5].
The use of computers has opened up the
possibility of screening a larger part of the molecular design space, leading to the reduction in both
the time and cost of developing new products. These approaches have their roots in mathematical
models and especially the development of Quantitative Structural Property Relationship (QSPR)
models which are mathematical models that relate the structure of the molecules to a specific target
property. The application of QSPR techniques is often related to solving what is denoted a forward
problem. The forward problem consists of predicting the property given a molecule. One class of
QSPR models is Group Contribution models (GC)[26]. In GC models, molecules are represented
using subgroups, which is a decomposition of the molecule. In some cases, these subgroups are
allowed to overlap. An example of molecules divided into first-order subgroups can be seen in figure
1.
Figure 1: Illustration of how GC models work from the paper Computer-aided molecular design: An
introduction and review of tools, applications, and solution techniques[5].
Regression is then used to predict how much each subgroup contributes to a property. Though
these methods have given good results, they still introduce a large amount of bias and often the re-
gression models are created using all the available experimental data, which leads to overfitting[24].
Additionally, these models have issues in prediction properties for larger molecules and isomers. The
==PAGE:8==
2
GC models have issues capturing interaction between atoms and their molecular bonds. A newer
model has tried to solve the issue by incorporating molecular bonds into the GC model. This method
is known as GC+ and was introduced by Gani et al. 2005[16]. Other popular areas in QSPR are
topological indices and signature descriptors[5].
In many cases in molecular design, the reverse problem is of interest, where the goal is predicting
molecular structures based on desired properties. This area of CAMD is an optimization problem.
The big problem with reverse designing of molecules is the size of the molecular space, as it requires
significant computational power exploring a huge amount of data. Additionally, the models solving
the reverse problem have to produce structures following a strict set of rules, such as atom’s valency.
These rules and requirements can be specified as a set of equations and inequalities[5]. This method
can be applied to solve design problems such as single molecule design. Single molecular design can
e.g. be described as the following problems.
• The feasibility problem, where there is no objective function used in the optimization problem
as the function to measure the performance of a molecule could use simulations that are
infeasible to run on all candidates. Instead, the aim is to reduce the feasible property space
into a manageable size of molecules. This is done by having a set of functions the molecules
must satisfy[5].
• Exact relationship between structures and performance, defines problems where objective func-
tion for the optimization is directly measurable based on a molecule’s structure[5].
• Minimizing distance to property targets, the goal is finding the closest molecule to a set of
desired properties. The objective function is used to rank the molecules in best to worst. The
metrics could be cost, availability, environmental friendliness etc.[5].
Another possible type of problem that can be desired to solve is a mixture problem. This problem
is harder to solve than the single molecule design problem as mixture properties must be calculated
and included in the problem, requiring solving the ratio of each compound, and thermodynamic re-
lationships, equation of state etc. must also be calculated in the optimization problem[5]. This type
of problem suffers from the large molecular space and is difficult to solve directly as optimization
problems. One way this problem has been circumvented is using problem decomposition. For a
single molecule design problem, one option is to apply very strict constraints, thereby reducing the
molecular space significantly. This could be strict structural constraints that are evaluated before
more complex constraints are calculated. For mixture design, the problem can be decomposed into
multiple single molecular design problems, where each of them can be solved using optimization
techniques[5].
One option would be to decompose the mixture problem into multiple feasibility
problems followed by a minimizing distance to property targets problem. The feasibility problems
goal would be to apply structural constraints, property constraints, and mixture constraints. The
final candidates can then be evaluated using the minimizing distance to property targets to find the
optimal candidate. Solving these subproblems individually is easier than solving them all at once.
Several techniques can be applied to solve these the subproblems. One approach is the generate
and test. The concept is simple, a large amount of molecules are generated over a set of require-
ments. The molecules are further reduced based on evaluations of constraints using QSPR models.
This method often can solve the design problem without solving complex optimization problems[5].
Another approach to solve the design problems is using mathematical optimization models such as
mixed-integer linear/non-linear programming(MILP/MINLP). In these methods, the molecular de-
sign is formulated as equations and constraints with the goal of either minimizing or maximizing an
objective function. Combining this with GC models allows finding solutions that maximizes/min-
imizes the object function based on local and global maxima and minima. The molecules found
through this method can afterwards be tested and screened[15]. Other methods that have been
applied to solving the molecular problems are genetic algorithms and heuristics[5].

In more recent years, artificial intelligence has increased in popularity and shown great potential
in solving many types of problems. Adapting these techniques to molecular design is a hot topic,
and one of the advantages is the versatility in inputs to neural networks. This allows for further
incorporation of molecular structures into the models. In current literature, molecules are mostly
represented using text, 2D images and mathematical graphs. These representations have been used
to create new QSPR models based on neural network architectures[48]. Recent research uses ar-
chitectures such as Graph Neural Networks(GNN) that have previously been used to model social
networks etc. As molecules can be represented as a mathematical graph, this has allowed incor-
porating more structural information into the neural networks. These models have been shown to
outperform the previous methods, such as group contribution[1].
A newer field of research in molecular design using AI is applying neural networks to the reverse
CAMD problems. These approaches rely on research developing generative neural networks to gen-
erate molecules as an output of the network. In literature, the models use all types of architectures
such as graph neural networks, variational autoencoders, generative adversarial networks, and re-
current neural networks[52]. The models are taught to learn a molecular space, and new molecules
are generated by sampling the learned molecular space. This approach can be combined with the
test and generate, allowing exploration of a bigger area of the molecular space. Molecular gener-
ative methods using AI have also been used to incorporate property information into the learned
models. This biases the model towards specific properties and techniques such as transfer learning,
reinforcement learning, Bayesian optimization, and conditioned neural networks, have been applied
in targeted molecular design[52]. Applying AI to a problem requires a huge amount of data. Molec-
ular design using AI has mainly been applied to drug discovery, as the molecular space is huge and
several databases are available with drug-like molecules[52]. In this thesis, the goal is to investigate
the potential of applying deep generative models for the molecular design problem in scarce data en-
vironments and compare it to the traditional MINLP approach. The goal is to address the following
questions.
• What are the existing methods used for molecular design using AI?
• How can these methods be applied to molecular design for scarce data environments?
• How do these methods compare to existing methods of molecular design, such as MINLP?
To address these questions, existing literature is explored to unravel architectures and techniques
applied for generative models for molecular design using AI and is described in section 2. A subset of
the techniques are then applied for molecular design on thermodynamic properties. This is an area
with scarce data, and to further improve generative models, public website such as PubChem[29]
and NIST[36] have been scraped and is described in section 3. This data is combined into new
data-sets, and a case study is further explored how these AI approaches work in the low data regime
of thermodynamic properties. The case study explored uses a generate and test approach where
molecules are generated from different architectures and methods. This is described in section 4.

Literature Review
This chapter provides information regarding several existing AI methods applied for molecular de-
sign.
The chapter is divided into multiple sections, where section 2.1 present different ways to
represent molecules for neural networks, and the following section 2.2 introduces metrics used in
literature to measure the performance of models. The last section, 2.3, introduces different archi-
tectures used for generative models for AI assisted molecular design.
2.1
Molecule Representations
For data-driven models, several options are available for representing molecules. The three major
approaches are 3D Based, Graph Based, and Text based. For a 3D structure, molecules are repre-
sented as a 3D grid using voxels. A voxel is a 3D dimensional cube with a specified width, length,
and height. All the voxels make up a three dimensional matrix. For every voxel, a one hot encoding
is created based on if a type of atom or bond is present in the voxel. This leads to one issue that
most voxels are empty and one option is to apply a spatial smoothing in the form e.g. spherical
Gaussians[12]. Additionally, the voxel suffers from the problem that molecules can have any type of
rotation in a 3D space.
A graph representation is based on a mathematical graph G = (V, E), where atoms in the
molecule are represented by a set of vertices in V and the set of edges E represents bonds connecting
two atoms. Additional information of the molecule can be incorporated by having additional feature
vectors with e.g. atom type and bond type. One issue is that a molecular graph is isomorphism[12].
A graph can be represented by many different adjacency matrices and is computationally expensive
to detect if two graphs are isomorphic.
The text based representation of the molecules is the most used approach for generative models.
Two common methods used are the Simplified Molecular-Input Line-entry System (SMILES) and
the International Chemical Identifier (InChI)[12]. SMILES are a sequence of characters building up
a molecule. The text representation is generated using depth-first traversal of the molecular graph.
One issue is the non-uniqueness of SMILES as they can be generated differently based on the starting
atom and struggles with representing isomers. This is solved by a modified version called Canonical
SMILES which ensures a SMILES is generating the same molecular graph. Differentiating between
isomers is solved by adding additional characters representing the bond direction.
An example
of a SMILES for benzene is c1ccccc1. SMILES have the advantage that to some degree they are
human-readable. An example of SMILES and InChI’s can be seen in figure 2.
Figure 2: Examples of molecules represented by SMILES and InChI.
Canonicalization is not perfect, and still suffers from issue that the canonical SMILES can be dif-
ferent depending on which algorithm is used to perform the canonicalization. In literature, SMILES
is the most used representation for generative approaches compared to graph based approaches.

2.2
Performance Measurements
In molecular design using AI, the performance of a given model is characterized through the following
metrics
• Percentage of valid molecules (Valid)
• Percentage of unique molecules (Unique)
• Percentage of novel molecules (Novelty)
These metrics are often calculated by generating a sample size of 30,000 molecules, and a molecule
is considered as valid if valency rules hold and no errors are present in the SMILES or other type of
output generated by the models. In the literature this verification is mostly handled by a software
framework called RDKit[33], and a molecule is considered valid if RDKit is able to convert e.g.
a SMILES string into a molecular representation. Uniqueness is a measure of how many distinct
molecules the model generated. Since SMILES can be different depending on the algorithm used to
generate them, canonicalization is applied to sanitize the smiles before testing the uniqueness. The
novelty is a measure of how many generated molecules are not present in the training-set.

2.3.1
Recurrent Neural Networks
Recurrent neural networks (RNN) are a type of neural network that uses sequential data. RNNs
have internal memory, allowing them to use previous feed data to calculate an output. Two of
the most commonly used RNN architectures are the Gated-Recurrent-Unit (GRU) and Long Short-
Term Memory(LSTM). An example of a GRU architecture can be seen in figure 3 where ht−1 is the
previous hidden-state, xt is the current input, and ot is the output at time-step t.
Figure 3: Architecture of a GRU.
The GRU has a hidden-state which is used to store information from previous time-steps. Inside
the GRU there are two gates, the reset gate and update gate. The reset gate is used to decide how
much of the previous state should be discarded, and the update gate is used to determine how much
from the previous state should be carried to the next state[8]. In the figure of the GRU, the reset
gate and update gate represent the following operations.
rt = σ(Wrxt + Urht−1)

2.3.1.1
cRNN
The Conditional Recurrent Neural Network is designed to generate molecules with specific target
properties. In the work by Kotsias et al.[31], SMILES are generated from RNNs. Text based models
often use a grammar, where all unique characters in the data-set are given a unique number or an
one-hot encoding. This allows turning a sequence of character into a matrix. For the cRNN the
grammar consists of the characters in the SMILES and two additional characters, the end sentence
and start sentence characters. At the start of every SMILES the start character is added and at
the end of the SMILES, one or multiple end characters are added. The number of end characters
added depends on the length of the SMILES. The adding of characters at the end is often referred
to as padding a sequence. Afterwards, the new sequence of characters are one-hot encoded using the
grammar[31]. The paper introduces two neural networks[31] with different conditional parameters.
The first neural network is trained using RDKit and a Quantitative Structure-Activity Relationship
(QSAR) models as conditional parameters to the SMILES. From RDKit the following properties are
used as conditional parameters.
• Logarithm of the Octanol-Water Partition Coefficient (LogP)
• Topological Polar Surface Area (TPSA)
• Molecular Weight (MW)
• Number of Hydrogen Bond Acceptors (HBA)
• Number of Hydrogen Bond Donors (HBD)
• Drug-Likeness Score (QED)
The QSAR model used is a measure of bioactivity. This neural network model is referred to as PCB
(PhysChem-Based). The second neural network model uses Fingerprints from RDKit as conditional
parameters and is referred to as the FPB (FingerPrint-Based) model.
The two neural network
models use the same architecture, and a high level overview of the process of applying the conditional
parameters can be seen in figure 5. The architecture consists of two parts. The first part is a dense
neural network to generate the initial hidden state of the RNN. The dense neural network receives
either a molecule’s properties or the fingerprint to output the hidden state. The dense neural network
consists of multiple dense layers with a ReLU activation function at the end[31]. The second part
is the recreation of the molecule based on the initial hidden state from the output from the dense
neural network. The recreation of SMILES is done by an RNN consisting of a multi layered LSTM.
The LSTM is unrolled for multiple steps t, and each output from a time-step ti is passed through
a linear layer with a Softmax activation function. Each of the outputs is a distribution over the

Variational autoencoders (VAE) were originally introduced in 2013 by Kingma et al.[30]. It is an
extension of the original autoencoder (AE) where the goal is first performing a dimensional reduction

2.3.2.1
CVAE
In the work by Lim et al.[35], a conditioned variational autoencoder is used for molecular design
by incorporating properties MW, LogP, HBD, HBA, and TPSA into the generation. The CVAE
embeds conditional information using a modified version of the objective function of the VAE.
Conditional variables c are concatenated with input for the encoder and the latent representation
before it is sent through the decoder. The conditional variables were normalized in the data-set. A
high level overview of the approach can be seen in figure 6.
Figure 6: Process of the CVAE from encoding to decoding.
The concatenation of the conditional variable changes ELBO to the following
E[log p(x|z, c)] −KLD[q(z|x, c)||p(z)]
(9)
As c is concatenated to the latent variable, the CVAE thereby learns to generate new molecules
with desired properties. The model uses SMILES in their canonical form, thus resulting in unique
molecular representation. The SMILES were concatenated with an extra character E, representing
the end of a string. Each character in the string is one-hot encoded individually, resulting in a
sequence of vectors. A learned embedding is then applied to every vector. Every vector in the
sequence is concatenated with the conditional vector with properties. This method of encoding the
SMILES is applied both in the encoder and decoder. For the encoder, the encoded SMILES string
with conditional parameters is sent through an LSTM where the hidden state ht at the last step is
used as input to two linear neural networks to predict the µ and log σ. The reparameterization trick
is then applied to calculate the latent vector z. The decoder is trained using Teacher Forcing similar
to the cRNN, where every character of the SMILES, is encoded character and concatenated with
conditional parameters. This is then sent through an LSTM, where every output is further passed
through a linear layer followed by a Softmax activation function[35]. This creates a distribution over
the characters and the cross entropy loss can be calculated between the correct characters and the
distribution for each character from the LSTM.
During sampling of the latent space to generate new molecules, the latent vector is sampled from
the distribution N(0, 1). The latent vector is concatenated with conditional parameters. The LSTM
in the decoder is unrolled up to 120 times, where for every time-step ti the output from the Softmax
activation function is sampled using a stochastic write-out process until the end string character
is sampled or the max length of 120 unrollings is reached. If the end character did not appear
in the 120 unrollings then the generated sequence was considered as an invalid SMILES. Using the
sampling based on a probabilistic distribution allows for multiple molecules to be generated from the
same latent vector with conditional input. The model’s performance is analyzed using a stochastic
write-out method, where 100 molecules are sampled from the same latent vector. All valid and non
duplicated molecules were further analyzed by the authors[35]. Some results can be seen in table
2 where the goal was to sample new molecules with similar properties to existing drugs. This is
performed by encoding the existing drug and using the latent vector from the encoding as input to
the decoder for the stochastic write-out process.

GraphVAE
GraphVAE is a graph variational autoencoder proposed by Simonovsky et al.[51] for molecular
design. It is implemented both as conditional and unconditional instance, allowing generation of
molecules with targeted properties. The graphs are represented by G = (A, E, F), where A is the
adjacency matrix, E the edge attribute tensor, and F the node attribute matrix.
The problem
is limited to molecules of k atoms/nodes to reduce complexity and memory usage. A high level
overview of the model can be seen in figure 7.
Figure 7: Architecture of the GraphVAE.
The encoder uses a feed forward network with edge-conditioned convolutions, which is an archi-
tecture proposed by Simonovsky et al.[50] in a previous publication. The architecture has similarities
to a graph neural network and uses convolutional neural networks. The network uses A, E and F
as input to embed the graph. Though, it is suggested that any form of graph embedding method
can be applied. The encoder is probabilistic, and a Gaussian distribution is enforced by having the
last layer in the encoder output 2 · c features. The first c features represent the µ and the last c
features represents σ. Then the latent representation is created using the reparameterization trick
as explained previously.
The decoder is deterministic and uses an architecture of a simple MLP with three outputs in the
last layer. The first output is eA which contains both node probabilities for a node being present
( eAa,a) and edge probability eAa,b.

Semi-Supervised VAE
Semi-Supervised VAE (SSVAE) is VAE trained using Semi-Supervised learning for molecular
design proposed by Kang et al.[27]. Semi-Supervised learning is a technique using both unlabeled
and labeled data in a supervised learning problem. The goal of using Semi-Supervised learning on the
VAE is to solve the problem of minimal experimental data for compounds being easily accessible,
as it is very time-consuming and expensive to perform property experiments. The incorporation
of Semi-Supervised learning uses a neural network to predict properties of the molecules where

Bayesian Optimization of the Latent Space
Several papers have experimented with Bayesian optimization[32][9][6][20] of the latent space,
both using VAE and adversarial autoencoders (AEE). AEE is an extension to VAE, where instead
it uses adversarial loss to regularize the latent encoding instead of KL Divergence. This can be used
to learn other types of distributions. Bayesian optimization is a class of machine-learning based
optimization methods where the goal is solving the problem[14]
max
x∈A f(x)
(11)
where f(x) is an objective function and is expensive to evaluate and A is a set of constraints. f(x) is
also referred to as a black-box function. The idea is then to use a surrogate model for modeling the
true function f(x) and define its prior. Initially, n points are sampled as a set of start observations
and Bayes theorem is used to obtain a posterior. Then in a continuous loop an acquisition function,
based on the posterior, is used to decide the next point to sample. The new point is scored using
the function f and is added as a new observations and loop is repeated with the new posterior[14].
In Bayesian optimization of the latent space, the set of observations is points in the latent space and
the optimization is in the form of finding latent representations with desired properties. Another
reason to use Bayesian optimization is to avoid dead regions in the latent space. Dead regions can
occur because of three reasons[20]:
• A sampling location is very unlikely under the prior.
• A latent space with very high dimensionality
• Inhomogeneous training data where certain regions are less present in the data-set.
In Griffiths et al.[20] a variation of Bayesian optimization is proposed, called Constrained Bayesian
optimization. A user specified confidence is introduced that a certain boolean constraint is satisfied.
The suggested constraint function is a trained neural network to predict the probability of a latent
representation decodes into a valid molecule. The idea is that this reduces the amount of sampling
of the dead regions in the latent space. The molecules generated from Constrained Bayesian op-
timization are compared to sampling using standard Bayesian optimization. The results from the
paper[20] can be seen in figure 8 and are based on performing 20 iterations of Bayesian optimization
with batches of 50 data points collected at each iteration, resulting in 1000 latent points decoded in
total.
Figure 8: Test of generated molecules using the baseline model and the constrained model using
Bayesian optimization. Source, Griffiths et al.[20].
Bayesian Optimization is applied for drug design and the performance is measured based on valid,
realistic, and drug-like molecules generated. Realistic molecules are defined as a generated SMILES
with at least 5 characters[20]. The unconstrained Bayesian optimization results in < 5% realistic

Generative Adversarial Networks
The Generative Adversarial Network(GAN) was introduced by Goodfellow et al.[19] and it is a
neural network consisting of two parts, the generator and discriminator. The generator goal is to
generate data, and the discriminator goal is to distinguish between real training data or fake data
generated by the generator. The generator is denoted with G and the discriminator with D. The
training of the two parts is a two player minimax game with the value function V (G, D):
min
G max
D V (D, G) = Ex∼pdata(x)[log D(x)] + Ez∼pz(z)[log(1 −D(G(z)))]
(12)
where D(x) is the output from discriminator for real data and D(G(z)) is the discriminators output
from the generator G(z). z is a noise variable following on a prior probability distribution pz(z).
The training of GAN is performed in two steps for each epoch. For each training epoch, the
discriminator is trained for k steps. Afterwards, the generator is trained for a minibatch. The rea-
soning for training this way is to prevent overfitting of the discriminator by becoming too confident.
Training for k steps results in the discriminator being maintained at a near optimal solution and the
generator slowly improving.[19]. The training of the GAN is a fine balance between the generator
and discriminator.
2.3.3.1
Stacked cGAN for Gene Expression Signatures
In the work by Mendez it al.[38] a stacked GAN is used for generating molecules with desired gene
expression signatures. More specifically, the neural network is conditioned to generate molecules that
have a high probability to induce a desired transcriptomic profile. Transcriptomic data have been
shown to be useful in the identification of new active molecules, drug repurposing, and prediction
of side effects[38]. The method is an alternative approach to bridge chemistry and biology. The
proposed procedure consists of two methods.
The first method is an autoencoder for encoding
SMILES into a latent presentation and decoding latent space representations to a set of grammar
production rules which can be converted back to SMILES[56]. The encoder is used to generate latent
representation of molecules and feed real data into two discriminators. A high level architecture can
be seen in figure 9.
The second method is a stacked GAN. Both of the GANs are based on a combination of cGAN
(Conditional GAN)[40] and WGAN-GP (Wasserstein GAN with gradient penalty)[3]. The authors’
reasoning for stacking two GANs, is that it has been shown to produce synthetic data with higher
resolution images for some problems in computer vision for text to image synthesis compared to just
using a single GAN[38]. The stacked GAN is split into two generators G0 and G1 and two discrim-
inators D0 and D1, and two conditional networks f0 and f1. Where the discriminator determines
the probability of the latent representation decoding into a molecule, and the conditional networks
determine if the latent presentation matches a gene expression signature. The first generator uses
Gaussian noise and a conditional parameter in the form of a gene expression signature to generate a
latent representation, while the second generator uses the latent representation from the first GAN
with the conditional parameter of the gene expression signature as input instead of the Gaussian
noise.

2.3.4
Graph Neural Networks
The first model of Graph Neural Network (GNN) was introduced back in 2008 by Scarselli et al.[47].
The idea behind the new model was, a lot of problems in real life can be modeled as mathematical
graphs represented by vertices connected by edges, G = (V, E). The GNN introduces a label for
every vertex and edges in the graph, and these labels are a vector stacked of features or properties.
Using molecular graphs as an example, the vertices represent atoms and the edges represent the
bonds connecting the atoms together creating molecules. The labels for a vertex in the molecular
graph can be represented using properties such as atom type, charge etc. The labels for the edges
are mostly represented using the bond type, bond stereo etc. Since the original introduction in
2008, the GNN has gained huge traction, resulting in a lot of different GNN architectures. In many
cases the GNN can be generalized as message passing neural network (MPNN), the MPNN can be
expressed based on three functions; the message passing function Mt[17],

node updates. After these t-steps, the final state is read out using the readout function.
2.3.4.1
GraphINVENT
In the work by Mercado et al.[39] a graph neural network referred to as GraphINVENT is designed
to generate new molecules and operates on the molecular graph. The concept used stems from a
previous published paper where GNN are used for property prediction. Using GNN for generative
models is a new area of research and still being explored. A high level architecture can be seen in
figure 10.
Figure 10: The architecture of GraphINVENT where the GNN Block differs from each trained
model.
The architecture of GraphINVENT is divided into two blocks. The first block is a GNN and
referred to as the GNN Block. In the paper, authors try out multiple architectures for the GNN
block such as, Message Passing Neural Network (MPNN), Gated-Graph Neural Network (GGNN)
etc[39]. The second block is the global readout block, which is performing the readout of the GNN
network from the first block. The global readout block uses both node- and graph-level information
to perform predictions regarding the next action to further build the molecule. The prediction from
the model is action probability distribution (APD) over the actions: add vertex (fadd), connect last
added vertex to another vertex (fconn), and terminate (fterm) for stopping the generation. For each
action, a multi-layer-perceptron structure is used to calculate the action’s probability along with

supporting information, such as which node to connect the new node to. The APD is calculated
using Softmax over the action space. The generation of molecules is performed by continuously
feeding into the model the graph generated in the previous step until a termination action or an
invalid action is sampled from the action space, similar to the concept of sequence sampling using
RNNs. For training the neural network, preprocessing of every molecule is required. The molecular
graph is decomposed into a sequence of actions to recreate the molecular graphs. A modified breadth-
first search (BFS) is used to explore the graph, making sure the same order of actions are always
achieved. This graph decomposition is then used to train the GNN using the Teacher’s Forcing

2.3.4.2
RL-GraphINVENT
RL-GraphINVENT[4] is an extension to GraphINVENT adding a reinforcement learning (RL)
as an extra step. The reinforcement learning is based on the REINVENT algorithm[43]. The REIN-
VENT algorithm has previously been used on RNN and other architectures for targeted molecular
design. Initially, a model is trained using GraphINVENT2.3.4.1 to generate molecules. Afterwards,
the trained model is further tuned using policy gradient reinforcement learning similar to REIN-
VENT. In REINVENT, the goal is to update the agent policy π from the prior policy πP rior,
increasing the expected score for the sequences of actions building a graph. The policy is parame-
terized based on the action probability distribution (APD) from GraphINVENT. Both REINVENT
and the proposed method uses an augmented log-likelihood based on a prior reference model. The
method differentiates itself from REINVENT by introducing a loss term. The loss term is based on
the best agent so far in order to remind the current agent of sets of actions that can lead to high-
scoring compounds, thereby accelerating the agent learning. This is the best agent reminder (BAR)

2.3.4.3
GCPN
In the work by You et al.[60] a Graph Convolutional Policy Network(GCPN) is used as a gen-
erative model. The model uses both node and edge features and is trained to maximize a property
function S(G) where G is the generated graph. The model is designed to be an iterative generative
process, defined as a Markov Decision Process (MDP). Using this MDP, the policy network only
needs an intermediate graph state to determine an action.
Figure 12: The stepwise process of generating molecules.
First, a graph is given as input with
unconnected scaffolds. Message passing is performed, and an action is generated. The graph is
updated based on the action and a reward is given.
The GCPN takes graph (G) as input and G is concatenated with unconnected scaffolds (C). The
scaffolds consist of atom types allowed in their model. This can be extended further by adding more
complex scaffolds such as a benzene rings or groups used in GC models. The graph is encoded based
on the adjacency matrix, node feature matrix, and edge-conditioned adjacency tensor[60]. After the
message passing and calculation of the final node embedding, an action is generated based on the
policy. An action is made up of four action components sampled in order:
• a1) Node ID from existing graph
• a2) Node ID from existing graph or scaffolds
• a3) Edge Type
• a4) Stop the generation
The action space can be applied to create new molecules by selecting different nodes, scaffolds and
bond types in a1, a2 and a3, and not selection to terminate for action component a4. All actions
have their own MLP with Softmax activation function to generate the probability for the action
probability distribution. What differs between each action is their input into the MLP. For action
a1, the node embedding is used. For action a2, the node embedding is combined with the embedding
from the selected node from action a1. Action a3 uses the node embedding from the selected node
from a1 and a2 as its input. The last action a4 uses an aggregation of the node embeddings to
determine the probabilities for stopping the generation. GCPN uses policy training for optimizing
the policy network based on Proximal Policy Optimization[60]. The network is pre-trained with
expert policies as it leads to better stability and performance. The expert policies are viewed as
existing molecules. The expertise is incorporated by selecting a random molecule from a data-set.
The molecule consist of a molecular graph G and a connected subgraph G′ of G. At a state st, any
action adding an atom or a bond G \ G′ can be taken to generate the sampled molecule.
The final rewards are defined over a sum of domain specific rewards and adversarial rewards. The
domain specific rewards consist of synthetic accessibility (logP), drug-likeness (QED), and molecular

intermediate steps a small reward is given if an action does not violate valency rules, otherwise a
small negative penalty is given. As for the adversarial rewards, a GAN framework is used where a
reward is calculated from the discriminator network. The discriminator employs the same structure
as the policy network.

complexity of parsing the data scraped from PubChem. PubChem allows scraping of their website

molecules’ JSON files with the Python extension BeautifulSoup[46]. In total 267,780 compounds

times and contains typing errors as mentioned in section 3.3. The reasoning for having CAPEC as
the least trustworthy, was that it does not contain any references to where the data was collected
from. From both NIST and PubChem the source of the data is available. The ordering of reliability
was used to select which of the databases to trust for a molecule’s experimental value. For each
of the sources, if a molecule’s property was present multiple times, then the measurements were
combined using the mean. Before combining the data, each of the data-sets were filtered based on
three rules.
1. A molecule must contain a carbon atom.
2. A molecule must contain at least two heavy atoms (All atoms except for hydrogen).
3. A molecule can only contain the following atoms: C, N, O, F, P, S, Cl, Se, Br, I.
The reason for including selenium is that all other reactive nonmetals from the periodic table were
included. For PubChem, an extra filter was used to filter out molecules where the collected data
had a high variance when multiple experimental data was available. Finally, once the databases
were combined into a single data-set, then outlier detection was performed. The outlier detection
was based on the Inter-Quartile Range (IQR) method. The data is sorted and anything that was
further than 3.0·IQR from the median were cut-off. Values further away from the three times inter-
quartile-range were considered to be extreme outliers. In total, three of the collected properties were
selected for a proof of concept and to limit and manage the complexity of the work conducted, the
following three properties were considered for further analysis.
• Boiling Point
• Melting Point
• Enthalpy of Vaporization at Standard Condition
One thing should be noted, it is not only the three previous rules reducing the amount of data in
the individual data-set. Another influence was that the databases contain multiple measurements
for the same molecules, and additionally a few of the molecules cannot be loaded correctly as they
have errors in their SMILES strings. The detection of duplicate entries and if the same molecule
was present in multiple of the data-sets was detected using the library RDKit[33]. Through RDKit
a SMILES can be loaded into a molecule representation, and the molecular representation can be
converted back into a SMILES following canonical rules and isomeric rules.
The canonical flag
ensures all SMILES are generated using the same algorithm, as different canonical algorithms can
produce different canonical SMILES. The isomeric flags ensured, e.g. bond direction, is included
in the SMILES allowing differentiation between isomers. The data for the selected properties are
described further in the following sections.

4
Computer-Aided Molecular Design (CAMD): A
case study
The generate and test approach is adopted to perform CAMD and compound screening. Generate
and test consists of generating new molecules and then based on an established design space, prop-
erties of interest are calculated and compounds are eliminated that do not adhere to the constraints
set for the design. Additionally, the screening of molecules can be performed using in-silico. In-silico
is a term covering experiments performed on a computer or using computer simulations[11]. This
is a very flexible method, allowing to screen a huge amount of molecules. The generate and test
approach is applied on a case study, where property predicting models are described in section 4.1.
As for the generation of molecules three approaches are applied. In section 4.2 baseline generative
models are used to generate candidates, and in section 4.3, conditional models are used to further
guide the generation. The last method explored is Bayesian optimization applied to the latent space
of both baseline and conditional models in section 4.4.
The case study is based on a paper Integrated Entrainer Design for Extractive Distillation using
The Infinitely Sharp Splits and Computer-Aided Molecular Design[2]. The design problem is formu-
lated as a CAMD procedure using mixed-integer Nonlinear programming (MINLP) to find the best
entrainer with regard to a set of process performance metrics. The extractive distillation process is
used to separate non-ideal mixtures with azeotropes present in the distillation column. Azeotropes
are a mixture of two or more liquids whose ratio relative to each other can not be changed by
performing a simple distillation. The vapor produced when the mixture is heated has the same
concentration ratio, thereby making it impossible to separate the liquids beyond a specific composi-
tion. The entrainer is added to the mixture changing the relative volatility and this can form a new
azeotrope with either of the components in the mixture. In the case study it is not desired that a new
azeotrope is created but this is not possible to account for in this thesis. The goal of the distillation
is to separate acetone-methanol using heavy entrainers. This mixture exhibits a minimum boiling
point around 328K with an acetone composition near 0.79 at 1 atm[2]. The entrainer is selected
based on it being liquid and that it is the compound with the highest boiling point. Enthalpy of
vaporization is an additional suggested constraint which was chosen in the original paper to account
for the energy requirement for the vaporization of the entrainer[2]. The distillation column for the
process can be seen in figure 18, where the top product in A is acetone and in B is methanol.
Figure 18: Flow of a typical extractive distillation process. The image is from the paper on the case
study[2].
==PAGE:38==
32
In the work by Aouichaoui et al.[2] the process of designing an entrainer is divided into multiple
steps
• Structural Constraints
• Pure component property constraint
• Mixture property constraints
• Process Performance Evaluation
In the Structural constraints, molecules are generated using functional groups similar to what is
found in group contributions. From these groups all possible candidates are generated that follow
the constraint of having the correct valency, number of times a group can occur in the molecule,
and the min and max amount of groups. Afterwards, in the Pure component property constraint
step, the valid candidates from step one are filtered based on required property constraints. The
properties are predicted using GC models. The properties used are melting point, boiling point and
enthalpy of vaporization. The last two steps are not possible in the context of the current work,
as mixture properties and thermodynamic phase equilibrium are not considered. The idea behind
generate and test is that a larger area of the molecular space can be explored. The requirement for
the pure compound constraints are as follows,
• Tfus < 298K
• 368K < Tb < 500K
• ∆H◦
vap < 67 kJ
mol
According to the paper[2] the compounds were exclusively made up of C, H, and/or O. The
groups considered were CH3, CH2, CH, C, OH, CH3COO, HCO, OCH, CH2O, COO, CH2COO,
COOH, and HCOO. Using MINLP produced a total of 480 structural compliant compounds and
340 of the candidates satisfied the pure compound property constraints.

4.2.1
GraphINVENT
GraphInvent[39] is implemented using PyTorch and uses the teacher’s forcing method to train the
neural network. GraphINVENT creates a by predicting one action at a time from the action prob-
ability distribution (APD). In order to make this possible, the data-set has to be preprocessed by
creating a canonical deconstruction path for each molecule. As mentioned in the published paper,
the preprocessing for MOSES took around 5.7 CPU days. The preprocessing time is a big issue with
the model and in order to use it for the case study, the preprocessing had to be optimized.
In the preprocessing step, the authors generate deconstructed molecules in batches. The decon-
struction is performed using a BFS (Breadth First Search)[39] algorithm, where the first atom of
the molecule that is visited is the one numbered 0 by RDKit. Using canonicalized SMILES, the
generated graph by RDKit will always result in the same numbering of bonds and atoms, as the
algorithm is deterministic. When deconstructing molecules, it is possible to generate the same action
from two different molecules. As an example, if we consider the SMILES CCCCC and CCCCN the
first four actions to reconstruct the molecules are the same, requiring the generation four connected
carbon atoms. Storing the same actions multiple times increases the training-set size, and instead
==PAGE:45==
4.2
Baseline Generative Methods
39
can be combined into a single action representing the action for both molecules. In their algorithm,
the deconstruction is stored in an array, and for every new action generated in the batch, the array
of actions is iterated over to see if the action has already been generated for the batch. This results
in many additional comparisons in the array than necessary, and increase in time as the array of
actions grow.
To prevent this, an optimization was performed by having an additional hash-map with the
hashed actions as the key and the index where in the array the action is stored.
This allows
looking up the actions in amortized O(1) time. This change improves on one of the key issues with
the implementation that even a small data-set takes a long time to preprocess. As a result, the
preprocessing time of MOSES was reduced to < 1 CPU days on a Ryzen 2600X with 6 cores up to
4.2 GHz. The preprocessed data of MOSES results in an 81.7 GB training set, 0.6 GB validation
set and 10.0 GB test set.

4.2.3
VAE
Based on experience and testing public available methods, a set of new VAEs are created. The models
are implemented using PyTorch. One issue in the literature is the focus on only the three metrics
of validity, uniqueness, and novelty. The key metric often forgotten is reconstruction, otherwise
the models are only learning a distribution over the training-set. This might allow the models to
generate new novel molecules, but they struggle to reconstruct the training data. This metric is very
important in order to to optimize on the latent space of the models using e.g. Bayesian optimization.
One could question if the models are actually learning the molecular space if they are not able to
reconstruct training data, and instead are just learning a distribution over the training data. This is
observed for the CVAE as it might be able to produce molecules, but it struggles with reconstructing
encoded molecules. VAE has a tendency to suffer from posterior collapse. Posterior collapse is where
posterior parameters are too weak or noisy, resulting in the decoder starting to ignore the latent
representation z sampled from the posterior qϕ(z|x). Suggested reasoning for posterior collapse is
an imbalance between the KL-divergence and conditional log-likelihood in the ELBO[37], where the
log-likelihood is often underestimated. This underestimation becomes even higher with the use of
the teacher’s forcing method. As an example, the decoder would likely have sampled the wrong
token at a time-step ti thereby ruining the predictions in time-step ti+1. Using the teacher’s forcing
method this is not the case, as the true character at time-step ti is given as input at time-step
ti+1. A suggested solution is introducing a KL-Annealing schedule to an alternative VAE called the
β-VAE[22]. The β-VAE adds a scalar to the KL divergence and the loss function is as follows
L = Eqθ(z|x)[log pϕ(x|z)] −βKLD[qθ(z|x)∥p(z)]
(21)
This has previously shown great promise for molecular generation in the work by Yan et al.[58],
where a beta = 0.1 is used. Though, authors forget to mention in the paper that they are bounding
the log variance predicted by the encoder. From the published code[57], a negation is applied to the
absolute value of the log variance. This results in the log variance becoming negative and is equivalent
to the standard deviation being bounded to < 1 because of the nature of the logarithm. In the same
paper, the authors point out others have previously used a different ϵ for the reparameterization
trick with σ = 0.01. Thereby reducing the variance introduced into the latent representation.
A solution to detecting posterior collapse is to add a measurement called latent quality. The
quantity is estimated by encoding a molecule 10 times, and each latent representation is decoded 10

times stochastically. The latent quality is a measure of the percentage of the decoding that results in
the original molecule before the encoding. To estimate a model’s latent quality, 10,000 molecules are
selected randomly from the training- and test-set. The selected molecules are encoded and decoded
as described.
For the following models, the reconstruction loss is calculated using cross entropy and the models
are trained with a β = [1.0, 0.1, 0.01]. The reasoning for β value of 0.01 is because the reconstruction
loss is even further underestimated for the following trained models.
The issue stems from the
reconstruction loss being averaged over the length of the SMILES tokens to not penalize the neural
network more for larger molecules compared to smaller molecules. With a very small β, the model
is also required to be trained for additional epochs to converge. The models with a β = [1.0, 0.1]
are trained for 50 epochs, while the β = 0.01 is trained for 100 epochs. The model trained with
KL-Annealing starts out with β = 0 and it is increased to the target value of β over 25 epochs.

As for the implementation of the models, one can choose to convert SMILES to a molecular

to the system in the form of additional memory as seen by GraphINVENT and CVAE. Instead all
the models are converting the SMILES in the model itself. This adds additional overhead while

Additionally, clipping of the gradient was used to prevent the exploding gradient problem. All the
models are trained on the DTU HPC clusters using Nvidia Tesla V100 GPUs. The models are
trained using a train, validation and test sets with a (0.8, 0.1, 0.1) split. The reconstruction loss and
KL-divergence loss is not a useful metric to report the performance of a generative model and is also
the reason it is not published in the covered literature in section 2. Instead for every epoch a small
sample of 100 molecules are generated from the latent space sampled using z = N(0, 1). The latent
space samples are decoded in two ways, the first way is to select the next character stochastically and
the second is to sample the next character with the highest probability. These two metrics help to see
if the performance is improving and additionally the second metric sampling the highest probability
character can indicate if a posterior collapse happened. If a posterior collapse occurred then the
model is learning a distribution and ignoring z resulting in a low amount of unique molecules.

run-time on GDB-9 which is ∼15 times slower per epoch compared to the RNN based encoder
results in approximate epoch time of ∼4 hours on MOSES and 3.5 days on eMolecules.

would be equivalent to the atom type being optional. It should be noted the H is not present a

4.4
Exploring the Latent Space
In this section, the latent space from two models are explored further, and Bayesian optimization
is applied to try an alternative approach to generate molecules with desired properties. The first
model is a baseline VAE model trained on GDB-9 and the second model is a transfer learning model
trained on BP-MP-HV. The models were picked based on having a high latent quality. To further
visualize the latent space, there are two main options, using Principal Component Analysis (PCA)
or t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding[55] (t-SNE). t-SNE is an unsupervised non-linear
technique for data exploration/visualization. The algorithm for creating the distribution works by
optimizing similarity measures between pairs in the high dimensional space and low-dimensional
space. For high dimensional space the similarity measure is based on euclidean distance and in low-
dimensional space the similarity is measured using a Student-t distribution using KL Divergence[55].
The resulting t-SNE can be controlled using perplexity and can be interpreted as a smooth measure
of the effective number of neighbors, usually with a value between 5 and 50[55]. PCA is not a good
option for visualizing the latent space in this case. With the two chosen models, with a latent size
of 256, each principal component was responsible for explaining around ∼0.45% of the variance.
Choosing the top two principal components would only be able to explain around ∼1% of the
variance in the latent points. Based on this, t-SNE was used to visualize the latent space. For
t-SNE you cannot calculate how much variance is explained as t-SNE is a non-linear projection. In
following visualizations, 1,000 data points from the training-set were selected at random, and each
SMILES was encoded ten times resulting in 10,000 latent points. In figure 32, the latent space can
be seen for the baseline model, where the latent points are colored based on the number of atoms of
a specific type in the individual molecules. Additionally, one visualization is based on the length of
the SMILES.

used by Griffiths et al. had a bad performance. Based on the results in this thesis, there seems to
be no reason to add extra complexity in the form of e.g. the Constrained Bayesian optimization.

5
Conclusion
In this thesis, the goal was to explore what existing AI methods have been applied to the molecu-
lar design problem, and evaluate how these methods perform in scarce data applications compared
to existing molecular design which is based on the concept of group contribution and solving a
mathematical optimization problem using MINLP. In conclusion, it has been shown that a range of
architectures have been applied in the field of drug discovery where architectures such a variational
autoencoders, generative adversarial networks, graph neural network have been applied using tech-
niques such as transfer learning, conditional models, Bayesian optimization, and semi-supervised
learning.
To further compare these techniques to a solution using MINLP, an entrainer design problem
reported by Aouichaoui et al.[2] was chosen, where the original problem was decomposed into four
subproblems of which current AI methods can solve two of them. The use of AI methods in molecular
design at current state are not able to fully replace existing MINLP techniques, mainly calculations
related to phase equilibria. For the case study, a generate and test approach was adopted to find
candidates that followed pure compound property and structure constraints but models were not
able to solve mixture constraints or process evaluation as they essentially relate to phase equilibria.
To compare the performance of the solution using MINLP and AI models, several AI techniques
were implemented and tested to measure the performance with scarce data. To perform the testing
of constraints, graph neural networks were applied for property prediction for Tb, Tfus, and ∆H◦
vap.
The implemented models were trained using additional collected data from NIST and PubChem and
had R2 ≥0.84 on all test-sets. The models did not perform as well as state-of-the-art which had
a performance of their data-set of R2 ≥0.99. As an extra verification step of the collected data,
models were trained on DIPPR alone and compared to models trained on NIST + PubChem. The
models performed on similarly for both Tb and Tfus with the difference for R2 was ∼1%. This
validated further that the AI models with extra collected data were good enough for testing the
property constraints.
As for the molecular generation, three categories of models were implemented: baseline, condi-
tional, and conditional with transfer learning using a VAE architecture. The baseline models trained
on the custom data-set produced around 2,000 out of the 30,000 generated molecules following the
constraints. The best baseline models were trained on a GDB-9 resulting in around 4,000 out of the
30,000 molecules following all constraints. To further improve these results, conditional models were
implemented with structural information in a novel approach. This approach can be concluded that
it allowed generating molecules with desired structures. This was confirmed by comparing baseline
models and conditional models trained on eMolecules. The baseline models produced no or almost
no molecules following the constraints, while the nest conditional model trained on eMolecules was
capable of producing around 3,500 out of the 30,000 molecules following the constraints. These re-
sults are very good and could be researched further by exploring using molecular groups from group
contributions models for conditional generation. As for the other conditional models trained with a
combination of property data and structural data, they had worse model metrics resulting in ranges
of 51−55% of the generated molecules being valid, and for the model trained with the least amount
of data, only 56% of valid molecules were unique. These metrics are lower than the baseline models,
where up to 98% of generated molecules were valid and over 90% of the valid molecules were unique.
Though, even with worse model metrics, the conditional models were able to perform similar or
better on the case study than the models trained on GDB-9. The best model produced 5,066 out
of 30,000 molecules following the constraints. This is an improvement of ∼24.5% compared to the
best baseline model, and it shows generative models are capable of working with a low amount of
data.
==PAGE:69==
63
As a step towards improving the conditional model, transfer learning was applied by making the
models learn property information during the transfer learning. The initial models were trained on
eMolecules. The resulting models had a significantly higher percentage of molecules being valid and
unique. The models produced in the range of 77 −95% valid molecules which is an improvement in
the range of ∼40 −72%. Applied to the case study, the transfer learning models were able to out-
perform their conditional counterparts with the best model producing 5,272 out of 30,000 molecules
following all constraints, which is an improvement of ∼5%.
Comparing the results of the AI models to the results from the original design problem solved
using MINLP. The best AI models produced ∼1400% more molecules following the constraint,
and the generative process using AI models can be repeated multiple times in order to generate
even more candidates. This shows that AI models can provide a larger list of candidates, and the
previous CAMD tools have limited generative power. Though not all approaches were a success.
Bayesian optimization on the latent space performed suboptimal for the implemented models. This
comes down to the complexity of having many dimensions in the latent space and multi objective
optimization is is more tedious than single objective optimization.
Based on the work in this thesis, there are multiple ways to further test generative AI models
and improve upon the work in this thesis. The suggested future work is as follows.
• Applying semi-supervised learning on conditional models similar to Kang et al.[27]. It would
be interesting exploring the implementation, thereby allowing the use of existing property
prediction networks as an initial state.
• The performance of the property models and the correctness of the collected data can probably
be improved further with attention directed to the data curation, however such tasks must be
performed by domain experts.
• Testing further the incorporation of structure data in conditional generative models with e.g.
groups from group contributions and exploring if it is possible to control the exact amount of
atom types or groups present in the generated molecules.
• Further investigate the graph based encoder by exploring more GNNs and hyperparameters.
• Exploring the possibility of adding groups from group contributions as scaffolds in combination
with the work of You et al.[60].
