Abstract
Combining neural networks and alpha-beta search has long been a goal of researchers
and chess programmers alike. It was largely believed that neural networks were too com-
putationally expensive to be used in the context of a brute-force search. While neural
networks were more accurate than the handcrafted evaluation functions used, the trade-
off in search speed was detrimental to playing strength.
However, the introduction of
eﬀiciently updatable neural networks (NNUE) in 2020 proved the before mentioned was
possible, alongside a significant gain in performance.
NNUE uses supervised learning,
and is trained on data generated by other chess programs in a multi-stage process. Data
sets are stored in the BINPACK format, which utilizes chains of positions to achieve high
compression. NNUE uses sparse input features, incremental updating and quantization
alongside CPU inference to reduce the computational impact of a large neural network.
We implement NNUE in our own program Manni, and through a tournament consisting
of several strong chess programs, find that NNUE results in significant performance gains
versus both other programs and its own non-NNUE version. We find that training loss is
only weakly correlated with playing strength, which reduces testing accuracy and causes
training to much more time-consuming.
In our individual contribution study, we find
that quantization of weights, allowing for the use of vector instructions, is by far the most
important aspect of search speed.

1
Introduction
The goal of this thesis is to describe how eﬀiciently updatable neural networks (NNUE) can
be used in computer chess programs (often called chess engines) to enhance their playing
strength. Specifically, NNUE acts as the evaluation function in an Alpha-beta search.
NNUE has several novel concepts that makes it interesting in both computer chess and
machine learning.
Unfortunately, NNUE has largely gone under the radar academically. This means that
no formal work describing NNUE has been created, and serves as a large barrier of entry.
Most information about NNUE sits in GitHub repositories and chat servers. Another goal
of this thesis is to collect and describe information about NNUE in a single source, such
that the technology can achieve a greater outreach.
This chapter will serve as an introduction to the concepts related to this thesis. We will
begin by giving a brief overview of chess and its rules. We will then proceed with a review
of the computer chess field, starting with its inception in 1950. Next, the core search
algorithm used alongside NNUE, Alpha-Beta search, will be described. Afterwards, we
will give a brief introduction to neural networks, how they learn and how quantization can
be used to enhance their inference speed. Finally, we will introduce our own chess engine
Manni, which is the program which will receive the NNUE implementation.
We must also note that this work builds in part on the author’s bachelor’s thesis Nielsen
2021. The bachelor’s thesis goes into great detail as to how a modern chess program is
created and the components it consists of. We believe that this thesis can stand on its
own, but we will omit some details as to not repeat the work done in Nielsen 2021.
1.1
The game of chess
This will be a brief introduction to the rules of chess. While most readers will be familiar
with chess, there are some important rules that are mostly only known by habitual players
of chess.
Chess is a game of two players, hence pieces are of two different colors: white and black.
White always has the first move, but otherwise both have the exact same conditions for
winning the game. There is no information hidden to either player, meaning that chess
has perfect information. Chess is also a zero-sum game, which means that any advantage
for White is an equally large disadvantage for Black.
Chess is played on a 8 × 8 board for a total of 64 squares, with the color of each square
alternating. The starting position of chess shows the 6 different pieces which are available:
pawn, knight, bishop, rook, queen and king. Both the raw board and the starting position
can be seen on Figure 1.1. The chess board with pieces present is most often referred
to as the position. The board can be divided in ranks and files. A rank is a horizontal
line across the entire board, similarly to a row in a table. This could for example be the
squares a1 to h1. Conversely, files are vertical lines, e.g. a1 through a8.
The ultimate goal of chess is to checkmate the king. A player is checkmated (and has
therefore lost the game) when their king is in check (i.e., being under attack) and no
possible move can bring the king out of check, such as by removing the attacker, moving
the king to safety, or blockading the attack using another piece.

1.1.1
The pieces of chess
Each player starts with 16 pieces in total: 8 pawns, 2 knights, 2 bishops, 2 rooks, 1 queen
and 1 king. Technically, the term piece excludes the pawn, with chessman being more
correct when referring to all pieces on the board. However, in practice both terms are
often used interchangeably. This work will exclusively use piece to refer to any possible
unit on the board, including pawns.
The pieces currently on the board are referred to as the material. This is often extended
to the material balance, which denotes the balance of the pieces on the board. If Black is
down a queen, the material balance is in favor of White, for example.
Pawn
The pawn is the weakest piece in chess. However, it is also the most numerous, with
each player starting with 8 pawns. This means that the strength of a pawn lies in their
numbers. They can effectively control the board and defend both unoccupied squares and
other pieces (including themselves). A lot of the complexity of playing chess well can be
grounded in control and management of pawns, both ones own and the opponents.
The pawn also has the most complex move set. The pawn can only move a single square
forward toward the opposite side of the board. The pawn can only capture pieces diag-
onally, but only in the same direction that it moves, and the opponent piece must be
directly adjacent. The move set of the pawn is shown on Figure 1.2. The right pawn in
the figure also shows that unmoved pawns can potentially move two squares. This opens
up for a concept called en passant in chess.
En passant (French for in passing) refers to a specific mechanism of capture available only
to pawns, and further only in the situation where a pawn has moved two squares. Say
that the g2 pawn on Figure 1.2 moves two squares to g4. The square g3 is now implicitly
marked as an en passant square, meaning that opposing pawns can capture the g4 pawn
as if it actually stood on g3. The effect of this mechanism is that executing the right
to move two squares does not allow opposing pawns to skip past each other and escape
capture.

The pawn has a final rule: promotion. Any pawn that reaches the back-rank (rank 1 for
Black, rank 8 for White) can promote to any chosen piece besides the king. Promoting to
the strongest piece, the queen, is most beneficial, but in rare cases underpromoting to for

Knight
The knight has the unique ability to ”jump” over pieces in an L-shaped pattern (see
Figure 1.3). Additionally, the knight captures in the same way as it moves. The knight
has few movement options when standing in a corner, and is typically seen as strongest
near the center of the board.
Slider pieces
The slider pieces are bishop, rook and queen. The name comes from how they move, i.e.,
how they ”slide” across the board. The rook can move any given amount of squares in a
cardinal direction (north, east, south, west), while the bishop can do the same but in an
inter-cardinal direction (north-east, south-east, south-west, north-west). We can also say
that the rook moves along ranks and files, while the bishop moves along diagonals. The
queen can be seen as a combination of the bishop and rook, possessing the ability to move
in both cardinal and inter-cardinal directions. Crucially, a slider piece can only move in
a given direction until it is blocked by another piece. The movement of all slider pieces
(including when a blocking piece is present) is shown on Figure 1.4.
Because the slider pieces have the most flexibility in terms of movement, they are also the
most powerful. This is especially true for the queen, whose strength is reinforced by each
side only having one each. Bishops are universally seen as weaker than rooks, as it turns
out that moving diagonally is the less impactful of the two.

The king has a move set identical to the queen, except that it can only move a single
square. This means that the king can be a powerful asset if played correctly, albeit it
must be played with great care to avoid getting checkmated. If the king is currently under
attack by a piece, the king is said to be in check. When the king is in check, the only legal

The king has an additional, special move called castling. Castling essentially swaps the
place of the king and a friendly rook. This is the only move in chess which can move two
pieces at the same time. Castling can either be short or long depending on which rook is
castled with. An example of short castling is shown on Figure 1.5. Short castling moves
the king towards the right rook while long castling moves the king towards the left rook.
These moves are also called king-side castling and queen-side castling respectively. A short
castle moves both the king and the rook two squares towards each other. A long castle is
the same, but the rook moves three squares instead.
A number of conditions must be present for legal castling:
1. The king nor the rook must not have moved from their start position.
2. The king must be not in check.
3. There must be no pieces between the king and the rook.
4. The traversed path of the king must not be under attack by any pieces. This includes
the final destination square.

1.1.3
Checkmate
If the king is in check and cannot escape check during the immediate turn, the player is
checkmated. This is the ultimate goal of any agent playing chess. In a tournament setting,
winning the game with checkmate awards the checkmating player with 1 point.
1.1.4
Draw adjudication
A chess match can be drawn through a multitude of ways. Draw by stalemate occurs
when the king has no legal moves, but is not in check. This can be seen as a ”failed”
checkmate, and is often performed accidentally by beginners.
The match can also be
drawn by insuﬀicient material in cases where checkmate is impossible, for example if both
kings are the only pieces left on the board.
There are two rules in place for preventing chess games from continuing in perpetuity:
three-fold repetition and the fifty-move rule. Three-fold repetition says that if the same
position has appeared three times, the match is drawn. The fifty-move rule says that if
the last capture or pawn move was more than 50 moves ago, the match is drawn.
In a tournament setting, a draw rewards each player with 1/2 points.

1.2
Review of computer chess
Computer chess is older than realized by most. Leonardo Torres (December 28, 1852 -
December 18, 1936) developed an electromechanical machine capable of playing chess in
1910 (Randell 1982). Torres’ work is arguably the world’s first chess computer, although
it was severely limited in both playing strength and capabilities. It was only able to play
the king and rook versus bare king endgame, and could only play as White, with further
limitations on the starting squares.
Not long after the development of the first computer, Alan Turing predicted in 1945
that computers would be suitable for playing chess (Turing 2005). The field of computer
chess was formally launched with the seminal paper Shannon 1950. In this work, Shannon
described how a computer might play chess, and relevant chess-playing heuristics. Modern
chess programs still build on many of the concepts described in the original paper. Shannon
also reasoned about the overall design of chess programs and the issues they face, and
divided them into two types:
1. Type A: Explore every possible variation (series of moves) up to length n. Then
employ a carefully crafted function which is capable of choosing the best variation.

2. Type B: Explore selectively and only the most promising variations, varying n
depending on the apparent strength of the given variation.
Type A is essentially a brute force search, which consider every possible move. Shannon
considered this type infeasible due to the high combinatorial complexity of chess. Fa-
mously, Shannon gave a conservative lower bound on the number of chess positions that
must be searched to find the best move at 10120. This is known today as the Shannon
Number.
Shannon was more interested in type B due to above, and saw type B as the more human
approach. Humans can play strong chess while searching only a miniscule fraction of the
chess game tree, by intelligently choosing which variations to consider. Unfortunately,
type B programs are also much more diﬀicult to create. You are essentially tasked with
creating a program that mimics human intuition and problem-solving. Shannon did not
create a concrete computer algorithm that could play chess. It was instead Turing that
devised an algorithm, although he lacked a computer which could run it, and was forced
to run the algorithm by hand with pen and paper in 1953 (Turing 2004).
Building on the ideas of Shannon 1950, there were several attempts to build a type B
program in the 1960s, notably Newell, Shaw, and Simon 1958, Bernstein and V. Roberts
1988 (originally published 1958) and Kotok 1962. It was also during this period that chess
began to be considered the drosophila of artificial intelligence by contemporary researchers
(Ensmenger 2011). The drosophila, also known as the common fruit fly, has served as the
model organism for the biomedical field for more than a century, playing a key role in a
wide variety of research (Tolwinski 2017). There was hope that chess could serve the same
role for the field of artificial intelligence (AI) and function as a stepping stone for more
advanced forms of AI. This meant that a significant amount of resources was allocated to
research computer chess, which in turn had a large part in shaping the field.
As computers grew more powerful and research advanced during the 1970s, type A pro-
grams proved to be far more powerful than type B programs. Type A programs were
easier to implement, more predictable, easier to reason about and their brute-force ap-
proach scaled better with hardware improvements.
The brute-force approach used is
specifically called alpha-beta search.
Kaissa (Adelson-Velskiy, Arlazarov, and Donskoy
1975) and Belle (Condon and Thompson 1988) are examples of such programs. Belle was
created in the early 70s and was continuously improved throughout the early 80s. These
programs saw success by incorporating ideas of type B programs, introducing selective
search algorithms in their brute-force search. Creating these programs requires extensive
domain knowledge, and many handcrafted features.
Despite decades of research, chess programs were still incapable of beating top-level human
players.
The closest attempt so far was in 1977, where the program Chess 4.7 beat
international master (the title directly below grand master) David Levy in a single game,
although it still lost the tournament 3.5 to 1.5 points (J. R. 1978). Deep Thought (Hsu
et al. 1990b) was the first chess program to beat a grand master, playing against Bent
Larsen in 1988. Deep Thought, thought to be the strongest chess engine at the time,
played two matches against then reigning world champion Garry Kasparov, but lost in
both cases (Hsu et al. 1990a). The team behind Deep Thought was sponsored by IBM and
created the successor Deep Blue (Hsu, Campbell, and Hoane 1995). In 1996, Deep Blue
was the first computer chess program to beat a chess world champion, defeating Garry
Kasparov 4-2 (Munakata 1996).
The defeat of Garry Kasparov was a monumental shift in the field.

1.2.1
Machine learning and chess
While the computer chess field raced on, there was an adjacent effort to introduce machine-
learning techniques, such as neural networks, as early as the 1990s. This effort was largely
considered unsuccessful, as many techniques failed to come even close to state-of-the-art
chess programs.
NeuroChess used a small neural network with 165 hidden neurons for learning an evalu-
ation function using temporal-difference learning (Thrun 1994). NeuroChess was a novel
piece of work, but ultimately failed in matching contemporary chess programs. The pro-
gram KnightCap further built upon temporal-difference learning (Baxter, Tridgell, and
Weaver 2000).
Notably, the authors considered the use of neural networks in evalua-
tion functions as infeasible in alpha-beta search, deeming the computational cost to be
excessive.
KnightCap reached human master level, but failed to reach state-of-the-art
like NeuroChess.
Efforts using Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCST) likewise failed when
compared to their alpha-beta based variants (Arenz 2012).
The first program to achieve grandmaster level performance was DeepChess (David, Ne-
tanyahu, and Wolf 2016). DeepChess used a Deep Neural Network (DNN) as its evaluation
function alongside alpha-beta search. The program was trained using supervised learning
on expert-level human games.
The first breakthrough came when Silver et al. 2017 introduced their program AlphaZero.
AlphaZero used a complex dual-headed policy and value DNN, alongside MCTS. Criti-
cally, AlphaZero was trained using reinforcement learning, meaning that the handcrafted
and domain-specific knowledge typically required for creating strong chess programs was
unnecessary. AlphaZero reached state-of-the-art performance in go, chess and shogi, the
first of its kind to do so.
AlphaZero is often considered a type B program, typically
searching only 50-100,000 nodes per second.
While the authors of AlphaZero quickly moved on to different domains, an open-source
version of AlphaZero, called LeelaChessZero (Lc0) was created in 2018 (The LCZero Au-
thors n.d.). Lc0 has continuously improved the AlphaZero algorithm, further enhancing it
with attention-based policies (Vaswani et al. 2023) and Monte-Carlo Graph Search (Czech,
Korus, and Kersting 2020).

Modern computer chess
Since chess engines have reached superhuman performance, there has been reduced aca-

diligent chess engine hobbyists, and are more akin to small, incremental progress rather
large intermittent leaps.
The primary drivers behind progress are the two programs Stockfish (The Stockfish de-

1.3
Minimax
This section is an excerpt from Nielsen 2021 with small modifications.
Minimax is an adversarial tree search algorithm that can be described succinctly as max-
imizing the worst-case scenario. In other words, given optimal play from the opponent,
what is the best possible action? It receives its name from the fact in a two-player game
there are two roles: a maximizing (abbr. Max) player and a minimizing (abbr. Min)
player. The algorithm is simple and intuitive, which makes it diﬀicult to credit a single
author with its invention, although it is most commonly given to Neumann 1928.
Take some arbitrary two-player game, with the only constraints being that it must be a
zero-sum game with perfect information. The game has the following possible outcomes:
player one wins, draw and player two wins. These can be represented by integer values
as S = {1, 0, −1}, and the outcome (or score) of any given game is denoted s ∈S. Say
that two players, p1 (Max) and p2 (Min), commence this game and that p1 starts. Since
p1 wants to the win the game, they would like to maximize s.
p2 will on the other
hand attempt to minimize s, since any outcome that is unfavorable for p1 will be equally
favorable for p2 (since we assume the game is zero-sum). The value of s is found through
a depth-first search, since a game of this type can easily be modeled as a game tree. In
this tree, each node represents the current game state. The edges between nodes are legal
moves available at the current game state. We first search down to the leaves of the tree
and propagate each value back up to the root. Exactly how the values are propagated
depends on whether the side to move is maximizing or minimizing. At each node, Max
always chooses the child with the largest value. The same is true for Min, except that the
smallest child is chosen instead.
This is best shown through a concrete example (Figure 1.6). At each level of the tree,
a label indicates whether the side to move is maximizing or minimizing s. Each node
contains a value, and reflects the expected outcome by reaching said node. As Minimax
uses depth-first search, we should not start our example at the root, but at the leaves.
Note that in this example, the leaves are also terminal nodes. A terminal node has no
children, and as a consequence signifies the end of the game. This makes it possible to
assign a value to each leaf. We dive straight down to the left-most terminal node, and find
a winning game state and value it as 1 (Figure 1.6). This value will be propagated to the
parent node, but not before the value of its sibling is found. The sibling node is a losing
position, and receives the value -1.
The parent node represents the Max player, who naturally chooses the left-most child, as
it contains the largest value (Figure 1.7). We use the same approach for finding the value
of the parent’s sibling.

1.4
Alpha-Beta Search
This section is an excerpt from Nielsen 2021 with small modifications.
The most important enhancement to Minimax is Alpha-Beta search (also called Alpha-
Beta pruning) (Hart and Edwards 1963). Principally, the algorithm functions like Minimax
with one key difference. The exact positional score is now bounded through α, the lower
bound, and β, the upper bound. The maximizing player is assured to score at least α,
whereas the minimizing player can score at most β.
If the maximizing player finds a
move to give a score s and s ≥β, that subtree can immediately be pruned. We know
that the opponent has a way of ensuring s = β, and will therefore never let this move
be played. Likewise, if the minimizing player finds that s ≤α, that subtree can too be
pruned.
Most importantly, this type of pruning is safe. This means that Alpha-Beta
finds the exact same moves as Minimax, while skipping inconsequential branches (Hart
and Edwards 1963). Note that the following description of Alpha-Beta search is almost
exclusively from the perspective of the maximizing player. Like in Minimax, the functional
difference between Min and Max is negligible.
The initial Alpha-Beta search at the root is called with the bounds α = −∞and β = +∞.
This can be interpreted as the bounds being unknown. It is important to realize that for
Alpha-Beta to accept a move as the best, that move must give a score that satisfies
α < s < β.
If s ≤α, we already found a move that is at least as good or better.
Whenever s ≥β we achieve a β-cutoff (also called fail-high) and that subtree is pruned.
As mentioned earlier, the opponent already knows a way to avoid this move. Conversely,
for the minimizing player this is called an α-cutoff when s ≤α. The move that raises α
to its maximal value is the best move in that position. Conversely, when no moves raise
α we fail-low. No moves in this position are adequate to see play.

Donor chess engine
In order to implement NNUE, we first need a powerful chess engine which can receive the
NNUE implementation. The choice of chess engine is named Manni, and can be seen as
an extension of Exo from Nielsen 2021 with some of the most lacking components replaced
with the implementation from Stockfish (The Stockfish developers n.d.). Manni does not
have state-of-the-art performance, but has all the most important concepts of such an
engine. We therefore believe that Manni is a good candidate to demonstrate the eﬀicacy
of NNUE and its interactions with modern search extensions.
The extensions to Alpha-Beta search exhibited by Manni are described below briefly.
Readers interested in more detailed explanations are encouraged to read Nielsen 2021.
Note that readers unfamiliar with state-of-the-art chess algorithms will likely find it diﬀi-
cult to understand the sections below. Describing these algorithms in detail is out of scope
for this thesis and not essential for understanding NNUE. Hopefully this will also make
it clear that a strong chess program is the result of a wide variety of techniques working
together, and that NNUE is small part of a greater whole.
1.5.1
Alpha-Beta Search
The foundation of the chess engine is Alpha-Beta search. This functions in accordance
with what was written previously, where large parts of the search tree can pruned without
sacrificing optimality.
The classical implementation of Alpha-Beta Search is often called Fail-Hard. In Fail-Hard,
it always holds that for a score s, α < s < β. In other words, α and β are hard bounds.
Manni uses the version of Alpha-Beta Search called Fail-Soft (Fishburn 1983), where s is
not always bounded by α and β. It has been shown that Fail-Soft searches fewer nodes on
average than Fail-Hard.
1.5.2
Iterative Deepening
Standard Alpha-Beta search finds the best move by constructing a search tree of depth d.
Instead of searching directly to depth d, Iterative Deepening (Slate and Atkin 1977) first
searches at d′ = 1, increasing by 1 until d′ = d. This has the effect of combining the benefits
of depth-first search and breadth-first search. One might think that the overall search load
would be greater, but it has been shown that utilizing the principal variation (the series of
moves that led to finding the best move), move ordering can improve dramatically. This

means that Iterative Deepening actually searches fewer nodes than standard Alpha-Beta
Search.
1.5.3
Transposition Table
In chess, the same position can be reached through a differing series of moves. Moves
that lead to the same position are called transpositions. Transpositions are a significant
hamper to the eﬀiciency of Alpha-Beta search, as the same positions will be searched
several times during the search. A transposition table (Greenblatt, Eastlake, and Crocker
1967) works by caching already seen positions and their result such that they can be
reused during the search. Move ordering can also be greatly improved if the best move is
stored alongside the evaluation. Transposition tables are one of the largest improvements
possible in Alpha-Beta search. A downside of transposition tables is that draw detection
is decreased because of the Graph-History Interaction (Campbell 1985).
The transposition table implementation in Manni is almost identical to the one used in
Stockfish.
1.5.4
Quiescence Search
If we simply stop the search and evaluate nodes at search depth d, there is an extremely
high chance of playing obvious blunders due to the horizon effect (Berliner 1973). There
are many positions whereby increasing d by as little as 1, tactical mistakes can be avoided.
Quiescence search (Beal 1990) seeks to remedy this problem by ensuring that the position is
quiet before the evaluation function is called. Quiet positions contain no obvious captures
or checks.
Quiescence search is important in the context of NNUE, as this means that we can assume
that the neural network only has to evaluate quiet positions.
1.5.5
Principal Variation Search
Principal Variation Search (Marsland and Campbell 1982) works by assuming that our
move ordering is optimal. The first child of a node, of which its edge is assumed to be the
best move, are dubbed PV-nodes. PV-nodes are searched using the inherited αβ-bounds
as usual. At Non-PV-nodes a null-window search is performed instead, where the αβ-
bounds are reduced to αchild = αparent, βchild = αparent + 1. Reducing the search bounds
means that these nodes (which make up a majority of the tree) can be searched much
quicker. The downside is that if the reduced window search provides an αβ-cutoff, that
node must be researched with the true αβ-bounds, wasting computation.
1.5.6
Late Move Reduction
Principal Variation Search assumes that our move ordering is optimal to perform less work
at nodes that are more likely to contain bad moves. Late Move Reductions (Romstad 2007)
assumes that moves are sorted in descending order, where the first move is the best and
the last move is the worst. Moves are then searched with a reduced depth according to
the formula
Rm = log(d + 1) + log(n + 1),
where Rm is the reduction for move m, d is the current search depth and n is the index
of m in the sorted move list.
1.5.7
Null Move Pruning
Null Move Pruning is a pruning technique to significantly increase the number of αβ-
cutoffs at a low computational cost. It works by introducing a null move to prove that
a position is overwhelmingly advantageous for the side to move. If the side to move’s
position is so strong that even after simply passing their turn and doing nothing (i.e.

likely that the position is so strong that the opponent will never let that situation occur
in the game.
1.5.8
Internal Iterative Deepening
When a position is retrieved from the transposition table, it is not always the case that
the best move for the position is stored alongside it. When the best move is available,
move ordering is improved significantly at that node. This concept is the basis of Internal
Iterative Deepening (Anantharaman 1991). In the case where no best move was stored,
a reduced search at the node is performed in order to find a replacement best move. On
average, the best move found at a reduced depth is close to or is the best move, saving
computation when using for move ordering.
1.5.9
Magic bitboards
Move generation for slider pieces is notoriously slow since available moves strongly depend
on the current state of the board. Magic bitboards (Fenner and Levene 2008) uses a perfect
hash function to pre-calculate move sets for slider pieces for all possible configurations.
This is further enhanced by employing the PEXT hardware instruction available on x86-
CPUs (Nielsen 2021). Manni can generate approx. 100 million moves per second due to
magic bitboards and various hardware optimizations.
1.5.10
Move ordering heuristics
Besides the move-ordering techniques mentioned so far, there are three additional heuris-
tics used for sorting moves: MVV-LVA, SEE and Killer Heuristic.
MVV-LVA (Most Vulnerable Victim – Least Vulnerable Attacker) is the simplest move-
ordering heuristic used in Manni. The principle of MVV-LVA is that if a piece can capture
a piece more valuable than itself, it is likely a good move. For example, pawn captures
queen is almost always a great move. Even if the pawn is recaptured afterwards by the
opponent, significant material has still been gained. Queen captures pawn is potentially
a good move, but also risky, as losing a queen for a pawn is catastrophic.
SEE (static exchange evaluation) takes MVV-LVA a step further by also considering po-
tential recaptures (Reul 2010). When pieces are captured in chess, there is a good chance
that a capture chain will be initiated, where several pieces are traded off the board. SEE
can evaluate the total material gain of a capture chain, making it more accurate than
MVV-LVA.
The Killer Heuristic says that if a move produced an αβ-cutoff at one node, it is likely
also good at sibling nodes (Akl and Newborn 1977).
Killer moves are ordered before
MVV-LVA, but after SEE.
1.5.11
Hand-crafted evaluation
Evaluation functions are generally built as a combination of n sub-heuristics hi weighted
by wi. A sub-heurestic only considers one perspective at a time, and takes a position p and
the side to move c ∈{W, B} as arguments. An example could be a heuristic that counts
the material on the board or the safety of the king. Each sub-heuristic is calculated for
both perspectives and finally subtracted from each other. This allows one to determine
which perspective is stronger for that particular sub-heuristic. By combining these terms,
a final meta-heuristic H can be calculated:
H(p) = (h1(p, W) −h1(p, B)) · w1 + · · · + (hn(p, W) −hn(p, B)) · wn,
where the final evaluation of position p is in this case from the perspective of White. As
a shorthand, we define Hi(p) = hi(p, W) −hi(pB).

to have shallow architectures, which restricts the quantization error.

2
Concepts of NNUE
NNUE operates on three core principles:
1. Incremental updating
2. Input sparsity
3. Quantization
One of the primary goals of NNUE is inference speed. In computer chess, strong programs
evaluate millions of positions per second. Taking that into consideration, there exists a
clear trade-off between speed and accuracy in terms of playing strength. A slower evalua-
tion speed means that search tree depth is reduced, potentially causing the engine to miss
important tactics. A deep and complicated neural network can exhibit superior accuracy
to any hand-crafted evaluation function, but at an excessive cost.
A unique aspect of NNUE is that since inference speed is paramount, networks are run on
CPU without use of hardware accelerators like GPUs to avoid memory synchronization
overhead. This puts further constrains on network size and complexity, and forces simple
network architectures.

2.3
Input sparsity
Input sparsity refers to the number of zero-valued elements in a(0). Good feature sets
are sparse, as this reduces computational complexity of the first hidden layer. The best
feature sets, as a side effect, require a complete recomputation of the first hidden layer for
king moves, and high sparsity is essential in these cases.
A decade of Deep Learning has shown that increasing the number of parameters of a model
improves generalization error, even when the number of parameters is greater than the
size of the training data (Li and Liang 2019). This trend also applies to NNUE models,
with the caveat that we cannot blindly increase the number of parameters due to inference
speed concerns.
As shown above, only the first hidden layer can benefit from incremental updating. This
means that all linear layers where l > 1 must be kept small to maintain speed. This has
the implication that the first hidden layer must possess the vast majority of parameters
in the network in order for the network to generalize well. The feature set P only has
size 768, which is not enough to produce state-of-the-art models. When NNUE was first
introduced, it was used in conjunction with the HalfKP feature set which has size 40960·2,
with the first hidden layer containing over 99.9999% of the models total parameters in a
typical NNUE architecture.

2.5
Quantization
The final concept of NNUE is quantization. Quantization is the process of converting the
model parameters from typically 32-bit floating point to a low-precision integer domain
(Jacob et al. 2018). Quantization reduces the accuracy of the model, but greatly increases
inference speed. The quantization error accumulates across layers and can be a problem
for deep networks. However, NNUE networks are typically shallow, causing this error to
be negligible.
The inference speed is mostly increased by the use of SIMD vector instructions. Newer
CPU models provide SIMD lanes that are up to 512-bit wide. If model parameters are
quantized to int8, this allows one to work on up to 64 weights at a time. This is a massive
performance increase that cannot be ignored, but has the downside that writing eﬀicient
SIMD code is diﬀicult.
The concrete quantization scheme used is shown on Figure 2.3, which is the same scheme
as used by Stockfish. There are likely many quantization schemes that work, and the one
presented here is not necessarily optimal. We start at the feature transformer (marked in
green), which quantizes the input to FP<i16, 127>. We have to use i16 here, as the input
has not been compressed by Clipped ReLU as with subsequent layers. The next step is
Clipped ReLU, which quantizes to FP<i8, 127> from FP<i16, 127>. Clipped ReLU, as
has been mentioned earlier, clamps values to the range [0, 1]. Because of the scale factor
used, 1f = 127q, we can therefore safely downcast i16 to i8 without losing any range, since
the largest value representable by an 8-bit integer is 28−1 −1 = 127.
As the input to L1 is quantized to FP<i8, 127>, we can calculate this layer extremely
eﬀiciently using SIMD. Fortunately, multiply-add SIMD instructions are automatically
upcasted to a 32-bit accumulator, which makes the process much smoother. L1 increases
the scale factor from 127 to 127·64. Since values will be upcasted to 32-bit, we can decrease
the quantization step size (by increasing the scale factor) without worrying about range.
As before, Clipped ReLU allows us to easily step back down to i8 because of the aggressive
clamping. L2 uses the exact same quantization as L1.
The final step involves a conversion from FP<i32, 127 · 64> to FP<i32, 600 · 16> and
finally FP<i32, 16>. The exact process here does not seem particularly essential, and is
more so a matter of preference wrt. the internal scale used for the chess program. Since
the output is in 32-bit, scale factors can easily be much larger than what is shown here.
Biases can be kept as 32-bit integers because of the upcast to 32-bit. Looking at Figure 2.3,
one might think that converting between the different scale factors would be computation-
ally expensive. This is potentially true, but we can use simple multiplication to minimize
the number of arithmetic instructions.
Let us consider the example of converting the
output of the feature transformer to the activation range of Clipped ReLU:

2.6
Incompleteness of positional encoding
Readers familiar with chess will notice that the encoding for P is missing some state of
the chess position:
1. Castling rights: the encoding does not consider which castling rights are still
available. This is not critical, as the search can naturally find positions where castling
rights have been expended, evaluate them and propagate the best position back to
the root node. In general, it is easier to evaluate the result of castling rather than
its potential.
2. Draw information: neither the fifty-move rule nor 3-fold repetition are needed
in the encoding. Draw detection can be handled exclusively in search, although the
Graph-History Interaction (Campbell 1985) caused by the transposition table makes
it unreliable.
3. En passant capture square: Quiescence Search (Beal 1990) ensures that the
evaluation function is only called in quiet positions. In practice, the network will
never see a position where an en passant capture square is active.
In short, the missing state from the encoding is implied through the Alpha-Beta Search
itself.
One could also argue that the time control is part of the state.
It is diﬀicult
to imagine how the time control could interact with the evaluation meaningfully, and is
usually considered entirely separate from the evaluation.

Feature sets
We have previously discussed the feature set P, which is likely the simplest encoding
possible for use with NNUE. However, it suffers from its small size and does not fully
take advantage of the benefits of incremental updating. We will define a base feature set,
originally introduced alongside NNUE (Nasu 2018), and then go on to show the various
new improved feature sets that have been created since the introduction of NNUE.
3.1
HalfKP

This makes sense intuitively, as the relation between the current pieces on the board and
one’s own king is the main cornerstone of chess.

kings to each other. As a remedy to these issues, a new feature set, named HalfKA, was
introduced in 2021 by Stockfish. HalfKA is a generalization of HalfKP that contains
explicit king features.

3.5
Accounting for perspectives
The features sets described so far gloss over how the White and Black perspectives are
combined. For example, |HalfKP| = 40960·2 means White and Black need 40960 features
each. Instead of using a single accumulator A for the output of the feature transformer, we
now accumulate each perspective separately by introducing a white accumulator AW and
a black accumulator AB. Splitting the accumulators into two provides easier bookkeeping
regarding feature updates and an important optimization for king moves. Say that White
plays a king move, for example Ka6 and that HalfKP is used. Because the accumulators
are split, only AW needs to recalculated – AB can be left as is. If only a single accumulator
A is maintained, king moves are twice as expensive computationally.
After both AW and AB have been calculated, they still need to somehow be combined to
be accepted as input for the next hidden layer. The most obvious option is to concatenate
the two accumulators. This produces a similar case as stated in the description of P where
the output evaluation is always anchored to a specific perspective. For example, if the
concatenation starts with AW , the evaluation is always from White’s perspective.

3.5.1
Tempo
Tempo is the idea that the side to move has an inherent advantage. Due to the Null
Move Observation (Beal 1990), it is almost always the case that the side to move has
a move available that will improve their position. Figure 3.4 shows two examples: one
position where this statement holds, and one position where this does not. To reflect this
advantage, it is common practice to add half a pawn in favor of the side to move in hand-
crafted evaluation functions. This is an extremely crude way of applying the side to move
advantage. By encoding tempo in the network architecture, we hope that a more holistic
approach to tempo can be learned. Optimally, the network will also learn to recognize
cases of zugzwang and adjust the evaluation accordingly.

3.6
Remarks about feature sets and their complexity
There is also an important aspect, which can easily be overlooked, of creating effective
feature sets. It comes down to the computational cost of the feature index function f.
We must remember that in the end, f will be called several times per node in the search
tree by the chess engine itself. With the current speed of Alpha-Beta-based chess engines,
this means that f can easily be called several million times per second. It is possible to
imagine extremely complex and powerful feature sets that perform well in the training
phase, but fail in practice because of the high cost of f. The points stated above means

and speed.

creates triples that relate each piece to the king. We could take it a step further and
further add another triple that relates each piece to the queen. This makes sense since
the queen and its relation to other pieces, like the king, is paramount to playing winning
chess. It is even possible to change the original (king square, square, piece) triple to (king
square, queen square, square, piece) to further improve the network’s ability to understand
the relationship between pieces. However, this means that instead of only recalculating
feature set indices on king moves (which are rare), we must also recalculate on queen
moves (which are comparatively common).

While improvements from the original NNUE concept can partly be attributed to the
evolution of features sets, there has also been important architectural additions.
3.7.1
Multiple subnets
The accuracy of the network can be improved by increasing the number of parameters.
The issue is that this negatively affects inference speed, and as such, parameter count for
NNUE must be kept low. Nevertheless, Stockfish provided a technique which increases
evaluation accuracy and parameter count but with minimal inference speed impact.
The final three layers of NNUE are referred to as the layer stack. To be precise, this refers
to the layers using weights W(1), W(2) and W(3) on Figure 3.3. The basic premise involves
using n layer stacks, where the ith layer stack is denoted Li. Some discriminator function
D is then used to select the layer stack LD(p) to use for the concrete position p. This means
that when n = 1, the architecture is unchanged. A good choice is to define D such that Li
is chosen based on the current stage of the game (e.g., opening, middlegame, endgame).
This is reasonable, as chess is played very differently in for example the opening versus
endgame. Using this approach, each layer stack Li can specialize in a separate stage of
the game.
This also means that n defines the number of stages we define chess to have. Human
players ”use” n = 3: opening, middlegame and endgame. Chess engines with HCE most
often omit the opening stage in their evaluation due to opening book databases. The
benefit of using neural networks is that technically any value of n is possible without
additional work from the developer. Stages are learned implicitly from the dataset during
training. Naturally there are diminishing returns, and large values of n are likely pointless.
Stockfish uses n = 8, which seems to be a good middle ground while still allowing much
finer and granular control than HCE. Furthermore, they define D as
D(p) = (count(p) −1)/4,
0 ≤D(p) < n,
where p is current position and count is a function which outputs the number of pieces for
a position. The function is graphed on Figure 3.6. The only performance overhead of this

it can be computed extremely eﬀiciently, and the number of pieces left on the board is a
reasonable indicator of the game stage.

The original NNUE architecture has, according to the Stockfish developers, issues both

powerful technique for improving the evaluation function of a chess engine. PSQT con-

Data sets
Generating one’s own data set is a daunting task, and one that is not yet well understood.
Because of this, the thesis is scoped such that only external data sets will be used. Specifi-
cally, these will be the same data sets used by Stockfish to train their own NNUE models.
We will still describe how these data sets are generated, and what makes for strong data
sets. For consistent results, the same set of data sets are used for training all models. A
short description of the data sets used and where they can downloaded can be found in
Appendix B. The data sets used are known to be strong, and have been used to train
state-of-the-art NNUE models.
The design of data sets for NNUE is simple. Each data point contains a position pi and an
evaluation of said position yi, considered the ground truth. The goal of the neural network
is then to match the provided evaluation according to a supervised learning paradigm.
Optimally, the best move and game result (win, loss or draw) should also be included for
data set filtering and tuning the loss function. These applications will be described in the
next chapter.
Finding yi is non-trivial since chess remains unsolved. Because engines are much stronger
at chess than humans (Duca Iliescu 2020), it makes most sense to generate yi using self-play
between two (often identical) strong programs. This is also what defines the irreducible
error during training, and provides an upper bound for how much the model can learn.
During each self-play match, the evaluation yi found in each position is continuously
recorded until game termination. This process can then be repeated until the desired
number of data points have been gathered. Generating data sets is a laborious process,
especially if accurate evaluations are desired.

4.1
The stages of training
NNUE is often trained in stages, where each stage contains a different group of data sets. A
data set group is a collection of smaller data sets, forming one larger single data set. State-
of-the-art NNUE models use as much as 7 stages, each using different hyperparameters and
data set groups to maximize playing strength. However, the core idea of using multiple
training stages can be explained by reducing the number of training stages to 2: a simple
stage and a complex stage. As a shorthand, we will also refer to these stages as stage 1
and stage 2, respectively.
4.1.1
The simple stage
A simple training stage is the first training stage, where the model starts from zero and
must learn from scratch. It has been shown that NNUE has trouble learning complicated
patterns from the get-go. The crux is that the data set used must not represent overly
complicated patterns or hyper-accurate evaluations. To put it simply, our ground truth
yi should (counter-intuitively) not aim to be as accurate as possible. It should simply
represent core patterns of chess that will allow the network to learn more complicated
patterns later on.
The strongest data sets used for this stage are generated by self-play using Stockfish with
a fixed search depth. Because yi must not be overly accurate, the search depth should
be relatively shallow. Empirically, it has been shown that searching with depth d = 9
generates strong results. At these depths, there is a strong chance that both the best
move and evaluation will be inaccurate. Stockfish can easily search to much greater depth
in seconds, and this data is therefore relatively quick to generate even for several billion
positions. It is also possible to search for a fixed amount of nodes per move instead, with
many strong data sets being generated using only 5000 nodes per move.
It is also useful to include positions where ”the right move is the wrong move”. While
learnable patterns certainly exist, it is also important to ensure that the network does not
46
Enhancing Alpha-Beta Pruning with Eﬀiciently Updatable Neural Networks (NNUE)
==PAGE:55==
because overzealous in applying these patterns. All patterns have exceptions, and it is
important that the network learns to recognize when common knowledge does not hold.
4.1.2
The complex stage
A complex training follows a simple training stage. At this point, the network should
be aware of the general patterns of chess and be ”primed” to learn complex patterns.
Positions and evaluation values are almost exclusively sourced from Lc0 games. Contrary
to the first stage, yi should seek to be as accurate as possible.
Data sets in this stage often include positions that are solved using endgame tablebases
(EGTB) with 7 or fewer pieces (Haworth 2014). EGTB have an inherent disadvantage in
that they are far too large (18.4 TB) to be contained entirely in RAM, and are therefore
heavily constrained by disk I/O speeds. This means that despite seeming beneficial at a
surface level, EGTB used during Alpha-Beta Search is only a marginal improvement. By
allowing this information to be encoded in the neural network, the I/O bandwidth issues
can be circumvented. Certainly, all EGTB positions cannot be contained in NNUE, but
its inclusion is still a net benefit.
Exactly because chess is not solved, there will be mistakes, or so-called blunders within the
data set. There have been efforts to ”deblunder” data sets after they have been generated,
but at least within the NNUE community, no noticeable performance benefit have been
gained from this.
The piece count distribution is also important for quality data sets. As an extreme exam-
ple, if the data set only contains positions with 5 pieces or fewer, the network will become
overly specialized and fail to generalize. A data set must be as balanced as possible and
represent a wide array of positions in order to generate strong models. This applies to
both stages of training.

4.3
The BINPACK format
Due to large and unwieldy data sets in the training of NNUE, Stockfish introduced the
specialized compression format BINPACK. The algorithm achieves compression factors of
20-40 by utilizing incremental updating through chains of positions.
Due to the high
compression, BINPACK is the preferred data format of Stockfish.
Note that data sets are compressed purely due to practical issues. If high speed storage
is not a limiting factor, data sets can be left uncompressed in whatever data format is
desired. However, if one wants to use external data sets, for example from Stockfish, do
note that these data sets are almost always in BINPACK format. This means that, for the
sake of cooperation within the community, adopting BINPACK makes sense.
Because of the complexity involved in decompressing the format, it is futile to implement
the algorithm directly in Python as the performance will simply be too poor. The de-
compression should be implemented in a high-performance language such as C or C++.
The language barrier can be crossed by using libraries such as ctypes (Python Software
Foundation 2023).
The documentation for BINPACK has shown to be lacking, making it diﬀicult to implement
without refering to the source code of the original implementation. BINPACK also requires
a chess engine that is capable of generation and making moves. Unfortunately, parts of
chess engine integration is implementation specific, meaning that some code must be copied
almost verbatim to make one’s own implementation. A contains a technical description of
BINPACK for those interested.
The NNUE trainer for Manni uses a self-made, multithreaded library for decompressing
BINPACK data sets written in C++.
4.3.1
Specification
The format has its basis in, like many other chess specific algorithms, incremental up-
dating. Data sets are usually generated from self-play, meaning that many positions are
highly similar. Instead of storing only a single position individually, a chain of positions
now exist in a block. The start of a block contains a root position, its evaluation and the
move leading to the next position in the chain. By applying this move to the root position,
the subsequent stored position is found. The next element in the chain now only needs to
contain the evaluation value and the move needed to reach the next position.
4.3.2
Disadvantages of position chains
Since BINPACK relies on position chains to achieve compression, this also means that com-
pression regresses if position chains are broken or entirely absent. For example, in the case
where each chain has length 1 (all positions differ by more than one move), compression
is equal to any binary storage format.
Since position chains cannot be broken without losing compression, this also means that
filtering or applying any transformation to the dataset must be done extremely sparingly,
if at all. In practice, it is easier to filter the decompressed data in real-time as it is loaded
rather than directly deleting entries in the BINPACK file.

4.3.3
Sampling fairly from multiple data sets
BINPACK files can be concatenated to trivially combine multiple data sets into one larger
data set. However, problems arise if we combine data sets naively.
Let A and B be two data sets in BINPACK format, and the combination of these two data
sets be C. Say that C is produced by concatenating A and B. This means that during
training, we will first perform backpropagation exclusively by data points from A, and
once all data points of A has been exhausted, we will exclusively use data points from B.
It would be more beneficial to sample in a balanced manner such that both data sets are
used uniformly during training.
The solution to this problem is to randomly sample BINPACK blocks from both data sets.
Random sampling also has the benefit that when multiple passthroughs of the data sets
are possible, the data will not be presented in the data order every time. In this example,
we could sample uniformly, i.e. p = 1/2. However, say that |A| = n and |B| = 2n. In
this case, each individual data point ai of A would be twice as likely to be sampled as
compared to bi.
The sampling probability p needs to account for the length of each data set, such that
each element of each data set is equally likely to be sampled. This can be done by setting
pi = k/ni,
where pi is the probability of sampling the ith sub-data set, k is the total length of all
data sets to be combined and ni is the length of the ith data set.

Training
We have already discussed the training process partially in the previous chapter about
data sets. This chapter will describe more concretely how NNUE is trained.
Because data sets change so frequently, the usual concept of an epoch, meaning when the
whole data set has gone through backpropagation, is not a useful definition in context of
NNUE. It makes it too diﬀicult to compare training length of runs using different data
sets. The NNUE community has therefore decided to define an epoch as 100 million (108)
chess positions. For example, if a neural network is trained with 10 epochs, it has been
trained on 1 billion (109) chess positions.

5.4
Correlation between loss and playing strength
While data sets used for fitting models are typically split into training and test partitions,
this does not make sense for NNUE. The problem is that loss is not particularly effective
at describing the strength of a chess engine. The only true way to test is through self-
play. The downside is that self-play is computationally expensive, as it requires at the
very least 1000 games to receive accurate results. This is too large of a bottleneck during
training, and therefore is usually left to after its completion. At this point, checkpoints
of the model at chosen epochs can be compared through self-play. Our experiments will
show the relationship between loss and playing strength.
5.5
Adjusting for quantization during training
We hesitate to refer to the following approach as quantization aware training or post-
training quantization (Rokh, Azarpeyvand, and Khanteymoori 2023) due to its simplicity
and not having a direct comparison in the literature. The quantization strategy used could
54
Enhancing Alpha-Beta Pruning with Eﬀiciently Updatable Neural Networks (NNUE)
==PAGE:63==
be seen as some mix of both strategies, and exactly resembles what Stockfish uses to train
state-of-the-art NNUE models.
All parameters remain in their unquantized floating-point space during training. After
each training step, the newly updated weights are clamped to the range which the quan-
tized values can represent. In other words, the weights are constrained such that their
values do not exceed what can be represented in their quantized form. Note that biases
are left unclamped, as they will be quantized using 32-bit integers, which do not suffer
from the same range issues. Furthermore, the accuracy issues presented by quantization
are not considered, since weights remain in floating point space during training. Lastly,
the actual process of quantization is performed after training is finished. No retraining
step to reclaim the lost accuracy is performed.
The idea of this quantization strategy is that the network learns to work within the bounds
of quantization, since weights are constrained each training step. However, the process is
also very simple, and its effectiveness is up for debate. Usually, post-training quantization
relies on a retraining step using the quantized weights. It has also been shown that DNNs
with lower complexity (NNUE models have very low complexity) take a larger performance
penalty when the retraining step is omitted after quantization (Sung, Shin, and Hwang
2016). This suggests that there is work to be done regarding quantization.
5.6
Data set transformation
As mentioned, BINPACK relies on position chains to achieve compression. If we break these
position chains, by for example removing even a single position, compression is reduced
dramatically. This has a few implications that must be solved for performant training
results.
Firstly, we have no means of shuffling the data set, as this breaks position chains. Usually,
the data set is shuffled after each epoch such that the model does not take on any systemic
biases. Position chains also have the disadvantage that they are inherently similar. As
mentioned previously, model learning is thought to be strongest when data set variety is
high. There are three components that help combat these issues.
Firstly, since a data set consists of multiple sub-data sets, the random sampling from each
allows for a degree of randomization. Secondly, we introduce the hyperparameter θskip.
This hyperparameter controls how many positions are skipped on average. If θskip = n,
it means that n samples are skipped before a single sample is included, i.e. samples are
included with probability p = 1/(n + 1). For example, if θskip = 3, 3 out of 4 samples
are skipped on average. When θskip = 0 no positions are skipped, and it must hold that
θskip > 0. Lastly, we introduce yet another hyperparameter θearly that functions similarly
to θskip. When θearly = n, the first n positions (hence ”early”) of a position chain are
skipped. Both θskip and θearly serve to reduce the similarity of positions encountered.
Because of quiescence search, it is (almost) guaranteed that the evaluation function will
only be called on quiet positions. Quiet positions have no obvious tactical threats, such
as check or capture. It is therefore an advantage to filter non-quiet positions from the
data set. Determining if a position is truly quiet is computationally expensive, and we
therefore use a simplified definition: the position is quiet if the best move is not a capture
and the side to move is not in check. We denote this hyperparameter as qskip.
Any data transformation or filtering also has the disadvantage of increasing the computa-
tional pressure on the data loader. Since NNUE models are relatively small, computation
Enhancing Alpha-Beta Pruning with Eﬀiciently Updatable Neural Networks (NNUE)
55
==PAGE:64==
is memory bound. Especially θskip and θearly have a significant impact on data decom-
pression, because so many positions are decompressed but unused.
5.7
Overfitting and the role of regularization
We argue that overfitting of the model is not an issue, and it is more likely the case that
the model is underfitting. Overfitting occurs when the model overzealously fits to the data
set, capturing noisy patterns that ultimately lower the model’s ability to generalize.
Overfitting typically happens when the model is too complex compared to the training
data. A NNUE model is not complex, and consists of simple components. The training
data is however, extremely complex and consists of several billion positions. The vast
majority of positions will only be seen once by the model. It therefore seems unlikely that
NNUE will overfit to the data set. Because we assume that overfitting is not an issue,
the use of regularization becomes moot. Our training process does therefore not include
any explicit regularization such dropout layers, batch normalization etc. This is consistent
with the training process that Stockfish uses.
5.8
Optimizer
The preferred choice of optimizer in the NNUE community is Ranger (Wright 2019), which
at its core is based on the Rectified Adam optimizer (Liu et al. 2021). Ranger is meant to
be a modern optimizer which implements the newest advancements in the field, such as
gradient centralization (Yong et al. 2020) and lookahead (Zhang et al. 2019). Ranger has
been the optimizer of choice for Stockfish since 2021.
The choice of learning rate decay is also based on recommendations from the authors of
Ranger. Wright 2019 suggests that a fixed learning rate will prevent optimal results, and
that the optimizer can end up ”hovering” around a local minimum if learning rate is not
eventually decayed.
Note that a newer version of Ranger exists (Wright and Demeure 2021), which is not the
optimizer used. It is possible that this version is more performant, but this has not been
tested.

Examining PSQT weights
Modern NNUE architectures use, as previously described, a technique that can be seen as
a much more refined version of Piece-Square Tables (PSQT). While the intuition between
the original PSQT used in HCE and the PSQT technique used in NNUE is reasonable, it
makes sense to examine the PSQT weights after training to see if it truly is analogue to
HCE PSQT.
The network examined uses HalfKAv2_hm with a feature transformer of size 1024 and
n = 8 PSQT stages. Because of the feature set used, it is also possible to retrieve PSQT
weights for the king. There are many combinations, and for that reason we can only show
a small subset. The chosen tables are friendly pawn at stage D(p) = 2 and friendly king.
Because king tables do not relate to another piece, it is possible to show all n = 8 tables
in this case. It should be noted that the values presented are not inherently tied to the
value of a pawn, and are therefore unitless.
We expect that the PSQT weights are interpretable to a degree and that most values
can be understood from a modern chess perspective. It is also important to remember
that PSQT functions in combination with the linear layers of the network. It is therefore
possible that any weakness of PSQT is compensated by the positional knowledge of the
linear layers.

Conclusion
We proved that NNUE is effective for improving the strength of a program when compared
to a handcrafted evaluation function (HCE). The strongest version of Manni used the
feature set HalfKAv2_hm alongside the PSQT and subnet enhancements, and placed
4th out of 11 programs in our tournament. NNUE beat the HCE version of Manni by more
than 400 Elo, and with the latter placing second last. We used CCRL ratings to estimate
that Manni is rated at over 3000 Elo. HalfKAv2_hm was found to be approx. 100
Elo stronger than HalfKP, although this includes the PSQT and subnet enhancements.
Manni is remarkably stronger than our previous work, Exo.
The data set and training process described proved to be successful. In particular, we
observed that omitting either of the two training stages results in a weaker program.
However, the weak correlation between loss and playing strength means that finding strong
training checkpoints is diﬀicult. Furthermore, we raised concerns about the accuracy of
this method as a result of the very spiky and erratic playing strength measurements. We
also saw that Manni scales very differently at short and long time controls.
The individual contribution study showed that vector instructions are by far the most
important factor tested, reducing search speed by an order of magnitude and resulting
in −463.16 ± 31.31 Elo when removed. Incremental updating was found to be far less
important against our expectations, losing only −68.63 ± 11.07 Elo when omitted. We
noted that the individual contribution study was cut short due to time constraints, and
that many more parts need testing.
The BINPACK data loader was found to be able to decompress more than 5 million positions
per second when using 6 threads, a result we consider satisfactory. Thread scaling was
found to be better than expected, although we only tested up to 6 threads.
While the implementation of NNUE proved successful in terms of playing strength, Manni
is still convincingly beaten by the current state-of-the-art Stockfish.
8.1
Future work
More parts of Manni need to be tested to understand NNUE better. In particular, many
hyperparameters were largely left untested during the individual contribution study, and
the effect of PSQT and subnets remain unclear.
Generating one’s own data set instead of relying on Stockfish and LeelaChessZero would
be academically interesting.
What makes strong data sets in NNUE is still relatively
unknown, and this could be a step towards clarification.
LeelaChessZero uses a dual-headed neural network that can both evaluate positions and
predict the best move. Move-ordering is particularly important in the context of alpha-
beta search, and there is reason to believe that a properly trained neural network could
outperform existing move-ordering algorithms.
There is room for improvement in the quantization process during training. Models are
trained in their unquantized form (although their range is artificially reduced) and are only
quantized post-training. It would be interesting to examine the impacts of quantization-
aware training and (proper) post-training quantization.

C
Project plan
The following is the project plan created at the beginning of this thesis.
C.1
Project description
After more than a decade of ever-slowing incremental progress, computer chess saw one
of its largest improvements with Eﬀiciently Updatable Neural Networks (NNUE) in 2020,
achieving the highly sought-after combination of Alpha-Beta Pruning and neural networks.
The project seeks to train an NNUE model from zero and implement its use in a modern,
optimized chess engine. The chess engine which will receive the NNUE implementation
is self-created by the author. Training data will be provided by Stockfish in the domain-
specific BINPACK format, requiring a custom, high-performance data loader. The various
architectural and training improvements made to NNUE since its inception will also be
examined.
A significant goal of the project is centralize information about NNUE, which currently
lacks formal description with knowledge scattered across multiple sources.
The effect of NNUE on playing strength will be measured both via self-play and round-
robin style tournaments against chess engines of varying strength.

C.2.1
Project plan description
The project plan is split into three parts: implementation of NNUE in a chess engine
(marked as blue), NNUE trainer development (green) and finally the report itself (brown).
The first month will be used for rapid development, such that a basic trainer and chess
engine implementation is functional. This means that the report will be deprioritized in
this period. The remaining months will be a mix between all three parts. The main focus
is to include the more advanced network architectures such as HalfKA and improvements
like Piece-Square-Tables.

C.3
Learning objectives
A student which has completed the project according to its description and time plan has
shown the following qualities:
1. identify their own project formulation and a corresponding time plan for its execution
2. find and describe the relevant literature within the scope of computer chess and
neural networks
3. describe the background and significance of neural networks in computer chess
4. describe the process of inference and backpropogation for utilizing and training a
neural network
5. understand and explain the choices and limitations of neural networks in alpha-beta
pruning and what effect NNUE has on these
6. apply the key optimization techniques used in NNUE like incremental updating,
vector instructions (SIMD) and quantization to increase inference speed of a neural
network
7. utilize the BINPACK-format for training data compression and understand the
short-comings and advantages of the format
8. be critical in relation to the theory, method and the trade-offs made by NNUE
9. design experiments that can demonstrate relative playing strength of a chess engine
10. Report the obtained results structurally, comprehensively, briefly, clearly, in a criti-
cally evaluating/concluding way, and of course also in accordance with good practice
for a written presentation within the discipline.
11. discuss, conclude on and put results of chess engine inter-play into perspective in
relation to the theory and empirical data.
