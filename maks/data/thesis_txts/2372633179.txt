Abstract 
Filamentous fungi can produce a variety of remarkable bioactive secondary metabolites, including 
polyketides, non-ribosomal peptides, terpenoids and hybrid metabolites, which display high 
chemical diversity. Fortunately, the public availability of sequenced genomes provides a better 
opportunity for elucidation of the metabolites’ biosynthetic pathway, thereby facilitating the 
identification of the genes encoding homologous enzymes, which are responsible for catalyzation 
of these complex molecules step by step. This is of great significance and broad prospects. 
3-methylorsellinic acid (3-MOA) is an important precursor metabolite in the library of bioactive 
products. In the model organism Aspergillus nidulans, cichorine derived from 3-MOA was firstly 
isolated and identified by Sanchez and coworkers and the NR-PKS gene pkbA/AN6448 required for 
synthesis of chichorine was also determined through replacing a putative transcription factor in 
the gene cluster with a promoter. Later on, a cluster of seven genes from AN6443 to AN6449 was 
found, including the PKS gene, AN6443 a transporter, AN6446 transcriptional activator gene and 
four other tailoring genes through a set of targeted deletions. Meanwhile, Klejnstrup and 
coworkers identified genes AN6444, AN6446, AN6447, pkbA/AN6448, AN6449, AN6450 and 
AN6451 belonging to cichorine gene cluster. This group discovered cichorinic acid in AN6446-Oex 
strain and the absence of cichorine, cichorinic acid and other nitrogen containing metabolites in 
AN6446-Oxe AN6444 strain strongly indicates that nitrogen is incorporated into the cichorine 
biosynthetic pathway through amino acid catalyzed by the NRPS-like enzyme AN6444. 
To verify the conjecture that AN6444 catalyzes the nitrogen incorporation into cichorine, we 
overexpressed the transcription factor gene AN6446, which is conveniently responsible to activate 
the cichorine gene cluster, from reference strain. This project determined that cultivation at 30℃ 
for 5 days is the optimal growth conditions for A. nidulans to produce metabolites related to the 
cichorine pathway. The UHPLC-TOF-MS analysis of the metabolite profiles of the AN6446 
overexpression strain demonstrated the presence of novel metabolites including nitrogen 
containing compounds. A major unknown compound named argicichorine was successfully 
purified by semi-preparative HPLC and then characterized by 1D and 2D NMR. The structure 
elucidation by 1D and 2D 1H and 13C NMR confirmed that argicichorine consists of an arginine 
backbone attached to cichorine via nitrogen. The work substantiates that gene AN6444 encodes a 
NRPS enzyme which catalyze arginine incorporation into argicichorine, and that argicichorine is an 
intermediate towards cichorine and other nitrogen containing compounds altogether provides 
new insight into understanding of the biosynthetic pathway of nitrogen incorporated 3-MOA 
derived natural products in Asperdillus nidulans.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Filamentous fungi 
Filamentous fungi, commonly known as mold, are those fungi that tend to form branches of 
mycelia consisting a mass of thread-like hyphae, but not like mushrooms which produce large 
sporocarps.  They are widely distributed in the soil, water, air and in vitro and in vivo of plants and 
animal, and form some visible villi, flocculent or cobweb colony in the humid and warm climate 
(Arora & Khachatourians 2001).  
Since the filamentous fungi have the capacity to produce a large number of natural products with 
antimicrobial or other biological activities, they are closely related to human life, especially playing 
an important role in agricultural and industrial production, environmental protection, 
pharmaceutical development and theoretic research of biology. For example, Aspergillus oryzae is 
an essential ingredient in the processing some soy bean products like soy sauce and miso (Abe et 
al. 2006); Penicillium roqueforti is used to give the characteristic texture, appearance and aroma to 
the blue-veined cheeses (García-Estrada C1 2016); penicillin, the first antibiotic found in the history, 
is produced by some filamentous fungi like Aspergillus nidulans and Penicillium chrysogenum 
(Brakhage et al. 2004); indole diketopiperazine alkaloids, which are used as antimicrobial agents, 
antiviral agents, anti-cancer agents, immunomodulators, antioxidants and insecticides, are 
normally found from the genera Aspergillus and Penicillum (Ma et al. 2016); Pichia pastoris is 
working as an engineering strain for heterologous protein production (Daly & Hearn 2005); White-
rot fungi are benefit for the bioremediation of environment pollution by degrading insecticides, 
herbicides and heavy fuels into environmental friendly elements (Christian et al. 2005); fungus 
with rapid growth rate and sexually reproduction, like Aspergillus nidulans, Saccharomyces 
cerevisiae and Neurospora crassa, are used as model organism for a long history to investigate cell 
biology and genetics, such as gene regulation, cell cycle interconnection, cell differentiation 
(Zámborszky et al. 2014; Denoth Lippuner et al. 2014; Brakhage 2012a; Engh et al. 2010); but also 
settled biomedical research problems and industrial uses because of the high protein secretion 
and a variety of post-translational processing (Gasser et al. 2013). 
On the other hand, filamentous fungi sometimes cause great harm to human life. They result in 
serious economic losses by moldy deterioration of agricultural products, textiles, food and other 
industrial products. Such as Ustilago maydis, a pathogenic plant fungus, causes smut disease in 
maize and teosinte (Matei & Doehlemann 2016). Also, some fungi can produce toxins which are 
poisonous to humans and animals. For example, alfatoxin produced by Aspergillus Flavus has a 
carcinogenic effect (Kew 2013). Animal feed containing zearalenone produced by Fusarium fungi 
is implicated in reproductive disorders of swine and other domestic animals (Cortinovis et al. 2013).

1.2 Aspergillus 
Aspergillus was first cataloged and named by Pier Antonio Micheli in 1729, according to structure 
of spore sharing the similar shape of an aspergillum (a holy water sprinkler) (Anon 2006). 
Aspergillus is a large genus comprising around 250 species, within which approximately one of 
third produce a sexual stage (Geiser 2009; Dyer & O&apos;Gorman 2011). Several of them have 
been fully sequenced and published, including A. aculeatus, A. kawachii, A. carbonarius, A. 
clavarus, A. fischerianus, A. flavus, A. nidulans, A. niger, A. oryzae, A. sojae and A. terreus 
(Klejnstrup 2012). The access of full genome has provided a link between metabolites and the 
responsible genes, giving a further elucidation of the biosynthetic pathway (Walsh & Fischbach 
2010). The detailed information about genes and enzymes in an organism allows it producing 
specific designed metabolites with enhanced structural feature and optimizing fungi as cell factory 
to produce a large quantity of known valuable bioactive compounds by gene engineering, pathway 
reconstructing and enzyme engineering (Chen et al. 2016). 
1.3 Aspergillus nidulans 
Aspergillus nidulans, known as Emericella nidulans when referring to 
teleomorph, is  one of most important research organism within 
fugal kingdom, since it has been used to studying eukaryotic cell 
biology for over 50 years including cell cycle control, metabolism, 
genetic recombination, genome evolution, chromatin, DNA repair 
and regulatory pathways (Martinelli & Kinghorn 1994; Osmani & 
Mirabito 2004; Nierman et al. 2005). Except the asexual state, A. 
nidulans proliferate via forming sexual spores, ascospores, and 
parasexual life cycle (PONTECORVO et al. 1953). This allows A. 
nidulans to reproduce in laboratory. The wild-type colony surface 
has woolly texture and a color of green with white mycelia in areas 
with cleistothecial formation while reverse is red brownish to olive 
color, while different mutations may lead to the production of other 
pigments and different morphogenesis (Figure 1.1) (Harris et al. 
2009).  
The genome of A. nidulans has been sequenced and published in 2005, showing the organism is 
distantly related to A. oryzae and A. fumigatus (Galagan et al. 2005). The genome sequence 
indicates the presence of various secondary metabolite gene clusters, of which 56 are putative 
secondary metabolism core genes including 27 polyketide synthase genes (PKS), 2 polyketide 
synthase-like genes (PKS-like), 11 nonribosomal peptide synthetase genes (NRPS), 15 NRPS-like 
genes, and 1 hybrid NRPS-PKS gene (Yaegashi et al. 2014). The fact that the number of metabolites 
obtained in laboratory conditions is far fewer than the number of putative secondary metabolite 
genes, indicates that many gene clusters are not active under given growth conditions. Therefore,

different techniques to investigate the responsibility of unknown and unexpressed genes in the 
pathways have been developed, including One Strain Many Compounds (OSMAC) strategy, 
epigenome manipulations, transcription factor manipulations, co-cultivation with bacteria. 
OSMAC strategy is to cultivate the fungi in different conditions comparing with the standard one. 
The growth condition can be different by changing temperature, cultivation time, medium 
composition, illumination intensity and time, aeration, culture vessel and adding enzyme inhibitors. 
All these factors can have influence on the production of the metabolites (Bode et al. 2002). For 
example, it was reported by Sanchez that cultivation of A. nidulans on 20 different culture 
conditions led to the production of orsellinic acid and two derivative F-9775A and F-9775B 
(Sanchez et al. 2010).  
Epigenome manipulations is to delete the epigenetic genes involved in the silencing of biosynthetic 
pathway, leading to the activation of the particular biosynthetic pathway. For example, the 
deletion of cclA in A. nidulans showed the activation of two new gene clusters, mdpG and osrA 
(Bok et al. 2009). 
In secondary metabolite gene cluster, the member genes share the common regulation of 
transcription factors, which can be overexpressed or repressed. Therefore, overexpression or 
repression of the transcription factor in the gene cluster provides a convenient way to control the 
whole gene cluster. By comparing the metabolite profiles, the presence or absence of the 
metabolites can link the gene cluster with the metabolites. 
Co-cultivation of microorganisms leads to the production of new metabolites because the 
interaction between different organisms induced the silent genes. For example, Schroeckh and 
coworkers activated the orsellinic acid pathway successfully by co-cultivating A. nidulans with a 
collection of 58 soil-dwelling actinomycetes (Schroeckh et al. 2009).  
1.4 Secondary metabolites 
Secondary metabolites, low molecular weight compounds, are normally found in bacteria, fungi 
and plants, which plays a role in intercellular communication, self-defense, infecting animal and 
plants (Yim et al. 2007; Brakhage & Schroeckh 2011; Spiteller 2015). In addition, some of them 
have been found with important medical applications, such as penicillins and cyclosporines (Böhm 
et al. 2013; Anjum et al. 2012). These are the reasons why identification of novel natural 
compounds becoming a popular research. Secondary metabolites have been classified into three 
major categories, according to their biosynthesis pathways: polyketides (PKs), non-ribosomal 
peptides (NRPs) and terpenoids (Andersen et al. 2013). In the following sections, biosynthesis 
features and activate genes of three major class will be described with the examples of 
metabolites from A. nidulans, especially the polyketides.

1.4.1 Polyketides (PKs)  
PKs are one of the most abundant natural product, synthesized by using acetyl coenzyme A (acyl-
CoA) units by polyketide synthases (PKSs). According to the catalytic activities of PKSs, their 
biosynthetic pathways are classed into three types: I, II, and III (Shen 2003).  
Type I PKSs are enzymes consists of several modules with defined functions, or an iterative single 
module with several catalytic domains. It is reported in A. nidulans there is only iterative type I 
PKSs (iPKSs) existing (Ahuja, Chiang, Chang, Mike B Praseuth, et al. 2012). The acyltransferase (AT), 
acyl carrier protein (ACP), and β-ketosynthase (KS) domains are the fundamental domains 
filamentous fugal iPKSs. In details, AT domain is working to select acyl exender units and transfer 
them to the growing polyketide chain; ACP domain is responsible to hold the growing acyl chain 
where the units loaded; KS domain catalyzes the decarboxylative condensation (Claisen-type) of 
the extender unit with the growing acyl chain by forming the C-C bound (Brakhage 2012b).  
According to the level of reductive processing on β-keto carbon of the elongation chain, iPKSs can 
be divided into three sub-classes: non-reducing (NR), partially reducing (PR), and highly reducing 
(HR) (Kroken et al. 2003; Ma et al. 2009; Crawford & Townsend 2010).  
The NR-PKs (Figure 1.2 A) are usually containing three necessary domains and a starter unit-ACP 
transacylase (SAT) domain at N-terminal and a product template (PT) domain between KS and ACP 
domains, which help shaping the structural results of NR-PKSs (Crawford et al. 2008; Chiang et al. 
2010). While in PR- and HR-PKSs, there is no SAT domains fond, but a series of optional domains 
related to reduction are introduced (Figure 1.2 B): a β-ketoreductase (KR) domain for reducing β-
ketone to a hydroxyl group; a dehydratase(DH) domain for dehydration reaction of hydroxyl group; 
a enoly reductase (ER) domain for hydrogenation reaction. In addition, a methylatransferase (MT) 
domain can also be introduced for C-methylation by using S-adenosyl methionine (SAM) as the 
carbon donor. And the methylation prefers the naturally programmed β-keto-dienyltetraketide 
substrate in the result of both chain length and functionalization (Cacho et al. 2015). When the 
elongation of the carbon chain finish, it released form PKS catalyzed by a thioesterase (TE)/ Claisen 
cyclase (CLC) domain or other β-lactamase-like enzymes (Chiang et al. 2010).

Type II PKSs consist of similar essential catalytical domain as in type I PKSs, but two KS domains: 
KSα and KSβ. The first one is responsible to catalyze the Clasiden condensation as how it works in 
type I PKSs; while the second one controls the length of polyketide. In addition, the reductive 
processes only happen after the whole polyketide is synthesized (Chan et al. 2009).  
Type III PKSs are distinctly different from type I and II PKSs since the elongation carries out directly 
on acyl CoA substrates instead of using ACP. The elongations are catalyzed by an iterative single 
module (Shen 2003).  
1.4.2 Non-ribosomal peptides (NRPs)  
NRPs are catalyzed by the non-ribosomal peptide synthases (NRPSs). NRPSs contain repetitive 
catalytic modules, in which each module is responsible for the incorporation of one amino acid 
into the growing peptide chain(Crawford & Townsend 2010). Three domains are essential in each 
module for the elongation of a peptide: an adenylation (A) domain for selection and activation of 
an amino acid by acyl adenylation with ATP; a peptidyl carrier protein (PCP) domain for substrate 
recognition and transportation; a condensation (C) domain for amide bond formation between 
two amino acids, see Figure 1.3 (von Döhren et al. 1999). Substrate recognition involves binding

4’PP is transferred by a 4’PP transferase (PPT) domain, which is normally encoded by another gene

modifications by other enzymes such as cyclase, oxidase, N-methyltransferase, glycosyltransferase 
and others (Walsh et al. 2001). At the end, the termination domain releases the achieved peptide

It was reported comprehensively by Hans von Dohren, within 44 gene clusters in A. nidulans, 12 of

the characterization of genetic or biochemical function about monomodular NRPS-like genes in A. 
nidulans have not been well studied (Yeh et al. 2012).  
1.4.3 Terpenoids  
Terpenoids is a group of natural products comprised of activated C5 isoprene units: dimethylalyl 
diphosphate (DMAPP) and isopentenyl diphosphate (IPP). They are assembled by condensation in 
a head-to-tail manner, then undergo similar modifications as mentioned for NRPs, such as 
cyclisation, oxidation, etc (Andersen et al. 2013). In A. nidulans, only one terpene gene cluster, 
synthesizing diterpene ent-pimara-8(14),15-diene has been identified by Kirsi Bromann and 
coworkers. This gene cluster including the genes for encoding 3-hydroxy-3-methyl-glutaryl-
coenzyme A (HMG-CoA) reductase (AN1593), pimaradiene synthase (AN1594) and geranylgeranyl 
pyrophosphate (GGPP) synthase (AN1592) (Bromann et al. 2012).

1.4.4 Hybrid metabolites  
Hybrid metabolites are the ones whose different parts are from different biosynthetic route 
origins. For example, in A. nidulans aspyridone is an PKS-NRPS hybrid metabolite that two PKSs 
together with five additional genes encoding nonribosomal peptide comprise the asperfuranone 
biosynthetic pathway (Chiang et al. 2009). Moreover, austinol and dehydroaustinol are PK-
meroterpenoids produced from polyketide and terpenoid precursors, which is encoded by a 
cluster of four genes including a polyketide synthase gene, ausA and a cluster of ten additional 
genes including a prenyltransferase gene, ausN, respectively (Lo et al. 2012a).  
1.5 Orsellinic acid- like pathway in A. nidulans 
Bok and coworkers reported that PKS MdpG and AN7909 is essential for F-9775A biosynthesis in a 
cclA deletion A. nidulans strain (Bok et al. 2009). Moreover, It is demonstrated by James F. Sanchez 
and coworkers that in A. nidulans a NRPKS with TE release domain encoding gene orsA (AN7909) is 
required to produce the orsellinic acid as well as two cathepsin K inhibitors: F- 9775A and F-9775B. 
Additionally, two bioactive aromatic compounds gerfelin and diorcinol were also isolated in the 
AN7909 deletion strain (Sanchez et al. 2010). The proposed F9775A and B and orsellinic acid 
biosynthetic pathway is shown in Figure 1.4.

1.6 Analytical chemistry 
Analytical chemistry is a study of using instruments and methods to separate, identify, purify and 
quantify the targets (Douglas A. Skoog, Donald M. West, F. James Holler 2013).In this thesis, 
approaches like ultra-performance liquid chromatography Time-of-flight mass spectrometry 
(UHPLC-TOF-MS), preparative high performance liquid chromatography (prep-HPLC), nuclear 
magnetic resonance spectroscopy (NMR) are employed, in order to isolate and characterize the 
natural compounds produced by A. nidulans. In this section, each of the separate part that makes 
up the experimental setup is discussed. 
1.6.1 Sample preparation 
Generally, a chemical analysis is performed on a small fraction of the material, which means a 
sample is required to represent as closely as possible the same composition of the material. In 
most instances, samples are made into solutions with a suitable solvent, in which the analytes of 
entire sample should be dissolved completely (Douglas A. Skoog, Donald M. West, F. James Holler 
2013). To extract natural products, the polarity of solvent is supposed to be take into consideration. 
A general principle is using non-polar solvent to extract non-polar molecules, and polar solvent can 
extract out polar substances. The pH of the solvent for extraction is also an important factor for 
sample preparation. The pH can be adjusted to guarantee the maximum extraction because of the 
ionization of compounds. Meanwhile, it needs to be assured that the compounds will not break 
down at that particular pH applied (Houghton & Raman 1998).  
1.6.2 Chromatography 
Chromatography is a technique to separate complex mixtures into individual parts. It utilizes the 
selective distribution of different substances in different phase to elute the mixtures from 
stationary phase by mobile phase. Since the different substances in the mixture move at different 
speed along the stationary phase, the separation of molecules is achieved eventually.  
Based on the purpose, chromatography can be analytical and preparative. Analytical 
chromatography refers to identify and quantify the relative ingredients of analytes in a mixture by 
using the separation of molecules with a small amount of material. In contrast, the aim of 
preparative chromatography is to isolate or purify the target molecules with relative larger 
==PAGE:19==
15 
 
amounts of material for downstream use (Hostettmann, K; Marston, A; Hostettmann 1998).  
Due to the various combinations of stationary and mobile phase, chromatography is classified to 
gas chromatography (GC) which use gas as the mobile phase, and liquid chromatography (LC) 
which use liquid as the mobile phase. Thought liquid chromatography can be carried out in a plane, 
these two main types of chromatography both are generally performed in a column as stationary 
phase with the mobile phase passing through. Either of them can be used for analytical or 
preparative application. 
According to the separation mechanism of the sample component in two phases, the classification 
of chromatography including adsorption chromatography, partition chromatography, ion exchange 
chromatography, size exclusion chromatography, affinity chromatography, and hydrophobic 
interaction chromatography (HIC), multimodal or mixed-mode chromatography and so on. 
1.6.2.1 HPLC 
In present days, high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) is generally applied in 
pharmaceutical, illicit drugs detection, and laboratory science, which has relatively high pressure 
around 50-250 bar than the traditional one (Siddiqui et al. 2017; Pesce et al. 2010). After loading, 
liquid sample is pushed by a liquid with high pressure (the mobile phase), form pumping, through 
a column with absorbent material packed in (the stationary phase), compounds with higher affinity 
for stationary phase will attach on it and be eluted later than the ones with higher affinity for 
mobile phase along the concentration gradient changing, thus the separation is succeeded (Figure 
1.8). According to the polarity of stationary phase, two sub-class are formed from HPLC: normal 
phase and reverse phase. In normal phase LC, the stationary phase is more polar than mobile 
phase. Silica is the most preferred adsorbent material because of the high sensitivity and sample 
capacity. For reverse phase LC, stationary phase, as a hydrophobic one, is more non-polar than 
mobile phase (Douglas A. Skoog, Donald M. West, F. James Holler 2013).

1.6.2.1.1 RP Column components and specification 
Since columns are tubular, internal diameter of them is usually from 0.050 to 4.6 mm and the 
length is from 30 to 250 mm. The columns are commonly packed with silica particle, which range 
of the size is normally from 3 to 50 μm and the pore size is between 100 to 1000Å (Liu et al. 2007). 
As a common packing for RF column, C18 (octadecyl carbon chain) is bonded to silica, which 
captures hydrophbic compounds by van der Waals interactions. Other substances like phenyl, C8 
and cyano can also be used for RP column (Suárez-Suárez et al. 2011).  
1.6.2.1.2 Solvent 
There are two main solvents as mobile phase applied in the HPLC channels A and B. Solvent A is 
aqueous, usually HPLC grade water with 0.1% acid. Solvent B is organic, usually acetonitrile or 
methanol with 0.1% acid. Normally, the acid added in solvent are formic, trifluoroacetic, and acetic 
acid, which improves the peak shape outcome and retention time, meanwhile provides protons in 
RP-LC/MS. Since trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) has been reported to suppress ionization of analytes in 
MS, the percentage of TFA will be lowered to 0.025-0.05% (SHOU & NAIDONG 2005; Annesley 
2003).  
1.6.2.1.3 Explorative solid-phase extraction 
Because of the complexity of microbial natural product and the difficulty of purification, a method 
called explorative solid-phase extraction (E-SPE) is developed, allowing a rapid exploration of the 
optimum purification strategy and an easier way of dereplication. E-SPE is performed on a set of 
columns containing SAX, SCX, Oasis MAX and LH-20 on a small scale. The strong anion exchanger 
(SAX) and strong cation exchanger (SCX) are used to separate negative charged molecules and 
positive charged ones, respectively. These allow the detection of carboxylic acid and amines in the 
extracts. In details, the resin of SAX is trimethylaminoproply on a high purity silica gel substrate (-Si 
(CH2)3NCl(CH3)3), in which the functional ligand is the positive charged quaternary amine group for 
capturing the anion. While, in SCX a benzenesulfonic acid bonded sorbent is used to separate the 
basic compounds from extract. Oasis MAX is a hybrid polymeric reverse phase anion exchanger 
with a poly backbone (divinylbenzene-co-vinylpyrrolidone) which can retain acidic compounds with 
the carboxylate function group and weaker anions of phenols and tautomeric enol, and also 
provide information about the relative polarity of the compounds. A ploymeric cross-linked 
dextran gel for size-exclusions is used in Sephadex LH-20 column. Altogether, this E-SPE can 
provide a comprehensive information about the charge, polarity and size of the compounds in 
extract, which potentially support a preparative scale(Wagenaar & M. 2008; Kanaujia et al. 2007; 
Månsson et al. 2010).

1.6.3 Spectroscopy 
1.6.3.1 Diode array 
Nowadays, diode array detector (DAD), as one type of UV detectors, is the most commonly used 
non-destructive detector to record the ultraviolet and visible (UV-vis) absorption spectra of 
samples after separation through a high-pressure liquid chromatograph. The light from the lamp is 
dispersed by diffraction grating when it is passing through and the amount of it is estimated for 
each wavelength in the diode array (Figure 1.9) (W.J. Lough，I.W. Wainer 1995). Compared with a 
standard UV-VIS detector, DAD has multiple photodiode arrays, allowing it to obtain information 
over a wide range of wavelengths at the same time, thus making high speed spectroscopy possible 
(Douglas A. Skoog, Donald M. West, F. James Holler 2013). This is important for analyzing complex 
mixtures when little information is available on absorbance. It also benefits for confirming whether 
the peak represent a single compound or it is a composite peak, and supporting the identification 
of the compounds by comparing their spectra to the database when the m/z of compounds are 
identical. Moreover, it allows the collection of single targeted compound in one fraction during the 
purification process (K. F. Nielsen et al. 2011).

1.6.3.1.1 UV spectra 
An Ultraviolet- Visible (UV) Spectra refers to an absorption spectra generated by the molecular 
absorbing the electromagnetic wave in the ultraviolet-visible spectral region. Generally, the 
wavelength is between 200 nm and 1000 nm. In UV spectra, the absorption properties of the 
compound to electromagnetic radiation are described by an absorption curve, which display the 
position of an absorption peak and the absorption intensity of the absorption spectrum. Molecules 
containing absorbent groups, known as chromophores, such as carbon and carbon double bonds (-
C=C-), carbonyl (-COOH), aldehyde (-CHO), carboxyl (-OH), azo group (-N=N-), nitroso group (-N=O), 
have the capacity to generate the absorption in ultraviolet-visible spectral region. When two or 
==PAGE:22==
18 
 
more chromophores are conjugated, the absorption wavelength of the molecule shifts toward the 
direction of long wave (Douglas A. Skoog, Donald M. West, F. James Holler 2013).  
1.6.3.2 Mass Spectrometry  
Mass spectrometry (MS) is a powerful technique to obtain abundant information about the 
structure of unknown compounds and the qualitative and quantitative known or unknown 
composition of mixtures, which ionizes analyte molecules, separates the ions based on their mass-
to-charge ratio, and output the electrical signal by converting the a of ions (Douglas A. Skoog, 
Donald M. West, F. James Holler 2013). It is usually performed in conjunction with LC where the 
compounds would be separated chromatographically before they are directed to the MS (Russell & 
Edmondson 1997).  
In details, mass spectrometer consists of three essential components with the respective functions 
(Figure 1.10): the ion source- where the components of sample are ionized by applying thermal or 
electrical energy, most of time to positive charged ions or negative ones; the mass analyser- where 
the ions are sorted by according to their mass-to-charge ratios; and the transducer- where the 
separated ions of particular m/z value are collected and the results are displayed in electrical signal 
(Douglas A. Skoog, Donald M. West, F. James Holler 2013).

1.6.3.2.1 ESI-MS 
Electrospray Ionization (ESI) is one type of method to form the desorption sources for mass 
spectrometric analysis, which converts the liquid sample directly into a charged aerosol spray by 
the high-voltage electric field. It provides a rapid, accurate and sensitive analysis in natural 
products discovery by solving the various structural identification problems (Martínez-Ramírez et 
al. 2012; Furtado et al. 2007; Wu et al. 2010; Li et al. 2016).   
One of the essential features of ESI is that it enables to simply converting liquid sample into 
aerosol at atmospheric pressure, which is the key conjugation of MS to HPLC for analysis. It is a 
combination of the separation ability of HPLC and the detection and identification capability of MS 
(Kind & Fiehn 2010). Moreover, ESI is the producing of multiple charged molecular ions, which 
enhances the mass range of the analyser to make the determination of the molecular weight of 
the compounds in the complex mixtures more accurate when compared with other atmospheric 
pressure ionization processes (Pitt 2009). 
Furthermore, ESI was shown to be the most sensitive ionization techniques for detecting large 
biomolecules like porphyrins and steroid (Leinonen et al. 2002; Świder et al. 2013). It also became 
a choice for detection and characterization of microbial natural products since it can be applied 
successfully to more than 90% of them. In negative mode, there are usually polar and basic 
molecules found; while in positive mode, there are often small aromatic acids or anthraquinones 
(Martínez-Ramírez et al. 2012). 
In addition, ESI is a soft ionization technique which generally produces no fragmentation of analyte. 
This leads to the molecules with attached protons ([M+H]+) or cation ([M+Na]+, [M+K]+, etc.), 
known as adduct ions, in the positive mode correspond to the base peak in mass spectrum. And 
adduct ions like [M-H]-, [M+Cl]- and [M+HCOO]]- are normally observed in the negative mode 
(Colegate and Molyneux, 208AD). 
1.6.3.2.2 Q-TOF- MS 
In order to determine the mass-to-charge ratio, there are several different mass analyzers can be 
applied to LC-MS: Single Quadrupole mass analyzer (QMS), Triple Quadrupole mass analyzer 
(TQMS), Ion Trap analyzer, Time-of-flight mass analyzer (TOF) and quadrupole Time-of-flight mass 
analyzer (Q-TOF). In this section, Q-TOF- MS will be discussed in detail. 
Quadrupole time-of-flight mass spectrometry is a hybrid mass spectrometry, combining a 
hyperbolic quadrupole mass filter quadrupole and collision quadrupole with time-of-flight device 
as the second mass selector (Ens & Standing 2005). Thus, it obtains the advantages from both the 
high throughput of quadrupoles to perform various tasks and the unlimited mass range of TOF 
spectrometers in both simple mass spectrometry (MS) and tandem (MS/MS) modes, which makes 
it an excellent choice for qualitative and quantitative analysis of  both targeted and nontargeted 
analytes in complex mixtures (Binkley 2010; Pozo et al. 2011). Furthermore, Q-TOF mass 
spectrometer is a stable device which is suitable for various ion source, since the collision 
==PAGE:24==
20 
 
quadrupole makes the instrument independent from the ion production process (Ens & Standing 
2005).  
For example, in 6550A iFunnel Q-TOF LC/MS system, after the ions produced by ion source passing 
through a thin skimmer, an octapole ion guide and patented lenses to obtain a high mass ion 
transmission and sensitivity within a wider range, the precusor ions are selected with target mass. 
And then the product ions and neutral fragments are generated in the hexapole collision cell, 
where all ions leaving with the almost identical energy guaranteed by linear axial acceleration and 
high collision gas pressure. This delivers better than 2ppm mass accuracy for MS and 5ppm mass 
accuracy for MS/MS. Later the product ions pass through another octopole ion guide followed by a 
quadrupole ion guide and the slicer, which allowing the ions to get together without too much 
vertical momentum. When they reach the pulser, the ions are accelerated to have equal kinetic 
energy to enter the flight tub. Because Ek = ½ mv2, where m is the mass of the ion and v is the ion 
velocity, the lower the mass, the greater the velocity, which means the shorter time the ion travels. 
The reflectron is applied for compensation of minor speed differences and improvement of 
resolving ability. Finally, the ions reach the microchannel-plate detector, where the signal of ion 
was converted from electrons to photons and back to electrons and displayed by ADC digitizer 
(Agilent | 6550A iFunnel Q-TOF LC/MS; Chernushevich, Loboda and Thomson, 2001).  
1.6.3.2.3 Mass Spectra  
A mass spectrum is usually presented in the form of histogram, where each bar representing an 
ion of a specific mass-to-charge ratio (m/z), while the length of the bar indicating the relative 
abundance of the ion. The base peak represents the most intense ion, which is assigned 
abundance of 100. Most of the ions in the MS are single charged, therefore the m/z value equals 
the mass (Douglas A. Skoog, Donald M. West, F. James Holler 2013). Different isotopes of a given 
element can easily be distinguished because of the high resolution. Additionally, results from the 
MS can be further supported by DAD to determine compounds that have similar properties but 
different UV absorption (Gacek-Matthews et al. 2016; Larsen et al. 2007).  
1.6.3.2.4 High resolution 
Mass resolution measures the minimum mass difference ΔM between two peaks in a mass 
spectrum. Normally, mass spectrometers can easily distinguish ions with difference is only one 
mass unit, and hence provide us with more detailed and accurate values for the molecular mass of 
a compound. However, high resolution MS can detect 10-4 amu differences in mass between two 
compounds, which allows the determination of elemental composition boundaries for compounds 
under 600 Da (Xian et al. 2012; K. F. Nielsen et al. 2011; Kind & Fiehn 2010; De Hoffmann E 2007). 
Sometime there are interferences resulting from polyatomic ions, elements having isotopes with 
essentially the same mass, doubly charged ions, and refractory oxide ions, which can be reduced 
or eliminated by high resolution spectrometer (Douglas A. Skoog, Donald M. West, F. James Holler 
2013). At the same time, it might be complex for data analysis, since plentiful data is obtained from 
one sample in an untargeted manner and the neutral or poorly ionized molecules would not be 
==PAGE:25==
21 
 
detected (Covington et al. 2017). 
1.6.3.3 NMR 
Nuclear Magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy is an analytical technique used by chemists to 
determine and establish the structure of molecules. The concept of NMR is that nuclei of many 
elemental isotopes like hydrogen (1H) and carbon (13C) have characteristic spin which produces 
their own magnetic field. When an external magnetic field is applied, the energy gap produces 
radio frequency named as Fourier Transform. When processing the Fourier Transform, a series of 
peaks is obtained called NMR Spectrum (Seger et al. 2013).  
Proton (1H-NMR) and Carbon NMR (13C-NMR) spectrum are primarily used to study the structure 
of molecules. A proton NMR spectrum consists of peaks which indicate the number of protons in 
the molecule. The position of the peaks termed as chemical shift shows the chemical environment 
they are in and is measured in parts per million (ppm). The peak height determines the number of 
proton under each NMR signal. The splitting of NMR peak in 1H-NMR indicates the number of 
neighboring protons. Two-dimensional (2D) NMR spectroscopy provides data plotted between two 
one dimensional (1D) NMR spectrums along horizontal and vertical axis. If the 2D plot along axis is 
between two 1H NMR spectrums, it is called homonuclear correlation spectroscopy (COSY). COSY 
spectrum provides connectivity between neighboring protons. Heteronuclear multiple-bond 
correlation spectroscopy (HMBC) is a 2D spectrum which determines Carbon to Hydrogen 
connectivity (Aue et al. 1976).

3 Results and Discussion 
The experimental work performed for the past half a year has been focused on the cichorine 
biosynthetic pathway derived from 3-methyl orsellenic acid (3-MOA) in the genetic model 
organism Aspergillus nidulans. The successful biosynthetic study of the cichorine pathway in A. 
nidulans by Klejnstrup and Sanchez (Sanchez et al. 2012b; Klejnstrup 2012) encouraged us to 
explore futher in two AN6446/pkbR over expression strains; NID762 and NID2066. The strain 
NID762 is AN6446 overexpressed in the integration site IS1 and strain NID2066 is AN6446 
overexpressed in both IS1 and IS5, which means the expression is stronger (Hansen et al. 2011). 
The reference strain of A. nidulans used in this study was NID3. The main objective of my research 
project was to isolate and characterize new intermediate and shunt metabolites from the cichorine 
gene cluster which were not reported by Klejnstrup et al (Klejnstrup 2012). We hypothesize that 
the characterization of new intermediate metabolites will give us insight how nitrogen is 
incorporated in cichorine 9 and other nitrogen containing compounds. We also aimed to 
determine the optimal growth conditions particularly temperature for A. nidulans metabolites.  
A. nidulans strains were analyzed by using UHPLC-DAD-TOF-MS and all the obtained data was 
dereplicated by Agilent Masshunter Qualitative analysis B06.00 software (Agilent Technologies, CA, 
and USA) and compared with compounds in ACD AntiBase database (Laatsch 2012). The 
Masshunter provides original experimental data as Total ion chromatogram (TIC). The metabolome 
data was then studied in the base peak chromatogram (BPC), the diode array detector (DAD) and 
mass spectrum. The results are described in the following sections and the experimental details 
can be found in Materials and Methods (Chapter 2).

3.2 Investigating optimum temperature and time for enrichment of 
selected natural compounds  
To investigate the optimum temperature for enrichment of natural compounds in Aspergillus 
nidulans NID762, the strains were grown as three-point inoculation in the MM agar medium at 30 ℃ 
and 37 ℃, respectively, for 5 days and 7 days. After five days of cultivation, NID762 formed smaller 
size colony at 30 ℃ than at 37 ℃.  There was brownish red pigmentation in the medium at 30 ℃ 
which was not observed in the NID762 grew at 37 ℃, see Figure 3.8. They displayed highly similar 
metabolites profiles with each other in different temperature and growth time, see Figure 3.9 and 
Figure 3.10. It was shown in the BPC that the production of novel metabolites from strain NID762 
was relatively higher when cultivated at 30 ℃ than 37 ℃ (Figure 3.9). We also reexamined when 
the strains grew to seven days, the production decreased visibly (Figure 3.10). Consequently, it was 
proved that growing on supplement MM medium at 30 ℃ for five days is the optimum conditions 
for NID762 strain to enrich the candidate compounds.

solid-phase extraction on a set of SPE columns with a small amount of crude extract (2 mg). Two

3.8 Proposed cichorine biosynthetic pathway derived from 3-MOA 
The characterization of argicichorine allowed us to propose a more detailed biosynthetic pathway 
for cichorine 9. The biosynthetic pathway (Figure 3.30) begins with one acetyl-CoA, two malonyl-
CoA, and one S-adenosyl methionine (SAM) to produce 3-dimethyl orsellinic acid 2 catalyzed by the 
PKS encoded by gene pkbA. two other pyrones 1 and 3 indicated that PkbA can produce different 
C6 and C8 polyketide scaffolds (Klejnstrup 2012). In the next step, O-methylation of 3-dimethyl 
orsellinic acid 2 is catalyzed by predicted O-methyltransferase encoded by AN6447. On one side 
AN6449 might catalyze the hydroxylation of the 6-methyl group in methoxy-3-MOA 4 to forming 
nidulol 5. On another side, 3-dimethyl orsellinic acid 2 undergoes a reduction reaction catalyzed by 
a putative oxidoreductase encoded by AN6450 to form 4-hydroxy-2-methoxy-3,6- dimethyl-
benzaldehyde 6, following by a similar hydroxylation to form 4-hydroxy-2-methoxy-3-methyl-6-
hydroxymethyl-benzaldehyde 7. It is followed by another oxidation to form the dialdehyde 8 by a 
predicted aryl-alcohol oxidase encoded by AN6445. The following reaction leading to argicichorine 
10 incorporation of nitrogen via arginine catalyzed by the NRPS enzyme encoded by AN6444, then 
undergoes a cleavage to the final target cichorine 9.

4 Conclusion and Future perspective  
This work has overexpressed the transcription factor gene AN6446, which is responsible to activate 
the pkbA gene cluster, in Aspergillus nidulans. A major unknown metabolite named argicichorine 
10 has been isolated, purified successfully from the overexpression strain. The structure 
elucidation demonstrates that argicichorine is an important intermediate towards cichorine and 
other nitrogen containing compounds. We substantiate that gene AN6444 encodes a NRPS enzyme 
which catalyzes the incorporation of arginine into argicichorine. Altogether it provides new insight 
into understanding of the biosynthetic pathway of nitrogen incorporated 3-MOA derived natural 
products in Asperdillus nidulans. This project also determined that cultivation of A. nidulans strain 
at 30 ℃ for 5 days is the optimal growth conditions to produce metabolites related to the cichorine 
pathway.  
In addition, the isolation and characterization of compound 11 with monoisotopic mass of 
[M+H]+=543.32 and compound 12 with [M+H]+=557.34 as well as compound 13 and 14, are 
expected to be performed in the follow up study. We speculate that compound 11 and 12 are 
relevant to argicichorine with prenylated side chains attached. Thus, further exploration of 
biosynthetic pathway of pkbA gene cluster could be achieved.
