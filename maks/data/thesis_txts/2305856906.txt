Abstract
Flexible pipes are a crucial instrument in transporting fluids between the oil platform
and subsea wells on the seabed. In the pursuit of reaching deeper and deeper oil fields,
the traditional approach of using steel armouring, is approaching a limit for what wa-
ter depths the materials can grant access to. In an attempt to avoid more expensive
measures, it has been proposed to use fibre composites for the armouring, which provide
much higher strength-to-weight ratios than steel. The introduction of fibre composites
however demands for a much higher level of detail for the analyses of the internal wire
stresses, in order to assess stability of the wires.
In this thesis an attempt is made to further develop an analytical theory, describing how
bending of a flexible pipe influences the internal tensile armour wire. Modifications and
additions are made to an existing analytical steel-wire derived theory. The influence of
the more compliant wire profile is studied w.r.t. the normal forces the wire sees. Fur-
thermore, the contact pressure under the wire is determined as well as the geometrically
determined curvatures of the wires. An attempt is also made to determine the shear
forces acting on the wire. This attempt was however not successful, as it was determined
that the analytical theory is not suited for determining these shear forces. It is suspected
that an numerical model is needed, which can solve the internal force equilibriums, and
in doing so determine the shear forces.
The validity of the derived theory is discussed thoroughly, and it is concluded that the
existing validity of the theory, for analysis of steel armoured pipes, is not sufficient to
base the validity of this theory on w.r.t. fibre composites. Furthermore, many sources
of possible inaccuracies of this theory are identified. Based on these conclusion a re-
commendation is made, to form a basis for studying and validating the deformations
of a fibre composite armoured flexible pipe. As it is very difficult to obtain the needed
information from a physical test, it is recommended to form a detailed numerical model.
This model has to be sufficiently detailed, to catch all the important phenomena, which
may influence the resulting deformation.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Unbounded Flexible Pipes
Unbounded Flexible Pipes are mainly used in the oil an gas industry. These flexible
pipes are used as risers, flowlines and jumpers for transporting fluids and gases between
offshore platforms and subsea wells. The flexible pipes compete with and complement
normal rigid steel pipes, which are also used as risers and flowlines. Although flexible
pipes are more expensive than their rigid counterparts, they offer many advantages w.r.t.
installation and operation. Installation is fast, since the flexible pipes can be produced
in very long sections, up to several kilometres, and transported on reels or turntables.
Due to the speed of installation, flexible pipes can be cost-effective compared to rigid
pipes. The biggest advantage of flexible pipes used as risers is their structural flexibility,
which is necessary for floating platforms.
1.1.1
Description of the Layers of an Unbounded
Flexible Pipe
Figure 1.1: Illustration of structural layers of an unbounded flexible pipe.
Figure 1.1 shows the structural layers of an unbounded, family III, flexible pipe. The
functions of the layers are as follows:
==PAGE:14==
2

• Carcass: Flat steel strip, which is bent and interlocked with itself, allowing some
relative movement along pipe axis. The function of the carcass is to give resistance
towards external pressure and forces.
• Inner Liner: Polymer extrusion. The function of the inner liner is to keep the
fluids in the bore and to transfers the internal bore pressure to the pressure armour.
• Pressure Armour: C-shaped steel wires wound in two layers, each wire inter-
locking two wires of the opposite layer, allowing some relative movement along
pipe axis. The function of the pressure armour is to provide radial resistance to
carry the internal bore pressure.
• Tensile Armour: Rectangular steel wire wound with low lay-angle. Even number
of layers wound in alternating direction. The function of the tensile armour is to
provide resistance in the axial direction, supporting tensile loads on the pipe.
• Outer Sheath: Polymer extrusion. The function of the outer sheath is to protect
the internal layers from external sea-water.
Other pipe layer compositions also exist, where some layers are omitted or exchanged
with other wire profiles. In addition, the pipe might also include insulation tapes outside
the tensile armour and an additional polymer extrusion on the outside to provide stiffness
and abrasion protection. Polymeric anti-wear tapes are normally laid on both sides of the
tensile armour of dynamic risers. High-strength anti-birdcaging tapes are often applied
on the outside of the tensile armour, to restrict radial movement during compressive
loads. Finally, auxiliary intermediate tapes are also included between most layers, to
aid production of the pipes.
Due to the severe loads a flexible pipe is subjected to, high strength steels grades are
needed for the wires. The steels also have to be relatively resistant to corrosion, due to
permeation of vapour through the inner liner, and due to the possibility of breaching of
the outer sheath.
1.2
Motivation for Using Fibre Composites
Due to the high amount of steel armouring, the flexible pipes are relatively heavy. A
typical 6” ID flexible riser may weigh well over 100 kg
m . Submerged into seawater, and
accounting for buoyancy, the relative weight is somewhat lower, but still substantial.
The relative weight of a liquid filled flexible pipe, submerged into water, is close to the
combined weight of the steel layers, since the density of the polymers is close to the
density of water.
The high weight of the flexible pipes becomes significant when using them for high water
depth purposes. The topside tension, supporting the weight of the riser section from
==PAGE:15==

the seabed to platform, may for 2 km water depth be of the magnitude of several mega
Newtons. This gives high requirements for the equipment on the installation vessels, the
hang-of structure on the oil platform, and finally also the tensile strength of the flexible
riser itself. The tensile strength of the riser can be increased by adding tensile armour
material to the riser, but this also adds additional weight to the pipe. The flexible riser
design is therefore approaching a limit for what water depths it can reach, unsupported.
The current steel armoured flexible pipes can reach water depths of more than 2000 m,
which is needed for some of the pre-salt fields, but it is difficult to reach depths of beyond
3000 m.
One method of reducing the topside tension, which is currently being used, is to add
buoyancy to the riser.
This is done by clamping buoyancy modules to a section of
the riser, lifting this section and thus forming a wave-shaped riser configuration. This
approach reduces topside tension significantly, but it adds expense due to the purchase
and additional installation time of the buoyancy modules. It also requires a longer riser,
since the wave configuration is longer than a free-hanging catenary configuration.

mour wires. Fibre composite tensile armour wire concept by the Flexibles
branch of National Oilwell Varco.
An alternative method of decreasing topside tension, is by using lighter material for the
flexible pipe. This is where fibre composites bring many wanted properties. Unidirec-
tional carbon fibres and glass fibres embedded in a resin matrix, can form composites
with similar tensile strength as steel, with a density of less than a quarter of the dens-
ity of steel. Generally, carbon fibre composites have higher tensile strength and lower
density than glass fibre composites, but they are also considered more expensive. Fibre
composites can have good fatigue performance, depending on the load situation. An-
other good property, is that fibre composites are not sensitive to corrosion, although
there may be other unwanted responses to chemicals and temperature.

1.3
Loading of a Flexible Pipe
During operation, a flexible pipe is subjected several types of loads, depending on the
application. Flowlines are subject to high external pressure and internal pressure, but
generally low tension and curvature with no dynamics. Risers can see the same pressures
at the seabed, but also the high tension topside, and well as bending curvature topside,
at the touchdown point with the seabed and at potential mid-water curves. Due to the
dynamic sea state, with waves and currents, both tension and curvature are dynamic.
Internal pressure can have some transients, due to clogging or changes in production,
but these are normally not considered a part of operation conditions.
The loads acting on a flexible pipe can be obtained by simulating the pipelines and
vessel motions, for a considered sea state, with numerical software packages, such as
the commercial OrcaFlex. The resulting pipe loads from such a simulation, can come
in various forms, such as time series, of tension and curvatures, or compressed to mean
values and amplitude values, of the cycling tension and curvature. The tension and
curvatures results vary throughout the pipelines, and specific locations may be chosen for
detailed wire stress analyses. Typical fatigue critical locations include the bend stiffener
area or bell mouth area at hang-off, the touchdown area of the riser and potential mid-
water areas with high curvatures.
1.3.1
Loading of a Tensile Armour Wire
The internal loading of a tensile armour wire can be analysed based on the external
loading of the flexible pipe. The response of a straight pipe to axis-symmetrical loads,
such as tension, pressure and torsion, is well understood, and has been described in for
example (Witz and Tan, 1992a) and (Sævik, 1992). The response to bending has also
been studied a great deal, which has resulted in a few models. In (Leroy and Estrier,
2001) a numerical model is developed, which assumes zero curvature gradient along
the pipe, and solves force and moment equilibrium for one wire representing each layer,
accounting for interaction between the layers. In (Sævik, 1992) the grounds were laid for
a simplified finite element model of the tensile armour layers of a pipe. This theory has
been developed further in (Sævik, 1993), and takes into account the effect of proximity
to an end fitting, where the wires are further restricted from movement.
Prior to the development of the numerical models, an analytical theory has also been
developed. The development of this theory is discussed in Chapter 2. This theory is still
widely used for stress analyses of steel armoured flexible pipes, and has some advantages
over the numerical models. Among these advantages are the computational cost of the

analysis, and furthermore that the analytical nature of the theory gives an insight into
the what drives the internal wire movement and forces. This analytical theory has been
developed for the analysis of steel wires. The stress state of an internal wire is dominated
by the axial stress, and since steel is an isotropic material, the other stress components
are not important as important as the axial stress, and are thus ignored. In this thesis,
an attempt is made to further develop this analytical theory, in order to make it suited
for studying fibre composite tensile armour wires.
Generally it has been considered reasonable to separate the axis-symmetrical analysis
and bending analysis of a flexible pipe. The total response to loading of a pipe is then
found by the super-position principle, as the sum of the deformations from the two
analyses. This approach is used for this analytical theory and also the numerical model
by (Leroy and Estrier, 2001). It is not clear if the numerical model by Sævik separates
these analyses.
1.3.2
A Brief Discussion of Fibre Composite Failure
Modes
In order to limit the scope of this thesis the objective is not to study the internal stress
state of the fibre composite wire, but rather at one abstraction level higher, to study the
loads acting on the wire, and the geometrical deformation of the wire. A brief discussion
is provided of the most normal stress and strain related fibre composite damages.
A unidirectional (UD) fibre composite laminate consists of a number of fibres embed-
ded into a polymer matrix. The function of the fibres is to provide strength, while the
polymer provides bondage between the fibres. The laminate can be described as an
orthotropic material, i.e. a material with different properties in the three principal dir-
ections. The properties in the direction, along which the fibres are aligned, is dominated
by the strength of the fibre material, and the stiffness can be approximated as the fibre
stiffness times the volume fraction of fibres. The properties are also dependent on the
length of the fibres. The properties of an UD laminate in the two other orthogonal dir-
ections are dominated by the matrix material, and can be considered equal if the fibres
are spread equally along both directions all the way through the thickness.
The most common fibre composite damages are as follows:
• De-bonding between fibre and matrix: Two types of de-bonding can occur:
parallel to the fibres and perpendicular to the fibres.
The perpendicular de-
bonding happens due to loads perpendicular to the fibres, where the strength
of the bonding is exceeded. This damage is accelerated due to the non-perfect
distribution of fibres in the matrix. At locations in the laminate cross-section,
where the fibres are concentrated, the localised strain of the matrix is increased,
since the matrix material has significantly lower stiffness than the fibre. At these
localised strain magnifications the bonding is more likely to break. The de-bonding
==PAGE:18==
6
1 Introduction
can lead to a crack growth between the fibres, which might happen suddenly, or
due to a fatigue driven crack growth. Parallel de-bonding happens due to loads
parallel to the fibres. This normally happens at the end of a fibre, when the shear
stress exceeds the strength of the bonding. The growth of a parallel de-bonding is
most common due to fatigue loading.
• Matrix cracking: Is the potential catastrophic result of perpendicular de-bonding,
where the cracks extend throughout the matrix, and are visible to the naked eye.
Matrix cracking normally initiates at the free ends of a specimen, in this case likely
at the edges of the laminates.
• Fibre fracture: Fibre fracture happens due to loads parallel to the fibres. Since
the cross-sectional dimensions and spread of the fibres is not perfectly equal, the
fibres do not carry completely equal loads. Individual fibres will therefore start to
fracture before the laminate in axially strained to its tensile strength. When the
first fibres have fractured, the adjacent fibres have to carry a higher load. Fibre
fracture can therefore spread across the cross-section, and can be caused by both
static and fatigue loads.
• Delamination: Delamination happens when the shear stress between laminates
exceeds the strength of the laminate bonding. This can happen due to a mismatch
in elastic properties of adjacent laminates, but also in general due to shear stresses
between the laminates from external loading. Delamination often starts at a free
edge. In the case of the considered fibre composite tensile armour wire, delamina-
tion may also happen in the bonding between the laminates and the cradle.
Considering the presented failures it is clear that the complete load situation of the
fibre composite wire is needed in order to analyse the stability of the composite. It is
also clear that it is important to account for the fatigue damages of the composite, as
micro-cracks can develop catastrophically.

CHAPTER 2
Analytical Model
Féret and Bournazel (1987) introduced an analytical method for calculating stresses
and slip in structural layers of unbounded flexible pipes during bending. This method
is based on an assumption that the wire follows a specified trajectory during bending.
The wire strains can then be calculated based on the deformation of this trajectory. In
(Féret and Bournazel, 1987) the trajectory assumed was the so called geodesic. The
description of the theory in this paper was very brief.
Witz and Tan (1992b) presented a theory, which also used assumed a specified trajectory.
Witz and Tan however disputed the geodesic assumption, and proposed the loxodromic
assumption instead. Based on this theory a method was presented for calculating the
axial strain of the tensile armour wire for curvatures up to slip initiation. The critical
curvature at which slip occurs was also determined. Witz and Tan predicted two slip
regions on the pipe circumference: One slip region starts at the extrados/intrados and,
with increasing bending curvature, propagates towards the axis of neutral bending on
the pipe cross-section; the other slip region starts at the axis of neutral bending and
propagates towards the extrados/intrados. The neutral axis slip region was expected to
be dominant, and the two regions were expected to merge for sufficiently large curvatures.
Witz and Tan further presented a way to calculate the bi-directional bending curvature
and axial twist of the tensile armour wire as a simple projection of the bending curvature
of the pipe on the local principle directions of the wire.
In his PhD thesis Sævik (1992) elaborated further on the mathematics of the wire strains
including the strains due to bending and twist of the deformed wire trajectory. Sævik
also developed a simplified numerical FEM model for simulating wire movements on a
flexible pipe. His numerical studies showed that the wires indeed follow a geodesic path
if no friction exists to restrict wire movement, e.g. due to lack of intra-layer contact
pressures. However, the studies also showed that if friction and contact pressure exists,
this will restrict the wires from moving transversely. A study with realistic pipe loads
and conditions showed that the wire trajectory was closer to the loxodromic than the
geodesic.
The commercial fatigue analysis package for flexible pipes and umbilicals, Bflex (Sævik,
2010), includes this analytical model as a basis for a hybrid analytical/numerical analysis.
This hybrid analysis discretises the considered pipe only along its length axis giving a
series of cylindrical elements. The analysis uses FEM to evaluate the pipe movement
due to external loads based on the pipe bending stiffness calculated by the analytical
==PAGE:20==
8
2 Analytical Model
method. The analytical model is then used to calculate the internal wire stresses.
The wire movements and associated stresses inside a flexible pipe is a complex phe-
nomenon to solve analytically. It requires some relatively extensive and strict assump-
tions and is not able to account for all physical phenomena inside the pipe. The ana-
lytical theory is also not able to account for all external loads, such as contact with
other structures. A numerical method could potentially account for all of these loads
and phenomena and with less restrictive assumptions.
One of the main reasons for using an analytical theory as opposed to numerical simu-
lations to evaluate wire stresses is the computational expense of such simulations. The
flexible pipe structure is complex and needs a high level of discretisation and many fric-
tion/contact surfaces in order to simulate exactly. It is also not possible to reduce the
problem to two dimensions. At the time of development of this analytical theory, it was
simply not possible to computationally solve a problem of this size. At present com-
puter clusters have enough memory and computational power to solve such 3D models,
but even though these simulations normally simplify some of the pipe structure, these
simulations can still take days to solve, for each load case, on a multi-CPU HPC cluster.
This is not feasible for e.g. fatigue analyses of flexible risers, where several hundreds
or thousands of load cases normally get analysed for each riser. It is possible to sim-
plify the simulated problem somewhat in order to reduce the computational cost of the
simulation. This gives some semi-numerical analyses.
Another advantage of an analytical theory is that it gives an insight into which paramet-
ers influence the internal wire movement and stresses. This theory is therefore still used,
almost 30 years after started development, e.g. (Skeie, Sødahl, and Steinkjer, 2012) and
(Sævik, 2011).
2.1
Assumptions and Limitations of Model
In simplifying the physical problem to make an analytical model, some assumptions have
been made, some of which are stated directly in literature, and some which are implicit
from the analyses. These assumptions are summarized here:
• Zero curvature gradient along pipe.
• The considered pipe section is far enough from end termination, that its effects
are not present.
• Coulomb friction with identical static and dynamic friction coefficients governing
friction between considered tensile armour layer and its adjacent layers.
• ”Plane surfaces remain plane” (Navier’s hypothesis).
An exception is made to
this assumption: after the frictional shear capacity of the tensile armour wires is

2.1.1
Wire Kinematics
Two wire paths have been proposed in literature, the geodesic and the loxodromic - both
names inspired by navigation.
Figure 2.1: Illustration of a loxodrome. Figure borrowed from (Davies, 2016).
A loxodrome on a sphere is illustrated in Figure 2.1. In navigation a loxodromic path has
the property, that it crosses all meridians at a constant angle. Consequently a loxodromic
path appears as a straight line on a map (using the Mercator projection), and allows for
navigation with a constant compass bearing. In wire kinematics, the initial wire path
on a straight pipe is a loxodromic path, since the wires are wound on the pipe with a
constant lay-angle. The argument for the loxodromic path during pre-slip pipe bending
is that friction restricts the wires from moving, and thereby keeping the initial lay-angle.
==PAGE:22==
10
2 Analytical Model
It has been argued that during post-slip bending, the wires will still be limited from
movement transversely to their own axes, due to other effects, such as embedding into
the adjacent polymeric layers.
Figure 2.2: Illustration of a geodesic on a Robinson projection. Figure borrowed from
(Furuti, 2016).
A geodesic path on a sphere (also called a great circle) represents the shortest path
between two points on the sphere. In navigation, the geodesic path is therefore the
optimal path to follow, but it gives a rather complex path on map projections - see
for instance Figure 2.2. In wire kinematics the initial wire path on a straight pipe is
also a geodesic path, since the wires follow the shortest path between the extrados and
intrados. During bending, the path moves on the surface transversely to its own axis in
order to maintain the shortest path between extrados and intrados. A consequence of
following the shortest path between two points on a surface is, that the curvature of the
path around the surface normal is eliminated, i.e. the transverse curvature κ2 = 0. It
has been argued that during post-slip bending, the wires will move towards the geodesic
path, since this minimises the transverse bending energy and the axial strain energy.
The geodesic path is sometimes assumed during pre-slip bending even though the wire
movement is limited by friction.
Figure 2.3: Illustration of loxodromic and geodesic paths on a bent pipe. Figure bor-
rowed from (Sævik, 2010).
==PAGE:23==

A comparison between the loxodromic and geodesic paths on a bent pipe is illustrated
in Figure 2.3. These two curves are often considered a limit curves for how the wire
may deform under bending (Sævik, 1992, p. 2.13). The actual deformed wire path is
therefore expected to be somewhere in between these two curves. In this thesis, the
loxodromic wire path is assumed.
2.1.2
Limitations
In addition to the assumptions made, the model also has some limitations for what sort
of loading it can be used for and which effects it is able to account for:
• Interaction between wire layers is not accounted for.
• Ovalisation of pipe cross-section during bending is neglected.
• Due to wire slip disrupting the symmetry around the pipe centre-axis, the model
can only analyse curvature in a single plane.
• During derivation of the theory some terms have been linearised w.r.t. bending
curvature. The theory is therefore only applicable for sufficiently small curvatures.
The magnitude of curvatures the theory is applicable for is not studied, but it is
assumed that the model is applicable for most realistic curvatures.
• The method is not able to account for non-constant (temporal and spatial around
circumference) pressure on outside of pipe, for instance due to contact with bend
stiffener or seabed.

When a rectangular wire is wound helically on a cylinder it is deformed in three direc-
tions:
• Normal curvature κ3 (curvature about X3)
• Transverse or geodesic curvature κ2 (curvature about X2)
• Torsion κ1 (torsion about X1)
In order to calculate the curvatures and torsion of a wire a few concepts from differential
geometry are defined.
The path of a wire lying on a pipe can be seen as a curve in space, parametrised in
terms of the arc length of the wire. The only two quantities needed in order to define
the space curve are the normal curvature and torsion along the curve.
At any point of this curve we can attach a tangent unit vector T, which is identical to
G1. The unit principal normal vector N can also be uniquely defined by differentiating
T w.r.t. the arc-length X1 and normalising the resulting vector. This is possible, since
the space curve is parametrised w.r.t. the arclength, which means it has constant speed
along the curve, and thus all acceleration is orthonormal to the tangent. Finally the
binormal vector B is defined as the cross-product B = T × N. These three vectors
make up the right-handed orthonormal base triad [T, N, B], also named the Frenet-
Serret frame. An illustration of the Frenet-Serret frame can be seen in Figure 2.7. The
Frenet-Serret frame is very similar to the G-triad earlier. Both triads are right-handed,
comprise of orthonormal unit vectors and share the tangent vector G1 ≡T. The only
possible difference between the two triads is a rotation, denoted ω, around the tangent
vector. Indeed, it can be shown, that if the wire path is a geodesic on the pipe surface,
the rotation ω = 0 and the two triads are identical.

CHAPTER 3
Modifications to the
Analytical Model
As the current analytical model in Chapter 2 is developed for steel reinforced flexible
pipes some assumptions and simplification have been made, which do not necessarily
hold for fibre composites reinforced pipes. The following topics are studied:
• In the analysis of steel wires it is assumed that when the wires are in the stick
regime (see Figure 2.9), the entire wire cross-section will strain in order to follow
the strain of the pipe. Since the fibre composite wire cross-section is heterogeneous,
it may not strain uniformly. The effect of the relatively low shear modulus of the
polymer cradle is studied.
• As already mentioned frictional shear forces occur between adjacent layers dur-
ing bending, restricting the relative movement between these layers. Since the
tensile armour layers are wound in opposite directions, these frictional shear forces
may lead to transverse shear stresses in the wires. These shear forces are often
considered negligible for isotropic steel wires, but may be significant for fibre com-
posite wires.
• The contact pressures on both sides of the tensile armour layers is currently as-
sumed constant across the circumference and not effected by bending. However,
since pipe bending influences axial wire stresses and the geometry of the wire paths,
bending is expected to influence the contact pressure under the wires.

duced, with the dimensions given in Table 3.1 and the wire profile in Table 3.2. The
dimension of the polymer cradle described in Figure 3.1, are reduced to a uniform thick-

3.2
Interaction Between Tensile Armour Layers
The analytical wire kinematics theory, dealt with in this thesis, is based on two funda-
mental assumptions which will be discussed here. The first assumption states that the
wire movement during pipe bending will follow the pipe movement in a certain way; in
this thesis specifically along the loxodromic curve. The second assumption states that
the layers adjacent to the considered tensile armour layer will follow Navier’s hypothesis.
These two assumptions are the reason for both the biggest strength and biggest weakness
of this theory. The biggest strength of the theory is that these assumptions enable us
to solve the problem analytically, which both makes the problem computationally inex-
pensive, and makes it possible to study the influence of the different parameters directly
from the formulas. The biggest weakness of the theory is, that these two assumptions
are used as a substitute for solving the full set of force equilibriums of the wires. This
reduces the problem to only considering the equilibrium in the longitudinal direction of
the wire, with the adjacent layers following Navier’s hypothesis.
Intuitively, there are several problems with the two assumptions, which will be discussed
below:

3.2.1
Compliance of Adjacent Layers
The first problem with assuming Navier’s hypothesis is the compliance of the adjacent
layers. The stiffness of the adjacent layers might not be very high. If we for instance
consider the outermost TA layer, then the force equilibrium illustrated in Figure 3.2
shows that there is a frictional shear force between the TA wire and the adjacent layers.
On the outermost side of the wire, this adjacent layer can be either a polymeric sheath or
an insulation layer (neglecting the auxiliary layers). Neither of these possible layers have
very high stiffness, and it is therefore likely that this adjacent layer deforms more than
the considered TA wire. In fact assuming Navier’s hypothesis, in this theory, implies
that the adjacent layer has infinite stiffness, which is very far from the truth in this case.
The interface on the inside of the innermost TA layer connects the tensile wires to the
pressure-carrying layers (carcass, inner liner and pressure armour). Even though these
layers combined give a relatively high longitudinal stiffness, this stiffness is also governed
by the frictional shear capacity between the interlocked wires in the carcass and also in
the pressure armour. The supporting surface under the innermost TA layer comprising
of these layers therefore also exhibit some kind of stick-slip behaviour and possibly shear
of the liner. This complicates the force equilibrium for the tensile armour layer further,
but the behaviour is not accounted for in this theory.
3.2.2
TA Layers Wound in Opposing Directions
On the inside of the TA wire, it adjoins the innermost TA layer. Assuming Navier’s
hypothesis here is also problematic, even though this layer does have a relative high
stiffness.
At the contact point between the two wires in Figure 3.6 both wires will
have a gradient of normal force going along the wire towards the extrados, hence both
∆FN pointing upwards. If the angle between the wires (α1 + α2) was small, it could be
argued that the frictional shear forces between the two wires would point mostly along
the longitudinal axis of the other wire. This is however not the case as the wires are
normally wound with a lay-angle, α, of about 25◦- 35◦in opposing directions. The
combined angular difference is therefore relatively large, meaning that the longitudinal
frictional shear force acting on one wire (see (3.2)) is angled more transverse to the other

Seeing the impact of the varying stiffness and directionality of the flexible pipe layers,

governing all of the layer deformations, it is not possible to formulate exact analytical

There it was shown,

frictional forces from the adjacent supporting layer to the laminate core of the wire. This
is what gives the axial deformation of the core. In Section 3.1 a uniform axial strain of

Discussion of Implications of Non-uniform Contact
Pressure
The non-uniformity of the contact pressures has some implications for some of the as-
sumptions, which are made for this theory:
==PAGE:59==
3.3 Contact Pressure Under Tensile Armour Wires During Bending

• Decoupling of axis-symmetrical analysis and bending analysis: One of the
implications of (3.22) is that the change in contact pressure under the wire depends
on the total axial stress of the wire, and not only from the stress arising from pipe
bending. This is due to the change in the normal curvature κ3. It is therefore
not possible do this bending analysis fully independent of the axis-symmetrical
analysis. This is however already the case for the slip mechanism discussed in
Section 2.2.4, where the contact pressure is needed for the analysis.
• Symmetry of slip mechanism: An other implication of the non-uniform contact
pressure is that the dynamics of the post-slip regime changes. The slip mechanism
discussed in Section 2.2.4 assumes uniform contact pressure, and as a consequence
gives symmetry of the transition between slip and stick v∗about the plane spanned
by Z1 and Z3 (see Figure 2.9) and antisymmetry of axial wire stress. This sym-
metry and antisymmetry gets disrupted in the post-slip regime, since the addi-
tional contact pressure on the tensile half of the circumference prolongs the stick
regime, and conversely the stick regime is shortened on the compressive side, due
to decreased contact pressure. The axial wire stress and contact pressure therefore
implicitly depend on each other leading to some sort of differential equation. This
differential equation has thus far not been derived, and may not have an explicit
solution. Intuitively, it is however expected that the accelerated wire slip on the
compressive side of the circumference, gives rise to a reduction in axial stresses on
the tensile side. Conversely the prolonged stick regime on the tensile side may lead
to increased axial stresses at high curvatures. It is therefore difficult to predict the
combined implications of the non-uniform contact pressure.
• Layers remain in contact: The total contact pressure given by (3.23) can be-
come negative, if the wire is in axial compression, and only low contact pressure
acts on the outside of the wire. This is not a physical phenomenon, but would
rather mean that the wire rises from the supporting surface. This would how-
ever void the validity this analytical theory, since it contradicts the assumption
presented in Chapter 2, that the layers remain in contact.
• Plane surfaces remain plane: Considering Figure 3.12 it is clear that the
net sum force of the contact pressures of the tensile armour wires acting on the
supporting surface in the considered cross-section is a force acting towards the
intrados of the circumference (in the Z2-direction). This will likely lead to some
degree of shear deformation of the pipe in the Z2Z3 cross-section. The magnitude
of this shear deformation has not been analysed.
• Ovalisation of pipe cross-section: Considering the non-uniform distribution
of contact pressures around the circumference seen in Figure 3.12 it is likely that
some degree of cross-sectional ovalisation occurs. The deformation of the cross-
section would however probably not give an oval shape, as the contact pressure
increases at the extrados and decreases at the intrados. This deformation would

3.4
Creep and Other Transient Mechanisms
The presented theory does not account for transient phenomena such as creep of the
polymers. It is however likely that polymer creep will occur, especially considering that
flexible pipes are often used for transporting fluids of elevated temperatures.
Since
friction transfers loads between the internal pipe layers, the polymer layers will see
some stresses. The wave periods, which the pipe moves according to, are measured in
seconds, and it is therefore not likely that any creep will happen during the periodic
pipe movement. On the other hand it is likely that the stresses in the polymers due
to static loads, will lead to polymer creep, which relaxes those stresses. This may lead
to some deformation of the pipe geometry, and therefore indirectly influence the cyclic
bending conditions.
Another transient mechanism, which is suspected might occur in a flexible pipe, is what
is here termed a ”transient slip”. This mechanism describes a slow transient slip, which
occurs even when the frictional forces are below the frictional capacity, due to repeated
cyclic loading. The motivation for this suspicion is that a flexible pipe will see extremely
many loading cycles, of different magnitude and period, during its lifetime. If a pipe
is bent to a mean curvature, which is below slip, and repeatedly loaded with bending,
tension and pressure variations, then it is expected that micro-slip will occur. This is
expected even if the loading is at such an level, that the wires should not slip by this
presented theory. Micro-slip will gradually over the many cycles allow the wires to slip
towards an unstressed state at the mean bending curvature. This mechanism is not
verified, but may be considered a possible source of inaccuracy for the presented theory.
3.5
Discussion of Theory Validity
In Section 3.2 and Section 3.3 some phenomena are identified, which introduce conflicts
with the stated assumptions. These conflicts do not necessarily render the presented
theory completely invalid and useless for analysing flexible pipes. Rather, they represent,
to greater or lesser degree, sources for inaccuracies of the presented theory. Common for
most of these phenomena is, that the magnitude of their influence depends on loading,
geometry and dimensions of the pipe and wire. The influence of these inaccuracies may
therefore be at an acceptable level for some cases, while they dominate the results for
other cases. The influence of some phenomena might never reach a significant level for
realistic pipes and loading, while other phenomena might pose a serious limitation to the
applicability of this theory. It is therefore important to scrutinise the presented theory
for possible inaccuracies. This can be done by comparisons to tests or more detailed
simulations.

It has to be stated, that the presented theory is not fully finished for analysis of fibre
composite wires, since the longitudinal shear loading of the wires has not been determ-
ined. As discussed in Section 3.2.4, this theory may not be suitable for determining this
loading. The presented theory is however able to give the longitudinal normal force,
contact pressure, curvature and torsion of the wire, as well as an upper bound limit
for transverse shear forces. The validity of these results has yet to be established. If it
is possible to get an conservative estimate of the longitudinal shear force, then it it is
possible to determine the complete internal stress state of the wire.
3.5.1
Validity for Steel Armour Analysis
The theory in Chapter 2 has been used for comparisons with fatigue bending tests
of steelwire armoured flexible pipes, for instance in (Sævik, 2011). This comparison
showed reasonable agreement with strain measurements and fatigue damage. Several
other fatigue bending tests have been performed by flexible pipe producers, but whose
results are confidential. In many of these tests, fatigue damage predictions, calculated
based on stresses from the existing theory, have given similar or higher fatigue damage,
than what is experienced in the tests. This provides confidence in the theory, in that it
gives conservative stress results.
Among the difficulties of making direct comparisons with physical tests, is the prob-
lem of determining correct material properties, which are used for the stress analysis.
Many material properties can be obtained relatively precise, and do not depend on cir-
cumstances. On the other hand a property such as friction coefficient can be tricky to
determine, since it depends on which material surfaces are connected, possible lubric-
ation and, in dealing with polymers, also on the temperature. At the same time the
friction coefficient can have a big impact on the stress analysis, as the frictional shear
capacity, and thus potential stress, scales linearly with it. Circumstances such as high
temperature and condensed water in the annulus can therefore influence the physical
behaviour of a pipe significantly.
Comparing fatigue predictions, based on stress analyses, with test results is also problem-
atic, due to the highly non-linear relation between stresses and fatigue damage. Small
differences in stresses or uncertainty in material SN curves can therefore give very large
differences in the results. Comparing with fatigue results is therefore only useful for
determining if a stress analysis is conservative or not.
Some numerical studies have been made w.r.t. the kinematics of steel TA wires. Sævik
(1992) showed that the loxodromic assumption was most realistic for wire bending. Leroy
and Estrier (2001) studied repeated bending around a non-zero mean bending curvature.
This study showed that the transverse curvature of the wires (at v = π
2) varied with
similar amplitude over many cycles, which indicates that they followed the loxodromic
curve. On the other side the study also showed that the mean transverse curvature moved
towards zero, which can be interpreted as a tendency to move towards the geodesic path
of the mean bending curvature.

3.5.2
Validation For Fibre Composite Armouring
Introducing fibre composite wires into the flexible pipes increases transverse as well as
longitudinal compliance of the TA layers of the pipe. This means that the comparisons
to tests, which have been made for steel armoured pipes, might not be representative
for fibre composite armoured pipes. Moreover, the existing comparisons with tests have
almost solely focused on longitudinal stresses and strains, which is reasonable, since it
is dominating w.r.t. fatigue of steel TA wires. It is also very difficult to measure other
aspects of the state of the wire since it is encapsulated in the annulus of a pipe within
other layers.
The stress state and sensitivity to non-longitudinal stress is much more complex for fibre
composite wires than for steel wires. A thorough validation of all aspects of the theory
is therefore needed in order to achieve confidence in the theory. This is very difficult to
do with full scale testing, since most aspects are not measurable. It is also very costly
to perform full scale tests. Small scale testing is more feasible, and definitely needed
for studying the response of the composite fibre to loading. This can for instance be
used for determining fatigue performance of the wire. Small scale testing is however not
possible for validate this theory, since the theory deals with the response of an entire
pipe, and how the pipe transfers external loads to the internal wires.
Axial strain, during full scale tests, is to some degree measurable, by an optical mon-
itoring system. This is done by embedding optical fibres into some of the wires and
transmitting light at the end of the fibre. Bragg gratings are etched at certain locations
along this optical fibre, and these gratings reflect a certain wavelength back along the
fibre. Straining the optical fibre at the bragg grating varies the wavelength which is
reflected, and by measuring the reflected light it is possible to calculate the strain at the
location of the grating.
Due to the difficulty of measuring the internal state in a flexible pipe, during tests,
simulating is the most realistic method to validate this theory. One way of simulating
the pipe is by full scale modelling the pipe in a FEA. FEA is however not an answer book,
and the FEA also has to be validated. This is very time consuming and computationally
expensive, but it has been shown to give good results (for steel TA wires), at least
w.r.t. longitudinal strain (Lukassen et al., 2016). If the model is able to reproduce the
longitudinal strain, it can be assumed that it reproduces many of the physical effects
correctly.
It is however impossible to fully include all physical layers and effects in such a FEA,
and the level of discretisation of the mesh is also limited, due to the wast number of
DOFs this produces. The simplification for FEA often includes neglecting the auxiliary
internal layers, and truncating the carcass, liner and pressure armour to one ortho-
tropic shell layer. Neglecting the auxiliary layer is not expected to introduce significant
sources of inaccuracies, as these layers are thin and have limited stiffness. These layer
might however introduce some shear deformation and influence the interlayer friction.
Substituting the three pressure-holding layers with a single orthotropic layer can give

3.6
Proposals for Further Work
If it is possible to create a highly detailed FEM model of a fibre composite armoured
flexible pipe, which sufficient confidence could be achieved for, then it can be used to
study the influence of the discussed phenomena. This could be used to determine which
of the phenomena need to be accounted for, and which can be disregarded. This FEM
model could also act as a basis to compare the presented theory with, and to identify
which areas need further development.
The presented analytical theory, with an assumed wire path and assumed Navier’s hy-
pothesis, might not be the best choice for studying fibre composite armoured pipes, due
to the inherent ”balanced” internal wire movements. While it might be well suited to
study the uni-axially dominated stress state of a steel wire, it has some shortcomings for
studying the shear stresses, which are critical for fibre composites. It might be therefore
be beneficial to study other methods. (Lambert and Felix-henry, 2012) uses the numer-
ical theory described in (Leroy and Estrier, 2001). This theory uses a finite difference
method to solve the force and moment equilibriums of each layer, considering inter-layer
interaction. The method considers one wire per layer, discretised along its length axis
and allows 2 DOFs per element, tangential displacement and transverse displacement.
This theory holds the same burden of proof w.r.t. validation for analyses of fibre compos-
ite wires. In (Leroy, Perdrizet, et al., 2010) this method has been compared to two FEM
models, with two levels of complexity. This comparison was done for a steel armoured
pipe and showed good agreement for axial stress and transverse curvature. Validation
specifically w.r.t. fibre composites wires has not been found in literature.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
The goal of this master thesis is to develop a theory for analysing the loading of a fibre
composite tensile armour wires on a flexible pipe during bending. This has been achieved
to the level permissible by the chosen analytical theory. The normal force of the wire has
been obtained, also when considering the shear deformation of the polymer cradle. The
normal and transverse curvature as well as torsion of the wire is obtained based on an
assumption of a loxodromic wire path. The contact pressure on both sides of the tensile
armour wires is obtained. Finally an upper bound limit for the transverse shear forces,
acting on a wire, are obtained. It has not been possible to determine the longitudinal
shear forces acting along a wire, due to the ”balanced” nature of the analytical theory,
where the assumption of Navier’s hypothesis leads to both sides of the wire deforming
at the same rate.
In general it has been found, that the approach, this theory uses, of using an assumed
deformation of the wire path, in stead of solving the internal force equilibriums, makes
this theory unsuitable for determining the shear forces acting on the wires.
The theory is very convenient for calculating normal forces, contact pressures as well
as curvatures of the wires. The validity of this theory can to some degree be based
on the fact that this theory is accepted and used for steel wires.
This validity, for
analysis of steel wires, is however only based on the experience, that the theory provides
conservative results for longitudinal stresses. The theory is therefore not validated for
contact pressures and wire curvatures. Furthermore a number of phenomena have been
identified, which might undermine the stated assumptions, which the theory is based on.
The influence of these phenomena is not known, and it may be increased by introducing
the more compliant fibre composite wires into the pipe; especially shear compliance is
much larger for fibre composite wires than steel wires.
It is therefore concluded, that even for the determined normal force, contact pressures
and wire curvatures, the theory lacks validation. The difficulty of validating the theory
has also been described. No physical bending tests of fibre composite tensile armoured
pipes are available, and even if such an expensive and difficult test should be performed,
then it is very difficult to measure and compare the relevant deformations and forces. The
best way of obtaining a basis for studying and comparison for validation, is therefore to
develop a detailed numerical model of the fibre composite armoured flexible pipe. This is
therefore proposed as the most immediate task for future work. This detailed numerical
model could then be used as a basis for future development of a method for analysing
fibre composite wires.
