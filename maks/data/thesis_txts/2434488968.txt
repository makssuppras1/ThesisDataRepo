Abstract
Aerodynamic admittances for bluﬀbodies have traditionally been determined using wind
tunnel experiments or conservative estimates. This project evaluates the quality of an
aerodynamic admittance for a circular disk normal to the direction of ﬂow determined
from Large Eddy Simulations against results produced in a wind tunnel and against an
empirical model.
The turbulent inﬂow was generated using the Synthetic Eddy Method to achieve a close-
to-reality spectrum of coherent structures in the ﬂow domain surrounding the disk.
In the bulk of the spectrum very good agreement is found between the three sources, while
for both very low frequencies and higher frequencies a deviation from the empirical model
was seen for both numerical data as well as experimental data. Attempts at explanations
to both deviations are covered, by investigating Kelvin-Helmholtz instability-derived struc-
tures in the ﬂow as well as regular vortex shedding, however, further investigation would
be necessary to fully understand thes behaviour in these regions of the spectrum.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The eﬀect of wind loading on structures have been a subject of great interest in the past
century, specially when it comes to certain types of structures. Famously, on November
7th in 1940, Tacoma Narrows, a suspension bridge, collapsed due to wind eﬀects acting
on it. This sparked the onset of research into the eﬀects of wind on structures. Alan
G. Davenport presented in 1961 a model for how slender structures would react when
exposed to a turbulent wind. This relationship between the incoming wind and the forces
felt by the structure is known as the aerodynamic admittance, which relates the spectral
resolutions of the wind velocities and the forces. An example is PF = g ⋅Pu where g is a
composite of the aerodynamic admittance function and other factors that play a role in
the correlation between the spectrum of drag forces and the spectrum of wind speeds. The
model has since been widely used in bridge design and engineering. However, the model he
proposed was only applicable to slender and line-like structures, such as bridges or very tall
buildings. Another model was needed for so-called bluﬀbuildings. In the following years,
Davenports work was adapted to bluﬀbuildings by B. J. Vickery and P. W. Bearman. But,
while the original work of Davenport kept being used and developed further, the bluﬀ-
body counterpart was seemingly left alone since 1970. While still applicable, many leaps of
technology have been made since then. The work that Davenport, Vickery and Bearman
did in the 1960s was all based on wind tunnel experiments and the same approach is still
widely used in the industry to this day. However, since then, computer power has been
growing exponentially allowing even personal computers to perform simulations of ﬂows.
This practise is known as Computational Fluid Dynamics, CFD. The present project will
focus on investigating how well it is possible to apply Davenport and Vickery’s models to
data found using CFD to determine the so-called aerodynamic admittance function for a
given bluﬀbody compared to results based on wind tunnel tests.

2.1
Turbulence
Fluid ﬂows can roughly be divided into two regimes, laminar and turbulent ﬂows. The
laminar ﬂow is calm and simple ﬂow, while the turbulent ﬂow is chaotic. While laminar
ﬂow is a very interesting subject for many applications, the turbulent ﬂows are of more
relevance to civil engineering ﬁelds. In 1982 Milton Van Dyke made a collection of very
detailed visualizations of ﬂuid ﬂows. Figure 2.1 shows an example of both ﬂow regimes.
When people speak of turbulence, they typically refer to the chaotic behaviour of the wind,
the sudden gusts of higher speed winds that can be quite localized. However, in order to
better understand it, it is necessary to see what is causing this gustiness.
Instabilities in ﬂows when they reach high enough Reynolds numbers can cause a swirling
motion, which typically originate from some irregularity in the terrain or objects that the
ﬂow passes. These swirls can then separate from their origin and travel into the bulk ﬂow
ﬁeld where they, over time, will transform or decompose into smaller swirls. The swirls in
a turbulent ﬂow are known as eddies or coherent structures, and can be explained as areas

Most signal processing relevant here requires the signal to be ergodic, which means that
the signal is a stationary random process. The stationary part means that the statistical
properties must not change over time. This means, that before a signal from a simulation
can be interpreted, it is crucial to check if the statistical properties relevant have converged.
The random process part means that the signal can not be governed by a deterministic
property, but must be truly random. This is also known as a stochastic process. Luckily,
turbulent ﬂows are typically ergodic, at least within certain windows.
Some aspects of a signal, however, can not readily be extracted from the raw time-series,
but is much easier to see in the frequency domain.

Block Averaging
The unmodiﬁed spectrum can be messy to look at, since the variation from frequency
to frequency can be very high. This is where block averaging becomes relevant. Block
averaging takes a large sample and divides into many smaller samples which are then
transformed and averaged over, giving a much smoother spectrum. This eﬀectively lowers
the total sampling time, which reduces the resolution of the frequency axis and increases
the lowest resolvable frequency, but does so to make the spectrum more readable.
The immediate danger here, lies in the risk of dividing the original sample up into too
small samples, leaving too few independent samples in the smaller samples. The result is
that the data might not be ergodic anymore, i.e. the signals statistical properties might
change over the course of the sample or be all together diﬀerent from the original sample.
Ideally, each block should consist of at least one thousand independent samples when
block averaging, to make sure that each block is a proper representation of the entire
signal [35]. One of the eﬀects of too small block size can be seen in Figure 2.4. Notice
how the highest frequencies begin to deviate from the raw power spectrum. This eﬀect is
essentially windowing.

2.3
The response of a structure to a turbulent wind
In civil engineering the inﬂuence of wind on structures have had a growing importance
in the past one hundred years. For many years, the primary method of including wind
eﬀects on structures was to use only the mean values of a wind, ignoring the inﬂuence
of turbulence and the much higher wind speeds that gusts could contain. It wasn’t until
1961 when Alan G. Davenport coined two terms, the aerodynamic admittance and joint
acceptance functions in [4], that the turbulence in any serious capacity was included.
2.3.1
Davenports model for slender, line-like structures
In his work of 1962, [4], Davenport establishes the hitherto standard for determining wind
loads, “In solving the overall wind loading problem (eﬀects of the mean wind as well as
gusts) it was previously found practicable to relate the gust loading to the concurrent
mean wind load averaged over an hour or so”[4]. Following this, he proceeds to deﬁne the
two functions that has deﬁned how wind loading has been approached ever since. First,
and mostly irrelevant for the present project, he deﬁnes the joint acceptance which “mea-
sures the correlation between the spatial distribution of pressure across the span and the
mode”[4]. The join acceptance function determines how the loads from the wind are ac-
cepted into diﬀerent modeshapes of the one-degree-of-freedom buildings he investigated,
namely bridges and cables. More interestingly for this project, he then goes on to in-
troduce the aerodynamic admittances. These are functions that translate how “forces are
generated not by the velocity ﬂuctuations at a point in the ﬂow but over some ﬁnite region
surrounding the cross section of the structure”[4]. He proposed three equations containing
ﬁve admittances.

2.4
Simulating Fluid Mechanics
The art of simulating a ﬂuid ﬂow through numerical modelling is called Computational
Fluid Dynamics. This entails the iterative solution of the governing partial-diﬀerential
equations in a given domain. Many commercial or open-source software packages exist to
make this process easier. For this project Siemens STAR-CCM+ has been chosen.
2.4.1
Discretization scheme
STAR-CCM+s approach is through a ﬁnite-volume discretization of the ﬂow domain, in which
the ﬂow domain is divided up in the a ﬁne grid where each cell contains information about
its own properties and for each interaction neighboring cells inﬂuence on each other is
calculated. The discretization is known as the meshing of the volume and the discretized
volume is called the mesh. Finite-volume discretization does not need to be regular, but
can be highly irregular and still perform well.
The way each cell interact with other cells is determined by the set of governing equations.
“The fundamental laws that govern the mechanics of solids are the same laws that describe
the mechanics of ﬂuids, namely, the conservation of mass, linear momentum, angular
momentum, and energy.” [32]. However, for this case, solving for the energy has been
omitted and as such conservation of energy is irrelevant and not solved for.

2.5
Turbulence scheme
When simulating a turbulent ﬂow, diﬀerent approaches can be taken to resolve the turbu-
lence, depending on which degree of precision is needed and how realistic the turbulence
needs to be, if mean values are suﬃcient or higher statistical moments are of more impor-
tance. The very precise and realistic solution would be to simulate every scale of turbu-
lence, from the biggest eddies down to the smallest scales where the turbulence turns to
heat through viscosity. This approach is called Direct Numerical Solution (DNS), which is
extremely costly in computer power and time since to resolve the smallest eddies, a mesh
that has even smaller cells is necessary. Very few engineering applications exist where this
degree of precision is necessary for turbulent ﬂows, however, fundamental scientiﬁc stud-
ies of turbulence often require this level of precision and even some engineering problems
24
==PAGE:25==
revolving around very low Reynolds number ﬂows use DNS. An example of fundamental
studies can be seen in [26]. In the opposite end of the scale is Reynolds-Averaged Navier-
Stokes (RANS) solutions. This approach uses the Reynolds decomposition principle to
divide the signal of the turbulent ﬂow into a time-averaged mean ﬂow and a ﬂuctuating
component. The turbulence in this case, however, will be modelled, rather than simulated.
This can produce very fast and quite accurate results in certain applications. However,
because the turbulence is modelled rather than simulated, this approach might not cap-
ture eﬀects of and on the turbulence correctly. In between these two extremes exist Large
Eddy Simulation (LES) and Detached Eddy Simulation (DES). Large Eddy Simulation
simulates eddies that are large enough to be resolved by the mesh, while smaller eddies are
modelled. This gives a very accurate simulation of the turbulence, assuming suﬃciently
detailed mesh. LES can still be quite costly in computational power and time, so Detached
Eddy Simulation uses the LES approach in the bulk of the ﬂow, but applies a RANS ap-
proach when the mesh is too coarse or regions near solid surfaces. For this project, Large
Eddy Simulation is the approach of choice, as the actual motion of the eddies are of great
importance here.
2.5.1
Large Eddy Simulation
In Large Eddy Simulation, a spatial ﬁltering, analogous to the temporal Reynolds decom-
position of the RANS approach, is applied. This means that every value is ﬁltered into a
ﬁltered, or large-scale, value and a sub-grid, or small-scale, value.

2.6
Turbulence Generation in LES
Generating a turbulent inﬂow in LES can take two approaches. Either, attempt to create
a turbulent inﬂow directly at the inlet boundary, as is most common, or provoke the
inception of turbulent ﬂow in a manner similar to the way this is done in wind tunnels.
The latter approach lets the ﬂow at the inlet boundary remain unperturbated, but then
proceeds to perturb the ﬂow very close to the inlet using modelled spires and/or lists.
This is the way turbulence is generated in wind tunnels, but Foti et al found in 2017
[8], that while some turbulent characteristics are successfully modelled, there are major
drawbacks, as they put it “there are some major discrepancies in the velocity variances
and shear stress because higher turbulence intensities exist far from the wall in the spire
generated boundary layer” [8]. The alternative approach was set forth by Nicolas Jarrin in
2006 [14] and 2008 (Ph.D.-thesis) [12], where coherent turbulent structures were generated
outside the ﬂow domain and convected in through the boundary surface of the inlet. This
method is known as the Synthetic Eddy Method. This has been built into STAR-CCM+
as the de facto standard of how to create turbulence in an LES-simulation.
2.6.1

Reynolds stresses that form the Reynold Stress Tensor SEM attempts to create realistic
turbulent inﬂow that outperforms the alternatives, such as random generation, spectral
generation or precursor simulations.

the inlet boundary, which has the size of the inlet plus one integral length scale on each

boundary generating a turbulet inlet.

3.2.1
Physics models
The physical models chosen for the simulation can severely impact the output from the
simulation, so naturally the choices that have been made must be clariﬁed. First of all, it
was chosen that the simulation can be run as incompressible ﬂow. This simpliﬁes many
things, as the density will be constant throughout the ﬂow domain. The reason behind
this choice is the slow velocities that will exist in the ﬂow domain of this project. The
wind tunnel experiments sought to achieve speeds upwards of 10 m
s . This is only a Mach
number of 0.03, well clear of any compressibility eﬀects. A consequence of this choice is
that the ﬂow will react instantly throughout the domain to any disturbances as though
the entire domain was one solid and rigid body.
The next model choice that must be explained is the segregated ﬂow solver.
The
segregated ﬂow solver solves the momentum equations one dimension at a time, where
the alternative coupled ﬂow solver solves all of them coupled [32]. This approach has
greater stability for lower Mach numbers, specially when compressibility eﬀects canbe
30
==PAGE:31==
neglected, as in this case.
The coupled solver requires a considerably lower Courant-
number (explained in Section 3.2.4), which would either slow down the calculations a lot
or risk introducing instability in the solution.
3.2.2
Turbulence modelling
When selecting which turbulence model to use, the purpose of the simulation plays a big
role, as mentioned in an earlier section. Since this speciﬁc project is very dependent on
the eﬀects of turbulence, modelling it using a RANS-approach would probably be a bad
idea.
This leaves LES, DES and DNS. While DNS would be an optimal approach to
this problem, result-wise, it is simply unfeasible considering the large ﬂow domain that is
being simulated. Resolving just a small fraction of the domain would require much more
computational power and/or time than what is available for this project. This puts the
choice between LES and DES. DES is very similar to LES, with the exception that as grid
sizes change or the ﬂow gets close to a surface, DES chooses to model the turbulence in
a RANS-fasion rather than simulating it like LES does. The choice is thus Large Eddy
Simulation.
3.2.3

simpliﬁcation allowing the user to enter a turbulence intensity in place of the six Reynolds

3.2.4
Mesh - Geometric convergence
The mesh sets the limit for how small eddies can be properly resolved by the simulation.
While a super ﬁne mesh is desirable, it comes at a great computational cost. The question
then arises, when is the mesh ﬁne enough for its current application?
The mesh that was used in this project is a regular trimmer mesh consisting of small
rectangular boxes with a constant size for the bulk of the domain, incrementally changing
size to match with other mesh types, such as the prism layer mesh surrounding any surface
to capture the boundary layer near walls. In Figure 3.6 an example of a trimmer mesh
and prism layer mesh can be seen. This makes up the bulk of the ﬂow domain.
For this project, three mesh sizes were considered for a regular trimmer mesh. 10mm,
15mm and 20mm base sizes were used. Initial tests were done on an empty tunnel to
see if reﬁning the mesh further would give better results, in the form of better spectral
distributions. The result of these can be seen in Figure 3.7, where it would seem that with
a ﬁner resolution, the break away from Kolmogorovs −5

frequency.

The Courant number expresses how many cells a particle passes through over the course
of one time step. Ideally, and mandatory for explicit solvers, the Courant number is below
1, as if the value is higher, there might be information not resolved. However, the Courant
number is coupled with the time step, meaning that reﬁning the mesh size to get smaller
eddies properly resolved in turn requires the time step to be decreased. Both of these
eﬀects are costly in computational time.
Testing the three mesh sizes showed that reducing base size does indeed resolve ﬁner tur-
bulence, but the computation time required to go beyond the 20mm base size outweighed
the extra precision of a better resolution.
Later, however, it was found that resolving the wake ﬁner than the bulk of the ﬂow could
explain a strange result, so a reﬁnement was added. In this volumetric reﬁnement, the
base size reduces to 5mm in an attempt to capture some high-frequency small-scale wake
curl-up eﬀects, similar to vortex shedding. The reﬁned wake can be seen in Figure 3.8
along with a closer look at the adaptation of the trimmer mesh to the ﬁne prism layer
surrounding the disk. The reﬁnement allows the resolution of ﬁner structures in a localized
volume, which is far cheaper than resolving the entire ﬂow domain.

3.2.5
Timestepping - Temporal convergence
Initially, the idea was, to run the simulation at a similar frequency as the pressure data was
recorded in the wind tunnel experiments, i.e. at 500Hz. However, the Courant number
for this time step, would be just around 1, leaving no room for speed up due to blockage
or turbulence. A lower time step would be preferable.
Before investigating the eﬀect of lower timesteps or diﬀerent time-marching schemes, a
brief look at the time model chosen for this project, the implicit unsteady time solver.
Since the segregated ﬂow model was selected previously, the only unsteady model that
is available is the implicit unsteady, as the explicit requires a coupled solution of energy.
Furthermore, since turbulence is inherently unsteady this leaves very few options for time
modelling in this LES-simulation, in fact only the implicit unsteady model. First order
implicit time integration approximates the values of the current time step based on the
previous timestep, while second order implicit time integration approximates the values for
the current timestep based on the two previous timesteps [32]. In both cases a backwards
diﬀerence scheme is applied to solve the partial diﬀerential equations.
With the only two parameters to be changed in this time model being the timestep and the
order of time integration, it was natural to investigate the eﬀect of lowering the timestep,
increasing the order or even both.
Figure 3.10 shows how both increasing the order of the time integration and lowering the
timestep increase the quality of the spectrum that is found for the free wind. Most im-
portantly, combining both yields even better results. It was concluded that the decreased
timestep increased the quality of the result without impacting the computational time too
much, while the increase in order had near zero impact on the computational time but a
huge impact on the quality of the spectrum. Combining both seems like the right choice
going forward as it contains improvments from both reﬁnements. Further decrease to the

Low frequency deviation
The fact that both the numerical data, the experimental data and Vickery’s own data shoot

Bearman states in his article of 1971 that “When the size of the energy
containing eddies become much larger than the size of the body, their main eﬀect will no
longer be to mix with the wake ﬂow but to cause an eﬀect similar to that of a slowly varying
mean velocity” [1, p. 184]. This combined with Davenports reasoning of his article of 1995
“The quotient of the mean speed and the frequency is eﬀectively a gust “wavelength””
[6, p. 237], a statement that assumes Taylor’s Frozen Field Hypothesis, the expectation
would be that for very low frequencies, the admittance should tend toward 1.0.

5.4
Bearman’s criticism
In 1971 Peter W. Bearman [1] set out to do a more thorough and structured examination
of the concept of an aerodynamic admittance in relation to bluﬀbodies compared to
what Vickery and Davenport had presented the previous decade.
He found that “As
nD/U tends to zero, aerodynamic admittance rises to a value less than unity. The simple
unsteady theory assumed an inﬁnite correlation length for the ﬂuctuating velocity whereas
in turbulent ﬂow, at very long wavelengths, the correlation length will be of the same order
as the integral scale. Therefore it can be argued that measured values of χ2(n) will be
less than unity”[1]. This would seem to explain why Vickery’s empirical model predicts an
admittance of 1.0 for low frequencies, but neither Vickery’s own experiments, Davenports
experiments, Bearmans experiments nor the results in this project, both experimental and
numerical, achieve a perfect 1.0-admittance at low frequencies. Bearman even continues to
specify an even more elemental issue with Vickery’s model, “‘The theory of Vickery, when
applied to solid plates, assumes that the turbulence approaching each element of plate area
behaves as if the body were in a stream where Lx/D = ∞”[1]. This is basically an extension
of the statement in the ﬁrst quote about the correlation length being assumed to be much
greater than it in reality is, at low frequencies. Even considering these comments, Bearman
also notes that “The measurements of χ2(n) made by Vickery ... are in agreement with
the author’s”[1], while results presented by Davenport in an earlier experiment show less

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this project, spatially developing channel ﬂow has been simulated using a Large-Eddy
Simulation in a representation of a wind tunnel based on the tunnel at Svend Ole Hansen
ApS. Measurements were made in the ﬂow ﬁeld in this channel to evaluate the turbulence
generated using the Synthetic Eddy Method along with measurements of the drag force
on a circular disk mounted normal to the ﬂow. Based on these measurements a spectral
analysis was conducted with the goal of determining the aerodynamic admittance of the
disk. The aerodynamic admittance that was found was compared to results from wind
tunnel experiments conducted by the author in the fall of 2017, as well as an empirical
model for the admittance presented by Vickery in 1965 and reﬁned by Davenport in 1995.
In the primary portion of the spectrum, a very good agreement was found, but deviations
from the empirical model was seen for non-dimensional frequencies below 0.124 for sim-
ulated as well as experimental data. This deviation could in part possibly be explained
by regular vortex shedding, as shown based on ﬁndings by Miau et al in 1997 and by the
empirical model assuming an inﬁnite lateral correlation in the oncoming turbulent wind,
which causes the model to conservatively over-estimate the aerodynamic admittance, as
indicated by Vickery and Bearman.
In the opposite end of the spectrum another deviation from the empirical model of Vickery
and Davenport was seen, again, both in the experimental and simulated data. It was found
that part of the cause for this could be a high-frequency instability caused by Kelvin-
Helmholtz instabilities along the periphery of the disk causing small worm-like coherent
structures to be shed at a high frequency. This is consistent with the ﬁndings of Tian et
al in 2016 and the coherent structures are visualized using a λ2 criterion visualization.
Finally, the numerical approach to determining the aerodynamic admittance can be called
successful, even though it might be impractical considering the time and computational
power needed to resolve the turbulence to a satisfactory degree. Several improvements
could be made to the simulations conducted in this project, including, but not limited
to, a ﬁner time-resolution, a ﬁner spatial resolution and longer simulation time. Further
investigations into the cause for deviations in both the high and low end of the spectrum
60
==PAGE:61==
could shed some light on the physics surrounding these cases.
