Abstract 
Depletion of natural resources as well as climate change require adequate measures for reducing 
energy consumption of buildings and industry. Between 30 and 40% of total Danish energy 
consumption is used for heating, ventilation and lighting in building (1). All building types have to 
reduce their energy needs in order existing environmental targets to be achieved. European 
Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD) requires all European members, including Denmark, to 
implements series of measures including declarations to be performed at the building level. 
Objective of this thesis is to investigate and propose strategies for energy and indoor environment 
optimization of Lyngby Store center. For the purpose, computational model was created including 
17% of the commercial part of the building. Limited information was available on the building, its 
systems and energy consumption. When it comes to calculations of energy demands of such big 
buildings, input data is of great importance for the correctness of the results. Calibration of the 
created model was done based on records of monthly energy consumption for heating and HVAC 
only. For doing so, internal and external load patterns were carefully determined and results of 
the computational model showed energy consumption of less than 10% difference compared to 
the consumption of investigated part of the real building. Temperature wise, model showed close 
values to these of the building, where for a short period of time, on-site measurements were done. 
Three optimization lines were followed when proposals were made. They include updates of the 
ventilation of the building, its systems and heat load from lighting. By dividing updates in such a 
way, it was possible to be followed how each change influences the energy and indoor 
environment performance of the building. Best performing models from optimization each line 
were then combined with each other in final optimized model. At the request of the building 
management company DEAS, additional optimization proposals were investigated and presented.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Background 
Population on the planet is rapidly growing and with this, the need for natural resources. Energy, 
provided by any means is vital need of the humanity. At the same time, because the intensive 
exploitation of the natural resources in the last two centuries, our present fossil fuel energy is still 
abundant but for short term only. This fact makes energy efficiency measures more actual than 
ever before. Last, but not least, pollution on nature has to be first lowered, in order climate change 
to be reduced. 
Concern over energy security, the social and economic impacts of energy prices, and growing 
awareness of climate change have led many countries to put greater emphasis on developing 
policies and measures that promote energy efficiency (2). Energy efficiency is a vital element in 
the green transition of the energy sector. Without extensive energy efficiency improvements, it 
would have been disproportionally expensive to meet energy demands with new and initially more 
expensive energy sources like renewable energy (3). 
Between 30 and 40% of total Danish energy consumption is used for heating, ventilation and 
lighting in building (1) and Lyngby Store Center is among them. Last renovations of the store center 
was done in 2003 and at the request of the owner, Danica pension, measures improving energy 
efficiency and indoor environment are to be taken. 
Building simulation tools have been used to evaluate energy performance and indoor environment 
of the building, when different optimization strategies are applied and investigated. Accurate 
weather data and right assumptions are important in order results to be considered as real. Actual 
weather data for 2015 have been used for calibration of the computational model, also mentioned 
in the thesis as As-built model. After calibrated, Danish Design Reference Year (DRY13) has been 
used for the investigation process in order extremes in weather to be avoided. 
1.2. Objectives 
The objective of this thesis is computational model of part from Lyngby Store Center to be created 
and later used for investigation of measures improving the energy efficiency and indoor 
environment of the building.

3.1. The building 
3.1.1. Envelope 
As a physical separator between the interior and the exterior, building envelope has a key role in 
keeping the desired indoor environment and making a building to be energy efficient. Lyngby Store 
Center was built in 1973 and unfortunately detailed information regarding all its envelop 
components was not available for the period when this thesis was being written. For the envelope 
components with missing information about layers, their thickness and thermal properties, 
assumptions were made based in the building regulations (BR72) which were acting in the period 
when the store center was built. Thicknesses of ceiling, roof slabs and walls were determined by 
section drawings, while their thermal properties according to BR72 
It was possible by visits on site, layers of the roof to be determined, but for walls and floors 
parameters have been determined by section drawings and BR72. Drawings can be found in 
Appendix A.  
 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
20 
 
Windows and skylights 
Lyngby Store Center big areas from its facades and roof covered by glazing. The investigated part 
from the building has skylights of near 1500m2 and window facades of near 840m2. According 
BR72, windows with opening area less than 60% of the total outer wall surface area should have 
had U-value of maximum 3.15W/m2K. Recommendations suggest two pane windows with 12mm 
of distance between each pane. 
Walls 
External walls, floor slabs and roof slab are made of concrete with thickness of 250mm. Because 
of lack of detailed information about the store center’s envelope, thermal properties will be 
assumed as the required from Danish Building Regulations from 1972 (BR72). According to (10), 
the U-value of external wall should not exceed 1.0 W/m2K for walls with weight over 100 kg/m2.   
Floor 
BR72 requires for partitions against unheated room, i.e. ground floor slab where parking is bellow, 
U-values to be under 2.0W/m2K. 
Roof 
Building regulations from 1972 require ceiling and roof surfaces that limit unheated spaces to have 
U-value of not more than 0.4W/m2K. Via visit on-site construction of roof was determined. It 
consists of 250mm of concrete covered with 5cm of light weight insulation. For water tightness, 
additional timber construction is built, seen on Figure 3-3, which is covered by waterproof asphalt 
layer. On the picture can be seen that insulation do not cover the concrete, but we were told that 
it is not in this way over the whole roof, but only on places where some maintenance have been 
done.

3.1.2. Interior 
Interior of the investigated part from Lyngby Store Center characterize with grouped small shops, 
several bigger stores, as Føtex and FONA and corridors and atrium. Shops are divided from 
corridors via glass walls and steel curtains mainly. Many of these walls have big opening are and 
during open time and some are completely open allowing corridors and shops to be almost merged 
in one thermal zone. On the other hand, even closed, many shops are using perforated steel 
curtains as separators from the corridors. All this allows circulation of air between the ventilated 
shops, corridors and atrium. 
Regarding thermal mass, interior of the store center can be considered as such with high thermal 
mass. Thermal mass can be described as a material’s resistance to change in temperature. 
Materials like concrete have high thermal mass when exposed and have big potential to store 
absorbed energy during and release it afterwards. In Lyngby Store Center, there is exposed 
concrete in most shops and in atriums and corridors as can be seen on Figure 3-4. Concrete in 
shops absorb released by the ventilation systems energy and release it afterwards, when systems 
do not function. By this way temperatures in the morning are closer to the desired ones, than it 
would be if high thermal mass was not available.

3.2. Thermal loads 
Thermal loads are the amount of energy that has to be added or removed from a building in order 
good indoor environment to be provided. There is such a close relation between load and demand 
that every heating or cooling load would require respectively corresponding cooling or heating 
demand. This relation is explicable when certain indoor environment in required. Even in relatively 
cold climates, as the Scandinavian ones, high heat load in combination with well insulated 
envelope could result in cooling demand most of the time from the year. Reducing heat loads as 
much as possible and meeting them as efficient as possible is a mandatory requirement for high 
buildings performance. Precise dimensioning of the ventilation system in every building, when 
such is used to maintain the indoor environment, requires to be adequate taken into account both 
external and internal thermal loads.  
External loads come mainly from heat from the Sun transferred inside a building through the 
building’s envelope, but also from earth and outside environment. The internal ones come from 
every possible heat source that will permanently or temporarily be located inside the building, 
such as lighting, equipment and people.

3.2.1. External thermal loads 
3.2.1.1. 
Direct solar gains 
Facades in big retail buildings usually consist of a lot of glazing, which allows the sunlight to enter 
the building and to create significant thermal load. Depending on the season, this thermal load 
could be desirable or not. During winter cold days for example, when building’s systems are 
running in heating mode, any heat from the Sun would be welcome and would lead to cost savings 
from heating. But that is not the case during summer and many days during spring and autumn, 
when a building is in cooling mode. Then any additional heat from outside would only increase the 
cooling demand and measures that reduce the direct sunlight from penetrating inside the building 
should be taken. 
In the case of Lyngby Storcenter, direct sunlight would significantly influence the climate in some 
zones inside the building. In corridors for example skylights cover significant part of their area.

3.2.1.2. 
Infiltration 
Infiltration can be defined as air entering or leaving the building through gaps in the building 
envelope. It convectively heats or cools interior spaces bypassing the insulation. Infiltration can be 
an enormous problem, for example US Department of Energy estimates that 40% of residential 
heat loss is due to infiltration (11). When mechanically ventilated, as Lyngby Store center, buildings 
are under positive pressure meaning that infiltration is reduced when system is operating. 
However, infiltration influences indoor temperatures outside working hours, which could increase 
energy consumption. 
 
3.2.2. Internal thermal loads 
For buildings like Lyngby Store center, as main internal heat loads, or also called internal heat gains, 
can be considered loads coming from lights, equipment and people. Lighting and most equipment 
heat loads are sensible heat, while the heat generated by human’s body metabolic rate is a 
combination of sensible and latent heat. It is assumed that heat load from lighting and equipment 
is equal to their energy power, while heat from humans depends on people’s physical activity. In 
shopping malls, energy consumption from equipment and lighting, respectively heat loads, are 
much higher compared to residential buildings and the reason for that could be explained by the 
==PAGE:26==

purpose of such buildings, their sizes, the expected number of visitors and the good indoor 
environment that has to be provided inside.

3.2.2.2. 
Thermal load from lighting 
Lighting loads are the energy that is used to power electric lights and for a building are often 
referred to lighting power density (LPD) measured in W/m2. It is considered that all the electric 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
28 
 
power of each lighting source is transformed into heat, and by convection this heat is released in 
the volume where the lighting source is installed. Shopping malls like Lyngby Store Center tent to 
have high LPD, because of the highly illuminated exposition areas in the shops. Therefore lighting 
loads need special attention in this and other similar buildings, because they add significant 
amount of heat.  
Lighting consumes nowadays near 20% of the total electricity consumption of a typical retail facility 
and 13% of the total electricity consumption of a typical supermarket facility. At the same time it 
increases the heat load of a building, hence its energy consumption for cooling. These two facts 
make lighting significant energy consumer, which must be taken seriously into account during 
energy simulations of buildings. But It also has great energy savings potential and lighting retrofits 
can save 30 to 50% of the light energy and 10 to 20% of the energy for cooling (12). 
 
From observation in several shops in Lyngby Store Center, it was notices that lighting mainly 
consist of conventional fluorescent lamps, halogen lamps and some LED’s. In common spaces like 
corridors and atrium, lights have been changed to LED characterized by much better energy 
efficiency, hence much lower heat emissivity. However, lights replacement inside each shop is 
strictly individual according to its needs determined by the design of the interior. Therefore, it is 
very hard precise estimation of the lighting power density to be done. In Table 3.4, the type of

4. Lyngby Store Center computational modelling  
This chapter would describe the assumptions that had been done when computational model of 
the investigated part of Lyngby Store center was created. Limited information was available on the 
building, its systems and energy consumption. Missing information was assumed based on 
literature studies over similar practices. 
4.1. IDA ICE software 
IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (ICE) is a program for study of the indoor climate of individual zones 
within a building, as well as energy consumption for the entire building. Normally, the system to 
be simulated consists of a building with one or more zones, a primary system and one or more air 
handling systems. Surrounding buildings might shade the building. The air inside the building 
contains both humidity and carbon dioxide. Weather data is supplied by weather data files, or is 
artificially created by a model for a given 24-hour period. Consideration of wind and temperature 
driven airflow can be taken by a bulk air flow model. Predefined building components and other 
parameter objects can be loaded from a database (17). 
4.2. IDA ICE model & assumptions 
Assumptions have been done when no precise information for modelled building was available. 
All technical parameters of Lyngby Store center envelope components and building systems 
couldn’t be found, so assumptions have been made. At first, as-build model was created with 
properties as close to the real building as possible. Calibration, method comparing energy 
consumption and temperatures between the model and the real building, confirmed that model 
gives reliable results and is adjusted for investigating different energy optimization strategies.

about the building, either by requirements in Building Regulations from 1972 described in

4.3. Calibration of IDA ICE model 
Calibration of the created in IDA ICE computational model has significant importance for the 
authenticity of the results from the proposed optimizations strategies. Calibration is carried out in 
two main stages. First stage is re-creation of the existing in Lyngby Store Centre’s envelope and 
systems in IDA ICE. Second stage is comparing the performance of the already created model with 
the performance of the real building in terms of energy consumption and indoor temperatures.  
Data for the energy performance of the real building is taken from www.MinEnergi.dk, which is a 
website allowing the energy consumption of the store center to be checked back in time. Bellow 
of the report, energy consumption from MinEnergi.dk is considered to be real consumption of the 
investigated area from Lyngby Store Center. The existing BMS doesn’t have the possibility to 
record data, but only to provide such instantly, which is recorded by the management stuff in 
MinEnergi.dk. The website provides a wide range of records for electricity, heating and cooling 
(21). For some of the energy meters, data is available for a longer periods back in time than for 
other meters. Records for HVAC for example are present from the beginning of February 2015, 
while records from heating are present from longer before.  
For the purpose of this thesis, data from two energy meters, measuring energy consumption from 
the investigated area only, are used. One meter measures electricity for HVAC, while the other one 
energy provided by fuel for hot water used both for domestic hot water (DHW) and for the heating 
coils of the ventilation systems. Real energy consumption of the investigated part from Lyngby 
Store Center and results from the IDA ICE model, for HVAC and heating with DHW, is presented on 
daily basis from 21th to 29th of October 2015. On monthly basis, HVAC is presented in the period 
between February – November 2015, while energy for heating and DHW for the period between 
January – November 2015. The reason February to be the first month regarding energy for HVAC, 
is that there is no recorded data on energy consumption before that in www.MinEnergi.dk. 
The energy consumption values from the computational model are the total energy that is 
purchased to the investigated part of the building with no primary energy factors included. When 
talking about “Fuel heating”, it is measured in terms of its heating (calorific) value in kWh.  
In addition to the comparison of the energy consumption between the computational model and 
the real building, temperature measurements are also carried out for ensuring better calibration. 
That is necessary, since the similarity in the energy performance between the real building and the 
IDA ICE model had to be accompanied with similar indoor climate in order the calibration to be as 
correct as possible. 
In total 17 temperature measuring devices are used for measuring the inside temperature 
conditions. 15 of them are located inside the commercial part of the investigated area and two in 
==PAGE:50==

the parking area bellow the commercial one. The measuring period for the calibration is between 
October 21st and October 29th 2015.

5. Optimization strategies  
The aim of the optimization strategies is to reduce the energy consumption for conditioning the 
building as well as for improving the indoor environment. The process of implementation of these 
strategies and their evaluation consists of the following steps. 
- 
Recreation of each optimization proposal in IDA ICE simulation software; 
- 
Simulation of the newly built model, adjusted according to each proposal; 
- 
Evaluation of the optimization proposal by comparing the results from the newly created 
model with the results from the model having the parameters similar to the real building 
and used in section 4.3 Calibration of IDA ICE model. The latter one is further called As-built 
model.  
 
Figure 5-1 
Optimization strategy scheme 
 
On Figure 5-1, the optimization strategy can be seen schematically. The optimization lines are the 
major lines that are followed in order optimization goal to be achieved. Main changes were made 
on the ventilation of the building, the type of lighting and the envelope. The specific changes in 
each line are shown as “Updates”, which are proposals for improvement of the current building’s 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
57 
 
condition. After evaluated, the updates from each line end up with updated model for this line 
including all updates or some of them. Finally, the updated models from each line are combined 
in a single model. 
This application scheme of the optimization strategy was chosen, because it gives possibility to be 
seen how big the influence is of each of the proposed updates compared to the performance of 
the As-built model. 
5.1. Ventilation updates 
Ventilation update strategies include measures improving the efficiency of the conditioning 
process of the ventilated zones as well as improvement of the indoor environment. Such measures 
are: 
As-built mechanical ventilation systems with natural night ventilation – as-build ventilation 
systems are combined with natural night ventilation provided by system of temperature controlled 
openable windows in corridors and atrium. This allows to be checked how much the energy 
performance and the indoor environment are improved with only applying automated window 
opening system. 
VAV systems - replacing of the As-built CAV systems with VAV systems. New mechanical 
ventilation systems with improved efficiency, described in 5.1.1, are used in simulations in order 
to be compared how the energy consumption and indoor environment change, compared to the 
ventilation systems from the As-built model. This proposal does not include usage of natural 
ventilation during and outside the working hours of the shopping center.  
VAV systems with natural night ventilation – natural night ventilation, provided by system of 
temperature controlled openable windows in corridors and atrium, is added to the already 
replaced mechanical ventilation systems with new, more efficient VAV systems. Night cooling of 
the investigated part of Lyngby Store Center is provided by natural ventilation only and no 
mechanical one runs at night. This measure allows to check how the energy performance and the 
indoor environment change with adding natural night ventilation to the already replaced 
mechanical ventilation systems. 
VAV systems with mechanical night ventilation mode – introducing mechanical night ventilation 
mode in the new VAV systems and removing the possibility for natural night ventilation. The 
conditioning process of the investigated part of Lyngby Store Center is provided only by the 
mechanical ventilation systems, day and night. This measure allows to compare the energy 
performance and the indoor environment when mechanical night ventilation is used, compared to 
natural night ventilation. 
VAV systems with hybrid night ventilation – the applied VAV systems have mechanical night 
ventilation mode, which is combined with possibility for natural night ventilation, so night cooling 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
58 
 
of the investigated part of the building is ensured by hybrid ventilation process. This measure 
allows to see how the energy performance and the indoor environment change when natural and 
mechanical night ventilation are combined together and compared to the performance from the 
previous proposals.  
Commercial part of Lyngby Store Center has shops for clothes, shoes, accessories, cafeterias,

5.1.2. Natural ventilation 
Night ventilation is a well-known method for heat extraction from buildings when unoccupied. 
Lyngby store center has relatively good geometry for implementation of such a ventilation 
strategy. A lot of skylights spread among the building in combination with big atrium with near 
18m of height, create very good opportunity stack effect to be used for removing the heat 
collected inside the building.  
==PAGE:62==

Natural ventilation is provided by automated openable skylights from the atrium and the corridors 
of the investigated area from Lyngby Store Center. The located in the center of the shopping center 
atrium is surrounded by corridors providing access to all the shops inside the commercial area of 
the building. In addition with its height of near 18m, atrium creates great possibilities for 
buoyancy-driven stack ventilation. This type of ventilation relies on density differences to draw 
cool, outdoor air in at low ventilation openings and exhaust warm, indoor air at higher ventilation 
openings (23), as shown on Figure 5-3.

strategy of the optimization proposal. 
Usage of natural ventilation is be examined during nighttime only. As an energy efficient 
ventilation method, natural ventilation is not easy to control as the mechanical one. It is very 
important the right control strategy to be applied in order energy savings to be achieved, without 
causing discomfort of the occupants.

season, different schedules for start and stop of the ventilation are used. Since the natural 
ventilation process is provided via openings in corridors and atrium, where no mechanical

5.1.4. As-built model ventilation with natural night ventilation 
First optimization proposal includes adding natural ventilation together with the existing 
mechanical ventilation systems. The purpose of such proposal is to be evaluated the energy saving 
potential of natural ventilation as well as its potential for improvement the indoor environment, 
when combined with the existing ventilation systems. Apart from the existing openable windows 
in corridors, natural ventilation is applied as automated skylight window opening control system 
in corridors and atriums as well. The system includes skylights located in the corridors and the 
atrium, which are set to be openable. Each of them opens if the operative temperature, in the 
zone where located exceeds a given value. IDA ICE software uses proportional controllers to open 
windows from 0% when the temperature value is bellow “LOLIMIT” to 100% when temperature 
reaches “HILIMIT”. “LOLIMIT” values is set to be 21oC, which means that above this temperature, 
windows would start open and when the values reaches “HILIMIT”, set to be 25oC, they would be 
100% open according to the area allowed to be openable.

5.3.4. Insulation of ground floor 
The last envelope update proposal refers to adding insulation layer on the bottom surface of the 
ground floor slab (on the parking’s ceiling). Parking level is not heated, so during winter 
temperatures are lower that these inside the heated commercial part, hence there are significant 
heat losses through floor. On the other hand, during summer lower temperatures in parking level 
would be beneficial to the cooling process. U-value of the ground floor slab from the As-built model 
is 2.09W/m2K, which after adding additional 10 cm layer of insulation became 0.3W/m2K. Thermal 
transmittance of ground floor slab was calculated automatically by IDA ICE, after the thickness and 
the materials of the slab was set. From section drawing it was sawn that thickness of the slab is 
25cm, and from visits on site could be seen that there isn’t any insulation attached on the bottom 
side of the slab. Table 2.3 describes requirements to insulation in case of renovation, according to 
which U-value of maximum 0.40W/m2K should be used. In order this value to be achieved, 
insulation layer of 10cm, having heat conductivity of 0.036W/mK, was attached to the parking 
ceiling. 
5.4. Final optimized model 
Final optimized model includes all the optimized models from the ventilation, lighting and 
envelope optimization lines combined in one single model. The purpose of this combination is to 
show how all updates affect the energy consumption and the indoor environment of the building 
when combined together.  
5.4.1. Ventilation updated model 
For ventilation updated model is chosen model with VAV systems with hybrid night ventilation. 
The choice is based on the fact that this is the most efficient model among these providing the 
required temperature indoor environment in all the ventilated zones.

5.4.2. Lighting updated model 
In terms of lighting, option with lighting power density of 50% LED is chosen. The choice isn’t made 
based on results, but on the following two assumptions. First, lighting power density 50% LED is 
more realistic in terms of achieving, since shops cannot be made to switch to LED, because it is 
their wish to do it. Second, by using 50% of LED, indoor environment and energy consumption are 
according to worse scenario, than in case of 100% LED. If in future lighting is updated to 100% LED, 
this would first decrease the internal heat load and release cooling potential, which could be used 
for future increase in outside air temperatures. At the same time, new updated mechanical 
ventilation systems have the potential to provide the amount of heat during heating season, which 
is supplied from higher LPD. 
5.4.3. Envelope updated model 
Envelope updated model combines all the envelope update proposals in one model. The purpose 
of this model is to show how all the envelope update measures could affect the indoor 
environment when combined with better ventilation means and lower heat load from lights.

7. Conclusion 
This thesis was aimed to analyze strategies for energy and indoor air quality optimization of Lyngby 
Store center. Three main optimization proposals were investigated related to update of the 
mechanical ventilation systems of the building, reduction of lighting power densities of artificial 
lighting and improvement of the insulation properties of the buildings envelope. Evaluation of the 
strategies was done by answering the flowing four questions. 
1. What was the today’s main problem of Lyngby Store center in terms of energy efficiency 
and indoor climate? 
Nowadays Lyngby Store center experiences bad temperature indoor environment and 
temperatures of near 30oC have been measured in September. It is supposed that even higher 
values are possible if measurements are done in July or August and calibrated computational 
model showed that values of nearly 32oC could occur. Unfortunately values for the total energy 
consumption were not available, but from what was known, building spends nearly 90kWh energy 
for DHW, heating and ventilation only, without cooling lighting and other consumption to be 
included. Since last renovation was made in 2003, building needs improvements of the 
conditioning systems and its envelope in order indoor temperatures and energy efficiency to be 
improved. 
2. How could new ventilation strategies improve the energy efficiency and indoor environment 
of the building? 
New ventilation strategies including current mechanical ventilation systems couldn’t exert much 
influence of the energy efficiency and inside temperatures. Natural ventilation in addition to the 
old ventilation systems added negligible savings of near 0.2% and negligible improvement of 
temperatures, still not allowing class II to be achieved. Since natural ventilation was applied 
through corridors and atrium, effect over the ventured zones which are closed when natural 
ventilation is on, was minor.  
Replacement of the current inefficient mechanical ventilation systems with new more efficient 
ones was the major improvement which should be done. However, without combining new 
systems with night mechanical or natural ventilation, class II in terms of indoor temperatures 
couldn’t be achieved in all the ventilated zones. Mechanical night ventilation had big energy saving 
potential and could provide the desired indoor climate achieving in total savings of 13.6%. If 
combined with natural night ventilation, savings grew to 19.1% and best results in terms of indoor 
temperatures was achieved. The model which gave most energy savings was the one where new 
VAV systems were combined with natural ventilation through corridors and atrium. Achieved 
savings were near 30% of the total energy consumption and only single zone, having 7% of the 
total investigated area, couldn’t comply with the number of hours above 26oC required from 
category II. Additional minor improvements could change that and allow natural night ventilation 
==PAGE:95==
 
 
94 
 
to become the most appropriate night ventilation mean for achieving the desired efficiency and 
indoor environment. 
3. How would reducing of lighting power density affect the energy efficiency and indoor 
environment of the building? 
Reducing of lighting power density in Lyngby Store center would mainly affect the energy 
consumption for lighting, but not for conditioning of the building. Moreover, indoor climate would 
be improved negligibly compared to the existing one. If 50% of all lights were LEDs, in each W/m2 
less heat load from lights total savings for the building would be 0.31 W/m2. Reason for this weak 
influence from the reduced LPD was the old ventilation systems, which were running at their 
maximum capacity not depending on the LPDs. 
4. How could new envelope update strategies improve the energy efficiency and indoor 
environment of the building? 
New envelope update strategies could influence the energy efficiency, but not the indoor 
environment of the building to the desired level. In fact only measures reducing heat gains from 
sun light could bring some savings of maximum 4.4% of the total energy consumption. When 
insulation properties of the envelope were improved, that led to additional energy for heating. 
When better insulated, building became in cooling mode even during the winter, because of the 
high internal heat gains from artificial lights. That required ventilation systems to increase their air 
flow rates, hence to heat bigger volume of cold outside air before release it inside. When all 
envelope update measured were applied, total energy consumption grew with near 21% 
compared to the As-built model. Envelope update measured would have limited positive influence 
over the energy efficiency and indoor environment if applied alone. Some of them could actually 
require more energy and as a reason for that was found to be the inefficiency of the existing 
mechanical ventilation systems expressed mainly in low heat recovery efficiency. 
5. How could changes of the interior improve the energy efficiency and indoor environment of 
the building? 
During the evaluation of the ventilation update proposals was noticed that natural ventilation 
could play much bigger role in conditioning process of Lyngby Store center. As mentioned above, 
geometry of the building allows natural driving forces to provide sufficient ventilation flow rate 
not only for the corridors and atrium, but also for the zones conditioned by the mechanical 
ventilation systems. Even though that leakage area of shops when closed was 1% of the area of 
their doors, air that circulated at night managed to facilitate a lot the cooling process during the 
day. If during cooling season, measures increasing the air circulation between shops and corridors 
are taken, natural ventilation might be enough and no mechanical one might be used when night 
cooling. This could of course have negative effect during winter when heat from shops would leak 
in common spaces, but for that period curtains having less leakage are could be used.

Appendix C. Thermal load calculation methods 
C.1. 
Equipment thermal loads 
C.1.1. Food supermarket (storage) 
Equipment heat load was taken from (11), where storage was considered as “Food service” 
premise. It is known that in the storage part there is also kitchen for preparing fresh food as well 
as bakery, which is the reason the storage part to be evaluated as “Food service”. As seen in Figure 
C-1, there are equipment and plug in loads separately listed, but for the purpose of this thesis, 
these two loads were considered as one equipment heat load. Because the values for “Plug loads” 
are given as peak values, when assumed it was reduced by 30%, so the total heat load for the 
storage was “Equipment power density” for “Food Service” equal to 9 W/m2 plus 70% of “Plug 
Loads” for the same space type, equal to 9.1 W/m2. The sum of these two values used as EPD in 
Table 4.5 is 18.1 W/m2.  
 
Figure C-1 
Example internal loads for different space types (11). 
 
C.1.2. Food supermarket 
Average Swedish supermarket spends around 320 kWh/m2 electrical energy (20) and it is claimed 
that the value has been taken from The Swedish Energy Agency 2010. The value was used as a 
reference value for the electrical energy use of the food supermarket in this thesis. According to 
the same source, 45% of this energy is spent for refrigeration which equals to 144 kWh/m2 annually 
or near 16 W/m2.  
==PAGE:115==
 
 
114 
 
Measurements from the food supermarket showed that there are near 60 m of vertical multi-shelf 
display cabinets and near 65 m of horizontal display cases. Laboratory measurements made by 
Lindeberg et al. 2010 has specified the performance of both types of display cabinets. Refrigeration 
equipment has specific influence over the indoor temperature. On one side, it adds heat from 
lighting, fans, anti-sweat heaters and defrost equipment, but on the other side refrigerators 
extract heat from the space they are placed in and contribute to the cooling of that space. In order 
to be evaluated overall contribution of this type of equipment to the heat balance in a premise, 
the values for the generated heat and extracted heat are necessary to be determined. 
During the temperature measurement period from 20.10 to 30.10.2015 the average operative 
temperature in the zone with the refrigerators was 14.9oC. For the calculations in the thesis, it was 
assumed that the average temperature in this area is 18oC, since higher temperatures are expected 
during the summer period.  
The refrigeration loads in display cases are dependent on indoor conditions in the supermarket – 
a higher indoor temperature and relative humidity increase the cooling demand and the energy 
required (27). Energy balance in vertical displace case in shown on Figure C-2. The arrows on the 
same figure hint about the interaction between the indoor environment and the refrigeration 
equipment.
