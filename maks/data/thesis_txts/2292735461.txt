Abstract 
The aim of this thesis is to assist in the development of new stainless steels for low temperature sur-
face hardening. The project primarily focuses on gaseous nitriding of austenitic stainless steel, but a 
ferritic steel is also included. The effect of alloying element contents and microstructure on the re-
sulting hardened case have been investigated. Finally, findings made during the experimental work is 
used to suggest new alloys using thermodynamic modeling. The report is a continuation of the aca-
demic work performed by the supervisors. 
Experimental steels have been nitrided in various conditions and characterized in terms of hardness, 
case depth, and identification of the phases formed. From these experiments, it has been found that 
increasing additions of nitride forming elements leads to a higher hardness, while a trend of increasing 
case depth was observed with increasing chromium content. 
It was also the intention to investigate if elements with a higher affinity to nitrogen than chromium 
(SNFE) could be added to preferentially form these nitrides while suppressing chromium nitride for-
mation. This was not convincingly accomplished, possibly due to the extremely small size of these, or 
formation of amorphous nitrides. 
Thermodynamic modeling in Thermo-Calc is used with the purpose of identifying new austenitic stain-
less steels for low temperature nitriding. A number of selection criteria, namely suppression of inter-
metallics, cost, martensite start temperature, and SNFE content, are used to identify the most prom-
ising candidates. 
To analyze the samples a number of different techniques are used. Thermogravimetry is used to meas-
ure weight gain of the samples during the nitriding process. X-ray Diffraction is used to calculate 
strains in the nitrided layer, as well as identification of phases formed. A Vickers micro hardness in-
denter is used to measure surface hardness and case depth. Metallographic sectioning combined with 
scanning electron microscopy and light optical microscopy are used to analyze diffusion depths and 
phases formed throughout the nitrided interface.

Resumé 
Formålet med denne afhandling er at hjælpe i udviklingen af nye rustfrie stål til lav-temperatur over-
fladehærdning. Projektet fokuserer primært på gas nitrering af austenitisk rustfrit stål, men en ferri-
tisk stål er også inkluderet. Indflydelsen af legeringselementer og mikrostruktur på det resulterende 
hærdede lag er blevet undersøgt. Endelig, resultater fundet under det eksperimentelle arbejde er an-
vendt til at foreslå nye legeringer ved brug af termodynamisk modellering. Rapporten er en forlæn-
gelse af det akademiske arbejde udført af vejlederne. 
Eksperimentelle stål er nitreret i forskellige tilstande og karakteriseret i henhold til hårdhed, hård-
hedsprofil, og identificering af dannede faser. Fra disse eksperimenter er det blevet fundet at øget 
overfladehårdhed kan opnås ved højere tilsætning af nitriddannere, mens en tendens til at stigende 
kromindhold øger hårdhedsdybden er blevet observeret. 
Det var også formålet at undersøge om elementer med højere affinitet til nitrogen til at danne nitrider 
end krom, kunne præferentielt danne disse nitrider og derved undertrykke dannelsen af kromnitrider. 
Dette er dog ikke lykkedes at kunne blive bevist, muligvis på grund af den ekstremt lille størrelse af 
disse nitrider, eller i tilfælde hvor de dannede nitrider er amorfe. 
Termodynamisk modellering i Thermo-Calc er anvendt med formålet at identificere nye austenitiske 
rustfrie stål til lav temperatur nitrering. Et antal udvælgelseskriterier, henholdsvis undertrykkelse af 
intermetalliske faser, pris, martensit start temperatur og stærk nitriddanner indhold er anvendt til at 
finde de mest lovende kandidater. 
Til at analysere emnerne er et antal af forskellige teknikker anvendt. Thermogravimetri er brugt til at 
måle vægtforøgelse under nitrerings processen. Røntgen-diffraktion er brugt til at beregne tøjninger 
i det nitrerede lag, samt at identificere dannede faser. En Vickers mikrohårdhedsmåler er brugt til at 
måle overfladehårdhed samt indhærdedybde. Metallografisk tværsnit kombineret med scannings-
elektronmikroskopi og lys-optisk mikroskopi er anvendt til at analysere diffusions dybden samt dan-
nede faser gennem den nitrerede grænseflade.

1 Introduction 
The aim of this project is to investigate the effects of alloying elements and microstructure on low 
temperature gaseous nitriding of stainless steel. The investigated alloying elements in this project are 
chromium, nickel, silicon, and aluminium. The effect of alloying contents could lead to a faster hard-
ening response, while suppressing the detrimental effect of chromium nitride formation, which com-
monly limits hardening of stainless steel to low temperatures, low case depths, and long treatment 
times. The goal of the project is therefore to improve the mentioned hardening characteristics of 
stainless steel. The experimental work comprises nitriding stainless steels with different alloying ele-
ment content, along with different pre- and post-treatments, and compare their hardened character-
istics. For the sake of comparison between samples, only temperature is varied during the nitriding, 
as the purpose is not to optimize the nitriding conditions.  
The reason for not performing all combinations of material and nitriding conditions are that the scope 
is not to find the ideal material to the ideal process, but rather to focus on the inherent result of 
varying alloying elements. Other nitriding temperatures are included merely to observe the effect of 
increased temperature. 
Finally, the observations made during the experimental work will be used as basis to suggest new 
stainless steel alloys. Their compositions are determined using thermodynamic modeling. 
The remainder of this chapter is dedicated to introduce the reader to the families of stainless steels, 
as well as their working principle. Problems related to stainless steel will be described, among which 
nitriding will be presented as a possible solution. Later sections in this chapter deal with optimizing 
the alloying elements to better suit the nitriding process. Finally, the working principle behind the 
tools used in this report will be presented. 
 
1.1 Stainless steel 
Stainless steel is an alloy comprising of iron and at least 11 wt% chromium [1], and other alloying 
elements, most commonly nickel. Chromium is responsible for making the steel stainless, by forming 
chromium into chromium oxide at the very surface. This mechanism is called passivation, and pre-
vents the metal beneath the oxide from coming into contact with oxygen, thus limiting any further 
corrosion.  
The reaction of Cr passivating when exposed to oxygen is 
 
4Cr + 3O2 ↔2Cr2O3. 
 
(1.1) 
 
Higher chromium content generally yields better corrosion resistance, but also higher price. This is 
because only adding high amounts chromium to iron yields undesirable mechanical as well as chemi-
cal properties, so addition of e.g. austenite stabilisers like nickel becomes necessary.

Stainless steel is categorized by microstructure, but also by alloying element content. In general, there 
are four main types of stainless steel: ferritic, austenitic, duplex, and martensitic. 
Ferritic stainless steel maintains iron’s Body-centered cubic (BCC) structure, seen in Figure 1.1, with 
additions of chromium and typically small amounts of nickel. Due to the ferritic structure, formability 
is quite low. 
Austenitic stainless steels are generally the most corrosion resistant type, and typically contain larger 
amounts of chromium. Austenitic stainless steel has a Face-centered cubic (FCC) crystal structure, 
seen in Figure 1.2, which means it has very good formability. Chromium is a BCC stabiliser, so moder-
ate amounts of FCC stabilisers, typically nickel, have to be added as well. This means austenitic stain-
less steels are generally more expensive. AISI 304 stainless steel, commonly called 18-8 because it 
contains 18 wt% Cr and 8 wt% Ni, primarily contains this high amount of nickel to stabilize FCC. A 
ternary phase diagram of the Fe-Cr-Ni system at 900oC is shown in Figure 1.3, from which it can be 
seen that modest amounts of austenite (γ)-stabilisers are required to obtain a fully austenitic struc-
ture when chromium is added.

1.1.1 Corrosion of stainless steel 
Uniform corrosion is generally low for all stainless steels, as long as the environment allows them to 
passivate. In practice however, stainless steel tends to corrode locally by pitting rather than uniform 
corrosion. Here, microscopic defects in the material are attacked preferentially, and a pit is formed. 
Within this pit, an aggressive environment is formed where the passivating film formed by chromium 
is not stable. This leads to local fast corrosion rates, which are difficult to determine. 
The corrosion performance of stainless steels can roughly be compared by the Pitting Resistance 
Equivalent factor (PRE, sometimes PREN). This is based on the effectiveness of specific alloying ele-
ments on preventing the formation of stable pits, where corrosion can continue. PREN indicates the 
beneficial effect of adding nitrogen. The formula for the PREN factor for austenitic stainless steel can 
be seen in equation (1.3) after [8, p. 44]. The unit is wt%. 
 
PREN = Cr + 3.3Mo + 30N 
(1.3) 
 
A higher number indicates better resistance to pitting. The reason for the factor of 3.3 to molybdenum 
is because it improves the passive film by increasing the pitting potential, as well as reducing the 
critical current density and primary passivation potential [9, p. 282]. 
The factor 30 to nitrogen is because it has a major effect on increasing the pitting potential, especially 
when combined with molybdenum. The mechanism behind this is not fully understood, but has been 
attributed to the formation of alkaline NH4
+, which help neutralise the acidic environment found in 
pits [10]. Various PREN factors exist for different steel grades, and some even take into account the 
prescence of tungsten and sulphur. The above PREN factor only applies to austenitic stainless steels, 
and only for comparatively low concentrations of nitrogen in solid solution, below 0.5 wt% . The au-
thor is not aware of a PREN formula applicable to low-temperature nitrided steels, with a nitrogen 
content up to 12 wt%. It can be expected that equation (1.3) is invalid at these concentrations.

1.1.2 Mechanical performance 
The two most common austenitic stainless steels, AISI 304 and AISI 316 are primarily designed for 
corrosion resistance and formability. As such, they do not exhibit good resistance to mechanical wear, 
and traditional hardening processes involving heating (nitriding, carburising) can sensitize the steel, 
resulting in loss of corrosion resistance. This occurs because the chromium readily reacts with oxygen, 
nitrogen, and carbon, forming their respective ceramic compounds: now there is too little metallic 
chromium available to form the essential passive oxide layer required for the alloy to stop corrosion. 
Efforts to improve surface hardness of stainless steel without negatively affecting corrosion perfor-
mance began in the 1980s with Kolster for carburising under the process Kolsterising[1], and Zhang 
and Bell for nitriding [11]. The principle in both cases is to diffuse atomic carbon or nitrogen into the 
substrate lattice, causing compressive stress, at low enough temperatures so that formation of re-
spectively chromium carbide/nitride cannot be formed. The problem of sensitization is thus avoided. 
Low temperature nitriding will be further described in 1.2 Nitriding.  
Intermetallic phases can form in stainless steel when subjected to elevated temperatures, even for a 
short amount of time. This is problematic, as it can lead to degradation of mechanical and/or corrosion 
properties. In stainless steel, three possible intermetallic phases can typically form, namely σ (sigma), 
Laves, and χ (chi).[2] 
 
1.1.3 σ-phase 
Sigma phase is an intermetallic phase containing 30 wt% Fe and ~50 wt% Cr [12, p. 1], but the for-
mation can vary widely depending on the type of steel [13]. Formation of this phase is therefore prob-
lematic, as the sorrounding lattice becomes depleted of chromium. It is a complex, tetragonal unit 
cell, which takes a long time to nucleate (normally hundreds of hours). Because of the very high chro-
mium content, the phase is hard but also very brittle. Even a small amount of sigma formation can 
therefore have a huge effect on the toughness. An example of the severity of sigma phase formation 
is given from the ASM Handbook: Toughness decrease at room temperature of 85%, after exposing 
AISI[3] 310 to 870oC for 3,000 hours [14]. 
Sigma formation rarely forms from gamma austenite, but more commonly from delta ferrite, where 
the transformation occurs about 100 times faster. Consequently, the addition of strong ferrite stabi-
lisers promotes the formation of sigma phase [12, p. 2]. In commercial alloys, specifically Si markedly 
accelerates the formation of sigma. In general, all elements that stabilize ferrite promote sigma for-
mation [14].  
Conventional 18-8 stainless steels such as AISI 304 and Mo-bearing AISI 316 are practically immune to 
sigma formation. However, steels with e.g. high chromium contents such as AISI 310 with 25 wt% Cr 
can develop sigma phase when exposed to temperatures of 590-870oC [15]. The intermediate phase

1.1.4 Laves phase 
Formation of Laves phase can occur in a number of austenitic stainless steels, but normally only forms 
in small amounts. It has a hexagonal unit cell, and precipitates intragranularly or on grain boundaries. 
It typically forms when Mo, Nb, or Ti is added, and as such it does not form in AISI 304, but can form 
in AISI 316 after long ageing times. Examples are given by T. Sourmail [13] on AISI 316 forming Laves 
phase after a minimum of 1,000 hours at temperatures of 625-800oC. 
As such, Laves phase is not a particular concern in this project, but could be an issue when designing 
new alloys with the particular elements (Mo, Nb, Ti), and a fully austenitic structure is desired. Laves 
phase formation is used in some high temperature steels steels as an additional hardening mechanism 
by allowing grain size control during annealing [16].

1.1.5 χ-phase 
This phase is mostly a minor intermetallic phase occurring in stainless steels containing molybdenum 
such as AISI 316 above 750oC after long ageing times, but can also occur in minor quantities in AISI 
321 [13][4]. This phase should thus only be an issue when cooling unfortunate alloy combinations down 
from annealing temperature.  
To summarise, the intermetallic phases described here can have a detrimental effect on the mechan-
ical properties of stainless steel. Their formation is however not likely to occur during low-tempera-
ture nitriding, but could be an issue during austenitizing treatment. 
 
1.2 Nitriding 
In this section, nitriding will be described. 
Nitriding is a process to increase the hardness of a metal by diffusing in atomic nitrogen. This can be 
done in several ways, where the methods fall in three distinct categories: gaseous, salt bath, and 
plasma processes. Only gas nitriding is dealt with in this work.  
Conventional gas nitriding is performed at temperatures of 480-590oC. The gas is a mixture of a nitro-
gen-bearing gas, such as ammonia, NH3, and hydrogen, H2, or molecular nitrogen, N2. The additional 
gases are not used in the decomposition reaction of the ammonia, but are added to control the ni-
triding potential, or protecting the equipment used[5]. As an example, addition of N2 is used in the 
experiments in this project to shield the Scanning Thermal Analysis (STA) electronics used for thermo-
gravimetry (TG) during nitriding from direct exposure to NH3. Addition of hydrogen is used to lower 
the nitriding potential by reducing the degree of NH3 decomposition, thus reducing the maximum 
concentration of atomic N at the surface of the sample. The nitriding potential is defined in equation 
(1.4), and the nitrogen activity in equation (1.5) [17].

Regardning the diffusivity in different unit cells, this is closely related to the packing factor: lower

Conventional gas nitriding relies on the synergistic hardening effect of lattice straining and formation 
of nitrides. By inserting nitrogen in interstitial solid solution in the lattice, compressive stress is gen-
erated. Nitride forming elements, typically Cr, Al, Ti, Mo, and V form nitrides with very high hardness. 
Iron is also capable of forming nitrides, of which there are two types, namely ε (epsilon), and γ’ 
(gamma prime) nitrides. The compositions of these are 𝜀:     Fe2N1−𝑧 and 𝛾′:    Fe4N1−𝑥. Both phases 
are hard and brittle, but γ’ is less brittle. The phases ε and γ’ comprise the so-called white layer, that 
can form on the outermost surface, with a typical thickness of 5-50 µm. The combined effect of nitride 
formation and lattice expansion when nitrogen is absorbed in solid solution results in a hardness in-
crease in the material, with a measurable penetration depth of up to 1 mm [4, p. 611]. 
The effect of adding nitride forming elements to nitriding steel is typically that the hardness profile is 
modified. By a high addition of nitride formers, the penetration depth of nitrogen is lowered, while 
the case hardness increases [19], [20], [21, p. 145]. 
This type of hardening is however not suitable for stainless steels, as the formation of chromium ni-
trides, CrN and Cr2N, can lead to chromium depletion within the surrounding matrix. This can lead to 
loss of the stainless behavior, as the chromium depleted areas are then not able to form chromium 
oxides Cr2O3, necessary for the passivation of the steel. A different hardening process is therefore 
required. 
 
1.2.1 Low temperature gas nitriding 
When performing gas nitriding at lower temperatures (<480oC), the precipitation and formation of 
chromium nitride does not immediately occur due to slow reaction kinetics at these temperatures[6]. 
Instead, nitrogen diffuses into the parent lattice and occupies octahedral interstitial sites, creating 
compressive stress. This is used especially on austenitic and duplex (ferritic+austenitic) stainless 
steels, to improve mechanical properties without negatively affecting corrosion resistance. A new 
phase is not formed, but the existing austenite’s composition is changed as it takes up nitrogen. This 
modified phase has several names, such as expanded austenite, S-phase, γN, or supersaturated nitro-
gen layer. Using this method, it is possible to dissolve up to 23.5-26.5 at% N [22], which is far above 
what is possible to dissolve in either α-Fe or γ-Fe. The highest solubility of N in γ-Fe can be seen in 
Figure 1.6, and is approximately 10 at% N at 650oC[7]. Thus expanded austenite can accomodate more 
nitrogen than what the phase diagram predicts; the expanded phase can therefore be concluded to 
be metastable.

1.3 Alloy modification of stainless steel 
In this chapter, the theory behind alloy modification of stainless steel will be described. Furthermore, 
a literature study into how alloying elements affect the nitriding response will be carried out. 
Common austenitic stainless steels such as AISI 304 and 316 are not designed with low temperature 
nitriding in mind. By modifying the alloy composition, a significantly improved nitriding response 
should be possible. However, alloying elements have to be chosen and added with care, as even low 
additions can lead to formation of new phases, and loss of corrosion resistance as well as mechanical 
performance as a direct result.

The alloy modification serves two purposes: to stabilise expanded austenite, and selective formation 
of nitrides other than chromium-containing ones. 
 
1.3.1 Strong nitride forming elements (SNFE) 
The role of nitride forming elements (NFE) in conventional high temperature nitriding is well under-
stood. These elements form nitrides in the diffusion zone, and thus help increase the hardness of the 
nitrided layer. In low temperature nitriding of stainless steel however, nitride formation is unwanted, 
as this results in chromium depletion. As mentioned earlier, stainless steel must contain at least 11 
wt% chromium in order to be stainless, and higher content means a higher PREN number. When chro-
mium nitrides form, it effectively means that chromium is consumed from the surrounding matrix to 
form chromium nitrides, which do not help form a passive layer. The chromium-depleted areas then 
corrode freely. It is typically near the grain boundaries this depletion occurs, as nitrogen and chro-
mium diffuses faster through grain boundaries, and chromium near the grain boundaries do not have 
to diffuse far to form nitrides. The Gibb’s free energy required to form nitrides at grain boundaries is 
also lower, due to the high boundary energy, which is beneficial for heterogeneous nucleation [5, p. 
269]. This effect is called sensitization in stainless steel, and is seen typically as intergranular corrosion. 
M. Egawa et al. [26] have performed studies into the onset of chromium nitride formation in plasma 
nitriding, and they found that, when increasing process temperature, nitride formation occurred at a 
later stage for some alloys. They argued that the composition must therefore have an influence on 
the suppression of chromium nitride formation, and some elements therefore have a higher affinity 
to nitrogen. The term strong nitride forming elements (SNFE) will be used to characterise elements 
with a higher affinity to nitrogen than chromium. This work assumes that the effect of SNFE on sup-
pression of chromium nitride formation observed in plasma nitriding is also true for other nitriding 
processes. 
The mechanism behind this compositionally dependent onset is suggested to be stabilisation of the 
expanded austenite. This is done by elements with a higher affinity to nitrogen than chromium, thus 
preventing chromium nitrides from forming [26]. T.L. Christiansen et al. have suggested adding SNFE 
to stainless steel also with the purpose of forming these nitrides without formation of chromium ni-
trides [11]. This would mean higher process temperatures can be used, while also achieving increased 
hardness. 
Increasing the temperature at which chromium nitride formation occurs is very beneficial, as this 
leads to shorter nitriding times possible, due to the previsouly mentioned thermally activated nature 
of nitriding. 
CrN formation in austenitic stainless steel occurs when it is more favourable to form chromium nitride 
than expanding the austenite, or forming other nitrides. 
When the internal compressive stress in the γN layer exceeds -7 to -8 GPa, cracking occurs, leading to 
relaxation of the layer [27]. This occurs because excessive amounts of nitrogen are introduced into 
the lattice. Relaxation can also lead to other problems, as expanded austenite can also decompose 
into ferrite and nitrides to reduce internal stress [28]. Specifically cracking of the expanded austenite 
layer is problematic, as this can lead to sub-surface corrosion [29]. The nitriding potential should

1.4 Identification of SNFE 
In this section, the method used to identify strong nitride forming elements (SNFE) will be described. 
These elements are characterised by having a higher affinity to formation of nitrides than chromium.  
The degree of affinity is determined by the Gibb’s free energy released upon formation of the nitride, 
determined by thermodynamic data. This can be visualised in the Ellingham diagram, where the en-
ergy is plotted against temperature. Here, lower energy signifies higher affinity. When elements are 
added which have a higher affinity than Cr to N, these will according to thermodynamics be formed 
first, and formation of chromium nitride is suppressed [4, p. 680]. An Ellingham diagram have been 
constructed using HSC Chemistry software [30], and shown in Figure 1.7.

1.5 Computational modeling of thermodynamics 
Computational modeling of thermodynamics is in this project used to predict phases formed in the 
alloys used, along with scanning for new possible alloys for low temperature nitriding with SNFE ad-
ditions. 
By using a numerical approach, thousands of possible alloys can be screened, without the need for 
accompanying experimental work, which would be very expensive. This can lay the outline for new 
possible alloys in the application of low temperature heat treating. 
Some care should however be exercised, as the software is based on thermodynamic equilibrium, and 
thus phases which are theoretically the most stable, might form very slowly and thus not be as detri-
mental as predicted. An example of this is the earlier σ, Laves, and χ-phases. To make the alloys more 
easy to work with however, formation of these are desired to be minimized.

In this section, Energy-dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS) will briefly be described. 
EDS analyses the characteristic X-rays generated when the sample is hit with an electron beam, in this 
work in a Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM). The X-ray is generated because an electron from typ-
ically a K or L shell is knocked out by the incoming electron beam, and is replaced by an outlying

electron from the L or M shell. If the electron originated from the L shell, jumping to the K shell, it is 
called Kα X-ray. If it came from the M shell, it is called Kβ. 
The energies released by these X-rays are characteristic for each element, but lower atomic number 
elements do not emit high energy X-rays in EDS. Lower energies tend to become reabsorbed by the

accurately. Identification of elements becomes easier with increasing atomic number Z. This is be-
cause heavier elements emit higher energy X-rays, that are thus less likely to be reabsorbed by the 
surrounding lattice, and less affected by Bremsstrahlung.  
EDS is in this project used to analyse concentration profiles, as well as the composition of intermetal-

3.3.1 Concluding remarks on thermogravimetry 
Composition was observed to affect the rate of mass increase for M1 & M2 nitrided at 450oC. Higher 
chromium is observed to increase the total mass uptake of nitrogen, possibly due to an increased 
number of nitrogen trap sites allowed by chromium. The mechanism will be further outlined in chap-
ter 4 Discussion. 
Increasing the temperature from 420 to 500oC leads to an increased mass uptake, as expected for a 
thermally activated process like diffusion. 
The effect of microstructure on nitrogen uptake has been investigated for two different alloys: a fer-
ritic in modified conditions, and an austenitic with varying martensite contents. The rate of mass in-
crease was observed to be affected by the initial microstructure for both samples. The intention for 
modifying the ferritic sample was to create an austenitic alloy with close to identical alloying elements, 
in order to compare diffusion rates between ferrite and austenite, where diffusivity through austenite 
was expected to be lower. The opposite was observed, suggesting austenite might not have been 
formed, and a mixture of martensite and ferrite have formed instead. This will be investigated further 
in section 3.5 Microstructural evaluation. 
The rate of mass increase for the austenitic-martensitic sample revealed that samples containing mar-
tensite obtained a higher rate of mass increase, suggesting the lower packing factor of martensite 
leads to increased diffusion rates. 
Due to the very small size and surface area of the specimens, the final mass increase of the samples 
should not be paid too much attention, as the mass gain is very small for all samples. If comparable 
results are to be obtained with this method, larger surface area samples should be used, e.g. foil sam-
ples. The results obtained here are however not crucial, as trends observed in thermogravimetry 
should also be comparable to cross-section analyses, e.g. case depth is proportional to total mass 
increase.

3.4.1 Surface hardness  
In this section, surface hardness measurements will be presented, and trends observed will be ana-
lysed. 
Surface hardness measurements are performed at two different loads: 10 and 50gf[22]. Lower hard-
ness values are measured at higher loads, in part because the thin hardened layer cannot support 
high loads. The higher load value is therefore a mix of surface and bulk hardness. 
Several measurements are necessary, as there is some spread in the data. This is because of effects 
such as local variations in grain orientations, proximity to grain boundaries, or local variations in layer 
thickness, implying different response from the underlying bulk. This is especially a problem at lower 
loads, where the indentation is smaller. On several samples it was not possible to distinguish grain 
boundaries, hence the need for boxplots rather than an average of several hardness measurements. 
Each sample has been indented 10 times at both loads. 
Surface hardnesses of samples nitrided at 420oC are shown in Figure 3.9 for 10gf indentation, and in 
Figure 3.10 for 50gf indentation. 
The pure alloys M1 (12.5Cr, 15.7Ni), M2 (15.5Cr, 12.5Ni), and M3 FCC (12.3Cr, 13.2Ni) enables com-
parison of the effect of Cr and Ni content on the produced hardness in a similar microstructure. From 
loads at 50gf, Figure 3.10, it can be seen that the Ni content does not have a significant influence on 
the hardness when comparing M1 and M3 FCC. Increasing Cr content does however increase the 
hardness, as seen from M2 at 50gf. From the measurement at 10gf, Figure 3.9, it is not possible to 
distinguish between M2 and M3 FCC. The cause for this is not quite understood, but could suggest 
experimental error. 
Because of increased hardness and diffusivity of N through martensite, an increased surface hardness 
is obtained for M3 if it is allowed to produce martensite (M3 AS & M3 Dry ice). The presence of mar-
tensite in the surface prior to nitriding can therefore mitigate the effect of lowering Cr content, as 
evidenced from M3 AS & M3 Dry ice, as seen both on Figure 3.9 & Figure 3.10. Different indentation 
forces have been utilized in the two figures. 
AISI 304 contains the highest amount of Cr, as well as containing some Si, which appear beneficial in 
producing high hardness at both 10 and 50gf indentations. 
When comparing differences between 10 and 50gf, the ranking is mostly similar. A notable disconti-
nuity is for M3 FCC, which has a decent hardness ranking at 10gf, but is much worse at 50gf. The cause 
for this could be that at 10gf some martensite hardening has occurred, which do not provide sufficient 
hardening at higher (50gf) loads. 
The ferritic sample 13Cr3Si cannot directly be compared with the other samples due to the different 
microstructure: ferritic stainless steels are generally stronger, thus harder, at room temperature com-
pared to austenitics. The very high hardness of this sample, apparent at 50gf, could also be attributed 
to the high Si content, as Cr content is lower when compared to AISI 304. Another strengthening 
mechanism of 13Cr3Si could also be martensitic hardening during the indentation.

From the hardness profiles for the austenitic alloys (M1, M2, M3 FCC, AISI 304) at 420oC, the influence 
of NFE (here Cr) is not immediately apparent; M1 and M3 FCC have similar Cr contents, but the latter 
obtains almost twice the case depth. The cause for this is not understood, but could be attributed to 
difficulty in determining the case depth consistently by physical indentation. Later optical measure-
ments suggest a less severe difference, presented in 3.4.2.1 Optical measurements of case depth, and 
discussed further in chapter 4 Discussion. If M3 FCC is disregarded, a trend of increasing Cr or NFE 
(Cr+Si) content leads to an increased case depth, as M1(12.5Cr) < M2(15.5Cr) < AISI 304(18Cr, <1Si). 
This suggests that NFE assist in trapping N in expanded austenite, while increasing diffusivity of sub-
sequent N. 
According to S. Mändl et al. [42], this is a point of contention in the literature. The reason for this is 
that the trapping model assumes an increase of the N diffusion coefficient when N equals Cr concen-
tration, which is not the case in earlier reported experiments. 
Nitriding at higher temperatures, here 450 and 500oC, the trend is repeated; here, too, the increasing 
NFE content leads to increased case depth, as M1 450 < M2 450 < AISI 304 450; and M1 500 < M2 
500. It can be expected that there is an upper limit on temperature or nitriding time at which the 
previous nitride formation will reduce the further diffusion of nitrogen in the sample, which is ob-
served for conventional high temperature (500-600oC) gas nitriding [19], [20], [21, p. 145]. This is 
however not observed in these experiments. 
When comparing the influence of microstructure on samples with the same composition, M3 in Figure 
3.15 & Table 3.7, it can be seen that the nitrogen penetration is greatly affected by the microstructure. 
Comparing M3 in austenitized to the martensite-containing condition, an increased case depth of a 
factor 2-3 is obtained. This verifies that nitrogen diffuses faster through martensite, due to the de-
creased packing density of the BCT unit cell, as described in section 1.1 Stainless steel. 
A similar result is obtained for the ferritic 13Cr3Si sample, Figure 3.17 & Table 3.7, when comparing 
the as-received to the BCT treated sample after nitriding (13Cr3Si BCT 420), although the increase is 
slightly lower. The BCT treatment of this sample was austenitized at 1020oC in pure N2, which pro-
duced martensite during cooling. This suggests that the nitrogen diffuses faster even through marten-
site than ferrite. It should however be kept in mind that the 13Cr3Si sample after the BCT treatment 
already has received some surface hardening because of the BCT treatment. This is shown in further 
detail during the microstructural evaluation of this sample in section 3.5.2 Ferritic 13Cr3Si steel. 
In terms of NFE contents, AISI 304 and 13Cr3Si alloys are approximately similar. Because of this, the 
diffusivity through BCC and FCC can be compared from a case depth perspective, and it is found that 
case depth growth through BCC (13Cr3Si) occurs at almost twice the speed as compared to FCC (AISI 
304). 
The purpose of the M3 420 LN2 sample is to perform a martensite treatment post nitriding to better 
support the expanded austenite phase. It is however apparent from the hardness profile that this did 
not succeed. The sample is further investigated in section 3.5 Microstructural evaluation.

3.4.3 Concluding remarks on hardness measurements 
To summarize the findings made from the hardness measurements, it is found that: 
Low temperature nitriding produces a very hard surface, approaching 2000 HV0.010 with applied pro-
cess parameters. The expanded layer does however not have a uniform hardness, as it decreases 
when approaching the substrate, evidenced from a drop in hardness at measurements with increasing 
load (10 and 50 gf). This corresponds well with the expected nitrogen concentration profile for ex-
panded austenite, shown in Figure 1.5 after [18]. 
Many repetitions are necessary when performing surface hardness measurements, as it is not possible 
to distinguish grains or precipitates in the surface, which affect the result. 
Upon nitriding above a critical temperature, the resulting surface hardness decreases. The cause for 
this is likely breakdown of the expanded austenite phase, accompanied by epsilon and chromium ni-
tride formation. This is not observed for the ferritic 13Cr3Si alloy, which suggests either that: presence 
of Si increases the temperature for which expanded ferrite breaks down, or, that expanded ferrite in 
general is more tolerant to higher low-temperature nitriding temperatures. A third possibility is that 
formation of Si3N4 counteracts the effect of expanded ferrite breakdown. The last theory could also 
explain the consistent hardness for AISI 304 at 420 and 450oC, despite only containing 0.6wt% Si. 
Nitrogen diffusivity is affected by the temperature as well as the microstructure: higher temperatures 
as well as lower unit cell packing factor leads to higher case depths. 
From the experiments on M1 and M2, because of their identical process histories (similar grain size, 
distribution and orientation), it can be concluded that higher NFE concentrations, at least as far as Cr 
is concerned, leads to increased hardness. From the hardness profile measurements, it has not been 
possible to distinguish if the Cr content affects the case depth, but a trend of increasing Cr content 
increases the depth is observed. Optical measurements also suggest this trend. To confirm this how-
ever, additional experiments are necessary in order to get better statistics, such as several samples 
and measurements, as this contradicts the literature: increased Cr content conventionally leads to 
increased hardness, but reduced case depth [21, p. 145]. 
The process history of AISI 304 is not identical to the model alloys M1 and M2, which is why a direct 
correlation between these three samples and their NFE contents should be scrutinized. Specifically, 
the effect of grain size could affect the resulting case depth in AISI 304 also. It will however be men-
tioned that the trend observed regarding case depth increases with increasing Cr contents is echoed 
for AISI 304.

3.5.1.1 
Model alloy M3 
Model alloy M3 is especially interesting, due to its propensity to form martensite. This sample has 
therefore been nitrided both in the as-received condition, in austenitized condition, and in sub-zero 
Celsius treated conditions, to investigate the effect of martensite on nitrogen diffusivity. Images of 
M3 are shown in Figure 3.19 (D)-(F). 
In the austenitized and subsequent nitrided condition (M3 FCC), Figure 3.19 (D), the expanded aus-
tenite layer is uniform, and similar to previous samples presented here. Note however that martensite 
formation has taken place below the expanded phase. This has likely occurred after nitriding, when 
the sample was returned to room temperature. Note also that martensite formation has occurred not 
close to the edge, but further inside the material. This indicate that diffusing nitrogen in front of the 
expanded phase could stabilize austenite, suppressing martensite formation. A thicker expanded 
phase is visible around the crack where a grain boundary passes through the layer, indicating that 
nitrogen diffuses faster through grain boundaries. Similar observations are reported by W. P. Tong et 
al., but for pure iron (BCC) [44].  
The austenite-martensite transformation of the substrate is interesting from a mechanical point of 
view, as this enables a secondary hardening of the underlying substrate, so that the expanded phase 
is better supported under high loads. This should reduce the tendency of cracking as a consequence 
of exceeding the load-carrying capability of the substrate. 
In the as-received condition, martensite is present at the outermost layer of the sample, as seen in 
Figure 3.19 (E) and Figure 3.19 (F). The expanded region is likely not expanded austenite, but rather 
expanded martensite. This cannot directly be confirmed by the LOM pictures, but XRD could be used 
to verify the crystal structure, as suggested by S.K. Kim et al. [45]. This has been done in chapter 3.6 
XRD results, section 3.6.1.1 M3 nitrided. 
Figure 3.19 (E) & (F) are of the same sample, indicating that martensite formation is not uniform 
throughout the interface. From these pictures, it can be seen that martensite affects the diffusivity of 
nitrogen. It is however not evident if nitrogen diffuses faster through martensite itself, or if the 
strained interfaces between martensite and austenite facilitates the increased diffusivity. The former 
is likely the major contributor, owing to the decreased packing factor of martensite. In any case, it can 
be seen that a thickness increase of the expanded phase by a factor of 2-3 has occurred when com-
paring the expanded martensite regions of Figure 3.19 (F) and the expanded austenite region of Figure 
3.19 (D) & (E). 
Due to the martensite content in the surface, it is somewhat difficult to evaluate the case depth of 
this sample, as martensite formation is not uniform across the surface. This could prove problematic

reached the average case depth. A need for a consistent, uniform martensite treatment would there-

From Figure 3.32, a trend of increasing Al content decreases the expanded depth is observed. It is

3.5.6 Concluding remarks on microstructural evaluation 
Summarizing the findings made during the microstructural evaluation, it has been found that obser-
vations made in section 3.3 Thermogravimetry correlate well with results observed here: increasing 
N uptake corresponds with increasing case depth. 
At high temperature (500oC), distinct phases are visible in addition to the expanded austenite. Use of 
additional optical methods (dark-field, polarized light), additional features in the interface become 
apparent, which are not immediately evident from bright-field images. 
Regarding the influence of alloying elements on case depth, an increased case depth is obtained with 
increasing Cr content. However, on the Al-diffused sample, a weak trend of increasing Al results in a 
decreased case depth, suggesting that nitrides which form easily (AlN) will form more readily than 
CrN. 
Furthermore, a homogenizing treatment has been identified which can produce useable Al gradients 
in stainless steel: 700oC for 1 hour in pure H2 gas, with a sample thickness of 1 mm. 
==PAGE:82==
Chapter 3 Results & interpretation 
72 
The effect of microstructure on diffusion depth is seen, where unit cells with a lower packing factor 
(martensite) allows for a thicker apparent case depth. It is however also observed that the produced 
martensite is not uniform across the surface, which makes measuring the average case depth difficult. 
Furthermore, boundaries between phases, especially seen for the Al-diffused samples, leads to a 
much increased diffusion “highway” for nitrogen to penetrate further into the sample, resulting in an 
apparent increase in the expanded depth close to this boundary. 
Post treatment after nitriding, observed for M3 in Figure 3.20 (D), could improve support of the ex-
panded phase, but earlier case depth measurements, shown in Table 3.7, suggest higher success when 
samples contain martensite prior to nitriding instead. 
The high nitriding potential has lead to a distinct new phase formed on top of the expanded layer. The 
brittle nature of this layer suggest nitride formation, most likely CrN and epsilon nitride.

3.7 Further simulations in Thermo-Calc 
In this section, trends observed based on the nitriding experiments will be used to suggest composi-
tions for new model alloys.  
As part of a previous report on the subject of scanning for possible alloys for low temperature nitriding 
of stainless steel, a script has been developed in C++ by Senior Researcher Kristian Vinter Dahl [48]. 
This script functions by calculating the equilibrium of phases formed at a specific temperature with 
varying alloy contents in Thermo-Calc. The script enables equilibrium phase calculation of thousands 
of possible alloys by modifying the alloying elements gradually after each calculation. The database 
used in Thermo-Calc in this section is exclusively TCFE 7.0. 
The program starts with an initial alloy, where alloying elements are modified in specific increments, 
listed in Table 3.19, along with metal prices obtained from [49]. The program combines thousands of 
possible alloy compositions, and calculate equilibrium phases formed for these at a specific tempera-
ture. The prices are used to estimate the overall price for the different candidate alloy compositions. 
Prices for Cr and Si originate from ferrochrome (FeCr) and ferrosilicon (FeSi), respectively. Fe is from 
a generic cheap steel, specifically HRB335[35]. Al and Ni are selected as pure ingots with a 99.7 and 
99.9% purity, respectively. All prices are listed EXW (pickup at the seller’s factory/depot). Full descrip-
tions of the materials are listed in Appendix 8.8 Metal prices. The actual price of the elements are not 
relevant, they are included merely to compare the price of the simulated alloys.

3.7.2 Additional considerations regarding new alloys 
In this section, additional considerations regarding the properties of the proposed alloys will be dis-
cussed. 
Casting of aluminum is difficult because of its high oxidation potential. This means that protective 
gases have to be used, as well as addition of oxygen-getters to the melt, which subsequently has to 
be mechanically filtrated out again. Likewise, when large amounts of Al are added to steel castings, 
reduction or removal of subsequent alumina formation is necessary, as Al oxides will not act as a SNFE. 
The validity of the numerically suggested alloys could also be questioned. This primarily relates to the 
kinetics of sigma phase formation, which commonly only occurs after hundreds of hours in stainless 
steel at elevated temperatures, as described in chapter 1.1 Stainless steel, section 1.1.2 Mechanical 
performance. Note also that for most of the suggested alloys ((B), (C), & (D)), sigma phase formation 
is much lower than what is calculated for AISI 304, seen in Figure 1.4, page 8. Further investigation 
into how much sigma phase could be tolerated in a new alloy could further reduce the cost of the new 
nitriding steel. As mentioned in section 1.2.1 Low temperature gas nitriding, nitrogen expanded aus-
tenite is not stable at higher temperatures, so sigma phase formation should not be a huge issue in 
these steels. 
More serious an issue could be the formation of silicides such as Cr3Si at low temperature, but the 
formation kinetics and mechanical impact of this phase have not been investigated further.

4 Discussion 
The discussion presented in this chapter is a summary of the observations made during the experi-
mental work in chapter 3 Results & interpretation.  
The aim of this project was to investigate the effect of alloying elements, specifically Cr, Ni, Al, and Si, 
as well as microstructure on nitriding. 
From the thermogravimetric measurements, a trend of increasing Cr content lead to a higher mass 
increase, suggesting that higher Cr allows for higher diffusion of N, seen in Figure 3.5. Later hardness 
profile measurements as well as LOM measurements confirmed this trend, seen in Table 3.7 and Table 
3.8, respectively. These are summarized in Figure 4.1 for M1-3 samples. Having a martensite-contain-
ing surface also allowed for a higher mass uptake, and corresponding increase in the case depth. 
Surface hardness measurements revealed that a higher hardness could be obtained with higher Cr 
contents, seen in Figure 3.10, suggesting that trapping of N by Cr with limited to no nitride formation 
allows for higher hardness. Furthermore, presence of martensite in the surface prior to nitriding also 
allows for higher hardness, and better support at higher loads. Martensite content was observed to 
compensate for the lack of Cr, as evident from M3 AS achieving superior hardness to even M2 both 
at 10 and 50 gf, despite the increased Cr content of M2. 
Hardness measurements also confirmed the concentration gradient of N present in the expanded 
layer when comparing hardness measurements at 10 and 50 gf: higher hardness values are observed 
at lower loads. This is because the N concentration is very high close to the surface, and then de-
creases through the expanded layer, as seen on Figure 1.5, page 10, when using an infinite nitriding 
potential. As such, the topmost layers of the expanded phase can be expected to have a higher hard-
ness, which decreases gradually when approaching the core. 
Comparing case depths, physical indentation and optical measurements are shown in Figure 4.1. At 
420oC, M1 and M2 case depths are approximately similar, as all three measurement types do not 
show the same trend. At 450 and 500oC however, a trend is seen: increasing Cr contents increases 
case depth. This is furthermore supported by thermogravimetric measurements on M1 & M2 at 
450oC, shown in Figure 3.5. Trends in case depths are difficult to distinguish, likely due to the small 
population size. The optical measurements appear more accurate compared to physical indentation, 
as the nitride-formed layer can be seen more clearly on the etched samples.

Based on the findings in the above experimental work, scanning for new possible austenitic stainless 
steels have been performed, using a C++ script made by the supervisor Kristian Vinter Dahl, which 
calculated equilibrium phase compositions in Thermo-Calc for some 20,449 possible new stainless 
steels. Equilibrium have been calculated at 900-1,100oC to simulate behavior during austenitizing tem-
peratures, and at 420-500oC to simulate behavior during nitriding temperatures. By using constraints, 
described in section 3.7 Further simulations in Thermo-Calc, promising alloy candidates have been 
identified which do not form unwanted phases neither during austenitizing or nitriding temperatures. 
These have been compared to conventional AISI 304 steel, and found to be more expensive, but be-
cause of favorable additions of Si and/or Al, superior performance from these steels could be expected 
in terms of nitriding and wear performance.  
By plotting the martensite start temperature Ms versus price of alloying elements, a trend of decreas-
ing price increases the Ms temperature has been observed. This is advantageous, as earlier results 
suggested steels with high Ms temperature more easily forms martensite, which is highly beneficial in 
increasing the case depth at similar nitriding conditions compared to fully austenitic and fully ferritic 
stainless steels. It should be kept in mind however, that a heat treatment step prior to nitriding is 
recommended, with the purpose of creating a more uniform martensitic surface. This should lead to

From thermogravimetry on 13Cr3Si in martensitic condition seen in Figure 3.6, it was found that a 
much-increased rate of nitrogen uptake occurred. The cause for this could be attributed to precipita-
tion of N2-gas porosities, which are subsequently “blown up” by an epsilon sublayer, resulting in faster 
than prescribed growth rates, as suggested by Somer & Mittemeijer [39]. XRD measurements on this 
sample also confirm the presence of epsilon nitride. This mechanism is however unlikely, as this pre-
cipitation should not occur at the low temperature of 420oC. Another possibility could therefore be 
that the mix of martensite and ferrite in this sample allows for a higher rate of nitrogen uptake. 
From the double-logarithmic plot on the remaining samples, the nitrogen uptake appears to follow a 
mix of diffusion controlled (slope 0.5) and activation controlled (slope 1) processes. 
Initial XRD investigations on M3 samples confirmed the effect of having a sufficiently high Ms temper-
ature could lead to martensite formation at room temperature. If the treatment temperature is too 
low however, martensite formation is impeded, as evident from LN2 treated sample, which had 
formed very little martensite, seen in Figure 3.1 and Table 3.1. 
On the subject of texture (preferential grain orientation) in the samples, some is present. This is seen 
from not all ferrite (martensite) [200] and austenite [220] reflections are visible on all M3 samples 
prior to nitriding, seen in Figure 3.1. This could explain why less martensite is observed on the sample 
M3 dry ice, which was not austenitized prior to XRD, compared to the as-received sample. 
From LOM images on 13Cr3Si nitrided at 500oC degrees, Figure 3.21, the nitrided interface can more 
clearly be seen. Especially from the polarized light image, distinct layers can be seen, where some are 
more severely attacked by the etchant. These are more thoroughly described in section 3.5.2 Ferritic 
13Cr3Si steel. 
XRD measurements on 13Cr3Si are somewhat difficult to analyse due to the severe peak broadening, 
especially at 420oC, seen in Figure 3.35. Only 2 peaks are distinguishable on this sample, but they are 
difficult to quantify as higher reflections are not visible. From the sample nitrided at 500oC, and com-
paring to the LOM images of this interface seen in Figure 3.21, the majority of the signal is likely pro-
duced by epsilon nitride, possibly combined with CrN.  
From XRD stress measurements, nitriding produced a higher stress in samples with a higher chromium 
content, evident from Figure 3.33 and shown in Table 3.17. Calculations are shown in Appendix 8.4.2 
XRD stress calculations. Produced stress did not appear to be affected by the microstructure, here 
volume fraction of martensite in M3, shown in Table 3.18. It has not been possible to quantify stresses 
on the 13Cr3Si steel, due to the aforementioned peak broadening, as well as bad grain statistics.

5 Conclusion 
This project has investigated the effects of alloying elements and microstructure on low temperature 
gaseous nitriding of stainless steel, where the investigated elements were chromium, nickel, silicon, 
and aluminium.  
It has been found that higher additions of chromium increases the expanded case depth, when nitrid-
ing at lower temperatures in the range of 420-500oC, using an infinite nitriding potential. This has 
been measured both by hardness profiles, and verified by LOM images of cross-sections. Surface hard-
ness upon indentation was also observed to increase with higher Cr contents. 
Nickel was not observed to influence the nitriding process appreciably in an austenitic structure. 
The purpose of adding Al and Si to stainless steel was to suppress CrN formation. This has not con-
vincingly been proven to have occurred, likely because of the high nitriding potential used. This re-
sulted in CrN being detected by XRD on all samples except for the high Si-containing 13Cr3Si ferritic 
stainless steel, which exhibited severe peak broadening, making quantification impossible. 
Increasing aluminium contents in the stainless steel has resulted in decreased case depths, when ni-
triding at 420oC. This is analogous to conventional high temperature nitriding, suggesting that prefer-
ential formation of AlN occurs, with limited formation of CrN. This is in agreement with thermody-
namic data for nitrogen affinity. 
By modifying the microstructure, a higher case depth was obtained when nitriding stainless steels 
with martensite in the surface. The resulting case depth was increased by a factor of 2-3 compared to 
the original fully austenitic structure. Furthermore, surface hardness likewise improved for marten-
site-containing samples, even when low amounts of martensite were present. 
Based on the aforementioned findings, thermodynamic modeling have been used to scan for new 
austenitic stainless steels containing high amounts of strong nitride forming elements (here Al & Si), 
added with the purpose of suppressing CrN formation. It was found that lean austenitic stainless steels 
(low Cr & Ni contents) have a higher Ms temperature while also being cheaper compared to high Cr & 
Ni containing steels. From this, it is concluded that lean austenitic-martensitic stainless steels are the 
most promising candidates for new low temperature nitriding stainless steels, especially when com-
bined with a pretreatment to produce high amounts of martensite in the surface prior to nitriding.

6 Perspective & outlook 
Putting this project in perspective, new aspects could be investigated in following works. In regards 
to the conclusion that higher chromium leads to increased case depth, a larger sample population 
could be used to verify if this is actually true. This is based on the inconsistent case depth measure-
ments made on samples M1-3.  
In order to produce new alloys with varying SNFE contents, edge-aluminizing by pack cementation 
have been used in this project, but the resulting case depth was difficult to analyze. A different ap-
proach could be used instead, such as: aluminizing samples of varying surface area to volume ratio, 
followed by a homogenizing heat treatment during austenitizing. This way, fully austenitic steels with 
varying Al contents could be produced, and the effect of SNFE contents could more easily be analysed 
by XRD, LOM, surface hardness, and hardness profiles.  
On thermodynamic modeling of new alloys, this is a valuable tool for calculating equilibrium condi-
tions. The practical application of these results could be of limited relevance, especially concerning 
phases which take a long time to form (e.g. hundreds of hours for sigma phase). Further investigation 
into intermetallic phase kinetics could lead to cheaper alloys with lower Ni content.  
Concerning the corrosion resistance of the proposed stainless steels, a PREN factor have not been 
calculated due to its inaccuracy post nitriding. From a corrosion perspective, experimental work into 
the effect of nitrogen expanded austenite on the PREN factor could produce interesting results. From 
the experimental work, it was observed that pitting occurred during etching in the substrate, but 
rarely in the expanded phase. 
Further on corrosion experiments, as expanded austenite is often applied to reduce corrosive wear, 
this is also a field where the effect of alloying elements and martensite content merits investigation. 
Especially inhomogeneous martensite formation in the surface might question the beneficial effect of 
increased, but irregular, case depth. 
Regarding the difficulty in detecting Si3N4 on 13Cr3Si, this could be investigated by glancing incidence 
XRD (GI-XRD), which is useful for detecting thinner sections of the sample, thus suppressing the signal 
from the underlying material. Additionally, GI-XRD could be used to detect how phases in the nitrided 
layers are distributed, by gradually removing the layers, followed by GI-XRD. 
Finally, manufacturing of the proposed alloy compositions would be the ultimate test to see if these 
steels actually produce better results after nitriding compared to conventional, commonly used AISI-
grade steels such as AISI 304 and AISI 316.
