This project  proposes a design process that creates a lively environment that in-
creases social sustainability in public spaces. Because a  human based design 
should be the leading factor for public space’s design to be able to create more 
sustainable cities. So: How to implement a human based design method process to 
improve social sustainability into the built environment by nudging an intention?. 
Using the concepts about social sustainability, lively planning, libraries, co-
creation, behavioral monitoring, conceptualization, design,  and prototyping  an 
study case was held at Dokk1’s Living Room, where co-creation activities and be-
havioral monitoring were done to make a concept and a design that where validat-
ed through a prototype at the same space.  
The most valuable outcome for this project was an specific design process to in-
clude users’ input into the built environment.  A practical and efficient way to in-
troduce user input into the design process is through a co-creation process com-
plemented with a smart behavioral monitoring. This information can be link with 
technological data by a concept to create a baseline for the design decision mak-
ing. Finally the validation confirms the success of the public space that finally will 
improve social sustainability.

An integrated human-centered approach is the leading factor, for public space de-
sign, to be able to enhance lively spaces and social sustainability among the com-
munity, as described by Cilliers et al (2015).  
In the design process developed during this thesis project, co-creation, behavior 
and design thinking form a large contribution, along with technical and engineered 
elements such as the use of sensors and data analysis. These strategies are meant 
to introduce user input into the design process to improve and validate the public 
space’s social function and sustainability. A case study design process that com-
bines quantitative and qualitative elements is the core of this thesis.

CONCEPTS ON SOCIAL INNOVATION INTO BUILT ENVIRONMENT 
For Andreasen et al (2015), a method is 
a goal-oriented simplification of engi-
neering activities as a standardized work 
description. A design method is a syn-
thesis of the steps of creative activities 
that can also be seen as a structured 
search, classification, discussion, evalu-
ation, and decision-making.  
The use of the design method depends 
on the designer’s mindset and know-
hows, “a condition for using a method is 
that the user can come to the same in-
sight as the method creator’s interpreta-
tion of the use situation.” (Andreasen et 
al , 2015, p.54).

For a design method to be widely used it 
needs: (1) good interpretation and un-
derstanding of the task, (2) properly 
judging appropriateness and timeliness 
of use, (3) imagining and planning the 
use of the method based on knowledge, 
experience and skill, (4) understanding 
the theory related to the method ap-

the method and proper procedure when 
designing; judging the validity of the 
method and its results.  
Then, a good method should not present 
difficulties on: (a) defining performance, 
(b) how methods are presented and for-
mulated, and (c) applying methods in a 
wider process. At the end, according to 
Andreasen et al (2015), a design process 
is a strategy to find satisfactory solu-
tions for design situations by materializ-
ing ideas.

Social sustainability, according to McKen-

This chapter describes the co-creation phase of the design process for a public 
space. According to Sanders & Stappers (2008) “emerging design practices will 
change what is designed, how we design, and who designs”…“transition from a 
product perspective to the purpose perspective”(pag.11) and this co-creation 
phase helps to define the what, how and who. 
TOPIC 
RELEVANCE, IMPORTANCE & MOTIVATION 
Co-creation is important because it is a way to interact with the user and make 
them part of the design. According to Ericksson et al (2015), involving the user 
gives broader and new perspectives on the projects. 
Sanders & Stappers’ (2008) research describes how architecture and planning are 
last among the design disciplines that explore designing for a purpose, design for 
experiencing and design for sustainability. They mention that designers have 
moved closer to the future user, moving from user-center to co-creation. Also, 
they stated the opportunity to bring co-design tools and methods to practice.  
Therefore, the co-creation tools and method of the built environment design pro-
cess is proposed as a link between the designing team and the users.  
 How to use co-creation as the most practical and efficient way to introduce 
user input into the built environment process design?

“Co-creation practiced at the early front end of the design development process 
can have an impact with positive, long-range consequences.”(Sanders & Stappers,

Using the co-creation process for defining real needs, problems and wishes that 
come straight from the user is a practical way to introduce the users’ input. The co
-creation activities efficiently give basic information for decision making to create 
a better built environment according to the users’ contributions at the beginning 
of the design process. At Dokk1’s Living Room this process gave basic information 
for decision making according to the needs problems and wishes from the visitors 
and the administration of this public space. 
The users’ needs, problems and ideas are found to create what can improve a live-
ly space—therefore its social sustainability—.This enhances the idea of the design 
process as a more sustainable design. 
IMPLICATIONS FOR THE DESIGN PROCESS 
Roles on the design process are changing, and with it the existing power struc-
tures. This process allows ideas and decisions can come from users and other 
stakeholders. At the same time, all the results are used as a decision making base. 
Documenting observation helps on the later informed decision making, and cre-
ates a better connection to the project. It is also a feedback source for prototypes 
knowing that people will not always act as you expect. 
Interviews improve the understanding of the target of users that reflects the 
“persona” for whom they design. It is a different process with every person, be-
cause of the interviewer faculties and the interviewee’s intention. Interviews go 
along with observations for prototype feedback, because users’ actions and expe-
riences can also get unexpected solutions and help users to appropriate the de-
sign and the space. Additionally, people have seen past mistakes, do not forget to 
ask for them not to be repeated.

The major limitations are the language rejections, the small timeframe for the co-

LEARNING REFLECTIONS 
After the methodology used on the Dokk1’s Living Room, every step has learnings. 
The participant observation should have started before the interviews because that 
would have improved the open-ended questions. The documentation on the ob-
servation has to be defined before it starts. After the participant observation, more 
accurate open-ended questions for the interviews can be created to improve the 
“persona” understanding, and past attempts need to be part of it. 
The design of the workshops activities should be done after having information 
from observations, interviews and polls. Furthermore, bigger projects and design

This chapter research is focused on behavioural patterns and monitoring these, 
and how this can inform a design process. 
According to Ghavampour et al (2017), a public space can be described as a physi-
cal configuration designated for functional activities (like reading or socializing), 
that boost a behavioral pattern. This behavior is labeled as an interactive dynamic 
between a functional configuration of the physical design elements and potential 
users’ perceived opportunities inside the space.  
Ghavampour et al (2017)’s behavioural description matches Keyvanfar et al 
(2014)’s, in which people constantly interact and adapt to their environment; they 
are not merely passive recipients of their surroundings. Keyvanfar et al (2014) state 
that: (1) people react according to the current conditions, and (2) they anticipate 
future discomfort and they choose how to adapt based on previous experience. 
Additionally, Keyvanfar et al (2014) described that the adaptive behaviour can be 
understood based on: (1) users’ cultural and social dimensions, and (2) users’ atti-
tude and beliefs. 
This behaviour, as it is dynamic and adaptive, can be nudged—defined as “the soft 
intervention to induce others to select accurately based on indirect method not 
straightforward representation” (Choe et al, 2012, page 196). This nudge can use 
space attributes like furniture, windows and lighting to create a space plan and de-
sign as described by Cha & Kim (2018), seeking to optimize the space utilization 
and maximize satisfaction. 
Another variable described by Jayarajah et al (2014, November), is that whether a 
person is alone or is part of a group influences their behaviour and changes their 
movement, actions and preferences. Nevertheless, they state that detecting if an 
individual is part of a group or not is a challenge.

According to Ghavampour et al (2017), monitor behaviour is performed by data 
collection of frequency of use, types of passive and active behaviours, and move-

Additionally, to measure user experience Izaki & Helme (2014) say that it is im-
portant to understand three factors: (1) movements and networks, (2) visibility and 
space, and (3) behaviour and experience. First, movement and networks describe 
how users access, occupy, move and relate to the space, with organic paths instead 
of predictable and organized paths. Second, visibility and space, where spatial ex-
periences are kept within a visibility reach that helps create movement patterns & 
social interactions; for urban scales, visibility and movement interaction is explored 
by simulation of walking values to find the user’s perspective with most visible 
surfaces; and for systems thinking, when you change a node (a visibility path) in 
the network, it modifies the edges and moves every node until equilibrium. Last, 
behaviour and experience, in which behaviour is explored by observing, measuring 
frequency, locating groups and task length to estimate behaviours and creating 
links with the movement paths and visual networks.  
According to Izaki & Helme (2014), behavioural monitoring is to understand and 
anticipate behaviours at specific times, during a typical day, and worst case sce-

IMPLICATIONS FOR DESIGN PROCESS 
According to Izaki & Helme (2014), the link between building environment design 
and descriptions of the space, with occupation of users and inhabitants, is imag-
ined and represented from experience and speculation. 
After the co-creation phase, monitoring is a supplement to the traditional qualita-
tive and intuitive manual methods for data collection used in studies of urban pub-
lic spaces, as said by Nielsen et al (2014). 
Public spaces behaviour can be understood by occupancy monitoring of the space, 
exploring the seven layers described on fig. 4-20: (1) Validating function monitor-
ing WHAT people do. (2) Monitoring interaction dependent on HOW close people 
are, defining the most likable places and the contexts that can enhance interaction 
with people being closer. (3) Studying circulation flows and walkable paths de-
pending on WHERE people move. (4) And HOW MANY people actually occupy the 
space. Nevertheless, as presented by Izaki & Helme (2014), the complexity of spa-
tial relationships, human behavior, and experiences is difficult to capture with cur-
rent software. 
As described by Cha & Kim (2018), understanding the behaviour with up to date 
information can help designers make better decisions, leading to more efficient 
use of the space and enhanced user satisfaction, because, as Izaki & Helme (2014) 
state, design can be based on circulation strategies and movement structures.

appropriately used or underutilized, they get reconfigured and incorporate new design

(2012).

Andreasen et al (2015) describe conceptualization methodologies and definitions. 
For them, conceptualization is the creation process by manipulation and combina-
tion of ideas into something previously unknown or unseen. Therefore, a concept 
is a detailed enough proposal to justify it as a good answer to an intention with a 
high probability of achievement and success. A concept needs to be drawn and de-
scribed so that others can understand it, and build on top of it (fig. 5-1). 
Andreasen et al (2015) define the encapsulation design model which is a sequence 
with: task, concept, design, business and use, as shown on fig. 5-2, circumscribing 
the exploration model at the task stage. The exploration model boosts the search 
and clarification of an idea pallet of conditions for development and success. The 
process is a loop that can be initiated in any order with five feed chains: need in-
terpretation, problem formulation, task perception and formulation, technology 
and idea elements, perception of ideas; as shown on fig. 5-3. 
The perception of ideas presents good solutions. The need interpretation defines a 
need (an unsatisfactory situation perceived by the designer, among specific users 
and activities) from user’s values, use activities, and situation identity. The problem 
statement defines the perceived problem. The task perception and formulation 
comes from the identity and range that were decided. And, the technology and idea 
elements allow the analysis of materialization and feature possibilities. This explo-
ration model gives a context understanding to create the goal formulation and en-
ter the concept synthesis model, shown in fig. 5-4 and described by Andreasen et 
al (2015). The goal formulation presents the criteria and requirements for the ex-
pected ideal solution. 
Andreasen et al (2015) define ideas as the initial thoughts, principles as how things

The rapid prototyping needs to accomplish certain characteristics in materials and

consequences. Additionally, parametric design can simplify the detailing and con-
struction of the rapid prototyping.

7.Discussions & 
Conclusions 
==PAGE:116==
ϭϬϱ 
 
 
A method based on lively planning that introduces user input can improve social 
sustainability into the built environment. Co-creation activities complemented with 
behavioral monitoring give a practical and efficient way to collect user input to cre-
ate a concept that enhances human based design. 
The concept synthesis is a baseline for design decision making and provides spatial 
geometries that reinforce the definition of an appropriate configuration. After the 
monitoring, behaviour can be designated for nudging change or for maximizing 
current behaviour. 
Once the validation process gives a positive outcome with a final experience at the 
space, users will increase social sustainability by improving social cohesion, acces-
sibility, equity, quality of life, culture, and space ownership.

IMPLICATIONS FOR THE DESIGN PROCESS 
Users need to connect and have a sense of belonging with the environment, in 
agreement with Cilliers et al (2015).  Co-creation changes roles of users and de-
signers, allowing needs and problems to come directly from the source. Function 
and ideas must come straight from the people who would use and create a unique 
space. 
Co-creation activities should be complemented with behavioural monitoring that 
proposes interaction distance, circulation, and occupancy to understand the real 
function needed at the space; because what people say needs to be complemented 
with people’s actions. Additionally, occupancy data need a parametric design of the 
interaction groups that lead to a new outcome: spatial geometries. 
A proper concept synthesis reinforces a good concept with a thorough and detailed 
ideation process. This concept synthesis allows an informed decision-making 
method based on users’ input.

Finally, the validation allows user’s feedback on the final experience, and a cycle 
improvement based on direct interviews to users and a new behavioural monitoring 
at the space. 
IMPLICATIONS FOR DOKK1’S LIVING ROOM 
The process was done on top of the sound, visual and movement dimensions to 
improve social sustainability as the final outcome. These dimensions are validated, 
because the sound and visual experiences are the most important features for us-
ers, with the silent experience and view out of the window as described in the Co-
creation Chapter.  
The co-creation phase gives an important amount of information, and it is well 
complemented by the behavioural monitoring, which reinforces the space attrac-
tors, defined as the newspapers and the view. This tendency increases support to 
the actual behaviour of the space, as described on the Monitoring Chapter. 
The silence feature has a major impact on the visitors. It also creates a contradic-
tion on the functionality that boosts the multiple zones. On the one hand, there is 
a want for silence, and on the other, there is a wish to socialize. Because of the 
proposed multifunction, there are two different concepts, and the space should be 
properly divided to allow the different functions to co-exist, as described on the 
Conceptualization Chapter. 
The design phase led towards a reconfiguration of the space and a sound absorb-
ing newspaper-stand (Design Chapter). Nevertheless, as described on the Co-
creation Chapter, the prototype did not have a big effect but the furniture redistri-
bution did.  
This reconfiguration effect changed the functional distribution, as described on the 
Monitoring Chapter, but it increased an unforeseen and ignored problem: the 
space becomes more welcoming for students, reason why the process has to cycle 
back to the co-creation phase and dig more into the student interest in relation 
with the visitors’ needs.

PROJECT LIMITATIONS 
This design process and study case had to be done within a five-month timeframe 
that included site location, planning, and the entire study case. At the same time, 
the only lively scale tried was the place-making/site-scale, as defined by the study 
case.  
This resulted in a reduce timeframe for each phase, with a negative impact in the 
co-creation phase, occupancy monitoring and ideation processes. 
Other limitations were the sensors’ set up, calibration and data collection, which 
had several delays that implied a reduction on the data quality and the monitoring 
time. Additionally, during the prototype building, both the budget and the availa-
bility of material and tools had an impact in the final design.

LEARNING REFLECTIONS 
Lively planning improves community and consequently social sustainability. By in-
cluding a participatory approach, design thinking needs to change. This participa-
tory design improves by complementing with behavioural monitoring, especially 
using technological sensors to do so. Nevertheless, it requires a learning process 
with the sensors’ installation, calibration and data collection of the sensors that is 
still ongoing.  
The conceptualisation process allows to integrate several sources ideas, including 
users’ input and creation of new solution spaces. Concretization and detailing of 
the concept can lead to a design, but a good final design requires a rapid proto-
typing to be validated. 
==PAGE:119==
ϭϬϴ 
 
Future projects can study the interactive process between the different designers 
involved, along with user psychologists for this integrated design process. 
Each phase can expand the research. The same design process can be explored 
with different lively- scales. The co-creation phase should be the next step with 
feedback interviews, to ensure the validation process, and to expand the needs and 
problems of the different visitor types. Future projects should have longer moni-
toring timeframes. For the ideation process, the number of ideas may increase.  
The design phase should also include several prototypes and models taking part of 
the co-designing process.

A human based approach should introduce user input as social innovation into a 
design process for the built environment. Social sustainability can be improved 
with a design process based on lively-planning and user input. 
There are two complementary ways to get user input: (1) collected straight from 
users and visitors with participation methods and co-creation activities, and (2) 
monitoring the space to understand behaviour using smart sensors for an accurate 
and simple process. 
The conceptualization stage is the perfect link between the compilation from the 
users’ input and the informed decision-making approach for the design. Engineers 
are instinctively drawn to fundament decisions into their specific expertise, using 
the concept synthesis baseline allows human-based decisions for all designers, 
even though this threatens the power structures of the existing design process of 
the built environment. 
Finally, the design process needs prototypes to be validated by the user, increasing 
co-creation in the process, and ensures the optimization of the outcome. 
At Dokk1’s Living Room, the design process lead to a sound absorbing newspaper 
stand and a reconfiguration of the space. Nevertheless, the student groups’ prob-
lem was maximized, and that requires a second cycle on the process. Therefore, it 
reinforces the cycling process, because changes in the public space may have un-
expected consequences.

CONCEPT DESCRITION 
The space attractors are comfortable and cosy furniture and a new element. The 
furniture should allow people to comfortably: read media, sit and walk. 
SPACE ATTRACTORS 
The main stakeholders directly involved are visitors that come to read media. 
The stakeholders that are indirectly involved are the visitors that come to social-
ize and the Library’s visitors that are attracted to the space.  
STAKEHOLDERS 
The space should create a context where visitors can individually sit to facilitate 
comfortable reading with an environment that allows a cosy and public experi-
ence. 
GOAL 
To overcome noise and crowdedness, the goal is to create a relaxed, cosy and 
silent space facilitating that visitors come to read.
