Abstract
In this research project, dynamic computer simulations were used to analyze the spatial
distribution of daylight and thermal comfort in Danish residential buildings. The study
focused on understanding the factors that inﬂuence thermal and daylight comfort and
discomfort, including general and local discomfort, overheating, high temperatures, illu-
minance levels, and adaptive approaches. The simulations were conducted using the IDA
ICE software, and data were extracted and analyzed using Python.
The research aimed to identify the optimal room typologies, orientations, and shading
types for dwellings in Denmark, focusing on balancing overheating and occupant comfort.
The study considered two diﬀerent cities with diﬀerent climates: Copenhagen and Rome.
The results of the simulations were analyzed using correlation matrix plots to determine
the optimal and suboptimal combinations based on the lowest and highest values of spatial
discomfort.
Overall, the results of the study suggest that the interplay between thermal comfort and
daylight is complex and dependent on various factors.
The optimal room typologies,
orientations, and shading types may vary based on the location and climate. By under-
standing these relationships, it may be possible to design energy-eﬃcient buildings that
eﬀectively balance thermal comfort and daylight, creating dwellings that are comfortable
for occupants and sustainable for the environment.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Current State
Climate change is a pressing issue that has been addressed through various agreements
and regulations. Several studies, many of which focus on building-related issues, have em-
phasised the need for change because buildings, including indirect emissions from power
generation, contribute to 28% of global energy emissions. In 2018, CO2 emissions from
buildings reached a record high of 9.6 Gt, marking the second consecutive year of increases.
While there was a trend of improvement from 2013 to 2018, largely due to a decrease in the
carbon intensity of power generation, the growing demand for electricity-powered devices
and the limited supply of carbon-free energy has led to a resurgence in building-related
emissions. Some European countries experienced record-high temperatures in 2018, such
as Spain and certain regions of Portugal, which led to an increased need for air condition-
ing and higher power usage. The data from recent years suggests that the deep energy
retroﬁtting of existing buildings must be signiﬁcantly accelerated, not only in emerging
countries but also in developed economies, as energy market developments are not keeping
up with the rapid growth of emerging economies.[1]
Buildings signiﬁcantly contribute to global and EU carbon emissions, accounting for about
40% of total ﬁnal energy consumption. In recent years, various initiatives have been imple-
mented to decrease the carbon footprint of buildings through energy-saving measures and
improvements in eﬃciency. [2] To meet its climate and energy goals and transition to clean
energy, the European Union has established policies and directives related to the building
sector that align with the Paris Agreement. The EU has set a goal of halving energy
consumption by 2050 compared to 2005, with a minimum energy eﬃciency of 32.5% by
2030. Additionally, buildings are a key focus in the EU’s decarbonisation eﬀorts, as energy
consumption for heating and cooling in buildings accounts for all such energy consumption
and 50% of the ﬁnal energy consumption. The European Commission Recommendation
2019/786 emphasises the importance of "energy eﬃciency ﬁrst" and the consideration of
renewable energy sources in building design and retroﬁt. [3]
The signiﬁcant changes needed to address climate change and reduce our reliance on fossil
fuels include modernising buildings. However, it is vital to consider the potential hidden
challenges of these changes in addition to the apparent beneﬁts. What are the possible
unintended consequences of the approaches taken in recent decades to address these issues?
It is necessary to modernise buildings to make a meaningful impact on climate change and
reduce our reliance on fossil fuels. However, these changes are also potential challenges
in addition to the obvious beneﬁts. The type of building materials used, the quality of

2.1
Legal background in Denmark
Overheating, thermal comfort, and illuminance/daylight regulations are essential consid-
erations in the design and operation of buildings, as they can signiﬁcantly impact the
health, well-being and productivity of building occupants. Overheating in buildings is a
growing concern, as high indoor temperatures can negatively impact human health and
well-being and increase energy consumption and greenhouse gas emissions. Several le-
gal frameworks and standards have been developed to regulate indoor temperatures in
buildings to address this issue.
In the European Union (EU), the Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD) sets
out the requirements for the energy performance of buildings, including the requirement
to avoid overheating in buildings. According to the EPBD, Member States should ensure
that the indoor temperature in buildings does not exceed the level at which thermal dis-
comfort occurs. The EPBD also requires Member States to establish national benchmarks
for indoor temperatures in buildings, considering the local climate and the intended use
of the building. [21] In Denmark, a relevant legal framework to set this benchmark is the
Danish Building Regulations (Bygningsreglementet), which sets out the requirements for
the indoor climate in buildings. According to the regulations, indoor temperatures should
be "constructed such that, under their intended operational conditions and at levels ap-
propriate for the human activities to be carried out in them" (6.2(1)). The regulations also
specify that buildings should be designed and constructed so that the indoor temperature
does not exceed 25◦C in summer and does not fall below 20◦C in winter. [22]
Illuminance/daylight regulations are also important considerations in the design and oper-
ation of buildings, as they can signiﬁcantly impact the health, well-being and productivity
of building occupants.
To address this issue, several legal frameworks and standards
have been developed to regulate illuminance/daylight in buildings. The Danish standard
DS/EN 17037:2018, "Daylighting - Requirements and recommendations for the design of
buildings", is a standard that guides the design and evaluation of daylighting in buildings.
The standard speciﬁes the minimum daylight levels that should be achieved in buildings,
depending on the room type and intended use. It also recommends that buildings be de-
signed and constructed so that the natural lighting in rooms and common areas is suﬃcient
to meet the occupants’ needs. This can be achieved using various daylighting techniques,
such as skylights, light tubes, and windows.
While the standard does not speciﬁcally
address spatial comfort in residential buildings, it provides useful guidance on the design
and evaluation of daylighting, which can help to ensure that residential buildings have an

2.2
Historical background
Various indicators can be used for thermal evaluation. These usually consider the radiant
temperature of the air and surrounding surfaces as a mathematical relationship or diagram.
Regarding the air temperature, relative humidity and air velocity, Leusden and Freymark
developed the comfort diagram in 1951. [24][25]
Houghton’s and Yaglou’s model considers air temperature, ambient surface temperature,
air velocity and relative humidity. For normal clothing and uncovered upper body humans,
they developed their scale to measure thermal comfort, the ﬁrst of its kind in 1923, which
proposed the eﬀective temperature (ET) index to give a model for measuring how air
temperature and humidity aﬀect comfort concerning each other. [26]
A more relevant example is the Ashrea 55 model, which was ﬁrst published in 1966 and
has been regularly modernised and updated since 2004.
This model considers several
important environmental factors, such as operative temperature, humidity, radiation, air
speed, and human factors, like clothing and activity. [27]
Probably the most signiﬁcant impact on the ﬁeld is connected to Fanger, who created
the comfort diagrams, which he published in the ASHRAE magazine in 1967 with the
title "Calculation of thermal comfort: introduction of a basic comfort equation". Povl Ole
Fanger was an outspoken critic of the approaches present at that time, as he felt those
did not take enough inputs into account in the calculations and were too simplistic for the
depth of the subject. [28]
The comfort level of individuals resting in winter, summer, and winter situations is deter-
mined by three levels of activity and four clothing categories. Other factors are considered
constants, and thermal comfort is always depicted as a function of two variables in a
standard coordinate system. There are a total of 28 diagrams, which have been further
divided into three subgroups. [28][29]
The air temperature is the same as the mean radiant temperature of the surfaces for two
of these groups. The third group, which has 12 diagrams, is our subject’s most commonly
used set.
These diagrams show the mean radiant temperature plotted against the air
temperature, but their applicability is limited because the air’s relative humidity is always
set at 50%. To account for changes in factors that are typically considered constants,
Fanger has created an additional 15 auxiliary diagrams. All of the comfort charts allow
for a 5% margin of discomfort.

An improved version of the comfort diagrams, the Predicted Mean Value (PMV) and the
Percentage Probability of Discomfort (PPD) are also the work of Fanger, who deﬁned
relationships to quantify them. This work was published in 1970 as Thermal Comfort:
Analysis and applications in environmental engineering. In this publication, he describes
how to calculate the PMV index, which incorporates the eﬀect of the thermal comfort
elements (air temperature, air velocity, mean radiant temperature, humidity, clothing,
and activity) into a score on a seven-point scale. This score was used to assess the overall
thermal comfort conditions of the human body. This method uses the state of air, the
eﬀects of the surroundings and even human factors, hence its complexity. While creating
the PMV index, Fanger also used the results of Nevins and McNall’s laboratory and ﬁeld
measurements, carried out in 1967, in which 20 subjects were tested at four diﬀerent
levels of activity per person, where skin temperature and sweat production were tested to
determine comfort. [31][32]
2.3
Importance
People have always strived to ensure good thermal comfort in their buildings. However,
every climate and season brings its own set of circumstances, with a potential for extreme
weather conditions and constant climate changes. The external conditions and the use
or required functions of our buildings can also vary with speciﬁc requirements. These
requirements vary from person to person, providing diﬀerent thermal comfort levels for
each individual. Winter and summer thermal comfort requirements are diﬀerent. There
are several attempts to deﬁne overheating, discomfort and generally the feeling of warmth
in areas. Overheating has a vague deﬁnition, and it is a condition that can vary from
person to person. There are certain studies and suggestions, though, trying to deﬁne it.
Arup and Cibse put warm to 25 ◦C, hot to 28 ◦C and heat stress to 35 ◦C, which has
serious eﬀects on the body. [33]
The main factors of thermal comfort are the air temperature, the degree and speed of
temperature variation and the temperature of surfaces, i.e. walls, ﬂoors, ceilings, windows,
and radiators. The airﬂow speed and the air’s humidity also inﬂuence thermal comfort.
Thermal comfort cannot, therefore, be characterised by a single parameter but by several
factors that must be considered simultaneously. [33][34]

2.4
Causes of Overheating
Many reasons can be responsible for an overheated living space and spatial discomfort in
buildings. Some of these may be overheating due to human action. These are internal
gains which can occur as a result of occupant behaviour.
• Occupant behaviour
Of all the devices that occupants can control, open windows have the greatest role
and impact on air temperature.
The use of windows varies from person to per-
son. An open window is closely related to thermal comfort, indoor and outdoor
temperature. The higher the proportion of open windows, the warmer the subjects
feel. The reasons given for closed windows were either to avoid draughts or for the
consideration of others.
The results suggest that windows are widely used by building occupants, much more
than shutters, whether inside or outside. However, window use is largely used as a
mitigating eﬀect and not as a preventive eﬀect, which is an essential circumstance
and raises the question of whether window opening can be considered a preventive
eﬀect directly related to overheating and discomfort since its dependence on human
factors makes it unpredictable. [34]
• Air pollution
As urbanisation continues, air quality is deteriorating, aﬀecting more and more peo-
ple. Improving comfort by opening windows in larger cities raises the question: can
thermal comfort be improved only at the expense of air quality? A study in urban ar-
eas also found that indoor carbon dioxide emissions were signiﬁcantly reduced when
windows were opened and ventilated, exceeding the standard, but indoor humidity
also fell below the minimum required by the standard. It was also observed that
signiﬁcant air quality improvements resulted from mechanical rather than natural
ventilation, but that post-ventilation humidiﬁcation was required in both cases. [35]
• Various equipment usage in the indoor area
Occupants’ use of heat-emitting devices can also inﬂuence internal temperature. As
an example, if an individual uses more heat-emitting kitchen equipment than others
in a small kitchen or apartment, this could contribute signiﬁcantly to the overall
heat level and aﬀect the comfort of the space. It is important to note that normal
equipment usage alone should not cause overheating, but it can exacerbate an already
overheated indoor climate.
Alternatively, external gains may occur due to the design of the building, resulting in the
following:
• Urban heat island eﬀect
Currently, more than 4 billion people live in urban areas, accounting for over half
of the global population. The United Nations estimates that this proportion will
rise to two-thirds by 2050, equivalent to 7 billion people in terms of population at
that time. This proportion is expected to be even higher in Scandinavian countries
(Figure 2.2), representing an increase of approximately 75% compared to today. This
rapid urbanisation is driven by access to improved sanitation, drinking water, clean
fuels, and a better quality of life. [36]

Daylight and spatial comfort
The research "Summertime overheating in dwellings in temperate climates" suggests that
summertime overheating in dwellings is a growing problem in temperate climates, par-
ticularly in ﬂats and small dwellings in cities. The issue is caused by a combination of
construction and refurbishment methods and climate change leading to more frequent and
severe heat waves. The elderly and vulnerable populations are particularly aﬀected by the
problem. Passive cooling measures, such as external shading and ventilation, can prevent
or mitigate the problem in most cases. The research also highlights the importance of
considering the occupants’ needs and providing intuitive, accessible, and useable devices
for controlling ventilation and shading. The use of air conditioning should be a last resort,
as it can contribute to the problem of global warming and may need to be more cost-
eﬀective in the long term. The research suggests that addressing the issue of summertime
overheating in dwellings is important in order to improve the health and well-being of
occupants and reduce the risk of heat-related illness and death. [40]
The study "Mutual Interaction of Daylight and Overheating in the Attic Space in Summer
Time" aimed to optimize the shading of transparent surfaces and heat accumulation in an
attic space located in an old building in order to create comfortable interior conditions. The
attic was renovated and expanded to create additional classroom space, but the original
truss was raised by 1.2 meters to create a space with suﬃcient headroom. Continuous
strips of vertical windows and skylights were installed to allow for natural light, which also
contributed to overheating in the summer. The study found that shading the windows
and skylights resulted in a 29% decrease in maximum temperature and an 8% decrease in
average temperature in the attic, but also resulted in a signiﬁcant decrease in illuminance
levels. The study also found that incorporating night ventilation, or leaving the windows
open overnight to allow for air exchange, helped to decrease the maximum temperature
further and improve the overall comfort of the space. However, the study concluded that
to achieve comfortable interior conditions in the attic fully, environmental technology such
as air conditioning or ventilation may be necessary in addition to structural design and
window shading. The study also discussed the importance of considering the speciﬁc needs

The article "Investigation of occupied/unoccupied period on thermal comfort in Guangzhou"
discusses the impact of window-wall ratio (WWR%) on thermal comfort and energy per-

unoccupied periods and considers the region’s conditions.
In terms of the occupied period, the study found that a WWR of 30% is most suitable for
achieving thermal comfort. Still, this value may vary depending on the consideration of
the thermal environment during the unoccupied period. During the summer months, high
WWR% (greater than 0.7) performed better in thermal comfort than low WWR% (less
than 0.3), with only a 3-degree Celsius diﬀerence in maximum indoor temperature and a
signiﬁcant reduction in minimum temperature during the unoccupied period. However,
high WWR% can lead to overheating in the winter, which can be mitigated through
shading measures.
The study also introduced the concept of a "cool storage zone," in which the cooling
capacity accumulated during the unoccupied period can improve thermal comfort during
the occupied period of the following day. A cool storage zone was found to increase with
higher WWR%, but it mainly impacted the initial stage of the occupied period and had
little eﬀect on peak temperature at noon. This is due to the sensible heat storage inside
the room, which prevents the cooling capacity from being used eﬀectively.
Overall, the study suggests that buildings with high WWR% have the potential to achieve
high levels of thermal comfort in Guangzhou, but it is important to consider the use of
shading measures and cool storage strategies to optimize energy performance and comfort.

2.6
Health eﬀects
The report "Risks to health, well-being and productivity from overheating in buildings"
notes that overheating in buildings can have various adverse eﬀects on health, including
heat stroke, dehydration, and respiratory problems. It can also aﬀect productivity and
cognitive function and negatively impact mental health, including causing stress, anxiety,
and sleep disturbance. The report cites research indicating that overheating in buildings
can harm vulnerable groups, such as the elderly, young children, and people with certain
medical conditions. It also notes that the risk of overheating will likely increase in the
future due to climate change, as the frequency and severity of heat waves are expected to
increase. The paper also recommends several measures to reduce the risk of overheating in
buildings, including improving the thermal performance of buildings, increasing the use of
natural ventilation, and promoting the use of shading and other passive cooling techniques.
It also recommends that the UK government and other relevant organizations take nu-
merous steps to address the risks of overheating, including updating building regulations
and guidance, conducting further research, and raising awareness of the issue.[44]
The report "Research into overheating in new homes" is a study conducted by the UK gov-
ernment’s Department for Business, Energy and Industrial Strategy (BEIS) on the risks
of overheating in new homes in the UK. The report notes that overheating in homes can
negatively aﬀect health and well-being, including heat stroke, dehydration, and respiratory
problems. It can also have psychological eﬀects, such as causing stress, anxiety, and sleep
disturbance. It also notes that overheating can negatively impact productivity, making
it diﬃcult for people to concentrate and perform tasks eﬀectively. The report discusses
several factors that can contribute to overheating in homes, including the poor thermal
performance of buildings, lack of natural ventilation, and high levels of solar gain. It rec-
ommends several measures to reduce the risk of overheating in homes, including improving
the thermal performance of buildings and increasing the use of natural ventilation.[45]
The two reports both discuss that certain groups, such as the elderly, young children,
and people with certain medical conditions, are particularly vulnerable to the negative
eﬀects of overheating in buildings. The "Research into overheating in new homes" report
notes that these groups are more likely to experience adverse health eﬀects, such as heat
stroke, dehydration, and respiratory problems. They are also more likely to experience
negative impacts on mental health, such as stress, anxiety, and sleep disturbance.
At
the same time, the other source similarly notes that these groups are more vulnerable
to the harmful eﬀects of overheating. It also cites research that indicates a more likely
to experience negative impacts on health, productivity, and cognitive function due to
overheating.

2.7
Conclusion from literature review
Overall, the literature study has highlighted the importance of thermal comfort and day-
light conditions in the design and operation of buildings. Legal requirements, standards,
and guidelines have been developed to ensure that the indoor environment in buildings is
comfortable and healthy for the occupants. Indicators such as the Leusden and Freymark
comfort diagram, Houghton’s and Yaglou’s model, and the eﬀective temperature (ET)
index can be used to assess thermal comfort and identify potential issues in a building.
In addition to thermal comfort, the literature review has also demonstrated the positive
impacts of natural light on the health and well-being of building occupants. Natural light
can improve mood, increase productivity, and enhance visual comfort. The use of energy-
eﬃcient artiﬁcial lighting systems can also improve the overall daylight conditions and
visual amenities of a building.
It is vital to consider both thermal comfort and daylight conditions in the design and
operation of buildings to create comfortable and healthy indoor environments. By follow-
ing legal requirements and standards, utilizing strategies such as orienting and shading
buildings to maximize the amount of natural light, utilizing passive cooling techniques,
and implementing energy-eﬃcient heating, ventilation, and air conditioning systems, we
can create environments that support the well-being of the occupants.

3.1.1
Limitations
Although the IDA ICE 5 beta version oﬀered exciting opportunities that were important
for developing this Thesis, it also presented challenges I encountered while using it. Some
of these diﬃculties included:
• After a grid simulation, an h5 ﬁle containing all relevant information is generated.
However, it is not possible to deactivate this function after the simulation has been
completed. The only way to eﬀectively "turn it oﬀ" is to add manually and then
remove planes. If no planes were present before the simulation, the program would
calculate results for all grid points rather than just the preset occupant points.
• If a parametric run is conducted using the new zone model and resulting in the
generation of h5 ﬁles, changing the model (and planes) and saving the model before
rerunning the parametric run will not result in new data for the modiﬁed grid.
Instead, the previous, outdated data will be produced. Running a basic simulation
ﬁrst is necessary to obtain further data, thereby updating the mathematical model.
• If a window’s "x" value is included in a parametric run and set to a value larger
than the original window size, the simulation may crash since IDA only allows the
window to be enlarged on the left side (in the negative "x" direction). While the
new window size may ﬁt within the wall numerically, the program may not place it
in the middle of the wall.

3.5.2
Hourly diﬀerences
The number of hours with temperature deviations aﬀecting comfort level was analysed
by calculating the number of hours in a year when above-average temperatures and day-
light diﬀerences occurred between the two selected points.(Section 3.4.1) An alternative
approach was not chosen, which would involve calculating the temperature diﬀerences at
each hour and averaging them for each simulation. It is because it may not accurately
reﬂect the conditions within a room, particularly in cases where a room with a higher
average temperature variation within the zone may not necessarily be more uncomfort-
able than another room with a lower average temperature variation with extremely high
peaks. Instead, the operative temperature was ﬁltered for a diﬀerence of +3 ◦C/-3 ◦C,
following recommendations and ﬁndings from the literature review and other sources. [42]
[70] It is important to note that while it is normal for the temperature within a zone to
ﬂuctuate, it is practically impossible to maintain it at the same temperature at all points.
These temperature diﬀerences often occur vertically due to the direction of heat ﬂow.
While the vertical temperature diﬀerence was not explicitly considered in this analysis,
it was measured within the same plane 0.6 m above the ground level. As such, the hor-

3.5.3
Annual Sunlight Exposure
The DS 17037 standard deﬁnition of sunlight exposure is as follows: "Sum of the time
(hours) (e.g. on a given day) within a given period during which the sun is above the
actual horizon with a cloudless sky, which may be limited by permanent obstructions such
as mountains, buildings, etc."[23]
Among the requirements of the DS 17037, the standard only covers the recommendation
for Daily Sunlight Exposure. In my case, however, it was essential to approach the problem
from the other side by investigating the values of Annual Sunlight Exposure that cause
discomfort.
[23] LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design) provides a
solution to address this issue.
LEED is a widely recognized building certiﬁcation system that promotes the construction
of healthy, eﬃcient, and cost-eﬀective green buildings.
Its certiﬁcation program oﬀers
a framework for achieving sustainability through requirements and goals. According to
LEED, Annual Sunlight Exposure is "the percentage of the regularly occupied ﬂoor area
that is overlit". Following the requirements stated in the standard, by calculating the per-
formance indicator "Annual Sunlight Exposure", I gained a clear picture of the percentage
of the area on the plane 0.6 m over the ﬂoor with at least 250 hours of illuminance over
1000 lux in the simulated year. [71]
3.5.4
Glare calculation
The standard includes Four indications on the requirements for daylight simulation, three
of which, namely "Daylight Provision", "Assessment for view out", and "Exposure to sun-
light", approach the requirements from a comfort perspective, while the fourth, Protection
from glare is the only one towards discomfort. Therefore, it is important to consider the
related calculations when evaluating the results. For the glare calculation, I examined how
big proportion of the plane is exposed to at least 3000 lux Illuminance for at least 5% of
the measured time (one year). The results of the calculations can be seen in Section 4.4.3
[23]
3.5.5
Useful daylight calculation
In my research on the subject, I also looked at the BREEAM certiﬁcation requirements,
which, like the primary, American-based LEED, include a concept that can be applied to
Illuminance comfort calculations. The criteria Mardaljevic and Nabil proposed initially in
2005 can ultimately be used as a shading performance indicator. The calculation is based
on the objective of ﬁltering out daylight while leaving not-too-dark and not-too-bright
values. The search range is narrowed down to values between 100 and 2000 lux. Values
below 100 lux are considered too dark, and values above 2000 lux are too bright and
prone to glare. In my case, it shows the percentage of the year when UDI was achieved.
Although the time during which a valuable light source is present in the room is always
an important indicator when characterizing a space, the calculation does not specify a
prescribed % value for the ratio of the area under consideration where the criterion should
meet. Since the subject of my Thesis is mainly based on the accurate detection of extreme
cases, I did not consider this factor in my subsequent calculations. [72]

A parallel coordinate plot, also known as a parallel plot or parallel axes plot [73], is a
graphical method for visualizing multi-dimensional data. It involves plotting each variable
on its vertical axis, with the axes parallel to one another and the data points represented as
lines connecting the variables. [74] This allows the viewer to see patterns and correlations
between the variables and identify outliers or unusual points in the data.
However, parallel coordinate plots can become diﬃcult to interpret when the number of
variables or the size of the data set becomes large. It is because the lines representing the
data points can overlap or become densely packed, making it diﬃcult to discern individual

most eﬀective way to visualize the data.

individual variables on the data (3.10). Other plots or analysis methods, such as regression
analysis or decision trees, may be more suitable if this is a desired feature.

3.6.3
Correlation matrix plot
I used a correlation matrix plot to best interpret and process the results. The pairwise
correlation shows the relationship between variables in the numerical form of a correlation
matrix, which can be plotted in a matrix table. Each cell of the table shows the correlation
between two variables on the "y" and "x" axis. The procedure is used, among other things,
to summarize data for more advanced analyses and as a diagnostic tool. The method is
also excellent for discovering speciﬁc patterns in our data and examining whether and in
what direction the correlation between individual elementary data is signiﬁcant. [77]
In the ﬁeld of statistics, many solutions to the problem of correlation equivalence have been
developed, the three most prominent being the work of Pearson, Kendall, and Spearman.
Pearson, credited with the Pearson Correlation, answers whether there is a relationship
between two or more quantitative variables and, if so, how close it is. The joint variation of
the variables is characterized by the Pearson correlation coeﬃcient, which can result in a
correlation coeﬃcient between -1 and 1. This number assumes the absence of a relationship
between the inputs when approaching 0 and their presence when close to -1 and 1. The
sign of the numbers indicates the presence of a negative or positive relationship.[78]
Kendall’s calculation of tau in 1975 means comparing each pair of ranks within the two
distributions under consideration. Also non-parametric, the natural ordering of the rank
data of some variables is examined in the calculation, whereby if the rank order is both
variables are identical (monotonically increasing/decreasing), then we speak of a concor-
dant pair. We speak of a discordant pair if the rank order is inverted for such a pair of
observations. In the case of equality, the pair is neither concordant nor discordant. [79]
Unlike the others, Spearman’s process is a ranking procedure where the calculation is not
done with values but with the number of lines. If the user wants to calculate a correlation,
but the normality condition is not met, Pearson’s "r" value may give a biased result. In
such a case, we can use a ranking procedure that is not sensitive to the normality condition.

5.1
Research questions
1. How does the shape of the room impact spatial comfort in terms of natural light and
temperature in both Copenhagen and Rome?
Based on the results, the shape of the room has a signiﬁcant impact on spatial com-
fort in terms of natural light and views in both Copenhagen and Rome. In general,
square-shaped rooms tend to have lower standard deviation values regarding opera-
tive temperature and illuminance, indicating that they may be more comfortable in
natural light and spatial thermal comfort than L-shaped rooms. In terms of room
geometry within the square shape category, the size of the room impacts spatial
comfort, with larger rooms tending to have higher levels of discomfort. However,
it is worth noting that these results may vary depending on the speciﬁc combina-
tion of window-to-ﬂoor ratio (WFR), shading type, and orientation that is used.
Additionally, the research paper suggests that L-shaped rooms may be more prone
to discomfort due to temperature ﬂuctuations compared to square-shaped rooms,
particularly in the case of facade windows. Overall, the shape of the room can have
a signiﬁcant impact on spatial comfort, and it is important to carefully consider the
speciﬁc room geometry when designing for optimal comfort.
2. How does the orientation of the room impact spatial comfort in terms of natural
light and temperature in both cities?
According to the ﬁndings, the orientation of a room can signiﬁcantly impact spatial
comfort in terms of natural light and temperature. In both Copenhagen and Rome,
it was found that external shading and a higher window-to-ﬂoor ratio (WFR%)
generally resulted in lower annual sunlight exposure and lower spatial discomfort in
all room typologies. On the other hand, combinations with internal shading and a
higher WFR% tended to have higher annual sunlight exposure and higher spatial
discomfort.
In terms of speciﬁc orientations, the research found that north and west orientations
generally resulted in lower annual sunlight exposure and lower spatial discomfort in
both cities. East orientations generally resulted in higher annual sunlight exposure
and higher spatial discomfort, while south orientations had a more mixed eﬀect
depending on the speciﬁc room typology and other factors such as shading type and
WFR%. Regarding operative temperature, the research found that north and east
orientations generally resulted in lower standard deviation values and lower spatial

deviation values and higher spatial discomfort, while west orientations had a more
mixed eﬀect depending on the speciﬁc room typology and other factors such as
shading type and WFR%.
Overall, the orientation of a room can signiﬁcantly impact natural light levels and
temperature and should be carefully considered in the design process to achieve
optimal comfort levels for the occupants.
3. What are the main factors that should be considered when designing new projects
in Copenhagen and Rome to achieve optimal levels of spatial comfort, and how do
these factors impact the comfort of the space?
Several other factors should be considered when deciding the most suitable com-
binations of room typologies, orientations, and shading types for new projects in
Copenhagen and Rome. These include the window-to-ﬂoor ratio (WFR%), the type
of window (facade or roof), and the type of shading (internal or external).
The
WFR% can signiﬁcantly impact the amount of natural light and heat entering the
space. In general, a higher WFR% will allow for more natural light and heat to
enter the room, and the appropriate choice of other combinations could lead to bet-
ter spatial comfort. The type of shading (internal or external) can also signiﬁcantly
impact spatial comfort. External shading can block direct sunlight and reduce glare
but may also block views. Internal shading, such as blinds or curtains, can also block
direct sunlight and reduce glare but may not be as eﬀective at reducing heat gain
and loss.
In addition to these factors, the research found that in the case of facade windows,
the temperature in Copenhagen was better than in Rome, while in the case of roof
windows, Rome was better than Copenhagen in terms of spatial comfort. Overall,
facade windows generally provide better spatial comfort than roof windows in both
cities. The room size was also found to be inversely proportional to spatial comfort.
These factors should be considered when choosing combinations of room typologies,
orientations, and shading types for new projects in these cities.
Overall, it is essential to consider all of these factors when designing new projects in
Copenhagen and Rome to achieve optimal levels of spatial comfort. It may also be
necessary to consider additional factors, such as the speciﬁc climatic conditions of
the location and the intended use of the space, to make informed design decisions.

speciﬁc to Copenhagen and Rome?

the location’s speciﬁc climate conditions and latitudes. The results of the study show

cities, and it is important to consider the speciﬁc conditions of a location when de-
ciding which combinations of room typologies, orientations, shading types and other
factors to use in new projects. The study found that the optimal combinations for
spatial comfort in Copenhagen and Rome diﬀered signiﬁcantly, suggesting that the
factors inﬂuencing spatial comfort can vary signiﬁcantly depending on location. In
other cities, therefore, with diﬀerent climates and latitudes, it is necessary to conduct
similar simulations and analyses that take into account the speciﬁc conditions of the
location. This may involve reevaluating the input parameters and determining new

more comfortable in terms of temperature ﬂuctuation compared to roof windows and
internal shading. However, it is worth noting that these ﬁndings may vary depending

by occupants and the energy eﬃciency of a building. By using advanced modelling
techniques, it is possible to examine the potential impacts of diﬀerent design choices
and to identify the optimal combinations of input parameters for a particular loca-
tion. This can improve the design and planning of new projects and optimize the
performance of existing and new buildings.

5.3
Limitations of the research
There are certain limitations and gaps in the research presented in this paper. One limi-
tation is that the study only considers two cities, Copenhagen and Rome, and it is unclear
how the results would generalize to other locations with diﬀerent climates and latitudes.
Additionally, the study only considers four room typologies, which may not fully capture
the complexity and diversity of building design and occupant behaviour.
Another limitation is that the study only considers a limited number of input parameters.
Other factors, such as the type of insulation, lighting systems and the presence of me-
chanical ventilation systems, could also impact spatial comfort and energy eﬃciency. This
study does not consider occupant behaviour in the simulations. It is essential to consider
how occupants use and interact with their environments to predict and optimize spatial
comfort accurately. Factors such as opening and closing windows, adjusting blinds and
shades, and using fans or other ventilation systems can signiﬁcantly impact the thermal
and daylight comfort of a space.
Finally, the study relies on computer simulations and data analytics to predict and op-
timize spatial comfort and energy eﬃciency, which may only partially capture the com-
plexity and variability of real-world conditions. Further research is needed to validate and
reﬁne these modelling techniques and to consider a broader range of input parameters and
building types.

Conclusion
In conclusion, the research project aimed to understand the factors inﬂuencing thermal
and daylight comfort and discomfort in Danish residential buildings using dynamic com-
puter simulations. The results of the study demonstrated that room size, orientation, and
window-to-ﬂoor ratio (WFR%) signiﬁcantly impact spatial comfort and that the use of
facade windows with external shading generally results in improved spatial comfort com-
pared to the use of roof windows with internal shading. Additionally, the study found that
combining these factors has a distinct impact on spatial comfort compared to consider-
ing each factor independently, highlighting the importance of considering their combined
eﬀect. The research also revealed that spatial comfort could not be solely determined
by average illuminance and overheating but rather as a combination of several factors.
Additionally, the ﬁndings indicated that facade windows generally provide better spatial
comfort than roof windows in Copenhagen and Rome and that room size is inversely pro-
portional to spatial comfort. In the case of facade windows, the spatial comfort in Copen-
hagen was greater than that in Rome, while in the case of roof windows, Rome was more
beneﬁcial than Copenhagen. These results inform the design of energy-eﬃcient buildings
that eﬀectively balance thermal comfort and daylight and may help design dwellings that
are both comfortable for occupants and sustainable for the environment. Future research
could examine the impact of other variables, such as occupant behaviour and building
materials, on spatial comfort in various climates, as well as the potential for utilizing ad-
vanced technology to optimize the design of room typologies for enhanced energy eﬃciency
and comfort.
