Abstract
Actively lubricated bearings has been an interest of research for the last
decades, as this allows for control of the dynamical characteristics of the
bearing, reducing vibrations and increasing stability. However, the mathe-
matical models needed when designing a controllable bearing, have yet to
be perfected and validated by comparison to experimental results. Existing
experimental results are primarily for each individual component isolated
from the system. This thesis focuses on the characterization of a control-
lable tilting pad journal bearing as a whole system, and then inspects the
performance of each component under diﬀerent operational conditions. Ex-
periments were conducted both pseudo-statically and dynamically in order
to characterize the oil-ﬂow, active forces and frequency response functions
for the entire system, as well as each component in the system. The acquired
results can be used to improve and reﬁne the mathematical models, in order
to design the optimal controller for tilting pad journal bearings.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In the industry, a continuous demand for increased productivity and eﬃciency
is crucial to remain competitive and preserve the environment.
In order
to achieve so, the demand on the performance of rotating machinery keeps
getting higher. This is where the application of active ﬂuid ﬁlm bearings
comes into play.
The idea behind an active bearing is to control the oil ﬁlm and hereby
dynamic properties of the bearing. By applying this to rotating machinery
a wide range of advantages can be achieved such as:
 Increased stability: Higher RPM can be achieved and hereby an
increase in productivity
 Decreased vibrations: Machinery components are less likely to suﬀer
from fatigue and hereby break, which results in an increase in eﬃciency
 Decreased friction: Less wear and waste of energy again resulting in
an increase in eﬃciency
In order to design the optimal bearing, an accurate model is crucial.
Through the last century, a big eﬀort has been made to reﬁne the mathe-
matical model representing the dynamics of ﬂuid ﬁlm bearings. However, in
order to verify the mathematical models, experiments have to be performed.
When dealing with active bearings, not only an accurate model of the bearing
is required, but also of the actuator.

1.2
Active Lubrication Introduction
It is a known fact that the dynamic behaviour of a hydrodynamic bearing
changes under diﬀerent operational conditions. This change in dynamic be-
haviour would be highly desirable to be able to control.
The bearing design of focus in this report is shown in ﬁgure 1.1, and aims
to control the dynamic behaviour by injecting high pressurized oil into the
clearance between bearing and rotor. The design was ﬁrst proposed by Santos
[10] in 1994. The idea is to use a high response servovalve to controls the
injection of the pressurised oil. By using this design, not only the dynamic
characteristics of the bearing are changed, but the oil injection can be used
to create vibration cancelling forces.

(TPJB). In 2010 a test rig was developed at the Technical University of
Denmark with the aim of conclusively determining the characteristics of such
a bearing. The bearing pads used in this test rig are shown in ﬁgure 1.1,

and consist of the tilting pad, pivot, injection nozzle and a force transducer

In order to design a controller for an active bearing, an accurate model of
only the bearing itself is insuﬃcient. As the servovalve serves as the actuator
in this conﬁguration, an accurate model of the servovalve is also required.
The aim of this report is to achieve a complete characterization of the
servovalve ”in-situ”. This will be achieved through a series of experiments,
where the oil ﬂow, transfer function and active control forces generated will
be determined under diﬀerent operational conditions.
A huge amount of
experimental data is generated with the aim of building a database for vali-
dation of actively-lubricated TPJB mathematical models.
1.3
Project Description
This project is entitled ”Active Control Force in Fluid Film Bearings - An
Experimental Parameter Study”. It is aimed to improve the understanding
of the diﬀerent parameters inﬂuencing the Active Lubricated Bearing (ALB)
through a series of experiments. This means that the project itself is very
experimentally orientated in order to achieve results to form the foundation
for validating mathematical models and designing the optimal bearing.
A chapter is dedicated to present the test rig used in the experiments.
Furthermore a user manual is developed in order to ease and in turn improve
future experimental work on the test rig by other students.
The goal of this project is to achieve a complete characterization of a
MOOG D765 servovalve which serves as the actuator in the test set up. This
is done through a series of experiments both with and without the rotor
mounted in the bearing. The oil ﬂow, active ﬂuid ﬁlm forces and frequency
response functions are determined for diﬀerent input signals, supply pres-
sures, rotational speeds and bearing eccentricities.

2.1.2
Test Rig Conﬁgurations
The test rig is equipped with various tools, making it possible to simulate
diﬀerent bearing conﬁgurations.
Free/Fixed Eccentricity
The eccentricity e is deﬁned as the distance between the rotor center and
the bearing center.
The eccentricity is usually expressed in terms of the
eccentricity ratio, ε = e/cb, where cb is the bearing clearance.
The test rig can be set up in two diﬀerent ways, ﬁgure 2.3. In conﬁgura-
tion 1, the rotor is free to move in the vertical direction (free eccentricity),
allowing one to measure the dynamical behaviour of the system. In conﬁg-
uration 2, the rotor is ﬁxed in the vertical direction (constant eccentricity),
allowing one to measure the oil ﬁlm forces.

2.1.3
Equipment
Figure 2.4 illustrates the test rig equipment. The rotor is driven by an electric
motor 1, which is equipped with a frequency transformer 2, allowing one to
change the rotational speed. The actual rotational speed is measured by an
encoder 3.
The high pressure oil is initially sent through an oil ﬁlter 4 to avoid
contamination.
Then it is sent to the servo valve 5, which controls the
direction of the ﬂow. The ﬂow to the upper and lower pad is measured by
two ﬂowmeters 6 and 7. The pressure and temperature in the injection hole
of the pads are measured by pressure sensors 8.
The system can be excited through a shaker 9. The amplitude of the
shaker is controlled by the ampliﬁer 10, and the resulting input force is
measured by the force transducer 11 and its signal conditioner 12.
The eccentricity of the rotor in the bearing can be ﬁxed by the ε-adjustment
screw 13, and the oil-generated forces is measured by the force transducer
14.
The response of the system is measured by two accelerometers 15 and
16 and their signal conditioners 17 and 18. Furthermore, the response is
measured by a displacement probe 19 and two other displacement sensors
20. Everything is connected via dSPACE CP1104 controller panel 21 to a
computer.
The oil system is a combination of a low and a high pressure system. The
oil is stored in a 20 litre tank 22. The temperature and oil level is read on
the side of the tank 23. The high pressure system is driven by the pump
and motor assembly 24. The pressure can be controlled on the knob 25, and
the actual supply pressure is read on the gauge 26. The low pressure system
assures that the the rotor is always lubricated and is driven by the pump and
motor assembly 27. The entire rotor-bearing assembly is mounted on an oil
pan. A suction line 28 is connected to the oil pan, assuring that excess oil is
transferred back to the tank.

Simulink and dSPACE Model
Simulink is a graphical programming environment for modelling, simulating
and analysing dynamic systems. It is developed by MathWorks, who are
also responsible for MatLab, which is used for the data processing. Simulink
is used in corporation with a dSPACE DS1104 board to acquire data from
multiple channels simultaneously. Simulink has been used to set up the data
acquisition program, see ﬁgure 2.6. For larger image, see user manual in
Appendix B.
Figure 2.6: Simulink Model
The green part of the Simulink model is used to acquire data and the blue
part is used to generate output signals. The model is set up to be able to
generate the following output signals: a constant value, a sweeping value, a
constant frequency sinusoidal and a linear chirp sinusoidal. The amplitude of
all outputs as well as shifting the outputs can be also be controlled. dSPACE-
ControlDesk has been used as the user interface and to control the Simulink
model, see ﬁgure 2.7.

Bearing Clearance
A very important dimension of a ﬂuid ﬁlm bearing is the clearance. The
inﬂuence on the pressure distribution and hereby forces will be presented in
section 4.1.
A common way of designing ﬂuid ﬁlm bearings is to produce a circular
bearing of radius RS, and then cut out a middle-section of that circle. This
design is called geometric pre-load. Figure 2.11 shows the principle of pre-
load of a bearing. Note how the center of the bearing/pad radius RS and the
center of the rotor R does not coincide for pre-load.
As mentioned, the bearing is produced to have a radius RS, and the rotor
a radius of R. The diﬀerence between these is deﬁned as the production
clearance cp:
cp = RS −R
(2.3.2)
The actual clearance of the bearing cb is deﬁned from the total height of the
bearing hb (distance between pads) and the radius of the rotor R as:
cb = hb/2 −R
(2.3.3)
The pre-load factor of the bearing mb is then deﬁned from the ratio between
the bearing clearance cb and the production clearance cp as:
mb = 1 −cb/cp
(2.3.4)
The pre-load factor of the bearing in the test rig is varied by changing the
height of the bearing hb. This is done by using diﬀerent spacing plates located
between upper and lower bearing housing, see ﬁgure 2.11.

Servovalve Characterization
When dealing with a controllable TPJB, it is extremely important to know
the behaviour of the actuator itself. In this design, the actuator is a servo-
valve, capable of directing the high pressure oil through an injection hole in
the middle of either the upper or lower pad.
This chapter presents the basic theory behind the dynamics of the servo-
valve. A series of experiments without the rotor mounted has been conducted
to validate the theory. This should assure that the measured performance of
the servovalve is not aﬀected by the dynamics of the journal.
3.1
Working Principle
The servovalve used in the experimental setup is a D765 series from MOOG.
It is a quite complicated device consisting of multiple components, working
together to direct the ﬂow as desired. In this section a short introduction to
the working principle of the D765 series servovalve is presented, see Figure
3.1. A good reference for obtaining general information on hydraulic control
systems is Merritt [5].

The MOOG D765 servovalve diﬀers from old fashioned servovalves, in
that the mechanical feedback has been accompanied by an electric feedback.
The servovalve is a 2-stage design consisting of pilot stage (torque motor and
ﬂapper valve) and a main stage (spool). A command signal (−10 V to 10 V)
is sent to the integrated controller, which operates the torque motor. The
torque displaces the ﬂapper valve creating a pressure diﬀerence across the
drive area of the spool and aﬀecting its movement. The position of the spool
is measured by a position transducer. The signal is fed back and compared
to the command signal. The controller then drives the pilot stage until the
error between command and feedback signal is zero.
The displacement of the spool is what directs the ﬂow. A command signal
of −10 V corresponds to full valve opening from Ps ⇒Pl and corresponding
Pu ⇒Pt. Reversely, 10 V corresponds to full valve opening Ps ⇒Pu and
corresponding Pl ⇒Pt. 0 V corresponds to the spool at centred position. To
avoid pressure shocks, the spool is usually under-lapped, meaning that with
the spool at centred position, a leakage ﬂow would be detectable to both load
ports (Pu and Pl).

Experiments
To characterize the behaviour of the spool dynamics, a series of experiments

Set Up
The experiments were performed with the oil-collector mounted in the bear-

Conclusions
It was found that the ﬂow through the servovalve is linear dependent on the
spool position around the leakage point. The slope of ﬂow curves Kv was seen
to increase as the supply pressure increases. Furthermore, the input voltage
resulting in equal ﬂow to both lines seems to be dependent on the supply
pressure. This means that when using the servovalve for control purposes,
the control signal should be placed around this leakage voltage.
The dynamics of the spool seem to follow the manufacturers guidelines. It
was found that the phase lag between input signal and spool position changes
signiﬁcantly under diﬀerent conditions. In general a command signal of 0.7 V
and a high supply pressure results in the smallest phase lag. When designing
a controller, this eﬀect has to be taken into account.

Active Control Forces
The main purpose of the controllable TPJB is to be able to generate vibration
cancelling forces. For these to work in a rotor system, the forces need to be
able to reverse quite quickly. In Chapter 3 it was established that a phase
lag between input signal and spool position manifests itself as the frequency
increases. To be able to use the forces generated by the oil injected through
the nozzle in the pads, a thorough examination of the frequency response of
the entire system is required.
This chapter builds on to the results obtained the previous chapter. The
theory behind the active lubricated bearing is presented, and a series of
experiments are conducted to validate the theory. Furthermore, experiments
are performed to completely characterize the frequency response from input
signal to active control force.
4.1
Pseudo-Static Testing
As a counterpart for the ﬂow characterization in section 3.2, the ﬂow and
generated forces have been found by sweeping through the input voltage. Of
course, this time, the rotor has been mounted, and the experiments have
not only been performed with various supply pressures, but also for diﬀerent
rotational speeds and eccentricities.
When considering the ﬂow, the same tendency as in the previous chapter
is to be expected. However, as the pressure on the load side of the servovalve
is now > 0 bar, one should expect to see a ﬂow from the load port/bearing
to the tank (back-ﬂow), when these are connected.
When considering the forces generated, the predictions are not as straight
forward, which is why the theory dealing with the pressure distribution in
ﬂuid ﬁlm bearings are reviewed.

Theory - The Modiﬁed Reynolds Equation
In order to understand the behaviour of the bearing, the Modiﬁed Reynolds
Equation (MRE) is examined.
The Modiﬁed Reynolds Equation builds on the classical Reynolds Equa-
tion, ﬁrst derived by Reynolds [9] in 1886. It has been expanded to include
the pressure generated from the injection nozzles in the pads by Santos and
Russo [11] in 1998. The Reynolds Equation is essentially a continuity equa-
tion, that can be used to calculate the pressure distribution in ﬂuid ﬁlm
bearings.
The equation will not be fully derived here, as it is out of scope for this
report. However, the foundations for deriving the equation will be examined
in order to understand the diﬀerent parameters inﬂuence on the pressure
distribution as well as the generated forces. The following derivation heavily
builds on the derivation presented in [11].

Theory - Transfer Functions
A transfer function in the frequency domain is essentially the same as a
frequency response. This means that they are obtained in the same fashion
as explained in section 3.3.2.
The relationship between input signal uv and active control force F can
be represented by the transfer function:
G(f) = F(f)
uv(f)
(4.2.1)
This transfer function is the combined transfer function for the entire
system dynamics. Mathematically, a transfer function can be stated as a
combination of multiple transfer functions as:
G(f) = G1(f) · G2(f) · G3(f) · ... · Gn(f)
(4.2.2)
This can be applied to the system by splitting it up into smaller systems
that can be analysed separately. In this project, the system has been split

4.3
Conclusions
It was found that the tendency shown by the Modiﬁed Reynolds Equation
resembles the one achieved through the experimental work. However, the
magnitude of the predicted forces are too high, and needs to be addressed
before using it for control design.
A complex ﬂow pattern was found in the injection nozzle as the rotational
speed increases. This also needs to be investigated further, and implemented
in the mathematical model.
It was found that the system can indeed be represented by a combination
of transfer functions for all subsystems.
The resonant frequency between
input signal and generated force was found to be less than 70 Hz, which
agrees with the works presented in [13].

Chapter 5
Conclusion
During this master thesis, an actively lubricated tilting pad journal bearing
has been characterized experimentally.
This has been done by analysing
the bearing as a whole system, and then inspecting the performance of each
component under diﬀerent operational conditions.
From the excessive experimental work performed during this thesis, it was
found that the ﬂow through the servovalve can be represented by a linearised
ﬁrst order approximation. The ﬂow-gain constant for spool position Kv and
the leakage ﬂow has been characterized for several operational conditions.
The dynamics of the servovalve is mainly inﬂuenced by the supply pres-
sure and input signal amplitude. The performance was found to be best at
an input amplitude of 0.7 V and highest possible supply pressure.
The Modiﬁed Reynolds Equation provides a useful tool in predicting the
pressure distribution and generated forces. However, the model needs to be
reﬁned in order to reﬂect the experimental results.
The phenomenon of back-ﬂow was proven as well as quantiﬁed during
the experimental work. The maximum back-ﬂow was found to occur at 3000
RPM. However, this needs further investigation to be exactly predicted by a
mathematical model.
The concept of representing the entire system by a combination of transfer
functions for subsystems was proven right, enabling one to detect the ”weak”
components in the system.
Through the system a phase lag builds up, leading to a 90◦phase lag for
an input frequency of just 70 Hz. For the servovalve to be used for control
purposes, this eﬀect has to be taken into account.
Conclusively, the experimental results obtained during this thesis has
built a useful and comprehensive amount of experimental data. This can be
used to validate and extend the usage of the modiﬁed Reynolds equation for
active lubrication and design of optimal controllers for tilting pad journal
bearings.

Bearing Clearance
A very important dimension of a ﬂuid ﬁlm bearing is the clearance. A com-
mon way of designing ﬂuid ﬁlm bearings is to produce a circular bearing of
radius RS, and then cut out a middle-section of that circle. This design is
called geometric pre-load. Figure 1.4 shows the principle of pre-load of a
bearing. Note how the center of the bearing/pad radius RS and the center
of the rotor R does not coincide for pre-load.
As mentioned, the bearing is produced to have a radius RS, and the rotor
a radius of R. The diﬀerence between these is deﬁned as the production
clearance cp:
cp = RS −R
(1.1.1)
The actual clearance of the bearing cb is deﬁned from the total height of the
bearing hb (distance between pads) and the radius of the rotor R as:
cb = hb/2 −R
(1.1.2)
The pre-load factor of the bearing mb is then deﬁned from the ratio between
the bearing clearance cb and the production clearance cp as:
mb = 1 −cb/cp
(1.1.3)
The pre-load factor of the bearing in the test rig is varied by changing the
height of the bearing hb. This is done by using diﬀerent spacing plates located
between upper and lower bearing housing, see ﬁgure 1.4.

Drive Control
In this use case, an induction motor controller is developed 
with the DS1104. The DS1104 is well suited for drive control: 
while in this use case the slave DSP system calculates the 
PWM signals, the PowerPC’s calculation power supports 
convenient simulation. With an experiment software such 
as ControlDesk® Next Generation (p. 120), measuring and 
parameterization can be performed during run-time.
Determining Values
One of the board‘s incremental encoder interfaces picks up 
the encoder signal of the motor, while two A/D converters 
are required to analyze the motor currents. The controller 
board calculates the control algorithm on the basis of the 
measured values and determines the corresponding pulse 
width modulation (PWM). The three-phase PWM signals are 
generated on the board‘s DSP subsystem and determine the 
converter‘s output voltage and frequency.

Saving the project
Ther are dependencies between files in a project which means that you can not just copy a 
project from one directory or computer to another.
First save the project + Experiment then take a Backup of the Project + Experiment. Now you 
have a zip file with the ControlDesk files that you can open on any one of the computers from 
Open Project + Experiment from Backup.   
-- End of example. Return to the Workshop.
Connecting more than one parameter to one instrument
This may have to be done later depending on how you have designed your Simulink file. 
1.) Create an Instrument and connect the parameter of the first Simulink block that you would 
like to change to the instrument. 
2.) Connect the second parameter from the second Simulink block by dragging it with the right 
mouse button instead of the left. You should then get a question of how you would like the 
second connection to be configured. Choose Connect as additional Write Variable.
Online Help
The online help in ControlDesk is called Help Desk and is a Explorer based help browser, it is 
simple to use. There is much functionality in ControlDesk which is not described in this docu-
ment, so please use the help if you need some special function of ControlDesk.
TIP
At the different stages in the control design process, e.g., parameter identification, feedback, 
feedforward design, etc. try to work with different Experiments and different Simulink files. 
When things start going wrong in the later stages is it essential that there exists a functioning 
Simulink file with an Experiment for an earlier design stage. This simplifies debugging and 
thereby decreases your design effort and time.

Two stage Servovalves
The D765 Series flow control
servovalves are throttle valves
for 3- and preferably 4-way appli-
cations.
The design of these valves is
based on the wellknown D761
Series. The mechanical feedback
has been replaced by an electric
feedback with a position trans-
ducer. An integrated electronics
closes the position loop for the
spool.
These valves are suitable for elec-
trohydraulic position, velocity,
pressure or force control systems
with extremely high dynamic re-
sponse requirements.
Principle of operation
An electric command signal (flow
rate set point) is applied to the
integrated position controller
which drives the pilot stage. The
thereby deflected nozzle flapper
system produces a pressure dif-
ference across the drive areas of
the spool and effects its move-
ment.
The position transducer (LVDT)
which is excited via an oscillator
measures the position of the
spool (actual value, position vol-
tage). This signal is then demo-
dulated and fed back to the con-
troller where it is compared with
the command signal. The con-
troller drives the pilot stage until
the error between command sig-
nal and feedback signal will be
zero. Thus the position of the
spool is proportional to the elec-
tric command signal.

System structure
The eddyNCDT 3010 is a compact, single-
channel system consisting of an eddy cur-
rent sensor, a sensor connecting cable and 
an amplifier electronics (signal conditioning 
unit). The sensors are factory-calibrated for 
aluminium (non-ferromagnetic) or steel St37 
(ferromagnetic). Using three-point linearisati-
on, the user can also compensate on site for 
other materials.
Temperature compensation
The eddyNCDT 3010 series is suitable in a wide 
temperature range. In the case of fluctuating 
ambient temperatures a stable output signal is 
very important for reliable measurements. Due 
to a patented temperature compensation me-
thod the eddyNCDT series 3010 offers a unique 
thermal stability, which no other system can 
offer.
The eddyNCDT 3010 is designed for industrial 
use in production plants, for machine control 
and for measuring and testing during in-pro-
cess quality assurance.
Synchronisation
If several channels of series 3010 operate si-
multaneously close to one another, a mutual 
interference is possible because of slight dif-
ferences in the oscillator frequencies.
This can be avoided by synchronisation. Two 
SMC connectors at the electronic box, one for 
oscillator signal output (sync out) and one for 
input (sync in) are standard equipment. The 
electronics operate independently as long as 
they are not interconnected. lf connected to-
gether, they automatically switch to synchro-
nised operation and are controlled by the first 
electronics (master). Any quantity of units can 
be synchronised by serial connection.

The VHZ gearwheel flow meter measures the flow by a volumetric 
principle, in which a pair of gearwheels is moved proportional to the 
flow rate. The movement of the gearwheels is measured through 
the enclosing housing wall by a sensor. The devices are suitable for

such as soaps, pasts, emulsions etc. which have a non-abrasive 
character.  Because  of  the  volumetric  functioning  principle,  the 
devices are almost completely independent of viscosity.

Handling and operation
Installation
The VHZ flow measurement device can be installed anywhere in 
the pipework system. A run-in section is not required. The direction 
of flow may be freely chosen. 
It should be ensured that no dirt particles (thread cutting swarf) can 
get into the flow space, as this could cause the blockage of the 
gearwheels.  It may therefore be  necessary to  install  filters up-
stream of the flow measurement device (mesh size 30 µm).
Note
The metering range end value can be programmed by the user via 
"teaching". Requirement for programmability must be stated when 
ordering, otherwise the device cannot be programmed. The ECI-1 
device  configurator  with  associated  software  is  available  as  a 
convenient option for programming all parameters by PC, and for 
adjustment.
The teaching option is not available for the pulse output version.
Operation and programming
The teaching process can be carried out by the user as follows:
●
The flow rate to be set is applied to the device.
●
Apply an impulse of at least 0.5 seconds and max. 2 seconds 
duration to pin 2 (e.g. via a bridge to the supply voltage or a 
pulse from the PLC), in order to accept the measured value.
●
When the teaching is complete, pin 2 should be connected to 
0 V, so as to prevent unintended programming.
The  devices  have  a  yellow  LED  which  flashes  during  the 
programming  pulse.  During  operation,  the  LED  serves  as  an 
indicator of operating voltage (for analog output) or of switching 
status (for frequency or pulse output).
In order to avoid the need to transit to an undesired operating 
status during the teach-in, the device can be provided ex-works 
with a teach-offset. The teach-offset point is added to the currently 
measured value before saving. The offset point can be positive or 
negative.
Example: The end of the metering range should be set to 80 %. 
However, only 60 % can be achieved without problem. In this case, 
the device would be ordered with a "teach-offset" of +20 %.. At a 
flow rate of 60  % in the process, teaching would then store a value 
of 80  %.
There are many more parameters which can be programmed by 
the ECI-1 device configurator if necessary.

Amplifier Circuit
Fig. 2 shows a schematic of the amplifier circuit. The connec-
tor wiring for sensor and signal output resp. power supply can 
be gathered from Fig. 3. Temperature output can be obtained 
from any piezoresistive pressure sensor with constant current 
excitation (Types 4065/67, 4090/91 and 4085). 
The tempreature signal is obtained from the thermal resistance 
change of the piezoresistive Wheatstone bridge. There is no 
need for a temperature sensor. The temperature signal must 
be calibrated at the works.
Calibration of the Amplifier
Standard pressure output is UA = 0 ... 10 V or IA = 4 ... 20 mA 
for FS. Smaller pressure ranges are available on request, e.g. 
UA = 0 ... 5 V or IA = 4 ... 12 mA for FS. 
Temperature compensation, signal linearization, and coarse 
adjustment of zero and range are provided by resistors mount-
ed in standardizing  plugs Type 4958A0. The resistor values 
may readily calculated or read from tables. When ordering a 
replacement pressure sensor for the amplifier Type 4618A, a 
new standardizing plug will be included in the delivery.
Zero fine and range fine are adjusted by internal potentio-
meters. Zero can be readjusted after opening the screw cap.
For replacing the pressure sensor, the standardizing plug must 
be also replaced. The values for the sensitivity and the zero 
of the sensor are given in the calibration sheet and can be 
adjusted with the built-in calibrator.
The temperature signal, however, can only be calibrated by 
Kistler.
Two additional internal potentiometers are available for adjust-
ing the limit switches. The switching levels may be adjusted 
with a variable pressure or with a voltage taken from the elec-
tronic circuit. With the cover of the amplifier housing open, a 
schematic of the electronics can be found inside which shows 
the positions of the potentiometers and slide switches.
Mounting
The amplifier housing Fig. 1 may be mounted to any support 
with M4 bolts. The aluminum housing is connected to ground 
in order to provide electrical shielding for the electronics.
