Abstract 
Disease outbreaks is a major threat to the aquaculture worldwide. Fish farms frequently witness the 
presence of multiple pathogens, exacerbating the recovery process and leading to high mortality rates. 
This threatens not only the fish welfare, but also the economic sustainability and food security.  
The dsRNA virus Piscine orthoreovirus 3 (PRV-3) and bacterial kidney disease (BKD) caused by the 
gram-positive Renibacterium salmoninarum (R. salmoninarum) bacterium, are two well-known threats 
to rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) and salmon in Europe. Despite being an old foe, little is known 
about the bacterium R. salmoninarum. This thesis aimed to evaluate the immune profile during early 
infection of PRV-3 and R. salmoninarum, respectively, by implementing high throughput qPCR 
technologies for simultaneous quantification of pathogen load and immune gene expression.  
From the broad range of immune genes investigated, the results showed overlapping host response 
between viral and bacterial infection and widely expression across different tissue samples. The immune 
genes irf1, ddx58, isg15, mx, rsad2, ifng, ifit5, cxcl10, ccl19 and socs1 was all found significantly 
upregulated at 2 wpc during both PRV-3 and R. salmoninarum infection. No common significantly 
downregulated genes were found in this study. During only PRV-3 infection, tlr3 and il12a was 
significantly upregulated, while il1β, jak1 and cd22 were found significantly downregulated. In 
response to a R. salmoninarum infection, tnf, gzma, saa, mmp9, tgfβ3, il8 and c3ar1 were all found 
upregulated at 2 wpc. The expression of cd8, IgM, tcrβ and gata3 was downregulated, indicating R. 
salmoninarum to possess immune suppressive capabilities. 
Additionally, the possibility of finding differentiator genes to distinguish between which pathogen is 
primarily affecting the host to improve treatment in fish farms was evaluated. The immune genes saa 
and tgfβ3 was highly expressed during R. salmoninarum infection, yet not expressed during PRV-3, 
indicating promising candidates as differentiator genes at 2 wpc.  
Further research is necessary to establish a complete immune profile timeline during the full course of 
the disease.

1. Introduction 
Most of the aquaculture production in Denmark focuses on rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) [68], 
and as the industry is expanding, outbreaks of pathogens become a major threat to not only the economic 
sustainability and food security, but to fish welfare in terms of preventing overfishing and maintaining 
natural fish stocks. Commonly, outbreaks in fish farms consists of multiple pathogens [55], making it 
extremely difficult for treatment and recovery. This leads to a high mortality rate in farms. Mortality 
rates which are yet to be replicated during animal trials and studies [32].  
Piscine orthoreovirus 3 (PRV-3) and bacterial kidney disease (BKD) caused by Renibacterium 
salmoninarum (R. salmoninarum) are two well-known threats to rainbow trout and salmon in Europe. 
Much is known about PRV-3, but despite being an old threat, very little is known about the bacterium 
R. salmoninarum. To establish an initial immune profile for rainbow trout during both a viral and 
bacterial infection, it is necessary to investigate a vast range of immune genes. As many aspects of the 
immune response against R. salmoninarum remains unknown, a broad range of potentially relevant 
immune genes, and not R. salmoninarum specific, are considered in this study. As different immune 
genes are expressed depending on whether the infection is viral or bacterial, there is the potential for 
finding differentiator genes.  
Like mammals, the immune system of teleost fish consists of two subsystems: the innate immune 
system and the adaptive immune system [2]. As a contrast to higher vertebrates, the immune response 
for fish is majorly dependent on the non-specific innate immune system [1]. The innate immune system 
is influenced by a healthy microbiota, and in contrast to fish in natural environments, fish in captivity 
do not develop a normal or protective microbiota. [28] 
The use of pathogen detection from genetic material for disease diagnostics, in both the field of 
veterinary and human diagnostics, is becoming increasingly utilised. The goal of this thesis is to 
implement high throughput qPCR technologies for simultaneous quantification of pathogen load and 
immune gene expression. Doing this will provide the opportunity in fish farms to determine with great 
likelihood, which pathogenic infection is primarily affecting the fish, and hereby potential treatment 
priority. From this, it is also desirable to continue research to establish an immune profile timeline, 
indicating different stages of infection, e.g., incubation, active replication, and latency.  
1.1. 
Diseases in aquaculture  
Outbreaks is one of the biggest threats to successful aquaculture. As a large number of fish is crowded 
together in a smaller area, fish are stressed and more susceptible to disease. In dense populations, such 
as fish farms, the diseases spread more easily. The development of disease and its severity is influenced 
by several variables connected to the host, the pathogen, and the environment [29]. Aquaculture is 
susceptible to bacterial, viral, and parasitic infections. Bacterial and viral infections are the biggest 
==PAGE:8==
Page 7 of 63 
 
threats due to them being highly infectious, and combined with a stressful environment, it often leads 
to high mortality. The control and management of potential disease outbreaks in fish farms, relies on 
infection prevention, optimal living conditions (e.g., population density, water quality) and only a few 
commercially available and approved antibiotics or vaccines. [29]  
1.1.1. Piscine orthoreovirus 
Piscine orthoreovirus (PRV) is a well-known viral disease affecting salmonid aquaculture worldwide. 
It was first discovered in 2010 in Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) [13]. PRV is a segmented, double-
stranded, non-enveloped, RNA virus, which belongs to the Reoviridae family [13].  
The genome of PRV is roughly 23.600 base pairs long and is composed of 10 RNA segments. These 
segments are classified into three size-based groups. The small segments (S1-S4) are roughly between 
1000 to 1300 base pairs, the medium segments (M1-M3) are between 2200 to 2400 base pairs, and 
lastly the large segments (L1-L3) are between 3900 and 4000 base pairs. So far, 13 open reading frames 
has been confirmed by studies, which encodes for a minimum of 11 proteins, 8 of them being structural 
components for the virus particle. The beforementioned S2, M1, L1 and L3 segments encodes for the 
inner capsid proteins: 1, 3, 2 and µ2. The other capsid proteins; 2, µ1, 3, and 1; are encoded by 
the segments S1, S4, M2 and L2. The remaining known proteins, which are non-structural proteins, are 
NS, µNS, and p13, and are encoded by the segments S1, S3 and M3. These are associated with 
replication and process in the infected host cell. PRV has an icosahedral structure [13] [15]. Figure 1 
shows the schematic representation of PRV. 
==PAGE:9==
Page 8 of 63 
 
 
Figure 1. Schematic representation of PRV. Important proteins, structural and not, are marked and presented. The 10 RNA

Three genotypes of PRV exists, respectively: PRV-1, PRV-2, and PRV-3. PRV-1 has been broadly 
found and studied in Atlantic salmon, Chinook salmon (Oncorhynchus tshawytscha) and coho salmon 
(Oncorhynchus kisutch) in both Europe and America. Only coho salmon in Japan has been detected 
with PRV-2, whereas PRV-3 has been heavily reported primarily in rainbow trout and brown trout 
(Salmo trutta) in Europe [12-13]. When studying the nucleotide alignments and amino acid sequence 
between the three genotypes, PRV-2 shares 80% and PRV-3 shares 89% identity to PRV-1. [12] The 
three genotypes of PRV have different abilities in regards of infecting and causing disease. PRV-1 is 
mainly associated with causing heart and skeletal muscle inflammation (HSMI), and PRV-2 causes 
erythrocytic inclusion body syndrome (EIBS). PRV-3 is associated with anaemia and heart 
inflammation. [12] The focus of this study is the genotype PRV-3.  
No vaccines are currently available for the protection against PRV, but studies have examined both a 
whole virus vaccine and a DNA vaccine, which demonstrated only partial protection against HSMI. 
[12] A major research point for protecting aquaculture is the potential strategy for successful 
immunization by cross-protection. Here, a low virulent virus variant is induced to cause a low-grade 
disease. Studies found successful cross-protection between the three genotypes, where a primary PRV-
3 infection completely blocked and protected against a PRV-1 infection. [13] 
1.1.1.1. 
PRV-3 
PRV-3 was first detected in 2013 in rainbow trout in Norway [30], but was not detected in Denmark 
until 2017, where an outbreak in a rainbow trout farm caused high mortality rate. [31] 
==PAGE:10==
Page 9 of 63 
 
During a study investigating rainbow trout in farms in Denmark, PRV-3 was found to have a very high 
prevalence [32]. In the examined farms, 72% were found to be positive for PRV-3, although few were 
present at very low levels. 100% of RAS farms tested positive, indicating that water recirculation 
increases the risk of being PRV-3 positive. On the contrary, only 50% of flow-through farms were 
found to be positive of the virus. Lastly, 87% of the grow-out facilities tested positive, compared to 
only 47% of the broodstock farms. While the presence of PRV-3 was high, only about 26% of the PRV-
3 positive farms were found to develop associated diseases. These affected farms were all RAS [32]. 
Transmission of PRV-3 occurs by horizontal transmission, either by contaminated water or direct 
contact with infected fish [32]. As the virus was detected in the majority of fish farms in Denmark, trade 
of asymptomatic infected fish and eggs contribute to the route of transmission [32]. The mechanism 
entry of the virus remains unknown, but replication functions by the cellular mechanisms of the 
erythrocytes. [33]  
The severity of the infection differs between hosts. The main clinical signs of infection are exhaustion, 
abnormal swimming pattern, and lethargic behaviour. [34] PRV-3 is known for causing anaemia and 
HSMI-like inflammation in the heart. Multiple studies found both PRV-1 and PRV-3 to induce a strong 
innate antiviral response when analysing the hosts red blood cells. Further, when performing expression 
analysis of common adaptive immune response genes, the results indicate both cellular and humoral 
responses are induced. A study in 2019 found that fish infected with either PRV-1 or PRV-3 produced 
a specific antibody against one of the outer capsid proteins, 1. This protein was then predicted to be 
the receptor-binding protein. [14] 
As PRV-3 is constantly being researched, more is learned about the immune response. Several immune 
genes have been confirmed to be positively correlated with the PRV-3 virus load. These are rsad2 
(viperin), mx, ifng and ddx58 (rigi). These genes correlate to some of the most widely reported virus-
induced genes and is associated with heavy antiviral activity in fish. [35] 
1.1.2. Bacterial kidney disease 
Bacterial kidney disease (BKD) is a significant infectious disease affecting salmonid aquaculture 
worldwide, especially Pacific salmonid aquaculture (e.g., rainbow trout). The infection is caused by the 
bacterium Renibacterium salmoninarum, and the inflammation varies from granulomatous to 
pyogranulomatous with a relatively low mortality rate. R. salmoninarum is an aerobic intracellular 
gram-positive bacterium, which is non-capsulated, non-motile and non-endospore forming [10]. Even 
though BKD is an old threat, first described in 1933, very little is known about R. salmoninarum and 
the disease itself. It is one of the relatively few gram-positive bacteria, which causes diseases in fish.  
Histologically, BKD is distinguished by the development of chronic inflammation in terms of 
granulomas and non-encapsulated granulomatous lesions and proliferation of epithelioid macrophages 
in the kidney, liver and spleen [10]. The renal kidney is affected by glomerulopathy or 
==PAGE:11==
Page 10 of 63 
 
glomerulonephritis. Other target organs include the heart, as infected fish potentially develop spongy 
and necrosis myocardium, and liver where hepatocytes separate and findings of fibrin within sinusoids 
during infection. [10] 
As R. salmoninarum is a slow growing pathogen, specific media has been developed for the cultivation 
of this bacterium, which significantly accelerates growth. This medium is called kidney disease medium 
(KDM), and includes KDM1, KDM2 and KDMC (with charcoal). The most popular method for 
isolating the bacterium is the medium KDM2, as bacterium cultivated on this produces smooth, shiny 
and creamy colonies [10]. KDM2 consists of 0.1% cysteine and 20% fetal bovine serum. 
1.1.2.1. 
Routes of infection and risk factors 
Transmission of R. salmoninarum can occur without intermediate host by vertical and horizontal 
transmission. Regarding vertical transmission, the bacterium has been found in coelomic fluid and will 
enter the egg yolk during fertilization. Elimination of the bacterium is therefore not possible by simple 
egg surface disinfections [10]. The entry of bacterium by horizontal routes includes gastrointestinal 
tracts and through eyes or potential skin lesions. R. salmoninarum cannot survive outside its host for 
longer than a week, therefore horizontal transmission via water is deemed limited, but not unfeasible. 
Previous studies of fish farms have found transmission of R. salmoninarum between cages, and even 
from farm to farm. A study from 2020 found evidence of the bacterium in low numbers being present 
even in externally healthy wild fish population. [10] 
There are several risk factors which studies have found contributing to outbreaks of R. salmoninarum. 
These risk factors include insufficient feeding, low levels of dissolved oxygen, pollution and the 
potential presence of harmful plankton. Stress due to temperature changes or transfer and handling, 
likewise, acts as contributing factors for outbreaks. Studies have found indication of stress risk factors 
to increase the mortality of the disease. [10] 
1.1.2.2. 
Clinical signs of infection and treatment 
BKD is a chronic condition, but as it is associated with relatively low levels of mortality, detecting the 
presence of the bacterium can be difficult. When infected by R. salmoninarum, external clinical signs 
can be noticeable. Infected fish display abnormal swimming behavior as they lose their equilibrium, 
and potential bulging of the eyes in form of unilateral or bilateral exophthalmia and corneal opacity. 
Further signs include ocular lesions, petechiae around fins, hemorrhages, blisters on the epidermis 
excreting hemorrhagic fluid, skin discoloration, swelling of the abdomen and following belly rash. [10] 
Internal clinical signs include swelling of kidney in particular, but also reported of heart, liver, and 
spleen with granulomatous lesions on the surfaces of these organs [10]. Haemorrhage in the internal 
organs and bloody fluid accumulations are also associated to BKD. In several species (e.g., Rainbow 
==PAGE:12==
Page 11 of 63 
 
trout, and Atlantic salmon) R. salmoninarum has been reported to invade the central nervous system 
(CNS) by the posterior ocular urea, resulting in brain lesions [10].  
There is currently no available vaccine against R. salmoninarum, which leads to the need for antibiotics. 
Some, such as erythromycin and enrofloxacin, have proved to be somewhat effective, but slow, with 
prolonged treatment being necessary. This prolonged usage of antibiotics has already led to the 
discovery of antibiotic resistant R. salmoninarum strains being reported [10]. 
1.1.2.3. 
Gram-positive bacteria versus gram-negative bacteria 
Most bacterial infections reported in fish are caused by gram-negative bacteria. An increase of gram-
positive bacteria affecting especially rainbow trout has been reported in recent years [61]. The 
difference between the two bacterial groups lies in the bacterial cell envelope. Gram-negative bacteria 
are enclosed by a thin peptidoglycan cell wall, which then is surrounded by an outer lipopolysaccharide 
membrane [62]. On the contrary, gram-positive bacteria lacks the lipopolysaccharide membrane, but 
instead has a much thicker peptidoglycan cell wall. Through this thick layer are threads of teichoic acids 
[62].  
Very little is known regarding teleost fish and their potentially different host response between gram-
negative and gram-positive bacteria. In mammals, gram-negative bacteria may be readily killed in the 
extracellular space by the compliment and antibodies [63]. In contrast, gram-positive bacteria require 
an elaborately organized host response consisting of intracellular killing by macrophages and 
neutrophils [63].  
Research revolving infection by gram-negative and gram-positive bacteria in mammals suggest a 
common host response of immune genes regardless of bacterial group; however, some studies found 
evidence of significantly higher expression of il1β, il6 and il8 in response to gram-positive bacteria 
[63], where gram-negative bacteria induced a higher amount of tnfα [64]. At current state, there is 
indication that gram-negative and gram-positive bacteria can potentially induce different host responses 
in teleost fish, but a lot remains unknown.  
In terms of treatment, gram-negative bacteria are generally more resistant to antibiotics due to their 
structure, often leading to significant mortality worldwide [65]. The antibiotic resistance is often caused 
by the hard-to-pass outer lipopolysaccharide membrane found in gram-negative bacteria. With gram-
positive bacteria lacking this extra layer of protection, they are more susceptible to antibiotics. [65] 
1.2. 
Immune system 
The innate immunity functions as the primary defence against pathogens in fish. It provides fast 
response against infection, however, does not provide long-term protection. The importance of the 
innate immunity in regards of pathogen entry is mainly caused by the inefficiency of the adaptive 
==PAGE:13==
Page 12 of 63 
 
immune response [1]. This inefficiency is limited antibody repertoire and slow lymphocyte 
proliferation, which both leads to delay in adaptive immune response [2]. 
Most of the primary and secondary lymphoid organs, except functioning bone marrow and lymph nodes, 
can also be found in fish. With the lack of these, there is strong potential for limited fully functional 
adaptive immune response [1]. In teleost fish, the crucial haemopoietic organ is the kidney. A broad 
spectrum of immune cells develops in the anterior kidney. Further, their lymphoid organs include 
spleen, thymus and scattered lymphoid cells present in mucosal tissues [2].  
Within the innate immunity, there is two subdivisions: cellular and humoral immune responses. The 
cellular immune response consists of physicals barriers such as mucus, epithelial cells, gills etc. These 
barriers are to prevent invasion of pathogens, however, if they are detected, specialised cells like 
macrophages and granulocytes will attempt to kill and digest the foreign pathogen [2]. The focus of this 
study lays mainly on the humoral immune response. The humoral immune response functions by 
employing a broad variety of proteins and glycoproteins. These proteins are capable of either inhibiting 
the growth of pathogens or destroying them.

transcription factors nf-κb, irf3 and irf7. tlr1/2/6 has the potential to recognise gram-positive bacteria. These

During a pathogen infection, pattern recognition receptors (PRR), such as toll like receptors (tlr), rigi 
and mda5, recognise the pathogen by distinguishing self from non-self-molecules within infected cells 
[22]. After recognition, various downstream signalling pathways are activated. mda5 and rigi (referred 
to as ddx58 in this study) activates MAVS (mitochondrial antiviral signalling protein), which is the 
pathway that triggers transduction signalling at the outer mitochondrial membrane. Toll like receptors 
activates the pathways, which senses the virus outside of the cell membrane and internally at lysosomes 
and endosomes [22]. As shown in figure 2, the pathways all lead to the activation of the transcription 
factor, nf-κb, which then leads to the expression of several interferons (IFN). The newly expressed type 
1 and III IFN activates the JAK-STAT pathway. This pathway leads to further expression of IFN-
stimulated genes (ISGs) and pro-inflammatory cytokines. 
1.2.1. Toll-like-receptors 
Toll-like-receptors plays a major role in mediating the immune response towards a wide range of 
pathogens and links the innate immunity with the adaptive one [4]. These small protein molecules can 
recognise the unique molecules of pathogens. These are known as pathogen-associated molecular 
patterns (PAMPs). By the recognition of PAMPs, TLRs stimulates an inflammatory response that 
activates the innate immunity [2]. Based on the localization, TLRs are classified into two subcategories: 
cell surface TLRs and intracellular TLRs. Cell surface TLRs will predominantly recognize 
microorganisms’ membrane components, such as lipids and proteins. As an example, tlr4 recognizes 
lipopolysaccharides, and tlr5 can recognize the flagellin of bacteria. The intracellular TLRs are focused 
on recognizing nucleic acids derived from pathogens, but also capable of recognising self-nucleic acids 
in an infection setting. As an example, tlr3 are capable of recognising double-stranded viral RNA and 
self RNAs attained from damaged cells. [7] 
1.2.2. Interferons 
Interferons are major effectors of cytokines and antiviral activity. Type 1 IFN are associated with the 
first line of defence against viral infection and is found to be a key factor in the immune response in 
fish. When compared to the classic type I found in mammals which consist of single exon IFN genes, 
fish have five exons and four intron genes [2][16]. Type 2 IFNs, also known as ifng, has likewise been 
confirmed in teleost fish.  
Type I IFN are activated by PAMPs and the beforementioned recognition of TLRs. As the TLRs 
activates the TRIF pathway, the induction of irf3 and irf7 stimulates the production of type 1 IFN as an 
immune response. ifng are activated by the Janus Kinase and Signal Transducer and Activator of 
Transcription (JAK-STAT) pathway.  
==PAGE:15==
Page 14 of 63 
 
After activation, they induce antiviral capability in cells, hereby inhibiting viral replication and 
obtaining defence against viral infection. Studies has confirmed that in a response to viral infection, fish 
cells secrete both ifnα and ifnβ [2]. Most studies have focused on type 1 IFNs in teleost fish and has 
found them to induce a range of IFN stimulated genes. These genes include rsad2, isg15 and mx. These 
IFN stimulated genes has all been linked to enhanced antiviral state [16]. ifng has been linked to a 
significant increase of both major histocompatibility complexes (mhcI and mhcII), indicating a valuable 
role regarding antigen presentation. Further, studies show induced gene expression of many immune 
genes (e.g., ISGs)) by both type 1 and 2 IFN, suggesting a cross-activation of the innate antiviral 
responses. [16] Lastly, ifng is associated with modulating inflammatory cytokines and chemokine 
expression.  
1.2.3. Cytokines and chemokines 
Cytokines are well-known as signalling proteins and important for inflammatory responses. Several 
studies have found the presence of all major cytokine families in fish. Especially critical cytokines such 
as interleukin-1β (il1β), il6, transforming growth factor-β (tgfβ), and tumour necrosis factor-α (tnfα) has 
been recognised in teleost fish. [1] Both il1β and tnfα are well studied and found to be proximal 
cytokines, which are initially released at the point of recognised infection. These enhance the secretion 
of distal cytokines, such as il6 and il8. ifng in fish is a strong inducer of proinflammatory cytokines 
such as il1β, il6, il12, and tnf. [1][16] 
il1β is one of the more well-studied cytokines in fish. The studies indicate that this cytokine is involved 
in the regulation of immune genes, lymphocyte activation and migration of leucocytes [17]. During an 
infection, one of the first expressed pro-inflammatory cytokines are il1β. This expression enables the 
immune system to respond quickly to an infection, resulting in the induction of a pro-inflammatory 
cascade response. [17]  
tnfα is a pro-inflammatory cytokine which is well-documented in serving a significant role in host 
response during infection. tnfα has a diverse range of responses, including induction of other cytokines, 
cell proliferation, differentiation, necrosis, and apoptosis [17]. As il1β, tnfα is one of the first expressed 
cytokines as a result from infection. In terms of anti-microbial response, tnfα can induce apoptosis, 
killing infected cells and inhibiting intracellular pathogen replication [17]. With this cytokine being a 
power anti-microbial response, studies have found several viruses evolving strategies to neutralise tnfα. 
This occurs by direct binding and inhibition. [17] 
tgfβ is a pleiotropic cytokine, meaning it regulates a vast spectrum of immune responses.  Studies has 
found it connected to regulation of cell development and survival of various leukocytes, e.g., dendritic 
cells, macrophages, and lymphocytes. tgfβ is well-studied in mammals but lack extensive investigation 
of its role in the immune system for teleost fish. The exact number of tgfβ in teleost fish is unknown, 
but at least 10 has been confirmed to be present in rainbow trout. These 10 include three tgfβ1, two 
==PAGE:16==
Page 15 of 63 
 
tgfβ2, four tgfβ3, and one tgfβ6 [50]. Recent research has demonstrated tgfβ1 showing strong immune 
depressing effects to suppress autoimmunity. In rainbow trout, tgfβ1 has proved to have both enhancing 
and suppressing effects of the respiratory burst activity. [17] [18] 
Chemokines are well-known for their regulation of development and homeostasis of the immune system 
[19]. The superfamily consists of roughly 40 different small cytokines which roles are to order immune 
cells to migrate to sites of infection. The superfamily is further divided into four subgroups: CXC, C, 
CC and CX3C. The subgroups are determined by the arrangement of the first two cysteine residues in 
their peptide structure [17]. il8 belongs to the CXC chemokine subfamily, and several studies has found 
evidence of it being a key factor in inflammatory responses. It is well-known for having chemo-
attractive properties specifically in the neutrophils of rainbow trout [20].  
A variety of pro-inflammatory cytokines are expressed following a bacterial infection. il6 is an 
important acute phase response cytokine [1] and functions as a mediator for the innate and adaptive 
immune responses. In rainbow trout, il6 is found to be induced by lipopolysaccharides (LPS), il1β and 
itself. As il6 can induce self-expression, this cytokine has the potential to either decrease or amplify the 
inflammatory immune response. In correlation to limiting host damage during inflammation, il6 can 
also down-regulate the expression of both il1β and tnfα. Studies has further suggested a significant role 
for il6 during immunoglobulin production and migration of leukocytes to sites of inflammation. [17] 
In teleost fish, the cytokine il11 consists of duplicates, il11a and il11b, both of which has been 
associated with having an important effect in both the antiviral and antibacterial defence. Studies has 
found that after a bacterial infection, il11 is highly upregulated in immune organs such as head kidney, 
liver, and spleen. [17] 
1.2.4. Lectins 
Lectins, also known as haemagglutinins, are proteins which agglutinate cells and precipitate 
glycoconjugates [6]. The specificity of lectins is determined by the monosaccharides or 
glycosaccharides which inhibit the induced agglutination or precipitation by binding to one of lectin’s 
sugar-binding sites [2][6]. Multiple studies have confirmed the presence of several lectin types in fish, 
who all plays a role in the immune response. Two major examples of lectins abilities include lectins 
potential in inhibiting pathogenic bacterial growth, and the mannan-binding lectin increasing 
phagocytosis in infected cells and inducing the complement system response. [2] 
There are two classes of lectins known in teleost fish, C-type and S-type. C-type lections covers a 
superfamily of membrane and extracellular proteins. S-type lectins are both intra- and extracellular but 
are unrelated to those of the C-type. [2][6]

1.2.5. The Complement 
One of the major mechanisms of the humoral immune response is the complement system. The 
complement system functions both as an initiator of the innate immune response and mounting of the 
adaptive immune response [2]. The complement system consists of plasma proteins, which will react 
with one another to induce a series of inflammatory responses to fight infection. It functions via a 
combination of three pathways, where only one needs to be activated to initiate immune response:  
i) 
The classic pathway 
ii) 
The lectin pathway 
iii) 
The alternative pathway 
The classic pathway is more common in mammals and involves the complex formation of antigen and 
antibody. The lectin pathway activates lectin-associated enzymes and the mannan-binding lectin (MBL) 
associated serine proteases (MASPs), which enhances the complement activation. This pathway is 
activated when lectins interact with the sugar moieties of the surface of some pathogens. When a 
pathogen is bound by the complement, phagocytosis is rapidly initiated and lysed after by macrophages 
or cytotoxic cells. Lastly, the alternative pathway, which is active in the serum of the fish. This pathway 
is antibody independent and is very prominent regarding defence mechanism in fish. [2][5]

pathway is predominant and is highlighted on the figure. This activation leads to the c3 convertase. The 
functions of each are mentioned in figure. Figure inspired by [5] and modified for this study using BioRender.  
The alternative pathway is activated by the spontaneous binding of complement protein c3b to the 
surface of a pathogen. c3b is the main effector molecule of the complement system, and functions by 
covalently binding to the pathogen targeting it for destruction by phagocytosis, as well as being another 
==PAGE:18==

peptide mediator of inflammation. c3b further binds the c3 convertase resulting in the formation of c5 
convertase. This convertase produces c5a, which is the most important small peptide mediator of 
inflammation [5]. c5b functions as a pathogen membrane-attack protein.  
The compliment system of fish exhibits bactericidal activity against non-virulent gram-negative 
bacteria, however not against virulent gram-negative bacteria or against gram-positive bacteria in 
general. This is caused by the presence of lipopolysaccharides in the cell walls of gram-negative 
bacteria. These activates the alternative pathways directly and causes lysis of the pathogen. [6] 
1.1.1. The adaptive immune response  
The adaptive immune response is activated, when B cells receptor (BCR) or T cell receptor (TCR) 
recognises the molecules created by the somatic mechanism and initiates a strong B- and T-lymphocyte 
response [2][17]. A major part of the adaptive immune system is the reliance on T cells, B cells and 
immunoglobulins (IG). IGs play a significant role in the immune response against pathogens and 
mediates a broad range of mechanisms. Among other, IGs mediate pathogen eliminations by 
phagocytosis, complement cascade activation and virus neutralization. [1]  
There are three isotypes of IGs present in bony fish, which are all produced by B cells. These are IgD, 
IgM and IgT. The IgM class tetramer is the most well-known and studied IG in fish and has eight 
antigenic combining sites. In fish serum, secreted IgM is found to be predominant, and thereby functions 
as the primary system antibody in fish [1]. The IG being predominant at mucosal surfaces is a newer 
found IG called, specifically for rainbow trout, IgT. This IG is known as IgZ in zebrafish (Danio rerio). 
IgT consists of at least three subclasses of proteins: IgT1, IgT2 and IgT3. IgT1 is expressed in mucosal 
and systemic lymphoid tissues, whereas systemic lymphoid organs mainly express the IgT2 subclass. 
IgT3 has been found in the serum of rainbow trout. Besides its involved in mucosal immunity, IgT is 
also associated with microbiota homeostasis [1]. The last known IG, IgD, has a similar sequence to the 
IgD found in mammals. The secreted version has however, been identified to have a different structure 
[1]. Research suggest IgDs plays a significant role in conferring protection at the gills, since it is a major 
site for pathogen entry. [1] [21]  
T cells are well known lymphocytes that plays a major role in the cell-mediated adaptive immune 
response. T cells are generally divided into cytotoxic T cells or T helper cells. These are distinguished 
by the expression of the glycoproteins, cd8 or cd4, respectively [56]. The cytotoxic T cells are capable 
of recognizing antigens in connection with mhcI, and hereby kill infected cells. This is mostly witnessed 
against virus infected cells. On the contrary, T helper cells express cd4 and a range of cytokines to 
mediate the immune response, in terms of e.g., effector cytokines, lymphocyte proliferation, and control 
against intra- and extracellular infections [56].

1.2. 
Previous studies and potentially relevant immune genes 
Besides the genes that became known when looking into the general immune response in teleost fish, 
and BKD and PRV-3 in particular, further journals were studied to select a broader selection of immune 
genes for analysing. The aim was to research previous studies to find potentially expressed immune 
genes of interest during a pathogenic infection. There are a few definitive genes expressed during a 
PRV-3 infection, but the spectrum for immune genes expressed during R. salmoninarum infection is 
rather unknown. Because of this, other bacterial infections in teleost fish were investigated.  
A study from 2005 by Tafalla et al. researched the expression of genes related to the early stage of 
infection in rainbow trout during viral haemorrhagic septicaemia virus infection. The study found that 
there was a significant increase in the expression of il1β at the early stages. Further, they found a 
significant induction of tgfβ, when analysing the spleen, at day 1, and another further significant 
increase in both the spleen and the liver at day 7 post-infection. An upregulation of the CXC chemokine, 
il8, was found only in the spleen. [23] 
A study from 2009 by Raida et al. researched the innate immune response in rainbow trout against 
primary and secondary infections with Yersinia ruckeri (Y. ruckeri). On the contrary to R. 
salmoninarum, Y. ruckeri is a gram-negative bacterium. This study concluded that there was a 
significant upregulation in the expression of il6, the acute phase protein serum amyloid protein a (saa), 
and significant down-regulations of the complement factors c3 and c5 [24]. The significant 
upregulations of il6 and saa was confirmed in a later study in 2020 by Zuo et al. [27], when investigating 
the same infection in rainbow trout. In this study, il8 and il17c was also found to be significantly 
upregulated in the early stages of infections. All four genes were found minimally expressed in the late 
stages of infection, indicating a significance in the early immune response. [27]  
In 2022, the functional and molecular immune response of rainbow trout during a bacterial infection 
was investigated by Fajardo et al. [20]. Besides some of the previously mentioned immune genes, this 
study found further evidence of significant upregulation of il10 and mmp9 following the first 24 hours 
of infection 
R. salmoninarum in Atlantic salmon. The focus was on genes known to play significant roles 
in immune regulation, such as tnfrsf11b and socs1, and T cell functions, such as rnf144a, il13ra1b and 
tnfrsf6b. [53] 
==PAGE:20==
Page 19 of 63 
 
Gorgoglione et al. [57] researched proliferative kidney disease in rainbow trout, which is caused by a 
parasite. Even though the disease is not bacteria caused, the study was of interest due to the nature of 
the disease. The theory was that there could be potential overlaps between genes expressed during that 
parasitic infection, and those expressed due to bacterial infection. This study found an upregulation of 
gata3 in the early stages of infection, and a further upregulation of cox2 and foxp3 in the later stages of 
infection [57].  
In 2022, Toubanaki et al. [58] published an article describing the fish host response during a viral 
infection. This research involved a broad range of immune genes and their differential expression in 
different fish species. As there is a potential in overlapping immune response regardless of pathogen 
type, this study further contributed to potential genes. Besides genes already covered in the current 
study, they analysed the expression of several chemokines in two fish species, S. sole and E. moara. In 
particular, ccl19 was found upregulated in S. sole, but downregulated in E. moara. Hepcidin, hp, and 
ifit5 was likewise found upregulated in multiple species. [58]

2.1. Primer design 
Based on the literature study, primers were designed for the immune genes which had previously been 
found significantly up- or downregulated in studies with bacterial and viral infections in both rainbow 
trout and Atlantic salmon. The primers were designed primarily based on FASTA sequences of the 
relevant genes using Ensembl.org, and lastly primer3.ut.ee for the final design. Lastly, all primer 
sequences were run through nucleotide BLAST (NCBI) to ensure no overlaps in expression of other 
undesired genes. 
The desired specifications for each primer sequence were primer size = 18-20-22, primer Tm = 59-60-
61 and product size ranges = 70-110. All primers were designed with the attempt of it overlapping one 
intron to decrease the chance of geometric DNA (OI = over intron). When this was not possible, the 
second-best option was designing the forward primer in one exon and the reverse in another (I = intron 
spanning). Last resort was designing the primer in the same exon. A table containing all primers can be 
seen in Appendix 1, where the method used (over intron, intron spanning, or same exon) and amplicon 
size is stated. Two primer pairs were designed for each individual gene. 
It was not possible to design primers fitting the previously stated requirements for some genes, and 
these were therefore excluded. Previously designed in-house primers are added to the table in Appendix 
1, of which a few has been published. A few was found in other studies. All primers not designed for 
this study has been appropriately referenced in the table.

2.3. Microfluidic qPCR 
2.3.1. RNA purification 
The RNA purification of samples from the BKD trial was performed using the NucleoMag Vet kit 
(Macherey-Nagel) following the manufacturer’s recommendations, homogenizing approximately 10 
mg kidney tissue in 600 µL buffer RA1 (Macherey-Nagel). 
The purification was completed using the KingFisherTM Flex (Thermo Fisher). Firstly, the lysis plate 
was prepared by dispensing 20 µL Proteinase K solution and 200 µL of the homogenized tissue samples 
into each well in the 96-well plate. Lastly, 180 µL buffer VL1 was added in each well and the mixture 
was mixed by pipetting up and down 3 times. The plate was then placed on shaker at 1000 rpm for 15 
minutes.  
3 wash plates were prepared as follows: W1: 600 µL buffer VEW1 in each well of empty Thermo 96-
well deep well plate. W2: 600 µL buffer VEW2 in each well of empty Thermo 96-well deep well plate. 
W3: 600 µL 80% ethanol in each well of empty Thermo 96-well deep well plate. A fourth plate, the 
elution plate, was prepared by adding 100 µL buffer VEL in each well of empty Thermo 96-well deep 
well plate. 
Continuing with the lysis plate, 20 µL B-Beads, 575 µL buffer VEB and 4 µL carrier RNA were added 
into each well. Lastly, the plates were inserted into KingFisherTM Flex as per instructed on the 
instrument display and the program of isolation of nucleic acids is ran.  
2.3.2. DNase treatment  
For the DNase treatment with DNA-free kit (AM1906, Ambion, Life Technologies), a typical reaction 
is 50 µL according to the manufacturer’s protocol. 5 µL of 10X DNase I buffer and 1 µL rDNase I was 
added to the RNA samples and mixed gently. As per the concentrations of RNA, the protocol for routine 
==PAGE:25==
Page 24 of 63 
 
DNase treatment was followed. 1 µL of rDNase I (2 U) was used for up to 10 µg of RNA in 50 µL 
reaction.  
These were incubated at 37 °C for 30 minutes. After incubation, 5.6 µL of resuspended DNase 
Inactivation Reagent was added to each (as 0.1 volume is recommended by manufacturers, and our total 
volume was 56 µL). The samples were incubated for 2 minutes at room temperature while occasionally 
mixing, before it was centrifuged at 10.000g for 1.5 minutes. The supernatant, which contains the RNA, 
was then transferred to clean 96 well plate.  
Lastly, the RNA concentrations were measured by Nanodrop (Nanodrop 1000, Thermo Scientific).  
2.3.3. cDNA synthesis 
The samples were diluted for total RNA input of 500 ng for cDNA synthesis. The cDNA synthesis was 
performed using the SuperScript® IV Reverse transcription kit (Thermo Fisher) in accordance with the 
manufacturer’s recommendations. Briefly, to do the primer annealing, 1 µL of 50 µM of random 
hexamers was mixed with 10 µL of RNA template, 1 µL of 10 mM dNTP mix and 1 µL nuclease-free 
water. This was then heated to 65 °C for 5 minutes and incubated on ice.  
To prepare the RT reaction mix, the following components were mixed: 64 µL 5x SSIV buffer, 16 µL 
100 nM DTT, 16 µL RNaseOUT™ Recombinant RNase Inhibitor and 16 µL SuperScript® IV Reverse 
Transcriptase (200 U/µL). The components are centrifuged and then mixed with the annealed RNA.  
The -RT (non-reverse transcription) reactions were made by combining 6 µL 5x SSIV buffer, 1.5 µL 
100 nM DTT, and 1.5 µL RNaseOUT™. 
When using random hexamers, the combined reaction mixture needed to firstly be incubated at 23 °C 
for 10 minutes. After, it was incubated at 50-55 °C for 10 minutes, before inactivating the reaction by 
incubation it at 80 °C for 10 minutes. After the samples were stored at -20 °C before use. 
2.3.4. Pre-amplification 
The pre-amplification was performed using TaqMan PreAmp Master Mix (Applied Biosystems, PN 
4391128). Primer solutions of 20 µM were prepared for each primer pair listed in Appendix 1, 
containing 10 µL forward primer, 10 µL reverse primer and lastly 30 µL low EDTA TE-buffer. 5 µL 
of each primer solutions were mixed with 495 µL low EDTA TE-buffer to obtain a 200 nM primer mix 
solution. 
A pre-amplification master mix (PreAmp mix) for 84 reactions was prepared with an extra +20% to 
insure enough mix for all reactions. This PreAmp mix consists of 302.4 µL TaqMan PreAmp Master 
mix, 201.6 µL low EDTA TE-buffer and 252 µL of the previously prepared 200 nM primer mix 
solution.

7.5 µL of the PreAmp mix is added to 96-well PCR plate along with 2.5 µL cDNA for all samples and 
placed in the thermocycler. The thermal cycling conditions were as follows: 95 °C for 10 minutes, 17/20 
cycles: 95 °C for 15 seconds and 60 °C for 4 minutes. 20 cycles were run for both R. salmoninarum and

2.3.5. Exonuclease treatment and serial dilutions 
Pre-amplified cDNA samples were treated with an exonuclease to remove leftover primers using the 
Exonuclease I (E. coli) kit (New England BioLabs, M0293L). The exonuclease was diluted to 4U/µL 
by adding 96 µL (due to there being 96 samples) of Exo 20U, 48 µL reaction buffer and 336 µL MilliQ. 
After 4 µL of Exo 4U was added to each pre-amplified sample and spun. This was then incubated on 
thermocycler with the following program: 37 °C for 30 minutes, 80 °C for 15 minutes and lasty held on 
4 °C.  
The samples were then centrifuged thoroughly and were diluted at 2 µL cDNA in 18 µL Low EDTA 
TE-buffer and stored at -20 °C. 
A pool was made of the undiluted pre-amplified exonuclease-treated samples for the serial dilutions 
and standard curves. 2 µL of each sample was added to the pool. The serial dilutions were made 5-fold 
as follows: P1: 10 µL pool + 10 µL low EDTA TE-buffer. P2: 2 µL P1 + 8 µL low EDTA TE-buffer. 
P3: 2 µL P2 + 8 µL low EDTA TE-buffer. P4: 2 µL P3 + 8 µL low EDTA TE-buffer. One full dilution 
row (P1-P4) was made for the PRV-3 samples, and one full and one P1-P3 (lack of space) was added 
for BKD samples. The pool of BKD samples consisted of samples pre-amplified at both 17 and 20 
cycles due to a lack of space on the IFC. Efficiency and differences at 17 and 20 pre-amplification 
cycles for kidney/BKD samples were checked later by standard qPCR on a few selected genes (see 
Section 2.5). 
2.3.6. High throughput microfluidic qPCR 
The qPCR was performed on the pre-amplified and exonuclease treated cDNA samples. It was done 
using the 96.96 Dynamic Array IFC (Fluidigm) as per the manufacturer's recommendations. Briefly, 3 
µL 2x Assay Loading Reagents master mix (Fluidigm), consisting of Assay Loading Reagent, was 
added to microtiter plate along with 3 µL 20 µM specific primer pair mix. This was placed in the fridge 
while preparing the pre-sample mix. The pre-sample mix consisted of 300 µL of 2x TaqMan Gene 
Expression Mastermix (Applied Biosystems, PN 4369016), 33 µL 20X DNA Binding Dye (Fluidigm), 
33 µL EvaGreen 20X (VWR, BTIU31000) and 99 µL 1x low EDTA TE-buffer. 4.5 µL of the pre-
sample mix along with 1.5 µL pre-amplified exonuclease treated cDNA was added to each well on the 
plate.

The control line fluid was added carefully to the chip before running the prime program on the HX IFC

2.4. Data analysis 
The raw data was transferred to Fluidigm Real-Time PCR Analysis software. An overview of the full 
heatmap from the software can be found in Appendix 3. Initial overview showed one replicate of 
samples 22-27-26, 22-27-27 and 22-27-29 for both the 17 and 20 pre-amplification cycles to be outside 
of the detection limit, indicating preparation error potentially during the cDNA dilutions. These were 
removed from the dataset. 
First step was to evaluate the no template control sample (NTC) and the -RT samples to assess run 
quality. Preferably, a Ct value over 5 was desired for these negative samples, as this means under 5% of 
the input was caused by geometrical DNA.  
Next step was analysing the melting curve of each primer. Here, each individual melting curve was 
evaluated by the curve, peaks, potential primer dimers etc. If only one sample was an outliner when 
examining the melting curve, and remainder looked promising, the one sample was removed from the 
dataset. 
Lastly, the standard curves were examined. Here it was generally desired to have an R2 < 0.95 and the 
efficiency around 0.8-1.20. Two 5-fold dilution rows were made for the BKD samples, one consisting 
of 4 dilution and one with 3. As the first dilution row looked promising, the latter was neglected. A 5-
fold dilution row were also made for the PRV-3 samples consisting of 4 dilutions, F1-F4. Some PCR 
inhibition was found in F1 and was therefore neglected.

For further analysis, the dataset was then exported to GenEx (Version 7, MultiD Analyses AB). First

ensure no invalid samples was present. This dataset was then transferred to Excel (Version 2305, Build

Before continuing, it was necessary to perform normalisation to the reference genes. To do this, the 
most optimal reference genes needed to be selected as 5 were tested. Appendix 4 shows the results from 
the reference gene analysis (geNorm and NormFinder) in GenEx, where individual reference genes 
were tested against others in pairwise variation. The desire in this analysis was to have a M-value 
(average expression stability) lower than 1, preferably as low as possible, as it refers to the most stable 
reference genes.  
When the optimal references genes were selected, it was possible to normalize with reference genes in 
GenEx. This provided a new dataset, which had the median of our reference genes subtracted from the 
sample values. Using this new dataset, the technical variation between cDNA replicates was examined. 
This was done by subtraction, and the primers which had a greater variation than ±1.5 was highlighted. 
For each primer, maximum 15% of the samples could vary more than ±1.5. This variation was also 
calculated for samples, and any with > 15% variation was removed from the dataset.  
The dataset was then downscaled further by calculating the average cDNA for each duplicate. This gave 
us the average ΔCt value. Using GenEx function “Validate Sheet”, it was possible to set a limit of a 
minimum of 85% valid data, whereas the program removed primers with too many missing values. The 
remaining NAN values (either from non-detectable samples or samples excluded earlier in the data 
analysis), were set to the correlating maximum ΔCt + 1 for each individual NAN sample. This correlates 
to half of the least measured sample.  
Last step was examining the relative quantities (RQ). Using GenEx, the dataset was normalised by the 
lowest ΔCt value. The lowest ΔCt value for each primer was set to 1, and the remainder was scaled 
correspondingly. The log2 of RQ values were likewise calculated for statistical analysis.  
From the log2 RQ values, a heatmap could be generated using R studio (Version 2023.03.0, Build 386 
"Cherry Blossom" for Windows) and the package “pheatmap”.  
2.4.1. Statistical analysis 
A fold change was performed to investigate significant up- or downregulation of any genes. This was 
performed on the previously described RQ values (scaled ΔCt value). To do this, it was necessary to 
scale the infected samples against the controls. There was only one control group in the PRV-3 exposed 
samples, and in the case of BKD, the KDM2 control group was used. The two control groups for the R. 
==PAGE:29==
Page 28 of 63 
 
salmoninarum samples were also tested against each other. To calculate the scale, the average of control 
samples for each primer was calculated, and following equation was used: 
𝑠𝑐𝑎𝑙𝑒=
1
𝑎𝑣𝑒𝑟𝑎𝑔𝑒 Δ𝐶𝑡𝐶𝑜𝑛𝑡𝑟𝑜𝑙 𝑔𝑟𝑜𝑢𝑝
 
The scale can be checked by the sum of the scaled control group equalling 1. The RQ values for infected 
samples were then scaled for each individual primer based on this scale. Calculating the average of the 
newfound scaled values, provided the fold change value. The fold change determines whether there is 
a relevant up- or downregulation, where values lower than 0.5 or higher than 2 is considered biologically 
relevant.  
To analyse if the up- or downregulation was significant, a simple ttest was performed on log2 
transformed RQ values, as gene expression is assumed to be normally distributed. The cut off was set 
to p < 0.05.  
2.4.2. PCA plots 
To investigate potential groupings of samples, a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was performed. 
This analysis is a widely used technique for data visualization to potentially identify principal 
components depending on the variables. The PCA plots was generated with Rstudio using the packages 
“FactoMineR”, “ggplot2”, “factoextra” and “tidyverse”. For the plot, the values used are the log2 
transformed RQ values. 
2.4.3. Histograms  
Histograms were made in Excel (Version 2305, Build 16.0.16501.20074, Microsoft) for visualization 
of expressed genes. The histograms were made based of the previously mentioned calculated fold 
changes.

4. Discussion 
A total of 47 primers, not including duplicates or reference genes, was tested against previously known 
positive PRV-3 and R. salmoninarum samples. The primers covered a broad range of immune genes. 
After data processing, 39 functioning primers were found for the PRV-3 samples, whereas 34 was found 
for the R. salmoninarum samples. The remainder of the primers were discarded for various reasons. 
Some was deemed not trustworthy, as primer dimers were present, and some samples were out of the 
detectable range when analysing with the Fluidigm Real-Time PCR analysis software. That some 
samples were out of the detectable range, does not necessarily mean that those primers were poorly 
designed, instead the corresponding immune genes could simply not be expressed.  
A higher number of non-detectable samples were likewise found, when analysing the R. salmoninarum 
positive samples from the 17-pre-amplification cycle, indicating that further amplification can be 
necessary when working with low concentration samples. Even though using both 17- and 20-cycle 
pre-amplification program results in an efficiency within the acceptable range, using 20 cycles provides 
a generally higher efficiency, indicating 20 cycles to be preferable.  
When analysing the efficiency of the different amplification programs for the R. salmoninarum samples, 
three genes were chosen. The average efficiency when using a 20-cycle pre-amplification program for 
those genes were: eef1a1 = 1.18, gzma = 1.05, and ifng = 1.05. Very similarly, the efficiency found for 
the same genes for the PRV-3 samples were: eef1a1 = 1.11, gzma = 1.10, and ifng = 1.06. It was 
generally witnessed that the efficiency of the PRV-3 samples was higher than those of R. salmoninarum 
samples. This was initially suspected to be due to potential tissue specific inhibition. In this study, the 
R. salmoninarum samples are kidney tissue, whereas the PRV-3 samples are spleen tissue. Studies has 
found high levels of melanin in the kidney, which has proved to be a potential RT-PCR inhibitor. 
Depending on the melanin concentration level, it is possible for melanin to either potentially block PCR, 
or even inhibit reverse transcription. [60] 
As it is only possible to compare the efficiency of those three specific genes in this study, as the first 
run was analysed using a shared pool for both the 17- and 20-cycle pre-amplification, further 
investigating is necessary to determine the possibilities of tissue specific inhibition regarding working 
with kidney samples.

which were not tested in the study [8] of which the PRV-3 samples were obtained from, the results are 
compatible for PRV-3. Interestingly, genes which has been associated with PRV-3 infections, such as 
ddx58, mx and rsad2 are also seen upregulated during R. salmoninarum infection, indicating their 
significance in the early immune response, regardless of pathogen type.  
Generally, a higher fold change was witnessed in the expressed genes during PRV-3 infection compared 
to the expression of the same genes during a R. salmoninarum infection. This was especially noticed in 
previously mentioned PRV-3 associated interferons stimulated genes such as isg15, mx and rsad2. isg15 
is well known for its antiviral defence in response to type 1 interferons [66], which is supported by the 
findings of this study (PRV-3: fold change at 14.85). Significant expression of isg15 during R. 
salmoninarum infection (fold change at 3.94) was likewise found, indicating a further valuable role 
against bacterial infections. Studies suggest overexpression of isg15 can results in triggering the 
induction of rsad2 [66]. rsad2, well-studied for its antiviral activity, was found highly expressed during 
PRV-3 infection (fold change at 32.66) compared to during R. salmoninarum infection (fold change at 
6.83). 
Other genes had similar expression, such as the transcriptional regulator, irf1, which activates genes 
involved in both the innate and adaptive immune response (PRV-3 = fold change at 2.45, R. 
salmoninarum = fold change at 3.25). In addition, ifng was upregulated (PRV-3 = fold change at 7.74, 
R. salmoninarum = fold change at 8.89), which most likely induced the upregulation of the chemokine 
ccl19 (PRV-3 = fold change at 2.97, R. salmoninarum = fold change at 2.89). A study by Sepahi et al. 
[67] found evidence that ccl19 in rainbow trout can modulate the expression of ccr7, ifng and cd8α in 
the head-kidney [67]. The expression level of the individual genes could range because of difference in 
host response (viral infection versus bacterial infection) and some of the immune genes can be tissue 
specific to a degree, therefore found at higher concentrations in the spleen compared to the kidney.  
A greater number of genes was analysed while using two sets of primers. Most of the gene expression 
for the individual genes was found coherent between the respecting primer sets. The genes ddx58, isg15, 
mx, rsad2, socs1 and ifit5 was all found significant and biologically expressed using both set of primers 
for both the PRV-3 and the R. salmoninarum samples. Only one set of primers was used for cxcl10 and 
ccl19, so no comparisons can be made. Furthermore, during data processing, primer set 2 for ifng 
(RB62) was discarded due to an unusual large range of values in both infected samples and control for 
both PRV-3 and R. salmoninarum, indicating possible contamination during primer preparation.   
irf1 (RB7) was upregulated respectively in the PRV-3 samples (p = 0.010 with a fold change at 2.45) 
and the R. salmoninarum samples (p = 0.0007 with a fold change at 3.25); however, the second primer 
set for irf1 (RB8), was neither biologically or significantly expressed in the PRV-3 samples, and not 
==PAGE:47==
Page 46 of 63 
 
biologically expressed in the R. salmoninarum samples. As both primer sets are from Sørensen et al. 
[8], but only primer RB7 was published, it is unknown whether the results for RB8 corresponds to those 
of the article. There could potentially be human technical error in this study’s case.  
The findings of these expressed immune genes support the theory of overlapping host response in 
rainbow trout during infection, while further suggesting widely expression as it was spleen and kidney 
tissue analysed, respectively for PRV-3 and R. salmoninarum. It is important to note, regarding this 
study, that it is uncertain whether expression for these 10 shared genes, would still be homogeneous if 
the same tissue was used for both pathogens. In this study, the PRV-3 and R. salmoninarum samples 
shared no significantly downregulated genes. The findings of these overlapping host responses at 2 wpc 
is a valuable starting point for further research. 
4.2. 
PRV-3: Significant and biologically expressed genes 
The genes which were found significant and biologically expressed in only PRV-3 or R. salmoninarum 
samples were of significant interest, as it could provide a potential for differentiating between whether 
a viral or bacterial infection are majorly affecting the fish, and hereby informing treatment type priority.  
From the PRV-3 samples, both tlr3 and il12a were found significant and biologically upregulated. The 
upregulation of tlr3 is as expected, as this toll-like-receptor recognises the double-stranded Piscine 
orthoreovirus and mediates the immune response. On the contrary, tlr3 is not capable of recognising 
the gram-positive R. salmoninarum bacteria and was reasonably not expressed. The proinflammatory 
cytokine il12a is a known subunit of il12, which studies has found to play a significant role in T cell 
stimulation and functions by linking the innate resistance and adaptive immunity [37]. This cytokine 
has been primarily reported in spleen and gills in rainbow trout [36], which could be a reason as to why 
it was not found in the kidney samples for R. salmoninarum. In mammals, studies have found a high 
level of il12 in response to a gram-positive bacterium infection. [38] 
Additionally, il1β, jak1, and cd22 were found significant and biologically downregulated at 2 wpc. il1β 
is known to be one of the first expressed cytokines to enable the immune system to respond quickly at 
sign of infection. Even though the samples used in this study is relatively early, 2 wpc, it is possible 
that an upregulation of il1β was only visible prior to the sampling point [39]. Further, Puente-Marin et 
al. [40] found evidence that rainbow trout during a viral infection exhibited an upregulation of il1β in 
the red blood cells but witnessed a downregulation of il1β in the spleen.  
jak1 has a dominant role in effecting the expression of inflammatory genes. Earlier in section 4.1. it 
was discussed that socs1 was heavily expressed (4-fold) during PRV-3 infection. SOCS proteins are 
major regulators of the JAK/STAT pathway, whereas socs1 can inhibit jak1 catalytic activity directly. 
socs1 functions by blocking subsequent phosphorylation. [41] As jak1 activates stat1 to form the 
JAK/STAT complex, it is consistent with stat1 not being found expressed in this study.  
==PAGE:48==
Page 47 of 63 
 
Little is known about cd22 regarding teleost fish. In mammals, cd22 functions as a negative B-cell 
antigen receptor regulator, believing to raise the antigen concentration threshold which are required 
before activation of B cells [42]. A study by Shivam et al [43] investigated a parasite infection in brown 
trout (Salmo trutta) and experienced a consistent upregulation of cd22 in the kidney, but saw an initial 
downregulation in the spleen, before witnessing an upregulation after 6 wpc. It is noteworthy, that both 
sets of primers for cd22 was discarded during the data processing of the R. salmoninarum samples 
because of very low correlation values. It therefore remains unknown whether cd22 would be present 
during this bacterial infection. 
A few contradictions were witnessed between the two sets of primers and their corresponding 
expression. For tlr3, both primers showed biological and significant expression. The second primer set 
for cd22 (RB108) for the PRV-3 samples, however, was significant, but showed no biological relevance. 
The cut-off to be biologically relevant was a fold change lower than 0.5 or higher than 2. The first 
primer set (RB107) for cd22 had a fold change of 0.47, where the second primer set was 0.58. This 
proves the fold change was just under and over the cut-off, respectively, and further research with a 
larger sampling pool can be necessary.  
For the gene il12a, primer set 2 (RB193) showed a biological relevance, but not significantly (p = 
0.078), potentially meaning less functioning primer, or since the correlation is relatively close to the 
0.05 cut-off, a larger sampling pool is needed for trustworthy results.  
Both sets of primer for respectively gene itln1 and il17c was discarded during data processing for the 
PRV-3 samples due to having a technical variation greater than ±1.5. It therefore remains unknown 
whether these two genes would have been expressed in the samples. 
PRV is known to induce a strong innate immune response, which is confirmed by expressed immune 
genes found in this study. On the contrary though, studies speculate the level of protection, as viruses 
are known for developing several evasion mechanisms against the type 1 IFN response [13]. A 
suppression of the type 1 IFNs could potentially be a contributing factor to the lack of expression of 
some immune genes. Some type 1 IFNs genes has however been found in this study, such as isg15 and 
mx.  A review by Vallejos-Vidal et al. [14] suggest the potential for PRV to display immune evasion 
mechanisms by comparing to another dsRNA virus, infectious pancreatic necrosis virus (IPNV). This 
virus has been reported to inhibit ifna1 activation. The infectious salmon anaemia virus (ISAV), which 
are known to affect primarily Atlantic salmon, has been found to inhibit both IFN transcription and 
production [14]. Further studies are necessary to investigate the potential of PRVs immune evasion 
capabilities.

4.3. 
R. salmoninarum: Significant and biologically expressed genes 
Compared to the PRV-3 samples, a higher number of genes was found significant and biologically up- 
or downregulated during only R. salmoninarum infection. The immune genes tnf, gzma, saa, mmp9, 
tgfβ3, il8 and c3ar1 were all found upregulated at 2 wpc.  
The cytokine tnf belongs to the tumour necrosis factor superfamily, where studies have confirmed its 
involvement in regulating a broad range of processes, including elements of the immune response [44]. 
In healthy rainbow trout, tnf is constitutively expressed in both gills and kidney, with upregulations 
occurring due to inflammation. This corresponds to the found upregulation of tnf in this study. It is 
suggested that fish tnf functions as mammal tnf by regulating macrophage functions as an immune 
defence [45].  Two tnf receptors, tnfrs11b and tnfrsf6b, for respectively tnf-11b and tnf-6b, was likewise 
investigated in this thesis due to suggestions by other relevant studies. Both sets of primers for the two 
receptors was however discarded during data processing, so it remains unknown whether they would 
be expressed during R. salmoninarum infection. 
The serine protease, gzma, is connected to cell-mediated cytotoxicity against virus infected cells and 
bacterial infections [46]. At 2 wpc, it was found to be upregulated 9-fold, indicating a significance in 
the early immune response. It was likewise found expressed in the PRV-3 samples (fold change 12.6, 
p = 0.054), however, the Pearson’s correlation was just over the 0.5 limit, meaning the expression 
showed no significant difference. It is important to note that this study contains a small samples size 
(PRV-3: 2 controls, 6 positive), where one sample can potentially skew the results, and that the 
Pearson’s correlation potentially is in the acceptable range in a larger sample set.   
Studies have found growing evidence showing differences in the host response against gram-positive 
vs gram-negative bacteria [47].  However, the upregulation of saa and il8 compliments the study by 
Zuo et al. [27], where after exposure to the gram-negative bacteria Y. ruckeri, these immune genes were 
similarly upregulated. saa is associated with being an effector molecule, while il8 is known be an 
encoder for other inflammatory cytokines. Even though the host response to gram-negative and gram-
positive bacteria will differ in some respects, some of the immune profile and initial response has proved 
to be similar.   
The upregulation of mmp9 corresponds to the current knowledge of the matrix metalloproteinases and 
their involvement in the immune response of teleost fish. Notably, studies have found a higher presence 
of mmp9 in the immune organs, where an upregulation is seen at the early stages of infection. This 
indicates mmp9 plays a relevant role in the early innate immune response [48]. 
With tgfβ being a pleiotropic cytokine, it is valuable in many processes. As mentioned, there is minimal 
knowledge revolving the transforming growth factors of teleost; however, research suspect they 
function as those in mammals [50]. This means, that besides modulating a broad range of biological 
==PAGE:50==
Page 49 of 63 
 
processes, transforming growth factors are considering to be immunosuppressive cytokines, but are also 
associated with promoting differentiation and homeostasis of inflammatory T cells [50]. In this study, 
tgfβ3 was found to be upregulated (9-fold) at 2 wpc.  
The expression of the complement factor c3a was measured by the expression of the receptor, c3ar1. It 
was found to be upregulated during R. salmoninarum infection, which it consistent with other studies 
revolving the complement components [51-52]. The upregulation of c3 indicates a valuable role in the 
innate immune response against bacterial infection [51]. In contrast, another study found significant 
downregulation of c3 in the liver during a gram-negative infection [24] at early sampling points. Slight 
upregulation was found after 4 wpc [24]. One study found evidence that c3 is involved with repairing 
liver tissue after gram-negative bacteria infection [51].  
On the contrary, cd8, IgM, tcrβ and gata3 were all found downregulated at 2 wpc. cd8 plays a major 
role in T cell recognition and are expressed on cytotoxic T-cells. Little is known regarding R. 
salmoninarum’s effect on cd8 in rainbow trout; however, Gnanagobal et al. [49] likewise witnessed a 
downregulation of cd8, when investigating R. salmoninarum in kidney samples from lumpfish. Notably, 
no expression of cd4 was found for either the PRV-3 or R. salmoninarum samples in this study. A 
previous study revolving PRV-3 found upregulation of both cd4 and cd8 in relation to heart pathology. 
This study worked with heart tissue, which could explain to lack of gene expression found, when 
analysing spleen and kidney tissue, respectively [30]. Gnanagobal et al. did also have significant 
downregulation of cd4 in the kidney during R. salmoninarum infection, indicating R. salmoninarum to 
have a degree of immune suppressive capabilities. [49] This theory could potentially be a factor as to 
why IgM and the T cell receptor, tcrβ, was found significantly downregulated. However, one study 
found upregulation of IgM during Y. ruckeri infection, but only specifically in the gills and liver, 
indicating a degree of tissue specificness. [27] 
Another gene related to T cell immunity, gata3, was also downregulated at 2 wpc. gata3 is the 
transcription factor that mediates the differentiation of Th2 cells [54]. The downregulation of these 
genes shows a possibility of R. salmoninarum being suppressive towards the adaptive immune response. 
[27] 
The majority of the expressed genes during R. salmoninarum infection was not analysed using two sets 
of primers. This was mainly because of previous validation. Only one set of primers were used for the 
immune genes tnf, gzma, saa, IgM and tcrβ. The two sets of primers for cd8, mmp9, gata3 and c3ar1 
complemented each other with similar results. During data processing, the second set of primers for 
tgfβ (RB202) and il8 (RB209) was discarded due to poor primer quality.

4.4. 
Differentiator gene between viral and bacterial infection 
As for finding a potential differentiator gene to determine which pathogenic infection is affecting the 
host primarily, a difference was found when examining individual significantly expressed genes for 
either of the pathogens. The differently expressed genes during PRV-3 infection and R. salmoninarum 
infection are summarized in table 7.  
By examining the generated heatmaps (figure 8 and figure 10), a major difference was initially seen in 
the expression of saa and tgfβ3, when comparing positive PRV-3 and R. salmoninarum samples. In the 
R. salmoninarum samples, saa and tgfβ3, belonged to the grouping of highly expressed immune genes, 
where in contrast, they were not found expressed during the PRV-3 infection. saa is known as an 
effector molecule and is a major acute phase protein found highly expressed in response to inflammation 
[26]. This protein has previously been associated with chronic inflammatory diseases, which supports 
this study’s findings of its relevanse against the chronic kidney disease caused by R. salmoninarum. 
tgfβ3 is a proinflammatory cytokine, and both genes, in this study, shows none to minimal antiviral 
activity, but instead plays a significant role in the early immune response against a bacterial infection.  
Several other immune genes, as mentioned in section 4.2. and 4.3., are differently expressed depending 
on the pathogenic cause. These individual genes could additionally serve to differentiate between which 
pathogen is affecting the host.

5. Conclusion 
For this thesis, the immune profile of rainbow trout during Piscine orthoreovirus 3 (PRV-3) infection 
and Renibacterium salmoninarum (R. salmoninarum) infection was analysed and compared. This was 
done with the additional aim of finding a differentiator gene to determine which pathogenic infection 
is primarily affecting the fish in fish farms, where outbreaks commonly consist of multiple pathogens, 
and hereby determining potential treatment priority. 
Primers were designed for a broad range of immune gene relevant to both viral and bacterial infections, 
followed by high throughput microfluidic qPCR to examine the gene expression. Additionally, the 
optimal number of cycles in the pre-amplification step for the R. salmoninarum samples was analysed, 
whereas a 20-cycle pre-amplification program was proved to be optimal.  
10 genes including irf1, ddx58, isg15, mx, rsad2, ifng, ifit5, cxcl10, ccl19 and socs1 was found both 
significant and biologically upregulated at 2 wpc in both the PRV-3 and the R. salmoninarum samples, 
indicating similar host response regardless of pathogen type and widely gene expression, as the tissue 
samples analysed was spleen and kidney tissue, respectively for PRV-3 and R. salmoninarum. PRV-3 
and R. salmoninarum shared no significantly downregulated genes in this study.  
A significant upregulation of toll like receptor, tlr3, and pro-inflammatory cytokine il12a was proved 
in response to PRV-3, while pro-inflammatory cytokine il1β, signalling protein jak1, and lectin cd22 
were found significantly downregulated. In response to a R. salmoninarum infection, 7 genes were 
found significantly upregulated. These include the pro-inflammatory tnf, gzma, acute phase protein saa, 
regulatory factor mmp9, inflammatory cytokine tgfβ3, chemokine il8 and complement c3a receptor 
c3ar1 were all found upregulated at 2 wpc. Additionally, T cell marker cd8, immunoglobulin IgM, T 
cell receptor tcrβ and transcription factor gata3 were all found downregulated at 2 wpc. These findings 
indicate R. salmoninarum to potentially have immune suppressive capabilities.  
saa and tgfβ3 were the highest expressed genes during R. salmoninarum infection, yet not expressed 
during PRV-3, making them ideal differentiator genes to determine which pathogen is primarily 
affecting the host at 2 wpc.  
5.1. 
Future perspectives 
The findings of this study are valuable for determining the immune profile in the early stages of 
infection, but it is necessary to continue research for different stages of infection to establish a complete 
immune profile for the full course of the disease (e.g., incubation, active replication, and latency). It is 
with high certainty that the immune genes are differently expressed throughout the different stages, as 
well as the expression of some immune genes being tissue specific.  
Optimally, having multiple sampling points and consistent tissue type samples, could lead the way for 
better host treatment and the overall improvement of aquaculture during dangerous outbreaks.
