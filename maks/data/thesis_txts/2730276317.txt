Abstract
The development and optimization of renewable energy resources are paramount
in today’s global energy landscape. This thesis presents a data­driven approach
to predict high resolution, wind turbine load time series coupled with sophisti­
cated dimensionality reduction techniques. The goal is to achieve precise fore­
casts efficiently, enhancing the reliability and performance of wind turbines,
and thereby supporting the scalability of wind energy as a key contributor to
sustainable power generation.
The study leverages the benefits of Deep Learning (DL) and signal dimensional­
ity reduction techniques such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and Dis­
crete Cosine Transform (DCT) to reduce the dimensionality of high­frequency
wind field data, fed to the neural network thus improving the final performance
it. The DL architecture utilized in this study was an LSTM (Long Short­Term
Memory) algorithm with their ability to ingest sequential data like time series. A
high­resolution (100Hz) data set of 1000 simulations/results of the offshore IEA
DTU 15 MW wind turbine model, was used for the training, validation and test
the LSTM network. The neural network was trained in taking dimensionallity­
reduced turbulent boxes as input along with selected operational parameters of
the turbine and then predicting load time series for specific channels as output
in an closed­loop/ autoregressive way facilitating in this ways an autonomous,
data­driven simulator. Incorporating these data­driven models to the wind in­
dustry, aims to significantly enhance the prediction speed and accuracy of wind
turbine loads, outperforming conventional methods and providing a more re­
sponsive and precise tool for load forecasting in wind energy management.
The dimensionality reduction analysis conducted in this study revealed some
insightful details regarding the application of the techniques used. Principal
Component Analysis (PCA) was notably more robust in terms of the number of
explanatory coefficients required to maintain comparable levels of variance/en­
ergy retention, thereby demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing energy with
fewer components, compared to the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) method.
i
==PAGE:4==
On the other hand, the DCT method exhibited a pronounced advantage in com­
putational performance. Specifically, the average time, took the DCT method
to processes a single turbulence box was 0.113 seconds, which is significantly
more time­efficient than PCA, that required 1.548 seconds for the same task,
yielding a time reduction of approximately 92.59%.
Two models were constructed and tested in this study, namely the ”PCA­model”
and the ”DCT­model” in several prediction scenarios. In the open­loop predic­
tion scenario, both the PCA­model and the DCT­model exhibited a commend­
able capacity to encapsulate the test set from a singular turbulence box. In the
case of closed loop predictions, single turbulence box scenario, the DCT model
managed to predict and capture the main trend of the load scale compared to the
original data, indicating a promising approach in constructing data­drive model
operating as autonomous simulators.
The research provides significant insights into harnessing machine learning and
dimensionality reduction techniques for constructing a data­driven models. The
findings lay the groundwork for future studies aiming to further enhance the
simulation of wind turbine loads, and contribute to the ongoing global efforts
in making wind energy more reliable and efficient.

Introduction
1.1
Broader Introduction
In recent years, the global energy landscape has undergone significant transformations, with a
marked shift towards sustainable and renewable energy sources. This transition is driven by
the dual imperatives of mitigating climate change and ensuring energy security. Wind energy,
with its abundant resources and minimal environmental footprint, has emerged as a frontrunner
in this renewable energy revolution [1]. As countries intensify their efforts to harness wind
energy, the installed capacity of wind turbines has witnessed exponential growth. As illustrated
in Figure 1.1, Europe only has seen a significant trend in new wind installations. Specifically,
Europe is expected to install 129 GW of new wind farms over the period 2023­2027, with the
EU­27 accounting for 98 GW of that [2].
Figure 1.1: New installations in Europe – WindEurope’s scenarios.
1.2
Problem scope definition
The rising prominence of wind energy in the global energy landscape has induced extensive
research into optimizing the design and operation of wind turbines.
Until today, the design of wind turbine structures is based on the application of partial safety
factors to estimated loads and material strengths as stipulated by the IEC 61400­1 standard [3].
These safety factors accommodate uncertainties in wind conditions, simulation software ac­
curacy, and material property variability. However, the true level of uncertainty can vary for
each wind farm, influenced by its unique operational history leading to a design may result in
turbines that are capable of operating beyond their initial planned lifespan [4]. In addition, dur­
ing the last decade, advancements in data acquisition systems and comprehensive measurement

campaigns have revolutionized the wind industry by providing an unprecedented volume of
continuous and high­resolution data. On the top of that, parallel advancements have been have
been simultaneously achieved in machine learning algorithms and computational power signif­
icantly enhancing the ability to analyze, understand, and predict wind turbine performance with
greater accuracy and efficiency[5].
Reflecting on the pivotal factors highlighted earlier, it is clear that the integration of comprehen­

1.3
Scope of project
Despite all the above, the intricacies of accurately forecasting and modeling the aerodynamic
and structural loads on these structures are amplified by the inherent unpredictable spatial and
temporal wind variability[6]. Although conventional methodologies, whether rooted in phys­
ical modeling or steady­state correlations, provide valuable perspectives, they often miss the
nuanced dynamics of wind turbine operations, while also exhibits high computational time and
resource requirements[7].
Based on the aforementioned, there is an evident and pressing need for advanced modeling
techniques that can both encapsulate the temporal and autoregressive behaviors of the wind tur­
bine while enhancing computational efficiency. Such advancements are essential to accurately
forecast the behavior of these systems in real­time while minimizing the associated computa­
tional overhead, thereby facilitating more agile and informed decision­making in wind turbine
management.
Over the past few years, machine learning techniques have seen substantial advancements and
are now being applied to wind energy sector which, in turn, has increasingly been adopting
these learning tools to enhance performance of wind turbines systems [6]. A notable subset
of machine learning, deep learning, employs algorithms known as artificial neural networks,
which are conceptualized on the human brain’s architecture. Within this framework, Recurrent
Neural Networks (RNNs) stand out for their ability to process sequences of data, making them
particularly suited for time series modeling. RNNs are distinguished by their internal memory,
which captures information about previous inputs to inform the processing of new sequences.
This wave of sophisticated neural network designs has revolutionized time series analysis, a
crucial element in assessing the performance of wind turbine systems. Deep learning models,
especially those using RNN architectures, have shown remarkable adaptability in deciphering
non­linear interdependencies within data sets. Their ability in learning from historical patterns
facilitates refined and more accurate predictions, surpassing the capabilities of traditional statis­
tical methods, which typically struggle with complex, non­linear data structures. This advantage
is particularly significant in the wind energy sector, where forecasting accuracy is paramount
and the variables are often interlinked in intricate ways.
Moreover, these sophisticated, data­driven models are now being incorporated within the dig­
ital twin frameworks of wind turbines. This integration has unlocked new potentials for real­
time performance monitoring, precise load prediction, and the optimization of wind turbines in

a virtual space that precisely reflects their physical counterparts. Digital twins, which are dy­
namic virtual representations of physical entities, are pivotal in executing simulations that guide
real­world modifications. This cutting­edge technology is gaining prominence across various
sectors, including wind energy.[8].
This thesis aims to exploit the capabilities of an RNN­based time series model, coupled with
high­resolution data decomposition methods like Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and
Discrete Cosine Transformation (DCT), to build data­driven models that predict high­frequency
time series wind turbine performance. Utilizing both historical data and high­fidelity measure­
ments, these data­driven models are designed to forecast aero­structural loads over short and
extended periods in a resource­efficient manner.
Benefits of Advanced Modeling Technologies in Wind Farm Management
The integration of advanced modeling technologies significantly enhances both the economic
and operational aspects of wind farm projects, offering substantial benefits to stakeholders in­
volved in the development, operation, and management, thereby reinforcing the strength and
stability of the wind energy industry. Some of the key advantages arising from these improve­
ments are outlined as follows:
• Bridging Knowledge Gaps: Enhancing Collaboration between Manufacturers and
Developers: Developers, who may lack detailed data from manufacturers, can use such
models for independent assessments and optimizations of their wind farms through direct
measurements and analyses. This empowers them to enhance performance monitoring
and asset management, fostering better collaboration between manufacturers and devel­
opers.
• Maintenance Strategies: Accurate models allow for the prediction of loads and stresses
on turbine components, facilitating the development of proactive and strategic mainte­
nance plans. This foresight can lead to significant cost savings and increased operational
uptime.
• Optimizing Operations: With a robust understanding of turbine performance, operators
can make informed decisions to adjust operations, ensuring turbines operate within op­
timal parameters, thereby maximizing energy output and minimizing unnecessary stress
on the system.
• Financial Planning: The predictive power of a detailed turbine model aids in financial
planning. By estimating future performance and maintenance schedules, budgets can be
set more accurately, avoiding unexpected expenditures.

Literature Review & Theoretical
Background
The field of wind energy has seen significant advancements in recent years. A critical part of
this development lies in understanding the forces of aerodynamics and structural integrity on
the wind energy structures under various conditions. This aspect is crucial as understanding
and accurately predicting the aero­structural loads can significantly enhance the operational
efficiency and longevity of wind turbines.
In the realm of wind energy forecasting, several statistical and machine learning methodolo­
gies have gained prominence due to their predictive accuracy and reliability. Some of the
most popular approaches employed in the wind energy field are: the Autoregressive Moving
Average (ARMA) and its extensions—Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA),
ARIMA with eXogenous inputs (ARIMAX), and Seasonal ARIMA (SARIMA)—have been
widely recognized for their effectiveness [9]. Additionally, Support Vector Machines (SVM)
have been acknowledged for their robust predictive capabilities, as noted by Xie et al (2023)[9],
and Markov Chain models, as detailed by [10] are also among the well­established approaches
utilized in this domain.
The literature reveals two significant gaps in the field of wind turbine performance prediction.
First, there is a deficiency in models that can process detailed, high­frequency data for accurately
predicting the structural and aerodynamic loads on turbines. This is a crucial oversight, as
most existing studies employ aggregated statistics, like 10­minute average wind values from
SCADA systems, which fail to capture the granular, time­sensitive characteristics of the data
[11],[12]. Second, while there has been considerable focus on forecasting wind power and
energy output, using deep learning algorithms such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
and Long Short­Term Memory (LSTMs) [13], [14], [15], [16] [17], the prediction of aero­
structural loads—a critical factor for the turbine’s integrity and operational efficiency—has not
received comparable attention. Addressing these gaps is essential for advancing the reliability
and effectiveness of wind turbine performance assessments.
This study aims to address the existing research gaps by leveraging the capabilities of Long
Short­Term Memory (LSTM) neural networks. Two distinct data­driven models are devel­
oped and scrutinized using this architecture, specifically for the purpose of forecasting high­
frequency load time series on wind turbines. The selection of LSTM is informed by its proven
efficacy in managing sequential datasets, which is essential for accurately predicting load pat­
terns and wind time series dynamics. The inherent ability of LSTM to remember long­term
dependencies makes it an ideal choice for this complex task, where past information is critical
for future prediction accuracy.
By utilizing a 100Hz dataset of 1000 simulations ( 3.1), these models are trained, examined, and
validated to predict high­resolution load time series, including ”Blade root moment Mx [kNm]”
and ”Tower base moment Mx [kNm]”. The models incorporate high­fidelity continuous wind
data as input which undergo a preprocessing step via two distinct dimensionality reduction
methods: Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT), opti­
mizing prediction speed. For clarity, these models will be termed the ”PCA­model” and the
”DCT­model” in subsequent references.

The turbulence data will be treated as exogenous input as they can impact the wind turbine
system without being influenced by it. The models will be architecturally designed to capture
the temporal dynamics of wind turbine operation, including the autoregressive behavior of load
sequences. Beyond these external factors, the models will be fine­tuned to predict critical op­
erational metrics of the turbine, such as ”Rotor azimuth [deg]”, ”Generator LSS torque [Nm],”
”Electrical power [W],” and ”Rotor rotational speed [rpm].” This approach is stemming from
the idea that, equipping the model with a comprehensive understanding of the turbine’s opera­
tional status allows it to detect correlations which are vital for grasping the multifaceted factors
dictating load behaviors, thereby enhancing prediction accuracy.
To elucidate the foundational concepts underlying the methodologies and technologies dis­
cussed, a comprehensive theoretical background of each aspect will be discussed in the sub­
sequent sections of this study.
2.1
Dimensionality Reduction Techniques
In this study two dimensionality reduction techniques were employed, highlighted in the EU­
funded (HORIZON 2020) project called HIPERWIND [18]. In this referenced study, the re­
searches mentioned these two dimensionality reduction techniques, namely Principal Compo­
nent Analysis (PCA), Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT). Both techniques offer unique advan­
tages in handling high­dimensional data, such as those encountered in wind turbine load sim­
ulations. Detailed descriptions and their associated mathematical representations are provided
below.
A third technique, Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) was considered as well in the ini­
tiation of this project, However, for the present problem, the methodologies of POD and PCA
exhibit a considerable overlap in both approach and outcomes. Consequently, given their simi­
larity and the desire to streamline the analysis, the application of POD was ultimately excluded
from this study .
2.1.1
Principal Component Analysis (PCA)
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a statistical method, invented in 1909 by Karl Pear­
son. Depending on the field of studies, the method can be also found as proper orthogonal
decomposition (POD) in mechanical engineering or singular value decomposition (SVD)[19].
This procedure employs linear transformations to project the original data vectors onto orthog­
onal axes/vectors, known as principal components. These components capture the predominant
variance within the dataset, optimizing its representation in a reduced dimensional space [20].
The following images provide a visual representations of the PCA method applied to a 3D
dataset. This illustrations showcase its reduction to a 2D plane. In the first visualization, Figure
2.1, the dataset is portrayed within a three­dimensional space, where individual data points
are distributed with respect to the x0, x1, and x2 axes. Each axis corresponds to a principal
component, with arrows colored in red, blue, and green indicating the direction of the first,
second, and third principal components respectively. Following the dimensionality reduction,
Figure 2.2 demonstrates the transformed dataset, now represented on a 2D scatter plot with axes
PC1 and PC2. This 2D representation captures the primary variances within the data while
simplifying its overall structure [21]

Covariance Matrix
The first step is to compute the covariance matrix to understand how each variable varies from
the others. The covariance between two variables X and Y is calculated as:

Cov(X, Y ) =

where:
• X and Y :
– These represent two random variables for which the covariance is being calculated.
They can be any two features or dimensions in a dataset.
• Cov(X, Y ):
– This represents the covariance between the two variables X and Y . It measures
the joint variability of the two variables. A positive covariance indicates that as X
increases, Y also tends to increase and vice versa. If negative, as X increases, Y
tends to decrease, and vice versa. A covariance close to zero suggests little to no
relationship between the variables.
• Xi and Yi:
– These represent the individual data points or observations for the variables X and
Y respectively. The subscript i denotes the specific observation number, ranging
from 1 to n, where n is the total number of observations.
• X and Y :
– These represent the means of the variables X and Y respectively.
• n:
– This denotes the total number of observations or data points in the dataset.
• p:
– Represents the number of variables or features in the dataset.
• p × p matrix:
– This denotes a matrix with p rows and p columns. The covariance matrix for a

2.2
Deep Learning networks ­ LSTM
A neural network ”learns” through an iterative process of adjusting the synaptic weights among
its neurons, enhancing the network’s ability to correlate input data with the expected output,
thereby improving predictive accuracy over time [27]. In this context, LSTM networks, which
belong to the broader family of neural networks, are specialized types of recurrent neural net­
works (RNNs) designed to process sequences of data while maintaining a form of memory. This
memory enables them to retain information over extended time periods, making them particu­
larly adept at tasks involving temporal dependencies, such as language translation, time series
prediction, and speech recognition[13].
LSTM architecture was introduced by Hochreiter & Schmidhuber [28] and were later refined
and popularized by many researchers in the field. LSTMs are explicitly designed to avoid
the long­term dependency problem of vanishing and exploding gradient and problems which
characterize the standard RNNs to learn to bridge time intervals in excess of 5­10 discrete time
steps between relevant input events and their associated outputs [28].
LSTM networks have been successfully applied in a wide array of domains, including but not
limited to natural language processing, speech recognition, and time­series prediction [29].
Their ability to capture temporal dependencies makes them a solid approach for modeling any
task that involves sequential data like wind and wind turbine load time series.
The core concept behind LSTMs is the cell state, which acts as a conveyor belt transferring
relevant information throughout the sequence of data [30]. The LSTM cell updates are usually
divided into four main parts, each involving several steps. Here are these parts expressed in
mathematical form: [5], [14], [31], [32]:

the cell state. It makes this decision using the sigmoid activation function (σ) as well as

a number between 0 and 1, where 0 means ” completely forget that” and 1 ”completely
keep that”. Last, Wf and bf are the weight matrix and forget­bias values accordingly.
The weight matrix determines how input and previous state information are combined
and transformed, while the bias shifts the transformation to help the network better fit the
data.

Architecture of LSTM model
The architecture of an LSTM model, plays a significant role in the ability if this specific neural
network to effectively capture temporal dependencies in sequence prediction tasks. This archi­
tecture directly influences how the model interprets input data and maps it to the target values
during training. Each component of an LSTM network, from input gates to memory cells, is
designed to recognize patterns across time, making LSTMs adept at handling tasks where the
sequence and timing of events are paramount[27].
An LSTM model architecture, typically begins with an input layer, which serves as the entry
point for the sequence data. It is responsible for receiving the input data and passing it on to the
subsequent layers. The input layer does not perform any computation or transformation on the
data, it merely passes them into the network (aka the next layer). Afterwards, the architecture
consist of one or more LSTM layers stacked together, that process the input sequence, perform­
ing complex computations, which includes the data from the input layer and also the recurrent
information from the previous time steps within the sequence. Each LSTM layer is composed
of multiple memory cells, where each cell includes the aforementioned gates. Following the
LSTM layers, the architecture usually incorporates one or more dense layers. In the context
of LSTM architecture, the term ”fully connected” means that every neuron in the dense layer
receives input from every LSTM unit defined in the previous layer. These dense layers serve to
interpret the features extracted from the sequence data by the LSTM layers providing a level of
abstraction that helps in mapping the processed input to the desired output. Last, the final layer
of the model’s structure, is the output layer which is typically a fully connected layer as well.
It can be configured to predict a single output or multiple simultaneous outputs and is capable
17
==PAGE:32==
of forecasting one or several steps into the future, depending on the task requirements.
Figure 2.9 depicts a standard LSTM network, illustrating the layered composition integral to its
function. The black arrow on the right indicates the data flow through the network. This archi­
tecture for example, might be configured to process three distinct inputs: wind speed, turbine
operational parameters, and air density, each represented by an input cell. Then, by performing
the corresponding computations, the model could for example predict three separate outcomes
(thus 3 output cells): aero­ structural loads of the turbine, operational efficiency metrics, and
schedules for predictive maintenance.

ber of units in the dense layers, and the design of the output layer, are called ”hyperparame­
ters” in the Deep Learning (DL) terminology, that can be tuned to optimize the model’s perfor­
mance[27]. However, it is important to be noted that, the architecture of the network might and
will vary based on the specific requirements of the model and the nature of the problem being
addressed. For this research, the structure of the network followed is the one described above,
apart from some declinations, which are referenced in the methodology section 3.4.

time step. This technique assists the model’s training process by directing it to learn
the correct sequence of outputs. It depends on the actual historical values rather than
its own potentially less accurate predictions, which is particularly beneficial during the
initial stages of training. In this context an open­loop prediction, implies that the model
predicts the output at time t based solely on the inputs up to time t, without leveraging
any of its preceding predictions. In essence, the model does not utilize its own predictions
to inform subsequent forecasts.

method is also known as the autoregressive forecasting. Here, the model uses its forecast
for time t as part of the input for making a prediction for time t+1. This closely replicates
real­world scenarios where actual future data points are unknown, thus the model must
rely solely on its own preceding forecasts to make future predictions. This approach
also facilitates an autonomous model which can couple or substitute the computational
expensive alternatives (HAWC2 simulation in this case).

outcomes. It is particularly useful for validating the model’s accuracy when historical data
is available, as it allows for a direct comparison between the model’s predictions and the
actual observed values. Moreover, the open loop forecasting approach can be leveraged
in the scenario of constructing virtual sensors, as explored in the study by Dimitrov [12].
Open­loop predictions enable the development of virtual sensors that can predict system
states or outputs by learning from a rich history of data. This is particularly advantageous
in scenarios where physical sensors may be impractical, unavailable, or cost­prohibitive
to implement. By utilizing a well­trained model on historical data, virtual sensors can pro­
vide high­fidelity monitoring and forecasting capabilities, augmenting or even replacing
the need for physical sensors in certain applications[12].

Training steps of learning network
Having delineated the various terms and concepts relevant to LSTM training, the key steps
involved in the training of a deep learning network are concisely summarized:
1. Initialization: Assign initial random weights to all neurons.
2. Forward Pass: Compute the output of the network for a given input.
3. Loss Calculation: Calculate the loss using a loss function, comparing the network output
with the true output.
4. Backpropagation: Propagate the loss backward through the network to compute the
gradient of the loss with respect to each weight.
5. Weight Update: Update the weights using an optimization algorithm (e.g., SGD, Adam)
in the direction that minimizes the loss.
6. Iteration: Repeat steps 2­5 for multiple epochs or until convergence.

The preprocessing of input data is a pivotal step in the neural network training workflow.
LSTM’s activation functions (eg sigmoid or tanh) that are sensitive to the magnitude of the in­

become unstable. Therefore, data standardization is an essential step in every machine learning
project[39]. According to the literature there several techniques used for scaling namely Z­score
Normalization, MinMax, MaxAbs, Robust Scaler and Quantile Transformer [39]. However, the
two methods tried and/or used from the author in this study are the Z­score normalization and
MinMax scaling.
Z­score normalization

the properties of a standard normal distribution with µ = 0 and σ = 1, where µ is the mean and
σ is the standard deviation. The standardized score of a sample x is calculated as:

3.3
Application steps of PCA & DCT
PCA
As explained in details in section 2.1.1, PCA is a statistical procedure that utilizes orthogonal
transformation to convert correlated features (variables) into a set of values of linearly uncor­
related features known as principal components. Following, is explained how PCA was imple­
mented pseudo­code wise for this project:
1. Load bin file: The turbulence box for the given set of dimensions (8192 × 32 × 32) is
loaded. This involves reading the box’s data from the .bin file.
2. Formatting the data: The 3­dimensional turbulence data is transformed. Initially stored
in a format of 8192 × 32 × 32, each plane of turbulence data is converted into a one­
dimensional format. This operation effectively flattens each 32 × 32 plane into a sin­
gle row with 1024 columns. Thus, the entire turbulence box is transformed into a two­
dimensional representation of 8192 × 1024, where each row corresponds to a flattened
plane of of the box.
3. Scaling the data: A scaling process as explained in 2.4 in performed. A previously saved
scaler is employed to scale the turbulence data. This scaling ensures that all features or
dimensions in the data have the same variance and are centered around zero, which is a
crucial step when using PCA.
4. Apply the PCA: The PCA algorithm is applied. The method is set to retain a fixed
number (50) of principal components, in this instance. These components capture the
most variance in the data information.
5. Final Dimension of Turbulence box: After the PCA transformation, the high­dimensional
turbulence data is reduced to a lower­dimensional space with a size of 8192 × 50. This
compact form retains the most informative features, which encompass the major variance
within the original dataset. Consequently, each row in this transformed dataset represents
a compressed version of the corresponding flattened 32×32 plane, now characterized by
the 50 most significant principal components that encapsulate the core dynamics of the
corresponding plane of the turbulence box.
Python libraries used
For the scaling process, the StandardScaler class from the sklearn.preprocessing module
is utilized [48]. Furthermore, the PCA transformation is conducted using the PCA class from the
sklearn.decomposition module [48].
DCT
The corresponding steps (loading, formatting, scaling, DCT application) executed to apply the
DCT are the same as described for the PCA method. The main difference in this approach,
is the number of frequency coefficients retained to explain an satisfying amount of energy for
each box.

Overall study’s Approach Synopsis
Hereby, is analyzed the approach followed in order to achieve the objectives of this study.
The two models (”PCA­model” and ”DCT­model”) were designed to ingest PCA and DCT­
treated turbulent boxes. Those turbulence data are served as exogenous input to the system,
since they are able to affect it but not get affected by it. In the training phase, along with the
exogenous inputs, the models were also given multiple wind turbine operational variables such
as, but not only: ”Rotor azimuth [deg]”, ”Blade pitch angle [deg]”, ”Generator LSS torque
[Nm]”, ”Electrical power [W]”, ”Rotor rotational speed [rpm]”. Those features, are crucial for
capturing the influence of turbine’s operational status on loads behavior, giving to the models a
more robust understanding of the underlying dynamics of the system.
During the models training phase, the ”teacher forcing” technique was employed. This involves
using the actual observed load values from previous time steps as input to predict the subsequent
load value, thereby helping the models to learn the correct mapping from the given sequence
to the output without depending on its own past predictions. Moreover, in order to leverage
the autoregressive nature of the LSTM network, during the training phase, the past values of
the requested loads (”Blade root moment Mx[kNm]” and ”Tower base moment Mx[kNm]”)
are given too. This enables the models to learn the autoregressive behaviors between past and
present load values, essential for accurate future predictions.
When testing the model (or simulating results) on unseen turbulence boxes the ”closed­loop”
approach was used. This means that once the model predicts a value at time t, that predicted
value was incorporated into the input data for predicting at timestep t + 1. This approach is
34
==PAGE:49==
closer to a real­world scenario where future true values are not available, and the model must
rely on its own predictions to forecast further into the future. Consequently, it establishes a data­
driven model for the specified wind turbine capable of efficiently and accurately predicting the
necessary aero­structural loads of the wind turbine.
Last, it is worth to be noted that the proposed methodology has the potential to enhance the
digital twin concept, where a virtual counterpart of the physical wind turbine system provides
real­time insights. This digital mirroring facilitates advanced diagnostics and analytics, paving

and these operations can take significantly longer as the size of the matrices increase.

4.1.4
Dimensionality Reduction Techniques: Final Remarks
In summarizing the dimensionality reduction techniques applied to the analysis of turbulence
boxes before their introduction to LSTM neural networks, PCA, DCT offer unique advantages
and limitations based on the nature of the data and the specific requirements of the study.
PCA exhibited and strong advantage in this study as it was able to reconstruct the original data
with fewer components than DCT, minimizing reconstruction errors such as MSE, MAE, and
RMSE. This suggests that PCA is highly adept at capturing the most significant variance within
the data, which is crucial for the LSTM’s ability to make accurate predictions. The fidelity of
data reconstruction with minimal components is especially important in complex systems where
the essence of the data needs to be preserved after dimensionality reduction.
In contrast, DCT’s main advantage lies in its computational efficiency. It is markedly faster
than PCA, which is a considerable benefit in scenarios where processing speed is critical, such
as real­time analysis or when dealing with very large datasets where computational resources
are a limiting factor. The speed of DCT might make it suitable for initial data processing stages
or applications where the highest level of reconstruction fidelity is not necessary.
In summary, PCA’s ability to efficiently compress data while retaining essential information
makes it highly effective for tasks where the quality of data reconstruction is critical for the
success of subsequent processing, such as the precise predictions required by LSTM networks.
However, when the computational speed is the overriding concern, and the trade­off against
precision can be justified, DCT emerges as a viable alternative. The choice between PCA and
DCT should, therefore, be informed by the specific requirements of the task at hand, balancing
the need for accuracy against the need for speed.

Conclusions
Before Conclusions ­ Disclaimer
When initially, embarked on this research journey, the predominant hypothesis was that the
dimensionality reduction techniques employed (PCA, DCT) would significantly affect LSTM
performance by streamlining turbulent data. This belief stemmed from the recognition that re­
ducing the complex dimensionality of turbulent boxes was critical for the forecasting accuracy
of the neural network. However, as the research progressed, a pivotal realization emerged:
while the dimensionality reduction technique indeed plays a role in data preprocessing, the
overarching determinant of the model’s success lies primarily within the architecture and con­
figuration of the LSTM itself. Throughout the procedure of this study, it became evident that
architectural decisions, such as the number of layers, the configuration of units within these lay­
ers, the scaling of input data, and other hyperparameters, plays in a driving role in the predictions
of the modes. This realization, combined with the author’s introductory­stage understanding of
neural networks, led to the recognition that the final results might not fully meet the original
intentions described in this projects.
Nevertheless, the author wishes to express that this endeavor has been a valuable learning
experience in machine learning, and particularly in its application to the wind energy
field. This experience has underscored the significant promise that lies at the intersection
of these two sectors, showcasing the transformative capabilities of integrating machine
learning with wind energy systems.

Final Remarks
Two methods of data­driven model construction, namely the ”PCA model” and the ”DCT
model,” utilizing LSTM architecture, were proposed in this study to predict aero­structural
loads on the IEA 15MW wind turbine design, specifically focusing on the ”Blade root moment
Mx[kNm]” and ”Tower base moment Mx[kNm]”. Several prediction scenarios were attempted,
involving open­loop and close­loop predictions in a single and multiple datasets.
Summarizing this research, the primary takeaways and consequences stemming from the cre­
ation and examination of the ”PCA model” and ”DCT model” with LSTM networks for fore­
casting aerostructural loads on the IEA 15MW wind turbine configuration can be summarized
as follows:
• The dimensionallity analysis performed indicated a strong advantage in the PCA method
when it comes to the retention of the most variance with the least components. In contrast,
DCT’s main advantage lies in its computational efficiency yielding a time reduction of
approximately 92.59%.
• Both the PCA­based and DCT­based models demonstrated robust capabilities in cap­
turing the dynamics of a test set derived from a single turbulence box using open­loop
prediction. This performance suggests that both methods of model construction are not
only suitable but also potentially robust for the development of data­driven models. Such
models are integral in the creation of virtual sensors, which are tools that can infer mea­
surements from available data in cases where direct sensor readings may not be feasible
or economically viable.
• In the closed­loop prediction scenario, operating under identical operational conditions,
the DCT model demonstrated a capability to partially capture the primary trend and the
overall range of the requested aero­structural loads. This outcome suggests that the DCT­
based approach is on the correct trajectory. Further refinements to the model could po­
tentially unlock its full predictive power, indicating a promising direction for subsequent
research and development efforts. This preliminary success lays a foundation for the
DCT model to evolve into a more precise tool for load prediction in wind turbines, with
the prospect of contributing to enhanced control strategies and optimized design in the
future.
The primary objective set forth by this author was to architect the models in a fashion that would
enable autonomous operation during the prediction phase. Furthermore, the aim was to facilitate
high­resolution forecasting of the imposed loads on unseen wind field conditions, adopting an
autoregressive approach.
However, it must be acknowledged that the initial intentions to successfully develop wind tur­
bine data­driven models, capable of functioning as comprehensive load simulators, were not
fully realized. This shortfall can be attributed to the author’s nascent experience in the intricate
domain of deep learning, which presented unforeseen complexities in realizing the envisioned
autonomous operational capabilities of the models. Specifically, the challenge lay in the effort
of optimally tuning the LSTM networks to handle the intricate temporal dynamics inherent in
wind turbine load data.
Despite all the above, this project, sets strong basis in the field of wind turbine load predic­
tion and paves the way for further research and development. The methodology and insights
gleaned from this endeavor lay the groundwork for development of advancing data­driven wind
52
==PAGE:67==
turbine models. Moreover, it underscores the potential and necessity for novel approaches in
the integration of deep learning techniques within the operational frameworks of wind energy
management.

C
Appendix C
Introduction
In Appendix C, the outcomes of the Principal Component Analysis (PCA) performed on a com­
prehensive set of 1000 turbulence boxes are presented. These boxes, with their original dimen­
sions of 8192x32x32, encapsulate the spatio­temporal dynamics of the velocity fields across
the x, y, and z directions. The core objective of this focused analysis was to understand the
underlying structures and assess the relative importance of each velocity component within the
turbulence boxes. Furthermore, this examination serves to justify one of the two reasons for
selecting only the u velocity component for the scope of this project
Results
The application of the PCA method yielded an explained variance ratio for each velocity com­
ponent within the dataset. As depicted in the plots of multiple random sets of turbulence boxes,
the analysis revealed a distinct pattern, where the first principal component predominantly cap­
tures above 50% of the total variance for each turbulence box, which is primarily attributed to
the u­direction (or x­direction). Subsequent scrutiny of the PCA components further indicates
that the second principal component is most reflective of the v­direction, and the third principal
component corresponds to the w­direction, respectively.
Conclusion
Given this finding, it became evident that the u component carried significant information about
the turbulence box, arguably more than the other components. Thus, for the sake of simplifying
the streamline of the study and focus on the most information­rich aspect of the turbulence
fields, it was decided to concentrate solely on the u component for the subsequent analyses.
