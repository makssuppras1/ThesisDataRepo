Abstract
An important challenge in multi-agent systems is to collaboratively reach a common goal
in settings where knowledge and capabilities are asymmetrically distributed among the
acting agents. Without a centralized coordinating instance or the ability to negotiate
a plan, agents must find implicitly coordinated plans. Up to this point, approaches for
implicit coordination lack the ability to exploit observations of other agents‚Äô actions. In
contrast, agents could infer additional knowledge based on how other agents have acted
in the past and the assumption that they behave optimally. This type of reasoning evokes
the game-theoretic concept of forward induction. In this project, we build on previous
work in the context of epistemic planning under implicit coordination, and propose a new
solution concept that incorporates forward induction in the planning algorithm. We show
that including forward induction reasoning removes the possibility for infinite executions
in some planning tasks and therefore, we are able to guarantee a solution for tasks that
previous approaches for implicit coordination failed to solve. Moreover, we implement the
proposed planning algorithm with forward induction and observe that it is able to solve
certain tasks more efficiently than a baseline planner without forward induction.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Cooperation is arguably one of the hallmarks of human intelligence. On a regular basis, we
engage in tasks that require collaborating with others in order to achieve a common goal.
One of the striking features of human collaboration is that the goal can often be achieved
with little to no communication among the agents. For example, consider the scenario
where two people, Alice and Bob, are involved in the relatively simple task of carrying
objects from one room to another: Alice may see that Bob has his hands occupied, so
she will hold the door open for him to walk through. Or consider, instead, the task of
cleaning a living room: if Alice sees Bob grabbing a broom, she will infer that he wants to
clean the floor, so she will collaborate by lifting up the carpet. With apparent ease, the
agents are able to infer each others‚Äô intentions and coordinate without explicitly agreeing
on what to do.
Parting from these examples, we identify two overarching ways of coordinating a plan
towards the goal: through explicit coordination, which is achieved through requests, com-
mitments to plans and, in general, negotiating what actions are to be performed by each
agent; and through implicit coordination, achieved by observing how others act and mak-
ing inferences based on the assumption that agents behave cooperatively. Due to the
ubiquity of cooperative planning tasks, it is important to equip artificial agents with the
necessary capabilities to foster coordination in multi-agent systems. In this work, we are
concerned with equipping artificial agents with the ability to find implicitly coordinated
plans. Under this notion of implicit coordination, agents are required to reason individ-
ually about the task at hand and autonomously compute a plan. We believe implicit
coordination can be particularly useful in situations where there is no centralized entity
capable of coordinating the agents, or where there does not exist a common channel of

communication, such as human-robot collaboration, where coordinating through detailed
instructions from the human can be inconvenient.
A notorious challenge in automated planning is that, in many multi-agent settings, knowl-
edge about the task and different capabilities are distributed asymmetrically among the
acting agents. With no centralized coordinating instance or without the possibility to ex-
plicitly agree on a joint plan, agents must individually reason about their own knowledge
and the knowledge and possible contributions of other agents.
To model this reason-
ing, we rely on the approach of epistemic planning presented by Bolander and Andersen
(2011). By using concepts from epistemic logic, epistemic planning enriches the planning
language with epistemic notions, including knowledge and beliefs. As we will see in subse-
quent sections, epistemic planning is suitable for handling the uncertainty brought about
by asymmetric knowledge distribution in planning domains.
The planning notion we described above, in which agents must autonomously find im-
plicitly coordinated plans to planning tasks where knowledge and capabilities are asym-
metrically distributed, was first formalized by Engesser et al. (2017), and further detailed
by Bolander et al. (2018); Engesser et al. (2020); Bolander et al. (2021). In this previ-
ous approach to implicit coordination, agents decide how to act on a given state without
taking into account the previous actions from other agents, that is, agents choose their
next action solely based on the current state they are in. However, in certain scenarios,
agents might be able to infer additional knowledge from observing how other agents have
acted in the past, and the assumption of their optimality. This type of reasoning evokes
the game-theoretic concept of forward induction. Although there is no unique definition
of forward induction across the game theory literature, it intuitively describes a way of
reasoning in which players try to find the most plausible explanation to (unanticipated)
moves of their opponents.

Consider the planning task depicted in Figure 1.1, presented by Engesser et al. (2020),
where two agents, Alice and Bob, must bring the lever to either the leftmost or the

rightmost position, which are the two goal positions. Alice can only pull the lever to
the left, and Bob can only pull the lever to the right. The challenge is the following:
Alice is only certain about the leftmost position being a goal configuration, although she
considers it possible that the rightmost position is also a goal. Symmetrically, Bob is
only certain about the rightmost position being the goal, but has uncertainty about the
leftmost position also being a goal. If agents were to act optimally, they would try to
pull the lever in their own direction, since they would try to act towards achieving the
goal that they are certain about. As such, Alice‚Äôs plan would consist only on pulling left,
and Bob‚Äôs plan would consist of pulling right. At execution time, these plans might result
in the infinite sequence (left, right, left, right...), which does not result in the goal being
achieved.
If, instead, agents were allowed to infer additional knowledge from the other agent‚Äôs
actions (i.e. through forward induction), we could have, for example, Alice learning that
the rightmost position is in fact a goal after Bob pulls the lever, as otherwise Bob would
not have made this move, assuming he is an optimally acting agent and wants to reach
the goal in the fewest number of steps. After removing her uncertainty about the goal,
Alice will then let Bob finish the task by pulling the lever all the way to the right. With
this type of reasoning, actions might then constitute implicit announcements or signals
to the other agents.
Bolander et al. (2018) and Engesser et al. (2020) suggest that incorporating this type of
reasoning in planning will help solve a wider range of cooperative tasks under implicit co-
ordination. The objective of this project is to directly address this proposition and redefine
planning under implicit coordination in the context of forward induction, by continuing
ongoing research in developing a planner that incorporates this type of reasoning.
Our main contribution is a new solution concept for planning under implicit coordination
that integrates forward induction reasoning in planning, allowing agents to infer additional
knowledge from the assumption of optimality of other agents‚Äô previous actions.
This
solution directly builds on the approach from Engesser et al. (2017); Bolander et al.
(2018) and Bolander et al. (2021), and can therefore be seen as a direct continuation of
their work on implicit coordination. An important contribution from this project is the
implementation of the proposed planner with forward induction, which is tested against a
baseline planner that does not include this type of reasoning, in variants of the task from
Figure 1.1. The results from these experiments show that forward induction is effective
for overcoming some of the limitations of previous approaches for implicit coordination.

Chapter 2
Related Work
Although the idea of finding solutions to cooperative planning tasks through implicit
coordination in multi-agent systems has been considered an interesting research topic for
a number of years, implicit coordination strategies have been mainly applied to the field
of robotics. For example, Isik et al. (2007) describe an approach to enable a team of
robots to coordinate implicitly (without negotiation), and apply this solution in a typical
coordination task from robotics: regaining ball possession in soccer. Also in the context
of robotic soccer, Pagello et al. (1999) present an approach to implicit coordination where
the only allowed form of communication is observing the behavior of other robots. Their
approach is based on two major components: utility prediction models, and knowledge
of the states of others, which allows robots to reason about other agents‚Äô belief states
and take their perspective. Anderson and Papanikolopoulos (2008) argue that explicit
coordination methods for team search do not scale to large robot teams due to limited
communication bandwidth and computational constraints, and they present an implicit
coordination search strategy focused on lowering messages between robots and reducing
interference.
The notion of taking other agents‚Äô perspectives is one of the crucial components that plan-
ners for implicit coordination have in common. Perhaps one of the earliest appearances
of explicit perspective-shifting in the literature of multi-agent planning is in the work by
Konolige and Nilsson (1980), where they describe a planning system in which agents are
able to represent other agents‚Äô beliefs, plans, goals and introspections about other agents.
In general, understanding how autonomous agents might model the mental states of other
agents has been a major topic of research in Artificial Intelligence. However, it is not
until recently that a more general notion of achieving implicit coordination in multi-agent

systems through perspective-shifts has been formally characterized. Engesser et al. (2017)
were the first to define the formal framework for implicit coordination that we directly
build upon: a decentralized planning approach where each agent must reason individ-
ually about the planning task and has to autonomously decide how and when to act.
They achieve this by building upon the epistemic planning framework by Bolander and
Andersen (2011), thereby using search in the space of epistemic states to find a solution.
Instead of eliminating all communicative abilities of the agents, the approach from En-
gesser et al. (2017) requires communication to be modelled as epistemic actions, which
are considered, together with ontic actions, during planning. This captures the intuition
that communication constitutes an action in and of itself, and even purely ontic actions
can play a communicative role as they may change the knowledge agents have about the
world. This way, instead of separating the stages of negotiation and communication from
the planning stage, communication is intuitively embedded in planning as an action itself.
Two of the key contributions from the work by Engesser et al. (2017) are: (1) the formal
characterization of perspective-shifts and their explicit incorporation in planning, which
is necessary for agents to reason about the possible contributions of other agents towards
the goal; and (2) the formal derivation of the notion of an implicitly coordinated plan
from the natural conditions that it should satisfy. As our solution directly builds on their
approach, we recapitulate these definitions in Chapter 3.
The approach by Engesser et al. (2017) was further detailed by Bolander et al. (2018).
In their work, Bolander et al. (2018) study how the agent types affect the successfulness
of the execution of the computed plan. They define lazy agents as those who have a
preference against using their own actions, and plan using other agents‚Äô actions instead.
They show that the problem with planning with lazy agents is that the execution can
easily result in deadlocks, as agents might infinitely wait for each other to act. A solution
to deadlocks is to have (naively) eager agents, which have a preference for their own
actions. This type of agents is called naively eager, since it turns out that their eagerness
might work against other agents‚Äô efforts towards the goal, and could even lead to livelocks
(infinite executions). Bolander et al. (2018) use an example equivalent to the lever task
from Figure 1.1 to illustrate the problem with this type of agents, which has briefly been
presented in the Introduction: if both Alice and Bob are naively eager agents, they would
both generate plans where, at every state, they try to pull the lever in their own direction,
regardless of their notion of how far they are to the goal position. The execution of these
plans could result in the sequence of actions that infinitely alternates between pulling left
and right, which is not a successful execution.

To address the problem of infinite executions, Bolander et al. (2018) define optimally eager
agents as those who always try to reach the goal in the smallest number of steps. As such,
agents should come up with optimal plans relative to their cost, which is defined as the
worst-case execution length. However, due to partial observability, agents might assign
different costs to the same plan and therefore, they are optimal with respect to their own
subjective cost of a plan. As it turns out, in non-uniformly observable settings, optimally
eager agents cannot prevent infinite executions. As mentioned in the Introduction, in the
example from Figure 1.1, optimally eager agents would produce plans where each pulls the
lever in their own direction, as they are optimizing for the goal position they are certain
about, which again could result in the infinite execution consisting of alternating between
pulling left and right.
Engesser et al. (2020) focus on tackling the problem of infinite executions on the plan-
ning task level, by allowing only one agent to try to act at a time. They achieve so by
introducing a token, with the idea that the agent who has the token is the only one al-
lowed to act, and the one to decide which agent will have the token next. Their proposed
that tokenization prevents infinite executions, and that their approach can generalize to
given planning tasks. They briefly show how tokenization prevents livelocks in the lever
planning task: initially, both agents try to take the token. The agent that obtains the
token ‚ÄĒwhich is decided non-deterministically‚ÄĒ is then able to pull the lever all the way
to its goal position as he keeps the token, preventing the other agent from sabotaging his
progress. As such, the execution is finished successfully. Both Bolander et al. (2018) and
Engesser et al. (2020) propose incorporating forward induction in planning as a major
subject for future work.
An additional improvement on the epistemic planning approach for implicit coordination
from Engesser et al. (2017) is the introduction of the notion of indistinguishability of epis-
temic states based on bisimulation, proposed by Bolander et al. (2021). In the approach
from Engesser et al. (2017), a solution to a planning task is a policy mapping states
to actions, which is required to be uniform, implying that agents must make the same
choice in states they cannot distinguish between. Originally, the approach by Engesser
et al. (2017) does not provide a precise formal definition of indistinguishability between
epistemic states, and it is therefore unclear how to formally check that the condition of
uniformity is satisfied. Bolander et al. (2021) propose that the uniformity condition should
be based on modal equivalence of states (i.e. states satisfy the same set of formulas), and
provide a novel partition refinement algorithm for computing unique representatives of
sets of indistinguishable states, which they include in their planner for finding implicitly
coordinated policies.

One of the applications of the multi-agent planning approach for implicit coordination
from Engesser et al. (2017) has been on a variant of multi-agent path finding (MAPF),
where the agents have uncertainty about the destination of other agents.
This path-
finding problem, called MAPF under destination uncertainty, or simply MAPF/DU, has
been studied by Nebel et al. (2019). In their paper, Nebel et al. (2019) drop the usual
assumption of MAPF that all movements are coordinated centrally before execution and
that the agents‚Äô destinations are common knowledge, and they analyze whether agents
are capable of coordinating their movements by observing the movements of other agents,
i.e. through implicit coordination. One of the key concepts in the approach from Nebel
et al. (2019) is replanning, which is used in order to account for unanticipated actions
from other agents. Moreover, they introduce the notion of i-strong branching plans, which
directly corresponds to what has been termed implicitly coordinated policies by Engesser
et al. (2017).
Nebel et al. (2019) suggest that, in solving MAPF/DU instances, taking into account
information from the action history in order to prune possible destinations might be
helpful for coordinating implicitly. Again, the use of forward induction in planning is
seen as a potential improvement to the existing approach for implicit coordination. Nebel
(2019) expands on the idea of using forward induction in solving MAPF/DU problems,
and suggests that agents could make use of observations made during execution to infer
additional information about the intentions of other agents. The author suggests that,
without the ability to make this type of inferences, plans might be longer than necessary
or some instances might even be unsolvable. Instead, by trying to explain the movements
of other agents by assuming that they act optimally (they try to come up with the shortest
possible plan) planning agents would be able to exclude some of the possible destinations
of other agents and thereby solve some otherwise unsolvable MAPF/DU instances.
To illustrate how making inferences from observations can help solve MAPF/DU instances,
Nebel (2019) presents the instance displayed in Figure 2.1, where the goal of a circular
agent C is to go to the cell marked by the solid circle, and the goal of square agent S is
to go to the cell marked with the solid square. The circular agent, however, is not certain
about the goal for the square agent, and considers the cell marked with an empty square
a possible destination for agent S. Similarly, the square agent considers the cell marked
with an empty circle as a possible destination for agent C. If agent S tries to solve this
instance, he will move to the bottom cell to allow agent C to move to any of her possible
destinations. However, after agent C moves to cell 2 (her actual destination), she cannot
guarantee that agent S can reach both of his possible destinations. As this situation is
symmetric, agents cannot guarantee a solution for this instance if they are not able to

Nebel (2019) argues that, if agents tried to explain the moves of the other agent, after
agent S moves to the bottom cell, agent C might rightly conclude that the actual goal for
agent S is cell 4, as otherwise agent S would have immediately moved to cell 1, assuming
he is acting optimally with respect to the shortest plan.
Nebel (2019) discusses how to generalize this kind of reasoning in the specialized setting
of MAPF/DU by using the notion of safe abduction, that is, making inferences from the
best explanation to an observation, that cannot lead to excluding destinations that are
still possible. First, they define an observation that needs an explanation as a sequence of
movements by some agent i. One could then try to explain an observation by generating
shortest plans to each of the possible destinations of agent i, with and without the observed
sequence as a prefix of the plan. If the plan to some possible destination vj of agent i
without the observation as a prefix is shorter than the plan to j with the observation as a
prefix, one could conclude that agent i cannot rationally try to reach destination vj. The
ideas presented by Nebel (2019) are meant to serve as a starting point for developing a
notion of implicit coordination that also takes into account the observation about actions
from other agents.
As mentioned in the Introduction, there does not seem to exist a formal definition of
forward induction in the game theory literature. Battigalli and Siniscalchi (2002) refer to
"forward induction reasoning" without providing a specific definition, though they propose
that this type of reasoning is motivated by the assumption that unanticipated strategic
events result from purposeful choices. Therefore, if a player observes an unexpected move,
she should try to come up with the most plausible explanation to this move and revise her
beliefs accordingly. Govindan and Wilson (2009) motivate the use of forward induction
in games by arguing that "other players‚Äô beliefs should allow interpretation of a player‚Äôs
actions as credible signals of private information or future intentions."

With the literature reviewed so far, it appears that forward induction can be particularly
useful for recognizing other agents‚Äô intentions and reducing the uncertainty about the goal
of the planning task, as illustrated by the lever example and the MAPF/DU instance.
There is a body of research dedicated to studying the plan recognition problem, where
the aim is to understand observed actions to attribute intentions, beliefs and goals to
the actor, and organizing the actions into a plan structure (Schmidt et al., 1978). More
recently, there has been specific interest in narrowing the gap between plan- and goal
recognition and planning (Ramirez and Geffner, 2009; Freedman and Zilberstein, 2017).
Work in this area can be seen as related work, as the aim is to identify the goals that
provide an explanation to the observed action sequences, similar to the notion of forward
induction. While incorporating forward induction in the planning algorithm can be seen
as a potential solution to integrating plan and goal recognition into a planning mechanism,
the approach we present in this paper does not rely on the formalism of the goal recognition
problem, but it is instead situated in the context of epistemic planning.

Chapter 3
Theoretical Background
In the context of planning under implicit coordination, we are interested in finding solu-
tions to planning tasks where capabilities and knowledge about the world are asymmet-
rically distributed among the agents. For agents to successfully plan and act in uncertain
environments, we need a framework that allows them to reason about first and higher-
order knowledge of other agents. For this reason, we approach this problem through the
theoretical framework of epistemic planning under implicit coordination proposed by En-
gesser et al. (2017), and further detailed by Bolander et al. (2018). Their approach builds
on Dynamic Epistemic Logic (DEL), which has proven to be suitable for planning in
uncertain environments and reasoning about other agents‚Äô perspectives. Moreover, their
framework proposes finding policies as solutions to planning tasks (as opposed to sequen-
tial plans) in order to handle the uncertainty about plans produced by each individual
agent.
In the following, we recapitulate the definitions of DEL, which we use as a formal frame-
work for planning. The definitions presented in this section are taken almost verbatim
from Engesser et al. (2017).
The definitions of epistemic languages, epistemic states and epistemic actions provided in
this section are relative to a given finite set of agents A and a given finite set of atomic
propositions P.

Chapter 4
Implicit Coordination with Forward
Induction
Existing approaches for implicit coordination systematically identify isomorphic (Bolander
et al., 2018) or bisimilar (Bolander et al., 2021) states. As such, policies are assumed to
be history-independent functions of the current state, where agents do not consider the
past moves of other agents. However, additional knowledge could be inferred from the
assumption of optimality of other agents‚Äô previous actions.
This type of reasoning is
similar to what has been termed in game theory as forward induction, where players try
to find the most plausible explanation to (unanticipated) moves of their opponents. We
borrow the term of forward induction from game theory, and incorporate it in cooperative
planning as a way of inferring knowledge from other agents‚Äô past actions.
In this section, we present a new approach for solving cooperative planning tasks under
implicit coordination that uses this notion of forward induction, and show how this new
solution concept is able to handle some of the issues of previous approaches for implicit
coordination. The underlying idea of our approach is to use the history of actions to prune
the search tree during planning, thereby removing uncertainty for the agents.
4.1
Preliminary Assumptions
For forward induction to be an effective solution for implicit coordination tasks, there has
to be a common understanding about the universal principles of the agents‚Äô rationality.
One could argue that, in planning, a rational agent is one that chooses the action that

makes the goal reachable in the fewest number of steps, given his knowledge about the

Although giving a formal definition of rationality is outside the scope of this
project, we try to describe the type of reasoning we believe agents should be able to do
in the context of planning with forward induction. To illustrate the notion of rationality
we attempt to capture with our solution, we build up on the lever example from previous
sections:
Example 8. Recall that, in the lever example, Alice is certain that the leftmost position
is a goal configuration of the lever, but she does not know whether the rightmost position
is a goal configuration. Symmetrically, Bob is certain about the rightmost position being
a goal configuration, but not about the leftmost position. If Alice observes Bob pulling
the lever to the right, she must be able to infer that the rightmost position is in fact
also a goal configuration, otherwise Bob would not have pulled right, assuming he is a
rationally acting agent and does not wish to sabotage the collective efforts to achieve the
goal. Therefore, if Bob pulls right, she should no longer consider the world where only l is
true possible. Similarly, if Alice pulls the lever to the left, Bob should no longer consider
possible the world where only the rightmost position is the goal, since otherwise Alice
would not have pulled the lever. By observing how the other agent acted, Alice and Bob
are able to gain knowledge about the actual goal configuration.
As explained previously, planning in the context of implicit coordination is done in a
decentralized manner, which means each agent must independently compute a plan. Each
individual agent plans from its own local perspective: for every state s and action i : a,
each planning agent j takes the associated local state sj and action (i : a)j. Note that
since the "acting agent" is i, the planning agent j should take i‚Äôs perspective when checking
whether the action is applicable. The notion of action applicability used for expanding
the search tree is explained in more detail in Section 4.2.
To facilitate the development and the presentation of our solution concept, we make the
following simplifying assumptions about the actions in our planning domain:
1. First, we assume all epistemic actions to be globally deterministic, that is, no
more than one event is applicable in each possible world.

Our forward induction planner takes several steps in finding a solution, which can roughly
be summarized as follows: First, we generate the search tree up to a certain cutoff depth.
Then, we compute costs for worlds and actions in a bottom-up pass through the tree.
Next, iterate on the cutoff depth by evaluating whether we should continue expanding
the tree based on the costs at the initial state. Once we are done expanding the tree,
we proceed to prune edges and worlds based on the costs computed in the previous step.
The pruned tree then induces the policy that is the solution to the planning task. In the
following sections, we explain each of these steps in detail.

4.5
Pruning the search tree
Pruning the search tree is the crucial step at which agents are able to reduce their un-
certainty through forward induction. The idea is to use the costs computed previously
to prune edges from actions i : a ‚ąąA that a "rational" agent i would not apply at world
w if such action was not optimal in the sense that executing i : a would lead to a state
that is further away from the goal. By pruning an edge (w, (w, e)) for some action, we
are then able to remove the world (w, e) from the set of possible worlds of the successor
state. This allows agents to learn that such world cannot be the true world if the state is
reached through the action of the pruned edge.
Pruning is done in a top-down traversal of the tree. Let k be the maximum depth of the
tree at the final iteration of tree expansion. Then, at at depth 0 ‚Č§j ‚Č§k of the tree, we
prune worlds and edges as follows:
1. If j > 0, prune every world w if its incoming edge is pruned, and prune all outgoing
edges of w.
2. If j < k, compute the agent cost for each agent i ‚ąąA at world w with actions i : a
applied at world w:
cost(w, i) = max
v‚ąľiw cost(v)
3. Then, for every outgoing edge from w denoting an action i : a for agent i, prune the
edge (w, i : a) if cost(w, i : a) > cost(w, i).
The agent cost of a world w for an agent i is, intuitively, the worst-case cost given the
worlds that agent i cannot distinguish from w. Then, from the actions that i can apply
on w, the planning agent should exclude those that an optimally acting agent i would not
have performed, i.e. those that have a cost higher than the agent cost of w for i. Note that,
when computing cost(w, i) at step 2, we only consider those worlds v indistinguishable
from w for i in the set of worlds remaining after the pruning done in step 1. The agent
cost for agent i is used for pruning the actions of agent i applied at a given world; since
no actions are applied at leaf-worlds, it is not necessary compute these costs in worlds at
the final cutoff depth k. As worlds are being removed from the set of possible worlds, the
pruning stage modifies the states in S. Pruning a search tree T results in a subtree T ‚Ä≤ of
T, as worlds and edges are removed from T.

4.7
A revised approach to cost assignment and prun-
ing
As shown in Example 18, our first planning approach fails to handle some tasks where
agents capable of forward induction are expected to find a solution. In this section, we
redefine how we assign costs to worlds and edges and how pruning is done based on this
new notion of costs, in order to overcome the shortcomings of our previous approach.
The underlying idea remains the same: we expand the search tree just as before, iterating
on the cutoff depth after assigning cost. Then, we prune the tree based on the costs
computed and finally extract the policy directly from the pruned tree. What is new in
this revised approach is that we keep track of additional costs that convey additional
information.

The underlying idea of pruning remains the same for this new approach: we prune an

perspective on the costs.

Chapter 5
Experiments
We have implemented a planner following the methods described in Section 4.2 through
Section 4.6 (and summarized in Algorithms 1, 2 and 3), capable of generating implic-
itly coordinated policies using forward induction. The planner has been implemented
in the C# programming language, and its source code is available at https://github.
com/victorianunezr/implicit-coordination. We refer to this planner as the forward
induction planner.
Moreover, we implement the planner described in Algorithm 2 in Bolander et al. (2021),
but our implementation leaves out the partition refinement step, and therefore skips bisim-
ulation contraction. This planner is based on AND-OR graph search, and does not incor-
porate forward induction reasoning. A caveat related to this particular planner is that it
requires for goal formulas to be common knowledge formulas so that agents stop acting
once the goal has been reached. Therefore, we modify the planning task when used by this
planner, by making the goal formula a common knowledge formula, and by adding public
announcements to the action library. We refer to this planner as the baseline planner.
These two planners utilize our own implementation of Dynamic Epistemic Logic. Our
implementation of DEL provides high-level models of DEL concepts such as epistemic
states, event models, epistemic actions and product update. Furthermore, we are able
to perform DEL-related operations, such as evaluating epistemic formulas, checking for
action applicability, and computing perspective-shifted states. The module containing all
DEL-related models and operations can be found under the directory src/DEL/ in the
code base.
We conduct a series of experiments on instances of the lever planning task defined in

Summary and Outlook
We have presented a new solution concept that incorporates forward induction reasoning
in planning under implicit coordination. We have showed how forward induction removes
some of the shortcomings of previous approaches for implicit coordination, such as the
possibility for infinite executions resulting from eager agents acting under partial observ-
ability. More specifically, we have seen how our first proposed solution is able to remove
the uncertainty the agents have about the goal in the lever planning task from Example
5, where the possibility for livelocks is unavoidable without forward induction reasoning.
By observing how the other agent acts and trying to explain this observation based on the
agent‚Äôs optimality, they are able to make inferences about the actual goal configuration.
By removing uncertainty about the goal, agents then know that, in some cases, the opti-
mal move is to let the other agent finish the task and are therefore able to avoid infinite
executions.
We provide an implementation of this first approach to including forward induction in
planning, and observe the length of the executions of policies generated by this planner for
variants of the lever task. From the experiments, we are able to conclude that the forward
induction planner we propose is in fact effective in endowing agents with the ability to
infer additional knowledge from observations, as evidenced by the execution lengths when
compared to a baseline planner. Therefore, a positive result is that the presented solution
concept with forward induction has demonstrated to be useful, in practice, for solving
certain epistemic planning tasks with distributed knowledge under implicit coordination.
However, this first proposed solution is not able to generalize to given planning tasks
that one could expect to solve with forward induction. Our first approach works well for

planning tasks in which agents have certainty about the cost of their actions with respect

a local perspective, some information is lost when propagating the costs to states where
other agents might know about the global costs of these actions. For instance, both agents
in the lever task from Example 5 know that pulling the lever in their own direction would
lead to a goal configuration, they just had uncertainty about the other end being also a
goal. Therefore, they are able to assign a cost to their actions from their local perspective
that corresponds to the objective (global) costs for these actions.
To overcome this limitation, we sketch out a new approach to assigning costs and pruning,
where agents are able to assign costs to actions both from a local and from a global
perspective. With this workaround, we are able to achieve forward induction reasoning in
planning tasks where the original approach did not produce the desired result, as shown
with the variant of the lever task introduced in Example 18.
In the process of gestating this solution concept, we made several simplifying assumptions
in order to facilitate the technical developments and to ensure the generated policies
satisfy the desired properties. For example, we assumed all actions in the planning task
to be globally deterministic, uniformly observable and universally applicable. Although
we do not believe out solution is unable to handle non-globally deterministic actions, this
assumption ensures that all world nodes in the search tree have at most one outgoing edge
for a given action. Together with the assumption of uniqueness of world names, we were
able to uniquely refer to each outgoing tree edge from a world w for action i : a with the
tuple (w, i : a). Lifting this assumption would therefore require to revise the definition
of edge costs to include the event name in the tuple, since, without this assumption, an
action could have several events applicable in one particular world, leading to several
outgoing edges for a given world w and action i : a.
Moreover, the assumption of uniform observability ensures all agents have the same notion
of the applicability of an action since, when closing under ‚ąľi, we obtain the same set of
designated events Ed, for all i ‚ąąA, as demonstrated in Example 9. Together with the
notion of possible applicability, this assumption ensures that all agents expand each state
in the search tree with the same actions, and are therefore able to generate one common
policy. Although this is not strictly a requirement of a planner for implicit coordination,
having one common policy directly removes the problem of inconsistent policies and the
need to replan, and ensures that the policies are implicitly coordinated, as all agents know
how to act when other agents expect them to do so.

A natural continuation of our work is to investigate how dropping the assumptions of global
determinism, uniform observability and universal applicability of actions in the planning
task affect the results of our solution. In general, when planning with event models as
actions, one expects to capture two particular sources of uncertainty, namely, partially
observable and nondeterministic actions. These two types of actions naturally fall outside
the assumptions of global determinism and uniform observability. As our solution concept
is developed within the framework of epistemic planning, which is meant to handle these
sources of uncertainty, lifting these two requirements is a logical next step in developing
our solution.
We expect, however, that after making the necessary adjustments, our
solution concept would be able to produce sensible results when generalizing to such
types of actions.
We have seen that, under these simplifying assumptions, our presented approaches are
able to produce sensible solutions to small planning tasks, such as the different variants
of the lever task. However, we have not analyzed or proved the properties of the policies
generated for any given task when we drop these assumptions about actions. As mentioned
in Section 4.6, proving (or disproving) that the generated policies for given planning
tasks satisfy the conditions of uniformity, applicability and per-agent determinism when
removing the assumptions above is left as subject of future work. Therefore, another
natural next step is analyzing the properties of policies generated by the proposed planners
with less restrictive action types.
Another simplifying assumption we made was that the product update semantics imply
that all states generated are distinct given the uniqueness of world names and event
names. As such, the definition of state equality is based on model identity and not modal
equivalence, and it is therefore possible that the extracted policy specifies different actions
for two states that satisfy the same set of formulas. We believe our solution can be further
improved by importing the ideas from bisimulation contraction (Bolander et al., 2021) and
therefore propose this as a subject of future work.
In developing our solution concept, we have observed that forward induction seems to
be particularly helpful in planning tasks where agents have uncertainty about the goal
configuration, as we have seen in variants of the lever task. With our pruning technique,

agents reduce their uncertainty about the goal. This appears to be similar to the filtering
process from goal recognition, where the idea is to eliminate hypotheses about the goal
that are not consistent with a sequence of observations (Ramirez and Geffner, 2009). In
general, we believe our forward induction solution is able to capture some of the problems
studied in the area of goal recognition and, although our solution does not build on the
formalism of the goal recognition problem, we expect it will be helpful in the development
of an integrated system for planning and goal recognition.
With the proposed solutions we are able to obtain implicitly coordinated policies where
agents are able to infer additional knowledge through forward induction.
It is worth
noting, however, that the problems we attempt to solve with forward induction do not
necessarily have a unique natural pruning principle, and therefore, we do not wish to
suggest that the method we present should be regarded as the "right" notion of planning
with forward induction. An interesting question related to the type of reasoning we intend
to capture is what happens when agents act based on incorrect beliefs about the goal of
the planning task. This would not necessarily constitute an optimal move towards the
actual goal, although other agents might take this move as a signal from which they try
to make inferences. It is therefore crucial that agents use the same level of reasoning,
such that they do not make moves that end up conveying incorrect information for other
agents.
All in all, inferring knowledge from observing the actions of other agents and the as-
sumption of their optimality appears to be a natural and sometimes necessary way of
reasoning to solve cooperative planning tasks under implicit coordination. We hope the
new solution concept we outline in this project serves as a starting point for integrating
forward induction reasoning in planning, and that it inspires future work that will serve
to incorporate new ways of reasoning in solutions for multi-agent planning under implicit
coordination.
