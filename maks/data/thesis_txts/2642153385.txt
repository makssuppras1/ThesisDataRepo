Abstract 
 
This thesis investigates the airflow patterns and thermal conditions of the plenum in diffuse 
ventilation systems. The supply velocity through the diffuse ceiling was assessed through 
computational fluid dynamic simulations. The geometry of cracks in the suspension system 
was not modelled in the CFD model, but the potential velocities of air jets entering the room 
through the gaps were evaluated based on the pressure conditions in the plenum. The velocity 
of the airflow entering the room through the porous media representing the ceiling was found 
to be of very small magnitude in all investigated cases. However, it was shown, that potential 
building components blocking high velocity airflows in the plenum could theoretically result 
in gap flows with velocities up to 0.43 𝑚/𝑠.  
 
The thermal conditions in the plenum and suspended ceiling were assessed in a full-scale 
experiment. Thermography was used to evaluate the flow patterns in the plenum and 
thermographic images indicated, that a significant reduction in the cooling capacity supplied 
to the room occurred along the flow path in the plenum. Measurements of the plenum air 
temperature showed a temperature gradient of up to 2.6 ℃ between the measured locations.  
A quasi-stationary energy model was developed to numerically evaluate the thermal 
conditions with diffuse ventilation. Based on the CFD simulations and the full-scale 
experiment, a zone model was proposed to account for the different thermal conditions in the 
plenum. Heat transfer mechanisms affecting the cooling capacity of the supply air were 
identified and energy balances describing the system were set up. The energy model was 
validated by the full-scale measurements and the reduction in the cooling capacity in the full-
scale experiment was found to be 23%. In order to evaluate the dynamic conditions, the 
transient heat accumulation of the building mass had to be taken into account. An initial 
approach to combine the energy model with a model of the dynamic heat accumulation was 
tested. However, the coupling of the quasi-stationary energy model and the transient 
accumulation model was not successful with this approach, and an alternative method to 
simulate the dynamics of the thermal conditions with diffuse ceiling ventilation was 
suggested.

Chapter 1 Background 
 
1.1 Introduction 
 
Today, almost all non-residential buildings are designed with mechanical ventilation. This is 
necessary to provide a comfortable and healthy indoor environment, an essential factor for 
securing the well-being and optimizing the performance of the occupants. The indoor 
environment domains that are mostly affected by ventilation are the thermal conditions and 
the air quality. These two domains are usually tightly interconnected, as they are both a 
product of the chosen ventilation strategy. The main purpose of ventilation is to maintain a 
satisfactory environment by supplying fresh air and removing heat, gaseous pollutants and 
particulate matter emitted by occupants and the building.  
 
A novel approach to comfort ventilation has been developed over the recent decade. In this 
principle, the ventilation air is supplied to the room through an acoustic ceiling providing a 
large inlet area. This ventilation principle is typically called diffuse ceiling ventilation but is 
also referred to as vertical ventilation. The principle behind diffuse ceiling ventilation is to 
supply clean air to a plenum above a suspended ceiling of acoustic tiles. The air diffuses into 
the room through the suspended ceiling, resulting in very low air velocities in the occupied 
zone. Thereby, draught risks are largely eliminated, and a higher cooling capacity can 
therefore be achieved with this ventilation principle. However, the cooling capacity of the 
supplied air can be significantly affected by heat transfers between the airflow and the 
surrounding constructions.  
 
This thesis aims to assess the air flow patterns in the plenum and the thermal interactions 
between the supply air and the surroundings for a diffuse ventilation system. Based on these 
investigations, a model to describe the temperatures and heat flows for such a system was set 
up and evaluated.  
 
 
1.2 Literature review 
 
A literature study was conducted to obtain a better understanding of the established 
knowledge on diffuse ventilation. The focus of the literature study was to get an indication of  
the resulting thermal conditions in the occupied zone when diffuse ventilation was applied, 
and to get an overview of the extent to which both flow and thermal conditions in the plenum 
have previously been investigated.  
 
The research on diffuse ceiling ventilation conducted until now has mostly focused on the 
performance of different diffuse ventilation systems in regard to thermal comfort in the 
occupied zone ((Fan et al., 2013), (Zhang et al., 2016), (Zhang et al., 2015b), (Mikeska et al., 
2015), (Kristensen et al., 2017), (Hviid et al., 2013a)), as well as different heat load scenarios 
and corresponding buoyancy forces and their impact on the airflow patterns in the room,

which have been assessed in several studies ((Lestinen, Kilpeläinen, Kosonen, et al., 2018), 
(Lestinen, Kilpeläinen and Kosonen, 2018), (Rahnama et al., 2020), (Zhao et al., 2020)). 
Full-scale experiments and CFD simulations have been the most employed methods to study 
the performance of diffuse ceiling ventilation.  
 
Hviid and Svendsen found, that diffuse ventilation only induced minimal risks of draught for 
the occupants. They investigated the performance of two ceiling types in a climatic chamber 
representing a classroom environment. The assessed ceiling types included a ceiling mounted 
with porous gypsum tiles and a ceiling mounted with perforated aluminum tiles. Through 
tracer-gas measurements it was concluded that the system provided almost perfect mixing of 
the room air. The air velocities at different sampling points were measured and positioned the 
diffuse ceiling into category A referring to CR 1752 in regard to maximum air velocities. 
Moreover, the paper concluded that no notable dissatisfaction was induced by radiant 
asymmetry caused by the cold suspended ceiling (Hviid et al., 2013a).  
 
 (Zhang et al., 2016) also evaluated the thermal environment in a test facility with a 
classroom layout ventilated with diffuse ceiling ventilation. A full-scale experiment was 
conducted during the study to perform a parametric analysis of the percentage of the ceiling 
utilized as ventilation inlet for diffuse ceiling ventilation. The ratios of 18%, 50% and 100% 
respectively of diffuse ceiling openings were investigated in the study. The experiment was 
furthermore used to validate a numerical CFD model of the room. The study indicated 
effective mixing of the room air which resulted in uniform air temperatures in the occupied 
zone. Furthermore, it was concluded that the diffuse ceiling system did not generate any 
draught issues in itself, even with high air change rates of up to 10.5 ℎ−1. Potential draught 
risks were indicated to be caused by present heat sources. The parametrical analysis of the 
opening area found that the ceiling configuration with a permeable ratio of 18% was able to 
handle the highest heat loads.  
 
Similar findings were made by (Rahnama et al., 2020) in a combined experimental and 
numerical study of the impact of heat load and active panel distribution on the cooling 
capacity of a diffuse ceiling ventilation system. The study was based on full-scale 
measurements performed in a laboratory supplemented by additional CFD simulations. A 
number of scenarios combining different heat load distributions with different distributions 
and ratios of active diffuse ceiling panels were analyzed. The study concluded that the 
highest cooling capacity was obtained in the scenarios with evenly distributed heat loads in 
the room. Of these scenarios, the ceiling configuration with a compact distribution of active 
panels showed higher performance in terms of cooling capacity than those with a more 
dispersed distribution of the active ceiling panels. Finally, it was established that a partial 
coverage of the ceiling with active panels could handle the highest heat loads in the room.   
 
The impact of heat load distribution on the cooling capacity of a diffuse ventilation system 
was also assessed by (Nielsen, 2015). In a full-scale experiment of a 27.9 𝑚2 room ventilated 
by diffuse ventilation, three different distributions (centered, evenly distributed and at one 
end of the room) of six heat sources of a total of 462 𝑊 were investigated. The cooling

capacity of the three scenarios was evaluated by the resulting vertical temperature gradient 
and air flow patterns, including the maximum velocities in the room. The study concluded 
that an even distribution of the present heat sources resulted in the highest cooling capacity of 
the system. Lestinen et. al. also investigated the difference of symmetrical and asymmetrical 
distribution of heat sources with diffuse ceiling ventilation (Lestinen, Kilpeläinen and 
Kosonen, 2018)(Lestinen, Kilpeläinen, Kosonen, et al., 2018). The experimental study of the 
asymmetrical heat load distribution identified higher air speeds and fluctuations in the room 
air compared to the symmetrical case. This led to the conclusion, that a symmetrical 
distribution of heat sources induced the highest cooling capacity of the two cases. The studies 
thereby confirm the conclusion of Nielsen et. al. and Rahnama et. al. 
 
The characteristics of the suspended ceiling were also assessed by Hviid and Svendsen. 
Thermography of different types of suspended diffuse ceilings showed the significance of the 
suspension grid system. By evaluating air flow measurements of the mounted ceilings and 
single tiles, the percentage of the air flow through the gaps in the suspension system was 
found to be 17% and 64% for the different systems investigated (Hviid et al., 2013a). Lastly, 
(Hviid et al., 2013a) found that the pressure loss for the suspended ceilings were 
approximately  1 −2 𝑃𝑎, thereby being in the range of 10-30 times lower than pressure 
losses of conventional systems. Zhang et. al. agrees that pressure losses are generally lower 
when applying diffuse ventilation systems, and points out that this can potentially result in 
substantial energy savings (Zhang et al., 2016).  
 
(Zhang et al., 2017) studied a system combining diffuse ceiling ventilation with a radiant 
ceiling. To investigate the effectiveness of the system a CFD model was developed to 
simulate both flow and heat mechanisms. The flow through the diffuse ceiling was modelled  
in the flow regime in order to simulate the flow patterns of diffuse ventilation. However, it 
was not possible for Zhang et. al. to combine this with a surface-to-surface radiation model in 
the software in order to evaluate thermal conditions in the same model. A separate radiation 
model, where the porous ceiling was modelled as a solid domain, was therefore built. Surface 
temperatures of the ceiling and its surroundings were obtained as the outputs of this model 
and could then be used as boundary conditions for the flow model. The study concluded that 
the presence of a diffuse ceiling decreased the cooling capacity of the radiant ceiling system 
but increased its heating capacity.  
 
The literature study revealed that several studies documented satisfying conditions in the 
occupied zone when applying diffuse ceiling ventilation. Studies on the cooling capacity with 
different heat load distributions agreed that evenly distributed loads yielded the highest 
cooling capacity. It was however concluded that previous research has generally focused on 
the conditions in the occupied zone, whereas documentation of the conditions in the plenum 
was very limited.

1.3 Objectives  
 
Due to the novelty of diffuse ceiling ventilation as a common ventilation principle in indoor 
environments, the concept of diffuse ventilation and its mechanisms has not yet been as 
comprehensively studied in the established literature as the traditional ventilation principles.   
 
A specific knowledge gap identified by HVAC engineers from Rambøll includes the 
understanding of airflow patterns in the plenum, and how these affect the heat transfer 
mechanisms between the building structure, the supply air and the resulting thermal 
conditions in the occupied zone. Zhang et. al. also suggested, that a dynamic investigation of 
the influence of the structural thermal storage capacity on the cooling potential with a diffuse 
ventilation system would be of interest.  
 
This project aims to contribute to the study of these knowledge gaps. The specific research 
questions to be answered are presented below.  
 
1.3.1 Research questions 
1) How is the supply velocity through the acoustic ceiling influenced by the air supply 
conditions and the presence of other building components in the plenum? 
 
2) How does the air distribution in the plenum affect the temperature of the supply air to 
the room? How can this be reflected in the calculation of the effective supply air 
temperature? 
 
3) What are the heat transfer mechanisms affecting the thermal conditions of the plenum 
and the room? 
 
4) How is the effective cooling capacity of the supplied ventilation affected when 
diffusing through the acoustic ceiling? 
 
5) How can the heat transfer between the supply air and the ceiling slab be calculated? 
How much does the thermal accumulation in the ceiling slab affect the effective 
cooling capacity of the supply air to the room? 
 
To investigate the distribution of the inlet air in the plenum a computational fluid dynamic 
(CFD) model was developed. The air flow pattern and pressure distribution in the plenum 
was modelled and provided data used to assess critical velocities of air jets through the cracks 
in the suspension system. 
A quasi-stationary model of the heat flows in a room ventilated by diffuse ventilation was set 
up based on different energy balances and validated through full-scale measurements. 
Finally, an approach to include the heat accumulation of the thermal mass of the system in 
the energy model was discussed.

1.4 Ventilation principles 
 
In this section the traditional ventilation principles (i.e. mixing and displacement ventilation) 
are compared with the novel diffuse ceiling ventilation principle. The section focuses on the 
different ways that draught issues are generated with the traditional ventilation principles, and 
how these are resolved when applying diffuse ceiling ventilation. Furthermore, the concept of 
diffuse ceiling ventilation is presented in detail.  
 
 
1.4.1 Traditional ventilation principles 
 
The most common ventilation principles applied in non-residential buildings are mixing 
ventilation and displacement ventilation. Both are widely used, however mixing ventilation is 
typically applied to offices and similar settings, whereas displacement ventilation is often 
used in other settings, where room heights are larger (Hashimoto et al., 2009). When 
applying mechanical ventilation to a space, it is important to ensure a satisfactory 
environment for the occupants. This includes the thermal conditions where attention should 
be paid to the possible thermal discomforts that may be induced by the ventilation. For 
mixing and displacement ventilation, this mainly includes draught issues caused by low 
temperatures and high air velocities induced by the air supply to the occupied zone. As the 
fresh air is supplied through diffusers with relatively small inlet areas, particularly with 
mixing ventilation but also with displacement ventilation, the risk of draught should always 
be evaluated when applying these concepts.  
 
Mixing ventilation is based on the idea of mixing fresh supply air with the air in the 
ventilated space in order to dilute any pollutants and maintain satisfactory temperatures. For 
this concept to be effective, good mixing in the room must be ensured, which is done by 
supplying air at high velocities through diffusers typically mounted in the ceiling. If the 
design of such a ventilation system is not considered carefully, local draught issues in the 
vicinity of inlet diffusers can easily arise due to locally high velocities (Shan et al., 2016). 
 
The risk of draught should also be considered when designing a system based on 
displacement ventilation. The concept of displacement ventilation is based on the idea of 
completely removing the polluted air from the occupied zone. By supplying clean air directly 
to the occupied zone with a temperature slightly colder than the room air temperature, the 
warmer contaminated air in the room is displaced upwards to the ceiling through the present 
buoyancy forces. In order for this ventilation principle to work, the inlet diffusers must be 
placed at a low level of the room and the outlet diffusers should be located in the upper level 
closer to the ceiling. The low placement of the inlet diffusers is necessary to ensure a high 
efficiency of the system, however, it entails a significant risk of draught in the vicinity of the 
inlets (Shan et al., 2016). Humans are very sensitive to the thermal conditions at ankle height, 
and the low inlet air temperatures can therefore cause draught issues for occupants (Schiavon 
et al., 2016).

The novel concept of diffuse ceiling ventilation offers an alternative air supply to the 
occupied zone. As described above, mixing ventilation entails a draught risk because of high 
local air velocities, while low air temperatures at ankle height is typically considered the 
main draught risk with displacement ventilation. Diffuse ceiling ventilation supplies air to the 
occupied zone with low impulse, which increases the potential cooling capacity of the 
mechanical ventilation compared to the traditional concepts as shown in the graph in Figure 1 
by (Nielsen, 2007) below. The curves in the figure represent the thermal comfort limit for the 
different ventilation systems and clearly illustrate the high cooling capacity of diffuse 
ventilation compared to the other ventilation concepts included in the graph.

idea as traditional mixing ventilation: to supply fresh 
air and dilute pollutants in the room. However, the 
system through which air is supplied to the room is 
very different.

supply fresh air over a suspended ceiling, using this

official standards but has been recommended to be 
moderate in the range of 1 −3 𝑚/𝑠 (Nickel, 2019). In

As the air is supplied to the occupied space over a large inlet area, the vertical velocity to the 
room is very low, generally below 0.03 𝑚/𝑠 (Nickel, 2019), thereby mitigating any risks of 
draught. The low velocities of the air supply into the occupied zone can be utilized to provide 
a higher cooling capacity than with traditional ventilation systems (Nielsen, 2007) (Nocente 
et al., 2020). This can be employed in spaces such as meeting rooms, where high thermal 
loads occur. 
 
 
Configuration of the suspended ceiling 
The diffuse ceiling is comprised of perforated acoustic tiles that are mounted on a metallic 
grid suspension system. A variety of these products can be found on the market. For the 
ventilation characteristics, the main differences between products are the density and thereby 
the permeability of the tiles and the uniformity hereof, as well as the tightness of the 
connections between the tiles and the grid system they are mounted on. The acoustic tiles are 
usually made of lightly compressed mineral wool painted on the visible side, allowing air to 
diffuse through the tile. The top of the tiles is open, only covered with a thin sheet of felt as 
shown in Figure 4.

Effective cooling capacity with diffuse ceiling ventilation 
As the ventilation air is supplied to the plenum before diffusing through the acoustic ceiling it 
is directly exposed to the slab above the plenum. The large surface area accommodates a 
potentially significant heat exchange between the supply air and the slab before the air enters 
the room. If the slab is not insulated toward the plenum, high heat fluxes can be transmitted 
from the overlying room and structure to the ventilation air. This effect can have a critical 
impact on the supply air temperature and thereby the effective cooling capacity supplied to 
the room by the ventilation (Yang et al., 2019)(Zhang et al., 2015a). During the literature

Additional characteristics of diffuse ceiling ventilation 
Another aspect of the diffuse ceiling ventilation concept is the desirable acoustic properties of 
such a system. According to CEN/CR 1752 the maximum allowable noise emitted by 
mechanical installations in offices for category B is 35 −40 𝑑𝐵 depending on the size and 
type of the room. Typically, ventilation diffusers generate noise of 25 −40 𝑑𝐵 each (Nickel, 
2018), and meeting the requirements can thus be challenging. However, with diffuse ceiling 
ventilation, inlet diffusers are avoided and replaced by the diffuse ceiling consisting mainly 
of acoustic tiles with a high acoustic performance (Rockfon). The reduction in necessary 
ventilation ducts entails a further acoustical benefit. Additionally, the absence of diffusers 
and reduction of ducting leads to a lower pressure loss of the system (Nocente et al., 2020). 
The resulting lowering of the specific fan power sustains a reduction of operational costs and 
overall the system requires a lower initial investment (Nocente et al., 2020). Furthermore, it 
can be argued that the “clean” design of a diffuse ceiling without diffusers improves the 
visual aesthetics from an architectural point of view. Finally, the use of a plenum and 
suspended ceiling as ventilation inlet and the thereby following minimization of necessary 
ducting can significantly reduce the overall building height (Bedre indeklima med diffus 
ventilation, Rockfon). This can also be utilized in already existing buildings, where diffuse 
ceiling ventilation offers an attractive solution for retrofitting mechanical ventilation (Jacobs 
et al., 2009).

2.1 Measurement data  
 
A dataset containing experimental measurements of supplied pressure and resulting air flow 
through acoustic tiles and a diffuse ceiling was provided by Rambøll. This data will later be 
used to model a suspended ceiling in a CFD model, as the relation between pressure losses 
and air flows given by the data set could be used to derive the necessary physical properties 
of the acoustic tiles for the CFD modelling.  
 
The dataset was based on data from two sets of measurements. In the first set of 
measurements, the relation between air flow and pressure was found for different acoustic 
tiles. This was done by passing air through single acoustic tiles at different air flows and 
measuring the corresponding pressure drop across the tile. As flow through porous medias is 
generally laminar, the pressure drop expectedly proved to increase linearly with increasing air 
flow.  
 
The second set of measurements were performed in a small room where air was supplied to a 
plenum through a flexible ventilation duct and then diffused into the room through different 
suspended ceiling systems. The pressure drop across the suspended ceiling was measured and 
related to different air flows. These measurements related to the suspended ceiling as an 
entity, however as the air flow through the acoustic tiles was known from the first

2.2 Defining the dimensioning “rule of thumb” for air supply to the plenum 
 
When designing diffuse ventilation systems, the air velocities resulting from the air supply 
cannot be directly assessed as with conventional ventilation concepts, where the maximum 
velocities can be calculated on the basis of the diffuser configuration and airflow. In the case 
of diffuse ventilation, the resulting velocities to the room and the occupied zone depend on 
the flow and pressure conditions in the plenum over the suspended ceiling resulting from the 
initial air supply conditions. If the system is well designed, diffuse ceiling ventilation has the 
potential to provide a remarkably uniform air supply to the room, however this does require 
some considerations in the design phase. For conventional ventilation, a margin of 15% is 
typically accepted for the balancing of individual diffusers and 10% for the total airflow to 
the space in order to ensure relatively even conditions (DS 447, 2013). As there are not 
diffusers that are balanced in the same way for diffuse ceiling ventilation these guidelines do 
not apply, and no corresponding regulations have been established. As the average air 
velocities are generally significantly lower with diffuse ceiling ventilation than with 
conventional ventilation, it is estimated that a higher tolerance for the deviation can be 
accepted when applying diffuse ceiling ventilation. Rambøll suggests increasing the tolerance 
to 30% for diffuse ventilation, meaning that the local air velocity can acceptably deviate from 
the average designed conditions by 30%. This guideline is closely related to the crack flows 
described earlier, as velocities through gaps and leakages in the suspension system will 
typically create the most significant deviations from the average supply velocity.

To obtain a deeper understanding of these airflow patterns, different situations were analyzed 
through the use of the CFD simulation tool COMSOL Multiphysics®. COMSOL 
Multiphysics® is a tool that can be used to analyze different physical phenomena, including 
flow and heat transfer. The possibility of coupling flow and heat transfer in the same model 
was the main reason to choose the simulation environment provided by COMSOL

3.1 Scope of the CFD simulations 
The analysis of airflow patterns in the plenum is an important prerequisite for the study of the 
thermal behavior of the system. Local differences in the airflow in the plenum can have great 
effects on the resulting conditions. Furthermore, local pressure maxima can induce elevated 
velocities, affecting the thermal conditions in the occupied zone. Thus, the main focus of the 
CFD analysis was to investigate the flow patterns in the plenum and the resulting air 
distribution.  
 
Initially, the design of the plenum was carefully considered. In order to reflect realistic 
conditions, different objects potentially present in a plenum were identified and considered. 
The identified objects included constructional beams, ventilation ducts, water pipes and cable 
trays, all potentially affecting the airflow patterns and distribution in a plenum. Two 
generalized cases were selected for this analysis: beams running in the same direction as the 
inlet airflow (case 1) and a beam/duct perpendicular to the inlet airflow thereby creating an 
obstacle forcing a sudden change of direction of the airflow (case 2). As a reference, an 
empty plenum with the same geometric properties was also simulated (case 0). The aim of the 
simulations was to investigate the following aspects of the airflow in the plenum:  
 
-

o Uniformity 
o Flow direction 
o Influence of obstacles 
o Local pressure maxima

3.2 The CFD model 
 
3.2.1 Modelling of the acoustic ceiling 
To work with diffuse ceiling ventilation in COMSOL Multiphysics® it was necessary to 
develop a method to simulate the suspended ceiling and airflow through it as realistically as 
possible.  
 
Ideally, the acoustic ceiling would have been modelled as porous tiles with a grid of small air 
gaps between them representing the grid suspension system. This way, both the laminar flow 
through the porous acoustic tiles as well as the more turbulent air jets entering the room 
through leaks in the suspension system would be included in the simulation. However, this 
was not practically realizable in the scope of this project, as it would have required much 
more detailed work with the modelled mesh as well as higher computation times. Instead, it 
was decided to model the diffuse ceiling as a homogenous plate. As the air flow patterns in 
the plenum and not the resulting air flows in the room were the main focus of this project, 
this simplification was considered acceptable. However, in order to reproduce realistic 
pressure conditions in the plenum, the homogenous plate had to take both the laminar air flow 
through the tiles and the air jets through the gaps in the suspension system into account. The 
main input characteristic of the material expressing the air resistance is the permeability, 𝑘. 
As this characteristic was not commonly known for the available products on the market, and 
even had to be modified to represent both tiles and gaps, a suitable value was found through 
iterations on the measurement data provided by Rambøll presented in section 2.1. The data 
documented the relation between the pressure loss and airflow through both the acoustic tiles, 
gaps and the entire ceiling respectively.  
 
As previously described, the relation between the air flow through the tiles and the air flow 
through the gaps in the suspension system is not constant but depends on the pressure 
difference across the ceiling. As the permeability had to represent the entire ceiling, this had 
to be taken into account, resulting in a function for the permeability dependent on the airflow.

3.2.2 Turbulence model 
 
The indoor environment investigated with the CFD model is mostly dominated by turbulent 
airflows. However, the flow through the suspended ceiling is considered laminar, as 
velocities are very low resulting in low Reynolds numbers. The airflows in the model are 
computed as single-phase turbulent flows, for which the Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes 
(RANS) equations are solved. (Chen, 1995) argues that the 𝑅𝑁𝐺 𝑘−𝜀 turbulence model is 
appropriate for simulating indoor airflows, and (Zhang et al., 2017) and (Zhang et al., 2016) 
applied it with diffuse ceiling ventilation with satisfactory results. However, COMSOL 
Multiphysics® does not support transport equations such as the 𝑘−𝜀 model for the porous 
media module applied in the CFD model and modeling the flow with the turbulence model 
recommended by the cited literature was therefore not an option. COMSOL Multiphyiscs® 
offers two turbulence models compatible with porous domains. The chosen turbulence model, 
the Algebraic yPlus model, is an algebraic turbulence model suitable for internal flows. It is 
generally less mesh-sensitive than the transport equation models, which is considered 
beneficial (COMSOL®, 2016). This turbulence model determines the turbulent viscosity 
from the local velocity and the distance to the nearest wall and can therefore be used with 
porous media domains in COMSOL Multiphysics®.  
 
 
3.2.3 Mesh refinement study 
 
When creating and computing a model in COMSOL Multiphysics®, the program finds 
solutions for the chosen physics (flows, heat transfer etc.) by solving partial differential 
equations for each element of the mesh. Therefore, the mesh and the relevant factors affecting 
it have a great impact on the precision of the final outcome and should thus be considered and 
evaluated. By default, COMSOL Multiphysics® provides a physics-controlled mesh which 
automatically creates a mesh depending on the physics chosen in the model. Another option 
is to manually create a user-defined mesh from scratch, or a third option is to edit the physics-
controlled mesh, which can be beneficial if there are areas of the model that are of specific 
interest.  
 
A finer mesh generally accommodates more accurate results, but also entails higher 
computation times, whereas a coarser mesh results in less accuracy but will require less 
computation capacity. Defining an appropriate mesh is thus a balance of the accuracy of the 
results and the acceptable computation time. 
  
In order to define a satisfactory mesh for the investigated model, a mesh refinement study 
was performed, evaluating the effect of different mesh sizes in a physics-controlled mesh. 
This was done by computing a parametric sweep on a factor scaling a reference mesh of size 
“normal” in a 2D model of a room with diffuse ceiling ventilation. The scaling factor ranged 
from 0.3 to 1.2, thereby modifying the mesh to be both finer and coarser than the reference 
mesh.

3.3.4 Analysis of maximum pressure differences and resulting crack flow velocities 
 
In this section, the influence of local maximum static pressures in the plenum on the resulting 
thermal comfort in the occupied zone is assessed. According to the rule of thumb described in 
section 2.2, the dynamic pressure of the supply air should not exceed 3 times the pressure loss 
over the suspended ceiling in order to secure thermal comfort for the occupants.  
 
The simulations analyzed in this study were performed for a situation where the rule of 
thumb was not complied with, i.e. the supply air velocity was higher than recommended 
(almost 3 times higher than the maximum recommended value in regard to the rule of thumb 
for the present pressure drop across the ceiling). It was therefore expected that the local 
pressure maxima generated in the simulations would be substantially higher than 
recommended. Identifying these local maximum pressures and finding their values made it 
possible to calculate what the resulting air velocities would be, if there was a gap in the 
ceiling at this location. 
The resulting air velocity entering the room shown in Figure 24 are of magnitude 0.004 𝑚/𝑠 
when the air diffuses through the porous media with elevated velocities reaching 0.007 m/s at 
the back wall. 
However, as the cracks are not geometrically represented in the models, the potential air 
velocities of the air jets entering the room through the cracks in the suspension system could 
not directly be evaluated from the COMSOL Multiphysics® simulations. Based on the values 
of the maximum local pressures the velocity of a potential crack flow could however be 
calculated. These results were then used to sustain the validity of the rule of thumb. 
  
The maximum pressures were identified in each investigated case. In case 0 and case 1, the 
maximum pressure was found on the back wall, where the supply airflow meets the wall, 
causing the velocity of the supply air stream to approach 0 𝑚/𝑠 and the static pressure to rise 
correspondingly. However, as the back wall is technically outside of the occupied zone, the 
maximum local pressure in the plenum over the occupied zone is therefore found, located 
0.1 𝑚 from the back wall. For the cases 2a and 2b, the maximum local pressures were 
expected to appear when the supply air stream meets the obstacle in front of the supply. This 
was confirmed in the simulations for both cases. The location of the maximum pressures and

3.4 Limitations of the CFD simulations 
 
In the following section some of the limitations of the simulations performed in COMSOL 
Multiphysics® are discussed.  
The modelled geometry was designed to represent a typical office area or similar and the 
selected objects in the plenum, i.e. an array of beams and two cases of a transverse duct in 
front of the inlet were chosen based on the idea, that these could potentially occur in most 
real cases, in order to obtain results that could conveniently be generalized and referred to in 
other situations. However, the simulations also entailed a number of idealizations and 
simplifications. The investigated room was designed to be symmetrical, which supported 
shorter computation times, but often also represents an idealization of the reality. Moreover, 
the suspended ceiling was simplified as one entity as previously described. Leaks in the 
ceiling and their influence on the resulting flow patterns were thereby not included in the 
model, and the simulations were therefore not directly suitable for evaluating the thermal 
environment in the room and the occupied zone. Furthermore, rising plumes from heat 
sources in the room could potentially interfere with the downward air flow from the plenum. 
However, the simulated environment was modelled isothermally, and the effects of buoyancy 
flows on the airflow pattern in the occupied zone and the resulting air velocities entering the 
room through the ceiling were not accounted for in the results. Likewise, thermal effects are 
not modelled in the plenum. As described in section 1.4.2 it is known that a heat transfer 
between the supply air and the surroundings takes place in the plenum before the air reaches 
the room and possible effects of these conditions cannot be evaluated in the isothermal 
simulations either.  
 
Due to the limitations of the timeframe for this project, creating a fine mesh in the simulated 
model was not possible. The performed mesh refinement study indicated that the inaccuracies 
following this were acceptable in relation to the objectives of the CFD analysis. However, it 
is recognized that the tendencies from a mesh refinement study performed in 2D cannot 
necessarily be transferred directly to the corresponding model in 3D, and the results from the 
2D mesh refinement study should therefore only be considered indicative of the performance 
of the mesh in 3D. The resulting mesh resolution was theoretically considered acceptable, 
which can be further sustained by a number of practical considerations. As already discussed, 
the suspended ceiling was significantly simplified in regard to the cracks, naturally inducing 
some discrepancy from the real world. The modelling of the suspended ceiling as one porous 
media also entailed an idealization of the single acoustic tiles. In the model, the tiles are 
modelled as being 100% uniform, however, density differences caused by the nature of the 
used materials (mineral wool) can create local differences in the tiles. When prioritizing how 
refined the mesh of a CFD model for analysis of diffuse ventilation should be, it should 
therefore always be considered, that the conditions are not always consistent in reality, and a 
high degree of accuracy in a simulation is not necessarily a prerequisite to investigate the 
behavior of the analyzed system adequately.

Chapter 4 Full-scale experiment  
 
A full-scale experiment was conducted in a small test room provided by Rockfon. The room 
was ventilated with diffuse ceiling ventilation through an acoustic ceiling. The objective of 
the full-scale experiment was to obtain temperature data that could give an indication of the 
air flow pattern and thermal conditions of the plenum and the resulting thermal conditions in 
the occupied zone. Furthermore, the measurements were used to verify the proposed energy 
model suggested in 0. 
 
During the experiment surface temperatures of the suspended ceiling and the slab above the 
plenum were measured with infrared thermography. Zhang et al. reported that thermographic 
images had shown lower surface temperatures of active panels than on passive panels during 
an experiment with diffuse ceiling ventilation in a classroom (Zhang et al., 2014). Lower 
surface temperatures of active ceiling panels can be caused by high airflow volume through 
the panels or by low air temperatures of the air diffusing through the panel. The 
thermographic imaging could thus be used to analyze the airflow pattern through the 
suspended ceiling. Furthermore, air temperature measurements from the plenum and room air 
were collected. In the first part of the experiment, the surface temperatures of the entire 
ceiling ventilated with different airflow rates were documented. During the second part of the 
experiment four data sets of air temperature measurements and corresponding surface 
temperatures were collected in four selected measurement points, which reflected the thermal 
conditions in the plenum, were collected.

Chapter 5 Energy model  
 
In this chapter the different heat exchange mechanisms occurring in a system ventilated by 
diffuse ceiling ventilation will be investigated. Both the CFD analysis and the full-scale 
experiment with diffuse ventilation showed, that the air spreads throughout the plenum before 
diffusing into the room. This entails that there is a large active surface contact between the 
supply air and the overlying construction, possibly affecting the thermal state of the supply 
air. A convective heat transfer between a warm slab and cold supply air can cause a 
significant reduction in the effective cooling capacity to the room. Furthermore, an increase 
in the effective supply air temperature to the room can also be expected from the interactions 
between the ventilation air and the suspended ceiling. The temperature of the suspended 
ceiling, and thereby the potential of the heat transfer from the ceiling to the ventilation air, 
depends on the convective heat transfers on both sides of the ceiling, as well as the radiative 
heat exchange with the slab above the plenum and the surfaces in the room. A model 
describing these heat flows was set up for the quasi-steady state conditions of the system, and 
validated by the measurements from the full-scale experiment.  
 
 
5.1 Zone model 
 
(Rahnama et al., 2020) found that the mass flow in the plenum decreased with increasing 
distance to the air supply due to the continuous diffusion of air to the room along the flow 
direction. The findings from the performed CFD simulations in this project are consistent 
with this observation. The simulations clearly illustrated that the air velocity in the plenum 
decreases greatly with the distance to the inlet along the flow direction as shown in Figure 26. 
This can, to some degree, be caused by friction losses, but is mainly due to the air diffusion 
through the ceiling as stated by (Rahnama et al., 2020). Similarly, the thermographic images 
as well as the different temperature data sets obtained in the full-scale experiment 
documented distinct temperature differences across the ceiling and the plenum. The identified 
patterns followed the same tendencies as found in the CFD simulations, with the coldest 
temperatures measured near the air supply to the plenum followed by increasing values along 
the flow direction indicated in Figure 34.  
 
The general findings on the investigated thermal conditions from the full-scale experiment 
can be interpreted as a result of the flow pattern identified in the CFD simulations. The flow 
pattern in the plenum is highly defining for the resulting thermal conditions, as it greatly 
impacts the size of the heat transfers between the ventilation air and the surface of the slab 
above the plenum as well as the suspended ceiling. The results of the second part of the 
experiment showed, that the air temperature in the plenum increased along the flow path, a 
result of the reduction of the cooling capacity. The heating of the plenum air related to the 
surface temperature measurements of the concrete indicated, that a substantial convective 
heat transfer can occur between the concrete slab above the plenum and the ventilation air.

When proposing a model for the thermal conditions of a room ventilated by diffuse ceiling 
ventilation, the differences in the flow conditions across the plenum are important to take into 
account in order to reflect the resulting differences in the thermal conditions. Therefore, the 
representativeness of the input values in the model that are related to the airflow should be 
considered carefully. A method to take the overall local differences into account in the 
thermal model was suggested to represent the air distribution in the plenum. The principle 
behind the method was to divide the plenum and the suspended ceiling into four different 
zones defined on the basis of the local flow characteristics.  By dividing the plenum into 
different zones, input parameters could be assigned different values and thus a more detailed 
model was achieved as a general averaging across the entire plenum was avoided and local 
variations could be reproduced by the model.  
 
The division into zones only applies to the plenum and the suspended ceiling, where local 
conditions both in regard to temperature and flow vary significantly. The room can be 
modelled as one air volume, as diffuse ventilation generally performs at a high ventilation 
effectiveness (Hviid et al., 2013a) and the temperature distribution in the room can be 
assumed to be uniform (Zhang et al., 2016)

5.2.2 Heat transfer mechanisms 
 
The diagram in Figure 43 shows the heat flows and types of heat transfers included in the 
model. The energy model was based on a set of equations describing the different heat flows 
shown in the figure. Energy balances were set up for the system as a whole, for the room air, 
for the plenum air, and for all surfaces. In order to implement the zone-method proposed in 
section 5.1, the energy model was expanded to a 4-zone energy model by splitting the plenum 
and the suspended ceiling into four zones considered separately. The division of the plenum 
into four zones was taken into account in the energy balances, such that all balances affected 
by the zone division were made on a zone level, while the energy balance of the room air and 
the floor were still on an overall room level. According to (Kristensen et al., 2017) diffuse 
ventilation induces good mixing of the room air, and air flow patterns in the room have been 
found to be governed by buoyancy forces (Wu et al., 2019). It is therefore estimated that 
horizontal temperature differences in the room are negligible, and the room air and the 
surface of the floor can therefore be modelled as one zone with good proximity. Heat flows 
calculated on zone level and the parameters related to specific zones (i.e. area and airflows) 
are marked with the index 𝑧 where 𝑧= 1. .4 for the 4-zone model in the following sections.

The energy balances of the 4-zone model are naturally coupled through the expressions of the 
heat flows. With a number of boundary conditions, the model could thereby be numerically 
solved in a calculation program, such as Excel and its built-in solver. The boundary 
conditions required as input to the 4-zone model are the ventilation air flow, the supply 
temperature of the air supply, the internal heat gain and the heat flow to or from the slab 
above the plenum and the floor construction. The heat flow between the construction and the 
air is determined by the heat accumulation of the structures. Heat accumulation is dynamic 
per definition, but by only considering the heat flow to the surface for a given moment, the 
flow can be used as a boundary condition for the quasi-stationary model.  
 
The resulting set of energy balances consists of 19 equations, 1 energy balance for the 
complete system, 2 balances on room level and a set of four balances for each zone. With the 
described input data, the system can be numerically solved. The output of the model are 
values of all temperatures included in the model as well as the modelled heat flows shown in 
Figure 43. The heat flows included in the figure are only representative for 1 zone, but the

5.2.3 Energy balance for the overall system

cooling effect supplied to the system through the ventilation air and the heat accumulation or 
the heat release (depending on the thermal conditions) of the floor structure and from the slab 
above the plenum. The system is considered adiabatic toward neighboring rooms based on 
the considerations that temperature gradients between occupied rooms would typically not be 
very large, and that the thermal mass of interior walls is generally not significant. Assuming 
adiabatic conditions for all walls also neglects the heat loss through the façade, which, in 
some buildings or during some times of the year, is not an acceptable assumption. For those 
cases, the transmission heat loss should be subtracted from the system.

the exhaust temperature equals the room air temperature.

5.3 Limitations of the 4-zone energy model 
 
As described, the 4-zone energy model is based on a number of different assumptions and 
simplifications. It was argued that the room air can be modelled as one zone, as good mixing 
of the room air generally characterizes diffuse ceiling ventilation. It is however a 
simplification that should be recognized. Considering the modelling of the thermal conditions 
in regard to the comfort of the occupants, the room air may be modelled as one zone, but the 
suspended ceiling is divided into zones in the proposed 4-zone energy model. Thereby, 
individual surface temperatures of the suspended ceiling for the four zones are found, which 
can be reflected in the resulting operative temperature in the occupied zone. 
 
Furthermore, the plenum air in every zone is considered uniform in the energy model. As 
with the room air, this assumes full mixing of the air within the zone, which is estimated to be 
reasonable for normal plenum heights. However, in cases where the plenum height is 
significant, the possibility of a vertical temperature gradient within the plenum cannot be 
rejected. This phenomenon poses an interesting subject for a thermal CFD study in the further 
research of the thermal conditions in the plenum.  
 
The modelling of the heat flows in the acoustic tiles also poses a possible source of error in 
the model. As the exact heat transfer mechanisms occurring inside the tiles were not the focus 
of this project, it was assumed that all energy transferred to the tiles by radiation was 
transferred on to the ventilation air diffusing through the tile. As it was argued for the room 
air volume, the heat capacity coefficient of the acoustic tiles is so insignificant compared to 
the magnitudes of the modelled heat flows, that it is a reasonable simplification to neglect the 
heat accumulation in the acoustic tiles.  
 
A significant source of error of the energy model is the constant value of the convective heat 
transfer coefficient. The size of the convective heat transfer is mainly determined by the 
velocity of the fluid driving the convective heat transfer (Cengel, 2013). The use of a constant 
value for the coefficient is not the best approximation for the application in the plenum, as it 
was shown by the CFD analysis, that the velocity magnitude of the supply air in the plenum 
had significant local differences. Assigning different values for the coefficient in the different 
zones would be a way to take this velocity dependency into account.  
 
The value for the convective heat transfer coefficient adapted for this project was based on 
the recommended values for the combined convective and radiative heat transfer coefficients 
in indoor environments provided by DS 418. However, as this value is intended for 
calculating the convection (and radiation) in indoor environments, it is most likely based on 
an assumption of free convection. Free convection is driven alone by buoyancy forces 
generated by density differences in the fluid (VDI Heat Atlas, 2010). However, as air is 
supplied mechanically to the plenum with a certain impulse, the convection is not only 
naturally driven, but may be considered as forced convection. A more in-depth analysis of the 
flow characteristics near the surfaces in the plenum is therefore needed to estimate more 
==PAGE:74==
Chapter 5 
Page 74 of 113 
 
accurate values for the convective heat transfer coefficients. Ideally, the coefficient should be 
expressed as a function of the local velocity of the applicable zone, and depending on the 
assumption of either free or forced convection, the coefficient may also have to reflect the 
direction of the heat flow (i.e. different values for upward and downward heat flow).

Chapter 6 Dynamic modeling of the thermal conditions with

In Chapter 5 a quasi-stationary 4-zone energy model of the thermal conditions and heat 
transfers with diffuse ventilation was described, and in section 5.4 it was validated through 
measurement results from the full-scale experiment. As the model was used to validate an 
already known situation, the size of the heat exchange of the plenum and room air with the 
slab above the plenum and the floor respectively could be found through iterations. However, 
in the investigated cases, the heat flux to or from the building mass was based was based on a 
fixed surface temperature. Initially, it was the intention that the heat flow from the structure 
to the room and plenum air could have been simulated and obtained through the CFD 
simulations performed for the project. However, due to the time limitations of the project the 
CFD simulations were performed isothermally, and the heat flows between the air and the 
structures were thus not modelled.  
 
As described, the developed energy model assumes quasi-stationary conditions of the 
investigated cases, entailing that no heat is accumulated anywhere in the system when the 
model is solved. Therefore, the accumulation heat flows are needed as input boundary 
conditions in order to solve the equation system.  
 
Further developing the 4-zone energy model to take the dynamics of the heat accumulation of 
the slab above the plenum and the floor into account would be ideal, as it would enable 
dynamic and not only stationary investigations of the thermal conditions and heat transfer 
mechanisms with diffuse ventilation.

calculation of the radiative heat exchange i.e. where temperatures in the 4.th power are 
included in the equation. Because of the non-linear equations this model could not be solved

6.2 Coupling of the quasi-stationary energy model and the transient heat 
accumulation model 
 
In order to set up a dynamic model for the system, the quasi-stationary energy model and the 
transient model of the heat accumulation in the structures must be coupled. As described 
above, the transient calculation provides the input data needed to solve the energy model and 
thereby obtain the resulting temperatures of the system. A concept for the coupling of the two 
models is suggested in the following section.  
 
The overall idea for a model combining the energy model and the accumulation model was to 
perform a dynamic calculation of the heat accumulation in the structure “interrupted” by 
quasi-stationary calculations of the energy model. The concept is illustrated in the diagram in 
Figure 54.

As shown in the figure, all boundary conditions for the calculation are initially determined. 
These values are loaded into a program, that solves the initial energy-balance for the system 
as described in section 5.2. The results of the energy model include the air temperature of the 
plenum and the room, which are used by the program as boundary conditions for the transient 
calculation of the heat accumulation in the structures. The transient calculation of the heat 
accumulation should now initiate a time loop of a specific duration. In the loop, the heat 
accumulation in the structure is calculated at small time steps until the specified time duration 
has been reached. When the loop has been completed, the resulting heat flow between the 
structure and the air (plenum and room) has been determined, and can now be used as an 
input for the energy model. The energy model is then solved again with the calculated heat 
flows. With the input heat flows from the accumulation model, the plenum and room air 
temperatures are solved in the energy model for this specific time step, and the heat 
accumulation calculation can continue with “updated” input values for the air temperatures.  
 
The objective of coupling the two models in this way was to develop a relatively simple 
program that could be used to model the thermal performance of a diffuse ceiling ventilation 
system over a longer time period such as a standard reference year as it is commonly done in 
the design process of ventilation systems.  
 
The graph in Figure 55 gives in indication of the desired outcome of the suggested program. 
It is noted that the graph is only included for illustrative purposes in order to show the 
principal desired outcome of the model. An analysis of the outcome of the proposed 
procedure showed a significant error in the coupling of the quasi-stationary and transient 
model resulting in unrealistic temperature and heat flow curves.

on the background of the negligible heat capacity of the room and plenum air compared to the 
thermal mass of the structure. Therefore, the coupling of the two models was proposed in a 
way, where the energy model is solved for in an outer time loop running from 𝑡=

temperatures is so closely related, that the sudden thermal changes of the system induced by 
load changes or variations in the ventilation schedule causes the resulting outcomes to 
fluctuate before approaching a more steady result.  
 
 
6.3 Considerations for further work 
 
As it had to be concluded that the proposed coupling of the two models was not suitable for 
the transient modelling of the thermal system, the final development of a dynamic model of 
the thermal conditions with diffuse ceiling ventilation was not completed. Based on the 
understanding of the system obtained through the work with the proposed model, it is 
suggested to couple the equations from the energy zone model with the discretized equations 
for the heat flows in the layers of the construction in one combined equation system. As the 
system would still be linear it could easily be solved for given time steps. With this approach, 
the energy model and the accumulation model are dependent on each other, and the time 
discrepancies between the determination of the temperatures and the corresponding heat 
fluxes are eliminated. The equation system could now be solved at a given time interval over 
a given time period, thereby providing a dynamic calculation of the system. The appropriate 
size of these time steps would have to be assessed through analysis of the time sensitivity of 
the system. With the proposed implementation of the heat accumulation model into the same 
equation system as the zone energy model, it can be expected that the heat accumulation will 
be defining for the maximum acceptable time resolution of the calculation due to the higher 
thermal mass of the structure. 
 
The dynamic program should ideally provide the opportunity to simulate a whole reference 
year as it is commonly done when designing ventilation systems. Alternatively, shorter 
representative time periods could be simulated indicating for example a summer and winter 
situation. These simulations should cover a period long enough to reflect the impact of the 
thermal building mass after significant changes in the boundary conditions, for example after 
sudden weather (external temperature) changes. Calculating time periods of these magnitudes 
with small enough time steps may pose a challenge in regard to the available calculation 
capacity of a standard laptop, and optimizing the calculation procedure and platform of the 
program may be necessary.

Chapter 7 Conclusion 
 
CFD simulations were used to investigate the pressure distribution in the plenum for different 
plenum configurations. Air was supplied to the plenum at velocities higher than 
recommended by the “rule of thumb” proposed by Rambøll, and calculations of the potential 
gap flows arising from obstacles blocking the airflow in the plenum resulted in values 
ranging from 0.24 −0.31 𝑚/𝑠 for 1 𝑚𝑚 gaps and from 0.34 −0.43 𝑚/𝑠 for 2 𝑚𝑚 gaps. 
As these values are all above the thermal comfort limit, the validity of the rule of thumb was 
confirmed for these cases and highlighted the importance of the plenum design.  
 
The isothermal CFD simulations were supplemented by full-scale measurements, where 
thermography was used as a method to document the surface temperature of the suspended 
ceiling with good results. Compared to the comprehensive resources required to perform 
thermal CFD analysis, thermography proved to be a valuable tool in the practical assessment 
of the performance of existing diffuse ceiling ventilation systems. The thermographic images 
indicated the airflow pattern in the plenum through temperature differences in the suspended 
ceiling, which were a result of local variations of the cooling capacity supplied to the room. 
The air temperature varied significantly within the plenum with a maximum measured 
temperature gradient of 3.3 ℃. Corresponding surface temperature variations of the slab 
above the plenum of up to 3.5 ℃ showed that heat transfers between the slab and the plenum 
air affected the cooling capacity of the supply air.  
 
The results of the full-scale measurements were reproduced by the 4-zone energy model and 
it could be concluded that the cooling capacity of the ventilation air was reduced with 23% 
before the air was supplied to the room.  
 
The developed 4-zone energy model provided an overview of the heat transfers affecting the 
supply air and showed that the cooling capacity is affected by the convective heat transfer 
between the slab and the plenum air but also by the heat exchange between the ventilation air 
and the acoustic tiles. The analysis of two air supply conditions for a room showed that the 
heat transfer from the slab to the plenum air was between 18 −53 𝑊/𝑚2 in the different 
zones with the highest heat transfer in the zones closest to the supply resulting in a total 
reduction of the effective cooling capacity of 31%. The model predicted a larger gradient of 
the operative temperature in the case with only one air supply to the plenum compared to the 
case with four air supplies.  
 
The energy model was set up for quasi-stationary conditions and did thereby not include the 
heat accumulation in the thermal mass of the system. To predict the changing heat transfer 
rate between the slab and the plenum and its dynamic impact on the cooling capacity of the 
ventilation, the transient heat accumulation must be coupled with the energy model. An 
approach of solving the accumulation model and energy model consecutively resulted in 
fluctuating results, indicating that the heat accumulation must be calculated simultaneously 
with the solving of the heat balances describing the whole system.  
==PAGE:88==

Successfully finalizing the dynamic model would create a valuable tool for the assessment of 
the dynamic performance of diffuse ventilation systems. With the heat accumulation of the 
thermal mass included in the model, the transient behavior of the system can be modelled, 
which could for example be utilized in the assessment of the night cooling potential with 
diffuse ventilation system.
