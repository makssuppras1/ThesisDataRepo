Abstract 
A comfortable thermal environment and good indoor air quality are essential for the occupant's health 
and well-being. The total volume room ventilation, such as mixing and displacement ventilation, is 
inefficient in providing clean air close to the occupant. In this regard, an advanced air distribution 
(AAD) based on removing pollution at the source and providing clean air to the breathing zone of 
occupants is a promising solution. The ventilated mattress (VM) that can remove pollutants from the 
source is an effective solution to capture bio effluents generated by human body before mixing with 
background air. Employing the VM has been shown to improve the room air quality at reduced 
background ventilation. However, the exhaust flow in the ventilated mattress can cause a nonuniform 
cooling effect which may be undesirable for the occupant in bed. Therefore, this thesis investigates 
employing a local heating solution to compensate for this local nonuniform cooling of the VM.  
The study aims to evaluate the performance of local heating of the mattress surface in mitigating the 
cooling effect of the VM. Also, the study aims to find the operational range and limits of the local 
heating in the VM and suggest modifications and improvements in the design of the VM. 
Two local heating sheets were placed inside the VM below the location of the feet and back of person 
lying on the VM. The local heating method was evaluated using experiments on thermal manikin lying 
on the VM. Experiments were performed at room temperatures 19oC, 23oC, and 28oC at an exhaust 
flow rate of 1.5 L/s, 3 L/s, 4.5 L/s, 6 L/s and 10 L/s through the VM. Measurements of the dry heat 
loss from the manikin’s body divided in 23 segments were performed with and without local heating. 
It was observed that as the exhaust flow rate of the VM increases, the dry heat loss of the manikin 
segments were also increased non uniformly, implying the cooling effect of the VM.  
A reference case at 23 oC without the VM, was assumed to be the thermally comfortable state for the 
occupant in the bed. When the VM was operated, the local heating was adjusted to bring the dry heat 
loss of the manikin segments close to the dry heat loss of the respective segments at the reference 
conditions.  
The study demonstrated that at 23 oC and the VM in operation,  the local heating could compensate 
for the local cooling for the studied range of flow rates through the mattress. However, it was not 
possible to compensate the dry heat loss from the thermal manikin at 19 oC room temperature when 
the exhaust flow through the VM was 6 L/s. In addition, the study showed adding thermal insulation 
on top of the VM substantially reduced the need for local heating.  Finally, it was shown that at 
elevated room temperatures in summer (28 oC) the VM needs to operate at very high exhaust flow rate 
to provide (around 10 L/s) a cooling effect to each of the body segments.

1 INTRODUCTION 
 
People spend a significant part of their lifetime indoors than outdoors. The indoor environment highly 
influences on health, productivity, and comfort of the occupants. Therefore, a healthy and comfortable 
environment is necessary for the quality performance of the occupants. Some studies have reported 
that a poor indoor environment can be costlier for the employer and building owner than the building's 
energy usage price [1]. Therefore, a good indoor environmental quality can improve the performance 
of the occupant while reducing absenteeism. Moreover, additional actions, such as opening windows 
that uncomfortable occupants take to make themselves, may have high energy implications if there is 
air conditioning or mechanical ventilation in the room [1]. 
A proper indoor environment comprises a good thermal climate, adequate ventilation, acoustic, and 
lighting. Adequate ventilation is essential for residential buildings and non-residential buildings to 
ensure a low health risk for occupants by reducing pollution concentration and avoiding the building 
damage caused due to condensation or mold growth. In addition, sensory pollution load from people, 
such as body odor from sweat and the skins' sebaceous secretions, foul breath, and gases from the 
digestive tract, can be present in the indoor air [2]. Additionally, health care facilities require high 
ventilation rates as the bio effluent emission is much higher in bedridden patients. With adequate 
ventilation, the contaminant concentration in the air can be diluted, and asepsis and odor can be 
reduced [3] 
In hospitals, providing adequate thermal comfort and ventilation based on individual preferences 
according to the diseases can reduce patients' recovery time. It is also equally essential to provide an 
effective airflow rate to protect medical staff from exposure to contaminants from patients. In [4]  a 
minimum total airflow rate of 10 L/s per person is recommended for a patient room. However, giving 
such high ventilation for the whole room is not energy efficient, and there is a possibility of draught 
discomfort due to high air velocity. Moreover, a clean ventilation air supplied far from the occupant 
can mix with the pollution before it reaches the occupant. Thus, the effectiveness of room ventilation 
in providing clean air to the occupant lying in bed is limited. Furthermore, this can contribute to the 
ineffective removal of body odor generated by compounds present in sweat, such as ammonia, 
reducing the perceived air quality [5]. In this regard, an advanced air distribution method that is 
personalized to supply cleaner air to the occupant and removing body generated bio-effluenets (i.e. 
pollution source control) can be an efficient solution for improving air quality and energy-saving by

reducing background ventilation levels [6]. 
1.1 
Thermal comfort 
 
In this section, a literature review on the past studies on thermal comfort is presented. 
 
1.1.1 Thermal comfort: room environment 
 
The thermal sensation of a person is mainly related to the whole body thermal balance. When the 
body's internal heat production equals the total loss of heat to the environment, thermal balance is 
obtained. Physical activity, clothing, and environmental parameters such as air temperature, mean 
radiant temperature, air velocity, and air humidity influence the thermal balance. The human 
thermoregulatory system automatically tries to modify skin temperature and sweat secretion in a 
moderate environment to maintain heat balance [7]. A literature review of thermal comfort studies 
conducted on hospitals and residential buildings is presented in this subsection. 
The thermal comfort of healthcare facilities and nursing homes is critical due to the vulnerability of 
the occupants. The hospitals also have widely varying individual thermal environment preferences as 
occupants can have different disease conditions. Furthermore, the space is shared with staff in good 
health conditions and higher metabolic rates with different thermal requirements. Such disparity 
between the occupant's thermal needs necessitates careful considerations. Several past research have 
studied the thermal comfort of occupants in hospitals. 
Khodakarami et al. [8] conducted a study to determine the requirement of occupants' thermal comfort 
in Iranian hospital patient rooms. The study considered patients with (1.39 clo) and without covering 
(0.49 clo) and staff with clothing of 0.88 clo. The study concluded that there is very little overlap in 
the acceptable air temperature range between staff and patients who cannot cover the body due to their 
medical condition. They reported to experience too cold indoor thermal conditions. The article 
suggested the need for different thermal zones for different user groups in the hospital.  
Hwang et al. [9] conducted an extensive field survey in a university hospital in Taiwan to determine 
the applicability of ASHRAE standard 55-2004 in hospitals for patients. The study assessed if the 
thermal environment stipulated by the standard is comfortable for the patients. The result showed that 
the patients in Taiwan prefer higher temperature than neutral temperature and the paper recommends 
a warmer environment for the patient than neutrality.  
Similarly, Smith and Ray [10] researched on patients' thermal comfort in hospital wards at a hospital 
in Scotland. The result shows that the patients obtain thermal comfort at air temperature between 20.5o 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
13 
 
C and 22o C, relative humidity in the range of 30% and 70 %, air velocity is less than 0.1 m/s, and 
mean radiant temperature was close to air temperature. Below the air temperature of 20.5 o C, the 
patient felt cold discomfort. Above 22 o C patients were becoming lethargic and dozy, but the 
complaints were not that strong.  
In [11] a field survey was carried out to assess the comfort temperature and thermal adaptation of 
patients and visitors in Malaysian hospitals. The study results showed that 22-28 o C was the thermally 
acceptable operative temperature for more than 86% of patients and hospital visitors. The thermal 
preference, overall comfort, and air quality were dependent on the health condition of the patients, but 
other characteristics like age, gender, and days hospitalized did not affect other thermal comfort 
parameters. The estimated mean comfortable temperature for patients was 25.3 o C and visitors was 
25.5 o C. 
Tartarini et al. [12] investigated occupants' thermal perceptions, preferences, and adaptive behavior in 
Australian nursing homes. A questionnaire survey and the measurement of local environmental 
parameters in their close proximity using portable equipment were used in the study. The result 
showed that inmates widely make behavioral adjustments on clothing and local air velocity control, 
such as opening windows or switching on personal cooling fans, to compensate for unsatisfactory 
thermal conditions. The adjustments in clothing insulation due to changes in operative temperature 
were found to depend upon age, metabolic rate and body mass index (BMI), and gender was not a 
significant factor. Furthermore, the residents were found to be more tolerant of temperature variations 
than non-residents. In summer, the neutral temperature for residents was 22.9o C, and for non-
residents, was 22o C. And the estimated comfort range for residents at a target level of PPD=10% is 
20-26.2 o C and for non-residents 19.8-24.2o C. 
For naked subjects without quilt covering, 29oC room air temperature was reported to be the 
thermoneutral temperature [13]. Furthermore, below 21oC  room air temperature, the sleep quality has 
significantly deteriorated [14] for naked subjects without covering. With a clothing level of 3.12 clo 
in the winter scenario (17 o C room temperature), a 23 o C temperature was comfortable for sleeping 
[15]. 
1.1.2 Thermal comfort: bed environment 
 
People spent a considerable amount of time in bed. Therefore, a comfortable bed environment is 
essential for the occupant's well-being, especially for bedridden patients in hospitals and elderly 
homes. Several past works have evaluated the thermal comfort requirements in bed microenvironment. 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
14 
 
It was indicated in [16] that the bed temperature had little variation between 28.6oC and 30.9oC when 
the room temperature was varied between 16oC and 25oC without any local heating and cooling. A 
similar conclusion was also made in [17].  An experimental study on the distribution of air temperature 
in the bed climate was conducted in [18]. They found that a comfort condition in the bed was achieved 
when the quilt temperature in contact with the human body reached 30.5◦C to 31.4◦C range. In 
addition, a temperature ranges from 29.9◦C to 30.9◦C and 26.8◦C to 28.3◦C was found to be the 
thermal comfort condition for bedding temperature in contact with below and side of the human body, 
respectively. 
These studies suggest that the occupant's thermoneutral temperature in bed is consistently around 30oC 
regardless of the bedding and ambient temperature. There could be inconsistency in the recommended 
bed temperature due to physiological features of subjects, differences in bed covering conditions, and 
different measuring points. However, all the above studies did not consider a personalized ventilation 
system in the bed. A consistent airflow around the occupant can influence the thermal comfort 
requirement by changing the equivalent insulation of bedding. In addition, air velocity, the direction 
of airflow, posture, relative humidity, the moisture permeability of quilts, sweating of people also 
affect the total insulation of the bedding system 
 
1.2 Ventilation 
 
1.2.1 Total volume room ventilation  
 
The air distribution in a room with occupant is generally done by two conventional mechanical 
ventilation principles, such as mixing ventilation or displacement ventilation. In mixing ventilation, 
clean outdoor air, either from the ceiling or wall, is supplied outside the occupant zone with high 
velocity. The velocity should be kept at a level that ensures an adequate mixing in the room, and, by 
the time air reaches the occupied zone, the air velocity has to fall to a required level. Mixing ventilation 
helps to maintain an even velocity and temperature distribution in the room by a high level of 
recirculation. The air pollutants get diluted by this recirculating flow and are finally removed through 
the exhaust opening. Complete mixing ensures a uniform air temperature and pollutant concentration 
in the entire volume of the room. 
In displacement ventilation, the clean outdoor air is directly supplied at a low velocity and low 
temperature to the occupied zone through a wall-mounted or floor-mounted ATD. The cooler supply 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
15 
 
air displaces across the floor level, and it replaces the polluted hot air to up. Then the air is removed 
through the exhaust, most often placed close to the ceiling. Due to the stratification of air, the 
ventilation efficiency, a measure of how quickly a contaminant is removed from the room, of 
displacement ventilation is higher than mixing ventilation. However, the room layout and position of 
the heat source influence the efficiency of displacement ventilation.  
 
1.2.2 Local ventilation system 
The room ventilation based on displacement and mixing ventilation is inefficient when the occupant 
is sedentary and the fresh air is supplied away from the occupant. In addition, the ventilation strategies 
try to maintain a uniform environment in the room which is not required when the number of the 
occupants are few. There could be substantial energy loss in maintaining background ventilation 
utilizing total volume  ventilation. Therefore, the local ventilation strategies explained below can be 
an effective method to increase the indoor air quality and result in energy savings. The local ventilation 
systems based on advanced air distribution can provide clean air close to the occupant, remove the air 
pollution, and generate heat locally, thereby creating a preferred microenvironment for the occupant. 
 
1.2.2.1 Advanced Air Distribution for hospital bed ventilation 
An advanced air distribution (AAD) based on providing a personalized microenvironment is a 
promising solution [6] for energy saving and higher IAQ. One of the most efficient locations for 
integrating the local ventilation system is the bed, considering the time spent by the occupants. The 
efficient removal of human bio effluents by bed integrated ventilation system effectively prevents 
and controls airborne infectious diseases [19] [20] [21]. An advanced AAD system for health care 
facilities, as shown in Figure 1-1, is reported in [19] . The AAD system comprises two devices named 
hospital bed integrated ventilation and a cleansing unit (HBIVCU) that removes the exhaled air close 
to the patient's breathing zone in the bed and prevents airborne cross-infection from patients in bed 
to the hospital environment. There are two air terminal devices installed close to the patients' bed and 
connected to the main unit which comprises of air conditioning and distribution box at the backside 
of the bed frame near the head of the patient. The contaminated air coming from the patient by the 
pulmonary activities is either removed to the room's exhaust system or cleaned by UVG light or other 
cleansing techniques embedded inside the main unit and then discharged back to the room. The clean 
air is supplied from the diffuser from one side of the bed in a horizontal direction towards the opposite 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
16 
 
direction to the exhaust diffuser. The clean air also supplies in a vertical and downward direction 
from two sides of the bed. The vertical air movement act as an air curtain that prevents the movement 
of polluted air to the patient in bed from the room. The downward air movement from two sides 
provides clean air to the breathing zone of the patient [22].

1.2.2.2 Source Control (Ventilated mattress) 
 
Removing pollutants from the source before mixing with the room air is an effective way to reduce 
the pollutant concentration in the room. Humans are a significant source of bio-effluents that can 
significantly reduce the quality of indoor air.  An AAD solution with source control in bed as shown 
in Figure 1-2 in was reported in [23], where the mattresses with an integrated local exhaust system 
known as a ventilated mattress (VM) were shown to effectively capture bio-effluents at reduced 
background ventilation, leading to energy savings and better indoor air quality [23].  
==PAGE:17==
 
 
17 
 
 
Figure 1-2: Ventilated mattress [23] 
The exhaust air from the VM could be connected to the exhaust of the background ventilation or the 
exhaust air could be filtered and discharged back into the room [5]. One of the important application 
of the VM is in hospitals and elderly home, where is important to maintain a high-quality indoor 
environment for the recovery of patients. Another important feature of the VM is the cooling effect 
provided as a result of the movement of air sucked through the mattress. This local cooling effect of 
the VM can enhance the thermal microenvironment of the VM, which can improve the occupant's 
thermal comfort [24]. Furthermore, the ability of the VM to create a thermal microenvironment can 
also facilitate better thermal comfort among occupants with diverse preferences and activity levels. 
 
1.3 Justification of present research 
 
Past research has demonstrated that source control using VM can effectively remove polluted air and 
increase air quality [5]. The local cooling effect of the VM was studied by analyzing the temperature 
and heat loss of the thermal manikin [24]. The study revealed that it is possible to cool the body 
segments of the manikin in contact with the mattress; thus, the VM can improve the thermal comfort 
of the people lying in bed in a warm environment. However, the cooling effect of the VM was found 
to be nonuniform, which can potentially cause thermal discomfort for the occupant. In such a scenario, 
the room heating setpoint might need to be increased to offset the VM's cooling effect, leading to 
increased heating energy demand. However, even with an increased room temperature setpoint, the 
occupant may not achieve thermal comfort due to the nonuniform nature of the cooling effect in the 
VM. In this regard, utilizing a local heating solution for the VM could be beneficial. However, no 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
18 
 
study in literature addressed local heating solutions for compensating the local cooling effect of the 
VM. Furthermore, the cooling Performance of the VM at lower room temperature, below 23oC, is not 
known.  
Local heating methods can be used to improve the thermal perception of the human body. In addition, 
local heating can effectively target the occupant and thus reduce the room/building heating, which can 
also lead to considerable energy savings. The commonly used local heating methods for beds are 
electric blankets and heated mattress pads. Results from studies [25] [26] confirm the effectiveness of 
local heating in improving bed thermal comfort in winter conditions. A detailed study of local heating 
methods for bed microenvironment available in the market and literature review is available in [27]. 
However, such heating solutions are developed to provide uniform heating for the bed 
microenvironment. Therefore, this thesis studies effect of targeted, localized heating solutions for the 
VM, which can mitigate the local cooling effect of the VM.

2 OBJECTIVES 
 
The main objectives of the present experimental research are:  
• To study the performance of different methods for local heating of the ventilated mattress surface and its 
control with focus on:  
• 1) the provided maximum local heating power in cool environment (i.e. lower range 
of room temperature) and  
• 2) not deteriorating the available cooling capacity of the mattress needed during high 
room temperatures 
• To identify the local heating method and its operational ranges providing thermal comfort in bed at 
minimal body heating asymmetry and needed power. 
• To suggest modifications and improvements in the design of the VM for providing preferred thermal 
comfort of users.

3 METHODOLOGY  
3.1 Experimental facilities 
 
The experiment was performed in a full-scale test room arranged as a single bedroom. The dimension 
of the room was 4.68 m × 4.7 m × 2.6 m (W × L × H). The occupant in the bed was simulated using 
a thermal manikin operating in comfort mode. In the comfort  mode of the manikin program, a deep 
body temperature of 36.4oC is maintained, and the thermal resistance of the skin (0.054 m2 oC/W) is 
simulated. The power output is then dependent on the actual external temperature of the manikin. A

3.1.2 Ventilated mattress  
The ventilated mattress (VM) used for the experiments was purchased from an online shop. The 
surface of the VM was made up of synthetic fiber, and it is permeable for air. The exhaust opening of 
the VM was located at the feet region. The exhaust opening at the feet was covered with a polyester 
mesh. The sucked exhaust air moved through the VM and was taken out from the top of the mattress 
at the left and right sides, as shown in Figure 3-3. There were no provisions for measuring and 
regulating the exhaust airflow rate of the VM. Therefore, it was required to modify the VM for the 
present research. 
==PAGE:22==

The local exhaust flow rate from the ventilated mattress (VM) was adjusted by using a three-brunch 
duct system (Figure 3-4). ''Branch 1'' was connected to the VM, and ''branch 2'' sucked air from outside 
the test room. A fan (working at a fan speed of 50 Hz) was connected to the main ''branch 3'', which  
was connected to  ''branch 1'' and ''branch 2'' with a T-piece. The exhaust airflow rate from the VM 
was controlled by manually adjusting the damper connected at ''branch 1''. The pressure drop across 
''branch 2'' and ''branch 3'' was measured using micromanometer FCO510 connected to ''branch 2'' and 
''branch 3''.  The flow rate was calculated at respective branches by multiplying the area of each branch 
of the duct with the measured pressure drop. The flow rate through ''branch 3'' was the sum of ''branch 
1'' and ''branch 2''. Therefore, by subtracting the flow rate of ''branch 2'' from ''branch 3'' obtained the 
exhaust airflow of the VM (''branch 1'')(Figure 3-3).

3.3 Experimental Conditions 
 
According to the ASHRAE standard [3], the specified ideal temperature for thermal comfort for a 
general patient room is 20°C - 24°C. Also, according to the Danish standard DS/ISO/TR 17772-:2018  
[30] standard in residential buildings for bedrooms of IEQ I and II category, the recommended design 
values of the indoor operative temperature in winter and summer are 21°C (for winter, IEQ I), 25.5°C 
(for summer IEQ I), and 20°C (for winter, IEQ II) and 26°C (for summer, IEQ II) respectively. 
Therefore, in this study, it is assumed that a room temperature of 23°C was thermally comfortable for 
the occupant lying down naked and covered up to the neck with a duvet of 600 g. The occupant is 
considered naked for the reference case because, in reality, some people like to sleep naked in bed. 
Furthermore, in hospitals and elderly homes due to some medical condition of the patient's demands 
to be naked in bed. 
As mentioned in the “Measuring instruments” section, manikin emulating the human subjects with 
comfort mode control was chosen. For the experiment, a summer period with a room temperature of 
28°C ± 0.5°C and winter period with a room temperature of 19 °C ± 0.5 °C were considered.  The 
winter and summer room temperatures are chosen to be slightly below and above the minimum and 
maximum temperature specified in the the DS/ISO/TR 17772-:2018 [30]. This is because it is 
hypothesized that the VM can provide thermal comfort at expanded lower and upper room temperature 
limits recommended in the standard DS/ISO/TR 17772. This is a well-known strategy  to achieve 
energy savings in buildings [31] [32]. The type of duvet was adjusted according to the room 
temperature so that a thin 600 g duvet was used for 23 °C room temperature case, 1055 g duvet was 
used in the case simulating winter conditions (19°C), and a thin sheet was used in the case simulating 
summer conditions (28°C). The manikin was lying in the supine position with a headrest on a pillow. 
For clothing, long sleeve pajama and short sleeve pajama were used during the experiments at room 
temperature of 19 oC and 23 oC. A short pajama including a singlet and shorts was used at 28oC room 
temperatures. 
Experiments were performed at the three room temperatures  and at an exhaust flow rate of 1.5 L/s, 3 
L/s, 4.5 L/s, 6 L/s, and 10 L/s with and without local heating. The summary of the studied cases is 
shown in Table 3-A.

3.4 Experimental procedure  
 
To keep the air temperature in the room stable, the total volume ventilation system and the manikin 
were working all the time. The measurements started after the steady-state level of the desired 
temperature in the room was reached. 
Before starting the experiments, the velocity in the room around the bed was measured to ensure the 
air speed was low around the bed. The measured air speed in the room was lower than 0.1 m/s. Firstly, 
the reference condition was created in the room by adjusting the temperature to 23°C. It is assumed 
that the reference case presents a thermally comfortable state. Then, different cases of experiments, as 
shown in Table 3-A, were conducted. The manikin's segmental and whole-body cooling effect for 
each of the cases discussed in Table 3-A was assessed by comparing the dry heat loss data obtained 
for the studied condition with that of the reference case.  
If the heat loss in manikin segments were higher than the heat loss of the respective segment in 
reference case, the local heating was provided. The local heating power was adjusted using a 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
32 
 
transformer to reduce the body heat loss and make it closer to the reference case. In addition to 
studying the variation in the occupant's clothing, the cases were repeated with short sleeve pajama, 
long sleeve pajamas, and short pajamas. Furthermore, the impact of bed insulation is also studied in 
this report. Figure 3-4 shows the manikin setup with long sleeve pajama, short sleeve pajama and short 
pajama, the position on bed thermal insulation is also shown in the figure.  
The temperatures measured below the left and right thigh of the manikin, close to the bottom part, 
below back, below upper back, and inside the VM at the exhaust duct were recorded along with 
manikin heat loss data and power used for the local heating (Figure 3-10). 
 
3.5 Data analysis and presentation. 
 
The manikin software logs the dry heat loss data every 10 seconds. In addition to the dry heat loss of 
every segment of the manikin, the manikin software also outputs weighted mean dry heat loss of all 
manikin segments, which is the mean value of dry heat loss of all body segments weighted according 
to the area of body segments. 
Data analysis was started first by calculating the average of logged dry heat loss of individual body 
segments of the manikin for each case. The average of dry heat loss was calculated for a ten minutes 
interval after the manikin reaches a steady state. Henceforth in this report, this averaged value of dry 
heat loss is called average dry heat loss.   
Similarly, the averaged value of weighted mean dry heat loss of all manikin segments provided by 
manikin software was also calculated for 10 minutes intervals after steady state. Hereafter, this 
averaged value is called the average weighted mean dry heat loss.  
The cooling effect of the VM was evaluated by comparing the data of average dry heat loss obtained 
of each body segment for the cases with a different exhaust flow rate of the VM against the reference 
case data. In addition, the weighted mean dry heat loss of the manikin's all body segments provided 
by the manikin software was also used for the comparative analysis. 
Two metrics were used to quantify the analysis presented in the next chapter.  
The difference between the average dry heat loss of each body segment and the average heat loss 
obtained in the reference case of the respective body segment (designated as ΔQ) was calculated first.  
ΔQ  was used to define ΔQmax and the root mean square deviation(RMSD) (ΔQrms) . 
Where ΔQmax of the measured case is the maximum value of the ΔQ among each of the body 
segments. ΔQmax was estimated as shown in equation (1):

5 DISCUSSION 
 
5.1 Thermal comfort of the body segments in contact with the VM and the body as a 
whole 
 
The suction of the VM continuously displaces the air in the bed microenvironment, and it is replaced 
by the air under the cover at ambient temperature, which is lower than the manikin segment 
temperature. This difference in temperature coupled with the airflow leads to increased heat transfer 
by convection between the manikin and air in the bed microenvironment.  In addition, there is 
conductive heat loss from the manikin body through the mattress cover. Different body segments of 
the manikin are exposed to the different microenvironments in the bed, leading to different dry heat 
loss. For instance, the dry heat loss from the crown, face, and neck exposed to the room air temperature 
is higher than other body segments exposed to the bed microenvironment. Even inside the bed 
microenvironment covered with duvet, there are differences in heat loss of the body segments.  For 
example, the chest, pelvis, and front thigh are in contact with the duvet and heatloss occurs by 
convection to air below the duvet and conductive heat loss to the duvet. The backside, back, and back 
thigh are firmly in contact with the mattress hence conductive heat loss to the mattress is dominant. 
However,  some areas of these segments may not be in contact with the mattress, therefore convection 
heat loss is for these areas are present. On the other hand, as feet, legs, and, arms have a larger exposure 
to the airflow through the VM with high convective heat loss and with limited conductive heat loss. 
The experimental results showed that the dry heat loss from the body segments of the manikin 
increases with the exhaust flow rate of the VM at all studied room temperatures. The heat loss is higher 
with a higher exhaust flow rate because a higher exhaust flow rate means more air in the bed 
microenvironment is displaced with cooler ambient room air at a higher velocity thus increasing the 
convective heat transfer. In addition, higher airflow through the VM also means that the mattress's 
effective thermal insulation is reduced, increasing conductive heat loss in the body segments in contact 
with the mattress.  The measured surface temperature on the VM also decreases with an increase in 
exhaust flow rate through the VM. The largest drop in surface temperature with respect to increasing 
the VM’s exhaust flow rate was observed below the back of the manikin and close to the bottom part. 
As explained, these body segments are firmly in contact with the VM. Thus, the drop in surface 
temperature also implies that the heat loss in these body segments ares primarily due to conduction. 
The temperature measurement inside the exhaust of the VM was almost the same for all the studied 
flow rates without local heating. This could be because the temperature difference between the 
==PAGE:64==
64 
 
 
ambient air flowing through the VM and the bed microenvironment remains the same. 
A previous study on thermal comfort evaluation study of VM [24] also showed that there was 
nonuniform cooling to the body segments of the manikin. The study also reported that the cooling 
effect increased with an increase in the exhaust rate of the VM, for manikin in the supine posture, the 
largest cooling was observed in the back and backside. This thesis also has simillar conclusions. In 
addition, a substantial cooling effect was also observed at the feet and arms in this study. This 
difference could be due to the change in the configuration of the VM. 
In this study, to reduce this heat loss from the body, the surface temperature of the bed in contact with 
the manikin is increased by giving the required heating level using the local heating sheet. Two local 
heating sheets were embedded in the VM below the mesh,  at feet and back. This configuration at the 
feet was chosen to avoid blocking of suction as shown in Figure 3-6. Also, for safety reasons, it is 
advantageous to place the local heating sheet below the mesh.  
With local heating, the temperature difference between the body and the surface of the VM is reduced, 
resulting in decreased heat loss from the body. However, it would be more effective to compensate 
the heat loss by providing heating through conduction than through convection as it was done in the 
present study.  An increase in the surface temperature of the VM in contact with the manikin segment 
can directly compensate for the conductive heat loss. Therefore, it is comparatively easier to reduce 
the heat loss in body segments such as the back and backside using the local heating sheet. Whereas, 
for body segments with primarily convective heat loss, which are not in firm contact with the VM, 
such as arms and feet, compensating with the local heating is more challenging. The surface 
temperature of the VM in the compensated case is highly dependent on the mode of heat loss from the 
manikin. For instance, for body segments with primarily conductive heat loss, the surface temperature 
with local heating (compensated) will be close to the surface temperature of the corresponding 
manikin segment at the reference case. Whereas for body segments with high convective heat loss, 
the surface temperature with local heating (compensated) will be higher than the surface temperature 
of the corresponding manikin segment at the reference case. This is because the heat transfer from the 
surface of the VM to these body segments is indirect. 
From the literature review, the bed temperature range for thermal comfort was consistently reported 
to around 28oC - 30oC. However, for compensating the heat loss in the VM, the bed surface 
temperature is higher than these values at some cases. The implication of this high temperature needs 
to be studied with human subject experiments.  The implemented heating method using a local heating 
sheet reduced the dry heat loss of the manikin. However, the effectiveness of the heating method 
==PAGE:65==
65 
 
 
varied depending upon the body segments and exhaust flow rate. For instance, for the room 
temperatures of 23 oC and 19 oC, the heat loss from the feet and arms was higher compared to other 
body segments. It may be because only a smaller area of feet and arms were in contact with the bed 
and duvet surface, resulting in higher exposure to the air movement, causing the cooling effect. Also, 
as mentioned before, the feet of the manikin are placed above the exhaust opening, which creates a 
higher dry heat loss in the feet segment. The possible larger exposure to the air movement caused by 
the VM for the feet and arms results in more considerable heat loss to these body segments, and a 
detailed numerical simulation would be required to analyze the airmovement in the 
microenevironment.  
At 23 oC room temperature without thermal insulation in the bed, it was possible to reduce the dry 
heat loss from the body segments to comfort level except at arms only using the local heating sheets.  
Furthermore, with thermal insulation in the bed, the thermal comfort level can be achieved without 
extra heating at 23 oC room temperature. The summary of heating provided at 23oC room temperature

room temperature itself from 19 oC to 23 oC. An example case with an assumed room of size 4.6 m x 
4.6 m X 2.6 m with one external wall is used for the calculation. The considered U value of the external

W, which is significantly less than the required heating to warm up the whole room air. Therefore, it 
can be concluded that a more energy-efficient method is to use the VM at 19 oC  with local heating 
than increasing the background room temperature. 
5.3 Recommendation for further development of the VM 
From the studies conducted for the VM, it was revealed that during winter conditions, the cooling 
effect of the VM is a constraint in implementing the VM. Although the VM can effectively remove 
pollutants, a compensation of cooling effect is needed in winter conditions. For this reason, it can be 
beneficial to change the exhaust connection location, where the air is taken out from the VM and 
discharged outside the room. If the exhaust connection location is placed exactly below the suction 
opening of the VM instead of being at the opposite end of the mattress near the head, it is possible to 
limit the cooling effect at upper body parts. Since, in this way there will be no air movement through 
the mattress but only at the feet. Thus, the cooling effect of the VM can be reduced as there is no 
airflow inside the VM below the person lying in bed. Therefore, to a certain extent, it is possible to 
reduce the heat loss from the person's body at low room air temperatures, while the VM removes the 
pollution load. This recommendation is possible for the exhaust of the VM connected to the exhaust 
of the background ventilation. Also it is possible to discharge the air back to the room, if an air filter 
could be placed either below the mesh where suction opening is located or in the exhaust duct before 
the fan. 
However, during summertime, cooling of the body is required for preferred thermal comfort of the 
occupant. Therefore, for summertime, there should be provision to pass the air through the VM below 
the body. So it could be beneficial the ventilated mattress to have  two connections for an exhaust 
duct,one from feet below the suction opening for wintertime and one near the head  for summertime 
==PAGE:70==
70 
 
 
as shown in Figure 5-2. This modification makes it possible to reduce the need for extra heating to the 
VM and make it energy-efficient.  
Furthermore, the study noted that the feet require more heating as the feet were placed exactly above 
the exhaust opening of the VM. So to reduce the need for heating at feet,  the possibility of the exhaust 
opening on two sides of the VM is shown Figure 5-3. In this way, the feet are not exactly above the 
exhaust opening, and the air movement causing heat loss at feet could be limited.Using a heated net 
above the suction opening  which can heat the air which flows through the mattress could also reduce 
the local heat loss of the body parts through the VM (by conduction).Finally, it has to be noted that 
all the recommendations given are based on the assumption that the results taken with manikin also 
holds true for humans.  
To make the VM energy-efficient, a VM control that coordinates the airflow through the VM and 
local heating element reduces the local cooling effect by implementing a closed-loop temperature 
control as a further improvement. This can be achieved by measuring the surface temperature of the 
VM and then automatically adjusting the local heating or flow rate of the VM. In addition, such control 
could be coordinated with room ventilation and heating to achieve optimal energy efficiency.

Also, in practice, it is harder to define the heating zone for the VM with human subject as the subject

5.4 Recommendations for human subject experiment  
 
The study shows that the heat loss is higher at feet, and arms and require more heating to compensate 
for the cooling effect of the VM. First of all, in human subject experiments, it has to be confirmed if 
such higher heat loss creates thermal discomfort for a person lying on VM. Furthermore, the heating 
level at feet depends on individuals; some people like cold feet; therefore, it could be better to use 
socks rather than local heating at these locations. Alternatively, rather than increasing the heating level 
at feet to reduce the increased heat loss, a heated blanket to reduce the high heat loss during the human 
subject experiment could be evaluated. Finally, for the conducted studies, the hand of the manikin is 
always placed below the blanket. The impact of arm position on heat loss is not investigated, in reality, 
==PAGE:72==
72 
 
 
the position of arms changes for a person lying on bed. Therefore, local heating at arms could be 
different for the human subject experiment, which needs to be verified. 
The heat loss is compensated in this project by increasing the local surface temperature of the VM, in 
some cases it is higher than 32 oC. The consequences of increased bed temperature need to be 
investigated during the human subject trials. 
For the study, only one position of the manikin was tested, but in reality, people lying on the bed have 
different positions. Therefore, the cooling effect of the VM and the effectiveness of the local heating 
when an occupant in bed with different positions can also be verified during the human subject 
experiment.

6 CONCLUSIONS 
 
This study was performed to improve the design of the Ventilated Mattress for providing preferred 
thermal comfort for the user. The experimental results reveal that: 
• The cooling effect of the VM increases with the exhaust flow rates of the VM. Thus the 
demand for heating increases with the increasing exhaust flow rate of the VM; 
• The cooling effect of the VM is nonuniform and can only be compensated using local heating 
elements; 
• Because of the convective heatloss due to air movement in the bed microenvironment, the 
surface temperature of the VM with local heating is higher than that in the reference case  
• It is possible to reduce the local cooling effect of the VM with two local heating sheets below 
the feet and back under the mesh of the VM and carbon fiber heating elements below the arms; 
• At 23oC room temperature, the local heating can compensate for the local cooling for the 
studied range of flow rates (1.5, 3, 4.5 and 6 L/s) through the mattress;  
• At 23oC room temperature, with the addition of thermal insulation on top of the VM, local 
cooling effect is eliminated, and thus, local heating is not needed; 
• At 19oC room temperature, it is not possible to compensate for the VM heat loss when the 
exhaust flow is 6 L/s., also the surface temperature reaches high value (38.2 oC). However, 
such a high exhaust flow rate may not be needed to remove the pollution load; 
• At 19oC room temperature, at 1.5 L/s and 3 L/s exhaust flow rate of the VM, the local heating 
can compensate the cooling effect, with a heating power 24 W and 41 W respectively without 
considering arms; 
• The carbon fiber heating elements placed below arms is required to eliminate the cooling effect 
of arms; 
• At 19oC room temperature,the addition of thermal insulation on top of the VM significantly 
reduced the need of local heating; at 1.5 L/s and 3 L/s exhaust flow rate of the VM, the needed 
local heating is reduced to 9W and 30 W without considering arms; 
• With the addition of the thermal insulation on top of the VM,  surface temperatures variability 
due to high exhaust flow is significantly reduced;  
• The pajama had minimal impact on the manikin heatloss, The difference in the heat loss with 
and without pajama was less than 6 W/m2 for most body segments;
