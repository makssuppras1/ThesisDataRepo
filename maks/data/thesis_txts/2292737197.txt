Abstract
Danish municipalities’ economy is under economical pressure, and at the same time a lot of
the municipalities have a challenge with public real estate, which is not used in an eﬃcient
manner. These years, the Danish society focuses a lot on sustainability, which is reﬂected in
the strategies and policies the municipalities run with. Many municipalities has never had
a strategy for public real estate, and now that they are forced to address the issue due to
economical pressure and sustainable goals, a lot of the municipalities do not have a method
for addressing the issue.
The aim of this project has been to ﬁnd a method for municipalities to use when addressing
the challenge with public real estate, which is not used in an eﬃcient manner.
Shared space is a strategic tool within space management, which can be used to optimize the
use of space. The aim is to ﬁnd a method where shared space is used to optimize the use of
public real estate.
Shared space: More than one player, who uses the same space either at the same time or
at diﬀerent times.
The motivation for the municipalities to implement shared space is that it will give sustain-
able beneﬁts in the areas of environment, social and economy and thereby add value to the
municipality and in the end provide a better service for the citizens of the municipality.
The project has followed a pilot project in Lundtofte to study an example on how shared space
can be implemented in public estate to optimize the use of public real estate. Lundtofte is
a part of Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality in Denmark north from Copenhagen, and the pilot
project looked at nine buildings and 12.660 m2 including a school at 7.120 m2.
The project in Lundtofte ended with a scenario, where up to ﬁve of the public buildings were
to be sold and the municipality would then have a decrease of square meters in Lundtofte by
up to 24 %. During the project the municipality focused on public participation which they
got a positive response on.

social responsibility by letting diﬀerent groups of the society interact. It will result in a im-
proved service which supports public participation in society and all together it will support
the sustainable development in the municipality.
This project will go on to show how service design can be used for improving a service oﬀered

Resumé
Danske kommuners økonomi er under pres, og samtidigt har en del kommuner udfordringer
med oﬀentlige bygninger, der ikke bliver udnyttet særlig eﬀektivt. I Danmark er der stor
fokus på bæredygtighed ,som også afspejler sig i kommunernes politikker og strategier. Mange
kommuner har ikke tidligere haft en ejendomsstrategi for de oﬀentlige bygninger i kommunen
og er derfor nu tvunget til at tage stilling til de oﬀentlige bygninger grundet økonomien og de
bæredygtige mål, som kommuner har sat sig. Ofte har kommunerne dog ikke haft en metode
for, hvordan man kan gribe denne opgave an.
Formålet med dette projekt har været at ﬁnde en metode, der kan anvendes af kommunerne
i deres udfordringer med dårligt udnyttede oﬀentlige bygninger. Shared space er et værktøj,
der kan bruges i space management, som kan benyttes til at optimere brugen af oﬀentlige
bygninger.
Shared space: Flere aktører, der gør brug af den samme plads, enten samtidig eller på
forskellige tidspunkter.
Motivationen for at benytte shared space: bæredygtige fordele i forhold til miljøet, et socialt
og økonomisk aspekt, som vil tilføre kommunerne værdi og i sidste ende være med til at
udbyde en bedre service til borgerne.
Metode Projektet har fulgt et pilot projekt i Lundtofte, en del af Lyngby-Taarbæk Kommune.
Det er et studie af, hvordan shared space kan implementeres I oﬀentlige bygninger med henblik
på at til at optimere brugen heraf. Projektets behandler 9 bygninger på 12.660 m2.

sælges og kommunen reducere antallet af kvadratmeter med op til 24 %. I løbet af Lundtoftepro-
jektet var der stor fokus på inddragelse af brugerne og borgerne, hvilket kommunen ﬁk en
meget positiv respons på.

2.1
Facilities management
Facilities management(FM) was ﬁrst introduced as a term in 1970 in USA and got even more
focus with the establishment of the International Facility Management Association(IFMA) in
1980. The vision for IFMA is to serve as the ultimate resource and representative for facility
management professionals and to be the leader in promoting excellence in management of the
work environment (IFMA, 2012). Today IFMA includes 22,659 members represented in 97
countries (IFMA, 2012).
The term FM was not introduced in Denmark until 1980, and with the establishment of
Danish Facilities Management Association(DFMA)in 1991 the knowledge and awareness of
FM increased (Jensen, 2008).
Today a lot of diﬀerent FM associations and institutions are promoting, developing and shar-
ing the knowledge of FM both locally in each countries and internationally.
You can ﬁnd many deﬁnitions for FM. European standard deﬁnes FM as "Facilities Manage-
ment is the integration of processes in an organisation to maintain and develop the agreed
services, which support and improve the eﬀectiveness of the primary processes" (standards,
2006). The deﬁnition is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1. Thus, FM is deﬁned as a support function
or secondary production to the core business or primary production. Figure 2.1 shows how
FM is used on a strategic, tactical and operational level. Jensen describes these three levels
as follows (Jensen, 2008, p.11):
Strategical tasks have a long-term nature and relate to policies and strategies for develop-
ment of the property portfolio and support functions in relation to the overall strategies and
objectives of a corporation and structural changes in the surroundings.
Tactical tasks include planning of speciﬁc changes in the property portfolio and support
functions, partly to meet the long-term policies and strategies, partly with a view to adapt
to other changes within the corporation, for instance related to the current state of the market.

2.2
Sustainable Development
In 1987 the UN Brundtland Commission released the Brundtland report named Our Common
Future. In the report UN deﬁned sustainable development as "development that meets the
needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own
needs" (Brundtland Commission, 1987). Today this citation is still the most widely used
deﬁnition on sustainable development even though the deﬁnition of sustainable development
has been discussed and criticised by many and new deﬁnitions of sustainable development
have been presented after the Brundtland Commission (Wheeler, 2004).
Sustainable development represents a process for "social progress and redirecting our economies
to enable all people to meet their basic needs and improve their quality of life while insuring
the natural systems resources and diversity" (Shah, 2007, p.14). The three objects social,
economical and environmental all represent issues, which impact the daily life of citizens, or-
ganisations and governments. Some of the issues within the three object are showed in ﬁgure
2.3.
Figure 2.3: Issues and criteria for the three sustainable development objects(Shah, 2007).

objects and their issues within; poverty, level of consuming, human rights, biodiversity. How-

2.2.1
Sustainable Development and FM
Many of the issues within the three objects of sustainable development can be inﬂuenced by
FM such as energy consuming, work environment, waste and recycling, health and safety ect.
Thus sustainable FM(SFM) can inﬂuence colleagues, the management and the community
around the organisation to understand the beneﬁts and impacts of sustainable development
(Shah, 2007).
Case studies have shown the importance of the top management support for adapting sus-
tainable FM(SFM) toward the level of sustainable development in the organisation (Jensen
and Nielsen, 2008). However, there is still a perception on green buildings as costly, unproven
and therefore more ﬁnancially risky (Broughton, 2006).
In SFM there is a massive focus on green buildings. Green buildings are measured by diﬀerent
indicators, which determine how green the building in question is. The indicators and how
they are measured can vary from country to country, but common for them all is they focus on
environmental aspect of a building as energy consumption, indoor climate and waste (Jensen,
2008).
Green buildings do not only have environmental beneﬁts, they also reduce the operation
cost due to reduction of energy and they improve the health of the occupants due to a
good indoor climate which again results in improved work performance and productivity
(Broughton, 2006). Exactly the economic beneﬁts are very important, as studies have found
that economic beneﬁts and human comfort aspects are some of the strongest motivations
for adapting SFM(Lee and Kang, 2013).
However, organisations may also have another
motive for adapting SFM. If an organisation has the chance of being associated with an
environment friendly workplace it can attract employees, since people feel they are helping
with environmental challenges by being involved with green buildings as that "it is the right
thing to do" (Broughton, 2006).
Community-based facilities do also support sustainable development by "provide the oppor-
tunity for genuine involvement of the communities in the design and management of services
and the urban environment" (Alexander and Brown, 2006) and "various studies have pointed
to beneﬁts in the form of personal satisfaction, a better social life, improved educational out-
comes, crime reduction and social inclusion" (Neighbourhood Renewal Unit (ODPM), 2003).
2.2.2
Public Participation
According to the Brundtland Commission, public or citizen participation is extremely im-
portant in order to meet the requirements for sustainable development. In the Brundtland

"requires not only a new era of economic growth for nations in which the majority are poor,
but an assurance that those poor get their fair share of the resources required to sustain
that growth. Such equity would be aided by political systems that secure eﬀective citizen
participation in decision making"(Brundtland Commission, 1987, sec. 28).

2.3
Space Management
As an FM task space management is primarily used on a tactical and operational level. The
tactical approach to space management is a result of the strategic for the organisation. Space
management does therefore not necessarily have a stratigic plan but is simply carried out on
the basis of the company’s strategic plan(Jensen, 2008).
Space management includes analysis’ of spaces to meet the demand of diﬀerent legislations.
Furthermore it includes changes in the physical frame of the organisation and how to adapt
the organisation and staﬀto the changes. The design of workplaces can have a very tactical
inﬂuence on a strategical plan for an organisation and is therefore an FM task and a part of
space management.
2.3.1
Space standards
To manage space it is necessary to make an analysis of the space and see what the space is
used for. In Denmark the space of buildings has to be assessed due to the diﬀerent regulations.
Shared space in this subsection 2.3.1 refer to toilet, bathroom, wardrobes, reception, garage,
canteen, kitchen, post rooms.
When space of a building is analysed with key indicators from a space management perspec-
tive, the key indicators can be divided in two:
1. Space used for typical functions, these indicators are used for planning and
covers for instance space per oﬃce.
The gross/net ratio is important and often used as a goal for space eﬃciency and as planning
tool when building new. The diﬀerence between gross space and net space is the structural
space, the gross/net ratio is typically between 1.1-1.15 (Jensen, 2008).

Changes in an organisation often result in relocation of staﬀ, churns, due to activity ad-

space strategy is process (Pratt, 1997). Reconstruction or maintenance can be a part of a

Shared space in urban planning
Shared space used in a larger scale is a relatively new tool used in facilities management.
However, the concept of shared space has been used for many years in urban planning and is
widespread as a tool to control the traﬃc and the use of the streets. Shared space in urban

Shared space requires extra attention and awareness of the surroundings, otherwise there can

With shared space in urban planning the users have to be aware of each other and the
surroundings which makes it necessary for an extended communication between the diﬀerent
users compared with other types of streets.
Shared space in FM
For many years the concept ’hot desks’ or shared workstations has been widely known concept
and there are many examples on it used in oﬃces design. Some motivations behind hot desk
have for the companies been the reduction of occupancy costs, and in some cases increasing
employee wellbeing and productivity at the same time (Kleeman, 1992).
Sharing space in a larger scale with external players, has not been practised in the same
extend. Even though it has been practised for many years in relation to public real estates
as schools in Denmark, who after the school-day has ended are sharing the facilities with
external associations.
Shared space in FM can be deﬁned as: "More than one player, who uses the same space either
at the same time or at diﬀerent times."

This paper is based on a case study. A case study is a study of one single case. Alan Bryman

alizability can be questioned. However, a case chosen for a study has by the researcher been
identiﬁed as a typical case that represents something more general, it is therefore possible to
look at a case as more than one individual sample (Bryman, 2008).

Case description
In Lyngby-Taarbæk Municipality, a new strategy for the public real estate was introduced in
2013. Lyngby-Taarbæk Municipality is based north from Copenhagen in Denmark. Due to
the new strategy, a pilot project was started in August 2014. The aim of the pilot project was
to make the use of the public real estate in Lundtofte - a residential area in Lyngby-Taarbæk
Municipality - more eﬃcient.
5.1
Lyngby-Taarbæk Municipality
The background for the project was a new strategy for the public real estate in Lyngby-
Taarbæk Municipality, which was introduced in 2013 (Udviklings– og Strategiudvalget, 2013).
The main goal for the strategy is to ensure that the public real estate in Lyngby-Taarbæk
Municipality is used eﬃciently. The strategy lines up following subjects.
• Space management
• Shared space
• Sustainable development
• Economy
• Service
Space management is introduced in order to map the buildings, map out how they are used
and how they can be more ﬂexible and used more eﬃciently. Shared space is introduced in the
strategy as a tool within space management and the goal is to use the public real estate more
eﬃciently. The municipality wish to use the ﬁrst type of shared use, which are illustrated in
ﬁgure 2.6, because it is a semi-closed community and second several of the facilities in the
municipality are going to be shared.
The municipality wants to focus on sustainable development, which is reﬂected in the strategy
for the public real estate. The focus is on reducing the CO2 emission and make the citizens
participate in the process towards making the municipality more sustainable.
A total economy for the public estate will make it possible to make a long term plan for
maintenance and renovation of the public real estate in Lyngby-Taarbæk Municipality.

• The associations do not use the public real estate in Lundtofte eﬃciently due to too

• The public real estate in Lundtofte is in need of a renovation.

How can the municipality use the public real estate in Lundtofte more eﬃciently?

Evaluation of step
The template Observation focuses on the users’ or target groups’ behaviour, while LPP focus
on the estate. That means that while SDTK begin by focusing on the users of the service and
their needs, LPP - to a greater extend - focus on how the service can meet the aim of the new
strategy. This means LPP start out the project by prioritising the need of the municipality
rather the needs of the users of the service.
The project was started by the municipality due to issues with the end product of the service;
the public estate, and not because the users were demanding changes. This might be the
reason why LPP and SDTK chose to focus on diﬀerent things during the observation.
In order to deliver the best service for the users and citizens and meet the needs of the target
groups, it is possible that LPP would have beneﬁted from observing the users doing their
activities, because the response from the target group has a big inﬂuence on whether the
service ends up being a success or not. However, the target group came with input to the
project at the workshops, thus they were included in the project and their views and needs
were discussed by LPP.
If LPP had only observed the users as SDTK does, without taken notes of the estates, LPP
could have lost the purpose of why the project was started and the frames sat for it and
instead given the target groups falls expectation on their inﬂuence of the service.
SDTK suggests to either make a descriptive reporting or observation scheme however, LPP
did non of them. During the initial walk, some photos were taken of the real estate, but no
impression of the estates were written down, although no notes were taken of the estates,
LPP did have documents on the budgets for renovation of the real estates, the rating of the

Evaluation of this step
In the interviews all four participants were positive concerning the ﬁrst workshop. However, it
is possible that the they would be more dissatisﬁed on how the others workshops evolved.xxxxx
After the third workshop a citizen, Ida Lindquist, from Lundtofte wrote a letter on how she
has perceived the public participation during LPP (the letter can be seen in appendix B12).
It is clear through the letter, that she was not satisﬁed with how the process has been. She
focuses on four aspects of the process; the wording of the introduction was "in a language
not ﬁtting with the occasion or the participants" (translated by author) which Sahin also
commented on in his interview, "it seemed as though many of the participants did not see the
context of their suggestion and the scenarios", the third aspect was about the discussion where
no one wanted changes for their own association but had no problem suggesting changes for
other associations, which created a hostile atmosphere. The last aspect was about the focus
group and the way the project group had chosen to invite the participants, which resulted in
diﬀerent participants attending the three workshops.
The feedback Lindquist has given can very well be the general view from the participants.
However, Thomas Henriksen did stand up after the last workshop and praised the process
even though he was not very satisﬁed with the ﬁnal scenarios. Other participants were also
positive on how the participants were involved, which indicated that at least not everyone
agrees with Lindquist.
During the interview, Hjorth talks about issues with public involvement. Some of this issues
are also represented in the feedback Lindquist gives. The content of the interview will be
discussed in the discussion.
During his interview, Sahin suggests to include a moderator in each group to guide the
discussion, which also could help clarify what the workshop is about and solve some of the
misunderstandings Lindquist refers to. The need of a moderator can, however, vary depending
on the type of focus group and their earlier experiences with workshops.
The four participants’ view on sharing are very diﬀerent from one another. While Hjort and
Starck-Sabroe are very pro sharing, Henriksen and Sahin, which are from associations, do
either not see sharing as a possibility for their own association or can see diﬃculties with
sharing due to diﬀerences between the associations. The diﬀerent views on sharing conﬁrms
what Lindquist writes in her letter.
Interviews are very time-consuming in qualitative research, but can give a detailed insight
in issues relevant for the case. It is diﬃcult to say whether their opinions were the general
view in the associations and between the participants at the workshop, since only four were
interviewed. However, they did show some diﬀerent views which were interesting and useful,
and furthermore, they gave an insight into the kinds of activities that are present at the
facilities in Lundtofte.
If the municipality is interested in getting more detailed information about the activities and
if they wish to make a tailor-made solution, then interview can be a tool to include in the
process. However, it is important to have in mind, that it can be very time consuming.

6.3
Focus group
In the new strategy for public estate in Lyngby-Taarbæk Municipality there is a section on
public participation and a wish to involve the public regarding projects like this one. Thus,
the project group knew from the beginning that they needed to involve the citizens and users
of the public real estate in Lundtofte.
The project group decided to involve the citizens and users through workshops. The original
plan was to arrange two workshops for a focus group, but due to time pressure, the project
group cut it down to only one workshop. Right before the workshop, however, the deadline
for the project was extended and thus LPP ended up being able to arrange a total of three
workshops with the focus group. The workshops each had a diﬀerent purpose besides involving
the citizens and users, and for that reason they will be described in diﬀerent chapters. This
section focuses on the ﬁrst workshop, and the second will be described in chapter 7, the third
will in chapter 9.
In the beginning, the project group was not very clear on what they wished the outcome of
the workshop to be. During the preparation of the workshop it was decided to be an idea
generation workshop, where the participants could come up with ideas to how the estate could
be used more eﬃcient. Apart from the idea, however, the group did not arrange anything in
particular. The workshop was given the name "Life in Lundtofte". The project group chose
purposely not to give the workshop a name including space management or shared space,
They feared the public might see space management as something negative as it is often
mentioned in connection with savings, which was resulted in poorer service.
Recruitment of group
The decision of who to invite and select for focus group was made by the project group. The
project group wished for the focus group to include representatives from the following.
- Associations using the public estate in Lundtofte
- Headmaster of Lundtofte school
- Politicians from the municipality
- Child and youth workers in the nearby after-school centre
- Citizens living in the nearby area
The invitations were sent out by e-mail (see appendix B1). The municipality and project
group did not have the e-mail accounts on all the members attached to the associations. The
invitations were therefore send to the chairman for each of the associations. Each chairman
could then forward the invitation to the members of the association. The list of the invited
associations can be found in appendix B2.
The invitation included a very short introduction to LPP and then invited the focus group
members to participate in a walk around Lundtofte to see the public real estate followed by a
brainstorm of what could be done with the estates in order to meet the aims for the project.

Discussion
Based on the case study the discussion will focus on three issues. First will the social research
methods be discussed and inﬂuence on the process. Next will the conﬂict of interest between
the municipality and the users be discussed and last will the level of service be discussed in
relation to a general or tailor-made service.
11.1
Service design and LPP
Service design include diﬀerent social research methods. Social research methods such as
interviews and focus group can be problematic to use because the municipality is able to
chose the questions and the participants. Thus because the municipality is not a neutral
player in this process, it will inﬂuence the outcome of interviews workshops through the
selection of participants and questions. The municipality can choose not to invite one or
several group of users and represent the users or the citizens in the municipality. Which
can goes against sustainable development if the municipality chooses to exclude group of the
community.
The municipality determined the content of the three workshop by that what outcome the
workshop should produce. If the social research instead is outsourced to an external neutral
part, the result can end up with not being suﬃcient because of the lack of knowledge in the
municipality and service.
Countings for one of the houses were misguiding, because of the municipality’s knowledge of
the users, the countings were adjusted, a neutral part, would not have had that knowledge.
Further can the amount of users rely on the season or events where the amount of users vary
signiﬁcant from other periods of the year. In LPP they were aware of the signiﬁcant increase
in the amount of users in S. Willumsens Vej 12 during the Ramadan and football, there still
were playing outside during the countings and thereby not using the indoor facilities.
As both Faga (Faga, 2006) and Bryman (Bryman, 2008) points out is public participation
not strait forward and the municipality has to be aware of its inﬂuence on the process to be
able to choose the right approach for targeting the research methods.

11.2
Level of service
In a municipality which value the social community life high, it is possible the municipalities
prioritize the tailor-made service before the general. However, it can be diﬃcult to use shared
space in a full tailor-made service, because the facilities has to be ﬂexible and accessible for
diﬀerent users.
Though a tailor-made service do not necessary have to block for other activities or users.
Lundtofte Community Center had a room, where the only activity in that room where sewing,
however it was only utilised 15 % (see Appendix C). With lockers to the equipment, the room
had potential of being used by others and it is possible the current users, would prefer to have
lockers which would make this scenario tailor-made toward the current users and could still
introduced shared space.
The decision of which level the service shall be at has to reﬂect in the research method and
how they are approached. During the research the municipality has to specify for the users,
which level the service shall be at to avoid misunderstandings and later conﬂict or resistance
from the users.
11.3
Conﬂict of interest
LPP has a diﬀerent focus than service design through the process.
LPP focused on the
municipality’s interests in relation to introduce shared space and reduce the public real estate
in Lundtofte, while service design focuses on the users’ need and experience with the service.
There then can arise a conﬂict of interest when LPP meets the users for the ﬁrst time at the
ﬁrst workshop. A conﬂict service design normally will not meet in the focus groups or during
the test of the service, because the scenarios intention of improve the user’s experiences with
the service. Opposite is there a extensive possibility the municipality will receive critic and
resistance towards the scenarios even though they have had one or two previous workshops.
The municipality oﬀers facilities for the associations because legislations say it has to. The
associations are dependent on public real estate, because not all the associations have the
possibility to go elsewhere to ﬁnd free facilities, similar facilities or have the money to rent
facilities, while the municipality is not dependent on the users. However, politicians of the
municipalities are dependent on the citizens satisfactory of how the municipality is running,
due to the politicians representation of the citizens. Though the group which are eﬀected
by the process or have an interest in it is a minority of the municipality and do therefore
not have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on this politic. The municipalities can therefore have very
diﬀerent strategies for handling the public real estate, when it comes to oﬀering facilities for
associations, depending on how important the associations are for the community and the
economic latitude in the municipality.
Using shared space as a space management tool can create uncertainty and resistance because
of changes in the physical frame but with as Weber describes can an open and early training
and communication may help increase the understanding the change more quickly accept the
change. Thus a continuing communication between the users and the municipality and public
participation is highly important for getting a successful process.

Chapter 12
Conclusion
The aim for this study has been to ﬁnd a method for municipalities to use to address the
challenge with public real estate, which is not used in an eﬃcient manner.
Shared space is a tool within space management, which can be used to optimize the use of
space. The aim is to ﬁnd a method where shared space is used to optimize the use of public
real estate. Service design has in this study been used as a frame for the method.
Lundtofte Pilot Project(LPP) was studied and compared with service design. The two meth-
ods have element in common, however, in several areas the two methods have diﬀerent ap-
proaches. Both the two methods followed the four phases:
1. Observe and listen
2. Scenarios
3. Testing the new service
4. Design Brieﬁng
The following seven focus areas are based on the literature study and case study of Lundtofte
Pilot Project. The seven focus areas will give a successful process for a municipality, which
wish to use shares space in public real estate.
12.1
Strategy for public real estate in the municipality
If the municipality has a strategy for public real estate it is important to be aware of its
contents, not to end up with a scenario, which goes against the strategy or not fulﬁl it. In
such case the design brief can be declined by the politicians and the process has to start all
over.

12.3
What to share and how to share
This is not necessarily something the municipality has to know before hand, but something

12.4
The level of service
Shall the municipality oﬀer a general or tailor-made service. Depending on how tailor-made
they want the service to be it can set some limitations for using shared space. The level of
service shall inﬂuence which methods are used for citizen involvement.
12.5
How shall the citizens be involved
Focus groups, interviews or other social research tools described used in service design. The
chose of research tools shall reﬂect on how general or tailor-made shall be and the motivations
for using shared space. In scenarios with tailor-made solutions it is necessary with more insight
in the users experience with the service and there will be an extensive need for interviews or
other dialogue based tools with users.
12.6
Keep the public involved and informed through the project
For the municipality to legitimise the process it is necessary to keep the public involved during
the entire process and inform them about the solutions.
12.7
Observe and listen, scenarios, test and brieﬁng
Use the four phases as a framework for the process. As with Lundtofte Pilot Project it can
be necessary to go back a step or two after testing the service.
Besides the seven focus areas it is important to follow the further progress with the public
real estate and the use of it after the concept of shared space had been introduced.
