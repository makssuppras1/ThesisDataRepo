Abstract
An increase in patient ﬂow has resulted in constant pressure on hospitals and especially
emergency departments (ED), which operate 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. Many ED
experience overcrowding due to increasing patient ﬂow. In order not to give in to pressure,
hospital managers are constantly searching for new ways to improve the processes and
to be able to create more eﬃcient utilisation of their resources. The aim is to secure a
balance between the incoming patient ﬂow and the system that treats the patients, by
reducing the patients’ length of stay (LOS) in correlation with the right composition of
staﬀ. Therefore, managers need tools which can help to optimise the system and which
can also handle the complexity of an ever-changing patient ﬂow, and still be time and
cost-eﬃcient. In order to accommodate the outlined need, a framework of simulation
can be applied. This framework oﬀers the possibility of studying "what-if" scenarios
by examine diﬀerent situations and thereby change the allocation of human resources
in order to reduce patients’ LOS. To investigate this a discrete event simulation (DES)
model has been compiled based on a real-life performance study of Hvidovre Hospital’s
emergency department, section 536 (HHED).
To conduct the study, a modiﬁed version of DEGREE problem-solving methodology cf.
Rossetti has been applied in order to ensure continuity and consistency in the preparation
of the model.
Thereby, essential elements are not overlooked.
The model has been
prepared in collaboration with a project team consisting of key stakeholders.
Based on the analysis, the results show that the treatment nurses and the staﬀallocated
to fast-track have too high a degree of utilisation. Therefore, the emphasis was put on
improving these resources with a special focus on treatment nurses, as this adjustment
will not result in any changes in the current ﬂow or layout.
The eﬀect of adjusting
treatment nurses in the general treatment ﬂow will ensure an improvement in LOS as
well as the utilisation rate for treatment nurses. However, if the fast-track’s opening
hours are extended, the most eﬃcient and eﬀective results are found if this is combined
with adding one extra fast-track doctor to the fast-track. By implementing the suggested
changes, the service goal will still be maintained.
The output of the thesis has given the project team a supporting tool for decision-making
in terms of strong arguments for the future if the current set-up is changed, as the model
provides useful knowledge about the eﬀects and consequences of adjusting the allocation
of human resources in order to create balance between staﬀand the incoming patient
ﬂow.

Introduction
The ﬁrst hospitals occurred in Denmark in the middle of the 17th century, which at
that time only consisted of a few rooms (Lægeforeningen 2016). Since then the numbers
of hospitals have increased along with the size and complexity to accommodate all the
diﬀerent processes the hospitals have to provide 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. The
healthcare systems are under a steady pressure, where the staﬀis struggling to man-
age the increasing old age dependency ratio (Cristea et al. 2020). The implication of
this is that there are more people visiting the hospitals due to the fact that people live
longer while the state funding is increasing along with the increasing demand. As an
outcome, hospital managers are on an ongoing endeavour to investigate the eﬃciency of
the existing healthcare systems and search for new ways to improve it. However, in a
complex healthcare system with many constraints in terms of resources, time and tight
budgets combined with the patients’ need for treatment at the highest service level, it
is important to understand the operations that takes place within a healthcare system.
Therefore, it gives rise for the use of operations management (OM) to plan, organise
and control the context of among other things providing treatment of patients (William
J. Stevenson 2012). A branch originate from OM, is healthcare operations management
concept which concerns making the best decisions for making the operations most eﬃ-
cient regarding using as few resources as needed combined with eﬀectiveness concerning
meeting the patients’ call. The healthcare service concept has changed from focusing
on optimising only resources utilisation to endeavour a more holistic view, by creating
balance between treatments for patients and improving eﬃciency for hospital resources
(Abo-Hamad & Arisha 2013).
In 2016 a new era called Healthcare 4.0 inferred from the context of Industry 4.0, where
automation and storage of data are facilitated by digital solutions such as cloud comput-
ing, Internet of Things and Big Data (Tanwar et al. 2020). The focus of Healthcare 4.0
revolves around making the healthcare system more predictable and personal for each
patient by generating real time information in order to improve the planning of care
trajectory. The development of digitalising record management in order to comply with
the regulations (Healthitoutcomes 2020, Siemens Healthcare 2019) have led to Big Data,
that can be applied for problem-solving or decision making by the use of an analytical
tool such as a simulation. The simulation model can be used to determine the current
and future behaviour of the system, in order to improve eﬃciency, cost-eﬀectiveness and
provide resource scheduling (Zeltyn et al. 2011).
Discrete-Event Simulation (DES) has proven that it can be used as an eﬀective tool to im-
prove and process modelling (Abo-Hamad & Arisha 2013). DES can among other things
be used by healthcare managers to evaluate performance or predict the consequences of
1
==PAGE:14==
operational changes (Abo-Hamad & Arisha 2013).
One of the most complex systems within a hospital is the emergency departments (ED)
where DES is very useful for tackling problems, with high capital intensive production
processes but limited resources such as rooms, beds, nurses, doctors etc. to determine the
best solutions in what-if scenarios (Abo-Hamad & Arisha 2013). In relation to staﬃng
the challenge is to cover all competence levels on each shift to accommodate the large
variation of emergencies. The stochastic nature of ED’s resources has made the system
more complex and volatile (Ahsan, Alam, Morel & Karim 2019). Reasoned the complexity
of the constraints most ED’s are experiencing having problems with too many patients,
lack of resources and patients are met with long waiting times before receiving treatment
(Moskop et al. 2009, Hamza et al. 2020). The overcrowding does not only aﬀect the ED’s
performance but likewise the patients’ satisfaction and the quality of care given (Ahsan,
Karim, FitzGerald, Morel & Burke 2019). In the attempt to minimise the overcrowding,
hospitals around the world have applied a Four-Hour Rule to limit the length of stay
(LOS) Ahsan, Karim, FitzGerald, Morel & Burke (2019), while others have focus on
immediately attended to patients with severely injured cf. triage (Lindberg et al. 2011).
According to Bhattacharjee & Ray (2014), the patients’ ﬂow through a care giving process
can be described in two ways; ’clinical’, which focuses on the progress in the patients’
health status, or ’operational’, which focuses on the patients’ ﬂow through locations. The
operational ﬂow can be improved in two ways. The ﬁrst one is to reduce the waiting time
(WT) between processes and the second one is to reduce the time wasted in every phase
by eﬃcient improvement of capacity planning (Bhattacharjee & Ray 2014).
This thesis will study the inescapable complexities on a case study at Hvidovre Hospital’s
emergency department (HHED), section 536. The aim is to address the challenges in the
decision making, by focusing on the operational level, regarding processes that the patient
ﬂow go through. In order to embrace the problems within HHED by looking into the
possibilities of resource planning according to staﬀfor the purpose of reﬁning the patient
ﬂow.
1.1
Aim and Objectives
The focus of this thesis will be on how staﬃng level can assist in preventing long waiting
times and overcrowding in an ED and thereby improving the patient ﬂow by minimise
the LOS. The aim is to study the patient ﬂow in HHED in a DES model, in the light
of diﬀerent scenarios with resource adjustments which will be assessed to see how this
will inﬂuence the patient ﬂow. To accommodate the thesis’ aim the following research
question is going to be examined:
Can a discrete event simulations model be used to analyse the patient ﬂow within
Hvidovre Hospital’s emergency department (HHED) in order to reduce patient length of
stay (LOS) by modifying and redistributing staﬀresources?
2
==PAGE:15==
This research question raises a number of sub-questions:
• Which activities are needed to analyse the balance between the staﬀresource allo-
cation and the patient ﬂow?
• How can a simulation model be designed to include details about staﬀresources
and patients?
• Which "what-if" scenarios are needed to analyse possible outcomes for HHED?
• How can the LOS, be improved when focusing on staﬀresource allocation?
To answer the objectives, a DES model will be set up, considering staﬃng, processing,
and real-life patient ﬂow based on historical data from "Sundhedsplatformen" referred
to as SP, registered by staﬀfrom Hvidovre ED and estimated processes-time by people
close to the processes. The data will be processed, analysed and then used to compute
the patients’ arrival rate and afterwards used for a comparison of the simulation models
output data, to verify the model. Furthermore, the project will reﬂect a set of project
speciﬁc learning-objectives, which can be seen in Appendix A.1. The project speciﬁc
learning-objectives are an add on to the general learning objectives for a master thesis at
DTU.
1.2
Scope and Limitations
The scope of the project is to focus on the processes within the ED, to develop a simplistic
simulation model as a supporting tool for the ED management, that aim to improve
patient ﬂow.
Following, the limitations for this project are stated.
• The thesis is in collaboration with a project team from Hvidovre Hospital consisting
of a Program Manager from "Healthy planning", a data specialist consultant from
HHED Finance and Planning department, HHED executive consultant, head nurse
and head medical secretary. The scope of the thesis is limited to focusing on a
performance study of HHED, with the aim of examining the current challenges and
exploring new opportunities by changing the staﬃng levels. The desired outcome
of the performance study is to create a supporting framework for decision making
that the project team are able to use for the purpose of improving the patient-ﬂow.
• Due to the framework of the thesis, it will only deal with the phases from problem
deﬁnition, phase I to experimental study, phase IV from the general simulation
methodology (Rossetti 2016).
• The project will not deal with cost factors which have an eﬀect on the strategic
level’s decision making. This is due to variability in the cost, which create a high
level of complexity to model the ﬁnancial elements.
• The project will not focus on restriction regarding the roster in proportion to the
healthcare area.
• The simulation model is limited to only focus on DES, due to the constraints re-
garding level of detail with limited resources and irregular arrival of patients, (Jun
et al. 1999).
3
==PAGE:16==
• LWBS (Patients who leave before being seen by a physician) are not considered in
this thesis, because there are solely looking at the resources, in order to improve
LOS. It is therefore not relevant for this study to look at patients who do not receive
treatment, but rather patients who come through the entire course.
• Limited knowledge on the healthcare system and processes.

2.1
Process Mapping of HHED
HHED performs many diﬀerent processes, which must be able to withstand a high patient
ﬂow with many variations, as the outcome can be many things, depending on the patient’s
injury. In addition, the service provided in the process must be performed at a high level.
This results in a wide range of demands on HHED’s resources. In the following section,
a few processes are selected and described in more depth, including triage and fast track,
since these processes deal with a high patient ﬂow of the total ﬂow in HHED. Additionally,
the patient groups handled in the HHED as well as service goals are described in the
following.
2.1.1
Triage
Each patient at HHED is assigned a clinical priority also referred to as triage category
which helps prioritising patients after physical condition by standardising the processes.
The beneﬁts of it, is a higher resources eﬃciency, reduction in WT for very sick patients
and improved treatment, (Lindberg et al. 2011). The beneﬁts are most needed by the
considerable demand for emergency care, due to ED overcrowding and limited resources,
(Abo-Hamad & Arisha 2013). The current used triage model at HHED shown in Figure
2.2 is an illustration of the ﬁve levels in composition with assessment parameters, where
the ﬁrst parameter outlines the priority of the patients’ injury. The second parameter
delineates if the patients’ injury is a life and mobility threatening from a scale from
very high to non. The next parameter stats the patient level from life-threatening to
unswayed. The last two parameters in Figure 2.2, outline the time the patient must wait
before receiving the treatment. The goal for HHED is to treat red patient immediately,
orange patients within 15 min, yellow patients within 60 min, green patients within 180
min and blue patients within 240 min corresponding to the arrival time.

Figure 2.2: Overview of the triage colour system (Hvidovre hospital 2020)
When the patients arrive at HHED they get the triage category by a treatment nurse,
or in the phone when contacting HHED before the arrival if it is a blue or green patient,
and is thus intended to follow a speciﬁc patient ﬂow.
Service goal
According to executive consultant Christian Rasmussen, HHED has established a number
of service goals based on the triage. The goals is based on time to treat, as illustrated in
Figure 2.2. The purpose of the service goals is to make visible the requirements placed
on the staﬀ’s service of the patient’s treatment, including the quality and the delivery
conditions under which they work. The goals are set and deﬁned by the management. It
is a wish on the part of the management that these goals are met by the staﬀand that
targeted work is done to meet them.
Patient-groups
According to executive consultant Christian Rasmussen, there exist three main patient
groups in the ED in proportion to the interface with the ED. The ﬁrst patient group is
deﬁned as a patient who only is treated in the ED. This could, for example, be a patient
who has cut himself and therefore only needs to be stitched up.
The treatment will
therefore take place only in the ED. The second patient group has sustained an injury
that requires further examination in the form of an X-ray. Thus, this patient group leaves
the ED to have the examination performed, after which they are sent back to the ED, to
receive the last part of the treatment before leaving the system to go home. The last and
third patient group are deﬁned as patients whose medical needs cannot be met in the ED.
Therefore, this patient group is received and supervised in the ED, after which a further
course of treatment is determined, which includes a transfer from the ED to another
ward in the house in preparation for the patient to be hospitalised. The third group
of patients could, for example, be an elderly person who has fallen and been seriously
injured. Therefore, the patient needs surgery, and does not return to the ED afterwards.
This thesis will mainly include the two ﬁrst patient groups in the simulation model, as
patient group one tells something about the total treatment time in ED, while patient
group two can give an indication of the transit time, to investigate whether it is the

treatment in ED or in X-ray that takes the longest time when looking at LOS for patient
group two. This move also releases a room as well as one or more resources for a while,
which thus provides the necessary resources for a new patient to receive a treatment.
Concerning patient group three, this will be included in the data, to ensure more variation
in the quantitative data.
The three patient groups are in the simulation model divided into two categories ac-
cording to their assigned triage colour and their further course of treatment. The triage
colours blue and green will fall under the category Damage treatment (S), while the triage
colours yellow, orange and red belong under Assessment (V). Patients in category (S) are
primarily treated in the fast-track, but if the fast-track is closed, they will receive the
treatment in the general treatment ﬂow. In contrast, patients in category (V) will receive
their treatment via general treatment, with the exception of triage colour red, which will
receive trauma treatment. Red patients are distributed either as (I) patients or as (V)
patients.
2.1.2
Fast-track
To ensure adherence with the service goals, executive consultant Christian Rasmussen,
launched the new initiatives regarding implementing a fast-track area at HHED in 2013.
Fast-track is a method that aims to reduce WT for patients with minor injuries and
illness, often patients with triage category green and blue (Devkaran et al. 2009, Haahr
& Stilbo 2020). The method is based on using the most competent resources, such as
medical/surgical specialised doctors who are responsible for the treatment of the patient
groups. The reason for only applying the method on green and blue patient groups, is
due to a high frequent occurrence in the system. The beneﬁcial eﬀects of using the fast-
track is a shorter value-adding time (VT), shorter WT, which improve the patient ﬂow
and aﬀect a higher patient satisfaction, (Haahr & Stilbo 2020). The fast-track is open
in the most busy time slots at HHED this applies from 15.00-23.00 on working days and
from 12.00-23.00 in weekends. In addition, to increased demand during these periods, the
second reason for keeping the fast-track open during the day is also the use of the most
skilled doctors (medical/surgical specialised doctors), who are of course also the most
expensive resources, and by limiting fast-track to daytime, the costs will naturally be
kept down (Haahr & Stilbo 2020). However, Christian Rasmussen, executive consultant
at HHED expresses a desire to extend the opening hours during daylight hours, as the
improvements in the implementation of the fast-track have had a good impact on the
overall patient ﬂow.
At HHED the fast-track room consist of a desktop where the secretary can keep journal
and two beds, so the performing of the process the staﬃng are able to treat two patients
at the same time. Besides the specialised doctor and a secretary, the fast-track team
consist of a doctor and a treatment nurse. The specialised doctor within the fast-track
are external consultants, however, they are considered as endogenous resources as they
are payed by HHED section 536.

Human Resources
Cf. Haahr & Stilbo (2020) bachelor thesis, the primary human resources within HHED

for the fast-track, as described in section 2.1.2, where the triage colours blue and green
are treated. Outside of the fast-track’s opening hours, the specialised doctor is involved in
other treatment processes that should happen. Then followed by the specialised doctor,
is a doctor with more than one year of experience coming. This type of doctor is involved
in the day-to-day running of treatment processes as well as a part of the SAT (See-And-
Treat) physicians, which is located outside the fast-track. The last kind of doctor is a
newly graduated doctor with a maximum of one year of experience.
Regarding the two kinds of nurses, the ﬁrst type is deﬁned as a basic nurse who perform
tasks such as a coordinative nurse, who have the responsibility to coordinate the nurses
on duty in relation to the number of patients received combined with allocation of rooms
for patients who are received by ambulance but is not categorised as a red patient.
However, the basic nurse are also allocated to the fast-track. The second type of nurses
are categorised as treatment nurses, they have a grand master degree that allows them
to complete the treating, if the conditions such as the extent of the injury and treatment
time are optimal. The treatment nurse are also able to be a part of a course of treatment
assigned together with a doctor and furthermore the treatment nurse are likewise able to
complete the treatment of patients. Treatment nurse is also included in the fast-track.
Additionally, the human resources of the ED is comprised of internal secretary and exter-
nal trauma doctors, which are borrowed resources from other departments in the hospital.
It is required that for a trauma patient who has been assigned the triage colour red, for
the hospital to acquire a trauma team, consisting of two trauma doctors. Besides the
trauma doctors, HHED put a team together consisting of two treatment nurses, one doc-
tor, and one secretary. In order to transport supine patients HHED utilise an orderly
who is employed in another department at Hvidovre Hospital. As the trauma doctors and
porters are exogenous resources that are not employed in section 536, they will therefore
not be deﬁned in the further course, as they are assumed to be free resources, which are
always available when they are needed. In addition to the above resources, the depart-
ment is managed on a daily basis by an executive consultant and a head nurse who are
managing all the administrative tasks.

2.3
Problems
To investigate the current situation at HHED in a structured way, a problem tree has been
applied to analyse it. The methodology is based on identifying the cause of the problems
by ﬁnding their main causal relationship. In Figure 2.5 it is highlighted that HHED is
currently experiencing problems with an unbalanced patient ﬂow due to a number of
causes. The problem tree is divided into two branches, which result in an unbalanced
patient ﬂow. One branch sheds light on HHED’s resources and the associated issues,
while the second branch of the problem tree includes the patient side and the issues that
fall under them.
Figure 2.5: Problem tree for HHED
Based on the ﬁgure, it has been decided that this thesis will only focus on resource
allocation on an hourly basis and LOS. The selected issues are selected in collaboration
with the project team. This is due to the opportunities within a simulation model for
investigating diﬀerent scenarios with allocation of resources and the supply for patients.
The LOS means something for HHED, but also for the patients’ experience. LOS is an
indirect overall measure of the eﬃciency of HHED stays. By keeping LOS down it will be
favourable in the form of reducing crowding and keeping an eﬃcient patient ﬂow (Sørup
et al. 2013). With a long LOS, patients might think it is a waste of time, as a result of
this it may aﬀect their overall satisfaction and experience of the course of treatment in
HHED. In order to reduce LOS, it is therefore important to allocate the resources correct
in proportion to the busy hours in HHED. Resource allocation is an important factor to
help balancing the patient ﬂow, although it can be diﬃcult to forecast as the patients
arrive irregularly. However, trends can often be demonstrated for which days of the week
that are the busiest, including which hours of the day based on historical data. As a
result it is possible to highlight which resource groups i.e. employees can be allocated in
a new and more proper way that helps balancing the patient ﬂow.

3.1
Simulation
When solving problems concerning healthcare operations management, a recommended
tool is simulation. This is because the tool can handle the challenges of decision-making
within a healthcare system. Simulation can be used for making a descriptive, predictive,
and prescriptive analytic, to present interconnected solutions for the managers at the
hospital, so they can make smarter decisions for better business outcomes (Dezire Pro-
jectPro 2019). The latter has proven to be an eﬀective method to improve patient ﬂow,
due to the way simulation illustrates a holistic picture of the system and thereby being
a supporting tool for decision-making for among others healthcare managers (Pegoraro
et al. 2020).
Compared to queuing models that are more suitable for problems with stationary arrival,
stable staﬃng level, etc, simulation can handle the dynamic behaviour with the com-
plexity of the healthcare system (policies of allocation nurses, doctors, beds for patients,
etc.). In addition to simulation, queuing models can be used to analyse part of the system
(Ahsan, Alam, Morel & Karim 2019).
The disadvantage with the simulation is that the output of the model is only as good as
the data and information that are given, so detailed data are needed (Mielczarek 2016).
Additionally, it is important to know, that the output of the analysis only gives a predic-
tion of the analysed case. However, simulation gives a big advantage with the capability
of modelling a whole system and its complex relationships between the resources within
the system, to analyse the behaviour in relation to diﬀerent scenarios in a dynamic way.
In addition, the aforementioned are less expensive than other analytical models for real-
world systems such as numerical solutions, where complex mathematics can describe a
real-world situation, and thereby it is possible to create experiments and see how the
system might behave in the given situation. Furthermore, this method involves a lot of
preliminary work to create a simulation model that contains all the variable and com-
plexity for a real-world problem. However, simulation has been used a lot within the
healthcare system, to predict future scenarios and improvements, but also to determine
bottlenecks for the current ﬂow, (Ahsan, Alam, Morel & Karim 2019).

3.1.1
Simulation Methodology
To ensure that the simulation model is developed correctly, it is necessary to follow
a methodology that ensures uniformity so that one does not overlook anything in the
preparation process that could negatively inﬂuence the model. In this thesis the modiﬁed
methodology version of the DEGREE problem-solving methodology is applied (Rossetti
2016). DEGREE is an acronym for:
• Deﬁne the problem
• Establish measures of performance for evaluation
• Generate alternative solutions
• Rank alternative solutions
• Evaluate and iterate during process
• Execute and evaluate the solution
By applying the modiﬁed DEGREE procedure it is possible to explicitly consider how
simulation will interfere with the overall problem-solving process (Rossetti 2016). The
reﬁned simulation methodology is divided into six phases and in Figure 3.3 below, all
phases are illustrated.
16
==PAGE:29==
Figure 3.3: Illustrate the general simulation methodology for using simulation for problem solv-
ing (Rossetti 2016)
The ﬁrst phase showed in the Figure 3.3, is the "Problem Formulation". The formulation
consist of ﬁve steps that altogether establish the foundation for the appreciation of the
problem and what are needed to be solved (Rossetti 2016). The ﬁve steps are:
• Problem deﬁnition, where it is evident what the problem is that is going to
be solved together with the speciﬁc objectives for the simulation model that are
achievable.
• Determination of the system, where the setup is speciﬁed to narrow the project
and to focus on the most important elements.
• Creating performance metrics, where the S.M.A.R.T-technique can be used
17
==PAGE:30==
which are workable to state achievable objectives. S.M.A.R.T stands for speciﬁc,
measurable, assignable, realistic and time-related (MacLeod 2012).
• Building a conceptual model, which will help in the process of building the
model within the simulation system together with giving a better understanding of
the complexity of the system.
• Documentation of the necessary assumptions, to narrow down the focus,
and only consider the most important processes that have an eﬀect on the problem,
which is going to be examined.
According to Rossetti (2016), the second phase consists of four steps (model transla-
tion, data input to the model, veriﬁcation and validation), with the focus on solving the
problem for the right reasons, where the measurements are in coherence with the stated
problem and the given objectives from the ﬁrst phase.
The third phase of the general simulation methodology revolves around the experimental
design and the analysis of the simulation results. This is where the relevant scenarios for
the research study are selected. After selecting the scenarios, the simulation is run and
output data is generated. The generated data is then analysed.
The fourth phase deals with consolidating that the right solution is found to the problem
by running the simulation a number of times, to see if the model and the conclusion
are suﬃcient. As seen is the Figure 3.3, this phase is iterative, and related back to the
third phase. The last two phases are ﬁve and six. These are about documenting and
implementing the model’s solution to the problem (Rossetti 2016). In this master thesis,
the last step is not examined, as explained in section 1.2.

3.4
Literature Review
In the following section, a review of previous studies regarding DES in the ED will be
carried out. The review centre around improving patient ﬂow by optimising the resources
utilisation within the deﬁned system, for the purpose of creating balance between the ED’s
resources and the patients.
A research carried out in 2005 in the United Kingdom, London by Komashie, Alexander
22
==PAGE:35==
& Mousavi (2005), concerning what-if scenarios to determine the cost-eﬀective means, if
one more resource in the shape of a bed, a nurse, or a doctor is used. The result showed
an improvement of 20 % in the patient ﬂow by adding either one more nurse or doctor.
The limitations were the lack of the resources’ diﬀerent competence level in the form of
experience combined with missing resources such as rooms, receptionist etc. (Komashie,
Alexander & Mousavi 2005).
Diﬀerently, in 2008, a research was carried out in Nashville regarding forecasting near-
future events in ED with DES. The objective of the research was to build a simulation
model which was supposed to simulate operating conditions in a near-future scenario
and compare the output of the model to reality, to check the reliability of a simulation
model according to beds and rooms, (Hoot et al. 2008). The limitations of the study, was
the narrow forecast, by only looking at a few patient ﬂows and institution-speciﬁc care
processes, including fast-track beds and designated psychiatric areas was excluded from
the simulation data. The conclusion of the study was that knowledge about the future
cannot stand alone as the solution towards the crowding problem, but actions based on
the knowledge is required (Hoot et al. 2008).
A study from 2013 concerning a case study about an ED within a teaching hospital in
north Dublin, Ireland. The research was aiming to link a balanced scorecard (BSC) with
the strategic-linked performance measurements to ensure continuous improvements (Abo-
Hamad & Arisha 2013). The improvement actions from the BSC were then evaluated by
a DES model. The objective of the study was to design a supporting decision-making
framework to improve the planning and eﬃciency of healthcare processes in order to
reduce the LOS. The outcome of the research showed a signiﬁcant improvement on LOS
with a decrease of 44% when focusing more on bed management by making sure that
there is beds enough for the patients who have been admitted and need a bed compared to
only increasing the ED staﬀor physical layout (Abo-Hamad & Arisha 2013). According
to Abo-Hamad & Arisha (2013) the limitation of the study was the cost factor was not
considered in the study. Furthermore, the resource utilisation rate was approximated due
to data being conﬁdential and not all staﬀresources was considered.
In 2014 a case study was constructed in collaboration with French hospital Saint Camille,
the DES model should give a better understanding of the behaviour in order to make
improvements in the ED operations performance, (Ghanes et al. 2015). The study only
took an asset in most relevant human staﬃng including doctors and nurses, in addition,
to improve the performance in coherence with the budget. Thereby other resources were
not considered.
The objective of the study was to only improve the LOS. From the
ﬁndings of the case study, it was proven that better human resource allocation had a
positive eﬀect on the performance. By increasing the current staﬃng budget with 10 %
it reduced the average LOS by 33 %, (Ghanes et al. 2015).
Another study carried out by Uriarte et al. (2015), was based on a Swedish ED, where the
goals were to improve the LOS and the WT for the patients, by adjusting the staﬃng level
including doctors and beds. The model was based on DES combined with simulation-
based multi-objective optimisation (SMO) technique.
The result of the study was a
reduction in 30 minutes for patients WT to meet a physician and 50 minutes reduction
in LOS. The limitation of the study was the nursing activity was ignored, (Uriarte et al.
2015).
23
==PAGE:36==
Another one carried out in 2015 was created in Iran, the objective of the study was to
minimise the total average of WT, subject to budget and resource capacity. According
to the resources, the study was considering receptionists, nurses, doctors, and beds. The
study was only focusing on the chest pain unit, and the appurtenant patient ﬂow, and
thereby excluding the rest of the ED. The results showed a reduction of 48 % of the
total average WT for a patient with meta-model-based optimisation, (Zeinali et al. 2015).
Memari et al. (2016) used DES in combination with queuing theory to improve the patient
ﬂow, by focusing on doctors and nurses while minimising associated costs. The result
of the study indicated that DES was more appropriate for a more complex ED system,
where the contrary queuing model is better for simple ED systems. The limitation of the
study was that no real data was used. The study was composed in the USA, (Memari
et al. 2016).
Pegoraro et al. (2020) used the framework based on the Decision-Making Trial and Eval-
uation Laboratory (DEMATEL) method with DES, to improve the eﬃcient management
by identifying improvement tasks based on the criteria deﬁned and thereby making more
eﬀective decisions. DEMATEL is a technique applied for solving complex problems by
segmenting them into groups and using multi-criteria decision making to determine the
order in the way of solving the problems (Li Chung-Wei 2009). The technique is good
with the cause and eﬀect relations among criteria that are existing in an ED (Pegoraro
et al. 2020). The case study was in collaboration with a Brazilian hospital, Sistema Único
de Saúde - SUS. The study proposed a framework, that was divided into three phases
(1. phase: Develop the DEMATEL, 2. phase: Data collection and building the DES
model, 3. phase: Simulation of the problems, to make the right decisions). According to
Pegoraro et al. (2020), the solution among other things helped the ED management to
make the right resource allocation to improve the productivity and reduce the WT and
LOS, and thereby increase the number of patients coming through the ED by applying
one more nurse in the triage. The limitation of the study was no real data were utilised
in the study and a main focus on triage and the onset of clinical treatment. (Pegoraro
et al. 2020).
From the aforementioned studies, it will be interesting to investigate how the Danish sys-
tem in 2019 will take place at one of Denmark’s largest hospitals emergency department,
HHED, by using DES to examine the balance between patient ﬂow and staﬃng.

Deﬁne Simulation Project
In this chapter, the ﬁrst phase of the general simulation methodology (Rossetti 2016)
is presented. The problem and system are deﬁned to give a clear understanding of the
problem. The performance metrics, which are tied to the objectives of the simulation
study, and the conceptual model are deﬁned. Finally, the model assumptions will be doc-
umented. This helps narrowing the focus to only consider the most important processes
that eﬀect the problem which will be examined.
4.1
Deﬁne the Problem
The motivation for performing this study is to ﬁnd a way to allocate the staﬃng in HHED
which will improve the service level, according to LOS. As requested by the project team
this performance study is going to provide an insight in the allocation of resources in
HHED. In addition, the model will provide a support decision-making framework to
the ED management, in which they can investigate diﬀerent "what-if" scenarios and
gain insight into the belonging results before implementing them. The objectives of this
simulation study are:
• Investigate how the resources can be allocated according to balance the patient
ﬂow and see how the system reacts through a simulation which is used to show the
outcome before implementing it to the actual system.
• Comparing the established scenarios regarding the average LOS in relation to op-
timising the patients’ LOS to be as short as possible.
• Comparing the utilisation of the staﬃng for the diﬀerent scenarios.

system. The system is deﬁned as Hvidovre Hospital’s emergency department, section 536
(HHED), as described in the Case study under section 2. In other words the system is
when the patient steps over the doorstep to the HHED, reverse the patient leaves the
system when he or she is not coming back to HHED again. The system is only based
on the layout as visualised in section 2.2.2, together with the adoption of the layout that
has been elaborated. In collaboration with the project team, it has been decided that the
model should only simulate the main ﬂow types performed in HHED. This is to simplify
the model. The ﬂows will be described in the Figure 4.2. The ﬂow is determined by

the entities that are deﬁned as the patients. The patient ﬂow is further divided into
the 5 triage colours. The resources that are put into the system, as described in section

• The average LOS the patients are in the system

• The average VT for processes patients are utilise in the system
• Utilisation rate of resources per hour of the day for the simulation period
• Average number of treated patients per day
• The Average time it takes before patients have arrived till they are triaged.
The selected performance metrics are approved by the project team and will be elab-

scribed in section 3.1.1. All performance metrics are speciﬁc and measurable, together
with the attainability using AnyLogic. The realistic view to the objective will be described
for each of the performance metrics together with the time-related aspect.
The LOS for patients is determined by the average amount of time they spend from they
enter HHED till they leave it again, either going home or being hospitalised. This is
relevant as it tells how fast patients are treated or decided to be hospitalised.
The average time patients wait for speciﬁc processes in the system tells how long it takes

The average VT patients are getting treated in speciﬁc processes in the system. This

the time slot. The analysis will be made in connection to study where the bottlenecks
are.
The average utilisation of resources per hour of the day in a week shows how much time
they are working with a patient versus not in use during the time slot. This is interesting
to see concerning optimising the number of resources, and possibly which kind of resources
are scarce.
The average number of treated patients in the system indicates how many patients who
have been treated in the ED. This is relevant to look at to see how many patients have
been treated in the system and also to see the demand during the simulation.
The average time patients are staying in the ED before they are triaged in the system.
This measurement tells how long it takes before a orange, red or yellow patient are getting
triaged. The analysis will be made in connection to study if HHED comply with their

4.5
Documenting Model Assumptions
After the preparation of the conceptual model of the system, the ﬁnal step in phase one
of the general simulation methodology is the explanation of the model assumptions. The
following assumptions for the simulation model have been made, to reduce the complexity
of the model:
• No distinction is made between the types of treatment.
• The patients and HHED staﬀmoves at a speed of 4 km/h. This has been determined
in collaboration with the project team.
• The time for a room to be cleaned is decided to have no inﬂuence on the patient.
• In the simulation model, the doctor with more than a year’s experience and the
newly qualiﬁed doctor have been merged into a single resource, doctor. This as-
sumption is made since both resources are part of the same processes.
• The SAT process is in the model assumed to be a part of the general course of
treatment.
• The specialised doctors within the fast-track are in the further work considered as
endogenous resources and will therefore be used as a resource in the model.
• The fast-track team always consists of a treatment nurse, a secretary, a doctor and
a specialised doctor.
• The orderlies are assumed to be available at all times as they do not belong to
HHED and will therefore occur in the model as a process time which symbolises
the travel time it takes to get to HHED.
• As outlined in section 2.2.2, HHED holds three oﬃces. In the simulation model
only two oﬃces will be evident, to state the reason for that decision two of the
three oﬃces are located in extension of each other.
• The simulation does not consider HHED’s executive consultant and head nurse as
they only sit with the administrative tasks and therefore do not have any interface
with the patients.
• It is assumed that there can only be one trauma patient in the deﬁned system at a
time.
• Regarding the duty roster for specialised doctors described in section 2.2.1, it ap-
pears that there are two specialised doctors present on the day shifts on weekdays.
The specialised doctors are allocated to the general treatment ﬂow.
Here, the
younger doctors have the opportunity to consult the specialised doctor during the
day shift, while the doctor performs administrative tasks. The specialised doctor
30
==PAGE:43==
allocated to the general treatment ﬂow in the day shift is not used in the further
course, as the person concerned does not have a direct impact on the treatment of
the patients.
• The plaster process will not be shown in the ﬂow, but has been included as part of
the process time for the treatment.
• Patients who have passed away during their stay in HHED are treated as patients
who have left HHED. Therefore, the prayer room and examination room 14 are not
included in the model.
The numbers for both speed and time are estimated from an average consideration. In
addition to the aforementioned model assumptions, there have been made assumptions
in section 2.2.2 regarding the utilisation of the rooms, in which all rooms can be used for
general examinations and treatments, which contributes with ﬂexibility in relation to the
room allocation.

Data Analysis
In this section, the processed data from section 5.2.2 have been analysed to extract quali-

combined with patient ﬂow information.
Arrival rate
To investigate the best ﬁt for the arrival data a calculation in Rstudio has been made
by applying the bestﬁt function. 3,565 bins that covers the number of patient arrivals
per hour distributed over the respective 22 weeks has been transferred to Rstudio. The
output of the bestﬁt showed the smallest mean square error (MSE)= 0,0199 by using the
Poisson distribution, see Appendix 14. The MSE is a good indicator but not a conclusion
point. However, since the incidence of arrival regarding patients is completely random,
Poisson distribution is used to calculate arrival rates (Salk & Toftegaard 2019).
To determine the lambda values for the arrival rate the total data set for patient arrivals
has been used. The lambda value is equal to the expected value of X, λ = E(X), where
E(X) is the average of the possible values weighted in terms of the probability that the
variable assumes that value (Wikipedia 2020a, 2019). For the ﬁtting, the ﬁrst thing done
is to compute a list of the arrival for every hour of the week so a histogram of the arrivals
could be built. Next a python function: curve-ﬁt from the package "Scipy optimize" is
applied to the histogram to minimise the squared error that corresponds to the distance
between every bar of the histogram and the theoretical value of the Poisson distribution
with the tested parameter. The calculated parameter is then the best ﬁt cf. the used
data.
The ﬁnished calculations are plotted in Figure 5.7 where the calculated arrival rate λ
are distributed over the 24 hours per day. The arrival rates, λ, are inserted as values in
AnyLogic, which generates patients into the model according to the day and the hour of
day. The lambda values contribute with stochasticity, that creates random variables as
an input to the model, to simulate the variation that takes place in the real world.

average calculation is made of the following KPI’s: LOS and VT. The calculated value is
then used to create a plot for each KPI. Each replication runs 30 days, to ensure that the
measurements of the system runs will not reﬂect the emptiness in the start-up period.
After raising the number of replications to 70, it was found that a steady-state across
replication was found at 50 replications. This can be seen from the practical analysis
illustrated in the steady-state plot of the VT in the general ﬂow in Figure 6.5.

Perspectives and Outlook
In this chapter a discussion of the simulation results presented in chapter 6 will be pre-
sented. The aim of the chapter is to clarify the intentions behind the selected methods
and input data used to transform the project. Furthermore, future work and reﬂections
will be propounded.
7.1
Choice of Method
According to Bhattacharjee & Ray (2014) a patient ﬂow of care giving process can be
described in two ways clinical and operational. The operational ﬂow which deals with the
processes and how it can be improved. In this thesis, the main objective was to develop
a method to improve the operational patient ﬂow by eﬃcient improvement of capacity
planning via the framework of a DES model. By using the same holistic approach as new
research use (cf. Pegoraro et al. (2020)), ), this project focuses on balancing the staﬀ
with the incoming patient ﬂow in a Danish hospital.
The ﬁndings of the simulation study have been well received by the project team, who have
gained new knowledge of the processes within HHED, by the imitation of the processes
over time.
The framework has initiated new ideas on how the existing setup can be
challenged by highlighting where and when in the ﬂow within HHED, there is a need
for adding resources. The simulation has contributed with evidence for the beneﬁts of
extending the fast-track opening hours. This change triggers the need for adding a doctor
to handle the extended opening hours. Thus, through the performance study, an attempt
has been made to minimise bottlenecks to ensure a better patient ﬂow by reallocating
the human resources within HHED.
Simulation is an analytic tool, used to demonstrate and evaluate the "what-if" scenarios
before and when they occur to see the course and eﬀect of future changes within a
real-life case under a predeﬁned system (Rossetti 2016). In the context of a predeﬁned
system, it is important to know all factors and their impact, as these factors inﬂuence
the behaviour of the system, these factors include controlled and uncertain inputs. This
understanding of the system is gained by long-term work, which is time consuming and
can be cost intensive. Due to the time limitations of this project, it has not been possible
to obtain this insight. Moreover, the system being simulated is stochastic, in the sense
that the behaviour of the system cannot be predicted. These obstacles contribute to some
uncertainties in the results, and therefore, the results appear as estimations which aim
to reﬂect reality (Rossetti 2016). Thus, the output should be considered as a benchmark
rather than a complete reﬂection of reality.
As described by several researchers, emergency departments are very complex and it is
70
==PAGE:83==
diﬃcult to build (Abo-Hamad & Arisha 2013, Ahmad et al. 2014, Hamza et al. 2020). The
simulation only shows a section of the real system, and in relation to this, some elements
have been neglected in the preparation of the models, including resources such as porters
and work processes such as plaster, blood samples, etc. A number of assumptions have
been deﬁned to simplify the model in order to cope with the complexity of the systems.
Therefore, blue and green patients are always pre-triaged before arriving to HHED as they
involve minor injuries, which can be identiﬁed at the initial contact with HHED. Another
parameter that should be considered before using the simulation method is that the tool
requires training in how to operate. Additionally, the expenses should be considered.
Since licenses for the simulation program can be costly, the ﬁnancial dimension should
be discussed before implementing the system (Dias et al. 2013).
According to Mielczarek (2016) simulation is a ﬂexible solution, as it provides possibilities
to modify and make changes in the program without having any consequences for the
real-life system. In addition, the tool is ideal for investigating risks that may occur when
implementing changes within the deﬁned system. Furthermore, the simulation tool can
be useful when looking at how the system behaves over a longer period of time and
it is possible to slow down the speed in order to study a speciﬁc process more closely
(Rossetti 2016).
This feature has been applied in this project to identify where the
bottlenecks appear within the system. Under the circumstances of the current setup, it
was found that the treatment nurses in the general ﬂow were under pressure due to a to
high hourly utilisation rate between 10.00-16.00 on weekdays. However, since the model
does not distinguish between the type of injuries, it does not distinguish between which
type of staﬀis needed for the treatment process, either. Thus, if the model comprised
the logic behind the choice of treatment resource, it could have turned out to be the
doctor who was the scarce resource in the general ﬂow. However, due to the limitation
of the applied simulation technique, DES, this logic Mielczarek (2016)states that ABS
is more suitable to simulate the interaction and relationships between agents to provide
a more precise understanding of the social interaction on a micro level.
In addition,
according to Abo-Hamad & Arisha (2013),the simulation technique DES is more limited,
whereas SD as well as ABS can analyse systems on a high meso to a high macro level,
by expounding the interconnected relationships between healthcare sections. This can
illustrate the interactions between HHED and the other departments, which may have
an impact on the processes in HHED. This allows, for instance, studies of trauma staﬀ
and interactions that occur in the chain of processes when a trauma patient is received
and of how this aﬀects the staﬀand the processes. The DES method focuses more on
the level of detail between micro- and meso level but cannot investigate maximum detail-
level as ABS. Taking these diﬀerences, beneﬁts and drawbacks into account, DES turned
out to be adequate for the purpose and the objective of this thesis. DES has provided
the techniques for building a ﬂow of processes with the staﬀ, to identify which overall
resources should be added, and to ensure the balance between the patient ﬂow and the
staﬀ(Mielczarek 2016, Rossetti 2016).
Input Data
The input data for the simulation model has been the focal point for the model, in order
to come as close to reality as possible, since the output of the model reﬂects the quality
of the input data (Rossetti 2016).

==PAGE:84==
As previously stated, a number of discrepancies were found in the historical data extracted
from SP in 2019. However, this does not apply to the arrival rates, as these are consistent
with previous studies conducted at HHED. Furthermore, it is consistent with a previous
study performed by Ghanes et al. (2015), as shown in Appendix E. However, uncertainties
occur in the distributions of the triage colours throughout the processes of the ﬂow.
This is due to a diﬀerence in the number of patient IDs from arrival to ADT-events.
Through dialogue with the Finance and Planning department, it was found that HHED
continuously has patients coming through the treatment ﬂows, which means that patients
arrived the day before the data extraction will not appear in arrival but in ADT-events.
Arrival 536 data only concern what time the arrival of the patient is registered, whereas
Arrival date-time from the ADT-event data set, indicates the time of the beginning and
the end of the treatment processes. This results in a diﬀerence between the two data
sets, which has the consequence of some patients being included only in one data set and
some in the other. This diﬀerence between the two dataset is equal to 7%. However,
alternatives to determine the distributions such as estimates from staﬀwere considered
to be even more uncertain. Therefore, it was decided to continue with the calculated
distributions from the SP data.
Regarding the process times, it was concluded that the process times in SP were not
reliable due to numerous delays in registrations. Therefore, we decided together with the
project team to use estimates for the process times instead of the process times from SP.
The methodology used for the estimation was a triangular distribution, where a minimum
value, mode, and maximum value have been estimated for the processes. A triangular
distribution has been applied due to fragile data use and because this distribution is ap-
propriate for estimations (K. Salk & Toftegaard 2019). To estimate the process times, the
Program Manager for Healthy Planning and the HHED ward nurse provided input, since
the both of them have useful knowledge of the time used in the diﬀerent processes, as they
have observed how the processes are performed. It would, however, have been preferable
if more estimations had been collected in order to establish a more valid database, and
to ensure that no overestimations were made. Therefore, it would have been proﬁtable to
consult the treatment nurses who possess micro level knowledge, given the fact that they
are more hands-on as compared to the doctors who are not part of the whole treatment
course. Due to the circumstances regarding COVID-19, this has unfortunately not been
possible. Based on the estimates received, most emphasis has been placed on the esti-
mates from the head nurse of HHED, as she is closer to the staﬀand the daily processes
performed and therefore has greater insight in the processes.
As a result of the unfortunate circumstances regarding COVID-19, it was not possible
for us to observe and experience the ﬂow and processes ﬁrst-hand and thereby add per-
sonal observations of the ﬂow at HHED. Due to our practical oriented approach, such
observations could indeed have raised the level of detail of the project. Personal obser-
vations would have provided a more coherent understanding of the patient ﬂow and of
how the individual processes are performed with the dynamics and interplay between
the resources that occur within each process. Furthermore, it would have provided op-
portunities to talk to the staﬀabout the ﬂow and the challenges they face on a daily
basis. Furthermore, if the time and resources had allowed, it would have been relevant
to conduct time studies of the duration on the processes, as well as on where the delay
in time registration occurs.

A brief tour of HHED guided by the executive consultant was undertaken. The tour
gave a sense of where the examination rooms were located, the size of the waiting rooms
etc. In order to ensure a correct understanding of the current ﬂow, an ongoing dialogue
with the project team in the form of video calls, regular meetings as well as written

It is important to involve stakeholders who are close to the operations and to do this at
early stages. This is to ensure proper alignment of the setup of the patient ﬂows with
the correct resource allocations. The stakeholders on a more operational level know the
internal logic behind the processes and the respective procedures for the individual pro-

queuing for fast-track treatment to receive treatment at the general treatment ﬂow, in
situations when the resources in the general treatment ﬂow do not have any patients to
treat. It was estimated by the project team that the addition of this would not change
the bigger picture.
7.2
Future Work and Reﬂections
The intention behind the framework was to provide the project team with a tool to illus-
trate and ﬂag the issues within HHED before the top management. A crucial and realistic
issue could be that there are periods of time during the week when the employees are
unable to keep up with the incoming patient ﬂow. In order to balance the ﬂow, addi-
tional treating staﬀis needed. This is to ensure that the LOS for patients is minimised,
and also that the utilisation rate of treating staﬀis decreased. It is desired to reduce
the utilisation rate, since, in some ﬂows of the model, it is observed that the staﬀhave
hourly average utilisation rates above the maximum of 80%. As a result, the staﬀdoes
not perform optimally, which may lead to stress and a poor working environment. The
model is a visually powerful tool that can show speciﬁcally where and when the system
is under maximum pressure. Similarly, the model can also be used to examine the best
possible solutions to ensure the optimal balance between patient ﬂow and resources in
order to reduce LOS and still comply with the service goal
The hospital management is an important stakeholder, for whom a simulation model
can be utilised as a tool to simulate future changes in the form of new trends in society
or modiﬁcation of the physical layout, which aﬀect the hospital as well as the patient
ﬂow. In this way, at a more strategic level, the model can provide information about
the eﬀect of "what-if" scenarios. Hvidovre Hospital is in the process of renovation, and
for this, simulation of work ﬂow can be helpful in illustrating what the optimal layout
would look like. The model can be used to determine which processes should be placed
where, for instance whether X-ray equipment should be placed inside or outside the
emergency department. Finally, the simulation can be extended to the entire hospital as
a tool to help the diﬀerent departments identify bottlenecks as well as the most optimal
composition of resource to satisfy the patient demand.
For future studies, it would be interesting to implement the proposed changes to see
73
==PAGE:86==
how the eﬀect of these inﬂuence the patient ﬂow in real-life. Moreover, since the cost
factor, due to its complexity, was not considered in this project, a cost-beneﬁt analysis
of the implementation would be relevant to carry out (Abo-Hamad & Arisha 2013). The
model could be further developed with more detail, in which, for instance, the porter
and the optimal number of treatment staﬀto be added to the fast-track if the opening
hours are extended, could be examined. The needed capacity of the fast-track room to
accommodate the patient demand could be determined, as it appeared from the analysis
that the treatment staﬀin the fast-track would become a scarce resource, if the opening
hours were extended from 10.00-23.00 on all days. In addition, it could be worthwhile to
investigate the eﬀect of X-rays being implemented in HHED, since analysis showed that
many patients were referred to this particular process as a result of a high ﬂow of blue
and green patients. Furthermore, it would be interesting to investigate if the addition of
more than one resource relative to the doctor or treatment nurse would reduce LOS in
the general treatment ﬂow, if the logic behind the choice of which resource is used the
most was built into the model.
For future work, it might also be of interest to make more use of concepts within Health-
care 4.0. By all accounts, it seems to be, however, only a matter of time before the
healthcare industry starts using the features provided by Healthcare 4.0. By embracing
and making more use of Healthcare 4.0 in the future, it will be possible to use the func-
tions within Healthcare 4.0 to improve the processes within the healthcare and increase
the service level (Siemens Healthcare 2019). Healthcare 4.0 provides the opportunity
to connect patients and the healthcare provider and thereby improve the processes and
workﬂows concerning healthcare because of a growing need due to populations ageing,
an increase in chronic diseases, and medical costs. One of the tools in Healthcare 4.0 is
the "Digital twins", which is a simulation model that creates a link between the real and
digital worlds by receiving real-life data quickly and process it directly into the model.
This allows the healthcare providers to optimise processes and workﬂows as it has been
done for some years in other industries such as manufacturing to detect outcomes and
eﬀectiveness of modiﬁcations. Another element of Healthcare 4.0 is the “Internet of Med-
ical Things” (IoMT) that revolves around mobile devices that sense and transmit medical
information on the consumer of the speciﬁc device (Siemens Healthcare 2019). This could
help the hospitals to use the stored data to forecast, make better decisions, and enter
better supply collaborations by using the data to be more transparent. However, this
new era also comes with an increased risk of cyberattacks, since the registered data in SP
contain highly sensitive personal information. Therefore, extra IT-security is required,
which must be supported by the supplier.

Conclusions
Due to the growing old-age dependency ratio, the need for health and medical treatments
is increasing correspondingly, which results in the overcrowding of emergency departments
where staﬀare under both physical and mental pressure. However, the staﬀresources are
not keeping up with this tendency. Therefore, there is an urgent need for improving the
patient ﬂow holistically by balancing the resources within the emergency department with
the incoming patient ﬂow. With the interference of Healthcare 4.0, where automatising
and storage of data are in focus, a wide range of possibilities and opportunities arise. A
framework that applies these features and obligate with the requirements listed above is
simulation. The framework allows stakeholders to investigate possible "what-if" solutions
to improve the setup. A DES model was built to analyse the patient ﬂow within Hvidovre
Hospital’s emergency department to reduce patients’ LOS by adjusting the number of
staﬃng. The project was carried out as a performance study. The model is based on
the theory of general simulation methodology cf. Rossetti (2016) with the required input
data. The input data consists of historical data from SP from 2019 regarding patient
arrival and corresponding treatment ﬂow. The duty roster and estimated process time
have been utilised in the development of the model.
The project was carried out in
close collaboration with the project team comprised of key stakeholders from Hvidovre
Hospital. In cooperation with the project team, realistic "what-if"-scenarios were deﬁned
to determine the outcome of the established changes in the simulation model. It was
decided to examine scenarios that incorporate an increase of the patient ﬂow of 10%,
extension of fast-track opening hours, and a combination of the two. All staﬀgroups
were examined regarding their utilisation rate, to investigate which staﬀmembers that
needed adjustments to be compliance with the obligation of not exceed the maximum
utilisation rate of 80%. Here it was found that the treatment nurses and fast-track doctor
were under pressure. Therefore, the scenarios were based on adjusting these resources.
The analysis prepared emphasised adjustments of treatment nurses, as this change would
not require any changes of the current layout of the ED. The result of the experimental
study showed an improvement on LOS was achieved with an additional treatment nurse
in the general ﬂow during weekdays from 10.00-16.00. This reduced the LOS by 2,48 min,
when the current patient ﬂow was increased by 10% and the utilisation rate of treatment
nurses decreased correspondingly. Likewise, it was stated that the staﬀallocated to fast-
track was under pressure and by adding an additional doctor to the fast-track combined
with an extension of fast-track opening hours the greatest improvement was achieved by
reducing the LOS with 22 min if the patient ﬂow was increased by 10%. However, to
improve this, expansion of the current physical layout of HHED is essential. Finally, the
study showed that it was recommended to perform X-ray scanning within the emergency
department. This performance study was well received by HHED management, where
some of the proposed changes already have been successfully implemented. Thereby, the
75
==PAGE:88==
framework of the model has contributed to supporting arguments about why the new
initiatives improve the patient ﬂow and reduce the LOS by balancing the staﬀwith the
incoming patient ﬂow. For future work it would be worth considering using more of the
concepts behind Healthcare 4.0, as it would help improving the processes and the service
of care given in the hospitals.
