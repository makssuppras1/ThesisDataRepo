Abstract
Explainability in machine learning is challenging due to the absence of formal frameworks and the
necessity for solutions tailored to specific cases. Insights into formal approaches to explainability can
be derived from causal discovery, where structural equation models map out variables. A particularly
intriguing issue is that of suppressor variables, which, although independent of the target variable, en-
hance classification performance by providing insights into data structures such as noise and covariance
[1]. These variables lead to untrustworthy explanations, which are not easily detected using current
explainability methods like sensitivity maps [2] or LIME [3].
In this project, we use EEG data as a case study for modeling and visualization to enhance explain-
ability. We investigate the potential of self-supervised learning for EEG data and introduce the efficient,
scalable, and domain-aware preprocessing pipeline called SPEED.
We employ an EEG transformer model from Kostas et al. [4] and benchmark a PyTorch reimple-
mentation of the model on four downstream EEG datasets. Contrary to the claims of Kostas et al. [4],
domain-aware preprocessing enhances pretraining outcomes.
Using preprocessed EEG data for a pretrained downstream model, we attain 86% accuracy for the
motor movement dataset of MMIDB (2 classes) [5], 90% accuracy for the artifact dataset of BC Bhutan
(5 classes), 75% accuracy for the P300 dataset of BCI@NER (2 classes) [6], and 61% accuracy for the
motor imagery dataset of BCICIV2a (4 classes) [7].
We train simple models to highlight issues in current explainability. Specifically, we train an Linear
Discriminant Analysis (LDA) and a Support Vector Machine (SVM) model on a synthetic dataset
containing so-called distractor signals, a type of suppressor variable. We demonstrate how common
explainability methods such as linear extraction filters, sensitivity maps, and LIME all mistakenly
attribute importance to the distractor variable. We provide analogous explanations for models trained
on EEG data.
Finally, to address these issues, we introduce the method of linear activation patterns (Pattern) by
Haufe et al. [8] and show how this data-driven approach does not attribute importance to distractor
signals in a synthetic dataset. We demonstrate that linear activation patterns can be generalized to
non-linear contexts by combining the methodology with local linearization techniques like LIME [3].
We term this method Pattern LIME. We illustrate the effectiveness of locally trustworthy explanations
and discuss the limitations of extending the use of Pattern LIME to high-dimensional data such as EEG
data.

with deep learning and its applications within neuroscience, particularly with a focus on the clinical
relevance. This interest has been there for our entire education but was sparked again with our in-
volvement in the EEG@Scale research project led by Prof. Lars Kai Hansen, where we explored the

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Do you think you can trust artificial intelligence (AI)? Many people are unsure about the implications of
the recent rise in AI. Led by the popularization of generative AI and large language models by OpenAI
in the fall of 2022, AI has emerged on a societal level as the next major technological advancement. The
immense hype around AI, with automation at its core, may sound promising, but how can we trust a
technology as new as AI?
The ideas behind modern machine learning and deep learning are not new; they have roots in
concepts like artificial neural networks and backpropagation that date back to the latter part of the
twentieth century. However, with the surge in big data and an exponential increase in computational
power, the capabilities of machines learning from data have grown with incredible speed.
As AI advances faster than regulatory frameworks can keep up, responsible development is more
critical than ever. In the Spring of 2024, the Artificial Intelligence Act was approved by the EU Council,
providing a common regulatory and legal framework for AI within the European Union.
The act
emphasizes the need for transparent, explainable, and trustworthy artificial intelligence.
If AI is a technology that alters the fundamental rules of modern society, understanding the under-
lying principles behind the decision-making processes of complex machine learning systems must be a
priority. People often hesitate to embrace technology if they don’t trust it, and a lack of understanding
and transparency may negatively impact how they view AI.
In this master’s thesis, we explore various exciting machine-learning methods and applications,
including domain-aware signal processing, self-supervised learning, explainability, and causality. We
hope this work can contribute to a better understanding of why AI models make decisions.
1.1
Learning without Labels
Fitting a model to a dataset is significantly easier when abundant labeled data is available. In many
cases, however, access to labels is limited, posing challenges in developing effective models and increasing
the risk of overfitting. This issue is particularly prevalent in fields like healthcare, where data like medical
time series and genomics may present challenges in obtaining and labeling samples because of high costs
or lack of trained personnel.
In such scenarios, leveraging unlabeled data becomes crucial. Self-supervised learning provides a solu-
tion by employing a two-stage training approach. Initially, a model is pretrained on unlabeled data.
Subsequently, the model applies the learned insights and structures to a smaller, labeled downstream
dataset during finetuning. A self-supervised approach is particularly advantageous in healthcare, which
frequently faces the challenge of vast amounts of unlabeled and limited labeled data. For instance, the
U.K.’s 100,000 Genomes Project, which sequenced 100,000 genomes from approximately 85,000 NHS
patients with rare diseases, including cancer, shows this scenario. By leveraging self-supervised learning,
the project can effectively analyze this unlabeled genomic data to improve diagnostics and treatments
[9].
Another instance is EEG data, which is relatively easy but challenging and time-consuming to label,
often requiring expertise from neurologists or EEG technicians. Part of this thesis has been carried out
under the research initiative EEG@Scale led by Prof. Lars Kai Hansen. EEG@Scale aims to explore
the possibilities of training a foundation model for EEG data based on the TUH EEG Corpus. Much

1.2
Why does AI need to be Trustworthy?
The need for trustworthy AI solutions comes from ensuring reliability, fairness, and accountability in
systems that increasingly impact human life. Understanding decision-making in intelligent systems is
an exciting and rapidly evolving field with frequent new research. Reliable interpretations are espe-
cially crucial in high-risk areas like healthcare, where decisions on diagnoses and treatments must be
accompanied by clear explanations to ensure patient safety and uphold ethical standards.
Regulatory frameworks for AI have become vital with advances in computing power and data avail-
ability, especially with generative AI and democratizing AI technologies. On December 9, 2023, the
Council and the European Parliament agreed on a revised Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act) to ensure
the safe, transparent, and traceable use of machine learning. The AI Act categorizes and regulates
AI applications by risk levels based on data format and intended use, emphasizing the need to bridge
theoretical research, legislation, and practical AI applications in industry and healthcare. The act was
approved by the EU Council in May 2024.
Public trust and acceptance are crucial for AI’s successful adoption across various sectors. Transparent
AI systems that clearly explain their decisions are more likely to gain user trust. Addressing issues like
bias and discrimination is key to trustworthy AI. AI systems trained on biased data can perpetuate unfair
outcomes. Techniques to detect and mitigate biases are essential for ensuring fair treatment. Explainable
AI helps identify and correct biased decision-making processes, promoting application fairness.
Moreover, understanding AI decisions helps improve and validate AI models. It allows developers
to refine algorithms, enhancing their robustness and reliability. Importantly, end-users like medical
professionals need to validate the models they use. Ensuring AI models operate as intended enhances
AI technologies’ overall quality and effectiveness.
1.3
How can we explain AI?
We make decisions all the time.
For the most part, we can account for our decisions and provide
reasonable explanations should the decisions be questioned. We are wired with a prior belief system
about our environment and the world that we live in, which makes it easier to reason about our decisions.
For example, why did you choose to stop at a red light? Because my model of the world tells me it is
best to wait for a green light if I want to reach my destination without getting hit by a car. The human
belief system and reasoning are causal by nature. We decide because of prior beliefs and experiences.
We have a causes for the effects of our decisions.
We naturally transfer this accountability or reasoning to machine learning models. After all, we
train these algorithms to make decisions for us - in some cases, they do it even better than us. These
decisions can considerably affect human lives in specific domains, such as health care and diagnostic

1.3.1
The Current State of XAI
Over the past few years, an entire catalog of Explainable AI (XAI) methods has been developed to
narrow the gap between the complexity and interpretability of machine learning models. Meta-analyses
provide an overview of XAI methods, concepts, taxonomies, applications, and challenges, focusing on
high-risk domains such as health care [10, 11, 12]. When discussing explainable AI, or simply XAI,
there are a few key concepts and distinctions to be aware of.
1. White vs.
black box models: Some machine learning models are inherently interpretable.
Linear models, decision trees, rule-based learners, and K-nearest neighbors are directly transparent
or decomposable. On the other hand, more complex models like support vector machines or deep
learning models are not directly interpretable.
Then, model simplifications, explainability in
feature relevance, or localization techniques are needed [10].
2. Global vs. instance-based XAI: Global explanations relate directly to the model. For example,
gradient-based methods usually provide global explanations, while sensitivity maps [2], LIME [3],
and SHAP [13] are instance-based. Instance-based explanations can be aggregated to provide
global-ish explanations. A funny example by Lapuschkin et al. [14] highlights the ”dangers” of
aggregating single explanations: Saliency of individual predictions of boat pictures points to the
wheelhouse, the sails, and the bow. However, an aggregated saliency map would indicate that to
detect a boat, look in the middle of the pictures. There is a close connection between XAI methods
being instance-based and model-agnostic.
3. Model-agnostic vs. model-dependent XAI: Model-agnostic methods such as SHAP [13] or
LIME [3] sample artificial points and infer the output using the decision boundary of some model.
Thus, the methods are quite reliant on the data as well as the model. Model-dependant methods
such as integrated gradient saliency [15] rely on the model architecture.
4. Feature Importance vs. concept-based XAI: Most XAI methods aim to attribute importance
or relevance to input feature. However, an aspect to consider is that input features, such as EEG
data, are often difficult to interpret. Instead, explanations could be defined using human-aligned
concepts, e.g., TCAV [16, 17].
Disclaimer: In the following introduction and the rest of the thesis, we use the terms interpretable
and explainable loosely and sometimes even interchangeably. Even though efforts such as by Doshivelez
et al. [11] attempt rigorous definitions of interpretability and explanations, finding consistency across
the literature is difficult (a substantial problem in itself [18]). We attempt to stay consistent in our
formulation.
1.4
Why does Causality matter?
Causality is the science of cause and effect. Causality is the attempt to map and structure directed
dependence when dealing with statistical variables. This happened because of that. In the context of
explainable artificial intelligence, causality may provide a more rigorous methodology for a science that
is not yet fully formalized. On a conceptual level, causality is a very useful way of thinking about XAI.
Why did the model predict this outcome? Because these features in the input data look like that.

Most current XAI methods do not work like this. They are not based on causality (or any other
rigorous methodology), and misconceptions about the output of explainability may be harmful. Across
the XAI literature, explanation methods express various properties of model behavior, feature impor-
tance, and structures in the data. When working on XAI, it quickly becomes evident: The type of
explanation needed depends on the context.
There are several reasons why current XAI methods can be misleading. For example, there can be a
significant difference between essential features to make predictions and the underlying process that
generates the data. Some information about the predictions may be relevant if you are debugging a
model but not for the underlying data-generating process of the dataset. A classic example of models
learning the ”wrong” thing is the case of the Clever Hans effect.
For example, instead of learning
the intrinsic features of horses in images, a watermark in the bottom corner could be used to make
predictions. Using explainability to debug which features the model uses to predict is very useful in
this case. Conversely, if we want to understand which features in data account for the actual horse, we
are interested in the underlying data-generating process.

predictions as
w⊤x = x1 −x2 = z + η −η = y
(1.6)
However, with standard explainability methods, we risk that X2 is highlighted as important in
predicting Y.
As an example, consider Figure 1.2(b) as an example. We look at cortical activity before and after
a stimulus. This stimulus could be asking a subject to close their left fist. A model might use the
baseline activity before the stimulus to detect increased cortical activity. The baseline activity is
not a cause of the task but may be useful in prediction. It is a suppressor variable. Misconceptions
about the importance of the baseline activity could lead to complications in critical situations,
such as during surgery, as discussed in Haufe et al. [8].

In practice, we often lack explicit knowledge about the causal structure underlying the data-generating
process, making it challenging to differentiate between types of explanations. For example, when analyz-
ing images of horses, dismissing irrelevant features like grass field is relatively straightforward. However,
interpreting brain data, such as from EEG recordings, is significantly more complex. The increased com-
plexity is mainly because EEG is an indirect imaging method, and we often deal with latent confounds.
EEG data does not present brain activity in easily understandable variables like Left motor cortex or
Occipital lobe. Instead, it captures brain activity in a data representation that is difficult for most
humans to interpret directly.
There is a need for (more) causal explainability methods to help distinguish different types of explana-
tions in machine learning models, particularly in domains like neuroscience, where the underlying causal
mechanisms are not readily apparent from the data alone. Traditional machine learning models excel at
identifying patterns and making predictions but often do so without regard to the causal relationships
that generate these patterns. This can lead to highly accurate predictive models that are not necessarily
explanatory in a causal sense, which is crucial for applications requiring deep understanding and precise
interventions, such as clinical decision-making.

Preprocessing for EEG Data
Historically, computational analysis of EEG has followed individual and case-specific approaches. Var-
ious factors like medical equipment, data formatting, and software tools have been in the way of a
unified approach to efficiently analyzing EEG data across different studies with varying settings and
objectives. This is a problem in machine learning, where such a generalization is exactly what we strive
for. As a part of this thesis, we propose a standardized and computationally efficient pipeline for EEG
model development. In particular, we focus on domain-aware preprocessing of EEG data and propose
the SPEED pipeline.
2.1
Artificial Intelligence for EEG
Electroencephalography (EEG) is a time series of medical data. Specifically, electrical impulses from
the brain are recorded at the head’s scalp. EEG data is recorded using several electrodes ranging from
2 (in a very sparse setting) to more than 200 electrodes (e.g., in a high-density cap used in hospitals).
Standard EEG caps comprise 19-64 channels. Electrical signals are usually sampled hundreds of times
a second, and routine EEG recordings last about half an hour.
EEG is a non-invasive method for capturing brain activity. However, EEG signals are incredibly
susceptible to noise. Surrounding electrical signals and biological artifacts from the subject may easily
obscure signals of interest. For this reason, careful preprocessing of EEG data seems necessary for a
machine-learning setting. In Figure 2.1, we see a general overview of the pipeline for developing EEG
models where preprocessing is highlighted with a red box.
Figure 2.1: A Pipeline for EEG model development. Box colors match Figure 3.1, which illustrates an overview
of the self-supervised model used in this project. The middle row illustrates the necessary steps for training and
evaluating an EEG model, the bottom row illustrates self-supervised learning for developing a general-purpose
EEG model, and the top ”row” illustrates transforming the preprocessed EEG data.

Generally, an AI-for-EEG pipeline should include (at least) the following steps:
1. Preparation of downstream EEG data, i.e., the data we want to model for classification. Often,
when it comes to EEG (and medical measurements in general), data formatting is not standardized.
With current formats for saving EEG data, storing labels is not an obvious task. In our pipeline,
raw EEG data is stored in the European Data Format1 (EDF) file format. Labels are stored as
annotations in the EDF file. If a downstream dataset is stored in a different format, it may need
to be reformatted to fit into SPEED, our preprocessing pipeline.
2. Preprocessing of EEG data. In this thesis, we propose SPEED, an efficient pipeline for pro-
cessing EEG data to obtain cleaner signals. A critical part of processing downstream data into a
format suitable for machine learning is storing patient-level information. We should avoid sessions
on the same subject when training and testing data when evaluating models. We propose storing
preprocessed EEG data and labels using the HDF5 binary data format. The data is readily loaded
for machine learning use with h5py2.
3. The core of the pipeline is model training. HDF5 files are loaded using the PyTorch dataloader
and can easily be split into training and test data. A machine learning model is fitted just on
the downstream data, or a model using a pretrained encoder is finetuned to the downstream data.
After training, the model is evaluated.
4. After validating the performance of the fine-tuned model, an end-to-end pipeline should include
explainability, i.e., either general model behavior or instance-based explanations. Later in this
work, we illustrate how to apply XAI to EEG models and assess if these methods are trustworthy.
We believe self-supervised learning is a reasonable approach to a general-purpose EEG model. In Figure
2.1, such an approach is included in the bottom row. For pretraining a transformer model, a massive
amount of high-quality, unlabeled EEG data is needed. This work uses the TUH EEG Corpus as a
pretraining dataset. We preprocess the TUH EEG Corpus with our proposed SPEED pipeline and
validate the transformer using a range of downstream datasets.
Lastly, the pipeline in Figure 2.1 includes the possibility of transforming the preprocessed EEG data,
i.e., using the Fourier transform. As discussed in Chapter 4, data transforms may serve performance
and explainability purposes. The pipeline does not include transforming data for pretraining, e.g., using
a vision transformer on spectrograms.
This chapter mainly focuses on the second step of the pipeline, the efficient preprocessing of EEG data.
We present existing tools, visit relevant theories within digital signal processing and mathematical
concepts, propose the SPEED framework, and illustrate the preprocessing results on relevant EEG
datasets. However, we start by presenting the datasets used in this thesis.

The MMIDB dataset
The EEG Motor Movement/Imagery Dataset (MMIDB) [5, 20] is a 64-channel EEG recording dataset
from 109 subjects that performed a series of motor and imagery tasks related to hand movement. In
short, the subject is presented with a visual cue as a target on either the left or right side of a screen.
The cue is processed in the visual cortex, and a plan for motor activity is carried out in the premotor
cortex (M2). The motor task is executed in the motor cortex (M1). The MMIDB dataset includes

In an ML setting, we wish to classify segments of the recordings when subjects open and close their

Data Formatting
When exporting the preprocessed files, we opt for the Hierarchical Data Format (HDF), specifically
HDF5. This format is designed and well-suited for storing and organizing large quantities of data. It
enables efficient data storage. The data is saved in chunks for each J × T data window, which is stored
sequentially on the disk. Additionally, a Fletcher32 checksum is calculated for the data to ensure its
integrity.
Alongside the data, the HDF5 format accommodates metadata, which includes the original file and
timeslot for each window.

2.3.3
Data Loader
Handling approximately 2TB of data distributed across 1000 files presents a significant challenge in
creating a data loader. The goal is to develop an efficient data loader that integrates seamlessly with
Torch’s DataLoader class. The primary challenge is to treat the distributed dataset as a unified entity,
enabling access to a specific data window by its index, regardless of the file it resides in. For example,
if block 1 contains 1000 windows and block 2 contains another 1000 windows, one should be able to
retrieve the window at index 1500, located in block 2.
Achieving this efficiently allows for dataset
shuffling, batch training, and more. Three key factors enable this:
• Chunking: Storing each window continuously in the HDF5 file leads to an approximately 100x
speedup when loading a single window.
• Prefix sum of lengths: Pre-calculating the cumulative sum of the lengths of each data file
enables quick determination of the correct file using the global index and then computing the
local index within that file.
• Binary search: Employing binary search to locate the specific file using the global index instead
of linear search significantly accelerates the process, especially given that there are over a million
data windows, each requiring a search.
The pseudocode for retrieving a data window is outlined in Algorithm 1. The data loader also incorpo-
rates programming-specific features, such as opening and closing the file each time to minimize memory
usage and error handling, which can be viewed in the code. Benchmarking the dataloader, we compare
linear vs. binary search and chunking vs. no chunking. The result can be seen in Table 2.2. The
speedup is significant, being about 50 times faster using chunking.

2.3.7
Quality Assessment
Quality assessment in the pipeline involves automatically determining whether a window should be kept
or discarded. This assessment occurs at various stages in the pipeline, and there are multiple reasons
for dropping a window. We choose to drop windows if there are insufficient channels to interpolate
the desired montage, either due to a lack of channels or because they were removed for being defective.
Specifically, at least half of the final channels must be present. Another reason for exclusion could
be the excessive dropping of Independent Component Analysis (ICA) components, leaving little of the
original signal intact. Lastly, the data may be poorly quality before or after processing.
Quality assessment begins with a straightforward bad channel detection using the PREP pipeline
methods (excluding RANSAC), followed by applying a notch filter to eliminate line noise and a com-
bination of a 1 Hz high-pass and a low-pass FIRWIN filter, usually at 70 or 100 Hz. Subsequently,
various quality measures are calculated, adopting the criteria recommended in the AutoMagic pipeline
[33]. These measures include:
• Ratio of Bad Channels (RBC)
• Ratio of Data with Overall High Amplitude (OHA)
• Ratio of Timepoints with High Variance (THV)
• Ratio of Channels with High Variance (CHV)
The specific hyperparameters of these criteria vary depending on where they are applied, i.e., more
noise is allowed at the beginning of the pipeline. A window is only kept if each criterion satisfies a

2.3.8
Bad Channel Detection
We identify ”bad channels” generally exhibiting a low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Removing these
channels is crucial because their presence adversely affects interpolation, average referencing, and inde-
pendent component analysis (ICA).
The foundation of bad channel detection is based on the Python implementation of the PREP
pipeline [29], a well-regarded pipeline that incorporates several advanced methods for identifying bad
channels. For further details, refer to the source. This pipeline initially detects flat/NAN channels
and removes low-frequency trends. Then, it identifies channels with extreme amplitudes (deviation
criterion), unusual high-frequency noise (noisiness criterion), a lack of correlation with other channels
(correlation criterion), and a lack of predictability by other channels (predictability criterion). This last
criterion involves calculating the correlation of each channel within a 5s window of EEG data to its
Random Sample Consensus (RANSAC) [37] reconstruction (using 50 samples from a subset of 25% of all
channels). The channel is deemed bad if the correlation falls below 0.85 in over 40% of instances. While
the RANSAC algorithm is notably resource-intensive, including it is optional. Bad channels identified
are then excluded from the dataset.
Originally, the PREP pipeline incorporated bad channel detection as part of its robust average
referencing. This process iteratively detects and interpolates bad channels to achieve a genuine average
reference. However, since interpolating every bad channel is not desirable, especially before conducting
ICA and due to computational demands, we opt not to use this approach.
2.3.9
Independent Component Analysis
Independent Component Analysis (ICA) stands out among various methods for isolating and eliminating
artifacts and noise from data. It represents the most computationally intensive step in the process
yet offers significant benefits, particularly through automation with ICLabel. Our choice falls on the
Extended Infomax ICA algorithm [38].
This method builds upon the original Infomax strategy developed by Bell and Sejnowski [39], effec-
tively managing mixed signal sources that exhibit both sub-gaussian and super-gaussian distributions.
It achieves this through a generalized learning rule that maximizes neg-entropy, allowing the algorithm
to adjust to different source distributions without needing predefined settings. Its main innovation is
the capability to alternate between sub- and super-gaussian conditions based on the input signals’ traits,
enabled by parameterized probability distributions.
Such adaptability is especially valuable when analyzing EEG data, given the wide range of statistical
characteristics of signal sources due to the complex nature of brain activities. The Extended Infomax
algorithm proficiently distinguishes between artifacts (like eye blinks and electrical interference) and
true brain signals, thereby enhancing the data quality for further analysis. It operates on a simple yet
flexible learning rule, refined with the natural gradient approach and bolstered by a stability analysis
to ensure dependable convergence.
2.3.10
ICLabel
The ICLabel project [40] is designed to automate the classification of electroencephalographic (EEG)
independent components (ICs), aiming to facilitate the analysis of EEG studies with many subjects and
enable the use of ICA decomposition automatically. This effort has produced the automated ICLabel
classifier, trained on over 6,000 labelled ICs, and is currently leading in accuracy. It assigns ICs to seven
==PAGE:29==
2.4 The SPEED Pipeline

categories: Brain, Muscle, Eye, Heart, Line Noise, Channel Noise, and Others. The ”Others” category is
a general catch-all for ICs with indeterminate noise or those containing signals that ICA decomposition
could not distinctly separate. In our methodology, we prefer to focus on the Brain and Other categories,
excluding the rest if the output probability for a label is above 0.8. This threshold and selecting labels
to exclude can be adjusted depending on the application’s specific requirements.
2.3.11
Data Splitting
The data is segmented into 60-second windows, a process facilitated by knowing the sampling frequency.
These segments, or data windows, are processed individually for several reasons. Firstly, most self-
supervised learning models require fixed-size inputs and 60 seconds is a commonly selected duration.
It has been demonstrated that these models yield better performance with this extended window size
[4]. The decision to split the data at the outset rather than at the end is due to several factors: 1)
many preprocessing methods do not scale well, making it impractical to process entire sessions for
numerous files and 2) bad channel detection might eliminate a channel for an entire session due to a
minor flawed portion of the recording 3) we can readily discard defective windows using quality metrics
without repercussions, whereas handling entire sessions would require greater caution.
Although it
could be argued that some methods, such as ICA, perform better with more data, we concluded that
the additional time required would not be justifiable for the pretraining dataset and would only be
feasible for smaller, downstream datasets.
2.4
The SPEED Pipeline
The SPEED pipeline is designed with self-supervised learning in mind. Particularly taking the challenges
posed by the TUH EEG corpus into account. The pipeline is implemented in Python using the MNE
library [26]. See a visual illustration of the SPEED pipeline in Figure 2.6. The pipeline can be described
in a range of steps and modules inspired by existing frameworks as well as the use case of self-supervised
learning:
1. The initial steps are simple yet essential. They include standardizing channel names, detecting
channel types, dropping non-EEG channels, setting a standard 10-20 montage, and dropping
channels not in a known montage.
2. The data is segmented into 60-second windows. We omit the final window if it is less than 60
seconds long. All subsequent procedures are carried out within these 60-second windows.
3. The quality of each data window is evaluated using a set of quality measures. We exclude any
window for which OHA < 0.8 or THV < 0.5 or CHV < 0.5 or BCR < 0.8.
These thresholds
were determined through manual reviews of over 100 EEG recordings. See details on the quality
measure in Section 2.3.7.
4. Line noise is removed using the Iterative Zapline Algorithm. The line noise frequency, usually
50Hz in Afro-Eurasia or 60Hz in America, is manually set. See Section 2.3.6 for details.
5. Bad Channel Detection is executed on a duplicate dataset. Identified bad channels are removed
from the original dataset. See Section 2.3.8 for details.
6. Robust detrending, a 0.5 Hz low-pass filter, and a 100 Hz high-pass filter are applied.
7. Average referencing is applied to the remaining high-quality channels.

2.4.3
Preprocessing of Downstream Datasets
When preprocessing the downstream datasets for the finetuning of the model and later classification,
we have to be more careful. Here, we know more about the desired signal, which we wish not to disrupt.
At the same time, annotated data is more scarce, and we cannot, in the same way, disregard bad EEG
windows, remove bad channels, and more. Luckily, downstream datasets are often higher quality, so it
will not be as problematic. At the same time, EEG data windows are often annotated a few seconds long,
which is too small to preprocess alone. However, as downstream datasets are at most a few gigabytes,
we can afford to preprocess an entire EEG session together before splitting it into labelled EEG data

2.6
Discussion
We benchmark and discuss whether SPEED has an effect in Chapter 3. Here, we address the limitations
of such a pipeline.
The ”industry standard” in machine learning is to allow complex deep learning models to process
large datasets with minimal preprocessing, especially in self-supervised learning. For electroencephalo-
gram (EEG) data, Kostas et al.
[4] pretrained a sophisticated transformer model on the extensive
Temple University Hospital (TUH) EEG Corpus with relatively basic preprocessing.
However, the
TUH EEG Corpus presents significant challenges due to variability in signal-to-noise ratio, equipment
used, recording lengths, and other factors. Preprocessing EEG data is computationally intensive and
often considered impractical on a large scale, particularly for pretraining, because the signal of interest
is not clearly defined.
Although not mentioned in this chapter, a significant portion of the work was dedicated to creating
an efficient and customizable implementation of SPEED. This allowed for multiprocessing and made it
feasible to preprocess the entire TUH EEG Corpus. Consequently, the innovative aspect of SPEED is
as much in its implementation as in its theoretical framework. It is intended to serve as a toolbox that
==PAGE:34==
24
2 Domain-Aware Preprocessing for EEG Data
enables quick and easy deep learning on EEG data, thereby fostering innovation. SPEED is designed
with AI in mind, which is why it is implemented in Python and comes with a dataloader for PyTorch.
2.7
Conclusion
This thesis introduces SPEED, an efficient preprocessing pipeline designed to handle variability across
one or more EEG datasets and merge them into a single preprocessed dataset suitable for self-supervised
learning applications. The Python-based pipeline provides a unified approach to processing EEG data.
Besides being a plug-and-play preprocessing pipeline, its efficient implementation allows for the prepro-
cessing of vast datasets. We also offer tools for reproducible preprocessing of the TUH EEG Corpus,
paving the way for the future development of pretrained EEG foundation models. See Chapter 3 for
the performance and discussion of SPEED.
To answer research question 1, we have proposed an end-to-end pipeline for self-supervised
machine learning for EEG data, including the domain aware-preprocessing pipeline of
SPEED.

In AI, terms like explainable and trustworthy generally evoke thoughts of demystifying the decision-
making processes of complex black-box models. Large deep-learning models, such as large language
(LLM), are not inherently explainable. Deep learning models have millions, if not billions, of parameters,
and there is no straightforward way to interpret individual parameters’ relevance. Model performance
often reaches expert levels, and as a result, understanding the decision-making process is essential.
A substantial part of this thesis work overlaps with the research initiative EEG@Scale to develop a
foundation model for EEG data, and (re)implementing and benchmarking a self-supervised EEG model
has been a focus.
To a large extent, the EEG@Scale initiate and much of the work done in this thesis is inspired by the

Particularly, we revisit choices related to the
pretraining of the transformer model. The project aims to intelligently utilize the massive amounts of

3.1.2
Self-supervised learning
Self-supervised learning (SSL) is a paradigm within machine learning where models learn from the
data without explicit human labeling. Unlike supervised learning, where models train on labeled exam-
ples, or unsupervised learning, which aims to find patterns in unlabeled data, self-supervised learning
leverages inherent structures or relationships within the data.
Self-supervised learning differs from
semi-supervised learning as no ”ground truth” is required. Self-supervised learning is often combined
with a supervised classifier that leverages the structures learned in the unlabeled data. Generally, a
self-supervised setting can be described as
1. Learning inherent structures in unlabeled data by pretraining an encoder.
2. Finetuning a supervised classifier using representations from the pretrained encoder.
The essence of SSL lies in using the data’s intrinsic information as supervision signals. These signals
can be defined using contrastive tasks to indicate how we expect the data to distribute in some latent
space.
3.1.3
Contrastive Learning
Contrastive learning is the primary driver in self-supervised learning and builds on a straightforward
assumption: Data points that are similar in input space should be similar in the embedding space.
This assumption is a compelling principle for learning structures in unlabelled data. The million-dollar
question is: How do you define similarity in EEG data? We have adopted the argument from Kostas
et al. [4] that ct, the context at position t should be most similar to bt, the encoded representation at
t, even though this input to the transformer is masked. This approach is called Contrastive Predictive
Coding (CPC) [45] and is used in Wav2vec [43] and adopted for EEG by Kostas et al. [4]. Specifically,
the self-supervised loss for a masked token at position t is defined as

3.2
A Self-Supervised EEG model
In this thesis, we examine a self-supervised transformer model for EEG data. As part of the EEG@Scale
initiative, we have re-implemented the model architecture from Kostas et al. [4]. Thus, there is not
much novelty in the model architecture, but our model is implemented in the framework of PyTorch
Lightning instead of DN3. For this reason, we do not spend much energy on the architectural details of
the models. However, for completeness, we introduce the key components of the self-supervised EEG
model.
In a self-supervised machine learning setting, there are two steps and two model parts, so to speak.
In this thesis, we refer to the transformer used for pretraining as the Pretrained EEG Model (PEEM)
and the downstream model used for fine-tuning as the Downstream EEG Model (DEEM). Find an
overview of the self-supervised learning setup in Figure 3.1.
3.2.1
Pretrained EEG Model
Using an approach inspired by Wav2Vec [43], the model architecture can be broken down into several
key components tailored for EEG data.
1. Feature Encoder: Preprocessed EEG data is inputted to a feature encoder consisting of con-
volutional layers that downsample the input and extract local features combined with positional
encoding to retain temporal information.
2. Transformer Module: The model’s core consists of multiple transformer layers. Each trans-
former layer has a multi-head self-attention mechanism that allows the model to capture dependen-
cies between different parts of the EEG signal. After the attention mechanism, each transformer
layer includes a feedforward neural network that processes the attention outputs to produce the
final layer representations.
The model is pretrained using masked segment prediction, resulting in a pretrained EEG model (PEEM).

3.3
Pretraining with SPEED
A significant claim by Kostas et al. [4] is that self-supervised EEG models can learn meaningful struc-
tures in the data regardless of the preprocessing level. Using contrastive learning, they demonstrate
that a transformer model pretrained on massive amounts of (basically) unprocessed EEG data performs
significantly better than without pretraining. Although this is an incredible advance for EEG modeling,
we believe domain-aware preprocessing of the data intended for pretraining can improve downstream
performance on task-specific EEG tasks.
3.3.1
Pretraining Experiments
We pretrain a self-supervised model on the entire TUH EEG Corpus. Moreover, we vary the levels of
EEG signal processing and illustrate the potential of domain-aware preprocessing in a self-supervised
learning setting. While the model architecture remains consistent with the framework established by
Kostas et al. [4], we experiment with different hyperparameters in the preprocessing pipeline. The pri-
mary objective is to evaluate whether pipeline modifications enhance downstream performance. Specif-
ically, we pretrain the transformer model on the TUH EEG Corpus preprocessed with three pipelines:
1. The SPEED pipeline: The pipeline includes bad window detection, channel detection, inter-
polation of missing channels, and standard preprocessing like line noise removal and band-pass
filters. See Section 2.4 for a detailed description.
2. The SPEED pipeline, including ICA: Artifact components like eye movement and muscle
activity are removed using ICLabel. See Section 2.3.10 for details.
3. The pipeline deployed by Kostas et al. [4] serving as the baseline pipeline.
4. No pretraining.

3.3.2
Additional Training Details
Pretraining is conducted using an ADAM optimiser initialised with a learning rate of 0.00002, weight
decay of 0.01, β1 = 0.9, and β2 = 0.98. We apply a 1cycle learning rate policy, as proposed by Smith
[1cycle], to adaptively adjust the learning rate during training phases. The training is executed with
a batch size of 64, optimising speed and stability. Model validation occurs every 50 batches, using the
same batch size across all validation datasets.
During training, we incorporate a technique called Random Temporal Cropping, which selectively
crops approximately 5% of the temporal dimension of the input data. This method aims to enhance the
model’s generalisation by varying the length and segment of the input sequence exposed during each
training epoch, reducing the risk of overfitting specific temporal patterns.
Training a model of a certain complexity on a massive dataset like the TUEG requires computational
resources. The dataset was copied to a local 3.5 TB NVMe SSD and trained on three NVIDIA RTX
A5000 GPUs with 24 GB VRAM. An Ampere GPU architecture allows for bfloat16 (brain floating
point), a 16-bit floating point format developed by Google Brain for use in AI [46].
It offers less
precision but a higher dynamic range, which is suitable for machine learning. We employed eight cores
for data loading using 2 x EPYC 9124 16c/32t 3.0 GHz CPUs to keep up with the GPUs.

3.5
Discussion
The results in Tables 3.3 and 3.4 are interesting in several ways. Namely, we benchmark a self-supervised
EEG model on four downstream datasets that compare positively to the results by Kostas et al. [4].
We demonstrate increased performance when using the domain-aware preprocessing pipeline of SPEED.
For all experiments, specific choices were made. The following section discusses some of these choices
and the findings related to the self-supervised model.
3.5.1
Artifact Removal
In the results, we find that the domain-aware preprocessing in the SPEED pipeline increases downstream
performance. The SPEED pipeline efficiently sorts out windows of poor technical quality and removes
channels with poor connectivity (much noise). We include experiments using Independent Component
Analysis (ICA) as an extra step to identify ”bad components”. We examine whether removing these
components for pretrain and downstream data increases the performance.
Looking at the results, we find the contrary: Results are consistently better without using ICA and
the ICLabel algorithm to remove components and artifacts. There may be a few different reasons for
this. First, we classify components using the ICLabel (see Section 2.3.10 for details). We have found
regular inconsistencies in the algorithm’s performance through manual inspection, which is natural,
given the diversity of EEG datasets. It is possible that inconsistent sorting of components negatively
influences downstream performance.
Another consideration is whether removing independent components makes sense for our purpose. We
==PAGE:45==
3.6 Conclusion
35
do not want to remove them for datasets where detecting biological artifacts is the goal. However, even
in cases where we want to classify cortical activity or sleep stages, does it not make sense to model
artifacts as well? It is possible to imagine having EEG data containing eye movement or other biological
artifacts leading to even better latent presentations. This analysis would be exciting to pursue in future
work.
3.5.2
Downstream Datasets
More meticulous preprocessing is often required for downstream datasets, which falls beyond this
project’s scope. These datasets are typically smaller and of higher quality, making them more suitable
for detailed analysis. We include a version of the SPEED pipeline intended for downstream datasets
but recommend individual inspection of the data for precise preprocessing.
However, SPEED still allows for standardized formatting of downstream datasets, allowing fine-
tuning and achieving near-state-of-the-art performance.
3.5.3
Hyperparameters
The specific choices made in designing SPEED were largely informed by previous literature and dis-
cussions with experts in the field. However, using it for pretraining without a clearly defined signal of
interest is unexplored territory. Therefore, more systematic and exhaustive experimentation with the
optimal choices would be ideal. We did investigate a few hyperparameters, but each time, it required
preprocessing several terabytes of data, pretraining on this data, and then fine-tuning several large
downstream datasets.
3.5.4
Future Work
The chapter’s objective was to investigate the effect of SPEED on self-supervised learning rather than
to develop a new model for EEG data. However, the transformer-based model documented in this
thesis is only the beginning. In the first version of the EEG@Scale toolbox, we hope to document more
experiments using these models (PEEM and DEEM). Specifically, we think a few key areas would be
interesting to explore further.
A natural next step is to explore using the full transformer model for classification. Kostas et al. call
this model Full BENDR. Then, as the ultimate aim is to train a general-purpose EEG model, we should
benchmark the model on as many downstream datasets as possible. Namely, we would like to include
subsets of the TUEG like TUAR (artifacts), TUAB (abnormalities), and TUSZ (seizures and epileptic
discharges), as well as sleep stage datasets like the Sleep-EDF Database Expanded v1.0.0 and emotion
data like the SEED dataset. We might also be able to draw on BrainCapture’s resources to record new
data.
Then, in explainability and representation learning, it would be very interesting to explore the inner
workings of the transformer model. First, much analysis could be made on the latent representations of
EEG data. Inspired by the work on EEG representation by Banville et al. [44] and the visualisations
by Savcisens et al. [47], we would love to investigate what underlying information the EEG model uses
to encode the data. Methods such as those presented by Pasad et al. [48] could also be fascinating to
explore for EEG data.

4.1
Machine Learning for EEG
Machine learning for EEG data is challenging compared to other data forms like text or images because
of the inherent difficulty in interpreting the signals. A neurologist or an EEG technician may find a
waveform representation useful in detecting spikes or other physiological patterns and abnormalities in
a clinical setting. From a signal processing point of view, spectrograms may provide useful information
about the magnitude of power in different frequency bands. Furthermore, as computational neuroscien-
tists, we may be interested in effective brain connectivity and only care about statistical relationships
between parts of the brain, introducing the need to formalize cortical regions as variables. To be more
specific, considering different representations of EEG for machine learning settings is useful for a few
reasons:
1. The question of interpretability. Who is the model’s end user, and how should model predictions be
presented and justified? Besides the end user, it is also vital that developers can properly evaluate
the performance of the models as well as the reliability hereof.
Human-in-the-loop pipelines,
including domain experts, can aid this process. Naturally, the entire field of XAI falls into the
interpretability argument, and we dive into that analysis and discussion in Chapter 5.
2. The question of model performance. Can various learning algorithms be fitted equally well using
different data representations? Machine learning for EEG (waveforms) has not been studied or
developed nearly as much as areas like text or images. Although work has been done on audio
and speech, e.g., Wav2vec [43], research on EEG signals is sparse.
With the rise of self-supervised learning, we see the effectiveness of training models on raw (or pre-
processed) EEG signals opening the doors for large-scale EEG modeling. However, the fact that EEG
signals are (only) indirect representations of neural activity begs the question: Are raw EEG signals
the best machine learning representation in performance and interoperability?

5.1
The State of Current XAI
One of the challenges in XAI is that there is no one solution to distinguish between important and
unimportant features. As demonstrated in Section 1.4, some features are essential for model prediction
but are non-causal in the underlying data-generating process.
Most explainability methods assign
saliency to input features to rank the degree of importance. However, these are still susceptible to
non-causal features.
The essence of the problem is that importance is not formally defined and is not guaranteed to relate
to the data-generation process in any way. As a result, such explainability methods can be challenging
to validate. We present existing approaches to formalizing explainability methods and a way to rank
feature importance in a more causal way.
5.1.1
Formalising XAI
One of the most significant issues, most likely because the field of XAI is still blossoming, is the lack of
formalism attached to the subject. The need for explainability in practical settings is excellent enough
that methods and solutions are developed on a case basis. Artificial intelligence is data-driven, making
solutions dependent on the data and, thus, the use case. A lack of general formalism makes a systematic
approach to a theoretical breakdown more difficult. However, inspired by existing literature, we present
a few different ways to regard XAI.
A very nice formalism on feature importance can be found in

5.7
Conclusion
We have identified and demonstrated what we believe is a critical issue within explainability: Current
state-of-the-art explainability methods attribute importance to suppressor variables. Specifically, we
demonstrate that for synthetic data, the linear extraction filter, sensitivity maps, and LIME all empha-
sized a particular form of suppressor variable, which we call the distractor signal. Additionally, we use
these methods to explain simple EEG models for EEG signals and spectrograms. While these meth-
ods produced meaningful results, it remains challenging to determine the reliability or if they are also
affected by suppressor variables. However, we noticed that the classifier uses some pre-event activity,
which could indicate an untrustworthy explanation.
To answer research question 3, one of the biggest challenges in current explainability
is the fact that state-of-the-art XAI methods like sensitivity maps and LIME attribute
importance to suppressor variables. We demonstrate this pitfall using a synthetic dataset
and provide practical examples of EEG explanations.

6.2.4
Pattern LIME
We want to generalize the ideas of Pattern to a non-linear setting. Here, we describe how we intend to
combine Pattern with the ideas for LIME to work with non-linear models. The general idea is to apply
Pattern on the local linear approximations made with LIME. This is not immediately straightforward,
and important challenges to highlight are
I Data-Agnostic vs. Data-Driven
• The LIME method is data-agnostic in that you can use the method without having access
to the training data.
• The Pattern is very much a data-driven method, as the idea is to incorporate correlations
between features.
II Instance-based vs. Global:
• The LIME method gives instance-based explanations. We can get a global explanation by
aggregating the results.
• The Pattern gives one (global) explanation which do not make sense in a non-linear setting.
In creating our new method, we will first assume that we have access to the training data and thus
sacrifice LIME’s data-agnostic property. This is necessary to estimate the covariance matrix and cannot
be done directly from the synthetic data.
The next huddle of using the Pattern method for instance-based explanations is harder to overcome.
The direct approach of estimating the covariance matrix ΣX from the training data and then applying
that to the local linear approximation from LIME will not work.
This is illustrated in Figure 6.3,

6.5
Discussion
The Pattern and Pattern LIME results are promising; however, we address a few noteworthy discussion
points.
6.5.1
Dimensionality of the Feature Space
One of the major issues with the Pattern LIME method is managing larger feature dimensions. For
extensive feature spaces, such as those found in EEG signals or spectrograms, the method becomes
impractical for several reasons:
1. Estimation of the high-dimensional covariance matrices: For EEG spectrograms with
D = 243200 features, we are trying to estimate D(D+1)
2
= 29, 573, 241, 600 parameters from only
about 500 samples for the BC Bhutan dataset and 5000 samples for the MMIDB dataset. This is
far fewer samples than required for a good estimate, making it a highly ill-posed problem. This
issue can be partly mitigated by using Pattern LIME with LASSO. However, this does not solve
the problem of the covariance matrix being needed in the sampling procedure for LIME.
2. The number of required samples: The LIME method needs a substantial amount of samples
to estimate the decision boundary [3]. We sample 10000 points for the synthetic datasets with 64
features for each instance. The same comparative number of samples would also be unreasonable
for a larger feature space.
One way to address these issues, also applied in the original LIME paper, is to group the features into
independent groups or segments. For example, for an image of a horse, this would be to segment the
image into the head, the body, the grass, the sky, and so on. This reduces the feature space dramatically
and allows us to sample these segments in LIME and estimate the covariance between these segments.
The quality of the explanations depends on the quality of this segmentation and sets many requirements
for the data representations.
6.5.2
Hyperparameters
The LIME method is extensively documented, with numerous variations available. In this project, we
have presented a simplified version of LIME to demonstrate the potential of combining it with Pattern.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
Throughout the thesis, we have included individual discussion sections for each chapter addressing the
immediate challenges and considerations regarding the work presented. In this last chapter, to conclude
the thesis, we reflect on our approach and present more general discussion points related to the research
questions.
7.1
A Unified Approach
In the first part of the thesis, we introduce an end-to-end pipeline for self-supervised learning applied
to EEG data. We primarily adopt methodologies from Wav2Vec by Baevski et al. [43] and BENDR
by Kostas et al. [4] to train a foundational model for EEG tasks. Specifically, we present the SPEED
pipeline, a domain-aware preprocessing framework to unify EEG datasets and enhance signal quality.
Like most medical data, EEG data is notoriously heterogeneous regarding data formatting and
feature dimensionality. Many parameters, such as the specifications of medical equipment, the number
of electrode channels, the sampling rate, and the method of event annotation, can lead to discrepancies
in data representations. From a machine learning perspective, we aim to develop a general-purpose
EEG model. However, it is crucial to discuss whether enforcing unification improves diagnostic support.
This is a persistent trade-off in AI. Does enforcing a unified approach on downstream EEG tasks and
training a general-purpose model compromise performance?
Our work demonstrates state-of-the-art results for the MMIDB and BC Bhutan downstream datasets.
However, for the BCICIV2 dataset [7] and the BCI@NER dataset [6], we achieve performances of 59%
and 75%, respectively, which are similar to other foundational models [4]. Nevertheless, these results do
not reach the performances reported in the literature (68% for BCICIV2a [56] and 86% for BCI@NER1).
The performance gap could be attributed to our focus not being on iterative improvement of down-
stream performance. It is possible that significantly higher downstream accuracies could be achieved
using DEEM. However, unifying the datasets may lead to the loss of crucial information. Such losses
might include dropping channels, altering the sampling rate, or making the datasets less representative
of the pretraining data.
The fixed set of electrode channels used for pretraining and downstream fine-tuning is likely the most
limiting factor of our current pipeline. In striving for a unified approach, the final set of electrodes
presented in Section 2.3.4 represents the maximum number of common channels. Note that considerable
effort is spent on channel name matching for some downstream datasets. In the SPEED pipeline, we
currently interpolate missing channels using information from all available channels (Kostas et al. [4]
discard extra channels). There might be more sophisticated methods to convert a higher number of
electrode signals to a fixed, lower number of channels used for training. Additionally, we lack a robust
solution for handling datasets with few channels, such as the P300 dataset benchmarked by Kostas et
al. [4] (where they simply zero out missing channels).

7.2
Data Representation and Concepts
Another trade-off is the processing and feature extraction level applied to EEG data before feeding it
into the model. A significant assertion by Kostas et al. [4] is that a foundation model for EEG data
can be trained without any data preprocessing. While this is not incorrect, in Chapter 3 we challenge
the notion that preprocessing does not enhance downstream performance. Although preprocessing the
TUEG data incurs time and computational costs, the resulting improvement in downstream performance
justifies these efforts.
In this work, we incorporate a substantial amount of spectrogram modeling primarily for inter-
pretability purposes. Spectrograms are particularly beneficial for artifact detection. For instance, we
observed an SVM achieving 91% accuracy across five classes, which is comparable to the DEEM on
EEG signal windows. This raises the question: would it be sensible to transform an entire pretraining
dataset, like the TUEH, into spectrograms and train a vision transformer instead? The data transfor-
mation cost is relatively low. However, our findings indicate that for the MMIDB, an SVM performs
better on raw EEG signals than on spectrograms. Therefore, we should further investigate the use of
spectrograms for various downstream tasks before committing to training a transformer.
Given the high interpretability of spectrograms, an intriguing idea is to train a model on raw EEG signals
and apply explainability techniques to the transformed data, such as spectrograms. This approach would
fall somewhere between traditional explainability and concept-based explainability. Although we have
not yet determined the best way to implement this, we are considering whether a segmentation approach
like LIME could be inspirational.
In future work, we aim to delve deeper into concept-based explainability. In our study titled ”Concept-
based explainability for an EEG transformer model” [17], we introduce anatomical and frequency-based
concepts for explaining an EEG transformer model. Transforming data into human-aligned concepts is
beneficial for interpretation and, in most cases, significantly reduces data dimensionality. An additional
aspect worth exploring is the approach used by the winners of the BCI@NER challenge in 2015, who
proposed using specialized covariance matrices as features and tools from Riemannian Geometry [57].
This could provide a promising direction for further research.
7.3
XAI for BCI
In Chapters 5 and 6, we have established that the field of explainability is difficult to approach because
of the lack of formalism and variance in desired outcome from use case to use case. We approached this
project from the perspective of causal discovery, only wanting an explanation that fits the underlying
data generation process. In reality, discovering relationships in the data is independent of any model
and what structures that model may learn from the data. A perfect model may learn the underlying
causal relationships, which is rarely the case in practice. Then, we might need to adjust the expectations
for the explanations that we can generate.
The natural question to ask is whether we prefer a model with high downstream accuracy or one
that accurately models the (underlying) system. This is a question that almost invites a philosophical
discussion of what AI models really should learn, see Appendix C for our philosophical considerations
on the subject. For example, for EEG and BCI, we should discuss if we wish to model artifact signals.
For the case of a motor imagery task, should we include the artifacts of hand movement or aim to model
only the cortical activity? If the use case is to train a model to help disabled people control artificial
motor movement, we do not want our models to pick up on unwanted muscle artefacts.
==PAGE:87==
7.3 XAI for BCI
77
The essential distinction to make for a model of brain activity is this: Are we interested in solving
some downstream tasks, e.g., seizure detection or motor movement? Or are we interested in learning
an accurate model of the human brain that we can use to learn how real brains function? We might not
be there yet, but it is not unimaginable that we could train models that we can perform interventions
on to learn about effective brain connectivity. As an example, a practical way of thinking about the
different purposes that a seizure detection model can have is this:
1. We aim to train a model that can detect epilepsy.
2. We aim to train an epilepsy model to better understand epilepsy.
3. We aim to train and explain a model to learn about what mechanisms the model uses to understand
epilepsy.
The first option describes classical machine learning, the second uses machine learning as a tool for
neuroscience, and the third provides theoretical insights into explainability. Again, we are bordering on
a philosophical discussion, but we believe such considerations are important for thorough and effective
research and development of explainable AI.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
In this thesis, we explore methods to enhance trustworthiness in AI systems. We introduce SPEED,
an efficient preprocessing pipeline for handling variability across EEG datasets and merging them into
a single preprocessed dataset suitable for self-supervised learning applications.
This Python-based
pipeline offers a unified approach to processing EEG data. We benchmark a PyTorch implementation
of an EEG transformer model adapted from Kostas et al. [4]. Using contrastive predictive coding, we
find that pretraining the transformer model on EEG data, properly scaled and preprocessed with the
SPEED pipeline, significantly improves pretraining. We evaluate the downstream performance on four
diverse EEG datasets. Overall, using a pretrained encoder markedly enhances performance, with the
domain-aware SPEED pipeline (excluding ICA) being the most effective for preprocessing. This results
in 1. 86% accuracy for MMIDB (2 classes), 2. 90% accuracy for the BC Bhutan dataset (5 classes), 3.
75% accuracy for BCI@NER (2 classes), and 4). 59% accuracy for BCICIV2a (4 classes).
We identify a critical issue in explainability: current methods often attribute importance to sup-
pressor variables. Specifically, the linear extraction filter, sensitivity maps, and LIME highlighted a
type of suppressor variable we call the distractor signal for synthetic data. We applied these methods
to simple EEG models for signals and spectrograms. Although they produced meaningful results, it re-
mains challenging to assess their reliability or whether they are also influenced by suppressor variables.
We observed that classifiers sometimes use pre-event activity, suggesting potentially untrustworthy
explanations. We demonstrate how linear activation patterns can enhance the trustworthiness and in-
terpretability of a linear discriminant analysis model, following the methodology by Haufe et al. [8, 49].
Applied to a synthetic dataset, this approach effectively removed suppressor variables and improved the
interpretability of linear models on EEG spectrograms. We extended this method to non-linear cases by
combining it with the Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations (LIME) method, resulting in a
new approach called Pattern LIME. We showed that Pattern LIME is effective with non-linear Support
Vector Machine (SVM) models for linear classification problems on synthetic datasets. Moreover, it
worked well on a non-linear XOR synthetic dataset for a multilayer neural network, providing valid
instance-based explanations.
Concretely, we found that:
1. Self-supervised machine learning for EEG data with domain-aware preprocessing could form the
basis for a state-of-the-art EEG foundation model.
2. Domain-aware preprocessing of EEG data can enhance the performance of a self-supervised EEG
foundation model, contrary to the claims by Kostas et al. [4].
3. A major challenge in current explainability is that common XAI methods attribute importance to
suppressor variables. We demonstrated this issue using a synthetic dataset and provided practical
examples for EEG data.
4. The method of linear activation patterns by Haufe et al. [8], which avoids attributing impor-
tance to suppressor variables, can be generalized to non-linear cases by combining it with local
linearization techniques like LIME.

A.2.2
Iterative Zapline Algorithm
The Zapline algorithm eliminates power line artifacts from multi-channel electrophysiological recordings,
such as EEG and MEG. It employs a combination of spectral and spatial filtering methods to remove
noise while retaining the original signals effectively [35]. It works as follows:
1. Data Splitting: The data matrix is divided into two parts using a Perfect Reconstruction Fil-
terbank:
• Noise-free component: X′ = X ∗K⊤
• Noise component: X′′ = X ∗(δ(0) −K)⊤
In this context, K denotes a square-shaped kernel with a duration of 1/ fline (where fline is the
frequency of the power line), and δ(0) symbolizes the Dirac delta function.
2. Denoising Matrix D: The denoising matrix D is crafted using the JD/DSS method [58], which
includes:
a) Establishing an artifact-amplifying bias filter B in the frequency domain.
b) Calculating covariance matrices C0 = X′′ · X′′⊤and C1 = (X′′ ∗B) · (X′′ ∗B)⊤.
c) Simultaneously diagonalizing C0 and C1 to discover a matrix M such that C0M = ΛC1M,
organizing components by decreasing artifact intensity.
d) Assuming the first d components encompass all artifacts, D is formulated as the multiplication
of the last J −d rows of M by the last J −d columns of its pseudoinverse M†.
3. Combining Components: The purified data ˜X is produced by applying the denoising matrix
and merging the components: ˜X = X′ + X′′D. This achieves a dataset largely devoid of power line
artifacts while maintaining the integrity of the underlying signals.
We derive the Iterative Zapline approach by iteratively applying this algorithm with d = 1 until no noise
components remain. This algorithm is practically implemented using the MeegKit in Python.

ABSTRACT
Electroencephalography (EEG) research typically focuses on tasks
with narrowly defined objectives, but recent studies are expanding
into the use of unlabeled data within larger models, aiming for a
broader range of applications. This addresses a critical challenge in
EEG research. For example, Kostas et al. (2021) show that self-
supervised learning (SSL) outperforms traditional supervised meth-
ods. Given the high noise levels in EEG data, we argue that further
improvements are possible with additional preprocessing. Current
preprocessing methods often fail to efficiently manage the large data
volumes required for SSL, due to their lack of optimization, reliance
on subjective manual corrections, and validation processes or inflex-
ible protocols that limit SSL. We propose a Python-based EEG pre-
processing pipeline optimized for self-supervised learning, designed
to efficiently process large-scale data. This optimization not only
stabilizes self-supervised training but also enhances performance on
downstream tasks compared to training with raw data.
Index Terms— Electroencephalography (EEG), Data Preprocess-
ing, Self-Supervised Learning
1. INTRODUCTION
As the processing of EEG measurements becomes more sophisti-
cated, the range of applications expands. Much EEG-based mod-
elling is focused on limited-scale, specific tasks, with fewer large-
scale projects targeting broader applicability. For limited-scale tasks
preprocessing is often a core dimension, to allow modelling from
noisy, small data sets. Such methods have not yet reached a level of
performance that excels across various tasks like in the case of large
language models (LLM) based AI. Nevertheless, some inspiration
is still drawn from LLMs, such as the application of self-supervised
learning (SSL) that makes use of unlabeled data. The most promi-
nent results have been achieved by Kostas et al. [1]. Their BENDR
model shows that while not effective on their own, SSL can improve
downstream task performance. The authors claim that deep learning
is sufficient to learn even from raw data, yet there is an important sci-
entific question: Does preprocessing help SSL? This is the question
we set out to test in the present work.
Current preprocessing methods do not scale to the substantial vol-
ume of data required for SSL. While current pipelines allow for man-
ual correction and validation of data, this makes them subjective,
and challenging reproducibility. Additionally, the correction process
is too task-specific for SSL, given the diverse nature of downstream
tasks. It can also lead to significant data loss, especially consid-
ering that the largest available EEG dataset, the Temple University
Hospital EEG Corpus (TUEG) [2], comprises EEG of highly vari-
able signal-to-noise. Most approaches become unfeasible with ter-
abytes of data, requiring robust, optimised pipelines capable of ef-
ficiently handling such large volumes. To address these challenges,
this paper introduces SPEED: Scalable Preprocessing for EEG Data,
a Python-based large-scale EEG data preprocessing pipeline tailored
for self-supervised learning. Our proposed pipeline is optimized for
massive data processing, efficiently leveraging hardware. We pro-
vide both pretraining and downstream procedures while avoiding re-
moving important information from the signal. We aim to establish
standardized procedures and enhance accessibility. Building upon
existing preprocessing pipelines, we maintain consistency while in-
troducing additional functionalities and improvements, motivated by
experiments. We demonstrate that preprocessing data for SSL mod-
els increases contrastive accuracy and yields better results in down-
stream task performance than training on raw data. Additionally, we
address the challenges of the TUH dataset by providing comprehen-
sive log files to enable reproducibility and further work with cleaned
data.
All code used in this research, along with references to the
datasets, are made publicly accessible.1.
2. BACKGROUND
EEG data varies among subjects, hardware, and environmental
factors and is susceptible to noise and artifacts. Therefore, a lot
of research in this field focuses on signal cleaning to extract the
genuine brain signal.
Historically, MATLAB has been the dom-
inant platform for EEG research, leading to the development of
numerous frameworks and pipelines for EEG analysis. EEGLAB
[3], Brainstorm [4] and FieldTrip [5] are some of the most widely
used frameworks. Other projects, such as the PREP pipeline [6],
aim to standardize larger-scale EEG preprocessing, incorporating
advanced techniques for removing line noise and perform robust
average referencing through iterative detection and interpolation of
artifact-contaminated channels. The Automagic pipeline [7] is de-
signed to aggregate currently available preprocessing methods and
provide objective standardized quality assessment techniques.
Based on Python’s open-source nature and widespread accessibil-
ity, MNE [8] offers a comprehensive suite of tools. Other libraries
and tools for bad channel detection were developed or adapted from
MATLAB, such as FASTER [9], Autoreject [10], and the adapted
PREP pipeline. However, available pipelines cannot handle the mas-
sive amounts needed for SSL and are not designed for SSL objec-
tives.
Kostas et al. [1] and Banville et al. [11] find self-supervision to
outperform or at least reach competitive performance compared to
supervised methods.
Banville et al. also find interpretable latent
structures in the learned embeddings, showing SSL efficacy in cap-
turing EEG-specific properties. However, they also perform mini-
mal processing using the same TUH dataset. In their NEURO-GPT

model, Cui et al. [12] preprocess their data using the Brainstorm soft-
ware performing simple bad channel detection, interpolation, and fil-
tering. We suggest a similar but more comprehensive amount of pre-
processing, showing that preprocessing biases models to even clearer
structures and better generalization and downstream results.
3. DATA
3.1. TUH EEG Corpus (TUEG) [2]
The Temple University Hospital EEG Corpus is a substantial col-
lection of clinical EEG recordings, consisting of 26,846 recordings
collected from 2002 to 2017, taking up 1.6 TB of storage. This
dataset is the primary data source for developing and testing our self-
supervised learning pipeline for EEG data analysis. It also serves as
the primary motivation as it is by far the largest public EEG dataset.
Still, there is a vast difference in the recordings in terms of equip-
ment used, task performed, length of recording, and quality of data.
3.2. Motor Movement/Imagery Dataset (MMIDB) [13]
The MMIDB dataset includes 64-channel EEG recordings from 109
subjects who performed motor and imagery tasks related to hand
movements. We are excluding participants S088, S090, S092, and
S100 due to missing data, resulting in 105 participants. We use ses-
sions 3, 7, and 11 where a target appears on either the left or right
side of the screen, and the subject opens and closes the correspond-
ing fist.
3.3. BrainCapture Bhutan Dataset v. 4.1 (BC Bhutan)
The BC Bhutan dataset includes 27-channel EEG recordings from
133 subjects across Bhutan. The data was collected in collabora-
tion with BrainCapture and is used for fine-tuning models. As part
of routine EEG recordings, the data contain the 5-second annotated
exercise windows Eye blinking, Eye movement, Eyes closed, Eyes
opened, and Jaw clenching.
3.4. BCI Challenge @ NER 2015 (BCI@NER) [14]
The dataset from the BCI Challenge at NER 2015 includes 56-
channel EEG recordings from 16 subjects who participated in 5
sessions each, totaling 80 sessions. This dataset includes two classes
for positive and negative feedback for P300 waves and it is used for
downstream benchmarking.
4. METHODS
The pipeline is built upon already existing preprocessing compo-
nents that are implemented and available in various packages in
Python. This section briefly introduces the methods we use in con-
secutive order as shown in Figure 1. The objective of SPEED is to
preprocess data into a uniform format suitable for machine learning
applications, ensuring high-quality data with consistency in chan-
nels and sample counts. We describe the general SPEED pipeline for
self-supervised pretraining and address any changes for downstream
datasets in a subsection afterwards.
4.1. Initial Setup of preprocessing
The initial steps are simple yet essential. They consist of standard-
izing channel names, detecting channel types, dropping non-EEG
channels, setting montage, and then dropping channels not in a
known montage. We then segment the data into 60-second windows
and omit the final window if it is less than 60 seconds in length. All
subsequent procedures are carried out within these windows.
4.2. Quality assessment
The process of quality assessment involves quickly determining
whether a window should be kept or discarded. This is not to waste
unnecessary time on too poor quality windows. We chose to drop
windows if there is an insufficient number of channels to interpolate
the desired montage, either due to a lack of channels or because they
are removed for being defective. Here we require at least half of
the final number of channels to be present. Another reason is the
excessive dropping of ICA components, leaving little of the original
signal intact. Lastly, the data may be of inherently poor quality,
either before or after processing. Quality assessment begins with
a straightforward bad channel detection using the PREP pipeline
[6] methods (excluding RANSAC), followed by the application of a
notch filter to eliminate line noise and a combination of a 1 Hz high-
pass and a 100 Hz low-pass filter. Subsequently, various quality
measures are calculated, largely adopting the criteria recommended
in the AutoMagic pipeline [7]. These measures include the Ratio
of Bad Channels (RBC), the Ratio of Data with Overall High Am-
plitude (OHA), the Ratio of Timepoints with High Variance (THV),
and the Ratio of Channels with High Variance (CHV). We exclude
any window where OHA < 0.8, THV < 0.5, CHV < 0.5, and
BCR < 0.8. These thresholds were determined through manual
reviews of over 100 EEG recordings.
4.3. Iterative Zapline
Next, we remove line noise using the Iterative Zapline Algorithm
[15]. The Zapline algorithm is designed to remove power line arti-
facts from multichannel electrophysiological data, such as EEG and
MEG. It combines spectral and spatial filtering techniques to achieve
effective noise removal while preserving the underlying signals [15].
The line noise frequency, usually 50Hz in Afro-Eurasia or 60Hz in
America, is manually set. The Python implementation MeegKit is
applied [15].
4.4. Bad Channel Detection
Bad channel detection is performed on a copy of the dataset, and
the identified bad channels are afterwards removed from the original
dataset. The objective of this process is to identify channels with low
signal-to-noise ratios (SNR). Dropping these channels is essential as
their presence negatively impacts interpolation, average referencing,
and independent component analysis (ICA).
The basis for bad channel detection lies in the Python implemen-
tation of the PREP pipeline. It initiates the detection by remov-
ing flat/NAN channels and low-frequency trends.
It then identi-
fies channels with extreme amplitudes (deviation criterion), abnor-
mal high-frequency noise (noisiness criterion), poor correlation with
other channels (correlation criterion), and weak predictability by
other channels (predictability criterion). The latter involves assess-
ing the correlation of each channel within a 5s EEG data window
with its Random Sample Consensus (RANSAC) [16] reconstruction
(using 50 samples from a subset of 25% of all channels). A chan-
nel is considered bad if its correlation is below 0.85 in more than
40% of instances. Due to the RANSAC algorithm requiring signifi-
cant resources, its inclusion is optional. The identified bad channels
are then excluded from the dataset. Originally, the PREP pipeline
included bad channel detection in its robust average referencing pro-
cess, which iteratively detects and interpolates bad channels to en-
sure a reliable average reference. However, as we do not wish to
interpolate every bad channel, particularly before ICA, and the high
computational demands, this method is not used.

4.5. Filtering & Average Referencing
We apply robust detrending, a 0.5 Hz high-pass filter, and a 100 Hz
low-pass filter. We use robust detrending because we aim to elim-
inate linear trends, even though the specific frequency of the high-
pass filter may vary as needed. Lastly, we apply average referencing
to the remaining high-quality channels [17], [18].
4.6. Independent Component Analysis
We proceed to the Extended Infomax Independent Component Anal-
ysis (ICA) [19] transformation, setting the number of components
equal to the number of remaining channels. We classify the com-
ponents using ICLabel [20] methods and exclude every indepen-
dent component not categorized as brain or other and with certainty
above 80%. The signal is reconstructed from the remaining inde-
pendent components. A further Bad Channel Detection (see Section
4.4) is conducted on a duplicate of the dataset.
Independent Component Analysis (ICA) stands out among vari-
ous methods for isolating and eliminating artifacts and noise from
data. It represents the most computationally intensive step in the
process, yet could offer significant benefits, particularly through au-
tomation with ICLabel. Therefore we add an option to skip the ICA
in the pipeline.
ICLabel: The ICLabel project [20] is designed to automate the
classification of electroencephalographic (EEG) independent com-
ponents (ICs), aiming to facilitate the analysis of EEG studies with
many subjects and enable the use of ICA decomposition automat-
ically. This effort has produced the automated ICLabel classifier,
trained on over 6,000 labelled ICs, and is currently leading in accu-
racy. It assigns ICs to seven categories: brain, muscle artifact, eye
blink, heart, line noise, channel noise, and other. The latter cate-
gory serves as a general catch-all for ICs with indeterminate noise or
those containing signals that ICA decomposition could not distinctly
separate.
4.7. Interpolation & Resample
Missing channel interpolation is crucial because it enables us to stan-
dardize EEG data from various sources and leverage information
from additional channels instead of discarding them. We interpolate
the channels that are missing from the final desired set, regardless
of whether they were deemed bad or were initially missing. The
==PAGE:107==
interpolation is done with Legendre polynomial expansion via the
minimum-norm method, as implemented in MNE Python. More in-
formation on these methods can be found in [21]. Afterwards, we
remove any extra channels, reorder the final channels, and resample
them to 256Hz.
4.8. Downstream Data Preprocessing
The downstream data preprocessing is done similarly to the pretrain-
ing data. The changes are highlighted in Figure 1 in dotted lines.
Those parts are omitted from the downstream processing. After the
initial setup, the downstream data is not split into 60-second win-
dows.
However, all data before the first and after the last event
marker is removed to avoid unclean sections in the signal. All subse-
quent procedures are carried out on the cropped recording. The qual-
ity assessment is skipped on downstream data because, in datasets
intended for downstream analysis, the decision to drop a window is
challenging, as it could increase the risk of Type I errors [22]. For
example, in attempting to detect seizures, which are characterized
by high-frequency noise, it is undesirable to discard windows with
high variance. While this issue remains relevant in a self-supervised
learning context with large-scale data, it becomes less prominent
since the specifics of downstream analysis are not predetermined.
Other parts in the pipeline that require dropping bad windows are
skipped because the downstream data is processed as one recording
and not split into windows.
5. EXPERIMENTS
5.1. Preprocessing of the TUH EEG Corpus
A significant contribution of this paper is the preprocessing of the
Temple University Hospital EEG Corpus. SPEED is designed for
multiprocessing and is executed in parallel across 30 cores on 2 x
EPYC 7302 and 30 cores on 2 x EPYC 9124, each equipped with
128GB of RAM, completing the task in under a week. The pre-
processing comprehensively logs each step, allowing for detailed in-
spection of each data window. This includes information on which
windows are dropped, identification of bad channels, channels re-
moved for not matching the montage setup, the labels and proba-
bility scores of independent components from ICLabel, components
that are excluded, and extra channels that are dropped.
5.2. Pretraining
We use the BENDR model from Kostas et al. [1] for our exper-
iments. This model is pretrained over 5 epochs using the prepro-
cessed TUH EEG Corpus with (1) SPEED, as depicted in Figure 1,
(2) SPEED w/ ICA, also shown in Figure 1, and (3) Baseline, as
employed in Kostas et al. [1], which involves only resampling and
zeroing out missing channels. For validation, we similarly prepro-
cess the three smaller downstream datasets (MMIDB, BC Bhutan,
and BCI@NER) in 60-second windows. The model with the lowest
validation loss from this phase is selected for subsequent probing.
5.3. Probing
We initiate probing by incorporating the pre-trained encoder layer
from the model, adding linear layers, and locking the pre-trained
encoder layer. This procedure aims to assess whether the pretrained
latent space provides an improved representation of EEG data when
processed using different preprocessing pipelines. The models are
probed using the labelled downstream datasets MMIDB, BC Bhutan,
and BCI@NER, which are preprocessed similarly and segmented
into 5-second windows.
We implement a 10-fold cross-validation with subject-based splits
to ensure that data from the same individual does not overlap across
the training, validation, and test sets. In each cross-validation cycle,
the designated test fold is split further into two distinct datasets: one
for validation and one for testing. The test dataset is used for on-
going evaluation and model selection throughout the training phase.
The validation dataset, on the other hand, is used exclusively for
the final evaluation of the model’s performance post-training. This
method guarantees that our model evaluation is thorough and accu-
rately represents the model’s effectiveness with new, unseen data.
This configuration is repeated five times resulting in 50 performance
estimates per experimental setup for each downstream dataset.

7. DISCUSSION
With our pipeline, we address a major challenge of large-scale EEG
data preprocessing pipelines.
However, a major consideration is
whether to incorporate ICA with ICLabel. This technique is well-
established in EEG preprocessing and analysis and is part of the
AutoMagic [7] pipeline. Our analysis indicates that including ICA
does not significantly improve the pretraining performance. Rather,
examining the probing results slightly reduces the downstream ac-
curacy when used in pretraining and significantly when applied to
downstream datasets. This suggests that the model might either miss
artifacts in SPEED, perhaps use them in classification or that ICLa-
bel incorrectly classifies independent components, leading to the re-
moval of significant data.
For downstream datasets, more careful preprocessing is often re-
quired, which is not the focus of this paper. These datasets are typ-
ically smaller and of higher quality, making them more suitable for
detailed analysis. Given that both the MMIDB and BrainCapture
Bhutan classification tasks involve muscle movement, it is unsurpris-
ing that removing the muscle artifact and eye blink ICs diminishes
accuracy.
==PAGE:109==
Another point of interest is that we are fine-tuning the model us-
ing the same datasets designated for validation during pretraining,
which could introduce bias. We adopt this approach as it aligns with
the methodology used in the paper by Kosta et al. [1]. During pre-
training, we use 60-second windows and all available data, both an-
notated and unannotated, with the latter making up the majority of
the dataset. Therefore, this potential bias is not a major concern.
8. CONCLUSION
The ’industry standard’ in machine learning is to let complex deep
learning models handle massive datasets with limited preprocess-
ing, especially with self-supervised learning. For electroencephalo-
gram (EEG) data, Kostas et al. pretrained a complex transformer
model on the massive Temple University Hospital (TUH) EEG Cor-
pus with rather rudimentary preprocessing. However, the TUH EEG
Corpus presents significant challenges due to its variability in signal-
to-noise, equipment used, length of recording, and more. This pa-
per introduces an efficient preprocessing pipeline designed to han-
dle variability within one or more EEG datasets and combine them
into a single preprocessed dataset useful for self-supervised learning
applications. The Python-based pipeline improves stability, conver-
gence, and contrastive accuracy during pretraining and produces a
more suitable latent space for downstream classification tasks. More
specifically, our probing results for downstream accuracy show a
significant improvement for several downstream classification tasks
when pretraining using our preprocessing pipeline compared to a
simple baseline preprocessing pipeline. Besides the plug-and-play
preprocessing pipeline, we also present tools for reproducible pre-
processing of the TUH EEG Corpus ready for future development of
pretrained EEG foundation models. In conclusion, our results form
evidence that physically motivated preprocessing is useful for self-
supervised learning of EEG representations.

APPENDIX C
Philosophical
Considerations on
Explainability
Navigating explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) is not easy. In the following, we attempt to address
some of the conceptual and philosophical difficulties that explainability may bring. But first, let us tell
you about a horse called Hans.
C.1
A story about a Horse
In the early twentieth century, largely due to recent publications by Charles Darwin, an immense
public fascination with animal intelligence prevailed. Darwin’s work challenged conventional views on
the uniqueness of human intelligence by suggesting evolutionary continuity between humans and other
animals. This laid the groundwork for a widespread interest in exploring and understanding animal
cognition.
One particular example that caught public attention was that of a horse owned by high school math
teacher Wilhelm von Osten. This horse, Clever Hans, became famous for demonstrating remarkable
intellectual abilities. In front of audiences, Clever Hans performed a variety of tasks that seemed to
showcase his mathematical skills. The horse could add, subtract, multiply, and divide - astonishing
onlookers by tapping his hoof to indicate the correct answers to the arithmetic problems that von Osten
and others posed.
However, the initial excitement and belief in Hans’ abilities prompted a formal investigation to
determine the true nature of his skills. Psychologist Oskar Pfungst conducted controlled experiments to
understand how Hans achieved these feats. Pfungst’s investigation revealed that Clever Hans was not
performing arithmetic or understanding riddles as humans do. Instead, the horse was highly sensitive
to subtle, involuntary cues from his owner and other humans during the demonstrations. Hans had
learned to detect changes in posture, facial expressions, and other body language signals that indicated
when he should start and stop tapping his hoof.
This discovery, known as the Clever Hans effect, highlighted the importance of considering unintentional
human influence in studies of animal cognition. But Clever Hans went on to signify even more than
animal behaviour.
The Clever Hans effect is remarkably relevant in machine learning, particularly
concerning the phenomenon known as shortcut learning [59]. In machine learning, shortcut learning
occurs when models exploit spurious correlations or unintended cues in the training data to make
predictions rather than learning the intended underlying patterns or principles. Like Clever Hans was
not truly solving arithmetic problems but instead responding to human cues, machine learning models
can sometimes appear to perform well on specific tasks by relying on shortcuts that do not generalize
well to unseen data.

C.1.1
Shortcuts
A classic example of the Clever Hans effect in machine learning involves the unintended use of wa-
termarks in horse images. Suppose a machine learning model is trained to classify images of horses.
However, it learns to rely on a watermark in the corner of the images rather than identifying actual
horse traits [14], see Figure C.2 for an illustration of identifying the watermark as important for a horse
image.
The model cheats!
(Klaus-Robert Müller – 2023)
This reliance on the watermark is a form of shortcut learning. The model minimizes the cost function
by exploiting the watermark as a shortcut. As a result, the model performs exceptionally well on the
specific dataset containing the watermarked horse images.
However, this performance is misleading. When the model is presented with horse images without
watermarks, it performs poorly, underscoring a critical issue: What information do we want our models
to learn? In the case of classifying horses, we aim for the model to understand and identify intrinsic
features of the animal, such as shape, size, and distinctive physical characteristics, rather than relying
on extraneous cues like watermarks.
C.2
What Makes a Horse a Horse?
Dismissing a watermark as important for classifying horses is easy.
While watermarks might corre-
late with horses in a specific set of images, they do not contain intrinsic information about horses.
Watermarks should not be part of the equation if we aim to model horses accurately.
Now, consider something like a grass field. In the real world, grass fields are often correlated with
horses, and most images of horses that we feed to our model likely contain a grass field. Should we
then consider grass field an attribute of a horse? This raises an essential question: What makes a horse
a horse? What features or attributes intrinsically describe a horse, and how do we best model those
features?

This dilemma echoes Plato’s Theory of Forms. According to this theory, the physical world does not
capture the true nature or form of objects. Instead, the true identity of things exists in Ideas. In this
framework, the Idea of a Horse represents the truest form of a horse, displaying the set of all important
features, which certainly do not include grass fields or watermarks. Is the Idea of a Horse what we want
our machine learning model to learn? To address these challenges, we can systematically divide the
goals of machine learning tasks into different categories:
1. Intrinsic Features: Identify and model the intrinsic attributes that define the object. This
might include shape, size, colour, and specific anatomical features for horses. In the platonic
sense, these features could be defined as the Idea of Horse.
2. Contextual Features: Understand the context in which the object typically appears. While
not intrinsic, these features can be useful for certain applications, such as recognizing that horses
often appear in grass fields. This is probably where we would classify suppressor variables.
3. Spurious Features: These features create an illusion of importance because they correlate with
the target variable within the training dataset. However, this correlation does not hold in broader
contexts, leading to poor model generalization when applied to new data. This could be features
like watermarks.
In a practical setting, this means improving data collection to ensure diverse and representative datasets,
engaging in thoughtful feature engineering to emphasize intrinsic qualities, and employing rigorous
validation techniques to identify and mitigate reliance on spurious correlations. This approach ensures
that our models perform well on training data and effectively generalize new data.

In Lopez-Paz et al. [60], a formalisation of the presence of a horse is presented. We count the
number C(A, B) of images in which the causal characteristics of objects in category B would disappear
if objects of category A were removed. We then say that the objects of category A cause the presence
of objects in category B when C(A, B) is significantly greater than the converse C(B, A). For example, if
removing grass makes it more likely that the horse disappears, and this likelihood is greater than the
converse, then we conclude that the presence of grass causes the presence of a horse.

C.3
Using AI should be like driving a car
Most people know how to drive a car, which involves basic actions like turning on the engine, shifting
gears, and steering. While most drivers have a superficial understanding of how cars work, this limited
knowledge is sufficient to operate the complex technology effectively.

The same principle should apply to artificial intelligence. To use AI systems efficiently and effectively,
we don’t need to understand every intricate detail of how they function. Instead, we should aim for
a robust understanding of AI’s capabilities and limitations. Currently, no one person can account for
every piece of complicated equipment in a modern car, but that doesn’t prevent ordinary people from
using them. Similarly, AI systems should be designed to allow users to interact with them confidently,
even without a deep technical background.
This perspective is crucial when considering the role of explainability in AI. True impact will come
from creating a natural and valuable language for humans to understand and interact with AI. When
AI systems can explain their decisions and actions intuitively and meaningfully, users can trust and
utilize these technologies more effectively, just as they do with cars.
