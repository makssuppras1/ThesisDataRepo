Abstract
The main objective of this Thesis is to study the behavior of steel framed buildings sub-
jected to the rare events of ﬁre following earthquake. Ignition is assumed to occur immedi-
ately after the earthquake, inducing permanent deformations, residual displacements and
an increase of the P −δ eﬀect. The damage sustained during the earthquake poses a risk
of reducing the ﬁre resistance of the structure.
An introduction to the origin of the issue is presented in the ﬁrst Chapter and several
historic events are described which led to the necessity to further investigate the phenomena
of post-earthquake ﬁres.
Relevant research background performed by other authors is
brieﬂy presented next. The Chapter is completed with a description of the objectives of
the Thesis and describes the approach and methodology applied in the study.
Relevant theoretical background is given in the next Chapter, introducing the state-of-
the-art of post earthquake ﬁres, meaning signiﬁcant ﬁndings from the research background
previously presented. The most relevant studies to the scope of this Thesis are described
along with their ﬁndings. In the following part, theoretical knowledge essential to under-
standing the behavior of the members during earthquake and ﬁre is described.
The design of the frames is described in the third Chapter, as well as details regarding
the modeling performed in the FEM software.
Chapters 4 and 5 contain the seismic
respectively ﬁre investigation. The frames are subjected to a number of 9 earthquakes, out
of which two most signiﬁcant are used further on in the ﬁre analysis. For the ﬁre part, the
frames are subjected to the standard ﬁre curve ISO834. Several scenarios are analyzed,
out of which two are described in detail for each structure.
The ﬁnal Chapter consists of discussions and comments on the results. It was concluded
that the reduction is not signiﬁcant, reaching up to 3% for global collapse, respectively 17%
for local failure of the beams. In some cases an increase of 4% have been recorded, which
are further explained in this Thesis. This conclusion can be extended to MRF structures
without any additional ﬁre insulating materials applied.
Diﬀerent collapse modes and
patterns are highlighted for the two structures related to the total height.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The performance of buildings exposed to ﬁre has been an important subject of research,
but after the World Trade Center collapse on September 11, 2001, even more interest has
been drawn to this matter [28]. Design of structures consists on taking into account one
accidental action such as ﬁre, impact or earthquake. Fires occurring after earthquakes
can cause signiﬁcant damage to structures located in areas which are prone to seismic
action. The phenomena of post-earthquake ﬁres are deﬁned as low probability with high
consequence events [24]. Several studies have shown that in most cases the damages and
human losses from a ﬁre following an earthquake may be greater than those caused by the
earthquake itself [44].
Multiple disasters provoked by post-earthquake ﬁres have been documented by Faggiano
and Scawthorn. The Great Lisbon earthquake from 1755, was one of the most destructive
earthquakes in history. It added up to 60, 000 −90, 000 deaths with 85% of the buildings
destroyed. The destruction was so great because the movement was followed by a total of
3 tsunami waves. Several ﬁres broke out which burned for 5 days [15].
The 1906 San Francisco earthquake and the 1923 Kanto earthquake in Tokyo [33] were the
largest ﬁres during peacetime in history documented by Scawthorn et al. The ﬁre in San
Francisco resulted in damage worth around 3 billion USD in today’s prices and between
3, 000 −5, 000 lives, from which 80% of the damage was due to the post-earthquake ﬁre.
Despite the strong shaking, the ﬁre was clearly predominant in the total damage provoked.
The water distribution system’s pipes were severed, mostly due to liquefaction of the soil,
therefore one of the solutions to put out the ﬂames was to use explosive to demolish
buildings in order to create ﬁre breaks. This lead only to more ﬁre spread as the gas pipes

The M7.9 earthquake in Tokyo concluded with the largest urban conﬂagration in history,
resulting in losses of around 140, 000 lives, the ﬁre accounting for approximately 78% of
the damage. The built environment, and the ignition scenario were favorable for an urban
conﬂagration [33].
Scawthorn also presented two events that took place in California. In 1989 the Loma Prieta
earthquake of a M7.1 magnitude resulted in damage worth 6 billion USD in direct and
indirect losses. The number of buildings destroyed was of 145 and 2, 500 damaged, while
960 homes were destroyed and 18, 000 damaged [33]. The 1994 M6.8 Northridge California
earthquake caused moderate damage due to the fast reaction of the ﬁre department. A
total of 30−50 ﬁres ignited, resulted in the collapse of several low-rise buildings [38].
The following year, another much more devastating event of ﬁre following earthquake was
reported in Kobe, Japan. The M6.9 1995 earthquake resulted in great damage due to
both seismic movement and the ﬁres that followed, adding up to approximately 142 ﬁres
which developed into several large conﬂagrations. Prompt ﬁre ﬁghting was obstructed by
collapsed buildings and rubble in the streets as many areas were inaccessible to vehicles,
while the water supply was limited to 3 hours [33].
The 1999 Marmara earthquake in Turkey was a catastrophic event as it resulted in the loss
of 17, 000 lives. The event did not results in any urban conﬂagrations, but a major ﬁre
ignited in a petroleum reﬁnery which burnt for several days [33].
One of the more recent events is the M9 Fukushima earthquake on March 11 2011, which
was shortly followed by ﬁre as well as a tsunami and a nuclear accident. A number of
278 events consisting of inland and coastal ﬁres were reported, resulting in 15, 898 deaths,
6, 155 injured and many missing. The ﬁres involved several oil tanks located in the bay
area which were destroyed by the tsunami resulting in the oil igniting; on the other hand,
ﬁre was spread by ignited cars and houses ﬂoating in the water.
Although it seems a
paradox, ﬁre poses a signiﬁcant concern in the event of a tsunami as aﬀected areas cannot
be reached [37].
There are many variables involved which contribute to the probability that a ﬁre may occur
after an earthquake such as the time of the event, electrical short circuits, direct contact
with an open ﬁre source, broken fuel tanks, rupture of gas and oil pipes [31]. Another key
aspect is the eﬃciency of the response. Access may be blocked by collapsed buildings and
the active ﬁre safety systems could be damaged leading to large urban conﬂagrations.
The main concern in design nowadays is that the current codes do not cover the design
for buildings subjected to post-earthquake ﬁres. The eﬀects of seismic motion and ﬁre are
treated as two accidental actions and it is assumed that they do not occur simultaneously,
more importantly their eﬀects are not super-imposed.
In reality, the resistance of the

in particular are in most cases provided with insulation as the ﬁre resistance of steel is
greatly reduced at high temperatures. After an earthquake, the remaining resistance of
the structure might not be suﬃcient to sustain a ﬁre or if greatly decreased, it might not
resist enough time for the ﬁre ﬁghters to access the building. Therefore, even if an adequate
ﬁre resistance is assured in normal conditions, the ﬁre following earth-quake may cause the
structure to collapse.
From the described examples, it can be seen that in some cases much greater losses are due
to the post-earthquake ﬁres rather than the earthquake itself. This suggests that a special
attention should be given to the eﬀect of ﬁre on damaged structures.
1.2
Research Background
Probably the most comprehensive view on post-earthquake ﬁres is given in the book Fire
Following Earthquake published by Scawthorn [33]. It documents past earthquakes in ur-
ban regions and includes models in ﬁre spread and multiple mitigation alternatives. It pro-
vides baseline information for engineers, ﬁre service, insurance managers and others.
Della Corte et al. [12] investigated the behavior of unprotected steel moment resisting
frames. A simple portal frame and two multistory frames are considered. The results show
that both investigated frames have a ﬁre resistance reduction for damage sustained during
earthquakes, but when designed to both ULS and SLS conditions, the reduction is less
than 10%.
H.Yassin et al. [44] analyzed the response of two-dimensional steel frames subjected to
seismic action and subsequent ﬁre.
Two frames were studied and the conclusion was
that the passive ﬁre protection of the building is most likely damaged by the horizontal
movement, while the post-earthquake ﬁre performance of the structure is reduced by the
lateral load induced deformations. Other studies on unprotected moment resisting frames
have been done by Zaharia and Pintea [47]. The study shows that buildings designed for
stronger seismic actions have a signiﬁcant additional resistance in case of ﬁre. However,
frames which sustained permanent damage have reported decreased resistance.
A methodology to determine the ﬁre resistance of buildings damaged by earthquake is
presented by Faggiano and Mazzolani [16]. Several performance levels are deﬁned that
correlate the seismic intensity with the damage extent. The conclusion is that the ﬁre
resistance of the structure is reduced only for signiﬁcant damage.

Khorasani et al. [24] introduced a probabilistic evaluation framework having the purpose

The post-earthquake damage did not have any signiﬁcant eﬀect on the ﬁre resistance of

The deterioration of steel connections was investigated by Petrina [30] based on previous

protecting material, depending on how much protection is removed, cross-sectional area of
the column and location of protection loss. Other studies done on this matter were done by
Arablouei and Kodur [2] on the eﬀect of insulation delamination on steel structures during

must be prevented in the plastic hinge zones on the beams, as this will drastically reduce
the ﬁre resistance.
1.3
Objectives
In the previous section, diﬀerent events were enumerated where ﬁre consequent to earth-
quake provoked immense damage. This project is focused on studying the performance of
steel frames when subjected to ﬁre following earthquake, observing collapse modes related
to diﬀerent heights of the structure. A case study building is chosen from literature and
a parametric study is performed to determine the correlation between the failure mode
and the total height of the building. The problem at hand is rather complex and diﬀerent
aspects must be considered, but the main concern is if a special design condition should
be implemented for buildings located in highly seismic areas, which would take account of
the ﬁre resistance deterioration due to earthquake damage.
The response of two structures having diﬀerent heights is investigated, focusing on the
global collapse mechanism and failure mode of individual members. This should oﬀer ad-
ditional information for understanding how do diﬀerent unprotected steel-frame structures
behave when aﬀected by post-earthquake ﬁres.

1.4.2
FEM Analysis procedure
Implementing the sequential analysis in Abaqus requires an understanding of the diﬀerent
analyses types available. This section consists of explaining the purpose and principle of
each step applied in the analysis.
An Initial step is given when creating a model. This includes the initial boundary conditions
and any predeﬁned ﬁelds. The following step consists on applying the gravitational static
loads acting on the frame using a general analysis. Thus, a Static General step is deﬁned
which can perform both linear and non-linear analyses. However, since all following steps
are dynamic, for consistency a Dynamic implicit step is applied also for the loading, as no
diﬀerences were noticed in terms of stresses and displacements. the Non-linear problems
may occur due to three sources of non-linearity: geometrical, material and boundary non-
linearity.
The natural frequencies and natural modes of vibration of the structure can be determined
using the classical Eigenvalue problem.
For the modal analysis, a linear perturbation
method is selected and the step Frequency is created. In this step it is not possible to
apply any loads, thus in order to obtain a correct response of the frame, the gravitational
loads have been applied as non-structural masses. The default Lanczos solver is suitable for
problems with symmetric mass and stiﬀness matrices and few degrees of freedom [36].
Seismic design according to the Eurocode [9] is performed by deﬁning a Response spectrum
step. Similarly a linear perturbation analysis is chosen and it is based on the previous
Frequency analysis, as it uses the modes of vibration to compute the linear response of
the system. It is not suitable for non-linear response due to large motions. While it is an
inexpensive method, the results obtained are rather approximate. Another aspect worth
mentioning is that this step is independent of the time domain and only the peak response
can be determined based on the frequency domain.
A more reﬁned method that simulates the earthquake action is represented by the Dynamic,
implicit step. It uses implicit time integration to calculate the dynamic response of the
structure.
Dynamic analysis is recommended when non-linearities are included in the
model. The response of the frame in time can be determined, but accurate results require a
large number of small increments which might require a large computational capacity.
The same Dynamic, implicit step is selected when simulating the temperature increase. It
was selected instead of a Static, general step because it can reach a more complex solution.
It is in general a more stable method, although as previously mentioned, it is more costly
from a computational point of view.

2.2
Structural response in elevated temperatures
Fire represents a signiﬁcant threat for steel structures as the material properties suﬀer a
rapid degradation with increasing temperature. Therefore, the design principles at am-
bient temperatures must be adjusted accordingly. Besides the reduction of the material
properties, steel elements are subjected to thermal expansion which introduces additional
stresses along the member.
Loading on the structure is diﬀerent when designing for ﬁre scenarios due to the fact that
it is very unlikely that the building is loaded to full capacity in the event of a ﬁre. As
a consequence, diﬀerent safety factors are used in the relevant load combination.
The
resulting loading is deﬁned as a load ratio, representing a fraction of the ultimate load
which would normally lead to the collapse of the structure.
2.2.1
Modeling the ﬁre
The behavior of a ﬁre may be modeled by a temperature-time curve given in diﬀerent
standards. The four stages of a ﬁre development include the incipient stage, the growth of
the ﬁre, the burning phase and the decay phase which are shown in Figure 2.6.
During the incipient stage, the fuel is heated producing smoke or other toxic products,
while this stage can last from several milliseconds to a few days.
Ignition of the fuel
can result in either a combustion which can continue for days or a ﬂaming combustion;
it is an exothermic chemical process which generates smoke as CO2 and C, producing

energy known as the ”heat release rate”. The growing phase is fuel-controlled and it is
characterized by an increase of the HRR and of the aﬀected area. The conditions can be
approximated by a two-layer model.
As the temperature increases, the ﬁre development may be divided into 2 parts: pre-
ﬂashover and post-ﬂashover phase. The ﬂashover point can be described as a transition
between a small object oriented ﬁre to a full room involvement and it is marked by the
moment when all the combustible materials in the enclosure ignite leading to very high
temperatures. The fully developed ﬁre (post-ﬂashover) is of particular interest in struc-

characterized as ventilation-controlled meaning that the rate of fuel pyrolysis is greater
than what can be burned by the available air supply, resulting in ﬂames reaching outside
of openings.
During the decay stage the ﬁre goes back to being fuel-controlled until all available fuel
is burned. This stage is assumed to commence when approximately 80% of the fuel is
consumed [34].

2.2.3
Non-linear properties
In order to correctly assess the response of steel when subjected to large forces, non-
linear behavior must be accounted for. These properties can be categorized as material
non-linearities which occur due to the stress exceeding the elastic limit and geometrical
non-linearities due to the additional stresses created by the large deformations.
Plasticity
Material plastic behaviour is deﬁned as the presence of permanent deformations after all
stresses applied are reduced to zero and the stress-strain curve is no longer linear as in
the elastic stage. A perfect plastic material is one that sustains permanent deformations
without any further increase of the applied load [41].
An example of the stress and strain distribution on a cross-section is presented in Figure
2.13, when increasing the load over the elastic limit Pel. In Figure (a) the material has
reached the elastic limit. Starting from (b) the section begins to plasticize as the stress in
the extreme ﬁbers reaches the plastic limit. The material is described as having an elasto-
plastic behavior and as the strain continues to increase in (c), the cross-section continues
to plasticize without any increase in the maximum stress. The maximum load is reached
in (d) where any further increase would lead to collapse. This phenomenon is known as
the redistribution of stresses.

Figure 2.13: Stresses and strains when load increases over elastic limit [41]
The total resulting stress can be determined as in Equation 2.19 which is the sum of the
recoverable elastic strain and the permanent plastic strain.
εtot = εel + εpl
(2.19)
This additional load increase is possible until the entire cross-section becomes plastic even
though no further moment increase is possible, the curvature can still become larger, this
rotation being known as the formation of a plastic hinge.

Geometrical non-linearities
When a member undergoes large deformations, the classical equilibrium conditions used in
structural mechanics are not valid as they are computed based on the undeformed shape,
not being updated with the deformation. This results a variation in the stiﬀness function
of the displacement.
When the strain or rotation in a member become very large, this deformed shape must be
considered taking into consideration the non-linear geometries.

Catenary action
Current structural ﬁre design of steel beams assumes a simpliﬁed procedure based on plastic
bending resistance or lateral torsional buckling resistance while the beam undergoes small
deﬂections. In other words, the concept is based on determining the critical temperature at
which the resisting bending moment equals the applied bending moment on the beam [8].
This is rather ineﬃcient, as expensive ﬁre protection is required or encasing the members
in concrete.
In reality, the deﬂections are much greater at increased temperatures, which is not taken
into account in the simpliﬁed design. As long as structural collapse and loss of stability
is prevented, large deﬂections can be introduced in the design and prove to be favorable.
When the members are greatly deﬂected, the bending of the beam results into catenary
action meaning that the compressive forces revert to tensile forces due to the very large
deﬂection. The bending resistance of the beam to the vertical loads is given by the resulting
catenary force. It is important that if catenary action is accounted for in design, the forces
acting on the adjacent structure must also be considered.
If a beam has suﬃcient axial restraint, catenary action will allow it to resist at very high
temperatures without collapsing.
This is because the resistance is not limited by the
material strength as before, but by the increasing deﬂection. In theory, the beam would
retain bearing capacity and not collapse until the steel begins to melt [46]. Therefore, any
steel beam could be designed to resist ﬁre without any protection.
The beams deﬂection and force induced by catenary action is greatly inﬂuenced by the level
of axial restraint. The lower the axial restraint of the beam is, the smaller the deﬂection
will be. On the other hand, a very stiﬀrestraint will result in high catenary forces. This is
beneﬁcial for the beam resistance in bending, but will greatly aﬀect the structure adjacent
to the beam ends. Introducing catenary action in ﬁre design requires a correlation between
the restraint stiﬀness and resistance of the structure to the forces developed in the ends of
the beam, as well as the maximum acceptable beam deﬂection [45].

2.3
Structural response to seismic action
In order to evaluate the buildings’ response to horizontal actions, the structural dynam-
ics problem is formulated for a simple structure in the ﬁrst part with a single-degree-of-
freedom system, followed by a more complex multi-degree-of-freedom system. The number
of degrees-of-freedom for analysis is the number of independent displacements that are
necessary to deﬁne the position of all masses relative to their original location [10]. Fi-
nally four methods which can be used for solving the diﬀerential equation of motion are
presented.
Two approaches given by the Eurocode [9] for evaluating the seismic action are described
here. The ﬁrst one is based on applying the response spectrum based on the modal response
and location of the building. The second approach is using a time-history analysis based
on a recorded or artiﬁcial accelerogram. These are also introduced in the validation of the
frame used in the seismic design benchmark.

2.3.4
Time history analysis
Another more accurate method which is suitable for representing the seismic motion for
both elastic and inelastic materials is in terms of ground acceleration time-histories. The
accelerations are given as accelerograms which may be of two types, artiﬁcial and recorded
or simulated accelerograms.
According to Eurocode 8 [9], a minimum number of 3 accelerograms must be used to assess
the seismic response based on time-history analysis. If recorded accelerograms are selected,
it must be assured that they match the seismic properties and the soil characteristics.
Moreover, it is necessary to perform a scaling of the acceleration to the value of ag · S,
meaning the product between the peak ground acceleration and a factor regarding the soil
type.

Chapter 3
Basis of Parametric Study
The main study is focused on comparing the response of two structures which have been
designed to resist seismic action.
One ﬁve-story building is taken from literature and
adapted to this investigation, while a second building is designed with the same longitudinal
and transversal dimensions and number of spans having a diﬀerent number of ﬂoors. This
Chapter describes the parameters used in the Finite element model and the initial design
of the buildings. In the ﬁnal part, the results of the modal analysis are reported for both
structures.
3.1
Finite element model
Two ﬁnite element software have been used in the design and investigation of the buildings.
Initially, the frames were modeled in SAP2000 where a design spectrum was deﬁned. A
two-dimensional analysis is performed while the structure is subjected to dead load, live
load and the horizontal seismic motion.
One of the reasons that SAP was chosen for
this step is the auto-design function, which allows a user to easily perform a veriﬁcation
according to the Eurocode.
After concluding that the buildings are properly designed, the same 2D frames are modelled
in Abaqus using wire elements. As shown in most studies [47], [16], [27], this simpliﬁcation
of the problem by analyzing only a two-dimensional frame seems to be suﬃcient. Further-
more, the computational eﬀort is greatly reduced rather than using a 3D model of the
entire structure.
The main structural elements used are Euler-Bernoulli beams, which do not permit trans-
verse shear deformations and plane section initially normal to the beams axis remain plane
and normal after deformation. Further reasoning on this choice can be found in Section
41

C.1.4. All members are deﬁned as B23 in Abaqus and the analysis used is the cubic inter-
polation function, which is more appropriate for distributed loads along the beam length.
By default any element has 3 integration points along its length, each point consisting of
5 section points where the stresses are calculated. A calibration analysis was performed
which is presented in Section C.1.4 resulting in a required number of 11 section points to
represent the plastic capacity of the beam. Additionally the number of integration points
is increased by meshing the element as 3 are not suﬃcient.
As shown in the calibration analysis a number of at least 20 elements are necessary to have
a proper representation of the non-linear part of the stress-strain curve. The meshing of
each element is presented in this following Section.
Initially the model consisted of a more detailed mesh, consisting of smaller elements located
near the support of the columns and the beams. This was required to have a realistic
representation of the plastic hinges which will form. The beams were divided into 20 equal
elements out of which the ﬁrst and last three are further divided into two each. Concerning
columns, 15 elements were used and the ends were further reﬁned similar to the beams.
The ﬁrst 4 segments were divided at each end of the members.
The length on which this member-end reﬁnement is performed is chosen assuming that
the extension of yielding on the beam will have a length approximately equal to the cross-
section height [32]. This reﬁned section is extended up to two times the cross-section height
for accuracy.
However, for the ﬁre analysis did detailed reﬁnement proved to be problematic, resulting
in convergence issues. Thus, a simpliﬁed version of the models was created, where the
reﬁnement of all members consisted on 20 elements.
This in is accordance to what is
speciﬁed previously, as the element length is less than the height of the smallest cross-
section, therefore the plastic hinge length can be approximated correctly. No signiﬁcant
diﬀerences could be seen in the two models for the seismic analysis.

Chapter 5
Fire Performance of Structures
This chapter presents the response of the previously described steel frames to increasing
temperature. Both structures are subjected to the same temperature variation, in case of
ﬁre acting directly and ﬁre following earthquake. The selection of earthquakes investigated
remains the same as in Chapter 4.
Initially, multiple ﬁre scenarios are deﬁned for the structures, selected on diﬀerent criteria
such as highest total strain sustained during the seismic motion, highest utilization ratio
in terms of load bearing capacity, level of constraint at the ends of the element and risk
of inﬂuencing adjacent structural members in case of local failure. It was observed that a
number of two-to-three ﬂoors are the most critical, therefore the diﬀerent scenarios were
placed at the respective levels. Two ﬁre following earthquake scenarios which are considered
as more relevant are presented in detail for each of the steel frames, respectively the collapse
mechanism is described.
A ﬁrst aim of this Chapter is to draw a conclusion on the eﬀect of damage sustained during
earthquake on the ﬁre resistance. Therefore, an initial comparison is shown for the time
until collapse in case of direct acting ﬁre and ﬁre following earthquake.
The second objective of the parametric study is to assess any particularities of the collapse
modes, depending on the diﬀerent total height of the frames. Similar scenarios applied
for the two frames are compared and diﬀerent patterns are identiﬁed and discussed in all
cases.

Conclusion and discussions
6.1
Summary
The main purpose of this Thesis was to compare similar scenarios involving direct ﬁre
action and post-earthquake ﬁre and evaluate diﬀerent collapse mechanisms and time until
failure with respect to diﬀerent building heights. A total number of approximately 300
analyses have been performed, including the benchmarks, validation and calibration of
diﬀerent parameters. The ﬁrst point of interest was to validate the results with literature
conclusions on research performed on similar structures and further investigate if in fact
PEF scenarios do indeed pose a threat to steel MRF buildings.
A second problem which was researched is concerning the response at diﬀerent heights of the
same structure. Therefore, two frames have been designed, one ﬁve-story and one ten-story
frame. Standard Eurocode design principles have been applied to obtain buildings which
comply with up-to-date norms. The ﬁre has been modeled using the ISO834 ﬁre curve,
The collapse mechanism of several scenarios have been compared and further discussed
based on the results.
Initial expectations were that a signiﬁcant reduction would occur in the ﬁre following
earthquake scenario time until collapse. This is mainly due to the P −δ eﬀect which can
be noticed as permanent inter-story drifts observed in the frame, as well as the plastic
hinges formed in the beams resulting in the redistribution of stresses. In fact this was not
necessarily the case, as for several scenarios a small increase in the time until collapse was
observed. The reduction of the bending moment in the hinge lead to smaller ﬁnal bending
moments being transmitted to the columns, increasing slightly the time for some PEF
scenarios.
Global collapse was always assumed to occur only after the columns have failed, as a result
111

112
either increase or decrease of the time until collapse was observed.
The frame is designed according to the strong-column weak beams concept meaning that:
plastic hinges form in the beams ends or at the base of the ground columns. As expected,
this lead to a decrease of the time until local failure, resulting in the beams collapsing earlier
in all post-earthquake ﬁres scenarios. In reality, global collapse might be directly caused
by this failure of the beams, as it was explained in the ﬁre analysis. Detaching of bolted
connections might result in the beam, respectively the slab above it to fall down and result
in a progressive pancake collapse. Therefore the initial expectations might be conﬁrmed.
If the structure has a very low redundancy, it may not be suﬃcient to redistribute the
stresses generated by the removal of the beam resulting in collapse. But this is rather
an uncertainty, greatly depending on the type of connection, dimensions of members and
other variables.
Even so, in one scenario of Frame B (scenario S1F) when the entire ground ﬂoor is on
ﬁre, failure of the columns is noticed to occur before the beams. That is due to the high
load-to-resistance ratio given by the storeys above. Another reason is that in some cases
for tall steel MRF, the cross-section of the beams must be increased to meet the inter-
storey drift limitation. This also inﬂuences the connection in case of welded joints, as larger
beams implicitly require larger welds which are more prone to fracture than small welds
[25].
Other important aspects are observed in the frames regarding the progression of collapse
for diﬀerent assumed scenarios. When entire upper stories are subjected to ﬁre action, by
comparing the second scenarios for each building detailed in Chapter 5, diﬀerent mecha-
nisms develop in the frames. For frame A (scenario S4F), the building tends to collapse
in on itself. When comparing with an equivalent scenario (S8F) from frame B, the latter
undergoes sway collapse due to the increased P −δ eﬀects as the upper stories displace
laterally. This poses a threat in particular to nearby constructions, as it can provoke a
great amount of damage if located for example in a very crowded area. This behavior has
been conﬁrmed also on other levels of the tall frame (scenario S4F), where the entire area
is on ﬁre.
Regarding PEF and DF scenarios’ progression of collapse, no diﬀerences can be seen for
the beam (except the time until failure) as it follows the same pattern: beams fail due to
the 3 perfect plastic hinges formation, followed by the columns. For some cases the failure
of the column diﬀers as it either fails due to pure compression or due to the formation
of 3 perfect plastic hinges. This is result of the stress redistribution taking place in the
frame.
The results are in accordance to the conclusions of other studies performed in similar con-
ditions by Della Corte et al.[12], Pintea and Zaharia [47] and Jel´ınek [21], as all concluded
that if frames are properly designed according to serviceability limit state, no signiﬁcant

Conclusions
Moment resisting steel frames, designed correctly to ultimate limit state and serviceability
limit state in particular meaning both to strength and deﬂection conditions posses suﬃcient
ﬁre resistance even after being damaged by an earthquake. This is valid only for uninsulated
structures and as long as the frame is designed to an earthquake of similar intensity. It
should be noted that this conclusion can be extended only to MRF frames, as the columns
are the main element resisting lateral action, resulting in an additional resistance in terms
of vertical loads. In case of braced frames, the braces resist lateral loading therefore a
higher load-to-resistance ratio is achieved in the columns.
No signiﬁcant diﬀerences were obtained for the time reduction until collapse, as the maxi-
mum decrease for frame A was of 17.36% in case of local failure, respectively 3% for global
collapse. In case of the tall building, a decrease of 14.6% is noted for local failure, while
a variation of 2.2% was observed for time until global collapse. This concludes that the
residual drifts and plastic regions in the frame do aﬀect the time until failure in terms of
strength. However, if a structure is designed to resist a certain amount of time by applying
==PAGE:130==

114
insulating material, the same deformations and displacements could lead to damage in the
insulation, which would result in a diﬀerent outcome. A similar study was performed by
Wrobel [43] on the same 2-dimensional 5-story frame, on which the loss of insulation was
simulated at diﬀerent percentages where large deformations are present. It was concluded
that in case of insulated materials, the reduction is indeed signiﬁcant, the frame collapsing
earlier.
Introducing catenary eﬀect in design permits the beams to resist loads even after exceeding
serviceability limit states criteria. Although this may seem favorable to apply in design, it
is in fact rather unsafe, as large forces are transmitted to the adjacent structure which can
easily be underestimated. Implementing this concept in normal design condition would
require a very good understanding of the process.
Based on the diﬀerent heights of the structures, it was observed that frames taller than
5 stories height pose a greater risk of undergoing sway collapse and damaging nearby
buildings. This occurred in all cases where entire upper stories were aﬀected by ﬁre, while
the 5-story frame always tended to collapsed in on itself. Therefore, the P −δ eﬀect did
in fact have a greater inﬂuence on the tall building.
Collapse of taller buildings where ﬁre is located at the ground ﬂoor may be represented by
brittle failure. As it was previously explained, there is a possibility that the beams do not
fail when the entire ﬂoor is on ﬁre, leading to sudden failure of the columns without any
warning, resulting in global collapse of the structure.
6.3
Further studies
Several aspects which could extended this study are brieﬂy discussed in this Section. Ad-
justments which could increase the accuracy of the results are proposed, as well as allow
additional collapse mechanisms to be observed.
 Application of insulating materials. A rather important aspect which was omitted
in this design is the presence of insulation on the members. The investigation was
based solely on observing the collapse mechanism when frames of diﬀerent heights
are analyzed, therefore the presence of insulation was disregarded. If for un-insulated
frames the diﬀerences were not signiﬁcant, the loss of a certain percent of insulation
due to inter-storey drifts would result in larger diﬀerences.
 Using a 3D model. The 2D simpliﬁed version was suﬃcient to observe most collapse
mechanisms, but in general design the action of the earthquake is assumed to act in
both direction. At least 30% of the earthquake on the second axis should be accounted
for, as acting on the frame at the same time. This could result in a diﬀerent response
of the structure, for example the frame could be subjected to torsional eﬀects.
==PAGE:131==
115
6.3. Further studies
 Material design. The current material model was applied in order to observe the
collapse after members have exceeded bearing capacity. By deﬁning a decay phase,
this could not be achieved, but a rather more realistic situation would be simulated
at individual element level.
 Vertical ﬁre spread. A scenario was applied in an attempt to simulate the loss of one
beam, leading to vertical ﬂame spread, the ﬁre aﬀecting two diﬀerent levels. However,
this is more diﬃcult to represent, as the possible detachment of the beam could not
be included within the model during the analysis.
 Steel braced frames. As it was concluded that MRF structures have a signiﬁcant
over-strength in the columns due to the lateral loads, it would be interesting to
investigate a braced frame structure. A larger utilization ratio is achieved in the
columns which does have an inﬂuence on the time until collapse.
 Multiple frame heights. This parametric study consisted of only two framed struc-
tures of 5 and 10 stories due to limited time. The study could be extended by looking
at the collapse of multiple buildings of diﬀerent heights such as 20,30, 40 stories high.
This would allow to better classify the collapse mode to diﬀerent height intervals.

In a dynamic analysis in SAP2000, the mass is by default lumped at the joints, whereas
the model used in the investigation performed in Abaqus has consistent mass matrix for-
mulation. Having the mass distributed over the entire length of the element requires a
higher computational eﬀort but gives more accurate results.
An Euler-Bernoulli element type is chosen for the frame designed in Abaqus, which does
not consider shear deformations. By default, SAP2000 does not have an option to chose
the element type, accounting for shear deformations. However, a diﬀerent theory is applied
which is neither a Timoshenko beam or Euler-Bernoulli, but it is in fact a combination of
the two, suitable for both short and long elements.

C.1.4
Sensitivity Analysis
The purpose of the sensitivity analysis is to reﬁne the results and reduce the level of
uncertainty in the model. It consists of re-adjusting the initial settings to determine the
impact of a variable and reach more accurate results.
Element type
The variation of diﬀerent parameters will inﬂuence the outcome of the analysis. In order
to obtain a solution of the desired level of detail, diﬀerent parameters are changed at a
time and the results are compared.
The ﬁrst criterion investigated is the element type which inﬂuences the mesh and integra-
tion. The diﬀerent standard types available in the Abaqus library are as follows:
 Euler-Bernoulli (slender) beams in a plane and in space
 Timoshenko (shear ﬂexible) beams in a plane and in space
 Linear, quadratic and cubic interpolation formulas
 Warping (open section) beams
 Pipe elements
 Hybrid formulation beams, used for very stiﬀbeams that rotate signiﬁcantly
The common naming convention used in Abaqus is presented in Figure C.3.
==PAGE:162==
Appendix C. Benchmarks
146
Figure C.3: Name convention of beam elements in Abaqus [36]
The element chosen for this validation is the Euler-Bernoulli beam in plane (B23). These
elements do not permit transverse shear deformations while plane sections initially normal
to the beams axis remain plane and normal to the axis (given that there is no warping).
For beams with uniform material, the cross-section dimensions should be less that 1/15 of
the typical axial distance for shear ﬂexibility to be negligible. The analysis used for the
Euler-Bernoulli beam is the cubic interpolation function, which is suitable for cases with
distributed loading along the beam.
The main diﬀerences in the Timoshenko beams are that a transverse shear strain is assumed
constant along the cross-section and the plane sections remain plane but are no longer
normal to the longitudinal axis after deformation. Therefore, Euler-Bernoulli beams are
very good at analyzing normal stress being better for bending dominated deformations
(thin, slender beams). On the other hand, the Timoshenko beams are good for describing
shear deformations e.g. thick beams.

failure mode.

C.2.3
Summary
Diﬀerent support conditions of the beams have been investigated when subjected to thermal
expansion initially. Afterwards, step-by-step an uniformly distributed load was applied
and eventually non-linear geometries have been accounted for. It was observed in the ﬁnal
case, that the tensile forces developing in the beams extend the time until load bearing is
exhausted by aﬀecting the bending moment. The capacity in this case is not depending on
the resistance, but rather on the amount of deﬂection that the beam undergoes.
In individual elements this seems like a favorable eﬀect, helping the beam resist longer. On
the other hand, structural members are never completely isolated, transmitting the stresses
to the adjacent members or structure. Even though it may seem to be beneﬁcial, improper
design might achieve exactly the opposite, resulting in failure of the structure.
