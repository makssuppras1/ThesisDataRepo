Abstract: 
 
Vibrio natriegens is the fastest growing non-pathogenic organism known to date, boasting a 
growth rate of less than ten minutes. Thanks to its speed V. natriegens has attracted 
increasingly more attention from the research and engineering community. As our 
knowledge of this fascinating bacteria expand, however, it is becoming increasingly clear 
that V. natriegens is not only a quick growing bacterium, but it has the potential to become 
a true biotechnological powerhouse. In facts, V. natriegens is genetically tractable and can 
be therefore engineered fit our necessities. Moreover, it has a remarkably flexible 
metabolism and is able to grow on a range of industrially relevant substrates. V. natriegens 
is a halophile and therefore proliferates in saline water, which could prove valuable as 
seawater based industrial fermentation is gaining traction. However, V. natriegens great 
potential is held back by its sparce engineering toolbox. A particularly desirable component 
would be a quick, user-friendly, and reliable genomic engineering system. In facts, genomic 
engineering is particularly valuable for industrially relevant organism. For large scale 
productions chromosomal expression is preferred to plasmid-based expression, as it is more 
consistent and does not require continuous selective pressure, often in the form of 
antibiotic, to maintain stability. Such a system of genomic engineering is already available in 
Escherichia coli, it is called Standardized Genomic Architecture (SEGA). Starting from only 
two reagents, a stock of SEGA cells and a linear DNA fragment, SEGA allows for quick and 
easy genomic integration resulting in the construction of a new strain expressing a gene of 
interest in just a couple of day. We decided to extend the philosophy of SEGA to 
V. natriegens and developed a project with a two-fold aim: on one side the expansion of 
V. natriegens limited molecular biology toolbox by characterization of new parts; on the 
other the development of a SEGA-like transfer system to quickly move constructs from SEGA 
strains into the chromosome of V. natriegens. We were able to introduce a new set of 
chromogenic proteins for V. natriegens that nicely complete the available set of mostly 
fluorescent proteins. Moreover, using flowcytometry we characterize the dynamics of four 
inducible promoter in V. natriegens, two of which are completely novel in V. natriegens. 
However, we were not able to develop the SEGA-like transfer system. This failure was not 
caused by any fatal flaw in the design of the system, but by the limited time available for the 
project. We therefore still believe that the development of the transfer system is both 
feasible and desirable.

1 Introduction: 
 
1.1 Vibrio natriegens biology and ecology: 
 
Vibrio natriegens is the fastest growing free-living bacterium known to date, boasting an 
astonishing doubling rate of less than 10 minutes1.  It is a rod-shaped gram-negative 
bacterium with a single polar flagellum2. V. natriegens is almost ubiquitous to the world’s 
marine environments, as it has been detected in a variety of habitats such as North 
American salt marshe1s, the Yangtze River estuary3, the Spanish Atlantic4 coast, and the Bay 
of Bengal5. As a halophile V. natriegens requires Na+ to support growth, but it maintains 
metabolic activity even in the absence of Na+ ions6,7. While it is tolerant to a wide range of 
temperatures and PH, it grows best at 37 C and pH 7.5. It belongs to the Vibrio genus, but 
unlike the more famous members of the genus such as Vibrio cholera, V. natriegens is not 
pathogenic. In facts, its genome does not contain genes homologous to known toxins or 
pathogenicity islands of other Vibrio species, and it is immune to the CTXφ bacteriophage 
that carries the cholera toxin genes8. To the best of our knowledge, only a single case of 
pathogenicity to the crab Portunus trituberculatus has been reported, but the V. natriegens 
strain responsible has not been characterised3. Due to the lack of human pathogenicity, 
V. natriegens is classified as biosafety level 1 bacteria.  
V. natriegens has a bipartite genome divided in two circular chromosomes: chromosome 1 is 
3.24 Mb long while chromosome 2 is 1.92 Mb8. Both chromosomes have an origin of 
replication and replicate simultaneously9, effectively doubling the DNA replication rate of 
V. natriegens. Since DNA replication rate is the limiting factor determining generation times 
in many bacteria, it has been hypothesised that the bipartite genome allows for 
V. natriegens extraordinary growth rate. However, Ramming et al.10 recently fused the 
V. natriegens genome into a single chromosome with a single origin of replication and 
observed no significant difference in growth rate between this strain and the wild type 
(WT). While it is unclear what evolutionary advantage the bipartition of the genome brings, 
analysing the distribution of genes across the chromosomes could shed some light. In facts, 
almost all the essential genes (272 out of 278) are located on chromosome 1, as are 96% of 
core genes11. Here, core genes are defined as genes whose knockdown by CRISPR 
interference led to a reduced growth rate in rich media. Many core genes are duplicated, 
with a growth supporting copy on chromosome 1 and a duplicate on chromosome 2. 
Transcriptional profiling showed, that during growth on rich media, the duplicates are often 
silenced, as they are transcribed at a 17 times lower rate than their growth supporting 
counterparts on chromosome 111. Chromosome 2 is potentially used as an “evolutionary 
test bed” for rarely used genes, similarly to what has been observed in other Vibrio 
species12.

The V. natriegens genome contains 11 rRNA genes which are located near the origin of 
replication11 13and regulated by strong promoters14. Consequently, V. natriegens is 
estimated to contain around 115 000 ribosomes per cell14. To match its impressive ribosome 
count, V. natriegens possesses an equally remarkable 129 tRNA genes15, likely allowing for 
high rates of translation. For comparison, Escherichia coli MG1655 has 7 rRNA gene, 
resulting in an estimated 70 000 ribosomes per cell, and 87 tRNA genes16. Protein synthesis 
is energetically expensive, therefore, maintaining a high translational rate requires high 
energy production. In facts, V. natriegens carbon uptake rate is more than double that of 
E. coli17 and, despite similarities in the relative carbon fluxes of the two bacteria, the 
absolute fluxes through the core metabolism of V. natriegens are significantly higher than 
those of E. coli18. V. natriegens ability to efficiently acquire substrate, coupled with its high 
metabolic rates and abundant translational machinery, could explain how it is able to 
sustain such an impressive growth rate. These characteristics also make V. natriegens a 
promising platform for protein production.  
 
1.2 A timeline of Vibrio natriegens research: 
 
V. natriegens was first isolated in 1958 from the mud of Sapelo Island salt marshes by 
William Payne, as part of a survey on uronic acid utilisation by bacteria1.  V. natriegens 
immediately gathered attention as Payne uncovered its exceptional growth rate6.  However, 
interest quickly died down and research in these early years was limited and focused on 
V. natriegens ecological role and placement on the phylogenetic tree. V. natriegens was 
initially called M11, and in 1961, it was identified as a member of the Pseudomonas genus 
and give the name Pseudomonas natriegens19.  In 1971 was reclassified as Beneckea 
natriegens, only to be officially moved to the Vibrio genus and acquiring its current name in 
19782,20. In the 1970s it was manly utilised for high school and undergraduate class 
demonstrations as its quick growth rate and short lag phase allowed for faster 
experiments21. In the 1980s V. natriegens saw its first biotechnological application as it was 
used to quickly estimate ticarcillin concentrations in human blood22. Ticarcillin is an 
antibiotic used to treat Pseudomonas aeruginosa infections, however at high concentration 
it is toxic to humans. V. natriegens liquid culture were spread on soy agar plates on which 
drops of patient’s blood were subsequently placed. Following a four-hour incubation, the 
clearing zone were measured to estimate tiracillin concentration.  
Further interest and research in V. natriegens remained limited until the 2010s (Figure 1B). 
In 2013, two draft genomes of V. natriegens were published by Maida et al.23 and Wang et 
al24. In 2016, V. natriegens experienced a great surge in interest as two simultaneous papers 
promoted V. natriegens as the next “genomic powerhouse”8,25. These papers included the 
first complete genome, the first transformation protocols, and the first engineered strain for 
increased transformation efficiency (the now widely used Δdns strain). Following these 
articles, developments of tools for V. natriegens picked up steam and 2017 saw the 
development of the multiplex genome editing system by natural selection (MuGENT)26, of a 
λ-like recombineering for small edits and deletions27, and of an alanine producing strain as a

first showcase of V. natriegens industrial potential17. In 2019, a commercial V. natriegens 
strain for improved protein production (Vmax) was launched by Synthetic Genomics Inc. 2019 
also saw the first published use of CRISPR interference in V. natriegens15. In 2021 an 
extensive Golden Gate part collection for V. natriegens was published28 and, in 2022, 
Stukenberg et al.29 developed an improved MuGENT system by adding CRISPR 
counterselection to the protocol. Stukenberg’s new protocol, dubbed the NT-CRISPR, is also 
the first successful use of CRISPR-Cas9 in V. natriegens. Previous attempts had failed due to 
Cas9 toxicity even in the absence of the target sequence. In facts, the NT-CRISPR system 
includes an anti-Cas9 protein to tightly regulate Cas9 activity.

1.3 V. natriegens industrial potential: 
 
Sustainable production of chemicals by microbial fermentation has become a promising 
alternative to overcome environmental issues caused by our heavy dependence on fossil 
resources. However, it is estimated that only 200 of the tens of thousands of commercially 
available chemicals are produced through fermentation, as many bioprocesses struggle to 
compete economically with their petrochemical rivals. The economic feasibility of bacterial 
fermentation productions is determined by three key performance metrics: volumetric 
productivity, titer, and yield. Moreover, the environmental sustainability of microbial 
fermentation is partially limited by its reliance on fresh water and edible sugars, which puts 
it in direct competition with food production. 
Volumetric productivity is the rate at which the product is formed per unit volume over a 
given time. It directly impacts capital and operational costs. In facts, an increase in 
volumetric productivity means that the same fermentation facilities and equipment can 
produce more, allowing for increased output without additional capital investment. 
Moreover, improved productivity shortens fermentation cycles, reducing the labour and 
energy necessary and, consequently, operational costs. The main factors affecting 
volumetric productivity are cell density, growth rate, and substrate uptake rate.17 Cell 
density and growth rate determine the amount of available biomass that can synthesise the 
product, while substrate uptake rate determines the amount of carbon that can be 
funnelled into the product. V. natriegens’ growth rate outpaces all other bacteria; when 
using glucose as sole carbon source, V. natriegens can reach higher cell densities than E. coli 
and a substrate uptake rate of 3.9 gglucose gbiomass−1 h−1, which is at least double the substrate 
uptake rate of other industrial strains.30 Due to these characteristics, V. natriegens has often 
been described as an industrial host with great potential for high productivity17,18. 
Titer refers to the quantity of the desired product per unit volume of fermentation broth at 
the end of the fermentation. Titer directly affects the cost of the downstream processing as 
working at higher product concentrations reduces the water that needs to be removed. 
Utilising smaller volumes in the downstream processes allow for reduction of the physical 
dimensions of the single units, making them cheaper. Moreover, most purification units rely 
on either osmosis or chemical affinity to separate the desired product from the 
fermentation broth and, therefore, are more efficient when the product is highly 
concentrated. In bacterial fermentation, titer is often limited by toxicity of the concentrated 
product to the bacteria. Since tolerance to the product is improved by adaptive evolution of 
the producing strain, V. natriegens has a chance to shine. In facts, its extraordinary growth 
rate allows V. natriegens to evolve faster, effectively reducing the time and cost of all 
evolutionary approaches to strain improvement. The effectiveness of this approach has 
been recently showcased by Anand et al., who developed a V. natriegens strain with 
improved tolerance to reactive oxygen species.31

Yield measures the mass of product obtained per mass of substrate consumed and directly 
affect operational costs as it determines the amount of substrate necessary to support the 
fermentation. The greatest limiting factor to yield is the synthesis of by-products that 
represent a drain on the available carbon. Since V. natriegens and E. coli have similar 
relative carbon fluxes18, they are likely to experience similar yields losses from synthesis of 
unwanted by-products. It is likely that V. natriegens fermentation will suffer slightly lower 
yield compared to E. coli-based productions, as there is a trade-off between biomass yield 
and product yield. 
The cost of feedstock, however, it is not only influenced by yield but also by the carbon 
source utilised. V. natriegens possesses exceptional nutritional flexibility, as it can utilise 
more than 60 different substrates as sole carbon source32. The most industrially relevant are 
sucrose and glycerol. Sucrose is an economically and environmentally advantageous 
feedstock that, while most E. coli strains cannot utilise it,32 supports even higher growth rate 
of V. natriegens than glucose33,34. Glycerol is a major by-product of the biodiesel industry; 
therefore, it is extremely cheap and not in competition with food production35,36. Moreover, 
Lim et al. 37isolated a V. natriegens strain able to metabolise alginate and support 
2,3-butandiol production from brown macroalgae feedstock. Brown macroalgae are an 
abundant, fast growing and cheap source of biomass that does not require arable land to be 
produced38,39. However, their use as fermentation feedstock is limited as most industrially 
relevant microorganism, including E. coli, are unable to catabolise the alginate contained in 
brown algae40.  
V. natriegens halophilic nature has important implications on its industrial potential. Its salt 
tolerance could allow for utilisation of marine water rather than fresh water for 
fermentation. As fresh water is an increasingly limited resource, using sea water would be 
both environmentally and economically advantageous41. Since saline water is also less prone 
to contamination, its use would reduce the need and cost of sterilisation42. Production of 
2,3-butanediol by V. natriegens in non-sterilised sea water has recently been reported43. 
However, fermentation vessels and equipment are commonly made of steel, which is 
susceptible to corrosion in the presence of salt.  This problem is further exacerbated by 
V. natriegens ability to promote copper corrosion under anaerobic conditions,44 as copper 
plating is often used to protect steel from salt. Newer corrosion resistant bioreactor designs 
potentially offer a solution to sea water induced corrosion36,45. Alternatively, the reliance of 
V. natriegens on NaCl salt could be reduced to allow growth in less corrosive fermentation 
broth. For example, Hoffart et al. designed a new fermentation media with sufficient Na+ ion 
to sustain V. natriegens growth but with only traces of Cl- ion that are responsible for steel 
corrosion. Pfeifer et al46. engineered a prophage free V. natriegens strain which showed 
reduced reliance on Na+ and outperformed the wildtype in competitive growth experiments. 
The use of less corrosive media is surely advantageous in the short term. Considering that 
multiple halophilic organism such as Debaryomyces hansenii47, Dunaliella salina48, and even 
a salt tolerant Saccharomyces cerevisiae41 are being put forward as future platform for 
industrial production, the halophilic nature of V. natriegens could prove a great asset in the 
long term as more equipment for large scale sea-water fermentation is developed.

Multiple studies have confirmed the industrial potential of V. natriegens by successfully 
engineering it to produce value-added chemicals that exhibit comparable key performance 
metrics to well-established industrial hosts. With only four deletions, Hoffart et al17. were 
able to engineer V. natriegens into an effective alanine producer, they obtained a slightly 
lower yield but an approximately 10-fold increase in volumetric productivity compared to 
specialised E. coli and Corynebacterium glutamicum 49production strains (Table 1). Similarly, 
Wang et al. engineered a V. natriegens strain into an effective melanin producer which 
outperformed other expression systems but not chemical synthesis. V. natriegens was used 
for bioremediation of selenite and production of selenium nanoparticle, which have 
multiple industrial and medical applications. Here V. natriegens even outperformed the 
model microorganisms for selenium nanoparticle production, becoming the most effective 
producer of selenium nanoparticles known to date50. Multiple studies showed V. natriegens 
potential as a 2,3-butandiol production platform. In facts, Eirin et al. obtained high 
volumetric productivity and a yield of 70 % the maximum theoretical yield. While Lim et al37. 
and Meng et a43l. were able to produce 2,3-butandiol using brown algae feedstock and sea 
water fermentation respectively, showcasing the great versatility of V. natriegens.

1.4 V. natriegens engineering toolbox: 
 
The V. natriegens engineering toolbox is still in its early stages of development. Remarkably, 
the first transformation protocol, a fundamental step in genetic engineering, was published 
just seven years ago. Currently, there are three plasmid transformation methods available 
for V. natriegens: electroporation, heat shock, and conjugation. Electroporation is the most 
efficient method, with transformation efficiencies ranging from 2 x 105 to 107 CFU/µgDNA 
depending on the protocol. Heat shock is slightly less efficient (105 – 106 CFU/µgDNA) but 
more convenient. Conjugation is cumbersome, slow, and relies on a supporting E. coli strain. 
While it is the least convenient method, conjugation is useful for plasmids whose 
transformation through electroporation and heat shock is challenging.  
The first genomic engineering protocol involves conjugation of an allelic exchange vector to 
delete, replace, or insert large DNA fragments (~6 kb) into V. natriegens' genome. The Cre-
loxP system is then used to remove and recycle the antibiotic marker. Alternatively, a

Multiple large fragments can be integrated simultaneously using the MuGENT system, which 
leverages the expression of V. cholera's TfxO gene to enhance natural competency of 
V. natriegens. Competent V. natriegens cells spontaneously uptake linear DNA fragments 
and integrate them into their chromosomes by homologous recombination. While 
convenient, MuGENT relies on unwieldly large homology arms (HA) (~3kb each). MuGENT 
efficiency has been recently improved through the addition of a CRISPR counterselection 
step to create the NT-CRISPR protocol. Additionally, CRISPR interference has been shown to 
work in V. natriegens.  
Golden Gate and Gibson assembly can for plasmid assembly directly in V. natriegens 
without relying on additional E. coli strains. A growing collection of characterised parts is 
available for V. natriegens. Multiple promoters from the Anderson collection, the 
endogenous rrnAp1 promoter, and the T7 system can be used for high constitutive 
expression. Moreover, several inducible promoters have also been demonstrated to 
function well in V. natriegens (Table 2). However, three of them rely on the same inducer, 
Isopropyl β-d-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG), and only two other chemical inducers are 
available, anhydrotetracycline (aTC) and arabinose. Multiple antibiotic resistance markers, 
such as ampicillin, kanamycin, and chloramphenicol, are functional in V. natriegens. Two 
counterselection markers, CcdB and SacB, have been reported. CcdB is part of a toxin-
antitoxin system, and its expression leads to cell death unless CcdA is co-expressed. SacB 
confers sensitivity to sucrose toxicity, but its effectiveness is limited in the presence of Na+ 
ions. Considering V. natriegens' efficient sucrose catabolism and high Na+ media 
requirement, SacB is not an ideal counterselection marker for this organism.

Multiple V. natriegens strains are available, with the first strain isolated, ATCC14048, still

1.5 SEGA, a quick and simple system for genomic integration: 
 
All fields of engineering greatly benefit from standardisation, as standardised systems often 
are more predictable, more reliable, and more accessible than ad hoc systems. 
Counterintuitively, standardisation also promotes innovation by codifying accumulated 
knowledge and defining a baseline from which new ideas can emerge. Moreover, 
standardised systems are more easily automated, allowing for higher throughput. The field 
of synthetic biology, however, has been slow to implement widespread standardisations. In 
facts, calls for implementation of standards have been commonplace for decades, but most 
standards are still in the innovator or early adopter phase. Most synthetic biology standards 
only focus on plasmid architecture. However, plasmid expression systems have multiple 
inherit limitations: plasmids have a limited capacity to carry DNA inserts due to their size 
constraints; they exhibit variability in their copy number within a host cell population, 
leading to heterogenous expression across the population; they are a metabolic burden as 
their replication require specialised machinery; they are intrinsically unstable, therefore 
require continuous selective pressure to prevent loss of expression over time. In contrast, 
chromosomal expression is more consistent and does not require the antibiotic markers to 
maintain stability. Use of antibiotic in large scale cultivations is both prohibitively expensive 
and dangerous, as it promotes the spread of antibiotic resistance. However, chromosomal 
integration techniques are often cumbersome, require numerous multiple plasmids and lack 
standardisation. To simplify recombinant genome technology and facilitate the transition 
from plasmids-based system to recombinant genomes, Bayer et al. developed the 
Standardized Genome Architecture (SEGA) for E. coli. SEGA allows quick and easy genome 
engineering using only a linear DNA fragment and an aliquot of competent SEGA cells.  SEGA 
cells carry λ-red machinery to allow for recombineering of the DNA fragment, and a landing 
pad containing the following functional subunits (Figure 2A): a visible reporter (Green 
Fluorescent Protein (GFP)) to allow for visual screening after integration; two control 
elements, a promoter (C1) and a translation initiation region (TIR) (C2), that regulate 
expression of the reporter at the transcriptional and translational level respectively; 
selection and counterselection markers; a “middle gadget” consisting of either a bicistronic 
design (BCD) that normalises expression levels, a functional tag, or a signal peptide. The 
SEGA integration protocol is elegantly simple (Figure 2B). First SEGA cells expressing λ-red 
recombineering machinery are electroporated with a linear DNA fragment. Depending on 
==PAGE:15==
 9 
 
the homology region the fragment and the landing pad, the fragment will be integrated in 
place of either the entire landing pad, the control element and the upstream 
counterselection marker, or the reporter and the downstream selection-counterselection 
marker (Figure 2C). Then the appropriate selective pressure is applied to the kill unmodified 
SEGA cells, finally the cells are plated, and visual screening is used to identify colonies with 
the desired modification.

1.6 SEGA collection: 
 
Bayer et al. have assembled and characterized a collection of strains featuring SEGA landing 
pads with various combinations of C1 and C2 control elements. These strains offer a wide 
range of expression levels and can be regulated using different inducers. Notably, the C1 
and C2 element combinations consistently maintain predictable activity when controlling 
the expression of different reporters, even in the presence of N-terminal tags or signal 
peptides. This feature allows for rapid and precise tuning of gene expression for a gene of 
interest (GOI). 
In practice, researchers can select a few SEGA strains with desired expression dynamics 
based on Bayer et al.'s characterization and employ the SEGA protocol to generate a new 
library of strains expressing the GOI. Subsequently, the expression of the GOI should be 
thoroughly characterized to identify the strain that best matches the desired expression 
dynamics. This streamlined approach leveraging the SEGA genome integration protocol 
accelerates the development of new strains, while the predictable expression offered by the 
SEGA library reduces the need for generating and characterizing a large number of strains 
for each new GOI. 
This SEGA strain collection serves as a valuable tool in biotechnology, and there is a strong 
desire to expand its application to other industrially relevant organisms, such as 
V. natriegens. Furthermore, the SEGA collection contains numerous parts that are not yet 
available in V. natriegens. The transfer of this collection into V. natriegens has the potential 
to accelerate the expansion of its emerging toolbox, enabling more efficient genetic 
engineering and biotechnological applications in this bacterium.

2 Project aims and engineering strategy: 
 
To build a SEGA-like system 4 functional tools are required: i) visible reporters for screening; 
ii) well characterised control elements to tune expression levels; iii) counter-selection 
markers; iv) a protocol for genome integration of a DNA fragment targeted through short 
homology regions.  
Multiple reporters have been successfully used in V. natriegens including GFP, the main 
reporter of SEGA. A small library of characterised promoters for V. natriegens is already 
available, however is limited to encompass only three chemical inducers. Therefore, its 
expansion is highly desirable. The V. natriegens toolbox also contains two counterselection 
markers: SacB and CcdB. As discussed in section 1.4, SacB effectiveness in V. natriegens is 
severely limited. CcdB is part of the CcdA-CcdB toxin-antitoxin system, which is mostly used 
for plasmid-based systems. CcdA-CcdB use as a genomic integrated marker in E. coli has 
been described, however it requires exquisite control over the relative expression of the 
two genes. The V. natriegens toolbox does not include a genomic integration protocol 
adequate for SEGA yet. In facts, Lee et al. recombineering only support small edits and 
indels, while the NT-CRISPR method requires large homology arms. Improving either system 
to match SEGA specifications and, subsequently, use it to build a SEGA-like system in 
V. natriegens is likely possible. Such a project, however, is outside the scope of a 5-month 
master thesis, particularly considering this is the first V. natriegens project carried out in the 
Norhølm lab and time will be necessary to set up basic material and methods.  
We defined a twofold aim for this project: i) the expansion of the current V. natriegens 
promoter library to include more chemically inducible promoters; ii) the development of a 
user-friendly protocol to transfer SEGA construct from E. coli into V. natriegens genome. 
This protocol will be heavily inspired by SEGA and apply SEGA’s philosophy to the available 
V. natriegens tools.  
Similarly to SEGA, our “transfer protocol” will rely on only two reagents: a linear DNA 
fragment and a stock solution of V. natriegens “transfer cells”. Transfer cells will carry a 
genomically integrated landing pad and a NT-CRISPR plasmid (Figure 3A). The landing pad 
will consist of a reporter gene flanked by two 3 kb homology arms that match the E. coli 
pstC locus, where the original SEGA landing pad is located. The NT-CRISPR plasmid, designed 
following Stukenberg et al. specifications, will allow genomic integration and CRISPR-Cas9 
counterselection targeting the V. natriegens landing pad. In the transfer protocol (Figure 
3B), a linear DNA fragment containing a SEGA construct flanked by pstC homology arms will 
be amplified from a SEGA strain and mixed with V. natriegens transfer cells. V. natriegens 
will uptake the DNA through Tfxo-induced natural competency and integrate it in place of 
the landing pad by homologous recombination. Then Cas9 will be induced to kill the cells 
that did not integrate the DNA fragment. Finally, cells will be plated, and reporter 
expression used for visual screening.

3.9 USER cloning: 
 
100 ng of each purified USER fragments was mixed alongside 1 µl of DpnI enzyme (NEB), 1 µl 
of CutSmart Buffer (NEB), 1 µl of USER enzyme (NEB), and nuclease-free water to 10 µl of 
total reaction. The reaction was incubated at 30 °C for 30’, then on a gradient starting from 
the melting temperature of the overhangs and decreasing by 1 °C every 5’ for a total of 30’, 
then at 10 °C for 30’. The reaction was chemically transformed into E. coli NEB5α. 
 
3.10 V. natriegens chemical transformation: 
 
This protocol was adapted from Stkenberg et al. 
 A preculture of V. natriegens grown overnight at 37 °C and 220 rpm. At the next day 50 ml 
of BHIv2 medium was inoculated with the overnight 1:100. This culture was grown at 200 
rpm at 37 °C at until an OD600 between 0.5 and 0.7 was reached. The culture was then 
transferred to pre-cooled 50 ml falcon tubes and incubated on ice for 10’, followed by 
centrifugation for 10’ at 3000 × g at 4 °C. The supernatant was discarded, and the pellets 
were resuspended in 20 ml cold TB buffer per 50 ml bacterial culture (TB buffer: 10 mM 
HEPESs, 15 mM CaCl2, 250 mM KCl, pH adjusted to 6.7 with KOH, then add 55 mM MnCl2, 
filter sterilized). The cells were again incubated on ice for 10’ and centrifuged for 10 min at 
3000 × g at 4 °C. The supernatant was removed and pellets were resuspended in 2.5 ml cold 
TB buffer per 50 ml starting culture and consolidated in a single falcon tube, before adding 
175 µL dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO). After another 10’ incubation on ice, 50 µl aliquots were 
prepared in 1.5 ml tubes stored at −70 °C until further use. 
Chemically competent V. natriegens were transformed by adding DNA to an aliquot of 
competent cells and incubated on ice for 30’. After 30’, cells were heat shocked in a heath 
block at 42 °C for 45’’ then incubated on ice for 10 min before recovery. The cells were 
recovered in 1 ml BHIv2 medium, shaking at 37 °C for 1 h at 700 rpm. After recovery, the 
cells were pelleted by centrifugation at 3000 × g for 1 min, the supernatant was decanted, 
and the pellet was resuspended in the remaining ~50 µl residual medium. The whole volume 
was plated on LBv2 plates containing the appropriate antibiotic and incubated overnight at 
30 °C. 
 
3.11 V. natriegens electroporation: 
 
This protocol was adapted from a combination of Lee et al. and Weinstock et al. 
A preculture of V. natriegens grown overnight at 37 °C and 220 rpm. At the next day 50 ml 
of BHIv2 medium was inoculated with the overnight 1:100. This culture was grown at 200 
rpm at 37 °C at until an OD600 between 0.4 and 0.6 was reached. The culture was then 
transferred to pre-cooled 50 ml falcon tubes and incubated on ice for 15’, followed by

centrifugation for 3’ at 3400 × g at 4 °C. The supernatant was discarded, and the pellets 
were resuspended in 20 ml cold VEB per 50 ml bacterial culture (VEB: 680 mM sucrose and 7 
mM K2HPO4, pH adjusted to 7 with NaOH 1 M filter sterilized). The cells were again 
incubated on ice for 15’and further centrifuged for 3’ at 3400 × g at 4 °C. This step was

Electrocompetent cells were transformed by adding 5o ng of DNA to an aliquot of cells. 
Transfer the mixture to a precooled cuvette (1 mm gap). Electroporation was carried out at 
400V, 25µF, 1000 Ω. The cells were recovered in 1 ml BHIv2 medium, shaking at 37 °C for 1 h 
at 700 rpm. 200 µl were plated on LBv2 plates containing the appropriate antibiotic and 
incubated overnight at 30 °C. 
 
3.12 Electroporation of E. coli ST18: 
 
50 mL of LB + 50 µg/ml ALA were inoculated with 500 µl of overnight pre-culture and grow 
to OD 0.5. The whole culture was spun down (4°C) at 3000 x g for 5’, resuspended in 1 mL 
cold water, and move to 1.5 ml Eppendorf. The cells were precipitated by centrifugation and 
resuspended in 1 ml of cold water twice more. Precipitated once more and resuspended in 
600 µl 15% glycerol.  The cells were divided in 50 ml aliquots. The aliquots were transformed 
by electroporation 18000V, 50 µF, 1000 Ω. Recovered for 1 hour in 1 ml SOC media + 50 
µg/ml ALA, at 37 °C at 700 rpm. 200 µl were plated on LB + 50 µg/ml ALA plates containing 
the appropriate antibiotic and incubated overnight at 37 °C. 
 
3.13 Conjugation of V. natriegens: 
 
This protocol was adapted from an unpublished protocol courtesy of Filippo Federici. 
E. coli donor strain ST18 was transformed via electroporation with the desired plasmid as 
described above. The donor ST18 strain carrying the plasmid of interest was grown in LB + 
50 μg/ml ALA and the recipient V. natriegens strain was grown in BHIv2. Both strains were 
cultivated until an OD600 = 0.4 – 0.6. A volume corresponding to 500 μL of OD600 = 0.5 was 
taken from each culture and transferred into one 1.5 ml Eppendorf tube, effectively mixing 
the two strains. The suspension was centrifuged for 3’ at 3400 x g. The supernatant was 
partially discarded, and the cell pellet was resuspended in the leftover liquid. The entire cell 
suspension was pipetted as a single drop onto a LB agar + 50 μg/ml ALA plate and left open 
to dry for 30’ under sterile conditions, then the lid was closed. After 5 hours of incubation, 
the cells were scooped from the mating spot and resuspended in 200 μl BHIv2. The 
suspension was vortexed to interrupt the mating and streaked with a loop on LBv2 agar 
plates supplemented with the appropriate antibiotic.

3.15 Genomic integration by NT-CRISPR: 
 
This protocol was adapted from Stkenberg et al. 
Precultures carrying the pGG032-NT-CRISPR were grown overnight (16–17 h) at 30 °C and 
200 rpm in 5 ml BHIv2 with 2 µg/mL chloramphenicol and 100 µM IPTG to induce tfoX 
expression. The natural transformation was started by adding 3.5 µl of the precultures to 
350 µL sea salt medium (28 g/L (Sigma, S9883)) with 100 µM IPTG in a 1.5 ml reaction tube. 
100 ng of DNA fragments for reporter insertion with 3 kb homologous flanks was added. 
Samples were briefly vortexed and then incubated statically at 30 °C for 5 h. In a subsequent 
step, 1 ml BHIv2 without antibiotics was added to the cells. For CRISPR-Cas9 induction, 
200 ng/mL aTc was added to the BHIv2 medium. Tubes were mixed by inversion and 
incubated at 300 rpm and 30 °C for 1 h. 100 µL of sample were plated on BHIv2 agar plates 
with 2 µg/mL chloramphenicol. For CRISPR-Cas9 induction, 200 ng/mL aTc was added to the 
agar plates.

4.1 Transformation methods and Origins of replications: 
 
Since transformation relies on the host's ability to replicate the plasmid, successful 
transformations indicate that the plasmid’s origin of replication is functional in 
V. natriegens. We tested whether a variety of plasmids could be transformed into 
V. natriegens by electroporation, chemical transformation, or conjugation. The plasmids 
contained different origins of replications, antibiotic resistance markers, and additional 
expression cassettes. We were able to transform plasmids carrying the pUC, pBR322, ColiE1, 
or P15A origins; while no colonies were obtained from attempted transformation of pBBR1, 
RFS1010, and pSC101 plasmids (Table xxx). We were particularly interested in the RK2 
origin, as it is utilised in the Bacterial Artificial Chromosomes (BAC) that are currently being 
developed in the Norhølm lab to transfer the SEGA system to a wider range of hosts. We 
successfully conjugated a BAC carrying the E. coli maltose operon into V. natriegens, while 
all other attempts at transforming RK2 plasmids failed. The presence of the Maltose-BAC in 
V. natriegens was confirmed by colony PCR (Figure xyz). The conjugation of the other RK2 
plasmids, pSEVA121 and the SAGA06, BAC into V. natriegens could not be tested, because 
we were unable to transform the plasmids into the ST18 E. coli strains.

4.2 Establishing a set of reporter proteins for use in V. natriegens: 
 
During these initial transformations, we failed to transform constructs carrying a GFP 
expression cassette, even when we were successful in transforming similar plasmids with 
different or no cargo (Table xxx). Since all the tested GFP cassettes contained the strong 
constitutive J23100 Anderson promoter as well as a BCD and translation initiation region 
(TIR) optimised for high expression from the chromosome, we hypothesised that excessive 
GFP production was causing toxicity in V. natriegens. Therefore, we assemble a plasmid 
expressing GFP from the weaker pTet promoter (pGG009), but we were similarly unable to 
transform this plasmid into V. natriegens. We attempted to further reduce GFP expression 
by building an TIR library through USER cloning. We were able to obtain a library in E. coli, 
but all attempts at transferring it into V. natriegens failed. As an alternative we produced a 
similar TIR library using the Q5® Site-Directed Mutagenesis Kit (New England Biolabs Inc.) in 
E. coli. Transformation of this new library into V. natriegens yielded multiple fluorescent 
colonies. We selected 12 colonies representing a range of fluorescence intensities and 
purified the plasmids they carried. We wanted to identify the gfp TIR of these 12 plasmids 
by Sanger’s sequencing, but the read quality of the samples was too low. Utilising Nanopore 
whole plasmid sequencing to sequence one of the plasmids from the library, we discovered 
that the pTet promoter and a 5’ fragment of the gfp CDS were triplicated. It is likely that 
similar duplications or triplications occurred in the other plasmids of the TIR library, which 
would explain the failure of Sanger’ sequencing them. Being unable to predict the effect of

4.3 Expanding the available library of V. natriegens inducible promoters: 
 
Inducible promoters are a cornerstone of synthetic biology. They allow for user control over 
gene expression and are essential for engineering genetic circuits and biosensors. To expand 
the pool of inducible promoters available for V. natriegens, we decided to characterise the 
activity, of a subset of the Marionette’s promoter collection originally developed for E. coli 
in V. natriegens. We focussed on the pVanCC, pTac, pTet, pBetI, pTtg, p3B5C, and pSalTTC 
promoters which are induced by vanillic acid, IPTG, aTc, choline chloride, naringenin, DHBA, 
and sodium salicylate respectively. We first exchanged the YFP reporter of the original 
Marionette plasmids with fwYellow (henceforth referred as the fwYellow-Marionette 
plasmids) and transformed both sets of plasmids into V. natriegens. Since fwYellow 
produced a more visible fluorescence (Figure sdf), we selected it as our preferred reporter 
for all following experiments. To evaluate the effect of the inducers on V. natriegens 
growth, we cultured V. natriegens strains carrying the fwYellow-Marionette plasmids in 
liquid culture with or without the relevant inducers and monitored OD600. Apart from 
naringenin that completely prevented growth, we observed no significant effect of the 
inducers on V. natriegens (Figure cvb).  We stored naringenin as a solution dissolved in pure 
DMSO and, for this experiment, we induced using a 100X naringenin stock. We suspect that 
the resulting 1% DMSO in the media prevented growth rather than the naringenin itself. To 
circumvent this problem in all following experiments, we stored naringenin in a more 
concentrated stock and diluted it in water to 100X just before using it for induction.

4.4 Double populations: 
 
 
From the flowcytometry histograms, we observed that V. natriegens carrying either the 
pTtg-fwYellow or the pSalTTC-fwYellow plasmids segregate in two population: a smaller 
non-fluorescent population and a larger fluorescent one (Figure rc6B and rc7B).  
Specifically, cells carrying the pTtg-fwYellow plasmid exhibited this division only in the 
presence of naringenin. Samples without naringenin only contained the non-fluorescent 
cells, likely due to lack of expression from the uninduced promoter. The non-fluorescent 
population was larger in the sample collected 8 hours after induction compared to the one 
collected 2 hours after induction, indicating a loss of fluorescence over time (Figure rc6C).  
To further investigate this phenomenon, we analysed the populations’ distribution on 
forward scatter (FSC) – side scatter (SSC) dot plots as a proxy for their physical 
characteristics. For cells carrying the pTtg-fwYellow plasmid and exposed to naringenin, the 
two populations also segregated on FSC-SSC dot plots, with the fluorescent cells exhibiting 
higher FSC and SSC values than the non-fluorescent cells (Figure dp1D).  Additionally, the 
fluorescent population displayed higher FSC values compared to V. natriegens cells without 
a plasmid (negative control) (Figure dp3B) and V. natriegens cells carrying the pTet-fwYellow 
plasmid induced with aTc (Figure dp3D). Since the fluorescent population showed similar 
fluorescence levels to the induced pTet-fwYellow cells, we concluded that the differences in 
FSC were not caused by fwYellow expression. Furthermore, the uninduced pTtg-fwYellow 
samples exhibited similar FSC and SSC values as the negative control (Figure dp1b and 
db3B). 
In contrast, cells carrying the pSalTTC-fwYellow plasmid exhibited double populations 
regardless of the presence or absence of sodium salicylate (Figure rc7C). Interestingly, the 
non-fluorescent populations did not change over time, as we observed comparable peaks 2 
hours and 8 hours after induction (Figure rc7D). Notably, the distribution of both the 
fluorescent and non-fluorescent populations on the FSC-SSC dot plots was similar to that of 
the negative control (Figure dp2 and dp3B).

5 Discussion: 
 
5.1 Transformation methods and Origins of replication: 
 
In terms of replication origins functional in V. natriegens, our findings largely corroborate 
existing literature. Specifically, we have confirmed that pUC, pBR322, coliE1, RK2, and P15A 
origins are functional, while pSC101 is not. Although the pBBR1 origin has been reported to 
work in V. natriegens, we were unable to transform V. natriegens with pSEVA131, the only 
pBBR1 plasmid we tested. However, our investigation was limited to a single plasmid, and 
we only made two transformation attempts (one by heat shock and one by electroporation). 
Therefore, it is likely that our results reflect a stochastic failure rather than V. natriegens 
inability to replicate pSEVA131. Plasmids relying on the RFS1010 have previously been used 
in V. natriegens (Lee et al), but they carried additional specialised machinery to support 
RFS1010 replication. We did not provide V. natriegens with this additional machinery and 
were unable to transform RFS1010 plasmids, showing that V. natriegens native machinery 
does not recognise RFS1010.  
After demonstrating higher transformation efficiency for plasmids purified from 
V. natriegens rather than E. coli, we did not investigate the molecular cause of this 
difference. Nonetheless, we hypothesise that a self-vs-non-self recognition system, likely a 
defence mechanism against viral infection, might be responsible for the observed 
difference. These results highlight the value of recent efforts to develop V. natriegens as a 
fully independent molecular biology platform, free from reliance on E. coli.  
We obtained consistently similar results from electroporation and chemical transformation. 
The only exception was pGG032-NT-CRISPR, a plasmid carrying the CRISPR-Cas9 
counterselection machinery, which we successfully transformed through electroporation 
but not chemical transformation. This plasmid carries the CRISPR-Cas9 counterselection 
machinery that has been shown to cause mild toxicity to V. natriegens even when not 
induced. Considering the higher efficiency of electroporation over chemical transformation, 
it is plausible that Cas9-induced toxicity reduced the efficiency to a point where chemical 
transformation 
failed, 
whereas 
electroporation 
remained 
successful. 
Conversely, 
conjugation proved successful where both other transformation methods had failed, which 
is consistent with previous research indicating that RK2 plasmids can only be delivered via 
conjugation.  
The primary objective of these experiments was to identify our preferred materials and 
methods for engineering V. natriegens. Based on our findings, we recommend heat shock as 
the preferred transformation method due to its convenience, reserving electroporation or 
conjugation for situations involving problematic plasmids.

5.2 GFP toxicity: 
 
Use of GFP in V. natriegens has been previously reported. However, we consistently failed 
to transform plasmid containing gfp into V. natriegens, suggesting a toxic effect. This toxicity 
could be caused either by the GFP itself or by its mRNA. Considering that transformation of 
a gfp TIR library also failed, we hypothesised that toxicity is caused by the mRNA. In facts, 
differences in TIR lead to up of 103-fold changes in protein levels, and it is unlikely that GFP 
maintains toxicity across such a wide range of concentrations. Moreover, translationally 
independent toxicity of GFP’s mRNA has been previously reported in E. coli by Mittal et al. 
Mittal et al. determined that mRNA toxicity is affected by both mRNA structure and 
concentration.  
We were able to express GFP in V. natriegens only using a construct with a triplicated 
promoter and gfp 5’. This does not help elucidate the mechanism of toxicity, as it likely 
affects both transcription and mRNA folding. Although the reason these plasmids are 
functional in E. coli but toxic in V. natriegens remains unknown, it is evident that a 
functional and reliable E. coli tool has proven problematic in V. natriegens.  This reinforces 
the need for re-characterisation of E. coli parts in V. natriegens, showcasing the desirability 
of a protocol to quickly transfer entire libraries of parts into V. natriegens.  
 
5.3 Chromogenic proteins complete the set of available reporters for 
V. natriegens: 
 
The currently available set of reporters for V. natriegens is mostly composed of fluorescent 
proteins, only containing one visible and one luminescent reporter (Table zxc) While 
fluorescent proteins are easily quantifiable, their detection can be impaired by background 
fluorescence and requires specialised equipment such a fluorometer, a flow cytometer, or 
an ultraviolet light lamp. Similarly, the Lux systems rely on a luminometer for detection. On 
the other hand, the chromogenic reporters we characterised are detectable by the naked 
eye. They are better visual reporters than LacZ, as they do not require any additional 
substrate. Chromogenic proteins also limitations: namely, their precise quantification is 
more challenging, and they need two days to develop a visible colouration.  
Due to these trade-offs, it is desirable to have a diverse set of reporters to allow researchers 
to choose the one that better suites their experimental needs. The reporters that we 
characterised nicely complement the pre-existing set of reporters available for 
V. natriegens. Notably, fwYellow is both chromogenic and fluorescent, effectively combining 
the advantages of the two classes.

5.4 Expanding the available library of V. natriegens inducible promoters: 
 
pTac and pTet had been previously characterised in V. natriegens. However, these 
characterisations were based on bulk population measurement. By using flow cytometry, 
we studied these promoters at single-cell level, effectively adding a new layer to our 
understanding of the promoters’ induction dynamics.  
 pTtg and pSalTTC are complete novel additions to the V. natriegens toolbox. Their inclusion 
increases the number of available chemical inducers from three to five and, therefore, 
represents a significant improvement. pTtg showed excellent induction dynamics, having 
the largest dynamic range of the promoters we tested and a titratable induction curve. In 
contrast pSalTTC leaky nature and narrow dynamic range limits its usefulness as an inducible 
promoter. However since it has been characterised alongside the other inducible 
promoters, it could be useful as a week constitutive promoter in situations where control 
over relative expression levels of multiple genes is important. For example, in section 2 we 
discussed how ccdA and ccdB can be used as a genomic counterselection maker if tight 
control over their relative expression is possible. We could now build such a system using 
pSalTTC and pTtg (Figure mnb). pSalTTC will be used to drive CcdA antitoxin expression, 
while pTtg will regulate CcdB toxin expression. Based on our data (table rth), when no 
inducer is present expression of CcdA from pSalTTC will be 10 times higher than expression 
of CcdB from the pTtg promoter, and cells will proliferate. Once pTtg is induced, its activity 
will increase by ~120 folds, CcdB concentration will surpass CcdA’s and lead to cell death.

5.5 Potential causes of the double populations: 
 
We hypothesised that the double populations we observed in V. natriegens carrying the 
pTtg-fwYellow or pSalTTC-fwYellow plasmids are the results of different phenomena with 
distict causes. 
For the pTtg-fwYellow samples, the relative size of the non-fluorescent population is 
growing through time; the fluorescent cells show different physical proprieties (as inferred 
from differences in FSC and SSC) from both the non-fluorescent and V. natriegens cells from 
other samples; these differences in physical proprieties are only observed in the presence of 
naringenin.  
We believe we are observing a stress response caused either by naringenin itself or the 
DMSO used contained in the naringenin stock. The non fluorescent cells could be either 
dead cells or dormant one who entered latency to escape the stressor.  The relative size of 
the non-fluorescent population is increasing through time as more cells are either killed or 
forced into latency by prolonged exposure to the stressor. Finally, the different physical 
characteristic of the fluorescent cells could be part of a stress defence mechanism or a 
direct symptom of stress. 
If this hypothesis is correct, it would highlight a severe limitation of naringenin as an inducer 
for V. natriegens. It is desirable to develop a new formulation for naringenin stock that does 
not contain DMSO. This would help isolate the effect of naringenin and DMSO in causing the 
stress, and potentially resolve the problem altogether if DMSO is the sole stressor. 
In contrast, for the pSalTTC sample, the relative size of the non-fluorescent and fluorescent 
remains constant through time; the two population have similar physical characteristic to 
each other and other V. natriegens samples; they segregate by different fluorescence even 
when not exposed to sodium salicylate.  
We identified three possible scenarios: i) non-fluorescent cells are metabolically inactive 
cells. ii) non fluorescent cells lost the plasmid. iii) non-fluorescent cells maintained the 
plasmid with a mutation that prevents fw-Yellow expression. 
For scenario i), we would expect the relative size of the non-fluorescent population to shrink 
with time as metabolically active cells outgrow them. Moreover, it would be peculiar that 
metabolically inactive cells are observed only when this plasmid is present. 
For scenario ii), P15A has been previously shown to have a 100% maintenance rate in V. natriegens 
even in the absence of selective pressure. Moreover, the growth media contains kanamycin. 
V, natriegens is naturally tolerant to kanamycin but show a severely reduced growth rate when not 
expressing a resistance gene. Therefore, we would expect the relative size of the non-fluorescent 
population to shrink as it is outcompeted by cells that maintained the plasmid. However, kanamycin 
resistance relies on phosphorylation of the antibiotic outside the cell, so a large population of 
resistant cells could potentially protect a smaller one of non-resistant cells from kanamycin toxicity.  
==PAGE:62==
 56 
 
In scenario iii), if a mutation on the plasmid prevented fw-yellow expression, the cells carrying the 
the mutated plasmid would not differ from the rest of the population in any charactheristic other 
than fluorescence. They would have a similar growth rate and physical charactheristic to their 
fluorescent counterpart. 
With the available data we are not able to confidently identify the cause of the observed double 
population. However, both scenario ii) and iii) are the result of a rare stochastic event, therefore is 
likely that repeating the experiment starting from a fresh monoclonal population colud resolve the 
problem. 
 
5.6 Optimisation of the regulators’ 5’ UTRs could rescue leaky inducible 
promoters’ functionality: 
 
So far, we have described inducible promoters as two component systems, where activity of 
the promoter is directly affected by the concentration of inducer. In reality, these are three 
component systems. The activity of the promoter is determined by the interaction between 
the promoter and the active regulator; the regulator activity is in turn determined by the 
inducer concentration. For these systems to work properly, the concentration of the 
regulator needs to be within defined functional boundaries.  
The three promoters that are leaky in V. natriegens (pBetI, p3B5C, and pVan) are regulated 
by repressors. In a repressible system, if the regulator is not functional or its concentration 
is too low, the system will appear leaky. We know that all the regulators are transcribed in 
V. natriegens as they share the same promoter (pLac without the operators). However, they 
have different 5’ UTRs. Each 5’ UTR was engineered to ensure optimal regulator expression 
in E. coli. Unfortunately, there is no correlation between the effectiveness of a 5’ UTR in 
E. coli and in V. natriegens. Therefore, we hypothesised that re-optimisation of the 
regulators’ 5’ UTRs for V. natriegens would lead to sufficient expression of the regulator and 
restore the functionality of the system.  
While it is possible that the regulators are simply not functional in V. natriegens intracellular 
environment, the partial inducibility of pVan we observed supports our hypothesis. In facts, 
if the regulator is functional but poorly expressed, its concentration could be sufficient only 
for partial repression. When the inducer is present at a sufficient concentration would lift 
this partial repression, causing the 2-fold increase in promoter activity we observed.  
Once again, our results show that parts that have been optimised for E. coli need to be re-
characterised and re-optimised to be used in V. natriegens.  
 
5.7 Transfer strains: 
 
As described in section 4.5, the assembly of the transfer strain has not been complete yet as 
proper screening is still required. However due to the cross talk between the inducers used 
==PAGE:63==
 57 
 
for the NT-CRISPR protocol and the promoters of our reporters, we observed fluorescence 
from the strain transformed with fragments without the SEGA homology arms, but not from 
the strains transformed with the fragments that included the SEGA homology arms. We 
propose two possible explanations for this difference: i) the fragments containing the SEGA 
homology arms are larger (~ 6 kb larger) and therefore their integration is more challenging; 
ii) the SEGA homology arms introduce large part of E. coli genetic material that affect 
expression of the reporter sandwiched between them. Screening by PCR is likely to 
elucidate which hypothesis is correct. 
If hypothesis i) is correct, then the transfer strain could be assembled by consecutive rounds 
of integration of smaller fragments. After which the transfer strain could be used as 
described in section 2, as all SEGA constructs are between 2-5 kb. 
Proof of hypothesis ii) would have more severe implications on our transfer protocol. As if 
we cannot maintain the 3 kb homology arms without affecting expression of the construct 
between them, a new genomic integration protocol that relies on shorter homology arm 
would be necessary to support or transfer protocol. Such a genomic integration protocol is 
currently not available for V. natriegens, and its development might require significant 
amount of time and effort.  
Both the pGG032-NT-CRISPR, that we are using to run the NT-CRISPR protocol, and the 
fragments we are trying to insert contain the tetR and lacI genesas well as the pTet and pTac 
promoters. These is likely to reduce efficiency of the protocol and greatly complicate 
screening. A better design of the fragments would have been either using one of the leaky 
promoters we characterised during this project or one of the constitutive promoters from 
the available V. natriegens toolbox. While this was surely an oversite on our part, it is also a 
symptom of the scarcity of the available tools for V. natriegens. The more limited the choice 
of parts, the higher the likelihood of accidentally designing construct that are incompatible 
with protocols developed by other groups. This accident clearly showcases the necessity of 
expanding V. natriegens toolbox, to allow this remarkable bacterium to truly fulfil its 
biotechnological potential.

6 Conclusion and outlook: 
 
We defined two aims of this project: on one side the expansion of V. natriegens available 
toolbox; on the other the development of a transfer system to quickly move construct from 
the SEGA collection int V. natriegens.  
We have clearly achieved the first one. We introduced a new set of chromogenic proteins 
that nicely complete the available set of mostly fluorescent reporters. Moreover, we have 
showcased the potential of these reporters by utilising them to characterise new parts. 
Specifically, we have introduced two inducible promoters completely novel to V. natriegens, 
and further characterised previously known promoters by studying them at a single cell 
level.  
Regarding our second aim, we admittedly fell short. Not only the transfer protocol we 
envisioned has not been developed and tested, but the transfer strain that were supposed 
to be the foundation of the system have not been engineered either. Our failure to do so, 
however, it is not caused by a technological failure or deeply flawed design, but rather by 
the narrow time constrains of the project. We are confident that it is still possible and 
desirable to develop the transfer system. In facts, when considering what the most valuable 
future V. natriegens research could be, our answer would undoubtedly fall on our transfer 
system.  
Our results showed time and time again, that E. coli tools work unpredictably in 
V. natriegens. Nonetheless, a majority of the tools now available for V. natriegens are just 
E. coli tools that have been shown to be functional in V. natriegens. This also apply to the 
tools that we just characterised. After working with V. natriegens for months, we are 
convinced that its best future prospects lie in the development and optimisation of tools 
specifically for V. natriegens. However, V. natriegens is still a very “young” biotechnological 
platform, therefore initial reliance on more established organisms is inevitable until a 
sufficient number of tools is available. The transfer system could greatly accelerate this 
initial stage and allow V. natriegens to truly come into is own. If fully developed and 
optimised, our system could even serve as a steppingstone towards the establishment of a 
true V. natriegens SEGA system completely independent from the E. coli one.  
A great amount of work from many researchers is necessary to develop V. natriegens into 
the “Powerhouse” that dr. Church envisioned, this project with its addition of 9 functional 
parts, is a small contribution to it.
