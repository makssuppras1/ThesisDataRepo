Abstract
The fabrication of small-diameter vascular tissues that mimic in vivo microvasculature
by forming a capillary-like network and remain viable over an extended period is crucial
for engineering in vitro 3D models in Tissue Engineering (TE). The integration of a stable,
perfusable, and functional vascular network in engineered constructs facilitates nutrient and
oxygen exchange, which is vital, especially in thick tissues, in order to achieve long-term
maintenance of the tissues and to build more accurate models that replicate in vivo conditions
effectively. This study aimed to develop a method for fabricating a biologically relevant
vascular tissue with a diameter of 100 µm that displays extended viability and the ability
to sprout into smaller capillaries with diameters ranging from 10-100 µm forming a vascular
network.
To achieve this objective, the approach we developed uses embedded bioprinting to
construct cell-laden filaments made of Human Umbilical Vein Endothelial Cells (HUVECs)
within the Self-Healing Annealable Particle-ECM (SHAPE) support bath, a matrix com-
posed of alginate and collagen. Mechanically-driven extrusion-based bioprinting is used to
deposit the bioink, which consists of cells suspended in growth medium and Geltrex, into the
support bath. After printing, the collagen is thermally crosslinked, ensuring shape fidelity
and high resolution and growth medium, supplemented with Vascular Endothelial Growth
Factor (VEGF), was added on the top of the gel. A systematic study was performed to
optimize the printing parameters such as the extrusion rate, printing speed, and needle size
and the research further investigated the impact of these parameters on cell viability. Fur-
thermore, the effects of VEGF, fibronectin and xanthan gum on cell adhesion and vascular
development were explored.
Key results revealed that embedded bioprinting into the SHAPE support bath main-
tained high cell viability for an extended period between 1-2 weeks. However, a vascular
development and angiogenic sprouting were not achieved. Despite our efforts, cells did not
establish cell-cell and cell-matrix connections, thereby suggesting that alginate might prevent
cells from adhering. Moreover, despite the addition of VEGF, no angiogenic sprouting was
observed, raising our concerns in relation to VEGF delivery strategies and diffusion through
the matrix.
This work underlines the potential of embedded bioprinting within the SHAPE sup-
port matrix for the fabrication of a stable, functional vascular network. While substantial
advancements were achieved in bioprinting procedure optimization and maintaining long-
term viability of the cell-laden constructs, further research should focus on investigating the
ECM composition and VEGF delivery methods to facilitate cell-cell and cell-matrix adhe-
sion and angiogenic sprouting. Lastly, this study provides valuable insights into the field
of vasculature engineering, potentially offering a method that establishes vascular networks
into engineered tissues.

The methodology of vascular tissue fabrication and formation of a capillary
network. (a) SHAPE composite formulation includes alginate microparticle
generation, neutralization of the collagen type I and mixing of the alginate mi-
croparticles with collagen at a ratio 2:1. Once the SHAPE composite is ready,
it is loaded on the well plate; (b) Bioink preparation first requires harvesting
of the cells. The cells are then resuspended into growth medium and Geltrex
to formulate the bioink at a cell density of 106 cells/mL; (c) Embedded bio-
printing into the SHAPE support bath uses a piston to extrude the bioink
from the syringe through the nozzle. After bioprinting, the filaments are in-
cubated at 37 ◦C, 5% CO2 for 30 minutes for annealing; (d) After annealing,
growth medium and VEGF is added on top of the gel. The medium and the
growth factor are changed every other day. The aspiration of the medium
occurs by tilting the plate to avoid disturbance of the gel, while the fresh
medium is is gradually added along the walls of the well, drop by drop; (e)
The bioprinted filaments are stained, and imaged in a fluorescent microscope
to observe angiogenesis. Created with BioRender.com . . . . . . . . . . . . .

and angiogenesis. Vasculogenesis occurs during embryonic development and
describes the de novo formation of blood vessels. Angiogenesis remodels the
blood vessels in response to biochemical cues such as hypoxia or the presence
of growth factors, thereby forming a new vessel from an already existing one.

Introduction
Tissue engineering (TE) is an emerging technology in the field of biomedical research
focused on two main objectives: the creation of functional biological tissues that can re-
store damaged tissues or facilitate the intrinsic healing processes, and the development of in
vitro models that replicate in vivo biological systems, providing valuable information for re-
search in drug testing, disease modeling, and better understanding of physiological processes
[12]. The working principle of TE is based on cell biology and materials science to develop
engineered structures.
In vivo, almost all metabolically active tissues comprise a hierarchical network of blood
vessels to ensure tissue function and viability [13]. Therefore, the incorporation of vascular
structures into engineered models is a key parameter for a greater accuracy of the model,
as it provides cell protection from insufficient oxygen and nutrients. [1, 14]. Furthermore,
depending on the diameter of the blood vessel in the vascular tree, a function alteration is
observed. More specifically, within this hierarchical network, larger blood vessels regulate
blood flow, while blood vessels at the microscale, known as the capillary network, support the
exchange of nutrients and oxygen to the surrounding tissues [1]. There has been significant
progress in vasculature progress due to advances in biomaterials, 3D bioprinting, and mi-
crofabrication. However, in vitro establishment of a dense, stable, fully mature, perfusable,
hierarchical vascular network that rapidly integrates into engineered structures presents a
significant challenge [15, 16].
Blood vessels consist of layers, which continuously interact in response to signaling cues
to remodel and retain adequate perfusion to surrounding tissues. Therefore, blood vessels de-
velop not only at the embryonic stage through vasculogenesis, but also in adulthood through
angiogenesis. Angiogenic sprouting typically occurs under nutrient-deprived and hypoxic
conditions triggering the endothelial cells (ECs) of that area to detach from a stable position
and branch out toward the site of angiogenesis. The process of angiogenesis is facilitated by
growth factors such as vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), basic fibroblast growth
factor (bFGF), and platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) [14].
There has been a strong focus on the development of small-diameter vasculature (<6
mm), because the fabrication of large-diameter vascular channels is a significantly less com-
plex process [17, 14]. Fabrication of small-diameter blood vessels, including small vessels and
microvessels, is typically a complicated process. However, due to the advancements of 3D
bioprinting, microfluidic technology and laser technology, the creation of more complex and
physiologically resembling structures of small-diameter vessels is currently feasible [18, 15].
3D bioprinting has gained ground in the construction of in vitro vascular models due to
its ability to print cells, biomaterials, and cell-laden biomaterials to fabricate 3D structures.
Various 3D bioprinting techniques have been used for the fabrication of vascular channels
such as extrusion bioprinting, inkjet bioprinting and light-assisted bioprinting. Extrusion-
based bioprinting is the most widely used technique for the construction of blood vessels,

olution and decreased cell viability compared to the other techniques [15]. To achieve higher
resolution and enable bioprinting with lower geometric restrictions and smaller size features,
embedded bioprinting has gained attention as a powerful alternative in recent years. The
poor mechanical properties of the bioinks which are usually based on soft hydrogels, prevent
the printed filaments from maintaining structural integrity and shape fidelity. Therefore, the
deposition of bioinks into a support bath rather than onto a surface exposed to ambient air,
inhibits possible deformations and collapse of the filaments after printing [19, 7].
An issue of vital importance when it comes to bioprinting functional 3D vascular tissues
aiming to integrate them in 3D engineered models, is the required fabrication time. The
creation of vascular vessels is typically time-consuming in such a way that the fabrication
time usually exceeds the rate of cellular metabolism. Consequently, embedding the engi-
neered vessels in tissue constructs is often insufficient to preserve cell viability, resulting in
a lack of nutrients and oxygen and therefore cell death before the establishment of a stable
vascular network [20]. Prevascularization refers to the implementation of already formed
vascular structures in the engineered tissues, addressing this problem. Seeding ECs within
suitable scaffolds like gels consisting of different ECM components, such as type I collagen,
fibrin, fibronectin, and basement membrane extracts like Matrigel, in order to facilitate ECs
proliferation, migration and connection, and enable self-assembly into capillary-like network
is a common approach [21, 16].
The aim of this project is to create a biologically relevant vascular tissue with a di-
ameter of 100 µm that maintains viability over an extended culture period and can sprout
into smaller capillary structures with diameters ranging between 10-100 µm. To meet this
objective, we employ embedded bioprinting using the Self-Healing Annealable Particle-ECM
(SHAPE) support bath developed by Kajtez et al. [7]. We hypothesize that extrusion-based
bioprinting of Human Umbilical Vein Endothelial Cells (HUVECs) into an alginate/colla-
gen support bath will promote ECs proliferation, migration, and connection, induce lumen
formation and facilitate angiogenic sprouting. To achieve this, both for the support bath
and for the bioink, we use materials based on natural hydrogels and ECM polymers, while
bioprinted filaments are also supplemented with VEGF to induce angiogenesis.
Toward addressing these objectives, we first focus on determining the printing param-
eters such as the printing speed and extrusion rate to bioprint lines of HUVECs with a
diameter of 100 µm in the collagen-based support matrix. Once optimization of the printing
process is achieved, we focus on maintaining viability over a long-term culture period of 1-2
weeks. Lastly, we investigate the effect of growth factors such as VEGF and fibronectin
on vascular development and angiogenic sprouting. Our fabrication process is presented in
Figure 1.
SHAPE composite is formulated as a long-term structural and functional matrix and
not as a sacrificial support [7]. Therefore, we believe that our approach has the potential
to develop a stable vascular network within the support bath, which can then be used as a

Background
2.1
The challenge of vascularization in Tissue Engineering
TE integrates tools from engineering and life sciences to create bioartificial substitutes
for organs and tissues that find application in regenerative medicine, drug development,
diagnostics and foundational research focusing on providing insights into cellular functions
in vivo, as well as revealing mechanisms of aging, disease onset, and progression [22]. The
precision and fidelity of these models, especially their ability to accurately replicate cell
behavior in native tissues, is essential to advance our understanding of cellular interactions
in tissues and organs [23].
The limitations of traditional two-dimensional (2D) cultures and animal testing, coupled
with advances in 3D cell biology and engineering, have contributed to the emergence of 3D
in vitro models as valuable alternatives. Compared to 2D cultures and animal models, 3D
models mimic in vivo conditions and replicate complex tissue structures of the human body
more accurately, increasing the reliability of clinical outcome predictions and drug toxicity
assessments, and acting as versatile tools for studying a wide range of diseases [23, 24].
One of the key challenges in TE for constructing 3D functional tissues is achieving
proper vascularization. Although 3D printing technology has driven progress in TE, a crit-
ical challenge remains: the establishment of a dense and efficient microvascular network to
support cell viability in engineered tissues [4]. The integration of a vascular network into
dense tissues is essential to facilitate nutrient and oxygen transport across the tissues and
allow the development of larger tissue models. Biofabricating vasculature is particularly
challenging as it requires the replication of a hollow, endothelialized lumen, a highly orga-
nized and hierarchically branched network structure, and a complex signaling environment,
all of which are essential for the effective function of both the microvasculature and the
entire tissue. Significant progress has been made toward each of aforementioned three ob-
jectives; however, no singular methodology has successfully combined all three to develop a
fully functional and multiscale engineered microvasculature. [1]

Vascular tissue is of vital importance when it comes to the delivery of sufficient oxygen
and nutrients to cells within tissues that exceed the millimeter scale in thickness.
The
majority of cells in vivo lie within 100-200 µm of a capillary to maintain adequate diffusion
of oxygen, nutrients and immune cells, while the vascular system also removes metabolic
waste products from tissues [20, 25]. Therefore, it is crucial to gain insight into the in vivo
processes of vascular structure, growth, and development as a foundation for engineering in
vitro tissues [20].
2.2
Histological structure of a blood vessel
The vasculature can be classified into three main vessel systems: the arterial system, the
venous system, and the lymphatic system [26, 2, 27].The arterial system carries oxygenated
blood away from the heart to tissues with larger arteries such as the pulmonary artery and the
dorsal aorta, branching into gradually smaller diameter arteries, arterioles, and capillaries.
Capillaries facilitate the exchange between blood and surrounding tissue. The venous system
collects deoxygenated blood from the capillaries and delivers it back to the heart through
increasingly larger venules and veins. Lastly, the lymphatic system of vessels contributes to
the transportation of interstitial fluid from tissues and organs, reintroducing it back into the
circulation [25, 27].
The vascular walls of the arteries and veins are made of three layers, called from the
inner to the outer side, the tunica intima, the tunica media and the tunica adventitia, while
capillaries are formed of only a monolayer of ECs covered by a discontinuous coat of pericytes
[28, 29]. The tunica intima is histologically separated from the tunica media and the tunica
media from the tunica adventitia by two sheet-like structures, known as internal elastic
lamina and external elastic lamina, respectively [26].
The main cells that participate in the composition of the vascular wall are ECs, smooth
6
==PAGE:18==
muscle cells (SMCs) and fibroblasts. The inner layer of the vascular wall contains a continu-
ous single layer of ECs that is connected to the basal membrane and promotes laminar flow
of blood through the vessel. ECs are present in the innermost layer that lines tha vascular
lumen in all blood vessels. The middle layer is composed of dense SMCs with fibrous and
elastic bands of tissue that contribute to the maintenance of elasticity and radial compliance
of the vessel. Lastly, the outer layer consists of fibroblasts, progenitor cells, perivascular adi-
pose tissue cells, and nerve endings and plays a crucial role in providing mechanical strength
to the vessel and adhesion to the surrounding organs [30, 2, 29, 3]. Figure 3 demonstrates
the arterial and venous vessel system, the connection between them as well as the individual
layers along with their cellular composition. Depending on the diameter of the blood vessel,
they can be classified into microvessels with diameters smaller than 1 mm, small vessels with
diameters between 1-6 mm, and large vessels with diameters greater than 6 mm [2].

When it comes to microvasculature, it is worth noting that blood vessels integrate into
the tissue they supply both structurally and functionally. Microvasculature includes three
types of vessels, arterioles, capillaries, and venules. Arteries branch into arterioles, which
have diameters between 10 and 200 µm. Arterioles have the same three-layer structure as
seen in macrovessels, but the thickness of the layers is reduced. Arterioles lead to capillaries,
which have diameters between 5 and 10 µm, and then empty the deoxygenated blood into
venules that are typically 8-100 µm in diameter. Multiple venules merge to formulate veins
[31].

2.3
Blood vessel formation
The formation of vascular networks takes place through two main processes: vasculo-
genesis and angiogenesis. Vasculogenesis can be defined as the de novo formation of blood
vessels from aggregated endothelial precursors, whereas angiogenesis involves the formation
of new vessels either by sprouting from existing vessels or by splitting an existing blood
vessel to create smaller ones [4, 32, 33].
In vasculogenesis, mesodermal stem cells differentiate into endothelial progenitor cells
due to the presence of certain biomolecules such as VEGF and therefore, angioblasts are
generated. Angioblasts move to specific areas and create separate blood islands, which unite
to form a vascular plexus and ECs. Lastly, ECs migrate and organize into tubal structures
that form a capillary network within a previously nonvascularized region. It is crucial to
note that vasculogenesis occurs mainly during fetal development and in the revascularization
of damaged tissues in adults. On the other hand, angiogenesis serves as the main process by
which new blood vessels develop in adults [13].
Sprouting angiogenesis begins with cell-to-cell signaling, identifying specific endothelial
cells as tip cells. These tip cells project long pseudopodia and migrate towards areas that
lack blood vessels as a reaction to angiogenic factors. This migration takes place by break-
ing down the basement membrane of the "mother" vessel and the surrounding interstitial
matrix. Neighboring cells differentiate into endothelial stalk cells whose primary function is
to proliferate and expand the new vessel by forming a lumen within the endothelial sprout.
Ultimately, the new vessel is connected to the already existing vessels through anastomosis,
forming a network to facilitate blood circulation [27, 34, 33].

2.4
Extracellular matrix and its role in vascular development
The ECM is a highly organized network of proteins and other macromolecules and plays
a pivotal role in the support of the vascular endothelium [35]. It acts as a microenvironmen-
tal niche providing biophysical and biochemical signals and supporting cell residence and
function within the body [36].
ECM is composed of various noncellular biological materials, that together with cells
contribute to the formation of tissues and organs. By delivering biochemical and biophysical
cues, ECM serves as a scaffolding material that offers structural and mechanical support to
tissues as well as a physical medium for the cells to adhere and function. Therefore, ECM
plays a key role in cellular functions such as survival, proliferation, migration, self-renewal,
and differentiation and acts as a reservoir for growth factors (GFs) and cytokines, which are
molecules that participate in modulating processes of tissue development, homeostasis, and
regeneration [36].
The composition of ECM depends on cell-secreted proteins and varies depending on the
type of tissue, developmental stage, age, and pathology. The major ECM components that
participate in the aforementioned procedures are fibrillar, structural, and adhesive proteins
such as collagen, elastin, laminin, fibronectin, and vitronectin, amorphous matrix macro-
molecules such as proteoglycans, glycosaminoglycans (GAGs), and hyaluronan, specialized
signalling molecules such as GFs, cytokines, and hormones. Additionally, proteases such as
matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) participate in the ECM remodeling by degrading ECM
proteins and allowing matrix turnover and cell migration [37]. It is important to note that
cells attach to ECM molecules through a variety of receptors on the cell surface, such as
integrins, while the ECM proteins provide cell-binding sites specific to these adhesion recep-
tors. The integrin-ligand binding is mediated by divalent cations such as Mg2+ and Ca2+
that are present in the ECM [36, 38, 39, 40].
Biophysical properties of the ECM play an important role in the regulation of cellular
functions. For example, mechanical properties of the ECM such as stiffness and viscoelas-
ticity, affect the cellular phenotype and alter cellular functions such as migration and gene
expression.
In addition, structural properties such as topography control cellular shape,
orientation, and alignment, while other properties such as permeability contribute to the
transportation of solutes and nutrients [36].
The adhesive interactions between the ECM and the integrins on the ECs surface of-
fer a scaffold that is crucial for maintaining the organization of vascular ECs into blood
vessels. Additionally, adhesion between the ECM and the ECs plays a significant role in
neovascularization, since this connection is a fundamental requirement for cellular processes
such as ECs proliferation, migration, morphogenesis, and survival, eventually contributing
to stabilization of the blood vessels. The formation of the lumen as well as the stability
and maturation of newly formed vessels is significantly influenced by the ECM composition
along with the regulation of its degradation and remodeling [41, 40, 42]. There are several
factors that contribute to the regulation of angiogenesis, such as GFs, cytokines, ECM cues,
9
==PAGE:21==
and integrins. The key GFs that regulate ECs migration, survival, proliferation, and mor-
phogenesis in vivo are VEGF and bFGF [35]. The adhesion of GFs to ECM is significantly
influenced by the composition and interactions of the matrix and can determine the out-
comes of angiogenesis. GFs interact electrostatically with heparan sulfated proteoglycans
in the ECM like heparin and syndecan, while non-proteoglycan ECM proteins with a less
negative charge like fibronectin show GF-specific interactions [43, 44].
ECM is the primary component of the vessel wall and in the arteries and veins, it
occupies more than half of the wall mass. The main components of the blood vessel wall are
collagens and elastin, while vascular wall ECM is also composed of fibronectin, microfibrils,
abundant amorphous or soluble proteoglycans as well as leucine-rich GAGs. The composition
of ECM varies across the different layers of the vascular wall [45].
The ECM components that play a key role in angiogenesis can be broadly classified
in relation to if they are a part of the interstitial matrix or the basement membrane. The
interstitial matrix includes fibrous proteins such as collagen and elastin, which give tissues
their mechanical strength, elasticity, and structural integrity. Collagen is the most abundant
family of proteins in the human body and provides many integrin-binding sites that cells
can adhere to. It can be classified into two categories according to their structure: fibrillar
(types I, II, III, and V) and nonfibrillar (type IV). The most prevalent type in the vasculature
is type I collagen. Furthermore, the interstitial matrix is also composed of adhesive glyco-
proteins such as fibronectins which bind to integrins to mediate cell adhesion, migration,
and cell-matrix interaction-processes that are crucial for angiogenesis. On the other hand,
the basement membrane mainly contains type IV collagen and laminin, which promote cell
adhesion and control blood-barrier functions. Laminins interact with proteoglycans, GAGs,
and collagens, thus strengthening the structural integrity of the ECM [43, 36].
Endothelial cells form capillary-like tubes on ECM proteins in vitro, which has been a
successful method to screen for factors that promote or inhibit angiogenesis [33]. A widely
used in vitro angiogenesis assay comprises culturing ECs on or within ECM components such
as collagen, fibrin or Matrigel anticipating that in adequate conditions, ECs will migrate
into the ECM-based matrix, change their shape and from a capillary-like network of tubular
structures [5, 35]. Endothelial cell adhesion to the ECM via integrin-collagen I interaction
activates or suppresses several signaling pathways, while an in vitro study presented how type
IV collagen promotes endothelial cell proliferation and angiogenesis [37, 46]. As discussed
above, collagen and fibronectin are key ECM proteins that interact with cells and play a
crucial role in various cell functions, thus being two ECM proteins of particular interest [42].

2.5
Extrusion-based bioprinting
In TE, 3D printing aims to produce living and functional tissues that efficiently mimic
the in vivo environment [47, 48]. Bioprinting is the term used when 3D printing involves
printing cells, while the printing materials are known as bioinks. The major bioprinting
technologies are extrusion, inkjet, stereolithography, and laser-assisted printing methods,
with extrusion-based bioprinting offering several advantages [48].
Extusion bioprinting or else known as direct ink writing is the most commonly used 3D
bioprinting method and is the process of selectively dispensing material typically from a sy-
ringe through a nozzle or needle using pneumatic or mechanical forces [49, 35]. The extruded
volume has a straightforward dependence on flow rate as a result of mass conservation, allow-
ing the piston velocity to be related to the extrusion velocity via the ratio of cross-sectional
areas. However, the extruder requires time to build sufficient pressure to prevail over the
yield stress of the bioink and this consideration requires optimization and tuning to achieve
11
==PAGE:23==
the appropriate extrusion at the start and end points of the printed structure [50].
The pneumatic system uses pressured air to force the material through the syringe
and then through the nozzle. Fluid flow and air compressibility can impact performance,
resulting in unstable dispensing throughout the process. In positive displacement dispensing,
a mechanical piston forces the bioink through the nozzle while being displaced laterally,
whereas in the screw-driven system, a screw rotates, moving the bioink out of the nozzle.
However, screw-based bioprinting causes the accumulation of high pressure in the bottom of
the syringe [49]. It should be noted that piston- and screw- based bioprinting techniques are
widely used for high-viscosity bioinks, although the screw-driven system has been reported
to cause increased cell damage [51].
Figure 6: Principles of extrusion-based bioprinting. The ink is extruded through a nozzle
utilizing a mechanical- or a pneumatic-driven extrusion system. Pneumatic-driven extrusion
uses compressed air, while mechanical-driven system uses a piston or a screw to extrude the
ink. The image is reprinted from [6].
As illustrated in Figure 6, extrusion bioprinting generates sustained filaments by ap-
plying a continuous extrusion force. These filaments are then deposited on the substrate,
which can be solid like a culture dish, liquid like growth medium or a gel-derived material,
and are stacked to create the desired structures [6]. Extrusion bioprinting enables printing
with higher cell densities compared to other methods. However, cells are subjected to shear
stress while passing through the nozzle and pressure in the syringe before extrusion, posing
the risk of reduced cell viability and function [48]. The final bioprinted formation depends
on many factors such as the diameter and movement speed of the nozzle, the viscosity of
the bioink and the extrusion speed, the extrusion pressure, and the surface properties of the
substrate [35].
2.5.1
Embedded bioprinting
Extusion bioprinting systems are the most widely used method for the fabrication of cell-
laden constructs that successfully replicate tissues and organs to construct physiologically
12
==PAGE:24==
relevant 3D in vitro models [52, 53]. However, one main limitation of 3D bioprinting is related
to the difficulty in printing complex multilayer structures due to the physical properties of
bioinks based on soft hydrogel matrices, which have the tendency to deform or collapse
after printing. The inevitable effects of gravity on hydrogels at the air-water interface make
printing intricate structures and maintaining shape fidelity a challenging task [19, 54, 55, 56,
57].
To address these shortcomings, embedded 3D bioprinting has arisen as a powerful al-
ternative for fabricating soft materials and tissues [7, 58, 59]. The principle of embedded
bioprinting is based on dispensing the bioink into a support bath instead of extruding it on
the top of a surface in ambient air. The support bath has the ability to behave as a solid at
rest and temporarily turn into a liquid around the moving needle tip when the stress applied
by the needle movement exceeds the yield stress of the support bath. This process facilitates
the precise deposition of the soft bioink and the stabilization of it as the support resolidifies
as the needle moves forward and the stress-induced deformation is relieved [7, 58, 19, 60].
The use of support bath-assisted systems allows for printing freeform structures with
lower geometric restrictions, higher resolution, and smaller size features, which is a crucial
factor for bioprinting vascular-like structures. Another advantage of these systems is the
possibility of using bioinks with lower viscosity, which can be beneficial for cell viability and
the overall biological performance of the printed structures[7, 58, 19].
Regarding the selection of materials that can be used for support baths, granular gels
have been of great interest for embedded 3D bioprinting, due to their excellent rheological
properties, which enable high-resolution printing. However, granular gels have been limited
to a very narrow range of biomaterials, making the adjustment of the extracellular microen-
vironment of the support bath a challenging task. For this purpose, Kajtez et al. proposed
a new approach [7, 58] that formulates a composite of two types of materials enabling easy
regulation of the cellular environment and facilitating precise 3D patterning. The hybrid
material, named as SHAPE, consists of a granular and a continuous phase, where the hy-
drogel microparticles (granular phase) provide an extended interstitial space that can be
occupied by a viscous, biofunctional polymer solution (continuous phase). SHAPE support
was generated by cell-inert alginate microparticles (≈70% volume fraction) and thermally
crosslinkable cell-adhesive ECM polymers (≈30% volume fraction). The paper presents how
the crosslinking of ECM polymers induces the annealing of the support, thus creating an
optimal 3D microenvironent for the generation, long-term maintenance and maturation of
human cells.

2.6
Bioinks and their rheological properties
As mentioned above, bioinks consist of living cells mixed with porous cytocompati-
ble biomaterials characterized by complex rheological behavior. These biomaterials can be
natural or synthetic polymers and are classified as hydrogels [61]. Natural hydrogels such
as alginate, collagen, gelatin, chitosan, agarose, and hyaluronic acid are widely used for
bioink formulations due to their ability to promote a favorable environment for cell adhe-
sion, growth, and proliferation [62, 51]. The rheological properties of the bioink can play
a key role in the printability, flow behavior, deformation, and shape fidelity of the bioink,
while they also have an impact on cell proliferation and differentiation [63].
The development of bioinks in extrusion bioprinting should include materials that show
shear-thinning behavior, viscoelasticity, rapid gelation characteristics while they can also
exhibit thixotropy [64, 62, 51]. In bioprinting, viscosity refers to the resistance of a bioink to
deformation caused by an applied force [65]. Shear-thinning behavior allows for a reduction
in bioink viscosity at a higher shear rate, which makes the bioink more easily printed by
orienting macromolecules in the flow direction [64, 66]. This non-Newtonian shear thinning
behavior decreases the shear forces applied on the cells within the needle, thus improving
cell viability [66]. The viscoelastic properties provide valuable insight in regard to the bioink
behavior before, during, and after bioprinting. More specifically, viscoelasticity allows the ink
to behave as an elastic solid prior to bioprinting, transition to a fluid while passing through
the needle, and return to its original gel-like state after deposition protecting cells from
shear stress as well as the structural stability of the printed filaments [64, 63]. Thixotropy
is a time-dependent behavior that refers to the viscosity-recovery behavior of the ink after
extrusion. The thixotropic behavior allows for a reduction in viscosity during printing and
ensures a rapid increase in viscosity after cessation of flow [62, 65].
14
==PAGE:26==
Although extrusion bioprinting allows for a broad range of bioink viscosities, higher vis-
cosities require higher extrusion pressures, potentially harming the viability and proliferation
of the encapsulated cells [66]. From the other side, an increased viscosity can enhance struc-
tural stability and shape fidelity, because once the viscous ink is deposited, it resists flow and
spreading, thereby maintaining cells in place and ensuring shape fidelity [17]. Moreover, an
increase in viscosity can hinder inhomogeneous cell distribution and sedimentation, which
come as an inevitable result of gravitational forces. Therefore, a common approach is the
addition of viscosity modifiers that prevent cell sedimentation such as xanthan gum [17, 49].
The size and geometry of the outlet used in extrusion-based bioprinting play a key role
in determining the applied shear stress on the cells, thereby affecting their survival rate.
Smaller needle diameters can result in needle clogging and demand higher extrusion rates
resulting in increased shear forces on the cells during printing, which affects cell viability and
function in a negative way [67, 61]. Furthermore, the magnitude of stress can lead cells to
experience different forms of cell death, including lysis, apoptosis, necrosis or cause changes
to their phenotype [61, 48].
To ensure shape fidelity and stability, it is important that the deposited bioink is so-
lidified or crosslinked after bioprinting. The most commonly used mechanisms for bioprint-
ing are ionic, photo, thermal, and enzyme crosslinking.
Thermal crosslinking is applied
in temperature-sensitive polymers such as collagen, which can be polymerized within 60
minutes at 37 ◦C and neutral pH [17].

Materials and Methods
3.1
Cell culture
3.1.1
Endothelial cell line
The endothelial cell line used for bioprinting was CI-huVEC from InSCREENex GmbH
(Cat. No.:INS-CI-1002). It is an immortalized human vascular endothelial cell line from the
umbilical vein [69]. CI-huVEC culture was performed under sterile conditions in a Laminar
Air Flow (LAF) bench and cells were incubated at 37 ◦C, 100% humidity, 5% CO2. Cells
were cultured in huVEC medium including basal medium and supplements (InSCREENex
GmbH, Cat. No.: INS-ME-1011) with 1% Penicillin/Streptomycin (P/S). The flasks were
coated with gelatin solution 0.5% by diluting the gelatin solution 2% (InSCREENex GmbH,
Cat. No.: INS-SU-1015-50ml). Cells were passaged and harvested at 70-90% confluency and
the number of passages did not exceed 20.
The gelatin solution 2% was pre-warmed in a water bath and diluted with Dulbecco’s
Phosphate-buffered saline (DPBS) (Gibco, Cat. No.: 14040-091) to a final concentration of
0.5%. A T75 cell culture flask is coated with at least 2 mL of gelatin solution 0.5% and
is placed in the incubator at 37 ◦C for at least 30 minutes. The huVEC medium and the
DPBS were also placed inside the water bath for approximately half an hour to warm up.
Once everything is ready, the medium is aspirated from the T75 flask using a glass Pasteur
pipette. Subsequently, the flask is washed with 2.5 mL of DPBS, which is then aspirated.
Once the flask has been washed, 2.5 mL of Tryple (TrypLE™Express Enzyme (1X), phenol
red, Gibco, Cat. No.: 12605010) is added to the flask and the flask is placed in the incubator
for approximately 10 minutes, because HUVECs need more time to detach from the surface
of the flask. After incubation, cells are checked under the microscope to confirm that they
are detaching from the surface. If needed, the flask is tapped hard to force the cells to
detach. If the cells still adhere to the flask, the flask is placed back in the incubator for 2
more minutes.
Once cells have detached, 2.5 mL of huVEC medium is added to the flask (1:1 ratio
with Tryple) and the cell suspension is pipetted up and down to ensure proper mixing
and flushed over the surface of the flask. The cell suspension is transferred to a 15 mL
falcon tube and a small amount of approximately 100 µL is aliquoted for counting. The
cells are then centrifuged at 200 x g for 4 minutes. Meanwhile, cell counting is performed.
The supernatant was then discarded and the pellet was resuspended in huVEC medium.
The amount of medium added is determined by the total amount of cells counted and the
adequate seeding density for HUVECs which is approximately 6,000 cells/cm2 (450,000 cells
in a T75 flask). Once the cells have been resuspended, the gelatin solution is aspirated
from the previously coated flask. For seeding, if hypothetically 1 mL of the cell suspension
contains approximately 450,000 cells, then it is transferred to the new flask and 9 mL of
the fresh huVEC medium is added, bringing the total volume to 10 mL (seeding density:
45,000 cells/mL). The flask is moved around in a figure-eight pattern so that the cells spread
21
==PAGE:33==
accross the entire flask surface. Finally, the flask is placed in the incubator at 37 ◦C and 5%
CO2. The medium is changed every 2 days.
3.1.2
Fibroblast cell line
The fibroblast cell line used for bioprinting was BJ-5ta(CRL-4001) from ATCC and is an
hTERT-immortalized fibroblast cell that was isolated from the foreskin of a male patient [70].
The cell line was deposited by Geron Corporation. BJ-5ta culture was carried out under
sterile conditions in a LAF bench and the cells were incubated at 37 ◦C, 100% humidity,
5% CO2.
Cells were cultured in Dubbelco’s Modified Eagle Media (DMEM)(Gibco,Cat.
No.:61965-026) with 10% Fetal Bovine Serum (FBS) and 1% P/S. Cells were passaged and
harvested at 70-90% confluency and the number of passages did not exceed 20.
The subculturing procedure for fibroblasts was very similar to the one described for
CI-huVEC in 3.1.1 with some variations. For culturing BJ-5ta, a T175 flask was used, since
fibroblasts have a higher proliferation rate than endothelial cells, and flask coating was not
required. Therefore, the quantities specified in the protocol described in 3.1.1 for a T75 flask
should be adjusted accordingly for a T175 flask. Moreover, the recommended cell density
for this cell line by ATCC was 3000-5000 cells/cm2.
3.2
SHAPE composite material preparation
3.2.1
Alginate microparticle generation
For the preparation of alginate-based microparticles, 1% w/v alginate solution (80-120
cP, Wako) was mixed in MilliQ water in a 100 mL glass jar for two hours using a magnetic
stirrer and a 80 ◦C water bath, ensuring that the magnet had the same size as the 100 mL
glass jar. In order for the temperature to reach and remain at 80 ◦C, a piece of aluminum
foil was placed above the water bath. Meanwhile, a 2 mg/mL CaCO3 (PlasmaChem GmbH,
Cat. No.: PL-CACOU-100g) solution was prepared in MilliQ water in a blue cap bottle and
a 1M NaOH solution was also prepared in MilliQ water using a sterile beaker. After 2 hours,
the alginate solution was mixed with the 2 mg/mL CaCO3 solution in a 1:1 ratio and stirred
for one hour at room temperature on the magnetic stirrer. As previously, the magnet had
to be the same size as the 100 mL glass jar. To induce gelation, after one hour, acetic acid
(99.9%) was added in a ratio of 1:500 and the solution was left to stir overnight at 650 rpm.
It was observed that gelation started immediately, while after two hours the solution already
appeared more viscous.
The next day, the gelled alginate solution was mechanically fragmented into microparti-
cles by homogenization at 15,000 rpm for 10 minutes using a T25 Ultra-Turrax homogenizer
(IKA) equipped with S25N-10G dispensing tool inside a LAF bench. Next, the fragmented
alginate slurry was divided evenly into 50 mL falcon tubes and centrifuged at 18,500 x g
for 20 minutes at room temperature in an ultra-speed centrifuge with a carbon fiber rotor.
A pipette was used to aspirate and discard carefully the supernatant without losing the gel
from the bottom. Lastly, the gel was resuspended in DMEM with 1% P/S at twice the
22
==PAGE:34==
amount of the alginate slurry. Moreover, 0.5% chloroform was added to the alginate slurry
to make it sterile and the pH was adjusted to 7 by adding 1M NaOH dropwise. The color
should turn from yellow to orange/red and the pH was tested on a pH strip. The slurry was
incubated overnight and was stored in the fridge until further use.
3.2.2
SHAPE composite formulation
Before bioprinting, the alginate microparticles were homogenized at 15,000 rpm for 3
minutes then, centrifuged at 18,500 x g for 10 minutes at room temperature maintaining the
same setup as previously mentioned. After centrifugation, the supernatant was aspirated
with a pipette and the alginate microparticles were stored in the refrigerator for subsequent
use.
The day of bioprinting,a 24-well plate was coated with Biosurfine (Tokyo Gosei, BIO-
SURFINE TM) to prevent cells from adhering to the polystyrene plate.
Biosurfine was
mixed with MilliQ water and 96% ethanol in a 1:1:1.5 ratio. The plate was UV-cured for 30
minutes at room temperature in a UV-cure box (Wicked Engineering CUREbox UV). The
plate was then kept in the fridge to cool during the SHAPE formulation process.
For the generation of the SHAPE composite, a 5 mg/mL stock of collagen type I was used
(Cultrex rat collagen I, R&D Systems, Cat. No.: 3447-020-01). The collagen stock solution
was diluted to reach a final concentration of 1 mg/mL and was neutralized by adding 4%
HEPES and 4% NaHCO3.
The alginate microparticles were mixed with the neutralized
collagen in a 2:1 ratio. For example, 3 mL of SHAPE composite consists of 2 mL of alginate
microparticles with 1 mL of neutralized collagen (0.12 mL NaHCO3, 0.12 mL HEPES, 0.16
mL of growth medium, and 0.6 mL of collagen).
In some bioprinting experiments, the
addition of fibronectin (Roche Diagnostics GmbH, Cat. No.:10838039001) was studied. In
these cases, fibronectin was added to the alginate slurry at a concentration of 5 µg/mL.
The procedure of SHAPE composite formulation was performed on ice to avoid collagen
polymerization and also, under sterile conditions in a LAF bench. Once the composite was
ready, it was transferred into the previously coated and cooled well plate and the plate was
centrifuged at 700 x g for 15 minutes at 5 ◦C to eliminate any bubbles from the SHAPE
composite. After centrifugation, the plate was placed in the refrigerator until bioprinting.
3.3
Bioink preparation
3.3.1
Fluorescent ink preparation
For optimizing the printing parameters of the procedure, a fluorescent ink was used to
conserve cell stock and gain familiarity with the bioprinting process. The ink was prepared
in a mixing cup. One mL of ink contained: 166 µL of Geltrex (Gibco, Cat. No.:A14133-02),
250 µL of 1% Xanthan Gum (Sigma Aldrich, Cat. No.: 11138-66-2) and 584 µL of fluorescent
beads (Duke Scientific Corporation, 5 µm, green, Cat. No.:G0500). The ink was speedmixed
at 2500 rpm for 30 seconds to eliminate bubbles.
23
==PAGE:35==
To load the ink, a 1 mL Hamilton syringe was used and attached to a 400 µm conical
plastic nozzle (Cellink, 22G, NZ3220005001). A small amount of alginate (≈100-200 µL)
was loaded into the syringe to fill the dead volume and drawn up and down within the
syringe and nozzle to remove any trapped bubbles. After ensuring that no air was trapped
in the nozzle, the ink was loaded into the syringe using the same conical nozzle and avoiding
again the inlet of air bubbles into the syringe. Subsequently, the nozzle was removed and a
0.5 inch 200 µm clear needle (Cellink, 27G, NZ5270505001) was attached to the syringe.
3.3.2
Cell-laden bioink preparation
Before starting bioink preparation, all equipment, including syringes, needles, nozzles,
and mixing cups, was sterilized under UV light for 30 minutes. Cells were harvested from
culture according to the protocol described in 3.1.1.
The cell density of the bioink was
adjusted to 10·106 cells/mL by first counting the cells and then calculating the total volume
of the bioink based on the desired cell concentration. The cells were then resuspended in
growth medium and maintained in the 15 mL Falcon tube until the bioink preparation.
The bioink was prepared in the same Falcon tube where cells were kept to avoid any po-
tential cell loss by transferring the cells. This approach allowed better control of the bubbles
in the bioink, as air bubbles remained at the bottom of the Falcon tube, and therefore it was
easier to avoid their inlet into the syringe. The bioink preparation took place under sterile
conditions in a LAF bench on ice. Two different bioink formulations were used throughout
the project, with the main difference being whether xanthan gum was included. Therefore,
one mL of bioink containing xanthan gum was made of: 166 µL Geltrex, 250 µL of 1% Xan-
than gum and 584 µL of warm huVEC medium containing 1% P/S and live HUVECs, while
the bioink without xanthan gum was made of: 166 µL Geltrex and 834 µL of warm huVEC
medium containing 1% P/S and live HUVECs. In one bioprinting experiment, fibroblasts
were printed instead of HUVECs. The bioink formulation of this experiment was exactly the
same as that including xanthan gum, the only difference being that live fibroblasts were used
instead of live HUVECs at the same cell concentration and using the same growth medium.
To load the bioink, a 1 mL sterile Hamilton syringe, a 400 µm nozzle, and a needle were
used. Two different needle sizes were investigated for bioprinting, one being the 0.5 inch 200
µm needle that was also used for the fluorescent ink and the other being a 0.5 inch 400 µm
blue needle (Cellink, 22G, NZ5220505001). As described for the fluorescent bioink, alginate
was loaded into the syringe to fill the dead volume and then the bioink was loaded in the
same way avoiding the inlet of any air bubbles.

4.1
2D cultures of HUVECs and fibroblasts
Prior to initializing the bioprinting process, it was crucial to familiarize ourselves with
the characteristics and behaviors of the chosen cell types, HUVECs and fibroblasts, through-
out the culturing process. These cells were chosen for bioprinting due to their fundamental
role in the formation of vascular tissue as described in 2.2. By culturing them under stan-
dard conditions, observing their growth patterns, interactions, and morphology, we gained
a better understanding of their phenotypic differences and how they establish connections.

Therefore, as observed in Figures 17,18, HUVECs exhibited their cobblestone-like mor-

4.2
Printing with fluorescent beads
The first goal of this project was to optimize the bioprinting process to fabricate a
vascular tissue with a diameter of 100 µm. To achieve this, we used embedded bioprinting
with the SHAPE composite as a support bath in a 12-well plate and a fluorescent ink. A
systematic study was performed, allowing us to determine optimal bioprinting parameters
such as extrusion rate before switching to experiments with cell-laden bioinks.
Initially, we tested four arbitrarily selected extrusion rates-150, 200, 300, and 350
nL/mm, as we deemed them a reasonable starting point.
We aimed for a low printing
speed to maintain shape fidelity and high resolution, thus, the printing speed was set to 1
mm/s. Moreover, we chose to print with a 200 µm diameter needle to achieve the thinnest
possible filaments. However, as the diameter of the needle decreases, the shear stress within
the needle increases. Therefore, in later experiments, we investigated the size of the needle
as a parameter to confirm that a 200 µm diameter needle does not compromise cell viability.

bar= 100 µm.

For all subsequent images, all the diameters measured correspond to the same reference

extrusion rates below 150 nL/mm should be examined under the same conditions. Moreover,
what was already apparent from the images is now supported by the calculated SDs, which
prove that the structure printed at 150 nL/mm has the lowest deviation and the highest
print accuracy.

5
Discussion
The aim of this thesis was to develop a bioprinting method for fabricating a vascular
tissue with a diameter of 100 µm that can sprout into smaller capillaries with diameters
ranging between 10-100 µm forming a vascular network. To achieve this objective, we used
embedded 3D bioprinting of HUVECs into the SHAPE support matrix composed of alginate
and type I collagen.
The first objective of this project was to determine the optimal printing parameters to
construct a structure with a diameter of 100 µm. The utilization of embedded bioprint-
ing allowed the creation of small-scale filaments, overcoming the drawbacks of conventional
extrusion-based bioprinting. To achieve this objective, we optimized the bioprinting process
by conducting experimental trials using fluorescent and cell-laden bioinks. We investigated
different extrusion rates ranging from 20-350 nL/mm and two different needle sizes with
diameters 200 and 400 µm. The printing speed was constant in all bioprinting experiments
and set at 1 mm/s. We chose to proceed with a low printing speed to achieve greater con-
trol over the bioink deposition on the support matrix, which ensured higher resolution and
shape fidelity. Furthermore, it should be noted that with lower printing speeds, less pressure
is required to extrude the bioink, thus decreasing the shear stress applied to cells during
extrusion and enhancing cell viability. The results of the optimization process allowed us to
conclude that adjusting the extrusion rate at 20 nL/mm, the printing speed at 1 mm/s, the
cell density at 10·106 cells/mL and the strand height and width at 100 µm using a 200 µm
needle allows filament fabrication with a width of 115.6 ± 16.0 µm (n=3, mean ± SD).
The second objective was to maintain cell viability for a prolonged culture period of
1-2 weeks. Viability tests were performed investigating the effect of the extrusion rate and
needle diameter on cell viability and exploring whether the supply of nutrients and oxygen
to the printed constructs was sufficient. Performing a cell count on the images in Figure 24,
we observed a small decrease in the survival rate (%) with an increase in the extrusion rate.
This small trend was also expected from the literature, since an increase in extrusion rate
requires also more pressure to extrude the bioink from the needle, and therefore subjects
cells to higher shear forces. Furthermore, we investigated the impact of needle size on cell
viability. As shown in Figure 28, cell viability is higher for the 400 µm needle than for the
200 µm needle, which confirms that a smaller needle size results in a higher velocity gradient,
thus increasing shear stress and cell damage [71]. However, the difference in survival rate
was approximately 10% for the two different needle sizes, thus we chose to proceed with a
small trade-off in survival rate to achieve the desired filament width of 100 µm. In addition,
we explored the effect of gel volume on cell viability in relation to the diffusion of oxygen
and nutrients. Figure 26 revealed that gel volume did not have an effect on cell viability.
This project established that printed HUVECs lines remained viable in the support matrix
for an extended culture period of 1-2 weeks.
Despite the fact that bioprinted HUVECs maintained high cell viability even after 11
days in culture, it became evident that no intercellular connections formed within this time
50
==PAGE:62==
frame. All figures of bioprinted constructs present HUVECs as individual dots, indicating
the lack of cell-cell adhesion and consequently the lack of lumen formation. These results are
contradictory to what we anticipated from literature where HUVECs organize themselves to
form capillary-like tubes in 3D collagen matrices [5], as presented in Figure 5.
To address the question whether the lack of adhesion to the ECM matrix was an issue
related to the HUVECs behavior inside the matrix or whether it reflected a matrix-related
limitation, we bioprinted with fibroblasts under the same conditions. After 3 days in culture,
it became clear that the fibroblasts formed spheroid-like aggregates. Despite the adaptability
of the fibroblasts to less favorable ECM environments, we observed that the fibroblasts did
not adhere to the ECM composite indicating a necessity for matrix optimization.
The cause behind the lack of cell adhesion to the ECM support for both cell types
remains uncertain. In an attempt to answer this question, while observing the adhesion of
HUVECs to the ECM, we first hypothesized that lumen formation might require a basement
membrane-like environment, thus suggesting the implementation of type IV collagen in the
matrix instead of type I. However, the inability of fibroblasts to form cell-matrix connections
indicated that the collagen type is not the cause of improper cell adhesion, since type I col-
lagen is one of the main products of fibroblasts [72]. Consequently, we focused our attention
on the properties of the matrix as a potential factor preventing cell-matrix adhesion. The
presence of alginate in the support matrix in a 2:1 ratio with collagen could be a contributing
factor that influences cell behavior. Despite the valuable properties of alginate as a bioma-
terial, such as biocompatibility, it is inherently bioinert and lacks adhesion sites to interact
with cells. Therefore, to further study the role of alginate in this issue, future investigations
could explore the modification of alginate with the incorporation of cell binding sites such as
RGD (Arg-Gly-Asp) peptides and the increase of collagen-to-alginate ratio. However, such
an alteration in the concentrations of collagen and alginate of the SHAPE support would
likely require additional fine-tuning of the rheological properties.
One of the main objectives of this thesis was to explore the effect of support factors such
as VEGF on vascular development and angiogenic sprouting. Literature suggests that vascu-
lar development occurs in response to proangiogenic factors such as VEGF and therefore we
anticipated that by adding VEGF to the bioprinted filaments, we would observe HUVECs
forming a lumen and also branching out of their stable sites to form a capillary-like network
with diameters ranging from 10-100 µm. Therefore, the addition of VEGF was explored at
different concentrations of 0, 2, 5 and 20 ng/mL. In Figure 30 we observed localized expan-
sions after the addition of VEGF for all concentrations investigated. However, HUVECs still
appeared as discrete dots, demonstrating the lack of intercellular connections, and therefore
we decided to remove xanthan gum from the bioink formulation. After the removal of the
xanthan gum, the localized swelling behavior was not replicated in Figures 32, 34, 36,and
38, where VEGF was still present at the same concentrations. Consequently, we concluded
that in this project, the addition of VEGF did not promote lumen formation and angiogenic
sprouting. In the future, the experiment using VEGF alongside with xanthan gum should
be repeated, which was not feasible due to time limitations. Furthermore, alternative ap-
51
==PAGE:63==
proaches of VEGF delivery could also be studied, such as the incorporation of growth factors
into the matrix or the bioink instead of the top of the gel or the addition of more than one
factors such as bFGF and PDGF to ensure that the lack of vascular development in this
project was not caused by the insufficient diffusion of VEGF within the support matrix.
Lastly, to mitigate the problem of weak intercellular connections, we investigated the
addition of fibronectin to the support matrix in conjunction with the removal of xanthan
gum from the bioink. Fibronectin is an adhesive glycoprotein that facilitates the interaction
between the ECM and cells. By interacting with cell surface receptors, such as integrins and
syndecans, fibronectin promotes cell migration, adhesion, and differentiation [73]. There-
fore, based on the literature, we decided to investigate the effect of fibronectin on vascular
development. Simultaneously, concerned that xanthan gum inhibits cell migration, prolif-
eration, and connection, we decided to remove it from the bioink formulation. From this
bioprinting experiment, we observed that neither the removal of xanthan gum nor the addi-
tion of fibronectin had a positive effect on vascular development. More specifically, although
brightfield images suggested that connections might develop across the bioprinted cell lines;
fluorescent imaging established that despite the addition of fibronectin and the removal of
xanthan gum, cells continued to appear as individual dots. Moreover, the alterations made
in the bioprinting procedure resulted in gel instability, leading to poor shape fidelity, as seen
in Figure 36.
An issue that arose during the experimental procedure was the existence of bubbles in
the bioprinted structures. Most of these bubbles were created during the SHAPE compos-
ite formulation, which lead us to to add the extra step of centrifuging the SHAPE-loaded
plate prior to bioprinting to the optimization protocol. Not all bubbles were eliminated by
centrifugation, as some also formed within the bioink during its preparation and during the
incubation period after bioprinting.
Furthermore, another problem that we encountered was the detachment of the gel from
the bottom of the well. This issue made media changes, fixation, and staining considerably
difficult procedures, because the medium was on the bottom and the gel on the top of
the well, and therefore, to aspirate the medium, it was inevitable to pass through the gel.
Centrifugation resolved the issue as centrifuging the plates before printing, the issue ceased
to occur.
Another critical challenge that emerged was the staining of bioprinted filaments when the
cell-laden constructs were fixed. The plates of multiple bioprintings were fixed and stained for
imaging. However, DAPI and phalloidin staining generated either a weak or no signal in the
fixed plates. The reason behind this remains uncertain, but one possible explanation could
be that the staining buffer did not diffuse through the support bath efficiently. To address
this challenge, we investigated different approaches such as overnight staining, increase in
stain concentrations, and the use of alginase to degrade the alginate slurry to facilitate the
staining buffer diffusion. Despite our efforts, time constraints did not allow us to resolve the
problem and optimize the staining process. An approach to resolve the issue could be to
52
==PAGE:64==
decrease the SHAPE gel’s thickness to facilitate the diffusion of the staining buffer. Figure
26 showed that cells remained viable in a lower and higher volume of SHAPE support, thus
indicating that the same bioprinting experiments could be repeated in 0.3 mL instead of 0.5
mL of gel volume.
In conclusion, this thesis presents the potential of using embedded bioprinting to fabri-
cate small-diameter vasculature and a capillary-like network into the SHAPE support bath
for an extended culture period. While the main objective of achieving a lumen formation and
angiogenic sprouting was not fully accomplished, our research provided valuable insights into
the complex interplay between ECM properties, bioink composition, effective growth factor
delivery and cellular responses.
Further studies should focus on the mechanisms behind
the cell-matrix interactions, as well as the effect of mechanical and rheological properties
of the SHAPE matrix on cell migration, proliferation, and adhesion to promote vascular
development and angiogenesis.

6
Conclusion
This thesis studied the application of embedded bioprinting into the SHAPE support
bath aiming to develop a new method for fabricating small-diameter vascular tissues, focusing
on maintaining extended viability and inducing angiogenic sprouting. The study established
a strong fabrication method for bioprinting HUVECs to construct cell-laden filaments of 100
µm diameter within the alginate/collagen support bath, indicating long-term cell viability
but falling short of forming intercellular connections and capillary-like network formation.
Despite this, the study offered valuable insights into the key role of matrix properties and
bioink composition on the cell behavior within a 3D environment.
The research first established the optimal bioprinting parameters to construct cell-laden
filaments with a diameter of 100 µm. Therefore, it was concluded that using a needle with a
diameter of 200 µm and adjusting the extrusion rate at 20 nL/mm, the printing speed at 1
mm/s and the cell density at 10·106 cells/mL, bioprinting of a vascular tissue with a diameter
of 100 µm can be achieved. Furthermore, it revealed that despite the fact that SHAPE
composite provided a stable environment to maintain viability of the bioprinted filaments
over an extended culture period, ECM properties need to be further investigated in order
to achieve cell migration, proliferation and adhesion. Despite the addition of VEGF, no
angiogenic sprouting was observed, which suggested that VEGF diffusion and bioavailability
might be limited within the matrix. Furthermore, the comparison with fibroblasts, which
did not demonstrate cell-matrix adhesion, emphasized the existence of matrix-related issues
such as the inability of alginate to interact with cells, suggesting potential modifications of
the ECM support bath such as the incorporation of RGD peptides or the adjustment of the
collagen-to-alginate ratio.
Moving forward, investigating different VEGF delivery methods and the addition of
more growth factors, exploring the potential of co-culturing HUVECs and fibroblasts, and
implementing dynamic culture conditions could pose potential research questions that would
shed light on the next steps. Overall, this work provides valuable insights into the needs of
vascularization in engineered tissues and the factors that hinder the creation of a perfusable,
functional vascular network.
