Abstract
Solving dynamical systems can be a computationally expensive task. Therefore, it is of great
value to investigate methods that can decrease the computational complexity of classical numerical
solvers. One approach is the reduced order modeling techniques, which can learn the dynamics
of a given system from data and use this to reduce the dimensionality of the governing equations.
However, learning model dynamics from data can be difficult, especially considering extrapolation
of nonlinear dynamical systems.
This thesis explores if and how computations of nonlinear partial differential equations can be
accelerated through the model order reduction technique, proper orthogonal decomposition (POD),
and how time­regression can be performed through non­intrusive methods. Moreover, this thesis
develops a novel reduction technique for the non­intrusive method, dynamic mode decomposition
(DMD), along with its extensions ­ the exact DMD and the extended DMD.
This thesis will furthermore introduce a new way of selecting dictionaries for the extended DMD,
and explain how to circumvent large matrix computations through singular value decompositions.
With the purpose of investigating numerical speedup, errors, and robustness, the methods will be
analyzed through 1­dimensional linear and nonlinear test equations. Here, there will be conducted
an investigation into how the data driven methods are affected by numerical errors in the training
set. Further investigations of the latent trajectories of the DMD methods will also be performed.
Followingly, the non­intrusive methods are tested on a highly nonlinear free surface of water. An
investigation into the latent trajectories alongside an error correcting extension will be performed.
From the development of a full order numerical scheme to non­intrusive reduced order models,
this thesis therefore provides an insight into how nonlinear dynamics can be estimated, and how
they affect model order reduction and time­regression techniques.

Introduction
In many applications, solving dynamical systems is a key part of understanding the physical behav­
ior of certain objects. In fluid mechanics, it can, e.g., be of great interest to know how the surface of
water behaves under various conditions. Dynamical systems explaining, e.g., free surface waves,
can be modeled via nonlinear time­dependent partial differential equations. These solvers can be
very costly to execute using classical numerical solvers, often resulting in multiple days worth of
simulation.
Therefore, it is highly desirable to speed up computations using data­driven models. The approach
taken in this thesis is considering a dimensionality reduction by using collected data to decrease
simulation time significantly. These methods works by intrinsically learning spatial system dynam­
ics to define a small set of basis functions, which can then be propagated through time. Especially
for nonlinear time­dependent problems, however, finding such a reduced basis that can properly re­
solve system dynamics is not so easy. A part of this thesis, therefore, investigates possible methods
for overcoming the challenge of nonlinear system dynamics.
Determining a basis for model reduction still leaves a time dependent system to be solved. In many
applications, the system dynamics are readily available to implement the reduced system. In other
cases, however, the system dynamics can be unknown. For example, if one were to learn dynam­
ics of observed data with complicated coupled differential equations, and/or possibly depending
on subsystems, it can be an advantage to also learn temporal system dynamics.
This thesis investigates the fully nonlinear potential flow problem for determining the free surface
of a body of water with a flat sea bed. This problem is not only nonlinear but is coupled with equa­
tions for the velocity potential in the entire body of water. This, therefore, motivates the use of
time­series discovery of the latent dynamics that were found from a basis reduction technique. Be­
cause the free surface is a dimension lower than the velocity potential problem, learning the model
dynamics intrinsically from data could potentially reduce the problem by an entire dimension.
1.1
Literature Review
This review dives into two different types of reduced order modeling (ROM) ­ intrusive and non­
intrusive. The latter of which seeks to find model dynamics completely without any prior physical
knowledge about the system. I.e., non­intrusive methods learn the time series as well as spatial
dynamics. This thesis, therefore, desires to determine a non­intrusive method to approximate free
surfaces found in the fully nonlinear potential flow equations, as mentioned previously.
Intrusive ROMs refer to the notion that the governing equations must be known in order to make
a model reduction. Thus, a reduced basis can be written into the equations, and from that, a solver
to the reduced problem can be formulated. There have been proposed a variety of different ways
to construct such a basis that is used in an intrusive solver. In this review, they have been split
up into two categories ­ linear and nonlinear reduction methods. The difference between the two
refers to the low­dimensional solution manifold they construct and is useful when considering the
dynamics of the governing equations. In general, one would want to have a non­linear manifold
construction to best represent nonlinear dynamics.
A linear reduction method that is widely used is the Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD)
[1], [2], [3]. The POD reduction is constructed via a Galerkin projection onto a low dimensional
manifold constructed by computing an SVD basis on a snapshot matrix computed by, e.g., a full
order method (FOM).

Proposed nonlinear reduction methods can also be extensions of the POD method, where e.g. a
Kernel­POD method is proposed in [4] that applies a kernel function to the original solution man­
ifold that creates a snapshot matrix for the POD reduction to be performed on. Other methods
of nonlinear reduction include the Discrete Empirical Interpolation Method (DEIM) [5]. DEIM
works by interpolating the nonlinear terms of a PDE in much fewer points than the full nonlinear
evaluation. It can thus be seen as an extension of the POD method, as it can reduce the nonlinear
terms to the same dimension as the linear term. Moreover, extensions to the DEIM procedure have
been proposed, such as the Randomized DEIM (R­DEIM) procedure [6]. This newly proposed
method utilizes various randomized algorithms to achieve a speed­up of the DEIM procedures
while it shows to achieve a similar error.
Alongside the neural network ”boom”, auto­encoders [7], [8] are another method that has arisen
for nonlinear model reduction, as neural networks by design achieve nonlinear manifolds thorugh
their nonlinear activation functions. Especially Convolutional auto­encoders (CAE) [9] are being
used to retain structured information of the solution [10], [11]. From the CAE structure, there have
been developments in using a compact decoder and over­collocation methods [12] to improve the
speed and precision of the CAEs. The compact decoder, or shallow decoder, is a decoder trained to
mimic the full decoders capabilities while containing much fewer parameters. Over­collocation is a
point selection method that increases performance by training the autoencoder on these. However,
a drawback of utilizing neural networks as a reduced basis method is its inability to extrapolate
from training data [13]. Variational Autoencoders (VAE) [14] used in e.g. [15], [16], and [17] is
a type of neural network that is better at estimating future state predictions, as it is trained to have
a probabilistic latent space. This makes out­of­data latent variables assume a known structure for
the decoder.
Non­Intrusive ROMs refer to the notion that the governing equations do not need to be known,
such that they can produce interpolation solutions by learning the dynamics of the system from the
data set alone. Some of the proposed methods in this section do not intrinsically act as a ROM,
but they can be conjoined with reduction techniques explained for intrusive ROMs, such that it is
possible to set up and learn system dynamics on a low­dimensional manifold without having to
construct problem specific solvers.
One method that works to achieve this is the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) approach [18],
[2]. This approach is based on Koopman operator theory and can, via a snapshot matrix, propagate
the dynamics of observed data. The Koopman operator is a linear but infinite operator that can
describe nonlinear dynamics [19], which is why approximations of this are sought after when es­
timating the nonlinear dynamics of a PDE. DMD type models are restricted to train on uniformly
distributed data, but can find closed form continuous solution for time regression. This method
furthermore comes with a lot of extensions [20] that work toward making the DMD robust, effi­
cient, or more precise. Robustness of the DMD algorithm can be obtained through e.g. adjusting
the DMD bases [21], [22], or implementing the exact DMD approach [2], [23], which adjusts the
column space used for basis reconstruction. The efficiency of the DMD algorithm can be achieved
by employing randomized algorithms such as a randomized SVD [2] for basis generation, utilizing
multiple CPU cores for a parallelized algorithm, or implementing a multi­fidelity DMD [24]. A
method that works toward ensuring more accuracy ­ especially in a nonlinear dynamical system
is the extended DMD (eDMD) [25]. Unlike, e.g., the exact DMD, the eDMD also approximates
the Koopman eigenfunctions through a predefined dictionary. The choice of dictionary can vary,
but, e.g., a kernelized method [26] has been developed to overcome high­dimensional dictionaries.
Other methods, such as dictionary learning techniques [27], have also been investigated, giving a
more robust model while sacrificing computing time.
In many works, such as the original and exact DMD, the Koopman eigenfunctions are assumed
to be identity. Other extensions that work toward more precision have also been developed. E.g.

the High Order DMD (HODMD) [10], which employs a time­delay embedding to better explain
nonlinear dynamics, or introducing the Adjoint Koopman Operator [20], [28] to simultaneously
estimate forwards and backwards dynamics.
Another approach to achieve a non­intrusive solver is to train a Feed Forward Neural Network
(FFNN) to understand the dynamics in the data [29]. A FFNN can be many things ­ in a way, it is
simply noting passing an object through a neural network. However, for non­intrusive modeling,
the FFNN seeks a solution to the 1­step system u(ti+1) = f(u(ti)), where f is approximated via
a neural network [29], [17]. Like with DMD­type methods, this approach is limited to fixed time
steps so that temporal data have to be uniformly distributed. An upside to choosing a technique like
this is the inherent nonlinearity that a neural network possesses, giving it an advantage in learning
nonlinear temporal dynamics. It does, however, inherit the drawbacks of neural networks, making
it slow to train and difficult to extract good extrapolations. Choosing to work with neural networks,
these hurdles have to be overcome in some other way.
A fairly new technique using neural networks for time­series regression developed in 2019 is the
Neural ODE [17], which is also a non­intrusive way of learning dynamics in data. This approach
has proven to be especially useful for learning nonlinear dynamics. As an extension to the FFNN,
the purpose of a Neural ODE is to determine continuous­time dynamics as opposed to 1­step dy­
namics for some fixed step size. Therefore, a benefit of the Neural ODE is that training data does
not need to be uniformly distributed, as some other methods require ­ e.g. a FFNN and various
DMD extensions. Another important note from the original paper is that gradients of the loss term
can be computed through a forward pass in the network, circumventing the costly backwards pass
through an ODE solver. However, because a forward pass is required through each step in an
ODE solver, one epoch of training often results in a lot of forward­passes through a possibly quite
complicated network, making the Neural ODE an extremely costly model to train.
Physics Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) [30] is a conjunction between the non­intrusive and
intrusive ROMs that has gained some attention recently. When saying that they work in between
the two, it is a way of saying that there is no need to construct a specialized solver for the reduced
basis, but the dynamics need to be known to construct a physics loss such that the network can learn
the dynamics of the governing equations. PINNs are not inherently a ROM method, but if they
are applied with some of the methods discussed previously, they can be used as a data evolution
method in a low­dimensional manifold. E.g. this is proposed in [31] where they combined a Neural
ODE with a physics informed loss to create a Physics Informed Neural ODE (PINODE) that could
describe nonlinear dynamics of the governing equations quite well.
Summarizing the literature review, it is clear that many of the methods that specialize in nonlinear
dynamic reconstruction are quite computationally expensive in the offline stage while being fast
in the online stage. While this is a general cost for reduced order modeling, methods that utilize
matrix decompositions for basis construction are often much faster in the offline stage. Depending
on the chosen model, it is possibly also faster in the online stage as well.

1.2
Structure of the thesis
This thesis follows a structure inspired by a previous thesis done by Claus L. Jensen [32]. This
thesis starts by introducing the theory behind the selected methods:
• Spectral method for full order solvers using Legendre polynomials
• POD and SVD for dimensionality reduction
• Exact DMD and extended DMD for time­series prediction
In chapter 1, the theory behind spectral methods are explained, as well as a stability analysis for
time integration schemes. Chapter 2 goes through reduced basis methods ­ herein different SVD
techniques leading to the POD formulation. Chapter 3 deals with non­intrusive methods, where
the exact DMD and extended DMD are presented, as well as a novel reduction technique for DMD
methods. Chapters 4 and 5 are testing methods on linear and nonlinear test equations respectively.
In the last chapter, the two non­intrusive methods (exact DMD and extended DMD) will be tested
on a free surface of water, modeled by the fully nonlinear potential flow equations.
Throughout this thesis, all computing times have been done on the same computer system to assure
that any conclusions based on comparisons between models are correct.

Reduced Basis Methods
Reduced Basis (RB) methods are used to explain a data set with fewer data points than the original
data set by finding the underlying structure in the data set. In this thesis, they are used to construct
ROMs for PDE type problems which reduce the full order system by one or more dimensions,
thus constructing a small set of ODEs that are much easier and faster to solve. The RB method
covered in this chapter is the proper orthogonal decomposition (POD). To explain the POD method,
however, the chapter first discusses different singular value decomposition techniques.

4
Non­intrusive methods
In this chapter, we will discuss different data­driven time­series regression methods that can be
used without knowing the governing equations of the PDE system. Non­intrusive methods are
beneficial for solutions of systems where the governing equations are not known, e.g., for measured
but structured data of a dynamical system. However, they can also be a useful tool for achieving
generality in methods, as some non­intrusive methods do not need to be as tailored to the system
as conventional numerical schemes. Combined with ROM techniques, these non­intrusive solvers
aim to achieve a generalized model that gains a numerical speedup compared to conventional
numerical solvers.
This chapter explains and derives the different non­intrusive regression schemes and how they can
be implemented on a reduced basis.
4.1

4.1.1
Special properties
The DMD gives a set of eigenvalues that are the only component used to predict the time series.
Therefore, we can derive a special property for specific types of solutions. Consider some eigen­
value λ, and lift it to the power i. For i →∞, λ can basically behave in 3 ways. If |λ| < 1,

u(x, t) with a bounded image will most likely not contain any true Koopman eigenvalues with an

4.3.2
Choosing a dictionary
Finding a dictionary that correctly spans all of the eigenfunctions proves quite difficult. In the
original paper [25], they proposed using several sets of basis functions, such as the Hermitian
polynomials, radial basis functions, and Jacobi polynomials. However, these were only tested
on low­dimensional ODE systems and require cross­products between function evaluations of the
coordinates. For PDE problems, this method is intractable. This has, therefore, sparked the interest
in using kernelized methods [26] to create the dictionary instead. The kernel methods, however,
require a fair amount of knowledge about the dynamics of the problem, as the chosen kernel gives
very different results. Other methods, such as a dictionary learning algorithm [27], have been
proposed. This paper proposes a neural network to create a dictionary, from which they formulate
a Jacobian to avoid back­propagating through an eigenvalue decomposition. If one employs this
technique, it could potentially be possible to define an encoder as the dictionary, making k ≪m
instead of the other way around. From the formulation of an eDMD, this also eliminates the need
of a decoder, as the eDMD method creates full state modes directly from the encoded data. An
extension of the dictionary learning technique developed in [27] could therefore be an approach
for future investigations.
However, we will draw inspiration from the Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy)
method developed in [2], [42], which for PDEs builds a dictionary of all combinations of deriva­
tives and nonlinear multiplications up to some order p and q respectively. E.g. if p = 1 and q = 2,
then one could for a 1­dimensional problem have the combinations {u, ∂xu, u2, (∂xu)2, u∂xu}.
SINDy is an equation discovery model that through a sequential thresholding algorithm cancels
out irrelevant terms for the dynamics of a model. Through the same choice of dictionary for the
eDMD model, it is of interest to see how this method can sort out irrelevant terms without explicitly
canceling them out.

When discussing the advection equation, the eDMD was verified to work as an exact DMD when
choosing the dictionary DI. However, for nonlinear problems, the exact DMD was showcased
as not being optimal. Therefore, in this section, we will try and apply the dictionary ˜Dp,q for
p = q = 2 to the eDMD algorithm and see if the results are improved.
To do so, we first gather snapshots into the matrices U and U ′, which corresponds to the matrices
D and D′ from the exact DMD. After each operation in the dictionary has been made, a rescaling
is put on each chunk so that each dictionary element carries the same weight, thus avoiding any 1
operation dominating the singular value spectrum. Once all operations are computed, the matrices
NU and NU′ can be determined. As mentioned previously in the thesis, the derivatives are found
numerically using high order differentiation with Legendre polynomials. Figure 6.19 shows the
spectrum of NU which converges much slower than the spectrum for the exact DMD (Figure 6.5).
This also means that to resolve the space of the eDMD properly, one need to consider more than
the 15 POD modes used in the exact DMD. However, it also shows that numerical errors of the
FOM solver mean a great deal when synthesizing data for the dictionary.

8
Conclusion and Future Work
The work in this thesis focused on finding viable reduced order model (ROM) techniques to obtain
faster computing times than a full order method (FOM) would otherwise be able to. The work
extended ROM techniques to nonlinear time­dependent equations, with the goal of finding a non­
intrusive ROM technique to estimate free surfaces of water.
The chosen technique to compute model reductions was the proper orthogonal decomposition
(POD), which projects full solutions onto a small latent space. From this basis creation, the thesis
chose to use an exact dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) as well as an extended DMD (eDMD)
to perform time­regression of data without knowing the underlying dynamics.
For the choice of FOM, this thesis chose to use a spectral solver using Legendre polynomials to
produce accurate data. To analyze how small data errors affect the models, the models were also
trained on analytical solutions to the selected partial differential equations (PDE).
In chapter 2, the thesis explained how to develop differentiation and interpolation schemes from
generalized Jacobi polynomials. These techniques were in later chapters used to develop FOM
schemes for the chapter relevant PDE. Moreover, the chapter discussed how to stabilize a time­
integration scheme and found a numerical bound for the explicit Runge­Kutta 4 scheme.
Chapter 3 introduced the chosen reduced basis method, POD. Along with introducing the POD the
chapter discussed how to utilize different kinds of singular value decomposition (SVD) techniques
to find the reduced POD basis. Furthermore, a randomized SVD was introduced to exploit the low­
rank structure of many snapshot matrices. It was concluded, that while the standard SVD scales
cubically with the input size of the matrix, the randomized SVD scales linearly. A caveat of the
randomized SVD, however, was its inability to reconstruct small singular values, which can have
a significant input for slow­decaying spectrums.
Chapter 4 developed the non­intrusive methods, exact DMD and eDMD. In this chapter a novel
reduction technique was discovered for DMD type models and extended to both the exact DMD
and eDMD. It was furthermore found that this technique led to halving of the latent dimension
found by the POD in many cases.
When the eDMD method was developed this chapter also explained how to utilize the POD modes
to circumvent computations of large matrices, effectively making it an extension of the exact DMD.
Once a set of methods was developed, the thesis tested them on two different 1­dimensional equa­
tions, the advection equation and Burgers’ equation. The advection equation is a linear PDE on
which the selected methods worked exceptionally well on. All methods were able to achieve ma­
chine precision accuracy in real time. Both the exact DMD and the eDMD was able to run long
simulations within one thousand of a second, where the POD lagged behind an entire order of
magnitude. Furthermore, offline computing times were comparable, as the latent dimension was
very low.
By further testing of the eDMD method for different dictionaries it was shown that the latent size
increased, even though accurate solutions were still obtained.
Burgers’ equation gave more troublesome results, as POD modes were not adequately sufficient
to capture dynamics in the training set which were present in the extrapolation set. For both DMD
methods latent trajectories tended to zero before extrapolation, effectively canceling out relevant
dynamics in the system. Because of this less relevant dynamics, such as subtle high frequencies
are amplified, making approximations bad.
Although the eDMD also suffered from zeroed latent dynamics, it was able to level out high fre­
quencies providing a significant improvement over the exact DMD. However, this did not come

without a caveat. When creating the dictionary numerical errors from the FOM solution were am­
plified considerably, making the POD spectrum converge significantly faster. Unfortunately, this
meant that important dynamics were attached to small singular values, which the randomized SVD
is bad at estimating. Therefore, an economy SVD was employed, resulting in huge performance
losses in the offline computing stage.
Furthermore, the eDMD method was quite unstable, and the number of basis modes to be used had
to be selected with great care.
However, these test equations showed that eDMD approaches can be beneficial for certain nonlin­
ear equations.
Chapter 7 then tested the non­intrusive methods (exact DMD and eDMD) on a free surface of water
created by the fully nonlinear potential flow (FNPF) equations. As expected the methods worked
well for smaller waves and more linear cases but had trouble doing vast extrapolation without
huge error accumulations. While some latent trajectories of the exact DMD could survive long­
time simulations, all latent trajectories of the eDMD method were quickly sent to zero.
Because approximations, however, did not appear to contain high­frequency errors, it was theorized
that the main issue was the latent trajectories. Because the free surface is a periodic solution, it is
known how the latent trajectories should behave. Therefore, by only changing the latent trajectories
approximations to the most nonlinear cases became much better. When doing this correction it
was discovered that high­frequency errors were indeed still present, and consequently the latent
trajectory corrections were also tested on the eDMD methods.
While approximations did not improve compared to the exact DMD, the high­frequency errors
were somewhat truncated.
Although DMD type models did not prove to be perfect for nonlinear PDE’s, this thesis did discover
that some DMD bases are viable for resolving nonlinearities. The extended DMD furthermore
showed that nonlinearities could be handled without introducing high­frequency errors.
8.1
Future Work
In this thesis, no method proved itself to be a ”silver bullet” when handling nonlinear PDE’s. How­
ever, it was discovered that the DMD modes might be able to resolve a system even though its latent
trajectories are wrong. Therefore, it could be interesting to examine how these latent trajectories
can otherwise be approximated. For example, it might be interesting to use a Neural ODE or other
nonlinear time­regression algorithms to approximate these latent trajectories instead of fixing the
DMD eigenvalues. Especially, if underlying physics are known for the dynamical system, the re­
cently proposed Physics­informed Neural ODE [31] shows great promise in recovering nonlinear
temporal dynamics.
Moreover, the extended DMD showed some potential, but the choice of dictionary makes it difficult
to estimate its true potential. It could be of interest to examine other dictionaries, and especially
introduce a dictionary learning element, so that the method can keep its generality.
This thesis has focused on methods that do not require the use of artificial neural networks, how­
ever, with the attention that neural networks are getting, it could be of interest to use these as
dimension reduction techniques. Some caveats for convolutional neural networks were discussed
in chapter 1, however, implementing a variational autoencoder to create a continuous latent space
as done in the original Neural ODE paper [17] could result in nonlinear model reduction with
extrapolation capabilities.
