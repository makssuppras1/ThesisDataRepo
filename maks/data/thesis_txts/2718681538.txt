Background 
Recently the genome of a bacterium isolated from a cold seep in the deep sea in China has been sequenced 
and reveals 34 encoding polysaccharide lyases (PLs). Those enzymes degrade acid uronic-containing 
polysaccharide using a b-elimination mechanism. Among those 34 predicted PLs, 15 are non-classified, 
meaning that the sequence homology to other CAZy classified PLs is too low to directly assign them into 
any existing family. This means they are potentially the first members of new families which infers a high 
potential of novelty in the  
Aim of the project 
The project aims to produce in E. coli and purify the recombinant non-classified PLs and other selected PLs 
found in the genome of the marine bacterium and to screen those enzymes against a bank of relevant 
uronic acid-containing polysaccharides. Those enzymes present huge potential for the discovery of new 
enzymatic activities, new families or new subfamilies of PLs. Part of this work involves using bioinformatic 
tools for exploring the sequences and their predicted 3D structures.   
Depending on the results of the screening assays and if any positive hits are identified, the student could 
be in charge of further characterization of those enzymes including determination of the biochemical 
properties of the enzymes, crystallization/structure solving and analysis of structure-function relationships.

Abstract
Over the last decades, advancement in the field of genome sequencing have grown ex-
ponentially. With known DNA sequences the encoded proteins can be predicted, while
the function for many of these protein sequences pose a larger challenge to uncover.
Only a tiny fraction of predicted proteins have been experimentally characterized and
while functional prediction based on sequence similarity alone is possible, many pro-
teins remains as hypothetical proteins of unknown function. Looking into non-classified
proteins in the genome of a marine organism Ruficoccus sp. ZRK36, 14 non-classified
polysaccharide lyases were selected which showed low similarities to known carbohy-
drate active enzymes. Investigation of the 14 PLs revealed a polyspecific PL named
RuPL0 M, which showed activities on poly-mannuronic acid (polyM), poly-hyaluronic
acid (poly-HA), poly-glucuronic acid (poly-GlcA), chondroitin sulfate A (CSA) and
chondroitin sulfate C (CSC). The enzyme showed relation to family PL17, which does
not currently have reported activities on CSA and CSC. The biochemical character-
ization of RuPL0 M showed temperature optimum of 45°C, pH optimum at 7.0, low
optimal salt concentration of 17 mM NaCl and positive effect of divalent ions par-
ticular Ca2+. Optimal conditions that converge from the natural conditions found
in the deep sea, which raises questions about the origin of Ruficoccus sp. ZRK36,
that currently is not well described. Additionally the enzyme showed endo activity on
polyM, poly-HA and CSA, which to date only have been reported for poly-HA and
poly-GlcA in the family PL17. RuPL0 M showed catalytic constant kcat of 12.7 ± 0.6
min−1 on poly-GlcA, 8.3 ± 0.2 min−1 on polyM, 5.3 ± 0.2 min−1 on poly-HA, 4.5 ±
0.1 min−1 on CSA and 4.8 ± 0.0 min−1 on CSC. Multiple sequence alignment and
structural analysis with AlphaFold2 generated model of RuPL0 M, showed conserved
catalytic residues in the active site corresponding to a Tyr’/Tyr β-elimination mecha-
nism, which is conserved in the PL17 family. Furthermore, the analysis showed largest
sequence similarity to the current PL17 glucuronan lyase (OPIT 527565) with 48.2%
and strucural alignment with rmsd of 0.987. Sequence analysis showed RuPL0 M was
related to PL17 1 and PL17 2, but with low sequence similarity indicating the likely
formation of a new subfamily within the PL17.

Introduction
Through the last several decades, advancement in the field of genome sequencing has
had a major breakthrough and the number of genes from various organisms and dif-
ferent environments acquired through to genome sequencing projects has increased
exponentially [9]. With the increase in known DNA sequences, the encoded proteins
derived from the DNA can be predicted. However, only a small fraction of these pre-
dicted proteins have been experimentally characterized [9]. This leaves a large amount
of data in the form of hypothetical proteins (protein sequences) of unknown function,
structure and potential applications, possibly with novel characteristics. The function
for a number of these proteins has been predicted purely based on sequence similarity
to known enzymes. Functional predictions on the basis of sequence similarity alone
can be very unreliable, when the sequence similarity to experimentally characterized
proteins is low. [8, 31] This can result in assigning wrong activities to proteins or
assigning proteins to wrong families in databases [31].
The biochemically unexplored protein sequences from genome sequencing harbor great
possibilities of novelty, which is also the case for one class of proteins, namely carbohy-
drate active enzymes (CAZymes). The unexplored protein sequence space of predicted
CAZymes has not been subject to systematic investigation. Sequence-based functional
predictions do not reliably describe the carbohydrate substrate for the given enzymes
[8, 9]. With the current technologies, the growth of predicted protein sequences de-
rived systematically from genome sequencing has become exponential, whereas the
biochemical characterization workflow of the large amount of predicted proteins does
not allow for an identical increasing rate. The expansion of the number of sequences
encoding CAZymes will continue to increase at a rate that cannot be followed by wet
biochemistry characterization.
To uncover novel activities within the field of carbohydrate active enzymes, the careful
selection of protein sequences to investigate is currently ongoing. Among the candi-
dates for novelty, a marine bacterium isolated from a deep sea cold seep in Nanhai,
China, Ruficoccus sp. ZRK36 whose genome has been sequenced, reveals 35 proteins
with similarity to polysaccharide lyases. Twenty of these 35 enzymes can be classified
in existing PL families, with the rest being annotated as non-classified (NC) because of
a too large divergence with known PLs. These 15 NC proteins thus have the potential
of novelty within the polysaccharide lyase class of enzymes. This thesis will investigate
14 NC from Ruficoccus sp. ZRK3 and aims to uncover new knowledge about these
polysaccharide lyases, their function and specificities. Furthermore, selected NC PLs
will be target for further biochemical characterization and structure-function analysis.
1.1
Carbohydrate active enzymes
Since 1998 the carbohydrate active enzyme classification database (www.cazy.org) has
been describing the enzyme classes of carbohydrate active enzymes including glycoside
hydrolases (GHs), glycosyl transferases (GTs), polysaccharide lyases (PLs), carbohy-
drate esterases (CEs), auxilliary activities (AAs) and associated carbohydrate binding
Page 7 of 79
==PAGE:9==
modules (CBMs). The main functions of the CAZy database are to maintain and up-
date the classifications within carbohydrate active enzyme families, to classify newly
acquired sequences from GenBank and the Protein Data Bank and to describe the
functional information about the aforementioned carbohydrate active enzyme classes
and their families [4]. Within the CAZy database the publicly available data includes
protein sequences from complete genomes downloaded from GenBank and CAZy en-
zyme classes including clans, families and sub-families in which the characterized car-
bohydrate active enzymes are described. The families in the CAZy database are built
around at least one biochemically characterized member, and adding family members
which share significant sequence similarity to the founding member. Within CAZy fam-
ilies functional diversity is frequent and a single family can cover multiple substrate
specificities, in spite of a conserved catalytic mechanism [4]. In order to minimize
errors in the identification of enzymes in the CAZy database, sequence alignment of
the CAZymes is performed after removal of ancillary modules from the proteins. This
modus operandi is exercised due to high sequence similarities between distinct proteins
in regards to their ancillary modules and results in lower identification error.
The GH class of CAZymes performs the hydrolysis of the glycosidic bond between
carbohydrate moieties. In the CAZy classification, the GH class is the largest with
1,170,883 members present in families as of writing. Within this class of CAZymes
some GH families are grouped into GH clans of related families sharing a common
structural fold, because the fold is more conserved than the sequence. The current
clans alphabetically range from GH-A to GH-R, with clan GH-A being the largest
with 26 GH families in the clan. Each family has a conserved catalytic mechanism and
catalytic machinery. Several of the GH families are further dissected into sub-families,
such as GH5, which currently has the largest number of sub-families. The sub-family
classification groups together CAZymes into clades within the family, de- pendent
on a higher sequence similarity threshold. The second largest class of CAZymes are
represented by the glycosyltransferases (GTs) with 1,009,293 sequences at the time
of writing.
The GTs catalyze the transfer of sugar moieties from activated donor
molecules to acceptor molecules, creating a glycosidic bond between the two moieties.
The transfer of sugar moieties underpins the biosynthesis of disaccharides, oligosaccha-
rides and polysaccharides, which the GTs can synthesize with a retaining or inverting
mechanism. Polysaccharide lyases (PLs) are the third class of carbohydrate active
enzymes in the CAZy database. They cleave uronic acid-containing polysaccharides
using a β-elimination mechanism, which generates a terminal unsaturated hexenuronic
acid sugar moiety. The PL families currently count 39,682 family members, divided
into 42 families. The carbohydrate esterases (CEs) are a class of carbohydrate-active
enzymes that catalyze the de-O-acylation or de-N-acetylation of substituted carbohy-
drates. The last class of enzymes described in the CAZy database is the auxiliary
activities, which cover redox enzymes that act in conjunction with other CAZymes.
In addition to the CAZymes classified in sequence-based families, a large number of
sequences are not yet classified in the current families and are denoted as the non-
characterized (NC) proteins. The enzymes categorized as NC display distant related-
ness to bona fide CAZymes but too weak for a reliable family assignment. The NC
category of CAZymes constitutes a reservoir for potential new families and/or new ac-
Page 8 of 79
==PAGE:10==
tivities (specificities). Currently there are 29,963 GHs, 30,461 GTs, 2,136 PLs, 2,982
CEs, 58 AAs and 2,996 CBMs in the NC categories for the different classes, open for
exploration.
During the past decades, the number of carbohydrate degradation enzyme sequences
has increased exponentially, and the number of sequences in the individual classes,
far surpass the number of biochemically characterized or structurally determined en-
zymes. Between 2013 and 2021 the number of sequences in the GH families increased
from 162,550 to 995,295, i.e. more than a sixfold, whereas the number of characterized
GHs only increased from 6,094 to 7,248.[8, 4] This slower progression has increased the
gap between the sequences and the characterized enzymes by more than 500%. This
gap is even wider when examining the number of CAZymes with an experimentally
determined 3D structure, which was 790 in 2013 and 1,567 in 2021 for the GH class.
[8, 4] Thus only about 0.16% of the total GH modules have a PDB structure available.
[4] This trend illustrates the immense gap between powerful genomic sequencing and
biophysical characterization and structure determination, which lag severely behind.
Whilst AlphaFold provides a way to reduce the gap with reasonably good structural
models [14], there is no tool to accurately predict substrate specificity of CAZymes.
Thus high throughput selection and experimental characterization techniques are nec-
essary as a strategic approach to reveal CAZyme activities and specificities. [4]
This master thesis will focus on the PL class to reveal activities and specificities within
the selected target enzymes. The discovery of novel PLs is of large interest because of
the potential biotechnological applications of this class of carbohydrate active enzymes.
1.2
Polysaccharide Lyases
Polysaccharide lyases (PL) cleave uronic acid containing polysaccharides using a β-
elimination mechanism, which is conserved throughout the PL class. The β-elimination
mechanism occurs between two sugar moieties, one being a uronic acid, with the end
products consisting of an unsaturated uronic acid containing sugar moiety and a new
reducing end [7, 18, 32]. PLs are found in a large variety of organisms, including
bacteria, bacteriophages, archea, fungi, algae, mammals and plants, where the PLs aid
in the decomposition of polysaccharides containing uronic acids. In conjunction with
the GHs, utilization of PLs is a strategical tool to catalyze the breakdown of oligo and
polysaccharides. [18, 20, 32]. Natural PL substrates are diverse and originates from
several organisms including bacteria, plants and animals. The natural substrates are
composed of relatively similar sugar residues arranged in repetitive blocks containing
an uronic acid. PL substrates that occur as oligo and polysaccharides consisting of
repetitive blocks. Each block can contain several sugar residues, with the last sugar
moiety of the block being linked by a 1,4 glycosidic bond to a uronic acid in the next
block. [32] Polysaccharide substrates binds in the active site of the polysaccharide
lyases. The interaction involves several of the sugar moieties. The stretch of interacting
sugar moieties on the enzyme surface are called ”subsites” and are numerated based
on the distance from the cleavage site.
The subsites are denoted n+ and n- with n=1,2,3,... depending on the distance from
Page 9 of 79
==PAGE:11==
the point of cleavage. The enzymatic β-elimination occurs between two sugar residues
at subsites -1 and +1, with subsite +1 being a uronic acid. illustrated in Figure 1. Af-
ter cleavage the carbohydrate moiety at subsite -1 becomes a new reducing end while
the +1 moiety becomes an unsaturated hexenuronic acid. [7, 18] The produced unsat-
urated hexenuronic acid shows typical absorbance in the 235 nm range, which can be
measured on with a spectrophotometer. As a monomer, the unsaturated hexenuronic
acid is not stable, spontaneously converts to linearized 4-deoxy-L-erythro-5-hexosulose
uronic acid (DEH), resulting in the loss of signal at 235 nm. [15]
In nature, the number of PLs encoded by the genomes of most organisms is rela-
tively small and is much lower than the number of GHs. [18]. One reason for this
low abundance is the necessary presence of a uronic acid in the PL substrates, which
only represents a small fraction of all the carbohydrate polymers. [18] The uronic acid
substrates include natural occurring biopolymers such as alginate, pectin, chondroitin,
dermatan, glucuronan, ulvan and xanthan. Alginate and pectin are particularly abun-
dant because they are constituents of the cell walls of terrestrial plants and brown
algae respectively. The natural substrates of polysaccharide lyases can be grouped
into three categories: alginates, pectin/pectate and glycosaminoglycans (GAGs)

GAGs are a diverse class of PL substrates, which include a wide variety of sugars and

families, Table 1. Despite this great substrate diversity, the catalytic mechanism of
the PLs is highly conserved throughout the PL enzymes and is very different from the

hydrolytic cleavage observed in the GH class. The facilitation of the C4 bond cleavage
by β-elimination involves a catalytic machinery that can have two configurations:
syn or anti (Figure 6), dependent on the specific substrate conformation and the
spatial substrate orientation in the active site [18]. The catalytic mechanism (Figure
6) of PLs consist of abstraction of the proton at C5 via a basic amino acid sidechain,
electron delocalization and charge stabilization of the carboxyl group on the subsite +1
moiety. Lytic cleavage of the C4 bond between subsite -1 and +1, facilitated by proton
donation by a catalytic acid to create a double-bond between C5 and C6 resulting in
a hexenuronic acid in position +1 and a new reducing end at position -1 [7, 18]. The
catalytic mechanism of the PLs can be divided into metal-ion dependent and metal-
ion independent. Through metal-ion independent catalysis the PLs utilize arginine,
asparagine or glutamine residues in the vicinity for substrate neutralization in addition
to tyrosine or histidine acting as the base and tyrosine acting as the catalytic acid [7,
20].

diversity throughout the families, with some families being distantly related, while
others share similar activities but different structural folds. The structural folds are
conserved within the PL families, and the generic PL folds represented throughout
the PLs are parallel β-helix, β-propeller, β-sandwich + β-sheet, triple strand β-helix,
Page 17 of 79

β-jelly roll, (α/α)3-barrel, (α/α)5-barrel, (α/α)6-barrel, (α/α)7-barrel. Several of the

consists of a (α/α)6-toroid appended to anti-parallel β-sheet (Figure 1), with the
catalytic site located between the two domains. PLs can also be appended to ancillary
domains such as carbohydrate binding modules, to promote sugar recognition and
tissue targeting [18]. Notably the number of structural folds is lower than the number
of PL families, with 9 different structural folds found in 41 families. This difference
reflects distant evolutionary relationships between several PL families, a connection

1.3
Polysaccharide lyase family PL17
Polysaccharide lyase family PL17 contains enzymes with activity on alginate and
derivatives thereof. The family contains two subfamilies, PL17 1 and PL17 2, with
the former currently having no characterized members. The specific activities in the
family are alginate lyase (EC 4.2.2.3) and oligoalginate lyase (EC 4.2.2.26), with the re-
ported activities in the subfamliy PL17 2 being oligoalginate lyase / exo-alginate lyase
(EC 4.2.2.26), oligomannuronate lyase / exo-acting mannuronan lyase (EC 4.2.2.26),
poly(β-mannuronate) lyase / M-specific alginate lyase (EC 4.2.2.3) [10]. Subfamily
PL17 1 is annotated and clustered based on sequence similarity, which ultimately
amounts to no reported activities in the subfamily. Currently 993 sequences are listed
in family PL17 members, with 335 in subfamily 1, 571 in subfamily 2 and the re-
Page 21 of 79
==PAGE:23==
maining 77 not linked to a subfamily. Functional characterization are reported for 15
members of the PL17 2 subfamily, including structural determination for two mem-
bers. Hyaluronan lyase and glucuronan lyase activities have been reported for two
members of the family PL17. [13] Family PL17 and characterized members of sub-
family PL17 2 mainly contains exolytic oligoalginate lyase activity, which is also true
for the structurally and functionally related family PL15. Family PL15 shows similar
3D fold and exo-type alginate lyase activity, as family PL17. Hyaluronate lyase (EC
4.2.2.3) and glucuronan lyase (EC 4.2.2.11) activity reported for two PL17 members,
are not categorized in a PL17 subfamily in CAZy. In CAZy, primarily exo-type ManA
specific activity has been reported throughout family PL17.[10]

the conjunction or interface between the (α/α)6-toroid domain and the anti-parallel
β-sheet domain. [13, 34] The catalytic mechanism utilized by PL17 enzymes is ini-

the glycosidic linkage is performed by a second tyrosine (Y258 in 7BJT/7BM6) acting

of this metal ion is more than 11˚A from the active site, suggesting a structural role of

with manual N and C terminal truncation to isolate the catalytic domain sequences. A

RuPL0 M sequence (GenBank) was aligned with five members of the PL17 family with
different substrate specificities a hyaluronan lyase: Acid 4041 (PL17 2), a glucuronan
lyase: OPIT 572565 (PL17 2) and three PolyM lyases: Alya3, AlgL17 and Alg17C
(PL17 1). From NCBI BLASTp the glucuronan lyase, showed highest sequence sim-
ilarity to RuPL0 M of the five targets with 48.18%. The hyaluronan lyase showed
the lowest 26.15% sequence similarity, while the three selected polyM lyases Alya3,
Alg17C and AlgL17 showed 29.63%, 26.92% and 33.33% respectively. However, with
high query cover for the glucuronan lyase and hyaluronan lyase the sequence id% rep-
resents the full length alignment better.
From the CAZymodO (Appendix 5) and
inspection of the AlphaFold2 model, the presence of a CBM was indicated, covering
amino acids 21-171, which is not present in any of the characterized PL17 members
(Appendix 6). From the two solved structures in the PL17 family Alya3 and Alg17C,
the residues proposed to be involved in catalysis, substrate binding and in metal bind-
ing are highlighted in Figure 18 and the catalytic mechanism is described as a Tyr’/Tyr
β-elimination, with two Tyr residues acting as general acid and base. Additionally,
an Asn/His pair stabilizes the negative charge on the carboxylate anion in subsite
+1 [13, 24, 34].
The Asn/His pair is conserved throughout the PL17 subfamily 2
(Appendix 6), but the His residue is lacking in RuPL0 M and the hyaluronate lyase
(Figure 18-19C). One of the Tyr residues acting as general acid and base are conserved
throughout all the sequences, whereas the secondary Tyr are only conserved for the
PolyM specific sequences Alya3, Alg17C and AlgL17. However, another Tyr residue
(yellow star, Figure 18) is identified in RuPL0 M, the glucuronan lyase and hyaluro-
nan lyase and is located in the catalytic cleft (Figure 19B-C). In the catalytic site of
RuPL0 M, Tyr653 is oriented opposite of the Tyr485, and structurally aligns with the
two Tyr residues in structure of Alya3 (Figure 19B-C). The structural conservation of
catalytic residues suggests the same Tyr’/Tyr β-elimination mechanism in RuPL0 M,
with the Tyr485/Tyr653.

Discussion
The investigation the NC PLs in the Ruficoccus sp. ZRK36, has not previously been
described.
Therefore, the investigation and unraveling of these proteins and their
specificities, have the potential for novelty. The marine bacteria posseses 35 PLs in
the genome, including families PL6, PL12, PL17, PL21, PL22, PL33, PL34, PL35,
PL38. The diversity of PLs in Ruficoccus sp. ZRK36, currently cover nine PL fami-
lies and harbors most of its classified PLs in the PL33, PL34 and PL38 families. In
total 13 of 20 classified PLs in the genome are within these three families. The activ-
ities related to these families as described in Table 1 are hyaluronic acid, gellan and
chondroitin lyase for PL33 and chondroitin lyase for PL35. Whereas, for PL38 the
reported activity is solely on glucuronan (alginate lyase unpublished). Commonalities
between the four different substrates are the presence of the GlcA moiety β1,4-linked
to another moiety. The chondroitin and hyaluronic acid are GAGs present in tissues,
Page 54 of 79
==PAGE:56==
whereas gellan and glucuronan both exist at part of the extracellular matrix (EPS)
produced by some bacteria, fungi and algae. These different kinds of polysaccharides,
produced by different sources can be part of the metabolic pathways in the Ruficoccus
species, due to substrate availability in the natural environment. The organism as
reported in NCBI, originates from a cold seep in the Chinese deep sea. On the bottom
of the ocean, the organism can thrive on sediment bio-material, from living organisms
in the surrounding environment. Sediment of various kinds such as GAGs could be a
carbohydrate source for the organism and the EPS degradation could indicate presence
of biofilms in the vicinity. Three alginate lyases PL6, PL17 and PL34 with the addi-
tion of RuPL0 M described in this work, could indicate a alginate degradation system.
The additional PL families present in the genome PL12, PL21 and PL22, corresponds
to GAG degradation for PL12 and PL21 and galacturonic acid degradation for PL22.
The galacturonic acid substrates are mostly present in terrestrial plants, as described
in [30], however also presence in some aqueous plants such as sea weed has been re-
ported [19, 25]. The PLs currently described in the genome, supports degradation of
components that could be readily available in the sediment on the ocean floor.
The 12 substrates used in the activity screening assays in this work, were selected
to target a broad range of PL substrates covering all the PL activities reported in
CAZy. However, the 12 substrates do not reflect the diversity of uronic acid-containing
polysaccharides in nature. As enzymes are highly specificity in relation to the bond,
sugars, subsites, modifications, degree of polymerization and physio-chemical param-
eters of their preferred substrates, the number of substrates used in this study is
relatively scarce. This could be an explanation for the low number of hits, from the
initial and individual screens conducted during this work. Probably the right sub-
strate for most of the enzymes tested was not included. Clear significant hits with
RuPL0 M was observed, however with activity on several substrates including polyM,
CSA, CSC, poly-HA and poly-GlcA. Hints of lower activities was indicated for some
of the other targets such as RuPL0 F and RuPL0 N. The target enzymes could also
be selectively active on low DP substrates and therefore not able to cleave long chains
of polysaccharides. Synergy assays with several enzymes could be a way to increase
the possibilities to find hits on the target enzymes, but this has yet to be done and
has a part of this work.
The biochemical characterization of RuPL0 M, showed unexpected properties of the
enzyme. The temperature optimum at 45°C, and very low activity in the 20-30°C
range does not support optimal enzymatic activity in the deep sea. RuPL0 M showed
highest activity at the lowest salt concentration, contradictory to the salt of the ocean
which is approximately 600 mM NaCl. The enzymatic activity diminished more than
60% at NaCl concentration higher than 100 mM. The thermal stability of RuPL M
illustrated in Figure 12, show a Tm at 43.95 ± 0.02°C and indicating that RuPL0 M is
unstable at relative high temperatures. The temperature optimum curve supports the
thermal stability of RuPL0 M (Figure 11A). The activity was reduced from 100% at 45
°C to about 30% at 50°C, indicating protein denaturation at this temperature, which
diminished the protein activity. RuPL0 M showed increased activity with addition
of divalent ions, illustrated in Figure 11D. The enzyme showed increased activity
with Ca2+, Mg2+ and Mn2+ compared to the no ion control. The activity with Ca2+
Page 55 of 79
==PAGE:57==
showed similar activity to the non EDTA treated control reaction, suggesting the
enzyme could require Ca2+. The activity with Ni2+ and Zn2+ was significantly lower
than for the other divalent cations and the no ion control, suggesting a inhibitory
effect of these cations. The lowest activity was observed with EDTA present in the
reaction, supporting the enzyme require ions. The ions present in the enzyme could
be a structural ion or act as a charge neutralizer, which has been reported for Ca2+
previously [34]. The biochemical properties are unexpected as they do not support the
biochemical features present in the natural habitat for Ruficoccus sp. ZRK36.
The substrates specificities uncovered for RuPL0 M: polyM, CSA, CSC, poly-HA acid
and poly-GlcA are currently not all included in the PL17 family, with which RuPL0 M
shares the highest sequence similarity and predicted structural fold.
Activities on
PolyM, poly-HA and poly-GlcA are currently described for members of the PL17
family. The activities on chondroitin sulfate A and chondroitin sulfate C, have until
now not been reported in the PL17 family. The kinetic parameters has not been deter-
mined for glucuronan lyase (OPIT5 72565) and hyaluronan lyase (Acid 4041) in family
PL17 reported in Mathieu et al. 2018 [21]. However, a PL5 with similar structural
fold Smlt1473 described in MacDonald and Berger 2014 [20] with polyspecificity for
polyM, poly-HA and poly-GlcA showed KM of 0.26 g·L−1, 0.55 g·L−1 and 0.14 g·L−1
respectively. The KM values determined for RuPL0 M was 2.2, 2.5 and 2.2 higher than
for Smlt1473, suggesting lower affinity for the substrates. Furthermore, the specific
Vmax was reported for Smlt1474 as 91.6 ∆Abs·min−1·mg−1, 71.5 ∆Abs·min−1·mg−1
and 940 ∆Abs·min−1·mg−1 for polyM, poly-HA and poly-GlcA respectively. The Vmax
determined for RuPL0 M showed maximum rates about 65, 80 and 427 times lower
than Smlt1474 for polyM, poly-HA and poly-GlcA respectively, indicating RuPL0 M
is slow compared to Smlt1473. Additionally, Smlt1474 showed different pH optimum
for the three different substrates which has not been tested for RuPL0 M. The pH
dependent activity of the different substrates, could also be a factor for RuPL0 M
activity, with only pH optimum for polyM tested during this work. On polyM Alg17C
from family PL17 showed kcat of 3414 min−1 more than 400 times the kcat determined
for RuPL0 M. The kinetic parameters for a mutated Alg17C with a H202L mutation,
in the Asn/His charge stabilizing pair was also reported. [24] The kcat was decreased
five fold in the mutated version and the His in the Asn/His pair is not conserved in
the RuPL0 M where a Ile is present. This could indicate the absence of the His in the
stabilizing pair, contribute to low activity. The low activity observed for RuPL0 M
compared to Smlt1474 and Alg17C could indicate the tested substrates possibly did
not include the correct substrate for RuPL0 M, and the activity observed could be
because of enzyme promiscuity. However, A enzyme RuPL033A from Ruficoccus sp.
ZRK36 previously described by our team showed kcat of 13.2 min−1 and 1.0 min−1 on
CSA and CSC respectively. The catalytic constants found for RuPL0 M was deter-
mined to 2.9 times lower on CSA, but 4.8 times higher on CSC. The enzymes RuPL33A
and RuPL0 M both from the Ruficoccus sp. ZRK36 investigated, showed activity with
same order of magnitude suggesting the enzymes are slow.
Bioinformatic investigation of the PL17 family illustrated in Figures 21 and 22 showed
relationships between RuPL0 M and family PL17. The tree and SSN illustrates that
RuPL0 M, BLASTp hits and PL17 with no subfamily assigned, groups together and
Page 56 of 79
==PAGE:58==
could form a potential new subfamily within the existing PL17 family. The potential
subfamily show relationship to both PL17 1 and PL17 2 and includes targets RuPL0 M
and RuPL0 N. RuPL0 M described in this work shows poly-specificity with several of
the substrates not reported previously for this family. However, the highest activity on
glucuronan, aligns with RuPL0 M show high sequence and structural identity to the
glucuronan lyase (OPIT5 72565) currently reported in the family. Chondroitin sulfate
A and C not reported previously in the family, have GalNAc β1,4-linked to GlcA,
where the β1,4-GlcA is common between CSA, CSC, HA and glucuronan.
Solely,
polyM does not possess the GlcA moiety, but is structurally composed of the C2
epimer β1,4-ManA.
The endo/exo activity was analysed by a time course on polyM and LC-MS on polyM,
CSA and poly-HA. The analysis indicates RuPL0 M to be endo specific on polyM
indicated on the time course (Figure 14), where no decrease in absorbance was observed
for the first 5 hours of the reaction. The production of ∆4,5-ManA moieties by a exo-
acting PL, would result in a decrease in absorbance due to the linearization of the
double bond. The LC-MS analysis support findings from the time course, showing no
production of ∆4,5-unsaturated monomers for polyM. Production of DP2-DP6 was
observed on polyM, whereas products with two to six sugar moieties was observed for
CSA and two to fourteen sugar moieties for hyaluronic acid. Endo activity in PL17
have been described previously, for the two members of the PL17 family hyaluronan
lyase (Acid 4041) and glucuronan lyase (OPIT5 27565).[21] The endo activity has not
previously been described for the polyM specific lyases in the PL17 family.
RuPL0 M show similarities with five members of the Pl17 family, in the context of
catalytic mechanism and structural fold.
RuPL0 M possess the identical catalytic
residues as reported in Park et al. 2014 and Jouanneau et al. 2021, with the exception
of a His residue. In the MSA and superimposition a single His was absent from the
Asn/His pair conserved in the polyM lyases. However, another His278 was present in
the catalytic site within 3.0 ˚A of the substrate molecule used (Figure 19B). Addition-
ally a Asn277 was adjacent to the His278 corresponding to a Asn/His pair, similar
to the catalytic residues reported in Alya3, Alg17C and AlgL17. RuPL0 M showed
highest sequence and structural similarity with the glucuronan lyase (OPIT5 27565)
from the PL17 family. With no solved structure for RuPL0 M or the glucuronan lyase
(OPIT5 27565), the AlphaFold2 models for the two proteins may be similar due to the
high sequence similarity of 48.18%. However, from the kinetics, sequence similarity
and structural similarity, the results point towards RuPL0 M being closely related to
the glucuronan lyase with more distance to the PolyM lyases and the hyaluronan lyase.
To further investigate RuPL0 M, the CBM was removed and the truncated version was
used to set up crystallization screens. The understanding of the structure of RuPL0 M,
is important in order to understand the catalytic mechanism and the polyspecificity of
the enzyme. The activity of ∆CBM-RuPL0 M on polyM was tested and showed the
same activity as the WT RuPL0 M. The role of the CBM in PLs are not understood
fully and it is possible the CBM influence the specificity. The ∆CBM-RuPL0 M was
tested on polyM but not on the other substrates. Therefore, it is not know if the
∆CBM-RuPL0 M retained activity on the other substrates, without the CBM. The
Page 57 of 79
==PAGE:59==
CBM could have influence on the RuPL0 M polyspecificity, and the removal of the do-
main could have potential influence on the enzymes promiscuity. Furthermore, crystal
structures will unravel structural ions present in the enzyme and binding of substrates
in the crystal structure will provide insight, into the structure-function relationship of
the enzyme. The target models generated by AlphaFold2, are expected to be good
approximations of the targets overall 3D structure, but with expected errors. The mod-
els are based upon computational machine learning algorithms (AlphaFold2), which
cannot incorporate several important features of protein structures. The AlphaFold2
models represent a single stable state of the protein, whereas proteins are dynamic and
can undergo conformational changes. Furthermore, proteins often include co-factors
such as metals, which the AlphaFold model does not account for. From several of
the models, long loops are present, indicating disordered regions which could not be
predicted from AlphaFold2.

Conclusion
The marine organism Ruficoccus sp. ZRK36 possess 35 polysaccharide lyases, hereof
14 non-classified enzymes was selected for exploration during the work conducted for
this thesis.
Bioinformatic investigation of the target enzymes along with structural predictions
with AlphaFold2 (Table 8) were conducted and showed 11 of the non-classified tar-
gets was predicted to have an (α/α)6-toroid + anti-parallel β-sandwich structural
composition. The two targets RuPL0 E and RuPL0 G showed to be two domains
from the same protein with RuPL0 E having a (α/α)6-toroid fold and RuPL0 G a
anti-parallel β-sandwich. Target RuPL0 K showed a structural prediction containing
a (α/α)6-toroid + anti-parallel β-sandwich structure with the addition of a (α/α)7-
barrel, corresponding to the family PL38 structure.
The substrate screens conducted on the target PLs, showed only significant activ-
ity with a single target RuPL0 M, which was selected for further characterization.
RuPL0 M showed to be related to family PL17, illustrated in the phylogenic PL17
tree (Figure 21) and the SSN (Figure 22). THe phylogenic tree show the separation
of the targets RuPL0 M and RuPL0 N from both current subfamilies in the PL17
family. Furthermore, RuPL0 M and RuPL0 N show separation from the two PL17
subfamilies with 35% cutoff in the SSN and indicates the formation of a possible new
subfamily. RuPL0 M showed from the biochemical characterization similarities with
the PL17 family, with activities on polyM, poly-HA and poly-GlcA which has been
reported in the family. Furthermore RuPL0 M showed activity on CSA and CSC,
which has not previously been reported in the family. RuPL0 M showed highest ac-
tivity on poly-GlcA with a kcat of 12.7 ± 0.6 min−1, KM of 0.31 ± 0.05 and a Vmax
of 1.27 ± 0.06 nmol·min−1. The activity on the other substrates shown in Table 12,
shows kcat 1.5, 2.3, 3.0 and 2.6 times lower for polyM, poly-HA, CSA and CSC than on
poly-GlcA. All the substrates except polyM had β1,4-GlcA as a component, with the
polyM having the C2 epimer β1,4-ManA as moieties. RuPL0 M showed endo activity
Page 58 of 79
==PAGE:60==
on polyM, poly-HA and CSA, but was not tested on poly-GlcA and CSC. The endo
activity has only been reported in the PL17 family for the hyaluronic acid (Acid 4041)
and glucuronan (OPIT5 27565), but has not been reported previously on polyM in
the PL17 family.
Substrate inhibition was observed on glucuronan above substrate concentration of
2.5 g·L−1. Biochemical characterization of RuPL0 M showed an optimal temperature
at 45°C, optimal pH at pH 7.0 and a low salt tolerance with the highest activity
seen at 17.5 mM NaCl. The effect of divalent tested, showed increased activity with
addition of Ca2+, Mg2+ and Mn2+, with Ca2+ showing the highest activity with similar
activity as the non EDTA treated control. Treatment with 2mM EDTA in the reaction,
stopped the enzymatic activity suggesting the influence of a metal ion on the catalytic
mechanism. The melting temperature of the enzyme was tested with DSF, resulting
in a Tm of 43.95 ± 0.02 °C. The low Tm for RuPL0 M indicates that the enzyme has
low stability at high temperatures.
Out of five selected PL17 members RuPL0 M showed highest sequence and struc-
tural similarity to the glucuronan lyase (OPIT5 27565) with 48.2% sequence identity
and rmsd of 0.987. In the proposed active site, RuPL0 M showed catalytic residues
matching the residues in the PL17 family for the Tyr’/Tyr β-elimination mechanism.
However, the His in the Asn/His pair reported in Alya3 and Alg17C is not present
in RuPL0 M. However, another Asn/His pair was located in RuPL0 M catalytic site,
with the His being conserved in the OPIT5 27565 and Acid 4041.
With information provided in this thesis, the classification of RuPL0 M as a PL17
member is possible. The investigation of the enzyme provide new insight into the
family and could be a initiator in the creation of a new subfamily. Furthermore, the
biochemical properties uncovered during this work for the RuPL0 M, raises questions
about the Ruficoccus sp. ZRK36. The finding does not support the physio-chemical
properties present in the organisms natural environment. Further investigation of the
organism, would provide better insight into the organisms degradation pathways and
give information about the available substrates and materials in the environment.
6
Further perspectives
From the work conducted during this thesis, the target RuPL0 M has been thoroughly
investigated, while the work has provided limited information about the remaining tar-
gets. With RuPL0 M in focus, more work needs to be conducted on the remaining
targets to elucidate the activities and characteristics of these non-classified PLs in the
Ruficoccus sp. ZRK36. The investigation of the remaining targets could be revised, as
part of future work to uncover more knowledge about this organism the large number
of PLs in its genome. During the work, only 14 substrates were tested during and
the expansion of substrates tested could provide new insights into the non-classified
PLs. Furthermore, additional investigation of RuPL0 M and solvin the structure could
provide better insight into the structure-function repationship of the target. The crys-
tallization of the catalytic domain of RuPL0 M was initiated, but did not form crystals
Page 59 of 79
==PAGE:61==
by the time of writing. Additional crystal screens should be conducted to find optimal
crystallization conditions and structure determination with and without bound sub-
strates, would provide further insight into the structure-function of RuPL0 M. The
polyspecificity of RuPL0 M with the activity on polyM, poly-HA, poly-GlcA, CSA
and CSC require further structural exploration to unravel the molecular basis of the
observed polyspecificity. Investigation of the N-terminal CBM with binding studies of
the isolated CBM and kinetic assays of the truncated RuPL0 M, could provide insight
into the relationship between the CBM, the activity and specificity of RuPL0 M. Ad-
ditionally crystallization and structure determination could provide insight into the
binding mechanism of the CBM to the substrates. The further investigation of the
non-classified PLs in the Ruficoccus sp. ZRK36, will improve upon the knowledge of
the organism, but also provide biochemical information about proteins that currently
cannot be functional annotated based on sequence alone.
