Abstract
i
Abstract
Regression models of power production and mechanical loads are a useful tool for wind
turbine performance assessment and optimization. In this thesis models have been fitted
on operational SCADA data from turbines of the La Sole du Moulin Vieux wind farm
to predict power and blade root flapwise DEL, edgewise DEL and flapwise moments.
The models’ ability to generalize to other turbines than they one the are trained on is
investigated through two main cases. Testing the power prediction model on another
wind farm, and testing the load prediction model on another turbine in the SMV wind
farm. In an attempt to improve the generalization ability of the models, features based
on physical considerations related to the geometrical layout of the wind farm were engi-
neered and tested. The features aims to parameterize the wake effects without making
use of the wind direction measurement which is thought to be detrimental to generaliz-
tion performance.
For the power production case, these features do not provide any improvements. It is
hypothesized that this is due to the optimized layout of the wind farm that the model
is trained on. Training a model on an artificially biased dataset with a large fraction
of wake incidents instead creates a situation where the wake parameterization features
improves the predictions.
For load prediction, improvements are generally obtained through the use of the wake
parameterization features. This is assumed to be due to the larger dependence on turbu-
lence of loads compared to power. The results initially obtained for load prediction using
a Multilayer Perceptron model is compared to results obtained for the main modelling
cases using other models: Polynomial Chaos Expansion, Gaussian Process Regression
and XGBoost. XGBoost is found to most often deliver the best performance, both on
the turbine it is trained on and when generalizing the model to the other wind turbine,
except in a few cases where PCE has better generalization performance.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The wind industry continues to undergo significant growth and wind power is now a
cornerstone in strategies towards a green transition of many countries’ energy sectors
across the planet. As of 2019, wind energy accounted for 15% of all electricity produced
in the EU [1]. Due to factors like technological improvements, industrialization and
standardization, supply chain maturation and market competition, the levelized cost
of wind energy (LCoE) keeps decreasing and in many cases LCoE has now reached a
lower level than traditional fossil alternatives [2]. Additionally, an increasing number
of wind energy project auctions are only accepting subsidy-free bids from developers.
The financial competitiveness of wind energy causes more and more investments in ad-
ditional wind capacity to be decided and thus a positive feedback loop that accelerates
the development of the technology further seems to be established.
Consequently, the already sizeable number of operating wind turbines can only be ex-
pected to increase drastically in the foreseeable future.
In this way, wind energy is
becoming a mature industry that needs to address challenges related to value optimiza-
tion of operating assets to a larger extent than earlier in the industry’s life cycle where
installation of new capacity was more in focus. This development has led to increased
interest in a range of topics within wind energy that all fall under the umbrella of
performance assessment and optimization. Some examples are power curve monitor-
ing, structural health monitoring, condition monitoring and predictive maintenance and
power forecasting. As modern utility scale wind turbines generate vast amounts of op-
erational data that can be used as input for data-driven models, it is becoming more
and more clear that such models are valuable tools when approaching problems within
these topics. Many tasks within these areas can be approached by fitting a mathemati-
cal model to predict a target variable given a set of input variables, i.e. by performing
regression analysis. The approach is sometimes also referred to as surrogate modelling
on account of the applications where the fitted model is built to approximate the output
of a computationally more complex one. But whether the target variable being mod-
elled is the output of e.g. a high-fidelity aero-elastic model or measured output from an
operating wind turbine, the aim will be similar: To obtain valuable insight into wind
turbine operations by fitting accurate and quickly evaluable prediction models.
This thesis is concerned with the problem of fitting these models. More specifically, it

explores machine learning algorithms as a way to fit models that predicts power output

1.2
Scope and research questions
The thesis focuses on the implementation and evaluation of different machine learning
(ML) models for regression/surrogate modelling of wind turbine power and loads. As
outlined in [3], ML models are already prevalent and achieving good performance within
many areas in wind energy, with Artificial Neural Networks (ANN) being the most fre-
quently employed model class. A similar conclusion can be drawn from the literature
review in ??. The thesis seeks to compare ANN models’ performance with several other
types of data-driven regression models on datasets consisting of SCADA data and blade
root bending moment measurements.
The task at hand is thus to fit models, f, that predict a target value (power or load)
given an M-dimensional feature set of SCADA data input, XT = (X1, X2, ..., XM):
f(X) = ˆy
(1.1)
By comparing the prediction with the true value, y, the model performance can be
assessed using suitable error metrics.
For this thesis two additional restrictions are
imposed on the feature set, X:
• Each X is constructed so that it does not contain time-lagged values of the target
y.
• Each X is constructed so that it does not contain time-lagged values of the features.
In other words, the problem is not approached from a time series perspective. These
delimitations are motivated mainly by a consideration of possible fatigue load prediction
model use cases. For load modelling, the measurement campaigns usually only involve
a few turbines of a wind farm because of the high costs associated with installation
and frequent calibration of the measurement instrumentation. For this reason, it is of
interest to evaluate the performance of the different ML models on datasets from other
wind turbines than they are trained on and - if possible - to use them on turbines where
no target load values have been measured, ruling out this channel as a possible feature.
Also, the definition of fatigue loads as Damage Equivalent Load (DEL) based on rainflow
counting and summation of the higher-frequency load cycles makes it less straightfor-
ward to justify that the obtained DEL values should exhibit noticeable time-dependence
with respect to both the signal itself and the input features.

1.3 Literature review
3
The ability of a model to make predictions for another turbine than it was trained on
will be referred to as generalization.
In the thesis, generalization ability is explored
for both load and power models. For power, a dataset from another nearby wind farm
operated by ENGIE Green was made available for tests. For loads, datasets from two
different turbines of the SMV wind farm are available making it a possibility to test
trained models on the other turbine.
The thesis objective is then to answer the following questions in relation to predicting
power and loads:
1. Which of the investigated ML models achieve the best performance on the turbines
they are trained on?
2. Which of the models generalizes best to other turbines?
3. Can the models’ ability to generalize be improved through feature engineering
based on physical considerations?
1.3
Literature review
1.3.1
Power modelling
Modelling of wind turbine power output is a well-studied problem with ML approaches
being most often applied to forecasting problems. Though this thesis is not directly
concerned with forecasting, many papers within this field can provide ideas on models
and performance. One such example is [4] where the authors approach forecasting with
methods that have significant carryover to the surrogate modelling in this thesis. First,
they predict the power output using a Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) model fore-
cast input and then try a two-step method where the power output is predicted based
on an initial forecast of the wind speed. A Feed-forward Neural Network (FNN) is found
to generally outperform a range of other ML methods such as Support Vector Machines
(SVM) and Random Forests. Many papers also deal with modelling the power curve
more directly. Within this research area, the chosen approaches are usually grouped
into classes of parametric models as in e.g. [5] or non-parametric ones. The latter case
includes many ML methods which essentially is the same approach to power modelling
as this thesis concerns. An example of an application of these can be found in e.g. [6]
where different kinds of FNN models are compared as prediction models in an online
power performance monitoring application. In [7] parametric models (multi-parameter
logistic expressions) are compared to different non-parametric models from the ML and
data mining domain. Using different metaheuristics for the parameter optimization, the
parametric methods can in some cases outperform the investigated machine learning
models, however a FNN is among the top models in most of the test cases.

Not surprisingly1, the most important input feature is found to be the wind speed. Poten-
tial positive effects of including other atmospheric variables like air density, temperature
gradients and ambient turbulence have been highlighted in recent papers, [8, 9]. Wind
direction is also found to be a frequently occurring input feature in power modelling. In
addition to the wind speed, [10] uses wind direction to fit a two-dimensional power curve
for the relative power output2 of the tightly spaced offshore wind farm Lillgrund. The
authors also propose a set of features to parameterize the wake effects and associated
drops in relative power for certain wind directions that the wind farm layout gives rise
to. For a given turbine in wake these features’ values are functions of the fraction of
the rotor area that is blocked by a wake (assumed to be cylindrically shaped) and the
distance to the upstream turbine responsible for the wake. It is shown how the wind
direction can be replaced by these geometric features at the cost of a modest drop in
performance of a FNN model on Lillgrund. With a model that then depends on a pa-
rameterization of relative wake interactions instead of wind direction values the idea is,
that the model can be transferred to make predictions at any site of arbitrary layout
with the same wind turbine model installed. This is tested by making predictions on the
Nørrekær Enge wind farm, which is an onshore wind farm in a straight line layout which
is significantly different from the grid-like structure of Lillgrund. When transferring the
model to Nørrekær Enge, an increase in Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Root Mean
Square Error (RMSE) from 3.5% to 6.1% and 4.1% to 6.7% is observed. The paper thus
provides a suggested method for improving the generalization ability of a data-driven
model in wind power making it very relevant for the work done in this thesis.
1.3.2
Load modelling
Earlier work on data-driven load modelling based on SCADA data is a bit more scarce,
presumably due to the comparatively smaller amount of load measurement datasets
available for research. However, earlier studies in the field have been identified, with
early examples being [11, 12].
The approach in here is to train an FNN to predict
DEL and load spectra for different loads. The model is trained on 120 hours of 10-min
SCADA data and load measurements from a Multibrid3 M5000 5MW onshore turbine
and tested on 30 hours of data. [12] also contains information on a modelling experiment
on two Nordex N80 turbines from the ECN Wind Turbine Test Station Wieringermeer
(EWTW) where a FNN model trained on one turbine is tested on the other. For in-
plane and out-of-plane DEL the standard deviation of the Mean Relative Error (MRE)
generally increases 50-100% when generalizing the model to another turbine, and much
more for the bias. The author partially attests this to difference in the observed rela-
tionships between loads and power between the two turbines. [13] is another example
also fitting an FNN model to provide a full wind farm fatigue load monitoring system.

1.3 Literature review
5
As part of the model validation procedure, this work also contains a two-turbine gener-
alization experiment on turbines from ECNs EWTW. Within normal turbine operation
conditions different load cases corresponding to different wake situations (undisturbed,
partial wake, full wake) are investigated and for the flapwise DEL the generalization
works noticeably worse in wake situations. The impact of wake situations in data-driven
load modelling are also investigated in [14], although not from a model generalization
point of view. For a turbine in an offshore setting (Baltic 1 wind farm) the performance
of a FNN model is investigated for data corresponding to six different cases of wake
situations. The model performance is generally lower for the cases where the turbine
is in wake, with the lowest scores being achieved for the single-wake case. The last
example of reported model generalization encountered is [15], in which an a Nonlinear
Autoregressive Exogenous (NARX) Neural Network model is fitted to predict the flap-
wise bending moment of an unspecified onshore turbine. Using 10-min datasets of 100
hours, the model is transferred to another turbine at a different site - The R2 value is
reported to decrease to 0.93 when transferring the model compared to 0.97 when training
a model on the new turbine. This reference is also the only example encountered which
approaches the load modelling based operational SCADA data with another model than
the FNN.
In terms of selection of input data for the model [16] provides an overview of the number
of times different common SCADA signals and their associated 10-min statistics have
been selected. The most often utilized signals are generator speed, power and pitch angle
with the mean values and standard deviation the most popular statistics. Wind speed
and wind direction are also fairly popular choices, but to a lesser extent than the afore-
mentioned ones. The authors then proceed to consider different strategies for selecting
an optimum set of features (predictors) from an initial set of all available SCADA signals
based on e.g. Spearman correlation thresholding, Recursive Feature Elimination, and
Principal Component Analysis. Based on this, the strategies resulting in relatively small
sets of features does not seem to result in overall worse model performance for an FNN
model fitted on an offshore turbine (the same as in [14]). A lot of this work is summed
up in [17]. [11, 12] are examples of a non-standard approach to feature set generation
as they also utilize spectral moments derived higher frequency data. Another approach
to input data set generation is found in [18] where the data is divided into three sets
according to production regimes: zero production and production below and above rated
power. Separate FNN models are then fit on each set with the goal of predictiong thrust
loads on the tower and transition piece of an offshore turbine. Approaches like this can
lead to concerns about sufficiency of data within each regime and some general insight
on the amount of 10-min SCADA data input needed to fit a robust load prediction model
is the subject of [19]. Here it is concluded that around two weeks of randomly sampled
data from a one-year measurement period is sufficient to obtain reliable results. [20]
then goes on to investigate the needed period of consecutive measurements in the case
of flapwise load prediction and reports that around 20 days is sufficient while seasonal
bias does not seem to be an issue.

Regarding the preferred choice of models the FNN model seems to be the completely
dominant approach within the papers surveyed for this thesis. Architectures mostly
range between 1-2 hidden layers with a range of 1-20 neurons in each layer usually being
investigated for optimum performance. One model with three layers (4 neurons in each)
was found in [21] which aims to predict 1s loads. In the cases where details on activation
function are reported, the sigmoid function is the preferred choice. To see reported re-
sults on other data-driven models than ANNs, it is necessary to turn to papers where the
modelled loads are simulated. In [22] a surrogate Polynomial Chaos Expansion (PCE)
model is used to predict load output from an aero-elastic code modelling a turbine in
Large Eddy Simulation (LES) flow. In [23] a PCE model is also used, along with other
different surrogate models as quadratic response surface and Kriging method. A com-
parison of a FNN and PCE performance is found in [24] where the three-layer FNN with
hyperbolic tangent activation function outperforms PCE for lifetime DEL prediction.
The same reference also provides an approach to a data-driven parameterization of wake
disturbances by inputting features with values for the distance and relative angle to the
disturbing turbine(s) for each training sample. Simulation-based studies are generally
found to be a useful addition to studies on measured SCADA. In [25] it is mentioned
how simulated loads can actually complement measured loads in a study because load
campaigns usually are not long enough to capture all relevant loading conditions and
[26] showcased how this idea can be put to work as transfer learning of an FNN.
1.4
Outline of the thesis
In chapter 2 an introduction to the SMV wind farm and the datasets that are made
available for the given study is provided. Some operational characteristics of the wind
turbines are described and an overview of the experimental setup of the load measure-
ment campaign is given. The process used to filter the datasets to only contain normal
operating conditions is also outlined.
In chapter 3 the main modelling problems being investigated are defined and the chosen
approach to model building is discussed. An overview of the terminology used when
reporting results for the generalization performance of the ML models is provided.
In chapter 4 a basic exploration and validation of the filtered datasets is carried out. The
aim is to validate the target variables and their correlation with SCADA measurements
and also to understand if any of the dataset shifts defined in chapter 3 are observed.
In chapter 5 the feature engineering carried out in an attempt to improve the ML models’
generalization performance is outlined. An overview of the different approaches tested
in the thesis is provided.

2.2
Wind Turbine: Senvion MM82
The Senvion MM82 turbines of SMV are rated at 2.05MW with a hub height of 80m
and a rotor diameter of D = 82 m. Operational characteristics of the turbine are shown
in Figure 2.2. Here, rotor speed, pitch angle and Tip Speed Ratio (TSR) are measured
values while power and thrust coefficients, Cp, Ct, and the normalized power curve are
provided by Senvion.
Figure 2.2: Senvion MM82 operational characteristics.
It is seen that the turbine is of the Variable Speed Variable Pitch type, as is the case
for most modern turbines of multi-MW rating. From the cut-in wind speed of 4 m/s to
around 9 m/s, the rotor speed of around 9 RPM is increased more or less linearly to it’s
rated value of 17.1 RPM. Hence, this defines the variable speed region. The increase
==PAGE:17==
2.3 Power datasets
9
in rotor speed with wind speed in this region is to maintain the TSR that allows for
obtaining the maximum Cp.
For wind speeds above 9 m/s, Cp starts do decrease since the rotor speed cannot be in-
creased above rated speed. Two additional regions of operation can be identified within
this range of wind speeds, depending on whether rated power has been reached or not.
For wind speeds below rated, the power is still increasing and the pitch angle is slowly in-
creased with wind speed from the fixed position at -1°. When rated power is reached, the
control objective becomes to not exceed this level in order to protect the generator. For
this reason, this region is designated as the constant power region. The constant power
level is achieved through continued pitch angle increase as a function of the increasing
wind speed in order to keep the rotor from accelerating. The pitch angle increase is
generally steeper than in the former region (called the constant speed region) where gen-
erator torque controls the rotor speed.
In relation to wake interactions, the operational characteristics also reveal that these
can be expected to be relatively more pronounced at lower wind speeds where Ct can
be seen to be highest. The sharp decrease in Ct is not observed until wind speeds are
high enough that the blades are pitched significantly.

2.4.1
Load measurement set-up
The load datasets provided for the project are recorded on the turbines SMV5 and SMV6.
The raw data is recorded with an in-house measurement system sampling at 100Hz. The
system is based on strain gauges utilizing Fiber Bragg Gratings which are optical fibres
that reflect light at certain wavelengths. When mounted on a wind turbine blade that
deforms under loading, a shift in wavelength of the reflected light will be detectable. This
shift can then be related to the strain of the blade through calibration. In order to mea-
sure flapwise and edgewise blade root bending moments four strain gauges are installed
90°apart in each blade on the same optical fiber. Two additional temperature sensors
are installed near the strain gauges to be able to correct the measured wavelengths ac-
cording to the temperature variations. The temperature law for wavelength correction
and the calibration of the corrected wave length is obtained by use of gravitational loads
during idling periods. Once the calibrated strain gauges have recorded load time series,
the statistics (mean, min, max and standard deviation) and Damage Equivalent Load
can be calculated. A more detailed outline of the system and calibration procedure is
available in [28].

CHAPTER 3
Modelling background
3.1
Approach to the regression problem
In the following, the approach to the regression problem adopted in the thesis is outlined.
As stated in the introduction the overall problem is to estimate a function/model f that
maps M-dimensional vectors of input features, XT
i = (Xi,1, Xi,2, ..., Xi,M) to a prediction
ˆyi of the target variable, yi. Here the index i = 1, 2, .., N is introduced to denote the
number of data samples available for modelling. From the modelling point of view, the
main decisions to be made then relate to the structure and to the algorithm chosen to
train a model.
The structure of the input dataset X has in this thesis been considered with a view
to the aim of assessing generalization ability of the models. An approach is therefore
chosen where the each input sample Xi consists of SCADA data from one particular
turbine. For power, data for NWT = 7 wind turbines is available. After filtering there is
63495 samples left per turbine in SMV (as per Table 2.1). For a set of M input features
these can be combined to a dataset, Xi,j, of dimension (7 · 63495) × M. A model fit
using training data from this dataset can be referred to as a ”turbine-agnostic” model
since it the training data does not provide any information on how the samples relate
to each other across turbines.1 This has the advantage that the model then is fitted to
predict an arbitrary turbine and is readily available to generalize to another wind farm.
Forming a dataset this way will also expose the model to a lot of similar but slightly
different examples of input-output relations. If this is considered equivalent to a dataset
with a small amount of noise added to the input, the noise has been shown to have a
regularizing effect [29]. This can be considered an added benefit, since an overfitted
model would be expected to have worse generalization ability.
The same approach is adopted for blade load modelling. The difference is that here
only one value per SCADA feature is recorded for every three simultaneous blade loads.
Therefore it is necessary to duplicate the SCADA features to form a dataset for fitting
a ”blade-agnostic” prediction model. If taking for instance the filtered SMV5 dataset of
10949 samples, it is collected to a dataset, Xi,j, of dimension (3 · 10949) × M, but there
will only be 10949 distinct samples of input features. In other words, during training
1As opposed to e.g. an approach where the feature set contains SCADA readings from multiple
turbines. In this case the model can learn some spatial correlations witin the wind farm but it is less
clear how to generalize such a model to e.g. another wind farm.

the model will see the same unique SCADA feature vector correspond to three different
blade loads. It is assumed that this approach will result in the model then being forced
to somehow interpolate between the three instances. Another approach could have been
to predict all three blade loads simultaneously, but this would probably anyway require
some kind of averaging afterwards if the aim is to apply the model to prediction of
loads on an arbitrary unseen blade. Another advantage of the adopted approach is that
increases the dataset size. This is considered very beneficial when the aim is to train a
model that can be generalized to other turbines.
3.2
Model generalization
This section outlines the cases and modelling experiments carried out in this thesis to
shed light on generalization ability of the trained machine learning models.
3.2.1
Main generalization cases
To assess the generalization ability of machine learning models, two main cases are
defined based on the data made available for the thesis:
1. Fitting a ”turbine-agnostic” model using SMV data and testing it on turbines in
the Nearby WF.
2. Fitting a ”blade-agnostic” model using SMV5 blade loads and testing it on SMV6
and vice versa.
The first case concerns power prediction while the other case is carried out for predi-
cion of flapwise DEL, edgewise DEL and flapwise moments. Edgewise moments are not
considered as they are expected to be almost purely driven by gravitational loads and
hence not sufficiently related to the SCADA measurements.
3.2.2
Dataset shifts
First, terminology that will be useful throughout the thesis when discussing the chal-
lenges related to model generalization is introduced.
Adopting parts of a taxonomy
proposed in [30] for transfer learning problems in wind energy, the problem can be
viewed as a task of fitting a model in a source domain and testing it in a different do-
main, called the target domain. The difference between the domain is to be understood
as a statistical difference between the joint distributions of input and output data, which
can be formulated as PS(X, y) ̸= PT(X, y). As the joint probability can be written as
p(X, y) = p(y|X)p(X), it is clear that the difference in joint distributions between source
and target domain can arise from changes in either the conditional probability of the
outputs (i.e. power and loads) given the inputs or in the distribution of inputs (or both).
==PAGE:25==
3.2 Model generalization
17
One resource dealing with these, [31], refers to a change in p(X), the input distribution,
as a covariate shift. The change in p(X|y) is generally referred to as a concept shift.
Because any of these can be expected to affect generalization ability of a trained model,
an attempt is made to understand whether any of these take place in relation to the
generalization cases defined.
3.2.3
Concepts of cross-validation
In the source domain, the fitted models will generally be evaluated by performing 5-
fold cross-validation (CV) in the source domain, i.e. on the turbine-agnostic dataset
for power or the blade-agnostic dataset for loads. Adopting standard terminology for
model validation from [32], the source domain dataset is split into 4/5 training data and
1/5 validation data is held out for out of sample prediction - the process is repeated 5
times. The mean error obtained on the 5 validation sets then theoretically constitutes
an unbiased estimate of the generalization error which is the error on unseen data. The
term generalization can thus be made to fit the domain-specific usage being explored in
this thesis which is basically about measuring the model’s performance on unseen data
- with the extra perspective that the unseen data is from a target domain that is very
likely to have a different joint probability of input and output data. Thus, the usual ML
concept of a test set of unseen data can be associated with the power and load prediction
models’ performance when generalizing the model to the Nearby WF in case of power
prediction or to the other turbine in case of load prediction. A high-level overview of
this terminology with examples is provided in Figure 3.1.
An additional concept of cross-validation is developed in this thesis as an exploratory tool
that can possible provide some more general insights on model generalization. Referring
back to the ”turbine-agnostic” approach to dataset formation outlined in section 3.1, it
is possible to assemble the dataset using a different number of turbines (less than 7) and
predict power on each turbines within SMV using dataset formed by collecting datasets
from other turbines. This then also constitutes an example of model generalization, but
in a sense the problem is expected to be easier than the main generalization case because
dataset shifts related to a change in geographical location are not expected. For power,
this exercise can then be considering sort of a stepping stone towards the main general-
ization case from SMV to the Nearby WF. It is referred to as ”WT cross-validation” in
the thesis.
The concept can also be applied to loads, although here collecting datasets of data for
up to 5 blades involves data collected on the same turbine that is part of the main
generalization case from SMV5 to SMV6 and vice versa. In this case it is thus more
an integrated part of understanding the generalization behaviour of the models. This is
referred to as ”blade cross-validation”.

The MLP model implemented in this thesis is documented in e.g. [32] and consists of

model parameters and settings are given here:
• Activation function: Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU)

• Trainig epochs: 10 for power prediction, 20 for loads
3.4.2
Polynomial Chaos Expansion
PCE regression aims to determine the coefficients of a spectral representation of a ran-
dom variable using a basis of orthonormal polynomials [34]. The model implemented
in this thesis relies on the ChaosPy library, [35]. The distribution of the input features
is estimated by Gaussian Kernel Density Estimation. The library uses the Three-Term-
Recurrence relation to compute the polynomial basis. The polynomial order is set to 2
to avoid overfitting and keep computational time lower.

3.4.3
Gaussian Process Regression
A Gaussian Process (GP) is a random process where any joint collection of the random
variables are jointly Gaussian [36]. Because GP modelling involves inversion of a covari-
ance matrix, exact GP regression is a challenge in terms of memory for most engineering
datasets with many data points. Hence, the model implemented in this thesis relies
on approximate inference (stochastic variational inference) as available in the library
GPyTorch [37]. Generally 1000 inducing points is employed for the approximation and
a Matérn kernel is chosen as the covariance model.
3.4.4
XGBoost
XGBoost is a gradient boosting algorithm that relies on ensembles of decision trees. [38]
This thesis relies on the Python library of the same name. The models trained are kept
to a maximum tree depth of 3 and 100 estimators to avoid overfitting.

CHAPTER 5
Wake parameterization
5.1
Wake effects and generalization
Some of the main characteristics of wind flow behind a turbine is a velocity deficit and
increase in turbulence [39]. Therefore, the power production and loading on a wind
turbine in wake of one (or more) other turbines can be expected to be affected. For
a turbine in any given wind farm, the geometrical layout of the farm will determine
which wind directions give rise to wake incidents and to undisturbed flow. Hence, it is
a plausible assumption that wind direction is a valuable input feature for data-driven
prediction models, because they are to some extent able to associate wind direction with
occurrence of wake situations and undisturbed conditions, respectively.
In cases where the above assumption holds true, it can be expected to affect performance
if a model is tested on a turbine in a farm of different geometrical layout. The different
layout could imply that numerical values of wind direction readings associated with wake
conditions during model training now correspond to undisturbed flow and vice versa. If
wind direction as an input feature can be detrimental to a model generalization, it
becomes interesting whether the wake effects can be parameterized in a different way.
In order to not impede generalization, the parameterization needs to offer a way of
substituting the numerical wind direction value with a measure of wake effects based on
the relative turbine positioning in the layout.
5.1.1

overview is provided of the observed wake effects in the power and load datasets. It is
necessary to understand how the effects relate to wind direction before attempting to
construct new input features.

Based on the overview of the wake effects’ relation to wind direction, new input features
can be constructed in an attempt to parameterize these effects and hereby improve
generalization abilities of models. Ideas for the approaches investigated in this thesis
are sourced from [10], [24] and [25].
5.2.1
Overlap Ratio modelling
The following approach to feature engineering is inspired heavily by ides in [10]. A quick
background of this paper is provided here to help develop the ideas and highlight some
key differences between the paper’s use case and this thesis. The paper aims to train an
FNN to predict a target defined as the relative power of Lillgrund which is an offshore
wind farm with a dense, grid-like layout. Using data of 1-minute (and higher) resolution,
the authors define relative power,Pr, as:

Pmax is the power of the highest producing turbine at the given timestamp and will
effectively be one of the undisturbed turbines. It is thus a similar definition to how the
WF efficiency was calculated in subsection 5.1.1, except here the calculation is done for
each sample, instead of within sectors. By definition the target then takes values in [0,1]

==PAGE:50==

The authors then create two features related to the ratio of overlap between a wake and
the rotor area. The wake is assumed to be cylindrical such that it’s area equals the rotor
area and the Overlap Ratio (OR) can then be calculated using the geometrical concept
of circle-circle intersection. For each wind direction, all NWT turbines are considered and
an OR in the range [0,1] is calculated for each turbine taking all upwind turbines into
account. A point on the rotor is considered blocked if it is in the wake of any upwind
turbine. In addition, an Overlap Distance (OD) that weights all points on the rotor
with distance to the blocking turbine - unblocked points are assigned a large distance of
20 times the rotor diameter, assumed to be a distance equivalent to full recovery of any
wake. To get a set of only two additional features to describe the collective wake effects
of the wind farm, an average over all turbines is taken.
Because the target is relative power of the full wind farm these farm average geometrical
features are very correlated with the target. In an earlier paper investigating these fea-
tures (together with slightly tweaked versions) Pearson correlation values in the 0.8-0.9
range are reported [40]. The authors from [10] then substitute wind direction with these
features and test the model on Nørrekær Enge wind farm which is an onshore wind farm
in a more or less linear layout. It seems very likely that using wind direction in this
case would have resulted in worse generalization performance, although the comparison
is not reported. The proposed features is a way of parameterizing the wind directions
with wake effects by using just two features. One advantage of this approach is that
it easily accommodates generalization cases where the number of blocking turbines is
different in the source and target domain.
In this thesis, the aim is instead to predict power and loads on single turbines, which
makes the use case different.

6.1
Model architecture and feature set
For the power prediction model the MLP architecture has been decided through manual
tuning. A model with three hidden layers with 200 neurons in each has been found to
work well. It is hypothesized that the construction of the dataset for a ”turbine-agnostic”
model provides a large dataset with some intrinsic regularizing properties such that a
relatively large number of neurons does not result in overfitting when fitting a model.
The remaining details of model settings and parameters are given in chapter 3.
Feature selection is carried out by manual trial and error. It is decided to not make
use of the rotor speed features altogether because of the direct mechanical coupling to
the generator. Although pitch can also be related to the power production through the
control system (as seen in Figure 2.2), the correlation is lower, cf. Figure 4.3. As such,
this feature is deemed acceptable and the following set of input features is decided:
• Wind speed (mean, standard deviation)
• Temperature (mean)
• Pitch (mean, standard deviation)
Due to the issues with the vane angle measurements on SMV3 described in chapter 2,
this feature is not included. Otherwise it would have been considered a useful feature po-
tentially providing additional information on the inflow to the model. Besides pitch and
the wind speed features, temperature is included due to the influence of temperature on
air density which is again directly related to the energy content in the wind flow. Wind
direction and the different wake parameterization features are included individually to
assess the effect on generalization performance, as reported in section 6.3.

Since the current dataset does not provide a case where the generalization of the power
prediction model improves by using wake parameterization features instead of the wind
direction, an additional experiment is conducted to demonstrate that artificial conditions
can be constructed where the wind direction becomes detrimental to generalization per-
formance, as also hypothesized in [10]. As opposed to in [10] which models relative wind
farm power, the difference is that single turbine power is predicted here. To demonstrate
a situation where a wake parameterization is an improvement over Wind Direction the
following dataset is constructed:
• The datasets for both SMV and Nearby WF are limited to the sector between
[180,200] degrees. According to Figure 5.1 this sector has considerable wake effects
for SMV but is undisturbed for the Nearby WF.
• An additional number equal to 50% of the dataset after the above limitation is
sampled randomly and included in the training set. This is in order to allow the
MLP model to observe all wind directions during training.
The constructed dataset for SMV thus has a much larger degree of wake incidents relative
to it’s total size than the original dataset. Comparing the cases of Average Overlap
features vs Wind Direction on this dataset yields the generalization performance plotted
in Figure 6.6. Here, it is clear the Average Overlap parameterization outperforms Wind
Direction both on SMV and in terms of generalization. By training the neural network
on a dataset on a biased dataset with a larger fraction of wake incidents and generalizing
the model to a dataset biased towards mostly undisturbed conditions, the network uses
the wind direction in a way that results in worse performance compared to the wake
parameterization.
The performance on SMV with Average Overlap features here is
actually on par with the results using the dataset. This is slightly surprising, since it
could be expected that the general learning problem would become harder with a loke
of wake incidents.

First, the problem of predicting loads on one single blade is investigated. The results
provided in this section is based on usage of the Average Overlap wake parameterization.
Generally, two initial approaches to this task could be to:
• Predict a given blade using datasets formed by the consisting of data for the other
two blades from the same turbine
• Predict a given blade using a dataset consisting of data for the blades of the other
turbine.
The first case can be considered generalization of a model to another blade than it was
trained for, but on the same turbine. Since the SCADA input is the same for all three
blades of a turbine, no covariate shift can take place between source and target domain
in this case.
This will thus provide insight on whether any of the measured blades’
loads are more difficult to predict, due to a concept shift in p(y|X) between source and
target domain. The second case is a first look at the generalization case where a model
is transferred to a new turbine, but only predicting one blade at the time. Figure 7.1

two top figures show that while a model with lower RMSE can generally be trained on

Generalizing from SMV5 to SMV6, the MLP is outperformed by XGBoost which fares

• The best performing model in terms on the turbine of which it is trained on is
generally XGBoost, closely followed by MLP. The difference in performance is not
large enough to clearly recommend one or the other.
• The best performance on the training turbine generally seems to translate to the
best generalization performance, hence the two models above are recommended.
• The best generalization results are obtained using the proposed Avg. Ov. wake
parameterization features in combination with Wind Direction, which is defined
as WD & Avg. Ov. approach.

8.4
Conclusions
Based on the edgewise DEL modelling results, the following conclusions regarding the
main thesis objectives are drawn:
• As for flapwise DEL, the best performing model in terms of performance on the
turbine it is trained on, are again XGBoost and MLP. Again, the difference in
performance is not very pronounced.
• As opposed to the flapwise DEL case, the best performing model on the training
turbine does not necessarily translate to the best generalization performance, hence
the two models above are recommended.
• The best generalization results are obtained using the proposed Average Overlap
wake parameterization features in combination with the Wind Direction, which is
also gives the best performance for flapwise DEL.

Discussion and Conclusions
For power, the wind direction was observed to not have any significant influence on the
results when using the full dataset. A possible explanation for this can be a consequence
of the way the dataset is formed to fit a ”turbine-agnostic” prediction model. With this
approach, the model observes a lot of different power values for a given fixed value of the
wind direction due to general variations in the wind speed. However, dropping the wind
direction or replacing it with one of the proposed wake effect parameterizations does
not seem to have any significant effect on model performance, neither on SMV where
it is trained nor on the Nearby WF where it is tested to assess generalization ability.
This could indicatethat the layout of the two wind farms constitute a case where the
wake effects are not observed often enough for a data-driven model to learn any clear
patterns. This is supported by the constructed experiment where the model is trained
on a reduced dataset consisting mostly of samples with a high degree of wake effects. In
this case, it becomes possible to detect a difference in peformance.
Another possible contribution to the observed independence of wind direction and any of
the tested wake parameterization features for model performance on both SMV and the
Nearby WF could also be the fact that the power is simply driven mostly by the wind
speed. When a turbine is in wake the recorded wind speed will automatically reflect the
velocity deficit causing the drop in power. That the wind is measured with a nacelle
anemometer obviously introduces some uncertainty because the anemometer itself is in
wake of the rotor, but on average the mean wind speed variation should account for
most of the observed power production. A wake parameterization feature could then be
seen as an attempt to provide information about when wake situations induce a certain
relation between increased turbulence, lower wind speed and the observed power which
the data-driven model is hopefully able to learn. But a reason for the model not im-
proving from having the wake parameterization input features could simply be that the
pattern associated is much more complex compared to mean wind speed as the most
important predictor. The conclusion drawn from the wake-biased experiment in subsec-
tion 6.3.1 could support this hypothesis in so far that the validation performance (i.e.
in the source domain) of the SMV model was perfectly on par with the model trained
on the full dataset having relatively more instances of undisturbed conditions.
A positive bias was observed when transferring the model to the Nearby WF. It is very
likely that this can in part be due to a general concept shift between the two domains.
