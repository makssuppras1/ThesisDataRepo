Abstract
In the expanding sector of aquaculture, the sustainable production of aquatic
organisms plays a vital role in the he increasing global need for protein. Diseases
pose a significant threat to aquaculture, harming the health and the productivity
of the aquatic organisms. Securing the welfare of these organisms is necessary
in order to achieve an extensive production. This study investigates the efficacy
of ultraviolet (UV) treatment, in combating the Infectious Pancreatic Necrosis
Virus (IPNV), while a medium pressure lamp (MP) of a collimated beam appa-
ratus was used. Throughout the experiment, the effectiveness of UV treatment
was assessed by comparing two distinct dilution factors (1:100 and 1:10.000) us-
ing PBS. Additionally, the impact of varying power inputs on log inactivation
under identical UV doses was investigated. The dilution of IPNV in different
solvents (PBS and Milli-Q water) with diverse UVT values (24% and 100%) was
carried out to evaluate the efficiency of UV treatment across different environ-
mental conditions. Results displayed that there are not significant differences
among the two dilution factors in respect to log inactivation. Moreover, higher
power inputs seem to offer a more efficient UV treatment. Using a 100% UVT
value, a 3.1 log reduction was achieved (≈99.9% inactivation of the virus) at the
dose of 30 [mj/cm2]. While at 24% UVT values, a 3.3 log reduction was noted
at 200 [mj/cm2]. The impact of the system’s temperature was also investigated
to determine whether it has any influence on the virus. Nowadays, there are not
many publications referring to the inactivation of IPNV using a MP pressure
lamp, different power inputs and dilution factors. By exploring the efficiency of
UV treatment, this study aims to contribute valuable insights to the aquaculture
field, pointing towards a positive direction for improving the welfare and the sus-
tainability of the disinfection aquaculture practices.
Keywords: IPNV, UV disinfection, Collimated beam apparatus, Medium
Pressure lamp

Introduction
1.1
Aquaculture Industry
Aquaculture plays a crucial role in meeting global demands for fish production, ad-
dressing essential requirements for human nutrition and animal feed [1].
The Food and
Agriculture Organisation of the United Nations (FAO) report showed that, in the year 2020,
the total production of aquatic organisms was recorded at 122.6 million tonnes. 87.5 million
tonnes were accounted for fish production. Expansions in Chile, Norway and China lead the
increase of the total production while Nigeria and Egypt had decrease in comparison with
the previous years. China leads global aquaculture production, accounting almost 92% of
the total output [2]. The importance of the aquaculture industry is great both in financial
terms but also in covering large amount of the global food needs. Based on Eurostat, in
Europe the country with the highest fish aquaculture production is Norway with around 1.6
million tonnes in 2021. In the same year, Denmark noted a 35.6 thousands tonnes of fish
production[3]. In Denmark there are aproximately 215 farms with rainbow trout being the
most farmed fish. The major part of the fish production occurs in fresh water recirculating
farms (flow-through systems) [4].

1.2
Aquaculture Systems and Fish Welfare
1.2
Aquaculture Systems and Fish Welfare
Aquaculture systems display a high variety, each of which meets unique requirements
of aquatic organisms. Some examples are the water-based systems such as cages and pens
both inshore and offshore. Land-based systems, including ponds and flow-through systems
(raceways). As well as, recirculating systems, that are ranging from high to low recirculation
capacity [5]. In aquaculture systems such as Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS), the
monitoring of the water quality is considered more efficient. They use filtration units (drum
filters) to remove bigger particles of feed or faeces, bio-filters so as to convert ammonia into
nitrate and disinfection tools, such as ozonation and Ultraviolet radiation [6]. Water quality
is one of the main parameters that can affect the fish growth and welfare. It is proven that
poor water quality correlates with high mortality rates in aquaculture. When the amount
of dissolved oxygen (DO), ammonia (NH3), carbon dioxide(CO2) or other pollutants are
altered negatively, then the fish welfare is affected. This happens especially in aquaculture
units where there no monitoring and control of the environment that fish grow [7],[8]. Even
the slightest alteration in one of these parameters can affect the immune system of the or-
ganisms and as a consequence, opportunistic pathogens can infect them [9]. Having a good
water quality means that there is a reduction in frequency of disease outbreaks on the aqua-
culture unit [10],[11].
The aquaculture industry faces a significant risk from diseases, potentially resulting in a
10-15% production cost. It is necessary to keep a good state of the water quality so as
to prevent the disease spread. The main disease problems that occur in aquaculture are
bacterial, parasitic, viral and fungal [12]. Denmark, is considered free of Viral Haemor-
ragic Septicaemia (VHS), since 2009. In 2021 experienced the first outbreak of Infectious
Hematopoietic Necrosis (IHN). While, occasional disease outbreaks of Piscine orthoreovirus
subtype 3 (PRV-3) and Infectious pancreatic necrosis virus (IPNV), occur. Furthermore,
among bacterial diseases, Rainbow trout Fry Syndrome and Enteric Red Mouth Disease are
considered relevant in fresh water aquaculture units [4].
Apart from the water quality as a parameter to mitigate diseases occurring in a unit, stock-
ing density plays an important role. The proper stocking density is needed to be maintained,
in order to reduce the frequency of the disease to be transmitted from one host to another.
Furthermore, the exchange of fish population that are not tested for the absence of any
pathogen should be avoided [13]. Also the amount of feed given to the fish should be mon-
itored. Excess amount of feed will accumulate in the aquaculture system, resulting in high
risk of unwanted pathogens to develop due to high organic load.
1.3
Infectious Pancreatic Necrosis Virus
Infectious Pancreatic Necrosis Virus (IPNV), was firstly named as acute catarrhal en-
teritis in 1951 by M’Goningle [14]. Due to histopathological studies of the species brook
trout (Salvelinus fontinalis), the named was changed to IPN by Wood et al. [15]. The virus
belongs to the family Birnaviridae of the genus Aquabirnavirus and was the first virus to
be in-vitro isolated in fish [16]. IPN is a non-enveloped virus with average size around 60
nm, with a double-stranded RNA [17]. The genome is approximately 6 kbp in overall size,

==PAGE:6==

with the two doubled stranded molecules designated as A and B. Segment A contains two
open reading frames, the largest of which encodes a polyprotein that forms two structural
proteins VP3 and VP2 and a viral protease VP4. The major capsid protein is VP2, which
contains the antigenic sites that are responsible for cellular binding and tropism, while the
inner capside protein VP3, contains antigenic determinants which are group specific. Seg-
ment B contains VP1 protein that is the RNA polymerase and exists as a genome-linked
protein VPg that binds tightly the two segments to their ends [18].
The disease affects primarily salmonids(e.g Atlantic salmon) but also it can affect different
type of fish species such as eels,turbot,striped bass and halibut. The life stage of the host
and the viral concentration can cause a variety of symptoms and high mortality rates [19].
The virus has the most persistent infectivity out of all the known fish virus, in both fresh
and salt water [20]. In the fresh water, IPNV primarily impacts fry post-yolk sac absorp-
tion, with salmonids being the most vulnerable, potentially causing mortality rates of up
to 80% in the population. While in the salt water,during the smoltification process it can
cause mortality up to 30% [21]. The disease displays clinical signs similar to other viruses.
The ”corkscrew” erratic swimming, darkening of the skin and anorexia are some of the most
frequent symptoms that IPNV causes [22].

The fish that survive the causative agent remain as carriers of the virus without indi-
cating any symptoms. Therefore, they can transmit the pathogen via the faeces, urination
and the reproductive system [24]. Apart from the main reservoir which is the fish as carriers,
there can be also other reservoirs. Isolation of IPNV has also been found in wild fish, birds,
sediments below the net pens and in shellfish [25],[26]. A recent study, used IPNV-exposed
blue mussels(Mytilus edulis) with the IPN strain WB, to determine whether they can infect
the fish species Atlantic salmon smolts(Salmo salar). There was a low frequency in trans-
mission of the specific strain from the shellfish to the Atlantic salmon and the authors claim

that in north Europe where Sp strain is more present the transmission may be higher [27].
A recent study has shown that the disease can also affect even the genetically modified fish
that display resistance to the virus. It is known that RNA viruses have a high rate of muta-
tions. Due to the rapid mutations of the virus genome, it can evade the defence mechanisms
of the genetic modified fish [28]. Another study indicated that the high mutation rates of
IPNV, alongside with the rapid replication of the virus in dense populations of aquaculture
and wild fish, can lead the creation of mechanisms that can avoid the immune system of
the fish. This makes the virus more persistent in certain environments, making it more
challenging to eradicate it [29].
The diagnosis of IPNV is mainly done by the isolation of the virus in cell cultures, followed
by identification of the isolate by neutralization test (ELISA) and PCR. Other qualitative
methods are also used such as histopathology and the observation of clinical signs [30], [31].
In the isolation of fish cells cultures, kidney is the preferred tissue for sampling, as it contains
high concentration of the virus [18].
In order to reduce the incident of a IPNV outbreak, vaccination is commonly used. Different
strategies for developing vaccines for IPNV have been tested. In Atlantix salmon, a living
virus vaccination was tested to be unsuccessful. For this reason, vaccination with inactivated
virus via the oral route, immersion and injection was used in rainbow trout, with injection
being the most efficient type. Unfortunately, these type of vaccination (inactivated virus)
is a very expensive method [32]. Nowadays, there are other different types of vaccines such
as DNA vaccines and sub-unit vaccines. DNA vaccines (oral delivery) containing the VP2
gene, have demonstrated reduced viral infection and a high degree of protection against the
virus [33],[34]. A recent publication [35], displayed that the production of recombinant VP2
sub-unit vaccine (IPNV-VP2), can have almost the same result as inactivated whole particle
virus vaccine (both for the Sp serotype). The authors, compared these two vaccine types.
Sub-unit vaccine displayed a 70% survivability of rainbow trout population that was tested.
While the inactivated vaccines displayed a 79% survivability. It can be concluded that the
reconbinant vaccine (sub-unit) can protect fish against IPNV with high efficacy. Another,
prevention method of IPNV, is the contribution of Quantitative Trait Loci (QTL). The
identification of various QTLs is associated with resistance against IPNV. It is a valuable
tool, used in breeding programs, such as the Norwegian Atlantic salmon. Having a better
understanding of QTLs information, can provide a better and precise selection in breeding
programs, to combat IPNV [36].
In 2023, IPNV was assessed weather it should be listed within the framework of the Animal
Health Law based, based on different criteria. The Animal Health Law is part of various
measures proposed by the European Commission, to deal with any hazards that affect the
agri-food chain [37]. The result of the report, states that it is uncertain weather IPNV should
be listed, as it does not meet most of the criteria. However, it is suggested that surveillance
activities should take place in order to provide information on the geographical distribution
of the virus. Also, research should be conducted in the areas where there is absence of data
or other limitations, so as to get a better understanding on the effect of IPNV in animal
welfare in Europe [38]. However, a number of compartments in Europe have gained official
freedom form IPNV by targeted surveillance.

Geographical distribution of IPNV in Europe
The disease was first found in Canada in 1940 in the Atlantic salmon (Salvelinus fonti-
nalis), and in 1950s it was found in the United States and spread throughout the world
[39]. In 1971 the first strain of IPNV was isolated in Europe and since then there are many
cases occurred until now [40]. The university of Santiago de Compostella in Spain has traced
some of the outbreaks of the IPNV in species level , based on the literature,in most of the
European countries and displayed them in a map. The Rainbow trout Oncorhynchus mykiss
is the primary host species in Denmark and the first case of isolation of IPN was in 1971
[41].

1.3.2
Classification of IPNV
There are two main ways of classification of aquatic Birnaviruses. The first method is
based on serological typing, where Aquabirnaviruses are divided into two serogroups, A and
B. The serogroup A has 9 different serotypes, while B serogroup only has one [42]. The
second approach, classifies the virus in 7 different genogroups, corresponding serotypes, type
strain and genotypes [43],[44]. In Denmark there are two serotypes of IPN virus. Serotype
Sp (Spajarup) and Ab (Abildt) make up serotypes A2 and A3, corresponding in genotypes 5
and 2,respectively. In Europe, most IPN outbreaks in salmonids have been associated with
genogroup 5 (Serotype Sp).

Ultraviolet Light
1.4
Ultraviolet Light
Ultra violet radiation (UVR), is one of the most common method used for pathogens
disinfection in aquaculture with high efficacy. In 1801, Johann Ritter discovered the UV light
by demonstrating that silver chloride is decomposed with higher efficacy by the invisible rays
beyond the violet [46]. In 1900, Niels Finsen, a Danish physician credited as the pioneer of
modern phototherapy, made a groundbreaking discovery in the treatment of lupus vulgaris,
a type of skin tuberculosis, using ultraviolet (UV) light. His remarkable contribution earned
him the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine [47].
UV radiation, is classified in 3 different categories, Ultraviolet A , B and C, based on their
wavelength (Figure 2).

The primary mechanism for microbial disinfection through UV radiation is the photo-
chemical reactions induced by UV on the genetic material. UVC and UVB are absorbed
significantly by DNA, leading to enhanced disinfection effects [49]. In contrast, UVA does
not induce DNA damage and does not engage in direct photochemical reactions with DNA.
UVA can produce reactive intermediates such as hydrogen peroxide and hydroxyl which can
damage the pathogens. RNA is more susceptible to oxidative damage in comparison with
the DNA [50],[51]. The two photo-products that are produced during the exposure to UV
are cyclobutane-pyrimidine dimers (CPDs) and 6-4 photo-products (6-4PPs). Even though
6-4PPs cause more significant disruptions in the structure of the DNA/RNA helix, the main
cause of the cells death is attributed to the cytotoxic effects of CPDs [52],[53].
UV Transmittance (UVT), indicates the UV light transmission factor in a water sample.
The UVT is expressed as a percentage % UVT. UV Transmittance is a measurement of the
ultraviolet light at 254 nanometers that is able to pass through 10 mm of water column. The
water quality of the water can affect the amount of light that can penetrate it. A high UVT,
such as 90 to 95 %, indicates the transmittance of the UV is high, and the water clarity
is at a satisfying level. As a consequence, the applied UV radiation will have the desired
impact on the pathogen population residing in the water [54]. Microorganism inactivation
can be achieved at UV wavelengths ranging from 100 to 400 nm. However, the wavelength
of 254 nm is proven to be the most effective one for disinfection. When the UVT values
are low, a higher amount of ultraviolet light is required, resulting in a higher operating cost
due to the required power [55]. A high inorganic and organic compound load can affect the
UVT and as a consequence reduces the efficiency of the applied photons to penetrate the
water column and reach the pathogens [56]. Elevated levels of suspended solids, can reduce
the efficacy of microorganisms inactivation by absorbing UV radiation, by the particles,

In RAS systems, the most common place of installation of the UV system is after the
biofilters just before the tank that contains fish. In flow-through systems, units are commonly
positioned both before and after the tank, prior to the discharge of water. This method
achieves the necessary level of disinfection at minimal costs[58].
1.4.1
Medium and Low pressure Lamps
Today, the two main lamps that are used for UV disinfection are low-pressure (LP)
and medium-pressure (MP) lamps. These two type of UV lamps are made of mercury. To
effectively transform electrical energy into radiation, mercury lamps need either elevated
electron temperatures or elevated temperatures of mercury atoms. Low pressure lamps emit
monochromatic light at 254nm [59]. They are cheaper compared to the MP lamps, operate
at lower temperatures, therefore they display less fouling and have longer operating life. On
the other hand, MP lamps are emitting light at multiple wavelengths. They require less
lamps than LP, in order to reach the same photon flux [60]. Many studies have shown that
MP lamps demonstrate increase cell inactivation and death efficacy, with reduced subse-
quent photo-recovery. Although the peak irradiation of low pressure lamps demonstrates
a closer association with the maximum absorbance spectrum of the nucleic acids (DNA
has an absorbance peak at the range of 250-262 nm and LP emitting at 254 nm), medium
pressure lamps display greater microorganism inactivation efficiency, providing also reduced
photorecovery [61],[62],[63]
1.5
Objectives
The aim of this study was to assess the efficiency of ultraviolet light as a tool for
disinfection. The pathogen that was selected as the main testing candidate was Infectious
Pancreatic Necrosis Virus (IPNV). In the course of this experiment, we evaluated (i) the
comparison among various dilution factors, (ii) the comparison of different power inputs
of the UV lamp to determine the more effective disinfection, (iii) the utilization of diverse
solvents and UVT values, and (iv) an examination of whether the system’s temperature
influences the virus during the trials.

2.1
Experimental Design
This project is a collaborative effort between DTU (Technical University of Denmark)
and Desmi Ocean. The experiment was conducted in the laboratories of the section of the
Fish and Shellfish Disease in the Technical University of Denmark. In this experiment, IPNV
was the only pathogen used as testing candidate. In all of the trials the same procedure was
followed. At first, the IPNV was propagated and stored in Eagles minimal essential medium
(EMEM). The viral stock was diluted in Phosphate buffered saline (PBS) or Milli-Q water,
in different concentrations depending on the experimental trial. Afterwards, the samples
were placed in small petri dish (35x10 mm) having a total volume of 1 mL (height of the
liquid in the petri dish is ≈0.5 cm) and were exposed in a UV lamp system provided by
Desmi Ocean in different UV doses. The exposed samples were titrated in BF-2 cells and
incubated for 7 days at 15 C. The results of the titration were obtained using a microscope.

2.2
IPNV Preparation
2.2
IPNV Preparation
The Sp serotype of the IPNV was tested in all of the trials. There were 3 different
type of dilutions of the IPN. Samples that were diluted 1:100 with PBS, samples that were
diluted 1:10000 with PBS and samples that were diluted 1:100 with Milli-Q water. PBS is
a non-toxic solution that prevents cell rupturing or shrivelling due to osmosis [64]. The first
type included dilution using PBS. The viral stock was diluted 1:100 with PBS. The samples
diluted 1:10000 with PBS went under two consecutive dilutions. The first dilution was 1:100
using Eagles minimal essential medium (EMEM) and then 1:100 dilution using PBS. The
reason for the consecutive dilution was to change the dilution of the viral stock, while at the
same time maintaining the same UVT value of the sample. The last type of samples were
diluted 1:100 but instead of PBS, Milli-Q water was used.
The UV Transmittance of the samples was measured using a spectrometer at 254 nm. The
samples containing PBS as solvant had a UVT value of 24%. The samples containing Milli-Q
water had a UVT value of 100%.
2.3
IPNV Quantification
During every trial, three replicates of each sample were used to assess variability across
technical replicates. The starting titers were measured prior the dilution and after the 1:100
and 1:10000 dilutions, in order to calculate the difference in viral concentration and measure
the log reduction. The titer of the viral stock is calculated as TCID50 /ml. The IPNV
concentration was measured by the titration of the 96 well-plates in BF-2 (cells 10% fetal
bovine serum (FBS), 4 mM L-glutamine, and 50mg/ml gentamicin). During the quantifica-
tion, various concentrations of the virus (10 fold dilution) were introduced into cells in each
well, from row A to G. The plates that were inoculated were placed in an incubator at at
15 ◦C for the duration of 7 days. After this time period, every cell plate was examined on a
microscope to determine whether or not there was any cytopathic effects (CPE). The viral
concentration was measured using the Reed and Muench method [65]. The formula used to
calculate the log reduction of IPNV is described bellow1:
Log Reduction = Log10(A) −Log10(B)
(1)
Where (A) is the number of viable virus before treatement and (B) is the number of
viable virus after the treatement[66].

exposed to UV (Figure5). The system ensures that the sample receives the precise amount
of radiation that is tested in every different trial. When the shutter opens, the sample is
exposed to UV radiation for the UV dose and time period which is pre-calculated. Once

Discussion
The decision to use IPNV as a candidate virus depends on various factors. IPNV ex-
hibits high resistance in UV disinfection and is considered one of the most resilient viruses
[45],[68]. Determining the required UV doses for disinfecting IPNV, allows us to assume that
applying these doses in aquaculture units would eliminate a significant portion of pathogens.
Furthermore, IPNV is a common virus found in many aquaculture facilities, posing a threat
to the welfare of the fish and the financial aspects of the units. An additional reason for
choosing this virus, is the ability to be measured on cell cultures, simplifying the experi-
mental processes. Furthermore, it is a extensively studied virus, enhancing the reliability of
research outcomes.
The ultraviolet transmittance (UVT) is one of the most important parameters, when it comes
to UV disinfection. In aquaculture systems it is vital to maintain a good water quality with
low turbidity and high UVT values, in order to have efficient disinfection. Nonetheless, there
are situations where low turbidity does not necessarily means better disinfection with UV.
Turbidity is a measure of the quantity of suspended solids in the water and is not related to
the organics or fine particles that tend to affect UVT . There are components that can affect
the UVT values such as humic acid, iron and colloidal solids [55]. UVT values can provide
a good insight of the UV doses needed to be applied in different aquaculture systems. For
example, tilapia is know to inhibit in turbid water [69]. Shrimps that are cultured in biofloc
have as well low turbid water. On the other hand salmonids, inhibit in an environment
with low turbidity [70]. Turbid water may require higher UV dosed, because UVT values
may be also low and vice versa. This helps the aquaculture units have a better control and
knowledge of the disinfection of the facility.
Moving on to the discussion of UV disinfection parameters. Throughout the whole experi-
ment, the starting titer of the all the samples examined, displayed deviation. This is why
it is important to compare each time the log reduction with the starting titers and the
evaluation of the log reduction as linear (Figures 9,11,10,12). All the different UV doses
were tested a different day. In every trial, the same experimental procedure was followed
including dilution of the viral stocks (with a ratio of 1:100 or 1:10000) and then exposing
the samples in UV radiation with subsequent titration seven days later. To avoid exper-
imental errors, the laboratory protocols were established strictly and attention was given
to maintain uniformity thought the different trials. None of the starting titers displayed a
log difference higher than 1, so it can be assumed that all the samples were tested without
experimental errors. This experimental variability can be explained due to the biological
dynamics complexity. This dynamic behaviour is influenced by different variables such as
cellular metabolism, the genome of the virus and inter-cellular signaling pathways. Even
under controlled experimental environment, these biological processes can lead to variability
not only the in initial titers but also in the titers measured after the UV exposure. This is
also why instead of displaying all of the results of the triplicates, the median values were
calculated and plotted.
An essential aspect in optimizing UV disinfection processes is to explore the impact and
significance of employing various power inputs. The different power inputs of the same UV
Page 30 of 33
==PAGE:34==
doses seem to affect the log reduction results. In the trials (Figures: 9 and 10), a higher
R2 value of 0.9 is evident at a power input of 6000W, and a similar pattern is observed in
trials with a dilution factor of 1:10000, where the R2 peaks at 6000W with an even higher
value of 0.96. This consistent trend, indicates that the data aligns more closely with the
trend-line, showcasing a stronger fit at higher power inputs. Using a higher power input
for a short time period, showcases a better disinfection than using lower power inputs with
longer exposure time. Unfortunately, all the research conducted in disinfection of fish virus
with UV, have not mentioned the power input that the UV system was operating on, during
their experiments. It may be assumed that a higher power input might allow for more rapid
and efficient disinfection within the same UV dose. Another, assumption is that the spectral
distribution of UV radiation at different power inputs could affect its disinfection capabili-
ties. Of course more research in demanded to understand why a different power inputs of
the same UV dose, display different results.
As this study initially involved diluting the starting viral stock at a ratio of 1:100, it became
essential to explore an alternative dilution factor. Opting for a 1:10000 dilution of the initial
titer serves as a valuable test. It could give as an insight into whether there are significant
differences in log reduction as the dilution factor increases or if the trend remains linear.
The only limitation of this study is that the UV doses of 60, 80 and 100 [mJ/cm2] were
not involved. Nonetheless, the conclusion of the results is that the 1:10000 dilution trial
displayed insignificant difference with the dilution factor of 1:100. A 3 log reduction (or
higher) is observed in almost all of the trials at 200 [mJ/cm2] UV dose. Although, three UV
doses were not included, it is clear that using a higher dilution factor does not affect the
linearity of the results. Additional experiments are needed, involving a broader range of UV
doses, increased repetition, and a more extensive set of dilution factors to be more certain
of this assumption. Furthermore, it is important to have caution when diluting the initial
titer extensively, as there is a risk of reaching a point where the titer becomes overly diluted,
leading to faster disinfection and overestimation of the results. Unfortunately, there are not
any studies that compare log reduction of IPNV or other pathogens with different dilution
factors, so as to have a comparison.
Apart from the significance of different power inputs and dilution factors, it was also neces-
sary to examine weather the temperature affected the viral samples during the exposure. It
is known that temperature is one of the main environmental factors affecting the stability
of many pathogens [71]. Due to the extreme temperature that the UV lamps emits, concern
was raised if this could affect the samples of this experiment that were placed inside the
shelf. The maximum temperature of the shelf, without using ice, would reach around 40 ◦C
in the time span of 70 seconds. Previous studies have examined the effect of the temperature
in IPNV. For instance, Stein H. et al.(1998) [72], described the stability of a scallop Pecten
maximus, IPNV isolate (Sp strain) conditions in water with various salinity. The virus was
exposed to various temperatures ranging from -80 to +40 ◦C via incubation. Significant
reduction (total inactivation) in viral viability was noted at both 30 ◦C and 40 ◦C, after
respective duration of 78 and 36 days. IPNV is considered one of the most heat-resistant
fish virus. Since the temperature of the shelf does not reach extreme temperature and every
test sample has a total duration of exposure no more than 104 second (Table 5), it can be
safely assumed that in this experiment, the temperature does not affect our results. Also
Page 31 of 33
==PAGE:35==
the usage of ice does reduce the temperature of the system by almost 10 ◦C in two of the
cases at UV doses of 180 and 220 [mJ/cm2] at the power inputs of 6000W. However, all
trials were conducted without placing ice in the shelf, since it was not necessary. One factor
that could possibly affect the log reduction of the virus could be the thickness of the sample
(IPNV+PBS or MilliQ) that is placed in the petri dish. If the liquid sample does not cover
all the surface of the petri dish or is not evenly distributed, then evaporation may occur due
to the high temperature and affect the results. Nevertheless, more experiments should be
conducted to assess this hypothesis.
Using Milli-Q water with high UVT value (100%), a higher log reduction was achieved. A
similar study [62], displayed differences compared to this study in the log reduction of IPNV.
Specifically, a medium-pressure (MP) lamp was used in saltwater with ultraviolet transmit-
tance (UVT) values exceeding 90%. The researcher observed a 2 log reduction with a 35
[mJ/cm2] UV dose. Interestingly, this experiment achieved a 3 log reduction with a lower 30
[mJ/cm2] UV dose. It is crucial to highlight that the precise UVT value of the water sample
and the power input of the UV lamp were not specified by the author. The differences in
log reduction between these two experiments may be attributed to variations in these pa-
rameters. It is important to note that the vast majority of the experiments published until
now, use low-pressure lamps (LP) as disinfecting tool of IPNV, so there is absence of data
and reports in order to make more comparisons.
Throughout this experiment, variations in log reduction under different UV transmittance
(UVT) values by employing diverse solvents, were also examined. Two types of solvents have
been used (PBS + media EMEM, Tables: 5,6 and Milli-Q + media EMEM, Tables:7). The
trials that contained PBS displayed low UVT values (24%). The PBS seems to be a UV
blocker which means that one of the components of this solution affect the transmittance of
the UV. On the other hand, trials containing MilliQ water displayed UVT values of 100%. In
real life environments it is really difficult to find water having so low and high UVT values.
The UV transmittance of water in aquaculture applications generally varies from 50% to
95% [58]. The media EMEM that was used to maintain the viral stock of IPNV had 0% of
UVT. That is why, all of the trials where diluted at least 1:100 with other solvents, so the
media will not affect the UVT values.
A recent study [73], used a medium pressure lamp to inactivate Infectious Salmon Anemia
virus (ISAV). The results, indicated that, with a UV dose of 22.5 [mJ/cm2], a 2.59 log inac-
tivation was achieved. Unfortunately, they did not manage to determine a UV dose for 3 log
inactivation. The author does not specify the UVT value of the serum used to expose the
virus to the UV lamp. The results seem to be similar with this experiment, although ISAV
is much more susceptible to UV than IPNV. For example, Helge L. & Cristian V. [74], found
a 3 log inactivation using 7.5 [mJ/cm2] for ISAV, while for IPNV the same log reduction was
achieved by using a UV dose of 246 [mJ/cm2], using a low pressure lamp (LP).
While the effectiveness of UV in eliminating bacteria is widely acknowledged, there seems
to be some uncertainty regarding the specific UV doses required for inactivating viruses
using a MP lamp. Most of the experiments include low pressure lamps. Research should
be conducted in the future addressing various issues such as tests of more virus using a
medium pressure lamp. More realistic approaches are also needed (experiments in-vivo or
in aquaculture facilities) to have a clearer overview of the UV disinfection tool. Apart from
Page 32 of 33
==PAGE:36==
these future research UV light as disinfection tool is a really efficient and sustainable way to
combat pathogens in the aquaculture industry.
5
Conclusion
The study aimed to evaluate the efficiency of UV water treatment in fish virus IPN.
Throughout the experiment, key factors where taken into account such us Ultraviolet Trans-
mittance, the temperature of the system, the various solvent types and also different power
inputs, among others.
There were no temperature effect on the efficacy of the UV disinfection in the system,
and the experimental conditions remained within a safe range. Regarding the solvents type
that were tested, it is revealed that PBS is a UV blocker while on the other hand Milli-Q
water is a highly transparent solvent. The dilution factors did not significantly affect the log
reduction, but the variations highlighted the need of more experiments for higher certainty.
Furthermore, higher power inputs showed higher disinfection capability in comparison with
lower power inputs of the same UV Dose. These findings provide valuable insights, high-
lighting the necessity for additional research to be conducted in the future.
Unfortunately the time consuming nature of this experiment and the limited time-frame
of the masters thesis, restricted the opportunity of exploring additional UV doses or testing
a higher variety of pathogens, including bacteria. Future research could also include exper-
imental trials using different samples, possibly water originating from aquaculture systems.
By doing this, the interaction of the various parameters that affect UV light and the dis-
infection capabilities can be examined. Another future experiment could include living fish
(in-vivo research) and assess the the UV disinfection in a system that resembles an aquacul-
ture facility. Finally, the operational cost of the medium pressure lamps in comparison with
the low pressure lamps with regard to the efficacy of the two types could also be examined.
