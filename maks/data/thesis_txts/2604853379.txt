Abstract
This thesis aimed to investigate the potential of internal solar shading in which venti-
lation is added in the cavity between the screen and the window. Additionally it also
aimed to investigate if calculations could make realistic representations of experimen-
tal data obtained for the mentioned system. The problem statement was investigated
through quantitative methods where results were obtained respectively through experi-
ments conducted in a construction designed for the purpose and through results from a
formulated mathematical model.
The purpose for the experiments and calculations was to respectively measure and cal-
culate the temperature difference between the inlet and exhaust air of the system. The
temperature difference calculated and measured would then give an indication of the
heat removed from the system.
The effect of ventilation added to the system was tested for three different screens (Screen
1, Screen 2 and Screen 3) combined with three different air ﬂows (0.10 m/s, 0.20 m/s and
0.25 m/s), respectively. The screens all consisted of a metal coated fabric, but their prop-
erties varied regarding transmittance, reﬂection, openness, emissivity and absorbance.
The calculations and the experiments showed that the performance of all the screens
tested were improved signiﬁcantly by adding ventilation to the cavity. The most promis-
ing results were obtained for Screen 1, that had the lowest openness and the highest re-
ﬂective factor. Average values for measurements conducted for Screen 1 combined with
a velocity of 0.25 m/s in the cavity, showed that the heat entering the room was reduced
by 36% when ventilation was added, compared to a set up for Screen 1 without ventila-
tion. Furthermore, a correlation between experimental and calculated data is proven to a
certain extent. However, it is acknowledged that assumptions and high margins of error
affected the results and the obtained results should only be seen as an indication of the
system’s performance.
Based on the results and the discussion it was possible to conclude the necessity of more
measurements as well as improved conditions for the experimental setup. Finally, sug-
gestions for improvements are made, as this research showed promising potential, justi-
fying further investigation.

Resumé
Formålet med denne specialeafhandling er at undersøge potentialet for indvendig solaf-
skærmning, når denne er brugt i kombination med ventilation mellem afskærmningen
og ruden. Derudover vil det undersøges, om realistiske efterligninger af forsøgsdata, målt
for det nævnte system, kan eftervises matematisk. Problemformuleringen blev undersøgt
via kvantitative metoder bestående af forsøgsresultater opnået ved forsøg i en specielt de-
signet konstruktion til formålet samt resultater udregnet via en matematisk model.
Formålet med forsøg samt beregninger er at, henholdsvis, måle og beregne temperatur-
forskellen mellem indløbs- og udløbstemperaturen i mellemrummet mellem ruden og
solafskærmningen. Den målte og beregnede temperaturforskel vil give en indikation af,
hvor meget varme der er fjernet fra systemet.
Hvilken forbedring der kan forventes når ventilation er tilføjet til systemet blev testet
for tre forskellige solafskærminger (Screen 1, Screen 2 and Screen 3) med tre forskel-
lige hastigheder af luftstrømmen i mellemrummet (0.10 m/s, 0.20 m/s and 0.25 m/s).
Ens for de tre solafskærmninger er, at de alle har en metalisk belægning, men derudover
varierer de med hensyn til transmittans, reﬂektion, åbningsgrad, emissivitet og absorp-
tion. Beregninger og forsøgsresultaterne viste begge at de testede afskærmninger alle
performede markant bedre når ventilation var tilføjet til systemet. Den solafskærmning
der viste de bedste resultater var Screen 1, som var den solafskærmning med den mest re-
ﬂektive belægning samt den mindste åbningsgrad. De opnåede gennemsnitsværdier for
denne solafskærmning viste, at den i kombination med en hastighed for luftstrømmen
på 0.25 m/s kunne reducere varmen, der ville trænge igennem systemet uden ventila-
tion, med 36%. Derudover viste resultaterne generelt en sammenhæng mellem de målte
og beregnede data. Dog skal der tages højde for de antagelser og måleusikkerheder der
ligger til grund for resultaterne. De opnåede resultater for de tre solafskærmninger og de
tre forskellige hastigheder skal derfor blot ses som en indikation og ikke et endegyldigt
resultat.
På baggrund af de opnåede resultater og den efterfølgende diskussion, var det muligt
at konkludere nødvendigheden af yderligere måledata, samt forbedrede betingelser for
forsøg. Afslutningsvist gives der forslag til forbedringer af forsøgsopstillingen, da der er
et potentiale for dette system og det derfor virker lovende at undersøge nærmere.

1. Introduction
When it comes to designing buildings, it is a challenging task to ﬁnd the right balance
between energy consumption, daylight, indoor climate and the well-being of the occu-
pants. It often ends up with compromises on either the architectural design or the ther-
mal comfort of the occupants if the energy consumption is to be kept low. One of the
architectural trends that challenge both the energy consumption, as well as the thermal
comfort of the occupants, are glass facades.
Glass is often seen as a dominant material in many modern facade designs. Glass of-
fers transparency, more daylight and a view for the occupants. Moreover, it makes the
building and surroundings look far more spacious. Glass facades offer a great amount of
advantages, but only as long as the facade design is an integrated part of the building de-
sign concerning energy consumption and indoor climate. Whilst a glass facade provides
the interior with natural light, it also tends to provide a serious amount of solar radiation,
depending on the orientation. To avoid overheating, solar controlled glazing and/or so-
lar shading is often added to the facade design. The most efﬁcient type of solar shading
is the use of external solar shadings. But external solar shading is not always an option,
e.g. due to maintenance, weather conditions, economy or just the lack of architectural
freedom for the facade design. In cases where external solar shading is not an option,
internal solar shading can be a good alternative. Except for being less efﬁcient, internal
solar shading provides many advantages such as; easier maintenance, longer life span,
less use of material, easier adjustment to interior design and more dynamic control com-
pared to many external shadings. Furthermore, internal solar shading is easy to integrate
in existing buildings. Therefore, it is relevant to investigate the potential for internal so-
lar shading, as it has the ability to reduce material use as well as the maintenance whilst
making the shading more dynamic and thereby less dominant in the facade design.
This project seeks to investigate the potential of a system where ventilation is added lo-
cally at the top of the gap between the internal solar shading and the window. This sys-
tem is known as an "Active Cavity Transition Facade" (ACT Facade) and the principle
is shown in Figure 1.1. The potential is investigated through experiments and a math-
ematical model and it is furthermore investigated if there is a correlation between the
measured and calculated results.

1.1
Case: The Expansion of Copenhagen Airport
The idea for this project arose from the upcoming expansion of Copenhagen Airport
where a facade of approximately 270 meters in width will consist of glass curtain walls.
Figure 1.2 shows a sketch of the expansion with the facade facing south.
Figure 1.2: One of the preliminary sketches of the extension of Copenhagen Airport.
As the facade is southwards facing, solar shading is an important parameter in the de-
sign. Due to requirements, such as easy access to maintenance and cleaning without
affecting the ﬂight trafﬁc taking place just on the outside of the facade, internal solar
shading would be the optimal solution. It is therefore suggested to use the ACT system
with ventilation between the internal solar shading and the glass facade. The ventila-
tion will remove a substantial amount of the heat generated between the facade and the

screen. Theoretically it should reduce the solar heat gain transmitted to the interior, but
as of now there is very little knowledge of the actual performance. Furthermore, there are
no calculation or simulation tools that support this kind of solution. As simulations and
calculations assist the decision making process, it is important that the calculations and
simulation software can provide realistic modellings of continuously evolving solutions
for solar shading. The case of the expansion of Copenhagen Airport created the frames
for this project and the tested window and screens are therefore chosen to represent re-

1.2
Problem Statement
Can the performance of internal solar shading be signiﬁcantly improved, if the accumu-
lated heat between the screen and window is removed through ventilation? Moreover,
can the performance of the system be modelled using theoretical equations, current
standards and existing software?
1.3
Hypothesis
The hypothesis is that if the ventilation can remove most of the heat accumulated be-
tween the internal solar shading and the window, it will reduce the amount of heat en-
tering the room and thereby improve the performance of the internal solar shading. It is
further suggested that the temperature difference (∆T ) between the inlet (room air) and
the exhaust air will give an indication of the heat removed by the system. It is expected
that ∆T can be calculated and that it will give an indication of ∆T measured in a real-life
situation. To test the hypothesis the following will be investigated:
First a mathematical model will be formulated in order to give an understanding of the
physical parameters affecting the system. This model will also work as a formula where
customised changes can be made to best ﬁt the theoretical relationships concerning the
system. The mathematical model will calculate ∆T and aim to simulate a precise forecast
of what to expect from the experimental data.
The next step is to conduct experiments in a construction designed for the purpose.
These experiments will measure the inlet temperature, corresponding to the room tem-
perature in a real-life situation, as well as the exhaust temperature. The experiments will
be conducted for a system with three different solar shadings combined with three differ-
ent air ﬂows, to further investigate the effect of different screen properties and velocities
in the cavity.
Finally the results from the mathematical model and the experiments will be interpreted
to see, if there is a correlation between the calculated and measured data.

2. Methodology
2.1
Introduction to Method
The hypothesis will be validated in a process consisting of two parts, as shown in Figure
2.1. This will be achieved by using quantitative methods for both parts. The purpose for
each part is to, respectively, calculate and measure the temperature difference between
the inlet temperature and the exhaust air temperature for the system, as this would give
an indication of the heat removed.
Figure 2.1: Diagram showing the two main parts of the method.
The ﬁrst part consists of a mathematical model, built to reﬂect theory and current stan-
dards within heat transfer and facades using internal solar shading. The goal is to simu-
late a real life situation, that should correspond to the data measured in the experimental
setup. When results are obtained from both the mathematical model and the experi-
ments, these will be analysed respectively and compared to each other to show if the
calculated results are reliable based on the measured data. All calculations and measure-
ments will be made for the system shown with the diagrammatic sketch in Figure 2.2.
The setup consists of a simple plywood construction allowing measurements of the inlet
temperature at the bottom of the system and the exhaust air temperature at the top. The
calculations and measurements are made speciﬁcally for the gap between the screen and
the window. This thesis will investigate the effect of the ventilation in the system with a
window combined with three different screens.

2.2
Mathematical Model
A mathematical model was formulated based on theory and current standards relevant;
DS/EN 410 [13], EN 14500 [12] and ISO 15099 [8]. It was designed with inspiration from
existing calculation software, such as WINDOW and IDA ICE (see section 2.5). The math-
ematical model was designed from scratch as this would allow customised changes and
features, compared to existing software. Furthermore, the aim was to provide a basic
understanding of the physical relationships in the experimental setup. The ideal out-
come of the mathematical model was to be able to simulate real-life situations for the sys-
tem. However, due to the simplicity compared to actual real-life situations, the aim was
instead to investigate relationships between different variables; these being the screen
properties and differing ventilation air ﬂow in the cavity.
In order to test the relationship between the different variables, the model had to calcu-
late how much heat it is possible to disperse for multiple combinations of screen proper-
ties and ventilation air ﬂows. The heat that is removed from the system due to ventilation,
is calculated via equation 2.1 [3], where ∆T = (Texhaust −Tin), which will be referred to as
T5 and Te respectively.
φremoved = qv ·cp ·ρ ·(T5 −Te)
(2.1)
T5 is the temperature of the air after it has passed through the cavity, making the tem-
peratures of the screen and the inner most pane (Pane 3) signiﬁcant for the temperature
of T5. Knowing the air ﬂow and the temperatures of the screen and Pane 3, it would be
possible to calculate T5. But as it is a system consisting of multiple surfaces, the screen
and Pane 3 will likewise be affected by the temperatures of their surrounding surfaces.
This results in a setup with ﬁve unknown temperatures, which are shown in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Unknown temperatures in the system; T1, T2, T3, T4 and T5.

The energy ﬂows interfering with the surfaces in this system are the heat gain from solar
radiation, radiant heat losses and the convective heat losses due to the ventilation. To
calculate T5, a heat balance equation must be made for each surface, accounting for the
heat gains and heat losses due to both radiation and convection. The result will be the
mathematical model, consisting of a system of equations with ﬁve equations and ﬁve un-
knowns and it will be possible to calculate T5 and thereby determine the heat removed
from the system.
The heat balance equations for the four surfaces (Pane 1, Pane 2, Pane 3 and the screen)
all consist of energy transfers by radiation and convection. The heat balance equation for
the exhaust air is only affected by convection as radiation does not affect the air tempera-
ture directly. Before going into detail with the different heat balance equations, the main
theory behind radiant and convective heat transfer, used in this project, is introduced.

Absolute Temperature
The absolute temperature is measured using thermocouples. Thermocouples consist of
two different metal wires, in this case copper and constantan. The one end is connected
to the logger and the other end is placed at the location of which the temperature are
measured. The difference in temperature in the two points produces a proportional elec-
tric current, which the logger converts to a temperature difference between its reference
temperature and the temperature at the measuring point. The accuracy of these mea-
surements is around ± 0.5 °C [4]. Furthermore, a delay of a few seconds can occur when-
ever there is a change in the temperature [3].

Data Processing
As the ﬁrst step in the processing of data, the data was retrieved from the logger and
prepared for further adjustments.
1. Measurements were made for every one second and stored in the data logger.
2. The data obtained from the measurements were transferred to Excel and an average

Data Plots

2.5
Programs
2.5.1
WINDOW
WINDOW is an open-source program designed by Berkeley Lab. WINDOW provides cal-
culations of total thermal window performance indices, including U-values, Solar Heat
Gain Coefﬁcients as well as shading coefﬁcients and the visible transmittance. The heat
transfer analysis are based on the updated rating procedure developed by NFRC, National
Fenestration Rating Council, which is consistent with ISO 15099. Recently there has been
an update making it possible to model complex shading systems, which was used in this
report to verify calculations on the system. [9].
2.5.2
IDA ICE
IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE) is a whole year and dynamic multi-zone sim-
ulation tool. It is designed to make accurate model of buildings and their systems to ﬁnd
the best comfort at the lowest energy consumption. IDA ICE reﬂects the latest research
and the results correspond well to measured data. IDA furthermore provides a detailed
pane-by-pane window and shading model for which representation of ventilated facades
can be made. This model is based on the current ISO 15099 standard. [7].

Elaboration of Performance
To elaborate on the statement that the performance of the system is improved signiﬁ-
cantly when adding ventilation, it is worth to consider the amount of heat that would
anyway be removed from the cavity through the window to the exterior. It was assumed
that this would not be a substantial amount, as the window have a relatively low U-value
of 0.61 W /m2K . If there is a temperature difference on each side of the window of 5°C,
this would result in a heat transfer of about 3 W through the window, seen from equation
4.1 and 4.2 [3].
φ =U · As ·∆T
(4.1)
φ = 0.61W /m2K ·1m2 ·5K = 3.05W

Compared to the heat removed by the ventilation, which on average is around 50 W, this
is insigniﬁcant.
Another aspect concerning the performance, is the derived positive effects the addition
of ventilation in the cavity might have. If the heat accumulated in the cavity is removed
it will result in a decrease in temperature of the screen. If the screen temperature is low-
ered, it will reduce the temperature asymmetry of the surfaces in the room and thereby
reduce the discomfort experienced by the occupants.

Interpretation of Method
4.2.1
Mathematical Model
When formulating a mathematical model that is affected by many different parameters
that are difﬁcult to determine, assumptions are inevitable. The assumptions are made
to simplify the calculations and make the mathematical model more transparent and
easier to interpret. The sections below discuss some of main assumptions made for the
mathematical model.
Heat Balance for the Window Panes
One assumption for the heat balances concerning the window panes is, that the temper-
ature of each pane is the same throughout the pane, meaning that both surfaces of one
pane have the same temperature. In reality this is incorrect, but due to the high thermal
conductivity of the glass it is a fair assumption to simplify the calculations. This assump-
tion reduces the number of unknowns to three instead of six, which is the amount of
surfaces for the three panes. This simpliﬁcation will lead to a higher margin of error,
although this is assumed to be insigniﬁcant.

4.2. INTERPRETATION OF METHOD
The Convective Heat Transfer Coefﬁcient hk34
The parameter that was most likely to cause uncertainty in the calculations was the con-
vective heat transfer coefﬁcient. As shown in the sensitivity analysis, (2.9), the system is
most sensitive to changes in the convective heat transfer coefﬁcient, which happens to
also be the coefﬁcient which was most difﬁcult to determine and no existing determina-
tion at similar conditions for hk34 ﬁts the actual conditions that apply for this setup. As
mentioned in the method, the determination of hk34 that came closest to the average was
chosen. This was done to get the most neutral results, as hk34 could vary from around 0.4
W /m2K to over 12 W /m2K depending on the equation chosen to determine the coefﬁ-
cient [10]. It is therefor important to keep in mind, that the calculated results would vary
greatly for different assumptions for hk34.
Fully Developed Flow
Another assumption that simpliﬁed the calculations was the assumption that the ﬂow in
the cavity was fully developed. Taken the entry conditions into account would make the
calculations more complex and further research would have been necessary. But as the
ﬂow is ﬁrst fully developed sufﬁciently far from the entrance of the cavity, a fully devel-
oped ﬂow will not be accomplished over the height of the cavity as it is only 1.5 m high
[10].
Other assumptions
Other, less signiﬁcant assumptions, are the ones that did not result in substantial differ-
ences in the results. These include the assumption that did not affect the heat balance
equation for T5 directly. An example is the assumption of Tsky = Te which affects the ra-
diant heat loss to the exterior from the outer most pane in the window, but not T5 directly.
Again this adds to the margins of error and will to some extend affect the results.

5. Further Research
There is a great potential in further research on this topic. The calculations and experi-
ments indicate that internal solar shading is signiﬁcantly improved when adding ventila-
tion to the cavity. But in lack of better conditions for the experimental setup, it would be
crucial to conduct more examinations of the system in controlled environments. More
accurate results would result in more accurate reference values, which is needed to im-
prove the correlation between the measurements and the calculated/modelled data.
Experiments
If this subject were to be researched further the following changes and improvements are
recommended:
• More controlled settings
• More thorough measurements of the solar radiation
• Measurements for an entire day without changing the velocity.
• At least three reliable measurement periods for each screen tested.
More controlled settings would eliminate many of the margins of error and make the
results more reliable. As a start it would be recommended to use an artiﬁcial sun to bet-
ter control the solar radiation on the system. If this is not an option, a more thorough
measurement of the solar radiation on and through the window is needed if experiments
are still conducted outside. Furthermore, the experiments should be supplemented with
continuously measurements throughout an entire day for one velocity, to further inves-
tigate if heat is accumulated in the construction. Finally, it is recommended to obtain at
least three sets of data for each screen tested as this would improve the reliability of the
results.
IDA ICE
Another subject that could be researched further is the use of the double facade sim-
ulation tool in IDA ICE. If more measurements are conducted and these show reliable
results, it could be tested if IDA ICE can imitate the measured data. This would be valu-
able for projects, like the expansion of Copenhagen Airport, as it gives the opportunity to
validate the performance in the early stages with a proven and legitimate software.
80
==PAGE:91==
6. Conclusion
By analysing the conducted experiments and calculations this thesis has proven that the
performance of internal solar shading can be signiﬁcantly improved by adding ventila-
tion to the system. Three different screens were tested with three different air ﬂows, re-
spectively. Average values for measurements conducted for Screen 1, with Fscreen = 53%,
combined with a velocity of 0.25 m/s in the cavity, showed that the heat entering the
room was reduced by up to 36% when ventilation was added. Additionally, a correlation
between experimental- and calculated data is proven to a certain extent. However, the
margins of error have to be taken into account as they have affected the end result.
For the mathematical model the uncertainties were mainly concerning the assumptions
of the convective heat transfer coefﬁcient for the surfaces in the cavity. The calculated
results were most sensitive to this coefﬁcient, that additionally was the most difﬁcult to
determine. Concerning the experimental data the high margins of error were a result of
the uncertainties for all the different equipment used, together with unexpected devi-
ations caused by the surrounding conditions. To obtain more reliable results from the
experiments, and thereby support the formulation of the mathematical model, the mar-
gins of error have to be reduced by either conducting the measurements in more con-
trolled settings or by conducting even more experiments. Furthermore, it is important
to determine the causes of the deviations in the results obtained from the experiments
by investigating the experimental setup further. The results obtained through the exper-
iments and calculations therefore only provide an indication of the actual performance
due to a considerable amount of assumptions and multiple margins of error.
Despite the margins of error for the experiments and the many assumptions for the math-
ematical model, the obtained data proves that there is a potential for the investigated
system. The results obtained from this investigation will hopefully be the beginning of
further research concerning the use of internal solar shading, as advantages for this type
of shading are potentially many many, especially when designing modern buildings.

function [LAT,delta,omega,gamma_s,psi_s,alpha_s,tr,ts,omega_s]=sun(day,lst,y,lat,
lon,lsm);
 
%SUN calculates the position of sun on the sky at times corresponding to vectors
%   
%[LAT,delta,omega,gamma_s,psi_s,alpha_s,tr,ts,omega_s]=sun(day,lst,y,lat,lon,lsm)
%   
%LAT: time in LAT (solar time) [h]
%delta: Vector containing declination [deg]
%omega: Vector containing hour angle [deg]
%gamma_s: Vector containing solar elevation [deg]
%psi_s: vector containing solar zenith [deg]
%alpha_s: Vector containing solar azimuth [deg]
%          east:alpha_s = -90,   west:alpha_s = 90
%          south:alpha_s = 0,    north:alpha_s = 180
%tr: Vector containing sunrise in local standard time [h]
%ts: Vector containing sunset in local standard time [h]
%    
%day: vector containing day numbers
%lst: vector containing local standard time [h]
%y: year
%lat: lattitude [deg] (optional: default Copemhagen 55.5 deg)
%lon: longitude [deg] (optional: default Copenhagen 12.5 deg)
%lsm: local standard time meridian (optional: default Copenhagen 15 deg)
%   
%Numbers refer to formulaes in The European Radiation Atlas
%
%References: 
%   The European Solar Radiation Atlas
%   École des Mines de Paris, 2000
%   
%   Duffie & Bechmann
%
%Written by: Toke Rammer Nielsen
%Creation date: 6 dec. 2000
%Last modified: 18 sep. 2001
 
if nargin==3
   lat=55.5;
   lon=12.5;
   lsm=15;
elseif nargin==2
   lat=55.5;
   lon=12.5;
   lsm=15;
   y=inf;
elseif nargin<2
   error('Wrong input')
end
 
%deg to rad
dg_rd=2*pi/360;
lat=dg_rd*lat; %[rad]
lon=dg_rd*lon; %[rad]

lsm=dg_rd*lsm; %[rad]
 
%Declination
day_1=2*pi*day/365.25; %(3.3.4)
if y==inf
   delta=23.45*sin(dg_rd*360*(284+day)/365)*dg_rd; %Simple [rad] (Duffie & Bechmann)
else
    %More precise calculation of delta with y as the year
    n0=78.8946+0.2422*(y-1957)-floor((y-1957)/4); %(3.3.2e)
    omega0=2*pi/365.2422; %(3.3.2d)
    t1=-0.5-lon/(2*pi)-n0; %(3.3.2c)
    omegat=omega0*(day+t1); %(3.3.2b)
    delta=0.0064979+0.405906*sin(omegat)+0.0020054*sin(2*omegat)-0.002988*sin
(3*omegat)...
    -0.0132296*cos(omegat)+0.0063809*cos(2*omegat)+0.0003508*cos(3*omegat); %[rad] 
(3.3.2a)
end
 
%Time in LAT
ET=-0.128*sin(day_1-0.04887)-0.165*sin(2*day_1+0.34383); %[h] (3.3.22)
c=0; %Correction for summertime not used
LAT=lst+ET+12*((lon-lsm)/pi)-c; %[h] (3.3.23)
 
%Hour angle
omega=(LAT-12)*pi/12; %[rad] (3.3.1)
 
%Solar elevation
gamma_s=asin(sin(lat)*sin(delta)+cos(lat)*cos(delta).*cos(omega)); %[rad] (3.3.5)
 
%Solar zenith
psi_s=pi/2-gamma_s; %[rad] (3.3.6)
 
%Solar azimuth
sine_alpha_s=cos(delta).*sin(omega)./cos(gamma_s); %(3.3.9)
cosine_alpha_star=(sin(lat)*sin(gamma_s)-sin(delta))./(cos(lat)*cos(gamma_s)); %
(3.3.8)
logic=(sine_alpha_s<0);
alpha_s=logic.*(-acos(cosine_alpha_star))+(logic==0).*acos(cosine_alpha_star); %
[rad] (3.3.7)
 
%Sunrise and sunset (with refraction)
check=(sin(-0.0145444)-sin(lat)*sin(delta))./(cos(lat)*cos(delta));
omega_s=acos(check); %(3.3.10)
logic1=check>=1;
logic2=check<=-1;
omega_s=(logic1==0 & logic2==0).*omega_s+logic1*0+logic2*pi; %[rad]
 
tr=12-12/pi*omega_s-ET-12*((lon-lsm)/pi)+c ; %(3.3.13a) in local standard time
ts=12+12/pi*omega_s-ET-12*((lon-lsm)/pi)+c ; %(3.3.13b) in local standard time
 
%rad to dg
rd_dg=360/(2*pi);
delta=rd_dg*delta;
omega=rd_dg*omega;
