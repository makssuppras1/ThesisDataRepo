Abstract
The pursuit of netôÙzero and carbonôÙneutral energy systems in response to escalating
emissions and climate change impacts has prompted nations, including the European
Union (EU), to commit to ambitious goals of achieving carbon neutrality by 2050. HowôÙ
ever, the transport sectorãs increasing emissions, majority of which comes from road
transport, poses a challenge. Electrification of vehicle fleets is seen as a potential soluôÙ
tion to substantially reduce GreenHouse Gas emissions. But electrification of transport
sector when combined with electrification of other sectors like heat will require substanôÙ
tial changes in future energy systems. As electricity sector transforms it supply from
fossil fuel based sources of energy like coal and natural gas to clean and variable reôÙ
newable sources of energy (VREs) like wind and solar energy, it would require extra
flexibility options in terms of storage and extra interconnections. Being able to adjust
the demand patterns of future electric vehicles (EVs) can benefit the overall energy
system by providing the aforementioned flexibility requirement. This thesis studies the
impact of flexibility mechanism that comes with EVs in terms of flexible demand that
can adjust when to charge and if required discharge the batteries of EVs on future secôÙ
tor coupled European energy system going towards 2050. In order to do so it utilizes
existing complex and sector coupled energy system models to study different future
scenarios, primarily a scenario with nonôÙflexible demand from EVs and another where
EVs have flexible demand. The results show a clear benefit of utilizing these flexibility
coming from flexible demand patterns of electric vehicle by reducing the total system
cost of the energy system. Results also indicate the optimal charging pattern would
require more local wind energy resources, can significantly reduce need for short term
storage and reduce curtailments of VREs when compared to a scenario where electric
vehicle poses a inflexible demand on the energy system. But this also requires altering
planning decisions for other flexibility providing options like requirement of more electric
interconnections, less hydrogen interconnections and more long term storage.

Introduction and Background
1.1
Introduction
Rising emission and related climate change impact has led to many countries to purôÙ
sue and plan for net zero or carbon neutral energy systems by 2050. As a signatory
of the Paris Agreement, the European Union (EU) has committed to ambitious targets,
including a 55% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 and achieving carbon
neutrality by 2050 [1]. Eurostat, the EUãs statistical office has recently shown that the
GreenHouse Gas (GHG) emission levels in the EU between 1990 and 2020 fell across
different sectors but the Transport sector has shown an opposite trend with increase of
7% where road transport accounted for three quarters of transport related GHG emisôÙ
sions. Electrification of transport can bring substantial GHG reduction [maybe cite some
source] reducing dependence on fossil fuels and facilitating the transition to a decarôÙ
bonized energy mix. But this reduction is dependent on how clean the electrical energy
used for transport sector are. Moreover widespread electrification of transport presents
significant implications for the European energy system. The increased demand for
electricity will necessitate the development of a robust and resilient infrastructure capaôÙ
ble of handling charging needs at scale. As Electricity sector transform its supply from
fossil fuel based energy sources like coal and natural gas to Variable Renewable Energy
(VRE) sources like Solar and Wind energy, more flexibility options are required. This
flexibility can come from the supply side via hydro power, gas peaking units and more
transmission capacity (which can allow supply of power coming from other regions) or
from building extra storage capacity (which can utilize the VRE supply when it is availôÙ
able and to use it later to meet the inflexible demand) and demand response with sector
coupling [2],[3],[4],[5],[6].
In order to reduce emissions from road transport sector, many countries have already
taken pledges and commitments for removing Internal Combustion Engine (ICE) vehiôÙ
cles in future [7],[8] . For road transport, converting from fossil fuel based ICE vehicles
to Electric vehicles (EVs) is seen as key enabler to reach ambitious climate targets as
they tend to have higher round trip efficiency when compared to other alternatives like
Hydrogen fuel cell vehicles (HFCVs) [9]. On the other hand for aviation and shipping,
where long distances needs to be travelled while carrying heavy payload, switching to
other alternatives like bioôÙfuels, ammonia or synthetic fuels seems to be a better option
[10].

1.2
Background and Literature review
One of the key challenges in managing the integration of EVs into the energy system
is ensuring their charging patterns align with Electric grid conditions. This becomes
even more important as other sectors such as heating is also electrified. If charging of
the EVs are not managed, it can take place simultaneously with heating demand peak.
This would require very high peak capacity from the electricity supply side. On the other
hand, if charging of EVs can be managed via smart charging/flexible charging schemes,
it can support electrification of other sector by pushing the charging to a later time hence
reducing the requirement of peak demand. Not only that, newer smart charging techôÙ
nologies like Vehicle to Grid (V2G) charging where an EV can also discharge the stored
energy in the batteries back to electric grid and support the grid when required. Hence
they can replace to some extent the need of short term battery storage which enables
integration of VRE by filling the gap between fixed demand and intermittent fluctuations
in VRE supply. This capability not only supports grid stability but also provides potential
revenue streams for EV owners through grid services.
Multiple studies in literature tries to analyze the benefits and impacts of integration of
EVs in the power and energy systems. [13] studied the effect of electric vehicles on
North European power systems and showed that EVs when charged intelligently can
help facilitate more wind power investments. [14] and subsequently using the same
model [5] showed that integration of EVs with V2G capabilities can not only bring subôÙ

integration of EVs, flexible charging strategies are crucial, enabling optimal resource
utilization, grid stability, and the integration of renewable energy sources.

Methodology
This section describes the methodology that was used for the study. It starts with brief
description of the energy system model that was used along with relevant details on
optimization runs and sectors that are included. Further, in this section modelling of
flexible EVs in the energy system is described. This gives the generalized description
of the modelling procedure. The specific scope, scenarios and input data that were used
are described in the section 3.
2.1
Energy system Model : Balmorel Energy system Model
In order to analyze future energy transition pathways, energy system models simulates
potential future energy transitions accounting for policies and interaction & synergies
between sectors [6], [14], [2], [3]. For this study, Balmorel Energy system model was
used.
Balmorel is an openôÙsource bottoms up energy system modelling tool [16]. It is an deterôÙ
ministic energy system model that minimizes total system cost from a socioôÙeconomic
perspective. It was first developed in [17] and the theoretical background described in
[18]. This model was selected as it already consisted of all the necessary input database
required for the study and has been used many times to study energy systems and its
planning for NorthôÙCentral Europe [19],[20],[21],[22]. The objective of the model is to
minimize the total discounted system cost in order to satisfy an exogenously defined
demand. The costs constitute of investment in capacity of generation, storage and
transmission technology, fuel consumption, Operation and Maintenance and Taxes on
emissions. Overall the model solves for the capacity of each technology and its operaôÙ
tion over a year.
The temporal and spatial resolution is flexible and divided into 3 layers each. The defiôÙ
nition of these layers can be flexible and doesnãt need to be very strict in terms of their
geographical or temporal meaning. In terms of spatial resolution, the three layers are
divided as countries, regions and areas. Regions in this study are modelled as electricôÙ
ity bidding zones and do have a geographical meaning. For example, flow of electricity
(electrical transmission lines) and hydrogen (hydrogen pipelines) between regions are
restricted by their capacities whereas heat cannot be exchanged between regions. ArôÙ
eas are defined based on specific sector it is representing. For example in electricity
sector each region is divided into different resource grades in order avoid uniform wind
and solar resources in the whole region. Whereas in the heat sector, areas are defined
based on temperature level for demand from industry, district heating network and indiôÙ
vidual users. More details can be found in appendix B and Fig 2 (c) of [6]. The layers
defining temporal resolution are year, seasons and terms. The lowest time resolution
terms defines the resolution at which the energy balance constraints will be defined in

2.1.1
Details of Optimization runs
The model is run for a set of years (here it is 2025, 2035 and 2045) in a rolling horizon
fashion with limited foresight about future. Hence while taking decision for any given
year, the capacity decisions from previous year are taken as exogenous. Having limited
foresight means that while taking decisions for a given year, parameters for the next year
is also taken into account [6]. For each scenario three consecutive optimization runs
are performed. First a Capacity development run is performed. This is usually done
with lower time resolution as this is the most time consuming computation. Then taking
as exogenous the capacities of technologies as decided from first run, the second run
optimises long term operational decisions such as planned maintenance and schedule
of longôÙterm storage. Planned maintenance results are then given to a stochastic outage
run which performs a monteyôÙcarlo simulations which decides to create artificial outage
of available capacity of generation technologies which is also expected to happen in
real life situations as small stochastic disturbances cannot be known precisely in long
term. Finally the 3rd run takes the capacity, decisions related to long term storage ,
expected (planned maintenance) and unexpected unavailability of some technologies as
exogenous parameters and simulates an hourly resolution Day Ahead market operation
for each day of the year one after another. Details on consecutive optimization runs
that takes place after capacity expansion optimization can be found in [23]. Temporal
resolution for each run is described below.
ãÂ Capacity development optimization run : 3h resolution, 8 weeks, ThursdayôÙSaturday
of each week
ãÂ Full year optimization run : 4h resolution, 365 days
ãÂ Day Ahead Market optimization run : 1h resolution, 365 days
2.1.2
Sectors
This study considers a coupled electricity, heat, transport and synthetic gas sector. ElecôÙ
tricity sector have a energy balance equation defined for each region. This means that
the total electrical energy demand for each regions needs to be balanced with generaôÙ
tion technologies, discharge of storage or via interconnections with other neighbouring
regions. Same goes for heat sector but these balance equations are defined for each
area instead. The synthetic gas sector consists of energy balance for Synthethic natôÙ
ural gas and hydrogen (H2). SNG energy balance is defined as international market
whereas H2 balance is defined for each region. Similar to electricity, H2 can be transôÙ
ferred between each region via H2 pipelines [21]. The Transport sector is assumed to
be fully decarbonize by 2050 in the model. The land based transport are assumed to
be fully electrified. This consists of flexible EVs (passenger cars, vans and trucks) and

2.2
Modelling of Flexible EVs
Modellling of flexible EVs in the current version of Balmorel was first introduced in [24]
in the form of EV addôÙon. This add on allows interaction of EVs with the electric grid
in the form of an aggregated virtual battery for each region. This interaction can be
activated in three different modes namely Dumb Charging or PC (Passive Charging),
Smart Charging with uniôÙdirectional charging capabilities or G2V (Grid to Vehicle) and
Smart Charging with biôÙdirectional charging capabilities or V2G (Vehicle to Grid). When
in Dumb Charging mode the vehicles are charged passively from grid as soon as they
are connected to the grid. In G2V mode, vehicles can be charged based on when it is
cheaper to charge (for example charging at night when having low residual load). The
V2G works similar to G2V mode but here the EVs are allowed to also discharge (give
energy back to the grid). In terms of the mathematics, this is modelled using flexible
SOC constraints which is further explained in section 2.2.1. As all the Evs are not always
connected to charger, the size (MWh) of the battery is time dependent based on how
many EVs are connected to the grid/charger at any given time. These time series are
generated based on travel behaviour of private vehicle owners in Denmark using Danish
National Travel Survey [25].
2.2.1
Mathematical Model
In this subsection, the mathematical model that allows EVs to act as flexible batteries for
the energy system is described. As the energy system model is defined as optimization
problem, first the extra variable and parameters that are required are defined followed
by the constraints. Overall all the EVs are defined here as an aggregated virtual battery
for each region. As with any battery, the aggregated battery can be characterized by 2
quantities, first the total battery capacity and second the total state of charge (SOC) of
the aggregated battery.
Whenever the EVs are at home or parked after completing all the trips for the day, it is
assumed to be connected to the grid and hence available. This means that these EVs
will be part of forming the aggregated battery which the model can decide to charge
or discharge as required. During trips it is not available to exchange energy with grid.
So in early morning all the EVs are connected to the grid and gridãs virtual battery is
the aggregation of all EVs. Then with time as EVs come back from their trip, they start
connecting to the grid/charger with lower charger than what they left with. During the
next day when the EVs need to leave again, the EVs needs to be charged to full before
they leave again the next day. And this process continues. The Figure 2.1 shows with
a small example this phenomena. In the beginning, both the EVs are fully charged to
100%. Then say 1 of the EVs leave the system in order to complete the trip. This means
that after one EV leave, the system is left with only 1 EV and it can decide to charge or

3.3.2
SôÙcurve based projections
As mentioned in section 1, studies have found that the major driver for larger diffusion
of EVs into the system majorly comes from policy incentives. Here we donãt change the
base assumption of full electrification of road transport by 2050 as supported by EU polôÙ
icy/commitments. But as noted in section 1 that the diffusion rate of EVs are different in
each region. Whereas the current base scenarios assumes same uptake rate for each
region in 2035 (1/3 of their final count in 2045). Hence New assumptions about projecôÙ
tions of total number of EVs for the year 2025 and 2035 were used for this scenario.
The percentage of cars which are fully electric (here described as diffusion rate of EVs)
are based on technology innovation diffusion process. [28] defines diffusion as process
of spreading innovations across individuals within a social system. In the context of the
project, innovation would be the use of EVs instead of standard ICE powered vehicle.
Within a social system, innovation will diffuse faster if there exist a perceived relative
superiority of the innovation with respect to existing convention. This superiority can be
based on anything from objective technical or economic reasons to practicality, physiôÙ
cal comfort or social recognition. The technology diffusion model applied here is based
on epidemic models which follows the process of contagious disease spreading in a reôÙ
gion. The underlying process here assumes that there are fixed total number of potential
adopters (total number of cars in the country or region). The adoption process starts
with some individuals adopting the innovation as it becomes available. As these indiôÙ
vidual mixes with nonôÙadopters in the social system, the nonôÙadopters starts adopting
the technology. As total number of adopters increases, the likelihood of a nonôÙadopter
meeting an adopter also increases and the diffusion rate increases. As soon as total
number of adopter increases more than nonôÙadopters, the diffusion speed decreases
again. This kind of technology diffusion model based on logistic curve has been used to
describe diffusion of innovation process for also other kinds of technologies like hybrid
cord [29], wind power in india [30], solar power in canada [31]. They have been also
used to model diffusion of EVs in UKãs regions [15]. Main motivation for using these
curves comes from [12], where the author estimates the sôÙcurve based on 8 years of
data (2010ôÙ2017). The purpose of fitting these models in the referred papers was not

5
Discussion
This chapter presents a discussion on the results in light of the objectives of the study.
It also discusses the limitation of the methodology applied in the study and suggests
possible improvements and future work. Finally, a comparison of the results obtained
from this thesis with other studies in the literature is provided.
5.1
Discussion of results
Results shows that electrification of transport sector would need installation of extra genôÙ
eration capacity in electricity sector in future. There exists a clear advantage in enabling
flexible charging of EVs for future sector coupled European energy system. If charging
patterns of EVs are not managed, short term flexibility requirement will not only be reôÙ
quired in the electricity sector but this requirement can also propagate to other sectors
that are being electrified. But this would also depend on other factors and sizing deciôÙ
sion of other technologies. Overall total peak capacity required from electricity sector
can be reduced and short term storage in form of utility scale batteries could be deferred
when charging of EVs becomes flexible. On the other hand more capacity installation
of electricity grid in form of interconnections between the regions would also need more
cooperation between the countries. The result also suggests combined analysis and
planning hydrogen and electricity grids should be considered as they can to some exôÙ
tent effect one another. This also means that cooperation between agencies that are
responsible for planning these grids and interconnection can be beneficial.
One of the remarkable results from this study was less production and capacity of SolarôÙ
PV and more installation of wind energy technology when smart and flexible charging
of EVs are enabled in the energy system. This could be due to considered availability
of EVs for charging at home and assumption of no charging at workôÙplace and public
chargers. Also the optimal charging pattern obtained from the V2G charging scenario
showed increased correlation with production from wind energy technology as most of
the charging of EVs were shifted to late night hours . Correlation of the charging patôÙ
tern were already negative with SolarôÙPV production in DUMB charging scenario which
became increasingly negative in V2G charging scenario. This suggests an inherent adôÙ
vantage in using wind resources for charging purposes. But as wind energy technology
might not be available for longer time periods (sometimes there could be no wind enôÙ
ergy for couple of days), this requires long term storage to able to compensate for the
periods when wind production cannot provide enough energy.
Differences between total energy production from different generation technologies were
observed between the capacity development run and DayôÙAhead run. Main differences
were observed in total production from CHPs and FUELôÙTOôÙELECT and FUELôÙTOôÙ
HEAT generation technologies. In capacity development run, the total energy production
from these flexible but nonôÙVRE sources of energy increased in V2G charging scenario

5.2
Limitation of the model
In this section, some of the limitation of the model used for this study are discussed.
First of all work place or public charging are not considered in this model. EVs when
allowed to charge from public charger in between trips and from chargers at workôÙplace
could benefit from cheap solar resources during the day. This could have an impact on
installed capacity of SolarôÙPV technology. One possible future improvement could be to
allow some of the charging to take place in between the trips.
Secondly, no cost associated to required distribution grid reinforcements are considered
in the model. Moreover EVs are spread out in a region and cannot be optimally placed to
reduce these investment costs. [33],[15] have shown that increasing diffusion of EVs in
the regions might need increasing capacity of distribution grids. Hence implementation
of distribution grid reinforcement cost for the peak capacity should be considered in
future works.
Third, EVs are considered as free batteries in the model. Whereas it might not be
possible to perfectly coordinate charging of all the EVs simultaneously by an operator
or aggregator for each region. Moreover behaviour also will play a role in how and when
charging takes place. Also flexibility that comes from EVs can also be used for other
ancillary services related to grid stability like frequency and voltage regulation and grid

congestion. This means that the advantages as shown in the V2G charging scenario
should be carefully interpreted.
Finally some of the scenarios that were planned/designed for comparison during the theôÙ

G2V charging scenario (where the charging of EVs are flexible but no biôÙdirectional
charging is allowed in the model) and V2G charging scenario could have shown how
much benefit could having a biôÙdirectional charging can bring over just unidirectional
charging. This was due timeôÙresolution that was selected for the study. Whenever the

developed where the minimum SOC levels were systematically increased to create a
situation where the battery levels of the aggregated fleet needed to be higher than the
base level. Similar issues were observed also within these scenario and these were
also later rejected.
5.3
Comparison to other studies
In this section, the results of this study is compared to other studies from literature.
Reduction in total system cost when charging of EVs are flexible are in line with other
studies in literature [13], [14], [24], [34]. [14] in their study showed that when V2G capaôÙ

also supported by the findings of this study however here the batteries are not fully elimiôÙ
nated. Having said that, their results also showed some opposite finding like reduction in
hydrogen storage and increased investments in solarôÙPV while the investments in wind
energy technology reduced when enabling V2G capabilities to EVs. This could be due to
the fact in this study the model considers only charging at home. Whereas in their study
the charging at workplace and public chargers were allowed. [24] which introduced the
modelling of EVs in the Balmorel model showed that flexibility from EV charging required
increased installation of electric grid and substitution of SolarôÙPV technology with wind
energy technology but without the consideration of sectorôÙcoupling. This thesis showed
that those results are still valid even with a sectorôÙcoupled energy system where onshore
wind energy technology are restricted. Another result of this study regarding reduction
in curtailment of energy production from VRE are also supported by the findings [34].

6
Conclusion
This thesis studies the impact of flexibility mechanism that comes with electrification
of transport sectors in future sectorôÙcoupled European energy system going into 2045.
Commitments from nations to remove GHG emissions from transport sectors would
require diffusion of electric vehicles in the overall vehicle stock. This would lead to extra
demand from the electricity sector and will necessitate extra capacity installation for
electricity production. Charging patterns of these vehicles will play a key role in decisions
for energy mix and sizing of flexibility in terms of interconnections and storage. As VRE
based energy system already needs to come with such flexibility providing technologies,
being able to adjust the charging patterns from EV can bring benefits to the whole system
but would also alter some other planning decisions. This thesis concludes the following
points.
ãÂ Flexible and smart charging of EVs can bring the significant reduction in total sysôÙ
tem costs, most of which comes from reduction in installed capacities of different
technologies.
ãÂ Flexible charging of EVs can also lead to reduction in VRE curtailments which will
help in integration of VRE.
ãÂ If a flexible charging of EVs can be mechanised, 80% of short term storage in
form of batteries can be avoided but this would require some differences in the
energy mix, especially that of VREs. Overall less installation of SolarôÙPV and more
installation of Wind energy technology. If installation of onshore Wind turbines are
restricted, it is still optimal to install Offshore Wind turbines locally in all the regions
that can have access to it.
ãÂ This additional benefits would require changes in sizing of other flexibility providôÙ
ing technology. More long term heat and hydrogen storage, less hydrogen interôÙ
connections between regions and more electric interconnection between regions
would be needed to support the energy system.
