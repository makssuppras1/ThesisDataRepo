1. Introduction 
1.1 Purpose and motivation 
Visual odometry(VO) is a process of computing position and orientation of a robot by 
processing a sequence of camera images. Moravec (1) was the first person to describe the idea 
of estimating egomotion from visual input in early 1980s. The term, Visual Odometry was first 
used by Nister (2) in 2004, as a reference to wheel odometry which estimate motion based on 
integration of wheel rotation. In the same manner, VO incrementally estimate motion over time. 
VO is a particular case of more general problem of structure from motion(SFM), which focuses 
on recovering relative camera poses and three-dimensional structure from a set of camera 
images (3). While SFM tries to estimate both 3D structure and camera position, VO focuses 
only on estimating camera position. 
VO is a widely used technique in the field of robotics. Localization of a robot within its 
surroundings is one of fundamental problems in robotics. In the outdoor use-cases, GNSS and 
wheel odometry are often used as a tool to localize a robot. However, in many environments 
GNSS signals are not available and wheel odometry can only be used on land robots. In these 
cases usage of VO is especially useful. Examples of environments for VO usage can range from 
indoors, underwater, aerial to even rovers deployed to explore Mars planet.  
This Master Thesis was conducted at DTU Space department, which often use UAVs to 
capture aerial data for the purpose of Earth’s exploration. In order to have reliable and accurate 
localization of an UAV, an Ultra-Tightly Coupled GNSS/INS system was developed (4). 
However, UAVs used by DTU Space sometimes needs to fly in GNSS denied environments 
such as underneath a forest line. Therefore, a VO system had to be developed to fill in missing 
trajectories, when GNSS signals are not available.  
1.2 Visual Odometry system 
This chapter introduces general overview of how VO works for monocular and stereo 
hardware setups. The Master Thesis is build upon the work of previous Master students and 
their work will be presents in this chapter, (5), (6). It also contains project objectives for this 
Master Thesis. 
 As mentioned in the previous chapter, the VO task is to estimate translation and rotation 
between two camera images. There exists multiple approaches to solve this problem. One of 
the divisions depends on the camera setup, where VO can be done with a single 
camera(monocular vision) or two cameras(stereo vision). The other division is based on how 
motion estimation is calculated from the frame in time 𝑡−1 to the frame in time 𝑡. Then the 
categories would be: feature based approach, direct photometric based method and semi-dense 
method. The feature based approach uses a sparse set of points that are found in both frames to 
calculate motion. The direct photometric method uses all pixels intensities in a pair of images 
==PAGE:7==
2 
 
to estimate motion by minimizing photometric error. The semi-dense methods are a 
combination of the feature-based and direct methods. The advantage of direct methods is that 
all information in an image can be exploited to compute motion, and therefore obtain more 
precise results. On the other hand, feature-based methods handle large frame-to-frame motion 
better and are more robust towards inconsistencies in the image appearance. Since this Master 
Project is a continuation of the previous work, it will continue to focus on the feature-based 
approach. 
A feature-based VO pipeline is illustrated in Fig.1.2. In general, it is applicable to both 
monocular and stereo vision. Firstly, images need to be acquired. In the second step, feature 
locations and descriptors needs to be detected on newly acquired images. Features are salient 
points on an image plane that represent reprojected 3D points. By extracting a descriptor around 
a feature location it is possible to match independently detected features across image pairs. If 
two features represent the same 3D point then they are called image correspondences. 
Alternatively, image correspondences can be obtained by tracking features from a one image to 
another. Based on image correspondences, it is possible to estimate location of a 3D point. In 
the fourth step, frame-to-frame motion can be estimated based on the obtained 3D points and 
image correspondences. The result is camera translation and rotation in relation to the previous 
image. Lastly, as an additional step it is possible to optimize camera positions using jointly 
many camera positions to obtain more accurate trajectory. 
 
Fig.1.2. Main components of visual odometry. 
The main difference between monocular and stereo vision is the way that 3D points are 
triangulated(the second and third step in Fig.1.2). In the next sections, it will be explained how 
triangulation works for the monocular and stereo vision. 
1.2.1 Stereo vision 
For the stereo vision, two images are captured at the same time from two different 
cameras. If the stereo calibration is done beforehand, it is possible to transform images to a 
common plane. This process is called rectification of images. It simplifies feature matching and 
triangulation, because the image correspondence location will be on the vertical lines on the 
image planes. Then, the search for image correspondences can be reduced to vertical lines. 
Also, the stereo calibration allows to calculate the baseline, which is a distance between optical 
centers of stereo images.  Knowledge of the baseline enables to calculate 3D point locations in 
scale. A stereo vision setup is illustrated in Fig.1.2.1. Rectified images in left and right image 
pair are used for 3D point triangulation. Then, image correspondences are obtained between

1.2.2 Monocular vision 
In order to use monocular vision at least three images are necessary to triangulate 3D 
points and estimate motion. The monocular vision is illustrated in Fig.1.2.2. First image pair is 
used to triangulate 3D points. The translation 𝑇𝑡−1 and rotation 𝑅𝑡−1 are computed, however it 
is not possible to know the absolute scale of the translation vector 𝑇𝑡−1. In the same way, the

be then used to scale the translation vector 𝑇𝑡. As a result, the relative scale frame-to-frame can

1.2.3 Previous work 
The Master Thesis is conducted as a continuation of J.Linde and R.Haaskjold work, (5) 
(6). J.Linde developed a stereo image acquisition platform that was meant to be used 
especifically for the VO task. This platform integrates two high resolution Matrix Vision 
cameras with an Odroid-XU4 ARM development board and a u-blox NEO-M8 GNSS receiver 
(5). Moreover, a stereo VO application was developed to process data acquired with the camera 
platform. It used SURF features, stereo matching and RANSAC outlier rejection to provide the 
feature points for motion estimation. Singular value decomposition was used to compute motion 
estimation. Thus, this work enabled to capture datasets with self-developed VO platform and 
provided basis for the VO application. R.Haaskjold work mainly focused on implementation 
and evaluation of motion estimation algorithms. His Master Thesis focused on comparison 
between 3D-2D and 3D-3D motion estimation algorithms. The conclusion was that the 3D-2D 
perform better than 3D-3D motion estimation algorithms. The algorithm that achieved the best 
performance was Perspective-n-Point(PnP) motion estimation algorithm. So, throughout this 
Master Thesis, a PnP algorithm is used for stereo motion estimation. R.Haaskjold also 
investigated methods to find a best subset of originally detected features for motion estimation. 
Since a great effort was put into optimizing feature detection and motion estimation steps of 
VO pipeline, this work has only briefly considered these steps of the VO pipeline.

1.2.4 Master Thesis objectives 
This Master Thesis focuses on improving motion estimation by modifying existing 
feature matching(step 3 in Fig.1.2) and adding Bundle Adjustment that refines camera poses 
after the motion estimation(step 5 in Fig.1.2).  Moreover, it is important to evaluate the accuracy 
of the developed VO system with the data collected by the DTU camera acquisition platform.

as a ground truth for comparison. The objectives of the Master Thesis are: 
 
• Improving feature detection and matching/tracking by using additional 
enhancement strategies 
• Developing Bundle Adjustment to refine camera poses after motion estimation  
• Collecting own datasets with camera payload and GNSS/INS payload mounted 
on DTU Space UAV 
• Evaluation of the VO algorithm on a collected dataset 
 
The project overall purpose is to fill in the missing location of an UAV, when GNSS 
signals are not available. Therefore, MATLAB programming language was used to develop the 
project, so that it can be effortlessly integrated with GNSS/INS software that was written in 
MATLAB. Importantly, the trajectory is processed offline and no real-time implementation is 
needed, therefore the focus has been on accuracy, rather than real-time execution.

2.1 Introduction 
In this chapter enhancement strategies for feature selection and management of the VO 
will be described and evaluated. In particular, the chapter will focus on keyframing, bucketing 
and feature tracking. Some of the mentioned strategies were done by the previous students,

As was seen from (6), the SURF features achieves better performance than ORB features 
when the VO software was evaluated on the KITTI dataset. From, (9) (6), it was concluded that 
the SURF features were more robust to illumination changes in underexposed images, detecting

2.3 Keyframe selection 
In the frame-to-frame VO algorithm without keyframe selection, all features in the current 
time t are matched to the previous t-1 features. In the VO pipeline with keyframing the 
subsequent frames are matched to the last keyframe. Keyframing is especially useful in the 
situation where camera movement is small compared to the distance to the scene points. When 
the intervals between frames are small, 3-D point triangulation exhibit large uncertainties and 
therefore decreases motion estimation accuracy (3). Small movements are often present when 
the UAV is starting its flight on ground or is hovering in a single place. In order to avoid large 
triangulated uncertainties keyframing should be used. A frame is selected as keyframe when 
not enough features are retained in the next image. If κkf is a number of features detected in the 
last keyframe and κt is number of features that were detected and matched to the keyframe 
features, then the ratio: 
𝛼= κ𝑡

is a parameter that decides when to create a new keyframe from the current keyframe. It 
happens when the ratio 𝛼< 𝛼𝑡ℎ is below a threshold. This threshold is a hyperparameter that 
needs to be tuned. 
There exists different keyframe update criterion, such as distance in between cameras, 
expected estimation error of initial camera motion or measures of uncertainty of 3D triangulated 
points related to low temporal flow disparity (10), (11). Due to time constraints only the simple 
solution based on retained keyframe-to-frame features ratio will be used. This strategy should 
lead to decrease in an accumulated drift error, because frames in between keyframes should not 
contribute to an increase in an accumulated drift. 
2.4 Bucketing 
Bucketing is a technique of detecting features by dividing an image into several non-
overlapping rectangles as can be seen in Fig.2.4.1. In each bucket features are extracted 
separately and then accumulated once all the buckets detect features.  
 
Fig.2.4.1. Example image from KITTI dataset sequence one with shown 3x4 bucketing. 
This is beneficial in several ways. Firstly, the features are distributed more uniformly 
across the whole image. Areas densely populated with features are reduced, while new features 
==PAGE:13==
8 
 
are present in previously empty parts. In this way, it is more likely that far and close features 
are used for the estimation process, which results in more precise estimation of egomotion (12). 
Secondly, when a scene rapidly rotates it is possible without bucketing that all feature points 
are concentrated in one part of an image. Then with a large rotation features will be lost in the 
next frame and no matches would be found to estimate motion. Bucketing prevents such a 
situation with uniformly distributed features, as long as scene is well textured. Thirdly, it was 
found that bucketing reduces the drift rates from biased scene points (12). The hyperparameter 
to set are the number and distribution of buckets. In next chapters, optimal value for these 
parameter will be found. 
During the evaluation phase of this project, it was found that even with bucketing 
sometimes features were clustered in high density areas. One of the examples can be seen in 
Fig.2.4.2. The data was collected on football field where white lines were painted on the ground. 
This caused the algorithm to detect majority of feature points on those white lines. High 
concentration of features caused problems with RANSAC motion estimation, because selecting 
only features from densely populated areas as inliers was enough for RANSAC to terminate 
with 0.99 probability of finding successful noise free model (13). Even though, selected features 
were noise free, their distribution in the image caused less accurate motion estimation.  
 
Fig.2.4.2. SURF Features locations marked as red crosses on the original image from a collected 
UAV data. 
In order to prevent RANSAC from selecting only the points with high density, reductive 
bucketing was introduced. In reductive bucketing, an image is divided into buckets to count 
how many features each bucket contains. Then, features were removed at random from all 
buckets that had more features than median number of features in buckets containing at least 
one feature. Number of features removed was such that the number of features after the  
reduction was equal to the median number of features before the removal. In Fig.2.4.3 can be

seen features selected to remove. As expected the removed features are from high feature 
density areas.

2.5 Parameter tuning 
As suggested in (6), it is necessary to tune hyperparameters of the VO algorithm in order 
to produce accurate results. Previously the parameters were chosen manually by observing what 
might be a good parameters set. In this work, a more programmatic approach is taken, where a

KITTI dataset 1st sequence with variants of parameters sets. Average absolute global position 
error will be used as a measure of algorithm’s performance. Ideally, the VO algorithm should 
be tested on more datasets to assure that those parameters are not overfitting into this particular 
dataset. However, processing many dataset is exhaustive computationally and not feasible in

2.6 Tracking 
Feature selection has a crucial influence on the final performance, since all the steps in 
the VO pipeline are dependent on reliable features. It has been shown that tracking using KLT 
tracker, instead of feature matching, can lead to a significant improvement in the VO algorithm 
performance. This approach to feature management was found in (14) (15) and (16). In the next 
chapters, KLT tracker will be explained as well as its integration into VO pipeline. It will be 
also evaluated on the KITTI dataset and own dataset.

stereo image pairs as well as consecutive frames in time. It is visualized in Fig.2.8.1. Consider 
a scenario were frame 1 and 4 are the keyframes. Firstly, SURF features were detected in both 
stereo images and matched together. The matched stereo features served as a basis set for 
relating keyframe features and features in the next frames. In frame 2, 3 and 4, stereo matches 
were found and matched to the last keyframe, which is the frame 1. After finding the matches 
motion is estimated for all frames in relation to the first keyframe. In the frame 5, number of 
matched features from frame 5 to the 1st keyframe is smaller than keyframing threshold(as 
discussed in the chapter 2.3). At this point frame number 4 is selected as a new keyframe and 
matching of features between frame 4 and 5 is done. Since the last keyframe is the frame number 
4, then all next frames are matched and their motions estimated to the frame number 4.  
 
Fig.2.8.1. Overview of VO algorithm based on feature matching. 
The VO algorithm with tracking differs from the algorithm described above in finding 
salient points in between frames. The overview of the VO with tracking can be seen in Fig.2.8.2. 
Without tracking, salient points detection was based on computing features in all images and 
then matching. For the tracking algorithm, the features needs to be detected only once in a 
keyframe. Afterwards, their locations are tracked frame-to-frame. At each frame, KLT tracker 
will return that some of the salient points could not be tracked to the next frame. If not enough 
points can be tracked to the next frame, then the last frame becomes a new keyframe and 
completely new features are detected for that frame.  
 
Fig.2.8.2 Overview of VO algorithm based on feature tracking. 
==PAGE:19==
14 
 
One of the advantages of tracking is significant speedup of the algorithm, because the 
costly computation step of finding descriptor and matching features in stereo and frame-to-
frame pairs of images is omitted. Moreover, the VO with tracking might perform better because 
it can retrieve more features over multiple frames with less outliers. This leads to less frequent 
update of keyframes and faster RANSAC inliers computation, therefore better and faster motion 
estimation. Also, memory requirements are smaller, because it is not necessary to keep 
information about descriptors in between keyframes. On the other hand, tracking fails when a 
frame-to-frame motion is large, because the KLT tracker assumes small local displacement of 
tracked points. This can be avoided by using a pyramidal version of KLT, but cannot be 
completely bypassed (20). The other disadvantage is that the KLT tracker can lose feature 
tracking, when an image locally is not very distinctive. In this situation, SURF features will 
handle the problem better, because the SURF descriptors are more sophisticated representation 
of a point’s neighborhood. With a more complex descriptor it is possible to distinguish correct 
matches more reliably.

2.8  RANSAC changes 
The RANSAC implementation builds on (5), (6), but has been further optimized within 
this work. In this chapter, RANSAC will be explained as well as proposed modifications.  
The RANdom SAmple Consensus(RANSAC) algorithm proposed by Fischler and Bolles 
(13) is a general parameter estimation approach designed to cope with a large proportion of 
outliers in the input data. RANSAC is a resampling technique that generates candidate solution 
by using the minimum number observations (data points) required to estimate the underlaying 
model parameters (21). In the Visual Odometry problem, the data points are point pairs in 
frame-to-frame motion that should represent the same 3D point projected into two images. 
Using points right after feature matching/feature tracking will produce poor motion estimation, 
because of the outliers in the data. Outliers mostly arises from the wrong feature matching, but 
might also come from inaccuracies in depth estimation of 3D points. Model parameters are the 
translation vector and the rotation matrix of relative motion estimation between image frames. 
Modifying existing motion estimation is not a part of this project, therefore this chapter will 
leave out detailed description of the motion calculation. Interested readers can find motion 
estimation algorithms used for this project described in (6).  
RANSAC algorithm is essentially composed of two steps that are repeated in an iterative 
fashion(hypothesize-and-test framework) (22): 
• Hypothesize: minimal sample set is randomly selected from the input dataset and 
the model parameters are computed using only the elements of the minimal sample 
set. In the VO problem, a minimal set needed to calculate motion is a set of four 
data points. After randomly sampling them, the translation vector and the rotation 
matrix is computed. 
• Test: in the second step RANSAC checks which elements of the entire dataset are 
consistent with the model initialized with the parameters estimated in the first step. 
For the VO algorithm, this step means that all 3D points are reprojected onto next 
image frame. If they are within a neighborhood of their 2D correspondences in the 
next frame, then they are classified as inliers. A reprojected point is in the 
neighborhood of its 2D corresponding point if its norm to that correspondence is 
smaller than the RANSAC threshold. This is visualized on Fig. 2.11.1. 
RANSAC algorithm terminates when the probability of finding a model with a better 
support than the current best model falls below a predefined threshold(typically 1%-5%) (23). 
The termination criterion is based on calculation of minimal number RANSAC iterations 
required to find outlier-free subset with an enough level of confidence. Minimal number of 
RANSAC iterations can be found as: 
𝑘 ≥log(1 −𝑧)
log(1 −𝜀𝑚) 
Where: 
• 𝑘 – minimal number of RANSAC iterations 
• 𝑧 – probability of finding a subset of outlier-free data  
• 𝜀 – probability of choosing an inlier every time a single point is sampled 
• 𝑚 – size of a minimal sample set 
==PAGE:25==
20 
 
Moreover, 𝑘 is adaptively changed at every iteration when more inliers are found than 
the previously best motion estimation. 𝜀 is updated as a ratio of number of inliers found to 
number of all data points. In this way, 𝑘 can decrease as the RANSAC algorithm finds more 
inliers. This leads to reduction in the necessary iterations.

First change is motion calculation after RANSAC terminates. In the previous version, 
when motion estimation algorithm found optimal inliers set from the dataset, motion estimation 
was computed only on minimal set of 4 inliers used in hypothesize step of RANSAC. This is 
not ideal, because more accurate motion can be obtained by usage of all inliers after RANSAC. 
Therefore, the motion calculation for PnP is recomputed after final inlier set is found.  
Second modification was made in the sampling part of a minimal set in the hypothesize 
step of RANSAC. As it turned out in some cases RANSAC sampled 4 points that were found 
in the same part of an image and terminated by finding inliers only from this part of an image. 
This situation was especially prominent when most of the feature locations were located in one 
part of an image. Previously described bucketing should prevent this issue, however in some 
cases, parts of images do not contain distinctive points of interest and SURF features cannot be 
found. Therefore it is important that RANSAC do not classify points in low density regions that 
can be used to correctly estimate motion as outliers. Inliers detected across all image are 
especially significant for the application of UAV with downward looking camera. In the 6DOF 
motion, where most 3D points are on the same plane(the ground surface below UAV), it is easy 
to fit an incorrect rotation that will affect next frames. Therefore, sampling is done in a way that 
sample points come from different image regions. A mean of X and Y coordinates of all points 
is taken and this mean is used to divide image into four regions from which a minimal sample 
set is drawn. The division can be seen in Fig.2.11.2. 
==PAGE:26==
21 
 
Fig.2.11.2. In the background images in time 𝑡 and 𝑡+ 1 plotted as falsecolor overlay. Point pairs 
between frames as green crosses and red circles, connected by a line. On top are drawn blue lines that 
show how image is divided for RANSAC sampling purposes. 
Third modification is a change of the RANSAC threshold that classifies a reprojected 
point as an inlier or outlier. Previously it was a fix number, defined as an algorithm parameter. 
If the reprojection was within 4 pixels radius then is was counted as an inlier. When working 
on the collected UAV dataset, it was apparent that with big translation between keyframe and 
a current frame RANSAC took more time to terminate. In the most extreme cases, it reached 
several millions of iterations before terminating. This was caused by the adaptive minimal 
iterations 𝑘, which was set to high number because RANSAC couldn’t find a subset of the 
dataset with a high number of inliers. To overcome this problem an adaptive RANSAC 
threshold was used. The threshold changes at each processed image frame based on a median 
distance between image points detected in an image 𝑡−1 and an image in time 𝑡, currently 
ranging between 3 and 8 pixels. In this way, the threshold for a small movement is the same 
and for large movement the threshold is increased, resulting in a faster RANSAC termination.  
Fourth modification is adding a threshold value for the maximal RANSAC iterations. If 
the algorithm reaches 30000 iterations, then it automatically terminates despite 𝑘 minimal 
number of RANSAC iterations. At this point, it is clear that the dataset contain too many outliers 
to successfully estimate motion. Usually it happens when the motion between a keyframe and 
a current frame is too large. When RANSAC terminates because of reaching the maximal

threshold, then the previous frame 𝑡−1 is set as a keyframe and current frame 𝑡 is matched to 
the newly set keyframe.

3. Bundle Adjustment 
3.1 Bundle Adjustment - introduction 
Bundle adjustment is a problem of refining a visual reconstruction to produce jointly 
optimal 3D structure and camera motion. Optimal means that the parameter estimates are found 
by minimizing some cost function that quantifies the model fitting error. In joint optimization 
the solution is simultaneously optimal with respect to both structure and camera variations (25). 
Using multiple view refinement, that corrects frame to frame estimation, allows to obtain 
trajectory estimation that has lower drift from ground truth. Usually, full bundle adjustment is 
the last step in visual odometry pipeline since it requires all camera poses with 3D points and 
2D points projections. It is possible to use windowed bundle adjustment, which optimizes only 
N last frames to lower computational demand.

Bundle Adjustment. First 10 frames of 1st sequence  of KITTI dataset will be processed with

yellow lines that show matching between them.  
Fig.3.6.1. The first image of KITTI dataset sequence one with measured features locations marked as 
red circles and 3D points reprojections as green crosses 
A series of perturbations is applied to all degree of freedom in the BA problem in order 
to test correctness and robustness of the optimization algorithm. Parameters that can be 
optimized over are: location and rotation of the cameras and locations of 3D points. The BA in 
this project is used mainly to refine the VO positions, therefore 3D points locations are less 
important. To save memory and computational cost only camera locations and rotations will be 
refined. At first, the BA will use gradient descent over window of 4 camera frames with stride 
1. In the following tests perturbations will be applied to first and third frame out of 4 frame in 
the window. This way it possible to test whether BA will correctly refine only the poses of 
shifted frames. The BA starts with learning rate of 1, which is a parameter that multiplies the 
added position update to a previous position.  Simple search for the optimal learning rate is 
conducted if the sum of reprojection errors is larger than initial error. When this happens 
optimization algorithms takes too large step, therefore learning rate is this situation is divided 
by 10. The BA terminates when it reaches the maximum iterations which equals to 25, when 
the reprojection error is smaller than 0.1% of the initial error or when a search for the learning 
rate takes too long(10 iterations). Usually gradient descent terminates because of the third 
condition, i.e. when steps with various learning rate are not able to decrease reprojection error.

4. Dataset collection 
4.1 Camera acquisition platform 
The camera acquisition platform was developed by Johannes Linde for the purpose of 
VO data collection (5). This platform contains two high resolution Matrix Vision camera that 
are triggered to acquire images by u-blox NEO-M8 GNSS receiver. Originally, Odroid-XU4 
ARM development board was used to save images. Due to the low achievable framerate the 
Odroid-XU4 was replaced by Lattepanda Alpha minicomputer (6). There were multiple issues 
mentioned in (6) with the camera acquisition platform: 
• It was not possible to capture data with a frequency above 4Hz. The solution was fixing 
software cross complied on Lattepanda, which had delay of 200ms in between image 
captures. Decreasing this value allowed for image acquisition with frequency of 20Hz, 
however the GNSS signal that triggered cameras could not be logged with this 
frequency. Therefore, an option to set different frequencies for GNSS trigger and 
camera images acquisition was added. 
• narrow dynamic range, often resulting in dark overall images when some parts of an 
image are bright. So solve this problem two modifications were made. Firstly, software 
on Lattepanda was expended, such that it is possible to set a minimal exposure time. 
Secondly, cameras are facing downwards with an angle of approximately 90 degree 
instead of 45 degree tilt. In this way, it is less likely that images contain bright parts 
coming from the sun above a horizon. 
• camera error that stopped data collection – this is the most serious problem that was not 
solved. Often during data acquisition the camera error occurs in the middle of a data 
collection in the left camera. The initial guess was that the Lattepanda board interfere 
with signal from the Matrix Vision left camera. To test this hypothesis the Lattepanda 
was replaced with an Intel NUC Minicomputer. However, the problem still existed and 
the Lattepanda was used again. Then, the cables connecting Lattlepanda to Matrix 
Vision cameras were replaced. Also, it was checked whether the same problems exists 
with vision matrix software to ensure it is not a problem with our data collection 
software that receives cameras images. In the end the problem was not solved and 
currently the best explanation for it is a loose USB port in Matrix Vision camera that 
causes connection problems.

The collected datasets were summarized in the previous chapter. The best quality dataset 
was the dataset number 2 and this dataset will be used for evaluation. As seen from previous 
analysis, the crucial flaw of this dataset is that the calibration between stereo cameras was not 
correct. 
5.1 Stereo vision evaluation 
The stereo vision system that was described in the first and second chapter will be 
evaluated on the second collected dataset. In the Fig.5.1 is shown the ground truth data acquired 
by GNSS/INS system and the trajectory estimated by the stereo VO. The start point is in the 
center (0,0) of the coordinate system. The UAV path was set to a fixed height of 8 meters, 
therefore most of the change of position was in the YX plane. Although the estimated UAV 
trajectory is 6DOF, trajectory is projected into XY plane for visualization purposes. The 
estimated trajectory differ from the ground truth significantly. The cause is the incorrect stereo 
calibration, which results in incorrectly triangulated 3D points. The 3D points are disturbed by 
an unknown rotation and translation in between the stereo cameras. The initial motion 
estimation is approximately correct, because the distorted 3D points do not affect the motion 
estimation in the forward direction as much ad they do during rotation. When the UAV starts 
to turn close to point (0,20), the estimated motion has large frame-to-frame translation to fit in 
the camera position into wrongly triangulated points. This results in the large gaps between 
fragments of the estimated trajectory, as well as the wrong shape of the estimated trajectory. In 
conclusion, it is not possible to use the original stereo VO on a dataset with an incorrect stereo 
calibration.

5.2 Monocular vision 
As was seen from the previous chapters, the stereo calibration of the collected datasets 
did not produced correctly rectified images. Although, calibration between the pair of stereo 
cameras was not correct, the intrinsic calibration of individual cameras was correct. Due to time 
constraints it was not possible to fix the rigidness of camera acquisition payload and capture 
more datasets. Therefore, in order to provide rough evaluation of what accuracy it is possible 
to obtain with camera acquisition platform, a simple monocular solution was developed. Using 
stereo vision, it should be possible to have more accurate results than using the monocular 
vision, because of a precise stereo calibration, resulting in accurate 3D points triangulation and 
motion estimation. Most accurate VO evaluated on KITTI dataset use stereo vision instead of 
monocular vision. As can be seen in (35) stereo methods are more accurate than monocular 
methods when used in the same motion estimation algorithms. Therefore, the proposed simple 
monocular algorithm can serve as a lower bound of accuracy of previously described stereo 
algorithm.  
The monocular vision was build with already implemented functions in MATLAB 
Computer Vision Toolbox and Mexopencv. Mexopencv is an open-source computer vision 
library written in C++ that can be called from a MATLAB script. The implemented monocular 
VO algorithm is described in the introduction chapter 1.2. SURF features were used with [4,4] 
bucketing and 200 maximal feature amount per bucket. As explained in the introduction the 
motion estimation used 2D-2D images correspondences to directly estimate motion and 3D-3D 
point correspondences to establish relative scale of the movement. The motion estimation used 
keyframing.

optical axis as it executed the planned trajectory. The one and a half rotation around optical axis 
is clearly visible in this plot. It can be concluded from this plot that the rotation error about Z 
axis is small, because both angles are close to each other throughout the flight. The error along 
the optical axis is small due to large frame-to-frame changes of angles about the optical axis. 
In comparison, the rotation in Y and X axis is small, therefore it is more difficult for the motion 
estimation algorithm to compute rotation accurately. The shapes of X and Y rotation are similar 
to the ground truth rotation curve, which suggests that the VO is capable to estimate small 
rotations around X and Y axis. However, even small errors in the heading and roll of the UAV 
leads to the increasing divergence in height estimation, which can be seen in Fig.5.3.2. The drift

The second factor that influenced the accuracy of the estimated motion is calculation of 
the relative scale. As was explained in the chapter 5.2, the relative scale accounts for the scale 
of the translation vector. In Fig.5.4.5 is shown the length of translation vector of the ground

incorrect relative scale affects the overall trajectory due to the incorrect lengths of translation

Noteworthy is the fact that the variation tends to increase when the travelled distance is 
higher(based on higher values of ground truth in Fig.5.3.4). The images are captured with a 
constant frequency of 20Hz and the time in between frames is constant. Therefore, the variance 
of the relative scale is influenced by the velocity of the UAV. The conclusion is that with higher 
velocity the relative scale computation has higher variance and it is harder to reliably estimate 
length of translation vector. 
Moreover, the relative scale can provide an indirect insight into accuracy of triangulated 
3D points. The relative scale is based on ratio of distances between 3D points in two subsequent 
frames. If the variance of estimated relative scale is high, then it indicates that the triangulation 
of 3D points is not accurate.

6. Discussion and future work 
6.1 Discussion 
The Master Thesis was focused on feature selection enhancement strategies and 
postprocessing of camera pose optimization. Additionally, the project objective was to evaluate 
performance of the developed VO on the data collected from the camera platform mounted on 
the DTU Space UAV.  
The first objective of the Master Thesis, developing and optimizing enhancement 
techniques for feature, was fulfilled. Bucketing and RANSAC algorithms were modified such 
that they produce better quality features for motion estimation. Moreover, bucketing and 
keyframing parameters, namely bucketing size and keyframing threshold, were fine tuned to 
achieve better results. The selection criterion for choosing optimal values was an average 
absolute translation error evaluated on KITTI dataset 1. One of the issues with choosing 
parameters in this manner, is that optimal parameter values might be the best for this particular 
dataset, but do not generalize to every dataset. Changes in illumination, different speeds of 
camera movement, very uniform areas in the image or moving objects might shift the optimal 
values of parameters for bucketing and keyframing. The best approach would be to test different 
sets of parameters on more datasets. High computational demand of computing trajectories for 
all values hindered extended search for optimal values. However a systematic approach to 
choosing the best values is still better than manual guess what the optimal values might be. In 
the end, keyframe threshold of 0.5 and bucketing size of [4, 12] were used throughout the 
project, as the VO with those parameters achieved the best accuracy. As a last enhancement 
strategy, a KLT tracker was used to track feature locations. It replaced feature matching for 
calculating image correspondences between a keyframe and a frame. This strategy proved to 
highly improve algorithm accuracy when used on a dataset with very distinctive images, such 
as in the KITTI dataset. When the KLT tracker was used on the collected dataset it failed to 
correctly identify image correspondences. The objective of the KLT tracker is to find a 
corresponding point by a usage of gradient intensity values around the tracked point in the 
previous image. This proved to be not sophisticated enough representation of local 
neighborhood of an interest point, and thus resulted in wrong tracking when used with less 
distinctive image that come from the collected dataset. 
In the following part of the Master Thesis, a Bundle Adjustment was developed to refine 
camera positions in regards to multiple frames at once. Firstly, the general problem of a 
windowed Bundle Adjustment was presented. Then, the necessary theory about unconstrained 
optimization was explained with a focus on different optimization algorithms. Unconstrained 
optimization was explained in the context of VO. The software for BA was developed from 
ground up. During evaluation, it was shown that it works on synthetic problems that are weakly 
non-linear, however fails when used in real world application. Despite correctly minimizing 
reprojection error, it sometimes diverges away from the ground truth. The explanation is that 
the BA finds steps that end up in a different local minimum of loss function than desired.  
Unfortunately, many problems arised with the camera acquisition platform. In result a 
significant amount of time had to be devoted to address those problems and limited time was 
==PAGE:63==
58 
 
left to the aforementioned tasks. The problems were: low frequency of captured stereo images, 
low dynamic range of captured images and connection issues between the onboard computing 
device and the Matrix Vision cameras. The first two were successfully solved. Most 
importantly, it was found that the supports that the cameras are mounted on are not rigid enough 
and move during flight, making it difficult to capture correctly calibrated stereo vision. The 
resulting datasets are usable, but cannot provide an accurate stereo calibration.  
The performance of the stereo VO algorithm was evaluated on the captured dataset. The 
obtained trajectory was inaccurate due to the stereo calibration issues. Therefore, a monocular 
VO algorithm was developed to assess if it is possible to use the collected data to estimate 
motion. As anticipated, it was possible to estimate motion that is close to the ground truth. A 
careful analysis has shown that there are two major causes of estimated trajectory divergence 
from the ground truth. The first factor is a drift of rotation around the X and Y axis. Slowly 
accumulated rotational drift leads to growing error in the height measurement that greatly 
contributes to the overall translation error. Secondly, the computed relative scale has high 
variance, especially when the UAV’s speed is increased. In result, the relative length of 
translation vector from frame to frame is not reliable. In the end, the monocular VO has shown 
that promising results can be obtained with data captured by camera acquisition platform. 
6.2 Future work 
Fixing issues with the camera acquisition platform should have the highest priority in 
continuation of this project. Making the camera supports more rigid will enable to capture the 
data that is consistent in regards to its calibration. Moreover, the USB ports in the Matrix Vision 
cameras should be replaced, because they are the most likely cause of connection issues that 
sometimes stops acquisition of images during tests. Then, the stereo VO algorithm should be 
evaluated with collected dataset, preferably with separated rotation and translation tests, such 
that they can be addressed individually. The parameter tuning should be further investigated in 
regards to other not mentioned in this Master Thesis parameters: number of detected features, 
threshold for features detection, maximal RANSAC iterations and RANSAC confidence level. 
Lastly, it is possible to boost VO accuracy by fusing it other sensors such as IMU.
