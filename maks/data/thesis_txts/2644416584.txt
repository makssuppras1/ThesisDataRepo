Abstract
The science of dispersing particles currently is a widely unexplored area. However, getting
a deeper understanding of the process and mechanisms could increase the eﬃciency, cutting
costs and improve quality of coatings. This study aimed to shed light on the mechanisms of
the dispersion process. Speciﬁcally transient particle size distributions of titanium dioxide in
acrylic binder during a dispersion were analyzed using a laser diﬀraction analysis unit (LDA).
Particle size distributions (PSD) were analyzed using and comparing volume moment mean
diameters D[4;3] across experiments, and comparing oversized particles measured by LDA with
measurements from the industrially used Hegman Gauge. Initially stability and repeatability
experiments were conducted for the LDA method. Subsequently, experiments were conducted
dispersing a simple titanium dioxide acrylic mill base using a water cooled high speed disk
disperser at three diﬀerent peripheral velocities. Measuring the transient particle size distri-
bution samples were taken at 0 min, 1 min, 6 min and 26 min, which were analyzed using
Hegman gauge and LDA. Additionally some samples were analyzed using scanning electron
microscopy (SEM) to investigate the applicability of the SEM method. Using a hydro MV cell
in the LDA, it was possible at some degree to track the transient particle size distributions for
three diﬀerent dispersion experiments. High variability between similar samples were observed,
though some degree of repeatability was observed. Diﬀerent transient particle size distribu-
tions between diﬀerent experiments were observed. Comparisons with Hegman gauge ﬁneness
of grind showed that a R2 = 0.68 correlation between the Dv(98) and the Hegman gauge was
observed. However, the LDA underestimated the oversized particles compared to the Hegman
gauge. Additionally it was found that SEM was only applicable for very dispersed particle
distributions. The results suggested that the LDA unit using a hydro MV cell was able to track
and measure the transient particle size distribution during a dispersion process to some degree.
Furthermore, comparing to a model using only erosion as mechanism the results correlate rea-
sonably, suggesting that for the dispersion of titanium dioxide in a high speed disperser the
main dispersion mechanism was erosion.

Thesis Description
For coatings, the pigment particle size distribution has a crucial role. Previously it has been
discovered that there is a correlation between increased coating performance and smaller more
evenly distributed pigment size distributions.
Thus, more eﬃcient dispersion processes are
needed to achieve these sizes faster for the dispersion to be more eﬃcient and less costly.
Models and theories have been developed to explain phenomena in the dispersion process.
Nevertheless the full size distribution of the pigments changing over the dispersion process is
not well described and investigated for coatings in the public literature. The current state of the
theories and measuring techniques are mostly based on the average or largest size of particles.
The aim of this project is to achieve a better understanding of the dispersion process and how
the entire particle size distribution changes over time. The particle size distributions will be
determined using a laser diﬀraction analysis (LDA) unit. Thus, preliminary experiments are
conducted to determine if this method is suitable for determining coating size distributions over
time. Experiments will be conducted using a high-speed disk disperser varying the rotational
speed to see if it inﬂuences the full particle size distribution measured by the LDA. Mapping
during the dispersion process is conducted, to understand how the particle size develops over
time with diﬀerent rotational speeds. Analysis will be performed by using and comparing results
from a the traditional Hegman gauges and the methods used in this study: laser diﬀraction
analysis and scanning electron microscopy.
Furthermore, the results will be compared and
tested against a particle size distribution erosion model produced by Kiil et al. [1].

Introduction
Coatings are essential for many aspects of our life. They are used for aesthetic or protective
purposes or a combination of the two, prolonging the lifetime or beautifying surfaces. Coatings
are applied to a surface or substrate which needs protection or a more appealing aesthetic.
Common for all coatings is that they contain pigments which grants the coating additional
properties. Diﬀerent pigments can be used to add color, mechanical and anti-corrosive proper-
ties to a coating and can even be used as biocides for prevention of biofouling in the maritime
industry [2][3]. Dispersions are solutions in which non-soluble particles are suspended in tar-
get solutions. In the case of coatings, pigments are suspended in solvent, binder and other
additives. In a stable dispersion system, the attractive and repulsive forces present keep the
particles separated and evenly distributed, thus the particles will not come close enough to-
gether to adhere to one another (agglomerate) [4]. When manufacturing a good and stable
coating, pigment size is a very important factor regarding the diﬀerent properties the coating
can achieve. For the most dominant white pigment on the market, titanium dioxide, the particle
size distribution has been reported to inﬂuence a wide range of properties, such as mechanical
strength, elastic modulus, color, gloss, tint strength, hiding power, hue, depth of shade have
been reported to be inﬂuenced by reducing the size of pigment particles. Therefore, low average
pigment sizes or low sizes, narrow particle size distributions is favorable from an performance
and economical point of view [5][6][7][8][9][10][11][12]. Hence, proper average pigment size con-
trol is needed to monitor that the pigment size in the coating is optimal for the speciﬁc purpose.
The overall coating manufacturing process can be divided into ﬁve steps; weighing of ingredi-
ents, mixing, dispersion of pigment particles, let-down, laboratory testing and canning. Firstly
the amount of the solvents, binders, pigments and additives are very carefully weighed accord-
ing to a formulation to achieve a good consistent coating. The pigments are mixed with as little
binder, additives and solvent as possible while still being under the critical pigment loading and
with a viscosity that is within the range of the dispersing equipment used [13]. The solution is
transferred to a container where dispersion (not to be confused with a dispersion solution) of
pigment particles will happen. This dispersion process is where the pigment agglomerates are
reduced in size by introducing mechanical energy to the system. This is a crucial and diﬃcult

2.1
Coating Composition & Properties
In the coating industry a wide range of constituents are being used to add speciﬁc properties
to speciﬁc coating depending on the application. The components can be divided into two
groups, the continuous and discontinuous phase. The continuous phase also called the vehicle
primarily consists of binder/polymer and solvent, and secondarily additives in small quantities
which can serve diﬀerent purposes to enhance the coating. The discontinuous phase are includ-
ing pigments, which consists of the primary pigments and possibly extenders/additives. The
binder is the primary layer of coating when applied after solvent has evaporated, which holds
most of the physical and chemical properties. The role of the binder is to bind to and car-
ry/stabilize pigment until the coating is cured forming the solid layer protecting the substrate.
The solvent serves as a diluent of the binder also allowing wetting of the pigment particles for
easier dispersion. Additives can be added to the coating to achieve diﬀerent physical, chemical
and rheological properties depending on the additive, like ﬂow agents, thickeners and more
[18][19][2][20]. Primary pigments contribute to most of the tinctorial properties of coatings like
tinting strength, gloss, hiding power, tinting tone which all are dependent on shape and size.
Extenders or ﬁllers are used to cut down on the cost of the coating, by adding these secondary
cheaper pigments to replace either binder or primary pigments without reducing the perfor-
mance of the coating if used correctly. Furthermore, they can be used to adjust the viscosity of
the coating. The extenders have a relatively low cost, though their properties are signiﬁcantly
worse compared to the primarily used pigments like titanium dioxide. Other additives like
wetting agents or dispersive agents can be added to the coating as well to promote desirable
properties or increased stability, otherwise diﬃcult to achieve. For example Wetting agents
promote wetting of pigments, and dispersive agents are used to prevent reagglomeration, thus
promoting stability and preventing smaller particle sizes to reagglomerate [18][19][2][20].
2.1.1
Agglomerates
When producing and storing pigments, the pigment form agglomerates which consists of indi-
vidual cohesive groups of primary pigments. The agglomerates are formed because of Brownian
motion and Van der Waals forces between the particles discussed in more detail in section 2.2.

The agglomerates are loosely adsorbed particles on edges and corners of the diﬀerent primary
particles, making them more loosely bound together due to less surface-surface interactions.
This makes them easier to disperse. Aggregates are more tightly bound particles where the
surfaces of the particles are facing each other, creating a large area of attraction, making them
harder to disperse. Agglomerates can also consist of aggregates bound loosely together [21].

In literature the distinction between agglomerates and aggregates is not very clear, and is some-

it comes to critical properties like hiding power, tinting strength, gloss, tint tone and more [11].
Therefore, it is preferable to optimize the amount and rate at which agglomerates are broken
down to primary pigments during the dispersion process [22].

2.2
Dispersion Process
In the coating industry the dispersion process is crucial process in making quality coatings.
Dispersion is conducted to break up agglomerates of pigment until a satisfactory particle size
distribution has been achieved.
Small particle sizes are directly correlated with important
physico-chemical properties of the ﬁnal product. Thus, if larger agglomerates are present in the
ﬁnal product, unsatisfactory dispersion has been conducted. Crucial physico-chemical proper-
ties like gloss, tinting strength, opacity, colour strength, hiding power, weather fastness, and the
stability are negatively aﬀected by large agglomerates. Hence, if the agglomerates of pigments
are not reduced to small uniform particles, the pigments are not fully utilized and weaken the
performance of the coating and the overall cost of the process [5][31].
For pigment agglomerates to be reduced in size or down to primary particles the cohesive forces
joining the primary pigments have to be overcome separating the agglomerate. The strength
of an agglomerate has a physical dimension of work per volume. The force needed to break the
agglomerate comes from the kinetic energy produced by shear stress introduced by dispersion
equipment like a high speed disk disperser, ball mill or plenty of other equipment [14]. The
forces of attraction that can be present in pigment agglomerations includes van der Waals forces
and hydrogen bondings depending on the chemical characteristics of the pigment [31][20][32].
The weak van der Waals forces are the ones most likely to occur, and can be divided into
three types, dispersion, polar and induction. Van der Waals forces are general and occurs for
all molecules polar as non-polar. They occur due to electrons moving around creating dipoles
in the molecule aﬀecting the neighbouring molecules. The induction forces occurs when polar
molecules are inﬂuencing non polar molecules, by the polar molecule inducing a dipole in the
non-polar molecule attracting the molecule. Lastly the polar forces are present when two polar
molecules are aﬀecting each other by permanent dipole-dipole interaction, which is signiﬁcant
for very polar molecules. The potential attractive energy provided by the diﬀerent types of van

2.2.1
Wetting
Wetting is deﬁned as the process where formation of a intimate contact surface between con-
tinuous phase and the discontinuous phase is established [31][20]. The wetting is established
by introducing the pigment to the vehicle. The vehicle will wet the pigments by displacing air
and other ’contaminants’ adsorbed to the surface of the pigments if the vehicle is able to wet
the pigment suﬃciently [32]. For good wetting the surface tension of the vehicle must be low
compared to that of the pigment. The rate of wetting is generally inversely proportional to
viscosity of the vehicle, and proportional to the surface tension of the vehicle. This means that
the rate of wetting and the degree of wetting conﬂicts in regards to surface tension. Though
good wetting is in most cases preferred since the rate of wetting can be altered by viscosity or
stirring rate [31][35]. After the initial wetting phase air trapped in the particles might remain.
During wetting a penetration of the pigments by the vehicle will increase the pressure inside the
capillaries, voids and pores, and assist breaking the molecule apart to release the air trapped
inside [31]. Furthermore, the presence of solvent instead of air will reduce the cohesive forces
explained in more detail in section 2.2.3.3. The pores of the pigments are penetrated due to
capillary forces. When the solvent is ’bridging’ between a suﬃciently narrow tube or void in
the pigment, the lower surface tension of the solvent (good wetting ability) and the adhesive
forces between pigment and solvent give rise to a pressure diﬀerential in the tube. The liquid
will equilibrate the pressure diﬀerential by rising in the capillary [31][36]. Subsequently to the
wetting process or in many cases simultaneously, the mechanical breakdown process begins.

2.2.2
Mechanical Breakdown
The process of breaking agglomerates apart has been named many things in literature, disrup-
tion, mechanical breakdown, deagglomeration, dispersion etc. [32][31][20][35][1]. This process
converts mechanical energy into applied shear stress on the pigments, which is the main driver
for the reduction in particle size. Depending on the process equipment used, at least two diﬀer-
ent types of breakdown mechanisms can occur. Smashing which can be accomplished either by
hammering, projectile impingement or smearing. Hammering, as the name suggests, hammers
the particles introducing breakdown of agglomerates. Particle impingement is accomplished by
ﬂinging particles against other particles, walls or other hard surfaces to accomplish breakdown
[32][31]. The other type of breakdown is smearing. Smearing occurs when the agglomerate
encounters a liquid ﬂow of diﬀerent velocity. The amount of shear stress is a function of the
ﬂow diﬀerence and viscosity of the liquid. This is why high viscosity is crucial. [32][31][20].

2.2.3
Stabilization
As particles steadily become smaller during the dispersion process, the attractive van der Waals
forces relative to volume increases. Hence, pigments will start to adhere to each other resulting
in the formation of an agglomerate. This means that the deagglomeration (breakdown) and
the reagglomeration (adsorption to each other) occur simultaneously if otherwise not stabilized,
which is unfavorable. Due to this mechanism, stabilization of the agglomerates created from
the dispersion, are required in all dispersion processes, to stabilize the formed smaller particles
[31][36]. To stabilize a dispersion diﬀerent main mechanisms can be utilized depending on the
dispersion type. To stabilize a dispersion the repulsive forces must be larger in magnitude than
the attractive forces. As main stabilizing mechanisms the repulsive electrostatic repulsion and
steric hindrance are utilized in mainly aqueous and non-aqueous coating dispersions respectively
[31].

Rheology is the technical science of ﬂow and deformation which, is of utmost importance to
the coatings industry at every step of the process. The rheology of a coating is a complex
phenomena connected to temperature, composition and in particular the shear stress/rate ap-
plied to the coating. The rheology of a coating, depending on the stage of the process, can
have tremendous role in success or failure of the coating. Ideally the coating can be applied
as a thin, homogeneous layer with a uniform thickness. To satisfy this, the rheology must be
perfect in this regard. If these properties is not achieved performance of the coating might be
severely reduced. Thus, a lot of time is used by the manufacturers working on the rheology of
the product, to ensure the right ﬂow and viscosity [45].

For rheology the viscosity is crucial. For dispersions of coatings the viscosity is a complex
parameter which changes according to shear stress, temperature, pH, vehicle composition etc.
[41][45][48]. The inﬂuence of the diﬀerent factors on viscosity will be explored in section 2.8.
For many ﬂuids like water, solvents the viscosity is in principle constant and not aﬀected by
changing shear stress and shear time. Such ﬂuids are named Newtonian ﬂuids. Though, this is
rarely the reality of coatings where changes hear stress and shear time dramatically change the
viscosity. Such ﬂuids are non-Newtonian ﬂuids. Most coatings are pseudoplastic non-Newtonian
ﬂuids. This means that most coating’s viscosity drops as shear rate increases. In this case the
coating is shear thinning (thixotropic). The pseudoplasticity is the result of having systems
with particle interaction, e.g. polymer solutions. The structural changes induced by shear rate
and stress decreases the viscosity. The structural changes can happen in three diﬀerent ways, by
aligning the molecules, by stretching the coiled molecule or by structurally breaking down the
ﬂocculates, resulting in less viscosity. Thus, viscosity is not a constant parameter, but highly
variable with respect to temperature, shear rate, shear stress, time and more. The rheology
during dispersion of pigments is highly important to the rate of dispersion, due to higher
shear rates being achievable at higher viscosities. Hence, higher viscosity during dispersion of
pigments is preferred to ensure eﬃcient dispersion [47][48].

Laser diﬀraction otherwise known as static light scattering and Fraunhofer diﬀraction is a fast

The measurements of the diﬀracted light are converted into a PSD by using the Fraunhofer or
Mie scattering theory. The Mie scattering theory correlates the scattered intensity of a particle
measured at diﬀerent angles of the particles to the size of the particle. The theory is built on
the fact that light diﬀracts of the perimeter of a particle. The angle of the diﬀraction relates
to the size of the particle. Large particles have a lower diﬀraction angle compared to smaller
particles. The Fraunhofer is a approximation of the Mie scattering theory, and is only valid for
particles 10 times larger than the incident wavelength, with a scattering angle of less than 30°
and over 20µm. However, unlike the Mie scattering theory, it does not require the refractive
index of the particle [69]. A major general assumption in both theories is that all the particles
are spherical. However, the shape of a particle has a large eﬀect on the diﬀraction angle, which

2.5.2
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is a very accurate way to measure particle sizes, though
very time intensive. The basic schematic visualization can be seen on Figure 16. It operates
using a electron gun which emits beam of energetic electrons with a energy in a range of 0.1-
30keV. The beam is accelerated to high energy and then focused using apertures, magnetic
lenses and/or electrostatic lenses and electromagnetic coils. The beam scans the specimen by
striking it in a closely spaced but discrete locations in a (x-y) pattern. In each one of the
locations, the interaction between the electron beam and specimen produces back scattered
electrons and secondary electrons. Back scattered electrons are beam electrons that are scat-
tered and deﬂected back by the electric ﬁeld of the atom in the specimen with a large fraction of
their energy intact. They are detected by the back scattered electron detector. The Secondary
electrons are electrons ejected by the atoms of the specimen being hit by the electron beam.
They have very low energies in the range 0-50 eV and are detected by the Secondary electron
detector. The detection for each location is digitalized to produce an image of the specimen.
The process requires vacuum to prevent unwanted elastic scattering. Although in modern SEM
the same results can be achieved by using a pressure chamber instead, thus not needing vacuum
[70][75].
Increasing the beam current will increase the size of the beam causing higher visibility of the
specimen, but it will also lower the resolution. It is important to ﬁnd the right balance between
visibility and resolution to get the best results [70]. Therefore, experience with the equipment
is key to getting good images.

2.7
Models Developed Related to Pigment Dispersion
Even though dispersion plays a huge role in the production of coatings, it is still more or less
looked upon as an art rather than a fundamental, scientiﬁcally underlain technical process.
This is due to the diﬃculty in accurately describing the wetting, disruption and stabilization
process and their interrelations. Even for simplest of processes it is a challenging task [59][14].
Despite this, a few mathematical models have been proposed trying to predict and describe
diﬀerent dispersion processes. The ones publicly available are shown in Figure 20.
Wahl’s et al. [59] model could adequately describe the data of oversized particles until mostly
indispensable or very slow dispersing particles were present. However it only described a single
pigment type (titanium dioxide) dispersion based on oversized particle sizes under the assump-
tion of a well mixed system. Which limits the applicability of the model. Wahl et al. proposes
the mechanism of deagglomeration to be due to the region near the periphery of the high
speed disperser disk. It is proposed that there is a well mixed region above the disk, and the
deagglomeration primarily happens at the periphery of the disk.
Lee’s et al. [38] model considered a two part-rupture mechanism to be the primary mechanism
of deagglomeration. After ﬁtting the data, the model performed adequately but not great,
which seems to invalidate the assumption that the rupture mechanism is the sole mechanism
when dispersing.
Lozano’s et al. [37] modelled calcium carbonate particles using a model, including both rupture
and erosion mechanisms with decent amount of parameters. The weighed contribution of each
mechanism was set to 50%. The model was able to predict the experimental data with a good
degree of accuracy. Thus, showing the importance of both the rupture and erosion mechanism.
Kretschmer et al. [39] developed a model by superimposing previous models that described
either the rupture, erosion and clustering of agglomerates independent of the others.
The
rupture mechanism was modeled by Manas-zloczowerit et al. [85] and showed the importance
of the shape of the agglomerate, how the critical shear stress was usually overestimated and
the kinetics of the rupture is independent on agglomerate size. The rupture and erosion was
both evaluated. Erosion takes place early from the surface at low shear rates. Whereas the
rupture was found to set in at at critical shear rate. Both mechanisms were found to contribute
to the deagglomeration when further increasing the shear rate.

able results. It was found that particles around the size of primary particles eroded oﬀthe
agglomerates in the rotor stator during dispersion. The particle size distribution compared
with experimental results perform well for some prediction and reasonable for others.
Kiil’s [1] model assumes batch mode with one type of pigment per dispersion, only spherical
particles, no reagglomeration, instantaneous wetting, agglomerate fragments are displaced to
smaller agglomerates according to the Weibull distribution, a minimum particle size diameter
where dispersion stops and that a linear erosion rate is the only dispersion mechanism. The
erosion constant used is a function of the type of pigment agglomerates, vehicle, dispersion
equipment considered, pigment concentration in the vehicle and the presence of dispersing
additives and moisture. The parameter is ﬁtted against experimental data, with reasonable
good results. The erosion accounts for most of the deagglomeation though it was discovered
that the rupture mechanism might be of inﬂuence in the initial stages of the dispersion process.
Winkler et al. [62] produced a model that calculates the probability of dispersion. The proba-
bility of dispersion is the probability of an agglomerate entering an active dispersion site and the
probability of the agglomerate encountering a shear stress large enough to break the primary
pigments physical bond forming the agglomerate. This model assumes that the active volume
where dispersion occurs is around the disk. The model is useful for empirical evaluation of the

2.8
Parameters Inﬂuencing Dispersion
The process of dispersion requires a large amount of mechanical power input over time. There-
fore, increasing eﬃciency of the dispersion process is paramount for cutting costs [1]. According
to Winkler et. al. [62], the eﬃciency can be improved by increasing probability of agglomerates
encountering shear stress high enough to cause de-agglomeration and decrease the amount of
re-agglomeration. This can be achieved by increasing shear rate/stress applied to the agglom-
erates, increasing the chance of the agglomerates encountering the shear stress, decreasing the
mechanical strength of the agglomerates, increasing exposure time and increasing the stability
of the particles produced [62][28].
2.8.1
Changing Shear stress Inﬂicted on Particles
Explained in section 2.3 and section 2.4 shear stress applied to the agglomerates is a function
of the ﬂow velocity diﬀerences in the viscosity of the vehicle. Parameters which change the
ﬂow velocity diﬀerence for high speed disk and bead mills are explained in section 2.4. For
thixotropic non water solvent based vehicles 5 parameters inﬂuences the viscosity: pigment,
binder, solvent, pH and temperature, as well as the shear rate and stress.
2.8.1.1
Pigments
It is well established that colloidal forces between the pigment particles control the rheology
of the vehicle. Increasing the amount of pigment will increase the overall steric stabilization
volume and decrease the amount of ”free” polymers. This will force the pigment particles closer
together increasing the ﬂow resistance. If too much pigment is added the amount of ”free” poly-
mers will go towards zero increasing the viscosity drastically [88][89]. Small pigment particles
have same steric stabilization layer thickness as larger pigment particles, due to the steric layer
being based oﬀthe size of the binder [36][42]. Therefore if the particle size is decreased the
overall steric stabilization volume will increase further increasing the ﬂow resistance and thus
the viscosity [90][91][89]. The increase in pigment volume and decrease in pigment size will also
Page 37 of 165
==PAGE:61==
2.8
Parameters Inﬂuencing Dispersion
3. February 2021
increase the shear thinning eﬀect. This is because high shear rate with high pigment loading in
laminar ﬂow will order the pigment particles decreasing the ﬂow resistance [92]. Additionally,
because smaller particles form a greater overall steric repulsion volume, a large shear thinning
eﬀect will occur when shear stress disrupt the ﬂocculation and stretch the polymers eﬀectively
decreasing their steric repulsion [93][94]. During the dispersion process the viscosity at zero
shear rate of the vehicles have shown to increase before it decreases. This is because, some
of the polymer is trapped inside the pigments during pigment loading which is then released
during de agglomeration increasing the amount of ”free” polymers and allows for better particle
packing [90].
2.8.1.2
Binder and solvent
The compatibility of the solvent and the binder inﬂuences the viscosity of the dispersion to a
high degree. The size and strength of the steric stabilization layer is heavily inﬂuenced by the
solvent and binder used. The viscosity of the binder dissolved in the solvent will also inﬂuence
the viscosity of the vehicle if a high amount of binder is used. If a low amount is used then
it is the viscosity of the solvent that inﬂuences the viscosity primarily [95][96]. For economic
reasons it is preferable to keep the amount of volatile compounds low, however it will increase
the viscosity [97]. High molecular weight of the binder also increases the viscosity of the vehicle
signiﬁcantly [96].
2.8.1.3
pH
Adding acid or base to the dispersion can be used to reach the isoelectric point of the pigment
particles. This has been reported to maximise the viscosity of pigment-water solutions without
binder. However for non-water solvent based coatings this is usually not used because the
primary stabilization comes from steric repulsion and low or high pH might decompose or alter
the adsorption properties of the polymers [98].
2.8.1.4
Temperature
Even a slight change in temperature will have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the viscosity. Therefore,
it is very important to know and control the temperature of the dispersion in the dispersion pro-
cess. Unfortunately because the vehicle is non-Newtonian it is diﬃcult to produce a predictive

==PAGE:62==

model for this behavior, which means experiments are usually required [48].
2.8.2
Changing probability of agglomerates encountering shear stress
The probability of agglomerates encountering shear stress can be changed by changing disk
height, disk type, peripheral disk speed and vehicle viscosity for high speed disk dispersers and
by changing bead size, density amount, shaft rpm and vehicle viscosity for bead mills. Similar
changes are possible for diﬀerent dispersion equipment but is not investigated further. The
consequences are explained in section 2.4.
2.8.3
Re-agglomeration
Re-agglomeration occurs when two particles interact with enough force to overcome the re-
pulsive forces. Like de-agglomeration this can also be seen as a probability. The probability
increases with the increase in pigment velocity, quantity and attractive force. This means the
dispersion process will ﬁnd a equilibrium when the average particle reach a size when the prob-
ability of de-agglomeration is the same as re-agglomeration [6][99]. At that point it is only
possible to lower the average particle size if the ﬂow velocity is lower, the area of the turbulent
zones are decreased and the steric repulsion layer stabilizing the particles is increased [100].

2.9
Conclusion to Literature Study
Coatings are widely used for their aesthetic and protective properties. The performance re-
quirements of coatings vary based on industry, but for most industries the pigment has an
important role in meeting those requirements. Multiple studies have reported the large inﬂu-
ence of pigment size, PSD and PVC on the ﬁnal performance of the coating product. The
dispersion process is therefore a crucial unexplored process in coating manufacturing. Disper-
sion occurs when a pigment agglomerate encounters a shear stress larger than the cohesive
forces of the agglomerate. However it quickly becomes very complicated when trying to predict
the wetting process, the strength of the agglomerates, the shear stress they encounter, the rate
of reagglomeration and the mechanism of deagglomeration. Furthermore it all changes over
time as the PSD of the pigment changes, which inﬂuences the viscosity (which inﬂuences the
dispersion rate) and strength of the now smaller agglomerates. To this day some models have
tried to explain the dispersion process, however most of them are not applicable for the coating
industry and do not predict PSD. Though they can be used to understand some of the mecha-
nisms during the dispersion to some degree. For high speed disk disperser and bead mills, the
magnitude of shear stress that the agglomerate encounter is predominantly depending on the
viscosity of the vehicle and the diﬀerence in ﬂow velocity between the agglomerate and vehicle.
The diﬀerence is how they transfer mechanical energy into ﬂow velocity diﬀerence. Changing
the viscosity can be done either by changing the temperature, the coating formulation i.e the
binder, solvent, additives and pigment or increasing shear stress as the coating is usually shear
thinning. The rate of reagglomeration i.e the stability of the dispersion, is a function of the
strength of the attractive van der Waals forces, the electrostatic and steric repulsion forces and
the frequency and velocity of which the pigment particles striking each other. At some point in
time during dispersion the process will encounter a equilibrium between deagglomeration and
reagglomeration and the particle size will stop decreasing. It is therefore crucial to maximize
the stability of the system to achieve low particle size. The eﬀect of the parameter changes on
the PSD during the dispersion of the pigment are not widely studied in the open literature.
Furthermore, the mechanisms involved during the dispersion are not well established and thus
the work performed in this thesis should provide a further understanding of the subject.

5.1.1
Obscuration Value
Initially a coating was produced to simply test how to analyze the coating using the LDA unit.
Thus, a well dispersed coating was taken to the LDA unit to be tested. The obscuration value
is a value for how much the laser light is obscured by particles when in the cell. Hence, the
more particles the higher obscuration value. The cell used for the LDA measurements was a
small cell (Hydro SV) of approximately 7 mL in volume. When adding sample to the SV cell
the obscuration value increased very fast. The small volume and high concentration of particles
in the coating made the sample transfer diﬃcult often leading to too high obscuration values
in the sample cell, and possibly to multiscattering. To minimize the amount of sample initially
the tip of a syringe dipped in the coating was used to transfer coating to the cell, which was
often was too much, leading to obscuration values of > 25% (we were aiming for 5-15 % to
prevent multiscattering. Thus, to better control the obscuration value, all samples were diluted
1:100 - 1:200 (weight based) with the binder solution used for the coating. Binder solution was
chosen to keep approximately the same ratio of solvent and binder as in the coating to prevent
binder and solvent shock eﬀects. Solvent shock eﬀects are non reversible agglomerations caused
by depleting the binder from the pigments when introduced to a solvent rich medium, thus

5.1.6
Considerations Regarding Hydro SV Cell
Only one experiment using acrylic binder was conducted due to these and previously found
inconsistencies. Other test experiments not presented in this report was conducted as well
yielding inconsistent results as well. It may have been possible to control the factors if the
measurements was conducted in a clean room and the cell glass was replaced with a glass type
resistant to scratches from titanium dioxide. Another disadvantage with using the LDA with the
hydro SV cell was the low maximum quantity of sample allowed during each measurement. This
was expected to be a major factor in the relatively large standard deviations when measuring
coatings which had not been well dispersed and contained few large particles which were not
always represented during the measurements. Thus, better sampling procedure should somehow
ensure the repeatability. Furthermore, the mechanism for adding samples in the hydro SV cell
was poorly designed for adding viscous liquids like acrylic binder solutions. The opening in
which the liquid was to be added, was too narrow for drops to be dropped eﬀectively into the
dispersant. The resulting eﬀect was that the sample drops ran by the wall of the cell into the
dispersant over long time leaving a trail of particles and binder sticking to the glass. This
eﬀectively complicated the measurements. Additionally poor cleaning, low volume of sample,
and dust easily settling on the glass, made the hydro SV cell very diﬃcult to use satisfactory.
Thus, for the hydro SV cell to have a chance of succeeding some considerations have been made
to accommodate those needs:
• LDA unit standing in a clean room to avoid dust and other contaminant to settle on the
glass of the cell and in the cell.
• Have better mixing in the cell to ensure complete mixing in the cell and no settling of
particles on the glass.
• Have stronger mixing for other dispersants to be used if need be.
• Have better equipment for transferring the sample into the cell without leaving a trail of
pigments on the glass.
Page 70 of 165
==PAGE:94==

3. February 2021
• Have harder glass in the cell, to prevent scratches during cleaning.
• Have a more eﬀective cleaning procedure to prevent contaminants from earlier samples.
The hydro SV cell was not capable of producing logical or repeatable results and thus were
abandoned. The medium volume (MV) cell was considered as an alternative, because of its
larger volume, and more standardized cleaning procedure, which was expected to yield easier
and better cleaning. Furthermore, the cell was considered easier to avoid dust settling on the
surface of the cell due to less interaction with the cell out of the LDA unit compared to the
SV cell. Another advantage of the MV cell was the stirring/mixing mechanism, which enables
more viscous liquids to be used as dispersant due to a centrifugal pump. Thus, testing of the
hydro MV cell were conducted and discussed in the next sections.

5.4.3
Improvements to the model
One way to improve the model, would be to implement reagglomeration into the population
balance, as it would increase the amount of larger agglomerates and decrease the amount of
smaller particles over time, thus lowering the overestimation of smaller particles and underesti-
mation of larger particles. The rate of reagglomeration could be assumed to increase with the
size of the agglomerate, just as the erosion rate, because a larger surface area would increase
the chance of encountering another agglomerate. However it would not be linear dependent, as
the total potential energy between to agglomerates would depend on their sizes. It would also
be dependent on the amount and size of the agglomerates in the mill base, which will inﬂuence
the chance of agglomerates striking each other. This could be simulated in the same way as
the erosion, just in the opposite direction. Additionally a rupture parameter could be included
into the model, to see if this additional mechanism could improve the initial dispersion stages
of the model.
Overall the one parameter erosion model was able to reasonably predict D[4:3] for low periph-
eral disk velocities. However it underestimated the size of the eroded fragments and the volume
density of the larger agglomerates. This became very apparent at higher peripheral disk ve-
locities and may require more parameters such as a re-agglomeration or rupture parameter to
better ﬁt the experimental data.

Conclusions
During testing of the hydro SV cell for the LDA, diﬀerent sources of error were possible operating
the hydro SV cell became apparent. The assumptions the LDA uses when calculating the PSDs
heavily inﬂuenced the results, introducing artiﬁcial peaks in the lower and higher particle sizes.
Furthermore, cleaning of the cell is a substantial and signiﬁcant part of the work when using the
hydro SV cell. The large variation in PSDs between samples are most likely due to the small
sample sizes in the cell and perhaps dust and other contaminants. The hydro SV cell is not
able to produce steady and repeatable results probably due to eventual contaminants, scratches
in the glass due to cleaning, the sample transfer process and the low cell volume in general.
The larger hydro MV cell was used as an alternative to the hydro SV. Xylene was found not
to be usable as a dispersant because of the inability of the LDA to detect large particles in the
mill base samples. Using acrylic binder solution (40 wt.% acrylic binder and 60 wt.% xylene)
enabled measurement of larger agglomerates, which also enabled tracking of the dispersion over
time. To decrease the viscosity and thus the analysis time the acrylic content was decreased
to 28 wt.%.
The change did not alter the PSDs achieved signiﬁcantly.
Experiments with
varying peripheral velocities shows that Hegman gauge is able to track the oversized particles
and distinguish the diﬀerent experiment types. Furthermore, the LDA seems to be able to
track a similar trend as the Hegman gauges. At lower peripheral velocities the dispersion was
slower than at higher velocities tracked by the LDA. Due to what appeared to be particles
settling in scratches in the glass imitating larger agglomerates, only ﬁve experiments (two at
6.3 m/s, two at 18.9 m/s and one at 12.6 m/s) were performed. Thus, more experiments needs
to be conducted for statistical certainty. However results suggest that the LDA is able to track
the transient PSDs of the experiments. Decent correlations could be found for the Hegman
gauge versus the LDA unit results regarding the largest particles using Dv(98) and Hegman
gauge results the correlation reached R2 = 0.68. Although, the Dv(98) values underestimated
Hegman gauge results by a fourth. Additionally, all the experiments shows that 80 % of the
volume consisted of 1 µm agglomerates or below initially before dispersing, according to the
LDA. This indicates, that the dispersion is largely a matter of dispersing only a relatively few
large agglomerates, which may be the reason why testing a small volume of sample showed
large STD. Comparing the results of D(4;3) with a similar study using a ball mill the same
Page 103 of 165

deagglomeration trend could be observed.
Comparing the Kiil erosion model to the experimental data, third rate order models were ﬁtted
for 6.3 m/s, 12.6 m/s and 18.9 m/s peripheral velocity respectively. The erosion model perfors
good at low peripheral velocities, especially for the cumulative results and D[4;3] results. This

However discrepancy between the frequency distribution simulation and the experimental data

This may indicate, that other mechanisms besides erosion such as rupture and re-agglomeration
might play a more signiﬁcant role at higher peripheral velocities. Using an optical microscope

the coating is very well dispersed. Only very similar sized particles would be seen, since the
magniﬁcation would make it impossible to make an image containing both a 100 µm and 1 µm
particle due to their massive size diﬀerence.

6.1
Future Work
The work conducted up until this point have not yet veriﬁed whether or not the LDA can be
used for particle size distribution measurements. Though, to some degree the LDA are able
to follow the same trend as the Hegman gauge. Thus, the LDA seems to be able to track
the transient particle size distribution to a degree. Ideally more experiments are needed to
signiﬁcantly tell if the LDA can be used to track the dispersion process. Further investigation
of pigment dispersion can continue into:
• Develop better cleaning procedure for the LDA, to prevent scratching of the cell glass.
• Develop better sampling procedure.
• More similar experiments to verify data.
• Change of parameters which might inﬂuence dispersion mechanisms.
• Experiments with other dispersion equipment to cross compare dispersion types.
• Test if the dispersion process diﬀers between diﬀerent pigments.
• Add a re-agglomeration or rupture parameter to the Kiil model.

A
Production of Coatings
To avoid having more variables than needed aﬀecting the dispersion process, all coatings were
produced similarly unless stated otherwise. A generic coating without any adjustments for
experimental parameters were chosen to be as in Table 13. For the materials used the PVC was
chosen to be 30 vol. % to get a high viscosity of the mill base. Acrylic, 3 was used as binder
system which consists of 40 wt. % acrylic binder and 60 wt. % xylene mixed. No additional
solvent was added to this solution to keep the viscosity high. For the dispersion and mixing of
materials a disk with a diameter of 4 cm was used. This was determined on the basis of the
diameter of the container. Producing of the diﬀerent coatings followed the procedure:
1. Weigh Xylene in container.
2. Put container between high speed disperser tightening clamps and tighten.
3. Install 4 cm disk on dispersing unit and lower until disk height has been met.
4. Install water cooling and start the water cooler at 15 °C.
5. Weigh acrylic binder.
6. Start rotation of disperser and slowly increase to 10 m/s during which acrylic binder is
added.
7. Wait for 10 min and stop rotation.
8. Weigh rheological agent (bentonite) and add to solution.
9. Start rotation and slowly increase to 15 m/s RPM.
10. Wait for 10 min and stop rotation.
11. Remove disk from solution by increasing the height of the disk.
12. Weigh titanium dioxide and add to solution.
13. Weigh dispersive agent and add to solution.
14. Mix by hand around 200 stirs.
Page 116 of 165
==PAGE:140==
3. February 2021
15. Note temperature of mill base and measure ﬁneness of grind x 3.
16. Take samples for LDA (3 samples).
17. Lower the disk to same position as initially.
18. Start rotation and increase quickly increase to speciﬁed dispersion speed 6.3, 12.6 or 18.9
m/s.
19. Wait for 1 min and stop rotation.
20. Note temperature of mill base and measure ﬁneness of grind x 3
21. Take samples for LDA (3 samples).
22. Redo step 18 to 21 dispersing for 5 min.
23. Redo step 18 to 21 dispersing for 20 min.
The dispersion velocity 12.6 m/s was chosen as being 2/3 of the dispersion velocity being 18.9
m/s. This was chosen to be able to more easily track the particle size distribution over time.
The container used for coating production had a cooling jacket, helping keeping the mill base
more cool than otherwise possible. Furthermore, the regulated cooling also ensured that when
starting the dispersions diﬀerent experiments had the same initial temperature every time.
