Abstract
Ecosystems and biodiversity worldwide are currently under threat by invasive species,
many of which have the ability to spread rapidly and cause significant damage to local
plant or wildlife. Locating and monitoring these species is essential in the efforts to com­
bat them, however, finding them via field surveys can be costly. One alternative to locate
these species is to use remote sensing. In this project, satellite images from PlanetScope,
along with field registrations of four different invasive species are examined: Beach Rose,
Giant Hogweed, Canada Goldenrod and Japanese Knotweed. Initially, a number of veg­
etation indices were analyzed in order to determine which index was most beneficial for
distinguishing the plant species from their immediate surroundings in the satellite images,
the two indices Red Chlorophyll Index (RCI) and Normalized Difference Vegetation Index
(NDVI) were determined to give the best performance. The remaining analysis focused
on Beach Rose, due to the amount of ground truth data. Three different machine learning
models were used to attempt to identify Beach Rose, two unsupervised models (K­means
and Gaussian Mixture Models) and one supervised (Random Forest). The unsupervised
models were unable to distinguish between Beach Rose and the surrounding vegetation
or sand, while the supervised model was able to predict Beach Rose fairly accurately, with
an accuracy of 86.81%, although with a tendency to overpredict the presence of Beach
Rose, due to the model mistaking bushes and hedges for Beach Rose.

Introduction
Invasive plant species are a significant threat to biodiversity and local ecosystems, and
can also have a huge impact on the usability of areas where the species is present. It
is estimated that invasive species cost 423 billion USD every year globally.[1] Invasive
species tend to spread rapidly and can be extremely resilient to control efforts, it is, there­
fore, vital to locate and monitor these species to effectively manage them.
Due to their resilience and rampant growth rate, many of these non­native species have
a negative impact on the biodiversity and ecosystems they inhabit.[2] A good example of
this is the most invasive plant species in Denmark, the Beach Rose. This species forms a
very dense root system enabling them to outcompete the local plant life. The presence of
the species also results in a significant decrease in the availability of sunlight. This lack of
sunlight furthermore leads to native species being shaded out of their habitat, resulting in
a significant decline in biodiversity. As the populations of local plant species dwindle, local
fauna also lose food sources leading to a decline in the diversity of the local wildlife.[3]
Biodiversity is essential for both nature and humans, it is estimated that over half of the
world’s GDP is dependent on nature[4], and with the changes caused by global warming,
its importance will only increase as the climate changes. The destruction and degradation
of ecosystems weaken nature’s ability to protect against the extreme weather caused
by global warming and furthermore undermine the natural regulation of greenhouse gas
emissions done by ecosystems.[5] The global loss of biodiversity is mainly caused by land
use changes, climate change, pollution, exploitation of natural resources, and invasive
species.[6]
In order to effectively combat these invasive species it is vital to locate and monitor them,
however, this endeavour can be costly. Many of these invasive species are widespread
and abundant, leading to field surveys being both expensive and time­consuming. Using
remote sensing could be a potential alternative that would enable mapping these species
over large areas. Some studies have already successfully mapped different plant species
quite accurately using satellite data.
In this project four invasive plant species are examined: Beach Rose, Giant Hogweed,
Canada Goldenrod, and Japanese Knotweed, which are all classified as invasive plant
species in Denmark. Aarhus municipality provided some field registrations of these spe­
cific plant species to use as ground truth data. Moreover, satellite images from Plan­
etScope were used to examine how these plant species can be distinguished from their
surroundings, as well as the input for several types of machine learning models, both un­
supervised and supervised, in order to determine whether it is possible to locate these
plant species using the PlanetScope data.

Beach Rose
Rosa Rugosa, commonly known as Beach Rose, is a plant native to North East Asia
and is currently the most widespread invasive plant species in Denmark. It is especially

prevalent in dry coastal habitats, where it often outcompetes the natural vegetation and is,
therefore, a significant threat to biodiversity and native plants. [3] Once it is established
the Beach rose is very capable of spreading using rhizomes (underground stems) and
can form large clusters of over 100 m2. [3] It is a perennial plant and flowers around June

2.1.2
Giant Hogweed
The plant Heracleum Mantegazzianum, commonly referred to as Giant Hogweed, origi­
nates from the Caucasus region and is undesirable in Danish nature. The plant sap is
poisonous and can cause skin inflammation upon contact with the skin. The plant can
displace other plants, and most animals will not live near the plant.[8] In Denmark Gi­
ant Hogweed tends to grow in moist habitats, such as marshes and meadows, or along
streams. However, it can also grow in drier areas, such as on grasslands or around in­
dustrial sites. Giant Hogweed is an umbrella plant that grows to about 2 to 5 meters in
height, with leaves that are up to 3 meters long.[8] It typically flowers during the second
or third year of life, from June to August. [8]
2.1.3
Canada Goldenrod
Solidago Canadensis, otherwise known as Canada Goldenrod[9], and Solidago Gigantea,
known as Giant Goldenrod[10] are invasive plant species originating from North America.
In the field registrations, these two species were both registered as Canada Goldenrod.
The two species look almost identical, the differences only visible upon close inspection,
and will thus be used interchangeably throughout the rest of this report. The Goldenrod
species tend to form dense populations, outcompeting or preventing native plants from
establishing themselves. They are very capable of reproducing and will spread rapidly
once settled. [11] Canada Goldenrod flowers from September to October [9] while Giant
Goldenrod flowers around August to October [10], both types are perennial plants, and
tend to thrive on fallow fields, forest clearings and along roads, but they are capable of
prospering in a wide variety of habitats. [9] [10]
2.1.4
Japanese Knotweed
Fallopia Japonica and Fallopia Sachalinensis, commonly known as Japanese Knotweed
and Giant Knotweed respectively [12] [13] are two closely related plant species originat­
ing from East Asia, and are both considered invasive in Denmark. The main distinc­
tion between the two is a difference in size, with Giant Knotweed obviously being larger
than Japanese Knotweed. A hybrid between the two also exists and is not uncommon.
[12] Japanese Knotweed is a very widespread invasive species in Denmark, whereas Gi­
ant Knotweed currently is not considered a significant threat, and control measures have
therefore been focused on Japanese Knotweed. [14] Both Japanese and Giant Knotweed
form tall and dense populations, wiping out other species due to lack of sunlight. Japanese
Knotweed flowers from September to October [12], while Giant Knotweed flowers around
October to November[13]. Both plants are perennial and tend to grow in slightly moist en­
vironments, in ruderal areas, or at forest edges. Japanese Knotweed is also commonly
found on roadsides.

2.1.5
Review of Related Literature and NIRAS project
Mapping plant species from satellite data has been the subject of numerous scientific
papers, as well as a project done by NIRAS for Aarhus Municipality.
In the paper Deep learning detects invasive plant species across complex landscapes us­
ing Worldview­2 and Planetscope satellite imagery by Lake et al. a type of invasive plant
species, called Leafy Spurge across a heterogeneous landscape in Minnesota, USA. The
species was detected using two types of satellite imagery, namely Worldview­2 and Plan­
etScope.[15] The WorldView­2 data has a higher spatial and spectral resolution compared
to the PlanetScope data, providing commercially available data with a panchromatic res­
olution of 0.41 meters and 8­band multi­spectral data with a resolution of 1.64 metres[16],
whereas the PlanetScope data has a resolution of 3 metres. Using convolutional neural
networks (CNNs) Leafy Spurge was detected with 96.1% accuracy using the WorldView­2
data, while the plant species could be detected with 89.9% accuracy using PlanetScope
data.[15] The CNN for the PlanetScope data was modified with a long short­term mem­
ory (LSTM) layer that utilizes phenology information from a time series of images. Using
the CNN­LSTM model the detection accuracy on the PlanetScope data was increased to
being on par with the WorldView­2 model with an accuracy of 96.3%.[15]
In the paper From UAV to PlanetScope: Upscaling fractional cover of an invasive species
Rosa rugosa written by Bergamo et al. the invasive plant species Rosa Rugosa (Beach
Rose) was mapped in different locations along the Estonian coastline.[17] This paper also
utilized data from PlanetScope combined with RGB images captured using an Unoccu­
pied Aerial Vehicle (UAV). A random forest algorithm was used in combination with 3D
canopy metrics and RGB­based vegetation indices to map Beach Rose thickets. Us­
ing this method a high accuracy was achieved with a sensitivity of 0.92 and specificity
of 0.96.[17] The Beach Rose presence/absence map was then used as training data to
predict the fractional cover using an Extreme Gradient Boosting algorithm (XGBoost) and
vegetation indices derived using PlanetScope data. Using the XGBoost algorithm a high
fractional cover prediction accuracies with a root mean square error (RMSE) of 0.11 and
coefficient of determination (R2) of 0.70.[17]
During the summer of 2023, NIRAS began a project mapping invasive plant species for
the Technical and Environmental Department of Aarhus municipality. The project entailed
mapping Beach Rose, Giant Hogweed, Canada Goldenrod and Japanese Knotweed for
the entire municipality. The goal on behalf of Aarhus municipality was to establish a base­
line for the spread of these species, for monitoring and control purposes. The project was
initiated in September of 2023, and as of April 2024 is still ongoing. The project utilizes a
time series of PlanetScope images, along with aerial orthophotos from two different time
stamps. In terms of methods, the project makes use of image segmentation and machine

learning for mapping possible locations of these species, using different methodologies
for each species depending on their characteristics. A semi­supervised K­means method
for image segmentation was applied to the orthophotos in order to identify the pixels in

the number of pixels that need to be analyzed. A Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN)

At this point in time (April 2024) the mapping of Beach Rose has been concluded, and the
mapping of Japanese Knotweed is in its final stages.

3
Data
This next section explores the data sources used for the project, such as the different
registrations of the invasive plant species, and how they were used to create rasters that
were used as ground truth data. As well as the other data sources used as input data for
further analysis.
3.1
Data Sources
3.1.1
Field Registrations
3.1.1.1
Aarhus Municipality
The ground truth data used for this project is primarily based on a number of field registra­
tions done by Aarhus Municipality gathered between 28/06/2023 and 27/08/2023, as part
of the mapping of invasive plant species done by NIRAS. The registrations were collected
using the web application Kollecto[18], which in this case enabled the user to register
the location of the occurrences of each plant species, including information about who
made the registration, and on which date, how large the cluster of plants was, images of
the plant, and additional notes. These registrations were completed by a small team of
biology students.
Aarhus municipality later provided an additional dataset consisting of registrations pro­
vided by citizens between 2015 and 2021. This dataset includes locations, their regis­
tration date, and whether control efforts were implemented. However, these registrations
were not verified by the municipality, so they should be used with caution.
As part of the NIRAS mapping of invasive plant species in Aarhus, another round of field
registrations was gathered, but this time focused specifically on Beach Rose. One of the
biology students who assisted with the field registrations from the summer of 2023, went
out with printed maps (using orthophotos as a background map) of some areas of interest
with large populations of beach rose. These registrations were completed on 19/2/2024.
This time the registrations were sketched on the provided maps for higher accuracy. It
was also noted whether some of the registered areas were made up of purely beach
rose, or if they were mixed with other plant species. For further analysis, only clusters
comprised of over 90% Beach Rose were kept and digitized using QGIS based on the
sketches provided.
3.1.1.2
GBIF
Another source of plant registrations used for the project is the Global Biodiversity Informa­
tion Facility (GBIF), which contains occurrence data for numerous invasive plant species,
including the four species examined in this project, and the data is open­access and cov­
ers most of the globe. The data within GBIF is supplied by its members, consisting of both
governments and institutions.[19] One such member is Arter.dk, which supplies its data to
GBIF.[20] Arter.dk is a database administered by The Danish Environmental Protection
Agency, the Natural History Museum of Denmark, the Natural History Museum of Aarhus,
and DanBIF (Danish Biodiversity Information Facility).[21] Citizens can create an account
on the site, and will then be able to report occurrences of plant and animal species. The
users can attach an image of the specimen, which species they have found and when
(both date and time), the exact location (either by using GPS location on their device,
or by picking out the location on a map), and any additional comments they might have.
Other users can then validate these occurrences to ensure the validity of the registrations
in the database.[22]

3.1.2
Google Satellite
Google Satellite map tiles are a dataset provided by Google and are primarily available for
use in Google Maps, but can also be imported as a XYZ tile layer in QGIS. Google Satellite
is a collection of orthophotography gathered using both satellite and airborne imagery.[23]
The source of the imagery used for Google Satellite depends on the location, and the
resolution therefore varies depending on the specific area. The imagery for some urban
areas is sourced from aerial photography, and will therefore have a higher resolution in
comparison to rural areas where the imagery is sourced from satellites. This is clear even
within the municipality of Aarhus, where the central areas have a high resolution and the
outskirts of the city have a much lower resolution. The imagery for these two areas also
seems to have been captured at different times, the imagery of central Aarhus is from late
in the summer (it was captured on 9/7/2023 according to Google Earth), and there is no
date provided for the imagery from the periphery, but we can assume that it is probably
from either fall or spring, due to the appearance of the vegetation in the imagery.
3.1.3
Summer Orthophoto
Another source that was used for polygon creation is the summer orthophoto from SDFI
(Styrelsen for Dataforsyning og Infrastruktur/The Danish Agency for Data Supply and In­
frastructure). The images were captured between 10/6/2023 and 12/6/2023[24] Although
Google Satellite was used as a reference when creating the majority of the polygons,
since it has the highest resolution, and was captured at a time when some plant species
were more visible, the summer orthophoto from SDFI could still be useful as a reference
in some cases. It could for instance be used to determine whether a cluster of Japanese
Knotweed has been cut down during the season since the appearance of a particular
cluster will change significantly between the two images if that is the case. The summer
orthophoto is also particularly useful for the areas of Aarhus where the imagery is sourced
from lower­resolution satellite imagery.
3.1.4
PlanetScope
PlanetScope is a satellite constellation comprised of approximately 130 satellites and is
operated by Planet. The constellation is capable of capturing the entire land surface of

A digital surface model (DSM) is a model that captures the surface of the Earth, including

created using airborne LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) data. A LiDAR sensor will

supplied and maintained by SDFI. In the current model, the data used for Aarhus is from
late March to mid­April in 2021. The plant species examined in this project would not
necessarily be visible on a DSM depending on its location and what the cluster of plants
is surrounded by, and most importantly if the plant is even visible at the time the data was

PlanetScope image alignment
During the NIRAS project for Aarhus municipality a significant misalignment between the

PCI Geomatica is a desktop software for geo­image processing, featuring tools for remote

After this initial alignment, the remaining PlanetScope images could be aligned to first
image in a similar way using GCPs that overlap between the images. For the remaining
PlanetScope images between 13 and 35 GCPs were used, aiming to reduce the residual
error as much as possible, resulting in the best possible alignment.

4.1
Vegetation Indices
Vegetation indices are a type of spectral transformation, that uses at least two bands and
are developed to enhance the contribution of vegetation characteristics. Since vegetation
indices are a simple transformation of the spectral bands, they enable reliable temporal
and spatial comparisons.[29]
4.1.1
NDVI
Normalized Difference Vegetation Index, also known as NDVI, is very commonly used
to assess the state of vegetation from sensor data. It is calculated using the following
formula:
NDVI = NIR −RED

The result is a measure of photosynthetic activity and ranges between ­1 and 1. Low
values indicate stressed vegetation, whereas high values indicate thriving and green veg­

4.1.2
GNDVI

to NDVI, but instead of the red band, it utilizes the green satellite band.
GNDVI = NIR −GREEN
NIR + GREEN
(4.2)
GNDVI is more sensitive to chlorophyll concentration in comparison to NDVI and is com­

4.1.3
EVI
The Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) is similar to NDVI and is used to quantify vegetation
greenness, however, EVI is more sensitive in areas of dense vegetation and furthermore
corrects for canopy background noise and some atmospheric conditions. EVI is calculated
based on the Red, Blue, and NIR bands, and can be calculated as follows:
EVI = G ·
NIR −RED

most commonly have the following values: G = 2.5, C1 = 6, C2 = 7.5 and L = 1. While
most other vegetation indices are sensitive to chlorophyll content, EVI is mainly respon­
sive to the canopy characteristics, such as the leaf area index (LAI), canopy architecture,
canopy type, and plant physiognomy.[29]

Classification Methods
4.2.1
K­means
K­means is an unsupervised machine­learning algorithm used for clustering. The algo­
rithm identifies groups in the data that are similar, called clusters. The K­means algorithm
stores the centroids used to define the clusters, and a point is then attributed to a particular
cluster if it is closer to that cluster’s centroid than any other centroid.[34]
The algorithm consists of three steps. In the first step, the algorithm decides on a set
of initial centroids using samples from the dataset. After this initialization, the algorithm
alternates between the remaining two steps. The second step assigns each point to its
nearest centroid, while the third step creates new centroids using the mean values of all
samples assigned to each of the previous centroids. The difference between the old and
new centroids is then computed, and these last two steps of the algorithm are then re­
peated until the difference is smaller than a certain threshold, which means that it repeats
until the centroids no longer move significantly.[35]
The algorithm strives to choose the centroids that minimise the inertia, or within­cluster
sum­of­squares criterion:

Where n is the number of samples x, c is the number of disjoint clusters that can each
be described by the mean µj of the samples within the cluster, otherwise known as the
centroids. The inertia is a measure of internal coherence within the clusters.[35]
One of the drawbacks of this method is that the inertia assumes that the clusters are
convex and isotropic, which might not actually be the case. It therefore doesn’t perform
well on clusters that are elongated or with irregular shapes.[35] This project utilizes a
K­means implementation from scikit­learn.

4.2.2
Gaussian Mixture Models
Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) is a type of unsupervised machine­learning model that,
for instance, can be used for clustering. It is a probabilistic model which assumes that
a mixture of a finite number of Gaussian distributions with unknown parameters can be
used to generate all data points.[36] GMM models learn by estimating the variance, mean
and weight coefficient for each cluster in the dataset. This process involves finding the
maximum likelihood that a specific data point is part of a certain cluster based on the
current variance, mean and weight coefficient of that cluster. They are usually solved us­
ing expectation­maximum (EM) algorithms, which involve estimating the class labels and
using them to update the weights and distribution parameters. This process is repeated
until convergence is reached on the optimal class labels, variance, mean, and weight
values.[37]
The Python library scikit­learn includes an implementation of the Gaussian Mixture Model,
which was used for this project. The function has a few different inputs that can be ad­
justed by the user, most importantly, the number of mixture components (how many clus­
ters the model will separate the data into), which is defined when calling the model.
In this project the input for the K­means and GMM includes the PlanetScope image from
the date when the invasive plant species are most easily distinguished from the back­
ground, furthermore, the DSM downscaled to the resolution of the PlanetScope image is
added, and lastly, two vegetation indices are calculated based of the PlanetScope data
and are added as extra bands in the data, at this point the data is a 3D array with the
dimensions: bands x height x width and it must be reshaped to a 2D array where each
row represents a pixel, and each column represents the spectral bands. The data is then
standardized using the StandardScaler from the preprocessing package in scikit­learn,
which standardizes the data by removing the mean and scaling to unit variance, and the
data is then passed to the model.[38] The model’s performance can then be measured
by comparing the resulting clusters with the ground truth data.
4.2.3
Random Forest
Random Forest is a type of supervised machine learning model, and it can be used for
both regression and classification tasks. The term supervised refers to the inclusion of
labelled target variables in the model.[39] In this case, the Random Forest algorithm from
scikit­learn is used.
The Random Forest algorithm is a model that consists of multiple decision trees. Decision
trees start with a basic question, in this case, it could be ”Does this pixel contain Beach
Rose?”, and from there, a series of questions can be asked, each question makes up
a decision node in the tree, which is used to split the data.[40] The decision trees aim
to find the best split to subset the data, typically using a Classification and Regression
Tree (CART) algorithm, and in the case of scikit­learn an optimized version of the CART
algorithm is used.[41] Decision trees are a type of ensemble learning method, meaning
that the predictions from the set of classifiers are aggregated to identify the most suit­
able result.[40] The most popular ensemble methods are bagging (otherwise known as
bootstrap aggregation) and boosting.

etation indices in terms of distinguishing all the plant species from their immediate sur­
roundings, with NDVI performing second best for Beach Rose, and GCI performing sec­
ond best for the remaining plant species, therefore RCI and NDVI were added to the data
used for the machine learning in the project. The performance of the vegetation indices
can very significantly depending on various factors, such as the spectral characteristics
of the specific plant species and the sensor type. So although these vegetation indices
performed best for this specific purpose this might not be the case for a different dataset,
or other plant species. The vegetation index with the worst performance was EVI, which
could be due to the index being more sensitive to canopy characteristics which is most
likely hard to capture from the satellite images due to their spatial resolution.
6.2
Unsupervised vs supervised models
When looking at section 5.2.1 and 5.2.2, it is obvious that the unsupervised machine
learning models were unable to accurately distinguish the clusters of Beach Rose from
their surroundings. This can be attributed to several different factors, as mentioned in
section 4.2.1 one of the weaknesses for the K­means model is that it assumes that the
clusters are convex and isotropic, causing the model to perform poorly on clusters that
have irregular shapes.[35] This could very well be the reason the K­means model’s sub
optimal performance, since the majority of the Beach Rose clusters have fairly irregular
shapes. Furthermore, the Gaussian Mixture Model assumes that the data is Gaussian
distributed[36], which, judging from the results, might not be the case for the data used in
this project.
Evidently the unsupervised machine learning models were not suited for the purpose of lo­
cating Beach Rose, however, switching to a supervised machine learning model improved
the results significantly. Although the model does seem to over predict the presences of
Beach Rose, it does predict the Beach Rose in the areas where the ground truth data
denotes a presence of Beach Rose, and the model also predicts the additional classes
quite accurately.

In the paper A Technical Review of Planet Smallsat Data: Practical Considerations for
Processing and Using PlanetScope Imagery by Frazier et al. several potential issues
with PlanetScope imagery are presented. One of the main issues stems from the way
the PlanetScope images are captured. Since the imagery is captured by a constellation
of smaller satellites instead of a single larger platform (such as Landsat) the images have
to be stitched together into mosaics for the analysis, which is also the case in this project
where the PlanetScope images for each date are comprised of three to four images in
order to cover all of Aarhus. Scenes that are adjacent to each other can have substan­
Mapping invasive plant species using satellite data
55
==PAGE:66==
tial variations in reflectance, for example, due to being captured by different platforms
and sensors. Each sensor in the PlanetScope constellation has its own unique relative
spectral response and calibration. The images that make up the mosaic can also have
variations due to changing illumination conditions or from being captured at different times
of day.[47]
Although all of the PlanetScope imagery undergoes geometric corrections by Planet, there
are still geometric variations that can pose a significant challenge when using the Plan­
etScope data. There can be substantial image­to­image pixel shifts in between the im­
ages, and the officially reported accuracies are stated as less than 10m root mean square
error at the 90th percentile according to Planet.[47] However, independent investigations
have noted horizontal offset positional errors from 4.8 m and even up to 19 m. Plan­
etScope data can clearly contain some considerable positional error, and additional geo­
metric processing is therefore vital if the images are used in conjunction with other images
or spatial data.[47] Due to the issues with alignment and potential reflectance differences
some additional pre­processing steps could be very beneficial for improving the results of
the model.
6.5
Suggested model improvements
One of the main problems with the Random Forest model, at least in terms of identifying
Beach Rose, is its tendency to mistake other types of bushes and shrubs or hedges for
Beach Rose. A potential improvement to the model could involve creating an additional
class in the y data containing bushes and hedges that are not Beach Rose. Creating
this additional class does, however, involve a little more due diligence when using the
Google Satellite images or summer orthophoto as a reference, since they can be hard to
distinguish from Beach Rose on the images. It is therefore vital when creating this dataset
to be absolutely certain that the polygons created only contain other types of shrubs, since
the model would be impacted very negatively if Beach Rose pixels are passed to the model
as not containing Beach Rose. Introducing this additional class could potentially aid in the
model’s ability to distinguish Beach Rose from other types of vegetation, which would be
a significant improvement.
Another option for improving the model could be to ensure more consistency within the
Beach Rose pixels used in the training data. Since the Beach Rose pixels were only
examined for each total AOI, it is likely that some of the training data could contain some
outliers that might affect the model performance negatively. Although, the area of the
AOIs were reduced to remove the pixels near the boundary between the AOI and its
surroundings, there could still be single pixels present within the AOIs that deviate from
the other pixels within the AOI. According to the confusion matrix for the latest iteration
of the model (figure 5.26) the model still erroneously predicts some sand pixels as Beach
Rose, this could be an indication that the training data might contain a few outliers that
causes the model to confuse sand and Beach Rose pixels. Examining the training data
more thoroughly on a pixel­to­pixel basis could therefore be another option for improving
the Random Forest model.

of the predictions very hard to verify. Assessing the performance of the model more ac­

accuracy of the model evaluation, which furthermore aids in the continued development,
due to the deeper understanding of the model and its performance.
Another option is to test and/or train the model on an entirely different area, for instance

Future development of the project could also include exploring other Machine Learning
models besides Random Forest for mapping Beach Rose. One possible option is to use

enable including smaller bushes of Beach Rose in the data, since Beach Rose populations
smaller than 3 by 3 meters couldn’t be included in the model when using the PlanetScope
data. Another compelling avenue could be to use other image sources besides satellite
images, for example aerial images, or images captured by an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle
(UAV), which could provide even higher resolutions.

Conclusion
This project examined four different invasive plant species, Beach Rose, Giant Hogweed,
Canada Goldenrod, and Japanese Knotweed within Aarhus municipality using satellite
images from PlanetScope. Field registrations of these plant species were collected by the
municipality, and they were used along with Google Satellite images and a summer 2023
orthophoto to create polygons containing populations of these plant species in order to
use them for further analysis. Some field registrations from GBIF were also used to add
some additional data. A number of different vegetation indices were examined: NDVI,
GNDVI, EVI, CVI, GCI, and RCI. They were inspected by comparing the VI values for the
polygons containing the plant species, with its immediate surroundings (created using a
buffer) to see which VI would create the largest difference, indicating that the plant species
is more easily distinguished from its surroundings. It was determined that RCI performed
best for all plant species, with NDVI performing second best for Beach Rose, and GCI
performing second best for the remaining species.
For further analysis a single plant species was picked for identification using machine
learning, Beach Rose was chosen due to this species having a significantly larger dataset
compared to all the other plant species. For all the machine learning models the vege­
tation indices RCI and NDVI were added as extra bands in the data, and a downscaled
version (made to fit the PlanetScope images) of the Digital Surface Model (DSM) was
also added as an extra band in the data. Two different unsupervised machine learning
models were examined, K­means and Gaussian Mixture Model, neither of which were
able to cluster Beach Rose accurately.
A supervised machine learning model, Random Forest, was also employed in the project.
A training and test area was created, for training and evaluating the model, respectively.
Training the model solely on Beach Rose pixels did not provide a good result, however,
after adding some additional classes (such as sand, grass, trees, etc.) to the ground
truth data, the predictions improved significantly. The final iteration of the Random Forest
model achieved an accuracy of 86.81%, this number could be slightly misleading however
since the ground truth data does not have complete coverage in the test area.
To finalize the project a prediction was made on all of Aarhus using the Random Forest
model. The model was able to predict all the instances of Beach Rose from the ground
truth data and was even able to predict some new presences of the species, which could
be confirmed by looking at images from Google Street View, and unused GBIF registra­
tions. It seems that there are some misalignment issues present, which could stem from
the PlanetScope data. Furthermore, the model does have a tendency to mistake bushes
and shrubs for Beach Rose, which is understandable since they are quite similar, and
future development of the model could for example include creating an additional class
for bushes and hedges to help the model distinguish between them.

Ecosystems and biodiversity worldwide are currently under threat by invasive species, many of which
have the ability to spread rapidly and cause significant damage to local plant or wildlife. Locating
and monitoring these species is essential in the efforts to combat them, however, finding them via
field surveys can be costly. One alternative to locate these species is to use remote sensing. In this
project, satellite images from PlanetScope, along with field registrations of four different invasive
species are examined: Beach Rose, Giant Hogweed, Canada Goldenrod and Japanese Knotweed.
Initially, a number of vegetation indices were analyzed in order to determine which index was most
beneficial for distinguishing the plant species from their immediate surroundings in the satellite im­
ages, the two indices Red Chlorophyll Index (RCI) and Normalized Difference Vegetation Index
(NDVI) were determined to give the best performance. The remaining analysis focused on Beach
Rose, due to the amount of ground truth data. Three different machine learning models were used
to attempt to identify Beach Rose, two unsupervised models (K­means and Gaussian Mixture Mod­
els) and one supervised (Random Forest). The unsupervised models were unable to distinguish
between Beach Rose and the surrounding vegetation or sand, while the supervised model was able
to predict Beach Rose fairly accurately, with an accuracy of 86.81%, although with a tendency to
overpredict the presence of Beach Rose, due to the model mistaking bushes and hedges for Beach
Rose.
