Abstract 
Among numerous liquid biofuels, biobutanol is considered an excellent fuel substitute as it can 
directly replace gasoline, without need for modifications to current combustion engines. However, 
the commercial production of biobutanol is based on pure cultures, which require expensive 
sterilization procedure and costly substrates. This limits the economic and environmental benefits 
of a sustainable biobutanol production.  Mixed microbial cultures (MMC) offer an alternative 
method to produce high-value chemicals and biofuels from low-value substrates and waste 
streams. Short chain carboxylic acids known as VFAs (e.g. butyrate) have been recognized as 
valuable precursors for production of biofuels. MMC can reduce VFAs to their corresponding 
alcohols using an electron donor. A MMC fermentation method for continuous production of 
biobutanol using hydrogen as electron donor and butyrate as the carbon source, is being developed 
under the GREENLOGIC project. In this thesis, the screening of best initial process conditions for 
pH, initial substrate concentration, additional carbon source (glucose or acetate) and hydrogen 
partial pressure (pH2) for butanol production was investigated. A design of experiment was 
elaborated with 3 conditions for pH (4.5, 5.5 and 6.5), 2 initial substrate concentrations (50 mM 
and 200 mM), 2 additional carbon sources (10 mM acetate, 10 mM glucose), and a fixed pH2 (1.5 
bar). This resulted in an initial set of 18 fermentations by anaerobic MMC, for a period of 10 days. 
The highest obtained butanol concentration (7.62 mM) was obtained at pH 5.5, 200 mM initial 
butyrate, no additional carbon source and 1.5 bar pH2. The best experiments were replicated for a 
thorough validation of optimum butanol production at pH 5.5. In order to determine additional 
strategies to improve butanol production, continuous daily replenishment of pH2 in the headspace 
was tested, showing no effect on butanol production. Across all experiments, butyrate was never 
fully consumed. It was hypothesized the initial blending and homogenization of the sludge could 
be a factor, and as such fermentation experiments with untreated sludge were performed. Other 
factors tested were the effect of addition of defined media and a lower initial pH2. A new maximum 
butanol concentration (14.50 mM) was obtained, showing a clear correlation with no pretreament 
of the sludge but no influence of media addition or initial pH2. These experiments also 
demonstrated acetate production, reaching concentrations over 90 mM. The presence of carbonate 
in the sludge and the high pH2 used most likely contributed to a previously inhibited step of  
homoacetogenesis, reinforcing the impact of the treatment step on the sludge.

1 Introduction   
The world’s energy demand is rapidly growing due to the global increase in population and 
industrialization. By 2035, it is expected that the energy consumption worldwide will further 
increase by 40 % [1]. Presently, 80 % of our current energy usage comes from the three main fossil 
fuel sources: crude oil, natural gas and coal [2]. 58 % is consumed by the transportation sector 
followed by 16.8 % from the chemical sector [3], [4]. However, fossil fuels are not considered 
sustainable and are associated with economic, ecologic and environmental concerns [5]. Moreover 
fossil fuels are a limited resource and at the current rate of consumption, it is estimated that crude 
oil, natural gas and coal reserves will last only for another 50, 60 and 120 years respectively [2]. 
Although, fossil fuels are an efficient energy source, the burning of them results in an increase of 
anthropogenic atmospheric concentrations of harmful greenhouse gasses (GHG) like carbon 
dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), among others, which leads to many 
environmental issues such as global warming, climate change, rise in sea level [6].  
To meet future demands for energy sources for the transportation sector, the development of 
sustainable, efficient and cost-effective biofuels produced from renewable resources has to 
increase. Biofuels produced from renewable resources such as starch, sugar crops, oil crops, 
agriculture residues, municipal solid waste (MSW) [7], have the potential to replace most fossil 
derived fuels used in the transportation sector. Biofuels are considered to be an environmental 
friendly energy source, since the utilization of biofuel reduce atmospheric GHG emissions from 
fossil fuels [8]. CO2 release from biofuel combustion can eventually be recaptured by bacteria and 
plants as it stems from organic material, hence not contributing to increase atmospheric GHG 
emissions [9]. Currently, the most utilized biofuels are bioethanol and biodiesel, developed as 
renewable alternatives for gasoline and diesel respectively [10]. However, bioethanol has some 
drawbacks, mostly due to its hygroscopic and corrosive features, which makes transportation by 
existing gasoline pipelines difficult. Instead, transportation is mainly carried out by trucks, 
inputting higher costs to its production.  
Biobutanol is an alternative to bioethanol, with great potential to be utilized as a direct replacement 
for gasoline. Biobutanol is more compatible with current fuel infrastructures due to its many 
superior advantages over bioethanol, which include being less corrosive, less hygroscopic and 
lower water solubility. Other competitive advantages include higher energy content, better ability 
to blend with gasoline and higher octane rating [6]. Furthermore, butanol can also be used as a

valuable feedstock for many industrial applications such as a solvent for paint, lacquer, detergent 
formulations, or cosmetics [11]. 
The commercial production of bio butanol is via the acetone-butanol-ethanol (ABE) fermentation, 
by pure cultures of anaerobic solvent-producing clostridium strains. However, this process suffers 
from some setbacks associated with low solvent yield (16-17 g/L) and productivity, due to butanol 
toxicity. Furthermore, the process suffers from high cost of feedstocks, which accounts 70 % of 
the total production cost, and high costs of downstream processes [12]. Significant efforts have 
been made to improve the efficiency of the ABE fermentation. Proposed strategies include 
developing new strains, use of less expensive carbon sources, process development and 
downstream processing (Figure 1) [13].

Most industrial fermentation processes today including the ABE fermentation, are usually 
performed by pure cultures, since sterile microbial single strains enables an easier control of the 
microbial growth and product formation. However, production of bulk chemicals with pure 
cultures is associated with high costs due to the requirement of sterilization process to prevent 
contamination, and the use of substrates which are generally more expensive [14]. Mixed microbial 
cultures (MMC) are an alternative to conventional pure cultures. MCC offer a high microbial 
diversity and are able to degrade and metabolize a variety of complex substrates. Furthermore, 
MMC are inexpensive, require no sterilization to prevent contaminations and allow continuous 
robust operations [15]. Steinbusch et al. (2008) [16] and Junicke et al. (2016) [17] reported the use

reduction of butyrate using MMC and hydrogen (H2) as electron donor. The major challenge in 
using MMC is the difficulties in predicting amount and composition of formed products. However, 
experimental investigation reports that specific operation condition can be chosen to manipulate 
the environmental conditions, which will give some control of the product spectrum [14]. 
2 Project outline 
At the PROSYS research center at the Technical University of Denmark the sustainable production 
of energy-rich biofuels and green chemical from low-value substrates and waste streams is being 
investigated under the GREENLOGIC project. A focus area of this project is the development of 
a MMC fermentation method for continuous production of biobutanol using hydrogen and butyrate 
as sole substrates. To get an optimum product yield, the process conditions for this method need 
to be studied and improved. Batch fermentations in 2 L bioreactors under selective conditions have 
already been conducted. However, at such scale only a few experiment can be successfully carried 
out in an adequate timeframe, due to the long running time for microbial adaption and growth. 
Therefore, it is necessary to screen the optimum process condition for butanol production using 
MMC at a smaller and more manageable scale. 
 
2.1 The objective 
The objective of this thesis is to determine the optimum process conditions for microbial growth 
and butanol production. More specifically, to screen for the best initial condition for anaerobic 
conversion of butyrate into butanol using a MMC, by designing an experimental campaign 
covering a suitable range of relevant process parameters. The screening will be conducted with 
experiments at small scale (0.2 - 0.4 L working volume). Relevant process parameters to be tested 
are hydrogen partial pressure (pH2), pH, substrate (butyrate) concentration and additional carbon 
source (acetic acid/glucose). The analysis of the experimental data will contribute to gain more 
insight on how these conditions affect the overall fermentation process.

3.1 Butanol  
3.1.1 Butanol properties  
Butanol (IUPAC name: butyl alcohol, butan-1-ol) is a four-carbon alcohol with the molecular 
formula C4H10O. Butanol is a colorless liquid, highly flammable and slightly hydrophobic with a 
strong alcoholic odor [18]. Butanol’s 4-carbon structure can be formed as either a linear-chain or 
a branched structure with different locations for the OH-group, which results in four isomeric 
forms: n-butanol, sec-butanol, iso-butanol and tert-butanol (Figure 2) [19].   
 
Figure 2: Molecular structure of the four butanol isomers [20]. 
N-butanol and sec-butanol are linear chained alcohols while isobutanol and tert-butanol have 
branched structures. The four isomeric forms have different properties and characteristic 
applications [18]. All isomers can be produced from fossil fuels by different methods. However, 
only the linear chain n-butanol can be produced biologically from biomass [19]. The important 
properties of n-butanol are summarized in Table 1.

3.1.2 Industrial application 
Butanol is a valuable chemical used in many industrial applications.  It is estimated that the global 
market of butanol will reach $247 billion by 2020, due to its biofuel potential [21].  Beside 
application as a biofuel, butanol is also used as a chemical feedstock in industries such as coating, 
cosmetic, fragrances, pharmaceutical, textile, and food. Approximately half of the annual 
production of butanol is used to produce butyl acrylate and methacrylate for the production of latex 
surface coatings, enamels and lacquers. Other important applications of butanol are as solvent in 
the production of paints, fats, vegetable oil; as an extractant in the production of antibiotics, 
hormones and vitamins; and as a swelling agent in the production garments from coated fabric and 
as additive in polishes and cleaners [18,22].   
3.1.3 Butanol as fuel 
Biobutanol is considered a superior biofuel on many aspects. It is the only biofuel capable of 
complete direct replacement of gasoline. Furthermore, its properties are far superior compared to 
other biofuels such as bioethanol and biomethanol (Table 2). Some properties that give butanol a 
competitive advantage are: (1) high energy density. Butanol’s energy density being 29.2 MJ/L is 
closer to gasoline (32 MJ/L), and contains about 30 % more energy than ethanol (21.2 MJ/L) [23]; 
(2) low volatility (saturation pressure), low vapor pressure, high flash point. Butanol has 13.9, 13.5 
and 6 times less volatility than methanol, gasoline and ethanol respectively. This characteristic 
along with low vapor pressure and high flash point, makes butanol much safer to use and easier to 
handle; (3) lower autoignition temperature. The lower autoignition temperature makes the engine 
easier to start in colder weathers; (4) viscosity. Butanol has higher viscosity than other fuels, which 
eliminates potential wear problems [22]; (5) good intersolubility. Without any engine 
modification, fuels with up to 15 % (volume) of ethanol can be combusted by standard car engines. 
With modification of the engine, the vehicles are able to utilize ethanol-gasoline blends, but only 
with blends up to 85 % ethanol [24]. Butanol can be blended at any ratio with gasoline (0 - 100%) 
without any engine modifications [22]; (6) less corrosive. Being less corrosive than ethanol, 
butanol can be distribuited through existing gasoline pipelines, whereas ethanol must be 
transported by truck or railway; (7) butanol is non-hydroscopic. Butanol-gasoline blends do not 
separate if water contamination occurs while being distributed or stored. This feature is also 
important in case of spilling, as it prevents contamination of soil and groundwater [22,24].

3.2 Production of biobutanol: The ABE fermentation 
3.2.1 History of ABE fermentation 
The ABE fermentation is one of the world’s oldest fermentation processes. The first butanol 
production through ABE fermentation was reported in 1862, when Louis Pasteur isolated a 
bacterium that could only grow in anaerobic condition. The bacterium could produce butyrate and 
butanol [22]. Years later, in 1912, Chaim Weizmann isolated a bacterium strain with strong 
acetone and butanol producing properties, using starch as a substrate. The strain was later named 
Clostridium acetobutylicum and has become the most studied and used bacterium strain for butanol 
production [10]. Weizmann took patent for his fermentation process in 1915 [25]. When World 
War I started, the demand for acetone rapidly increased as it was required for the production of 
smokeless gunpowder cordite. This stimulated the industrial development of the ABE fermentation 
in England, with the aim of producing acetone as the main product. Butanol was at that time an 
unused byproduct, which was stored for later efforts to salvage it. In this process, maize was the 
most promising substrate among various tested feedstock such as potatoes and chestnut. After 
World War I, the interest in butanol started to grow. As the automobile industry developed, the 
development of a quick-drying lacquer for the finish of car bodies was needed. Nitrocellulose 
lacquer was being developed for that purpose, and the demand for a proper solvent for the 
production of low viscosity nitrocellulose was required. Here, it was discovered that butanol and 
ester butyl acetate could be used as solvents. Soon the demand of butanol to serve as a solvent in 
==PAGE:20==
LITERATURE STUDY 
7 
 
different manufacturing processes increased, which led to installation of new fermentation plants 
in the British Empire, Canada and the USA. The Commercial Solvents Corp. installed a plant for 
solvent production, which by 1927 consisted of 96 fermenters with a separate volume of 50,000 
gallon, producing 100 tons of solvent per day, of which 60 tons were used for lacquer production 
[25]. When the Weizmann patent expired in 1935, numerous new clostridium strains were isolated. 
At least 18 different patents for new strains were released in the period of 1935-1941. These new 
strains had the ability to use different carbon sources, including molasses which increased the 
production of acetone and butanol in many of the large companies such as Commercial Solvents 
Corp., Publiker Industrial Inc., US. Industrial Chemicals Co., and the Western Condensing Co. 
[22]. When World War II began, the demand for acetone increased again due to the manufacturing 
of munitions. Therefore, industrial ABE fermentation started in countries such as Brazil, South 
Africa, Russia, Japan [18] and production capacity expanded in the U.S.A. and in the U.K. [25]. 
To meet the demanded capacity, novel equipment of alcohol fermenters and multiple-column 
continuous distillation units were installed. By 1945, the ABE fermentation was the main 
production route for butanol production. As World War II ended, the decline of the fermentation 
industry slowly began, with a drastic decrease in the 1950’s and 1960’s. The two main reasons for 
this decline were the rapidly growth of the petrochemical production route of butanol and the price 
increase of the feedstock molasses. Due to cheap oil prices the petrochemical route was favored 
[26]. Furthermore, the price of molasse increased drastically due to the large amounts used for 
cattle feed. These factors contributed to the closing of many industrial ABE fermentation 
production plants in South Africa, Brazil and Russia. In the 1970’s, due to the fossil fuel crisis, the 
interest in biofuels renewed. Many researchers worked on physiology optimization and 
development of new downstream processes for the ABE fermentation and facilities started to 
emerge again in Chin and Brazil. At that time, ethanol was considered the main alternative fuel. 
However, because of some limitation of ethanol as a fuel and the properties of butanol being more 
similar to gasoline, renewed interest in biobutanol production began in recent years. Therefore,  
many plants and companies are working on developing a more efficient and economical biobutanol 
process [22].

3.2.2 Metabolic pathway 
Fermentation using Clostridia strains was for a long time the only known route for biobutanol 
production. Clostridia are usually very anaerobic rod-shaped, spore-forming gram positive 
bacteria. Many different clostridia strains have been isolated throughout the years, but the four 
main butanol producing strain are C. acetobutylicum, C. beijerinckii, C. saccharobutylicum and C. 
saccharoperbutylacetonicum [22]. These bacteria can grow on a variety of carbon sources, such 
as monosaccharides (glucose, xylose), oligosaccharides (sucrose, lactose) and polysaccharides 
(starch, xylan). The fermentation process can be divided into two main phases: acidogenesis and 
solventogenesis [22].

conversions are marked with red [22]. 
The microorganism first degrades the carbohydrates into hexoses and pentoses by glycolysis to 
form pyruvate. The hexose sugars are metabolized via the Embden-Meyerhof pathway, and the 
pentose sugars are metabolized via the pentose phosphate pathway. The degradation of 1 mole 
==PAGE:22==
LITERATURE STUDY 
9 
 
hexose equals 2 mole of pyruvate with net formation of 2 mole ATP and NADH, while degradation 
of 3 mole pentose equals 5 mole pyruvate with 5 mole of ATP and NADH [27].  Then pyruvate is 
converted to acetyl-CoA, CO2 and reduced ferredoxin by the enzyme pyruvate 
ferredoxin:oxidoreductase (Pfor) in the presence of coenzyme A (CoA). A significant amount of 
H2 is also produced during the conversion by interplay of Pfor, reduced and oxidized ferredoxins 
and hydrogenases. Acetyl-CoA is condensed, reduced or dehydrated to CoA-derivatives 
(acetoacetyl-CoA, butyryl-CoA) [22]. Acetyl-CoA and CoA-derivatives are responsible for acid 
formation (acidogenesis) and/or solvent formation (solventogenesis). During the acidogenesis 
formation of acids such as acetate and butyrate occurs, and during solventogenesis the formation 
of acetone, butanol and ethanol. 
3.2.2.1 Acidogenesis  
During acidogenesis, acetate and butyrate are produced (typical ratio 1:2) [9]. Acetate is produced 
from acetyl-CoA, which is first converted to acetylphosphate by the enzyme phosptransacetylase 
(Pta), and then acetate kinase (Ack) converts acetylphosphate to acetate. In the production of 
butyrate, 2 molecules of acetyl-CoA are combined by a thiolase (Thl) to form acetoacetyl-CoA. 
Then, further conversion to 3-hydroxybutyryl-CoA, crotonyl-CoA and butyryl-CoA is done by b-
hydroxybutyryl-CoA dehydrogenase (Hbd), crotonase (Crt), and butyryl-CoA dehydrogenase 
(Bcd), respectively. Butyryl-CoA is converted to butyrate in a two-step conversion by 
phosphotransbutyrylase (Ptb) and butyrate kinase (Buk) [19].  Acidogenesis takes place under 
specific conditions such as pH above 5 and iron limitation [26]. The formation of acetate and 
butyrate from acetyl-CoA produces 4 and 3 mole of ATP, respectively. Furthermore, NADH 
consumption and formation during butyrate production is neutral, while acetate formation results 
in an increase of NADH levels [28].  
3.2.2.2 
Solventogenesis 
The production of acids during the acidogenesis phase results in a drastic decrease of pH of the 
culture. When extracellular pH is lower than 4.5, significant amounts of undissociated acids are 
present, which will diffuse into the cell cytoplasm. The anaerobic microorganism cannot therefore 
maintain a constant internal pH (~1 unit higher than external pH), which decreases in parallel to 
the external pH. External pH higher than internal pH is crucial for living cells, but when acids 
dissociates into the cytoplasm it causes the proton gradient over the cytoplasmic membrane to 
==PAGE:23==
LITERATURE STUDY 
10 
 
collapse, which results in cell death. As a response, the cellular metabolism shifts to the 
solventogenesis pathway where the previously produced acids (acetate, butyrate) are re-
assimilated to produce acetone, butanol and ethanol. This increases the external pH and allows 
longer cell survival. Final solvent concentration at end fermentation is usually 2% (v/v) [9,29] For 
solvent formation, the enzyme acetoacetyl-CoA:acyl-CoA transferase (CtfA/B) converts butyrate 
and acetate back to their respective CoA-derivative, butyryl-CoA and acetyl-CoA. In the case of 
acetone formation, acetyl-CoA is reduced to acetoacetate by CtfA/B.  Acetoacetate is then 
converted to acetone by the enzyme acetoacetate decarboxylase (Adc). Butyryl-CoA and acetyl-
CoA are converted respectively to butanol and ethanol by the same enzyme. Butyryl-CoA and 
acetyl-CoA are reduced respectively to butyraldehyde and acetaldehyde by aldehyde/alcohol 
dehydrogenase (AdhE). Furthermore, AdhE converts butyraldehyde and acetaldehyde to butanol 
and ethanol, respectively,  in the presence of NAD(P)H [22].   
 
3.2.3 Challenges of the ABE fermentation  
Presently, fermentation plants for production of butanol in operation worldwide are based on the 
traditional ABE fermentation. The carbon source used is either starch or sugars from molasses, 
and distillation is the preferred recovery method. While, ABE fermentation is currently more 
economically competitive than it was two decades ago, there are still a significant number of 
drawbacks that need to be improved to increase its efficiency and competiveness towards the 
synthetic butanol production route. The main drawbacks includes [26,30]: 
1. High substrate cost 
2. Low butanol concentration (< 13 g/L) due to inhibition during fermentation  
3. High cost of butanol recovery operation (distillation) 
3.2.3.1 High substrate cost 
Substrate cost is the highest expense in an ABE fermentation [31] and accounts for over 70 % of 
total production cost [32]. Based on an economical assessment from 2008, of an ABE fermentation 
plant in China using corn as substrate, the production of 1 ton of butanol requires 4 to 4.5 tons of 
corn [30]. In 2011, the price of corn in China reached 2000 yuan/ton ( US$ 290/ton), meaning 
one ton of butanol would require approximately $US 12000 for only substrate. In addition, 
producing one ton of butanol also requires 13~25 ton steam, 20~30 ton water and 700~1000 kWh 
electricity [30]. The prices of other traditional feedstocks such as molasse (US$ 100/ton) [33] have 
==PAGE:24==
LITERATURE STUDY 
11 
 
also grown rapidly throughout the years. Therefore, a lot of effort has been made in searching for 
cheaper renewable alternatives, and has become an essential requirement for improving the 
sustainability of the ABE fermentation. In recent years, lignocellulosic feedstocks have been 
suggested as a cheaper alternative due to the large quantities of sources such as MSW and 
agricultural wastes (e.g. wheat straw, rice straw [34]) that are available at low costs [35]. However, 
the main issue with lignocellulosic feedstocks is the difficulty to obtain fermentable monomeric 
sugars. Therefore, to gain access to cellulose, hemicellulose and other sugar compounds (e.g. 
galactose, arabinose), intense pretreatment and enzymatic hydrolysis are needed which are 
typically expensive processes. Furthermore, the pretreatment of lignocellulosic feedstocks tends 
to produce certain inhibitors such as lignin and furfural which, due to toxicity, slow the growth of 
the clostridium species and reduce the yield of butanol.  Current studies are therefore focused on  
alternative pretreatment methods, that form less inhibitors and efficiently improve the enzymatic 
hydrolysis [36,37]. Another suggested low-cost alternative is algae biomass due to its vast 
availability. Different studies have been conducted on various algal feedstock’s with clostridium 
species for the production of biobutanol [26,38,39]. However, this method is in the early stages of 
development and still needs to demonstrate economic feasibility for larger scale production [35].  
3.2.3.2 Low butanol yield  
Low butanol yield is mainly due to butanol toxicity towards microbes. The clostridium species 
hardly tolerate more than 2 % (v/v) butanol [40]. Compared to the other end products (acetone, 
ethanol) of ABE fermentation, butanol is the most toxic. This arises due to the hydrophobic nature 
of butanol, which allows it to dissolve into the lipid bilayer, which increases the membrane fluditity 
of the cell.  Consequently, essential nutrients and molecules are leaked into the media and 
important cellular functions such as maintenance of ion gradients, ATP production and trans-
membrane molecular transport are disrupted [41]. The intolerance of microbes towards butanol 
leads to a very low final solvent yield (<20 g/L), and butanol concentration usually don’t reach 
more than 13 g/L, before cell growth is inhibited [30]. Among suggested solutions to enhance 
butanol yield is to improve the butanol tolerance by construction of mutant clostridium strains or 
other microorganisms. An example of a genetically engineered solventogenic clostridium strain is 
C. beijerinckii, which is found to be superior over other mutant strains in terms of  butanol 
tolerance, butanol production, growth rate and butanol: acetone ratio [42]. Qureshi et al. (1999) 
==PAGE:25==
LITERATURE STUDY 
12 
 
[43] reported butanol concentration of 19.6 g/L produced from a mutant C. beijerinckii BA101 
strain. Other clostridium strain such as strain PJC4BK reached concentrations up to 17.64 g/L 
[29,44]. However, genetically-engineered strains of clostridium with higher butanol tolerance have 
only improved ABE concentration to a minor extent.  
3.2.3.3 High butanol recovery cost 
A major consequence of butanol toxicity is a low final butanol concentration (<13 g/L). To process 
the low butanol concentration, high-energy (70-100 MJ/kg butanol) [45] of the traditional 
distillation recovery process is required. This results in an increased recovery cost. According to 
economic assessments, if the final butanol concentration could be raised from 12 g/L to 19 g/L the 
recovery cost would decrease by half [30,46]. As butanol concentrations remain low, various in 
situ butanol recovery technologies that are less energy demanded and inexpensive have been 
investigated. These include pervaporation, gas stripping, liquid-liquid extraction and adsorption. 
Pervaporation includes separation of the ABE solvent by a selective membrane, where the 
permeate is removed as vapour by vacuum or sweep gas under low pressure [47]. This technique 
is considered to be energy efficient. Qureshi et al. (2005) [48] reported an energy requirement of 
13.8 MJ/kg-butanol for pervaporation. However, the potential of pervaporation at large-scale 
application is limited by the high cost of the membrane as well as the likelihood of fouling [49]. 
According to Qureshi et al. (2005) [48], liquid-liquid extraction and absorption is amongst the 
most energy efficient in-situ removal methods with requirement of 8.9 MJ/kg-butanol and 8.2 
MJ/kg-butanol, respectively. Liquid-liquid extraction involves the removal of butanol from the 
fermentation broth, by a water-insoluble organic extract (e.g. oleyl alcohol, decanol, biodiesel) 
[50]. However, this method is not as effective when compared to other in-situ removal methods, 
due to low concentrations of butanol in the extract. Furthermore, the solvents used are usually 
toxic for the microorganism [47]. In absorption, butanol is being absorbed into the surface of 
absorbents (e.g. silicalite) in a packed column and afterwards recovered by heat treatment or 
displacing of absorbents [50]. However, absorption is also associated with low biocompatibility of 
absorbents with cell culture as well as low butanol selectivity of absorbents. Lastly, gas stripping 
involves the sparging of recycled fermentation gasses (CO2 and H2) or other anaerobic gasses (e.g. 
O2-free N2 gas) through the fermentor, which separates the ABE solvents from the broth. The 
separated ABE solvents can be recovered in the condenser. Advantages of gas stripping includes 
==PAGE:26==
LITERATURE STUDY 
13 
 
simplicity, no requirement of expensive equipment and no fouling to the culture. However, the 
energy requirement is reported to be 21.9 MJ/kg-butanol [48], which is higher when compared 
with the reported energy requirements of other in-situ recovery methods. Furthermore, gas 
stripping is associated with low selectivity for butanol separation [47].   
All these methods have their respective advantages and disadvantages and depending on operating 
modes, different methods appear to be superior. According to Outram et al. (2017) [51], gas 
stripping and pervaporation are favored for batch fermentations, adsorption and gas stripping are 
favored in fed-batch fermentations and gas stripping are favored in continuous fermentations. 
However, among all these described techniques, several reviews consider pervaporation and gas 
stripping as the most promising in situ recovery technology for butanol fermentation [47,52,53]  
3.3 Anaerobic fermentation 
3.3.1 Mixed microbial cultures 
In nature, microorganisms coexist in communities. An example is soil, which contains a mixed 
microbial environment of protozoa, bacteria, fungi and algae. The dynamics in these communities 
are driven by multitude of interactions, which can be metabolic, regulatory and/or signal-based. 
The interaction between the different microbial groups can be positive or negative. Positive 
interaction can occur when one group benefits for the excreted metabolites of another, while 
negative interactions can occur from competition of nutrients, electron donors and acceptors, light 
or physical space or secretion of toxins or other inhibitory compounds. MMC is based on the same 
principles of high microbial diversity as mixed communities found in nature [54], and has arisen 
as an alternative to pure cultures. MMC contain two or more microorganisms which may be of one 
microbial group (e.g. all bacteria) or combination of different groups (e.g. bacteria and fungi) [55]. 
MMC can be artificially created which is typically applied in food fermentation [55] or based on 
natural/ecological selection. Activated sludge isolated from the effluent of an industrial waste 
treatment process can be used as a MMC in fermentation processes. The sludge typically comes 
from wastewater treatments plants, sewage plants or anaerobic digester plants and contains an 
undefined microbial consortia, is rich in nutrients and that is able to utilize a variety of organic 
matter. The microorganisms in the sludge have been submitted to transient conditions due to the 
irregular feeding and variations in electron acceptor presence, having the ability to continuously 
adapt to changes in nutrients [56].

3.3.2 Mixed microbial cultures vs. pure cultures 
Fermentation using MMC offers several advantages over pure cultures, which would enable a 
more sustainable and economical production of biofuels and/or bulk chemicals (e.g. volatile fatty 
acids). Some of these advantages include: (1) better substrate utilization. The (high) microbial 
diversity enables the culture to degrade and metabolize mixture of complex substrates such as 
lignocellulosic materials and organic waste. This is due to the complementary metabolic pathways 
existing amongst the species. The variety of different substrates capable of being metabolized 
could offer cheaper and/or new options for substrate choice [57]; (2) no required sterilization 
process. MMC require no expensive sterilization procedure to prevent contamination. Thus, 
working with MMC under unsterile conditions on industrial scale will significantly reduce the 
production costs [58]; (3) robust and continuous operation mode. For pure cultures, most 
fermentations are performed in batch or fed-batch mode, as the chances of contamination increases 
with longer incubation time. MMC are robust, which means the system has a high ability to protect 
its function against internal and external perturbations. This is linked to the high microbial 
diversity of MMCs [58]. An example is phage contamination, which is significantly reduced in 
MMCs compared to pure cultures [55]. There is a larger establishment of phage resistant strains, 
which means that if one strain is defeated, another phage resistant strain will take over and carry 
on the fermentation; (4) avoidance of un-wanted byproduct or intermediates. In some cases a 
specific group of the microbial species present in the MMC, can degrade the inhibiting 
intermediates or un-wanted byproducts [58].   
However, despite having its advantages over conventional pure culture fermentation, MMC 
fermentations do not find wide application at industrial scale, due to some limitations. The 
optimum control of the balance between the microorganisms involved is still not well understood 
and requires further research. In addition, even though contamination in MMC are unlikely to 
occur it is still necessary to do rigorous check during commercial process. However, due to the 
high number of species present, the detection and control of contamination is complex. Another 
large reason for MMC not being widely used in the industry is the difficulties of controlling the 
amount and composition of product formed [55]. This is also because the factors that determines 
the product spectrum are still not well understood. It is commonly suggested that the specific 
operation conditions can be chosen to manipulate the environmental conditions. This allows some 
control of the product spectrum as it affects the thermodynamics of the conversion processes, and

therefore the metabolic conversion of interest can be favored, which gives a more efficient grow 
of the desired catabolic product [15,57].    
3.3.3 Anaerobic conversions in mixed microbial culture fermentation  
Anaerobic conversion of organic substrates by MMCs is very complex. Different microorganisms 
present catalyze a number of reactions (hydrolysis, primary fermentation, secondary fermentation), 
that form synergistic and competitive interactions. This results in a complex microbial 
environment with a versatile metabolic capacity [15]. There are different possibilities of pathways 
and reactions that can occur depending on the type of microorganisms that dominates in the MMC. 
The product spectrum it strongly influenced by substrate choice and different operational 
parameters [14]. Figure 4 demonstrates possible conversions in MMC fermentation based on 
carbohydrates as substrate [59]. One of the most common commercially applied anaerobic 
fermentation process is anaerobic digestion (AD). The process is applied in waste management 
systems where organic waste are converted into biogas (CH4, CO2). MMC are the key in AD, in 
which the microbes execute complex functions simultaneously to degrade the organic matter into 
biogas and other products. The robustness of AD crucially depends on the syntrophic activity of 
microorganisms available, i.e., nutrient for one species are provided by products from other 
species. Functional redundancy also contributes to the robustness of AD. This means that the loss 
of one species is not detrimental to the performance of AD, as more than one specie performs the 
same function [60]. The process of AD can be divided into four steps: hydrolysis, acidogenesis, 
acetogenesis and methanogenesis [61]. The first step of reaction is hydrolysis of the organic 
material by excreted enzymes by the microbes to sugars, fatty acids and amino acids. In the 
following acidogenic step, the fermentative microbes assimilate these compounds and converts 
them to the H2, CO2, alcohols (e.g. ethanol) and VFAs (e.g. acetate, formate, butyrate) through 
primary fermentation reactions (Figure 4 (a,b)). The third stage of AD is acetogenesis in which 
acetogenic microbes converts VFAs and alcohols to acetate, H2, CO2, under relatively low H2 
partial pressure (<10 pa) through secondary fermentation reactions. Under higher partial pressure 
(>250 pa), the reaction of homoacetogenesis is favored in which CO2 and H2 is converted to acetate 
(Figure 4(c)) [62]. Finally, methanogenesis occurs with acetate being converted to CH4 
(aceticlastic methanogenesis) (Figure 4(f)) or H2/CO2 being converted to CH4 (hydrogenotrophic 
methanogenesis) (Figure 4(d)). Aceticlastic methanogenesis generates approximately 70 % CH4 
while hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis generates 30 % [63].

Anaerobic fermentation based on MMC could offer a sustainable production of different 
chemicals, by utilization of a wide range of waste or low value substrates. There are several 
potential application of MMC fermentations. These includes: (1) production of biodegradable 
polymers (e.g. 3-hydro-xyalkanoic acid (PHAs) and poly-3-hydroxybutytric acid (PHB). Johnson 
et al. (2009) [64] reported PFA contents up to 89 % of dry cell weight, during continuous 
fermentation with MMC; (2) hydrogen production. Shin et al. (2005) [65] reported H2 production 
from food waste by MMC, which resulted in maximum yield of 2.20 mol H2/mol hexose; (3) 
production of medium chain carboxylic acids (MCCA). VFA are key intermediates in AD and are 
utilized as substrate for further fermentation through secondary fermentation reactions. However, 
VFAs are valuable products themselves as they can be used as key intermediate in production of 
other chemicals in a separate fermentation process. This includes MCCAs that can be produced 
through an anaerobic pathway in MMCs known as chain elongation (Figure 4 (g)) [15]; (4) 
production of alcohols. VFAs can also be used as building blocks for the production of liquid 
biofuels such as bio butanol or bioethanol in a separate MMC fermentation process. The biological 
reduction of VFAs to the corresponding alcohol can occur in the presence of an electron donor 
(Figure 4 (e)) [59]. The reaction is catalyzed by at least two enzymes. The electron donors are 
required to increase the energy density of the product and furthermore, to remove oxygen in the 
form of water. Commonly used donors are formate, CO or H2 [66].

3.4.1 pH 
pH is a very important operational parameter that can contribute to control the metabolism, and 
has a direct effect on numerous microbial parameters such as growth rate, carbon source 
consumption and substrate conversion efficiency. The fraction of undissociated acids in the broth, 
which can infuse the cell membrane, is also influenced by the pH [57]. However, it is not very 
clear at what pH butanol production will be dominant. Some studies using pure cultures have 
demonstrated optimum butanol production with an initial pH’s of 6 to 8. Najeeb et al. (2015) [12] 
investigated optimum initial pH for maximum butanol production by a pure culture of C. 
acetobutylicum. The authors found that that an initial pH of 6-6.2 resulted in maximum production 
of 83.9 mM and 84.7 mM, respectively. Shen et al. (1999) also used pure culture of B. 
methylotrophicum to grow on CO at pH of 6, and obtained a butanol concentration of 4.45 mM. 
Other studies with pure cultures such as Phillips et al. (2015) [74] went as low as pH 4.5 and 
produced 14.7 mM of butanol. Liu et al. (2014a) [70] and Liu et al. (2014b) [71] used MMC 
fermentation for butanol production with syngas and had an initial pH of 7.5 and 8, respectively. 
However, the reaction of methanogenesis is favored at pH between 6 and 8.3 thus increasing the 
concentrations of byproducts in the broth [15]. Kim et al. (2014) [75] found that a pH below 5 
significantly inhibited the methanogenic activity in anaerobic processes. Therefore, a strategy to 
minimize the production of methanogenic products (CH4, CO2) is to operate the fermentation at 
a slight acidic pH between 4.5-5.5 [15,16]. This has been applied in some butanol producing 
studies such as Steinbusch et al. (2008) [16] and Perez et al. (2013) [72], which used an initial pH 
of 5 and 5.5 respectively.

3.4.2 Substrate concentration  
Substrate concentration is another important factor to consider. Rodriguez et al. (2006) [14] 
developed a thermodynamic model for anaerobic MMC fermentation, that predicts product 
formation from glucose with effects of pH, pH2 and substrate concentration. The authors reported 
that substrate concentration would have an effect on product spectrum, with higher substrate 
concentrations resulting in higher product concentrations. However, if substrate concentration 
becomes too high it might inhibit the growth of the microorganisms [76,77]. Steinbusch et al. 
(2008) [16], Junicke et al. (2016) [17] and Liu et al. (2014a) used substrates concentrations of 50 
mM, 20 mM and 17 mM butyrate, respectively. No studies with an initial butyrate concentration 
higher than 50 mM for butanol production using MMC has been found in the literature. Previous 
studies using pure cultures have reported that adding butyrate to the culture enhanced butanol 
production [12,78,79]. Perez et al. (2013) used a higher initial butyrate concentration of 150 mM 
and obtained 13.3 mM butanol with syngas fermentation of a pure culture with a mutant C. 
ljungdahlii.  Holt et al. (1984) [80] used a pure culture of C. beijerinckii NCIMB 8052 with the 
supplement of 10 mM butyrate. The results showed that the supplementation contributed to initiate 
early solvent production, but no increase in final butanol concentration was observed. However, 
Wang et al. (2013) [79] found that the addition of 40 mM to the same pure culture of C. beijerinckii 
NCIMB 8052  led to a 31 % increase in butanol titer. Lee et al. (2008) [78] also investigated 
butyrate supplement in different concentrations of 9, 18 and 36 mM and also found that the higher 
concentration of 36 mM enhanced the butanol production significantly. In addition, the authors 
found that addition of 36 mM acetate increased concentrations as well. Al-shorgani et al. (2012) 
[68] studied the effect of glucose with various concentrations of butyrate to produce butanol in a 
pure culture of C. saccharoperbutylacetonicum N1-4 [68]. The study reported that butanol 
production from butyrate in the absence of glucose, resulted in lower concentrations when 
compared to the fermentations with glucose present. Moreover, it was found that specific 
combinations of glucose and butyrate are responsible to influence optimum butanol production. In 
this case, the combination of 5 and 10 g/L glucose with 5 g/L butyrate and combination of 15 and 
20 g/l glucose with 10 g/L butyrate demonstrated increased butanol concentrations.

3.4.3 Hydrogen partial pressure 
The pH2 is an important parameter that affects the thermodynamic feasibility of certain reactions, 
and thereby can contribute to the limitation of competitive reactions. Furthermore, pH2 is used as 
a central strategy to ensure the reductive conditions in the fermentation and also influences the 
product spectrum [14]. Most fermentation studies produces butanol from syngas [70–72], and so 
far only Steinbusch et al. (2008) [16] and Junicke et al. (2016) [17], have been found in the 
literature to use H2 as electron donor for butanol formation. The microorganisms present in the 
culture converts 1 mole of butyrate with 2 moles of H2 [16]. Perez et al. (2013) [72] report that the 
use of H2 instead of syngas is likely to improve the conversion of butyrate to butanol, as formation 
of by-products such as acetate and ethanol could be prevented. The authors found in the study that 
H2 theoretically can provide the required stoichiometry of 1 ATP and 2 NADH2 to activate and 
reduce n‐butyrate to n‐butanol. 
H2 has an effect on the oxidation state of the electron carriers in the system. The full mechanism 
on how pH2 affects the reduction of VFA is not yet fully understood. In most thermodynamic MMC 
fermentation models it is assumed that NADH/NAD+ are involved as electron carrier for the 
dissolved H2 [14]. However, experimental work so far could not find a direct connection between 
the two. This might point towards a more complex mechanism with alternative electron carrier 
such as ferrodoxin [15]. In thermodynamic models for MMC that consider NAD+ form as the 
electron carrier, it has been reported that a high pH2 (~1.5 bar) will result in a high NADH/NAD+ 
ratio, making oxidative processes less thermodynamically favorable and under some conditions 
not feasible [14]. This means that a high pH2 (~1.5 bar) will reduce VFAs to their corresponding 
alcohols [15]. Furthermore, maintaining a high pH2 (~1.5 bar) is also essential for reduced product 
formation, as it has been observed that with decreasing pH2 the reduced product was oxidized. 
Steinbusch et al. (2008) [16] and Junicke et al. (2016) [17] used values of 0.5 bar and 0.0012 bar 
and butanol production at 3.66 mM and 2 mM was reported, respectively. Obtaining higher butanol 
concentration may be possible if pH2 is ~1.5 bar.

5.1.1 Effect of pH on butanol concentrations 
Figure 8 and Figure 10 show the butanol concentration profiles of the experiments. As expected, 
the pH of the medium strongly influence the fermentation.  
The experiments with an initial pH of 4.5 resulted in residue levels of butanol or no butanol 
production (Experiment S, X, O, Q). Out of the six experiments with an initial pH of 4.5, only two, 
experiments M and U, demonstrated butanol formation in concentrations of 0.44 mM and 0.29 
mM, respectively. Steinbusch et al. (2008) [16] reported that a reduction to alcohol is 
thermodynamically more feasible at lower pH and elevated pH2. However, a pH of 4.36 and a pH2 
of 1 bar results in the minimum reaction energy of -20 KJ required for maintenance and growth. 
Although a too-low pH would not contribute to further enhance the production of butanol, it was 
still interesting to investigate butanol production close to the thermodynamic boundary. Moreover, 
pH below 5 inhibits the formation of by-products (e.g., methanogenesis) in MMC [15,16]. The 
pH2 was elevated to 1.5 bar. In doing so, the conversion of butyrate to butanol would be 
thermodynamically more feasible (Figure 7).  
 
Figure 7: The effect of pH and pH2 on ∆𝐺′[82]. 
As expected, the results revealed that when operating at a pH of 4.5 and 1.5 bar pH2, butanol 
production was minimum. Butanol production was achieved in four experiments, P, N, Y and T, 
out of the six experiments with an initial pH of 5.5. Experiment P showed the best butanol

5.1.2 Effect of substrate on butanol concentrations 
Several studies [68,78–80] have shown the influence of butyrate concentration on butanol 
production. For instance, Wang et al. (2013) [79] found that the addition of 40 mM to a pure culture 
of C. beijerinckii NCIMB 8052  led to a 31% increase in butanol concentrations. Lee et al. (2008) 
[78] also investigated butyrate supplement in different concentrations of 9, 18 and 36 mM, and 
found that the higher concentration of 36 mM enhanced the butanol production.. In the syngas 
fermentation experiment with MMC conducted by Liu et al. (2014a) [70], an initial butyrate 
concentration of 17 mM resulted in a butanol concentration of 13.3 mM, while the experiments 
conducted by  Steinbusch et al. (2008) [16] with MMC using H2 as electron donor obtained a 
butanol concentration of  3.66 mM  with an initial butyrate concentration of 50 mM. In these 
experiments, the initial butyrate concentration was chosen as 50, 100 (control point) and 200 mM. 
Figure 12 and Figure 13 present the butyrate concentration profiles for the initial screening. 
Overall, butanol concentration was the highest with an increased initial butyrate concentration. 
This trend was observed in experiments P, T, Y (Figure 12), J and L (Figure 13), which had an 
initial butyrate concentration of 200 mM. When comparing experiments with a 200 mM butyrate 
concentration with their equivalent experiments with 50 mM butyrate, it resulted in 1.07-10.64 
fold increase in butanol concentrations. It was therefore found that 200 mM is the most favorable 
concentration for butyrate (Table 12). However, despite having butanol formation, butyrate was 
never fully consumed in the majority of the experiments. The same was found for the control point 
fermentations CPA, CPB and CPC. This can also be reflected by the biomass concentrations, as it 
appears that a decreasing trend is occurring in all experiments properly due to no butyrate being 
consumed for growth (Appendix B2: Figure 47 and Figure 48). The ability of the culture to 
consume butyrate was only confirmed in experiment R (Appendix B1: Figure 36) and N (Figure 
12). Even so, no butanol was detected in experiment R, and maximum butanol concentration in 
experiment N only reached 1.09 mM.

5.1.3 Improvement of butanol concentrations 
Once it was confirmed that butanol can be produced and maintained in experiments T and Y, an 
additional 10-fold of media was added to experiment Y, and the pressure of experiment T was 
rebuilt with 1.5 bar of H2. This was conducted at the tenth day of fermentation to evaluate if the 
addition of media or continuous replenishment of H2 had any influence on the butanol production. 
The fermentation for experiments Y and T continued up to 14 days. The results showed a 10.2% 
decrease in butanol concentration for experiment Y from Day 10 to Day 14. This result indicates 
that addition of media does not contribute to enhanced butanol production. However, experiment 
T demonstrated an improved butanol production, attaining a 12% increase in butanol 
concentration. This result suggests that a continuous rebuild of H2 might contribute to increasing 
butanol concentrations. Therefore, a new set of experiments was performed to evaluate the 
continuous effect of continuous replenishment of H2 (discussed in section 5.2).    
Furthermore, it should be noted that the use of an older sludge might have caused lower butanol 
concentrations for the experiments prepared in the 2nd set (experiment N, T and Y). This 
assumption has been made when comparing the results from the three control point fermentation 
of each set (CPA=1st set, CPB=2nd set, CPC=3rd). The results showed that butanol concentration 
of CPB (0.44 mM) is 78.2 % and 63.5 % lower when compared to CPA (1.93 mM) and CPC (1.15 
mM), respectively (Figure 8 and Figure 10). The inoculum that was used in the 1st set of 
fermentation (CPA) was the same used in the 2nd set (CPB). However, there was an interval of 15 
days between the preparation of the 1st set and 2nd set, meaning that the sludge used in the 2nd set 
in the fermentation was 15 days old. This might be a main reason as to why the butanol 
concentrations for CPB were significantly lower than CPA and CPC. In the 3rd set of fermentation, 
fresh inoculum was prepared and used, and the results show that the butanol concentration of CPC 
is more comparable with CPA. Therefore, the experiments should be prepared with fresh sludge.

consume butyrate. However, it has been demonstrated that the biomass is actually capable of 
consuming butyrate. In the reactor where the sludge comes from, the inlet has 2 g/l butyrate and 1 
g/L acetate. At the effluent, there is 0 g/L of both, meaning that the sludge is consuming both acids. 
The apparent metabolic inactivity of the sludge used in this study might be due to a few reasons.

experimental conditions, or possibly the pretreatment might be affecting the activity of the 
microbes to some degree. Compared to the conditions tested, the rector that the sludge comes from 
has a higher pH and no H2 atmosphere. The pH in the fermentations might be too acidic for the 
cells to be able to cope, as there are microbes that are quite sensitive to these growth conditions. 
The pure H2 atmosphere might affect the conversion. Although thermodynamically, the reaction

butanol by another strain during their experiments of syngas fermentation in MMC.  
Concurrently, with the oscillating concentrations of butyrate, an increase is observed, which

might occur during the fermentation that could cause the increase in butyrate concentration. The 
high butyrate concentrations might be linked to the reactions of lactate oxidation to butyrate (Eq.

The presence of these acids in the media induces higher use of the metabolic pathway that 
generates butyrate [87]. High concentrations of acetate have been detected in these experiments. 
In addition, lactate formation was detected on the second day, but its concentration started to 
decrease and production stopped at Day 6 (Figure 21). Nevertheless, a significant increase in 
butyrate concentration in the experiments was observed between Day 2 and Day 6.

Figure 22 shows a significant increase of acetate produced in the experiments, which continued 
throughout the fermentation to a maximum of 147.98 mM in experiment B2 and a minimum of 
95.98 mM in experiment B4. The acetate production during the fermentation might be caused by 
either acetogenesis or homoacetogenesis. Acetogenesis is the production of acetate by 
heterotrophic microbes from oxidation of butyrate (Eq. (6) in Table 17). At pH 7, the standard 
Gibbs free (∆Gr
°′) energy for the reaction is quite positive. However, in order to have a reaction, 
∆Gr
°′ needs to be negative, which can be achieved with a very low partial pressure of H2 (<5.89∙10-
5 bar under standard condition) [89]. In this case, the experiments had very high partial pressures 
(0.2 bar and 1.5 bar), meaning that the reaction is not favored and therefore acetogenesis 
conversion usually does not happen. Homoacetogenesis is acetate production by autotrophic 
microorganisms that use H2 to reduce CO2 to acetate (Eq. (5) in Table 17).  Homoacetogenesis is 
favored at high H2 partial pressure (>0.005 bar) [83]. As we do not have any CO2 added in the 
headspace, the carbonate would have to come from the sludge. In the rector where the sludge 
comes from, precipitation of the carbonates occurs due to the pressure inside the reactor. In order 
to verify that there is carbonate in the sludge, the solid phase and liquid phase of the untreated 
sludge was tested. The liquid phase had 100.62 mM carbonate while the solid phase had 612.81 
mM carbonate meaning a significant amount is present in both phases. Therefore, 
homoacetogenesis conversion is a favored reaction in these conditions.   
In the experiments of optimal conditions for butanol production and the continuous replenishment 
of H2, acetate formation also occurred, but at lower ranges (max. 48 mM). This can again be 
explain by the lack of pretreatment to the sludge. When blended, carbonate present in the solid 
phase is being solubilized, as it is freer in this environment. Hence, the carbonate content in the 
sludge is reduced.

6 Conclusion   
In this project, the aim was to screen for optimum initial conditions for butanol production. The 
effect of pH in values of 4.5, 5.5 and 6.5 was evaluated. The results showed that operating at a pH 
of 4.5, close to the thermodynamic boundary, resulted in minimum butanol production or no 
butanol production at all. All the experiments with pH 6.5 demonstrated butanol production with 
maximum concentrations of 5.0, 4.16 and 4.50 mM in experiment J, K, L, respectively. However, 
it was found that a higher amount of by-products was formed in these experiments. Operating at a 
pH of 5.5 demonstrated a 7.62 mM concentration of butanol in experiment P. Furthermore, 
production of byproducts was at its lowest. When investigating the effect of initial substrate 
concentration, it was found that higher butyrate concentrations led to higher butanol titers.  The 
experiments with a 200 mM butyrate concentration resulted in 1.07-10.64 fold increase in butanol 
concentrations, when compared with their equivalent experiments with 50 mM butyrate. 
Furthermore, it was found that adding an additional carbon source in the form of acetate did not 
contribute to enhance butanol concentrations. This was demonstrated in experiment I and T, which 
had a 1.51-fold and 2.93-fold decrease in butanol concentrations, when compared to their 
equivalent experiments with no acetate. In addition acetate was not being consumed by the culture. 
However, when adding glucose as the additional carbon source, it was consumed and a significant 
increase in butanol production occurred in experiment K and L. When comparing experiments K 
and L to their equivalent experiments with no glucose, it resulted in a 5.86-fold and 2.87-fold 
increase in butanol concentration, respectively. Yet, butanol concentrations in these experiments 
were still under 5 mM and the experimental results obtained showed that higher butanol 
concentrations could occur with butyrate as the sole substrate (experiment P, 7.62 mM). Therefore, 
it was determined that the optimum butanol production conditions are pH of 5.5, 200 mM substrate 
concentration, no additional carbon source and 1.5 bar pH2. In order to determine additional 
strategies to improve butanol productions, a new set of experiments was performed, in which the 
effect of continuous replenishment of H2 was tested. This set included duplicates of the best 
butanol-producing experiment with pH of 5.5 (Experiment N, P, T, Y). The results showed the 
concentration of butanol in experiment N1 and N2 were considerably enhanced with almost 4.7-
fold increase, when compared to experiment N (1.09 mM) from the previous fermentations. Yet, 
the other experiments when compared to their duplicates from previous sets decreased by 89-97 
% for experiment P1 and P2, 73-75 % for experiment T1 and T2 and  47-50 % for experiment Y1

and Y2. The results of the qualitative measurements of H2 gas in each experiment showed that 
over 90 % H2 was present each day in the bottles, meaning that the overall hydrogen consumption 
during the experiment was low.  
From the experiments of optimum conditions for butanol production as well as the experiments 
with continuous replenishment of H2, it was observed that butyrate was never fully consumed. It 
was hypothesized the initial blending could be a factor. Therefore, a new set of experiments was 
conducted to evaluate the effect of the inoculum pretreatment method. It was found that the initial 
blending reduces the performance of the MMC to produce butanol. A 1.90-fold increase in butanol 
concentrations (max. 14.50 mM in A4) was obtained showing a clear correlation with no 
pretreament of the sludge. This is likely due to the shear forces induced by mixing which might 
destroy the cell membrane or the enhanced contact with oxygen. An increased acetate production 
above 90 mM was observed at the end of fermentation in the experiments with untreated sludge. 
A new set of experiments was conducted to test if homoacetogenesis is occurring during 
fermentation. The experiment showed a decrease in butyrate concentration while an increase in 
butanol concentration simultaneously occurs, which demonstrated the ability of the microbes in 
the sludge to consume butyrate. Butanol concentration reached maximum of 13.54 mM and 12.56 
mM in experiment AC3 and AC4, respectively. Interestingly, it was observed that a metabolic shift 
occurred possibly due to the increase in pH in which the microbes started to consume butanol and 
produce butyrate again. Acetate concentration reached 74.02 mM and 90.84 mM, respectively, 
indicating that homoacetogenesis is occuring in the fermentation. The carbonate from the sludge 
and the pH2 contributed to the reaction of homoacetogenesis. Although it appeared that there are 
some kind of dependence on butyrate on the reaction, which needs further investigation.
