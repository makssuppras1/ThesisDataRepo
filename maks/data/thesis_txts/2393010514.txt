ABSTRACT 
Storage technologies currently represent a major obstacle in the transition from fossil 
fuel usage to renewable resource based energy. As chemicals represent one of the most 
efficient ways to store energy, electrochemical CO2 reduction allows for a potential 
solution to this problem by using harvested renewable energy to convert CO2 into a 
neutral carbon emission fuel. Nevertheless, none of the catalysts investigated thus far 
are active, selective and stable enough to make this technology a major industrial sector. 
Therefore, the project aims at experimentally studying the behavior of gold catalysts, 
mainly with respect to their stability under CO2 reduction conditions. The main 
objective is to investigate the stability of gold nanoparticulate catalysts, with defined 
loadings and sizes, in comparison to its bulk counterpart. Electrochemical 
characterization techniques are employed, coupled with electrochemical surface area 
measurements and transmission electron microscopy, to analyze morphological 
evolutions of the studied catalysts. 
It is indicated in the present work, through the development of a successful degradation 
procedure, that nanostructured catalysts may possess a higher stability than their bulk 
counterpart. Alongside, it is found that the degradation of a polycrystalline gold 
electrode is accompanied by a decrease in roughness, whereas nanostructured electrode 
deactivation is ascribed to nanoparticle agglomeration.  
Constant potential electroreduction was compared to simulated start-stop conditions and 
found to be a harsher environment towards nanoparticle agglomeration. In addition, 
relationships between catalyst activity and nanoparticle electrode preparation methods 
are uncovered throughout the report. It is found that Nafion, the binder used to prepare 
the electrodes, improves the quality of the drop casted inks, but it blocks CO2 from 
accessing catalytic sites.  
Electrochemical surface area methods have been studied, and a comparison between 
lead underpotential deposition (Pb UPD) and oxide reduction is made. The latter was

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Motivation 
Society is currently dependent on energy carriers to sustain its daily routine, namely, 
fossil fuels that are considered not to be sustainable due to greenhouse gas emissions. 
The central role of renewable energies (solar, wind, etc.) is now evident, along with 
energy storage solutions, to make them competitive with fossil fuels. Direct renewable 
energy storage would allow continuous exploitation of natural resources. Currently, 
energy can be stored in different forms: electrical (e.g. capacitors), thermal (e.g., in 
water or phase transition materials), mechanical (e.g. flywheels, compressed air), 
chemical (e.g. gasoline, methane, methanol, etc…) and electrochemical energy 
(batteries). [1] The most promising strategies among the aforementioned technologies 
are chemical storage and batteries, due to their high energy density. This fact is 
exemplified by the graph in Figure 1, where the deliverable peak power (e.g. 
acceleration in a car) is plotted versus the specific energy (e.g. driving time before 
refueling). This chart has the common name of Ragone Plot. [2] 
 
Figure 1 Ragone Plot reprinted from [2].

From Figure 1, it is possible to conclude that chemical energy is the winning strategy, as 
it maximizes both these criteria, with gasoline and methanol being the best options. 
Batteries currently dominate the electronic industry market and are already used for the 
transportation sector. Even so, they have an overall lower performance than 
conventional liquid fuels, limiting their implementation in sectors such as aviation or in 
some industrial sectors that require prolonged usage cycles. Hydrogen as a chemical 
fuel (used in fuel cells) is currently dependent on expensive precious metals to be 
sustainably produced by electrolysis. [1] 
Catalytic electroreduction of CO2 is a very promising technique to control the 
atmospheric concentration of this greenhouse gas, while producing valuable chemicals 
(methanol and others). These can be used to store renewable energy in a carbon-neutral 
fuel. In spite of its desirability, this process is yet to be incorporated as a major 
industrial sector. [3] CO2 reduction poses a challenge:  none of the catalysts investigated 
thus far is sufficiently active (amount of molecules converted per unit time, expressed as 
a partial current at a given potential) or selective (overall percentage of the different 
products, expressed as Faradaic efficiency) to make this technology industrially viable. 
Electrode stability represents the second challenge in CO2 electro-reduction. This is a 
very important issue that may heavily affect the introduction of these technologies in the 
current energy infrastructure. Indeed, the effectiveness of this device is directly 
correlated to its lifetime.  
At a fundamental level, most of the current research efforts focus on the discovery of 
selective catalysts, that is, electrodes that will favor the formation of fuels and other 
useful products from CO2 over other competitive reactions, such as the hydrogen 
evolution reaction. On the other hand, studies concerning the stability of the catalysts 
are scarce, especially those in the form of nanoparticles. Among the several transition 
metals studied, gold was found to have exceptional selectivity towards the reduction of 
CO2 to CO and promising activity (partial current density) at rather low overpotentials. 
[4] Ongoing research aims at further increasing the catalytic activity of gold electrodes, 
by creating nanostructures of this material.  Stability studies of these nanopoarticulate 
catalysts are scarce.

The motivation of the present work is then to investigate the stability of both bulk and 
nanostructured gold electrodes for CO2 reduction, to understand the mechanisms behind 
deactivation and to propose strategies to extend their life time.  
1.2 Theoretical background – Electrochemistry and CO2 reduction 
Electrochemistry is the field arising from chemical reactions that are driven by an 
electric voltage or spontaneous reactions that generate a net flow of charge. The first 
observation is that an electrochemical reaction (as an electron transfer between two 
species) is a consequence of an electromotive force. As an example, if two wires are put 
in a solution and connected to a generator, an electromotive force (or voltage) will be 
imposed between the two ends of the wires inside the solution (called electrodes). With 
this picture in mind, electrochemical reactions can only occur in pairs, that is, if a 
reaction occurs on one end of a wire there will be an opposing reaction on the end of the 
other wire. One of the reactions includes a donation of electrons from the reactant 
(oxidation reaction) and the other reaction will happen as an acceptor of electrons from 
another reactant (reduction reaction), commonly called half-cell reactions.  
Without the use of a generator connected to the wires, currents can be produced by 
spontaneous reactions, as in a fuel cell, due to a difference in chemical potential 
between the two ends of the wires. The two devices described are illustrated in Figure 2.

the electrode being at η V below or above the equilibrium potential, respectively. 
Electrocatalysis is the field arising from the need of accelerating the rate of these 
reactions happening at the interface between the electrode catalyst (gold) and the 
reactants (CO2). 
The catalytic properties of the electrode are determined by the binding energies of the 
reactants, the products and intermediate compounds, to its surface. The overall accepted 
mechanism by which the reactions occur is through adsorption, which provides a lower 
energy pathway for the reaction to occur. In order for the reaction to occur, the reactant 
should have an adequate binding energy to the catalyst. If the binding energy to the 
products is too strong, the active sites will be blocked by the products and the reaction 
will not proceed. Therefore, a good catalyst will have a low binding energy to the 
products [2].

Taking into account that catalyst performance is accessed from the quantitative analysis 
of the reaction products, tools to compare the activity of different electrode 
morphologies are needed. In most of the literature, the catalytic activity is reported in 
the form of current per surface area. The surface area used to normalize the current can 
be either the geometrical surface area (current density) or the experimentally determined 
active surface area (specific current density), which takes the surface roughness into 
account. Several reports in the literature normalize the total current recorded to the 
geometrical surface area of the electrode, not taking the surface roughness into account. 
Under these circumstances, nanostructured electrodes will always perform better than 
their polycrystalline counterpart. On the other hand, a normalization of the catalytic 
activity to an experimentally determined electrochemical surface area (ECSA) can 
provide further information about the catalytic activity of the electrodes. This can be 
done by dividing the current density by a roughness factor (𝑅𝑓), defined as the ratio 
between the true surface area and the geometrical surface area, 𝑅𝑓=

Research groups have reported how nanostructured gold catalysts increased the activity 
and selectivity with respect to its bulk counterpart, even when the specific surface area 
was accounted for. [5], [6] Factors such as the number of catalytic active sites (e.g. 
number uncoordinated sites), and the electronic state created by nanostructures are 
reported throughout literature as possible reasons behind stabilization of the reaction 
intermediate COO-, which would explain this enhanced performance. [5]–[8] These 
studies indicate that the reduction mechanism is highly dependent on the catalyst 
morphology (amount of facets, edges and corners available to act as catalytic sites) 
which influences the binding energies of the different reactants. 
In order to benchmark the catalytic activity of different nanostructured Au electrodes, 
with particular focus on Au nanoparticles, CO partial current data points available in the 
literature are plotted along with the activity of polycrystalline Au from the work of Hori 
et al. [4] The points represented in Figure 3 are normalized to the true surface area, 
calculated with the roughness factors given in each study. The data corresponding to the 
etched polycrystalline Au is plotted as given in the study without roughness correction 
in blue. The points corresponding to the CO partial current density for the different 
nanoparticles studied in literature are plotted in green. References for each of the points 
are given in the caption of Figure 3.

In a recent paper, Rogers et al. studied gold nanoparticles embedded in graphene 
nanoribbon composite electrodes. They have reported (as shown in Figure 3) an 
increase in catalytic activity from 3.76 to 8 mA/cm2 with respect to traditional carbon 
black supported nanoparticles. The increased specific activity was ascribed to substrate-
nanoparticle effects, as the same nanoparticles (8 nm) supported on conventional carbon 
black did not show an enhanced performance. [9] This is illustrated in Figure 3, where 
the two classes of materials are directly compared but due to the logarithmic y scale, the 
difference seems smaller. The nanoribbon-supported catalysts and the carbon black 
nanoparticles are represented in a square and spades, respectively, in Figure 3. 
Noteworthy, both catalysts achieve a remarkable activity, although the specific current 
density was assessed only at one electrode potential.  
Kanan et al. have also shown in another study that the presence of grain boundaries on 
20 nm Au nanoparticles supported on carbon nanotubes increases their selectivity and 
catalytic activity towards CO2 reduction to CO [10]. This study is represented in Figure 
3, in green dots. The density of grain boundaries was controlled via different annealing 
treatment temperatures from 200 to 400 ºC. They found the CO activity to scale linearly 
with the density of grain boundaries (determined with TEM), showing that grain 
boundaries are highly active catalytic sites. The H2 activity was also monitored and 
found to decrease when more grain boundaries were present. This is illustrated in Figure 
4 below, where the black dots correspond to the nanoparticles with biggest amount of 
grain boundaries.

1.3.2 Stability of bulk and nanostructured electrodes 
The stability of CO2 electroreduction catalysts is a major challenge in the 
implementation of this technology. The cost of the fuel obtained is directly related to the 
life time of a CO2 reduction catalyst, as the maintenance costs (i.e. replacement of the 
catalysts) will be reflected in the price of the final product. This becomes a crucial issue 
to make the CO2 reduction fuels competitive against fossil fuels. Even though Au 
nanostructures have been electrochemically characterized in several studies, stability 
investigations on Au nanoparticles for CO2 reduction are scarce. The present section 
aims at comparing the different investigations on the stability of nanoparticles and bulk 
Au electrodes, in separate studies. Some of the existing controversies on this subject 
will also be presented. 
It is known that a catalyst will de-activate over time under CO2 reduction conditions. 
Clear evidence of this decay is, for example, a decreasing current when holding the 
electrode at a constant potential. However, the fundamental degradation mechanism of 
polycrystalline Au remains unknown. One of the few studies aiming at clarifying this 
question was done by Augustynski et al. who report that a continuous decrease in 
current is recorded during constant potential CO2 reduction. [11] This is illustrated in 
Figure 7, where the current produced by a polycrystalline Au electrode is seen to 
decrease considerably in the first 25 minutes (~1600 s) of electrolysis.

1.3.3 Analytical tools to study the degradation of nanostructured catalysts 
The surface morphology is an important parameter to take into account during an 
electrochemical characterization. Especially when the electrode stability is investigated, 
precise quantification of the nanoscale electrochemical active surface area is desired. 
Electrochemical surface area (ECSA) techniques are highlighted in this chapter as a 
desirable way to investigate the morphology of nanostructured electrodes. 
Several analytical tools such as chemisorption and physisorption techniques based on 
the adsorption of gas molecules, for example Brunauer-Emmet-Teller technique (BET) 
[14], scanning electron microscopy (SEM) [13], atomic force microscopy (AFM) [5]

and several electrochemical surface area (ECSA) determination methods, have been 
used to measure the surface area nanostructured catalysts. [9], [10], [14] The main 
drawback of these methods with regards to ECSA measurements is the fact that they 
often rely on image analysis (SEM and AFM). In the BET case, a large amount of 
sample is necessary to precisely probe the area and high temperatures are employed 
which could modify the nanostructure features. [14] These factors make electrochemical 
methods very effective when coupled with other analytical tools, namely TEM 
(discussed further in this section), which allow for an accurate benchmarking to perform 
the quantitative ECSA determination. 
The ECSA can be determined from the double layer capacitance or via electrochemical 
impedance spectroscopy (EIS) (if the equivalent circuit is known). Other methods rely 
on the formation of a monolayer of a certain species which is then stripped off as a 
consequence of an electrochemical reaction such as underpotential deposition (UPD) (as 
explained in section 2.4.2.2) or metal oxidation/reduction (as explained in section 
2.4.2.1). ECSA measurements are quite inexpensive and easy to do (when compared to 
SEM, AFM or BET) and can provide quantitative analysis if the charge coverage of the 
deposited monolayer (i.e., foreign metal or surface oxide) is known.

1.4 Scope of the thesis 
In the present study, nanostructured gold catalysts will be investigated with particular 
interest towards their stability. The evolution of the catalyst under CO2 reduction 
conditions will be monitored: selectivity and catalytic activity to CO2 reduction will be 
correlated to the evolution of the catalyst features. In particular, the reaction selectivity 
(products will be measured via gas chromatography) will be correlated to the catalyst 
surface area (measured electrochemically with lead underpotential deposition) and 
particle size distribution (measured with TEM images). 
Initially, the goal will be to demonstrate the validity of ECSA methods to measure the 
surface area of gold nanoparticles (with defined catalyst loadings and particle sizes) and 
of bulk Au electrodes. Lead underpotential deposition is compared to the oxidation of 
an AuO layer as ECSA determination method. The influence of the preparation method 
is then investigated with Pb UPD. On the second phase, the study will focus on the 
activity of Au nanoparticles and polycrystalline electrodes and its comparison to 
previously reported data 
Following, the aim was to successfully develop a degradation procedure that can be 
applied to probe the degradation of the samples, with the aforementioned 
electrochemical characterization tools. Namely, Pb UPD and TEM will be used to 
measure the morphology after CO2 reduction conditions and to compare them to as 
prepared electrodes. This procedure will then be used to investigate the influence of the 
morphology (nano-particles versus bulk Au and ink preparation method) on the stability 
of the electrodes.  
The overall goal of the project is to identify the main degradation mechanisms and to 
point out possible strategies to extend the lifetime of gold catalysts for CO2 
electroreduction.

2 MATERIALS AND METHODS 
2.1 Overview 
Being the goal of the project to correlate surface morphology and catalytic performance 
changing with time, the procedure revolved around comparing etched bulk Au and Au 
nanoparticles over CO2 reduction conditions. This section aims at clarifying the overall 
procedure used to investigate the stability of Au catalysts which was performed by the 
following steps: 
 Initial ECSA determination; 
Since Pb2+ can contaminate the CO2 reduction setup, these measurements are carried 
along in a different cell. Therefore, the data for the initial area is measured from a 
sample that is used solely for this purpose. 
 Three successive chronoamperommetries coupled with gas product analysis; 
The main part of the procedure, degrading the sample, is done by imposing CO2 
constant potential reduction conditions in three consecutive chronoamperommetries. 
The first and last ones, done for the sake of measuring the performance of the catalyst 
(gas product analysis), and the second one (carried for 15h30min) with the purpose of 
degrading the sample. The electrolyte was changed between the second and last 
chronoamperommetries 
 Pb UPD on the already degraded sample; 
By employing ECSA measurements (Pb UPD) after the three chronoamperometries, it 
is possible to correlate morphological changes with the difference in products collected 
before and after. This is carried out in a separate cell, also used for the initial ECSA 
measurements. 
 TEM imaging (nanostructured electrodes); 
TEM imaging was applied to samples subjected to the degradation procedure. This 
allowed a comparison between independently prepared samples, either subjected to 
degradation or not.

2.2 The three electrode setup 
The three electrode cell composed of a working (WE), counter (CE) and reference (RE) 
electrodes coupled with a potentiostat, as schematized in Figure 12, is the most 
commonly used electrochemical setup. The three electrodes are immersed in an 
electrolyte that ensures ionic conductivity between the CE (connected to the blue wire) 
and the WE (connected to the red wire), closing the electrical circuit. A proton exchange 
membrane is placed between the WE and the CE, ensuring that any reactants or 
products in the two compartments are not mixed and achieve ionic contact at the same 
time. The importance of separating the CE and WE compartments arises from the need 
of avoiding, for example, CO2 reduction products being oxidized by traveling to the CE 
or dissolved CE material travelling to the WE. 
The crucial feature of the setup is allowing the monitoring of electrochemical reactions 
by recording the current and potential at the working electrode which is only possible in 
a three electrode configuration. The potential is measured between the RE and the WE 
and the current is measured between the CE and the WE. In three electrode setups, no 
current is passing between the RE and the WE which is ensured by a highly non-
==PAGE:31==
Chapter 2: Materials and Methods 
 
Ricardo Filipe Paz Moldes Duarte – October 2017 
  
31 
 
polarizable RE. While reactions are happening, the potential of the CE will change to 
keep up with the measured cell current.  A ceramic frit placed between the two 
compartments has the purpose of not allowing contaminants to pass from the RE to the 
WE compartment. 
 
Figure 12 Representation of the CO2 reduction H-cell used and tubings connecting it to the gas 
chromatograph. 
In this setup, the half-cell reactions occurring in the WE are monitored and several 
electrochemical characterization techniques can be performed. This makes the CE 
generally irrelevant, as it is used as a sink/source of electrons, unless it interacts 
electrochemically with the reactions occurring in the WE. From a practical standpoint, 
the potentiostat will stop the measurement if the current is above 10 mA or if the 
voltage across the WE and CE (cell potential) is above 10 V (potential limits of the 
potentiostat).  Through various potential control techniques, the electrochemical 
properties of the WE can be probed by measuring the current, which can be positive or 
negative depending on the reaction process occurring in the WE. A positive current is 
recorded if electrons are flowing out of the WE (oxidation) and a negative one if 
electrons are flowing to the WE (reduction). This means that processes of oxidation, 
reduction (of species on the electrode surface or atoms in the electrolyte) are measured. 
Similarly, adsorption, surface reconstructions or changes in the double layer capacitance 
can be probed.

In the present work, the working electrode compartment is where CO2 reduction takes 
place. The gas inlet is connected to a bubbler that is placed inside the WE compartment. 
The gas outlet is then connected, through a pump, to a gas chromatograph which can

compartment, accumulating the products formed. In this way, the purge mode is used to 
saturate the electrolyte with CO2 or to discard remaining products from previous 
experiments. The loop mode is used during CO2 electrolysis experiments, when it is 
desirable to accumulate the CO2 reduction products prior to quantitative analysis.

immersed in a beaker full of water that is boiled in a heating plate at around 250ºC. This 
procedure (rinsing and boiling) is repeated two times after the glassware is removed 
from the piranha bath.

2.4 Electrochemical characterization techniques 
The following section aims at presenting the different electrochemical characterization 
techniques used in the present work. 
2.4.1 Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy 
Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), explained in detail in [20], consists of 
applying alternating current (a sinusoidal function) to the WE and measuring the 
resulting current. The WE current response will have a phase shift (that depends on the 
frequency of the applied current) with regards to the potential applied. The quantity 
impedance (𝑍= 𝐸𝐼
⁄ ) is a complex number that is then measured as a function of the 
frequency of the applied potential wave. The result from an EIS technique, called 
Nyquist plot, is the negative of the imaginary part of this number plotted versus its real 
component. 
In the present work, this technique was used to determine the Ohmic drop between the 
RE and the WE which is required for an accurate potential control of the WE. A 
Nyquist plot is constructed by measuring the impedance from 100 kHz to 1 MHz 
frequency with a 10 mV amplitude sinusoidal wave. Typically, the Nyquist plot will 
display a straight line (resistor in series with a parallel resistor-capacitor). The Ohmic 
drop is then taken as the intersection between the straight line and the x axis. The 
potentiostat is then automatically set to correct the potential for 85% of the ohmic 
losses. The ohmic drop correction works in a feedback loop where the potential is 
adjusted after the current is measured. This system will yield an error if the potential is 
corrected for 100% of the ohmic losses. This measurement is performed before each 
electrochemical technique, yielding values of around 100 Ω for 0.1 M KHCO3 
electrolytes and around 15 Ω for 0.05 M H2SO4 and 0.1 M HClO4 electrolytes. 
2.4.2 Cyclic voltammetry 
Cyclic voltammetry (CV), explained in detail in [21], is a widely used electrochemical 
technique for electrode characterization.  It consists in sweeping the electrode potential 
along a triangular potential waveform (schematized in Figure 14) with a certain scan 
rate (V/s) and in recording the resulting current. CV can be used with many different

2.4.2.1 Surface area determination from AuO reduction 
A common and simple method to determine the electrochemical surface area of a

applying an anodic potential and then scanning to cathodic potentials. This is achieved 
by applying the CV technique, reducing the oxide layer formed in every cycle. The 
reduction of a metal to an oxide is highly dependent on the potential applied. The metal 
surface will oxidize partially or it will passivate completely (bulk oxidation) if the 
potential is too anodic.  However, the goal for a proper surface area measurement is the

2.4.2.2 Surface area determination from lead underpotential deposition 
Lead underpotential deposition (Pb UPD) is achieved by scanning a sample very close

2.5 CO2 reduction gaseous product analysis – Gas 
chromatography 
The assessment of the catalyst activity, in the form of partial currents and reaction 
selectivity, relies on an accurate measurement of the formed products. A gas 
chromatograph (GC), also described in [2], is used throughout the present work to 
perform this task. As mentioned before, it is connected to the electrochemical cell when 
it is operated in a loop mode allowing the direct injection of the products to the GC 
column. The different compounds in the injected gas are then separated and quantified 
in the appropriate detector. Figure 15 shows a schematic of a GC. 
 
Figure 15 Schematic representation of the different components in a GC. 
The basic principle behind product separation is related to how long the products take to 
travel through the column. After the products are injected along with an inert gas 
(carrier gas, forming a mobile phase) they are separated by their different interaction 
with the material in the column (stationary phase). The compounds with lower affinity 
are retained less and will elute first. In order to change the retention times of the 
different compounds, the temperature of the column or the pressure of the carrier gas 
can be changed.  
Following the elution of the different products, their amount is detected in one of the 
two detectors on the GC. The two different detectors present in the GC used in this 
work are the thermal conductivity detector (TCD) and the flame ionization detector

(FID). The former detector is based on thermal conductivity differences between the 
mobile phase and a reference gas. When the mobile phase flows through the resistor it 
changes its temperature with respect to the temperature of the reference resistor. The 
signal is then detected in the form of a voltage difference between the two resistors, 
where the carrier gas and the mobile phase are passing through. This detector is used to 
detect H2 products as well as N2 or O2. The FID is based exposing the mobile phase to a 
H2 flame. The flame ionizes the compounds that then travel through a nozzle with a 
positive bias voltage that repels the ionized compounds towards a collector plate. This 
collector plate is then connected to an ammeter that measures the current associated to 
the ions hitting it. The FID is used in the present work, in conjunction with a methanizer 
(operated with Ni catalyst at 350ºC), to measure CO.  The methanizer converts CO to 
methane, which is then quantified in the FID. The GC used in the present work has two 
columns: the first one separates carbon-based compounds (e.g., CO and CO2) which 
then go to the methanizer and FID; the products that are not separated in this column, go 
to a permanent gas column (capable to separate N2 and H2 compounds) positioned 
before the TCD.

2.6 Transmission Electron Microscopy 
Transmission electron microscopy (TEM), described in [22], is currently the 
characterization technique that enables the highest resolution among microscopy 
techniques due to the small size of the probing particle, the electron. An image is 
generated after the electrons are transmitted through the specimen investigated. The 
TEM apparatus is able to probe a sample in three different operating modes: mass-
thickness contrast, diffraction contrast and phase contrast. In the present work, since it 
is desired to measure nanoparticle sizes, only the mass-thickness contrast is used and 
the latter two operating modes are not discussed. The mass contrast operating mode is 
based on differences in the amount of electrons transmitted in different incident points 
of the sample. Points in the sample with higher thickness or heavier elements will 
transmit a smaller amount of electrons, appearing as darker areas in the resulting 
picture. The images taken with the TEM are analyzed with the Image J program to 
measure the diameter of the particles. This process is performed for a sample size of 
about 350 particles in different locations of the grid to build a particle size distribution. 
TEM investigations rely on very thin samples, otherwise electrons would not be able to 
travel through the sample. A grid made of Lacey carbon on a Cu mesh is used to hold 
the Au/C nanoparticles that is deposited on top of it. To analyze the Au/C catalyst after 
they are subjected to the degradation procedure, the electrode is sonicated in ethanol 
until all of the Au/C film is suspended. The obtained solution is very diluted and 
therefore it is left at 80ºC to evaporate the residual ethanol, until a suspension of 
particles is seen with the naked eye (suspension turns darker). A solution with a 
concentration of 0.1 g/L Au/C catalyst is used to measure the initial particle size 
distribution. 3 μL of the given solution are deposited on top of the TEM grid.

3 RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
3.1 Initial electrochemical surface area 
The determination of the electrochemical surface area (ECSA) is crucial task in the 
present work, as it represents the most direct and simplest method to track the 
morphological and surface changes of the catalyst under reaction conditions. Moreover, 
the determination of the ECSA enables activity comparisons between Au/C supported 
nanoparticles and polycrystalline Au, through normalization of the current to the surface 
area measured. 
3.1.1 Electrochemical surface area from AuO reduction 
We start by examining the CVs recorded following the procedure described in section 
2.4.2.1. Here, the ECSA is obtained by reducing the oxide layer and integrating the peak 
associated with this reaction. The knowledge of the AuO surface charge coverage 
(0.778 mC/cm2 [15]) is then used to convert the charge into surface area. This simple 
method is important for the present work, to get familiar with the setup and to compare 
this method with Pb UPD done in the present work.  
The scope of this experiment was to study the influence of the applied anodic potential 
on the measured surface area. Since a monolayer is expected at a specific (and 
unknown) anodic potential, this is carried out by repeating the procedure from section 
2.4.2.1 for different anodic potentials. The electrochemical surface area determined by 
this method was investigated for different electrodes: polished and etched 
polycrystalline Au (1 cm2 geometrical area) and to two drop casted Au/C inks with the 
same I/C ratio of 0.5 but different nanoparticle loadings (5 and 10 μg of Au 
nanoparticles).

3.1.2 Electrochemical surface area from Pb UPD 
Results from lead underpotential deposition, achieved by scanning the WE potential 
very close to the equilibrium potential of the Pb2+/Pb reaction in a lead perchlorate 
solution, are presented in this section after following the procedure explained in section 
2.4.2.2. These measurements are based on the stripping of a reduced lead monolayer, 
similarly to the oxide reduction. Integration of the anodic peaks recorded, allow an 
estimation of the true surface area from the charge coverage value of 300 μC/cm2 used 
by Hamelin et al. [14] In an initial stage, it was important to determine the initial 
surface area of several independently prepared electrodes. Alongside, it was desired to 
study the influence of parameters such as the scan rate and the I/C ratio on the resulting 
surface area. 
The first parameter studied, the scan rate, is compared with the results from Rouya et al. 
who found Cu UPD to vary minimally with scan rate from 10 to 50 mV/s. [15] At the 
same time, it is desirable to reproduce the two peaks characteristic with Pb UPD on Au 
associated with the different facets. A sharp peak at associated with (111) facets and a 
broader peak corresponding to both (110) and (100) facets are expected, as mentioned in 
section 1.3.3. 
In a second phase, the influence of the Au/C nanoparticles preparation method was 
studied. Namely, the influence of the I/C ratio (a measure of the electrode’s Nafion 
content) and nanoparticle loading is correlated with the measured ECSA. The I/C ratio 
was an interesting variable to investigate, as it affects the quality of the drop casted inks. 
Inhomogeneities were often seen with a naked eye, such as particle pile up in the edge 
of the film or macro agglomerates. 
In an effort to study the aforementioned dependences, the Pb UPD procedure described 
in section 2.3.1 was applied to a freshly etched Au foil using 20 and 50 mV/s scan rate. 
This procedure was repeated three times for each scan rate where the sample was etched 
between measurements. Two representative CVs are plotted in Figure 20.

It is established that a gold bulk oxide/hydroxide layer can be formed if the anodic 
potential exceeds a threshold between 1.8 and 2 V vs RHE. [23] Nonetheless it is very 
difficult to determine the onset potential for bulk oxidation of the Au electrodes. On top 
of this, as indicated by the work of Tremiliosi-Filho et al. the charge coverage of the 
AuO layer changes with the anodic potential applied. [24]  
The underestimation of the ECSA of polycrystalline Au could, in theory, be due to a 
wrong charge coverage from the work of Rouya et al. [15] On the other hand, this 
would be inconsistent with the agreement between the values registered with the Au/C 
nanoparticulate catalysts. Instead, we hypothesize that for an applied potential of 1.6 V 
vs RHE, a monolayer coverage is not obtained for the polycrystalline Au and that this is 
the cause for ECSA underestimation. The bulk Au oxidation onset potential of 1.8 V 
given in by Cherevko et al. [23] would be coherent with this argument since an anodic 
potential limit of 1.6 V vs RHE was used.

recorded for the different loadings, shown in Table 2. Noteworthy, a higher amount of

two times more binder. It is interesting to note that even if the loading is doubled, the 
area per mass is unchanged which could mean that the Nafion itself is not blocking Pb2+ 
ions from measuring catalytic sites. This observation would be coherent with studies 
from Cherevko et al. [25], who have demonstrated that dissolved transition metals (from 
the cathode in a fuel cell) can travel through the Nafion membrane and deposit in the 
anode electrode. 
It is concluded in this section that Pb UPD is able to identify deposition quality features. 
The sample with the highest Nafion content was identified to have the largest ECSA per 
catalyst mass. We hypothesize from the results presented in the current section that 
Nafion does not block the reduction and adsorption of Pb atoms on the Au nanoparticle 
surface. Alongside, it has been shown that AuO reduction ECSA method possesses 
several limitations, making Pb UPD a more reliable technique to evaluate the 
morphology of Au/C catalysts.

3.3 Stability investigations 
This section presents the results of the stability tests carried out according to the 
procedure described in section 2.1. Chronoamperometric curves are correlated with the 
GC analysis, the Pb UPD and TEM particle size distributions. By doing so, we try to 
relate morphology changes (e.g., particle size) with the reaction performance and 
stability. The results obtained with a 0.5 cm2 etched foil are discussed along with the 
Au/C catalysts with a chosen preparation method of 5 μg loading and an I/C of 1. On 
the one hand, these parameters were chosen to maintain a low loading, which was found 
to decrease the likelihood of a potential or current overload happening between the CE 
and WE (reaching a threshold of 10 V or 10 mA). On the other hand, this was the 
sample that displayed the best deposition quality, as discussed in section 3.1.2, which 
made the ECSA characterization to be more accurate between initial and final 
measurements.

4 CONCLUSIONS 
The stability of bulk Au electrodes has been studied in the present work and compared 
to that of nanoparticulate Au catalysts supported in carbon black and deposited on a 
glassy carbon substrate. The electrochemical performance (i.e., CO and H2 partial 
current densities and faradaic efficiency), the electrochemical surface area and particle 
size distribution (for Au/C catalysts) were measured before and after degradation 
procedures of CO2 electrolysis. Etched Au electrodes were observed to deactivate over 
about 30 minutes, whereas Au/C nanoparticulate catalysts seem to degrade slightly over 
15h30min of CO2 reduction conditions.  
The degradation of bulk Au electrodes was characterized by a decrease in selectivity 
towards CO evolution, along with a decrease in electrochemical surface area. An ECSA 
normalization of the initial and final activities has evidenced a maintained total current 
per surface area, but with a shift in faradaic efficiencies. The latter indicates that surface 
reconstructions are taking place during CO2 reduction, making the surface more prone to 
catalyze the H2 evolution reaction. The ECSA decrease of bulk Au electrodes was 
ascribed to a decrease in roughness or to irreversibly adsorbed contaminants, explaining 
the dropping total currents, but not the changes in catalyst selectivity. 
Stability investigations on Au/C catalysts record a slight drop in current, observed over 
15 h 30 min of cathodic potential hold, accompanied by a maintained selectivity and a 
decrease in ECSA. TEM analysis enabled the determination of particle size distributions 
which have evidenced that agglomeration is the cause of the ECSA decrease. 
Experiments conducted in the present work evince that nanostructured Au/C catalysts 
appear to be more stable than polycrystalline Au. This increase in stability is likely due 
to the Nafion and the carbon black that prevent restructuring of the Au nanoparticles.  
An investigation of CO2 reduction under oscillating potential conditions, as opposed to 
constant potential hold, has been carried out with TEM analysis. Results show increases 
in mean particle size which are more pronounced under constant potential conditions.

These findings indicate that the stability of Au electrodes should not be compromised 
by the intermittent use of the CO2 electrolytic device. 
Pb UPD has been compared with AuO reduction, as ECSA determination methods. The 
latter was found to hold an amount of uncertainties whereas Pb UPD has been 
successfully used to probe Au/C film deposition quality, differences in ratio of catalytic 
sites (between bulk and nanoparticulate Au catalysts) and surface area changes over 
degradation conditions.  
Among other interesting findings related to the preparation methods of the Au/C films, 
the initial mass normalized surface area (measured via Pb UPD) was observed to 
increase with an increase in Nafion content.  This is likely given by an increasing film 
quality and a minimization of inhomogeneities associated to the ink drying procedure 
on the glassy carbon substrate (i.e. particle pile up in the edge of the film or 
agglomerates). However, a lower current was measured for samples having a higher 
Nafion content, which means that some catalytically active sites are being blocked by 
the ionomer. These sites are likely to be still accessible for the Pb ions (Nafion is 
permeable to ions, less to gases such as CO2) and therefore they are probed by the Pb 
deposition. As such, the Pb UPD probes catalytic active sites that are unavailable for 
CO2 reduction. 
In conclusion, a reproducible procedure to analyze the stability of nanostructured gold 
catalysts was implemented. This procedure was used to compare Au/C supported 
nanoparticles to an etched Au foil electrode using various electrochemical analytical 
tools. Further investigations could aim at understanding the behavior of Au with respect 
to CO poisoning. A thorough analysis of this aspect might shed some light on the 
discrepancies in the stability between bulk Au electrode and nanoparticles.
