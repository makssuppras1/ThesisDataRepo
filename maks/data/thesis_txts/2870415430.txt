1 Introduction 
1.1 Status of the global environment 
The rapid expansion of global industrialization and heavy reliance on fossil fuels have led to 
increased greenhouse gas emissions, accelerating global temperature rise and raising 
environmental concerns (Nirupama et al., 2025). Human-caused climate change is already 
affecting many weather and climate conditions in every region across the globe. This has led to 
widespread adverse impacts and related losses and damages to nature and people (IPCC, 2023). 
To tackle the climate crisis, mitigation targets have been agreed upon. The Paris Agreement's 
central aim is to keep a global temperature rise this century well below 2 degrees Celsius above 
pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit the temperature increase even further to 1.5 
degrees Celsius (UNFCC, 2015). 
According to the last IPCC report, global mean surface temperature has already reached 1.1°C in 
2011-2020 above pre-industrial levels, and atmospheric CO2 concentrations are higher now than 
at any time in at least 2 million years. Changing the course to limit global warming to 1.5 degrees 
C (2.7 degrees F) will instead require deep GHG emissions reductions in the near-term. Even if 
countries achieved their climate pledges (nationally determined contributions or NDCs), they 
would reduce GHG emissions by just 7% from 2019 levels by 2030, in contrast to the 43% 
associated with limiting temperature rise to 1.5 degrees Celsius (Fransen et al., 2022). As the 
IPCC makes clear, the world needs to peak GHG emissions before 2025 at the very latest, nearly 
halve GHG emissions by 2030 and reach net-zero CO2 emissions around mid-century, while also 
ensuring a just and equitable transition (Boehm S & Schumer C, 2023).  
Furthermore, environmental challenges such as biodiversity loss, water scarcity, terrestrial 
acidification, and eutrophication are becoming increasingly critical. Ecosystems are under 
growing pressure from human activities, with land use and land-use change being key drivers of 
biodiversity loss on both global and regional scales (Souza et al., 2015). Additionally, soil 
acidification is a major environmental and economic concern worldwide, and mainly results from 
atmospheric sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides produced by fossil fuel combustion and 
agricultural fertilization (Xiao et al., 2020) 
Water scarcity is another pressing concern. The projected increase of the world population 
growth rate suggests an increasing demand for food, which will directly impact water 
consumption for agricultural production. Moreover, more frequent droughts driven by climate 
change are expected to intensify irrigation needs, increasing competition for water resources 
between agriculture and other economic sectors (Mancosu et al., 2015). Water availability is also 
affected by freshwater eutrophication. Increasing phosphorus discharges from human activities, 
particularly agricultural production reach surface waters and contribute to eutrophication, a 
leading cause of impairment of many freshwater ecosystems. Consequently, biodiversity 
in aquatic ecosystems is reduced and the use of potable water becomes limited (Ortiz-Reyes & 
Anex, 2018).  
1.2 Power-To-X implementation 
Demand for energy has increased by 15% over 2013 to 2023. Currently, there is a slowdown in the 
global energy demand. In advanced economies, overall energy demand declined on average by 
0.5% per year over the past decade. In contrast, in developing economies, there was an increase 
==PAGE:5==
2 
 
of 2.6% per year for the same period driven by a rise in the population, rise in the size of the 
economy and an increase in industrial output (IEA, 2024d). Additionally, a key aspect is that 
consumption is a function of affluence, meaning that the increasing affluence in many nations 
typically leads to larger consumption and, as a result, to larger national environmental footprints 
(Rosa & Dietz, 2012; Gebara & Laurent, 2023). Global final energy consumption currently stands 
at 445 EJ, being responsible for 20 604 Mt CO2 (i.e. 0.046 kg CO2/MJ). This rises steadily to over 
530 EJ by 2050 in the Stated Policies scenario projected by the IEA (IEA, 2024d), while the level of 
GHG emissions should reduce to 1 149 Mt CO2 to reach the agreed climate goals. 
To cover this energy demand, a mix of complimentary solutions to successfully lower carbon 
emissions on a global scale and mitigate climate change is needed. While the electricity demand 
is increasingly being covered by renewable energy sources such as wind and solar, some 
industries being ‘hard-to-electrify’ depend on other solutions. In this context, Power-to-X (PtX) 
technologies stands out as a promising pathway for the required large-scale transformation 
of energy systems (Koj et al., 2019). By providing carbon-neutral alternatives to fossil fuels, 
Power-to-X offers a viable pathway to significantly lower carbon emissions in carbon-intensive 
sectors that are difficult to electrify and account for around 30% of global emissions. These 
sectors include heavy-duty transport, shipping, chemicals production, and industry (where high 
temperatures are needed) (Yang et al., 2022).   
PtX technology is defined as a power unit that transforms electric power ‘P’ into hydrogen or 
hydrogen-based energy vectors or chemicals (Sorrenti, 2023). ‘X’ is an umbrella term for different 
energy sources: power-to-gas, power-to-liquid, power-to-fuel, power-to-chemicals and power-
to-heat (Wulf et al., 2020). A key stage of this concept is the production of hydrogen by water 
splitting in an electrolyser. The required electricity often comes from renewable energy 
generation, for example wind or photovoltaics, either directly or in the form of certificates, and 
less frequently from the grid (Wulf et al., 2020). To claim the fuel as renewable, the PtX facility has 
to be powered by renewable electricity, such as required by legislation in some jurisdictions 
(European Union, 2023). Currently, the vast majority of hydrogen is produced from steam 
methane reforming (SMR), and oil or coal gasification (CG)  (Incer-Valverde et al., 2022), thus 
hydrogen obtained from PtX would decrease the use the hydrogen production from fossil fuels. 
Although there is no general definition of the term PtX, some authors describe it as the conversion 
of electricity from a renewable source into an alternative energy source that can be utilized, 
transported and stored more readily (Hermesmann et al., 2021). However, some PtX applications 
require continuous production for economic viability due to the high investment costs, which can 
be threatened if using exclusively fluctuating energy sources (Bellotti et al., 2022). For this reason, 
as suggested by Sillman et al. (2024), in the present study, PtX technologies were not only 
categorised as those using renewable energy sources with direct connection to the renewable 
energy facility/plant, but also those connected to the grid, which can contain both renewable and 
non-renewable sources depending on the time and location. 
EU member states have committed to phasing out fossil fuels over time and their replacement 
with green hydrogen and other PtX products (European Comission, 2023). The EU Commission 
itself signals the start of a clean hydrogen economy in Europe, together with renewable energy 
projects, in particular, wind and photovoltaic; therefore, the European Green Deal becomes 
essential in the European recovery strategy. The EU Power-to-X ambitions includes the aim of 
installing 40 GW of renewable hydrogen electrolysers in the EU by 2030 (Genovese et al., 2023) 
up from less than 0.1 GW in 2021 (Hydrogen Council, 2021)  The goal is to produce up to ten 
million tons of renewable hydrogen in the EU (Genovese et al., 2023). 
==PAGE:6==

To achieve the goal, economic incentives have been proposed. For instance, the European 
Union's Clean Energy Package includes provisions aimed explicitly at PtX implementation and 
support mechanisms for renewable hydrogen and synthetic fuel production. These regulations 
foster a favorable market environment, encourage investment, and drive innovation in European 
PtX technology (Skov & Schneider, 2022). Additionally, the EU has established a new funding 
scheme, Important  Project  of  Common  European  Interest  (IPCEI),  that  aims  to  support  
countries  that  are  not  yet  active  in  PtX development.  An IPCEI on hydrogen is  currently under 
development and aims to close the gap between research and development projects and 
commercialization (Wulf et al., 2020). 
By 2019, demonstration projects had already been implemented on every continent except 
Africa, with Europe leading the way with 154 projects (Chehade et al., 2019). As of June 2020, 220 
PtX research and demonstration projects in Europe were identified across 20 countries, with an 
increasing number becoming interested in these technological concepts. By 2026, installed 
capacity is expected to reach 1410 MW. A clear regional focus has been established with France 
and Germany at the forefront of PtX development. Germany’s publicly funded initiatives across 
multiple sectors and France’s high-investment industry projects are key drivers of this 
acceleration. Both countries plan to install around 500 MW of capacity by 2025 (Wulf et al., 2020).  
However, operational commercial-scale PtX projects remain scarce. Projects of this kind have 
been developed only in a few countries, for example, Finland and Denmark. Finland recently 
launched its first industrial-scale green hydrogen plant, marking a significant milestone (Fuel Cell 
Works, 2025). Meanwhile, in Denmark, the PtX plant in Kassø, the world’s first large-scale 
commercial e-methanol production facility has just produced its first batch of green hydrogen 
(Fuel Cells Works, 2025). 
1.3 Power to Hydrogen and absolute environmental sustainability 
Although many PtX pathways have been developed so far, only around one third of the projects 
are processing hydrogen into other fuels and products (Incer-Valverde et al., 2022) meaning that 
the primary PtX product continues to be hydrogen. Power-to-Hydrogen (PtH) process has gained 
significant attention due to its potential to address critical energy and environmental challenges. 
By converting electricity into hydrogen, PtH offers a promising solution for integrating fluctuating 
renewable energy sources, such as wind and solar, into the energy grid (Dolci et al., 2019).  
The environmental sustainability of PtH technology has been evaluated lately with an increasing 
number of Life Cycle Assessment studies. The majority of studies consider Power-to-Gas or 
Power-to-Transport with climate change being the most analysed environmental impact category 
(Koj et al., 2019). The review performed by Bhandari et al. (2014) identified nearly 20 LCAs of 
hydrogen production technologies, all of them employing electrolytic technologies. Sillman et al. 
(2024) analyzed different LCAs on PtX pathways, including PtH and compared climate emissions 
derived from hydrogen production using electricity grid, fossil fuels and renewable energy. 
Studies only focusing on green hydrogen that compared different technologies for hydrogen 
electrolysis and different sources of renewable energy were also identified. Gerloff (2021) 
performed a comparative life cycle assessment of the three major water electrolysis technologies 
considering different energy scenarios in Germany and  Delpierre et al. (2021) performed an LCA 
of hydrogen production using wind energy, comparing two different water electrolysis systems in 
the Netherlands. 
==PAGE:7==
4 
 
Despite the LCA progress in PtH technologies, assessments of its life cycle impacts in relation to 
the planetary boundaries, critical for ensuring safe ecological thresholds, are currently limited. 
Such thresholds can be assessed using absolute environmental sustainability assessment 
(AESA). This method consists of an evaluation in which an activity’s environmental performance 
will not depend on the performance of the anthropogenic reference system, but reflect its 
performance relative to absolute environmental boundaries (Ryberg et al., 2020). AESAs 
determine absolute environmental boundaries which are based on the planetary boundaries (PB) 
framework developed by Rockström et al. (2009). PBs aim to delineate and quantify levels of 
anthropogenic perturbation that, if respected, would allow Earth to remain in a “Holocene-like” 
interglacial state, meaning that the Earth system would remain below a “safe operating space” 
(SOS) for global societal development (Steffen et al., 2015). In such a state, global environmental 
functions and life-support systems remain similar to those experienced over the past ~10,000 
years rather than changing into a state without analog in human history (Richardson et al., 2023).  
Dillman & Heinonen (2023) developed an absolute sustainability assessment of hydrogen 
production including hydrogen obtained through coal gasification, SMR, green and blue 
hydrogen. The study focused on the climate change impact category and assessed the 
performance of different hydrogen production scenarios in relation to the global climate goal. To 
refine the projections in hydrogen production, Weidner et al. (2023) conducted a prospective life 
cycle analysis to evaluate the environmental sustainability assessment of large-scale hydrogen 
production. The study acknowledged that most sustainability assessments to date have been 
carried out using the life cycle inventories (LCI) of today. However, most of the new hydrogen 
production capacity will be deployed in the future, in which not just technical parameters have 
improved (e.g., efficiency) but the decarbonization of the global economy likely has progressed, 
potentially reducing the environmental impacts of these technologies (Pehl et al., 2017).  
The literature suggests that there is a lack of studies that consider the different projections in the 
technology development that PtH will need. The electricity mix or the fuels’ environmental impact 
will most likely change in the years to come and this needs to be considered. Global assessment 
models projected in the next years offer this information according to different emission 
pathways but their use in PtH LCA’s is still scarce. Furthermore, as indicated by Weidner et al. 
(2023) future work should conduct absolute sustainability studies of entire energy systems to 
ensure energy needs are covered with the SOS. This implies testing how PtH will fit into the entire 
energy systems considering the global energy demand. 
Moreover, as PtH technologies are providing better energy efficiency and lower impact-intensity, 
this may result in a reduction in the per unit energy price, which may drive further increased 
consumption and thereby rebound effects. The increased energy consumption would, therefore, 
lead to an increase in total energy use and greenhouse gas emissions that should be considered 
when evaluating the progress towards the global climate target. Additionally, since PtH 
technologies are intensive in material use and primary energy demand e.g. mining for minerals, 
this could bring along an increase in other environmental impacts, e.g., ozone depletion, 
acidification, and human toxicity impacts. Thus, burden-shifting between one impact category to 
another might be overlooked (Koj et al., 2019).

1.4 Aims and objectives 
The aim of this thesis is to assess the development of Power to Hydrogen implementation and 
their performance within the global energy system considering the planetary boundaries. With 
increasing energy demand, there is a large pressure on the technologies to scale even faster, 
raising the question of whether they can sufficiently meet the needs for climate change mitigation 
and whether this is possible without causing adverse effects on other environmental impacts.   
Taking into consideration the projections in energy demand made by the IEA, this study will 
explore if the projected PtH implementation can be considered absolutely environmentally 
sustainable and can contribute to sustainable energy systems. The objectives are: 
• 
Assess the prospective environmental impacts of projected PtH technologies in 2030 
from a cradle to gate in a life cycle perspective and evaluate different scenarios for PtH 
systems to explore their role in reducing environmental impacts within the global energy 
system. 
• 
Conduct an absolute environmental sustainability assessment (AESA) of global PTH 
considering the assessment years 2023 and 2050. 
• 
Provide recommendations on the PtH potentials and pitfalls with regards to meeting 
future demands while respecting absolute environmental boundaries.

2 Methods 
2.1 Overall assessment methodology  
The project was developed in 4 stages: literature review, data collection and model development, 
impact assessment, and absolute environmental sustainability assessment (Figure 1). In the first 
step, a literature review was conducted where the final energy consumption for 2023 and 2050 
as well as state of the art of PtH technology was identified. In the second step, the global energy 
mix was modelled in 2023 and in 2050 following three different projections made by the IEA. In 
2023 it was assumed that hydrogen in the energy mix was produced from fossil fuels, while in 
2050, it was assumed to be electrolytic hydrogen (PtH). In a third step, Brightway2, a python 
package for environmental impact assessment was used to calculate the environmental impact 
of the global energy system as well as the environmental impact of the renewable hydrogen 
production. To account for changes in technologies’ efficiency in the upcoming years, the 
premise python tool was used with REMIND, an Integrated Assessment Model. Finally, in a fourth 
step, the global environmental thresholds for the energy sector were determined following two 
different sharing principles and these were compared to the environmental impact of the global 
energy mix depending on the assessment year.

2.2 Literature review 
A literature review was conducted to gather the necessary data for this study, focusing on two key 
aspects. First, it aimed to identify a reference for total energy consumption, including both 
historical data and projections for 2050. Second, it examined the current status of PtH 
technology, its state of the art, and relevant life cycle inventories, identifying datasets that could 
be replicated for this analysis. 
2.2.1 Global Energy mix   
Among the different sources to extract the global energy demand (WEC, 2019; IRENA, 2024), the 
energy demand was taken from the World Energy Outlook 2024 published by the International 
Energy Agency (IEA, 2024d). The report is a widely referenced source of energy analysis and 
projections (Khatib, 2012). It identifies and explores the biggest trends in energy demand and 
supply, and it includes global historical and projected data for three modelled scenarios. The 
dataset selected in this study was A.2 World Total Final Energy Consumption. 
To examine future energy trends, the World Energy Outlook (WEO) uses a scenario approach 
based on the Global Energy and Climate (GEC) Model (IEA, 2024b). In the WEO, the GEC Model is 
used to explore various scenarios, each of which is built on a different set of underlying 
assumptions about how the energy system might evolve over time. The GEC Model relies on three 
key inputs: macro drivers such as population and economic growth, techno-economic factors 
such as fossil fuel availability, electricity generation costs, other technology costs and emerging 
clean technologies. Additionally, policies serve as input data to design and calculate different 
energy scenarios (IEA, 2024b). 
Based on the GEC Model, the WEO sets three different scenarios: The Stated Policies Scenario 
(STEPS), the Announced Pledges Scenario (APS) and the Net Zero Emissions by 2050 Scenario 
(NZE). The first two scenarios are exploratory meaning that they define a set of starting conditions, 
such as policies and targets, and see where they lead based on market dynamics and 
technological progress of energy systems. In contrast, the NZE Scenario is designed to achieve a 
specific outcome in the future: net zero emissions from the energy sector by 2050. This trajectory 
is consistent with keeping the temperature rise in 2100 below 1.5 °C (with at least a 50% 
probability) with limited overshoot (IEA, 2024b). Unlike the exploratory scenarios, the NZE 
Scenario follows a normative approach which starting with a target works backwards to identify 
the necessary pathway to achieve it.  
In this research, the status in 2023 and the three scenarios proposed by the WEO in 2050 are 
considered, following different energy mixes and therefore different levels of hydrogen 
consumption. The energy mix of each projected scenario in 2050 is presented in Figure 2.

2.2.2 Power to Hydrogen projects in 2050  
In the mix provided by the IEA, energy from hydrogen was not further specified. Therefore, to 
provide more granular information about hydrogen in 2050, another review was conducted. The 
aim was to determine the global electrolyser capacity in 2050 and the type of electrolysers 
predominantly used in that year. Many different projects in different parts of the world were 
identified in the news, company reports and associations’ statements (IRENA, 2024; Hydrogen 
Insight, 2023; Hydrogen Council, 2024). However, details such as the capacity of the plant, status 
or type of technology used were not reported in all of them.  
In order to uniformize the data sources and have an overview of the hydrogen production facilities 
with a wide coverage, the Hydrogen Production and Infrastructure Projects Database, published 
by the IEA was selected as reference (IEA, 2024c). The database tracks data on projects to 
produce hydrogen for energy or climate change-mitigation purposes and the development of 
hydrogen infrastructure. It includes projects that have the objective of reducing emissions 
associated with producing hydrogen. Among the technologies covered in the database are water 
electrolysis, hydrogen produced from fossil fuel with CO2 capture, biomass and other low carbon 
technologies. For this database, there were considered only the hydrogen supply technologies 
relying on water electrolysis. Among the water electrolysis technologies, the database covers: 
AE, PEM and SOEC and a fourth type labelled as “Unknown PtX” for which the electrolysis type is 
undisclosed.  
The electrolyser capacity in 2050 could not be directly obtained from the database since some 
projects that now are at early stages of development do not disclose the capacity and also in the 
meantime until 2050, new projects not yet included in the database could be announced. 
According to the database, electrolyser capacity could reach 520 GW in 2030 with projects still 
at early stages of development. Due to lack of information, this was assumed to be the capacity 
in 2050 as well. Regarding the share of AE, PEM and other types of electrolysers, some projects 
do not disclose the type of electrolysers used. Therefore, in this study a simplification was done 
and there were only considered the two main electrolysers currently used: AE and PEM. To date, 
the use of AE is predominant, however PEM is expected to provide nearly 40% of the green 
hydrogen market by 2050 (Lejeune et al., 2024). Considering this, it is assumed that the rest of 
the electrolysers will use AE technology.

2.3 Data collection and model development  
Once the relevant information for hydrogen technology in 2050 was retrieved, it was incorporated 
into the global energy mix. It was assumed that by 2050, hydrogen will be produced entirely 
through water electrolysis (AE and PEM), whereas in 2023 it is primarily derived from steam 
methane reforming and coal gasification.  
With this information, an attributional LCA was conducted to calculate the environmental 
impacts of the global energy mix needed for the AESA. Additionally, the environmental impacts 
of hydrogen production were assessed separately, using 1 kg of hydrogen as the functional unit. 
A cradle-to-gate approach was applied for the assessment years 2023 and the three projected 
scenarios in 2050. 
The background system included all the energy sources that cover the global energy 
consumption. Inventory data for these energy sources was obtained from LCI databases for 2023 
and 2050 as described in Section 2.3.1. Meanwhile, for the foreground system, a specific dataset 
for hydrogen production technologies was considered for the modelling as described in Section 
2.3.2. 
2.3.1 Background system 
For the year 2023, the Ecoinvent database v3.10 was used to model the background system as it 
reflects the current state of the art of technology of processes in different industry sectors. 
Conversely, for the year 2050, to account for the changes in technology in the future in the life 
cycle inventory, the Prospective Environmental Impact Assessment (Premise) tool was used 
(Sacchi et al., 2022). Currently, Premise is the most consistent way of conducting changes to the 
background system of an LCI (Bruhn et al., 2023). Premise is a Python-based tool for prospective 
life cycle assessment which allows users to project the Ecoinvent 3.10 database into the future 
by using scenarios from Integrated Assessment Models (IAMs). The scenarios reflect projected 
energy policy trajectories, include emerging technologies, modify market shares, and assess 
changes in technologies' efficiency.    
Since  the IEA scenarios (STEPS, APS and NZE) suggest specific mixes in 2050, it is important to 
use IAM scenarios that would match the characteristics of each IEA scenario. In this project, the 
IAM ‘REMIND’ (Baumstark et al., 2021) was selected since among the IAMs is the only one that 
includes a wide range of scenarios that allows a more accurate matching with the IEA scenarios. 
REMIND is an energy-economy general equilibrium model linking a macro-economic growth 
model with a bottom-up engineering-based energy system model. Each scenario in REMIND 
presents a Shared Socioeconomic Pathway (SSP) along with a Representative Concentration 
Pathway (RCP). The SSPs are scenarios that examine how socioeconomic factors such as 
population, economic growth, education, urbanisation and the rate of technological 
development, might change over the next century and shape future societies. RCPs, on the other 
hand, describe different levels of greenhouse gases and other radiative forcings that might occur 
in the future (Hausfather, 2018). 
The basis for the matching between IEA and REMIND scenarios was their estimated global mean 
surface temperature (GMST) increase following a similar reasoning by Wei et al. (2024)  These 
scenarios were SSP2-NDC, SSP2-PkBudg1150 and SSP2-PkBudg500 and are presented in Table 
1. Scenario SSP2 ‘Middle of the Road’ (Medium challenges to mitigation and adaptation) 
considers a pragmatic approach aligned with following a path in which social, economic, and 
technological trends do not shift markedly from historical patterns. The NDC scenario represents

2.3.2 Foreground system  
In the foreground system, the life cycle inventories made by Delpierre et al. (2021) were used. 
They developed an ex-ante LCA of hydrogen produced in a PtH plant in the Netherlands for the 
year 2050 as proposed by Cucurachi et al. (2018). An ex-ante LCA scales-up an emerging 
technology using likely scenarios (e.g. using expert help, extreme views, learning curves for 
similar technologies) of future performance at full operational scale. The application of ex-ante 
LCA allows testing alternative policy interventions, putting claims of environmental sustainability 
to the test, and supporting early design improvements and sound investments (Cucurachi et al., 
2018). 
==PAGE:14==
11 
 
Del Pierre et al. (2021) explored  two possible scenarios for future hydrogen production having a 
clear distinction between “high” development of hydrogen technology (scenario A) and “low” 
development (scenario B). Scenario A assumes strong policy support and stakeholders’ 
involvement that promotes hydrogen production via electrolysis, contributing to optimizing these 
technologies. Scenario B assumes the absence of policy support, dispersed efforts in 
stakeholders’ involvement and predominancy of hydrogen produced by SMR and CG. Based on 
these conditions, the scenarios differ in the technical parameters considered such as the 
lifespan of the plant and the electrolysers, the system and stack capacities, the efficiency of the 
electrolysers and the material consumption. 
In this study, the optimal conditions for hydrogen production are being considered in order to 
determine if even in the most suitable conditions, the global energy mix will be deemed as 
absolute sustainable. For this reason, scenario A is selected and adapted in order to reflect the 
average PtH production globally in 2050. One change was that in the operation of the PtH plant 
the electricity was modelled to come from the grid (global electricity mix) instead of dedicated 
renewable wind energy generated in the Netherlands as in the original inventory. Additionally, the 
plant capacity was modified in order to account for the 520 GW that it is assumed to exist globally 
in 2050 as mentioned in the previous section. 
The ex-ante LCA focused on the two most common alternatives of electrolysers: Alkaline 
electrolysers (AE) and Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolysers. A description of both 
technologies is available in Section 6.1 of the Supplementary Information. These technologies 
are often selected by project demonstrations (50% of the demonstrations assess AE and 42% 
PEM). The situation differs greatly for SOEC electrolyser, which despite being a promising 
technology is much less mature covering only 8% of the demonstration projects (Chehade et al., 
2019). Therefore this technology was not considered for the current study. 
2.4 Impact assessment 
The LCA model was developed using the Brightway2 Python package (Mutel, 2017). The impact 
assessment method selected was ReCiPe 2016 (Hierarchical), which allows to transform the list 
of life cycle inventory results into 18 midpoint and 3 endpoint indicator scores. These indicators’ 
scores express the relative severity on an environmental impact category.  
From the 18 midpoint indicators provided by ReCiPe, only 11 were selected. The criterion for the 
selection of the indicators was that they enabled matching with the absolute thresholds for the 
AESA. Additionally, to gain a better understanding of the results, the assessment of endpoint 
categories was included. These indicators show the environmental impact on three higher 
aggregation levels, being the 1) effect on human health, 2) biodiversity and 3) resource scarcity.  
For the production of hydrogen, the functional unit was defined as 1 kg of hydrogen at a pressure 
of 20 bars. Production of hydrogen in 2050 represented by the LCI from Delpierre et al. (2021) was 
compared with production of hydrogen in 2023. The process selected for this was “market for 
hydrogen, gaseous, low pressure - CN” and it was assumed to be representative of the state of 
the art of hydrogen production in 2023, with a combination of SMR and CG technologies. For the 
global energy mix, the functional unit was defined as 1 kWh, however to account for the total 
environmental impacts to be used in the AESA, the results were scaled based on the projected 
total energy consumption in each scenario (See Table 3).

2.5 Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessment 
Once the results of the LCA are obtained, the next step is conducting the AESA. This begins with 
calculating the global environmental thresholds, also named global SOS. This study considered 
different environmental thresholds: some are derived from the planetary boundaries  framework 
when directly linked to the indicator’s metric, while others are taken from previous studies that 
estimated a global SOS for the indicator.  
The global SOS must then be down­scaled. This is done using allocation principles based on 
distributive justice theory, assigning a share of the SOS to products, sectors, or services. In this 
study, two sharing principles are applied, and the results are normalised to obtain the Absolute 
Sustainability Ratio (ASR). This ratio links the environmental impact of the energy sector to its 
allocated SoSOS. Thus, it is possible to determine whether it can be considered sustainable. 
2.5.1 Calculation of global environmental thresholds 
Gebara & Laurent (2023) conducted an AESA for national energy systems, establishing thresholds 
for 11 indicators that align with those considered in the current study. For the climate change 
indicator, they applied the “budgeting method”. This method is suitable to assess cumulative 
indicators that are particularly time-dependent such as climate change. In this method, the 
threshold is defined by a maximum allowance of accumulated impacts. As the years go by, the 
threshold has to be adjusted based on the remaining budget. In contrast, for indicators with time-
independent threshold values, they defined “steady state” thresholds., which remain constant 
regardless of the assessment year. In this study, the budgeting thresholds were adjusted to the 
assessment years 2023 and 2050, while the steady state thresholds were applied directly. 
2.5.1.1 Climate change 
In the literature, there are four boundaries commonly used: the 1.5 and 2 degree Celsius warming 
Paris Agreement targets and the planetary boundaries of 350 ppm CO2 and 1 W/m2 radiative 
forcing in 2100. To ensure consistency with the units of the environmental indicators expressed 
in kg CO₂eq, these boundaries were converted following the considerations detailed by Gebara & 
Laurent (2023). Thresholds were calculated for both 2023 and 2050. 
Regarding the Paris agreement targets, the global carbon budget for the period 2010–2100, 
derived from Robiou Du Pont et al. (2017), served as the starting point. To determine the remaining 
budget for each assessment year, historical emissions up to that point were subtracted. 
Historical emissions from 2010 to 2023 were taken from the Emissions Database for Global 
Atmospheric Research (EDGAR) available in Crippa et al. (2024). For estimated emissions from 
2024 to 2050, data from the IPCC projections was extracted from Kriegler et al. (2022). To get an 
annual budget in CO2eq/year, those values were distributed equally over the assessed period of 
78 years and 51 years for 2023 and 2050 respectively. Results are presented in Table 4.

2.5.2 SOS Allocation principles for the energy sector 
After defining the global thresholds, they need to be scaled to the level of the energy sector. To do 
this, it is necessary to set sharing principles to assign the share of maximum allowance 
corresponding to the energy sector. This can be done using one or the combination of ethical 
sharing principles derived from distributive justice theory such as utilitarianism (maximization of 
utility in society), egalitarianism (equality among individuals), prioritarianism (greater benefits for 
individuals in a worse situation), sufficientarianism (granting everyone has “enough”) and 
grandfathering (acquired rights – current level of impacts) (Gebara & Laurent, 2023; Hjalsted et 
al., 2021). Since there is currently no consensus about which principles to follow, it is 
recommended to use different sharing principles (Ryberg et al., 2020). In this study, two sharing 
principles based on acquired rights and utilitarianism were applied to assign the share of the SOS 
(SoSOS) to the energy sector.  
Principle 1 (acquired rights): This principle downscales the thresholds based on the status quo 
level of impacts (also often called grandfathering) to estimate the share for the energy sector.

absolutely sustainable or if it is only performing better than before. Normalization compares the

whether the global energy mix is sustainable or not in 2023 and in 2050 with the inclusion of 
electrolytic hydrogen. This was done by calculating the Absolute Sustainability Ratio (ASR) using 
Equation 3. The actual use of the SOS (actual environmental impact) remains constant for the

3 Results and discussion 
3.1 Environmental impact assessment 
The environmental impact assessment was applied to both the global energy system and the 
specific process of hydrogen production. 
Regarding the global energy mix, environmental impacts are projected to be lower in all 2050 
scenarios compared to 2023 across several categories, including climate change, fossil resource 
scarcity, terrestrial acidification, freshwater eutrophication, particulate matter formation, and 
ozone formation. This reduction aligns with the significant decline in energy consumption 
observed in the APS and NZE scenarios, driven by enhanced energy efficiency and electrification. 
Additionally, the share of fossil fuels in the energy mix decreases, favoring low-carbon electricity 
sources (IEA, 2024d). 
Regarding hydrogen production, its demand as a final product in 2023 is minimal (0.0018%) 
compared to other energy sources. However, by 2050, its usage is projected to increase to 0.35%, 
2%, and 4.2% in the STEPS, APS, and NZE scenarios, respectively. In absolute numbers, the 
increase is 200-fold in the STEPS scenario and nearly 2000-fold in the NZE scenario. Hydrogen in 
today’s energy system is predominantly used as a feedstock rather than a fuel, produced on site 
by steam methane reforming of natural gas or coal gasification without CCUS (IEA, 2024b). In a 
transition to a net zero energy system, by 2050, use of low-emissions hydrogen expands to new 
applications in sectors in which emissions are hard to abate, such as heavy industry, long-
distance transport, the production of hydrogen-based fuels or electricity generation and 
storage(IEA, 2024c). Therefore, the hydrogen demand increases accordingly. 
Even though the total amount of hydrogen increases considerably by 2050, environmental 
impacts related to hydrogen production decrease across multiple categories, including climate 
change, fossil resource scarcity, terrestrial acidification, freshwater eutrophication, particulate 
matter formation, and ozone formation (see Figure 3, Figure 4 and Figure 6). For climate change, 
among the scenarios, the status in 2023 shows the highest impact, while NZE exhibits the lowest 
impact, followed by APS and STEPS. The impact of hydrogen production decreases from 0.62 to 
around 0.06 kgCO2 eq/kWh in 2050, meaning a 90% reduction of emissions. These results are 
largely influenced by the electricity mix used for hydrogen production. As noted by Delpierre et 
al. (2021), the environmental impact of hydrogen production is primarily determined by electricity 
consumption at PtH plants rather than the materials used in manufacturing, emphasizing the 
critical role of the electricity mix. Thus, the true environmental impacts of each scenario relies 
almost ultimately on the realized electricity mix.

Regarding terrestrial acidification, in 2023, the primary contributor is hard coal within the 
electricity mix. By 2050, as coal usage declines, a new impact emerges from the increased use of 
nickel in electrolyzers. This shift is reflected in the growing impact of platinum group metal 
mining, a process required to obtain nickel. Additionally, as the energy mix shifts towards 
increasing renewable energy, the use of biofuels takes relevance. For simplification, in this 
model, biofuels in the electricity mix used for hydrogen production were modelled with the 
ecoinvent process of bioethanol from sweet sorghum: “ethanol production from sweet sorghum”. 
As it is observed in Figure 5, the impacts due to sweet sorghum production become relevant in 
2050 as well, however this should be interpreted not only as if the impacts derived from a specific 
crop but as an increasing impact of biomass in general used for energy purposes in this indicator. 
Impacts in 2050 for the STEPS scenarios is close to the impact in 2023. This is explained by the 
impact of electricity used to produce 1 kg of hydrogen at the plant. In 2050, hydrogen is produced 
entirely from electricity, with an energy mix that is still heavily dependent on fossil fuels. In 
contrast in 2023, hydrogen is produced from CG to a great extent, therefore the electricity 
demand its low but since the presence of fossil fuels in the mix is considerably high, the impacts 
are very similar to STEPS 2050. This is a trend that it is not observed for climate change since for 
that indicator, in 2023 there is the impact of the mining process of coal which adds is intensive in 
greenhouse gas emissions in general.

higher than the allocated share, whereas under Principle 2, it is approximately 93 times higher. In 
2050, among the projected scenarios, the Net Zero Emissions (NZE) scenario performs best but 
still exceeds the allocated share, by factors of 2 and 13 under P1 and P2, respectively. In contrast, 
under the Stated Policies Scenario (STEPS), the impact in 2050 could reach up to 43 times the 
allocated planetary boundary.  
As discussed in Section 3.1, hydrogen's share of total final energy demand remains low both in 
2023 and 2050 across projected scenarios. Even with an increase in electrolytic hydrogen 
production in 2050, this is insufficient to remain within the SOS. Results in Section 3.1 suggest 
that increasing electrolytic hydrogen production could help reduce coal and natural gas demand, 
thereby lowering environmental impacts such as climate change and particulate matter 
formation (PMF). Nevertheless, the cost of hydrogen production from PtH plants could refrain its 
widespread adoption since producing low emissions hydrogen today is 1.5 to 6 times more 
expensive than unabated fossil-based hydrogen (IEA, 2024c). 
The negative values that appear when calculating the global thresholds for climate change 
indicate that there is no remaining budget and instead there is a need to account for carbon 
capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS) technologies. As a result, the allocated SoSOS would be 
zero meaning that the denominator in the ASR equation would be zero, and the ASR would tend 
to infinity and it would not be possible to calculate it. In Table 8 those cases are represented by 
the “” symbol and a dark red color. 
This study assessed the global energy mix with hydrogen production using electricity from the 
projected electricity grid, which led to SOS transgressions for climate change. However, Weidner 
et al. (2023) conducted an AESA only from hydrogen and found that when hydrogen is produced 
exclusively from wind power, the allocated SOS for hydrogen for climate change is not exceeded. 
Future research should study the use of dedicated renewable energy for hydrogen production in 
the global energy mix and determine if with this set up, other global environmental thresholds are 
transgressed. As it was presented in section 3.1, the choice of a low carbon footprint energy mix 
could represent trade-offs in other impact categories. 
For fossil resource scarcity, principle 1 assigns a high share with 90% of the global threshold for 
the energy sector. The assigned share is surpassed in all scenarios except NZE, under P1, where 
the ratio is 0.45. This is in line with the fact that water electrolysis will become less carbon-
intensive along with the low-carbon energy transition (Wei et al., 2024). The decrease needed in 
fossil fuels in the production of hydrogen would help to relief the dependance of fossil fuels. 
However, according to the IEA (2021), scaling up electrolyzer deployment at the pace required in 
the NZE scenario presents a significant challenge due to current limitations in manufacturing 
capacity as well as ensuring sufficient electricity generation to support this expansion.

particulate matter formation, terrestrial acidification, and marine eutrophication, all scenarios in

Freshwater eutrophication, land occupation, and water consumption are the only indicators

allocated share is exceeded in all scenarios. Regarding water scarcity, the global energy mix 
remains sustainable according to both principles in 2023. However, by 2050, only with P2 the 
projected scenarios can be deemed sustainable, while under P1, the threshold is exceeded by 
17% in the APS scenario and 12% in the NZE scenario. Both indicators are closely linked to 
agricultural activities, which are expected to have a significant impact due to the increased 
reliance on biofuels in 2050 (IEA, 2024c). 
Additionally, water availability in countries planning to develop PtH installations should be 
considered. Approximately 40% of planned low-emission hydrogen production projects are 
located in water-stressed regions, making the use of diverse water sources and sustainable 
management strategies essential (IEA, 2024c). While electrolytic hydrogen production in 2023 is 
primarily concentrated in China, Europe, and the United States, together accounting for 
approximately 75% of global hydrogen production, the regional distribution is expected to shift 
by 2050. According to the Global Hydrogen Review (IEA, 2024c), regions with strong renewable 
energy potential, such as Latin America and Africa, could each represent 17% of the installed 
capacity, altering the global hydrogen landscape. Therefore, the assessment of water-related 
impacts must be conducted at a local scale, since environmental boundaries for water and land 
use depend on the local availability and scarcity of freshwater and land, varying significantly 
across locations. In contrast, the planetary carbon boundary can be applied to any specific 
region, regardless of location (Fang et al., 2015). 
Overall, while low emissions hydrogen production offers environmental benefits, they remain 
limited compared to the overall impacts of the full energy mix, particularly given the high energy 
demand in the STEPS scenario. Moreover, although the NZE scenario presents a promising 
pathway, it is still not absolutely sustainable when considering the climate change indicator. The 
findings are consistent with other studies. Lejeune et al. (2024) quantified the planetary footprint 
of global hydrogen production considering SSP1 - PkBug500 and concluded that even in the most 
sustainable scenario, global hydrogen production is unsustainable. The study detected potential 
trade-offs since using biomass-based sources for hydrogen production will significantly impact 
the biosphere integrity boundary. This confirms that the development of PtH technologies should 
be viewed as one component of a broader strategy to effectively address the impact on all the 
planetary boundaries.  
Finally, the results align with Weidner et al. (2023), who found that the transgression of the SoSOS 
in certain environmental indicators is less a consequence of hydrogen production itself and more 
a reflection of final energy consumption exceeding sustainable levels or agreed targets. 
Therefore, staying within planetary boundaries would require either tremendous  development of 
efficiency gains through alternative energy provision systems or a reduction in societal energy 
consumption.

3.3 Discussion on feasibility 
The feasibility of the global energy mix to remain below its assigned share of the SOS depends on 
two key factors: reducing the global energy demand and developing technologies to replace the 
use of fossil fuels.  
The first factor, related to reducing the global energy demand, is particularly challenging. Energy 
consumption continues to rise, especially in emerging and developing economies, driven by 
increased production and consumption and increasing wealth. A critical consideration is the 
energy rebound effect, where efficiency gains fail to offset rising energy use, leading to higher 
overall emissions (Brännlund et al., 2007). As energy efficiency improves, the effective per-unit 
price of energy services decreases, often stimulating higher energy consumption. Additionally, 
an indirect rebound effect could occur as cost savings from efficiency improvements allow for 
increased spending on other goods and services, which require energy for production and 
delivery. These behavioral, economic, and systemic responses ultimately drive higher overall 
consumption, potentially undermining sustainability benefits (Guzzo et al., 2025) 
The second factor involves the development of technologies to reduce dependence on fossil 
fuels. However, to date, challenges faced by project developers hinder faster progress. Producing 
renewable hydrogen today is generally one and a half to six times more expensive than unabated 
fossil-based production. Additionally, regulatory and demand uncertainty due to slow 
development of the market of low-emissions hydrogen have caused several projects to be 
cancelled (IEA, 2024c).   
Thus, achieving sustainable energy systems with a high share of PtH requires societies to further 
rely on technologies that promote reduced consumption and actively prioritize redirecting 
investments toward renewable sources. 
3.4 Uncertainties and limitations 
The study conducted involved some uncertainties derived from assumptions that in some cases 
were needed to fill out data gaps. 
Regarding the modelling of the global energy mix, the selected processes from ecoinvent include 
only emissions associated with the production of different energy sources, excluding emissions 
derived from their consumption. Emissions from the use of the energy sources e.g. combustion 
of hard coal were not considered due to uncertainties in their calculation. Additionally, the energy 
sources reported in the total final energy consumption of the IEA include their uses as feedstock 
and also as fuels. Including emissions from combustion would have meant to disaggregate them 
by their use as feedstock or fuel, a task that is recommended to perform in further research to 
increase the accuracy of the results.  
Furthermore, when modelling the global energy mix, it was assumed that the distribution of 
energy production by country remains constant over time. However, this distribution is likely to 
evolve in future years. This would depend on the availability of resources and the national policies 
established in each country.  
For simplification of biofuels modelling, ethanol coming only from sorghum crops was used as a 
reference for all biofuels. However, this assumption may not hold for 2050, as first-generation 
biomass might be deprioritized for energy production due to increasing global food demand. 
Instead, second-generation biomass could become the primary source for biofuel production. 
==PAGE:31==
28 
 
Moreover, it is important to mention that in the APS and NZE scenarios, the increased use of 
biofuels in the global energy mix may lead to indirect land-use changes, which could impact the 
climate change indicator. However, they were excluded from the modelling. If included, the 
climate change impact would likely be even higher. 
The SSP scenarios and IAMs used in combination with LCA primarily focus on costs, energy, and 
greenhouse gas emissions. As a result, estimating indicators beyond climate change (e.g., 
toxicity, eutrophication) carries the risk of over- or underestimation. This limitation in capturing 
diverse environmental impacts poses a significant challenge for decision-making, as it may lead 
to unintended burden shifts across impact categories (Bruhn et al., 2023). Additionally, the 
choice of REMIND scenarios can significantly influence the results. Therefore, it is recommended 
that future studies should explore different IAMs or SSPs to better assess the implications of 
technology evolution forecasts on climate impacts.  
This study aimed to assess the absolute environmental performance of the global energy mix, 
including electrolytic hydrogen, in a specific year. However, the grandfathering allocation 
principle is particularly sensitive to the reference year chosen for defining the status quo 
(Hjalsted et al., 2021). Therefore, to establish a benchmark and evaluate the environmental 
impact trends of the global energy mix over time, conducting a dynamic assessment in future 
research would be beneficial. Additionally, environmental budgets calculated are sensitive to the 
choice of sharing principle. Thus, when conducting an AESA, it is important to detail and justify 
the sharing principle applied so that the results can be interpreted properly.  
Finally, potential trade-offs due to planetary boundary interactions, as explored by Lejeune et al. 
(2024) were not considered in this study. Assessing critical interactions between Earth’s 
biophysical systems remains an important area for future research since this could lead to a 
strong amplification of impact.

4 Conclusion and recommendations 
This thesis assessed the development of PtH implementation and its performance within the 
global energy system from an absolute sustainability perspective. This study aimed to cover the 
data gap in the literature since most of the sustainability assessments have been carried out 
using LCIs of today, however most of the hydrogen production capacity will be deployed in the 
future with efficiency and decarbonization increases. Results obtained were compared to the 
current status of the energy mix (2023) where hydrogen production relies mostly on fossil fuels. 
Even though the total amount of hydrogen increases considerably in 2050 in all the projected 
scenarios, its impact per kWh decreases around 90%, from 0.62 to 0.06 kg CO2 eq/kWh when 
compared to 2023. The decrease is mainly due to the electricity mix used in the operation of the 
PtH plant. In 2023 it was mainly relying on fossil fuels, whereas in 2050, the mix changes 
according to the progress on the climate target projected by the IEA. Results show that the 
materials needed for the construction of the PtH do not have a high impact for the climate change 
indicator. Nevertheless, burden-shifting is observed when analyzing other impact indicators such 
as terrestrial acidification where the mining operations for obtaining nickel take relevance. At the 
same time in marine eutrophication, the production of ethanol, in this model represented by 
sweet sorghum production presents increasing impacts. 
After evaluating the environmental impact of electrolytic hydrogen independently, the global 
energy mix is evaluated. From that broader perspective it is observed that despite the high 
potential of PtH to contribute to lower the impacts in the climate change category, its share is still 
low to be enough to have a considerable effect in the global energy mix. 
Additionally, energy consumption is expected to increase by 20% in the STEPS scenario when 
compared to 2023. However, in the APS and NZE, it decreases by 2% and 23% respectively. In all 
three scenarios, the energy efficiency increases to a different degree which is represented by the 
premise datasets. The increase in energy consumption in the STEPS scenario can be explained 
by a rebound effect, while for APS and NZE the rebound effect is expected to be limited due to 
stricter policies implementation, higher efficiency levels and the increase of electrification in 
different sectors. Energy consumption has decreased caused by high energy efficiency, meaning 
that the positive effects (energy efficiency) are not being offset by higher consumption at the user 
level. 
Furthermore, results from the AESA show that the SoSOS has been transgressed in around half 
of the indicators analysed, including climate change indicator, despite the decarbonization of the 
energy mix in the projected scenarios. Results evidence that PtH implementation cannot meet 
climate change mitigation targets by itself and that a more stringent reduction in fossil fuels 
consumption is needed along with carbon capture technologies. Additionally, it is important to 
note that the scenarios and energy mixes projected by the IEA in 2050 are set around the climate 
change category, which may cause burden shifting by decreasing the use of fossil fuels and 
increasing electrification, as well as the share of renewable energy in the energy mix.  
It was observed as well that the transgression of the SoSOS in certain environmental indicators 
is driven less by hydrogen production itself and more by final energy consumption exceeding 
sustainable thresholds or agreed targets. Therefore, remaining within planetary boundaries 
would require either significant efficiency improvements through alternative energy systems or a 
reduction in overall societal energy consumption. Additionally, some ASR could not be calculated 
==PAGE:33==
30 
 
since for some approaches such as the planetary boundaries threshold, the global 
environmental threshold has already been surpassed.  
Based on the results, to remain within the planetary boundaries, policy makers should consider 
that the scalability of PtH has to be done at a faster pace. To date, its development has been 
hindered by regulatory and demand uncertainty. Thus, slow development of the market of low-
emissions hydrogen has caused several projects to be cancelled. However, it is important to 
consider that PtH is just one component in a broader set of strategies that should be formulated 
considering not only climate change but other environmental impact categories. At the same 
time, the possible rebound effect caused by increased energy efficiency should be taken into 
consideration. Therefore, along with the different technologies aimed to lower emissions, there 
is a need of reduction in energy consumption.  
Future research should explore the impact of carbon capture technologies and assess whether 
they have the potential to keep the global energy mix's impacts below the SoSOS across various 
environmental indicators. Such analysis would provide a more comprehensive understanding of 
the global energy mix model and enhance the accuracy of the results.

6 Supplementary information 
6.1 AE and PEM electrolysers 
AE is the oldest and the most mature technology. This type of electrolyser contains two 
electrodes separated by a gas-tight diaphragm and immersed into a liquid electrolyte (see Figure 
12). An alkaline solution consisting of 30 % aqueous potassium hydroxide (KOH) is commonly 
used as an electrolyte (Dahiru et al., 2022). Besides KOH, other alkaline electrolytes such 
as sodium hydroxide (NaOH), sodium chloride (NaCl) are less commonly used. The anode is 
usually made of nickel or nickel coated steel, while the cathode is made of steel activated by a 
coating with different catalysts (Bhandari et al., 2014) to allow quick pairing of atomic hydrogen 
into pairs at the electrode surface and increases the rate of hydrogen production (Koroneos et al., 
2004) 
In contrast to AE, PEM electrolyzers do not require any liquid electrolyte. The electrolyte here is a 
thin solid polymer electrolyte (membrane) (see Figure 12). The most commonly used membrane 
in commercial systems is Nafion (Carmo et al., 2013). On both sides of the membrane, thin 
electrodes are directly bonded to the surface making a membrane electrode assembly. The 
electrodes contain noble metal catalysts, typically platinum-based at the cathode and iridium-
based at the anode (Bareiß et al., 2019) which are used due to the corrosive and acidic nature of 
the proton exchange membrane. However, the high cost of these materials and the membrane 
materials has been a major challenge of PEM process  (Dahiru et al., 2022). Despite being used 
in small-scale installations to date, PEM offers stronger potential for further technological 
developments such as higher efficiency. Additionally, the dynamic power response is more 
flexible, making it suitable to deal with intermittent electricity production (Delpierre et al., 2021).
