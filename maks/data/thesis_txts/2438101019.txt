Abstract
This Master Thesis is focused on an analysis made for European Energy to investigate if they
should invest in the PV/T solar technology in Esbjerg and to see how suitable the investment of
an extra-large pit storage in Denmark and Esbjerg would be. Thus, the goal is to do an appropri-
ate assessment in both ﬁelds. This objective is divided in two diﬀerent parts, in one, a technical
analysis is done on the PV & PVT, in the other, the role of seasonal extra-large heat storage in
2020 & 2030 is analyzed through the Balmorel model.
Regarding the methodology, the applied approach consisted of two separate process for each
objective. For the ﬁrst one, a PV & PV/T analysis has been performed under diﬀerent conditions,
ﬁve PV/T panels and a PV panel were used in this study. Then, concerning the second objective,
to address the role of heat storage from an energy systems perspective two types of seasonal pit heat
storage (extra-large pit storage and large pit storage) were analyzed . For this study, the Balmorel
model was used to investigate the Danish energy system in four diﬀerent scenarios. Moreover, the
model was ﬁrst upgraded by the addition of the grid tariﬀand a more realistic solar heating proﬁle
with an average of the solar heating full load hours across Denmark.
The results from the PV & PV/T analysis showed that the mean ﬂuid temperature has a central
role in the PV/T panels thermal performance, the lower the mean ﬂuid temperature the higher
the thermal performance. In addition, it inﬂuences the electrical eﬃciency of the PV part through
cooling the PV cell, the lower PV cell temperature the higher the electrical eﬃciency. Therefore,
results obtained show that the combined performance of a PV/T panel achieves a higher yearly
performances for Esbjerg than the simple PV.
Results from the Balmorel model pointed out that investing in new extra-large pit storage is
more proﬁtable than investing in large pit storage. Not only because of the total system cost,
but also because it provides more penetration of renewables and less CO2 emissions in the Danish
energy system than when the large pit storage is installed.
These results support it would be very proﬁtable for Denmark to invest in new generation ca-
pacities of solar heating and extra-large heat storages by 2020 & 2030 as well as combine both.

Chapter 1
Overview of the thesis objective
European Energy plans to start the EE GigaStorage project in 2020 at Esbjerg [8]. Esbjerg is situ-
ated on the West coast of Denmark. This project provides an energy storage solution for district
heating and cooling, in which the storage system will store energy from wind turbines, solar panels
and surplus heating from waste incineration. Using large heat pumps, EE GigaStorage boosts and
stores the energy in water ponds.
The overall objective of this thesis is to analyze which solar technology is more suitable to be
used in Esbjerg within PV/T or PV. Simultaneously an other objective has to be tackled, which is
to study the role of heat storage for the same area and in Denmark for 2020 & 2030.
In order to approach the ﬁrst step, an analysis of diﬀerent solar technologies has been done.
Five PV/T panels and one PV panel from the market were taken to study their behaviour under
test conditions and Esbjerg conditions. Moreover, a study of the PV cell temperature dependent
on the electrical performance was developed in order to see how this is inﬂuenced. This methodo-
logy was undertaken with the aim to compare the behaviour between PV and PV/T panels under
Esbjerg conditions.
Subsequently, to engage the second step, after suitable input data from European Energy was
obtained, the Danish Energy System was simulated with the Balmorel model in order to evaluate
the role of heat storage under four scenarios from 2020 to 2030.
The whole thesis structure has been divided into Introduction, Methodology, Data, Results and
Discussion&Conclusion. In each part the procedure of the two main steps will be found separately.

2.1
State of the art of solar technologies
Until recently photovoltaic and solar thermal energy technologies were not compatible. In most
cases, one of the technologies has to be chosen. Those possibilities have led companies to experi-
ment and overcome this barrier by developing a new technology: a hybrid solar panel (PV/T).
Solar energy is usually utilized after it has been converted into certain energy forms suitable
for certain applications. These forms include electrical energy and thermal energy, which can be
produced by photovoltaic/thermal (PV/T) systems. In this hybrid system, both electrical energy
and thermal energy are produced at the same time.[9]
One of the main objectives of using PVT panels is to increase the electrical eﬃciency of PV, by
decreasing the normal operating cell temperature by cooling the panel with a ﬂuid or air. In this
project the thermal eﬃciency of PVT panels with and without PV in operation is investigated. The
thermal eﬃciency is strongly inﬂuenced by having a PV on top, which can be in operation or not
(further called as PV on and PV oﬀ). Besides this, the performance of a PV/T panel is very de-
pendent on the energy losses from the top surface. A schematic of a PV/T panel is shown in ﬁg. 2.1.
As [10] explains, in the solar industry the most common form of encapsulation is cross-linkable
ethylene vinyl acetate (EVA). One of the disadvantages of EVA ﬁlm is that it is not UV-resistant
and therefore protective front glass is required for the UV screen. For standard modules that use
EVA encapsulation, usually a layer of tedlar composite (tedlar polyester tedlar (TPT)) is used for
the backing. This is a thin, opaque ﬁlm which protects the solar cells from humidity, harsh physical
and chemical environments, and guarantees total electrical insulation.
In a standard ﬂuid-based system, a working ﬂuid, typically water or glycol, is then piped through
a heat exchanger (typically a series of pipes in an insulated box), which is ﬁxed to the back of a
PV panel. The heat from the PV cells is conducted through the heat exchanger and absorbed by
the working ﬂuid (presuming that the working ﬂuid is cooler than the operating temperature of
the cells). The heat collected can then be transferred to a thermal store.

Seasonal heat storage technologies
Seasonal heat storages are considered essential for district heating systems due to the ﬂexibility they
oﬀer integrating diﬀerent ﬂuctuating renewable energy sources, [11]. The four main seasonal heat
storage technologies are tank thermal energy storage systems (TTES), pit thermal energy storage
(PTES), aquifer thermal energy storage (ATES) and borehole thermal energy storage (BTES), [2].
Large scale water pit thermal energy storages are viable solutions both economically and envir-
onmentally since these storages are simple in construction and relatively cheap. The construction
of the storage is cheaper than the thermal energy storage tank because less structural material
is required for the construction. Besides this, the larger the seasonal storage is, the better is the
thermal performance, [12].

4.1
Model choice
The plan was to identify a suitable modelling tool for analyzing the integration of renewable energy
into an energy system. According to literature [25], it is evident that there is no modelling tool that
addresses all issues related to integrating renewable energy, but the perfect modelling tool depends
completely on the speciﬁc objectives which should be achieved [25]. The main reason for choosing
the Balmorel model between all the existing energy system models was the system aggregation,
because the main objective of this part of the thesis was to analyze the heat storage in Denmark
and in Esbjerg, which the last is an area. For example TIMES-DK or EnergyPLAN models have
3 DH groups [26] whereas the Balmorel model handles 21 DH areas, so there was the possibility to
easily disaggregate the Esbjerg area and study it alone.
The Balmorel model was chosen because it is an optimization tool that can simulate multiple
years in an hourly-time step resolution. It optimizes both operation and investment of a combined
electricity and District heating (DH) system in an international perspective, focused on minimiz-
ing annualized costs of the energy system. The system aggregation is ﬂexible at three levels (11
countries, 21 DH areas and 2 regions in DK), thus it was easy to isolate Esbjerg.
Other reasons for choosing Balmorel over other models are that aside from the usual exogenous
input of heat and electricity demand, it includes fuel prices and emissions, eﬃciencies and costs of
plants, as well as hourly distribution of demands and production from RE sources. In addition, it
includes capacities of existing plants, which is very relevant for the possible new generation capa-

4.2
The Balmorel model
Balmorel (for BALtic Model of Regional Electricity Liberalized) is an open-source linear optim-
ization model, which was developed by Hans Ravn et al.
in 2001 and is implemented in the
GAMS modelling language. As mentioned before in section 4.1, the Balmorel model[27] simulates
investment and operations of a combined electricity and District heating system in an interna-
tional overview, minimizing an objective total cost function. The model is designed through a
socio-economic perspective, to fulﬁll the energy demand of a selected time horizon in the chosen
countries. Supply of electricity and heat are given by generation technologies such as storage types
(electricity and heat), variable renewable generation (solar, hydro and wind), thermal power plants
(extraction and backpressure), electrical and fuel boilers and heat pumps. Electricity transmission
between regions is constrained by available capacities, while distribution of electricity and heat is
represented by losses and costs, [27].
In the model, the investment decisions are made depending on the operation and investment
costs. Investments may be made in generation technologies and in electricity transmission capacity.
Therefore, the model optimizes long-term investments and short-term energy dispatch.

4.2.1
Energy system modelling
The model base algorithm employed is called BALBASE4 (BB4), [27]. There are currently four
base models utilized in Balmorel. The version BB4 allows for solving a sequence of years with en-
dogenous simultaneous investments considering a perfect foresight for all the years time segments,
[27]. The purpose of addon BB4 is to permit a model with endogenous investments that treats two
or more years in an integrated fashion. BB4 additionally do employ a good look-ahead mechanism
for investment decisions. For example, if the future is diﬀerent (e.g. environmental goals), then
there may be investments now that will appear unfavorable later.
Geographically, the model is divided into three categories: countries, regions and areas. The
countries modelled in this thesis are Denmark, Norway, Finland, Germany, Netherlands, Sweden
and United Kingdom. Each country is divided into a number of regions and the regions are divided
into areas.
Countries and regions allow electric power transmission between each other given
inter-connectors. However, in the areas the heat demand of each is balanced by district heating.
Figure 4.2 shows an illustration of the geographical structure of the Balmorel model, [29]. Denmark
was modelled as a country which contains 2 regions (East called 2 and West called 1) and 14 areas
(7 in each region). Regarding the area resolution, Denmark is modelled in 10 DH areas. The DH
areas total demand and hourly variation proﬁles are deﬁned by projections created by the Danish
Energy Agency, [30]. For the remain countries, a minimal amount of DH areas is selected.

ﬁrst year of the submodel, and perform the next optimization including the endogenous optimiza-

Time slices selected
The modelled years are 2020 and 2030 with equal weight on the objective function. Both years were

installed is 20 years.
Due to high computational time, the time resolution within each simulated year was limited to
8 weeks, each week was simulated with 84 hours, which in total is 672 individual time steps. This
decision was made in order to have as reasonable computational time (below 10 hours) and still
have a good number of weeks. The eight chosen seasons, which are S06, S12, S18, S24, S30, S36,
S42, S48, capture distinct heating demands, wind proﬁles and solar thermal proﬁles and ought to
represent a typical Danish year.

4.5
Model upgrades
In this section, the development from a given reference model to an upgraded model is explained step
by step. The countries modelled are Denmark, Norway, Finland, Germany, Netherlands, Sweden
and United Kingdom. Even though in this thesis the focus was on Denmark, the additional coun-
tries are included to understand the energy systems eﬀects of the electrical power exchange between
them and the impact in Denmark.
Two parallel models were run, one focused on Denmark and the other on Esbjerg, the second
model has Esbjerg as a disaggregated area from DK1 Large.
As a starting point, it was decided to use the reference model based on section 4.3. Even though
the Flex4RES database seemed reasonably good, it lacked a electricity grid tariﬀapplied to the
heat pumps and electric boilers, as well as solar heating proﬁles that are compatible with Denmark
and therefore a good approximation of the solar heating full load hours in Denmark.
Hence, a modiﬁcation of these three inputs was done. The addition of these modiﬁcations to
the model (electricity grid tariﬀand solar heating proﬁles with its correspondents full load hours)
was in order to upgrade model.
4.5.1
First modiﬁcation: Grid tariﬀ
Thus, in order to upgraded the model, ﬁrst, the electricity grid tariﬀwas added to the heat pumps
and electric boilers, which inﬂuences the total production from both technologies.
This addition implies socioeconomic costs of utilizing the electricity grid. Nowadays, no tariﬀ
is imposed to the DH network in the model, the loss is the only thing that is considered in the DH
network. Therefore, adding a tariﬀto the DH network will be a future work, but it will not impact
consumption, only increase costs.
The socioeconomic cost of utilizing electricity is stated in the socioeconomic assumptions [34],
it is in particular the value 193 kr/MWh. This value was converted to the appropriate currency
used in the model and added it to the heat pumps and electric boilers variable operating and main-
tenance costs.

Chapter 7
Discussion
7.1
Limitations of the Energy System Model
• The ﬁrst idea was to build a scenario with the new extra-large pit storage technology plus
the standard large pit storage from the Technology Catalogue. The aim was to observe the
competition between the extra-large pit storage and the large pit storage for large areas. This
was not possible due to the Balmorel model’s limitation concerning same technology types
per area. The problem was that if both pit heat storages are implemented in the model at the
same time, the model will choose to install both in the same area. In that case, the model will
use the unload capacity of the large pit (which is faster) to charge the sum of both storage
capacities and this is totally wrong. The problem can be ﬁxed but due to lack of time it was
decided to separate them in two diﬀerent scenarios (EE and TC).
• No tariﬀimposed in District heating made the DH network cheaper than it is.
• Further investigation regarding the charging of the DH network and connection between water
tank and pit storage should be tackled.
7.2
Future investigations
Future works could extend the thesis scope and depth to include several other components unex-
plored due to time and logistical constraints.
• Develop the PV/T technology as a new technology in the model including data from the
market.
• Add solar proﬁles for all the areas in Denmark or at least ﬁve solar proﬁles, which can be
calculated based on the DMI report.[22, 23]
• Further development in the methodology for selecting the time slices can be completely
changed. Currently it is almost impossible to simulate the entire year due to computational
time and computers capacity, so a selection of the weeks that better represent the whole year
is needed.
• Other projects as TIMES-DK shows that the solar PV is more used in the system than what
it is obtained in this thesis. A challenging future work could be investigate why with these
studied scenarios the model choose to invest less in solar PV than what was expected, even
though the solar PV data is based on the same sources.

Chapter 8
Conclusions
This chapter is dedicated to summarize the conclusions explained in the chapters 5 and 6 about
the PV/T solar technology and the role of the heat storage in Esbjerg and Denmark.
8.1
Conclusions regarding PV/T solar technology
• From the analysis it can ﬁrstly be concluded that PV/T panels with PV ON have a thermal
performance of about 12% lower than panels with PV OFF at a normal mean ﬂuid temper-
ature of 10◦C, due to transformation of solar irradiation to electricity. Since the PV cells are
placed above the thermal part, the cells utilize part of the solar radiation. Thus the thermal
part, placed below the cells, obtains less solar radiation to produce heat. Besides this, similar
results were obtained from analysis regarding the variation of the collector tilt. Following, all
panels show the same trend for diﬀerent collector tilts. Nevertheless, the PV/T with PV ON
combination of thermal and electrical performance achieves higher total output than PV/T
with PV OFF.
• The second conclusion which can be made is that the mean ﬂuid temperature plays a central
role in the thermal performance. Results have shown that yearly thermal performance is
highly inﬂuenced by the mean ﬂuid temperature. Moreover, setting the mean ﬂuid temper-
ature to 10◦C, the PV/T with PV OFF, creates yearly performances which are in average
12% higher than PV/T with PV in operation.
This diﬀerence continues to increase un-
til 70%, which corresponds to a mean ﬂuid temperature of 50◦C. However, in both modes
(PV ON/OFF), the lower mean ﬂuid temperature is set the higher thermal performance is
achieved.
• When comparing the electrical modules of the PV/T panels, results show that the PV cell
temperature plays a central role in the photovoltaic conversion process, as the electrical
eﬃciency of a PV module depends linearly on Tc value decreasing with it. The lower PV cell
temperature the higher the cell eﬃciency. The best cell performance was obtained for the
Fototherm panel, after which the Twinsun and the Wiosun panels performed the best.
• Moreover, electrical eﬃciencies of PV panels depend on yearly calculations of the PV cell
temperature (Tc). The performance varies during the year, in summer PV panels have a low
performance. This is due to the high PV cell temperature (40◦C).
• Results show that the combined perfomance of a PV/T panel with the PV in operation achieve
the highest yearly performance. Thus, Racell and Wiosun panels are the most suitable to be

8.2
Conclusions regarding the heat storage
• The improvement of the model has provided a further realistic overview of the energy system
in Denmark and an enhancement in the obtained results.
• The fact that the TC scenario, in which the large pit storage is installed, has the highest total
system cost in Denmark in 2020 & 2030, and adding both years, this scenario also has the
highest fuel prices and CO2 tax, implies that the European Energy Company made the right
choice in installing an extra-large pit storage in Esbjerg. Thus, the results showed that more
new investments in heat capacity of extra-large pit storage around Denmark is proﬁtable and
have a big role, not just through the lower total system cost, but also due to the greater
penetration of renewables and the decrease in CO2 emissions.
• Adding the limitation on waste incineration in Esbjerg will just increase the total cost in
Denmark by 1%. Beside this, it will increase the new investments in solar heating for 2030
even more, which could be interesting for European Energy future projects, as this technology
seems to be the perfect one to charge the pit storage.
• It was interesting to see how for both Denmark and Esbjerg, the model decided to invest
more on heat storage in the scenarios that have the extra-large pit storage, which is the
one is going to be installed by European Energy, than when the system has the Technology
Catalogue standard large pit storage.
• Regarding the production in Denmark, if no limitation is applied to onshore wind, the total
production will be higher with the extra-large pit storage installed in the system that the
large pit storage. Nevertheless, just focusing on the production by the seasonal pit storage
turns out that in 2020 the highest production will be by large pit storage whereas in 2030 the
highest will be by the extra-large pit storage.
• Concerning the total production in Esbjerg, the highest production was obtained by the
scenarios which have the extra-large pit storage installed. Although the results show that will
be more heat production by the large pit storage during the year due to its higher unloading
capacity (665 MW) than from extra-large pit storage, which has an unloading capacity of
around 310 MW for Esbjerg.
• From the sensitivity analysis can be concluded that if the solar heating systems in Denmark
will improve their performance, leading to an increase of the full load hours by about 910
FLH, then the total system cost will decrease whereas the solar heating production extremely
increases.
8.3
General conclusions
The results show that in Denmark it will be proﬁtable to invest in new generation capacities of
solar heating by 2020 & 2030. Therefore, combining heat storage and solar heating is the suitable
path.

could be an attractive future project.

This means that on the one hand, when the electricity prices are low, the PV/T technology can
produce electricity and supply the system while its producing heat and charging it to the storage.
On the other hand, when the electricity prices are high, the PV/T technology can use the mode
