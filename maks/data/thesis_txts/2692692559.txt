Abstract
The ability of a navigator or onboard computer control system to maintain an accurate
positioning estimate is crucial for the overall safety of the vessel and required according
to International Maritime Organization Resolution A.529. This task is further complicated
by sailing in open water where the Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) is often-
times the sole provider of positioning information, since there are no external objects or
landmarks. As such, a vessel seeking a higher degree of autonomy must ensure that the
onboard systems are able to recognize malfunctioning in the GNSS.
This thesis approaches the problem of making the navigation system of a marine sur-
face vessel cyber-resilient. Standard marine vessel sensor systems are considered and
additional sensing systems are proposed such that a GNSS-independent navigation sys-
tem can be formulated. An observer is thus designed to fit this purpose, utilizing the
Marginalized Particle Filter framework for an Inertial Navigation System aided with mag-
netic anomaly field maps. This results in navigation state estimates which are compared
against GNSS measurements to form residuals utilized for fault detection.
All components of the monitoring system are tested in simulation. The navigation system
is able to achieve sufficient navigation accuracy despite not using the GNSS measure-
ment. The statistical properties of the residual signals are then analyzed and change
detection algorithms designed. The resulting detection modules demonstrate the ability
to detect a cyber-attack, deemed subtle according to the literature, on the GNSS.

Introduction
Trustworthy and accurate sensor information is crucial for the operation of a vessel, even
more so for vessels operating with limited to zero reliance on human intervention. Unin-
tended faults or cyber-attacks in marine navigation systems pose a great risk to the ability
of the vessel to operate safely, if at all.
According to International Maritime Organization (IMO) [19], ‚ÄúThe navigator needs to be
able to determine his position at all times.‚Äù As such, the challenge of identifying either a
faulty or spoofed sensor and managing the navigation has been left to the navigator. This
creates a reliance on human operators to notice and compensate for such malfunctions in
real-time where in open-water navigation, the availability of information from the environ-
ment can be sparse. This problem becomes debilitating with respect to vessels striving for
higher levels of autonomy as here the vessel must function as expected with minimal or
potentially no human intervention, leaving identification and handling of erroneous sensor
information up to the on-board navigation system.
The problem becomes increasingly difficult considering that when sailing through open
water there is little to no information to be taken from the environment through traditional
perception sensors like radar or vision systems. As such, information sourced from on-
board sensing systems which do not make assumptions regarding the availability of other
vessels or identifiable objects will be vital. This is where alternative positioning systems,
such as terrain-aided, can rise to meet the requirements.
Problem Statement
The problem that this thesis deals with is making the navigation system of a marine surface
vessel cyber-resilient in open-water sailing scenarios. The overall problem is to create a
condition monitoring system for the vulnerable sensing systems, i.e. Global Navigation
Satellite System (GNSS), which can be divided into subproblems addressing Navigation
and Detection defined as:
‚Ä¢ Navigation: Defined as the ‚ÄúAccurate determination of the vehicle state (e.g. posi-
tion, velocity, and attitude)‚Äù [8, chp. 1].
‚Ä¢ Detection: ‚ÄúBeing able to decide when an event of interest has occurred‚Äù [23,
chp. 1].
which then results in the following problem decomposition:
‚Ä¢ Navigation: Develop sensor fusion algorithms for the robust estimation of naviga-
tion parameters.
‚Ä¢ Detection: Develop algorithms for the detection of GNSS malfunctioning due to
faults or cyber-attacks.
Note, that what is referred to as the vehicle state in [8] is also called the navigation state
or navigation parameters, which is the terminology that will be used here.
Contribution
The approach of this thesis is to combine aided navigation with detection theory in order
to build a monitoring system for the GNSS. This is possible by constructing a navigation

The navigation module foundation is a standard Aided Inertial Navigation System, where
the version developed here will substitute the GNSS with the high accuracy magnetome-
ter and integrate various standard marine vessel sensors. The navigation state estimate
will then be utilized, together with GNSS measurements, to form residuals of position and
velocity. The statistical properties of the residuals are thereafter analyzed and appropri-
ate generalized likelihood ratio tests are derived. The detection module thus applies the
derived generalized likelihood ratio tests to the residuals for fault monitoring.
In conclusion, the novel part of this contribution is the designed GNSS monitoring system,
i.e. the application of a terrain-aided navigation system, designed with respect to a marine
surface vessel, for generating residuals to be used in the fault detection of the GNSS. This
monitoring system is also, thereafter verified against a fault scenario inspired by [3]. The
fault will be injected as a change of mean in the starboard velocity of the craft and the
resulting monitoring performance will be presented.

Literature Review
As this thesis handles topics from different disciplines, the literature can be divided by
topic: Vulnerabilities and Detection, Navigation, and Other. Vulnerabilities and Detec-
tion have been grouped together as many manuscripts documenting a vulnerability also
make attempts towards detection. Navigation covers works which document the usage
of terrain-motivated sensors in conjunction with more typical navigation sensor systems.
Other contains works which do not necessarily fit so well in the aforementioned categories,
but nonetheless contain information which has effected this work in a significant way.
Vulnerabilities and Detection
Vulnerabilities to cyber-attacks have been shown for the Automatic Identification System
(AIS) [2]Õæ the Electronic Chart and Display Information System (ECDIS) [48, 49]Õæ the radar
system [50]Õæ the GNSS [14]. Here, emphasis is placed on GNSS: jamming and spoofing
represent the most threatening scenario since this system is the primary source of position
information onboard vessels.
The GNSS system‚Äôs vulnerability to cyber-threats has been demonstrated for both simple
and complex attack strategies in [35], [21], and [43]. In [31] an analysis is made regarding
the effects of spoofing in sensor fused systems, in this case GNSS with Inertial Measure-
ment Unit (IMU). The capture of a Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) by GNSS spoofing
is presented in [25]. Several spoofing and detection strategies are compared in [45], in-
cluding pseudo-range monitoring, fusion with IMU, and physical antenna setup changes.
GNSS fused with IMU detection is documented in [26]. In [3] Bhatti et al. demonstrate
spoofing a vessel, leading the navigator astray, and the difficulties in timely detection with
real sea-trials. Prior to this thesis, we have also conducted work in analyzing the fault diag-
nosis potential on a marine vessel during coastal navigation with typical sensor systems
in [41].
Navigation
Alternative methods of positioning have also been investigated in the literature, although
mostly for aircraft and not typically as a method for detecting faults in the GNSS. In his
thesis, [6] proposes an inertial navigation solution utilizing a magnetometer and magnetic
anomaly field charts as the aiding sensor as opposed to a GNSS system, this is made
possible with the help of a Rao-Blackwellized Particle Filter (PF). Feng et al. [9] utilize
geomagnetic information to bound the errors of an Inertial Navigation System (INS) in a
passive geomagnetic navigation system, they introduce a mean diurnal correction method
and demonstrate the performance increase of compensating for diurnal variations. In [51],
Teixeira et al. demonstrates, experimentally the performance of a particle filter magnetic
navigation implementation for underwater robotic vehicles, the solution is validated in a
trial using real magnetic and navigation data using standard navigation sensors and a
total field magnetometer. In [22], Jung et al.l utilizes a magnetometer in additional to
other standard marine surface vessel sensors for unmanned navigation. In [28, 27] Lager
et al. demonstrate the navigation potential of using terrain information from anomaly field
and depth maps.
Other
Other works which have impacted the author of this thesis include [10] and their con-
tributions towards marine system modeling and control, [24, 23] and their contributions
towards detection theory, [4] and their contributions to systematic fault monitoring, and [8,
15] in their descriptions of Earth reference frames and inertial navigation systems.

the trajectory development, navigation state estimation performance, and the detection
design and performance.

This chapter presents a discussion of the results introduced in
chapter 6.

Frame Transformations
Given that one is working with several frames, it is vital to be able to rotate between frames.
There are three main ways to represent such a rotation: Euler angles, Direction Cosine
Matrix (DCM), and quaternions.
Euler angles are an intuitive representation but unfortunately, they suffer from a variety of
problems stemming from their sequential nature, e.g., singularities. The DCM approach
solves some failings of Euler angles, although not without its own problems, e.g., orthonor-
malization as well as an increase in dimension. Quaternions allow for a mathematically

intuition and increased complexity.

Terrain-aided positioning, in the context of this work, will look into utilizing terrain informa-

3.3.1
Magnetic Navigation
Magnetic navigation is an attractive alternative to many of the other aforementioned po-
sitioning methods for several reasons, the most significant of which are: availability and
trustworthiness.
Unlike other positioning methods, e.g., vision or landmark-based, magnetic anomaly fields
are available world-wide in addition to not being strongly affected by daylight nor weather
conditions. In addition to availability, the magnetic anomaly field is also much more difficult
to jam. As stated in [6], the power from the magnetic field of a dipole decays with a distance
d at the rate of d‚àí5 while radio signals decay at a rate of d‚àí2. This means that disrupting a
magnetic field source at some distance d requires much more energy than a radio-based
source.
The greatest drawbacks of magnetic navigation present themselves in questions of po-
tential accuracy. Magnetic navigation is affected by the magnetic fields generated by the
vessel in question, which can be difficult or even impossible to calibrate without proper
equipment. Magnetic navigation also suffers from poor map quality, especially naval nav-
igation, given that many magnetic anomaly surveying missions are flown at altitude and
downwards-continuing readings is an error prone operation [6, sec. 5.3], although it can
be possible [44, 30].

4.2
Inertial Navigation
A typical navigation solution which will be explored here is inertial navigation. Inertial
navigation is a form of dead-reckoning where acceleration and angular velocity measure-
ments are integrated to estimate the position, velocity, and orientation to form an INS.
Generally, this does not work very well over long time horizons, as both gyroscopes and
accelerometers suffer from bias drift over time, which if integrated will result in divergence
of estimates from true values.
This is more of a problem for cheaper sensors, expensive IMU units feature high-accuracy
accelerometers and gyroscopes with much slower bias drifts. That being said, eventually
they will still driftÕæ as a result an IMU provides very accurate information over a short time
period but needs assistance to retain accuracy. This is where aiding comes in, a lower
frequency aiding sensor, e.g. GNSS, can be used to estimate errors and re-calibrate the
IMU online.

7
Discussion
This chapter will discuss the results presented in chapter 6, the structure has been split
to mirror the division of chapter 6.
7.1
Trajectory
The trajectory and resulting measurement data that was used in section 6.1 is derived from
a kinematics-based simulation. As mentioned, this is due to not being able to go on a sea
trial and capture actual measurements, which would have been the ideal outcome. That
being said, although the kinematics trajectory served its purpose, assuming no availability
of real data, a dynamics based simulation could be interesting for the added realism, e.g.
in nonzero roll/pitch dynamics.
Utilizing [12], one could assume a certain vessel class, e.g., container, and utilize the
corresponding dynamics implementation with some waypoint-based guidance law, e.g.,
lookahead-based steering [10, p. 10.3.2]. The ideal case is without a doubt still with data
taken from a sea-trial, but this modification could add additional realism should a sea trial
not be possible.
7.2
Estimation
Let it be stated that the achieved navigation performance, detailed in table 6.4, was suf-
ficient for detection. That being said, it could be improved and some areas of potential
improvement will be discussed below.
7.2.1
Tuning
With this MPF framework there are a couple of tuning parameters, see table 6.1, which
were either taken from real sensor datasheets or chosen slightly arbitrarily to encourage
a good simulation performance.
The latter of which includes parameters like the driving noise in the water current model
ww, see section 4.5.1, or the tuning factor for the roughening noise cadd as well as initial-
ization parameters from table 6.3.
The initialization parameters were chosen to reflect the certainty of different states, for
example the positioning uncertainty was set quite low assuming the vessel starts from a
known location, e.g. a dock. The tuning factor was motivated by [46], but it was chosen
by hand according to the simulation data, so there is little guarantee that it is optimal or
suited for real data. In addition, the water current driving noise was also selected by hand
according to the simulation data, so a similar problem arises.
Ideally, given data from a sea trial, one would run an optimizer for determining the best
value of cadd and studies can be done to determine a realistic value for ww.
7.2.2
Magnetic Navigation
This section will discuss some drawbacks of magnetic navigation, specifically
1. The potential inconsistency of map spatial variance in some regions
2. Handling of other complications in the magnetometer
As one can see in fig. 6.2, there are both regions with high variety in the anomaly fieldÕæ
but there is also a valley of sorts down the middle. This means that the value derived
Cyber-resilient Navigation System for Highly Automated Vessels
63
==PAGE:76==
from the map information is a function of location. As such, navigation in a short time
horizon can potentially suffer from such areas of low informationÕæ that being said over a
longer time horizon it should in principle even out as the vessel moves out and through
regions of higher interest. A similar estimation accuracy degradation can likely also be
experienced for a vessel traversing either parallel or orthogonal to the contour lines as
this case contains reduced information along the yb axis.
There are of course additional complications associated with magnetic sensors which
have not been explicitly handled here. First, there is the calibration of the vessels mag-
netic fieldÕæ this is out of scope for this thesis, but it is crucial for the success of an actual
implementation. Second, there is the handling of the temporal variations, i.e., the diurnal
variation. This is a problem to which there exist demonstrated solutions either in the form
of being in contact with a local geomagnetic center for updates, by online estimation and
compensation as in [6, 9], or by filtering as in [51, 52]. Given how many bias terms already
need to be estimated, a solution which does not necessitate additional bias states could
be preferable.
7.2.3
Other Terrain Information
Another aspect relevant to the navigation which was not approached here is the potential
for additional terrain information sources. Combining the anomaly field navigation with
additional maps could increase the robustness of the estimation and aid in reducing the
shortcomings of any single strategy.
For example, in [28, 29, 27], Lager et al utilize depth maps in conjunction with magnetic
anomaly field maps and such an approach could be expanded to utilize gravitation, celes-
tial navigation, or any other sources of navigation information. This is an advantage of the
PF framework, the flexibility regarding measurement functions and information statistics.
7.2.4
Accelerometer Aiding
A final improvement to the estimation system which could be implemented is utilizing the
accelerometer for roll/pitch aiding. This was not done here as the estimation performance
in the orientation was fine without, but for more dynamic behavior or for increased robust-
ness it could be considered.
In [8] described a standard attitude and heading reference system, and how to monitor
the acceleration measurement to detect significant accelerations beyond the acceleration
due to gravity. The idea being that if the vessel is not accelerating then the gravity vector
can be used to calibrate the orientation assuming the vessel, on average, is approximately
level. This same approach is also covered in [11, sec. 14.4.3], specifically in the context
of marine vessels.

7.3
Detection
The monitoring, overall, worked quite well given the properties of the residuals: success-
fully detecting a fault in both the velocity and position signals within a time frame which
would prevent significant deviation from the nominal path.
That being said, there are still other strategies which could be attempted and potential
improvements to be made.
7.3.1
Fault
The fault applied in section 6.4 was simply a change in the mean of the velocity and a
ramp in the position, the size of which was motivated by [3]. This is not necessarily the
most stealthy fault as the change comes abruptly.
This fault type was chosen for its simplicity, but it could be interesting to see if there is a
profound effect on the performance with a more stealthy attack strategy.
7.3.2
Non-Static H0
The monitoring approach applied in section 6.4 first, dimensioned the H0 characteristics
utilizing a section of nominal residual data. This assumes that the residual statistics are
constant and that the training data is completely representative, which may not necessarily
be the case.
An alternative would be to have a second, larger window, running behind the GLRT, up-
dating the H0 characteristics. This is beneficial because now the representation of H0 can
update online and as a result can attempt to follow along with any variations that occur
in the nominal residual characteristics. Of course, upon detecting a fault the adaptation
should pause such that the characteristics corresponding to H1 are not learned. This
adds a second layer of complexity to the GLRT but also allows for the relaxation of some
assumptions as well as increased flexibility.
7.3.3
Uniform Distribution
As can be seen in section 6.4.2, although the argument can be made that the position
residual under H0 more or less follows a Weibull distributionÕæ under H1 it seems to deviate
a lot. The detection algorithms considered in this thesis have only considered cases
where H0 and H1 are given by the same distribution. For the position residual it could
be interesting to consider the case where H0 is Weibull distributed and H1 is perhaps
uniformly distributed instead.
Given that the fault is a change in the mean of the velocity, it could make sense that the
resulting effect on the position residual, i.e. a ramp, corresponds to a uniform distribution
with an ever-increasing upper bound. As a result, one could perhaps expect better perfor-
mance from the Weibull/Uniform GLRT than the Weibull/Weibull and perhaps even better
than the Gaussian/Gaussian.

7.3.4
Non-parametric Methods
As can be seen in section 6.3.2, the position residual does not fit particularly well to any
parametric distributions. A plausible alternative could be to pursue parametric methods,
e.g. a Kernel density estimator or Gaussian mixture model.
Take the Kernel density estimator as an example, the detector in this case would also
utilize two windows, similarly to section 7.3.2 where one is for learning H0 and the other
is for testing deviations.
7.3.5
Innovation
An alternative to the residuals introduced in section 6.3 could be to monitor the innova-
tion term of the estimator for statistical deviations. Applying detection theory utilizing the
innovation signal as a residual is a known technique, documented in texts like [31, 5].

Conclusion
This thesis has addressed designing a condition monitoring system for the GNSS of a
marine vessel sailing through open water in an effort to make the navigation system cyber-
resilient. In order to achieve this, a monitoring system consisting of a navigation and a
detection module was designed.
The navigation module was based on traditional AINS, intended to leverage standard
marine vessel sensors and exclude the GNSS, instead utilizing a high accuracy magne-
tometer for terrain aided position through magnetic anomaly fields. It was shown that this
navigation solution was able to produce sufficiently accurate navigation state estimates
which were, since the lack of GNSS aiding, completely removed from the considered
vessel vulnerabilities. These navigation state estimates are then compared with GNSS
measurements in order to formulate residual terms of position and velocity.
The detection module leveraged these aforementioned residuals and applied statistical
change detection algorithms to serve as a malfunctioning alarm. The statistical properties
of the residuals were analyzed and relevant GLRTs were designed accordingly. It was
shown that the GLRT change detection performance was sufficient in both velocity and
position in registering the fault within an acceptable time frame.
In summary, the navigation module is able to estimate the navigation parameters with
sufficient accuracy independently of the GNSS and the detection module is able to detect
the malfunctioning before significant deviation from the nominal path.
