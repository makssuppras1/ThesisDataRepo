Abstract 
Leuconostoc spp. are commonly known as spoilage microorganisms in deli-meat products where 
they can cause a bulging appearance of the packages that is undesirable to the consumer. DMRI has 
developed a model for prediction of growth of Leuconostoc spp. in deli-meat products. The model 
can be used to predict when spoilage may occur and establish a suitable shelf-life, but before 
applying the model in decision-making, it needs to be validated for a larger range of deli-meat 
products. Therefore, this Master Thesis performed challenge tests to evaluate the performance of 
the model. Eleven batches of Danish deli-meat products with varying environmental factors and 
storage temperature were inoculated with a co-culture of L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides. In 
addition, the 16S rRNA gene was sequenced when concentrations were at 8-9 log10(CFU/g) to 
determine the species of the co-culture causing spoilage. L. carnosum predominated in all batches, 
and L. mesenteroides was also present except in batches preserved with both Na-acetate and Na-
lactate. The evaluation of DMRI’s model was performed using µmax values from the present study 
and from other studies of Leuconostoc spp. in fresh meat and deli-meat products. A total of 57 
observed and predicted µmax values of Leuconostoc spp. were compared and resulted in bias and 
accuracy factor values of 0.89 and 1.44, respectively. Thus, the model slightly underestimated the 
growth in deli-meat products which categorises it as fail-dangerous. It was, however, within the 
acceptable ranges of bias and accuracy factors normally applied for microbial spoilage models 
deeming the model validated for its application for deli-meat products. Nevertheless, the model 
could be improved on some parameters like temperature and combination of preservatives. More 
studies on Danish deli-meat products in challenge tests and storage trials would be needed for the 
model development, thus the model can become fail-safe rather than fail-dangerous. Furthermore, 
it was observed that the risk of bulging packages began at 6.5 log10(CFU/g) in certain situations 
where the product formulation caused a slow µmax of Leuconostoc spp., rather than at the expected 
concentration at 7 log10(CFU/g). The correlation between CO2 production and concentration of LAB 
made it possible to create a traffic light system for the risk of bulging deli-meat packages, where the 
risk increased at higher microbial concentrations. DMRI’s Leuconostoc spp. growth model was found 
valid for application in deli-meat products, but before it can become of use by deli-meat producers, 
improvements could be made to ensure prediction of µmax agrees with the observed µmax at 
temperatures above 5 °C and in products preserved using both Na-lactate and Na-acetate.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background 
The focus in food microbiology has usually been food safety issues related to pathogens, but it is 
also necessary to focus on microbiological spoilage as a parameter for food quality (Wareing et al., 
2010). The shelf-life of a food product becomes shortened when the quality of the food is reduced 
due to spoilage. Eventually, it leads to food waste and thereby, economic losses. (Sofos, 2014). Meat 
products contribute to a significantly large part of food waste from households where the Danish 
consumers experience that one of the foods, they throw out the most, are deli-meat products. More 
than half of the times Danes throw out food, it is due to spoilage, whereas over a third list the date 
marking on the label as the cause (Landbrug & Fødevarer, 2020; Ministeriet for Fødevarer, Landbrug 
og Fiskeri, 2021). In Denmark, 814,000 tons of food are wasted every year. Retail and households 
account for nearly half of it, with households being the second most contributing link to food waste 
in the value chain (Ministeriet for Fødevarer, Landbrug og Fiskeri, 2021). The consumer demands 
fewer preservatives in the food product, and at the same time, requires food to have a longer shelf-
life (Bruun, 2021; Ross et al., 2014).  
Making it possible for the food products to last longer is one of the key solutions to reduce food 
waste (Spada et al., 2018). There are other ways to preserve the food product than to add 
preservatives. The surrounding atmosphere in the package of the food can become a less favourable 
environment for aerobic spoilage microorganisms by applying vacuum or modified atmosphere 
(MA) in the package (McMillin, 2020; Taylor et al., 2019). However, removing oxygen (O2) from the 
packaged food creates a favourable environment for other spoilage microorganisms like lactic acid 
bacteria (LAB). With their ability to grow in anaerobic conditions, pH around 6, and refrigerated 
temperatures, psychrotrophic LAB like Leuconostoc spp. are selected for when O2 is removed from 
meat products (Bautista, 2014; Lonvaud-Funel, 2014; Mejlholm and Dalgaard, 2013; Raimondi et al., 
2021). Specifically, Leuconostoc carnosum and L. mesenteroides subsp. mesenteroides are 
associated with spoilage of deli-meat products which are packaged with MA or vacuum (Bautista, 
2014; Samelis et al., 2006). They exist in nature and contamination along the value chain is the entry 
points for LAB in deli-meat products (Bruun, 2021; Lonvaud-Funel, 2014). The microorganisms that 
remain in a food product after packaging forms the microbial composition of that food (Zagorec and 
Champomier-Vergès, 2017). The microbial composition in food products can be determined e.g., 
==PAGE:11==
Page 2 of 94 
 
with 16S ribosomal ribonucleic acid (rRNA) gene sequencing. The analysis of the 16S rRNA gene 
displays the microbial diversity as well as composition (Takayasu et al., 2019). Especially for food 
spoilage, this analysis can explore which microorganism(s) is the potent spoiler(s) by characterizing 
its dominance in the microbial community (Dourou et al., 2021; Sameli et al., 2021). However, 
knowing that L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides are present in the food does not tell us whether the 
food is spoiled or not. Quantification is necessary as spoilage by LAB do not cause quality loss unless 
they grow to high concentrations, not before around 7 logarithms of colony forming units per gram 
(log10(CFU/g)) a variety of signs of deterioration can be seen (Mejlholm and Dalgaard, 2013; 
Taormina, 2021). Gaseous formation of carbon dioxide (CO2), production of ropy slimes and souring 
are among the most common results of spoilage activity by Leuconostoc spp. in deli-meat products 
(Bautista, 2014; Raimondi et al., 2021).  
A mathematical model in predictive microbiology builds upon the fit of data achieved from 
microbiological studies to a mathematical equation (Pradhan et al., 2019). Thus, challenge studies 
must be performed to determine the growth of spoilage microorganisms under specific intrinsic and 
extrinsic conditions of the food (Serraino and Giacometti, 2014). For deli-meat products, Danish 
Meat Research Institute (DMRI) has experienced a demand in the meat industry for a mathematical 
model predicting the growth of Leuconostoc spp. that the industry can use to predict when an 
existing or new deli-meat product is spoiled (Meinert, 2022). 
The model was constructed with several variables explaining the environmental conditions relevant 
to the deli-meat products. The effects of these variables were determined by 27 data points of 
growth responses of L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides inoculated in challenge studies of a food 
model system mimicking the Danish deli-meat kødpølse. The varying product characteristics and 
conditions, thus constructed the area of application for the model, shown in Table 1.

The model combines the effect of temperature, pH, sodium nitrite (Na-nitrite), Water Phase Salt (%) 
(WPS%), sodium lactate (Na-lactate) and sodium acetate (Na-acetate) on the growth of L. carnosum 
and L. mesenteroides for MA-packaged deli-meat products. To my current knowledge, there is no 
other model available for predicting growth of Leuconostoc spp. in deli-meats at such a variety of 
conditions.  
The application of predictive models stretches from proactive to reactive actions (Braun and 
Sutherland, 2006). However, the application of such models cannot be trusted for decision-making 
before the models have been validated by performing an evaluation (Pérez-Rodríguez and Valero, 
2013; Ross et al., 2014). The evaluation will indicate the quality or performance of the model, which 
assess its reliability (Pradhan et al., 2019; Ross et al., 2014). This is done by performing additional, 
independent challenge studies, representing a wide range of deli-meat products different from 
those used for model development (Serraino and Giacometti, 2014). Different indices are then used 
to evaluate the comparability between the predicted and observed growth, classifying the model as 
good, acceptable, or not acceptable (Pradhan et al., 2019; Dalgaard and Mejlholm, 2019). 
Once the DMRI model predicting the growth of Leuconostoc spp. has been successfully validated, it 
will become available at www.dmripredict.dk as a free tool. The user of the model includes deli-
meat producers and other companies which need an easy platform to gain quick scientific 
knowledge about the growth of Leuconostoc spp. in their deli-meat product(s). Thus, the time when 
a product is spoiled can be predicted and applied to establish an appropriate shelf-life for the 
product. Furthermore, the tool can be used to investigate which product characteristics are required 
to reduce growth of Leuconostoc spp. and delaying or even avoiding spoilage (Meinert, 2022). 
2. Aim of thesis 
The aim of the present thesis was to validate DMRI’s mathematical growth model of Leuconostoc 
spp. in MA-packaged deli-meat products. In this project, growth curves for the co-cultures of L. 
carnosum and L. mesenteroides subsp. mesenteroides were generated in selected deli-meat 
products through challenge tests at different storage temperatures. From the growth curves, 
estimates of maximum specific growth rate were derived and used to evaluate a mathematical 
growth model for Leuconostoc spp. that was recently developed by DMRI. For the model evaluation, 
various criteria, both graphical and mathematical, were used to determine the acceptability of the 
predictions and within which field of application the mathematical model can operate.

3. Deli-meat products 
Deli-meat products are considered as Ready-to-eat (RTE) food products which have been pre-cut 
and -packaged (Sheen and Hwang, 2008). Different terms exist like cold meat cuts, sliced meat, 
luncheon, and sliced cold meat, but throughout this report, it goes by the term deli-meat products.  
Food packaging is important as it protects the food from different external variables like O2 and 
contamination. Deli-meat products are processed meats which are not sensitive to reduced level of 
O2 in the atmosphere as opposed to fresh meat (Brody, 2014). The low level of O2 would cause 
discolouration of the fresh meat (Kontominas, 2014).  
RTE foods are categorised as a very-high-risk food product for presence of Listeria monocytogenes 
by the United States Food and Drug Administration (US FDA). L. monocytogenes is a foodborne 
pathogen, which can infect humans through consumption of contaminated food, especially RTE 
(Bhunia, 2018). Other than RTE foods can cause foodborne diseases from bacteria, the quality of the 
food product can be reduced due to spoilage of bacteria (Sofos, 2014). Food spoilage cannot only 
be caused by growth of microorganisms, it can also be due to enzyme activity or non-enzymatic 
chemical changes, and as well physical damage or pest infestation. No matter the cause, it makes 
food spoilage a problem as food is wasted when the product is either inedible to humans or the 
edibility is reduced (Azad et al., 2019; Sofos, 2014). The focus onward is on food spoilage caused by 
microorganisms, which is also the most common cause of those mentioned (Azad et al., 2019).  
3.1 Packaging conditions in relation to spoilage 
LAB are the most common microbial cause of spoilage in processed meat. Pseudomonads are 
sensitive to curing and heat treatment and are thus usually not causing spoilage in these products 
(Kontominas 2014). Gases are used in packaging of food products to inhibit the growth of 
microorganisms. The application of a modified composition of the gasses will indirectly change the 
ecology of the microbial environment, thus promoting growth of some microorganisms while others 
are inhibited (Azad et al., 2019). Specifically for deli-meats, a modified atmosphere packaging (MAP) 
can consist of CO2 and nitrogen (N2). The air is removed from the package and filled with the desired 
packaging gas mixture, changing the initial gaseous environment surrounding the food. In the 
European Union (EU), packaging gases are gases other than air (McMillin, 2020).  
==PAGE:14==
Page 5 of 94 
 
Oxidative reactions and aerobic bacteria metabolism are two major spoilage factors which both 
requires O2, however, when reducing or excluding O2, the shelf-life of the food product is extended, 
protecting against aerobic spoilage microorganisms (Brody, 2014; McMillin, 2020). Instead of O2, N2 
can be applied (Azad et al., 2019). N2 is used to prevent collapse of the package which occur due to 
high solubility of CO2 in the meat tissue when the meat is packed under high CO2 concentrations 
(Kontominas, 2014). Furthermore, as an inert gas, N2 does not react with the compounds in the food 
product, and neither is it absorbed readily at the surface of the food. Thus, it is desirable to use as 
a filler gas in MAP (McMillin, 2020). While, CO2 has bacteriostatic feature towards microorganisms 
which cause an increase of the lag phase duration and generation time during the exponential phase 
(Kontominas, 2014). At CO2 concentrations above 20%, a reduction in microbial growth occurs. 
However, not all microorganisms are inhibited by CO2, gram-negative bacteria are more susceptible 
to CO2 than gram-positive bacteria. Furthermore, MAP containing CO2 facilitates growth of LAB. If 
levels above 40% CO2 is used, there is a possibility of the package collapsing, especially when stored 
in a refrigerator. The low temperature in the refrigerator, solubility of CO2 in fat, and water of the 
food product is increased, thus resulting the package to collapse (McMillin, 2020).    
A good barrier towards O2 and water are relevant properties for the packaging material applied for 
MAP (Kontominas, 2014). In this experiment, polypropylene (PP) is used as the packaging material. 
It has a good microbial barrier and better water-vapor barrier than polyethylene (Brody, 2014). 
3.2 Signs of spoilage in MA- and vacuum-packaged deli-meat products 
Even though preservation like food packaging is used to extend shelf-life by slowing or eliminating 
the growth of bacteria, the nutrients can still be utilised by spoilage microorganisms for growth, 
reducing the quality of the meat product (Bautista, 2014). For the food to become spoiled, first, 
contamination during the food chain must happen for the bacteria to become present in or on the 
food, secondly, proper environmental conditions should be present for growth of spoilage 
microorganisms, and lastly, the spoilage microorganisms should grow to high concentrations above 
7 log10(CFU/g) (Sofos, 2014; Taormina, 2021; Zagorec and Champomier-Vergès, 2017). However, 
microbial spoilage in meat is not as simple as those steps. It is a complex process which not only 
depends on the microorganism itself, but also their interactions with the meat product and the 
other microorganisms present (Zagorec and Champomier-Vergès, 2017). 
==PAGE:15==
Page 6 of 94 
 
With presence and growth of spoilage microorganisms in meat products, different signs of 
deterioration can occur, such as colour and texture defect, odour and taste, biogenic amine 
production, and gas production. These effects may be the result of microbial metabolism where 
new metabolites were produced from consumption of other metabolites (Bautista, 2014; Zagorec 
and Champomier-Vergès, 2017). The different signs of deterioration are illustrated in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1. A simplified illustration of spoilage in a vacuum- or MA packaged deli-meat product.  
The conditions in MA or vacuum-packaged deli-meat products select for growth of LAB, which may 
cause greening, souring, and formation of slime to occur (Bautista, 2014; Taormina, 2021). Peroxides 
produced by Lactobacillus spp. and Leuconostoc spp. can cause a discolouration of myoglobin 
leading to greening on the deli-meat. Another product from LAB can lead to defect of the texture. 
Slime can appear on the surface of the meat due to the production of polysaccharides forming 
biofilm on the outer surface of the deli-meat (Bautista, 2014; Zagorec and Champomier-Vergès, 
2017). On the other hand, nutrients originating from the preservation can be used by LAB, making 
the meat sour, especially if there is more than 1% sugar in the preserved meat products packaged 
with MA or vacuum (Bautista, 2014). Also, vacuum-packaged deli-meats stored at low temperature 
provides an environment for growth of psychrotrophic, facultative anaerobic species where 
heterofermentative LAB can produce CO2 from hexoses. The formation of gas gives the package a 
bulging appearance (Lonvaud-Funel, 2014; Zagorec and Champomier-Vergès, 2017).

4. Spoilage by Leuconostoc spp. 
Blowing, bulging, and swelling packages are all terms about the formation of gas in the packaging of 
meat and meat products (Erkmen and Bozoglu, 2016; Huys et al., 2012; Zagorec and Champomier-
Vergès, 2017). This sign of deterioration can occur due to growth of LAB like Leuconostoc spp. usually

4.2 Microbial composition in deli-meat products 
Depending on the conditions during processing, storage, distribution, and retail displaying, the 
microbial composition in the deli-meat product can become dominated by spoilage microorganisms 
(Nychas and Drosinos, 2014). To determine the microbial community in a food product, 16S rRNA 
gene sequencing can be performed (Takayasu et al., 2019). The 16S rRNA gene is widely present in 
bacteria, making it possible to identify the bacteria at species level (Wang et al., 2015). When 
targeting the gene, usually the V3 and V4 regions are used as they are the must conserved regions 
of the gene (Nelson and Stewart, 2020). Next-generation sequencing (NGS) can be used to sequence 
the gene. In this project, NGS of illumina was used, where the clusters of deoxyribonucleic acid 
(DNA) fragments were amplified, followed by binding of chemically modified nucleotides containing 
a fluorescent tag. The measurement of the fluorescent signal indicates the addition of a nucleotide 
to the growing strand (illumina, 2022a,b). 
Cauchie et al. (2020) performed 16S rDNA metagenetic analysis on MAP minced meat samples 
inoculated with a co-culture of Brochothrix thermosphacta, Pseudomonas spp. and L. gelidium. 
Samples were analysed during 13 days of storage, and identified at genus level, presented in Figure 
3 as the relative abundance (%) of taxa.

meat. However, after the first storage days, Leuconostoc became the dominant genus of the

4.3 Heterofermentative growth of L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides 
Carbohydrates and nitrogenous compounds are present in the water phase of the meat, where 
microorganisms can utilise them for growth (Nychas and Drosinos, 2014). These small-molecular-
weight substances can be transported through the membrane of the microorganism. The 
transportation occurs by active transport, which is usually associated with a group translocation. 
Here, it transports the substance actively through the membrane and during the passage it becomes 
phosphorylated, appearing in the cell as sugar phosphates. Glucose, fructose, among others are 
transported by the carbohydrate phosphotransferase system (PTS) where a phosphate group is 
added to the substance by phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP). This mechanism is referred to as PEP:PTS 
(Elbeshbishy, 2014).  
After the transportation, heterofermentative LAB like L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides catabolise 
the substances through the pentose phosphate pathway, which is simplified in Figure 4, producing 
lactic acid, CO2, and ethanol and/or acetate (Raimondi et al., 2021). Unlike other LAB, Leuconostoc 
spp. produce only D-lactate and not L-lactate as well (Lonvaud-Funel, 2014). Homofermentative LAB 
uses the Embden–Meyerhof–Parnas (EMP) pathway which only yields lactic acid as the end product 
(von Wright and Axelsson, 2012). This pathway and the pentose phosphate pathway are different 
from each other. The latter include the enzyme xylulose-5-phosphate phosphoketolase which is 
important for transforming xylulose-5-phosphate to glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate and acetyl 
phosphate, producing lactic acid and ethanol, respectively, as shown in Figure 4 (von Wright and 
Axelsson, 2012). Candeliere et al. (2021) found the xylulose-5-phosphate phosphoketolase enzyme 
encoded in the core genome of L. carnosum. 
The pentose phosphate pathway yields 1 adenosine triphosphate (ATP), while EMP pathway yields 
2 ATP from catabolism of glucose. However, heterofermentative LAB can get an extra ATP when 
catabolising pentoses instead of hexoses like glucose. Lactic acid, acetate, and 2 ATP are the end 
products with no formation of CO2 and ethanol (Holland and Liu, 2011; von Wright and Axelsson, 
2012). CO2 formation occurs in the prior steps to the entry point of pentose as either ribulose-5-
phosphate or xylulose-5-phosphate as shown in Figure 4, thus CO2 is not produced when pentose is 
metabolised. The additional ATP is produced when acetate is formed due to presence of organic 
molecules like acetaldehyde and pyruvate which can act as alternative electron acceptors, forming 
acetate instead of acetyl phosphate (von Wright and Axelsson, 2012). 
==PAGE:21==
Page 12 of 94 
 
Moreover, L. mesenteroides can metabolise citrate, while L. carnosum lack the genes for citrate 
metabolism (Raimondi et al., 2021). L. mesenteroides can use citrate as an energy source, however, 
it cannot solely rely on citrate as its primary energy source, and therefore it requires fermentable 
carbohydrates as well. The pathway of citrate metabolism uses other enzymes than those in the 
pentose phosphate pathway to form pyruvate. Other than CO2 and lactic acid, pyruvate can be 
converted to volatile flavour compounds like diacetyl (Holland and Liu, 2011).

5. Predictive modelling 
Microorganisms’ responses to environmental factors can be predicted by a mathematical model. 
This is based on culturing those microorganisms under controlled conditions, assuming 
reproducibility and predictability of their responses (Pradhan et al., 2019; Braun and Sutherland, 
2006). Applying predictive microbiology models can describe growth in specific foods and predict 
the shelf-life of the food product as well as assessing the microbial risks (Braun and Sutherland, 
2006; Mahdinia et al., 2020). Thus, depending on the problem and method used, different models 
can be constructed as listed in Table 4 (Ross et al., 2014). In this project, the problem is growth of 
microorganisms causing spoilage, where data of this is collected using viable counts as the method. 
With these, a growth model is created to obtain growth rate.

The challenge of predictive models lies in the development of them. The models need to consider 
the complexity of the food spoilage, but also the entire microbial ecology of the food. Thus, 
modelling food spoilage is not straightforward, however, they must accurately predict spoilage of 
foods to be industrially robust (Braun and Sutherland, 2006). 
5.1 Types of predictive models 
To predict growth, the choice of which type of model to use is within the two main groups of models: 
kinetic models and probability models. Kinetic models predict the rate of responses like growth and 
death, whereas probability models predict the likelihood of growth or toxin production by 
microorganisms within a certain time frame (Ross et al., 2014). The focus in this project is kinetic 
models as the growth rate is of interest for predicting food spoilage in deli-meat products. Growth 
rate can influence the length of the shelf-life of a food product (Braun and Sutherland, 2006). 
A mathematical model can be mechanistic or empirical, but a mixture of them is also applied. 
Mechanistic models focus on the process from theory to prediction, interpretating the observed 
responses. On the other hand, empirical models are based on observations of the nature and from 
which theory is developed (Braun and Sutherland, 2006). Furthermore, depending on the media and 
its environmental factors, the model is either a generic or specific model. The generic model is 
constructed by using media in a range of environmental conditions, which apply to different food 
products. While a specific model is limited to its application as it is based on a modification of 
environmental factors of a particular food product (Braun and Sutherland, 2006).  
There are different types of mathematical models, where the three common categories are: 
primary, secondary, and tertiary.  
5.2 Primary models 
In primary models, the response of microorganism under specified controlled conditions is 
measured over time. The changes in the density of the microorganism population are pictured as 
the four kinetic processes in Figure 5 (Mahdinia et al., 2020; Pradhan et al., 2019). When 
constructing a growth model, at least eight data points is included to form the curve and positioned 
to identify the phases (Betts and Walker, 2004). 
==PAGE:24==
Page 15 of 94 
 
 
Figure 5: The four kinetic growth phases for log cell number over time (Mahdinia et al., 2020). 
The four kinetic growth phases consist of the lag, exponential, stationary, and death phases. Firstly, 
the microorganisms adjust themselves in the new environment before their growth begins. From 
the lag phase, the growth of microorganisms is initiated which then grow exponentially during the 
exponential phase. These two phases can both be predicted, however, predicting the lag time is 
more difficult than growth rate predictions. The reason for this is that the lag time is dependent on 
many different parameters, ranging from the prior history to the current state. Thus, not only affects 
the prior growth conditions, so does the degree of cell injury, its physiological state and size of the 
inoculum, the kinetic growth phase of the microorganism at inoculum, and the current 
environmental conditions. With all these variable factors, the length of the lag time varies (Betts 
and Walker, 2004; Braun and Sutherland, 2006; Pérez-Rodríguez and Valero, 2013). On the other 
hand, the growth rate is more or less only influenced by the current environmental conditions which 
the cell quickly respond to the environment and displays that in its growth rate (Braun and 
Sutherland et al., 2006; Ross et al., 2014).  
The growth becomes constant when the population level reaches its maximum in the stationary 
phase, which then transits to the death phase where the concentration of nutrients or physiological 
state of cells decreases, causing the number of microorganisms to decline (Pérez-Rodríguez and 
Valero, 2013). 
Different primary models exist as growth models, thermal, and non-thermal inactivation models. 
Furthermore, the common types of growth models include Gompertz model, Logistic model, and 
Baranyi and Roberts model (Mahdinia et al., 2020; Ross et al., 2014). The growth model used in this 
==PAGE:25==
Page 16 of 94 
 
project is the primary model by Baranyi and Roberts (1994). This is a growth model which is semi-
mechanistic and biologically based, and as a dynamic model, it considers the time-varying 
temperatures when describing the microbial growth (Mahdinia et al., 2020). For a sigmoidal curve 
like Baranyi and Robert. The limited time during the exponential phase where the maximum rate 
occurs is used for calculation of the growth rate (Pradhan et al., 2019). 
The specification of intrinsic and extrinsic factors is unique for each primary model, where multiple 
primary models together form the range for area of application of a model (Mahdinia et al., 2020). 
5.3 Secondary models 
From each primary model, different parameters can be estimated such as the maximum specific 
growth rate (µmax) and lag time from growth models. The primary models become a part of the 
secondary model, increasing the complexity of the model. The secondary model predicts how the 
different parameters from the primary model is affected by the environment conditions, either one 
or multiple extrinsic or intrinsic factors (Mahdinia et al., 2020; Ross et al.; 2014). The different 
relations between microbial responses and the environment can be described in kinetic growth 
models, time-to-growth models, growth/no-growth models, inactivation/death-models, and 
survival models (Braun and Sutherland et al., 2006). The most common secondary models used for 
describing the relation are artificial neural networks (ANNs), the square root model, the cardinal 
parameters models, and the response surface model (Mahdinia et al., 2020). In this project, no 
secondary model was constructed of the µmax values estimated from Baranyi and Roberts model, 
instead they were used to evaluate DMRI’s developed growth model.

5.5 Tertiary models 
Both primary and secondary models are a part of the tertiary model as it uses a parameter from the 
secondary model to construct the primary model, thus predicting the response of the micro-
organisms. This occurs within a user-friendly interface, where values of intrinsic and extrinsic factors 
are entered. Both online web-tools as well as software programmes exist, for example ComBase 
(www.combase.cc), DMRIPredict (www.dmripredict.dk), and Food Spoilage and Safety Predictor 
(FSSP) (http://fssp.food.dtu.dk/), respectively, which are based on multiple primary and secondary 
models obtained at different conditions (Mahdinia et al., 2020; Pradhan et al., 2019; Ross et al., 
2014). However, the predictive model must be evaluated before becoming a tertiary model. Such 
model will then need to be maintained through updating and determining whether the model is still 
of relevance (Braun and Sutherland, 2006).  
5.6 Benefits and limitations of predictive models 
The benefit of predictive modelling is the wide range of situations where this approach can be 
utilised. These situations can be within product innovation, operational support, and incident 
support, where predictive modelling helps provide quantitative information on the response of 
microorganisms (Pradhan et al., 2019; Ross et al., 2014). When used proactively or reactively, the 
model assesses objectively (Braun and Sutherland, 2006; Ross et al., 2014). This is a powerful tool 
as a better understanding is made of the impact each variable produces (Braun and Sutherland, 
2006). On the other hand, limitations also exist about initial conditions, model relevance, response 
variability, user-friendliness technology, and empirically of models (Pradhan et al., 2019; Ross et al., 
==PAGE:27==
Page 18 of 94 
 
2014). Constructing the model based on laboratory conditions differentiates from the actual 
conditions which an imperfect representation of growth is given. When the model does not reflect 
the actual food system, the study design does not include the exposure at the actual conditions, 
which causes a tendency of either consistently under- or overpredicting the growth. Specifically for 
sigmoidal curves like Baranyi and Roberts (1994), the growth rate from experiments can be higher 
than the actual growth rate (Pradhan et al., 2019). Also, the growth rate can differ because of the 
distribution of the microorganisms in the actual food product is not necessarily homogenic as in the 
food in the experiment. Furthermore, the experiments which the model is based on does not include 
the natural microbial composition. Growth is also affected by metabiosis, but the model may not 
account for the interaction between microorganisms, in which their production of compounds 
favouring the environment for certain microorganisms (Braun and Sutherland, 2006). Thus, not 
every single variable affecting the growth of microorganisms is implemented in the model. This leads 
to a simplification of the reality. A simple situation may be represented in a model, but as the effect 
of the conditions on foods are complex, and the conditions are dynamic, the prediction of the 
absolute quantity in a specific food product is limited (Pradhan et al., 2019; Ross et al., 2014). These 
inputs of the model are variable; thus, the output will be variable.  
As previously mentioned, the primary models are based on unique intrinsic and extrinsic factors, 
which makes modelling limited to be within the range of the factors studied. Importantly, 
predictions of responses can only be made within the area of application, and not beyond. Thus, the 
models can only be used for interpolation and not for extrapolation (Ross et al., 2014). A method 
where the chosen environmental factors are incorporated in the study design, is a challenge study.  
6. Challenge studies 
A mathematical model predicting growth of a microorganism in a food product can be developed 
from microbiological challenge tests. A challenge test stimulates the process of the food product by 
artificially contaminating a food product with a selected microorganism. It is performed under 
controlled conditions in a laboratory (Spanu et al., 2014; Lanni et al., 2022). 
Depending on the goal of the study, different types of challenge tests can be performed. One would 
be to determine or prove the elimination of a pathogen, while another study would focus on 
spoilage to understand the performance and stability of a food product (Eurofins Scientific, 2021).

6.1.1 Temperature 
Temperature is one of the most important parameters for microorganisms. It can have a 
preservative effect on the food product and increase the length of the shelf-life. This occurs when 
the temperature level is non-favourable for the microorganism (Nowshad et al., 2021). However, 
when the temperature is within the growth range of the microorganism, lag time decreases while 
the growth rate increases. The microbial growth is influenced by the temperature as it affects the 
structure and efficiency of the lipid bilayer of the membrane, and the enzymes in the microorganism 
(Ross and Nichols, 2014). The temperature does not only determine the rate of microbial growth, 
==PAGE:29==
Page 20 of 94 
 
but also the composition of species as some microorganisms thrive more than others at certain 
temperatures. The preferred temperature for growth of a microorganism varies. A microorganism 
that grows well in either warm, room temperature, or cold environment, is classified as 
thermophiles, mesophiles or psychrophiles, respectively, whereas psychrotrophs only tolerate cold 
environments (Azad et al., 2019; Nowshad et al., 2021). The challenge test in this project is 
performed at 3, 4, 7 and 8 °C, as shown in Figure 6. Both L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides grow at 
3-8 °C as they are psychrotropic species of Leuconostoc (Huys et al. 2012; Raimondi et al., 2021; 
Wareing et al., 2010).   
For microorganisms to grow at these temperatures, it requires presence of water in the liquid phase 
of the food product, connecting extrinsic and intrinsic factors (Nowshad et al., 2021). Specifically for 
all the chemical reactions occurring inside the microorganism. Thus, the availability of water 
determines the activity of the metabolic reactions in the cell. The deli-meat products used in this 
project had aw between 0.96-0.99, which makes it possible for the spoilage microorganisms to grow 
in that food (Azad et al., 2019).  
6.1.2 Atmospheric gases 
Another extrinsic factor is atmospheric gases. As with temperature, the different types of gases and 
the level of them can have a preservative effect of microbial growth as well as determining which 
species are active in the food product (Azad et al., 2019; Nowshad et al., 2021). The microorganisms 
can be inhibited by either the direct toxic effect of gases like CO2 and O2 or indirectly by replacing 
O2 with N2. The activity of species depends on the availability of O2, classifying them as aerobic, 
anaerobic, or facultative anaerobic, because they require O2, absence of O2, or either one of them, 
respectively (Azad et al., 2019; Elbeshbishy, 2014). As shown in Figure 6, 70% N2 and 30% CO2 was 
used as the gas composition of the packages in this project. With no O2 present, the anaerobic 
microorganisms use the pentose phosphate pathway to produce lactic acid, CO2, and ethanol and/or 
acetate (Raimondi et al., 2021). 
6.1.3 pH 
Within the food product, intrinsic factors also influence microbial growth and species activity.  
Depending on the pH value, different types of microorganisms and species can be present. Bacteria 
usually grow at a pH around 6, yeasts between 4.5-6.0, followed by molds growing at 3.5-4.0 
(Nowshad et al., 2021). Specifically, for Leuconostoc spp. like L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides, 
==PAGE:30==
Page 21 of 94 
 
their optimal growth is at 6.5 (Huys et al., 2012). This was similar to the pH level used in some of the 
deli-meat products in this project, with the lowest pH level at 5.4 as seen in Figure 6. For the 
microorganism, the pH conditions are important for optimal cell function including enzyme activity, 
reaction rates, protein stability, and nucleic acid structure. Therefore, their internal pH (pHi) is 
maintained by homeostasis using transmembrane proton gradient (Coton and Leguerinel, 2014).

6.1.4 Inhibitory substances 
Soluble nutrients are available to the microorganisms, as they can enter the microorganism through 
its cell wall, carried by free water (Nowshad et al., 2021). Carbohydrates and nitrogenous 
compounds of the added nutrients and preservatives are catabolised by the pentose phosphate 
pathway, whereas undissociated form of the added organic acids enter the cell and reduce microbial 
pHi when it is dissociated in the cytoplasm. This can cause spoilage, while the latter can inhibit the 
growth of spoilage microorganisms (Coton and Leguerinel, 2014; Nychas and Drosinos, 2014). 
 
Other than using challenge studies to construct a mathematical model to determine the growth of 
microorganisms or pathogens in a food product, the challenge studies are also important when 
evaluating such constructed mathematical model (Serraino and Giacometti, 2014).

7. Evaluation of models 
7.1 Bias and accuracy factors 
Prior to 1996, general statistical methods like mean-squared error (MSE) and R squared (R2) values 
have been the only measures to evaluate the performance of predictive microbiological models. At 
that time there was neither a standard method nor a set of criteria for how a model was validated, 
thus objective factors were needed to make the evaluation comparable. However, in 1996, Thomas 
Ross proposed the use of two simple indices to evaluate models in predictive food microbiology. 
(Ross, 1996). The bias (Bf) and accuracy (Af) factor developed by Ross are still in use today.  
The factors were based on the common concept of evaluating models by comparing predicted and 
observed values. From this premise, the indices were developed. The indices could be used for any 
time-based response, thus as lag time, time to an n-fold increase, and maximum specific growth 
rate. The original equation was displayed with generation time (Ross, 1996).   
The deviation between the predicted and observed values was used as the basis of the indices. The 
values of generation time by a microorganism were varying (±) due to the microorganism itself and 
its environment, thus, the average was taken using the number of observations (n). As the ratio 
between predicted (pred) and observed (obs) was included, it gave the relative average deviation. 
To get equal weight for over- and underprediction, logarithm to 10 was applied. The geometric 
mean was achieved by using antilogarithm. This created Bf (Eq. 2) (Ross, 1996).

Bf and Af are multiplicative factors. Bf indicates that the model on average, under- or overpredict, 
while Af indicates the spread of observations around the predicted results of the model. Thereby, a 
Bf above 1 refer to a predicted generation time which is longer than the generation time observed, 
thus the model is categorised ‘fail-dangerous’, and opposite when Bf is below 1, then the model is 
‘fail-safe’. This is applied for time values, whereas if the rate values were compared, the 
categorisation is opposite. A Bf below 1 would make the model ‘fail-dangerous’, predicting a slower 
growth rate than what is observed. For Af, the larger the Af value is, the less accurate is the average 
difference between predicted and observed values. For example, the model predicts on average a 
factor of 2 different from the observed values if Af was 2. (Ross, 1996). The performance criteria for 
Bf and Af is listed in Table 6 for growth of spoilage microorganisms.

When the observed values are equally distributed above and below the predictions, Bf is equal to 1. 
For Af, a value of 1 show that on average, the predictions are close to the observations. These two 
scenarios indicate a perfect agreement between predicted and observed values (Pérez-Rodríguez

Ross (1999) cf Mejlholm et al. (2010) stated performance criteria for Bf that differs from those listed 
in Table 6: (i) 0.95-1.11 good; (ii) 0.87-0.95 or 1.11-1.43 acceptable and (iii) <0.87 or >1.43 
unacceptable. The reason behind the difference is that Mejlholm and Dalgaard (2013) suggested 
other ranges for acceptable and unacceptable Bf values than those originally used from Ross (1999) 
cf Mejlholm et al. (2010). The range 0.85-1.25 was based on a suggestion by Dalgaard (2000) to use 
0.75-1.25 as acceptable Bf for models predicting the growth of spoilage bacteria. However, when 
allowing over- and underestimation by 25%, the underestimation of the µmax values of 
psychrotrophic Lactobacillus spp. showed to have a greater effect on the shelf-life prediction than 
==PAGE:33==

an overestimation. Thus, there was not an equal balance around the ‘true’ value of the shelf-life 
prediction. The effect of underestimation was suggested to not be more than 15%, changing the 
range for Bf values to be acceptable within 0.85-1.25 (Mejlholm and Dalgaard, 2013). 
Mejlholm and Dalgaard (2013) also suggested an acceptable limit for Af. A value below 1.5 was 
acceptable. This was based upon the observations from the study by Mejlholm and Dalgaard (2013). 
They observed a systematic deviation between the predicted and observed µmax values when Af 
values were above 1.5, using their newly developed model for growth of psychrotrophic 
Lactobacillus spp. Furthermore, Bf ranges from below to above the value 1, whereas Af only operates 
equal to or above the value 1. The differences are presented in positive values due to Af measuring 
the absolute deviation. Therefore, Af can never become below 1 (Betts and Walker, 2004). 
The indices are used together, providing an overall evaluation of the agreement between the 
predicted and observed µmax values, determining whether the developed growth model can be used 
with sufficient confidence (Braun and Sutherland, 2006; Mejlholm et al., 2010).  
7.2 Differentiation of the terms: evaluation, performance, and validation 
Even though the definition and application of these indices have stayed the same since 1996, 
different terms like ‘evaluation’, ‘performance’ and ‘validation’ have been used about how to get a 
validated model using those indices (Braun and Sutherland, 2006; Dalgaard and Mejlholm, 2019; 
Mataragas et al., 2006; Mejlholm et al., 2010). A fourth term, ‘verification’, has appeared in the 
literature, however, it was not kept as a common part of the terminology of predictive models in 
food microbiology. Verification is described as the stage where the model is evaluated against data 
sets other than those used for developing the model, whereas a validation of the model is 
performed with the data sets from the development of the model (Betts and Walker, 2004; Braun 
and Sutherland, 2006). This separation of terms has not occurred afterwards, thus it is not relevant 
in this present project to further mention verification as a separated stage from validation.  
Throughout the literature, ‘evaluation’ is used as the term describing the kind of process the 
developed models go through, that the model needs to be assessed before it is successfully 
validated (Dalgaard and Mejlholm, 2019; Mataragas et al., 2006; Mejlholm et al., 2010). However, 
it has not always been uniformly used. Mellefont et al. (2003) reported a differentiation in the 
literature, applying ‘evaluation’ and ‘validation’ interchangeably as the term referring to the 
==PAGE:34==
Page 25 of 94 
 
assessment of a model. They suggested the use of the more general term ‘evaluation’ rather than 
‘validation’. ‘Validation’ could imply confirmation of the predictive model by studies in production 
environment, and such investigation may not be performed for every model (Mellefont et al., 2003). 
Several authors refer to the indices bias (Bf) and accuracy (Af) factors as ‘model performance’.  They 
all agree that this is how the model is evaluated (Braun and Sutherland, 2006; Dalgaard and 
Mejlholm, 2019; Mataragas et al., 2006; Mejlholm et al., 2010). Once the model has been evaluated, 
the model is either valid or not for its application (Betts and Walker, 2004; Dalgaard and Mejlholm, 
2019; Mataragas et al., 2006). 
Mejlholm and Dalgaard (2013) suggested to use at least 20 data points from experiments for model 
evaluation to reach stable values Bf and Af. The experiments can be data from challenge tests, 
scientific literature, or artificial media (Ross et al., 2014). Preferably including naturally 
contaminated (NC) products reflecting the actual food system (Dalgaard and Mejlholm, 2019; 
Pradhan et al., 2019). If not, the inoculated products should have an inoculum level of 102 - 104 
CFU/mL or g for the microorganisms to get a long enough exponential phase to estimate their 
growth (Betts and Walker, 2004).  
Storage conditions and product characteristics from the experiments must be within the area of 
application of the model, otherwise, the predictions are not accurate (Dalgaard and Mejlholm, 
2019). It can be difficult to fulfil for data from literature as not all information is available or it is 
outside the area ranges of the model (Ross et al., 2014). Furthermore, an internal validation is 
performed when the data used to develop the model is also applied in the evaluation of that model, 
whereas an external validation is based on independent data (Betts and Walker, 2004; Pérez-
Rodríguez and Valero, 2013). In this present project, external validation is performed using 
experimental data from laboratory experiments intended to evaluate the model as well as data from 
other studies of DMRI projects and from published scientific literature. 
7.3 Evaluation techniques 
Other than Bf and Af, the model can also be evaluated by the graphical representation of the 
predictions against observations. This assesses whether the predictions over- or underestimate the 
growth rate of the microorganism(s) similar to the result of Bf, however, even though this graphical 
==PAGE:35==
Page 26 of 94 
 
analysis is simpler to utilise than Bf, it does not include statistical results (Betts and Walker, 2004; 
Pérez-Rodríguez and Valero, 2013).  
When the data is plotted, the equivalence line distinguishes two zones, fail-safe and fail-dangerous, 
which respectively over- and underestimate the predictions for growth. This assessment is 
illustrated in Figure 7 (Betts and Walker, 2004; Pérez-Rodríguez and Valero, 2013). 
 
Figure 7. Graphical assessment of predicted values against observed values of µmax or generation time. The 
fail-safe zone (green) is presented on left-hand side of the equivalence line (orange), which indicates perfect 
agreement between the values. Fail-dangerous zone (red) is found below the equivalence line. 
Similar to the graphical assessment, the acceptable simulation zone (ASZ) method provides a simple 
and graphical interpretation, reporting the number of observations within the acceptable range. 
This is performed without statistically describing the results as to how close the predicted and 
observed values are to each other. Different from Bf and Af, ASZ compares the maximum population 
density (Nmax values) (Dalgaard and Mejlholm, 2019; Pradhan et al., 2019). The model is considered 
acceptable, when at least 70% of the predicted log10(CFU/g) were within the ASZ of  0.5 
log10(CFU/g) (Martinez-Rios et al., 2020; Møller et al., 2013). 
No matter the method used for model evaluation, the developed model should also make biological 
sense, ensuring realistic interpretation. For example, an increase of the growth rate occurs as the 
temperature increases, whereas the growth decreases during stressful conditions for the 
microorganism (Betts and Walker, 2004).

scraped meat. For 6 hours with 5 min rotations at 6 revolutions per minute (rpm), and 5 min break,

8.3 Preparation of inoculum 
All 11 strains were individually transferred from the respective frozen tube to approx. 10 mL BHI-B 
in test tubes using inoculation loops (10 µL, Dacos, Denmark) and, subsequently, incubated for five 
days enrichment at 20 °C ± 1 °C (incubator, Binder GmbH, Germany). Enriched cultures were spread 
on APT-A with inoculation loops and incubated at 20 °C ± 1 °C for five days. Before used in the 
inoculum, one colony of the individual strains were transferred to separate tubes with approx. 10 
mL BHI-B and incubated for five days at 20 °C ± 1 °C. The enriched suspensions were mixed in the 
ratio 1:1 of the 11 strains, creating a co-culture with the concentration of approx. 2-7×108 CFU/mL. 
The inoculum was prepared by diluting this co-culture in FK-water to reach the final cell density of 
approx. 2-7×104 CFU/mL. Cell density level of each mono-culture as well as co-culture were checked 
by performing 10-fold dilutions in FKP-water, and 1 mL aliquots of the appropriate 10-fold dilutions 
were deposited in empty Petri dishes (90 x 16.2 mm, Frisenette, Denmark). Pour plating was used 
with APT-A and an extra layer APT-A on top. The plates were incubated at 20 °C ± 1 °C for five days. 
8.4 Challenge test 
In a sterilised environment as described in Section 8.2 Deli-meat products, the top slice (of 1 cm) for

modified atmosphere (MA) consisting of CO2/N2 30%/70% (Free Standing Chamber Machine, 
Multivac, Germany). Except for samples from the initiation of the experiment which were not 
packed and instead quantified for initial cell density level (Section 8.6 Quantification of growth). 
Also, three samples from each batch were vacuum packed in the plastic bags.  
The MAP and vacuum-packed samples for each batch were stored at different temperatures (3 °C, 
4 °C, 7 °C and 8 °C ± 1 °C), see Table 7 above. In addition, eight empty packages were placed at 3 °C 
and 8 °C to test the O2 and CO2 permeability of the plastic bag during the storage period.

8.6 Quantification of growth 
Throughout the storage period, inoculated samples at each temperature were analysed at varying 
intervals which were based on predictions from version 1 (only Batch 5) and 2 (the other batches) 
of the developed model by DMRI as well as observing the growth from the counts of CFU. In total, 
8-10 samplings were performed for each batch. A sample consisted of three packages from a batch, 
with some exceptions using only two. The packages were extracted and discarded after analysis. 
End of storage for a batch was determined when the sample had entered the stationary phase. 
The top slice of each sample was placed into a sterile PE bag in a sterilised environment. Each bag 
was automatically filed with 180 mL FKP-water (Dilumat® Expert, bioMérieux, Inc., France), and then 
homogenised for 1 min at 260 rpm with a Stomacher (Stomacher® 400, Seward, United Kingdom). 
One mL of the homogenised sample was diluted in 9 mL FKP Dilucup™, and 1 mL aliquots of the 
appropriate 10-fold dilutions were deposited in empty Petri dishes. Pour plating technique was used 
with APT-A, and after the agar had solidified, a layer of APT-A was added on top.  
Almost the same process was performed for the sterile samples at initiation (day 0) as well as when 
the cell density had reached its maximum in the sample for each batch. The sterile samples were 
diluted with 180 mL FKP-water before using 1 mL for pour plating with APT-A.  
The plates were incubated at 20 °C ± 1 °C for approx. five days and at the end of the incubation 
period, the colonies were counted. This result was translated into log10(CFU/g) for each of the three 
replicates of the deli-meat batch and expressed as average log10(CFU/g) and standard deviation (sd, 
±) of the replicates for each batch.

The last step in Figure 9 is to determine the microbial composition from the results of illumina. 
CelerSeq (DMRI software) used illumina data and metadata to construct a heatmap of the relative 
abundance at species level. The heatmap is used to ensure that the inoculum is the dominant 
species at spoilage and to investigate whether the two Leuconostoc spp. grew similarly or to ensure 
the growth of the inoculum occurs and whether the two Leuconostoc spp. grew similar or if one 
predominated in certain types of deli-meat products. The result is used to determine which species 
cause spoilage in the deli-meat product, where spoilage is indicated by a bulging appearance of the 
package due to the production of CO2 by the predominate species in the product.

Both the adjusted R2 and SE of fit were used to determine which ComBase DMFit model best fitted 
the observed data. From these values, the Baranyi and Roberts model fitted the data better than 
Trilinear, Biphasic Model and Linear Model. An adjusted R2 close to 1 and a SE close to 0 indicates 
that the model predicts the values as observed and with limited variation in the values between 
samples (IBM, 2022a,b). 
Built inside DMFit is an F-test which measure the significance of fitting between the potential models 
and experimental data. If a model or a model parameter like lag phase or asymptote is found to be 
non-significant, DMFit discard that type of model from the options (Baranyi, 2017). In the few cases 
where the options included a Baranyi and Roberts model with or without lag phase or asymptote, 
other statistical methods were used to determine which type of model to move forward with. A 95% 
confidence interval was created for the lag phase (Eq. 12), using the estimated lag time (hours, h) 
and the SE value displayed by ComBase as ± from the lag time estimate.

growth model, an F-test was also performed to statistically test the null hypothesis (NH) (no lag

10. Discussion 
10.1 Microbial composition in deli-meat products 
This Master Thesis aimed to evaluate a developed Leuconostoc spp. growth model by DMRI using 
µmax from challenge tests and storage trials with growth of Leuconostoc spp. in deli-meat products 
and fresh meat. In deli-meat products, L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides were previously isolated 
by DMRI as the predominant strains in the Accelerated shelf-life project (Svenningsen, 2021; 
Svenningsen and Koch, 2021). They were also the main Leuconostoc spp. causing gas production in 
sliced vacuum-packaged cooked ham in Finland and Greece, respectively (Samelis et al., 2006). 
Furthermore, L. carnosum dominated in sliced, cooked ham in MAP from six European countries, 
and caused package swelling (Raimondi et al., 2019).   
During the quantification of growth in the present experiment, no selective media were used to 
distinguish the two Leuconostoc spp. which made it impossible to model the microbial interaction. 
The deli-meat producing companies would have one or both present in their product without 
distinguishing them in their microbial tests. Even so, it would have been interesting to observe the 
interaction between L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides, which impact they had on one another, and 
how it affected the growth model prediction. Cauchie et al. (2020) modelled the microbial 
interaction for B. thermosphacta, Pseudomonas spp., and L. gelidum in fresh meat. Between the 
populations, a Jameson effect potentially occurred rather than a pray-predator trend as they 
observed inhibition of the two other microorganisms when the dominating species reached its 
maximum population density. 
However, these are not the only species associated with meat spoilage in deli-meat products. The 
Accelerated shelf-life project by DMRI showed dominance of Lactilatobacillus sakei aside from L. 
carnosum and L. mesenteroides (Svenningsen, 2021; Svenningsen and Koch, 2021). The same was 
found in the studies by Raimondi et al. (2019) and Samelis et al. (2006) where dominance of L. sakei 
occurred in sliced, cooked ham in MAP and vacuum-package, respectively. Even though L. sakei is 
homofermentative and therefore does not produce CO2 and swell the packages (von Wright and 
Axelsson, 2012), it could have been interesting to include L. sakei in the challenge tests as it would 
have given a better simulation of the natural microbial composition and their interaction in deli-
meat products. This could provide a better model both during the development and evaluation, and 
==PAGE:60==
Page 51 of 94 
 
thus, ensuring a prediction of growth closer to the reality. Another approach would be to perform 
storage trials. The use of naturally contaminated deli-meat products allows for interaction between 
the microorganisms and predicting the response of different microorganisms. However, it relies on 
natural presence of the target microorganism(s) in at least some of the samples, and the ability to 
identify them on selective media (Betts and Walker, 2004; Braun and Sutherland, 2006). 
10.2 Effect on µmax 
The inoculum level in challenge tests should not be too high otherwise it is not possible to estimate 
growth. With the commonly used level of 2 log10(CFU/g), the inoculum can fully enter the 
exponential phase before entering the stationary phase (Betts and Walker, 2004). Thus, the 
performed challenge test used this inoculum level which is also shown in Table 12 as N0.  
Furthermore, it was possible to get the growth rate in the performed experiment because the 
growth curves had at least eight data points which was positioned along the curve to identify the 
end of lag phase, mid- and end-exponential phase as suggested by Betts and Walker (2004). 
Generally, the performed experiment showed a higher µmax in both Kødpølse and Sandwichskinke 
when they were stored at 7-8 °C compared to 3-4 °C. Four of the seven different product types were 
stored at both low (3-4 °C) and high (7-8 °C) temperature, and the ratio between the growth rate at 
the two temperatures is calculated in Appendix K. When the temperature raised from 3 or 4 to 7 °C 
or from 4 to 8 °C, almost a two-fold increase of µmax occurred. This observation is consistent with 
growth of L. carnosum from MAP sausages and sliced, cooked ham. When the temperature was 
doubled, µmax increased by two-folds (Raimondi et al., 2021).  
L. mesenteroides grew slower than L. carnosum at 3-4 °C, while their growth rates were similar at 7-
8 °C in Kødpølse2020 project by DMRI (Koch, 2020). However, both Samelis et al. (1998) and 
Vermeiren et al. (2005) observed a slower growth of L. carnosum than L. mesenteroides. Samelis et 
al. (1998) identified the microbial composition during the production and storage of vacuum-
packaged sliced, cooked ham at 4 °C. Vermeiren et al. (2005) inoculated the two Leuconostoc spp. 
as a co-culture on modelled cooked ham stored anaerobically at 7 °C. The vacuum-packaged sliced, 
cooked ham had similar product ingredients to Sandwichskinke in the present experiment, and with 
a pH at 6.3, while the modelled cooked ham had a lower WPS% than Sandwichskinke. Samelis et al. 
(2000) observed a switch in the dominate species due to packaging conditions. L. mesenteroides 
==PAGE:61==
Page 52 of 94 
 
dominated vacuum-packaged cooked ham, but when the respective samples were air-stored, L. 
carnosum replaced the dominate species. The presence of O2 selected growth of L. carnosum, and 
as O2 residuals were observed in MAP deli-meat products from the present experiment (Appendix 
H), it may have affected the microbial composition to be dominated by L. carnosum. Thus, the 
different packaging condition may be why L. carnosum grew faster than L. mesenteroides in the 
present experiment compared to what were observed by Samelis et al. (1998) and Vermeiren et al. 
(2005). Furthermore, the switch in domination can be because of the variation between strains from 
the same species (Ross et al., 2014).  
10.3 The risk of bulging deli-meat packages 
The visibility of a bulging appearance of the packages depended on the gas-permeability of the 
package and the relation between product and head space. A previous DMRI project studied the 
effect of different package permeability on CO2 levels (Thapa, 2021). The growth rate was not 
affected by the permeability of the package material, while an increase of O2 and decrease of CO2 
was significant in the package with highest permeability at an oxygen transmission rate of 50 mL/m2 
(Thapa, 2021). With such permeability, it would not be possible to observe a bulging appearance of 
the package.  
In the performed experiment, the head space to product ratio was 0:1 in vacuum-packaged deli-
meats, while 10:1 for MAP. Due to the limited head space in packages with vacuum, the swelling 
becomes more visible than in MAP. Thus, the deli-meat products were vacuum-packaged in this 
performed experiment to observe the bulging appearance. 
The samples in this present experiment were only sequenced at one time point, when they were 
estimated to have reached 7 log10(CFU/g). This would show the dominance at the concentration 
associated with spoilage. It is not until the spoilage microorganisms have grown to 7 log10(CFU/g) 
that the spoilage becomes visible (Taormina, 2021). However, in the present challenge test, the 
sample for determining the microbial composition was overall taken at concentrations close to 9 
log10(CFU/g). At this concentration, signs of deterioration had already begun to occur. Therefore, it 
could have been interesting to determine the microbial composition at 6.5 and 7 log10(CFU/g) as it 
is at these concentrations, where the production of CO2 accumulates and thus, the bulging 
appearance starts to become visible. This would have showed which of the two Leuconostoc spp. 
==PAGE:62==
Page 53 of 94 
 
had an impact on the beginning of CO2 production causing spoilage. To my current knowledge, no 
study has characterised the microbial composition at this level. Raimondi et al. 2019 observed 
bulging packages of naturally contaminated cooked ham which at the end of shelf-life were 
dominated by L. sakei and L. carnosum, and LAB had grown to 7.7 log10(CFU/g). MRS-A isolates of 
the cooked ham were identified at species level by 16S rRNA gene sequencing with MiSeq illumina 
similar to this performed experiment.  
The microbial concentration was observed to influence the concentration of CO2 in the performed 
experiment with MAP Sandwichskinke. The lowest concentration of CO2 (23%) at the end of storage 
was observed in a batch of Sandwichskinke (Batch 5) which also had the lowest Nmax at 6.5 
log10(CFU/g) compared to the other batches. Two other Sandwichskinke batches (Batch 7 and 11) 
had low level of CO2 (around 25%), and the spoilage microorganisms had the second and third 
slowest µmax in Sandwichskinke, respectively, but still grew to 8 log10(CFU/g). However, the bulging 
appearance of vacuum-packaged deli-meat did not begin until the growth had reached its Nmax 
(above 8 log10(CFU/g)) unlike the other batches of Kødpølse and Sandwichskinke where the swelling 
was already visible at 7 log10(CFU/g).    
10.4 Production of deli-meat products 
Overall, the product characteristics measured (Table 11) agreed with the formulation in the recipe 
(Appendix B) of the 11 batches performed in this experiment. However, pH of Sandwichskinke 
product type SV3 did not match with what was expected. SV3 had a pH at 6.5 instead of 6.3 which 
may be due to the difficulties that occurred around Sandwichskinke in the making of the products. 
A clear fluid secretion was observed in two of the Sandwichskinke product types (SV1 and SV2). 
Around 150 mL fluid pr kg SV1 product was secreted at the start of the experiment. Furthermore, 
gel was also secreted from SV3, and the product had a non-homogeneous appearance. It is 
important to choose the relevant environmental conditions for the food product to predict the 
growth of spoilage microorganisms as close to the reality as possible (Braun and Sutherland, 2006; 
Bureau Veritas, 2022). Moreover, the food product needs to be able to contain those conditions for 
determining its ability to support or inhibit growth of spoilage microorganisms. Therefore, based on 
prior observations of the cooked products, the formulation of SV1 and SV2 remained the same even 
though it contributed to a fluid loss, while phosphate was added to the brine of SV3. The changes in 
the formulation depended on the product’s ability to bind NaCl.

Page 54 of 94 
 
10.5 Effect of preservation on abundance of spoilage microorganisms in deli-meats 
L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides were inoculated on both Kødpølse and Sandwichskinke, and these 
were also found by 16S rRNA sequencing to dominate the microbial composition of these products. 
The sequencing showed the expected growth response where the co-culture grew in the batches. 
In all products, L. carnosum predominate by average 75% to 99%, followed by 0 % to 24% on average 
for L. mesenteroides. The study by Vasilopoulos et al. (2008) did also find predominance of L. 
carnosum in cooked ham from a Belgian producer. However, Samelis et al. (2000) found 
predominance by L. mesenteroides in vacuum-packaged sliced, cooked ham from Greece, where it 
outgrew L. carnosum. The findings by Samelis et al. (2000) agrees with Vermeiren et al. (2005) as L. 
mesenteroides grew most rapidly in a modelled cooked ham compared to L. carnosum which was 
one of the slowest. The difference of predominant microbial species in similar products can be due 
to variations in factors as raw materials, the contamination in the production environment including 
the slicing equipment, and the processing conditions during cooking, but also after production 
where storage temperature, pH and water activity can influence (Vasilopoulos et al., 2015).  
Furthermore, the relative abundance varied depending on presence of preservatives and the 
temperature in the performed experiment. L. mesenteroides was not found relatively abundant in 
one Kødpølse and one Sandwichskinke where a combination of Na-lactate and Na-acetate was used 
in the product. As Na-lactate alone, did not affect the relative abundance of L. mesenteroides to the 
same extent, it was likely a result of the combined use of these organic acids or a result of Na-
acetate. Samelis and Kakouri (2021) showed less prevalence of Leuconostoc spp. in frankfurters with 
addition of a combination of 1.8% Na-lactate and 0.25% Na-diacetate. The isolation frequency of L. 
carnosum and L. mesenteroides were both lowered by approx. five-fold in frankfurters with Na-
lactate and Na-diacetate compared to frankfurters with Na-lactate. L. mesenteroides may still be 
present in the frankfurters as well as for deli-meats of this Master Thesis.  
In this study, the relative abundance of L. mesenteroides increased with higher temperatures. 
Kalschne et al. (2015) isolated spoilage microorganisms from vacuum-packaged sliced, cooked ham 
by a Brazilian producer, stored at 4 C and 8 C. The predominant LAB were homofermentative 
Latilactobacillus spp. (mainly L. curvatus and L. sakei) followed mainly by L. mesenteroides, where 
the abundance of L. mesenteroides increased from 4 C to 8 C. This is like the performed experiment 
with less abundant L. mesenteroides at 3-4 °C compared to 7-8 °C.

10.6 Performance of the model 
Based on the performed evaluation of DMRI’s Leuconostoc spp. growth model in deli-meat products 
(Eq. 1), the area of application includes temperatures (3-8 °C), pH (5.8-6.3), WPS% (2-6%), added 
Na-nitrite (0-150 ppm), and added Na-acetate (0-0.5%) and Na-lactate (0-3%). Furthermore, MAP 
was the only packaging condition used for the products to build the DMRI model. The same was 
used in the performed experiment as well as in the other DMRI projects and scientific papers. The 
package condition may influence the growth of Leuconostoc spp. as L. mesenteroides were observed 
by Samelis et al. (2000) to dominate in vacuum-packaged cooked ham, while L. carnosum dominated 
in air-stored cooked ham. However, Björkroth et al. (1998) observed the specific spoilage 
microorganism in vacuum-packaged sliced, cooked ham to be L. carnosum. Future studies on the 
effect of different package conditions on the abundance of Leuconostoc spp. could be performed to 
determine the application of the model when deli-meat products are packaged differently. 
The evaluation data extracted from DMRI projects and scientific papers included values outside the 
range of the model’s area of application, e.g., pH and temperature of fresh meat. Overall, it did not 
affect the performance of DMRI’s model to the extent that it was unacceptable and could not be 
validated. A potential reason for this could be that all the tested products covered the same internal 
and external parameters as the products used to construct the model. The model has shown its 
ability to predict growth of Leuconostoc spp. in fresh meat which had environmental factors outside 
the range of area of application. The temperature was above 8 °C as the fresh meat was stored at 
10-12 °C, and pH was below the lower limit of the model (pH 5.8) with pH of fresh meat at 5.49-
5.60. Compared to including all 57 µmax of deli-meat and fresh meat products (Bf 0.89; Af 1.44), the 
model performed similarly for data of only deli-meat products with Bf and Af values of 0.86 and 1.35. 
From Bf and Af of all the data, the model on average, underestimated µmax of Leuconostoc spp. in 
deli-meat and fresh meat products. Thereby, the model is fail-dangerous as it predicts a slower 
growth than what was observed in the products, and lead to spoilage before the end of shelf-life. It 
is better for the model to be fail-safe than fail-dangerous to avoid the consumer experience spoilage 
problems, however, if the model is too fail-safe, the model may have limited benefits as it would 
predict a shorter shelf-life than needed as suggested by Betts and Walker (2004).  
Five of 22 scenarios tested on the model had Bf values outside the acceptable range of 0.75 to 1.25, 
while all had Af values within the acceptable range of < 1.5. The 17 scenarios within the acceptable 
==PAGE:65==
Page 56 of 94 
 
range of Bf were between 0.86 to 1.08, and Af values were between 1.25 to 1.48. The model 
performed best when evaluated against samples stored at 5 °C (Bf 1.01; Af 1.25), followed by 
excluding 8, 10 and 12 °C (Bf 1.03-1.04; Af 1.39-1.42). The model was mainly built by growth data of 
products stored at 5 °C, which may be the reason for obtaining an almost perfect agreement 
between the predicted growth by DMRI’s model and the observed growth at this temperature. 
During the development of the model, only one data point was performed at 8 °C, which may be 
the reason why the model had difficulties predicting growth of Leuconostoc spp. in deli-meat 
products stored at 8 °C. This can be observed in Figure 13 where half of the data points at 8 °C (blue 
colours) lies outside the acceptable range of Bf and Af (dotted lines). Excluding data points at 8 °C 
did improve the performance of the model to have almost perfect agreement about Bf. Compared 
to all the data (Bf 0.89; Af 1.44), the model performed better when data points at 8 °C from 
Kødpølse2019 project were only removed (Bf 0.95; Af 1.39), but not as good compared to removing 
all data points at 8 °C (Bf 1.03; Af 1.39). Even though the majority of 8 °C data points outside the 
range in Figure 13 is from Kødpølse2019 project. The difference between products at 8 °C from 
Kødpølse2019 project and the other 8 °C data points is the high pH around 6.5. The model was 
mostly developed from pH values around 6 than at 6.5 for products stored at high temperature. 
Bf and Af values do not display the ability of DMRI’s model to predict no growth in Batch 5. Since 
Batch 5 was predicted to have a µmax at 0, no log transformation could be performed. Thus, it was 
not possible to include µmax of Batch 5 in the calculation of Bf and Af, even though growth was 
observed in the performed experiment. The data used to develop the model included two data 
points with µmax at 0 which both had low pH (around 5.3), Na-lactate and Na-acetate, no Na-nitrite, 
and WPS% of 3. The WPS% was not as high as in Batch 5 which had Na-lactate, Na-acetate, and 
WPS% of 6. Another data point from the model development had no organic acids, but a high WPS% 
(around 6%) that lead to a slow growth rate. The growth response of these data points could be the 
reason why the model predicted zero growth of a deli-meat product with Na-lactate, Na-acetate, 
and a high level of WPS%. Thus, the multiple hurdles control the growth of L. carnosum and L. 
mesenteroides. It took approx. 17 days until growth initiated in Batch 5. The DMRI’s model did not 
include a lag phase as most of the data did not have one, and thereby, no lag phase was predicted.  
The growth in Batch 5 reached approx. 6.5 log10(CFU/g) when small air bubbles became visible in 
the packages. Usually, the bulging appearance is reported to begin at 7 log10(CFU/g) (Taormina, 
==PAGE:66==
Page 57 of 94 
 
2021) which was also observed in the other batches in the present study, but not for Batch 5 due to 
a slow growth. Such deli-meat products where a bulging appearance may begin to occur already 
from 6.5 log10(CFU/g) is relevant to predict. Then, the deli-meat producers can consider such 
product formulation to reduce the growth of L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides. More studies with 
highly preserved deli-meat products needs to be made because if slow growth occurs in that 
product, the model may predict it as zero growth. Bf and Af cannot compare zero growth with 
growth, and thereby, such cases cannot affect the performance of the model as it is not included in 
the calculations of Bf and Af. 
The scenario where the model performed best was when only the data from storage at 5 °C (Bf 1.01; 
Af 1.25) were included. However, that was closely followed by using data without including samples 
stored at 8, 10, and 12 °C as well as just without 8 °C from Kødpølse2019 project which had Bf of 
0.95 and 1.03-1.04 and Af of 1.25-1.42. Interestingly, the dataset at 5 °C consists of deli-meat and 
fresh meat products, where the latter has similar environmental factors as pH and temperature to 
the area of application, but without the addition of preservatives. The data points at high 
temperatures were mainly for fresh meat, but some deli-meat products were also stored above 8 
°C like some Kødpølse batches from Accelerated shelf-life project by DMRI were stored at 10 °C. The 
model was not built with data points at 10 °C and 12 °C, which is probably the reason for the model 
not performing well at these temperatures. 
Cauchie et al. (2020) inoculated fresh meat with L. gelidum, and the model showed no difficulties in 
predicting growth of another Leuconostoc spp. than the species used to build the model. The 
application of DMRI’s model is specific for growth of L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides, but more 
studies than just Cauchie et al. (2020) are needed to test the performance of the model with growth 
of other Leuconostoc spp. than L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides. 
Other than being able to predict growth in fresh meat products, the model showed potential for 
hybrid meat products. DMRI’s Accelerated shelf-life project had two meat products with added 30% 
vegetables where the model predicted growth to be in almost perfect agreement with the observed. 
Even though there may not be completely consensus about the acceptable range for Bf and Af 
values, the perfect agreement between observed and predicted µmax remains at 1, which apply to a 
few of the 22 scenarios where the scenario with data only including 5 °C is closest. Thus, the model 
has been successfully validated for its application.

10.7 Industrial impact 
The deli-meat producers were involved in the establishment of the area of application for the model 
together with DMRI. The communication with the food industry ensured the model reflected actual 
products which were or would be displayed in supermarkets. Such collaboration continued through-
out the process of developing and evaluating the model. This Master Thesis showed the model had 
difficulties in predicting growth in certain products, and whether to perform more studies with e.g., 
extended pH ranges were determined by DMRI and representatives of deli-meat producers.  
In DMRIPredict, the growth curve would appear with predicted spoilage. As an extra feature, risk of 
bulging appearance of vacuum or MA-packages will be illustrated on the curve, shown in Figure 15. 
The traffic light colours applied in the displayed growth curve indicates the level of risk. The result 
is simple to read which is important when quick answers are needed by the deli-meat producers.  
 
Figure 15. Draft of how the displayed growth curve may be illustrated in DMRIPredict with traffic light colours 
to indicate risk of bulging deli-meat packages with green (no risk), yellow (intermediate risk), and red (risk). 
Even after the model is validated, the area of application can be extended. The model should 
continue to be updated to account for future products like hybrid products that consists of a mixture 
of meat and vegetables. Importantly, before such a model can be of use, the model should consider 
the amount of meat in the product, but that requires a definition of what is meant by meat to avoid 
confusion around entering meat content and thus, misreading the predicted growth of that product. 
Furthermore, the model can be used for all combinations of product characteristic parameters and 
values of them within the area of application. That makes it possible for deli-meat producers to 
enter an unlimited number of different product formulations to know the product characteristics 
required to reduce growth of Leuconostoc spp. to delay or avoid occurrence of bulging packages.

11. Conclusion 
DMRI’s Leuconostoc spp. growth model is valid for application in deli-meat products based on the 
performed model evaluation. The bias and accuracy factors (0.89 and 1.44, respectively) are within 
the acceptable range for the performance of the model when evaluated on data both within and 
outside the area of application. However, excluding samples stored at 8 °C, 10 °C and 12 °C improved 
the performance of the model, and the model was best when it was evaluated against deli-meat 
and fresh meat products stored at 5 °C for growth of L. carnosum and L. mesenteroides. At these 
scenarios, the model was slightly fail-safe (Bf = 1.01) making sure that spoiled products will not reach 
the consumers. Including all data points in the evaluation made the model slightly fail-dangerous (Bf 
= 0.89) indicating that a too long shelf-life can be predicted from the model which is not wanted. 
Therefore, collection of more data for further development of the DMRI model has been suggested. 
L. carnosum was the predominant species in the products at the end of shelf-life, though L. 
mesenteroides was still relative abundant. However, preservatives and temperature influenced their 
relative abundance in the deli-meat products. L. mesenteroides was not detected in products with 
added Na-lactate and Na-acetate, while it became increasingly abundant at higher temperatures. 
The level of these spoilage microorganisms and their µmax affected the CO2 production. Through the 
performed experiment, it was found that the level of CO2 increased to unacceptable levels at 
Leuconostoc spp. concentrations above 6.5 log10(CFU/g) when a beginning bulging appearance of 
the package was observed. These correlations can display the risk of bulging packages in certain 
product formulations. Thus, the valid model will include a traffic light system of the risk of bulging 
packages. This model is useful for product development and establishing a shelf-life with quick and 
reliable scientific answers through an easy-to-use platform. Deli-meat producers with little or no 
microbial scientific background can learn how their existing as well as new product formulations 
impact spoilage and risk of bulging packages. Before the model is fully ready for the users, 
improvements of the model are needed to get closer to a perfect agreement between observed and 
predicted µmax rather than being in the outer range of acceptable performance and avoid predictions 
in the fail-dangerous area. Thus, challenge tests and storage trials are needed to improve 
performance of the model on Kødpølse, but mostly Sandwichskinke as it was not included in the 
model development. Also, the model could include and test other types of deli-meat products that 
are a hybrid mix of meat and vegetables to ensure application in diverse types of deli-meat products.

12. Future perspectives and experiments 
The model is valid for application in deli-meat products based on its acceptable performance in the 
model evaluation against different product characteristics and storage conditions of deli-meat and 
fresh meat products. However, it may not be easy to use at DMRIPredict. The user is required to 
enter WPS% which some may mistake as the added salt concentration (%). To avoid this 
misunderstanding, DMRIPredict should allow entry of added salt concentration (%) and water 
content (%) and have the WPS% calculated by the tool. It could also be speculated whether the 
growth predictions could be improved if the model operated with the concentration of 
preservatives in the water phase rather than the added amounts. Before such translation can be 
done, it is, however, necessary to establish how much of the added amounts of preservatives are 
present in the water phase. 
The model needs to be improved in certain areas. Thus, the next step in the process is to perform 
more studies to both develop and validate the model. More data at 8 °C are needed to build the 
model as it did not perform well for products stored at this temperature. Same for highly preserved 
products which was not predicted by the model to provide growth even when growth was observed. 
In addition, to determine if the model is valid for use both within and outside the range of the 
model’s area of application, more studies are needed. Challenge tests on deli-meat products should 
be performed with other strains of Leuconostoc spp. than those used in this present experiment and 
development of the model, and with other bacteria to study the microbial interaction in deli-meat 
products. Challenge tests and storage trials can be with deli-meat products and other products like 
hybrid deli-meat products. 
With the experience from this Master Thesis, performing pre-tests with Sandwichskinke would have 
determine an optimal method for controlling pH in pork shank. This would have ensured pH of the 
product to be within the model’s area of application. Furthermore, if this Master Thesis were 
performed again, evaluation of model performance should include batches of deli-meat products 
stored at 5 °C which were inoculated by the co-culture and not a single inoculum species as the 
other studies used to build the model. Lastly, more attention on predicting growth in the 11 batches 
should have been done to sample closer to 7 log10(CFU/g) to determine the relative abundance of 
beginning bulging packages. This would have avoided sampling at 8-9 log10(CFU/g) where the 
spoilage microorganisms that caused bulging appearance of the package was predominated.

Appendix K: Growth rate ratio between low and high temperatures 
Four of the seven different deli-meat products were stored at two temperatures. The ratio between 
the low and high temperature was calculated to determine how much faster was the growth of the 
inoculated co-culture at the highest temperature of the two. 
An example of the calculation is shown for Batch 10 and 11 (Eq. 37). 
𝐺𝑟𝑜𝑤𝑡ℎ 𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑒 𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜= 𝑜𝑏𝑠 µ𝑚𝑎𝑥𝑎𝑡 ℎ𝑖𝑔ℎ 𝑡𝑒𝑚𝑝𝑒𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑢𝑟𝑒
𝑜𝑏𝑠 µ𝑚𝑎𝑥𝑎𝑡 𝑙𝑜𝑤 𝑡𝑒𝑚𝑝𝑒𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑢𝑟𝑒→0.099
0.053 = 1.84 
(Eq. 37)
