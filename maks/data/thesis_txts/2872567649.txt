Abstract
Polyolefins like polyethylene (PE) and polypropylene (PP) are the most common polyôÙ
mers found in ocean garbage patches. Due to long exposure in harsh environments,
these materials are often degraded and hard to recycle. This thesis aimed to investigate
the degradation of ocean plastic (OP) collected in the Great Pacific Garbage Patch and
explore how restabilisation and reactive extrusion could improve the material quality.
The OP was analysed with FTIR, DSC, TGA, rheology, and tensile testing. Surface degraôÙ
dation was confirmed, while the core of the flakes showed less damage. The material
lacked antioxidants and degraded further during extrusion unless stabilised. An antioxiôÙ
dant mix was therefore added to protect the material during processing.
To upgrade the OP, different additives were introduced, including a trifunctional monomer
(TMPTA), two reactive silaneôÙbased additives (Nexamite R201 and R203), and a polyôÙ
olefin compatibiliser (Vistamaxx). Their effects on strength, elasticity, and compatibility
were assessed. A Design of Experiment (DOE) was used to compare the results and
predict an optimal material. While the optimal formulation could not be tested due to time
limits, the study showed that combining additives can lead to a strong and ductile material.
The results show that even degraded OP can be reused if treated carefully. With restaôÙ
bilisation and reactive extrusion, the properties can be improved, offering a path to more
circular use of ocean plastic.

Introduction
Plastic pollution has become one of the most pressing environmental challenges of our
time, with millions of tons of plastic waste entering our waterways each year. [1, 2] Over
the past decades, linear consumption patterns have led to a substantial buildôÙup of plastic
waste in our society. This issue has become worse due to low global recycling rates,
mismanaged waste disposal systems, and cultural habits that contribute to the widespread
use of singleôÙuse plastics. [2] As a result, a significant amount of this waste enters the
oceans, where it persists for decades, breaking down into microplastics and causing harm
to ecosystems and wildlife. [1]
This thesis focuses on this waste, more specifically, polyolefins (POs) that are found in
the ocean. POs, such as polyethylene (PE) and polypropylene (PP), have been shown
to make up over 80% of debris floating in the ocean, due to their buoyancy in water. [3,
4, 5] POs are widely used in our daily lives for their versatility and durability. They have
a good environmental resistance that improves their lifetime and ensures product with
high performance. This resistance to environmental factors, which is beneficial during
the use phase, turns into a major challenge when these materials enter the environment,
leading to the accumulation of waste. However, over time, POs are still prone to degradaôÙ
tion through oxidation processes, which slowly alter the materialãs properties. [6, 7] The
degradation leads to changes in the polymer chain, such as chain scission, crossôÙlinking,
branching, and loss of mechanical integrity, that ultimately reduce the performance of the
material. [8, 9, 10]
To protect materials from degradation, stabilisers and antioxidants are used during proôÙ
cessing which slow down the degradation process. Antioxidants work by neutralising free
radicals and preventing oxidative chain scission, while stabilisers absorb or block harmful
radiation, thereby helping to preserve the structure of the polymer. [11, 12, 13] These
additives play a crucial role in maintaining mechanical properties, thermal stability, and
overall performance of the material. However, over time, stabilisers and antioxidants are
gradually used up, which is especially problematic for ocean plastics because they are
exposed to harsh conditions and physical wear for years or even decades. This prolonged
exposure can deplete the stabilisers and antioxidants, leaving the material vulnerable to
further degradation. [13]
Despite this, degradation in POs typically starts from the surface and works its way inward.
[14, 15] This means that while the outer layers of the plastic may be severely degraded,
the core of the material could still contain enough additives to provide protection from
degradation both during and after recycling. This gradient in degradation raises an imôÙ
portant question: Is the remaining antioxidant content in the core enough to stabilise the
material during recycling, or is additional restabilisation necessary to restore its quality?
Understanding this balance is key to figuring out how to effectively recycle ocean plastics
and turn them into useful materials again.
While the core may offer some protection, the recycling process introduces new chalôÙ
lenges that affect the quality of the final product. The mechanical mixing during recyôÙ
cling of the material distributes the nonôÙdegraded, virginôÙlike core evenly throughout the
polymer matrix. [14] This process effectively dilutes the degraded regions, leading to an
improvement in the materialãs overall mechanical properties and potentially creating the
perception that the material has been ãrevivedã without additional additives. [16] However,

this uniform distribution of degraded material also introduces more sites for oxidative or
thermal degradation to initiate, which can cause the material to lose its strength and quality
more quickly once recycled. [10, 16, 17, 18] This highlights the need for strategies to not

stability.
A promising approach to improving the stability of POs, and one that this thesis will foôÙ

1.1
Purpose of Thesis
While recycling offers a potential solution, the degradation of materials in the ocean poses
significant challenges, including the loss of stabilisers and reduced material quality. CurôÙ
rent recycling methods often fail to restore the properties of severely degraded plastics,
limiting their usability in highôÙperformance applications. This thesis aims to address these
challenges by exploring the potential of reactive extrusion and restabilisation to improve
the quality and durability of recycled ocean plastics. By doing so, this research contributes
to the development of more sustainable recycling practices and supports the transition to
a circular economy for plastics.
1.2
Goal of Thesis
The primary goal of this thesis is to investigate the degradation of polyolefins found in
ocean waste and to develop strategies to improve their quality through reactive extrusion
and restabilisation. Specifically, this thesis aims to:
1. Understand to what extent ocean plastic has degraded and its impact on material
properties, including thermal and oxidative stability.
2. Explore the correlation between degradation and processability, identifying key facôÙ
tors that influence the recyclability of ocean plastics.
3. Evaluate the effectiveness of reactive extrusion in improving the properties of the
material by introducing stabilisers, antioxidants, and other reactive agents.
4. Develop practical methods to improve the longôÙterm stability and performance of
recycled ocean plastics, enabling their use in highôÙperformance applications.
By achieving these goals, this research aims to contribute to the development of more
sustainable recycling processes and support the broader effort to reduce plastic pollution.

This chapter provides an overview of POs, focusing on how they end up in the ocean,
how they degrade, and the challenges of recycling them. We will start by exploring POs,
such as PE and PP, and why they are so common in marine plastic pollution. Next, we will

2.1
Polyolefins and their Way to the Ocean
POs are a family of polymers consisting of long hydrocarbon chains with a general strucôÙ
ture of (CH2CHR), where R is an alkyl group.[19] POs offer a unique combination of duraôÙ
bility, low cost, and versatility. This has led to their dominance in the plastic industry, with
PE (R=H) and PP (R=CH3) alone accounting for 45% of total plastic production in 2023,
equivalent to over 185 million tonnes. [20] Their large use in singleôÙuse products means
that they are also among the most frequently discarded plastics, contributing significantly
to global plastic waste. [1, 2]
One of the key characteristics that make POs commercially valuable apart from their low
manufacturing cost is their tunable material properties. PE and PP exist in several forms
as seen in Figure 2.1 including lowôÙdensity polyethylene (LDPE), highôÙdensity polyethyôÙ
lene (HDPE), and isotactic polypropylene (PP). The properties of POs such as flexibilôÙ
ity, strength, and chemical resistance, vary depending on the polymerisation methods
used and molecular structure. For example, LDPE, with a highly branched structure, is
commonly used in thin films and flexible packaging. HDPE and PP, on the other hand,
have more rigid and crystalline structures and are often used in containers and industrial
applications. These variations not only affect performance in manufacturing, they also
influence how these plastics behave in the environment.

plastic pollution in marine environments. Studies have shown that over 80% of all material
on the sea surface consists of POs, with PE and PP making up 51% and 30%, respectively.
[4, 5] As you may have noted, this is disproportionate to their global production share of
45%. One reason for this over representation on the sea surface is the low density of

2.2
Degradation of Polyolefins
Throughout their lifetime, polymers are exposed to a variety of physical, chemical, and
biological factors that initiate chemical reactions that can lead to changes in molecuôÙ
lar weight and chemical composition, known as degradation. Degradation involves irreôÙ
versible changes in the polymer chain and can cause undesirable changes in appearance,
such as texture or colour, and mechanical properties, such as tensile or impact strength.
As illustrated in Figure 2.4, degradation can modify the polymerãs chain length through
processes like chain scission and crossôÙlinking or alter its chemical composition by introôÙ
ducing functional groups such as carbonyl or hydroxyl groups [10, 9, 16]. These changes
ultimately affect the materialãs mechanical, thermal, and chemical stability. [35, 36]
Figure 2.4: Examples of major pathways in polymer degradation. (a) Chain branching (b)
CrossôÙlinking (c) ChainôÙscission (d) Oxidation & Functionalisation.
While POs, such as PE and PP are known for their good resistance to many environôÙ
mental factors and biodegradation, they are still susceptible to degradation under certain
conditions. Common factors that lead to degradation in POs are UV radiation, heat, meôÙ
chanical stress, oxygen, moisture, and exposure to other chemicals. [37, 7, 38] Some of
these factors, such as heat and mechanical stress, are more frequent during processing,
while others, like UV radiation and oxygen, are naturally occurring during the use phase
of the material. [36]
During processing, polymers are exposed to harsh conditions, with high temperature and
in many cases a lot of mechanical stress. While it is desired to process POs in the absence
of oxygen, to reduce degradation during processing, it is rarely achieved. The high soluôÙ
bility of oxygen in POs combined with air and moisture getting into the equipment allows
for oxidative degradation to take place. [18, 17] Depending on the oxygen availability,
either thermal degradation or thermoôÙoxidative degradation will take place. [39]
In contrast, degradation during the use phase is often slower but can be equally impactful
over time. During this phase, temperatures are typically milder, and mechanical stresses
are lower. However, the availability of oxygen is much higher and the degradation process
can occur over much longer time spans. In this environment, UV radiation serves as
the primary energy source, driving photoôÙoxidation, which causes chain scission and the
formation of carbonyl groups. [7, 38, 40, 41, 42, 43, 44] For POs that have been floating in
the ocean for years or even decades, this type of degradation has shown to be severe.[3,
14, 31, 8]

PE and PP are relatively resistant to photoôÙoxidation since the polymer backbone of POs
do not contain any unsaturated bonds, which is necessary for initiation. However, small
amounts of impurities or structural abnormalities in the material can allow photoôÙinitiated
degradation to take place. [6] Comparing them, PP is less stable than PE due to the
presence of tertiary carbon atoms in its backbone. These tertiary carbons have weaker
CôÙH bonds and form more stable radicals upon hydrogen abstraction, making PP more
prone to oxidative degradation. In contrast, PE contains mostly primary and secondary
carbons, which are more resistant to radical formation and degradation. [35]
Given the central role of oxygen in both thermoôÙoxidative and photoôÙoxidative degradation,
a deeper understanding of oxidation mechanisms is necessary.
2.2.1
Oxidation of Polymers
The reaction between molecular oxygen and polymers is an autocatalytic process, comôÙ
monly referred to as autoxidation. This reaction happens spontaneously and begins with
an induction period, during which oxygen uptake is relatively slow, and hydroperoxyl side
groups (ôÙOOH) gradually form along the polymer chain. [13] The length of the induction
period varies significantly depending on the materialãs composition. For example, if iniôÙ
tiators are present, the induction time can be very short, whereas adding stabilisers like
antioxidants can extend it significantly. Over time, the buildup of hydroperoxides leads to
an exponential increase in the oxidation rate, causing the material to become more brittle,
as demonstrated by Guillet et al. [45]. This explains why conventional PO products conôÙ
taining stabilisers often show little change for years, followed by a rapid decline in quality
within weeks or months. [46] However, due to the limited diffusion of oxygen in solid PO,
this exponential increase eventually slows down. [13]
Autoxidation is a freeôÙradical chain reaction process and similar to polymerisation reacôÙ
tions, it consists of three distinct steps: initiation, propagation, and termination, as seen
in Figure 2.5. [47]
1. Initiation:
The initiation step begins the oxidation process. Chemical bonds in the polymer
chain are broken forming alkyl radicals (RãÂ) (Reaction 1). These radicals are genôÙ
erated by energy sources such as heat or light, mechanical stresses like shear or
impact, or chemical impurities. Although these radicals are somewhat stabilised by
delocalisation, they can quickly react with molecular oxygen to form peroxyl radicals
(ROOãÂ) (Reaction 2).
2. Propagation:
The peroxyl radicals abstract hydrogen atoms from other polymer chains, forming
hydroperoxides (ROOH) (Reaction 3). The buildup of hydroperoxides is slow beôÙ
cause Reaction 3 involves breaking a CôÙH bond, which requires higher activation
energy than Reaction 2. As a result, under normal atmospheric conditions, ReacôÙ
tion 3 determines the overall oxidation rate.
Hydroperoxides can further decompose into two radicals, similar to the initiation reôÙ
action, as shown in Reactions 4a and 4b. The newly formed alkoxyl and hydroxyl
radicals can continue the chain reaction by abstracting hydrogens from other polyôÙ
mers (Reactions 5, 6a, and 6b). Among these, Reaction 6b, known as öýôÙscission,
has the most significant impact on the material. öýôÙscission is a chainôÙbreaking reacôÙ
tion that cleaves the polymer chain into fragments containing carbonyl compounds,
such as aldehydes and ketones.

2.2.2
Biodegradation of Polyolefins
The degradation mechanisms discussed so far have been abiotic, driven by physical and
chemical factors, such as UV radiation, heat, and oxygen. While abiotic degradation is
the primary pathway for PO degradation, it is important to note that biodegradation can
also occur under certain conditions. In general, POs are resistant to microbial attack due
to their long, stable hydrocarbon backbones and lack of functional groups that enzymes
can target. However, oxidative degradation products, such as shorter polymer fragments
containing oxygen (e.g. carbonyl or hydroxyl groups) can become biodegradable. These
fragments are broken down by microorganisms into smaller molecules like acids or esters,
which can eventually be mineralised into carbon dioxide and water. [37]
2.2.3
Preventing Degradation in Polyolefins
For most applications, degradation is undesirable, and to prevent it, additives such as
antioxidants (AOs) are added to polymers. AOs are classified into two main types: chainôÙ
breaking and preventive, and are often used in combination to prevent degradation throughôÙ
out the whole lifeôÙcycle of the material. [49, 13]
ChainôÙbreaking AOs, commonly referred to as primary AOs, function by slowing down or
stopping oxidation processes after they have begun. They achieve this by interrupting
the propagation step of the autoxidation cycle (Figure 2.5, Reactions 2 and 3), where the
AOs neutralise the propagating radicals ((ROOãÂ) and (RãÂ). ChainôÙbreaking AOs can act
as either electron/hydrogen donors or acceptors. Donor AOs, such as hindered phenols
and aromatic amines, reduce ROOãÂ to ROOH. Acceptor AOs, such as quinones and
stableôÙfree radicals, oxidise alkyl radicals into nonôÙreactive products. [13]
In contrast, preventive AOs, commonly referred to as secondary AOs, focus on stopping
oxidation before it even starts. The most common type, peroxide decomposers, such as
phosphites and sulphur compounds, break down hydroperoxides before they can genôÙ
erate free radicals. Other preventive AOs include UV absorbers, which protect against
sunlight, and metal deactivators, which prevent metals from accelerating oxidation. [13]
ChainôÙbreaking and preventive AOs are often used in combinations to improve their effecôÙ
tiveness, creating synergistic effects. However, not all AO interactions are beneficial, with
certain AOs interfering with each other or even accelerating degradation. As described
earlier in this chapter, the unknown composition of the ocean plastic combined with the
large variety of commercially available AOs can create complexities when stabilising the
material. Careful selection of AOs is therefore necessary to avoid interference between
AOs. [50]
One of the challenges with AOs is their mobility and volatility, which can impact their longôÙ
term effectiveness. Highly mobile AOs not only diffuse rapidly towards the oxidation site
and react fast with the radicals, but also diffuse easily towards the surface and leave the
polymer, without being effective. [49, 12]

Carbonyl Index - FTIR
As previously mentioned, oxidation produces gradually more carbonylôÙcontaining comôÙ

Oxidative Induction Time - DSC
Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) can determine oxidative stability through a method

(210ãÎC, 6 bar) whereas the degraded material showed OITs of around 5 minutes (180ãÎC,
6 bar). Similar values have been seen for nonôÙstabilised virgin PP with OITs of around

2.3.3
Rheology
Since POs are nonôÙNewtonian in the molten state, shear thinning is common. Shear
thinning is when the viscosity decreases with increasing shear and has shown to indicate
changes in the polymer structure due to degradation. [57, 58] A study by Cuardi et al. [59]
investigated thermal oxidation of HDPE by comparing viscoelastic properties for different
degradation temperatures and times, as well as the impact of oxygen during processing.
They showed that in an oxygen deficient environment there was an increase in viscosôÙ
ity as temperature increased, but no change in shear thinning. However, in an oxygen
rich environment, shear thinning became severe as processing temperatures and times
increased. This behaviour has been consistent in literature and it has been suggested
that the increase in viscosity as temperature increases is due to thermal degradation that
causes crossôÙlinking and chain branching. On the other hand, the shear thinning effect
has been explained by oxidation, causing chainôÙscission. [57, 58]

2.4.1
Mechanical Recycling of Polyolefins
The growing accumulation of plastic waste, has made recycling a critical component of
global efforts to reduce pollution and transition to a circular economy. [30] Mechanical
recycling is the most common approach and involves melting and reprocessing plastic
waste into new products. While this method is effective for relatively clean and undeôÙ
graded materials, it often fails to restore the properties of severely degraded plastics. [60]
As mentioned earlier in this chapter, during recycling the material is exposed to high heat
and mechanical stresses, which can further degrade the polymer and reduce its perforôÙ
mance. The degraded parts are also able to evenly distribute during recycling, which
introduces more sites for degradation. As a result, studies have shown that recycling of
degraded POs improve the quality temporarily but quickly loses its strength, limiting its
use to lowerôÙvalue products. [10, 16, 17, 18] Studies have shown that unstabilised recyôÙ
cled POs become brittle and lose ductility, while stabilised POs have been able to retain
their ductility. [61, 17]
Generally, mechanical recycling of POs follows similar trends regarding changes in molecôÙ
ular structure and properties as seen in Figure 2.6. [60] For PE, recycling increases chain
branching over time, leading to an increase in viscosity and a decrease in crystallinity,
modulus, and elongation. This increase in viscosity makes PE harder to process after
recycling through methods such as injection moulding. [62, 63] PP, on the other hand,
shows opposite behaviour. PP tends to undergo chain scission during recycling, resultôÙ
ing in a decrease in molecular weight. Studies have shown that the degree of crystallinity
for PP stays stable while the viscosity decreases after multiple extrusions, indicating that
the material is slowly breaking apart. [64, 65] For oxidised material that contains many
reactive sites, these changes in molecular structure are even more severe.

2.4.2
Reactive Extrusion
To address these challenges that mechanical recycling faces, reactive extrusion has emerged
as a promising technique for upgrading recycled plastics. Reactive extrusion combines
mechanical recycling with chemical modifications, allowing for the introduction of stabilisôÙ
ers, compatibilisers, or other reactive agents during the extrusion process. In reactive
extrusion, the polymer melt is mixed with reactive additives, such as peroxides, maleic anôÙ
hydride, or epoxy compounds, which can modify the polymerãs molecular structure. [65,
66, 67] For example, peroxides can induce controlled chain scission or crossôÙlinking, deôÙ
pending on the processing conditions, while maleic anhydride can graft functional groups
onto the polymer chains, improving compatibility with other materials.
Over the years, extensive research has been conducted to modify POs through reactive
extrusion, and many methods for modifying the polymerãs molecular structure have been
developed. One of the main modifications studied is the introduction of longôÙchain branchôÙ
ing (LCB) along the polymer backbone. The LCB can improve the melt strength of the
material, which can be crucial for processing. [68, 66, 65] By using peroxideôÙcontaining
additives, reactive sites can be created during extrusion, enabling the formation of LCB
as seen in Figure 2.7. [66] However, achieving this requires precise control over processôÙ
ing conditions. For instance, a study by Stanic et al. [66] compared the effectiveness
of six different peroxides (see Table 2.1) in improving the melt strength of PP by the use
of LCB. The study found that processing conditions, particularly temperature, were critôÙ
ical. At 180ãÎC none of the peroxides successfully increased the molecular weight, and
the viscosity of the material decreased significantly. When the temperature was raised
to 240ãÎC, only one (PODIC C126) of the six peroxides tested was able to improve the
melt strength and molecular weight. However, while higher temperatures improved melt
strength, this also led to a reduction in mechanical properties for both virgin and modified
PP. This highlights the importance of balancing processing conditions and performance
factors when using reactive extrusion to upgrade recycled POs.
The main challenge with using peroxides is that the linear structure of the polymer is hard
to sustain. [67, 69] When heated up, the peroxide containing additives form free radicals

be challenging, especially for polymers with similar properties like PE and PP. These
materials are difficult to separate using conventional methods such as densityôÙbased or
optical sorting, and advanced equipment is often required to accurately distinguish beôÙ
tween them. As a result, PE and PP are frequently found together in recycled feedstock.
However, PE and PP are immiscible, meaning they do not blend well, and their mixtures
often exhibit poor mechanical properties due to weak adhesion between the two phases.

4.1
Collection and Pretreatment of Ocean Plastic
Plastic collected from the surface of the ocean was provided by The Ocean Cleanup, and
had been collected during seven expeditions in the time period between June 2022 and
May 2023 in the GPGP. The ocean plastic (OP) was provided as flakes of ca 5ôÙ12 mm
(see Figure 4.1) and had been pretreated at an industrial recycling facility during January
2024. The pretreatment consisted of five steps to ensure that the plastic was sorted into
fractions and any impurities were removed:
1. Sorting: The material was sorted into fractions depending on material grade (polyôÙ
mer type), colour, and object type (crates, buoys, etc.). Larger pieces of the material
was sorted by hand and smaller pieces were sorted with NIR sorting machines.
2. Shredding: Fractions were separately shredded into flakes.
3. SinkôÙFloat: A sinkôÙfloat technique was used to remove metals and other heavy
particles from the flakes, as well as removing loose dirt from the material.
4. Washing: The material went through two washing cycles with cold water to remove
sea salt and other impurities from the materials surface.
5. Drying and storage: After washing the material was dried with hot air and stored
in bigôÙbags.
The fraction analysed in this thesis was from a fraction called ãmixed cratesã, consisting
of mostly black fishing crates of rigid PE and PP. The ratio of PE/PP in the material was
estimated by The Ocean Cleanup to be 50:50. Separation into pure PE and PP streams
was not feasible, as the NIR sorter used in the pretreatment was unable to detect polyôÙ
mer types in black plastics. This ãmixed cratesã fraction was of particular interest for two
main reasons. First, surface degradation from environmental exposure introduces reacôÙ
tive sites in the polymers, offering an opportunity for chemical modifications typically not
possible in virgin polyolefins. Second, developing strategies for processing and upgrading
hardôÙtoôÙrecycle mixed PE/PP streams could improve their value and allow for the black
streams to be utilised in highôÙend application.

4.4
Restabilisation with Antioxidants
As previously mentioned, the material had clear signs of oxidative degradation, suggestôÙ
ing that there were little oxidative protection left and making the material vulnerable to
further degradation. Furthermore, as the first homogenisation trial showed, the material
degraded and became brittle and oily after long exposure to heat and mechanical stress.
Therefore, some initial restabilisation trials were done to assess the need for antioxidants
in order to reduce excessive degradation during extrusion. Samples of homogenised
material were extruded with and without AOs (1 wt% Irganox 1010 and Irgafos 168, 50:50
mix), referred to as OPôÙAO and OP unstabilised. The results showed a clear difference,
with the unstabilised material becoming brittle and snapping apart easily with little effort.
The samples with AO on the other hand had more ductile behaviour with more flexibility,
allowing for bending and twisting of the extruded material. Therefore, it was decided to
include the AO mix in all samples.
To quantify the oxidative stability of the OP, oxidative induction time (OIT) was used and
compared to PP (containing stabilisers and AOs). The samples were heated up to 190ô¯C
(10 K/min) under nitrogen flow and kept at the temperature. After 10 minutes at 190ô¯C,
the nitrogen flow was switched out for air flow for half of the samples and kept as nitrogen
flows for the other half.

precise concentration optimisation. As a result, a design of experiments (DOE) was esôÙ

Specifically, the aim with the DOE software was to analyse the results from the study

DOE Benchmark and Reference Materials
To compare the different material formulations, reference materials and benchmarks were
defined with the goal of providing more depth and relevance into the DOE. Without these
reference points, it would have been difficult to determine whether changes in material
properties were true improvements or simply differences. Multiple reference materials
were included to avoid missing important information that might be overlooked if the analôÙ
ysis only focused on OP. Therefore, both virgin POs and different variations of OP were
included: virgin HDPE, virgin PP, virgin HDPE/PP blends, OPôÙHOM, and the unstabilised
OP. This allowed for the OP to be fairly compared to its derivatives as well as to commerôÙ
cially available options.
Although several reference materials were used, it was important to define strict benchôÙ
marks for the interpretation of quality improvement. While no specific application or purôÙ
pose had been chosen for the OP, the most relevant benchmark was considered to be
a virgin like alternative that could be commercially used. For this, two HDPE/PP blends
were prepared (one containing 8 wt% VM and one without) to act as primary benchmarks
with a few additional target values for certain desired properties. Both blends were proôÙ
cessed in the same way as the OP samples to minimise variability and allow for a fair
comparison. The specific benchmarks targets will be described and discussed later on in
this chapter.

Summary of Results and Basis for DOE Outcome
Rheological analysis showed clear changes in elasticity and viscosity between samples,
especially with reactive additives. Materials containing R201 and R203 behaved more
elastically and had lower loss tangent values across the shear rate range. These changes
suggest successful branching and crossôÙlinking during processing. TMPTA and VM also
influenced the viscoelastic behaviour, with TMPTA broadening the molecular weight disôÙ
tribution and VM increasing the complex viscosity at higher shear rates.
Tensile testing supported the findings from rheology. Additives had a visible effect on stiffôÙ
ness, strength, and elongation. R201 and R203 did not show major improvements on their
own, which could indicate that too large concentration of the additives were used. TMPTA
increased stiffness and tensile strength, while VM improved elongation and toughness,
especially when combined with TMPTA. The AO mix improved both strength and strain at
break compared to unstabilised OP, likely by slowing down degradation while still allowing
some reactivity.
DSC results were less conclusive, but still showed that all DOE samples had a similar
degree of crystallinity, ranging from 42 % to 48 %. Minor shifts in crystallisation behaviour

total crystallinity.
While the material analysis provided valuable insight into how the additives affected the
material properties, the DOE study was used to optimise additive usage and improve the
overall quality of the blend. As discussed earlier in this chapter, the HDPE/PP blends were
used as the main reference to define benchmarks and parameter targets. For mechanôÙ
ical properties, the HDPE/PP blends consistently showed higher performance in terms
of strength (tensile and yield) and stiffness. However, since several additive formulations
also showed improved elongation and toughness, the benchmarks were adapted to reflect
a balance between strength and flexibility (aiming to maximise all mechanical properties).
As good compatibility between phases was one of the goals, the fracture location from
tensile testing was also included, where failure in the shaft (rather than near the shoulders)
was considered a positive outcome.
For the rheological results, both loss tangent and the crossover point were included as
parameters. The HDPE/PP blends displayed more viscous behaviour, so a higher loss
tangent value was chosen as the benchmark. For the crossover point, the aim was to imôÙ
prove melt strength and processability. Therefore, the benchmark was set to favour mateôÙ
rials with higher molecular weight (i.e., lower crossover frequency) and broader molecular
weight distribution (i.e., lower crossover modulus).
As previously discussed, the results from DSC and FTIR (crystallinity and carbonyl index)
were less reliable and showed limited trends. Because of this, their importance in the DOE
was kept lower. Still, the aim for crystallinity was set to maximise it, as higher crystallinity
is typically associated with more virginôÙlike behaviour in semiôÙcrystalline polymers. For
carbonyl index, the aim was to minimise it, as a lower value could indicate better oxidative
stability and less degradation.

4.14
Future Work
While this thesis provided important insights into the degradation and restabilisation of
OP, several areas remain unexplored. One of the key limitations was time. The DOE
analysis successfully identified an optimal additive formulation, but the actual material
could not be prepared and tested before the thesis deadline. Producing and analysing this
optimised material would be a natural continuation of the work, and would help confirm
the model predictions and assess if the combined additives actually would deliver the
expected improvements.
In addition to testing the optimal formulation, longôÙterm ageing tests would be useful to
better understand the stability of the materials over time. While many materials showed
promising strength and toughness directly after extrusion, their performance under acôÙ
celerated ageing or weathering conditions could provide insight in material quality over
time.
Another area that could be expanded is the assessment of oxidative stability using OIT.
In this thesis, OIT testing was mainly focused on unstabilised and AOôÙstabilised OP. By
including more formulations, especially those with reactive additives but no added antioxôÙ
idants, it would be possible to understand the individual contribution of each additive to
oxidative stability. With this, possible new solutions for slowing down degradation could
be found. Furthermore, since no OIT was found for the OPôÙAO, optimising the AO content
in the OP could support more sustainable product development.
The thesis also showed that TMPTA had a promising effect at low concentrations. HowôÙ
ever, due to the limited amount added, it was difficult to fully understand its mechanism.
Testing TMPTA at higher contents could reveal if crossôÙlinking or other reactions occur
more clearly, and help to better explain the rheological and mechanical results observed.
It could also strengthen the additiveãs influence in the DOE outcome.
In terms of methodology, the DOE was effective for identifying good combinations of the
selected additives. However, another approach would be to first screen a larger set of difôÙ
ferent additives to identify the most promising ones. A broader screening would allow for
more options and increase the chance of finding highly effective stabilisers or compatibilisôÙ
ers. After that, a more targeted DOE could be run on only the bestôÙperforming additives.
This twoôÙstep strategy might avoid overly focusing on the initially chosen additives.
Finally, the results also showed that the OP material was reacting by itself. This indiôÙ
cates that some changes may happen during extrusion even without additives. It could
therefore be interesting to explore simpler methods to improve quality, such as adding
small amounts of virgin HDPE or PP. These ãnonôÙadditiveã options may offer practical
and costôÙeffective ways to improve processability and mechanical properties.

4.15
Recommendations
The first and most important recommendation from this thesis is to avoid plastic waste in
the first place. The optimal solution would be if ocean plastic did not exist at all. If plastic
use cannot be avoided, it is important that it is disposed of in a proper way. Preventing
mismanaged waste is always better than trying to solve the problem afterwards.
For those working with plastic collected from the environment or other degraded waste
materials, the material should be sorted and cleaned as good as possible before recycling.
While this thesis focused on a blend of PE and PP, it is recommended to separate different
plastic types to create more homogenous materials. Blending multiple polymer types
is rarely a longôÙterm solution and should be avoided when possible. However, when
dealing with existing mixed waste, using compatibilisers (such as VM) can help improve
the properties of the material and make it more suitable for reuse.
Another key recommendation is to always restabilise heavily degraded materials before
processing. Without antioxidants, the material is very sensitive to heat and shear, which
leads to further degradation and brittleness as also shown in this thesis. Stabilising the
material beforehand helps to protect it during extrusion and gives it a better chance to be
reused in higherôÙvalue applications.
For anyone planning to scale up this type of reactive extrusion, it is important to assess the
safety of the process. Some additives, especially the reactive ones, can be exothermic
or form toxic byôÙproducts. This was not assessed in this thesis (apart from chemical risk
assessments prior to any preformed lab work), but it should be considered for industrial
operations to ensure safe handling. For nonôÙreactive additives like VM, this is less of a
concern, but still worth keeping in mind.

5
Conclusion
This thesis set out to explore how degraded polyolefins (PE and PP) from the ocean
can be restabilised and upgraded through reactive extrusion. While the environmental
challenge of ocean plastic remains the most important message of the thesis, the results
show that with the right treatment, even heavily degraded plastic can still be used again.
From the initial tests, it was clear that the plastic retrieved from the ocean had lost much
of its stabilisers and was sensitive to further degradation. This made the material hard
to process and required both fast and mild processing conditions. Still, the degradation
also introduced reactive sites that allowed the material to be modified, if handled properly.
The key challenge was to balance the positive effects like branching and compatibilisation,
without causing too much chainôÙscission that would weaken the material.
The additives tested in this study gave valuable insight into how the material could be
improved. The VM additive increased flexibility and helped compatibilise the PE and PP
phases. TMPTA improved strength and toughness, while the Nexamite additives created
more elastic and structured networks. However, their effect depends strongly on concenôÙ
tration, and more trials are needed to better understand how to get the best results.
The DOE method worked well in analysing the many variables and finding an optimal
formulation. Although the optimal material could not be tested in the lab due to time limits,
the statistical model gave useful predictions and confirmed that combining additives can
lead to synergies. At the same time, this thesis showed how complex the recycling of
degraded materials can be, with many small changes in structure affecting the end result.
Finally, even if the longôÙterm stability was only briefly studied, the results highlight the
importance of antioxidant restabilisation. Without it, further degradation happens easily
and leads to brittle and unusable materials. For future work, understanding the longôÙterm
performance and refining additive concentrations could improve the quality even more.
To summarise: while degraded ocean plastic poses many challenges, this thesis shows
that with proper restabilisation, reactive extrusion, and smart material design, it can be
brought back to life. It is not a perfect solution, but a step in the right direction towards
more sustainable use of plastics.
