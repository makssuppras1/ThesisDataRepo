Abstract
To ensure a competitive green energy sector, increasing the proﬁtability of wind farms is valuable. Two
aspects of the wind farm life cycle include determining the turbine placement and designing the cable network
that connects them. The two individual problems have been studied to great depth. However, optimizing
the turbine placement without considering the cable layout can lead to suboptimal positioning from a cable
infrastructure perspective, resulting in high cable costs. This thesis investigates whether including a simple
estimate of cabling costs during the turbine placement phase can lead to more cost-e!cient wind farm
layouts.
To estimate cable costs, we introduce and validate upper and lower bounds based on distances between
turbines and the substation - the ﬁnal connection point before electricity is transmitted onshore.
The
bounds are combined into a single estimate, where each turbine can be associated with a contribution to the
total cost. We demonstrate how this estimate can easily be incorporated during the preprocessing phase of
turbine positioning optimization, introducing a semi-integrated approach.
We implement a scalable Variable Neighborhood Search algorithm for the turbine placement problem and
a matheuristic for the cabling layout, demonstrating that the semi-integrated approach can save companies
millions of euros in cable installation costs, with only a minimal impact on overall power production. The
proposed method requires modifying only a single input parameter in the turbine placement problem, allowing
existing solution techniques to be used — making the approach both practical and impactful.

Introduction
The number of climate and weather related disasters has increased ﬁvefold over the past 50 years [1]. Sea
levels are rising nearly three times faster today than at the start of the 20th century [2] and decline in
biodiversity is increasing at an unparalleled rate [3].
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) states that climate changes are, without doubt,
caused by human activities through greenhouse gas emissions [2]. The IPCC further states that global surface
temperatures in 2020-2021 measured 1.1→C above 1850-1900 levels and changes exceeding 1.5→C may cause
harmful and irreversible e”ects.
Action, although arguably inadequate, has been taken by global leaders. The Paris Agreement [4], enacted
on November 4th, 2016, aims to limit the increase to “well below 2→C” and is currently signed by 195 parties.
Moreover, the 7th and 13th of the United Nations (UN) sustainability and development goals [5] from 2015
aim to “ensure access to a”ordable, reliable, sustainable and modern energy for all” and “take urgent action
to combat climate change and its impact,” respectively. In line with this, the European Union (EU) aims
for a 42.5% share of renewable energy by 2030 [6].
Wind power investments, among others, are a valuable step toward a sustainable future. In 2022, wind
power accounted for 53.6% of domestic electrical supply in Denmark [7]. To attract even more companies
to invest in green energy, such as wind farms, proﬁts must be competitive with common, non-sustainable
sources.
Therefore, the focus must be on increasing its economical proﬁtability—boosting revenue from
power production while reducing costs for customers.
Increasing proﬁts requires a thorough understanding of the wind farm life cycle, from planning to tearing
it down. It covers six key stages: project development, bidding, detailed design, construction, operation &
maintenance, and decommissioning [8]. The work begins with project development, where site data—such
as soil conditions—is gathered, feasibility is assessed, and the wind farm layout, including turbine placement
and cable routing, is designed. This phase also involves negotiations with suppliers and contractors to secure
agreements and cost estimates for the bidding process.
A successful bid opens up for the detailed design stage, where every aspect of the park is reﬁned before
construction begins. Once built, the wind farm enters operation—the ﬁrst stage where revenue ﬂows in.
Finally, at the end of its lifespan, the farm is carefully dismantled, ensuring minimal environmental impact.
This thesis delves into the design phase of wind farm development, focusing on how site data informs the
placement of turbines and the cabling that connects them. A wind farm developer typically starts with a
target energy output and the speciﬁcations of the wind turbines they plan to use. From there, the number
of turbines needed can be determined—but deciding where to place them is far more complex.

Traditionally, wind turbines were arranged in a regular grid, with optimization e”orts primarily focused
on maintaining adequate spacing between them [9]. However, modern wind farms utilize more advanced
optimization techniques, resulting in irregular turbine spacing [10, 11].
Placement is now inﬂuenced by
multiple factors, including the predicted power output at each location based on expected wind patterns.
Additionally, soil conditions and sea depth play a crucial role in determining construction costs. Another
vital consideration is the wake e!ect—a phenomenon where turbines disrupt wind ﬂow, e”ectively “stealing”
wind from those behind them and reducing overall energy production. In other words, a turbine shadows for
downwind turbines. Optimizing turbine placement is therefore a balancing act between maximizing power
generation and minimizing losses caused by wind interference.
Once the locations are set, the next challenge is linking them together. Every cable must ultimately lead
to a substation—whether directly or by weaving through other turbines. From the substation, electricity is
transmitted onshore; however, this phase of the cabling falls outside the scope of this thesis. Several factors
come into play in the cabling process, including cable capacities, costs, restricted areas within the wind farm,
and power losses due to resistance.
Naturally, longer cables drive up costs and energy loss—implying that closely spaced turbines are more
economical. However, this clashes with turbine placement principles, where spacing them farther apart min-
imizes the wake e”ect. These two challenges pull in opposite directions, meaning that an ideal turbine layout
minimizing wake might come with steep cabling costs, ultimately undermining the project’s proﬁtability.

Literature review
The wind farm layout problem (WFLOP) and wind farm cable routing problem (WFCRP) are two widely
studied topics due to the signiﬁcant installation costs and revenues from projected energy generation. Tra-
ditionally, the two have been solved sequentially, where the WFLOP solution is used as an input to the
WFCRP [13]. This section gives a brief overview of the progress in both cases, along with recent work in a
combined solution approach.
The WFLOP problem consists of placing k wind turbines within a given area, A, while maximizing power
production and minimizing installation costs. It is common for restricted areas to exist within A, where
turbines cannot be placed. These areas are often called obstacles. To avoid damage due to turbulence from
other turbines, a minimum separation between turbines, d, must be upheld. Furthermore, the area A can
be treated as either a continuous space or discretized into a set of W available positions. In this thesis, the
latter approach is considered.
Studying the WFLOP problem was ﬁrst done in 1987 by Kaminsky [14] for a set of turbines on a line. The
paper assumed a ﬂat terrain and a single, ﬁxed wind direction, parallel to the line of turbines. Given the
placement of the ﬁrst and last turbines on a line, the problem considered is determining the location of the
remaining turbines. A non-linear optimization problem was deﬁned and solved using a generalized reduced
gradient method on instances up to 11 machines.
A two dimensional instance was considered in 1994 by Mosetti et al. [15]. The authors represented the
wind farm layout as a binary sequence, establishing a one-to-one correspondence between binary digits and
potential turbine positions. They applied a genetic algorithm [16] in which mutation involved ﬂipping a
bit, and crossover produced two o”spring from two parent layouts. During crossover, a segment of the ﬁrst
parent’s sequence was passed to the ﬁrst child, while the rest of that child’s sequence was ﬁlled in using
the second parent’s sequence. The second child was created in a similar manner, using the complementary
segments from the two parents. Their algorithm was tested on three scenarios: (1) constant wind speed and
direction, (2) constant wind speed with multiple wind directions, and (3) variable wind speed and direction.
However, in all cases, the number of available turbine positions did not exceed 100.
Chen et al. [10] was among the ﬁrst to propose methods of solving large-scale instances with up to 5376
available positions. In addition to the windmill placement, the authors considered two turbine heights. That
is, not only did they determine the turbine locations, but also wether it should be a (relatively) short or tall
turbine. A nested genetic algorithm [16] was employed, in which an outer genetic algorithm generated turbine
layouts, and each layout was subsequently assigned a height conﬁguration by an inner genetic algorithm.
In 2016 Fischetti et al.
[17] solved instances with up to 20,000 available positions using a matheuristic
approach. For reference, a matheuristic can be considered a hybrid between mathematical optimization and

metaheuristics [18]. To improve the current solution, a MIP model is solved iteratively according to the
proximity search [19] method. In short, the proximity search algorithm takes the current solution, s, with
objective value f(s), and solves a modiﬁed MIP model with output s→. The objective is to minimize the
distance between s and s→while adding a constraint implying that the new solution should be better than
the current solution, i.e. f(s→) > f(s) in the case of maximization.
More recent work includes Cazzaro and Pisinger [11], which adapts the variable neighborhood search (VNS)
[20] heuristic to the WFLOP and solves instances with over 21,000 available positions. The method includes
a shake phase that diversiﬁes the search by perturbing the current solution using various strategies, such as
randomly moving turbines. Moreover, a local search phase is applied, using 1-opt (move one turbine to a
better nearby position) and 2-opt (simultaneously move two turbines to the best pair of nearby positions)
to improve the solution until no further gains are possible.
Recall from section 1 that turbines shadow for one another, referred to as the wake e”ect. Determining the
wake from a turbine requires Computational Fluid Dynamics simulations, although estimation techniques
avoiding this have been proposed. Among the models most widely used is the Jensen model [21] that portrays
the wake as a trapezoid. However, in our formulation of the problem, the wake e”ect coe!cients are given
as inputs to the WFLOP, which means that the solution methods to the WFLOP are independent of the
model used to calculate the wake e”ect. Moreover, it is common to assume a linearly additive property when
estimating the wake e”ect, meaning that we can evaluate the wake a”ect at any turbine by summing the
wake a”ect caused by the other individual turbines. Although this thesis assumes a linear additivity, other
papers, such as Zhang et al. [22], have focused on the nonlinear properties through their proposed constraint
programming and mixed integer linear programming models.
With the results from the WFLOP, i.e. the turbine positions, the WFCRP aims to determine the cabling
between them. Given a set of cable types, with associated costs and capacities, the aim is to connect the
turbines to a substation - either directly or indirectly through other turbines - in the most e!cient way. This
thesis assumes a single ﬁxed substation and no limits to the number of cables connected to it. Moreover,
the cable capacities must be upheld. That is, each cable has a maximum load, which, based on information
about power production, can be translated into a maximum number of supported turbines.
The ﬁrst mixed integer programming (MIP) model for the WFCRP with multiple cable types was outlined
in 2010 by Fagerfj¨all [23]. Ways of avoiding cable crossings was proposed by Bauer and Lysgaard [24]. The
authors formulated the WFCRP as a Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) and solved it using the Clark and
Wright savings heuristic [24]. Given the large number of potential cable crossings, and the corresponding
constraints, the problem is addressed by iteratively adding only the violated constraints to the model,
continuing this process until all constraints are satisﬁed.
Handling power losses due to resistance in the cables is shown in Fischetti and Pisinger [25]. The authors

introduce a preprocessing method to model power losses by replacing each original cable type with a set of
sub-cables of varying capacities. These sub-cables incorporate both the installation cost and the expected
revenue loss due to power dissipation, which increases with the number of turbines connected.
A number of heuristic algorithms to handle the cable routing, such as simulated annealing, tabu search
and VNS, were introduced by Cazzaro et al. [26]. Here, the authors concluded that tabu search and VNS
performed the best, before Cazzaro and Pisinger, in a following paper [27] showed that using a matheuristic
approach outperforms the heuristics of the previous article. In addition, methods of avoiding obstacles were
introduced in the latter work using a visibility graph. A visibility graph connects to nodes if and only if they
“see” each other, i.e. the straight line connecting them does not cross an obstacle.
Combining the WFLOP and the WFCRP was ﬁrst done by Fagerfj¨all [23], but the author concluded that
the problem was only solvable for small instances due to computational complexity. In 2022 Fischetti et al.
[12] proposed a branch-and-cut method on an integrated WFLOP and WFCRP model that was tested on
instances with up to 100 positions. The ﬁrst model to run on large-scale instances (over 21,000 positions)
was proposed in 2023 by Cazzaro et al. [11]. The authors outlined a VNS approach to solve the combined
problem. Compared to the integrated model of Fischetti et al. [12], separate models are deﬁned for the
WFLOP and WFCRP. In each iteration of the VNS, the solution of the WFLOP is modiﬁed, followed by
the WFCRP. The authors concluded that the combined approach showed notable improvements from the
sequential approach.

However, unlike the approaches of
Fischetti et al. [12] and Cazzaro et al. [11], we propose a semi-integrated method that incorporates estimated

simultaneously avoiding excessive cable costs that would otherwise arise from ignoring cabling considerations
during the WFLOP.

Formal Model
In this thesis we solve two problems, the wind farm layout problem (WFLOP) and the wind farm cable
routing problem (WFCRP), using a semi-integrated approach. The goal of the WFLOP is to choose the
optimal placement of wind turbines in order to maximize revenue from power production. Given a set of
turbine locations, the aim of the WFCRP is to ﬁnd the cheapest way of passing the electricity from the
turbines to the substation. This section provides a formal deﬁnition of the two problems, beginning with
the WFLOP. In the next section, section 4, we demonstrate how to incorporate an estimate of the cable cost
into the objective function of the WFLOP, thereby developing a semi-integrated approach.
3.1 | Wind Farm Layout Problem (WFLOP)
The goal of the WFLOP is to choose individual wind turbine locations considering the following elements:
• Increasing power production in free wind
• Reducing power loss due to wake a”ect
• Reducing construction costs
To determine a high-quality solution, the following inputs are provided:
• The set of available turbine positions, W
• A list of construction costs B at each available position
• The number of turbines, k, to place
• A distance matrix, D
• The minimum distance d between turbines
• A power and wake matrix, P
Before continuing, the power matrix is deﬁned. Given n available turbine positions, the power matrix, P,
is an n ↑n matrix. The diagonal elements Pi,i represents the power production at position i in free wind,
namely with no other turbines nearby. An element Pi,j, where i ↓= j, corresponds to the loss of power at
position j due to wake from a wind turbine placed at position i. This value is negative. By summing Pi,j
over all positions i where a turbine is located, we can quantify the total wake e”ect exerted on the turbine
placed at j. In order to compare the values of the power matrix, P, to the costs in B, the values in P are
converted from units of power (MW) to units of revenue (Me). This is done using an energy price factor
(EPF) that will be deﬁned in subsection 7.5.

5.1 | Wind Farm Layout Problem (WFLOP)
The WFLOP is solved using a metaheuristic approach. In particular, variable neighborhood search (VNS)
[20]. Before introducing VNS, we ﬁrst provide some background information. In the context of the WFLOP,
any solution belongs to a deﬁned neighborhood of solutions, meaning that solutions within the same neigh-
borhood share certain similarities. The speciﬁc deﬁnition of a neighborhood depends on the chosen criteria.
For example, if the solution space were represented as a two-dimensional grid, one could deﬁne neighborhoods
based on either a shared y↗coordinate or a shared x↗coordinate. Under the ﬁrst deﬁnition, the points (1, 2)
and (6, 2) would be considered neighbors, whereas they would not be under the second deﬁnition.
The main idea of VNS is that multiple neighborhood deﬁnitions exist, and the algorithm systematically
switches between them in each iteration. This allows us to search di”erent solution spaces — ideally enabling
us to escape local minima. A detailed explanation of VNS will be provided in subsection 5.1.2; however, the
ﬁrst step of the algorithm concerns generating an initial solution.
5.1.1 | Wind turbine placement - initial greedy solution
An initial solution to the WFLOP is found by taking advantage of the similarities between our problem and
the PDSP, as described in subsection 3.1. The PDSP is NP-hard, which can be showed by reduction to
the clique problem [29]. Approximation algorithms exist for cases where the triangle inequality is satisﬁed,
such as the greedy heuristic presented by Ravi et al.
[30] with ratio ε = 4.
Kincaid [31] presented a
metaheuristic approach, using simulated annealing and tabu search on cases with up to 25 nodes. Pisinger
[28] proposes a set of upper bounds to the PDSP, as well as an exact branch-and-bound method capable
of solving moderately large problems, speciﬁcally instances with up to 250 nodes within a 15-hour time
limit. However, to initialize the algorithm, a greedy heuristic is employed, which often produces an optimal
solution. Based on this observation, the author concludes that there is reason to believe that the greedy
heuristic—running in O(n3) time—may perform well on large instances that are otherwise infeasible to solve
to optimality.
Due to the similarities between the WFLOP and PDSP, we adopt the greedy heuristic used by Pisinger [28]
to the WFLOP. The heuristic begins by placing a turbine at each available position, before removing the
“worst” turbine, one at a time. Turbines are removed until we reach the desired number of turbines, k.

5.2.1 | Grouping turbines
Dividing turbines into groups is a key aspect of the cabling matheuristic. Conceptually, we aim to group
turbines based on the angle of the vector between the substation and each turbine. In other words, if one
were to stand at the substation and look in a speciﬁc direction, all visible turbines should belong to the same
group.
To better understand the beneﬁts of this approach, consider an alternative where turbines are randomly
assigned to groups.
With turbines positioned on multiple sides of the substation, the optimal solution
would either require a large number of direct connections to the substation or extensive cable crossings to
interconnect turbines. As a result, the likelihood of achieving an optimal solution would be low, as many
turbines would end up with multiple direct connections when shared links could have been more e!cient.
Additionally, the probability of excessive cable crossings would be signiﬁcantly higher.
In order to create the groups, a sweep algorithm similar to that described by Cazzaro et al. [26] is utilized,
where obstacles are taken into consideration. To begin, each turbine is associated with a set of angles. How
these angles are determined will be explained in the following paragraph. Further, the turbines are sorted
based on these angles and placed in groups based on their ordering. That is, the ﬁrst n turbines are placed
in the same group, then the next n turbines are placed in the following group, and so on.
Before concluding this subsection, we explain how each turbine is associated with a set of angles. In addition
to the size of each group, as well as the turbine, substation and obstacle locations, the sweep algorithm takes
two inputs. These are the start angle, measured from the positive x-axis with the substation considered to
be the origin, as well as the sweep direction, i.e. clockwise or counterclockwise. Now, before each turbine
is associated with a set of angles, they are associated with a set of edges, corresponding to the shortest
path from the substation to the respective turbine, avoiding obstacles. It is in fact these edges that are
mapped to angles. The ﬁrst angle is calculated by considering the angle between the input start-angle and
the ﬁrst edge, measured with respect to the sweep direction. Moreover, the remaining angles are given by
the angle between the previous edge and the current edge, measured with respect to the sweep direction.

After the turbines have been grouped, the MIP model is solved for each bundle before the solutions are
combined. A key question now arises: what constitutes the optimal grouping? As described in the previous
subsection, it seems logical to place nearby turbines in the same group. That being said, how large should
the group be? Should they have the exact same size? What if we move one turbine to the next group? This
leads us to the solution method utilized, matheuristics [18], a hybrid between mathematical optimization and
metaheuristics. Essentially, the turbine grouping is determined using classical metaheuristic techniques, such
as local search and pertubation, while the solution to the individual groups is determined using mathematical
optimization-solving a MIP model using Gurobi [32] in Julia in this case.
Before providing the full algorithm, two of the main tools utilized are explained, namely the pertubation
method and the local search. The input angle to the sweep algorithm, explained in subsection 5.2.1, is stored
between iterations. The perturbation method involves randomly modifying this angle before rerunning the

sweep algorithm. When modifying the groups, randomness is placed on the size of the groups. That is,
values a and b are given as inputs to the algorithm and each time a group is created a size is randomly
chosen from a uniform distribution on the interval [a ↗b, a + b]. All remaining turbines will be placed in the
last group. Once all groups are ﬁnalized, the MIP model is solved to optimality, or until the time limit is
reached, for each group.
The turbine ordering, calculated in the sweep algorithm, is utilized in the local search. The local search
consists of moving a small number of turbines to neighboring groups and resolving the MIP model for the
modiﬁed groups. Two groups, say i and j are considered neighbors if there are no turbines belonging to
other groups between the turbines of group i and j in the list of ordered turbines. A group i is considered
to be of higher order if the turbines of group i are higher in the ordered list of turbines than that of group
j. Group j is then said to be of lower order than group i. Speciﬁcally, for all neighboring groups, i, j, we do
the following: Assume i is of higher order than j. First, the n turbines of lowest order in group i are moved
to group j. After the MIP model has been solved, the original groups are restored before the n turbines
of highest order in j are placed in group i. Again, the MIP model is solved for the modiﬁed groups. In
this thesis, a ﬁrst-improvement approach is used, i.e. the solution is modiﬁed as soon as an improvement is
found. This method is run until no improvements are found. Note, we keep a record of all groups modiﬁed
without achieving an improved objective value to prevent redundant calculations.

6.1 | Obstacles

when developing solutions to bypass obstacles.

6.1.3 | Shortest path between substation and all available turbine positions
In the end, we want an estimate of the shortest distance from each available turbine position to the substation.
One way of doing this would be to create a visibility graph with all available vertices, as well as the obstacle
corners, before running Dijkstra’s algorithm [36] with the substation as the starting point.
We recognize that the shortest path from an available position to the substation is one of two types. The
ﬁrst sort is a direct segment from the turbine position to the substation. This is the case if the direct edge
does not cross any obstacles. On the other hand, if the available position is not visible from the substation,
the path cannot be direct and we arrive at the second variety. Then any shortest path visits solely a set of
obstacle corners before reaching the substation, according to Lemma 15.1 in Berg et al. [35]. In this case,
the ﬁrst segment is a direct path from the turbine position to an obstacle corner. Moreover, the shortest
path would equal the length of the segment from the turbine position and to the ﬁrst obstacle corner, plus
the shortest distance from the speciﬁc corner and to the substation. When calculating the shortest path
from all positions to the substation, we take advantage of this observation.
As we will see in subsection 7.1, the largest synthetic instance has 21,709 available positions, shown in
Table 5.
On the other hand, there are at most 132 obstacle corners in all synthetic instances [37].
As
mentioned, we want to use Dijkstra’s algorithm [36], with a running time of O(V + E), where V and E
equals the number of vertices and edges, respectively. With n available turbines and c obstacle corners,
we have an order of (n + c)2 edges to process, giving a running time of O((n + c)2).
That being said,
we also need to consider the running time of producing the distance matrix. There are (n + c)2 potential
edges, and for each edge we must check whether it intersects c edges. This yields a total running time of
O((n + c)2 + (n + c)2c) = O((n + c)2c). Hence, instead of running the shortest path algorithm with the
base station, obstacle corners and all available turbine positions, the algorithm can ﬁrst be run with the
base station and obstacle corners, before using these results to determine the shortest path for all available
turbine positions.
Given the shortest distance from the substation to all obstacle corners, we now explain how to determine
the shortest distance between the substation and all available turbine positions. Given an available position
p and the substation s, we either return the distance of the direct path between p and s if s is visible from
p, or we ﬁnd an obstacle corner visible from p that could potentially give the shortest path. Instead of
checking whether all corners c are visible and then calculating the potential path length from p ↙c ↙s,
we recursively consider the endpoints of the obstacle edges intersected by the line segment from p to s.
The algorithm deﬁned in Algorithm 3 shows how the shortest distance to the substation is computed for a
position, p, not visible from the substation, s, given one of the edges intersected by the line segment between
p and s. Essentially, in each recursive call we consider the two endpoints c1, c2 of the current obstacle edge.
If the endpoint ci is visible from p, we report the distance from p to ci, plus the distance from ci to s. On

6.2 | Cable Crossing
Although it is technically possible to install two intersecting cables, it is highly discouraged due to the
substantial increase in costs and maintenance requirements. This is the reasoning behind the cable crossing
constraint (Equation 19) in the MIP model described in subsection 3.2.
Unfortunately, with |V | vertices and a magnitude of |V |2 edges, this can potentially lead to a number of
constraints on the order of |V |4.
To avoid the large number of constraints, the MIP model is ﬁrst run
without the constraint in Equation 19. Once the optimizer is done, a check for crossing edges is run. For all
intersecting edges in the solution, a constraint prohibiting the intersection is added to the MIP model before
Page 40 of 80
==PAGE:48==
Asta S. Rustad
Implementation Details
the model is rerun.
Dealing with cable crossings is done di”erently in the cabling matheuristic described in 2. Although the MIP
model avoids intersections within a group of turbines, it does not guarantee no intersections between groups.
This is handled by recognizing that the optimal cabling of each group is determined sequentially. Hence, by
locking the cables of the previous groups and avoiding all edges that intersect with placed cables, we avoid
cable crossings. This can of course lead to sub-optimal solutions, where a cable is locked that prevents a
highly proﬁtable edge from being opened. As with metaheuristics in general, the expectation is that, given
enough iterations, the algorithm will eventually discover the more favorable cabling.

In all cases, tuning is done by choosing three instances from Table 5. The speciﬁc instances are outlined in

8.3 | Wind Farm Cable Routing Problem (WFCRP)
For the WFCRP, we outline the tuning process for three parameters in the matheuristic described in section
5.2.2: the number of turbines to move between groups during each iteration of the local search (num-to-
move), the average number of turbines per group (group-avg), and the variance in group size (group-var)
when turbines are grouped at the end of the sweep algorithm (section 5.2.1).
The tuning process applied is the same as the one described in the previous subsection. The main di”erence
is that the tuning is done on instances D (zone 0), E (zone 1) and F (zone 0) from Table 5, as instances A,
B and C, particularly A and C, simply had too few turbines, meaning that the optimal solution was often
reached in all cases. Moreover, note that the WFCRP is a minimization problem, so the parameter leading
to the lowest objective value is given the best rank.
To begin, group-avg and group-var are ﬁxed to 8, and 6, respectively. By varying num-to-move, Figure 18
shows that moving zero turbines leads to the overall best performance. This is surprising, as it essentially
means that the local search should be removed. At the same time, the local search is slow, since the MIP
model is run to optimality each time the groups are modiﬁed. Even though we avoid running the MIP model
on groups that have been optimized earlier, it seems time is better spent by simply allowing the matheuristic
to run the sweep algorithm as many times as possible.

Discussion of Results and Algorithms
Overall, incorporating a cable cost estimate into the WFLOP encourages turbine placements closer to the
substation, potentially lowering cable costs while slightly de-emphasizing the value of power production
revenues and construction costs. In most instances, this approach allows companies to increase proﬁts by
millions of euros. In the rare case that the semi-integrated approach results in a worse overall solution, the
company will not incur a loss in proﬁts, as the solution from the sequential approach can simply be used
instead.
As seen in section 9, the semi-integrated signiﬁcantly reduced the relative cable costs by up to 18%, while the
revenues from the WFLOP decreased by at most 0.09%. The reason we do not see a relatively large e”ect on
the integrated objective value is simply because the revenue from the power production is signiﬁcantly larger
than the cable costs, given our data. However, cable costs should be paid as the wind farm is built, while
revenues from power production are estimates that may take years to materialize. Hence, saving millions of
euros during the construction phase can be highly beneﬁcial for companies, as it lowers the barrier to entry,
and the importance should therefore not be underrated.
10.1 | Comparison to Existing Approaches
In contrast to the branch-and-cut algorithm proposed by Fischetti et al. [12], which was limited to instances
with up to 100 available positions, our method demonstrates signiﬁcantly greater scalability, having been
applied successfully to instances with over 11,000 available positions.
However, a notable gap remains
between 11,000 and 21,000 available positions, which is addressed in the work by Cazzaro et al. [44]. As
discussed in section 9, while our method is tested on the same instances as in the previously cited study,
direct comparison is challenging due to the unspeciﬁed EPF employed by the authors. That being said, by
calculating the relative di”erences in objective value found by Cazzaro et al. [44] and applying them to the
objective values of the WFLOP and WFCRP in our sequential approach, we saw that in all but one case, our
method led to greater savings. Still, one can argue that this is not a completely fair comparison. Although
cable costs are on a relatively similar scale, the WFLOP objective values reported in their results [44] are
roughly three times lower than ours. As a result, cable costs carry greater relative weight in their objective,
likely prompting the algorithm to tolerate higher power losses to o”set the relatively expensive cabling. This
helps explain why their reductions in both power production and cable costs are more signiﬁcant than in
our results, as shown in Table 11.
Moreover, in this thesis we have introduced a practical and scalable way of computing an upper bound on
the integrated problem, which was not available in the work by Cazzaro et al. [44] or Fischetti et al. [12].
The former does not address the integrated upper bound while the latter’s branch-and-cut method is limited
Page 69 of 80
==PAGE:77==

to small instances, and the LP relaxation of the full problem is “arguably very poor” [12]. However, our
calculation of upper and lower bounds can be done in polynomial time (O(n3)), where we observed a relative
gap of 1% ↗9% on large-scale problems.
10.2 | Limitations and Potential Improvements
Ideally, we wish to run our algorithms on instances as large as those in Cazzaro et al. [44], if not larger. In
terms of the matheuristic to solve the WFCRP, we saw in subsection 9.5 that it seems like the algorithm
does not always completely converged within the 2-hour time limit. This could, for instance, be solved by
increasing the running time of the algorithm for larger instances. Further, the 2-opt procedure in the local
search of the VNS is computationally heavy. Recall that the 2-opt considers each pair of turbines in the
solution and tests all possible combinations of placements within their respective m “closest positions.” As
described in subsection 5.1.2, with |T| turbines and |W| available positions, the running time of one iteration
is O(N 2
t m2|W|). For the larger instances, one could instead use a 1-opt procedure, where only one turbine
is considered at a time and all m closest positions are tested in order to ﬁnd an improving solution. This
yields a running time of O(Ntm|W|). Although each individual iteration of 1-opt is expected to yield smaller
improvements than a 2-opt iteration, as any move considered by the 1-opt is also available to the 2-opt, the
ability to run signiﬁcantly more iterations may outweigh this limitation.
Moreover, a pitfall of the semi-integrated approach is that the estimated cable cost only depends on the
distance between a turbine and the substation. However, for example, having two turbines at a distance of
d and 2d from the substation can lead to quite di”erent cable costs depending on their relative angle to the
substation. Consider Figure 33, where the turbines are located on the same side of the substation in one
case, and on opposite sides in the other case. The former would lead to signiﬁcantly lower cable costs, but
our cable estimate would not favor one over the other.

Further, a penalty is applied when a turbine is placed far from the substation. This e”ect multiplies if
additional turbines are located in the same distant area — for instance, the objective function would be
penalized roughly twice as much if two such turbines are placed far from the substation. However, in reality,
the additional cable cost incurred by placing a second turbine in the same remote area is relatively small,
while the potential gain in power production could be signiﬁcant.
Currently, the cable cost estimate is based solely on the shortest path between each turbine and the sub-
station, without accounting for the spatial relationships among turbines. However, what if we incorporated
relative turbine placement into the estimation, similar to how wake e”ects are calculated? Speciﬁcally, when
evaluating the wake e”ect on a turbine, all other turbines are considered, with penalties increasing as tur-
bines are placed closer together. In a similar fashion, we could introduce a penalty — or even a reward —
based on speciﬁc spatial conﬁgurations of turbines.
For example, a small reward could be added to the objective function when a set of turbines and the
substation are aligned in a straight line (and lie on the same side of the substation). This reward could
gradually decrease as the alignment deviates from a straight path.
Such a reward mechanism could be
embedded in the o”-diagonal elements of the modiﬁed power matrix R, originally deﬁned in subsection 3.1
and later redeﬁned in subsection 4.2.

Conclusion
Given the ongoing challenges resulting from climate change and rising global demand for sustainable energy,
maximizing the proﬁtability of renewable energy projects like wind farms is becoming increasingly important.
As proﬁts grow, subsidies can be reduced, and the attractiveness of wind farm investments rises, accelerating
the overall transition to green energy. This thesis set out to explore whether incorporating a simple estimate
of cabling costs during the turbine placement phase could lead to more economically e!cient wind farm
designs, without the need for overly complex optimization techniques.
To estimate cable costs, we introduced upper and lower bounds based on the distance between turbines and
the substation. The ﬁnal estimate was deﬁned as a linear combination of these bounds. A key commonality
between them is that their calculations can be broken down into individual turbine “contributions,” allowing
each position to be associated with an estimated share of the total cabling cost. This estimated contribution
can easily be integrated during preprocessing of the WFLOP by adjusting the diagonal of the input power
matrix, which captures both the power output at each turbine position and the wake-induced losses. In
fact, by adding a lower bound estimate to the input power matrix and computing an upper bound for the
WFLOP — using, for instance one of the upper bounds proposed by Pisinger [28] — we have introduced a
novel method for deriving an upper bound on the integrated problem.
We implemented a scalable VNS algorithm for the WFLOP and a matheuristic for the WFCRP, demon-
strating that the semi-integrated method can save companies millions of euros in cable installation costs
with only a minor reduction in overall power production. Saving cable costs is particularly beneﬁcial as
they should be paid as the wind farm is built, while income from power generation is based on long-term,
uncertain estimates that unfold over several years.
As far as we are aware, there have been two previous attempts at the problem. Compared to the branch-
and-cut method by Fischetti et al. [12], tested on instances with up to 100 positions, our proposed method is
scalable on instances over 11,000 available positions. The range between 11,000 and 21,000 available positions
remains a notable gap, which is handled in the work by Cazzaro et al. [44]. As the previously cited work
utilized the same test instances as was done in this thesis, a direct comparison between the results would
have been interesting. Unfortunately, this requires knowledge on the EPF utilized in the project, which was
not disclosed. Furthermore, our method introduces a practical upper bound computed in polynomial time,
something not available in earlier approaches. To our knowledge, this is the ﬁrst approach that o”ers a
reliable upper bound suitable for large-scale instances.
Future work includes adapting the algorithms to handle larger instances, for example by using a 1-opt
method instead of 2-opt in the VNS local search. Additionally, future improvements could reﬁne the cable
cost estimates by accounting for the relative positions of turbines — basing the estimate on turbine-to-turbine
Page 73 of 80
==PAGE:81==

relationships rather than treating each turbine independently.
Regardless of future reﬁnements, the core advantage of the proposed method lies in its simplicity and
compatibility. Since only one of the inputs to the WFLOP is modiﬁed, the semi-integrated approach enables
the use of existing, state-of-the-art methods for both the WFLOP and WFCRP. This means that, if a
company already has implementations of the two solution methods, shifting from a sequential to a semi-
integrated approach requires minimal changes. Yet, the potential savings are substantial — making the
method not only practical, but also highly impactful in real-world applications.
