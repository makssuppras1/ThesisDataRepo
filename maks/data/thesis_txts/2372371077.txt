In November 2014, Dr. Manuel Linares of the Polytechnic University of Catalonia, and Dr. 
Jérôme Chenevez, of the Technical University of Denmark, initiated a Swift-XRT monitoring of four GCs 
– 47 Tucanae, Messier 28, Messier 62 and Terzan 5 – with the purpose of finding new low-luminosity 
transient LMXBs, detecting short and faint outbursts from known transient NS LMXBs and monitoring 
the X-ray activity of the known MSP population to search for more MSP – NS LMXB transition pulsars. 
Spectra, positions, images and light curves have been produced, using the Swift online tool 
of the University of Leicester, for the above mentioned GCs. The results have then been analysed with 
the Xspec software. Spectra have been tested using various different models, in order to evaluate the best 
fit possible, for single observations and for each overall cluster. Missing observations have been discarded 
while observations that presented too few data or poor fitting have been merged together with other 
neighbouring ones within a maximum time window of plus or minus 4 days from the modified Julian 
date of the problematic observation. Positions and images have also been obtained, in order to determine 
the locations of the brightest sources in the clusters. 
Light curves have been extracted to evaluate whether and when a cluster was in outburst 
and the eventual presence of X-ray bursts. Terzan 5 and Messier 28 have undergone outburst phases, and 
both clusters presented bursts, observed by Swift: two the former and one the latter. Terzan 5 also showed 
a particular burst-like feature, which luminosity peak was not high enough to consider it as a burst. 
The luminosity excess of each cluster, with respect to its average luminosity in quiescence,

Globular clusters (GCs) are efficient factories of X-ray binaries and millisecond pulsars (MSPs). 
High angular resolution Chandra observations have uncovered numerous faint X-ray sources lurking in 
the cores of Galactic GCs. Many of these are quiescent neutron stars (NSs) in low-mass Xray binaries 
(LMXBs) and binary MSPs, and definitive proof of the connection between these two types of neutron 
stars has been recently found in the globular cluster M28 [1]. 
Both quiescent NS LMXBs and MSPs show low level of X-ray activity 10ଷଷ≤𝐿௑≤10ଷହቂ
௘௥௚
௦ቃ and 
10ଷଶ≤𝐿௑≤10ଷଷቂ
௘௥௚
௦ቃ, respectively, that cannot be detected with present all-sky X-ray monitors. These 
active periods can be due to enhanced accretion, ”mini-outbursts”, the formation of an accretion disk 
around a MSP or to changes in the pulsar wind – companion star interaction. 
Chandra/XMM observations are typically years apart, and hence these low-level X-ray activity 
goes often unnoticed. 
This project, conducted at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) under the supervision of 
Dr. Jérôme Chenevez and with the help of Dr. Manuel Linares of the Polytechnic University of Catalunia, 
is relative to the study of neutron stars (NSs) in four globular clusters (GCs) from the data collected by 
the Swift satellite from October 10th, 2010, to present days. 
The four GCs taken into consideration in this project are 47 Tucanae, Messier 28, Messier 62 and 
Terzan 5.

47 Tucanae, discovered in the namesake constellation by Nicolas Louis de Lacaille in 1751, who 
erroneously misinterpreted it as the nucleus of a comet, is one of the most massive GC in our Galaxy, and 
also one of the brightest that appears in the sky, with a very bright and dense core, which was the subject 
of an extensive search for planets through the Hubble Space Telescope. Also known with the abbreviation 
47 Tuc and the acronym NGC104, it is at a distance of about 4.5 [𝑘𝑝𝑐] from the Sun, with the centre of 
the cluster in the position determined by 00 ℎ  24 𝑚  05.67 𝑠 in Right Ascension (RA) and by 
−72 °  04 ′  53.20 ′′  in Declination (D) [2]. 
No planets have been found so far in the GC, although some were expected around Sun-like stars, 
confirming the hypothesis that planets are rare in globular clusters. However, 47 Tucanae's core contains 
anyway a high number of stars of scientific interest, such as X-ray sources and binary star systems: 
cataclysmic variable stars (CVs), with white dwarfs accreting from their companions, and low-mass X-
ray binaries (LMXBs), with neutron stars not currently in the accreting phase but still observable by the 
X-rays emission coming from their hot surface [3]. 
47 Tucanae has also at least 23 millisecond pulsars (MSPs) known [4], [38], the second largest 
pulsar population in a GC [5]. The accretion of material, in a previous X-ray binary phase, from 
companion stars, is thought to be the reason of their spinning up [5]. The case of 47 Tucanae W, instead, 
suggests that the mass transfer from the companion to the central object, a neutron star, is still occurring, 
indicating that this is a system that is transitioning from an accreting LMXB to a millisecond pulsar [6]. 
The emission in the X-ray band of many millisecond pulsars in 47 Tucanae has been detected 
thanks to the Chandra X-ray Observatory, [7], while observations with the Fermi Gamma-ray Space 
Telescope detected, for 47 Tucanae, the first gamma-ray emission from the millisecond pulsars of a GC 
[8]. 
The eventual presence of a Black Hole in the centre of 47 Tucanae is still under debate. Although 
Hubble Space Telescope data would constrain the mass of a possible BH to less than approximately  ∼
1500 𝑀⨀, [9], where 𝑀⨀ is the mass of the Sun, it has been announced in February 2017, according to the 
motions and distributions of pulsars in 47 Tuc, that in the cluster’s core might be located an intermediate-
mass BH of around ∼2200 𝑀⨀ [10].

Messier 28, discovered in the constellation Sagittarius by Charles Messier in 1764, is a GC also 
known with the abbreviations of M 28 and NGC 6626. It is at a distance of about 5.5 [𝑘𝑝𝑐] from the Sun, 
with the cluster centre in the position determined by 18 ℎ  24 𝑚  32.89 𝑠 in Right Ascension and by 
−24 °  52 ′  11.40 ′′  in Declination [2]. 
M 28 has 18 RR Lyrae type variable stars known so far, and it was the first GC in which a 
millisecond pulsar, PSR B1821–24, was observed, event occurred with the Lovell Telescope at the Jodrell 
Bank Observatory in 1986 [11]. In the following years, the Green Bank Telescope detected other 11 
additional millisecond pulsars within M 28, for a total of 12, making it the cluster with the third largest 
known population of pulsars, behind Terzan 5 and 47 Tucanae [12]. It also contains 2 known NS transients 
[38]. 
 
 
 
Messier 62, discovered in the constellation Ophiuchus by Charles Messier in 1771, is a GC also 
known with the abbreviations of M 62 and NGC 6266. It is at a distance of about 6.8 [𝑘𝑝𝑐] from the Sun, 
with the cluster centre in the position determined by 17 ℎ  01 𝑚  12.60 𝑠 in Right Ascension and by 
−30 °  06 ′  44.50 ′′ in Declination [2]. 
M62 contains a high number of stars of scientific interest, such as 89 variable stars, mostly of the 
RR Lyrae type [13], but also at least 6 known MSPs and 1 binary BH candidate [40].

Terzan 5, discovered in the constellation Sagittarius by Agop Terzan in 1968 [15] and initially 
named Terzan 11, was catalogued by the Two-Micron Sky Survey as IRC 20385 [16]. Also known with 
the abbreviation Ter 5, it is at a distance of about 6.9 [𝑘𝑝𝑐] from the Sun, with the cluster centre in the 
position determined by 17 ℎ  48 𝑚  05.00 𝑠 in Right Ascension and by −24 °  46 ′  48.00 ′′  in Declination 
[2].  
The cluster follows a complicated and not yet well determined orbit around the Galaxy centre, 
but it is currently moving towards the Sun at a speed of approximately 90 ቂ

Its core is very small, around 0.5 [𝑝𝑐] in diameter [18], while the volume mass density is very high,

The discovery, in 2009, that in the cluster at least two generations of stars with different ages, 12

formation of many close binaries, including binary systems containing a neutron star [22]. 
Furthermore, in addition to discrete X-ray sources, a diffuse non-thermal X-ray emission, a high

5. The high energy gamma rays are thought to be originated in the magnetosphere of millisecond pulsars, 
while ultra-high energy gamma rays are probably produced via inverse Compton scattering of relativistic 
electrons emitted by the pulsars off the cosmic microwave background radiation [20].

The major goal of this project was the study of NSs in the four above mentioned GCs using the 
Swift X-ray Observations. 
More particularly speaking, the following goals have been achieved: 
1. 
Plotting the spectral parameters as a function of time to evaluate the trend  
and use them to detect problematic observations, with too few counts or bad fitting; 
2. 
Checking the spectra of single observations for the four GCs, previously  
obtained by Dr. Jérôme Chenevez and Dr. Manuel Linares and already published on the 
website www.iac.es/proyecto/SwiftGloClu; 
3. 
Merging single observations with too few counts, which were also close in  
time (plus or minus 4 days from the modified Julian date of each problematic observation); 
4. 
Fitting the spectra with different combinations of models, in order to  
evaluate the best fit; 
5. 
Inspecting the light curves of the clusters, in order to verify  
the presence of phases of outbursts and bursts; 
6. 
Speculating, through inspection of positions and images, about the  
positioning of the brightest sources in the clusters; 
7. 
Processing global spectra, positions and images for each GC,  
considered as the merging of all its observation together, in order to compare their general 
trends.

Swift is a multi-wavelength space observatory aiming to study the Gamma-ray Bursts (GRBs). 
The on board instruments work together to monitor GRBs and their afterglows in the Gamma-ray, X-
ray, Ultraviolet and Optical wavelength ranges [25]. 
Gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) are the most powerful explosions in the Universe since the Big Bang: 
occurring more or less once per day, they are brief and intense, such as flashes from all different directions 
lasting from a few milliseconds to a few hundred seconds [24]. 
Swift is included in the NASA's medium explorer (MIDEX) program and was launched into a low-
Earth orbit (LEO) on November 20, 2004, at 17:16:00 UTC, on a Delta II 7320-10C expendable launch 
vehicle. Headed by the NASA Goddard Space Flight Center, the mission was created as a partnership 
between the United States, which operates through the Pennsylvania State University, and an 
international consortium with the United Kingdom, with the University of Leicester and Mullard Space 
Science Laboratory, and Italy, with the participation of the Italian Space Agency (ASI) and the Brera 
Astronomical Observatory. 
==PAGE:22==
8 
 
The Swift Mission Operation Center (MOC), which commands the satellite, is hosted by the State 
College, Pennsylvania, and operated by the Pennsylvania State University. The Swift Science Data Center 
(SDC) and archive are instead located at the Goddard Space Flight Center in Washington D.C. 
Swift data are available to the scientific community via data centres in the three countries and 
other ground stations, the main of which is in Malindi, Kenya, at the Broglio Space Centre, operated by 
ASI. 
 
 
The satellite sends a burst's location to ground stations in a few seconds after it is occurring, giving 
the possibility to both ground and space based telescopes to observe the burst's afterglow. 
The Swift GRB Mission consists of a robotic spacecraft, called Swift, which helds 3 co-aligned 
instruments on board, to enable the most detailed observations possible: 
 
 
The Burst Alert Telescope (BAT)
 
The X-Ray Telescope (XRT) 
 
The Ultra Violet – Optical Telescope (UVOT)
 
The XRT and UVOT are able to produce sub-arcsecond positions and multi-wavelength light 
curves for GRB afterglows. The BAT, instead, is a wide Field-of-view (FOV) coded-aperture GRB imager 
that is able to determine arcminute GRB positions on board within a time window of 10 ÷ 20 [𝑠]. 
The spacecraft has the possibility to slide rapidly and autonomously, using very efficient 
momentum wheels, to the position the BAT is pointing to, in approximately 50 [𝑠]. Its name is, in fact, 
not an acronym but a direct reminding of this slew capability, and a reference to the namesake nimble 
bird (Swift). 
Positions and images by the various instruments are then sent as soon as available from the 
spacecraft via the Tracking and Data Relay Satellite System (TDRSS) to the Gamma Ray Coordination 
Network (GCN). The GCN transmits the first results to the scientific community through the Internet for 
a rapid response and to allow eventual follow-up observations by other ground and space based 
==PAGE:23==
9 
 
telescopes. When the satellite passes over the ground station in Malindi, more detailed data are broadcast 
to the data centre, which processes and releases them for public access within half an hour. 
Swift is available, in between GRB events, for other scientific observations, and the scientific 
community, universities and other organizations can submit proposals for particular requests. 
 
 
 
The aim of the large field-of-view telescope BAT is to detect GRB events and determine 
their coordinates in the sky. 
In order to observe bursts with different intensities, durations and characteristic, the BAT has a 
large dynamic range and trigger capabilities: it uses a 2-D coded aperture mask and a large-area solid-
state detector array for the detection of weak bursts, and its large FOV for that of bright bursts. Long 
gamma-ray emissions from bursts can also be simultaneously observed together with the XRT and 
UVOT, because the BAT coded aperture FOV includes the fields-of-view of the other two instruments. 
With the BAT it has also been possible to produce a sensitive hard X-ray all-sky survey within 
two years [24]. 
The instrument is able to cover a large fraction of the sky: in the fully coded mode this is 
approximately 1.4 [𝑠𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑎𝑑], while in the partially coded mode about 3 [𝑠𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑎𝑑] of the 12.6 [𝑠𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑎𝑑] 
corresponding to the full sky (4 𝜋 solid angle). The BAT computes the position of an event, with an 
accuracy of 1 ÷ 4 [𝑎𝑟𝑐𝑚𝑖𝑛], within a time window of 15 [𝑠]. This very first position information is 
immediately sent to the ground stations, so that some ground based telescopes can point and start 
observing the GRB.

Rather than the traditional Uniformly Redundant Array (URA) pattern, the BAT coded aperture

telescope requires the aperture to be much larger than the detector plane, which is not uniform since 
there are gaps between the detector modules [26]. 
A reduction of 95 % of the background from the isotropic cosmic diffuse flux and the anisotropic 
Earth albedo flux is obtained with a graded-Z fringe shield, which is located both under the detector 
plane and around the mask and the detector plane. This shield is formed of layers of Pb, Ta, Sn and Cu, 
thicker close to the detector plane and thinner close to the mask [26]. 
Groups of 128 detector elements are assembled into 8 𝑥 16 arrays, each connected to 128-channel 
readout Application Specific Integrated Circuits (ASICs). Each detector modules contains two of these 
arrays, further grouped by 8 into blocks. Due to this hierarchical structure and the nature of the coded 
aperture technique, the BAT can tolerate losses of individual pixels, individual detector modules and 
blocks without losing the ability of burst detection and localisation. The CZT array usually operates at 
the nominal temperature of 20 [℃], with temporal and spatial thermal gradients kept in the range of 
±1 [℃] [26]. 
A figure-of-merit (FOM) algorithm decides if the detected event is important enough to request a 
slew manoeuvre of the spacecraft, and the command is imparted if the new detection has a higher merit 
than the pre-programmed observation. The FOM is implemented completely in the software and can be 
varied by adjusting the parameters of the actual criteria or by adding new criteria [26]. 
The BAT can operate in two modes: the survey one, in order to produce hard X-ray survey data, 
and the burst one, in order to produce a burst position. When in the survey mode, the BAT collects count-
rate data in 5 [𝑚𝑖𝑛] time bins for 80 energy intervals [27]. When, instead, a burst is detected, the 
instrument switches into a photon-by-photon mode with a ring-buffer, in order to save pre-burst 
information. The burst trigger algorithm searches for excesses in the detector count rate, and the burst 
trigger threshold, which has a typical value of 8 𝜎, is adjustable, spanning in the range 4 ÷ 11 𝜎 above 
background noise and constant sources [27]. 
Its imaging capability is a crucial characteristic of the BAT. Every 5 [𝑚𝑖𝑛], the instrument collects 
detector plane maps, which are included in the spacecraft telemetry stream. To detect and locate the 
position of a source, sky images are searched and consulted. The on-board software, following the burst 
trigger, makes sure that the trigger corresponds to a point source, hence avoiding to consider many 
background sources, such as magnetospheric particle events and flickering in bright galactic sources [27]. 
An event time-stamping with the BAT has a relative accuracy in the order of 100 [𝜇𝑠] and an absolute 
accuracy with the spacecraft clock in the order of 200 [𝜇𝑠] [27].

The XRT is able to capture images and perform spectral analysis of the GRB afterglows. The 
instrument renders a more precise location of the GRB, with a typical error circle of 2 ÷ 5 [𝑎𝑟𝑐𝑠𝑒𝑐] in

The XRT uses a Wolter Type I X-ray telescope with 12 nested mirrors, focused onto a single Metal-

The mission of the Swift satellite is, as previously said, to discover GRBs and measures their X-
ray and UV/optical afterglows with arcsecond accuracy, going on with the spectral observation until they 
fade out. Swift has found approximately 90 bursts per year since the beginning of the mission, has 
performed the most accurate study of GRBs and has detected the most distant objects in the Universe. 
Thanks to the Guest Investigator programme and its flexible Target of Opportunity programme, its 
versatility makes of Swift an ideal tool for monitoring transient and variable sources. Data from the on-
board instruments are available to the scientific community through the Swift online website, at the link 
www.swift.ac.uk, where it is also possible to build X-ray products and find more information about the 
mission and GRBs [36]. 
Using this online tool, it is in fact possible to build XRT light curves and spectra, or to obtain 
standard, astrometric and enhanced positions of any object observed by Swift. An additional function 
allows to receive these products directly via e-mail, once the processing is complete. Although it is a very 
versatile instrument, the service is designed merely to study point-sources only, while results for extended 
==PAGE:33==

sources may be not correct [36]. Open issues, related to data analysis and generated products, are 
discussed and resolved in the XRT digest pages. 
 
 
 
Despite the primary target of the mission is to detect GRBs and study their X-ray and optical 
afterglows, Swift can also perform observations of other transient astrophysical sources, such as 
supernovae, cataclysmic variables (CVs), active galactic nuclei (AGNi) and comets. 
The characteristics that make of Swift a suitable tool for a wide range of Targets of Opportunity 
(ToOs) rely on the rapid response capability to hard X-rays, the presence of co-aligned instruments 
covering the X-ray and the UV/optical bands and the instruments planning schedules, with commands 
that can be changed or added within a matter of hours. Usually, ToOs are reviewed at the daily planning 
meetings and, when approved, included into the schedule as soon as possible. ToOs do not prevent Swift 
to respond to GRBs, but they should use more than one instrument and it is required that the target’s 
position in the sky is more than 46 ° from the Sun, more than 21 ° from the Moon and more than 6 ° from 
the orbit pole [36]. The most preferable ToO requests are those at a high hour angle from the Sun, 
particularly greater than 9 h, and with durations between 1 ÷ 20 ∙10ଷ [𝑠] [36]. Furthermore, a significant 
amount of charge can be deposited in the CCD pixels by very bright optical sources, affecting the on-
board recognition system of X-ray events. Thus, it is important, before requesting a ToO, to check for 
these sources in the FOV of the desired target [36]. 
There is also the possibility to realise tiled observations, in case of a source error region larger than 
the XRT FOV, corresponding to a diameter of 23.6 [𝑎𝑟𝑐𝑚𝑖𝑛] [36].

The predicted structure of a NS consists, regarding the inner part, of a core, a degenerate neutron 
fluid mixed with a few percentage of protons and electrons with a radius of approximately 10 ÷ 15 [𝑘𝑚]

the interior [42]. 
A crust of ions and electrons, with thickness of ~1[𝑘𝑚], surrounds the core, and approaching the 
inner layers it changes to neutron-rich nuclei, and neutrons and electrons. The crust is rigid, due to 
Coulomb forces between the ions, though protons in the inner parts can be superfluid and the outermost 
layer is a gaseous atmosphere which is thought to be originally Fe, but also lighter elements since the star 
is accreting matter from ejected material and the interstellar medium ISM. The atmosphere thickness is

The cooling of a neutron star, born with an initial temperature of ~10ଵଵ [𝐾], occurs through core

is usually detected in the X-rays [42]. 
The thickness of the atmosphere of a NS is usually enough to absorb X-rays, and, considering that 
high-energy photons tend to come from deeper layers than photons of lower energy, determining the 
surface temperature directly from measured spectra has not been realised so far. In fact, fitting the 
observed spectra with a blackbody model returns temperatures higher than the actual surface

All fast spinning pulsars with strong magnetic fields are surrounded by a power wind nebula 
(PWN), which represents the predominant feature in most X-ray observations of NSs. For example, the 
Crab pulsar is surrounded by a PWN radiating strongly from the radio range to [𝑇𝑒𝑉] γ rays, and 
approximately 95% of its X-ray emission comes from the PWN [42]. 
The PWN radiates isotropically and can thus easily be observed even if the pulsar itself is not 
detectable because of a beaming effect [42]. 
A PWN is formed by a cloud of highly relativistic particles, electrons and positrons, and a 
magnetic field of the order of 𝐵= 10ିହ÷ 10ିଷ [𝐺], some orders of magnitude higher than the average 
interstellar magnetic field, which has a value of 𝐵ூௌெ= 10ି଺ [𝐺] [42]. 
Electrons moving according to the magnetic field emit synchrotron radiation, whose spectrum can 
be represented by a power-law continuum, and this is considered as the signature of non-thermal emission 
[42]. 
To be slowed down, the particles should pass through a shock located close to the pulsar, where 
the emission is brightest, and, once at the shock, the particles are accelerated into the power-law 
spectrum. Before the shock all the velocities of these electrons are directed outwards, while after the shock 
they change directions, becoming randomly oriented. During the path throughout the nebula, the 
velocity of the electrons remains approximately the speed of light [42]. 
The PWN should appear as a hollow feature in the proximity of the pulsar, due to the fact that 
there is no radiation coming from the orderly flow before the shock, followed by a bright shock around 
the pulsar, and then, since the particles lose energy travelling outward through the envelope, a decrease 
in brightness. The form of the shock looks like a torus surrounding the pulsar, because of the cylindrical 
geometry of the field of the spinning star. Moreover, as depicted in Figure 3.1, the near side of the torus 
appears brighter due to Doppler brightening effect, since the radiation emitted by relativistic electrons is 
strongly concentrated in the direction of motion [42].

In the LMXB, the presence of the compact object distorts the mass-losing star into a characteristic 
pear–shape with the vertex on the inner Lagrangian point [42]. The low-mass companion star has evolved 
and, since it fills its Roche lobe, it is losing mass, which flows onto the central object. This flowing matter 
forms a stream that impacts the accretion disc, forming a bulge, and, due to viscous forces, it is gradually 
accreted onto the compact object, where the X-rays are emitted [42]. This mass transfer is stable only if 
the donor star is less massive than the compact object [42]. In the case of NS systems, the donor star has 
then to be less massive than the Sun, causing the LMXB to be usually optically faint and with the donor 
star often undetectable [42]. 
The distribution of the observed LMXBs around the Galaxy is concentrated in and around its 
centre. In fact, these systems are associated with the older population II stars in the Galactic Bulge, and 
therefore the mass of their donor must have a mass comparable or lower than that of the Sun, because 
stars with mass > 1 𝑀⨀ would have evolved and disappeared long ago [42]. 
The main difference between the LMXB and the HMXB models relies on the mechanism of mass 
transfer. The material that leaves the donor star cannot flow directly onto the compact object, due to its 
angular momentum, but has to orbit around it, generating an accretion disc. In the HMXB, instead, the 
presence of the stellar wind carries very little angular momentum, and it is thus unlikely for an accretion 
disc to be formed, at least a large one. In a LMXB the wind is very feeble and an eventual resulting X-ray 
component would be completely undetectable. In this kind of systems, the disc is the responsible for many 
of the behaviours observed [42].

Thermonuclear burning of the accreted H and He into heavier elements is expected, both stable 
and unstable, depending on the rate at which accretion of matter onto the NS proceeds, being piled up 
and compressed towards regions of increasing density and temperature [66]. 
During a thermonuclear burst of type I, a thermonuclear runaway occurs when the heat cannot 
be transported as fast as it is produced and the burning layer becomes thermally unstable, yielding to a 
shell flash up to 10ଷ଼÷ 10ଷଽ[𝑒𝑟𝑔] in tens of seconds [67]. 
Within a few seconds, the majority of the energy is at the outermost layers of the neutron star 
and, while the photosphere cools down, this energy is emitted thermally [62]. If the magnetic field of the 
NS is strong enough, it can stabilise the nuclear burning, which may explain the fact that thermonuclear 
bursts have not been observed so far from accreting NSs in HMXBs [67]. 
Stable thermonuclear burning on accreting NSs has not been directly observed to date, and it is 
thought that it is overcome by the higher-efficiency accretion-powered emission, however, at very high 
rate mass transfer 𝑀̇ , up to and above the Eddington mass accretion rate, the thermonuclear burning is 
believed to occur only stably [66]. 
At the transition limit between the two states of burning, stable and unstable, an oscillatory 
burning regime is probably marginally stable on the NS surface [68], producing Millihertz quasiperiodic 
oscillations (mHz QPOs) [69].

If we assume, for a given observation, that the luminosity in excess with regards to the average 
luminosity of the cluster, intended as the mean luminosity obtained merging all the observations together 
and weighted on the exposure time, is relative to only one source, known or unknown, whose location is 
indicated by the position given by the satellite, taken with its error, we can infer some characteristics of 
that source by comparing the spectral products.

Non-thermal emission from MSPs can be produced by two different mechanisms. 
Such a high value of the photon index is uncommon when the non-thermal emission from a MSP 
is thought to originate in a shock wave by the interaction of the pulsar wind with the wind from the 
donor star or the interstellar medium, via the synchrotron or inverse Compton radiation of highly 
accelerated particles at the shock front and in the downstream flow from the shock [74]. This mechanism 
produces in fact spectra with a photon index of Γ ≅1.1 ÷ 1.7 [75]. 
In the most luminous MSPs, such as B1821–24 and B1937+21 [74], the narrow X-ray pulse profiles 
indicate that the non-thermal emission is beamed and must originate in the pulsar magnetosphere, near 
the light cylinder, and is probably synchrotron and curvature radiation from charged particles 
accelerated to relativistic speeds in the strong magnetic field. Non-thermal emission from MSPs can thus 
also be produced in the pulsar magnetosphere, f the magnetic field is strong enough, but not even this 
magnetospheric emission can be as soft as the observed one, since the typical values of the photon index 
are never higher than 2 [73]. 
As seen in many MSPs, a possible alternative could be a thermal component of the X-ray emission 
produced from the heated polar caps of the MSP [75], even though this could not explain the amount of

an apparent temperature rise, during the decay [91].

gravitational potential energy [91].

gradually change during time [93]. 
Burst properties are also thought to be correlated with the non-burst intensity, such as when, 
during the rise, the burst peak flux and the burst interval decrease as the non-burst flux increases [93]. 
Therefore, it is possible to conclude that surely the feature observed by Swift during ObsID 
00032148031 has a distinctive behaviour with regards to typical bursts, such as longer duration or lower 
peak luminosity, but that, at the same time, they also share some of the same properties, such as shape 
of the count rate curve as a function of time, cooling and, most likely, the generation mechanism. 
It is worth noting that when mHz quasi periodic oscillations are present, the mHz QPO frequency 
during the outburst peak is the same as the burst rate [94]. This is due to the fact that the mHz QPOs are 
formed by a series of faint and frequent bursts, which actually makes the distinction between bursts and 
mHz QPOs somewhat arbitrary, and based on conventions, such as a certain limit value for the recurrence 
time, usually 350 [𝑠] [94]. 
Therefore the burst-like feature, if not a real burst, as most likely it is not, lies just in between 
these two extremes.

First theorised as an end state of stellar evolution by Robert Oppenheimer and George Volkoff
in 1939, the first neutron star was discovered by Jocelyn Bell and Antony Hewish in 1967 [41], and they 
actually represents the only explanation for radio pulsars [42]. 
Bell, then a student, testing antennas for a new radio telescope, noticed signal that repeated itself 
not every 24 hours, corresponding to the solar day, but every 23 hours and 56 minutes, corresponding to 
the sidereal day, indicating that the source was anchored in the sky and not to Earth. 
The signal was firmly periodic and it could only be explained by the rotation of a small object 
with a strong gravity, otherwise it would have broken up: a neutron star was the only reasonable 
explanation [43], and the first radio pulsars, CP 1919, had just been discovered. 
 
 
 
 
A neutron star has generally a strong magnetic field, which can be first approximated by a 
dipole: it probably originated in the progenitor star, being then compressed and concentrated by the 
gravitational collapse when the supernova produced the NS [42]. 
The magnetic dipole is usually not aligned with the rotation axis, so that the magnetic and 
rotation poles are in different position. With the rotation of the star, a pulse of radiation can be observed 
every time one of the magnetic poles is in the field of view of the observer, which can happen once or

All fast spinning pulsars with strong magnetic fields are surrounded by a power wind nebulae

detectable because of a beaming effect [42].

The Uhuru satellite, in 1971, observed two pulsating sources, that showed Doppler shifts and X-
ray eclipses [42]. 
Both were binary systems in which the X-ray emitting object must have been a rapidly spinning 
NS, because a white dwarf would have disrupted itself due to the centrifugal force, being the period so 
short. 
The short binary period suggested that the two components were interacting, such as exchanging 
mass, due to their close proximity. 
Gravity was thus thought to be the source of the X-ray energy: some matter is transferred from 
the companion to the compact object, releasing consequently a large amount of gravitational energy as 
X-ray radiation. 
 
 
 
There are two forms that binary systems can assume: high-mass X-ray binaries (HMXBs) and low-
mass X-ray binaries (LMXBs). 
In HMXBs, the material transferred onto the compact object is given by a powerful stellar wind 
from an early-type and massive star. 
If, though, the mass-losing star has a low mass, it does not have a strong stellar wind and the 
mechanism that power the X-ray emission cannot be the same. 
In LMXBs, in fact, the material is transferred onto the compact object from a companion star that 
has evolved to fill its Roche lobe, the surface of equal gravity, through its Lagrangian point 𝐿ଵ.

could be modulated on the orbital period, if its orbit is eccentric, by a higher factor than that for a simple 
stellar wind from the B star.

pear–shape with the vertex on the inner Lagrangian point. The low-mass companion star has evolved 
and, since it fills its Roche lobe, it is losing mass, which flows onto the central object. This flowing matter 
forms a stream that impacts the accretion disc, forming a bulge, and, due to viscous forces, it is gradually 
accreted onto the compact object, where the X-rays are emitted. This mass transfer is stable only if the 
donor star is less massive than the compact object. In the case of NS systems, the donor star has then to 
be less massive than the Sun, causing the LMXB to be usually optically faint and with the donor star 
often undetectable [42]. 
The distribution of the observed LMXBs around the Galaxy is concentrated in and around its 
centre. In fact, these systems are associated with the older population II stars in the Galactic Bulge, and 
therefore the mass of their donor must have a mass comparable to lower than that of the Sun, because 
stars with mass > 1 𝑀⨀ would have evolved and disappeared long ago [42]. 
The main difference between the LMXB and the HMXB model relies on the mechanism of mass 
transfer. The material that leaves the donor star cannot flow directly onto the compact object, due to its 
angular momentum, but has to orbit around it, generating an accretion disc. In the HMXB, instead, the 
presence of the stellar wind carries very little angular momentum, and it is thus unlikely for an accretion 
disc to be formed, at least a large one. In a LMXB the wind is very feeble and an eventual resulting X-ray 
component would be completely undetectable. In this kind of systems, the disc is the responsible for many 
of the behaviours observed [42].

Discovered in 1975, these X-ray sources were initially detected in globular clusters [42]. Their 
dramatic X-ray events usually last less than 1 [𝑚𝑖𝑛] and have an energy of 10ଷଽ [𝑒𝑟𝑔] [42]. 
The beginning of the burst has a very rapid duration of around ~ 1 [𝑠] and occurs simultaneously 
at all energies, while its decay, instead, differs in the various energy ranges. For the coolest material, 
corresponding to lower energies, the burst shows a distinct tail, whereas the decay at higher energies is 
sharper, meaning that the temperature profile of the burst begins high and falls with the progression of 
the burst itself, from approximately ∼ 30 ∙ 10଺ [𝐾] at the peak to ∼ 15 ∙ 10଺ [𝐾] just after a time of the 
order of ∼10 [𝑠] [42]. 
The X-ray spectrum of a burst appears generally remarkably similar to that of a cooling 
blackbody. Moreover, in addition to the association with globular clusters, X-ray bursters have been 
generally observed in the Galactic Bulge, indicating it as an intrinsic property of Bulge sources and a 
distance, for the majority of these sources, of ∼5 ÷ 10 [𝑘𝑝𝑐]. After an initial expansion, the radius of the 
blackbody sphere necessary to emit the observed X-ray radiation in the cooling tail has turned out to be 
always in the range of ∼10 ÷ 15 [𝑘𝑚], exactly the expected size of a neutron star. Following all the 
previous considerations, it has been inferred that only NSs had the required properties in order to give an 
explanation to the detected details of X-ray bursts from many X-ray sources [42]. 
The most accepted model regarding bursts predicts an unstable nuclear burning occurring on the 
surface of a NS, yielding to an explosive thermonuclear flash of radiation, which peaks in the X-ray band 
[42]. 
The accretion of matter flowing onto the NS from the accretion disc creates a surface layer of 
hydrogen, which undergoes a stable nuclear fusion, yielding to the production of an underlying layer of 
helium. If the density and temperature of this helium layer increase to the critical point, it also begins to 
undergo a nuclear fusion generating heavier elements such as carbon. Despite the similarity, the helium 
==PAGE:111==
97 
 
fusing process is inherently unstable, which makes the helium to be rapidly consumed and produces a 
thermonuclear flash, observable as an X-ray burst. In the meanwhile, new hydrogen is steadily accreted 
from the disc, and after a similar, but not exact, period of time, another burst will happen. 
The recurrence time between bursts is correlated to the rate at which matter is accreted onto the 
NS: when it increases, then the burst frequency also increases, since the necessary fuel to generate the 
burst is accumulating more quickly. 
It is also believed that non-uniform burning around the neutron star surface is the possible cause 
for the accumulation of fuel which remains unburnt from the previous burst, and this fuel can trigger 
another bursts just after a short time, even of the order of minutes from the preceding one [42]. 
The thermonuclear flash model is also able to explain the observed ratio between the energy 
released in steady accretion and the energy emitted in an X-ray burst. 
The gain in kinetic energy of an hydrogen atom falling in the potential well of a NS is about ∼
100 [𝑀𝑒𝑉], which is emitted again, mainly as X-rays, in the moment it comes in touch with the NS surface, 
while when, after a short wait, that same atom is involved in the nuclear fusion generating the burst, it 
releases an energy of only around ∼1 [𝑀𝑒𝑉]. This ratio of ∼100 times more energy in steady emission, 
averaged over time, than in burst emission is exactly what detected in most X-ray bursters. 
Moreover, the observer would never observe anything if the fusion energy was released in a steady 
burning process, instead of occasional bursts, because it would be overcome by the gravitational energy 
emission [42].

The environment of a GC, with an extremely high stellar density in the core, is the main cause of 
compact binary formation. Two mechanisms have been thought to be the reason why a NS can acquire 
a companion in that environment. The first one occurs when a compact object captures a single star. 
==PAGE:121==

Unless they collide, they cannot be trapped into a stable binary orbit because they have a too high kinetic 
energy, but this excess of kinetic energy can be dissipated through tides [42]. 
As the compact object comes closer to the future companion, it distorts it, raising tides in it and 
absorbing energy, and, if this process occurs rapidly enough, the two stars remain bound to each other. 
The second mechanism happens when a compact object interacts with a wide on-interacting binary and 
a complex triple-star event takes place. The result is that the compact object replaces the lower mass star 
in the initial binary system, ejecting it [42]. 
Although the percentage of encounter for the triple-star event is higher, such wide non-interacting 
systems appear to be very rare in GCs. Thus the tidal capture mechanism is thought to be the most 
important one [42]. 
The theory that predicts an inevitable collapse of each cluster, due to  the high central density, 
with the formation of a massive BH, would instead be contradicted by this second mechanism of binary 
formation, which could even reverse the collapse, because the stars ejected from triple-star interactions 
could receive a velocity high enough to let them escape the cluster [42].

star, a direct hit is not able to disrupt the star, since its core is small and dense. and a very short period 
LMXB can be formed, with a spiral-in of the NS through the red giant’s extended atmosphere [42]. 
In the other two interactions out of three, the NS captures the other star, creating a LMXB whose 
typical orbital period is in the order of hours, for an interaction with a main sequence star, and usually 
longer than 10 [ℎ] when a red giant is involved [42]. 
Red giants are rarer, but also more present in the core and much larger, thus they represent easier 
targets to be hit by the compact object. It is thought that around 10 % of collisions involve red giants 
[42]. 
During this short-lived process, the NS slows down and accretes material, emitting copious X-rays, 
which disperse the red giant’s atmosphere. 
At the end of the spiral-in phase, the NS and the remaining WD will be tight in an orbit with a 
period of the order of ∼10 [𝑚𝑖𝑛] [42]. 
==PAGE:123==
109 
 
If instead the NS captures a main sequence star around its turn-off point of evolution, or a sub-
giant, the following mass transfer will be unstable, because even the flow of a small amount of matter 
from the companion will yield the star to overfill its Roche lobe, and thus to transfer more material, and 
the process will repeat itself. The star outer envelope will then rapidly expand and incorporate the NS, 
letting the situation become very similar to the case of collision between the NS and the red giant [42]. 
Whit the completion of the mass transfer phase, a detached NC remains: it is old, because created 
in the early stage of the GC from a massive star, and its magnetic field has decayed, thus it would have 
relatively little effect on the accretion process after the capture of another star [42]. 
The accretion disc extended down virtually to the surface of the NS, and the eventual transfer of 
angular momentum from accreted matter would have spun up the NS to the velocity of material in the 
innermost orbit of the accretion disc, which corresponds to periods of just a few [𝑚𝑠], ususally less than 
10 [𝑚𝑠] and down to a minimum of 1.6 [𝑚𝑠]. With this kind of periods, the NS is re-cycled as a radio 
pulsar. This behaviour has been observed in some GCs such as 47 Tucanae [42]. 
The re-born pulsar emits a powerful pulsar wind which is thought to be able to ablate its 
companion star, yielding to its complete evaporation within a period of time of about 10଻ [𝑦𝑒𝑎𝑟𝑠]. Weak 
X-rays are radiated by the shocks that forms near the degenerate companion star, permitting their 
detection as low-luminosity sources in GCs [42].

The environment of a GC can also lead to the formation of binary radio pulsars, systems formed 
by two NSs. Evidence has been found that LMXBs and MSPs swap their partners through exchange 
interactions with detached NSs in the dense GC cores, as represented in figure 3.13 [42]. 
It is believed that double NS systems evolve to shorter periods through the emission of 
gravitational waves (GWs), and can eventually coalesce and produce short γ-ray bursts. Therefore, 
LMXBs and MSPs in a GC form a continuum on the binary evolutionary sequence, corresponding to the 
latter stages of figure 3.13, and gives a hint on how GCs form and remain stable over very long timescales 
[42]. 
Finally, the following results have been inferred from observations of external galaxies [42]: 
- 
Elliptical galaxies usually contains twice the number of GCs than spiral galaxies; 
- 
Most X-ray sources in elliptical galaxies are LMXBs,  because of the absence of  
high-mass stars; 
- 
Some of the GC sources could be BHs, since they have luminosities high enough to  
let them be considered appropriate candidates; 
- 
LMXBs are preferentially observed in bright GCs, showing that they scale with the  
cluster mass, which increases the percentage of collisions; 
- 
In spiral galaxies, most X-ray sources are not within GCs, while in elliptical  
galaxies the number of X-ray sources within GCs is approximately equal to that around 
the disc of the galaxy.

variability over timescales spanning from [ℎ𝑠] to [𝑦𝑒𝑎𝑟𝑠], 31 being chromospherically active binaries, 
while 70 being background source populations not concentrated around the cluster center [51]. Among 
these sources, a total of ∼25 ÷ 30 MSPs, and anyway less than 60 at a 95 % level of conﬁdence, and ∼
300 NS were estimated [51].
